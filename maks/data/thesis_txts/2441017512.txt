Abstract
This report is a theoretical and practical study of several GNSS positioning models. The ﬁrst part
shortly covers basic GNSS theory, and introduces the challenges of precise GNSS positioning, through
a presentation of the observation equations and all their related errors, biases and delays.
In the next part the theory behind PPP, RTK, and NRTK models is described, while ﬁnally the
current state-of-the-art PPP-RTK model is presented.
Presentations are then backed up by practical positioning measurements in order to demonstrate the
features and capabilities of current technologies except PPP-RTK, since no such system is currently
implemented in Denmark. Tested methods include post-processed PPP, single-baseline RTK, VRS,
MAX and i-MAX, which are all available through Leica SmartNet.
A PPP-RTK system, called TAPAS, is however currently being worked upon by the Technical Uni-
versity of Denmark, and this report should thus also be seen as an introductory study of that system,
even though it wasn’t up-and-running at the time of making of this project.
It is clear from the work presented here, that current RTK methods all perform equally well in
good measurement conditions, this should also be expected since, as will be made clear, these RTK
methods are more or less one and the same.
A PPP solution is marred by the fact that long convergence times are needed in order to establish a
precise position, and while RTK models achieve sub-cm standard deviations in less than 5 seconds,
PPP only achieves the same with more than 20 min of initialization.
While it is unlikely that a PPP-RTK system will perform better than current RTK systems in these
conditions, it will be made clear that this new system has several other advantages, and a wholly
diﬀerent approach than RTK.

Introduction
The space department of the Technical University of Denmark (DTU Space) is currently working with
Styrelsen for Dataforsyning og Eﬀektivisering (SDFE) on a high accuracy GNSS correction system
in the city of Aarhus. This project, named TAPAS (Testbed in Aarhus for Precision Positioning and
Autonomous Systems), will (when completed) deliver GNSS corrections of unprecedented quality in
real-time, and thus enable sub centimeter positioning within the network coverage area in Aarhus.
The system will make use of SDFEs current reference stations scattered throughout Denmark, and
another 12 stations established in a dense network in and around Aarhus in order to ensure very
precise error corrections within the city.
This report is a study of GNSS augmentation methods such as PPP, RTK and Network RTK,
leading to a presentation of the state-of-the-art PPP-RTK method, which is implemented in TAPAS.
It should be noted that the name PPP-RTK is adopted for this report, since it seems to be the most
common term for the technique, although in literature names PPP-SSR or PPP-AR are sometimes
also used. This naming discussion is shortly revisited later in the report.
Besides the theoretical studies, measurements on current technologies are performed in order to
further demonstrate their functional diﬀerences.
The purpose is to give readers an insight into the workings behind both current augmentation sys-
tems and the TAPAS system, in order to provide a better understanding of how the new PPP-RTK
method implemented in TAPAS is diﬀerent to older techniques, and why this is the way of the future.
It would have been interesting to include measurements on the actual TAPAS system, to see how
it actually performs compared to the current technologies, however due to the time constraint on
this report, as well as the fact that TAPAS, at the moment of writing this, is not operational, has
pushed such measurements into the future work category. As such this report may in many ways be
considered a preparation for actual measurements on TAPAS, which has to be performed in order to
ensure that it lives up to the requirements set by the project test plan.
When reading this report, it should be noted that terms such as method, approach, technique and
technology are used interchangeably to describe the presented GNSS correction models - in other
words, it makes no diﬀerence whether it is denoted PPP-RTK method, PPP-RTK approach, PPP-
RTK technique, or so forth.
Furthermore when citations are referred to directly, they will appear in line with the text, and normal
pitch size as: [1], whereas if they are used indirectly to refer to the source of a certain expressed
value or equation, they will appear in superscript as: [1].
While the report is primarily focused on TAPAS and current GNSS augmentation techniques, the
basics of GNSS positioning will be shortly described in the ﬁrst chapter, in order to introduce even
new readers to the diﬃculties of precise GNSS positioning, and thus explain why augmentations
systems are even necessary.

2
Theory
This chapter will present the concepts and theory behind the a wide range of positioning approaches
concluding with the PPP-RTK approach, which builds on the other presented methods PPP and
RTK, and is used in the TAPAS system. To ease the theoretical deductions and equations only the
Global Positioning System (GPS - the US GNSS) will be used in the the derivations presented in this
chapter. TAPAS will make use of both GPS and GLONASS (and later also Galileo) observations,
and any of these could be used as an onset for the theory. However GPS is well documented in both
literature and in the oﬃcial Interface Speciﬁcations (IS) found on the oﬃcial gps.gov website, and
this makes GPS an easy choice.
It isn’t diﬃcult to translate equations to GLONASS observations (or to multi-GNSS observations
for that matter), but it adds little to the understanding of the fundamental concepts to do so.
Because it is chosen to work with the GPS constellation, the ﬁrst part of this chapter will be a very
short introduction to the essential basics of GPS. Next the chapter goes in a more general direction,
and describes observation equations and the basic approach for determining receiver/user position.
The following sections details several diﬀerent positioning models, amongst others the PPP and RTK
models, and these are fundamental in understanding, not only the techniques used in the PPP-RTK
approach that is incorporated in TAPAS, but also why this approach is ground breaking and perhaps
the most optimal solution for precise positioning.
The PPP-RTK model itself is explained in detail in the ﬁnal part of this chapter, and among other
things undiﬀerenced (UD) network parameter estimation and Integer Ambiguity Resolution (IAR)
are described. Though this will not be an in-depth explanation of all concepts, the PPP-RTK model
will be described thoroughly enough that an intuitive understanding of the concepts is gained.
Lastly the chapter ends with a comparison of the PPP-RTK model to other positioning models to
make the many advantages of PPP-RTK clear.
2.1
Basic GNSS positioning
In this section the basics of GNSS positioning are explained, starting with an introduction to the GPS
constellation and its design, as the following theory and positioning methods will only be described
in relation to the GPS constellation only. As mentioned this is mostly to simplify the theory, and
any positioning method applies equally well to all GNSS (or even multiple GNSS with the inclusion
of inter system biases (ISB)).
In this section focus will be on the GPS receiver observations, and the observation equations which
are the basic information used for any positioning. Observation equations will be explained and
presented along with a short description of all error sources, and how (and to which extent) they
complicate positioning.
Finally a general solution to a simple code based observation will be presented through linearisation
of the observation equations and an iterative least squares (ILS) solution.

2.1.1
The Global Positioning System
As mentioned earlier this is just a quick presentation of GPS, since this in itself is not the topic of
this report, although it here serves as fundamental knowledge to understand the following position-
ing models. As said GPS is well documented, and for further understanding of the system look for
instance to [1], [4], [7], and [11] or the oﬃcial GPS IS [3].
GPS is the US GNSS, and was originally developed for military purposes, but because of an incident
with a Korean aircraft in 1983, it was made available to the public very early in its experimental
phase[6]. The ﬁrst GPS satellite was launched on 22nd of February 1978, and in December 1993
Initial Operating Capability was declared with 24 operational satellites in orbit. Back then however
the publicly available Standard Positioning Service (SPS) was degraded to only provided positioning
accuracies of around 100 m [SOURCE!!]. In May 2000 the Selective Availability (SA) degrading
was turned of (in part due to development of diﬀerential GPS services to improve civilian accuracy)
greatly improving the SPS accuracy.
Today the GPS constellation consist of 31 operational satellites [SOURCE!!], transmitting signals
on up to three frequencies, and provides a strong global coverage with accuracies of 5-10 m for ordi-
nary SPS users. Currently GPS has the largest operational constellation of any GNSS, and this also
means that in general GPS provides better coverage compared to other GNSS [SOURCE!!].
For comparison ﬁgure 2.1.1.1 which has been borrowed from [1] shows a quick comparison between
all existing GNSS.
GPS is widely used, and some form of GPS L1 C/A solutions are applied by almost all gadgets con-
taining a GNSS receiver (smart-watches, smart-phones, tablets, tracking devices, etc.). It is run by
the US government, and freely available to anyone with a GPS receiver without any subscription-fee.
Furthermore the system is well documented with interface speciﬁcations[3] (IS) available online at
www.gps.gov, describing code, navigation messages, parameters, orbits, etc. (current IS document
is IS-200H[3]).

The most widely known (and used) code for GPS is the Coarse Acquisition (C/A) code used on
the in-phase of the L1 band transmission. The C/A code is a Pseudorandom Noise (PRN) code
- more speciﬁcally a Gold code. Gold codes have the unique property that they have very little
cross-correlation, and this is essential when using the CDMA, since this is the property that makes
it possible to distinguish between signals on the same frequency.
In praxis the C/A code is a 1023 bit long binary sequence generated by two 10-bit shift registers. It
has a chipping rate of 1.023 Mbps meaning a full sequence of the C/A code lasts 1 ms[8] (the code re-
peats itself each ms). When terms like code-based solution, code-based receiver or code observations
are used, these refer to the fact that the signal code (C/A or other) are used to determine position
or pseudoranges.
The P-code should also be shortly mentioned. As the C/A code the P-code is modulated on to the
L1 carrier - unlike C/A however, the P-code is also transmitted on L2 [SOURCE!!]. The code
lasts not 1 ms, but an entire week, and is generated by outputs from four 12 bit shift registers with
a chipping rate of 10.23 Mbps. It may be encrypted as a so-called P(Y) code, to be used only by
military equipment.
For design and theoretical purposes the P-code is somewhat important since signal timing delays are
deﬁned from the signal delay between the L1 and L2 P-codes. This is discussed further in section
2.1.3.

While the C/A or P code is used to distinguish between signals, the actual information transmitted
by the GPS satellites are encoded in the navigation message. This is a binary message with a chipping
rate of 50 bps and has a total length of 37500 bits, giving it a total duration of 12.5 minutes. These
37500 bits contain information from each individual SV such as ephemeris (positioning parameters),
SV health, data accuracy, GPS time, full constellation almanac, etc. The structure and contents of
the navigation message are described in full in [3].
Given that GPS uses a quadrature phase shift keying modulation, two signals (or data transmis-
sions) may be modulated onto a carrier. Here the C/A code is modulated onto the in-phase, while
the p-code is modulated onto the quadra-phase, such that the full transmitted GPS signal, ss(t), on
L1 may be given as[16]
ss(t) =
p
2Pc(Cs(t) ⊕Ds(t))cos(2πfL1t)+
p
2PP(P s(t) ⊕Ds(t))sin(2πfL1t)
(2.1.1.1)
where Pc and PP are the power of the signals, Cs is the C/A code sequence, P s is the P-code sequence
and Ds is the navigation message. The symbol ⊕denotes the XOR operation.
As a ﬁnal note the cheapest and therefore the most widely used GPS (GNSS) devices are single
frequency (SF) code-based receivers. These provide quick, but rough SPS solutions (accuracies in
meters range) based on the L1 C/A code, which is often suﬃcient for users of smart-phones, smart-
watches or GPS navigation. It is however also possible to use the phase of the (L1) carrier wave
itself to determine position. Such phase based positioning is generally more diﬃcult, requires more
advanced software, and more time to ﬁnd a solution, but also generates a much more precise position
than the code based alternative. One of the advantages of the TAPAS project is that it provides so-
called undiﬀerenced (UD) corrections usable by both code and phase based receivers, thus allowing
ultra high accuracies for phase based systems, but also quick and precise positions with SF code

2.1.3
Observation errors
As mentioned the key to achieving high accuracy positioning is to eliminate or at least heavily reduce
errors in the observation equations. There are of course diﬀerent ways to do this, and the positioning
models presented in the following sections, each applies its own approach.
In general there are diﬀerent ways of handling observation errors to achieve high accuracy with
GNSS. Here the three methods are adopted from [15] with a few adjustments:
 Elimination - Canceling an error by use of diﬀerencing (or in part linear combinations as the
ionosphere-free combination).
 Estimation - The error is estimated as an unknown in the positioning process (like receiver
clock).
 Correction - Errors are modeled by other sources and broadcast to the user (as with ionospheric
corrections in the GPS navigation message).
[15] describes how diﬀerent positioning models may handle receiver and satellite biases. In general
none of the mentioned methods are necessarily better than other, it is rather a question of what
support or what set-up is needed for each approach. The descriptions of positioning models will refer
to these deﬁnitions of error handling, and explain further how errors may be estimated, eliminated
or calibrated for each speciﬁc positioning method.
In general however errors may be divided into three categories: Satellite dependent, receiver depen-
dent and distance dependent, which in short may be described, and handled as:
 Satellite dependent: These include any error term that may be ascribed to the satellite, that
is; satellite clock and hardware biases.
These may be corrected by values delivered by the navigation message as in simple SPP, cor-
rected by a Continually Operating Reference Station (CORS) network as in PPP, or eliminated
by between-receiver diﬀerencing as in DGNSS and RTK.
 Receiver dependent: These include receiver clock and hardware biases as well as phase center
oﬀsets.
Such errors may be handled through estimation as in the standard SPP model, or be eliminated
by between-satellite diﬀerencing, as is the case in DGNSS and RTK.
Page 9

 Distance dependent: This category includes all errors that are unique to the path between
satellite and receiver, and as such they pose the greatest challenge, since they may in no easy
way be eliminated. They include ﬁrst and foremost tropospheric- and ionospheric delay.
Often these errors are handled in several steps. Usually they are ﬁrst reduced by corrections
determined by models or by linear combinations. Next residuals are either estimated or further
corrected by values determined by a CORS network.
This will be described to a much greater extent for each positioning model in the following sections.
In the following subsections all error terms will be explained to provide a better understanding of

Atmospheric delays are split into two groups: Ionospheric and Tropospheric delays, and these have

The tropospheric delay is delays caused by the lower atmosphere. Troposphere often refers to the
part of the atmosphere only 10 km above the surface, which is the part that is aﬀected by weather.
In GNSS however tropospheric delay refer to delays in both the troposphere and stratosphere which
thus includes a range of 0 to about 60 km above the surface[6].
As with ionosphere these delays are caused by reduced speed and refraction of the signal, which pro-
longs the signal path, and will thus cause a delay compared to the straight path between satellite and
receiver. Unlike the ionosphere, the troposphere is non-dispersive for frequencies below 30 GHz[5],
meaning that refraction for L1, L2 or L5 bands are not frequency dependant - they are the same.
For modeling purposes tropospheric delays are often considered a sum of a wet and a dry component.
The latter is related to air density, which is highly correlated to surface pressure, and is thus easily
modeled, while the wet part is related to temperature and the highly variable water vapor content,
and is therefore diﬃcult to determine solely from meteorological measurements at the surface.
Fortunately the dry component make up about 80-90% of the total tropospheric delay, and in this
way a large part of the delay may be removed in post-processing, using for instance the Saastamoinen
model (presented in [1]), with either current meteorological data or standard values.

remove most of the dry component.

Atmospheric errors in general poses a special problem since they (along with orbit errors) are distance
or position dependent. This means that each position has a unique atmospheric delay, and where
other errors may be handled or determined externally, atmospheric delays must be in some way be
determined at receiver position.
This is an important fact, since these (especially ionospheric) delays are often signiﬁcant, and must
be reduced for even standard positioning to be acceptable (<10 m). For precise positioning this
becomes even more important, and in such cases the atmospheric errors may in general be handled
in the following way: The receiver reduces tropospheric delay through modeling, and if possible uses
an IF combination to reduce ionospheric delay. Next residual delays may be handled by in two ways:
1. Residual delays are estimated as unknown parameters (along with x-, y-, and z-coordinates) in
the positioning solution. The drawback with this solution is that it requires several observations
(often observations for several minutes) in order to determine residual delays precisely. This is
called a ionosphere-ﬂoat solution.
2. Residual delays are modeled by external sources (or by the receiver, using external input).
This leads to fast, precise positions, but requires a regional network of reference stations in
order to adequately determine atmospheric delays at receiver position. This is often called a
ionosphere-weighted solution.
These two approaches are seen in respectively PPP and RTK methods, and the consequences will be
presented and explained in the coming chapters.

bias terms.

Phase Center Oﬀset and Phase Wind-up
These terms will only be described very shortly, since they are omitted in the observation equations
(2.1.2.5) and (2.1.2.6).
Phase center oﬀset (PCO) basically describes the oﬀset from antenna reference point (ARP) to the
antenna phase center (APC). The observed distances (both code and phase) are determined between
the phase centers of the satellite and receiver antenna, while the wanted distance is that between
ARPs, and thus PCO must be taken into account.
Most often however, these PCOs are provided by the antenna manufacturer, and are therefore easily
handled.
Phase wind-up is the eﬀect due to satellite or receiver antenna rotation, which may, due to GNSS
signals being circularly polarized, be interpreted at the receiver as a change in phase (and therefore a
Page 18
==PAGE:26==
September 30, 2018
change in range), thereby adding an error to the phase observation (but only the phase observation).
It is possible to compute wind-up corrections[2], which is relevant for high accuracy positioning, since
one full rotation of 360◦causes an error of one wavelength[1] - that is about 20 cm for L1.
Phase wind-up however only has to be handled at the moment of satellite rotation, and is therefore
not something, that generally has to be accounted for, which is why it will be disregarded for the
remainder of the report.
Multipath
Multipath happens when a reﬂected signal is received, and thus the corresponding observation will
be larger due to the signal path being longer than the straight path.
This happens for instance when a receiver is placed in between tall buildings, or when there is no
line-of-sight between receiver and satellite, and only a reﬂected signal may be received.
Multipath may be a large problem, but it depends a lot on receiver position, and it is thus very
diﬃcult to handle, and therefore it is omitted in the observation equations in this report.
Due to the diﬃcult nature of multipath, it is expected to be the most signiﬁcant error on positions
provided by both TAPAS and other methods, since however measurements in this report is performed
in open rural areas, multipath should be very close to zero.
Site displacements
When it comes to the cm level positioning obtainable with the TAPAS network, there is one last
source of errors that has to be accounted for: Site displacement. Though often a minor contribution
to positioning error compared to other eﬀects, it still has to be accounted for to achieve the wanted
precision. Site displacement terms are also omitted from observation equations, since they may be
modelled, and thus removed (or calibrated) in pre-processing stages.
Although there are several physical eﬀects contribution to site displacement, here shall be mentioned
only four of them: Plate motion, solid Earth tides, ocean loading, and pole tide.
Plate motion is the displacement eﬀect from the movement of the tectonic plates in the Earth.
This eﬀect constitutes an error of up to 0.1 meter a year[1], but for users it is easily accounted for
by using an up-to-date reference frame such as the ITRF14, or in Denmark by using the ETRF89 in
which the Eurasian plate coordinates are deﬁned static.
Solid Earth tides are periodical deformations of the ’solid’ Earth surface by gravitational pull from
the Sun and Moon, and works much like ocean tides. This eﬀect induces horizontal displacements
of about 0.3 m and horizontal displacements of about 5 cm[1], and as such obviously needs to be ac-
counted for in precise positioning. Fortunately modelling removes by far most of this contribution[1].
Ocean loading is the (primarily vertical) displacement of the crust due to the load of a nearby
ocean. Although eﬀects are usually minor, in coastal regions coordinate change may be up to 10
cm[1]. Although the eﬀect on Aarhus is unknown, it is a coastal town, and thus deﬁnitely aﬀected
by ocean loading.
Pole tide is the site displacement caused by the moving rotational axis of the Earth. This eﬀect
may cause a vertical error of about 2.5 cm and a horizontal error of about 0.6 cm[1].

2.2
Point Positioning Models
Point positioning models are the standard of GPS. It involves determining absolute position with the
use of a single receiver, and could be the GPS L1 C/A code-based solution, but a point positioning
model could also be related to a DF receiver using both L1 P- and L2 P-code to determine position
through ionospheric free combinations.
In this section the basic Single Point Positioning (SPP) method and the newer and more accurate
Precise Point Positioning (PPP) method will be described. The ﬁrst being somewhat similar to
the code based solution presented in the previous section, while the other, more accurate solution,
is based on phase observations and a ﬂoat solution to the phase ambiguity with a little help from
precise satellite corrections computed by a global network and broadcast to the user via satellite
or wireless data network. Note that these corrections are of course more accurate than corrections
broadcast with the GPS navigation message.
Point positioning models are often the most simple to use in practice, since they require nothing but
a single receiver and access to satellites (a clear sky). For PPP receivers need to be able to receive
and handle correction data properly (if not handled in post-processing), but compared to setting up
base stations, reference stations or extra receivers, this is easy, and especially in desolate or rough
environments, with no nearby reference stations, SPP or PPP approaches are clearly preferable.
2.2.1
Single Point Positioning - SPP
The simplest of the positioning approaches is SPP. This method is based on using a single standalone
receiver, with no external input, except for the GPS navigation message. The approach is here de-
ﬁned as purely code-based, and while the most simple and ordinary is to use the GPS L1 C/A code,
dual- or even multi-frequency code-based solutions would also fall under the SPP category.
While this method is easy to use, and often cheap to implement (requires a single GPS L1 C/A chip
set), it is also the least accurate positioning method, and as seen in table 2.2 errors might be in the
range of 5-10 m[1].
The solution closely follows the OLS described in section 2.1.5, and as with any inverse method,
more observations leads to better results, and therefore precision might be improved by observing
several GNSS at once, and using several frequencies - which may also be used to remove ionospheric
contributions via IF combinatinos. However it still doesn’t change the fact that other (especially
phase based) methods are more accurate.
For the SF GPS receiver the SPP method is basically the method described by equation (2.1.5.7)
and (2.1.5.9). It is clear from equations (2.1.2.5), that in no way is it possible with observations
purely on the C/A code to estimate all the error terms. Even though 100 satellites were observed,
they would all still add more unknown parameters than they would resolve. This basically means,
that the only way to accurately solve the positioning problem is by using corrections provided by
the navigation message, perhaps combined with an internal standard model of the troposphere (for
instance a Saastamoinen model with standard meteorological input), and then lumping residuals into
a single error term es for each observation, as was done in the deductions in section 2.1.5.
Dual- or Multifrequency receivers may take advantage of the IF combination in order to remove
the ﬁrst order eﬀect of the ionosphere, which will improve positioning a lot compared to using the
Klobuchar corrections included in the navigation message.
This approach is often used in Precise Point Positioning (PPP), because removing ionospheric delay,
makes it easier to determine the phase ambiguity.

2.2.2
Precise Point Positioning - PPP
Compared to the SPP model there are two important diﬀerences that distinguishes the the PPP
model:
1. Satellite orbit error, satellite clock error and maybe also satellite code hardware bias corrections
are supplied from an external source. These corrections are determined by a global continuously
operating reference station (CORS) network, and then transmitted to the user for instance by
satellite as ﬁgure 2.2.2.1 illustrates. In this way observing more satellites does not add more
unknown parameters to positioning, thus allowing for higher redundancy (more equations than
unknowns), when tracking several satellites.
2. PPP techniques use phase observations for very accurate positioning. Due to the fact that
phase ambiguities cannot be resolved as integers, and precise ionospheric corrections are not
provided, this however leads to long convergence times for positioning.
Figure 2.2.2.1: Illustration of the PPP concept. a) A networks of base stations monitor satellites and
determine GNSS corrections; b) corrections are uploaded to a satellite and broadcast to users; c) The user
applies observations and GNSS corrections to enable PPP. Borrowed from [1].
In real-time PPP corrections are send to the receiver via satellite or wireless data network, and the
receiver applies the corrections to ﬁnd its position.
PPP may also be handled via post-processing, in which receiver observations are processed on a
computer afterwards, often leading to better accuracies due to the availability of IGS Rapid or Final
products for both orbits and satellite clocks (see table 2.1).
Both real-time and post-processed PPP require ≈30min[1] of observations in order to determine a
precise position.
Several appraoches to PPP exists. In [1] three such approaches are mentioned:
1. SF PPP ionosphere corrected
2. SF PPP ionosphere ﬂoat
3. DF PPP ionosphere ﬂoat
This list displays the fact that ionospheric delay is one of the most diﬃcult errors to handle in PPP,
and one of the more signiﬁcant reasons that long convergence times are needed for PPP solutions. If
no precise external corrections are available, there are often two ways for the PPP method to handle
ionosphere: It is either corrected by modeling (ionosphere corrected), which is often based on global
Page 24
==PAGE:32==
September 30, 2018
ionospheric maps, and thus not very precise, or ionosphere is estimated as an unknown ﬂoat in the
positioning solution (ionosphere ﬂoat).
Most often a DF receiver is used in PPP in order to make use of an IF combination. In this way
(ﬁrst order) ionospheric delays are eliminated, and only ionospheric residuals need be estimated.
Furthermore since clock corrections from for instance the IGS are based on the IF combination,
they need not be processed any further, and ﬁnally, again if using IGS products, code biases are
unnecessary due to the fact that the IF combination per deﬁnition is without bias.
Still for this report it has been chosen to present only a real-time SF ionosphere ﬂoat model. This is
both to restrict the use of space, but also because one of the deﬁning features of PPP-RTK is, that
it provides corrections for SF users. Therefore a presentation of a SF approach here, will allow for
better comparison with the SF PPP-RTK model presented later.
While most errors in SPP are corrected by broadcast models and parameters, for PPP a CORS
network estimates precise satellite orbits, satellite clock errors, dts and code biases, ds
j, which are

2.4
Relative Positioning Models
The term relative positioning refers to the fact that position of a user receiver (referred to as the
rover) is determined relative to that of a pivot receiver with known coordinates, often referred to as
a master station, reference station, base station or just base (depending on its exact function), while
the vector (or distance) from base to rover is referred to as the baseline.
This section will primarily deal with real time kinematic (RTK) methods, that builds on diﬀerencing
techniques, as RTK models make use of DD observations in order to remove all satellite and receiver
clocks and biases. Furthermore distance dependent errors, such as ionosphere and troposphere (and
orbit errors), are modeled for the rover position in order to precisely correct rover observation equa-
tions for these delays, leaving only position in the code observations, and position and DD phase
ambiguities in the phase observations. In short the key features of RTK is:
1. Uses phase observations in order to achieve high accuracies.
2. RTK makes use of DD observations on a rover-master station baseline, thus constraining phase
ambiguities to integer values in DD integer ambiguity terms.
3. Network RTK solutions provide calibrations of distance dependent errors (ionospheric delay,
tropospheric delay and orbit errors) at the rover location, to further reduce the number of
unknown in the observation equations, thus enabling short initial convergence times.
The exact implementations of these features varies for diﬀerent RTK methods, as will be seen in the
following sections.
While in the PPP model phase ambiguities and satellite hardware phase bias could not be dis-
tinguished, and as such the ambiguity could not be determined as an integer, here the use of DDs
ensure that the phase ambiguity may be determined as an integer. This applies an extra constraint
on N s
r,j in least squares solution (such as equation (2.2.2.7) in the PPP case), and with advanced
integer ambiguity resolution (often denoted integer AR or IAR) methods, N s
r,j can be determined as a
constant integer in a short time, leading to both fast and precise positioning without the convergence
time of PPP. This is essentially what makes RTK methods both unique and widely used.
There are several approaches to RTK, but commonly the rover must receive both raw observa-
tions from a master base and corrections for the distance dependent errors.
On short baselines (up to about 10 km for SF receivers and about 50 km for DF receivers) it is often
suﬃcient to apply only raw observations at the rover location, as it may be assumed that over such
short distances atmospheric errors at the base and rover are the same[1], and as such DDs eliminates
both tropospheric and Ionospheric delays. On longer baselines however it is often necessary to model
the atmospheric errors to provide a rover with precise corrections for its own location.
Such corrections most often stem from a network of reference stations (a CORS network), and these
techniques are therefore denoted network RTK or NRTK.
RTK is a widespread technique, that is used heavily especially for surveying purposes[6] because
of the fast and precise positions it delivers. This however comes with the requirement that the rover
has to be in close range of a known base station or within range of a CORS network.
Here the single-baseline RTK solution will ﬁrst be presented as the basis of RTK models, while the
following section on network RTK methods will present three diﬀerent NRTK approaches, and ex-
plain how both baselines and corrections are handled diﬀerently for each approach. Although RTK

2.4.2
Network RTK
Due to the fact that some errors are heavily distance (or location) dependant it is often diﬃcult for
a base station to calibrate rover delays precisely when baselines are more than 10 km[1]. One way to
extent the range of RTK is to establish several reference stations in a network, often connected via a
central processing facility (CPF), that manages calculations and contact to the rover [SOURCE!!!]
- not unlike the PPP method illustrated in ﬁgure 2.2.2.1, although reference stations in an RTK
network must be within a much shorter range (no more than 100-200 km[1]) than in PPP.
In reality all NRTK methods builds on the same premise: Form DD observations on a master-rover
baseline, and interpolate distance dependant errors to rover location. What sets NRTK methods
apart is how the interpolation is handled - is it done at the CPF or the rover, what interpolation
method is used, how is network data handled, and so on.
As such the simplest type of NRTK is to select the reference station nearest to the rover as the
master station, and then make use of a single-baseline RTK solution. This however doesn’t really
extent range, since the baseline still has to be < 10 km in order to adequately eliminate the distance
dependent errors. Neither does the single baseline method take advantage of the actual network.
However, a few other methods exists which uses the network extensively to achieve precise atmo-
spheric calibrations for a rover within the network range. Three of these methods will be presented
here; the Virtual Reference Station (VRS), the Fl¨achen-Korrektur Parameter (FKP), and the Master-
auxiliary Concept (MAC). In general all these solutions rely on some kind of interpolation of network
data to determine the distance dependant errors at the rover location. To ensure a proper network
solution, all three methods require atleast three reference stations located within a distance of 20-
100km of one another[18].

VRS is the oldest network positioning strategy[18]. The simple concept is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.4.2.1,
and is best described as a single-baseline solution using observations from a ﬁctitious base near the
rover. These ﬁctitious observations are generated in a CPF from the observations in the network.
To make this work, the rover ﬁrst determines a rough position using the code observations and
navigation message corrections, which is then transmitted to a CPF. The CPF gathers data from
all involved reference stations, and interpolates dispersive and non-dispersive errors to the receivers
approximate position, and in this way generates a ﬁctitious observation from a nearby base, which is
transmitted back to the rover, that essentially uses this data in a short single-baseline RTK solution,
between the rover and a nearby (virtual) reference station (at, or close to the approximate rover
location). The user solution is thus exactly as described in section 2.4.1.
For network proceesing the CPF may use diﬀerent strategies for interpolating the distance dependant
errors. In [18] four diﬀerent interpolation techniques are mentioned:
 Plane Triangles
 Inverse Distance Weighting
 Least Squares Method for Polynomial Coeﬃcients
 Collocation
Several other methods could probably be used to the same eﬀect, but no further description of any
of them will be presented here. These methods are only mentioned, to show that there are indeed a
number of ways interpolation may be handled.
As with both PPP and other RTK methods, VRS can be applied in post-processing, and thereby
use IGS ﬁnal orbits to achieve even better accuracies.

Master-auxiliary Concept
The MAC was ﬁrst described in [19], but has since been reﬁned in many ways. In [22] the seven
steps involved in a general MAC approach is described as:
1. Transmission of data to the network CPF: Raw data of both code- and phase observations
are sent from all reference stations to the network CPF.
2. Network ambiguity resolution: Phase observations from all stations are reduced to a com-
mon ambiguity level. Citing [22] this is deﬁned as: ’Two reference stations are said to be on
a common ambiguity level if the integer ambiguities for each phase range (satellite-receiver
pair) have been removed (or adjusted) so that when double diﬀerences are formed the integer
ambiguities cancel’. This concept will be further described later in this section.
Page 36
==PAGE:44==
September 30, 2018
3. Site selection: There are two approaches to site selection; 1) With two way communication
between rover and CPF, the rover may transmit its approximate position, with which the CPF
may select data from only the reference stations nearest to the rover (a usual approach is a
radius of 70 km from the rover[18]). 2) Sets of reference stations may be predeﬁned by the
network, and corrections may then be broadcast to rover users[22].
4. Formation of the network messages: One reference station is selected as the master (often
the one nearest to the rover) for which full observations are transmitted. For all other (auxiliary)
stations, only between station SD corrections are transmitted.
5. Transmission of corrections: The CPF sends the MAC data to the rover.
6. Localisation of errors: The rover uses the data provided by the network to interpolate
dispersive and non-dispersive errors to its own location. Several available interpolation methods
are listed in [22].
7. Determination of rover position: Using the network information and interpolated errors,
the rover resolves its ambiguities and determines position.
As with all other RTK methods the approach of MAC is to form DD observations between the rover
and master station, and use the network data to calibrate tropospheric and ionospheric delays to the
rover position in order to reduce the DD phase observations so that they consist of only geometric
range, ρ, and integer ambiguity, N, as is the case with equation (2.4.1.1).

MAC solutions are often divided into two categories: Master-Auxiliary Corrections (MAX) and in-

MAX is the typical MAC approach, where the rover receives all network corrections, and inter-
polates distance dependent errors itself. This may be handled via broadcast, where corrections from
ﬁxed cells (a predetermined network size) are used, and as such the user must make sure to employ
the appropriate correction stream.
The rover may however also transmit its approximate location to the CPF, which then selects an
appropriate network, limiting the amount of information that has to be transmitted back to the rover.
i-MAX works much like VRS, where the rover sends its approximate location to the CPF, which
then handles interpolation for rover position, and then transmits interpolated data back to the rover.
Since this method works like VRS it is useful for legacy model rovers, where the MAX method is
not implemented. The approach has some advantages over VRS: ﬁrst of all it uses a physical master
station, meaning kinematic applications are not a problem. Furthermore i-MAX uses rover position
to select an appropriate network size, thus limiting the amount of processing required in the CPF.

RTK Summary
In general the various NRTK approaches are very much alike, and the thing that really sets them
apart is basically where and how the distance dependent errors are handled.
A way to diﬀerentiate NRTK methods is by network level. That is, at what stage of the network
process are the corrections transmitted to the rover. The diagram of ﬁgure 2.4.2.5 shows the general
network processing stages, and also illustrates the fact, that in the end all NRTK methods come
down to some sort of VRS-like solution - that is DD baseline observations and a correction of the
DD distance dependent errors.
Figure 2.4.2.5: The relationship between diﬀerent NRTK approaches. Borrowed from [1]
As seen in ﬁgure 2.4.2.5 data from the network may be transmitted to the rover at ﬁve diﬀerent
stages: (A)-(E), and each of these stages deﬁnes an NRTK approach as follows:
A At this stage all network data exists only in a format of raw observations. In other words here
you would transmit all network observations to the rover, thus giving the rover all available
information on the network, but also requiring the rover to do a lot of calculations, including
network ambiguity resolution, before a solution could be found.
The only NRTK solution
currently operating on this stage is a single-baseline solution (and thus not actually an NRTK
solution)
B Once a common network ambiguity level is reached, data may be transmitted to the rover,
allowing the rover itself to run interpolation on the distance dependent errors. This is the
MAC (or MAX) solution.
C Interpolation of distance dependent errors may be done in many ways. At this stage interpola-
tion is handled without the rover transmitting its location to the network, and thus allows for
corrections to be broadcast. The only current way to do this is FKP, which thus corresponds
to (C) in ﬁgure 2.4.2.5.

D If network receives rover position (or raw observations) it may interpolate distance dependent
errors to the rover location, and transmit VRS observations back to the rover, which the rover
may use to determine position. This is the VRS method.
E Finally the CPF may itself use the VRS observations and rover observations to determine
rover position. In this way the CPF handles all calculations, and the rover simply transmits
observations, and receives its position back from the CPF.
As a note, transmission of position (most often rover position delivered to the CPF) is handled in
the NMEA 0183 format, while raw observations and corrections transmitted both within the network

PPP-RTK
PPP-RTK is somewhat a fusion of the PPP and NRTK models in that the user receives corrections to
satellite errors (orbits, clocks and biases - including phase bias), along with atmospheric corrections
delivered by a CORS network, thus allowing for much faster convergence times than ordinary PPP.
In general the PPP-RTK solution has much more in common with the PPP methods, than the RTK
methods, in that PPP-RTK broadcasts UD corrections, allowing users to determine position fast
and precisely without the use of a baseline and DD observations. Since in PPP-RTK the network is
used to estimate all UD parameters individually, this approach is (like PPP) a state space approach
or state space representation (SSR), and therefore PPP-RTK models are also sometimes referred to
as PPP-SSR or (since the addition of satellite phase bias corrections allows for integer ambiguity
resolution) PPP-AR.
In fact PPP-RTK hasn’t got much in common with RTK solutions, and the ’-RTK’ term most likely
stems from the fact that PPP-RTK makes use of a regional CORS network, like NRTK solutions
do. It is however important to recognize (as will later be clear) that user integer ambiguities, will
in fact become DD ambiguities as in an RTK solution - this is what actually ensures that the phase
ambiguities are integer resolvable in the PPP-RTK solution.
In short PPP-RTK uses a regional CORS network to estimate all parameters in the UD obser-
vation equations. This means that PPP-RTK provides corrections for dts, ds
j, δs
j, T s
r , Is
r, and satellite
orbits. Compared to traditional PPP solutions the addition of a satellite phase bias correction allows
for integer ambiguity resolution, while the addition of atmospheric corrections allow for minimal
convergence times. Furthermore the use of UD corrections makes the system accessible to SF users,
not capable of forming IF combination, although such SF users would most likely experience longer
convergence times due to the larger ionospheric residuals[20]. A very dense CORS network should
alleviate this, and allow for precise ionospheric corrections even for SF users.
In short, some of the advantages of PPP-RTK compared to PPP or RTK solutions are:
 Provides corrections to satellite errors as PPP (orbits and clocks) and atmospheric corrections
as RTK, but also corrections to satellite phase bias.
 Corrections allows for fast integer ambiguity resolution without using a DD baseline solution.
With ionospheric corrections this is even possible for SF users due to the UD nature of the
corrections.
 PPP-RTK provides corrections in state-space, meaning corrections to individual errors are es-
timated by the network. This decreases the required bandwidth[20], it also allows for individual
update rate for corrections[20], and ﬁnally it also makes it possible to better model the errors
on the individual corrections[20].
 Because of the SSR, PPP-RTK allows for very large inter-station distances (several hundred
kilometres[20]), although this may have consequences for ionospheric corrections especially im-
pairing SF users.
 No information (no parameters) is eliminated in the network due to the use of UD observations.
This for instance means that satellites visible by only a few stations may still be processed by
the network, as opposed to diﬀerenced approaches, where satellites must be visible for ALL
network stations[1].

2.5.1
The Common Clock (CC) Model
The common clock (CC) model is named for the fact that the user applies the same clock corrections
to code- and phase observations - ergo; while diﬀerent corrections may be applied to code- and phase
observations, they have the clock correction in common.
There are several possible common clock models, but this report shall adhere to the model described
in [28]. The same model is also described in [25] although derived from a between-satellite SD set of
network observation equations.
In the following derivations precise orbits are as mentioned still assumed, however to get the full
picture of PPP-RTK it is relevant to introduce two further concepts to the observations:
1. Diﬀerential Code Bias (DCB): This concept was shortly mentioned in section 2.1.3 in
connection with the Time Group Delay, TGD. DCB is a more general term describing the
diﬀerence between code hardware biases on diﬀerent frequencies. As such receiver DCBs can
be described as: DCBr = dr,1 −dr,2, while satellite DCBs are determined as: DCBs = ds

2.5.2
Integer Ambiguity Resolution
Since one of the main advantages of PPP-RTK is to ensure that phase ambiguities may be resolved
as integers, as this is essential to achieve the wanted cm accuracies, this section will shortly describe
how the ambiguities are actually resolved as integers in the positioning solution.
Page 48
==PAGE:56==
September 30, 2018
Essentially an ordinary LS solution employing for instance the user phase observations for the CC
model (equation (2.5.1.8)), does not take into account that one of the parameters is integer, and will
solve them all as ﬂoats. What is needed is therefore a way to apply the constraint that ˜
M s
u,1 is integer
to the LS solution. Furthermore it is important that this modiﬁcation to the LS solution is still easily
computable in order to keep real time positioning, since one of the advantages of PPP-RTK is that
one does not have the long convergence times of PPP, but may achieve a precise position ﬁx in a
matter of seconds.
In general a system of equations, such as the phase observations, where some parameters are in-
tegers while others are reals, is referred to as a mixed-integer linear system[1], and parameters of such
a system may be estimated by use of the following three steps:
1. Float Solution: The ﬁrst step simply consist of solving the system using a standard LS solution.
As such, the fact that some parameters are integers is ignored. Therefore this is step is also
called the ﬂoat solution, since all parameters will be estimated as ﬂoat values.
2. Integer Solution: In this second step the integer constraint is accounted for, thus mapping
selected parameters to integer values.
3. Fixed Solution: Finally, when integer values are determined, the rest of the parameters must
be updated to reﬂect this change.
Now, while steps 1 and 3 are rather straightforward, and both are easily computed (for details see
[30]), several solution exists for step 2.
The most simple integer mapping function is of course rounding the ﬂoat solution from step 1. This
however does not take into account the covariance of the ﬂoat ambiguities[1], and thus compared to
other integer mapping algorithms it will more often fail to determine integer ambiguities correctly.
As said there are several solutions, however one of the most recognized and widely used algorithm
is the LAMBDA[8] (Least-squares AMBiguity Decorrelation Adjustment) method, which is also the
solution presented here. LAMBDA builds upon the integer least squares ambiguity estimator along
with the use of a decorrelating transformation of the ﬂoat solution[1].
LAMBDA

method is a large topic, the presentation of the model will be short and not very detailed, but more
of a general introduction to the basics of the method - that is the integer least squares approach and
decorrelation - to provide an intuitive understanding of its function.

2.5.3
Conclusions
This concludes the introduction to PPP-RTK. As was seen this is somewhat a combination of the
PPP and the RTK methods, in that it provides corrections to satellite parameters AND interpolated
ionospheric corrections from a local reference station network.
As such it combines the advantages of both approaches with few disadvantages.
It delivers cm
precision in seconds, it is robust to station failures, and delivers UD corrections that may be applied
even by SF users, and may be used basically by anyone with a data connection. This makes PPP-
RTK the perfect ﬁt especially for precise urban positioning, as 4G coverage in cities makes it easy
to retrieve correction data from a network, and thus potentially making it available to thousands of
users - for instance in Aarhus. All it requires is the maintenance of a few reference stations with a
clear view to the sky, and a precisely known position, along with a CPF that continually determines
corrections, and broadcasts them to the users.
Remember though, that PPP-RTK is an SSR method, and may thus be considered an advanced PPP
model, more than an RTK method, which uses OSR corrections. Therefore a perhaps more correct
term for PPP-RTK would be PPP-SSR.

Method
One of the purposes of this report is to compare existing PPP, RTK and NRTK solutions. While
as mentioned it would be interesting to compare with the PPP-RTK implementation in TAPAS, as
of now the TAPAS stream is not yet up and running, and as such measurements on the PPP-RTK
system is not possible.
Instead the procedures that has to be used in testing TAPAS, when it is ready, are adopted for mea-
surements performed in Dyrehaven on current positioning solutions. In this way the measurement
procedures are tried out, so that the set-up is ready for measurements on TAPAS, and furthermore
data is collected on current technologies, that may be compared to the TAPAS PPP-RTK solution
when the time comes.
Dyrehaven is situated near the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), and has for many years been
used as a training ground for surveying in Denmark, and is as such a good venue for GNSS and RTK
measurements, due to the fact that it is littered with static benchmarks given in ETRS89/UTM32
using DVR90 for heights. Likewise the Leica SmartNet stations (used for RTK and NRTK solutions
in these measurements) work out of the same reference system, and because of this all it is natural
that all measurements will be carried out within that system as well.
The analysis will be a qualitative comparison of the available RTK methods, and will follow the
TAPAS test-plan to remain somewhat valid for the TAPAS project. Benchmark B016 in Dyrehaven
is chosen for measurements, and this ﬁts well with the requirements of TSR-140 and TPR-140 de-
scribed in the TAPAS test-plan[35].
This chapter will now describe how measurements for the comparison of diﬀerent RTK methods
are made. The ﬁrst section details the setup, presenting the chosen benchmark in Dyrehaven, the
equipment used, SmartNet coverage at the benchmark, and the mount points selected for the mea-
surements. The second section details the actual measurement procedure: when and how measure-
ments are made, conditions for the measurements and references to what requirements of the TAPAS
test plan it could fulﬁll, also remarking on how the procedures may be translated into actual TAPAS
measurements.

Results
All data processing for results are handled in Matlab, and it has been chosen here to present results

Static measurements
An important note on the static measurements, is that since positions in practice could not be logged
every second, in this case standard deviations have not been calculated once a minute, but instead in
batches of 60 positions equivalent to maybe 2-3 minutes. This has been done in order to reasonably
compare one batch to another.
This of course means that requirement TSR-160 cannot be readily veriﬁed, since batch sizes doesn’t
match any speciﬁc time, it will be seen that it is of no consequence since standard deviations on all
methods are below the required 1 cm already on the ﬁrst batch.
It should further be noted, that receiver positions were of the ﬁxed type even before the ﬁrst mea-
surement could be made, meaning integer ambiguities had already been resolved, ensuring maximum
precision from the start.
Figure 4.0.0.1 shows 3D plots all logged positions from the four static measurements each corrected
by the mean value, as well as 2D projections to make both horizontal and vertical distributions more
clear.
The ﬁrst thing to notice is that all positions independent of RTK approach is located within a
3x3x3cm cube, which means that all positions are obviously very precise, especially the horizontal
positions, which are almost all within a 1x1cm area.
Furthermore looking at the 2D projections two things are immediately clear in all four measurements:
positions in spread wider in the vertical direction than the horizontal directions, and most often po-
sitions are spread wider in the northing, than the easting direction. The latter becomes especially
clear in ﬁgure (a) of 4.0.0.2 - 4.0.0.5, which shows the distribution of positions in the horizontal plane.
This is as expected. Both observations are due to the satellite geometry. The best positions would
be achieved with satellites evenly distributed all around the receiver, and while the satellites are
often quite evenly distributed in the horizontal plane, they are all situated above the receiver, thus
giving a larger up/down error. Furthermore in latitudes this far north, satellite constellations are
often weaker in the north/south direction, due to the fact that the inclination of GNSS orbits are
relatively low (55◦for GPS to 64.8◦for GLONASS[6]), and therefore satellites are most often posi-
tioned south of a receiver in Dyrehaven.

PPP measurements
The static PPP measurements are handled a bit diﬀerently than the RTK measurements. First and
foremost the PPP positions are calculated in post-processing using the raw observation data recorded
between 17:40 and 18:12. This raw data is converted to a RINEX ﬁle (.18O format) using Septentrios
RXtools, and the ﬁle is then uploaded to an online service (https://webapp.geod.nrcan.gc.ca/geod/tools-
outils/ppp.php?locale=en) that applies orbit and satellite clock corrections, and determines receiver
position.
The positions are received in geodetic coordinates, which is converted to East, North, Up (ENU)
coordinates using a Matlab program written for that speciﬁc purpose. Having positions in ENU
coordinates makes it much easier to compare the PPP measurements to the RTK measurements.
The actual ENU transformation shall not be described here, but if interested the calculations can be
found in the ConvENU.m and RotENU.m ﬁles supplied in the appendix.
It should be noted that compared to the RTK measurements, it was possible to log raw observations
with a 1 Hz logging rate, and therefore a total of 2020 observation sets were logged during the ap-
proximately 33 minutes of measuring.

5
Conclusion
In this report general GNSS theory has been described, and several positioning models have been
presented and evaluated both theoretically and practically. The theoretical part ended with the
PPP-RTK model, which is the current state-of-the-art, and although no practical measurements
could be made with this speciﬁc model, the theoretical advantages of the PPP-RTK approach has
been presented.
For the practical tests, one of the most obvious conclusions, is that clearly current RTK meth-
ods fulﬁlls TAPAS requirements for a test site deﬁned by TPS-140 and TPR-140 of the test plan,
and as such there’s no reason to believe that the TAPAS system shouldn’t fulﬁll the same require-
ments.
Another thing that will also be true for TAPAS, is as was seen in the point test, that the error
induced in horizontal positioning by vertical alignment of the carbon rod may actually be respon-
sible for 100% of the error in positioning. In other words; taking advantage of such precise GNSS
positioning also entails very high demands on aligning the rover receiver.
For current RTK methods, there’s no evidence in the measurements to suggest that these meth-
ods perform diﬀerently at all - meaning all of them have basically no convergence time, and deliver
positions with sub-cm standard deviation. This is exactly as expected; as previously seen the dif-
ference in NRTK methods is primarily a matter of where calculations are handled (CPF or rover),
while the single-baseline RTK solution will perform just as well, as long as the baseline is short (<
10 km), which it is in Dyrehaven.
It is also clear how the current SSR method, PPP, performs compared to RTK. As was shown
PPP requires a long time (≈20 min) in order to converge on a precise position, and even longer time
(30+ min) in order to achieve standard deviations in the same range as RTK methods.
This convergence time should be eliminated in PPP-RTK, which is the next generation of SSR meth-
ods, due to the fact that integer ambiguities may be resolved in the ﬁrst epoch, which should ensure
convergence times and accuracies comparable to the RTK methods.
In such good measurement conditions as was the case here, it cannot be expected that PPP-RTK
methods will deliver much better positioning than current RTK methods. PPP-RTK however does
hold many other advantages compared to RTK, such as better coverage, single-frequency capabilities,
broadcast capabilities, etc.
Finally, before tests on the actual TAPAS system are preformed, the 1Hz logging rate of RTK posi-
tions should be looked into. Otherwise it will be exciting to see how TAPAS performs, and there is
no doubt that PPP-RTK is the way of the future.
