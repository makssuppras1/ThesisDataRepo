Long term energy and emission pathways for the Swiss metal industry
Abstract
Decarbonisation of hard-to-abate industries is, besides sector coupling are one of the major
challenges for reaching carbon neutrality. The study at hand generates insights into the long-
term development of energy demand, fuel use and carbon emissions for the Swiss metal industry.
The metal sector in Switzerland no longer has any primary production and thus offers a
unique case with no direct process-related emissions. Nevertheless, it has to cover its high-
temperature processes, which are difficult to decarbonize, with alternative fuels. By using a
technology-rich bottom up modeling approach, several scenarios were analyzed that simulate
energy pathways for the Swiss metal industry, . Already today, conventional technologies have
low cost advantages in the Swiss metal sector, resulting in strong reduction of CO2 emissions
through energy efficiency. Each of the three analyzed scenarios leads to energy savings of
over 30% and reduction of CO2 emissions of over 35%, even in a Business as Usual scenario.
This allows for steep decarbonisation curves and achieving CO2 neutrality by 2045, with no
further interventions than following existing policies in the wake of the Swiss Energy Strategy
2050. Particularly notable is that this tipping point is reached at 100 CHF/tCO2, so that green
hydrogen becomes less expensive than natural gas and replaces it. In the case of an ambitious
climate scenario, high-temperature processes can switch to green hydrogen and hydrogen
from the SMR process between 2025 and 2035 and lead to zero emissions.

Introduction
In line with increasing global efforts towards energy efficiency and decarbonisation, Switzerland
committed through the Paris Agreement to halving its greenhouse gas emissions by 2030
compared to 1990. By 2050, Switzerland aims to achieve net zero CO2 emissions. A major
role in energy efficiency and decarbonisation holds the industry, as a hard-to-abate sector.
In Switzerland, ambitions were framed by a national referendum in 2017, where the Swiss
Energy Strategy 2050 (SES) was put in place as a legislative directive. It requires the electricity
consumption to drop until 2035 by 13% per capita from 2000 levels and a reduction of final
energy consumption of 43% per capita. [1]
1.1
Swiss Energy System
The Swiss electricity mix is comprised of low-carbon electricity supply, while liquid and solid
fuels are mostly imported. Net electricity generation in 2019, on pre-pandemic levels, was
generated mainly from hydropower with 56.4% and nuclear power with 35.2%. Other renewables,
such as solar PV, wind and biomass have a relatively small contribution. [2] The Swiss Energy
Strategy 2050 foresees a phase-out of nuclear power, equivalent to one third of currently used
electricity generation capacity, due to safety concern as a main reason, but continuing to
operate existing plants. [3] Core of the strategy are promotion are extra generation from
renewable energy sources and energy efficiency improvement. The latter will be especially
relevant for the industry, which is considered one of the key sectors to achieve the above
mentioned SES goal.
Industry sector is the third largest contributor to Swiss energy consumption, behind households
and transport. With 150.6 PJ per year it accounted for about 19.5% of total final energy demand
in Switzerland in 2019. From 2000 to 2019 total final energy demand for the industry sector
decreased by 6.4% and its share decreased from 19.0% to 18.0%, before raising to 19.5% in 2020.
However, due to the global Covid-19 pandemic and possible demand distortion, causing a
demand drop for energy in the transport sector, 2019 values are chosen here. [4]

1.2
Swiss Metal Sector
The industry sector in 2018 accounted for 18% of Swiss total 37 Mt of CO2 emissions. Since
Swiss metal sector employs exclusively secondary production of steel and aluminium (see
Appendix Appendix A), CO2 emissions are related to carbon intensity of burned fuels. [5]
Fuel related carbon emissions in both metal subsectors, steel and non-ferrous metals, stem
mainly from natural gas used in high-temperature processes for melting, casting and further
processing the metals, with minor shares of coal in the iron metal process. The Federal Office
for the Environment reports a CO2 emission factor for steel production of 8.5 kgCO2/t in 2019,
which increased from 7.6 kgCO2/t in 2010. [5]
Switzerland is a net importer of steel, consuming about 2.3 Mt of steel, and producing about
1.6 Mt of steel per year, resulting in a primary energy intensity of production of 5.0 GJ/t of
steel. [6] (See Appendix Figure A.1). Compared to a global energy intensity of 18.6 GJ/t of
mining and producing steel and additionally 23.7 GJ/t of mining and production of iron, Swiss
energy intensity for steel production is more than three times less than the global average. [7]
[8]
Non-ferrous metals are characterized with an immense energy intensity for primary production
such as mining, transport and refining in high temperature processes above 1000°C. [9] Aluminium
is a crucial non-ferrous metal for manufactured goods of many end-use sectors, such as automotive,
aviation or robotics. [10] For non-ferrous metals, aluminium is the dominant metal, not only
regarding energy intensity, but also in terms of material consumption, as shown in Appendix
Figure A.1.[9] It accounts for a over 75% in final energy consumption of the non-ferrous sector,
which was comprised mainly by natural gas with 1.6 PJ and electricity with 1.3 PJ in 2018,
contributing to 54.4%, and 43.8% respectively.[11]
Figure 1.2: Final energy consumption in Swiss steel and non-ferrous metal industry [11]
Estimations for energy consumption diverge substantially for Swiss subsectors, such as the
metal industry. The Swiss Federal Office of Statistics reports final energy consumption for the
metal sector to be at 10.8 PJ in 2018 and 10.1 PJ in 2019, whereas the International Energy
Agency (IEA) estimates them to be about 20% higher at 12.9 PJ in 2018 and 12.2 PJ in 2019,
respectively. [11]

1.3
Research Gap
Most analyses of energy consumption and carbon emissions identify the high energy saving
potential by moving away from conventional primary steel production through the blast furnace
route. However, findings and suggestions such as the Organisation for Economic Co-operation
and Development (OECD) Steel Commitee recommendation to advance electrification and
switch to EAF steel recycling route or primary production route through direct reduction
of iron, using renewable hydrogen as reduction feedstock, are not applicable to Switzerland.
[12] [13] Other recommendations include demand response and overcapacity reduction and
downscaling of primary production facilities, lacking country level representation, especially
for a unique industry like the Swiss metal sector. [14] Switzerland is a net importer of metals,
including steel and aluminium, and already employs exclusively secondary production. Reducing
capacity further would possibly lead to carbon leakage to surrounding EU countries, [15] and
therefore the sector requires an analysis more focused on production specific processes.
There is a broad range of studies on potentials of technologies and production routes, including
the EAF route. However, they often disregard the interaction with the energy system and do

Several studies identified improvement potential for energy efficiency (EE) in industry. Many
previous studies have focused on decarbonisation and life-cycle-analysis of industries of certain
end products, e.g. robotics [10] and automotive industry [16] or more specifically with respect
to the metal production. [17] However, it is important to trace back to the CO2-intensive
production of metals used in these industries, such as aluminium and steel production.
In depth analysis for the German metals sector was done by Blesl and Kessler, assessing a
wide spectrum of energy saving potential, possible through technological improvement, and
based on a modelling approach by Kuder. [9] [18] While Zuberi et. al [19] focused on electrical
energy improvement potential for the Swiss industry, Bhadbhade et al. went further to conduct
a detailed bottom-up study of economic energy and CO2 saving potential in the Swiss metal
sector. However, they lack an comprehensive insight into the interaction of these industries
with the power sector.
Energy system models on the other hand have a good representation of the system interaction
but industrial sectors are modelled in a simplified way.[20] This paper attempts to fill this
gap with a technology-rich bottom-up modelling approach. It is intended to contribute to
the understanding of which different trajectories are possible and how the techno-economic
potentials unfold on the metal sector. It will provide insights into how different scenarios of
demand, policies and power sector development affect fuel consumption and CO2 emissions
in the future development of the Swiss metal sector.

Methodology
The Swiss TIMES Energy system Model (STEM) is a bottom-up techno-economic optimization
model. TIMES is an established framework for energy systems analysis. Its optimization
algorithm is based on linear programming, optimizing for total discounted system costs. It
includes capital and operation costs and costs for infrastructure, energy commodities, as well
as for processes of conversion and end-use technologies. [21]
STEM adapts the TIMES framework, adjusted to Switzerland with a horizon for system long-
term energy system modelling. It is suitable for the analysis at hand, since it has a detailed
temporal resolution of 24 hours per day for Saturday, Sunday and a representative weekday.
Seasonality is accounted for by a characteristic pattern for each of the four seasons, spring,
summer, autumn and winter. [22] In this analysis the model aggregates five year time steps,
which is a reasonable time span in long-term energy system modelling. [22] Spatial resolution
in the study at hand is just one region. It is well-fitting and applied for different sector-specific
process modelling exercises with a bottom-up modelling approach. [23] [24] In this study
it is suitable because of its dynamic and technology-rich approach. It provides a bottom-
up optimization model that allows for an improvement, expanding the prevalent modelling
approach with a metal sector module, product flow modelling of metal industry specific end-
products, including their manufacturing routes, material flow and balances, besides the common
and specific energy flows.

Emissions
All coal from the energy balance is assigned as fuel use. Even though small amounts of
bituminous coal serve primarily as fuel, they sometimes can help adjusting material properties.
Due to their primary application, they included in the model as fuel input, and not as material
input, leading it to be replaceable by other fuels. This ensures consistency with other data
sources in the Swiss energy statistics. [5] Oxygen on the other hand is assumed to be necessary
for uniform burning and heat distribution in the EAF and therefore modelled as a necessary
material commodity with a fixed input factor, while it could in reality also be used for its fuel
purpose.

2.4
Future Technology Implementation
Although the technology has been known for years, twin roll casters and thin slab casters are
not yet in wide-spread use because quality assurance is still a challenge and just a limited scope
of products can be produced by it. [31] [32] However, they have great potential for energy and
cost savings. This is due to the fact that they can directly process the hot metal strands output
from continuous casting over several rolling steps. This leads to saving space due to spatial
joining and allows for achieving higher process energy efficiency, as the temperature drop
from the previous process is reduced. [33]
Waste heat recovery is allowed from 2020 onward with a waste heat recovery maximum
potential of 11.4% for waste heat from electric arc furnaces, and casting operations and hot
rolling and 9.6% for melting and rolling operations of aluminium.[34] and Appendix B. The
waste heat can only be used for processes that use a 300°C lower temperature level to account
for exergy potential and technical implications such as heat loss during transmission.
2.5
Scenarios
BAU - A Scenario based on continuing operations "Business As Usual"
This scenario is built on the assumption, that current market dynamics continue to unfold
without any major intervention and steering by policy-makers, exploring where the sector
might go. EU-ETS price is assumed to slowly increase to 50 e/tCO2. It aims to investigate the
trajectory the industry would take without guidance and ambitions of policy makers regarding
its future energy consumption and CO2 emissions. Its sensitivity is checked with a worst-case
scenario, accounting for demand to increase according to IEA growth forecast.[35]
EPOL - A Scenario based on strong energy efficiency policies
This scenario is a strong energy efficiency scenario, based on the implementation of the Swiss
Energy Strategy 2050 and implications, such as a phase-out of nuclear power in Switzerland.

Results
3.1
BAU
In the BAU scenario, total primary energy consumption is rapidly reduced until 21.4% by 2025,
mainly due to the partial replacement of coal in process heat by gas and the installation of thin
slab casting and twin roll equipment. Waste heat utilization for steel scrap preheating and
low-temperature processes such as steel annealing and aluminium rolling is also increasing.
However, gas remains the dominant energy source for high-temperature processes. In the low-
temperature range up to 200°C, high-temperature heat pumps will be used from 2040 to replace
gas as the energy source, being. In the low-temperature range up to 200°C, high temperature
heat pumps will be installed from 2040, replacing gas and oil as energy sources in space and
water heating in the BAU scenario and thus contributing to CO2 emission abatement from
this sector. For space heating heat pumps are used until 2040, but starting with investments
from 2025 they are replaced by solar thermal heating. Solar thermal heating replaces fossil
heat pumps completely by 2040. However, it also competes with other energy sources without
direct fuel emissions, such as electric water boilers, heat pumps, or biomass, which are not
used for process heating in any of the scenarios and would therefore be available for space and
water heating. There was no significant deviation in the demand growth scenario, the current
capacities for electricity and gas technologies were increased proportionately, without hitting
any tipping points in the future energy supply or equipment installation
Figure 3.1: BAU Scenario results. See detailed in Appendix E
CO2 emissions in the BAU scenario decrease by 37.6% until 2045, but remain stable afterwards.
All possible processes until then are electrified and high-temperature processes all switched
to natural gas, since no more oil and gas capacity is installed. However, 0.14 Mt, about half
of the base year 0.30 Mt of CO2 emissions remain in the BAU scenario. This is due to the fact
that despite the increasing cost of natural gas and the advancement of carbon capture and
hydrogen technologies, natural gas remains cheaper than the use of hydrogen as an energy
carrier.

CLI
In the CLI scenario, the cost of conventional hydrogen from steam methane reforming (SMR)

This is mainly due to the unfavorable meteorological situation in Switzerland, where no large

Discussion
4.1
Limitations and Scope
In general, scenario analyses are inherently imprecise. They cannot predict the future, but
only simulate and describe how one might arrive at a certain state in the future and what the
trajectory looks like and find probable likely intervention and tipping points. Furthermore, the
model is based on the assumption of rational actors, which take the best path for themselves.
However, human actions are always subject to emotions and decisions are often made myopically
without analyzing the future in detail. This study also cannot answer how likely the analyzed
scenarios are or explain causal relationships resulting from political intentions. Another limit
arises from the one way coupling with the metal sector model with the energy system. Fuel
prices are taken as given and different pathways have no interactive feedback on the energy
system, but only suggest what they might look like. However, the present study can identify
future trends and pathways into a possible future situation and potentials for saving fuels,
costs, and emissions, and can assist in the decision-making process for investments or policy
instruments.
4.2
Comparison with other studies
Bhadbhade estimated that the economic energy saving potential of the Swiss industry is at
11-15%, while the technical potential is even higher at around 19%, which is a bit lower than
what was found to be economically optimal in the BAU scenario until 2025 (21%). [36] He also
identifies similar processes that can lead to energy savings and the potential he describes is
reflected as a deployment in the existing pathways. The magnitude is similar, while the model
at hand might overestimate the potential in the first years due to the assumption of coal being
a pure energy carrier and not needed for quality reasons.
The deep decarbonisation scenario CLI finds an unusual trajectory compared to other studies.
It switches from green hydrogen to conventional hydrogen with carbon capture and storage
after 2040 which diverges from most decarbonisation and power sector simulations, seeing a
strong cost decrease for green hydrogen. [37] [38] This is mainly based on two causes. First,
a strong expansion and cost reduction of large scale renewables, especially offshore wind
in the North Sea and photovoltaics in Southern Europe and other sunny regions. Second,
a strong learning progression and thus a strongly declining cost curve, similar to the past
for photovoltaic. In addition, there is the intermittency, which makes electricity available
for almost nothing during hours of peak production. However, Switzerland has only limited
potential for both, large scale wind and solar, for geographical and meteorological reasons.
There is no coastline or flat landscape with constant wind and no large areas with strong
and constant solar radiation. Furthermore, hydrogen from preferential locations must be
transported which leads to further cost increase for industrial application in Switzerland,
whereas domestic hydrogen does have a lesser need of additional infrastructure cost. Therefore,
in Switzerland it is possible that over time the technological maturity and cost reduction of
carbon capture and storage will lead to conventional hydrogen from steam methane reforming
becoming cost competitive.

similarly steep decarbonization curves and energy efficiency measures. [23] [24] However, in

4.3
Further work
The next step should be to combine industry sector models to find synergy potentials. Sector
coupling is one of the most difficult challenges for the reduction of CO2 emissions besides the
decarbonization of hard to abate industries. These synergy potentials could be for example
between metal and pulp and pater industry, where a lot of waste heat is generated from metal
processes, if production plants can be combined by sensible spatial planning.
Additionally, further work should contribute towards an understanding of potential repercussions
of changes in fuel demand from the metal or other subsectors on the energy sector. That
should lead to smart future harmonization of sectors, including fuel prioritisation when sectors
compete for fuels like biomass and hydrogen for decarbonisation.

B
Appendix: Temperature level aggregation
Melting and Casting
The melting temperature of steel can reach up to more than 1500°C, while that of aluminum
in pure form is around 660°C. Here, however, the high temperature process of >750°C was
assumed, but it can also be higher because aluminum often occurs in alloyed form or other
materials have to be melted out of the metal scrap. From 750°C the last temperature interval
begins, above which no further differentiation is made because the firing processes and furnace
types remain the same and therefore energy efficiencies and fuel consumption are also similar.
Rolling and Extrusion operations
Rolling and extrusion of both aluminum and steel can take place at a wide range of temperatures.
Directly after continuous casting, the metals come out of the melting furnace slightly below
melting temperature. When they are further processed directly, they often still have temperatures
at 1200°C for steel, and 600°C for aluminum respectively.
By spatial transfer during the production process heat is lost, which can also happen intentionally
in the course of the production process for material properties. The semi-finished parts can
either be cold rolled or heated again. Here they have been assigned to an estimated temperature
interval depending on the process, as the exact process including temperature is very product-
specific. For the casting of aluminum in a foundry, it is assumed that the material must be
remelted.
Tempering, Quenching and Annealing
Metal finishing processes for semi-finished and cast metal goods, such as tempering, quenching
and annealing can take place at a wide range of temperature levels, from 100°C to, depending
on the alloy and desired material property such as reducing brittleness and increasing toughness.
Highest temperatures can be up to recrystallization temperature, where steel-alloys change
their crystalline composition between 600°C and 700°C.
The chemical composition and alloy of a steel determines material properties such as hardness
or brittleness. There are various methods of hardening metal alloys to further modify them
after production. In tempering, quenching and annealing processes, workpieces are heated and
cooled down at within a certain time frame, so that their crystal structure changes between
ferrite, austenite and martensite, adjusting material properties to suit the needs of the final
application. The transformation rate is controlled by different cooling media, such as water,
oil or air. [44]

Appendix: Economic data
Discounting time value of money
The discount rate was taken to reflect for the value of future investments and costs to society
from a socio-economic perspective, assuming long-term and forward-looking action. However,
this comparatively low rate does not reflect the risks of the individual players in the market
and their decision-making process. The Swiss authority gives an unchanged WACC of 3.83%
between 2018 and 2022 and NY Stern discounts the metals and mining sector at 6.0% and energy
sector at 4.6% in 2021. [45] [46] However, the paper aims to meaningfully show how different
energy and emission scenarios can evolve, targeting more the scientific and policy community
than business decisions. Thereby socio-economic desirable and future oriented decisions are
made, e.g. CO2 taxes, which is assumed especially in policy-driven scenarios, the EPOL and
CLI scenarios. OPEX were assumed 3% of CAPEX, further non-energy related costs, such as
labour costs were not regarded in the study at hand.
Cost of steel plants according to Worrell

The two main sources are the EU BAT reference paper in 2013 and the assessment of Worrell
et al. in 2008. [25] [28] Since Switzerland is a wealthy country with a modern industry, it is
assumed that these technologies were installed for all installations after 2015. For aluminium
a cost factor of +10% per Mt of capacity is assumed to account for smaller quantities and
missing scale effects and for foundries an extra cost of 2/3 of an integrated plant are assumed.
Equipment, shares and energy intensity is derived from Fraunhofer Institute and validated
