Abstract
This thesis numerically investigates the centrifugal effects encountered in the three-
dimensional finite element method, using the commercial program ANSYS. Simple
rotor systems are modeled to analyze the gyroscopic effects, along with advanced
boundary conditions applied in the form of both rigid and force-distributed con-
straint equations. A detailed review of the ANSYS Help Documentation led to a
deeper understanding of stress stiffening and spin softening. These centrifugal ef-
fects were tested on a rotating ring, blades, and flexible discs and are validated by
comparison to existing literature.
Additionally, fiber composites are introduced in the hopes of advancing rotordy-
namic analysis of composite driveshafts, which are commonly modeled in literature
with various approaches based on either beams or shells. Due to limitations on lay-
ered elements in ANSYS, the present could not perform a full rotordynamic analysis
including gyroscopic effects. Instead, a standard modal analysis was carried out and
compared to relevant literature. Potential solutions to these limitations are presented
in the future work.

Read articles from Giancarlo Genta and consider every aspect of the analytical model (Ma-
trices from different sources). A comparison should be made in ANSYS, to see whether it is
possible to gather said matrices and achieve the same results as Genta. With this, validation
of ANSYS modeling is achieved, such that further analysis can be conducted.
Research literature of mathematical modeling of rotors with composite materials. This lays the
groundwork for implementing composite rotor modeling in MATLAB and ANSYS. With this
knowledge, one should seek to model an article example in ANSYS to validate the composite
modeling capabilities in ANSYS.
The ANSYS composite rotor modeling should then be implemented to work in MATLAB code
by importing mesh and matrices. This should result in automation of solutions for composite
rotor FESS models.
Lastly, a study of the practical aspects of composite rotor modeling should be conducted to

clude the project and be a base for further analysis of composite rotor modeling.

Chapter 1
Introduction
As an engineer, you will find the applications of rotordynamic analysis incredibly
useful when designing jet engines in the aerospace field, or crankshafts, gearboxes,
driveshafts, and so on in the automotive industry. Rotating machines are also preva-
lent in the energy market, where the first thing that comes to mind are probably
wind turbines. In modern times, all of these are modeled with rotordynamics and
thoroughly studied before being released to the market. Any rotating component of
a machine can have a critical effect on the operation of a machine.
It seems paramount to study the works of Giancarlo Genta, who has released numer-
ous articles and books on the subject of rotordynamics. Notably the book ”Kinetic
Energy Storage : Theory and practice of advanced flywheel systems” from 1985 [1], at-
tempts to provide a ”state-of-the-art” comprehensible insight into the topics related
to flywheel energy storage systems. It is based in a world where the increase in en-
ergy use demands an improvement of storage systems and technologies used in the
energy sector, while simultaneously having an energy crisis just a decade before in
the 1970s.
This project is based on the work of another master’s thesis written by Anton H.
Kristoffersen & Rasmus M. Lennarts-Johansen called ”Overview of State-of-the-art
FESS, with Integration of 3D Rotor Modelling into an Active Electromagnetic Levitation
System”[2]. As evident from the title, it focuses on studying the development of
state-of-the-art flywheel energy storage systems, beginning with literature research
followed by initial mathematical modeling principles, and then modeling an Active
Magnetic Bearing system. Their literature research is extensive and maps important
areas of the design and improvement of FESS performance. Their procedure was to
export system matrices from ABAQUS to MATLAB, and thereafter perform analysis
with active magnetic bearings within a partially existing MATLAB program. This
project seeks to continue where Anton & Rasmus left off the three-dimensional mod-
eling, as they did not achieve satisfactory results for the centrifugal effects encoun-
tered in 3D. The main culprit being the modeling of stress stiffening and subsequently
spin softening, primarily focusing on the article ”On Centrifugal Softening in Finite El-
ement Method Rotordynamics”[3] released by G. Genta in 2014. Therefore, the goal of
this project is to thoroughly investigate the possibilities of modeling the centrifugal
effects in rotordynamic analysis with the finite element program ANSYS. Not only
to personally figure out the ways of modeling in ANSYS, but also to share and doc-

1.1
Literature Research
A great deal of the mathematics for the rotordynamic topics are found consolidated
in the book “Dynamics of Rotating Systems” also by G. Genta[4]. This book forms the
backbone of this literature research, attempting to cover all the basics of rotordynam-
ics and go beyond what is usually associated with the subject, as stated in it’s pref-
ace. It consists of two parts, with the first focusing on classic rotordynamics with the
basic assumptions of linearity, steady-state operation, and some degree of axial sym-
metry. The second part dives into the advanced rotordynamics, focusing on dropping
some of these assumptions. Without these assumptions and diverging from classical
rotordynamics, its contents are more regarded as research topics than fundamental
guidelines on the matter.
The contents of ”Dynamics of Rotating Systems” is briefly summarized in it’s preface
as well. Not all is relevant for this thesis, but many will give an insight and under-
standing of the subject at hand. Here is a short overview of the different chapters,
starting with Chapter 1 which serves as the Introduction. It explains and illustrates
basic concepts, diagrams, and methods qualitatively, such as the equation of motion
and its matrices, Campbell diagrams, critical speeds, instability, and unbalance. Even
experts should read it to understand the notation and perspective used.
Chapters 2 through 5 marks the introductory chapters of the Basic Topics part of the
book, with Chapter 5 focusing on continuous systems, explained as ”...more of aca-
demic rather than of practical relevance.”. These introductory chapters cover essential
concepts in rotordynamics. First, the Jeffcott rotor in Chapter 2, the simplest rotor
model with even the most basic rotordynamic interests cut out, only damping is in-
cluded. Chapter 3 expands on this by using a model with four degrees of freedom
to study gyroscopic effects, representing the behavior of rigid rotors on flexible bear-
ings. The lateral behavior of flexible rotors is modeled as discrete parameter systems
and then elaborated on in Chapter 4. The discretization of systems leads to the Finite
Element Method, which is also briefly mentioned. With a background in mechanical
vibrations, as taught in undergraduate courses, reading these three chapters should
serve as a branch into rotordynamic analysis.
Chapters 6 through 8 wrap up the Basic Topics part and extend the basic knowl-
edge of rotordynamic analysis previously covered. These additional basic topics are
still within the assumptions of classical rotordynamics and seek to introduce more
complex topics for general rotor systems. First the combination of anisotropy and
isotropy on rotors and supports in Chapter 6, where closed-form solutions of the lin-
earized steady-state systems still exist if either the rotor or support is isotropic. These
introduce problems related to backward whirling and instability ranges. Chapter 7

centrifugal effects encountered in solid modeling. A separate article by G. Genta,
released 9 years after the book, gives the three-dimensional solid modeling another
look. Likely because commercial finite element codes had caught up to include these
effects explained in Chapter 15.

Chapter 2
Rotordynamic analysis
The base of rotordynamic analysis is the inclusion of the gyroscopic matrix for a sys-
tem’s equation of motion. The equation of motion on general form for rotordynamics
is written as the following by G. Genta in [4]
M¨q(t) + (C + G) ˙q(t) + (K + H)q(t) = f(t)
(2.1)
While the general dynamic equation is presented by the ANSYS Rotordynamic Anal-
ysis Guide[5] as
M{¨q} + (G + C){ ˙q} + (K + B){q} = {f}
(2.2)
They are similarly presented, but ANSYS presents the rotating damping matrix B,
which is evidently equal to the circulatory H matrix introduced by G. Genta, as he
explains it contains the terms linked with internal damping of rotating elements[4]. The
generalized vector coordinate q can hold both translational and rotational degrees of
freedom. The mass M, damping D and stiffness K matrix are well known from other
solid mechanic problems. The main difference from normal vibration problems to
the rotordynamic approach is the gyroscopic matrix G.
The stiffness matrix is likely the most characteristic part, as it holds information about
the flexibility of the system. The damping matrix can be neglected when no damping
is present, whereas the stiffness matrix is always necessary. Both the gyroscopic and
circulatory matrix are proportional to the spin speed, while the stiffness and damping
are usually constant in simple systems. However when introducing elements such
as journal bearings to the system, the stiffness and damping properties will cause the
matrices to depend on the spin speed, often on its square[4]. Geometric effects such
as stress stiffening and spin softening will be additions to the base stiffness matrix,
and these terms will also be proportional to the spin speed squared.
The mass matrix will hold the weight of the system in discretized form. While it
seems self-explanatory, it is not necessarily easy to grasp the importance of select-
ing the correct modeling assumptions. Take for example the rigid disc of a Jeffcott
rotor[4]; the disc is applied as a point mass, which is not a perfectly accurate repre-
sentation of the real behavior. Rotational inertia should also be included, and this
is done in the gyroscopic matrix. The gyroscopic matrix G contains inertial terms,
hence it is conservative, meaning they don’t extract any work from the system[6].

2.1. JEFFCOTT ROTOR
When the spin velocity increases as a results of externally applied torque on the rotor,
the gyroscopic moment becomes critically significant due to the resulting precession.
The inclusion of gyroscopic effects has also been studied in the DTU course 41514
Dynamics of Machinery - which is presented by the supervisor of this thesis, Ilmar
Santos. The course initially deals with analysing multibody system to grasp the idea
of mode shapes of complex coupled systems. It continues into rotordynamic anal-
ysis of an experimental shaft setup including analysis of both free conditions and
ball bearing boundaries to investigate the orbits of rotating structures, as well as un-
balance response of undamped systems. The effects of rotor-bearing interaction was
also studied with respect to soft-mounted and hard-mounted bearings related to in-
vestigating the rigid-body modes in soft-mounted and seeing it change with respect
to bearing stiffness. Lastly with an introduction of modeling journal bearings on the
shaft system. Part of this was to fathom the instability principles of rotordynamic
analysis related to the damping properties of fluid film lubrication in journal bear-
ings. The added damping allows for safer conditions when passing critical modes, as
investigated with the unbalance response. However the journal bearings are prone
to instability caused by the centering of the shaft, which leads to chaotic behavior.
2.1
Jeffcott Rotor
Giancarlo Genta introduces the concept of a simplified rotor model, consisting of a
point mass on a massless shaft, which due to Jeffcott’s work in 1919 is often called a
Jeffcott rotor. Genta is quick to point out that this is not a completely correct attribu-
tion, since the model was originally analyzed by August Föppl in 1895 and discussed
by Stodola and Belluzzo in early 20th-century turbomachinery literature[4]. Despite
the models’ simplicity, it yields qualitative insight into the dynamics of simple ro-
tordynamic considerations. This report will only consider undamped scenarios. For
the undamped Jeffcott rotor, this means the overall stiffness k, providing the restoring
force in the system, can be considered either the shaft stiffness, the bearing stiffness
or a combination of the two. The model is similar to the classic example of a box on
a frictionless surface, attached to a spring on a fixed wall. The natural frequency of
such system will be the well recognized ω =
q
k
m. This is independent of the spin
speed, and leads to the flexural critical speed being equal to the natural frequency of
the nonrotating system[4].
Similarly, G. Genta introduces the Rankine rotor and a ”true Jeffcott Rotor” - in which
it is believed he refers to the Jeffcott rotor with the Coriolis term included - in his
article ”On Centrifugal Softening in Finite Element Method Rotordynamics”[3]. In both
systems the spin softening effect is included, and the comparison is there to show
the importance of the coriolis term (Or gyroscopic effect). The Rankine model pre-
dicts that the system becomes unstable above the critical speed, but the inclusion of
the coriolis term changes that outcome. It is this very effect that makes it possible to
operate the rotor above the critical speed due to the self-centering provided by the
gyroscopic moment[3]. A side effect of including the skew-symmetric and conser-
vative gyroscopic matrix, is that the natural frequencies will split up once the rotor
starts spinning. This is due to the phenomenom of whirling, where the rotor mode
will either rotate around the neutral axis in one or another direction, leading to the

2.2
Numerical Methods
The discretization of the model revolves around splitting up the geometry into parts
that each can contribute to the system at their location. Essentially the model is now
multiple systems that are linked to each other through shared boundaries. Each of
the systems will be modeled as an element, of which different types of formulations
exist, just like there are simple Euler-Bernoulli beam theory and the slightly more
complex Timoshenko beam theory. The element properties will for example be mod-
eled based on said theories, and are generally formulated based on energy princi-
ples. This methodology of discretizing geometries into element is called the Finite
Element Method (FEM)[7]. The basic terminology includes the elements being a set
of connected nodes, and a set of elements making up the mesh. The mesh is the repre-
sentation of the mathematical model, of which material properties and geometrical
influence are described. Said mesh will have loads and boundaries defined in the
model, and will then be solved as a large matrix eigenvalue problem for the case of
modal analysis. This project has focused on using the commercial FEM code AN-
SYS Mechanical APDL. The ANSYS company offers many solutions and Mechanical
APDL is just one of them. It is a program that has the GUI resembling the gray and
clunky layouts of a Windows 95 operating system, but it is what the finite element
courses at DTU introduced, hence it seems obvious to choose this program.
2.2.1
Eigensolvers
There are a couple of different methods to chose from when selecting a solver for the
modal analysis. In ANSYS, there are few solvers and each with its own set of options.
For undamped eigenvalue problems, the Block Lanczos eigensolver is applied, and
for rotordynamic problems the Damped eigensolver is used. There are also other
solvers available for more specific use-cases.
2.2.1.1
Block Lanczos
To perform modal analysis on simple undamped systems in ANSYS, one could use
the Block Lanczos solver. It is an eigenvalue extraction method available for symmet-
ric eigenvalue problems. The Block Lanczos method varies from the classical Lanczos
by employing a block of vectors compared to a single vector to perform the Lanczos
recursions[8].

The amount of numerical integration in FEM refers to the number of Gauss points
used to compute the FE results. The order of integration determines what shape
functions will be accurate. The use of an integration rule of less that the full order
of the element is called ”underintegration” or ”reduced integration”. This can be
beneficial since it will result in less computational time, but it can also introduce
some problems. Underintegration has a softening effect due to some polynomial
terms vanishing at Gauss points of low order, making no contribution to the strain
energy. This can result in zero-energy deformation modes or hourglassing.[7] The
SOLID186 element uses reduced integration by default, so one should be aware of the
risks of running reduced integration before concluding anything from an analysis.

2.2.3
Modeling Constraints
Any structural analysis in finite element will need boundary conditions, or else the
results of applied forces will result in rigid body movement, meaning infinite so-
lutions or no solutions. Even in cases of ”perfect equilibrium” will there be solver
problems due to numerical instability, hence it is necessary to apply the correct set of
restrictions to the models degrees of freedom.
2.2.3.1
Simple DOF restrictions
Usual boundary conditions Apply structural displacements on parts of the model to
either enforce translational or rotational movement to the geometry, or set it equal to
zero for it to fix the specified DOF. In ANSYS this is the most basic way of constrain-
ing a model, and it can be done by simple selecting the wanted nodes, keypoints,
lines, or areas. All boundary conditions applied to any geometric definition will be
transferred to the nodes connected, for example areas.
2.2.3.2
Constraint Equations
CERIG This command is used to define a rigid region[12]. It relates a nodes by au-
tomatically generating constraint equations. This form of constraint can be pretty
harsh on modeling boundaries correct, since it will allow no deformation related to
the original shape of the geometry, all depending on the restrictions put on the inde-
pendent node.
During testing of this method on 3D elements, it was found that whatever was put
into the Ldof field options for the command, it would display the error message ”Label
ROTZ is not an active degree of freedom. The CERIG command is ignored.”, which is fine
if i were actively trying to make the ROTZ DOF rigid, however that was not the case.
Even the default option ALL, which resorts to ”All applicable degrees of freedom”,
resulted in this error. It may be due to its extended description saying ”If 3D, generate
6 equations based on UX, UY, UZ, ROTX, ROTY, ROTZ; if 2D, generate 3 equations based
on UX, UY, ROTZ.”, likely meaning it will try to make constraint equations on these
DOFS no matter what, but since the 3D solid elements only have UX, UY, and UZ, it
will not work on these. Of course the command works and is used in examples from
the webpage, it just does not fit 3D modeling. This is actually also explained in the
notes for the command: ”Also, nodes must be connected to elements having the required
degree of freedom set (see Ldof above).”[12]

of a single point. This can be done with the the RBE3 command, which distributes an

Rotordynamic Analysis Guide
The most basic interpretation of performing rotordynamic analysis guide is by intro-
ducing the gyroscopic matrix to the system of equations. This was also the procedure
in the course 41514 - Dynamics of Machinery, along with the investigation of journal
bearing interaction and the instability thereof. The Rotordynamic Analysis Guide[5]
only focuses on the stationary reference frame analysis. It is enough to get going
with rotordynamic analysis based on just the inclusion of the gyroscopic matrix for
simple geometries axially symmetrical around the spin axis, such as simple rotor se-
tups. Initially beam elements will model a rotor setup representing a Jeffcott rotor[4],
however with inertia is accounted for, hereafter called the simple rotor setup.
The guide introduces the two most important additional commands for performing
rotordynamic analysis. The main output is the Campbell diagram, which essentially
is a multi-step modal analysis. ANSYS will automatically take care of appending
solutions to the results file for each time the SOLVE command is used. The gyroscopic
effect is applied in the reference frame by passing CORIOLIS,ON,,,ON. The first ON
is for activating the gyroscopic effect, and the second is for activating the stationary
reference frame[12]. Along with this, the spin speed is specified with either OMEGA or
CMOMEGA, in which the prior is applying to the entire model, whereas the latter only
applies to a specified component. Usually it is advised to use the component specific
command to avoid unwanted physics from entering, such as a spinning bearing -
which should be still. Remember it is technically the stator that is modeled and the
rotor is the spinning component. An example of how the simple Campbell analysis
goes is given below. The defined component shaft holds for example the beam and
mass elements or just the solid elements defined. Any bearings or contact elements
are neglected. The OMEGA command allows only for rotational speeds around the
Cartesian coordinate axis’, while the CMOMEGA command also allows an axis defined
by two points. In the below example it is just applied around the Cartesian X-axis

Extracting Data from ANSYS
While it may be more efficient to solve large problems inANSYS, it certainly is not the
most user-friendly in terms of postprocessing. It is therefore of great interest to lay
out a method for exporting data fromANSYS, both for results from solutions obtained
and the system matrices themselves. Most information from any ANSYS analysis can
be saved to the workspace with the *GET command, and the corresponding command
documentation is thus; massive. It is also possible to get the natural frequencies
found in a Campbell analysis. This can be done in a simple *DO loop that saves each
modes natural frequency nmode for each modal solution step nstep. One must pass
the PRCAMP command for the data to be available to actually get, before entering the
following code:
*DIM,Freq_Table,ARRAY,N_modes, N_modes ! Table for frequencies
*DO, Nstep, 1, N_steps ! Loop over each Campbell step
*DO, Nmode, 1, N_modes ! Loop over each mode
*GET, w_n, CAMP, Nmode, FREQ, Nstep ! Get frequency
Freq_Table(Nmode, Nstep) = w_n ! Store the frequency
*ENDDO
*ENDDO
For further data related to stability, whirl and critical speed, refer to Table 158: *GET
Postprocessing Items, Entity = CAMP in the Command Reference[12]. This ANSYS
table with the frequencies can then be converted to a dense matrix in ANSYS using
the *DMAT command. This dense matrix can then be exported as a matrix file with by
subsequently passing the *EXPORT command as:
*DMAT,CampbellMatrix,D,IMPORT,APDL,Freq_Table
*EXPORT,CampbellMatrix,MMF,filename.mtx
For the *DMAT command; first the name for the new matrix, D for double precision
values, import to load the matrix, APDL to specify an array parameter name saved
in the workspace and lastly the name of the array to be loaded. Followed by the
*EXPORT command with the new matrix name, specifying MMF[14] file format and
then the wanted output file name filename.mtx. This file can then be loaded in
MATLAB with the short function
1
function matrix = load_campbell(filename)
2
mtx = importdata(filename); % Extract numerical data
3
sz = str2num(mtx.colheaders{1}); % Gather size of dataset
4
ws = reshape(mtx.data,sz); % Natural frequencies
5
end

arrays in the ANSYS workspace. This is done with the *SMAT, which stands for sparse

wanted file is of type .full, hence one should pass the FULL option instead of the
previously used APDL in the *DMAT command, and then specify the corresponding full
file for the current jobname; jobname.full, followed by either STIFF, MASS or DAMP.
Pretty self explanatory that these will then save the stiffness, mass and damping ma-
trix, respectively. In ANSYS, the gyroscopic matrix is part of the damping matrix,

The other method includes scanning each line of the file, also using the fscanf, but by

static analysis, which will be relevant when centrifugal effects are accounted for.

agreement across the spectrum, apart from the disc modes where SOLID185 drasti-

around the circumference than the SOLID186 due to the lack of mid-nodes. The pro-
portional scaling or the irregular mesh refinement does not add additional elements

the SOLID185 elements dramatically overestimates the disc modes. This is also made
worse by the shape functions in use, as it is described as a lower-order element than

Chapter 3
Centrifugal Effects in FEM
In this chapter, the centrifugal effects that arise in FEM using 3D elements are to
be understood. The base for this investigation arise from the article ”On Centrifugal
Softening in Finite Element Method Rotordynamics”[3] written by Giancarlo Genta and
co-author Mario Silvagni (which - based on an e-mail correspondence with Genta
- had responsibility for the numerical modeling). Centrifugal softening is an effect
sometimes found in finite element rotordynamics, and in some instances impact the
natural frequencies enough that they vanish. The author questions the phenomenon
itself and the mathematical models causing it to be included. It aims to shed some
light on said centrifugal softening and its influence on three-dimensional FEM rotor-
dynamics. A couple of different models are addressed analytically and some numer-
ical models are built. He claims all cases have no strong centrifugal softening to be
found[3].
The investigation of centrifugal effects in this chapter begins by diving deep into the
ANSYS Help Documentation, which is subsequently followed by a theoretical under-
standing of the terms and effects at hand. This is mixed between the ANSYS Theory
Reference[8] and Chapter 15 from Dynamics of Rotating Machines[4], as previously
mentioned in the introduction.
The following sections will dive into recreating specifically the Rotating Ring ex-
ample from Genta’s On Centrifugal Softening in Finite Element Method Rotordynamics
article and recreate the results in ANSYS to then investigate the numerical composi-
tion of this problem. Furthermore, alternative examples - such as blades, membranes
and thick discs and thin walled cylinders - affected by the centrifugal effects will be
analyzed.

3.1.1
Advanced Rotordynamic Analysis
As the Rotordynamic Analysis Guide[5] only focus on the stationary reference frame,
it is relevant to look into other guides in the documentation. A good place to start
is the Advanced Analysis Guide[10] Chapter 5: Rotating Structure Analysis. It sets
out to address the differences between using a stationary reference frame and a rotat-
ing reference frame, explaining that the inertia effects due to rotational velocity are
nonlinear and are automatically included in analysis using NLGEOM,ON, and the lin-
earization and reference frame setting is controlled by the CORIOLIS command. The
equation of motion including effects in the rotating reference frame is given as[10]
[M]{¨u} + ([G] + [C]){ ˙u} + ([K] + [B] + [Ksoft]){u} = {F}
(3.1)
With the new additions being the spin softening matrix Ksoft, while B is also seen
in the introduction to the previous chapter. Notably no stress stiffening is present,
and it is assumed it is put under the stiffness matrix itself. These are also velocity
dependent like the gyroscopic matrix G, which are also called the Coriolis matrix
in the rotating reference frame[10]. For the stationary reference frame, the rotat-
ing damping matrix is produced when damping is present in a rotating part, and
in the rotating reference frame the effect only comes from stationary bearings[10].
Assuming this means only when damping is specified for the bearings, the effect
is not analysed due to the systems being undamped by choice. The spin softening
matrix Ksoft changes the stiffness of the structure in a rotating reference frame[10].
The guide states: Without the inertia effect applied via the CORIOLIS command, the pro-
gram does not generate the G and B matrices - note however, based on testing this fea-
ture, the spin softening matrix Ksoft will be created if the rotating reference frame
is selected. This will be expanded upon in just a moment. It is thus possible to
perform a rotordynamic analysis without including the gyroscopic matrix. With no
other effects, the results will however be constant over the speed range. The Corio-
lis effect and gyroscopic matrix G is turned on and off with the Option flag, while
the reference frame is selected with the RefFrame flag in the command with fields:
CORIOLIS, Option, --, --, RefFrame, RotDamp, RotMass. The other fields are
either unused or irrelevant, since RotDamp is for the rotating damping matrix B and
the RotMass is just a printout of the rotor mass summary, which either way is only
supported in the fixed reference frame. The ”--” fields are unused. To select a sta-
tionary reference frame one selects RefFrame as ON, and vice verse it is OFF for a
rotating reference frame.
3.1.1.1
Reference Frame Selection
To get a good overview of what reference frame to select, the ”5.2 Choosing the Ap-
propriate Reference Frame Option” in the Advanced Analysis Guide[10] gives a good
insight. They state three general cases of rotating structure layouts, each of which
allow for good analysis results.
If the mass of your rotating structure is on the rotational velocity axis (line model), those
two approaches are equivalent. - Meaning a beam model will yield equivalent results in
either reference frame, if converted between the two. This is done in ”5.4.1. Example:
Campbell Diagram Analysis of a Jeffcott Rotor”. Notably, the stationary reference frame
31 of 72
==PAGE:41==
3.1. ANSYS HELP DOCUMENTATION
requires the model to be axisymmetric. Reason being that asymmetry will lead to
unbalance - a type of external periodic excitation.
With increasing rotational velocity and mass times radius product, the stationary reference
frame results become less precise, but the simulation of a typical rotordynamics 3D solid shaft
(where the product of mass outside the centreline and radius is small) can still be performed
in this reference frame with good accuracy. - Indicating 3D models are still comparable
to beam element models as long as the shaft is slender enough. It is unclear where
the limit of slenderness is, and this is not investigated in this report since it would
require a large amount of 3D models and a good systematic approach to getting the
right insight. But even so, the knowledge that it can be less accurate will usually be
enough for selecting the right reference frame.
For the cases where mass is distributed far from the rotational axis, the rotating refer-
ence frame is advised. The guide states: For large mass times radius product and/or large
rotational velocity, the rotating reference frame approach must be used, even if the 3D struc-
ture is axisymmetric. In this type of analysis, the prestress effect due to the rotational velocity
must be included. This opens up for analyzing structures that er not axisymmetric, for
example blades that do not rotate about their own axis. For the Rotating Ring model,
this is especially important, since it has no mass on the rotational axis. The prestress
effects are also to be accounted for in these types of analysis, this is expanded upon
after explaining the spin softening criteria.
Lastly, a note for later investigating the possibilities of composite modeling, the
CORIOLIS command states explicitly ”Elements with layered section properties do not
support Coriolis effects (rotating and stationary reference frames).”[12]
3.1.1.2
Spin Softening
Continuing to the section ”5.3. Using a Rotating Reference Frame” the spin softening
effect is again mentioned as simply ”In a dynamic analysis, the Coriolis matrix and the
spin-softening matrix contribute to the gyroscopic moment in the rotating reference frame;
therefore, the program includes the spin-softening effect by default in dynamic analyses when-
ever you apply the Coriolis effect in the rotating reference frame ((CORIOLIS,ON)).” This
does not say much about the criteria, but looking into the Theory Reference on spin
softening in Chapter 3.5[8] reveals what needs to be fulfilled for the spin softening
contribution to be active. It is included when the Coriolis effect is activated in the
rotating reference frame, as previously stated, due to being part of the equations for
a rotating structure. Then there are three other effects that activate it; large deforma-
tion, prestress and linear pertubation with the spin softening matrix not set to zero,
each of which is an addition to the tangent matrix[8]. In these cases he spin softening
matrix will be always there, except in the case of turning it off by passing the SPOFF
option with the PERTURB command[12]. The theory reference on spin softening adds
a physical insight from to equations with: ”...it may be seen that there are spin softening
effects only in the plane of rotation, not normal to the plane of rotation.”[8] This is relevant
for most flexible structures, such as the ones analyzed later. Lastly the guide points
out that ”Because of the spin-softening effect, the damped eigensolver (MODOPT,DAMP) is
recommended.”, under the Modal analysis information subsection[10]. Looking at the
spin softening from a mathematical point of view reveals some relations between the
physical model and the frame of reference. The ANSYS Theory Reference[8] defines

3.1.1.3
Stress Stiffening
It is difficult to put a finger on the best introduction to where stress stiffening is
introduced, but Chapter 8.4.2 Stress Stiffening in the Structural Analysis Guide[15]
gives a nice physical interpretation of it. It states that ”The out-of-plane stiffness of a
structure can be significantly affected by the state of in-plane stress in that structure. This
coupling between in-plane stress and transverse stiffness, known as stress stiffening, is most
pronounced in thin, highly stressed structures, such as cables or membranes. A drumhead,
which gains lateral stiffness as it is tightened, would be a common example of a stress-stiffened
structure.” And then continues by saying some systems may need a large deflection
analysis, while others is obtainable with linear theory. The way to active the stress
stiffening linear analysis is by using the PSTRES,ON. This seems easy at first, but in

3.1.1.4
Linear Pertubation
The general procedure explanation on Linear Pertubation is long and tedious for
different types of analysis, but in short it is an iterative solution method, often the
Newton-Raphson procedure is used[15]. It is only useful to do a linear pertubation
analysis if the linear or nonlinear effects from the base analysis is of interest, such as
the prestress achieved by a rotational velocity. For more information on indepth in-
formation about the many aspects of linear pertubation analysis, it is recommended
to refer to the Structural Analysis Guide Chapter 9 explaining just this. It ”offers
simplicity and ease of use”[15].
There are a couple of commands needed for a successful linear pertubation pre-
stressed modal analysis with respect to rotordynamics. The commands are gathered
from the Rotordynamics Analysis Guide Section 5.3. Solving for a Subsequent Camp-
bell Analysis of a Prestressed Structure Using the Linear Perturbation Procedure[5] and the
Structural Analysis guide Section 9.5. Considerations for Rotating Structures. The for-
mer more so giving example command lines with no values apart from relevant field
options, and the latter giving two examples with real geometry and values. There
are an additional 5 new lines of commands issued, not counting the PSTRES,ON. The
commands here are not the only ones needed to fully perform the analysis, so look
into Appendix D or the help guides mentioned above to get a complete look at what
is necessary.
• RESCONTROL,LINEAR,ALL,1 - Controls file writing for multiframe restarts
• CAMPBELL,RSTP - Prepares the result file for a subsequent Campbell diagram
of a prestressed structure
• ANTYPE,STATIC,RESTART,,,PERTURB - Specify analysis type and restart status
• PERTURB,MODAL,AUTO,,INERKEEP - Sets linear perturbation analysis options.
(AUTO can be exchanged with SPOFF to exclude spin softening)
• SOLVE,ELFORM - Starts a solution, and with the ELFORM option it Reforms all
appropriate element matrices in the first phase of a linear perturbation analysis

striction. But the insight could be relevant for other geometries and materials.

3.3
Alternative Examples
To further investigate the centrifugal effects, a couple of other comparisons are to be
made. Firstly there are blades in the rotating reference frame that could have criti-
cal behavior when modeled as beams being part of a propeller or turbine assembly.
Usually these are modeled as rigid bodies when assuming the propeller or turbine
itself are axially symmetrical[4].
Secondly, thick discs are usually modeled as a rigid body being infinitely-thin , mean-
ing the flexible behavior of those are neglected, which for many cases is a good
assumption[4]. When discs become thinner, they begin to act like membranes and
plates in which the natural frequencies may begin coincide with the bending modes
of the rotor shafts[4]. The disc-shaft interaction will be investigated shortly to bring
more understanding to the simple rotor system and it’s natural frequencies. With this
in mind and the modeling of centrifugal effects in place, the simple rotor system will
be revisited, to see whether the simple analysis is enough or if significant deviations
are seen.
Lastly, a short investigation of thin-walled structures representing a FESS - with mass
away from the rotational axis - are performed to see the influence of modes occurring
in the wall itself.

Flexible Discs
As was just concluded in the section about blades, it is not super efficient to model
thin geometries as solid elements, so the following will focus more on the theory de-
scribed by Genta in Dynamics of Rotating Systems Chapter 14 - Dynamics of rotating
discs and rings[4]. To grasp the dynamics fully and apply it to the previously inves-
tigated simple rotor system, it is best to present the disc-shaft interaction that occurs
for thin discs, such as membranes. Afterwards, the simple rotor system is revisited
to see the impact of the centrifugal effects, that are now well understood.
3.3.2.1
Disc-shaft Interaction
When describing plate or membrane modes it is relevant to talk about diametral
(m) and radial nodes (n) in terms of the shapes[4]. The radial nodes determine the
amount of ”peaks and valleys” from center to edge of the disc, while the diametral
nodes determine the amount around the circumference of the disc. The concept of
modal nodes in different directions is similar to that of rectangular plates[21]. Sim-
ply a change in coordinate system, meaning instead of the x- and y-directions in the
Cartesian coordinate system for rectangular plates, it utilize the r- and θ-directions
in the cylindrical coordinate system. The visual representation of the first 16 com-
binations of membrane mode shapes from [4] can be seen in Figure C.6 attached to
the Appendix C.4. The natural frequencies for the membrane are presented as pro-
portional to the spin speed as ω = βΩ, where β is a constant that depends on the
boundary conditions and solution to the partial differential equation describing the
membrane vibrations[4].
The membrane clamped on the inside does not only exhibit out-of-plane modes, how-
ever in-plane modes resembling that of the rotating ring first appears at very high
rotational speeds, as can be seen by performing a rotordynamic analysis in ANSYS
and constraining the out-of-plane displacement such that it only solves for in-plane
modes. This is completely plausible since the thickness in the radial direction for a
membrane is many times larger than a ring, hence way more ”energy” is needed to
reach that mode. If the right conditions are met for geometry and material, the disc
in-plane and out-of-plane modes may start to intertwine like seen in the case of the
rotating ring. When the disc becomes much thicker, it may reduced to being modeled
as a rigid body[4]. This is relevant to consider when designing systems of semi-thin
discs that reach operational speeds that could interfere with the modes.
There is disc-shaft interaction between the flexural modes with diametral nodes
m = 0 and m = 1. For m = 0, only axial motion is transferred, but for m = 1,
the tilt of the disc couples with the bending mode of the shaft it sits on. This mode
resulting in coupled bending is seen in Figure 3.14b. The color represents the total
vector displacement sum as plotted in ANSYS. Both of these disc-shaft interactions
are seen in the simple rotor system, where the coupled bending mode is caught at
around the 120 Hz mark, and the axial mode at around the 600 Hz mark. Modes of
higher diametral node count (m > 1) are seen as local modes that do not couple with
the bending vibrations of the shaft[4]. This can be interpreted easily by imagining
all modes of two diametral nodes or higher will have som sort of symmetry that will
cancel out the deformation in the center.

Chapter 4
Fiber composites
4.1
Industrial Applications
A lot of literature focus on the static analysis of composites, as it is widely used in
the aviation, energy and automotive industry. For example wind turbine or heli-
copter blades made of composites[22]. These are subject to rotordynamic analysis,
and considering the previous investigation of how rotating blades are not affected
by gyroscopic forces it may seem very possible to setup such analysis in ANSYS.
Similarly carbon fiber wheels for high performance cars are an object of high rota-
tional speeds with cyclic symmetry, as well as composite driveshafts that need to
withstand high torques. These are used due to their improved stiffness and reduced
weight compared to metals, but modeling these are tricky, as will be discussed later.
Anton & Rasmus dedicated a section of their work[2] to the State-of-the-art rotor de-
sign principles. They focus on introducing the analytical and practical limitations
using composite rotors in regards to Flywheel Energy Storage Systems. They do this
by deriving the stress states in different rotor configurations, and relating it to the
problems of kinetic energy preservation. Ultimately, a thin rim is ideal for compos-
ites regarding the radial stresses, but in relation to kinetic energy storage it is not
practical, as stored kinetic energy decreases with reduced thickness. They go on to
present different attempts to solve this issue, such as multi-rim designs focused on
putting the rotors in a compressive prestressed state to counter the radial stresses
achieved under high rotational speeds. Or for example the use of varying material
properties throughout the composite structure, such hybrid fibers, or even only us-
ing fibers to rid of the matrix needed. It is concluded that more research may be
needed in the field to make room for commercial expansion, since most companies
with previous success is are not operating anymore.

4.3
Literature Review of Composite Rotor Modeling
Many different authors have tried to come up with modeling of anisotropic rotors.
Genta introduces the concept of anisotropic rotors in the simplest terms in Chapter 6
of Dynamics of Rotating Systems[4]. At it’s base, the introduction of anisotropy in the
rotor will lead to the fixed reference frame observing properties that vary periodically.
This can be solved if the rotating reference frame is used, in which the equation of
motion is still a differential equation with constant coefficients. Genta presents the
introduction of anisotropy in general, but does not specifically focus on composite
structures. A good remark from the chapter says: The presence of an elastic anisotropy
of the rotating parts of the system causes the occurrence of an instability range spanning
61 of 72
==PAGE:71==
4.4. ANSYS COMPOSITES
from the lowest to the highest critical speed. This pretty much sums up that there are
challenging regions of operation when anisotropy is introduced in the model.
Multiple authors have sought out to model the a composite driveshaft in a helicopter
as presented by Zinberg and Symmonds in [24]. It was not possible to find this source,
as it is stated as being ”presented at the 26th Annual Forum of the American helicopter so-
ciety in 1970”. Despite this, multiple authors use it as a comparison when trying out
new models of composite rotor beam modeling. The Zinberg and Symmonds pre-
sentation seemingly describe a thin-walled circular tube of boron/epoxy composite
with simple supports, which have been tested experimentally and compared with
the Equivalent Modulus Beam Theory (EMBT)[24]. According to Ben Arab in [25],
this formulation identifies an equivalent longitudinal Young’s modulus and equiva-
lent shear modulus by using laminate theory for symmetric stacking sequences. With
Singh and Gupta [26] pointing out it does not take shear deformation into account.
Singh and Gupta[26] first comprehends the work of Zinberg & Symmonds[24], and
Henrique dos Reis et al.[27] to then compare it with their own use of the EMBT and
introduces their layerwise beam theory (LBT) approach. Around 10 years later in
2004, Gubran and Gupta[28] expands on these findings based on the lacking results
for unsymmetric layups. Their work seeked to modify the EMBT and LBT to account
for the unsymmetric stacking sequence and different coupling effects. Along with
these, they also provided a summary of four other theories from Kim and Bert[29],
and Chen and Peng[30]. A couple of years later, Sino et al.[31] seeks out to investi-
gate the dynamic instability of the internal damping in rotors. This is done with a
homogenized finite element beam model accounting for the internal damping. The
results are then compared to that of Gubran and Gupta[28], Chen and Peng[30], Singh
and Gupta[26], as well as the original problem defined by [24]. The conclusion is
that instability thresholds can be greatly highly sensitive to chosen laminate param-
eters[31]. In 2017, Ben Arab then releases a study based in Equivalent Single Layer
Theory (ESLT)[25], followed up by a Modified ESLT[32]. Both seek to take each lam-
ina of the composite into account, regarding copuling effects as well. Similarly Ben
Arab has released a Layerwise Shaft Theory (LST)[33] approach which takes the de-
formations through the thickness into account.
There was an attempt to implement the Equivalent Single Layer Theory from Ben
Arab[25] in MATLAB, but without success. It is unclear whether it is due to faulty
coding or some mistake in the equations. It is believed that more time spent on it
could make it work as intended and is thus left for future studies.

sections allow for the use of the CORIOLIS command, but since it is not explicitly
layered elements, it is assumed it may be possible.
Since composites are often designed as shell structures, the layered shell elements are
a good choice when needing to model a composite geometry in FEM. Even though the
possibilities of a rotordynamic analysis with layered shell elements is not possible, a
simple modal analysis at standstill is still a viable step to take in order to check the
modeling capabilities within ANSYS in regards to composite structures. No attempt
on using the preintegrated composite cross sections was made and it is left for further
studies, despite initially being in the scope of this project to model composites with
gyroscopic effects in ANSYS. To make sure the modeling of a composite shaft is done
correctly, an article is used to compare the resulting natural frequencies. The article
in question is also a work by Safa Ben Arab focusing on a composite rotor unbalance
response based on ESLT[34]. The article has a case study with normal steel discs
and a steel shaft on anisotropic rotor supports to validate the unbalance modeling.
A subsequent unbalance response of a composite shaft is then performed, but first
a study on fiber orientation and stacking sequence is performed. These effects have
also been investigated in the DTU course 41516 Anisotropy and fiber composites, but for
stress analysis. Here it serves to gain an understanding of the effect on the natural
frequencies. It starts out by simply performing a modal analysis at standstill for
different stacking sequences and provides the natural frequencies for the three first
bending. These will be compared to the model made in ANSYS.
The composite shaft model is now created using shell elements. These are not de-
scribed in the previous elements section, and will also be left out here. But in short
there is two structural 3D shell elements; SHELL181 and SHELL281, the prior with
4 nodes at each edge of the rectangular element, and the latter also with midnodes
- resulting in 8 total nodes. For the simplicity of modeling, a fine mesh will be used
alongside the 4 noded SHELL181 element. Each lamina of the laminate is then speci-
fied with commands related to defining sections - see the ANSYS Element and Com-
mand References for more[9][12]. The mesh with 2830 nodes and 2858 elements cre-
ated can be seen on the three resulting bending modes shown in Figure 4.1. There are
simply supported boundary conditions imposed on the ends of the shaft - meaning
no translations, but rotation is allowed. This is done with the rigid-surface constraint
explained in Section 2.2.3. The model properties are presented as:

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The use of ANSYS solid elements for rotordynamic analysis has been successfully
validated and compared to results previously obtained in ABAQUS. It highlights dis-
crepancies between the two, which may just be mesh generation, but ultimately it
shows both programs are capable of rotordynamic analysis. Additionally, the use of
support bearings and point masses with inertia have been used to model boundary
conditions and rigid discs. This was accompanied by the rigid-surface and force-
distributed constraints, which provides the ability to relate a region of nodes to a
reference point called the pilot node. This helped greatly in constraining the models
correctly and capturing the proper dynamics of the geometry. With a comparison
between different element types and shape function differences, it was shown how
higher-order elements significantly improved result accuracy. Along with the cor-
rect constraints, this showed the importance of selecting appropriate modeling tech-
niques when analyzing in FEM. Careful validation against established benchmarks
should not be neglected, since these are based in theory.
Digging into the many guides in the ANSYS Help Documentation helped pinpoint
various analysis considerations. This includes the selection of reference frames based
on the model geometry, especially axisymmetry and mass distribution. The centrifu-
gal effects, stress stiffening and spin softening, were studied in detail along with
theory from Genta. This provided a solid foundation for future users with the tools
to perform advanced rotordynamic analysis in ANSYS, and other programs with sim-
ilar methods of including the effects. In addition to working in ANSYS, methods for
exporting system matrices has been developed, enabling more alternative solution
procedures and further post-processing in MATLAB.
The Rotating Ring example from Giancarlo Genta was a centerpiece of this study,
focusing on investigating the differences in modeling with geometric nonlinearities
versus linearized prestress effects, as well as including these centrifugal effects. It
proved that it is excessive to perform an analysis that accounts for geometric non-
linearities, since the linearized prestress effects yield better computational efficiency
and equally accurate results. Despite G. Genta concluding ”In all cases no strong cen-
trifugal softening is found.” in [3], it is obvious based on the present work that cen-
trifugal softening is indeed present, but in cases where stress stiffening is rightfully
included, as it should be in the rotating reference frame, it does not result in vanishing
natural frequencies - which is likely the reasoning behind Genta’s conclusion.
65 of 72
==PAGE:75==
Along with these analyses, the importance of proper constraints in the model was
addressed. It was found how the use of constraint equation elements in ANSYS pro-
vided a connection between different regions in the finite element model. Such as
forming rigid regions with the rigid-surface constraint or movement in an average
sense with the force-distributed constraint. The exclusion of these was shown to
only yield correct results in simplified analyses.
Further numerical investigation of the system matrices was performed by revisiting
the simple rotor system and taking a deeper dive into the inclision of different ef-
fects. This highlighted the importance of selecting the correct analysis approach and
reference frame selection. The centrifugal effects have additionally been studied for
blades modeled as rotating beams, to further validate the implementation of solid
elements and comparing it to shell elements and analytical studies.
Per the problem statement, fiber composites were studied, but not to the extent ini-
tially planned for. This was caused by the reveal that ANSYS does not process the gy-
roscopic effects of layered elements. Despite this, modal analysis was performed on
a composite shaft and compared with results from an article. As an alternative, mul-
tiple other articles focused on presenting composite beam modeling theories were
researched. There was an attempt to implement the Equivalent Single Layer The-
ory, but without success. The recommendations for future work include exploring
the use of preintegrated composite sections and further validation of the modeling
techniques presented from the literature.
One should lay out the modeling needs in terms of relevancy to the needed results.
If only the shaft behavior is of interest, it could be beneficial to model with beam
elements and point masses. To get a more accurate representation of the shaft defor-
mations, 3D elements are advised, while still possibly using point masses for rigid
discs. When disc modes and coupling effects are crucial to investigate with respect
to operational conditions, it may me necessary to model the flexible discs or even
blades. Similarly it is also possible to separate the analysis, as is often seen with
singular blades and discs.
In summary, this thesis has provided a numerical investigation and validation of
the centrifugal effects encountered in three-dimensional finite element analysis. The
findings presented in this study serves as a guide and stepping stone into further
analysis with centrifugal effects in the field of rotordynamics.

Wrapping up on the investigation of this masters thesis, i want to address some ar-

Enhanced Export Features
The ANSYS coding language (APDL) is not easy to dance with. For now it is pretty
cumbersome to export system matrices and results from the Campbell diagram. My
best guess is that an experienced programmer can dive into the documentation for
scripting properly in ANSYS, and hopefully make script that will automatically ex-
tract the necessary data instead of posting lines upon lines of code into the current
working macro for ANSYS input. The ANSYS Programmer’s Reference[35] preface
states:
• Part I - Guide to Interfacing with Mechanical APDL
This guide describes a group of utilities as well as a set of FORTRAN routines
that you can use to directly access the ANSYS database. You can also use these
capabilities to access data in any of the binary files that MechanicalAPDLwrites
or uses.
• Part II - Guide to User-Programmable Features
Mechanical APDL provides a set of FORTRAN functions and routines that are
available to extend or modify the program’s capabilities. Using these routines
requires relinking the Mechanical APDL program, resulting in a custom version
of Mechanical APDL. The program provides an external commands capability
which you can use to create shared libraries available to Mechanical APDL (ei-
ther from ANSI standard C or FORTRAN). You can use this feature to add cus-
tom extensions to MechanicalAPDLwithout the need to rebuild the Mechanical
APDL executable.
My understanding of this is that there are possibilities to use ANSYS in other ways
than injecting code to the GUI and seeing the results in the program. This is already
partly proven by the fact that results have been extracted in the present work. With
this and in combination with the ANSYS Parametric Design Language Guide[36],
it should therefore be possible to program subroutines that can be called from the
ANSYS macros, or as buttons in the program itself.

5.1.2
ANSYS 2D Axisymmetric Modeling
The ANSYS Technology Showcase[16] holds an interesting piece of work that may
be interesting to look into for saving computational efficiency. It is called Chapter
14: Rotordynamics of a Shaft Assembly Based on a Representative Model of Nelson-
Vaugh Rotor. It is stated that the example illustrates the application of rotordynamics
on a Nelson-Vaugh rotor model, and promises to demonstrate the following[16]:
• General axisymmetric meshing of a 3D geometry
• Disc and bearing modeling
• Gyroscopic effects in rotating structures and modal analysis
• Campbell diagram analysis
• Determination of critical speeds
• Unbalance response analysis
• Orbit plot
• Performance benefits of 2D axisymmetric models
Notably it investigates the benefits of 2D axisymmetric modeling and compares it
to a solid 3D model. Since this thesis has primarily dealt with the 3D elements, it
would be interesting to pick up on this method of modeling with 2D axisymmet-
ric elements. The investigation of gyroscopic effects merely refers to the splitting
of mode shapes by the gyroscopic matrix, and not the centrifugal effects previously
studied. According to the Element Reference[9], the Axisymmetric Solid elements
SOLID272 and SOLID273 has the capabilities related to the centrifugal effects previ-
ously investigated: Coriolis effect, Large strain and deflection, Linear perturbation,
and Stress stiffening. Hence it would be a good spot to try and take these centrifugal
effects into account for more complicated systems, such as the rotor setup analyzed
in the Dynamics of Machinery Course.
The 2D axisymmetric analysis also shows the ability to investigate orbits by combin-
ing the model with massless beam elements. This is a helpful tool when analyzing
shafts with discs and bearings applied along the shaft, since these points will be piv-
otal points and have significant influence on the mode shapes. The corresponding 3D
modes can be harder to grasp since the line elements are more intuitive than the solid
elements. If modeling discs as point masses, there will of course not be any local disc
modes. Additionally, unbalance response in terms of harmonic analysis is a strong
tool to posses in FEM, as this will help predict the behavior of damaged systems.

Journal Bearings in ANSYS
While reading documentation for the COMBI214 element, it was discovered that they
support modeling as journal bearings[9]. This has potential to be combined with the
advanced modeling techniques presented in this report. It should aid the future use of
ANSYS as a stable in validating modeling and analysis done in other programs with
use of journal bearings. Ideally one could try to combine the shaft setup from Dy-
namics of Machinery in 2D axisymmetric elements - as just proposed above - with the
journal bearings and centrifugal effects; stress stiffening and spin softening. The com-
bination of journal bearings, stress stiffening and spin softening effects should yield
more accurate results in comparison with any experimental data gathered. Speaking

5.1.4
PyANSYS Possibilities
The last two areas of future work potentially has conflicting of interest. The MATLAB
export optimization tries to improve the communication between the two programs,
but the more advanced analysis in ANSYS does not necessarily translate directly into
simple system matrices that can be handled by MATLAB, especially not the inclusion
of journal bearings, as these change properties depending on the rotational speed. A
solution to this may be to go another direction and use Python instead of a MAT-
LAB/ANSYS hybrid work routine. This can be done with API’s from the PyANSYS
ecosystem such as PyMAPDL, PyMechanical etc.[38]. These makes it possible to run
the ANSYS programs from python instead of using the ANSYS GUI. Using the Me-
chanical APDL application is also just a not-at-all fancy way to avoid programming
directly in a command window. Avid users of Python may find this option as a gift
compared to becoming an expert at using the Mechanical APDL GUI and using two
programs at once. Running the APDL program from Python may also prove to be
attractive since the workspace of APDL would then already be inside an existing
program used for postprocessing results. Additionally this will also serve to be in
compliance with recent DTU guidelines on a more python focused programming ex-
perience across all courses and academic work.
5.1.5
Composite Shaft Modeling
Since no proper rotordynamic analysis of composite shafts was successfully created
in this project, it is of great interest to provide a stepping stone for others seeking
to perform dynamic analysis of composite structures. There are two proposed ways
to go on about this; first is to try and implement the theory laid out in the litera-
ture review of composites, and second is to give preintegrated composite sections a
chance in ANSYS. Assuming both will work with enough time dedicated to the case,
it seems like a good idea to do both and make a comparison. Since it is unknown
whether it is possible to use the gyroscopic effect for preintegrated beams or shells
in ANSYS, it seems like a good possibility to simply try it out and see if it works.
This would essentially validate the different modeling approaches described in the
literature review for composites.

the Simple Rotor setup. It considers both loading the system matrices as .mtx-files

DAMP, as described in Section 2.2.1. Thereafter, it plots these solutions as Campbell
diagram in a large subplot and also manually converts a set of data from the station-
ary reference frame to the rotating reference frame. Lastly it formats these figures
with a formatfig function.

% analysis to get modes and the lambdas
352

353

zeros(size(K)) eye(size(K))];

eye(size(K)) zeros(size(K))];
