ABSTRACT 
Magnesium (Mg)-based alloy scaffolds produced by using the material extrusion technique 
have been widely considered as biodegradable implants. Unfortunately, controlling their 
degradation rates in human physiological environments is still challenging. One of the best 
ways for solving this challenge is designing Mg alloys with alloying elements that increase 
corrosion resistance. Biodegradable Mg-4Zn porous scaffolds were successfully fabricated 
using extrusion-based 3D printing and following with debinding and sintering. The aim of this 
master's thesis is to investigate the influence of microstructures on the localized corrosion 
behavior of extrusion-based 3D-printed biodegradable Mg-4Zn porous scaffolds intended for 
use in bone implants. The unique combination of the 3D printing technique and the Mg-4Zn 
alloy offers great potential for bone implant applications due to their biocompatibility and 
mechanical properties. However, the localized corrosion susceptibility of these scaffolds 
remains a concern that needs to be addressed to ensure their long-term functionality and 
structural integrity. 
 
In this study, a comprehensive methodology was employed to analyze the microstructural 
features and their impact on localized corrosion. The microstructures, phase compositions and 
surface morphology before and after immersion tests were characterized. The ion 
concentration analysis was conducted to evaluate the localized corrosion behavior of the 
scaffolds under aqueous conditions. To gain deeper insights into the corrosion mechanisms, 
valuable information on the chemical compositions, surface properties, and local 
electrochemical behavior of the scaffolds were assessed with various techniques. 
 
The results of this study demonstrated the significant influence of microstructures on the 
localized corrosion behavior of the Mg-4Zn porous scaffolds. The presence of interconnected 
porosity, grain boundaries, and secondary phases was found to contribute to the initiation and 
propagation of localized corrosion. Furthermore, the corrosion mechanisms were investigated 
using the combination of electrochemical and surface characterization techniques and the 
potential sites prone to corrosion attack were identified. 
 
The findings of this research contribute to the understanding of the factors influencing 
localized corrosion in extrusion-based 3D-printed Mg-4Zn porous scaffolds, thereby 
providing valuable insights for the development of more corrosion-resistant and durable bone 
implants. The knowledge gained from this study serves as a foundation for future research and 
optimization of microstructural designs in biomedical applications. 
 
Overall, this study highlights the significance of considering microstructures in the 
development and production of porous scaffolds designed for bone implants. This is done 
with the ultimate objective of enhancing the extended operational efficiency and clinical 
results of these biomedical implants. 
 
Keywords: 
Magnesium alloys, Corrosion mechanism, Microstructures, Experimental methods, Corrosion 
media, Extrusion-based Scaffolds

1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1. 
Localized corrosion and porous biodegradable Mg-based 
metallic scaffolds 
The potential of porous biodegradable Mg-based metallic scaffolds as optimal bone 
substitutes is widely acknowledged [1], [2]. The latest developments in additive 
manufacturing (AM) have stimulated its utilization to produce Mg-based scaffolds that 
possess geometrically ordered porous structures [3]. The efficacy of manufacturing Mg 
scaffolds has been demonstrated through extrusion-based AM, accompanied by debinding and 
sintering. This method has proven to be advantageous as it circumvents certain critical 
challenges that arise while using powder bed fusion (PBF) technique [4]. 
 
The electrochemical activity of Mg-based alloys is widely known to significantly undermine 
their strategic role in clinical applications. The pure magnesium scaffolds demonstrated a 
significantly elevated rate of in vitro biodegradation. While the spontaneous corrosion of Mg-
based alloys is advantageous for the development of bioresorbable implants in medical 
applications, it also presents a significant challenge due to the unregulated degradation of the 
materials [5], [6]. As a result, corrosion poses a considerable limitation for the diverse array 
of biomedical applications that employ Mg-based materials. The corrosion resistance of Mg-
based alloys in aqueous environments is commonly perceived to be inferior when compared 
to other lightweight alloys like aluminum (Al) alloys. This is attributed to the development of 
a non-protective oxide/hydroxide layer on the surface [7]. 
 
Considerable research efforts have been directed toward investigating diverse approaches to 
enhance the corrosion resistance of Mg and its alloys, such as alloying, purification, surface 
modification, and surface coatings [8]. The enhancement or controlling of the corrosion 
resistance of Mg alloys has prompted the creation of various protection techniques that rely 
on alloying and microstructural manipulation, such as grain size and crystallographic texture, 
etc. [9], [10].  
 
However, the protection or controlling techniques, which are dependent on the manipulation 
of material composition and microstructure, have the potential to facilitate the onset of 
localized corrosion and accelerate its progression. Localized corrosion (as opposed to 
generalized corrosion) has been observed in small and localized areas on orthopedic scaffolds. 
Localized corrosion, including intergranular corrosion (IGC), galvanic corrosion, pitting 
corrosion, crevice corrosion, exhibit a rapid penetration rate into the metallic scaffolds. In fact, 
localized corrosion typically demonstrates a significantly greater corrosion rate compared to 
general corrosion, often by several orders of magnitude [11]. These localized corrosion 
processes might degrade the mechanical properties of the scaffolds in the very initial stage of

Despite the extant knowledge of the corrosion mechanisms of Mg alloys, there are still several

widely accepted conclusion. The current findings are subject to debate, and the 
microstructures cause a complex interaction, with different environmental conditions on the 
corrosion behavior of Mg alloys, making it impossible to study each microstructure 
completely independently [12]–[14]. Investigating such phenomena on the surface in order to 
gain a comprehensive understanding of corrosion processes, particularly at localized sites, still 
acts as a huge challenge [15]. 
1.2. 
Introducing the project 
In this master’s project, binary Mg-Zn alloy scaffolds were fabricated using extrusion-based 
AM with a geometrically organized and completely interconnected porous structure from pre-
alloyed Mg-4Zn (4 𝑤𝑡% Zn) powders as the material of choice. Pure Mg scaffolds made in 
the same way served as a reference group for research on the impact of the Zn addition on the

This master’s thesis aims to enhance comprehension of the impact of microstructures in

analysis methods, electrochemical analysis methods, and surface analysis methods will be 
employed to investigate the corrosion mechanisms happening on the surfaces of both Mg-

2.1. 
Scaffolds for bone tissue engineering using AM 
Osseous tissue, also known as bone, has become the second most transplanted tissue 
throughout the world and the demand for it keeps growing [16]. Each year, billions of people 
in the world undergo bone fractures directly or indirectly caused by severe falls, traffic 
accidents, infection, aging, trauma, tumor resection, congenital disabilities, and bone 
disorders [17], [18]. In addition, since the bones have a poor natural ability to heal by 
themselves when the bone defects are diagnosed as critical sized, artificial prosthesis 
transplants and bone grafting are widely used to either repair or even replace the natural bones 
through surgical treatment in order to treat bone defects and injuries [19]. Typically, a critical 
sized bone detect means a bone gap larger than 2 𝑐𝑚 in the human body [20], [21], for 
example, the lower limit of critical tibial defect is 2.5 𝑐𝑚 [22]. Therefore, the global need 
keeps growing for developments in artificial bones, also known as bone implants, in order to 
assure an improvement in the outcomes of orthopedic therapies [23]. The ultimate goal of 
introducing bone implants into the bone defect is to promote the remodeling of the recipient 
patient's own cells, so cytocompatibility, mechanical strength, stiffness, corrosion resistance 
and wear resistance of bone implants are the most significant properties, and these properties 
can be modified with architectures, manufacturing methods and compositions of bone 
implants [24]. 
 
Bone consists of both organic components, such as the protein collagen (Type I collagen 
makes up 97 𝑤𝑡. % of extra cellular matrix protein), and inorganic mineral phases, such as 
hydroxyapatite (𝐶𝑎10(𝑃𝑂4)6(𝑂𝐻)2 or 𝐻𝐴), which is embedded in a collagen-based organic 
matrix and lends its strength to the overall framework of bone and bone tissue [25].

Scaffolds for bone tissue engineering using AM 
 
Cancellous bone and cortical bone are the two types of osseous tissues that make up bones, 
and the spongy cancellous bone is surrounded by cortical bone with less porosity. The 
cancellous bone, also known as the innermost layer of bone, has a porosity range of between 
50% and 90% by volume with pore sizes verifying from 200𝜇𝑚 to 1000𝜇𝑚. On the other 
hand, cortical bone is the thick outer layer of bone. It has a porosity of less than 10% by 
volume, which makes cortical bone stiffer than cancellous bone, and cortical bone is mainly 
made up of micrometer-scale osteons and haversian canals that fit together with blood vessels, 
canaliculi, and other similar components within the restricted porosity [26], [27]. The 
remodeling, maturation, differentiation, and resorption processes that occur in both types of 
bone are dynamic processes, and they are regulated by interactions among osteoblast, 
osteocyte, and osteoclast cells [28].  
 
A scaffold, as an interconnected porous structure, is preferred for bone regeneration [29]. An 
ideal scaffold can be said to have 4 very important advantages: first, the ability of the scaffold 
to take the desired shape for a critical size defect is called the form of a scaffold, the second 
advantage refers to its ability to provide mechanical support at the defect site, and the third 
advantage requires the scaffold to be firmly fixed to the bone defect to prevent any unwanted 
movement, and the last advantages is how it can help stimulate bone synthesis relying on cells, 
proteins, growth factors, etc. [30]–[33]. 
 
Using AM techniques, it is possible to manufacture tissue-like constructs with a diversity of 
biomaterials in ways that were not possible in the past. These constructions can have complex 
geometries and surface characteristics, and they can have a variety of functionalities that are 
suitable for different locations in the human body [34]–[37]. AM techniques can be divided 
into the following seven process categories: material extrusion (MEX), powder bed fusion 
(PBF), directed energy deposition (DED), vat photopolymerization (VPP), material jetting 
(MJT), binder jetting (BJT) and sheet lamination (SHL) [38], [39]. 
 
Among the seven categories, MEX has the greatest potential to produce orthopedic porous 
scaffolds that provide the four advantages mentioned in the previous paragraph, since MEX is 
capable of fabricating multifunctional and complex scaffold designs by employing a wide 
range of powdered raw materials via a simple manufacturing process without distortions, 
residual stresses, cracks or metallurgical defects, which are intrinsic shortcomings of laser-
based AM implants [40]. 
 
A variety of metallic materials with great corrosion resistance inside the human body, 
including stainless steel (𝐹𝑒−𝐶 alloys), titanium (𝑇𝑖) alloys, nitinol (𝑁𝑖−𝑇𝑖 alloys) or 
cobalt-chromium (𝐶𝑜−𝐶𝑟) alloys, zirconia (𝑍𝑟𝑂2), and tantalum (𝑇𝑎) alloys, have been used 
in the production of porous biomaterials as permanent scaffolds [41]. These metallic bone 
implants require subsequent surgery to remove the implants from the human body and have a 
certain probability of causing secondary damage in the human body, for examples, 
unnecessary infection, ultimately leading to failure of treatable bone disorders, or even worse 
[42], [43]. The removal surgeries accounted for almost 30%  of all orthopedic medical 
resources and they do cost patients a lot of money and put a heavy burden on the healthcare 
system [44]. In addition, the permanent implants may release harmful ions and cause cell 
death and bone destruction if they are still in the human body for a long extra time after they 
should have been removed [45].

200 𝐺𝑃𝑎 for stainless steel, around 230 𝐺𝑃𝑎 for 𝐶𝑜−𝐶𝑟 alloys, and around 115 𝐺𝑃𝑎 for

these permanent bone implants contribute to the stress shielding effect on the bones [48], [49], 
although these metallic materials exhibit great biocompatibility, mechanical strength, and 
corrosion resistance. Due to the stress shielding effect, the bone underneath the implants will 
lose its density if the implants bear too much of the applied load while the bone receives 
insufficient stresses during the remodeling and adapting process [49]–[51]. 
 
As an alternative, degradable biomaterials can effectively avoid removal surgeries needed for 
permanent implants with the benefits of full tissue healing and function restoration [52]. On 
the basis of their fundamental manufacturing components, biodegradable materials may be 
grouped into three main categories: polymers, ceramics, and metals [53], [54]. Most of natural 
polymers are insufficient to bear the stress during the degradation process in the bone defects, 
due to their weak mechanical properties, thus, only a few natural polymers, such as collagen, 
keratin, and chitosan, are suitable for medical implants [52], [55]. Like natural polymers, 
synthetic polymers are biocompatible, biodegradable and suitable for new tissue formation, 
however, acidification of the bone tissue surrounding the synthetic polymer implants can lead 
to inflammation, and the poor mechanical properties of synthetic polymers can cause fracture 
of implants at the early stage [55]–[58]. 
 
Metallic and ceramic biomaterials are better for harder tissues, like bones, whereas  polymeric 
materials are the best for treating soft tissues [55]. Biodegradable ceramics have high 
development potential as orthopedic implant materials because of good osteoconductivity and 
corrosion resistance, but the strength of biodegradable ceramics is much lower than that of 
permanent ceramics, which limits the medical applications of absorbable ceramics, alongside 
with extremely high rigidity, poor brittleness and poor fracture toughness [59]–[61]. Iron(Fe)-
based [62], [63], zinc (Zn)-based [64], and magnesium (Mg)-based [65] alloys are the three 
most employed types of biodegradable metallic materials for bone implants with the stronger 
mechanical support during inflammation and repair and lower mechanical degeneration 
during bone remodeling than degradable polymers and ceramics [66], [67]. 
 
However, extrusion-based AM scaffolds made from biodegradable metallic materials are not 
ready for either commercial or orthopedic usage within the human body. The fundamental 
challenge is a shortage of biocompatible and degradable metallic materials made by using the 
MEX technique. No substance possesses all the 4 advantages mentioned in the previous 
content. Magnesium and its alloys are good contender for biodegradable metallic materials 
that only remain in the human body momentarily for the purpose of bone healing [68]. They 
are subsequently dissolved, and finally replaced by bone tissues. In addition to being essential 
to human metabolism, magnesium may be found in the naturally occurring form in bone 
tissues [69], [70]. The excess amount of degraded magnesium particles can be dissolved by 
phagocytes such as macrophages and can be excreted through kidneys and urine [71], [72]. 
 
The spontaneous corrosion degradation is a significant advantage of AM Mg-based scaffolds, 
however, the high corrosion rate also brings three main drawbacks. First, the high corrosion 
rate of biodegradable Mg-based metallic materials reduces the mechanical stability of the 
scaffolds [73]. Second, the degradation of Mg-based materials releases an enormous amount 
of hydrogen gas, 𝐻2, which potentially damages bone tissue and even induces gas embolism 
eventually [6]. Last, even though the human body can regulate the pH of the body fluid and 
blood, a quickly corroding Mg implant will cause local alkalization together with a fast 
hydrogen evolution process, which can negatively impact pH-dependent physiological

The key strategy to avoid the three issues mentioned in the previous paragraphs is slowing 
down the degradation rate of AM Mg-based scaffolds in the human body so that Mg and its 
alloys can be widely used in the bone tissue engineering field. The three main measures to 
improve corrosion resistance, i.e., slow down degradation rate, are surface coating [76], 
purification [77], and alloying [78], [79]. Alloying will alter the microstructures of AM Mg-
based scaffolds [80]. Moreover, microstructures, such as grain size, second phase particles 
and micropores, not only affect the dissolution process, but also complex interactions between 
these microstructures occur and simultaneously affect the corrosion process [12]–[14]. Thus, 
understanding the corrosion mechanisms and processes of AM Mg-based scaffolds, 
particularly localized corrosion, mainly impacted by microstructures, is essential to 
developing improved approaches to controlling the degradation rate [15]. 
2.2. 
Mg-based orthopedic scaffolds produced by MEX 
Material Extrusion (MEX) has by far the most widespread adoption of all the seven AM 
techniques [39], as inexpensive printers and readily available materials can be found at offline 
hardware stores, and they are also available through various suppliers such as Amazon on the 
Internet even for hobbyists just getting started with AM. In addition, for researchers with 
certain technical needs, high-end and large industrial AM machines can be purchased from 
countless companies. In chapter 2, the basic procedures of MEX, especially the Direct Ink 
Writing (DIW) technology, will be explained and the metallic alloys with Mg as the basic 
material that are used to form scaffolds through MEX will be discussed. 
2.2.1. Classifications of MEX 
The AM processes of MEX include six different technologies, namely Fused Deposition 
Modelling (FDM), Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF), Fused Granulate Fabrication (FGF), 
Fused Pellet Fabrication or Fused Particle Fabrication (FPF), Powder Melt Extrusion (PME) 
and Direct Ink Writing (DIW). Among the six technologies, FDM and FFF, as shown in Fig. 
2-1, are the two most widely used technologies so far. The production processes of FDM and

The remaining four technologies require further research and development. Among them, 
FGF and FPF are categorized as new MEX technologies that utilize pellet raw materials, 
whereas PME and DIW are new MEX technologies that utilize powder raw materials [38]. 
 
DIW, also known as Robocasting, is a promising AM technique for fabricating scaffolds 
using magnesium-based alloys. With relatively lower working temperature compared with the 
other five MEX technologies, DIW builds 3D structures by layering extrudate, i.e., the 
resulting extruded material, forced out through a nozzle or nozzles when pressure is applied to 
the powder-based feedstock in a reservoir or reservoirs [81]. With the constant applied 
pressure, the extrudate will maintain a constant cross-sectional diameter and will flow at a 
consistent rate, and if the nozzle or nozzles, with the help of robot, travel over the depositing 
surface at a constant velocity that corresponds to the consistent flow rate, the diameter of the 
extrude will stay constant. When the feedstock is extruded through the nozzle, the extrudate 
must be in a semi-solid state but will solidify immediately after extrusion, and the materials 
used for DIW should be viscoelastic, allowing the materials to come out of the nozzles 
continuously. These properties and states can help retain the shape of the printed layers when 
the extrudate is stretched over the solidified surface [39]. Additionally, a stable and solid 
structure of scaffolds will be formed only when the extrudate has firmly bonded with the 
solidified surface that has been extruded several seconds ahead. 
 
For DIW processes with multiple powder-based materials, multiple materials can be pre-
mixed and stored in a reservoir as one feedstock, which can be forced out of the reservoir 
through only one nozzle [82], as shown in Fig. 2-2. The pre-mixed feedstock can be prepared 
by mixing various powdered raw materials with a polymer binder. The raw materials and the 
polymer binder should be sufficiently compatible with each other so that the unwanted 
interaction between the raw materials and the polymer binder can be avoided during the 
process of mixing, material extrusion, post AM debinding and sintering. The other way to 
build a structure via DIW with multiple raw materials is to integrate more than one nozzles 
with one working panel, and different raw materials are extruded out of the reservoirs by the 
corresponding nozzles according to the design requirements, finally products with complex 
multi-material interfaces are completed by multi-material extrusion [40].

Mg-based orthopedic scaffolds produced by MEX 
 
The potential of 3D printing (3DP) scaffolds using Mg alloys is promising due to the lower 
temperature treatment enabled by DIW [83]. The Mg alloys exhibit low melting and boiling 
points. However, the two predominant PBF techniques, namely selective laser melting (SLM) 
and selective laser sintering (SLS), employ high-energy lasers that may induce thermal effects 
on Mg alloys, such as insufficient porosity, oxidation, loss of alloying elements, and thermal 
cracking,  owing to the substantial heat input [84], [85]. The utilization of extrusion-based 
DIW provides precise regulations of 3DP temperatures, resulting in a reduction of thermal 
effects on Mg alloys during the manufacturing process. 
 
Furthermore, the utilization of extrusion-based DIW presents enhanced adaptability in 
scaffold design and fabrication parameters, such as pore size, porosity, strut width, strut 
spacing, and scaffold geometry. The technique facilitates accurate control of the deposition 
rate and layer thickness, leading to the creation of scaffolds possessing customized properties 
that are suitable for particular applications [83]. This flexibility inherent in extrusion-based 
DIW renders it a suitable method for the production of complex and customized scaffolds 
with Mg-based alloys, which are frequently required in biomedical applications. 
2.2.2. Mg and its alloys for fabricating extrusion-based scaffolds 
By applying the DIW technology, Mg-based alloys can be successfully fabricated into 
scaffolds  with a completely interconnected porous architecture that can be used as bone 
implants [83], [86], [87]. Mg-based alloys exhibit four significant properties, such as 
biocompatibility, osteogenesis, mechanical properties similar to natural bones and 
biodegradability, which make them well-suited for temporary bone implants, particularly for 
applications that require temporary support during bone healing and recovery. 
 
The primary consideration for a Mg alloy scaffold as an orthopedic implant is its 
biocompatibility. The Mg-based alloys for fabricating the temporary implants must be non-
toxic and should not elicit any inflammatory or allergic responses within the human body [69]. 
As such, it is advisable to refrain from utilizing Mg-based materials containing toxic elements 
and allergic elements, given that toxic elements are known to be harmful to human health, 
whereas allergic elements are capable of inducing allergic responses within the human body. 
On the contrary, the addition of nutrient elements in Mg-based metallic scaffolds has the 
potential to enhance the regeneration of natural bone tissues [88]. A classification of the most 
frequently employed alloying elements in biodegradable Mg-based scaffolds is presented in

Ca is a fundamental element that constitutes bones, and Mg-Ca alloys have been shown to 
possess favorable mechanical properties and stimulate bone formation. The addition of Ca to 
Mg has been found to improve grain refinement, decrease the grain size and increase the

are brittle, and this brittleness can trigger cracking and increase the degradation rate as a result 
of galvanic corrosion. Ca is considered a biofunctional element, and the level of 
biocompatibility is deemed satisfactory provided that the quantity of Ca introduced into a Mg-
Ca alloy does not exceed 1𝑤𝑡%, which limits the effect of Ca on Mg-Ca alloys.

crystalline phase. 
 
Zn, one of the most essential nutrient elements in the human body, is a significant alloying 
element added in Mg-based alloys for the application as bone implants. The effects of the Mg-
Zn alloys, which are created by combining Mg and Zn, include the strengthening aging effect 
and strengthening solid solutions with about 6.2𝑤𝑡% solubility limit. Moreover, Zn aids in 
preventing the damaging corrosion effects of iron and nickel impurities that might be found in 
magnesium alloys. Compared to pure Mg implants, Mg-Zn alloys have better mechanical 
properties due to their increased strength and ductility. Mg-Zn alloys accelerate bone 
regeneration and have strong biocompatibility. The capacity of Mg-Zn alloys to generate 
bone-like appetite in situ, which may improve bone regeneration, is one of the main 
advantages. The possible cytotoxicity of the breakdown products, which may have an impact 
on the nearby tissues and cells, is a drawback of Mg-Zn alloys.  
 
Sn participates in biological processes in the human body as a nutritional component and 
improves the stability of the internal body environment. Sn is added to Mg to increase the 
tensile strength and corrosion resistance of the alloys. While Sn itself is not toxic, most Sn-
contained salts have the potential to cause cancer. In fact, no precise role for Sn element in the 
human body has been discovered, and its biological purpose is still unclear. 
 
Sr can partially replace Ca in a variety of biochemical processes in the human body because it 
has similar chemical characteristics to Ca, and Sr can also provide the tissues that contain Sr 
with extra strength. In addition, Sr absorbs extra Ca into the bones. Like Mg-Ca alloys, Mg-Sr 
alloys help refine grains and enhance the corrosion resistance of Mg. Due to their potential for 
promoting angiogenesis and osteogenesis, Mg-Sr alloys have drawn interest for use in 
orthopedic porous scaffolds. It is shown that Mg-Sr alloys can enhance bone formation and 
stimulate the formation of new blood vessels, which can promote tissue regeneration. 
However, Mg-Sr alloys also have limitations similar to Mg-Ca alloys. The mechanical 
strength of Mg-Sr alloys may also be a limitation in load-bearing applications.

2.3. 
Microstructures and corrosion behavior of Mg alloys 
It has been demonstrated in recent research that the microstructures of Mg alloys, including 
factors such as grain size, the distribution of grain boundaries and second phase particles, as 
well as the presence of micropores, have notable impacts on the corrosion behavior of these 
materials. For example, grain refinement of Mg alloys results in a modification to their 
mechanical properties and corrosion behavior by altering the grain size, the density, and 
distribution of grain boundaries and even second phase particles of Mg alloys. The two most 
significant alloying elements in an Mg alloy are identified by the first two letters of the name 
using the following codes, according to the standard designation of Mg alloys, for example, 
A-Al, Z-Zn, K-Kr, and E-rare earth elements. In this chapter, a comprehensive review is 
conducted about the influence of microstructures on the corrosion behavior of Mg alloys 
potentially implanted during bone surgeries as biomaterials. Additionally, a brief insight into 
the microstructures and corrosion behavior is present, in order to provide knowledge for the 
design of biodegradable Mg alloys with better corrosion resistance. 
2.3.1. Grain size and corrosion 
It has been confirmed that the corrosion of Mg alloys depends on the grain size, although the 
conclusions of different studies are contradictory [11], [101]. The effect of grain size on the 
corrosion behavior of Mg alloys prepared by rapid solidification under different cooling rates 
was investigated, and the delay in the initiation of filiform corrosion can be ascribed to the 
refinement of grain size and the creation of a supersaturated solid solution of a singular 𝛼−
𝑀𝑔 phase within the alloys [102]. The fine-grained 𝐴𝑍31𝐵 alloy demonstrated superior 
corrosion resistance in comparison to the hot extruded 𝐴𝑍31𝐵 alloy [103]. One possible 
reason for the improved corrosion resistance observed in finer-grained Mg alloys is that the 
process of grain refinement could potentially reduce the residual stress on the surface films 
resulting from the disparity between the Mg oxide/hydroxide layer and the underlying Mg 
alloy substrate. Yet, there are many other studies reporting a reduction in corrosion resistance 
with a decrease in grain size. The corrosion resistance of the 𝐴𝐸21 alloy was found to be 
inferior after decreasing the grain size by undergoing an equal channel angular pressing 
(ECAP) treatment [104]. A reduction in the corrosion resistance of pure Mg was noted 
subsequent to the grain refinement through the application of strain [105]. The addition of Zr 
to the Mg-xZr-2Sr alloy results in a reduction in grain size, however, there is a corresponding 
decrease in the corrosion resistance of the alloy in SBF [106]. Therefore, it has been 
concluded by these studies that the corrosion resistance of Mg alloys experiences a decline 
with grain refinement. 
2.3.2. Grain boundaries and corrosion 
Intergranular corrosion (IGC) is a common phenomenon observed at the grain boundaries of 
Mg alloys, resulting in localized attacks on the affected regions [107]. There is a belief among 
certain studies that grain boundaries function as barriers against corrosion, ultimately slowing 
down the corrosion kinetics [108], [109]. On the contrary, it has been noted by other studies 
that the presence of grain boundaries can serve as crystal defects, eventually leading to an

increase in the corrosion rate of Mg [110]. It is notable that the variety of alloys and the range 
of grain sizes ought to be considered when the effect of grain boundaries on the corrosion 
behavior of Mg alloys is discussed. It was observed that 𝑍𝐾60 fabricated through the quick 
cooling involved in the SLM technique, with refined grains and more grain boundaries, 
demonstrated improved mechanical characteristics and decreased corrosion rates [111], [112]. 
This observation is in line with the proposition that grain boundaries function as corrosion 
barriers. 
 
The aforementioned studies indicate that there is still a lack of consensus regarding the 
corrosion behavior of Mg alloys in which grain size and grain boundaries impact. Additional 
research is required to acquire a deeper understanding of the impact of grain size and grain 
boundaries on the corrosion behavior of Mg alloys. 
2.3.3. Secondary phases and galvanic corrosion 
Mg exhibits high chemical reactivity and can start reactions with other alloying elements, 
resulting in the formation of secondary phases, also known as intermetallic compounds. These 
intermetallic compounds tend to precipitate along the grain boundaries and inside the Mg 
grains. The corrosion properties of Mg alloys are significantly influenced by these secondary 
phases. It has been illustrated that the properties of Mg alloys, including corrosion behavior 
and mechanical properties, are often influenced by the volume fraction of the secondary 
phases, and this volume fraction is determined by the concentration of alloying elements 
present in Mg alloys [113]–[115]. It is evident that the volume fraction of the secondary 
phases increases with increasing concentration of alloying element in binary Mg alloys 
beyond its solid solubility. Mg alloys with higher concentrations of alloying elements present 
secondary phases that form continuous networks along grain boundaries. These phases are 
more thermodynamically stable than the Mg matrix, resulting in an accelerated cathodic 
reaction and an inhibited anodic reaction during polarization tests. 
 
The degradation of the Mg alloy matrix is expedited by the coupled galvanic corrosion effect 
between the Mg alloy matrix and the secondary phases, with the latter serving as the cathode 
during the process [116]. Secondary phases in Mg alloys exhibit distinct electrochemical 
potentials. When the secondary phase possesses a greater positive potential than the Mg alloy 
matrix, multiple primary galvanic cells are generated, which will subsequently affect the 
corrosion behavior of the Mg alloys. Furthermore, the secondary phases have a complex 
influence on the corrosion properties of Mg alloys, especially ternary alloys with multiple 
alloying elements. Overall, the corrosion behavior of Mg alloys is notably influenced by the 
volume fraction, distribution, and electrochemical potential of the secondary phases. 
2.3.4. Micropores and corrosion 
Metallic Mg alloy orthopedic scaffolds, which are also produced by AM, exhibit a higher 
susceptibility to degradation in comparison to solid bulk Mg alloy orthopedic implants. This 
is attributed to the increased exposure of interconnected macropores, typically 200 −
600 𝜇𝑚,  to body fluids [66]. Furthermore, it should be noted that in this particular 
circumstance, the interconnected network of micropores (with a range of diameters from 2 −
50𝜇𝑚) presents in the Mg alloy scaffolds produced by AM, results in a heightened area-to-
volume ratio that is exposed to body fluids. This increased surface area-to-volume ratio 
potentially renders the scaffold more vulnerability to corrosion.

After an Mg alloy scaffold is implanted in the human body, the micropores act as traps for 
body fluids and create an ion-rich localized environment that promotes pitting or crevice 
corrosion. Pitting and crevice corrosion occur due to rapid corrosion in micro-localized areas 
and are associated with the failure of the passive layers. The pitted areas, also called pits, are 
usually small and hidden by corrosion products or deposits in body fluids. The corrosion 
products or deposits create a localized habitat by forming a barrier between the micropores 
and the body fluid, and they also make it challenging to keep track of micropores. Pits are tiny 
but very corrosive, and they may also be shallow, circular, subterranean, undercut, or 
horizontal. Pits can spread downward, piercing the substrate [117]. The environment within 
the pit includes chlorides from body fluid, which increases the passive film's susceptibility to 
breakdown. More Mg-based substrate corrodes as the thickness of the protective layer 
diminishes, which raises the pH of localized area around the pits. The corrosion rate increases 
when the localized environment becomes more aggressive. Pitting corrosion is known to be 
dangerous to implants made of Mg alloy because it causes metal fatigue fractures and stress 
corrosion cracking, and the presence of micropores speeds up pitting corrosion, which causes 
an implant to fail at the early healing stage [97]. 
 
Crevice corrosion is a type of localized corrosion that occurs in a narrow gap resulting from 
contact between metals or between a metal and a non-metal. This phenomenon is typically 
caused by a difference in oxygen concentration between the gap and the surrounding solution 
[118]. Crevice-induced corrosion, also known as crevice-enhanced corrosion, can arise when 
the presence of corrosion products within crevices leads to the formation of a distinct 
environment that differs from the surrounding bulk material. The phenomenon of privileged 
corrosion in micropores can be attributed to the distinct environmental conditions that exist 
within the pores, further resulting in the breakdown of the passive layer on the surface. The 
morphology of micropores has an impact on the process of degradation. Micropores that are 
irregularly shaped and unevenly distributed are particularly vulnerable to corrosion as a result 
of the accumulation of aggressive ions at the corners of the micropores [119]. It has been 
established that micropores are present within stainless steel components manufactured 
through SLM and these micropores have a diameter of approximately 40 𝜇𝑚 and are 
susceptible to corrosion in chloride solutions [120]. Similar localized corrosion processes in 
the microporous of stainless steels may occur to Mg alloy in body fluids. 
 
The presence of micropores on the surface of Mg alloys can lead to the formation of an 
unstable oxide or hydroxide protective layer when exposed to body fluids. This phenomenon 
can result in a significant decrease in the corrosion resistance of Mg alloys and an increase in 
local degradation [121]. Because hydroxide ions are formed and can get trapped in the 
micropores, surfaces near the micropores of Mg-based scaffolds and surfaces inside the 
microporous network are more vulnerable to localized pH changes than other surfaces that are 
not near the micropores. The resulting poor passive layer, affected by local pH changes, is 
more likely to be damaged [122]. Mg alloys are more prone to pitting corrosion due to the 
existence of micropores. 
 
Yet, there exists limited research on the micropores of extrusion-based AM Mg alloy 
scaffolds concerning corrosion behavior, with a particular emphasis on localized corrosion. 
One of the main reasons is that the micropores of extrusion-based AM Mg alloy scaffolds are 
produced with the development of AM technology. Despite the limited research on the 
influence of micropores in Mg alloys produced by AM on corrosion behavior, insights can be 
obtained from existing studies on the influence of micropores in other metallic biomaterials 
produced by AM on corrosion behavior.

2.4. 
Degradation of Mg and its alloys as biomaterials 
The application of Mg alloys in the production of temporary implants is attributed to their 
intrinsic biodegradability. The inherent biodegradability of Mg alloys implies that temporary 
implants made of Mg alloys are expected to undergo spontaneous degradation upon exposure 
to psychological body fluids. The temporary implant serves the function of stabilizing the 
fractured bone and furnishing the requisite reinforcement during the bone recovery period 
until the latter half of the osseous union, which involves bone regeneration, is achieved. In 
this chapter, corrosion mechanism of temporary Mg-based implants in aqueous environments 
is provided. Additionally, the impact of diverse alloying elements on the biodegradability of 
Mg alloys and the influence of varying concentrations of alloying elements in Mg alloys on 
degradation are also discussed. 
2.4.1. Corrosion mechanism 
The inadequate corrosion resistance of Mg alloys in aqueous environment compared to other 
lightweight alloys, such as aluminum (Al), can be attributed to the elevated inherent 
dissolution inclination of Mg, which is only marginally restrained by the films of corrosion 
products, and the films are non-protective oxide or hydroxide layers on the surface [7]. 
Furthermore, the existence of impurities and second phases that function as regional cathodes 
result in the accelerating of localized galvanic corrosion. All physical and chemical features, 
reactions, changes, and processes that take place when Mg alloys are exposed to an aqueous

stages, as shown in Fig. 2-3, have a critical impact on all forms of corrosion of Mg alloys [5],

2.4.3. The effect of Zn concentration 
Using Zn as a case study, it has been observed that Zn has the ability to transform impurities, 
such as Fe and Ni, which have a negative effect on the corrosion of Mg alloys, into

results in the formation of secondary phases and refinement of grains, which consequently 
impacts the corrosion behavior of Mg alloys [13], [115], [139]. It has been found that the 
precipitation of the secondary phases of Mg-Zn intermetallic compounds from the Mg matrix 
occurs when the Zn content reaches 3% in Mg alloys [140]. Current research indicates that the 
addition of Zn can improve the corrosion resistance of Mg alloys. However, a different 
viewpoint suggests that an overabundance of Zn may have a negative impact on the corrosion

Novel methodologies and tools have been developed to investigate the localized corrosion

microelectrochemical techniques, such as scanning Kelvin probes (SKP) or scanning Kelvin

2.5.1. Selecting electrochemical techniques to investigate localized 
corrosion processes of Mg alloys 
SKP/SKPFM 
The correlation between the ex-situ surface potential distribution of Mg alloys obtained 
through SKP/SKPFM and the corrosion potential implies that these techniques can be 
regarded as supplementary methods for investigating the localized corrosion process of Mg 
alloys [142]. The primary benefit of this approach is its simplicity in conducting the 
experiment, as it does not require the construction of intricate microelectrode probes or the 
restriction of electrolyte conductivity. Moreover, the measurements can be conducted in an 
ambient air environment [143]. Despite the potential of SKPFM to overcome the low-
resolution limitation, it is necessary to undertake probe calibration and take into account the 
concerns related to the stated limitation associated with SKP. Regardless of these limitations, 
SKP has caught the interest of certain researchers, and the examination of localized corrosion 
mechanisms in magnesium and its alloys delivers significant insights into localized corrosion 
behavior. For instance, Gao et al. [144] used SKP to characterise the corrosion process of the 
Mg-3Nd-1Li-0.2Zn alloy in 3.5% NaCl solution. 𝑀𝑔12𝐿𝑎, 𝑀𝑔12𝐶𝑒, and 𝑀𝑔3𝑁𝑑 are the 
examples of rare earth-rich secondary phases that Hurley et al [145]. found using SKPFM. 
These secondary phases were observed to have no direct relationship between their voltaic 
potential and open circuit potential.

SECM 
The adoption of SECM is highly advantageous in the investigation of localized corrosion 
mechanisms in Mg alloys, owing to its outstanding spatial resolution and chemical sensitivity 
[142], [146]–[148]. It is well known that magnesium surfaces corrode spontaneously. The 
phenomenon of spontaneous corrosion of magnesium surfaces is widely acknowledged. Even 
so, the abnormal production of hydrogen (𝐻2) in this substance when subjected to anodic bias 
demands significant evaluation. The process of corrosion is often accompanied by an 
acceleration in the production of 𝐻2 , which occurs as a result of the polarization of 
magnesium from its open circuit potential (OCP) to a more positive potential [149]. The 
occurrence in question is frequently denoted as negative differential effect (NDE) or 
disproportionate hydrogen evolution. The employment of substrate generation-tip collection 
(SG-TC) has become a significant technique in analyzing the corrosion activities on the Mg 
alloy surfaces. The Platinum (Pt) probe is used to catalyze the oxidation of 𝐻2, which is 
generated due to the corrosion of the Mg alloy surfaces in either spontaneous or non-
spontaneous corrosion process [150].   
 
Due to its high susceptibility to corrosion, the surface of magnesium will experience the 
extensive formation of 𝐻2 bubbles. The operation of the SG-TC mode during the oxidation 
process of the 𝐻2 gas originating from the corrosion surface reveals that the constancy of the 
Faradaic current is attributed to the production of a significant quantity of gas bubbles 
enveloped in 𝐻2  gas. This occurrence significantly impacts the measurement probe, 
particularly when the magnesium surface functions as the anode electrode [151]. 
 
Conversely, the redox competition mode exhibits restricted efficiency when employed to 
examine the corrosion process of magnesium surfaces due to the minor involvement of 𝑂2 in 
the corrosion mechanism of magnesium materials. Merely a minor portion of the 𝑂2 that has 
dissolved in the electrolyte in proximity to the corroded surface undergoes electrochemical 
reduction in this situation [152]. Thus, it is important that SECM has the capability to only 
capture topographical images of corroded materials at different stages [153]. The 
effectiveness of this mode is restricted owing to the growth of 𝐻2 on the surface, as the 
potential range employed to measure the oxygen concentration at the probe is almost entirely 
covered by the 𝐻2 oxidation range [154]. As a result, the hydrogen oxidation currents often 
dominate over the oxygen reduction currents [155]. 
 
Jamali et al. [149] measured the local current produced by hydrogen evolution on the surface 
of the AZ31B alloy in a simulated physiological fluid using the SG/TC mode of SECM to 
identify the Al-rich inclusions. By using SECM, Lamaka et al. [156] identified the locations 
of pitting corrosion on the AZ31B alloy surface. In order to analyze the corrosion of AM60 in 
NaCl solution and monitor the emerging and propagation of pitting corrosion on the surface 
of AM60, Liu et al. [157] employed the feedback mode of SECM.

2.6. 
Discussion and future research 
The development of Mg-based biomaterials is complicated by the significant issue of 
localized corrosion. The degradation behaviors of scaffolds based on Mg are significantly 
influenced by factors such as microstructures, alloying elements, and surrounding 
physiological environments. The corrosion properties of Mg alloys are significantly impacted 
by the intricate microstructure of each Mg alloy. Therefore, it is imperative to conduct 
separate investigations for different Mg alloys. Meanwhile, the microstructure of Mg 
scaffolds can be influenced by the production method, architecture, and production process. 
 
An area of inquiry involves investigating the potential influence of secondary phases, such as 
intermetallic particles and precipitates, on the promotion of localized corrosion. The 
aforementioned phases have the potential to serve as nucleation sites for corrosion, thereby 
accelerating the process of material degradation. Through comprehension of the genesis and 
conduct of secondary phases, it is possible to develop magnesium-based alloys that exhibit 
enhanced resistance to corrosion. 
 
An additional area for prospective investigation relates to the impact of surface treatment on 
localized corrosion. The application of surface treatments, such as anodization, can induce 
alterations in the surface properties of a material, leading to an enhancement in its resistance 
to corrosion. However, the impact of these treatments on localized corrosion remains 
uncertain. Subsequent research can focus on examining the impact of diverse surface 
treatments on the initiation and propagation of localized corrosion. 
 
It is essential to investigate how the implant environment affects localized corrosion. The 
corrosion behavior of biomaterials made of Mg alloys may be significantly influenced by the 
surrounding physiological environment, including factors like pH, oxygen concentration, and 
ion concentration. Future research should look at how varied physiological environments 
affect the initiation and propagation of localized corrosion.

Future research on localized corrosion in Mg-based scaffolds should concentrate on figuring 
out how secondary phases, surface treatments, and surrounding physiological environments

2.7. 
Conclusions and project objectives 
The following conclusions can be made based on the literature study: 
 
• The degradation properties of Mg-based scaffolds can be affected by AM process and 
AM parameters through the modifications of the microstructures of the scaffolds. 
 
• Microstructures, such as grain size, grain boundaries secondary phase particles and 
micropores, do not only affect degradation process of Mg-based implants alone, but 
also complex interactions between these microstructures occur and simultaneously 
affect the corrosion process. 
 
• The addition of alloying elements, like Ca, Mn, Zn and Sr, increases the corrosion 
resistance of Mg alloys, while the alteration of microstructures accelerates the 
localized corrosion rate. 
 
• Significant progress has been made in monitoring localized corrosion processes of Mg 
alloys with the help of higher resolution scanning electrochemical techniques. 
 
• Distinct ions will have distinct impacts on the corrosion of Mg alloys in in vitro 
immersion tests in SBFs with ion concentrations, and various ions will have different 
effects on the localized corrosion behavior of Mg-based scaffolds. 
 
The following research goals are generated taking into account the research motivation and 
the literature study to better understand the corrosion behavior of two biomaterials in standard 
r-SBF and diluted r-SBF solutions:  
 
• To investigate the electrochemical behavior of Mg-4Zn and pore Mg scaffolds and to

• To confirm the effect of the different concentrations of ions in r-SBF on the corrosion 
behavior of Mg-4Zn scaffolds. 
 
• To adopt localized techniques for corrosion and electrochemical behavior monitoring

Mg-4Zn and pure Mg samples before and after immersion tests in two solutions. 
 
• To characterize the localized corrosion production, corrosion layer and passive layer.

3.2.1. Sample preparation 
All the experimental procedures were carried out on the surface of the samples that had been 
polished to a mirror finish. All scaffolds were embedded in non-conductive clear epoxy resin 
using a mould of 2.5 𝑐𝑚 in diameter by cold mounting. The application of cold mounting was

The cross-sections of each embedded sample were ground using SiC papers (Struers, 
Denmark) of grid size from FEPA 𝑃 # 1200 to FEPA 𝑃 # 4000 on a grinding equipment 
(LaboPol-21, Struers, Denmark). Each paper was applied for 2 minutes. Isopropanol was 
employed as a lubricant during the grinding process in substitute of water to prevent any 
potential corrosion reactions on the cross-section. Following the grinding process, the samples 
got a cleaning step with an isopropanol bath in an ultrasonic cleaner (5510R-MT, Branson, 
US) for 10 minutes. This cleaning step was done to remove any residual particles that may 
have been present on the cross-sections after grinding, thereby reducing the risk of surface 
scratching during the next polishing step. 
 
The cross-sections were polished for 6 minutes using a polishing equipment (LaboPol-5, 
Struers, Denmark), with a 1 𝜇𝑚 polishing suspension (DP-Paste P, Struers, Denmark) applied 
to a circular porous neoprene polishing cloth (MD-Nap, Struers, Denmark), while isopropanol 
was used as a lubricant instead of distilled water. After the polishing step, a mirror-like 
smooth and polished surface was obtained. The sample surface was rinsed with isopropanol 
immediately after polishing to remove the polishing suspension, and then the samples were 
ultrasonically cleaned with the isopropanol bath again for 20 minutes to completely get rid of 
polishing diamonds from the polishing suspension, which could potentially affect the 
following characterization investigations by stuck on the sample surface.

3.2.4. X-ray diffraction 
X-ray diffraction (XRD) technique is a highly effective approach for identifying chemical 
compounds based on their crystalline structure. This method enables the identification of 
various compounds or phases that possess identical compositions. XRD relies on the principle 
of wave interference. When two light waves with identical wavelengths propagate in the same 
direction, their interference can be constructive or destructive, dependent on their phase 
difference. Constructive interference takes place when two waves have a phase difference of 
𝑛𝜆 (where n is an integer) and are said to be inphase. Conversely, completely destructive 
interference occurs when two waves have a phase difference of 𝑛𝜆/2 and are referred to as 
being completely out of phase. 
 
The crystallographic planes of a crystalline solid have the ability to diffract X-ray beams (Fig. 
3-8). Two incident waves, namely beam 1 and beam 2, that are inphase, undergo deflection as 
they interact with two crystal planes, denoted as A and B. The waves that get deflection will 
not exhibit inphase characteristics unless the basic law of diffraction, as shown in Eq. (3.1) is

3.5.4. Ion concentration changes before and after immersion tests 
Inductively coupled plasma-optical emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES) is a valuable analytical 
technique utilized for both qualitative and quantitative analysis of elements present in a given 
sample. The principle of ICP-OES involves the excitation and resulting emission of atoms or 
ions within an inductively coupled plasma (ICP) generator. ICP-OES presents numerous 
benefits such as exceptional sensitivity, an extensive dynamic range, the ability to analyze 
multiple elements, and minimal detection limits. 
 
The ICP-OES procedure starts with the introduction of a liquid sample into the ICP-OES 
instrument via a nebulizer, which transforms the liquid into a finely dispersed aerosol or mist. 
The resulting aerosol, after nebulization, is introduced into an ICP which is generated by the 
application of radio frequency energy to the gas, typically argon, within the welding torch. 
The plasma achieves temperatures within the range of 6,000 𝐾−10,000 𝐾, i.e., 
5,726.85 ℃−9,726.85 ℃, leading to the nebulization and ionization of the liquid sample. 
The atoms and ions in the liquid samples interact with the electrons and other ions in the high-
temperature plasma to be promoted to higher energy states or to become ionized. When atoms 
or ions that have been excited or ionized return to a lower energy state, they produce specific 
light with a particular wavelength. The emitted light of each element is distinctive and 
correlates to electronic transitions that are exclusive to that element. The emitted light is 
collected and sent via a spectrometer, which divides the light into its constituent wavelengths 
using a diffraction grating or another optical component. The intensity of the emitted light at a 
specific wavelength is recorded by a detector, such as a photomultiplier tube or charge-
coupled device (CCD). 
 
The recorded intensities of the emitted light at different wavelengths are converted into 
spectra representing the ion concentrations of the liquid samples. The intensities of the 
emitted light exhibit a proportional relationship with the concentration of the corresponding 
element present in the liquid samples. The acquired spectrum is subjected to comparison with 
calibration curves that have been produced using standard reference materials with established 
elemental concentrations. This methodology facilitates the quantitative determination of the 
elements present in the ions of the liquid samples.

Fourier transform infrared Spectroscopy 
The Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) method is a commonly employed approach in the field 
of vibrational spectroscopy. FTIR spectroscopy is a technique that uses the Fourier transform 
method to acquire an infrared spectrum across a broad range of wave numbers simultaneously. 
FTIR method exhibits a significantly superior signal-to-noise ratio in comparison to the 
dispersive method. 
 
FTIR Spectroscopy enables the determination of the molecular compositions and structures of 
a given specimen. This is achieved by measuring the infrared absorption or emission spectrum 
of the specimen. FTIR spectroscopy operates on the fundamental principle of the interaction 
between infrared radiation and chemical functional compounds present in each sample. Upon 
transmission of infrared light through a specimen, specific wavelengths of light are absorbed 
by the molecules of the sample, thereby inducing vibrational or rotational motion. The 
specific frequencies of these molecular vibrations and rotations are characteristic of the 
chemical functional compounds present on the sample surface. 
 
An FTIR spectrometer emits a wide range of infrared radiation, commonly in the mid-infrared 
range. The infrared light is divided into two separate beams by Michelson interferometer. One 
of the beams serves as the reference beam, while the other beam (the specimen beam) is 
directed toward the surface of the specimen. The specimen is positioned within the trajectory 
of the specimen beam. Upon transmission through the specimen, the infrared radiation 
receives selective absorption by the molecules present on the specimen surface, resulting in a 
reduction in the intensity of the corresponding wavelengths. The reference and specimen 
beams go through disparate trajectories prior to recombination. The recombined beams 
generate a distinct pattern of interference, commonly referred to as an interferogram. The 
interferogram receives a mathematical transformation using the Fourier transform technique, 
thereby converting the time-domain data into a spectrum in the frequency-domain. The 
spectrum obtained illustrates the absorption or emission of infrared radiation by functional 
compounds present on the specimen surface over a range of frequencies. The 
spectrum exhibits distinct peaks that correspond to the various molecular vibrations and 
rotations present on the specimen surface.

3.7.1. Surface topography and surface potential evaluation 
This project used a specialized technique named atomic force microscopy-scanning Kelvin 
probe force microscopy (AFM-SKPFM) to measure the surface topography and Volta 
potential difference (VPD), also known as contact potential difference (CPD). AFM-SKPFM 
is a combination of AFM and Kelvin probe force microscopy (KP-FM). In addition, AFM is a 
surface analysis technique that employs an ultra-small and sharp probe tip, commonly less 
than a diameter of 10 𝑛𝑚 and less than a height of 5 𝑛𝑚 at the apex, located at the end of a 
cantilever, which is subsequently scanned across the surface of interest. During this 
movement, the forces of interaction between the tip and the sample are evaluated. This 
technique is based on the principles of the scanning tunnelling microscope (STM) and the 
stylus profilometer [178]. 
Surface topography 
The standard AFM components of AFM-SKPFM are employed in tapping (intermediate) 
mode to acquire high-resolution topographic images of the detected surface. In tapping mode, 
the cantilever is exposed to an external oscillation at a fixed resonance frequency (𝜔). The 
oscillation amplitude during tapping mode experiences a change due to the alteration of the 
tip-sample interaction as the tip-sample distance varies. The changes in oscillation amplitude, 
in relation to the reference amplitude, are employed as feedback signals to acquire the surface 
topography of the specimen. The tapping mode is commonly denoted as amplitude 
modulation.

When applying the tapping AFM-SKPFM operation mode, the tip-sample distance is stably 
maintained at a predetermined constant. However, the interaction between the tip and sample 
is disrupted by either attractive or repulsive inter-atomic forces, including electrostatic, 
magnetic, van der Walls forces, and dipole-dipole interactions, which leads to a fluctuation in 
the amplitude of the applied oscillation on the AFM cantilever and the tip, as depicted in Fig. 
3-15. In AFM using amplitude modulation mode, alterations in the oscillation amplitude serve 
as the feedback signals for generating topographical maps. As illustrated in Fig. 3-15, the 
oscillation amplitude exhibits an upward trend with an increase in the tip-sample distance, 
which can be attributed to the reduction in tip-sample interaction, and vice versa.

developed to avoid the electron transformation between two distinct materials with a 
corresponding potential difference, commonly referred to as VPD or CPD, as depicted in Fig. 
3-18 (c).

3.7.2. Surface monitoring of electrochemical activities  
Electrochemical activities on a sample surface can be monitored by using scanning

application of a polarizable ultra-micro electrode (UME), that is capable of being polarized

simultaneously observing the electrochemical reactions at specific locations. A UME 
commonly consists of Pt wire enclosed in a glass capillary. The UME is moved across the 
sample surface, which is fixed at a set height, via a 𝑋, 𝑌, 𝑍 positioner. By scanning the tip of

In SECM, a surface under investigation is scanned while faradaic currents flowing through 
the tip are monitored, and there are several operation modes available. In the feedback mode 
(Fig. 3-21), ferrocenemethanol (FcMeOH) or other redox mediators is introduced to the 
electrolytes in order to track how closeness to the studied sample influences the rate of its 
redox conversion at the UME. The substrate may receive either positive or negative feedback 
depending on whether it is conductive or insulating. On insulating surfaces, the current 
travelling through the UME for the redox conversion of FcMeOH diminishes, creating a 
negative feedback effect, due to a geometric barrier to its diffusion. Conversely, when current 
flows across the conductive locations on the sample surface, it might encourage the redox 
conversion of FcMeOH on the sample and result in positive feedback, which increases the 
current in the UME. In the feedback mode of a spontaneously corroding system, it is not 
essential to polarize the investigated substrate with bipotentiostat, because in such case, the 
substrate is under an open circuit condition, where electron transfer processes can still happen 
at the substrate, although Substrate polarization is still a choice rather than a necessity in this 
situation.

4.2.1. OCP 
The shifting trend of the OCP curves of the Mg-4Zn specimens in various electrolytes was

4.2.4. Short discussion 
In summary, it was determined that the corrosion potential and corrosion resistance of Mg-
4Zn specimens surpassed those of pure Mg specimens, and the corrosion rate of Mg-4Zn 
specimens was slower in comparison to that of pure Mg specimens. In solutions containing 
ions of the same type but varying concentrations, the corrosion potential and corrosion 
resistance of a given sample (Mg-4Zn or pure Mg) demonstrated an inverse relationship with 
ion concentrations. Particularly, as ion concentration decreases, corrosion resistance and 
potential will increase, while the corrosion rate will decrease. 
 
The corrosion reactions of both Mg-4Zn and pure Mg scaffolds in standard r-SBF were 
widely recognized. This phenomenon was accompanied by the generation of numerous 
hydrogen bubbles, which significantly interfered with the SECM measurements during 
subsequent monitoring of electrochemical activities on the sample surfaces in standard r-SBF 
[182]. Based on the findings of electrochemical measurements, it was determined that 
the 100-time diluted r-SBF would serve as the optimal electrolyte for conducting following 
SECM measurements aimed at surface monitoring of corrosion activities. Moreover, the 
morphological characteristics and corrosion products of both Mg-4Zn and pure Mg cross-
sections were obtained before and after 1 −ℎ𝑜𝑢𝑟 immersion in standard r-SBF, 100-time 
diluted r-SBF and UPW, while the topography and VPD of the sample surfaces were 
measured using AFM-SKPFM before and after immersion tests in 100-time diluted r-SBF.

4.3.4. Short discussion 
The claims presented in subsection 4.2.4 were further supported by the analysis obtained from 
in vitro immersion tests. The corrosion potential (𝐸𝑐𝑜𝑟𝑟) and corrosion resistance of specimens 
containing Mg-4Zn exhibited superior characteristics in comparison to those composed of 
pure Mg. Additionally, the corrosion rate of samples containing Mg-4Zn was comparatively 
lower than that of samples composed solely of Mg. The corrosion potential (𝐸𝑐𝑜𝑟𝑟)  and 
corrosion resistance of a given sample, specifically Mg-4Zn or pure Mg, in solutions 
containing ions of the same type but varying ion concentrations exhibited an inverse 
relationship with the ion concentrations. 
 
 Furthermore, it was concluded that within the duration of 1 −ℎ𝑜𝑢𝑟 in vitro immersion tests, 
Mg-4Zn and pure Mg samples were immersed in aqueous environments, whereby Mg 
component was susceptible to corrosion reactions, while Zn remains unaffected. The 
explanations for this phenomenon could be attributed to the fact that while the addition of Zn 
in Mg-4Zn scaffolds enhances their general corrosion resistance, the nobler corrosion 
potential of Zn compared to Mg results in the acceleration of Mg corrosion as an anode 
through galvanic corrosion. During the initial stage of corrosion or degradation within a span 
of one hour, only Mg experiences corrosion attacks. 
 
Corrosion reactions were observed on the surfaces of both Mg-4Zn and pure Mg samples, 
accompanied by the generation of a significant quantity of hydrogen bubbles, regardless of 
the types of buffered solution employed. The presence of hydrogen bubbles is likely to 
significantly hinder the SECM measurements during the following monitoring of the 
electrochemical activities on the sample surfaces (both Mg-4Zn and pure Mg samples). The 
optimal electrolyte for monitoring in situ surface corrosion activities of the Mg-4Zn and pure 
Mg samples in vivo was determined based on the analysis of in vitro immersion tests. It was 
found that a 100-time dilution r-SBF would be suitable for subsequent SECM measurements, 
aimed at observing the localized corrosion behavior on the surface of the scaffolds in the 
solution with the same ions as human body fluid. In support of the statement made in 
subsection 4.2.4, the surface topography and VPD were assessed through the utilization of 
AFM-SKPFM both prior to and subsequent to 1 −ℎ𝑜𝑢𝑟 in vitro immersion conducted in 
100-time diluted r-SBF.

4.5.2. Short discussion 
In the measurements of AFM-SKPFM, the grain boundaries and IMPs on the surface of Mg-
4Zn scaffolds were observed, and the grain boundaries were 28 ± 10 𝑛𝑚 below the sample 
surface, while the IMPs were 150 ± 100 𝑛𝑚 above the sample surface. However, on the 
surface of pure Mg scaffolds, the grain boundaries were observed to be 30 ± 10 𝑛𝑚 below 
the sample surface. 
 
During the 1 −ℎ𝑜𝑢𝑟 in vitro immersion of Mg-4Zn samples in 100-time diluted r-SBF, 
galvanic corrosion occurred near the IMPs on the surface of the Mg-4Zn sample, and the 
IMPs served as cathodes in this process. Some IMPs were covered by a corrosion layer as a 
result of the slow accumulation of corrosion products, but the corrosion layer that covered this 
portion of the IMPs was unstable, so the covered IMPs might turn into the locations of pitting 
corrosion. The other portion of the IMPs that were not shielded by the corrosion layer 
was still used as the cathodes in galvanic corrosion to accelerate the rate of local corrosion. 
During the immersion, IGC and corrosion products formed simultaneously in the grain 
boundaries of the Mg-4Zn surface and at proximity to the grain boundaries. Finally, the grain 
boundaries were entirely covered by the corrosion layer created by the formation of corrosion 
products, and the corrosion layer that was developed nearby kept spreading further. 
Additionally, it was discovered that the intersection of the IGC- and grain-surface-
generated corrosion layers were where pitting corrosion might occur. Galvanic corrosion and 
IGC both increased the localized corrosion rate when IMPs were dispersed along the grain 
boundaries, however, in this case, it was hypothesized that the impact of IGC on the 
localized corrosion rate was more severe than that of galvanic corrosion. Micropores on Mg-
4Zn surface were the site of crevice corrosion. 
 
During the 1 −ℎ𝑜𝑢𝑟 in vitro immersion of pure Mg samples in 100-time diluted r-SBF, IGC 
and corrosion products were formed in the grain boundaries of the pure Mg surfaces, as well 
as in the immediate proximity to the grain boundaries. Ultimately, the grain boundaries 
were entirely enveloped by the corrosion layer resulting from the accumulation of corrosion 
products. Furthermore, the corrosion layer that developed in close proximity to the grain 
boundaries continued in its expansion outward. Like the phenomenon observed on the Mg-
4Zn surface, it was also discovered on pure Mg surface that the intersection of the IGC- and 
grain-surface-generated corrosion layers were where pitting corrosion might occur. 
Simultaneously, the locations where pitting corrosion occurred appeared an uneven 
distribution across the pure Mg surface. Furthermore, regions in proximity to 
the micropores were susceptible to crevice corrosion. 
 
The topographical and VPD maps of the sample surfaces after 1 −ℎ𝑜𝑢𝑟 in vitro immersion in 
100-time diluted r-SBF proved the aforementioned points in this subsection. In addition, the 
sample surfaces of both Mg-4Zn and pure Mg scaffolds exhibited irregularly distributed sites 
of pitting corrosion.

To summarize, it was concluded that the presence of grain boundaries on Mg-4Zn surfaces, 
which were considered crystal defects, accelerated localized corrosion. IMPs (𝑀𝑔𝑍𝑛2) on 
Mg-4Zn surfaces induced galvanic corrosion, which also increased localized corrosion, as 
clearly represented in Fig. 4-25 (a-b). Furthermore, the locations where pitting corrosion 
happens were non-uniformly distributed on Mg-4Zn surfaces, thereby enhancing the 
surface roughness and accelerating localized corrosion. The localized corrosion of the Mg-
4Zn surfaces was further accelerated by the crevice corrosion near the micropores on the Mg-
4Zn surfaces. 
 
On the other hand, the microstructure-induced localized corrosion phenomena observed on 
the Mg-4Zn surface were also observed on pure Mg surfaces. However, there was no any IMP 
on pure Mg surfaces that resulted in galvanic corrosion. The crystal defects present in pure 
Mg surfaces, specifically the grain boundaries, had the potential to increase localized 
corrosion, as shown in Fig. 4-25 (c-d). Furthermore, the locations where pitting corrosion 
appears were irregularly dispersed across the Mg surfaces. This phenomenon worsened the 
surface roughness of pure Mg scaffolds and accelerated localized corrosion. The localized 
corrosion was worsened by the crevice corrosion that happened in close proximity to the 
micropores on the pure Mg surfaces.

During the 1 −ℎ𝑜𝑢𝑟 in vitro immersion in 100-time diluted r-SBF, while the overall 
thickness of the corrosion layers on the sample surfaces (both Mg-4Zn and pure Mg 
scaffolds) were observed to be on the rise, the localized corrosion layers were likely 
to experience generation, degradation, reduction, cracking, thickening, and regeneration. 
Furthermore, it was noticed that the corrosion layers on pure Mg surfaces were comparatively 
thinner than that on the Mg-4Zn surfaces. Additionally, the surface roughness of the corrosion 
layers on pure Mg surfaces was inferior to that of the corrosion layers on the Mg-4Zn surfaces. 
Consequently, this phenomenon resulted in an accelerated corrosion rate of the pure 
Mg surfaces in comparison to the Mg-4Zn surfaces. 
 
The occurrence of crevice corrosion was observed at the interface of the struts and the 
resin on the surface of both pure Mg and Mg-4Zn samples. Specifically, this phenomenon 
occurred at the interface of struts and resin on the sample surface, resulting in a localized 
corrosion rate that was higher at the interface of struts and resin than at the center of struts. 
Moreover, due to the irregular shape of the interface between the struts and resin, which 
was characterized by an irregular curve and unique interfaces, crevice corrosion was probably 
to occur at the interfaces of the identical strut and resin. The extent of crevice corrosion, 
which occurred at the interface of the identical strut and resin, also exhibited variations.

Conclusion 
CHAPTER 5 
5 CONCLUSION 
The present investigation explored the assessment of localized corrosion behaviors on the 
cross-sections of biodegradable Mg-4Zn and pure Mg scaffolds before and after 1-hour in 
vitro immersion in aqueous environments. These scaffolds were fabricated using extrusion-
based AM technique and were subjected to post-AM debinding and sintering. This study 
indicated the first case in point of such an analysis. The present study comprehensively 
investigated the localized and overall corrosion behaviors on the cross-sections of Mg-4Zn 
scaffolds in comparison to pure Mg scaffolds in various aqueous environments. The objective 
of this investigation was to evaluate the impact of the microstructures on the localized 
corrosion behaviors of Mg-4Zn scaffolds as a biodegradable bone implant. 
 
Based on the analysis in this project, it is possible to draw the following conclusions: 
 
- 
During the in vitro immersion in the same liquid, the Mg-4Zn scaffolds demonstrate 
enhanced overall corrosion resistance when compared to the pure Mg scaffolds. 
 
- 
When exposing the same material (Mg-4Zn or pure Mg scaffolds) to varying liquid 
environments for a duration of 1 hour, with identical ion types and pH levels, the 
corrosion rate of the material increased proportionally with the concentration of ions 
present in the liquid. 
 
- 
After the in vitro immersion, irrespective of the immersion liquid applied, the cross-
sections of the samples (both Mg-4Zn and pure Mg) exhibited solely magnesium 
oxide (𝑀𝑔𝑂) and magnesium hydroxide 𝑀𝑔(𝑂𝐻)2 corrosion products. In other words, 
the corrosion layer exclusively comprised magnesium oxide (𝑀𝑔𝑂) and magnesium 
hydroxide 𝑀𝑔(𝑂𝐻)2. 
 
- 
The presence of the microstructures, such as IMPs, grain boundaries and micropores 
accelerate the localized corrosion rate, while the addition of Zn in Mg-4Zn specimens 
increases the corrosion resistance compared to the corrosion resistance of pure Mg 
specimens. 
 
- 
Grain boundaries in the cross-sections of Mg-4Zn and pure Mg scaffolds take on the 
role of crystal defects during localized corrosion due to the occurrence of IGC inside 
the grain boundaries and in close proximity to the grain boundaries.  
 
- 
The overall corrosion resistance of the Mg-4Zn scaffolds is higher than that of pure 
Mg scaffolds thanks to the refinement of the grains, but due to the refinement of the 
grains, the grain boundaries in the cross-sections of the Mg-4Zn specimens become

more and denser. In turn, compared to pure Mg scaffolds, a Mg-4Zn scaffold has a 
greater localized corrosion rate. 
 
- 
In the cross-sections of the Mg-4Zn specimens, galvanic corrosion occurs close to the 
IMPs, and during this process, the IMPs serve as cathodes to speed up the local 
corrosion rate of the Mg-4Zn scaffolds. 
 
- 
The locations where pitting corrosion happens exhibit an uneven distribution

- 
The passive layer formed on the Mg-4Zn surface in an aqueous environment is thicker 
and smoother than the passive layer formed on the pure Mg surface in the same 
aqueous environment. Therefore, compared to pure Mg scaffolds, the overall corrosion 
rate of Mg-4Zn scaffolds in liquid environments is lower. 
 
- 
The duration of the passive layer cycle on the Mg-4Zn cross-sections, which involves 
processes such as generation, degradation, reduction, cracking, thickening, and 
regeneration, is comparatively longer than that of the pure Mg cross-sections. Thus, 
the overall corrosion resistance of Mg-4Zn scaffolds is greater than the pure Mg 
scaffolds in a liquid environment. 
 
- 
The phenomenon of crevice corrosion is observed appearing at the borders of struts, 
leading to a raised localized corrosion rate in the borders of both Mg-4Zn and pure 
Mg struts, as compared to the localized corrosion rate at the center of the struts.

Research recommendations 
CHAPTER 6 
6 RESEARCH 
RECOMMENDATIONS 
According to the findings of this study, the limited scope of the research makes it not enough 
for achieving a comprehensive comprehension of the localized corrosion characteristics 
exhibited by Mg-4Zn and pure Mg scaffolds. Some future research recommendations are 
proposed for the continuous studying of this topic: 
 
- 
Analysis about the localized corrosion behavior on the Mg-4Zn and pure Mg scaffolds 
with longer in vitro immersion time in various liquids at 37 ℃. 
 
- 
Individual contribution of each ion in the standard r-SBF to corrosion behavior of Mg-
4Zn and pure Mg scaffolds at 37 ℃. 
 
- 
In vitro immersion tests at 37 ℃ of Mg-4Zn and pure scaffolds in blood plasma 
mimicking with proteins. 
 
- 
In-situ surface monitoring of electrochemical activities on the Mg-4Zn and pure Mg 
surfaces using SECM technique with a smaller scanning step and a thinner UME, such 
as a diameter of 1 𝜇𝑚. 
 
- 
The determination of the volume distribution of IMPs in the Mg-4Zn surface and the 
effect of the volume distribution on the localized corrosion characteristics. 
 
- 
Investigation of the corrosion characteristics on the Mg-4Zn and pure Mg scaffolds in 
the liquid circulations at 37 ℃. 
 
- 
The assessment with the thickness evolution of the corrosion layers on the Mg-4Zn 
and pure scaffolds. 
 
- 
Enhancement of biodegradation in extrusion-based AM Mg-Zn and pure Mg scaffolds 
using coatings on the scaffolds and within the strut spacing and micropores 
 
- 
Introduction of bioceramics or biopolymers to Mg-4Zn and pure Mg scaffolds to 
further improve their biodegradation for orthopedic and clinical applications.
