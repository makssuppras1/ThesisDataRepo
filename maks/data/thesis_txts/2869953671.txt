Abstract
Keystroke dynamics authentication is a form of authentication that relies on a subject’s
unique typing rhythm and patterns. Traditionally, temporal features, such as hold time
for specific keys, have been used by researchers exploring the topic, aiming to achieve
the best performance among a handful of classifiers. However, many other non­temporal
features can be extracted from a user’s interaction with their keyboard and computer.
While several studies suggest that features like the side preference of a Shift key or error
correcting frequency are beneficial for increasing classifier accuracy, different keystroke
dynamics features can be interdependent, providing the most value in combination with
temporal features. The most eventful combinations can furthermore depend on a chosen
classifier, as well as the type of text used to collect the features. This thesis aims to
expand the understanding of what feature combinations are the most beneficial in several
different contexts, given combinations of temporal and less­used, non­temporal features,
including a feature that captures human­computer interaction.

1
Introduction
Keystroke dynamics ­ based authentication emerged as a concept around the 1980s,
with articles such as ”Individual Differences in the Keystroke Patterns of Touch Typists”
by T.R. Gaines et al. (1980). Since then, keystroke typing data has been recognised
as displaying unique qualities attributed to the individual typing. Later articles exploring
this emerging research area, such as ”Typing Patterns: A Key to User Identification” by
A. D. J. Roth(1984), explored the phenomenon of keystroke dynamics more precisely for
the practical uses of authentication and security systems. Since then, the concept has
branched out into a significant research area.
While the potential of practical keystroke dynamics authentication systems has been im­
proved dramatically through advancements in machine learning and an expanding avail­
ability of behavioral metrics since the concept originated, its challenges are still prevalent,
making it a better option for hybrid or multi­factor authentication, rather than a stand­alone
authentication mechanism.
One of the most significant challenges this concept is faced with can be seen as char­
acteristic of most means of biometric authetication ­ insufficient authentication accuracy
and performance. The nature of identifying a user via their typing pattern relies on that
typing pattern being unique and consistent enough for it to be distinguishable from other
individuals’ typing behavior. External influences, such as emotional states, inebriation
or changes in energy levels, can alter typing behavior. Most people’s typing patterns are
prone to changing with age or other external influences like injuries. With typing behaviors
naturally evolving over time, initially collected typing metrics associated with an individ­
ual can become relatively obsolete for determining the identity of a user. Furthermore,
differences in hardware used for data collection pose a significant challenge in what data
can be used for evaluating a user’s identity. The choice of a keyboard type or a device,
including tablets and touchscreen devices, can introduce noteworthy differences in typing
patterns.
Despite these weaknesses, keystroke dynamics authentication is quite a lightweight and
cheap authentication method, as the typing data can be collected without specific hard­
ware, making this approach affordable, easily implemented and giving it a wide reach over
a multitude of devices.
In recent years, a high level of attention and research effort has been dedicated to the
development and performance evaluation of classifying algorithms that predict the user’s
identity. However, a large amount of research is performed using publically available
datasets limited to temporal features extracted from typing patterns ­ a much smaller
amount of works investigate non­temporal feature impact on authentication.
A multitude of non­temporal and statistical typing features can be derived from records
of typing behaviour, provided the choice of typed text and the environment allows that.
When combined with temporal features, these alternative features can improve authenti­
cation accuracy to varying degrees. For example, features like average typing speed or
preference for the side on which a person chooses to click a modifier key while capital­
ising a letter, can reveal a lot about a particular person’s typing habits. Combined with

2
Problem statement
While many relevant contemporary academic articles explore keystroke dynamics authen­
tication in an effort to determine the conditions for highest authentication accuracy, the
features and their combinations are usually limited to classic temporal features. Most re­
search papers use robust existing databases(discussed in chapter ”Establishing a feature
database”), as they offer a benefit of readily available data. In addition, these databases
contain a large amount of samples. The focus is often placed on selecting the best per­
forming classifying algorithm with respect to the features that can be derived from these
databases.
It is recognised that different classifying algorithms will vary in accuracy with respect to
specific feature combinations in the supplied data. A comparatively small amount of arti­
cles are based on the premise of having the resources to test how more obscure features,
such as key preference or individual typing speed, can affect the accuracy.
The objective of this thesis is to explore how temporal and non­temporal feature combina­
tions can affect the accuracy of most popular machine learning classifiers, and whether
or not it is beneficial for authentication purposes to include them in the evaluation.
2.0.1
Research questions and objectives
The research questions of this thesis are stated as follows:
• What are the best temporal and alternative keystroke dynamics feature combina­
tions for different types of text?
• Can alternative features be as impactful or even more impactful than temporal fea­
tures in best feature combinations accross different classifiers?
• What are the best temporal and alternative keystroke dynamics feature combina­
tions for popular machine learning classifiers, like SVM, Random Forrest and K­
nearest neighbors?
• What features are the least beneficial for different text lengths, types and classifiers?
• How significant is the impact of pairing alternative and temporal features depending
on the length of typed text and chosen classifier?
• In case the performance of feature combinations varies across different text types
and classifiers, what could be the reasons behind the varied performance?
In an effort to answer these questions, the following goals will be satisfied:
• Collect a database containing traditional temporal typing features (Up­Down time,
Down­Up time, Down­Down time, Up­Up time, trigraph) and lesser explored non­
temporal features (shift­key location preference, typing speed, typing accuracy, re­
action time to visual triggers before typing, statistical features).
• Explore a variety of temporal and non­temporal combinations of features in terms
of authentication accuracy, precision, recall, f­1 score.

3
Background research
Background research in related scientific articles serves as an entry point into this project,
between selecting appropriate classifiers and awareness of features investigated by pre­
vious scientific initiatives, to achieve a high reach and connect suggested findings.
In order to get a good grasp on the state­of­the­art techniques and details, research ar­
ticles were collected in two phases ­ the first one being recent literature reviews, and
the second one ­ articles related to exploring the viability of keystroke dynamics features,
specifically. Both Google Scholar and DTU FindIt were used to find the majority of relevant
articles. However, since in general non­traditional keystroke dynamics features and their
impact is relatively sparsely covered by available literature accessible through search en­
gines, certain articles were sourced via the references in literature reviews. In both cases,
articles based on devices that do not have a traditional keyboard were not considered,
with words like ’smartphone’, ’android’, ’touch­screen’ or ’mobile’ being excluded from
the search. The terms were matched by appearance anywhere in the article for Google
Scholar, and by title in DTU FindIt. The first 25 results for feature­specific search were
selected for each search engine. For literature reviews, the first 5 papers were selected in
the same way. During the selection process, the articles were first pruned out by their ab­
stract, and after that, by the introduction. If either pointed to the article not being relevant
for the paper, the paper was removed from further evaluation. Furthermore, only articles
written less than 10 years ago were evaluated. For literature reviews, only articles from
the last 5 years were taken into consideration. A table showing all evaluated articles for al­
ternative feature purposes can be seen in the appendix, figure A.1. Of 50 articles, 13 were
relevant for alternative keystroke dynamics feature investigation purposes, discussed in
this section. It is to be mentioned that the articles that did not get selected were not re­
lated to this topic precisely, but still provided insights into other essential parts concerning
this project ­ mainly feature selection and methods, discrepancies of keystroke dynam­
ics, as well as establishing a keystroke dynamics database. However, the main theme
addressed by the relevant articles had to be related to exploring alternative keystroke
dynamics features or their combinations, introducing new types of features or comparing
feature combinations.
For articles sourced on Google Scholar, the search criteria consisted of including the
phrase ’keystroke dynamics features’ as well as at least one of the following words:
”typing” ”keyboard” ”non traditional” ”alternative” ”non conventional” ”selection” ”useful­
ness”, and excluding words like ”android” ”touch screen” ”smartphone” and ”mobile”. For
DTU FindIt, the advanced search was subject to the following: title:(alternative OR non­
traditional OR non­conventional OR ”usefulness of” OR ”selection of”) AND title:(”keystroke
dynamics” OR typing OR keyboard OR keystroke) AND title:features.
For sourcing literature reviews, Google Scholar was used under the terms ”keystroke
dynamics reviews”, and the first 5 results were taken into consideration. There was no
filtering for the latter, considering the reviews would broadly target keystroke dynamics, in
an effort to derive the current coverage of different features, classifier trends and relation,
and similar.

3.1
Previous research into the topic
A 2022 literature review[1] by Soumen Roy and colleagues, based on 127 high quality
research articles within the area of keystroke dynamics authentication, shows that a nearly
61% of research articles were conducted with only temporal features being explored in
the classification process, while combined features accounted for 25% of all reviewed
articles.
Figure 3.1: A figure taken from a comprehensive literature review[1] of Keystroke Dy­
namics authentication articles shows research tendencies and characteristics, such as
classifiers, features and text types used to collect data
A similar literature review by Tsvetkova and Bakhteev [2] investigates keystroke dynam­
ics features in the context of forensics, and as a part of the investigation, identifies the
most commonly referenced features from over 600 papers on keystroke dynamics au­
thentication. According to this literature review, non­temporal features like input speed
get mentioned 64% of the time, while characteristic overlaps associated with temporal
features (such as negative UU time) are mentioned 62% of the time. However, other
non­temporal features get mentioned significantly less ­ frequency of error is mentioned
in only 19% of articles, use of service keys ­ 15%, n­graphs (for example, trigraphs) 12
%, with other statistical features and pressure force mentioned even less than 10%.
While keystroke dynamics is a well­researched topic, comparatively few studies investi­
gate non­traditional typing feature impact for classification accuracy.
A study conducted in 2017 [3] by Alsultan and Warwick and Wei investigated non­temporal
features like average typing speed, the percentage of negative UD and UU times, shift
key side preference, as well as text editing behaviors in users (such as the keys used for
error correcting). The number of left Shift keys pressed before the capitalised letter (LSB),
negative UD and negative UU time rate, along with the number of CapsLock keys had a
postive weight in decision trees. LSB and negative UD time rate were significant to the
performance of both decision trees and SVMs. This study was carried out on a private
database collected for the research involving 30 individuals. A total of 8 typing tasks were
carried out on user’s own devices, and consisted of freely typed (transcribed) text. This is
in contrast to many studies where tasks were limited to a relatively short text, like a strong
password, that is repeated a number of times.

A study established in the consecutive year [4] by the same authors further investigates
combinations of the aforementioned features and traditional timing features. The study
is conducted in the same setting (only free­typing based on transcribing text), using Ant
Colony Optimisation for feature selection with SVMs, as well as decision trees, once again
suggesting an improvement when non­traditional features are included.
A research article by Nataasha Raul and colleagues [5] Investigates traditional temporal
features like UU, DU, UD, UU and usage of Caps Lock, left and right Shift Keys, as well as
number key preference ­ whether the users use a numpad at the top of the keyboard, or
a dedicated numpad on the right ­ Implying that the research is carried out on a keyboard
that has that arrangement, likely not a laptop keyboard. The data set was built from the
records of 30 users, with each typing in a secure password ’India@2018’ 15 times in a
row, meaning that the reserach only addressed users typing in a fixed complex text in one
session. Performance was evaluated with classifiers like the Gaussian mixture model,
SVMs, Random forest algorithms and a linear regression algorithm. The features were
evaluated both as stand­alone and in combinations ­ temporal features, non­temporal
features, a 40% to 60% of non­temporal and temporal division and vice versa. Two types
of features were trained using different algorithms. The highest classification accuracy
achieved was 91%, with 40% of non­temporal and 60% of temporal features combined.
However, this study is not very specific in terms of what combinations of features are
generally the best for each classifier.
A 2018 study by Junhong Kim and colleagues [6] investigated free­typing from over 150
users in English and Korean. The researches extracted data from press and release
events to derive traditional temporal keystroke dynamics features like hold time, DD, UU,
UD and DU time. However, the study emphasises the importance of relative typing speeds
for all digraphs.
Another article, produced in 2020 by Blaine Ayotte et al. [7] investigates how useful tradi­
tional temporal features like hold time, UU, DD, UD an DU times are to the random forest
classifier concerning free­typing problems. While no non­temporal features or combina­
tions are investigated in this research, it nevertheless makes an important insight into how
different temporal features are not equivalent in usefulness for classification ­ something
that will be further relevant to this research. The paper shows that UU time is the most
useful feature in this context when more than 50 character samples are evaluated, and
hold­time is the most relevant characteristic if the sample size is less than that, while UD is
the least useful feature regardless of sample size. When it comes to combinations of tem­
poral and non­temporal features, this may be an important factor to consider ­ whether of
the best performing single temporal features retain their dominance when combined with
non­temporal features.
Another 2018 paper by Agata Kolakowska [8] investigated how useful selected keystroke
dynamics features are for recognising emotional states of participants. The research is
based on free­typing text, and assesses the importance of hold times for single keys, as
well as digraphs and trigraphs as well as number of events for digraphs and trigraphs and
frequency of using keys like backspace, enter, space bar or delete. Furthermore, stan­
dard deviation and means of temporal features were included in the evaluation. The study
concludes that after feature selection, generally speaking the best subset of features for
evaluating emotional states consists of a combination of dwell time, digraph and trigraph
means and standard deviations, as well as the frequency of using the delete button, while
acknowledging that the metrics are best tuned on an individual basis to determine emo­
tional states. Interestingly, the frequency of other special keys has not made it into the

final selection. This study is significant to consider, because it focuses on deriving the
best subset of features for the classification purpose, considering the inter­dependencies
between different features, unlike most other similar studies. In this research, a similar
approach will be taken, considering the goal is to derive the best combinations of features
for classification purposes.
A 2018 by Jiacang Ho and Dae­Ki Kang article [9] investigates a lesser ­ used Naive
Bayes algorithm, on a dataset containing subsets of temporal features, such as hold time
and UD time, as well as some statistical values. In the paper, it is acknowledged that for
the selected algorithm, separation of sub­datasets results in a higher performance than
a dataset that merges both values. The takeaway from this research is that depending
on the structure of a selected classifier, processing pure features may result in a better
performance than combining features, even when both features are temporal, and would
intuitively increase authentication accuracy when processed as combined.
A paper by Zahid Syed and colleagues [10] introduces the concept of event sequencing
when building feature vectors for keystroke dynamics authentication, and acknowledges
that incorporating features like Shift key side preference can improve classification ac­
curacy while combinations of temporal and non­temporal features render higher authen­
tication accuracy with shorter feature vectors. This contributes to reduced computation
complexity and escaping a potentially high dimensionality ensuing from temporal features,
which can lead to a phenomenon known as curse of dimensionality, leading to reduced
performance in some classifiers.
A relatively new 2023 paper by Yutong Shi et al. [11] combines traditional keystroke dy­
namics temporal features (H, DU, DD, UD, UU) with mouse dynamics, such as press and
release time, movement speed per movement direction, type of actions and similar, ex­
panding the traditional approach with a new dimension through a set of new behavioural
features. Both feature types are mapped to the same subspace, and evaluated against
a number of popular classifiers, including K­NN, SVM, Decision Trees and Neural Net­
works. Fused feature vectors provide a favorable result at around 89% accuracy with
their own authentication adaptation network. This research shows that combining a user’s
keystroke patterns with non­typing human­computer interaction features can be a good
strategy for keystroke dynamics authentication systems.
The study published in 2016 by Hayreddin Ceker and Shambhu Upadhyaya [12] relied
on flight and dwell times of 6 most popular digraphs (he, re, th, an, to, er) ­ 34 partici­
pants were asked to answer a survey of questions and transcribe a speech, among a few
other tasks. Each task required at least 500 characters to complete, and digraphs with a
frequency of less than 50 were excluded, along with digraphs with a latency surpassing
200ms. This strategy suggests that it would be more efficient to correctly classify a user
based on the most common letter sequences ­ given enough samples. SVM is used as
the classifier, resulting in good performance.
Finally, a study conducted by Ioannis Tsimperidis and colleagues [13] attempted to inves­
tigate which features, including temporal features and non­temporal features like error
correction, are the best for simultaneously determining the age and gender of a subject.
While the results as to which type of features were the optimal choice were inconclu­
sive, the paper highlights that there is a significant benefit in combining different types of
features for a higher classification accuracy.
Overall, the state­of­the art related to this topic shows that while some viability studies
have been conducted, there is a lack in coverage towards more specific aspects of the

4.1.1
GREYC2009
The GREYC2009 database[14][15] was established in 2009 to create a benchmark for
keystroke dynamics authentication databases and investigating classifying algorithms.
To collect the raw data, test subjects were supposed to interact with GreyC software,
specifically created for this purpose. Over 133 individuals participated in the research,
typing the phrase ”greyc laboratory” between 6 and 107 times. On average, the users
provided 51 inputs with most attending at least 5 sessions. The raw data consisted of
press and release events with recorded timestamps, and the derived features stored in
the database are release/release, press/press, press/release, and release/press of the
same key or two consecutive keys. The users were not allowed to make errors during the
typing process ­ if a mistake was made, the password had to be re­typed.

4.1.2
LOY2004
A study of keystroke dynamics authentication[16] was conducted in order to investigate
keystroke pressure on a specialised keyboard as a feature and it’s impact on authentica­
tion accuracy. A database of 10 user profiles, containing 10 samples of keystroke latency
and keyboard pressure instances was established, with the users typing in a password
’try4­mbs’ during each repetition. The process of collecting data included the users typ­
ing a complex password into an authentication system a total of 15 times. However, this
study emphasised the importance of users being able to type in the password naturally,
resulting in only the first 5 repetitions being discarded. For evaluating classifier accuracy,
statistical features like minimum, maximum and average values, kurtosis and skewness
were derived from both the derived latency and pressure measurements.
4.1.3
DSN2009
The DNS2009 database [17] [18] was established as a part of a research paper investigat­
ing anomaly detection algorithm performance, and is used by many contemporary works
examining keystroke dynamics classification. The database contains records from 51 in­
dividuals typing in a secure password ”.tie5Roanl” over 8 sessions. Traditionally derived
temporal features like hold times, DD and UD times are derived from the data.
4.1.4
BIOCHAVES
The Biochaves dataset was established for a paper centered around Down­Down interval
histogram equalisation. This dataset contains data from 47 individuals who have com­
pleted four typing tasks, including typing simple phrases like ‘chocolate, zebra, banana,
taxi’ by proficient and non­proficient typers, and portuguese phrases like ‘computador cal­
cula’, as well as a free­typing excerse, on a keyboard that had a brazillian layout. In total,
47 people participated in data collection 10 and 8 for the first tasks, and 14 and 15 in the
last two tasks ­ meaning that there still was a relatively small amount people to provide
data per task. The first three tasks had a total of 10 repetitions of the same phrase/word
each, collected over two sessions. The free­text excercise was repeated 10 times in a row
­ and each free text row contained 110 characters. Similarly to the GREYC2009 database,
for static text typing, samples with error corrections (backspace or delete buttons) were
not accepted in order to reduce the amount of bad samples with irregular typing. However,
error correction was allowed in the free typing exercise.
4.1.5
Clarkson
The Clarkson university database[19] was established to support the development of
Keystroke Dynamics Authentication. 39 users completed two sessions over the span
of 11 months. Each session consisted of typing fixed texts of varying complexity (yesno­
maybe, bahaNe312!, ballzonecart), free­typing excercises where the users had to answer
questions, along with transcribing a text that was displayed to them. Apart from traditional
temporal features like latencies, digraphs and trigraphs, the users’ facial expressions and
hand movements were recorded during the study.

Factors affecting typing
While many works have discussed that varying energy levels in users can affect temporal
keystroke dynamics features, it’s important to discuss how similar circumstances could
also affect the extracted non­temporal features.
Firstly, while the software introduces several different phrases to be typed, each session
contains 10 repetitions per task. As the user types more of the same phrase, they become
more proficient in typing it, which could result in, for example, a lower error correction rate,
or a faster typing speed due to impatience in repetitions. However, the hypothesis is that
these factors would eventually equalize within the user’s typing pattern. To derive the
averages of these metrics and remove outliers, the metrics will be normalised under the
z­score metric ­ missing metrics will be filled with the average values of values within the
z score range.
Furthermore, features like reaction time are experimental, and this work aims to explore
whether they can be consistent enough to be valuable for classification. If one’s reaction
time could enhance authentication value, it will be worthwhile to investigate wether this
feature could be valuable for continuous authentication ­ reaction times of the user inter­
acting with a particular software interface could be adapted to be variable during different
times of the day, for example.

5
Classification
In keystroke dynamics authentication, machine learning problems can assume several
different formats, namely identification can be approached as one­class or multi­class
classification problem.
The concept of one class classification is based on evaluating one group of samples
(in this case, typing records of a particular user), labeled as ’true’ or 1, against a group
of other samples (belonging to other users), labeled as ’false’ or 0. Certain classifiers,
such as support vector machines, are inherently designed to solve binary classification
problems. In a dataset that has more than two classes (in our case, more than two users),
these classifiers can be adapted to work by evaluating two sub­sets of user records as a
binary classification problem. For example, in a dataset of users A, B and C, the problem
could be broken down into evaluating user A against user B, user A against user C, and
user B against user C.
Multi­class classification can adhere to a one­vs­one (OvO) or a one­vs­rest (OvR) paradigm.
In the case of OvO, all class pairs are evaluated, while in OvR, every class gets evaluated
against another class in binary sub­problems. In a dataset of users A, B and C, an OvO
approach would include comparisons of pairs (A,B), (B,C), (A,C), while an OvA approach
would lead to an evaluation of A against B and C, B against A and C, and C against A
and B.
OvO is an approach that will produce N(N­1)/2 classifiers from a dataset of N classes,
compared to OvR producing N classifiers for N classes, therefore making the latter com­
putationally less taxing. However, in an OvR approach, a set of ’positive’ examples will
be compared to all negative examples, of which there will be vastly more, leading to an
imbalanced problem and poorer classification boundaries as a result. Considering that
the newly derived dataset only contains 24 classes of subjects, and some subjects will
have less samples than others, it is more appropriate to aim for the one­vs­one approach.
Here the priority for classification is precision, while the computational resources are not
a crucial consideration due to a small dataset size ­ however, this can vary based on the
classifier and possible modifications by tuning.
5.1
Classifiers
A crucial part of any keystroke dynamics authentication system is a machine learning
classifier. The final accuracy metrics of a system can significantly vary depending on
the chosen classifier. Referring back to figure 3.1, some of the most popular classifier
types include tree­based classifiers like random forest, support vector machines, Neural
Networks and distance­based classifiers. Each classifier type operates differently, and
is best suited for particular set ups, and with each classifier, there are side effects to be
aware of, discussed in this section.

5.1.1
Random forest
Random forest classifiers are similar decision tree, and work by combining results from
multiple decision trees made of random subsets of the dataset throughout the classifica­
tion process. The predictions from these individual trees are averaged to derive a final
classification result (a process also known as majority voting).
This classifier type is sensitive to high data dimensionality when features in a feature vec­
tor introduce noise. Feature vectors would most likely have to be filtered through feature
selection methods to achieve optimal results. However, in general, a high dimensionality
benefits this algorithm, as decision trees need to have a sufficient amount of data to be
distinctive enough in a keystroke dynamics dataset. Furthermore, while a risk of overfit­
ting is smaller in random forest in comparison to regular decision trees, it is still a factor
to consider when fine­tuning the depth of sub­trees. Because this classifier is based on
decision trees, features that are more cardinal in nature would likely benefit it more. Such
features could include CapsLock usage or Shift key side preferences, for example.
5.1.2
K­NN
The K nearest neighbors algorithm is perhaps the simplest, and is based on the con­
cept of finding the distance of k nearest neighbors for each feature cluster in a multi­
dimensional feature space, and sorting these distances in an increasing order, with the
dominant amount of shortest distances determining the label of a sample (majority voting).
The logic behind this mechanism relies on the assumption that data points belonging to
the same class will be located the closest to each other. Commonly used distance met­
rics include the Euclidean distance and the Manhattan distance. The parameter k can
be tuned for the most optimal classification results ­ and it is significant for the accuracy
of this algorithm to make sure that supplied data has a minimal level of noise. Feature
selection and scaling are commonly performed techniques to allow the most valuable fea­
tures the most significant weight. Furthermore, feature selection is significant to avoid the
curse of dimensionality ­ a phenomenon where all distance vectors produce almost equal
distances due to a high number of features, rendering poor classification performance.
Therefore, a lower number of features may actually benefit this classifier ­ provided these
features have similar distances for particular users for optimal performance.
5.1.3
SVM
Support vector machines are a type of classifier that evaluates the points of feature vector
points belonging to one subject against feature vector points belonging to another sub­
ject in a multi­dimensional feature space, creating a multi­dimensional hyperplane that
allows the points from different subjects to stay separated from one another, at a largest
possible margin. For multi­class classification problems, the strategy of adapting SVM to
work is splitting the dataset to create multiple binary classification problems. Therefore,
features that are the most linearly separable will likely be the most beneficial (for linear
SVM models).

to find the most optimal candidate for practical purposes. One such study compares the

5.2
Feature selection and scaling
With the aim of this work being to investigate the weight of extracted temporal and non­
temporal features and their combinations in a context of multi­class classification, a vast
number of features has been recorded and extracted. Therefore, without considering
problems associated with high dimensionality, feature vectors of certain combinations
could become too massive, rendering worse classifier performance as a result.
In order to reduce dimensionality for specific cases, a variety of techniques are available
as pre­processing steps, available as filter and wrapper methods. In feature filtering, rel­
evance of features is evaluated independently of the chosen classifier, through measures
like feature variance (low variance features being less meaningful) or correlation of fea­
tures (highly correlated features being less meaningful). Filtering methods can be used as
a general pre­processing step, and are certainly more favorable for large datasets, con­
sidering better computational efficiency when compared to wrapper methods. However,
because the features are considered in isolation or relative isolation, this is not a good fit
for evaluating features like key side preference, for example, that may have low feature
variance, but a strong impact when paired with other features, acting as a distinguishing
factor. In that regards, wrapper methods, which are based on the concept of training a
model on a subset of given features to select the most significant ones, evaluate features
with respect to the best combinations for a particular classifier. While more computation­
ally complex due to multiple classifier evaluations, these methods are a more optimal fit
for the topic explored in this work, with a smaller dataset being investigated, and a high
amount of features that may serve as distinguishing features in an overall combination,
rather than standalone features.
Sequential feature selection is a wrapper­type feature selection method that, through eval­
uating classifier performances upon adding and removing features, finds the optimal com­
bination of features in a model. There are three phases to this method called forward and
backward elimination, and bi­directional selection, where features from the first two steps
are combined. During the forward elimination phase, the process starts with an empty set,
and features are greedily added by relevance for improving interim classifier performance
until adding further features no longer improves the result, or the performance is made
worse. The backward selection phase begins by evaluating a set of all features, and then
iteratively removing least useful features until the performance is reduced by removing
even more features. For the purposes of this work, SequentialFeatureSelector from the
python scikit library with a forward selection direction will be used. A concrete number
of features to select will be provided, depending on the investigated text and selected
classifier.

Another wrapper­type method to be referred to in this work is Recursive Feature Elimi­

Features are recursively removed until an optimal number of features is settled, with the
remaining features being the most valuable for classifier performance. Once again, to im­

in accuracy occur depending on the number of features. It is also to be noted that only
classifiers that contain attributes for feature importance can be evaluated with this feature
selector ­ this will include random forrest and SVM implementations.
Another pre­processing step relevant to classifiers like K­NN and SVM is feature scaling.
Due to the collected features being measured in different metrics (for example, temporal
features are measured in milliseconds, while features like key preference are a fraction
symbolising precentage), in certain models certain label values might dominate over oth­
ers by sheer magnitude, and must be scaled to achieve adequate performance. The
StandardScaler, also available in the python scikit library, will be used to perform this
action.

Conclusion
Through extensive experimentation, this thesis shows that non­temporal features have an
undeniable impact in keystroke dynamics classification, with their influence being nuanced
depending on the type and length of text being written, as well as the chosen classifier.
For all classifiers, combinations of temporal and non­temporal features enhance perfor­
mance. In the case of short complex text, this impact is the most profound, with classifiers
like SVM and K­NN performing the best with feature vectors in which the majority of the
best features are non­temporal, indicating that in a combination, they may have a more
significant weight than certain temporal features. Regardless of the text type or classifier,
it was shown through applied feature selection methods that the best performing feature
combinations contain anywhere from approximately 30% to 70% of features that are not
temporal. While the performance achieved by selected classifiers is good provided the
combinations, it could likely be improved even more by more precisely tuning the classi­
fiers. Furthermore, the findings of this research could be strengthened even more given
more participants and a larger amount of sessions and repetitions, and perhaps a more
controlled environment.
For purposes of further research, provided additional time and resources, it would be
worthwhile to expand the list of non­temporal features, and investigate the effect of them
in combinations with temporal features. A few suggestions could be average hold times for
the most frequently used letters and digraphs, average time for spacing between words,
and frequency of punctuation marks in freely typed text. Investigating this topic on freely
­ typed text could provide new insights into how typing habits change in with the environ­
ment and knowledge of being recorded, or pressure of having to complete a particular
writing task. Furthermore, the topic of combining human computer interaction behaviours
not constricted to typing could potentially increase performance of some classifiers, and
provide new insights into keystroke dynamics. For example, the reaction time feature in­
vestigated in this thesis could be extended to mouse interactions, to get a more varied
perspective of users interacting with their devices. It would be especially interesting to
find out how these interactions occur in a setting where the flow of a user and device
interaction is not constricted by time or a particular task ­ a style perhaps more suitable
for continuous keystroke dynamics authentication set ­ ups.
