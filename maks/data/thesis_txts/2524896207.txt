Summary (English)
The cocktail party problem is about isolating voices in a noisy environment. We
aim to solve the two simpliﬁed sub problems; noise removal and ﬁngerprinting
of sine waves. By delving into diﬀerent deep learning architectures, implement-
ing them and testing them several deep learning models that show promising
results are found. The models are incrementally made more complex as the
problem gets harder, going from noise removal, to data reconstruction to ﬁnally
ﬁngerprinting.
A deep learning framework is developed to increase productivity and ease of
data handling i.e. including new features, switching datasets, and having several
network models. The thesis concludes with a model for noise removal and ﬁnger
printing.
We found that some models work better for some tasks, complex
networks might not work well for simple tasks, vice versa.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Noise reduction and voice isolation in speech without losing ﬁdelity of the
speaker’s voice are hard problems. We will attempt to solve this by implement-
ing a type of learning algorithm known as neural networks speciﬁcally using deep
learning. Deep learning has recently seen a very large increase in popularity,
and is very well suited for a problem such as cleaning noise from speech. It has
shown to be a good solution for hard problems, where there is plenty of data
to be collected, and the problem is quantiﬁable, i.e. it can be determined how
wrong a prediction is compared to the original. As there are vast oceans of data
of speech from audio books, podcasts, music, etc, gathering data is not a prob-
lem. This thesis aims to isolate and ﬁlter noise from speech using deep learning.
With this technology the living standards for people with hearing impairments
could skyrocket. For the average person this could help eliminate static or cross
talk when on the phone or in a Skype conversation.
1.1
The cocktail party problem
The cocktail party problem is a widely known problem that describes the issues
when identifying sounds in a noisy environment.
Imagine being at a party. The music is loud, there are loud conversations all

around, and yet, you are able to have your own conversation. That is if you
have normal hearing [FEDS07]. A person with normal hearing can use both ears
to get directional information to gate incoming sounds, making it possible to
focus attention on a conversation in a noisy environment. This is done without
requiring conscious eﬀort.
If a person’s hearing is impaired on either one ear, or such that the person needs
hearing aids, the ability to get directional information of sound is very limited.
This makes it very hard for hearing impaired people to follow a conversation in
a noisy environment [HLC04].
1.2
Application
Solutions to the cocktail party problem can be applied in many diﬀerent ways
in many diﬀerent areas.
For instance, voice activated products are becoming a normal occurrence in our
daily lives, especially with the latest advances in deep learning to understand
speech. Some examples are Apple’s Siri, Google’s Google Home and Amazon
Echo. Being able to improve the signal-to-noise ratio will potentially make these
products even better at understanding commands.
Another application can be for hearing aids. Following the cocktail party prob-
lem, it is very problematic to ﬁlter speciﬁc sounds if directional data is not
available. Directional microphones for hearing aids exist, and even though they
make it easier to ﬁlter noise, they are more prone to environmental noise, and
even a small breeze outside can cause problems [Tho00]. With microphones
without these problems, and then using the solution to clean the sound can be
a viable approach.
Up-sampling of poor sound quality could be another use. This can be usable
when talking with another person across a connection with low bandwidth. Pro-
grams such as Skype use very high compression when detecting a low bandwidth,
causing the receiver to hear a very “robot”-like voice. Using deep learning, super
resolution of sound is possible and could remove these problems.

1.3
Project plan
The plan of this project is to create a neural network that, given a noisy signal
of speech, can remove the noise and return the clear speech. In the case that the
noise is another speaker, it should be possible to select between the two signals.
Furthermore, to make it easier to create diﬀerent models with diﬀerent inputs, a
TensorFlow framework will be created which will handle much of the boilerplate
code that is common for diﬀerent models.
1.4
Report overview
The report is structured into the main chapters theory, framework, related works,
deep learning models and discussion.
The theory chapter explains the theory used throughout the thesis. The frame-
work chapter deals with analysis, design and realization of the underlying frame-
work for the diﬀerent models. The related work chapter gives a summary of some
of the papers that gave inspiration to some of the solutions presented in this
thesis. The deep learning models chapter is about the diﬀerent models used
and how they are developed. Finally discussion takes the results of the models
and reﬂects upon them in relation to how and why they work, and how they
compare to the results in other papers.

could be with the housing price trend.

Machine Learning
Machine learning is a subﬁeld of computer science that gives computers the
ability to learn speciﬁc tasks without being explicitly programmed to it.
There are several branches of machine learning, it includes but is not limited to

2.1.3
Artiﬁcial Neural Networks
Neural networks are inspired by the simplest computational unit of the brain,
neurons. Neurons are connected with synapses (wi), from which they receive
input signals along the dendrites (wixi) and produce output along the axons.
The axon (xi) sends the output along the synapses to other neurons via the
dendrites.

(b) Artiﬁcial neuron.

A neural network is basically a network of neurons. A value x0 enters a neuron
and has the weights and biases of the neuron applied to it x0w0 + b0 . The
weights are mutable and inﬂuence the strength of each input. A neuron has the
same amount of weights, as the amount of features(x0...i) in the input. This
means that each feature from the input gets a weight applied to it w0x0 +
b0, w1x1 + b1, wixi + bi. These values are then summed up, passed through
an activation function and sent along as the output of that neuron as seen on

2.1.4
Deep Learning
A deep neural network is just a neural network with multiple layers. With more
layers comes the chance of ﬁnding more correlations. A deeper network allows
for solving more complex task such as self-driving in cars, voice recognition,
object detection in images, and much more. A simple deep neural network can
be seen in Fig. 2.3.
Deeper networks does however also have a chance to overﬁt on the training
data. Overﬁtting means that rather than learn some general rules about the
data, the data is simply memorized.
This means that giving the network a
training example, it will produce a good output, but if an input it has never
seen before is given to it, it will just output garbage.
Deep learning was ﬁrst popularized in 2012 when AlexNet [KSH12] won the
ImageNet 1 classiﬁcation challenge and showed that deep convolutional networks
could identify what is in an image better than humans could. This has then led
to a resurgence in deep learning since most of the algorithms used are quite old,
such as the perceptron which was derived in the 1950’s [Nie15].
Figure 2.3: A simple deep neural network.
With deeper nets generative models also become an opportunity. A generative
model generates new data from some other date for example; WaveNet generates
speech from text. One type of generative model is know as an autoencoder.
This works by encoding some input to a lower state and then decoding it to
the original size. This way the network can infer from the patterns learned, to
reconstruct or generate the missing or destroyed data.

2.2
Traditional noise cancellation
Before computers had enough power and before enough data was available to
train neural networks, alternative algorithms were used to try and clear up a
signal of speech.
Some traditional noise cancellation methods involve multiple microphones and
signal processing, to extract the best speech possible.
For a simple two microphone example, a microphone could be one placed in front
of a speakers mouth and the other placed near the speakers ear. The microphone
near the ear will catch mostly the surrounding noise and the microphone near
the mouth will catch mostly speech. It should then be able to subtract the signal
from the ear mic from the speech mic and achieve noise free speech. However,
the ear mic will likely catch some components of the speech which will also be
subtracted from the voice. This results in the loss of some ﬁdelity in the speech
which means that the speaker’s voice will sound like he talks into a can or with
a robotic voice.
One widely used method for solving the cocktailparty problem is indepen-
dent component analysis (ICA) [HO00].
The idea in ICA is that there is
the data x which is a random vector x = (x1, ..., xn)T and some components
s = (s1, ..., sn)T . The goal is then to transform x into s using a static linear
transformation matrix W, such that s = Wx. The idea is that since the compo-
nents in s are independent, they can be separated using a linear transformation.
The common approach for most of the noise cancellation techniques, is that
they take the original signal, and alter it in certain ways to try and eliminate
the undesired components.

3.1.1
Activation functions
The activation function is an abstraction of the potential ﬁring of a neuron in
the brain. It enables the neural network to approximate “any” function. The
activation function receives a vector and performs a point-wise operation on

3.1.3.1
Gradient descent
Gradient descent is a way to minimize an objective function J(θ), parameterized
by a model’s parameters θ ∈Rd by updating the parameters in the opposite
direction of the gradient of the objective function ∇θJ(θ), with regard to the
parameters of the network. The learning rate (η) determines the size of each
step (update) taken.
Batch gradient descent - BGD
also known as vanilla gradient descent,
computes the gradient of the cost-function for the whole dataset.
θ = θ −η · ∇θJ(θ)
(3.11)
After computing the gradient, it updates θ and is ﬁnished. This can be incredibly
slow as the run time increases linearly with the size of the dataset, and can be
impossible if the dataset does not ﬁt memory.
Stochastic gradient descent - SGD
computes the gradient of the cost-
function for each example x(i) and label y(i).
θ = θ −η · ∇θJ(θ; x(i); y(i))
(3.12)
SGD improves upon BGD by being faster and being able to run online. Due to
frequent updates this means it has a high variance, this can cause the objective
function to ﬂuctuate heavily.

3.1.7
Pooling
It is common practice to insert pooling layers between blocks of convolutional
layers in a convolutional network. Pooling reduces the spatial size of the rep-
resentation to reduce the amount of parameters of the network. This reduces
computational time and helps control overﬁtting. The pooling layer operates in-
dependently on each ﬁlter of the input and resizes it spatially. There are several
forms of pooling with the most common being max pooling [Kar17].
The pooling layer takes a ﬁlter of some size, applies the max operation and
returns a single value. The ﬁlter is then moved by a stride of some size and run
again for every depth slice along both the width and height. With ﬁlters of size
2 × 2 and a stride of 2 the representation will discard 75% of the activation and
reduce the height and width by 50%.
Figure 3.9: Figure from [Kar17]
Pooling layers can also apply other operations. Previously average pool was used
in place of max pool. The pooling works almost identically, however, instead of

or L2-norm.

Deconvolution
Deconvolutional layers have the unique “ability” to reverse the eﬀect of a normal
convolutional layer. This has several purposes from increasing the dimensional-
ity of an input, restoring the shape of an image after several convolutions.
3.1.8.1
Upsampling - Transposed Convolution
The transpose convolution maps from a lower dimensional space to a higher,
whilst keeping the convolutional connectivity pattern as seen on Fig. 3.8. This
is achieved by swapping the forward and backward passes of a convolution. The
transposed convolution can be thought of as the gradient of some convolution
with respect to its input. Compared to the regular convolution where the for-
ward pass and backwards pass are computed by multiplying w with C and CT
respectively, the transposed convolution is computed by multiplying with CT
and (CT )T = C. It is possible to emulate the transpose convolution with regular
convolutions though this involves zero-padding.
The transpose convolution can also have padding, and strides applied to it,
however we will not go into this further. For an in depth guide on convolutional
arithmetic [DV16] can be read.

4.1.1
Architecture of a neural network
A neural network can be split into a few parts. As illustrated on Fig. 4.1, a
simple network consists of data, which is fed into a model, which outputs some
result. This structure will be referred to as a pipeline throughout the chapter.
The architecture does however vary a bit depending on what state the network
is in.
In the training state, the data might need to have certain properties
that makes the training operation fast, and ensure the network does not overﬁt.
The network might have some additional operations that make it better at
training, but should not be present when validating the model. For instance,
having a way to backpropagate through the network is only necessary when
training. Furthermore, the backpropagation depends on the training loss which
is calculated in the output state.
During training, the interesting parts of the output are the loss, accuracy and
other metrics for measuring the performance of the network, which should be
logged in reasonable intervals to make monitoring of the training possible. Dur-
ing production however, the only thing that matters is the actual output, which
should be saved or made available for further processing or presentation.
To give an example; given a network which is being trained to remove noise from
a signal, has 3 stages. During training, we have some clean signals which we
process so they contain noise. The noisy and the clean signal are then passed
onto the model.
The model takes the noisy signal and produces an output.
The clean signal is passed through as is. In the output stage, the output of
the network and the clean signal are compared. This comparison is used to
backpropagate through the network to train it, and to create the needed metrics
for monitoring the network.
After a certain amount of steps of training, the model has been improved. To
ﬁnd out how much, the validation data is passed through the pipeline. This
follows the same pipline as training except the data has never been seen by
the network before.
Furthermore, this data should not be used in the back
==PAGE:43==

propagation pass since it is used to measure the network on unseen data.
The production phase almost follows the same pipeline. However, the produc-
tion example is already noisy, so additional noise should not be applied. Fur-
thermore, a clean version is not available, so this is not passed on to the model.
Some preprocessing is still done, such as shaping the data in a way the model
expects, and can work on. The noisy signal is then sent to the model which
produces an output. Since there is no “correct” result to compare the output to,
it is not possible to produce any metrics or to backpropagate.

To give an overview of the exact functionality that is needed, each feature of
the framework will be brieﬂy described, and a summary is given in the end.
The framework should make it easy to create a clean architecture by creat-
ing extensible components that can be easily put together to a pipeline. The
components should be reusable and ready to ’plug and play’ in other networks.
Much of the functionality of the framework is about gluing together the diﬀerent
parts of a network, so the framework should make easy to use wrappers for
common boilerplate code.
Since a lot of time is used on tweaking hyperparameters and other settings,
these should be easy to alter between runs.
Furthermore it is to be expected that a lot of auxiliary tasks will be needed

it to the hard drive. It should be easy to create this task and make it callable
from the command line.
To summarize the features:

2. Extensible components
3. A feature to make it easy to set hyperparameters
4. A way to create command line scripts

First, two placeholders are created. These are entrypoints to the graph, a place
to input data into the graph. This returns the two tensors x and y. These are
then added together which creates an add operation in the graph. The output
of the operation is a new tensor, z, that is the sum of the previous two tensors.
The addition is then evaluated by starting a Session. With that Session, data
is sent into the graph, and the output of the addition can be retrieved. In the
example x is assigned to 2 and y to 3. The sum of that is of course 5.
Of course this example is very simple and does not show the power that Ten-
sorFlow has. In reality a network will consist of many operations, much more
complex than simple addition, and each tensor will likely have thousands of
elements instead of being a simple scalar.
Tensors can have diﬀerent shapes. A shape of () (empty) means it is a scalar,
a shape of (N) means it is an N-D vector, a shape of (N, M) means it is an
N × M matrix and so on. A typical tensor in this project might have a shape
of (64, 10000, 1) which means it has 64 × 10000 × 1 = 640000 elements. This
tensor holds 64 times 1 second of a piece of sound (assuming a samplerate of 10
kHz), and there are several of those tensors. Even a simple addition between
all elements of two tensors is gonna take a while on a CPU, but is very fast and
trivial for a GPU since it can do it in parallel.
One thing to note on the shape of a Tensor, is that operations involving two
tensors often requires the shape of the Tensors to be compatible.
It is for
instance possible to add a scalar to any tensor. This will just add the scalar to
each element of the tensor. However, more complicated shapes needs to have
equal shapes for operations to go well.
4.2.3
Need for framework
There already exists diﬀerent frameworks that aim to make it easier to create
deep neural networks. To mention a few; Keras is a framework that puts an easy
to use API on top of either TensorFlow or Theano. TF-slim is a TensorFlow
framework that also makes it easier to build deep neural networks by simplifying
the creation of common layers.
So why is there a need for additional frameworks? It seems that many frame-
works tries to make it easier to use other frameworks by adding additional layers
of abstraction on top. That is not the goal of this framework.
The goal of this framework is to try to force a certain architecture that decouples
diﬀerent parts of a neural network.

To take TensorFlow as an example; there are TFRecords. These are a certain
ﬁleformat that TensorFlow knows how to parse and how to input into a graph.
However, this requires the user to convert all of his data to the TFRecords

By decoupling data handling from the neural network, it also enables a user to

Pipeline
During development of the pipeline, it quickly became clear that it was not
viable to have one big pipeline, and then iterating over the whole thing.
Figure 4.3: Overview of the ﬁnal framework.
The original pipeline is meant to be used for sequential runs, however, Ten-
sorFlow’s graph are meant to be oﬄoaded to the GPU, and if each training
==PAGE:51==
4.3 Realization
35
run has to wait for previous pipes to ﬁnish running, it will slow training down
signiﬁcantly. Furthermore, TensorFlow has an implementation of a Queue, this
makes it possible to produce tensors of data on a second thread, such that the
graph does not have to wait for training data.
As such, the pipeline has been implemented as multiple smaller pipelines, one
for each segment in the basic architecture, and then the overall pipeline glues
the diﬀerent pipes together.
This enables the framework to e.g. make a thread run on the data pipe, which
feeds the data into a TensorFlow queue. The queue then acts as glue between
the data and the network.
An overview of the ﬁnal framework can be seen in Fig. 4.3. This shows a pipeline
consisting of two smaller pipes.
The ﬁrst pipeline is the data handling pipeline. Here the data loaders for the
source of the data, a feature manager to extract features and a queue to place
the features belong.
The second pipeline has the queue as source, the neural network that transforms
the data, and ﬁnally there are the stages that treat the output in some fashion.
The two pipes run on their separate thread which are synchronized around the
queue.
4.3.3
Data handling
The data is created/loaded in the DataLoader. This is the ﬁrst sub pipe of the
pipeline. A DataLoader fetches the basic data. This could be the names of the
ﬁles with the data on the disk or the seed from which the data is created. Each
datapoint is yielded from the DataLoader one at a time.
The data from the DataLoaders is then fed into a FeatureManager.
The
FeatureManager takes the data and feeds it through multiple Features to ex-
tract the desired features for the network. This is for instance useful if the data
is the name of audio ﬁles on disk. The name of each ﬁle can be shuﬄed, and
split into train, validation and test dataset easily, as the names do not ﬁll much
in memory. Each ﬁlename is then fed into the FeatureManager, that extracts
features from the ﬁle, such as the actual sound data. The sound is then parsed
on to the next feature extractor this could I.e. make a fourier transform if that
was desired by the network or any other transformation on the data.

4.3.4
Output and visualization
To use the output of the network for monitoring, the Stage class is created. By
extending this class, and registering it, it will be called in certain intervals as
the thread loops over the pipeline.
There are several diﬀerent stages. The ﬁrst is the TrainingStage. This is called
every loop, and evaluates the training operation in the current Session.
This is followed by the SummaryStage. This is run every number of steps that
corresponds to every 5-10 minutes. It takes the diﬀerent metrics and runs the
summary operation on the Session.
Finally there is the SavingStage. This is responsible for saving and restoring
a network. The stage receives a call before the threads starts running its loop
so it is able to set things up. It uses this call to check if the current name
of the network already exists as a checkpoint, and restores the graph to that
checkpoint if that is the case. This is useful for pausing and resuming training.

graph can later be used.

Command line execution
The command line execution part takes inspiration from Django2, which is a

Django follows a mantra called the DRY (Don’t Repeat Yourself) principle.
This literally means “do not write the same piece of code more than once”.
This is also the reason the framework has command line execution. By making
scripting part of the framework, it is easier to reuse existing code, and if speciﬁc
tasks are often copied between diﬀerent networks, it is easy to make it a general
script in the framework.
The way scripting is implemented is with the BaseCommand class. The by ex-
tending this class it is possible to specify command line arguments, and the
framework will automatically parse the arguments and run the command with
the arguments.
The way a command is called, is by having a python script inside the module
called commands which has a single class called Command. The Command class
should extend BaseCommand. The name of the python ﬁle will be the name of
the script. This mirrors the way Django scripts are made.

5.2
Removal of noise using binary masks
Some algorithms for ﬁltering noise from a monaural source exists. One example
is an algorithm created by [HYWW13]. There, a deep neural network (DNN)
is used to estimate a binary mask that can be applied to the signal to remove
the noise. They split a signal into regions of time-frequency, and then they use
the DNN to classify each region as a speech or noise region.
Even though the method successfully ﬁnds and eliminates noise, the resulting
speech is very robot-like and not very nice to listen to. This is again because, if
a time-frequency region is the mix of noise and speech, and the algorithm masks
it out, some of the ﬁdelity in the voice is also lost, resulting in a jarring sound.
However, removing noise from speech can have other practical uses than for
improving human comprehension. For instance, [WW16] uses it to improve the
robustness of speech recognition systems.

With networks surpassing the 100-layer barrier, a new type of layer is proposed

The information ﬂow follows the schematic: each layer is directly connected to

All layers between the dense blocks are transition layers that consist of a BN,

The collection of feature-maps can be viewed as the global state of the network,

5.4
U-Net
This paper presents a possible solution to reducing training time and training
data needed for convolutional neural networks. This is achieved by creating a
contracting path to capture context and a symmetric expanding path that en-
ables precise localization. These networks are built upon the “fully convolutional
network” [LSD15].
The main idea is to supplement the usual contracting network with successive
layers, where pooling is replaced with upsampling operators, thereby increasing
the resolution of the output. The features found in the deconvolutional stage
is then combined with the upsampled output which is used for a successive
convolution that then can assemble a more precise output based on the two
layers l1 and l2.

An important addition to the network is the large amount of feature channels in
the upsampling stage, which allow the network to propagate context information
to higher resolution layers.

Batch Normalization
Batch normalization was ﬁrst introduced in the paper. [IS15]. Batch normaliza-
tion introduces normalization into the model architecture by normalizing across
the batch. It has two modes one for training and one when it is not. When
training, each mini-batch is normalized and added to a total norm variable.
When the network is not training the variable with the total norm is used to
normalize. Batch normalization allows for more leeway in selecting learning rate
and acts like a regularizer which in some cases can eliminate the use of dropout.

The range and the frequency is enough to generate 200 unique sinewaves. This
is not very much, so in order to increase the amount of data, a unique phase and
amplitude is also chosen. Using this, the sinewave is generated. The formula
can be seen below in Eq. 6.1 with x as the range, p as the phase and A as the

y = sin(x + p) · A

randomly shifted compared to each other. This creates even more unique data.
The amplitude simulates the loudness of a signal. That means the network is
trained to both isolate loud and not so loud speakers. This in turn also creates
even more data to train on.
Depending on what problem that is being solved, either a random chunk is

Discussion
This section contains an analysis and discussion of our results, a discussion of
the state of the art network models and how they helped us. Furthermore, it
deals with the challenges we faced and ﬁnally we discuss the future work needed
to get better results from both the network and the framework.
7.1
Results
This section will be used to reﬂect on the results obtained from the diﬀerent
models created in this thesis.
7.1.1
Convolutional networks
Looking at the results from the denoise/missing chunk problem we saw a clear
diﬀerence in network performance depending on the complexity of the data. All
the networks solved the problem, but we noticed that the simpler network such
as the autoencoder, Fig. 6.5, worked better than some of the more advanced
and deeper networks. This is most likely due the diﬃculty of the problem with
respect to the complexity of the data.
==PAGE:84==
68
Discussion
We conﬁrmed this as with more complex data, two sine waves summed together
as an input, the more advanced networks, Fig. 6.7 and 6.9, were the clear win-
ners. The problem could have been made more challenging by increasing the
size of the missing chunk. Reconstructing a larger chunk should be quite sim-
ple, due to the repetitiveness of sinewaves. As real speech normally isn’t that
repetitive we decided not to pursue this further.
7.1.2
Hyperparameters
We tweaked a lot of hyperparameters such as the learning rate, number of ﬁlters,
the size of ﬁlters, batch size, etc. The thing to take away here is that bigger is
not always better. For instance, increasing the learning rate made the weights
explode, which in turn made the gradients explode.
Increasing the amount of ﬁlters in each layer, increases the amount of learnable
weights. If increased too much and the amount of features are too few, the risk
of overﬁtting is much higher.
7.1.3
Deeper networks
Having more layers in the networks made it harder to train. This may partly
be due to the simplicity of the data, but also because of the problem with
vanishing/exploding gradients. We saw from the results, that applying some of
the solutions that alleviate the vanishing gradient problem, such as GLU and
U-Net, made the deep networks better at learning.
7.1.4
Embedding sizes
From the results it seems that a larger embedding makes the networks sig-
niﬁcantly worse at predicting. We can also see that the smaller embeddings
correlates with the frequency of the sine wave.
Our hypothesis is that the network simply stores the width of the wave in
the embedding, and uses it to basically become a sine wave generator. The
problem comes from the data not being diverse enough. A human voice can
for instance contain several diﬀerent components such as octave, accent, general
loudness, etc. There may be too few diﬀerent components in the sine waves for
==PAGE:85==
7.2 Comparison with related works
69
the network to make use of a larger embedding, such that the large embedding
simply spreads the information too thin.
7.1.5
Data
The reason we decided to use generated data even though it made training of
deeper networks harder, was due to availability of servers.
With the generated data came the beneﬁts, that since the data is rather simple,
it was possible to train a network and get results very quickly, even on less
powerful computers. This in turn made it possible to test a lot of ideas more
quickly since the networks converged much faster.
7.2
Comparison with related works
The networks created in this thesis take a lot of inspiration from diﬀerent state
of the art papers. Some of the experiments directly relate to speciﬁc papers,
which we will compare here.
7.2.1
Batch normalization
One experiment was to see how batch normalization would work for cleaning
noise. We immediately saw better results. The authors of the original batch
normalization paper claims that they achieved a 14x speedup of convergence.
While we did see a massive improvement, it was hard to test if it matched their
claim.
The experiments we did with batch normalization mainly consisted of where
to place it and how many layers it should be placed in. We tested with batch
normalization in the beginning, middle, last and in multiple layers. The results
show that having batch normalization in the ﬁrst layer gave us the best results.
This makes sense since that makes the batch normalization aﬀect all layers.
==PAGE:86==
70
Discussion
7.2.2
GLU
The GLU proved to be better than regular convolutional layers. We also tried
some of the experiments the authors of the GLU-paper tried. Speciﬁcally we
tried to use TanH on the non gated unit since that was the units used by
WaveNet. The results proved the authors right.
We got the best results from the linear units, but while the authors and WaveNet
both got good results from TanH, we got terrible results.
7.2.3
U-net
The U-Net paper described in Sec. 5.4 helped us create the model structure.
We imitated the concatenation from early layers to later layers to help increase
the connectivity in the network. This worked very well for denoise of a single
sinewave, and decent for dual sine wave. Instead of using transposed convolu-
tions we ended up using depth to space layers. This meant that the structure
was changed slightly and now only has one concatenation. We found later, that
having a convolution after the concatenation helped the network derive more
patterns from the concatenated layers. We found that for ﬁngerprinting, the
U-Net model worked worse compared to the modiﬁed model structure we ended
up at.
7.2.4
Dense net
Another experiment was with a DenseNet model, which is described in Sec. 5.3.
We found that the extra connectivity between each layer did not give our net-
work any performance increase, quite the opposite. The network got slower and
had a tendency to overﬁt. The authors does however state that it works best
for very deep networks. It could also be because of the complexity of the data.

7.3.1
Binaural simulator
This thesis was birthed from the FairyTech project created by Søren and Casper
Kaae Sønderby. FairyTech was an implementation of U-Net and used the Lib-
riVox dataset [PCPK15]. The ﬁrst task was to take the input sound, which was
in mono, and make a binaural simulator to convert it to stereo. This is due
to the cocktail party problem, where stereo sound makes it easier for people to
isolate the desired sound.
Creating the binaural simulator took quite a while because of the challenges
faced with TensorFlow (Sec. 7.3.3). Using python loops would mean data would
have to be transferred from the GPU to the CPU, executed sequentially and
transferred back to the GPU. This is slow. Instead, the data was transformed
using tensor operations. The tensor operations were hard to grasp at ﬁrst, but
was a good introduction to working with tensors in TensorFlow.
Some additional challenges occurred during the development of the simulator.
We were trying to both solve the problem with the simulator, and try to get
an overview of the code provided by Casper and Søren, however, they were
also updating the code, introducing breaking changes that made it harder to
understand their code while implementing our own. Furthermore TensorFlow
released version 1.0 which introduced further breaking changes to the API.
The implementation of the binaural simulator was tested with the initial model
created by Casper and Søren, and the results monitored in the TensorBoard. We
tested diﬀerent hyperparameters to see if we could get better results, however,
with our limited understanding of the network, we had a hard time trying to
ﬁgure out what worked and what did not work. Furthermore, the server with the
dataset was often occupied, making it hard to train with diﬀerent parameters.
Due to the problems with the server being occupied, being unfamiliar with the
code, not knowing the data, etc., we decided that we had to make our own
architecture and build our own network from scratch.
7.3.2
In the neural networks
Backpropagation was, to us, initially a black box that computed the gradients
when asked. After diving into the algorithm we now have a much better under-
standing of how each weight and bias in the network gets their gradients, and
how the gradients aﬀects the activation function and can cause them to vanish
or explode.
==PAGE:88==
72
Discussion
Digging into the convolutional layers gave us an understanding of what each
ﬁlter actually outputs. It showed that the ﬁlter size and activation function has
a big say on the ﬁnal output. The ﬁlter size of the convolution in our case is
128 × 1 where a normal ﬁlter size for images usually is around 3 × 3. We found
that in the case of sinewaves and sound in general, we needed wider ﬁlters, i.e.
more information to get an output that had some semblance of the input. This
makes sense, as sound has a time dimension. This means, to get something
sensible we need to look at a bigger spectra as any information found in the
audio ﬁle will need more than 9 samples. The sinewaves have a sample rate
of 11024 which means 9 samples equals 8.0e −3 milliseconds and 128 samples
equals 1.1 milliseconds.
Using ﬁlters that were wider than 256 did not give
very promising results. This could be because of the repetitiveness found in
sinewaves, but perhaps also because the ﬁlter would generalize too much.
The activation function also holds sway in output. We noticed early on that
using sigmoid or ReLU often would lead to either exploding or vanishing gra-
dients. We decided to use the leaky ReLU activation function. Later we found
that gated linear units worked even better.

It was a challenge to determine what change in the network caused improvement
or detriment. We learned that changing the network too much between training
passes would lead to us having to retrain several networks with each individual
change to determine what was good or bad. This lead us to improve the network
incrementally instead.

7.3.3
In TensorFlow and the framework
TensorFlow has a lot of great tools to create neural networks. This does however
give big overhead when trying to comprehend how it works. Some of the big
challenges we faced when learning TensorFlow includes the computation graph,
the mindset in creating operations, and how to manage variables inside the
graph and inside a session.
The computation graph was challenging to understand at ﬁrst. Usually, when
a line of code is executed, it returns a result. With TensorFlow, a line of code
returns a tensor that represents the result, but ﬁrst when evaluated in a session.
Not being able to just print the result or use normal debugging methods was
challenging.
7.3.3.1
Multiple simultaneous pipelines
Since a neural network will be evaluated for training, validation and testing
datasets, it was necessary to give the network diﬀerent inputs. Since the size
of the data might change depending on the dataset, diﬀerent placeholders are
needed. For TensorFlow, this means that the entire graph has to be recreated.
However, certain variables need to be the same for it to be the same network.
For instance, the learned kernel in a convolution should be the same kernel
during training and testing.
In TensorFlow, this is done with variable scopes. Variable scopes enables the
developer to reuse variables between graphs in the same session. However, even
though the variable scopes are well documented, it was a challenge to ﬁgure
out that this was the feature needed to be able to have separate training and
validation graphs.
==PAGE:90==
74
Discussion
7.3.3.2
Speed of TensorFlow queue
The TensorFlow queue is meant to speed up training by having data ready for
the GPU at all times. However, we noticed after implementing the queue that
compared to using feed_dict, that training time was doubled.
The TensorFlow queue has a method called dequeue_many which takes as many
tensors as required, concatenates them together and returns them. This seems
like the perfect method to get batches of data for training. However, it appears
that this was the reason for why training slowed down.
Apparently, dequeue_many does multiple memcpy operations to concatenate the
tensors together to batches. Using feed_dict, we fed a whole batch which is
only one memcpy operation.
The solution was to feed batches into the queue, and then just dequeue one
batch at a time.
7.3.3.3
Feature special cases
Being able to take some data, see what shape it has, and assuming that it is
the same for all data, does not always hold true. It is also possible that not all
the generated features should go all the way into the TensorFlow queue (and
consume memory), but should rather just be used by another feature extractor.
The way to tackle these special cases is through python meta classes. This makes
it possible to alter the way diﬀerent features are treated through the pipeline.
An example of how this is used; in this thesis we create our own data, and
initially, that data is the frequency from which the sine curve should be created.
Since this data is irrelevant for the computation graph, it would be wasted
resources to put it into the queue. We can thus create a class Meta, and tell it
to ignore those ﬁelds. This makes them available in the feature pipeline, but
does not waste resources further down the pipeline.

Conclusion
In this thesis, several neural networks that could cancel noise and isolate speak-
ers were created. Furthermore, a framework, that makes it easy to create net-
works as small reusable components, was created.
In order to create neural networks, the cocktail party problem was ﬁrst intro-
duced followed by an explanation for some of the core concepts and theory in
neural networks. Following this, we create an analysis, design and implementa-
tion of the framework.
The framework is implemented in python, and is focused on the DRY (Don’t
Repeat Yourself) mantra.
The presented framework is based on our initial
knowledge of the deep learning domain, however, throughout the thesis, we
achieve a deeper understanding of the domain, that has helped us so we can
improve the framework in the future.
We create several neural network models through an iterative process, where the
data and the structure of the model are improved incrementally. The earliest
models were suited for the earliest data, and as we made the problem harder,
the complexity of the models followed suit.
The models are implemented as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), with
varying architectures that mimic U-Net, DenseNet and other state of the art
==PAGE:94==
78
Conclusion
CNNs.
These architectures are a result of us exploring the state of the art
models that we have summarized in the related works chapter.
To implement the data handling we mainly used NumPy, and for training neural
networks we used TensorFlow. Using TensorFlow was initially hard because it
introduces new concepts such as a computation graph, but eventually became
trivial to use.
Some of the key points learned from the thesis:
• The quality and amount of the input data makes a big diﬀerence whether
a neural network will perform well.
• Having a good framework that automates a lot of the boilerplate, makes
it easy and fast for prototyping.
• The performance of a deep learning architecture varies depending on the
task it should solve (ImageNet is not optimal for sound).
• For optimal reconstruction of data, a lower level representation is needed
like the latent layer in autoencoders.
• Complex and deep networks require diverse and complex data to be able
to perform well.
This thesis has given us a very good understanding of the challenges in deep
learning, pitfalls to avoid and good practices to follow when creating new models
for solving new problems.
