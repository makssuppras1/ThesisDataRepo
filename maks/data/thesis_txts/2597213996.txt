Abstract
Glass-fibre-reinforced polymer (GFRP) rock bolts are increasingly being used in under-
ground excavations. A numerical model using ABAQUS/Standard was constructed and
used to analyse the influence of various parameters on the shear behavior of a fully
grouted GFRP rock bolt installed in a host rock. Parameters studied include the bolt
inclination with respect to the shear plane, diameter of the rock bolt, strength of the host
rock and width between the joint. The main findings from this study were that the shear
resistance was influenced by bolt inclination, bolt diameter and joint width while not sig-
nificantly by host rock strength. Furthermore, the stiffness of the system was seen to
be affected by all four parameters studied. Finally, the results from the numerical model
could not be validated due to a lack of experimental data covering the shear behavior of
GFRP rock bolts.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background for present research
The use of glass-fibre-reinforced polymers (GFRP) has over the last decades been used
in increasing number of civil engineering applications. However, lack of awareness for
the material and concern over its performance in shear has lead to a slow embrace in
the tunnelling industry for use as rock bolting. Rock bolting for stabilizing the rock mass
in underground excavations has been used in rock engineering for more than a century
all over the world. The rock mass may open and slide along fractures or create new
fractures. Here rock bolting helps to improve the strength of the rock mass, control the
relative movement of the rock mass and clamp the fractured rock together. Rock bolts
are in general designed to act in tension, but can also be loaded in shear. Shear failure
of rock bolts often occur in an excavation with highly fractured rock mass (Kaiser 1996).
fig. 1.1 illustrates a rock bolt under shear loading due to joint movement of the rock mass.
Figure 1.1: Bending of a rock bolt from shear loading (L. Li 2016)
The study of the shear behavior of GFRP rock bolts is far from comprehensive and most
studies focus on steel rock bolts. Likewise are many of the analytical models of the shear
behavior of rock bolts developed based on steel and assuming a plastic stage in the
material behavior (Jalalifar and N. Aziz 2010; Ferrero 1995; Spang and Egger 1990). A
comprehensive numerical modelling is therefore carried out to get a better understanding
of GFRP bolts subjected to shear loading.

1.2
Research objectives and methodology
The aim of this project is to study different factors influencing the shear behavior of a GFRP
bolt embedded in concrete under static loading. The main objectives of this project are
to:
• 1) Categorize and summarize the existing literature on the shear behavior of GFRP
bolts.
• 2) Develop a finite element model of a GFRP bolt embedded in concrete.
• 3) Investigate influencing factors on the shear behavior of the GFRP bolt using the
finite element model.
Large scale laboratory and in situ tests are ideally recommended for studying the shear
behavior but can be expensive and time consuming. Numerical modelling techniques
offer an alternative to laboratory and in situ testing for studying rock mechanical prob-
lems. Therefore the influence of different factors on the shear behavior is studied using
numerical modelling.
In additional to the above mentioned objectives, this thesis also provides a description for
a suggested test setup, including test procedure and test facility. This test setup is meant
to offer guidance for executing a laboratory for studying influencing factors on the shear
behavior.

2
Literature review
2.1
Rock bolt loading conditions
Rock bolts may be subject to both tension and shear loading from opening and sliding of
fractures in the rock mass. An example is the sliding of a wedge block shown in fig. 2.1.
The block will tend to slide along the joint plane into the tunnel running at an angle to the
rock bolt. This angle is called the bolt inclination and is denoted as α. The sliding of the
block can be decomposed into a horizontal and vertical displacement, denoted Dp and
Ds respectively. The horizontal displacement cause tensile load in the rock bolt, while the
vertical displacement cause shear load in the rock bolt.
Figure 2.1: Example of loading condition of a rock bolt in a tunnel
2.2
GFRP
Glass-fibre-reinforced polymer (GFRP), also known as fibreglass, is a composite of glass
fibres embedded in a resin. The resin is used to bond the glass fibres together while also
coating and protecting the fibres against abrasion or corrosion. Furthermore, the resin
contributes to the overall strength of the GFRP. The fibre glass is strong in tension and
the resin weak, whereas the resin is strong in compression and fibre glass is weak. In
this way the two materials in unison create a composite that makes up where the other
material is lacking.
Applications using GFRP usually draw on its high strength to weight ratio whereby reduc-
ing the weight of the reinforcement and corrosion, erosion and wear resistance compared
to conventional steel. However, no design codes for fibre-reinforced polymer (FRP) rein-
forced concrete elements has been developed and the responsibility of structural safety
falls on the designer. A lack of experimental data on long-term degradation of mechanical
properties therefore often results in higher factors of safety as a common design practice
leading to higher costs (Gudonis et al. 2013).

2.2.1
Glass fibres
A wide range of glass fibres exist for a variety of applications depending on the chemical
configuration and manufacturing process. In common for them all is a similar stiffness but
varying strength and environmental resistance. E-glass is the most common glass fibre
type because of its high tensile strength, good environmental resistance and low cost
compared to other glass fibre types. Other fibre types include C-glass for high corrosion
resistance, AR-glass for high alkaline resistance, S- and R-glass for high strength and
D-glass for its dielectric attributes.
Some of the negative characteristics of GFRP is among others a low stiffness compared
to steel and brittle material failure. GFRP does not yield and so does not offer significant
warning before failure, as the failure is abrupt and occurs shortly after peak load. Further-
more, if the matrix material degrades, the GFRP may experience a decrease in strength
from chemical corrosion from chemicals such as alkalies.
2.2.2
Matrix material
The matrix material is an essential aspect of the FRP for several reason. The matrix
material is responsible for load transfer between fibres and a major part in the FRPs en-
vironmental resistance. The combination of fibre and matrix types gives a huge variety of
functional and structural characteristics for the designer to choose from for the application
needed. The most common matrix materials used are polyester, vinylester and epoxy,
although many other types of matrix material exist.
2.2.3
Anisotropy of GFRP
An unidirectional fibre composite will exhibit orthotropic material behavior which has three
planes of symmetry. Furthermore, it will have the same transverse properties in every di-
rection assuming the fibres are homogeneously distributed in the matrix. This is seen as
an infinite number of symmetry planes with the fibre direction can be drawn. A represen-
tation of such an element with its planes of symmetry is seen in fig. 2.2. The focus of this
study is the GFRP bolt which is characterised by such orthotropic behavior.

2.2.4
Long-term degradation
Like many other composites, GFRP experience decrease in strength over time. The long-
term strength may decrease as much as up to two to three times that of the short-term
strength (Gudonis et al. 2013). After about 1 year the loss in static strength was found
to be 30% for e-glass polyester tendons (Wolff and Miesseler 1989; Taerwe 1993). One
study found that half of samples tested at sustained stress of 50% of short-term strength
failed after about 7 years while some samples even failed at 33% of short-term strength
(Slattery 1996). Another study found that hand laid-up GFRP had lost 60% of its short-
term static strength after 10 to 15 years (Marsh 1998). A stress ratio, much similar to a
safety factor, of 3.5 is used in design of GFRP composites for pressure vessels under
permanent load mainly to account for this reduction in tensile strength under sustained
load (Barbero 2017).
2.2.5
Shear behavior
The matrix material is normally the dominating factor with shear loading, as fibres do not
contribute to strength perpendicular to the fibre direction. However, experimental data
with focus on shear strength suggests that crushing of the grout adjacent to the shearing
plane and extension of the bolt may cause the bolt to fail in tension instead of pure shear
(Thomas 2019; X. Li et al. 2016; Jalalifar, Naj Aziz, and Mohammad Hadi 2005; P. Pells, S.
Pells, and Pan 2018). This is beneficial for the GFRP as the tensile strength is higher than
the shear strength. However, the shear performance of GFRP bolts is not fully understood
2.2.6
Factors affecting the shear behavior of rock bolts
The influence of different parameters on the shear resistance of GFRP bolts has not been
studied in great detail. Most studies instead focus on steel, as this in general has been the
norm for rock bolts. Although steel and GFRP behaves differently, it may still give some
ideas of factors influencing the shear behavior. In general, the factors can be grouped into
three categories: i) rockmass, ii) the reinforcement system, and iii) loading conditions.
Bolt inclination
Several studies found that joints reinforced with inclined GFRP bolts instead of perpendic-
ular ones had a higher shear capacity as more of the tensile strength of the bolt is being
utilised (Thomas 2019; Hovgaard 2019; Connor 2014). Spang and Egger 1990 however
found that for low angles of friction in the joint, the bolt inclination did not have any influ-
ence on shear capacity for steel bolts. Furthermore, the stiffness was found to increase
with inclination for GFRP rock bolts compared to perpendicular ones (Connor 2014; Hov-
gaard 2019). Finally, the shear displacement was found to decrease with smaller angles
of bolt inclination (Connor 2014; Hovgaard 2019).
Rockbolt diameter
L. Li et al. 2016; Spang and Egger 1990 found that larger diameter steel rock bolt has a
higher shear resistance which increases linearly with the cross section area of the bolt.
Spang and Egger 1990 also found that shear displacement at peak shear load to be
proportional to the bolt diameter.

Host rock strength
One study using steel bolts found the shear resistance not significantly influenced by the
strength of concrete (L. Li et al. 2016). Spang and Egger 1990 found that for a frictionless
shear surface no effect could be observed from varying the rock strength on steel rock
bolts. Thomas 2019 however found that concrete strength did have an influence on shear
resistance when comparing across studies using GFRP. The peak shear load was found
to be higher in stronger materials up to a point after which it would decrease again. Alun
hypothesize this effect may be because of the host rock being too strong and restricting
the bending of the bolt thus instead failing similar to shear cutting of the bolt.
Grout strength
The shear resistance was found to first decrease and then increase with increasing grout
strength when compared across studies using GFRP rock bolts (Thomas 2019).
Joint width
Snell, Kuley, and Milne 2017 observed that increasing the joint width, the open width
spanned by a steel bolt, only had a small decrease in shear resistance. Furthermore, the
larger the gap, the higher shear displacement at failure.
Joint roughness
The roughness of the joint was found by Spang and Egger 1990 to have a large influence
on the shear resistance. The difference in shear resistance was found to be as high as
50% between a rough and very smooth joint surface. Furthermore, they found the shear
displacement at peak shear load was not influenced by the joint roughness, while the
stiffness of the system was found to increase with roughness of the joint.
Pretension of rock bolt
A study by X. Li et al. 2016 found that the shear resistance of GFRP rock bolts was
increased with the increase of bolt pretension. Furthermore, the stiffness of the system
was found to increase while the shear displacement at failure was found to decrease with
increase in bolt pretension.
2.2.7
Failure
Several modes of failure exist for a composite such as GFRP. Failure modes include fiber
failure and matrix failure and interaction among these types of failures. Failure modes
include but is not limited to:
• Fiber failure due to tension, compression or shear.
• matrix failure due to tension, compression or shear.
• many others.
For simple states of stress failure can be predicted by comparing the current stress with
the strength. However, for more complex states of stress information about the interac-
tion among modes becomes necessary. Ignoring the fact that the material is subject to
multiaxial states of stress may lead to unsafe failure predictions. This is caused when two
6

modes of failure interact negatively on each other and cause failure at a lower state of
stress than if the two modes of failure were acting separately. Typical cases of interaction
that interact negatively are according to Barbero 2017:
• Transverse tensile and shear stress. This interaction both cause the same type of
matrix cracks.
• Longitudinal compressive and shear stress. The shear stress cause a reduction of
the longitudinal compressive strength.
Researchers have developed several failure criteria through the years. However, no uni-
versal criteria has been developed to reliably predict failure. Furthermore, in some cases
huge differences can be found between individual criteria where the result can differ up
to 570 % (Soden, Hinton, and Kaddour 1998). Major shortcomings for some failure cri-
teria developed include artificial interaction between modes of failure that do not interact,
failing to include interaction among modes that do interact or failing to identify the critical
modes of failure (Barbero 2017).
2.3
Numerical modelling
A challenge with studying rock bolts is the amount of independent variables needed to
be studied in order to evaluate the effect on a dependent variable (L. Li et al. 2016). For
instance, the bolt inclination may be dependent on the bolt diameter, joint width, rock
strength, grout strength, rate of displacement when testing and so on. Furthermore, the
size of the test blocks can become rather large for large diameter rock bolts in order to
prevent splitting of the test blocks or when simulating underground geological conditions.
Thus capability and size becomes limiting factors for the testing facility along with price
per test. Therefore, there is ample opportunity for further research and understanding of
the interaction between rock bolt, grout and host rock with regards to the material shear
behavior and mechanical behavior in general.
Numerical modelling can overcome some of these issues by avoiding insufficient confine-
ment and make it easier to examine contributing factors to a greater extent. However,
the shear behavior of a GFRP rock bolt embedded in a host rock can be difficult to model
because of the difference in material stiffness and mechanical behaviors. Furthermore,
a challenge is also to model the contact between these materials correctly (L. Li 2016).
Finally, the numerical model should always be validated through experimental data to
ensure that the model is developed correctly and represents reality.
Numerous numerical models have been developed by researchers to simulate shear be-
havior of rock bolts, however, nearly all have been based on steel rock bolts. Noteworthy
is that some researchers chose to model the grout and concrete with elastic behavior
only. Elastic models are more suitable when only dealing with small strain, whereas large
strain requires elasto-plastic behavior to describe the yielding and failure of the material.
Researchers that did use elasto-plastic behavior for the concrete typically used either

2.4
Shear tests for rock bolts
There are limited standard tests that evaluate the shear behavior of rock bolts and as such
several test configurations exist. In general two types of test setups has been used by
researchers: i) single shear tests, and ii) double shear tests. fig. 2.3 illustrates the concept
of a single and double shear test respectively. Single shear tests are characterised by
shearing of the rock bolt in one joint plane, whereas double shear tests are characterised
by shearing in two joint planes of either one continuous rock bolt or two discontinuous rock
bolts. Examples of different test setups for both types can be found in fig. 2.4. Issues with
both types has to be addressed. For the single shear test, a reported issue is an non-
equal load distribution in the two blocks, where the shearing is no longer paralleled to the
joint surface but instead rotates. An reported issue with the double shear test is the rock
bolt only rupturing at one end and not both at once.

Full scale testing methodology
Due to the COVID-19 pandemic the planned full scale testing was never performed as all
laboratory work had to be put on hold. However, the following methodology was devel-
oped and may serve as inspiration for further testing. This chapter describes the prepa-

ferent angles of embedded with 2 different diameter bars. The angles are between 90◦
and 50◦normal to the joint with 10◦increments. In addition to the push-off tests, compres-
sive strength tests are performed on cylindrical specimen from the same concrete and
mortar batchs as the host rock and grouting used for the tested specimen. An overview
of the tests planned is shown in chapter 3.

3.1
Test setup
3.1.1
Specimen description
The test is performed as a push-off test with a GFRP bolt installed with grouting between
two concrete blocks to simulate conditions in the rock. The specimen proposed is seen
in fig. 3.1. The specimen features space for 3 bolts which can be tested separately with
the same angle. This decreases the amount of host rocks that has to be cast as one host
rock pair can be used 3 times. For each reuse, the mid-span surface must be smooth,
thus angle grinding the remaining GFRP bolt from the previous test if necessary. The
GFRP bar is placed at mid-depth and the orientation varied at mid-span. Orientations
can be varied between sets of 90◦, 80◦, 70◦, 60◦and 50◦. Steel reinforcement at the head
is provided to protect the concrete from crushing during the test. High strength concrete
with a characteristic compressive strength of 80 MPa is used for the concrete blocks. A
cement grout with characteristic compressive strength of 45 MPa is used for grouting.

3.1.2
Specimen preparation
The hole for the GFRP and grout is made with a steel rod wrapped in a thin plastic conduit
to simulate rifling. The steel rod with the plastic conduit must have a total diameter equal
to the grout diameter. The steel rod and plastic conduit is removed when the concrete is
set.
Alternatively the concrete elements can be cast solid and holes for the grout and GFRP
made with a hammer drill once the concrete is set. This method may however damage the
concrete and make it harder to control the angle. Drilling should be initiated from the side
which will be at mid-span as the drill will leave a uneven surface when breaking through
the other side.
For installing the GFRP, a rubber gasket is installed between the two concrete blocks with
a 6 mm gap and the hole is plugged in one end. The grout is pumped into the hole leaving
space for the bolt to be inserted and allowing excess grout to get out. Allow for a minimum
of 28 days for the concrete to fully cure and a minimum of 3 days for the grout.
Four linear variable differential transformers (LVDT) are mounted to the specimen to
measure longitudinal and vertical deformations. Placement of the LVDTs is illustrated in
fig. 3.2. The LVDTs are mounted on the specimen by wooden plates glued to the surface
and the pins are touching a angled plate also glued to the surface.

3.1.3
Testing procedure
The push-off test is performed horizontally on a test frame that is capable of transferring
horizontal forces to a high strength floor. The test frame setup is seen in fig. 3.3. A
benefit to performing the test horizontally is that there is no need to design a safety feature
for catching the concrete elements once the GFRP fails as would have been need for a
test performed vertically. The specimen is simply supported from horizontal and vertical
movement by roller supports and should be positioned in the center line of the actuator
piston and supports to avoid introducing moments. The supports for the specimen is seen
in fig. 3.4. Masonite plates are placed between the actuator and test specimen as well as
between the roller supports and test specimen to level out any irregularities.
The push-off test is performed using displacement control with a displacement rate of 0.5
mm/min. The load and displacement of the actuator and displacements from the LVDTs
are logged during the test with a data logger and data should be logged as fast as the
equipment allows. The LVDTs must be calibrated prior to testing. The test ends after
breaking the GFRP fully or it is assessed that continued loading will result in damage to
the test setup. After the push-off test is done, the mid-span surface must be made smooth
again and thus angle grinding the remaining GFRP bolt from the finished test if necessary.
The rock host pair may be reused and a new GFRP installed allowing for minimum 3 days
for the grout to cure.
Concrete and grout cylinders are tested to evaluate the compressive strength of the host
rock and grout used for the push-off test at the time of testing.

4.1.1
Meshing the Model
Three dimensional eight-node reduced integration elements (C3D8R) are used to model
all parts. Meshing the model is a balance between accuracy and computational time. In
general, a finer mesh will produce a more accurate result but will at the same time increase
the computational time. A mesh of 2 mm around the area of focus and 10 mm at the outer
part is used. The mesh for the base model can be seen in fig. 4.3 This mesh is found to
be adequate without compromising to much on the accuracy of the results.
Testing the sensitivity of the mesh is done for a 1 mm, 2 mm and 5 mm mesh around
the area of focus and a mesh of 10 mm at the outer parts for all three. A difference in
failure load of 6 % is found between the 1 mm and 2 mm mesh while a 30 % difference is
found between the 2 mm and 5 mm mesh. The difference in failure load from 1 mm to 2
mm mesh is deemed acceptable by the author when counterbalancing the computational
time. However, the large difference in failure load from 2 mm to 5 mm mesh is deemed
to high by the author to be acceptable.

Contact Surfaces
The contact between the GFRP and grout is defined in the Interaction Module as a surface
to surface contact with small sliding. The master and slave surfaces are choosen as the
GFRP and grout respectively. This is done based on the ABAQUS analysis user’s guide
20

which suggests the master surface should be the surface of the stiffer body, which in this
case is the GFRP. The choice of master and slave surfaces has lower effect on the re-
sults with surface to surface contact interaction. This is formulated according to ABAQUS
analysis user’s guide and is also found when testing both cases for the current model.
The small-sliding tracking approach can be used as the sliding of the GFRP along the
grout is relatively small. Furthermore, the small-sliding tracking approach is beneficial for
computational saving and added robustness. The sensitivity of using small-sliding versus
finite-sliding is found to be less than 1 % for the failure load. Finally, the added robustness
in the small-sliding tracking approach is needed for the parametric study of the angles of
shearing. Here, using finite-sliding tracking approach, Abaqus aborts jobs preemptively
whereas the small-sliding tracking approach is able to complete the analysis.
The interaction properties between the grout and GFRP is defined by tangential behavior,
normal behavior, cohesive behavior and damage. Tangential behavior with a penalty
friction formulation is used with a coefficient of friction of 0.5 (Yuan and Muhammad Hadi
2018). The default normal behavior is used with a ”hard” contact pressure-overclosure
relationship in order to ensure minimal penetration of the slave surface into the master
surface. Furthermore, separation after contact is also allowed. The cohesive behavior
is used with uncoupled stiffness coefficients corresponding to the young’s modulus of
the grout. Furthermore, damage with maximum nominal stress is used with the tensile
strength of the grout in both normal and shear direction. Both cohesive behavior and
damage is modelled in order to mimic the cohesion of the grout. Finally, bolts are often
manufactured as threaded bars allowing for mechanical interlocking between the grout
and bar. However the threading is not modelled in order to simplify the model, thus the
ability to study this effect on the model is lost. However, according to L. Li et al. 2016, the
contact surface properties are less important than the interlocking effect of the threading.
To account for this, a higher adhesive strength is suggested.
The grout and host rock is modelled as one part with two separate sections. The contact
between these two materials is not defined explicitly. Instead it implicitly act as normal
behavior not allowing for penetration of one material into the other and tangential behavior
as rough friction formulation not allowing for sliding between the two materials and full
cohesion between the two, not allowing them to separate. Using one of the two methods
described in section 3.1.2 for creating the hole within the host rock will yield a rough
surface and allow for well bonded host rock and grout. However, using a diamond cup
drill or casting the host rock with a smooth insert will not satisfy this assumption. In this
case, the rock bolt and grout bond strength will be higher than that of the host rock and
grout as seen by a pull-out test from L. Li 2016.

Discussion
6.1
Cutting of the GFRP
An issue was encountered when determining the compressive shear failure for bolt incli-
nations. This issue is also reflective of the results obtained, as two different behaviors
was found above and below 65◦bolt inclination. Characteristic for angles above 65◦is
the compressive shear failure of the GFRP happening in the corner between the GFRP
and the joint plane. Whereas, for angles below 65◦the failure happens further into the
host rock. An illustration of the difference can be seen in fig. 6.1.
The position of failure is significant as failure by the corner will have a higher shear stress
contribution than further into the host rock thus failing at lower shear load. This distinction
is significant as crushing of the grout is not modelled. As found from Hovgaard 2019, the
grout around the GFRP by the joint plane is found to crush. Furthermore, the compres-
sion and tension zone is also found to occur further into the host rock from literature on
numerical studies on steel rock bolts (Grasselli 2005; Jalalifar and Naj Aziz 2007).
The maximum compression starting in the corner may be indication of a singularity issue
where the grout should have been crushed and thus not be able to transfer stress. How-
ever, the compression zone is seen to move further into the host rock with decreasing rock
strength when looking at results from varying the host rock strength. This indicates that
the host rock may be too strong compared to the GFRP and restricting the bending of the
bolt. Failure is thus more akin to shear cutting of the bolt as also suggested by Thomas
2019. This problem is more prevalent for high bolt inclinations due to the sharper angle
pushing on the GFRP.
Mimicking crushing of the grout by introducing a slight curve did not correct the problem,
as the failure would now just happen where ever the grout and GFRP came into contact
for the high strength host rock.

6.2
Failure criteria
The original Azzi-Tsai-Hill criterion posed problems with the transverse stresses for the
model. This was made obvious as the corresponding shear load using the original failure
criteria would have been minuscule along with the shear displacement. This problem was
prominent for all parameters studied. The input for stiffness of the GFRP, grout and host
rock was double checked but did not yield any obvious errors. This indicates that either
the used material models or failure criteria does not represent the test adequately. All
failure criteria covering GFRP found in literature took into account the transverse stress
and would all have given a similar outcome. Therefore the failure criteria was modified to
neglect the transverse stresses in order to have some kind of tangible outcome. However
this poses a problem with the reliability of the model and outcome found.
The failure criteria used includes both compressive shear and tensile shear failure modes.
In the parametric study, all were found to fail under compressive shear failure, however
the tensile shear failure is still included in the results. This is because the compressive
strength of fibre reinforced composite varies immensely based on the test method used.
For a similar GFRP, the compressive strength was reported ranging from 520 to 1150
MPa, thus a difference of 75%, based on the test rig used (Soden, Hinton, and Kaddour
2004). Thus the used failure criteria in the model does not necessarily represent the
compressive strength accurately and so the tensile shear failure combination criteria is
also included for comparison.
The in-plane shear strength used is based on the study by Hovgaard 2019, which however
only includes a very limited set of data. However the study is based on a very similar
GFRP and similar test method to the base model. Furthermore, Hovgaard assumes a pure
shear stress state which is not necessarily the case as bending of the GFRP will introduce
other stresses. This shear strength is however used as compared to the shear stress as
reported by Soden, Hinton, and Kaddour 2004, as this would have given corresponding
minuscule shear loads for all parameters studied.

Comparing data across studies can be problematic as the influence of all other factors
affecting the shear behavior must not be neglected. For instance, comparing the shear
load based on the host rock strength from different studies requires all other variables to
be the same as it will otherwise have an influence. Therefore when comparing data from
literature to the model, it is more useful to compare effects seen within a study than the
actual value unless modelling the exact same problem. A very limited amount of data
exist studying the shear behavior of GFRP rock bolts, thus making it hard to validate the
findings in the model.
6.4
Steel versus GFRP rock bolts
Although much of the literature is based in part on steel rock bolts, it is important to note
that GFRP and steel do not behave in the same way. GFRP does not yield but instead
failure is abrupt and shortly after peak load. This characteristic must be emphasized
as shear displacement is dominated by plastic deformation. During the plastic stage it
requires only little additional force to further shear the rock bolt which the GFRP cannot
benefit from. Therefore additional experimental studies using GFRP is needed in order to
properly evaluate the model and the results from the parametric study.

7
Conclusions
A parametric study of the shear behavior for a GFRP rock bolt in a push-off test was
carried out using ABAQUS/Standard. The four factors studied were: i) bolt inclination to
the shearing plane, ii) bolt diameter, iii) strength of the host rock, and iv) width between
shearing planes. Validation of the model was not possible due to lack of experimental
data and concerns over the strength of the GFRP parameters modelled pose a problem
of the reliability of the model. However, the following conclusions are drawn based on the
parametric study:
• Decreasing the bolt inclination was found to increase the shear resistance and the
stiffness of the system.
• The diameter of the rock bolt was found to increase the shear resistance linearly
proportional to the section area of the bolt, while also proportionally increasing the
shear displacement at failure.
• The host rock strength was found to not significantly affect the shear resistance,
except for very low strength rock showing a lower shear resistance. Furthermore,
the stiffness of the system was found to increase with the strength of the host rock.
• Decreasing the joint width was found to increase the shear resistance and stiffness
of the system.
7.1
Recommendations
Based on the research presented in this study, the following recommendations are made
for further study.
• Laboratory tests investigating the influence of the four parameters covered in this
study for validation of results found in the numerical modelling.
• Further research into the failure criteria of GFRP rock bolts in shear loading condi-
tions.
• Laboratory tests considering the long-term mechanical properties of GFRP rock
bolts and its influence on the shear behavior.
