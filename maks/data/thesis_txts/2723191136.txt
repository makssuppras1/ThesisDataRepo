Abstract 
The production of biotherapeutics is rapidly increasing due to the global prevalence of various 
chronic diseases. To ensure safety, efficacy, and potency the production of biotherapeutics re-
quire complex and expensive manufacturing processes. In fact, about 80% of the total costs of 
manufacturing is attributed to the downstream processing steps isolation and purification-
packed resin column chromatography, being the main unit operation used to capture and polish 
product. However, resins are very expensive, their packing requires special expertise, and need 
cleaning and validation procedures. They also exhibit high pressure drops limiting residence 
times and productivity of the process.  
This study presents a novel fibrillated and carded lyocell cellulose nonwoven fabric which has the 
potential to be a high-capacity, high-throughput membrane for chromatography, thus potentially 
replacing packed resin column chromatography. Various cellulose nonwoven fabrics were pro-
vided, with varying basis weights and number of passes through a hydroentangling step during 
their processing: 100 gsm 1pass, 100 gsm 2pass, 125 gsm 1 pass, 125 gsm 2pass, 75 gsm 1pass 
and 75 gsm 3pass (where the 75 gsm samples were manufactured later).  
The mentioned fabrics were characterized by their average fiber size, surface area, thickness, 
basis weight, porosity and relative permeability. The characterization results successfully helped 
understand the fiber size distribution of the fibrillated fibers within the fiber matrix, and lead to 
a rapid engineering and manufacturing of new nonwovens according to the obtained data (man-
ufacturing of the 75 gsm samples). The flow permeability experiments resulted in low pressure 
drops and high relative permeability values between 3.65 x10-14 m2 and 3.21 x10-13 m2.  
Moreover, the membranes were tested for the ability to functionalize them to create a salt-tol-
erant mixed mode chromatographic membrane with cation exchange and hydrophobic interac-
tions capabilities. To achieve this, the nonwoven fabrics were chemically modified through direct 
ligand coupling and through creating polyGMA grafted layers by UV-induced irradiation and by 
heat-induced grafting, with subsequent functionalization to activate the membrane with the 
mixed mode capabilities. Among the three methods used to functionalize the membranes to give 
them binding capabilities, the UV-induced grafting led to the highest SBC = 124.5 ± 9.6 mg/mL 
for 100 gsm 1P sample. The second highest SBC=98.55 ± 23 mg/mL was obtained from the heat-
induced grafting, and finally SBC=13.95 ± 7.4 mg/mL was obtained for the direct coupling method 
for 100 gsm 1P sample.  
The dynamic binding capacities of the later manufactured 75 gsm samples were tested using pIgG 
resulting in DBC10%= 46 mg/mL at RT=0.1 min for 75 gsm 1P and DBC10%= 51 mg/mL at RT=0.1 min 
for 75 gsm 3P.  
The excellent flow properties and high binding capacities confirmed by five consecutive cycles of 
pIgG capture at varying retention times, provide significant evidence that the fibrillated and 
carded lyocell cellulose nonwoven fabrics might be a suitable alternative to packed bed resin 
chromatography, for low-cost, sustainable, higher productivity manufacturing of therapeutic 
mAbs.

1 Introduction 
The global prevalence of chronic and deadly diseases such as cancers, rheumatoid arthritis, 
multiple sclerosis or autoimmune disorders brings attention to the increasing market demand for 
therapeutic proteins (such as monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) and antibody fragments), gene 
vector and vaccine manufacturing in the biopharmaceutical industry. In fact, by 2025, the global 
biopharmaceutical market is expected to hit half a trillion dollars1,2. 
Even though biotherapeutics dominate today’s manufacturing pipelines, their production is 
expensive, placing an enormous burden on patients who need them and on the healthcare 
systems.  
To ensure efficacy, potency and safety of therapeutics, the production of biopharmaceuticals 
requires reliable and efficient separation processes.  
It has been reported that as much as 80% of the biotherapeutics manufacturing costs are 
accredited to the downstream processing isolation and purification steps3. Moreover, packed-
bed column chromatography alone, contributes to a substantial large proportion of the 
manufacturing costs.  
The explosion of new biotherapeutics is leading to new higher industrial requirements for single-
use, more flexible, high-capacity, high-throughput processes that can be easily used for an 
extensive range of biologics4. As a result, the biopharmaceutical industry started searching for 
alternative materials capable of replacing the traditional packed-bed columns. Among the 
researched materials, nonwoven fabrics have revealed to possess several special characteristics 
such as engineered highly porous structures, large surface-to-volume ratios, and the ability to 
control fiber shape, diameter and porosity5. Nonwoven fabrics are fabric-like materials made 
from fibers or web-like structures which are bonded together through heat, chemical or 
mechanical methods – and are neither woven nor knitted, see Figure 16. 
 
Figure 1: Lyocell cellulose nonwoven fabric 
==PAGE:12==
2 
 
Note that the fibers themselves don’t have pores, but the structures obtained from the random 
orientation of the fibers create porous structures within the fiber matrix.  
Ion-exchange nonwoven membrane chromatography was revealed to be a good alternative 
promising several advantages over packed-bed chromatography, including, cheaper to 
manufacture, fast flow-rates, low pressure drops, elimination of cleaning and validation steps for 
single use applications7,8.  
A challenge, however, has been the fact that membranes have low protein binding capacity 
caused by low specific surface areas, compared to the high separation efficiency of traditional 
packed-bed chromatography5.  
Within protein purification, it is very important to have a high binding capacity for the target 
proteins while low non-specific binding for other unwanted proteins (eg. host cell proteins)9.  
For these reasons, a considerable amount of work has been put on studying nonwoven 
membrane chromatography, most of which was on surface functionalization methods for various 
applications and how to maximize the surface area for protein binding8,10–14.  
Previous studies have demonstrated the possibility of building three-dimensional (3D) polymer 
brushes structures containing numerous binding sites onto nonwovens to solve the binding 
capacity issue. This was done by grafting hydrophilic glycidyl methacrylate (GMA) on 
polybutylene terephthalate (PBT) hydrophobic non-woven membranes by attaching ligands 
through the GMA’s epoxy end groups, by using ultraviolet (UV) light irradiation and by heat-
induced polymerization, thus increasing the specific surface area available for binding10,11,13,15,16. 
The results from these studies have shown uniform polyGMA layers on the PBT membranes, with 
good binding capacities.  Even though the performance of ion-exchange membranes is affected 
by the functionalization, membrane characteristics such as fiber diameter, fabric surface area, 
membrane thickness, porosity, as well as 3-dimensional grafting and weight gain of grafted layers 
also have an impact on the overall performance9.  
Lyocell cellulose is a nonwoven fabric material with hydrophilic properties, very sustainable, 
cheap to produce and very importantly, its surface area can be easily increased during its 
manufacture through the process of fibrillation. For this reason, cellulose could potentially 
achieve high productivity and throughput in chromatographic processes. However, its 
characteristics and performance have not yet been documented in literature.  
1.1 Background and motivation 
Some of the issues associated with packed bed chromatography are: 
• The high costs associated with packed bed chromatography are owing to the expensive 
affinity resins, which also require special packing expertise and need cleaning and 
validation after every single purification cycle (buffer usage, time and labor)17–19.  
• Low throughput - long flow paths generate relatively low throughputs because they 
need to be operated at low flowrates, otherwise they would create very high pressure 
drops20. 
==PAGE:13==
3 
 
• High pressure drops - The reason for the high pressure drops, which typically tend to 
increase during the process, can be attributed to bed consolidation (media deformation 
caused by the heterogeneity in the packed bed) and accumulated solute molecules20–22. 
Typical commercial resin packed chromatographic columns have pressure limits at 8 bar, 
but their operation is in the range between 2-4 bar, such as in the case of POROSTM 
Protein A Affinity Resins: MabCaptureTM A and MabCaptureTM A Select or MabSelect 
PrismA23,24. 
• The intraparticle diffusional limitations leading to long residence times in the resin, 
making column chromatography a slow process, thus dampening the productivity 25. 
• Difficulty scaling-up25. 
Membrane chromatography could potentially address these issues due to their advantages: 
• Nonwoven membranes are cheap to manufacture.  
• Nonwoven fabrics offer the ability to adjust several characteristics such as the pore size, 
porosity, fiber diameter, fiber shape and are easy to engineer5.  
• Due to their largely porous structure, they can be operated at high flowrates with low 
pressures and minimized diffusion limitations 5,20,26.  
• 
Membrane chromatography can eliminate the intraparticle diffusion limitation issue since 
the ligands are attached to the surface of the membranes, and thus convective flow will 
lead the solutes directly to the ligand-modified surfaces, rather than the much slower 
intraparticle diffusion which is rate controlling in chromatographic beads, see Figure 
25,20,26. Due to the advantage in mass transfer, membrane chromatography is suitable for 
extracting trace solutes from large capacity feeds, while maintaining their natural 
conformation and biological activity27.  
• Regarding the scaling-up issues, membranes are easy to expand to large-scale compared 
to packed bed systems. Membranes can be rolled up and wound into a spiral module, 
which takes up a very small volume while having a large membrane surface area. In fact, 
membrane adsorber can be easily integrated with already existing chromatographic 
equipment, minimizing investment costs28.  
• Single-use devices – due to their low production cost, nonwovens have the potential to 
be used in the development of disposable devices, thus preventing cross-contamination 
between different batches and eliminating the cleaning-in-place (CIP), steaming-in-place 
(SIP) and validation steps indispensable for the operation of stainless-steel equipment29. 
This would further reduce production costs.  
• Sustainable material with minimal negative environmental impacts.  
==PAGE:14==
4 
 
 
Figure 2: Difference between membranes versus resin bead chromatography. Obtained from22  
However, the main disadvantage of membrane chromatography, compared to resin 
chromatography is the low protein binding capacity caused by the low specific surface area, thus 
restricting their applicability, and making it the bottleneck in various membrane 
applications20,30,31. However, this issue could be solved as well, given that research has shown 
that it is possible to increase the protein binding capacity of membranes by developing 3D 
polymer brushes structures containing numerous binding sites.  
Addressing this disadvantage will be one of the focuses of this report.  
1.2 Nonwoven fabric samples 
Novel fibrillated and carded cellulose nonwoven fabrics manufactured by The Nonwovens 
Institute at the North Carolina State University have been provided to Dr. Ruben Carbonell’s 
research group with the scope of conducting research and understanding the characteristics of 
these novel fabrics within protein purification applications. 
Cellulose is a polysaccharide consisting of a linear chain of many glucose units, and is commonly 
found in the walls of plant cells where it serves as a structural component32. Lyocell is an eco-
friendly form of regenerated cellulose obtained by dissolving wood pulp and dry jet-wet spinning, 
which will be further explained later in the report. Fibrillation is part of the manufacturing process 
and is responsible for increasing the surface area of the fibers by breaking them down into finer 
ones33. Fibrillation is done by passing the membrane through high speed water jets, a process 
also called hydroentangling. The number of times that the membrane is passed through this 
process refers to number of passes “P” or 1P, 2P as it will be referred to throughout the report. 
Lastly carding is the process through which the fibers are untangled and aligned in one direction 
parallel to each other making spinning process smoother33. This process impacts the basis weight 
of the fabric g/m2, also referred to as gsm throughout the report. 
The novel cellulose nonwoven fabrics provided by The Nonwovens Institute are the following: 1P 
100 gsm, 2P 100 gsm, 1P 125 gsm, 2P 125 gsm and later 1P 75 gsm and 3P 75 gsm.

1.3 Scope 
The scopes of this report are: 
1. The characterization of the physical properties of the provided novel cellulose 
nonwoven fabrics, including surface area per mass of fabric (m2/g), basis weight 
(g/m2), thickness (mm), average fiber size (µm), overall porosity (%) and pressure drop 
vs flowrate (relative permeability) relations.  
The first round of samples provided are 100 gsm, 2P 100 gsm, 1P 125 gsm, 2P 125 gsm, which 
were manufactured in the same batch.   
Then based on their characterization, an assessment of whether the initial membranes met the 
requirements for use as membranes for bio separations or not is done, and further collaboration 
with the NWI to make samples with properties that better address these needs is arranged. As a 
result of this assessment, the samples 75 gsm 1P and 3P were later manufactured and provided 
for further characterization studies.  
2. After the characterization is completed, it is of interest to develop a salt-tolerant 
mixed mode chromatographic membrane with cation exchange and hydrophobic 
interaction capabilities.  
2.1 This scope will be done by using two techniques: (1) direct coupling of a ligand to 
the surface (epichlorohydrin), and (2) create a grafted polyGMA layer that can 
increase the volume available for binding (through ultraviolet (UV) grafting and 
heat-induced grafting). The grafting will be followed by subsequent 
functionalization of the grafted layers to create the mixed mode chromatographic 
membrane (with MPCA). 
2.2 Later, the binding performance studies (static and dynamic binding capacities) are 
done using polyclonal immunoglobulin G (pIgG) as a model to the expensive 
therapeutic counterpart monoclonal IgG, which are widely manufactured for 
biotherapeutic applications. 
Therefore, this project is mainly divided into two parts (1) the characterization of the lyocell 
cellulose nonwoven fabrics and (2) testing of the nonwoven membranes in terms of their grafting 
abilities and binding capacities as a mixed mode chromatography membrane. Completion of this 
project will lead to a more thorough understanding of the properties of the lyocell cellulose 
nonwoven fabrics and their potential applications in industrial bio separations. 
2 Theory  
2.1 Common monoclonal antibody downstream purification process 
Nowadays, most protein therapeutics are produced through fermentation by genetically 
modified microorganisms, mammalian cell cultures, from pooled human plasma or transgenic 
plants 34–36. In view of the fact that these drugs are for human consumption and patients are 
exposed to them for long periods of time, it is of crucial importance that those drugs are of 
highest purity7. The high safety standards require elaborate downstream processing such as 
==PAGE:16==
6 
 
isolation and purification procedures to ensure complete removal of host cell proteins (HCP), 
endotoxins, prions, or any other type of contaminant from the final product. These elaborate 
isolation and purification procedures include cell homogenization, centrifugation, filtration, and 
column chromatography (affinity, ion exchange and/or size exclusion).  
 
Figure 3: Monoclonal antibody (mAb) downstream purification process flow diagram. Obtained from37 
See Figure 3 for the typical mAb downstream purification process flow diagram.  
In a typical mAb downstream process, after the mAbs are secreted by mammalian cells in the 
bioreactor, a few harvest steps are done in order to remove any cell debris, thus resulting in a 
clarified fluid. Harvest steps generally comprise of centrifugation and/or depth filtration. Most 
mAbs purification processes include a capture/recovery step using affinity chromatography 
(protein-A based). Protein A chromatography is one of the most used affinity chromatography 
methods within biomanufacturing. The technique relies on the reversible and specific binding 
between the immobilized Protein A ligand and mAbs38. This step yields a relatively high degree 
of purity and recovery of mAbs from the clarified solution. To further increase the purity of the 
resulting mAbs, anion and/or cation exchange chromatography are used to remove remaining 
contaminants, HCP, DNA or viruses. Low pH holds and viral nanofiltration are common viral-
inactivation steps involved in the downstream purification processes to ensure viral clearance. 
Last, but not least, the purified mAbs are concentrated using ultrafiltration and diafiltration steps 
into the final formulation buffer37,39. 
Depending on if the proteins are expressed intracellularly or extracellularly or if they are in the 
form of inclusion bodies, there might be additional purification steps required. Based on 
economic analysis of manufacturing of biotherapeutics, it has been revealed that isolation and 
purification steps in the downstream processing account for as much as 80% of the overall 
production costs3.  
==PAGE:17==
7 
 
Several studies have investigated how to reduce the costs of biomanufacturing. Through genetic 
modifications, cell culture growth conditions optimization, and protein expression techniques are 
used to reduce the number and concentration of side-products (contaminants) and  increase the 
titer of the target proteins in the cell culture37. Common titer levels of mAbs in mammalian cells 
(70% of mAbs are produced in Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) mammalian cells), are in the range 
of 0.04 to 13 g/L40. In perfusion reactors, the product is concentrated through the continuous 
removal of medium, so that the titers are even higher, such as 27 g/L 40. It is desirable to have 
high titers, however this also leads to significant financial stress being put on the downstream 
purification operations. In an attempt to reduce these costs, the industry is looking for and testing 
new approaches to reduce the number of separation steps, increase the yield and purity and 
reduce costs associated with operating the separation devices.  
To reduce the costs of the downstream purification processing in large-scale industrial 
applications, it is of great interest to focus on the isolation and purification steps, since they cause 
the highest costs. It is therefore desired to implement inexpensive and robust affinity ligands thus 
replacing the very expensive protein-A based affinity chromatography, for mAbs manufacturing, 
for example37. 
There is also a strong interest in the development of inexpensive separation devices such as 
membrane filtration and membrane chromatography. In fact membranes have already been 
used in downstream filtration processes to separate cells and cell debris through microfiltration, 
to exchange buffers or concentrate product through diafiltration and ultrafiltration, and also to 
remove other contaminants and viruses through nanofiltration37. They have also been used in 
the development of novel membrane devices for capture and purification chromatography 
(capturing of either contaminants or product)37.  
The traditional packed bed chromatography presents several disadvantages such as diffusion 
limitations, large pressure drops across the column, low throughputs, high costs of resins, and 
trouble scaling up. Membrane chromatography is a promising alternative to reducing the 
intraparticle diffusions which cause significant transport resistance in packed bed 
chromatography, and enhance the dynamic binding capacities, especially when separating large 
molecules such as antibodies37. The pressure drops across membranes are lower compared to 
across packed beds, owing to the larger porous structures and high permeabilities, thus allowing 
higher flowrates and consequently higher product throughputs. Moreover, membrane 
chromatography can be easily scaled up37.  
In order to reduce costs associated with the production of protein therapeutics, the industry is 
advancing towards the implementation of disposable bioreactor technologies which come in 
presterilized bags containing stirrers, measuring ports (for pH, temperature, oxygen levels), and 
all the required accompanying  equipment needed for carrying out a microbial fermentation or 
cell culture37. The advantages of single use technologies are that the bags can be discarded after 
use eliminating CIP, sterilization and validation steps which would otherwise be required when 
using stainless steel reactors. In fact, chromatographic setups are too expensive to be discarded 
after single use, thus such disposable devices would bring a breakthrough in industry and lead to 
major cost reductions37.

2.2 Biopharmaceutical market and downstream protein purification 
Polyclonal and monoclonal antibodies, recombinant proteins, plasmid DNAs and vaccines belong 
to the group of biopharmaceuticals which have experienced an enormous segment increase 
within the pharmaceutical market due to their need in fighting life threatening and disabling 
diseases.  
In 2021 the biopharmaceutical market size stood at 389.3 billion USD and is expected to reach 
720.8 billion USD by 2023 with a CAGR growth of 7.1%41. Due to the prevalence of increasing 
chronic diseases, the global demand for biotherapeutics is increasing rapidly with hundreds of 
potential new products currently undergoing clinical trials41.  
As mentioned earlier high purity of biopharmaceuticals is a must due to safety and health of 
patients, therefore numerous processing steps are involved in the downstream protein recovery 
and purification processes. Packed bed chromatography is the main workhorse amongst the 
other steps in the downstream processing, due to its high resolution separation, high binding 
capacities and reliability7. The porous resins are commonly functionalized with affinity ligands, 
hydrophobic groups, or ion exchange capabilities, making them a versatile and efficient method 
for separations. Meanwhile, due to the high costs of resins (costs up to 20,000 USD per liter), 
packed bed chromatography is the most expensive unit operation29.  
Not only that, but it also has large pressure drops, low throughput, intricate scaling-up procedure, 
large amounts of buffer consumption and time-consuming validation and regulation 
procedures7,42.  
The known large pressure drops caused by the resins dictate the design of the columns to be 
wide in diameter and short in terms of height thus reducing the flow path, and the packing is 
laborious and time-consuming, requiring specific expertise. Additionally, filtration and 
centrifugation of the culture fluid is absolutely necessary prior to the packed bed 
chromatography step to prevent clogging the column with cells or debris, which would otherwise 
cause even higher pressure drops.  
Moreover, due to the small size of the pores within the resins (~100 nm), larger molecules only 
bind onto the external surface area of the resins, which in fact accounts for a small fraction of 
their total surface area. Therefore, packed bed chromatography usually has low binding capacity 
for macromolecules37,43. To solve this issue, resins without pores, but small diameters can be 
used instead, however they too result in very large pressure drops37.  
Packed bed chromatography columns are used over a hundred cycles before being repacked 
again in industry, due to the fact that resins are very expensive and not appropriate for single-
use purposes. Considerable amounts of buffer are used for cycle of regeneration, accompanied 
by validation and regulatory costs associated with cleaning, sterilization and packing37.

Many alternative methods have been employed to overcome the limitations imposed by packed 
bed chromatography, such as precipitation, crystallization, aqueous two-phase extraction, high 
performance tangential flow filtration, and membrane chromatography5. Amongst these 
methods, membrane chromatography is a promising alternative to overcoming the limitations of 
packed bed chromatography.  
2.3 Membrane Chromatography in protein purification 
2.3.1 Fundamentals of membrane chromatography 
To better understand how membrane chromatography works, its fundamentals will be explained. 
The membrane by itself does not have binding capabilities, therefore its surface is modified with 
affinity ligands or ion exchange charged groups to target specific proteins. During the separation, 
convective flow brings the feed solution containing solutes to the surface of the membrane, thus 
the separation is based on the adsorption of target proteins onto the surface while the rest flows 
through, see Figure 4.  
 
Figure 4: The fundamentals of membrane chromatography. Obtained from 44 
The elution of the bound proteins is caused either by a change in the ionic strength of the buffer 
(ion exchange) or by a change in hydrophobicity (in hydrophobic interaction chromatography), 
for example. 
Regarding the pressure drops in membrane chromatography are normally low due to their overall 
high porous structures created by the fiber matrix31. This also enables the binding of larger 
macromolecules (Mw>250,000 Da) to have access to the binding sites on their surfaces31,45.  
Additionally, adsorptive membranes are easier and cheap to manufacture, which makes them 
great to be used in disposable devices.  
2.4 Typical membrane adsorbers 
To be able to successfully utilize membranes within bio separation applications it is crucial to 
choose the right membrane supports, and strategy for the covalent coupling of ligands onto their 
==PAGE:20==
10 
 
surface. A membrane support should fulfill the following criteria: (1) a porous structure to allow 
the interaction of the target proteins with the ligands; (2) a hydrophilic and neutral surface to 
impede nonspecific interactions of the target proteins with the membrane; (3) accessible reactive 
groups for further attachment of charged groups or specific ligands; (4) a mechanical structure 
to provide stability for withstanding transmembrane pressures; and (5) inexpensive fabrication 
to enable easy industrial application46.  
Various materials have been used to manufacture adsorptive membranes, namely cellulose, 
polyamide, polysulfone, polyethylene, polypropylene (PP), amongst others46,47.  
Cellulose has long been studied due to its hydrophilicity and ease of coupling of affinity ligands 
to its hydroxyl groups48.  Polyamide has the advantage of having mechanical rigidity and narrow 
pore size distribution, however their disadvantage is that they display non-specific protein 
adsorption and low numbers of terminal amino groups. Polysulfone has good film-forming 
properties, thermal resistance, good biological compatibility and is chemically stable. 
Polyethylene, PP and polyester materials can be used to manufacture adsorptive membranes 
through two steps: (1) grafting of a copolymer containing reactive groups onto the surface of the 
porous membrane succeeded by (2) coupling of functional groups (step also called 
functionalization)15,44,47. 
2.5 Interactions modes and configurations 
Membranes can have various adsorptive mechanisms, acting as anion or cation exchangers, 
affinity, hydrophobic or reverse phase chromatography.  
Anion exchange membranes have strong (quaternary ammonium) and weak (diethylamine, DEA) 
basic functionalities. On the other hand, cation exchangers have strong acidic (sulfonic acid), and 
weak acidic (carboxylic acid) groups attached9,10.  
Affinity membranes have been coupled with a wide range of ligands, such as protein-A or protein-
G, and low-molecular mass ligands37.  
Hydrophobic membranes have been studied by attaching -C4H9, -C8H17 and phenyl groups.  
Various configurations of chromatographic membranes have been studied, depending on their 
applications, such as stacked membranes, hollow fibers and radial flow membranes, see Figure

Regarding the choice of target protein used in the experiments, the reasons why pIgG was chosen

Cation exchange is a chromatographic system where the charge of the stationary ligand is 
negative and the net charge of the molecule of interest is positive, given that opposite charges 
attract each other.  
 
Figure 6: Net charge of a protein depending on its pI and pH 
This means that during the equilibration and sample application steps the pH of the buffer should 
be smaller than the pI of pIgG. This will ensure that the net charge of pIgG will be positive, thus 
binding to the negative ligand on the cellulose membrane.  
The elution step in a cation exchange system can be done through two mechanisms (1) changing 
the pH of the buffer and (2) changing the salt concentration in the buffer.  
The mechanism (1) is based on the fact that when the pH increases above the pI of pIgG, its net 
charge will change to negative. Given that the cation ligand which is attached onto the nonwoven 
is also negative, these two charges will repel each other, leading to the release and elution of 
pIgG.  
The mechanism (2) is based on increasing the salt concentration in the buffer. This can be 
achieved by adding sodium chloride, where Na+ has a positive charge and Cl- has a negative 
charge. By increasing the amount of salt, there will be more Na+ ions than pIgG, and these will 
bind to the cation ligand which is attached onto the nonwoven, thus releasing and eluting the 
pIgG.

However, one of the issues with using ion exchange chromatography, is that often a desalting or 
dilution step are necessary to ensure proper binding of the target protein to the ion exchanger 
ligand. To avoid doing these steps, salt-tolerant ion exchange ligands have recently been 
introduced. This means that the binding of the target proteins will work at higher salt 
concentrations compared to traditional ion exchange53.  
As mentioned, traditionally in ion-exchange the target proteins are bound onto the column 
followed by their elution through increased salt concentrations. What this means is that the 
fermentation media would need to be salt-free prior to the exchange chromatography step, 
adding to the costs and time required for production. It is important to understand that in 
normal, natural environments (such as the body or plants) or in fermentation medias, most 
biomolecules are in the presence of salts. Thus, desalting or dilution steps might cause 
degradation or inactivate the target proteins53.  
Therefore salt-tolerant ion exchange chromatography allows a more straight-forward process 
with less steps and costs53.  
Hydrophobic interaction chromatography is a chromatographic system where the target protein 
is introduced into the column in a high-salt buffer. The hydrophobic amino acids in proteins form 
protected hydrophobic areas when folded in aqueous solutions, allowing the hydrophilic areas 
at the surface to interact with the hydrogen atom of water molecules. When salt is added into 
the aqueous solution it promotes the interaction of the water molecules with the salt ions, 
instead of the hydrophilic parts of the proteins. Thus, the addition of salt promotes interaction 
between the hydrophobic and hydrophilic surface regions of the ligand and the target protein 
(where hydrophobic binds hydrophobic, and vice-versa) thus leading to selective separation.  
==PAGE:24==
14 
 
This means that during the equilibration and sample application steps a high salt concentration 
buffer is desired since this will expose the hydrophobic regions of the ligand on the membrane 
while the water molecules bind to the salt ions. This will facilitate the adsorption of the 
hydrophobic regions of the pIgG to the hydrophobic areas of the ligand on the membrane, see 
Figure 8. 
The elution step in the hydrophobic interaction chromatography system is then done through 
lowering the salt concentration of the buffer, which will lead the water molecules to bind to the 
hydrophilic areas of the ligand, thus releasing and eluting the target protein pIgG.  
 
Figure 8: Hydrophobic interaction chromatography steps: sample application and elution 
Thus, in mixed mode chromatography, the sample application is based on (1) opposite charges 
of ligand/target protein and (2) the presence of salt due to hydrophobic interactions. The elution 
is a combination of the cation exchange strategy (1) charge of protein (due to pH and pI), (2) 
presence of salt (increase causes elution), and hydrophobic interaction strategy (3) presence of 
salt (decrease causes elution).  
In order to have a mixed mode chromatography, optimized conditions for each of the strategies 
in the sample application and elution steps are very important to achieve a selective separation. 
Moreover, it could be that one of the chromatography techniques is more dominant than the 
other, depending on the target protein.

2.7 Nonwovens  
Nonwovens are defined as an engineered fibrous mesh, which has been given a specifically 
designed structural integrity by the use of physical and/or chemical techniques, excluding knitting 
or waving6.  
Depending on their application, nonwovens can be either disposable or durable and their main 
functions include absorbency, bacterial barrier, filtering, liquid repellency, softness, sterility, 
amongst others. Several of these properties can be combined together to create fabrics meant 
for given purposes. Some of the common products made with nonwovens are: filters, disposable 
diapers, civil engineering fabrics/geosynthetics, insulation, sanitary napkins, wall coverings, 
wipes, amongst many others6. 
Due to the vast application and product range, the global nonwoven fabrics market size has been 
valued at 41.5 billion USD in 2021, and is estimated to reach 67.93 billion USD by 2030, with an 
increasing CAGR of 5.6% between 2022 and 203054.  
2.7.1 Manufacturing of nonwovens 
Nonwoven fabrics are made by firstly forming a web of fibers of continuous filaments, followed 
by their bonding to secure and maintain the structure. Another additional step that is not always 
used or necessary is the fabric finishing to engineer the specific fabric properties. At last, an array 
of processes will convert the nonwoven into a multitude of finished products as mentioned 
above.  
The techniques for the formation of the nonwoven fibers can be divided into three major 
categories: drylaid, wetlaid and spunlaid. The drylaid materials originate from textiles, wetlaid 
materials from papermaking and the spunlaid products from polymer extrusion and plastics.  
Following the formation of the fibers, they need to be bonded. The bonding takes place through 
either mechanical, chemical solvent and/or thermal processes. Mechanical bonding includes 
needlepunching, stitchbonding and hydroentanglement (by using water jets). Chemical bonding 
works by adding adhesive binders to the webs via spraying, printing, foaming or saturating 
techniques. The solvent bonding softens or partially dissolves fibers using a solvent which will 
create a self-bonded structure. Thermal bonding uses heat and sometimes pressure to fuse or 
weld the fibers together and is often applied after other bonding techniques to obtain smoother 
fabric surfaces.  
The degree of bonding will impact the fabric strength, porosity, softness, flexibility, and density. 
Normally bonding is carried out “in-line” with the web formation, however it can also be done 
on its own as a separate operation. Utilizing several bonding processes is also a common 
procedure. 
The different processes used in the production of nonwovens can be seen in Appendix A6.  
2.7.2 Raw Materials 
Natural fibers such as wool, silk and cotton have been long used as clothing materials for the 
human body. While wool and silk are composed of polymeric chains of amino acids, cotton is

as nylon, Dacron or polyester, have evolved to meet needs of modern society55. Dacron, nylon

As the global population increased so did the demands for multifunctional textile materials made

PBT has a low manufacturing cost, high tensile strength, is stable to heat, and it is easier to modify 
compared to other materials. Additionally, PBT has also been approved for use in products that

2.7.3 Production of lyocell cellulose fibers  
Lyocell fibers were first developed in 1980 by Courtlaulds Fibers in the UK and later on by Lenzing 
AG in Austria63. Since cellulose cannot be melted directly to form fibers, as it will melt, the 
processing requires a dissolution step. Several solvents are available for direct dissolution of 
cellulose, however N-methyl morpholine-N-oxide (NMMO) is known to dissolve cellulose forming 
spinnable polymer matrixes, without any further addition of chemicals64,65.  
The general processing starts by swelling and dissolving oak or birch wood pulp in NMMO solvent, 
forming a slurry. The slurry is then filtered, and spun on a conveyer, by blowing air through 
spinnerets onto a conveyer, turning the cellulose into thin, long fibers. An optional step is to card 
the fibrous webs, meaning that the fibers are aligned parallel to each other in the direction that 
the web is formed.  
This is followed by the washing step, where hydroentanglement through high velocity water jets 
will bond the fibrous webs by interlocking and entangling the fibers about each other. 
Hydroentanglement causes fibrillation, by breaking the larger fibers into smaller and finer ones 
which are supported by the large fibers.  
Depending on the desired level of fibrillation hydroentanglement can be done in numbers of 
passes – one pass, two passes, three passes, and so on. Each pass is one full coverage of the 
entire nonwoven surface by the water jets. The mechanism of hydroentanglement can be seen 
in Figure 10. The degree of fibrillation will impact several properties of the fabric, such as the 
opacity, permeability, tensile and tear strength.  
Moreover, the hydroentanglement process also determines the basis weight of the fabric in 
terms of kilograms of fabric per square meter of membrane.

high reading. Therefore, it is important to have a foot with the fit size appropriate for the samples 
under study.

happens to be placed onto a thicker spot of the fabric, the measured thickness will be different 
than if it were placed onto a thinner spot of the fabric. Any heterogeneities in the smoothness of 
the fabric will cause variations in thickness.  
For this reason, is important to have many replicate measurements from various spots of the

2.8.6.3 Bimodal distribution 
The fiber structure and size have an important impact on permeability and performance of the 
membrane in terms of binding capacities.  
Regarding the fiber structure, fibrillation plays a big role in the permeability of fibrillated 
nonwoven fabrics.  
==PAGE:36==

Various analytical, numerical and experimental works have been published on studying the 
permeability of unimodal fiber diameter systems, but there are no easy-to-use expressions for 
media with bimodal fiber diameter distributions. The reality is that a big proportion of fibrous 
filters and membranes are made up of coarse and fine fibers with varying diameters.  
In such systems, the fine fibers contribute to a high filtration efficiency (influencing pressure drop 
too), while the coarse fibers provides overall mechanical rigidity82. The higher the fibrillation, the 
higher the flow resistance in the sections that have smaller fibers. Thus, if the membrane is not 
fibrillated homogeneously, or just not fibrillated enough, the fluid will take the path of least 
resistance and easily flow through the areas with less fibrillation (coarse fibers), see Figure 15. 
For this reason, the Blake-Kozeny equation cannot just be used, as mentioned earlier.  
Perhaps the reason why there are no simple expressions to predict pressure drops in bimodal 
distribution media is due to the high number of independent but coupled variables, such as 
species mass fractions, solid volume fraction, pressure drops, number of coarse and fine fibers 
and radii of coarse and fine fibers and limitation to 2D models with rows of regular fiber 
arrangements82–85. Plus the heterogeneity in the orientation of the fibers should be taken into 
account in a 3D model 82.  
Fortunately, Vahedi Tafreshi et al. has tried to find a unimodal equivalent diameter for a bimodal 
medium through solving the air flow field in a series of 3D geometries, thus enabling the 
permeability of bimodal media82. His work was simulation based, for Reynold numbers smaller 
than 1 to ensure Stokes flow was dominant (laminar flow regime). It is also important to note 
that at Reynolds numbers lower than one, there is a linear relationship between the flow velocity 
and pressure drops86.

2.9 Nonwovens advancements in recovery and purification processes – a review 
Several studies have been conducted to investigate the capacity and throughput of product 
capture of nonwovens with applications within the biotechnology field.  
Burger C. et al. suggested that membranes consisting of small diameter fibers and interconnected 
pores have a unique advantage because they imply a higher permeability, which can also 
withstand fouling better88. Moreover, Ma Z. et al. has demonstrated that electrospun 
polyethersulfone affinity membrane is an ideal spin column packing material for fast protein 
purification with comparable IgG binding capacity of 4.5 mg/mL related to other affinity 
membranes. He also noted lower pressure drops as a result of the larger pore sizes 89.  
Menkhaus T. J., et al. showed that due to lower operating pressures and reduced intra-particle 
diffusion, flow rates during membrane adsorption have the capability of being several orders of 
magnitude higher than in packed bed absorption. However, he noted that membrane systems 
have very low adsorption capacities due to their small binding surface areas. Therefore, he tested 
the possibility to surface functionalize nanofibers with 3D-nanolayers by atom transfer radical 
polymerization (ATRP) creating selective adsorption sites, and consequently a larger binding 
surface area. His results showed that the functionalized nanofibers had substantially higher 
adsorption capacities compared to resin beads, while maintaining fast adsorption kinetics and 
lower pressure drops90.  
These promising studies led to further research on the potential applications of nonwoven 
membranes within the recovery and purification of biotherapeutics, with the focus of increasing 
the available specific surface areas for binding.  
Inexpensive non-woven membranes such as PP and PBT received much attention and interest for 
this purpose due to their interconnected porous structures, high surface area and low cost of 
manufacturing. And they have been used to make wound dressings, 3-D scaffolds for cartilage 
and bone regeneration, drug delivery and filtration and separation devices88,91–94. 
Matsumoto H. et al. also successfully developed cation and anion exchange fibers through 
sulfonation and quaternization of polystyrene and poly(4-vinlylpyridine) fibers, respectively. His 
experiments resulted in novel functional nonwoven fabric material with large adsorption 
capacity95. 
Surface initiated polymerization turned out to be a useful technique for introducing polymer 
layers with diverse surface properties according to their desired applications11,96. However, the 
issue with the aforementioned methods is that they required surface modification methods such 
as plasma treatment, γ-ray irradiation or high-energy electron beam, which can be complex and 
require complex instrumentation97–99. A much easier and inexpensive method is the use of UV 
irradiation grafting or heat-induced grafting, which have both been reported in literature5,15.  
==PAGE:39==
29 
 
Heller et al. has successfully grafted PBT nonwovens with polyGMA using heat-grafting with 
benzoyl peroxide as (Bz2O2) as a thermal initiator 15. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images 
showed a complete, uniform and conformal grafted layers on the PBT fibers, which could very 
easily be further functionalized as ions exchangers for protein capture. Equilibrium binding 
capacities of 200 mg/g for hIgG were achieved for a 20% weight gain using the heat induced 
grafting method15.  
2.9.1  High-capacity studies – state-of-the art 
As previously mentioned protein binding capacity is one of the most important parameter to take 
into account when choosing a media to capture and polish a protein using a stationary phase 
(resins or membranes)100. This parameter is very important because it tells how much membrane 
is needed to purify a certain amount of protein. In other words, this means that the binding 
capacity dictates how much protein can be purified by a specific mass or volume of membrane.  
There are two measures for binding capacity namely static binding capacity (SBC) and dynamic 
binding capacity (DBC).  
SBC is often also referred to as total protein capacity and is typically measured in batch mode 
inside a beaker. SBC is normally reported as the maximum amount of protein bound to a 
chromatographic membrane at a specific protein concentration and given solvents. To be able to 
obtain the maximum protein binding capacity, an excess of protein is loaded or mixed inside a 
beaker. The SBC varies significantly depending on the specific protein used in the studies100.  
On the other hand, DBC represents the maximum amount of target protein that can be loaded 
onto a column under operating conditions before a significant breakthrough of unbound material 
takes place. The DBC is measured using realistic experimental conditions, such as using a sample 
with the target protein of interest and applicable flowrates. DBC is determined by loading the 
sample with a known concentration of the target protein and then following and recording the 
flow-through. The protein will start binding to the membrane until reaching a certain break point 
(saturation of the membrane) before unbound protein will start flowing out through the 
column100.

2.10 Grafting mechanisms 
2.10.1 UV-grafting 
UV-light based polymerization is used heavily in commercial applications because it is more 
robust and simpler compared to other grafting mechanisms. However, one of its limitations is 
the penetration depth of the light source. This means that nonwoven fabrics that are thick, or 
have small pore structures, will not get sufficient grafting in the depth of the material caused by 
the lack of UV-light penetration15.  
The reason why GMA is vastly used for UV-grafting polymerization is due to its epoxy end groups 
which enable the covalent attachment of a vast number of chemical ligands such as carboxylic 
acids, amines, amongst others106. Moreover, the GMA polymerization conforms to the curvature 
of the fabric mat, resulting in a uniform and consistent thickness of the grafted polymer layer 
throughout the fabric.  
UV-grafted polymerization of polyGMA also requires a photoinitiator, in this case benzophenone 
(BP), which undergoes homolysis in the presence of the UV-light. This creates a radical which can 
remove a proton from the polymeric nonwoven surface, and thus creating an initiation site for 
the polymerization to take place31,107–109, see Figure 18 and Figure 19.

Therefore a 20% weight gain is desired for the grafting experiments throughout the report.  
2.10.2 Heat-induced grafting  
Thermally induced surface polymerization is a technique that can graft polymer layers on 
nonwovens independent of pore size, shape, thickness, or morphology of material, not as in the 
case of UV-grafting. In fact, heat-induced vinyl polymerization is used industrially to make bulk 
polymerizations and has recently also been tested on nonwoven membranes for applications in 
bio separations.  
Similarly, to UV-grafting, heat-induced grafting also requires an initiator (thermal), such as 
potassium persulfate or benzoyl peroxide. The solution is usually let to sit for an hour allowing 
the initiator to adsorb onto the surface of the membrane. Following the adsorption step, it is 
important to increase the temperature of the GMA solution, to allow the decomposition of the 
initiator into its radical form, thus creating initiation sites at the surface of the membrane and 
allowing polymerization to take place.  
A study done by Heller et al. tested the GMA monomer concentrations of 5% (v/v), 10% (v/v), 
30% (v/v) and 40% (v/v). His results showed that at 5% (v/v) no grafting was observed and at 10% 
(v/v) only 5% weight gain of polyGMA was obtained. At 30% (v/v) the grafting resulted in 20% 
weight gain of polyGMA, and for polymerization times of up to 2 h, the extent of grafting over 
time demonstrated a linear relationship, suggesting a first order rate of grafting as a function of 
monomer concentration. On the other hand, at 40% (v/v) a very rapid and uncontrolled bulk 
polymerization took place leading to a complete solidification of the grafting solution. Other 
studies have also observed the same phenomena, where above a certain GMA concentration 
threshold the polymerization in solution takes over the polymerization onto the surface of the 
fabric15,112,113.  
Moreover, the same study showed that increasing the temperature during the grafting increases 
the rate of decomposition of the thermal initiator. Three different temperatures were tested and 
the results showed that at 70°C the grafting is very slow with only 8% weight gain, and at 80°C 
and 90°C the polymerization is much faster and capable of achieving 20% weight gains in 
approximately 3h for 80°C and 2h for 90°C15.  
Therefore, the best parameters for heat-grafting are with 30% (v/v) GMA solution and at 80°C.  
2.10.3 Comparison 
When comparing these two methods, based on polyGMA PBT grafted nonwovens, the rates of 
protein adsorption are several times faster, reaching equilibrium within minutes for the heat-
grafted method. UV grafted polyGMA nonwovens require hours to reach equilibrium. This means 
==PAGE:45==
35 
 
that the polyGMA grafted layers formed by heat induced grafting, are thinner, more dense, 
compared to UV grafted layers at equivalent % weight gains15.   
Grafting by UV radiation based free radical polymerization is known to create polymer brushes 
which are anchored onto the fiber surface, and these tend to be very linear and flexible, like a 
tentacle. Due to the rearrangement capabilities of the polymer brushes, a 3D binding 
environment is created, which allows proteins to pack efficiently throughout the whole volume 
of the grafted layer15.  
However, grafting by heat induced free radical polymerization is not as controlled. Compared to 
the UV method, heat induced polymerization is characterized by higher rates of chain transfer, 
which consequently leads to highly branched polymer chains and particularly cross-linked 
polymer networks15, as can be seen at the bottom of Figure 20.  
Moreover, a visual comparison using SEM images of cross sections of PBT fibers grafted with both 
UV and heat induced grafting, clearly showed a distinct separation between the polyGMA layer 
and the PBT fiber for the UV grafted method. However, this distinction was not visible for the 
heat grafted nonwoven, which could be explained if the density of the heat grafted polyGMA 
layer was very similar to that of the PBT fiber, and thus unable to be distinguished by the SEM 
microscope.

As was mentioned previously, one of the limitations of the UV-grafting method is the penetration 
depth that the light source can reach within the fabric. Whilst heat induced grafting allows the 
grafting of materials that are thicker and more complex.  
For both grafting methods, it has also been shown that increasing the molecular weight of the 
target protein leads to a decrease of molecules bound, for identical degrees of weight gains.

resulting polymer matrix has less accessible binding volume, a stiffer structure which blocks 
efficient packing of proteins or, smaller pore structures that are simply out of reach for larger 
proteins. The reason for this could be the increased degree of polymer branching and cross-
linking of GMA which does not take place with the UV induced grafting method15.  
2.11  Functionalization 
After a desirable grafting degree is obtained, it is crucial to activate the surface by functionalizing 
it, to reduce nonspecific protein adsorption and to enable covalent attachment of ion exchange 
or ligands to adsorb the target product or contaminant8.  
The epoxy end groups of polyGMA can covalently bind to various chemical groups, depending on 
the desired functionality, to create mixed mode chromatographic membranes for bio separa-
tions.  
In fact, the activation of the epoxy groups onto the polyGMA grafted membrane to create a 
cation-exchange membrane has been proved successful with sodium sulfite (Na2SO3) and 
iminodiacetic acid (IDA) 4,9.  
However, for creating a mixed mode chromatography membrane with both cation and 
hydrophobic interactions capabilities, a ligand with more than one functional group is needed. 
Therefore 2-Mercaptopyridine-3-carboxylic acid (MPCA) is chosen for this purpose, see Figure 
21.

and a pyridine ring; C-binding of MPCA to the epoxy group of GMA thus creating a mixed mode 
chromatography ligand with cation and hydrophobic interaction capabilities. 
Please see Figure 22 for the illustration of how the MPCA is used as a multi modal ligand.  
The carboxyl group on the MPCA acts as a cation exchange ligand. Given that the pKa of carboxylic 
acid is ≈5 and the buffer pH=5.5 (ph>pKa), ionization will take place and the  COO- group will be 
available for binding to pIgG, due to the loss of the hydrogen atom and a net negative charge.

Both equilibration, sample application and elution buffers have 150 mM NaCl, thus making the

In the elution step, the pH is increased to pH=9, ionizing pIgG and thus leading to a net negative 
charge. This will release pIgG from the MPCA carboxyl group, since they will both have net 
negative charges.  
The equal presence of salt in all buffers (150 mM NaCl in both equilibration and elution) makes 
this system salt-tolerant, and it allows the system to be selectively separate for hydrophobic 
molecules, besides enabling the pyridine group to be hydrophobic.  
This also means that in this case, the cation exchange interactions are dominant over the 
hydrophobic interactions. In other words, the salt concentration is not changed during elution, 
however, the hydrophobic interactions still take place allowing for the hydrophobic interactions 
to take place. However, the dominant interaction is the cation exchange due to the changes in 
pH, which vary the net charges of pIgG, leading to its separation.

2.13 Column Packing 
Once the nonwoven membranes are grafted and functionalized, they need to be cut into discs 
and packed correctly inside the column. The packing of the several discs of nonwoven has to be 
done in such a way that the discs are flat and horizontal, with no indentations or rough edges,

The preparation and qualification of packed columns is very important to ensure robustness and 
safety of the final product. Column efficiency tests are regularly done before starting a 
purification process in order to qualify and monitor the performance of a packed column114.  
A column efficiency test is performed by analyzing the residence time distribution for a tracer 
substance passing through the column, and this is usually referred to as a pulse test. A small 
volume of a tracer, normally 1% of the total column volume, is injected through the sample 
application loop using the AKTA Pure instrument. The broadening of this pulse is analyzed by 
measuring the chromatographic peak at the column outlet, see Figure 25.

symmetry given by the peak asymmetry factor 𝐴𝑠, and (2) peak broadening is described by the 
equivalent number of theoretical plates 𝐻𝐸𝑇𝑃.  
The 𝐻𝐸𝑇𝑃 for a particular component is related to its elution volume and width of the tracer 
peak as it appears in the chromatogram.

4.1.3 SEM 
The SEM images taken at magnitude 500x and 5000x can be seen in Table 8. The reason for taking 
these two magnitudes was to be able to observe the overall fibrillation distribution within the 
fabric matrix (500x), as well as observe an individual fiber and how fibrillation affects its structure 
(5000x).  
It is of interest to compare the different number of passes against each other at the 5000x 
magnification, given that increasing the number of passes increases fibrillation.  
It is observed that when comparing the 100 gsm fabric at 1P and 2P, there is more fibrillation in 
the 2P sample – small hairy fibrils. It can also be observed that the texture of the fiber itself 
changes with increasing number of passes – the fiber surfaces of the 1P sample looks smoother, 
while the fiber surfaces of the 2P sample seem to have parallel indentation lines across their 
length, creating a texturized surface.  
The same is also visualized when comparing 125 gsm fabrics 1P and 2P, where the surface of the 
fibers is more texturized and uneven and more small fibrils are seen on the surface of the fibers.  
The biggest impact that the number of passes has on the fibers can be seen when looking at 75 
gsm 1P and 3P samples. The 75 gsm 1P sample already shows a higher degree of fibrillation 
compared to 100 and 125 gsm 1P samples, since the fiber matrix is thinner and thus their 
structure is more fragile leading to a higher degree of fibrillation at equal water-jets velocities 
used in the hydroentangling step. However, when looking at the 3P sample, the fibers start to 
lose their structure and becoming very fibrillated.  
When looking at the 500x magnification images, it is possible to see a bigger view of the 
fibrillation distribution with various very fibrillated clusters spread across the fiber matrix. In all 
cases, it is observed that the higher the number of passes the greater the number of fibrillated 
clusters.  
Another important observation from the 500x magnification images is the heterogeneity of the 
overall samples due to the changing degrees of fibrillation across the fiber matrix. The high-
velocity water jets don’t impact every single fiber uniformly and therefore there is heterogeneity 
of the structure at a micron-scale.

Based on these images, the basis weights as well as the number of passes have an impact on the

weight and the larger the number of passes, the greater the degree of fibrillation.  
This makes sense since the same amount of energy originating from the water jets affects less 
area density in the case of lower basis weights, compared to the higher basis weights, thus 
leading to greater fibrillation impacts. In other words, more energy incident on the material per 
gram leads to more fibrillation, as shown by Equations (2.3) and (2.4). 
It is desirable to have larger degrees of fibrillation, as it should correlate with larger surface areas, 
which will lead to higher binding capacities. However, it is important to keep in mind that while 
increasing the fibrillation may increase binding capacities, it also weakens the fiber matrix due to 
the process of breaking the fibers into smaller fibrils making them thinner and more fragile. While 
it is desired to have a large number of fibrils, it is necessary to still have large fibers to support 
the fiber matrix and keep a sturdy structure.  
It is expected that the samples with greater degree of fibrillation such as 75 gsm 3P will be 
correlated with greater binding capacities due to higher surface areas in future experiments.

diameter size estimations.

(1) measuring 10 replicates of one layer; 
(2) measuring 2 layers stacked upon each other, measured only once (no replicates).

measurements.

However, when including 75 gsm 1P in this comparison, the result shows that its effective fiber

One explanation for this outcome is based on the fact that the 75 gsm 1P sample is first of all

produced in the same production batch, while the samples 75gsm 1P and 3P were produced in a 
different batch and day, and thus there might have been some differences in the manufacturing 
of these two batches.  
This result may suggest that perhaps it is only relevant to compare samples with the same (or at 
least similar) thickness and/or basis weights.  
When comparing equal basis weights with varying number of passes, it is observed that the 
higher the number of passes the smaller the effective fiber diameter. The explanation for this 
result is because the higher the number of passes the higher the fibrillation (expecting to have 
more fine fibers), thus creating more resistances in the airflow leading to lower air permeability 
values. The lower air permeability values will then correlate with smaller effective fiber 
diameters.

However, from this plot, it is seen that at a low flow rate, the line bends down. This behavior can 
indicate that there is some compression of the membranes within the column. This bring 
attention to the importance of the packing of membranes inside the column, since it can affect 
the flow. 
In order to ensure that the Darcy’s law applies to these experiments and that Equations (3.2) and 
(3.3) can indeed be used to describe the flow behavior, the Reynolds number for this system was 
also calculated. Given a water density of 1000 kg/m3, a viscosity of 0.00089 Pa*s, an average pore

diameter of 10 µm (which is the highest 𝑑𝑓 determined), and at the highest value of flow rate 
employed in this experiment (16 mL/min), the calculated Reynolds number is Re=0.38.  
Under the flow regime where Re<1 the flow and pressure drop in the porous media follows 
Darcy’s law.  
Note that the permeability measurements shown in Table 18 do not take into account the extra-
column pressures originating from the system itself. Even though several attempts to achieve 
that data were performed by using a column packed with only 3 layers to serve as a blank, and 
then having 15 layers out of which 3 would account for the blank and 12 for the actual column 
(thus subtracting the pressure drop of the 3-layer column from the 15-layer column, and 
obtaining pressure for a 12-layer column). However, when the pressure drops of the blank 
column were subtracted from the packed column, the data would scatter across the plot. 
Therefore, these measurements were not utilized, and the measurements of the 15-layered 
columns were utilized without any blank.  
This might suggest that the actual permeabilities of the membranes are at least one order 
magnitude higher than the stated values above. Also, only the 50 mM NaCl (dry packing) method 
had triplicate runs, the rest were run only once.  
The results show that the permeabilities of the membranes with changing buffers do not vary 
significantly (the permeabilities are within the same order of magnitude), with the exception of 
75 gsm 1P and 3P samples.  
When looking at the more viscous 30 mg/mL BSA buffer permeabilities compared to the others, 
it is seen that the viscosity does not have a great influence on the permeability, except for the 75 
gsm samples. These measurements could suggest that the relative permeability of the 
membranes is independent of the viscosity of the buffer.  
When comparing the permeabilities with 50mM NaCl buffer wet packing versus the dry packing 
methods, the permeability of the dry packed method tends to be higher, even though the 
differences are not very significant for 100 gsm and 125 gsm samples. When looking at the 75 
gsm samples, there is a greater discrepancy between the two packing methods. The fact that the 
dry packing permeabilities are higher in all runs could indicate that the polyGMA layers are 
susceptible to some swelling, as documented in literature. This bring attention to the fact that 
the storing conditions of the membranes, or just the choice of buffer could influence the flow 
properties of the membranes.  
One of the reasons why the permeability behavior of the 75 gsm samples across the different 
buffers tested are (1) lower than that of the 100 gsm and 125 gsm samples and (2) show very 
different permeabilities amongst each other where k=dry packing > water > wet packing > BSA, 
could be because (1) these samples were ran separately with a different batch of buffers and fritz 
and (2) at least the sample 75 gsm 3P is highly fibrillated, which could in fact had restricted the 
flow through the fiber matrix leading to low flow permeabilities. However, the fritz used for the 
75 gsm samples was not new, and it could have been clogged from previous use, thus impacting 
the permeability measurements.

structure of the polyGMA brush, as also seen in Figure 20. Since the polyGMA structures are 
longer it increases the volume available for binding. And because the branches are more linear

When setting up the methods for the DBC10%, in the AKTA Pure system, the sample application 
step was programmed to stop whenever the absorbance of sample reached 105 mAU.  
When 10% breakthrough is reached, it means that the column has been saturated completely 
with pIgG, thus product is not being bound any more and will go to waste. Since very often 
biomanufacturing products are expensive to manufacture, a DBC10%, prevents large amounts of 
valuable product from being wasted in this process, while ensuring that the membrane is being 
saturated and utilized at its full capacity.  
4.2.4.2 DBC10% experiment 
The samples 75 gsm 1P and 3P were chosen to be used in the following DBC10% experiment 
protocol.  
The nonwoven membranes were grafted in the laboratories at North Carolina State University 
and shipped to Denmark. The 75 gsm 1P sample had 8% weight gain and 3P sample had 10% 
weight gain. It is also important to note that the GMA used to graft these membranes was made 
in the laboratory and was different than the initial GMA used for the previous grafting

5 Discussion  
Based on the initial characterization results on the 100 gsm and 125 gsm 1P and 2P samples, it 
was decided to manufacture the 75 gsm 1P and 3P samples. From the surface area measurements 
it was confirmed that increasing the fibrillation of the membranes, such as in the case of the 75 
gsm 3P sample, has increased the surface area as well. The production of the new membranes 
with lower basis weights and higher fibrillation was a decision made based on the initial data, 
and it successfully lead to membranes which better address the biomanufacturing needs within 
the chromatography field. However, a few points need to be mentioned here regarding the later 
manufactured membranes 75 gsm samples.  
Even though the surface area of the 75 gsm 3P sample was higher compared to that of 1P sample, 
leading to higher levels of grafting as seen in Figure 34, it also led to lower relative permeabilities 
shown in Table 18  for bare membranes and in Table 30 for functionalized membranes, due to 
the larger number of small fibrils as was determined in Table 13 with 𝑛𝑓= 74.2%. 
This suggests that at a certain point of fibrillation, the flow permeabilities will start being 
negatively impacted. Therefore, a compromise would need to be found between either a higher 
binding capacity, or higher flow throughput.  
On this note, since the nonwoven membranes have highly porous structures and product reaches 
the mixed mode ligands through convection instead of diffusion (as in the case of resins), even if 
their binding capacities were not as high, but due to their high flowrates, the productivity to be 
achieved could be higher than that of resin chromatography. For the purposes of this discussion 
an example comparing resins to the novel lyocell cellulose membranes will be presented below.

6 Conclusions 
The scopes of this report were successfully accomplished, regarding the characterization of the 
novel lyocell cellulose nonwoven membranes, followed by the development of a mixed-mode 
chromatographic system through various techniques such as UV-induced, heat-induced grafting 
and direct-coupling to achieve high-capacity and high-throughput membranes which could be 
successfully used in bio separations applications. 
A few conclusions to be drawn are: 
• Nonwoven membranes can be easily fibrillated through hydroentangling to achieve large 
surface areas, such as in the case of the sample 75 gsm 3P, with 0.37 (m2/g) of membrane, 
as seen in Table 14.  
• SEM is a great method for visualizing the structure of the fine and coarse fibers and a 
good practice for understanding the variability and distribution of their sizes, as shown in 
Table 8. 
• The effective fiber diameter method is useful for determining a fiber size to represent the 
entirety of the nonwoven fabric, however, it should be coupled with manual 
measurements and a bimodal distribution to have a better understanding regarding the 
fraction of coarse and fine fibers.  
• The determined high relative permeabilities between k=3.65x10-14 to k= 3.21x10-13 are 
promising and comparable to permeabilities for resins. Recall that these permeabilities 
did not take into account a blank column which could have provided information 
regarding the extra-column pressure, and more correct permeability values.  
• Viscosity of the buffer does not influence the relative permeability (except for the 75 gsm 
samples which might have either been caused by the frit utilized since it was not new, or 
could be due to finer fibers increasing the resistance in the flow lowering the 
permeability), as was seen in Table 18. 
• Among the two methods used to graft the nonwoven fabrics, the UV-induced grafting and 
the heat-induced grafting had similar %weight gains. The determining factor on which 
method to use for later experiments was based on their SBC results, since 
• Both grafting methods (UV and heat-induced grafting) caused some loss of material, 
attributed to the washing of short fibers with small diameters. To avoid underestimating 
the %weight gains, it is good practice to take into account the weight loss during the 
process.   
• When comparing the SBC results from the three grafting/functionalizing methods, it was 
observed that the UV-induced grafting had the highest SBC followed by the heat-grafting 
and finally the direct ligand coupling. Table 24 showed that sample UV-3 (20 min grafting) 
and a weight gain of 15% had an SBC=124.5±9.6 mg/mL, followed by the sample HG-3 (20 
min grafting) and a weight gain of 6.97% had and SBC=98.55±23 mg/mL, and the direct 
coupling method had an SBC=13.95±7.4 mg/mL. This might be due to the structure of the 
polyGMA chains which are more flexible and linear in the UV-induced grafting, thus 
creating a higher 3D volume available for proteins to bind. The reason why the direct 
coupling method had the lowest SBC, could be due to a lower area for binding when 
==PAGE:95==
85 
 
compared to the other methods which utilize grafting to increase their available area for 
binding.  
• When comparing the UV-induced grafting of the various samples in Figure 34, it can be 
concluded that the 75 gsm 3P sample had the highest %weight gains of 18.9 ±1.9%. This 
is due to its higher degree of fibrillation (increased surface area) and low basis weight 
(allowing UV-light to easily penetrate through the fiber matrix). 
• Increased %weight gains are correlated with larger surface areas as seen in Figure 34, and 
larger surface areas are correlated with higher levels of fibrillation from Table 14.  
• Even though high SBC were achieved, the standard deviations were quite high as shown 
in Table 24. These high standard deviations were caused by the inherent heterogeneity of 
the nonwoven fabrics.  
• Lyocell cellulose nonwoven fabrics studied in this report have reported high DBC10% at 
short residence times such as DBC10%=46 mg IgG/mL column at RT=0.1 min for 75 gsm 1P 
sample and DBC10%=51 mg IgG/mL column at RT=0.1 min for 75 gsm 3P, as shown in Table 
29. 
• The repetitive use of these functionalized membranes (through 5 consecutive DBC10% 
cycles at differing RTs) still led to comparable DBC10% with other commercially available 
membranes and resins such as Sartobind S membrane (20 mg IgG/ mL for 0.15 min RT), 
Mustang S membrane (22 mg IgG/ mL for 2.85 min RT) and Capto S resins (55 mg IgG/ mL 
for 0.5 min RT), as previously show in Table 2. This result indicates that even though 
nonwoven membranes are great in single-use applications, they might also have the 
potential to withstand repetitive use.  
• Given that the relative permeabilities of the bare cellulose membranes (k=1.14x10-13 m2 
for 75 gsm 1P sample, and k= 9.26x10-14 m2 for 75 gsm 3P sample from Table 18) were 
very close to those of the grafted and functionalized membranes (k=1.73x10-13 m2 for 75 
gsm 1P sample, and k= 9.65x 10-14 m2 for 75 gsm 3P sample from Table 30) it could suggest 
that the membranes keep their high flow permeabilities, and do not get negatively 
impacted by the chemistry applied to them (grafting and functionalization). 
• Lyocell cellulose nonwoven fabrics show promising results to be used as a high-capacity, 
high-throughput material in biomanufacturing, with increased productivity compared to 
traditional resin based chromatographic systems, as exemplified in Table 31.  
• Higher productivity nonwoven fabrics developed for chromatography would require less 
equipment and could therefore lead to lower costs compared to resin chromatography, 
along with reduced CIP, SIP, validation and qualification steps if used as single-use 
devices.

7 Potential Improvements  
When there are so many variables and parameters, such as the heterogeneity in manufacturing 
the fibrillated nonwovens, varying basis weight and fibrillation, surface areas and degrees of 
%weight gain which can all influence the permeability and binding capacities, it is difficult to point 
out which of these has the greatest impact on creating a high-capacity, high-throughput 
membrane material.  
Surely, the research and studies done throughout this report shed light into the possibilities and 
potential applications, but it would be of interest to determine which of the various parameters 
has the most impact.  
This could be achieved through: 
• Including a larger number of replicates (especially in the permeability measurements). 
• If a larger number of replicates were run for the permeability measurements, relations 
between the permeability and parameters such as porosity %, fiber diameter or thickness 
of membrane could be deduced, thus having a better understanding of what are the 
parameters influencing the permeabilities of these novel lyocell cellulose fabrics.  
• On the same note, it would be valuable to have a standardized packing procedure, thus 
minimizing any possible compression of the membranes in the column.  
• Determining the extra-column pressure drops by having a blank column and subtracting 
its pressure contribution from the actual permeability measurements.  
• Following standardized procedures (for example a more rigorous way of obtaining GMA 
could potentially increase the reproducibility of the grafting experiments).  
• The surface area was determined by using the air permeability and effective fiber 
diameter, however there is another method which could be used: the Brunauer-Emmett-
Teller (BET) surface area analysis, which is a multi-point measurement of a material’s 
surface area (m2/g) through gas adsorption analysis. This would potentially give a more 
realistic surface area measurement.  
• Optimizing the elution buffer to ensure all pIgG is released from the membrane. 
• Finding a way to represent the SBC and DBC10% as a function of %weight gain 
(SBC/%weight gain, for example), to reduce the number of variables when expressing 
binding capacities. However, it is important to mention that ligand density is also an 
important characteristic which impacts the %weight gain (as previously mentioned the 
heat-grafted polyGMA chains are more dense and short with smaller 3D volumes, 
compared to the UV-induced grafting method). A study looking into the ligand density 
and its correlation to the %weight gain and SBC or DBC10% would be very beneficial in this 
case.

8 Future Perspectives  
While the nonwoven lyocell cellulose samples developed and studied in this thesis show 
promising results, it would be relevant to test more samples with even lower basis weights or 
higher degrees of fibrillation. This would allow a better overview and understanding of how the 
various nonwoven physical properties affect throughput and capacity, and what their limitations 
are.  
This report only focused on functionalizing the nonwoven fabric with cation-exchange and 
hydrophobic interaction capabilities, and therefore the next step would be to develop and test 
anion-exchange with hydrophobic interaction capabilities, thus expanding the applicability of the 
lyocell cellulose nonwoven membranes.   
The binding capacity experiments demonstrated high SBC and DBC10% capabilities when tested 
using pIgG. The next step if the membranes would continue to be developed into a sellable 
product, would be to use therapeutic mAbs as the target product for the tests (given that 
therapeutic mAbs are a very valuable product commonly manufactured for biotherapeutics).  
Given that the experiments in this report were done with ideal conditions where pure solutions 
of pIgG were utilized, it would be interesting to test the membranes with real fermentation 
culture media (real case scenario) and calculating DBC10% in realistic conditions.  
Another topic which this project did not cover was purity, since pure pIgG solutions were used. 
However, if a real case scenario study were to be performed from real fermentation culture 
media, purity of the separation of mAbs would be very relevant to look upon.  
Moreover, given that the focus of this report was mainly put on the downstream processing, it 
would be interesting to focus on the upstream processing and perhaps apply the membranes in 
an in-situ product removal set-up.
