Abstract
Within ship design there has been a common practice to perform propulsion analysis
and evaluate propeller design and performance, based on calm water conditions. The
flow in this condition does not resemble that in an ordinary sea condition and so the
test results might differ from the experienced performance. The focus of this thesis is to
establish a calculation tool for propulsion analysis including ship motions and variation
to the flow of water into the propeller. The purpose is to investigate the propulsion
performance as a ship sails in a seaway with waves of various characteristics.
The tool includes ship motion calculations for heave, pitch and surge motions based
on strip theory presented by Salvesen, Tuck and Faltinsen, as well as Gerritsma and
Beukelman. Further, the tool implements two methods for calculating the added re-
sistance in waves. The first being a DTU-developed design tool based on regression
analysis of various ships from a large database, and the latter a semi-empirical method
based on previous experiments and geometrical corrections, with an emphasize on the
short waves region.
Lastly the tool combines these two operations in order to perform a propulsion anal-
ysis. Based on simple propeller parameters, the polynomials from the Wageningen B-
series calculations are used for extracting the necessary parameters to predict the thrust,
torque and open-water efficiency of the propeller. The wake velocities are then calcu-
lated by including the ship motions and waves to establish the variation in ship speed
as caused by waves.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In merchant shipping, a ship is but a vessel for its cargo. The ship itself is not of value
compared to the cargo. Especially for container ships, this is true. Ask a naval architect
and he might disagree, but a ship owner or operator will not. They will want to see
their cargo moved from A to B in the fastest, most efficient and cheapest way possible.
Therefore it is vital to ensure exactly this. It goes without saying that propulsion plays
a crucial part in terms of moving cargo. No propulsion, no movement, no money. This
makes propulsion analysis an important part of the design process. Through the years it
has and still is a very common practice to perform a propulsion analysis in calm water,
with a steady and ideal inflow of water into the propeller. This ensures a simple and
fast overview of the propellers characteristics and performance. Unfortunately the sea is
very seldom calm and the flow steady, which calls for a more precise and realistic form
of propulsion analysis. By including waves and ship response in the analysis, the actual
performance of the propeller in a given sea state can be predicted. This will be the focus
of this thesis.

1.1.1
Ship Design
1.1.1.1
Propulsion System
Part of the ship design process is to decide on a suitable propulsion system, which consists
of the engine, gear, shaft and propulsor, roughly explained. When this system is based
on calm water conditions modified by a sea margin to account for heavy weather, it
might be too powerful for its purpose and there might be effects from the waves that are
unaccounted for which leads to challenged in regards of the propulsion system. Therefore
it would be very beneficial to be able to model the realistic conditions in the open sea
by including the effects of waves, early in the design process.
1.1.2
Ship Operations
1.1.2.1
Weather Routing
Figure 1.1: A vessel facing severe weather conditions [25].
Heavy weather is a ship’s nemesis and presents a great number of challenges if sailing
through it. Not only does heavy weather imply delays or standstills, but also danger
to ship, cargo and crew. Avoidance of heave weather is therefore of great interest. An
interest that has pushed forward the concept of weather routing.When planning a route
for an upcoming voyage, there has become more and more emphasis and focus on weather
forecasts and reports. Modern onboard systems processing forecasts and doing real-time

1.1.3
Fuel Efficiency
Time was of the essence in the world of shipping some decades back, where the fastest
route were the best. After the rise in bunker fuel prices, slow-steaming became a standard
principle in the industry. Ever since, the fuel costs has been a major focus and shipping
companies strive to keep them as low as possible. This has shifted the perception of an
optimal route from time-saving to fuel efficient, but ideally a combination of the two.
Optimizing propulsion is an excellent tool for lowering fuel consumption. Several factors
affect fuel consumption, not only the ship speed i.e. the engine output. Ship resistance
consisting of calm water resistance, residual resistance and viscous resistance, and added
resistance from encountering waves, plays a major role in fuel consumption.
Other
contributors include wind, current, various onboard systems e.g. waste heat recovery.
All of these parameters are taken into account when calculating and comparing optional
routes for the optimal voyage that is a compromise of the mentioned parameters.
Additional considerations are taken depending on type of vessel and waters. Ice is
crucial to consider where ever that is likely to encounter, as well as piracy for vessels
that sail close to known hot spots for piracy. Sulphur Emission Control Areas (SECA)
and Emission Control Areas (ECA) are waters where strict rules applies for airborne
emissions, in order to reduce pollution. Sailing in these sea areas puts requirements to
the sulphur content in the fuel and also the efficiency of the engine and purity in the
exhaust, therefore some ships has an interest in avoiding these areas if not needed.

2.2
Resistance
The total resistance of a ship is one of the main focus areas in ship design because it
defines the demand for propulsion, meaning the force needed to overcome the resistance
and move the ship through water. A division of ship resistance can take various forms,
but a rough and suitable form for this project is two parts: calm water resistance and
added resistance due to waves and wind.
2.2.1
Calm Water Resistance
Calm water resistance is the actual resistance occurring when pushing/towing a sub-
merged ship hull through calm water, i.e. no waves or wind. Calm water resistance has
two main components, wave resistance and viscous resistance. As the ship hull moves
through calm water, two sets of waves are formed and together they create a so-called
Kelvin pattern, see Figure 2.3. The transient waves propagate perpendicular to the ships
centre-line and the divergent waves propagate almost parallel to the forward shoulder of
the hull. Alongside wave making resistance, both wave breaking and spray can add to
the total wave resistance.
The viscosity of the water creates stresses along the hull, both tangential and normal
stresses. Large parts of a ship can be perceived as a flat plate where friction induces
stresses, but as the hull shape changes the flow separates in certain regions. This gives
rise to the residuary resistance where the form effect accounts for those components [1].
Figure 2.3: Pressure distribution of hull clearly showing the Kelvin wave pattern.
As mentioned in Section 1, calm water has been the usual condition when deciding
upon suitable propulsion system, which means that calm water resistance has been given
a lot of attention throughout the years. As a result, many solid and validated methods
exist for its prediction.
Historically, model tests has been the most common way of evaluating resistance
[29]. Ship models replicated and scaled down from their full-scale counterpart are being
==PAGE:22==
10

towed by trolleys through towing tanks. A strain gauge connecting the model and the
trolley are being used to measure the force, which in turn gives the resistance that the
ship model are facing in the water. In order to successfully scale the results to full-size,
several similarities needs to be scaled:
• Geometric similarity - Shape and size
• Kinematic similarity - Speed
• Dynamic similarity - Forces
After years of model testing, a great deal of model data is available related to the
form of standard series hull forms, such as the Series 60. By systematic variation in hull
shapes of ship models, a large database of results from the tests has been established
and can be utilized for new designs of similar shape. In addition to standard series,
evaluation methods based on regression analysis of the numerous model tests as well as
full-scale measurements, has proved to be an efficient and quick way of predicting the
resistance. Common ones include those by Holtrop & Mennen [12] and Guldhammer &
Harvald [11]. For this project the latter method has been used for resistance prediction
and will be explained later in this report.
The computational power has improved to such an extent over the last decades that
simulations by computational fluid dynamics (CFD) has become a common method for
evaluating resistance. It still remains a quite time- and power-consuming affair, but
provides results of high quality and detail. The brilliance of CFD is the ability to run
simulations quite early in the design process and then continuously improving the design
before running a costly model test in a towing tank late in the process [30]. As computer
power and algorithms are improving, the accuracy will likely get rather close to that of
an experiment and maybe match its analog counterpart.

2.2.2
Added Resistance
Figure 2.4: Illustration of the various components contributing to the resistance for a
ship sailing in a seaway with constant speed [3].
As a ship sails in a seaway, it will encounter waves which will increase the resistance
and disturb the steady, forward motion. Since there always exists waves at sea, small or
large, added resistance is a key component to include in the evaluation of a ships total
resistance, in order to better tailor a propulsion system with enough power to uphold
the wanted forward motion. Added resistance which is basically the longitudinal wave
drift force, implying that there are drift forces for all 6 degrees of freedom, is part of a
larger topic called seakeeping. Interest within seakeeping and manoeuvring lies in the
unsteady wave forces and their disturbance of the given forward motion or heading, drift
forces can also be caused by wind from various headings, but in this project there will
only be focus on head sea and wind, namely the mentioned longitudinal drift forces.
When a wave encounters an object in its path, the original wave pattern is disturbed
and forms a different pattern after having passed the object. This transition from one
pattern to two patterns can be referred to as diffraction, which is the first of the two ma-
jor components regarding added resistance, taking place mainly in the higher frequency
region where the ship motions are small. This effect is also called the reflection effect,
since the added resistance is caused by the reflection of incident waves.
An effect of the encountering waves and the diffraction is when the oscillating waves
induces ship motions, primarily pitch and heave motions. As the ship is oscillating, it in
turn accelerates the surrounding water and there is a transfer of energy caused by this
damping resulting in an increased resistance for the ship, this phenomenon is called the
motion effect in this project, but is also known as the radiation effect.

into two groups; near-field methods and far-field methods. The first one is based on
an integration of the fluid pressure acting on the submerged surface of the hull and

2.3
Ship Motions
Knowledge about the wave induced ship motions and the response in a given sea state, is
quite useful. It can be used for hindsight based statistics, real-time predictions or future
route planning and optimization. It may save fuel, lives and cargo. Heavy motions and

response can greatly reduce propulsion and thus the efficiency lowers; it can endanger the
hull structure, crew and cargo; and also vastly reduce comfort for crew and passengers.
The behaviour of a ship at sea sailing in irregular waves is highly complex. There are
many factors to take into consideration and it represents a very non-linear system. This
makes it hard to compute and predict, and therefore it hasn’t been emphasized quite as
much through the years. Especially at the design stage, where the naval architect has
been compelled to use the ship performance and effective power in calm water conditions
and the maximum bending moment as his main design criteria, without being able to
consider the operational profile to some extent. Naturally this gives a higher safety mar-
gin, but may leave the ship over-dimensioned for its purpose, i.e. more costly and causes
an unnecessary expense for the shipowner. Also in some cases, e.g. container vessels
and liquid carriers, not knowing the motions and response of the vessel in operation
can lead to a dangerous or ineffective voyage. Containers can break their lashes and
fall overboard or the liquid in the tanks can create a free surface effect which seriously
hampers the stability and maneuverability of the ship [27].

In terms of initial methods and theory regarding the ship motion problem, the con-

first stated as a hypothesis, but has later on been frequently implemented and validated,
especially for vertical motions and loads. From then on there has been advancements

2.4
Strip Theory
For the linear ship motion strip theory developed by Salvesen [27], these are some of the
assumptions it is based on:
• The vessel has a slender hull, implying that the length/breadth-ratio is large (Lp-
p/B>3), in addition to small gradual variation in the hull geometry
• The speed of the vessel should not exceed Fn = 0.4
• Potential theory, i.e. the fluid is considered to be incompressible, irrotational and
non-frictional
• Rigid hull, no deformation will occur due to waves and motions
• Deep water, i.e. the water depth is larger than the wave length
• The presence of the hull does not affect surrounding waves, only locally
• Small motions, to enable linearity
In 1957, Korvin-Kroukovsky and Jacobs [16] presented what later became known
as the strip theory, mainly for heave and pitch motions in head seas. It was the first
motion theory which was suitable for numerical calculations and provided results with
a sufficient accuracy for engineering appliance [27]. Later on this theory has been the
background for multiple variations and interpretations of the strip theory. One of the
more commonly used is the Salvesen-Tuck-Faltinsen (STF) strip theory method, which
will be used in this project, a thorough explanation can be found in Section 2.5.
At the initial design stage, the time spent on running tests, trials and heavy simula-
tions might not be too important, since it might be quickly altered or rejected. Firstly
quick and relatively easy computational tools are needed, where there is few and easy-
to-alter-parameters, not too much human interaction so the human input errors are less
and output data which is easy to comprehend [15]. Later on in the design process or
the design spiral, which is an iterative way of working in a ship design process and can

Strip theory is a linear slender body theory, suitable for ships with a large length-
/breadth ratio and relatively small motions. It is simplified to ease computations and
based on potential flow theory, so viscous effects are neglected. In addition the theory
assumes moderate speed, deep water, rigid hull, wall-sided hull sections. The strip the-
ory works best for head seas, with waves propagating perpendicular to the ships centre

in the low encounter frequency range, and in order to avoid the large motions, a critical
assumption for the strip theory is an high encounter frequency. This helps to lower the
order of the problems and keeping the theory simplified. That being said, heave and
pitch motions are dominated by the hydrostatic forces in the low region of encounter
frequencies, so inaccuracies in the hydrodynamic coefficients will not affect the results
too much. By diving the ship hull into transverse strips as can be seen in Figure 2.8, the
theory calculates local hydrodynamic properties at each strip and by integrating over
the length of the ship it is possible to obtain the total hydrodynamic coefficients needed
to solve the equations of motions.

2.6
Propulsion Analysis
Figure 2.12: A simulation of the inflow to the propeller [5].
As mentioned briefly in the first section, it is a common practice to perform propul-
sion analysis in calm water. Usually ships has mainly been designed according to trial
or service condition and only been given a sea margin to account for any added loads or
resistance. Traditionally this has been sufficient enough to meet the set requirements,
but another important aspect is that of computational power, as well as knowledge. Up
until recent years, computers has not been capable of computing ship resistance and
response set in a sea environment, because of the oceans irregular waves and non-linear
aspects. Recreating a fully developed and realistic sea state, although scaled down, in
a towing tank or an ocean basin has been proved hard to do as well. Lacking ways to
test or simulate certain scenarios and concepts, makes even harder to discover unknown
effects in addition to improving and developing theory. As a result, propulsion analysis
has usually been simplified and many aspects concerning it still remains an area of dis-
cussion, which is the case of wave-induced added resistance, yet to be elaborated later
on in the thesis.
Not only does the sea represent unpredictability and non-linearity, but also variations.
In particular, variation of flow to the propeller. When performed in calm water, the
inflow to the propeller is steady and close to laminar. Ensuring a relatively predictable
condition for the analysis, when in fact the flow is more turbulent and fluctuating in
ocean waves, see Figure 2.13.
Worth mentioning is also the aspect of fuel cost and how it has affected the ways
ships are designed and operated. Accounting for 47 per cent of the voyage cost, fuel
cost plays a major role when deciding upon ship speed [31]. Directly affecting the fuel
cost is the oil price which is cyclic, as with the shipping industry or at least some of
the segments, meaning that it will fluctuate. The changes in oil price has left its mark
throughout the recent decades, especially when it comes to how the ship is designed
and operated. When the oil price is low, as was the case in the early 1970s, the focus
is mainly on keeping maintenance costs low and the power output high, leaving less

displacement of the ship creates waves both fore and aft, and together these to effects
distorts the flow of the water and creating a wake field behind the hull, see Figure 2.16.

3.3
Added Resistance
Added resistance is a disputed topic when it comes to choice of method. There are
several methods for prediction of added resistance in waves, but since there still is an
uncertainty about how added resistance affects a ship and how to predict it in a detailed
manner, one method could be as good and valid as another.
Two method has been utilized in this project. The first being an in-house-developed
tool at DTU.
3.3.1
DTU Added Resistance Tool
The design tool developed at DTU, is more or less a product of two MSc theses, Mar-
tinsen(2016)[20] and Nielsen(2015)[24]. The latter being the original design tool, which
estimates the added resistance for ships having large block coefficients based on main
dimensions. From this tool, Martinsen(2016), developed a more advanced and compre-
hensive design tool, including a larger diversity of ships with small block coefficients.
The design tool is based upon a few sets of ship geometries, with different block coeffi-
cients to cover several ship types, which then are scaled by a number of scaling ratios
in order to represent a quite large part of the world’s fleet in terms of dimensions. Then
the added resistance in waves are calculated by using the two methods mentioned in
section 2.2.2.1, and implemented in MATLAB as described in Afshar(2014)[1]. Added
resistance curves are produced for several ship speeds at different headings, which then
finally are interpolated in order to produce more curves for values in between. At last a
large database is established consisting of added resistance curves for a great number of
ship types. The design tool only requires a small number of main parameters as input,
and is therefore suitable for an estimation of added resistance early in the design process:
• Lpp - length between perpendiculars [m]
• b - breadth [m]
• T - draught [m]
• Cb - block coefficient [-]
• VS - ship speed [knots]
• β - ship heading angle [degrees]
• HS - significant wave height [m]
• TZ - zero upcrossing period [s]
• ρ - mass density of water [kg/m3]
• g - gravitational acceleration [m/s2]

CHAPTER 5
Discussion & Conclusion
One major weakness of this project is the lack of validation. Besides the ”visual” val-
idation or resemblance, there is no comparison data to verify the results produced by
the calculation tool. Experience and logical reasoning has been the best tool for inter-
pretation of the results, which is a major limitation. That being said, the tool does
produce some reasonable and valid results. The weakness does not lie with the tool
alone, but also with the assumptions and limitations connected to the theory and meth-
ods implemented. Strip theory assumes rather ideal conditions and values, let’s say for
frequency. If the frequency is too high or too low, the strip theory may fail and pro-
duce some oddities. This is also the case with the added resistance methods and the
flow estimation. In the extreme regions, whether it being geometry, frequency or wave
height, the tool provides results that seems a bit off. If a prolonged time simulation of
the propulsion analysis is performed, then there occurs some major errors, where the
amplitudes has an exponential growth, this may be due to a build-up of numerical errors.
Simplifications has been done in several areas. The geometry sections are rather coarse
and does not fully resemble a ship, instead the project should have been based on an
actual ship and its detailed dimensions, along with data from various tests for validation.
The frequency has been set separately and are not directly connected to the sea state
parameters. Although the wave height has been changed for various cases, they should
have been correlated. Another weakness is connected to the MATLAB-proficiency of
the author. The structure, the build-up and the use of functions could have been a lot
more streamlined and effective. For example the time simulation of the flow to the pro-
peller, should probably have been calculated by utilizing one of the ordinary differential
equation (ODE) solvers within the MATLAB toolbox for better time integration.
The purpose of this thesis has been to establish a calculation tool for performing a
propulsion analysis which includes the effect of waves and wave-induced ship motions.
The results produced clearly shows a significantly effect on the instantaneous velocity
of the ship due to waves. The tool written is a simple tool which only requires a small
set of input parameters, is capable of calculating the heave, pitch and surge motions
of the given ship, along with added resistance in waves by two different methods and
lastly combining them in a propulsion analysis that performs a time simulation of sev-
eral attributes of interest. The tool has a relatively straight-forward structure, with
explanatory lines of code and it is quite easy to alter the input parameters, the output
data and the aspects to include in the calculation. The tool is a good starting point for
optimization, incorporation of other methods, expansion of operations to perform

CHAPTER 6
Future Work
As mentioned, there has been used simplified methods in several steps during the project.
Both due to limitation on the scope, but also the time limit. The work performed in this
thesis can then to some extent be considered as incomplete. However, these are some
suggestions towards future work and development of the calculation tool produced in
this project:
• Consider multiple headings - In the ship motion calculation, added resistance
and propulsion analysis, the only heading that has been included is for head sea,
except for the DTU design tool in which others are included. Considering the
other headings as well is important for a seakeeping tool as this one.
• Validation by physical tests or CFD - It would be highly interesting to check
the results from this project against that of physical tests or from CFD simulations.
• Re-write the code - It would be beneficial to re-write the code. For increased
efficiency and accuracy and also for easy debugging and troubleshooting.
• Considering irregular waves - The calculations performed in this project has
been for regular waves and should for increased validity and realism, consider
irregular waves.
• Include all the motions - As the other headings are included, it is natural to
consider all six degrees of freedom, making it a more complete seakeeping tool.
• Consider other seakeeping loads - Considering the other drift loads acting on
a ship in a seaway, the loads acting on the rudder and wind from other directions
would also help to complete this tool.
• Implement calm water resistance - By implementing a calculation for the
calm water resistance and incorporate the added resistance in waves along with
the wind resistance, the tool would produce a complete resistance analysis and
remove dependence on input from other software.
• Cavitation - When calculating propeller performance, it is crucial to include
cavitation in the consideration. It would have been especially interesting in this
project as the effect of waves would affect the cavitation, as the variation of wake
flow and the resulting pressure pulses would probably show some interesting results
on cavitation.
