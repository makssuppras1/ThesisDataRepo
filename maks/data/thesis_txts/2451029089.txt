Abstract
Taylor’s frozen turbulence hypothesis supposes that turbulent structures do not change over time and
advect at the mean wind speed.
This hypothesis is used frequently to overcome the diﬃculty of
measuring ﬂuid motions both temporally and spatially. It has also been the subject of numerous studies
in order to identify and correct its failings.
Using LES data of the atmospheric boundary layer at
varying spatial scales and resolution, a spatio-temporal spectrum and its corresponding autocorrelation
is constructed in order to investigate Taylor’s hypothesis and some of the corrections that rely on
comparatively limited information and discuss its use in large scale experiments and in wind farm siting.

Introduction
1.1
Taylor’s Hypothesis
Turbulence is a phenomenon in ﬂuid mechanics broadly deﬁned by the AMS glossary as "irregular
ﬂuctuations occurring in ﬂuid motions." These ﬂuctuations can be characterized by complex energetic
structures, or eddies, of various scales. The kinetic energy contained in these structures dissipates
through smaller and smaller eddies before becoming heat [1]. In the atmospheric boundary layer (ABL),
much of the energy introduced to the larger turbulent structures comes from drag between the moving
air and the Earth’s surface creating shear and the ﬂow of heat between the Earth’s surface and the
atmosphere. Naturally these turbulent structures move in space while constantly varying in shape and
energy over time.
However, resolving both spatial and temporal components of turbulence is of interest to many studies of
turbulence and the atmospheric boundary layer, and it is diﬃcult to measure wind speed simultaneously
in space and time since most instruments eﬀect the ﬂuid ﬂow by being a physical object the ﬂow must
respond to, so measuring spatially precludes temporal results while measuring temporally only allows
for a single spatial point. Thus, for ﬂuid ﬂow experiments it is most common to have a single point
mast measuring wind speeds temporally.
Taylor’s frozen turbulence hypothesis links the temporal and spatial qualities of turbulent structures
by assuming that the wind advects past the desired measurement area faster than the structure can
change [2]. Even though the structures constantly change, they change slowly in comparison to the
overall wind velocity that pushes the structures across space. Essentially, if the mean wind speed U is
known, the wind velocity as a function of space and time u(x, y, z, t) can be simpliﬁed to an equivalent
expression of velocity as a function of the spacial coordinates and distance in the mean wind direction
scaled by the mean advection velocity x
U [3],

Thus, measuring wind speed as a function of time in one spatial location allows for the inference of
the turbulence intensity along the mean wind direction within a desired distance. This naturally is an
assumption that becomes less accurate for longer periods of time and larger distances. However, it has
historically been necessary when forming turbulence experiments due to the diﬃculty of resolving tem-
poral and spatial scales with conventional measurement techniques. Recently small-scale experiments
have been able to use particle image velocimetry to reduce the reliance on the assumptions of Taylor’s
hypothesis. Considering it is well documented that large turbulent structures advect at velocities con-
sistent with Taylor’s hypothesis, but eddies at scales in the inertial subrange and smaller advect more
slowly [4][3][5], it seems particularly valuable for experiments concerning the smaller scales to avoid
using Taylor’s hypothesis for results.
Due to its near ubiquity of use, Taylor’s hypothesis has had a myriad of discussion concerning its accu-
racy in a variety of conditions with a variety of measurement and simulation techniques complete with
many suggestions for corrections to error. Other potential sources for error than the dissipation of eddies
is the eﬀect of shearing forces on turbulence and the tendency of convection velocity for turbulent eddies
to be dependent on the size of the structure, with smaller structures convecting at an unsteady [6] and

Many experiments have been conducted to test the accuracy of Taylor’s hypothesis and some of the
corrections. This includes two-point coherence measurements from real data at various turbulence inten-
sities [10], which uses gathered temporal data from two spatial locations and compares the streamwise,
crosswise, and vertical velocity components of the spatial and temporal coherence with direct application
of Taylor’s hypothesis. Conclusions include that error is greatest in the streamwise velocity component
and smallest for the crosswise component, error increases with wavenumber (“apparently due to spec-
tral aliasing caused by both the changing magnitude and direction of the convection velocity”), and
error increases as turbulence intensity increases. Wilczek et al. analyzes the eﬀect of height on wall
bounded turbulence and discusses the eﬀect of LES resolution on Doppler broadening and decorrelation,
concluding that the Kraichnan and Tennekes random-sweeping hypothesis [11][12] in which small-scale
eddies are “passively advected by large-scale energy-containing eddies in a random fashion”[13] explains
a Doppler shift and Doppler broadening of higher frequencies, which is qualitatively understood in a
spectrum or autocorrelation plot as a decrease in convection velocity and an increase in the thickness
of contour bands, respectively. Other notable conclusions to this thesis are that Doppler shift is less
pronounced at lower height, and that LES resolution signiﬁcantly impacts the quantitative details of the
wavenumber-frequency spectrum, particularly in underestimating the magnitude of Doppler broadening,
but maintains qualitative features.

1.1
Taylor’s Hypothesis
However, indirect application of Taylor’s hypothesis has been advantageous for wind turbine siting
standards because turbulence intensity can be inferred for the site from a single measurement location
from a mast. Currently, turbulence intensity is calculated using the standard deviation σ and the mean
wind speed U with some adjustments to account for the damage from larger variations in turbulence
according to the IEC normal turbulence model. However, as previously stated Taylor’s hypothesis at
higher turbulence intensities becomes less accurate, in particular for larger wavenumbers [10] and high
shear [14], so modiﬁcations to its application may reduce risk and error in the calculation of turbulence
intensity at a site. Furthermore, new measurement techniques, e.g. LiDAR, are providing potentially
more cost eﬀective and less environmentally invasive wind measurements that include more than one
spatial point [15]. This can be beneﬁcial both for turbulent study experiments that now experience less
interference from measuring in many spatial locations for a longer time and for wind turbine siting as
more wind measurements in a greater area can increase the accuracy of turbulent intensity calculations
and potentially reduce risk when considering lifetime fatigue of the blades since the majority of blade
damage is caused by loading from turbulence [16].
Figure 1: A sketch of Taylor’s frozen hypothesis from a spatio-temporal spectrum along the mean
wind direction provided by Moin [5]. If Taylor’s hypothesis were completely accurate, the contours
would actually be represented by a bisecting straight diagonal line.
Since LiDAR is becoming more common in wind turbine siting as turbines become larger and the cost of
construction oﬀshore makes LiDAR the better economic decision in comparison to a met mast,[17] the
added precision of spatial and temporal wind speed measurements allows for more precise construction of
the turbines that may reduce materials cost or decrease uncertainty in turbine failure rates. Examining
Taylor’s frozen turbulence hypothesis and considering its shortcomings and accuracy at large spatial
scales can improve the current method of calculating turbulent intensity and inﬂuence design constraints
of large wind turbines. Considering modern wind farms can occupy vast spaces of land (e.g. Roscoe
wind farm in Texas occupies more than 400km2), Taylor’s hypothesis can be used in conjunction with
LiDAR to estimate turbulent intensity at large spatial scales with more accuracy and few measuring
stations.
Therefore, this thesis involves an examination of Taylor’s frozen turbulence hypothesis on high spatial
resolution, smaller scale LES and lower resolution but large spatial domain LES in a conventionally
stable atmosphere with a consistent mean wind speed and constant roughness in a ﬂat plane in order
to determine the accuracy of the hypothesis for very large turbulent structures that carry large amounts
of energy and may be inﬂuenced by various forces not accounted by the hypothesis such as the Coriolis
force. Various studies have determined the small scale turbulence for which the hypothesis is inaccurate

2.1
Simulation
The data used in this analysis is derived from Large Eddy Simulations (LES) of diﬀerent scales conducted
by Jacob Berg [18]. The models both simulate the same atmospheric and geologic conditions: weakly
stable stratiﬁcation in a ﬂat landscape with surface roughness z0 = 0.05m. This means that orography
does not eﬀect the ﬂow and the initial surface heat ﬂux Q0 = 0.
LES strives to resolve the Navier-Stokes equations numerically for scales above the chosen resolution,
acting as a low pass ﬁlter for the smallest temporal and spatial scales in order to reduce the computational
demand of direct numerical simulation. Therefore, information is lost and error introduced due to not
resolving the smallest scales. The atmospheric surface layer, or the portion of the atmospheric boundary
layer (ABL) directly inﬂuenced by the ground, requires a much ﬁner resolution to resolve accurately due
to the smaller characteristic length scale of turbulence. [19]
The model simulates a dry ABL with ﬂat geometry using the pseudo-spectral LES code of the National
Center for Atmospheric Research under the Boussinesq approximation following the work of Deardorﬀ
(1970), Moeng (1984), and Moeng and Wyngaard (1988) ([20], [21], and [22], respectively) using the
equations:

also shows the smaller scales having a faster convection velocity similar to the full domain. Therefore,
this may indicate resolution eﬀects in the simulation more than coriolis forces advecting the smaller
scales more quickly when modelling a suﬃciently large domain.
Clearly, the spectra do not behave the same way. Wilczek et al. found that lower resolution simulations
overpredicts decorrelation time scales with reduced Doppler broadening.

Convection Velocity Adjustments
Most of the convection velocity corrections calculated previously either do not account for a nonlinear
relationship between the wavenumber and frequency spectra or do not account for smaller scale turbulent
structure advecting faster than the mean wind speed, so most of the corrections tested in ﬁgure 12 fail
to correct the convection velocity. Considering the convection velocity is typically above the mean wind
velocity for small turbulent scales in the large domain simulation, the correction proposed by equation
2 fails outright and will not be shown. Figure 24 shows the two corrections proposed by Wills. Since
the Doppler broadening is so reduced in the large scale simulation, the corrections do not adjust the
convection velocity as much in comparison to the small scale simulation: 3.7 and 4.3ms−1, respectively.
6
Conclusion
Using data from two LES simulations of the same conditions with diﬀerent domain sizes and resolutions
of the atmospheric boundary layer at two heights, a series of spectra and autocorrelations have been
analyzed in order to investigate Taylor’s frozen turbulence hypothesis.
Each simulation provided a
diﬀerent set of conditions regarding the accuracy of the hypothesis, and had their own distinct insights.
The small domain simulation follows the conclusions of Tong [10] in which Taylor’s hypothesis is more
accurate for the crosswise and vertical velocity components.
However, for the streamwise velocity
component the convection velocity was better represented at nearly all scales by a velocity much slower
than the mean wind speed, and corrections that adjust as a function of wavenumber were less accurate
than choosing a single velocity. This may be due to the simulation not having a large enough domain to
represent the energy containing scales for that velocity component as evidenced by ﬁgure 11a. However,
for all three velocity components the autocorrelation shows that Taylor’s hypothesis overestimates the

The large domain simulation follows Taylor’s hypothesis more accurately for all velocity components.
Therefore, the domain contains the energy-containing scales and ﬁgures 18 and 21 show a better balance
between the integral length and time scales and the domain size/sample rate. However, the behavior
of the small scale turbulent structures in the large domain simulation indicates the opposite of the
typical convection velocity. Smaller eddies typically are advected slower than the larger eddies, causing
the typical error in Taylor’s hypothesis that has been well documented with many diﬀerent methods
for correcting, but in this simulation they advect more quickly than the larger scales. While this may
indicate forces that eﬀect smaller scales more than larger scales, such as the coriolis force, are causing
this unexpected behavior, the same behavior is seen in the larger simulation when only considering
a domain the same size as the small scale simulation.
Therefore, it may be a result of the lower
resolution of the large simulation, as would the very long decorrelation times for the streamwise velocity
component. Wilczek et al. [13] discusses some limitations and issues concerning low resolution LES
that are seen in these results. However, the large scale LES behavior for the larger turbulent structures
follows Taylor’s hypothesis quite accurately. This matches expectations well, as the energy containing
structures are expected to advect at the mean wind speed.
Further studies may consider comparing the results of the large scale simulation to similar LES models
of that scale in similar conditions to analyze and conﬁrm whether Coriolis forces are noticeable in
the smaller turbulent structures.
Also, comparisons to LiDAR data gathered in similar conditions
can provide experimental support to the conclusions drawn from LES. Potentially, combining LiDAR
data and Taylor’s hypothesis can reduce risk regarding calculating turbulence intensity for wind farms
encompassing a large spatial domain.
