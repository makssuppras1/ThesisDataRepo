Abstract
Balanced armature receivers have in the last decades gained popularity due to their
small size, large sound output and energy eﬀiciency. They are often used in high-
fidelity in-ear headphones and hearing-aids, where the demand for high quality, low
distortion sound reproduction at high sound volumes is a key parameter. The work-
ing principle of these balanced armature receivers is however inherently nonlinear.
The nonlinear working principle carries along a number of unwanted nonlinear ef-
fects in the frequency response of the sound output of the receivers, which ultimately
reduces the sound quality of balanced armature receivers, especially at high sound
volumes.
At the time of writing, there has to the author’s knowledge not been published
any work where perturbation analysis is conducted on a balanced armature model,
describing the armature dynamics and electro-magnetic circuit. Nor has any ex-
perimental research into the mechanical stop, the system backbones, the nonlinear
damping ratios or the resonance frequency increase caused by the electro-magnetic
system been found.
This present work presents a number of experimental methods for determining
receiver nonlinearities, as well as the results of these experimental investigations.
Furthermore, a theoretical investigation, using perturbation analysis, of a model de-
scribing the armature and electro-magnetic dynamics is presented. The theoretical
analysis shows that the analysed model is able to describe all the nonlinear phe-
nomena identified in the experimental investigation, with the exception of a strong
nonlinearity caused by the armature impacting an internal mechanical stop. In the
experimental analysis, the possible nonlinear effects of magnetic saturation has not
been investigated and nor is this effect included in the theoretical model analysed.
In the experimental analysis, nonlinear effects such as harmonic distortion, non-
linear resonance amplitude-frequency relations as well as nonlinear damping are
identified. Furthermore, effects specific to the electro-magnetic systems are iden-
tified, such as an increase in resonance frequency, electric damping due to electric
resistance in the coil and softening restoring forces.

Introduction
This thesis concerns the identification, analysis and mathematical modelling of in-
herent nonlinearities in balanced armature loudspeakers, often known as balanced
armature receivers (BARs) or balanced armature drivers (BADs). The work includes
experimental testing of a balanced armature receiver, subsequent identification of
characteristic nonlinear features of the receiver, and analysis of an analytical model
describing the identified non-linearities.
The balanced armature principle has been around since the late 1870s, where
the first patents outlining the principle were granted [1, 2]. The balanced arma-
ture principle saw widespread use up until the 1930s, after which the moving coil
loudspeakers largely took over [2]. In recent years, however, balanced armature re-
ceivers are again being developed and produced in volume due to their small size,
large sound output and high energy eﬀiciency compared to moving coil receivers.
These characteristics make the balanced armature receivers the preferred choice for
hearing aids as well as high-end in-ear headphones. In order to create high fidelity
sound reproduction, hearing-aid and earphone manufacturers rely on the linearity
of the receivers across the entire frequency range of the devices. Balanced armature
receivers are however inherently non-linear due to the nature of the electro-magnetic
working principle and nonlinear damping. These non-linearities can be a source of
distortion, especially noticeable at high sound levels and high frequencies, at which
in particular hearing-aids often operate. Therefore, it is imperative to identify, un-
derstand and be able to model the inherent nonlinearities of balanced armature
receivers in order to improve the fidelity of the hearing-aids and in-ear headphones.
This can be achieved by mitigating the effects of the nonlinearities, either by e.g.
design or signal control.
In 1954, F. V. Hunt derived an equivalent electrical circuit using an electro-
mechano analogy for the magnetic circuit of a balanced armature receiver, cf. fig-
ure 7.8 in [1]. Further research into balanced armature receivers and the inherent
nonlinearities of these are however confined to the last decade, with Jensen [3,
4] pioneering research into nonlinearities of balanced armature receivers using Fi-
nite Element Modelling (FEM) and electro-mechano-acoustic (EMA) analogy [5] to
derive a Lumped Element (LE) model described in an equivalent electrical circuit.
Further research into balanced armature receiver nonlinearity has almost exclusively
revolved around LE modelling of the balanced armature receivers: Klippel were in
2016 granted a patent [6] which, in similar fashion to Jensen, describes a nonlinear
LE model for balanced armature receivers. This model has been implemented and
tested in [7] which found discrepancies between experimental data and model pre-
dictions. Subsequent research has broadly speaking revolved around improving the
LE model proposed in [4, 6]. In [8], Klippel later improved upon the model by in-
cluding magnetic saturation and allowing for asymmetries caused by an offset of the
armature rest position, [9] included asymmetrical magnetic flux, [10] included mag-
netic fringing and magnetic flux leakage, [11] tried to improve the electro-magnetic

A noticeable limitation of the applied LE method is the representation of the
armature as a single point mass with a spring and a damper applied, thus only
including a single mode of the armature vibrations. To improve this, [13] includes
more modes using modal expansion in a ”LE multimode model” whereas [14, 15]

of LE modelling with FE analysis or Boundary Element Modelling (BEM) used
for modelling the acoustics effect are described in various forms in [16–21].
In
the literature review, no publications were found on experimental identification of
nonlinearities in balanced armature receivers or analytical perturbation analysis of
Equations of Motion (EOMs) describing balanced armature receivers.
In all the research into balanced armature receivers mentioned above, the au-
thors are trying to create LE models capable of describing the balanced armature
receiver output by including, to various degrees, the inherent nonlinearities in the
system such as magnetic, electromagnetic or acoustic. The response of the system
is then simulated numerically and the response of the receiver predicted for specific
parameter values. Thus, to analyse the effect of various nonlinearities in the LE
models, parametric studies has to be performed using numerical simulation. These
parametric studies get increasingly complicated as more and more parameters are
included in the models, severely reducing insights into the effects of the different
nonlinearities. This thesis therefore takes a different approach than LE modelling
and simulation in order not just to simulate the receiver output, but to gain insights
into the sources of nonlinearities; which nonlinear effects are protruding in the re-
sponse, what causes them and which physical parameters have the greatest effect
on them.
This present work consists of both experimental testing and theoretical analysis.
Experimental testing of a balanced armature receiver is conducted using mainly a
laser-vibrometer, measuring the vibrations of the armature as well as sound pressure
measurements, to identify nonlinearities and the nature of these. The frequency
response backbones of the relevant resonances are determined, the damping as a
function of amplitude and velocity is investigated, and the response is analysed for
secondary resonances as well as higher harmonics. Theoretical investigation using
perturbation analysis is conducted on a LE mathematical model found in the litera-
ture which describes the receiver dynamics, and the effect of various parameters on
the identified nonlinear vibration effects is investigated. The approximate solution
obtained using perturbation analysis is numerically validated.
Chapter 2 provides background on the design and working principle of balanced
armature receivers and presents an extensive list of magnetic force expressions for
balanced armature receivers found in the available literature. Chapter 3 describes
the experimental setup, the methods used for testing the balanced armature receiver,
and outlines the methods used in the signal processing. The observations made
during the experimental analysis and the experimentally identified nonlinearities
are presented in section 3.4. In chapter 3, a theoretical analysis of a mathematical
model describing the receiver dynamics is conducted, which includes perturbation
analysis, a subsequent a parameter study and numerical validation. Inchapter 5,
the results of the experimental investigation are discussed in relation to the insights

2
Background
This chapter provides background on the design and working principle of balanced
armature receivers and presents a collection of magnetic force expressions for bal-
anced armature receivers. Section 2.1 describes the design and working principle of
balanced armature receiver, and section 2.2 descrribes and compares the different
magnetic force expressions found in the literature.
2.1
Balanced Armature Receivers
Balanced armature receivers are fixed coil devices which relies on the movement of
an armature within a magnetic field to create sound via a membrane. This working
principle is different from traditional loudspeakers, which are moving coil devices
where a coil attached to a cone moves within a magnetic field. Figure 2.1 illustrates
the construction of a typical balanced armature receiver.
Figure 2.1: Cross-section of a typical balanced armature receiver (E-shaped armature).
(Courtesy of Sonion A/S and [4]).
Balanced armature receivers consist of a flexible armature, which is clamped or
hinged at one end, opposite to the drive pin. The drive pin connects the armature to
the sound producing membrane. A fixed coil surrounds the armature and magnetises
it, thus resulting in movement of the armature within the magnetic field created by
the permanent magnets.
The armature can generally be of three types, as illustrated in fig. 2.2: U-shaped,
E-shaped and M-shaped [22].
The U-shaped armature generates more vibration
along the axis of the armature, whereas the other types of armatures reduce vibra-
tions in the direction parallel to the longest dimension of the armature [22]. The
U-shaped armature is connected to the permanent magnet housing in one place (top
or bottom of the housing), whereas the E- or M-shaped armatures are connected to
the permanent magnet housing at each side of the housing. The magnetic circuits
of the E- and M shaped armatures thus differ from the U-shape armature. This
change in the magnetic circuit affects the way force is generated on the armature
[17].

2.1.1
Working principle of the balanced armature receiver
When there is not flowing any current through the fixed coil, the armature is bal-
anced between the two magnets, as the top and bottom magnets are trying to attract
the armature with equal forces. Any imbalances in the magnetomotive force of the
permanent magnets could result in the armature sticking to one of the magnets.
This is avoided mainly by the restoring force of the clamping of the armature, thus
keeping the armature balanced when an imbalance of magnetic forces are present.
When the coil is exited by a current, a magnetising field is created around the coil,
which in turn magnetises the armature. Depending on the direction of the current,
the armature is either attracted to the upper or lower magnet and through the drive
pin, the membrane is moved thus producing sound which is omitted through the
spout.
Despite the armature being clamped at one end, there is still a risk that the arma-
ture can stick to one of the two permanent magnets. This can, amongst other things,
happen if the current sent through the coil is suﬀiciently large for the magnetic force
to overcome the restoring forces of the armature clamping and the membrane, or
if the receiver is dropped resulting in large deflections of the armature. In order
to mitigate this risk, some receivers include a ’mechanical’ stop, which limits the
deflection of the armature thus ensuring that armature deflection cannot reach the
point where the armature will risk sticking to one of the magnets.
The electro-magnetic working principle of balanced armature receivers is inher-
ently nonlinear, as any deflection of the armature inevitably causes a change in the
magnetic circuit and thus in the electrical inductance [3]. Besides the inherent non-
linear working principle, a number of other sources of nonlinearities are present in
balanced armature receivers, with nonlinearity caused by magnetic saturation being
dominant in most balanced armature receivers according to [8].

2.2.7
Summary on the magnetic force expressions
Throughout section 2.2, six different expressions for the force acting on the arma-
ture in a balanced armature receiver has been described. The expressions have been
found through a literature study, where the first expression for the magnetic force
is found in the 2011 paper by Jensen [3]. All the subsequent five magnetic force
expression appear to build upon the 2011 Jensen model, which acts as a ’baseline’
model for balanced armature receiver dynamic. The Klippel models [6, 8] includes
saturation, the Tsai and Huang model [9] includes asymmetry effects, the Xu, Jiang
and Hang [16, 18] model includes asymmetries as well as the effect of magnetisation

is concerned with U-shaped armatures. All the additions to the models mentioned
above yield very long magnetic force expressions with a significant increase in con-

complicates a study of the effects of each parameter on the frequency response of the
models. A parameter study should instead be carried out with the simplest model
that is still able to describe the nonlinear effects found in experimental investiga-

models are build, it is recommended to conduct a parameter study using the Jensen
model.

3
Experimental Analysis
Two experimental analyses are conducted and reported in this chapter. The first
investigates nonlinear effects in the sound output of a balanced armature receiver,
whereas the second investigates nonlinear effects in the vibrations of the armature
of the balanced armature receiver. In order to mainly study the armature dynamics
and the electro-magnetic working principle driving the armature, the acoustic effects
of the receivers are reduced as much as possible for these experiments but not entirely
removed. The two-folded testing strategy allows for a comparison between the non-
linearities in the sound output and the armature vibration. This comparison aims
at determining if any of the nonlinear effects are caused strictly by the electro-
magnetic/mechanical working principle of the receivers, or if some are strictly caused
by the acoustic effects.
In this chapter, the experimental setup, experimental procedures and signal pro-
cessing methods are described in sections 3.1 to 3.3. The results obtained using
these methods are described in section 3.4.

3.1.4
DUT Fixation
The fixation of the DUT is an important parameter, as the coupled system of the
fixation device and DUT can have resonances within the frequency range under
study, or otherwise affect the output sound pressure levels/armature response. The
effect of the stiffness of the fixation is investigated by measuring the vibration of
the DUT and the vibration of the fixation device for four different types of fixations
with various stiffness. The aim of the fixations study is to determine whether the
fixation stiffness significantly affects the dynamics of the DUT. The four different
fixations allows for a comparative analysis of the effect changing fixation stiffness.
The four tested fixations are illustrated in fig. 3.8 and listed below:
• Free hanging: A free hanging fixation where the DUT is suspended from the
coupler using a soft rubber tube, allowing the DUT to vibrate as freely as
possible.
• Double-sided tape: A thick piece of insulating double-sided tape is applied to
the backside of the DUT and attaches it to a large piece of metal.
• Beeswax: A very thin layer of beeswax is applied to the backside of the DUT
and attaches it to a large piece of metal.
• Drill-vice: A heavy drill-vice, which has been demagnetised, is used to fixate
the DUT by clamping the sides of the DUT.

3.2
Experimental procedure
Three different experimental procedures are employed for identifying non-linear ef-
fects in both the DUT sound output and the armature vibration response:
1. Frequency sweep. The amplitude of armature vibrations or the sound output
is measured as the input signal is sweeping over a predefined interval ranging
from 100Hz to 20kHz, covering the audible range.
2. Frequency spectrum (constant frequency excitation). A frequency spectrum is
made of the armature vibrations for each of the resonance frequencies identified
in the frequency sweep.
3. Ring outs (Free decaying vibrations). Ring-out measurements are made for
armature vibrations at each of the resonance frequencies identified in the fre-
quency sweep.
3.2.1
Frequency sweeps
The frequency sweeps responses are obtained by sending a sweeping sine excitation
signal with a defined amplitude to the DUT and then measuring the response am-
plitude. The frequency of the sine signal sweeps slowly through a defined frequency
interval, starting from the lowest frequency in the interval. The response ampli-
tude is found by Fast-Fourier-Transforming (FFT) the time-series response for a
small interval of the frequency sweep, and the amplitude is then extracted as the
max. amplitude in the FFT spectra. The input frequency within this interval is
considered constant if the sweep rate (Hz/s) is low. A low sweep rate ensures that
the resonances have time to fully develop. The amplitudes are found for a number
of excitation frequencies within the sweep interval, thus making up the frequency
response curve.
The frequency sweeps are conducted for the DUT sound output as well as the
armature vibration measurements.
This is done using two experimental setups:
Experimental setup C for the sound pressure measurements and experimental setup
B for the armature vibration measurements. The signal generation, data gathering
and initial signal processing (signal processing done by the Polytec and PULSE
software) is therefore conducted using two different systems.
Armature frequency sweeps
Experimental setup B and the Polytec PSV Acquisition software is used for mea-
suring frequency sweep responses of the armature vibrations. The PSV Acquisition
software has in in-build macro PeakHoldSettings.bas which uses a Peak Hold av-
eraging algorithm. Based on the number of requested FFT amplitude points and
the frequency sweep interval, the macro calculates the average number and the sam-
ple time. It is possible to select the overlap of the FFT spectra time series, which
effectively is how the sweep rate is controlled. In order to determine whether a re-
duced sweep time of 258s (corresponding to a 75% overlap using 3185 spectra) fully
captures the resonances compared to a sweep time of 1020s (corresponding to a 0%
overlap using 3185 spectra), two frequency sweeps are conducted using both sweep
times for two different input voltages: ≈1.4V and ≈2.2V. The frequency sweeps are
illustrated in fig. 3.18.

3.2.3
Ring-out measurements
The ring-out measurements are conducted on the armature vibrations using experi-
mental setup B. The armature is excited by a pure sine signal with a frequency cor-
responding to each of the resonance frequencies identified in the frequency sweeps.
After the transient vibrations has decayed away, time-series measurements of the
armature vibrations are initiated and the excitation signal is then turned off. The
measurements thus include the steady-state vibrations as well as the free decaying
vibrations (the ring-out).
As explained in section 2.1, the armature vibrations within the permanent mag-
netic field induce a current in the coil. As there is resistance in the coil, a loss
of energy (heat energy) caused by current running through the coil is expected if
the coil is part of a closed electric circuit. This loss of energy will effectively act
as added damping to the system. When the excitation signal is turned off by set-
ting the excitation signal to 0V, the amplifier will short circuit, thus creating a
closed circuit with the coil. Figure 3.20 illustrates two cases where the amplifier has
short-circuited. Figure 3.20a illustrates the system without the physical switch and
fig. 3.20b illustrates the system with the physical switch. When the physical switch
is activated, the coil is no longer part of a closed circuit, and thus no current will
run through the coil. A smaller damping ratio is therefore expected for this case.

In order to be able to distinguish between mechanical damping (i.e. external
viscous damping or internal damping in the armature) and the damping caused by
the amplifier short circuiting, two different input-signal cutoff methods are employed.
A cutoff conducted by physically disconnecting the system and a cutoff conducted
digitally using the data gathering and signal generating software (PSV Acquisition).
When using the digital switch method, the electro-magnetic system is still engaged
and the effects caused by the electro-magnetic system are expected to remain. The
physical switch method effectively disengages the entire electro-magnetic system,
and effects unique to the electro-magnetic system is expected to disappear.
Physical switch procedure
The excitation signal is physically turned off by activating the physical switch in-
cluded in the circuit after the NAD amplifier, cf. fig. 3.5. In order to ensure that
the entire ring-out is recorded, the following work flow is used:
1. The pure sine excitation signal is turned on using PSV Acquisition signal
generator.
2. The data gathering is turned on in the PSV Acquisition software after 10s,
thus ensuring that transients has died out.
3. The physical switch is activated immediately after.

Displacement-series calculation
In order to determine resonance frequency and damping as a function of arma-
ture vibration amplitude, the velocity-time series have to be transformed into a
displacement-time series by numerical integration. As the time-series is integrated,
even small ’imperfections’ of the velocity-time series will result in large ’imperfec-
tions’ in the displacement data. Furthermore, in fig. 3.32 it is noted, that the sudden
signal cutoff of the physical and digital switch excites unwanted slower vibrations.
45
==PAGE:53==
3.3.
Signal processing
The effect of these velocity-series ’imperfections’ has to be reduced in order to be able
to analyse the corresponding displacement data. Therefore, the following scheme
for obtaining the displacement-time series is used:
1. The velocity data is highpass filtered with a cutoff frequency defined as half
the excitation frequency. The unwanted slow vibration is thereby omitted.
2. The mean of the highpass filtered velocity data is subtracted.
3. The velocity data is then numerically integrated using Matlab’s cumtrapz
function to obtain a displacement-time series.
4. The mean of the displacement data is retracted, thus obtaining the displacement-
time series used for subsequent analysis.

Effect of the cutoff method
Three different excitation signal cutoff methods has been used when generating the
ring-out measurements. Two methods relies on a digital cutoff of the signal, the
’digital switch’ method and the ’modified Heaviside’ method. These digital cutoffs
allow the amplifier to short circuit after the excitation signal is turned off, thus
creating a closed circuit with the coil. This is explained in detail in section 3.2.3
and illustrated in fig. 3.20. The closed circuit allows for current to flow through
the coil after the excitation signal has been put to zero, meaning that the electro-
magnetic effects of the system is expected to remain during the ring-out. The third
cutoff method, the ’physical switch’, utilises a physical switch which ensures that the
electric circuit containing the coil is not closed. Hereby, no current can run through
the coil during the ring-outs and the electro-magnetic effects of the system are thus
expected to disappear. In figs. 3.60 and 3.61, the normalised backbones and linear
equivalent damping ratios for resonance 1 using the three different cutoff methods
are illustrated. The damping ratios at velocity estimates lower than 0.1 m/s have
been omitted, as the amplitude of the vibrations were too small to consistently
estimate the damping ratios.

3.4.5
Summary of experimental observations
Throughout section 3.4, the nonlinear effects in the sound output as well as internal
dynamics of the balanced TEC-30087 receiver has been investigated. The results
are summarised below.
Sound output results
Three resonances were identified in the sound output of the receiver in the frequency
interval [100Hz 10kHz]. The backbones of each of the nonlinearities all indicated
effects of softening restoring forces. A distinct feature of the backbone of resonance
1 was the presence of a strong non-linearity, which occurs for input voltages of 1.3V
and higher. The damping ratios estimated from the sound out were found to not be
constant, with the damping ratios at resonance varying linearly and the damping
ratios of other resonances varying in a quadratic manner. Furthermore, it was found
that the backbone of resonance 1 appeared to be affected by the large damping ratio.
Armature output results
In the armature vibration response, three resonances were also identified. The three
resonances cover an interval of [100Hz 20kHz]. The first resonance in the arma-
ture response was found to be the same as resonance 1 in the sound output. It
was therefore concluded that this resonance is mainly an armature resonance. The
second and third resonances in the armature response were not found in the sound
output. The backbones of the first and third armature resonance indicated softening
restoring forces, whereas the second resonance indicated a hardening restoring force.
The strong nonlinearity from the sound output resonance 1 was also found in the
armature response resonance 1. The strong nonlinearity was investigated, and it
was concluded that the strong nonlinearity is caused by the armature hitting a me-
chanical stop. The mechanical stop was found to allow for armature tip deflections
of approximately 85µm.
The damping ratios for each of the three armature resonances were found to
all be more or less constant, with the estimated constant damping ratios being
approximately 15%, 7% and 2.3% for resonance 1-3 respectively. The constant nature
of the armature damping ratios were in stark contrast to the varying damping ratios
in the sound output. The varying damping ratios were therefore found to be mainly
acoustic effect.
Ring-out results
The ring-out method allows for analysis of the system with the electro-magnetic sys-
tem/dynamics removed. By comparing measurement with and without the electro-
magnetic effects, it was found that the electro-magnetic system increases the res-
onance frequency of armature resonance 1 by approximately 30%. Furthermore,
the electro-magnetic system affects the restoring forces in the system in a softening
manner. This was found to align with the working principle of balanced armature re-
ceivers, where the magnetic excitation force increases with armature displacement.
The comparison also revealed that the viscous and internal mechanical damping
accounts for a damping ratio of around 10% whereas the electro-magnetic system

Frequency spectra results
From the armature response frequency spectra, it was found that for all three res-

impacted at resonance 1, a significant increase in harmonic distorting is observed
and extra peaks in the spectra occurs at n/2 times the excitation frequency. At
resonance 2, signs of internal resonance were found as the first odd higher harmonic
is very close to the resonance frequency of resonance 3.

4
Theoretical modelling and
validation
The dynamics of the balanced armature receiver has been modelled in a range of dif-
ferent publications [1],[4]-[36]. The first model of the working principle of a balanced
armature receiver was published by Hunt in 1954 [1]. The next attempt at modelling
the balanced armature were not published before 2011, where Jensen [3, 4], on the
basis of the work done by Hunt, derived a set of equations which govern the dy-
namics of the internals of the balanced armature receiver using lumped parameters.
The subsequent attempts at modelling the receiver has all been based more or less
on Jensen’s 2011 model, with various extra parameters included, such as geometric
asymmetries and magnetisation effect of various components (cf. section 2.2). The
Jensen 2011 model therefore makes up a sort of ’baseline’ model.
In order to gain insight into the basic working principle of a balanced arma-
ture receiver without having to keep track of more parameters than necessary, the
’baseline’ 2011 Jensen model is analysed. The aim of the analysis is to get an under-
standing of how the different parameters of the system affect the amplitude response,
and in particular the nonlinear effects observed and described in section 3.4. An-
other advantage of analysing the baseline model is that the limitations of the model
can be found, and an estimate of how well the baseline model is able to model the
nonlinear amplitude response can be determined.

4.4
Summary of theoretical modelling
Throughout section 4.2, a model describing the armature dynamics has been anal-
ysed.
The model is derived from the Jensen 2011 paper [3], which proposed a
nonlinear lumped element model consisting of two coupled governing equations; an
equation governing the armature vibrations and an equation governing the electric
system. The model is only able to capture a single resonance peak as the model is
a lumped element model where only the armature has been modelled by a spring, a
damper and and a point mass.
A perturbation analysis was carried out on a nondimensional, Taylor-expanded
version of the Jensen 2011 model. The analysis revealed that for a constant am-
plitude input voltage Ein, the amplitude of the current θ is inversely proportional
to the input voltage frequency Ω, meaning that the force generated on the arma-
ture decreases with increasing excitation frequency. It was furthermore showed that
the model was able to exhibit a left bending backbone, damping dependent back-
bones, both odd and even higher harmonics, and an equivalent resonance frequency
which increases as the coeﬀicient determining the magnitude of the nonlinearities
increase. Furthermore, two damping mechanisms were found, namely a mechanical
damping in the form of viscous damping as well as an electric damping which has a
dependency on the DC resistance in the coil.
A parameter study of the four parameters p, γ, ζ, β was conducted. It revealed
that the parameter p, which is the forcing parameter, increases the amplitude of the
frequency response, with the amplitude following the damping dependent backbone.
The ζ parameter was found to control the effective mechanical damping in the
system, as well as being able to decrease the equivalent linear resonance frequency.
The β-parameter was found to control the electric damping of the system. This
effect was however not captured by the MS solution but was instead found through
simulation. The γ-parameter was found to both control the nonlinear effects such as
backbone bending as well acting as a forcing term. This means that the unwanted
nonlinear effects cannot be reduced without also reducing the sound generation
eﬀiciency of the receiver.
The MS approximation was able to predict odd order higher harmonics of with a
frequency of three times the excitation frequency. The ratio between the amplitude
of the main resonance Ωand the 3Ωamplitude was determined as r = 16γa2, mean-
ing that the magnitude of the harmonic distortion is controlled by the γ-parameter
and increases with the squared amplitude. Through simulation, it was concluded
that the nondimensional, Taylor-expanded version of the Jensen 2011 model is able
to also predict even order higher harmonics.

5
Discussion
In this chapter, the effects of the identified nonlinearities and the analysed theoretical
model describing the armature dynamics are discussed, along with the accuracy and
limitations of the model. Additionally, methods for fitting a nonlinear model are
explained and extensions to the theoretical model are also discussed.
5.1
Identified nonlinear effects and their impact
In section 3.4, a number of nonlinear effects were found in both the sound output
and in the armature vibrations. These nonlinear effects are in general unwanted as
they can cause harmonic distortion, or they amplify certain frequencies (resonance
frequencies) in a nonlinear manner, i.e. the resonance frequencies change depending
on the amplitude.
The most prominent nonlinear effect observed both in the sound output and in
the armature vibration measurements was the vibro-impact caused by a hard me-
chanical stop. It was found that when the mechanical stop was impacted, the ratio
of the amplitude of the first even order higher harmonic compared to the main fre-
quency was increased 3.5 times, and the ratio of the first odd harmonic increased 45
times. Assuming that the higher harmonics increase by the square of the amplitude
for both the even and odd order higher harmonics (cf. eq. (4.78)), the amplitude
corrected increase of the amplitude ratio of the higher harmonics are approximately
45% and 1900% for the even and odd harmonics respectively. This very large in-
crease in harmonic distortion will likely reduce the quality of speech understanding
and sound reproduction in hearing aid devices. Thus, impacting the mechanical stop
should therefore be avoided during operation in hearing-aids. It was found that the
mechanical stop was impacted during the sound measurements at an excitation
voltage of 1.3V at 1920kHz and at a measured sound level of approximately 130dB.
Whether this is within normal operating range is yet to be determined, however
by identifying this limit, it is possible to program the hearing aid device controller
such that it does not exceed the found limit. When designing a new receiver, it
would also be good practice to consider how to increase the amplitude at which the
armature can vibrate before impacting the mechanical stop.
Measurements revealing at which excitation voltage the armature impacts the
mechanical stop are of relevance in order to determine within which limits the re-
ceiver is able to operate without too much harmonic distortion. If the receiver under
test does not allow for measurements directly on the armature, backbones found us-
ing frequency sweeps of the sound output can be used to estimate of the voltage
needed for the armature to impact with the mechanical stop.
The most characteristic feature of nonlinear mechanical systems are their back-
bones. The backbones contain information on how the resonance frequency depends
on vibration amplitude, but more importantly, the characteristics of the backbones
contain information on the behaviour of nonlinear restoring forces. In the sound
output, all three identified resonances had left bending backbones, when ignoring

5.3
Magnetic saturation
In this thesis, the nonlinear effect caused by magnetic saturation has not been inves-
tigated. Magnetic saturation is the state reached when an applied external magnetic
field cannot increase the magnetisation of the armature further and the flux density
more or less levels off, resulting in the armature force levelling off. According to [8],
magnetic saturation is the most dominant nonlinearity in most balanced armature
receivers. As the current in the coil increases, the magnetic saturation reduces the
effect of the current on the force generated, cf. section 2.2.3. Therefore, to deter-
mine the effect of magnetic saturation, the eﬀiciency of the receiver, defined as the
ratio between current and armature amplitude, would have to be experimentally
determined for increasing excitation signal amplitudes. Magnetic saturation would
thereby be identified by a decrease in receiver eﬀiciency. This effect could be char-
acterised as nonlinear receiver eﬀiciency. Whether this nonlinear effect also affects
other nonlinear characteristics such as the backbones or higher harmonics have not
yet been determined. To determine the effect of magnetic saturation, perturbation
analysis should be conducted on the model proposed in [8], which includes nonlinear
saturation in the force term.
5.4
Mitigation of nonlinear effects
The approximate MS solution revealed that all nonlinearities, except for the me-
chanical stop, are governed either solely by the γ-parameter or by a combination of
the γ-parameter and the viscous damping parameter ζ. In order to reduce the effect
of the nonlinearities, the γ-parameter, cf. eq. (5.1), has to be reduced. There are two
options for reducing γ. The first option is to reduce the numerator in eq. (5.1). As
µ0 is the permeability of air and thus assumed constant, the numerator can only be
reduced by either reducing the size of the permanent magnet Ag or by reducing the
magneto motive force F0. The second option is to increase the denominator. This
can be done be increasing the mechanical stiffness of the armature kM and effective
distance between the permanent magnets D, while reducing Ag and Fm. Both op-
tions mentioned will also have the effect of reducing the eﬀiciency of the receiver, as
the magnetic force is reduced in each of the options. This parameter investigation
of γ confirms the findings of the parameter study in section 4.2.2. It was found
that the γ parameter controls the nonlinear effects such as backbone bending as
well acting as a forcing term. As found in eq. (5.1), the unwanted nonlinear effects
therefore cannot be reduced without also reducing the sound generation eﬀiciency
of the receiver. A reduction of the damping terms ζ and β will on the other hand
increase the eﬀiciency. However for very low damping, the MS approximation can
become unbounded as seen in fig. 4.3 resulting in stability issues. The different
parameters therefore all have to be a compromise between stability, magnitude of

Conclusions
Throughout this report, an experimental analysis of the nonlinear effects in the
sound output as well as the armature vibrations of a balanced armature receiver
has been presented. The experimental setup used for conducting the measurement,
the experimental procedures and the signal processing methods has been reported.
Using the outlined experimental procedures and methods, a number of nonlinear
effects were found in both the sound output and the armature vibrations. In the
sound output analysis, three resonances were identified within a frequency interval
of [100Hz 10kHz]. The backbones of all three resonances bent to the left, thus indi-
cating that softening restoring forces are governing the dynamics of each resonance.
A very characteristic strong nonlinearity was moreover identified in the frequency
response for resonance 1. This strong nonlinearity was likewise found in the reso-
nance frequency for resonance 1 in the armature vibrations. Due to the presence
of the strong nonlinearity as well as similar resonance frequencies, resonance 1 in
the sound output and resonance 1 in the armature vibrations were concluded to be
the same resonance. The strong nonlinearity was investigated further, and it was
found that the strong nonlinearity was caused by the armature impacting a hard
mechanical stop inside the receiver. The mechanical stop was impacted when the
amplitude of the armature tip was approximately 85µm, resulting in a measured
sound pressure level of approximately 135dB.
The mechanical stop impact was found to induce a very large increase in har-
monic distortion, with the ratio of the amplitudes of the higher harmonics com-
pared to the main frequency amplitude in the armature vibration increasing by up
to 1900%. This large increase in harmonic distortion is assumed critical in terms of
sound quality at high acoustic outputs. The damping ratio was also determined for
each of the resonances in the sound out, as well as the three resonances analysed in
the armature vibrations. The sound output damping was found to be nonlinear. It
was concluded that nonlinear damping is mainly an acoustic effect, as the damping
ratio of the armature vibrations were more constant.
The experimental analysis included the use of ring-out measurement. The ring-
out experiments allowed for the electro-magnetic part of the system to effectively
be turned off. By comparing the system with and without the effects of the electro-
magnetic system revealed a couple of nonlinear effects inherent to the electro-
magnetic system. The resonance frequency was found to be increased by 30% and
damping ratios were found to be increased by approximately 30% by the electro-
magnetic system.
The characteristic softening type restoring force were likewise
determined to be a feature inherent to the system with the electro-magnetic part
included.
In an attempt to gain further insight into the mechanisms and parameters af-
fecting the identified nonlinear effects, a model describing the armature dynamics
of balances armature receivers were analysed. The analysed model was derived by
Jensen in 2011, including his own 2014 errata, and includes the nonlinearities in-

herent to the working principle of a balanced armature receivers. The model does
however not include magnetic saturation. The model was nondimensionalised and
four parameters were found to govern the armature dynamics; a forcing parameter
p, a viscous damping parameter ζ, an electric damping parameter β and a combined
parameter γ.
The forcing parameter was found to only influence the amplitude
of the steady state vibrations, the ζ and β parameters influenced the damping of
the system and the γ-parameter both controlled the strength of the nonlinearities
as well as the forcing. The nonlinear effects governed by γ were found to be the
bending of the backbone as well as the higher harmonics, which were found to in-
crease with the square of the armature amplitude. Reducing the γ-parameter would
therefore decrease the nonlinear effects found in the stationary armature response.
However, as the γ-parameter also influences all forcing of the armature, a reduced
γ will also reduce the sound producing eﬀiciency of the receiver. The analysis of
the 2011 Jensen model thus concludes that it is not possible to reduce the nonlinear
effects without reducing the eﬀiciency of the receiver. The values of the different
parameters therefore all have to be a compromise between stability, magnitude of
nonlinear effects and receiver eﬀiciency. Such compromise will most likely vary de-
pending on the application of the receiver. Finding the right compromise is therefore
imperative for receiver performance. The parameter study and approximate Multi-
ple Scales solution can therefore be of great aid when deciding on the compromise
between eﬀiciency and sound quality, thus enabling customisation of receivers based
on specific applications.
The 2011 Jensen model was found to be able to describe all the nonlinear phe-
nomena identified in the experimental investigation, except for the mechanical stop,
which is not considered by the model. This implies that the different extensions
to the model, found throughout the literature, do not include any new nonlinear-
ities which are needed to explain the experimentally identified nonlinear effects.
Whether the model extensions increase the accuracy of the model in terms of sim-
ulation of the armature response has not been investigated. As mentioned above,
the most notable nonlinearity not included by the Jensen 2011 model is magnetic
saturation. Magnetic saturation is expected to decrease the eﬀiciency of the mag-
netic force generation on the armature. The eﬀiciency of the force generation on
the armature has not been experimentally investigated, and therefore it cannot be
concluded whether magnetic saturation significantly affects the receiver eﬀiciency in
a nonlinear manner.
The experimental and theoretical methods employed in this thesis were very
specifically aimed at identifying, describing and analysing the nonlinear effects found
in the sound output and armature dynamics of balanced armature drivers. To the
knowledge of the author, there has not been any previously published work regard-
ing the experimental identification of nonlinear damping and system backbones of
balanced armature drivers. Nor has the nonlinear effects specific to the electric-
magnetic system, such as the increase in resonance frequency and electric damping,
been experimentally determined. The identification of the mechanical stop, and at
which amplitude the impact occurs as well as the nonlinear damping, has also not
been described in the literature. The perturbation analysis of the equations gov-
erning the armature dynamics constitutes a new departure within the analysis of
balanced armature drivers and offers unique insights into the effect of the different

6.1
Future work
The perturbation analysis and approximate solution were found to only be accurate
for small values of γ and β ≈0. Further Multiple Scales analysis should be conducted
with the aim of at least including the β parameter in the approximate solution. This
would likely increase the accuracy of the MS solution, as well provide a means to
study the effect of the β parameter.
Throughout the perturbation analysis, a couple of atypical features of the back-
bones and frequency response were identified, with the most notable being the damp-
ing dependent backbone. In section 4.2, the backbone was only approximated using
the amplitude peak. The actual backbone was however not determined. Further
investigation into damping dependent backbones should be conducted. A publica-
tion on the matter was identified in [39]. Further analysis into vibro-impact systems
should also be undertaken in order to theoretically describe the frequency spectra
peaks at 1
2Ω, which were found to occur when the armature impacted the mechanical
stop.
The extensive experimental work conducted throughout this thesis did reveal a
number of areas which are still to experimentally investigated. The most important
areas still to investigate are listed below, alongside the questions these investigations
should answer:
1. Magnetic saturation. Is the receiver eﬀiciency affected by magnetic saturation?
2. Fixation analysis. How does the fixation affect nonlinear response character-
istics? How is the receiver response affected when operating in hearing aid
devices?
3. Frequency spectra of the sound output response to a constant frequency exci-
tation. What types of higher harmonics are occurring in the sound output?
4. Displacement-time series investigations on the mechanical stop. When exactly
is the mechanical stop impacted? Does the frequency spectra exhibit chaos-like
behaviour right around impact amplitude?
5. Measurements on the membrane and other parts of the armature. What types
of resonances occur? Pure armature, pure membrane or pure acoustic, such
as Helmholtz resonances?

Future work
The experimental work listed above would, among other insights, aid in determin-
ing which features to include in the mathematical model of the receiver dynamics.
Additions to the Jensen 2011 model could e.g. be the inclusion of the membrane,
modelling of the armature as a beam and then using modal expansion to include the

of the location of internals components such as the permanent magnets.

mapping of the internals of the receiver in order to locate the component which the
armature impacts. If it turns out not to be the permanent magnets, the component
acting as the mechanical stop can then possible be relocated, thus improving high
acoustic load performance of the receivers.

be included in a mathematical model. The current Jensen 2011 model only models
the armature dynamics. However, an investigation with the aim of determining how
to include these effects should be conducted. If successful, the nonlinear damping,
identified as an acoustic effect, could be included and insights into these mechanisms
could be gained.
An investigation into the effect of using a U-shaped armature should also be
conducted. This would include a new experimental analysis of a receiver that utilises
a U-shaped armature, as well as a new perturbation analysis using the model for
U-shaped armatures described in [17].
