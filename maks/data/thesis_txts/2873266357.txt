Abstract
As hybrid work becomes a permanent feature of the modern workplace, companies face increas-
ing pressure to understand and report its impact within environmental, social, and governance
(ESG) frameworks. This thesis investigates how hybrid work is positioned in corporate ESG
reporting and examines the role of interdepartmental collaboration, particularly between HR
and sustainability departments, in shaping these disclosures. Through a qualitative study com-
bining thematic analysis of 18 sustainability reports from companies and expert interviews from
two companies, the research ﬁnds that while hybrid work is frequently acknowledged as a tool
for employee well-being and talent retention, it is rarely quantiﬁed or integrated into environ-
mental metrics. Emissions related to employee commuting (Scope 3, Category 7) are reported
inconsistently, and remote work-related emissions are often overlooked. Moreover, HR and
ESG departments typically operate in silos, sharing little data or strategic alignment. To ad-
dress this gap, the thesis proposes a practical calculation tool to support standardized reporting
on commuting emissions and recommends organizational strategies to begin fostering cross-
functional collaboration. Overall, the ﬁndings highlight hybrid work as a cross-cutting issue
that is underrepresented in ESG reporting, despite its potential relevance across both social and
environmental dimensions.

1 | Introduction
The COVID-19 pandemic was a signiﬁcant event in history and the need for hybrid and ﬂexible
work arrangements became apparent as businesses tried to adapt to this new reality. The global
pandemic forced organizations to redeﬁne their work structures and this lead to the worldwide
implementation of hybrid work strategies (Hopkins and Bardoel 2023, Brînzea and Oancea
2022).
There are several beneﬁts to implementing hybrid work models, as studies indicate that in-
creased ﬂexibility leads to higher job satisfaction and improved work-life balance for employees
(Zaric, Perovic, and Jasarovic 2024). Organizations also beneﬁt, as they can lower operational
costs by reducing ofﬁce space and limiting unused areas (Hopkins and Bardoel 2023). Hybrid
work models further enable businesses to broaden their talent pool by removing the need for
proximity to a physical ofﬁce, allowing them to hire based on skills rather than geographical
location (Tahlyan et al. 2024). Additionally, since fewer people commute to the ofﬁce, hy-
brid models offer short-term environmental beneﬁts, such as reduced congestion in larger cities
(Roberto et al. 2023).
However, hybrid work is not without its challenges. A big issue when it comes to hybrid work
is communication barriers (Vartiainen and Vanharanta 2024). Many hybrid teams rely on tools
like emails and messaging platforms, and while these tools are helpful, they can also lead to
issues such as delayed responses, misinterpretations of messages, and an overload of informa-
tion. Other problems may also arise when using hybrid work models, as remote workers often
struggle with limited career advancement opportunities. This is because in-ofﬁce workers have
better networking opportunities (Audrin et al. 2025) than their remote colleagues. Feelings of
loneliness and isolation are also frequently reported among hybrid workers, especially when
remote work is the dominant mode (Ferrara et al. 2022). Without regular in-person contact,
employees may experience a lack of belonging and diminished connection to their team, which
can affect both well-being and engagement. Organizations also struggle with maintaining com-
pany culture and fostering engagement in work environments where teams are not located in
the same place (Franzen-Waschke 2021). This makes leadership especially challenging and de-
mands new management strategies to ensure cohesion and motivation across distributed teams.
While the implementation of hybrid work in organizations has indicated clear short-term bene-
==PAGE:11==
2
ﬁts as well as challenges for both the employees and employers, the implementation was driven
by necessity and potential long-term effects are still uncertain. As companies are now required
to reﬂect more holistically on their practices, the introduction of stricter non-ﬁnancial reporting
requirements from the European Union (EU) may give valuable insights into these potential
issues.
Non-ﬁnancial reporting, now known as Environmental, Social and Governance (ESG) report-
ing, has become a big part of how corporations show transparency and accountability in recent
years, driven by growing concerns over sustainability by stakeholders. Hybrid work even has
the potential to impact all three dimensions of ESG reporting. Environmentally, "E", hybrid
work can reduce commuting-related emissions and ofﬁce energy consumption, contributing to a
company’s carbon footprint reduction goals (Crow and Millot 2020). Socially, it inﬂuences em-
ployee well-being, work-life balance, inclusion, and diversity (Franzen-Waschke 2021), which
are key components of the “S” in ESG. From a governance, "G", perspective, hybrid work
challenges traditional management practices and requires updated policies to ensure account-
ability, data privacy, and equitable treatment of employees regardless of their work location
(Waizenegger et al. 2020). In the EU, the evolution of ESG reporting has been shaped by a
series of regulatory directives aimed at promoting corporate responsibility and transparency.
As companies adapt to these new requirements, ESG reporting frameworks can end up being an
important puzzle piece in how companies structure their sustainability initiatives. The frame-
works not only ensure legal compliance but also help companies identify workplace trends, such
as the rise of hybrid work. Given that hybrid work is still a relatively new tool for many busi-
nesses, it is essential to examine how it ﬁts into companies’ sustainability goals and employee
well-being within the ESG frameworks.
But addressing hybrid work in a meaningful and strategic way in ESG reporting would require
collaboration across departments, particularly between Human Resources (HR) and sustainabil-
ity teams. HR departments typically manage policies related to employee well-being, inclusion,
and ﬂexible work arrangements, while sustainability teams oversee environmental impact as-
sessments and ESG reporting. However, these departments have historically operated in silos,
and cross-functional collaboration remains limited (Waal et al. 2019). As hybrid work intersects
with both social and environmental dimensions of ESG, better coordination between HR and
sustainability departments may be necessary to ensure consistent reporting, integrated strategy-
making, and the development of supportive internal policies. Understanding how, and whether,
==PAGE:12==
3
such collaboration takes place is crucial to assessing how organizations embed hybrid work into
their broader sustainability efforts.
1.1
Aim of Thesis
On this foundation, the purpose of this thesis is to examine whether hybrid work is incorporated
into organizations’ ESG reporting; speciﬁcally within the environmental and social dimensions,
and, if so, how it is represented. Furthermore, the thesis explores the role of interdepartmental
collaboration in shaping how hybrid work is addressed in ESG reporting. The thesis aims to
identify how companies can best utilize interdepartmental collaboration to improve on their
ESG reporting practices, especially when it comes to the incorporation of hybrid work and the
environmental and social implications that hybrid work has on an organization.
Based on the aim of this thesis, three guiding questions have been developed to structure the
investigation:
1. How do companies incorporate hybrid work into their ESG reporting, particularly within
the environmental and social pillars?
2. What role does interdepartmental collaboration play in shaping hybrid work’s contribu-
tion to both environmental impact and employee well-being?
3. What practical steps can companies take to improve collaboration between HR and sus-
tainability departments to enhance ESG reporting?
To address these questions, this thesis will conduct a literature review with a focus on hybrid
work and its inﬂuence on the corporate work structure during and after the COVID-19 pan-
demic. In the literature review, an overview of ESG reporting frameworks and their relevance
to hybrid work will be given. Lastly, a short introduction to interdepartmental collaboration is
given, as well as a more in depth description of how information silos, a big challenge when
it comes to interdepartmental collaboration, inﬂuences both hybrid work and ESG reporting
practices. Then, data will be collected through multiple ways. First, an analysis of published
sustainability reports will be done to see how companies handle their data regarding hybrid
work. This is done to explore how companies are prioritizing hybrid work in their external
communication regarding sustainability. Then, several interviews will be conducted to give a
more qualitative picture of how individual companies are responding to the challenges regard-
ing ESG reporting and hybrid work to see how these two subjects overlap internally in the
==PAGE:13==
4
company. Through the interviews it will be explored whether or not departments collaborate
when it comes to hybrid work and ESG reporting as well as different departments prioritization
when it comes to hybrid work.
The results from the sustainability reports and interviews will be synthesized into a thematic
analysis, highlighting the various ways hybrid work is addressed externally in ESG reports and
how interdepartmental collaboration between HR and sustainability teams inﬂuences this ap-
proach. Through this analysis, a clearer understanding of the current practices and gaps in ESG
reporting related to hybrid work will be achieved. In the discussion, viable steps to promote
interdepartmental collaboration are presented, along with a practical tool developed as part of
this thesis to help companies standardize their calculation methods for commuting emissions.
The discussion also outlines the limitations of the study and identiﬁes areas for further research
on the topic. Lastly, the conclusion synthesizes the main ﬁndings and reﬂects on the broader
signiﬁcance of hybrid work as a cross-cutting ESG issue, emphasizing the importance of inter-
nal collaboration, strategic alignment, and early adoption of good practices as companies move
toward more integrated and forward-looking ESG reporting.

2 | Literature Review
This literature review provides an overview of the academic and practical research relevant
to hybrid work and its intersection with environmental and social sustainability, and ESG re-
porting. It begins by mapping the evolution of hybrid work, particularly since the COVID-19
pandemic, and explores its environmental and social implications. Then an examination of
theories and frameworks on interdepartmental collaboration, with focus on what good collabo-
ration is and how silos, especially information silos are hindering collaboration in both hybrid
work settings and with ESG reporting. Finally, it contextualizes hybrid work within the grow-
ing regulatory landscape of ESG reporting, particularly focusing on the EU’s Corporate Sus-
tainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and the European Sustainability Reporting Standards
(ESRS). Together, this form the foundation for analyzing how hybrid work is positioned within
sustainability reporting frameworks.
2.1
Literature Search
To quantify the existing research regarding the evolution of hybrid work from the beginning of
the COVID-19 pandemic until now, a literature search was conducted using DTU Findit, the
academic literature search engine for DTU students and employees. The aim was to identify
academic literature related to how hybrid work has evolved over the past ﬁve years. The ﬁve-
year time frame was chosen to capture the full evolution of hybrid work beginning with the
onset of the COVID-19 pandemic in early 2020, as this period marks a major shift in how orga-
nizations operate, driven by global lockdowns and the rapid adoption of remote work practices
(Chaﬁ, Hultberg, and Yams 2022) as mentioned in the introduction. The search was to iden-
tify the general introduction of hybrid work in organizations, as well as hybrid work’s impact
on organizational operations, and its inﬂuence on environmental and social sustainability. The
search included combinations of keywords such as Hybrid Work, Environmental Beneﬁts and
Sustainability. Other articles going back further than the last ﬁve years have been identiﬁed
through the literature discovered with this search.
The second literature search focused on theories and frameworks about how different depart-
ments collaborate within organizations. No limit was set on the publication year, as important
theories on collaboration may have been developed before hybrid work became widespread.
The search was carried out using both DTU Findit and Google Scholar, since DTU Findit alone
did not provide enough relevant material. The same keywords were used across both plat-
==PAGE:15==
6
forms: Corporate Collaboration Theory, Interdepartmental Collaboration, Interdepartmental
Data Sharing, and Interdepartmental Communication.
The ﬁnal literature search explored the topic of ESG reporting. The search query was the
keywords ESG Reporting. On DTU Findit, this yielded several academic publications and books
discussing frameworks, regulations, and future trends in ESG reporting. However, because
much of the most recent and applicable information on ESG reporting is published as grey
literature, this part of the search was supplemented with Google searches. These were used
to locate ofﬁcial EU Directives and Regulations, as well as technical calculation guidelines on
relevant frameworks.
2.2
The Rise of Hybrid Work
Hybrid work, as we know it today, have accelerated because of the COVID-19 pandemic, but
it was starting to take shape even before due to advancements in technology and changing
preferences in work culture (Bailey and Kurland 2002).
The concept of more ﬂexible work models began to take shape in the late 20th century, as more
employees sought to have better work-life balance than previously seen. More ﬂexible work
policies were gradually introduced in the 1980’s and 1990’s. Roberto et al. 2023 point to the
fact that the evolution of digital innovations, such as the internet, reshaped the way workers
approached their work. It was possible for some workers to work from home, but it was not a
solidiﬁed part of the typical work culture, it was more seen as an exception than the norm.
By the early 2000’s, remote working options were starting to be more accepted due to the access
of high-speed internet, cloud-based tools, and more robust communication technologies (Zaric,
Perovic, and Jasarovic 2024, Chaﬁ, Hultberg, and Yams 2022). Based on this, the hybrid model,
a mix of in-ofﬁce and remote work, came to be, as more organizations experimented with al-
lowing employees to work remotely on select days. Some of the early adopters of hybrid work
were typically in the tech industry, where employees’ roles were already well-suited for hybrid
work (Vartiainen and Vanharanta 2024). Unfortunately many of these work models were still
seen as experimental and companies still focused on maintaining the traditional ofﬁce environ-
ment because of concerns about productivity and workplace culture (Brînzea and Oancea 2022,
Roberto et al. 2023)
==PAGE:16==
7
The COVID-19 pandemic hit in 2020 and forced the shift to hybrid work across nearly every
industry (Audrin et al. 2025). Governments worldwide implemented lockdowns and social dis-
tancing politics, which forced many organizations to involuntarily make the shift to forms of
hybrid work almost overnight. Most companies made big investments in their digital infrastruc-
ture to maintain the ﬂow in their businesses, with the use of Zoom, Microsoft Teams and Slack
(Chaﬁ, Hultberg, and Yams 2022). In the beginning, hybrid work was seen as a temporary solu-
tion to an abnormal situation, but many businesses quickly recognized that employees remained
productive while working remote and that lead to certain re-evaluations of standard workplace
structures. (Brînzea and Oancea 2022)
By mid-2021, hybrid work had become the preferred model for many organizations as it could
combine the beneﬁts of hybrid work with more structured in-ofﬁce collaboration (Zaric, Per-
ovic, and Jasarovic 2024). Many companies had formalized hybrid work policies, where they
implemented structured schedules and redesigned the physical ofﬁces (Hopkins and Bardoel
2023).
As hybrid work has become the new norm, several studies have been done, quantifying the
beneﬁts and challenges of the changing work culture. The next sections will go through research
on the environmental, social and organizational implications of hybrid work during the last 5
years.
2.2.1
Environmental Implications of Hybrid Work
One of the most widely recognized environmental beneﬁts of hybrid work is the reduction in
carbon emissions from commuting (Tao et al. 2024). Traditional ofﬁce-based work requires
daily travel, which often involves private vehicles or public transportation, that all contribute to
greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. Studies indicate that shifting to a hybrid model signiﬁcantly
decreases transportation-related emissions, particularly in urban city centers where congestion
and pollution levels are high (Brînzea and Oancea 2022). For example, Hopkins and Bardoel
2023 highlight that post-pandemic hybrid work adoption in Australia led to measurable reduc-
tions in urban air pollution and then emphasize the sustainability advantages of hybrid work
models. On the other side another study from the University of Sussex indicate that hybrid
work does little for carbon emissions as the majority of hybrid workers end up traveling fur-
ther each week than ofﬁce-based workers (Caldarola and Sorrell 2022). The study states that
for hybrid work to be effective, hybrid workers would have to work remote at least 3 times a
==PAGE:17==
8
week. This has been identiﬁed through three major ﬁndings. First, hybrid workers often travel
farther per week than in-ofﬁce workers, primarily due to both longer commutes and additional
non-work-related travel. Secondly the study shows that intra-household travel increases; other

effect is particularly pronounced in regions with high car dependency, such as the United States,
where commuting accounts for a major portion of individual carbon footprints. However, while
transportation emissions decrease, residential energy consumption increases due to heating,
cooling, and electricity used during work hours. The same study by IEA (Crow and Millot
2020) suggests that working one day from home increases residential energy consumption be-
tween 7% and 23%. This means that the net sustainability impact of hybrid work depends on

regional energy grid compositions and seasonal variations as seen in Figure 1. For example,
in colder climates or areas with fossil fuel-dependent electricity grids, the emissions reduc-
tion from avoided commutes may be partially offset by increased home energy consumption.
Conversely, in regions with renewable energy integration and energy-efﬁcient buildings, hybrid
work can lead to a net positive environmental impact. Hybrid work has not only affected resi-
dential energy consumption but also in-ofﬁce energy consumption. When fewer employees are
in ofﬁces daily, companies can reduce heating, cooling, and electricity usage, leading to lower
overall carbon footprints.
Another major environmental impact of hybrid work is its inﬂuence on ofﬁce space utilization
and urban development. As the demand for large corporate headquarters declines, some organi-
zations are even downsizing their physical ofﬁce footprints. A notable example is Meta, which
in 2023 paid to exit its lease on a large London ofﬁce after struggling to ﬁll the space due to its
organizational shift toward hybrid work (Pringle 2023). This trend has also driven the strategy
of re-purposing commercial buildings into shared or ﬂexible workspaces, which reduces the
need for new ofﬁce construction and its associated environmental impact (Roberto et al. 2023).
This is signiﬁcant, as the construction sector historically has one of the biggest carbon emissions
issues (UNEP 2023). In response, the real estate sector has increasingly embraced sustainable
building practices, with existing ofﬁce spaces being redesigned to incorporate energy-efﬁcient
materials, improved insulation, and green building certiﬁcations (Zaric, Perovic, and Jasarovic
2024).
A challenge that comes with the implementation of hybrid work is the larger reliance on digital
tools. Video calls, cloud storage, and online collaboration increases energy demand from data
centers, which require signiﬁcant electricity to operate (Hopkins and Bardoel 2023). At the
same time, working remotely makes it harder to maintain consistent sustainability habits across
an organization, as employees have different approaches to energy use, waste management, and
other environmental practices at home (Roberto et al. 2023).
2.2.2
Social Implications of Hybrid Work
Hybrid work gives several advantages for employees, organizations, and society. One of the pri-
mary beneﬁts is the improved work-life balance it provides. Employees gain greater autonomy
over their schedules, which allows them to differentiate their time more efﬁciently between pro-
fessional and personal responsibilities (S. and S.N. 2023). Research by Franzen-Waschke 2021
==PAGE:19==
10
underlines that workers with hybrid arrangements often report lower stress levels and improved
overall well-being due to more control over their own work environments.
Another key advantage is the increased accessibility to diverse talent pools. Hybrid work has
made it easier for organizations to recruit and retain employees from different geographical
locations (Silva, Castelló-Sirvent, and Canós-Darós 2022). Brînzea and Oancea 2022 points
out that businesses, who implement hybrid models can attract highly skilled professionals who
prioritize ﬂexibility and hybrid work options. It has become a business opportunity for com-
panies wanting to have a competitive advantage. This shift has also contributed to greater
workforce diversity, as individuals with disabilities or caregiving responsibilities beneﬁt from
ﬂexible working arrangements (Lake and Maidment 2023, Taylor et al. 2022)
Additionally, hybrid work has contributed to more productivity for employees. Employees
working remotely for part of the week are able to better manage distractions and focus on deep
work when at home, while when they are in-ofﬁce they can collaborate to foster innovation.
Pillai and Prasad 2023 found that organizations implementing structured hybrid work strategies
observed increased efﬁciency and employee engagement compared to fully remote or fully in-
ofﬁce setups.
The potential issues with hybrid work shows when it comes to its inﬂuence on workplace col-
laboration and communication. While hybrid models provide employees with greater ﬂexibility,
they also introduce barriers to spontaneous interactions and informal knowledge sharing. Re-
search by Bu!a, Thompson, and ˙Zak 2024 emphasizes that hybrid teams often experience difﬁ-
culties in maintaining strong interpersonal connections due to reduced face-to-face interactions.
Virtual communication tools and online meetings can sometimes replace organic ofﬁce discus-
sions, leading to misinterpretations and a loss of social cohesion among employees (Vartiainen
and Vanharanta 2024).
Employee well-being is another major social concern in hybrid work models. The ability to
work remotely can reduce stress associated with commuting and offer employees greater au-
tonomy over their schedules. However, studies have also found that hybrid work can blur the
boundaries between professional and personal life, leading to work-life conﬂict and increased
mental fatigue. Ferrara et al. 2022 notes that while hybrid workers report higher job satisfac-
tion due to ﬂexibility, they are also more prone to feelings of isolation and burnout, especially
when clear boundaries between work and personal life are not established. Furthermore, hybrid
==PAGE:20==
11
work can introduce otherwise not seen inequalities, as employees with larger living spaces or
dedicated home ofﬁces may ﬁnd hybrid work easier to feel comfortable in, while others may
struggle in less favorable home environments (Alsulami, Mabrouk, and Bousrih 2023).
Hybrid work has also inﬂuenced career progression and workplace visibility. Employees who
spend more time in the ofﬁce may have greater access to leadership, mentorship, and networking
opportunities, leading to potential bias in promotions and career development. Tahlyan et al.
2024 highlights concerns among employers that remote employees could be overlooked for
advancement compared to their in-ofﬁce counterparts.
2.3
Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive
The beginning of ESG reporting in the EU can be traced back to the 1990’s, when larger cor-
porations ﬁrst began adopting voluntary Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) initiatives (EU
2001). Many corporations started these practices due to external pressures from stakeholders
to be more transparent and CSR was becoming a solid business case. In 2001, the ﬁrst big
political directive came with the introduction of the European Union Eco-Management and Au-
dit Scheme (EMAS), which set guidelines for organizations to evaluate and improve on their
environmental performance (EU 2008). In 2003, the EU launched its CSR strategy, encourag-
ing companies to integrate social and environmental reporting into their business models (EU
2003).
The real shift toward mandatory ESG reporting in the EU happened in 2014 with the introduc-
tion of the Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD). This directive required companies with
more than 500 employees to report on their non-ﬁnancial information, including environmental,
social, and governance data (EU 2014). It was designed to improve transparency and compa-
rability of non-ﬁnancial information across the EU, pushing corporations in a more sustainable
direction aligned with broader governmental goals, such as the United Nations Sustainable De-
velopment Goals (SDGs), adopted in 2015. The most recent regulatory development in ESG
reporting happened in late 2022, beginning of 2023 as a part of the European Green Deal1 with
the introduction of the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), which is an ex-
pansion of the NFRD to gather even more information on corporations and their impacts on the
environment and their surroundings (EU 2022). The CSRD introduced binding, streamlined
reporting standards in the form of the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS),
1A project that aims to make Europe the ﬁrst continent be carbon neutral by 2050 (EU n.d.)

factors in new and innovative ways. However, they also require careful planning and continuous
evaluation to ensure that the ﬂexibility they offer doesn’t inadvertently lead to new forms of
stress, burnout, or disengagement.
When assessing S1 - Own Workforce and hybrid work models, several key data metrics can be
used to quantify data regarding the impact hybrid work has on employees. In April 2022 the Eu-
ropean Financial Reporting Advisory Group (EFRAG) published an exposure draft deﬁning the
data metrics needed for the S1 standard (EFRAG 2022). Data metrics for assessing employee
well-being include participation rates in well-being programs, such as mental health initiatives,
and counseling services.
Hybrid work is as mentioned in section 2.2.2 often seen as a means to improve work-life bal-
ance, as it allows employees to manage their professional and personal lives with more ﬂexibil-
ity. To evaluate this, companies can track metrics such as the number of hours worked per week,
including overtime and ﬂexible work hours. By comparing these metrics with the frequency of
remote work days versus in-ofﬁce days, organizations can gauge how employees are balancing
their workloads. Data on commuting time is another important metric, as hybrid work affects
commuting, which gives employees more time for personal activities and give the feeling of a
lessened workload.
Job satisfaction is directly impacted by hybrid work arrangements, as employees who enjoy
ﬂexibility and autonomy are often more satisﬁed with their jobs. To assess job satisfaction,
organizations can use regular employee satisfaction surveys that include questions about work
environment, ﬂexibility, career development opportunities, and overall engagement with the
company. Data on turnover and retention rates can also be telling. Employees who are dissatis-
ﬁed with hybrid arrangements may leave, while those who are satisﬁed are more likely to stay.
Furthermore, performance reviews and career progression data can provide insights into how
hybrid work affects employees’ professional growth and recognition within the company.
Hybrid work also has a direct impact on corporate carbon footprints, which makes E1 – Climate
Change a relevant ESRS standard. For this standard, several GHG emissions-related metrics be-
come particularly relevant. As mentioned in section 2.2.1, hybrid work has the potential to alter
an organization’s carbon footprint, primarily by changing how energy is consumed, how space
is used, and how employees commute or travel for business. To assess this impact, organi-

zations should focus on data metrics across Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions categories, each of
which captures different aspects of a company’s climate-related performance. The methods of
quantifying these different impacts are derived from the GHG Protocol, a global standardized

Calculating Scope 3 Emissions, which outlines three calculation methods for Category 7 emis-

emissions by using the daily commuting distance per employee, broken down by transportation
mode (e.g., car, bus, train, metro, bicycle, airplane). These daily distances are then converted

2.4
Interdepartmental Collaboration
Interdepartmental collaboration refers to the coordinated efforts of individuals or teams from
different organizational departments working together toward shared objectives. It involves
the exchange of information, alignment of goals, and integration of diverse expertise to solve
complex problems or deliver cross-functional outcomes (Loving 2021).
Effective interdepartmental collaboration is deﬁned by coordinated, goal-oriented interactions
between departments that enable the organization to address complex issues effectively. Unlike
ad hoc cooperation or simple data sharing, effective collaboration is characterized by shared ob-
jectives and integrated workﬂows that reﬂect a commitment to a collective outcome rather than
individual departments’ priorities (Loving 2021). High-performing collaborative efforts are fur-
ther supported by strong interpersonal trust and psychological safety, which enable departments
to exchange knowledge openly and resolve conﬂicts constructively (Roussin 2008,Zhang and
Xu 2024. As (Bigdeli, Kamal, and Cesare 2012) emphasize, trust is a key enabler of interdepart-
mental information sharing, particularly when collaboration requires the handling of sensitive
or non-standardized data.
Successful collaboration also depends on open communication and the development of a shared
language across departments. When departments can communicate transparently, misunder-
standings are easier to avoid and alignment becomes easier (Loving 2021). This is reinforced
by an organizational culture, that values cross-functional teamwork and rewards results made
in collaboration rather than individual performances (Ewim et al. 2024, Nguyen et al. 2018,
Holland, Gaston, and Gomes 2000). In addition, the use of shared digital platforms and clearly
deﬁned rules and processes helps structure collaboration and maintain consistency (Jin and Dai
2024, Beyerlein et al. 2003). However, as Jin and Dai 2024 also argue, technology is only effec-
tive when used in collaboration with governance structures that already promote shared values
and trust across departments.
One of the bigger challenges when it comes to interdepartmental collaboration is the fact that
departments often operate independently in organizations, which can inadvertently create infor-
mation silos, which are isolated pockets of knowledge within these departments, teams or even
individual roles.
==PAGE:27==
18
2.4.1
Information Silos
Information silos are created when employees or teams primarily interact within their own
groups and have limited visibility into the activities or knowledge of others. In hybrid work
environments, physical separation combined with asynchronous workﬂows can amplify this
challenge as employees may miss informal knowledge-sharing moments, such as hallway con-
versations or impromptu desk-side chats, that otherwise helped bridge organizational divides
(Waizenegger et al. 2020). Moreover the use of multiple, non-cross-collaborative tools, such as
databases, across teams can lead to poor information access and hinder cross team collaboration
(Waizenegger et al. 2020).
Silos are not only an issue when it comes to hybrid work, ESG reporting also has its problems
with silos, especially when it comes to collaboration. Unlike ﬁnancial reporting, which typically
falls under the responsibilities of a centralized ﬁnance team, ESG reporting requires input from a
broad range of organizational actors, such as HR, operations, procurement, sustainability teams,
and IT. When these units all operate in different silos, data collection becomes fragmented,
inconsistent, and even prone to error (Runyon 2025). This results in reports that are not up
to standards, because they are either incomplete, have rough estimate calculations or lack the
transparency and traceability demanded by investors and regulators.
Furthermore, ESG data often exists in non-standardized formats across different systems, which
complicates collaboration and analysis. For instance, carbon emissions data may be tracked in
one software tool, diversity metrics in an HR platform, and supply chain data in Enterprise Re-
source Planning (ERP) systems. Without interoperable data infrastructure and clear governance
frameworks, companies struggle to produce credible and audit-ready ESG disclosures (Kotsan-
tonis and Serafeim 2019). Information silos also prevent strategic alignment. As an example,
sustainability teams may have ambitious de-carbonization goals, operational units may not be
fully engaged or even aware of these objectives (Ioannou and Serafeim 2017).
This literature review has examined the rise of hybrid work, its environmental and social impli-
cations, the evolution of ESG reporting frameworks, and the signiﬁcance of interdepartmental
collaboration in sustainability efforts. While hybrid work is often presented as a strategy to
reduce emissions and improve employee well-being, the literature reveals that its actual im-
pact depends on a variety of factors, including commuting patterns, home energy use, and the
organizational capacity to measure and report on these elements. At the same time, ESG report-
==PAGE:28==
19
ing is becoming increasingly regulated through frameworks such as the CSRD and ESRS, yet
clear guidance on how to include hybrid work in disclosures remains limited. And there is very
limited, if any, literature on the connection between ESG reporting and the implementation of
hybrid work. An important insight across the reviewed literature is the role of collaboration, or
lack thereof, between departments like HR and sustainability. Organizational silos are shown to
hinder data quality and communication between teams or departments.
This review establishes the foundation for the purpose statement of this thesis, which is to exam-
ine how hybrid work is incorporated into ESG reporting, and what role does interdepartmental
collaboration play in shaping its contribution to both environmental impact and employee well-
being. This research is relevant as organizations seek to align ﬂexible work practices with in-
creasingly stringent ESG requirements. The aim of the study is to investigate whether and how
companies address hybrid work within their ESG strategies, and to what extent interdepart-
mental collaboration facilitates or hinders this process. By combining insights from existing
research with empirical ﬁndings from expert interviews, the thesis aims to identify best prac-
tices and potential gaps, supporting organizations in improving both the accuracy of their ESG
disclosures and the well-being of their employees.

3 | Methodology
This section outlines the research design developed to answer the research questions: (1) How
do companies incorporate hybrid work into their ESG reporting, particularly within the environ-
mental and social pillars? (2) What role does interdepartmental collaboration play in shaping
hybrid work’s contribution to both environmental and employee well-being? And (3) What
practical steps can companies take to improce collaboration between HR and sustainability de-
partments to enhance ESG reporting? This study uses a qualitative approach to explore how
companies adopt hybrid work and ESG reporting, and how these two areas are connected in
practice. Since this is a developing topic and the goal is to understand different perspectives
and explore the structures that exist inside companies, a qualitative method is a good ﬁt. It
allows for a deeper look at what factors are relevant when it comes to hybrid work policy de-
cisions, what priorities companies have when it comes to ESG reporting, and how these things
are both communicated and collaborated on internally between departments.
As part of the qualitative approach, this research relies on two main methods: a thematic anal-
ysis (Clarke and Braun 2006) of published sustainability and integrated annual reports from 18
companies published in the years 2023 and 2024, and a thematic analysis of semi-structured in-
terviews with employees from companies that practice hybrid work and are doing sustainability
reporting, either by complying with the CSRD or preparing to do so. This strategy was chosen
to gain both an internal and external perspective on how hybrid work and ESG reporting are
connected in practice. Analyzing sustainability and integrated annual reports provides insight
into how companies publicly frame and communicate their ESG strategies and how they report
on hybrid work in that context. While the interviews offer a deeper understanding of how hy-
brid work policies is developed in organizations and how that is translated to their sustainability
practices, if at all. The interviews also allow to examine what kind of collaboration happens
between departments and how that looks in practice.
3.1
Data Collection
The decision to combine sustainability reports and interviews as the data sources for this thesis
is made based on the need for multifaceted data. The sustainability reports have potential to
capture how companies communicate externally to their stakeholders in regards to their sus-
tainability practices and with that the connection to hybrid work. While the interviews have
potential to give the internal perspectives of how companies that are navigating the implemen-
==PAGE:30==
21
tation of hybrid work and how they work with such polices in regard to their ESG reporting
practices.

The results reveal that hybrid work is inconsistently incorporated into ESG reporting across
Danish companies, with substantial variation in methods, priorities, and departmental respon-
sibilities. While many companies report on commuting-related emissions, few explicitly link
these to hybrid work practices, and environmental reporting frameworks rarely capture hybrid’s
full impact. Instead, hybrid work is more often positioned as a social initiative, associated
with employee well-being, ﬂexibility, and talent retention. Moreover, interdepartmental col-
laboration between HR and ESG teams remains limited and informal, further reinforcing the
separation between environmental metrics and people-centered policies. Together, these ﬁnd-
ings point to a gap between hybrid work’s potential dual impact and its current treatment in

4.1
Incorporation of Hybrid Work in ESG Reporting
This section examines how and whether hybrid work is incorporated into ESG reporting. The
ﬁndings draw on the themes Institutional Integration of Hybrid Work, Methodologies and
Measurement, and Practical Tools and Action Steps, along with their associated sub-themes,
that are available in Table 12 in the methodology section.
First, taking a dive into the theme of Institutional Integration of Hybrid Work and taking
a look at the Frameworks and Compliance sub-theme it is identiﬁed that all 18 companies
report on scope 3 emissions, however LEGO A/S has no scope 3 data in their report, they
only acknowledge the existence of such emissions. The fact that all reports address scope 3
aligns with them mentioning getting ready to or already complying with the CSRD, which as
mentioned in section 2.3, makes it mandatory to report on scope 1, 2 and 3. Despite scope 3
being mandatory, the Scope 3 Category 7: Employee Commuting, which has been the focus for
this analysis, looks vastly different from company to company. Under the same sub-theme, it
was also noted that none of the companies explicitly connect hybrid work to other environmental
frameworks. Hybrid work is not mentioned in relation to the EU Taxonomy, TCFD, or other
reporting standards beyond general compliance, which are some of the frameworks that many
of the reports state to also use in collaboration with the CSRD framework.
Then, looking at the theme of Methodologies and Measurement, several sub-themes became
of interest in the exploration of how hybrid work is incorporated in ESG reporting. Firstly, it
was identiﬁed under the sub-theme Average Data Approaches, that six companies - Danfoss
A/S, Haldor Topsøe A/S, Hempel A/S, WS Audiology A/S, Grundfos Holding A/S and Ørsted
A/S - state they use some type of average-data method. These methods rely on country-level
or global commuting patterns, employee headcounts, and publicly available emission factors
from sources such as DEFRA9 or BEIS10. They do not require direct input from employees but
assume representative behavior based on regional or national data.
Then, under the sub-theme Survey-based Approaches, it was noted that four companies reported
using employee surveys to estimate commuting emissions; Cowi A/S, Rambøll Group A/S,

5 | Discussion
The purpose of this discussion section is to interpret and contextualize the ﬁndings of this study
in relation to the broader literature, the regulatory landscape, and the research questions posed
in the introduction. While the results section presented the empirical evidence collected through
sustainability report analysis and semi-structured interviews, this section reﬂects more critically
on what those ﬁndings mean for theory, practice, and future developments in ESG reporting,
particularly in relation to hybrid work.
This section is structured to mirror the study’s three central research questions. The ﬁrst subsec-
tion addresses how hybrid work is incorporated into ESG reporting, with particular focus on the
environmental and social pillars. The second examines the organizational dynamics that shape
this reporting, especially the role, or absence, of collaboration between HR and sustainability
departments. The third research question, which focuses on the practical steps companies can
take to improve such collaboration, is not answered directly in the results. Instead, it is ad-
dressed through the targeted recommendations presented in Section 5.3, which are grounded in
both the empirical ﬁndings and established change management frameworks. Based on these
ﬁndings, an emissions calculation tool is also presented as a practical response to the reporting
challenges identiﬁed, particularly those related to Scope 3, Category 7 emissions.
By organizing the discussion around the three research questions, this section does more than
just describe how hybrid work is currently reported in ESG disclosures. It also digs into why
companies report the way they do. This includes uncovering the internal structures, priorities,
and barriers, such as lack of collaboration or unclear responsibilities, that inﬂuence reporting
practices. The goal is to explain why hybrid work, even though it clearly relates to both environ-
mental and social sustainability, is still rarely addressed in a meaningful way in ESG reports.
The discussion also highlights what would need to change, both inside organizations and in
reporting frameworks, for hybrid work to be taken more seriously and consistently included in
ESG disclosures.
5.1
Hybrid Work is a Social ESG Element
One of the main ﬁndings of this study is the limited visibility of hybrid work in ESG reporting
practices. In many cases, hybrid work is not mentioned at all, and when it is, references are
brief and typically located within the social dimension of sustainability reporting. Very few
==PAGE:63==
54
companies offer detailed accounts of their hybrid work policies or explain how such practices
ﬁt into their sustainability strategy, Ørsted being a notable exception as they mentioned it in their
DMA as a material topic. Some companies, like Hempel and Coloplast, mention hybrid work
in the context of employee satisfaction, well-being, or efforts to accommodate diverse work
arrangements. However, these mentions remain surface-level, and the environmental dimension
of hybrid work is almost entirely absent. There are likely several reasons why hybrid work
receives such limited attention in ESG reporting, particularly in relation to its environmental
impact.
Firstly, this lack of prioritization suggests that hybrid work remains a low-proﬁle topic within
the ESG landscape, especially on the environmental side. This is particularly striking given the
extensive academic literature suggesting that hybrid work has considerable potential to reduce
emissions through fewer commutes, lower ofﬁce energy use, and more efﬁcient space utilization
(Tao et al. 2024, Hopkins and Bardoel 2023; Zaric, Perovic, and Jasarovic 2024). Despite these
ﬁndings, most companies do not appear to view hybrid work as a signiﬁcant contributor to their
environmental performance, or, at the very least, do not report on it as such.
The interviews conducted as part of this study help explain this disconnect. For example,
Maersk explicitly stated that hybrid work was not considered a material topic within their envi-
ronmental strategy, primarily because their largest sources of emissions lie in other areas, such
as shipping and logistics. While they acknowledged that hybrid work may have greater rele-
vance in other sectors, they did not view it as impactful enough within their own operations to
justify dedicating resources to it.
Importantly, Maersk also emphasized that if hybrid work were to be more fully integrated into
their ESG reporting, particularly under Scope 3, Category 7: Employee Commuting, it would
require a standardized, accessible tool to streamline data collection and calculation. Without
such infrastructure in place, the administrative burden of tracking and reporting emissions from
hybrid work was seen as too high for what they perceived as a relatively small gain. In this
sense, hybrid work is not excluded because of a lack of awareness, but rather due to a perceived
lack of return on investment in both environmental impact and reporting efﬁciency.
This insight reveals a wider barrier to integrating hybrid work into ESG frameworks: the gap
between academic insights and the operational priorities of companies. Even when the envi-
ronmental potential is well-documented in the literature, it is unlikely to be reﬂected in corpo-
==PAGE:64==
55
rate reporting unless organizations have a compelling reason to act, whether regulatory, reputa-
tional, or operational. At present, hybrid work is still largely framed as an internal HR initiative
rather than a cross-cutting sustainability issue. Bridging this divide will likely require not only
stronger internal collaboration, but also clearer external incentives, frameworks, and tools that
help companies identify and capture hybrid work’s ESG value.
Yet, the issue extends beyond organizational priorities, the design of the ESG frameworks them-
selves also presents structural obstacles. Going back to the the literature review, the design of
the CSRD and its supporting standards (ESRS) are organized in a way that does not easily
accommodate topics that span multiple ESG dimensions. In order to comply with the CSRD,
companies must conduct a Double Materiality Assessment (DMA), where they assess a ﬁxed set
of topics deﬁned by the ESRS. These topics are divided strictly into environmental (E), social
(S), and governance (G) categories, with limited or no overlap between them.
This rigid categorization presents a challenge for hybrid work, which has clear relevance to
both environmental and social dimensions of sustainability. While academic literature has em-
phasized this dual impact, the ESRS offers limited guidance or encouragement for companies
to assess such cross-cutting topics in an integrated manner. For companies that have not previ-
ously considered hybrid work through an environmental lens, there is little support within the
DMA process to identify or evaluate it as an environmental issue. This is particularly evident
in ESRS E1 – Climate Change, the standard most directly linked to emissions reporting. Scope
3, Category 7: Employee Commuting, as deﬁned by the GHG Protocol, is included under E1
and represents the only category where hybrid work might be relevant, although it is not ex-
plicitly mentioned. Furthermore, reporting on hybrid work-related emissions, such as increased
electricity use in employer’s homes, remains entirely optional within this category. As a result,
companies tend to default to framing hybrid work within the social pillar of ESG, if they include
it at all.
This structural issue echoes broader concerns raised in recent literature. Breijer et al. 2025,
writing for the European Business Review, point out that while the CSRD aims to harmonize
sustainability disclosures, its reliance on strictly segmented E, S, and G categories can prevent
organizations from effectively reporting on cross-cutting or integrated topics. They argue that
the subjective nature of the materiality assessment process, combined with the rigid framework
of predeﬁned standards, creates room for discretionary omissions and reduces the transparency
and comparability that the CSRD was designed to achieve. In the case of hybrid work, this
==PAGE:65==
56
means that even when it has demonstrable environmental relevance, it is often excluded simply
because it does not clearly ﬁt into one reporting silo.
One potential way to encourage companies to treat hybrid work as an environmental issue would
be to make its inclusion mandatory under Scope 3, Category 7. At present, companies can
choose whether or not to report on emissions from hybrid work, and most opt not to. Among the
18 reports analyzed in this study, only Hempel explicitly reported on emissions from employees’
homes. Mandating this reporting would compel companies to evaluate the emissions impact
of hybrid work more seriously and could uncover reduction opportunities that are currently
overlooked.
However, such a requirement would come with trade-offs. Companies are already burdened by
the scale of reporting obligations under CSRD and often focus only on minimum compliance, in
categories with low prioritization. This is especially true in the employee commuting category,
where reporting is inconsistent and rarely goes beyond basic estimates. As highlighted earlier,
many companies do not prioritize hybrid work and commuting-related emissions because they
are seen as less material than other Scope 3 categories, such as purchased goods, transport,
or business travel. In this context, hybrid work is not ignored due to irrelevance, but because
it competes for attention with higher-emission, higher-visibility categories. This means that
stronger regulation could increase hybrid work’s visibility, but may also intensify the reporting
burden for companies already struggling to meet complex requirements. Any move toward
mandatory reporting would therefore need to be paired with guidance, standardization, and
low-barrier tools that make implementation more manageable and meaningful.
In addition to the optional nature of hybrid work reporting, another major challenge lies in how
inconsistently companies calculate emissions, even for mathematically simple categories like
employee commuting. While the GHG Protocol offers general methodologies, the actual calcu-
lation practices vary widely. Among the 18 sustainability reports reviewed, no two companies
used the exact same approach. Some used average national data, while others relied on internal
surveys, but even within those categories, data sources and assumptions differed. This lack of
consistency undermines one of the primary goals of the CSRD: to improve transparency and
comparability across organizations.
When companies report the same emissions category using such varied methods, it becomes
nearly impossible to make meaningful comparisons or assess progress over time. It also calls
==PAGE:66==
57
into question the accuracy and credibility of Scope 3 disclosures. A good example of this is
Rambøll, which used a survey-based approach to calculate commuting emissions, generally a
more precise method than averages. However, they relied on data collected in 2019, well before
the major shift to hybrid work. As working patterns have changed dramatically since then
(Pabilonia and Redmond 2024), the survey data is now likely outdated and no longer reﬂects
current commuting behavior. Even when a more advanced method is used, outdated inputs can
undermine the quality of the results.
Moreover, even when companies make a genuine effort to report using close to best practice, the
complexity of their methods can obscure understanding. For instance, Pandora uses a relatively
detailed and technically sound approach to calculate commuting emissions. However, the way
this method is presented in their report is highly technical and difﬁcult for external stakeholders
to interpret. This reduces the accessibility of the report, particularly for non-expert readers such
as investors, NGOs, and policymakers, and thereby limits its usefulness. In other words, both
how emissions are calculated and how clearly the methodology is communicated are crucial to
building stakeholder trust and ensuring transparency.
Additionally, research by Vieira, Longo, and Mura 2024 highlights that Scope 3 inventories
frequently rely on estimations due to limited primary data availability from suppliers and other
value chain actors. This dependence on proxy data or assumptions introduces signiﬁcant un-
certainty and variability in reported emissions, further complicating efforts to ensure accuracy
and reliability. Complementing this, a study by Korca, Costa, and Bouten 2023 emphasizes that
differences in reporting boundaries and calculation methods across companies, as seen in this
study, expands the problem by reducing the comparability of sustainability disclosures. Even
companies within the same sector may deﬁne their scope and data sources differently, making
it difﬁcult to benchmark performance or track progress consistently.
To address these challenges, clearer guidance and standardized tools for calculating emissions
from commuting and hybrid work could play a pivotal role. Such tools would promote con-
sistency, reduce reporting burdens, and help companies recognize hybrid work as more than an
HR policy, but as a potentially impactful sustainability strategy. Importantly, this is not just a
technical issue. As the ﬁndings suggest, limited collaboration between HR and sustainability
teams also contributes to poor data sharing, disconnected priorities, and underdeveloped report-
ing processes. Addressing emissions in Scope 3, Category 7 may therefore require both stronger
external frameworks and improved internal collaboration.

5.2
The Role of Interdepartmental Collaboration — or Lack Thereof
When it comes to interdepartmental collaboration, the ﬁndings of this study reveal that there
is virtually no active collaboration between HR and sustainability departments in relation to
ESG reporting, particularly on cross-cutting issues like hybrid work. The only identiﬁed form
of collaboration is limited data sharing, and even this is constrained by legal concerns, partic-
ularly around GDPR compliance when sharing employee-related information. Based on the
interviews, the primary reason for this lack of collaboration appears to be a misalignment in
perceived responsibility. HR departments generally do not view hybrid work as an environ-
mental matter, but rather as a tool for talent retention and workplace ﬂexibility. As a result,
they do not see the relevance of contributing to sustainability reporting, nor do they prioritize
cross-departmental engagement on this topic.
This ﬁnding is consistent with existing literature, which frequently highlights that many organi-
zations still operate in functional silos, where departments focus narrowly on their own domains
with limited collaboration. Silos often arise naturally as organizations grow and specialize;
they allow departments to focus on their own goals, streamline decision-making, and protect
domain-speciﬁc knowledge (Waal et al. 2019). From this perspective, silos are not inherently
dysfunctional, they can support clarity, accountability, and professional depth.
Silos are often reinforced by structural elements such as reporting lines, performance metrics,
and departmental incentives. For instance, if HR is evaluated based on retention and engage-
ment metrics, while the sustainability team is measured by emissions reductions or compliance
with ESG regulations, there is little immediate incentive for them to collaborate. Over time,
this separation of goals and KPIs can harden into institutional habits, where departments in-
teract only when necessary and resist collaboration that is not clearly aligned with their core
responsibilities.
Beyond structural reinforcement, silos are also sustained by cultural and behavioral patterns.
According to Waal et al. 2019, employees often develop strong identities within their own units,
which can lead to a sense of "us versus them" when interacting with other departments. Com-
munication norms, informal networks, and even physical workspace layouts can further deepen
the divide, limiting opportunities for cross-functional dialogue and learning. In such contexts,
collaboration is not only difﬁcult, it may not even be perceived as necessary or beneﬁcial. As a
result, organizations miss opportunities to integrate knowledge, share resources, and coordinate
==PAGE:68==
59
action on issues that require joint ownership, such as sustainability or hybrid work policy.
According to Waal et al. 2019, silos can reduce system-wide awareness and weaken network
responsiveness, making it difﬁcult to address issues that span multiple functional areas. ESG
reporting is one such issue. It often requires input from departments like HR, sustainability,
ﬁnance, and operations, yet when silos are strong, these groups may lack both the incentive and
the structure to share knowledge or coordinate action. As the Bento, Tagliabue, and Lorenzo
2020 notes, attempts at cross-functional collaboration frequently fail not because of bad in-
tentions, but because of unclear ownership, misaligned incentives, and the absence of formal
integration mechanisms. When hybrid work is perceived strictly as an HR matter, and ESG as
the sustainability department’s responsibility, collaboration falls through the cracks. The result
is that hybrid work, despite having environmental relevance, is rarely addressed in environmen-
tal reporting.
Research even shows that silos not only hinder the development of a coherent sustainability
strategy but also increase the risk of greenwashing, particularly when companies address topics
like climate, social impact, or governance from isolated departmental perspectives (Pedersen
2024). This risk arises because fragmented communication and responsibility can lead to in-
consistencies in what is reported externally versus what is practiced internally. When depart-
ments operate independently, sustainability claims made by one unit, such as a bold emissions
reduction goals from the ESG team, may not align with the actual practices or priorities of
other departments, such as procurement, operations, or HR. Without mechanisms for coordina-
tion and cross-checking, this disconnect can result in overstated, unveriﬁed, or even misleading
ESG narratives. For example, a company may publicly announce that it has reduced its Scope
3 emissions through hybrid work policies, while its HR department simultaneously enforces
a return-to-ofﬁce policy that signiﬁcantly increases commuting, but it is never updated in the
reported data. In such cases, well-intentioned sustainability messaging becomes misleading by
omission. In worst-case scenarios, companies may unintentionally report progress that is not
supported by actions, or fail to disclose contradictions between policies and performance. This
not only undermines stakeholder trust but can expose the company to reputational damage, reg-
ulatory scrutiny, or accusations of greenwashing, all of which could have ﬁnancial and legal
consequences.
While organizational silos can offer internal structure and clarity, dismantling them, or at least
building stronger bridges between departments, is essential for addressing cross-cutting issues
==PAGE:69==
60
like hybrid work in ESG reporting. Bento, Tagliabue, and Lorenzo 2020 suggests that silos
are not easily broken down through intention alone, but doing so requires deliberate structural
and cultural changes that enable collaboration without undermining the strengths of specialized
departments.
Interdepartmental collaboration is often presented as a logical solution to the challenges posed
by organizational silos. However, building meaningful collaboration is rarely straightforward,
it does not happen overnight or as the literature states; through intention alone. Moving from
isolated functions to integrated processes requires incremental efforts, cultural shifts, and the es-
tablishment of a shared mindset and purpose across departments. While this study has focused
speciﬁcally on how collaboration affects ESG reporting practices, the ﬁndings also highlight a
broader point: collaboration should be seen as a two-way process. It is not simply about shift-
ing additional responsibilities onto the sustainability department or expecting HR to contribute
without incentive. Research shows that employees are less likely to engage meaningfully in
ESG initiatives if they do not understand how their departmental responsibilities connect to
broader sustainability goals. When sustainability is seen as the sole responsibility of a dedi-
cated team, typically within CSR or ESG functions, other departments often feel detached or
even burdened by requests for data or cooperation, especially when ESG topics fall outside their
perceived mandate (Windolph, Harms, and Schaltegger 2014). Instead, effective collaboration
must be rooted in mutual beneﬁt and shared accountability, where all departments recognize
how their contributions support more accurate, efﬁcient, and strategic ESG reporting, and ulti-
mately, the organization’s long-term sustainability performance.
This recognition, that effective collaboration depends on shared accountability and perceived
relevance across departments, points to a broader, foundational principle within sustainability
reporting: the importance of stakeholder engagement. In the context of interdepartmental col-
laboration, internal stakeholders such as HR, ﬁnance, operations, and sustainability teams must
not only be involved, but meaningfully engaged in shaping how ESG reporting is approached
and operationalized.
Academic literature increasingly points to the strategic importance of engaging internal stake-
holders early and meaningfully in the ESG process. Adams et al. 2007 argue that stakeholder
engagement should not be treated as a post-hoc consultation activity but as a foundational ele-
ment of effective sustainability reporting. They emphasize that when internal stakeholders are
actively involved in deﬁning ESG priorities and interpreting data relevance, they are far more
==PAGE:70==
61
likely to support cross-functional initiatives and ensure the quality of reporting.
Another factor limiting interdepartmental collaboration in ESG reporting is the generally low
level of knowledge and awareness about sustainability within non-ESG departments. As Ec-
cles and Klimenko 2019 points out, a lack of ESG literacy across organizational functions is a
common barrier to effective integration and cross-functional cooperation. This issue was also
reﬂected in this study, where the HR interviewee explicitly stated that they had little or nothing
to do with ESG reporting, an indication of limited exposure or training on the subject. When
departments like HR are not sufﬁciently informed about the goals, processes, or relevance of
ESG reporting, their ability to contribute meaningfully is signiﬁcantly reduced. This knowl-
edge gap not only hinders collaboration but also reinforces the perception that ESG is the sole
responsibility of the sustainability team, rather than a shared organizational priority.
5.3
Recommendations
Given the persistent siloed structure observed across the organizations examined in this study,
immediate and large-scale interdepartmental transformation is unlikely. However, foundational
steps can be taken to initiate and encourage collaboration between HR and sustainability de-
partments, particularly around cross-cutting ESG topics like hybrid work. To structure these
recommendations, this study draws on Kotter’s 8-Step Process for Leading Change (1995), a
well-established framework for managing organizational change in complex, often resistant en-
vironments.
Kotter’s model is widely used because it provides a structured yet ﬂexible approach to organi-
zational transformation, beginning with the creation of urgency and culminating in the imple-
mentation of new behaviors. The framework is particularly relevant here because it emphasizes
the importance of building momentum gradually and acknowledges that lasting change requires
both cultural and structural shifts, something which is highly needed in the siloed organizations
examined in this study.

While Kotter’s full model includes eight steps, this study focuses exclusively on the ﬁrst: cre-

conditions for change, such as shared understanding, perceived relevance, and internal motiva-
tion, are not yet in place. Most departments still operate in isolation, and collaboration is often
seen as unnecessary or burdensome. Under these circumstances, more advanced steps, such
as forming strategic visions or empowering broad-based action, would likely be premature or
ineffective.
Therefore, this recommendation section concentrates on how organizations can build an initial
sense of urgency around interdepartmental collaboration, speciﬁcally between HR and sustain-
ability teams. By focusing on this ﬁrst step, the aim is to lay the groundwork for future change
by helping key actors recognize why collaboration is necessary, how it can add value internally,
and who must take responsibility for initiating the process.
The ﬁrst and most essential step in initiating interdepartmental collaboration between HR and
sustainability departments is to create a sense of urgency. Without a shared understanding that
change is both necessary and beneﬁcial, attempts to collaborate across departmental boundaries
==PAGE:72==
63
are unlikely to gain traction, especially in organizations where collaboration is often viewed as
inefﬁcient or irrelevant, as observed in this study.
As mentioned earlier, this study has shown that ESG reporting on cross-cutting topics like
hybrid work suffers signiﬁcantly from the lack of interdepartmental collaboration. Speciﬁcally,
hybrid work is typically treated as a HR issue, framed around work-life balance, and rarely
recognized for its environmental implications, such as reduced commuting emissions. As a
result, this topic falls through the cracks of ESG reporting structures, leading to sustainability
reports, that have the potential to report on more correct data, than they are doing now.
Creating urgency involves helping stakeholders recognize that the current siloed approach to
ESG reporting poses both strategic risks and missed opportunities. While regulatory frame-
works such as the CSRD and the European ESRS mandate greater integration of environmental,
social, and governance dimensions, compliance alone is rarely sufﬁcient to drive meaningful in-
ternal engagement. Research shows that employees often do not understand ESG regulations
or see them as relevant to their own work; in fact, only 19% of employees globally report un-
derstanding ESG regulations and their organizational impact (EY 2024). As a result, framing
collaboration purely in terms of regulatory pressure is unlikely to generate engagement across
departments.
Instead, organizations must complement compliance narratives with a focus on internal value
creation and purpose. This means highlighting how ESG collaboration, particularly between
HR and sustainability teams, can streamline workﬂows, reduce duplication, and create more
credible and complete reporting. It also involves making ESG personally relevant to employees
by connecting it to their day-to-day roles and motivations. For instance, when hybrid work
is framed not just as a regulatory topic, but as an opportunity to demonstrate impact on both
employee well-being and climate goals, it is more likely to resonate across the organization.
For HR departments, collaborating with sustainability teams offers an opportunity to reinforce
the strategic value of hybrid work by linking it not only to employee well-being but also to
environmental and organizational performance goals. By demonstrating that hybrid work con-
tributes to reducing commuting-related emissions and supports the company’s broader climate
targets, HR can more effectively advocate for ﬂexible work policies at the leadership level. This
is particularly relevant in a post-COVID context, where major companies such as Amazon, Star-
bucks, Apple, Google, and Disney have rolled back hybrid work in favor of traditional ofﬁce
==PAGE:73==
64
models (Staff 2025, Telford and Abril 2024). Reframing hybrid work as both a people-centered
and environmentally impactful initiative positions HR as more than a personnel function, it be-
comes a strategic contributor to the company’s long-term ESG agenda. This perspective not
only strengthens the case for maintaining ﬂexible work models but also helps build a more
integrated understanding of workforce strategies across departments.
For sustainability professionals, collaboration with HR opens the door to new data sources
and narratives that enhance ESG reporting. Topics such as commuting patterns, workspace
occupancy, and employee behavior all play a role in calculating Scope 3 emissions, yet this data
often resides in HR systems. Gaining access to and contextualizing this information can lead
to more complete and credible environmental reporting. In addition, closer collaboration can
lessen the resource burden on sustainability teams, who often spend signiﬁcant time gathering
data from multiple departments or external databases and verifying its accuracy. Streamlining
this process through direct cooperation with HR not only improves efﬁciency but also supports
more timely and reliable reporting. Moreover, working with HR can help sustainability teams
communicate more human-centered stories in their reports, which may resonate more strongly
with internal stakeholders, employees, and the general public.
Beyond these department-speciﬁc beneﬁts, increased collaboration has the potential to reduce
redundancy and internal inefﬁciency. When departments operate in silos, similar data is col-
lected twice, misaligned messages are sent externally, and reporting cycles are unnecessarily
extended. By developing shared processes and goals, even in small, incremental ways, organi-
zations can free up resources, improve accuracy, and reduce frustration across teams.
It is also critical to recognize the cultural and emotional dimensions of urgency. People often
resist collaboration not just because they are busy, but because it introduces uncertainty: Who
owns this topic now? Will collaboration undermine my team’s autonomy? Will it require skills
I don’t have? Creating a sense of urgency must therefore be accompanied by empathetic com-
munication that acknowledges these concerns. It is not about blaming individual departments
for past shortcomings, but about inviting them to be part of a better solution, one that aligns
with their own goals and values.
Creating urgency cannot be left to chance. It requires active leadership. Senior executives and
ESG leadership teams must clearly communicate that collaboration is not only expected but
supported and valued. This includes making space for cross-functional discussions, recognizing
==PAGE:74==
65
early efforts, and connecting collaboration to the company’s broader strategic goals.
However, urgency must also be championed by mid-level managers, particularly within HR and
sustainability, who are close enough to operational realities to understand what’s feasible and
where quick wins might be possible. These managers can identify overlooked synergies, surface
practical barriers, and propose low-risk pilot initiatives that demonstrate value early on. Internal
communications teams, too, can play a role by shaping narratives that highlight the beneﬁts of
collaboration, through case studies, internal newsletters, or learning sessions. When employees
see real-world examples of what collaboration can achieve, the concept becomes more concrete
and less abstract.

Creating a sense of urgency is not about manufacturing crisis, nor is it about applying pressure
without support. It is about building shared recognition that the current way of working, while

highlighting both the external risks of continued fragmentation and the internal beneﬁts of cross-

To illustrate how cross-departmental collaboration can address shared challenges and deliver
tangible value, this study also introduces a practical tool developed to support ESG departments:
a Scope 3 Category 7 Emissions Calculation Tool.
This tool is designed to help organizations more easily calculate emissions related to employee
commuting, one of the most commonly reported, yet inconsistently calculated, Scope 3 cate-
gories. As highlighted in the empirical ﬁndings, companies often struggle with this category
due to fragmented data ownership: HR departments typically hold commuting and work-pattern
data, while ESG teams are responsible for translating that data into reportable emissions ﬁgures,
if they can even access it due to privacy regulations.
The calculation tool streamlines this process by providing a standardized methodology and input
structure. However, it relies on interdepartmental collaboration to function effectively. HR must
supply relevant, GDPR-compliant data on commuting behavior or hybrid work frequency, while
the ESG department interprets and processes this data within emissions reporting frameworks.
Without this basic level of cooperation, even the most efﬁcient tools cannot deliver their full
value.
The following subsection explores how and why this tool was developed, how it works in prac-
tice, and what it reveals about the institutional barriers and opportunities, for operationalizing
collaboration in the ESG reporting process.
5.3.1
Scope 3: Category 7 Emissions Calculation Tool
As shown in both the sustainability report review and the interview conducted with A.P. Møller
- Maersk A/S, one of the key challenges in ESG reporting lies in the inconsistent methodologies
==PAGE:76==
67
used for calculating Scope 3 emissions. While Scope 3 reporting is now mandatory under the
Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), the lack of standardization undermines
two of its core goals: comparability and transparency. Different assumptions, data sources, and
calculation methods lead to emissions ﬁgures that are difﬁcult to evaluate across companies,
and even within the same company over time.
One speciﬁc area where this inconsistency is especially visible is in Category 7: Employee
Commuting. While all the companies included in this study report on Scope 3 emissions to
some degree, their approaches to employee commuting vary signiﬁcantly. Six out of 18 compa-
nies rely on generic average-data models, ﬁve use company-speciﬁc employee surveys, another
ﬁve combine multiple methods, and three provide no methodological explanation at all. This
diversity of practices not only limits the accuracy and relevance of the data but also suggests
that commuting emissions are often treated as an afterthought, rather than as a meaningful and
manageable component of a company’s emissions proﬁle.
This lack of prioritization was further underscored in the interview with Maersk, where it was
noted that employee commuting is not currently a focus area in their ESG reporting. However,
Maersk emphasized that they would be open to improving their methodology if a simple, plug-
and-play tool existed—one that could reduce the time and internal resources required to collect,
analyze, and report commuting-related emissions.
In response to this gap, this thesis proposes a Scope 3 Category 7 Emissions Calculation Tool.
The tool is designed to provide a standardized, user-friendly, and resource-efﬁcient way for
companies to estimate emissions from employee commuting. While technically straightfor-
ward, the tool cannot function in isolation: it requires basic interdepartmental collaboration,
particularly between HR, who hold or can collect relevant commuting or hybrid work data,
and ESG departments, who are responsible for emissions reporting. This makes the tool not
only a methodological response but also a practical test case for the kind of interdepartmental
collaboration discussed throughout this thesis.
The tool adopts the distance-based method as mentioned in section 2.3.2 and is designed to be
ﬂexible and adaptable, regardless of how much company-speciﬁc data is available. At a mini-
mum, it requires only two key inputs: the number of commuting days per week and the one-way
commuting distance from home to the workplace. All other data points can be based on reason-
able assumptions if more detailed company-speciﬁc data is unavailable. Table 17 outlines both

One of the key strengths of the tool is its ability to adapt to changing work patterns, such as
ﬂuctuations in the number of days employees work from home. As hybrid work becomes more
dynamic, commuting behavior is no longer ﬁxed and may vary seasonally, across roles, or in
response to internal policy changes. This variability has direct implications for emissions es-
timates, making it essential that companies collect commuting data regularly and update their
inputs accordingly. If more employees begin working from home more frequently, emissions
from commuting may decrease, but without updated data, the tool would continue to reﬂect
outdated assumptions, reducing the reliability of reported ﬁgures. The tool is designed to ac-
commodate these changes by allowing users to easily modify commuting frequency or distance,
and it encourages companies to collect data multiple times a year to capture evolving patterns.
This reinforces the idea that Scope 3 reporting is not a one-time exercise, but an ongoing pro-
cess that must keep pace with how people actually work. In this way, the tool not only improves
reporting accuracy but also helps organizations remain responsive to structural changes in their
workforce.
Despite its strengths, the tool has some limitations. First, it relies on EU-based emission fac-
tors, which makes it most applicable to companies operating within Europe. For organizations
with global operations, regional differences in transport infrastructure, energy mixes, and fuel
sources may lead to less accurate estimates. Future versions of the tool could be improved by
allowing users to select region-speciﬁc emission factors or geographic settings. Second, while
the tool is designed to be easy to use, its accuracy depends heavily on input quality. In cases
where data is not regularly updated, estimates may quickly become outdated. For example,
Rambøll Group A/S continues to use commuting survey data from 2019, which may no longer
reﬂect current commuting patterns. To address this, future iterations of the tool could include
built-in reminders or integration with digital systems to support automated, periodic data col-
lection. Third, the current version of the tool does not yet account for emissions from home
ofﬁces, such as electricity or heating used during remote workdays. While this is a relevant area
for future expansion, the immediate priority has been to create a standard, accessible calcula-
tion method for commuting-related emissions, an area where companies currently struggle with
consistency and comparability. Including home ofﬁce emissions will be considered in the next
phase of development, once a reliable framework for commuting data has been established.

reporting but also serves as a practical mechanism for strengthening interdepartmental collab-
oration. To use the tool effectively, companies must facilitate coordination between HR and
sustainability teams. HR departments typically hold, or can easily collect, key data related to
employee commuting patterns, work arrangements, and location, all essential inputs for the
tool. Meanwhile, sustainability teams are responsible for processing these inputs, applying the
correct emission factors, and integrating the results into broader ESG reporting frameworks. By
working together, these departments can streamline data ﬂows, reduce duplication of effort, and
improve the accuracy and transparency of Scope 3 reporting. This shared process encourages
a mutual understanding of how hybrid work inﬂuences environmental impact and helps each
team see their role in a larger sustainability context.
5.4
Limitations
While this study provides valuable insights into the relationship between hybrid work and ESG
reporting and how interdepartmental collaboration shapes that process, there are several limita-
tions associated with both the sustainability report analysis and the semi-structured interviews
that needs to be taken into account.
One major limitation of analyzing sustainability reports is that they are prepared for external
stakeholders and may reﬂect a company’s strategic messaging rather than a fully transparent
account of its sustainability practices. Companies may selectively highlight positive sustain-
ability efforts while downplaying challenges, making it difﬁcult to assess the actual impact of
hybrid work on environmental sustainability and employee well-being. Additionally, since hy-
brid work is not a mandatory reporting category under the CSRD, its mention in reports varies.
Some companies provided more detailed insights, while others never even mentioned it.
Another limitation concerns the timeframe of the reports analyzed. Since the study includes
only reports from 2023, 2024, and 2023/24, a period that coincides with the early stages of
CSRD implementation, it captures a relatively short and transitional phase in companies’ re-
porting practices. Many organizations were still adapting to the new regulatory requirements
and were likely in their ﬁrst or second cycle of CSRD-aligned reporting. As such, reporting
structures, priorities, and methodologies were still evolving. The ﬁndings should therefore be
viewed as a snapshot of emerging practices rather than mature or fully established approaches
to ESG reporting.
==PAGE:80==
71
The semi-structured interview data is also subject to limitations, particularly in terms of sample
size. With only two participating companies, the insights gathered may not be fully represen-
tative of broader industry practices. Organizations operating under different business models
or workplace cultures may report on hybrid work and approach interdepartmental collabora-
tion in different ways. In particular, the dynamics between HR and sustainability departments
are likely to vary depending on organizational structure, leadership, and resource allocation.
This small sample size limits the ability to generalize the ﬁndings related to interdepartmental
collaboration.
Furthermore, when the interviews were conducted, the European Commission had just proposed
a delay in CSRD reporting obligations for certain companies, in response to concerns that many
organizations were struggling to meet the new requirements. This regulatory uncertainty inﬂu-
enced at least one of the companies interviewed, which was in the process of interpreting how
the proposed changes might affect its future reporting obligations and level of compliance. As
a result, some responses reﬂected a cautious and provisional approach, shaped more by imme-
diate adaptation than by long-term strategy. This context may have inﬂuenced how seriously
certain reporting categories, including Scope 3 emissions and hybrid work, were prioritized in-
ternally. Consequently, certain ﬁndings from the interviews should be viewed as capturing a
transitional moment rather than fully ﬂeshed out ESG reporting practices.
Despite these limitations, the combined analysis of public sustainability reports and more in
depth interviews provides a valid and robust foundation for answering the research questions.
The ﬁndings reveal consistent patterns in how hybrid work is currently framed within ESG
narratives, and they highlight structural barriers, such as lack of methodological consistency
and limited interdepartmental collaboration, that are relevant for both scholars and practition-
ers. While broader studies are needed to validate these ﬁndings across different contexts, this
research offers an important early contribution to the understanding of hybrid work and collab-
oration within sustainability reporting frameworks.
5.5
Further Research
While this study has given many interesting results when it comes to the treatment of hybrid
work in ESG reporting and the structural barriers posed by organizational silos, it also surfaces
several important gaps that warrant further academic research. These gaps span methodological,
organizational, and technical domains, each offering potential for future research to build a more
==PAGE:81==
72
comprehensive understanding of how cross-cutting sustainability topics can be better integrated
into ESG reporting frameworks and corporate practices.
One immediate avenue for further research lies in expanding the empirical scope of both in-
terviews and document analysis. This study was limited to two semi-structured interviews and
18 sustainability reports from large, often multinational companies operating within or near the
EU regulatory sphere. While sufﬁcient for exploratory analysis, this relatively narrow sample
limits the ability to generalize the ﬁndings. Future research should adopt a broader sampling
strategy, encompassing a larger number of companies across multiple sectors, geographies, and
organizational sizes. Particularly important would be the inclusion of small and medium-sized
enterprises (SMEs), which are often underrepresented in ESG scholarship but face distinct chal-
lenges in aligning with regulatory frameworks like CSRD.
Moreover, comparative studies across sectors with differing operational emissions proﬁles (e.g.,
logistics vs. knowledge-based industries) could provide a more nuanced understanding of when
and how hybrid work is considered material. For example, companies in IT or professional
services may view hybrid work as more strategically relevant to their emissions and well-being
goals, while manufacturing ﬁrms may not prioritize it due to operational constraints. Under-
standing these sectoral differences could help tailor ESG tools, guidance, and reporting expec-
tations more appropriately.
The ﬁndings of this study suggest that interdepartmental silos remain a persistent barrier to inte-
grated ESG reporting, particularly regarding hybrid work. However, more granular research is
needed to unpack the speciﬁc organizational mechanisms that facilitate or hinder collaboration
between HR, sustainability, and other relevant functions such as ﬁnance and operations.
Future studies could adopt a comparative case study methodology to examine how different
organizational structures, such as matrix models, integrated ESG governance committees, or
cross-functional project teams, inﬂuence the depth and frequency of collaboration. Addition-
ally, the role of leadership and mid-level management in shaping interdepartmental dynamics
deserves deeper exploration. Are there particular leadership styles or incentive structures that
foster more effective collaboration? How do middle managers mediate between strategic ESG
goals and day-to-day operational constraints?
There is also a need to better understand the informal cultural dimensions of collaboration.
==PAGE:82==
73
Ethnographic or action research methods could be valuable here, allowing researchers to ob-
serve how sustainability and HR professionals interpret roles, responsibilities, and the perceived
value of joint work. These insights could inform the development of more targeted organiza-
tional interventions, such as shared performance indicators, collaborative training programs, or
ESG literacy campaigns.
Another key area for further investigation is the methodological inconsistency observed in
Scope 3, Category 7 emissions reporting. As this study demonstrates, the lack of standardiza-
tion in calculating employee commuting emissions undermines comparability and credibility,
which as mentioned before is core goals of the CSRD. Future research could aim to develop,
test, and validate standardized methodologies for calculating hybrid work-related emissions,
including the integration of emissions from home ofﬁces (e.g., heating, electricity, and digital
infrastructure usage).
This would involve technical research into how energy use can be fairly allocated between
personal and professional spheres in home settings, a complex but increasingly relevant chal-
lenge. Additionally, interdisciplinary collaboration between sustainability science, data ethics,
and privacy law scholars is needed to explore how GDPR-compliant data-sharing mechanisms
between HR and ESG teams can be structured to allow meaningful yet legally sound emissions
accounting.
Longitudinal studies that track how companies’ methodologies evolve over time, especially as
regulatory requirements mature, would also be valuable. Do early adopters of rigorous com-
muting emissions accounting maintain or abandon their efforts? What institutional, regulatory,
or resource-related factors inﬂuence their choices? Answering these questions can help policy-
makers identify leverage points for promoting more consistent and durable reporting practices.
While this thesis introduces a practical emissions calculation tool for Scope 3, Category 7, its
implementation remains theoretical. Future research could test this and similar tools in live
corporate environments to assess their usability, scalability, and actual impact on ESG reporting
quality. Field experiments or pilot studies across companies of different sizes and sectors could
provide data on user adoption, data accuracy, interdepartmental engagement, and time/resource
efﬁciency.
==PAGE:83==
74
Furthermore, these tools could be evaluated not only in terms of technical output (i.e., emis-
sions accuracy) but also in terms of organizational learning. Do they spark new conversations
between HR and sustainability teams? Do they help establish reporting routines or institutional
memory? Mixed-methods approaches, combining surveys, tool usage analytics, and interviews
could generate a comprehensive picture of how ESG tools function as both technical and cul-
tural mechanisms within organizations.

6 | Conclusion
This thesis set out to explore how hybrid work is positioned in corporate sustainability efforts,
speciﬁcally through the lens of ESG reporting. As hybrid work models have become an inte-
gral part of modern organizational life, shaped in large part by the COVID-19 pandemic, they
carry both environmental and social implications that intersect with emerging sustainability
regulations. However, the integration of hybrid work into ESG practices remains uncertain and
inconsistently applied. This thesis examined not only whether and how companies incorporate
hybrid work into their ESG reports, but also what organizational dynamics, particularly inter-
departmental collaboration, shape this process. The purpose of this thesis was guided by three
research questions that set out to deepen the understanding of hybrid work, ESG reporting and
interdepartmental collaboration.
The ﬁrst question aimed to identify how companies incorporate hybrid work into their ESG
reporting, particularly in the environmental and social pillars. This was done through a
qualitative analysis of both 18 sustainability reports and expert interviews from two different
companies. The results indicate that hybrid work is inconsistently addressed across both di-
mensions of ESG reporting and is primarily, but barely, framed through a social lens, with only
limited and indirect environmental treatment.
In the social pillar, hybrid work is most commonly presented as part of a broader narrative
around workplace ﬂexibility, employee well-being, and talent attraction. Many companies posi-
tion hybrid work as a response to changing employee expectations, often linking it to increased
autonomy, improved work-life balance, and efforts to create a more attractive and inclusive
work culture. These references tend to appear under ESRS S1: Own Workforce, and align
with general trends in post-pandemic workforce strategy. However, these mentions are typi-
cally high-level and anecdotal, with only one company mentioning it in the DMA. None of
the analyzed companies support their statements with quantitative indicators, such as ﬂexibility
uptake rates or employee satisfaction scores, and none track changes over time or sort the data
across roles, genders, or geographies. In short, while hybrid work is acknowledged as relevant
to social sustainability, it is not yet treated as a structured or measurable social performance
topic in ESG reporting.
In the environmental pillar, the treatment of hybrid work is even more limited. Most companies
report on Scope 3 Category 7: Employee Commuting, but very few explicitly link commuting
==PAGE:85==
76
emissions to hybrid work policies. Those that do tend to mention reduced commuting as a gen-
eral outcome of ﬂexible work arrangements, without offering further analysis or quantitative
breakdowns. Only one company in the sample included remote work emissions in its Scope
3 calculations by estimating energy consumption from home ofﬁces, despite this being an ac-
cepted and increasingly relevant methodology under the GHG Protocol. This omission suggests
that companies often fail to recognize the full environmental implications of shifting work pat-
terns, especially as responsibility for energy use becomes more distributed between ofﬁce and
home.
Moreover, hybrid work is rarely recognized as a material topic. In only one case was hybrid
work explicitly identiﬁed as material to the company’s ESG strategy. Even among companies
preparing for or claiming alignment with the CSRD and ESRS frameworks, hybrid work was
either entirely absent or treated as a minor, context-setting detail. No companies formally linked
hybrid work to their climate transition plans, ESG targets, or scenario analyses. This absence in-
dicates a conceptual gap, which is that hybrid work is widely practiced, and it affects emissions,
resource use, and employee well-being, but it is not framed or measured as a sustainability issue
within current ESG structures.
Taken together, these ﬁndings highlight a disconnect between operational practices and ESG
reporting. Hybrid work is common, and its ESG relevance is evident in theory. Yet within
corporate reporting, it remains underdeveloped, inconsistently framed, and largely unmeasured.
This points to a missed opportunity, that by not incorporating hybrid work more explicitly and
rigorously into their ESG disclosures, companies risk overlooking a key area of both environ-
mental and social impact, particularly one that is expected to grow in relevance under evolving
regulatory frameworks.
The second question set out to understand what role interdepartmental collaboration play
in shaping hybrid work’s contribution to both environmental impact and employee well-
being. This question was answered mostly by the use of the expert interviews and a literature
review regarding the inﬂuence of organizational silos in companies and general theory regarding
interdepartmental collaboration. Here, the results show that the lack of structured collaboration
between departments signiﬁcantly undermines the ability of companies to understand, measure,
and report the dual ESG impact of hybrid work.
Across both case companies, it became evident that HR and sustainability departments operate
==PAGE:86==
77
in parallel, but not in partnership. HR departments are typically responsible for developing
and implementing hybrid work policies, tracking employee well-being and engagement, and
communicating internally about workplace culture. Meanwhile, ESG departments focus on data
collection, emissions reporting, regulatory alignment, and the preparation of ofﬁcial reports.
Although both departments interact with hybrid work in meaningful ways, they do so from
different angles and without coordinated strategies or shared ownership.
The expert interviews revealed that there are no formal processes or systems in place for in-
terdepartmental collaboration related to hybrid work. Communication between HR and ESG
professionals occurs on an ad hoc basis, often limited to requests for data. Moreover, legal and
practical barriers further complicate collaboration. For example, data privacy regulations such
as GDPR create uncertainty around what kinds of employee-level data can be shared between
departments, particularly regarding commuting patterns, working hours, or location-based ac-
tivity. In practice, this means that potentially valuable data remains locked within departmental
silos, even when it could serve multiple reporting and strategic purposes.
The literature reviewed in parallel supports these observations. Research on organizational
silos consistently ﬁnds that fragmented structures impede coherent sustainability strategies, lead
to inefﬁcient data usage, and reinforce the perception that ESG is the domain of specialists
rather than a shared organizational responsibility. This study afﬁrms those patterns: in the
context of hybrid work, siloed departments lead to siloed reporting, where environmental data
is disconnected from workplace policies, and social data is omitted from external disclosures.
The consequences of this disconnect are both technical and strategic. On the technical side,
the lack of collaboration prevents companies from accurately quantifying the environmental
beneﬁts (or trade-offs) of hybrid work, such as reduced commuting emissions or changes in
ofﬁce energy consumption. On the strategic side, it weakens the narrative coherence of ESG
reporting: companies may highlight hybrid work in internal communications as a key part of
their people strategy, while failing to mention, or only brieﬂy mentioning it, in their formal ESG
documents. This creates inconsistencies and makes it harder for stakeholders, such as investors,
regulators and employees, to assess whether hybrid work is being leveraged as a sustainability
asset.
The ﬁndings also highlight an important cultural barrier: neither HR nor ESG departments view
hybrid work as part of their core mandate. HR professionals see it as an internal operational
==PAGE:87==
78
concern, while ESG professionals consider it too human-focused or lacking in standardized
metrics. As a result, hybrid work falls through the cracks, not because it lacks ESG relevance,
but because no one is structurally incentivized to make the case for its inclusion.
In short, the results clearly show that interdepartmental collaboration is not just a nice extra,
it is a prerequisite for capturing hybrid work’s full ESG impact. Without clearer ownership,
shared goals, and mechanisms for regular communication, companies will continue to report
only partial versions of hybrid work’s role in sustainability. Addressing this gap will require not
just organizational redesign, but also a shift in mindset: recognizing that cross-functional topics
demand cross-functional solutions.
The third and last question was to develop and identify what practical step companies can
take to improve collaboration between HR and sustainability departments to enhance
their ESG reporting. This question was addressed through a synthesis of the interview insights
and literature on interdepartmental collaboration and organizational learning in the context of
sustainability. The results indicate that while collaboration between HR and ESG teams is cur-
rently limited, there are identiﬁable, actionable steps that companies can take to begin bridging
this gap, provided that collaboration is supported by leadership, enabled by tools, and made
relevant to each department’s own goals.
One of the most immediate and recurring insights from the expert interviews is that collab-
oration needs to be made visible and valuable to both departments. At present, cooperation
between HR and ESG teams is often viewed as an additional burden, with unclear beneﬁts or
outcomes. To change this, companies must create clear incentives and urgency around why
collaboration matters, both for improved ESG reporting and for internal decision-making. This
includes demonstrating how HR data (such as ﬂexibility metrics, employee survey responses, or
retention statistics) can enhance ESG disclosures, while also showing how sustainability efforts
can support HR’s goals, for instance by reinforcing employer branding or meeting employee
expectations around climate-conscious workplaces.
A ﬁrst step is to identify shared ESG touchpoints between departments; topics or indicators
that neither HR nor ESG can fully report on alone. Hybrid work is a clear example: it inﬂu-
ences commuting emissions (Scope 3, Category 7), but also affects employee well-being and
inclusion. Once such shared topics are recognized, companies can establish joint reporting re-
sponsibilities, ensuring that both departments contribute to data collection, interpretation, and
==PAGE:88==
79
narrative development. This approach helps break down silos by giving departments a reason
to engage and a shared stake in the outcome.
A practical contribution of this thesis is the development of a standardized, easy-to-use tool
designed to support ESG reporting on hybrid work, speciﬁcally in relation to Scope 3 Cate-
gory 7 emissions from employee commuting. The tool aims to reduce the reporting burden on
sustainability teams by providing a clear structure for collecting and calculating emissions data
that accounts for hybrid work patterns. At the same time, it is designed to be accessible for
HR professionals who may not be familiar with ESG frameworks, enabling them to contribute
relevant data without requiring technical expertise. By simplifying the data collection process
and aligning it with reporting needs, the tool helps establish a shared structure and language
between HR and sustainability departments—laying the groundwork for more consistent and
collaborative reporting practices.
However, tools alone are not sufﬁcient. The interviews highlighted the need for clear gover-
nance structures and ownership models. For example is the need for executive leadership to set
the tone and expectations. Without top-down support, collaboration is unlikely to move beyond
informal efforts. Leaders can play a key role by publicly endorsing interdepartmental collabo-
ration, allocating time and resources to joint projects, and embedding collaboration goals into
broader ESG or HR strategies. For example, setting shared performance indicators or including
collaboration efforts in department-level evaluations can reinforce that this work is not optional,
but essential to delivering on sustainability commitments.
Finally, fostering a culture of collaboration requires making the value of cooperation tangible at
the employee level. When individuals in HR and ESG roles see that collaboration makes their
own work easier, more impactful, or more meaningful, they are far more likely to invest time
and energy in it. This can be achieved through small wins, such as co-developing a hybrid work
survey or piloting a shared reporting template, that build momentum and trust across teams.
In summary, improving collaboration between HR and sustainability departments requires more
than goodwill, it demands structural support, technical enablers, and cultural change. By iden-
tifying shared goals, providing accessible tools, and embedding collaboration into governance
processes, companies can begin to close the gap between the operational reality of hybrid work
and its underdeveloped presence in ESG reporting. Doing so will not only improve the accuracy
and coherence of disclosures, but also unlock the strategic potential of hybrid work as a lever
==PAGE:89==
80
for both environmental and social performance.
This thesis set out to investigate a complex and underexplored area: the integration of hybrid
work into ESG reporting, and the organizational dynamics that inﬂuence how it is represented.
To do so, a two-part qualitative approach was employed, consisting of a thematic analysis of
18 corporate sustainability and integrated reports, combined with expert interviews with HR
and ESG professionals from two companies. This mixed strategy was chosen to capture both
the external communication of ESG priorities and the internal structures that shape how those
priorities are understood and operationalized.
From the get go, the expectation was that hybrid work would be inconsistently reported but
increasingly recognized, particularly given its growing relevance in the post-pandemic work-
place and its intersection with both social and environmental performance metrics. While these
assumptions were partially validated, hybrid work was indeed inconsistently addressed, the ex-
tent of its absence from formal ESG frameworks and lack of internal coordination around its
reporting was more pronounced than anticipated.
Reﬂecting on the research process itself, one of the most valuable aspects was the ability to
analyze both external documents and internal priorities and routines. The sustainability reports
allowed for a broad view of how hybrid work is positioned publicly, while the interviews pro-
vided a behind-the-scenes understanding of why certain data is, or is not, reported. This pairing
revealed the limitations of relying on ESG reports alone to assess corporate sustainability prac-
tices, particularly for cross-cutting topics that do not ﬁt neatly within one reporting category or
department.
At the same time, the research was shaped by several practical and contextual limitations. The
number of interviews was limited, which constrained the diversity of perspectives. The com-
panies involved were both based in Denmark, a country with relatively progressive ESG and
labor standards, meaning the ﬁndings may not generalize directly to other regional or regula-
tory contexts. Moreover, the evolving nature of ESG regulation, particularly around the CSRD
and ESRS frameworks, means that current practices may shift rapidly in the coming years.
Nonetheless, these boundaries helped focus the research on capturing a snapshot of current
practice, with relevance for companies already preparing for these frameworks.
Ultimately, the ﬁndings reveal not only what is missing from ESG reports, but also why it
==PAGE:90==
81
is missing, and what could be done differently to improve the reports. They point to a broader
need for companies to build the internal bridges required to report on topics that do not ﬁt neatly
within both traditional departmental categories and the regulatory frameworks they are trying
to follow. In that sense, the thesis also serves as a reﬂection on how sustainability is governed
within organizations, and how topics like hybrid work, despite their day-to-day relevance, can
remain structurally invisible in formal reporting systems. This makes it especially important
to identify and promote good practices early as companies begin aligning with the CSRD, a
relatively new and evolving framework. Establishing integrated approaches from the outset can
help ensure that cross-cutting topics like hybrid work are not overlooked, but instead embedded
into the broader logic of sustainability governance from the beginning.
