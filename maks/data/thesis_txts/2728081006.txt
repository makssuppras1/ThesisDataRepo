Abstract  
This thesis explores the nature-based carbon market, specifically focusing on identi-
fying market barriers and opportunities for entrepreneurs. Through expert interviews 
and a comprehensive literature review, the research investigates the interrelation of 
political, economic, social, technological, environmental, and legal factors influencing 
the market's development and the commercial potential of nature-based carbon off-
sets. The findings emphasize critical market barriers, including reputational concerns 
of investors and buyers, challenges in ensuring carbon credits' integrity and long-
term validity, and uncertainties surrounding policy frameworks and regulatory devel-
opment. On the other hand, the study highlights promising opportunities such as 
technological advancements for accurate carbon quantification, the recognition of 
social impact, and the emerging environmental service market. While the thesis re-
frains from offering pre-packaged recommendations, it provides a robust overview, 
empowering entrepreneurs to make informed decisions and encouraging further tar-
geted market research. The research contributes to understanding the potential of 
the nature-based carbon market and its role in combating climate change while de-
livering multiple benefits to ecosystems and communities.

1. 
Introduction 
The interaction with the environment and its imposed impact on Earth over the next 
decades will be critical to saving the planet from environmental devastation. Histori-
cal and ongoing human activities, such as burning fossil fuels for energy and increas-
ing global temperatures, cause the increased concentration of greenhouse gases 
(GHG) in the atmosphere. Known as global warming, this effect significantly impacts 
the planet's ecosystems, human health, and the economy (Baim, 2023).  
Climate change is human-induced and affects weather and climate extremes world-
wide (IPCC, 2023). Alarming signs of climate change include, among others, the 
global sea level rise, increasing intensity and frequency of hurricanes, longer wildfire 
seasons, decreasing sea ice cover, and the world's plant and animal species being 
threatened by extinction (NASA, 2023). Whether the severity and impacts of climate 
change will persist forever will be determined by future human actions (Baim, 2023). 
A landmark in the multilateral climate change process was the Paris Agreement. At 
the 2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference, 196 countries signed a legally 
binding international treaty to limit global warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-
industrial levels by the end of the century. It was the first time a binding agreement 
brought together all nations to combat climate change (United Nations , 2023).  
Over seven years later, the Emissions Gap Report shows that the world is “… far 
away from the Paris Agreement goal […]. Policies currently in place point to a 2.8-
degree Celsius temperature rise by the end of the century” (UNEP, 2022). Hans-Otto 
Pörtner, co-chair of working group II of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate 
Change (IPCC), clearly stated: “The scientific evidence is unequivocal: climate 
change is a threat to human wellbeing and the health of the planet. Any further delay 
in concerted global action will miss a brief and rapidly closing window to secure a 
livable future“ (IPCC, 2022).  
Despite the rapid decarbonization of the economy, nature-based solutions (NBS) 
possess the potential to significantly contribute to mitigating climate change. Accord-
ing to the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), NBS can deliver up to 
one-third of the globally required emission reductions by 2030, specifically up to 11.7 
==PAGE:9==
2 
 
GtCO2e per year, through protecting, restoring, and sustainable management of nat-
ural carbon sinks and reservoirs. In addition to their carbon reduction potential, NBS 
is known for delivering many co-benefits to society, including biodiversity conserva-
tion and climate change adaptation (UNEP, 2021). However, the possibility will only 
be realized with the necessary finance channeled into nature's resiliency. Currently, 
USD 133 billion per year gets invested into nature (86% public, 14% private), but the 
investment amount needs to triple by 2030 to meet global targets in climate change, 
biodiversity, and degradation (UNEP, 2021).  
The voluntary carbon market has been recognized as a critical tool for facilitating 
private capital flows into high-quality natural activities. If properly used, the voluntary 
carbon market may help businesses and organizations fulfill their net-zero goals by 
incentivizing the decarbonization of their worldwide operations and protecting and 
restoring the natural world (World Economic Forum, 2021). 
The number of companies who pledge carbon neutrality is rising significantly, and 
most must rely on compensation for their remaining emissions. Companies voluntar-
ily buy carbon credits to offset those emissions, a verified emission reduction unit, 
each equivalent to one ton of carbon, issued by private certifications standard 
(Nicolas Kreibich, 2021).  
According to Bloomberg NEF, the growing demand for emission compensation leads 
to the predicted market value of $1 trillion in 2037 “if several fundamental issues are 
addressed." In the same report, Bloomberg NEF announced that the voluntary mar-
ket shrunk 2022 by about 4% "due to fears of reputational risk from purchasing low-
quality credits" (BloombergNEF, 2023). 
Missing clarity, transparency, and accountability are just a few of the barriers that 
need to be overcome to fully utilize the potential of natural climate solutions and 
strengthen voluntary carbon markets to support climate and nature investment at 
scale. Innovation and collaboration will be instrumental in expanding the volume and 
integrity of carbon markets (World Economic Forum, 2021).  
Entrepreneurs are well-positioned to overcome possible issues with creative busi-
ness ideas and technical, financial, and social innovation (Malen, 2017). To assess 
the commercial potential and to successfully capitalize on the business opportunities

1.1 
Research Objective 
The research objective of this thesis is to uncover potential market opportunities and 
barriers within the voluntary carbon market, specifically in the context of nature-
based solutions. The aim is to examine how political, economic, social, technological, 
environmental, and legal factors are related to further market development and influ-
ence the commercial potential of nature-based carbon offsets.  
The objectives are to create a concise and comprehensive overview of the voluntary 
carbon market. With an extensive introduction to the critical elements of the market, 
entrepreneurs will be equipped with a solid knowledge base for conducting further 
market research and feasibility studies. The outcome of this research will enable 
them to confidently navigate within this complex, fragmented, and dynamic environ-
ment of the voluntary carbon market to facilitate the successful integration of long-
term sustainable business ideas. 
This research is essential because it contributes to realizing innovative solutions that 
harness the potential of the voluntary carbon market to unlock funding and technol-
ogy for nature-based solutions. This research drives sustainable entrepreneurship 
by providing entrepreneurs with a holistic understanding of the voluntary carbon mar-
ket and its dynamics. It fosters the implementation of impactful initiatives that can 
effectively combat climate change.

2.1 
Carbon Markets  
Carbon markets are a market-based mechanism designed to limit greenhouse gas 
emissions by putting a price on them. It is a central component of climate policy and 
can be understood as a trade system to purchase and sale of credits that allow com-
panies to offset a certain amount of CO2e (Calel, 2013). Carbon allowances or                  
-credits are defined as certificates that represents amounts of removed or kept out 
greenhouse gas (GHG) from the atmosphere, which can be defined as (Alvarez, 
2023):  
1 allowance = 1 ton of CO2 = equivalent amount of other GHG, N2x* and PFC’s** 
*N2x: Nitrous Oxides  
 
 
 
      **PFC’s: Perfluorocarbons 
Carbon markets can be divided into regulatory and voluntary carbon markets. The 
regulatory market, also known as the compliance market, is regulated by law and 
trades the emissions allocated by governments to industrial facilities. The voluntary 
market provides companies with opportunities to offset their GHG emissions by fi-
nancing carbon reduction or climate action projects (Alvarez, 2023). Both categories 
are further discussed in the following two paragraphs.  
2.2 
Compliance carbon markets  
Under the authority of governments and the United Nations (UN) the compliance 
carbon market (CCM) got implemented and regulated. According to the Organization 
for Economic Cooperations and Development (OECD), an emission trading system 
(ETS) is the most efficient way to promote decarbonization by creating economic 
==PAGE:13==
6 
 
incentives for industries to reduce their carbon footprint and force the transition to-
wards more sustainable practices.  
The CCM is characterized by a cap-and-trade scheme, where the cap defines the 
maximum amount of CO2e that is allowed to be emitted by a particular industry cov-
ered under the scheme. The cap gradually decreases over time to achieve set emis-
sion reduction targets. The CCM typically includes industry sectors that represent a 
significant source of GHG, such as power generation, manufacturing, transportation, 
and other certain heavy industries. Due to the limited number of activities for only a 
few specific sectors, the ability of CCM to scale is limited (Baim, 2023).  
Each company within those industries receives a specific number of tradable carbon 
allowances over a certain period. If a company decreases its emissions below the 
maximum, the unused allowances can be sold to a company that needs additional 
allowances. The CCM operates on an international, national, and regional level, 
while each country or region may have its own specific carbon market design and 
rules. Examples for relevant carbon trading schemes are the European Union Car-
bon Emission Trading System, the US California Cap and Trade System or the Que-
bec Cap and Trade System in Canada (Alvarez, 2023). 
2.3 
Voluntary Carbon Market 
Voluntary carbon markets (VCM) are self-regulatory and created by private organi-
zations. This market developed based on net-zero commitments by the private sec-
tor. Companies communicate their ambitious commitments about climate change 
mitigation measurements but lack cost-effective options to reduce their emissions. 
The VCM provides them with a practical and reasonable approach to meet their vol-
untarily set goals (Steyn, 2022). Every entity can willingly participate in the offset 
market which results in a higher potential for scaling and thus larger environmental 
impact (Baim, 2023). According to McKinsey, the annual demand for carbon credits 
could reach up to 2.0 gigatons of carbon dioxide (GtCO2) by 2030 and up to 13.0 
GtCO2 by 2050. The resulting market size for 2030 is expected to reach US$ 50 
billion. (C. Blaufelder, 2021). 
==PAGE:14==
7 
 
The key players of the voluntary carbon market are financers, project developers, 
registries, and buyers, accompanied by validation/verification bodies and bro-
kers/traders (Baim, 2023) and are further explained below. 
2.3.1 
Financers 
The process of every carbon project starts with securing the necessary funding 
through financers such as banks, asset managers, or capital market participants. 
They provide capital to carbon project developers with the aim to rising their invest-
ment rates into green development and meet ESG demands (Baim, 2023).  
2.3.2 
Project Developers 
Project developers represent the supply side of the VCM and use the capital from 
the financer to build environmentally regenerative projects, which reduce or avoid 
carbon emissions and provide further environmental services. The new project needs 
to be signed up for credit issuing registry, further explained in the next chapter. A 
project developer accompanies the carbon project from start to finish and is respon-
sible for the successful interaction with any other stakeholders with the aim of having 
the climate positive effects of the project certified and then selling them (Baim, 2023). 
2.3.3 
Registries/Standards  
Due to the absence of national and international legislation, rules and criteria for 
voluntary emission reduction credits are defined by registries or standards (Kollmuss, 
2008). Those organizations verify and validate carbon projects to determine the pro-
ject’s objective and the volume of the reduced or avoided emissions. Carbon offset 
projects have to full fill a minimum of requirements to be able to issue high-quality 
carbon credits. They have to ensure additionality, no overestimation, permanence, 
exclusive claim and provide additional environmental and social benefits (Favasuli, 
2021).  
The four major VCM registries, who generate the majority of the world’s VCM offsets, 
are the Gold Standard, Verra (Verified Carbon Standard), the American Carbon Reg-
istry (ACR) and the Climate Action Reserve (CAR) (So, 2023).  
Every registry/standard provides its own methodology for quantifying and verifying 
emission reductions for different nature types, including forestry, agriculture, and 
==PAGE:15==
8 
 
wetlands. Methodologies outline the method for data collection, emission factors and 
calculation techniques to assess the carbon impact of the project. An example for 
that is the Verified Carbon Standard from Verra.  
2.3.4 
Validation and Verification Body  
All carbon project related documentation needs to be assessed by an approved third-
party auditor, a validation and verification body (VVB). The VVB undertakes on-site 
visits to ensure the prescribed procedures were followed and data is accurate.  
After the successful verification and approval from both the VVC and the carbon reg-
istry, the carbon credits are issued in form of an electronic certificate to the project 
developers and can be held, sold, transferred, or retired. The issued carbon credits 
get registered to a centralized ledger together with other related project documents 
and verifications (Baim, 2023).  
2.3.5 
Brokers/Traders/Retailers 
Brokers or traders link the supply and demand side by facilitating transactions be-
tween buyers and sellers, usually acquiring a commission. They are characterized 
by having deep knowledge and understanding of the market, which enables them to 
lead customers to the credible projects generating high-quality carbon credits 
(Favasuli, 2021).  Digital marketplaces are an example of traders offering different 
portfolio carbon projects, reducing the time a customer would have to spend to en-
gage directly with project developers. In recent years, online marketplaces for carbon 
credit trading have been growing (Alvarez, 2023).  
2.3.6 
Buyers  
The demand side of the VCM is represented by companies and individuals, willing to 
offset their emissions. In contrast to the compliance markets, buyers participating 
voluntarily in the VCM to reach their net-zero emission targets (Favasuli, 2021).  
According to the Growth Hub, there are four key benefits from setting and achieving 
voluntary net-zero targets that affect the short, medium and long-term success and 
growth of a business (THE GROWTH HUB, 2022):  
• 
Profitability 
==PAGE:16==
9 
 
There is a direct correlation between sustainable practices and cost-savings. 
• 
Sustainability  
For example, by integrating circular economies, to protect businesses from 
wider environmental changes.  
• 
Reputation  
Customers prefer buying from sustainable companies, which are taking ac-
tions to reduce their environmental impact.  
• 
Investment 
The attraction of capital depends on businesses carbon reduction strategies 
or a demonstration of how the investment would contribute to a low-carbon 
economy.  
2.4 
Nature Based Solutions  
Despite a massive and rapid decarbonization, a signification contribution from na-
ture-based solutions (NBS) is required to achieve the Paris Agreement goal of 1.5°C. 
(UNEP, 2021). The International Union for the Conservation of Nature (IUCN) de-
fines them as “actions to protect, sustainably manage, and restore natural and mod-
ified ecosystems that address societal challenges effectively and adaptively, simul-
taneously benefiting people and nature” (IUCN, 2023).  
Nature-based solutions include terrestrial (forests, soil/agriculture land and wetlands) 
and aquatic solutions. This thesis focuses on land-based solutions, even though 
coastal and marine ecosystems offer additional climate mitigation potential. Together 
their estimated emission reduction and removal potential is approximately 12 GtCO2e 
per year by 2030. By 2050 this could be scaled up to 18 GtCO2e. The estimated 
contribution of nature-based solutions related to forests is approximately 62 per cent. 
24 per cent are expected to be accounted for solutions from soil and agriculture land, 
ten per cent for wetland solutions. The remaining four per cent will come from coastal 
and marine ecosystem solutions (UNEP, 2021).  
Nature-based solutions to climate change, while primarily deployed for carbon re-
duction, can have significant impacts on both people and natural areas, offering co-
benefits such as biodiversity preservation and local economic improvement. How-
==PAGE:17==
10 
 
ever, they also carry the potential for negative externalities, potentially harming bio-
diversity, and communities. Biodiversity loss, a distinct but interconnected threat, of-
fers immense benefits, known as 'natural capital' or 'ecosystem services' (Cambridge 
Zero Policy Forum, 2021). 
In addition to the differentiation according to nature types, carbon offset projects can 
also be differentiated according to their approach to mitigate climate change. Carbon 
avoidance projects refer to the prevention of carbon emissions before being released 
to the atmosphere in the first place. Carbon removal refers to the process of removing 
carbon dioxide from the atmosphere after it has been emitted. Carbon sequestration 
is a type of carbon removal, which involves capturing and storing carbon in natural 
sinks (Babacan O., 2020).

Improved Forest Management 
==PAGE:18==

Climate change forest management entails optimizing existing forests to effectively 
remove and store greenhouse gases in biomass, mitigating land-use competition 
risks associated with afforestation and reforestation (Cambridge Zero Policy Forum, 
2021). 
Reforestation/Afforestation 
Reforestation involves re-establishing forests, leading to net negative emissions in 
the long term, but it may cause short-term emissions due to volatile organic com-
pound (VOC) release. Afforestation, on the other hand, involves growing forests on 
previously non-forested land, capturing significant carbon over time, but it faces chal-
lenges related to land-use competition and potential risks (Cambridge Zero Policy 
Forum, 2021). 
Forest Conservation  
Avoided deforestation involves protecting forested areas that would have been 
cleared, leading to reduced emissions and potential mitigation of other deforestation-
related harms, such as biodiversity loss. However, challenges in establishing a cred-
ible counterfactual have raised concerns about its effectiveness in achieving climate 
goals, despite its popularity as a nature-based solution for climate change 
(Cambridge Zero Policy Forum, 2021). 
Soil Carbon Sequestration  
Soil carbon sequestration involves changing land management practices to increase 
soil carbon content and remove CO2 from the atmosphere. However, it requires on-
going maintenance to prevent CO2 re-emission, and efficacy in certain contexts re-
mains limited, posing a risk of increased emissions (Cambridge Zero Policy Forum, 
2021). 
Biochar 
Biochar, produced from biomass, can be applied to soils for long-term carbon stor-
age. However, there are risks associated with soil health due to potential accumula-
tion of heavy metals and organic contaminants, and the long-term effects of biochar 
addition remain uncertain (Cambridge Zero Policy Forum, 2021). 
Enhanced weathering 
==PAGE:19==
12 
 
Enhanced weathering, which involves accelerating natural CO2 absorption through 
mineral silicate addition to soils, holds promise for greenhouse gas removal, but its 
full potential and environmental impacts are yet to be understood. Large-scale min-
ing, transportation, and processing of minerals may result in greenhouse gas emis-
sions and negative environmental consequences. The technique's long-term effects 
and reversibility require further research, and field pilot studies are essential as cur-
rent experience is primarily lab-based (Cambridge Zero Policy Forum, 2021). 
Wetland Conservation  
Conserving wetlands safeguards crucial habitats that effectively absorb CO2. How-
ever, these areas face threats from human impacts, including agriculture. As this 
thesis discusses terrestrial solutions, the wetland section focuses exclusively on 
peatlands, a type of soil covered by water and known for its enormous carbon storage 
potential. Protection and restoration methods for peatland habitats are well-docu-
mented and readily available. Coastal kelp, seagrass, and mangrove habitats, con-
sidered valuable 'blue carbon' resources, are globally threatened and have also the 
potential for restoration (Cambridge Zero Policy Forum, 2021).  
Wetland Restoration  
Wetland and peatland restoration projects have the potential to serve as significant 
carbon sinks by removing additional carbon from the atmosphere (Cambridge Zero 
Policy Forum, 2021). 
Further analysis will be conducted by categorizing data based on nature types, with-
out considering the specific characteristics of each project type. 
2.5 
Literature Review  
Voluntary carbon markets represent a nascent area of scholarly inquiry, and the pre-
sent literature review seeks to present a comprehensive assessment of academic 
research encompassing the diverse landscape of opportunities and barriers within 
these markets. To cover the most recent developments in this dynamic field, the 
study particularly focuses on reports and articles from 2021 onwards. Organized by 
distinct perspectives, such as macro level, project level, nature type and geography, 
the review aims to offer a holistic overview of the subject matter. 
==PAGE:20==
13 
 
At the macro level, scholars highlight the importance of governance legitimacy in the 
market (Ahonen et al., 2022) and suggest that Article 6 of the Paris Agreement could 
foster convergence between VCM and compliance markets (Fattouh & Maino, 2022; 
Kreibich & Hermwille, 2021). Exchange-traded derivative products are seen as in-
strumental in supporting market development by offering standardized solutions and 
improving transparency (Gregor Spilker, 2022). Additionally, designing cost-effective 
carbon markets is underscored as crucial for ensuring environmental integrity (Betz, 
2022). 
At the project level, transparency, quality, and cost are identified as key challenges 
in the voluntary carbon market (Si Chen et al., 2021). Nature-based solutions are 
seen as promising strategies for climate mitigation and conservation, but compre-
hensive carbon accounting that includes all carbon reservoirs and changes in their 
condition is emphasized for accurate evaluation (Keith, 2021). Furthermore, Streck 
(2021) cautions against potential harm to local communities from increased offset 
volumes, proposing public-private partnerships as opportunities for project develop-
ment and carbon removal technologies. 
Geographically, research has explored the impact of carbon emission trading sys-
tems in China, demonstrating significant promotion of energy conservation and emis-
sion reduction in pilot provinces and cities (Zhang, 2022; Zhongfei Chen, 2021). 
Despite the wealth of literature on potential barriers and market opportunities within 
the voluntary carbon markets, an overarching theme emerges – a lack of holistic 
perspective. While the reviewed studies delve into specific aspects of the market, 
they often fail to provide a comprehensive understanding of the complex and dy-
namic nature of the voluntary carbon markets. To grasp the true potential and impact 
of nature-based solutions within the voluntary carbon market, an integrated approach 
is essential. This calls for research that not only analyzes individual elements but 
also creates a contextualized view of the market environment, considering factors 
such as regulatory shifts, stakeholder interactions, and emerging technologies. By 
embracing this broader perspective, future research can better inform strategies for 
sustainable entrepreneurship and the successful integration of nature-based climate 
solutions in combating climate change.

Methodology 
This chapter provides a detailed presentation of the applied research methods to 
answer the research question of this thesis. It explains in detail how the desired re-
sults in form of a concise and comprehensive overview of both, the influencing factors 
as well as the identified opportunities and threats of the nature-based voluntary car-
bon market were achieved.  
This study used a qualitative strategy focused on understanding the interplay of dif-
ferent influencing factors in the context of identified market opportunities and barriers 
within the voluntary carbon market. The data collected in the interviews with selected 
companies was qualitative, focusing on descriptive information about the nature type 
specific carbon market. An inductive approach was used, which allows conclusions 
to be drawn from observations and pattern recognition, and on the basis of which a 
general summary was concluded about the nature-based carbon market.

To successfully enter the nature based VCM, a wholistic understanding of the market 
environment is one of the key success factors for every entrepreneur.  
The PESTEL analysis helps to structure the evaluation process of a complex and 
dynamic business environment. It is a business analysis method first mentioned in 
1968 by L. Fahey and V.K. Narayanan within their essay “"Macro-Environmental 
Analysis in Strategic Management" (Liam Fahey, 1986).  
The acronym PESTEL is formed by the dimensions considered: Political, economic, 
social, technological, environmental, and legal. For each of these dimensions, addi-
tional subtopics were defined, enabling a goal-oriented analysis of the literature to 
ensure the relevance of the content for entrepreneurs. The PESTEL dimensions and

Soil/Agriculture – Agreena  
Agreena is a Denmark-based Agriculture Tech startup founded in 2018. It recently 
completed its Series B funding and has so far secured funding of $ 77.6 million

The startup manufactures, verifies, and sells carbon credits derived from regenera-

the optimization of follow-on crops, the use of organic fertilizers, and the optimal

Agreena aims to provide a trustworthy solution for offsetting environmental impact.

by farmers and monitor the progress of implementing regenerative farm practices

carbon credits by following leading science and standards: The quantification of GHG

In the next step, a multidimensional analysis is conducted to identify differences and

Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA) is a case-based approach to investigate sit-

2017).  
The aim is to validate and enhance research findings by comparing literature-based

real-world perspectives. Additionally, analyzing differences and similarities among 
nature types provides a comprehensive understanding of variations within the na-
ture-based VCM, ensuring reliable conclusions and recommendations. 
All insights from the literature analysis and expert interviews are transferred and

Additionally, the analysis is structured by nature type, creating a vertical structure

Forests 
Forests – Political  
The literature and the expert interview recognize the importance of international 
frameworks for addressing climate change and promoting forest carbon offset pro-
jects. The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) 
plays a significant role by providing guidelines for methodologies, safeguards, imple-
mentation, and financing mechanisms (United Nations Climate Change, 2023). The 
United Nations Strategic Plan for Forests (UNSPF) is a global framework for sustain-
able forest action (Nakamura, 2019). Country-specific programs and incentives, like 
the Forest Legacy Program (FLP) in the United States (Forest Service, 2023), the 
International Climate and Forest Initiative (NICFI) in Norway (Ministry of Climate and 
Environment Norway, 2022), and the National Forest Climate Fund in Germany 
(Federal Ministry of Food and Agriculture, 2021), are mentioned in the literature. The 
interview emphasizes the variable stages of engagement with governments in differ-
ent countries, particularly in the voluntary carbon market space. Despite differences, 
both sources underscore the importance of international cooperation and tailored 
approaches when dealing with forest carbon offset projects in the political market 
environment. 
Forests – Economic 
Both the literature and the expert interview recognize the economic significance of 
forest carbon offset projects (European Commission, 2023), with revenue from car-
bon credits acting as a critical incentive for forest landowners (Sharma S., 2022). 
The literature emphasizes their cost-effectiveness (Grafton et al., 2021), though prof-
itability can vary based on sequestration rates (Nabuurs et al., 2007) and disturbance 
risks (Haya et al., 2023). In contrast, the interview highlights the higher risk associ-
ated with forestry projects, affecting their attractiveness to investors and leading to 
increased scrutiny on additionality and permanence. Market fluctuations are evident, 
with an initial uptick in interest post-COP in Glasgow and a decline caused by a con-
troversial newspaper article. This combination of insights offers a comprehensive

stakeholders effectively in these projects (Favretto N., 2020).

4.4.1 
Threats/Barriers  
Reputational Concerns of investors and buyers 
Climate-driven motivations aside, investors and buyers are attracted to voluntary car-
bon offset projects for economic reasons (as discussed in Chapter X), which are 
closely tied to public perception. Apprehensions about potential greenwashing lead 
to hesitancy and increased scrutiny of carbon offset projects. A telling example is the 
recent article guarding carbon credits, leading to a drop in demand for forest carbon 
credit, resulting in fewer project initiations. Additionally, a lack of understanding of 
the actual carbon removal mechanisms and awareness of complex ecosystem func-
tionalities contributes to reputational risks. Forest Carbon and Wetlands experts em-
phasize the importance of clear communication in accessible language to persuade 
investors and demonstrate environmental engagement to the public. 
Integrity and long-term validity of carbon credits 
Another reason for the need for more confidence from the demand side is the uncer-
tainty surrounding the integrity and long-term validity of purchased credits. Experts 
from Agreena in Soil/Agriculture attribute this to the need for more well-defined qual-
ity indicators for carbon credits (e.g., additionality, permanence) and the ability to 
measure them reliably. Moreover, the effectiveness of carbon sequestration in natu-
ral ecosystems can be influenced by climate change, natural disasters, and land-use 
changes. 
Policy Frameworks and Regulatory Development 
Uncertainties surrounding changes in policy frameworks and regulatory develop-
ments also contribute to hesitancy among buyers. For instance, the potential expan-
sion of the compliance market to include further industries under binding reduction 
targets could render previous voluntary efforts by carbon credit buyers obsolete. Po-
litical developments are closely monitored, and experts view them as a potential 
threat to the market. The demand side desires a clear signal from international poli-
tics affirming the reliability and future-proof nature of voluntary carbon credits to con-
tribute to the environment. 
==PAGE:46==
39 
 
Future entrepreneurs entering the nature-based Voluntary Carbon Market (VCM) 
must know the potential barriers and threats that may impact their projects. The rep-
utational concerns among investors and buyers call for entrepreneurs to prioritize 
clear communication and transparency in their projects. Addressing the need for 
more understanding of carbon removal mechanisms requires entrepreneurs to edu-
cate stakeholders and promote awareness of the environmental benefits of their ini-
tiatives. 
Entrepreneurs must develop robust measurement methods and quality indicators for 
their carbon credits to overcome credit integrity and long-term validity uncertainties. 
This will instill confidence in buyers and investors, increasing credit demand. More-
over, entrepreneurs should closely monitor and engage with policy frameworks and 
regulatory developments to adapt their strategies accordingly. 
While these barriers may seem daunting, they also represent opportunities for entre-
preneurs to differentiate themselves in the market. Entrepreneurs can gain a com-
petitive edge by building trust through transparent practices, offering reliable meas-
urement methods, and actively engaging with policymakers. Additionally, as the de-
mand for carbon offset projects grows, entrepreneurs who can effectively address 
these barriers will be well-positioned to capitalize on the increasing interest in nature-
based solutions for carbon sequestration. Ultimately, by navigating these challenges, 
entrepreneurs can pave the way for a successful and impactful entry into the nature-
based VCM.

such as blockchain and cryptocurrency could enhance accountability and reliability,

tration in natural ecosystems. By leveraging remote sensing, satellite imagery, and

to local communities and stakeholders. In line with expert insights from Forest Car-
bon and Wetlands, incorporating social standards, such as the Climate, Community, 
and Biodiversity standards, into nature-based offset projects has been demonstrated 
to elevate the value of carbon credits, presenting an additional incentive for investors

nature-based VCM initiatives.

tary carbon market into a broader environmental service market. Such a transfor-
mation necessitates clear definitions, guidelines, and technological measurement 
options to address concerns of transparency and complexity. By embracing this shift 
towards a more comprehensive approach, the market can enhance its environmental 
impact and lay the groundwork for a more sustainable and socially responsible fu-
ture. 
Advancements in technology within the VCM offer transparency and accuracy, pre-
senting opportunities for entrepreneurs to build stakeholder trust. The growing de-
mand for socially responsible investments creates a market for nature-based projects

entious consumer base. As the VCM evolves into a broader environmental service
