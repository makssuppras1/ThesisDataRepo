Abstract 
Corynebacterium glutamicum is an aerobic microorganism frequently exposed to conditions 
triggering oxidative stress. This exposure entails the intracellular accumulation of Reactive 
Oxygen Species (ROS), known to cause damage to vital cell components, as DNA. To date, 
the interconnection of oxidative stress and DNA damage in Corynebacteria, and the subsequent 
impact on cell viability, have not been addressed. To what is more, it is noteworthy that 
C. glutamicum possesses mycothiol as its main non-enzymatic antioxidant to avoid elevated 
ROS concentrations. Thus, for the purpose of this study, a mycothiol deficient mutant strain 
was employed. To address the aforementioned gap in research, C. glutamicum WT and a 
mycothiol deficient mutant strain were equipped with genetically encoded biosensors reporting 
oxidative stress and DNA damage. Specifically, a transcriptional-based biosensor based on the 
promoter region recA was introduced to genetic constructs already expressing the protein-based 
oxidative stress biosensor Mrx1-roGFP2. These strains were additionally equipped with 
plasmid pJC1_recA_E2 Crimson to report induction of DNA damage successfully. During in 
vivo characterization, the new C. glutamicum reporter strains were exposed to stresses in plate 
reader experiments, causing alterations in the 380/470 nm excitation ratio of the Mrx1-roGFP2 
biosensor. Moreover, increased transcriptional activity of the promoter recA was observed 
when C. glutamicum cultures were exposed to the alkylating agent methyl methanesulphonate 
(MMS), known to induce DNA damage. The increased DNA damage levels were quantified 
by the fluorescence intensity of E2 Crimson protein at 650 nm, while subsequent measurements 
of the ratiometric intensity of Mrx1-roGFP2 at 380/470 nm indicated higher intracellular ROS 
concentrations as a result of DNA damage. Treatment of C. glutamicum shake flask cultures 
with hypochlorite indicated a stronger oxidative shift for the MSH-deficient mutant strain, 
paired with elevated DNA damage levels. This finding implies that higher intracellular ROS 
levels are linked with an equivalent impact on DNA damage. Furthermore, viability 
measurements introduced with Impedance Flow Cytometry (IFC) revealed an additional 
correlation of oxidative stress and DNA damage with gradual cell death. This was indicated as 
five hours after the application of oxidative stress, 41% of the MSH-deficient cells were not 
viable compared to 19% for the wild-type strain.  
This study reveals that there is an interconnection between oxidative stress and DNA damage 
in C. glutamicum. The results obtained by these two fluorescent proteins' signals showed that 
increased concentrations of ROS proportionally induced DNA damage levels and vice versa.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Stresses in industrial-scale fermentation  
It is widely known that exposure to fluctuating environmental conditions and ineffective 
mixing in industrial-scale fermentation triggers gradient formation, which causes stresses to 
the cultivated microorganism. From a physiological point of view, these fluctuations by 
inefficient mixing lead to alterations of the fermentation environment of the cell and cause 
multiple gene responses expressed with time variability (Nadal-Rey et al., 2021). In general, 
gradients in bioreactors can cause population heterogeneity and affect cells at different levels 
such as the genome, transcriptome, metabolome, and proteome (Lencastre Fernandes et al., 
2011; Oldiges & Takors, 2005). Furthermore, these alterations have been proven to 
significantly influence process parameters as productivity and the yield on the substrate. 
Therefore their understanding is critical from both an academic and industrial point of view 
(Crater & Lievense, 2018). 
1.1.1 Origin of ROS  
Oxidative stress is considered a significant factor leading to the development of cancer and 
neurodegenerative diseases, like Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s diseases (Benzi & Moretti, 
1995; Emerit, Edeas, & Bricaire, 2004). Malfunction of the respiratory chain located at 
eukaryotic mitochondria causes the formation of reactive oxygen species (Fridovich, 1978). In 
normal conditions, the respiratory chain balances reactive oxygen species (ROS) inside the 
cells. Generation of ROS is triggered during respiration of the microorganism by incomplete 
stepwise reduction of molecular oxygen (Imlay, 2019).  ROS are oxidants and include 
superoxide anions (O2-), peroxy radicals (ROO•), hydroxyl radicals (OH•), alkoxy radicals 
(RO•), and hydrogen peroxide (H2O2). Furthermore, other events capable of causing oxidative 
stress are redox reactions in the microorganism’s environment, autoxidation of enzymes, and 
production of hydrogen peroxide from competing microorganisms or macrophages (Haugland 
et al., 2012; Imlay, 2008a). An important field of study for ROS generation mimicking actual 
production conditions during aerobic industrial fermentations is bioreactor cultivations at 
different aeration levels. ROS generation is proportionally dependent on the collision 
frequency between redox enzymes and oxygen. During aerobic bioreactor fermentations, cells 
are usually exposed to oxygen concentrations above air saturation, triggering the formation of

ROS (Imlay, 2003, 2008b; Korshunov & Imlay, 2006; Turrens, 2003; Turrens, Freeman, & 
Crapo, 1982). 
1.1.2 Interconnection of oxidative stress and DNA damage 
As mentioned, ROS, including superoxide anions (O2-), hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), and 
hydroxyl radicals (OH•), can cause several types of intracellular damage in compartments such 
as proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids. Notably, it has been reported that hydroxyl radicals (OH•) 
tend to carbonylate proteins, break down DNA, and peroxidate lipids (Dwyer, Collins, & 
Walker, 2015). Different types of ROS are precursors for the oxidation of dCTP and dGTP 
pools, resulting in base misincorporation of DNA and subsequent double-stranded breaks 
(DSBs) via disruption of repair intermediates (Fan et al., 2018; Foti, Devadoss, Winkler, 
Collins, & Walker, 2012; Gruber & Walker, 2018). Damages conducted by ROS are regarded 
as secondary compared to the primary stress. However, multiple circles of ROS accumulation 
could be arisen, making ROS an overwhelming self-amplifying process which could be the 
fatal step for the bacterial response to the primary stress. Nevertheless, it is still not determined 
whether endogenous ROS are adequate to cause lethal damage since consequences from the 
primary stressor the following ROS accumulation have not been distinguished (Imlay, 2015a). 
Hong et al. reported that while E. coli cells were exposed to antimicrobial stress, and 
afterwards, the stressor was detached, accumulation of ROS and cell death were still occurring 
(Hong, Zeng, Wang, Drlica, & Zhao, 2019). The same study revealed that inhibition of ROS 
accumulation by exogenous catalase support delayed significantly or completely inhibited 
post-stress death (Hong et al., 2019). 
1.1.3 DNA damage  
Several cellular stress conditions could cause damage to DNA molecules of an organism as the 
presence of toxic metabolites in the cellular metabolism, exposure to UV light, environmental 
ionizing radiation, chemical exposure, or replications errors (Figure 1)(Christmann, Tomicic, 
Roos, & Kaina, 2003; Roos & Kaina, 2006). DNA damage response is activated initially with 
DNA double-strand breaks (DSBs) and single-strand breaks (SSBs) (Christmann et al., 2003). 
DNA is more susceptible to stresses or nucleases when it is in the form of two single-stranded 
strands (e.g. transcription, replication), therefore single-strand breaks are favourably generated 
upon stress conditions (Richard, Bolderson, & Khanna, 2009).

1.2 Corynebacterium glutamicum - A model organism and amino acid 
producer 
The studied microorganism is the Gram-positive, rod-shaped, aerobically living soil bacterium, 
Corynebacterium glutamicum, which was primarily isolated as an L-glutamate-excreting 
microorganism by Kinoshita and Udaka in 1957 (Kinoshita, Udaka, & Shimono, 1957). This 
non-pathogenic, non-sporulating, and non-motile bacterium is a member of the 
Corynebacterianeae family, which includes Corynebacteria, Mycobacteria, Rhodococci, 
Nocardia, and phylogenetically related bacteria that belong to the class of Actinobacteria 
(Stackebrandt, Rainey, & Ward-Rainey, 1997; Ventura et al., 2007; Zhi, Li, & Stackebrandt, 
2009). Corynebacterianeae includes multiple pathogenic species like Mycobacterium 
tuberculosis, Mycobacterium leprae, and Corynebacterium diphtheriae, causing tuberculosis, 
leprosy, and diphtheria diseases, respectively (Smith, 2003; Stackebrandt et al., 1997). 
Specifically, tuberculosis was responsible for 1.4 deaths in 2019, constituting the most 
infectious disease worldwide (WHO, 2019).

In contrast with other Gram-positive bacteria, Corynebacterianeae have a unique cell wall 
composition, which besides the plasma membrane, involves a mycolate layer that functions as 
an extra permeability barrier similar to the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria (Kieser 
& Rubin, 2014; Marrakchi, Lanéelle, & Daffé, 2014; Puech, Bayan, Salim, Leblon, & Daffé, 
2000; Zuber et al., 2008). This mycolate layer is highly hydrophobic and vital for the 
organism’s viability and protection against toxicity (Bhatt, Kremer, Dai, Sacchettini, & Jacobs, 
2005; Brown et al., 2007). Furthermore, another characteristic of Corynebacterianeae suborder 
is their high guanine (G) and cytosine (C) content into their genomic DNA. Thus, 
C. glutamicum serves as a model organism due to the structural similarities with pathogenic 
bacteria. In addition, its genome has been completely sequenced, genetic manipulations are 
performed efficiently, it is generally recognized as safe, and it proliferates on various carbon 
sources (Ikeda & Nakagawa, 2003; Kirchner & Tauch, 2003; Unthan et al., 2014).  
Apart from its importance as a model organism, C. glutamicum is widely used industrially for 
the large-scale production of amino acids, such as L-glutamate used as a flavor enhancer in the 
food industry, and L-lysine employed as a feed additive (Becker, Gießelmann, Hoffmann, & 
Wittmann, 2016; Eggeling & Bott, 2015; Heider & Wendisch, 2015; Wendisch, Jorge, Pérez-
García, & Sgobba, 2016; Yang & Yang, 2017). Nowadays, around 5 million tons of amino 
acids are produced annually from C. glutamicum producer strains (Wendisch et al., 2016). In 
addition, other amino acids produced by C. glutamicum are L-valine, L-alanine, L-glutamine, 
L-leucine, and L-isoleucine, while compound production is steadily increasing, highlighting 
the utmost importance of this bacterium in biotechnological production (Hermann, 2003). 
It has been proven that various environmental and industrial-scale stress conditions are causing 
oxidative stress to microorganisms. C. glutamicum is cultivated in large fermentation vessels 
where besides nutrient sources, parameters such as oxygen concentration and pH have to be 
monitored (Hermann, 2003; Ikeda & Nakagawa, 2003). Oxygen concentration in fermentation 
vessels should be selected to enhance production and minimize oxidative stress formation 
which may lead to DNA damage of the microorganism. Hitherto, the interconnection between 
oxidative stress and DNA damage and the subsequent effect on cell viability in C. glutamicum 
has not been investigated.

1.3 Defensive mechanisms of C. glutamicum against oxidative stress 
C. glutamicum is a microorganism that grows aerobically in its natural environment and during 
industrial fermentation, is being exposed to reactive oxygen species (ROS). Generation of ROS 
is triggered during respiration of the microorganism by incomplete stepwise reduction of 
molecular oxygen (Imlay, 2015b, 2019). An excess in the amount of the generated ROS could 
lead to damage of vital cell components, like proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids (Ezraty, 
Gennaris, Barras, & Collet, 2017; Imlay, 2013). Therefore, it is of significant importance to 
hold multiple defense systems in order to combat oxidative stress. The deleterious effect of 
ROS on biomolecules is prohibited by the activity of enzymatic and non-enzymatic antioxidant 
defense systems, which are presented in Figure 2, and their functions are analyzed in the 
following sections.  
 
Figure 2: Representation of enzymatic and non-enzymatic antioxidants acting against ROS 
production in C. glutamicum. 
1.3.1 Enzymatic antioxidant systems 
C. glutamicum possesses enzymatic detoxification systems regulated by the superoxide 
dismutase (SOD), the peroxidase, and the catalase. These systems can react and handle 
extracellular concentrations upon 1M (Lange, 2012). Specifically, superoxide catalyzes the 
conversion of superoxide anion to hydrogen peroxide (2 O2- + 2 H+ → H2O2 + O2), hydrogen

peroxide is reduced to water and oxygen by catalase (2 H2O2 → H2O + O2), while peroxidase 
catalyzes the oxidation of hydrogen peroxide to water (RH2 + H2O2 → R + 2 H2O) (Park et al., 
2005).   
1.3.2 LMW thiols as non-enzymatic antioxidant systems 
Low molecular weight (LMW) thiols are operating as non-enzymatic antioxidant defense 
systems in bacteria and eukaryotes in order to preserve a reducing environment inside the 
cytoplasm (Van Laer, Hamilton, & Messens, 2013). Specifically, eukaryotes and most Gram-
negative bacteria are utilizing glutathione (GSH) as their main LMW thiol, while Gram-
positive phylum Actinobacteria, as Mycobacterium smegmatis and C. glutamicum, are using 
the GSH surrogate mycothiol (MSH), which is a thiol-cofactor for multiple redox enzymes 
(Imber, Pietrzyk-Brzezinska, & Antelmann, 2019; Newton, Buchmeier, & Fahey, 2008; Reyes 
et al., 2018; Tung, Linzner, Van Loi, & Antelmann, 2018). Mycothiol operates like glutathione 
surrogate in the detoxification of ROS and further thiol-reave compounds in order retain the 
reduced state of the cytoplasm (Loi, Rossius, & Antelmann, 2015; Newton et al., 2008; Reyes 
et al., 2018; Tung et al., 2018).  
Under oxidative stress, MSH is oxidized to mycothiol disulfide (MSSM), whereas the action 
of NADPH- dependent mycothiol disulfide reductase (Mtr) increases the reduced MSH redox 
potential, as it catalyzes the reduction of MSSM back to MSH (Jothivasan & Hamilton, 2008; 
Kumar, Nartey, Shin, Manimekalai, & Grüber, 2017). It has been shown that overexpression 
of Mtr in C. glutamicum increases stress tolerance and the MSH/MSSM ratio upon exposure 
to ROS, alkylating agents, and antibiotics (Si et al., 2016). Furthermore, upon treatment of 
C. glutamicum with hypochlorous acid (HOCl), 25 S-mycothiolated proteins have been 
acknowledged to be inhibited by S-mycothiolation, including peroxiredoxins (Mpx, Tpx) and 
methionine sulfoxide reductases (MsrA, MsrB) (Chi et al., 2014; Pedre et al., 2015; Tossounian 
et al., 2015). Consequently, this fact indicates that MSH is an essential thiol for the protection 
of the microorganism upon oxidative stress, as its absence triggers oxidative stress sensitivity, 
which leads to impaired growth behavior and potential loss of cell viability (Chi et al., 2014; 
Liu et al., 2016; Tung et al., 2019).

1.4 Previous studies of Corynebacterium glutamicum under oxidative stress 
Various studies in the past had investigated the physiological role of antioxidants in 
C. glutamicum; however, almost all of them solely used artificial oxidants for ROS induction 
(Chi et al., 2014; Liu et al., 2016; Pedre et al., 2015; Tung et al., 2019; van der Heijden et al., 
2016). It has been shown that C. glutamicum, as an organism with an aerobic lifestyle, is highly 
robust when oscillations of high and low oxygen concentration occur in bioreactors (Käß et al., 
2014). The importance of the non-enzymatic antioxidant Mycothiol for the robustness of 
C. glutamicum in stirred bioreactors during aerobic fermentation was recently shown by 
Hartmann et al. (Hartmann, Clermont, Tung, Antelmann, & Seibold, 2020). In this study, 
C. glutamicum WT and the MSH deficient C. glutamicum strain, which expressed the Mrx1-
roGFP2 oxidative stress biosensor, were cultivated at elevated oxygen levels (pO2) in stirred 
bioreactors. The mycothiol deficient strain showed a growth defect when exposed to pO2 levels 
above 20% (Hartmann et al., 2020). The strong oxidative shift revealed by the Mrx1-roGFP2 
biosensor for pO2 levels above 20% indicated the essentiality of mycothiol for robustness 
maintenance of C. glutamicum during aerobic fermentation.  
1.5 Bacterial cell response to DNA damage 
In general, the SOS repair pathway in bacteria is an inducible gene regulatory network activated 
when increased DNA damage levels appear inside the cells (Erill, Campoy, & Barbé, 2007; 
Schröder & Tauch, 2010). During bacterial growth conditions, SOS genes are regulated 
negatively by the action of LexA repressor protein (Kreuzer, 2013; Montelone, 2006). This 
protein is bound to the promoter region of SOS regulon genes and is responsible for balancing 
their expression limits. When DNA damage is induced, RecA protein is activated as a 
coprotease since it forms a filament of RecA (RecA*) on single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) and 
catalyzes the self-cleavage reaction of the LexA repressor (Aertsen & Michiels, 2006; Erill et 
al., 2007; Kreuzer, 2013; Montelone, 2006). Consequently, as shown in Figure 3, this liberates 
the SOS-regulated genes involved in DNA repair mechanisms and increases their expression 
levels. Gene expression levels of C. glutamicum (ATCC 13032) and LexA deficient 
C. glutamicum NJ2114 were compared with a combination of bioinformatic patterns and the 
genome-wide expression data. It was demonstrated that LexA controls the expression of 48 
SOS genes corresponding to 36 transcriptional units (Jochmann et al., 2009).  In this study, 
C. glutamicum cultures were cultivated in the presence of mitomycin C, a chemical agent

1.6 Genetically encoded biosensors 
Through the years, variable terminologies have been employed to describe biosensors (Pickup, 
Hussain, Evans, Rolinski, & Birch, 2005). In general, biosensors could be characterized as 
analytical devices capable of detecting a particular analyte in samples with a wide range of 
concentrations (Pancrazio, Whelan, Borkholder, Ma, & Stenger, 1999). They were introduced 
by Clark and Lyons during the 1960s and have gained vast research interest until today due to 
the various biosensor types which are applicable in a wide range of industrial areas, as the food 
industry, medical science, clinical sector (Clark & Lyons, 1962; Mehrotra, 2016). Genetically 
encoded biosensors have been developed by agglomerates of optical transducers embodied 
with fluorescence active proteins, which enable the intracellular detection of a produced 
metabolite and its conversion to a measurable output (Lippincott-Schwartz & Patterson, 2003). 
These fluorescent protein-based biosensors are easily manipulated and inserted into living 
organisms. At the same time, they can monitor real-time molecular dynamics in time and space, 
which is of major importance for the proper functioning and regulation of complex processes 
at the cellular level.

1.6.1 Types of genetically encoded biosensors 
Two different types of genetically encoded biosensors are widely used: the fluorescent-
reporter-proteins (FRPs) and the transcription factor-based biosensors (TFBs). FRPs interact 
in real-time to perturbations of intracellular parameters and metabolites like ATP, NADPH or 
pH (Botman, van Heerden, & Teusink, 2020; Deng et al., 2021; Goldbeck, Eck, & Seibold, 
2018; Reyes-Fernández & Schuldiner, 2020). Consequently, this allows the fast monitoring of 
alterations that occur to internal parameters of multiple microbial strains without being affected 
by incidents that happened in the past (Franz Hartmann, Weiss, Shen, Smahajcsik, & Seibold, 
2021). Fluorescent reporter proteins were effectively introduced for the determination of 
oxidation states and intracellular metabolite levels upon environmentally applied alterations. 
On the other hand, TFBs are easily engineered and therefore are the most widely used 
biosensors. TFBs consist of an effector, which is an activator or repressor protein and regulates 
the transcriptional activity of a particular promoter. When an event occurs, the transcription 
factor effector complex transduces a response via activation or repression of a downstream 
reporter gene, outputted by a fluorescent protein's intensity. In contrast with FRPs, real-time 
monitoring of alterations is constrained, as TFBs signal is affected by transcription, translation, 
degradation, or maturation of the fluorescent protein. Nevertheless, TFBs offer the advantage 
of a stable signal upon perturbations of environmental conditions and make them a powerful 
tool for high throughput screening approaches for the selection and optimization of biocatalysts 
(Eggeling, Bott, & Marienhagen, 2015; Schallmey, Frunzke, Eggeling, & Marienhagen, 2014). 
A high throughput technique widely applied with genetically encoded biosensors is 
fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS). 
1.6.2 Monitoring redox-dynamics in living cells with redox-sensitive fluorescent 
proteins. 
During the last two decades, fluorescent protein-based redox sensors have been widely applied 
for research in the field of redox biology. Nowadays, genetically encoded probes inside specific 
subcellular domains are considered well established and capable of monitoring redox dynamics 
in real-time (Ma et al., 2011; Saitoh et al., 1998). The first reported fluorescent protein-based 
redox biosensor was reported by Winther et al. in 2001, where insertion of two cysteine 
residues to the β-barrel surface of a yellow fluorescent protein (YFP) (Porqué, Baldesten, & 
Reichard, 1970; Sido et al., 2005). Upon oxidation of the biosensor probe, a reversible disulfide 
bond was formed, which led to a structural modification of the protein domain and a 50% 
fluorescence decrease in contrast to its reduced state. Similarly, cysteine disulfide bridges were

introduced to the surface of green fluorescent proteins (GFPs), which led to the formation of a 
series of redox-sensitive GFPs called roGFPs (Gallegos et al., 1996; Lu et al., 2013; Masutani, 
Ueda, & Yodoi, 2005). Opposite to rxYFP, roGFPs consist of both protonated and deprotonated 
fluorescent chromophores and are dual-excitation ratiometric probes. Remarkably, the roGFP2 
biosensor responds to two excitation maxima at 405 nm and 488 nm, which variate upon

1.7 Biomass and viability estimation methods 
Biomass concentration is regarded as the most substantial variable in cultivations, and through 
the years variable techniques have been applied for in situ and on-line biomass measurements 
(Olsson & Nielsen, 1997). Some methods related to biomass measurements are the plate count 
method (CFU), optical density (OD), flow cytometry by live-dead staining, cell dry weight 
(CDW) or monitoring of the metabolic activity (e. g. by estimation of substrate or oxygen 
consumption). The plate count method involves the preparation of dilution series of bacteria in 
a sterilised environment to count colony forming units (CFU) on an agar plate supplemented 
with the appropriate medium. It is considered an easy to use and relatively cheap method; 
however, it is labour intensive and time-consuming in the case of multiple samples. In addition, 
other technologies applied for bacteria quantification as a replacement of the plate method, 
such as fluorescent-based techniques (FACS) or polymerase-based (qPCR), are characterized 
as well labour intensive and require sample processing with expensive reagents (Kant et al., 
2018). 
An arising cell viability method for single-cell analysis and particle estimation, which is 
established in this work, is Impedance Flow Cytometry (IFC). It has been applied successfully 
in various studies related to a broad range of cell types, i.e. tumor cells (Zhao et al., 2014), 
yeast cells (Opitz et al., 2019), plant spores (Canonge, Philippot, Leblanc, Potin, & Bodin, 
2020), Red Blood Cells (RBCs) (Zheng, Shojaei-Baghini, Azad, Wang, & Sun, 2012), and 
bacteria detection in drinking water (Clausen et al., 2018). IFC enumerates a plethora of 
advantages compared to other cell quantification methods, as it is fast, non-invasive, sensitive 
and label-free. Furthermore, IFC detects every single cell or particle inside a sample 
independently of the viability state or the bacterial species. This comes in contrast with the 
plate count method where non-culturable bacteria with an intact membrane and metabolically

active (however not able to divide) are not detectable (Byrd, Xu, & Colwell, 1991; Fakruddin, 
Mannan, & Andrews, 2013; Kikkeri, Breazeal, Ren, Pruden, & Agah, 2018; Nyström, 2001). 
Consequently, IFC displays results of bacterial populations more accurately by considering 
cells which are underestimated by the traditional plate method. 
For this work, the BactoBox device from SBT Instruments (Herlev, Copenhagen), which uses 
the principles of impedance flow cytometry through microfluidic sensors, was used. The core 
technology is based on a microfluidic flow cell that uses integrated electrodes to detect and 
assess cells and particles in a diameter range of 0.5-5 μm. Specifically, a distinct change in 
current magnitude is observed when an intact cell flows between the electrodes of the 
microfluidic flow cell, and an event is detected. This alteration in magnitude takes place due to 
the cell’s intact membrane and cytoplasm and differs from the respective change caused by 
mere particles not possessing an intact membrane. Therefore, an accurate count of viable cells 
and mere particles is obtained in less than 3 minutes.

2. Scope of the work 
Microbes with an aerobic lifestyle, as C. glutamicum, experience unpredictable conditions 
since they are often exposed to various stresses like oxidative stress or pH stress. These 
parameters affect significantly cellular physiology and, therefore can trigger complex gene 
responses in their effort to adapt to the updated conditions. Apart from stress conditions that 
bacteria encounter in their natural environment, they also experience stresses during industrial-
scale fermentation as part of ineffective mixing that induces gradient formation in bioreactor 
tanks. The formation of Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS) has been shown to induce DNA 
damage and inevitable damage of vital cell components. 
It has been recently reported that oxidative stress arises at standard conditions and when 
C. glutamicum is exposed to escalating aeration profiles in lab-scale bioreactors. The scope of 
this work is to explore further the influence of oxidative stress on the cellular responses of the 
model organism C. glutamicum and analyze the stress responses as a consequence of ROS 
formation. To achieve this, the protein-based genetically encoded oxidative stress biosensor 
Mrx1-roGFP2 is linked with a transcriptional-based biosensor based on the promoter region 
recA, which is able to report induction of the DNA damage stress response. These new 
constructs will be applied to C. glutamicum WT and the MSH-deficient mutant (C. glutamicum 
ΔmshC). The latter strain is more susceptible to ROS formation due to the absence of the 
mycothiol (MSH), which is the main low molecular weight thiol and non-enzymatic 
antioxidant in C. glutamicum. Consequently, this combination will allow the monitoring of 
oxidative stress and its affection to cell physiology when C. glutamicum cultivations are 
exposed to various concentrations of chemicals and antibiotics in shake flask cultivations.

3.4.1 Strain maintenance 
All strains used and constructed in this work were stored as glycerol stocks at -80o C. Regarding 
glycerol stock preparation, 5 mL of each strain were cultivated overnight (30o C, 250 rpm) in 
glass tubes with the addition of required antibiotics. During the following morning, 400 μL 
from each overnight cell suspension were mixed with 600 μL of 50% glycerol (final glycerol 
concentration: 30%) in prepared sterile 1.5 mL tubes and were stored at -80o C.

phase. Subsequently, the culture was divided into two subcultures, cells were harvested via 
centrifugation (4000 rpm, 4o C, 10 minutes) and washed twice with 0.9% NaCl, while the one 
subculture was inactivated by 0.5% isopropanol treatment (10 minutes, RT) which led to 100% 
dead cells. Thus, mixtures of different ratios of Live/Dead cells were prepared, including 100% 
dead and 100% alive cells, and three different cell viability methods (see section 3.8) were used 
to determine the viable percentage of the cells for each sample.  
3.5 Preparation of crude cell extracts  
For the preparation of crude cell extracts suitable for in vitro fluorescence measurements, the 
obtained frozen pellets of E. coli strains (section 3.4.1) expressing the sensor proteins were 
washed twice in potassium phosphate buffer (100 mM, pH 7.0) and finally resuspended in 3 
mL of the same buffer solution. Next, microtubes (final volume 1.5 mL) were prepared by the 
addition of 200 μL of glass beads (0.1-0.25 mm) and 1.3 mL of the resuspended pellets, 
followed by cell disruption performed by a Ribolyzer (Precellys TM Control Device, Bertin 
Technologies) at 6000rpm. This step was repeated 3 times for 30 seconds each time. Between 
disruption and during all extraction steps, the tubes were kept on ice. After disruption, cell 
debris and glass beads were removed via centrifugation (12000 rpm, 20 minutes, 4o C), and 
cell lysate was transferred in new tubes.

3.6. Molecular biology methods 
To generate C. glutamicum strains harboring both oxidative stress and DNA damage biosensor, 
a series of C. glutamicum strains (C. glutamicum WT_Mrx1-roGFP2; C. glutamicum 
ΔmshC_Mrx1-roGFP2) were equipped with the plasmid pJC1_recA_E2 Crimson additionally 
to their genetic construct, to report induction of the DNA-damage response. This procedure 
required three stages: the preparation of electrocompetent cells, the transformation of plasmids, 
and the validation of transformation through PCR.  
3.6.1 Plasmid DNA purification  
The plasmids used for the generation of C. glutamicum reporter strains were obtained by strains 
E. coli DH5α pJC1_recA_E2 Crimson and E. coli DH5α pJC1. Overnight cultures of these 
strains were prepared with a single colony and 5 mL LB medium (with or without antibiotics, 
37o C, 200 rpm). Subsequently, plasmid purification was conducted using the commercially 
available GenElute Plasmid Miniprep Kit (Sigma Aldrich). The procedure was performed 
following kit instructions and by using sterile Milli-Q water as an elution buffer. 
The DNA concentration in purified plasmids was measured by NanoDrop Lite 
Spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific) with the selection of dsDNA measurement. Initially, 
blank measurements were conducted with 1 μL sterile Milli-Q water, followed by 
measurements of 1 μL obtained by the sample. A rough indication of purity was taken, with 
final sample concentrations of 123 ng/μL for E. coli DH5α pJC1_recA_E2 Crimson and 93 
ng/μL for E. coli DH5α pJC1. 
3.6.2 Preparation of electrocompetent C. glutamicum cells 
Regarding the preparation of electrocompetent C. glutamicum cells, single colonies of 
C. glutamicum WT_Mrx1-roGFP2 and C. glutamicum ΔmshC_Mrx1-roGFP2 were inoculated 
with 5 mL of BHIS medium and incubated overnight (30o C, 200 rpm) until the cells reached 
their late stationary phase. Then, 3 mL from each overnight culture were transferred in shake 
flasks supplemented with 50 mL fresh BHIS medium and cultivated (30o C, 200 rpm) upon 
their early exponential growth for 3 hours until an OD600 of 1.75 was achieved. Because of the 
cruciality of the harvesting procedure, solutions (TG buffer, 10% glycerol), consumables 
(Falcon tubes, Eppendorf tubes), and equipment (centrifuge) were pre-cooled during these 3 
hours of cultivation, and all steps were carried out on ice. Before harvesting, the shake flasks 
were cooled down in a water-ice-bath for 10 minutes, while the whole culture was transferred 
in pre-cooled 50 mL Falcon tubes. Then, cells were harvested via centrifugation (4500 rpm, 10

inverting tubes), followed by a second double washing step with 10 mL 10% glycerol. Finally, 
for cryopreservation, pellets were resuspended in 1 mL of 10% glycerol, and 100 μL aliquots 
were prepared and stored immediately at -80o C. 
3.6.3 Transformation of pJC1_recA_E2 Crimson and pJC1 plasmids in C. glutamicum 
For the transformation of plasmids, aliquots of competent cells (100 μL) were thawed on ice 
and 5 μL (containing approximately 450-600 ng) of each plasmid were added on each aliquot. 
After 10 minutes and while cells and plasmid were on the ice, C. glutamicum cells with the 
plasmid were transferred to ice-cold electroporation cuvettes, and the electroporation was 
conducted by a Micro PulserTM (Bio Rad Laboratories) at a voltage of 2.5 kV. Subsequently, 
addition of 900 μL of BHI medium was performed, whereas cells and plasmids supplemented 
with the medium were transferred back in a sterile 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube. Tubes were exposed 
to heat shock (46o C, 6 minutes), cooled down to 30o C on ice, and transferred to 4 mL BHI 
medium inside 15 mL falcon tubes. Regeneration was conducted while falcon tubes were 
incubated (30o C, 200 rpm) for one hour. Finally, 0.1 mL were used for plating on selection LB 
agar plates, and the remaining cells were concentrated by centrifugation (3500 rpm, 10 minutes, 
room temperature) and plated to enhance the possibility of obtaining colonies of the new 
strains. All plates were incubated at 30o C for two days and formed visible colonies verified by 
cPCR.

3.8 Cell viability methods 
3.8.1 Plate count method 
In this work, the plate count method was used to determine the CFU/mL of various samples. 
First, sterile cell resuspensions were diluted to OD600 values of 10-3, 10-4, or 10-5 and 100 μL 
were pipetted to a fresh agar plate with or without antibiotics. Subsequently, plating glass beads 
were added to the plates, and by moving the plate back and forth, cells were evenly and wholly 
spread over the agar surface. Glass beads were removed from the agar plates, and incubation 
of E. coli strains was performed for 24 hours at 37o C, while for C. glutamicum strains the 
incubation conditions were 48 hours at 30o C.  
Analysis and counting of formed colonies were conducted by Phenobooth+ Colony Counter 
(Singer Instruments), which has a closed chamber for imaging and analysis of colonies on agar 
plates. This fast and easy to operate device uses a scientific-grade camera that produces plate 
images of 23 megapixels, and afterwards, specific filters based on the size and circularity of 
colonies are applied for fast colony counting.   
3.8.2 Impedance Flow Cytometry (IFC) measurements 
Impedance Flow Cytometry (IFC) measurements were performed by using the BactoBox 
device (SBT Instruments, Herlev, Denmark). Cells from harvested cultures were diluted with 
sterile 5% 1 x PBS solution to an OD600 of 10-3 and a final sample volume of 3 mL. Samples 
were transferred to specially shaped vials compatible with the device, and the total amount of 
intact cells per milliliter (cells/mL) was detected in a period ranging from 30 seconds to 3 
minutes. 
3.8.3 LIVE/DEAD BacLight Bacterial Viability Kit 
The well-established cell viability method with the LIVE/DEAD BacLight Bacterial Viability 
Kit was applied via staining of the variable bacterial suspensions. The principle of this method

is based on the differential exclusion of dyes, where cells are stained red or green depending 
on their membrane integrity. Particularly, bacterial cells with a disrupted membrane emit red 
fluorescence, whereas cells with an intact membrane emit green fluorescence. For this method, 
inactivated cells treated with isopropanol were mixed in different proportions with untreated 
cells. 
Following the inactivation procedure of C. glutamicum WT cells with isopropanol treatment, 
a 1:1 solution of 3.34 mM SYTOTM 9 dye and 20 mM of propidium iodide dye was prepared 
following the instructions provided by the Kit. Next, 200 μL of different samples were 
transferred to 96-well black bottomed microplates in triplicates with the addition of 3 μL of the 
prepared mixture and were incubated for 15 minutes at room temperature and complete 
darkness. Subsequently, the 96-well microplate was relocated to the plate reader for 
fluorescence detection by setting an excitation scan at 470 nm and emission scans at 650 nm 
and 510 nm indicating the presence of dead and alive cells, respectively.

4. Results 
4.1 Validation of methods of analyzing the viability of non-growing cells 
The scope of this experimental series was the establishment of a suitable method capable of 
analyzing the viability of cells that have been treated with extracellular stressors. Two 
experimental series were conducted for the method determination. The first experiment 
described in this section concerns C. glutamicum cell inactivation through isopropanol 
treatment, whereas the latter includes penicillin G treatment in different concentrations during 
C. glutamicum microfermentation in BioLector systems. Different viability methods were used 
and compared in terms of accuracy, speed, and easiness in handling. 
4.1.1 Inactivation of C. glutamicum cells with isopropanol treatment 
C. glutamicum WT cells were grown in LB medium for 5 hours when the main culture was 
divided into two subcultures. Following cell harvesting and washing, one subculture was 
treated with 0.5% isopropanol (v/v) which led to a state considered to be composed of 100% 
dead cells. The second subculture was not treated with isopropanol and was regarded as a 100% 
alive reference. Samples of different proportions of Live/Dead cells were prepared, including 
100% dead and 100% alive cells. Different viability methods were conducted for the 
determination of the percentage of viable cells. Impedance Flow Cytometry (IFC) 
measurements were established with the BactoBox device and compared with the widely used 
cell viability method, LIVE/DEAD BacLight Bacterial Viability Kit for microscopy and 
quantitative assays (Thermo Fischer Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA), and the traditional plate 
count method. 
4.1.2 Establishment of Impedance Flow Cytometry (IFC) measurements via 
BactoBox device. 
IFC measurements were conducted with the BactoBox device from SBT Instruments. Different 
proportions of samples in terms of live/dead cells were prepared and diluted with 5% 1 x PBS 
solution to an OD600 of 10-3 (sample volume 3 mL). The total amount of intact cells was 
detected by the microfluidic flow cell in a period ranging from 30 seconds to 3 minutes. The 
results of cells/mL plotted against the known percentage of alive cells are presented in Figure 
4. As it could be observed, there is a linear relationship between the percentage of viable cells 
of the samples and the output of BactoBox (cells/mL).

4.1.4 BioLector experiment with penicillin G treatment 
C. glutamicum’s efficiency in producing and secreting glutamate is favored upon treatments 
leading to disturbance of its cell wall or membrane integrity. Particularly, notable treatments 
that trigger glutamate production involve antibiotics (Penicillin, Ethambutol), anaesthetics, or 
biotin limitation (Eggeling, Krumbach, & Sahm, 2001; Radmacher et al., 2005). Regarding 
antibiotic treatment, Penicillin G is used to inhibit transpeptidase activity, leading to cross-
linking of cell wall peptidoglycan and following glutamate secretion by C. glutamicum (Nara, 
Samejima, & Kinoshita, 1964; Walsh, 1989). Therefore, in the context of establishing viability 
measurements with IFC, a microfermentation of C. glutamicum WT was performed with the 
addition of penicillin G in different concentrations.

microfermentation systems

the results are depicted in Figure 7. Specifically, the growth of C. glutamicum WT was optimal

this threshold, C. glutamicum cells of both strains did not indicate growth. Therefore, samples

5. Discussion 
C. glutamicum, as a microorganism with an aerobic lifestyle, is often exposed to unpredictable 
environmental conditions leading to oxidative stress with the formation of reactive oxygen 
species (ROS). For this work, it was hypothesized that oxidative stress in C. glutamicum results 
in DNA damage with a subsequent effect in cell viability. The interconnection between 
oxidative stress, DNA damage, and the subsequent effect on cell viability in C. glutamicum 
have not been studied yet. To address this question, the protein-based genetically encoded 
oxidative stress biosensor Mrx1-roGFP2 was equipped with a transcriptional-based biosensor 
based on the promoter region recA and able to report induction of the DNA damage stress 
response. Furthermore, impedance flow cytometry (IFC) was utilized to determine the 
potentially lethal effects of ROS in cell viability. 
 
5.1 In vitro characterization reveals signal stability of the fluorescent 
proteins 
Before conducting physiological studies regarding the interconnection of oxidative stress and 
DNA damage in C. glutamicum, the fluorescence signal of protein cell extracts was analyzed 
through in vitro characterization. These experimental series were important for monitoring the 
response of fluorescence protein signals upon chemical treatment. Also, they determined 
whether their coexistence in one strain efficiently reported changes in oxidative stress and DNA 
damage. 
Initially, cell extracts of E. coli BL21(DE3)_(pEt11_Mrx1-roGFP2) were treated with different 
concentrations of DTT and hypochlorite, causing alterations in the 380/470 nm ratiometric 
intensity, as depicted in Figures 13a and 13b. This result indicated variability of the Mrx1-
roGFP2 oxidative stress biosensor upon reduction and oxidation of the probe and it established 
it as a reliable tool for investigating dynamic changes in the intracellular mycothiol redox 
potential in C. glutamicum. Furthermore, cell extracts of E. coli DH5α_pEkEx2_E2 Crimson 
were treated with various chemicals to examine possible E2 Crimson signal alteration at 650 
nm. The findings presented in Figure 16 report that the fluorescence intensity of E2 Crimson 
protein remained stable upon chemical treatment and allowed its usage for reporting DNA 
damage in cultures treated with a chemical stress factor. Finally, both E. coli strains' cell 
extracts were mixed to exclude the effects of E2 Crimson protein in the oxidation degree of the 
Mrx1-roGFP2 biosensor (Figure 15). All these individual findings were significant for

proceeding to the dual-reporter strains construction and ensure that fluorescent signal 
alterations during in vivo experiments will originate from physiological effects of stress applied 
to the microorganism. 
 
5.2 Absence of mycothiol in C. glutamicum lead to high intracellular ROS 
concentrations  
Mycothiol (MSH) is a thiol-cofactor for a plethora of redox enzymes and gets oxidized to 
mycothiol disulfide (MSSM) upon application of oxidative stress. It requires the activity of the 
mycothiol disulfide reductase (Mtr) for the maintenance of a highly reducing MSH redox 
potential (EMSH) (Jothivasan & Hamilton, 2008; Kumar et al., 2017; Tung et al., 2019). In 
C. glutamicum, Mtr catalyzes the reducing reaction of MSSM to MSH and its overexpression 
has been proven to enhance stress tolerance with increased MSH/MSSM ratio under ROS 
exposure (Si et al., 2016). Hypochlorite stress results in the chlorination of thiol groups and the 
formation of sulfenyl chloride (Cys-SCl), an unstable intermediate that additionally reacts with 
proximal thiols for the formation of disulfides or overoxidation to Cys sulfonic acid. Therefore, 
when C. glutamicum cells are exposed to hypochlorite stress, S-mycothiolation activity is 
required to avoid lethal overoxidation of Cys residues (Chi et al., 2014). Mycothiol catalyzes 
S-mycothiolations in C. glutamicum. When hypochlorite stress occured, 25 S-mycothiolated 
proteins were identified, including antioxidant enzymes, (e.g. Tpx, Mpx, AhpE) and the 
methionine sulfoxide reductases MsrA and MsrB (Chi et al., 2014). Multiple biochemical 
studies emphasized the importance of the mycoredoxin-1 (Mrx1)/MSH/Mtr redox pathway in 
the de-mycothiolation processes and regeneration of antioxidant functions inside the cells (Loi 
et al., 2015; Reyes et al., 2018).  
The fluorescent protein roGFP2 is used as a biosensor due to the extensive exhibited dynamic 
range, pH stability, brightness, and resistance to photoswitching (Bhaskar et al., 2014; Hanson 
et al., 2004). Hypochlorite addition leads to oxidation of the two cysteines placed on each side 
of roGFP2 chromophore with the generation of a strong disulfide bond that outputs an 
increased fluorescence intensity at 380 nm and an associated decrease at 470 nm, whereas DTT 
treatment causes opposite results to the spectrum (Hanson et al., 2004). Therefore, 380/470 nm 
fluorescence intensity ratio is utilized as a reporter of the redox state of C. glutamicum cells. 
Thus, the covalent coupling of roGFP2 fluorescent protein to the mycothiol-specific 
oxidoreductase Mrx1 generated the oxidative stress biosensor Mrx1-roGFP2, which responds 
mainly to fluctuations in mycothiol redox potential.

The importance of non-enzymatic antioxidant thiol, mycothiol in C. glutamicum’s resistance 
against ROS formation was highlighted when hypochlorite was added to exponentially 
growing cultures. Specifically, both C. glutamicum WT and ΔmshC experienced a growth 
deficit upon hypochlorite addition. However, growth was restored sooner for the wild-type 
strain (Figure 25a) compared to the C. glutamicum ΔmshC strain (Figure 25b). Regarding the 
ROS levels, treated C. glutamicum WT cells recovered completely from oxidative stress pulse 
3 hours after its application, when the identified oxidation degree was equal to the reference 
wild-type culture (Figure 26a). In contrast, mycothiol deficient strain was susceptible in 
hypochlorite treatment and reported inability in ROS overwhelming with an oxidation degree 
of 1 (fully oxidized), three hours after the stress application (Figure 26b). These findings are in 
line with previous studies in C. glutamicum, which had shown increased intracellular ROS 
levels and higher oxidative stress levels for the MSH-deficient strain compared to the 
respective wild-type strain.   
5.3 Elevated intracellular ROS concentrations resulting in increased DNA 
damage levels 
In this study, genetic C. glutamicum constructs possessing the integrated protein-based Mrx1-
roGFP2 biosensor were equipped with the plasmid pJC1_recA E2 Crimson to report induction 
of DNA damage response. Upon DNA damage, transcription of recA is induced, and the 
activity of SOS response is introduced with the mediation of LexA cleavage by RecA 
activation. In addition, RecA is involved in multiple DNA repair pathways, as the repair of 
double-strand breaks, daughter-strand gaps, and SOS mutagenesis. Therefore, promoter recA 
was chosen to be introduced to the C. glutamicum constructs while the red fluorescent protein 
E2 Crimson was used as an output reporter to quantify DNA damage levels. Fusion of the recA 
promoter to a reporter gene led to the formation of an effective biosensor with a sensitive 
response which allowed monitoring of DNA damage levels when cells were treated with a 
genotoxic agent. 
Results regarding DNA damage levels for the two strains further revealed the importance of 
mycothiol’s role in C. glutamicum. Specifically, as observed in Figure 27, increased 
fluorescence intensities at 650 nm were obtained from treated C. glutamicum cells of both 
strains one hour after the oxidative stress application. This indicated the activation of the SOS 
response upon increased DNA damage levels, with induced transcription of recA and higher 
fluorescence signals outputted by the E2 Crimson fluorescent protein. Mycothiol deficient 
strain presented higher DNA damage levels, especially two and three hours after shaking flask

treatment, and showed more than two times higher SOS response signals than the reference 
cultures due to the increased transcriptional activity of recA (Figure 27). In addition, treated 
wild-type cultures were also experiencing higher DNA damage levels; however, stressed cells’ 
fluorescence intensity at 650 nm was maintained to values approximately 50% higher (one to 
three hours following treatment) compared to the respective untreated cultures. Thus, the 
results demonstrated that the biosensor constructs reporting DNA damage via the 
transcriptional activity of the recA promoter are responding and show increased DNA damage 
levels upon elevated intracellular ROS concentrations. In addition, it was concluded that the 
presence of mycothiol as a non-enzymatic antioxidant system enhanced stain’s defense activity 
against oxidative stress, and later caused lower levels of DNA damage. 
5.4 ROS leads to lethal damage of C. glutamicum cells 
Monitoring of C. glutamicum cell death upon stress application provides significant insights to 
the study. Keeping track of the number of cells surviving, following stress induction, is 
beneficial for two main reasons. To begin with, cell viability estimation is essential for 
understanding the implications that oxidative stress and DNA damage have on the total cell 
population. IFC measurements were conducted in various time points following stress 
application to ascertain whether higher DNA damage levels result in lower numbers of viable 
cell populations. The second reason is to determine the extent to which the E2 Crimson 
fluorescence intensity signal was influenced by cell death. Specifically, dead cells cannot 
activate the DNA damage response with increased transcriptional activity of recA. Therefore, 
when mixed populations of viable and dead cells exist in a sample, the actual fluorescence 
intensity of E2 Crimson protein, indicating DNA damage levels, is underestimated. Therefore, 
quantification of viable cells via IFC is of major importance for obtaining reliable results in 
terms of the fluorescence signal detected per viable stressed cell. 
Viability measurements with the IFC technique showed that mycothiol presence is catalytic for 
C. glutamicum cell survival. For samples obtained during the first two hours following 
hypochlorite treatment, cell viability was not shown to be affected for both wild-type and MSH 
deficient strain (Figure 28), in contrast with oxidative stress and DNA damage levels which 
were significantly increased at the same time. Cell death for the mycothiol deficient strain was 
initially noticed three hours after stress application because the SOS response of the strain was 
not sufficient to prevent lethal damages upon multiple circles of ROS accumulation. Strain’s 
inability to overwhelm fully oxidized conditions was decisive for cell viability with increased 
proportions of dead cells during the 8th (30% of cells) and 9th (41% of cells) hour of growth.

On the other hand, the wild-type strain in the presence of MSH successfully managed to 
partially retain its reduced cytoplasm environment and avoided high DNA damage levels and 
cell death three hours after hypochlorite addition. For the upcoming hours, viable cell 
concentration was slightly decreased, with losses of 8% and 19%, during the 8th and 9th hours, 
respectively. These results demonstrated a more sufficient response of cells possessing MSH 
in their detoxification system, both in preventing high intracellular ROS concentrations and 
retaining their membrane integrity. Additionally, it was concluded that higher DNA damage 
levels indeed lead to lower numbers of viable cell populations. 
5.5 Increased MMS concentrations trigger intracellular ROS levels 
proportionally 
In this work, during in vivo characterization of biosensor signal studies, C. glutamicum cultures 
were exposed to MMS, a DNA damaging agent shown to increase the oxidation degree of the 
Mrx1-roGFP2 biosensor. The results presented in section 4.4, demonstrated slightly lower 
growth for cultures treated with medium (100 μg/mL) MMS concentration. At the same time, 
the growth defect was significant upon the highest MMS concentration (800 μg/mL) compared 
to the untreated cultures (Table 9). In addition, DNA damage existence enhanced the 
transcriptional activity of the recA promoter, resulting in three different fluorescence signals 
of E2 Crimson protein at 650 nm. As presented in Figure 22, the medium concentration of 
MMS led to a twofold increase of fluorescence intensity of the E2 Crimson protein. At the 
same time, the highest stress levels gave approximately six times higher intensity than the 
reference cultures of both wild-type and MSH-deficient C. glutamicum strains.   
Despite the increased DNA damage levels, alterations in the oxidation degree of the Mrx1-
roGFP2 biosensor were revealed depending on the concentration of the DNA alkylating agent 
(Figure 23). Specifically, even the medium MMS concentration was shown to induce the 
activity of mycothiol for maintenance of the reducing state of C. glutamicum cytoplasm and 
system detoxification from ROS. Cells of the wild-type strain partially eliminated the formation 
of ROS subspecies; however, an accountable increase in the oxidation degree of Mrx1-roGFP2 
biosensor was observed with a value of 0.55 compared to OxD of 0.4 for the untreated WT 
cultures. Regarding the MSH-deficient strain, there were no significant changes observed in 
the oxidation degree of the Mrx1-roGFP2 biosensor upon medium MMS concentration 
compared to untreated cultures. However, upon MMS concentration of 800 μg/mL, both 
C. glutamicum WT and the MSH-deficient strain showed high intracellular ROS levels with 
fully oxidized biosensor values (OxD-1 for both strains).

As already mentioned, the interconnection of oxidative stress and DNA damage in 
C. glutamicum has not been investigated. In similar studies conducted for yeast cells, it was 
revealed that upon MMS application, the intracellular ROS subspecies levels were increased. 
Higher ROS levels can be dangerous for cell growth; however, they have been involved in 
multiple cell signaling processes for cell detoxification (Apel & Hirt, 2004; D’Autréaux & 
Toledano, 2007; Genestra, 2007). Upon DNA damage conditions, cells must react to retain 
their genome integrity and subsequently continue through the cell cycle (Rowe, Degtyareva, & 
Doetsch, 2008). Therefore, activation of the stress response system is further induced via ROS-
mediated signaling, which is essential for cell survival. 
These findings implied that the DNA alkylating agent MMS is most likely inducing oxidative 
stress by increasing the intracellular ROS levels in C. glutamicum. To clarify this statement, 
other factors that could affect the output Mrx1-roGFP2 biosensor signal should be excluded.  
During in vitro characterization work, the presence of E2 Crimson cell lysate on the top of the 
Mrx1-roGFP2 biosensor did not influence the oxidation degree (Figure 15). Furthermore, 
minor changes in the OxD of Mrx1-roGFP2 biosensor of the MSH-deficient strain between the 
reference culture and medium MMS treated cells showed that mycothiol deficiency was not 
crucial for increasing the oxidation levels. These results reveal that the application of MMS in 
growing cells triggers ROS formation as a consequence of a general genotoxic response. 
Therefore, it is concluded that there is a strong interconnection between DNA damage and 
oxidative stress in C. glutamicum, which operates as a bi-directional reaction when one of these 
events is detected inside the cells. 
 
5.6 Future research 
5.6.1 Combination of FACS analysis with Impedance Flow Cytometry will provide 
a powerful tool for understanding the interconnection of oxidative stress and DNA 
damage in C. glutamicum 
Multiple cell viability assays have been applied in different industrial fields such as medicine, 
food, and the biotech industry to determine the viability of unculturable environmental species 
and examine bacteria susceptibility against extracellular stresses (Stiefel, Schmidt-Emrich, 
Maniura-Weber, & Ren, 2015). The most applied methods involve indirect measurements of 
cell states without providing further indications about the competence of cell growth and 
division. All these techniques, which are using parameters such as membrane potential, redox 
==PAGE:72==
 Discussion 
61 
 
indicators, nucleic acid stains, or reporter gene systems, are based on different principles and 
criteria for dead and alive bacteria classification (Bogosian & Bourneuf, 2001; Trevors, 2012). 
The most accepted criterion for the distinguishment of dead and alive cells is the maintenance 
of membrane and cellular integrity. Nevertheless, in some cases, cells have been found to retain 
their membrane integrity but were metabolically inactive, whereas other cells that lost their 
ability to grow and divide, were still able to produce the wanted compound.  
Following IFC measurements which effectively estimated cell viability of samples of the two 
different strains, emerging interest arises about the state of heterogeneous cell populations. 
Specifically, after oxidative stress application, transcription of recA was induced with increased 
DNA stress levels outputted by E2 Crimson fluorescence intensity at 650 nm, and the BactoBox 
device indicated different cell concentration levels depending on the strain and the applied 
chemical concentration. However, flow cytometry does not provide detailed information about 
the single-cell level. Thus, an interesting application that will provide further statistical 
information about oxidative stress and DNA damage levels of heterogenous populations is 
FACS analysis (Fluorescence-activated cell sorting). This method is a derivative of flow 
cytometry and allows the physical sorting of a heterogeneous cell mixture into separate 
populations. FACS will provide cell separation by analyzing metabolic content, protein 
expression, and nucleic acid content into phenotype. Therefore, FACS will determine the 
percentage of cells that are both under oxidative stress and DNA damage by maintaining their 
membrane integrity, which will quantify the actual fluorescence intensity signal originating 
from different DNA damage levels. 
5.6.2 Application of oxidative stress in lab-scale stirred bioreactors 
Shake flask experiments with the cultivation of the new dual reporter strains provided 
interesting insights regarding the interconnection of oxidative stress and DNA damage. For 
further data extraction and optimization of cultivation monitoring, the same experiment could 
be scaled up and take place in stirred bioreactors with the application of different aeration 
levels. While cultivated in fermentation tanks, C. glutamicum is exposed to high oxygen 
concentrations, often surpassing the air saturation, which sets a challenge for the intracellular 
mycothiol redox balance. However, C. glutamicum is highly robust during oscillations of high 
and low oxygen concentrations in bioreactor cultivations due to the presence of the non-
enzymatic mycothiol.  
==PAGE:73==
 Discussion 
62 
 
Nevertheless, it was reported that C. glutamicum ΔmhsC_Mrx1-roGFP2 for pO2 ≥ 30% could 
not overcome ROS detoxification. Therefore, the suggested experimental set-up for future 
research is the cultivation of the new dual reporter strains in stirred bioreactors by applying 
two different constant pO2 levels. The first case would be the cultivation of C. glutamicum WT 
and MSH-deficient at pO2 = 10%, mimicking the reference cultures' state without expected 
stress application and ROS formation. The latter case will concern the cultivation of the two 
strains at pO2 = 30%, with increased expected ROS levels for the MSH-deficient strain. 
Therefore, samples from the fermenter will be obtained at various time points and prepared for 
fluorescence measurements which will detect oxidative stress and DNA damage, according to 
the procedure followed during shake flask cultivations. 
An advantage of this procedure is the possibility of monitoring pH during aerobic fermentation, 
something that will exclude the effects of alterations in the fluorescence signal due to pH 
fluctuations. In addition, due to the increased volume of the cultures compared to shake flask 
experiments, sampling for omics such as metabolomics, proteomics, and transcriptomics is 
more accessible. Omics sampling will provide further insights into the timing of events taking 
place at the cell level when oxidative stress is applied. Specifically, upon oxidative stress 
application, mycothiol levels could be detected through metabolomic analysis. In addition, 
transcriptomics would be useful to detect the transcriptional activity of recA when the SOS 
response is induced. Following these methods, a genomic analysis will provide further inputs 
regarding the effect of DNA damage on the genome. Thus, comparing the biosensor signals 
with the respective omics analysis will verify the biosensors’ precision level and identify 
potential time delay in event detection via biosensor utilization.
