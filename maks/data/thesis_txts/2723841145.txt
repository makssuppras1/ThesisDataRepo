Abstract
This Master thesis presents a study on Finite Element Limit Analysis (FELA) of plane rein-
forced concrete structures with explicitly defined reinforcement bars. The main objective
of this work is to develop the formulation of the one-dimensional bar element, which is
independent from the finite element mesh of hosting concrete model. This work investi-
gates the effectiveness and accuracy of the developed method in calculating the ultimate
carrying capacity of plane reinforced concrete structures.
The study begins with an overview of the principles of FELA, including plasticity theory,
material models, yield criteria, and optimisation, and their application to analysis of rein-
forced concrete structures. Next, the formulations of the one-dimensional bar elements
with the constant stress and linear stress variation are presented, as well as principles of
its implementation in FELA for solving limit analysis problems.
The thesis then presents several application examples that demonstrate the effectiveness
and accuracy of the FELA method with explicitly defined mesh independent reinforcement
applied to plane reinforced concrete structures. These include the analysis of a deep
beam with uniform load, deep beam with concentrated load, and wall with an opening.
The results obtained from these case studies are compared with the results of analytical
calculations and FELA models with smeared reinforcement, and a good agreement is
observed.
In conclusion, this thesis highlights potential of the mesh independent reinforcement bar
element to become a good alternative to the current FELA modelling techniques of explic-
itly defined reinforcement, as well as discusses the element limitations. It also provides
recommendations for future research and development in this field, such as alternative
element formulations, investigation of the element sensitivity to the mesh size and the
perspectives of its implementation in 3-D volumetric analysis.

Introduction
The design of the reinforced concrete structures is a broad engineering discipline. Rein-
forced concrete is used in residential and industrial buildings, infrastructure and energy
projects, marine structures, and in many other construction areas. The structural concrete
elements range from simple ones (e.g. beams and columns) to much more complex (e.g.
pile foundations with pile caps, shear keys, etc.).
The key task for a structural design engineer is to make sure that these structural elements
are safe to carry the loads, meet serviceability criteria, and are feasible in construction.
Modern engineering has a wide range of analysis and calculation methods for the design
of reinforced concrete, both analytical and numerical. Finite Element Method (FEM) is
one of them.
The increased power of the modern computers has made FEM calculations a routine en-
gineerтАЩs task. In the design of reinforced concrete, FEM calculations are used for the
analysis of the global structure behaviour, such as shown on Figure 1.1, and for the anal-
ysis of individual structural components (e.g. walls, beams, slabs, etc.).
The predominant type of reinforced concrete FEM analysis done in the engineering indus-
try is an analysis with linear elastic concrete behaviour (both in tension and compression).
While conceptually it is wrong to treat concrete like this, this is generally a conservative
approach, and is permitted by the European Concrete design code EN 1992-1-1. How-
ever, this conservative approach results in larger reinforcement and concrete quantities
compared to designs done by more rigorous analysis methods.
Figure 1.1: Example of a complex FEM model of the tunnel where concrete structures
were modelled with the linear elastic material behavior.
In light of the Global Sustainable Goals and DenmarkтАЩs Global Climate Action Strategy
it is clear that concrete industry shall move in the direction of optimising design of struc-
tures to reduce material consumption. Therefore, development and implementation of
more rigorous analysis methods, which can help optimise structures and reduce material
quantities, is a very relevant subject for the concrete industry in Denmark.

One of the relatively recent numerical analysis methods is Finite Element Limit Analysis
(FELA). Unlike тАЭtraditionalтАЭ FEM methods in structural analysis, which are based on a con-
stitutive relation between stresses and strains, FELA is based on limit analysis framework
and rigid-plastic material model. The main purpose of FELA is to maximize the collapse

Some of very common engineering problems, where FELA is being applied more and
more - is analysis of the plane structures, such as shear walls and deep beams. The

been developed that are suited for FELA. These elements can be used with different

structure with reasonably uniform reinforcement, such as shown on Figure 1.2(a).

Theoretical background
In this chapter the theoretical background for the project is presented. It has to be noted
that the topics covered in this chapter can be very complex and cannot be covered in
this work in all detail. The references are made to the sources, where these topics are
comprehensively addressed. Here only essential theory is presented in a compact form
to give most relevant background for this work.
2.1
Limit analysis and theory of plasticity
There exist many analytical methods for calculation of the ultimate carrying capacity of
concrete structures. These methods are well known in the design and construction in-
dustry, some more than others. Examples of these calculation methods are strut-and-tie
method, homogeneous stress field method, stringer method, yield line method, etc.
All of these calculation methods lie under the framework of limit analysis - structural anal-
ysis field dedicated to the development of methods for direct assessment of the collapse
loads of the structural models. The limit analysis of reinforced concrete in its fundamentals
has theory of plasticity, which comprises of three parts presented below.
Lower Bound theorem
If for the loaded body it is possible to find statically admissible stress distribution, such
that the corresponding stresses are lying within the yield surface, then the applied load is
lesser than or equal to the collapse load. The found stress distribution is called safe and
statically admissible.
Upper Bound theorem
If for the loaded body it is possible to find kinematically admissible collapse mechanism,
and the external work done by applied load equals the internal work done by internal
stresses, then the applied load is greater than or equal to the collapse load.
Uniqueness theorem
If for the loaded body it is possible to find a load magnitude which results in a safe and
statically admissible stress distribution, while for the same load magnitude a kinematically
admissible collapse mechanism is found, then the found load is true collapse load.
The theorem of plasticity and its application in particular to the reinforced concrete struc-
tures is thoroughly presented in the fundamental book Limit Analysis and Concrete Plas-
ticity by Nielsen and Hoang (2011).
This work mainly focuses on the Lower Bound theorem. The finite elements and numerical
formulations of limit analysis presented in this work are established in such way that the
safe and statically admissible stress distribution can be found.
2.2
Finite Element Limit Analysis
Similarly to the manual limit analysis methods mentioned above, the objective of the finite
element limit analysis is to find the ultimate collapse load of the structural model.
In the manual analysis methods, the accuracy of the found collapse load often depends
on the skills and experience of the designer. In the case of lower bound methods, it is
Finite Element Limit Analysis with Mesh Independent Reinforcement
5
==PAGE:11==
experience to find stress field which maximizes the load, for example suitable strut and
tie model. For upper bound methods it is experience to identify collapse mechanism,
which minimizes the load, for example, correct yield lines. Manual setting of optimisation
is possible for some problems, where optimisation variables would often be geometric
parameters (e.g. width of the struts, angle of the yield lines, etc.).
In FELA the model domain is discretized in advance, prior to optimisation. The opti-
misation variables become discretized stress and displacement fields. As with manual
methods, there exist lower bound and upper bound FELA formulations. There also exist
mixed formulations, which are somewhat in between the lower and upper bound. Mixed
formulations do not ensure strict equilibrium conditions in the entire model domain, but
the application of the mixed elements showed good results in finding the exact collapse
load, both in terms of accuracy and convergence (Herfelt, 2017).
In this work, mixed element is used as basis element for the concrete disk. However,
the formulation of the FELA is set up as a lower bound problem. The principles of set-
ting up lower bound FELA are presented in this work with reference to Olesen, Poulsen,
and Andersen (2019). For the upper bound FELA methods reader is referred to Olesen,
Poulsen, and Andersen (2019) and Herfelt (2017).

2.4
Material model
Typical stress-strain curves for reinforcement steel and concrete under uniaxial loading
are presented on Figure 2.2.
The steel stress-stress curve (fig. 2.2 (a)) is characterised by a linear elastic part followed
by a typical yielding plateau at the yield strength limit fy, both in tension and in compres-
sion. The yielding is followed by hardening until the steel strength fu is reached, and then
by softening until failure. For normal reinforcement steel grades with a strength of 400 to
500 MPa, the strain at yielding lies within 0.2% to 0.3%. The ultimate strain however is
much larger, and for class B reinforcement is generally above 5%.
The concrete stress-strain curve (fig. 2.2 (b)) has a linear elastic part, followed by some
reduction in stiffness until peak compression strength limit fc, which is then followed by
softening and failure by crushing. Similarly on the tension side, there is a linear elastic
part, and after reaching tensile capacity fct there is quick loss of strength.
Figure 2.2: Typical stress-strain curves for (a) reinforcement steel and (b) concrete.
Both above presented stress-strain relations are successfully used in the elastoplastic
finite element analysis. In the framework of the limit analysis, however, the constitutive
stress-strain relationship is replaced by a rigid perfectly plastic material model. The rigid-
plastic material stress-strain curves for concrete and steel are shown on Figure 2.3.
The rigid-plastic material model is more of a mathematical model and does not reflect
physical material behaviour. It assumes no deformations until the stress reaches plastic
limit.
Figure 2.3(a) shows rigid-plastic stress-strain curve for the reinforcement steel. The plas-
tic limit is the yield strength of steel fy. As noted above, the elastic strains of reinforce-
ment steel before yielding are generally much lower than the plastic strains. Therefore,
the rigid-plastic material model is a reasonable assumption for steel.

Element formulation for limit analysis
This chapter covers mathematical formulation of the finite elements.
The equilibrium
equations are established between nodal forces of the finite elements and the element
stress parameters. Three elements are presented - mixed plane stress element, bar ele-
ment with constant stress, and bar element with linear stress variation.
3.1
Mixed plane stress element
There are several elements developed by various researchers for the finite limit analysis of
concrete. The first plane stress finite element has been presented by Poulsen and Damk-
ilde (2000). This element is a strict lower bound element and enforces strict equilibrium
conditions between element boundaries.
In this work the mixed plane stress element presented by Krabbenh├╕ft (2016) is used for
modelling of reinforced concrete disc. The mixed element does not enforce strict equi-
librium along boundaries. However it converges faster compared to strict lower bound
element, and also it was considered to be more flexible for the further implementation
of the bar element. The formulations for the mixed element are presented further with
reference to Herfelt (2017).

Bar element
The bar element formulations presented in the following sections are the core of this
project work. The overall idea is similar to the plain stress element. For the model system
to function as a whole - the stresses from the bar elements must be distributed to the
triangle element nodes. In order to do this - an equilibrium matrix for a single bar element
is established, which sets up relation between the stress parameters in the bar element
and the forces in triangle element nodes.
The equilibrium matrix is established for two types of bar elements. One with the constant
stress, and second with a linearly varying stress along the bar element.

Conclusion
Development of mesh independent bar elements
With global digitization of the AEC (Architecture, Engineering and Construction) industry,
the development of the Finite Element Methods is also moving forward both in academia
and in the commerial software sector. This work presented formulation and application
of the new one-dimensional bar element, which is particularly applicable to the Finite
Element Limit Analysis.
The developed bar element can be implemented in the computational limit analysis of
plane concrete structures to model individual reinforcement bars. The particular advan-
tage of the element is that the reinforcement bars can be defined independently from the
finite element mesh of the concrete body.
The modelling can therefore be done in the following steps: Geometry of the structural
model is defined тЗТModel is discretized with the plane stress elements тЗТReinforcement
bars are defined individually тЗТReinforcement bars are discretized following the mesh of
the plane stress elements.
Such modelling process is simpler and more straight-forward compared to the current
alternatives of either discretizing the bars together with plane stress element mesh, or
modelling it as plane element strip with high mechanical reinforcement ratio.
The underlying principle of the mesh independent bar element is force (tensile capacity)
contribution to the plane stress element nodes, where the global system stress field equi-
librium is ensured. The element is defined to work with the mixed plane stress 7-node
triangle element. Two types of the bar element were developed - one with the constant
stress along the bar, and second with the linear stress variation along the bar. The imple-
mentation in FELA is done using the lower bound method.
The element has been tested and compared both to the structural models which have
exact solution and to the models where individual reinforcement was modelled by plane
stress element strip. Overall the element showed good results, in particular the linear
stress bar.
However, the element also showed to be sensitive to the model mesh, and to the size
of the hosting plane stress element. This was in particular seen in the model with three
bars, where all three bars crossed through individual relatively small triangle elements.
The constant stress element was more sensitive than the linear stress element.
Perspectives and future work
Overall it can be said that the new ways of explicit reinforcement bar modelling are desired
in the industry. As mentioned in the introduction, the individual bar modelling where bars
are not discretized with the host system can already be found in commercial software.
Recent article by Andersen et al. (2022) shows how smeared reinforcement approach can
be successfully applied to model solid concrete structures. The bar element presented
in this work is promising with regard to the possible implementation in three-dimensional
structures. The bar element passing through the face of tetrahedral element would ease
and enhance the analysis possibilities of 3d solid structures.

At the current stage though further tests of the developed element are required, in partic-
ular to identify why the element is sensitive to mesh shape and size. As mentioned in an
example with three bar tests, possible cause it disproportion to the nodal force contribu-
tion between the bars and concrete. Small concrete element size becomes an issue when
the force contribution from the bar element is significantly larger compared to the force
that can be transferred to the surrounding elements. This needs to be studied further.
The formulation of the element equilibrium matrix presented in this work, can be called
somewhat simple and robust. Different formulations can be investigated. One of possi-
bilities to get the nodal force distribution is to do an integration of the product of stresses
along the bar and node shape functions over the element area. However, this will not
necessarily solve the sensitivity issue.
Another areas of improvement are stress continuity and bond stress consideration. The
elements developed in this work, similar to the concrete elements, allow for discontinuity
in stresses along the bar. For the constant stress bar elements the continuity obviously
cannot be established. But for the linear stress bar elements, there is a possibility to
add constraints in the SOCP optimisation, such that bar stresses at the triangle element
boundaries are equal. With regard to the bond stresses, the possibility to limit the rate of
stress change along the bar can be further investigated.

This Master thesis presents the development and implementation of the one-dimensional bar el-
ement suitable for explicit modelling of reinforcement bars under the framework of Finite Element
Limit Analysis of plane concrete structures. The developed element is independent from the finite
element mesh of the hosting concrete model, which is advantageous from the modelling and calcu-
lation perspective. Several application examples demonstrate the effectiveness of the element and
compare it with the current methods of modelling locally placed reinforcement.
