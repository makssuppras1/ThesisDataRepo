III 
Abstract 
 
The Vessel Speed Reduction Program (VSRP) is a solution that the ports of Los Angeles and Long Beach 
introduced in May 2001 and refined in the years after to mitigate the increased emissions related to 
shipping. This program levers on the fact that reduced fuel consumption in the port’s proximity results 
in lower local emissions. Several papers and theories regarding the reduced speed initiatives, often 
referred to as slow steaming, will be presented in the literature review. This thesis studies the 
implications and opportunities behind the port policies based on the Automatic Identification System 
(AIS) ship-call data, containing the 2019 port calls to and from the Spanish port of Valencia. Fuel 
consumptions, costs, SO𝑥𝑥,  NO𝑥𝑥, PM10, PM2.5 and CO emissions are calculated for 15,529 trips.  
The results obtained by recalculating the data flow under four different scenarios are compared with 
the baseline case of non-compliance to the VSRP. Moreover, a sensitivity analysis, with the definition 
of nine assumptions, is pursued. This approach has allowed to observe how results change with a 
variance of three variables (i.e., speed limit zone length, the speed limit in the limited zone, monetary 
incentive entailed by the program).   
The analysis shows that compliance to the VSRP results fuel reductions of 1-2 % for the overall trip in 
the cases where the ship operator accepts to endure a trip delay, while it can lead to overall trip fuel 
consumption increases ranging from 1 to 10 % in the cases where the vessel increases the speed prior 
to the VSRP zone. In the port proximity areas, the program can result in fuel reduction of up to 
approximately 20%, and the costs for every kg of abated local pollutant (i.e., SO𝑥𝑥,  NO𝑥𝑥, PM10, PM2.5 
and CO) ranges from approximately 1 to 100 $/kg, depending on the emission species. A cost-benefit 
analysis has been pursued both from the ship operator point of view and from the port authority. 
Results show that both could benefit from the application of this policy under certain circumstances.  
It has been discovered that vessels categorised as containerships and general cargo account 
respectively for the highest and lowest fuel consumption and fuel cost levels. Vessels with capacities 
ranging between 5,101 and 10,000 TEU accounted for the highest overall trip fuel consumption and 
cost. 
The findings presented are inherently entangled with the characteristics of the individual port and 
ship-calls in the considered dataset.

1. Introduction 
 
Greenhouse gas emissions of international shipping have increased from 977 million tonnes in 2012 
to 1,076 million tonnes in 2018, an 8.4% increase compared to the value registered in the Third IMO 
GHG Report published in 2014. The 9.6% increase between 2012 and 2018 is quite significant, 
considering that the share of shipping emissions in global anthropogenic emissions consisted of 2.89% 
in 2018 (higher than the 2.76% measured in 2012). Furthermore, according to the economic growth 
rates assumed, the IMO has projected that emissions will increase from about 90% of 2008 emissions 
in 2018 to 90-130% of 2008 emissions by 2050 (International Maritime Organization, 2020). However, 
a special mention should be made for the years 2020 and 2021, when total emissions generated are 
expected to be lower due to the outburst of the COVID-19 pandemic (March et al., 2021).  
Reducing fuel consumption is one of the solutions to address this problem. This thesis focuses on one 
facet of this topic, particularly the Vessel Speed Reduction Program. This program was first introduced 
by the ports of Los Angeles (POLA) and Long Beach (POLB) in May 2001 as a measure to reduce 
particulate matter, nitrous oxides, and greenhouse gasses in the port’s surroundings. Given the 
proportional relation between emissions and vessel speed, the program targeted the threshold speed 
of 12 knots in the 20 nautical miles zone in the port’s proximity. The program has always been 
voluntary in nature (Port of Los Angeles, 2020).  
Nonetheless, some changes in the years following the rollout have been noticed. The kick-off version 
dated 2001 did not include any monetary incentive. The first significant change occurred between 
2005 and 2006 when POLB added publicity and financial incentives to increase participation in the 
program. A green flag would be awarded to vessels that achieved 100% yearly compliance with the 
program. These operators were invited to a recognition ceremony, together with ship operators 
participating at 90% or higher (Port of Los Angeles, 2020). Table 1 shows the compliance levels to the 
program form 2015 to 2020.

Furthermore, any vessel with 90% or higher yearly compliance to the program would benefit from a 
15% rebate on the dockage fee for the first day upon arrival on 100% of the annual vessel calls. In 
2009 the POLA implemented the monetary incentive in the program as well. Additionally, a second 
speed zone of 40 nautical miles was introduced for both ports. The condition of 90% or higher 
compliance was maintained. The monetary incentive consisted of a 30% rebate on the dockage fee

for the first day upon arrival in the case of the adherence to the 40 nautical miles zone (Linder, 2018). 
Besides, the participation in the years since 2009 has been rising steadily, with more and more ship 
operators aiming to benefit from the rebate on the dockage fee, which is stated in the port’s tariff 
regulatory documentation and depends on the vessel’s type and size (Port of Los Angeles, 2021a).  
This project aims to assess the AIS data of one year of port calls for the Spanish port of Valencia, 
studying the possibility to reduce the environmental impact in the proximity of such port through the 
implementation of the VSRP. Different scenarios and modalities of application of this program will be 
presented and analysed, considering numerous factors, such as emissions saved, time penalties and 
cost reallocations, that such initiative inherently entangles. The feasibility, costs, and benefits for the 
main actors (i.e., the ship operators and the port) are analysed.  
On the one hand, pursuing the VSRP generates negative spillovers such as time delays and increased 
operating costs and fuel consumption for the ship operator. Furthermore, the port’s possible 
introduction of financial incentives for the ship operators to participate in the program is considered. 
On the other hand, the emissions reduction in the port areas and other positive aspects for the two 
stakeholders, such as social legitimacy, publicity and eco-efficiencies, are also discussed. The research 
question will be formulated as an Alteryx data flow model, where final results include the variation of 
the emissions in the proximity of the ports and the monetary payback for the ship operators to comply 
with the VSRP. 
 
1.1. Thesis Purpose 
 
The purpose of this project is to calculate the fuel consumption and cost variations under the four key 
scenarios presented, reductions in the emissions due to the implementation of the VSRP program in 
the port of Valencia and assess its feasibility, with a very much hands-on approach on the available 
data for a year on port calls. 
Alteryx Designer has been depicted as the ideal advanced automated analytics tool.  Over the project’s 
entire duration, an Alteryx flow has been developed to efficiently clean, blend and analyse the 
extensive data collection. Alteryx, a data science developer company founded in 1997, has led the way 
for digital transition for some of the largest companies in the world (Customer Center | Alteryx, 2021) 
and was recently recognized as one of the most innovative companies in data science (Fast Company, 
2021). 
This data science tool was exploited in many of its functions to successfully plot and automate the 
desired formulas and calculations. Moreover, it allowed a collection of results in different scenarios 
and provided the necessary output for the sensitivity analysis. 
The outcomes generated by processing the data with the Alteryx designed flow has represented the 
fundament that allowed to depict the best possible trade-offs, strategies, and approaches to optimize 
the version of the program currently in use in the POLA and POLB. Benefits and costs for both the port 
and the ship operators have been considered.

2. Literature Review 
 
A thorough literature review has been conducted to efficiently and successfully undertake the 
required analysis and calculations that fulfil the thesis's purpose. This activity has provided with the 
necessary background knowledge to step into the investigation and later discussion around the 
experimental findings. This review will give an overview of some relevant peer-review articles 
published concerning the Vessel Speed Reduction Program and a series of results and formulations 
for the fuel consumption and environmental emissions of vessels. Moreover, relevant insights from 
the extensive 4th IMO GHG Study on reducing GHG emissions from ships will also be discussed. 
 To ensure the consistency of the data and the credibility of the references collected, a systematic 
literature review has been conducted, mainly through two online research libraries; DTU Findit and 
Google Scholar. On the one hand, the Vessel Speed Reduction Program has not been extensively 
treated in the available literature accessible through such libraries, thus did not require an intense 
screening process. On the other hand, numerous models and theories have been published concerning 
the optimization of vessel speed and the topic of slow steaming in general. Slow steaming is the 
deliberate reduction in the sailing speed to reduce fuel consumption and pollution from emissions 
(Alexa McPherson, 2019). Therefore, regarding this aspect, the initial 298 articles published in the last 
five years accessible on DTU Findit have necessarily been scoped down to what is believed to be the 
most relevant 20 peer-review articles. The criteria for this selection have been to prioritize the models 
and theories that see the emissions reduction driven by the speed optimization in certain areas (e.g., 
Emission Control Area). Papers that offered an optimized solution in particular weather or technical 
conditions associated with the vessel type were not considered in scope. The reason for this approach 
is that the first ones show to have more touchpoints (i.e., similar variables and constraints) with the 
optimization and initiatives for the port areas that this project intends to present. 
The following paragraphs will dive into the papers observed as the major contributors to the 
background knowledge of the project. These papers have been clustered into three categories. 
 
2.1. Speed optimization research and models 
 
One of the first and earliest papers to be placed in this category investigated the environmental 
benefit in reducing 𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2 emissions from international shipping with the application of slow steaming 
strategies (Cariou, 2011). The results showed that slow steaming initiatives in 2009 and 2010 resulted 
in a reduction of 11% of emissions in 2011. Besides, the critical finding presented in the article was 
that these strategies are only sustainable given a fuel bunker price of at least 350-400 $/ton for the 
main container trades (Cariou, 2011). Nowadays, it could be argued if this price is still accurate, given 
the last years’ price fluctuations and the current steady increase of the fuel bunker price curve. The 
price evolution between 2019 and 2020 of low and high sulphur fuels is presented in Figure 1, along 
with the price differential (Kevin Saville, 2021).  
==PAGE:14==
 
 
 
4 
 
Figure 1: Price spread between low and high Sulphur fuels between 2019 and 2020- source: (Knowler, 2020) 
 Nonetheless, the idea behind the author’s statement is still valid. If bunker prices were to fall while 
freight rates and inventory costs increase, the ship operators would most likely operate the vessels at 
higher speeds. Thus, for slow steaming strategies to remain appetible for the ship operators, a balance 
between such variables must be maintained, not allowing the fuel bunker prices to fall below a certain 
threshold. An alternative solution to keep this balance could be represented by governmental 
restrictions and regulations on speed limits or perhaps tax levies and cap-and-trade systems.   
Another essential attempt to model the optimal economically efficient speed reduction to mitigate 
the 𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2 emissions has been done by James J. Corbett et al. (Corbett et al., 2009). The authors have 
set their model to maximize the profit for ships calling on US ports while considering a series of 
constraints that aim to enhance the environmental impact in the model. The first relevant finding is 
that a fuel tax of around 150 $/ton fuel will lead to 𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2 reductions of approximately 20-30 %. 
Secondly, this reduction translates into costs between 30$ and 200$ per ton of 𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2 saved for the ship 
operators (Corbett et al., 2009).  
The taxonomy study by Psaraftis and Kontovas (2013) highlights the importance of the speed factor 
contributing to the 𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2 emissions produced worldwide by the shipping industry. In this qualitative 
study, possible modal shifts and the state of the shipping market are taken into consideration. It is 
noticeable that Psaraftis and Kontovas affirm that in a depressed market situation, slow steaming 
becomes a prevalent practice due to the fuel cost savings entailed. The emissions reduction become 
a positive side-effect of such a strategy (Psaraftis & Kontovas, 2013).  
Additionally, this paper presents a taxonomy on a series of models, where speed is one of the decision 
variables. These models are differentiated amongst ones in which emissions are a considered variable 
and ones in which such term is not computed.  In conclusion, the question “Instituting speed limits?” 
is posed. Lindstad e al. (2011) is cited as in favour, while Cariou and Cheaitou (Cariou & Cheaitou, 
2012) cited as counterarguments for this proposal, often presented by the lobbying groups (Psaraftis 
& Kontovas, 2013).  
Faber (2012) has extensively treated this argument. In his paper, Faber assesses the proposal brought 
forward to the IMO by the Clean Shipping Coalition (CSC). The impacts of vessel speed reduction on 
emissions are studied, and an analysis of the legal feasibility of regulated slow steaming is also done. 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
5 
This study concludes that, although there is still much uncertainty in the correlation between engine 
load and carbon emissions, it can be seen that overall vessel speed reduction results in lower air 
emissions. Regardless of whether the speed limit is imposed by law or the speed reduction is enhanced 
voluntarily (Faber, 2012).  
 Considering the paper mentioned above by Psaraftis and Kontovas (2013), the critical difference 
between imposing speed limits and slow steaming is mentioned. This topic represents an essential 
matter of interest shared with this project. The authors state that slow steaming is inherently a 
voluntary response, and this differentiates it from speed limits, which are an imposed measure. 
Moreover, if the speed limit is above the ship operators‘ speed voluntarily chosen, it becomes 
superfluous. The authors conclude that setting speed limits is an emission reduction initiative that 
entails a series of factors that must inevitably be assessed. It is realistic to assume that overall costs 
might outweigh its benefits (Psaraftis & Kontovas, 2013). 
D. Zhuge et al. (2020) also described the difference between slow steaming and speed limits. Their 
research is aimed to define the impact of vessel speed reduction programs. This paper is, amongst the 
latest publications, one of the most relevant references for this project. The result that the authors 
set to achieve consists of the computation of a mixed-integer nonlinear mathematical model to 
minimize the fuel and vessel operating costs, minus the dockage refunds (i.e., the incentive provided 
by the port for compliance with the program). This model appears to be, to the author’s knowledge, 
a new solution to find the balance for adherence to the VSRP, considering the speed limit and a limited 
number of ships simultaneously. Zhuge et al. define three types of voyages between two consecutive 
ports of call. From a Non-VSRP complying port to another Non-VSRP complying, from a VSRP 
complying to a Non-VSRP complying and from a Non-VSRP complying to a VSRP complying. The last 
combination, from VSRP port to VSRP complying port, is avoided through the introduction of dummy 
Non-VSRP compliant ports in between the two.  
The authors present two algorithms, the first being enumerative and the second being a piecewise-
linear approximation algorithm. The conclusions presented are that the first algorithm results suitable 
for problems with numerous routes but a limited number of VSRP ports while vice versa for the latter. 
Through these two interesting algorithms, the model can generate the optimal or near-optimal 
solution on the compliance of VSRP, with a possible reduction of costs for the shipping operators 
(Zhuge et al., 2020). Although the work of Zhuge et al. is remarkable, the focus is placed solely on the 
costs for the ship operators. Two essential aspects that the VSRP initiative entails are just mentioned 
in this research. The first is the emissions reduction embodied by the program. It is acknowledged that 
such program generates a cut in the emission contribution in the proximity of the port area; however, 
the emissions generated are not computed in the equations on which the model stands, which 
highlights a gap in the research. Secondly, this model does not enhance the port’s perspective.  The 
dockage refund value is found considering the ship’s operator’s possible savings. Still, it would be 
valuable to understand the limitations and interests that influence a port from offering a determined 
value for the dockage refund for a complete understanding of the program.  
The topic of speed in relation to emissions reduction has been extensively assessed in the last decade. 
This literature review highlights the difference between slow steaming and mandatory speed limits. 
Digging even further into this matter, it is worth mentioning another contribution by Harilaos N. 
Psaraftis (2019). In his article, the environmental benefits and feasibility of these two options are 
compared. Psaraftis compares a speed limit case to a levy case and demonstrates that under the same 
speed reduction (and hence the same 𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2 emissions), a speed limit is more profitable than a bunker 
levy. Other qualitative aspects that affect the paper’s study, such as flexibility, number of ships, freight

rates, and ship size, are mentioned. For these reasons, the author concludes that the speed limit 
option shows more complications than the other (Psaraftis, 2019).  
 
2.2. Regulatory insight 
 
This paragraph presents a review of the policy implications of applying speed reduction initiatives to 
reduce the environmental impact in the interested areas of application. The first essential reference 
investigating the speed reduction initiatives from a legal feasibility point of view is the paper by Faber 
(2012). On the legal side, the author states that the imposition of the speed limit depends on the when 
and the how such a limit is to be imposed. Indeed, a global agreement would allow a state to set the 
limit on ships sailing not under its flag; nonetheless, the control over non-party states would be 
unachievable if this is not in place. Moreover, the complexity would increase if the limitation would 
be imposed not only on a state level but on an even more specific regional level. Faber (2012) 
considers this kind of situation counterproductive, as it would be highly complex to regulate 
efficiently. It could generate market distortions, given the relative ease of changing flag for a ship 
(Faber, 2012). 
Although this project sets to assess the benefit of the VSRP as a voluntary measure, as done in the 
Ports of Los Angeles and Long Beach, it is worth mentioning that the legal implications of a mandatory 
speed limit in ports do not seem to be insurmountable, according to Faber’s study. Such limitation 
could be implemented with fewer drawbacks in the entrance to ports compared to a global sea 
coverage (Faber, 2012). 
The second paper that has guided the understanding of speed limitation and speed reduction 
initiatives is represented by the Fourth IMO GHG Study (2020). It has been acknowledged that the 
trend to reduce operating speeds, although existing for some, has not been pursued by all ship types 
over the last years. For instance, oil tankers and container ships have seen an increase in average 
speeds during 2015 and 2016 compared to other years during the period of study. Moreover, 2015 
and 2016 account for the highest rate of total 𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2 emissions growth, and the IMO states that 
operating speeds are a vital driver for this phenomenon. Other spillovers that are intertwined with 
the trends in operating speeds are also the possible fluctuations in the market and behavioural trends 
that guide the ship operators.  
For the topic of vessel speed reduction in the proximity of ports, there is a gap in the research 
presented by the IMO, as no references or reflections on such initiative are made. However, an 
important aspect highlighted and worth assessing is the increased usage of Continuous Monitoring 
Datasets (CMDs) by the shipowners and operators to optimize fleet performance. Discussions have 
been ongoing regarding the use of bottom-up or top-down approaches to assess global fuel 
consumption. The bottom-up method, also known as the activity-based approach, heavily relies on 
AIS data obtained by satellite. On the contrary, the top-down process is based on fuel sales. The 
bottom line is that improving the technologies and rolling out innovations such as the CMDs 
mentioned above will reduce uncertainty and possible bias affecting the registered results in future 
studies.  
Other essential references for the study of the VSRP initiative are the program guidelines and 
compliance data diffused by the Ports of Los Angeles and Long Beach. These documents present the 
annual policies and regulations that allow a ship to comply with the program and the yearly 
compliance rates. This information has proven most valuable during the presentation of the VSRP 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
7 
program application in the two Californian ports in Section 1 and has served as an inspiration for this 
project to apply the VSRP concept to the port of Valencia and other ports around the globe.   
Lastly, the regulations retrieved from the Port of Valencia online portal have proven vital to 
understand the pricing strategy for the dockage fees at the port (Port Authority of Valencia, 2021e) 
and have provided relevant insight and financial statistics that the port has obtained in the past years 
(Port Authority of Valencia, 2021d). 
 
2.3. VSRP focus 
 
Linder (2018) paper has been an important milestone in this literature review. This paper has provided 
a qualitative analysis of the VSRP program in the ports of Los Angeles and Long Beach, stating the 
benefits and results in the first years of the program, and points out the main reasons that push the 
shipping companies to take part in it (Linder, 2018).  
The first critical contributions obtained from this paper are the relevant insights concerning the history 
of the VSRP program in the ports of Los Angeles and Long Beach and the results and compliance levels 
during the inaugural years of the program.  
An interesting comparison between the cost/benefit of the implementation of the VSRP versus a fuel 
switch, both to achieve a reduction of emissions, is presented. The author is of the opinion that VSRP 
represents a more reasonable solution mainly for three reasons. The first is that the VSRP enhances a 
larger 𝑁𝑁𝑁𝑁𝑥𝑥 reduction. Secondly, controlling the use of a specific type of fuel rather than another is 
hugely resource-demanding and hard to put in place on a large scale. 
On the contrary, the VSRP would be easy to implement and monitor, given the increasing reliability 
and accuracy of the AIS technologies. Linder concluded by stating that the success of the VSRP in the 
future would strongly depend on the influence of the public and regulatory pressures on the shipping 
industry. The main reasons for compliance to the VSRP highlighted through the author’s survey are 
public pressures, publicity, and social legitimacy. It becomes clear that those economic motivations 
are not the only driving factor in a more environmentally aware industry. 
A second milestone in the literature review, significantly crucial for this project, is the paper by Thalis 
Zis et al. (2012). This paper examines emissions reduction port policies based on ship-call data. It has 
resulted of fundamental importance for the analysis as it has provided with the necessary 
formulations of fuel consumption in the different phases of the vessel route (i.e., during sailing, 
maneuvering and at berth). Such formulas will be presented, and their application discussed in the 
methodology and analysis chapters. The benefits and feasibility of cold ironing (i.e., the process of 
shore power provision) are also discussed. Still, in this paper, the topic of slow steaming in the 
proximity of port areas has been considered chiefly relevant, together with the fuel consumption 
formulations mentioned above. Insight on the emission factors has also been assessed by the authors, 
and together with the emission factors collected in the fourth IMO report, has provided the relevant 
coefficients and approaches to calculate the emissions generated by the studied vessels. 
The authors conclude that the emissions generated depend on a multitude of factors and that the 
impact of port operations on the surrounding area is related to three main categories. These are 
maritime operations, in-port operations, and generated traffic outside the port’s gates.  The results of 
their analysis show that full compliance of calling vessels with a speed reduction scheme can lead to 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
8 
𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2, 𝑆𝑆𝑆𝑆𝑥𝑥 and 𝑁𝑁𝑁𝑁𝑥𝑥 reductions by 8–20 per cent, 9–40 per cent and 9–17 per cent, respectively. 
Furthermore, increased reduction potential is observed for ports presenting shorter berth durations 
and larger ships (Zis et al., 2014).  
Finally, it is stated that the presented framework for emissions estimation strongly depends on the 
characteristics of the port in matter. Another interesting paper focusing on the results obtained 
through the VSRP is written by Ahl et al. (2017), who concluded that a discount policy with prices 
adjusted to operator type would help boost compliance to the program even more. These price values, 
ranging from 268$ to 759$ per hour, are established by applying a discrete-choice model that 
estimates the adherence to the program based on dockage-fee savings and speed-reduction 
associated costs (Ahl et al., 2017).  
This study has provided handy insight for this project and has raised awareness of the influence of a 
ship operator type in the computation of a speed-reduction model. It must be noted that the variable 
considering the emissions saved was not included in the model, and this highlighted a gap regarding 
the benefits associated with the compliance to the port.

4.1. Key Parameters present in the dataset 
 
The project sets its foundation in an extensive dataset of AIS port calls for departures and arrivals from

Each row indicates a trip, either with arrival at the Port name from Last port or departure from Port 
name to Next port. The Port name specifies the port at which either the departure or arrival was 
registered, and in this project is always represented by the port of Valencia. The value for the Move 
type allows understanding whether the vessel arrived at the Valencian port (value=0) or departed 
from it (value=1). The Timestamp indicates the precise moment in time (for either the arrival or the 
departure), in a minute-level detail. 
The first step required in the data analysis consisted of cleaning and filtering erroneous rows due to 
data missing or repetitions. First, it was noticed that the IMO identification code was 0 for 19,729 rows 
and NULL for nine rows. Nonetheless, this was not seen as an insurmountable impediment since the 
MMSI, and Ship name parameters were present for every row, allowing to identify all the vessels 
present correctly. The Draught was not in scope for the analysis and was therefore disregarded. Last 
port country and Next port country helped to avoid mistakes when it came to recognizing the exact 
location of the ports; however, they did not provide meaningful added value and were thus not 
included in the analytical analysis. 
A significant data cleansing was required when looking at the Last port and Next port parameters. 240 
and 259 NULL values were detected for the Last port and Next port variables, respectively. The 
formerly only represented an issue when the NULL value appeared in combination with a 0 value for 
the Last port variable. The reason is that in such a case, the Move type indicated a trip arriving from

such combination of parameter values were identified and removed. 
Under the same reasoning, the latter only represented an issue when the NULL value for Next port

Furthermore, it was noticed that some trips presented Valencia as the same port of origin and 
destination. Filtering 0 (arrival at Valencia) for Move type, 9,590 rows were discovered to have 
Valencia as Last port and disregarded. Similarly, filtering 1 (departure from Valencia) for Move type,

At this stage, a further scrimmage of the data present in the primary dataset was needed, as only the

considered. This is because the project’s focus lies on the trips where the standard zone for speed 
reduction could be applied and where the trade-offs of this initiative could be considered, not only in

The ship’s sailing speed represents another necessary parameter that was not included in the original 
dataset. It is essential to point out that average speeds were considered constant throughout the 
different zones of study, as a refined and more accurate drill-down of the speeds at every instant of 
the trips was not available in the dataset. For this activity, the MarineTraffic search engine was 
exploited. MarineTraffic is the world’s leading provider of ship tracking and maritime intelligence 
(MarineTraffic, 2021). George Evgenidis and Eleanor Bradley, Solution Advisors from the Data Services 
(API) team at the company MarineTraffic were consulted to retrieve the most reliable data possible. 
Unfortunately, most of the vessel parameters could not be provided for the ships present in the 
dataset, and the necessary assumptions and approximations made are explained in the following 
paragraphs.  
Therefore, the Clarksons World Fleet Register research portal (World Fleet Register, 2021) was 
exploited to fill the gaps still present after the research through MarineTraffic.  
Firstly, it was necessary to understand what ship type each row of the dataset was including. Thus, the 
MMSI, Ship name and IMO (where available) were used as keys for the research. All the vessels in the 
dataset were successfully looked up, either through MarineTraffic or the Clarksons World Fleet 
Register, and the 824 ships contributing to the 13,266 trips were classified into the five categories: 
1. Bulk Carrier 
2. Containership 
3. Oil/Chemical Tanker 
4. General Cargo 
5. Ro-Ro 
The ship type represents a cornerstone for the analysis because approximations on SFOC, EL, EP, BP, 
EF, and dockage tariffs are often dependent on such variables. The SFOC is the Specific Fuel Oil 
Consumption [g/kWh], defined as the fuel consumption for a unit of power developed in a unit of time 
(MarineGyaan, 2021). EL stands for Engine Load, the ratio determining the capacity of the engine to 
produce power, and negatively correlated to the engine speed (Vaibhav Rajendra Wakode, 2021). EP 
indicates the Engine Nominal Power [kW]. Finally, BP is the Boiler Power [kW] acronym, which 
indicates the nominal power for the boiler engines.  
These values could have been singularly researched for all the 824 vessels through the Clarkson World 
Fleet Register. Nonetheless, not all the values were present, and the API services consultants at 
MaritimeTraffic were also unable to provide such detailed information for all the vessels present in 
the dataset. Therefore, for some vessels, these parameters were estimated considering different 
scientific papers as sources.

4.3. Key activities and variables

6. Discussion 
 
6.1 Sensitivity Analysis 
 
This section presents a series of sensitivity analyses on the impact of the different model parameters. 
This procedure consisted in recalculating the outcomes of the model under alternative assumptions 
and this entailed a series of benefits. 
Firstly, it helped to test the robustness of the results. For instance, in the initial data runs an error in a 
data field, which resulted negative due to a spelling mistake in a formula, was depicted and corrected. 
Secondly, it provided the results that allowed for an increased understanding of the relationships 
between input and output to the model. This was also essential to lay the ground on which the 
discussion, presented in the next section, is founded.  
The different assumptions for the sensitivity analysis have been made variating the three key variables 
(VSRP speed zone length, speed limit in VSRP zone, monetary incentive) presented in Section 4.3. The 
outcomes obtained for every scenario presented in section 5 are compared with one another as well 
as with the baseline sensitivity case S, obtained assuming the same values that the VSRP entails in the 
POLA and POLB, which are: 
• 
VSRP zones of 20 or 40 NM 
• 
Speed limit of 12 knots in the VSRP zone 
• 
15% or 30% rebate on the dockage fee for the first day upon arrival at the port depending on 
which VSRP zone the vessel complied with. 
The nine sensitivity cases that have been made are the following: 
1) S1: + 5NM on the imposed speed limit zone (zone limits at 25 and 45 NM). 
2) S2: - 5NM on the imposed speed limit zone (limits at 15 and 35 NM). 
3) S3: + 10NM on the imposed speed limit zone (limits at 30 and 50 NM). 
4) S4: - 10NM on the imposed speed limit zone (limits at 10 and 30 NM). 
5) S5: - 1 Knots on the imposed speed limit (limit at 11 knots). 
6) S6: - 2 Knots on the imposed speed limit (limit at 10 knots). 
7) S7: + 2 Knots on the imposed speed limit (limit at 14 knots). 
8) S8: +5% rebate on dockage fee for VSRP zone 1 (20NM) compliance (for a 20% rebate) and 
+10% rebate on dockage fee for VSRP zone 2 (40 NM) compliance (for a 40% rebate). 
9) S9: +10% rebate on dockage fee for VSRP1 compliance (for a 25% rebate) and +20% rebate on 
dockage fee for VSRP2 (for a 50% rebate). 
The overall results for each run under every assumption listed above and in all the four scenarios 
presented in Section 5 can be found in the Appendix. These results have been filtered based on the 
ship type and on the ship capacity. Furthermore, not all the trips are included in all the scenarios, as 
explained in Section 5. For instance, in cases S5 and S6 for Bulk Carriers the number of trips complying 
with scenario 4 was over 50% lower than the ones complying with scenarios 1 and 2. Thus, to 
guarantee an effective comparison of the results, the grand total values have been translated into unit 
values by dividing them by the number of trips that accounted for the specific scenario and sensitivity 
case. All the trip numbers can be found in the Appendix A.2.. Nonetheless, to ensure a clearer 
understanding of the results, they have been grouped according to the scenario, or to the adjusted 
variable. The following paragraphs will present the grouped findings.

7. Conclusion 
 
In this thesis the application of four scenarios and a sensitivity analysis with nine different assumptions 
have been brought forward to evaluate the possible application of the VSRP considering the AIS port-
calls for the port of Valencia. An Alteryx data flow was designed to assess the scenario and sensitivity 
analyses and has allowed to depict the cases where the outcomes of the VSRP were more 
advantageous.   
The major contribution that this thesis has provided is sixfold.  
• 
It has contributed to fill a gap highlighted in the literature, where a sensitivity study of the VSRP 
has not been effectively established yet.  
 
• 
It has successfully provided an analytical tool that demonstrates the variance in fuel consumption, 
cost, emissions and benefits for the key stakeholders for any vessel for which the parameters 
specified in Sections 4.1 and 4.2 are available in the different studied cases. It has demonstrated 
that vessels categorised as containerships and general cargo account respectively for the highest 
and lowest fuel consumption and fuel cost levels. Looking at the ship capacity, it has been 
observed that vessels with capacities ranging between 5,101 and 10,000 TEU accounted for the 
highest overall trip fuel consumption and cost. 
 
• 
It has highlighted the importance of the industry that the cargo belongs to and of the time factor. 
It has been acknowledged that Scenarios 1 and 2, which are the most beneficial for the emissions 
not only in the port proximity but in the overall trip, could be pursued by those stakeholders that 
can withstand a few hours delay. On the contrary, it has raised awareness that for those vessels 
that do not accept such delays, the overall 𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2 environmental impact of the trip would be higher 
with the implementation of the VSRP. This is a significant finding, as it should be acknowledged 
that rolling out the program in different ports might entail an increase of the average global sailing 
speeds and thus a worldwide 𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2 emission increase. Therefore, the VSRP requires further study, 
with the international organizations thoroughly assessing this aspect, to ensure that the benefits 
registered in terms of local pollutants emissions in the port proximity are worth the global 
increased 𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2 emissions.  
 
• 
It has depicted a weakness in the current pricing model in use at the POLA and POLB. The 
approximation of a certain % of rebate on the daily dockage fee cost has shown to be ineffective 
for various ship operators. The reasons can be found in the different times at berth spent by the 
vessels studied, and a pricing model that offers an incentive weighted on the actual hours spent 
at berth would improve the consistency of the economic returns offered by the port to the ship 
operators.  
 
• 
It has proven that the emissions aimed to be reduced in the port proximity entailed a different 
cost for abatement depending on the emission specie. Therefore, a port could consider the cost 
per kg calculated in Section 6 when setting the budget to achieve their target emission reduction 
goals. 
 
• 
The sensitivity analysis has shown that the impact of the VSRIP zone variable has resulted slightly 
more effective than the variation of the speed limit variable. Positive variations of the monetary 
==PAGE:72==
 
 
 
62
incentive alone have instead proven to only benefit the ship operators, and a disadvantage for the 
port. 
 
7.1. Study limitations 
 
Certainly, the gaps present in the dataset, which has been the starting point for this research, are 
something that must be noted. Several assumptions have been taken around the missing parameters 
needed for the analysis, as described in Section 4.2.   
Some approximations, such as assuming to have a constant sailing speed for large distances could be 
improved with a more extensive exploitation of the machine learning vessel tracking tools and models 
that are emerging in the shipping industry in these years. Another approximation is represented by 
the decision to not consider weather conditions and current effects and how those would influence 
the final outcomes. Besides, the distinction between speed over ground and speed through water was 
not included, with the former, obtained via satellite AIS data, considered the best option for this 
research.   
Furthermore, some of the constants for the financial aspects of the topic have been retrieved in papers 
published some years back, and more updated values would have improved the quality of the analysis. 
Lastly, the efforts to get in touch with the port authority of Valencia did not materialise. Hence, it has 
not been possible to collect further input to improve the research. The feedback will necessarily need 
to be collected after the finalisation of the thesis and noted for future improvements to the research.  
 
7.2 Future research 
 
The bottom-up method, also known as activity-based approach, heavily relies on AIS data obtained by 
satellite. This method has been selected for the analysis of the VSRP, and in future studies it could be 
integrated with data collected with a top-down method, which is based on the fuel sales. The latter 
methodology usually provides lower fuel consumption estimates (Psaraftis, 2021), and for this reason 
using the former guarantees a safer approach, but it would still be valuable to challenge the accuracy 
of the analysis. 
Another aspect that could be integrated with the future developments of the program is the process 
of Virtual Arrival. 
This strategy consists in ensuring an agreement between the ship operators and the port to reduce 
the vessel’s sailing speed in order to meet a revised arrival time when a delay at the discharge port is 
foreseen (INTERTANKO, 2010). In combination with the VSRP this would result most beneficial, and 
some of the trips that had prioritised a scenario 3 or 4 compliance, could instead comply under 
Scenarios 1 or 2. This would entail not only lower emissions and fuel consumption in the VSRP zone, 
but also in the zone prior to this one. Furthermore, minimising vessel waiting times would reduce the 
safety in the port area, as congestion and vessel movements in anchorage would be lower 
(INTERTANKO, 2010). 
In the Conclusion, the suggestion to the port to optimize the pricing model for the monetary incentive 
that the VSRP entails has been highlighted. Another suggestion has been made to the IMO and other 
==PAGE:73==
 
 
 
63
relevant national maritime organizations to assess the risk that obtaining reduced local emissions in 
the port proximity might come at a higher cost in terms of 𝐶𝐶𝐶𝐶2 global emissions. These are aspects 
that require further studying and where not only mathematical models, yet also strategic decisions 
are to be taken.
