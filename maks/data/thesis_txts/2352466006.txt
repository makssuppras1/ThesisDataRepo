Abstract
Charged quantum dot (QD) devices have many potential applications in quantum tech-
nologies through their implementation as a photon–spin interfaces. For example, by ma-
nipulating the spin degrees of freedom of such systems, a series of entangled photons may
be generated [1,2], which may then be used in quantum computing schemes. Recent exper-
imental work has demonstrated key properties of charged quantum dots, among these are
the increased decoherence of a singly charged QD in a cavity compared to its neutral coun-
terpart [3], the eﬀect of the host nuclear spin environment on the optical properties of a
QD [4] and Faraday rotations of linearly polarised light through interaction with a charged
QD in a cavity [5,6]. The progress in experimental techniques within the ﬁeld of quantum
electrodynamics in the context of charged QDs necessitates an extensive quantum theory
describing the most central of the observed eﬀects.
This master’s thesis describes the quantum theory of a charged quantum dot in a cav-
ity. We develop a consistent microscopic theory describing the non-unitary time-evolution
of the system due to internal dynamics and interactions with the electromagnetic environ-
ment. Further, we derive a an extension to input-output theory taking the polarisation of
light into account.
With this theoretical framework, we study in detail the dynamics of Faraday rotations
of light scattered oﬀa charged QD and compare it to simpler, semi-classical results [1]. We
discuss how Faraday rotations can be used for generating spin–polarisation entanglement
between a QD and a photon.
Further, we model the host nuclear spin environment as an eﬀective magnetic ﬁeld [4,7].
This enables us to estimate the eﬀect of a ﬂuctuating nuclear background on the properties
of light scattered oﬀthe cavity, particularly the state of polarisation.
Last, we propose a scheme for robust spin–polarisation entanglement using an external
magnetic ﬁeld to tune the QD transitions, while screening the ﬂuctuations of the nuclear
spin environment.
We demonstrate highly eﬃcient enhancement of the entanglement
ﬁdelity using this scheme compared to a simpler scheme that does not mitigate the eﬀects
of nuclear ﬂuctuations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Entanglement plays a central role in quantum computing and communication [8–13]. For
the realisation of many applications and computational schemes in quantum computing,
generation of entangled states is crucial. However, the inherent decoherence of a quantum
system due to interactions with its enviroment remains the most signiﬁcant challenge for
successful generation and retention of entanglement. Understanding the physical processes
leading to decoherence is necessary to mitigate it and improve the current state of quantum
devices.
A III-V semiconductor quantum dot (QD) can be charged with a single electron by
embedding it in an asymmetric PIN diode structure and a applying a small forward bias
voltage (see Fig. 1.1). Charged QDs constitute a promising platform for entanglement-
based devices due to their internal spin-1/2 degree of freedom and spin-polarisation de-
pendent optical selection rules. These features allow for entanglement of the QD spin and
the polarisation of a photon. This mechanism can be used to generate a string of single
entangled photons. This is possible through two principal schemes: First, the charged
QD can be used directly as a single photon source emitting a string of entangled pho-
tons [2]. Second, a conventional single photon source [14–17], emitting single photons in
the same polarisation state, can be used in combination with a charged QD employed as
an entanglement gate [1,18,19] to subsequently entangle the emitted photons.
Alternatively, a charged QD can be used as a spin qubit in itself, rather than a photon
source or gate. In such an application, photons can be used to entangle remote QD spins
and perform read-out of the spin state [18,20,21].
The main decoherence processes relevant for a charged QD are phonon interactions [23–
27], electron cotunneling [3] and coupling to the nuclear spin environment [4,7,28]. While
phonon interactions are present in neutral QDs as well, cotunneling and spin environment
coupling are particularly detrimental to charged QDs. Coupling to the spin environment
will be considered in detail in Ch. 4 of this thesis.
Experiments with charged QDs have recently gained a lot of attention, and great
achievements have been reached.
In terms of the application of a charged QD as an
entanglement gate, promising results with so-called Faraday rotations have been observed
[5,6,29,30]. A Faraday rotation is a spin-dependent rotation of light polarisation, which
will be introduced in Ch.
3.
Further, an entangled single photon source based on a
charged QD [31] and a dark exciton scheme [32], respectively, has been demonstrated. In

Another crucial resource is spin state preparation in charged QDs. This has been done

The quantum theory of charged QDs is less developed. A key part of the early theory is
based on results from single two-level system in a cavity and extended to cover the charged

This approach holds well within a simple approximation,
where the QD spin is not coupled to environmental degrees of freedom or a magnetic ﬁeld.
Later, more detailed theories have been presented to support experimental results [4, 5].
These treat the system fully quantum mechanically and allow for the description of more
complex spin dynamics. However, they are presented in a phenomenological way without
a microscopic derivation. To follow the rapid evolution of experimental results and explore
new ways to overcome decoherence, the development of a consistent quantum theory for
charged QDs is necessary. This is the main focus of this thesis.
This thesis will ﬁrst go through the fundamental quantum electrodynamics theory for a
charged QD in a cavity. This is done in Ch. 2 through a review of existing theory of charged
and neutral QDs along with the necessary generalisations, extensions and clariﬁcations to
ensure consistency of the theory. After this, the concept of optical Faraday rotations is
introduced in Ch. 3. Here, the quantum theory developed in Ch. 2 is used to describe the
behaviour of the system in the context of Faraday rotations and is compared to simpler
models.

Chapter 2
Fundamental theory and methods
2.1
A charged quantum dot in a cavity - model description
Here we will derive a model for a charged quantum dot (QD) in a cavity. Fig. 2.1 shows the
physical system of interest as well as a schematic of the dynamical model that is derived
in this section. The system consists of a charged QD coupled to a polarisation-degenerate
cavity. The cavity is driven by a polarised continuous wave (CW) laser through one of the
mirrors. The cavity mode is described by a quantised electromagnetic ﬁeld mode resolved
in two polarisations and the laser driving is represented by a parametric semi-classical
coherent ﬁeld that drives the cavity mode. The QD thus only interacts with the driven
cavity mode and not directly with the laser.
In the following sections we will start from the general form of the minimum-coupling
Hamiltonian and derive the light-matter interaction Hamiltonian for the QD and the
cavity ﬁeld from there. This light-matter interaction enforces a set of important spin-
polarisation selection rules that will be discussed further. Subsequently, we will construct
the full Hamiltonian including laser driving and the energies of the QD and cavity. Taking
dissipation and incoherent dynamics into account, we will lastly derive a quantum optical
master equation for the system.

2.1.2
Electronic states in a charged quantum dot
To get an explicit form of the dipole moment operator, we need to restrict our Hilbert
space to a set of relevant states. In the simplest picture of a neutral QD, we consider a
two-level system consisting of a ground state and an optically excited state, corresponding
to a single exciton in the QD.
For a charged QD there is an excess electron in the conduction band, which introduces
a spin-1/2 degree of freedom to the Hilbert space. To this end, we will restrict the Hilbert
space to the neutral ground state and the spin-resolved charged ground and excited states,
totalling ﬁve QD states (see Fig. 2.2).
Working in a basis of spin eigenstates along the z-axis (taken to be the cavity axis,
often coinciding with the QD growth direction), we may resolve the charged ground state in
these two spin eigenstates. For notational convenience, we shall label them as |0λ⟩, λ = ±,
|0+⟩≡|↑⟩,
|0−⟩≡|↓⟩.
(2.3)
Throughout this thesis, the notations (|↑⟩, |↓⟩) and (|0+⟩, |0−⟩) will be used interchangably,
depending on the context.
The excited states arising from optical excitations of these ground states are called
trions and are found by adding an electron-hole pair to the ground states (see Fig. 2.2).
As we have chosen to restrict the Hilbert space to contain only the lowest excited states,
the excited electron must reside in the s-shell of the conduction band, where the excess
charge electron is as well. This forces us to take Pauli’s exclusion principle into account
when writing down the excited states: To excite, for instance, the |↑⟩ground state, we
need to create the electron-hole pair such that the new electron in the conduction band is
in |↓⟩. The hole left behind in the valence band will then have the opposite spin state, |⇑⟩.

orbital, the spin state must be antisymmetric. Conclusively, the electrons are always in
the singlet state, and the spin of the trion states is carried entirely by the spin of the hole.

Before moving on to the full Hamiltonian of the system, we shall elaborate on the
spin states entering the derivations above. The conduction band electrons in the zero-
angular momentum s-orbitals are fundamentally spin-1/2 particles and thus have total spin
S = 1/2 with the possible projections along a given axis of ±1/2. Holes in the valence band
are built from p-type orbitals, which have total orbital angular momentum L = 1. The
total spin-orbital angular momentum quantum number for the holes is thus J = 3/2, corre-
sponding to the angular momentum projection states |J = 3/2, Jz = ±3/2⟩, |J = 3/2, Jz = ±1/2⟩
[39, 40]. Usually the subset |J = 3/2, Jz = ±3/2⟩are called heavy holes and the subset
|J = 3/2, Jz = ±1/2⟩are called light holes. In strain-isotropic QDs, heavy and light holes
are degenerate, but by introducing strain anisotropy, the true hole spin eigenstates become
superpositions of heavy and light hole states [40, 41]. This is referred to as heavy hole–
light hole mixing. Here we shall assume that the QD of interest in strain-isotropic, and the
light and heavy holes are degenerate. The hole spin states relevant for optical transitions
are then the heavy holes. This is due to conservation of total angular momentum: In
an optical transition corresponding to annihilation of a trion and emission of a circular
e+-polarised photon (which has the angular momentem state |J = 1, Jz = +1⟩), the total
state of the solid-state system and optical ﬁeld evolves as

Numerical solution of master equation
Throughout this thesis we will solve various master equations numerically, either by ﬁnding
the time evolution or the steady state solution of the master equation.
This is done

equations.
The steady state solver in QuTiP formulates the steady state equation, Lρ = 0 as a
linear vectorial equation, Mρ = b, and uses sparse LU decomposition to solve this. If the
system does not have a unique steady state, the matrix M is not regular and the solution
to the linear vectorial equation is not unique. As an example, this will be the case for the
master equation (2.20), if γc = 0 or Γ = 0. In this limit, the system is just two uncoupled
two-level systems, where the steady state depends on the initial state preparation.
If this is the case, or if the time evolution of the system is of interest, the master
equation solver in QuTiP is used instead.

3.1
Faraday rotations
Consider a cavity containing a charged quantum dot (QD) prepared in an eigenstate of Sz,
the spin operator along the z-direction. Spin state preparation can be achieved through
optical pumping in the presence of a constant magnetic ﬁeld perpendicular to the optical
axis [5, 22, 33, 34]).
The only allowed transition is then |0+⟩↔|X+⟩, which interacts
only with |+⟩-polarised light.
When the cavity is probed with light, the two circular
polarisation components will thus interact diﬀerently with the system (see Fig. 3.1). Light
in the circular polarisation state |+⟩will thus fully interact with the coupled cavity–QD
transition, seeing what is referred to as a ‘hot’ cavity [18]. Light in the |−⟩-polarisation,
on the other hand, will not interact with the transition and will thus eﬀectively see an
empty or ‘cold’ cavity. The eﬀect of the QD–cavity system on incident linearly polarised
light is thus a phase diﬀerence between the circular components. In the absence of losses
during interaction, the eﬀect of this phase diﬀerence is a rotation of the polarisation angle,
a so-called Faraday rotation [18]. Faraday rotations have been observed in experiments
with charged QDs. Early experimental results report Faraday rotations from a charged
QD of ∼15µrad [30] and demonstrate its application in a spin read-out scheme. Later
results report macroscopic Faraday rotations of 0.03π rad [5,29] and 0.35π rad [6].
When characterizing a cQED device designed for Faraday rotations, two important
measures must be considered: First, what is the probability that an input photon is lost
during the interaction?. Second, given that a photon couples correctly out of the cavity,
what polarisation state has it obtained?
In the following sections, we will study these questions, ﬁrst using a simple, semi-
classical theory where losses induced by spontaneous emission are neglected. Second, we
will consider a more complicated, but still semiclassical theory, where the eﬀect of losses
are taken into account. Last, a fully quantum mechanical model is considered, and the
diﬀerence between the three models is discussed. We will be considering a system based
on a single-sided cavity, where one mirror is perfectly reﬂecting and the other is partially
reﬂective, such that no light is transmitted through the system and the reﬂected state is
the primary object of interest. In the calculations to follow, we will work with models,

3.2.3
Full implementation at the master equation level
The output quantum state obtained when driving the charged QD–cavity system with
linear polarised light can also be calculated using a full numerical steady state solution of
the master equation for the system, (2.20), where the two spin manifolds and polarisation
modes are included at the Hamiltonian level.
In order to gain insight into the most
fundamental properties of the model and its structure, we shall set the cotunneling and
charging rates to zero, (γc = Γ = 0), and initialise the system in |↑⟩. For this conﬁguration,
the spin state of the QD will stay unchanged throughout the time evolution, which has
been conﬁrmed a posteriori for the numerical calculations presented here.
As in Sec. 3.2.2, the expectation values of the Stokes operators gives the Poincaré
angles, φ and θ. In contrast to the semiclassical calculations, we need to use the output
ﬁeld Stokes operators, (2.43), since we only have access to the intracavity density matrix.
The Poincaré angles, are plotted in Fig. 3.5, and by comparing this to Fig. 3.4, we conﬁrm
good agreement between the semiclassical model including losses and the solution of the
full master equation.
3.2.4
Discussion
In sections 3.2.1, 3.2.2 and 3.2.3 we have calculated the Poincaré angles of the reﬂected
optical output state obtained by sending a linear polarised CW ﬁeld into a one-sided
cavity with a charged quantum dot prepared in |↑⟩. The three sections present calculation
methods of increasing complexity, but with good agreement between the results. In Sec.
3.2.1, where a lossless, semi-classical interaction has been assumed, a perfectly linear
polarised output state was obtained, and the linear rotation angle was calculated as a
function of laser detuning. This was reﬁned in Sec. 3.2.2, where the eﬀects of losses through
QD spontaneous emission were taken into account within this semi-classical framework.
26
==PAGE:27==
The QD losses lead to ellipticity of the output state, but the rotation angle, φ/2, has
shown to be in close agreement with the one obtained from the lossless theory. Further,
by calculating the Poincaré angles from a full implementation of the dynamics at the
master equation level, the semi-classical results including losses are reproduced. From this
we can infer two conclusions: First, the semiclassical theory assumes that the optical state
stays pure throughout the interaction, whereas the master equation approach allows the
purity to be reduced. So for the cases studied here, we can assume that the optical state
stays pure as a good approximation. Second, we can separate the dynamics for the |+⟩-
and |−⟩-components by using their amplitude reﬂectivities.
However, these results stem from a simple master equation, where the two spin man-
ifolds are not coupled. It can be assumed that the semi-classical separation of dynamics
relies on this. If one considers spin-dephasing or cotunneling, the two spin manifolds will
be incoherently coupled, and the semi-classical model can no longer be expected to hold,
since it does not provide a description of these cross-manifold dynamics.
Calculations taking such dephasing processes into account must be set up in a fun-
damentally diﬀerent way. When the spin manifolds are incoherently coupled, the steady
state of the system will be unique and thus independent of the initial spin state. In that
case, one could deﬁne a time-integrated reﬂectivity rather than using the reﬂectivity in
the steady state limit.

Chapter 4
Interactions with nuclear spin
environment
Up to this point, we have considered the interactions between a quantum dot (QD) and its
electromagnetic environment. Although many of the desired properties of charged QDs
arise due to the light-matter coupling dynamics, the interaction between a QD and its
host material must be taken into account. The optical QDs of interest consist of alloys of
semiconductor compounds in group III and V. All nuclear isotopes of the semiconductors
in these groups carry a spin that interacts with the spin of the excess electron in the QD.
In this chapter, we consider the dynamics arising due to the coupling of a QD resident
electron to its host nuclear spin environment. Many interesting processes take place due
to this rich environment, where many-body eﬀects potentially play an important role.
Coupled spin problems are notoriosly complicated, and many diﬀerent models adressing
such problems have been studied.
Perhaps the most famous among these is the Ising
model [49], which describes neighbour interactions in a spin lattice.
Modern work presents treatments of the nuclear spin bath using a quantum ﬁeld theory
approach [50], a Markovian open systems approach in the presence of a strong magnetic
ﬁeld [51] or a semi-classical mean ﬁeld approach [7]. Other work studies the dynamic
polarisation of the nuclear spin bath for an optically driven charged QD using Krauss
formalism [52,53].
In this chapter, we will ﬁrst introduce the concept and Hamiltonian of the hyperﬁne
spin-spin interaction. This is a fundamental coupling mechanism between two spins, which
can easily be generalised to describe the interaction between the QD electron/hole spin
and the nuclear spins.
Afterwards, we will start out with a simple model consisting of a spin-1/2 particle
coupled to an incoherent, non-interacting bath of spin-1/2 particles via an exchange in-
teraction, along the lines of Ref. [51]. Later, we will consider a more complicated model,
where the coherence time of the bath is assumed much longer than the dynamical time
scale of the system, and the nuclear spins can be regarded as a ﬂuctuating magnetic ﬁeld,
similar to [4,7].

Chapter 5
Pulsed optical probe
Up to this point, we have primarily investigated the steady-state limit of the charged
QD system under the condition that the probing light ﬁeld is monochromatic. Although
this approach provides important insight into the fundamental light-matter interactions
of the system, it does not capture all of the operational features of the charged QD when
implemented as a photon gate. For the desired operation of the system as a photon gate,
it should ﬁrst be prepared in an initial spin state. Subsequently a number of photons
will be sent to the gate and interact with the system within a ﬁnite time, during which
coherent and incoherent processes take place.
When calculating properties of the system under a CW probe ﬁeld we are only seeing
the steady state eﬀects ocurring an inﬁnite time after the interaction has started. The
steady state properties of the system will in all realistic cases be independent on the initial
state, because spin relaxation processes leave the spin state in an equal mixture. One could
of course as a preliminary calculation neglect spin relaxation, but in that case the weak
optical driving ﬁeld will drive the spin state as discussed in Sec. 4.3.3. This will result in
a steady state that does not represent the state of the QD during interaction either.
A full quantum-mechanical treatment of the problem should describe the propagation
of a photon with a wave packet towards a cavity containing a charged QD. This requires
that the electromagnetic environment is included in the system Hilbert space, most easily
done by assuming that the cavity is coupled to a waveguide in which the photon propagates.
Such a calculation would require a signiﬁcant modiﬁcation of the model developed in this
thesis. Previous work present the solution of similar problems using the scattering matrix
technique [59,60], scattering input-output theory [61] or solving the full time-evolution of
the photon wave packet [62, 63]. However, the full photon scattering problem is outside
the scope of this thesis, where we treat the problem through an open systems approach.
To this end, we shall represent the problem by use of a weak pulsed driving of the cavity
mode. Although this introduces semi-classicality to the interaction process, it captures
important features arising due to the ﬁnite interaction time imposed by the photon wave
packet.
In the following sections, the Hamiltonian and Liouvillian of the problem will be pre-
sented along with a set of tools for calculating properties of the system with a time-varying
driving rate. After this, we will study the properties of the system subject to the pulsed
probe, including limitations due to the ﬁnite frequency width of the pulse and dephasing
43
==PAGE:44==
due to a ﬂuctuating nuclear spin environment. Last, we shall discuss how the system be-
haves under an external in-plane magnetic ﬁeld and show that spin-photon entanglement
can be achieved using this conﬁguration.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and outlook
In this thesis we have studied the optical properties of a charged quantum dot in a cavity
with emphasis on its application as a spin–photon entanglement device. We have discussed
the fundamental light-matter interactions as well as the more complicated dynamics arising
due to the host nuclear spin environment of the quantum dot.
In Ch.
2, we have formally derived a quantum optical master equation describing
the non-unitary dynamics of a charged quantum dot in a cavity driven by a continous
wave optical probe laser. Many features of this master equation has been presented in the
litterature up to this point, but a consistent development of the model has been missing.
Further, the formalism of input-output theory has been generalised to polarisation resolved
ﬁelds. This formalism is necessary for calculation of reﬂectivities, transmittivities and
quantities related to the ﬁeld emitted by the cavity.
Ch. 3 describes the physics of Faraday rotations, which are rotations of linearly po-
larised light when scattered oﬀa charged quantum dot in a cavity.
The results from
semiclassical models are compared to the quantum optical master equation derived in Ch.
2 with good agreement. The quantum optical master equation is then used to calculate
properties not available using the semiclassical descriptions.
The dynamics of spin dephasing is discussed in Ch. 4. Here, a Markovian model for a
single spin-1/2 particle coupled to a bath of host nuclear spins is derived. Further, a more
complicated model is introduced, where the host nuclear spin environment is modelled as
an eﬀective ﬂuctuating magnetic ﬁeld. The modiﬁed light-matter interactions induced by
this eﬀective ﬁeld are studied in detail.
In Ch. 5, a modiﬁed model is developed and studied. In this model, the continous
wave optical probe is replaced by a pulsed optical probe. This allows for an investigation
of the eﬀect on Faraday rotations induced by the nuclear spin environment. We have
found that the ﬂuctuations in the nuclear spin environment leads directly to ﬂuctuations
in the polarisation of output light generated through a Faraday rotation.
Further, we propose a scheme for spin–polarisation entanglement that is robust to
ﬂuctuations in the Overhauser ﬁeld. This scheme is based on the application of an external
magnetic ﬁeld in the Voigt conﬁguration, which is used to tune the transition lines of a
charged quantum dot in a cavity. We demonstrate stability of this scheme with respect to
ﬂuctuations in the nuclear Overhauser ﬁeld and ascribe it to retention of clean selection
rules and associated screening of ﬂuctuations. To our knowledge, this scheme has not
53
==PAGE:54==
been proposed before and might constitute a key resource for the application of a charged
quantum dot as an entanglement device.
Outlook
A natural improvement of the model considered in this project is a formalism describ-
ing single photon scattering fully quantum mechanically. In our model, we use a weak
semi-classical pulsed probe, which drives a quantised cavity mode. In a fully quantum
mechanical description of the scattering problem, a single photon is propagating towards
the cavity and interacts with the quantum dot–cavity system, before it is reﬂected. With
a formalism describing this scattering problem at the single photon level, the indistin-
guisability of the scattered photon could be calculated, and the eﬀect of the ﬂuctuating
nuclear background on indistinguishability could be calculated. Further, it would be pos-
sible to calculate whether the proposed entanglement scheme using an external magnetic
ﬁeld introduces any loss of indistinguishability to the photons.
Besides from an improvement of the formalism, it would be interesting to study the
extension of the proposed entanglement scheme to generation of multi-partite entangled
states, for example a linear cluster state [2].

Appendix D
Adiabatic elimination of cavity
In this appendix we develop a signiﬁcant simpliﬁcation of the full charged QD–cavity
system introduced in Section 2.1. By eliminating the cavity adiabatically, we are left with
a semiclassical description of the light-matter interaction, which enables exact analytic
calculations of the quantum dynamics of the QD. This adiabatic elimianation is only valid
in the perturbative limit of QED, where g/κ ≪1. To derive the adiabatic elimination,
we will restrict our model to a two-level system (TLS) coupled to a driven cavity with a
single mode. The results of this derivation can then subsequently be transferred to the
larger system of a charged QD in a polarisation resolved cavity.
We shall start with the standard master equation for a TLS in a cavity driven by a
continous wave laser, (A.20),
˙ρ = −iδ[σ†σ, ρ] −iν[a†a, ρ] −ig[a†σ + aσ†, ρ] −i[ηa† + η∗a, ρ] + κ
2L(a)ρ + γ
2L(σ)ρ,
(D.1)
where σ = |0⟩⟨X|, |X⟩(|0⟩) denotes the excited (ground) state of the TLS and a is the
cavity mode operator.
δ = ωX −ωL is the TLS–laser detuning, ν = ωc −ωL is the
cavity–laser detuning, g is the cavity–TLS coupling rate, η is the cavity driving rate, κ is
the total cavity power damping rate and γ is the rate of spontaneous emission from the
TLS to other optical modes than the cavity mode. L(A) = 2(A · A†) −{A†A, ·} denotes
the standard Lindblad super-operator. In the following sections we will ﬁrst perform an
adiabatic elimination of the cavity and derive the corresponding master equation for the
TLS. Subsequently we will extend the TLS master equation with eliminated cavity to cover
the spin-QD system and derive analytic results for the reﬂectivity and transmittivity for
this system. The general technique for adiabatic elimination used in Section D.1 is adopted
from Ref. [66], although the derivation here proceeds beyond the scope this, where only
limiting cases are studied.
