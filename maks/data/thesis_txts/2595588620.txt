Abstract
The demand for renewable energy is increasing, and oﬀshore wind energy is expected to be
one of the main competitors in the future energy market. Oﬀshore monopile foundations
experience various non-linear wave loads during extreme sea states. The secondary load
cycle is a non-linear load happening at the rear of a monopile after a steep wave has passed,
and it has often been associated with ringing. As yet, very little research on the secondary
load cycle has been performed for irregular 3D waves, and there is neither a closed-form
expression nor any other model of the cycle. In this thesis, the appearance of secondary
load cycles was investigated in 15 diﬀerent irregular wave experiments, both for 2D and
3D waves, and for four diﬀerent water depths. A total of 1599 experimental secondary
load cycle events were used in a data-driven model in order to predict cycles in irregular
wave simulations, using either linear or non-linear kinematics. A structural model with
one degree of freedom was used to simulate the loads on the monopile, and life-time fatigue
was calculated for each simulation with a view to comparing it to reference simulations.
The secondary load cycles were seen to reduce the equivalent moment by up to 10% in
most cases. A geometric down-scaling of the structure was applied in order to increase
its natural frequency. It was found that the eﬀect of the secondary load cycle was greater
when its frequency was up to 2.5 times the natural frequency. For secondary load cycle
frequencies around 2 times the natural frequency, the equivalent moment was increased
by up to 15%.

Introduction
One of the most important tasks and greatly debated subjects of the century, is to be
able to meet the highly expanding demand on energy. In 2015, members of the United
Nations signed to achieve the 17 Sustainability Development Goals, which aim to assure a
sustainable future. The goals are all to be achieved before 2030. One goal is to ensure ac-
cess to aﬀordable, reliable and clean energy for all of the world’s population. This means,
that a transition from fossil fuels to renewable energy is a key factor to accomplish this
goal. At the Paris Agreement in 2015, nations worldwide agreed to limit global warming
by keeping the global temperature changes below 1.5◦C. At this point, 189 countries have
ratiﬁed (Paris Agreement, 2015). This goal is to be accomplished mainly by phasing out
the consumption of fossil fuels and substitute it by alternative energy like wind energy,
solar PV and hydro power. In Denmark, the politicians agreed that renewable energy will
substitute coal power within 2030 and that the country will be fully independent of fossil
fuels in 2050 (Energistyrelsen, 2018).
It is expected that wind energy will be the main power source in Denmark. Denmark is
a pioneering country in wind turbine technology, and the research and development have
been expanding since the oil crisis in 1973. With a very low elevation and a coastline
that reaches up to 8750 km, the country has a climate very suitable for wind turbines.
Investments in research has led to the fact that the price of onshore wind energy is now
competitive with the energy price on fossil fuels and that oﬀshore wind energy will be a
cheaper alternative to oil and gas in the nearest future (Capion, Hasforth, Poulsen, & v. h.
E. Grunnet, 2019). As the highest wind speeds are found at sea, the development of oﬀ-
shore wind farms is highly attractive. Constructions of oﬀshore wind farms are also highly
desirable as the scale often can be increased both in terms of the wind farm size, but also
because the wind turbines at sea can be larger. Land-based wind turbines in Denmark are
restricted to a distance within four times the total turbine height of neighbouring citizens,
while oﬀshore wind turbines do not have the same restrictions to size. This means that
the size and number of oﬀshore wind farms are expected to increase in the coming years.
The Danish Energy Agency is planning to expand with three new oﬀshore wind farms
that collectively can produce 2400 MW (Energistyrelsen, 2019). One of the challenges in
building oﬀshore wind turbines is the use of foundations. Currently, the most common
type of foundation is the monopile. These foundations are cylindrical structures, which
are hammered into and mounted in the seabed. A transitional piece to mount the turbine
is located on the top above the water surface. The monopile is the most cost-eﬃcient type
of foundation since it is easily mass-produced. The monopile does, however, have some
disadvantages for water depths > 30 m, as the diameter needs to be increased dramati-
cally to increase the stiﬀness of the structure. At deep water, other foundation types as
jacket structures or ﬂoaters might therefore be of choice, but as these structures are more
expensive, the monopile is still the best option for shallow to intermediate water depths
(Ram´ırez, Fraile, & Brindley, 2019). As long that there are accessible shallow water sites
which have the conditions that makes it suitable for wind farms, the monopile foundations
will still be relevant many years to come. Research on wave loads on cylindrical bottom-
mounted structures are therefore extremely important.
Oﬀshore wind turbines are produced with a lifetime of around 25 years, where they are
expected to be operating nearly all the time. It is, therefore, important that they can
withstand all environmental conditions both in terms of wind speed and turbulence in-
tensity but also in terms of wave loads. Wave loads on monopiles have been studied in
many years, and both analytical as well as numerical models have been developed to pre-
1
==PAGE:13==
dict these loads with great accuracy. However, some non-linear phenomena in the close
vicinity of the cylindrical structure are not understood in detail.
A non-linear phenomenon, known as ringing was discovered on the Heidrun and Draugen
oil ﬁelds in the Norwegian sea. Ringing is a sort of high frequency vibration that appears
in the structure for medium-sized waves to large waves when they pass the cylinder. The
consequences of ringing can be catastrophic and lead to fatal damage on the wind turbines,
thus it is important to take its eﬀect into consideration (Grue, 2013). It is not yet exactly
understood which wave properties result in ringing, but research has shown, that ringing
might be related to another phenomenon called the secondary load cycle, which was orig-
inally referred to as the secondary oscillation and was discovered by Grue, Bjørshol, and
Strand (1993) in down-scaled wave tank experiments. The secondary load cycle is a small
force exerted on the structure by the wave after the wave crest has passed the structure.
Although there have been developed theories on the secondary load cycle, there is not
a complete consistency between them. Experiments with the secondary load cycle have
been done with regular waves and focused waves, but the research with irregular 3D waves
is very limited.
In this thesis, 2D and 3D irregular waves from physical model tests are examined to give
a statistical overview of the appearance of the secondary load cycle.
Correlations be-
tween non-dimensional wave parameters and the magnitude and period of the observed
secondary load cycles are investigated with the purpose of including the cycle in a simple
linear wave model. The wave interactions on the monopile are simulated for cases with
and without the cycle, and the structural responses are compared in order to identify how
critical the secondary load cycle is.

Literature review
The secondary load cycle was originally described as a secondary oscillation by Grue et al.
(1993) after it was observed in force measurements from experiments. The experiments
were performed in a wave tank using focused (transient) wave groups. By increasing the
wave height, the secondary oscillation would appear. It was observed that the start of
the secondary load cycle occurred when there was about 1 cylinder radius between the
rear of the cylinder and the wave crest. Based on the experimental results, the secondary
oscillation occurred about one quarter of the wave period after the force peak of the
wave. After the force peak, the force on the cylinder becomes negative. This is because
the horizontal ﬂuid acceleration is negative and because the diﬀerence between the free
surface elevation on the front and the rear of the cylinder is decreasing, and hereby that
the hydrostatic force on the cylinder is decreasing. Observations in the force signal showed
that the secondary load cycle always appeared as a small increase in the force, at the time
when the net force is negative. Based on this, Grue et al. (1993) concluded, that there
was a low-pressure at the rear of the cylinder, and that the secondary load cycle was a
suction force. This force was represented by a horizontal force acting about one cylinder
radius below the free surface, and can then be described as a free surface eﬀect (Grue et
al., 1993).
The time at which the secondary oscillation was present in the force signal was about 15%
of the local wave period (measured between two zero up-crossings), and the magnitude
of the secondary load cycle was up to 11% of the peak-to-peak force of the wave. In the
experiments, Grue et al. (1993) also showed, that the occurrence of the secondary load
cycle was dependent on diﬀerent wave parameters. The ﬂuid acceleration was characterized
by the non-dimensional numbers kD or kR, where D and R are the diameter and radius
of the cylinder, respectively, and k is the wave number. Since the secondary load cycle
was seen to appear for steeper waves, linear wave kinematics became invalid and the wave
number was found from the non-linear dispersion relation (Phillips, 1974):

Other results from the literature have shown to be fairly consistent with the work that
Grue et al. (1993) had performed. A study worth mentioning was performed by Chaplin,
Rainey, and Yemm (1997), who have done wave tank experiments on bottom-ﬁxed struc-
tures, in order to investigate ringing response. The secondary load cycle was here also
mentioned to have an inﬂuence on ringing. Their results included bottom-ﬁxed cylinders
of diﬀerent diameters, where the ratio between the secondary load cycle magnitude and
the peak-to-peak force was measured up to 8% for the smallest diameter and up to 12%
for the largest diameter. Although the presence of the secondary load cycle is similar,
results from (Chaplin et al., 1997) show that the secondary load cycle would not become
pronounced at Fr > 0.6. The magnitude of the secondary load cycle also appeared to be
correlated to both the steepness, ηm, and the Froude number, with the steepness being
the most important parameter.
A study on non-linear eﬀects caused by wave-impacts were performed by Rainey (2007).
He explained that ringing is a transient high-frequency structural vibration, which is in
the region of the 3rd harmonic of the wave frequency and has therefore previously been
studied using 3rd order weakly non-linear methods. However, he suggested that ringing
is a strongly non-linear phenomenon that occurs when a local breaking wave propagates
around the cylinder. This forms a cavitation bubble behind the cylinder which then col-
lapses. The burst of the bubble is what he suggests is the source of the secondary load
cycle, and the ringing of the structure is thus not related to the 3rd harmonic of the wave
frequency.
Studies on bottom-ﬁxed cylinders in irregular waves performed by Riise, Grue, Jensen,
and Johannessen (2018). A total of 2166 wave events were investigated, where a high-pass
ﬁltering method was used on the wave exciting moment to extract the two largest peaks
of the signal: the main force peak and the secondary load cycle. The ratio between the
two largest peaks were then found to be correlated to both the KC and the Fr, with the
Fr being the most important parameter. The magnitude of the secondary load cycle was
found to be up to 12%.
One of the most recent studies on the secondary load cycle is a detailed numerical investi-
gation performed by Ghadirian and Bredmose (2020). The focus in this paper was to give
an accurate explanation of the physics of the secondary load cycle using a state-of-the-art
coupled CFD solver. It was found that the secondary load cycle was mostly correlated
4
==PAGE:16==
with non-hydrostatic terms in the vertical momentum equation, and that the main reason
for the cycle is a suction pressure happening at the back of the cylinder. The suction
pressure arises as the wave crest passes the cylinder and creates a water column on the
back of the cylinder. Due to gravity, the water column accelerates downwards, and below
the water column, the wave kinematics leads to a suction force, which contributes to an
additional positive force enhancement in the force history. Here, it was also observed that
the secondary load cycle acts in the region from just above still water level to 1.5 cylinder
diameters below.
Until this point, various physical explanations to the secondary load cycle have been given,
and the conditions of its appearance have been studied both through experiments and ad-
vanced numerical models. However, only limited research on the secondary load cycle in
3D irregular waves has been done, and there exist neither a closed-form expression nor any
other model of the phenomenon. In this thesis, 2D and 3D irregular waves from physical
model tests are examined in order to ﬁnd the local conditions of the wave-monopile inter-
actions that lead to secondary load cycle events. A data-driven model is then constructed
using data from experiments and used to generate secondary load cycles and add them to
the wave forces in a simple structural model. Last, structural responses of the secondary
load cycle model are compared with loads of a reference model through estimated fatigue
loads. Structural scaling is used in order to manipulate with the natural frequency of the
model structure and the critical limits of scaling are discussed.

3.9
Fatigue loads
Cyclic loads on structures will over time degrade and weaken the materials and the damage
will in the end lead to structural failure. This is known as fatigue. Typically, the fatigue
of a material is characterized by the SN-curve or W¨ohler curve, which relates the stress on
the material to the number of cycles before failure. More cycles of low stress magnitudes
are needed for a failure than for cycles with a larger stress magnitude.
An oﬀshore wind turbine in irregular waves will experience many stress cycles of diﬀerent
magnitudes and the cycles become diﬃcult to count. A way to count the cycles are by
using the Rainﬂow Counting algorithm deﬁned by Rychlik (1987). This method works by
counting all the cycles in a stress time series and calculating their magnitudes. Further-
more, it makes sure that no cycles are counted twice. The Rainﬂow Counting algorithm is
shown as a sketch in Figure 3. First the stress time series is turned 90 degrees. From all
the minima starting from the ﬁrst minimum, a line is drawn as if it was rain ﬂowing from a
roof (hence the name). The lines stop when reaching a minimum equal or smaller than the
minimum, of where the line originated. The lines also stop if they are blocked by another
line. Same procedure is done for the maxima, where the line stops when a maximum
larger than or equal to the maximum of where the line originated is reached. Each line
13
==PAGE:25==
then represents half a cycle and the magnitude is determined by the range from starting
position to ending position. This is shown as the dashed line in Figure 3. The cycles are
then sorted and binned and a histogram of all the cycle stresses and the number of counts
is generated. In this project, the fatigue is based on time series of the total moment, Mtot.
From applying the Rainﬂow Counting the number of cycles, ni are counted, each with a
moment, Mi. For a simulation time, Tsim, the total fatigue lifetime equivalent bending
moment can be found from the equation (Blasques & Natarajan, 2013):

Data from physical model tests
The experimental data used in this project were provided from physical model tests from

2013). In this section, the purpose of DeRisk and Wave Loads is brieﬂy explained, and the
wave model test setups for both projects are described. In both projects, the tests were
carried out at DHI in a 35 meter long and 25 meter wide shallow water basin, in which
waves were generated using paddles in one end of the basin. The waves were absorbed in
the other end of the basin using rock berms to avoid as much wave reﬂection as possible.
4.1
The DeRisk Project
The DeRisk project started out in 2016 and was running to 2019. It involved 9 partners,
including departments on DTU, other universities and energy companies in Scandinavia
and England. The aim of the project was to investigate design load cases for ultimate limit
state (ULS) wave loads for oﬀshore wind turbine substructures and to hereby present de-
signs with low risk of failure. In order to make a reliable analysis of the hydrodynamic
loads, DeRisk combined advanced numerical models with model test cases. The exper-
imental setup included a model monopile scaled 1:50 with a model diameter of 14 cm
and two diﬀerent water depths at 40 cm and 66 cm. In real scale, this corresponds to a
diameter of 7 m, with water depths of 20 m and 33 m. The monopile was made of PVC
and had a high stiﬀness. To measure the free surface elevation, wave gauges were placed
in areas with 20 cm meters (model scale) between them close to the cylinder. In Figure 4
is shown an overview of the placements and numbers of the wave gauges compared to the
monopile. Force transducers at the top and on the bottom of the cylinder were used to
measure the wave forces. The free surface elevation was measured with a sampling time
of ∆t = 0.0498 s and the force was measured with a sampling time of ∆t = 0.0055 s.
The test cases from DeRisk included diﬀerent irregular sea states of both 2D and 3D
waves. Irregular waves were generated using the JONSWAP spectrum and diﬀerent sea
states with signiﬁcant wave height, Hs, varying from 5.8 to 11 m and with spectral peak
period, Tp, varying from 9 to 15 s. 6 hour measurements were performed with 2D waves
and 3D waves were measured for more than 72 hours. All provided test cases were with
extreme sea states with return periods of 10 years to 1000 years.
4.2
The Wave Loads Project
The Wave Loads project was running from 2010 to 2013, and was a research collaboration
of DTU Wind Energy, DTU Mechanical Engineering and DHI. It was funded by ForskEL
and Statkraft. The aim of the project was to improve wave load models used for oﬀshore
wind turbines. The achievements of the project included the development of numerical
wave models, CFD and aeroelastic tools, and physical model tests.

The secondary load cycle was observed in the force history after the main force peak. Its

cycles are described here:
• The ﬁrst type of secondary load cycle event is shown in Figure 5a. Here, the
appearance is very noticeable and well-deﬁned. After the main force peak, the force
drops to a local minimum, increases again to a local maximum, where it hereafter
goes to the minimum of the force. For this type of secondary load cycle, none or
barely any ringing was associated with the event. The occurrence of this event was
rarely observed.
• The second type of secondary load cycle shown in Figure 5b. It is described
by being very pronounced and with ringing response clearly associated with the
secondary load cycle.
This event was especially seen after wave forces of great
magnitude.
• The third type of secondary load cycle had a small increment on the force
slope, but a small local minimum before the cycle was not noted. An example of
this is shown in Figure 5c. In some cases, the secondary load cycles of these types
were barely visible, and it was thus hard to decide if they should be included in the
analysis. Ringing response associated with these events was also sometimes detected,
and therefore this type of secondary load cycle was included in the analysis.
• The fourth type of secondary load cycle is present after a slamming wave has
hit the structure shown in Figure 5d. In this situation, it is diﬃcult to isolate the
ringing response created from the secondary load cycle from the response created
from the main force peak. Most of these events were included in the analysis.

secondary load cycle occurrences.

5.2
Investigation of characteristic wave parameters
The aim was here to separate all irregular waves in the measurements and for each wave
calculate characteristic parameters and evaluate the presence of the secondary load cycle
in each wave. In the DeRisk data, the initial waves were smaller than the rest of the waves,
19
==PAGE:31==
so the ﬁrst 50 seconds were discarded. All zero down crossings, Tz , for the free surface
elevation, η, and the minimum value of η between each zero down crossing was found.
The distance between two minimum values was denoted as the trough-to-trough period,
Ttt. The waves were separated between the troughs, and in each trough-to-trough period
a maximum free surface elevation was found and denoted as ηm. There are small random
diﬀerences in using Ttt and Tz, which can be seen in the correlation plot in Figure 35a in
Appendix A.
For each separated wave, the local wave number, k, was calculated from the nonlinear
dispersion relation (Equation 1). The wave local number was non-dimensionalized by the
radius of the monopile, kR, The local wave steepness or slope of the waves were deﬁned
by the non-dimensional value kηm (Grue & Huseby, 2002; Chaplin et al., 1997). The local
Keulegan-Carpenter number was approximated to KC = πηm/R, deﬁned in Equation 2.
All separated waves were then registered in the (kηm,kR)-plane and divided into a group
of waves associated with secondary load cycles and a group of waves where no secondary
load cycle was observed. This is shown in Figure 6 for all waves from the experimental
DeRisk and Wave Loads data. Ring-shaped periodic patterns are seen for kR > 0.25.
Waves corresponding to these points are very short in time, and due to the resolution of
the time array, these waves have the same periods, and from the non-linear dispersion
relation they get the same wave number.
Waves with the same KC-number will fall on the same straight line, since kR/kηm =
KC/π. The line KC = π illustrates when the maximum amplitude equals the radius of the
monopile. For points below the line of KC = 10, the KC-number is higher, and the points
are dominated by drag forces (Chaplin et al., 1997). Secondary load cycles are observed
for KC number in range of 2.3 and 10.9, where ∼96% lies within KC = π and KC = 10.
According to the experiments done by Grue and Huseby (2002), the observations of the
secondary load cycle were for KC in range of 4-9. The results from both Grue and Huseby
(2002) and Chaplin et al. (1997) showed that a pronounced secondary load cycle would
appear when the steepness, kηm, exceeds a value of 0.3. The experiments from DeRisk
and Wave Loads revealed that secondary load cycles were observed for kηm > 0.05 and
that a large part of the cycles appeared for waves with a steepness of less than 0.3, where
a major part of these cycles were pronounced (see Figure 42 in Appendix A). Whether or
not the secondary load cycle is pronounced seems to be more a function of KC than of the
steepness. Increasing wave steepness is also seen to have an inﬂuence on the probability of
a secondary load cycle event, but only when kR is small. This implies that the amount of
secondary load cycles per wave increases with the KC-number. This is expected as the KC
number is proportional to ηm, and the secondary load cycles per wave, shown in Table 3,
was generally higher for extreme sea states.

5.4
Magnitude, period and starting time of the secondary load cycle
It has been noted that the occurrence of the secondary load cycles is highly dependent on
KC, Fr and kηm, and that the probability of this phenomenon increases by all these num-
23
==PAGE:35==
bers. The KC-number, especially, was seen to have a small area where the appearance was
uncertain. So far, this research has only classiﬁed waves to cause or not cause secondary
load cycles, while the magnitude and period of the cycle have been neglected. For a proper
investigation of the secondary load cycle, it is therefore important to not only know when
it happens but also how much the cycle contributes to the total force and for how long
it lasts. Various methods have been used to extract the magnitude of the secondary load
cycle from the total in-line force signal. The most common deﬁnition of the magnitude of
the secondary load cycle was proposed by Grue et al. (1993), and the deﬁnition was also
applied by Chaplin et al. (1997) and again by Grue and Huseby (2002). This deﬁnition is
as follows: Below the secondary load cycle in the force history, a line being tangent in two
points of the secondary load cycle is placed. A parallel line above the other line is placed,
so it is a tangent to an upper point of the secondary load cycle. The magnitude of the
secondary load cycle is then deﬁned as the vertical distance between the two lines. This
is shown as Fslc in Figure 9. Another method was seen in Riise et al. (2018) in which an
experimental measurement of the total bending moment was highpass-ﬁltered to extract
the structural part. This left two high peaks, where the ﬁrst was related to the main force
and the latter was related to the secondary load cycle. In this project, the deﬁnition from
Grue et al. (1993) is used.
In the sketch, Figure 9, it is also shown that the secondary load cycle peak is seen shortly
after the main force peak of the instant wave. The time between will be deﬁned as Ts,
where the ﬁrst touching point of the lower tangent line deﬁnes the beginning of a sec-
ondary load cycle. An alternative measure of the starting time, Tηm, is deﬁned as the time
it takes from the wave crest to pass until the secondary load cycle starts. The period of
the secondary load cycle, Tslc, will be deﬁned by the time between the two touching points
of the lower tangent line.
For all DeRisk and Wave Loads tests, the parameters Fslc, Tslc, Ts and Tηm will be mea-
sured for each noted secondary load cycle event. These parameters will be normalized and
correlations between these and the non-dimensional local wave parameters kηm, KC, Fr
and kR will be presented. At ﬁrst, the results are presented in two groups: one group for
all the DeRisk data and one group for all the Wave Loads data. The main diﬀerence here,
is that both the cylinder diameter, the water depth and the sea states are diﬀerent. The
correlations will be tested using student’s t-test (Brockhoﬀ, Møller, Andersen, Bacher, &
Christiansen, 2015). Hereafter the DeRisk test are grouped into water depths of 20 m and
33 m, and these are compared. Also, the 2D cases and 3D cases of the same sea state
from DeRisk are compared. ANOVA-tests (Brockhoﬀet al., 2015) will be used to test the
correlations for signiﬁcant diﬀerences between groups.

of the secondary load cycle is here denoted by Ts.

5.6
Concluding remarks
Four non-dimensional numbers were decided to characterise a local separated wave in
an irregular sea state. These numbers were: kηm, KC, Fr and kR. The probability for
secondary load cycle occurrences were seen to increase with both KC and Fr, and were
seen to appear for waves with KC > π and Fr > 0.2. It was decided that KC was the
best estimator for the probability of occurrence. A logistic regression model was proposed
in order to ﬁnd the occurrence probability based on KC. The logistic regressions were
seen to depend on the signiﬁcant wave height of the test case. Characteristic secondary
load cycle parameters were found for each local wave and normalized. This included the
magnitude, F ′
slc = Fslc/(ρgπR2/k), the period, Tslc/Ttt, the starting time measured from
the main force peak, Ts/Ttt, and measured from the crest, Tηm/Ttt. Their dependence
to the four non-dimensional wave characteristics were shown in correlation plots. It was
35
==PAGE:47==
found that F ′
slc, Tslc/Ttt and Tηm/Ttt were all mostly correlated to kηm and least correlated
to KC. Ts/Ttt was mostly correlated to kR. A t-test explained, that there was a signiﬁcant
diﬀerence between DeRisk and Wave Loads data for 4 correlation plots and ANOVA-tests
explained that there were signiﬁcant diﬀerences between water depth in the DeRisk data
for 3 correlation plots. Furthermore, a Weibull distribution were with high accuracy ﬁtted
to the measure Fslc/Fpp. Last, ringing was seen to increase with both F ′
slc and Tslc/Ttt,
however, the correlations were weak.

6
Modelling of the secondary load cycle
This section contains a description on how the linear wave theory was used to model
the wave forces from the experiment, and how the structural response was estimated.
Wave kinematics were modelled from the free surface elevation, η, either directly from
the experimental data or randomly generated from the JONSWAP spectrum. Three cases
were also modelled using non-linear kinematics. In subsection 5.3, the probabilities of
occurrence of the secondary load cycle were estimated based on the experimental data,
and here these probabilities were used to predict which waves the secondary load cycles
appeared with. The magnitude, Fslc, period, Tslc, and starting time, Ts or Tηm were also
predicted based on their experimental relationship to kηm, KC, Fr and kR. These relations
were shown in subsection 5.4. Predictions were made by either least squares regression or
by an artiﬁcial neural network, and diﬀerences between the methods were evaluated. The
prediction of Fslc, Tslc and Ts were used to model an additional force which was added to
the simulated forces. Reconstructions of the original force signals without secondary load
cycles were used as reference.
6.1
Reconstructing kinematics from experiment
The goal of this process was to reconstruct the force signals from the DeRisk experiments
using linear wave theory. Wave kinematics was created from the measured free surface
elevation. In case of 2D waves a single wave gauge close to the monopile was used, due
to the assumption, that the wave fronts only had small variations.
For all 2D waves,
WG9, shown in Figure 4 was used. For 3D cases both WG9 and WG19 were used and
averaged. The ﬁrst 50 seconds of the time series were discarded as the initial waves were
not representative for the sea state. The signal of the free surface elevation was ﬁrst low-
pass ﬁltered at 4fp, with fp being the peak frequency, to eliminate the vibrations from
wave gauges which would lead to sudden changes in the velocity and very sharp peaks
in the acceleration. The cut-oﬀfrequency of 4fp was chosen because it removed the high
frequent vibrations and reduced wave reﬂections from the cylinder without damaging the
main features of the signal. The signal of the free surface elevation was then transformed
to the frequency domain by using the FFT function in Matlab. The particle velocity, u,
was generated from the spectrum of η as shown in Equation 48. Likewise, the particle
acceleration, ut, was generated. For tests with a water depth of 33 m, both u and ut
were generated in discrete nodes with 1 m equally spaced diﬀerence from seabed to SWL.
For DeRisk test 09, a part of the time series of the original and the lowpass-ﬁltered free
surface elevation are shown in the top of Figure 19. The middle and bottom plot show u
and ut, respectively, where u and ut are calculated at SWL. The particle velocity is seen
to be in phase with the free surface elevation, where the particle acceleration is leading by
a phase shift of 90 degrees. Mark that the lowpass-ﬁltered free surface elevation does not
reach the same amplitude for the steep wave in Figure 19. This means that the simulated
values of ηm will be slightly underpredicted. By not ﬁltering the free surface elevation,
the sharp peaks could be captured, but the abrupt and sudden changes in η would lead
to extreme values of u and ut. These issues are occurring because linear wave theory is
applied on a non-linear experimental wave signal. Using this method is questionable, as
the free surface elevation was measured and hence contained non-linear eﬀects and these
eﬀects are not reproducible using linear wave theory.

Model containing non-linear kinematics.
The sea states from both the DeRisk and the Wave Loads experiments were all models
of extreme weather. The return period shown for the DeRisk experiments is an indicator
of how rare this sea state is. Waves in extreme sea states are governed by plenty of non-
linear eﬀects and therefore linear wave theory is not suﬃcient for describing those waves
(M´ehaut´e, 1976). Figure 20 shows the validity of the diﬀerent wave theories based on the

Wave Loads test cases are on the border between Stokes 2nd order and Stokes 3rd order
wave theory.
The linear wave theory is seen to be inadequate to reproduce the same number of steep

Evaluation of secondary load cycle model
An evaluation of the method secondary load cycle implementation using Equation 68 was
performed for the experimental reconstruction of DeRisk test case 09.
Here, the two
diﬀerent methods of amplitude approximation are evaluated. Likewise, the placement of
the cycle is evaluated based on whether the model was using Ts or Tηm as a starting time

6.10.1
Amplitude approximation
In subsection 6.8, the adding function, Fadd, in Equation 68, was deﬁned, and the ampli-
tudes were approximated in two diﬀerent ways. Afterwards, the secondary load cycles were
added in the wave simulations and force reconstructions as the one in Figure 27 was pro-
duced. From the reproduced force signal, the secondary load cycles were again measured
by ﬁtting lines to the cycle, which is explained in subsubsection 5.4.1. From the line mea-
surement, the magnitude and period of the secondary load cycle, were estimated. These
values were then compared to the values ﬁrst predicted. The relative diﬀerence between
the calculated and predicted magnitude, Fslc, and period, Tslc, are shown in Figure 29
as histograms. The relative diﬀerence between the measured and predicted force is a lot
larger when using amplitude approximation 1 than 2. This is expected as approximation 2
adjusts the amplitude to match the predicted Fslc. The error of amplitude approximation
2 is caused because the line used in order to add the force deviates from the actual tangent
line. This is seen in Figure 26b.
Both amplitude approximations are also seen to induce an error in the period. The rel-
ative error is for all cycles negative, showing that the measured period is smaller than
the predicted period. This is again because of the discrepancy between the line used to
implement the cycle and the tangent line used to measure it, shown in Figure 26b. As
Fadd(τ (i)) = 0 and Fadd(τ (i) + T (i)
slc) = 0, then the diﬀerence between the force, F and
F + Fadd close to these boundaries is small. This results in the touching points of the
tangent lines to be closer, than the two points used to add the force. Amplitude approxi-
mation 1 gives a larger error than amplitude approximation 2. When using approximation
2, the mean error for Tslc is greater than the mean error for Fslc. This is because this
amplitude approximation was optimizing Fslc.
It is clear that amplitude approximation 1 is inadequate as it has a large systematic error
for both Fslc and Tslc. Using this method would result in underestimations of both param-
eters, and the only advantage of using this method is, that it could estimate and model
secondary load cycles directly from the free surface elevation. Amplitude approximation
2 gives a smaller error on Fslc, but it still gives a large systematic error on Tslc, making it
the better method of the two and were therefore used for all calculations.
Using amplitude approximation 2 gives some limitations, as it is not possible to extract
the correct secondary load cycle parameters from the simulation. For this project, it was
not relevant to extract the parameters from the simulations, and the errors of Fslc and
Tslc occurring from implementing Fadd were tolerated. An alternative method of imple-
menting Fadd would be to solve iteratively for Fslc, Tslc and perhaps Tηm and for each
iteration update the tangent line and these parameters until the true tangent line would
be obtained. This method was not tried.

7.1
Comparing fatigue of all simulations
The simulations were to be compared through a single number, representative for the sim-
ulation: the life-time equivalent moment, Meq, deﬁned in Equation 51. First, cyclic loads
from the total moment time series were counted using the Rainﬂow Counting algorithm
presented in (subsection 3.9). The used Rainﬂow algorithm script was created by Nieslony
(2020). Resulting Rainﬂow histograms are shown in Figure 30, where the left column con-
tains results for test 09, and the right column contains results for test 20. Two histograms
are shown together in each plot, where one represents counted cycles for a simulation with
secondary load cycles and the other is for a reference simulation.
In general, there is not much diﬀerence between the secondary load cycle simulation and
the reference, but there is a tendency that the secondary load cycle simulation has more
counts for lower moments, while it has fewer counts for higher moments. This indicates
that the magnitude for some of the moment cycles has been reduced.
The monopile used in test 20 was scaled down to a water depth of 20 meters, which in-
creased the natural frequency by 65% and yielded a higher number of cycles with low
frequency. The lower depth also caused the static and dynamic moments to be smaller.
According to this, there were more cycles of lower moments for test 20 than for test 09,
and this is the reason for the major diﬀerence between the two.
The equivalent moment in each case was calculated with a W¨ohler exponent of m = 4
and the number of equivalent cycles of neq = 107. These parameters are common for oﬀ-
shore foundations (Blasques & Natarajan, 2013). The simulated time, Tsim was the total
full-scale time of the simulation. The lifetime, Tlife was estimated to 25 years, which is
a realistic design lifetime for oﬀshore wind turbines. The lifetime equivalent moment was
calculated in each case.
The simulations containing secondary load cycles were denoted Meq,slc and the reference
was denoted Meq,ref. The ratios of these numbers are presented for each test case in Fig-
ure 31 for all simulations. It is seen that all ratios are below 1, meaning that secondary
load cycles added to the signal were reducing the equivalent moment. For test cases on
33 m (test 02 to 12), Meq,slc is between 95% and 99.5% of Meq,ref, and for test cases at
20 m depth (test 14 to 24), the ratios are between 78% and 95%. For simulations with
experimentally reconstructed waves and waves from OceanWave3D the ratio was smaller
than for linear waves.
As the secondary load cycle was expected to increase the equivalent moment, it was not the
case for these simulations. The reason for this was ﬁrst and foremost, that the secondary
load cycles did not excite the natural frequency of the monopile used for the simulations
because the secondary load cycle frequencies fslc = 1/Tslc were around 4 times the natural
frequency. The additional force cycle acting in the positive direction at a time where the
rest of the force was negative resulted in a small damping to the force and moment. The
small damping decreased the amplitude of the moment cycles happening right after the
secondary load cycles. An example on how the moment amplitudes depend on the position

ondary load cycles were added resulting in more damping eﬀects for these simulations.
More secondary load cycles were also predicted in simulations using linear reconstructed
waves or nonlinear waves compared to linear waves. According to this, Meq,slc, is consis-

Fatigue loads for down-scaled wind turbines
In the previous analysis, it was seen how the addition of the secondary load cycle to the

7.3
Concluding remarks
The result in Figure 34 shows, that for this model the secondary load cycle starts to
become critical when the frequency of it is less than 2.5 times the natural frequency of the
structure. It was also shown that for simulations with secondary load cycle frequencies
more than 2.5 times the natural frequency, the eﬀect of the secondary load cycle was acting
like a small damper instead of exciting the structure. This was because the force of the
secondary load cycle is a positive increase, where the net force is negative. This made a
slight reduction to the force and moment amplitudes and thus a lower equivalent moment.
This was seen for all simulated cases for DeRisk test cases of 33 m and 20 m.
According to this model, the secondary load cycle’s eﬀect on the ﬁrst natural frequency
of the wind turbine will not become an issue for future monopile foundations, as their size
is expected to increase. However, higher natural frequencies of the secondary load cycle
may still be excited, and the eﬀect on the resonance in jacket structures is still in need of
investigation.

8
Conclusion
An investigation of test results for irregular waves passing a model monopile was com-
pleted in order to discover secondary load cycle events in the force history. A total of 78
hours (full scale) of test results were examined, with 2D and 3D waves for water depths
of 20 m 33 m with a monopile diameter of 7 m, and 2D wave for water depths of 42.s
m and 52.4 m with a monopile of 6 m. A total of 1800 secondary load cycle events were
analysed, whereas 1599 was used in modelling. The occurrences of secondary load cycles
were increasing with the local KC-number and Fr-number, with KC being the most im-
portant. A logistic regression was used to model the occurrence probability based on the
local KC-number in irregular wave simulations.
The ratio of the secondary load cycle magnitude to the peak-to-peak force was seen
to follow a Weibull distribution of high accuracy. Three characteristic non-dimensional
parameters were used to describe the secondary load cycle: A normalized magnitude,
F ′
slc = Fslc/(ρgπR2/k), a period ratio, Tslc/Ttt, and a starting time ratio, Ts/Ttt or Tηm/Ttt.
It was found that Tηm was the most precise estimate for starting time and that both F ′
slc,
Tslc/Ttt and Tηm/Ttt were most dependent on kηm, but also dependent on Fr and kR. A
multi-variate least squares regression used the non-dimensional numbers kηm, Fr and kR
as input parameters to predict F ′
slc, Tslc/Ttt and Tηm/Ttt. Likewise, correlations between
the kηm, KC, Fr and kR, and F ′
slc, Tslc/Ttt and Tηm/Ttt were used to train a neural network
model. The least squares model and neural network model were very similar in predicting
the secondary load cycle parameters.
Wave simulations were done, and the kinematics were either reconstructed directly from
experimental free surface elevation, generated randomly using linear wave theory, or gen-
erated using OceanWave3D. The secondary load cycle was modelled with a single peak
of a sinusoidal function, where the amplitude and period were predicted from the data-
driven models. A simple force model with 1 degree of freedom was used to model static
and dynamic forces and bending moments on the monopile. The predicted secondary load
cycles were here included in the wave forces and wave moment. It was found that the
secondary load cycle did not have much impact on the dynamics of the structure, since
the frequency of the secondary load cycle was 3.5 to 5 times larger than the natural fre-
quency. Equivalent moments were calculated for each simulation, and it was found that
all simulations including secondary load cycles had equivalent moments between 80% and
99.5% of the equivalent moment of reference simulations. This concluded that the addi-
tion of secondary load cycles damped the structural response because it acted like a small
positive force, when the net force was negative.
The monopile and the waves from the simulation of 33 m water depth were scaled down
in order to increase the natural frequency of the monopile and investigate the limit at
which the secondary load cycle would result in resonant response.
It was found that
the equivalent moment for simulations including secondary load cycles would be higher
than reference simulations for depths below 12 m, corresponding to the secondary load
cycle frequency being 2.5 times the natural frequency. When the secondary load cycle
frequency was about 2 times the natural frequency, the equivalent moment was increased
by up to 15% of the simulations where secondary load cycles were not added. Designing
wind turbines with natural frequencies close to the secondary load cycle frequency could
then lead to fatigue failure and a shorter lifetime than what they were designed for. As
wind turbines are expected to increase in size, the down-scaling done in this project is not
relevant for future monopile foundations, and according to this model, the secondary load
cycle will not be of any risk for the ﬁrst natural frequency of the next-generation monopile
64
==PAGE:76==
foundations. However, there are still reasons for investigating the eﬀect of the secondary
load cycle on higher harmonics of the structure, and the eﬀect on jacket foundations.
9
Future work
This section contains ideas and reﬂections on how this subject can be studied further, and
how limitations of the models used in this project can be solved by replacing them with
more advanced models.
9.1
Finite element model of wind turbine
In the numerical analysis in this project, the model wind turbine only had one degree of
freedom, which was seen to be too simplistic. As the frequency of the secondary load cycle
was up to 5 times the natural frequency, a wind turbine with higher order frequencies
and mode shapes is preferred. This model could either be made from experimental data
or obtained from ﬁnite element methods. A ﬁnite element model wind turbine could be
designed, and the natural frequencies and mode shapes could be found from a free response
calculation. By using ﬁnite element methods, the wind turbine could also be designed
in various sizes and the monopile designed for diﬀerent water depths. In this project,
structural scaling was used in order to increase the natural frequency of the monopile.
This resulted in scaled structures with low physical meaning. With a ﬁnite element model,
geometric scaling can be avoided, and the natural frequencies can be manipulated by e.g.
making the monopile narrower without making it shorter or by decreasing the thickness
of steel in the monopile.
9.2
Aeroelastic tools
Throughout this project, only an idle wind turbine was considered. A next step would be
to include the wind speed. The wind speed could be implemented as an external load in
this model, however, there is an increasing number of commercial aeroelastic tools which
could be used. HAWC2 is a state-of-the-art aeroelastic model developed by DTU. An
oﬀshore wind turbine model on a monopile foundation can be designed and all loads and
dynamic responses for the wind turbine can be calculated. The secondary load cycles can
be precalculated from the data-driven model and included as an external point force in
the model. In this way, the eﬀect of the secondary load cycle can be investigated in a
wind-wave condition and the dynamics loads can be studied for more degrees of freedom.
9.3
Regression models
Both a least squares model and a neural network were used as regression models to predict
characteristic secondary load cycle parameters from non-dimensional wave parameters.
The precision for the two models was similar. An improvement could be made to the
neural network by adding more input parameters. The normalized secondary load cycle
magnitude, F ′
slc, exhibited great variance which was also seen to be a function of the wave
parameters. This variance was, however, not captured by any of the models. As a result,
the regression models made underpredictions of the secondary load cycle magnitude. A
neural network model including the variances could be trained in order to estimate the
variance better and in that way reach the magnitudes that were else underpredicted.
65
==PAGE:77==
9.4
Secondary load cycle prediction
In subsection 6.10, the secondary load cycle function, Fadd (Equation 68), was evaluated.
It was a cosine function, with a vertical translation, and it had three parameters: A start-
ing time, τ, governed by Tηm, an amplitude, A, governed by Fslc and a period, Tslc. It was
seen, that when a secondary load cycle was modelled with a set of predicted parameters,
the same parameters were not obtained when they were measured from the simulated force
signal in the way deﬁned in Figure 9. For this project, the errors were noticed, but the
modelling and implementation of secondary load cycles were not improved much. Espe-
cially the errors for Tslc were critical, as the measurements were always smaller than the
values predicted.
The secondary load cycle function could be improved by letting the amplitude, frequency
and phase be variables and by estimating them iteratively so that they match the mea-
surements. This could be done as follows:
1) Predict Tηm, Tslc and Fslc and use them to generate and initial secondary load cycle
function Fadd (Equation 68) and add it to the wave force simulation.
2) Measure Fslc, Tslc and Tηm from the deﬁnition in Figure 9.
3) Calculate an error between the measured and predicted variables, and change the
amplitude, frequency and phase of the secondary load cycle function to account for
the errors.
4) Do step 2 and 3 again until the errors are below a threshold.
This addition could make the secondary load cycle function more precise, and it would
also be possible to measure secondary load cycles from a simulation.

Statistical test results
This appendix contains a small description of the t-test and the ANOVA-test. The data

The two-way t-test is used to compare the means of two independent samples and decide
if there is a signiﬁcant diﬀerence.
A null hypothesis for the test is, that there is no
signiﬁcant diﬀerent. Based on a critical value governed by a signiﬁcance level and the
degrees of freedom, the null hypothesis is either accepted or rejected. The two-way t-test
calculates the t-value based on the two populations:

The n-way ANOVA-test can be used for comparing the means of samples which belong

of squares, SS(g), within each group, and compare it to the total sum of squares, SST.
