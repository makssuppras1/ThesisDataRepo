Abstract
Cement production is a major industrial emitter of greenhouse gases, motivating rapid uptake
of Solid Recovered Fuel (SRF) and other alternative fuels. Yet high SRF substitution is
constrained by fluid, solid hydrodynamics in calciners where cohesive raw meal (Geldart C)
and heterogeneous SRF must co fluidize without excessive segregation or fines loss. This
thesis couples laboratory hydrodynamics with a techno economic analysis (TEA) to identify
operating regimes and policy/market conditions that enable high SRF use.
Experimentally, a lab scale pulsed fluidized bed was built to co fluidize cement raw meal
with secondary particles (spherical plastics/steel, biomass like rice/corn/coffee, and industrial
SRF pellets). We varied total gas flow, pulsation frequency and duty ratio, and quantified
vertical hold up via layerwise sampling after 60 min runs. Particle properties dominated
vertical distribution: denser/compact particles accumulated near the distributor, while low
density/large or oblate particles preferentially occupied mid upper layers under identical
aeration.
Pulsation details moderately shaped mixing: 10 Hz pulsing or adding a small continuous base
flow reduced extreme bottom accumulation versus a 2.5 Hz fully pulsed baseline, whereas long
"off" periods concentrated fuel just above the distributor. Excessive flow (e.g., 40 L min−1)
induced jetting and remained bottom heavy; a mid range pulsed flow (about 25 L min−1) best
balanced mixing without bypass. Steady state profiles converged after approximately 60 min.
The TEA compares a coal baseline with two action cases on a 1 Mt/ clinker line across
Germany (± EU ETS) and India (brownfield/greenfield): AC1 (only SRF heat substitution)
and AC2 (AC1 + pyrolysis oil co product). Metrics include NPV, IRR, payback, coal
displaced, and CO2 avoided, with sensitivities to TSR, SRF price, ETS price, and FuelFlex
CAPEX.
SRF alone yields large environmental gains but negative NPVs under current assumptions
(e.g., in Germany, moving from 25% to 100% SRF avoids ∼56.5 kt CO2 yr−1 yet NPV
remains ≤−e222 M even with ETS). In contrast, adding pyrolysis oil flips project economics
at high TSR: in Germany, NPV shifts from ≈−e264 M (AC1) to +e86 M (AC2) without
iii
==PAGE:5==
ETS, and to +e129 M with ETS at 100% SRF; in India, brownfields reach positive NPV
beyond 70% SRF and greenfields turn positive at full substitution (+e39 M).
Breakeven oil prices fall sharply with higher TSR (e.g., ∼e300/t in Germany w/o ETS;
∼e225/t in India brownfield at 100% SRF), while CAPEX mainly shifts NPV/IRR without
changing breakeven oil.
Overall, the work identifies (i) hydrodynamic "windows" that minimize segregation in raw
meal/SRF beds and (ii) system conditions, especially co product monetization and carbon
pricing, under which high SRF substitution becomes investment grade. These insights provide
actionable guidance for tuning pulsation/flow and for policy or market design to scale SRF
in cement.

Introduction
1.1
Environmental, Economic, and Policy Considerations
1.1.1
Environmental Impact of Cement Production
Cement production is one of the largest industrial sources of anthropogenic CO2 emissions,
accounting for roughly 8% of global CO2 and about a quarter of all industrial direct CO2
emissions [1]–[3]. In modern dry-process plants, most calcination occurs in the precalciner
upstream of the rotary kiln; the precalciner typically supplies on the order of 60% of the total
kiln-system fuel and achieves about 90% calcination of the raw meal before sintering [4], [5].
Process (calcination) CO2 and fuel-combustion CO2 dominate cement’s footprint; in 2019,
global cement emitted ∼2.3 Gt CO2, with roughly 1.4 Gt from calcination, 0.6 Gt from kiln
fuels, and 0.3 Gt from electricity [6]. Figure 1 shows a schematic of a modern dry-process
cement plant with its main emission points.
Figure 1: Schematic of a modern dry-process cement plant and its main emission points [7].
Beyond CO2, cement manufacturing also emits conventional air pollutants: sulfur dioxide
(SO2), nitrogen oxides (NOx), particulate matter (PM), volatile organic compounds (VOCs),
and other species [8], [9]. Thermal NOx formation dominates in kilns, with flue-gas NO
comprised of about 95% NO and 5% NO2 under high-temperature conditions [9]. In 2019, the
global cement industry’s SO2 emissions exceeded those of the entire EU-27 economy by 3%
[10]. These co-pollutant burdens, together with CO2, make cement a classic "hard-to-abate"
sector [2], [3].
Demand for cement has plateaued or declined in many developed economies, while infras-
tructure build-out in developing regions drives continued growth in output [1], [3], [11]. This
divergence shapes decarbonization pathways: for example, Germany and other EU producers
have achieved high substitution of alternative fuels (AF) for coal - the EU-average thermal
1
==PAGE:7==
substitution reached ∼58% in 2022 and several plants exceed 80% [12]–[14] - supported by
stringent waste and energy policies. By contrast, India’s average thermal substitution has
historically been low (a few percent), though road maps aim to increase it as supply chains
and permits improve [15], [16]. In effect, developed regions focus on retrofitting existing
plants with the latest technologies, while developing regions can adopt best-available designs
in new capacity [1], [3].
Despite these challenges, many mitigation options exist. Energy-efficiency improvements,
lower clinker-to-cement ratios via supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs), and fuel
switching to lower-carbon or waste-derived fuels can all reduce emissions. Breakthrough
routes , such as carbon capture and storage (CCS), oxy-fuel combustion, electrified calciners,
and novel clinker substitutes , are also under development [1], [2]. Each approach targets
different sources of emissions (process vs. energy) and faces its own readiness, cost, and
regional barriers.
1.1.2
Economic Viability of Alternative Fuel Utilization
Integrating alternative fuels such as Solid Recovered Fuel (SRF)1 and Refuse-Derived Fuel
(RDF)2 can reduce direct CO2 emissions while diverting waste from landfill [1], [13]. SRF is
of particular interest because its production standards yield a more consistent fuel quality
(higher calorific value and lower impurities) than generic RDF, making it more suitable
for stable co-processing in cement kilns. In Europe, alternative fuel substitution recently
surpassed 50%, and individual plants have demonstrated technical substitutions above 80%
[14], [18]. Globally, however, fossil fuels still supplied about 90% of kiln thermal energy in
2022 [2], indicating substantial headroom for diversification.
The economics of alternative fuel adoption hinge on local waste policies and logistics. In
regions with landfill bans or high landfill levies and established waste-to-fuel supply chains,
alternative fuels can lower fuel costs and hedge against fossil fuel price volatility. However,
enabling co-processing also requires investment in new feeding systems, fuel preparation, and
emissions controls. These retrofit costs and operational expenditures can be substantial [13],
[14], [19]. As a result, alternative fuel utilization remains slower than potential: higher effective
costs compared to coal and inertia in market frameworks continue to limit competitiveness.
Strong policy support (e.g. carbon pricing, subsidies, or mandates) is often needed to bridge
the remaining gap and accelerate alternative fuel growth.
1.1.3
Policy and Regulatory Frameworks
Effective policy frameworks use a mix of instruments to address costs, quality, and quantity
of cement decarbonization. For example, carbon pricing (taxes or emissions trading) raises
the cost of fossil fuels, improving the relative economics of SRF; performance-based cement

standards (e.g. required strength or composition) can incentivize innovation in low-carbon
mixes; and green public procurement can guarantee markets for low-carbon cement. Special
support (grants or revenue guarantees) for first-of-a-kind projects (such as CCS hubs or
FuelFlex installations) can help overcome early cost hurdles. These measures must also
address carbon leakage risks: mechanisms like the EU’s Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism

In parallel, air-quality regulations and best-available-technology (BAT) guidance constrain
NOx, SO2, and PM emissions, which must be coordinated with alternative fuel use and clinker
reduction to avoid trade-offs. For instance, co-processing SRF typically increases chlorine or
alkali inputs, which can affect cement quality and furnace equipment. Policymakers must
therefore ensure that emission limits and fuel standards (e.g. for alternative fuel purity)
are aligned with decarbonization goals. In practice, different economies emphasize different
levers: Germany’s cement sector relies heavily on strict waste bans and high carbon prices,
whereas India’s roadmap highlights capacity-building for waste fuel logistics. Aligning these
environmental, economic, and policy levers is essential to scale up near-term options like SRF
while also maturing deeper decarbonization technologies [1]–[3].
1.2
FuelFlex and Segregation
To expand the use of alternative fuels, FLSmidth developed FuelFlex® Pyrolyzer, a compact
pulsed fluidized-bed unit integrated into the kiln preheater. In FuelFlex, hot cement raw
meal from the preheater is used to dry and pyrolyze incoming SRF in a near-isothermal,
pulsed-fluidized bed [20]. The sensible heat of the raw meal rapidly devolatilizes the SRF,
producing combustible gases and char that are then routed to the calciner. Because the
fuel conversion is staged upstream, the material entering the calciner is dry and highly
reactive, allowing plants to substitute well above 80% of calciner fuel with SRF in practice
[20]. This contrasts with direct SRF firing, where moisture and volatiles can suppress the
flame and leave residual char or cause accretions. The FuelFlex approach yields more uniform
combustion conditions and reduced peak temperatures, which in turn lowers thermal NOx
formation [20]. Furthermore, the SRF fraction is typically biogenic or waste-derived, so every
ton of SRF used translates into a ton of fossil CO2 avoided (subject to sustainability criteria)
[21].
Despite these advantages, operating FuelFlex involves challenges. The pulsed fluidized bed
must handle a mixture of fine, cohesive raw meal and heterogeneous SRF fragments. In a
steady flow, such mixtures would tend to segregate (with lighter or larger SRF particles rising
to the top) and possibly form channels or collapses. FuelFlex uses pulsation (periodic on/off
airflow) to keep the bed expanded and well-mixed, but this requires careful tuning of the
pulsation frequency and duty cycle. Non-uniform SRF feed or extreme fuel heterogeneity
could still lead to uneven conversion or local defluidization. Additionally, high gas velocities
and particle abrasion can cause wear in the reactor internals. Managing these fluid-dynamic
issues-segregation, mixing, and elutriation of fines-is therefore central to realizing FuelFlex’s
benefits. Proper design and operation ensure that SRF and raw meal remain well-contacted,
maximizing fuel conversion and delivering the expected NOx and CO2 reductions [22]–[24].

2
Objectives and Scope
The overarching research focus is summarized as:
How can the utilization of Solid Recovered Fuel (SRF) in cement production be optimized to
significantly reduce reliance on fossil fuels while maintaining process efficiency and economic
viability using different policy tools?
Approach and methodology.
This thesis adopts a multidisciplinary approach combining laboratory-scale experiments,
fluidization analysis, and system-level modeling. Experimentally, we study a pulsed fluidized-
bed system that co-fluidizes fine cement raw meal (Geldart Group C) with a secondary particles.
We systematically vary the total gas flow rate, pulsation frequency, and pulsed/continuous
flow ratio, and we tune the open/closed duty cycle to stabilize the bed. These tests generate
regime maps of fluidization quality, particle segregation, and mixing.
At the system level, we build a techno-economic framework to evaluate SRF deployment
with and without FuelFlex. We compare scenarios of a conventional kiln (fossil baseline) to
variants using SRF in the calciner, and to cases with FuelFlex-enhanced co-processing. The
model quantifies investment costs (retrofits, feeders, emissions controls), operational costs
(fuel, electricity), and benefits (reduced coal purchases, waste tipping revenues, carbon credits
under schemes like the EU ETS). We examine how key drivers, SRF supply prices, carbon
prices, retrofit CAPEX, and local waste policy, affect the business case. This framework
is applied to regional case studies (Germany and India) to capture how different market
structures and regulations influence feasibility. By linking the fluidization insights to economic
outcomes, the analysis reveals which technological and policy combinations enable high SRF
substitution in practice.
By coupling fluidization experiments with techno-economic analysis, this work provides
actionable guidance for optimizing SRF use in cement. The combined evidence identifies
fluidization regimes that maximize SRF conversion, quantifies the conditions under which
those regimes are cost-competitive, and outlines the enabling policies needed to translate
laboratory performance into industrial practice. In doing so, the thesis advances both the
process-level understanding and the deployment-ready pathways needed to reduce fossil fuel
dependence in cement production.
2.1
Experimental Analysis of Fluidization Dynamics
In the experimental component, we investigate how hydrodynamic behavior in a lab-scale
pulsed fluidized bed affects secondary particles conversion when co-processed with cement
raw meal. The key focus is on segregation and mixing in a binary bed of fine raw meal and a
light secondary solid phase.
A purpose-built fluidization setup is used to systematically study operating conditions. We
vary:
4
==PAGE:10==
• Gas velocity. Total superficial gas velocity is adjusted to map its effect on bed
expansion, bubbling behavior, and particle segregation between secondary particles and
raw meal.
• Pulsation parameters. The pulsation frequency and the pulsed-to-continuous flow
ratio are tuned to enhance fluidization of the cohesive raw meal. We also vary the
open/closed time fraction of each pulsation cycle to find stable operation.
• Bed composition. Different secondary particles-simulant materials (light, irregular
solids) are co-fluidized with a standard cement raw mix to represent a range of fuel
properties.
For each test condition, we measure the degree of mixing (uniformity of distribution). These
outcomes are used to infer the expected SRF conversion efficiency in a FuelFlex like system.
2.2
System-Level Economic and Policy Analysis
The system analysis evaluates the broader economic and policy context for SRF integration.
We compare scenarios involving SRF substitution against a business-as-usual coal-fired
baseline. Key elements include:
• Techno-economic modeling.
We estimate the capital and operating costs for
retrofits (e.g. FuelFlex installation, SRF handling), as well as savings from reduced coal
consumption and potential revenues (waste tipping fees, sale of carbon credits). Fuel
price differentials and carbon prices are varied in sensitivity studies.
• Policy assessment. We identify which policies can bridge the cost gap for SRF. This
includes carbon pricing mechanisms, waste disposal regulations (landfill taxes/ban),
and incentives or mandates for fuel switching. We consider how policies might differ in
effect on quantities (e.g. required AF share), on costs (e.g. subsidies/taxes), and on
product standards (e.g. cement performance requirements).
• Regional case studies. Using Germany and India as examples, we examine how local
market structures (fuel supply chains, power mix, waste policies) affect SRF viability.
For instance, strong waste diversion policies in Germany give SRF a low or negative net
cost (via tipping fees), whereas in India the developing waste market makes SRF supply
more expensive. We explore how instrument designs (carbon price levels, blending
mandates, or waste policy) could enable high SRF adoption in each context.
This analysis clarifies the investment and policy conditions under which high SRF usage
(with or without FuelFlex) is economically and operationally attractive.

3.2
Overview of Fluidization and Its Industrial Relevance
Fluidization is a hydrodynamic phenomenon in which a bed of solid particles is suspended
and behaves like a fluid due to the upward flow of a liquid or gas. At low fluid velocities, the
particles remain fixed in position while the fluid percolates through the voids (a packed or
fixed bed). As the fluid velocity increases, a critical point is reached, the minimum fluidization
velocity (Umf), at which the upward drag and buoyant forces counterbalance the weight of
the particles, causing them to lift and become suspended in the flow. This marks the onset
of fluidization, as illustrated in Figure 2 [25], [26].
7
==PAGE:13==
Figure 2: At minimum fluidization velocity, the drag and buoyant forces balance the weight
of the particles, leading to the onset of fluidization [25].
Beyond this point, various fluidization regimes emerge depending on the fluid velocity, particle
characteristics, and bed geometry. These include bubbling, slugging, turbulent, fast, and
pneumatic (or conveying) fluidization, as shown in Figure 3. Each regime exhibits distinct
flow patterns and operational characteristics suited to specific industrial applications. For
example, bubbling fluidization occurs just above Umf in gas-solid systems, where gas forms
discrete bubbles that rise through the bed. This regime is widely used in catalytic cracking
reactors in petroleum refining [27]. Slugging fluidization, typically observed in narrower
columns, involves the formation of large gas pockets that periodically expel bed material;
while useful in certain batch operations, slugging is generally avoided due to its inherent
instability. Turbulent and fast fluidization regimes involve more vigorous mixing and particle
entrainment, preferred for processes requiring enhanced heat/mass transfer, such as fluid
catalytic cracking (FCC) and biomass gasification [25], [26], [28]. At even higher velocities,
particles become fully entrained in the gas stream in circulating fluidized beds (CFBs). CFBs
are widely applied in large-scale coal combustion (with in-situ desulfurization via sorbent
addition) due to their high efficiency and low emissions [28].
Fluidized beds offer several advantages, including uniform temperature profiles, excellent gas
solid contact, and scalability. Consequently, they find applications across various industries:
in chemical engineering for catalytic reactions like polyethylene and acrylonitrile production;
in energy systems for combustion and gasification of solid fuels; in pharmaceuticals for coating,
granulation, and drying [29]; and in environmental engineering for wastewater treatment and
flue-gas cleanup.

FuelFlex Pyrolyzer offers several targeted advantages within the cement production process.

traditional limits caused by incomplete combustion and operational instabilities. In particular,
plants have reported raising calciner fuel substitution well above 80% without adverse

minimize NOx formation by creating more reducing conditions during the initial pyrolysis

system, which in turn can reduce downtime and maintenance.

reliable operation. Achieving optimal pulsed fluidization is critical: if the pulsation frequency

Handling extremely fine, cohesive raw meal (Geldart C powder) alongside heterogeneous

Pyrolyzer) with the main kiln and calciner operations [28]. It is also important to consider
the net energy balance of the pre-treatment: any auxiliary energy consumption should not
outweigh the gains. For instance, a study on biodrying of waste before incineration found
that the added electricity for drying negated environmental benefits (yielding slightly higher
net CO2 emissions and cost than direct combustion) [32]. FuelFlex Pyrolyzer mitigates this

3.3.3
Role of Fluidization Dynamics in Fuel-Flex Systems
The performance of FuelFlex Pyrolyzer is intrinsically linked to its fluidization dynamics.
Efficient operation requires careful tuning of parameters such as particle size distribution
of the bed material, gas velocity and pulsation frequency, and the geometry of the reactor
vessel. The pulsed-fluidization approach is employed specifically to overcome the well-known
tendency of cement raw meal often behaves as Geldart Group C powders to form channeling
or plugs under steady flow [33]. By periodically interrupting the gas flow, pulsation breaks up
incipient agglomerates and channels, thus maintaining the raw meal in a fluidized state and
ensuring intimate contact with the SRF particles. This robust mixing and quasi-homogeneous
temperature distribution are critical for ensuring the SRF is fully pyrolyzed. Any deviation
from the finely-tuned operating regime (for instance, if pulsation frequency is too high,
the bed may behave as if under near-steady conditions; if too low or with too long an
off-phase, the bed could collapse and stratify) can lead to incomplete fuel conversion. Thus,
an understanding of fluidization behavior—segregation tendencies between SRF and raw
meal, mixing efficiency, and particle entrainment (elutriation) rates—is vital [34], [35]. These
hydrodynamic factors directly influence the extent of SRF’s thermal conversion and the
quality of fuel gas delivered to the calciner.

fluidization theory can be extended to account for non-idealities like cohesion when dealing
with challenging materials.
3.4.2
Geldart Classification: Concepts and Relevance
Not all particles fluidize in the same manner. Geldart’s classic classification (Figure 5)
categorizes particles into four groups based on their size and density difference relative to the
fluid, providing a heuristic for expected fluidization behavior [40]. The groups are:
• Group A: Aeratable powders (typically 30–100 µm, low particle density). These
particles fluidize easily and exhibit smooth expansion; gas bubbles form only when the
gas velocity significantly exceeds Umf. Many catalyst particles (e.g., FCC catalyst) fall
into this group.
• Group B: Sand-like particles (roughly 40–500 µm, moderate density). They fluidize
readily but tend to form vigorous bubbles immediately above Umf. Silica sand and
many granular chemicals are Group B.
• Group C: Cohesive fine particles (typically < 30–40 µm or very low density). These
powders are difficult to fluidize; interparticle forces (van der Waals, electrostatic, or
capillary if moisture is present) dominate over weight, causing particles to stick together.
Group C powders often form clumps or channeling instead of fluidizing homogeneously.
Flour, talc, filter dust, and cement raw meal are examples. Special measures (mechanical
agitation, vibration, pulsation, etc.) are usually required to fluidize Group C materials.
• Group D: Spoutable or large/denser particles (generally > 1 mm or very high density).
13
==PAGE:19==
They may be too heavy to fluidize uniformly; instead, they are often processed in
spouted beds or other specialized reactors. Examples include coarse coal or biomass
chunks and some metal ores.
Figure 5: Geldart’s classification of particles into Group A, B, C, and D, as a function of
particle size and density difference between solid and fluid [41]
The Geldart classification is highly useful for anticipating fluidization characteristics. For
instance, knowing that cement raw meal is a Group C powder explains why a standard
fluidized bed might perform poorly (severe channeling and agglomeration) with this material
and justifies the use of enhancements like pulsation in FuelFlex Pyrolyzer. Conversely, an
alternative fuel that is shredded into Group B-sized particles would be expected to fluidize
and mix much more easily with the bed material. In general, applying the classification helps
in reactor design and scale-up by guiding what auxiliary measures (if any) are needed to
achieve stable fluidization for a given particle type [25], [40].
3.4.3
Challenges in Fluidizing Geldart C Particles
Geldart Group C particles present unique challenges due to their strong interparticle cohesion.
When attempting to fluidize such fine powders (e.g., cement kiln dust, fly ash, or alumina
fines), one often observes poor fluidization quality characterized by: minimal bed expansion,
the formation of stable channels through which gas jets upward, large agglomerates or "powder
clumps" that quiver but do not separate, and erratic, large pressure fluctuations. The cohesive
forces (van der Waals forces between particles, sometimes enhanced by static electricity or
moisture films) in Group C can greatly exceed the force of gravity on a single particle, so the
particles tend to move in unison as aggregates rather than independently.
Practically, this means the minimum fluidization velocity is ill-defined or very close to the
velocity that leads to entrainment, making it hard to find a stable operating regime. To
14
==PAGE:20==
fluidize Geldart C powders, special strategies are needed to disrupt interparticle bonds. Gas
pulsation (periodic surging of flow) is one such strategy: the alternating high-velocity bursts
and low-velocity intervals can break apart incipient channels or clusters, giving the particles
brief moments of weightlessness that promote re-distribution. Other approaches include
mechanical vibration of the vessel, introduction of mechanical stirrers or flow baffles, addition
of flow conditioners (e.g., inert coarse particles or nanoparticles that coat and separate the
fine particles), or using electromagnetic fields if the particles are susceptible. These methods
aim to impart additional agitation forces to overcome cohesion. Without such measures,
Group C particles often remain defluidized or behave more like a plugged, oscillating solid
than the bubbling "fluid-like" beds observed with Groups A and B[25], [33], [40]. In fact, in
one study even a 10 µm alumina (a highly cohesive powder) that channeled under steady
airflow was successfully fluidized when a high-frequency (10Hz) gas pulsation was applied to
periodically break up the aggregates[42].
3.5
Fluidization Dynamics: Segregation, Mixing, and Elutriation
When two or more solid materials are fluidized together, or when particles undergo attrition
and size change in a fluidized bed, several important dynamic phenomena occur that affect
reactor performance. In the context of FuelFlex Pyrolyzer (co-fluidizing cement raw meal and
SRF particles), understanding these phenomena is critical to achieving uniform processing.
This section reviews the mechanisms of particle segregation and mixing , the entrainment of
fine particles known as elutriation , common experimental methods to study these effects ,
and specific findings from studies on cohesive Geldart C systems.
3.5.1
Mechanisms of Particle Segregation and Mixing
In fluidized beds containing particles of different size or density, there is a natural tendency
for segregation: differences in gravitational settling and drag forces cause certain particles to
concentrate in different regions of the bed. A classic scenario is a binary mixture of "jetsam"
(heavier or smaller particles) and "flotsam" (lighter or larger particles)[34]. Generally, denser
or smaller particles (jetsam) tend to percolate downwards and concentrate near the bottom,
while larger or lighter particles (flotsam) tend to rise to the upper part of the bed, especially
at higher gas velocities where buoyant forces on larger particles are significant as shown in
Figure 6. These tendencies are counteracted by mixing processes: turbulent eddies, bubble
movement, and particle collisions that redistribute particles [35], [43]. Gravitational forces
tend to segregate by density (heavier particles sink), while drag forces tend to segregate
by size (small particles follow gas streamlines more easily and can be carried upwards).
Interparticle forces (cohesion) can also play a role: fine particles may stick to coarse ones
or form agglomerates, effectively changing the segregation behavior by binding flotsam and
jetsam together.
Quantitatively, segregation and mixing can be assessed by sampling concentration profiles
or using indices like Lacey’s mixing index or the coefficient of variation of composition. A
perfectly mixed bed would have a uniform composition throughout (aside from stochastic
fluctuations), whereas a segregated bed shows a concentration gradient. A number of studies
15
==PAGE:21==
in fluidized beds and similar systems (like vibro-fluidized or shaking beds) have demonstrated
that increasing the fluidization velocity up to a point tends to improve mixing (because
bubbles and circulation currents intensify), reducing segregation, but very high velocities can
also cause more elutriation of fines or throw the lighter particles out of the bed entirely [34],
[44]. The introduction of external perturbations, such as pulsation, has been found to further
enhance mixing by repeatedly disrupting any nascent layered structure and promoting the
interchange of particles between top and bottom layers [45], [46].
Avoiding segregation is particularly important in the FuelFlex Pyrolyzer system, since SRF
particles must stay in close contact with the hot raw meal for effective heat transfer. If SRF
(which may be less dense or larger than raw meal) floats to the top of the bed, its residence
time and conversion could be reduced. Ensuring good mixing often involves matching the
fluidization characteristics of the components. For example, Bai and Si [47] showed in a
cold-bed study (simulating biomass pyrolysis) that uniform mixing of wood particles with
sand was achieved only when the two had similar Umf and were operated at an optimal
sand-to-wood ratio (around 3:1 by volume) and moderate excess gas velocity (3–5 Umf);
otherwise, partial fluidization or segregation occurred. Inevitably, some degree of segregation
may occur if particle properties differ greatly, so operational parameters must be tuned to
mitigate it as much as possible.

3.5.2
Overview of Elutriation Phenomena in Fluidized Beds
Elutriation refers to the process by which fine particles are carried out of the fluidized bed by
the gas flow. Particles whose terminal settling velocity is lower than the upward gas velocity
in the upper part of the bed (or freeboard) can be entrained and eventually leave the reactor,
16
==PAGE:22==
often being captured by cyclones or filters downstream. In fluidized combustion or reactor
systems, elutriation represents both a loss of material (which may be reactant or catalyst)
and a potential source of emissions (particulate matter carryover).
A key factor governing elutriation is the superficial gas velocity in the freeboard above the
bed. At low velocities, even fines tend to remain in the bed either because they are not fully
detached from larger particles or because any that become airborne fall back before reaching
the exit. As velocity increases, a threshold is reached where a notable fraction of the fines
is continually blown out. In cohesive systems, this threshold may exhibit hysteresis or a
nonlinear onset because fine particles often exist in multiple states: some are free, some are
stuck on larger particles, and some form aggregates. At low gas speeds, most fines remain
adhered or settled; at sufficiently high speeds, the hydrodynamic forces can overcome adhesion
and lift them out. Smolders and Baeyens [43] observed that in beds of cohesive powders,
there is often an initial period of de-agglomeration as gas velocity increases, during which
the rate of fine particle elutriation spikes nonlinearly. Santana et al.[49] reported that for
certain Geldart A/C borderline powders, elutriation and attrition rates rose significantly once
a critical air velocity was exceeded, indicating the disintegration of aggregates into individual
particles that could then be carried away. Broader measurements of fines elutriation from
bubbling beds confirm the sensitivity to freeboard hydrodynamics and velocity[50].
Besides velocity, other factors influencing elutriation include the freeboard design (height
above the distributor and any expansions in diameter), gas distributor disturbance (bubbles
erupting at the surface can throw particles into the freeboard), and the presence of internals or
secondary air injection which can help knock down particles. Strategies to mitigate elutriation
involve either capturing the particles (e.g., internal baffles, enlarged freeboard to reduce
velocity and allow particles to fall back, cyclones for recovery) or preventing them from
becoming free (e.g., by promoting their agglomeration so they are effectively larger/heavier).
Some designs use overhead dust return loops to reintroduce elutriated fines back into the bed.
In summary, elutriation is an important design and operational consideration because excessive
loss of fines can alter the bed’s particle size distribution, affect reaction yields, and cause
downstream fouling. In the case of FuelFlex Pyrolyzer, fines could be generated by attrition
of raw meal or char. If these fines elutriate, it not only means loss of fuel or bed material but
could also foul the preheater or kiln if not properly captured. Understanding the balance
between adhesion and drag on fine particles in this system is therefore vital. The interplay
between cohesive forces and gas velocity means there may be a window of operation where
fines generation (by attrition) is manageable and elutriation is low; operating outside that
window could either lead to defluidization (if velocity is too low) or high carryover (if velocity
is too high) [51], [52].
3.6
Experimental Methods for Investigating Segregation, Mixing,
and Elutriation
Studying segregation, mixing, and elutriation in fluidized beds requires carefully designed
experiments, since these phenomena involve spatial and temporal variations in particle
17
==PAGE:23==
distributions. A variety of techniques have been used:
3.6.1
Techniques for Studying Segregation and Mixing
One common approach to assess mixing/segregation is to take samples from different vertical
sections of a fluidized bed (during stable operation, or quickly after shutting off fluidization)
and analyze the composition. This yields concentration profiles of one component vs. height.
Another approach uses visualization: high-speed cameras or endoscope probes can capture
images of the bed, which are then analyzed (sometimes with image processing or tracer
particles) to determine how particles are distributed. Digital image analysis combined with
color tagging of different particles has been used to map segregation patterns. Additionally,
non-invasive tomographic methods (like electrical capacitance tomography, ECT) have been
employed in research to visualize density distributions inside opaque beds.
A simple but insightful method is the bed collapse experiment. In this method, the bed is
fluidized under steady conditions; then the fluidizing gas is abruptly stopped. As the bed
collapses under gravity, one can record the transient pressure drop and bed expansion/con-
traction. The decay profile of pressure drop can indicate how homogeneous the mixture was
and how gas permeates through the collapsing bed (which reflects the distribution of fine vs.
coarse particles). A well-mixed bed tends to collapse more uniformly, whereas a segregated
bed shows a concentration gradient and can exhibit multi-stage collapse as different layers
settle at different rates.
For quantification, indices like Lacey’s mixing index or the Segregation Index (which compares
the variance of composition in samples to the theoretical variance for a completely segregated
state) are used.
These give a single-number measure of the degree of mixing (with 1
being perfectly mixed, 0 being completely segregated). In pulsation studies, researchers
like Asif [45] and Lim [46] measured how mixing indices improved when intermittent gas
pulses were introduced, showing that the increased agitation from pulsation can counteract
segregation. Similarly, Rasul et al.[53] and Tang et al.[54] demonstrated that periodic flow
modulation significantly reduced the separation of light and heavy fractions in vibrated
or pulsed fluidized beds. Early dense-phase characterizations of fine powder beds under
bubbling/expansion conditions (e.g., pressure fluctuations and collapse behavior) also provide
insight for interpreting such data [55].
3.6.2
Techniques for Studying Elutriation
Elutriation is typically studied by measuring the rate of carryover of fines from a fluidized bed.
Experimentally, one way to do this is a batch elutriation test where a known quantity of fines
(or a bed that generates fines via attrition) is fluidized, and the outlet gas is passed through
a filter or cyclone; the captured fines over time provide an elutriation rate. By varying the
superficial gas velocity and measuring the steady-state fines loss rate (or total loss after a
fixed time), one can derive elutriation rate constants. The classical theory often describes an
elutriation rate constant Ke for a given particle size, defined by dM/dt = −KeM for mass
M of that size remaining in bed, at steady flow conditions.
18
==PAGE:24==
Initial studies on fine powders (Geldart A and C) highlighted that elutriation rate is sensitive
to gas velocity and also to how easily particles break apart. Santana et al.[49] found that for
certain cohesive powders, at low velocities the elutriation was extremely low because particles
tended to remain stuck together (so their effective size was larger than the cut-off), but
beyond a threshold velocity, those aggregates broke down and fines were rapidly produced and
elutriated. They also noted that prolonged fluidization could lead to more fines generation
by attrition, gradually increasing the carryover. Smolders and Baeyens[43] emphasized the
influence of bed diameter and freeboard: a tall freeboard allows many fines to fall back,
reducing net elutriation, whereas a shallow freeboard (or one with gas velocities not much
lower than in the dense bed) leads to higher carryover.
Modern studies sometimes use laser diffraction or on-line particle counters in the exhaust line
to get real-time measurements of particle size distribution of elutriated material [56]. Others
have employed tracer particles (fluorescent or radioactive) to specifically track how particles
of a given size move and leave the system[57].
In summary, experimental investigation of these phenomena often combines direct sampling,
imaging, and indirect inference via pressure drop analysis. For the purpose of this thesis,
understanding which techniques are available helps in designing the lab-scale experiments
(for example, using bed collapse tests to gauge mixing effectiveness, or filters to quantify fine
loss under different pulsation conditions).
3.7
Review of Segregation, Mixing, and Elutriation Studies on
Geldart C Particles
Fine cohesive particles (Geldart C) have been the subject of specialized studies due to their
tendency to exhibit both segregation (when mixed with coarser particles) and significant
elutriation (due to attrition and low terminal velocities). Here we highlight some key findings
from literature that are particularly relevant to a raw meal (fine powder) + SRF system:
Geldart and Abrahamsen (1981) observed that even wide size distribution powders show a
degree of size segregation when fluidized, with finer fractions concentrating preferentially
in certain regions, although the overall effect was less pronounced in vigorously bubbling
beds [58]. Liu and Kimura (1993) studied binary mixtures of fine alumina particles with
coarser ones and identified that the fine fraction can exist in three states: (i) free fines
dispersed in the gas, (ii) fines adhering to larger particles, and (iii) fines in agglomerates of
their own [35]. Each state contributes differently to segregation and elutriation: adhered fines
effectively travel with the coarse particles (reducing segregation but also not being elutriated
on their own), whereas free fines segregate quickly (rising to the top and being carried out)
and agglomerates behave as intermediate-sized pseudo-particles.
Smolders and Baeyens (1997) examined cohesive flour particles fluidized in a column with a
de-fluidizing tendency and noted that using a draft tube or a confining device could stabilize
the spout/fluidization and reduce fines carryover [43]. They underscored the importance of
distributor design and freeboard height in mitigating the loss of fines for cohesive systems.
19
==PAGE:25==
Santana et al. (1999) provided early quantitative data on attrition and elutriation for a
cohesive powder; they found that attrition of parent particles generated fines that then
elutriated, and both rates were highly dependent on the air velocity, showing an exponential
rise beyond the minimum fluidization point [49].
More recent investigations have leveraged computational modeling to complement experiments.
For instance, Kukukova et al. (2009) developed modeling approaches for mixing of cohesionless
vs. cohesive particles, indicating that simple diffusion-based models of solids mixing are
inadequate when interparticle forces are significant, and more complex, rate-based descriptions
of agglomerate formation and breakage are needed [59]. Philippsen et al. (2015) extended
these ideas, attempting to simulate the fluidization of cohesive particles by introducing
effective medium models for agglomerates [60]. Similarly, Anicic et al. (2020) performed
CFD simulations of a dense bed with glass and plastic particles and found that using an
adjusted drag law (EMMS model) and higher inter-particle friction successfully reproduced
the experimentally observed segregation patterns [61].
Pulsation and other external aids have proven particularly useful for Geldart C systems.
Pablos et al. (2017) demonstrated in a conical spouted bed containing a fine powder that
applying a pulsing flow and using a fountain confiner dramatically reduced the extent of
elutriation while maintaining stable operation [51]. The pulsed flow effectively broke up
clusters of fine particles, but because of the spout configuration and confiner, those particles
were not immediately lost and could settle back. This suggests that pulsation, combined with
clever reactor designs, can extend the operating range for fine particles by reducing both
segregation (keeping fines in circulation rather than clumping at the top) and elutriation
(capturing entrained fines back into the system). Indeed, Mukhopadhyay et al. (2024) showed
via CFD-DEM that low-frequency sinusoidal pulsations can be deliberately used to enhance
density-driven segregation in a binary mixture (useful for separating particles of different
density) [62], although in the FuelFlex Pyrolyzer context the goal is to minimize segregation.
From these studies, it is evident that key parameters influencing segregation and elutriation
include the gas velocity (relative to Umf and particle terminal velocities), particle size ratio
and density differences in the mixture, the level of interparticle cohesion, and the geometry of
the system (bed diameter, freeboard height, presence of internals). Approaches that enhance
mixing (such as pulsation or vibration) generally help counteract segregation, and those
that promote controlled agglomeration or capture of fines (such as confined spouted beds or
secondary cyclones) help reduce net elutriation. These insights form a basis for designing the
experiments and interpreting results for the FuelFlex Pyrolyzer context, where maximizing
mixing (for fuel conversion) while minimizing fines loss are dual goals.
3.8
Strategies for Enhancing Fluidization Performance
Confronted with the difficulties of fluidizing Geldart C powders, researchers and engineers
have devised numerous strategies to improve fluidization quality. These methods aim to
either reduce interparticle forces or to introduce additional energy/motion to the system to
break apart aggregates. This section outlines several approaches relevant to the present work,
20
==PAGE:26==
with emphasis on pulsation (Section 4.1.1) given its central role in FuelFlex Pyrolyzer.
3.8.1
Approaches to Improve Fluidization of Geldart C Particles
Geldart C particles (fine, cohesive powders typically smaller than 30–40 µm) often require
special techniques to achieve and sustain fluidization.
Below are commonly employed
approaches:
3.8.1.1
Pulsation of Gas Flow.
Imposing a pulsating or oscillatory component on the
fluidizing gas is a proven technique to enhance fluidization of cohesive powders. In practice,
this means the gas flow rate is periodically varied (for example, via a programmable valve or
piston) rather than kept steady. The rapid swings in gas velocity impart strong transient
forces on the particle assemblage, periodically lifting and dispersing clusters that would remain
stuck under steady conditions. Studies have shown that pulsation reduces the minimum
fluidization velocity and improves bed expansion for fine powders [63], [64]. For instance, Ali
and Asif [64] demonstrated that introducing flow pulsations when fluidizing a hydrophilic
fumed silica (a nanopowder that normally agglomerates) led to a several-fold decrease in
Umf and a substantial reduction in agglomerate size, resulting in far more homogeneous
fluidization. Likewise, Akhavan et al.[65] reported up to a 72% reduction in the Umf of
nanoparticle agglomerates under pulsed flow (compared to steady flow) by eliminating crack
formation in the bed. The mechanism is that during the high-flow part of the pulse, drag
forces temporarily exceed cohesive forces to break apart agglomerates, while during the
low-flow part, the bed partially settles which avoids runaway pressure drop or excessive
entrainment. Key pulsation parameters include frequency (typically on the order of 1–10Hz
for small-scale units), amplitude or flow-rate swing (often characterized by the ratio of
maximum to minimum flow), baseline flow (the minimum flow between pulses), and duty
cycle (fraction of time at high flow vs. low flow). Optimization of these parameters is often
empirical; too high a frequency, for example, can make the pulsation ineffective (the system
doesn’t have time to respond to each pulse and it approximates steady flow), whereas too
low a frequency or too long an "off" period can allow the bed to collapse completely and form
persistent channels or layers.
3.8.1.2
Mechanical Vibration.
Applying external mechanical vibration to the entire
bed (for example, mounting the column on a shaker) is another way to assist fluidization [66].
Vibrations (typically in the 20–200Hz range, with small amplitudes of a fraction of a millimeter)
introduce oscillatory accelerations to particles, which can help dislodge stuck particles and
prevent the formation of enduring arches or channels. Mawatari’s study mentioned earlier[38]
explicitly combined vibration with fluidization to achieve Group C powder fluidization. This
approach is commonly used in small-scale or lab units, though scaling it to industrial units is
challenging due to equipment size and potential mechanical stress from vibrations.
3.8.1.3
Additive Particles (Mixing Aids).
One strategy to handle cohesive powders
is to mix them with a fraction of coarser, non-cohesive particles (Geldart B or A) that act as
spacers. These larger particles can mechanically disrupt bridges between fines and improve
21
==PAGE:27==
bed permeability. Another related approach is nano-particle coating, where nanoparticles
(like fumed silica) are mixed with fine powders so that they adhere to the surface of larger
particles or form a thin coating on fine particles, thereby reducing interparticle contact and
cohesion. Van Ommen et al.[63] discuss how very small amounts (on the order of 0.1wt%) of
aerosil nanoparticles added to a cohesive powder can dramatically improve its flowability and
fluidization, essentially converting a Group C behavior toward Group A/B behavior.
3.8.1.4
Combined Methods.
Often, techniques are combined (e.g., pulsation + vi-
bration, or vibration + additives) to achieve robust operation. For extremely challenging
materials, an integrated approach may be necessary [67]. For example, pulsing the flow while
gently vibrating the vessel could exploit the benefits of both methods.
3.8.2
Impact of Pulsation on Fluidization Dynamics
As pulsation is a central strategy for FuelFlex Pyrolyzer, it warrants a closer look regarding
how it influences the key dynamics of segregation, mixing, and elutriation discussed above.
3.8.2.1
Effects on Segregation and Mixing.
Pulsed-flow fluidization significantly
alters mixing patterns in fluidized beds. By periodically introducing high-velocity bursts,
pulsation generates larger and more frequent bubbles or voids in the bed, which promote
circulation of solids. Ireland reviewed pulsation effects and noted that oscillatory flow can
induce a sloshing motion that enhances both axial and radial dispersion compared to steady
conditions [68]. In binary systems, Lim (2019) observed that a low-frequency pulsation (on
the order of 1–2Hz) was particularly effective in reducing segregation between light and heavy
particles; the mechanism is that bed expansion and contraction during each pulse cycle carry
jetsam upward and allow flotsam to intermingle more than in steady bubbling[46]. The
improvement in mixing due to pulsation depends on the frequency and amplitude of the
pulses. Zhang (2019) found that there exists an optimal pulse frequency at which mixing
(or segregation efficiency, in their case) is most enhanced; if the frequency is too high, the
benefit diminishes or even reverses, and if too low, the bed undergoes cycles of fluidization
and collapse that can re-segregate particles during each collapse [69]. Saidi(2014) likewise
reported that higher pulse frequencies (up to 4Hz in their tests) yielded greater segregation
reduction (i.e., improved mixing) and faster attainment of a well-mixed state, attributing
this to the elimination of gas channeling and dead zones by the frequent pulses[22]. However,
beyond an optimum point, additional increases in pulse frequency or intensity may produce
diminishing returns.
3.8.2.2
Effects on Elutriation.
Pulsation’s effect on fine particle carryover (elutriation)
can be double-edged. On one hand, breaking apart agglomerates means more fines become
available to be entrained; on the other hand, the periodic relaxation of gas velocity between
pulses can allow some entrained particles to fall back. Ali and Asif [64] found that the
time-averaged rate of fines elutriation increased under pulsed flow compared to continuous
flow at the same mean velocity, due to the higher instantaneous gas velocities achieved
during each pulse. However, recent work by Abedi and Nasr Esfahany [70] showed that at
22
==PAGE:28==
sufficiently low pulsation frequencies, the elutriation of fines can actually be reduced relative
to steady flow. In experiments fluidizing a cohesive flour powder, they observed that very
low-frequency pulses (approximately 0.03–0.1Hz, essentially a long-period on-off pulsing)
drastically cut the fines loss, especially when a continuous base flow was maintained with
small superimposed pulses. At 3Umf, a 0.033Hz pulsed+continuous airflow regime yielded
about 75% less fines carryover than a steady flow at the same average velocity[70]. The
explanation is that gentle, infrequent pulsation allows aggregates to remain partially intact
and avoids constantly shattering them, whereas higher-frequency pulsation (e.g., > 0.25 Hz
in their study) completely broke apart all clusters and led to a sharp increase in elutriation.
These findings indicate that the influence of pulsation on elutriation is highly sensitive to
the pulse parameters. In designing FuelFlex Pyrolyzer, this means the pulsation must be
tuned to promote mixing and fluidization of SRF/raw meal without excessively throwing fine
particles out of the reactor.
3.8.2.3
Industrial Application and Scale-up Considerations.
Pulsation technology
has been implemented at laboratory and pilot scales, and a few industrial applications (e.g.,
pulsed fluidized bed dryers) have been reported, but its widespread adoption in large-scale
fluidized reactors is still emerging. As reviewed by Saidi et al.[23], pulsed beds tend to
exhibit regular pressure oscillations, more uniform bubble size distributions, and elimination
of persistent channeling, all of which point to improved fluidization quality (especially for
difficult materials). They also noted enhancements in heat and mass transfer rates under
pulsation, beyond what is achievable in steady operation. Nonetheless, scale-up challenges
exist: the repeated stress of pulsation on equipment can cause accelerated wear or erosion,
and the generation of pulsation (e.g., large flow modulating valves or pulsators) must be
engineered for reliability. There may also be pressure fluctuations propagated upstream of the
bed, which could affect blowers or feed systems if not dampened. Additionally, ensuring that
pulsation uniformly affects a very large cross-sectional area (in an industrial unit) may require
multiple pulsation sources or carefully designed distributors. These practical issues have not
been extensively reported in the literature and represent an area for further research[23],
[68]. Overall, however, the benefits demonstrated at smaller scales suggest that, with proper
design, pulsed fluidization can be a powerful tool to handle feeds like SRF and fine raw meal
that are otherwise challenging. The experimental work will therefore pay particular attention
to how pulsation frequency and amplitude influence mixing and elutriation, providing data
that can guide scale-up to an industrial FuelFlex Pyrolyzer unit.
3.9
Environmental, Economic, and Policy Considerations
While the technical feasibility of using SRF in cement kilns (through innovations like FuelFlex)
is crucial, the broader context of why such an effort is important, and under what conditions
it becomes viable, involves environmental benefits, economic drivers, and regulatory support.
This section discusses the sustainability motivations , the economic viability of alternative
fuel utilization , and the policy and regulatory frameworks that influence the adoption of
technologies like FuelFlex .

3.9.1
Sustainability and Environmental Impact in the Cement Industry
The cement industry is one of the largest industrial sources of CO2 emissions (accounting for
roughly 7–8% of global anthropogenic CO2) primarily due to the calcination of limestone and
the combustion of carbon-intensive fuels (usually coal or petcoke) [1], [71], [72]. Introducing
alternative fuels such as SRF can yield environmental benefits on multiple fronts [73]. Firstly,
when SRF contains a biogenic fraction (e.g., paper, wood, textiles), the CO2 released from
that portion can be accounted as zero-rated under ETS rules when sustainability criteria
are met, thus directly reducing net fossil CO2 per tonne of clinker produced [74], [75]. For
example, replacing 50% of the calciner fuel with biomass-based waste could cut direct fossil
CO2 emissions by a similar proportion (commonly on the order of a few hundred kg CO2 per
tonne clinker, depending on baseline fuel and efficiency) [13], [76]. Secondly, many wastes
contain less sulfur and ash than coal, so co-processing them can lead to lower SO2 emissions
and less clinker dust waste [13].
Another major environmental benefit is the reduction in NOx emissions. Cement kilns
generate thermal NOx due to high flame temperatures. The use of pretreated SRF via
FuelFlex creates a more staged combustion environment (since volatiles are released and
burned over an extended zone rather than all at once in the calciner burner). This staging,
as well as partial fuel conversion under oxygen-starved conditions in the pyrolyzer, can
significantly cut NOx formation at source. Empirical data from a FuelFlex demonstration
indicate noticeable drops in calciner NOx after switching a large fraction of fuel to SRF
pyrolysis gas, presumably because the calciner sees a more uniform and slightly more reducing
combustion atmosphere [77]. These effects are in line with established staged-combustion
and reburning principles for NOx control in cement systems [78].
Co-processing waste also alleviates the environmental burden of waste disposal. Every tonne
of SRF used in a kiln is a tonne of waste not sent to landfill or incineration. Landfilling
organic waste leads to methane emissions (a potent greenhouse gas), and uncontrolled burning
of waste (or low-end incineration) often results in toxic emissions and local pollution [79]. In
contrast, cement kilns operate at very high temperatures (often 850–1100◦C in calciners and
>1400◦C in the sintering zone) with long residence times and a strongly alkaline environment,
which together ensure near-complete destruction of organics (including hazardous constituents
like POPs) and capture of acid gases [80], [81]. The Basel Convention’s technical guidelines
acknowledge that properly operated cement kilns provide an environmentally sound method
for disposing of hazardous wastes via co-processing, as the process can recover energy and
incorporate the mineral residues into clinker [82]. Indeed, the mineral content of waste (ash)
essentially gets recycled into the cement product, reducing the need for natural raw materials
like shale or iron corrective; in Europe, on the order of ∼5% of the raw material feed in
clinker comes from waste-derived sources and industrial by-products [83].
However, environmental considerations also impose limits and requirements. Chlorine and
other halogens in waste need to be managed, as excess chlorine can cause buildups and
affect emissions (kilns may need to have bypass systems to bleed out chlorine-rich gas or
dust when using high-SRF rates). Mercury and other heavy metals present in certain wastes
are a concern because they can vaporize in the kiln and some fraction can escape with flue
24
==PAGE:30==
gases. Regulators often set strict emission limits for kilns co-processing waste, and operators
must implement robust monitoring and sometimes install additional emission control devices
(like activated carbon injection for mercury) to comply [81], [84]–[86]. Pilot demonstrations
confirm that co-processing can meet stringent limits; for instance, an industrial trial in Turkey
showed that co-firing processed RDF with petcoke in a cement kiln kept air emissions within
regulatory norms [87].
From a sustainability perspective, the use of SRF in cement fits into circular economy
principles: waste that cannot be recycled materially is used to replace fossil resources, thereby
"closing the loop" by recovering energy and material value that would otherwise be lost. In
many European countries, co-processing is a well-established pillar of waste management.
For example, in recent years the average thermal substitution rate (TSR) of alternative fuels
in European cement plants has exceeded 50% (reaching ∼58% in 2022) as shown in Figure 7
, with several plants achieving very high substitution for months at a time [83]. Globally, the
TSR is much lower (on the order of ∼6–8%), indicating the untapped potential especially in
developing regions [76]. Each tonne of coal avoided not only reduces CO2 but also conserves
non-renewable resources and can reduce local air pollutants (since many waste fractions, e.g.,
plastics, are lower in sulfur than coal) [13].
In summary, the environmental rationale for technologies like FuelFlex is strong: reduced
greenhouse emissions, beneficial use of waste (reducing landfills and pollution), and potential
decreases in harmful emissions through better combustion control.
These benefits are
contingent on operating within environmental regulations and ensuring emissions remain
low [82], [85]. The next subsections discuss how economic and policy factors determine
whether these environmental gains can be realized in practice at scale.

3.9.2
Economic Viability of Alternative Fuel Utilization
Adopting SRF at scale in cement kilns ultimately hinges on clear economic advantages.
The cement sector is highly cost sensitive, and any new fuel or pre-treatment must be cost-
competitive or deliver compensating financial benefits. Studies indicate that co-processing
alternative fuels can lower fuel expenditures and improve overall energy efficiency in cement
plants [88]. A practical assessment weighs capital expenditure for enabling equipment against
operating costs (fuel, electricity, maintenance) and monetizable benefits (avoided fossil
purchases, tipping revenues, and carbon-related credits or savings). In many regions, the
combination of fuel price differentials and tipping (gate) fees is decisive: plants may be paid
to accept waste-derived fuels, effectively turning SRF into a revenue stream; even without
tipping fees, SRF is often cheaper per unit energy than coal, so a wider spread directly
reduces operating cost per tonne of clinker [13], [89]. Carbon pricing and credits further
strengthen the business case. Under systems such as the EU ETS, the biogenic fraction
of SRF reduces fossil CO2 emissions and associated permit needs, enabling either the sale
of surplus allowances or the avoidance of additional purchases. Emerging market designs
in India (ETS/CCTS) could similarly monetize lower CO2 intensity, while instruments like
the CBAM and the Monitoring and Reporting Regulation (MRR) increase the value of
decarbonizing clinker by tightening transparency and compliance requirements [74], [90]–[92].
These benefits must be balanced against the costs of retrofitting FuelFlex or comparable
systems. Required investments include the pulsed fluidized reactor and modifications to
SRF feeding, handling, and control. Operating costs can rise due to additional blower power
(pressure drop), maintenance, and any implications for raw-meal handling. Projects are
typically attractive when fuel savings and carbon-related revenues deliver a payback within a
few years, especially when supported by concessional or climate-linked finance [13], [76].
Fuel quality and process yield are also central. Low or inconsistent calorific value, high
moisture, and composition variability can cap substitution rates or increase specific heat
consumption (an efficiency penalty). Pre-drying or other upgrading steps while adding cost
may be justified to reach high thermal substitution rates (TSRs)[13], [81]. Equally, bankable
economics depend on reliable logistics and supply: long-term, quality-assured contracts
with waste processors or municipalities, plus transport and pre-treatment (shredding, metal
removal, pelletizing), determine the effective delivered fuel cost. Co-locating SRF facilities
near kilns or vertically integrating supply can materially cut logistics costs and risk[13], [89].
Opportunities for by-product valorization can further improve returns. In advanced configura-
tions such as FuelFlex, the pyrolysis gas displaces fossil fuel in the calciner, char can be burned
in the kiln system, and condensed volatiles may yield an oil that if qualifying as a recycled
carbon fuel (RCF) under EU rules could access higher-value transport markets (see Section
5.2.1)[93]. Finally, upstream policy and market dynamics shape viability: stronger recycling
can lower SRF calorific value, whereas stringent landfill diversion policies often increase SRF
availability and tipping fees both of which tend to improve project economics [13].
In the context of this study, preliminary economic analysis indicates that the viability of
integrating a FuelFlex unit improves significantly under scenarios of higher coal prices or the
introduction of carbon taxes. For example, if coal prices were to double, the fuel savings from
26
==PAGE:32==
using mostly SRF could justify the investment readily. Similarly, a carbon tax of, say, $30
per ton CO2 could tilt the balance in favor of SRF, given the large CO2 reductions attainable.
Conversely, if coal is cheap and there’s no cost to carbon or waste disposal, the incentive is
low. That’s why in developing countries like India, the economics often require either policy
support or innovative business models (like getting paid to solve a city’s waste problem). As
shown in case studies and best-practice reviews, aligning economic incentives (via subsidies,
carbon credit revenues, etc.) can reduce payback periods to a few years [13], whereas without
them, a project might not break even over its lifetime [76].
In summary, the economics of SRF utilization in cement are case-specific but generally depend
on fuel cost differentials, policy-driven prices (carbon, waste), and the efficiency with which
the technology can be integrated. The literature suggests that while technical feasibility has
been proven (especially in Europe), scaling it up in new markets will require careful attention
to these economic factors [13], [76].
3.9.3
Pyrolysis Oil as Marine Fuel
One intriguing aspect of the FuelFlex concept is the possibility of producing a liquid fuel
(pyrolysis oil) as a co-product, which could have external value. Pyrolysis oil from biomass
or waste has been studied as a potential marine fuel, where it could replace heavy fuel oil
or marine diesel in ships. It has some advantageous properties: it ignites easily, has good
lubricity (beneficial for engines), and can be stored and handled with similar logistics as
conventional fuels. However, raw pyrolysis oil also has challenges: it is often acidic, unstable
over long periods, has high oxygen content, and can be viscous. Upgrading is required to
make it suitable for marine engines.
Recent literature gives mixed but hopeful results. Letoffe et al. (2024) performed a techno-
economic analysis of using hydrogen to upgrade straw-derived pyrolysis oil into a marine
fuel [94]. They report that hydrotreated pyrolysis oil (at ∼350◦C, ∼200 bar H2 for ∼2 h
in their process) achieved a higher heating value of ∼35 MJ/kg (up from ∼27 MJ/kg in
raw bio-oil) and met ISO 8217 marine-fuel properties [95], [96]. Another study by Prussi
et al. (2024) applied an International Maritime Organization life-cycle method to waste-wood
pyrolysis oil and found greenhouse gas savings of over 70% compared to marine fossil fuels [97].
Sharma et al. (2024) investigated blending pyrolysis oil (derived from digested biomass) with
other biofuels (used cooking oil, animal fats) to improve properties; the blends could meet
marine fuel standards and remained stable over 8 months, though viscosity was on the higher
side [98]. For plastic-derived pyrolysis oils, studies note low oxygen content (since plastics
are hydrocarbon), making them closer to diesel but with components (e.g., olefins/aromatics)
that typically require hydrotreating; after upgrading, engine tests are generally promising,
with attention to NOx [99].
In context, if a cement plant integrated FuelFlex and found that producing a separate oil
stream was feasible (by condensing some vapors), it could potentially have a product to sell
to the marine fuel market. This would however only make sense if the additional complexity
is justified by significantly better economics (i.e., the oil sells at a good price). It might also
27
==PAGE:33==
require that the oil meet criteria to count as a renewable or recycled fuel (for instance, the
EU’s classification of recycled carbon fuels might allow marine use of plastic pyrolysis oil
to count towards emissions targets) [93]. It’s worth noting as an emerging opportunity that
could further improve the economic appeal of systems like FuelFlex by providing a secondary
revenue stream.
Despite the promise, it’s important to note the production efficiency: pyrolysis oil yields from
SRF might be moderate (a lot of the energy could remain in the char or gas). Also, unless
the cement plant is close to a port or a refinery, delivering the oil to a marine market adds
cost. So this idea is a longer-term, strategic consideration. Still, it shows how alternative fuel
systems in cement could evolve into multi-output systems (solid product = clinker, gaseous
fuel to kiln, liquid fuel for external use), effectively turning a cement plant into a combined
materials-and-fuels factory.
3.9.4
Policy and Regulatory Frameworks
Policy support is often the catalyst needed to implement significant alternative fuel projects,
especially in regions where they are not yet common. Both environmental regulations (that
might compel emission reductions or waste diversion) and proactive incentives (that make
sustainable choices economically attractive) play a role.
In the European Union (and similarly regulated markets) The Industrial Emissions Directive
(IED) sets strict emission limits for cement kilns, and co-processing of waste is allowed only
under conditions that emissions remain within these limits (with continuous monitoring).
The Best Available Techniques (BAT) reference for the cement, lime and magnesia sectors
includes co-processing as compatible with environmental protection when proper controls are
applied [85], [100], [101]. Waste policy (e.g., the Waste Framework and Landfill Directives)
creates drivers for co-processing via landfill diversion and high landfill taxes in several Member
States; as a result, SRF/RDF markets have developed to supply industry. Standardization
of alternative fuels (EN 15359 and ISO 21640 for SRF) helps both suppliers and users by
specifying quality in terms of calorific value, chlorine, mercury, etc.; many cement companies
only accept SRF meeting a certain class, facilitating long-term contracts [17], [102], [103].
Climate policies (EU ETS and the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism for imports)
increasingly put a price on carbon, implicitly making coal less competitive and biomass/waste
more attractive. With free allowances being phased down, a plant that doesn’t reduce fossil
usage will incur higher costs [74], [92]. On the incentive side, some EU countries provide
renewable energy credits or public funding support in specific cases (project-level or state
programs), which can improve project economics [104].
In India (and many emerging economies), the Solid Waste Management Rules (2016) explicitly
recognize co-processing in cement kilns for certain wastes such as RDF, and policy guidance
has promoted minimum RDF usage (e.g., 5%) within designated distances to catalyze
municipal cement partnerships [105], [106]. Implementation challenges center on enforcement
and supply-chain maturation-particularly segregation and RDF production to specification-
where technical guidance (e.g., GIZ) emphasizes moisture and chlorine control for kiln
28
==PAGE:34==
compatibility [89]. On the climate front, India is building a carbon market architecture
(CCTS/ETS readiness) that could reward plants demonstrating lower CO2 intensity through
credits or easier compliance [90], [91]. Financially, central and state programs have at times
provided capital subsidies for waste-to-energy projects, and multilateral development banks
have supported waste systems where co-processing plays a role [76]. Extended Producer
Responsibility (EPR) for plastics can also direct funding to the co-processing of non-recyclable
plastics, creating an additional revenue stream for cement producers.
To ensure safe and acceptable operation, regulators often require trial burns and proof of
emission compliance before granting full permission for high TSR operation. Community
perception and political will also matter; in some places, public concern about waste burning
can delay projects, even if emissions are controlled.
In summary, a combination of mutually reinforcing measures is typically needed: push policies
that divert waste from dumps and drive decarbonization (e.g., landfill bans, mandatory RDF
usage, emission standards, carbon pricing); pull policies that improve project economics (e.g.,
subsidies, tax breaks for green investments, soft loans, and inclusion in renewable targets);
and facilitation measures that lower implementation barriers (e.g., fuel quality standards,
streamlined permitting for co-processing, and PPP models for secure waste supply).
Internationally, organizations like the Global Cement and Concrete Association (GCCA)
advocate for recognizing co-processing in global agreements (for instance, the upcoming global
treaty on plastic pollution)[107]. The UNEP Turning off the Tap report (2023) on plastic
pollution notes that where plastics cannot be prevented, reused or recycled, they should
be directed to environmentally sound disposal rather than polluting the environment, with
controlled industrial routes (such as cement kilns) often cited in policy debates as last-resort
options [107], [108].
3.10
Conclusion
The literature review has highlighted both the technical and systemic context for the utilization
of SRF in cement production.
On the technical side, fundamental fluidization studies
underscore the challenges of handling Geldart C powders and heterogeneous mixtures, but
also point to solutions like pulsation and staged conversion to enhance mixing and prevent
defluidization; staged conversion (as in FuelFlex) can improve combustion control and may
lower NOx [77], [78]. Prior research on segregation and elutriation indicates that optimizing
parameters such as gas velocity and applying external excitations can significantly improve the
contact between waste fuel and bed material, which is essential for efficient SRF conversion.
However, gaps remain in fully predicting and controlling these behaviors for industrially
relevant conditions, reinforcing the need for targeted experiments to refine our understanding
in the specific context of cement raw meal and SRF.
On the broader scale, the review demonstrates that co-processing SRF aligns with global
sustainability goals by mitigating waste disposal issues and reducing fossil fuel dependence.
Economically, while the concept is attractive due to potential fuel cost savings and carbon
benefits, actual viability is highly sensitive to local conditions like waste supply chains, energy
29
==PAGE:35==
prices, and climate policy. In mature markets (e.g., Europe), high waste fees and strict
carbon costs have made high SRF substitution a reality, supported by decades of operational
know-how and public acceptance [83]. In emerging markets (e.g., India), there is clear
potential and growing policy interest (e.g., mandates for RDF use, evolving carbon credit
mechanisms), but challenges in infrastructure and economics still limit implementation [89],
[105], [106]. The literature suggests that with appropriate policy support-such as capital
subsidies, carbon pricing, and robust regulatory standards-technologies like FuelFlex can
become attractive and widespread [13], [76]. Indeed, international frameworks (like the UNEP
guidance on plastic waste management and Basel Convention guidelines) acknowledge the
role of cement kilns in safely recovering energy from waste when other options are exhausted,
an endorsement that policymakers can build upon [82], [108].
In conclusion, the literature establishes that optimizing SRF use in cement kilns is a promising
pathway to lower the carbon footprint and contribute to waste management solutions, but it
is a multidimensional challenge. The forthcoming research will build on this foundation to
deliver new data and analysis, driving toward a model of cement production that is both
energy-efficient and aligned with circular economy principles.

4.3
Early Experimental Setup and Procedure
4.3.1
Experimental setup
The bench apparatus is a transparent polycarbonate fluidized-bed column comprising: (i) a
plenum chamber at the base, (ii) a gas distributor plate immediately above the plenum, (iii)
a modular dense-bed section, and (iv) a conical transition to the freeboard. Components are
flanged for leak-tight assembly and visual access.
4.3.1.1
Purpose of the early tests
These exploratory tests were conducted to (i)
verify leak-tight assembly and basic operability of the bench reactor, (ii) map the qualitative
transitions between fluidization regimes for the cement bed under steady flow, and (iii)
establish a sensible range of airflow and pulsation settings to be used in later quantitative
studies.
The experimental apparatus comprises a transparent polycarbonate reactor designed for
visual observation of the fluidization process. The reactor includes several key components: a
plenum chamber located at the base, a gas distributor immediately above the plenum, and
a dense bed section positioned directly above the distributor. A conical section transitions
smoothly from the dense bed to the freeboard region, facilitating clear observation and
analysis. All components are interconnected via flanges, ensuring structural integrity and
airtight operation.
4.3.1.2
Gas supply and control
Air was supplied through a fast on/off valve for flow
pulsation and a mass-flow controller for setting the mean volumetric rate ˙V (reported in
L min−1 at ambient conditions). A needle valve downstream of the regulator maintained
sufficient line pressure above the distributor pressure drop.
4.3.1.3
Definition of flow pulsation
In this work, pulsation refers to a periodic, on-off
modulation of the inlet gas flow using a fast valve. One pulsation cycle consists of an open
period topen followed by a closed period tclosed; the pulsation frequency is f = 1/T with
the cycle time T = topen + tclosed (units Hz). The duty ratio is δ = tclosed : topen. When a
continuous baseline flow is present, we denote the ratio of the pulsed component to the steady
(continuous) component as P/C. A fully pulsed condition corresponds to zero baseline flow,
i.e. P/C →∞.

4.3.2
Preparation of Cement and System Assembly
For each run, 730 g of dried cement raw mill material were weighed. Prior to each experiment,
the reactor was thoroughly vacuumed to eliminate residual particles from preceding trials. The
fluidization setup was then assembled, ensuring airtight sealing of all joints and connections,
and the fresh cement sample was carefully introduced into the test column.
4.3.2.1
Flow-Rate Adjustment and Testing Protocol
After assembly, the airflow
was initially set to 5 L/min. Following each adjustment of flow rate, the column was gently
tapped to stabilize the bed structure and minimize compaction from previous settings. A
5 min waiting period was used to approach steady conditions, after which systematic visual
observations of bed behavior were documented.
Airflow rates were subsequently increased in steps of 5 L/min to locate the critical range
of transitions. Within that range, finer steps of 2 L/min were used to delineate regime
boundaries precisely.
4.3.2.2
Classification of Fluidization Regimes
With increasing superficial gas velocity
Us, the bed progresses through the canonical gas-solid fluidization regimes sketched in Fig. 11:
(i) fixed bed (or delayed bubbling), (ii) bubbling (aggregative) fluidization, (iii) slug flow,
(iv) turbulent fluidization, (v) fast fluidization, and (vi) pneumatic conveying. For clarity we
relate our qualitative labels to these regimes as follows:
• Ratholes ⇒fixed bed / delayed bubbling: gas percolates through stable channels
while most particles remain essentially static.
• Islands ⇒early bubbling: localized regions lift and settle with small bubbles; the
majority of the bed is only weakly agitated.
• Pulsation clusters ⇒bubbling/incipient slugging: semi-coherent packets oscillate,
indicating larger voids and more organized oscillations.
• Transition ⇒slug →turbulent: mixed patterns with intermittent slugs breaking up
into a more uniformly agitated suspension.
• Fully fluidized ⇒turbulent/fast: continuous, uniform motion; at sufficiently high Us
the system approaches fast fluidization and eventually pneumatic conveying.

velocity Us: fixed bed (delayed bubbling) →bubbling (aggregative) →slug flow →turbulent
fluidization →fast fluidization →pneumatic conveying.[118]
4.3.3
Fluidization Regime Mapping (Cement-Only Baseline)
Under a close-open ratio of 1 (equal open and closed durations), a Pulsed/Continuous (P/C)
ratio of ∞(fully pulsed flow, i.e. no continuous baseline), and a pulsation frequency of 2.5Hz
(valve open/closed each 200ms), a bed of cement raw meal (with no secondary material
added) was tested while gradually increasing the airflow. The following flow regimes were
observed as the superficial gas velocity increased:
• 5L/min: Ratholes with islands - distinct narrow channels formed (ratholes) while
isolated portions of the bed oscillated up and down (islands).
• 10L/min: Few ratholes; islands dominate - channeling was reduced and most movement
was in localized vertical oscillations of bed regions.
• 15–19L/min: Pulsation clusters - semi-coherent clusters of particles moved in an
organized, pulsating manner (most of the bed exhibited motion).
• 20–23L/min: Transition - mixed flow patterns appeared, indicating the approach to
full fluidization.
• ≥24L/min: Fully fluidized - the entire bed was in continuous, uniform motion (full
fluidization observed at 24, 25, and 30L/min).
37
==PAGE:43==
These observations show a clear progression from channelized flow (ratholes) to pulsation-
dominated motion, then a brief transition region, and finally full bed fluidization at or above
about 24L/min. It is notable that this initial test was performed with only cement raw
meal (no secondary additive) to establish the baseline behavior. For subsequent tests, we
therefore considered the bed to be fully fluidized (under pulsed conditions) once the airflow
reached roughly 24L/min for the cement alone. This preliminary regime mapping informed
the selection of operating ranges for later, more detailed experiments.
4.3.3.1
Experimental Limitations and Sources of Uncertainty
The principal
limitation of this methodology is the inherent subjectivity of visual assessment, which can
introduce observer variability. Prolonged operation also caused particle adhesion on the
reactor walls, obstructing lateral views and restricting observation primarily to a top-down
perspective.

4.3.4
Preparation of Cement Mix and Setup
4.3.4.1
Why a binary mix?
A binary mixture (cement + secondary material) was used
to probe mixing and segregation mechanisms under both steady and pulsed aeration. The
secondary phase served as a visible tracer and as a surrogate for light, irregular alternative
fuels used industrially. The objective was to quantify vertical redistribution, identify operating
windows that suppress segregation, and assess the influence of pulsation on mixing quality.
4.3.4.2
Test with Coffee
The primary bed material was dry cement raw mill material.
A test batch of 1500 g was used per experiment. To remove residual moisture, the cement
was dried overnight at 100 ◦C. As a secondary material, coffee beans were added at 10 %
of the cement mass (150 g for a 1500 g cement batch). Before each test, the apparatus was
vacuumed to remove remnants from earlier experiments. The dried cement and coffee beans
were then mixed to ensure an even initial distribution of the secondary material.
Once prepared, the test column was assembled, all connections were leak-checked, and the
freshly mixed sample was introduced. The airflow valve was opened gradually and the system
was brought to a stable operating pressure of 5 bar.
4.3.4.3
Flow-Rate Testing Protocol
The initial airflow rate was set to 30 L/min,
slightly above the previously identified minimum fluidization flow rate for cement alone
(24 L/min). At 30 L/min, visible ratholes suggested incomplete fluidization of the binary bed;
therefore, tests were also performed at 50 L/min.
For pulsation, the frequency was set to 2.5 Hz (one cycle every 0.4 s), with the valve timing
configured to 200 ms open and 200 ms closed (T = 400 ms, δ = 0.5). A fully pulsed condition
(P/C →∞, i.e. no continuous baseline flow) was investigated.
4.3.4.4
Procedure
Each test ran for 30 min. Afterward, samples were collected from
each disk level and stored in individual containers. The samples were sieved using a 0.6 mm
mesh on a Retsch AS 200 control shaker, and the masses of dry cement and secondary
material were recorded separately.
To assess vertical distribution, samples were taken every 2.5 cm along the stack, enabling
a detailed evaluation of how airflow and pulsation redistributed the mixture through the
column.
4.3.5
Mixing Test with Coffee Beans (Light Secondary Material)
For this binary-system trial, coffee beans were used as a light secondary material to study
mixing and segregation in the cement. The coffee (150g, about 10wt% of the cement mass)
was thoroughly premixed with 1500g of dried cement raw meal before fluidization. The bed
was then fluidized under fully pulsed air (P/C→∞, 2.5Hz pulsing) at two different flow
rates (30L/min and 50L/min) for 30minutes. Figure 13 shows the measured coffee mass
fraction (coffee mass / total sample mass) in the bed as a function of height from the bottom,

At 30L/min, the coffee was very clearly segregated towards the bottom of the bed. The top
sections contained virtually no coffee, the middle sections had a gradually increasing coffee
fraction, and the bottom segment showed a pronounced spike (the coffee concentration in the

somewhat, spreading the coffee more broadly through the lower part of the bed (though the
mixture was far from uniform, and the upper layers were still essentially coffee-free in both

4.3.5.1
Limitations
The irregular shape and brittleness of the coffee beans introduced
challenges in this experiment. Cement particles tended to adhere to the bean surfaces,
so the collected samples often had a greater mass of "coffee" (coffee + attached cement)
than the initial 150 g added. Additionally, some beans fractured during fluidization and
sieving, generating fine coffee particles that skewed the mass balance. As a result of these
issues, repeated tests yielded inconsistent total recoveries of coffee, reducing confidence in
the quantitative segregation measurements for this material. These limitations highlighted
the need for careful material handling and informed the decision to explore other secondary
materials (like rice) for more detailed studies.
4.3.6
From Premixing to Top-Loading of the Secondary Material
In the initial series of experiments, the secondary material was thoroughly mixed with the
cement base in an attempt to achieve a uniform and well-mixed sample before fluidization.
This approach was intended to minimize distribution variability and facilitate controlled
testing of airflow and segregation behavior.
Despite careful preparation, substantial non-uniformity persisted across the column regardless
of the mixing method. Hand shaking produced considerable variation, with error rates ranging
from 1% to 25% and a maximum segmental difference of 37 percentage points (measured as
the deviation of each segment’s secondary-to-total ratio from the overall average). Machine
shaking under non-optimal settings performed worse, yielding errors between −1% and −28%
and maximum differences up to 50 percentage points. Only under ideal machine-shaking
conditions did the error narrow to 0-7%, with the maximum segmental difference reduced to
14 percentage points. These findings indicate that, even with deliberate and repeated mixing
in the absence of airflow, achieving true uniformity is difficult and may be impractical in
real-world systems.
To increase experimental relevance and streamline preparation, pre-mixing was abandoned in
favor of placing the secondary material directly on top of the cement in the test column, mir-
roring industrial practice in FuelFlex and other alternative-fuel technologies where secondary
fuels are not pre-blended with the raw mill feed. This top-loading method enables clearer
observation of segregation dynamics (by tracking how airflow rate and duration redistribute
material across bed segments), improves practical relevance by replicating common plant
feeding configurations, and enhances efficiency by eliminating time-consuming pre-mixing
and allowing more test conditions to be evaluated within the same timeframe.
4.3.7
Comparison of Loading Methods
Under a close–open ratio of 1, a Pulsed/Continuous ratio of ∞, and a pulsation frequency of
2.5 Hz (200 ms open / 200 ms close) at 25 L/min for 60 min,100 g rice was introduced by
three methods placed as a top layer before start, sprinkled from the top after fluidization
had begun, and placed as a bottom layer with 1500 g of cement raw mill. Test are run to
determine how feed method alone biases segregation and to identify a procedure that best
approximates a distribution throughout the bed.

Figure 14: Rice mass fraction versus distance from the bottom at 25 L/min for 60 min for
three loading methods for 100g rice with 1500g raw mill
Figure 14 plots rice mass fraction versus distance from the bottom for the three methods. Top
loading before start yielded a smooth gradient with very low fractions aloft and increasing
toward the bottom (e.g., 0.002 at 17.5 mm, 0.011 at 15 mm, 0.025 at 10 mm, 0.099 at 5 mm,
peaking at 0.222 at 2.5 mm). Introducing rice after start shifted material slightly upward
and reduced the bottom peak (e.g., 0.001 at 17.5 mm, 0.039 at 10 mm, 0.101 at 5 mm, 0.177
at 2.5 mm), indicating less bottom accumulation and more hold-up in the mid lower region.
Bottom loading produced the strongest segregation, with near-zero fractions above 10 mm
and a pronounced bottom enrichment (0.055 at 5 mm; 0.317 at 2.5 mm).
Top-loading before fluidization clearly provides the best starting condition for mixing in
this system. It produced the least segregated final state and is also reflective of industrial
practice (since in many calciner systems, alternative fuel is fed from the top or side rather
than injected at the bottom). Accordingly, we adopted the top-loading approach for all
subsequent experiments with rice. In contrast, introducing the secondary material after
fluidization begins or feeding it from the bottom can bias the material toward the lower
bed, leading to a less homogeneous distribution. In an industrial setting, this suggests that

feeding SRF into the bed at start-up or from the top of the vessel would promote better
initial dispersion, whereas dumping fuel in after the fact or from beneath the bed would likely
result in a more stratified (bottom-heavy) fuel layout. As always, these extrapolations to
real systems should be treated as qualitative guidance the lab conditions here are simplified,
but the trends provide insight into how feed strategy alone can influence fuel mixing.

4.4.5
Sensitivity Analysis
To assess the robustness of results beyond the Base and Action Cases, four parameters
with outsized influence on techno-economic outcomes are varied: the SRF substitution rate
(α), the SRF price, the EU ETS CO2 price, and the FuelFlex CAPEX. Each reflects real-
world uncertainty driven by fuel markets, policy, technology deployment, and site-specific
constraints. The ranges are grounded in industry practice and literature.
4.4.5.1
SRF substitution rate (α)
The thermal substitution rate determines how much
fossil energy (coal) is displaced by SRF and thus directly drives both OPEX and emissions.
Achievable thermal substitution rate (TSR) varies with fuel quality, process constraints (e.g.,
chlorine/alkali control and associated bypass operation), and feeding system design. European
experience shows high but heterogeneous alternative-fuel uptake, while best-practice plants
demonstrate very high thermal substitution rate (TSR) in favourable conditions [1], [143].
Therefore sweep α from moderate to ambitious levels (e.g., 25%, 50%, 75%, 100%) is used
to capture cases where SRF uptake is constrained by supply or kiln operability, as well as
upper-limit cases that test the technology’s headroom.
4.4.5.2
SRF price (gate fee or cost)
SRF economics exhibit unusually wide variance
because prices are often set by bilateral contracts, logistics, and the local balance between
waste supply and co-processing demand. Historically, cement kilns could even be paid to accept
certain RDF/SRF streams (i.e., a negative net fuel cost), whereas in tighter markets high-
quality SRF can carry a positive price [143]. For example, evidence from Germany indicates
up to e20 revenue per tonne of RDF (including transport) in some cases, underscoring the
possibility of negative costs [143]. To reflect this uncertainty transparently, SRF energy price
varied at four discrete levels: −2, 0, 2, and 4 e/GJ (with negative values representing a gate
57
==PAGE:63==
fee paid to the plant). This span covers scenarios from continued availability of low/negative-
cost SRF to conditions where SRF procurement becomes comparatively expensive on an
energy basis.
4.4.5.3
EU ETS CO2 price
Carbon price strongly modulates the value of fossil dis-
placement. Prospective analyses of the expanded ETS framework (including the so-called
“ETS 2”) project materially higher CO2 prices by 2030 depending on policy stringency, with
published ranges extending from roughly e71/t to e261/tCO2 [137]. Test of e70, e140, and
e210/tCO2 are used as representative low/medium/high cases. This allows us to quantify
how rising compliance costs amplify the economics of SRF substitution (and, where applicable,
ETS credits for avoided emissions).
4.4.5.4
FuelFlex CAPEX
As a relatively new configuration, FuelFlex capital cost is
uncertain and may improve with learning and scaling. For context, functionally similar
solutions have reported investment needs in the e3–10 million range depending on capacity
and site works [143]. Against this backdrop, and to bracket first-of-a-kind versus matured
deployments, FuelFlex CAPEX is varied from e5 million (optimistic/learning case) through
e10 million and e20 million to e30 million (conservative/high-cost case). This range reveals
break-even conditions and highlights how cost reduction (or support mechanisms) would shift
NPV, IRR, and payback. Overall, these ranges are chosen to reflect plausible market and
policy uncertainty while spanning the operating envelope observed or anticipated in literature
and practice [1], [137], [143]. They enable a transparent appraisal of which drivers matter
most and under what conditions the Action Cases remain attractive.

5.1
Experimental Analysis of Fluidization Dynamics
This subsection explains why each experiment was run and how the observations refine
the protocol. Building on materials and apparatus described in 4.2 4.3.1, we move from
surrogates to industrially relevant fragments: first spherical particles (plastics and steel) to
isolate pulsation, density, and size effects (5.1.1), biomass-like irregular particles (rice, corn,
coffee) under identical flow (5.1.2) followed by parametric sweeps with rice to probe pulsation
modes and total airflow (5.1.3), and finally industrial SRF samples to translate trends into
practice (5.1.4). Each test reports layer-wise mass fractions after run time (typically 60 min)
unless noted, culminating in implications for SRF co-processing (5.1.5).
5.1.1
Segregation of Spherical Particles (Plastics and Steel)
5.1.1.1
Effect of pulsing intensity on light spherical plastic balls (3 mm PP)
We
first varied gas-pulsing parameters with 3 mm Polypropylene (PP) spherical plastic particles
in the finalized setup to map how operating conditions shape vertical distributions. Three
variations were tested: (1) a longer closed period (open:closed = 1:3 at 2.5 Hz, i.e. short
bursts of air followed by longer pauses), (2) a lower pulse frequency (1 Hz, keeping 25 L/min
amplitude and 1:1 duty), and (3) a higher flow rate (40 L/min at 2.5 Hz, 1:1 duty). These
adjustments either reduced the intensity of fluidization (case 1 and 2) or increased it (case 3),
allowing us to observe the impact on segregation of the light plastic balls.
61
==PAGE:67==
Figure 22: Vertical distribution of 3 mm PP plastic balls under different gas-pulsing conditions.
The base case (2.5 Hz, 25 L/min, 1:1 duty) achieved a relatively uniform spread of PP in the
lower bed. With an extended "off" period (1:3 duty), PP remained concentrated near the
bottom. Fewer, larger pulses (1 Hz) and a higher continuous flow (40 L/min) both injected
more PP into mid-bed layers (5—10 mm range)
Under the base case pulsation, the 3 mm PP particles were fairly well distributed through
the lower half of the bed. Around 5—10% of the PP was present in each layer from 5 mm
up to 10 mm height, and a small tail (approximately 2—5%) extended into the 12.5 mm
layer. Essentially, the pulsing kept the light PP continuously re-mixed, preventing it from
completely settling out at the bottom or escaping to the top.
When the closed interval was tripled (1:3 duty, meaning much shorter air bursts and longer
pauses), the PP balls settled significantly more. The bottom 2.5 mm layer’s PP fraction
roughly doubled compared to the base case, and almost no PP was found above the 10 mm
layer. The long rest between pulses allowed the buoyant PP balls to fall back and stay near
the distributor before the next pulse could lift them, leading to a strongly bottom-heavy
profile.
Reducing the pulse frequency to 1 Hz (while keeping the same total flow per cycle) led to a
different kind of segregation. In this case, a pronounced PP peak appeared in the mid-bed
62
==PAGE:68==
(around the 7.5 mm layer), but there was also substantial PP at the bottom - essentially a
bimodal distribution. Infrequent but more powerful pulses were able to throw a portion of
the PP far into the bed, yet the extended pause between pulses then allowed a lot of it to
settle back, resulting in accumulation at both the bottom and mid levels and very little in
the top.
Increasing the overall airflow to 40 L/min at 2.5 Hz produced a profile somewhat similar
to the 1 Hz case, in that much more PP was found in the middle layers compared to the
base case. The stronger constant flow lifted a greater share of PP into the 5—10 mm region
(with the peak PP fraction around 7.5 mm). However, even with this high velocity, PP did
not show up in the topmost segments; instead, vigorous mixing likely caused particles that
reached the upper region to be quickly carried out or to fall back down into the mid-bed.
The bottom layer still retained a considerable portion of PP as well.
In summary, the base-case pulsing yielded the most uniform vertical distribution of the
light PP surrogate. Both substantially gentler fluidization (long closed periods) and much
more intense fluidization (very low frequency pulses or very high velocity) resulted in PP
concentrating at certain levels of the bed rather than being uniformly mixed. This suggests
there is an optimal range of pulsation intensity where segregation is minimized. In that
optimal regime, the gas flow is enough to continually lift and mix the fuel particles with
the raw meal, but not so extreme as to either blow the fuel out of the bed or create large
oscillations that allow it to settle in between pulses.

From an application standpoint, these findings suggest that using lower-density fuels (or
altering the fuel to effectively lower its density, for instance by making it porous) and
minimizing the particle size can greatly improve how uniformly the fuel is distributed in a
cohesive raw meal bed. A heavy, dense fuel might otherwise sink to the bottom and under
perform unless fluidization is very vigorous, whereas a lighter, smaller fuel is more likely to
be carried into the reacting zone of the bed.

5.1.2

each material, the bed was sectioned at fixed distances from the distributor (2.5—20 mm)

and shape (aspect ratio/friability) shift vertical solids hold-up under otherwise identical flow
condition-insights useful for anticipating behavior of heterogeneous SRF fragments.

Virtually no secondary material was found above 20 mm for rice and corn. In contrast, coffee

• Rice (baseline behavior): Most of the rice grains remained near the bottom (with

5.1.3
Mixing Results with Rice (Effect of Gas Flow and Pulsation Parameters)
Using rice as the chosen secondary material and the standardized procedure established above
(top-loaded fuel, 60 min run, dried cement bed), we carried out a series of tests to examine
how different gas flow regimes and pulsation parameters affect the vertical distribution of the
secondary material. In particular, we explored two main factors: (1) the characteristics of
the pulsed flow (frequency and the presence of continuous airflow components), and (2) the
total airflow rate through the bed. Below, we present the results of these tests, each designed
to isolate the influence of these factors on mixing/segregation behavior.
5.1.3.1
Effect of Pulsation Frequency and Flow Mode
To assess the impact of
how the gas flow is pulsed, four operating modes were compared (all at an average flow of
25 L/min for 60 min): (i) a base case with fully pulsed flow at 2.5 Hz (200 ms open / 200 ms
closed, 50% duty cycle, no continuous component), (ii) a high-frequency pulsing case at 10 Hz
(also 50% duty), (iii) an extended-closed pulsing case with a 3:1 closed:open time ratio (75%

of each cycle closed, effectively a shorter burst of air followed by a longer off-period, still
at 2.5 Hz overall frequency), and (iv) a mixed pulsed+continuous flow case, where 75% of
the total flow was delivered in pulses (18.75 L/min pulsed at 2.5 Hz) and 25% as a constant
baseline (6.25 L/min steady flow). In all scenarios, the total mean airflow was 25 L/min and
the rice was top-loaded initially. Figure 28 shows the resulting rice distribution profiles for

The different pulsing modes produced some noticeable differences in how the rice distributed
in the bed:
• Fully pulsed base case (2.5 Hz): This case exhibited a baseline segregation pattern
- the rice concentration increased steadily with depth, from almost zero in the top layers
to a peak of roughly 20—22% in the bottom layer. There were no sharp concentration

jumps, just a smooth gradient toward the bottom.
• Higher frequency (10 Hz): Increasing the pulsation frequency while keeping the
same overall flow led to a slightly more distributed profile. Compared to the base case,
a bit more rice remained in the lower-mid sections (e.g. around 10 mm height), and the
bottom layer held a slightly smaller fraction. Essentially, the higher frequency pulses
prevented a small portion of the rice from settling completely to the bottom, spreading
it out a little more evenly in the lower regions.
• Extended closed phase (3:1 closed:open): Introducing a longer "off" period (with
short intense bursts of flow) caused a different pattern. A pronounced band of rice
appeared just above the distributor: the sample at about 7.5 mm height contained
the highest rice fraction among all the modes tested. Meanwhile, the very bottom
layer had somewhat less rice (19%) than in the base case. This suggests that the long
pauses allow the aerated channels to collapse and rice to settle, and when flow restarts,
some rice is swept up slightly into the bed rather than packing exclusively into the
bottommost layer.
• Pulsed + continuous combination: The addition of a continuous airflow component
(6.25 L/min) to the pulses tended to keep the rice heavily in the lower part of the bed,
but without the distinct layering seen in the extended-closed case. The bottom layer’s
rice fraction was high (comparable to the base case), and intermediate layers showed
modest amounts of rice without any extreme peaks or gaps. Notably, this was the only
mode where a trace of rice was detected even in the 20 mm layer (near the top of the
bed), indicating that the continuous component helped carry a very small portion of
rice higher than pulsing alone did.
In summary, modifying the pulsation frequency and pattern had a moderate influence on
segregation. Higher-frequency pulsing (10 Hz) or introducing a continuous flow component
both resulted in a slightly more uniform distribution in the lower bed (reducing the extreme
bottom accumulation), whereas using very intermittent bursts (extended off periods) caused
the secondary material to concentrate in a layer just above the bottom. These trends imply
that, in principle, one could tune the gas flow pattern to influence fuel mixing: for example,
using higher pulse frequencies if a broader distribution of fuel is desired, or adding a steady
airflow if the goal is to keep more fuel near the base. However, the differences observed
were relatively subtle , none of these modes completely eliminated segregation , and thus
any practical benefits would likely need to be confirmed in a real system. We present these
findings as indicative of possible adjustments rather than definitive rules, given that full-scale
combustion systems have additional complexities not captured in our cold-bed model.

To
separate the roles of total gas throughput and the nature of flow (pulsed versus continuous)
on mixing, we conducted tests at various flow rates and modes. Figure 29 summarizes results
for four cases: (i) the standard pulsed base case at 25 L/min (2.5 Hz pulsing, no continuous

The key outcomes from these comparisons are as follows:
• 40 L/min pulsed: At this high flow rate, the bed was vigorously fluidized and

secondary material.

• 25 L/min pulsed (base case): This case (described previously) is the reference,

enriched with rice, actually had a somewhat lower fraction than in the 15 L/min case.

heights, but overall the ability to segregate by weight was impaired (not all the rice
could percolate straight to the bottom due to stable channels or stagnant zones).
Overall, there appears to be an optimal window of gas flow for mixing the secondary material
in this system. Among the conditions tested, the mid-range flow of about 25 L/min (when
pulsed) gave the most balanced results: it was sufficient to fluidize and circulate some rice
into the lower-middle bed, but not so high as to cause severe channeling. Lower flows (near
minimum fluidization) led to nearly all the secondary particles sinking to the bottom (with
poor renewal of material in the upper regions), whereas a much higher flow induced jet-like
behavior that again left the bed with most of the secondary material at the bottom and little
in between. In addition, the comparison between pulsed and continuous operation at the same
flow highlights that pulsing is advantageous for keeping lighter fuel particles low in the bed
(anchoring them near the bottom where, in a combustion scenario, the higher temperatures
might be), whereas a continuous flow tends to carry them upward and distribute them more
evenly (which could help mixing but might lift fuel away from the hot zone).
In practical terms, these findings suggest that by adjusting the total airflow and pulsation
strategy, operators could influence where the fuel resides in a fluidized bed. If the goal
74
==PAGE:80==
is to maintain more fuel in the lower region (for example, to ensure it stays longer in a
high-temperature zone for complete burnout), using a pulsed flow at a moderately above-
threshold velocity may be beneficial. On the other hand, if a more uniform fuel distribution
is desired (to avoid localized fuel-rich pockets), one might opt for a higher continuous flow
or higher-frequency pulses, accepting that some fuel will be carried upward. Importantly,
extreme conditions should be avoided: too low a flow leads to poor fluidization and dead
zones, while too high a flow can create bypassing channels. These conclusions are drawn
from our lab-scale cold model; hence, they should be applied with caution. Real industrial
systems will have additional influences (such as fuel moisture release, chemical reactions, and
larger-scale circulation patterns) that could modify how these trends manifest. Nonetheless,
the qualitative insights provide a useful starting point for thinking about how to control fuel
particle distribution in a fluidized-bed co-firing scenario.

before the pellets began moving. Within a few seconds (by Frame 4), this void closed as the
heavy WKE pieces became entrained, and by Frame 6 onward the bed motion was uniform.
After this start-up phase, the steady-state profiles described above were established. This
emphasizes that sufficient run time is needed to reach equilibrium mixing conditions.

The trends seen with these diverse surrogates can be qualitatively mapped onto actual SRF

• Light, bulky SRF fractions (fluff, films, foils; akin to Blue River 8 mm):
These are likely to reside in the mid-bed and be lifted out more readily. To manage

this, such materials could be fed deeper in the bed and vigorous but controlled pulsing
should be maintained to continually remix them.
• Large but light pieces (akin to Blue River 16 mm): Their size may prevent
them from reaching the top despite low density, causing them to oscillate between lower
and mid layers. Pre-shredding or size reduction might be necessary so that these pieces
can be transported into hotter regions of the reactor.
• Denser, compact chunks (akin to wood pellets/WKE): These will tend to
accumulate near the distributor, elevating the risk of stagnant zones and hot spots.
Ensuring sufficient base flow condition (via pulsing or distributor design) to lift these
heavy particles is crucial. Introducing secondary air or strategically placing feed points
can also help distribute dense fragments more evenly.
5.1.5
Implications for SRF Co-Processing
The systematic trends observed in our experiments provide insight into how SRF might
behave when co-processed in a cement raw meal fluidized bed. To maximize fuel-meal contact
and conversion, the goal is to minimize segregation of the fuel. Our key findings suggest a
few guiding principles:
• Favor lighter and smaller SRF particles: Low-density, fine fuel particles (analogous
to the PP or small Nylon balls) were the most uniformly dispersed in the bed, reaching
higher into the material and maintaining contact throughout the bed height. Such
particles are expected to have more sustained exposure to heat and oxidizing conditions,
potentially improving burnout and energy release.
• Avoid large, dense fuel chunks: High-density or oversized pieces (analogous to the
5 mm POM) tended to segregate at the bottom, where they have limited interaction
with the rest of the bed. Unless fluidization is extremely vigorous, these pieces would
burn slower and less completely. Pre-processing SRF to remove or shred such fragments
can help prevent dead zones in the reactor.
• Tune fluidization parameters: There appears to be an optimal range of gas-pulsing
intensity for mixing Geldart C powders with lighter fuels. In our tests, a moderate-
frequency, moderate-duty pulsing (the base case) kept particles well mixed. If the flow
condition was too gentle (long off-periods or low frequency), even light fuels settled out;
if it was too aggressive (high velocity or very large pulses), the system became unstable
and fuel either clustered at certain levels or got carried away. Thus, maintaining
intermediate pulsation settings (sufficient to disrupt channels but not so high as to
bypass the bed) is important for effective co-processing.
Overall, by selecting appropriate fuel particle characteristics and operating conditions that
avoid segregation, one can improve the mixing between SRF and the raw meal. Better mixing
should lead to more uniform heat transfer and more complete combustion of the fuel. We
note, however, that these conclusions are drawn from lab-scale surrogate experiments; actual
cement plant conditions are more complex. Nonetheless, the trends identified-such as the
79
==PAGE:85==
benefit of finer/lower-density fuel and the existence of a pulsing "sweet spot"-offer useful
starting points for optimizing SRF co-feeding. In practice, plant operators may use these
insights to adjust fuel preparation (e.g. particle size reduction, blending) and fluidization
settings to enhance SRF utilization while maintaining stable kiln operation.
An additional practical insight from this study is the importance of allowing sufficient time
for the system to reach steady-state mixing. We observed that immediately after introducing
the secondary material and starting the pulsed flow, the fuel surrogate can appear more
mixed (e.g. temporarily occupying higher layers) before settling into a stable distribution.
Short tests (on the order of a few minutes) captured these transient effects and did not reflect
the true extent of segregation. By about 60 minutes of run time, however, the vertical profiles
became consistent and matched those from much longer tests (e.g. 4 hours) within a few
percentage points. Therefore, in both laboratory experiments and industrial practice, it is
advisable to wait for the bed to reach a steady condition before evaluating performance. In
the context of operations, after any major change (such as adjusting fuel feed rate, airflow, or
pulsing parameters), a stabilization period on the order of tens of minutes may be needed for
the fuel distribution to equilibrate. Accounting for this lag can help ensure that measurements
of combustion efficiency, emissions, or material sampling truly represent normal operating
behavior rather than transient start-up conditions.

Action Case 1 (AC1): SRF substitution
SRF substitution (without pyrolysis
oil revenue) yields mixed results. In all regions, higher SRF thermal substitution rates (TSR
from 25% up to 100%) lead to substantial coal displacement and CO2 emissions reduction.
For example, moving from 25% to 100% SRF in Germany displaces ∼116,000 t coal annually
and avoids ∼56,500 tCO2/yr. Similar near-linear scaling of coal avoided and CO2 avoided is
seen in India (e.g., ∼170,700 tCO2/yr avoided at 100% SRF in a brownfield plant). These
environmental benefits, however, do not translate into positive project economics without
additional incentives. In Germany without carbon pricing (no ETS), NPVs remain negative
at all SRF levels (e.g., NPV ≈−e340 million at 25% SRF, improving to ≈−e264 million
at 100% SRF). IRRs are undefined (cashflows never turn positive), and payback periods
exceed the project horizon. Even in Germany with a carbon price (EU ETS) crediting
biogenic CO2 avoidance, the SRF-only case does not achieve profitability under the given
conditions—though there is an observable uptick in NPV (e.g., at 100% SRF, NPV improves
from about −e264 million without ETS to −e222 million with ETS). In India, where no
explicit carbon price is assumed in the base case, SRF-only scenarios likewise yield negative
NPVs (e.g., ≈−e168 million at 100% SRF in a brownfield plant). Notably, India’s brownfield
projects fare slightly better than greenfield (owing to lower upfront CAPEX), but neither
achieves breakeven through SRF substitution alone. In fact, at low SRF rates (25%), the
NPV is often more negative than the coal baseline—reflecting the added retrofit costs and
SRF expenses not yet offset by fuel savings. Only at the highest substitution rates does the
operational fuel cost savings nearly compensate for costs (e.g., in India, moving from 0% to
100% SRF improves NPV by ∼e2–3 million, essentially breaking even in cashflow terms
relative to the already-negative baseline). Overall, SRF-only deployment appears to be a net
cost under current conditions, unless augmented by policy credits or other revenue.

Sensitivity Analysis of Key Drivers
To pinpoint the principal economic drivers and the influence of policy measures, a sensitivity
analysis was conducted on three critical parameters: (A) FuelFlex CAPEX, (B) SRF fuel
price, and (C) carbon credit (ETS) price. All sensitivities were run at a representative high
substitution level (75% SRF) to stress-test near-feasible cases.

(A) Retrofit CAPEX sensitivity (± e5–30 million).
The additional
investment for high-TSR capability (FuelFlex) was varied to assess capital intensity effects.
As expected, higher CAPEX shifts NPV downward in a near-linear fashion. In the German
scenario with ETS and pyrolysis (a relatively favorable case), raising the CAPEX from
e5 million to e30 million causes NPV to drop from ∼+ e393 million to ∼+ e368 million
and IRR to slip from ∼12.6% to ∼10.6%, with payback lengthening by only a few years
84
==PAGE:90==
(from 13 to 16)—still within an acceptable range for large projects. In contrast, in a marginal
scenario like India greenfield (with pyrolysis), the same CAPEX increase (5 →30 million)
can be the difference between a modestly positive NPV (+ e33 million, IRR 10.2%) and a
borderline case (+ e8 million, IRR ∼8.5%). Notably, breakeven oil prices remain essentially

for operating revenue enhancements.

(B) SRF price (gate fee) sensitivity.

5.2.3
Discussion: Key Drivers and Policy Implications
5.2.3.1
Coal vs. SRF Cost differentials
The cornerstone of SRF’s appeal is the
potential cost saving per energy unit. Our analysis shows that if SRF is available at a
sufficiently lower effective price (even negative when considering waste gate fees), project
economics improve one-for-one. In regions like Europe with high landfill taxes and mature
waste markets, SRF suppliers often pay cement plants to take waste fuel, creating a strong
operating margin incentive [36].
By contrast, in emerging markets where waste tipping fees are low or non-existent, cement
producers may face a cost to procure quality SRF, eroding the fuel savings. Policy can help
here: enforcing strict landfill diversion (so that waste must go to recovery) and enabling
financial transfers (tipping fees or subsidies) for SRF use can effectively reduce the fuel
cost to cement plants. India’s Solid Waste Management Rules (2016) already push in this
direction—they mandate that cement kilns within 100 km of an RDF plant must use at least
5% RDF, and forbid landfilling of high-calorific waste [149], [150].
This assures a basic demand for SRF and, in principle, should encourage municipalities to
supply RDF at minimal or negative cost. The results indicate, however, that 5% is too low a
substitution to impact economics; raising mandated TSR levels or providing direct financial
incentives for higher SRF use (e.g., subsidy per ton of SRF co-processed) would have a far
greater effect on project viability.
5.2.3.2
Opportunity with Carbon Credits
Monetizing the CO2 emissions avoided by
substituting SRF (which contains a large biogenic fraction) is pivotal for financial feasibility,
especially in regions aiming for net-zero trajectories. The EU ETS, for example, substantially
improves all metrics in our German cases. With a carbon price signal, even "climate benefit
only" projects (SRF-only) nearly break even at high TSR, and when combined with pyrolysis
revenue, the returns become quite attractive. The analysis suggests an ETS price on the
order of e100+ per ton is needed to fully drive positive returns for high SRF adoption absent
other revenues—a level that is within reach given tightening climate policies.
Moreover, the upcoming Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) will further pressure
European cement producers to reduce fossil CO2 or else pay for imports, adding impetus
for alternatives [148]. In India, a direct carbon price is not yet applied in cement, but the
planned Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) is poised to fill this role. The CCTS will set
emissions intensity targets for cement plants and award tradeable carbon credit certificates
(CCCs) to those who overachieve their targets, with trading via power exchanges and a
national registry [151]. This effectively creates a revenue stream for emitters that cut CO2
beyond mandated levels. SRF substitution, with its large emissions reduction per ton of coal
avoided, could enable plants to generate and sell such credits, improving project cashflows.
Although our calculations did not include CCTS credits (due to the scheme’s nascent
status), it is reasonable to expect that a well-implemented CCTS (or a carbon tax) would
significantly raise the NPV/IRR of SRF projects in India, analogous to the ETS effect in
Europe. Policymakers should ensure that co-processing of waste qualifies clearly for credit
89
==PAGE:95==
generation—recognizing the biogenic CO2 and fossil offset—to encourage cement companies
to invest in FuelFlex upgrades. In the absence of a formal carbon market, other mechanisms
like feed-in tariffs for waste heat, tax credits for emissions reduction, or results-based climate
finance could bridge the gap.
5.2.3.3
Pyrolysis oil Revenue turns NPV positive
The option to integrate waste-
to-oil pyrolysis is a game changer in our analysis, often making the difference between a
negative and a positive NPV. This reflects the principle that turning a portion of the waste
into a marketable commodity greatly enhances the project’s profitability and risk profile.
However, realizing this revenue depends on market and regulatory factors: the oil must have
a buyer and a favorable price. In Europe, one promising avenue is to use the pyrolysis oil as
a refinery feedstock qualifying under the EU Renewable Energy Directive’s Recycled Carbon
Fuels criteria.
If recognized as such, refiners can purchase the oil to count towards their renewable targets,
providing a stable offtake. Policy support in the form of product standards and offtake
agreements for recycled fuels can thus underpin the revenue side. Our sensitivity results
(Figure 36) show that at high SRF shares, breakeven oil prices drop to the low hundreds of
e/t, which is within the typical range for heavy fuel oil or crude substitute. In fact, with
strong carbon and waste policies, the project could tolerate even lower oil prices (near zero).
This implies that even in scenarios of oil price volatility or oversupply, the project remains
safe as long as the SRF-related savings and credits are in place. Thus, pyrolysis integration
not only boosts returns but also hedges against policy or market risk by diversifying the
value streams (energy savings, carbon credits, fuel product sales).
5.2.3.4
Supporting CAPEX only is not enough
The need to invest in fuel handling,
dosing systems, and preprocessing (for higher SRF quality) is a known barrier. Our analysis
confirms that CAPEX, while important, is not the primary deal-breaker if the other levers
(fuel cost, carbon, revenue) are aligned. For example, even a e30 million retrofit can be
acceptable in Germany or India brownfield when high SRF and revenues are achieved (IRRs
∼8–15%). Nonetheless, access to low-cost financing or blended finance can improve the NPV
and shorten payback significantly, making projects more palatable to investors. Institutions
like multilateral development banks or green funds (e.g., EIB, IFC) are increasingly willing
to support such circular economy projects, especially if they align with climate goals.
In India, public–private partnership models and viability gap funding may be employed to
defray upfront costs of MBT facilities that produce SRF. On the O&M side, maintaining
SRF quality (calorific value, moisture, chlorine, etc.) is critical to sustain high TSR without
operational issues. Standards like EN 15359 / ISO 21640 classify SRF by quality tiers, and
cement kilns require certain specs (e.g., NCV > 21 MJ/kg, <15% moisture, chlorine usually
<0.8% w/w). Adhering to these specs may increase preprocessing costs, but it is necessary
for safe and efficient co-processing. Regulatory support in terms of technical guidelines and
sharing of best practices (such as those by the German UBA [152] and the IFC [13]) can help
streamline operations and reduce perceived risk for operators adopting SRF at large scale.

==PAGE:96==
5.2.3.5
Waste Recycling and SRF Availability
A subtle factor is the availability of
suitable waste feedstock in the face of evolving circular economy policies. Europe’s aggressive
plastic recycling targets mean that a portion of what is currently going to SRF might be
diverted to recycling in the future. Indeed, as of 2022 roughly two-thirds of sorted plastic
waste in the EU still does not get recycled—it is incinerated (often as SRF/RDF), landfilled
or exported [153]. This leaves ample room for SRF usage now, but as recycling infrastructure
scales up, cement plants may face tighter supply or higher prices for the high-calorific fractions
(since those could be chemically recycled instead).
Policymakers face a balancing act: ensure non-recyclable residuals have a path to co-processing
(which is higher on the waste hierarchy than landfilling) while also not undermining recycling
efforts. In practice, there will remain a significant stream of non-recyclable, high-calorific
waste (multi-layer plastics, contaminated fractions, etc.) even under ambitious recycling
scenarios. Cement kilns are well-positioned to utilize this "residual" stream. Establishing long-
term contracts between cities/waste firms and cement companies, with quality requirements
and indexing, can secure feedstock for both parties.
The Indian context, with its SWM Rules and national "Waste-to-Energy" initiatives, is moving
toward such integration: many cement plants have already partnered with municipalities to
take segregated combustible fractions. Continued policy support to strengthen these linkages
(for instance, enforcing the ban on landfilling >1500 kcal/kg waste and providing viability
gap funding for waste processing plants near clusters of cement factories) will bolster SRF
supply and quality [149], [150].
5.2.3.6
Summary
This work has demonstrated that SRF co-processing can be economi-
cally viable and environmentally impactful, but only under an enabling framework that brings
together favorable fuel economics, additional revenue streams, and policy incentives. High
thermal substitution rates are essential to unlock scale benefits—incrementally replacing coal
by 25% or 50% showed proportional emissions gains but insufficient financial returns, whereas
pushing towards 75–100% (where technically feasible) is what shifts the needle on profits.
The business-as-usual baseline (fossil fuels with no carbon cost) is clearly unattractive in the
long run, especially as sustainability pressures mount.
By combining multiple value drivers—waste gate fees or low SRF cost, monetized CO2
reductions through carbon markets, and innovative by-product valorization (e.g., pyrolysis
oil)—the SRF/FuelFlex pathway can outperform the status quo, delivering both private
returns and societal benefits. However, achieving this in practice requires concerted support:
strong policy signals (carbon pricing, landfill diversion laws, co-processing mandates), clear
regulatory standards (for emissions and fuel quality, so that projects can operate reliably
within environmental norms), and financial mechanisms to lower initial hurdles (green finance,
public incentives). The comparison of Germany and India exemplifies this: Germany’s mix
of high waste fees, stringent landfill bans, and ETS pricing creates a relatively conducive
environment where even near-breakeven projects become worthwhile with a slight nudge.
India, in contrast, is just beginning to implement the needed policies (RDF usage rules, a
carbon market, etc.); with these coming into force and appropriate financial support, the
91
==PAGE:97==
economic case for SRF there could quickly strengthen, particularly for brownfield retrofits
which are closer to viability.
Ultimately, deploying SRF at scale via FuelFlex technology offers a compelling decarbonization
strategy for the cement industry, turning waste into a valuable resource. Realizing its full
potential will depend on aligning economic drivers with climate and waste management
policies—a classic example of the circular economy in action, where what was once a cost
(waste disposal) becomes a profit center and an environmental solution.

==PAGE:98==
6
Conclusion and Future Work
The experimental investigations showed that applying controlled flow pulsation can sub-
stantially improve the mixing of fine cement raw meal with heterogeneous secondary fuels.
Particle density and shape strongly influenced segregation: under steady flow, dense, compact
particles (such as metal beads) settled near the distributor while light or oblate particles
(such as plastic spheres, biomass) tended to accumulate in the mid to upper bed. Introducing
pulsation altered this pattern. For example, higher frequency pulses (10 Hz) or adding a
modest continuous airflow reduced the extreme bottom layer concentration of fuel compared
to a 2.5 Hz fully pulsed baseline, whereas very long "off" periods produced a band of secondary
particles just above the bed. A moderate mean flow (around 25 L min−1 in our setup) with a
1:1 duty ratio achieved the most uniform solids distribution without channeling, although
no tested mode completely eliminated segregation. These findings imply that appropriately
tuned pulsation regimes can enhance solid mixing in a FuelFlex Pyrolyzer, thereby improving
the expected conversion of SRF.
The techno economic analysis then quantified under what conditions the high SRF operating
regimes suggested by the lab work become financially viable. We modeled a 1 Mt/yr clinker
line (in both German and Indian contexts) under a coal baseline and two action cases: 100%
calciner heat from SRF (AC1), and the same plus recovery of a pyrolysis oil co product (AC2).
The results indicate that SRF substitution yields substantial CO2 savings but requires very
favorable economics to be attractive. In particular, the fuel cost spread was a dominant
driver: if SRF can be supplied at effectively zero or negative cost (e.g., via tipping fees) and
carbon credits are captured, even a no value pyrolysis oil case could break even. Including
the value of the pyrolysis oil co product made the investments much more attractive at high
substitution rates. For example, in the German scenario adding pyrolysis oil flipped the net
present value from deeply negative to strongly positive at 100% SRF (even without any ETS
credit), whereas in the India case high SRF shares became viable for both brownfield and
greenfield plants once pyrolysis revenues and high carbon prices were assumed. Overall, the
combined evidence from fluidization experiments and system modeling identifies the specific
pulsation conditions and policy incentives that could enable maximal SRF use in cement
kilns under realistic constraints.
This study has several limitations that suggest directions for future work. The experiments
were performed at laboratory scale with surrogate fuel particles and steady state operation, so
real industrial SRF mixtures, different particle size distributions, transient startup behavior,
or long term attrition were not captured. Likewise, only equilibrium hold up profiles after
extended runs were measured; studying the dynamics of mixing, fine entrainment, and
fluidization under varying pulsation programs could yield further insights. In the techno
economic model, assumptions about SRF prices, retrofit costs, and policy incentives carry
uncertainty, and some features were omitted (for example, the India case did not include
potential carbon credit revenues). Future research should therefore extend the experimental
campaign (for instance using authentic SRF materials and larger or pilot scale reactors)
and refine the economic framework (by incorporating carbon credits, alternative plant
93
==PAGE:99==
configurations, and cost learning for FuelFlex equipment). Developing computational models
of the pulsed bed could also help translate the laboratory findings to industrial scales. By
addressing these uncertainties and scale up challenges, subsequent work can build on this
thesis to guide the deployment of FuelFlex systems that maximize alternative fuel use while
ensuring operational stability and economic viability.
