Abstract
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is an increasing problem worldwide, and it is well known that
one of the key drivers of AMR development is the overuse and misuse of antibiotics.
Two
bacteria, S. pneumoniae and S. aureus, are at high risk of developing AMR and these are
commonly seen with influenza as secondary bacterial infections (SBIs). Influenza frequently
causes epidemics, and during the winter months, up to 20% of the population in the Northern
Hemisphere becomes infected.
Every year target groups are offered an influenza vaccine in Denmark to prevent hospitalisations
and deaths due to influenza. To investigate the connection between the influenza vaccine and
the development of SBIs, the aim of this thesis is to test whether changing the current influenza
vaccine strategy in Denmark could lead to a decrease in SBI related hospitalisations and deaths
as well as the overall use of antibiotics in SBI and influenza hospitalised cases. This is done by
creating two models, one describing influenza transmission and one describing hospitalisations
and deaths with influenza and SBIs. Both models consist of ordinary differential equations
(ODEs) and are primarily validated on data from Statens Serum Institut (SSI). Previous models
have focused on influenza transmission and how to decrease it using different vaccination efforts
or antiviral medication, but not much modelling has been done connecting influenza infections
to concurrent or subsequent bacterial infections.
The results of the influenza model suggest a high burden in not vaccinated 0-6 and 20-49 year
olds of 2,160 and 1,886 infected per 10,000 individuals, respectively. The highest burden in
vaccinated individuals is seen in 64+ year olds with 828 infected per 10,000 individuals. The
high burden in 20-49 year olds is usually not found in other studies. The SBI model estimates
that 3,726 hospitalisations are due to influenza only infections, while 584 are due to an SBI.
Furthermore, a total of 271 deaths are estimated where 28% are due to an SBI. In the hospitalised
individuals, 3,116 receive antibiotics which is 72% of all hospitalisations and only 19% of these
3,116 individuals have an SBI.
When combining the two models, it is shown that alternative vaccine strategies can be used to
decrease the number of SBI hospitalisations and deaths as well as the overall use of antibiotics.
The best vaccine strategies are to vaccinate 79% of 50-64 year olds or 50% of 7-19 year olds, but
due to different constraints they might not be possible to implement. It is therefore concluded
that the best intervention is to vaccinate 30% of 7-19 year olds, since this would decrease the
SBI hospitalisations and deaths as well as the overall use of antibiotics with between 11 and
14%.

Resumé
Antimikrobiel resistens (AMR) er et stigende problem i verden, og det er velkendt, at en af
de vigtigste drivkræfter for udviklingen af AMR er overforbrug og misbrug af antibiotika. To
bakterier, S. pneumoniae og S. aureus, er i øget risiko for at udvikle AMR, og disse ses ofte
sammen med influenza som sekundære bakterielle infektioner (SBI’er). Influenza forårsager ofte
epidemier, og i vintermånederne er op til 20% af befolkningen på den nordlige halvkugle smittet.
Hvert år tilbydes målgrupper en influenzavaccine i Danmark for at forhindre indlæggelser og
dødsfald på grund af en infektion. For at undersøge sammenhængen mellem influenzavaccci-
nen og udvikling af SBI’er, er formålet med dette projekt at undersøge, om en ændring af den
nuværende influenza vaccinestrategi i Danmark kan føre til færre SBI indlæggelser, dødsfald
og generel antibiotikaforbrug i forbindelse med indlæggelser. Dette undersøges ved at lave to
modeller, en der beskriver influenza smitte og en der beskriver indlæggelser og dødsfald med
influenza og SBIs. Begge modeller er opbygget af ordinære differentialligninger (ODE’er) og er
primært valideret mod data fra Statens Serum Institut (SSI). Tidligere modeller har fokuseret
på influenza smitte og hvordan man kan nedbringe den ved hjælp af forskellige vaccination-
sindsatser, men der er ikke lavet meget modellering, der forbinder influenzainfektioner med en
samtidig eller efterfølgende bakterieinfektion.
Resultaterne af influenzamodellen tyder på en høj byrde blandt ikke-vaccinerede 0-6 og 20-49
årige med henholdsvis 2.160 og 1.886 smittede pr. 10.000 individer. Den højeste byrde blandt
vaccinerede ses hos 64+ årige med 828 smittede pr. 10.000 individer. Belastningen blandt 20-49
årige er normalt ikke et fokus i andre studier. SBI modellen estimerer, at 3.726 indlæggelser
skyldes influenza alene, mens 584 skyldes en SBI. Desuden estimeres i alt 271 dødsfald, hvoraf
28% skyldes en SBI. Blandt de indlagte modtager 3.116 antibiotika, hvilket er 72% af alle
indlæggelser, og kun 19% af disse 3.116 personer har en SBI.
Når de to modeller kombineres, kan det konkluderes at influenza vacciner kan bruges til at
reducere antallet af SBI indlæggelser og dødsfald samt det samlede antibiotikaforbrug.
De
bedste vaccinestrategier er at vaccinere 79% af 50-64-årige eller 50% af 7-19-årige, men på
grund af forskellige begrænsninger kan de muligvis ikke implementeres. Det konkluderes derfor,
at de bedste resultater vil være, hvis 30% af 7-19-årige vaccineres, da dette vil reducere SBI
indlæggelser, SBI dødsfald og det samlede antibiotikaforbrug med mellem 11 og 14%.

INTRODUCTION
Sofie Skovgaard Larsen
1
Introduction
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is one of the greatest threats to public health today, and
an estimated 133,000 deaths were attributable to resistant bacteria in 2019 (Mestrovic et al.
2022). AMR develops when microorganisms acquire the ability to adapt and continue growing
in the presence of medicine that would have killed them previously (Dadgostar 2019). One of
the key factors in the development of AMR, is the overuse and misuse of antibiotics in the
healthcare system. Overuse is seen in the hospital setting, where antibiotics are sometimes used
before a diagnosis is made (Dadgostar 2019). Misuse can be connected to doctors meeting the
expectations of patients to receive antibiotics, even if the infection is not proven to be bacterial
(Fletcher-Lartey et al. 2016).
Of the leading pathogens in AMR development, Streptococcus pneumoniae and Staphylococcus
aureus, are most commonly seen as secondary bacterial infection (SBIs) to influenza infections.
These bacterial infections, primarily manifested as pneumonias, occur because the infection with
influenza promotes infection with a bacterial pathogen, either at the same time or soon after
the clearance of the virus (Rowe et al. 2019). Influenza infections are typically most severe in
younger children, older adults, and immunocompromised individuals, and SBIs typically affect
the same groups (Hendaus et al. 2015). For this reason, the AMR issue adds another layer,
complicating the treatment of these infections (Dion and Ashurst 2023; Tyrrell et al. 2021).
Every year, target groups in Denmark are vaccinated against influenza to prevent serious illness
and deaths due to influenza infection (Sundhedsstyrelsen 2024a), but could the influenza vaccine
also be used to protect against SBIs? The idea is that if fewer individuals are infected with
influenza, fewer people will develop an SBI. Therefore, this thesis aims to develop two models:
One for estimating the transmission of influenza during an average influenza season in Denmark,
and one for estimating the number of hospitalisations and deaths that can be attributed to
influenza only and to community-acquired respiratory SBIs seen with influenza.
These two
models will be created based on the vaccine strategy used in the 2023/24 season and calibrated
on the number of influenza cases, hospitalisations, and deaths from the 2022/23 season.
After the two models have been validated, alternative influenza vaccine strategies will be tested
to see if an addition to the current vaccine program can lead to a decrease in hospitalisations
and deaths in individuals admitted with an SBI. Furthermore, the use of antibiotics to treat
hospitalised individuals with influenza only infections and influenza with an SBI will be esti-
mated and compared between the different vaccine strategies. The model should be able to guide
decision-makers in the healthcare system on an alternative use for the influenza vaccine, namely
decreasing the SBI hospitalisations and deaths in addition to the overall use of antibiotics to
treat influenza and SBIs.
This thesis will start with a background section covering the influenza and SBI infections, as
well as the use of antibiotics to treat influenza. This will be followed by an overview of the
AMR problem and an introduction to compartment models. Afterwards, the data used in the
model will be presented followed by an introduction to the influenza model. Then, the SBI
model with hospitalisations and deaths will be shown together with the method for parameter
optimisation. This section will end with a description of the sensitivity analysis, the alternative
vaccine strategies and scenario analysis of the vaccine effectiveness (VE) parameters.

Background
In this section, theory about the influenza virus and secondary bacterial infections (SBIs) will
be provided. Afterwards, the challenge of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) in bacteria will be
presented, followed by an overview of how antibiotics are used in respiratory infections. The
section will end with an introduction to compartment models as well as a look into previous
models.
2.1
Influenza virus
The influenza virus causes acute respiratory infections in humans. It is estimated that 290,000 to
650,000 deaths annually are attributable to influenza infections worldwide. The influenza virus
is divided into four types: A, B, C, and D, with A and B being responsible for seasonal epidemics
and pandemics in humans. The virus is transmitted via aerosols from infected individuals and
replicates on the epithelial lining of the respiratory tract. The influenza virus accumulates small
antigenic changes in the amino acid sequences of the hemagglutinin (HA) and neuraminidase
(NA) molecules, a process called antigenic drift. This allows the virus to evade the human
immune system even if it has developed antibodies from a previous influenza infection. Antigenic
drift most frequently occurs in influenza A and is the reason a new vaccine is recommended
every year to account for the circulating strains (WHO et al. 2022).
In the Northern Hemisphere, influenza epidemics occur during the winter months and infect up
to 20% of the population. The disease is usually mild with fever, sore throat, and body aches,
but can sometimes be severe. Individuals at risk of a severe disease course are young children,
pregnant women, immunocompromised, and the elderly (Tyrrell et al. 2021).
2.1.1
Influenza vaccine
The seasonal influenza vaccine is used in Denmark with the primary purpose of preventing
hospitalisations and deaths due to influenza infection (Sundhedsstyrelsen 2024a). Twice a year,
the World Health Organization (WHO) revises the influenza strains in the vaccine by comparing
them to the circulating strains (WHO et al. 2022). In Denmark, the seasonal vaccine is offered
to target groups from October 1st till January 15th for free. In the 2023/24 season, the target
groups included individuals over 64 years old, individuals with chronic diseases that could result
in severe influenza, pregnant women in 2nd or 3rd trimester, and children between 2 and 6 years
old. The last group was offered a live attenuated vaccine as an intranasal spray, while the rest
of the target groups were offered an inactivated attenuated vaccine given as an intramuscular
injection (Sundhedsstyrelsen 2024a).

2.2
Secondary bacterial infections
Influenza infections are often complicated by secondary bacterial infections (SBIs), which occur
during or soon after the influenza infection. The bacteria most commonly associated with SBIs
are S. pneumoniae and S. aureus (Mochan and Sego 2023).
Several biological mechanisms explain why influenza infection increases the risk of bacterial
infection. During an influenza infection, there is dysregulation of the cytokine response, allowing
bacteria to invade the lung epithelium. Additionally, an influenza infection alters the activation
of effector cells, leading to reduced recruitment of macrophages and neutrophils. As a result, the
bacteria is not effectively cleared by the innate immune response. Finally, influenza-induced
damage to the epithelial lining of the respiratory tract, promotes the adhesion of bacteria,
particularly S. pneumoniae (Mochan and Sego 2023).
2.2.1
S. pneumoniae
S. pneumoniae is a gram-positive bacterium commonly found in the nasopharynx of humans.
Approximately half of the adult population is colonised with this bacterium, while the rate
is lower in children.
S. pneumoniae is a frequent cause of community-acquired pneumonia
(CAP), an acute infection often requiring hospitalisation (Dion and Ashurst 2023). Pneumonia
caused by S. pneumoniae most commonly affect older individuals aged 65 and above, children
under 2 years, and individuals with chronic diseases such as COPD and asthma.
Infection
with S. pneumoniae occurs when the colonised bacteria reach the lower airways, triggering an
inflammatory response (Dion and Ashurst 2023).
In addition to CAP, S. pneumoniae also causes invasive pneumococcal disease (IPD), which
occurs when the bacteria reach sterile sites of the body such as the blood or the central nervous
system (CNS) . The two most common IPDs are pneumococcal meningitis and pneumococcal
bacteremia. Meningitis occurs when bacteria cross the blood-brain barrier, invading the fluids
and tissues surrounding the brain and spinal cord, whereas bacteremia occurs when the bacteria
invade the bloodstream. These infections are most prevalent in very young children and older
individuals (SSI 2020).
In 2007, the PVC7 vaccine, which covers 7 serotypes of S. pneumoniae, was included into the
Danish Childhood Immunisation Program. This was replaced in 2010 by the PVC13 vaccine,
which covers 13 serotypes. Since the introduction of the vaccine in 2007, the incidence of S.
pneumoniae infections in young children has decreased from 55 per 100,000 to 19 per 100,000
in 2019. A smaller decrease in incidence among older individuals is also seen, likely due to herd
immunity. Despite the overall decrease in incidence, infections caused by serotypes not included
in the vaccine have increased (SSI 2020). For individuals aged 65 and above, the PPV23 vaccine,
covering 23 serotypes, was offered for free from 2020 until the beginning of 2023, with a vaccine
uptake exceeding 70%. This initiative was a pilot program, and the vaccine is no longer provided
free of charge (Sundhedsstyrelsen 2023a).

2.2.2
S. aureus
S. aureus is a gram-positive bacterium commonly found on mucous membranes and skin of
humans, with approximately half of all adults colonised. Certain groups, like hospitalised indi-
viduals, immunocompromised, and regular users of needles, have a higher proportion of coloni-
sation. While S. aureus on the skin does not cause infections, it can lead to serious infection if
it enters the bloodstream or internal tissue, such as through wounds. These infections include
bacteremia, skin infections, pulmonary infections, among others.
The pulmonary infections
include pneumonia that is typically seen following an influenza infection (Taylor and Unakal
2023).
2.3

Various studies have investigated the use and types of antibiotics administered during influenza
hospitalisations. In a study from Norway by Fjelltveit et al. 2022, it was found that 69% of

Another Norwegian study by Debes et al. 2023 focused on adults hospitalised with viral res-
piratory tract infections from 2015 to 2018, estimating the extent of the inappropriate use of
antibiotics to treat these. They conclude that 77% of influenza patients received antibiotics
despite lacking signs of bacterial infection.

2.4
Antimicrobial resistance
Bacterial AMR occurs when changes in bacterial DNA make drugs, typically used to treat them,
less effective. AMR is attributed to various factors, with the overuse of antibiotics being the
most recognised. Antibiotics kill susceptible strains of bacteria, allowing resistant strains to
survive and multiply. Another significant factor is the inappropriate prescription of antibiotics,
where studies have found errors in choice or duration of treatment in 30% to 50% of cases.
Additionally, the excessive use of antibiotics in agricultural animals has been linked to the
promotion of AMR in bacteria that can infect humans (Ventola 2015).

2.4.1
AMR in S. pneumoniae
According to the 2022 DANMAP report, there were 560 cases of IPD in that year. Among these

to 5,203 between 2007 and 2015. This demonstrates that the issue with resistant strains of S.
pneumoniae is not diminishing in Europe. The majority of these cases occur in older individuals,
with the highest rates observed in individuals 64+ years old (Ventola 2015).
2.4.2
AMR in S. aureus
The population-level modelling analysis, mentioned in the last section, also examined infec-

resistance type, with the median number of annual cases rising from 112,782 to 143,947 infec-
tions between 2007 and 2015 in the EU and European Economic Area. These infections are
predominantly seen in children under 2 years and older individuals (Cassini et al. 2019).
In 2022, 2,996 confirmed MRSA cases were seen in Denmark. 51% were infections, while the
remainder were found through screenings. This reflects an overall increasing trend since mon-
itoring started in 1994, although it is not as high as in 2018 where 3,669 MRSA cases were
recorded (Ribeiro Duarte et al. 2023).
2.5
Compartment models
Epidemiological modelling aims to predict and/or understand how diseases spread within a
population. One of the most widely used models is the compartment model, which divides the
population into compartments based on disease state. The simplest of these models is the SIR
model, with three compartments: Susceptible (S), Infectious (I), and Recovered (R).
These models are described using ordinary differential equations (ODEs), which dictate how
individuals transition between compartments over time. Because compartment models describe
disease transmission between individuals, they are commonly used to describe infectious dis-
eases, like COVID-19 and influenza.
A usual extension of the SIR model is the SEIR model, which includes an additional com-
partment for Exposed (E). This addition makes the model more realistic by accounting for the
latent period between successful transmission and the time the individual is able to pass on the
infection to others (Keeling and Rohani 2011).

One of the challenges in creating compartment models, is the discrepancy between medical and
infection status. The figure below visualises the difference.
Figure 1: Difference between medical status and infection status
In compartment models, the infection status is modelled, but available measurements are typi-
cally related to the medical status. Despite discrepancies in duration, the measured incubation
period is often used as a substitute for the latent period needed in the model. Similarly, the
symptomatic period is used to describe the infectious period (Keeling and Rohani 2011).
Another common extension to the SIR model, is to divide the population in age or risk groups.
The ordinary SIR model assumes homogeneity and that all individuals are equally likely to
have contact, while adding age or risk groups allow for differentiation in how the different sub
populations behave.
2.5.1
Gamma distributed latent and infectious period
To make the SEIR model more realistic, the Exposed and/or Infectious state can be split into
multiple sub states. This complexity is added to have more control over how long individuals
stay in these states.
In an ordinary SIR model, the latent and infectious period (recovery time) are exponentially
distributed with mean duration 1/ν and 1/λ, respectively. Since they follow an exponential
distribution, there are some individuals that become infectious/recover instantly and some that
take an unrealistically long time to become infectious/recover. This is not feasible since the
periods are usually much more centrally distributed around a mean duration (Lloyd 2001).
To address this, more sub-states can be added for Exposed and Infectious, which will result in
the periods becoming gamma distributed and therefore more centrally distributed.

Adding these additional states and thereby saying that the latent and infectious periods are
not exponentially distributed, makes sure that the basic reproductive ratio (R0) is not under-
estimated. If R0, the number of individuals one infectious individual can infect before dying or
recovering in a totally susceptible population, is underestimated, there is a risk that the results
from the model are too optimistic. This may lead to an underestimation of the control measures
needed to manage the infectious disease (Wearing et al. 2005).

2.5.3
Models of influenza and secondary bacterial infections
Many models have been published predicting the spread of influenza.
Some are created to
test the impact of different vaccination strategies. A model by Arino et al. 2008 investigated
the impact of using antiviral medication and influenza vaccination on the number of infected
individuals during an epidemic. Another model by Pitman et al. 2012 investigated whether
a paediatric vaccine program could reduce general practice consultations, hospitalisations and
deaths due to influenza. Using this model, it was concluded that vaccinating 50% of the 2-18
year olds would bring down, for example, deaths by 76%. A model by Baguelin et al. 2013
tested vaccination in different age groups and concluded that the biggest reduction in number
of infected was seen when extending the current vaccination program (primarily targeting older
individuals) with vaccines to 5-16 year olds. Other influenza models have modelled immunity
propagation between influenza seasons to investigate the reduction in transmission between
seasons due to previous vaccinations and infections (Hill et al. 2019).
Not a lot of mathematical modelling have been done to connect influenza infections to a sub-
sequent or concurrent bacterial infection. One model by Henneman et al. 2012 coupled the
influenza infection to a subsequent bacterial infection and tested influenza vaccines, quarantine
and treatment of the SBI, to see the impact on deaths. The model is simple and only contains
five ODEs and no sub populations. Another model published by Chien et al. 2012, models an
influenza pandemic similar to the 1918 pandemic and connects it to SBIs. Most research on
SBIs is still trials estimating influenza cases with and without an SBI (Klein et al. 2016; Qiao
et al. 2023; Shrestha et al. 2013).

3.1
Age groups in the models
When using a SIR model, the population is only divided by infection state. Population het-
erogeneity can be captured more accurately if further division is considered.
The choice is
usually between age and risk groups (Keeling and Rohani 2011). In this thesis, age groups
will be created to better describe contact patterns and disease course. When selecting the age
groups, a combination of vaccine risk groups and transmission dynamics for airborne diseases
are considered.
It has been shown that school children have a higher incidence of influenza than other groups.
This is supported by observations that overall incidence in the population decreases when schools
close, and increases when they open (Worby et al. 2015). In the Danish vaccination program,
individuals above 64 are offered vaccination due to their risk of severe infections and hospital-
isations (Sundhedsstyrelsen 2024a). Children between 2 and 6 years are offered a vaccine to
decrease transmission to older individuals and reduce the risk of severe infections like pneumonia
(WHO et al. 2022).

3.3
Influenza model
The first model is a model of influenza transmission in the Danish population, and is referred
to as the influenza model. The model is built in a SEEIIR framework with five sub populations
representing the five age groups previously described.
Two sub-states for exposed (E) and
infectious (I) are modelled to make the latent and infectious period gamma distributed, as
described in section 2.5.1. The modelled time scale is days. In the model it is assumed that all
individuals recover.
The model period spans from the beginning of week 38 to the end of week 22 in the following year.
The start date was chosen because the first vaccines in the 2023/24 season were administered
in week 38. Week 22 was selected as the end data to include the majority of seasonal influenza
cases. This period comprises 37 weeks, equivalent to 259 days. The figure below shows the
confirmed cases of influenza during this period in 2022/23.

2023, start and end date of model is shown as dashed lines
The start and end date of the model are visualised with dashed lines, showing that the model
period contains the majority of the confirmed influenza cases observed between the summer of
2022 and the summer of 2023.
In the influenza model, births and deaths are not included because the model period is less than
a year. This ensures that the population remains constant at 5,941,388 individuals.

infected with influenza and the same individual becoming infectious.
Since there are two exposed states the mean time between exposure and becoming infectious
is 2 · 1/ν, this period is also known as the latent period. Since there is not much evidence in
literature that this time varies between age groups and vaccination status this is kept as one
parameter common for all age groups (CDC 2024, WHO 2023).
λi - Recovery rate
λi is the recovery rate for individuals in age group i. It is a measure of how long an individual
is infectious and can pass on the infection to others. It is the rate used to move individuals
between the two infectious states and from the second infectious state to the recovered state.
There are two states for infectious, and therefore the mean infectious period is 2 · 1/λ. Very
little evidence in the literature points to differences in recovery rate between vaccinated and
not vaccinated individuals, and these are often assumed to be the same in models of influenza
transmission. Therefore the recovery rate will only differentiate between age groups (Mercer
et al. 2011; Li et al. 2021).

3.3.3
Modelling procedure
It was decided to run the influenza model in a stochastic framework. For this purpose, the R
package SimInf is used (Widgren et al. 2019). This package utilises continuous-time Markov
chains in the Gillespie stochastic algorithm to produce more realistic epidemiological models.
It also includes the option of timing different events, which in this case will be vaccinations by
moving susceptible individuals from the not vaccinated track to the vaccinated track.
Additionally, the package can simulate a large number of realisations, allowing exploration of
a range of possible epidemiological outcomes. For the results, 100 realisations of the influenza
model will be computed. Most of the results will be presented per 10,000 individuals to allow
comparison between age groups. The recovered compartment will be used to investigate how
many individuals have been infected with influenza during the period, as the influenza model
assumes that all infectious individuals recover.
3.4
Parameter estimation of the influenza model
In this section, the parameter estimation for the influenza model will be explained. As de-
scribed in sections 3.3.1 and 3.3.2, different parameters need to be estimated. The only known
parameters are δi, the numbers in the contact matrix cij and the total population size N. To
estimate parameters, an optimisation procedure is created in R using the function nmkb() from
the package dfoptim. Here selected objectives will the optimised and the case data from SSI
will be used to compare estimates to.
The age groups in the SSI case data and in the model do not match, as seen in figures 4 and
5. Since the SSI case data will be used in the optimisation, it was decided that parameters
optimised for age groups 2, 3 and 4 will be the same. This ensures that the model results
can be compared to the SSI case data. Additionally, most estimations in literature are split
into three categories: Children, adults and 64+ which supports the decision to estimate one
parameter for age groups 2, 3 and 4 instead of three.
As described in section 2.5.2, ascertainment probabilities need to be estimated to compare
confirmed cases in the SSI case data to the estimated number of influenza cases. Here it is only
realistic to estimate three ascertainment probabilities: one for age group 1, one for age group
2, 3, and 4 combined, and one for age group 5.
These probabilities have been estimated in previous modelling studies to range between 0 and
5% (Hill et al. 2019) and to be 1.06% for children, 0.64% for adults and 1.39% for elderly
population (Baguelin et al. 2013). Both models were developed before the COVID-19 pandemic,
so it is reasonable to assume that more tests are conducted today, potentially leading to higher
ascertainment probabilities. This assumption is supported by the number of influenza tests
conducted in Denmark, available in the SSI case dataset. In the 2018/19 influenza season (the
last normal influenza season before COVID-19), 263,056 influenza tests were conducted, whereas
1,046,732 tests were performed in the 2022/23 season.

3.4.1
Objectives in the parameter estimation
For the optimisation procedure, different objectives were considered: The time of the peak in
the influenza model should be close to the peak observed in the 2022/23 influenza season. While
the magnitude cannot be directly compared, it is reasonable to assume that influenza cases in
the Danish population peak at the same time as confirmed cases. Ascertainment probabilities
should also be realistic, ranging from 0 to 10%, with higher probabilities for children and the
elderly compared to the rest of the population.
Additionally, multiple studies on incidence rates in not vaccinated individuals were considered
for comparing to the results of the model. A systemic review and meta-analysis by Somes et al.
2018 covering 32 randomised controlled trials (RTCs), concluded that the attack rate (incidence)
among not vaccinated individuals during an influenza season was 22.5% (95% confidence interval
(CI): 9-46%) for children, 10.7% (95% CI: 4.5-23.2%) for adults, and 8.7% (95% CI: 7-10.8%) for
individuals 64+ years old . This implies that approximately 22.5% of not vaccinated children
would be infected with influenza. Therefore, a goal is to have the incidences for age groups 1
and 2 around 22.5% (the age groups containing children), age groups 3 and 4 around 10.7% and
age group 5 around 8.7%. It should be noted that the incidence for the elderly was only based
on a single study.
The different objectives, mentioned above, were tested in different combinations and the best
overall results, measured as the smallest combined sum of squared errors (SSE), were seen when
optimising the 3 ascertainment probabilities, the peak time for the combined age group 2, 3, 4
and the attack rate for age group 1. In practise, this means that for each objective the SSE is
calculated between the estimated value and the true value. Before combining them, the SSEs
are normalised to make sure that they have equal weight in the optimisation procedure.
3.4.2
Bounds for the estimated parameters
In order to make the parameter optimisation realistic, allowed bounds for the parameters need
to be set.
The parameters that will be optimised are σi, ν, λi, I0i and the ascertainment
probabilities.
σi is the transmission probability and three values are estimated: σ1, σ2,3,4 and σ5. These
parameters are not measurable, and it was decided to create a lower bound of 0.001 for all 3
parameters. The upper bound of σ2,3,4 and σ5 is set to 0.5 while the upper bound for σ1 is
set to 0.6, to reflect that young children have a weaker immune system and have not build an
effective antibody response to influenza (Munoz 2002).
ν is the latency rate and it is used to measure the time from becoming infected to becoming
infectious (latent period), calculated using the formula 2 · 1/ν since there are two sub-states
for exposed. The lower bound for ν is set to 0.5 and the upper to 2. This results in a latent
period between 1 and 4 days, consistent with estimates from the Centers for Disease Control
and Prevention (CDC) (CDC 2024).
λi is the recovery rate for influenza. The recovery time can be calculated with the formula
2·1/λi, due to the two sub-states of infectious. The bounds for λ1 and λ5 are set to 0.2 and 0.5,
resulting in a recovery time between 4 and 10 days. λ2,3,4 is bounded by 0.26 and 0.8, which
results in a recovery time between 2.5 and 7.7 days. This matches what is estimated by the
CDC (CDC 2024).

The mean of the 50 realisations of the influenza model will be
calculated, and used as the input to run 1 realisations of the SBI model. For each parameter
combination, the number of SBI hospitalisations and SBI deaths are saved together with the
values of the parameters.
This data will be used to look at linear correlations between parameters and between parameters
and the two outcomes, by calculating the Pearson correlations. It is expected that minimal
correlation will be found between parameters, since their values are drawn independently from
uniform distributions. The correlations will be calculated using the R function rcorr estimating
the Pearson linear correlations as well as their corresponding p-values from the package RHmisc
(Harrell Jr 2019).
Besides the linear correlations, the partial rank correlation coefficient (PRCC) will be calcu-
lated between the parameters and the two outputs. PRCC estimates the relationship between
a parameter and the outcome, while keeping all other parameters fixed. The magnitude of
the PRCC is used to evaluate how important the uncertainty around the parameter is when
estimating the outcomes. The importance of the parameters can then be compared by looking
at the magnitude of the PRCC. Here the R function epi.prcc from the package epiR will be
used (Stevenson 2015).
For all tests mentioned above, a significance level of 5% will be used.
3.8
Alternative vaccine strategies
The models presented will be used to test different vaccine strategies by modifying the τi(t)
matrix described in section 3.3.1. Four alternative vaccination strategies will be tested:
1. Remove vaccinations in the 2-6 year olds
2. Increase the vaccine uptake in the 2-6 year olds
3. Targeted vaccinations to the school children (7-19 year olds)
4. Change target group from 64+ years to 50+ years
Strategy 1: Remove vaccination of 2-6 year olds
Sundhedsstyrelsen concluded after the 2023/24 influenza season, that the vaccine uptake in the
2-6 year olds during the last three seasons has not been high enough to justify the vaccine effort.
Therefore, the live attenuated vaccine will not be offered in the 2024/25 season and onwards
(Sundhedsstyrelsen 2024b). Due to this it is relevant to see what effects removing these vaccines
will have.

Strategy 2: Increased vaccine uptake in 2-6 year olds
When the live attenuated vaccine was offered for the first time in 2-6 year olds in the 2021/22
season, the ambition from Sundhedsstyrelsen was to vaccinate 85% of the age group. In the next
season, this was downscaled to 75% due to the low vaccine uptake (Sundhedsstyrelsen 2023b).
Therefore, it is relevant to investigate what the impact would be, if it was possible to reach
a high vaccine uptake. For this vaccine strategy, vaccinating 80% of the 2-6 year olds will be
tested.
Strategy 3: Vaccinate a bigger proportion of school children
According to the calculations in table 3 only 0.58% of children between 7 and 19 years were
vaccinated in the 2023/24 season. A study by Worby et al. 2015 investigated how different age
groups, impact the magnitude of an influenza epidemic. Here it was concluded that the age
group, that had the largest role in propagating an influenza epidemic, was children between
5 and 17 years. They also estimated that vaccinating this age group would have the highest
impact. Therefore, it is relevant to test vaccination of the 7-19 year olds. Since there is a big
uncertainty on how large the vaccine uptake would be, three scenarios will be tested: 15%, 30%
and 50%. Another reason to test different percentages is to see how big the difference is when
going from a low vaccine uptake of 15% to a moderate uptake of 50%.
Strategy 4: Change target group from 64+ to 50+ years
It is known that the influenza vaccine has a lower effectiveness in older individuals than in other
age groups (SSI 2023). Therefore, it is relevant to test what happens if the target group was
changed from 64+ year olds to 50+ year olds. With the current vaccine strategy, the uptake
in 64+ year olds is 79%, while it is only 22% in 50-64 year olds. Therefore, it will be tested
what happens if 50-64 year olds have the same vaccine uptake as 64+ year old. Since this would
demand a lot more vaccines, an additional test will be performed, with vaccinating 60% of 50-64
year olds.
3.8.1
Comparing vaccine strategies
To test the above vaccine strategies, they will be compared to the baseline model, where the
current vaccine strategy described in section 3.2.2, is used. The goal is to decrease the number
of SBIs, which will be assessed by comparing SBI hospitalisations and deaths as well as the
overall use of antibiotics. For the last goal mentioned, it was decided to include influenza only
hospitalisations as well, since it has come to light that a lot of antibiotics are used in these
hospitalisations. Furthermore, the required number of vaccines will also be compared, to be
able to assess whether the number of vaccines is feasible in the Danish vaccine program.

3.9
Vaccine effectiveness (VE) scenario analysis
Besides testing alternative vaccine strategies, it is also relevant to investigate what happens
to SBI hospitalisations and deaths, if the vaccine effectiveness (VE) is changed. VE changes
every year depending on how well the strains in the vaccines match the circulating strains.
Furthermore, VE is difficult to estimate due to its calculation in observational studies not
being standardised and not always being adjusted for confounders (Lewnard and Cobey 2018).
Therefore, the two models will be used to test what happens if parameters related to VE (δi
and αi) are changed. These two sets of parameters govern the reduction in force of infections
(VE against infection) and reduction in hospitalisation rate (VE against hospitalisation) due to
influenza vaccination, respectively.
The baseline, using the parameters described in sections 3.3.1 and 3.5.1, will be compared to
different values of δi and αi. For each result, only one parameter is varied while keeping the
others fixed at their baseline values. Since both δi and αi are percentages the values tested will
be baseline value ± 10%. The "high VE" will refer to the lowest value tested for each parameter
since they are multiplied with force of infection and hospitalisation rate, respectively. The "low
VE" will refer to the highest value tested. For example, the baseline value of δ1 is 0.135 (VE
against infection: 86.5%), resulting in the "high VE" value tested being 0.035 (VE against
infection: 96.5%) and the "low VE" being 0.235 (VE against infection: 76.5%).
For each
parameter combination, the mean of 100 realisations of the influenza model is computed, to
create 1 realisation of the SBI model.

5
Discussion
In this section, the different parts of the thesis are discussed. First, the model framework will be
examined followed by a discussion of the data foundation. Next, the results from the baseline
models are looked into and afterwards the different vaccine strategies are discussed. The section
will end with a recommendation to an alternative vaccine program, followed by how the models
can be improved and expanded.
5.1
Model framework and assumptions
In this section, the models developed are the discussed. Overall two models have been developed,
one describing the transmission of influenza and one describing the hospitalisations and deaths
seen with influenza and SBIs.
Influenza model
Different considerations were made when creating the influenza model. It was decided to use
a compartment model, specifically the SEEIIR model, as this is a very common model used
for infectious diseases. Furthermore, the compartments were divided into five age groups to
better capture the population’s heterogeneity. Although the age groups could have been further
divided into risk groups, adding "high" and "low" risk groups would have doubled the number
of ODEs and, therefore, the number of parameters to estimate. Additionally, the age groups
function as a form of risk groups, since it is known that small children and older adults have a
higher risk of infection as well as a higher morbidity (Tyrrell et al. 2021).
Another decision made was regarding the length of the model period. The influenza season in
Denmark, as defined by SSI, runs from week 40 to week 20 of the following year (SSI 2023). It
was decided to initiate the model two weeks earlier, in week 38, since the vaccination program
started in this week in the 2023/24 season. Another option could have been to initiate the model
in week 40 and have a number of individuals start in the vaccinated track, corresponding to the
number of individuals vaccinated in weeks 38-40. The end date in week 22 was chosen because
investigating the SSI case data showed that there are still quite a few new cases between week 20
(May 15th) and week 22 (May 29th). Cutting the period to week 20 would likely underestimate
the number of influenza infections.
Regarding parameters in the influenza model, some were estimated and some were found in
the literature. The parameters governing the decrease in the force of infection in vaccinated
individuals, δi, were estimated from the VEs against infection, which were found on the SSI
website with data from the 2022/23 influenza season (SSI 2023). In the influenza model, it was
decided not to differentiate between influenza A and B, and the VE against infection used in the
model is a simple mean of the two VEs for each age group. It would have been more realistic
to either only model influenza A cases or to use a weighted mean of the VEs, depending on the
number of influenza A and B cases in the season.
In the influenza model, the force of infection dictates that a susceptible individual is equally
likely to be infected by all four infectious compartments (I1N
i
, I2N
i

Baguelin et al. 2013.
This is a simplification since studies have shown that viral shedding
in infectious individuals is not constant (Carrat et al. 2008). Since viral shedding is highly
dependent on the individual, it is generally not modelled in epidemiological models.

A large part of estimating parameters for the influenza model, was to be able to link the
estimated cases to data. In this thesis, it was decided to use the laboratory-confirmed cases
from SSI and link them to the estimated cases by using the ascertainment probabilities, like
in Baguelin et al. 2013 and Hill et al. 2019. Other datasets that could have been used, were
the number of influenza-like illness consultations monitored by doctors and reported to SSI or
the voluntary reporting in Influmeter, where individuals can report if they have had influenza-
like symptoms (SSI 2024a).
The challenge with using these datasets, would be that many
diseases can manifest with symptoms similar to influenza. Therefore, it was decided to use the
laboratory-confirmed influenza cases from SSI.
SBI model
One of the main choices made for the SBI model was which bacteria to include. Besides esti-
mating how many SBIs were attributable to S. pneumoniae and S. aureus, Klein et al. 2016 also
estimated that P. aeruginosa, S. pyogenes, H. influenzae, K. pneumoniae, and M. pneumoniae
each accounted for 2.5-6.5% of the SBIs. It was decided to only include S. pneumoniae and S.
aureus for several reasons. First, they account for an average of 63% of the SBIs with a 95%
CI of 30-96% and are therefore the majority. Secondly, they are the most commonly seen as
respiratory infections. It was therefore decided to disregard all other bacterial infections.
In general, it was a challenge to find bacteria-specific parameters, and another choice could
therefore have been to model SBIs as a whole group instead of two specific SBIs. The only
difference between the two SBIs in the model ended up being the percentage of hospitalisations
due to each. Thus, it was also decided that when vaccine strategies were compared, the results
from the two SBIs were combined.
Since there was a large uncertainty in the percentage of hospitalisations due to influenza only,
influenza with S. pneumoniae and influenza with S. aureus, it was decided to update these
values at each time point. In practice this meant creating a matrix with 3 columns (one for
each infection) and 259 rows (number of days in the model period), where each row sum to
1. The values were drawn from normal distributions covering the distributions of the three
infections. This matrix has been used for all realisations of the SBI model. It was decided not
to update the matrix for every realisation, since it would add extra complexity.
Modelling framework overall
Early in the model development, it was decided to create two separate models, where the results
from the influenza model would feed into the SBI model. The primary reason for this was the
parameter estimation of the influenza model. Estimating the influenza parameters, without
considering hospitalisations and deaths, ensured that the primary focus was on having the
correct peak time and number of infected, instead of also focusing on matching hospitalisations
and deaths.

Since it was decided to create two models, the types could differ, and it was chosen to model one
in a stochastic framework. Selecting the influenza model ensured that results from both models
were stochastic. The results from the SBI model are stochastic due to the stochastic input from
the influenza model. It was decided not to run both models in a stochastic framework, as this
would significantly increase computational time. For instance, each of the 100 realisations of
the influenza model would then be used to generate 100 realisations of the SBI model, resulting
in 10,000 realisations in total. Following the current approach, each of the 100 realisations of
the influenza model is used to run 1 realisation of the SBI model, resulting in a total of 100
realisations.

Data and its use in the models
To validate the models, various data sources have been used. The data on influenza cases are
collected by SSI and are available for download. There were two challenges related to using
this data in the influenza model. The first challenge was linking laboratory-confirmed influenza
cases to the estimated cases, as described in the previous section. The second challenge was the
mismatch between the age groups modelled and those in the SSI data. Furthermore, the age
groups in the SSI vaccine data did not match the age groups in the SSI case data. The figure
below illustrates the age groups used in the models compared to those available in the data.
Figure 33: Comparing the model age groups to the age groups avail-
able in the different datasets
The figure illustrates that regardless of which age groups were chosen, some data would need
to be divided.
Since 2-6 year olds receive a different vaccine compared to the rest of the
population, it was decided to create a cutoff between 6 and 7 years. The age group 7-19 year
olds was chosen to model the children in the school system. The age group 20-49 year olds
was selected to align with the vaccine age data. The 50-64 year olds were selected primarily
to test vaccine strategies in this age group. The age group 64+ year olds is commonly used
in influenza data and modelling, and it matches both vaccine and case data. Overall, the age
groups were primarily selected to align with vaccine data because the goal was to test different
vaccine strategies. As a result, the case data does not match well with the selected age groups.
This caused the parameter estimation for the models to become less precise, as the parameters
for the middle three age groups were estimated as one in both models.

Contact matrix
To estimate the force of infection, the number of contacts individuals in one age group have
with other age groups was needed. For this a contact matrix is usually used, but it can be
implemented in different ways. In this thesis the generalisation of the POLYMOD study to the
Danish population was used (Prem et al. 2017). The data is given in 5 year intervals from 0-4
to 75-80 years and to use the data in the influenza model, these intervals were aggregated into
larger age groups resembling the age groups in the model. The two youngest age groups do not
match these aggregated age groups. For the youngest age group, 0-6 year olds, it was decided
to use the 0-4 year age group from the study. Another option would have been to aggregate 0-4
and 5-9 to describe 0-6 year olds, but since most children in Denmark start school when they
are 6 years old, it was concluded that the contact pattern of 0-4 year olds would be better at
describing 0-6 year olds than the combined contact pattern of the 0-4 and 5-9 year olds. For
age group 2, 7-19 year olds, it was decided to use the aggregated 5-19 age group from the study.
Regarding the contact patterns, seasonal variation could also have been included (Keeling and
Rohani 2011). It could be done by changing the overall contact matrix, for example to account
for small children having more contacts with e.g. grandparents during Christmas than the rest
of the year. The seasonal variation could also be included by scaling the number of contacts up
and down during different periods. An example of this is a model developed by Baguelin et al.
2013 where the number of contacts were weighted differently whether it was a weekday or not.
Another example is the implementation of a sine wave scaling the contact matrix, as done in
Pitman et al. 2012. Common for the two is that the model periods are multiple years (30 and
14 years) and therefore seasonal forcing by scaling the contact matrix is much more important
to estimate recurrent epidemics. Since the model period in the thesis is less than a year and to
avoid having more parameters to estimate, it was decided to use the contact matrix as it was
and not change it during the model period.
Hospitalisations and deaths
An assumption that potentially had a significant impact on the SBI model was how the SSI
influenza hospitalisations and deaths data was used. The model assumes that all influenza and
influenza with SBI hospitalisations and deaths are captured in the influenza data. However,
there is a risk that this underestimates the combined number of hospitalisations and deaths
because individuals hospitalised with for example S. pneumoniae pneumonia might not be
tested for influenza as well. This means that if the pneumonia was actually an SBI with an
influenza infection, it would not be captured.
It is known that SBIs are a large risk in small children under 2 years (Arranz-Herrero et al.
2023). However, in the baseline model only 1 SBI death was estimated for the youngest age
group (0-6 year olds), so it seems like the two cannot both be true. From Danmarks Statistik
data on how many deaths are due to infectious diseases in different age groups is available. In
2022, only 3 deaths were seen for children under 10 years (Danmarks Statistik 2022). Therefore
the 1 estimated SBI death might not be far off.

Antibiotics estimations
In the SBI model, the antibiotic use in influenza hospitalisations is a simple calculation using the
number of hospitalisations and percentages found in a few different studies reflecting how many
influenza hospitalised individuals are treated with antibiotics without signs of a bacterial infec-
tion (Debes et al. 2023; Fjelltveit et al. 2022; Schober et al. 2023). These studies investigated
either children 0-16 years old or adults. The studies in adults had a median age above 65 years,
meaning the estimated values from these studies were difficult to generalise to younger adults.
This makes the overall antibiotic use estimation more uncertain. To make this estimation more
correct, studies in younger adults would be needed. An article from The Lancet by Longevity
2023 has found that individuals above 64 years consume around 50% more antibiotics than
younger adults. This clearly shows that the studies mainly focusing on the older population,
cannot be generalised to the younger adults.
In the study conducted by Debes et al. 2023, the antibiotics used were also categorised based
on the WHO AWaRe classification.
WHO divides antibiotics into three categories, Access,
Watch and Reserve, based on their associated risk of developing resistance in bacteria. Access
antibiotics have a narrow spectrum and lower risk of resistance development, while Watch
antibiotics pose higher resistance risks with broader activity. Reserve antibiotics are reserved
as a last resort in treatment of multi-resistant bacteria (Zanichelli et al. 2023). It would be
relevant to estimate how the antibiotics were distributed in the hospitalised individuals, but
in the study the median age was 73 years, and therefore it is difficult to generalise to all age
groups modelled. Furthermore, the study is only based on 951 individuals in one country. For
these reasons, this extra division was not included.
5.3
Results
In this part of the discussion, the result section will be looked through and key elements will
be discussed and reflected upon.
Influenza model
First the parameter estimation for the influenza model is investigated.
Overall the biggest
weakness, as mentioned during the data discussion, was that with the current data it was not
possible to estimated one parameter for each age group. This results in parameters for 7-64
year olds being estimated as one. It is not realistic to assume that disease dynamics for a 7
year old is the same as for a 64 year old. Another way of setting up the estimation could be
to estimate against age groups that do not match perfectly. For example, parameters related
to 20-49 year olds could have been estimated using the SSI case data for 15-44 year olds. The
problem would then be to make sure that the total number of influenza cases still matches the
total number of laboratory-confirmed cases using the ascertainment probabilities. Furthermore,
it would increase the number of parameters that needed to be estimated from the current 13 to
21 and thereby make it more difficult for the optimiser to converge. To investigate if this is a
common problem in influenza modelling, published models were looked into. In Baguelin et al.
2013, the age groups selected matched the age groups from the case data. In Pitman et al.
2012, 100 age groups were modelled, one for each age, and the model was validated against data
collected between 1979 and 1981. Neither of the studies therefore had the same problem with
matching age groups as in this thesis.

Sofie Skovgaard Larsen
In the validation of the influenza model, it was surprising that the estimated attack rate for
20-49 year olds was close to the attack rate estimated for 0-6 year olds (19.5% vs. 22.5%).
In literature, the consensus is that adults have a much lower attack rate than children but
this was not the case in the model results (CDC 2024; Worby et al. 2015). Therefore it is
possible that the 19.5% is unrealistically high. A reason for this could be that the model did
not include immune propagation, meaning that earlier influenza infection and vaccination were
not captured. Adults have been exposed to many strains of influenza, and since their force of
infection is not downscaled with a factor describing protection due to earlier influenza infection
or vaccine, the attack rate can be artificially high.
Looking at the 64+ year olds, the attack rate in not vaccinated was estimated to be 16.8%.
This is quite a bit higher than the goal of 8.7%, but since the goal is based on 1 RTC, it is
difficult to say whether this is an unrealistic number.
From the results of the influenza model, the highest burdens in not vaccinated individuals were
seen in 0-6 and 20-49 year olds with 2,160 and 1,886 infected per 10,000, respectively. In the
vaccinated track the highest burdens were seen for 20-49 and 64+ year olds with 867 and 828
infected per 10,000, respectively. Here it is again clear that the burden in 20-49 year olds is
higher than expected, and this again highlights the problem mentioned above for the attack
rate for this age group.
The burden in 7-19 year olds, disregarding vaccine status, was estimated to be below 1,300 per
10,000, and thereby the third lowest among the five age groups. This stands in contrast to other
modelling studies of influenza where this age group is regarded as one of the most important
in propagating the influenza epidemics (Worby et al. 2015; Baguelin et al. 2013; Pitman et al.
2012). A possible explanation could be that the transmission probability for the combined 7-64
year olds was estimated to be 0.1 while it was 0.24 for 0-6 year olds. It could be that the
transmission probability for 7-19 year olds should be closer to the one estimated for 0-6 year
olds. Another explanation could be the problem with not including protection due to previous
influenza infections and vaccines. Since 7-19 year olds have been exposed to fewer influenza
strains than, for example, 20-49 year olds, including a parameter governing the protection from
previous seasons would likely change the dynamics.
SBI model
The parameter estimation of the SBI model was set up using the same framework as for the
influenza model, and here the hospitalisations and deaths rates were estimated against the SSI
case data. When looking at the validation of the baseline SBI model, a potential problem was
seen for the 64+ year olds. Even though there was no distinct peak in the data, the estimation
peaked too late, which meant that the curve had not decreased to zero at the end of the model
period. This could potentially have made the dynamics of the SBI model a bit off compared
to the development in data. A way to mitigate this, could have been to add peak time to the
objectives in the parameter estimation. Then the total number of hospitalisations and deaths,
which are the current objectives, would likely not be exactly the same as seen in the data, but
it would maybe have created a dynamic more true to the data.

DISCUSSION

When looking at the results from the SBI model, most hospitalisations and deaths were seen
in 64+ year olds, which is no surprise due to their weaker immune system and poorer VE. The
burden translated to 15.7 hospitalisations per 10,000 individuals. The second highest burden
was seen in 0-6 year olds, with 11.7 hospitalisations per 10,000. This is expected due to the
youngest children having a weaker immune system and therefore SBIs are more commonly seen
in this age group (Hendaus et al. 2015). This trend is also seen in a systemic review by Arranz-
Herrero et al. 2023 covering 48,259 influenza patients. Here the largest burdens of SBIs were
seen in children and older adults.
This thesis highlights that antibiotics are often used in hospitalised individuals even when there
is no confirmed bacterial infection. The most effective measure against this, would be to ask
doctors to not prescribe antibiotics before a test for bacterial infection is positive, but the reality
is that this is not feasible in the today’s society.
In an article by Matthiessen et al. 2022, the antibiotic resistance crisis is investigated using
systems thinking. This method looks at a given problem and investigates how all the pieces
connect to and effect each other. One of the tools is the iceberg model, which describes what
patterns and trends are seen on the surface, while the mental models and underlying structures
are described as being under the water. The mental models describe the assumptions and values
that individuals have for the problem. Regarding the antibiotic resistance crisis, some of the
mental models are the believe that when you go to the doctor, you expect to get medication
since this is necessary to feel better. Another mental part is that a doctor would rather treat
with antibiotics to be on the "safe" side. Some of these underlying structures makes it difficult
to convince both doctors and patients that holding back on antibiotics might be the best course
of action in the long haul.
Sensitivity analysis
In the sensitivity analysis, two calculations were made: Pearson correlation coefficients measur-
ing linear correlation and PRCC. In the linear analysis, the most important parameters were
the recovery rate and transmission probability for 20-49 year olds (λ3 and σ3). Again this was
a curious result since this age group is usually not seen as an important contributor to an in-
fluenza season. It is possible that the importance of the age group was also enhanced by its
large size, since it consists of 38% of the Danish population. A way to mitigate this could be to
select age groups that are more equal in size, but then the problem with fitting to the SSI data
would again be relevant. Another option is again to include a parameter governing previous
protection in the influenza model.
When the sensitivity was evaluated using the PRCC, the same general trend was seen. When
looking at SBI hospitalisations, the recovery rate for 7-19 and 64+ year olds (λ2, λ5) also had
an impact. This draws the picture that in general how fast individuals recover from influenza
has a big impact on how many end up in the hospital with an SBI. The reason these parameters
are so important must be that a longer recovery time results in the infectious individual being
able to spread the infection to more people. When investigating the parameters important to
the number of SBI deaths, it is clear that most of the parameters are related to the 64+ year
olds. This makes sense since most SBI deaths are seen in this age group.

Alternative vaccine strategies
The main goal of this thesis was to test different vaccine strategies and see how they impact
the number of SBI hospitalisations and deaths as well as the overall use of antibiotics.
The largest decrease across all outcomes was seen if the vaccine uptake in 50-64 year olds was
increased to 79%. This would however increase the number of vaccines needed by 45.1% and it
might not be possible. It would increase the workload on doctors and pharmacies, but on the
other hand this age group would maybe be more likely to take the vaccine, then for example
school children, since 50-64 year olds are in bigger risk of complications.
Besides vaccinating 79% of 50-64 year olds, the next best strategy was to vaccinate 50% of the
7-19 year olds, and this increased the number of vaccines required by 28.7%. Here the challenge
becomes to get them to accept a vaccine against a disease that typically is not serious to them.
The same issue was seen under the COVID-19 pandemic, where 5-11 year olds were encouraged
to accept the vaccine and the uptake ended up being 40% compared to, for example, 83% in
18-29 year olds (Danmarks Statistik 2023; Sundhedsstyrelsen and Statens Serum Institut 2021).
It is important to note that a COVID-19 vaccine or a negative test was needed for many things
during the pandemic, so this might have incentivised more to accept the vaccine. This will
likely not be the case if 7-19 year olds were asked to accept the influenza vaccine. When looking
at the more moderate, and maybe more realistic, vaccine strategies the most effective was to
vaccinate 30% of the 7-19 year olds.
Another relevant thing to investigate, was what the impact would be when the vaccines were
removed from 2-6 year olds from 2024 and onward. The number of SBI hospitalisations was
estimated to increase with 4.1%, where 45.8% of them are 0-6 year olds. This is not a very
large difference, and it suggests that, with the current vaccine uptake, the vaccine benefits do
not outweigh the cost of the program. This is also the conclusion drawn by Sundhedsstyrelsen
(Sundhedsstyrelsen 2024b). If it was possible to increase the vaccine uptake to 80%, which is
similar to the goal set by Sundhedsstyrelsen in the previous years, it would decrease SBI hospi-
talisations by 13%, where 46% of them are 0-6 year olds. Similarly the number of individuals
receiving antibiotics would decrease by 12%. This supports why the vaccine program for 2-6
year olds was introduced in the first place. A reason for vaccinating 2-6 year olds, instead of
7-19 year olds, is that the younger children are more at risk of serious complications following
an influenza infection. The parents should therefore, in theory, be more likely to vaccinate the
youngest age group since it would have a bigger direct effect.
In most modelling studies investigating how the influenza vaccine can be used to decrease visits
to the doctor, hospitalisations and deaths due to influenza infection, it is concluded that the
most effective vaccine strategy is to vaccinate children under 18 years on top of the program of
vaccinating individuals above 64 years (Baguelin et al. 2013; Pitman et al. 2012). The results
from testing the different vaccine strategies on reduction in SBI hospitalisations and deaths
as well as antibiotic use, suggested that the most effective, when disregarding the number of
vaccines required, was to vaccinate 79% of 50-64 year olds, but this was closely followed by
vaccinating 50% of 7-19 year olds. This indicates that the results found in modelling studies
of influenza where the goal is to decrease hospitalisations and deaths due to influenza, can be
translated reasonably well into having a similar impact on SBI hospitalisations and deaths.

Two models connecting influenza infection to SBIs were also found during the research. Both
models by Henneman et al. 2012 and Chien et al. 2012 modelled an influenza season similar to
the 1918 pandemic. During influenza pandemics it is believed that SBIs are more common, than
during epidemics, and therefore the results cannot be compared directly (Wang et al. 2011).
The model by Henneman et al. 2012 consist of 5 ODEs and no populations dynamics. They
estimate that if 22% of the population is vaccinated, the pandemic with influenza and especially
S. pneumoniae could be prevented. The model is set up such that all vaccines are given before
the start of the season, and this again makes it difficult to compare. Chien et al. 2012 focus on
S. pneumoniae carriage and how to use antibiotics prophylactically. Again this is outside the
scope of this thesis, and is not easily compared.
VE scenarios
In the analysis of the VE impact on SBI hospitalisations and deaths, it became clear that the
vaccines ability to protect against influenza infection in 20-49 and 64+ year olds were the most
important. This indicated that if the VE against infection could be increased, especially for 64+
year olds, it would have a large impact. Multiple experts, including Allan Randrup Thomsen
from Copenhagen University, have stated that a high dose influenza vaccine with four times as
much antigen is available in Denmark but it is not included in the vaccine program (Braagaard
2023). Administering this vaccine to 64+ year olds would therefore likely decrease the number
of SBI hospitalisations and deaths.
Sundhedsstyrelsen stated in March 2024 that individuals above 70 years will receive an adju-
vanted vaccine which is proven to be better than the current vaccine given to this age group
(Sundhedsstyrelsen 2024c). The high dose vaccine introduced in the last section will still not
be in the vaccine program, even though it was offered to individuals above 85 years in 2020-22.
No matter which of the alternative vaccines, the high dose or the adjuvanted vaccine, was offered
to individuals in the 64+ age group, it is likely that it would have a positive effect on the number
of SBI hospitalisations and deaths.
5.4
Recommendations
Based on the analysis performed in this thesis, the largest decrease in SBI hospitalisations, SBI
deaths and overall use of antibiotics in influenza and SBI hospitalisations was to supplement
the current vaccine strategy with vaccinating 79% of the 50-64 year olds or 50% of 7-19 year
olds. Since both strategies require a large increase in vaccines, and 7-19 year olds might not be
convinced to accept the vaccine, these strategies are likely unrealistic.
Among the rest of the vaccine strategies vaccinating 30% of 7-19 year olds gave a better result
than vaccinating 60% of 50-64 year olds and would require ∼76.000 less vaccines. The results
of vaccinating 80% of 2-6 year olds showed why this was implemented in the vaccine program,
but since it has not been possible to reach a high vaccine uptake, this will not be recommended.
It is therefore deemed that the most realistic addition to the current vaccine strategy would be
to implement a vaccine effort towards the 7-19 year olds and see if a minimum of 30% per year
would be willing to accept the vaccine.
Another recommendation is to offer the 64+ year olds a better vaccine since the VE against
infection has proven to be a key factor in decreasing SBI hospitalisations and deaths. This
would mean to either offer them the high dose or the adjuvanted vaccine.

5.5
Future perspectives
To this thesis many adjustments and expansions could be made with more data and more time.
In the influenza model it would be very relevant to include a parameter, scaling the force of
infection, that captures protection from previous infections and vaccinations. This would likely
mitigate the possibly artificial large burden that is seen in 20-49 year olds.
This project only covers SBIs due to S. pneumoniae and S. aureus admitted to the hospital. It
could also be relevant to look at respiratory SBIs that occur outside the hospital, but this would
require data where doctors in general practice test for both influenza and bacterial infection.
Another option could be to do an observational study where individuals are regularly tested for
both influenza and bacterial infections.
SBIs with S. pneumoniae and S. aureus are estimated to account for 63% of the SBIs so either
adding more bacterial species or estimating SBIs as a whole could make the model results more
realistic. The SBIs in this thesis only cover community-acquired respiratory infections, but it
could also be interesting to include other types of SBIs. Here it could be relevant to look into
healthcare-associated infections. It is estimated that 7% of hospitalised individuals in developed
countries end up with a healthcare-associate bacterial infection (Khan et al. 2017).
When investigating SBIs, it would be relevant to divide the infections by whether they are
caused by bacteria susceptible or resistant to commonly used antibiotics.
It is known that
infections with resistant bacteria are more difficult to treat than susceptible bacteria, resulting
in a higher mortality in individuals with resistant bacterial infections (D’Agata et al. 2008).
An improvement to the model would be a better estimate of how many individuals are hospi-
talised with an SBI, since it is not believed that all of these infections are captured as an influenza
hospitalisation in the SSI dataset. It could maybe be possible to combine the SSI dataset with
a dataset containing the respiratory bacterial infections found in hospitalised individuals.
Another possible improvement could be to optimise how the parameters for the two models are
estimated. At the moment, the biggest problem is that parameters for 7-19, 20-49 and 50-64
year olds are estimated as one age group. To combat this, the data provided on influenza cases
would need to be divided differently to better match the age groups in the models. In general,
it would also be an advantage if the two datasets from SSI were split into the same age groups,
which is right now not the case.
Since recovery times seem to be some of the most important parameters, it would also be relevant
to obtain more accurate values and representations of them.
There is a general consensus
that viral shedding is not constant during the infectious period, even though this is how it is
implemented in the influenza model (Aoki and Boivin 2009; CDC 2024). To take this biological
fact into account, a model framework besides ODEs would likely have to be implemented, like
an individual based model (IBM). The downside of an IBM is the long computational time since
each individual is modelled individually, and this would be difficult to do on the whole Danish
population.

Another thing that could be relevant to investigate, when recovery times are so important for
the outcomes, is that quarantine maybe could be an effective tool to decrease the number of SBI
hospitalisations and deaths. Quarantining individuals infectious with influenza would remove or
limit their ability to infect others and therefore effectively lower the time an infectious individual
have to infect others (Keeling and Rohani 2011). When looking at the number of laboratory-
confirmed influenza cases during the COVID-19 pandemic, it is clear that the restrictions,
including social distancing and quarantine of close contacts and infected with COVID-19, had
a large impact. From week 10 2020 to week 5 2022 (almost two years) only 5,378 laboratory-
confirmed cases of influenza were recorded while the number was 42,276 from week 38 2022 to
week 22 2023 (less than a year) (SSI 2024b).
Regarding the vaccine strategies, it could also be relevant to test combinations of them. Maybe
it would be possible to decrease the number of vaccines needed if a mixture of 7-19 and 50-64 year
olds were vaccinated, but still have a large decrease in, for example, antibiotics use. Another
interesting thing could be to create an optimisations procedure that is given a maximum number
of vaccines, and then distributes the vaccines to where they would give the largest decrease in
SBI hospitalisations and deaths as well as the overall use of antibiotics.
Besides model expansions, it could also be relevant to investigate the reason why the vaccine
uptake in 2-6 year olds did not reach a satisfactory level. Finding out if it was an active choice
by parents or if the vaccine program was not well enough advertised, could help shape a future
vaccine effort in young children.
6
Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis was to test different influenza vaccine strategies and see the impact on
hospitalisations and deaths with SBIs as well as the overall use of antibiotics to treat individuals
admitted to a hospital with influenza infections and SBIs. Decreasing the use of antibiotics
should then decrease the risk of further development of AMR.
To do this, two model were developed: One describing influenza transmission in the Danish
population and one describing the hospitalisations and deaths associated with influenza and SBI
admissions. Both models were divided into five age groups to differentiate the disease dynamics
seen in these groups and they were both developed using an ODE framework. The influenza
model was modelled in a stochastic framework and the number of infectious individuals each
day was used as input for the SBI model. The SBI model was run in a deterministic framework.
The influenza model was able to capture the dynamics seen in the 2022/23 influenza season.
The model estimated a high burden among not vaccinated young children as well as an expected
high burden in vaccinated older individuals. In both tracks, a high burden was seen in 20-49
year olds, which contradicts previous studies conducted. The SBI model was able to capture the
dynamics seen in hospitalisations and deaths, but more data would allow for a more accurate
estimation of SBI related hospitalisations and deaths.
The sensitivity analysis showed that
influenza recovery rates have a big influence on the number of SBI hospitalisations and deaths.
Furthermore, it was shown that improving the VE against infection in the oldest age group
would decrease the number of SBI hospitalisations and deaths significantly.

Appendices
A
Vaccine augmentation to fit the five age groups
The vaccination data described in section 3.2.2 is available in 2 different datasets, one for age
groups and one for target groups. The age groups are in 10 year intervals 0 −9, 10 −19, ..., 80 −
89, 90+ and the target groups are 2-6 years, Pregnant, Increased risk, +64 years, Nursing home
resident and Other. As mention in 3.1 the age groups used in the models are age group 1 (0-6
years), 2 (7-19 years), 3 (20-49 years), 4 (50-64 years) and 5 (64+ years).
Age group 5 is extracted directly for the target groups dataset from the targets +64 years and
Nursing home resident. Therefore it is assumed that all residents in nursing homes are at least
65 years old.
Age group 4 is extracted by combining age groups 50-59, 60-69, 70-79, 80-89 and 90+ from the
age group dataset. From this the data from age group 5 is subtracted.
Age group 3 is extracted by combining age groups 20-29, 30-39 and 40-49 from the age group
dataset.
Age group 2 is extracted by combining age group 0-9 and 10-19 and subtracting the target
group 2-6 years. This also assumes that all individuals vaccinated in the age group 0-9 is either
in the target group 2-6 years or 7-9 years, meaning no vaccination to 0-1 years old.
Age groups 1 is the target group 2-6 years from the target groups dataset.
The data is weekly, so to extend it to daily vaccinations the weekly vaccinations are split evenly
into the 5 first days of the week.
