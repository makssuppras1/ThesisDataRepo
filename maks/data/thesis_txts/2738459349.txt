Preface
This thesis is the final project and fulfilment of a M.Sc. in biomedical engineering at
the Technical University of Denmark. The work in this project has been conducted in
collaboration with Department of Health Technology, Technical University of Denmark,
Kongens Lyngby, Denmark and Stanford Center for Sleep Sciences and Medicine, Stanford
University, Palo Alto, CA. The objective of the project was to investigate new valuable
features related to idiopathic rapid eye movement sleep behavioural disorder iRBD and
narcolepsy type 1 NT1 through analysis of the electroencephalogram (EEG), conducting
an advanced coherence analysis and by the use of different similarity measures applied
to hypnodensity features (sleep stage probabilities). The workload of the project was 35
ECTS and the project period was 10th of January - 10th of July 2024.

Abstract
The purpose of this thesis was to detect new biomarkers for two neurological disorders with
sleep disturbances. The new features should be added to large already well functioning
machine learning models in the future. The features were extracted from polysomnographic
recordings. A sub-aim of this thesis was to investigate possible local and global sleep
patterns in brain regions.
The first disease investigated was idiopathic Rapid Eye Movement sleep Behavioral Disorder
(iRBD) to find potential early biomarkers for Parkinson’s Disease (PD). The second disease
investigated was narcolepsy type 1 (NT1).
An overall hypothesis was tested with an exploratory approach, by fulfilling three aims.
The hypothesis was that new features uncovering unique coherence patterns within specific
frequency bands and sleep stage synchronization could serve as potential biomarkers
for early disease identification and differentiation of Parkinson’s Disease and narcolepsy
[1, 2]. Aim 1: Perform an advanced coherence analysis considering the temporal, spectral
and spatial information from an EEG recording [3].
Aim 2: Perform a sleep stage
synchronization analysis using similarity measures for comparison of hypnodensity features
(sleep stage probabilities) [4, 1, 5]. Aim 3: Implement a classifier to extract the valuable
features from the advanced coherence analysis and the sleep stage synchronization analysis,
to make two disease specific profiles. The methods applied to solve the three aims were, aim
1: coherence analysis comparing all electrode combinations using EEG signals of different
lengths. Aim 2: An automatic high-frequency sleep staging algorithm (U-sleep) was
implemented to calculate hypnodensity features (sleep stage probabilities). The similarity
of sleep stages and transitions of sleep stages in different electrodes, were investigated using
correlation and dynamic time warping. Aim 3: A boosted decision tree was implemented
to extract the valuable features from each of the two analysis performed. A disease specific
profile was set up for NT1 and another profile was set up for iRBD. The NT1 model had
a F1-score of 0.86 and an accuracy of 0.80. From the NT1 model, it was found that the
features from the advanced coherence analysis were less important to the model compared
to sleep stage synchronization features. It was found that REM sleep and sleep stage
transitions related to REM sleep differed significantly more between the two electrodes
C3M2-C4M1 in NT1 patients compared to controls. The same patterns were observed for
the wake stages in NT1.
The iRBD model had an F1-score of 0.64 and accuracy of 0.77. From the iRBD model, it
was found that the coherence gamma band in REM sleep could be a global phenomenon
since both inter-hemispheric and intra-hemispheric activity were significantly higher in
==PAGE:5==
iRBD patients compared to controls. The sleep stage synchronization analysis showed
that N1 activity with a 1 second resolution was more similar for patients compared to
controls in the F3M2-F4M1 electrodes. This activity could be a local phenomenon.

Resum´e
Form˚alet med denne afhandling var at undersøge nye biomarkører for to neurologiske lidel-
ser med søvnforstyrrelser. De nye features kan tilføjes til allerede velfungerende machine
learning modeller i fremtiden. Featurerne blev udtrukket fra polysomnografiske optagelser.
Et delm˚al med denne afhandling var at undersøge mulige lokale og globale søvnmønstre i
hjerneregioner.
Den første sygdom under søgt i dette projek var idiopatisk Rapid Eye Movement Sleep
Behavioral Disorder (iRBD). Denne sygdome blev undersøgt for at finde potentielle tidlige
biomarkører for Parkinson’s sygdom (PD). Den anden sygdom, der blev undersøgt, var
narkolepsi type 1 (NT1). En overordnet hypotese blev testet med en eksperimenterende
tilgang ved at opfylde tre form˚al for projektet. Hypotesen var, at nye features kan afslører
unikke sammenhængsmønstre inden for specifikke frekvensb˚and og synkronisering af søvn
stadier. Disse features kunne fungere som potentielle biomarkører for tidlig diagnosticering
og differentiering af PD og NT1 [1, 2].
Form˚al 1: Udføre en advanceret kohærensanalyse med henblik p˚a den temporale, spektrale
og rumlige information fra EEG optagelser under søvn [3]. Form˚al 2: Udføre en analyse
af synkronisering af søvn stadier ved hjælp af similarity measures til sammenligning af
hypnodensity features (sandsynligheder for søvnstadier) [4, 1, 5]. Form˚al 3: Implementere
en klassifikations algoritme til at udtrække de værdifulde features fra den advancerede ko-
hærensanalyse og analyse af søvnstadiers synkronisering for at skabe to sygdomsspecifikke
profiler.
Metoderne anvendt til at løse de tre form˚al var, form˚al 1: kohærensanalyse, der sam-
menligner alle elektrode kombinationer ved hjælp af EEG signaler af forskellige længder.
Form˚al 2: En automatisk højfrekvent søvnstadie klassificerings algoritme (U-sleep) blev
implementeret til at beregne hypnodensity features (sandsynligheder for søvnstadier).
Ligheden mellem søvnstadier og overgange mellem søvnstadier i forskellige elektroder
blev undersøgt ved hjælp af korrelation og dynamisk time warping. Form˚al 3: Et boosted
decision tree blev implementeret til at udtrække de værdifulde features fra hver af de to
analyser, der blev udført. Der blev oprettet en sygdomsspecifik profil for NT1 og en anden
profil for iRBD.
NT1 modellen havde en F1-score p˚a 0,86 og en accuracy p˚a 0,80. Fra NT1 modellen s˚as
det, at featurerne fra den advancerede kohærensanalyse var mindre vigtige for modellen
sammenlignet med featurerne udtrukket fra analysen af synkronisering af søvnstadier. REM
søvn og søvnstadie overgange relateret til REM-søvn adskilte sig signifikant mere mellem
de to elektroder C3M2-C4M1 hos NT1-patienter sammenlignet med kontrol personer. De
==PAGE:7==
samme mønstre blev observeret for det v˚agne stadie hos NT1 patienter.
iRBD modellen havde en F1-score p˚a 0,64 og en accuracy p˚a 0,77. Fra iRBD modellen
blev det fundet, at sammenhængen i gamma b˚andet i REM søvn kunne være et globalt
fænomen, da der s˚as aktivitet b˚ade inter-hemisfærisk og intra-hemisfærisk. Aktiviteten
var markant højere hos iRBD patienter sammenlignet med kontrol personer. Analyser af
synkronisering af søvnstadier viste, at N1 aktiviteten med en opløsning p˚a 1 sekund var
mere ens for patienter sammenlignet med kontrol personer i elektroderne F3M2-F4M1.
Denne aktivitet kunne være et lokalt fænomen.

5
Introduction and motivation
Narcolepsy and Parkinson’s disease (PD) are two neurological disorders with sleep distur-
bances [11, 1]. Both diseases are very disabling and have a major impact on the patients’
lives, freedom, and control over their own bodies. To this day, no cure has been found,
only treatment of symptoms in both diseases. PD and narcolepsy remain challenging to
diagnose and treat due to their complex and heterogeneous nature [12, 13].
PD is a neurodegenerative disease and causes degeneration of the dopamine secreting
neurons. In PD early detection and accurate profiling in the prodromal stage1 could lead
to detection of early biomarkers for the disease. This would eventually improve the diag-
nostics and treatment for a better prognosis [2]. However, it is difficult to investigate early
stages in PD patients since the disease has already progressed, but a research group has
found a possible early stage of PD to be idiopathic Rapid Eye Movement sleep Behavioral
Disorder (iRBD). Nearly all iRBD patients had developed PD after a 15 year follow-up [14].
Opposite to PD, narcolepsy has a rapid onset and does not have detectable prodromal
stages like PD [12]. However, detection of biomarkers within narcoleptic patients would
give a better understanding of the disease, which could optimize the treatment. Narcolepsy
is caused by a destruction of hypocretin producing neurons. This is most likely because
of an autoimmune reaction [1]. Narcolepsy causes mixing of sleep stages. For narcolepsy
type 1, cataplexic attacks may occur, causing patients to experience momentary paralysis
in parts of their body. Furthermore, patients may experience hallucinations while being
conscious and awake [12].
PD and Narcolepsy can be studied through electroencephalography (EEG) recorded using
polysomnography (PSG) in sleep studies [2, 1]. The general perception of sleep is a global
brain phenomenon, meaning that the whole brain is always affected by the same sleep
stage. However, this paradigm may fail to describe the full concept of sleep. Findings
in a study suggested that sleep might also be a local phenomenon in the brain. It was
found that the sleep onset was not at the same time in all parts of the brain. It could take
up to 10 minutes between the first and the last part of the brain entering sleep activity.
This was found from intracranial recordings in humans [15]. For both PD, iRBD and
narcolepsy the local perception of sleep is an interesting factor to investigate, to gain a
better understanding of local brain activity during sleep. This thesis is an exploratory
study investigating local sleep and finding possible new meaningful features to add to
already existing machine learning models for narcolepsy [1], PD or iRBD [5].

5.2
Clinical background
5.2.1
Sleep stages
Sleep is defined as a reversible behavioural disengagement and unresponsiveness from the
environment. Two overall stages of sleep have been found: Rapid Eye Movement (REM)
sleep and Non REM (NREM). NREM sleep can be further divided into N1, N2 and N3.
The NREM sleep stages describe the depths of sleep, with N1 being the lightest sleep
stage and N3 as the deepest sleep stage. EEG recordings of NREM sleep will appear
synchronous and contain K-complexes, sleep spindles and high voltage slow waves. During
NREM sleep the brain will be inactive and muscle tonus will be present. Oppositely, REM
sleep has episodic bursts of eye movement, no muscle tonus is present, and the sleep stage
is associated with dreaming and an active brain. The sleep onset in healthy subjects will
enter through NREM sleep and progress to REM sleep after at least 80 minutes of NREM
sleep. Several sleep cycles of approximately 90 minutes per cycle will occur during the
night. The full night should contain 75-80% NREM sleep and 20-25% REM sleep [16].
Sleep is scored following the guidelines from the American Academy of Sleep Medicine
using 30 second epochs. Epochs in this regard is a fixed time interval. They define the
following relevant bands for sleep staging: delta waves (1-3 Hz), theta waves (4-7 Hz),
alpha waves (8-12 Hz), beta waves (13-30 Hz) and gamma waves (30-100 Hz)[17].
Wake: will contain alpha waves (8-13 Hz) over the occipital region recorded with eyes
closed, and eye blink frequency is 0.5-2Hz [17].
N1 sleep: Has an activity range of theta waves (4-7 Hz). Slowing of background frequen-
cies will occur and mixed frequency content will account for 50% of the 30 second epoch
[17]. N1 is the initial stage of sleep. It is a very light sleep associated with a low arousal
threshold. Only a small stimulus is required to wake the patient. A severely disrupted
sleep contains large amounts of N1 sleep. Generally only 2-5% of the night should be in
this sleep stage [16].
N2 sleep: Contains K-complexes, being sharp negative waves followed by an instant
positive spike. The frequency of these waves is 0.5 Hz. One or more K-complexes and
sleep spindles should be present in a 30 second epoch. Sleep spindles are waves with a
frequency of 11-16 Hz with a duration of ≥0.5 seconds [17]. N2 follows N1. It is a deeper
stage of sleep, which should constitute 45-55% of the night. High-voltage slow waves occur
as the sleep stage progresses [16].
Page 3 of 87
==PAGE:24==
Natasja Bonde Andersen
July 10, 2024
N3 sleep: Slow wave (delta) activity with waves in the frequency range 0.5-2 Hz and a
peak-to-peak amplitude ≥75 µV measured over the frontal region [17]. N3 follows N2.
The criteria for N3 is high-voltage slow waves in the EEG accounting for 20-50% of the
time. This sleep stage constitutes for 3-8% of the night [16].
REM sleep: Low amplitude, mixed frequency EEG. Rapid eye movements and saw tooth
waves, 2-6 Hz waves over the central region should be present [17]. REM sleep follows
N3. As the night progresses more REM sleep will occur. In the first sleep cycle only
1-5 minutes REM sleep appears. This sleep stage should constitute 20-25% of the night [16].
5.2.2
Polysomnography
Polysomnography (PSG) is a combination of multiple recording methods to score sleep. It
is used for sleep studies. According to the American Academy of Sleep Medicine, the PSG
recordings contain EEG, electrooculogram (EOG), chin electromyography (EMG), airflow
parameters, effort parameters, oxygen saturation and body position [17].
5.2.3
Electroencephalogram
EEG is a non-invasive method for recording electrical activity of the brain, placing
electrodes on the scalp. The general setup and electrode placement for EEG recordings
are the 10-20 system EEG. The electrode placement of the system identifies several brain
regions (pre-frontal, frontal, temporal, parietal and occipital) on both right and left side
of the scalp [18]. However the American Academy of Sleep Medicine suggests a simplified
version of the system for PSG recordings, suggesting F4M1, C4M1 and O2M1 as necessary
and F3, C3, O1 and M2 as backup electrodes. Several important frequency bands can be
extracted from the EEG recordings [17].
5.2.4
Parkinson’s Disease and Rapid Eye Movement sleep behavioural disorder
Parkinson’s Disease is a degenerative disorder of the basal ganglia function. The symptoms
are a variable combination of tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia and postural changes. It is a
progressive destruction of cells in substantia nigra, destroying the dopamine producing cells.
The onset of the disease is in the late 50’s of age. The cause of the disease is not specifically
known, but associated causes are cerebrosal vascular disease, brain tumors, repeated head
trauma and degenerative neurological disease as the primary cause. Alpha synuclein
Page 4 of 87
==PAGE:25==
Natasja Bonde Andersen
July 10, 2024
accumulates in neurons forming Lewy-bodies and causes neurological degeneration due to
misfolding of the protein. The general believe is that PD arises in the lower brain stem
and spreads to the rest of the brain. It is slowly progressing and has its onset years before
the motor symptoms arise[13].
Two research groups have challenged this perspective and published a study each (’Brain-
First versus Gut-First Parkinson’s Disease: A Hypothesis’, Borghammer, P. et. al. (2019)
[19]) and (’Body-first versus brain-first biological subtyping of Parkinson’s disease’, Bohnen,
N. et al. (2020) [14]) suggesting a common theory about having two phenotypes of PD.
The hypothesis is that two different phenotypes of PD may be present. The aggregation
of the misfolded protein alpha-synuclein may start other places than the central nervous
system (CNS). One of the phenotypes may have the onset in the CNS and the other one
may have the onset in the peripheral nervous system (PNS). Keep in mind that the PNS
theory is considered controversial [19].
According to the article (Borghammer, P. et al. (2019) [19]) the misfolded proteins should
have an onset in the gut and be transported through the vagus nerve to the olfactory
bulb, a structure in the brain connected with the nose. The study included iRBD patients.
iRBD is a parasomnia defined by loss of REM sleep atonia and dream-enacting behaviors,
meaning that the patients move during sleep, when they should not. After 15 years of
follow-up, nearly all iRBD cases had converted to a manifested synucleinopathy e.g. PD.
For the iRBD patients, more than 50% in the study had a normal dopamine innervation
in the nigrostriatal area, at the time of the study. The autonomic damage to the PNS was
more severe for these patients, than the damage to substantia nigra. The study suggested
that iRBD seemed like a general marker for a more progressive phenotype of PD with
worse motor and non-motor symptoms [19].
In the second study (’Body-first versus brain-first biological subtyping of Parkinson’s
disease’, Bohnen, N. et al. (2020) [14]), they had setup a multimodal imaging case-control
study. The hypothesis was the same as the other article, but this time more elaborated
on the PNS first phenotype, being associated with iRBD. The explanation was, that the
pathology could arise in the peripheral body and ascend via the vagus nerve to the brain.
This new phenotype would reach the pons before the substantia nigra causing dementia
before motor symptoms. The CNS first phenotype would reach the substantia nigra first,
which causes motor symptoms before dementia [14].

5.2.5
Narcolepsy
Narcolepsy is one of the most common neurological causes of chronic sleepiness. With this
said it is a rare disease and most likely an autoimmune disease. It has not been directly
verified. In general, it is caused by the loss of hypocretin producing neurons in the lateral
hypothalamus. The lower concentration of hypocretin can be measured in the brain and
cerebrospinal fluid. The hypocretin neurons regulate the sleep-wake behavior. Hypocretin
stabilizes wakefulness and is secreted more during the day [20].
Two types of narcolepsy exists, type 1 (NT1) and type 2 (NT2). NT1 is much more
understood regarding pathophysiology compared to the NT2. This project focuses on
NT1. The symptoms of NT1 are excessive daytime sleepiness. The patients wake up
well-rested, but after a couple of hours they feel excessive sleepiness. The patients have
abnormal patterns of REM sleep since it can occur at any time of the day, where healthy
individuals only experiences REM sleep during the night. The NT1 patients can experience
hallucinations (hypnagogic and hypnopompic) and they have cataplexic attacks. Cataplexy
is sudden muscle paralysis starting in the face and neck muscles. It can affect the whole
body and is often triggered by positive emotions. The patients are fully conscious during
the muscle paralysis. This symptom is only occurring in NT1. Researchers have found a
gene which may increase the risk of getting NT1. Over 90 % of all NT1 patients carry the
HLA class II allele DQB1*06:02 [20].
The anatomy and function of the hypocretin neurons are clusters of neurons in the lateral
hypothalamus producing hypocretin, which is released into the CNS. The hypocretin
neurons innervate several regions promoting arousal and suppress REM sleep. The loss of
the neurons will cause poor regulation of REM sleep and timing of the circadian rhythm.
Under normal circumstances REM sleep would occur after at least 60 minutes of sleep, but
in NT1 patients it may occur a few minutes after sleep onset. Furthermore, REM sleep
can occur during daytime causing the patients to experience dream like hallucinations and
muscle paralysis while being awake. This may be because of dysfunction in the brain stem,
normally regulating paralysis during REM sleep. The abnormal sleep architecture is seen
as behavioral state instability [20].

In this section a review of relevant articles will be presented. Since this thesis included
several neurological diseases, a coherence study, an automatic sleep-stage algorithm and
the search for early biomarkers for PD and NT1, these will be investigated. The main

PD and NT1 are two neurological disorders with sleep disturbances[11, 1]. In general sleep
is considered a global phenomenon. A review study What is sleep exactly? Global
and local modulations of sleep oscillations all around the clock, Andrillon, T.
et. al (2023) [15] suggested that local sleep should be taken into consideration when
investigating sleep patterns. The main motivation was to explore the physiological origins
of local sleep, since it remains unclear. The paper mentions and criticizes the use of only
three electrodes for scalp EEG recordings.
Interestingly the paper finds sings of uni-hemispheric sleep in several animals, while the
other hemisphere showed awake like behavior. Furthermore, findings in humans with
arousal disorders had appearances of wake-like patterns of neural activity in some regions,
like the motor cortex and cerebellum. This could explain the parallel recovery of mobility,
whereas other brain regions still show sleep-like activity at the same time. The paper
mentions that local sleep investigations can shed light on REM parasomnias like RBD
because of the patients high brain activity associated with wakefulness and episodes of
paralysis at the same time. This reveals a mix of brain activity.
Additionally the dynamics of sleep onset seems to have a certain relevance since differences
of sleep onset have been detected. A duration of approximately 10 minutes between first
and last brain region before transitioning into sleep were measured. The conclusion of
the review study was that although, local aspects of sleep mostly have been investigated
with a focus on NREM sleep, there is a growing literature on local aspects of REM sleep.
However, the notion of local REM sleep is challenging since REM sleep is characterized by
both neuronal and peripheral changes e.g. muscle atonia [15].

only a global phenomenon. This will be the inspiration for investigating sleep as a local
phenomenon in this thesis. The focus of this project was to investigate the relation between
EEG recorded from different EEG channels, which has specific anatomical locations on
the scalp. This could reveal new spatial information about sleep patterns taking local
sleep into account.
Since this thesis involved an advanced coherence analysis for detecting early biomarkers in
PD and NT1 several papers were used as inspiration.
A coherence study Neurophysiological biomarkers of Parkinson’s Disease, Shani
Waninger (2020) [2] was performed comparing PD patients with age and sex-matched
controls. The study used coherence as a measure of connectivity in the brain, and con-
cluded that EEG was a sensitive measure of resulting functional changes in the brain. The
article suggested that EEG coherence could be a measure of disease progression. In the
study the patients were awake during the recordings. The findings were changes in the
cortico-cortical coupling along with changes in the cortico thalamic coupling and excessive
beta coherence. The same article mentions that PD has multiple phenotypes and is very
heterogeneous, why it is difficult to detect early stages. The loss of dopaminergic neurons is
due to aggregation of alpha-synuclein. The nigrostriatal circuits (the area of the brain with
the dopaminergic neurons) regulates the thalamo-cortical activity, which can be measured
with EEG. Functional connectivity and synchronization are affected by the nigrostriatal
circuits. Cortical connectivity within and between the cortical regions may reveal network
changes that reflect abnormal activity. Abnormally high beta (8-13 Hz) and gamma (25-40
Hz) frequency synchronization were observed across multiple cortico-cortical regions in
PD. This may be because of impairment in the dopaminergic tone [2]. The main relevance
of this article was the purpose of investigating neurophysiological biomarkers of PD using
EEG and coherence. The findings of excessive beta and gamma coherence are relevant
findings to keep in mind for the possible results.
To extend the coherence analysis, inspiration was found from the paper EEG connec-
tivity across sleep cycles and age, Bouchard, M., et. al. (2020)[3]. The study
aimed to evaluate how EEG connectivity differed during sleep, comparing young and older
individuals. The recordings used for this study were PSG measured overnight using 20
EEG channels. Sleep stages were visually scored according to American Academy of Sleep
Medicine using 30 second epochs.
A comparison across the sleep cycles was performed. They used an imaginary coherence
Page 8 of 87
==PAGE:29==
Natasja Bonde Andersen
July 10, 2024
analysis to estimate the functional connectivity of brain regions, and comparing specific
sleep stages between sleep cycles. An example could be N3 in sleep cycle 1 compared
with N3 in sleep cycle 2. This measure was compared between group 1, being the elder
population, and group 2 being the younger population. The results indicated that age
modified sleep EEG connectivity. The direction and the magnitude of these effects differed
between sleep stages and cycles. The assumption for the investigations was: During sleep,
brain regions functionally disconnect from each other. The breakdown of global long-range
connections is replaced by an intensified local cortico-cortical connectivity. The frequency
bands used for the coherence analysis between electrode pairs were averaged in low delta
(0.3–2 Hz), high delta (2–4 Hz), theta (4–8 Hz), alpha (8–12 Hz), low sigma (12–14 Hz),
and high sigma (14–16 Hz). The results of the study were, that older subjects showed lower
overall connectivity during N2 (high delta, theta, and low sigma frequencies). In N3 and
REM older subjects had higher overall connectivity in the EEG in the lower frequencies.
The results also demonstrated that the brain showed lower overall connectivity during
REM sleep, than during the N3 sleep stage, especially in younger individuals [3]. The
inspiration found from this study was coherence calculations within a sleep stage, but
also observing the pattern of sleep stage coherence across sleep cycles. This would be an
interesting investigation for both RBD and NT1 patients.
The use of coherence as a measure, when investigating NT1 does not seem to be the
straight forward method to use, since it was difficult to find studies of narcolepsy using
this measure. In the paper Coherence analysis of EEG during cataplexy attacks
in narcoleptic adult patients, Erbil, N. et. al. (2018) [21] coherence were used to
investigate the EEG of adult NT1 patients during a cataplexic attack. The methods used
were PSG recordings, and the use of emotional triggers to provoke a cataplexic attack.
Seven attacks were recorded in three patients. It was a rather small sample size, however
the study made some findings. The coupling of slow waves increased in temporal regions
during the attacks. Coherence between the right side of temporal regions and frontal
areas increased in slower waves (<4 Hz). The coherence of other slow waves (4-7 Hz)
was pronounced in the left temporal region, when compared with almost all regions of
the cortex [21]. The relevance of this study, is to confirm that other research groups use
coherence as a meaningful measure to investigate NT1. The investigation of coherence
between all electrodes in this study may reveal new sleep patterns in NT1 patients not
previously known.

Regarding machine learning and RBD an article Detection of REM sleep behaviour

from this paper was to investigate the sleep stage transitions for RBD, and use automatic
sleep stage algorithms, and a classification tree to predict RBD. Furthermore, this article
could possibly benefit from future results in this thesis.

Articles have suggested that local sleep is an unexplored phenomenon compared to global

new sleep patterns.
Regarding coherence studies, it was used in different fields and had been widely used for
RBD, but not in the context of NT1. Furthermore, the sleep stage coherence across sleep
cycles was an interesting take on the study for both RBD and NT1 [15, 3].

6.5
Outlier detection and removal
An extended version of this section can be found in the appendix 10.2.
Outlier detection was performed on the restructured PSG files containing the raw EEG
recordings. The purpose of this outlier detection and removal was to exclude extremely
noisy recordings due to dysfunctional or loose electrodes. The algorithm should detect flat
lined signals or channels with extreme amplitudes for a longer period of time, since these
signals were non-biological. The amplitudes of the EEG channels were highly dataset
dependent due to hardware setup, therefore the outlier detection was done within the
respective datasets. By the use of ’np.median()’ and ’np.std()’ several dataset dependent
and channel dependent thresholds were calculated to evaluate the channel quality on a
30 seconds epoch basis. If the 30 second epochs exceeded the thresholds set, it would
be labelled as ’bad’. For each channel the percentage of bad signal was determined and
added to a ’percentage array’. This was done for all channels in all patients. An exclusion
threshold was calculated based on the percentage array. The threshold was calculated
using equation 1.
Exclusionthreshold = 5% + factor · min(percent array)
(1)
Where 5% was the baseline and then a factor depending on the dataset was chosen, and
lastly the minimum value of the ’percent array’ was used. The exclusion threshold took
patient based information into account.

6.6.1
U-sleep: an automatic sleep staging algorithm
An extended version of this section can be found in the appendix 10.3.
U-sleep is an automatic high frequency sleep staging algorithm designed by the group
(Perslev M., Jennum, P.J., et al. U-Sleep: resilient high-frequency sleep staging. (2021))
[4]. In this thesis the network was applied to the data, to predict sleep stage probabilities
also known as hypnodensity features. No traning of the model was performed.
Page 16 of 87
==PAGE:37==
Natasja Bonde Andersen
July 10, 2024
The algorithm is a pure feed forward convolutional neural network, and it was mapping
EEG to high frequency sleep stage representation. This was followed by sleep staging in a
fixed-time epoch size decided by the user. It assumes 5 different sleep stages: wake, N1,
N2, N3 and REM sleep. The network was trained and evaluated on a large and diverse
dataset consisting of 15660 PSG recordings. The network can label sleep stages at shorter
intervals than 30 seconds and have a prediction rate of ≥1/30 Hz. The network used was
the U-sleep-EEG v2.0. and it took one EEG channel as input. No EOG were used in this
project [4].

encoder blocks, decoder blocks and the segment classifier.
The overall structure of the network consisted of three parts: 1) encoder, 2) decoder, 3)

they were restructured and had outliers removed.

Decoder:

feature [4].

Coherence analysis
The pre-processed EEG signals were used in this analysis. All electrodes present were
paired in all possible combinations using the function ’itertools.combinations()’. An ex-
tended explanation of the same concept was given earlier in section 6.6.3.
The primary calculations in this part of the project were coherence, which was calcu-
lated from various lengths of EEG signals. Coherence is a measure of similarity be-
tween to electrodes taking the frequency content into account. The coherence function
’scipy.signal.coherence’ was used throughout the code and calculated the measure in
equation 10.
Coh(f) =
|SAB(f)|2
SAA(f) · SBB(f)
(10)
Where SAB(f) was the cross spectrum of signal A and B, and SAA and SBB were the
power spectrum of signal A and B. Coherence is sensitive to changes in power and changes
in phase relationship between signal A and B [22]. This measure is used to describe
connectivity in the brain [3].
A function ’coherence base function’ was developed. The input was a time signal. The
Page 25 of 87
==PAGE:46==

function calculated the average value of coherence within each frequency band: delta, theta,
alpha, beta and gamma waves. The 5 frequency bands were calculated by indexing in the
frequency axis and coherence output from the built-in coherence function. The indexes
used for the frequency bands were: delta (1-3 Hz), theta (4-7 Hz), alpha (8-12 Hz), beta
band (13-30 Hz), gamma band (31-64 Hz) [17] . The output of the coherence base function’
function were the 5 average coherence values, one for each frequency band.
Coherence measures calculated in this project:
• Average coherence for the full night: This was calculated by loading the full
length of the recorded signal into the coherence base function’. The output were
average coherence values in each of the frequency bands for the full night. In total 5
features per electrode combination were extracted from this calculation.
• Average coherence for 30 seconds epochs: This was done by looping over
every 30 second intervals in the signals and loading the 30 second epochs into the
coherence base function’. The output of the function was average coherence for all
30 seconds epochs within the 5 frequency bands. In total 5 features per electrode
combination were extracted from this calculation.
• Sleep stage coherence: A hypnogram generated with U-sleep was used to determine
the sleep stages and their indexes. This was done for every 30 seconds epochs. For
every epoch the coherence was calculated using the coherence base function’. The
output of the function were the average coherence for each of the 5 frequency bands
for each sleep stage of the 5 sleep stages. These calculations had an output of 25
features per electrode combination.

6.8.1
Supervised learning, crossvalidation and data split
The boosted decision tree is a supervised learning method. In this case classification were
performed of two classes ’diseased’ or ’control’. In supervised learning, the models tried to
predict y from the input x. This was showed in equation 13.
y = f(x, w) + ϵ
(13)
where w were tuneable parameters and ϵ was noise. The learning consisted of selecting
parameters w based on the x training data. When performing supervised learning, the
dataset was split into training and test set. In this case 5-fold cross validation were used
to split the data. In figure 8 the concept was visualised. Cross validation splitted the data
set into 5 subsets, where the model was trained on several of the subset of the data and
tested on the remaining subset. This process was repeated multiple times with different
datasplit and subsets. Using 5-fold cross validation the training dataset accounted for 80
% of the data and the test set for 20 %. Cross validation was used to prevent overfitting.
The use of 5-fold cross validation resulted in a total of 5 models. The performance of the
models was calculated based on an average of the 5 models, by comparing Y test to the
predicted values in Y predicted. A thing to remember: A model can never be tested on a
data split used for training it [29].

6.8.2
Decision trees - a classifier
The boosted decision tree was build from several classification trees called decision trees.
A boosted decision tree was build from training several weak decision trees and combining
them. The trees were trained one at a time, and passing on the knowledge to the next
tree. The boosting also involved gradient based learning, which is a way of optimizing the
learning [30]. The concept of gradient boosting was elaborated in section 6.8.3.
The general concept of training one classification trees was based on a tree like structure,
where all the features were placed at the roots of the tree. During the training several
questions were asked to classify the data. Each question formed a branch. At some point
the leafs of the tree were formed containing groups of features. This was the end of the
model training. If only one type of label either ’diseased’ or ’control’ were present in the
one leafs, it was considered ’pure’. The impurity measures were calculated by counting the
amount of misclassified features in each leaf and putting them into an impurity function
I(vk), where v was the relative size of the groups in the leafs and k was the branch number.
Several impurity functions exists, some examples could be Entropy(v), Ginni(v) and
ClassError(v). From this the purity gain ∆can be calculated, where the impurity function
was compared with the purity in the roots before training the model [29]. The purity gain
was used to evaluate the models, but also to evaluate if another branch was necessary in
the model. If the purity gain dropped by adding a new branch it decreased the model
performance. This concept of evaluating the model performance when adding or removing
specific branches is called ’pruning’. It can also be performed when the full tree have
been trained, and then it can be cropped afterwards. This was a way of controlling the
complexity of the model and avoid overfitting [29].
6.8.3
Gradient boosting and decision trees
Gradient based learning was used to boost the learning of the decision tree. The term
boosting was defined as a group of weak learners combined to become one strong learner.
Boosting was an additive model and an ensemble technique. The concept of training the
boosting algorithm was explained in this section [31].
Five steps were presented and formulas were added when moving forward. The gradient
boosted decision tree could have different loss functions. For the upcoming example the
loss function used was L = 1

prediction0 = R0

2) Compute the residual, which was the difference between predicted value and all the
observed values in the target variable Y train for the first classification tree F0(xi) with
one node [31]. For trees more advanced than the initial F0(xi) the predicted values would
be a sum of residuals from previous trees. The observed value would remain the same

3) Create a new classification tree F1(xi) to predict the residuals based on the residuals of
F0(xi), where ’i’ was the number of data points. The new tree was built like any other
classification tree were each node could be selected with an impurity function as described

6.8.4
Implementation of Boosted Decision Tree
The boosted decision tree was implemented using the function ’XGBClassifier()’. The
function used supervised learning and tried to optimize an objective function consisting of
a loss function and a regularization term. The model ’XGBClassifier()’ was chosen for
this thesis because it could handle missing values in the data. This was essential for the
project, since a different number of electrodes were present in the datasets and it caused
missing values. The settings used for the function ’XGBClassifier()’ were n estimators=2,
max depth=2, learning rate=0.5, objective=’binary:logistic’. The n estimators were the
amount of boosted decision trees, and it was equivalent to the number of boosting rounds.
The max depth of the tree was max depth for the base tree. This measure controlled
the tree complexity. The learning rate was a measure that controlled the step size when
the objective function was minimized using a gradient based method. The objective was
binary and logistic because there were two classes to predict [32, 33]. A description of the
XGBoost classfier documentation can be found in the appendix 10.4.1.

8
Discussion
The main investigation in this thesis was to find new valuable features related to iRBD
and NT1 through analysis of the EEG, an advanced coherence analysis, and by the
use of different similarity measures applied to hypnodensity features in a sleep stage
synchronization analysis. This was done with the purpose of getting a better understanding
of PD, iRBD and NT1.
The main findings for NT1 were: 1) REM sleep and transitions of sleep related to REM
sleep differed more between the two electrods C3M2-C4M1 in NT1 patients compared
to controls. 2) The wake stage and sleep transitions related to the wake stage in the
C3M2-C4M1 electrodes differed more in NT1 patients compared to controls. 3) The
coherence features calculated added little to no value to the model.
The main findings for iRBD were: 1) Coherence in the gamma band in REM sleep was
significantly larger in iRBD patients compared with controls for the electrode combinations
F3M2-F4M1, F3M2-O2M1, C4M1-O1M2, C3M2- O1M2 and O1M2-O2M1. 2) Furthermore,
N1 and the transitions related to N1 sleep were mostly related to epoch size 1 second.
N1 activity had significantly higher similarity in the frontal electrodes in iRBD patients
compared to controls. 3) In general coherence features added more value to the model
compared to the sleep stage synchronization features.
8.1
Discussion of aim 1
The aim tested if new valuable features could be found from an advanced coherence
analysis considering the temporal, spectral and spatial information from an EEG recorded
Page 55 of 87
==PAGE:76==
Natasja Bonde Andersen
July 10, 2024
during sleep.
The spectral information was extracted and investigated using the coherence measure,
since the definition of the term is the correlation of two signals taking the frequency
information into account [22].
The coherence analysis for NT1 did not seem to have a large importance value based on
the results. None of the coherence models seemed to learn anything useful, when compared
to the corresponding baseline models. Some importance values were found. Despite a
quite low value for all of them they will now be discussed. The electrode combination
C3M2-C4M1 and C3M1O2M1 were the most important ones according to the model,
however they were the electrodes present for all patients in the gathered dataset. This
could indicate a bias towards these electrodes when dealing with the coherence features.
The alpha, beta and gamma frequencies added importance to the model. Furthermore,
the sleep stages N3 and REM had importance values. Looking into the information about
the sleep stages and frequency importance from the coherence features, another article
found alterations in NREM and REM sleep for NT1 patients. A study showed that during
REM sleep the power spectra of NT1 patients showed significantly higher power density
for frequency bins from 0.5-1.5 Hz (delta), from 8.5-9.5 (alpha), and from 17.5-25 Hz
(beta). The study suggested that, since an increase in fast frequencies (15.5–34.5 Hz)
has been reported in normal subjects during REM sleep, their results might indicate
a ‘leakage’ of EEG frequencies typical of normal REM sleep into NREM sleep in NT1
patients [37]. These results were calculated using other methods than the ones used in this
thesis, however power spectral density of signals are involved in the coherence measure [22],
making the results comparable. The observations regarding sleep stages and frequency for
the coherence features in this study could be explained by similar mechanisms. Despite
similar results it is important be critical about minor tendencies in the data. The coherence
features for NT1 in this thesis were not significant.
The coherence model for iRBD performed better than the corresponding baseline. From
the feature importance plots, 50 % out of the 10 most important features were coherence
features. The features were related to the coherence in the gamma band during REM
sleep in different electrode combinations. Further analysis showed that the electrode
combinations found in these features were inter-hemispheric (F3M2-F4M1, F3M2-O2M1,
C4M1-O1M2 and O1M2-O2M1) and intra-hemispheric (C3M2-O1M2) activity. All the
interactions above had an effect on the model. The coherence in the gamma band during
REM sleep were all significantly larger for iRBD patients compared to controls. In a

cycle and for iRBD the sleep cycle 4 was the most important one. Overall these measures
did not seem to add much value to the models.
8.2
Discussion of aim 2
Aim 2 tested if new valuable features could be found from a sleep stage synchronization
analysis using similarity measures for comparison of hypnodensity features. When evaluat-
ing the results, the features extracted from the sleep stage synchronization analysis added
value to the investigation of both NT1 and iRBD. The hypnodensity measure have been
used for NT1 before [1]. For iRBD a study suggested to use a measure including sleep
staging [5], which was acted upon in this thesis.
For NT1, 9 out of 10 features of the most important features for the NT1 full model
were from the sleep stage synchronization (SSS) analysis. From the NT1 SSS model, the
performance was much better than the corresponding baseline model. All features were
related to either REM or wake sleep stages. This would make sense when taking the
pathology of the disease into account, where the REM sleep is dysregulated. In a review
study, it was stated that NT1 patients experienced fragmented nocturnal sleep, instability

hypocretinergic tone may lead to this instability of behavioural states [12].
In this analysis the spatial information was taken into account. The most important
electrode combination for these features were C3M2-C4M1 followed by C4M1-O2M1. This
is an important information since these three electrodes C3M2, C4M1 and O2M1 were the

For NT1 the most important epoch size was 15 seconds. The features related to this

and a differentiated hypnodensity. These features described both sleep stages and sleep
stage transitions. The epoch size of 15 seconds, could indicate that transition between
wake and other sleep stages had a duration of a couple of seconds, because it may have
been recorded in the 15 seconds epoch. This suggestion was based on the review study

8.3
Discussion of aim 3
Aim 3 tested if a classifier could extract the valuable features from the advanced coherence
analysis and the sleep stage synchronization analysis, to make two disease specific profiles.

it should be able to handle NaN values. This was the case for the boosted decision tree

observations were made.
In general the NT1 coherence models performed close to their corresponding baseline
models, which had AUC values way above 0.5. This was regardless of the dataset used.
Having a closer look into the features and presence of electrodes for the NT1 datasets, an
explanation of the model performance was found. For the Baseline NT1 full model the
10 most important features were extracted to investigate which features, the increased
learning was based upon. The increased performance was based on several coherence
features related to the the sleep cycles 1 and 4, the electrodes C3M2-C4M1, the frequencies
theta, delta, and the sleep stages N3 and REM.
The combination of features related to REM in sleep cycle 1 and N3 in sleep cycle 4 can
be explained. In some patients REM sleep did not appear in the first sleep cycle, and

8.4
Limitations and future perspective
The limitations of this study for the NT1 models were the NaN values caused by the missing
electrodes. This added a bias to the model. It was a factor that could have been controlled
by a more complete electrode setup for the datasets used. For future work a full electrode
setup should be used to prevent biases from the study setup. The individual effects of fac-
tors causing missing values cannot be separated by the models in this project. This made it
difficult to evaluate and interpret if the sleep patterns were local or global for NT1 patients.
Regarding the coherence calculations the ECG were not removed from the signals. This
was a limitation since it could have an effect on the calculations. The ECG signal has a
greater amplitude than the EEG signal, and it also causes a steady rhythm, which can
be seen in the raw EEG [40]. For future projects this artefact should be removed before
performing coherence.
In future projects several additions could be made to this project. First of all the most
important features extracted from this project could be used in large already well func-
tioning machine learning models like the ones in the articles Neural network analysis

Conclusion
The main investigation of this thesis relied on one overall hypothesis and three aims. The
aims have now been tested in an exploratory study. The hypothesis was that features
extracted from an advanced coherence analysis and a sleep stage synchronization analysis
using similarity measure comparing hypnodensity features, could serve as potential new
biomarkers for NT1 and iRBD (a possible early stage of PD [14, 19]). The two analysis
should extract the information from EEG recorded during sleep. This would eventually
improve the understanding of the two diseases.
The new extracted features could be used in larger machine learning models already
developed to optimize their performance.
The three aims were related to the extraction of new features from aim 1: an advanced
coherence analysis taking the temporal, spatial and spectral aspects into account. Aim
2: sleep stage synchronization was investigated using the similarity measures dynamic
time warping and correlation. Aim 3: a boosted decision tree was used to extract valuable
features for two disease specific profiles.
For NT1 it was concluded that the coherence features were less meaningful than the
features from the sleep stage synchronization analysis. The coherence models in this
analysis did not perform very well compared to the baseline models.
Furthermore, the NT1 sleep stage synchronization analysis found that REM sleep and
Page 61 of 87

Natasja Bonde Andersen
July 10, 2024
sleep stage transitions related to REM sleep differed significantly more between the two
electrodes C3M2-C4M1 in NT1 patients compared to controls. The same patterns were
observed for the wake stages in NT1. The overall model performance was good, however
the baseline models had a high performance because of possible biases towards missing
electrodes. This made it difficult to conclude if any of the results were related to local or
global sleep patterns for NT1 patients.
For iRBD the coherence of the gamma band in REM sleep, it seemed like a global phe-
nomenon and the coherence of the REM sleep gamma band differed significantly more
in iRBD patients compared to controls for the electrode combinations: F3M2-F4M1,
C4M1-O1M2, C3M2-O1M2, F3M2-F4M1 and O1M2-O2M1.
The sleep stage synchronization for iRBD showed that N1 activity with epoch size 1, had
a significantly higher similarity for iRBD patients compared to controls in the F3M2-F4M1
electrodes. This activity seemed like a more local phenomenon.
A boosted decision tree was used as a classification model for extraction of the new valuable
features. The model had a general high performance, however in some cases the baseline
models had a rather a high performance as well. This was due to NaN values in the data
caused by missing electrodes and absence of certain sleep stages in specific sleep cycles for
some patients. The model was suited for this thesis. For future work the missing values
should be mitigated by having a more complete electrode setup.
This thesis found some new valuable features and methods for investigating neurological
disorders with sleep disturbances. However, this investigation only seems to scratch the
top of the iceberg. More investigation related to early biomarkers extracted from the EEG
should be conducted for NT1, iRBD and PD.

10.2
Outlier detection and removal
Outlier detection was performed on the restructured PSG files containing the raw EEG
recordings. The purpose of this outlier detection and removal was to exclude extremely
noisy recordings due to dysfunctional electrodes. The full algorithm is shown in figure 28.
Figure 28: The outlier detection algorithm. Several dataset dependent thresholds were
calculated to evaluate if a channel should be excluded.
An example of a dysfunctional recording would be caused by the electrode falling off during
the recordings. Another term to use about the phenomenon is a ’popped’ electrode. This
Page 67 of 87
==PAGE:88==
Natasja Bonde Andersen
July 10, 2024
would lead to non-sense recordings and non-physiological data. The signals would either
have extreme amplitudes or be flat lined. An algorithm was designed to be triggered, when
extreme amplitudes were present or a signal was close to zero for a longer period of time.
The amplitudes of the EEG channels were highly dataset dependent due to hardware
setup, therefore the outlier detection was done within the respective datasets. The outlier
detector was designed by calculating the overall median and standard deviation for all
channels in one dataset. This was done using the ’np.median’ and ’np.std’ functions. The
calculations were performed for both the raw time signals and for the spectrum of the
signals. The spectrum was calculated using the ’scipy.fft.fft’ function. The reason for using
the spectrum of the signals was, that a flat lined signal has a spectrum with white noise
or close to 0 frequencies [41].
After calculating a threshold for the overall median and standard deviation, the algorithm
calculated median and standard deviation for the time signal and spectrum in 30 seconds
epochs. The median and standard deviation from the 30 second epochs were compared
to the overall median and standard deviation for the time signal and spectrum. Based
on the comparison it was decided if the electrode had popped for the 30 second epoch or
not. If the electrode had popped, the epoch was marked as ’bad’. For each channel the
percentage of bad signal was determined. This was saved in a ’percent array’ containing
percentages for all channels in a patient. A threshold was calculated for when to exclude
the full channel from the study. This threshold was determined by equation 26.
Threshold = 5% + factor · min(percent array)
(26)
Where 5 % is the baseline and then a factor depending on the dataset was chosen, and
lastly the minimum value of the ’percent array’ was used. The exclusion threshold includes
more information than just a noisy signal and takes patient based information into account.
This outlier detector is only excluding the extremely noisy recordings. The electrodes
excluded were saved in a CSV file and removed from the dataset.
10.3
U-sleep: an automatic sleep staging algorithm
U-sleep is an automatic high frequency sleep staging algorithm designed by the group in
reference [4]. In this thesis the network was applied to the data, to predict sleep stage
probabilities. No traning of the model was performed. The following is an into depth
description of the network.
The network is a purely feed forward convolutional network, and it is mapping EEG and
Page 68 of 87
==PAGE:89==
Natasja Bonde Andersen
July 10, 2024
EOG to high frequency sleep stage representation. This is followed by sleep staging in a
fixed-time epoch size decided by the user. It assumes 5 different sleep stages: wake, N1,
N2, N3 and REM sleep.
The network is trained and evaluated on a large and diverse dataset consisting of 15660
PSG recordings. The network can label sleep stages at shorter intervals than 30 seconds
and have a prediction rate of ≥1/30 Hz. The architecture was inspired by a U-Net
architecture, which is used for image segmentation. The original network takes one EEG
channel and one EOG channel as input. The network used in this thesis is the U-sleep-EEG
v2.0, taking only one EEG channel as input, meaning that no EOG were used in this
project. The batches used during training of the network were randomly selected, and it
was trained using a hold-out method. The network is invariant to the electrode placement.
When comparing U-sleep performance with human doctors, no significant difference
were found in performance, except for scoring the sleep stage N3. Furthermore, u-sleep
performance matches the performance of other networks trained specifically to one dataset,
when using the same dataset as input [4].

of three major parts 1) encoder, 2) decoder, 3) segment classifier. NN: Neural network.
The figure is color coded according to which layers were used in the encoder blocks, decoder
blocks and the segment classifier.
The overall structure of the network consists of three parts: 1) encoder, 2) decoder, 3)
segment classifier and it can be seen in figure 29. Before loading the signals into the

Decoder:
The decoder up-scaled the compact features from the encoder. The output resolution
of the decoder matched the resolution of the original input signals. The output of the
decoder was a complex representation of sleep stages. The encoder consisted of 12 decoder
blocks. Each block performed nearest-neighbour upsampling with a kernel size of 2. It

(kernelsize 2, stride 1) to the upsampled feature maps and used ELU activation function

normalization of the corresponding encoder block, by using a skip connection. This was
done before maxpooling. Convolution and batch normalization was applied to the stacked
feature maps. The output of the decoder was an intermediate representation of sleep
stages at high frequency of 128 Hz [4].
