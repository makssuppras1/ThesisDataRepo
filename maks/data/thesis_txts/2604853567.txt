degree in Environmental Engineering. The study started in February 17th 2020 and the ﬁnal report

Abstract
Climate warming is projected to increase droughts especially in the mediterranean region due to an
increase in atmospheric evaporative demand (AED) and decrease in precipitation. In this study we
analyzed how ecosystems and the hydrological function of watersheds in Spain responded to changes
in AED over the past 15 years (2002-2017). The assesment was performed on diﬀerent K¨oppen-
Geiger bioclimatic types and on land cover types based on CORINE land cover classiﬁcation. We
have used Remote sensing (RS) products of potential (ETp) and actual evapotranspiration (ET)
and precipitation (P) data for Spain. The RS product of ET was cloud contaminated therefore we
proposed a novel method where ET was gap-ﬁlled using a temporal ecohydrological approach. The
theoretical framework of Budyko was used to determine the responses of ecosystems to higher aridity
levels over a 15 year time span. The Budyko framework relates the evaporative index (ET/P) to the
aridity index (ETp/P) of watersheds. A resilient ecosystem was deﬁned as one that is able to channel
precipitation into more evapotranspiration during periods of increasing AED. Although we were not
fully able to validate the RS ET dataset for all watersheds it showed high agreement for watersheds
of a size of 100-1000 km2 (R2 = 0.67) and K¨oppen-Geiger bioclimatic type Csb. Watersheds showed
diﬀerent responses in ET/P and ETp/P depending on bioclimatic type, where dry climates (Bsk,
Csa and BWh) had a clear increase in ETp/P without similar increase in ET/P. This indicated
that ecosystems was not able to keep up with the increasing water demand which potentially can
lead to further water stress and temperature increases, amplifying the risk of droughts. Analysis on
land cover types did not show the same clear tendency suggesting that bioclimatic type is a stronger
determinating factor for how ecosystems respond to increasing AED though the two classiﬁcation
systems are closely related. The trends in ETp/P and ETp/P for watersheds with wetter climates
(Cfb and Csb) showed either relatively stable conditions or increases of comparable magnitude in
ET/P and ETp/P, indicating a higher responsivity to increasing AED in these regions. Only trends
in ETp could be classiﬁed as statistically signiﬁcant with a Mann-Kendall trend test (α = 0.5). The
lack of statistical signiﬁcance could be attributed to a small sampling size (15 years). The ﬁndings
of this study corresponded with other studies that also found increasing aridity and decreasing
precipitation in Spain.

Introduction
Drought represents an ever-present threat to life depending on freshwater resources. Consequences
of drought spans from environmental, agricultural and socioeconomic impacts [Loon et al., 2016].
In contrast to other weather disasters such as hurricanes and storms, there is no precise deﬁnition of
drought making it hard to deﬁne in space and time [Noguera et al., 2020], [Wilhite and Glantz, 1985].
Climate change in Spain have been conﬁrmed by numerous studies, and is reﬂected in an increase
in climate aridity, attributed to higher air temperatures, decreased relative humidity and increased
Atmospheric Evaporative Demand (AED) [Vicente-Serrano et al., 2020a]. AED is the demand for
water from the atmosphere and depends on surface net radiation as well as climatic conditions such
as water vapour pressure and temperature [Vicente-Serrano et al., 2019]. Spain has experienced an
increase in climatic extremes such as droughts over the last century [Garc´ıa-Haro et al., 2014]. At
the same time studies based on satellite imagery have found a general increase in vegetation activ-
ity across Spain over the last 35 years [Vicente-Serrano et al., 2020a], causing a higher evaporative
demand from vegetation. Much research have gone into investigating droughts and climate change
in Spain [Noguera et al., 2020], [Mendiguren Gonz´alez et al., 2019] [Paneque, 2015]. However it is
not known how the increase in evaporative demand due to increased greening, AED and aridity, will
aﬀect ecosystems and the hydrological function of watersheds in Spain.
Droughts have been a recurring problem in Spain, with most recent periods of droughts taking
place from 1942–1945, 1980–1983, 1991–1995, 2005–2008, 2012 with the latest drought in 2017
causing crop losses, severe damages to ecosystems and wildﬁres [Albiac et al., 2013], [Vicente, 2017].
A precise deﬁnition of drought is widely debated as droughts can not be deﬁned and dated exactly
opposite of other weather phenomenons as huricanes or ﬂoods. Drought is the absence of water and
evolves slowly, becoming more severe as the days without water increases [Wilhite and Glantz, 1985].
Droughts are a normal climate phenomenon but the ocurrence and extremety of droughts are being
attributed to climate change [Albiac et al., 2013]. They are not exclusive to certain areas, but can
happen in both high and low rainfall regions, which makes it a global problem. Droughts are a
temporary lack of water and are often deﬁned according to the deviation from the normal amount
of rainfall an area receives. This is critisized for providing a very narrow view on droughts as they
can not only be caused by changes in rainfall patterns [Loon et al., 2016]. The causes of droughts
are often distinguished into four diﬀerent categories [Wilhite and Glantz, 1985], [Paneque, 2015]:
Meterological drought is the decrease in rainfall compared to what is ”normal” for a certain
1
==PAGE:13==
area and season. Agricultural drought is linked to plant’s deﬁency of water, where the lack of soil
humidity can be caused by a decrease in rainfall as well as an increase in evapotranspiration. Whether
it is experienced as drought is dependent on the growth stage of the crop. Hydrological drought
is often one of the consequences of a decrease in prepcipitation, where hydrological drought is seen
as the decrease of water in streams and ﬂoods as well as in subsurface hydrology. Socioeconomic
drought is the human inferred drought, where the demand for water by humans exceeds the supply
of water. This can be the demand for water for drinking as well as water for producing economic
goods. In this case droughts can be initiated because of landuse practices or an already existing
drought situation can be worsened or shifted from one drought type to another.
Understanding drought dynamics and their drivers are central to identify implications for ecosys-
tems and the hydrological function [Vicente-Serrano et al., 2019], [Loon et al., 2016].
Droughts
usually develops over longer periods of time and can take months to reach maximum intensity
[Noguera et al., 2020]. Long term studies of drought trends for environmental systems are limited
[Vicente-Serrano et al., 2020b]. Long term studies are therefore needed to ﬁll the knowledge gap on
the consequences of droughts in relation to ecosystems.
Abscence of precipitation is the main component of droughts [Vicente-Serrano et al., 2019].
Therefore drought indicators using meteorological data such as the Standardized Precipitation In-
dex (SPI) [McKee et al., 1993] have been developed, based on standardization of the anomali in
precipitation.
Later in order to account for AED in drought assessment the The Standardised
Precipitation-Evapotranspiration Index (SPEI) was developed where AED and temperature data
were included in the assessment [Vicente-Serrano et al., 2010], [Vicente-Serrano et al., ]. Both SPI
and SPEI can be used to monitor drought in real time, evaluate the intensity and determine the onset
and ending of a drought event [Vicente-Serrano et al., ]. However, those indices are related to mete-
orological conditions and does not take into account how the land surface is responding to droughts.
In order to evaluate the long term interactions between climate, water and vegetation in the sub-
surface the Budyko framework is used [Creed et al., 2014]. The Budyko framework describes the
relationship between precipitation and long term average water and energy balances at watershed
scales [Sposito, 2017]. The Budyko framework relates a watershed’s potential evapotranspiration
(ETp) to the actual evapotranspiration (ET) normalized to precipitation (P). ET is the combined
term for evaporation of water from soil and open water surfaces and water transpired from plants
[Bonan, 2008] and ETp represents the theoretical amount of water that would evaporate if unlimited
water was available [Priestley and Taylor, 1972]. The two terms represents the supply (ET) and the
2
==PAGE:14==
demand (ETp) for water in watersheds. ET is a key component when assessing the causes and risks of
droughts [Jiang et al., 2019]. Approximately two thirds of precipitation falling on land stems from
evapotranspiration making it a vital factor in the hydrologic cycle [Vicente-Serrano et al., 2014].
Holling (1973) deﬁned a resilient ecosystem as one that is able to absorb change while maintain-
ing ecosystem function [Holling, 1973]. Creed et al. (2014) used the Budyko framework to deﬁne
and evaluate hydrologic resilience in watersheds where a hydrologic resilient system is one that is
able to maintain hydrologic function under climate change [Creed et al., 2014]. This means that
watersheds during drying conditions are able to channel the excess energy from climate warm-
ing into more evapotranspiration on the expence of a decrease in water yield [Creed et al., 2014],
[Vicente-Serrano et al., 2019]. A similar way of understanding resilience have been applied in studies
on ecosystems resilience [Velde et al., 2013], [Garc´ıa et al., 2008]. In a humid ecosystem the increase
in energy from climate change can result in more precipitation channeled into evpaotranspiration
increasing photosynthesis and plant productivity [Vicente-Serrano et al., 2019]. Opposite in an en-
ergy limited ecosystem where water is scarce the increase in energy can lead to decrease in plant
photosynthesis [Kimm et al., 2020] and increase in temperatures as energy is transformed into heat
[Bonan, 2008].
Spain is a hot spot for climate change [Vicente-Serrano et al., 2020a] and is a climatologically di-
verse country in Europe with both energy and water limited regions [Kottek et al., 2006]. Countries
in the mediterranean region are at risk of severe droughts due to an increase in AED and decrease in
precipitation [Vicente-Serrano et al., 2004]. Increases in AED will always translate into increases in
aridity but does not necessarily result in a similar increase in droughts [Vicente-Serrano et al., 2019].
Therefore we want to investigate if ecosystems in Spain will be able to keep up with higher AED,
e.g. to increase the actual evapotranspiration proportionally to the increase in evaporative demand.
Such an analysis requires high resolution (1 km) estimates of ET. ET accounts for 70 % of
precipitation on land and is of major importance for the watercycle and terrestrial ecosystem health
[Ajami, 2020].
However, accurate estimates of ET are diﬃcult to obtain.
Field measurements
from ﬂux towers remains the most accurate method but have low spatial variability and are only
implemented in limited areas [Karimi and Bastiaanssen, 2015], [Zhu et al., 2020]. Remote Sensing
(RS) provides an eﬃcient alternative to ground based measurements of ET, allowing large ar-
eas of the surface of the earth to be mapped [Kupfer and Emerson, 2005]. Data from RS covers
3
==PAGE:15==
more than 10 years of earth observations but the data is often incomplete because of cloud cover
[Kandasamy et al., 2013]. A range of methods to obtain complete datasets for long term RS data
have therefore been developed. These approaches can overall be divided into three diﬀerent cate-
gories [Weiss et al., 2014]. One method is gap-ﬁlling based on spatial variabels, including kriging
where surrounding data is used to interpolate missing values [Addink, 1999], another method is
gap-ﬁlling based on temporal variables, where data from previous timesteps are used, such as a
climatological yearly proﬁle [Kandasamy et al., 2013]. Third are methods combining both spatial
and temporal variables, where gap-ﬁllling is either performed using the nearest pixel values or with
previous days if no pixel values are available close by [Kang et al., 2005], [Weiss et al., 2014]. More
complex methods to gap-ﬁll ET data have focused on using machine learning methods that takes
into account both the spatial and temporal dimensions [Ji et al., 2018]. ML is very computationally
demanding and mostly robust for small homogeneous areas [Nikolaou, 2020] as a consequence they
can be diﬃcult to implement on a larger scale with only limited data.
In our assessment we used RS products of evapotranspiration retrieved from the Moderate Reso-
lution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS). The data is highly contaminated with clouds. Previous
approaches to gap-ﬁll RS ET for Spain have used a climatological yearly proﬁle, but this is likely to
overestimate ET in cloudy days as it uses the average of ET in sunny days to gap-ﬁll cloudy days
[Mendiguren Gonz´alez et al., 2019]. Therefore we suggest a temporal ecohydrological approach to
gap-ﬁll cloudy days in RS data where ET in clouy days is related to ET in sunny days assuming
soil moisture is constant between sunny and cloudy days. Based on this dataset we aim to assess
how ecosystems in Spain have responded to changes in the evaporative demand during the past 15
years (2002-2017).

5.1
Study area: Spain
Spain is a part of the Iberian Peninsula sharing the space with Portugal. The country comprises
an area of 505.990 km2 and have grown from 38.8 million in 1990 to 49 mio people increasing the
pressure on water resources [The World Bank (A), 2019]. Climate scenarios depicts a temperature
increase of 1.9 C and a reduction in rainfall of 6 % adding further stress to already waterstressed areas
[Garc´ıa-Vali˜nas, 2018]. The water distribution among sectors in Spain is approximately 68 % for irri-
gation, 13 % for urban uses, 14 % for refrigeration and 5 % for industrial purposes [T. Estrela, 2012].
In ﬁgure 2 in the dataset description displays the diﬀerent land cover types according to CORINE
land cover classiﬁcation of Spain. Statistics from the World Bank suggest that 50 % of the land is
used for agriculture where 14 % of agriculture land is irrigated [The World Bank (B), 2019].
Spain is known for having a variety of climates and landsacpes, as well as high diﬀerences in
temperature and rainfall across the country.
Spain can overall be distinguished into 5 diﬀerent
Koppen-Geiger bioclimatic types (see ﬁgure 1 in the dataset description) [T. Estrela, 2012].
• Hot-summer Mediterranean Climate (Csa): This climate type is found in the south-west
part of Spain and is characterized by dry and warm/hot summers and cool and wet winters.
• Warm-summer Mediterranean Climate (Csb): This climate type is mainly present in the
north-west part of Spain and is characterised by less warm summers compared to Csa, and
limited rain in the driest month in summer.
• Oceanic Climate (Cfb): This climate type is dominant in the north part of Spain. These
regions are cooler and have a more regular precipitation pattern through out the year.
• Semi Arid(Bsk): This climate type is dominant in the southern and middle parts of Spain.
These areas are characterized by a less dense vegetation and a dry season that stretches into
autumn.
6

• Warm-summer continental Climate(Dfb): This is the least dominant type of the ﬁve
mentioned and is mostly found in north-east part of Spain.
It is characterised by a more
humid climate and with no signiﬁcant changes in precipitation over the course of a year
[Cunha et al., 2011].
As many other countries in the Mediterranean region Spain has an irregular rainfall regime and
a high spatial variability in rainfall across the country, making it especially vulnerable to droughts.
Precipation ranges from 150 - 2500 mm/year [Castro et al., 2005]. Predominantly most rain is found
in the northern region of the country and more dry areas are found inland and along the south-
east coast [Garc´ıa-Moll´a et al., 2019]. This is why Spain is often divided in a dry and a wet Spain,
with a isohyet of 600 mm/year [Garc´ıa-Moll´a et al., 2019]. Extreme variability in precipitation have
previously caused problems in Spain. In the beginning of 1990’s Spain experienced a long drought
that caused severe problems for agriculuture and forests [Luis et al., 2011]. Later in the end of the
1990’s this situation was the opposite and heavy rains led to ﬂoods, causing human and ﬁnancial
losses. Much research have therefore been focused on predicting and understanding water resources
in Spain to inform water policy decisions and mitigate extreme rainfall events as well as droughts
[Luis et al., 2011].
5.2
Spatial Datasets
In order to investigate the spatial and temporal trends in ET and how it is related to precipitation
and ETp a range of datasets have been used. In this study three diﬀerent dataset for evapotran-
spiration were used: Potential Evapotranspiration (ETp), Actual Evapotranspiration (ET) and
Reference Evapotranspiration (ETo).
Potential evapotranspiration
ETp data was modelled from remote sensing observations from the Moderate Resolution Imaging
Spetroradiometer (MODIS) on board the NASA satelite Terra. MODIS observes a wide spectral
range of electromagnetic energy at diﬀerent time steps every day [Lindsey and Herring, 2000], which
was used as input in the Priestley-Taylor equation to obtain ETp. The Priestley-Taylor requires
inputs of net radiation, soil heat ﬂux, slope of the saturation vapor pressure-temperature curve, the
psychrometric constant γ, which relates the partial pressure of moist air to the temperature and a
empirically derived constant α, set to 1.26, which was empirically derived by Priestley and Taylor,
7
==PAGE:19==
for calculation of ETp [Lhomme, 1997], [Priestley and Taylor, 1972]. The dataset of ETp was was
provided by [Mendiguren Gonz´alez et al., 2019] and is given in W/m2 and has a resolution of 1km
x 1km pr. pixel and a temporal resolution of one daily time step. Dimensions are 5660 days x 834
pixels (latitude) x 1115 pixels (longitude). Measurement period covers 15.5 years from 2002-07-04
to 2017-12-31.
Actual evapotranspiration
The ET dataset was obtained from [Mendiguren Gonz´alez et al., 2019]. In order to model ET a top-
down approach were used, where ETp from the Priestley-Taylor equation were down regulated by
environmental constraints and meteorological observations to obtain ET. This was done using the
model developed by Fisher et al. [Fisher et al., 2008]. The model downregulates ETp using inputs of
net radiation (Rn), normalized diﬀerence vegetation index (NDV I), soil adjusted vegetation index
(SAV I), maximum air temperature (Tmax), and water vapor pressure (ea) [Fisher et al., 2008]. The
dataset of ET is in W/m2 and has the same spatial and temporal resolution as the dataset for ETp.
Reference Evapotranspiration
Reference Evapotranspiration (ETo) is the evapotranspiration from a reference crop, which is deﬁned
by FAO as a uniform grass with ”crop height of 0.12 m, a ﬁxed surface resistance of 70 s m-
1 and an albedo of 0.23” [FAO (a), ].
Data for ETo was obtained from Tomas-Burguera et al.
(2018), who used the Penman-Monteith equation with inputs from meteorological data of maximum
and minimum temperature, relative humidity, wind speed and sunshine duration to model ETo

same extent and resolution as the ETp and ET datasets.

in the synthetic year. This dataset was biassed towards sunny days, causing the issue that cloudy
days could achieve estimates of ET above sunny days when gap-ﬁlling. This datset was compared
with the gap-ﬁlling approach applied in this study. The extent and dimensions of the dataset is the

6.2
Validation of gap-ﬁlled evapotranspiration dataset
The gap-ﬁlling of the ET dataset was evaluated in order to determine if it produced a reliable
dataset that could be used for analysis of ecosystem responses in relation to higher aridity levels.
The evaluation was performed using diﬀerent validation approaches as described below.
Long term annual discharge
Calibration and validation of hydrologic models with streamﬂow observations is a widely used ap-
proach within hydrology and remote sensing [Immerzeel and Droogers, 2008], [Jiang et al., 2020]. In
this study we applied a water balance approach to validate RS ET against observed discharge from
streams and rivers. In watersheds excess precipitation (P) that is not evaporated or transpired is
expected to be found as discharge (Qobs) into rivers and streams in long term. Assuming that there
is no change in storage in the system, this can be described by:
Qobs = P −ET
(4)
Over long time scales the water balance is assumed to be in steady state, where precipitation
that is not evaporated or transpired will be equal to Qobs, eg. there is no storage of water in the
soil or ground. However on a yearly basis the water balance is transient and inﬂow will therefore
not necessarily be equal to outﬂow. In this study we validated the gap-ﬁlling of ET by calculating
a predicted discharge (Qpred) for each watershed over the years 2003-2013. Qpred was compared to
stream gauge measurements of Qobs for each watershed. Faults in the estimates of ET and imprecise
measurements of P can be a reason for imbalance between Qpred and Qobs. On the contrary ET
can be estimated correctly but reservoirs, where water is stored can be cause for false imbalance
between Qpred and Qobs. The amount of P and ET for each watershed was converted from mm/day
into a yearly average in hm3/year using the area of the watersheds. The validation for the long

term water balance was evaluated for the 6 most frequent land cover types and 5 most frequent
bioclimatic types.
Yearly data for Qpred and Qobs were calculated in hydrologic years. A hydrologic year is the sum
of all precipitation during one year. When calculating in hydrologic years the storage of precipitation
in snow and ice is included in the yearly sum of precipitation [USGS, 2016].

6.3
Temporal trends in annual and seasonal ET and ETp
Temporal trends in ET, ETp, precipitation, ETp/P and ET/P were assesed with Ordinary Least
Squares linear regression, where the coeﬃcients β1 and β2 are chosen so to minimize the Residual
sum of squares. The relationship is described by:
ˆyi = β1 + β2 ∗xi
(8)
19
==PAGE:31==
where Yi is the estimate of the climatic variable, Xi is time and β1 is the intercept and β2 is the
slope [Dougherty, 2016].
In order to detect whether the trends could be considered signiﬁcant the non-parametric trend
test Mann Kendall was used. This test does not require that the data is normal distributed and can
handle outliers in data [Hamed and Rao, 1998]. It is often applied in hydrology and climatology.
The null hypothesis of the test is that there is no monotonic trend in the data and the alternative
hypothesis is that there is a monotonic upwards or downwards trend in the data. The Mann-Kendall
trend test computes the sum of positive and negative values between every value (xk) and all the
preceding values (xj) in a time series [Gilbert, 1997]. This can be used to detect a posibble trend in
the data and a p-value. The p-value is the probability of obtaining test results at least as extreme as
the ones observed given the the null hypothesis is true [Helsel and Frans, 2006]. For the signiﬁcance
test in this study a signiﬁcance level of α = 0.05 was used. A p-value below 0.05 means that there
is less than 5 % probability of rejecting the null hypothesis if it was in fact true. The temporal
trends over the measurement period was calculated for ET, ETp, P, ETp/P and ET/P. These
were interpreted in relation to the most prevalent land cover and the climates types.
6.4
Ecohydrological assessment of temporal trends: Budyko Framework
The Budyko framework has often been used to examine the responses between climate, vegetation
and hydrological systems in watersheds [Gentine et al., 2012], [Creed et al., 2014], [Wang and Hejazi, 2011].
In this study we applied the Budyko framework to assess trends in evaporative index (ET/P) and
aridity (ETp/P). The Budyko framwework is an eﬀective tool to analyze the long term energy and
water balances in watersheds. The framework describes the relation between the evaporative index
(ET/P) and the dryness index (ETp/P) for watersheds [Sposito, 2017]. Budyko stated that there
exist a functional relationship between the climatic variables; precipitation (P), ET and ETp such
that [Sposito, 2017]:
ET = f(P, ETp)
(9)
The Budyko framework is used to quantify the water-energy balances of watersheds either as
energy limiting conditions or water limiting conditions, described by the limitations:
ET →ETp as P ↑∞
(10)
ET →P as ETp ↑∞
(11)

manifestating in a low sensible heat ﬂux as energy is going into evapotranspiration [Sposito, 2017].
The scenario in equation 11 represents conditions where water is limiting and energy is in excess.
ET is then limited by the amount of available water from P. The Budyko diagram can graphically
be illustrated as seen in ﬁgure 6. Watersheds can be plottet on the Budyko diagram based on their

The gap-ﬁlling of ET was deﬁned to follow a decision tree approach either choosing values from
the closest sunny day (ETsunny) or the same cloudy day in the dataset of ETp (ETpcloudy). In
practice very few days of ETp was chosen to gap-ﬁll ETcloudy.
The gap-ﬁlled ET dataset has
therefore mainly been gap-ﬁlled with previous or future values of the ET dataset. If values from the
ETp dataset is not used then this method of gap-ﬁlling resembles the data imputation method Last
Observation Carried Forward (LOCF) [Liu, 2016]. A method where the last observed value is used
to impute. This way of imputating a dataset have the beneﬁt that it is simple, but the drawdown is
that the dataset can become very artiﬁcial as the same values are used repeatedly. In the gap-ﬁlling
of ET this was especially evident in areas where there was consecutive days of missing data, such
as what was seen in the pixel from Asturias, where 52 % of the data was missing. This resulted in
long periods where ET plateaued, which is not representative of reality in evapotranspiration. The
advantage of this gap-ﬁlling method is that it is fairly easy to run on large areas and can prove to
be a reliable gap-ﬁlling approach if shorter periods of data are missing, such as in the Donana pixel.
The method for gap-ﬁlling ETp showed less artiﬁcial than ET where only in rare cases was
previous data used directly to gap-ﬁll. The method to gap-ﬁll ETp resembled the method developed
by FAO, to calculate crop ETc [G. Allen et al., 1998b] . Using this method for imputation makes
the imputation more valid as the proportionality between ETo and evapotranspiration is already
established. The ETp dataset was more ”natural” in that sense that the ﬂuctuations followed the
ﬂuctuations in ETo when data was missing opposite of the imputation of ET that became very
static in cloudy days. Since the two datasets are gap-ﬁlled with two diﬀerent gap-ﬁlling methods
it meant that the two datasets does not have the same bias. This was cause fot uncertaintis when
trends in the two datasets were compared.

Conclusion
In this study the aim was to evaluate a high resolution remote sensing product of evapotranspiration
at annual and seasonal time scales in order to identify and interpret drying and wetting trends in the
land surface. The analysis was done at a pixel and a watershed level corresponding to diﬀerent land
cover and bioclimatic types. Long term trends in actual evapotranspiration (ET), potential evap-
otranspiration (ETp), precipitation and in evaporative index (ET/P) and aridity index (ETp/P)
were assessed on a temporal and spatial scale to understand whether ecosysemts could keep up with
increasing atmospheric evaporative demand (AED).
Analysis of temporal trends in evaporative index (ET/P) and aridity index (ETp/P) showed
strongest increases in aridity, and less increases in evaporative index. This was mainly attributed
to an increase in ETp and a decline in precipitation and unchanged ET. The increase in evapora-
tive index was therefore mainly attributed to a decline in precipitation and not an increase in ET.
This was in line with climate projections for the Mediterranean Basin where an increase in AED
and decrease in precipitation is predicted in future climate projections. This was further conﬁrmed
on a watershed level, where the strongest long term increase for watersheds was seen in aridity
and lower in evaporative index. In drier climates (Bsk, Csa and BWh) this was especially evident
where all watersheds had increasing trends in aridity index, that was not followed by an increase
in evaporative index of similar magnitude. The watersheds were therefore suﬀering from aridiﬁ-
cation where vegetation transpiration was not able to keep up with the increase in AED and the
increased greening in Spain as no excess water was available. The precipitation deﬁcit most likely
resulted in temperature increases reinforcing a stronger AED, amplifying the risk of droughts and
possible ecosystem collapse. This indicates low hydrologic resilience of watersheds and can have led
to substantial reduction in runoﬀto rivers and streams having consequences for hydrologic systems
dependent on the water resources. Analysis of the hydrologic resilience of the watersheds is highly
relevant but is beyond the scope of this study.
Watersheds with humid/wet climates (Cfb and Csb) did not experience trends in a certain di-
rection opposite of the watersheds in dry climates. Wet watersheds in the north experienced the
strongest increases in aridity with increases in evaporative index. However these areas are in general
energy-limited, why it resulted in an increase in vegetation productivity and a possible reduction in
discharge to rivers and streams. The connection betweeen AED in humid regions is though much
81
==PAGE:93==
more complex compared to water-limited areas, where there already exist a large imbalance between
precipitation and AED.
An increase in greening over Spain was found in previous studies which further ampliﬁes land
evaporative demand. The conditions in the south of Spain was mostly water-limited (ETp/P > 1).
In these areas it is not likely that vegetation can keep up with the increasing water demand leading
again to further water stress unless water from ground and surface water are used for irrigation. This
can have implications for hydrologic systems that are already under water stress because of an exten-
sive irrigation scheme in Spain. This could shift drought conditions from agricultural/meteorological
drought and towards hydrological drought with potentially permanent eﬀects on hydrological sys-
tems if the drying trends continues.
Trends for ETp was found to be statistically signiﬁcant where the majority of trends in ET and
precipitation were not statistically signiﬁcant according to a Mann-Kendall trend test. Eventhough
the Mann-Kendall trend test showed that the majority of trends were not statistically signiﬁcant,
the ﬁndings of this study are in line with similar climatological studies and with climate projections
for Spain. In this context the trends are likely to be seen in the future but due to a small sampling
size (15 years) the Mann Kendall trend test did not classiﬁy the trends as statistically signiﬁcant.
A part of this study was to develop and evaluate an ecohdydrological approach to gap-ﬁll cloudy
days in RS ET data using ET and ETp in the closest sunny days and under an assumption that soil
moisture was constant. Validation against long term average observed discharge showed the best
ﬁt for ET in watersheds with a temperate, dry summer, warm summer climate (CSb) (R2 = 0.79)
and land cover type ”Moors and heathlands” (R2 = 0.95) and for medium sized watersheds (100-
1000 km2) (R2 = 0.67). Validation against all watersheds was though very inaccurate (R2 = 0.07).
Validation of ET against two eddy covariance measurement sites yielded poor results for one site
with RMSE =0.987 mm/day, compared to an average in situ ﬂux of 0.63 mm/day. This was though
attributed to the modelling of ET and not the gap-ﬁlling as such. Validation against measurements
from the second site showed that RS ET data captured the minimum and maximum values cor-
rectly, with a similar pattern in ET but with an oﬀset betwen modelled and measured ET yielding
a high RMSE of 0.84 mm/day compared to the average in situ ﬂux of 1.37 mm/day. The gap-ﬁlling
methodology did not prove to perform better than a climatological average but yielded very similar
82
==PAGE:94==
results. It is though likely that the gap-ﬁlling will be better if the data contained fewer consecutive
days with clouds as there then will be a closer connection between ET in cloudy and sunny days.
The new methodology is more sound than a climatological average, for daily and monthly time
scales, but validation with long term data, did not show beneﬁts because it was averaged over 15
years, which is the same as using a climatological average.
Drought is a complex phenomenon and needs to be adressed at diﬀerent scales and evaluated
based on multiple factors. This study showed that ecosystems in drier regions in Spain can not keep
up with the evaporative demand and the increase in greening. This will result in rising temperatures
in already arid areas and may cause humid areas to become more arid. Droughts can be caused
by meterological conditions, but depending on how they are handled can shift from meteorological
droughts to hydrological, agricultural and societal. Therefore understanding of hydrological systems
and ecosystems is crucial to avoid worsening a drought situation.
