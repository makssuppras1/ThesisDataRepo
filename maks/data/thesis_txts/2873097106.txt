Abstract 
As aquaculture seeks more sustainable solutions, microalgae like Tetraselmis 
suecica (T. Suecica) offer a promising avenue for carbon capture and nutrient 
recycling. This study evaluates the physiological and biochemical responses 
of T. Suecica cultivated under varying CO₂ conditions; 0.04% (ambient), 5%, 
10%, and industrial flue gas. The study further seeks to correlate cultivation un-
der microscope with cultivation in a 2L photobioreactor systems in hopes of lev-
eraging the former for rapid screening future species of algae. While ambient 
CO₂ produced the highest biomass (8.1%), elevated CO₂ reduced growth, likely 
due to pH-related stress. However, 10% CO₂ led to higher chlorophyll, carote-
noid, and lipid content as well as a higher amount of polyunsaturated fatty acid 
composition which is suggestive of a metabolic shift toward valuable com-
pounds. The correlation between photobioreactor and microscopic experiments 
could not be found and the primary objective of observing a full cell division for 
growth dynamics could not be used.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Research Goal and Hypothesis 
As global ecosystems continue to be disrupted by pollution, habitat loss, and cli-
mate instability, human intervention has become increasingly necessary to re-
store or replicate ecosystem services. One sector that exemplifies both the 
challenge and the opportunity of this moment is aquaculture (the industrial culti-
vation of fish and other aquatic organisms). While aquaculture is often promoted 
as a sustainable alternative to wild fisheries, its environmental impacts, includ-
ing release of nutrient waste, high energy consumption, and carbon emissions, 
demand contemporary solutions. This study positions microalgae, particularly 
the species Tetraselmis suecica (T. suecica), as a biologically potent, ecologi-
cally resilient, and biotechnologically valuable solution to these challenges. 
The primary objective of this study is to evaluate and quantify the growth behav-
ior and biochemical composition of the microalga T. suecica under varying car-
bon dioxide (CO₂) enrichment levels. By applying both microscope based and 
photobioreactor (PBR) based experimental frameworks, the project aims to de-
termine how pure CO₂ and flue gas influence algal growth dynamics, physiologi-
cal responses, and lipid accumulation. The species T. suecica was selected for 
its large cell size, which allows for detailed visual observation under a micro-
scope, and for its relevance as a feed additive in aquaculture. This research 
contributes to the broader vision of integrating microalgae into circular economy 
systems, where they may serve as biological agents for carbon capture and 
conversion to biomass in conjunction with industrial outputs such as aquacul-
ture effluent (Goswami, 2022). 
To achieve this, a dual experimental design was employed. Microscopic obser-
vations were made using time-lapse imaging to assess individual cell growth, 
behavior, and morphology. In tandem, large-scale cultivation trials were con-
ducted using 2-liter photobioreactors (PBRs) under standardized conditions as 
per Patrinou 2023. In both systems, the effects of elevated levels of CO₂ were 
==PAGE:9==
 
9 
evaluated: 0.04% (ambient), 5%, and 10%. The 10% CO₂ concentration imple-
mented in the photobioreactor trials was selected to mimic the conditions pre-
sent in industrial flue gas processed at Frederikshavn Forsyning.  
Research Question and Experimental Goals: 
How does elevated CO₂ concentration affect the growth dynamics, lipid accu-
mulation, and biochemical composition of T. suecica in controlled cultivation 
systems? 
Goals: 
1. Successfully grow T. suecica directly with supplemented carbon dioxide gas 
2. Estimate the growth dynamics of a microalgae from microscopy 
a. Accurately illustrate the life cycle with a microscope video time 
lapse 
3. Correlate growth dynamics grown under microscopy with the same condi-
tions in the photobioreactor trials 
4. Identify the growth dynamic implications of microalgae cultures grown in 
both heightened CO₂ concentration environments and under standard at-
mospheric conditions.  
The central hypothesis of this research is that microalgal growth can be directly 
sustained using flue gas and that higher concentrations of CO₂ will enhance 
growth rate and lipid accumulation (Carneiro, 2019). It was further hypothesized 
that growth patterns observed under microscopy can be reliably correlated with 
those observed in the PBR setting, allowing for scalable interpretation of micro-
algal behavior under industrial conditions. 
1.2 Bioremediation with Microalgae 
In recent years, the cultivation of marine microalgae at lab and pilot scales has 
gained considerable attention as a sustainable feedstock solution for aquacul-
ture. Microalgae represent the fastest-growing photosynthetic organisms on 
Earth (Ighalo 2022). Their high surface area to volume ratio enables them to 
==PAGE:10==
10 
rapidly absorb CO₂, nutrients, and light, making them exceptionally efficient at 
converting inorganic carbon into biomass (Beardall 2016). Unlike terrestrial 
plants, microalgae can fix 10 to 50 times more CO₂ from sources like industrial 
flue gas, a major contributor to anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions (Pa-
tel, 2025). This high efficiency is due to their rapid growth rates, simple cellular 
structures, and the ability to grow quickly in environments that maximize photo-
synthetic activity such as a well-lit photobioreactor or the surface of the ocean. 
This process of removing CO₂ from the atmosphere is known as phytoremedia-
tion and refers to the use of microalgae to remove or “fix” pollutants such as 
CO₂ and nutrients from the water or air (Beardall, 2016). While the CO₂ is even-
tually released again through respiration or decay, its temporary removal offers 
a significant mitigation strategy, particularly when the resulting biomass is uti-
lized for high-value applications such as aquafeed or biofuels (Barros, 2019). 
1.3 The Green House Effect and Flue Gas 
The greenhouse effect that is driven by increased concentrations of greenhouse 
gases such as CO₂, methane, and nitrous oxide has led to an increase in 
Earth’s heat retention and ongoing climate instability (Ramaswamy, 2001). 
Though CO₂ comprises less than 1% of atmospheric gases, its long atmos-
pheric lifetime and strong absorption in infrared bands allow it to exert dispro-
portionate warming effects (Ramaswamy, 2001). Industrial sources, particularly 
those involving fossil fuel combustion, continue to be the dominant contributors 
to this trend. Flue gas is a byproduct of combustion in coal-fired plants and re-
sults from the processing of sludge with biodigesters for aquaculture facilities. 
Flue gas contains high levels of CO₂ and presents both an environmental prob-
lem and a benefit as a potential biological feedstock. Research has demon-
strated that flue gas streams typically contain around 10% CO₂ and has been 
the chosen the concentration explored in this study (Ramaswamy, 2016). 
However, flue gas is not composed of CO₂ alone, it also contains impurities 
such as sulfur oxides (SOₓ) and nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) (Ramaswamy, 2016). 
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
SOₓ can lower pH and stress algal systems when concentrations exceed toler-
ance thresholds, whereas NOₓ may be assimilated by some algae as a nitrogen 
source (Ramaswamy, 2016). These additional components alongside elevated 
CO₂ create both challenges and opportunities for increased growth dynamics in 
flue gas-based cultivation systems. Prior studies have demonstrated that Tetra-
selmis species can be cultivated effectively using untreated flue gas, even when 
recycled growth media result in elevated sulfate concentrations. These observa-
tions affirm the genus's resilience and highlight its potential role in sustainable 
aquaculture systems (Ramaswamy, 2016). In this study, NOₓ and SOₓ levels 
present in the flue gas were not artificially modified but were applied as-is from 
the biogas combustion source, with measured concentrations of NOₓ ranging 
from 69–88 mg/Nm³ and SOₓ remaining below detection limits.  
1.4 Species of interest: Tetraselmis Suecica  
T. Suecica belongs to the order Chlorodendrales within the green algae class 
Chlorodendrophyceae and are flagellated unicellular photoautotrophs self-sus-
tain by converting sunlight into chemical energy (Larkum, 2016). Known for their 
fused thecal walls and adaptive versatility, Tetraselmis can thrive in both marine 
and freshwater systems, and occupy benthic and pelagic niches across a wide 
range of environmental conditions (Kumar, 2022). These species exhibit toler-
ance to salinity gradients from 0 to 90 ppt, pH ranges of 6.5 to 10.5, and tem-
peratures between 17°C and 39°C (Barberena, 2009). This versatility is further 
complemented by their ability to form resting cysts and survive unfavorable con-
ditions, allowing for reactivation when environmental conditions improve (Ku-
mar, 2022). 
Biochemically, T. suecica cells are rich in chlorophyll (types A and B), carote-
noids, essential fatty acids, amino acids, and polysaccharides (Sedjati, 2020). 
These attributes make them valuable as feed for larval fish and crustaceans 
(Patrinou, 2023). Lipid accumulation is a key trait of interest in aquaculture feed 
development (Patrinou, 2023). Algal lipids are stored in both cell vacuoles and 
==PAGE:12==
12 
cell membranes which can account for up to 80% of wet weight/cell volume un-
der stress (Zhou, 2022). These lipids include structural molecules like phospho-
lipids and glycolipids, as well as storage molecules such as triacylglycerols 
(TAGs), many of which are rich in polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs). Environ-
mental cues such as nitrogen limitation, elevated light intensity, and CO₂ enrich-
ment can redirect algal metabolism toward lipid biosynthesis, an effect that is 
central to the objectives of this study (Negi, 2015). CO₂ enrichment often lowers 
the pH of the growth medium due to the formation of carbonic acid, which can 
alter the availability of inorganic carbon species. These pH shifts can affect en-
zyme activity and cellular homeostasis, to further influence metabolic pathways 
and promote lipid accumulation as a stress response.  
 
Figure 1: Life Cycle of Tetraselmis Illustrated by Barerena et al. 2009 
 
The life cycle of T. Suecica includes both vegetative growth through binary fis-
sion and the formation of resistant cysts under adverse conditions. During favor-
able conditions, motile planktonic cells undergo cell division, producing daugh-
ter cells that continue active growth. Under environmental stress, such as high 
salinity or elevated pH, the cells transition into non-motile resting stages, which 
==PAGE:13==
 
13 
may exist individually or in pairs. With prolonged stress or aging cultures, these 
resting cells encyst, developing thick-walled, ornamented structures capable of 
surviving desiccation or nutrient depletion. Upon return to favorable conditions, 
cysts will germinate to release multiple motile daughter cells, thereby reinitiating 
the vegetative cycle.  
1.5 Photosynthetic Carbon Fixation 
Photosynthetic carbon fixation in T. suecica is carried out in the chloroplasts via 
tightly regulated biochemical pathways (Sedjati, 2020). Light energy, captured 
by photosystems I and II, drives the photolysis of water, production of ATP and 
NADPH, and subsequent fixation of CO₂ via the Calvin cycle (Abiusi, 2014). A 
key enzyme in this cycle, Rubisco, catalyzes the formation of 3-phosphoglycer-
ate, which is converted into sugars and other metabolic precursors (Abiusi, 
2014). However, CO₂ availability in aquatic systems is limited by diffusion con-
straints and the bicarbonate buffer system, a chemical equilibrium that shifts 
dissolved inorganic carbon into bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) or carbonate (CO₃²⁻) 
forms as pH increases (Lachmann, 2017). T. suecica and other algae overcome 
this challenge using carbon-concentrating mechanisms (CCMs), which actively 
import and convert bicarbonate to CO₂ near the active site of Rubisco, optimiz-
ing carbon fixation even under limiting conditions (Beardall, 2016). 
1.6 Microalgae Biotechnology in RAS Systems  
As microalgal biotechnology continues to develop, its integration into aquacul-
ture systems is becoming increasingly practical and impactful (Barros, 2019). 
Microalgae are utilized in wastewater treatment, nutrient recovery, and as a 
sustainable alternative for feed supplementation (Goswami, 2022). Aquaculture 
contributes significantly to global feed protein supply, and with climate change 
intensifying environmental pressures, the demand for sustainable practices is 
growing. Feed production accounts for up to 69% of the environmental impact 
==PAGE:14==
14 
associated with salmon aquaculture (Kuempel, 2023), and replacing compo-
nents of fishmeal with microalgal biomass offers a strategy to reduce ecological 
degradation and improve supply chain resilience. 
Integrated systems now recycle water and nutrients, enabling microalgae to 
grow on aquaculture effluent, aligning with circular economy principles where 
CO₂ and waste outputs are repurposed into algal biomass for feed or fertilizer 
(Reyimu, 2017). Despite economic and technological barriers such as cultiva-
tion and extraction costs, innovations in photobioreactor design, renewable en-
ergy use, and natural light systems have improved the feasibility of algal appli-
cations in aquaculture (Barros, 2019). When powered by sustainable energy 
and supported by nutrient recycling, these systems can approach net-zero car-
bon emissions (Li, 2024). Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS) are closed-
loop fish farms that reuse water to reduce environmental discharge. While re-
ducing freshwater use and enhancing biosecurity, they generate nutrient-rich 
waste from feed and fish metabolism. These waste streams often require en-
ergy-intensive treatment and release greenhouse gases like nitrous oxide. Mi-
croalgae such as T. suecica offer a solution by assimilating nutrients while pro-
ducing biomass for reuse. Cultivated on RAS effluent and flue gas, they function 
as treatment agents and sustainable feedstocks. 
Feed in RAS typically depends on wild-sourced fishmeal, contributing to over-
fishing. Microalgae provide a renewable alternative rich in polyunsaturated fatty 
acids, amino acids, and proteins suitable for aquafeed. Wastewater-grown al-
gae can supplement or replace fishmeal, transforming waste into usable inputs. 
However, the nitrogen-to-phosphorus (N:P) ratio in RAS effluent that often ex-
ceeds the ideal 16:1 Redfield ratio due to high-protein feeds can limit algal 
growth. Adjusting feed formulations or supplementing phosphorus can improve 
biomass yield and nutrient recovery. Integrating microalgal biotechnology into 
RAS mitigates pollution, reduces dependence on marine resources, supports 
carbon capture, and advances sustainable aquaculture. This study explores 
these themes through experiments on T. suecica under elevated CO₂ and flue 
==PAGE:15==
 
15 
gas, using microscopy and photobioreactor cultivation to evaluate its potential 
for carbon capture and sustainable feed production. 
1.7 Microalgae as a Feed Additive in Sustainable Aquaculture  
 
Manipulating microalgae to enhance their content of carotenoids, chlorophyll, 
total lipids, and particularly polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs) is of growing in-
terest to aquaculture companies seeking to improve feed quality, fish health, 
and production sustainability while reducing reliance on marine-derived ingredi-
ents (Wells, 2017; El-Sayed, 2023). Increasing lipid content in aquaculture 
feeds enhances energy density, allowing fish to meet their metabolic needs 
more efficiently while sparing protein for growth (Güroy, 2012). Lipids also con-
tribute to improved growth rates, feed conversion efficiency, and flesh quality, 
particularly by boosting omega-3 content (Turchini, 2009). Optimizing lipid use 
in feeds can reduce environmental impacts by lowering nitrogen waste and de-
creasing reliance on fishmeal (Gao, 2022). 
Within this broader lipid group, polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs), particularly 
omega-3 types like EPA and DHA, are essential components due to their critical 
roles in fish growth, immune function, and reproduction (Adarme-Vega, 2014). 
Many farmed species, especially marine fish, cannot synthesize these fatty ac-
ids and must obtain them through their diet. PUFAs support healthy cell mem-
branes, enhance stress resistance, and improve reproductive success and lar-
val development. They also enrich the nutritional value of fish fillets for human 
consumption. As demand for sustainable feed alternatives grows, PUFA-rich 
sources such as microalgae are becoming increasingly important in aquafeed 
formulations (Borowitzka, 2013). 
Carotenoids, such as astaxanthin and beta-carotene, are potent antioxidants 
that help protect fish and shrimp from oxidative stress, enhance immune func-
tion, and contribute to pigmentation, which is especially important for species 
like salmon and ornamental fish (Gouveia ,2003; Shah, 2016). These pigments 
also improve reproductive performance and larval development. Chlorophyll, 
while less prominent, contributes to antioxidant activity and may support gut 
==PAGE:16==
16 
health and detoxification (Hemaiswarya, 2011). When included in feeds, espe-
cially from natural sources such as microalgae, both pigment groups enhance 
the overall health, stress resilience, and market value of cultured species (Gar-
cía, 2022). 
This study explores these themes through experiments on T. suecica under ele-
vated CO₂ and flue gas, using microscopy and photobioreactor cultivation to 
evaluate its potential for carbon capture and sustainable feed production. By 
combining carbon mitigation with targeted manipulation of algal metabolism to 
enhance lipid content, chlorophyll, carotenoids, and polyunsaturated fatty acids, 
this research contributes to the development of integrated solutions that reduce 
environmental impact while improving the nutritional and functional value of aq-
uaculture feeds.

2. Experimental Design and Methodology 
2.1 Overview 
Sterile T. suecica cultures were grown in WALNE medium under continuous 
LED lighting at 60 PAR and constant aeration with inline CO₂ delivery controlled 
by pH. Cultures were maintained in 2 L vertical photobioreactors (PBRs) across 
three CO₂ treatments: atmospheric (0.04%), moderate enrichment (5%), and 
high enrichment (10%). Each trial lasted 11 days to allow cultures to reach the 
stationary growth phase. Daily sampling involved removing 30 mL of culture for 
optical density (OD) measurements at 540 nm and 750 nm using a UV-Vis 
spectrophotometer, with fresh medium added to maintain volume and nutrient 
balance. pH and temperature were monitored with calibrated probes. 
OD readings were correlated with biomass through dry weight and ash-free dry 
weight determinations. Biomass concentration was calculated using OD to bio-
mass pre-determined conversion equations:  
• 
Dry weight (g/L) = 0.7122 × OD₇₅₀ + 0.023 
• 
Dry weight (g/L) = 0.6586 × OD₅₄₀ + 0.0249 
Microscopy trials were performed using a Leica DMIL LED inverted microscope 
at 100x magnification. Cultures were maintained in 9.9 mL sealed microreac-
tors, continuously aerated with humidified CO₂ at 1 L/min. Algal samples were 
imaged every five minutes over 24–72-hour periods using LAS X software. The 
same CO₂ concentrations as the PBRs were used. Deionized water was period-
ically added to counteract evaporative loss. 
Algal behavior and growth were analyzed by time-lapse image analysis. Manual 
cell counts were performed and cell movement, pigmentation, and division pat-
terns served as qualitative indicators of health and developmental stage. 
==PAGE:18==
18 
Lipid content was quantified via the Bligh and Dyer extraction method. One 
gram of freeze-dried biomass from each treatment was subjected to centrifuga-
tion, methanol–chloroform extraction, and nitrogen-assisted evaporation. The 
lipid extract was stored for fatty acid profiling via gas chromatography mass 
spectrometry (GC-MS) at DTU Food, targeting key polyunsaturated fatty acids 
(PUFAs) such as DHA, EPA, and ARA. 
Chlorophyll a content and total carotenoids were quantified using UV-Vis ab-
sorbance following pigment extraction with methanol. Samples were sonicated, 
incubated at 8°C for 24 hours in darkness, and centrifuged. Absorbance at 666 
nm and 470 nm was used to calculate pigment concentrations using (Lichten-
thaler, 1987) equations. 
2.2 Photobioreactor Trials 
Maintaining precise pH levels was critical, as the photosynthetic efficiency of mi-
croalgae and their ability to sequester carbon were closely linked to the chemi-
cal conditions of the growth medium. An unregulated addition of CO₂ depressed 
the pH below optimal levels would reduce biomass productivity and, in extreme 
cases, cause total culture collapse. To mitigate this, a pH-stat control system 
was used to allow for precise modulation of CO₂ input and preserve ideal 
growth conditions. 
In parallel with the microscopy trials, T. suecica was cultivated in vertical photo-
bioreactor (PBR) systems. Three identical bioreactors, each with a working vol-
ume of 2 liters, was continuously aerated and illuminated by constant LED light-
ing at an intensity of 60 PAR. 
The culture medium used in all trials was WALNE medium, a nutrient-rich solu-
tion suitable for marine microalgae. Each bioreactor was equipped with pH and 
temperature sensors for daily monitoring, and a sparger system for CO₂ delivery 
was installed at the base of each reactor to ensure uniform gas to liquid ex-
change. 
==PAGE:19==
 
19 
To ensure uniformity in starting conditions, algal inoculants were derived from a 
single sterile culture with a known optic density of 0.878 and equal volumes of 
both inoculum and growth medium were distributed into each reactor (2 mL of 
nutrient medium, 200mL algae culture and 2 L seawater). Apart from CO₂ lev-
els, all abiotic parameters, including temperature and salinity, were standard-
ized across all setups. (temperature = 23 C, Salinity = 34ppt)

Throughout the experiment, daily measurements were taken to monitor algal 
growth. These included optical density readings at 540 nm and 750 nm, dry 
weight, and ash-free dry weight. These metrics enabled calculation of growth 
rate and biomass accumulation.

2.2.1  pH to Carbon Dioxide Correlation 
A separate experiment was conducted to characterize the relationship between 
CO₂ concentration and the resulting pH of the growth medium. When CO₂ was 
bubbled into a saltwater medium, it dissolved and reacted with water to form 
carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), which then dissociated into bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) and 
hydrogen ions (H⁺). This process caused a measurable decrease in pH and 
served as a proxy for CO₂ absorption. 
This mechanism was studied using a system that consisted of a saltwater solu-
tion, an air bubbler delivering CO₂, and a data logger connected to a pH probe 
and CO₂ sensor. The system recorded real-time changes in pH and dissolved 
CO₂, collecting data every 30 seconds. Steady-state pH values were correlated 
with specific CO₂ concentrations: 0.04% CO₂ corresponded to pH 8.08, 5% CO₂ 
to pH 6.39, and 10% CO₂ to pH 6.07. 
An air bubbler was used to introduce CO₂ into a saltwater medium that mim-
icked the conditions in which the Tetraselmis species was grown. A pH probe 
recorded the resulting changes in pH, and a CO₂ sensor measured the dis-
solved CO₂ (pCO₂). This setup provided the necessary reference pH values for 
the photobioreactor and microscopy trials at specific CO₂ concentrations. 
Before CO₂ was introduced, the saltwater solution remained at its initial pH, de-
termined by its buffer capacity and any pre-existing dissolved CO₂. As CO₂ bub-
bling began, it dissolved in the solution and formed carbonic acid, which dissoci-
ated and released hydrogen ions, resulting in a rapid decrease in pH. This de-
crease followed an exponential decay pattern: the initial drop was steep, then 
gradually slowed as the system approached equilibrium with the CO₂ concen-
tration in the gas phase. Once steady state was achieved, the pH stabilized at a 
lower value dictated by both the partial pressure of CO₂ and the solution’s buff-
ering capacity.

2.2.2 Optic Density to Biomass Correlation 
Measuring optical density (OD) of the algal culture was essential for estimating 
biomass concentration. The objective of this protocol was to establish a reliable 
correlation between optical density and biomass in the microalgal culture sys-
tem. By employing standardized measurements of dry weight and ash-free dry 
weight, an OD-to-biomass curve was generated to facilitate estimation of algal 
biomass. 
OD measurements were taken using a spectrophotometer at designated wave-
lengths. Each measurement used the same cuvette to minimize variability. OD 
values were recorded for different culture concentrations, specifically 50/50 and 
30/70 ratios of medium to algae. These readings were logged in an Excel 
spreadsheet for subsequent correlation with dry weight data to establish a 
standardized biomass estimation method. 
Microalgal biomass was also quantified through manual cell counting. A 10 mL 
sample was placed in a cytometer, and the resulting counts were used to build 
a growth curve for determining the specific growth rate. 
To estimate biomass concentration, OD measurements were taken daily at 540 
nm and 750 nm from each photobioreactor. A dilution series was prepared, and 
absorbance was measured at both wavelengths to establish a relationship be-
tween OD and dry weight. Ash-free dry weight and total dry weight were also 
measured to validate biomass accumulation. 
For dry weight measurements, 47 mm GF/C glass microfiber filters were pre-
combusted at 450°C for 2 to 4 hours to remove residual organic material. Once 
cooled, filters were weighed on an analytical balance and documented in Excel 
prior to use. A known volume of algal culture, pipetted with consistent aeration 
and mixing, was filtered through the pre-weighed filters to capture the biomass. 
The filters were rinsed with deionized water to remove salts and remaining me-
dium, leaving only algal material for measurement. These filters were then dried 
at 105°C for 2 to 4 hours, cooled, and re-weighed to determine total dry weight. 
==PAGE:24==
24 
To determine the organic fraction of the biomass, dried filters were combusted 
at 450°C in a muffle furnace for 2 hours. After cooling, the filters were weighed 
again to measure the remaining ash. The difference between the pre- and post-
combustion weights provided the ash-free dry weight, which was recorded in 
Excel. 
OD values were then plotted against the corresponding dry weight measure-
ments to produce a standard curve. This correlation allowed for rapid, non-de-
structive biomass estimation in future experiments, supporting accurate moni-
toring of algal growth conditions and system optimization.

2.3.1 Cell Count Methods 
To estimate algal coverage in microscopy images, two complementary image 
analysis methods were employed to increase robustness and reliability, thresh-
old-based segmentation with contour extraction and color-based pixel classifica-
tion. The threshold-based method was most effective for in focus images to 
have precise delineation of algal regions. The color-based method remained us-
able for slightly out-of-focus images, although it introduced a higher risk of mis-
classifying non-algal regions. 
The threshold-based approach operated by first converting each microscope 
image from color to grayscale to simplify processing. A Gaussian blur was ap-
plied to reduce image noise, followed by a fixed, manually selected intensity 
threshold to generate a binary image where darker regions were interpreted as 
algae. This binary image was then inverted so algal regions appeared white and 
the background black. A contour detection algorithm identified the boundaries of 
connected white regions, which were used to create a binary mask with algae 
encoded as one and background as zero. Algal coverage was calculated as the 
proportion of mask pixels representing algae. Visualization tools were optionally 
used to qualitatively assess segmentation accuracy. 
==PAGE:28==
28 
The cell-counting code formed part of the same microscopy analysis pipeline. It 
reused the binary image created during thresholding and applied an area filter 
to remove contours below a certain size, reducing false positives. The remain-
ing contours were counted as individual cells. Additional functionality automati-
cally discarded blurry images and used timestamp metadata to sort the dataset 
chronologically, ensuring that only high-quality and properly ordered images 
were analyzed. Percentage area coverage over time was also tracked, with 
black representing 0 and white representing 1. In the color-based method, a sin-
gle well-defined image was manually selected for reference, from which repre-
sentative RGB color profiles for algae and background were automatically ex-
tracted using the contour-based segmentation method described above. For 
each pixel in the remaining images, the Euclidean distance in RGB space was 
calculated relative to the algae and background reference vectors. Each pixel 
was then individually classified as either algae or background based on which 
reference color it was closest to. Algal coverage was estimated by calculating 
the proportion of pixels identified as algae

2.4 Laboratory Analysis 
 
2.4.1 Lipid and Fatty Acid Analysis 
Total lipid extraction was carried out using the Bligh and Dyer method (Bligh, 
1959). Each analysis used 1 g of freeze-dried algal biomass obtained at the end 
of a PBR trial. The biomass was centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 10 minutes to re-
move excess water and then freeze-dried at -20°C. For small-scale lipid analy-
sis, subsamples weighing between 1 and 20 mg wet weight were placed in pre-
weighed glass vials and dissolved in a 2:1 chloroform: methanol mixture at 20 
times the volume of the sample. Homogenization was performed using ultra-
sound sonication to ensure full disruption of the algal cells. 
The homogenized solution was filtered through Whatman filters, and 0.88% 
aqueous potassium chloride was added to aid in phase separation. After centrif-
ugation, the lower lipid-rich chloroform layer was filtered and collected. The fil-
trate was evaporated under a nitrogen gas stream, redissolved in chloroform, 
==PAGE:30==
30 
and transferred to a second pre-weighed vial for an additional evaporation step. 
The final lipid content was determined gravimetrically and expressed as a per-
centage of the original wet biomass weight. 
For gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) preparation, a 100 mL 
sample was prepared by dissolving algal biomass or a sample-loaded GC filter 
in a chloroform: methanol solution. An internal standard (C23:0 methyl ester) 
was added in concentrations tailored to the sample’s lipid content. The standard 
was kept under refrigerated or frozen conditions until use. Samples were soni-
cated and stored at -20°C for 24 hours. 
 
To evaluate the biochemical composition of the extracted lipids, fatty acid me-
thyl esters (FAMEs) were prepared and analyzed using GC-MS. This process 
quantified the proportions of essential polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs) in 
the biomass. For each analysis, a 100 mL sample was processed by placing ei-
ther biomass or a GC filter into a clean glass test tube. A 2:1 chloroform:metha-
nol mixture was added in sufficient volume to fully immerse the sample. An in-
ternal standard (tricosanoic acid methyl ester (C23:0)) was added. 
 
The tubes were flushed with nitrogen and sealed with aluminum lids. Samples 
underwent sonication in an ice-cold ultrasound bath for 20 minutes and were in-
cubated at -20°C for 24 hours to complete lipid extraction. After incubation, the 
lipid extract was transferred to GC vials and labeled. 
Solvent evaporation was performed by directing nitrogen into the vial at 60°C for 
20 minutes, ensuring that samples did not over-dry. FAME synthesis followed, 
using a transesterification reagent consisting of acetyl chloride and methanol in 
toluene (66 mL toluene, 85 mL methanol, 15 mL acetyl chloride). The solution 
was prepared under continuous magnetic stirring. Vials were capped tightly and 
heated in an aluminum block at 95°C for two hours. During this reaction, sodium 
bicarbonate was bubbled with nitrogen to serve as a base trap for neutralizing 
any acid vapors. 
==PAGE:31==
 
31 
Following the reaction, vials were cooled to room temperature, and 0.5 mL of 
5% NaHCO₃ was added to neutralize remaining acid. The upper organic phase 
was extracted using heptane containing butylated hydroxytoluene (BHT) to pre-
vent lipid oxidation. This extraction step was repeated to maximize recovery. 
The combined organic phase was dried under nitrogen at 60°C and reconsti-
tuted in 0.5 mL of chloroform. The final sample was flushed with nitrogen, 
sealed, and stored at -80°C until further analysis by GC-MS. 
All glassware used throughout the lipid and fatty acid analysis was meticulously 
cleaned, acid-treated, rinsed with ultrapure water, and oven-dried at 200°C to 
eliminate contamination and ensure accuracy.

2.4.2 Chlorophyll and Carotenoid Analysis 
Chlorophyll a and total carotenoid concentrations were determined through light 
absorbance measurements using a UV-visible spectrophotometer, following the 
protocol described by (Lichtenthaler,1994). 
==PAGE:32==
32 
Three freeze-dried biological replicates were selected from each CO₂ treatment 
group. Each replicate was weighed and transferred into a centrifuge tube. To 
prevent pigment degradation, all sample handling was conducted under low-
light conditions. Two milliliters of absolute methanol were added to each tube, 
and the samples were briefly vortexed for 5 seconds. Sonication was then per-
formed in an ice-cooled water bath at 45 kHz for 15 minutes to disrupt the algal 
cells and facilitate pigment extraction. 
After sonication, samples were stored at 8°C for 24 hours in darkness. Vials 
were wrapped in aluminum foil to minimize the risk of light-induced oxidation. 
Following incubation, samples were centrifuged at 4000 rpm for 5 minutes. The 
resulting supernatant was carefully transferred into clean spectrophotometric 
cuvettes for analysis. 
Absorbance measurements were taken at wavelengths of 470 nm and 666 nm. 
Pigment concentrations were calculated using the following equations provided 
by (Lichtenthaler, 1987):

High-purity reagents and solvents were used throughout the pigment analysis, 
including chloroform, methanol, toluene, and heptane, all of HPLC grade. The 
internal standard used for reference in related analyses was C23:0 methyl ester 
(Sigma-Aldrich). Acetyl chloride and sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO₃), both ana-
lytical grades, were also used during fatty acid quantification procedures. 
All glassware used in the analysis was rigorously cleaned to prevent contamina-
tion. This process included detergent washing, immersion in an acid bath, multi-
ple rinses with both hot and ultrapure water, and final drying in an oven at 
200°C.

2.5  Statistical Analysis 
 
Two-way ANOVA was used to assess the independent and interactive effects of 
time and CO₂ treatment on biomass growth in PBR trials. Post hoc comparisons 
were performed using Tukey’s test to determine pairwise differences between 
treatments across time points. For pigment, lipid, and fatty acid data, one-way 
ANOVA was used to compare means across CO₂ treatments, followed by eval-

cant (e.g., lipid content, pigment levels), trends are noted and discussed based 
on biological relevance. All statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad

3.1.1 Photobioreactor Discussion 
Despite expectations that supplemental CO₂ might stimulate growth, the ele-
vated CO₂ conditions likely led to acidification of the culture medium (Loch-
mann, 2017). This is consistent with published studies showing that excess CO₂ 
can decrease pH to suboptimal levels, inhibiting photosynthesis and nutrient 
solubility (Yen, 2015). In our trials, reactors exposed to 5% and 10% CO₂ likely 
experienced acidification due to carbonic acid accumulation. This could explain 
==PAGE:40==
40 
the consistent underperformance of elevated CO₂ conditions, despite the theo-
retical advantage of increased carbon availability. 
The early advantage seen with CO₂ supplementation, particularly in the 10% 
condition during days 2–4, likely stems from abundant carbon availability. How-
ever, this benefit was transient and diminished by mid-culture, likely due to acid-
ification or carbonic acid buildup, as supported by literature suggesting reduced 
fatty acid desaturation and metabolic inhibition under high CO₂ (Li, 2014; Patri-
nou, 2023). Although 5% CO₂ showed modest promise during early growth, the 
10% treatment ultimately suppressed growth. 
Atmospheric CO₂ supported the highest biomass production due to balanced 
nutrient use and optimal pH conditions. The final biomass data and growth dy-
namics suggest that atmospheric CO₂ maintained more optimal conditions for 
growth, possibly due to pH stability and more efficient nutrient assimilation (Al-
momani 2019). While initially counterintuitive, the result that atmospheric CO₂ 
produced the highest biomass aligns with existing literature showing that exces-
sive CO₂ can lower the culture pH to suboptimal levels, potentially inhibiting 
photosynthesis and nutrient assimilation (Larkum, 2016). 
According to Peter Schulz (2016), microalgal species such as T. suecica grow 
optimally at a near-neutral pH range of 7.5–8.0. Under atmospheric CO₂, pH 
likely remained within this range, supporting better nutrient solubility and meta-
bolic stability, especially for phosphate, which can precipitate out of solution at 
lower or excessively high pH. In contrast, the unbuffered CO₂-enriched cultures 
likely dropped below this optimal range, suppressing growth. This is especially 
relevant for T. suecica, which thrives at a pH near 7.5–8.0. 
While CO₂ enrichment did not enhance biomass in this study, it may offer bene-
fits under different conditions, such as nitrogen limitation or higher salinity, 
which are known to drive lipid accumulation which is another research goal for 
this study (Carneiro, 2019). In such cases, the metabolic stress from pH 
changes could shift resource allocation toward storage compounds like lipids. 
==PAGE:41==
 
41 
Pigment variation may also reflect adaptive responses to CO₂ and light stress 
(Henriques, 2007). 
It is important to note that CO₂ and pH were not decoupled in this study, mean-
ing the observed inhibitory effects of high CO₂ may be confounded by pH 
stress. Future studies that pair elevated CO₂ with active pH regulation could 
yield very different results. Under these controlled conditions, high CO₂ levels 
might enhance growth rather than inhibit it, particularly during exponential 
phases where carbon demand is highest. This pairing could unlock the benefits 
of CO₂ enrichment without triggering the physiological stress associated with 
acidification (Almomani, 2019). Such studies would be especially useful in as-
sessing not just biomass productivity but also biochemical outputs such as lipid 
content, pigment composition, and protein yield, which may respond differently 
to CO₂ and pH interactions. Adjusting nutrient regimes and incorporating pH 
monitoring could enable a more refined understanding of how CO₂ enrichment 
can be optimized.

3.2.1  Microscope Discussion 
Although we anticipated observing direct growth and division events under the 
microscope to ideally capturing a complete life cycle through time-lapse imag-
ing, all attempts were ultimately unsuccessful. Consequently, we relied on sur-
face coverage analysis from still images as a proxy for culture proliferation. No-
tably, no mass die-off was observed in either treatment. The absence of cell 
mortality, particularly under flue gas exposure, is significant. This suggests that 
while conditions may not have supported rapid growth, they also did not exert 
lethal stress on the cultures. 
This steady surface coverage found in both flue and atmospheric microscopy 
trials may result from physiological stress responses that trigger life cycle ad-
justment strategies. For example, in Tetraselmis species, smaller cells, when 
exposed to stress, often undergo programmed cell death, while larger counter-
parts persist and continue dividing (Arora, 2015). This population-level hetero-
geneity allows certain subpopulations to endure environmental fluctuations and 
sustain community growth (Arora, 2015). 
Such adjustments to life cycle stages such as the formation of resting cells in 
response to stress likely explain why the comparative trials did not show a large 
increase or decrease in cell numbers over time, and why active cell divisions 
were not captured. The persistence of larger, stress-tolerant cells likely contrib-
utes to the steadiness in surface coverage observed under flue gas treatment. 
The high-CO₂ conditions may mimic environmental stressors such as nutrient 
limitation or acidification, thereby triggering cellular stress-response pathways. 
Under such conditions, cells often shift their metabolic priorities, increasing lipid 
and pigment synthesis while modifying division dynamics. This stress-induced 
phenotypic plasticity may promote survival and gradual regrowth, in line with 
previous findings in Tetraselmis (Arora, 2015). 
==PAGE:44==
44 
These observations suggest that the algae may have entered a physiological 
state focused more on maintenance and survival than on active division. Sev-
eral factors may have contributed to the absence of clear growth during the mi-
croscopy trials. For example, the duration of exposure may have been too short 
for cultures to fully adapt to the altered gas conditions. External stressors like 
salinity fluctuations from evaporation or consistently high average light intensity 
could have disrupted the normal life cycle and prevented visible propagation. 
The microscopy trial results still support the broader conclusion that flue gas ex-
posure promotes survival-oriented adaptations in T. suecica and that despite 
these stressors it may indicate an ability to survive in suboptimal environmental 
conditions. These include structural specialization, increased pigment content, 
and resilience within subpopulations. While active proliferation was not cap-
tured, the ability of the culture to maintain coverage and avoid decline under in-
dustrial gas conditions is itself indicative of robustness and tolerance, both im-
portant considerations for future applications

3.3.1 Pigment Discussion 
 
These findings suggest that under elevated CO₂ T. suecica may shift metabolic 
priorities from biomass production toward stress-response pathways, such 
as photoprotection and pigment accumulation. This is consistent with existing 
literature indicating that carotenoid biosynthesis is upregulated under stress, in-
cluding acidification, high light, or oxidative pressure (Demming, 2002; Lemoine, 
2010). 
 
Carotenoids, including astaxanthin and lutein, serve dual roles in photosynthetic 
organisms: they assist in light harvesting and protect chloroplast components 
by neutralizing reactive oxygen species (ROS) (Ferreira, 2007).  The increased 
carotenoid content observed here could reflect activation of photoprotective 
mechanisms in response to CO₂-induced stress, especially under conditions of 
unregulated pH. This aligns with reports that carotenoids accumulate alongside 
triacylglycerols (TAGs) during stress conditions, as both share biosynthetic pre-
cursors (Zhekisheva, 2005; Grünewald, 2001). 
 
Chlorophyll content also rose significantly with increasing CO₂, suggesting that 
photosynthetic capacity may have been maintained or even intensified, possibly 
as a compensatory mechanism to better utilize excess inorganic carbon. This 
mirrors findings by Schüler et al. (2020), who observed pigment accumulation 
under thermal and light stress, reinforcing the idea that environmental inputs di-
rectly modulate pigment synthesis. 
 
==PAGE:47==
 
47 
These pigment shifts illustrate the plasticity of Tetraselmis metabolism under 
changing environmental conditions. While biomass accumulation was highest 
under atmospheric CO₂, 10% CO₂ induced the greatest pigment yield, indicat-
ing potential for value-added biochemical production even under suboptimal 
growth conditions. 
 
These findings align with physiological models of photosynthetic adaptation de-
scribing that photosynthetic organisms deploy both short-term and long-term ac-
climation mechanisms to regulate pigment composition and balance excitation 
energy between photosystems I and II (Larkum, 2016). Carotenoids play a criti-
cal role in mitigating photoinhibition, especially under stress scenarios involv-
ing CO₂, light, and pH imbalance (Henriques, 2007). 
 
Given their antioxidant properties and high commercial value in aquaculture 
feed, carotenoids represent an attractive biochemical target (Henriques, 2007). 
Our data suggest that CO₂ enrichment, despite reducing biomass, may en-
hance biochemical value through increased pigment yield.  
 
3.4  Total Lipids Results 
 
Lipid content in T. suecica. showed minor variation across CO₂ treatments, with 
the highest average lipid recovery observed at 10% CO₂ (approximately 9.6%), 
followed by atmospheric CO₂ (around 8.6%), and 5% CO₂ (about 8.4%). How-
ever, our results revealed there was no statistically significant differences 
among these treatments (P-values > 0.83), indicating that while a trend was

3.4.1 Total Lipid Discussion 
Despite the lack of significance, the upward trend in lipid accumulation under el-
evated CO₂ mirrors patterns observed in pigment biosynthesis and points to a 
metabolic shift toward storage compound production under mild stress. The 
highest lipid content occurred alongside peak carotenoid and chlorophyll levels 
at 10% CO₂, suggesting a coordinated redirection of carbon into metabolites 
when photosynthetic and environmental conditions deviate from the optimum. 
The increase in lipid content under 10% CO₂ aligns with known stress-induced 
biosynthetic responses in microalgae. When exposed to stressors such as car-
bon oversupply, nutrient limitation, salinity, or high light, algae often divert meta-
bolic energy away from proliferation and toward the synthesis of triacylglycerols 
(TAGs). (Falcão, 2019). This redirection supports survival under suboptimal 
==PAGE:49==
 
49 
conditions and is well documented in Tetraselmis species and other green al-
gae. The biochemical mechanism likely involves acetyl-CoA carboxylase-medi-
ated fixation of HCO₃⁻ to malonyl-CoA, a precursor in fatty acid elongation. (Fal-
cão, 2019).  According to Beardall, this dark inorganic carbon fixation occurs 
more rapidly in the light and contributes to lipid synthesis, though the fixed car-
bon is eventually released as CO₂ during fatty acid chain formation. This ex-
plains why increased lipid production does not necessarily correlate with re-
tained biomass or net carbon gain. 
The acidification of the culture medium under high CO₂ levels may simulate nu-
trient limitation by restricting enzyme efficiency or nutrient uptake, which triggers 
stress pathways typically activated during nitrogen or phosphorus scarcity. Liter-
ature has shown that such pH shifts can act as cues for lipid accumulation even 
under nutrient-replete conditions, as in studies by Schulz (2016) and Fon-Sing 
(2016). This effect may explain why lipid accumulation increased at 10% CO₂ 
but not at 5%, which appeared to support growth without triggering the stress 
threshold necessary for lipid synthesis. 
The comparatively low lipid content under 5% CO₂, despite being an enriched 
environment, suggests a threshold effect in metabolic stress perception. Moder-
ate CO₂ enrichment may enhance photosynthesis and growth but is insufficient 
to reroute carbon toward storage lipids. Only under elevated or combined 
stresses, such as excessive CO₂ and nutrient stress, do Tetraselmis cells priori-
tize lipid synthesis over division. This pattern has been observed in other stud-
ies, where only severe or compounded environmental pressures lead to signifi-
cant lipid accumulation. (Montoya-Vallejo, 2023). 
The correlation between lipid and pigment content in this experiment also re-
flects shared metabolic pathways and stress responses. Lipid biosynthesis, par-
ticularly of storage lipids like TAGs, often occurs alongside carotenoid produc-
tion as both respond to oxidative stress and utilize overlapping biosynthetic in-
termediates (Shi, 2020).  This dual accumulation increases the biochemical 
value of the culture, even if total biomass is not maximized.

3.5.1 Fatty Acid Discussion  
Our findings demonstrate that CO₂ enrichment, particularly at 10%, significantly 
enhances total PUFA content and promotes consistent EPA (20:5 n-3) biosyn-
thesis, supporting trends observed in previous studies. While SFA levels re-
mained stable across treatments, elevated CO₂ concentrations shifted fatty acid 
profiles by increasing PUFA content, with the highest levels observed in the 5% 
CO₂ group. This aligns with studies such as Zhang et al. (2024), which report 
CO₂-induced increases in PUFAs across various microalgal species. 
For example, Skeletonema costatum cultured under high CO₂ conditions exhib-
ited elevated production of total lipids and long-chain PUFAs like EPA and DHA 
(Yang, 2021). Similarly, in Phaeodactylum tricornutum, CO₂-driven overexpres-
sion of glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PDH) enhanced NADPH avail-
ability, a key cofactor in PUFA biosynthesis via the oxidative pentose phosphate 
pathway (OPPP) (Zhao, 2019). These molecular pathways may help explain the 
consistent EPA detection in the 10% CO₂ group, as NADPH is essential for fatty 
acid desaturation and elongation. 
Moreover, a meta-analysis by Feng (2023) concluded that CO₂ concentrations 
up to 6% can significantly elevate PUFA yields in species like Nannochloropsis 
oculata, consistent with our observation of maximal PUFA levels at 5% CO₂. 
Collectively, these results reinforce that CO₂ enrichment serves as a potent 
==PAGE:54==
54 
modulator of fatty acid metabolism, with promising biotechnological potential for 
enhancing nutritionally valuable lipids in microalgal systems.

4. Conclusion 
This study investigated how elevated CO₂ concentrations, including industrial 
flue gas, affect the growth dynamics, lipid accumulation, and biochemical re-
sponses of Tetraselmis in controlled cultivation systems. While the hypothesis 
predicted that higher CO₂ would enhance both growth and lipid productivity, re-
sults revealed a more nuanced outcome. Elevated CO₂, particularly at 10%, 
consistently suppressed biomass accumulation compared to atmospheric condi-
tions which is likely due to acidification from unregulated CO₂ dosing. This con-
tradicts expectations of growth enhancement and highlights the critical im-
portance of pH regulation when supplementing CO₂. 
However, elevated CO₂ did promote increased pigment and lipid accumulation, 
supporting the hypothesis that stress from high CO₂ can trigger metabolic shifts 
toward secondary metabolite production. This was most evident in the 10% CO₂ 
treatment, where chlorophyll, carotenoid, and lipid levels peaked despite re-
duced biomass. This is suggestive of a redirection of metabolic energy from 
growth to compounds for biochemical resilience. 
Fatty acid methyl ester (FAME) analysis further revealed that elevated CO₂ al-
tered lipid profiles in favor of nutritionally valuable polyunsaturated fatty acids 
(PUFAs), particularly eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA). Total PUFA levels were sig-
nificantly higher in both CO₂-enriched groups compared to atmospheric con-
trols, with the 5% CO₂ group exhibiting the highest overall PUFA content. Nota-
bly, EPA was consistently detected only in the 10% CO₂ group, suggesting that 
higher CO₂ levels may activate or stabilize biosynthetic pathways specific to 
long-chain n-3 fatty acids. These findings align with previous research on micro-
algae such as Phaeodactylum tricornutum and Skeletonema costatum, where 
CO₂ enrichment stimulated NADPH-dependent PUFA biosynthesis, including 
EPA and DHA production. This reinforces the potential of Tetraselmis as a plat-
form for high-value lipid production under carbon-rich conditions. 
==PAGE:56==
56 
Microscopy trials further illustrated these stress responses, with flue gas-treated 
cultures showing consistent surface coverage over the entirety of the trial. 
These results underscore Tetraselmis’ physiological plasticity under industrial 
gas exposure, although attempts to directly correlate microscopic growth dy-
namics with photobioreactor performance were inconclusive due to methodolog-
ical limitations and divergent outcomes between systems. Nonetheless, cultures 
grown directly with flue gas survived and proliferated, demonstrating the viability 
of integrating microalgal systems with aquaculture waste streams. 
The inability to decouple CO₂ enrichment from acidification means that the in-
hibitory effects observed under elevated CO₂ may be confounded by pH stress. 
Future studies that regulate pH alongside CO₂ dosing could more accurately 
determine the optimal conditions for maximizing both growth and biochemical 
yield. While atmospheric CO₂ yielded the highest biomass due to stable pH and 
more complete nutrient assimilation, elevated CO₂ conditions revealed potential 
for enhanced pigment and lipid production. These findings suggest that Tetra-
selmis can support cultivation goals in sustainable aquaculture for both phy-
coremediation and as a component for circular bioeconomy systems.

5. Sources of Errors 
Major sources of error in this study may have arisen from inconsistencies during 
laboratory procedures, particularly in the preparation of total lipid and fatty acid 
samples for gas chromatography. FAME analysis showed that fatty acids ac-
counted for an average of 90% of total lipids, which may reflect experimental 
variability or inaccuracies during extraction, transesterification, or quantification 
steps. In the photobioreactor trials, the CO₂ tank occasionally ran out before re-
placement, causing unplanned fluctuations in pH and potentially impacting 
growth and metabolite accumulation. In the microscopy trials, only one flue gas 
exposure trial could be conducted due to limited gas availability, reducing the 
ability to assess reproducibility and variation across replicates. These limitations 
could be solved in future experiments from the implementation of tighter control 
over gas delivery systems and more consistent sampling methods.
