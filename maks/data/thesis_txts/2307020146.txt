Abstract
Operational modal analysis has proven to be a useful tool for modal analysis
within the ﬁeld of civil engineering. The beneﬁts of the methods translate
to the ﬁeld of rotor dynamics, where structural health monitoring and in
situ vibrational testing is becoming a large topic. By exciting a rotor system
through active bearings the OMA methods can be used for parameter esti-
mation without taking the rotor out of service. The methods are evaluated
experimentally on diﬀerent systems and compared to results from classic ex-
perimental modal analysis. The experimental results shows that the OMA
methods are capable of obtaining good estimates of the natural frequencies
and mode shapes, but the Damping ratio estimates are inaccurate.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
The development of active control in the conventional tilting-pad journal
bearing, has transformed it from a passive machine element into an active
mechatronic element. The possibility of controlling the oil ﬂow to the bear-
ing and thus the forces acting on the rotor has not only made it possible to
manipulate the rotor system dynamics but also allows for in situ excitation
of a system without taking it out of service. However if the forces acting on
the shaft is not easily accessible classical experimental modal analysis(EMA)
might not be the best tool to identify the modal parameters. As an alterna-
tive Operational modal analysis(OMA) can be applied without knowledge of
the exact force input.
OMA has mainly been used within civil engineering as an alternative to the
classic forced vibration testing. The development has been driven by the
multiple issues combined with applying experimental modal analysis to large
civil engineering structures. The main beneﬁt of the OMA techniques is that
instead of inducing a forced vibration by means of eg. shakers, static dis-
placement or controlled explosions, the ambient vibrations from for example.
wind, water or traﬃc are used to obtain the response signal.
Within the ﬁeld of mechanical engineering the interest for the OMA tech-
niques has increased as they provide a non-invasive alternative to the EMA
techniques. Many diﬀerent OMA techniques have been developed in both
time and frequency domain, and some utilizes a combination of methods.
Taking machinery out of a production line is costly and by avoiding it
the expenses of making modal analysis on industrial machines can be low-
ered signiﬁcantly. In the long term, if the identiﬁcation schemes are accurate
enough, regular analysis will allow for structural health monitoring of rota-

Chapter 2
Theory
2.1
Modal Analysis
The development within both mechanical and civil engineering is resulting
in a rising demand of strong light weight constructions with increasing com-
plexity. As the complexity of the structures increase so does the need to
characterize their dynamic behaviour. The discipline of characterizing the
modes characterising a dynamic system is called Modal Analysis. In theory a
system has an inﬁnite number of modes each with a corresponding frequency,
damping ratio and mode shape. These are called the modal parameters.
Several diﬀerent analysis methods exist in both time and frequency do-
main. The time domain identiﬁcation, analyses the free decays of a system for
example the classical response to an impuls-hammer. The general idea of the
Frequency domain techniques is that the response to continuous excitation
will be a summation of the exited modes. Most techniques use Fourier trans-
formation to decompose the signal into a frequency spectrum from which the
modal parameters can be extracted.
2.2

in high frequency components not related to the original signal [7]. If the
high frequency components are higher than the Nyquist frequency they will

2.3
Operational Modal Analysis (OMA)
As earlier mentioned the classical EMA techniques requires the system in
question to be taken out of service while the experiments are performed,
making the modal analysis a costly operation. Using OMA, the experiments
can be performed by exiting the system through actively controlled bearings
while the system is in service. The main reason to use OMA is that it does
not need any measure of the input force to estimate the modal parameters.
They do however rely on several assumption about the input force for the
theory to be applicable.
The general assumptions in the OMA theory:
• Input force has white noise characteristics, i.e an even energy dis-
tributed in the frequency range of interest.[2]
• The input forces are uncorrelated
• The forces are randomly distributed over the entire structure [10]
• The system is time invariant and the response is stationary

• The mode shapes are geometric orthogonal
Furthermore the system has to be lowly damped to ensure that the cor-

• Ibrahim Time domain (ITD)

Chapter 6
Rotor system analysis
The rotor experiment is the main focus of the project. The goal is to be able
to obtain the modal parameters of the rotor system using OMA and evaluate
the accuracy of the OMA methods compared to EMA results.
6.1
Experimental Conduct and Signal Pro-
cessing
Two diﬀerent rotor conﬁgurations have been analysed during the project:
The light disc conﬁguration and the two disc conﬁguration. Three diﬀerent
experiments was run for each disc conﬁguration.
Low pressure experiment where only the hydro dynamic lubrication is
used and the system is excited with the shaker.
High pressure (shaker) experiment where hybrid lubrication with 2 bar
hydrodynamic lubrication and 60 bar hydrostatic lubrication are used
in the bearing while the system is excited with the shaker.
High pressure (ALB) experiment where hybrid lubrication with both 2
bar hydrodynamic lubrication and 60 bar hydrostatic lubrication are
used in the bearing while the system is excited with the servovalves
through the ALB.
Each of the experiments was conducted with both white noise and chirp.
In the High pressure (ALB) experiment the servovalves signals are adjusted
around a DC level of 2.35 for servovalve 1 and 2.30 for servovalve 2 resulting
in equal ﬂow from both pads. In all the rotor experiments the rotor have
been run at 1000 rpm for a duration of 600 seconds with a sampling rate of
3000 Hz.
52
==PAGE:59==
Master Thesis
Chapter 6. Rotor system analysis
With each conﬁguration it is important to let the temperature reach its
equilibrium, because the oils viscosity is reduced as the temperature rises.
When the oil viscosity is reduced, it changes the damping and stiﬀness of
the ﬂuid ﬁlm in the bearing. As this makes the system time varying it will
violate one of the basic assumption. Aconvergence study of the temperature
can be found in appendix B along with a discussion of the inﬂuence on the
measurements.
6.1.1
Preprocessing
The pure signal from the displacement sensors will never show the exact
displacement, as several sources of pollution is present in the signal;
Mechanical run out: Errors due to the surface roughness of the shaft.
Electronic run out: Errors due to material impurities altering electronic
properties of the material.
To compensate for these errors the run out is measured before the experi-
ments. The measurements are taken with a sampling frequecy of 6000 Hz,
and with a angular velocity of around 40 rpm to get a high resolution of the
shaft surface. By pairing the data with the angular position of the shaft, a
reference data base is created and used to subtract the run out value.
6.1.2
Postprocessing
The data obtained with the displacement sensors shows several harmonic
multiplicities of the rotor velocity. Therefore a comb-notch ﬁlter is applied
to the measurement data to assist the OMA algorithms. When appliyng a
ﬁlter to data used in OMA, it is important to apply the same ﬁlter to all
signals [2].

6.2.4
Summary
It can be seen that in the three diﬀerent experiments with the one disc con-
ﬁguration, the EMA results are very consistent. Both the identiﬁed natural
frequencies and damping ratios are fairly close even with excitation through
the bearing. Overall the OMA algorithms returns fairly good frequency es-
timates and are for the most part within 4 Hz of the EMA results. The
estimated damping ratios are however not as good. The ITD identiﬁcation
returns the estimates closest to the EMA results, but does still not seem very
consistent. The SSI method generally returns very high damping estimates
often more than twice of the EMA result. The FDD, on the other hand,
generally estimates low damping ratios; less than 50 % of the EMA result.
The mode shapes obtained by the OMA algorithms are fairly consistent and
generally matched the EMA mode shape with a few exceptions. The OMA
algorithms perform a bit worse in the experiment with ALB excitation, prob-
ably because the system is not excited as well.

The EMA results in the three experiments with two discs show that the sys-

quencies only changes around 1 Hz, the damping increases from around 0.06
to 0.1 with high pressure and 0.085 with excitation through the servovalves.

The OMA algorithms performs very well in the experiments with shaker ex-
citation where the frequency estimates generally are within 2 Hz of the EMA
results. In the experiment with excitation through the ALB the algorithms
does not perform as well, and especially the time domain methods returns
low frequencies using white noise. The damping estimates are generally not
very good and shows the same tendencies as in the 1 disc experiments. The
ITD algorithm return quite accurate damping estimates in the low pressure
experiment, but does not perform convincingly with high pressure. The SSI
algorithm still obtains large damping estimates in the same magnitude as
with the 1 disc conﬁguration. The FDD estimates seems reasonable in the
low pressure experiment, but with high pressure they are less than half of
the EMA results. The inaccuracy of the dampening estimates could be be-
cause of insuﬃcient sampling time. However, according to Chauhan et al.
[4], it is a common problem with experimental OMA identiﬁcation and they
conclude that the main reason for the error of the damping estimates is vi-
olation of the assumptions used in the theory. The mode shapes obtained
with OMA are quite consistent with horizontal excitation and agree well with
the EMA result. However, abnormal mode shapes occur more often in the 2
disc experiments with vertical excitation, and especially the FDD algorithm
has problems identifying the correct mode shape. The OMA algorithms did
not perform as well with ALB excitation as in the previous experiments,
but again it might be due to the system not being properly excited by the
servovalves. This is discussed further in section 6.4.

As it can be seen in the FRF comparison (ﬁgure 6.1, 6.2, 6.15 and 6.16) the
system changes signiﬁcantly when the ALB is used for excitation. The large
ampliﬁcation below 75 Hz is believed to be caused by the excitation through
the bottom pads disturbing the ﬂuid ﬁlm. Furthermore the natural frequency
and damping ratio is increased because the high pressure lubrication adds
stiﬀness and dampening to the bearing. In this light the ALB excitation can
not be considered non-invasive. To reduce the inﬂuence from the servovalve
excitation, an experiment was conducted where the DC level send to the
servovalves was adjusted gradually switching the excitation ﬂow to be solely
from the top pads. By exciting in this way, the oil ﬁlm at the bottom pads
are left undisturbed, while the system is excited from the top pads only.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The main objective of this project was to evaluate the Operational modal
analysis methods applicability in dynamic rotor systems. The theory of the
EMA method and three diﬀerent OMA methods, namely ITD, SSI and FDD,
has been covered. A simulation has been used to perform a parameter study
of the user inputs to the OMA algorithms in order to get an understanding
of their inﬂuence on the estimated modal parameters.
The identiﬁcation
methods has been applied on data from an initial experiment with a simpliﬁed
windmill to gain experience with the algorithms.
Foundation
It was found that the ﬂexible rotor system was coupled with the dynamics of
the foundation on which it is mounted. The foundation was analysed with
both EMA and OMA to survey the implications of the coupling. Six distinct
natural frequencies was identiﬁed using EMA and their mode shapes has been
shown. The OMA methods in time domain was only capable of identifying a
few of the natural frequencies whereas the FDD algorithm identiﬁed frequen-
cies corresponding to the ones found with EMA. none of the OMA methods
succeeded in estimating meaningful mode shapes. Dampening plated was
installed between the concrete ﬂoor and the steel foundation to resolve the
issue. The analysis showed that by installing the damping plates the con-
strains induced by the contact between the foundation and the concrete ﬂoor
was removed making it act as a free plate, and strengthened the coupling.
It was shown that with the dampening plates ﬁve natural frequencies were
excited with horizontal excitation, and six with vertical excitation.

Chapter 8. Conclusion
Rotor
The modal parameters has been identiﬁed experimentally for the rotor set up
with two diﬀerent ﬂywheel conﬁgurations. It has been shown that the OMA
identiﬁcation methods are capable of obtaining good estimates of the natural
frequencies and mode shapes. The damping ratio estimates obtained with
OMA was generally inaccurate. The ITD method obtained the best damping
estimates but was still found to be inaccurate. The estimates from the SSI
algorithm was generally very high, whereas the FDD obtain damping ratios
of low values. It was found that the OMA methods performed worse with
servovalve excitation, but the estimates was still reasonable in most cases.
The servovalve excitation using both pads was found to change the system,
but by exciting through the top pads only the inﬂuence can be reduced.
8.1
Further Work
8.1.1
OMA
The OMA damping estimation has shown to be unreliable in the mechanical
applications throughout the project. Therefore it is necessary to examine
the cause for the uncertainties in the estimation. It is believed that better
estimates can be obtained by increasing the sampling time. Therefore an
experiment should be performed to investigate if the damping estimates will
converge towards the EMA results when the timeseries is increased. Further-
more the system should be analysed with OMA using top pad only excitation.
this could be done for diﬀerent pressures and servovalve gains to ﬁnd the least
invasive conﬁguration. An uncertainty estimation should be made to evalu-
ate the stability of the modal parameter estimates obained with OMA. The
uncertainty estimation should be done by repeating the experiments and
identiﬁcation process and calculate the empirical uncertainty.
8.1.2
Rotor set up
Regarding the rotor set up, several improvement could be done to improve
the quality of experiments. As thoroughly discussed, the rotor system and
its foundation are strongly coupled. To solve this issue the rotor will have to
be installed on another foundation of suitable dimensions. The pump driving
the high pressure oil ﬂow does not keep the oil pressure constant. The general
pressure level is correct but approximately every 20 second the pump makes
a strange sound and the pressure drops 20 bar. As this can be seen on all

position in the bearing especially in vertical direction. a convergence study
was performed with 60 bar high pressure lubrication and an angular velocity
