Abstract
Worldwide affluence growth is continuously increasing resource use and pollutant emissions at a rate
exceeding reductions achieved through technological development. The majority of environmental impacts
are caused by the most affluent citizens, all while a large share of the global population still lack adequate
living conditions.
This paper explores the possibility of fulfilling material requirements for Decent
Living Standards (DLS) for the entire global population while keeping environmental impacts below
planetary boundary thresholds. A hypothetical sufficiency lifestyle adhering to activity levels set by the
DLS framework was created assuming current state-of-the-art technology. Utilizing an Environmentally
Extended Input-Output (EEIO) model hybrid, EXIOBASE v3.3.18, consumption-based impacts from
the DLS scenario were calculated and compared to LCA-based planetary boundary thresholds adapted
to EF3.1 metrics. The IPAT equation was used to investigate how changes to population (P), affluence
(A) and technology (T) factors may influence the environmental impacts of the DLS scenario. Results
show that the DLS scenario exceeds planetary boundaries for 6 out of 14 impact categories; ”Climate
Change”, ”Freshwater Eutrophication”, ”Water Use”, ”Particulate Matter”, ”Resource Use, fossil” and
”Resource Use, Minerals and Metals”. Impact categories ”Ionizing radiation” and ”Ozone Depletion ”
could not be quantified due to limited elementary flows in the EEIO model. Shifting to a vegetarian
diet, a fossil-free energy system and implementing large scale changes to agricultural practices greatly
reduces the environmental impacts of the DLS scenario. However, affluence and technology changes were,
in this case, not enough to reduce impacts to levels within all planetary boundary thresholds. Impacts
to ”Climate Change”, ”Water Use”, ”Particulate Matter” and ”Resource Use, Minerals and Metals”
remained above planetary boundary thresholds after modification to affluence and technology. The study
highlights the necessity for significant behavioral changes and radical technological advancement to ensure
human well-being globally while staying within the ecological limits of the planet. Results are affected by
uncertainties stemming from translation of specific materials requirements to aggregated product groups,
translation of products of physical flows to monetary units and limitations of the LCIA method due to
limited emission flows. Improved translation of material requirements to input-output fitting data, further
scenario testing on technology improvements and inclusion of more elementary flows to the IO-model is
recommended to strengthen the robustness of these conclusions.

Introduction
Throughout the last 11,700 years, planet Earth has experienced an unusually stable climate, giving rise to
the establishment of human civilizations, rapid population proliferation and increasing affluence. Since the
beginning of the industrial revolution, increasing production and consumption of products and services
has caused a steadily increasing pressure on the environment at a rate exceeding reductions achieved
through technological development [Steffen et al., 2015a]; [Wiedmann et al., 2020]. Humanity is facing a
great paradox, with technological and industrial growth destroying nature and threatening the existence
of both ourselves and our descendants, but at the same time, further advancement of technologies may
be necessary to stop the continuing destruction of nature and ensure our continuing existence [AtKisson,
2006]. The concept of ‘sustainability’ is an emerging societal goal with increasing attention, which in
scientific terms means to be in a state where a system can continue indefinitely. In a societal sense,
sustainability is the goal of having a society where humans can safely co-exist on Earth for a long time,
achieved by maintaining ecological, social, and economic imperatives. The term, however, does not have
an agreed-upon definition, with the most quoted definition stemming from the Brundtland Commission
of 1987, which states that:
”Sustainable development is development that meets the needs of the present without compromising
the ability of future generations to meet their own needs” [WCED, 1987]
In 2015, the UN created the ”2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development” to work towards protection
of the planet while at the same time ensuring the well-being and rights for everyone [United Nations,
2015]. The agenda, and its 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), was adopted by all UN member
states with the goal to ensure peace and prosperity for the people and planet. However, evidence suggests
that the 2030 Agenda has had limited political impact so far ([Biermann et al., 2022]). A recent report
published by the UN states that the SDGs are currently off track and that previous efforts to eliminate
hunger, poverty and reduce inequalities have stalled or regressed [United Nations, 2023]. Global hunger
levels have surged to levels not witnessed since 2005 and, if current trends persists, 575 million people
will continue to live in extreme poverty in 2030. In contrast, the wealthiest 10% of population account
for 25-43% of overall environmental pressure to the planet [Wiedmann et al., 2020].
”Unless we act now, the 2030 Agenda will become an epitaph for a world that might have
been.”
- Ant´onio Guterres, Secretary-General, United Nations [United Nations, 2015]
1
==PAGE:8==
As growing research indicates, a continuing functioning of Earth’s systems, such as the atmosphere, oceans,
forests, biogeochemical flows and biodiversity, is imperative for a prospering global society [Griggs et al.,
2013]. Therefore, sustainable development should be seen as a nested concept, where safeguarding of the
Earth’s system processes is a prerequisite not only for environmental sustainability but also for social
and economic sustainability. There is currently a significant possibility that we are at risk of affecting
the stability of the Earth’s systems and triggering abrupt environmental changes to the environment
[Rockstr¨om et al., 2009]. The Planetary Boundaries (PB) framework, which was developed by [Rockstr¨om
et al., 2009] and later updated ([Steffen et al., 2015b]), was established to prevent the destabilization of
Earth system processes and define a ”safe operating space” (SOS) for humanity to stay within with respect
to the planet’s biophysical and geophysical processes. The framework introduces and quantifies limits
for 9 system processes, for which it is deemed necessary to define planetary boundaries: climate change,
biosphere integrity change, interference with biogeochemical flows (N and P), stratospheric ozone depletion,
ocean acidification, freshwater use, land-system change, atmospheric aerosol loading and novel entities (or
chemical pollution) [Steffen et al., 2015b]. Thresholds were defined for each system process as the amount
of pressure where we are at risk of triggering unacceptable changes. From this, PBs were established as an
amount of pressure where we are still well below these thresholds but are exceeding the SOS which should
not be transgressed to ensure we are not causing unacceptable and irreversible environmental change
([Rockstr¨om et al., 2009]; [Steffen et al., 2015b]). In terms of environmental sustainability, 6 out of of
9 PBs have been exceeded according to the newest assessment of the planetary boundaries [Richardson
et al., 2023].
In the now well-known Doughnut Framework by [Raworth, 2012], the PB framework were combined
with the concept of social boundaries, with social sustainability encompassing social justice, equity and
overall well-being. The Doughnut illustrates ”a safe and just operating space” for humanity, between
a social foundation preventing severe human deprivations and an environmental ceiling preventing the
breaching of nature’s critical thresholds, with the goal of ensuring both human and planetary well-being.
However, social sustainability is a notion shaped by socio-cultural factors and it is therefore difficult
to agree on a common definition and quantification [V´elez-Henao and Pauliuk, 2023].
The issue of
combining social and planetary well-being is further complicated by the fact the social and environmental
well-being are interdependent [Raworth, 2012]. Multiple studies have been conducted in recent years
proposing basic access levels needed for social sustainability. In a study from 2018, Rao and Min defined
a set of universally needed material requirements to fulfill basic human needs and ensure well-being
[Rao and Min, 2018]. These basic access levels, referred to as Decent Living Standards (DLS), includes
2
==PAGE:9==
the need for adequate nutrition, shelter, thermal comfort, clothing, hygiene, and mobility as well as
access to education, healthcare and means of communication [Rao and Min, 2018]. Others have further
elaborated on this work, estimating DLS energy requirements ([Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a]), material
consumption ([V´elez-Henao and Pauliuk, 2023]) and comparing global decent living requirements to
planetary boundary thresholds ([Schor, 2023]; [Schlesier et al., 2024]. All above-mentioned studies follow a
bottom-up modelling approach, or process-based LCA, which provides extensive detail and understanding
at product level, but suffers from the limitation of a cut-off boundary, thereby not accounting for a
fraction of underlying processes. This is because it is not practically feasible to include all unit processes
needed to provide the reference flow and therefore, not all background processes to the system can be
accounted for [Bjørn et al., 2018]. Moreover, process-based LCA typically focuses on physical products
and processes and often omits intangible services, such as maintenance, education, healthcare or social
activities, due to a lack of intangible services in LCA databases. These services can in some cases have
significant impacts [Mattila, 2018]. Environmentally extended input-output models (EEIO) have gained
increasing interest in recent years due to its complete coverage of the economy allowing for extensive
large-scale analyses. EEIO are based on regional or national accounts describing economic activities and
enables the opportunity to assess environmental impacts of products and services within our economy
without the limitation of a cut-off boundary [Majeau-Bettez et al., 2011]. EEIO includes indirect processes
and feedback relationships between processes and sectors, ensuring reasonable model completeness with
limited effort. In return, EEIO have other notable downsides such as a coarse product granularity of
aggregated product categories causing model simplification and low technological detail. EEIO assumes
fixed technological and trade coefficients for a certain year, making the information static and thereby
not accounting for future technological improvements and changes to trade relationships [Lef`evre, 2023].
Furthermore, since the data collection and harmonising of IO data take a large amount of time, the IO
data is often a few years old. Lastly, EEIO contains a limited number of elementary flows, which can
cause an underestimation of impacts, especially regarding toxicity and human health.
A study by conducted by Schor concludes that material requirements needed to fulfill DLS globally are not
absolute sustainable given present technological levels and demographic conditions and to reduce impacts
of globally available Decent Living Standards to a level within the planetary boundaries, technological
advancements are necessary [Schor, 2023]. Technology is one of three factors in the well-known IPAT
equation, which explains human’s impact on the environment as the product of population size (P),
affluence (A), and technology efficiency (T) in a brief manner (Equation 1). The modelling of a global DLS
scenario given the current population (P) and technology level (T) represents a complete reformulation
3
==PAGE:10==
of the Affluence factor (A) in the IPAT equation, given unchanged P and T.
Impact (I) = Population (P) · Affluence (A) · Technology (T)
(1)
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate the potential environmental impacts from a hypothetical global
DLS scenario in comparison to the planetary boundary thresholds using an environmentally extended
multi-regional input-output model (EE-MRIO). Constructing a new final demand vector based on DLS
material requirements to represent a world adhering to a sufficiency lifestyle is a novel approach that will
be explored and evaluated. Moreover, it will be investigated how changes to P, A and T in the IPAT
equation could affect environmental impacts from the global decent living scenario.

2
Methods
This section outlines the methods used to carry out a comparison of environmental impacts from a global
decent living scenario with global planetary boundaries and investigate the changes in environmental
impact from modifying population, affluence and technology levels. Supplementary documentation to the
steps taken is included in SI 6. Overall, the main steps taken are:
• Constructing a global decent living standards scenario in hybrid EXIOBASE
• Implementing LCIA in EXIOBASE
• Compare impacts from sufficiency lifestyle scenario to planetary boundary thresholds
• Investigate impact change from modifying population, affluence and technology levels
2.1
Study design overview
This study takes basis in the proposed inventory of material requirements needed for the DLS scenario,
as explained in [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a] and also takes inspiration from changes to the DLS
scenario made by [Schor, 2023]. The DLS scenario is transferred to an input-output (IO) model using
Python, namely hybrid EXIOBASE, by converting the DLS activity levels to fit aggregated product
groups in EXIOBASE. Each DLS activity segment is modelled in Excel and Python is used to create a
final demand vector including all activity segments and to calculate the consumption-based impacts from
the scenario. The environmental impacts are calculated from the elementary flows using characterization
factors (CF) following the European Environmental Footprint (EF) LCIA method using CFs from the
EF 3.1 version. Finally, the impacts are compared to the planetary boundaries by creating sustainability
ratios of impacts compared to the planetary boundaries adapted to EF3.1 metrics. The before-mentioned
steps will be further elaborated in the sections below.
2.2
Model environment - environmentally extended input-output model (EEIO)
Consumption based impacts from the DLS model is calculated using the EEIO model ”hybrid EXIOBASE
3”(version 3.3.18) [Merciai and Schmidt, 2023], which is processed using Python (version 5.4.2). The term
”hybrid” indicates that the flows of services and products are compiled in mixed units. In contrast to the
regular EXIOBASE 3, where all flows are compiled in monetary units of MEURO (millions of euros), the
hybrid version includes physical flows compiled in mass units, energy flows compiled in terajoules (TJ)
and services compiled in MEURO. The inclusion of physical and energy flows in addition to monetary
5
==PAGE:12==
flows makes it easier to set up a final demand vector based on product data and this is why the hybrid
EXIOBASE 3 is the chosen environment for the model.
The hybrid EXIOBASE 3 IO tables covers
48 regions, including 28 EU member countries, 15 major economies and five rest-of-world regions. The
version contains the same regions as the regular EXIOBASE 3 (28 EU countries, 16 major economies and 5
rest-of-world regions) with the exception of Taiwan, which has been excluded due to lack of data [Merciai
and Schmidt, 2018].
The product classification in EXIOBASE are based on NACE (”Nomenclature
of Economic Activities”) codes, which are statistical classification codes for economic activities in the
European Union (EU) [Eurostat, 1996]. However, as the latest and currently used classification is ”NACE
rev. 2”, it should be noted that EXIOBASE are based on the old classifications from ”NACE rev. 1”.
Moreover, where EXIOBASE includes 200 product codes, hybrid EXIOBASE only includes 164 product
codes, because some products groups have been further aggregated (namely petroleum products and
chemicals).
EEIO models are based on multi-regional output models (MRIO) which is founded on the principle that
the total output of an economic sector is equal to the sum of the demand for that sector’s output from
other sectors and the final demand. The fundamental equation for computing the total output x in an
MRIO analysis is given by:
x = (I – A)–1 y
(2)
where x represents the gross output matrix of both upstream output and final output with dimensions
mn×m, where m is the number of countries and n is the number of sectors within each country [Mattila,
2017]. The term (I – A)–1 is referred to as the Leontief inverse, comprising the identity matrix I and the
direct requirements matrix A, both having dimensions of mn × mn. y represents the final demand vector.
EEIO is further extended with environmental impacts:
d = S(I – A)–1 y
(3)
where, S is a matrix with direct environmental impact coefficients per product, and d is the resulting
inventory of emissions to the environment from the system.
2.3
Development of a global sufficiency scenario
The baseline scenario is based on DLS’s proposed activity levels as quantified in 2020 by [Millward-
Hopkins et al., 2020a] adopted and modified from [Rao and Min, 2018]. Millward Hopkins et al. proposes

Shelter and living conditions
The shelter and living conditions segment includes the need for living space, thermal comfort, furniture
for housing and illumination.
The sufficient living space is modelled to reflect a consistent annual rate of dwelling construction. Sufficient
living space is assumed by Millward-Hopkins to be 20 m2 of functional space with an additional 10 m2 per
person and assuming a household size of 4.9 cap/household [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a]; [Statista,
2022]. This equals 14.08 m2 per person of housing space. The shelter construction is modelled with the
EXIOBASE category ”Construction work (45)” given in monetary units and the shelter space in converted
to monetary units based on a construction price of 1500 e/m2, which is based on a average construction
10
==PAGE:17==
price from multiple sources [Turner & Townsend, 2023]; [Costmodelling, 2024]. Because of the coarse
product structure of EXIOBASE, it is not differentiated between urban and rural areas, nor between
single-family housing (SFH) and multi-family housing (MFH) for the shelter construction segment. This
is a simplification of Millward-Hopkins et al.’s DLS model as a consequence of the coarse EXIOBASE
product aggregation [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a].
Thermal comfort is modelled as the electricity needed to keep the indoor temperature to a comfortable
temperature. The thermal comfort interval is between 15.6 ℃and 21.1 ℃[Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a]
where the shelter space is either heated or cooled to stay within this interval. The modelling approach
follows [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a], basing the thermal heating demand per country to the number
of heating degree days (HDD) and cooling degree days (CDD) per years calculated by [Atalla et al., 2018].
Thereafter energy demands for heating and cooling are calculated per country by [Millward-Hopkins et al.,
2020a] in MJ/m2/yr based on data from [GBPN, 2012]. Based on the average energy requirements per
country for both urban and rural areas, and population share living in urban and rural areas, a total
energy use per country is calculated. The energy use is modelled in EXIOBASE as electricity distributed
between electricity sources following each country’s current electricity mix. The electricity distribution
will be further elaborated in section 2.3.2.1.
A sufficient amount of furniture per household is assumed by Millward-Hopkins to be 1000 kg, all
with a lifetime of 25 years [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a]. Furniture is modelled using the product
code ”Furniture, other manufactured goods n.e.c.
(36)”, which includes the manufacturing of a mix
of all furniture types and related product with the exception of stone, concrete and ceramic [Eurostat,
2008].
Illumination of the shelter is modelled following the same assumption as [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020b],
that 33 % of the floor space assigned per person is illuminated for 6 hours each day every year. The
energy use is calculated assuming state-of-the-art LED lighting following the same approach as [Millward-
Hopkins et al., 2020a] who have used values provided by [Cullen et al., 2011] and [de Monasterio, 2012].
The resulting electricity use is ∼34 MJ/cap/year and is the same for all regions.

Clothing
The clothing segment comprises the amount of clothing necessary to maintain a comfortable thermal
temperature both indoors and outdoors and well as regular washing of clothes. The clothing segment
thus includes clothing materials plus water, heat and the production of a washing machine. The amount
of clothes needed to satisfy the DLS is set 3.03 kg/cap/yr and is modelled with the product code
”Textiles (17)” [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020b]. The necessary washing of clothes according to DLS
is 78 kg/cap/year [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a]. The washing is modelled as the production of one
75 kg washing machine per household with a lifetime of 20 years plus water and electricity needed for
this amount of washing. The water and electricity use needed for washing is 13 L water/kg clothes and
0.15 kWh/kg clothes [Safaei, 2013]. The water is converted to monetary units using a conversion factor
of 0.00884 e/L water [Fischer, 2022].

Education and Healthcare
The education and healthcare segment is modelled as the construction of education and healthcare facilities
and the energy use needed for operation of the facilities. The need for education space is assumed to be
10 m2 of educational space for the share of the population between ages 5-13 years per country, following
the approach of [Schor, 2023]. The need for healthcare space is set to 1.6 m2/cap assuming 8 beds for
1,000 capita, as proposed by [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a]. In EXIOBASE, the demand of healthcare
and education is modelled as the construction of these healthcare and educational facilities as well as the
energy requirements for daily education and healthcare activities.
Communication and Information
The communication and information segment expresses the right to participation in society by decent
access to information and communication [Rao and Min, 2018]. The DLS scenario assumes that the need
for communication and information can be satisfied by one computer per household and one phone per
person from 10 years and above [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a]. The communication and information
segment is modelled by including the manufacturing of one computer with a weight of 2.04 kg and a lifetime
of 10 years per households and one phone with a weight of 365.5 g and a lifetime of 5 years per person over
10 years. The computer manufacturing is modelled with the product code ”Computer and related services
(72)” and phone manufacturing with the product code ”Radio, television and communication equipment
and apparatus (32)”. Moreover, the electricity use is modelled, both from the equipment use and for the
electricity used by the network and data centres required for communication and data storage following
the same approach as [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a] and using energy values taken from literature
([Deng et al., 2011],[Ercan et al., 2016];[Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a]).

Implementing distribution of electricity sources by country
Because of the high technological resolution for electricity in EXIOBASE; where the consumption is
distributed between 12 types of electricity sources, the electricity consumption of the DLS scenario is
distributed between the electricity technologies in the same proportions as the actual demand in 2011.
The electricity technology proportions are varied between the countries because there is a large global
variation of electricity technology use across the world. The electricity use per country is found from
the sum of all demand categories (matrix Y) in hybrid EXIOBASE and the electricity percentages per
country are calculated as the amount of one electricity type divided by the sum of all electricity types

2.4
Life Cycle Impact Assessment
An LCIA is an evaluation of the environmental impacts of the modelled system, which is evaluated by
translating elementary flows from the life cycle inventory into corresponding impacts on the environment
from these elementary flows. Many different LCIA methods exist, however, where the regular EXIOBASE
has a range of potential LCIA methods already implemented, none are included in the hybrid EXIOBASE
version. The environmental extension in the hybrid version of EXIOBASE only provides environmental
impacts as elementary flows of emissions to the environment and does not provide LCIA methods to
translate the emission flows to environmental impacts. To calculate potential environmental impacts, the
EF LCIA method is implemented following the approach described by [Beylot et al., 2019]. The EF LCIA
method considers the following 16 impact categories: ”Climate change (CC)”, ”Ozone depletion (ODP)”,
”Eutrophication marine (MEU), ”Eutrophication, freshwater (FEU)”, ”Eutrophication, terrestrial (TEU)”,
”Acidification (AC)”, ”Land use (LU)”,”Water use (WU)”, ”Freshwater ecotoxicity (ECOTOX)”, ”Particulate
Matter (PM)”, ”Photochemical ozone formation (POF)”, ”Ionising radiation (IR) ”, ”Human toxicity,
cancer (HTOXc)”, ”Human toxicity, non-cancer (HTOXnc)”, ”Resource use, fossils (FRD)” and ”Resource
use, minerals and metals (MRD)” [European Commission, 2021]. However, IR and ODP were excluded
from this study due to lack of elementary flows related to IR and few substances related to ODP [Beylot
et al., 2019]; [Merciai and Schmidt, 2018]. Moreover, there are a very limited amount of elementary flows
in EXIOBASE for the impact categories ECOTOX, HTOXc and HTOXnc compared to the large amount
of substances normally assigned by the EF method to these impacts categories. These categories should
15
==PAGE:22==
therefore be viewed with caution. The approach assigns a characterisation factor (CF) to all EXIOBASE
elementary flows by systematic classification, relating them to relevant impact categories, where the CFs
are taken from the EF3.1 reference package ([European Commission, 2024];[Bassi et al., 2023]).
The
linking of EXIOBASE flows to the EF 3.1 categories follow the same approach as used in [Beylot et al.,
2019], of which data has been adapted and modified (for more information see SI 6.5. Characterization
factors (CF) are assigned to all EXIOBASE elementary flows with linkage to the 14 impact categories
and constructed in a matrix ”c”. The environmental impacts are calculated with the following matrix
calculation:
e = C · g
(7)
where C is the CF matrix and g is the inventory of emissions to the environment. The final characterisation
factors per impact category is seen in SI 6.5 and the full matrix can be seen in Excel in SI 9.3.
2.5
Comparing impact with Planetary Boundaries
The consumption-based impacts of a DLS scenario is quantified using MR-EEIO and using this method,
the scenario can be compared against the impacts of the current world (as modelled in EXIOBASE (for
the year 2011). However, it can only be concluded if the DLS scenario is better than the current world
and not whether or not the scenario is sustainable in absolute terms. To do this, the environmental
burdens of the scenario are assessed against environmental tolerance limits to how much pressure nature’s
systems processes can withstand before we are at risk of triggering unacceptable environmental changes
[Rockstr¨om et al., 2009]. The DLS scenario has a global scale and can thus be directly compared to
PBs.
However, the PBs do not directly link with the 16 impact categories implemented with the EF LCIA
method. The main focus of the EF LCIA method is not on assessing global system processes, but on
the performance assessment of products and services in relative terms. A previous study by Sala et al.
connects the planetary boundaries to the 16 EF impact categories and developed LCA-based PBs fitted
to the EF3.0 method [Sala et al., 2020]. To compare the environmental impacts quantified with the EF
LCIA method, these LCA-based PBs adapted to the EF LCIA method’s model and indicators are used.
These PBs are directly linked to the EF3.0 impact categories, however, the land use unit had been changed
between EF3.0 and EF3.1. The land use PB is therefore adjusted from the previously used unit ”kg soils
loss” to fit the new unit ”Pt” which is an index based on soil erosion and additional three soil properties
[JRC, 2024];[De Laurentiis et al., 2019]. The LCA-based PB thresholds, are seen in Table 3 below and are

2.6.2
Changing affluence
The affluence factor of the I-PAT equation represents the fact that a higher global affluence yields higher
global impacts on the environment from increased consumption of products and services.
The DLS
framework first presented by [Rao and Baer, 2012] is a quantification of what access levels should be a
minimum right for all in order to ensure decent living conditions. Therefore, it would not be possible to
analyze the effects of the lowering of affluence levels below the DLS scenario. However, many countries
already enjoy access levels well above the DLS scenario, with [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a] finding that
100 out of 119 studied countries already have an energy consumption higher than the DLS.
To reflect the social differences that naturally occur within a society, uneven societal affluence levels is
implemented equally within all countries. Inequality is implemented by incorporating uneven consumption
levels, allowing part of the population to have more than the established DLS activity levels.
The
modelling of inequality takes inspiration from the approach described in [Millward-Hopkins, 2022a] and
uses the relative consumption pattern of an ideal distribution for ”fair” inequality shown in [Millward-
Hopkins, 2022b] which follows income ratios reported by [Kiatpongsan and Norton, 2014]. The relative
consumption pattern is shown in Figure 1 below, representing the distribution of consumption relative the
the DLS consumption level between the poorest (0%) and richest (100%), with the highest consumption
being five times higher than the DLS activity levels. By implementing the consumption pattern seen
in Figure 1, the average consumption level will be 2.34 times highers than the DLS scenario.
It is
not differentiated in affluence levels between countries, by giving already rich countries higher activity
levels.

change with an increased meat share displacing plant-based foods. Because the food consumption amount
remains the the same, so does the need for heating of food, cold storage and water use. Next, housing
space is increased, as this is one of the most probable consumption activities to increase with increasing
affluence. When the housing space increases, so does the need for illumination and thermal heating as
well. The amount of furniture is not changed, because it is argued that while it may be needed extra
furniture for large housing space, it should still be sufficient with the same amount of large furniture such

and heating. For the mobility the approach is based on the assumption by [Millward-Hopkins, 2022a],

clothing segment, the amount of clothing is increased but the amount of washing is assumed to remain

all vegetarian or vegan diet would affect the environmental burden from the DLS scenario. A change in
food composition is a change in diet is a behavioural change, and not a change in affluence levels. On the

and more self-regulation of eating-habits due to educational resources and alternative rewards, which is
why a higher obesity level can be seen in population groups of less affluence [Sacco, 2017]. It is therefore

the possibility of behavioural changes to promote vegetarian and vegan diets. To model a vegetarian and

2.6.3
Changing technology
The technology factor (T) represents the impact intensity of consumed goods and services, where improvements
in technology efficiency reduces the impact intensity of the overall DLS scenario.
To improve the
technology factor, changes to technology are performed in several ways, both by changing the final demand
of the DLS scenario, changing the consumption between industries by modifying the inter-industry supply
chains and updating coefficients of impact intensities per unit products. The implementation of technology
improvements is focused on segments of the DLS scenario that contributes with most environmental
impacts. An overview of all technical improvements is seen in Table 7 below and the full Python script
implementing all technology scenarios is seen in SI 9.11. The following paragraphs explain all implemented
technology scenarios.
First, 4 scenarios for improving the production of food products in the nutrition segment is investigated
by reducing or shifting the consumption of goods and services between industries.
This is modelled
by modifying the production recipe’s in the inter-industry (A) matrix of the EEIO model, reducing
input requirements that cause environmental impacts or shifting the inputs to less impacting inputs.
The approach of modifying industry input requirements is adopted from [Wood et al., 2017] where the
technical coefficient columns in the A matrix of size z × z can be modified with the formula:

Investigated improvements within the nutrition sector include shifting to all organic food, shifting to
seasonal fruit and vegetables, purchasing locally produced food and reducing food waste at both production
level and household level. Technology improvements of organic farming, seasonal fruit and vegetables and
local production scenarios are adopted from sufficiency lifestyle scenarios by [Vita et al., 2019]. Shifting
to organic farming entails the removal of fertilizers and chemicals inputs to the production of all food
25
==PAGE:32==
products. Changing to seasonal fruit and vegetables mean that less energy is required for heating of
greenhouses, thereby reducing the energy requirement of food and vegetables in the supply chain. Eating
local food means that less transportation of food products are required to supply the final demand of
products and therefore, inputs of mobility services are reduced for agricultural products. The reduction
rates for organic farming, local purchase and seasonal foods are based on assumed adoption levels assumed
by Vita et al. based on backcasting workshops and expert judgement [Vita et al., 2019]. Next, food waste
is reduced both at production and consumer level. Food waste within the supply chain leads to a reduced
required input of food products to supply the final demand of food products and is modelled by changing
the inter-industry (A) matrix in the same way as the other three improved nutrition scenarios.
The
reduction rate of food products at production level varies for different food products and is based on
current global food losses in the supply chain [Gustavsson et al., 2011]; [Petljak, 2021]. In contrast to the
other technology changes, the reduction of food waste at the consumer level is modelled by reducing the
final demand of food products. Food waste at consumer level is eliminated by reducing the nutritional
demand of all food products included in the final demand of the DLS scenario by 17%, based on estimates
from the United Nation’s Environment Programme [UNEP, 2021]. The reduction is possible because
food waste was included in the DLS scenario and the final calorific content after reducing food waste is
2077 kcal/cap/day, which is still within the food requirement of 2000–2150 kcal/cap/day established by
[Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a].
In addition, one technology improvement scenario is investigated for the DLS shelter segment as well. The
technology improvement entail shifting to natural construction materials. This scenario and substitution
potential of materials is also adopted from Vitaa et al., based on backcasting workshops and expert
judgement [Vita et al., 2019]. Inter-industry inputs to the ”Construction (45)” product of materials such
as cement, lime, plaster, iron and steels are shifted to materials of stone, sand, clay, wood products,
bricks tile and other non-metallic mineral products. Even though this improvement is intended for the
shelter segment, the ”Construction (45)” product is also used for construction of education and healthcare
facilities and consequently also affects the education and healthcare segment.
Next, the electricity distribution used for the DLS scenario segments is changed to originate from
only fossil free electricity sources such as wind, hydro, solar, biomass and nuclear energy. This is not
performed in the same manner as the previous scenarios. This is because it is preferable to maintain
the same overall use of electricity per product, and by following the approach explained in Equation 9,
the reduction by percentage of one electricity source substituted with the increase of another would not

necessarily lead to an equal substitution.
To change the electricity source used for the final demand
of electricity, the electricity distribution between technologies, which was implemented as explained in
2.3.2.1, is first changed to reflect the electricity mix per country in 2023 [Ember, 2024]. Then, a new
electricity technology source distribution containing only fossil free technologies is created per country by
shifting the distribution to all fossil free technologies based on the 2023 per-country distribution between
the fossil free electricity technologies. The electricity distribution for 2023 and the electricity distribution
for the fossil free technology scenario can be seen in SI 9.8. This new per-country electricity distribution
containing only fossil free technologies is used to attribute the electricity demand needed for the DLS

inter-industry matrix A. The python script for changing the inter-industry matrix is seen in SI 9.11.
Lastly, the satellite accounts in hybrid EXIOBASE, which represents the emission intensities per unit of
product, is modified to reflect a reduction in emission intensities from agricultural products due to an
undefined hypothetical technology change. This is done by investigating the emission intensity trends
in EXIOBASE 3 between the years 2011 and 2022. Since hybrid EXIOBASE is only available for the
year 2011, trends are investigated for emission flows using EXIOBASE 3 for which data is provided for
for years 1995 to 2011 and provided through now-casting for the years 2012-2022. Emission flows for 25
agricultural products are first investigated in hybrid EXIOBASE and matched with the corresponding
emission flow in EXIOBASE 3. The emission intensities for years 2011 to 2022 are compared and shows
a decreasing trend but with fluctuations across the years, indicating the need for basing the change on
trend analysis. The extracted satellite data (emission intensities) for years 2011-2001 can be seen in SI
9.9. The average change over time for agricultural emission flows and trend lines can be seen in Figure 12
in SI 6.8. Trend analysis is performed for emission flows disaggregated per product code but averaged
over countries to reduce the amount of data. The linear trend is then continued to the year 2050 while
assuming a maximum emission reduction of 90%. It is also assumed that no emission intensities are
increased. The satellite accounts for the 25 agricultural products in hybrid EXIOBASE are then modified
to reflect this emission intensity reduction by 2050. The overall reductions in emission intensities by 2050
can be seen in SI 9.10.

Combining population, affluence and technology
Finally, the changes to factors P, A and T are combined to model the environmental impacts from
the DLS scenario changes to P (modelling with a 2050 population) changes to A (including consumption

inequality and vegetarian consumption diet) and changes to T (technology scenarios presented in Table 7).
All investigated changes are combined to investigate the environmental impacts of a global decent living
scenario with a population projected for 2050, fair inequality in consumption levels and all investigated
technology changes implemented.
The combination of P, A and T is modelled using the projected
2050 population following medium fertility levels. For changes to A, the vegetarian diet is implemented
together with the activity level changes related to affluence inequality (presented in Table 5). However,

For changes to T, all scenario implementations (presented in
Table 7) are implemented. Lastly, a best case scenario is implemented where population increase, affluence
inequality and the technology scenario Natural Construction Materials is excluded. Population increase
and affluence inequality is disregarded in the best case scenario because it results in higher impacts and
the technology scenario is disregarded because it results in a very high increase to Resource use, minerals
and metals.

4
Discussion
4.1
Comparison of results with other studies
This study finds that adhering to a sufficiency lifestyle alone is not enough to ensure global absolute
sustainability because environmental impacts from providing global decent living requirements lead to
a transgression of 6 out of 14 impact categories. This is in line with previous studies who also report
that Planetary boundaries are transgressed from global sufficiency scenarios without impact reduction
from technology development [Schor, 2023]; [Schlesier et al., 2024]. Schor finds that ”Climate Change”,
”Freshwater Eutrophication”, ”Particulate Matter”, ”Resource Use, fossil”, and ”Resource Use, Minerals
and Metals” exceeds PBs [Schor, 2023], which is in accordance with the results from the present study
(see Figure 24 in SI 8.1). Contrarily, Schor did not find PB for Water Use to be exceeded and instead
found that Land Use and Freshwater Ecotoxicity are exceeded. Schlesier et al., who also investigated the
absolute sustainability of the DLS requirements, considers a different set of impact categories which can
not be directly compared [Schlesier et al., 2024], but reports that the SOS are transgressed for CO2, GWP,
biodiversity and N boundaries following a global sufficiency scenario. However, both studies find that
adhering to decent living standards greatly reduces impacts compared to the current world environmental
pressure. The reduced impact reported by both Schlesier et al. and Schor moving from present world
to a sufficiency lifestyle is not evident from this study. The reasons for this may be several. Firstly, it
may imply that impacts from the DLS scenario have been overestimated. Overestimation of the DLS
scenario could arise from ambiguous translation of specific material requirements to aggregated NACE
product groups, which may contain several products. It may also arise from uncertainty connected with
translating physical flows to set monetary units. On the other hand, it may not be an indication of
overestimation, but the result of all upstream processes being included by use of an IO-model. From the
structural path analysis, it is seen that 1st and 2nd supply processes account for a large share of impacts
within some impact categories (Table 29 in SI 7.2). This study also finds that changing to a strictly
vegetarian global diet, restructuring the agricultural sector and shifting to a completely fossil-free energy
demand is not enough to reduce ”Climate change”, ”Water Use”, ”Particulate matter” and ”Resource
use, minerals and metals” to absolute sustainable limits. Schlesier et al. similarly reports that with a
potential future population of 10.4 billion people, a DLS scenario shifted to fossil-free energy demand does
not reduce all impact categories within the SOS of PBs [Schlesier et al., 2024]. Schlesier et al. also find
in agreement with this study, that the agricultural sector has a much larger contribution to total impacts
in a sufficiency scenario than current world impacts due to a lower reduction in food supply compared to
44
==PAGE:51==
the high reduction in material footprint in a sufficiency scenario [Schlesier et al., 2024]. However, for a
population of 10.4 billion, Schlesier et al. concludes that all impact categories can be complied with at
a 73% probability by further reducing the sufficiency demand to a ”low” sufficiency scenario”, using an
exclusively fossil-free energy provisioning systems, halting cropland expansion and changing material use
construction to wooden buildings. So while absolute sustainability were not reached in the present study,
there may by hope of a decent living scenario within PBs. This low sufficiency scenario, however, implies
a significant reduction of per capita material requirements below the baseline DLS scenario.
4.2
Uncertainties and limitations
4.2.1
Uncertainty related to inventory of DLS scenario
Because the scope of the study entails the entire global population the modelling precision is naturally
crude. While the DLS framework grant the same basic access levels for everyone, climatic variation,
urbanization levels and demographic differences affects the amount of material requirements fulfilling
basic access levels. Basic regional dependencies are accounted for for in many segments but could in other
segments be expanded. For the clothing sector, a standard amount of clothing is assumed across all regions
where arguably, climatic variations should have an affect on the requirements for clothing. However, since
the clothing segment has a small contribution to the overall impacts, changing this assumption is not
expected to have a large impact. Furthermore, it can be argued that some of the model assumptions
established by Millward-Hopkins in the DLS framework favour western consumption behaviour.
An
example of this is the established necessity of one washing machine per household which may incorporate
unnecessary privilege in the DLS framework and which is misconstrued as a necessity in today’s western
societies.
Another is the assumed household size, which by Millward-Hopkins is established to by 4
cap/household and which is increased to 4.9 cap/household in this study. Household size could be more
closely differentiated to local levels to better account for cultural differences, especially because this
parameter concerns the overall shelter segment and availability of appliances specific to the households
in other segments. Yet, it should be thoroughly evaluated whether large household sizes are a result
of a cultural aspects or if it is merely a sign of poverty, for which the household size should not be
differentiated.
Thermal comfort within the shelter segment is modelled with the assumption that all heating is based
on electricity.
This deviates largely from reality, where heating of housing comes from a number of
sources such as electricity, natural gas, district heating, oil and other derivatives. The preferred modelling
45
==PAGE:52==
approach would be to consider all sources of heating and differentiate the heating source according to
current heating patterns. The simplification was performed because many of the EXIOBASE product
groups relevant for heating were represented in monetary units. A conversion from unit of energy (TJ)
to a monetary unit could lead to significant uncertainties since energy prices vary greatly both between
countries and over time. A more satisfying representation of heating using several heating systems would
therefore be time-consuming and future research should improve this modelling approach.
4.2.2
Uncertainty of modelling a DLS scenario using EEIO
The utilization of an IO model proved to come with some complications. One on the greatest difficulties
during the model setup was the translation of specific material requirement to a limited number of
aggregated product groups.
In some cases, this proved to be an advantage, such as for the product
category ”Furniture; other manufactured goods n.e.c. (36)”, which includes furniture of different types
and materials in a ratio that mirrors normal consumption patterns. However, it also includes items that
are not furniture (toys, jewellery) which is consequently modelled as a part of the furniture. The use of
aggregated product groups in the modelling especially results in uncertainties for the mobility segment
where the product groups, given in monetary units, also includes transportation of cargo. This share of the
product group therefore had to be taken out based on external data of passenger vs cargo transportation.
Translation from physical flows to a monetary unit also results in large uncertainties, because it is difficult
to assess whether the monetary values in EXIOBASE can be mirrored using external price data to translate
material requirements. This uncertainty is relevant for all product categories modelled in monetary units
(see Table 1).
Next, the inclusion of direct emissions from the DLS scenario’s demand (Fy) has been very limited in
the present study and has been included only for the mobility segment. This is due to the limited time-
frame of the study, as direct emissions must be modelled as specific emission flows for each activity. The
trade-off in model complexity and uncertainty was evaluated, where it was decided that inclusion of direct
emissions in any case would be restricted to a few substances and that substantial uncertainties would
be inevitable. Nevertheless, direct emissions are included for the mobility segment because impacts from
mobility mostly comes from direct emission during combustion of fuels. However, direct emissions are
also present in other segments, particularly for thermal heating in the shelter segment. If thermal heating
of housing were differentiated between different heating sources, a significant amount of direct emissions
could come from combustion of fuels used for heating (natural gas, coal, oil). Thus, thermal heating
faces uncertainties from both energy source and direct emission exclusion, and impacts from the shelter
46
==PAGE:53==
segment are possibly underestimated. Further development on this study should seek to reevaluate the
modelling of thermal heating.
4.2.3
Limitations to modelling a drastically altered world using EEIO
No previous study has constructed a new alternative final demand vector to replace final demand in an
IO-model. Prior studies conducted investigating sufficiency scenarios are often based on process-based
LCA or energy estimations. As the investigated DLS framework is based on fixed material requirements,
it is not suitable to base the model on present demand, which is why final demand was created on the
basis of DLS materials requirements. Nevertheless, the EXIOBASE data are established from national
accounts which mirrors historical conditions, meaning the tiered supply for any EEIO demand is grounded
in past flows within and between industries. This means that the representation of environmental impacts
from the DLS scenario to a large extent still takes basis in current consumption patterns. In reality, large
scale changes in consumption behaviour may drastically affect production behaviour, changing production
recipes between industries in ways that are difficult to predetermine. For example, a large scale reduction
in electricity use would lead to a re-prioritization of electricity mix where the cheapest electricity is
used. This will most likely favour already established renewable energy because continuing running costs
are essentially free compared to e.g. continuing oil extraction. Yet, renewable electricity technologies
are often spatially bound (hydropower) or climatically variable (wind turbines and photovoltaic panels)
which could restrict the global scale extent of renewable technologies. Potential systemic rebound such
as this, stemming from large scale changes in consumption, is not accounted for in the present study and
it is recommended that subsequent studies investigate this more closely.
4.2.4
Limitations to LCIA Model
The environmental extension of hybrid EXIOBASE poses several limitations influencing the robustness
of the environmental impact assessment. Because of the limited number of elementary flows available in
hybrid EXIOBASE, many impact categories are underestimated to an unknown extent because several
elementary flows, which in reality contributes to the impact categories, are missing from the assessment.
This is especially true for ”Ionizing radiation” and ”Ozone depletion‘”, which could not be adequately
quantified due to missing elementary flows. It is also relevant for Human toxicity (cancerous and non-
cancerous) and Freshwater ecotoxicity which, according to EF 3.1, is affected by 1321-7566 different
substances [Beylot et al., 2019]. For instance, pesticides, insecticides and metal emissions to water, which
influences human toxicity and ecotoxicity, are not included as elementary flows in hybrid EXIOBASE
47
==PAGE:54==
[Crenna et al., 2019]. For a higher specificity of emissions, the regular EXIOBASE 3 could have been
used instead but this in turn would lead to other modelling limitations from the restriction to solely
monetary units.
The robustness of the applied LCIA method is tested by comparing the absolute sustainability of global
environmental pressure documented in EXIOBASE (ref. year 2011) to global environmental pressure
results reported by other studies using the same framework for PB thresholds adapted to the EF metric
[Sala et al., 2020]; [Schor, 2023]. Results on PB exceedance are in alignment with both studies for all
impact categories except freshwater eutrophication and freshwater ecotoxicity (see Table 32 in SI 8). Yet,
comparing the study results with SRs presented by Schor, the SRs from current pressure reported in the
present study are generally lower [Schor, 2023]. This may indicate an underestimation of impacts possibly
stemming from the LCIA implementation. It is recommended to perform additional analysis using several
LCIA methods for increased model confidence.
4.2.5
Considerations on technology implementations
The use of IO as a framework has a number of pros and cons. One of the main pros is that is allows
for a fast assessment of environmental reduction effects from lifestyle options and technology scenarios.
Furthermore, all scenarios can be assessed within one common modelling framework allowing for easier
comparison between scenarios and also enables the possibility of accounting for interaction effects between
scenarios. When multiple scenarios implementations are carried out together, which will be necessary to
meet absolute sustainability levels, these interaction effects can be significant and difficult to capture
without using an integrated modelling approach. However, because of the coarse aggregation of product
groups used in IO models, implementation of scenarios are performed on aggregated product groups and
outcomes from investigating very specific changes are thus limited. Also, the way technological changes
have been implemented does not account for potential long term responses to technology changes and
rebound effects. System rebound effect was intentionally left out of the framework which was used for
modelling technology scenarios to better understanding the direct effects of specific interventions [Wood
et al., 2017].
4.3
Recommendations for stakeholders and future research
Even though a clear decrease in overall impacts could not be found for the DLS scenario it represents a
lowering of environmental impacts from countries with high consumption levels and an increase in impacts
48
==PAGE:55==
from poor developing countries from increased access to basic material requirements. The research shows
that DLS material requirements can be provided to everyone by without strong increases in environmental
impacts compared to current pressure. As the UN 2030 Agenda work towards ensuring well-being and
rights for everyone on the the planet, the DLS scenario constitute improvements to SDGs 1, 2 3, 4, 7, 10,
12 [United Nations, 2015]. This research highlights the importance of adopting and promoting sustainable
consumption patterns to sustain a large global population while staying within the PBs.
The study also investigates lowering of population levels to reduce the environmental impacts and population
growth remains a considerable concern for future well-being. However, the topic of population limits is
often ethically debated and any policy aimed at limiting the population size is considered highly coercive
[UNEP, 2012]. Regardless, the UN DESA states that policies intending ro reduce fertility rates would
have a minimal impact on immediate population growth, as the majority of population growth is driven
by past growth and which is reflected in the young age structure [UN DESA, 2022b]. The population
prospects set forward by the UN should be expected and the investigations of population limits for the
impact categories should only be viewed as indications of our proximity to the PBs.
The study highlights the need for radical technological advances to bring impacts from a global population
adhering to DLS to absolute sustainable levels. The DLS scenario assumes rapid and widespread adoption
of advanced technologies, with assumptions adopted by [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a] built upon
assumptions of state-of-the-art technology.
The investigated scenarios for technology improvements
showed that there are many possibilities for reducing the impact intensities of our consumption pattern and
that the reduction potential is great. The transition to renewable energy sources is imperative for reducing
the environmental pressure and achieving absolute sustainability in the near future and policymakers
should prioritize developing policies to accelerate the transition to renewable energy. Further research on
advancements in agricultural practices and investigate ways to limit food waste is also essential, given the
limitations to reduce food quantities within the DLS material requirements and significant environmental
impacts from food production. Innovative practices such as precision agriculture, sustainable farming
techniques, plant-based diets and food waste limitation initiatives should be explored for their potential
to reduce environmental pressures.

5
Conclusion
This study has sought to assess the absolute sustainability of providing materials needs aligned with the
DLS framework to the entire global population, and how modifications to factors Population (P), Affluence
(A) and Technology (T) of the IPAT equation may affect the environmental pressure from the DLS
scenario. The study finds that meeting DLS material requirements for the entire global population given
current technological conditions is not absolute sustainable. Impacts above planetary boundary thresholds
are seen for ”Climate change”, ”Freshwater eutrophication”, ”Water use”, ”Particulate matter”, ”Resource
use, fossils” and ”Resource use, minerals and metals” and indicates that large scale technological development
is necessary to reduce the pressure from a global DLS scenario to absolute sustainable levels. Compared
to the current pressure for normal product and service demand in 2011, the environmental pressure from
the decent living scenario are lower for impact categories related to ”Human Health” and ”Resource use”
but for impact categories related to ”Climate action, water and terrestrial life protection”, the pressure is
generally higher. Food products contributes to a high share of increased impacts within ”Climate action,
water and terrestrial life protection”, suggesting that providing sufficient nutritional needs for a global
population of 8-10 billion people will be challenging. Modifications to population, affluence and technology
finds that adhering to a vegetarian diet poses a significant potential for impact reductions and, following a
vegetarian diet, all impact categories except ”Water Use” results in impact significantly lower than current
pressure. This shows that changing consumer behaviour has great potential to reduce environmental
impacts. Transitioning to renewable or fossil free energy also greatly reduces the environmental pressure
within most impact categories and reducing food waste at household level and within the supply chain
shows potential for impact reductions also. Nevertheless, combining changes to both consumer behaviour
and technological advancements were not enough to reduce the impacts within all impact categories to
absolute sustainable levels. Impact issues still remain for ”Climate Change”, ”Water Use”, ”Particulate
Matter” and ”Resource Use, Minerals and Metals”. Results were influenced by uncertainties from the
translation of material requirements to aggregated product groups, conversion of physical product flows
to monetary units, and limitations of the LCIA method due to limited emission flows.
Overall, this study shows that extensive, large-scale technological advancements are necessary to decouple
global basic material requirements and human well-being from unsustainable environmental impacts.
Shifting to renewable energy shows significant reduction potentials and is imperative for reducing our
environmental pressure. Future research may prioritize advancements in agricultural practices since this
holds great potential for further reduction of the impacts from a global DLS scenario. Technological
50
==PAGE:57==
advancements may also be investigated for building construction, which causes large impacts to ”Resource
Use, Materials and Metals”. Lastly, future research involving DLS requirements using IO models should
aim to enhance the robustness of results by improving the translation of material requirements to
parameters fitting for IO models.

Supplementary information related to methods
6.1
Environmentally extended input output model (EEIO)
Reasoning for using hybrid EXIOBASE
Although conventional LCAs typically provides more detail at product level, it neglects some of the
underlying activities needed to fulfill final demand due to the cut-off practice and thereby underestimate
environmental impacts [Majeau-Bettez et al., 2011]; [Suh and Huppes, 2002]. EEIO, on the other hand,
cover the entirety of an economy to an infinite order, removing the uncertainty of truncation bias and
makes it possible to include all underlying activities. This is relevant for this study’s modelling case,
where the focus of the study is to model a hypothetical global consumption scenario.
On the other
side, a downside with EEIO is that it operates at an aggregated level and provide restricted information
on specific process level. Thus, while it ensures better model completeness, EEIO provides only course
granularity of product categories and somewhat lack of product documentation, which is a modelling
disadvantage when the original global consumption scenario is based on product and activity inputs.
Another downside to EEIO is that the information is static
Hybrid EXIOBASE 3.3.18
EXIOBASE 3 is a global multi-regional environmentally extended input-output (EEIO) database.
It
provides information on economic activities, environmental pressures, and resource use associated with
production and consumption activities across regions and sectors of the global economy. The database
includes input-output tables based on national accounts for the years 1995-2011 as well as tables based
on nowcasting to extend the time period to later years.
The algorithm used to construct the multi-
regional hybrid supply and use tables (MR-HSUTs) was developed within the European Union (EU)
FP7 project ”DESIRE” [Merciai and Schmidt, 2018].
EXIOBASE 3 covers 49 regions, including 28
EU member countries, 16 major economies and five rest-of-world regions and has a sector classification
consisting of 200 products and 163 industries. The nomenclature in EXIOBASE are based on NACE
(”Nomenclature of Economic Activities”) codes, which are statistical classification codes for economic
activities in the European Union (EU) [Eurostat, 1996]. The NACE classification currently used by the
EU and European businesses are ”NACE rev. 2” from 2008, where the NACE code classification have
been revised to include activities that didn’t exist in NACE Rev. 1”, ensure more relevance to the real
world economy and improve comparability with other international and national classification systems
[Eurostat, 2008]. However, hybrid EXIOBASE 3 follows the old classification system from ”NACE rev.
59
==PAGE:66==
1”.
In this study, a hybrid version of EXIOBASE 3 (version 3.3.18) is used. The term ”hybrid” indicates that
the flow of services and products compiled in mixed units. In contrast to the usual EXIOBASE, where all
flows are compiled in monetary units of MEURO (millions of euros), in the hybrid version physical flows
are compiled in mass units, energy flows are compiled in terajoules (TJ) and services are compiled in
MEURO. Where normal supply and use tables are based solely on monetary balances, the multi-regional
hybrid SUTs (MR-HSUT) are also based on mass and energy balances [Merciai and Schmidt, 2018].
The hybrid input-output tables provided by EXIOBASE have been derived from supply-use tables and
been computed based on the Stone method, assuming by-product technology for the period 2000-2011
([Merciai, 2021]; [Merciai and Schmidt, 2018]). In contrast to the regular EXIOBASE version, the hybrid
version only includes 48 regions, as Taiwan is excluded due to considerable lack of data [Merciai and
Schmidt, 2018]. Moreover the hybrid PxP input output table only includes 164 products instead of 200,
as some product groups have been aggregated.

6.3.0.1
Nutrition
The necessary materials for sufficient nutrition includes food, storage and heating for food preparation.
The sufficient amount of food intake per day is set to 2000–2150 by [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a],
however in this study the nutrient intake is set to 2500 kcal/cap/day in line with the EAT-Lancet planetary
health diet [Willett et al., 2019]. This is to account for 17% food waste ([UNEP, 2021]; [Gustavsson et al.,
2011]). The dietary composition of the food intake is based on EAT Lancet’s planetary health diet’s
macro-nutrient intake composition (g/day) [Willett et al., 2019]. The distribution between plant-based,
meat-based and other animal-based foods is thereafter converted to fit the distribution assigned to each
segment in the DLS as stated by [Millward-Hopkins, 2022b]. The food products is assigned to the best
possible aggregated EXIOBASE product codes through investigation of product code content described
in [Eurostat, 1996].
[Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a] coarsely assumed that 50% of calories have been cooked for lack of
better data. Assuming half of all calories are cooked and an energy intensity of 0.8kJ/cooked kcal, the
overall yearly energy use for cooking of 1250 calories per day is ∼0.37 GJ/capita/yr.
Cold storage

==PAGE:67==
is modelled following [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a] approach assuming a state-of-the-art refrigerator
using 120kWh/yr (only 20% of average energy use which [Cullen et al., 2011] states are feasible) with a
resulting cold storage electricity requirement of 0.11 GJ/yr/cap. Transportation and distribution of food
items have been disregarded.
The water use needed for nutrition is assumed to be 15 L, with 5L assigned to drinking water and 10L
assigned to cooking and kitchen use [Gleick, 1996]. The heating of the water in assumed included in the
energy use for cooking already modelled. Including water use in the nutrition segment differs slightly
from the DLS scenario, where all water use needed for both nutrition and hygiene are modelled simply as
water use [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a], whereas in this study the water is assigned to the appropriate
segment.

Thermal comfort is modelled as the electricity needed to keep the indoor temperature to a comfortable
temperature. The thermal comfort interval, where no heating or cooling is needed, is set to be between
15.6 ℃and 21.1 ℃[Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a]. If the temperature is below 15.6 or above 21.1 ℃the
housing must be respectively heated of or cooled down to stay within the comfortable threshold. Heating
degree days (HDD) and cooling degree days (CDD) are calculated per country by [Millward-Hopkins
et al., 2020a] from historical global climate data by [Atalla et al., 2018]. Thereafter, [Millward-Hopkins
et al., 2020a] estimates energy demands for urban and rural areas [MJ/m2/year] by combining floor space
per capita with data from the Global Building Performance Network ([GBPN, 2012]) on energy demands
[kWh/m2] for heating and cooling of various scenarios such as different regions and urban/rural areas etc..
[Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a]’s calculated direct energy intensity (MJ/m2/year) for thermal heating is
differentiated between urban and rural areas, where the intensity is generally higher per square meter for
urban areas. To model for the highest possible technology the energy requirement data for urban areas
is found from multi-family housing data as MF housing have the lowest energy requirements [Millward-
Hopkins et al., 2020a]. For rural areas singly family housing performance is used since there were no
energy requirement data for multi-family housing. Except for this, there is no differentiation between
multi-family and single-family housing, because the EXIOBASE database have no way of differentiating
between this.

The furniture manufacturing accounts for 69.5% of the category.

manufacturing of goods such as jewellery, music instruments sports goods and games and toys, which are
products that are ”not elsewhere classified”. These categories account for the remaining 30.5%.

6.3.0.5
Education and healthcare
The need for education space is modelled per country varying with the percentage of population in school
age between 5-13 years of age with an educational space of 10m2 per pupil [Millward-Hopkins et al.,
2020a]. For the energy use, educational buildings need electricity use of 14-19 kWh/m2 for heating and

(GBPN) and it is assumed that this accounts for 50% of total energy, making the total energy use 28-38

The need for healthcare space is modelled assuming the need for 8 hospital bed per 1,000 capita and
total healthcare space of 200m2 per bed, giving a total hospital floor space of 1.6 m2/cap. The energy
use is based on heating and cooling energy requirements for Hospitals provided by GBPN and assumes
this accounts for 50% of the buildings total energy use [GBPN, 2012]. Next, this is multiplied with 3
as studies suggest that hospitals only account for one third of the healthcare sector’s energy use [Pichler
et al., 2019]; [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020b].

Communication and infrastructure
The DLS scenario assumes that the need for communication and information can be satisfied by one
computer per household and one phone per person from 10 years and above [Millward-Hopkins et al.,
2020a]. One computer per household is modelled with a lifetime of 10 years and assuming an average
weight of 2.04 kg [Alexander, 2023]. The computer electricity use is modelled with a yearly electricity
use of 220 MJ/yr per household, a value taken from [Deng et al., 2011]. The phone is modelled with a
weight of 365.5 gram and a lifetime of 5 years [Schor, 2023]. The yearly electricity use of a phone is set
to 27.7 MJ/year, which the ”high range” value taken from an LCA by [Ercan et al., 2016]. The highest
use value is used, as [Millward-Hopkins, 2022a] stated, because a phone in the DLS scenario is expected
to accommodate many needs and represent the functionality of many other electrics’ such as cameras,
GPS etc. [Grubler et al., 2018]. The energy use connected to network and infrastructure are taken from
[Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a] who have taken high range values from an LCA of a phone by [Ercan
et al., 2016] and assumed that a laptop uses 5X the network and data usage as a phone. The electricity
use for network and infrastructure are is 5 times higher than than the direct electricity used by phones
and laptops. Moreover [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a], who have translated production and distribution
of phones and laptop to embodied energy states that the energy use by network and infrastructure is
64
==PAGE:71==
twice as high as the manufacture and use combined.
6.3.0.7
Mobility
The mobility segment concerns the requirement of ”assisted” transportation necessary for well-being.
People generally do not wish to spend more then one hour of communing per day and if the transportation
is less, the living options of people will be strictly limited [Schafer, 2000].
The modelling follows
the approach of [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020a] as documented in [Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020b].
The baseline acceptable threshold of the DLS segment is set to 7,000 km/cap/year as suggested by
[Grubler et al., 2018], and modified using a function of population densities of countries for urban and
agricultural areas. The resulting mobility requirements ranges between 5,500 km/cap/yr (Belgium) to
11,900 km/cap/yr (South Africa). For the 5 RoW regions, the mobility requirements are calculated as an
average transportation distance from the countries within each region following the equation:
pkmaverage =
P(pkmi · popi)
P pop
(13)
where i represents all countries with the RoW region (WA, WL, WF, WE and WM). The mobility
requirements are thereafter split between different transportation modes; car, bus, train, air and non-
motorised transport (walking or cycling), directly following the approach of [Millward-Hopkins et al.,
2020a]. The transportation shares are set to constant distances per capita for air (1066 pkm/cap/yr) and
non-motorised transport (1251 pkm/cap/yr) while the remaining mobility requirements per country are
split between the train (40%), bus (40%) and car (20%) segments.
The passenger kilometers per modal share is thereafter converted to fit the EXIOBASE database, as
explained the following sections.
Mobility by air
For air transport, the sufficiency scenario allocated a fixed value of 1066 pkm/cap/yr for all countries.
Using hybrid EXIOBASE, the best way to model the air transportation is by assigning the passenger
kilometres to the product code ”Air transport services (62)” which includes the transportation of passengers
or freight, by air or space [Eurostat, 1996] and is given in EXIOBASE as MEURO. As the passenger
price of air travel varies greatly, calculating the price per pkm from passenger travel expense is deemed
an unacceptable way of converting the travel distances to monetary units.
Instead the final demand
in MEURO for the product code Air Transport services depicting the ”real world” is extracted from
65
==PAGE:72==
EXIOBASE. Based on data from the International Air Transport Association (IATA) it is assumed that
approximately 15% of revenue stems from cargo transportation, which is subtracted from the real world
EXIOBASE demand [IATA, 2023]. Thereafter, data on total passenger kilometers per country are used
to find pkm/cap/year the yearly passenger kilometers per capita per country ([Graver et al., 2019]),
from where the ”Air transport services (62)”’s final demand is scaled per country from the current world
pkm/cap/year to the sufficiency scenario’s pkm/cap/year with the formula:
YDLS = YCW · dDLS
dCW
(14)
where Y is the final demand vector and d is the distance in pkm. For the countries where transportation
data was not found, as well as the 5 rest of world categories, the global average pkm/cap/yr was calculated
and used. Overall, the final demand of Air Transport services is around 75% of the 2011 world demand
and 88% of the Air Transport Services allocated to passenger transport. For most EU countries the final
demand has a considerable decrease, however, India and the RoW Africa region sees a large increase.
Mobility by non-motorized vehicles
For transportation by non-motorized vehicles, the sufficiency scenario allocated a fixed value of 1251
pkm/cap/yr for all countries. The use of non-motorised transportation, such as biking and walking, does
not cause environmental pressure and it is assumed that impacts from manufacturing of equipment is
negligible.
Mobility by train
The required transportation by train is dependent on the countries’ population densities and is modelled
as explained in 2.3.2. In EXIOBASE, the train transportation is modelled using the category ”Railway
transportation services”, given in monetary units, which includes transportation by rail of both passengers
and freight [Eurostat, 2008]. Note that this product does not include Urban and suburban transit systems,
which instead is included in ”other transportation systems”.
Again it is deemed unsuitable to base
EXIOBASE modelling on train ticket prices, and the final demand for ”railway transportation services”
is extracted from EXIOBASE. 70% of the final demand per country is assigned to passenger transport
based on data from Germany ([Bundesnetzagentur, 2023]), however, this is a crude estimate as the
freight/passenger share could vary greatly between countries and other documentation is scarce. Data
on total railway passenger kilometers per country are used to calculation the pkm/cap/year [UIC and
OECD, 2023] and the final demand for rail transport is, in the same way as air transport, scaled by
66
==PAGE:73==
data on current rail pkm to the mobility requirements in the DLS scenario as calculated with formula
14. For the countries where transportation data was not found, as well as the 5 rest of world categories,
the global average pkm/cap/yr is calculated and used.
The final demand of ”railway transportation
services” turned out to 7 times the current world demand, and 10 times the current world demand for
only passenger transportation. Because this could be due to uncertain calculation methods becuase of
uncertaint of demand allocation to the passenger kms, it was decided that the final demand of ”railway
transportation services” could have a maximum increase of 10 times the current world per country which
led to the DLS scenarios final demand to be 5.4 and 8 times larger than railway and only passenger
railway demand respectively.
Mobility by bus
Bus transportation was modelled using the EXIOBASE product code ”Other land transportation services”,
which includes scheduled passenger transportation by urban or suburban transport systems such as by
bus, tram, underground metro systems etc., as well as freight transport via road. Base on EU data,
passenger transport represent only 30% of the product category, as freight transport represents the rest of
the category [Eurostat, 2009]. Another approach is used to estimate the final demand for this sector, as
correct passenger data for all these systems can not be found. The 2011 passenger-km in this category was
backtracked from EXIOBASE’s per country emission factors of kgCO2/EUR in 2019 [Climatiq, 2020] and
assuming an emission intensity of 22 gCO2/pkm for the the sector based on the lowest emission intensity
value for buses (seeing that the service group also includes low-emission transport such as trmas and
metros) [IEA, 2020], which resulted in a per country estimate price per pkm (MEUR/pkm) which was
multiplied with the mobility requirements (pkm/cap/yr). This results in an average global price of 0.08
EURO/pkm and a total global final demand for ”Other land transportation services” that is 1.7 times
the 2011 final demand.
Mobility by car - manufacturing and fuel use
To model the mobility requirements assigned to car transportation car manufacturing is modelled with the
product category Motor vehicles, trailers and semi-trailers (34) [MEURO] and fuel is modelled using the
product category ”Refined petroleum” [tonnes] which is an aggregated product category consisting of many
petroleum forms such as Motor Gasoline, Aviation Gasoline, Kerosens, Gas/Diesel Oil and others.
Millward-Hopkins et al. suggests a car weight of 1.4t (as adopted by [Chester et al., 2013]), an occupancy
rate of 3 pers/car ([Millward-Hopkins et al., 2020b]) and assumes that embodied energy can be cut by
67
==PAGE:74==
33% [Allwood and Cullen, 2011]. By assuming that functioning car share systems to ensure the access
to a car without ownership, the lifetime of a car is estimated to be 240,000km of total driving distance
[Green NCAP, 2022]. The total number of cars needed to fulfil the scenario requirement is calculated per
country as following:
No. of carsn =
MOBn · populationn
occupancy rate · car lifetime distance
(15)
where MOBn is the mobility level per country [pkm/cap/year] , popn is the population per country, the
occupancy rate is 3 [cap/car] and the total car lifetime distance is 240,000 km. The average price per car
was set to 24,439 MEURO based on European data [ICCT, 2021] and thereafter modified per country
using index points of country price in relation to world average [World Bank, 2017]. Thereafter, the total
final demand is calculated as the total price per country of all manufactured cars and then reduced by
33% as a way of accounting for more efficient manufacturing.
Car fuel consumption is set to 0.033L/km, which is the fuel consumption stated for the most fuel-efficient
hybrid car by [Falsou, 2023], to include the highest efficiency possible. The density of petrol is 0.737 kg/L
and so the fuel consumption in tonne can by calculated from the fuel consumption and total driven km
(as total passenger kilometer divided by car capacity of 3 cap).

6.5
Implementing environmental impact categories
The Environmental Footprint (EF) LCIA method were implemented to hybrid EXIOBASE following the
same approach as explained in [Beylot et al., 2019] and using supplementary data provided from personal
communication [De Laurentiis, 2024]. The following section explains the approach as performed by [Beylot
et al., 2019], of which average CFs have been provided. The approach assigns a characterisation factor
(CF) to all EXIOBASE elementary flows by systematic classification, relating them to relevant impact
categories, where the CFs are taken from the EF3.1 reference package ([European Commission, 2024];
[Bassi et al., 2023]). Many EXIOBASE flows have an identical match in EF3.1 and are used directly.
Where identical matching is not possible a proxy CF is used. The proxy CFs are based on based on
a corresponding pollutant or chemical group. Aggregated flow groups in EXIOBASE are estimated as
either a weighted or arithmetic average of available CFS or as a weighted average of the emissions using
the produced amounts at EU-27 or global level. As the characterisation factor implementation provided
by [Beylot et al., 2019] is used, which study focused on the EU member states, some misweighting of
emissions at global level is possible.
For the impact category FRD, the EF method did not directly
provided with the necessary unit and calorific values provided by the PEFCR Guidance document were
used [European Commission, 2017]
Two of the 16 EF impact categories, IR and ODP, are excluded from the study because EXIOBASE lacks
the elementary flows that relates to ionizing radiation and have very few ozone-depleting substances, as
reported by both [Beylot et al., 2019] and [Huysman et al., 2016]. The elementary flows and CFs assigned
to the remaining 14 impact categories are stated in tables 13 to 26 below. The full matrix and background
data can also bee seen in SI 9.3.

Supplementary information for implementing affluence inequality
The DLS activity levels that are increased with increasing affluence are increased linearly with the
consumption distribution as shown in Figure 1, where the average applied change is an increase of 234%.
The calorific intake of food not increased, instead the food composition is increased. The share of meat
increases and displaces plant-based foods, while ”other animal-based food products” (eggs and dairy
products) stays the same. As the amount of food remains the the same, so does the need for heating of
food, refridgeration and water use. The housing space is increased, as this is one of the most probably
consumption increases with increasing affluence. When the housing space increases, so does the need for
illumination and thermal heating as well. The amount of furniture is not changed, because it is argued
that while it may be needed extra furniture for large housing space, it should still be sufficient with the
same amount of large furniture such as beds, sofas, tables etc. For hygiene it is assumed a large use of
water. For the clothing segment, the amount of clothing is increased but washing remains the same. The
education and healthcare segments stays the same, as a higher affluence does not lead to an increased
need for hospital or education space. For the communication segment it is assumed that there is no need
77
==PAGE:84==
for more phones (as everyone >10 years already has one) or increased phone usage. For laptops, the
amount of laptops is increased and both the direct energy usage and indirect energy use from network
and data centres increases proportionally. The resulting maximum consumption level, for the richest
population fraction, and the average consumption levels from including inequality in affluence levels is
seen in Table 27 below.
