Abstract
To compute good estimates the risk of pluvial ﬂooding in urban applications, 2D over-
land ﬂow models based on shallow water equations are often used. The solution of these
equations is based on a computational mesh. Flexible meshes consisting of triangles and
or quadrangles are highly adaptable to the modelling problem, which can give diﬀerent
beneﬁts such as increased resolution in certain areas. The drawback is that creation of a
good mesh can be time consuming. The aim of this project is to ﬁnd a method for gener-
ating combined ﬂexible meshes for use in urban areas. To improve ﬂow routing via roads,
quadrilateral mesh is generated on the roads except for road intersections and triangular
mesh elsewhere.
A GIS procedure was developed which can identify road stretches excluding road in-
tersections and save them as polygons. The method was shown to work well for most
roads, but it sometimes fail in complicated areas. The found road polygons were meshed
in MIKE Zero using the quadrilateral transﬁnite interpolation for the road polygons and
triangles for the rest of the domain. In order to automate the process of deﬁning the
polygons for transﬁnite meshing, a script was written, which takes the road polygons as
input and writes them in the mdf ﬁle format, so they could be exported and meshed easily.
This script works very well for the most common type of road polygons and ignored the
rest, so very little cleaning of input data is necessary. The meshes were tested with a rel-
atively basic 2D overland ﬂow model and the results were compared to the standard 2×2
meter structured mesh and other triangular meshes. The results showed that good ﬂow
representation can be achieved with the combined meshes if the resolution in the areas
of interest is high enough. The attempt to create an easy to automate mesh generator is
therefore successful, but some improvements are needed before it becomes robust enough
for a wider use.

Introduction
It is generally believed that the ongoing climate change will lead to more extreme weather.
In many parts of the world this would lead to increased ﬂood risk (IPCC, 2014). This
would be in form of sea surges, ﬂash ﬂoods or pluvial ﬂooding. At the same time, the
global urbanization trend increases consequences of ﬂooding (IPCC, 2014; United Nations,
2014). Therefore, appropriate urban planning and management of ﬂood risk is important
to avoid large consequences in the future (Hall and Penning-Rowsell, 2011). When working
with climate change adaption, modelling is an important tool to assess the damages of an
event and the eﬀect of implementing an adaptation measure.
An important part of ﬂood modelling is to determine where the water ends up on
the surface in case of a severe rain event. There are many ways to do this. The sim-
plest approach is to make a GIS analysis of the surface to determine depressions where
water would theoretically gather in case of excessive rain. In reality, there is usually an
interaction between the surface and a sewer system, which may change the ﬂood map
dramatically compared to the depression analysis (DANVA, 2011). In the more complex
alternatives, diﬀerent modelling software such as TUFLOW or MIKE Flood can be used
to model the drainage system and the surface in combination. State-of-the-art in this ﬁeld
is a coupled 1D-2D model. Here, the water in the drainage system is modelled with a 1D
hydrodynamic model and the surface ﬂow is modelled using 2D hydrodynamic equations.
Exchange between the two systems is possible to give a more realistic simulation of the
situation (Butler and Davis, 2011). These models tend to give the most accurate results,
but they are relatively demanding when it comes to computer power (DANVA, 2011).
Therefore, a way to improve computational speed while still maintaining a high accuracy
of the output is desirable.
Many of the available 2D ﬂood modelling packages are based on solution of the shallow
water equations (Neelz et al., 2010). Typically, 2D ﬂood modelling is based on a digital
elevation model in raster format, in which the terrain elevation is given in each grid cell.
Another way to store the elevation is in a triangulated irregular network (TIN), and
2D simulations based on a TIN (or ﬂexible mesh) is possible in software like MIKE 21
(Bolstad, 2016; DHI, 2015a). The TIN has the advantage of being adaptable to features
on the surface, meaning that it is possible to add more or less points in areas of greater
or lesser importance. This may save simulation time and gives more accurate results in
areas of interest compared to a raster based elevation model, which does not have this
ﬂexibility (Bolstad, 2016; Harris, 2013).
When running computer simulations of 2D overland ﬂow it could therefore be an
advantage to use a ﬂexible mesh for the surface representation. However, the process of
generating a suitable mesh can be time-consuming because the speciﬁcations of the mesh
tend to inﬂuence the accuracy of the model result. Many mesh generators exist that can
generate well shaped meshes of either triangular or quadrilateral shape (Ho-Le, 1988; Frey

and George, 2008). While well shaped meshes are important for good results, it is not
the only factor inﬂuencing the model results (Babuska and Aziz, 1976; McRae, 2000).
In Harris (2013) and McRae (2000) it is shown that mesh resolution, element size and
alignment of the mesh with the physics to be modelled also contribute to the accuracy of
the results.
A tool to make generation of ﬂexible meshes more accessible, could be a ﬂexible mesh
generator, which could automatically generate meshes well suited for modelling of surface
ﬂow. Nielsen and Skougaard (1990) and Kashiyama and Okada (1992) have worked on
development of automatic mesh generators for use with shallow water equations. However,
their mesh generators are for modelling ﬂow in coastal regions and therefore rely on water
depth data, which are not available when modelling 2D overland ﬂooding, where the
surface is mostly dry. A mesh generator is therefore needed which is adapted to generation
of meshes for urban application. Cities are very diverse environments in terms of surfaces
and structures and it would therefore be ideal if the mesh generator could take information
about this into account.
During rain events in cities a part of the water landing on impermeable area is often
led to the roads, from whence it enters the urban drainage system. Roads are generally
constructed so that water can run oﬀthe middle to the sides. Also, as the terrain is rarely
completely ﬂat, roads usually have a slope from one end to the other which will determine
the water ﬂow until a drain is reached. If the infrastructure is overloaded, the roads may
become important transport routes of water on the surface (DANVA, 2011). It is therefore
reasonable to assume that the water will ﬂow either along the road or perpendicular to it
and a good way to align the mesh with the ﬂow would be to generate quadrilateral mesh
to over the roads. Flow directions cannot really be assumed for road intersections, so
quadrilateral mesh elements do not seem suitable there. Often, data on where the roads
are, is available. Therefore, an automatic method for ﬁnding road areas, but ignore areas
at road intersections, and output them as polygons has to be developed as part of the
work in this project.
Project aim and report outline
The aim of this thesis is to make an improved ﬂexible mesh for 2D ﬂood modelling in a
way that is easy to automate. The improvement consists of generating quadrilateral mesh
on roads and triangular mesh elsewhere to better align the mesh with the ﬂow direction.
The work is implemented in three steps:
1. A methodology for automatically ﬁnding the road parts that should have quadrilat-
eral mesh from GIS data is developed.
2. Computational meshe are produced using the road parts from the ﬁrst part to com-
bine quadrangles and triangles.
3. A 2D overland ﬂow model of an urban catchment is run with MIKE 21 with the
combined meshes as well a normal raster based grid. This is to be able to compare
the model performance in terms of computational speed, ﬂood prediction accuracy
and numerical stability.
The division of the work into these three main tasks makes it natural to present
the work in three chapters each presenting the relevant theory along with the method,

Dyrup catchment in Odense, Denmark. An orthophoto of the area can be seen in ﬁgure

Theoretical background
In MIKE 21 the water ﬂow on the surface is calculated using the shallow water equations.
In this chapter, these equations are presented along with the ﬁnite volume method, which
is used for discretization (DHI, 2015a). A short introduction to stability and working with
ﬂexible meshes is also given. Theory which is more speciﬁc for work in other chapters is
described in the relevant chapters.
2.1
Shallow water equations
The shallow water equations are derived from the Navier-Stokes equations, which can
describe the velocity ﬁeld in a ﬂuid at a given point in time (Rousseau et al., 2015;
Kurzbach, 2014). When the scale in the x and y directions is much larger than in the z
direction, the acceleration in vertical direction becomes very small. Therefore the Navier-

depth. Then, by assuming hydrostatic pressure, the shallow water equations are obtained
(Kurzbach, 2014). In DHI (2015a), the MIKE 21 FM documentation, the shallow water
equations are written in the following form, where equation 2.1 is the momentum equation
and equations 2.2 and 2.3 are the continuity equation in the x and y direction, respectively:

2.2
Finite volume method
The shallow water equations have to be solved numerically (Rousseau et al., 2015). One of
the numerical methods available is the ﬁnite volume method, which is widely used in ﬂuid
mechanics (Moukalled et al., 2016). The ﬁnite volume method works both with structured
meshes (like a raster grid) and with unstructured meshes (such as a triangulated surface)
(Rousseau et al., 2015). The partial diﬀerential equations to be solved are transformed
into simpler algebraic equations by integrating them over each mesh element. They can
then be solved as a system of algebraic functions (Moukalled et al., 2016). An important
feature of the ﬁnite volume method is that the ﬂux entering an element is equal to the
ﬂux leaving, meaning that the mass balance is not violated. This means that the ﬁnite
volume method is conservative (Moukalled et al., 2016; Rousseau et al., 2015).
2.3
Numerical stability
Stability in numerical methods refers to the behaviour of the discretized equations when
solving them with an iterative solver. If the system is stable, the numerical error is kept
within limits over the simulation time (Moukalled et al., 2016). For an unstable system,
there is a risk of the errors to grow out of control. The way of ensuring stability depends on
the solution method used to solve the equations. In an explicit scheme, as used in MIKE
21 FM, stability can be ensured by lowering the time step (Moukalled et al., 2016). The
Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy (CFL) number expresses the necessary condition for stability.
In order for the calculation to be stable, the time step has to be chosen to that the CFL
number is below 1 (DHI, 2016a). In MIKE 21 FM, the CFL number is deﬁned for each
element as (DHI, 2016a):
CFL = c · ∆t
∆x
(2.4)
Here ∆t is the time step length, ∆x is the spatial resolution and c is the celerity, where
MIKE 21 uses the celerity of tidal waves, c = √g · h. In other words, the CFL number
describes how far, in terms of grid points, the water travels in one time step (DHI, 2016a).
In order to ensure that the simulations run well MIKE 21 FM uses a variable time step,
where the CFL number is checked during simulation and the time step length adjusted
accordingly (DHI, 2016b). The simulation time is therefore heavily inﬂuenced by the CFL
number. As such, the time step chosen by the software can be seen as an indicator of
stability of the system. From the deﬁnition in equation 2.4 it is clear that if the minimum
edge length (spatial resolution) decreases, the time step has to decrease as well in order
to keep the CFL number down. Furthermore, it can be seen that if water depth increases,
stability can be ensured by either reducing the time step length or increasing the minimum
edge length.
2.4
Working with meshes
Because the ﬁnite volume method calculates the water volume on each mesh face and over
each mesh edge, the mesh in itself is very important for the result of the calculations. If
9

the mesh is too coarse, the result will have too large errors to be useful. On the other
hand, increasing the number of mesh elements will increase simulation time. Meshes can
be divided into two classes, structured and unstructured. The cells in structured meshes
are connected very simply to their neighbors and can be stored in 2- or 3-dimensional
arrays. They are therefore eﬃcient in terms of storage volume (Frey and George, 2008).
The unstructured (or ﬂexible) meshes have a more random connectivity between cells
and are therefore more storage consuming (Harris, 2013). Their great advantage is their
ﬂexibility. They are adaptable so it is possible to increase resolution in important areas
and have less points in less important areas (Bolstad, 2016; Harris, 2013). For the ﬁnite
element method it is proven that very big or small angles in the mesh give bad results and
should be avoided (Babuska and Aziz, 1976). This also seems to be the case for the ﬁnite
volume method. It has been shown that aligning the mesh with the physical properties
e.g. ﬂow, improve the modelling results(Harris, 2013).
Finally, because meshes are only a tool to solve the equations in a model, the result
can never be better than the model, no matter how good a mesh is generated.

3
GIS manipulation
As discussed in section 2.4, it can be beneﬁcial in the solution of shallow water equations
if the mesh and the ﬂow direction is aligned, as it gives a more simple routing of the ﬂow.
Therefore, roads are usually projected so that water can run oﬀto the side and usually
slope from one end to the other, it is assumed that a quadrilateral mesh will be a better
choice of mesh for the roads, as the ﬂow and the mesh will be better aligned than with a
triangular mesh. On the other hand, the ﬂow in road intersection areas is less predictable
and will therefore still be covered by a triangular mesh. The goal of the GIS manipulation
presented in this chapter is to develop a method for identifying road stretches as polygons
without including road intersections. The intention is to make a generic approach for this
type of road data, and similar catchments, so it would be easy to automate. The road
polygons can later be meshed with quadrilateral mesh.
The method described in this chapter relies on a polygon covering the roads, which
is cut at road intersections so that each road leaving an intersection is cut oﬀfrom the
intersection. Three main steps are followed: creation of the road polygon, creation of lines
used for cutting the polygon at intersections, and the cutting step itself. From the input
data it does not require many steps to create a big polygon that covers the roads. On the
other hand, the procedure for creating cutting lines is not straightforward, as intersections
are not marked in any way in the input data. Consequently, this part of the method
consists of much more steps. An overview can be seen in ﬁgure 3.1.
• If relevant, the roads are grouped based on width.
• A buﬀer is made around the road lines.
• From the buﬀers, the centers of the road intersections are identiﬁed and marked with
a point.
• Lines for cutting the road polygon are created based on the marking point.
The big road polygon found previously can now be split using the lines for cutting. In the
following, each step of the method is described in more detail.
All GIS work in this chapter was done using Python 2.7 and QGIS 2.18 Las Palmas
with the extension packages SAGA 2.1.2 and GRASS GIS 7.

3.2.2.1
Deﬁnition of road width

First, if the width of the roads vary too much, they should be divided into groups of
roads of similar width to ensure that buﬀering in the next steps will work as intended. As

diagonal of the intersection of the two smallest roads.

into the next road, it may be necessary to cut the road lines where the lines continue from

explanation of this is illustrated later, in 3.2.2.2.
3.2.2.2
Fixed distance buﬀer on road lines
Now a ﬁxed distance buﬀer is applied to the entire road line layer. The width is the same

3.2.2.3
Adding points at crossroads
From the road buﬀers, a negative buﬀer is now made. The idea is that this buﬀer should

on the middle of the road, but still small enough so that the buﬀer edges do not meet in
the crossroads where the roads are thicker. When a negative buﬀer overlaps with itself no
polygon is formed, which means that polygons are left in areas that are so wide, that the
buﬀer edges do not meet. In the upper part of ﬁgure 3.4 these polygons are shown on top
of the ﬁxed distance buﬀer from 3.2.2.2, from which they have been derived.

3.6
Discussion
In general, the results of the GIS manipulation are satisfying as most straight road stretches
are correctly identiﬁed and turned into a road polygon. However, the method also have
some areas where it could be improved, which will be discussed in the following.
3.6.1
Input data
One thing that could improve the result could be better input data. The road lines used
for this procedure had a couple of problems. The most noticeable problem was that some
lines were not complete, which made it necessary to use the buﬀer method described in
3.2.1 to create a polygon that covered the road area. In another case, some lines were
incorrectly marked as driving road. Two clear examples were a house which got covered
by road polygon, shown in ﬁgure 3.12, and the parking area seen in ﬁgure 3.18. The lines
which were wrongly categorized could be corrected without too much eﬀort by manually
changing their category in the attribute table. It could be debated whether the driveway
shown in ﬁgure C.1 could also be a candidate to be categorized as something else than
driving road. Repairing the lines where they are not complete would on the other hand
take a long time especially on a data set with more holes than the one used here.
3.6.2
Creation of road polygon
Generally, the buﬀer method works well. In the areas with smaller holes in the road lines,
the big road polygon has an almost normal shape as seen in ﬁgure 3.11. The road shape
is a bit irregular in these places, but this should not impact a mesh created from these
polygons much. It could, however, be a problem if the pieces missing in the road lines were
bigger. In that case it should be considered to manually correct the holes even though it
is time consuming. Otherwise, it should be considered if an automatic method should be
developed to cover the holes, for example by extending the road lines on each side of the
hole
From the case study, it was found that the buﬀer method had the problems mentioned
in section 3.5.1. These include places where details in the road polygon are fused together
and corners are rounded at intersections. The case where the details are merged together
is not necessarily bad. As these areas become wider than the road they belong to, they
will be treated as a road intersection in the step for creating cutting lines. This is mainly
good since it may be hard to create a good quadrilateral mesh in these areas, depending
on the mesh type. The transﬁnite interpolation mesh used in this thesis is deﬁnitely not
well suited for road polygons with these details on. The downside is that a road part
that could have been meshed with quad mesh will be covered with triangular mesh, which
might impact the surface ﬂow routing.
In the case of rounded corners, the importance varies. In most cases the curve from
the intersection of two roads is within the road cutting circle from subsection 3.2.2.4. This
means that the roads will get cut. In rare cases the circle covers the crossing deﬁned by
the original road lines, but not the curve on the road crossing polygon. This situation has
occurred in ﬁgure 3.15 (red arrow) and means that the resulting polygon cannot be used
for quadrilateral meshing as the road intersection is still included.
Furthermore, the buﬀer method is not well suited for roads that are close to each other
as in ﬁgure 3.12 because the area between the roads also becomes part of the polygon.
Another example is shown in C.2. The ﬁrst problem with this is mainly that the area

between the roads will not be meshed with triangles. The diﬀerence between triangular
and quadrangular mesh is probably not important for the result in such small areas, so
it is of lesser concern. The second and most important problem is that the quadrilateral
meshing method used in the rest of this project does not cope well with these shapes. The
circles used to create cutting lines have a ﬁxed width and can therefore not handle very
broad areas as in ﬁgure 3.12 or C.1.
A possible method for improving the results of the road polygon creation could be to
ﬁnd another method. One suggestion is to make a ﬁll of the area enclosed by the road
lines. This would also be a hard task because all holes on the lines should be corrected
and and because of all the places where there are no lines at road ends. Otherwise the
buﬀer method could be improved by having a check to see if two roads are close to each
other, maybe by tracking pairs of lines, and make sure that the buﬀer made from these
lines would not dissolve.
3.6.3
Creation of cutting lines
A common issue with the method is the size of the circle which is used to cut the road
covering polygon. In some cases the circle is not big enough, in other cases it gets too
big and and covers an unnecessarily large area. The latter is especially common in the
Tranehøjen area with many small roads. Two types of problems can occur when the circles
are too small. One is the case where cutting lines are created around the point, but in the
wrong places like shown in ﬁgure 3.15. The resulting road polygons are not likely to be
well suited for covering with a quadrilateral mesh using transﬁnite interpolation because
the road shape becomes strange. Furthermore, it would mean that quadrilateral mesh is
created on a road intersection. If the circles are bigger than needed, area that could have
been covered with quadrilateral mesh is covered with triangular instead, which is maybe
not a large problem as the mesh will be usable for simulations anyway, but it is against
the objective of the method.
The second type of error caused by small too small circles arises if too few cutting lines
are created around the insuﬃciently large circle. It means that the intersection marker
point lies on a part connected to the road polygon after cutting the road covering polygon.
As a consequence this road part is removed In the spatial query. An example where this
has happened is shown in ﬁgure 3.17 and a case where it will happen can be seen in ﬁgure
C.1.
There are also several places where the method ﬁnds a road crossing even though there
are no intersections. Examples of this was presented in ﬁgure 3.14. The problem with this
is the same as for the too big circles just described.
A way to avoid the ”false positives” in the cutting line creation step could be to make a
search procedure to check around each cross road marking circle if it is covering more than
one road. This could for example be done by counting the number of polyline intersects,
as there should be more than four if there is more than one road.
This method could be extended to also include a solution to the problem with too
small or big circles. It could be done by checking the length of the simpliﬁed lines that
are produced as a part the line generation step in 3.2.2.4. If one the lines is much longer
than the road width or the other lines, it would mean that it does not lie perpendicular
to the road but is skewed somehow. For example, at the error shown in ﬁgure 3.15, the
line to be used for cutting has a length of around 18 meters before being put into the line
extension script. As the widest road in the Dyrup area is around 13 meter, it is clear that

As will be discussed in chapter 4 it is often an advantage for mesh generation if there are as
few constraints as possible in the shape to be meshed. It would be ideal to get the resulting
road polygons simpliﬁed compared to the input road lines, which contain many points. As
will be further described in section 4.3.2, it would be best to do the polygon simpliﬁcation
on the big road polygon before cutting with the cutting lines. A straightforward method
is to use the GIS function Simplify Geometries. This method fails when using the GIS
tool Polygon-line Intersection to cut the simpliﬁed version of the big road polygon because
he polygon gets cut in the wrong places. This is a ﬂaw in the Polygon-line Intersection
tool. Therefore Simplify Geometries is used on the resulting road polygons instead, after
running the GIS procedure. Notice that the results shown in subsection 3.5.3 are the road
polygons before simpliﬁcation.
The problem with using Simplify Geometries is that it is not possible to control the
minimum distance between points. This can be important when creating a mesh as it
may limit size of mesh elements. The shape of the polygons is changed a little during
simpliﬁcation, but the error is not expected to be big compared to general uncertainty in
modelling. I should be considered if a script could be written to cut the road polygons as
well, if they are simpliﬁed.
A possibility is to ﬁnd a simpliﬁcation algorithm which removes the unnecessary points
while at the same time takes a minimum distance between points into account so that
points will not lie too close but the shape of the polygon will still be close to the original.
In some cases a resulting road polygon has a strange shape. This may be due the input
data or an error in the method. An example is the road polygon which is found next to
the parking area in ﬁgure 3.15. Here the polygon takes a sharp turn and has a very acute
angle. This is improved by simpliﬁcation, but will still be hard to mesh for some mesh
types.
First, the input data should be cleaned to avoid shapes which are not driving road
as already mentioned in 3.6.1 because several errors can be avoided this way. Very sharp
angles are not good in any mesh and if the alternative simpliﬁcation algorithm mentioned
above is developed it could be expanded to include a check for acute angles. Because roads
do not usually have these angles it may be considered safe to simplify them away.
The way that the GIS procedure works now makes the roads covered by quadrilateral
mesh in the areas where the roads make a sharp turn but there is no intersection. It can
be discussed whether this is a good idea because nothing is known about the ﬂow at these
points. Usually, it would be expected that the water either follows the road around the
corner or runs oﬀthe road. This could cause some trouble with the mesh alignment, but
there will always be mesh lines pointing outwards so that the water can be transported to
the road side, if not routed in the most direct way. Since many roads are designed to be
a bit higher in the middle, most of the water will likely be transported close to the sides,
anyway. From there it can easily be routed away by the triangular mesh.

3.6.5
Potential for automation
The method has not yet been automated, but apart from the steps where decisions have
to be made (the negative buﬀer and size of circles around intersection marker points) this
should be rather simple to do. Python integration is possible with both QGIS and ArcGIS.
In ArcGIS, there is also the possibility of using the Model Builder Tool, at least for parts
of the method. In the previous parts of the discussion, suggestions have been made how
to make the method work better in ways not requiring manual correction.
An important consideration is also whether a complete automation is desirable. Road
data from diﬀerent areas will almost certainly contain diﬀerences from the Dyrup data
set and there is a risk of new situations which have not been taken into consideration
when developing this method. The optimal situation would be to have a generator which,
with the road lines as input, could produce perfect road polygons, but, as problems are
still likely to arise, it may be better to automate some of the steps so it is possible to
inspect the results and correct errors manually (if any) before going on. Especially after
the process of creating the cutting lines, this would be good as it is the most error prone
part of the procedure. However, even with only partial automation, this method will be
much faster than digitizing the road polygons for meshing by hand, especially if some of
the correcting steps discussed above are considered.

4
Mesh generation
Unstructured computational meshes of diﬀerent shapes exist, but the ones most used in
2D simulations are the triangular and quadrilateral meshes. Good triangular meshes are
generally easy to generate, while quadrilateral meshes can be more tricky (Atalay et al.,
2012). By ”good” is meant meshes with elements that do not have overly large or small
angles, as described in section 2.4. On the other hand, the quadrilateral meshes often have
a lower number of points, which reduces the simulation time. Which mesh type that gives
the most accurate results depends on the application (Benzley et al., 1995; D’Azevedo,
2000). Regardless of the mesh type chosen, it is important that the mesh is adapted to
the problem it is used to solve.
In this report, the aim is to improve modelling of water ﬂowing on the surface in
an urban environment, so a combination of triangular and quadrilateral mesh is used to
ensure a better water ﬂow routing on the roads. This is done by creating quadrilateral
mesh on the roads, where the ﬂow is assumed to be either parallel or perpendicular to the
the road, and triangular mesh elsewhere. The roads to be covered by quadrilateral mesh
are already found as polygons by the GIS method explained in chapter 3. The software
used for mesh generation is MIKE Zero.
4.1
Theory
In the following section, the theoretical background for the chosen mesh types is explained,
as well as how they are implemented in MIKE Zero. The triangular mesh chosen is a
constrained Delaunay triangulation. The quadrilateral mesh is generated using transﬁnite
interpolation.
4.1.1
Triangular Meshing
A triangulation of a set of points, P, in a 2 dimensional plane can be seen as a subdivision of
the plane. The points in P are the vertices of the triangulation and the faces are triangular.
The faces of the triangulation cover the convex hull of P. The convex hull is the smallest
convex polygon, that contain all points in P. (de Berg et al., 2008; Yvinec, 2016). Many
triangulations are possible for the same point set. Two diﬀerent triangualtions of the same
point set are shown in ﬁgure 4.1.
A Delaunay triangulation is a triangulation of P that fulﬁlls the property that a cir-
cumscribed circle (a circle going through all three vertices) of any face in the triangulation
do not contain any other of the points in P (van Kreveld, 1997). Edges in the Delaunay
triangulation are called Delaunay edges. An example of a Delaunay triangulation can be
seen in ﬁgure 4.1b. A desirable property when the triangulations are used as computa-
tional meshes is that from all possible triangulations of P, the Delaunay triangulation will

4.1.3
Quadrilateral meshing
There are many diﬀerent ways to create a quadrilateral mesh. They are usually divided
into direct and indirect methods. In the direct methods, the quadrangles are generated
directly from the input points/lines, while the indirect methods create the quadrilaterals
from an existing, triangular mesh (Johnen et al., 2015). An example of a direct method is
the Paving algorithm, which uses an advancing front method to mesh a polygon (Blacker
and Stephenson, 1991). The Blossom algorithms are examples of indirect methods because
they are based on the pairing of triangles from a triangular mesh to form quadrilaterals
(Kolmogorov, 2009; Remacle et al., 2012). The method used in this project is the transﬁ-
nite interpolation method. It is another direct method based on subdivision of the domain,
and is quite simple to implement (Ho-Le, 1988). MIKE Zero’s method for doing quadri-
lateral meshing is the transﬁnite interpolation method (DHI, 2015b).
In order to construct a quadrilateral mesh of a polygon in 2 dimensions using transﬁnite
interpolation, the polygon border has to be subdivided into four polylines . The borders
can be named ”West”, ”South”, ”East”, and ”North” as seen in ﬁgure 4.5. The directions
along the lines are denoted by ξ and η. They vary between 0 and 1 along the polylines and
indicate the distance along the polyline divided by the total length of the polyline(Ho-Le,
1988).

4.2. Combined mesh generation in MIKE Zero
4.2
Combined mesh generation in MIKE Zero
In the MIKE Zero Mesh Generator combined meshes can be created. Quadrilateral meshes
are made using the transﬁnite interpolation method and triangulations are generated with
the mesh generation software Triangle described in 4.1.2 (DHI, 2015b). First the quadri-
lateral mesh is generated in the polygons where it is wanted. The places where the mesh
lines meet the polygon boundary are converted to vertices, i.e. constrained points in the
mesh generation.
Then the triangular mesh is created in two steps in the rest of the
domain to ensure that the triangulation connects with the quadrilateral mesh in points
where the lines from the quadrilateral mesh touch the polygon boundaries.
1. In the ﬁrst step the mesh is allowed to place Steiner points on the polygon boundaries.
2. The Steiner points on all boundaries that are not quadrilateral mesh domains are
converted to vertices. The triangular mesh is then deleted.
3. Triangular mesh is created again, this time not allowed to place Steiner points on
boundaries, only using the vertices (DHI, 2015b).
4.3
Method
The test areas from Dyrup are to be meshed with a combination of triangular and quadri-
lateral mesh. As the aim is to automate the procedure as much as possible, the deﬁnition
of polygons, mesh type, size constraints and so on for the road polygons will be deﬁned
in a python script. This script deﬁnes points, arcs and polygons and writes this and the
information for quadrilateral meshing to the mesh deﬁnition ﬁle format. The area can then
easily be meshed with the mesh generator in MIKE Zero. In the following, the MIKE Zero
terminology for geometries is used. There are two types of points. Nodes are points that
indicate the end of a line. Other points are called vertices. Polylines are referred to as
arcs.
4.3.1
The mesh generation in MIKE Zero
If quadrilateral mesh is wanted in an area, this area has to be covered by a polygon.
Thereafter the polygon settings have to be edited to tell the mesh generator that quadri-
lateral mesh is wanted in the area. End arcs and maximum edge lengths in both directions
also have to be deﬁned. When polygons are deﬁned and properties are speciﬁed for each
individual polygon, and a boundary has been drawn around the area of interest, a mesh
can be generated. MIKE Zero does this by ﬁrst generating the quadrilateral mesh in the
right areas, then cover the remaining area with triangular mesh (DHI, 2015b).
The mesh generation is saved in a mesh deﬁnition ﬁle, which is a text ﬁle with the
ﬁle extension .mdf. In the mdf ﬁle, information is stored about the settings of the mesh
generation ﬁle. This can be links to input data sources, map projection, triangulation
settings etc. It also contains all geometries deﬁned in the mesh generator: points, arcs
and polygons, made up from connected arcs (DHI, 2016a).
Because the mdf-ﬁle is a text format it is relatively simple to write the point, arc, and
polygon deﬁnition strings using another program and add it the mdf ﬁle, either by writing
directly to the mdf ﬁle or simply copying the deﬁnition strings into the mdf ﬁle. To be
able to do this, the format of the deﬁnitions has to be understood. As the attempt to ﬁnd

points of the polygon get simpliﬁed away, which would make the constraint generation
script fail for that polygon. The risk, however, is considered very small, and it should be
fairly simple to correct this, as will be discussed in 4.6.2.2.

4.3.5
Limitations
A method for identifying all four boundaries for road polygons which were only cut in one
end was not developed. Polygons which had a shape that was not suitable for quadrilateral
meshing (such as t-shapes) are also ignored in the script. The script presented in section
4.3.3 is written in such a way that only polygons associated with two road end lines will
be written to the mdf ﬁle. This means that the script is robust to all polygons that are
only cut once or polygons cut more than twice (such as t-shapes). A problem that is not
handled is the cases where a polygon has been cut in two places, but one of them is not
the end of the road piece (as seen in ﬁgure 3.15, red arrow). These are not ﬁt for meshing
with the transﬁnite interpolation, so they are important to handle. A method for avoiding
these shapes in the ﬁrst place is considered in the GIS discussion in section 3.6 and a way

4.6
Discussion
The mesh generator seems to perform reasonably well with the input provided. For most
mesh combinations the meshes seem well shaped, especially for area 1. For area 2 only
very few manual corrections are needed to obtain seemingly good meshes. This means that
it seems like a very fast and simple way to create a mesh for an urban area without too
complicated structures. The method also leaves room for improvement, which is discussed
in the following.
Mesh quality is discussed based on the results from section 4.5. The quality of the ﬂood
modelling does not depend on mesh quality parameters alone but also on topography and
the equations used in the modelling. It is therefore not possible to say for sure how the
meshes perform before using them for modelling, which is done in chapter 5.
4.6.1
Mesh size constraints
As already mentioned in the results, the quality of the mesh varies depending on the
size constraints for the quadrilateral and the triangular mesh. The quadrilateral mesh
is generated without consideration for the triangular mesh, so its size constraints should
be chosen so that the triangular mesh has the road polygons has reasonable boundary
conditions. When the quadrilateral mesh has a much lower resolution than the triangular,
it looks like the mesh generator creates bigger polygons in order to avoid small angles,
when looking at ﬁgure 4.9. This lower resolution, together with the small angles, should
be kept in mind because of the uncertainty of the results that will follow. If much ﬁner
mesh is placed at the roads the triangles adapt well, by inserting more points to make
triangles with good angles. The problem with this is that the many small mesh elements
will give longer computation times.
Because the generation of quadrilateral and triangular mesh are not combined in the
mesh generator it is not really possible - within this project - to automatically ﬁnd and
set the best mesh size for the quadrilateral mesh from the triangular size constraints. It
could be done by calculating the side length from a triangle with chosen angle and area
constraints. This is not seen as a serious deﬁcit of the method, since mesh size should not
only be based on the the mesh analyzer output. It is also important to include knowledge
about the phenomenon to be modelled and the needed resolution in diﬀerent model areas.
Selecting mesh size forces the user to consider these things.
4.6.2
Geometry related problems
During work with the meshing and inspection of results, several issues were detected.
Many of them were related to the geometry of the input data to the constraint script and
the output.

are a few cases which can not be handled and are excluded from the mesh.
The case with only one road end line especially occurs at smaller, blind roads which
are not wide enough at the ends to get an intersection marker in the GIS step described

4.6.2.2
Distance between points in polygons
In the mesh generator in MIKE Zero all points given as input in the mdf-ﬁle are included
in the mesh. Therefore mesh generation does not work, or give very bad polygons, when
points in the polygons are on top of or very close to each other. In cases where points are
extremely close, the mesh might look like shown in ﬁgure F.3, where a node and a vertex
are almost on top of each other.
As mentioned in 4.3.2.3, a check was built into the constraint generation script where
the distance between the nodes and the nearest polygon was inspected and corrected.
Later, it was decided that using Simplify Geometries on the road Polygons before inputting
them to the constraint creation script instead of MIKE Zero’s Clean Data. When using
Simplify Geometry, there is a small risk that the tool chooses to simplify one of the four
end points away, which would make the boundary assignment part of the script fail for that
polygon so that it is not included in the mesh. This should be relatively easily be to avoid.
One possibility would be to write a script that after simpliﬁcation checks the polygons to
see if the end points have been removed and reinsert them if they have. Another possibility
already mentioned in the GIS procedure discussion in 3.6, a simpliﬁcation function can
be written ourselves. This function could make sure that end points are preserved during
simpliﬁcation.
The GIS simpliﬁcation method does not take care of minimum spacing between points.
In a few cases, the distance becomes the constraints of the meshing, but in these cases
there are several meters between points. Whether the point spacing matters depends a
lot on the mesh size and on the shape of the individual polygons as demonstrated for
the sharp road turn seen in ﬁgure 4.11. Point spacing after simpliﬁcation with Simplify
Geometries is not found to be a big problem of the mesh generation. In cases where it
becomes a problem, the points could still be corrected manually. Alternatively, the mesh
generator should analyze the resulting mesh and move points a bit apart as was done
manually for the road turn shown in ﬁgure F.1. Doing it automatically introduces the risk
of changing road polygons in ways that are not acceptable. As discussed in section 3.6 it
is not always desirable to automate everything completely as a certain knowledge about
data helps getting the best model results in the end.
4.6.2.3
Complicated polygon shapes
As already mentioned, the method can sort polygons out which has too few or too many
road end lines to make sure that the polygons have a possibility to meshed. However,
not all polygons which have two end lines are optimal for transﬁnite interpolation. This
is for example seen in ﬁgure 4.8. In the mesh generator, the longest of two opposite arcs

end of the road is broader than the other, as the it might lead to very narrow mesh faces on
that road. This is a consequence of using transﬁnite interpolation for quadrilateral meshing

as the ones marked with red circles in ﬁgure 3.10.
The solution to this problem is not straightforward. The sharp angles may in many
cases mean than an error has occurred in the GIS procedure. In some cases, however it
may be a part of the road, for example if there is a turning area. In the ﬁrst case, the
shapes are wrong and need correction. In the second the turning areas are also not wanted
since the mesh lines would disperse as the area to cover becomes wider. Also, depending
on the area and the mesh resolution there is a risk of intersecting mesh lines.
A correction mechanism could for example be included in a simpliﬁcation script as
already mentioned in the GIS discussion in section 3.6. Here the angles in the polygon
border lines could be checked and corrected if too big, for example by removing a point as
was done manually for the parking area error seen in ﬁgure 4.10b. A correction to the road
width in these areas would often also be necessary. In the cases where the road polygon
has more or less the same width along the length it should be possible to compare the
width in the area being corrected with the ”usual” road width and correct if the deviation
is too big. If the wide area is at the end of the road polygon as is the case of the as the
parking area error it would be harder to repair automatically. This is because roads often
are a little wider at the road ends to make room for structures such as turning lanes. The
algorithm does therefore not know where the real road is.
4.6.3
Discussion of quadrilateral mesh type
The choice of transﬁnite interpolation mesh over other methods for quadrilateral mesh
generation can be discussed. An advantage is that it is built into the MIKE Zero mesh
generator which makes it easy to get started with creating combined meshes.
If the
roads have a regular shape without too sharp angles and point constraints the transﬁnite
interpolation can give very good results with the mesh well aligned with the roads.
The drawbacks of the method has already been touched upon. The main problem is
that it is not very robust with regards to which shapes that used for quadrilateral meshing.
If one end is too wide or there is a wide area like a turning road, the mesh might get a
shape that is less than optimal for modelling water ﬂow. Therefore, diﬀerent methods have
been suggested above to cope with the diﬀerent problems. A diﬀerent approach could be
to use another mesh type than transﬁnite interpolation. In section 4.1.3 is mentioned
both direct and indirect methods for quadrilateral mesh generation. As the mesh wanted
here is a combined of triangles and quadrangles, an indirect method is proposed, where
ﬁrst the area is meshed with a triangular mesh, having the road polygons as constraints.
Afterwards an algorithm like the Blossom-Quad (described in Remacle et al. (2012)) can
be used for recombining the mesh elements in the road polygons into quadrilaterals.
In Remacle et al. (2012) it is demonstrated that it is possible to construct a well aligned

can also be created for road polygons this way. It is also likely that it will cope better with
cases where a road end is wider than the other and if there is a turning area as discussed
in 4.6.2.3. Also, the whole step of identifying the four borders for transﬁnite meshing

work as constraints for the triangular meshing, so also for this method the road polygons
should be simpliﬁed.

5.1.3
Boundary conditions
Much of the surface water in the Dyrup area naturally accumulates in Odense Stream
which runs next to the area to the east. The boundary conditions in area 1 and area
2 are therefore all on the lowest areas of the eastern border of the catchments. These
boundaries are Speciﬁed Level boundaries. For area 1, boundary conditions are speciﬁed
in two areas because of the ﬂow patterns in the catchment. In the grid based model, the
boundary condition on the lower eastern corner is 27.0 meter while it is set to 27.1 meter
on the upper eastern corner. For the mesh based model the numbers are 27.55 and 27.0
meter. The diﬀerence is because the models do not have exactly the same model extent.
In area 2, the speciﬁed level boundary condition is set in the bottom of the depression
on the eastern border and the level is set to 18.5 meter for the grid based model and to
18.3 meter for the rest. The speciﬁed level boundary conditions are all a little higher than
the surrounding area, so a bit of water runs into the system from there. All boundary
conditions have a linear soft start of 300 seconds.
5.2
Model Setup
When running the ﬂood model, the aim is not to get as close to reality as possible. The
aim is to compare the performance of the ﬂexible meshes generated in chapter 4 and a
structured, quadratic grid, based on a DTM. Therefore there is no particular emphasis on
choosing all parameters so that the best possible representation of a pluvial ﬂooding in the
Dyrup catchment is achieved. It is also partly due to the fact that only a limited amount
of data is available for the catchment, so no calibration would be possible. Also, the sewer
system in the area is ignored in the model to focus more on comparison of overland ﬂow
results.
The surface ﬂow model is set up in MIKE 21 FM to enable the use of ﬂexible mesh.
The software edition used for the modelling is MIKE 2016 with service pack 3. For all
simulations, the maximum time step is set to 2 seconds an the minimum is set to 0.01
seconds. The simulation time is set to four hours. The inland ﬂooding module is selected
and higher order solution is chosen both for time integration and space discretization.
Advanced ﬂood and dry is chosen and the drying depth, ﬂooding depth and wetting depth
are set to 0.01 m, 0.02 m and 0.03 m, respectively. The precipitation event is input as a
time series with a soft start interval of 100 seconds. The initial conditions and boundary
conditions depend on the area as described above. For all speciﬁed level boundaries a soft
start time interval of 300 seconds is chosen. For parameters where nothing else is stated,
default values are used.

5.5.1
Model setup
After running the simulations it became clear that the simulation time was too short.
Usually the simulation is run for longer time than the rain series to make sure that the
water has settled so water levels are more comparable. The rain intensity of the CDS-rain
is very low for the last period of time, which gives the water some time to settle. When
inspecting time series outputs for the t4q9 and t6q16 simulation for area 1 and for t6q16
and t12 for area 2 it is seen that the ﬂow speeds are very low at the end of the simulations
except in a few small places. In area 2, the water is still ﬂowing down to the eastern
depression along the road Dyrupg˚ardvænget, so the water depth here can not be trusted
too much. This means that the inundation results would not change much apart from
in the mentioned area. Flooded areas might become a bit smaller. As this is the same
for all model runs, the results are still relatively comparable even thought the water at
Dyrupg˚ardvænget should not be considered too much. Ideally, all simulations were run
again with a longer simulation time to make sure.
It is seen from comparing the result maps from area 1 and 2 that there is a big diﬀerence
in the ﬂooding extent of area A marked with a circle in ﬁgure 5.4 and H.7. The diﬀerence
arises because area 1 covers a smaller area to the south west than area 2 does. There is
therefore a larger area to contribute with water in the area. From looking at the area 2
ﬂooding extent maps it is seen that the ﬂooded area in the south west corner is not so
much bigger, which might be the reason why more water is distributed to area A in the
area 4 simulations. Comparing this to the elevation map shows that a boundary condition
should have been placed in the south western to lead the water out of the system to avoid
modelling of unrealistic ﬂoods. The boundary conditions are the same for all comparable
simulations, so this should not impact the results too much.
Buildings are not considered in the modelling. The data are in form of a digital terrain
model (DTM), so height of buildings is not included. However, in some cases houses are
important for ﬂood routing (DANVA, 2011). It could inﬂuence the importance of the ﬂood
routing via roads. In the two model areas most of the houses are single houses, which leave
more ﬂow paths open than apartment blocks or terraced houses. It should be possible,
in a relatively simple way, to create meshes which has building polygons as constraints so
they can be excluded from meshing. Based on experience from mesh generation in chapter
4, there is a risk that increased complexity of mesh constraints will lead to more problems
in meshing (small angles and areas). For the purpose of mesh comparison in these areas,
building constraints do not seem very important.
5.5.2
Numerical instabilities
When inspecting the results the simulations are generally seen to run in a stable way. A
noticeable exception, mentioned in section 5.4.1, is the simulation running with a t6q16
mesh on area 2. In a point near the eastern border the current speeds become unrealisti-
cally high, up to 40.3 m/s. This is not expected since the t6 mesh simulation runs without
problems. The maximal current speeds of both simulations in the problematic area can
be seen in ﬁgures I.1 and I.2. A consequence of the very high current speed is a very
long simulation time because MIKE Zero makes the time step smaller in order to avoid
numerical problems.

5.5.3
Eﬀects of simpliﬁcation
In the results in table 5.2, 5.3 and 5.4 as well as in the produced maps, diﬀerent eﬀects of
the resolution changes are seen.
5.5.3.1
Floods on roads
Overall it can be seen from the ﬂood extent maps that the triangular and combined meshes
give similar results. Judging by the CSI in table 5.5 and 5.6, both for area 1 and area 2
the t6q16 mesh performs worse than the t6 mesh. This is not the case for the t4q9, t12q25
and the corresponding triangular meshes. The diﬀerence in mesh size between road and
the rest of the terrain is quite large for the t6q16 mesh. Therefore there are much more
points on the road for the triangular mesh, which is most likely the reason why it has
longer computation time and better result. The diﬀerence in size is smaller for the t4q9
and t12q25 meshes, which is probably the reason why the diﬀerence in CSI is smaller for
t4, t4q9, t12 and t12q25 simulations.
The representation of road ﬂooding generally gets worse with decreased resolution.
This is to be expected, since the number of points on roads decreases.If a mesh has lower
resolution, a larger amount of data points are included in the interpolation. The resulting
smoothing of elevations masks the fact that the road is often lower than the pavement
next to it. This makes the road a less eﬃcient ﬂow route and results in less ﬂoods on
the roads. This can be seen on the roads in both model areas in appendix H and ﬁgure
5.4 as the road ﬂoods agree less with decreasing resolution. A clear example of this is
seen in the eastern corner of area 1 mark on ﬁgure. Here it seems that the combined
mesh simulations are worse at transporting the water along the road. When inspecting
the heights in the underlying mesh it is seen that it is because the height interpolation
makes the water run oﬀthe street to the north on the coarser meshes.
One straightforward way to improve ﬂow routing on roads would be to increase the
quadrilateral mesh resolution. It would, however, increase the simulation time. Another
option could be to subtract a value - maybe 10 cm - from the elevations in the road areas
to make water more likely to ﬂow there in spite of the averaging from the interpolation.
It would make the water more likely to ﬂow on the roads, but also less likely to leave
the road again. It could also be tried to use another DTM for interpolation of elevations.
In this project a 2×2 meter DTM was used, while they are now available in resolutions
down to 0.4 meter in Denmark. It is possible that it would give more accurate data in the
points. The interpolation method itself also plays a role.

5.5.3.2
Number of points in mesh
It was shown in ﬁgure 5.1 that the number of mesh faces is very important for the simu-

to get the best mesh parameters in terms of angle sizes and minimum areas, the quadri-

on roads because of the interpolated elevations.
It is therefore expected that the t12q9 simulation generally gives better results for the
roads than the t6q16 and t12q25. The simulation time is much faster than for the t6
and t6q16 meshes because of the lower number of elements, but is slower than the t12

with a maximum triangle size of 12 m2. The ﬂood results also look much better than for

but considerably faster than a 2×2 mesh. As mentioned in section 4.5 the t12q9 still has
potential for improvements, which could improve the minimum time step. A mesh like
the t12q9 could therefore be a good compromise between needing to route water on roads
and to know where in the terrain the ﬂoods occur in situations where the 2×2 meter grid
would be too slow.

Discussion
The most important points from the discussions are summarized in the following. For
more details refer to the discussions in the individual chapters.
GIS manipulation
The GIS manipulation process was partially successful. Many road polygons were correctly
identiﬁed, but the method failed in several areas. In these areas, the results were not
suitable for quadrilateral meshing and would have to be removed manually. In order to
avoid the errors, several suggestions for improvements have been made. First, the input
road lines data have to be critically examined to make sure that they show only roads
where the ﬂow direction can be assumed.
The big road polygon that covers all the roads is good in the cases where the roads
are straight and simple and are not too close to other roads. Especially larger, irregular
shapes such as parking areas or driveways to industrial complexes causes problems. The
solution could either be to write a function that could check if roads close to each other
and adjust buﬀering or simply to remove potentially bad areas from the input data.
Creation of lines for cutting the polygons could also be improved in a couple of places
to avoid situations where roads belonging to an intersection are not cut oﬀ. A ”searching
circle” approach is suggested where the area around an identiﬁed road intersection to see
if there is indeed an intersection and where to cutting lines could ideally be placed.
While developing the method a strong focus has been on the automation potential.
This means that it would be relatively easy to implement the method and the suggested
improvements to a GIS script. It is hard to foresee and avoid all future errors, so the
script should maybe me made semi automatic to give users possibility to improve on the
results along the way.
Mesh Generation
Generation of meshes with combined quadrangular and triangular elements for area 1 runs
without problems. In area 2, which contains more polygons, some problems with the mesh
around the road polygon constraints occur. It is seen that no mesh sizes cope well with
sharp road line turns and acute angles. Otherwise it depends on the mesh size where
the minimum edge lengths, area and angle occurs. It is hard to change to road polygons
automatically in order to improve this, as it may change the road polygons in a way that
is not acceptable
A central part of the mesh generation is to identify the suitable road polygons and
deﬁne four sides so that they can be meshed using the transﬁnite interpolation method. At
the moment the method can only cope with roads with two road end lines placed correctly.
59
==PAGE:68==
Chapter 6. Discussion
Methods are proposed which can check the other polygons and deﬁne the usable parts of
them as road polygons.
The choice of quadrilateral mesh type has been discussed. The transﬁnite interpolation
method has the great advantage that it is easy to get started and that it usually aligns well
with roads. The drawbacks are that it is not robust against polygons with a bad shape. If
another mesh generation algorithm was used, the steps for identifying the four sides could
be left out. Also polygons of more varying shapes could be meshed with quadrangles so the
mesh generation step would be less dependant on the GIS manipulation result. The need
for leaving out or manually correct polygons would probably decrease but it is impossible
to avoid entirely.
Modelling
In the modelling results it is conﬁrmed that the number of faces in the mesh is a very
important factor for the simulation time. Another important aspect was seen to be model
stability.
Because MIKE 21 FM regulates the time step in order to avoid numerical
problems, instabilities result in long simulation times.
In the one case with stability
problems, the cause was found to be a steep slope in combination with the mesh layout
at the slope and large water amounts. It could be investigated more if slopes could be
detected automatically and the mesh adapted accordingly.
The results shown that the success of leading water via the roads generally depend
more of the mesh resolution on the roads than on the shape of the mesh. In practice, a
lower resolution leads to get more averaged, higher elevation values in the mesh points
on the roads, which means that they becomes a less obvious ﬂow route. One suggested
solution could be to lower the elevation values in the road areas a little bit. Alternatively
the resolution of the quadrilateral mesh can be increased.

Conclusion and future work
Conclusion
A GIS based method was developed to create polygons at roads without including road
intersections. The method is created for work with normal roads and can handle some
irregularities here. It is not suited for work with paved open spaces such as parking areas or
squares. The method is successful overall, but leaves room for improvement in some places
where the geometry is more complicated. The method and the proposed improvements
have been developed in a way that is easy to automate. There is therefore a potential for
a fast, automatic method for creating road polygons for use in quadrilateral meshing.
The mesh generation works generally well. Transﬁnite interpolation was chosen as
mesh generation method for quadrangles, and a script to automatically make the road
polygons ready for meshing in MIKE Zero was developed.
The script produced good
results, but at the cost of leaving out some polygons from the quadrilateral mesh. The
mesh generation itself went very well except for one case, which was caused by errors in
the input data. When working with mesh constraints like the road polygons, it is possible
to alter the constraints manually to get a better mesh.
The meshes tested against the baseline agreed on where the larger ﬂoods would occur.
Simulations based on triangular or combined meshes were slower than the baseline if the
number of mesh elements was comparable. Several of the tested combined meshes were
worse than their fully triangulated counterparts because the mesh resolution on the roads
became too low.
In a mesh where quadrilateral resolution was increased compared to
the triangular, the road ﬂood representation greatly improved. At the moment, it is not
possible to say how much the ﬂow aligned road mesh improves the result, because the
height values on the roads from interpolation determined the road ﬂow in so many of the
simulations. Further work is needed to determine eﬀect of this.
It was also seen from the modelling that the more reﬁned triangular and combined
meshes have a risk of instabilities at steep slopes. On the other hand these meshes are
considerably slower than the baseline so it is not likely that they will be favored. The
lower resolution meshes seem to run without any stability problems.
Finally, it can be concluded that the ﬁndings of this project can make it simpler and
faster to produce road aligned ﬂexible meshes for use in urban surface modelling, especially
if the suggested improvements are implemented.
Ideas for future work
Many ideas have come up to what could be improved or further investigated on this
topic. Some were already touched upon in the discussion parts. For example, it could
be interesting to use another mesh generation method for quadrilateral mesh such as the

Chapter 7. Conclusion and future work
Blossom-Quad algorithm. An indirect method like the Blossom algorithms are especially
interesting since the aim is to make meshes consisting of a combination of quadrangular
and triangular elements. It is likely that another meshing algorithm would be more robust
to diﬀerent road polygon shapes.
The ﬁndings of McRae (2000) that showed that mesh alignment improved results and
computation speed were based on r-reﬁnement of the meshes. This means that the number
of faces and points in the mesh remains constant, but points are reallocated to ensure the
wanted resolution. In this thesis, it has not really been investigated how the results changes
if the number of elements is held constant. The number of elements has changed between
the meshes, and it could be interesting to see how mesh alignment with the same element
number performed.
It could also be interesting to look into how to cope with the worst mesh constraints
arising from the road polygons, as this has to be done manually at the moment.
The results and discussion in chapter 5 showed that care should be taken with the
mesh close to steep slopes. In order to improve the automated mesh generation further it
seems like an important step to investigate how slopes could be taken into account. This
would make the meshes more robust and useful in a wider range of applications.
Another idea which was mentioned in the discussion in chapter 5, is to include buildings
in the mesh. It could mean that some ﬂow patterns change, and it could be interesting to
see how the results are inﬂuenced.
It could be interesting to see if the geometries used in this project could be used to
improve modelling in other ways. For example the road covering polygon could be used
to change the Manning number on roads as the friction is expected to be less on a road
than in a garden. In an area like Dyrup with a large green area, this could possibly have
an inﬂuence on the result.
