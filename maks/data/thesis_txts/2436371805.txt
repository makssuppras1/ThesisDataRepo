Abstract
Recent advances in wind turbine technology are pushing down the cost of electricity from
wind power. To compete in this market, there is a need for reliable and robust optimiza-
tion to streamline and enhance the wind turbine design process. This thesis presents a
framework for wind turbine optimization in cooperation with the Suzlon Blade Science
Center to meet this demand. The Matlab optimization package minimizes the levelized
cost of energy of a wind turbine via optimization of design variables related to the blade
geometry and basic wind turbine parameters. The package consists of a blade geometry
module created by the Master’s student coupled to blade element momentum, structural,
and levelized cost of energy modules provided by the Suzlon Blade Science Center. The
bundle of modules deﬁne an objective function for which Response Surface Methodology
and pattern search optimization techniques are applied to ﬁnd the blade design and turbine
conﬁguration which minimize the levelized cost of energy. Response Surface Methodol-
ogy under-performs when the design space is large, however reductions in the design space
lead to consistent and desirable results. Pattern search performs well with a large design
space and displays reasonable computational time, though the scaling of computational
cost with problem size is unpredictable. Each method is evaluated and pattern search is
chosen as the preferred method for future optimization. Suggestions for maximizing the
utility of Response Surface Methodology and improving the performance of pattern search
are provided, with an aim towards larger optimization problems and global optimization.
Signiﬁcant improvements in the levelized cost of energy of the optimized designs bolster
the methodologies employed in this package and suggest a promising avenue for future
wind turbine optimization.

Chapter1
Introduction
Wind energy is becoming increasingly cost competitive with fossil fuel powered gener-
ators. IEA predicts the levelized cost of energy (LCOE) of wind power will be 24-30%
lower in 2030 compared to 2014. The reduction will be even greater by 2050, at 35-41%
[Wiser et al., 2016]. In 2017, rapid innovations in offshore wind caused prices to drop 47%
below 2015 auction prices, making offshore wind cheaper than nuclear and gas generators
[Renewable UK, 2017]. Higher hub heights and larger rotor areas are cited as major rea-
sons for these improvements, resulting in higher capacities for both onshore and offshore
turbines [McKenna et al., 2016]. As wind turbines become larger and technologies such
as ﬂoating offshore turbines become more prevalent, design, manufacturing, and logistical
requirements become increasingly difﬁcult to satisfy [McKenna et al., 2016]. At the same
time, capital costs are the most substantial contributors to the LCOE, since wind turbines
alone comprise nearly 64%-84% of the total investment costs of onshore wind projects in
Europe [IRENA, 2012]. The result is an increasing interest in optimization to provide a
methodology for achieving wind energy advancement while meeting the numerous design
constraints.
The high stakes of wind turbine investment spur the demand for increasingly high per-
formance optimization tools. As the ﬁeld of numerical optimization grows, optimization
softwares are being developed to assist engineers and other professionals in the design and
planning processes. Many commercial packages are tailored to meet the needs of particu-
lar industries by combining an analysis tool with an optimizer [Parmee and Hajela, 2002].
However, these softwares are not the only options for optimization. Programs like Matlab
and Mathematica are continually being equipped with additional features and toolkits to
handle more complex and sophisticated problems. Many industries already have licenses
for these softwares, and with appropriate implementation, these tools can play an impor-
tant role in achieving industry objectives.
While much academic research has been done in the ﬁeld of optimization, applying these
solutions to industry problems presents a variety of challenges. One of the challenges that
1
==PAGE:11==
has been cited is a lack of understanding between the optimization engineer and industry
representative [B. and Tus¸ar, 2013]. This challenge is further originates from the absence
of a clearly speciﬁed problem statement and by the intricacies of industry-speciﬁc opti-
mization. Even when results can be achieved, they may not be practical to the industry
due to external factors [B. and Tus¸ar, 2013]. Through cooperation with the Suzlon Blade
Science Center (SBSC), a division of Suzlon Energy dedicated to blade research, this the-
sis seeks to address these challenges and bridge the gap between academic and industry
optimization. This is accomplished through joint problem formulation and critical evalua-
tion of the product.
This thesis documents the creation of a Matlab optimization package for wind turbine
blade design. The ﬁnalized package is applied to a series of optimization problems with
an increasing number of design variables. The package is composed of several modules
coupled to Response Surface Method [Khuri and Mukhopadhyay, 2010] and pattern search
optimization processes [MathWorks, d]. While this thesis presents the optimized blade de-
signs and wind turbine conﬁguration for the largest optimization problem, the results are
largely intended to demonstrate the ability of the modules and the optimization methods
to produce feasible, competitive designs. The ultimate goal of this thesis is the develop-
ment of a ﬂexible package that can consistently solve problems of varying size with an
assortment of design variables. As optimization methods improve, there is a need for a
framework to solve increasingly complex optimization problems related to wind turbine
design. This thesis aims to develop this framework to assist in the advancement of cost
competitive wind turbine design. In this way, the evaluation of the software package de-
veloped for this thesis is perhaps most relevant to the goals of the SBSC.
An overview of optimization components and algorithms are provided in the Literature Re-
view Chapter, with special attention to wind turbine optimization. The objective, variables,
constraints, and inputs of each of the optimization problems are selected and justiﬁed in
the Problem Formulation Chapter. A description of the modules provided by Suzlon and
the implementation of Response Surface Method and pattern search is described in the
Methods Chapter. The Results and Discussion Chapter presents the optimized wind tur-
bine for the largest optimization problem, including performance metrics for each solver.
The package is evaluated and its strengths and weaknesses are identiﬁed. The Conclusions
and Recommendations Chapter provides suggestions for improvements and further work
regarding the material presented in this thesis. The relevance of the optimization package
to the wind industry is discussed.

Chapter2
Literature Review
Optimization is a large and growing ﬁeld with widespread applications. For this reason,
it is essential to identify the components and methodologies most relevant to wind turbine
and blade optimization. This chapter provides information about optimization and how
it is typically performed in the wind industry. The information and assessment provided
will be used to formulate and justify the methods described in this thesis to solve the
optimization problems proposed by the SBSC.
2.1
Components of Optimization
An optimization problem consists of an objective function, variables, and constraints on
the variables. The objective function f(x) is written in terms of the variables x for which
the variable settings that will maximize or minimize the function are to be found. Con-
straints c are functions of the variables expressed as a set of equalities E and inequalities
I. A generalized optimization problem is formulated as in Equation 2.1 [Nocedal and
Wright, 2006].
minimize
f(x)
(2.1)
subject to
ci(x) = 0, i 2 E
ci(x) ≥0, i 2 I
In many cases, the objective function cannot be written in explicit mathematical form.
In these cases, Response Surface Methodology provides a means for approximating the
objective function across the design space through a series of experiments. Finding the
3
==PAGE:13==
maximum or minimum of this approximated function is done using an optimization algo-
rithm [Nocedal and Wright, 2006], for which there are many search methods. Alterna-
tively, a search algorithm may be applied directly to the objective function to search for a
local or global minima. These methods are either gradient based, relying on estimations of
the objective function derivatives, or derivative-free methods, which rely on other meth-
ods to locate the extrema [Nocedal and Wright, 2006]. The methods for solving different
types of optimization problems naturally depend on the characteristics of the problem
[Chehouri et al., 2015]. General categories of optimization problems are unconstrained or
constrained, linear or nonlinear, and single or multiobjective [Nocedal and Wright, 2006].
The class and complexity of an optimization problem depends largely on the resources
available and industry goals. Examples of typical objectives, variables, constraints, and
methodologies used in the wind industry for turbine and blade optimization are provided
in the following sections.
2.2
Objectives for Wind Turbine Optimization
An early approach to wind turbine design was to increase the power coefﬁcient of the
turbine [Chehouri et al., 2015], deﬁned as the ratio of the electrical power to the kinetic
power of the wind. While increases in the efﬁciency of wind turbines have improved
the performance of wind turbines, there are several drawbacks to this approach. First,
blades designed for high aerodynamic efﬁciency tend to have high chord lengths at the
root, high twist angles, and a large taper, making manufacturing and transportation difﬁ-
cult [Chehouri et al., 2015]. Further, the power coefﬁcient is a function of the tip speed
ratio, and so the most efﬁcient performance is only achieved at one wind speed. For these
reasons, the approach to blade optimization shifted from maximum efﬁciency to maximum
power.
Maximizing the power extracted by a wind turbine is a common goal for wind turbine
optimization [Burton et al., 2011], and thus there is a trend towards increasing the rotor
size and hub height to increase the turbine capacity [McKenna et al., 2016]. This is par-
ticularly relevant for wind turbines designed for low wind speed regions, where a larger
rotor is preferred. Larger turbines, however, require more complex logistical services like
custom-built cranes to handle heavy components [McKenna et al., 2016], and overall have
higher component costs. IRENA estimates that the tower and blades together account
for 48.5% of the component cost of a wind turbine [IRENA, 2012]. Further, structural
challenges associated with the increased weight and loads on large wind turbines demand
innovations in design and material science, including strong but light carbon ﬁber blades.
While larger rotors will produce more power, increasing the annual energy production
(AEP) and thus the revenue of a wind facility, the associated challenges of large turbines
will contribute to higher costs. For this reason, a related objective that has been used in
the wind industry is minimization of the rotor weight [Chehouri et al., 2016]. In general
though, larger turbines tend to have lower costs of energy. This is because the contribution
to the AEP from a larger rotor area scales quadratically with the blade radius while a higher
hub height increases the mean wind speed, contributing cubically to the AEP [McKenna
4
==PAGE:14==
et al., 2016]. Another reason for favorable trends towards larger rotors is the economies of
scale of the manufacturing of wind turbines, indicating that larger projects see greater cost
savings than smaller projects [Islam et al., 2013].
A ﬁnal optimization goal is to minimize the levelized cost of energy (LCOE), a com-
mon metric for predicting the cost competitiveness of an electrical generator. The LCOE
considers the total costs and AEP [Mone et al., 2017] to determine the price at which the
electricity would need to be sold to break-even during the lifetime of the generator, typi-
cally twenty years for a wind turbine [Ziegler et al., 2018]. Often used to compare elec-
trical generators, a low LCOE suggests that a plant is more cost competitive than a higher
LCOE alternative. While the LCOE is a convenient metric, real investment decisions are
based on intensive and speciﬁc studies. For example, the LCOE does not incorporate the
value of dispatchable generators, the impact of the current resource mix [EIA, 2018], or
public acceptance of an energy technology. The LCOE often does not consider tax incen-
tives, though these can be incorporated with additional modiﬁcations to the LCOE model.
Since the LCOE is limited to site-speciﬁc comparisons of electrical generators, an alterna-
tive approach called the levelized avoided cost of energy (LACE) is often recommended.
The LACE represents the costs that would have been incurred if a particular generator
had not been deployed [EIA, 2018]. In this way, the LACE incorporates the effects of
the resource mix, dispatchability, and utilization to provide a more robust comparison of
the competiveness of different generators. The LCOE and the LACE can often be used
together to determine the overall cost of a project versus its value to the electricity grid.
Despite the limitations associated with the LCOE estimation, it remains a widely used
metric that can provide useful insight into the feasibility of an energy source when appro-
priately applied. In the context of turbine design, under the assumption that all parameters
remain ﬁxed, the LCOE can be used to assess the impact of alterations to a design and its
impact on the cost of energy. More speciﬁcally, many of the shortcomings of the LCOE es-
timation can be avoided by limiting its use to one particular generator, i.e., a wind turbine.
Further, the LCOE encompasses many design objectives including maximizing efﬁciency
and power while minimizing weight. For this reason, it is a widely agreed that wind tur-
bine optimization should focus less on aerodynamic performance and more on lowering
the cost of energy to make wind turbines more cost competitive [Chehouri et al., 2015].
2.3
Variables and Inputs
The selection of the type and number of design variables is a crucial step in formulating an
optimization problem. For blade design, typical design variables include the chord, twist,
and thickness distributions. Other important variables include basic turbine characteristics,
like the rotor diameter, rotational speed, nominal power, and hub height [Chehouri et al.,
2015]. Variables can be either discrete (integer) or continuous (real number) [Nocedal
and Wright, 2006]. Smooth functions with continuous variables are generally easier to
optimize than functions with discrete variables [Chehouri et al., 2015]. Of course, the more
numerous the design variables, the greater the possibility for improvements in the design.
5
==PAGE:15==
However, a large number of variables demands more computationally costly optimization.
In general, the number of design variables should be minimized while still providing an
appropriate scope and ﬂexibility of the design space.
In addition to design variables, the models that constitute the objective function consist of
ﬁxed parameters that are frozen throughout the optimization process. Depending on the
complexity of the models used to formulate the objective function, the number and type of
inputs can vary. Examples of inputs include airfoil data, structural properties [Bagherpoor
and Xuemin, 2017], and wind data [Hansen, 2008]. The performance of the optimized
design relies on accurate inputs that promote desired properties. For example, a desirable
airfoil is insensitive to leading edge roughness and has good stall behavior [Hansen, 2008].
Likewise, some materials, such as carbon ﬁber, are appealing for use in wind turbine
blades because of their high stiffness and low density [Mishnaevsky et al., 2017]. Finally,
a realistic LCOE model provides good estimations of parameters like material costs and
the discount rate. These examples demonstrate that it is especially important to provide
accurate inputs to the model to achieve the best results.
2.4
Constraints
The constraints on wind turbine design are numerous and originate from logistical, perfor-
mance, and safety requirements. For many kinds of optimization methods, increasing the
number of constraints within an optimization problem increases the complexity of ﬁnd-
ing a solution, and so the most important constraints should be included [Chehouri et al.,
2016]. Examples of typical constraints in wind turbine design include loads and bending
moments, noise, and natural frequencies [Chehouri et al., 2015]. These constraints will be
brieﬂy discussed and their relevance to conceptual wind turbine optimization will later be
explored.
Constraints on the blade geometry, such as the chord, relative thickness, and twist, are
intended to minimize aerodynamic loads, tailor the behavior of the blade, and to fulﬁll
safety and logistical requirements. For example, an appropriate ground clearance dictates
the maximum blade length with respect to the tower height [Chehouri et al., 2015]. Like-
wise, the maximum tip deﬂection must ensure appropriate tower clearance [Bagherpoor
and Xuemin, 2017]. Blade geometry parameters can be constrained to ensure desired
aerodynamic performance. For example, constraints on the blade twist can impact the an-
gle of attack to ensure the stall region is avoided in the event of a gust [Chehouri et al.,
2015]. The maximum chord length must be constrained to facilitate transportation, but
should be large enough to withstand extreme loads [Chehouri et al., 2015]. A minimum
relative thickness may be speciﬁed to ensure a good lift to drag ratio and smooth stall be-
havior [Bak, 2017]. The relative thickness can also be constrained to achieve a smooth
airfoil shape, desired separation characteristics, and appropriate strength [Chehouri et al.,
2015].
6
==PAGE:16==
Minimizing loads is essential to wind turbine design because failures and maintenance
accrue a signiﬁcant cost throughout the lifetime of a turbine. IRENA indicates that opera-
tions and maintenance can account for 11-30% of the total LCOE of onshore wind turbines
[IRENA, 2012], highlighting the importance of limiting loads during the design phase. In
addition to the costs of preventative and corrective operations, turbine downtime resulting
from failure or maintenance halts production and revenue. Gearbox failures result in the
longest downtime between 6-13 days, with an annual failure rate of around 0.125 fail-
ures per year [Sheng, 2013]. Blade failures result in a downtime between 3-11 days, with
an annual failure rate of between 0.125-0.25 failures per year. Over a 20 year lifetime,
for these component failures alone, the total downtime can be up to 90 days. Downtime
costs are especially relevant in offshore wind, where accessibility to the turbine by heli-
copter or by boat is not always guaranteed due to excessive wind speeds and wave heights
[R¨ockmann et al., 2017]. In addition to constraints on the blade aerodynamics, loads can
be constrained in an optimization problem by minimizing stresses and strains predicted
by an anisotropic beam model [Chehouri et al., 2015], or similar beam theory. In relation
to load minimization, wind turbines must also display structural integrity under normal
and extreme wind conditions. Descriptions of ultimate and fatigue loads are given by the
design load cases deﬁned in the international standard IEC 61400-1 [IEC, 2005]. These
loads can be predicted with aeroelastic simulations under normal and extreme turbulence
and subject to constraints.
A constraint related to load mitigation is the overlap of the blade natural frequencies with
the excitation frequencies resulting from the rotor motion [Maalawi and Negm, 2002].
To ensure there is an adequate difference between the rotational frequency and the blade
frequencies, the ﬁrst ﬂapwise whirling mode can be constrained to be higher than the 3p
frequency, or three times the rotational frequency [Chehouri et al., 2015]. This mode is es-
pecially important in blade design because it is typically the lowest frequency blade mode.
Thus, ensuring separation between this mode and the excitation frequencies prevents res-
onance, which contributes to instability and fatigue loads [Maalawi and Negm, 2002].
A ﬁnal constraint typical within wind turbine rotor design is noise resulting from the in-
teraction of the airfoil with a turbulent inﬂow [Liu, 2017]. Noise measurement standards
are described in IEC 61400-11 [IEC, 2012], though regulations regarding wind turbine
noise vary among countries. Since actual noise data can only be obtained after a tur-
bine is installed, theoretical constraints on noise should be implemented during the design
phase. Aerodynamic noise is inﬂuenced by rotational speed [Liu, 2017], so simple models
can constrain the tip speed to reduce noise [Chehouri et al., 2015]. More complex noise
constraints involve speciﬁc airfoil selection [Hansen, 2008], limiting the lift and lift-drag
ratios [Chehouri et al., 2015], and considering the entire structural design [Liu, 2017].

2.6
Optimization Techniques
Upon formulating the objective, variables, and constraints of an optimization problem, an
appropriate method should be chosen to ﬁnd the minima, and ideally, the global minima of
8
==PAGE:18==
the function. Since optimization methods cover a large ﬁeld, this section will discuss and
evaluate Response Surface Methodology, genetic algorithms, and pattern search methods.
All three methods have the potential to locate the global minimum of the objective func-
tion, the variable settings that give the absolute smallest value of the objective function
within the design space. While global optimization is not guaranteed, it is desirable for
many optimization problems to ﬁnd the best overall design within some predeﬁned bound-
aries. These three methods are derivative-free, as opposed to gradient-based, which tend
to locate local optima. The advantage of gradient-free optimization is the ability to operate
on non-differentiable functions, which are common in engineering optimization.
Response surface methodology (RSM) is a framework that was ﬁrst developed through
the 1950’s to 1970’s to approximate and optimize a response function subject to con-
straints [Khuri and Mukhopadhyay, 2010]. RSM entails a series of experiments, known as
a design of experiments (DOE), where various combinations of design variables are input
to the objective function and the value of the objective function for each experiment is
recorded. The experimental data can then be ﬁt with a linear or quadratic model to give
an equation for the predicted response. This response function is maximized or minimized
using a number of techniques. The appeal of RSM is its simpliﬁcation of a complex objec-
tive function to a low order function, however the design of experiments greatly impacts
the quality of the results of RSM. Certain experimental designs have characteristics that
minimize or stabilize the predicted response variance, leading to better approximations.
Orthogonality, rotatability, uniform precision, and robustness are four such characteristics.
Further, design optimality criteria determine the goodness of ﬁt of the mean response to
the predicted response, which is desirable for an accurate response surface [Khuri and
Mukhopadhyay, 2010].
RSM designs of experiments have been proposed for ﬁrst and second order models. Com-
mon choices are factorial designs like 2k and 3k designs, where k is the number of design
variables and 2k and 3k are the numbers of total experiments required in the factorial de-
sign block [Khuri and Mukhopadhyay, 2010]. 2k is a ﬁrst order design suitable for linear
models in which each variable can take on a normalized value of 1, corresponding to the
upper boundary of the design space, or -1, corresponding to the lower boundary of the de-
sign space. Similarly, 3k is a second order design suitable for quadratic models in which
each variable can take on one of three normalized values (-1,0,1) [Khuri and Mukhopad-
hyay, 2010]. Thus, RSM relies on the bounded constraints of each design variable to deﬁne
the design space and to evaluate the behavior of the objective function across the design
space. Variations of these designs include fractional designs, which limit the number of
experiments by a fraction of the total possible variable combinations. This is important
for a large number of variables, since the number of experiments grows with O(n!). Other
DOEs for RSM consist of a partial or full factorial design with an additional design block.
For example, a central composite design is a second order design that consists of a full
or fractional 2k design with the addition of axial and center point experiments [Khuri and
Mukhopadhyay, 2010]. The central composite design for n = 2 variables generated in
Matlab by the ccdesign function is shown in Figure 2.1. The full factorial block consists
of 22 = 4 total experiments. The repeated center point experiment is relevant for stochas-
tic problems, where variability in the response is expected. The ability to customize RSM

to satisfy the solver requirements of robustness and accuracy makes it an appealing option
for optimization.

An alternative to RSM, genetic algorithms (GAs) are popular class of optimization meth-
ods known as evolutionary algorithms. Introduced in the 1960s and 1970s, GAs are based
on the principles of Darwin’s Theory of Natural Selection [Keller, 2018]. MathWorks
provides thorough documentation of their genetic algorithm solver [MathWorks, j]. GAs
deﬁne a ﬁtness function used to select the elite variables in a generation. Elite variables are
those that minimize the objective function. The ﬁtness function corresponds to the degree
of satisfaction of minimizing or maximizing the objective function. To generate the next
iteration, the elite variables experience crossover and mutation. Crossover involves mix-
ing the solutions to achieve convergence, while mutation changes variables randomly to
deviate from a local optimum in search of the global optimum. After a speciﬁed number
of iterations, the process is terminated and the solution is reached. Linear and bounded

The ﬁnal optimization method discussed is pattern search. Pattern search is a direct search
optimization technique, meaning that it does not require gradients, but rather searches the
objective function in predeﬁned directions [Nocedal and Wright, 2006]. Pattern search
is a family of optimization methods that encompasses other methods such as coordinate
search and multidirectional search algorithms [Torczon, 1995]. Unlike coordinate search,
which changes one variable at a time, pattern search applies a mesh to search in many
directions within the same iteration [Nocedal and Wright, 2006]. Like GAs, MathWorks
offers documentation describing the patternsearch function, including the polling method
used to explore the design space [MathWorks, e]. During pattern search polling, the ob-
jective function is evaluated at an initial guess and at surrounding points known as a mesh.
If the objective function values at any of the mesh points are lower than the initial guess,
this point is adopted as the new initial guess and the mesh is extended. This process con-
tinues until no mesh point gives a lower objective function value than the initial guess. At
this point, the process is repeated but the mesh size decreases, narrowing in on an optimal
point. The algorithm typically ends when the mesh size has become sufﬁciently small or
after some maximum number of iterations. Nonlinear constraints for pattern search can
also be handled with an Augmented Lagrangian method, similar to the method employed
for genetic algorithms [MathWorks, m].
Figure 2.2 shows an example of pattern search polling for n = 2 variables [MathWorks,
e]. In this case, the initial guess x0 is provided and a mesh of size 1 is built. The mesh
points are polled in sequence and the ﬁrst polled point that produces a lower value of the
objective function has a value of 4.5146. This becomes the new iterate x1 and the mesh is
increased to size 2. Following an unsuccessful poll, the mesh size is instead decreased by
a factor of 2 until the mesh size becomes smaller than a speciﬁed tolerance.
Pattern search, like all optimization techniques, is subject to fail. This can happen if the
mesh overextends, missing a location in between the center point and the polled point
where a smaller objective function value exists. This can happen even with the very small
mesh sizes that are implemented towards the end of the algorithm. Ways to limit this risk
are to further reduce the stopping tolerances or to poll several points in random search
directions if a poll fails [Chinneck, 2014]. Since pattern search is a general optimization
method, many variations on the method can be implemented to improve the performance
of the algorithm depending on the type of optimization problem to be solved.

2.7
Software
RSM, GAs, and pattern search are only a few of the many optimization methods used in
the wind turbine design process [Chehouri et al., 2015]. For blade optimization, a suite
of software tools are required to model wind ﬂow, aerodynamics, and structural dynamics
[Chehouri et al., 2015]. These softwares rely on CFD, blade element theory, and ﬁnite
element analysis to model the behavior of a wind turbine. Many of these softwares are
either built with optimization capabilities, or can be paired externally with optimizers. For
example, XFOIL can be coupled to a Matlab or Python optimization algorithm to design
an optimal airfoil [Mattar et al., 2016]. As programs like Matlab have expanded into more
sophisticated programming, aerodynamic, structural, and economic models can be written
and optimized within Matlab using features like the Optimization Toolbox [MathWorks,
i] and Simulink Design Optimization [MathWorks, n]. While the optimization algorithms
discussed in this chapter are encompassed in a variety of softwares, there are some stan-
dards that should be upheld when choosing a solver. Generally, an optimizer should be
• Robust - perform well for a variety of similar problems and initial guesses
• Efﬁcient - fast without requiring excessive storage
• Accurate - insensitive to small changes in input data or rounding errors [Nocedal
and Wright, 2006]
Optimization softwares are distinguished by the types of problems they solve, the algo-
rithms they employ, the programming language, and the programming environment. Be-
cause some softwares may be better suited for certain problems, a package should be
chosen to give the best performance within the scope of the optimization problem.

Problem Formulation
Evidently, the variables, objectives, and methodologies relevant to wind turbine optimiza-
tion are vast. To deﬁne the scope of this thesis, it is relevant to discuss the goals of the
Suzlon Blade Science Center. In particular, the work documented by this thesis belongs
to the larger initiative, Suzlon Turbine Optimization Framework (STORM). The goal of
STORM is to change the way wind turbine design is traditionally accomplished by apply-
ing systems thinking to the design process with the overall goal of minimizing the levelized
cost of energy. To achieve this goal, the SBSC desires the creation of a basic blade design
and evaluation tool that is coupled to an optimization process. The result of this package is
an optimized wind turbine that encompasses basic turbine parameters and a blade design
deﬁned by fundamental geometric distributions. All results presented in this thesis have
been normalized as described upon presentation to ensure conﬁdentiality of any designs,
modules, and inputs provided by the SBSC. While these results are important deliverables,
the SBSC is largely interested in the methodology employed in developing the optimiza-
tion package. For this reason, a rationale will be provided for all decisions made, as well
as suggestions for future optimization.
The components of the optimization package include three Matlab tools: one blade de-
sign module created by the Master’s student and blade element momentum (BEM) and
structural modules provided by the SBSC. The Master’s student’s task was formulated by
the SBSC and includes the creation of the blade geometry module, coupling of the three
modules, and optimization of predetermined design variables. The ﬂow of data through
the modules is illustrated in Figure 3.1. The variable inputs to the package are of two
types: geometric, describing the blade geometry via the chord, twist, relative thickness,
and blade length, or turbine, describing the tip speed ratio (TSR) and the nominal power.
The blade geometry variables are inputs to the blade geometry module, which generates a
complete blade design. The blade design and turbine variables are inputs to a BEM mod-
ule, which calculates the AEP and the steady loads on the turbine. These steady loads are
extrapolated to extreme loads, which are inputs to a series of structure modules. The blade

Criteria and Evaluation
To maintain a focus on methodology and to achieve coherence between academic and
industry goals, it is crucial to determine appropriate criteria for the optimization package
that will be delivered to the SBSC. The literature review suggests that an optimization
process should be robust, efﬁcient, and accurate [Nocedal and Wright, 2006]. Additionally,
the SBSC has speciﬁed that the software package should be “ﬂawless, lean, and effective.”
These goals have been synthesized into the following list of package criteria that will be
used to evaluate the ﬁnal product:
• Require inexpensive software
• Well documented and transparent
• Easily navigable and ﬂexible
• Robust against errors
• Consistently produce reasonable results
• Reasonable computational time
These metrics determine the success of the package and so it is essential to specify appro-
priate evaluation methods. First, the requirement of inexpensive software will be measured
by the minimization of costs associated with purchasing new software by the SBSC. This
criteria will be achieved by utilizing Matlab and its Global Optimization Toolbox for this
thesis. Second, the SBSC requires a reasonable execution time for the software. This will
be ensured by minimizing coding inefﬁciencies and making prudent selections regard-
ing optimization methods. Computational time will be measured, and costly operations
will be identiﬁed. Additionally, information about the scaling of computational time with
the number of design variables will be provided to inform future optimization. Third, a
well documented and transparent package will contain appropriate in-code commenting
accompanied by a description of the functioning of the package provided by this thesis. A
live demonstration to the SBSC will be presented by the Master’s student to assist in the
package delivery. Fourth, an easily navigable package should require minimal time and
effort for a new user to execute. Likewise, a ﬂexible package can be readily manipulated
to solve variations or extensions of the original optimization problems. This criteria will
be qualitatively evaluated by discussion with the SBSC. Fifth, robustness against errors is
a benchmark of a well written program. In particular, robustness against errors generated
from unusual or unexpected inputs can be easily tested by the user, and is an important
consideration during the development of the program. Finally, the package is expected to
consistently give reasonable and predictable results. Reasonable results are well-aligned
with known data and are subject to the expertise of the SBSC. Evaluating this criteria will
require consultation with the SBSC on the results of the optimization problems.

3.3
Objective
While there are a myriad of potential objectives to wind turbine design and performance
optimization, the SBSC selected a single nonlinear objective function: minimization of
the levelized cost of energy. Minimization of the LCOE has several advantages in the
context of conceptual design. First, the LCOE model can be either simpliﬁed or extended
to incorporate a variety of factors related to the cost of building and maintaining a wind
turbine. This ﬂexibility facilitates optimization in the conceptual design phase or for more
speciﬁc applications. A related advantage of optimizing the LCOE is the ability to account
for numerous factors while executing single objective optimization. For example, while

a large AEP will generate more revenue, it leads to increased turbine loads, requiring
more material and maintenance costs. Rather than formulating this as a multiobjective
optimization problem (minimize loads, maximize AEP), these competing objectives are
coalesced into a single economic parameter that accounts for both behaviors. Finally,
the LCOE is a widely recognized measure of the cost competitiveness of an electrical
generator, and as such, the results of the optimization will be relevant to the wind industry.

3.5
Constraints on Design Variables
As discussed in the literature review, constraints can complicate optimization and so re-
ducing the number and complexity of constraints is desirable. The constraints on the geo-
metric design variables were provided by the SBSC and are listed below. These guidelines
reﬂect a reference blade design and are intended to produce designs that are reasonable
and feasible, based on experience of the SBSC.
• Twist: constant until max chord, descending or constant after max chord, 0◦at tip,
continuous, positive in all regions
• Chord: ﬁxed chord at root, ﬁxed max chord, descending or constant, continuous,
ﬁxed shape in root and tip regions, positive in all regions
• Relative Thickness: constrained at root, low gradient at root and tip, descending or
constant, continuous, positive in all regions
To simplify the task of an optimization algorithm, the above constraints on the geometric
variables have been directly coded into the blade geometry module. In other words, an
optimizer is not directly informed of these constraints, but rather, the constraints are im-
plicit in the objective function. This ensures that any variable inputs that are tested during
optimization will always produce designs that are consistent with these constraints. While
the objective function is written to ensure that these constraints will never be violated, it
is still relevant to deﬁne the boundaries on the design space for both the geometric and
turbine design variables that the optimizer will explore. Thus, the constraints relevant to
the optimizer can be categorized as bounded constraints on all design variables. The scope
of this search region will be quantiﬁed in the Methods Chapter.
Nonlinear constraints present additional challenges for optimization in general, and the
class of optimization problems explored in this thesis is no exception. As detailed in the
literature review, there are many other possible constraints relevant to wind turbine de-
sign. These include constraints on noise, natural frequencies, and stress. The primary
challenge is that these constraints cannot be explicitly expressed in terms of the design
variables shown in Table 3.1. The secondary challenge stems from the desire to sim-
plify the optimization problems by eliminating nonlinear constraints altogether. In light of
these challenges, the objective of the optimization has been selected to penalize designs
that display undesirable behavior. Unlike a penalty function that responds to violations of
speciﬁed constraints, the choice to minimize the LCOE as the objective innately penalizes
19
==PAGE:29==
undesirable designs. For example, designs that result in high loads are penalized by an
increase in the LCOE resulting from large blade and tower costs. Thus, a design that re-
sults in excessively high loads will likely not be selected by an optimizer. This is a natural
approach, since an advantage of the LCOE objective is its ability to account for a wide
range of factors that inﬂuence the cost of the wind turbine. Further, this method eliminates
the need for explicit nonlinear constraints that would complicate optimization.
With the blade geometry guidelines satisﬁed directly within the objective function and the
elimination of nonlinear constraints, the only constraints relevant to optimization are the
bounds on each design variable that deﬁne the design space to be explored by an optimizer.
The objective function and bounded constraints can thus be expressed by Equation 3.5.

3.6
Optimization Methods
With the optimization problems deﬁned, an appropriate methodology must be selected.
The class of optimization problems can be described as nonlinear with bounded con-
straints. Gradient-free methods will be explored in this thesis since the derivatives of
the objective function cannot be readily calculated and differentiability of the objective
function is uncertain. The methods chosen are crucial to meeting the aforementioned cri-
teria of the optimization package. With the focus on methodology, the SBSC has indicated
an interest in the performance of Response Surface Methodology and a comparison of
this approach with a different optimization technique. As discussed, the principle of RSM
is that a complex objective function can be approximated through design of experiments
by a quadratic model shown in general form in Equation 3.6 [Khuri and Mukhopadhyay,
2010]. This multivariable quadratic function is then subject to a search algorithm to locate
its optimum, for which the optimization problem is also indicated in Equation 3.6. An
advantage of RSM is that the number of required objective function evaluations is ﬁxed,
compared to methods that search the objective function directly for an optimum. A second
advantage of RSM is the potential to locate the global minimum of the objective function
by sampling the objective function throughout the entire design space. This contrasts opti-
mization algorithms that begin searching the objective function at a speciﬁed initial point
and locate a local minimum. For these operations, the initial guess is critical, and unfortu-
nately, difﬁcult to select with any degree of certainty. A related advantage of the quadratic
model is that the global optimum is readily located by the search algorithm because of

the absence of multiple local minima in a quadratic function that likely exist in the actual
objective function. In light of these beneﬁts, RSM is the ﬁrst optimization method consid-

While it is a primary goal of this thesis to exploit the capabilities of RSM, there are disad-
vantages to this method that will be explored. First, RSM alone is not guaranteed to locate

an obstacle in solving the larger optimization problems with RSM if the time required for
each objective function evaluation is too large. This will be demonstrated numerically in
the Methods Chapter.
As a result of the scaling issue and in an effort to provide an alternative optimization
method for comparison, a pattern search algorithm is implemented for this thesis. The
patternsearch function in Matlab is included in the Global Optimization Toolbox. The
appeal of pattern search is the potential to locate a global minimum through a series of
expansions and contractions of the mesh that polls the objective function at each iteration.
Another advantage is the ability to specify nonlinear constraints, which could be useful
for future optimization. Finally, as a direct search method, pattern search does not require
gradients of the objective function, which is desirable for these problems. The implemen-
tation of pattern search will be described in more detail in the Methods Chapter.

Blade Geometry Module
The blade geometry module receives as inputs a vector of several points that describe
the chord, relative thickness, and twist distributions at several radial locations along the
blade. Figure 4.1 shows the ﬁnal locations of the geometric design variables chord DC,
relative thickness DTC, and twist DT. The blade pictured is a generic wind turbine blade

of the design variables will be explained in the section Number and Location of Design
Variables and shown in Table 4.1. The inputs DC, DTC, and DT originate from a design
of experiments in the case of Response Surface Method, or an initial guess in the case
of pattern search optimization. The module then applies an interpolation scheme between
these design points and several control points to complete the blade design for the 66 blade
elements that are evaluated by the BEM module. Naturally, the interpolation scheme that
is selected will have a large inﬂuence on the resulting design and thus its performance.
For this reason, the number of design variables used to describe each geometry, the radial

• simple, not requiring many design variables which increase the computational cost
of optimization.
• ﬂexible, able to produce a wide range of designs to thoroughly explore the design
space.
• predictable, producing designs that, based on prior knowledge, are reasonable and
likely to give desired results.

search, which explore a range of values for each variable, resulting in a vector of inputs

is described by interpolation between two variables located at 40% and 80% blade length,
respectively. For the ﬁrst variable at 40%, a normalized range of values from 0.5 to 0.7
should be explored. For the second variable at 80%, a range of values from 0.6 to 0.8
should be explored. It can be seen that a design might be considered in which the normal-
ized chord at 40% blade length is 0.7, while the normalized chord at 80% is 0.6. Clearly,
these inputs will produce a design that violate the requirement for a descending distribu-
tion. However undesirable this may be, it is also not preferred to limit the exploratory
range for each variable to prevent possible overlap. A simple solution to this problem is
to deﬁne the input variables as a percentage of the previous variable, rather than in their
real units. In the example, the design variable at 80% blade length can be deﬁned as a
percentage between, for example, 5 and 95% of the design variable at 40% blade length.
This solution allows ﬂexibility in the blade geometry module while ensuring monotonic
descent of the blade geometries along the blade radius.
4.1.2
Number and Location of Design Variables
Choosing the number and location of the design variables that describe the blade geome-
tries is important for the quality of the output designs, and is highly inﬂuential in determin-
ing the computational cost of the optimization problems. As will be described, the com-
putational time required to solve each optimization problem using RSM scales nonlinearly
with the number of design variables. Thus, design variables should be used sparingly, and
only included when they contribute signiﬁcant ﬂexibility to the blade geometry module or
produce substantial improvements in the resulting design. Like the choice of interpolation
method, the location of the design variables along the blade length will greatly impact the
designs that will be explored during optimization. In general, these points were chosen
to be equally spaced along the blade and are positioned at locations where there are large
changes in the distribution of the reference blade. This choice enables the blade geome-
try module to produce designs that are comparable to the reference blade, while enabling
variability of the designs at critical locations.
Determining the best number of design variables is not necessarily straightforward, since a
design described by interpolation between only a few variables may not have the ﬂexibility
to generate a wide variety of designs. On the other hand, a larger number of degrees of
freedom has potential to create signiﬁcant improvements in the blade design and wind
turbine conﬁguration, though at the expense of costly computation. For this thesis, it is
preferred to limit the number of design variables used to describe the blade geometries.
The rationale is to limit computational time and complexity appropriate for the conceptual
design phase while simultaneously providing a tool capable of solving larger optimization
problems with minimal modiﬁcations. This ﬂexibility is a criteria for evaluation of the
optimization package and will be later demonstrated.

4.1.3
Control Points
The ﬁnal facet of the blade geometry module is the implementation of control points in the
geometric distributions. Unlike design variables, control points are not considered inputs
to the blade geometry module. Control points are predeﬁned within the blade geome-
try module and are intended to regulate the shape of the distributions in regions like the
root to max chord and the tip to comply with the guidelines provided by the SBSC. The
control points automatically adjust to changes in the design variables and are modelled
after a reference design. The control points for the twist, chord, and relative thickness are
described.
The twist distribution only requires one control point based on the requirement that the
twist at the tip should be 0°. The remainder of the twist distribution is controlled by the
three design variables.
The requirements given for the chord distribution indicate that the designs should have
the same shape from the root to the max chord and at the tip as the reference blade. In
27
==PAGE:37==
these regions, control points are implemented to mimic the shape of the reference blade
while providing smooth transitions between the root, mid, and tip regions. Between the
root and max chord, the chord distribution is dictated purely by four control points that
have been chosen to best replicate the shape of the reference blade. The tip region of
the chord requires a more complex set of control points since there must be a perfectly
smooth transition between the mid region, where the design variables are located, and the
tip region. Thus, the control points at the chord tip must change depending on the design
variables. These three control points are located at 97, 99, and 100% blade length. The
97 and 99% control points are deﬁned to have the same chord percentage relative to the
closest design variable as the reference blade. Thus, these two control points will increase
or decrease proportionally depending on the value of the ﬁnal design variable. The control
point at the tip (100% length) is constrained to the value of the chord at the tip of the
reference blade. This method guarantees a desirable tip shape while ensuring a smooth
transition between the ﬁnal design variable and the tip region.
The relative thickness requirements dictate that the relative thickness is ﬁxed at the root
with a gradient of zero. To achieve this, two consecutive control points are implemented
at the root with the ﬁxed value and slightly smaller than the ﬁxed value. The purpose of
slightly reducing the second control point is to provide a smooth transition between the
root shape and the relative thickness explored by the design variables along the length of
the blade. A similar approach is implemented at the tip, where two consecutive control
points are utilized with values equal to the relative thickness of the reference blade at the
tip. These control points ensure zero gradient in the tip region.
4.1.4
Blade Geometry Constraints
As previously mentioned, to reduce the complexity of the optimization problem and to
maximize the exploratory potential, constraints have been embedded in the objective func-
tion via the blade geometry module. These constraints are essential for this formulation
of design variables because most of the design variables are expressed as a percentage of
the previous design variable. For example, the relative thickness should not be lower than
its minimum for the reference blade. However, for a given design variable of the relative
thickness, it is desirable for an optimizer to explore a wide range, (between 5 and 95%
of the previous design variable, for example). This may produce a reasonable design if
the value of the previous design variable is large, and so 5% of this previous value gives
a new design variable that does not violate the minimum constraint. On the other hand, if
the previous design variable is low, then the subsequent design variable might be too low,
violating the constraint. Rather than limiting the exploratory range and therefore elimi-
nating possible designs that meet the constraints, bounds are implemented directly in the
blade geometry module such that inputs that violate constraints will produce outputs that
do not violate constraints. This choice maximizes design space while preserving the use
of percentages for the design variables to ensure descending designs.
These constraints are implemented at the end of each blade geometry function. For the
chord, any values of the resulting design that exceed the maximum chord limit are con-
strained to this limit. Likewise, a minimum is implemented equal to the minimum chord
28
==PAGE:38==
on the reference blade. The twist may not be smaller than 0°. The relative thickness is
constrained between the minimum and maximum of the relative thickness of the reference
design.

While BEM theory is a widely used method, it operates under several assumptions. First,
the theory assumes that the rotor can be approximated by continuous annular discs, equiv-
alent to an inﬁnite number of blades. This assumption can be corrected with Prandtl’s tip
loss factor. Another correction to BEM theory is the Glauert correction, which accounts
for changes in the relationship between the thrust and the axial induction factor for large
induction factors. Finally, BEM theory assumes that each annular element behaves inde-
pendently of the others and that the inﬂow is steady, i.e., no atmospheric turbulence. This
assumption is not realistic for actual wind turbine operation. For this thesis, the steady
loads are adjusted prior to use by the structure modules to ensure appropriate blade and
tower structural designs.
4.3
Tower and Blade Structure Modules
The steady loads calculated from the BEM module are scaled up to reﬂect the extreme
loads experienced by the turbine as a result of atmospheric turbulence. These extreme
loads are inputs to the tower and blade structure modules. The tower module executes an
optimization problem where the tower wall thickness is incrementally increased until it
can withstand the extreme loads. The resulting tower design indicates the mass of steel
required to construct the tower. A similar optimization process occurs in the blade structure
module, in which the shell and girder are incrementally thickened to ﬁnd the required
amount of material to withstand the extreme loads. The mass of steel required by the

This section describes the implementation of Response Surface Methodology, the ﬁrst
optimization method explored in this thesis. This includes the design of experiments,
linear regression models, and optimization of the response surface. Information about
the scaling of the number of evaluations of the objective function with the number of

4.5.1
Design of Experiments
As described, the blade geometry module accepts as inputs a vector of design variables that
describe the blade length and the chord, twist, and thickness distributions at certain radial
locations. For RSM, these inputs are generated through a design of experiments process.
Several functions exist in Matlab to generate experimental designs. These designs consist
of combinations of integers for which 1 represents the upper bound of the exploratory
range of a variable and -1 represents the lower bound. Each vector of variable settings
can be translated into real values for evaluation by an objective function. Evaluating the
objective function for all combinations of vectors gives a set of data that represents the
objective function at various points throughout the design space. These data can then be
ﬁt with a model which is then minimized to ﬁnd the optimized variables. The goal of
approximating the objective function with a quadratic function is to avoid a signiﬁcant
number of evaluations of the objective function and ideally capture the global extrema of
the objective function.
A common DOE used for RSM is a central composite design. Two types of central com-
posite designs are inscribed and circumscribed designs, illustrated for three variables in
Figure 4.12. These designs consist of a full or partial factorial block, outlined in blue, an
axial block, outlined in red, and the center point. Circumscribed designs provide good
approximations across the entire design space [MathWorks, a], though they require vari-
able settings that lie outside of the speciﬁed range (> 1, < −1) within the axial block.
The extent to which the experiments exceed the bounds of the design space is selected
to ensure rotatability of the DOE, and increases with an increasing number of variables.
In the case where the range of variables to be explored by the RSM are true constraints,
this variability can be avoided by using an inscribed design. The inscribed design has the
same shape as the circumscribed design, but limits the exploratory range strictly to the
boundaries of the design space. An inscribed central composite design is the chosen DOE
for these optimization problems because the speciﬁed boundaries for the design space are
strict.
Upon choosing a design of experiments, the boundaries of the design space for each of
n variables should be deﬁned. The upper boundary (coded by the DOE as a vector of 1s
with length n), lower boundary (vector of -1s with length n), and the central design (vector
of 0s with length n) for the chord, relative thickness, and twist distributions are shown in
Figures 4.13 to 4.15. These distributions have been normalized with the max of the center
design. The dashed lines indicated the radial locations of the design variables for each
distribution, previously shown in Figure 4.1 and listed in Table 4.1. The boundaries on the
design space were chosen in coordination with the SBSC to reﬂect a wide range of designs

4.5.2
Fitting Response Surface
Upon evaluation of the objective function for the full set of experiments, the data is ﬁtted
with a linear regression model. Typically, response surfaces y are described by a quadratic
model with coefﬁcients β on squared terms, interaction terms, linear terms, and a constant,
where x are the predictors subject to lower lb and upper ub bounds, shown in Equation
3.6. It should be noted that a linear regression indicates that the coefﬁcients β are ﬁrst
order, though the predictors themselves are not. In this case, the linear regression meth-
ods search for the best linear coefﬁcients for the quadratic model. Building a model that
accurately represents the objective function encompasses an entire ﬁeld of statistical mod-
elling. For simplicity, several regression methods are tested in this thesis and the method
that provides the result with the lowest LCOE is chosen for each problem. This practice
is intended to realize the full potential of RSM and ultimately maximize its utility as an
optimization method.
The simplest regression method explored is the ﬁtlm function in Matlab speciﬁed with a
quadratic option [MathWorks, b]. This function creates a full quadratic model using an
42
==PAGE:52==
ordinary least squares approach. Relying on this model alone assumes that each term in
the quadratic function has a valid contribution to the estimation of the response surface.
In reality, not all predictors share a relationship with the response. This is measured by
the p-value, where a large p-value indicates that a predictor is not statistically signiﬁcant
and should be excluded from the model. The stepwiselm function in Matlab systemati-
cally includes and removes coefﬁcients from the full quadratic model based on the p-value
[MathWorks, c].
Another method to improve the regression is to implement a robust quadratic least squares
ﬁt. Robust regression provides an alternative in situations where the assumptions of ordi-
nary least squares fail [Penn State Eberly College of Science, 2018a]. A common instance
of this is the presence of outliers in the experimental data. Under ordinary least squares,
outliers tend to have a disproportionately large inﬂuence over the model. Robust regres-
sion applies a weight vector to reallocate the inﬂuence of the experimental results on the
model.
The ﬁnal method explored in this thesis for achieving an accurate predicted response is
to transform the LCOE before completing one of the three regression methods discussed
above. A common transformation is to take the natural logarithm of the response variable
prior to regression. This transformation is useful for capturing nonlinear relationships
between the predictors and the response [Penn State Eberly College of Science, 2018b].
The effect of taking the natural logarithm is to linearize otherwise nonlinear characteristics
of the response data. This method may improve the ability of the response surface to
predict the objective function.
These regression options result in six total types of models to apply to the experimental
data: possible transformation of the LCOE combined with a full quadratic, stepwise, or
robust ﬁt. The beneﬁt of exploring these regression techniques is that they are applied
after the design of experiments, and therefore require only six additional evaluations of
the objective function. Testing different linear regression models is further necessitated
by the unknown nature of the objective function and the importance of a good model in
achieving global optimization. Differences between the optimized design from RSM using
the different modelling options will be quantiﬁed in the Results and Discussion Chapter.
4.5.3
Optimizing Response Surface
Upon performing the linear regression, the resulting quadratic model for the LCOE should
be minimized. This is accomplished with the fmincon function in Matlab [MathWorks, f].
fmincon is a gradient based optimization tool for minimizing constrained functions which,
by default, uses an interior point algorithm. The interior point method searches the middle
of the design space rather than the surface for an optimal point [Weisstein, a]. In this
application, the variables have been normalized and so the constraints are simply bounded
for each variable from -1 to 1 for an inscribed design. The algorithm returns the normalized
variable settings at the optimum value for the LCOE, which can be extrapolated into actual
variable values. Since six regression models were used, six sets of variable settings are
acquired upon optimizing the response surfaces. Each set of variables are used as inputs
43
==PAGE:53==
to the objective function to evaluate the six ﬁnal designs. Of the resulting six LCOEs, the
lowest value is chosen as the preferred design. An assessment of the goodness of ﬁt of
each regression model will be provided in the Results and Discussion Chapter.
4.5.4
DOE Scaling
The factorial nature of the design of experiments implemented in this thesis suggests an
obvious limitation of RSM due to the rapid scaling of the number of objective function
evaluations with increasing number of design variables. To combat this issue, the design
of experiments function utilized in Matlab follows either a full or a fractional factorial
scheme depending on the number of design variables. The purpose is to limit the total
number of experiments required by a full factorial design, particularly for large problems.
While these options can be manually adjusted to give a higher or lower number of ex-
periments, the default ccdesign(‘type’,‘inscribed’) function call has been used to produce
the DOEs for this thesis. Figure 4.20 shows the scaling of the size of the design of ex-
periments with increasing number of design variables. The ccdesign function provides
repetitive center designs relevant for stochastic problems, as shown in Figure 2.1. These
extra center point runs were removed for this thesis, since no random parameters exist
within the objective function. The maximum number of variables optimized in this thesis
is 11, for which 151 evaluations of the objective function are required to complete the
design of experiments. Beyond 15 design variables, the number of experiments increases
substantially, with 524,329 experiments required for 20 design variables. While this may
not be a challenge if each objective function evaluation is very brief, one evaluation of the
objective function requires between 30 seconds to a minute to complete. For a large prob-
lem, this could result in days of simulation time to complete only one iteration of RSM.
Of course, a goal of this optimization package is to provide a software that is efﬁcient and
timely. This issue will be addressed in the Proposal of Final Methods section.

described brieﬂy in the literature review. Pattern search polls the set of mesh points in
the surrounding region of the design space deﬁned by the poll method. The default poll
method GPS Positive basis 2N is implemented in this thesis and deﬁnes a normalized mesh
with ±1 in each variable direction, resulting in a total of 2N mesh points. For example,
three design variables produces the following six point mesh:
N = 3
(4.13)
[1 0 0], [0 1 0], [0 0 1], [−1 0 0], [0 −1 0], [0 0 −1]
If the objective function is smaller at one of the polling locations than the center location,
that location becomes the new center value. A complete poll option can be implemented
where all mesh points are polled and the smallest objective function value is chosen, rather
than the ﬁrst location where a reduced objective function value is found. This of course,
can signiﬁcantly slow the pattern search process and is avoided in this thesis to minimize
computational costs. Another option for minimizing the computational time is to enable
the cache option. This records all previous polling results and uses the value of the ob-
jective function in the cache when polling a location very close to a location in the cache.
This is particularly useful for reducing redundant objective function evaluations and has
been implemented in this thesis. Finally, the polling order can be speciﬁed for random,
success (same direction as previous best poll), or the default, consecutive order (speciﬁed
by the poll method). A consecutive polling order was implemented for this thesis, though
other polling options, including the polling method, could be explored in future work.
Pattern search is typically successful if it terminates for the following reasons. First, the
mesh size has decreased to smaller than the mesh tolerance after an unsuccessful poll,
indicating that the algorithm has narrowed in closely on what is believed to be the global
optimum. Second, the distance between the previous iterations and the mesh size are
both less than an X tolerance after a successful poll, indicating that a sufﬁciently small
step size has been reached and no further accuracy in the variable values can be achieved.
Finally, the value of the objective function in the previous iterations changed less than a
function tolerance and the mesh size is smaller than the X tolerance after a successful poll,
indicating that the algorithm has already narrowed down signiﬁcantly and no signiﬁcant
improvements in the objective function have been achieved. Pattern search implements
default values for the mesh, X, and function tolerances. These default values are on the
order of 10−6, however this degree of accuracy is not necessary for these optimization
problems and results in excessive computational time. The tolerances have been changed
to 10−4 to encourage termination of the algorithm while preserving accuracy of the results.

5.1
Optimized Design - Problem 9
The results for the largest optimization problem are given in the following sections. The
largest problem was ﬁrst solved using RSM and pattern search with the same design spaces
(full bounds) illustrated in Figures 4.13 to 4.15. The impact of narrowing the boundaries
of the chord, thickness, and twist design variables for RSM is detailed, and suggests a
secondary application of RSM in which the initial boundaries of the design space are
further reduced to the boundaries illustrated in Figures 4.17 to 4.18 (reduced bounds). The
results are shown for the largest problem since a large number of variables tests the full
potential of the optimization program.
In general it is difﬁcult to predict the impact of varying the chord, relative thickness, and
twist distributions on the blade structure, AEP, or LCOE due to the complexity and in-
terdependence of the variables. As will be discussed in the Relationship Between Loads,
AEP, and LCOE section, there is a trade-off between a high AEP resulting from large
51
==PAGE:61==
aerodynamic power and the increased costs for the material required to withstand the cor-
responding high loads. The complexity of the blade geometries with respect to the ob-
jective function warrants the use of an optimizer to determine the ideal balance. For this
reason, the focus of the discussion for this chapter will be on the performance of the meth-
ods rather than the details of the optimized designs. There are however, several concepts
related to the blade geometry variables that can be brieﬂy discussed here.
First, increasing the chord c alone will increase the lift force L as indicated by Equation
4.3. The drag D will also increase, but not as much as the lift if Cl > Cd. This will result
in a higher rotor torque, aerodynamic power, and AEP. On the other hand, a larger chord
will also increase the loads on the blade, requiring more material and increasing the blade
cost. Further, for the same relative thickness, increasing the chord length results in a lower
absolute thickness, leaving less space for the inner structure of the blade. If there is not
adequate space for the required shell and girder thickness, the blade design is infeasible.
In this sense, the blade outer geometry that determines the aerodynamic performance is
coupled to the blade inner geometry that must withstand the loads imposed by the blade
aerodynamics. Additionally, this reveals a dependence between the chord and relative
thickness, and is the reason why the ﬁfth optimization problem optimizes the chord and
relative thickness together.
Second, reducing the blade relative thickness alone will generally improve the AEP be-
cause a thinner blade experiences less drag than a thicker blade. Contrarily, increases in
the blade loads resulting from a thin blade will demand signiﬁcant material in the shell
and girder to obtain an adequate second moment of area, thereby increasing the turbine
cost. This is because a thin blade is disadvantaged with regard to the second moment of
area (compared to a cylinder, as previously discussed) due to the reduced distance from the
centroid of the cross section to the shell mass. This suggests that there is some optimum
between increased material costs and increased aerodynamic performance inherent to the
relative thickness design.
Finally, the twist angle β for a blade element is used to determine the local angle of attack,
shown by Equation 4.2. As discussed, the angle of attack is used to determine the lift
and drag coefﬁcients, where maximizing lift and minimizing drag is the overall goal. An
increased twist angle will decrease the angle of attack, which, in the region before stall,
will reduce the lift and increase the drag as shown in Figures 4.6 and 4.7. However, the
twist angle is not a fully transparent variable since the pitch angle contributes to the angle
of attack (Equation 4.2), and is actively varied during operation to give the optimal angle
of attack and thus the optimal power. The difference between the pitch angle and the twist
angle is that the twist angle is local, and can vary along the blade length. The pitch angle,
however, is applied to the entire blade. This indicates that the relative twist angle along the
blade radius is more informative than an offset in the twist, since the pitch angle acts as a
corrective measure to the entire blade to maintain optimal power. Of course, maximizing
the aerodynamic power comes at the expense of increased loads, which must be handled
with appropriate blade and tower structures and will increase the cost of the turbine.
Certainly, the AEP and the turbine costs have conﬂicting effects on the LCOE, however
their relative impact is not apparently obvious and depends of the tuning of the module
52
==PAGE:62==
parameters. The relative importance of the AEP and the blade cost for this series of mod-
ules will be explored in the Relationship Between Loads, AEP, and LCOE section of this
chapter.

These results indicate that there is a degree of uncertainty inherent to RSM. This uncer-
tainty originates from a lack of information about the shape and nature of the objective
function. For example, a quadratic model might not adequately represent the intricacies
of the true objective function. This is especially true if the objective function displays
irregularities, several extrema, or is not continuous across the design space. Further, it is
possible that the minimum of the quadratic model does not align with the minimum of the
objective function, and so RSM alone cannot be said with certainty to be able to locate
either the local or global optimum. In addition to uncertainty about the objective function,
particular regions of the design space could be more or less accurately approximated with
a response surface. Reducing the design space to perform RSM over several iterations
can mitigate this failure and increase the likelihood of locating the optimum. Employing a
variety of linear regressions has also been shown to create improvements in the resulting
designs, and is warranted since there does not appear to be one preferred model. The de-
gree to which RSM succeeds is largely a result of the nature of the objective function and
the variety of methods and models employed. Overall, gradually reducing the design space
seems to be an effective implementation of RSM because it tends to increase the goodness
of ﬁt and improve the ability of the quadratic model to represent the objective function.
In general, these qualities tend to give better designs. However, it has been discussed that
this is not true for all reductions of the design space and is likely inﬂuenced by factors like
the variable boundaries and the type of optimization problem.

5.1.5
Relationship Between Loads, AEP, and LCOE
It has been discussed that there is a competing relationship between AEP and the loads
on the turbine with regard to their impact on the LCOE. Examining Equation 3.1, it is
evident that to decrease the LCOE either the costs should be reduced or the AEP should be
increased. However, increases in the AEP resulting from larger blades or aerodynamic im-
provements will inevitably increase the loads on the blades, and thus the structural costs.
The results of all three optimization methods suggest that the balance of these compet-
ing objectives is yielded with smaller blades and a smaller nominal power than the center
design. To examine these relationships, the blade mass, AEP, and LCOE from the exper-
iments performed in the second implementation and second iteration of RSM have been

5.1.6
Summary of Results
Only the results for the largest optimization problem have been presented, but a summary
of the results for all problems using RSM with large initial bounds, RSM with reduced
initial bounds, and pattern search are presented in Table 5.1. All of the results with the
exception of one problem show improvements in the normalized LCOE compared to the
reference value (LCOE < 1). While these are positive ﬁndings, this does not directly
speak to the robustness of the optimization methods, particularly if the RSM center de-
sign or the pattern search initial guess already give improvements to the LCOE. A more
informative measure of the success of the optimization methods is their ability to produce
increasingly better designs with increasing number of design variables (Problem 5 and
larger). RSM with a large initial design space performs most poorly by this standard, with
many of the problems producing a larger LCOE than any of the previous designs. For
62
==PAGE:72==
problem 5, the resulting design gives an LCOE larger than the reference value, suggesting
that this method fails for this problem, and in general does not perform consistently. How-
ever, it is noted that the best of all results was produced by this method when optimizing
the twist, chord, relative thickness, and blade length (Problem 7). An LCOE of 0.9029 is
nearly a 10% improvement on the reference value. Again, this signiﬁcant improvement
is promising, but does not validate the optimization method. Given the inconsistency of
this method, this result is likely an anomaly and it cannot be guaranteed that these sorts of
improvements could be replicated under similar circumstances using this method.
Both RSM with reduced initial bounds and pattern search show improvements in the de-
signs with increasing numbers of design variables. This is true in all cases with the excep-
tion of the ﬁnal problem, in which the TSR is included as a design variable. In this case,
the LCOE increases from the previous problem, indicating that the reference value for the
TSR was preferred over the resulting value. This could be because the LCOE is sensitive
to small changes in the TSR, and so the inherent inaccuracies of RSM or the stopping
tolerance of the pattern search algorithm will prevent optimization to the required degree
of precision. In general however, the increase in the LCOE between Problems 8 and 9 for
RSM reduced LCOE and PS LCOE is slight and does not speak to the performance of the
optimization methods overall.
For six of the problems including the largest, pattern search produces lower LCOE results
than the reduced RSM. This indicates that pattern search is likely the preferred method
with respect to the desirability of the results. Further, RSM has been shown to fail when
given a large design space and only performs consistently when the design space is signif-
icantly reduced. Pattern search, on the other hand, is capable of producing reliable results
while exploring a large design space. By probing a larger design space, pattern search is
more likely to achieve substantial improvements in the LCOE that lie outside of the fea-
sible region for RSM with reduced bounds. As a result pattern search is more practical
because it can operate independently over a large design space.

Evidently, RSM and pattern search have varying strengths and abilities in the context of
wind turbine optimization. One of the crucial measures of the feasibility of these two
techniques for optimization in the wind industry is the possibility to achieve desirable
results in a reasonable time. Figure 5.17 shows the scaling of the number of objective
function evaluations and CPU time with the problem size for both RSM and pattern search.
The RSM objective function evaluations have been doubled from the theoretical results
presented in Figure 4.20 and also include the six additional evaluations resulting from
the linear regression models. The evaluations and time required to perform RSM can be
predicted in advance based on the design of experiments, as shown previously in Figure
4.20. The same cannot be said of pattern search, for which the number of evaluations of the
objective function depends on a variety of factors, including the initial guess, the stopping
tolerances, the polling method, and the nature of the objective function. Thus, it is difﬁcult
to predict the computational cost of pattern search. For under 11 design variables, pattern
search is more costly than RSM, though the computational time for the largest problem
is still under 24 hours. It should also be noted that the initial guess provided to pattern
search already had evaluated to a low LCOE, and the convergence plot given in Figure
5.13 suggests that the polling process did not reveal signiﬁcant changes in the objective
function. Again, this could be the result of locating a local minima, or perhaps the initial
guess was particularly strong. In any case, providing a worse initial guess could further
increase the computational time required by pattern search to locate the optimum. This
should be further tested to both determine the true computational cost of pattern search
and to test its ability to locate the global optimum. For over 15 design variables, it has
been demonstrated that RSM becomes exceedingly costly, and would most likely not be
64

the preferred method. While pattern search is executed within a reasonable time frame for
these experiments, the conclusion that pattern search is likely less costly than RSM for
large problems may no longer be valid if the stopping tolerances are lowered, the initial
guess is changed, or the number of variables is increased.
5.3
Package Evaluation
In keeping with the goal of providing a useful software package to the SBSC for opti-
mization of wind turbines and blades, the package should be evaluated with respect to the
criteria described in Chapter 3. As demonstrated, the three methods presented perform
differently in terms of the quality of the results and the computational expense. Overall,
pattern search has been shown to give the best results, and while the CPU time is larger for
pattern search than for RSM, it remains within a reasonable limit for the problems solved.
For this reason, the package will be evaluated with a focus on pattern search optimization.
First, the software should be inexpensive, which, as already discussed, has been accom-
plished by the use of Matlab, with the addition of the Optimization Toolbox, the Global
Optimization Toolbox, and the Statistics and Machine Learning Toolbox. Second, the
code should be well documented and transparent. This has been accomplished by thor-
ough commenting of the code and a brief description in the heading of each script. The
software has also been presented in person to the SBSC. The opportunity to demonstrate
and discuss the code makes the tool more useful to the SBSC and successfully bridges
the gap between academic and industry research. It also accomplishes a third criteria,
which is navigable and ﬂexible software. In particular, the code was written to be able to
solve a new optimization problem with any combination of the design variables used in
this thesis. The number and location of the geometric design variables can also be readily
changed. The pattern search options are ample and easily dictated, though require some
prior knowledge to select the best settings. The ﬂexibility of the software makes it readily
applicable for similar types of optimization problems that materialize within the STORM
project. A fourth criteria is that the package should be robust against errors. The only
known error exists in the blade structure module, in which a very thin blade input does
not have the internal space to accommodate the required shell and girder thickness. This
error can be avoided by determining more appropriate boundaries on the relative thickness
variables. Fifth, the software should consistently produce reasonable results. It has been
demonstrated in solving the nine optimization problems that the blade designs and turbine
setup results are feasible. This is of course subject to appropriate inputs and constraints.
Finally, the SBSC requires a reasonable execution time for the software. As shown in
the Scaling section, pattern search is more computationally costly than RSM, though the
largest problem is still solved in under 24 hours. While the scaling of pattern search com-
putational time for more than 11 variables is unknown, it has been seen that the scaling
of the design of experiments for RSM is prohibitively high. Thus, pattern search remains
the preferred method for initial experimentation with larger optimization problems. The
structure module is the most expensive function, requiring around 30 seconds per evalu-
ation, though this varies depending on the blade geometry. The code has been written as
65
==PAGE:75==
efﬁciently as possible, and if statements have been implemented wherever possible to skip
any redundant or unnecessary computations depending on the problem type. Disabling the
graphical user interfaces implemented in the BEM and structure modules could speed up
computational time. Opportunities for parallel processing or faster computation may be
possible, and should be considered by the SBSC for solving larger problems.
5.3.1
Strengths and Limitations
Overall, the development of the framework was successful, and the software meets the
criteria outlined by the SBSC. One of the greatest strengths of the software is its ability
to be easily manipulated to include different design variables. The processes are stream-
lined, and so it is easy to navigate the software and the ﬂow of data is logical. Another
strength of the software is the quality of the results, particularly for pattern search. The
pattern search optimizer consistently generates improvements in the objective function for
an increasing number of design variables. Further, the optimized designs show signiﬁcant
improvements compared to the reference value, indicating that there is vast potential for
LCOE optimization within wind turbine design. While this is encouraging and opens up
a new avenue for wind turbine design, it raises the obvious question of global optimiza-
tion, which is not guaranteed by the optimization package. Global optimization in general
is difﬁcult to guarantee, but several suggestions will be discussed in the ﬁnal chapter to
increase the likelihood of locating the global optimum.
A limitation of the optimization package, as with most optimization problems, is the com-
putational time. While this is somewhat unavoidable, the computational time has been
minimized through a number of choices regarding the blade geometry module and the
optimization methods. For example, the number of variables used to describe the blade
geometries was signiﬁcantly reduced to only those required to capture a reasonable shape
for the distributions. For RSM, the default design of experiments provided by Matlab’s
ccdesign function that limits the DOE to fractional factorial designs for larger numbers of
variables was used. For pattern search, the cache option was selected and the tolerances
were increased to encourage the algorithm to terminate. Consistent with the expectations
of the SBSC and criteria for optimization in general, minimizing the computational time
was a priority throughout the software development. Further improvements to the compu-
tational time are possible and should remain a priority for future optimization.

Chapter6
Conclusion and Recommendations
The task of this thesis was to develop a framework for blade and turbine optimization. The
framework has been described, including exploration of Response Surface Method and
pattern search optimization in Matlab. The results for the largest optimization problems
have been provided, including performance metrics for the modules and the optimizers.
Most importantly, the package has been assessed and the beneﬁts and disadvantages of
each method have been discussed. From these ﬁndings, several conclusions and recom-
mendations will be made to guide future wind turbine optimization by the SBSC, and
within the wind industry in general.
6.1
Recommendations
First, the interpolation method is critical to the exploration of the design space, particu-
larly when the geometric variables are described in only a few points. The interpolation
scheme implemented by the pchip function has been shown to produce predictable, mono-
tonic distributions. While the interpolation schemes implemented in pchip between two
or three points may be adequate in the conceptual design phase, the number of design
variables should likely be increased for optimization problems in which the results will di-
rectly inform blade design. First, increasing the number of design variables that describe
the blade geometry makes the choice of interpolation method less relevant because more
blade elements will be deﬁned via optimization rather than interpolation. The only con-
sideration in increasing the number of design variables is the added computational cost,
though this is likely outweighed by the substantial economic gains associated with even
a small reduction in the LCOE. To roughly quantify these gains, consider a wind turbine
with a 20 year lifetime and an AEP of 10,000 MWh/yr. A small improvement to the
LCOE of e 0.01/MWh will save e 2,000 over the lifetime of the single turbine. From an
67
==PAGE:77==
industry perspective, this kind of gain warrants thorough investigation at the expense of
computational time and power.
Second, the choice to minimize LCOE as the objective has shown to be fruitful, but comes
with the added requirement that the cost model is accurate. For this reason, the cost model
should be critically examined. The model should include as many factors as possible and
continually be updated with the most current parameters. Of course, under the assumption
that the cost model is accurate and robust, the results presented in this thesis indicate that
there are substantial gains to be made from optimizing the LCOE. It is recommended to
continue this avenue of optimization with special attention to the completeness of the cost
model.
Third, reducing the design space for RSM tends to give better and more consistent results.
Further, testing the six different regressions reveals better designs and only requires several
additional evaluations of the objective function. In all cases however, RSM fails to produce
designs with an LCOE as low as pattern search within the same design space, and thus it
can be said that RSM fails to locate the global minima. In general, RSM is not robust for
these optimization problems, possibly because of the nature of the objective function. For
this reason, a direct search algorithm such as pattern search is recommended over RSM.
This is especially true if the number of design variables should increase, since the scaling
of the design of experiments leads to prohibitively large computational costs. Overall,
pattern search is the preferred optimization method from this thesis.
Fourth, the options available to the pattern search algorithm in Matlab demand more thor-
ough investigation. In particular, the polling method and polling order should be explored
for the best options. In the event that locating a global optimum is preferred over mini-
mizing computational time, complete polling should be enabled. Fortunately, there is an
option to parallelize computation of the objective function when implementing complete
polling for pattern search [MathWorks, k]. This is strongly recommended for future op-
timization by the SBSC because it increases the likelihood of locating a global optimum
while minimizing the computational time. A related topic is the implementation of nonlin-
ear constraints, which can also be parallelized to reduce computational time [MathWorks,
k]. For future optimization, constraints on loads, noise, or natural frequencies may need to
be implemented. This can be accomplished by writing a function for which the speciﬁed
nonlinear equality and inequality constraints are to be satisﬁed by pattern search [Math-
Works, d]. Clearly the options and scope of pattern search have yet to be fully exploited in
this thesis, and so these possibilities should be considered by the SBSC when implement-
ing this tool.
Fifth, pattern search is not guaranteed to ﬁnd the global optimum. However, there are
options that will increase the chance of global optimization, such as enabling complete
polling, as mentioned. In this thesis, the initial guesses were generally strong, which could
indicate that pattern search was only locating a local optimum. A way to test the ability of
pattern search to locate the global optimum is to determine if the resulting design is insen-
sitive to the initial guess. That is, the user should provide a weak initial guess to determine
if pattern search locates the same design. This analysis is recommended to test the merit
of pattern search as a global optimization tool for these types of optimization problems.
68
==PAGE:78==
Further, the Global Optimization Toolbox in Matlab contains several different solvers, in-
cluding genetic algorithms, particle swarm, and multistart optimization [MathWorks, i].
These solvers may perform well for the type of optimization problems presented in this
thesis and should be considered as alternatives to pattern search or complementary in the
goal of global optimization.
Sixth, it is impossible to predict the number of objective function evaluations required for
pattern search to terminate. Depending on the degree of accuracy required, the X, function,
and mesh tolerances can be increased to encourage termination or decreased to prolong ter-
mination. Naturally, a faster termination may decrease the chance of identifying the global
optimum. A related option for pattern search is to increase the mesh expansion factor to
explore a wider region when a poll has been successful. This can increase the chance of
escaping a local minimum to ﬁnd the global optimum, though will prolong the algorithm.
Likewise, reducing the mesh contraction factor will result in more thorough polling of the
design space, but will lengthen the computational time. For the purpose of this thesis,
the focus was on minimizing computational time while still achieving improvements in
the LCOE. In the future, the degree to which the stopping tolerance and mesh expansion
and contraction factors impact the quality of the results should be explored by the SBSC
upon determining standards for computational time and global optimality. The same is
true when considering the number of design variables and the size of the design space for
future optimization.
From these conclusions, it is recommended that the next topics to explore should be
• implementing nonlinear constraints.
• selection of the best polling method/polling order.
• the options recommended in this chapter to achieve global optimality.
• increasing the number of design variables.
Of course, these recommendations are intended to improve the robustness of pattern search
on the blade and turbine optimization problems presented in this thesis. Other paths may
be taken to achieve additional, related objectives of the SBSC.
6.2
Relevance
The work presented in this thesis has been tailored to meet the demands of the SBSC and
to bridge the gap between academic and industry optimization. However, this thesis is also
intended to address greater demands within the wind industry and the ﬁeld of optimization
by documenting the creation of a software for wind turbine optimization. Speciﬁcally, the
development of the blade geometry module, coupling of the modules, and employment of
RSM and pattern search have resulted in a useful methodology for related optimization
69
==PAGE:79==
within wind turbine design, while simultaneously revealing important gaps in the prac-
tice and knowledge of optimization within wind energy. Perhaps most signiﬁcantly, the
improvements in LCOE demonstrated in this thesis suggest that LCOE optimization has
yet been to be exploited, though its potential to increase the cost competitiveness of wind
energy is substantial. Paired with increasing expertise in global optimization methods,
LCOE optimization could potentially revolutionize wind turbine design, further increas-
ing the competitiveness of wind generation compared to other electrical generators. The
framework provided in this thesis and the suggestions for future work thus contribute to
both academic and industry development of wind turbine optimization.
