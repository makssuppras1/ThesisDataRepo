Abstract
Hanger cables are an integral part of a suspension bridge and the associated lifetime is
highly dependent on fatigue exposure, often localised at cable clamps or sockets, caused
by bending and transverse forces. The current method of obtaining this behaviour is by
extensive testing of cables and sockets (DS/EN1993-1-11, 2007). Obtaining a numerical
way of assessing hanger stress properties could optimize the fatigue determination pro-
cess. In this thesis, a multi-scale concept is applied to obtain an overview of the internal
stress distribution in a hanger cable. The concept involves creating models of diﬀerent
complexity to understand the problem at hand, where forces experienced by a global
system can be transferred to a speciﬁc localised system. In this thesis, a simpliﬁed global
model and a complex local model of a suspension bridge are created. Initially, a global
beam model of the Great Belt link suspension bridge is created, with the purpose of ob-
taining section forces in the hanger cables. The suspension bridge model is modelled in
its dead load geometry and excited by an extreme wind load, thus obtaining said forces.
A general beam model is necessary, due to the suspension bridge cable system inherently
being non-linear, thus the forces experienced by a hanger cable are complex to predict.
To implement the hanger section forces, a representative Great Belt link hanger cable
is modelled. The rope type used for the hanger cables on the bridge is of the type full-
locked coil rope. This cable is modelled in Abaqus to determine the stress distributions
occurring in said model. A reduced version of the most common cable found on the
Great Belt link was used, to reduce computational time. Large stress concentrations are
expected from the interaction of cable wires, where wires can either experience point, line
or surface contact due to the geometry and composition of a locked coil cable. This is
conﬁrmed by the results as well, where especially point contact induces large localised von
Mises stresses. One wire, in particular, produced a von Mises stress which was 43% larger
than the expected Navier’s stress at that given location. It is concluded that the model
can be used as a tool to aid in understanding wire interaction and resulting stresses,
which could be advantageous in fatigue analysis.
The cable model displayed a moment dissipation behaviour as well when exposed
to bending, where the moment magnitude gradually diminished towards the centre of
the cable length. Potential further work could include looking into this behaviour in
particular, as well as tuning the many parameters used to deﬁne the cable composition.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Cables are used as a structural member in many diﬀerent ﬁelds, where a light element
capable of handling immense tensional forces is needed. These abilities are especially
utilized in diﬀerent types of bridges, e.g. cable-stayed and suspension bridges. Cables in
these environments can be exposed to harsh conditions, where vibrations can be induced
from many diﬀerent sources, leading to a deterioration of the cable through fatigue. This
expedited fatigue due to the environment is most likely to occur at anchorages, sad-
dles or clamps, where ﬂexural and transverse stresses can be expected, as mentioned in
DS/EN1993-1-11, 2007. The stresses induced at these points can be hard to account
for when determining the lifetime of a cable since the fatigue is highly aﬀected by the
speciﬁc structural cable design and the cable cross-section speciﬁcally. According to ibid.,
the eﬀective fatigue detail category, used to estimate the lifetime of the cable, should be
determined through tests which represent the actual conﬁguration of the structural cable
design. The ﬂexural and transverse stresses likely to occur should be reproduced and the
detail category should be evaluated. It goes without saying that this is a very resource
demanding process. If tests are not possible, tabulated values can be used to determine
the fatigue detail category, which is given for a small selection of cable types.
Determining fatigue parameters for a cable without a laborious test setup could be
advantageous when designing constructions where cables are included in the structural
design. A computer model, where the cable behaviour and interaction between wires
in the cable are accounted for, can possibly circumvent the elaborate and resource de-
manding testing process, by simulating the cable conﬁguration instead. A common com-
putational method used to simulate structural behaviour is the Finite Element Method,
which is widely used in the construction industry when addressing complex mechani-
cal behaviour. A sophisticated Finite Element model could possibly approximate the
simulated equivalent of a test setup, where the conﬁguration of the cable is considered.

CHAPTER 1.
INTRODUCTION
1.2
Chain of Thought
In this thesis, the structural cables acting as hanger cables in the Great Belt Bridge are
investigated. A procedure to determine the forces experienced by the hanger cables needs
to be determined, to be able to apply a grounded approach to the fatigue problem. The
hanger cables in question are located on the Great Belt Bridge, which is a suspension
bridge. Suspension bridges are utilised for long unsupported spans, where other bridge
solutions are not possible. Suspension bridges are superior to other bridges in their abil-
ity to cover long distances without the need for vertical support. However, this is not
without any drawbacks, as the suspension bridge’s abilities are made possible by a system
of connected cables between the pylons, the anchoring points, and the deck of the bridge.
This cable system entails a highly non-linear response to external loads which can make
the overall behaviour of the bridge hard to foretell. The load situation for a given hanger
cable can therefore be hard to predict, especially in load cases where the bridge is loaded
transversely, e.g. transverse wind exposure. Because of this, it is therefore deemed nec-
essary to construct a simpliﬁed model of a suspension bridge to determine realistic load
situations for a hanger cable. The suspension bridge model can be exposed to loads where
the non-linear behaviour is taken into account, which can provide valuable information
when determining fatigue stresses in the hanger cable.
Deterioration of hanger cables is expected over time due to dynamic loads which are
the main cause for cracking in fatigue analysis. This deterioration can be intensiﬁed sig-
niﬁcantly if the cable is exposed to loads inducing oscillatory motions. To determine the
eﬀect of external loads in a hanger cable, a local version of the cable ought to be modelled
as a solid structure. This model will include the wire composition and geometry of the
cable as well as the ability to simulate contact between each wire. Through a solid model
of the cable, the exact location and magnitude of stresses induced by external forces are
determinable, creating a strong tool for fatigue analysis. The process of transferring ex-
ternal forces aﬀecting a suspension bridge into internal forces in the hanger cable, which
can be used in the local model of the cable, is shown in ﬁgure 1.1.
This thesis will conceptualise a multi-scale approach to hanger cable analysis, by ac-
counting for suspension system behaviour, when determining the internal stresses in a
cable.

Chapter 2
Theoretical background
2.1
The Suspension Bridge
The eastern part of the Great Belt Bridge is a suspension bridge, consisting of 3 spans.
The structural composition of a suspension bridge mainly consists of the pylon towers,
anchoring points, and a cable system, namely the main cable and the connected hanger
cables which carry the bridge deck. In order to carry the load of the bridge deck, the main
cable is elevated above sea level by the pylon towers and is supported at each end of the
cable by anchors embedded into the ground. This section will focus on the formulation
and behaviour of the relevant structural components required for a suspension bridge in
relation to this thesis.
2.1.1
The Cable as a Structural Member
Cables are widely used for construction purposes, in both dynamic and static environ-
ments. Especially in relation to bridge constructions, where large demands in both tension
and compression need to be met, the cable is utilized for its strong tensile strength and
high ﬂexibility. A cable can not carry any loads in compression, because of its low resis-
tance to bending. If a cable is subject to a non-tensile load, the geometry of the cable will
change into a shape where the load is carried axially. A result of this is the characteristic
cable curve (also known as the catenary curve), which occurs when a cable is suspended
between two points and is subject to the dead load of the cable, induced by gravity. A
suspended cable will sag vertically due to a given load, thus the suspended geometry of
a cable is very dependent on the distribution of the vertical load.
2.1.1.1
The Equivalent Beam
In contrast to linear elastic beams, the behaviour of the cable geometry is highly non-
linear and the method of superposition is therefore unusable in relation to deformed
cable shapes. A cable can be described by a corresponding beam, which will be subject
to a stiﬀness magnitude determined by the applied load and geometry of the given cable
(Gimsing and Georgakis, 2011). The formulation of this equivalent beam can be seen
in ﬁgure 2.1. The equivalent beam creates an easy connection between applied loads
to a cable and the corresponding structural cable forces. If the distribution of the line
load q(x) along a cable is known, these forces can be applied to a beam instead, where
the internal forces of the beam are converted to forces experienced by the cable. The

2.3
Numerical Applied Theory
When modelling large or complex structural systems, an analytical approach can be hard,
if not impossible. Numerical approaches exist where these problems can be solved by dis-
cretizing a system into smaller components which communicate through an analytical
approach. The discrete parts of the system are called elements and the method used to
solve these problems in this thesis, is called the ﬁnite element method (FEM).
The FE method is used in multiple ﬁelds, including the structural, and is a strong tool
when knowledge of the behaviour of a complex system is desired. The FEM approach for
structural solutions characterises the static system by the equilibrium shown in equation
2.29:
K · V = R
(2.29)
Where K is the stiﬀness matrix and describe overall stiﬀness of the system, V is the
displacement vector for the system and R the load vector. If the displacements of the
system are known, the resulting forces can easily be calculated, but often this is not the
case. Instead, the loads applied to a system are known and knowledge of the displacement
is desired. Diﬀerent approaches to solve the system are used depending on the problem.

The implicit method approach will always try to satisfy equation 2.30 for every time
step ∆t, when determining displacements in a dynamical structural system. The implicit
method will have an initial guess of the state of the system at a speciﬁc time step,
typically using the Newmark time integration method, and will thereafter use iterations
until a certain margin of error has been reached. The iterative approach is necessary
when a non-linear response occurs from external loading of a structural system, which
is often approached using the Newton-Raphson method. For very non-linearly behaving
structural systems, the implicit method can be computationally heavy because of its
many iterations. Therefore, the implicit method can be undesirable if highly non-linear
behaviour is expected.
2.3.2.2
Explicit
The explicit methodology is a strong tool, when applied to dynamic problems.
The
method is applied by solving the equation of motion explicitly, by dividing the response
into discretized time pieces ∆t. In contrast to the implicit analysis, no iterative steps

2.3.3.2
Contact in Abaqus/CAE
Contact implementation diﬀers between diﬀerent software solutions, thus prior to apply-
ing contact in a software, general knowledge on how contact mechanics are implemented
should be obtained and Abaqus is no diﬀerent. In Abaqus it is possible to apply general
contact, implying that Abaqus is establishing contact, where it sees ﬁt. Applying this
type of contact might lead to issues, thus Abaqus allows the user to deﬁne where contact
should be applied, described as contact pairs. The contact pair is computed by a master-
slave contact algorithm, where one surface, the master surface, is allowed to penetrate the
other, slave surface. This works much like the mass/surface relation described in section
2.3.3.1. Penetration can be seen in ﬁgure 2.18, where the master surface penetrate the
slave surface. Obtaining proper contact is thereby very dependent on this relation. If
the slave surface is poorly meshed, the master surface unrealistically penetrate the slave
surface. This becomes pronounced with diﬀerent geometries, where sharp corners or dif-
ferent curvatures of the surfaces can occur. In order to retain proper surface interaction,
the following rules should be followed:
1. The slave surface should have considerably ﬁner mesh.
2. The slave surface should be softer or of similar hardness.

The master/slave surface contact is maintained by an appropriate contact formula-
tion. Described earlier was the Lagrange Multiplier (hard kinematic) and the penalty
formulation, both available in Abaqus. The Lagrange multiplier has the highest precision
as the contact constraint is strictly enforced, however this requires a very reﬁned mesh
and solutions might not converge. The penalty method instead allow a small deviation
from the constraint, thereby inducing a small error, but have higher convergence pos-
sibilities and does not impact incrementation as much. Abaqus/Explicit does not allow
for a strictly enforced Lagrange method, thus the penalty method is the general choice
in explicit modelling. When deﬁning the interaction properties, a tangential and normal
behaviour should be chosen. The tangential option behaves with respect to the frictional
capabilities of the surfaces.
When modelling 3D solids, continuum elements are applied, noted C3D in Abaqus.
The continuum elements utilized in this thesis are C3D8R and C3D10M, as these element
types are often advantageous when modelling contact. The C3D8R element is an 8 node
linear brick element with reduced integration. The reduced integration results in only 1
integration point being present, thus lowering the computational time. Reduced integra-
tion elements can be prone to the hour-glassing eﬀect. The implications of hour-glassing
can be signiﬁcant, when few elements are responsible for a bending eﬀect, thus reduced
integration elements should be applied in ﬁner meshing. With an ﬁne mesh, the C3D8R
element allow for modelling of complex geometry with low memory usage.
Contact problems often cause stress concentrations, which second order elements
model with higher accuracy. Second order elements have mid-side nodes, which increase
their accuracy, but also increase the memory usage.
Second order elements are thus
favourable for small, but complex contact problems. However, most elements do not
comply well with all contact formulations. The tetrahedron element C3D10M is there-
fore advantageous, when modelling contact, as the modiﬁed formulation allow for correct
modelling of diﬀerent contact formulations. The tetrahedron elements has a high accu-
racy when modelling complex surfaces, compared to the brick, but also requires a large
amount of elements with high memory usage.

Multiscale model of the Great Belt link
Suspension System
A ﬁnite element model of the Great Belt Link suspension system is created. The purpose
of the model is to obtain a detailed insight of hanger behaviour by dividing the model
in diﬀerent scales. The idea behind analysing the bridge in diﬀerent scales is to create a
general model, highlighting an area of interest. The area of interest is then modelled in
further detail.
The method implies constructing a general model, providing a global illustration of
the bridge behaviour. The global bridge behaviour is obtained with a coarse beam model
of the suspension system. A general model can provide an impression of severe loading
conditions for diﬀerent cables. A chosen cable can then be analysed in greater detail. In
the present model, this entails a reﬁnement of the cable boundary by the implementation
of a more detailed bridge girder. The model is then excited dynamically in order to obtain
representative load conditions of a hanger cable. Results from this model are intended
implemented in a complex solid element model of a cable segment, covered in chapter 5.

Mesh & Elements
The global model is described as a beam bridge, referring to the utilized element type
"beam".
Beam elements are often used when a global structural system is analysed,
because of their simple formulation and low computational demand. Beam elements de-
scribe the general behaviour of a structural member with a given constant cross-section
and stiﬀness. Beam elements formulated in 3-dimensional space, are generally elements
with 6 degrees of freedom in displacement and bending around all three axes at both ends
of the element. The applied beam element in Abaqus is B31, referring to a 2-node linear
beam in 3D space. The element is shear ﬂexible, noting that it allows shear deformation.
The mesh size of the diﬀerent parts depends on the expected loading. Seeding of
the main cable is kept relatively ﬁne, to model the main cable curve realistically. The
mesh of the bridge girder (beam part) and pylons is chosen to be equal to the meshing of
the main cable, as neither the behaviour of beam elements on either part is of interest,
as each part only contains few elements. Hanger seeding is depending on the expected
mode of deformation, thus when modal analysis and dynamic analysis of the bridge are
implemented, the seeding is increased drastically. The hanger seed is chosen to model
dynamic behaviour, however increasing the seed of all hangers has a computational cost,

4.2.1.5
Introducing an Initial Stress State
The modelled beam geometry is related to the desired permanent state geometry. Per-
manent state members are previously loaded during construction, by the weight of each
member. Applying the related member loading to the model will induce additional de-
ﬂections and stress contributions, which should be inherent in the permanent state of
the model. Thereby a geometric model loaded by its own weight, should not deform. In
order to counteract this measure, an equivalent pre-stressing is applied to the elements.
Various ways of applying pre-stress can be used, by techniques like controlled ﬁctitious
temperature change or inducing stress in the element. In the present model, a pre-deﬁned
axial stress ﬁeld is used with the feature pre-deﬁned ﬁeld. Since the ﬁelds are pre-deﬁned,
the stress ﬁeld should be established in advance.
The pre-deﬁned stress ﬁelds are applied axially, thus for cables the stress ﬁeld is
deﬁned by the tension force in the cable. Applied geometric formulation of the main cable
curve, shown in equation 2.9, assume uniformly distributed loading in the permanent
state. Transferring this assumption to equation 2.12 a related tensile force in the cable is
estimated. The tensile force is then divided by the cross-sectional area Amc to obtain the
cable pre-stress ‡mc(x). The obtained expression varies with the longitudinal coordinate,
making it cumbersome to apply in the model. In the model, each geometric linear part
of the cable, is assigned a separate predeﬁned ﬁeld with a corresponding magnitude. The
assigned magnitude corresponds with the stress magnitude of the element center. The
pre-stressing has a symmetry axis, at the center of the main span and peak loading just
after the pylon, due to the modelled saddle exit angle, as seen in ﬁgure 4.8.

4.2.1.6
Dead Load Application
Geometric properties of the main cable curve are based on the evenly distributed line
loads ga, gm and gb. Introducing this dead load in a static analysis, each component of
these loads can be applied to their inherent part. However, line loads do not contribute to
the inertia term of the EOM, shown in equation 2.30. Applying line loads as dead-load in
the model would neglect the mass-matrix and dynamic behaviour for later load steps and
would not be representative. Instead, the dead load is applied as mass in a gravitational
ﬁeld. Mass applied in this sense, depend on the material density given in table 4.3 and
the corresponding proﬁle area. The bridge-girder dead-load, contains girder self-weight
as well as SIDL, applying the cross-sectional steel area of the girder, which would un-
derestimate the deck loading. Instead, an equivalent area is applied to the generalised
cross-section, in order to obtain a representative deck mass.
Unfortunately, applying mass in this manner violates initial assumptions, which are
utilized to create the geometry. The suspension system is created based on uniformly

depending on the scope of a project further detailing is required. The present scope is

obtain realistic behaviour at a hanger of interest, 5 girder sections are modelled.

4.3
Dynamic Excitation
To obtain the largest displacements and thereby forces experienced by the hanger cables
on the Great Belt Bridge, the model is loaded dynamically. To obtain these values, it is
assumed that the bridge is loaded transversely by a wind load, while one of the hanger
cables is experiencing a dynamic load varying with the natural frequency of the cable.
Practically, this is done in multiple steps. First, the general bridge deck and cables are
loaded in a transverse direction, where after a single cable is excited in a step by itself. To
load a cable in its resonance frequency, it is deemed necessary to know the value of this
natural frequency, which is determined in section 4.3.1. The magnitude and formulation
of the wind load is discussed in section 4.3.2.
4.3.1
Modal Analysis
The natural frequency of the hanger cables can be obtained by two diﬀerent approaches.
Either by using the analytical approach shown in equation 2.8 for a simply supported
cable in tension, or by extracting the values in Abaqus using the method explained in
section 2.3.1. To extract the natural frequencies of the cables from the Great Belt Bridge
model, the method discussed in section 2.3.1 is used. The structural system is in its
ﬁrst step, loaded by its dead load until static equilibrium has been reached. Hereafter,
linear perturbation is assumed, allowing equation 2.32 to be valid. As mentioned earlier,
Abaqus uses the eigen solver called the Lanczos method, which provides both natural

4.5
Summary & Discussion
In the present chapter, a global beam model of the great belt link is created in the FEA
software Abaqus. Initially, the object is to simulate the permanent state of the bridge and
implement a shell bridge-girder submodel implementation. The golden standard for a
pre-stressed permanent state model is zero deﬂection. From the results of the model, this
is not met. However, with a deﬂection to span ratio of 0.17 ‰, it is deemed acceptable
concerning vertical displacement. There are still some signiﬁcant eﬀects, not covered by
the pre-stress routine. The imbalance in the pre-stressing, will in a cable system induce
a reconﬁguration of the curve. This behaviour is illustrated by the longitudinal displace-
ment of the cable system and the lift of the main span. Implementation of the sub-model
has no signiﬁcant interference with the vertical deﬂection pattern, thus the sub-model
implementation is suﬃciently accurate, concerning displacement transfer. Small inaccu-
racies are however highlighted by the horizontal displacements, as asymmetry is apparent.
Following the permanent state, an excessive wind load is applied. The wind load is
purposely large, in order to invoke large stresses. During static loading, the cable system
acts like a large swing, with big transverse deck displacements and a slight lift, seen in ﬁg-
ure 4.21b. This swinging motion is expected for a suspension system, thus increasing the
conﬁdence in the global model’s lateral behaviour. As an extreme case, the hanger pair is
assumed to be exited at the ﬁrst natural frequency given in table 4.6, meanwhile loaded
by an extreme wind load. The application of the sinusoidal loading is a rough estimate of
the eﬀects of dry galloping and the cable is violently excited. The excitation increases the
total section moment around the second local axis by almost 350 % when compared to
the assumed static swing. During such dynamic loading, the hangers bending properties
are crucial to understanding the true stress in the cable. Multiple oscillations of this
magnitude, illustrate the necessity to understand the stress distribution internally in the
cable, due to bending.
From the model, the largest section moments are obtained in the cable socket. This
behaviour was expected and also the reasoning behind increasing the complexity of the
deck around an investigated hanger. Even during extreme weather conditions, the ob-
tained bending stress in the cable is considerably lower than the normal stress, which is
reasonable due to the enormous weight of a bridge. However, the bending does induce a
considerable bending stress of 15.7 MPa, which is 3.8% of the pre-stressing.

Analysis
A general model of a bridge is a great tool to assess the global response of the bridge
and also the cables. If a detailed description of the internal cable behaviour is of interest,
more complex models are required. In the following section, a detailed model of a locked
coil cable is created and loaded, in order to obtain a more nuanced picture of the hanger
behaviour on a suspension bridge. In relation to the Great Belt Bridge model, the most
common hanger is of type CS1, which is the cable type used in the dynamic analysis of
the global model. The chapter intends to investigate the internal eﬀects of the hanger
type CS1.
Simulations are produced, to obtain a sense of the stress distribution in a cable cross-
section. Stress distributions are of special interest, as concentrations and magnitudes
can diﬀer from estimates based on beam theory. Stress distributions might vary due to
dynamic loading, inducing stress ranges larger than otherwise expected. Loading ranges
are the basis of fatigue calculations, thus investigating stress concentrations due to bend-
ing is of special interest. Furthermore, it is desired to obtain information regarding the
behaviour of a cable cross-section, to implement this in a more general model.
Leading up to simulations like these, a detailed cross-section is created. Expected
stress distributions over a cross-section are formulated, to supply the model with realistic
boundaries. Implementing realistic boundaries serves the purpose of obtaining concise
results from smaller segments. Simulations of the model are primarily done by high-
performance computing, HPC, as we obtained access at the DTU Risoe Sophia Cluster.

5.1.5

The prevalent initial problem-solving methodology is implicit modelling, however, when
solving some complex objectives, the explicit methodology is a handy tool. In relation to
the cable segment, the large number of equilibrium equations constituted by the contact

explicit approach is applied to a static problem. Abaqus ensure stability of the time inte-
gration scheme, by automatically controlling the incrementation by equation 2.34, thus

suﬃciently slow loading velocity.

5.1.5.1

5.2
Load Formulation
Load formulation is an essential part of the model, as the loading will act as the bound-
aries of a cable segment. Loading is formulated in this section as stress ﬁelds. Diﬀerent
loading situations are investigated, covering the pre-stressing and bending of a cable.
Good load formulation and application will include that the model will have good coher-
ence with expected behaviour. If load formulation and application are done to perfection,
the model will be in balance and no boundary uncertainties will occur.
Formulation of the pre-stressing includes taking into account the change in axial stiﬀ-
ness and applying realistic stress ﬁelds accounting for the circumferential force, created
by the spiral geometry. The bending load situation has a curved varying normal ﬁeld,
varying with not only the radial coordinate but also the tangential. These formulations
are described in sections 5.2.1 and 5.2.2.
5.2.1
Pre-stressing
In order to obtain a realistic behaviour of the cable, the permanent hanger state is sim-
ulated. The permanent hanger state should be simulated according to a hanger axial
stress ‡H. A simple method of applying load is by prescribing a section force or displace-
ment at each end of the cable segment. Loading would be applied on the end surfaces
by a kinematic coupling constraint. Coupling constraints lock certain dofs to follow a
single point, entailing that the surface acts as a single unit. However, this will restrict
each wire from exhibiting free movement due to the loading. This inhibition of wire
movement induces boundary eﬀects, increasing the required segment length in order to
obtain feasible cable results. Formulating accurate applied eﬀects will decrease boundary
issues. Another subject of correct loading is to obtain an appropriate helix tightening.
Tightening is only obtained by a pure normal loading if at least a full lay length is simu-
lated. For smaller segments, a corresponding axial torsion has to be applied to the section.
Instead of prescribing a force or displacement across a section, the segment surfaces
are loaded by varying stress ﬁelds, allowing each wire to react independently based on
the load. Axial loading of a cable with an external magnitude, induce two local stress
components related to each wire, as described in section 2.2.2. Both components are
formulated with respect to a cylindrical coordinate system, with the origin in the centre
of the core strand. The axial stress distribution ‡N, is altered according to the changing
helical angles, following the described expression of ‰N, shown in equation 2.23. Ax-
ial stress distribution thereby depends on the radial coordinate, due to the helix angles
dependency, as shown in equation 5.10. The related tangential component ‡T is found
by the geometric relation between the axial loading and the helix angle of a wire, as
expressed by equation 2.22.

5.5
Summary & Discussion
Cable mechanics is a complex matter, both in relation to structural behaviour and wire
interaction. Each wire can move semi-independently and react to the adjacent wires by
contact mechanics. As the results seen above are based on such a complex model, model
behaviour is to be evaluated. Initially, restrictive supports at each end were removed, in
order to allow internal movement of the wires, however, this required formulating a set
of stress expressions in section 5.2. The formulations act as boundaries, if the boundaries
are not applied coherently with the true behaviour, a larger model is required. Avoidance
of these insecurities is attempted by optimizing the applied stress boundaries, however
before comparing with at least a full lay length, an unwavering trust in the boundaries can
not be obtained and some degree of boundary eﬀects must be assumed. As a preventive
measure, presented results from a single model are obtained for the longest simulated lay;
half a lay. With an increased distance from the boundary formulations, possible bound-
ary eﬀects should be reduced. The validity of the boundaries is studied by controlling the
general behaviour of the segment. The model is a quasi-static model, thereby movement
due to the inertia of the wires should not be present. The kinetic energy of the system
is suﬃciently low as presented in ﬁgure 5.14b. When the energy restriction is complied
with, the system can be considered static.
In each step, two boundary loads are applied, one regarding the normal direction and
one in the ◊z-shear direction. The boundary load in the normal axis refers to the general
loading direction. If the loading is applied in the normal axis without the shear compo-
nent, the wires would straighten and large unwanted translations would occur. As this
does not happen in the presented simulation, the obtained boundary loads represent the
behaviour decently. Flawless boundary loads would induce a consistent stress distribution
along the length of the cable, however, small variations do occur. With this in mind, the
boundary loads seem adequate for studying the behaviour of a cable. Additional stress
magnitudes are found at the free body supports, which are hard to avoid.

Studying eﬀects related to a pair of external forces and displacements, the simulation

with the intended loading. The normal force related to this stress ﬁeld Nsim is approxi-
mately 5% less than the estimated Nes (equation 5.22). The deviation seems reasonable,
as the stress is applied across a surface and the surface area varies from a solid, thus the
loaded area is less. The diﬀerence in area between solid and cable is approximately 5%
(calculated in appendix G), which corresponds well with the discrepancy. A 1 to 1 rela-
tion between area and normal force is not expected, as the normal stress is not uniform,
thus the voids closer to the centre would have a higher relative magnitude. Regarding
elongation, a discrepancy is also found between simulation and estimate in equation 5.23,
yielding a diﬀerence of 4% which seem realistic. Deviations can be induced by many
factors, two factors not related to the speciﬁc FEM are the expected nominal stiﬀness
reduction or the geometric change of the wire helix angle, as they displace radially. An-
other reason could be measurement related, as the displacement is calculated as a mean
elongation of segment ends. As seen in ﬁgure 5.18 each layer moves diﬀerently, with a
signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the core and Z-wires. The core is the only wire with sup-
port conditions, thus it is not free to move in both directions along the cable axis. This
implies a large deﬂection in one end as seen in ﬁgure 5.18a, where the 1st O-wire layer
moves in a similar manner. The Z-wire layers elongate in both ends, with almost identical
magnitudes. The coupled movement seems to be due to the large contact surfaces, as the
1st layer experiences line interaction along the full length of the core and Z-wire layers
have the full contact surface. The 2nd outer O-wire layer acts somewhere in between, with
variations along each wire. The mean displacement seems to be consistent with what is
expected, despite a translation between layers. The general behaviour under pre-stressing
seems to behave realistically, with section forces and deformation only deviating a little.
Loading the segment further according to ﬁgure 5.14a, the cable is subject to a stress
ﬁeld with the purpose of inducing bending.
The desired bending moment is applied
around the x-axis, thus the moment should be close to the estimate in this direction and
insigniﬁcant around the y-axis. Similar to the pre-stressing, section moment in the sim-

ﬁeld is created continuously. However the impact of the voids on the moment of inertia

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Cables are capable of handling immense tensional forces relative to the size of the mem-
ber. An ability which makes it applicable to a wide range of civil engineering structures.
The largest and most prominent of structures is bridges, where the cable-stayed bridge
and suspension bridge come to mind. With the suspension bridge in focus, an elaborate
cable system is utilised with main cables and hangers. However, the lifetime of each
hanger is heavily inﬂuenced by the support conditions, due to increased stress variations
at clamps and sockets. In order to possibly circumvent elaborate testing, a multi-scale
FEM concept is developed and utilised to determine stress concentrations in hanger ca-
bles.
A multi-scale concept with the purpose of analyzing the hangers is set up, by initially
creating a global beam model, in order to understand the section forces in a hanger and
then applying these to a more detailed solid model. However, a simple model of a sus-
pension system requires a large model of a bridge. The model created in this thesis is
of the Great Belt Link. The modelled suspension bridge geometry corresponds to the
permanent state and is afterwards exposed to an extreme case of wind. Wind loading is
applied to obtain eﬀects due to bending of a hanger. Eﬀects of interest during bending
are the section forces, where the forces corresponding to the point of the highest section
moment are chosen. Following this cable analysis, the section forces are transferred to a
reﬁned solid model of the hanger.
The hangers on the Great Belt Link consist of locked coil cables, which like other
cables are created by wires in diﬀerent layers. Opposed to a solid geometry, each wire
moves and create contact with the adjoining wires. This contact induces stress concen-
trations that are diﬃcult to estimate. A solid model of a speciﬁc cable on the great belt
link was created, as the reﬁnement of the multi-scale approach. However, the sheer size
of a model makes it next to impossible to simulate full cable lengths. Instead, a modiﬁed
cable was implemented, with only a segment of the length and a reduced cross-section,
however, this made it possible to study the internal eﬀects of the cable. Even with these
reductions, the model size is still large and simulations take hours if not days, depending
on the simulated cable length.
Simulations of the solid cable model, with load equivalents of the extracted section
forces from the global bridge model, were performed. All simulations indicate that stress
concentration between wires of both circular and Z-geometry were apparent, and well

above the total expected stress intensity. This pattern was observed during both loading
periods, namely pre-stressing and bending of the cable, indicating that contact behaviour
has an impact during pre-stressing and bending. Analysing the bending behaviour of the
cable, stress ranges were of special interest in regards to fatigue determination. The stress
ranges were expected to exceed the stress ranges induced by bending of a solid beam.
This did not occur, however, the biggest stress components were found at contact points
internally in the wire.
In regards to the applicability of the model, a parametric study was set up with the
purpose of mapping diﬀerent variable’s inﬂuence on axial stiﬀness and bending stiﬀness.
The tested parameters were friction, pre-stressing magnitude and length of the model,
with all parameters showing a fairly constant bending stiﬀness, corresponding to that of
the cross-section. The idea of a stiﬀness study was to introduce a more accurate beam
stiﬀness in the global model, as cables are known to have an insigniﬁcant bending stiﬀness,
so small that it is often neglected. However, the obtained stiﬀness is not representative
of true cable behaviour, as it is equal to an equivalent beam. Instead, from the study of
diﬀerent lengths a variation in moment along the cable was observed, indicating that the
cable does not experience homogeneous bending. This relation should be investigated
further.
From the multi-scale approach representative section forces were obtained from a
global suspension bridge model and applied to a solid cable. The solid cable exhibited
a complicated stress distribution, with internal wire contact introducing dominant stress
concentrations. This display the potential in applying FEM tools to assess stress states
inside cables and possibly to indicate the remaining capacity of a locked coil hanger cable.
Thoughts on Further Investigation
During a project of this size, a multitude of diﬀerent subjects and themes are touched
upon, however, this entails a series of subjects not covered or explored in depth. In order
to further understand the behaviour of the cable, additional studies uncovering diﬀerent
parameters of inﬂuence should be performed. In this thesis considerable bending stresses
were found in the interaction of Z-wire layers, the contribution on the bending stress
from adding additional Z-wire layers, corresponding to the real hanger geometry, could
be quite relevant. Included in this study, could be an investigation of the input parame-
ters inﬂuence on stress concentrations. Where an initial study of mesh validation should
be performed, in order to further verify the stress concentrations.
A potential series of studies could consist of investigating the change in cable be-
haviour due to diﬀerent lengths. Described, in section 5.4.4.1 the moment varies along
the length of the cable. Studying the inﬂuence of a moment variation in detail could
yield a description of the moment variation with the cable length, with respect to the
given cross-section. A description of this sort could be implemented in a global model,
thus obtaining more precise cable behaviour and initial results from the suspension bridge
model. Another essential length related parameter of a cable is the lay length, which is
held constant throughout the thesis. However, this geometric feature is deeply ingrained
in the stiﬀness of the cable and should thus be investigated further.
