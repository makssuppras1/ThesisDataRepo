Abstract 
The transition to renewable energy is a critical global priority, driven by the need to mitigate 
climate change and reduce reliance on fossil fuels. Biomass-derived syngas offers a promising 
alternative, as it enables the conversion of organic waste into biofuels through gasification and 
subsequent biological processes. Among these, syngas biomethanation has emerged as a viable 
pathway for sustainable methane production, leveraging microbial consortia to convert carbon 
monoxide (CO), carbon dioxide (CO₂), and hydrogen (H₂) into methane (CH₄) under anaerobic 
conditions. 
This study focuses on the experimental validation of the optimized conditions of syngas 
biomethanation in a co-current Trickle Bed Reactor (TBR), a system chosen for its enhanced gas-
liquid mass transfer capabilities. TBRs offer superior gas retention times and biofilm-mediated 
microbial activity, addressing key limitations of traditional reactors such as Continuous Stirred 
Tank Reactors (CSTRs) and Bubble Column Reactors (BCRs). The research evaluates the impact 
of varying gas flow rates and liquid recirculation on methane yield and process efficiency. 
The experimental setup consists of an anaerobically sealed TBR system with a controlled syngas 
composition of 45% H₂, 25% CO₂, 20% CO, and 10% N₂. The study examines microbial 
conversion efficiency, reactor performance, and operational stability, employing analytical 
techniques such as High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) and Gas Chromatography 
(GC) for process monitoring. Results demonstrate that optimizing gas-liquid interactions 
significantly enhances methane production, with high CO conversion efficiencies observed under 
thermophilic conditions. The experimental validation complied with the model and showed the 
highest methane productivity of 9.02 mmol/Lbed/h at a syngas flow rate of 7.2 mL/min and liquid 
recirculation rate of 1600 L/Lbed/d. The experiments also revealed that decreasing the liquid 
recirculation rate from 1600 to 830 L/Lbed/d did not affect the syngas conversion and methane 
productivity as predicted by the model which suggests that the performance of the overall process 
is not compromised and the process more efficient by consuming less power.  
The findings contribute to the advancement of biofuel technologies by providing scalable insights 
into syngas biomethanation. This research underscores the potential of TBRs in achieving high 
purity biomethane production, offering a pathway for integrating renewable natural gas into 
existing energy infrastructures. Future work will focus on addressing residual process 
inefficiencies and assessing long-term reactor stability for industrial applications.

1 Introduction 
1.1: Global energy landscape and transition to renewables 
1.1.1: Growing need for Renewable Energy 
The increase in demand for renewable energy is due to two main factors: the global energy crisis 
and the need to address climate change. The energy crisis, triggered by the economic rebound 
following the COVID-19 pandemic and worsened by the Russian invasion of Ukraine in 2022, led 
to soaring energy prices worldwide, particularly in Europe. This crisis highlighted the volatility of 
fossil fuel markets, driving both governments and industries to seek more stable and sustainable 
alternatives. This shift was evident in major policy responses, such as the European Commission's 
RE Power EU plan and the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act, which aimed to both stabilize energy 
costs and accelerate renewable energy deployment. The objective of the RE Power EU plan is to 
increase the production of renewable energy from 40% to 42.5 % by 2025. The plan also mandates 
that rooftop solar power be installed on all public buildings [1]. About 80 new renewable energy 
policies, mostly in the form of fiscal or financial incentives, were announced in 2022 on the 
demand side. Only three nations—Spain, Portugal, and Turkey—had targets or policies in all four 
end-use sectors by year's end, out of the 94 that had policies or targets in at least one end-use sector 
(Fig. 1). The four major sectors of energy consumption were determined to be Buildings, Industry, 
Transport, and Agriculture [1].

At the same time, the urgency of decarbonizing economies to meet climate commitments, such as 
the Paris Agreement, has pushed renewable energy to the forefront of energy policy. Ever since 
the 1980s, each passing decade has been warmer than the previous one and the main cause of this 
is the emission of greenhouse gases (GHG). The largest emission of GHG is due to the use of fossil 
fuels such as coal, oil, and gas. Over 75 % of global GHG and nearly 90 % of all carbon dioxide 
emissions are due to the use of fossil fuels [2]. This issue has drawn the attention of many 
companies and the government, leading the 196 state parties of the United Nations Framework 
Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) to adopt the Paris Agreement, which has the goal of

decarbonization by recycling the existing carbon, both organic and inorganic and limiting the 
global average temperature increase below 2 ℃ by 21st century [3]. Together, these factors have 
significantly bolstered the global push toward renewable energy, making it a critical component 
of future energy strategies.  
1.1.2 Renewable Energy Options 
Renewable energy sources are derived from naturally replenished resources, making them 
sustainable and environmentally friendly. The primary types of renewable energy include solar, 
wind, hydroelectric, geothermal, and biomass energy (Fig. 2). Solar energy harnesses sunlight for 
electricity and heat, wind energy utilizes wind flows to generate power, and hydropower leverages 
the kinetic energy of flowing water. Geothermal energy taps into the Earth’s internal heat.

Among these, biomass energy plays a crucial role due to its ability to convert organic materials, 
such as plant and animal waste, into usable energy. Biomass is particularly valuable because it is 
versatile and can be converted into electricity, heat, or biofuels through processes like combustion 
and gasification. Unlike other renewable resources that depend on specific geographical conditions, 
biomass is available globally, making it an accessible and continuous energy source. Additionally, 
biomass offers the significant advantage of reducing greenhouse gas emissions by recycling carbon 
within the ecosystem, contributing to both energy generation and waste management [5].

1.2 Biomass: A Key Resource for Renewable Energy 
1.2.1 What is Biomass? 
For many years, we have produced energy from biomass, primarily wood. In many parts of the 
world, direct combustion is still the method of choice for using biomass as it has been done in the 
past. Examples of biomass resources include wood and wood waste, aquatic plants, algae, animal 
waste, crops and their waste byproducts, municipal solid waste (MSW), and food processing waste 
[6]. The biomass we get from plants is created when sunlight, water, and CO2 in the atmosphere 
react to produce the carbohydrates that make up biomass's building blocks. Normally, less than 
1% of the solar energy received is converted by photosynthesis into chemical energy that is stored. 
The chemical bonds that hold together the structural elements of biomass store the solar energy 
required for photosynthetic processes. When biomass is effectively processed, the carbon in it is 
oxidized, producing CO₂ and water. This process releases the energy stored in the chemical bonds, 
which becomes available as an energy product when combined with oxygen [7].  
One of the most important characteristics of plant-based biomass is its versatility. It can be 
converted into various forms of energy through several processes, including direct combustion, 
gasification, pyrolysis, and anaerobic digestion [7]. These processes enable the generation of 
electricity, the production of heat, and the creation of biofuels that can replace fossil fuels. 
Additionally, biomass contributes to waste reduction, as it utilizes materials that would otherwise 
be discarded, such as agricultural residues and municipal solid waste [6]. This dual benefit of 
energy generation and waste management highlights biomass as a key player in the global 
transition to a more sustainable energy system. 
1.2.2 Biomass conversion technologies 
Biomass conversion technologies are essential for transforming organic material into energy, and 
they can be broadly categorized into thermochemical and biochemical processes. Thermochemical 
processes, such as combustion, gasification, and pyrolysis, involve the application of heat to 
decompose biomass into energy-rich products. Combustion is the simplest method, burning 
biomass directly to produce heat and electricity. Gasification, on the other hand, converts biomass 
into syngas, a mixture of carbon monoxide, hydrogen, carbon dioxide, and methane, which can be 
used for electricity generation or further processed into biofuels [8]. Pyrolysis, a similar process, 
involves the thermal decomposition of biomass in the absence of oxygen to produce bio-oil, which 
can be refined into biofuels. These technologies enable the efficient use of biomass in power 
generation and heat production which helps in reducing the reliance on fossil fuels. 
Among these technologies, gasification offers several advantages. Compared to conventional 
combustion, it is more energy-efficient and provides greater control over emissions, which lowers 
the generation of dangerous pollutants like particulate matter and volatile organic compounds. 
Additionally, gasification is flexible enough to work with a range of biomass feedstocks, such as 
municipal solid waste and agricultural residues, making it suitable for a variety of organic material 
sources [9]. Despite these benefits, challenges remain, such as the need for gas-cleaning

10 
 
technologies to remove impurities like tar, which can inhibit downstream processes. Nonetheless, 
advancements in gasification technology are addressing these issues, making it a key pathway in 
the utilization of biomass for sustainable energy production [10]. 
1.2.3 Biomass Gasification 
Gasification is a thermochemical process that converts biomass into a synthesis gas (syngas) 
composed mainly of hydrogen (H₂), carbon monoxide (CO), Carbon dioxide (CO2), and methane 
(CH₄). This process occurs at high temperatures (typically 900-1200°C) in an oxygen-limited 
environment, preventing complete combustion and enabling the partial oxidation of the biomass. 
Gasification involves several key stages, including drying, pyrolysis, oxidation, and reduction, 
each contributing to the breakdown of the biomass into simpler gaseous molecules (Fig. 3). The 
produced syngas can be used for various applications, including electricity generation, chemical 
and biofuel production [11]. The capacity of gasification to process a variety of biomass feedstocks, 
such as organic waste, agricultural residues, and even lignocellulosic materials into syngas, is a 
noteworthy benefit. The flexibility of gasification makes it a promising technology for both 
decentralized and large-scale bioenergy production [10]. 
 
Figure 3: Gasification process steps [12] 
One of the main advantages of biomass gasification over traditional combustion methods is its 
higher energy efficiency. This is achieved by gasification, which turns biomass into syngas, a more 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
11 
 
valuable and cleaner byproduct that can be used as a feedstock for the synthesis of chemicals and 
biofuels as well as a cleaner way to produce electricity. As gasification takes place in an oxygen-
controlled atmosphere, it generates fewer air pollutants like sulfur oxides and particulate matter. 
The ability to use syngas in gas engines or turbines significantly reduces emissions and raises the 
efficiency of using biomass in general [9]. Furthermore, gasification is more adaptable to different 
feedstocks, enabling the processing of low-grade materials like municipal solid waste and 
agricultural residues, thereby expanding the scope of biomass utilization [10]. 
Biomass gasification has many benefits, but it also has some drawbacks, both financially and 
technically. The formation of tar during the gasification process, which can clog machinery and 
lower syngas production efficiency, is one of the main problems. Technologies for tar removal and 
gas cleaning are essential to guarantee the quality of syngas for subsequent use in chemical 
synthesis or energy production [13]. Furthermore, especially when scaled up for industrial uses, 
gasification systems can be complicated and expensive. Adding sophisticated cleaning 
technologies drives up expenses even more. Additionally, there are operational difficulties like 
ensuring a steady supply of feedstock and managing the process parameters to guarantee the best 
possible syngas output [9]. Nevertheless, ongoing advancements in reactor design and gas cleaning 
technologies are addressing these limitations, making gasification a viable solution for the future 
of renewable energy [14]. 
1.3 Fermentation 
1.3.1 What is Fermentation? 
Fermentation is a metabolic process in which microorganisms, under anaerobic or aerobic 
conditions, convert organic matter into biofuels or biochemicals. This process plays a vital role in 
biofuel production, especially in the sustainable conversion of biomass into valuable fuels and 
chemicals. There are two principal types of fermentation processes: liquid fermentation and gas 
fermentation. Liquid fermentation involves the microbial breakdown of substrates dissolved in a 
liquid medium, whereas gas fermentation utilizes gaseous substrates like carbon monoxide (CO), 
carbon dioxide (CO₂), and hydrogen (H₂) for microbial conversion into biofuels [15]. 
1.3.2 Liquid Fermentation 
Liquid fermentation has long been used in industrial biofuel production, including the production 
of bioethanol, where microorganisms metabolize dissolved substrates such as glucose. It is a well-
established method that uses a wide range of microbial strains that are adapted to produce specific 
target products, which is one of its main advantages. Additionally, the procedure is scalable for 
industrial production because of its ease of control over fermentation parameters, such as pH, 
temperature, and nutrient levels. As seen in Fig. 4, using corn or sugarcane as feedstocks in 
bioethanol production plants is an excellent example of large-scale liquid fermentation processes 
[15]. 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
12 
 
 
Figure 4: Ethanol production process using corn (in USA) and sugarcane (in Brazil) as substrate

In liquid fermentation, the incomplete hydrolysis of carbon-rich lignocellulosic biomass is 
primarily due to its complex structure, comprising cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin. While 
cellulose and hemicellulose are polysaccharides that can be enzymatically broken down into 
fermentable sugars, lignin is an aromatic, recalcitrant polymer that resists enzymatic degradation. 
Even with pretreatment methods like steam explosion, acid hydrolysis, or enzymatic treatment, a 
significant portion of lignin remains unhydrolyzed, resulting in incomplete carbon conversion. 
This inefficiency stems from the fact that microorganisms in liquid fermentation are unable to 
metabolize lignin, which leads to only partial utilization of the available carbon content [17]. The 
incomplete hydrolysis of cellulose and hemicellulose is further exacerbated by the formation of 
inhibitors during pretreatment, such as furfural and hydroxymethylfurfural (HMF), which inhibit 
microbial fermentation and reduce overall yields [18]. 
Liquid fermentation systems thus struggle with incomplete utilization of the carbon content, 
especially when dealing with lignocellulosic biomass. The presence of lignin, which constitutes a 
significant portion of the biomass, presents a major bottleneck in the hydrolysis and fermentation 
process. Despite the advancements in pretreatment technologies, the inability of liquid 
fermentation to fully break down and convert lignin into fermentable sugars highlights its 
limitations. These inefficiencies drive the search for alternative processes, such as gasification 
followed by gas fermentation, which allows for more efficient carbon conversion by transforming 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
13 
 
biomass into syngas—a gas mixture that can be fully utilized by microbes during fermentation 
[18]. 
1.3.3 Gas Fermentation 
Gas fermentation is an innovative approach for the microbial conversion of gaseous substrates like 
carbon monoxide (CO) and hydrogen (H₂) into valuable biofuels such as bioethanol and 
biomethane. One of the significant advantages of gas fermentation is its potential to achieve near 
100% carbon utilization, making it highly efficient compared to traditional liquid fermentation. By 
utilizing syngas, a mixture of CO, H₂, and CO2 derived from biomass gasification, gas fermentation 
bypasses the limitations posed by lignin and other complex organic fractions that are not easily 
metabolized in liquid fermentation [19]. Microorganisms, including acetogens and methanogens, 
play a crucial role in this process, metabolizing syngas components to produce biofuels with high 
carbon recovery [20]. 
However, gas fermentation faces challenges, particularly in achieving effective gas-to-liquid mass 
transfer. The efficient dissolution of gaseous substrates into the liquid medium is critical to ensure 
that microorganisms have access to enough CO, CO2, and H2. Without proper gas-liquid mass 
transfer, the microbial conversion rate can be significantly hampered, reducing overall process 
efficiency. To address this, advanced reactor designs, such as bubble column reactors and trickle 
bed reactors (TBRs), are commonly employed. These reactors enhance gas-liquid contact and 
ensure continuous substrate supply to the microorganisms, optimizing biofuel production [21]. 
Despite these technical challenges, gas fermentation remains a promising method for sustainable 
biofuel production, particularly in producing biomethane for energy applications [22]. 
1.4 Syngas Biomethanation: A Sustainable Pathway 
1.4.1 Overview of Syngas Biomethanation 
Syngas biomethanation is a promising biotechnological process that involves the biological 
conversion of syngas (a mixture of CO, CO₂, and H₂) into methane (CH₄) through the activity of 
microorganisms like methanogens. Syngas is typically produced by the gasification of biomass 
and waste materials, offering a sustainable way to harness carbon-rich feedstocks that would 
otherwise be underutilized. The biological methanation process occurs under anaerobic conditions, 
where carboxydotrophic hydrogenogens and hydrogenotrophic methanogens reduce CO and CO₂, 
respectively, using H₂ as an electron donor to produce methane [23]. Unlike catalytic methanation, 
which requires elevated temperatures and pressures, biological methanation operates at milder 
temperatures (35–65°C) and ambient pressures, making it an energy-efficient alternative. 
Furthermore, this biological process has shown resilience to syngas impurities such as sulfur 
compounds, a common issue in catalytic systems [24]. 
A major advantage of syngas biomethanation is its ability to support circular economy initiatives 
by transforming industrial waste gases into renewable natural gas (RNG), which can be injected 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
14 
 
into natural gas grids. Syngas biomethanation also has the potential to mitigate carbon emissions 
by sequestering CO₂ and converting it into methane, which can be stored or utilized as a cleaner 
energy source. The key challenge, however, lies in enhancing gas-liquid mass transfer rates due to 
the poor solubility of syngas components like hydrogen in water, which limits the methanation 
rate [23]. Strategies to improve mass transfer, such as reactor design optimizations and the use of 
porous packing materials to increase surface area, are crucial for the scalability of this process [25]. 
1.4.2 Microbial Pathways in Syngas Biomethanation 
The microbial pathways involved in syngas biomethanation are critical to understanding the 
efficiency and product yield of the process. The primary archaea involved are hydrogenotrophic 
methanogens, which convert H₂ and CO₂ into methane via hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis. In 
this pathway, hydrogen serves as the electron donor, and CO₂ acts as the electron acceptor, 
resulting in methane production. Another pathway is carboxydotrophic methanogenesis, where 
carboxydotrophic methanogens use CO as both the carbon and energy source, reducing it to 
methane. The interactions between different microbial communities, including acetogens that 
convert CO into acetate, play a significant role in methane yields [23].  
Different species of bacteria and archaea collaborate symbiotically to transform syngas into CH4.  
pH has an impact on the activity of the microorganisms involved in the biomethanation process. 
For example, the optimal pH range for archaea growth is 6.5–8.5 between 35 ℃-65 ℃, with a 
relatively narrow range for growth. Consequently, syngas biomethanation would be most effective 
at a neutral pH since it would be subject to the same pH limitations as the biocatalysts[26]. 
Operational temperature is another important factor that affects syngas conversion in addition to 
pH. Prior research shows that an increase in temperature from mesophilic (20 ℃- 40 ℃) to 
thermophilic conditions (40 ℃- 85 ℃) positively impacted the microorganisms for biomethane 
production [24].  
Furthermore, the performance of syngas biomethanation and the CO conversion pathway may be 
strongly impacted by the microbial makeup of the inoculum. The composition of syngas plays a 
crucial role in the biomethanation process, alongside environmental conditions. Carboxydotrophic 
hydrogenogens play a pivotal role in this process by oxidizing CO, utilizing it as a carbon and 
energy source for growth and metabolite production. Thus, biologically converting CO to H2 and 
CO2. After this, the hydrogenotrophic methanogens convert CO2 and H2 to CH4 [27]. The

1.4.3 Advantages and Challenges of Syngas Biomethanation 
The advantages of syngas biomethanation are numerous, with one of the most significant being its 
potential for 100% carbon utilization, turning waste gases into methane, a valuable energy carrier. 
Compared to traditional waste treatment or fossil fuel consumption, syngas biomethanation allows 
for the capture of carbon from industrial processes and its conversion into a storable and usable 
form of energy. This process not only reduces greenhouse gas emissions but also creates a 
renewable source of methane that can be directly injected into natural gas grids or used in fuel 
cells for electricity generation [23]. The flexibility of syngas biomethanation also extends to 
feedstock, with various types of biomasses and organic waste being suitable for gasification to 
produce syngas, thus promoting sustainable waste management [24].  
However, challenges remain in scaling up syngas biomethanation for industrial applications. A 
primary limitation is the low solubility of syngas components, particularly hydrogen, in the liquid 
medium, which can slow down the overall conversion rate. Additionally, achieving high methane 
yields depends heavily on optimizing gas-liquid mass transfer, which requires advanced reactor 
designs such as trickle bed reactors (TBR). While TBRs offer higher surface area for microbial 
growth and improved gas-liquid interaction, maintaining consistent methane production rates at 
larger scales and under variable conditions is still a challenge [24]. The cost of setting up and 
operating these reactors, particularly in terms of recirculating liquids, must also be addressed for 
the broader adoption of this technology. 
1.5 Biomethane: Characteristics and Applications 
1.5.1 What is Biomethane? 
Biomethane is a renewable form of energy derived from the anaerobic digestion of organic 
materials such as agricultural waste, manure, or food waste. It is essentially purified biogas that 
has been upgraded to remove impurities such as carbon dioxide (CO₂), water vapor, and hydrogen 
sulfide (H₂S), resulting in a gas that is approximately 98% methane (CH₄). The upgrading process 
ensures that biomethane has a similar composition and calorific value to natural gas, making it a 
versatile fuel that can be used in existing gas infrastructures without modification. The production 
of biomethane is a critical component of the circular economy, as it turns waste materials into 
energy, contributing to waste reduction and sustainability [28]. 
The composition of biomethane allows for various applications, from residential heating to 
electricity generation and even as transportation fuel. It can be injected into the natural gas grid or 
used directly in vehicles as compressed biomethane (CBM) or liquefied biomethane (LBM), 
contributing to the decarbonization of energy systems. Given its renewable nature and 
compatibility with existing gas infrastructure, biomethane is seen as a vital part of the energy 
transition towards lower carbon emissions [29]. 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
16 
 
1.5.2 Features and characteristics of biomethane 
Biomethane boasts several key features that make it a valuable renewable energy source. It is 
highly efficient for heat and electricity production. Moreover, because biomethane is produced 
from organic waste, it has a low carbon footprint, particularly when compared to fossil-derived 
natural gas. Biomethane also significantly reduces greenhouse gas emissions, especially methane, 
which would otherwise be released into the atmosphere during the decomposition of organic waste. 
In addition, biomethane can be produced locally, contributing to energy security and reducing the 
reliance on imported fossil fuels [29]. 
1.5.3 Disadvantages and challenges of biomethane production 
Despite its many advantages, there are several challenges associated with biomethane production. 
One of the primary issues is the high cost of upgrading biogas to biomethane quality. The process 
requires advanced technology to remove impurities like CO₂ and H₂S, and this can lead to 
increased operational costs, especially for smaller production facilities. Additionally, the 
infrastructure for biomethane upgrading and distribution is still underdeveloped in many regions, 
which can hinder its large-scale adoption. Moreover, while biomethane production is renewable, 
the availability of feedstock is geographically dependent, and supply can fluctuate based on 
seasonal or economic factors [28].  
Another challenge is the competition for feedstock between biomethane production and other uses, 
such as composting or biofuel production. This can lead to concerns about resource allocation, 
particularly when food waste is considered a potential feedstock. Furthermore, while biomethane 
is effective in reducing emissions, its production still generates some greenhouse gases, 
particularly during the upgrading process. The technology to minimize these emissions is still 
evolving, and further research and development are necessary to optimize the sustainability of 
biomethane production processes [30]. 
1.6 Overview of Different Reactor Types for gas fermentation 
1.6.1 Continuous Stirred Tank Reactors (CSTR) 
Continuous stirred tank reactors (CSTRs) are widely used in various biochemical processes (Fig. 
5), including gas fermentation, due to their simplicity and ease of operation. CSTRs ensure 
homogeneity in the reactor by maintaining constant agitation, which helps to maintain a uniform 
temperature and substrate concentration throughout the vessel. This mixing capability makes 
CSTRs highly effective for microbial fermentation processes where substrate distribution is critical 
for microbial growth and product formation. However, CSTRs also require significant energy for 
agitation and gas-liquid mass transfer, which can increase operational costs, especially in large-
scale applications. In syngas fermentation, CSTRs have been shown to offer consistent 
performance, but gas-to-liquid mass transfer limitations can affect productivity [31].

1.6.2 Bubble Column Reactors 
Bubble column reactors (BCRs) are another reactor type commonly used in gas fermentation (Fig. 
6). BCRs utilize a simple design in which gas is bubbled from the bottom of the reactor through 
the liquid medium, allowing for gas-liquid mass transfer. One of the key advantages of bubble 
columns is their efficient gas-liquid mass transfer, driven by the large surface area provided by the 
bubbles. The performance of a BCR can be optimized by adjusting the gas flow rate, the type of 
gas sparger, and the column's height-to-diameter ratio. Due to their simplicity and cost-
effectiveness, BCRs are attractive for industrial applications. However, mass transfer in BCRs can 
be limited by factors like bubble coalescence and gas residence time, affecting the overall 
efficiency of syngas fermentation processes [26]

Figure 6: Bubble Column reactor [32] 
1.6.3 Packed Bed Reactors (PBR)  
Packed bed reactors (PBRs) consist of a stationary packed bed, through which the liquid and gas 
phases pass (Fig. 7). The primary difference between PBRs and TBRs is the absence of continuous 
liquid flow in PBRs, which makes them suitable for processes with lower liquid throughput. The 
gas-liquid mass transfer occurs as the liquid flows over the surface of the packed bed, promoting 
microbial growth on the packing material. While PBRs offer a high surface area for biofilm 
formation and are effective in certain fermentation processes, they are prone to clogging and gas-
liquid flow irregularities. These factors can affect long-term stability making PBR less suitable for 
continuous syngas fermentation compared to TBRs [31]

1.6.4 Minimal liquid bioreactors (MLBs) 
Minimal Liquid Bioreactors (MLBs) feature a hose-like structure with a high internal surface area, 
where archaea are arranged along the gas flow path (Fig. 8). This configuration enhances gas-
liquid transfer, ensuring abundant nutrient supply to microorganisms in the upward liquid stream 
and maximizing gas-to-archaea interaction in the downward phase. While MLB systems show 
promising performance, they have been minimally investigated in the literature, with only a few 
studies addressing this specific reactor design. This limited exploration is largely due to challenges 
in maintaining stable microbial communities within the confined structure, as well as difficulties 
in managing biofilm growth, which can lead to clogging and reduced efficiency over time. [26]. 
 
Figure 8: Minimal Liquid Reactor [26]. 
 
1.6.5 Hollow Fiber Membrane Rectors (HFR) 
Hollow Fiber Membrane Reactors (HFRs) utilize porous hollow fiber membranes to facilitate the 
syngas biomethanation process (Fig. 9). The hollow fibers act as a medium for gas diffusion, 
allowing H₂ and CO₂ to pass through the membrane into the liquid phase where hydrogenotrophic 
methanogens reside. This design enhances gas-liquid mass transfer by providing a large surface 
area for interaction between the gases and microorganisms. Separation of the gas supply and liquid 
medium ensures efficient substrate utilization and high methane purity. Despite their compactness 
and potential for high methane conversion efficiency, HFRs face challenges such as membrane 
fouling, which increases operational costs. However, their ability to achieve high CH₄ yields under 
controlled conditions makes them a viable option for syngas biomethanation [31].

1.6.6 Trickle Bed Reactors (TBR)  
Trickle bed reactors (TBRs) are becoming increasingly popular for syngas fermentation due to 
their efficient handling of gas-liquid-solid interactions. In TBRs, the liquid phase trickles down 
through a packed bed of inert material, while the gas phase flows either co-currently or counter-
currently through the packed bed (Fig. 10). The biofilm formation on the solid support enhances 
mass transfer and stabilizes microbial activity. TBRs are particularly beneficial for syngas 
fermentation due to their ability to improve the mass transfer of sparingly soluble gases like CO 
and H₂, which are crucial for biogas and biomethane production. However, challenges such as 
channeling, clogging, and uneven gas distribution need to be addressed to optimize their use in 
industrial settings [26].

1.7 Trickle Bed Reactor: Chosen Reactor for Syngas Biomethanation 
Trickle Bed Reactor (TBR) was selected for syngas biomethanation due to its superior efficiency 
in facilitating gas-liquid-solid interactions and minimizing mass transfer limitations. TBRs utilize 
a packed bed structure, where biofilm grows on the packing material, allowing for enhanced 
microbial interactions with the gas phase (CO, H₂, CO2) for methane production. The thin film of 
liquid trickling over the bed ensures a steady supply of nutrients to microorganisms without 
needing high energy inputs for mixing, which is a crucial advantage over more energy-intensive 
reactor designs such as CSTRs [31]. Additionally, the use of packing materials optimizes gas 
retention time, improving CH₄ production efficiency in comparison to other reactor configurations 
like BCRs [26]. 
Compared to other reactor types, TBR offers a higher methane production rate and more complete 
gas conversion. When supplemented with H₂, studies have shown that TBRs can achieve methane 
purity levels exceeding 95%, making them ideal for grid injection—a critical parameter for biofuel 
production at commercial scales. In contrast, BCRs and HFRs, while providing adequate gas-liquid 
contact, tend to have lower gas retention times, reducing their efficiency in biomethanation 
processes. Additionally, the low energy requirements of TBRs, coupled with high mass transfer 
rates, make them economically viable for large-scale biomethanation systems. [26]. These factors 
make the TBR an attractive choice for syngas biomethanation, particularly in systems aiming for 
sustainable and high purity biomethane production. 
1.8 Mass Transfer Coefficient (kLa) and Its Importance in Gas Fermentation 
The volumetric mass transfer coefficient (kLa) is a crucial parameter in gas fermentation, 
representing the efficiency of gas dissolution into the liquid phase. It is used to quantify the rate at 
which gases like H₂, CO, and CO₂ transfer from the gas phase into the liquid medium, where 
microbial conversion occurs. The mass transfer rate is expressed as:

where: 
kLa is the volumetric mass transfer coefficient (h-1) 
C* is the equilibrium concentration of the gas in the liquid (saturation concentration), and  
C is the actual gas concentration in the liquid 
A higher kLa indicates better gas dissolution, improving substrate availability for microorganisms 
and enhancing bioconversion efficiency. 
1.8.1 Why kLa Varies Across Reactor Types 
The kLa value depends on several reactor characteristics, including size, geometry, packing 
material, agitation, and gas flow rate. Different reactors offer varying levels of gas-liquid contact: 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
22 
 
• Trickle Bed Reactors (TBRs) have enhanced gas-liquid interfacial areas due to biofilm 
formation on the packing material, leading to moderate kLa values. 
• Bubble Column Reactors (BCRs) rely on gas sparging, where smaller bubbles increase 
surface area, but coalescence can limit kLa. 
• Stirred Tank Reactors (CSTRs) achieve higher kLa through intense agitation but require 
more energy. 
• Hollow Fiber Membrane Reactors (HFRs) have the highest kLa due to direct gas diffusion 
through membranes. 
Since kLa determines gas availability for microbial metabolism, comparing reactors based on their 
kLa values is essential to selecting the most efficient system for syngas biomethanation. 
1.8.2 Comparing Mass Transfer Coefficients of Reactors 
The mass transfer coefficients (kLa) of various reactors, as outlined in Table 1, demonstrate 
significant variation based on the reactor type, gas composition, and operating conditions. Stirred 
Tank Reactors (STRs) exhibit relatively high kLa values, ranging from 154.8 to 288 h−1, largely 
due to intense agitation and high gas flow rates. Packed Bed Reactors (PBRs), on the other hand, 
rely on the use of packing materials like polyurethane sponges, which provide an increased surface 
area for gas-liquid interaction but result in lower mass transfer coefficients, typically around 163.6 
h−1. Bubble Column Reactors (BCRs) show more variability, with kLa values ranging from 40 to 
113.7 h−1, depending on the type of sparger used and gas composition. Hollow Fiber Membrane 
Bioreactors (HFRs) significantly outperform other reactors with kLa values reaching up to 1096.2 
h−1 due to the high surface area provided by microporous hydrophobic membranes, which enhance 
gas-liquid mass transfer efficiency. 
Among all reactor types, Trickle Bed Reactors (TBRs) demonstrate an optimal balance of mass 
transfer efficiency and operational simplicity. Despite operating at relatively lower mechanical 
agitation levels, TBRs achieve high kLa values, particularly with gas mixtures like syngas. The 
high surface area provided by packing materials such as ceramic Intalox saddles, combined with 
liquid recirculation, ensures efficient gas-liquid contact. For instance, TBRs operating with 45% 
H2, 25% CO2, 20% CO, and 10% N2 achieved kLa values as high as 6.18 h−1 for H2 and 3.3 h−1 for 
CO. This efficiency, coupled with the flexibility to handle various gas compositions, makes TBRs 
particularly suitable for processes involving syngas fermentation, biohydrogen, or bioethanol 
production.

1.9 Gas fermentation in different reactors

biomethanation using different configurations and operating conditions. Continuous Stirred Tank

microbial biofilms. Additionally, TBRs operate efficiently under moderate energy requirements, 
making them economically viable for methane production at larger scales. The comparative data 
in Table 2 underscores the TBR’s suitability for syngas biomethanation, specifically in terms of 
optimizing methane output through stable microbial activity and efficient gas flow.

commercial-scale methane production. The structured packing material within TBRs not only 
supports gas-liquid-solid interactions but also sustains high microbial activity at these increased 
temperatures. Comparatively, these thermophilic setups achieve productivity levels that 
considerably exceed mesophilic setups, highlighting TBRs' adaptability and superior gas retention 
and conversion efficiency across different temperature regimes.

1.10 Reactors for methane production by gas fermentation 
Table 3 highlights the operational findings and limitations across reactor types, focusing on their 
suitability for biomethanation. CSTRs maintain stable microbial environments conducive to 
uniform substrate distribution, supporting methane yields as high as 355.8 mL/L/day. However, 
this reactor type experiences reduced methane production efficiency at high gas flow rates, often 
due to substrate inhibition and high energy demands for mixing, it also has limitations in gas-liquid 
mass transfer. BCRs, on the other hand, have shown higher mass transfer efficiency and achieve 
sufficient methane production under low pressure and moderate gas flow rates, with methane 
purity around 78-96%, though their dependency on precise bubble retention limits continuous 
methane output. The operational simplicity of BCRs does not fully compensate for these mass 
transfer limitations, which restrict their scalability in high-throughput applications. 
In contrast, TBRs demonstrate stable productivity under continuous flow conditions, as shown in 
Table 3, making them ideal for commercial-scale methane production and grid injection. TBRs 
also excel in gas retention, enhancing mass transfer and enabling nearly complete CO and H₂ 
conversion—a critical factor in meeting the study's objectives. Despite these advantages, TBRs do 
face challenges such as channeling and potential microbial inhibition at high CO concentrations. 
The findings and limitations in Table 3 suggest that while TBRs provide a strong foundation for 
achieving high methane yields, optimizing flow rates and mitigating potential biofilm-related 
inefficiencies remain essential for maximizing productivity in syngas biomethanation.

2 Aim of the Study 
This study aims to investigate and optimize the process of syngas biomethanation within a Trickle 
Bed Reactor (TBR) to enhance methane production efficiency. With increasing global demand for 
renewable energy, particularly biomethane, this research will experimentally explore the effects 
of various gas and liquid flow rates on the biological conversion of carbon monoxide (CO), carbon 
dioxide (CO2), and hydrogen (H₂) into methane (CH₄). This study seeks to identify optimal 
operating conditions that maximize gas-liquid interaction and improve the overall methane yield 
from syngas fermentation. Previous studies have highlighted the potential of TBRs to overcome 
mass transfer limitations inherent in gas-liquid-solid systems, making this reactor type an ideal 
choice for syngas biomethanation [31]. 
Model predictions have indicated that increasing the gas flow rate leads to an enhancement in CO 
conversion and methane productivity due to improved gas-liquid mass transfer, ensuring greater 
substrate availability for microbial communities. The greater availability of syngas components 
ensures a higher substrate supply for microbial consortia, which in turn enhances methane yield. 
This relationship highlights the importance of optimizing the gas inflow rate to achieve higher 
conversion efficiencies and sustained reactor stability. In contrast, the model also predicts that 
variations in the liquid recirculation rate follow a sinusoidal trend concerning the concentration of 
CO, CO2, and H2 dissolved in the liquid. Initially, increasing the liquid recirculation enhances mass 
transfer and microbial accessibility to dissolved gases. However, beyond a certain threshold, 
further increases in recirculation rate do not necessarily translate to higher methane yields and may 
instead lead to inefficiencies due to excessive energy consumption [73].  
Thus, the two major tests that will be conducted to verify the model predictions experimentally, 
including systematically varying the syngas inflow rate and liquid recirculation rate to evaluate 
their impacts on methane production. Periodic increases in syngas inflow aim to optimize gas 
availability for microbial consortia, enhancing methane yield without overstressing the system. 
Reducing the liquid recirculation rate aims to enhance the volumetric mass transfer coefficient, 
and minimize energy consumption, making the process more sustainable and cost-effective for 
industrial applications. These changes are significant as they balance productivity and operational 
efficiency, contributing to scalable and economically viable biomethanation processes. 
The experimental validation conducted in this study will provide insights into the scalability of 
TBR-based syngas biomethanation systems, evaluating their technical and economic feasibility 
for large-scale applications. This comprehensive approach not only aims to optimize methane 
production but also addresses the challenges of gas-liquid mass transfer, reactor design, and 
microbial community stability, making significant contributions to the field of renewable energy 
and biofuel production.

3 Materials and Methods 
The Materials and Methods section presents a comprehensive overview of the experimental setup 
and procedures used in this study to investigate the gas fermentation process with a mixed 
microbial consortium. It details the equipment employed, the composition of the growth medium, 
sampling methods, and the analytical techniques used for data collection and analysis. By 
thoroughly documenting the materials and methodologies, this study ensures transparency and 
reproducibility, enabling a clear understanding of the experimental design and supporting the 
interpretation of the results. 
3.1 Fermentation setup 
The experimental setup in Fig. 11, consists of an anaerobically sealed Trickle-bed reactor (TBR) 
(1) with an empty bed volume of 220 mL, where liquid trickles onto polypropylene/polyethylene 
packing material with a surface area of 800 m2/m3 and a density of 1 g/cm3, which exhibits 
microbial growth on its surface. Gas is fed from the top of the reactor from a 50 L syngas cylinder 
(2) containing a gas composition of 45% H₂, 25% CO₂, 20% CO, and 10% N₂, which is controlled 
by a mass flow controller (MFC) (3). The liquid and gas flow co-currently through the packed bed 
and into an anaerobically sealed reservoir (4) of 1.1 L and the liquid volume is maintained at 450 
mL with the help of a level controller (13). The liquid fraction is then recirculated to the top of the 
reactor at a rate of 1600 L/Lbed/day with the help of a peristaltic pump (5). This liquid recirculation 
line has a sampling port (6), which is used to take liquid samples. Fresh media that contains 
vitamins and minerals required for cell growth is also pumped at a Hydraulic retention time (HRT) 
of 4 days from the media bottle (7), with the help of another peristaltic pump. To maintain the 
pressure in the media bottle, a tube (8) filled with nitrogen is attached to it, which fills the 
headspace after the liquid media is pumped into the reactor. A magnetic stirrer (9) is employed 
below the reservoir and the media bottle (7) to ensure proper mixing and avoid accumulation of 
solids (cells or inorganic precipitates). Two TBRs are run as a duplicate with the same reactor 
design and operating conditions (pH and Temperature). 
Two probes monitor the pH and temperature of the broth. The pH is maintained using a pH PID 
controller (10), which regulates the acid and base peristaltic pumps, set to adjust the pH and keep 
it in between the range of 6.9-7.1 by adding acid/base from the prepared acid/base bottles. 2M 
phosphoric acid (H3PO4) was used as an acid and 2M potassium hydroxide (KOH) was used as a 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
37 
 
base. A water bath (11) is used to control and maintain a constant temperature of 60 ± 0.5 ℃ by 
using a Temperature PID controller (12), water was continuously recirculated through a water 
jacket covering the reactor and the reservoir, ensuring optimal growth conditions.

3.3 Sampling 
3.3.1 Liquid Sampling 
Samples were collected three times a week to make sure the fermentation was going as planned 
and yielding the intended product; process control and monitoring are crucial. The liquid sample 
was taken by a 5 mL syringe from the liquid port. Firstly, the 5 mL sample is taken and discarded, 
then the syringe is attached back again and then we flush the syringe with the liquid 4-5 times. 
Then 1.5 mL of sample was collected in an Eppendorf tube to make a sample for the HPLC. 
Another liquid sample of 2-2.5 mL was collected in a falcon tube to measure the pH and the optical 
density (OD). This process was repeated with the second reactor as well. 
The following sampling was done with the help of the liquid samples for pH control: 
• pH:  
An automated system comprising a pH meter, a pump, and base and acid solutions regulated the 
pH inside the reactors. When the pH falls outside a preset range (6.9-7.1), the pH sensor sends a 
signal to the pump, which gradually adds base or acid to the reactor as needed. The added acid and 
base only served to bring the pH to the appropriate level rather than engaging in a chemical reaction 
with the components inside the reactor. To check if this system is working properly, tests are 
performed regularly by taking liquid samples from the media and testing its pH offline.

HPLC shown in Fig.12, was used to analyze the amount of volatile fatty acids and organic alcohols 
produced during fermentation and see if it is under stress. The sample preparation involves the 
following steps: 
• Centrifugation: 
The samples collected from the media in the Eppendorf tubes were kept inside the centrifuge. It 
was important to make sure that the tubes were closed well before and they were placed

symmetrically, the centrifuge spins at high speeds which separates the components of the sample 
based on their weight and density. The centrifuge was set at a speed of 10000 rotations per minute 
for 10 minutes, which helped in separating the mixture of cells from the liquid. This process helped 
in purifying the samples for HPLC analysis by removing the contaminants that might have 
hindered the analysis. The centrifuge effectively separates the solid microbial cells (biomass) from 
the liquid phase (supernatant). This separation is crucial as the supernatant contains dissolved 
metabolic products, such as volatile fatty acids (VFAs) and alcohols, which are key components 
for further analysis using HPLC. By removing the suspended cells and other particulates, the 
centrifugation process ensures that the supernatant is free from impurities that could interfere with 
analytical measurements, thereby enhancing the accuracy and reliability of the results. 
• Acidification: 
For this step, two other Eppendorf tubes were required for the clean sample (supernatant of the 
centrifuged sample). Since the sample pH was 7 and the eluent in the HPLC had a pH of 2-3 
(12mM H2SO4), the pH of the sample was reduced by using H2SO4 with a concentration of 10 % 
(w/w). Firstly, 1 mL of the sample was put inside a clean Eppendorf tube with the help of a 
micropipette, and then 30 µL of H2SO4 was added to it to reduce the pH of the sample below 3. 
The sample's acidification helped in the analysis as it optimized the separation of the components.  
• Filtration: 
The acidified samples were passed through a filter to remove any solid particles that might have 
interfered with the HPLC analysis. The filter used for this was made of nylon and had a pore size 
of 0.22 µm. The sample was taken from the Eppendorf tubs and put into sterile vials which were 
marked with the date, reactor number, and name for better identification. The strong acidity of 
H2SO4 helped to separate the compounds in the mixture.

The HPLC analysis focuses on identifying volatile fatty acids (VFAs) and organic alcohols as key 
indicators of system performance. Variations in their concentrations can reflect stress within the 
reactor. For instance, an increase in intermediate VFAs (e.g., acetic acid or propionic acid) may 
suggest incomplete fermentation or microbial inhibition. Similarly, the appearance or rise of less 
common products, such as iso-butyric acid or formic acid, could signal stress. A reduction in the 
production of desired products like methane or ethanol indicates suboptimal conditions, potentially 
due to pH imbalances, temperature shifts, or substrate limitations. These insights enable real-time 
monitoring of reactor health and optimization of the fermentation process for maximum 
productivity. 
The RI method was used, which runs for 45 minutes and at 63 ℃ to analyze each sample. The 
Shimadzu HPLC, equipped with column number 447031, was employed with a 20 µL injection 
volume and a flow rate of 0.6 L/min. This method relies on detecting changes in the refractive 
index of the mobile phase as the analytes pass through the detector, making it ideal for analyzing 
compounds such as alcohols and volatile fatty acids (VFAs) that do not strongly absorb UV or 
visible light. The specific properties of the compounds, the stationary phase material in the 
separation column, and the composition of the mobile phase played crucial roles in the separation 
==PAGE:45==

and quantification process. Precise temperature control was maintained to prevent baseline drift, 
ensuring accurate and reliable detection of these key metabolic byproducts. 
Analysis of the organic acids and alcohols produced during fermentation is necessary to determine 
our reaction's productivity and make any necessary adjustments to optimize productivity. These 
substances enabled us to identify and correct any errors as well as assess the reactor's performance 
under varied circumstances.

3.4 Calculation of Carbon yield and electron yield 
Carbon yield (C yield) and electron yield (e yield) are critical metrics for evaluating the efficiency 
of bioconversion processes, such as syngas biomethanation. Carbon yield measures the proportion 
of carbon from the substrate that is successfully converted into the desired product, reflecting the 
process's ability to utilize carbon resources effectively. Electron yield, on the other hand, tracks 
the efficiency of electron transfer from the substrate to the product, which is essential for 
understanding energy balance and microbial metabolism. These yields are calculated using the 
carbon moles (C-mol) and electron moles (E-mol) of both reactants and products given in Table 6. 
Carbon yield is determined by comparing the total carbon moles in the product to those in the 
substrate, while electron yield involves the ratio of electron moles transferred to the product 
relative to those available in the substrate. Together, these metrics provide a comprehensive 
understanding of the process efficiency and guide optimization for improved performance.

3.4.2 Electron Yield 
To calculate electron yield, the rate of production of the desired product (in mmol/min) is 
multiplied by its electron number (number of moles of electrons transferred per mole of the product 
during its formation). This value is then divided by the total amount of electrons available from 
the substrate. The electron number for a product is determined based on the stoichiometry of the 
redox reactions involved in its formation. This calculation helps quantify how efficiently electrons 
from the substrate are utilized in producing the target compound, providing insights into the 
electron balance and energy efficiency of the process.

4.1 Impact of Syngas Inflow Rate on Methane Productivity 
The experiments conducted in Trickle Bed Reactors (TBRs) TBR1 and TBR2 demonstrated that 
methane productivity strongly correlates with syngas inflow rates (3.60–7.20 mL/min). A steady 
increase in the syngas inflow rate from 3.60 to 4.80 mL/min on day 71 in TBR1 enabled the reactor 
to reach steady-state conditions, improving methane productivity and maintaining a consistent gas 
consumption profile. A similar trend was observed in TBR2, confirming the reproducibility of the 
process. The methane productivity of TBR1 and TBR2 are shown in Fig. 19 and Fig. 21, 
respectively, where the steady state for each condition is highlighted by yellow. The syngas 
conversion, i.e., the conversion of H2, CO, and CO2 in TBR1 and TBR2 are shown in Fig. 20 and 
Fig. 22, respectively, where the steady state for each condition is highlighted by yellow. 
Upon further increasing the syngas inflow rate from 4.80 to 6.00 mL/min on day 92, methane 
productivity in TBR1 reached approximately 7.0 mmol/Lbed/h. Comparatively, TBR2 exhibited 
higher gas consumption efficiencies, with hydrogen (H₂), carbon monoxide (CO), and carbon 
dioxide (CO₂) consumption rates of 95%, 85%, and 10%, respectively, versus TBR1's 
corresponding rates of 86%, 67%, and 15%. These discrepancies, initially attributed to analytical 
inconsistencies, were resolved following the replacement of the gas chromatograph (GC) column 
on day 279, as discussed in Section 5.3. 
Compared to literature values, methane productivity achieved in this study aligns well with 
reported methane evolution rates of 8.22 mmol/Lbed/h in Trickle Bed Reactors operating under 
similar conditions [58]. Other studies report that peak methane production rates of 11.8 
mmol/Lbed/h, achieved at moderate gas flow rates, with declines observed at higher flow rates due 
to CO inhibition [72]. These findings validate the observed performance trends, particularly under 
optimized inflow rates.

4.1.1 Trends in Gas Consumption 
Figures 19 and 20 illustrate methane productivity and gas consumption profiles in TBR1, while 
Figures 21 and 22 present the corresponding data for TBR2. Key observations include: 
1. Hydrogen (H₂) Consumption: H₂ exhibited the highest consumption rates across both 
reactors, with nearly complete utilization (96.20 ± 2.44 %) achieved in TBR2 and (87.32 
± 1.37 %) achieved in TBR1 at gas inflow rates (6.00 mL/min). This aligns with the 
stoichiometric requirements of syngas biomethanation, where H₂ serves as a critical 
electron donor for CO₂ reduction to methane. 
2. Carbon Monoxide (CO) Consumption: CO consumption followed a similar trend, 
though at slightly lower efficiencies (85.44 ± 0.84 % in TBR2 and 69.18 ± 2.86 % in TBR1). 
Lower CO consumption in TBR1 during certain periods coincided with transient 
operational disturbances. 
3. Carbon Dioxide (CO₂) Accumulation: CO₂ consumption was relatively lower, with 
notable accumulation observed in TBR1 at high syngas inflow rates (7.20 mL/min). This

trend is consistent with literature findings, where CO₂ accumulation reduced microbial

Gas consumption rates displayed distinct transient phases during operational interruptions, such as

temporary declines in gas consumption and methane productivity, emphasizing the importance of 
maintaining stable operational conditions. 
4.1.2 Comparative Analysis Between TBR1 and TBR2 
Despite operating under identical conditions, TBR2 consistently outperformed TBR1 in terms of 
gas consumption efficiency and methane productivity. Post-GC column replacement, methane 
productivity at inflow rates of 6.00–7.20 mL/min closely matched the model predictions further 
validating the robustness of the experimental setup. These findings align with previous studies 
reporting near-complete consumption of syngas components under optimized conditions [56][57]. 
4.1.3 Methane Productivity at Maximum Inflow Rates 
During the final experimental phase, syngas inflow was increased to 7.20 mL/min on day 309 in 
TBR1. TBR 1 and TBR 2 achieved peak methane productivity rates of approximately 7.82 ± 0.30 
mmol/Lbed/h and 9.10 ± 0.17 mmol/Lbed/h. The difference in productivity can be seen due to the 
varying microbial concentration which was analyzed with the help of Optical density (OD). The 
OD of TBR 1 was approximately 2.53 ± 0.19 and for TBR 2 it was 2.92 ± 0.26. Thus, both the 
TBRs had different methane productivities under steady state. These results demonstrate the 
potential of TBR systems to operate efficiently at high inflow rates, achieving methane yields 
comparable to those reported in large-scale biomethanation studies [58][72]. 
4.1.4 Comparative Analysis of Results from Literature (Serial Numbers 18-26 in Table 2) 
The results from this study were compared with findings reported in the literature for methane 
production under various conditions (refer to Table 2, serial numbers 18–26). Key comparisons 
include: 
1. Methane Productivity Trends: Methane productivity in this study peaked at 9.10 ± 0.17 
mmol/Lbed/h at 60°C and 7.20 mL/min syngas inflow rate, closely aligning with the values 
reported for TBRs under similar thermophilic conditions, such as 10.6 mmol/Lbed/h at 60°C 
[61] and 15.4 mmol/L/h at 55°C [60]. 
2. Gas Conversion Efficiencies: CO and H₂ consumption efficiencies in this study (up to 
95%) were comparable to literature reports. For instance, studies at 55°C achieved 100% 
CO conversion after 27 days, with H₂ conversion rates exceeding 94.7% [56,72]. The

observed efficiencies highlight the capability of TBR systems to operate effectively under 
optimized flow rates and microbial conditions.  
3. Reactor Volume and Operating Conditions: This study’s reactors (220 mL) performed 
comparably to larger systems, such as a 5 L reactor achieving methane productivity of 17.6 
mmol/Lbed/h at 60°C [59]. While larger reactors offer higher absolute productivity, gas 
retention times and flow dynamics are critical factors influencing performance, as seen in 
the 0.25 L system achieving 2–3 mmol/Lbed/h [58]. 
4. Temperature Effects: Methane production increased significantly under thermophilic 
conditions, consistent with literature trends. For example, studies at 60°C achieved 
methane yields approximately double those under mesophilic conditions (37°C), 
demonstrating the impact of elevated temperatures on methanogenic activity [23,61]. 
5. Inoculum Variability: Differences in microbial consortia among studies, such as the use 
of thermophilic digestate [56] or mixed cultures from wastewater [57], influence gas 
consumption and productivity. This study’s inoculum maintained stable performance 
across varied gas inflow rates, reflecting robust microbial activity. 
Overall, the findings from this study align with established literature, demonstrating the 
effectiveness of TBR systems in achieving high methane productivity and gas conversion 
efficiencies. The observed trends, such as enhanced methane yields under thermophilic conditions 
and efficient syngas utilization, underscore the importance of optimizing reactor parameters for 
large-scale biomethanation applications. 
4.1.5 Significance of Steady-State Operation 
The results highlight the critical importance of achieving and maintaining steady-state conditions 
for optimal reactor performance. Periods of transient operation, such as those caused by electrical 
fluctuations or analytical inconsistencies, significantly impacted gas consumption and methane 
productivity. However, the resilience of the system allowed for recovery and stabilization, 
underscoring the robustness of the TBR design for syngas biomethanation.

Discussion 
The experimental validation aligns well with the model’s predictions, confirming that optimized 
gas flow rates enhance methane yield while demonstrating that liquid recirculation can be reduced 
without significantly impacting reactor performance. This highlights the feasibility of reducing 
energy inputs while maintaining efficient biomethanation. The sinusoidal trend in liquid 
recirculation further suggests that optimization strategies must balance energy efficiency with 
process stability. Future studies should explore further reductions in liquid recirculation to 
determine the absolute minimum threshold before performance deteriorates significantly. 
These findings contribute to the broader objective of making syngas biomethanation in trickle bed 
reactors, both more efficient and scalable for industrial applications. 
4.2 Effect of Liquid Recirculation Rate on Methane Production 
The experiments conducted in Trickle Bed Reactors (TBRs) TBR1 and TBR2 demonstrated that 
the liquid recirculation can be decreased to save up on power consumption, which is predicted by 
the model as well (1600-830 L/Lbed/d). A steady decrease in the liquid recirculation rate from 1600-
1000 L/Lbed/d on day 359 in TBR1 enabled the reactor to reach steady-state conditions, improving 
methane productivity and maintaining a consistent gas consumption profile. A similar trend was 
observed in TBR2, confirming the reproducibility of the process. The methane productivity of 
TBR1 and TBR2 are shown in Fig. 23 and Fig. 25, respectively, where the steady state for each 
condition is highlighted by yellow. The syngas conversion, i.e., the conversion of H2, CO, and CO2 
in TBR1 and TBR2 are shown in Fig. 24 and Fig. 26, respectively, where the steady state for each 
condition is highlighted by yellow. 
Upon further decrease in the liquid recirculation rate from 1000-830 L/Lbed/d on day 398, methane 
productivity in TBR1 reached approximately 6.82 ± 0.03 mmol/Lbed/h, whereas in TBR 2 methane 
productivity remained almost at the same value (8.93 ± 0.05 mmol/Lbed/h) as at liquid recirculation 
rate of 1600 L/Lbed/d, the main reason for the discrepancies in the performances of both the TBRs 
was the density of microbial communities in the respective TBRs. Comparatively, TBR2 exhibited 
higher gas consumption efficiencies, with hydrogen (H₂) and carbon monoxide (CO) consumption 
rates of 87.68 ± 1.07 % and 64.38 ± 1.98 %, respectively, versus TBR1's corresponding rates of

4.2.1 Trends in Gas Consumption 
The liquid recirculation rate plays a crucial role in enhancing mass transfer efficiency and ensuring 
an even distribution of nutrients to microbial communities in the reactor. A comparative analysis 
of gas consumption trends at varying recirculation rates indicates that an optimal range exists, 
beyond which additional increases do not yield significant productivity gains. This trend is 
consistent with the hypothesis that excessive recirculation can lead to stress on microbial biofilms, 
reducing their stability. 
4.2.2 Comparative Analysis Between TBR1 and TBR2 
To evaluate the impact of liquid recirculation, the performance of two trickle bed reactors (TBR1 
and TBR2) was compared under identical gas flow conditions and varying liquid recirculation 
rates. The results showed that TBR1 experienced a drop in methane productivity and gas 
conversion rates after changes in liquid recirculation, while TBR2 remained relatively stable. 
Despite these initial variations, both reactors reached a stable operating state within approximately 
two weeks. This suggests that while recirculation-induced stress can temporarily affect microbial 
activity, the system eventually adapts and regains stability over time.

4.2.3 Methane Productivity at Reduced Recirculation Rates 
When the liquid recirculation rate was gradually decreased, methane productivity remained nearly 
unchanged, indicating that the system was not heavily dependent on high recirculation for effective 
gas-to-liquid transfer. This suggests that the process can operate efficiently at lower recirculation 
rates, potentially reducing operational costs. Additionally, the gas conversion efficiency did not

liquid flow rates highlights an opportunity to optimize energy consumption by lowering 
recirculation rates while maintaining productivity. This finding provides a promising pathway for 
improving the energy efficiency of trickle bed reactors without compromising methane yield.

To experimentally validate the findings, the reactor was operated under optimized conditions for

yield while maintaining stable microbial activity. Further tests under varied conditions reaffirmed

biofilm retention, paving the way for process scale-up in industrial applications.

Discussion 
The findings from this study demonstrate that liquid recirculation plays a vital role in optimizing 
methane production in trickle bed reactors. By fine-tuning the recirculation rate, a balance can be 
achieved between improving mass transfer and maintaining biofilm integrity. Excessive 
recirculation, while beneficial in the short term for gas dissolution, leads to diminished long-term 
stability due to increased shear stress and reduced gas retention times. The comparative analysis 
with literature further validates the significance of this parameter in reactor performance. 
Ultimately, the ability to control and optimize liquid recirculation makes trickle bed reactors a 
viable solution for large-scale syngas biomethanation, offering enhanced process stability and 
higher methane yields under industrial conditions. 
5 Conclusion 
This study aimed to experimentally validate the model predictions for syngas biomethanation in a 
Trickle Bed Reactor (TBR) by analyzing the effects of varying liquid recirculation rates and syngas 
flow rates on methane productivity. The experimental results confirmed that optimizing these 
parameters significantly influences gas-liquid mass transfer, microbial activity, and overall 
methane yield. A key finding was that increasing the syngas flow rate enhanced CO conversion 
and methane productivity, supporting the model's prediction that greater gas availability improves 
substrate utilization by methanogens. However, beyond a certain threshold, excessive gas flow 
rates led to reduced retention times, limiting microbial interaction and decreasing conversion 
efficiency. Similarly, while moderate liquid recirculation improved gas-liquid contact and mass 
transfer, excessive recirculation did not proportionally enhance methane yields, aligning with the 
model’s sinusoidal trend prediction. Despite these successes, some discrepancies between 
experimental and predicted values highlight areas for further optimization. Differences in gas 
consumption and methane productivity in both the reactors suggest that additional factors, such as 
microbial adaptation, biofilm heterogeneity, and reactor hydrodynamics, may require further 
investigation. Future research should explore hydrogen supplementation to enhance syngas 
biomethanation efficiency, ensuring complete conversion of carbon from syngas into methane. 
Additionally, the impact of different microbial consortia on process stability and methane yields 
should be studied, as well as the potential benefits of alternative reactor designs, such as hybrid 
systems combining TBRs with membrane bioreactors. Investigating long-term operational 
stability under industrial-scale conditions, optimizing gas-liquid mass transfer strategies are also 
promising research directions. In conclusion, this study provides strong experimental validation 
for the proposed model and confirms that syngas biomethanation in TBRs is a viable pathway for 
sustainable methane production. The findings contribute to the advancement of biofuel technology 
and lay the groundwork for scaling up biomethanation processes for industrial applications.
