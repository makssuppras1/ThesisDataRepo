Abstract
In this thesis we have done a computational study on second order topological 2D materials. Re­
cent advances in theoretical classification of topological crystalline materials have made it possible
to calculate invariants, which can characterise almost all 2D materials with rotational symmetries
and with finite spin­orbit coupling (Schindler et al., 2019). We have implemented algorthims to
calculate nested Wilson loop invariants in a tight­binding environment and rotation indicator in­
variants within the density functional theory code GPAW (Mortensen et al., 2005), (Enkovaara
et al., 2010). The nested Wilson loop was used to characterise Pb2Te2 as trivial, and various chal­
lenges were presented which makes it impractical for high­throughput calculations. We found two
non­trivial material candidates from rotations eigenvalues, MoS2 was found to realise a 1b ob­
structed atomic limit (OAL). A trimer was localised on the 1b Wyckoff position and two dimers on
1c, which resulted in fractionalised corner charges Qc = 2
3e. Similarly, HfTe2 also had two dimers
on 1c realising a 1c OAL, which had corner charges that weren’t fractionalised. Finite flake calcu­
lations were done in order to study the boundary states depending on the geometry of termination.
The edges were seen to host either localised corner charges or 1D metallic edges depending on the
formal polarisation vector. In either case the boundary state only appeared when the respective
Wyckoff position was cut on the boundary.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Topological matter is a fairly new term within the field of condensed matter physics, but has roots
in a system which is certainly is not in the ground state of the material. Initially the quantum hall
effect was a property of an electronic system under strong magnetic fields and low temperatures,
which caused the electrons to enter cyclotron orbits. When a given cyclotron orbit was filled,
an interesting insulating state appeared where so called skipping electrons appeared on the edges.
This displayed the essence of what later would become topological matter, that is, insulating matter
with conducting edges.
The question was then whether a material ground state could exhibit such an exotic state of matter
intrinsically, without the need for external magnetic fields. The search began with the first model
for topological matter namely the anomalous quantum hall insulator (Haldane, 1988) (Later coined
Chern insulator) in which neatly arranged complex hopping parameters served the same purpose
as the magnetic field. The Chern insulator was a difficult model to realise in a real material due to
the assumption of broken time reversal symmetry. But it served as inspiration for a time­reversal
symmetric model where two Chern insulators with opposite spins were coupled together, which
is known as the quantum spin­hall insulator (Kane and Mele, 2005) (Later coined 2D topologi­
cal insulator). The 2D topological insulator turned out to be much easier to realise, and the field
has since then made tremendous progress both theoretically and experimentally. Since then has all
non­interacting gapped topological phases been classified according to the three fundamental sym­
metries Time­reversal, particle­hole and chiral symmetries for any dimension (Ryu et al., 2010).
Experimentally, this has lead to numerous new material discoveries for potential applications such
as spin­momentum locking for use in spintronics, heat­dissipation­free surface states for battery
storage and the topological quantum computer paradigm.
Recently the inclusion of crystalline symmetries such as rotations has lead to a concept called topo­
logical crystalline insulators, some of which has been shown to display higher order topological
effects. That is, insulating materials where the boundary is in fact not conducting, but rather the
boundary of the boundary becomes conducting. Thus in a 3D second order topological insulator
such as Bismuth1 (Schindler et al., 2018), the bulk and the surfaces are insulating but the hinges
are conducting. In 2D materials, a second order topological insulator has insulating bulk and edge,
but localised charges on the corners. Thus the modern definition of topological matter is that there
exist an obstruction to adiabatically reach the atomic insulator, where all atoms are isolated from
each other (Bradlyn et al., 2017).
In this thesis, we will study 2D second order topological insulators from first principles where the
topological protection due to the rotational symmetries of the lattice. The invariants that classify

In the atomic limit, the distance between molecules are increased to infinity, such that electrons
have no way of moving from one molecule to another. We can identify that the electron must be lo­
calised on the center of the molecule as is required by inversion symmetry. This is a general feature
of the trivial atomic limit, namely that localised electrons are located on the atomic sites. In a trivial
insulator we can slowly increase the distance between atoms until reaching the atomic limit, this
process is obstructed in a topological material. The obstructed atomic limit however has primarily
two facets called ’Wannier obstruction’ and ’obstructed atomic limit’(OAL). Wannier obstruction
is the stronger condition where even constructing localised electronic orbitals is prohibited. This
is the case for metals and traditional first order topological insulators such as the Chern and Z2
insulators, where metallic boundary states cannot be localised. The obstructed atomic limit is very
similar to the trivial limit where it is possible to create localised orbitals. However now the elec­
trons can be localised away from any nuclei and thus when approaching the atomic limit, the band
gap must close when the localised electrons jump to the nuclei. In this thesis we will work only
with materials in OAL phases. In the SSH model we find an obstructed limit when electrons are
localised on the dimers formed between unit cells as is allowed by inversion symmetry as seen in
Figure 2.1c.
Clearly the electronic structure of OALs and trivial insulators is fundamentally different at a mi­
croscopic level. But when considering the electronic band structure, very little information can be
seen at a glance. Instead we see apparent changes when considering the neutrality of the chain.
In the trivial atomic limit the chain is clearly neutral with N electrons located at N dimers, as per
construction. However in the obstructed atomic limit, there are N electrons to be placed on N −1
dimers in the bulk. The last electron then cannot occupy the state on either edge due to symmetry,
and it would be forced to fractionalise in order to distribute its charge evenly. The two edge states
would furthermore need to be degenerate in energy due to inversion symmetry, only one of which
would be occupied at neutrality. This brings us exactly to the filling anomaly, which is the appar­
ent situation where we cannot fill the occupied bands in a way that respects both the symmetry
and charge neutrality while simultaneously remain gapless. We can define the filling anomaly for
systems which can be divided into n symmetry related sectors as (Benalcazar et al., 2019)

2.4
Wannier bands
In the 1D case there only exists one type of Wilson loop, that is where the contour C is a straight
line k →k+2π. However, in higher dimensions we are met with the challenge of choosing ’which
Wilson loop contour should one construct?’. You can in principle construct any arbitrary contour
in the Brilluoin zone that could yield a non­trivial topological invariant (Bradlyn et al., 2019). The
problem of general Wilson loop classification is out of scope for this thesis.
Here we limit ourselves to straight line Wilson loops starting from a specified point k1 = (kx, ky)

The analysis is organised into two parts of similar structure, first nested Wilson loops then rotation

3.2
DFT calculation details
The DFT calculations has been carried out using the DTU physics developed GPAW Python pack­
age (Mortensen et al., 2005), (Enkovaara et al., 2010), (Larsen et al., 2017). In calculations of band
topology we require well calculated bond lengths and angles, and a qualitative description of the
band structure. Since there is no need for quantitatively accurate band features, we use the Gen­
eralised Gradient Approximation that is due Perdew, Burke and Ernzerhof (PBE). The boundary
conditions are chosen to be periodic in all directions, although since we consider two­dimensional
crystal, we add a considerable amount of inter­layer vacuum to ensure no coupling. Whenever

possible the starting point for our calculations are the downloadable structures in the C2DB. The
structures are then iteratively relaxed using the Broyden­Fletcher­Goldfarb­Shanno (BFGS) algo­
rithm with a high energy cutoff PW = 800 eV, and fine k­point density of 12Å−1. The total
energy and density is finally calculated on a symmetrical Monkhorst­Pack (25, 25, 1) grid, which
is the starting point for extracting wave functions. Using this fixed density, we then sampled the
k­space depending on the required calculations. For parallel transport calculations we use a very
asymmetrical Monkhorst­Pack of 7 k­points in the direction of the loop, and 200 points parallel to
it. Where the large density in the parallel direction is required in order to check which bands are
gapped. Note if one wants to do Wilson loop contours which are not orthogonal, one might consider
alternative k­point sampling schemes, such as the Cunningham points (Cunningham, 1974). For
Rotation indicator invariants we only need to sample the wave function in high symmetry points
(Γ, K, K′, M) for hexagonal unit cells and (Γ, X, M, Y ) for rectangular unit cells (Setyawan and
Curtarolo, 2010), which is considerably more computationally lightweight.

Wannier bands
The Wannier bands (or Berry phase spectra) was introduced as a powerful way to analyse a given
material, as it is adiabatically connected to a edge Hamiltonian spectrum. However, the reality is
rarely as neat as the simple toy models which are used to showcase the physics.1 We will therefore
now take it a step further and analyse Wannier bands of real materials, which might not be as
simple, but still rather illuminating. For this purpose we have chosen a Z2 insulator, and a highly
symmetric insulator with the possibility of being non­trivial.
The first experimentally fabricated topological Z2 insulator we a 2D HgTe/CdTe quantum well
structure. And so it is fitting that we look at the Wannier bands of a 2D HgTe structure in the space
group P3m1. The Wannier bands calculated are shown in Figure 3.4, where we have the extra

3.4
Pb2Te2
When finding materials that are topological crystalline insulators using Wilson loop invariants,
one needs to be particularly careful of a few things, which we will demonstrate using Pb2Te2 as
an example. Pb2Te2 is in the Space group P4/nmm, with two Pb atoms located on 2c and two Te
atoms located on C2 rotated 2c Wyckoff positions. This crystal has been chosen because it has both
C4 symmetry and inversion symmetry. Therefore in reality when characterising this material one
can simply calculate inversion indicator invariants. We can therefore use the inversion invariants
to test the result of the Wilson loop invariants.
Note that in the C2DB, this material has a different calculated space group, however this due to
convergence issues of the structure where the unit cell and atomic positions vary slightly from the
symmetric structure. We therefore find the wrong symmetry group using the spglib python library
with default parameters. One can see it is wrong by looking at the calculated site symmetries of
the Wyckoff positions, which are all 1 in this case, that is, no symmetries at all. By increasing the
tolerance of spglib one finds the mentioned Space group and Wyckoff positions.
To demonstrate the issues, observe the berry phase spectrum in Figure 3.5a, calculated directly
using the structure from the C2DB. The only indication that this material could have higher order
topology is the fact that the groups of bands are separated by a gap. It would be very difficult to
choose bands for the nested procedure as it is clearly not particle­hole symmetric. However, the
first image of the Wannier spectrum is related to the projection of the 2D unit cell which can be
placed anywhere. Thus translating the unit cell can likewise move the first image of the Wannier
spectrum. Thus we have a chance to recover the particle­hole symmetry if we can find the right unit
cell. The most consistent way to place the unit cell found, was to set the origin on the symmetry
axis (in the case of rotations) or on the symmetry point (in the case of inversion). Pb2Te2 has a
fourfold axis of rotation on each atomic site and an inversion point in the middle of the 4 atoms.
Thus we plot the the Wannier spectrum for these two unit cell choices in Figures 3.5b, 3.5c. We
observe that for each symmetry we find a different particle­hole symmetries.

Numerical stability
We saw in general doing the finite flakes calculations, that the charge distribution of the corner
charges would change quite a bit depending on the size of the flake. In general if the flake was too
small, the exponentially localised states would still be finite at the crossover between two symmetry
sectors, resulting in a spontaneous breaking of the symmetry. For medium sizes we saw that the
states were properly symmetrically localised, and thus we used these sizes unless specified. In
the limit of large structures on the order of 10.000 atoms, we saw that the symmetry was broken.

Summary of MoS2
We have calculated the rotation invariants of the main valence bands of MoS2 which showed that
the highest occupied band has a Wannier center localised on the Wyckoff position 1b in the crystal
lattice, and two deep bands with Wannier centers localised on the sulfur dimer on 1c. Thus it was
classified as a 1b OAL or 1b + 1c OAL to provide more detail. The Wyckoff position 1b is not
occupied by any atomic nuclei, and hence the Wannier center must move when approaching the
atomic limit. The movement of the Wannier center charge corresponds to closing the electronic gap,
which is a topological quantum transition. We therefore see that monolayer MoS2 is a topological
crystalline insulator protected by the C3 rotational symmetry and Time­reversal symmetry. The
bulk­boundary correspondence was studied for various types of flake terminations. Tools that were
useful for characterisation were the boundary cutting of the particular Wyckoff position 1b, and the
polarisation vectors. The polarisation vector was particularly nice for MoS2 as it was the vector
going from 1a Wyckoff position to the 1b Wyckoff position. This is in the armchair direction,
which turned out to be important for the boundary effects of different geometries. We saw that
second order topological corner charges occurred when the geometry had armchair edges and the
corner of the flake cut through the Wyckoff position 1b. These corner charges were shown be
fractionalised with a magnitude of 2/3e, which was predicted from the rotation eigenvalues. They
were shown on both a triangular flake and a hexagonal flake, where they were shown to be stable
against symmetry obeying edge perturbations. We discovered that by breaking the filling anomaly
you could calculate the polarisation from the change in charge density. This was done for both a
large hexagonal and triangular flake.
In contrast, when boundary had zigzag edges and cut through the 1b Wyckoff position, we observed
the appearance of one­dimensional metallic edges. The most realistic triangle (Bollinger et al.,
2001) was also simulated, although the boundary did not cut through the Wyckoff position 1b. The
material did not have conducting edges or corner charges at the Fermi level, however a metallic
edge did appear on the sulfur dimers at 0.08 eV above the Fermi level. This is likely to be due to
the deep bands with localised wannier centers on Wyckoff position 1c. Metallic edges is typically
associated with conventional first order topological phases. We therefore see that MoS2 can display
both characteristics both of a first­ and second order topological crystalline insulator depending on
the boundary termination.

Chapter 4
Conclusion
We have implemented contemporary tools to calculate topological invariants which predicts the
existence of protected fractionalised corner charges in 2D materials. The mechanism which forced
quantised charges to manifest on the corners were seen to be analogous to the dimerisation of the
SSH model. As a consequence the material entered an obstructed atomic limit where electrons
could be localised on Wyckoff positions away from the atoms of the crystal. The Wannier center
of the electron is then topologically protected because its symmetry representation is incompatible
in some sense with the rest of the lattice (Bradlyn et al., 2017). When those Wyckoff positions
were cut by the boundary of finite geometries, a filling anomaly were seen to appear, where only
a fraction of the symmetry related corner states could be occupied at neutrality. This was exactly
the conditions required for fractionalised corner charges to appear.
The invariants were calculated with (nested) Wilson loop calculations and rotation eigenvalues.
The Wilson loop had the advantage of having a straight forward interpretation in terms of pro­
jected Wannier centers. The implementation of nested Wilson loops was partially successful, with
only a tight­binding algorithm working properly. The code was tested on Pb2Te2, which had both
inversion symmetry and C4 rotational symmetry. We found it to be trivial, with both inversion
symmetry invariants and the Wilson loop invariants. The nested invariant was found using a Wan­
nierized tight­binding Hamiltonian for the 12 highest occupied electrons. Nevertheless the nested
Wilson loop procedure had some limitations in its applicability, alone it could classify only C2
symmetric crystals. Together with rotation eigenvalues they are also able to classify C3 and C6,
but it is not possible to classify C4 symmetric crystals completely.
The rotational indicator invariants were simpler computational wise, and fully classifies C3 sym­
metric systems. The relation between these invariants and Wyckoff positions were a bit more
difficult, as one needed to use the group theoretical tools of topological quantum chemistry (Brad­
lyn et al., 2017). The algorithm to calculate rotation eigenvalues were implemented directly in the
density functional theory code GPAW, and were tested on As2 for verification which was found
in the expected 3c obstructed atomic limit. Using these invariants, we predicted monolayer MoS2
to be a higher order topological insulator hosting fractionalised corner charges of magnitude 2/3e.
From rotation invariants we inferred that it had one state localised on the 1b Wyckoff position, and
two states localised on the 1c Wyckoff position. The primary boundary states indicated that it was
a 1b OAL, however the 1c did have physical consequences as well. We also found HfTe2 to be in a
1c OAL and ZrSe2 to be in a 1a OAL, however none of them were expected to host fractionalised
corner charges. Interestingly, HfTe2 turned out to still have non­fractionalised corner charges.
We studied several finite geometries of MoS2 and HfTe2 as they provided complimentary infor­
mation on the boundary effects due to the similarity of the two dimers on 1c. We first confirmed

the localised corner charges on hexagonal flakes, where the states appeared on the expected 1b
corners for MoS2 and on the 1c corners for HfTe2. In order to analyse different boundary termina­
tions we used that fact that both materials have a topological polarisation in the armchair direction.
Consequentially all geometries with armchair edges displayed the (fractionalised) corner charges.
When the polarisation vector was parallel to the surface normal, we saw 1D metallic edges appear
on the condition that the edge cut through the Wyckoff positions of the dimer. Triangular MoS2
flakes was special in this regard as conducting edges were both present on edges cutting through
1b and 1c (0.08eV above the Fermi level). In contrast, triangular HfTe2 flakes had only conducting
edges when the 1c Wyckoff position were cut along the edges as expected. Thus we saw that even
though the fractionalised corner states in MoS2 is due to the 1b Wannier center, the two 1c Wannier
centers can still have real boundary consequences. It is therefore essential to know which Wyckoff
positions has topologically bound dimers and not only the corner or edge manifestations of specific
geometries.
In a one dimensional chain, we could calculate the topological polarisation directly from breaking
the filling anomaly, which was also shown to be the case for MoS2. But As2 had a filling anomaly,
but was found to not have a topological polarisation, and conversely HfTe2 did not have a filling
anomaly but did have a topological polarisation. Thus, the connection between polarisation and the
non­trivially occupied Wyckoff positions is not clear and should be studied further. We note that
the polarisation vector of MoS2 and HfTe2 points at the Wyckoff position 1b and 1c, respectively.
One might speculate that the polarisation is calculable and points towards the Wyckoff position,
if it has a multiplicity of one. However you would then ask ”How does MoS2 choose between 1b
and 1c” since it contain a non­trivial Wannier center on both.
Originally the field of topological crystalline insulators were concerned with higher order multi­
pole moments, however it was difficult to generalise for non­rectangular systems. It was realised
that without breaking the symmetry of the crystal, one could predict the appearance of fractional
corner charges. These corner charges can then be directly related to the possible multi­pole mo­
ments that can appear upon breaking the symmetry. Thus the fractionalised corner charges became
main higher order topological boundary effect. The fractionalised corner charge magnitude were
calculated from a linear combination of topological bulk invariant. However the invariants them­
selves turned out to have a real space interpretation within the bulk, which directly relates to where
the corner charges appear. The real space interpretation of the invariants is central to classify all
crystalline symmetries, and thus modern approaches look for so called (partial) real space invari­
ants which directly calculate the topological protected occupancy of Wyckoff positions (Kooi et al.,
2021), (Song et al., 2020). These invariants are an extremely useful tool because we can predict
which boundary terminations will result in conducting edges1 and corner charges as we have seen
in this thesis.
