Abstract
In this report an tin hypothiodiphosphate (SPS) crystal is characterised and analysed
for application in a self organising cavity with an adaptive Fabry-Perot filter, a proven
method for making multi longitudinal mode lasers single mode. The characterisation
is primarily into the polarization manipulating behaviour of SPS crystals. In reviewing
the crystal for intracavity operation a series of two-wave mixing experiments are exe-
cuted, both in the SPS and in a barium titanate (BaTiO3 ) crystal. The measurements
on the BaTiO3 crystal is mainly for comparative purposes. A series of measurements
is executed using the SPS crystal to find the response time and coupling strength,
Γ dependence on light intensity, I. It is found that for I = 4 W/cm2 the coupling
strength can be up to Γ = 0.8 cm−1, and hence a self organising cavity could be in
reach. Additionally a dependence of the fast response time on light intensity is found
across both polarizations and two crystal propagation directions. Unfortunately the
crystal polish does not allow for intracavity operation, as the polished facets are too
rough, and would cause cavity losses.

1 | Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Since the first laser was built in 1960, the area of application have grown immensely,
and is now an integrated part of of daily life, so so well that many people do not realise
it. The biggest application is for optical communication, transmitting ones and zeros
across the globe. Lasers have a massive application potential in bioanalysis, where
the high intensity can be used for photoluminescence measurements and Raman
spectroscopy [2]. Photoluminescence does not require a low linewidth, however high
powers are often desired to have a high signal to noise ratio. Raman spectroscopy
does require, close to, monochromatic light, and hence solid-state lasers have been
used in the past, however with the advancement of tapered laser diodes and clever
cavity designs, narrow line laser diodes with high power can be manufactured. Ta-
pered laser diodes are incredibly sensitive to feedback, and can fluctuate heavily in
output power, which is undesirable [3].
The photorefractive effect was first discovered in the mid 60’s in lithium niobate at
the Bell Laboratories [4] when researching semiconductor transistors, light emitting
diodes, gas lasers and much more. The basics of the photorefractive effect is that
the light incident to the photorefractive material can move the electrons, and hence
change the refractive index of the material. If an interference pattern is present in
the crystal, the electrons will follow the pattern, making a periodic grating, similar to a
Bragg Volume grating, only it’s made by the light so for that wavelength it is perfect,
meaning only a small deviation in wavelength will significantly change the effect of
the grating [4].
A typical laser diode has several longitudinal modes competing for the finite gain
in the chip [5], meaning the outcoming light will not be sufficiently narrow for applica-
tions such as Raman spectroscopy, optical communication etc. Introducing a pho-
torefractive crystal in the cavity will force the the oscillations to a single longitudinal
mode, due to the nature of the photorefractive effect, which will be most pronounced
for the strongest mode.

1.2. PROJECT DESCRIPTION
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.2
Project Description
The project builds on two previous, smaller, projects also carried out by me, the first of
which tested the response of several photorefractive crystals at 800 nm, using a self
organising cavity with an adaptive Fabry-Perot filter (SOCwAFP) system [1, 6]. The
other project was based on the tin hypothiodiphosphate crystal used in this project,
but was primarily characterised by a green (532 nm) laser, which the crystal absorbs
almost completely.
The work described in this report is the work done in the thesis period, however
a few measurements from the previous work has been included for context, i.e. the
absorption spectrum of SPS.
In this project I have characterised a 1% Tellurium doped SPS crystal, polished
by the company Photon Laser-Optik, which has left the surface relatively uneven, as
the crystal is quite soft. Initially it was the plan to put the crystal in a laser cavity,
but the surface roughness has made this impossible, as the scattering will yield too
much of a loss to have overall gain. The poor surface quality meant the project had
to take a different direction, and has mainly focused on characterising the crystal,
finding its dielectric axes, and focusing heavily on general two wave mixing, which is
the working principle of the self organising cavity.
For reference purposes, and to see if the setup was at all working, a Rhodium
doped barium titanate crystal was used, as these had shown responses in the near
infrared beyond 1 µm [7]. The problems with barium titanate are that the response
is comparatively slow, and the crystal has considerable scattering, as its main appli-
cation is as a phase conjugated mirror.
1.3
Related Work
This particular crystal was made for the group I have done the project with, so no one
has characterised it much, other than Prof. Dr. A. Grabar who did x-ray diffraction to
gather information about the crystal axes. As such, the work done here isn’t copied
from anyone in particular, but rather worked out as it’s done.
Originally the plan was to produce a SOCwAFP similar to the one presented by
[6, 8] or [9], or suggested in [1]. However due to the specific crystal, the weight will
instead be on TWM.
Two wave mixing is an often used technique to test the photorefractive response
of a given crystal, and is seen in papers presenting new dopings in materials i.e.
[10–17], and is an important parameter to have when designing a SOCwAFP, as it
will determine the reflectivity of the Fabry-Perot filter. One could also build onto this,
as the wavelength is determined by the strongest mode in the cavity, one could have
a master oscillator outside the cavity, and inject it temporarily, where after the cavity
would be stable on the introduced wavelength, provided there is the gain available to
have that mode lasing in the cavity, without external injection [18].

Gaussian beams have their name from the distribution of intensity, which follows
a Gaussian distribution [5]. These beams are practical from a mathematical point of
view, as even under focusing or collimation they will maintain the Gaussian intensity
distribution, and when focusing the beam, it can obtain the smallest spot size, com-
pared to any other beam, e.g. top-hat, of the same size. To characterise a beam

2.2. THE PHOTOREFRACTIVE EFFECT
2.2
The Photorefractive Effect
In condensed matter one of the material properties dictating the refractive index is
the distribution of electrons. In insulators and semiconductors There are no free
electrons, and so the electron distribution is constant in time, without outside pertur-
bations [19]. In photorefractive (PR) materials the light can move the charge carriers
from their place, by exciting them to, so-called, trap states where they can move in
the crystal. This is what is sketched in Figure 2.3. When two beams interfere in
the photorefractive crystal the intensity profile will have hills and valleys of intensity
(Section 2.1). The charge carriers will most often be moved from the high intensity
regions, and less so from the lower intensity regions, as there is a higher number of
photons to interact with the charge carriers in the high intensity region. This means
that after some time the electron density will follow the intensity pattern [1, 20]. At
least this will be the case if the drift process happens more often than the diffusion
process, enabling the charge carriers to stay in their displaced position. This in turn
will create a refractive index grating phase shifted to the intensity pattern [17, 20].
In Figure 2.3 the basic ways charge carriers can move in semiconductors are
sketched, the leftmost panel shows a photo active carrier being excited from its bound
space, and can drift (middle panel), while in the upper state it will drift according
to an electric field e.g. an interference pattern. The third panel shows a diffusion
in the lower level, a mechanism where the charge carriers will diffuse back to the
neutral electron distribution. The diffusion is not photo active, meaning it will happen
constantly and at a constant speed. When there is an outside application of energy,
e.g. an interference pattern, this will push the electron distribution to a certain state,
but the diffusion will counteract this with some speed. Since the drift moves charge
carriers with some speed, it can be translated to a current, I. The passive diffusion
speed is often referred to as the dark current Idark [1].

2.3
Tin Hypothiodiphosphate
This thesis is mainly focused on one photorefractive crystal, tin hypothiodiphosphate,
Sn2P2S6; henceforth referred to as SPS. SPS is a ferroelectric2 semiconductor, ex-
hibiting photorefractive effect from wavelengths at its bandedge, around 500 nm, up
to 1.3 µm [12]. Besides the PR effect, SPS exhibits birefringence, optical activity and
magneto-optic properties. On top of that, it has a refractive index of 2.8 at 1064 nm,
making the normal incidence Fresnel reflection 22%, and the refraction will diminish
the incidence angle greatly.
Birefringence is a phenomenon where polarization in different direction will have
different refractive indices. This means that if light comes in with polarization along
two of the axes of the dielectric ellipsoid or indicatrix3, one polarization component
will be retarded relative to the other, changing the polarization of the beam. The
birefringence can be either uni- or biaxial depending on whether the material has two
or three different refractive indices, respectively. Birefringence will be examined a bit
further in simulations, Section 3.1, and experiments, Section 4.2.
The optical activity comes from the asymmetry of the crystal lattice, and is hence
only observed when light is propagated out of the plane in which the crystal is sym-
metric. Optical activity rotates the polarization, contrary to the birefringence which
changes the state of the polarization, it remains linear from the optical activity. The
two effects can of course combine, where the optical activity can rotate the polar-
ization into one experiencing birefringence. The optical rotation will compensate for
itself, when the light is back-propagated through the crystal [5].
The crystal used in this work has been doped with 1% Te, making the TWM faster
and have higher coupling strength, compared to nominally pure SPS [11].
For TWM in SPS there are two time constants, connected to two types of charge
carriers. One is optically active and has a positive charge, the other is not optically
active and has a negative charge. The optically active charge carrier is mainly re-
sponsible for the fast response, where the optically passive charge carriers has a
slower response [25]. Intuitively this makes sense, as the optically active charge car-
riers respond to the light, and will distribute them selves after this interference pattern,
whereas the optically passive charge carriers will respond to the faster carriers, mak-
ing their response naturally slower.

2.5.2
Faraday Effect
The Faraday effect is intuitively similar to optical rotation, in that it rotates the polariza-
tion of light that enters a volume exhibiting the Faraday effect. The main difference
is that for optical activity the rotation is reversed when the propagation direction is
changed, where as for the Faraday effect, it is not [5]. For the Faraday effect a beam
of light that is propagated through the material and then reflected back on itself, ex-
periences the rotation twice [5]. The rotary power, ρ, is proportional to the magnetic
field, B, and the Verdet constant, VF, and is given in rad/m. VF is a material constant,
and varies greatly [5]. SPS has a VF = 115 rad/(T m) at 632 nm [31]. This is quite
high compared to some of the most magneto-optic materials [31].
The Faraday effect can be used to make an optical isolator (OI) used to protect the
laser from feedback from the system. They work by having a magneto-optic crystal
with a polarization beam splitter (PBS) before and after, the first one making sure the
incident light is linearly polarized, the light then propagates through a crystal which ro-
tates the light 45 degrees because of the Faraday effect. The second PBS is rotated
45 degrees, compared to the first one, to match the rotation of the crystal, meaning
when the light comes back at 45 degrees, it is rotated another 45 degrees and re-
jected by the first PBS, often dumped on a metal surface. The isolation achieved by
such devises can exceed 60 dB [32].

If one were to think the crystal axes provided were untrue, the crystal could be cut

Polarization Detuning
The interference pattern underlying the TWM is highly polarization dependent. Elec-
tromagnetic waves of orthogonal polarization will not interfere [4, 5], hence detuning
the polarization should extinguish the interference pattern. To verify that this was the
case the HWP that is ensuring parallel polarization after the PBS was turned a full
360 degrees, for both polarizations. Of course, only a 90 degree turn was needed,
strictly speaking 45 would be enough, due to the symmetry of the HWP. A full 360
degree turn was chosen to avoid problems with a possibly faulty HWP. The recorded
data from the oscilloscope is presented in Figures 4.12 and 4.13. To the left in both
figures is the moment both beams are turned on, to indicate both the level of the two
beams without TWM, and to show the power level when TWM is in effect. Both fig-
ures have 1 to 1 power ratio, a lattice constant, Λ, of 193 nm, and the total power is 94
mW. The HWP is placed in C1 for the vertical polarization and C2 for the horizontal.
Figure 4.12 generally has a lower power level than that of the horizontal polar-
ization due to the Fresnel reflections, Rvertical = 0.239, Rhorizontal = 0.210, This also

5.2.1
Counter Propagating
After the investigation into the principle axes of the crystal, it was deemed plausible
that A. Grabar had it right, meaning the c-axis was parallel to the z-axis, i.e. the
counter propagation configuration along the z-axis can potentially give high TWM
gain [21]. With the end goal of intracavity operation in mind, this is good as it would
mean an effective Fabry-Perot filter.
While it would be interesting to look into a variation of the grating spacing, as the
crystal is polished, this will not be possible, as the refractive index does not allow for
sufficiently angled incidence, to massively impact the grating spacing. Secondly to
achieve a high grating spacing on the order of microns, co-propagating is needed,
due to the high refractive index reducing the effective wavelength.
Power Ratio Variation
Due to many articles reporting excellent gain for TWM when the signal to pump ratio
is increased [13, 23, 42], this was of course tested, but the initial results were not
showing the positive trend one could hope for. Furthermore in an intracavity sense,
the two beams are going to have powers close to each other, assuming a high re-
flectivity out coupling mirror. Obviously if the out coupling mirror ”only” reflects 50%
of the light, the power ratio is going to be 1:2 for the two beams.
As a test a measurement of a 1:10 power ratio was carried out. The result is
non-conclusive but indicate an increase of absolute gain from 6% for a 1:1 ratio,
to 10% for a 1:10 ratio, throughout the 3.3 mm of crystal. Computing the coupling
strength, Γ, for both measurements gives around 3.3, indicating the crystal is diffract-
ing equally efficient for both ratios. The data for these computations are plotted in
Appendix B.4.1.
An explanation as to why the increase in absolute gain is not great could be that
the grating contrast is not great enough to overcome the dark current as well. The
dark current in SPS is reported to be quite high; 120 W/cm2 [24], far greater than the
around 1 W/cm2 inside the crystal for these measurements .

6 | Conclusion
When starting this project the plan was to use the tin hypothiodiphosphate (SPS)
crystal as an intracavity element to stabilise the laser, and hence a laser diode was
chosen that could work with a cavity larger than the chip itself, the external cavity
diode laser (ECDL). Early in the project it was realised that the crystal would never
work in a cavity with the polish it has, as the surface has a roughness that introduces
a great loss. Additionally the crystals facets curves making it a lens, which is fairly
easily compensated with some lenses for this project.
Initially the plan was to make the same type of measurements on barium titanate
BaTiO3 as was done on SPS, however as it turns out the materials have different
material parameters, and this ended up not being fully realised. This is because
the two-wave mixing (TWM) observed was too erratic to make sense of, let alone
compare to SPS. The theory being that because of the scattering and beam fanning in
BaTiO3 , it is difficult to get efficient TWM. The experiments including BaTiO3 did bring
good things with it though. Firstly it introduced a procedure to ensure that there were
no reflections from one channel to the other, which clouded the TWM measurements.
Additionally the laser was changed as a result of the seeming instability of the ECDL,
and a more reliable master oscillator with power amplifier (MOPA) was employed. As
it turned out, the change of laser did not help the instability in BaTiO3 significantly,
but the MOPA was easier to control.
Having changed the laser and found the TWM to still be unstable, the measure-
ments on the BaTiO3 crystals were ceased. The time was instead invested in a more
thorough investigation of TWM in SPS. In an effort to investigate the impact of in-
tensity on several parameters of TWM, e.g. response time and coupling strength, a
series of measurements were carried out, but no strong trends were observed, and
hence the beam size was reduced to increase the intensity.
Measuring the response time was a difficult matter as the computation involved
a fitting process in which the specific point chosen could have a great impact on the
response time. However through all the experiments there was a general trend that
the fast response time was getting faster with increasing intensity, which is backed up
in the literature. For the slow response times there were no clear trends across the
experiments. This could be due to the way the fitting function is defined, in that the
slow response time will decrease if the fast response time becomes sufficiently fast. It
could also be because the slow response time does not vary much with intensity, and
the lack of trend could indicate there is neither increase or decrease in the response

Looking at the coupling strength, it makes a lot of sense these increase with in-
tensity, which is indeed the trend found in the literature as well. It was difficult to
get an expected magnitude for the coupling strength, as the coupling strengths pre-
sented in literature are often of longer grating spacings. Additionally the intensity
used here is at least an order of magnitude lower, than what is often used. This was
a choice made primarily to protect the crystal from overheating, as the SPS crystal
has a crystallographic phase change at 337 K. Based on the sources of coupling
strength, the magnitude found in this project is reasonable, especially considering
the light intensity used. The dark current has been reported as high as 120 W/cm2,
which is considerably higher than the 4 W/cm2 used for the TWM measurements in
this project. Yet up a coupling strength of up to 0.8 cm−1 is observed at just 4 W/cm2.
Although the high dark current is worrying for the intensity one would expect in
the cavity of a diode laser, it is shown here that TWM is indeed possible at these
intensities.
6.1
Outlook
Based on the findings, it should be possible to use an SPS crystal as the photore-
fractive element in a self organising cavity, if it had a better polish both in terms of
smoothness and facet curvature. It exhibits decent coupling strengths, the grating
spacing and light intensity considered, and a fast response.
If time had permitted I would have liked to make measurements with a higher
beam intensity, to see if the coupling strength would keep increasing. Especially since
the Fabry-Perot filter will have increasing selectivity with coupling strength, hence
more circulating power would mean higher possibility of single mode operation. A
problem with intracavity operation could be the beam quality not being the clean and
relatively Gaussian beam used for these experiments. However people have been
self organising cavities with BaTiO3 , so it should be possible for SPS as well.
Thinking of the works of N. Debreuil et al. [18] who used a master laser to con-
trol the circulating wavelength of self organising cavity with an adaptive Fabry-Perot
filter (SOCwAFP) configuration, and it keeping said wavelegth post-exposure. Sub-
sequently looking at the work of A. Brignon et al. [34] who used a BaTiO3 crystal to
spatially clean up the spatial profile of a laser beam. Maybe one could combine the
two, to have a SOCwAFP oscillating and then injecting a Gaussian beam using a
master source. if the cavity could maintain the lasing if the master source was turned
off. It is probably not feasible to turn off the master source in this configuration, at
least in SPS. Based on the configuration employed by A. Brignon et al. the Gaussian
beam is angled compared to the pump light. This would result in the pumped Guas-
sian beam exiting the cavity at an angle, and the remainder of the pump light could
then be used to keep the lasing at a single longitudinal mode.

F | Initial Project Plan
Title : Laser stabilisation with photorefractive materials 
 
Student : Louis Evind-Poulsen, s153689 
Supervisors: Anders Kragh Hansen and Ole Bjarlin Jensen 
 
Technical University of Denmark, Department of Photonics Engineering. 
Period : February 17th - 2020 to July 17th - 2020 
Abstract 
Single frequency lasers are widely used today in applications from cooling of atoms to optical 
communication systems. Often it is desired to have compact systems, requiring the use of diode lasers, 
which can be engineered to have a narrow linewidth by employing an intracavity grating, such as a surface 
grating or a volume Bragg grating. These solutions are not ideal as such laser systems are unstable when 
exposed to unintentional extracavity feedback, which means that expensive and bulky optical isolators are 
often necessary. 
The use of intracavity photorefractive crystals can enhance the inherent frequency stability of a diode laser 
system, potentially reducing the impact of feedback. Furthermore, the use of photorefractive crystals as a 
frequency selective element rather than surface or volume gratings means that spatial fabrication precision 
is no concern, as the light will form the grating, rather than the engineers designing the grating having to 
make sure the grating is uniform.  
The goal of this project is to study photorefractive crystals that respond well in the near infrared and use 
them as an intracavity element to narrow the linewidth of a ridge waveguide laser diode from a few nm to 
a single frequency. If time allows, techniques for tuning the laser without moving parts using sawtooth 
current patterns to the diode may also be explored.
