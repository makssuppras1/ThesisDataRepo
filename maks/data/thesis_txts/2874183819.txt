Abstract
This thesis investigates the dynamic behavior of multiВӯmodular floating systems conВӯ
nected by hinges, with a particular focus on developing a numerical framework for modelВӯ
ing the coupling effects introduced by the hinge connections. The study was motivated by
the need to reduce the dependence on physical model testing and to establish a general
methodology that can be applied across various floating array configurations.
The work begins with the formulation of a coupling restoring matrix for a simplified twoВӯ
barge system. The matrix is derived from first principles, based on the relative displaceВӯ
ment at the hinge, and captures the contributions of surge, heave, and pitch motions.
The numerical model was then implemented in HydroD v7.3, using the Wadam panel
method solver to compute the Response Amplitude Operators (RAOs) of the three aforeВӯ
mentioned motions. Several assumptions were made to simplify the model, including
linear hydrodynamics, rigidВӯbody motion, and the neglect of viscous damping, consistent
with reference experimental studies.
The results demonstrate that the proposed methodology reproduces hingeВӯinduced restorВӯ
ing forces and moments effectively, providing good agreement with the reference experВӯ
imental work. The general formulation of the restoring matrix represents a significant
outcome of the thesis, offering a tool that can be easily applied to a variety of multiВӯbody
floating systems.
This work contributes to the advance of numerical modeling of hinged floating structures
by providing both a theoretical framework and a practical implementation strategy, with
potential applications in wave energy converters, floating bridges, and modular offshore
platforms.

Introduction
1.1
Context and Motivation
The evolution of offshore engineering has seen a significant transition from the analysis
of single, large floating bodies to the study of complex multibody systems. These sysВӯ
tems, composed of interconnected modules, find application in several ways: floating
solar panels (FPVs), Wave Energy Converters (WECs), and massive floating platforms
used for logistics and habitation, [1]. Although their higher functionality and adaptabilВӯ
ity make them very attractive, the immense technical difficulties in accurately predicting
their dynamic behavior prevent them from being widely adopted. One of the main driver
for this research is the high cost of traditional physical model testing. Experimental tank
tests are considered the standard for validating hydrodynamic models, but they are ofВӯ
ten very expensive and time consuming, in particular for large scale and more complex
designs, [2].
Moreover,the lack of reliable and publicly available experimental data further complicates
the validation process, making it difficult to set benchmarks for new numerical models.
Therefore, the development of a general, reliable and costВӯeffective numerical method to
replicate experimental results is a primary objective, as it can significantly reduce design
costs and accelerate the development of new sustainable ocean technologies.
In this thesis, the dynamic response of a twoВӯbarges array connected by hinge mechaВӯ
nisms is investigated. The study is inspired by the experimental setup described in the
work of Zhang et al., [3], which provides a benchmark for our numerical predictions.
1.2
Relevance and Challenges
Modeling hingeВӯtype connections in frequencyВӯdomain panel methods remains a great
challenge in the offshore industry. While some commercial hydrodynamic solvers allow
for limited stiffness and damping matrices to be defined, the mathematical coupling of
motions between bodies requires tailored formulations. A significant part of this work is
dedicated to developing and validating such formulations and investigating their impact
on the simulated responses of the system.
This modeling approach is not only relevant for reproducing specific experiments but also
to be able to define a generalized formulation for multibody hingeВӯcoupled systems in orВӯ
der to enhance the applicability of boundary element method (BEM) solvers to a several
types of multiВӯmodular floating structures. This would support costВӯeffective design proВӯ
cesses, reduce dependence on large scale experimental data and allow for early stage
parametric analyses.
1.3
Objectives of the Thesis
The principal aim of this thesis is to develop a general coupling stiffness matrix for hingeВӯ
connected multibody floating systems and to implement it within the HydroD numerical
environment. The model is tested against the reference Zhang et al.вҖҷs experiment [3],
with a focus on replicating motion amplitudes and trends under wave excitation.
The thesis also aims to:
вҖў provide a physical and mathematical derivation of the hingeВӯinduced restoring forces
and momentsНҫ

2
Literature Review
2.1
State of Art
Predicting the forces and motions experienced by floating bodies in waves is fundamental
to marine design. The field of hydrodynamic analysis, which provides the theoretical and
computational tools for this task has its origin in the early work of Hess and Smith, [4],
who developed a numerical solution based on the integral equation derived from GreenвҖҷs
identity. This approach, known as the panel method, discretizes the body surface into a
series of elements to solve for different flow variables. Works like the ones from Katz and
Plotkin [5] and Turnock et al. [6] established the theoretical framework for these methods.
Early applications focused on isolated bodies, as studied by Sahin et al. [7], who apВӯ
plied a lowВӯorder panel method to predict the hydrodynamic characteristics of underwater
vehicles. Their work highlighted the critical balance between computational cost and acВӯ
curacy, finding that lowВӯorder models could provide good results for complex geometries
without the high computational demand of other highВӯorder models. Furthermore, NewВӯ
man, [8], extended this analysis to deformable bodies, a crucial aspect for large, flexible
floating structures.
For multibody systems, the state of the art has evolved from simple approximations to
more complex and detailed coupled analyses. Koo and Kim [1] provide a review of these
methods, dividing between:
вҖў No Hydrodynamic Interaction (NHI): each body is analyzed independently, neglectВӯ
ing any interaction effects. It is computationally affordable but can lead to a high
level of inaccuraciesНҫ
вҖў Separated Matrix Method (SMM): a partial approach that accounts for some hyВӯ
drodynamic interaction but often neglects offВӯdiagonal terms, resulting in significant
discrepancies in predicting relative motionsНҫ
вҖў Combined Matrix Method (CMM): the most accurate approach, where the full dyВӯ
namics of multiple hulls and connecting elements are solved simultaneously in a
single matrix. This method is computationally intensive but essential for accurate
predictions of system responses, especially for adjacent bodies.
More recent work applies these principles to modern challenges. An example is the study
of Zhang et al., [3], where the hydroelastic behavior of modular floating structures conВӯ
nected by hinges is investigated, confirming that hinge stiffness plays a significant role
in reducing bending moments and distributing forces. Another interesting research on
multiВӯmodular devices comes from Jiang et al., [2], in which extensive model tests on a
large floating solar array were conducted, providing valuable insights into motion perforВӯ
mance and the influence of complex rope connections. Their work underscores the need
for models that can accurately capture these complex coupling mechanisms.
2.2
Key Challenges in Multibody System Analysis
Despite the advances in numerical modeling, several key challenges remain in the accuВӯ
rate analysis of multibody floating systems, like:
вҖў hydrodynamic interactions: the interaction between adjacent bodies is a nonlinear
and complex phenomenon. The waves radiated by one body can interfere with the

incident waves on the near bodied, leading to amplified or dampened motions. This
is particularly critical in systems where the goal is to harness relative motion for enВӯ
ergy conversion, such as WECs. For accurate predictions, it is essential that these
interactions and the offВӯdiagonal terms in the hydrodynamic matrix are accurately
representedНҫ
вҖў mechanical coupling: the connections between floating modules, whether rigid, like
a solid link, or flexible, like hinges, ropes, or mooring lines, highly affect the systemвҖҷs
dynamics. The forces exerted by these slender members are highly coupled with
the motion of the main bodies. For example, Saitov and Jiang [2] observed that an
increase in connecting rope stiffness can lead to dangerous snap events in mooring
lines, highlighting the intricate connection that numerical models must capture. The
investigation of Das and Sahoo, [9], on sway, roll, and yaw motions also provides a
strong basis for understanding these complex coupling effectsНҫ
вҖў gap resonance: when two floating bodies are in close proximity, the fluid in the gap
between them can exhibit a significant resonant response. This phenomenon can
cause large, high frequency wave elevations and forces that are difficult to model.
As presented in Zou et al.вҖҷs work, [10] , potential flow models without proper damping
can tend to overestimate the Response Amplitude Operator (RAO) in the vicinity of
gap resonance frequencies, necessitating more advanced numerical methods like
FEM or BEM to capture these effectsНҫ
вҖў experimental data validation: to validate any numerical model, it is important that its
outcome matches real world observations. However, as discussed by Jiang et al.
[2], even controlled model tests can have uncertainties due to factors like material
properties, homogeneous mass distribution, and water absorption. This highlights
the need for continuous research and comparison between field measurements and
the numerical models.
The shift towards multibody floating systems for clean energy and sustainable ocean deВӯ
velopment presents a compelling case for the offshore industry. The economic challenges
of physical modeling, together with the lack of experimental data, have placed numerical
simulation as a key tool in this field. As demonstrated by the work of Sahin et al., [7], and
Koo and Kim, [1], the industry has moved from simplified approximations to sophisticated
coupled methods. However, challenges related to accurate representation of hydrodyВӯ
namic and mechanical coupling, as well as complex fluid phenomena like gap resonance,
are still of high interests. Future research should focus on improving the reliability of
numerical models and establishing a robust framework for validation to unlock the full
potential of these offshore structures.

3.1
Hydrodynamic Panel Method
The hydrodynamic behavior of floating bodies subjected to wave excitation is often studied
using potential flow theory. This theory simplifies fluid dynamics by assuming the fluid to
be:
вҖў inviscid, meaning there is no internal fluid friction and, consequently, the fluid visВӯ
cosity Вө = 0Нҫ
вҖў incompressible, the density is constant, implying the divergence of the velocity vecВӯ
tor is zero, вҲҮВ· вғ—u = 0Нҫ
вҖў irrotational, no vorticity is present, meaning that the curl of the velocity vector вҲҮГ—вғ—u =
0
These assumptions allow the complex flow field to be described by a simpler scalar veВӯ
locity potential П•, where the fluid velocity вғ—u = вҲҮП•. The governing equation for such a flow
is the Laplace equation:

This equation holds true throughout the fluid domain. When appropriate boundary conВӯ
ditions are applied over all surfaces bounding the fluid flow, a unique solution for the
potential П• can be obtained.
A significant advantage of LaplaceвҖҷs equation is its linearity, which permits the linear suВӯ
perposition of solutions. This property is fundamental to panel methods, as it allows comВӯ
plex flows to be constructed by summing the effects of many basic flow elements, such
as sources and doublets.
3.1.1
Boundary Element Method
Among the various numerical approaches available for solving potential flow problems,
the Boundary Element Method (BEM), and specifically its implementation through panel
methods, has become a standard in marine hydrodynamics for modeling radiation and
diffraction problems.
This method was pioneered by Hess and Smith in the early 1960s, as noted by Sahin et
al. (1997) [7], and further detailed by pioneers like Hess (1973, 1990) and elaborated in
texts such as Katz and Plotkin (1991) [5].
The core principle of the panel method involves representing the bodyвҖҷs wetted surface
by a series of discrete elements, or вҖқpanels.вҖқ Each panel is provided with a distribution of
singularities, typically sources, representing fluid injection or suction, and doublets, repВӯ
resenting a pair of closely spaced source and sink. The strength of these singularities on
each panel is initially unknown. The problem is then solved by enforcing specific boundary
conditions on each panel, ensuring that the fluid flow behaves realistically at the bodyвҖҷs
surface and in the farВӯfield.
The software HydroD (v7.3) is employed in this study, which uses the Wadam panel
method solver to discretize the wetted surface of each floating body into a set of panВӯ
els.

3.1.3
Boundary Conditions and Hydrodynamic Coefficients
To solve the potential flow problem, specific boundary conditions are enforced on each
panel:
1. zero normal velocity on the body surface: for a nonВӯlifting body, the fluid cannot
penetrate the bodyвҖҷs surface. This means the normal component of the fluid velocity
on the body surface must be zero. This condition is used to determine the source
strengths (Пғ)Нҫ
2. Kutta Condition: this condition is applied for lifting surfaces, like fins. It is applied at
the trailing edge, ensuring finite velocity and determining the dipole strengthsНҫ
3. disturbance potential vanishes at infinity: the effects of the body on the fluid flow
diminish far away from the body.

then solved to determine the unknown singularity strengths, or potentials, on each panel.

3.1.4
Viscous Damping
In this study, the numerical model did not include any viscous damping matrix. Viscous
damping, which arises from fluid friction and vortex shedding, is a physical phenomenon
not accounted for by potential flow theory.
By not introducing an adВӯhoc viscous damping term, the analysis focuses on the core
hydrodynamic effects that the panel method is designed to capture: the inertial forces
(added mass) and the wave energy dissipation (radiation damping). This approach alВӯ
lows for a clearer assessment of the modelвҖҷs performance in replicating the experimental
results of the hingeВӯconnected multiВӯbody system, isolating the contribution of the develВӯ
oped coupling matrix from other complex fluid phenomena.
3.1.5
Relevance to the Study
Panel methods are preferred in this context due to their ability to model complex geomeВӯ
tries and to accurately capture hydrodynamic interactions between multiple bodies with a
relatively moderate computational cost. The accuracy of the method increases with the
density of panel discretization, but this comes at the expense of increased computational
time. This tradeВӯoff between accuracy and computational efficiency is a key consideration
in numerical simulations.
This method is essential in evaluating the hydrodynamic coefficients that are directly used
in the equation of motion for floating structures. In particular, the panel method allows for
the accurate assessment of multiВӯbody interaction effects, which are central to this study.
The HydroD (v7.3) software, with its Wadam panel method solver, provides the necesВӯ
sary platform to compute these interactions, enabling the development and validation of
the robust and general coupling restoring matrix that accurately reflects the mechanical
interaction at the hinge level. The ability of the panel method to handle these complex
interactions makes it an indispensable tool for analyzing the dynamic response of the
hingeВӯconnected multibody system investigated in this work.

4
Numerical Model Development
This chapter details the development of a numerical model designed to simulate the dyВӯ
namic behavior of multiВӯbody systems in waves. The primary objective is to create a
general and reusable approach for generating the coupling restoring matrix, which can be
applied to any configuration of floating bodies connected in series.
The development began with a simplified twoВӯbarge case to establish the core principles,
which can then be systematically extended to a generalized NВӯbody system with a generic
relative position between bodies.
4.1
Model Assumptions
The numerical model is based on several key assumptions to simplify the complex hydroВӯ
dynamics of the system:
вҖў linear hydrodynamics: the model assumes small amplitude motions and linear wave
theory, in order for the principle of superposition to be appliedНҫ
вҖў potential flow theory: the fluid is assumed to be inviscid and incompressible, thus
neglecting viscous effects and allowing the flow to be governed by the Laplace equaВӯ
tion, as introduced in the previous chapter Ch.3Нҫ
вҖў frequencyВӯdomain approach: the dynamic response of the system is analyzed in the
frequency domain, assuming a steadyВӯstate sinusoidal response to regular wave
excitationНҫ
вҖў rigid body assumption: each floating body is defined as a rigid body, so no structural
deformation are allowedНҫ
вҖў negligible viscous damping: the model does not include viscous damping effects,
focusing solely on restoring and hydrodynamic radiation damping forces computed
by the Wadam solver present in the HydroD (v7.3) softwareНҫ
вҖў hinges as linear springs: the hinges are modeled as linear springs, providing transВӯ
lational stiffnesses, kx and kz, to constrain relative motion. For computational simВӯ
plicity, the rotational stiffness (kОё), which significantly influences pitch motion, is asВӯ
sumed to be equal to the vertical stiffness (kz). This approach differs from the findВӯ
ings of Zhang et al. [3], who indicate that pitch motion is predominantly governed
by the hingeвҖҷs rotational stiffnessНҫ
вҖў no nonВӯlinearities: nonВӯlinear effects from collisions or large rotations are not inВӯ
cluded in this model.

HydroD Implementation
5.1
Overview of HydroD v7.3
HydroD (v7.3) is a module within the Sesam software suite developed by DNV, widely used
for the hydrodynamic and structural analysis of floating structures. The software provides
tools for frequency and timeВӯdomain simulations, integrating hydrodynamic coefficients
from the Wadam panel method solver with userВӯdefined mass and stiffness data.
The choice of HydroD (v7.3) for this study is motivated by:
вҖў the possibility to import and mesh arbitrary 3D geometries for panel method comВӯ
putationsНҫ
вҖў the ability to model multiple interconnected bodies using userВӯdefined coupling maВӯ
tricesНҫ
вҖў a direct integration of frequencyВӯdependent hydrodynamic coefficients, allowing an
userВӯfriendly RAOs computationНҫ
For these reasons, HydroD is a suitable software for this study due to its simplicity and
versatility.

The soft hinge configuration presents two distinct peaks for the resonance frequency,

hinge, where one barge moves up while the other moves down, what we would call a

6.1.3
Pitch Motion
Pitch is quite sensitive to the wavelength of the incident waves. A body will experience
its maximum pitch response when the wave crest is under one end of a body and the
trough is under the other, creating a large moment. This condition typically occurs when
the wavelength is approximately equal to the bodyвҖҷs length leading to a strong resonance.
Soft Hinge Case
As shown in Fig.6.5b, the soft hinge system leads the two bodies to behaves indepenВӯ
dently to each other. This is a direct consequence of the low hinge stiffness, which alВӯ
lows relative motion between the leading Bi and trailing Bi+1 barges. Each body has its
own pitch response characteristics, and the overall system shows two distinct resonance
peaks.
For a wave heading of Оұ = 90в—Ұ, as shown in Fig.6.5a, the pitch motion is almost absent.
This is because pitch is a rotation about the longitudinal axis, and a wave approaching
from the side does not create the necessary moment to induce this motion.

Discussion
7.1
Dynamic Response of the MultiВӯModular System
The results of the study validate the numerical model and provide insight into important
hydrodynamic phenomena by offering a thorough examination of the dynamic behavior of
the twoВӯbarge hinged multibody system.. The analysis of the 90В° wave heading confirmed
that the system is symmetrical about the xzВӯplane. As expected, the heave and pitch
RAOs for Body Bi and Body Bi+1 were nearly identical, demonstrating that the regular
waves affect both barges in the same way due to the symmetrical nature of the incoming
wave train. This consistency between the leading and trailing bodies at a 90В° heading
serves as an important initial validation of the modelвҖҷs setup.
It is clear that the stiffness of the hinge is a major factor in how the system responds to
changes in motion. The numerical model successfully replicates the distinct behaviors of
soft and hard hinge configurations, demonstrating its ability to simulate various connection
types.
In the soft hinge configuration, the flexibility of the connection allows for more complex moВӯ
tions. This is most clearly seen in the heave RAO, which exhibits two distinct resonance
peaks. This behavior is a direct result of the two barges acting as a coupled oscillator
system. The first peak, at a lower frequency, corresponds to an inВӯphase motion, where
both barges move in the same vertical direction, much like a single rigid body. The second
peak, at a higher frequency, is a result of the flexible hinge allowing for a pronounced outВӯ
ofВӯphase motion, a phenomenon often referred to as the вҖқseesawвҖқ effect. This opposing
motion is a key characteristic of multiВӯbody systems, like wave energy converters (WEC),
and highlights the modelвҖҷs ability to capture the effects of flexible connections.
In the hard hinge case, with a high stiffness (kx = kz = 1Г—108N/m), the system behaves
as a single, large, rigid body. This is reflected in the single resonance peak observed in
the heave and pitch RAO curves. The increased mass and moment of inertia of the comВӯ
bined body creates a bigger resistance to wave forces, resulting in a significantly reduced
response amplitude compared to the flexible configuration. This behavior is consistent
with a single oscillator model, where the combined mass and waterplane area result in
a single natural heave period. Just as a single springВӯmass system has one natural freВӯ
quency at which it oscillates more, the rigidly coupled barge system possesses a single
natural frequency for each degree of freedom.
The wave heading plays a crucial role in determining the interaction between the two
barges. While the 90В° case shows a symmetrical response, the 180В° degree case shows
the effect of wave interference. In this configuration, the waves interact with the barges
sequentially: the waves radiated by the leading body Bi interfere with the incident waves
on the trailing body Bi+1. As a result, the heave and pitch RAOs of the trailing body
are often significantly higher than the ones from the leading body, in particular at the
resonance frequencies. The amplified motion for the trailing body is a key aspect for
multiВӯmodular devices, like wave energy converters, as the relative vertical and rotational
motions at the hinge are what is harnessed for wave energy conversion.
In the same way, the pitch motion appears sensible to wavelength, with the maximum reВӯ
sponse occurring when the wavelength is roughly equal to the bodyвҖҷs length, so fo higher
wave periods, creating the largest pitching moment. In the hard hinge case, the pitch is a

tricate, wave headingВӯdependent pitch behavior further confirms its reliability in simulating
both rigid and flexible connections.
7.1.1
Effect of Wave Period on Multibody Response at 180В° Heading
The dynamic response of the barges in the 180В° wave heading is strongly influenced by
the incoming wave period, which can be divided into three distinct regimes.
For low wave periods, the barges react in a similar way, with very similar RAOs for all
degrees of freedom. This occurs because the waves are small and likely moving too
quickly for the system to fully respond. In this highВӯfrequency regime, the barges do not
have sufficient time to develop distinct motions, causing them to behave almost identically,
as if they were moving together.
In the intermediate wave period range, the waveвҖҷs wavelength becomes comparable to
the length of each barge. This is the resonance range, where the systemвҖҷs response is
most dramatic and complex. Here, the sequential interaction between the barges is most
pronounced. For example, in heave motion, when the leading barge is on the crest of a
wave, the trailing barge is simultaneously in a trough, and vice versa. This pronounced
outВӯofВӯphase motion is a key characteristic of multiВӯbody systems and demonstrates the
energy transfer between the two coupled barges.
Finally, for high wave periods, the behavior of the barges becomes similar to the lowВӯ
period case. This is because the waves become so long that the two coupled barges are
effectively riding a single wave crest or trough. The barges respond as a single, large
body, and the hydrodynamic interactions between them diminish. As a result, the RAOs
for both barges tend to become very similar again, exhibiting a uniform motion comparable
to a single, much longer floating structure. This confirms the modelвҖҷs ability to capture the
full spectrum of waveВӯbody interactions, from rapid, localized responses to largeВӯscale,
uniform motions.
7.2
Extending the Numerical Framework
The flexibility of the developed formulation represents a key outcome of this study. The
numerical approach can be easily extended to account for additional motion degrees of
freedom, such as sway, roll, and yaw, to provide a more detailed dynamic analysis. To
compute the RAOs along the yВӯaxis (sway and roll), for example, it is only needed to
translate the problem along that axis and repeat the same computational steps. This
highlights the modular and adaptable nature of the solution, which can be applied to a
wider range of hydrodynamic problems.
Furthermore, the matrix formulationвҖҷs core strength lies in its only dependence on the
coordinates of the center of gravity of each body with respect to the global coordinate
system. This simplicity allows it to be a generalizable and efficient method for modeling
complex systems.
In a multibody array where bodies are coupled two by two in a sequence, the framework
can be applied iteratively. This would result in N вҲ’1 12x12 matrices, where N is the
number of bodies, with each matrix representing the coupling of an adjacent pair. This
approach is highly flexible and can function as a general rule for simulating a wide vaВӯ
riety of multibody configurations, including different body shapes and array setups, thus
enabling analysis of even more extensive and complex modular structures.

8
Conclusion
This study investigated the dynamic behavior of multiВӯmodular connected floating sysВӯ
tems, focusing in particular on the formulation and implementation of a coupling restoring
matrix able to accurately describe the coupled motion of a multibody system. Starting
from a two hinged bodies configuration, the study developed a numerical approach that
could easily be extended to a general NВӯbody system. The formulation was expressed in
terms of the relative positions of the hinge and the bodiesвҖҷ centers of gravity, providing a
general framework applicable to a wide range of floating array configurations.
The numerical model was implemented in HydroD v7.3 using the Wadam panel method
solver, which enabled the computation of the response amplitude operators for the conВӯ
strained degrees of motion.Several assumptions were adopted to simplify the problem,
including linear hydrodynamics, rigid body behavior, and neglecting viscous damping.
These assumptions are consistent with the reference experimental study and allowed diВӯ
rect comparison of numerical and physical results.
The results demonstrated that the proposed methodology successfully reproduced the
coupling effects at the hinge and captured the essential dynamic interactions between
the barges.
Beyond methodological contributions, this study also highlights the practical significance
of numerical modeling for floating arrays. By replicating experimental results within a valВӯ
idated hydrodynamic solver, the approach reduces the dependence on physical model
testing, leading to a considerably timeВӯandВӯcost effective way to model the complex beВӯ
havior of multiВӯmodular structures. Moreover, the generalized restoring matrix offers leads
to a more flexible and efficient design of hinged floating structures.
Future work should address the limitations of the present model, particularly the absence
of viscous damping and nonlinear effects, which may influence system behavior in realistic
sea states. Extending the methodology to include these effects, as well as investigating
larger and more complex arrays, would enhance the applicability and robustness of the
developed numerical model.
