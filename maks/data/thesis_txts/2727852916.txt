Abstract
Although bifacial solar cells have been invented since 1960 only recently bifacial photo­
voltaic (PV) modules have been deployed on a large scale. Therefore, field failure and
degradation studies specific to bifacial modules are still lacking, necessary to quantify
the long­term reliability of bifacial PV plants. This project investigated the field perfor­
mance and degradation of bifacial PV modules deployed at Denmark’s Technical Univer­
sity (DTU) Risø campus, a research­focused PV park in collaboration with European En­
ergy A/S in Denmark, to identify potential underperforming strings and modules. Hence,
onsite module characterization tests for identifying degraded modules were performed,
some of which were characterized in more detail through laboratory testing to determine
and quantify the type and extent of the degradation modes. The characterization meth­
ods included visual inspection, infrared thermography, current­voltage flash testing, and
electroluminescence imaging of bifacial p­type monocrystalline PERC modules that were
installed at Risø over a 5­year period. It was found that the weighted average degrada­
tion rate of all tested bifacial PV modules was equal to 0.355%/year and the degradation
rate seems to had an incremental effect, with the panels installed for a longer duration,
experiencing higher degradation rates but further research should be done to validate
this. Evidence has been found that the position of the string and tracker had an effect
on the degradation rate of the modules as the more internally were located in the layout
the higher the degradation rate but further studies should be conducted to validate this
trend. The most degraded panel that was found, with an average annual degradation rate
of 1.04%, after the electroluminescence imaging showed early signs of potential induced
degradation, justifying the scientific attention to this particular degradation mechanism and
the long­term consequences in the total energy yield. Moreover, the measured bifaciality
factors corresponded to the literature’s average bifaciality factor of the PERC technology
(70%). Furthermore, electroluminescence imaging showed a negligible number of cell
cracks affirming the strong mechanical sturdiness of the double glass structure of bifacial
PV modules. Lastly, an analysis of the correlation between the cell technology and the
materials could not be underdone due to the fact that all the panels in Risø had the same
cell technology (PERC p­type) and the exact bill of materials was not transparent from the
official modules’ datasheet.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Solar energy has grown in significance as a renewable energy source, with photovoltaic
(PV) systems being considered essential to the forthcoming green transition on a world­
wide scale. This rapid growth and potential will likely continue as well in the following
years according to the International Energy Agency (IEA) [1].
The global solar PV power capacity in 2021 reached 900 GW from 734 GW in 2020 [1],
corresponding to roughly 25% growth in only one year. Figure 1.1 shows the total global
solar PV power capacity from 2018 to 2021 with utility­scale solar PV parks representing
more than 50% of that capacity. It is important to note that this data is constantly chang­
ing as new installations are added and older ones are decommissioned, so the current
installed solar PV power capacity may be even higher.
In order to reach the goal of net zero emissions by 2050, even more prominent growth
should be realized. Table 1.1 shows the key deployment milestones for Solar PV accord­
ing to the IEA. The stated data of 2020 are real and not a prediction or goal. Furthermore,
according to the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA), the global weighted
average Levelized Cost Of Energy (LCOE) for utility­scale solar PV in 2021 was 0.048
USD per kilowatt­hour (kWh), one of the lowest among others [2].

1.2
Motivation
Due to their significant potential for improved energy yield, bifacial PV modules, which
have the capacity to absorb light from both the front and rear side, have grown in popularity
in recent years compared to conventional monofacial PV modules. Figure 1.2 and Figure
1.3 show the increasing market share of the bifacial PV cells and modules. It is essential
to note that those are forecast projections and not real data.
One notable advantage of bifacial PV systems is that they decrease the LCOE signifi­
cantly, up to 16% lower than conventional systems, and produce up to 35% more energy
[4].

Field degradation and failures study of bifacial PV modules

In a nutshell, they just have started to be implemented on a large scale. The bifacial
PV modules may have advantages but also have additional issues, notably with field
degradation and faults.
Degradation is the term used to describe the gradual decline in the performance and ef­
ficiency of PV modules. This decline can be brought about by a number of variables,
including environmental conditions, materials used, and manufacturing flaws. Bifacial
PV modules can be particularly affected by degradation and failures since these modules
have the extra intricacy of having two functional surfaces. There are plenty of degradation
studies conducted for monofacial panels but no extended bibliographic literature review
particularly for bifacial. A degradation study can be used to optimize the design, man­
ufacturing, and performance of PV panels and to ensure their compliance with industry
standards and regulations. It can also help with quality control, help customers decide
which bifacial panels should invest in, and ensure that the panels can reliably generate
electricity over their intended lifespan.
1.3
Aim and scope
This master’s thesis will examine the power degradation of fielded bifacial PV modules by
analyzing and testing four different models of PV modules at DTU Risø PV Park in Den­
mark. Therefore, on­site characterization tests and further detailed lab tests of degraded
panels will be performed. The operation time of each PV model is different but no more
than 5 years.
The term characterization refers to the process of evaluating the electrical and physical
properties of solar cells, modules, and systems. The characterization process involves
measuring and analyzing various parameters such as power output, efficiency, current­
voltage curves, spectral response, temperature coefficients, and durability, among others.
Out of all these parameters, more attention and focus will be paid to accurate power output
and current­voltage (IV) measurements of PV panels at Risø PV Park through flash test­
ing. The complementary characterization methods that will be used are visual inspection,
infrared thermography, and electroluminescence imaging. Overall, this thesis project will
deliver statistical data on degradation and quantify the type and extent of the degradation
modes. Moreover, a detailed analysis of the causes associated with field degradation and
faults in bifacial PV modules and the identification of key problems through data analysis
will be presented. This study can potentially point out and enable the development of
more effective and efficient bifacial PV systems.
1.4
Objectives
In order to accomplish the goal of the thesis, the following objectives should successfully
be reached:
1. Undertake a comprehensive literature review about bifacial PV degradation and fail­
ures.
2. Characterize 65 1 bifacial p­type monocrystalline PERC modules of different man­
ufacturers, production years, and formats that were installed at Risø over a 5­year
period.

Bifacial Photovoltaic Technology
In this chapter, a brief history of bifacial PV technology will be presented together with the
main bifacial PV cells, with their respective advantages, disadvantages, and performance
parameters. Finally, market trends and forecasts will be analyzed, addressing the ongoing

2.1
Brief history and evolution
The first bifacial solar cell was developed in 1954 at Bell Labs by Russell Ohl and was
an n­type integrated back cell with 5% efficiency [5]. Later, in 1961 bifacial cells were
proposed in a patent in Japan by Hiroshi Mori [6]. From 1974 to 2000, bifacial cells were
developed mostly for space applications and utilized for Russian satellites. In the mean­
while, Andreas Cuevas et al. (1982) presented the terrestrial potential of the technology
in 1982 and in 1986 ISOFOTON started the industrial production of the first bifacial PV
modules [7], [8]. In 1998, Thomas Nordman and Luzi Clavadetscher installed the first bifa­
cial double glass modules in noise barriers in Switzerland [9]. All these applications were
of course much more expensive than the standard panel technology during that time. In
2010 begun the low­cost mass production of bifacial cells from companies such as Solar
Wind, PVGS, Yingli, LG and Sunpreme. Following big interest, the first bifiPV workshop
was established in Konstanz in 2012 [10]. Finally, the first bifacial system on a MW scale
was installed in Japan by PVGS with a 1.25 MWp capacity [11].
Figure 2.1 shows the brief history of the bifacial PV technology with the most important
events pointed out. More recently, the bifacial solar cell production increased dramatically
which can be seen in the world market share from the annual ITRPV report (Figure 1.3,
Figure 1.2)[12].

into bifacial. The process to convert them is simple and does not add significant cost, on

can achieve frontside efficiencies up to 23% with bifaciality factors between 70% ­ 80%

3
Bifacial PV Degradation Modes and
Faults
Bifacial PV modules are subject to both degradation and faults that can impact their per­
formance and reliability. Degradation can be defined as the gradual decline in the per­
formance (%/year) and efficiency of PV modules, while the term fault refers to specific
issues or problems that can cause sudden or significant power loss. Faults require re­
pairs or component replacements to restore the system to proper working conditions. It is
important to distinguish between degradation and faults and to further categorize degrada­
tion into two subgroups: material degradation and degradation from operating conditions.
The global PV market has a wide range of materials used in PV BoM according to ITRPV
2022 (Appendix Figures A.1, A.2, and A.3). As a result, different materials can be associ­
ated with different degradation mechanisms. This chapter will analyze the most important
degradation modes in bifacial PV modules, their contributing factors such as environmen­
tal conditions, and materials, and moreover, the most suitable detection method for each
one. Ultimately, an analytical literature review will be presented to provide a comprehen­
sive understanding of the current state of the art in the field.
3.1
Main degradation modes
PV systems can experience degradation over time due to various factors, including en­
vironmental conditions, manufacturing defects, and material properties. The degradation
of PV systems can result in a reduction in power output, decreased efficiency, and even
complete system failure. Therefore, it is crucial to understand the various modes of degra­
dation and faults and develop effective strategies for mitigating and optimizing their per­
formance. In this context, this section will introduce some of the main degradation modes
and faults that can affect PV systems and explore their impact on the performance, reli­
ability, warranty as well as the timescale, when they occur, and how fast they progress.
Finally, the most optimal detection method for each specific failure mode will be provided,
taking into account the overall consensus within the scientific community, if any.
Figure 3.1 shows three typical scenarios for silicon PV panels’ power degradation for infant
failures, middle­life failures, and normal wear­out failures. Junction box failures, string
interconnections flaws, glass breakage, and loose frame typically would correspond to
sudden and significant power loss. Similarly, potential induced degradation (PID), diode
failures, and backsheet failures would also lead to significant power loss but, they often are
considered middle­life failures. All the other depicted degradation modes lead to constant
but minor power loss during the whole lifetime. While approaching the end of its lifetime,
corrosion of cells and interconnection will be the main reasons for failure.
According to Jordan et al. (2017) and Kim et al. (2021), the most common degradation
modes in the last 10 years were hotspots (33%), internal circuit discoloration (20%), glass
breakage (12%), encapsulant discoloration (10%), cell breakage (9%) and PID (8%) [30],
[31]. Figures 3.2 and 3.3 show the different types of failures and degradation modes
percentages according to Kontges et al. (2014) [29]. Of course, in the last 10 years, a
lot of parameters have changed, such as the material used, cell technologies and bifacial
market share, so it is not credible to draw conclusions about specifically bifacial modules.

Moreover, discoloration and ”yellowing” of the encapsulant can occur from extended ex­

Series resistance
The series resistance arises from the busbars, soldering bonds, and junction box termi­
nating contacts [34]. Over time, the series resistance is gradually increasing, leading to
lower voltage, power, and efficiency of the solar cell.
Shunt resistance
The shunt resistance represents the silicon impurities and defects that provide high­conductivity
alternate paths for the current (ISH). After prolonged exposure to sunlight, the shunt re­
sistance decreases, thus significantly affecting the performance of the solar cell [35].

Anti­reflective coating
Another factor linked to internal circuit degradation is the anti­reflective coating (ARC). The
ARC is a layer on the PV cell that reduces the reflection of the sun’s rays which can in­
crease the absorbed light. A common example of ARC used in most PV cell technologies
is Silicon Nitride (SiNx) due to its high refractive index. The ARC can be deposited on the
silicon’s surface with a variety of methods like sputtering or plasma­enhanced chemical
vapor deposition (PECV D). The refractive index of Si is equal to 3.4 and SiNx ranged
between 1.9 and 2.4 at visible wavelength [36].
ARCs are commonly made from transparent oxide materials, and the degradation of these
coatings can be attributed to the diffusion of atoms between the emitter region of the solar
cell and the ARC. This process of inter­diffusion can occur in both directions, resulting in
a reduction in the effectiveness of the ARC and ultimately leading to a decrease in the
performance of the solar cell [37].
In bifacial cells, there are ARC layers on both the front and back surface. Chapter 2
depicted numerous ARC layers for the various PV cell technologies. A potential way to
detect a degraded ARC is by doing spectral reflectance measurements with a spectropho­
tometer. If the ARC is degraded, the solar cell would show an increase in reflectance [38].
Junction box
The junction box is a small container on the rear side of the panel that protects the cell
connections to the external terminals and encloses the bypass diodes for shadow and
hotspot protection. It is a very critical component, as poor manufacturing processes, poor
adhesion, and not reliable soldering can potentially lead to a fire in extreme situations due
to the high resistance. Moisture can also penetrate the junction box leading to corrosion
of the interconnections. Feasible detection methods for junction box defects are visual
inspection and infrared thermography which is further analyzed in Chapter 4.
3.1.3
Mismatch
Cell mismatches occur when a PV module’s cells have different electrical properties from
one another. This could happen as a result of different production methods, shading, or
cell ages. As the module’s total current output is constrained by the current output of the
weakest cell, cell mismatch potentially results in power losses in the module. Hotspots
and increased vulnerability to degradation may also result from cell mismatches. Bifacial
PV modules can experience more cell mismatches due to non­uniform light distribution
according to Kitayama et al. (2021) [39].
Cracked cells
Many factors, including mechanical stress, thermal stress, and manufacturing flaws, can
lead to cracked cells. Improper transportation, inappropriate handling, and wrong instal­
lation of panels lead to mechanical stress. The power output of the PV module may
be significantly reduced as a result of these cracks since they can lead to hotspots, cell
shunting, and mismatches that decrease electrical conductivity and current. The direc­
tion of the cracks, compared to the fingers and the busbars, plays an important role in the
power loss percentage. In bifacial cells, this power loss affects also the rear side and the
rear side power gain respectively. Moreover, damaged cells may be more vulnerable to
degradation from moisture and temperature changes in the environment. To stop addi­
tional damage and ensure maximum performance, it is advised to repair or replace the
module, if delamination is also noticed. Cell cracks are extremely difficult to be identified

straightforward detection method of delamination is visual inspection. Delamination be­

3.1.4
Light induced degradation
As crystalline silicon solar cells are exposed to light for the first few hours, a phenomenon
known as light­induced degradation (LID) causes the cells’ initial efficiency to drop quickly.
The interaction between oxygen and boron­oxygen defects, which arise throughout the
cell production process, is what gives rise to this phenomenon. Because of this, the open­
circuit voltage (VOC) and short­circuit current (ISC) of the cells decrease, thus decreasing
the solar cell’s overall efficiency. A typical percentage of power loss due to LID is 2%
according to [42] and 3% according to [43]. It is unsure if manufacturers expose the
solar cells prior and incorporate this loss to the mentioned rated power in the end. In
theory, only conventional p­type boron­doped wafers are affected by LID and not the n­
type phosphorous­doped [42].
3.1.5
Temperature induced degradation
All PV panels are rated at Standard Test Condition (STC). STC conditions refer to 1000
W/m2 irradiance, cell temperature of 25°C, and air mass 1.5. Air mass is the cosine of
the zenith angle of the sun, corresponding to air mass 1 when the sun is at its zenith. But
while the PV module is operating outside, the temperature is most of the time difference.
An increased cell temperature leads to a small increase in ISC and a big decrease in VOC.
This temperature effect in the IV curve can be seen in Figure 3.15.
PV module manufacturers include the current, voltage, and power temperature coefficient
in the datasheet. The power loss is approximately ­0.3% to ­0.5% for every °C above 25°.
During operation, PV modules can exceed STC 25°C and in tropical regions reach easily
60°C, especially when the wind is absent [44]. According to Lamers et al. (2018) bifa­
cial irradiance strongly increases the heat input and temperature even more compared to
monofacial panels [45]. Therefore, it is clear that prolonged elevated temperatures de­
grade PV panels. Furthermore, temperature changes can potentially damage solar cells
due to consecutive expansions and contractions of the metallization, leading to mechan­
ical stress and further degradation.

3.1.7
Potential induced Degradation
Potential Induced Degradation (PID) refers to a phenomenon that occurs in PV systems
when there is a high potential difference between the cells of the negative section of a
string and the grounded frame for p­type cells or of the positive section of a string for
n­type cells. Typical utility­scale PV system voltage levels are 1000V and 1500V. The
higher the voltage the more common the appearance of PID [46]. Sodium ions (Na+)
migration plays a crucial role in the development of PID [47]. This potential difference
leads to leakage current from the cells to the soil. Also, PID reduces the shunt resistance
leading to a further increase in the leakage current over the years [48]. A combination of
factors that help the appearance of PID is the high voltage, the high temperature, and the
high moisture. There are different types of PID such as ”PID of the shunting type” (PID­
s), ”PID of the polarization type” (PID­p) and ”PID of the corrosion” (PID­c) with slightly
different effects in the IV curve. However, both types are reversible to an extent with
appropriate procedures [39]. PID can potentially lead to up to 30% power loss and can
affect both p­type and n­type wafers however, heterojunction cells are more resilient to this
[49] [50]. Lastly, PID is one of the most frequent problems in crystalline silicon cells, and
compared to monofacial, bifacial modules and specifically their rear side is affected more
[51], [50]. The combination of bifacial PERC double glass with EVA encapsulant is more
prone to PID than monofacial due to the increase of Na+ ions from the glass according to
[39]. Figure 3.16 shows an electroluminescence image of a PID­affected module. Darker
cells appear around the edges of the module corresponding to the current leakage to the
grounded frame.

Literature review
This literature review aims to discover and summarize the diverse range of studies related
to degradation and explain key patterns and gaps in our understanding. Below are sum­
marized all the key findings from the literature research during this master thesis project.
First and foremost, it has been shown that lab flash testing is the best method for calcu­
lating the power and consequently, the degradation rate of a PV module [54]. Likewise,
northern latitudes seem to have a higher degradation rate but further research should
be done [54]. The most contributing factors for degradation are hot spots, EVA discol­
oration, delamination, PID, finger and interconnection damage, and corrosion [31], [30].

==PAGE:34==
Younger/new modules degrade faster and are affected more by PID, possibly due to un­
ethical nameplate overrating, focus on aggressively dropping the prices, not focusing on
quality, improper handling, and installations [55]. In most cases, the encapsulant and
the backsheet play a major role in the module degradation due to material interactions
[56]. Photovoltaic modules with c­Si technologies show median degradation rates in the
0.5–0.6%/year range with the mean in the 0.8–0.9%/year range. However, reports on
degradation rates tend to focus on not so well working systems, so the majority of real
PV systems are expected to show lower degradation rates [56]. At present the biggest
manufacturers of bifacial modules provide an annual average degradation rate of 0.45­
0.55%. After thermal cycling tests, the amount of front and back side degradation for
bifacial modules are aligned and similar [57]. According to Wolhgemuth et al (2018) in
the future, failure modes such as corrosion, discoloration, delamination, soldering bonds,
and broken interconnects will be declined and failure modes such as broken cells and
glass, PID, and arcing due to higher and higher voltage systems will likely increase [40].
Like most of the current studies that also are aligned with the projection of ITRPV2022,
Sinha et al. (2021) concluded the necessity to shift away from EVA encapsulant and
recommended glass­glass construction for hot and humid environments with high wind
speeds and glass­transparent backsheet constructions for regions with low humidity, high
ultraviolet radiation and high risk of hail and alkali corrosion [58].
In more details regarding the discoloration and delamination, studies have been made
without the encapsulant, that found that the bifacial panels’ rear side passivation layer
is more sensitive to UV lighting and the power loss of the rear side is 2.4%/year and
1.4%/year for the front side [59]. Albadawi et al. (2020) study in outdoor extreme desert
conditions found that the rear side power loss due to discoloration is equal to 1.3% ­ 1.88%
and 0.39% ­ 0.78% for the front side [60]. Furthermore, bifacial PV due to the double glass
structure tends to trap more acetic acid leading to higher delamination probability [61]. To
mitigate the discoloration and delamination a change to POE or TPO encapsulant is of
high importance [62].
Microcracks that can be caused by environmental causes such as humidity, wind load,
snowfall, thermal cycling, and mechanical stress such as inappropriate packaging, trans­
portation and installation impact bifacial PV in the same way as monofacial PV, propor­
tionally with the active area. Bifacial PV modules are more resilient to microcracks from
mechanical stress because of the more robust double glass structure. A potential miti­
gation measure for microcracks is to increase the number of busbars and reposition the
outer busbars closer to the cell’s edge [63]. At the system level, microinverters, MPPT
strategies and charge controllers can lower the losses from underperforming strings [64],
[65].
Regarding PID, Sporleder et al. (2019) showed that the electrochemical formation of SiO2
and the contamination with Na, K and Ca seems to play a major role in the degradation
mechanism [66]. Bifacial PV cells are more prone to PID compared to monofacial due to
the effect of the electromagnetic shielding of the passivating AlOx, SiNy layers and the
bulk Si [67]. Moreover, bifacial PV modules with double glass and EVA encapsulant are
more susceptible to PID as there is an increase in the availability of Na+ ions from the
glass. A mitigation measure is to use polyolefin encapsulants that can prevent or reduce
the occurrence of PID [67]. According to Luo et al. (2017) and Carolus et al. (2019) rear
side of bifacial PV is affected more compared to the front side [51], [50]. Depending on
the different stages of PID, it can be partially reversed or prevented in cell or system level
[51], [50], [68], [69], [70].

LID is more significant for PERC cell technology and p­type multi­crystalline silicon cell
can lose around 10% of their power output [71], [72]. There is a relation between the
dopant boron and the residual traces of oxygen in the silicon that occurs mainly in Cz­Si
and Fz­Si [49], [73]. This type of LID is known as BO­LID and can be totally reversed with
simultaneous illumination, annealing, and a temperature of 60­300°C [74]. LeTID occurs
if, during the manufacturing process, the solar cells are exposed to high temperatures and
steep cooling. LID and LeTID affect more the rear side of PERC modules than monofacial
AL­BSF [75]. LeTID can affect both p­type and n­type cells and can be reversed, but
not completely, with high­temperature treatments [49], [76], [73]. Bifacial PERC ms­Si
experience less LiTID than monofacial PERC ms­Si cells due to less hydrogen in the rear
SiNx : H layer [77]. Furthermore, Zhang et al (2020) showed that for low irradiance both
monofacial and bifacial had the same degradation of 1.3% but as the irradiance increased
the degradation of the bifacial increased only 0.1% compared to the 0.6% for monofacial
[77].
Regarding soiling, Luque et al. (2018) showed that the average power loss for bifacial
PV was 0.236% while for monofacial 0.301% in a study in Chile [78]. It was found that
the degradation was approximately 21.6% lower for bifacial PV. Moreover, the rear side
soiling for bifacial PV did not have a significant effect with an average loss of 0.039%, 85%
lower than the front side. Bhaduri et al. (2019) showed that the soiling loss was similar
for bifacial and monofacial PV, 0.39% and 0.40% respectively, in Mumbai and 0.02% for
vertical bifacial PV [79].

IV curves
Measurements of IV curve characteristics show precisely the short circuit current, the
open circuit voltage, and the maximum power point, among other important parameters
of a PV module. Figure 4.6 presents the most important parameters of the IV curve. More­
over, IV curves can be obtained in the field or in a laboratory. In the field, an IV tracer
is necessary but will not be considered as it is out of this project’s scope. A typical mea­
surement inside the lab requires an artificial light source, a testing bench, a temperature
sensor, a reference cell with the same spectral response as the module to be tested, and
a data acquisition system.
IV curve measurements are one of the most accurate characterization tests in order to
calculate the power loss and, consequently, the annual degradation rate that is one of the
main objectives of the project [54]. Depending on which kind of defect the PV modules
have, the main parameters that change are the series resistance (Rs) and the shunt re­
sistance (Rsh) according to the single diode equivalent model. The influence of these can
be seen in Figure 4.6. Figure A.22 in Appendix shows all the possible detectable failures
that can derive from IV curves according to Kontges et al. (2014) [29].

6
Conclusions
The main objectives of this project were to do a comprehensive literature review and to
further characterize bifacial panels of 4 different designs in DTU Risø PV Park. The litera­
ture review about the degradation and faults of bifacial panels was presented in Chapter 3
section 3.2. After the characterization tests, the average degradation rate and the bifacial­
ity factor of the panels were calculated and tried to be associated with different parameters
such as the position of the panels, the string and the tracker position, the cell technology,
and the material used to be constructed. The percentage difference of the IV parameters
also has been analyzed and compared to the datasheet values.
After carefully analyzing all the data the main derived conclusions are summarized below:
1. The weighted average annual degradation rate of all the tested bifacial PV modules
in Risø PV Park is equal to 0.335% (Modules tested, N=65).
2. The average annual degradation rate of the tested bifacial Trina Duomax in Risø PV
Park is equal to 0.46% (N=35).
3. The average annual degradation rate of the tested bifacial LONGi LR4 72­HBD­
435M in Risø PV Park is equal to 0.09% (N=10).
4. The average annual degradation rate of the tested bifacial LONGi LR6 60­HBD­
310M in Risø PV Park is equal to 0.11% (N=14).
5. The average annual degradation rate of the tested bifacial Risen RSM72­6­375BMDG
in Risø PV Park is 0.54% which is the highest among the others (N=6).
6. Bottom row and top row panels, from the FT section, have approximately the same
annual degradation rate in the FT section, equal to 0.47% and 0.51%, respectively
(NBOTTOM=10, NTOP =25).
7. The more years the panels are deployed the higher the average annual degradation
rate but further research should be conducted.
8. The modules’ measured bifaciality factor corresponds to the average bifaciality fac­
tor of PERC technology (Calculated Min=66.5%, Max=70.5%, Avg=68.96%).
9. The average annual degradation rate stated in the datasheet for all the panels is
more conservative, meaning that the measured degradation rate was lower, except
for the RSM72­6­375BMDG panels which slightly exceed the stated value (+0.04%).
10. There is evidence that the position of the string and tracker has an effect on the
degradation rate of the panel as the more in the center of the FT system was located,
the higher the degradation rate, but more data points should be gathered to validate
this trend.
11. EL imaging of the bifacial Trina Duomax 295 showed strong evidence of increased
cell interconnection defects (Avg ≈16 per panel) possibly due to manufacturing
flaws. However, further research should be conducted to find the exact cause.
12. Longi LR4­72­HBD­435M and Longi LR6­60­HBD­310M back side degraded faster
than the front side.
Field degradation and failures study of bifacial PV modules
49
==PAGE:62==
13. The panel with the early signs of PID was the most degraded, with a 1.04% annual
degradation rate, which justifies the attention to this particular type of degradation
mechanism and the long­term outcomes in the total energy yield of a PV Park.
14. The burnt mark panel did not show any internal damage which shows the importance
of flash testing and EL imaging as more concise and accurate characterization tests
compared to visual inspection.
15. Electroluminescence images showed a negligible number of cell cracks (2 in total)
indicating a strong mechanical sturdiness of the double glass structure of bifacial
PV modules.
16. The panel that fell down from the 2­axis tracker had zero cell cracks affirming the
previous point.
Further research
To examine the correlation between the degradation rate and the cell technology different
panels should be tested but due to the fact that Risø PV Park had the same cell technol­
ogy modules (p­type mono PERC) it was unattainable. The exact bill of material of each
module was not clear enough from the official datasheets so it was also challenging to ob­
serve any degradation trend associated with it. In general, more data from random panels
should be gathered in order to validate the correlation of position. A completed degrada­
tion spatial heatmap with the exact position of all the panels would be useful to support this
hypothesis. Moreover, the last type of the bifacial panel, Risen RSM120­8­585BMDG, in
Risø PV Park, has to be tested and incorporated into the study for a more holistic view.
Research about the lower irradiance, 200 W/m2, should also be done in order to find a ref­
erence to compare the measured IV parameters and calculate the percentage difference
(no reference values from datasheets). Last but not least, the accuracy and uncertainties
of the flash testing and EL imaging procedure should be examined thoroughly for more
generalized and trustworthy conclusions.
