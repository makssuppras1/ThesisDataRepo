Abstract
In this study chalk from ﬁve formations are examined. The chalk is from
R˜A¸rdal and Stevns in Denmark, Mons in Belgium and two hydrocarbon
bearing formations from Central Graben in the North Sea. The porosity is
measured by grain density measurements and bulk density measurements.
The P-wave velocity is measured so is the speciﬁc surface area, which are
used as classiﬁcation of the chalk. The indirect tensile strength is measured
using the ’brazil’ method with the samples in three saturation states; dry,
water saturated and isopar saturated. Isopar is a synthetic laboratory oil,
which simulates hydrocarbon in the subsurface. Based on these measure-
ments a model for the indirect tensile strength is made based on the porosity,
with no regard to the diﬀerences in the formations.

Introduction
The main objective in this report is to examine the indirect tensile strength
of chalk saturated with diﬀerent ﬂuids. The tested chalk is from ﬁve diﬀerent
locations, two in Denmark: Rørdal and Stevns, one in Belgium, Mons and
two reservoir formations from the North Sea. The chalk was saturated with
water and isopar. Dried samples of the same chalks were also examined.
To measure the indirect tensile strength, a Brazilian test was performed on
each sample. The sonic velocity in each sample was measured, both dry
and saturated with isopar or water. This was done to see if a correlation
between indirect tensile strength and sonic velocity exists.

The strength characteristics of chalk changes with the pore ﬂuid. Exper-

Not all water molecules can be removed from chalk, even when dried at
250 ◦C. (Madland, 2005) Therefore the prevailing theory used to be capillary
pressures acting between the chalk grains with very small amounts of water
between them.
This capillary force then disappears when more water is
added to the chalk.
If this was the case the same would happen, if the
saturating ﬂuid was 100 % miscible with water. This would be the case for
methanol. As shown in Figure 4, the strength for methanol saturated chalk
is greater than that of water saturated chalk, which discounts the capillary
theory.
Several mechanisms behind this weakening of chalk have been proposed.
(Madland, 2005):
• Capillary forces, as mentioned above.
• Chemical dissolution
• Electrical surface charge and oriented dipole layers
• Adsorption pressure model
In (Madland, 2005) Madland argues that the adsorption pressure model
is the main mechanism. This mechanism is based on chalk grains adsorbing
a layer of water. This layer of water then creates a small distance between
the grains, and thereby reduces the cohesion between them.
Another mechanism that eﬀects the strength of the rock is a chemical
one.
This is precipitation and dissolution of ions into the ﬂuid near the
grain contacts. (Madland, 2006) Based on experiments done with synthetic
seawater (SSW), it was found that the amount of SO2−

A stress wave is wave propagating through a medium.
In this case the
medium is chalk. The two main stress waves are P- and S-waves. P-waves are
called primary waves or pressure waves, while S-waves are called secondary
waves or shear waves. P-waves travel faster than S-waves and are therefore
the ﬁrst measured wave in a case, where both waves are induced, e.g. and
earthquake. The diﬀerence between the waves is shown in Figure 5.

The P-wave velocity is an indicator of the strength of the rock. The wave
will propagate more rapidly through stiﬀer and denser material. In a dry
sample the stiﬀness and density of the air is assumed to be zero. Therefore
only the stiﬀness and density of the chalk itself is taken into account. As

6.8.5
Validity of tests
A brazil test is only valid, if the breakage starts in the center of the sample.
If the sample breaks in the radial plane, the test is invalid. This can happen,
if a soft material between the sample and the platens is used. (Colback et al.,
1966) The fracture can originate on periphery of the disc, close to the load
points. Ehrnford found that this is due to tensile stress build up on the
periphery. The conclusion was that the width of the load, should not exceed
15% of the diameter of the disk and not be smaller than 10%. At a load
width of 15% of the diameter, the maximum tensile stress is applied to 45%
of the loaded diameter.
Using a load width below 10% of the diameter,
the tensile stress applied to periphery of the specimen is greater, than the
tensile stress applied to the center of the disk. This will create a failure on
the periphery and thereby invalidate the test. The research was done by
means of ﬁnite element modeling. (Ehrnford, 1981) A tensile stress higher
than the tensile strength on the periphery will cause development of cracks
near the contact points, as shown in Figure 12

Geology
7.1
Chalk
Chalk is a sedimentary rock. This means the rock is composed of many small
sediments. Unlike clay and sand, which are sedimentary rocks too, chalk is
composed of fossils. These fossils originate from shells from sea organisms.
In shallow waters, the organisms live on the sea ﬂoor. The shells from these
are composed of aragonite, calcite and Mg-bearing calcite. Since aragonite
and Mg-bearing calcite rapidly dissolve and reprecipitate as calcite, hence
the porosity is quickly lost, and therefore decrease the oil bearing potential.
The porosity will rarely be above 25 %.
If the sediments are deposited at a deep water depth, the calcite will

of opal frameworks, the rock will become diatomite.
Both of these rock
types are porous, with a porosity of up to 50 %.
Both these rocks will
form oil and gas reservoirs, but the nearly impermeable diatomite is very
challenging to produce from. In this report only chalk is considered, since
the petroleum reservoirs in the Danish North Sea are all chalk reservoirs.

The samples are examined and deviations from a perfect cylinder is

with masking tape. The masking tape ensures a low friction interface be-

to ensure a smooth motion limiting the axial forces. The force transducer
is moved down after contact, to ensure there is no pressure on the sample.

8.10.1
Deviations from standard
The tests have deviated from the standard.
These deviations are shown
below.
• Diameter of samples ASTM D3967-08 speciﬁes that the recom-
mended core size is 54 mm, but should be at least 10 times the grain
size of the material. The grain size for chalk is under 10 µm(Røgen
et al., 2001), therefore a diameter less than 54 mm, is acceptable. A di-
ameter of 38 mm was used to increase the number of samples. (ASTM,
2012)
• Length of samples Traditionally discs for brazil tests are the length
of the radius of the plug. This is also speciﬁed by ISRM(Hatheway,
2009). In this study the samples were cut to a length of 20 mm. This
was done to ensure no end eﬀects in the sonic velocity measurements.
The manual for the equipment states that samples must have a mini-
mum length of 20 mm.
• Core quality Chalk’s characteristics are closer to hard soil, than
those of rock. Hence, creating smooth cores is very diﬃcult. ASTM
D3967-08 speciﬁes; ”The circumferential surface of the specimen shall
be smooth and straight to 0.50 mm”. This is diﬃcult to obtain, and
cores were drilled out as smooth as possible.
• Masking tape The masking tape used was 0.1 mm thick. The ISRM
standard speciﬁes a double thickness masking tape with a thickness of
0.2-0.4 mm.
• Lubrication The ASTM standard speciﬁes that the ball bearing should
be lubricated. In the experiments the ball bearing was not lubricated.

typical ’stress-strain’ plot for a good fail is shown in Figure 47. This is not
always the case, some times a small piece of the sample will spall oﬀat the

10.5
Geology
The model proposed in this study is based data from all ﬁve formations. This
is done even though the formations exhibit diﬀerent geological properties.
The reservoir samples have been buried much deeper (3000m compared to
500-750m for outcrop), which has induced larger grains and pore ﬁlling
cementation This is evident in the diﬀerence in porosity and speciﬁc surface
area.
Also the mineralogy of the non-carbonate fraction of the chalk is
diﬀerent, which could eﬀect the tensile strength.

11
Conclusion
In this study ﬁve chalk formations are studied. These are Mons in Belgium,
Stevns and R˜A¸rdal from denmark and two hydrocarbon bearing reservoir
chalks from Central Graben in the North Sea. Thirty samples were plugged
and cut from each formation, so the brazil test could be performed ten
times on each formation-ﬂuid combination.
For the reservoir formations
fewer specimens were made, due to material scarcity.
The chalk was classiﬁed by measuring the Speciﬁc Surface Area, the
Bulk Density, the P-wave Velocity and the Grain Density. The bulk density
was both measured using the Archimedes method of submerging in water
and via geometric measurement of the cylinder and the dry weight.
A third of the samples were kept dry, a third were saturated with chalk
saturated water and the last third saturated with Isopar-L, a synthetic lab-
oratory oil. The saturation was veriﬁed by weighing the samples, and when
the weight stopped increasing, the samples were assumed saturated. The P-
wave velocity was measured on the saturated samples. The indirect tensile
strength was measured by the ’brazil test’.
The three main results are:
• The main tensile strength controlling mechanism is the porosity.
• The water weakening eﬀect is acting on the tensile strength; the water
saturated samples were weaker than the dry samples, while the isopar
saturated samples were nearly as strong as the dry samples.
• A model for the tensile strength from the porosity was made using
both outcrop and reservoir results.
• Isolating the reservoir formation and modeling only that, did not result
in good models.
Three models were created, one for dry indirect tensile strength, one for
isopar saturated indirect tensile strength and a last one for the water satu-
rated indirect tensile strength. These models are all based on the porosity.
The models and the data is shown in Figure 71.

A.4.2
Frequency
In addition to the sonic velocity through the sample, the frequency of the
signal was measured. This was done my measuring the time at four points,
were the signal crossed the axis based at the noise level, before the signal.
Figure 73: Example of frequency measurements on an arbitrary sinus curve.
Figure 73 shows an example of an idealized wave. The four points were
the wave crosses the axis are shown in Table 46. The time from point 4 to
point 1 is the wavelength of 1.5 waves. Divide this by 1.5, and the wavelength
for one wave is found. In this case π s. The frequency is 1/wavelength, the
frequency in this case is 1/π Hz ≈0.32 Hz.
