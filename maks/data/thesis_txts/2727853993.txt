Abstract
This project focuses on exploring the generation of high-quality audio from images for
the automatic sonorization of video sequences, with the aim of advancing AI-generated
audio. Challenges in achieving realistic audio synthesis arise from the sensitivity of the
human ear and the alignment of video with audio content. We designed, built, and tested
a pipeline to process video sequences using state-of-the-art AI components for video, text,
and audio, based on multimodal data representations.
The pipeline starts with the CLIP model for image processing and includes AudioLDM,
CoCa, and VAE models to enable audio generation from images. For evaluation, we used
a novel metric called ImageBind for embedding extraction. The use of pre-trained models
helps to save time and resources during the process.
We demonstrated the potential of the proposed validation scheme in assessing the quality
of the generated audio and its consistency with the video input.
To ensure ease of use and collaboration, a GitHub repository is provided, providing a
user-friendly platform for sound generation. The integration of audio, image, and text
embeddings contributes to the development of powerful audio models. In particular, the
approach of caption-based audio generation effectively captures semantic relationships,
thereby enhancing the contextual relevance of audio synthesis.
The inclusion of MOS
ratings allows subjective opinions and different user preferences to be taken into account
when evaluating the audio.
The proposed pipeline was able to sonorize short video sequences in a way that is consistent
with user preferences. The quality of the audio delivered matched user needs and received
good gradings in our subjective tests.
This project will foster innovative opportunities for future research in audio generation,
leading to advances in realistic and high-quality AI-generated audio. Future work shall
supplement the tests included in this Thesis and implement these for real applications.
This project was conducted in collaboration between DTU, UPM, and AWS research
teams.

Introduction
Over the past decades, Artificial Intelligence (AI) technologies have advanced rapidly,
leading to various breakthroughs in image and video generation. Despite these advance-
ments, generating realistic audio using AI systems remains a major challenge. This is
primarily due to the complexity of generating high-quality audio that can be discerned
by the human ear, which is far more sensitive than the human eye, and to automatically
match video and sound content [M. R. Berg 2020].
In fact, while there are several platforms capable of generating images from descriptions,
such as OpenIA’s DALL-E [M. D. M. Reddy et al. 2021], there are relatively few platforms
that can generate high-quality audio from descriptions or images [Sheffer and Adi 2022,
Iashin and Rahtu 2021]. Audio generation is still lagging behind for three main reasons: the
lack of large datasets of high-quality text-audio pairs [Huang et al. 2023], the complexity
of modeling long continuous audio data and producing it in real-time, and the lack of
methods to manage them eﬀiciently [Steinmetz and Reiss 2021].
This project specifically aims to investigate the feasibility of generating high-quality audio
from images, with the goal of advancing the state of the art in AI-generated audio. To
achieve this objective, the project focuses on the development and evaluation of new AI
schemes capable of producing realistic audio that matches the visual content of images,
producing a consistent sonorization of a scene based on image frames and agnostic models.
This project not only advances the state-of-the-art in AI-generated audio but also explores
new objective metrics for audio quality evaluation, particularly in terms of matching the
audio generated with the image representation. As part of this research, a pipeline that
will be used for this purpose has been developed.
1.1
Problem Statement
The aim of this project is to develop a pipeline that can generate high-fidelity, high-
quality audio content from visual representations, which has the potential to revolutionize
the consumption and interaction of audiovisual content. This innovative technology not
only provides new avenues for creative expression in the film and video game industries
but also serves as a valuable tool to streamline the sound design process, which often
involves multiple steps and meticulous attention to detail.
Traditionally, sound designers have to go through various stages in the sound design
process, including recording, editing, and mixing, to achieve the desired audio quality
[PITCHBENDS 2022]. The proposed pipeline can significantly assist sound designers in
these stages by offering a novel approach to generate audio based on visual representations,
reducing the need for extensive manual recording or acquisition of audio elements. This
can save valuable time and effort in the sound design workflow, allowing sound designers
to focus more on the creative aspects of their work. It may also allow content creators to
rapidly build mock-up for their pieces, and to collect ideas during the creative process.
For instance, during the recording and design stage, sound designers often rely on original
audio recordings or audio libraries to capture the necessary sounds. With the proposed
pipeline, sound designers can generate specific audio elements directly from visual rep-
resentations, eliminating the need for extensive recording sessions or searching through

Given the current state of research and development, generating audio directly from an
image remains an unexplored territory. Therefore, this project proposes a groundbreaking
pipeline, which is yet to be realized as a practical application.
By utilizing advanced models, the process of extracting coherent audio representations
from video game images is explored, which will be thoroughly explained in the subsequent
sections of this report. The pipeline incorporates these models to generate a corresponding
sound that captures the essence of the input image, as depicted in Figure 1.1.

1.2
Thesis Objectives
The main objective of this project is to automatically enrich short, simple video sequences
with audio content. To this end, there are some objectives to be achieved:
1. Investigate the feasibility of generating high-quality audio from images.
2. Advance the state of the art in AI-generated audio.
3. Develop and evaluate new AI models capable of producing realistic audio that
matches the visual content of images.
4. Develop objective metrics for audio quality evaluation, particularly in terms of
matching the audio generated with the image representation.
5. Combine different approaches to achieve a comprehensive understanding of generat-
ing realistic audio using AI systems.
6. Present the research at the TECNIACUSTICA 2023 Congress.
7. Develop an open-source pipeline for generating audio directly from images.
8. Explore the process of extracting coherent audio representations from video game
images.
9. Incorporate and evaluate CoCa and AudioLDM models in the “Zero-shot sonorizing
pipeline” module.
10. Design a novel metric to assess the alignment between visual scenes and generated
audio.
11. Understand how audio generation can be conditioned using Variational Autoen-

State of the Art
Storyboards visually outline the progression of a story scene by scene. They are com-
monly used in the film, performance, and video game industries to plan and organize
the main elements, viewpoints, and events. Score creators and sound designers rely on
these storyboards to plan their work. By utilizing multimodal correspondence between
self-supervised image and audio representations, sound sequences (ambient music, sound
effects, audio parts, etc.) can be developed to complement the video sequence with a
multimodal experience.
Likewise, there is an incremental interest in using audio generation as this ability is useful
for many real applications, such as sound or video editing automation, enhanced experience
of immersion in virtual reality, object detection and tracking, and even assistance for people
with visual impairments.
Deep learning techniques are being used in audio generation from video sequences due
to their ability to learn complex patterns and relationships in data.
However, audio
generation from video sequences is a challenging task, as it requires the model to capture
the nuances and dynamics of the audio. Some of these are highly correlated with the visual
information in the video, while others may provide additional information and context.
In this work, we will focus on the first, to ensure that both video and audio content
complement each other.
Careful examination and study of state-of-the-art technologies have been undertaken to
determine their applicability in addressing the challenges posed by our problem. We are
looking for a “Zero-shot sonorizing” pipeline (see Figure 2.1) that takes advantage of re-
cent advances in the literature on sound generation, as discussed earlier in Section 1.1.1
and will be illustrated in more detail in Figure 2.12.

2.1
Deep Learning in Sound Generation
When it comes to realistic sound generation, identifying the most appropriate deep learn-
ing architecture is an ongoing area of research that is closely related to audio representa-
tion. Achieving high-quality sound synthesis presents unique challenges, such as capturing
complex audio features and preserving perceptual fidelity. Researchers continue to explore
and develop architectures that effectively address these complexities to advance the field
of realistic sound generation (sound effects and ambient audio are the sounds targeted in
this project) [Library 2021]. This section discusses deep learning alternatives for sound
generation found in the literature.

2.1.1
Generative Models
Generative models are important tools in the field of deep learning, enabling the creation
of new data samples by learning the underlying probability distribution of the training
data through deep neural networks1. These models then sample from this distribution to
generate new data.
Natsiou et al. [Natsiou and O’Leary 2021] outline each sound representation’s different
advantages and disadvantages, as this choice is one of the most significant factors in the
development of a model for audio synthesis. Among the possible generative models to be
used, the following can be found:
1. Autoregressive Models. This method encompasses generative models in which
a new sample depends on previous ones. Autoregressive models are easy to train
and can operate data in real time, even though the generation is sequential and,
consequently, slow.
They can be applied in sound generation.
WaveNet [Oord,
Dieleman, et al. 2016] and MelNet [Vasquez and Lewis 2019] are significant papers
on autoregressive models.
2. Variational Autoencoder (VAE). This deep learning architecture includes two
different networks: an encoder and a decoder. The encoder models a latent distribu-
tion and, using the Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence [Joyce 2011] as a loss function,
it minimizes the reconstruction error and maximizes a regularization term, resulting
in a latent space. Then, the decoder can take a point from the latent space, that
allows for generative capabilities, and map it back to the original input space to
create a reconstructed output. The main application of a VAE is sound generation.
The paper Differentiable Digital Signal Processing (DDSP) [Engel et al. 2020] uses
VAE networks.
3. Adversarial Learning. This method presents the main architecture of the Generative
Adversarial Network (GAN) model, which consists of two neural networks matching
against each other. Specifically, it is based on two models: the Generator (G), which
maps random noise to a preferable representation (when it comes to audio, the rep-
resentation could either be in the form of unprocessed audio or a spectrogram2),
and the Discriminator (D), which takes the preferable representation and differen-
tiates between real and fake data. Adversarial learning methods can be applied in
text-to-speech applications, speech synthesis, speech enhancement, or symbolic mu-
sic generation. WaveGAN [Yamamoto, Song, and J.-M. Kim 2020] is a generative
model based on GANs.
4. Normalizing Flows. Normalizing flows are a type of generative model that includes
multiple simple distributions used to transform input data into latent representa-
tions. Some applications of this method include the generation of realistic speech
waveforms and the synthesis of new audio samples. a flow-based generative network
is WaveGlow [Prenger, Valle, and Catanzaro 2019].

are summarised in Table 2.1.

2.1.2
Diffusion Models
Diffusion models are a deep learning tool that aims to generate high-quality samples by
iteratively refining a given initial distribution. In diffusion models, the generation process
starts with a random noise vector or an initial distribution. The model then gradually
“diffuses” the noise by applying a sequence of diffusion steps. At each step, the model
refines the noise vector by applying a diffusion process that gradually adds more structure
and complexity to the data. The diffusion process typically involves applying a series of
transformations to the noise vector. These transformations are often based on stochastic
differential equations or Markov chain Monte Carlo methods [Tolver 2016]. The diffusion
steps are designed to progressively reduce the noise level and increase the richness of the
generated samples.
Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM), developed by Ho et al. [Ho, Jain, and
Abbeel 2020] consists of a diffusion model for high-quality image synthesis using latent
diffusion probabilistic models [Rombach et al. 2022] in the pixel space.
These models
parameterize a fixed Markov chain to produce samples matching the data after a finite
time. In other words, they learn a data distribution by gradually denoising a normally
distributed variable.
Diffusion models also offer significant potential for audio generation tasks. They excel
at capturing complex audio distributions, providing controllable synthesis, handling long-
term dependencies, and mitigating problems such as mode collapse [Kong et al. 2020, S.
Luo et al. 2023]. These models generate rich and realistic audio samples while allowing
the user to control the synthesis process. However, as stated in [Kreuk et al. 2022], dif-
fusion models also present challenges, including computational complexity, training data
requirements, potential mode collapse, hyperparameter tuning, limited interpretability
and control, diﬀiculties in modeling long-term structure, and sensitivity to noise levels.
Despite these drawbacks, ongoing research aims to address these challenges and further
enhance the capabilities of diffusion models for realistic and high-quality audio synthesis.

CLIP
Contrastive Language-Image Pre-Training (CLIP) [Radford et al. 2021] is a neural network
model by OpenAI that consists of two main components: a vision model and a language
model. The vision model is responsible for processing and understanding images, while the
language model handles the textual descriptions. These two models are trained together
in a way that enables them to learn a joint representation of images and text, thanks to
the encoders built into its architecture. Specifically, CLIP uses a ViT-like transformer to
obtain visual features and a causal language model to obtain textual features. Both the
text and visual features are then projected onto a latent space of the same dimension. The
dot product between the projected image and text features is then used as a similarity
score, as shown in the lower left of Figure 2.3.

During training, CLIP is exposed to a large dataset containing pairs of images and their
associated textual descriptions. Specifically, their best-performing CLIP model is trained
on 256 GPUs for two weeks, which is similar to existing large-scale image models. The
goal is for the model to learn to associate the correct image with its corresponding text.
To achieve this, CLIP uses a technique called contrastive learning.
Contrastive learning [Hadsell, Chopra, and LeCun 2006] involves creating positive and
negative examples for the model to learn from. In the case of CLIP, positive examples are
pairs of images and texts that match each other, while negative examples are pairs where
the image and text do not correspond. The model is trained to maximize the similarity
between positive examples and minimize the similarity between negative examples.
To measure similarity, CLIP uses a similarity metric known as the cosine similarity. It
calculates the cosine of the angle between the embeddings (the meaningful numerical rep-
resentations) of images and texts. Higher cosine similarity indicates a stronger relationship
between the image and text. This form of similarity is preferred to the more conventional
Euclidean or Mahalanobis distances for highly dimensional spaces and when the number
of classes is also large, as it overcomes the curse of dimensionality.
Once trained, CLIP can be used for various tasks such as image classification, image
generation from textual prompts, and text-based image retrieval. It has shown impressive
capabilities in understanding and reasoning across different modalities, bridging the gap
between vision and language. More importantly, it can be used to connect images and
text with other representations, including audio.
As shown in Figure 2.3, CLIP uses two types of encoder: an image encoder and a text
encoder. It is important to note that the CLIP architecture does not include decoders.
These encoders play a crucial role in the CLIP architecture by converting images and text
into embeddings.
• Image encoder: The image encoder in CLIP is based on a Convolutional Neural
Networks (CNN).
Specifically, CLIP uses a variant of the ResNet architecture,
which is a widely used deep learning model for computer vision tasks. The ResNet
architecture [He et al. 2016] consists of several layers of convolutional and pooling
operations that extract hierarchical features from images. These features capture
different levels of abstraction, from simple edges and textures to more complex visual
concepts. The image encoder processes the input image through the ResNet layers

BERT models [Devlin et al. 2018] are pre-trained on large amounts of text data and

Contrastive Language-Audio Pre-Training (CLAP) [Wu et al. 2023] is a LAION’s model

language descriptions. In this case, CLAP trains both an audio encoder and a text encoder

The architecture of this system, shown in Figure 2.4, consists of two audio encoders and

Im2Wav [Sheffer and Adi 2022] is a label-free system for audio generation for image-guided
open-domain. “Label-free” means that the model does not require explicit audio labels,
such as text descriptions or categories, during the training or generation process. Instead,
it uses image information to guide the audio generation process.
The importance of avoiding labels in this context lies in the potential limitations and
challenges associated with relying on labeled data. Labeling audio data can be subjective,
time-consuming, and costly. In addition, obtaining high-quality labeled audio data for
different domains and applications can be challenging. By adopting a label-free approach,
Im2Wav avoids these problems and focuses on using the visual information provided by
images to generate corresponding audio. However, the lack of explicit labels can also in-
troduce limitations and challenges. Without labels, it becomes more diﬀicult to control
or specify characteristics, styles, or attributes of the generated audio. It may be more dif-
ficult to ensure that the generated audio meets specific requirements or desired outcomes.
In addition, evaluating the performance or quality of the generated audio becomes more
subjective and challenging without explicit labels for comparison.

the objects contained in the images. To do that, Sheffer et al. use an audio encoder-decoder

SMs
Socratic Models (SMs) [Zeng et al. 2022], on the other hand, provide a framework in which
multiple large pre-trained models can be composed through a prompt without training to
perform new downstream multi-modal tasks. This is an alternative method for composing
pre-trained models that directly use language as the intermediate representation through
which the modules exchange information with each other.
By prompting a guided language-based exchange between a Visual Language Model (VLM)
and an Language Model (LM), SMs can generate captions. To begin with, the VLM is
utilized for zero-shot identification of various categories such as places, objects, image
types, and the number of people present.
The top-k results in each category can be
substituted into a language model prompt. Secondly, using the language prompt informed
by VLM, a text completion function generates n candidate captions. This step uses a
non-zero next-token sampling temperature to ensure diverse but sensible results across
the n candidates. Finally, the VLM scores the n captions with the image, and the caption
with the highest score is returned.
Although the code for this model is available [Maria Attarian 2022], the evaluation of the
captions generated by this model led this project to consider other image-to-text models.

2.5
Audio Synthesis Evaluation
The assessment of the sound quality generated is critical to ensure that the sound gener-
ation system satisfies the desired quality requirements, performs effectively, and is appro-
priate for its intended application.
Audio synthesis involves numerous aspects that can be evaluated. These aspects include
the quality of sound reproduction, the realism of generated sounds, the level of expressive-
ness, the ability to capture different sound characteristics, and the fidelity to the original
audio source.
In the research already commented on in Section 2.1, Natsiou et al. [Natsiou and O’Leary
2021] explore the evaluation of the audio synthesis of these generative models, which
remains under study. Despite the numerous attempts, the metrics used are less reliable
than the subject evaluation of human listeners.
The most commonly used evaluation
techniques are:
1. Perceptual evaluation. This perceptual technique evaluates metrics such as Mean
Opinion Score (MOS) [Streijl, Winkler, and Hands 2016] or Confidence Interval (CI)
[O’Brien and Yi 2016], and makes use of Amazon Mechanical Turk [Ipeirotis, Provost,
22
Zero-shot sonorizing of video sequences
==PAGE:35==
and Jing Wang 2010] to find individuals with specific characteristics to be evaluated.
2. Number of Statistically-Different Bin (NDB). This clustering technique is
used to estimate the diversity of the synthesized sounds. The NDB technique is
based on the premise that spectrograms of diverse synthesized sounds will have var-
ied statistical distributions across different bins, whereas spectrograms of similar or
less diverse sounds will have narrower statistical distributions. Thus, NDB quanti-
tatively measures the diversity of synthesized sounds based on the analysis of their
spectrograms.
3. Inception Score (IS). This is a metric adopted by GANs and used to correlate with
human evaluation. This metric is maximized when the generated samples belong to
just one class and each class is predicted equally often.
4. Distances-based metrics. This evaluation includes metrics to measure the dis-
tance between the original and the sound-generated representations.
5. Spectral Convergence (SC). This metric evaluates the mean difference between
the original and the generated spectrogram and can be also considered as the mini-
mization of the Griﬀin-Lim algorithm [Griﬀin and Lim 1984].
As we have just seen, various objective and subjective metrics are available to evaluate
audio generation. However, when it comes to assessing the perception of the generated
audio, particularly its coherence with respect to the intended sound image, we encounter
a domain with vast unexplored territory. Despite the existence of some evaluation tech-
niques, there is no standardized approach for comprehensively capturing the intricacies
of sound generation and its alignment with the desired sound image. Additionally, the
absence of a comprehensive benchmark dataset further hinders evaluation [Mumtaz et al.
2021]. To address this, collaborative efforts are needed to establish standardized protocols
and metrics. Perceptually informed metrics and diverse benchmark datasets can help en-
sure consistent and reliable evaluation, and drive advances in audio generation techniques.
In reviewing the literature studies discussed in the previous Section 2.4, the metrics used
for evaluation are examined in each case.
In Make-An-Audio, Huang et al. evaluate models using objective and subjective metrics
over audio quality and text-audio alignment faithfulness. Among the metrics used in this
paper to measure audio fidelity, Fréchet Inception Distance (FID), Kullback–Leibler (KL)
divergence, and Melception-based can be found. The CLAP score is also introduced to
measure audio-text alignment. As a subjective quality measure, this study evaluates Mean
Opinion Score (MOS).
• The FID [Heusel et al. 2017] is a popular metric used to evaluate the similarity
between two sets of images by comparing their probabilistic distributions in a feature
space. It was introduced as an image quality measure in GAN but has since been
widely used for various image generation tasks.
FID can also be adapted for audio generation. By converting audio samples into
spectrograms and using an Inception-based model to extract features, FID can mea-
sure the similarity between real and generated audio distributions. A lower FID score
indicates higher quality and similarity. This objective metric provides researchers
with a quantitative evaluation of audio generation models, complementing subjective
assessments and facilitating comparisons and benchmarking.

• Melception-based is an objective measure of audio quality. By training the Melcep-
tion network on a large dataset of audio signals with known quality levels, this meter
can learn to predict the quality of new audio signals based on their mel-spectrograms.
Compared to traditional objective audio quality metrics, such as Perceptual Evalua-
tion of Audio Quality (PEAQ)9, the Melception-based metric has several advantages.
In the first place, this method requires no original reference signal to be used. It
also has the ability to handle various forms of audio distortion such as noise, rever-
beration, and compression artifacts. In addition, the method produces a continuous
quality score that provides more comprehensive information than a binary pass/no-
pass classification.
• CLAP score is a metric adapted from the CLIP score [Hessel et al. 2021] to the au-
dio domain and a reference-free measure that illustrates personalized text-to-audio
results. For comparison, realism is measured by the 1-MSE10 distance between gen-
erated and original audio, and faithfulness is measured by the CLAP score between
the generated sample.
Regarding the subjective evaluation, MOS is used to assess the audio quality and text-
audio alignment11 faithfulness by respectively scoring MOS-Q and MOS-F. MOS-F stands
for Mean Opinion Score-Full, which is a subjective test used to evaluate the overall quality
of a complete audio signal, including speech, music, and background noise. Whereas, MOS-
Q stands for Mean Opinion Score-Quality, which is a subjective test used to evaluate the
quality of a specific audio component or attribute, such as clarity, loudness, distortion, or
noise.
Both the MOS-F and MOS-Q tests are conducted by presenting a group of listeners with a
series of audio stimuli and asking them to rate the quality of the audio on a scale, typically
ranging from 1 (worst) to 5 (best). The scores of several listeners are then averaged to
give the MOS score for each stimulus.
In DiffSound, FID, KL, and Audio Caption Loss are used as objective metrics. In this
research, Yang et al. developed the metric Audio Caption Loss to measure the difference
between the predicted and ground truth audio features, which are used to condition the
language model. This loss measures the alignment between the audio features and the
corresponding words in the caption.
In AudioLDM, the main objective metrics used to evaluate the quality of the sound
generated are Fréchet Distance (FD), Fréchet Audio Distance (FAD), Inception Score (IS),
and KL divergence.
FAD [Kilgour et al. 2019] is a metric used to evaluate the similarity between two sets of
audio samples by comparing their probabilistic distributions in a feature space. FAD is
inspired by the FID metric used in image generation tasks.
To compute FAD, two sets of audio samples are required: a real audio dataset and a
generated (or synthetic) audio dataset. The real dataset represents the ground truth or
the desired audio samples, while the generated dataset contains audio samples generated

FAD uses a feature extractor, the pre-trained deep neural
network model VGGish [Hershey et al. 2017], to extract high-level audio features from
both the real and generated audio samples. These features are then used to compute the
mean and covariance of the feature distributions for each dataset.
Next, FAD measures the distance between the two distributions using the Fréchet distance,
which takes into account the means and covariances. This distance metric captures both
the similarity of the means (representing the quality of the samples generated) and the
similarity of the covariances (representing the diversity and distribution of the samples
generated).
The output of FAD is a single scalar value representing the distance between the real and
generated audio distributions. A smaller FAD value indicates a closer match between the
real and generated audio samples, implying better quality and similarity. To evaluate the
output of the FAD, it is important to establish a baseline or reference point. This can be
done by calculating FAD scores between different real audio datasets or by comparing the
FAD score of the generated samples with a benchmark dataset representing high-quality
audio.
As for the subjective evaluation, in this study, Liu et al. recruited six professionals to
carry out a rating process for the generated samples, which were to be rated based on the
Overall quality (OVL); and the RELevance to the input text (REL) on a scale from 1 to
100. The participant had to rate these metrics for each audio file generated, given the
text description.
Both OVL and REL are metrics for evaluating text generation models, as they capture
different aspects of the quality and appropriateness of the generated text. OVL measures
the quality of the generated text in terms of its linguistic properties, while REL measures
the relevance and coherence of the generated text with respect to the input text. In prac-
tice, in order to produce high-quality and relevant output, a good text generation model
should achieve high scores on both metrics.
On the other hand, there are studies on the basis of deep audio embeddings. Comparison
and Analysis of Deep Audio Embeddings for Music Emotion Recognition [Koh
and Dubnov 2021] is an example in which musical emotion semantics for the MER task
without expert human engineering are represented. Music Emotion Recognition (MER)
is the study of the automatic detection and analysis of emotions expressed in music. It
involves the use of computational models and algorithms to recognize emotional content in
musical pieces by extracting features from audio signals. MER has applications in music
recommendation, therapy, and entertainment. Challenges include the subjective nature of
emotions and cultural differences in their perception. Ongoing research aims to improve
accuracy and incorporate multimodal cues to better understand emotional responses to
music.
Another research using embeddings is ImageBind. It uses pairs of modalities representing
images and another modality (I, M) to learn a single joint embedding by encoding them
into normalized embeddings. The embeddings and the encoders are then optimized using
an InfoNCE loss [Oord, Yazhe Li, and Vinyals 2018], so that the normalized embeddings
remain closer in the joint embedding space, thus aligning I and M. Although ImageBind
was initially trained using modalities paired with images (I, M), two pairs of modalities
were observed to align in the embedding space.
A model such as ImageBind, which learns joint embeddings for multiple modalities, can

Development
This chapter presents the development of the VAE and the Zero-shot sonorizing pipeline
of this project, as well as the tests that we performed for the evaluation. At the same time,
traditional FAD and MOS evaluation techniques are used to evaluate the fidelity of the
generated sound with respect to the source image. In addition, we propose embedding-
based techniques for the evaluation of the generated audio.
4.1
Audio Generation: Variational Autoencoder
The process starts with a VAE and a collection of audio data containing the GAPS chunks
and corresponding animal sounds. A pre-processing step is then performed, which involves
converting the WAV audio files into spectrograms using Librosa1.
Once the spectrograms are obtained, they are passed through the VAE’s encoder (see
VAE architecture in Subsection 2.1.1). The encoder maps each spectrogram into a lower-
dimensional latent space representation that captures the essential features and patterns
present in the audio.
After encoding, the latent representation is passed through the VAE decoder. The decoder
reconstructs the spectrogram from the latent space, effectively converting it back to the
original audio format. The reconstructed spectrogram is then converted to a WAV format.

presented our approach to the problem of generating sound from an image.

4.3
Audio Generation Evaluation Metrics
Once we have generated the audio with our pipeline, we want to find a way to evaluate
it. One of the goals is to evaluate audio fidelity without relying on an audio reference.
Despite an extensive literature search, no existing system has been found that allows the
evaluation of audio fidelity in relation to the source image. This project proposes objective
and subjective metrics that can assess the quality of generated audio samples without the
need for a reference sound for comparison.
In addition, the project proposes metrics that explore the use of multimodal embeddings
to quantify the fidelity of the generated audio. For example, one approach examines the
consistency between modalities by assessing the similarity between the image and audio
embeddings extracted from the ImageBind model. Another approach creates a novel met-
ric based on the distance between embeddings, which measures the dissimilarity between
the generated audio embeddings and the desired ideal audio embedding.
4.3.1
Traditional Evaluation Metrics
We chose Fréchet Audio Distance (FAD) to measure audio similarity and Mean Opinion
Score (MOS) to assess perceptual quality. These metrics provide robust and comprehensive
evaluations for our audio generation model.

• FAD: We use the FAD implementation used in this project, available on GitHub
[Tan 2023], to compute the Fréchet distance between the generated and the reference

4.3.2.1
Inconsistency Metric
One of the proposed alternatives in this project for assessing the fidelity of the sound in
the absence of reference audio is the Inconsistency measure. This objective metric is based
on the distances between embeddings of different modalities, including image, text, and
audio, with the primary goal of quantifying the differences or disparities between these
modalities.
Inconsistency = 2 × cos_dist(eimage, etext) −cos_dist(eimage, eaudio)
(4.1)
The Inconsistency proposed metric (see Formula 4.1) is calculated by subtracting the
cosine distance between the image and audio embeddings (referred to in Formula 4.1 as
eimage and eaudio, respectively) from twice the cosine distance between the image and
text embeddings (referred to as eimage and etext, respectively). This expression provides
an indication of the inconsistency or dissimilarity between the modalities, allowing for
a quantitative assessment of potential disparities or discrepancies within the multimodal
data.
To perform the analysis, embeddings of images, captions, and audio are extracted from
ImageBind, providing a representation of each modality.
The cosine distance is then
computed by comparing two embedding vectors: one between image-text and the other
between text-audio. The cosine distance quantifies the dissimilarity between the vectors
by measuring the angle between them.
The cosine similarity (see Formula 2.6), from which the cosine distance is derived, ranges
between -1 and 1, with 1 indicating identical vectors, -1 indicating completely opposite
vectors, and 0 indicating orthogonal or unrelated vectors. Consequently, the cosine dis-
tance ranges from 0 to 2, where 0 indicates identical vectors and 2 indicates vectors that
are completely dissimilar or opposite in direction.

VAE definition
On the other hand, once we have pre-processed all our data, the data path is fed into our

from the learned distribution and capture the underlying structure of the data.

As noted in Subsection 2.1.1, the VAE learns to represent and generate data by encoding
it into a lower dimensional latent space and then decoding it back into the original space.
It consists of an encoder and a decoder network.
The VAE learns to encode an input spectrogram into a lower dimensional latent space,
allowing eﬀicient data representation. The encoder network maps the input spectrogram
to the latent space, and the decoder network reconstructs the spectrogram from the latent
code. The VAE is trained by optimizing a loss function consisting of a reconstruction loss
(measuring the difference between the input spectrogram and the reconstructed spectro-
gram) and a regularisation term (measuring the difference between the learned distribution
and the standard normal distribution using KL divergence). The goal is to train the VAE
to generate realistic samples from the learned distribution and to capture the underlying
structure of the data.
Below, the definition of the VAE [María Sánchez Ruiz 2023] can be found according to
the code implemented for this purpose.
1. Encoder:
The VariationalEncoder class defines the encoder network. It takes an input spec-
trogram x and processes it through a series of convolutional layers with batch nor-
malization and ReLU activation3. The output of the convolutional layers is then
flattened and mapped to the mean µ and standard deviation σ of the latent dis-
tribution.
The µ and σ are obtained by applying linear transformations to the
flattened output. The latent code z is sampled from a normal distribution using the
reparameterization trick4, where z = µ + σ · ϵ and ϵ is a sample from a standard
normal distribution. As discussed earlier, the KL divergence between the learned
and standard normal distributions serves as a measure of the difference between the
distributions and is incorporated into the loss function in the learning process.
2. Decoder:
The Decoder class defines the decoder network. It takes the latent representation,
z, as input and passes it through linear layers to map it back to a 3D tensor5.
The 3D tensor is then unflattened to a 4D tensor and processed through a series of
transposed convolutional layers to gradually increase the spatial resolution. Batch
normalization and ReLU activation are applied after each transposed convolutional
layer. The final output is obtained by applying a transposed convolutional layer
with sigmoid activation6 to ensure the output values are between 0 and 1.
3. Variational Autoencoder:
The VariationalAutoencoder class combines the encoder and decoder networks.
It takes an input spectrogram x, passes it through the encoder to obtain the latent
code z, and then passes z through the decoder to reconstruct the spectrogram.

6.1
Audio Generation: Variational Autoencoder
The variational autoencoder is based on a prior pre-trained one used in three published
contributions (Cámara and Blanco 2022a; Cámara and Blanco 2022b). It has a five-depth
layer symmetric encoder and decoder. In all cases, (2,2) strides were used to reduce the
computational cost, except for the encoder’s last layer and the decoder’s first layer. It
uses leaky-ReLU activation functions, with a drop of 0.1. Batch normalization is applied
at all layers to prevent the problem of gradient fading or explosion. In the last stage of
the autoencoder, a sigmoid activation function reconstructs the spectrogram.
To put the VAE to work, the first task is to pre-process WAV files and convert them into
spectrograms, which will serve as inputs. Figure 6.1 shows an example of the spectrogram
obtained from a dog barking sound in our database. As can be seen, the warmer the
colors, the greater the power and the presence of sound in the audio signal.
Figure 6.1: Three-second Spectrogram Representing a Dog Sound

• Hop length = 512
• Sample rate = 48 KHz

The ability of the VAE to reconstruct the signal from latent space depends largely on the
quality of the learned representations and the effectiveness of the training process. If the
VAE has learned meaningful and informative latent representations during training, the
process of sampling from latent space and subsequent decoding should result in accurate
reconstructions of the original signals.
A well-trained VAE should have a smooth and continuous latent space where similar
data points are close together. This ensures that when random samples are taken from
the latent space, they correspond to meaningful data points that can be decoded into
coherent signals. However, since the dog sounds in our database are diverse, and a larger
number of samples to enrich the training was not available, some reconstructions, such as
the one in Figure 6.3, can be further improved.
Another important factor in VAE reconstruction is the effectiveness of the decoder in
converting latent space samples back to the original data space. If the decoder is powerful
and well-trained, it should be able to accurately reconstruct the signals from the latent
space samples.
The choice of hyperparameters greatly influences the results of the reconstruction. Train-

Audio Generation: Zero-shot Sonorizing Pipeline
Recent advances in the field have revealed the existence of more robust systems that do
not require training from scratch. Given the significant computational and time resources
required to train a model from scratch, which were unfortunately unavailable for this
project, alternative methods were investigated.
This allowed the project to use pre-trained models or transfer learning techniques, saving
significant computational time and resources. By building on existing models, the project
was able to focus more on adapting the models to the specific task at hand, resulting
in faster development and potentially comparable performance with the added benefit of
reduced training times.
6.2.1

Generating a single prompt from an image highlighted the need to generate more captions
per image to increase the variability of the text samples. This variability is essential in
order to find more coherent audios that fit well with the corresponding image.
At the end, 600 captions are generated from the 600 images in this dataset by passing
them through this single-prompt pipeline. As a result, 600 sounds are generated.

6.2.2
Multi-prompt Audio Generation
The generation of audio from multiple prompts revealed that the captioning of the image
could accurately describe its visual content, but not its acoustic content.
This “problem” of image captioning, which focuses mainly on describing the visual content
but neglects the acoustic content, has been addressed by databases such as AudioCaps. In
AudioCaps [C. D. Kim et al. 2019], the captions are designed to include both the visual and
acoustic aspects of the corresponding images, providing a more complete and informative
description of the scenes.
A total of 18068 images of the high-resolution dataset are divided into eleven scenes (as
mentioned in Annex B.3). To avoid redundancy, starting from the image selected as the
reference image, only the 10 images before and 10 images after the reference image are
selected, for a total of 21 images per scene. As there are eleven scenes, the total number
of images available for this part of the research is 231. Running these images through
the multi-prompt pipeline results in a total of 4620 captions, and thus a total of 4620
generated audios.
6.3
Audio Generation Evaluation Metrics
This section presents and discusses the results of the evaluation of the metrics.
6.3.1
FAD
The result of comparing the 24 reference audios for a given scene and the 420 automatically
generated audios for that scene is FAD = 50.03.
To interpret the FAD value, it is essential to have a reference point or baseline for com-
parison. Typically, a lower FAD value indicates that the audio embeddings of the two sets
are more similar or closer in their distribution. Conversely, a higher FAD value indicates
that the audio embeddings are less similar or more distant in their distribution. In tasks
where the audio generation model is expected to produce high-quality and realistic audio
samples similar to the reference data, typical FAD values might be in the range of 10 to
30 or lower.
A possible reason for this high value could be that the generated data contains a lot of
noise and other audio artifacts that are not present in the “ground truth” data, resulting
in very different distributions between the ground truth and the generated data.
Another possible reason could be the amount of data compared. According to Appendix
B of the FAD paper [Kilgour et al. 2019], “an ideal amount would be about 5000 audio
clips or around 7 hours. This is a lot of data for evaluation purposes and will often not be
available. While not as stable as larger evaluation set sizes, we begin to get usable results
from about 300 audio clips or 25 minutes of audio”. Therefore, a larger number of audio
files to compare would be appropriate for this analysis of the FAD metric.

Conclusions
This chapter presents the main conclusions and future directions of the project.
7.1
Conclusions
1. We have successfully devised a working pipeline capable of sonorizing short video
sequences. The pipeline integrates the use of pre-trained models from the litera-
ture, which can save time and computational resources in audio generation tasks.
Customization and fine-tuning of these models can lead to improved performance,
making them a valuable approach for eﬀicient and effective results.
2. We have evaluated different variations of this scheme, using multimodal representa-
tions of image-to-audio and image-to-text-to-audio. The schemes produced different
results with respect to the proposed distance metric for measuring the similarity of
audio samples. We successfully validated this metric through informal preliminary
tests tailored to the time constraints of the project. These tests provided valuable in-
sights into the effectiveness of the metric and demonstrated its potential for assessing
audio fidelity in line with the project’s objectives.
3. The current state of the art lacks an available model for users to generate audio from
images. We have successfully integrated state-of-the-art components (published in
2022 and 2023) based on embeddings, which in some cases, produce accurate results.
As a result, this project provides a GitHub repository [Ruiz 2023g] that allows users
to generate sound from their own images, providing a valuable and accessible tool
for audio generation.
4. The proposed embedding distance of audio, image, and text has shown great po-
tential in creating powerful models for audio generation. Leveraging information
from these different modalities allows the development of sophisticated and versatile
audio generation systems capable of producing high-quality and contextually rele-
vant audio representations. The successful integration of embeddings in this project
highlights their importance in advancing the field of audio generation and paves the
way for exciting possibilities for future research and development in this area.
5. The subjective tests conducted in this research project align with the objective met-
rics, which enhances the credibility and robustness of the results. This alignment
further solidifies the significance of this research in the field of audio generation and
its valuable contribution to the advancement of AI-generated audio.
Human opinions of the generated audio, as assessed by MOS, vary as they are
subject to individual interpretation and imagination. This subjectivity highlights
the importance of considering different perspectives and preferences when assessing
the quality of audio generated from images.
Despite the differences in opinion,
the MOS rating remains a valuable tool for understanding user perceptions and
preferences in audio generation tasks.
By recognizing the influence of individual
imagination, audio generation models can be refined and tailored to better match
user expectations and preferences.
6. The audio generation using the intermediate step of captioning allows the auditory
content of images to be taken into account, as they can be described to generate
Zero-shot sonorizing of video sequences

==PAGE:76==
sound. This approach proves beneficial in capturing semantic relationships between
visuals and audio, leading to more contextually relevant and expressive audio rep-
resentations. Using captions as a bridge between images and audio can improve the
audio generation process, creating more engaging and meaningful audio experiences
for users.
7.2
Future Directions and Limitations
1. Currently in progress, the development of a website for automatic sonorization of
short video sequences using the proposed pipeline presents complexity, primarily
due to the integration of resource-intensive AI models like CoCa, AudioLDM, and
VAE, requiring GPU utilization. The team is focused on optimizing performance
and ensuring seamless user interaction while employing the novel metric, ImageBind,
for reliable evaluation. The website aims to advance AI-generated audio and offer
an eﬀicient tool for automatic sonorization.
2. Extend the subjective tests to supplement automatic tests and compare results.
3. Development of a website to evaluate the audio fidelity and coherence with the
input image. This website will provide a user-friendly platform for assessing the
quality of audio generated from images and determining how well it aligns with the
visual content, potentially enabling larger-scale evaluations and gathering diverse
perspectives. However, challenges related to accuracy, privacy, and scalability should
be considered during its development.
4. Training a predictive model using embedding distances to match images with ap-
propriate audio representations shows promise. However, the challenges of ensuring
accuracy and the diversity of datasets need to be addressed.
Nevertheless, this
approach has the potential to improve audiovisual synthesis and create more per-
sonalized and meaningful experiences.
5. The use of databases of subjective measurements will be essential. This approach
can lead to more human-like audio generation from images, creating emotionally
resonant and immersive experiences.
However, challenges in data collection and
integration must be overcome to ensure its effectiveness in optimizing system per-
formance. Despite these hurdles, the incorporation of subjective measures promises
significant advances in the creation of compelling audiovisual content from images.
6. Compare the method of generating audio from images using captions with alternative
approaches that avoid caption-based techniques. Exploring these alternatives will
help us understand their strengths and weaknesses and guide us in developing more
effective and contextually relevant audio generation methods.
7. Extend tests to supplement our analysis.
Particularly to address other types of
sequences and audio, extend the subjective measures, and correlate them to objective
ones. This is critical to complete the proposed metric analysis and continue the work
on automatic sonorization introduced in this Thesis.

B
Detailed Explanations
This annex describes the theoretical background and the main technologies used in this
project.
B.1
Conceptual Framework
B.1.1
What is an embedding?
An embedding is a special representation or code for things like words, images, or sounds.
Imagine you want to describe different animals. Instead of using long sentences or detailed
descriptions, embeddings give you a shorter way to represent each animal. For example,
an embedding might represent a cat as a specific set of numbers. These numbers capture
important features or characteristics of the cat, such as its fur, whiskers, and meowing
sound. The clever thing about embeddings is that similar things, like different cat breeds,
will have similar numbers. So, if you have an embedding for a Siamese cat, it will be closer
to the embedding for another Siamese cat than to an embedding for a dog. This way,
embeddings help us understand relationships and similarities between things in a more
eﬀicient and compact way.
B.1.2
Do all of the embeddings speak the same language?
No. Embeddings are representations of data that are typically created through various
algorithms or models. Each embedding technique may have its own way of understanding
and encoding information.
For example, in natural language processing, word embeddings are often used to repre-
sent words in a numerical form. Various word embedding algorithms, such as Word2Vec
[Church 2017], can encode words based on the context in which they appear in a large
corpus of text. These algorithms consider the surrounding words and their relationships
to capture the meaning of a word.
Similarly, in image processing, image embeddings can be created using techniques such
as convolutional neural networks (CNNs). CNNs analyze the visual features and patterns
within an image to produce a compact representation. Different CNN architectures or pre-
trained models may have different ways of extracting and encoding these visual features.
Therefore, the specific method used to create embeddings, such as the algorithm or model
employed, can significantly influence how information is understood and stored within the
embeddings.
B.1.3
Embedding-based metrics
When discussing embeddings for images, text, and audio, a very useful approach to under-
standing large amounts of information is presented. Consider a scenario where a collection
of images, written words, or sounds needs to be analyzed for similarities or categoriza-
tion. In such cases, embeddings serve as specialized codes that simplify and streamline
the information. They condense essential elements into a more compact and structured
format. For example, an image embedding can encapsulate the primary characteristics of
an image using just a few numerical values. This simplification enables computers to com-
pare images, understand their representation, and identify similarities more effectively.

Similar benefits can be observed in the context of text and audio. The use of embeddings
allows rapid identification of word similarities, document classification, and even sound
matching. As a result, embeddings act as valuable tools that improve the understanding
of large datasets through improved eﬀiciency and organization.
The advantages of embeddings for each data type are as follows:
B.1.3.1
Image Embeddings
1. Similarity Comparison: Representing images as embeddings allows for an effec-
tive comparison of their similarities by measuring the proximity of their embeddings
in the embedding space. Images with similar content or visual characteristics exhibit
embeddings that are closely positioned.
2. Classification: Embeddings can serve as input features for training classifiers in
tasks such as object recognition, scene understanding, or image captioning.
3. Image Retrieval: Embeddings can enable the development of eﬀicient image search
engines, facilitating the retrieval of similar images based on the similarity of their
embeddings.
B.1.3.2
Text Embeddings
1. Semantic Understanding: Embeddings capture the semantic meaning of words,
sentences, or documents, facilitating comparison and measurement of their similar-
ity.
Words with similar meanings exhibit embeddings that are proximate in the
embedding space.
2. Text Classification: Embeddings can be utilized as input features to train classi-
fiers for sentiment analysis, topic classification, or spam detection.
3. Information Retrieval: Embeddings enable eﬀicient search and retrieval of text
documents based on their semantic similarity.
B.1.3.3
Audio Embeddings
1. Speaker Recognition: Embeddings capture unique characteristics of individual
speakers, enabling tasks such as speaker identification or verification.
2. Audio Classification: Embeddings can be employed to classify audio data into
different categories, such as music genres, speech/non-speech, or sound event detec-
tion.
3. Audio Retrieval: By comparing the embeddings of audio clips, systems can be
developed to retrieve similar audio content, supporting applications like music rec-
ommendation or content-based audio search.
B.1.4
What is a spectrogram?
Imagine a song or a sound. This sound is made up of different pitches or tones, and a
spectrogram helps us to see these different pitches visually.
Think of a spectrogram as a picture. On this graph, the horizontal axis represents time and
the vertical axis represents different frequencies or pitches. Moving from left to right on
the graph advances the timeline, showing the evolution of the sound over time. Similarly,
moving up and down the graph indicates transitions between different pitches or frequency
components.
The colors or brightness on the graph represent the strength or intensity of these pitches
at different times. Brighter colors can mean louder or stronger sounds, while darker colors
can mean quieter or weaker sounds.

By analyzing a spectrogram, one can observe how different frequencies evolve over time, al-

B.1.5
SLERP
Spherical Linear Interpolation (SLERP) [Barrera, Hast, and Bengtsson 2004], illustrated
in Figure B.1, is a method used to smoothly transition between two points on a sphere or
a 3D rotational space. Imagine two points on a sphere: p0 and p1. SLERP finds a smooth
path between them.
Figure B.1: SLERP Interpolation
The SLERP formula is:
SLERP(t) =
(sin((1 −t) · θ)
sin(θ)
)
· p0 +
(sin(t · θ)
sin(θ)
)
· p1
(B.1)
Where:
• t represents a parameter that goes from 0 to 1. It determines the position on the
path between p0 and p1. If t is 0, the position is at p0, and if t’ is 1, the position is
at p1.
• θ (theta) represents the angle between p0 and p1 on the sphere.
To calculate SLERP, the sine of ((1-t) * θ) is divided by the sine of θ and then multiplied
by p0. Next, the sine of (t * θ) is divided by the sine of θ and multiplied by p1. Finally,
these two results are added together to give the interpolated p0long the spherical path.
By varying the t parameter from 0 to 1, one can smoothly transition between p0 and p1
on the sphere.
B.1.6
Deep Neural Netwoks
Deep neural networks [Cichy and Kaiser 2019] are powerful tools used in AI to solve
complex problems. Think of them as a network of interconnected virtual brain cells called
artificial neurons that work together to process information and make predictions.
To understand deep neural networks, imagine a task like identifying different objects in
images, such as cats or dogs. A deep neural network is made up of several layers, each

==PAGE:89==
containing many artificial neurons. These neurons are organized in a hierarchical structure,
with information flowing from the input layer to the output layer.
In the early layers, the network learns to recognize basic features, such as edges or simple
shapes.
As information passes through each layer, the network gradually learns more
complex features, such as eyes, ears, or tails. Finally, in the output layer, the network
combines these features to make a prediction, such as whether the image contains a cat
or a dog.
To train a deep neural network, a lot of labeled examples are needed. The network needs
to be shown many images of cats and dogs, along with their corresponding labels, so that
it can learn to make accurate predictions. The network adjusts the connections between
neurons, called weights, based on the differences between its predictions and the correct
answers. Through this iterative process, the network gradually improves its ability to
correctly classify new, unseen images.
Once trained, the deep neural network can be used to make predictions about new, unseen
data. It can quickly process large amounts of information and provide accurate results,
enabling applications such as image recognition, speech recognition, natural language pro-
cessing, image-to-sound synthesis, and more.
B.1.7
Batch Normalization
Batch normalization [Santurkar et al. 2018] is a technique used in deep learning to improve
the training process of neural networks. Think of it as a normalization method for neural
network layers that helps them learn more eﬀiciently and produce better results.
In deep learning, neural networks are trained using batches of data (a subset of the entire
dataset). The idea behind batch normalization is to normalize the activations (the values)
of each layer within these batches during training. This helps to keep the layers of the
neural network in the ‘sweet spot’ of their activation functions, making it easier for them
to learn and adapt to the data.
B.1.8
ReLu Activation
The Rectified Linear Unit (ReLu) activation [Yuanzhi Li and Yang Yuan 2017] is a simple
but powerful activation function used in deep learning. It acts like a “switch” that turns
on when the input is positive and turns off when the input is negative.
The ReLu activation function is computationally eﬀicient and helps neural networks learn
faster during training. It also helps to overcome the vanishing gradient problem that can
hinder the training of very deep networks. Another benefit of ReLu is that it introduces
sparsity into the network, meaning that some neurons remain inactive (output zero) for
certain inputs. This sparsity can lead to more eﬀicient and compact representations within
the neural network.
B.1.9
Pearson Correlation Coeﬀicient
The Pearson correlation coeﬀicient measures the linear relationship between two variables.
The value of the coeﬀicient ranges from -1 to 1, where:
• A coeﬀicient of -1 indicates a perfect negative linear correlation, meaning that as
one variable increases, the other decreases in a perfect linear fashion.
• A coeﬀicient of 0 indicates no linear correlation, i.e. there is no linear relationship
between the variables.
