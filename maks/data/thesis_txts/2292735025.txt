Abstract 
The global societal dependency on maritime transport (MT) is extremely high as our trade needs heavily rely 
on the efficiency of MT, which has thus grown proportionally to the growing traded volumes. The MT sector 
transports 90% of global merchandise and was the fifth largest contributor to air pollution and CO2 emissions 
in 2010. Urgent actions are therefore highly needed. However, the International Maritime Organization 
(IMO), the overarching regulatory organ, highlights that MT is the most environmentally friendly means of 
transportation and claims that many regulative actions have already been taken towards eliminating adverse 
environmental impacts from shipping. The objectives of this thesis are to 1) Identify existing legislative gaps, 
2) Derive recommendations that will help reduce emissions to the atmosphere, thereby improving the overall 
environmental performance of MT, and 3) Contribute to facilitating political decision-making within MT. A 
combination of methods were used to meet these objectives. First, I investigated the international 
framework within MT. Secondly, a Driver-Pressure-State-Impact-Response analysis was performed to reveal 
key environmental and regulatory issues and potential responses that could be taken to address pollution of 
MT. Finally, a preliminary stakeholder analysis (SA) was completed including semi-structured interviews 
intended to include societal perspectives to identify potential responses that may be most socially 
acceptable. It was found that the MT is a highly heterogeneous and complex sector. The analysis of the 
regulatory framework shows that there are currently partially conflicting and overlapping different levels of 
legislation in force and highlights that there is a lack of transparency due to e.g. inaccessibility of 
documentation. Despite of this, it seems clear that the regulation is unambitious as it only covers some 
greenhouse gases (GHGs) and pollutants such as CO2, SOx and NOx and does not regulate highly problematic 
emissions of particulate matter (PM). Via the DPSIR analysis it was found that MT is contributing significantly 
to the deterioration of air quality, in particular in harbour and near-coastal areas, and is therefore responsible 
for significant adverse impacts on both the environment and human health. A key limitation here was 
insufficient monitoring that limited data availability. To overcome this issue, the Monitor, Report, Verify 
(MRV) system should be implemented on an international level as soon as possible and on-board sensors 
should be made mandatory. The SA proved that a vast number of stakeholders exist and that they have 
different interests, roles and perceptions of the environmental concerns related to shipping. Key 
stakeholders were found to have high importance and influential impact included in particular IMO, EU and 
Member States, whereas other stakeholders, e.g. the public, have a potential to become very proactive and 
gain a high level of importance and influence, if they find a path to mobilisation. The size of this sector and 
the vast amount of stakeholders and interests were found and shown to be a key SA limitation that hampers 
valid results. Overall, results from the analyses conducted here indicate that a multifaceted approach is 
needed, which should include 1) better cooperation between the different regulative agencies; 2) a more 
ambitious regulatory framework where market-based measures (MBM) are integrated; and 3) a 
comprehensive use of the stakeholder theory and participation should be implemented in the process of 
prioritising more ambitious regulation on the agenda. Finally and most importantly, the IMO and EU should 
assist nations in planning for enforcement strategies by providing tools such as the MRV system.

Resumé 
Det globale samfunds afhængighed af maritim transport (MT) er ekstrem høj da vores handels-behov i høj 
grad er afhængig af effektiviteten af MT, der er vokset proportionelt med den voksende handels-volumen. 
MT sektoren transporterer 90 % af de globale varer og var den femte største årsag til luftforurening og CO2 
udledninger i 2010. Presserende handlinger er derfor i allerhøjeste grad nødvendige. Dog understreger den 
Internationale Maritime Organisation (IMO), som er den overordnede regulerende enhed, at MT er den mest 
miljøvenlige form for transport og hævder, at mange lovmæssige tiltag allerede er taget for at eliminere 
uønskede miljømæssige indvirkninger fra skibsfart. Formålet med dette speciale er at 1) Identificere 
eksisterende lovmæssige huller, 2) Udlede anbefalinger, der kan afhjælpe yderligere udledninger til 
atmosfæren og derved forbedre den overordnede miljømæssige præstation fra MT sektoren, og 3) Bidrage 
til at muliggøre politisk beslutningstagen i MT sektoren. En kombination af metoder har været brugt til at 
opnå disse formål. Først undersøgte jeg den internationale lovgivning på området. Dernæst benyttede jeg 
Driver-Pressure-State-Impact-Response 
analyse 
til 
at 
blotlægge 
miljømæssige 
og 
lovmæssige 
problemstillinger samt potentielle virkemidler der kunne bruges til at håndtere forurening fra MT sektoren. 
Afslutningsvis udførte jeg en stakeholder analyse (SA), der inkluderede semi-strukturerede interviews med 
henblik på at inkludere sociale aspekter for at identificere potentielle tiltag der om muligt er acceptable for 
samfundet. Resultaterne viste, at den MT sektor besidder en høj grad af heterogenitet og kompleksitet. 
Analysen af lovgivningen har vist at der pt. er delvis modstridende og overlappende lovmæssige niveauer og 
understreger at der er mangel på gennemsigtighed grundet for eksempel utilgængelige dokumenter. På trods 
af det, er det utvetydigt at lovgivningen uambitiøs, da den kun delvist omfatter drivhusgasser og forurenende 
elementer såsom CO2, SOx og NOx og ikke eksempelvis udledning af problematiske partikler. Via DPSIR 
analysen blev det fundet at MT sektoren markant bidrager til forringelse af luftkvaliteten, især i havne og ved 
kystnære områder, hvilket gør MT sektoren ansvarlig for signifikante uønskede indvirkninger på miljøet og 
helbredet. En væsentlig analytisk begrænsning var at ufuldstændig overvågning var årsag til mangelfuld data. 
Dette kan omgås ved at indføre det såkaldte Monitor, Report, Verify (MRV) system på globalt plan hurtigst 
muligt og indbygget sensor burde gøres obligatorisk. SA’en viste, at der er et enormt antal af aktører på 
området, der har forskellige interesser, roller og opfattelse af de miljømæssige problemer i relation til 
skibsfart. Nøgle aktører viste sig at have en høj grad af vigtighed og høj indflydelse, især IMO, EU og 
Medlemslandene, hvor andre, eksempelvis offentligheden, har et potentiale til at blive proaktive og opnå en 
høj grad af vigtighed og indflydelse, hvis mobilisering af dem bliver muliggjort. Størrelsen af sektoren og det 
høje antal af aktører blev identificeret og viste sig at være en vigtig begrænsning i analysen, der fordrejer 
resultaterne. Samlet set, indikerer resultaterne fra analyserne at en nuanceret tilgang er nødvendig, hvilket 
burde inkludere 1) bedre samarbejde mellem de regulerende organer, 2) en integreret og mere ambitiøs 
lovgivning der inkluderer markedsbaserede tiltag, og 3) omfattende brug af aktør teorien burde 
implementeres i processen af prioritering af mere ambitiøs regulering. Afslutningsvist og allervigtigst, burde 
IMO og EU assistere medlemslandene i planlægningen af håndhævelsesstrategier ved at stille værktøjer, 
såsom MRV systemet til rådighed.

1. Introduction and scope 
It is no secret that anthropogenic emissions of air pollutants are contributing to a variety of impacts on the 
environment and human health, among which climate change is perhaps the highest in the public agenda 
(IPCC, 2014). Air pollutants are transboundary in nature, as they can be carried by winds hundreds or 
thousands of kilometres prior to settlement (Ontario Ministry of the Environment, 2005; Hultberg et al., 
2006; Miola et al., 2010). This underlines that air pollution is not only a local issue, but certainly, can be a 
regional and even a global challenge. 
The consequences of these emissions include: 
 
Decline in air quality as well as deterioration of ecosystems and their services (Eyring et al., 2005a; 
IPCC, 2007; Fenger, 2009; Gaffney and Marley, 2009; Miola et al., 2010; IPCC, 2014) 
 
Sea-level rise, which is affecting low lying islands and coastal societies in general and to an extent 
that evacuation of the inhabitants is becoming reality (IPCC, 2007; Climate Institute, 2010a; IPCC, 
2014); 
 
Intensified heavy rainfall, which is often causing flooding and landslides to follow (IPCC, 2007); 
 
Sever long-term droughts, which on a regular basis result in societal collapse and migration due to 
crop failure and starvation as well as illness (IPCC, 2007; 2014). 
Despite these severe impacts, only a fraction of the existing anthropogenic air pollutants have been 
investigated for their impacts (a few hundred out of 3000) (Fenger, 2009). Carbon dioxide (CO2), perhaps the 
most investigated and monitored greenhouse gas (GHG), is the major anthropogenic contributor to global 
warming. However, a variety of many other compounds like NOx and SOx pose a problem (Andersen, 2012). 
In recent times, attempts to identify sources and reduce emissions to the atmosphere have focused on 
sectors like e.g. road transportation and energy production, as these sectors are among the main contributors 
to the decline in air quality and climate change on a local and global scale. Initiatives to reduce emissions 
from these sectors have been undertaken, such as the EURO standardisation in EU for road transport (EC, 
2015b + 2015c) and the Directive 2001/80/EC (EUR-Lex, 2015a) for power plants. This has resulted in a 
significant decline in emissions of some pollutants in Europe (e.g. NOx), but, ecosystems continue being 
affected by the long-term consequences of e.g. acidification and eutrophication.  
Humankind has been sailing the oceans since before explorers like Christopher Columbus crossed the Atlantic 
Ocean in 1492 to discover the New World (Biography.com, 2015a); since before the Polynesians in 1000 AD 
were commuting between their 1000 islands covering an area bigger than Russia (Biography.com, 2015b); 
since before the Viking raids in the northern European region in 800 AD (danmarkshistorien.dk, 2015). Today, 
we are sailing the oceans more than ever, though technological progress has changed the scenery of maritime 
transport (MT) completely and so have the environmental consequences. Especially from the 1970s and 
onwards the evolution of the industry has been substantial, and the growth rate of the global merchant-fleet 
has been high as has the fuel consumption (Eyring et al., 2005a).  
Within MT, however, regulation of emissions of air pollutants is still lax and far less stringent than other 
sectors although all pollutants mentioned above, and more, are major constituents of the emissions from 
combustion in ships. For instance, MT seems to be a somewhat overlooked sector as the CH4 and N2O GHG 
emissions from international shipping is not included in the Kyoto Protocol (EEA, 2000) and according to 
NGOs “[w]e don’t have a regulation” to promote air pollution control technologies (Press-Kristensen, 2015b).  
==PAGE:15==
9 
 
Data concerning the composition of fleet structure, engine use and environmental performance prior to 1970 
is limited. What is known is that shipping is the most environmentally friendly form of transportation in terms 
of emissions per tonne of cargo (European Union Strategy for the Baltic Sea Region (EUSBSR), 2013; Det Blå 
Danmark, 2015). For instance, shipping a tonne of goods from the Port of Melbourne, Australia to the Port 
of Long Beach in the US (12,770km) result in less emitted CO2 than transporting the same goods by truck 
from Dallas to Long Beach (2,307km) in the US (World Shipping Council (WSC), 2015a). Similarly, shipping a 
bottle of wine from France from Europe to New York in the USA equals to one transporting one bottle of 
wine from the American State of California to New York by truck. The environmental performance within the 
MT sector continues to improve, especially due to the new super tankers such as Maersk Line’s Triple-E that 
can carry more than 18,000 twenty-foot equivalent unit (TEU)1 containers (Lachmann Christensen, 2015; 
Parkinson, 2015). 
Not only has the increasing size of ships helped improve the overall environmental performance of MT, but 
technological advances have also contributed to improvements throughout the years. For instance, a 
container ship built in 2008, which has a capacity of 4500 TEU, fuel efficiency has increased by 35% compared 
to a similar ship from 1985 (WSC, 2015a). On a per-mile cargo volume basis, carbon efficiency has increased 
by 75% when comparing a ship from 2007 (12,000 TEU) with a ship from 1976 (1500 TEU). The introduction 
of the concept of slow steaming brought on by the economic crisis in 2008 has also further improved the 
environmental footprint of shipped cargo as a reduction of speed of mere 10% can lead to fuel savings of up 
to 19% (Faber et al., 2012). In addition, slow steaming provides flexibility during voyage and when nearing 
harbour in terms of timing, thereby being able to catch up on delays and avoiding queuing for berthing, 
respectively. 
However, these positive developments and sound characteristics within the MT sector are overshadowed by 
the fact that shipping is to a higher extent still contributing to a number of global issues caused by emissions 
of air pollutants. About 30 years ago, shipping was the cause of around 10-20% of existing marine pollution 
(Drel´, 1988). Since then, this burden, including the burden of air pollutants, has steadily increased in parallel 
with the growth of the sector. More than 20 years later, Miola et al. (2010)  state that MT “is the fifth largest 
contributor to air pollution and carbon emissions” and that there is an “urgent need to take action”. This is 
well illustrated from Figure 1 that depicts the progress of NO2 emissions from 2007 until 2020 in and around 
Denmark. Land-based sources have significantly decreased, however, maritime sources have remained 
almost constant. 
However, only recently has the focus turned towards reducing emissions of air pollutants from MT on an 
international level. The first separate law regarding air pollution from ship emissions did not step into force 
until May 19 2005: the Annex VI of MARPOL of the International Maritime Organisation (IMO) under UN 
(IMO, 2015a).

1.1. 
Problem statement, objectives and approach 
Maritime transport (MT) significantly contributes to global emissions of air pollutants affecting the 
environment and society at many levels. To reduce the emissions of air pollutants on the environment and 
their effects on wellbeing of humans, it is necessary to take steps that drastically reduce or eliminate the use 
of fossil fuels (Hultberg et al., 2006). 
At the moment, MT is undergoing regulatory changes and this is the focus of the current international debate. 
These changes are at present mainly focusing on emissions from exhaust gas. Therefore, the focus of this 
thesis is to investigate how combining technological and regulatory measures can reduce vessel-sourced 
emissions to the atmosphere. Vessel-sourced emissions are here defined as the exhaust gas from the 
combustion process in the use-phase. The focus will be trade-related shipping as this is the major fraction of 
international shipping (shipping between ports of different countries excluding military and fishing vessels as 
stated by IMO (2009; 2014) and in coherence with IPCC 2006 Guidelines).

The purpose of using the DPSIR framework in this case is to perform an environmental assessment of the 
contributions from international shipping on the environment caused by air pollution and to facilitate the 
communication of the result for decision-makers to assist them in framing suitable measures (EEA, 2003). To 
my knowledge, this has not previously been done on a sectorial level within Maritime Transport. 
Further, it draws out the cause-effect dynamics that exist in the field and simplifies the complexity as it can 
be used for multidisciplinary purposes and as it has the capacity to portray interlinked environmental 
concerns with anthropogenic pressures (Tscherning et al., 2012). This makes the DPSIR framework a good 
choice for creating an overview of the environmental issues and it is in this context used to compensate for 
the missing environmental insight in an SA.  
International shipping is a highly complex arena with multiple stakeholders from multiple cultures. In 
addition, legislation extends to international (UN), European (EU) and national levels bringing more 
complexity to the topic. The multiple nationalities with different interests and areas of concern can add a 
challenging process when new initiatives are on request for implementation. This high level of complexity 
and the number of stakeholders involved calls for a greater “infusion of values and hence non-scientific (non-
cognitive) knowledge [to be brought] into the debate.” (Glicken, 2000).  
Towards overcoming this complexity, performing an SA therefore seems to be an obvious choice, as it can 
provide insight and overview of the real complexity of this topic (Glicken, 2000; Lienert, 2015a). This form of 
analysis assists in reaching an understanding of the involved key stakeholders, thereby gaining knowledge on

how to manage them to attain successful implementations of e.g. legislative measures. By utilising the gained 
knowledge and understanding of these stakeholders, successful implementation of the identified Responses 
from the DPSIR framework and RF becomes more possible to achieve. Further, by integrating DPSIR and SA 
in an overall analysis, a holistic insight is attained as they complement each other. This adds a societal and 
environmental coherence of the issues within international shipping. 
1.1.3. Scope limitations 
A wide range of aspects and environmental considerations can be the object of reflection within the field of 
international shipping as a vast number of elements exist. For instance, aspects such as ballast water, 
refrigerants, PAHs from paints, marine litter, land-based port activities as most of the vehicles/machineries 
used run on diesel, and many more (Miola et al., 2010). In addition, vessel-types are abundant, however, 
many of these are only a minute fraction of the world fleet and accounted for elsewhere, e.g. military, fishing 
and yacht vessels. Similarly, domestic and inland shipping is of minor importance on a global scale. All of 
these are interesting objects for further investigations, however, the focus will be limited to vessel-sourced 
exhaust gas from the combustion process from ships during the operational use-phase as this is the object 
of significant debate at the moment. Further, as this area of business involves an immense amount of 
stakeholders, stakeholder analysis should be seen as a preliminary investigation or a pilot project.

2.2. 
The DPSIR framework 
DPSIR refers to Drivers (or Driving forces), Pressures, State, Impacts and Responses (Svarstad et al., 2008). 
The DPSIR framework has been under development for about twenty years before the structure illustrated 
by Figure 3 was adopted (EEA, 2003) and modifications have continued (Waheed et al., 2009; Cooper, 2013). 
It is a cause-effect framework (Svarstad et al., 2008; Tscherning et al., 2012) and from the mid-90s, the EEA 
has integrated the DPSIR framework into the agency’s state of the environment reports and its approach to 
conduct research (EEA, 1995 cited by EEA, 2003).  
The DPSIR framework is an analytical tool that can assist in communicating and structuring the scientific 
knowledge concerning the state of the environment and causal factors in a simplified manner (Svarstad et 
al., 2008). This knowledge is often gathered from environmental indicators, which, according to the EEA 
(2003), “have become indispensable to policy-makers”. Thus, the purpose of using the DPSIR framework is to 
facilitate policy-makers’ understanding of the area by translating scientific knowledge into comprehensible 
information useful for policy-makers. In addition, the purpose is to reveal all aspects of interactions between 
1) anthropogenic activities, 2) environmental impacts caused by these activities, and 3) the societal responses 
to these impacts. This is done by keeping four objectives in focus (EEA, 2003):  
1. To provide sufficient information on environmental concerns so that the severity is realised by policy-
makers 
2. To give aid in policy development and prioritisation of issues from the identification of key factors 
causing environmental issues 
3. To monitor effects and effectiveness of policy responses, and 
4. To raise public awareness 
It has been widely used in the evaluation of environmental issues (Tscherning et al., 2012). 
 
 
Figure 3: Illustration of the DPSIR framework. 
2.2.1. Utilisation of the DPSIR framework 
The utilisation of the DPSIR framework requires some definitions of what is actually meant by “Drivers”, 
“Pressures”, “State”, “Impacts” and “Responses”.  
==PAGE:21==
15 
 
Drivers are normally considered anthropocentric needs such as food, water and shelter (basic survival needs) 
or e.g. the need for economic growth (Svarstad et al., 2008). They portray social, demographic and economic 
development. By using these definitions, the Drivers were identified as being the needs of today’s globalised 
society. This was done by looking at the overall global societal context in connection to international shipping 
and examining the relevant literature from the scientific community. 
The identified needs, Drivers, cause a number of Pressures on the environment, which are normally defined 
as environmental stressors and/or pollutants (Kristensen, 2004; Svarstad et al., 2008). An example could be 
land use for agricultural purposes or emissions of e.g. greenhouse gases (GHGs) to the atmosphere. 
Identifying these was done primarily by investigating the relevant scientific literature within international 
shipping that is focusing on emissions while keeping in mind the scope of this thesis. Especially the IPCC Fifth 
Assessment Report (AR5) (2014) and the Third IMO GHG Study 2014 provided relevant data. In addition, 
communication with different stakeholders also revealed existing Pressures and which of them that are of 
more or less concern. 
The third element of the DPSIR framework is the State. The State depicts the present environmental state 
using the physical, biological and chemical circumstances as parameters in a qualitative and quantitative 
manner (Svarstad et al., 2008). Examples could be change in temperature or gravity, photosynthesis or 
eutrophication, and redox reactions, respectively. In connection to the identification of the Pressures 
through the literature research, the State of the environment, along with the Impacts on the environment, 
was simultaneously revealed through the sources highlighted above.  
Changes in the State due to Pressures causes various Impacts on the environment (Svarstad et al., 2008). 
That is, resource availability, ecosystem services or human health are a few examples that are and could be 
affected. It was chosen to integrate state and impacts as these have similar characteristics in this case. 
The final element of the DPSIR framework is the Responses, which refer to societal responses that are actions 
taken by governmental organs, groups or individuals (Svarstad et al., 2008). They are directed at ameliorating, 
impeding, adapting, mitigating the effect of, and/or amending these changes of the State. The recommended 
Responses to these elements are often policy recommendations, but can also be initiatives taken by groups 
and individuals. When identifying these, the main focus was directed at shipping related responses, which 
was accumulated around the Pressures. The actions of the primary (IMO) regulatory organisation was 
reviewed (chapter 4 Regulatory framework (RF)). In addition to the existing international framework, also 
recommended actions from other organisations were in focus. Further, through the stakeholder analysis 
Responses from groups and individuals were also identified. 
2.2.2. Limitations of DPSIR framework 
Several limitations exist in the DPSIR framework that one should be aware of when using it. It has been argued 
that the outcome of the analysis is quite dependent on the analysts’ perception of the field investigated and 
therefore, the outcome can become biased and potential policy options can be marginalised (Svarstad et al., 
2008; Tscherning et al., 2012). DPSIR has also been criticised for having an “implicit hierarchical structure” 
that eliminates those directly affected from taking actions other than towards Impacts (Tscherning et al., 
2012). Moreover, the framework does not indicate how to address the subsequent implementation or 
Responses of these measures and there is a tendency of marginalisation of Drivers and Responses as these 
are most often focused on global and national aspects, thereby overlooking local initiatives. Additional 
claimed overlooks are features such as complex interrelations, multidirectional causalities and social and 
economic concerns. Further, the dynamics of the topical area it evaluates is omitted (Svarstad et al., 2008) 
==PAGE:22==
16 
 
and is based on expert knowledge only, which excludes indigenous knowledge of possible importance 
(Tscherning et al., 2012). This may lead to undesirable or adverse outcomes. 
Towards overcoming these issues, the work conducted in this thesis also tries to integrate social aspects 
through the SA, thereby also finding alternative views and knowledge on the issue to limit a biased 
perspective and marginalisation of issues. This is to better comprehend how to take actions in this process 
to facilitate subsequent implementation. 
2.3. 
Stakeholder analysis (SA) 
The stakeholder concept origins from the corporate world of strategic management and first appeared in a 
memorandum at the SRI International, Inc. in 1963 (Freeman, 2010). It was defined as “those groups without 
whose support the organisation would cease to exist” (cited by Freeman, 2010) to provide management with 
the general perspective that stockholders were the only group that management needed to concentrate their 
attention on. 
A stakeholder analysis (SA) is a decision-support tool that can assist in facilitating the implementation of a 
project, policy, or the like, by involving those that are influenced by or can influence it (Rietbergen-McCracken 
and Narayan, 1998; Schmeer, 1999; Varvasovszky and Brugha, 2000; SSWM, 2015)). “[I]t is never too early or 
too late to” integrate an SA in a project process (World Bank, 1996), however, it is a tool intended to be part 
of a project throughout the planning and implementation phases (Roeder, 2013), and in some cases it is even 
used after project implementation has ended (Varvasovszky and Brugha, 2000).  
By using this tool, scientific knowledge is augmented with nonprofessional knowledge (Glicken, 2000). It 
thereby assists in the performance of the right science with the right participation in the right manner. This 
is to identify resources (human, monetary, technical, etc.) that can be used in the process, to foresee caveats 
and problems, and to find approaches to steer around these or minimise them. When used in this manner, 
it can support project managers to draw up strategies for how to achieve success with project 
implementation (Roeder, 2013) as participation of stakeholders can be paramount to a successful project 
result (Glicken, 2000). 
It is a stepwise procedure, although this is not always clarified in the existing literature. Depending on what 
guidelines one uses, there are more or less steps necessary to conduct an SA. In this thesis, the following 
steps are considered: 
1. Identification of stakeholders 
2. Importance and influence of stakeholders 
3. Interests of stakeholders 
4. Strategic plan for the inclusion of stakeholders. 
The first three steps is the main body of the SA. After investigating stakeholders by using these three steps, 
the fourth step is completed as part of chapter 7 Discussions and recommendations.  
2.3.1. Utilisation of stakeholder theory 
A number of possible approaches on how to conduct a stakeholder analysis (SA) exist (Rietbergen-McCracken 
and Narayan, 1998; Glicken, 2000; Varvasovszky and Brugha, 2000; AcountAbility, 2005; 2011; SSWM, 2015; 
Roeder, 2013, to mention a few).  Rietbergen-McCracken and Narayan (1998), Glicken (2000) and SSWM 
(2015) have a very structured, stepwise approach with focus on the stakeholders, whereas AcountAbility 
(2005) is focused on promoting who is accountable for sustainable development. Varvasovszky and Brugha 
==PAGE:23==
17 
 
(2000) put more attention on the timeframe aspect of doing an SA, whereas Roeder (2013) concentrate on 
laying the basis for managers that are doing SAs. 
In this methodology set-up, several different approaches on how to conduct an SA were combined. The 
starting point was the approach by Schmeer (1999), which was used for the construction of a questionnaire 
and guidelines for interview procedures. Rietbergen-McCracken and Narayan (1998) and SSWM (2015) have 
been used for structuring the analysis procedure where elements of the two have been combined and 
adapted to this specific case. 
On the basis of Schmeer (1999), Annex D, a questionnaire was outlined (see Appendix A). This was adapted 
to the area of international shipping, excluding some questions, rephrasing others and including further 
relevant questions. This questionnaire was then used during all interviews as a base for conducting semi-
structured interviews. After the first interview, reflections were made and two additional questions were 
added (Question 3 and 4, see Appendix A). To increase coherency, the interviewee of the first interview was 
then contacted by e-mail and asked to answer questions 3 and 4. The results were then analysed and 
categorised by using the identified steps (see subsequent subchapters). In total, three interviews were 
conducted. 
Further, for preparation prior to and during each interview, selected guidelines, presented in Schmeer (1999), 
were followed. Firstly, a minimum of secondary literature on the stakeholders that were subject for an 
interview was review; secondly, appointments with the stakeholders were arranged well in advance; and 
thirdly, if clarification was needed a question was asked maximum twice before moving on to the next 
question, except for question 7 as this was a rather complex question and key to the analysis. Therefore, 
extra time was used and further elaboration made. 
2.3.1.1. 
Definition of a stakeholder 
To start the stepwise procedure, it is necessary first to define what a stakeholder is. This is to attain precise 
demarcation of and distinction between stakeholders and non-stakeholders, as the definition used will 
determine who the stakeholders are. This is important in the process of identifying stakeholders.  
The definition of a stakeholder is a relative term, which implies that it is context-specific (i.e. e.g. time, place 
and project dependent) (World Bank, 1996; Glicken, 2000). Therefore, the stakeholders influenced by or that 
have the capacity to influence a project or policy amendment e.g., can change during the ongoing planning 
and implementation phases. Consequently, an SA is an iterative process as it is necessary to revisit the 
procedural steps of the tool (World Bank, 2015). 
As the literature of stakeholder theory is substantial, many definitions of what a stakeholder is exist. In 1997, 
ICRA proposed the definition that “[s]takeholders are all those people who have a stake (or share) in a 
particular issue or system. Stakeholders can be groups of people, organisations, institutions and sometimes 
even individuals.” If one has “a stake” in an issue, it is assumed that one has an interest in and is potentially 
influenced by the issue. However, those who can potentially influence the “particular issue or system”, but 
do not have a stake in it are not included in this definition. 
According to Engi and Glicken (1995), “[a] stakeholder is an individual or group influenced by — and with an 
ability to significantly impact (either directly or indirectly) — the topical area of interest.” This definition 
include many stakeholders, however, the stakeholders that do not have the “ability to significantly impact” 
will be excluded. Rietbergen-McCracken and Narayan (1998) propose two definitions. These are: 
==PAGE:24==
18 
 
1. “[T]hose affected by the outcome—negatively or positively—or those who can affect the outcome of a 
proposed intervention” and 
2. “Stakeholders are people, groups, or institutions which are likely to be affected by a proposed 
intervention (either negatively or positively), or those which can affect the outcome of the intervention.” 
The first definition is very broad on the inclusion of stakeholders as “those” include everyone, e.g. individuals, 
groups, companies, governments, however by using the word “outcome” the process leading to the outcome 
is somewhat neglected, thereby excluding stakeholders that could be affected during the process. However, 
as previously noticed, a stakeholder is a relative term and can change during e.g. a project process. In 
addition, a stakeholder analysis is highly process-related as step 4 (chapter 2.3.1.5 Step 4: How to make a 
strategic plan) is dealing with where during the project process the stakeholders should be included. The 
second definition builds on the first one, although it is more specific in the inclusion and exclusion of 
stakeholders. Furthermore, they both draw a parallel to the definition of Engi and Glicken (1995), as they 
include those who can affect (influence) and be affected (influenced). 
“Actors (persons or organizations) who have a vested interest in the policy that is being promoted are 
considered stakeholders in the process” is how Schmeer (1999) defines a stakeholder, which, contrary to 
Rietbergen-McCracken and Narayan (1998), puts the process in focus, not the outcome. Moreover, the 
definition is limited to policy-making, which can be both political and organisational. In addition, by replacing 
the word “stakeholder” with “actor”, stakeholders that are influenced by, or could influence the process are 
excluded. The definition limits the inclusion to the persons or organisations that take action, “actors”, and 
have a stake in the process. Overseas Development Administration (ODA, 1995) provides a similar definition, 
which is used by WHO (2014), “[s]takeholders are persons, groups or institutions with interests in a project or 
programme”, where “interest” is the common denominator. 
The World Bank (2015) defines the following: “A stakeholder is any entity with a declared or conceivable 
interest or stake in a policy concern. […] Stakeholders can be of any form, size and capacity. They can be 
individuals, organizations, or unorganized groups.” This draws parallels to the definitions of both ICRA (1997), 
Schmeer (1999) and ODA (1995), but as ICRA (1997) it does not include those who can potentially influence 
the “policy concern” without having a stake. 
In this thesis, the final choice fell on the definition proposed by Schmeer (1999), however, the term 
“stakeholder” will be used. 
2.3.1.2. 
Step 1: How to identify stakeholders 
When assessing if someone classify as a stakeholder, it is important to carefully consider the following 
questions (adapted from Rietbergen-McCracken and Narayan (1998) and SSWM (2015)): 
 
Who are the people/groups/institutions/organisations that are interested in the intended 
project outcome? What is their role (polluter, regulator, direct consumer, indirect consumer, 
etc.)? 
 
Who are the potential beneficiaries of the project outcome? 
 
Who may be adversely impacted by the project outcome? 
 
Who may have an impact on the initiative, either positively or negatively? 
 
Have supporters and opponents been identified? 
 
Have vulnerable groups been identified, if any exist? 
 
What are the relationships among the existing stakeholders? 
 
Are there any new stakeholders that are likely to emerge due to the project?

With respect to this thesis, the point of departure was to initiate the SA with a brainstorm on the basis of my 
inherent knowledge on the topic. In this process, some key stakeholders were identified such as shipping 
companies, clean-tech companies, NGOs and so on. The result of this brainstorm was then used to start 
investigating secondary sources such as relevant published research articles, reports and news magazines to 
identify and map out stakeholders and categorise them to the extent possible. This was done by using 
keywords such as: maritime, shipping, international, emissions, pollution, etc. Throughout the development 
of this thesis, on a weekly and monthly basis maritime magazines and online news portals (such as the Danish 
Maritime Magazine, SustainableShipping and ShippingWatch) and newspapers (such as Ingeniøren), were 
accessed to attain information on key stakeholders within the field. In addition, the archives of these sources 
were investigated, however, some of these require subscriptions and therefore information availability is 
limited. Subsequently, all this information was used to contact selected stakeholders and conduct interviews 
on the basis of the questionnaire developed for this purpose (see Appendix A for questionnaire). 
A vast number of stakeholders exist as international shipping implies an international arena exists. For 
simplicity, these have been aggregated into groups of somewhat homogenous stakeholders. Therefore, not 
all stakeholders were interviewed. In addition, it has been difficult to establish two-way communication with 
stakeholders, as many do not respond to e-mails even after communication via telephone. Further, the point 
of departure has been to identify key stakeholders, which are considered representatives for each of these 
categorised groups, e.g. shipping companies, educational institutions, Member States. Those that to a 
different extent participate in the public debate are included in the analysis. It is assumed that stakeholders 
that do not participate in the debate are neutral in the matter of policy change. While the latter is certainly 
a questionable assumption, lack of information on the opinion of these stakeholders made it difficult to 
include them in the analysis. Therefore, the abovementioned criteria have been applied to select key 
stakeholders. “Key stakeholders are those who can significantly influence, or are important to the success of 
the project” (ODA, 1995). 
In addition, some aggregated groups were divided into sub-groups. This was done where a group 
differentiate such as business companies that have different areas of expertise. After the completion of these 
groupings, stakeholders were then categorised as primary, secondary and tertiary (Lienert, 2015a): 
 
Primary stakeholders are direct beneficiaries or detrimentally affected groups such as end-users, 
consumers, business companies or local citizens. 
 
Secondary stakeholders are intermediary stakeholders that advice and assist primary stakeholders 
during the project. These can be consultants, NGOs, governmental offices or experts. 
 
Tertiary stakeholders are external to the project. Decision- and policymakers such as governmental 
bodies, politicians, and district level bodies belong to this category.

After having identified the key stakeholders and their influential relations it is important to provide an 
assessment of what their interests are. This is done with the purpose of having the needs of the stakeholders 
in focus, to gain an understanding of their perception of the intended project, and to direct the project 
according to this understanding and these perceptions, all with one thing in mind: a successful project 
outcome. Interests are revealed by asking the following questions (adapted from Rietbergen-McCracken and

What are the stakeholder’s expectations of the project? 




 
What stakeholder interests conflict with project goals? 


2.3.2. Limitations of SA 
Several limitations exist when doing a stakeholder analysis that one should be aware of. Some of these are 
described here. For instance, an SA is time-consuming and can be expensive in terms of travel costs 
(Rietbergen-McCracken and Narayan, 1998; Lienert, 2015a; 2015d). The latter depends highly on the field of 
interest; if the project takes place in a local environment or in an international arena, such as international 
shipping. Doing an SA single-handedly is not recommendable and further exaggerates these elements as it 
can add to a biased outcome. In this perspective, it would be optimal to be a group of people consisting of 
nuanced and cross-disciplinary individuals when conducting an SA (Schmeer, 1999). In an attempt to 
compensate for this, however, I have included stakeholders into the analysis preparation via questionnaires 
posting questions to stakeholders aimed at revealing what their view on other stakeholders are (see 
Appendix A Questionnaire). However, when interpreting data in the analysis a form of biased judgement is 
again a risk. 
Another key limitation of SA is that there is always a risk of overlooking (important) stakeholders, which can 
result in a distorted impression of the situation (Lienert, 2015a). Often, groups that are overlooked are the 
ones without resources or outside the “normal” frame of society, i.e. women, poor people, homeless, etc. 
(Rietbergen-McCracken and Narayan, 1998). Overlooking stakeholders can also be interpreted as intentional 
exclusion, where the purpose is to direct the discussion in a specific manner to avoid attention on certain 
stakeholder opinions. 
In addition, the opinion of a stakeholder is subjective. The communication from a stakeholder to an analyst 
is therefore highly subjective, which again can result in a distorted interpretation of the issue. Further, the 
analyst is also at risk of misinterpreting the stakeholders’ opinion (Lienert, 2015b; 2015d). 
Another shortcoming is to miss opportunities; most often, this happens by failing to involve supportive 
stakeholders as these may be able to provide considerable resources in the process of the project 
(Rietbergen-McCracken and Narayan, 1998).

Fuel oils and gaseous fuels are used for propulsion of a ship, however, the former is more commonly used as 
storage conditions are less complex (Andersen, 2012). These consist primarily of hydrocarbons in different

additional fuel consumption and soot emissions is a trade-off. This dilemma is referred to as the “diesel-

4. Regulatory framework (RF) 
According to Psaraftis (2015a), the regulatory framework of international shipping is “a patchwork”. Three 
different levels of regulation exist: 
 
IMO 
 
EU 
 
National legislation 
In addition to these levels, a port can set its own conditions and fees when ships are at berth. On an 
international level, the IMO is the main responsible agency and the overarching of the three (IMO, 2015d). 
The European Union (EU) can draw up supplementary, complementary or even more stringent legislation 
that applies within the EU territory (Psaraftis, 2015b). National legislation schemes also apply, thus adding 
additional complexity to the shipping sector. Especially, North America has been proactive with introducing 
additional legislation. Furthermore, due to the concept of “flag of convenience” (FOC) that de-facto allows 
to sail under a certain nation different from the ship-owner’s nation, understanding such legislative puzzle 
becomes a real challenge. On top of that, additional regulation schemes for inland waterway shipping exists; 
these, however, will not be considered here. Nor will EU, national or port legislative frameworks. Only IMOs 
international regulatory framework is subject to investigation as it is the overarching regulation. 
To bring some clarity to such a complex regulatory framework, this chapter aims at presenting some of the 
key existing pieces of legislation on shipping concerning air emissions related to exhaust gas and at 
establishing connections among them. This is expected to 1) establish a common knowledge base, 2) support 
the development of the DPSIR framework and 3) help identify areas where improvement and/or increased 
transparency and coherency of such framework are particularly needed.  First, an overview of the 
international (IMO) regulatory framework is given, then the decision-making process within the IMO is 
presented. The regulatory framework considered includes the MARPOL Annex VI of the IMO.  
This will further help identify any legislative gap, overlap, contradiction and any area where existing 
legislation is under ambitious. Other parts of regulation within the IMO concerning international shipping 
could be relevant in other environmental matters and will be included where relevant, though not in an 
exhaustive way. 
4.1. 
Overview of the International Maritime Organisation’s Regulatory Framework 
“IMO is the global standard-setting authority for the safety, security and environmental performance of 
international shipping” (IMO, 2015d). It is a specialised agency under the United Nations (UN). It currently 
includes 171 Member States and three Associate Members (IMO, 2015e). More than 98% of the World’s 
merchant shipping tonnage is obliged to comply with the IMO regulatory framework (IMO, 2015f). 
The IMO was established in 1958 (IMO, 2015g), at a time when the most important task was to ensure 
maritime safety, rather than environmental protection (IMO, 2011; IMO, 2015h; 2015i). Prior to that, a 
somewhat unstructured international cooperation already existed (such as the International Convention for 
the Safety of Life at Sea of 1948 (SOLAS) and the International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution of 
the Sea by Oil of 1954), but the establishment of the UN enabled the formation of the IMO. 
Presently, IMO has designed and adopted 53 international treaty instruments (IMO, 2011) and numerous 
protocols and amendments (IMO, 2015g). Currently, the main role of the IMO is to ensure 1) the creation of 
a universal regulatory maritime framework that provides a fair and competitive market and 2) the 
==PAGE:35==
29 
 
development of new conventions and updating existing ones. More recently, the focus area under IMO 
domain has widened to include a range of other aspects, e.g. facilitation of international maritime traffic, 
carriage of dangerous goods and pollution prevention from ships.  
The IMO consists of, among others, a number of committees and sub-committees. The one dealing with any 
issue on prevention and control of pollution from the sector is the Marine Environment Protection 
Committee (MEPC) (IMO, 2015f; 2015j), which was established in 1973 and assembles three times biannually 
(IMO, 2011). Its key task is to design adoptions and amendments of conventions and other regulation. This 
work is assisted by, among others, the Sub-Committee on Pollution Prevention and Response (PPR), which 
reports to the MEPC. In addition, the Joint Group of Experts on the Scientific Aspects of Marine Environmental 
Protection (GESAMP), established by several UN Agencies in 19694 and co-sponsored by IMO, is the IMOs 
scientific advisor in the area of marine environmental protection (GESAMP, 2015). 
The IMO has a main convention regarding pollution from ships, which is the International Convention for the 
Prevention of Pollution from Ships, known as MARPOL 73/78. In addition, seven other conventions relating 
to prevention of marine pollution exist (IMO, 2015k). Moreover, of the 53 treaty instruments so far adopted 
by the IMO, 21 make explicit reference to environmental protection issues (IMO, 2011). 
In 1973, the International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships (MARPOL) was adopted 
(IMO, 2015l), but before it entered into force the Protocol of 1978 was designed due to several serious 
accidents in 1976-77. Therefore, the 1973 MARPOL and the Protocol of 1978 were combined and entered 
into force 2 October 1983 (IMO, 2015m), ten years after the original convention was adopted. At present, 
the MARPOL 73/78 is accepted by 152 Member States, which represents 99.2% of the world’s merchant fleet 
(IMO, 2015n). 
The MARPOL Convention encompass six annexes, which focus on both prevention and mitigation of pollution, 
be it accidental or operational. These are: 
 
Annex I Regulations for the Prevention of Pollution by Oil (entered into force 2 October 1983) 
 
Annex II Regulations for the Control of Pollution by Noxious Liquid Substances in Bulk (entered into 
force 2 October 1983) 
 
Annex III Prevention of Pollution by Harmful Substances Carried by Sea in Packaged Form (entered 
into force 1 July 1992) 
 
Annex IV Prevention of Pollution by Sewage from Ships (entered into force 27 September 2003) 
 
Annex V Prevention of Pollution by Garbage from Ships (entered into force 31 December 1988) 
 
Annex VI Prevention of Air Pollution from Ships (entered into force 19 May 2005) 
Adopting the regulations included in Annex I and II are mandatory for all Member States, whereas annexes 
III, IV, V and VI contain additional, but optional, regulation (IMO, 2015n). Annex VI came about in 1997 when 
an additional protocol was designed to amend the existing Convention (IMO, 2015p). As Annex VI specifically 
deals with air pollution from ships, it is of key importance for this thesis and it is presented hereafter. 
4.1.1. MARPOL Annex VI 
The amendment of 1997 contains the so-called Annex VI, which includes a focus on air pollution from 
international shipping. It entered into force in 2005, eight years later, and has been revised in 2008 with the

4.1.1.3. 
Energy Efficiency Design Index (EEDI) and Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan 
(SEEMP) 
The Energy Efficiency Design Index (EEDI) and the Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan (SEEMP) are part 
of the first mandatory climate change treaty since the Kyoto Protocol and they focus on CO2 emissions (IMO, 
2015æ). According to the former Secretary-General of the IMO, Efthimios E. Mitropoulos 2(2015), the NOx 
Technical Code “was an historical landmark in IMO’s work, leaving the environment as the sole winner in 
intensive negotiations that lasted four and a half years. It has come as a testimony to IMO’s ability to rise to 
the circumstances and deliver a substantial contribution to the world’s efforts to address climate change. I 
hope it sets a precedent to follow.” 
The EEDI is mandatory for all new ships built since 1 January 2013 and it aims at increasing energy efficiency 
on a technical level, thereby focusing on engines and on-board equipment. According to the EEDI, different 
ship types and sizes must comply with different requirements for energy efficiency, which is measured in CO2 
g/capacity mile. Every five years, this level will be adjusted to align with the technological progresses and 
further push stimulation and innovation in the development of cleaner technical solutions. At the moment, 
the fraction of international shipping that overall emit 85% of CO2 is included under the EEDI regulation (IMO, 
2015æ). 
The SEEMP is mandatory for all ships regardless of when they were built (IMO, 2015æ). It has a cost-effective 
angle on operational measures to be taken to increase energy efficiency and provides a monitoring tool, the 
Energy Efficiency Operational Indicator (EEOI), so that shipping companies can follow, optimise and manage 
their ships efficiency performance. This is done through e.g. fuel efficiency measurement or a change in the 
propeller cleaning frequency.

4.1.2. Decision-making process 
It has been claimed that the decision-making process within IMO is too slow and inefficient, therefore, to 
shed a light on this and to complete the overview of the RF and how implementation proceed, it is important 
to look into the decision-making process of the IMO. The IMO consist of an Assembly, a Council (the two 
main bodies), five main committees and a number of sub-committees (IMO, 2015c; 2015h). The main 
committees are listed below: 
 
Maritime Safety Committee (MSC) 
 
Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC) 
 
Legal Committee 
 
Technical Cooperation Committee  
 
Facilitation Committee 
Any of these (Assembly, Council and the five Committees) can act as an organ for the potential development 
of new conventions or amendments of existing conventions (IMO, 2015c). Once a (multinational) convention 
or amendment has been adopted, it usually opens for signature for a specified period for Member States to 
ratify the agreement. If a Member State does not sign within this period, it can become a party to the 
convention/amendment by use of accession. 
The IMO decision-making process towards creating and entering into force a new Convention (or an 
amendment of an existing one) can be somewhat inefficient and very time consuming (IMO, 2015t). As 
mentioned, this was the case for e.g. MARPOL Annex VI, which was adopted in 1997, but did not enter into 
force until 2005 (IMO, 2015a), as stated in the beginning of chapter 4.1.1. 
One factor that typically contribute to significant delays (or even prevents) the entering into force procedure 
of conventions/amendments, is the process of acceptance of the Parties of a convention/amendment. For 
most conventions/amendments, the requirement for them to enter into force was previously that two thirds 
of the Member States of the Parties needed to accept them. This procedure caused prolongations especially 
if the States of the Parties were numerous (IMO, 2015c). To give an example of substantial delay, SOLAS 1960 
had six amendments from 1966-73, of which none received sufficient acceptance to enter into force. 
Eventually, a new convention combining the previous with the amendments was redefined as SOLAS 1974 to 
replace the old one. The same occurred with the MARPOL Convention, for which the Protocol of 1978 finally 
included the parent Convention from 1973, which had not yet entered into force (IMO, 2015a). 
In the early 1970s, towards making such process more straightforward and efficient, a procedural change 
was implemented. By applying the concept of “tacit acceptance”, the procedure has been “reversed”. At 
present, in fact, a convention/amendment enters into force at a specified time unless objections from a 
specified number of Parties have been submitted beforehand (IMO, 2015c). On a general level, the phase for 
an amendment to enter into force has been reduced to a period of 18-24 months, which is a significant 
improvement. As the pace of technological and technical changes are developing rapidly, this has been a 
necessary step to take to keep existing conventions updated and designing new conventions and 
amendments (IMO, 2015c). However, Secretary-General Sekimizu of the IMO points out, that “there are still 
several conventions for which a slow pace of ratification and a lack of implementation are serious causes for 
concern” (IMO, 2015u). In addition, the initial phase of coming to an agreement on issues and drafting 
compromise texts can take years of negotiation (2IMO, 2015). 
The responsibility of enforcement of the RF designed by IMO does not lie within the IMO. Once 
conventions/amendments have entered into force, the individual governments of the Member State Parties 
==PAGE:40==
34 
 
are then responsible for enforcement. In general, it lies upon the flag State (see footnote 2, page 23 for 
definition) to issue penalties for non-compliance occurring in international waters, regardless of who has 
revealed the offence. For non-compliance occurring in national waters, the port State (the country of which 
the port is situated) carries the responsibility for e.g. detaining the ship. 
As an endnote to chapter 4 I draw attention to a statement made by Glicken (2000) on “[t]he participatory 
process [that] must be as carefully managed as the technical side of the risk assessment if the process is to 
succeed in eliciting useful information from the correct people and in getting all necessary parties to agree to 
the outcome.” After going through the regulatory framework on international shipping, it has become clear 
that this seems especially important in connection to the IMO procedure for taking legislative actions.

Drivers (D) 
Drivers are anthropocentric needs (Svarstad et al., 2008). According to Fenger (2009), there are in principle 
three drivers through which humanity puts pressure on the environment: 
 
The population size 
 
Our requirements for material standard of living 
 
The technology used to obtain it 
The drivers identified are all interconnected (see Figure 12). Population growth leads to an increase in the 
demand for commodities (=consumption), which leads to a need for economic growth, international trade 
and technology development. This again leads to consumption. Economic growth further leads to 
globalisation, international trade and technology development, all of which lead back to economic growth. 
Globalisation further leads to more international trade. The need for international trade leads to a demand 
in transport and technology developments, and the former and the latter both lead to globalisation. In 
addition, technology developments lead to economic growth and, to complete the circle, population growth 
became possible due to technology developments.

Concurrently with the rise of the industrial revolution followed also an acceleration in population growth. 
According to theguardian.com (2009), “[p]opulation growth is the driving force behind our wrecking of the 
planet”. Since the 1960s, the world population has almost tripled. In 1950, we were 2.5 billion people 
(Jackson, 2007). Presently, we are more than 7.3 billion people (worldometers, 2015). This is “certainly too 
many” (Fenger, 2009). Over the next 15 years an additional 2.1 billion people will be born adding up to 9.4 
billion (UN, 2015). We are already using resources beyond the capacity of the planet (Ehrlich, 2012) and

Fenger (2009) that the size of the human population is causing an environmental pressure. Moreover, as is 
depicted in Figure 13, an increase in population growth is coupled with an increase in consumerism, which is 
coupled with an increase in trade (EEA, 2013; IMO, 2011; IMO, 2015d; IMO, 2015i). 
The demand for food and consumer goods has therefore been increasing and shipping is now transporting 
everything from food like bananas, frozen meat and grain to fuels like oil and gas, but also our clothes and 
building materials (ITF, 2015).

Another key driver of international shipping is the need to support a growing international trade (see Figure

Since the mid-1800th century, following the industrial revolution and a progressively increasing activity of

From 1985 through 2006, international seaborne trade increased by 135% (IMO, 2011) and is currently 
transporting 90% of merchandise globally (EEA, 2013; IMO, 2011; IMO, 2015d; IMO, 2015i). This indicates 
that, if shipping did not exist, globalisation or trade would only exist to a limited extent. At some point in the 
chain of transport, raw materials are transported by ship and some merchandise can only be transported 
through seaway due to the large amounts and transoceanic destinations (Bilgili et al., 2014). An example of 
this is liquefied natural gas (LNG) due to its special containment (Andersen, 2012). 
Further, the growth rate of trade due to the increasing demand for consumer goods across international 
boundaries has caused and will continue to cause an increase in transportation volumes (Figure 14) (UNCTAD,

cost”. This tendency is reflected in the global resource use, which grew almost by a factor of 10 during the 
last century (see Figure 13) (IGDP, 2012). 
Further, due to our “careless” behaviour (Fenger, 2009) we throw away products that are still in good 
condition simply because we want a new and improved version with added design changes (Leonard, 2007). 
99% of the products that we buy end up in the trash after only 6 months. This consumption behaviour is 
encouraged by advertisement; however, the concept of planned obsolescence, where companies 
intentionally produce merchandise “designed for the dump” also contributes to more consumption (Leonard, 
2007). Depending on interpretation, this corresponds with the third driver of Fenger (2009) and highly 
contributes to an increase in trade leading directly to an increased need for international shipping. 
“Advances in transportation and communications technology have greatly facilitated trade and information 
exchange and have begun to truly interlink the planet.” (Jackson, 2007). The development of the economy 
and societies relies to a high extent on globalisation (Danmarks Statistik, 2015). Cross-national dependency 
is part of globalisation due to cross-boundary investment, labour and capital movement etc. and many 
corporations participate in the so-called value-chain concept. Globalisation of today’s society has become 
possible due to technological advances within transportation in general and the access to fossil fuel (The

transport modes, which has opened up to long-distance commercial activities, e.g. international trading5; in

Globalisation, trade and economic growth have in a self-perpetuating way significantly contributed to job 
creation and wealth (Jackson, 2007). “Continuous trade expansion and access to global markets can help

wealth and the increasing demand for commodities have accelerated job creation.

that “sea transport will continue growing apace with world trade.” (IMO, 2015f). The result of meeting these 
needs as a global society thereby enhance the already existing pressure on the natural environment and 
human health. As economic growth increases, so does total seaborne trading, which results in an increase of 
the world fleet that again contributes to more fuel consumption and thereby more emissions (Eyring et al., 
2005b). These emissions are the result of meeting the presented needs.

N2 and O2 are major components of both the intake air and exhaust gas

the combustion process. It mainly passes through without interaction. O2 
is an indispensable component for the combustion process. Without it, 
there would be no combustion (Colls, 2002). Therefore, what is emitted in 
the exhaust gas is excess O2. In addition, water vapour is a GHG in principle 
(Andersen, 2012). However, it is not considered as such as the 
concentration in the atmosphere is kept at a constant level due to 
precipitation. Hereon after, N2, O2 and water vapour will be disregarded, 
as these are not considered as pollutants, which implies that CO2 is here 
seen as the major component of the exhaust gas. The problematic primary 
compounds from emissions of exhaust gas from ships include CO2, CO, NOx, SOx, hydrocarbons (HCs) and 
particulate matter (PM) (Eyring et al., 2005a). Several of these compounds are ozone precursors (IMO, 2014).

where possible bottom-up estimates are used unless otherwise stated.

anthropogenic emissions (ECLIPSE programme, 2010 found via Laurent, 2015). This may not seem 
remarkable, however, in near-coastal areas levels are above average (Mueller et al., 2011). According to CEC 
(2002) cited by EEB (2004), ship emissions have been estimated to contribute between 20-30% of air 
concentrations of secondary inorganic particles in most coastal areas”. The same applies for harbour areas. 
For instance, emissions of PM due to shipping are in Copenhagen around 4-7% (DK EPA, 2009a).  
5.2.4. Hydrocarbons (HCs) 
The formation of hydrocarbons (HCs) is highly dependent on engine maintenance (Andersen, 2012). Thus, 
poor engine maintenance are likely to cause high emissions of HCs; especially, bad engine timing, and fouled 
air filters contribute significantly to emissions. HCs emitted from exhaust gas are a result of incomplete 
combustion, but also particles of unburnt fuel and lubricating oils are the cause of HCs (Andersen, 2012; 
Kristensen, 2012). They exist in both a gaseous and non-gaseous (liquid or solid) phase (Andersen, 2012). 
==PAGE:49==
43 
 
These comprise a myriad of compound configurations of different size, at minute concentrations, and 
consisting of C, H, O, N and S such as volatile organic compounds (VOCs) (Andersen, 2012; Kristensen, 2012). 
Emissions of VOC come from non-combusted fuel (Kristensen, 2012). The highest emissions occur when the 
engine load is low or when idling (Andersen, 2012), but also a high fuel/air mixture ratio causes HC formation. 
Although this analysis investigates only emissions of exhaust gas from international shipping, it is worth 
mentioning that particularly oil tankers cause a fraction of HC emissions emitted as VOCs, which escape from 
the pressure relief valves on the cargo tanks. 
5.2.5. Nitrogen oxides (NOx) 
The formation of NOx (NO, NO2 and a limited amount of N2O) is highly dependent on the organic nitrogen 
content in the fuel (Andersen, 2012; Kristensen, 2012). HFO can comprise substantial quantities of organic 
nitrogen and thus contribute to formation of NOx during combustion. In addition to engine load, diesel 
engines, which is the most widespread engine type used within international shipping (chapter 3 Maritime 
background), are causing higher NOx formation than e.g. spark ignition. NO is formed in the combustion 
process (90-95% of NOx). NO2 (and the N2O) is formed when passing the last part of the exhaust system 
(Andersen, 2012). Subsequent the emission of NO to the atmosphere additional NO2 is formed by oxidation 
of NO. The NOx emissions burden is estimated to be close to 17 million tonnes per year (Table 6), which is 
equivalent to 13% of global anthropogenic NOx emissions per year (IMO, 2014). However, the multi-year 
average estimate from 2007-12 is 18.6 million tonnes emitted NOx, showing somewhat of a decrease in NOx 
emissions.

5.2.6. Carbon monoxide (CO) 
The main product of incomplete combustion is CO. Generally, the fraction of CO is small, however, it highly

the exhaust gas (Andersen, 2012). The yearly emissions burden of CO from international shipping is estimated 
to be 806,000 tonnes (2012) (IMO, 2014), which corresponds to ≈0.08% of global anthropogenic emissions.

5.3.1. Poor air quality 
Adverse effects on the environment, the climate and human health are a result of poor air quality (EEA, 
2015a). Exposure to air pollutants is occurring both directly and indirectly. Especially coastal areas are

and as it can be seen from Figure 17 there is an indication of the MT being a hot-spot source of air pollution 
along both national and international shipping routes and thereby responsible for a significant contribution 
to air emissions. On a regional scale, both NOx and SOx indirectly play a significant role in the formation of

5.3.2. Poor water and soil quality 
Precipitation becomes acidic when SO2 is converted to sulphuric acid (H2SO4) and can cause serious damage 
to areas with ambient low alkaline conditions such as lakes and forests (Andersen, 2012). Northern 
Scandinavia falls under that category and has experienced groundwater acidification and the destruction of 
aquatic life in fresh water lakes and large areas of forest land. 
For instance, acidification of spruce forests was a serious problem in e.g. Sweden, Norway, Germany and 
Poland among others in the 1970s, where trees were showing signs of ill conditions such as loss of needles 
(Hultberg et al., 2006). The soil profile had become strongly acidic due to precipitation containing converted 
SO2 and NOx compounds stemming from traffic and power plant emissions (oil and coal) (Fenger, 2000; 
Hultberg et al., 2006).  
5.3.3. Poor ecosystems state and services 
Air pollutants cause damage on ecosystems and their services (EEA, 2015a; 2015b).  Both flora and fauna are 
suffering and “local biodiversity loss could be considerable” (EEA, 2015b). Forests are key providers of 
biological diversity and ecosystem services and although the area of forest has grown by an additional 17 
million hectares since 1990 in Europe (EEA, 2015c), along with agricultural vegetation, they provide reduced 
yield (EEA, 2015b). It should be noted, though, that more than half of the expansion of forest area did not 
occur naturally, but is due to planted forest. Some of the ecosystem services a forest provides are absorption 
of carbon from the atmosphere, and water and soil protection. 
Loss of species (both flora and fauna) and habitats continues, which results in a loss of resilience (EEA, 2015b). 
54% of grassland ecosystems in Europe are recognised to be “unfavourable - bad” (EEA, 2014). This is partly 
due to the deposition of airborne nitrogen, which creates favourable conditions for plant species that prefer 
nutrient rich conditions, thereby, outcompeting plant species that favour nutrient-poor habitats. 
==PAGE:54==
48 
 
Oceanic algae blooming is an annual reoccurring event that transpires due to eutrophication caused by 
nutrient overload in the aquatic environment that comes from NOx emissions (DMU, 1999; Miola et al., 2010). 
These reoccurring events are triggered by a change in weather conditions and can consist of toxic algae with 
possible fatal outcomes for the marine fauna and illness to humans, especially children (DMU, 1999). This, in 
turn, affects both the tourism and the fishery sector. It should be noted, though, that the major contributing 
source to nutrient overload is agriculture.  
5.3.4. Increase/decrease in global mean temperature 
The global mean temperature is dependent on the degree of radiation absorbed, converted into heat and re-
emitted by the earth and atmosphere (EEA, 2015e). Radiative forcing (RF) can be both negatively (cooling) 
and positively (warming) affected by air pollutants from shipping exhaust gas (EEA, 2013). For instance, 
sulphate aerosols contribute to a negative RF whereas CO2 emissions contributes to a positive RF. NOx 
contribute indirectly to both. The net RF from international shipping is estimated to be strongly negative (– 
0.409 W/m2 (– 0.737 to – 0.047)) thereby causing a global mean temperature decrease. 
Global warming is perhaps the environmental impacts that has received the greatest attention in the last 2-
3 decades. It is caused by emission of GHGs (mostly CO2) in the atmosphere that trap infrared (IR) radiation 
inside the atmosphere and cause the so-called greenhouse effect. While the so-called natural greenhouse 
effect keeps Earth’s temperature stable and relatively high, it is widely recognised that man-made emissions 
of GHGs are responsible for the increasing temperatures that Earth is experiencing since the industrial 
revolution. This is confirmed – among many other proofs of evidence – by the fact that while Earth’s 
temperature are naturally and cyclically changing over long time periods (e.g. the ice-age cycles), the rate of 
increase experienced especially over the last century is unprecedented and by far higher than ever occurred.  
Since 1979, a receding of about 40% of the Arctic ice has been registered (Roach, 2005), which is further 
boosting the warming mechanism due to the change in surface albedo (i.e. ice cap reflects radiation back to 
the atmosphere a lot more than dark water). There is a widespread scientific consensus that this is largely 
due to human-driven global warming (Granier et al., 2006). On the positive side, sailing routes open up, which 
significantly shortens the sea routes. Instead of having to go southbound through the Panama or the Suez 
Canals when travelling from Europe to eastern Asia it will be possible to save some 7000km travel or one 
third (Roach, 2005; Granier, 2006). This in itself will potentially reduce air emissions. On the negative side, 
local and regional pollution will increase significantly in a pristine area. This will decrease air quality, e.g. 
tropospheric ozone will increase by a factor of 2-3 (Granier et al., 2006) and up to 40% (relative) light 
absorbing carbon aerosol (LAC)16, which may enhance climate feedback mechanisms (Lack et al., 2008). 
Therefore, although the fraction of LAC seems insignificant, it can have a significant impact on the Arctic ice 
melting as atmospheric particles absorb as much as 20-25% of solar radiation (Hänel, 1987 cited by Herrmann 
and Hänel, 1997, p.4053). It should be noted though, that scattering of solar radiation by PM could also cause 
cooling among others. 
In addition to contributing to aerosol warming, atmospheric sulphur aerosols can actually also contribute to 
counterbalance global warming through both direct and indirect negative forcing by inducing an increased 
cloud cover (Andersen, 2012). Clouds contribute to negative radiative forcing by reflecting incoming radiation

5.3.5. Poor human life quality 
Human health is at a local and regional scale directly affected from air emissions from shipping especially 
with respect to emissions of SOx, NOx, CO and PM. These pollutants in fact are involved (individually or in 
combination) in a number of processes that are dangerous for human beings. As previously observed, 
emissions of these compounds are associated with a variety of environmental impacts (Table 7). 
Each year, some 4-500.000 people die prematurely within the EU and millions fall ill (Climate Institute, 2010b; 
Danish Ecological Council, 2013; WHO, 2014; Ågren, 2015) due to air emissions that cause increases in 
mortality and morbidity rates (Swedish NGO Secretariat on Acid Rain, 2005; Climate Institute, 2010b; Miola 
et al., 2010; WHO, 2015a; 2015b). According to WHO (2015c) in 2012, the premature death rate due to air 
pollution has been estimated to be around 7 million people globally. 3.7 million of these deaths are a result 
of ambient17 air pollution (Who, 2014). However, low- and middle-income countries are most severely 
affected with a rate of 88% of these deaths (WHO, 2015d). 
At an EU level, this results in a cost for society estimated to 330-665 billion €/yr. (Danish Ecological Council, 
2013). The air pollutants that are primarily affecting human health are at the moment NO2 (a NOx), O3 and 
PM (EEA, 2015a). Emissions from shipping cause about 50,000 premature deaths in Europe/yr., which is 
mainly caused by PMs, and 10,000 of thousands are experiencing morbidity (Press-Kristensen, 2015b). This 
is a death rate of more than 10% on a European level. 
According to Andersen (2012) “SOX is harmful to humans when inhaled by harming the lung functionality and 
increases the frequency of respiratory infections”. For instance, with respect to SO2, concentrations higher 
than 500 ppb can be fatal to fragile people such as the elderly. At lower concentrations, SO2 can cause an 
increased risk for lung and heart diseases, respiratory problems, chest pain and eye irritation. However, 
harmful levels are rarely reached, at least within EU (Hjorth, 2015a).  Concentrations below 20 ppb are 
nontoxic to healthy people. 
Formation of tropospheric ozone also endangers human health (Morgan et al., 1998; Granier et al., 2006) as 
well as vegetation (Granier et al., 2006). CO and NOx along with unburned hydrocarbons are precursors that 
add to the accumulation of ozone in the troposphere through photosynthesis. The limiting factor of the 
production of tropospheric ozone in remote areas such as the Arctic is the availability of NOx. 
PM is extremely harmful to human health as it can penetrate deep into the lungs and enter the blood veins 
(Mueller et al., 2011; WHO, 2014b; Oeder et al., 2015) and also play a somewhat complex role in connection 
to climate change (Hjorth, 2015a). It is associated with an increased number of cancer incidents, in particular, 
lung cancer, but also cardiovascular and respiratory diseases is caused by PM (WHO, 2014b). Increased 
morbidity and mortality is closely connected to exposure of high concentrations. 
5.3.6. Construction damages 
Not only does acidic precipitation cause environmental problems. It also provokes the degradation of 
buildings, in particular, limestone buildings are at risk as they dissolve (Andersen, 2012). In addition, also 
monuments, especially the ones made of sandstone, suffer from degradation due to acidic precipitation 
caused by SOx emissions (Fenger, 2000).

Responses (R) 
The Responses considered here fall into four overall categories: 
1. The initiatives that have been taken; 
2. The recommendations by stakeholders on how to move forward; 
3. Further initiatives that have been identified via the DPSIR analysis on international shipping and that

5.4.1. Legislative responses of the IMO 
Different legislative measures have been implemented by the IMO on an international level. These are 
focused on the Pressures and concern SOx and NOx. Legislation on emission limits of these components have 
entered into force globally and certain geographical areas, the ECAs (chapter 4.1.1.2 Emission Control Areas), 
are bound to even stricter limitations. See table XX for details on emissions limits and chapter 4.1.1 MARPOL 
Annex VI for more general details. 
Another key issue that has been addressed to some extent by the IMO is the energy efficiency of ships, which 
indirectly influence emissions as the fuel consumption is lowered. These initiatives include the Energy 
Efficiency Design Index (EEDI) and the Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan (SEEMP), which are 
mandatory technical and energy efficiency measures that promote the use of engines and other equipment 
of higher energy efficiency standards and focus on cost-effective operational adjustments to improve the 
energy efficiency and environmental performance, respectively. The attention here is on CO2 and GHG 
emissions, however, all other emissions decrease when fuel consumption is lowered and overall, it is believed 
that these initiatives have improved the environmental performance of ships. 
==PAGE:57==

5.4.2. Recommended responses by different stakeholders 
Several responses have been recommended by different stakeholders over the last couple of years. One of 
the more pronounced measures discussed are Market-based measures (MBMs) (Psaraftis, 2012; 2015b; EC, 
2015f; IMO, 2015z; Press-Kristensen, 2015b). Market-based measures are deemed necessary by some to 
assist the GHG emissions reduction from shipping and some of the IMO Member States have made proposals 
for several alternative approaches that could be taken. For instance, the emissions trading scheme (ETS) has 
been suggested by Norway, United Kingdom and France as a global ETS for MT. In structure, the ETS is similar 
to the European ETS already in place where a specified number of ship emission units are auctioned off 
corresponding to the sector-wide net emissions cap. 
Another highly debated initiative is the so-called Monitor, Report and Verify (MRV) system (ECSA, 2013). The 
purpose of this system is to monitor, report and verify CO2 emissions from ships above 5000 GT calling at 
ports (ECSA, 2014). This is done by the inclusion of three factors namely CO2 emissions, distance sailed and 
cargo-related information. It is at the moment being implemented in EU and it will become operational by 
2018, but the IMO seems somewhat hesitant to implement a MRV system due to the complexity concerning 
cargo-related information. 
One could imagine this system to be broadened to deal with other compounds as well, such as PM, HCs and 
CO, and include not only during port calls, but the complete journey. In this connection, it could be reasonable 
to assume that by installing an on-board sensor in the chimney, similar to that in power plants, it would be 
possible to measure the emissions of the different compounds in the exhaust gas throughout the route and 
transmit the data via the AIS to satellite for data collection. Port State Control (PSC) and remote sensing, or 
sniffers, could then be used to check if the on-board sensor is functional or may have been tampered with. 
5.4.3. External recommended responses 
When it comes to identifying appropriate responses, it is important to realize that the Drivers, State and 
Impacts lie mostly outside the shipping domain. For instance, the responsibility of the issues of population 
growth does not belong to MT. This is a global governmental issue and steps taken towards resolving this 
issue are such as family planning programmes in the developing countries (WHO, 2015e) and the one child 
policy in China (Potts, 2006). Another key example of an issue of global governmental responsibility is to take 
steps towards changing consumer behaviour, which could be done by e.g. different taxation instruments on 
consumer goods where a tax is put on non-environmentally friendly products and activities and a subsidy is 
added to environmentally friendly ones (EEA, 2014b); running campaigns and encourage consumer networks 
to share and exchange instead of throwing out and buying new. An example of this is for instance the 
Facebook group called “Storskrald”; and implementing recycling steps both during production and after end 
use phase. Here, the MT is actually contributing as the IMO has implemented regulatory measures for ship 
recycling through the Hong Kong Convention (IMO, 2015y). 
The State and Impacts of the environment in the framework of this thesis can in a log-term perspective only 
be improved by reducing the Pressures, that is, by dealing with the source of the problem. For instance, the 
air quality can only be improved by reducing emissions of air pollutants. Soil and water quality can of course 
be improved on a short-term perspective by adding lime to the respective ecosystems, however, it would 
remove the symptoms for a moment, but not prevent the problem from reoccurring (Hultberg et al., 2006).

Flag of convenience (FOC) Member States 
Panama, Liberia, the Marshall Islands, Hong Kong and Singapore holds more than half of the world’s gross 
tonnage (GT) in terms of FOC (Table 10), where Panama has the largest fleet in the world (BBC, 2014). 
However, as can be seen from Figure 20, these countries own only a fraction of the ships sailing under their 
flag.  
The role of this group is to provide the shipping industry with benefits such as avoidance of strict regulatory 
frameworks imposed by their respective countries, cheap labour, no or low taxation and open registry (easy 
registration). “Over 3000 boats are registered under the Panama flag, because the registration fees are low 
and the labour laws are very benevolent.” (PanamaShipRegistry.net, 2015). However, this claimed 
benevolence fades when “the country turns a blind eye to its "responsibilities in order to acquire higher 
registration".” (BBC, 2014). 
This group would be adversely affected by a change in regulation that has the purpose to improve 
environmental conditions by setting more stringent limitations, as they would lose part of the advantage 
they hold due to the FOC. However, they are conceived as a group that is “consistently beset by allegations 
of corruption” (BBC, 2014) and take the path of least resistance in terms of procedural obligations (BBC, 
2014). Therefore, they are seen as opponents that would possibly object to any inconvenient tightening of 
regulations thereby negatively affect the policy-making.

The Baltic Sea Region (BSR) and other regions 
“The Baltic Sea States are leading actors in shipping.” (EUSBSR, 2013). It is one of the most heavily trafficked 
regions in the world with more than 100,000 passages through the Kattegat-Skagerrak passage around 
Denmark (Danish Ecological Council, 2011). The cooperation of the BSR crosses eight of the nine countries in 
the Baltic Sea. These are Finland, Sweden, Denmark, Germany, Poland, Lithuania, Latvia and Estonia. Its 
purpose is to reinforce cooperation and promote a more balanced development within the region, as well as 
strengthening EU policies and the implementation of these. 
The Mediterranean Sea Region (MSR) is in many ways similar to the BSR in terms of geographical conditions, 
as it too has an almost confined sea with an opening at the Strait of Gibraltar of only 14km at the most narrow 
point (UNEP, 2015; worldatlas, 2015). It covers all coastal states surrounding the Mediterranean Sea (UNEP, 
2015), including Greece, the biggest fleet in the world in terms of both real nationality and ownership (DWT) 
(UNCTAD, 2014). In 2013, the highest amount of goods (570 million tonnes) transported by sea within the EU 
(EU-28) passed through the Mediterranean Sea (Eurostat, 2015). Short sea shipping of goods between main 
EU-28 ports and ports located in the Mediterranean was 570 million tonnes in 2013. 
This group would benefit from a successful policy change as the environmental performance of International 
shipping would improve, thereby improving the state of the environment in e.g. the BSR (WWF, 2015). 
According to WWF (2015), “[t]he Baltic Sea is one of the most threatened marine ecosystems on the planet”. 
Further, economic benefits would occur since, e.g. Denmark is a nation with a substantial clean-tech 
businesses (EUSBSR, 2013; Det Blå Danmark, 2015) and additional environmental restrictions would create a 
demand for their products. The BSR is seen as a supporter as it “intends to increase the levels 
of environmental sustainability” among others by providing “an integrated framework for improving the 
environmental condition of the sea” (EUSBSR, 2015). 
The United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) developed the Regional Seas Programme in 1974, 
where the MSR, as the first region, proactively adopted an Action Plan in 1975 (UNEP, 2015). Along with the 
==PAGE:63==
57 
 
North American and other regions like the Caribbean Region, East Asian Seas, Eastern Africa Region etc., the 
MSR is assumed to share the opinion of the BSR.  
The Black Alliance 
This stakeholder group consist of Member States that joined forces in an attempt to delay or have more 
stringent policy measures omitted (Østergaard, 2013a), therefore they are seen as opponents towards a 
policy change and would be adversely affected by it. The countries involved are Russia, Greece and Poland 
among others. It should be highlighted that Poland is also included in the BSR and Greece in the MSR, which 
gives an example of stakeholders that are contradictory. 
National authorities in general – an example 
It has been difficult to place this group as it is in principle a tertiary stakeholder. However, a government is 
at the end a Member State. Therefore it was chosen to place this group here. National authorities can be 
such as Ministries of Trade, Commerce, Industry, Environment or the like. Only a few of the proactive 
stakeholders will be presented here due to the demarcating measures taken. In Denmark, the two authorities 
that connects to international shipping in this context are the Danish Ministry of Environment and Food and 
the Danish Ministry of Business and Growth.  
The Danish Environmental Protection Agency (DK EPA) is one of four agencies that belong under the Danish 
Ministry of Environment and Food (DK EPA, 2015). Its role is to create a decision base for the Minister for the 
Environment (DK EPA, 2009a). This is done through projects of monitoring the developing state of the Danish 
environment and collect data and knowledge on environmental impacts. This information is then translated 
into reports and guidelines made publically available to ensure quick and easy access of updated knowledge 
for interested parties. In addition, the DK EPA then implements political decisions taken by the Danish 
government and parliament of which about half are derived from European directives, as the predominant 
element of the DK EPAs’ work is international. 
Further, the DK EPA works closely with environmental centres, municipalities and regions, and has a strong 
connection to Danish businesses, in which they approve market products, and control and supervise 
compliance with regulation. According to the DK EPA (2009a), “Danish enterprises are increasingly being 
affected by the global market, and the environment is a competition parameter in line with other factors.” 
One could assume that this is no different for companies in other countries. The Danish government, i.e. the 
DK EPA, is well known for its work for improving the environment and promotion of green initiatives to 
achieve better environmental performance. 
As there is little transparency on what ministries are the decisive ones in regards to IMO regulation, and as it 
is highly likely that that differs from nation to nation, further, as the Member States comprise of 171 
individuals, it is seen as too complex a group to further include in the analysis and therefore no further 
individual investigation is made on this group. It will implicitly be included under the above groupings.

Shipping companies 
Besides the environment, shipping companies are the primary target for the policy change. Shipping 
companies have a reputation of being in a “dirty” line of business (Lund, 2013), which correlates with the fact 
that they are the polluter in this case. Further allegations of “shipping [being] a conservative and actually too 
conservative line of business” and that “[t]he attitude of the industry is most often that any new regulation is 
by default negative” (former CEO of DNV GL Henrik O. Madsen, cited by Vogdrup-Schmidt, 2015) seems to 
support this to some extent.  
Sub-grouping could be made as there are “companies that are keen to integrate environmental concerns in 
their strategies, companies that are indifferent and finally those that have a negative attitude to 
environmental regulations, which they see mainly as a technical and financial burden” (Hjorth, 2015b). The 
number of shipping companies is endless, however, only a few companies are presented here as examples 
of representation of this group. One of these is Maersk Line, “the world’s leading container shipping 
company” (Maersk Line, 2015b). 
Maersk Line represents the group of “environmentally concerned” shipping companies. The mission of 
Maersk Line is “to maintain a leadership position in container transports” (Maersk Line, 2015c). The company 
goal is to make a return of at least 10% (Brejnebøl Knudsen, 2015). Albeit these very profit-oriented 
declarations, Maersk Line also has a high focus on the environmental impacts it is responsible for and is highly 
proactive on the area. For instance, it set a target of cutting its CO2 emissions by 40% per container (baseline 
= 2007) (Maersk Line 2015b). In 2014, this goal was reached and the company has now set a new target to 
cut emissions by 60% by 2020 (Andersen, 2015; Maersk Line 2015b). Maersk Line would most likely benefit 
from the policy change as they have invested capital in voluntarily measures that other companies have not. 
Should a successful policy change occur, then other companies would be obliged to take similar measures to 
comply with new regulation. Meanwhile, Maersk Line can, however, gain a competitive advantage by 
branding itself as being a “green” company with certain environmentally ethical standards. In the light of 
this, Maersk Line is defined as a supporter of more strict requirements. 
Not all shipping companies have that angle on environmental performance, however, it has not been easy to 
identify specific companies that have a “negative attitude to environmental regulations” and try to avoid 
investments in more sustainable shipping. A general example could possibly be Russian companies, as these 
have been concerned with the forthcoming regulatory measures on e.g. NOx (Østergaard, 2013b). According 
to Press-Kristensen (2015b), many Greek companies are within this category. “Almost all Greek shipping 
companies seem to be stuck in the 18th century” (Press-Kristensen, 2015b). They do not want any 
environmental regulation and are supported by Polish Members of the European Parliament (MEPs), 
therefore one could suspect Polish shipping companies to be in this group as well. Additionally, Chinese 
shipping companies from the Far East come from a region where environmental issues are not taken 
seriously. 
Technology companies/Maritime clean-tech industry 
This group is the key to solving the environmental problems that shipping is causing. They are the 
subcontractors and their role is to come up with solutions. They produce design optimisation of the ship, 
engines, paints ballast water treatment, etc. They are manifold, however, the focus here will be on the clean-
tech companies that design solutions for exhaust emissions improvements such as scrubber companies. 
==PAGE:65==
59 
 
Examples of these are Haldor Topsoe that deal with SCR-systems, Alfa Laval in Scrubber technology and MAN 
Diesel & Turbo who develop engines (Godske, 2013; Lund, 2013; Østergaard, 2013a). 
If a policy change becomes successful, these companies would highly benefit from it as the demand for their 
products would increase, which would lead to an increase of their profit (Østergaard, 2013b). They support 
the policy change, which is seen by large investments in research and innovation for solutions that can live 
up to new requirements (Østergaard, 2013c).  
Oil and refinery companies 
Contrary to the clean-tech companies are the oil and refineries. These will not be focused on, however, it 
should be noted that a more stringent regulatory framework would not be beneficial to this stakeholder 
group, as their technological foundation is the cause of pollution. According to Hjorth (2015a) and supported 
by Press-Kristensen (2015b), “refinery companies might potentially make more money if the use of distilled 
fuels in shipping is extended while they do not have an economical interest in measures aiming at reducing 
fuel consumption”.  
6.1.1.3. 
The public 
The public is divided into two types of citizens, the Local citizens and General citizens, to distinguish between 
those that are more severely impacted over those that feel minor impacts from air pollution from 
international shipping. The Local citizens are identified as people who live in the vicinity of a harbour, which 
is typically in a larger city. They would benefit from a policy change by gaining better air quality. They would 
thereby experience better living conditions as one would expect they would have less sick days, less asthma 
attacks etc. (Press-Kristensen, 2015b). They may not be able to affect the policy change as quite often this 
type of stakeholder is overlooked and overheard. Therefore, they are also considered a vulnerable group. 
However, if they organise and build a cooperation with NGOs, this could very well change. There can be both 
supporters and opponents in this group. Opponents could typically be citizens that potentially could be 
affected on an environmental, social and/or economic level, e.g. by working in companies that would be 
adversely affected by the policy change. 
The General citizens are all other citizens. These are probably more indifferent and less concerned with the 
policy change as it will affect them only indirectly. Therefore, they tend not to notice the origin of the effect 
and they therefore believe they will not be affected, or only experience minor effects.

6.1.2.1. 
Shipowners’ and shipping associations 
The European Community Shipowners’ Association (ECSA) represents the national shipowners’ associations 
in the EU and Norway, which corresponds to 40% of the world fleet GT (ECSA, 2015a). National shipowners’ 
associations represent the shipping companies in the respective countries (Danish Shipowners’ Association,

the nation, they could be either supporters or opponents. ECSA supports a policy change (ECSA, 2015b), 
however, e.g. Intertanko does not (Kristiansen, 2013a). Similar attitude can be found in the UK Chamber of 
Shipping (Grønvald Raun, 2015b), however, the reluctance from both associations are possibly due to 
practicalities than actual resistance towards environmental initiatives. 
6.1.2.2. 
Networking societies 
There seem to be a tendency for creating network societies at the moment. Examples of these are already 
presented in chapter 6.1.1 Primary stakeholders, BSR, MSR and BA. These are “governmental alliances”. The 
networking societies presented here are cross-disciplinary and/or business oriented. Examples are: 
 
Partnerships (Green Ship of the Future (GSF)) 
 
Coalitions (World Shipping Council (WSC), Trident Alliance)  
 
Knowledge-sharing societies (Maritime Development Center of Europe (EMUC), European 
Network of Maritime Clusters (ENMC)) 
These were categorised together as they express similar points of view and have similar procedural 
approaches towards regulatory changes. For instance, Green Ship of the Future is a Danish platform where 
the Danish maritime community can “explore, develop and demonstrate ambitious technical solutions” and 
focus on the progress of more environmentally friendly shipping and shipbuilding simultaneously (GSF, 2015). 
It recognises “the ecological responsibility [that is] nested in the maritime industry”. In addition, they would 
all benefit from a regulatory change. 
WSC is a “coordinated voice” on behalf of the liner shipping industry (WSC), which attempt to solve the “most 
challenging transportation problems” through “actionable solutions” by cooperating with other stakeholders 
(WSC, 2015c). Their members carry about 160 million TEU/year, which corresponds to approximately 90% of 
global liner vessel capacity (WSC, 2015e). The Trident Alliance is a coalition of shipowners and operators 
(Raun and Kristiansen, 2014; Trident Alliance, 2015a). It is a fairly new coalition, founded in July 2014 and is 
focusing on the importance of the enforcement of sulphur regulation and try to ensure a fair competitive 
market (Trident Alliance, 2015b). 
The EMUC is a professional knowledge-sharing society that “proactively” works towards the advancement of 
maritime transport as a business by e.g. supporting maritime research and innovation in Denmark (EMUC, 
2015). ENMC is a similar exchange platform of which EMUC is a member (ENMC, 2015). Hereon after these 
stakeholders will be treated as an integrated network group.

an agency of the EU and an information source (EEA, 2015d). The role of the ACU is to provide “a scientific 
support to the policy-makers in the EU”, where they mainly try to acquire an understanding on the impacts 
of traffic on air pollution, more specifically shipping traffic (Hjorth, 2015b). Their research aim at providing a

The media 
A number of news portals and magazines exist having a focus entirely directed at international shipping, 
therefore part of the media seems to be quite proactive in this area. Bunkerworld, sustainableshipping, 
shippingwatch are examples of the more pronounced media in this area. However, it seems that the mass 
media such as nationwide newspapers are not so active in this area. For instance, a search on Infomedia gave 
seven hits in selected national newspapers (1990-2015). A similar search on EurActiv, the European media 
specialised in news on policy, gave 749 hits out of 29062. For further details on the searches, see Appendix 
D. The media has a special role as it can be a mouthpiece for all stakeholders such as technology companies 
by promoting new technology, the public by helping in getting the attention of politicians, the shipping 
companies by giving announcements of quarterly or yearly revenue. In the same way, stakeholders can use 
the media to draw attention to specific issues of the policy change, both positive and negative. 
The media can be a beneficiary by a change in policy. Their role is to report on the issue. It will give 
opportunities for news reporting on the policy change itself, who will be affected by it, in what way they will 
be affected and how they will deal with the change they are facing. How the media present the featuring 
story can be crucial to how stakeholders will take position on an issue (Hwang et al., 2008). Therefore, they 
can indirectly affect the change in policy both in a positive and negative direction.

6.1.3.1. 
International authorities 
Two main regulatory organisations exist, the International Maritime Organisation (IMO) (described under 
chapter 4 Regulatory framework (RF)) and the European Union (EU), where the former, the IMO, is the 
overarching regulatory organisation within international shipping (IMO, 2015b; 2015c). It has the purpose to 
articulate the regulatory framework for international shipping on all areas such as safety of the crew, 
maintenance of equipment on board and waste disposal for the merchant fleet above 100GT (IMO, 2011). 
The International Maritime Organization 
The IMO could possibly benefit from a change in (its own) policy as the shipping industry including IMO is, as 
mentioned, presently considered a dirty business although they promote themselves as being sustainable 
(IMO, 2011). These changes could alter the general perception in a positive direction. It is a regulator and can 
thereby affect the policy change in both a negative and positive way. For instance, as described in chapter 
4.1.2 Decision-making process, the decision-making process can be comprehensive and slow, thereby 
resulting in nothing. However, the regulating steps taken are helping reductions of emissions. The visions of 
IMO is “to eliminate, or reduce to the barest minimum, all adverse environmental impacts from ships.” (IMO, 
2011), which express a supporting role for policy improvements, and it is doing that by “continuously pursuing 
a pro-active approach to enhance implementation and enforcement, both by flag and port States, including 
a pro-active action plan to ensure that shore-based reception facilities for ship generated waste keep up with 
international regulatory requirements.” (IMO, 2015l). 
The European Union 
The EU is here considered to include the European Commission (EC), the European Parliament (EP) and the 
Ministers Council. They are the regulator within Europe and would benefit from a change in policy as societal 
costs due to air pollution would decrease (see chapter 5.3.5 Poor human life quality). They have a positive 
impact on the issue (Press-Kristensen, 2015b).

7. Discussions and recommendations 
In this chapter the overall results of the analyses will be discussed and recommendations will be provided. 
7.1 Regulatory framework analysis 
The purpose of the regulatory framework analysis was to provide an overview of the international maritime 
regulatory framework and identify gaps to assist decision-makers in identifying key focus areas to further 
lower emissions in general. 
From the analysis, it was found that there is a substantial lack of transparency within the regulatory 
framework of the IMO. The mere fact that documentation is not publically available makes it very difficult to 
thoroughly analyse regulation and adds a risk of ending up with a somewhat incomplete analysis. Further, 
the fact that there are several regulatory frameworks (IMO, EU and national) further adds to the complexity 
of the area. 
It is clear that the decision process in IMO has been extremely slow, but due to the concept of tacit 
acceptance (implemented in the early 1970s) this has improved considerably and conventions, protocols and 
amendments are now entering into force before they become out-dated. In this way, a reduction of 
pollutants can be achieved more rapidly. However, there still seems to be room for improvements as the EU 
is more efficient in implementing new regulation in the area (Hjorth, 2015). Additionally, the concept of flag 
of convenience (FOC), the choice of sailing under a different flag than the owners nation, could possibly have 
a negative influence on the decisions taken. Further, conception and implementation of legislation regulating 
emissions keep being postponed and setting of unambitious reduction targets seems to be reoccurring 
(Østergaard, 2013a; Bartels, 2014a). 
There is definitely room for improvements in relation to the range of pollutants being regulated. The 
regulatory “patchwork” is currently only including CO2, NOx and SOx (and indirectly PM) that are somewhat 
regulated. Therefore, there is a huge regulatory gap on other important pollutants such as CO, PM and HCs, 
which should not be neglected in view of the harmful effect they have on the environment and human health.  
Low-hanging fruits are plentiful and there is a huge potential for international shipping, and shipping in 
general, to become a “true” green mode of transportation in several areas, as technological solutions are 
ready to be implemented. However, regulatory incentives for this to happen seem to be missing or 
inadequate. In addition, another matter exists that should be dealt with urgently: the lack of enforcement of 
current regulation. This creates the base for non-compliance that continues to jeopardise the health of the 
environment, etc. and further, it creates a distorted competitiveness within MT. A restructuring of the 
decision-making process within the IMO, a higher degree of cooperation between key stakeholders, further 
information collection and knowledge-sharing through networking societies and NGO pressure can help alter 
the present situation to a more environmentally favourable one. 
7.2 DPSIR analysis 
The objective of using the DPSIR-analysis was to derive recommendations that help reducing emissions to 
the atmosphere, thereby improving the overall environmental performance of MT. This was done by 
identifying key environmental issues. 
The results of the DPSIR analysis show that it is of paramount importance that shipping emissions are 
mitigated because they are causing adverse environmental effects, including a (minor, at the moment) 
contribution to climate change, acidification and eutrophication, thereby endangering the resilience of 
ecosystems and their services. In particular in connection to ecosystem services, the Arctic Sea is of high 
==PAGE:87==
81 
 
concern. Further, emissions cause a severe risk to human health. Millions of people experience daily adverse 
effects, in particular in harbour cities and near-coastal areas, and a decrease in statistical lifetime expectancy 
is reality. In connection to this, the DPSIR analysis further supports the findings in the regulatory analysis that 
the current regulatory framework is unambitious and does not live up to the urgency of this matter as 
emissions limits are too high and do not sufficiently prevent emissions impacts.  
It has been difficult to find information on the fractions of the primary sources of pollutants in the exhaust 
gas of ships. There is a lack of transparency and monitoring and the only available values are estimated values 
of which most have been estimated through modelling. These were found for both top-down (fuel sales 
based) and bottom-up (activity-based data) approaches. However, the differences were significant and both 
methods are affected by substantial uncertainties (IMO, 2014). Further, definitions of and distinction 
between some of the compounds are unclear and overlapping in several cases (VOCs, HCs, BC). In this 
context, it was especially difficult to find values for PM (PM10, PM2.5 and UFP) as it is not covered by existing 
legislation, again due to a lack of monitoring. Additionally, few values found were somewhat outdated (2010). 
Since then, significant changes of the regulatory framework, such as ECAs (NOx and SOx Emission Control 
Areas) that influence emissions have occurred. Although on a global level this could be of minor importance, 
locally there could be significant changes in emissions concentrations when assuming compliance with these 
regulations. These elements give a clear indication of a monitoring problem both on a global and sectorial 
scale. The EU is currently on the way of implementing the Monitor, Report and Verify (MRV) system for MT 
to CO2 emissions, while implementation is still under discussion in the IMO. One could consider if such a 
system should include all pollutants and be implemented in other sectors to get a more precise depiction of 
emissions from the MT and of global emissions, especially PM. 
The main emissions issue from MT is currently SOx and NOx on both a global and local scale as MT is currently 
responsible for 12% and 13% of global emissions, respectively. On a local scale, PM should be carefully 
considered, as MT is responsible for up to 55% of overall PM emissions in harbour areas. All three compounds 
are highly environmentally and human health damaging. However, the main focus within MT is directed at 
CO2 emissions, which seems quite skewed and somewhat out of proportion as MT is currently “only” 
responsible for ≈3% of global CO2. In fact, besides being a GHG, CO2 does not lead to other remarkable 
adverse effects on the environment or human health (Andersen, 2012). At the moment, other sectors are 
cutting emissions of CO2, thereby reducing their share of global emissions and indirectly increasing the share 
of MT CO2 emissions. Therefore, if MT does not stay at the forefront of reducing its CO2 emissions it will risk 
increasing its share of global CO2. If that happens, the MT may lose its element of being able to promote itself 
as the most environmentally friendly mode of transport. However, the drawback of this is that currently other 
significant pollutants are ignored, disregarded and neglected. 
7.3 Stakeholder analysis (SA) 
The SA has revealed that there are an immense number of stakeholders in this field, which increases the risk 
of overlooking stakeholders in general. In this respect, data collection has been difficult and problematic. This 
preliminary analysis has further shown that the IMO, the EU and the Member States are the most important 
and influential key stakeholders, however, if mobilised both the media, oil and refinery companies and the 
public can become quite important and influential, respectively. Further, there is an “intermediary” group of 
stakeholders such as the shipping companies and the networking societies that are quite important and have 
a potential of becoming more influential than their present status if they continue to be trendsetting and 
proactive. 
Special attention should be brought to Member States and shipping companies for several reasons. These 
being: 
==PAGE:88==
82 
 
1. Member States possess the final regulatory voting power 
2. Both groups are multiple, highly diverse in nature and highly complex 
3. Available information on these groups is limited or not easily accessible 
The group of Member States is encompassed in a shade of complexity. Firstly, there is a lack of transparency 
of the final decision-takers. For instance, it has not been possible to identify who in the Member States 
actually takes the final decision on whether or not to ratify or accede to an amendment etc.; whether it is 
the Ministry of Finances, the Ministry of the Environment, the Ministry of Transport, etc., or several of them 
in cooperation, is not clear. Secondly, the many intertwined cross-groupings make it difficult to see a clear 
demarcation of the subgroups. Thirdly, in connection to the former points made, the Black Alliance has also 
shown to be particularly difficult to analyse. 
A similar trend of complexity is seen in the group of shipping companies. In this case, size matters as it seems 
to be a decisive factor to their participation in the on-going debate. The bigger the company, the higher the 
degree of participation and proactive behaviour, network involvement and possible influence. Maersk is a 
perfect example of this as it is part of the Trident Alliance, the WSC and the GFS, etc. Further, due to FOC and 
the complexity of ownership of both ships and companies it is extremely difficult to identify mediocre or 
small companies and attain information on them.  
Moreover, it also gives a distorted understanding of the actual difference between the “environmentally 
concerned” and the “environmentally uncommitted” shipping companies as – unsurprisingly - there is not 
much info about the environmentally uncommitted ones and stakeholders are reluctant in sharing info about 
them. As a consequence, one could develop the misleading impression that the shipping business is an overall 
green business even though it is not necessarily the case. It also seems reasonable to think that the 
“environmentally uncommitted” companies will never advertise themselves with ”We are black, but cheap” 
as this would most likely be bad for their business.

7. 4. Lack of transparency and data accessibility  
In general, it was experienced during data collection that there is a general lack of transparency within MT 
and information availability has been inconsistent and incomplete. This has specifically been the case when 
introducing elements of the MT in chapter 3 Maritime background where the language used by e.g. 
theguardian.com revealed itself to be complex and overlapping. Further, while researching the regulatory 
framework in chapter 4 Regulatory framework (RF) regulatory framework I discovered that documentation 
of regulatory decisions during IMO MEPC meetings are only available to participants, which hampers detailed 
analysis. In addition, present information on emissions needed to complete the DPSIR analysis in chapter 5 
indicates that there is a widespread lack of monitoring of pollutants. This is mostly for the MT but to some 
extent also global emissions, e.g. other sectors also have information gaps. These gaps contribute to 
uncertainties, which makes it difficult to depict an exact overview of the current situation and impede 
efficient decision-making. Likewise, in chapter 6 Stakeholder analysis (SA), the SA revealed information gaps 
on stakeholders, in particular the group of Member States showed difficult to complete a thorough analysis, 
as it was not transparent who the participants representing the Member States at IMO MEPC meetings were 
and how they were interacting on a broader level. 
7.5 Limitations of RF, DPSIR and SA 
It is always important to be aware of the limitations of the methods used to analysis an environmental issue 
at the science-policy interphase such as emissions from MT. When interpreting the results of the regulatory 
analysis, one has to be alert to the fact that, although it is the overarching within MT, the EU is also highly 
active in this area. Therefore, by not including this part of the regulation in the analysis, there is a risk of 
ending up with a somewhat distorted result, as important regulatory pieces may be missing. The analysis 
would have been more complete if EU regulation had also been included. 
In regard to the DPSIR analysis, one of the main limitations often mentioned in the literature is thatthere is 
no indication of how to address subsequent implementations of Responses, and one can see this limitation 
materialise itself in this analysis in the form of the SA as that was needed to further use the results from the 
DPSIR analysis for implementation measures. Another limitation that revealed itself was that the only place 
that the MT sector was actually responsible for making responses was towards the pressures. Drivers, State 
and Impact was found to lie outside the MT sector. 
Finally, the SA has a high risk of excluding stakeholders, intentionally and unintentionally. By excluding for 
instance the stakeholder group “Others” after step 2 the remaining stakeholders’ interests and concerns 
became dominating. Had the exclusion not been made, the direction of the interests and concerns may have 
been different. Additionally, important pieces of information may have been lost and certain opinions 
eliminated thereby producing a distorted result.  
7.1.1. Additional concerns 
As this analysis has focused on a policy change, it was highly relevant to choose the definition proposed by 
either Schmeer (1999) or the World Bank (2015) as these definitions both refer directly to policy issues. At 
the end the choice fell on the definition proposed by Schmeer (1999) for several reasons: 
1. Schmeer (1999) puts the process in focus, which I find important when doing an SA. 
2. The definition highlights that it is a policy that is being promoted, which is the case in this situation. 
3. Stakeholders that can mobilise to take further actions may influence the project. 
==PAGE:91==
85 
 
Another important point is that the source information is not scientific. Mostly, websites of the individual 
stakeholders and the media has been used and three interviews of different stakeholders have been 
conducted. Therefore, the analysis could depict a different situation than actual reality, as for instance 
companies promote themselves through their websites by highlighting their strengths and positive sides, not 
necessarily their actions; the media can twist a story and take things out of context to make it more 
sensational with the purpose to sell more; stakeholders may not know all aspects and perceive elements 
differently than the actual context. In connection to this, other sources of information could have given a 
somewhat different result. 
In addition, in the literature it is highly recommended not to do an SA singlehandedly as it was done here. It 
can lead to a biased analysis and overlooks that could have been avoided if a group of people, preferably 
with different qualifications and backgrounds, had worked together. Further, being Danish could also add a 
Danish angle of biasness, however, it should be kept in mind that Denmark is a shipping nation. Although, 
attempts were made to be as unbiased as possible, a certain unconscious bias cannot be ruled out. 
7.1.2. Alternative analysis tools 
Alternative approaches to clarify the complexity and interactions of emissions and air pollution issues from 
international shipping have been considered while keeping in mind that “[t]he right tool needs to be used for 
the right purpose”, (Hass & Palm, 2012). However, no matter which tools one choses for analysis purposes, 
one should acknowledge that, “[t]ools are only as good as the people who use them”, (Varvasovsky, 2000). 
Highly relating to the DPSIR framework, other frameworks such as DPSWR and DPSEEA exist, however, in this 
case these did not fit in the same degree as the DPSIR framework does. DPSWR concentrates more on the 
welfare of the human environment, and not the Environment as such (Cooper, 2013), and DPSEEA is more 
appropriate in connection to risk assessment of chemicals as it includes dose-response effects (Waheed et 
al., 2009). The DPSIR framework has therefore been chosen as it includes the overall environmental impacts 
with concluding “Responses” that are needed to further contribute to turning around the setting of 
international shipping from a “black” to a “green” business and secure competitive conditions within it. 
A cost-benefit analysis (CBA) is used to decide if a project will result in an overall surplus of social well-being, 
such as analysing the cost-benefit ratio of changing engine technologies or flue gas cleaning; or when policies 
are (re-)considered. Therefore, a CBA could rather be part of the final recommendations than an actual use 
of tool, as the scope of this thesis deals with making policy recommendations. It is the economics of welfare, 
a way to ensure that public funding is used in an efficient way in major public investments (Pearse, Atkinson 
and Mourato, 2006). Practical decision-making is now recognised as the major appraisal technique for public 
investments and public policy. The essential theoretical foundations of CBA are: benefits are defined as 
increases in human wellbeing (utility) and costs are defined as reductions in human wellbeing. It could 
therefore support/oppose the individual recommendations and aid decision-makers in their choice selection.  
Another consideration has been to do an environmental risk assessment (EnRA). It would have been good to 
have as a background source of information; however, it falls outside the scope of this thesis’ framework. 
Had the point of departure been to determine whether to export consumer goods like e.g. organic orange 
juice from Brazil or Spain to Denmark, tools such as carbon footprint and life cycle assessment (LCA) would 
have been appropriate to use as it was done by e.g. Knudsen et al. (2011). Similarly, the human appropriation 
of net primary product (HANPP) could have been an interesting choice of indicator if the main approaching 
angle was to compare and recommend an alternative fuel usage, such as biofuel, as this indicator draws an 
image of the area size and the intensity of the land used for production (Haberl, Erb and Krausmann, 2013). 
==PAGE:92==
86 
 
However, in this case, the focus is not on the production chain. On the contrary, the point of departure is on 
optimisation and advancement of international shipping with the purpose of making a shift from a “dirty” 
form of transportation to an overall “green” mind-set within the business on all levels, organisational, 
technological and legal. 
Action research was also considered to be an interesting way to approach this topic, however, it was seen as 
necessary to conduct an SA first to figure out who to include in such an initiative. Therefore, this would be a 
good tool to use in connection to further SAs. 
7.2. 
Recommendations 
7.2.1. More transparent and ambitious regulatory framework 
To overcome the decline in air quality and decrease emissions of pollutants further legislative measures 
should be considered at several levels. 
From the regulatory analysis, it is clear that a more transparent and ambitious regulatory framework is 
needed. A first step towards that could be to strengthen the cooperation between the IMO and EU. Second, 
further improvements to the decision-making process within the IMO could be beneficial and speed up the 
process of new regulation. In this connection, it may be relevant to learn from other organisations such as 
the EU as their implementation procedure seems to be faster. In addition, enforcement of existing regulation 
is paramount to the environmental performance of the sector and to good business practise in general. 
Both through the regulatory analysis and the SA it became obvious that Emission Control Areas (ECAs) should 
be expanded and emissions requirements within them should be further restricted. On a short-term basis, 
this should be implemented on a regional level. On a long-term basis, at a global level. 
The regulatory analysis showed that the Energy Efficiency Design Index (EEDI) could be further expanded, for 
instance with an inclusion of energy labelling of ships to apply to all categories (at present only 10 categories 
are included) would further benefit the innovative and technical development within shipping as well as assist 
consumers in choosing a greener form of transportation. Moreover, schemes for forced retirement of ships 
built e.g. before year 2000 and not meeting EEDI requirements should be investigated and, if meaningful 
from an environmental perspective, be implemented. Further, the DPSIR analysis showed that there is a need 
for similar regulatory measures to be taken on emissions of e.g. PM. In connection to this, more regulatory 
initiatives for enhancement of the use of state-of-the-art/best available technologies similar to the EEDI and 
SEEMP should be developed and implemented at a global scale. This will also help making incentives for 
further boosting innovation and technological improvements. 
The SA showed that innovation and technology development could also be further promoted by the use of 
subsidiary measures and taxation, as it is currently done in many sectors, e.g. renewable energy and waste 
management sectors. Further, it is essential to start using market-based measures, thereby shifting the 
pollution burden from a societal to a sectorial one and using the polluter-pays-principle to include 
externalities, which was also expressed by several stakeholders. 
7.2.2. Implementation of requirements of monitoring emissions 
The DPSIR analysis revealed several issues on emissions data such as significant uncertainties and gaps. Since 
2004, it has been a requirement for all ships to carry on board the Automatic Identification System (AIS) for 
all international voyaging of size 300GT or more, and all passenger ships regardless of size. About 40,000 
==PAGE:93==
87 
 
ships have the AIS installed. This system gives static, dynamic and voyage related information such as IMO 
number, length and beam, call sign and name, vessel type; position, time, course and speed over ground, 
heading, rate of turn; draft, possible hazardous cargo, and destination, respectively. As the complexity of 
collecting data on emissions from shipping is so high (Miola et al., 2010) and as a lot of these data are already 
used for emissions calculations, one suggestion to make data collection very simple would be to make the 
requirement of having a similar system for emissions. A system that can easily detect the amount of emissions 
and the composition of these emissions. It could be designed as an expansion of the AIS that includes 
emissions inventory. This would not only be beneficial for research, but also give an easy overview of whether 
or not the ship is complying with the emissions regulation, thereby, facilitating control and enforcement. 
In connection to this, on-board sensors would be ideal. These are already used on power plants to monitor 
emissions and ensure that limit values are obeyed. Further, they can monitor several compounds. Of course, 
measures need to be taken to assure that tampering is not possible. Remote sensors, the so-called sniffers 
or drones, could be used in this context, and also to assist in verification. However, remote sensors are not 
suggested as an alternative to on-board sensors, as the former has a potential to be more inaccurate. 
The Monitor, Report, Verify (MRV) system would be an obvious improvement of data monitoring and 
collection from MT. It would highly help improve the lack of transparency and data within the sector, and, if 
successful implementation was to occur, other sectors may also benefit from implementing such a system. 
Again, here the use of on-board sensors could facilitate data collection if included in the framework. 
The promotion of alternative fuels should be increased, however, the barrier of infrastructure needs to be 
overcome. LNG seems to be a very good alternative to conventional fuel as it has no emissions. The only 
concern is that there is a risk of CH4 slip during refuelling. 
7.2.3. More comprehensive SA and stakeholder involvement 
In this thesis, a preliminary SA was performed that provides an overview of the current relations and positions 
of the involved stakeholders (Varvasovsky, 2000). SA is an iterative process (Varvasovsky, 2000) and it is 
recommended to continue attempts to complete a full SA within the arena of international shipping to 
further map the complexity of stakeholder relations. Specifically, focus of a more comprehensive SA should 
be on 1) analysing the change in positions, if any, 2) to help designing improvement  actions and measures, 
and 3) attempting to achieve the best overall environmental outcome (i.e. beyond climate change) in a 
medium and long-term perspective. 
Therefore, in particular, an additional and more profound SA on stakeholders found to be important and 
influential should be made. However, it would be beneficial to also conduct SAs on the other groupings. In 
connection to this, a tool such as action research could be found useful in the process of attaining and 
collecting data on stakeholders. 
Voluntary measures such as the initiative of the Trident Alliance could and should be further encouraged, 
possibly through additional SAs and with incorporated action research.

8. Conclusion 
This thesis attempted to identify and analyse key legislative gaps and key stakeholders and inconsistencies in 
the MT sector. The overarching goal of this analysis is to derive recommendation that could effectively help 
MT to become more sustainable from an environmental perspective. This included analysis of the key 
regulatory measures that can reduce vessel-sourced emissions to the atmosphere. 
International shipping is responsible for 2.2% of global CO2 emissions, 12% SO2 and 13% NOx emissions and 
it was found that a multi-criteria integrated approach is needed to address this problem. Existing 
legislation/policies/regulation should be revised, tightened and improved with the purpose of lowering 
overall emissions to a level similar to (or lower than)  land-based transportation and inland shipping. Towards 
this goal, a pre-condition seems to be that the IMO and EU strengthen their efforts and achieve a higher 
degree of cooperation on a legislative level to harmonise existing legislation while designing new and more 
effective measures and actions to further reducing emissions from shipping. While emissions reduction 
strategies should set quantitative reduction targets (e.g. overall reduction of GHG emissions), it is also 
extremely important that a broader range of pollutants is regulated, as for instance PM.  Any strategy or 
action aiming at emission reduction, anyway, can be successful only if key stakeholders are involved in the 
decision making process, so that implementations of legislative and policy measures is facilitated and non-
compliances are reduced. 
Unfortunately, there seems to be a reluctance attitude towards reduction of emissions limits even though 
air quality is in decline; climate change is further affecting living conditions, not only for the human race, but 
also for ecosystems in general, e.g. island societies disappearing due to an increase in ocean levels etc. 
Further, non-compliance with current legislation/policies is a reality, as no or little enforcement exists. This 
contributes to a state of distortion of competitive conditions. 
By re-writing and upgrading existing legislation and policies with inclusions of technology improvements, 
thereby not only focusing on fuel content, and lay out a strategy for further improving cooperation between 
stakeholders, these flaws could be reduced. By implementing these initiatives not only will the air quality 
improve significantly, but climate change could be slowed down, ecosystems could recover and ecosystems 
services return to their original state, human health and living standards could be significantly improved and 
societal costs reduced significantly.

Short introduction 
I’m a MSc. Student at DTU Environment and I’m conducting a study in connection to my thesis “Towards 
sustainable shipping: Closing the technological and legislative gaps” to explore the opinions of several 
important stakeholders who have a vested interest in the limitations of emissions of air pollutants from 
international shipping. To improve legislation without disregarding stakeholders’ interests, it is crucial for me 
to obtain your opinion and that of your organisation. 
I plan to conduct about 5 interviews face-to-face, and hopefully around 20 in total by use of e-mail to produce 
a comprehensive stakeholder analysis (SA) that is, at the same time, as objective as possible. The information 
obtained through these interviews will be for the direct use of the SA, and will be presented in the final report 
of my thesis. The interview will last for about 1½-2 hours. 
 
I would now like to ask you some questions about your opinion regarding the field of international shipping 
with a focus on emissions of air pollutants, the relevant connected legislation and the stakeholders that play 
a role. 
 
Air pollution and international shipping: 
1. What do you know about air pollution and the impacts of it on human health and on the 
environment? 
2. What is your perception of emissions and air pollution from international shipping? 
a. Are they significant in your opinion? 
b. Do they cause problems to human health and the environment? 
3. Do you see any opportunities within international shipping to mitigate emissions? 
4. Do you see any barriers within international shipping in connection to the mitigation of emissions? 
The interviewee directly: 
5. What is your organisation/department’s role/business in connection to international shipping?

and contributing to the field of international shipping and emissions of air 
pollutants? 
ii. “Influential” meaning being able to influence the policy-making of shipping
