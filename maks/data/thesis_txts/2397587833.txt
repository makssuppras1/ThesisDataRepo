Executive Summary
The service supply chain has been digitized over the years. E-economic business models have
been created and used to improve collaboration and transactions between supply chain partners.
Along with the digitization the service supply chain becomes more vulnerable. Which increases
risk related occurrences within the chain. Therefore, is the aim of this study to identify the
relevant risk factors for digital supply chain.
An extensive literature search was conducted in the ﬁeld of digital supply chain and service
supply chain management to address the research aim. From the literature a risk framework
was developed to classify risk and cluster risk factors. A survey for digital service companies was
carried out to determine practical aspect of the risk. The literature and survey demonstrated that
digital service companies have to be aware of their employee related risk and data/information
security.
Even though, other risk factors may not be completely ignored.
However, digital
service companies have to identify their risk if they are going to assess, mitigate and/or monitor
it.
The framework can be used as a foundation to further research where the risk factors are
used for mitigation and monitoring. The research can be used to help digital service companies
to explore and identifying their risk factors. It can make the companies consider risk factors
which they would otherwise ignore.

Abstract
Purpose - The service supply chain is becoming more digitized every day. As the service supply
chain is digitized, its vulnerability increases. Those can for example be viruses within systems
and ﬁnancial scams which can lead to decrease in market share, increased cost, ruined reputation
and more. This research explores how companies can identify their risk. Furthermore, if there
are any connection and misalignment related to risk between literature and practice.
Design/methodology/approach - The research is conducted with an extensive literature re-
view and a survey to address the research question and its sub-questions. The literature review
explores the digital service supply chain and service supply chain management. A qualitative
survey is conducted and connected to the literature through within case analysis and cross case
analysis.
Findings - The ﬁndings suggest that digital service companies should focus on operation risk
and supply risk when they identify their risk categories and factors. As human resources and
data/information security are valued most vulnerable of the risk factors explored. However,
most importantly service companies should identify their risk using information search and in-
terviews with experts.
Practical implications - The research can be applied to a company with its risk framework.
By using the framework, companies can discover risk factors which they may have neglected and
decide if the risk factors are relevant for them.
Originality/value - Most risk related researches are conducted where manufacturing compa-
nies are considered along with their risk factors. There is lack of research associated with risk
in digital service supply chains. Therefore, contributes this research to that gap by exploring
the risk factors aﬀecting service supply chain along with proposes a risk framework which can
be applied in practice.
Keywords: service supply chain, digital, risk, risk identiﬁcation, risk assessment, service

Introduction
The traditional supply chain has been digitized by big multinational hub companies in order to
become closer to real-time economy and to minimize manual work with the goal to integrate
information (Korpela, Mikkonen, Hallikas, & Pynn¨onen, 2016). However, these large companies
are not able to design or establish end-to-end information integration (Korpela, Hallikas, &
Dahlberg, 2017) without involvement of small and medium sized enterprises (SMEs)(Korpela et
al., 2016).
New digital business models have been created by e-economy (M¨az¨areanu, 2013). These models
require internal organizational systems which are systems to improve collaboration and trans-
actions between supply chain partners digitally and are then used within digital supply chains
(Xue, Zhang, Ling, & Zhao, 2013). According to Korpela et al. (2017), digital supply chain has
”electronic data exchanged over the internet between business partners and value-added service
providers”. Huddar et al. (2017) adds that digitization leads to improvements in reliability,
agility and eﬀectiveness.
However, increased risk is expected when the supply chain is digitized (Xue et al., 2013;
M¨az¨areanu, 2013). Viruses in mobile operating systems or vehicle on board computers have
increased, as well as spam both via email and on the web, over the years (M¨az¨areanu, 2013).
Increased frequency of phishing scams occurs in banks and ﬁnancial institutions (M¨az¨areanu,
2013). The consequence of ignoring those kind of risk may for example be decreased market
share, raised production cost, companies reputation ruined etc. (Rakheja, Bhushan, & Gupta,
2013).
Existing literature displays a gap regarding supply chain risk management. Researches have
over the years been focused on risk related to traditional manufacturing supply chains and not
as concerned with digital service supply chains. (Ho, Zheng, Yildiz, & Talluri, 2015; Sengupta,
Heiser, & Cook, 2006; Sampson & Spring, 2012; Niranjan & Weaver, 2011; Vilko & Ritala,
2014). Therefore, the research questions (RQ) are:
RQ1: How can service companies identify their risk factors?
RQa: What risk factors are the ones that have biggest consequence for service
companies to be aware of?
RQb: If any, what misalignment is between the literature and the practice?
The research questions will be addressed through extensive review of literature and followed by
survey to service companies which utilize digital supply chains. The survey will be applied and
compared to the literature in order to identify main risk and misalignment.
The article is structured as follows: Chapter two represents the literature where concepts and
theories relevant to the research are acknowledged. Evolution of the framework applied is demon-
strated in chapter three. Chapter four illustrates data collection and validation in the method-
ology part. Findings of the survey is represented in chapter ﬁve and research analysis in chapter
six. Discussion related to the analysis is demonstrated in chapter seven. Finally, section seven
states the conclusion, where challenges and limitations of the research are mentioned along with
suggestion for further research.

2.1
Digital Service Supply Chain
In the last decades the importance of service has increased, as well as value creation within supply
chains has turned from production-based to service-dominant in the global economy (Vilko &
Ritala, 2014; Y. Wang, Wallace, Shen, & Choi, 2015). Contribution of the service sector has
become the largest share to each nation economy (Gupta, 2016). Which is expected as the sector
includes IT, ITES, banking, insurance, tele-communication, hospitality, health-care, education,
professional services (Gupta, 2016; Y. Wang et al., 2015) food industry, product design, mass
customization operation programs and logistic service providers (Y. Wang et al., 2015), tourism
(Qin & Zhang, 2013) and more.
Important factor of supply chain systems is service. As the supply chain is deﬁned to have a
product, origin and consumption, the service supply chains should be divided into two diﬀerent
types. That is service only supply chain (SOSCs) and product service supply chains (PSSCs).
(Y. Wang et al., 2015) The ﬁrst one, SOSCs, has intangible product where the supply chain is
composed of service providers, costumers as well as other supporting units which trade resources
to transform them into support and pure services, and deliver to costumers (Baltacoiglu et al.,
2007). Another deﬁnition of this type of supply chain describes it as a two directed system
which includes a costumer, a service provider and an initial service provider (Sampson, 2000;
Demirkan & Cheng, 2008). On the other hand, PSSCs has both service element and tangible
product as a ﬁnal outcome of the supply chain (Y. Wang et al., 2015). Ellram et al. (2004)
deﬁne PSSCs management as ”the management of information, processes, capacity, service per-
formance, funds, and forward reverse ﬂows of tangible goods from the earliest supplier to the
ultimate end customer, including the return and/or disposal of any tangible goods purchased”.
In this research the focus will be on SOSCs and here after the phrase service supply chain means
SOSCs.
Digital service supply chain is network of service providers that grant end customer some service
(Ghavamipoor & Golpayegant, 2016). Some advantages are related to digital supply chains as
they become more forward looking, connect and relate data sources, generate data-driven plans
through data visualization, improve collaboration and enter the world of digital products and
services (Huddar et al., 2017).
Service literature proposes that the diﬀerence between the service supply chain and manufactur-
ing supply chain can be traced to IHIP attributes, that is intangibility, heterogeneity, insepara-
bility of production and consumption and perishability (Zeithaml, Parasuraman, & Berry, 1985;
Fitzsimmons & Fitzsimmons, 2000; Nijssen, Hillebrand, Vermeulen, & Kemp, 2006). First, in-
tangibility signiﬁes that no physical product is made (Gupta, 2016; Flipo, 1988; Zeithaml &
Bitner, 1996; Gr¨onroos, 2000; Bebko, 2000) and therefore more intangible resources than in
manufacturing ﬁrms (Kianto, Hurmelinna-Laukkanen, & Ritala, 2010). The main resources are
diﬀerent expertise among personnel and decision makers (Nordenﬂycht, 2010; Jansen, Curseu,
Vermeulen, Geurts, & Gibcus, 2011). Secondly, heterogeneity can be detected in diﬀerent ﬁrms,
service personnel, customers and their needs (Edvardsson, Gustafsson, & Roos, 2005). This
implies that every input and output is unique, and hence, diﬀerences in quality, risk, and man-
agement processes (Vilko & Ritala, 2014; Gupta, 2016). Thirdly, inseparability capture that
service, consumption and production happens at the same time (Edvardsson et al., 2005; Gupta,
2016). Which leads to high collaboration among the service provider and customer, to some
extend (Tether & Hipp, 2002; Edvardsson et al., 2005). Lastly, perishability takes into account
that service can not be stored (Vilko & Ritala, 2014) and therefore is time dependent (Zeithaml
et al., 1985; Gr¨onroos, 2000). However, if perspective of customer is acknowledged the service
value can be stored for example as an experience (Edvardsson et al., 2005). The IHIP attributes
all contribute to risk management in service supply chain and make service supply chain more
challenging to manage (Vilko & Ritala, 2014; Gupta, 2016).
In SOSCs, supplier is the key actor in the supply chain and therefore supply chain management
plays a crucial part (Baltacoiglu et al., 2007). However, there are couple of diﬀerent deﬁnitions
related to service supply chain management (Sakhuja, Jain, & Kumar, 2012). They have in
common that service supply chain management includes management of information, processes,

capacity, service performance, and funds from the ﬁrst supplier to the end customer (Ellram
et al., 2004; Baltacoiglu et al., 2007). Those tasks are applied through separate relationships
with customers and suppliers (Cho, Lee, Ahn, & Hwang, 2012). Ellram et al. (2004) categorize
seven tasks in service supply chain processes, involving: information ﬂow, capacity and skills
management, demand management, customer relationship management, supplier relationship
management, service delivery management, and cash ﬂow. Baltacioglu et al. (2007) made fur-
ther developments on Ellram’s model (Vilko & Ritala, 2014). They suggest a service supply

management (SPM), and information and technology management (ITM).
2.2
Supply chain risk management
Couple of risk deﬁnitions exist (Rakheja et al., 2013). According to Lowrance (1980) risk is
deﬁned as negative variation in business outcome variables such as revenues, costs, proﬁts, etc.
Miller (1991) deﬁnes risk as measurement of probability and severity of adverse eﬀects. Yates
and Stones (1992) go further with their deﬁnition and state that risk refers to the variance in
outcomes or performance that cannot be estimated in advance. Holton (2004) adds that risk
only occurs when there is exposure and uncertainty. Therefore, risk is deﬁned as the probability
of incurring losses where the amount of loss must be feasible and uncertainty occurs (D. Wu,
Olson, & Dolgui, 2015; Rohaninejad & Bagherpour, 2013).
To understand what supply chain risk management is, it is necessary to acknowledged risk
in supply chain (Vilko & Ritala, 2014). According Ho et al. (2015) supply chain risk is ”the
likelihood and impact of unexpected macro and/or micro level events or conditions that adversely
inﬂuence any part of a supply chain leading to operational, tactical, or strategic level failures or
irregularities”. Waters (2007) deﬁnes supply chain risk as ”a threat that something might occur
to disrupt normal activities and stop things happening as planned”. Therefore, aims supply chain
risk management to identifying and managing risk in service supply chain including the whole
service supply chain system (Vilko & Ritala, 2014), and collaboration between supply chain
members (Maje & Sunjka, 2014).
Holistic approach is required to state supply chain risk management process (Hallikas, Karvonen,
Pulkkinen, Virolainen, & Tuominen, 2004; Hollstein & Himpel, 2013). These processes include
four steps: risk identiﬁcation, risk assessment, risk management actions/mitigation and risk
monitoring (D. L. Olson & Wu, 2008; Nijssen et al., 2006; Hallikas et al., 2004; Ho et al., 2015).
The supply chain risk management process should be constant and circular, which means that
the enterprise should begin identifying risk factors again when indicators or signals of changes
occur (Hallikas et al., 2004). Figure 2.1 illustrates the supply chain risk management processes
and their connection. In this research, the ﬁrst two steps are only explored.

2.2.1
Risk Identiﬁcation
The ﬁrst step of the supply chain risk management process is risk identiﬁcation (Ho et al.,
2015). It is a foundation phase in the process which includes information search, transmission
and ﬁltering (Hallikas et al., 2004). This phase includes recognizing risk types, factors or both
(Ho et al., 2015), as well as future uncertainties (Hallikas et al., 2004; Tummala & Schoenherr,
2011). By recognizing the risk the decision maker is aware of potential events or circumstances
that might occur (Hallikas et al., 2004). The risk factors can be diagnosed by literature review,
examining historical failures and expert interviews (Ganguly & Guin, 2013).
Reliance on other ﬁrms has to be considered when risk is identiﬁed in a network environment.
That can be done with use of tree-like model, where risk is categorized in groups and causes
is then described as a hierarchy. Here, the special characteristics of the supply chain become
visible on the lower levels of the hierarchy. For the combination of risk assessment and risk
identiﬁcation, the tree model has to be expanded to a checklist of risk factors. (Hallikas et al.,
2004)
Various method exist regarding risk categorization (Khayyam & Herrou, 2017). One approach
involves categorizing risk according to the processes of supply chain (Tummala & Schoenherr,
2011; Yu & Huatuco, n.d.; Khayyam & Herrou, 2017). A second approach considers risk from

2.2.2
Risk Assessment
Over time, a wide variety of qualitative and quantitative risk assessment methods have ap-
peared (Ho et al., 2015; Wehn & Evers, 2015). Each method contains diﬀerent advantages and
disadvantages when compared to one and other (Serpell, Ferrada, Rubio, & Arauzo, 2015). In
order to choose appropriate management action for the identiﬁed risk factors, the risk has to be
prioritized and assessed accordingly (Hallikas et al., 2004; Tummala & Schoenherr, 2011). Risk
assessment proposes the likelihood of an event happens and the consequences of it (Harland,
6
==PAGE:15==
Brenchley, & Walker, 2003). It is essential to rank risk factors due to their impact which helps
the decision maker to know which risk to focus on (Institute), 2004).
Enterprises has to consider more than just ﬁnancial consequences, such as cost, and be aware
of intangible consequences, such as trust, reputation and knowledge. Those are hard to put a
tangible value on but have aﬀect ﬁnancial losses in the long run. (Hallikas et al., 2004)

Demand risk is a risk category which accounts for internal risk within the supply chain but is

of the supply chain (Samvedi et al., 2013; Hajoary, 2015; Ho et al., 2015). Table 3.3 illustrates
diﬀerent risk factors for demand risk.

together along with deﬁnition and references.
According to Wang and Yang (2007), increased number of competitor is an element within this
category. Hallikas et al. (2004) states that risk related to replacement by competitors or if

competitors can oﬀer better service as one element, as do Qin and Zhang (2013) and Rakheja et

Both Rangel et al. (2015) and Tuncel and Alpan (2010) express that high competition in market

short life time of a service may have negative impact on a supply chain. Service level needs also

Finally, the methods of analysis is demonstrated in section 4.6, where within case analysis and
cross case analysis are explored.

Survey analysis
From the survey a ordinal data is collected and analyzed. As the data is ordinal, and therefore
non parametric, mean and standard deviation can not be applied. As well as, any parametric
analysis based on normal distribution. However, it is suitable to use none distribution meth-
ods for example tabulations, frequency and chi-squared statistics. In addition non parametric
procedures, which are formed from rank, median or range, are acceptable. (Allen & Seaman,
2007)

==PAGE:35==
The chosen non parametric method is Kurskal Wallis H test, which was applied for each risk
category to compare the risk factors within and used to compare risk categories to each other.

4.6
Analysis methods
The purpose of this research is to know which risk factor, if any, is of more importance than
another, both from literature and practice.
In addition, to identify diﬀerences between the
literature and practice. The analysis method is applied from Yin (Yin, 2009) and is the approach
in two steps, called within case analysis and cross case analysis.
4.6.1
Within case analysis
Within case analysis furnishes a deep understanding of patterns of each case and how they are
conducted (Saunders, Lewis, & Thornhill, 2009). In this type of analysis unrelated tables are
constructed with data from literature review. The data from the literature review has already
been categories in chapter 3. The within case analysis provides the relevancy of each risk factor
and risk category. The relevancy is measured by using a Survey scale and an Author scale.
The Survey scale is based on results of the survey, where the median of a risk factor is used as
foundation of the scale. The respondents where asked to rank risk categories and risk factors on
a scale from 0 to 5, where 0 represents not important and 5 very important. The detailed rank
can be explored in table 4.2.
The Author scale is based on the framework conducted from the literature review. The scale
depends on how many authors refer to speciﬁc risk factor or risk category. The total number
of authors which the risk factors are conducted from is 28 and therefore the Author scale is
generated from that total number. The scale is formed on a scale from 1-5. Zero is dropped as
it is considered that the authors would not mention an risk factor if it was not important. Rank

5
Kruskal Wallis test
The Kruskal Wallis test is performed for the survey results of risk categories, demand risk
factors, operation risk factors, supply risk factors, information risk factors, ﬁnancial risk factors
and macro risk factors. The test consists of ﬁve steps, which are ranking the data by ignoring
groups, calculating total rank for each group, computing the H statistic, ﬁnd the critical chi-
square value and ﬁnally comparing the H statistic to the critical chi-square value. However,
before perform the tests four assumptions have to be acquired (Deviant, 2010).
1. The dependent variables have to be measured at ordinal scale, which is the scale that the
survey uses.
2. The independent variables should consist of two or more groups. This means that the risk
categories and risk factors should be independent of each other.
3. The data should be collected in independent observations. Each participant of the survey
should be answering unconnected to the others.
4. The data for each group should have the same distribution, that is have the same shape.
Table 5.1 displays the results of the Kruskal Wallis test for each risk category and among the
risk categories.
The tests demonstrate that there is signiﬁcant diﬀerence in relevancy score
between risk factors between risk categories and within supply risk and ﬁnancial risk. As the
H0 hypothesis is rejected for those groups and therefore the data gathered for those groups.
However, the test also illustrates that there is no signiﬁcant statistic diﬀerence in relevancy
score between risk factors within demand risk, operation risk, information risk and macro risk.
As the H0 hypothesis is accepted but it does not mean that the hypothesis is true only that not
enough samples where collected to suspect otherwise. Appendix E illustrates the calculations of
the test between the risk categories and within each risk category.

7
Discussion
The discussion is divided into three main sections.
First, the ﬁndings from the analysis is
discussed. Then, the research connection with theory is explored. Lastly, how the research can
be used in practice is demonstrated.
7.1
Summary of ﬁndings
As mentioned in section 2.2.1, identifying the risk is a crucial part in risk assessment, risk
mitigation and risk monitoring. It is the starting point of every risk evaluation and needs to
be conducted carefully. The risk can be recognized through information search, both companies
historic failures as well as literature, and through interviewing experts in the ﬁeld.
The risk category which most focus should be on according to this research are operation risk
and supply risk. Then demand risk, macro risk and, lastly, ﬁnancial risk along with information
risk. This was expected when the literature had been examine. However, this result has to be
taken with notice as more evidence and data is needed to get deﬁned results.
The analysis has revealed that the risk factors human resources (HR) and data/information
security risk (DSR) score highest of the risk factors with total score of 7. As the null hypothesis
for the Kruskal Wallis for supply risk factors, which HR is a part of, is rejected the score for
Survey scale needs to be taken with notice. However, the results for DSR can be determined as
correct as the Kruskal Wallis reveals that the data can be taken seriously. However, there are
more risk factors which need to be considered.
Couple of misalignment are revealed between the literature and practice. Most frequent diﬀer-
ence between scales is 2, as 22 risk factors get it. Misalignment of 1 is with 11 risk factors and
diﬀerence of 3 get 5 risk factors. Misalignment of 4 has the least frequency of misalignment and
is also the highest diﬀerence. The risk factors, reputation risk (RR) and technology connections
(TC), have a diﬀerence in scale of 4 and belong to the demand risk category. One reason for this
could be that the world is frequently changing and it is possible that the literature is lacking
behind. Another is that few responses were collected for the data and with more data it could
result in diﬀerent solution. The surprise is how many misalignment are between literature and
practice.
7.2
Implication on theory
As many researches have explored the risk factors of manufacturing supply chains this research
contributes to the gap which exist in the digital service supply chains. This research collects risk
factors from twenty eight researches and combines them in order to detect importance of service
risk factors along with misalignment between literature and practice.
This research can be
foundation to further research in this area as the risk factors explored can be used to investigate
their mitigation and monitoring.
35
==PAGE:44==
7.3
Implication on practice
This research illustrates what risk factors aﬀect digital service companies generally. Therefore,
can the study help digital service companies to explore and identify their existing risk factors. It
can get them to consider risk factors which they would otherwise ignore. However, it is necessary
for companies to evaluate if the risk is critical to themselves.
8
Conclusion
This research is conducted to identify risk factors within digital supply chain along with rank-
ing them according to importance along with discovering misalignment between literature and
practice. Through the research, risk categories and risk factors have been identiﬁed and ranked,
accordingly. Attention is drawn to be especially aware of human resources and data/information
security. Even though the other risk factors should be considered and evaluated as well. How-
ever, there is some misalignment between literature and practice as most of the risk factors have
diﬀerence between Author scale and Survey scale.
Even though the sample size of the research can be applied to exploratory research, the limita-
tions need to be addressed. Firstly, no signiﬁcant statistical meaning can be achieved from the
result. Nevertheless, can the result be used as indication rather than veriﬁcation. Secondly, in
the survey it is overlooked to ask respondents to specify their title within the companies. Those
information could be used further to value the validity of their response. As respondents from
the higher level of a company have better overview over the supply chain than ones from the
lower level.
The research identiﬁes future research possibilities in this area of perspectives. Firstly, more
research is needed in the digital area as few authors mentioned technology as a risk, which gives
room for improvements. It could be interesting to use those risk factors to investigate a whole
digital service supply chain. Secondly, as the service industry is wide it would also be recom-
mended to focus on a part of it and conduct similar research. Thirdly, future research should
emphasis on the defects this research identiﬁes by applying reasonable tools and frameworks.
