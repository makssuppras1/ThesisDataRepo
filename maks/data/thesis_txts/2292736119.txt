Abstract
The goal of this project is to derive an actuator fault diagnosis mechanism for a rotary-wing UAV, that
lets the pilot knowing when there is a fault and eventually its estimated magnitude. The objective of using
a detector is having a good knowledge of the vehicle’s status, to make a good decision about continuing
operating the vehicle or not.
First, a model of the system is derived in order to be able to estimate their outputs. Then, the measured
outputs and the estimated ones are compared to obtain the residual signals that are insensitive to changes in
the working point of the system but sensitive to changes in the physical system.
The detection process is carried out by using a GLR test with each of the residual signals. Two different
versions are tested, one estimating the set of parameters ˆθ0 recursively in the monitoring process and the
other one estimating ˆθ0 off-line previous to the monitoring process, obtaining different results.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
A multi rotor helicopter is a type of aerial vehicle that possesses more than two rotors. Different number
of rotors affects the manoeuvrability and the payload of the vehicle. These vehicles are generally agile and
easy to pilot.
These facts allow to use them in a large variety of applications such as surveillance, aerial photography
and video recording, among others. They possess a high degree of reliability due to its simple mechanisms.
1.1
Motivation
When ﬂying a multi rotor vehicle, unexpected events can occur due to external factors that are not
under the pilot’s control. These unexpected events can be harmless like soft wind but they can also lead to
catastrophic situations such as the battery disconnection which will produce an inevitably crash if produced
on ﬂight. This case, however, is an extreme situation. There are many other fault scenarios in between the
previous examples whose consequences could be avoided or at least be softened if detected on time.
The consequences that a fault could lead to are diverse. At least it could be expected to be the end of the
mission if the vehicle starts behaving erratically or not responding to the pilot’s or Flight Control System’s
commands. In the worst case scenario, the vehicle could crash causing personal and material damages.
Fault detection is an essential part of the control system needed to keep the vehicle working as close to
its original performance as possible and in the safest possible way in the presence of faults.
1.2
Problem Formulation
The variety of faults that can occur while ﬂying a multi rotor vehicle is extensive. A basic classiﬁcation
of the faults is done based on the nature of the component where the fault occurs. The elements that form the
vehicle are classiﬁed in two different categories:
• Actuators: Are the elements in charge of transforming the outcome of the ﬂight control’s algorithms
into physical actions, in this case, thrust and torque. Motor controllers, motors and propellers are
included in this category .
• Sensors: Are the elements that transform physical actions into electrical signals that are interpreted by
the Flight Control System. This category includes accelerometers, gyroscopes, GPS, barometer, etc.
According to this classiﬁcation it is possible to ﬁnd actuator faults and sensor faults. This is one possible way
of classifying but there are more. Based on how the fault appears it is possible to make another classiﬁcation:

• Abrupt: the fault appears suddenly producing a step-like change in the signals affected. An example
of this type of fault is if a motor gets disconnected on ﬂight.
• Incipient: the fault develops slowly producing a drift effect in the signals affected. An example
representing this case could be the deterioration of the motors’ ball-bearings over time that will
progressively increase their friction and therefore their performance will be reduced.
The ﬁrst objective of this project is to diagnose the presence of a fault in the actuators of the vehicle. To
do so two outputs, angular velocity ω and current consumption i, are constantly monitored by a statistical
based detection algorithm. The second objective is to estimate the fault’s magnitude and nature. This is done
by comparing the relative size of the change in the measurement with the expected signal when a fault is
detected.
1.3
Literature Survey
Fault detection and diagnosis of unmanned aerial vehicles (UAV) is an active area of research since the
technology evolves quickly allowing the use of more powerful microprocessors and the rapid expansion
of the use of UAV in many applications. (Cen et al. 2014) proposes a fault diagnosis scheme where a set
of residual signals is built using an adaptive observer to estimate the system states. It uses the angles and
angular rates of the vehicle as states. (Shariﬁet al. 2010) uses a state observer to estimate the outputs of each
rotor and compares them with their reference value. If the difference between the two values remains higher
than a threshold for a considerable period of time the fault is detected. In (Heredia et al. 2005) a detection
method for sensor faults in autonomous helicopters is developed. To do so, the system states are estimated
by using an observer and the behaviour of the vehicle is compared against the estimated behaviour.
With respect to the brushless DC motor, a particular component of the UAV, different approaches have
been used to detect and isolate faults in them. (Lee and Hsieh 2012) studies bearing faults detection and
identiﬁcation by analysing the current signal. (Tashakori and Ektesabi 2013) presents a fault diagnosis
technique to detect switch faults of three phases motor controller by analysing the measured line voltages of
the motor. (Hyeon Bae and Vachtsevanos 2009) uses also current measurements in the detection process.
1.4
Thesis Overview and Contribution
The organization of this thesis can be summarized as follows:
• Chapter 2 describes the system set-up used to generate and acquire the data used in the rest of the
project.
• Chapter 3 explains the techniques used to obtain a model of the system formed by a motor-controller
and a 3-phase DC motor. It also compares the measured outputs with the model’s outputs.
• In Chapter 4 statistical analyses of the measured signals and of the difference between measured and
estimated signals are carried out. These analyses lay the ground for Chapter 5.
• Chapter 5 develops two different fault detectors based on the statistical properties of the signals. Some

In this thesis the angular velocity of the motor is monitored together with the consumed current in order

CHAPTER 2
Problem Analysis and System Setup
2.1
Problem Analysis
The objectives of this project are to detect the appearance of faults in the actuators and to estimate
their magnitudes. To do so it was decided to monitor angular velocity of the motors as well as its current
consumption, since the appearance of any fault is reﬂected in these signals. The motor controller provides
the current consumption measurement through the I2C bus. Regarding the angular velocity ﬁrst it was
thought to use an optical encoder, however due to the big dimensions and mechanical complications to install
it it was discarded. Instead it was decided to use a hall effect sensor, taking advantage of the permanent
magnets being placed in the rotor, instead as in the stator (as happens in other types of electrical motors). It
was decided to use this type of sensor due to its small size, which allows it to be placed inside the motor.
Another reason why it was chosen is that it gives an accurate measurement that is not affected by noise, as
an analog sensor could be.
In order to be able to operate the motor as well as to take the needed measurements a Teensy USB Board,
Version 3.1 was used. Once the system was prepared to be operated and its outputs to be measured it was
needed to generate signals that were independent from the system’s working point but dependent to the
presence of faults in the system. To do so a dynamic model of the system was obtained. The difference
between the measured outputs and the estimated ones were used as input for the fault detection algorithm.
Then, in order to select an adequate GLRT detector it was needed to ﬁnd out the statistical distribution
describing the residual signals. Once this was done the detection algorithm was built and tested in order to
ﬁnd out the design parameters that gave the best performance.

2.2.2
Brushless DC motors
As explained before, the motor is the element in charge of transforming electrical energy into mechanical
energy. Due to their good power to weight ratio these motors are widely used in the radio controlled aircraft
world. Moreover these motors have some advantages over the conventional brushed DC motors:
• Absence of brushes:
• Less mechanical wear increasing the motor’s reliability.
• More efﬁciency as less power is dissipated due to friction.
• Use of electronic commutation:
• Permits ﬁnest control on the motor’s speed.
• Makes possible more control options such as speed limiting.
Working principles
Even though the numerous advantages that these motors present front the conventional brushed motors,
they need some electronic equipment to start working, which makes more difﬁcult their initial set up and can
be seen as a disadvantage. They need to be controlled by an electronic circuit, formed mainly by a six power
transistors and a microcontroller that switches them when required. These motors are formed by two parts:
rotor and stator. The rotor is formed by a set of permanent magnets, in the case of the motors used in this
project, the rotor is formed by 14 magnets.
The stator contains three coils that when energized generate an electromagnetic ﬁeld that makes the
rotor move. Two coils are energized at a time, whereas the third coil is affected by a counter electromotive
produced by the spinning rotor. By using this characteristic, the motor controller is able to track the motor’s
speed and to manage properly the switch timing of the transistors.

should be if the system remains healthy.

As it was observed in Chapter 3, the model is not capable of matching the system perfectly due to
the presence of unmodeled dynamics and some external factors that affect the system, such as ambient
temperature. It is also important to explain that the temperature of the motor is also time varying, as it
depends on the working point and the load that the motor faces. The temperature of the motor affects its
electrical components by varying their values as well as mechanical elements by varying their dimensions.
All these variable parameters, although they are really small in the range of temperatures in which the
motor work, are not taken into account by the model; therefore differences have to be expected between the
estimated outputs and the real outputs.
In the presence of a fault, however, the differences between estimated and real outputs are larger than in
the non faulty case and this is the characteristic exploited by the detection algorithm in Chapter 5.
In Fig. 4.8 an example of how the real and the estimated outputs deviate in the presence of a fault is
shown. In this case the fault was produced by a piece of paper that was thrown to the propeller while
spinning. The paper got stuck increasing the drag and therefore the load. As a consequence, the spinning
rate was decreased whereas the current consumption was increased. As observed, the estimated outputs,
however, remained in the same line as they were before the fault. This is also conﬁrmed by the residual
signals, which oscillate around zero in the period previous to the fault and then change their mean value.

5.1
Statistical Change Detection
As seen in Chapter 4 the parameters of the statistical distributions of the residual signals εω(t) and εi(t)
change in the presence of a fault. Therefore fault detection could be achieved by monitoring changes in the
statistical distributions.
To do so, the Generalized Likelihood Ratio Test is used. This is a hypothesis test, in which the null
hypothesis is that the distribution of the analysed data remains unchanged with respect to the distribution in
a non faulty case whereas the alternative hypothesis is that the data are no longer distributed according to the
null hypothesis.
If θ is the set of parameters that deﬁnes the statistical distribution that the analysed data follow and θ0 is
the particular set of parameters that deﬁne the statistical distribution of the data in the absence of a fault,
then:
H0 : θ = θ0
(5.1)
H1 : θ ̸= θ0
(5.2)
The selection between the null hypothesis and the alternative one is based on the value of a decision
function. This function is deﬁned as the ratio between the maximum likelihood of the data pertaining to the
distribution under the H1 hypothesis and pertaining to the distribution in the H0 case. Assuming that data
available for the analysis are deﬁned as Z = [z(1), ..., z(N)] then the decision function is deﬁned as:
g(Z) = L(ˆθ1; Z)
L(ˆθ0; Z)
(5.3)
where ˆθi is the maximum likelihood estimate of θi and L(Z; ˆθi) is the value of the likelihood function on
the analysed data using the parameters ˆθi
There are two types of erroneous decisions that can be made:
• Decide that H1 is true while H0 is true.
• Decide that H0 is true while H1 is true.
The ﬁrst one means that the detector raises an alarm when there is no fault. This is called a false alarm. The
second one means that the detector does not detect a fault that has occur.
The probability of false alarm and the probability of missing a fault are deﬁned as:
PF A = p(H1; H0)
(5.4)

5.2.2
Selection of design parameters of the detector
The detector depends on few parameters that are a designer’s choice and affect its behaviour. These
parameters are listed below:
• M is the size of the data array that will be tested, Z.
• M0 and M1 are the sizes of the data arrays used to obtain ˆθ0 and ˆθ1 respectively.
• A is the amount of new elements to be incorporated in the array Z at every iteration. It is called
window overlap.
• γωsup and γωinf are the thresholds against which the decision function is compared to decide between
H0 and H1. The logic to decide is: if H0 is true and gω(Z) > γωsup then it is decided that H0 is no
longer true until gω(Z) < γωinf .
The desired behaviour for the detector must fulﬁl the following requirements:
• Have a low probability of false alarm, which is deﬁned in Eq. 5.4. This probability depends directly
on the value of the threshold γωsup, the higher the threshold the lower the probability of false alarm.
• Have a low probability of missing a detection, which deﬁned in Eq. 5.5. As before, it depends on the
threshold value γωsup. The higher the threshold the higher the probability of missing faults.
• Present a low detection time, which is deﬁned as the difference between the time when the alarm is
raised and the time when the fault really occurred. It is affected mainly by the parameters M, M0 and
M1 and to a lesser by the threshold γωsup. The larger the vectors used the more reliable the statistic
test will be but also the bigger the detection delay.
Selection of the window size parameters, M, M0 and M1
In this section the selection of the parameters M, M0 and M1 is treated. The length of the arrays must be
large enough to let the data behave in the same way that was observed in the preliminary study. However an

Selection of the window overlap parameter
This parameter named in this project as A affects directly the detection delay because it is needed to
wait until A measurements have been made in order to run the detection algorithm. The selection of this
parameter is a trade-off between reducing the detection delay τd and using excessive computational power.
In this project it has been chosen A = 50 since it was observed that τd was not reduced with smaller values
of A but the computational power was notably increased in terms of simulation time.
In the case of implementing the detection algorithm in a vehicle this is an important characteristic to consider,
as typically this is a task for microcontrollers and they are limited in computational power.

5.3.2
Selection of design parameters of the detector
As in the previous detector, this one also depends on some parameters that are to be selected by the
designer. The parameters are the same as in the previous case and therefore they are already described in
Section 5.2.2. The only difference lies in the name of the thresholds that in this section are called γisup and
γiinf instead of γωsup and γωinf .
Selection of the window size parameters, M, M0 and M1
For the same reasons explained in Section 5.2.2 the relative size of M0 and M1 is set to half the size
of M. A preliminary study of εi(t) shows that its standard deviation is similar under both cases, faultless
and faulty. However, the mean value of the signal changes notably in the presence of a fault. A simple
test in order to pre-estimate what the size of M should be is made. It shows that the ratio between the
standard deviation of the signal and the change in the mean value is not as small as it would be desirable.
This means that the change produced in the parameters of the distribution of the residual signal εi(t) when
a fault occurs might be difﬁcult to detect if using small window size parameters due to the large standard
deviation of the signal. Therefore large values of M and consequently of M0 and M1 are needed. For
instance, Fig. 5.24 shows the residual signal εi(t) containing a fault at time 190 seconds approximately. In
this case, the standard deviation of the signal was around 0.20 A whereas the absolute value in the change in
the mean value of the signal when the fault is produced is around 0.45 A.
This produces that if choosing a small array M the rate of false alarms will be too high, since the detector
could identify some of the peaks in the signal as a fault. To avoid this the threshold could be set up higher
but then the detector could become insensitive.
This is shown in Fig. 5.25, which shows that the detector is insensitive to the fault produced since the

5.4
Combination of both detectors.
This section discusses different approaches of combining the two co-existing alarms, which are called
warnings from now on, into one single alarm that serves as a notiﬁcation to the operator or control system
that something is wrong in the vehicle’s actuators and allowing them to take the appropriate actions. In
order to make this combination it was decided to use combinational logic. There are diverse types of logic
combinations, however, only two of them can be used. One of the requirements is that when both warnings,
or continuing with the analogy, the two inputs to the logic circuit are at level ’high’, the alarm, or output of
the logic circuit should be also at ’high’. Moreover, when the warnings are at level ’low’ the alarm must be
at ’low’ as well. Therefore there are only two logic functions that fulﬁl these conditions:
• AND: the output is the product of the inputs using Boolean algebra.
• OR: the output is the sum of the inputs using Boolean algebra.
Choosing one function over the other will change the way the alarm is raised. If the ’AND’ option is selected
the alarm will raise when both warnings are at ’high’. This means that the global fault detection delay will
be as high as the highest fault detection delay of the warnings. However this solution has the advantage
that reduces the probability of false alarm. If both decision functions were independent the probability of
false alarm will be reduced to the product of the probability of false alarm of each signal. However, as some
correlation between the signals is expected, the probability of false alarm as a result of the combination of

If a higher probability of false alarm is allowed, better fault detection delay can be achieved by raising

When operating this type of aerial vehicles the timing has an important role since a failure in any of the
actuators will produce destabilizing effects on the vehicle that could lead to an accident. Hence the earlier
the fault can be detected and eventually corrected the better. Keeping in mind the priority of a good fault
detection time delay it is recommended to use the ’OR’ combination since the ’AND’ option is too restrictive
and delays the detection some extra seconds, the exact amount depends on the study case. As explained
before, the probability of false alarm with this solution is higher than with the ’AND’ logic, however, even if

In the absence of any fault both equations will ideally return a value around 100, since the residual
signals will be around zero.In the presence of a fault, they deviate from zero, and depending on the nature of
the fault they will become positive or negative. For example, if a propeller hits something during the ﬂight
and gets partially broken then the angular velocity residual εω will become positive whereas the current
residual will become negative. In this case X1 < 100 and X2 < 100.
If a different fault occurs, for example a motor’s ball bearing failure or something attached to the
propeller, the angular velocity measurement will drop, making its residual to become negative whereas the
current needed to keep the motor spinning will be increased, becoming its residual positive. In this case
X1 > 100 and X2 > 100.
In order to determine whether or not it is safe to keep the vehicle ﬂying, a threshold can be set for these
two variables in order to decide if the mission can continue or if it should be stopped. However, since a
study of how the thrust developed by the actuator depending on the size of the propeller, angular velocity or
current consumption has not been done in this project it is difﬁcult to select an adequate threshold.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
In this thesis a fault detection system has been designed to monitor the actuators of a rotary-wing
UAV. The objective was to be able to identify the appearance of faults in the motors or propellers of the
system and possibly estimate the magnitude and nature of the detected fault. The overall objective has been
accomplished, since as shown in Chapter 5, the detection system is in fact able to detect the appearance of
faults in the actuators and estimate their magnitude. Two types of faults have been simulated and correctly
detected in this project:
• A propeller breakage which reduces the motor load since the air drag is reduced.
• An object tangled in the propeller, like leaves of a tree or a plastic bag, which increases the motor load
since the air drag is increased.
This detector can be also used to monitor the motor and propeller of a ﬁxed-wing UAV since they typically
have the same structure as the ones used in this project.
The detection system was designed with the idea of being executed on-line, therefore its implementation
in the vehicle using a microcontroller should be easy only needing slight modiﬁcations.Regarding the
hardware requirements it would be needed a microcontroller that can handle as many hardware interrupts as
motors in the need of being monitored, as well as an analog to digital converter (ADC) to read the power
supply voltage. An I2C bus can also be needed in order to communicate with the motor controllers, but this
will depend on the user’s particular case.
Even though the tests showed good results in terms of all the faults were detected and no false alarms
were raised, the performance of the detector is to be improved in terms of reducing the detection time
which, specially for the detector based on the current measurement, is higher than desirable. To achieve this,
different options, which have already been discussed in Chapter 5, can be considered:
• Increase the sampling rate
• Find a more precise way of measuring the current consumption.
If used alone, this detector can be helpful to notify the pilot about the presence of a fault; however, if
combined with an adaptive control system that reconﬁgures depending on the detected fault, it could give the
system the capability of keeping a good performance even in the presence of a fault.
