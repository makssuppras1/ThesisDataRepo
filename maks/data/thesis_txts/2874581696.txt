Abstract
Western Australia (WA) has one of the highest rates of rooftop solar installations in the
world which is causing disrupted operation of its isolated electricity grid. This work seeks
to understand the role wind energy has historically played in the evolving energy system
and the role it can play as part of a new green hydrogen industry.
Firstly, the accuracy of simulated wind generation in WA using the Correlations in Renew-
able Energy Sources (CorRES) tool with ERA5 reanalysis data is quantified by comparing
with historical measurements. The simulation captures variations in mean daily and sea-
sonal output both at an individual plant and aggregate level and accurately reflects the
spatial variation between wind farm locations up to 740 km apart. Small scale variations
are not captured, consistent with other studies.
By extending the wind generation simulation to new locations, the benefits of increased
geographical diversity are quantified and found to be highly complementary to solar in
all locations, and reasonably complementary to existing wind generation at new south-
ern locations. A flexible electrolyser load powered by simulated wind and grid firming is
modelled with the operational strategy to ramp down during periods of high demand to
allow wind power to be redirected into the grid and sold at a high price and avoid high
hydrogen production costs. Operating in this way can benefit the grid and support new
wind capacity.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Context
The power supply in Western Australia (WA) is predominantly provided by coal and gas
with approximately one fifth of underlying demand met by renewable power which is gen-
erated from sixteen grid connected wind farms and a significant number of solar panels
on residential rooftops. The capital city of Perth is geographically isolated from the rest of
Australia, and of reasonably low population density. As such the electricity network is an
island grid which extends over a large area. The rapid transition away from traditional fos-
sil fuel generation to distributed and renewable energy resources is currently threatening
grid stability and changing how it is operated.
Despite having a good wind resource, there has been a slow uptake of wind energy in
Western Australia compared to other parts of the world. However momentum to transition
to renewable generation is building, as evidenced by the state government’s announce-
ment to retire all state owned coal fired power stations early “in response to massive
uptake of rooftop solar and renewables” [1].
The federal and state governments and industry have recognised hydrogen as a potential
alternative fuel in the transport, industrial and power generation sectors both domestically
and abroad. Hydrogen can be produced with no emissions using electrolysis of water
powered by renewable energy (green hydrogen). Electrolyser loads can support higher
uptake of renewable energy generation and can offer demand response services to the
grid. Given the large quantity of untapped renewable resources, geographical proximity
to high power demand markets in Asia and vast capability in production and export of
liquefied natural gas (LNG), there is an opportunity for WA to become a world leader in
the export of hydrogen and support the transition of Australia’s highest emissions intensive
industry while maintaining its economic benefits.
Correlations in Renewable Energy Sources (CorRES) is a tool that has been developed
and validated by DTU Wind Energy to simulate wind energy production in Europe. It has
the potential to be used anywhere in the world as it uses wind speed reanalysis data as its
primary input, which is available globally. It can provide long-term time series simulations
to support understanding of the wind resource at a new location.
1.2
Objectives
There are two objectives of this research. Firstly, to investigate whether simulation using
CorRES is reflective of actual grid connected wind farm generation in WA. The effective-
ness of correcting the wind speed bias and estimating wake losses is assessed using a
number of visualisations and error metrics to compare the modelled generation against
historical measurements. In doing so, the accuracy and limitations of the simulation are
understood, to inform the uncertainty of subsequent model prediction in surrounding ar-
eas.

The second objective is to develop an understanding of how potential wind farm develop-
ments at new locations compare with existing generation and demand. This is achieved
by simulation of current and new wind farms in order to understand how increasing geo-
graphical diversity of their location impacts overall variability. A hypothetical ‘future state’
is envisioned, whereby generation time series of potential new sites for development are
modelled. Operational strategies using flexible electrolyser loads powered by simulated
wind and grid firming are then tested to explore how this pairing might enable higher pen-
etration of wind generation in the existing grid.
1.3
Scope
Figure 1.1: Coverage of the South West Interconnected Network in WA [2]
The study applies to wind generation facilities connected to the South West Intercon-
nected System (SWIS) which is made up of the island network and the market under
which it is operated, referred to as the Wholesale Electricity Market (WEM). The network
services approximately 1.3 million customers in WA and extends as shown in fig. 1.1, cov-
ering an area of approximately 600 km by 600 km. Simulation of current grid connected
wind farms is undertaken using publicly available information regarding their capacity,
location and type of turbine. The CorRES tool itself is not freely available, as such sim-
ulations are undertaken by qualified personnel at DTU and the results made available to
the writer.

The simulation results are compared with actual wind farms’ historical output, which has
been made publicly available for each half hour trading interval of the WEM by the Aus-
tralian Energy Market Operator (AEMO) since 2007. The data include wind farm gener-
ation, associated outage and curtailed periods, wholesale prices and system operational
demand. Rooftop solar generation is behind the meter and therefore not measured. The
effects of rooftop solar generation is discussed extensively, and while historical generation
data from utility solar farms is used briefly in this work, solar generation is not simulated
in the analysis.
Operation of the network is governed by the WEM rules which are currently undergoing
reform, effects of some aspects of the reform will be discussed qualitatively, but are not
considered in the analysis.
The costs to produce hydrogen are also outside the scope of this work, as a number of
assumptions and variables are required to describe the process, storage, transport and
scale of a hydrogen development. The focus is simply on the technical opportunities that
are available when a flexible electrolyser load is paired with variable wind generation.
1.3.1
Thesis Outline
The remaining chapters are described as follows:
• Chapter 2: Literature Review
A summary of the current status of the electricity network and market in WA is pre-
sented in section 2.1 where current issues are discussed and grid connected wind
farms are introduced. Section 2.2 discusses the literature surrounding wind gener-
ation variability, and how this might be overcome using geographical diversity and
demand response. The current status and opportunities for green hydrogen produc-
tion are introduced in section 2.3. Then, section 2.4 describes how wind generation
is simulated by CorRES using reanalysis data, the Global Wind Atlas and estimated
wake losses. The literature is summarised in section 2.5.
• Chapter 3: Method
The inputs into the CorRES simulation are described in section 3.1 for existing wind
farms and potential new locations. The steps involved in interpretation, aggregation,
cleaning and visualisation of observed data are described in 3.2. How the simulation
is compared with observations is described in section 3.3.
• Chapter 4: Results
Section 4.1 begins with insights and trends from the electricity market data, which
provides context as to how wind generators currently participate. Results of the sim-
ulation are then compared with observed wind speed and wind energy generation in
sections 4.2 and 4.3 to validate the tool and quantify its performance. The results of
simulated generation at new locations are presented in 4.4, examples are provided
of how this information might be used to site locations which are complementary to
existing generation and test operational strategies for green hydrogen production.
• Chapter 5: Discussion
The findings of the electricity market data analysis, current generation analysis and
new location generation analysis are discussed in section 5.1, section 5.2 and sec-
tion 5.3 respectively. Section 5.4 discusses the limitations of the analyses and sec-
tion 5.5 proposes how to address limitations or further extend the analysis.
• Chapter 6: Conclusion
Finally, outcomes from this study are summarised.

2.1.1
Energy Transformation
The rapid uptake of residential solar PV and other distributed energy resources (DER) in
the urban regions of WA is posing an increasing challenge to the reliability of the SWIS.
The use of behind the meter solar power has caused a dramatic reduction of the average
operational demand in the middle of the day, resulting in an advanced ‘duck curve’ daily
profile. This refers to the appearance of morning and afternoon peaks in demand due to
households obtaining their power from solar in the middle of the day. This is observed to
have worsened each year in the first subplot of fig. 2.3. The term ‘operational demand’
is used to describe demand which is met by grid generation, i.e. excluding rooftop solar,
and differs from ‘underlying demand’ which can only be estimated. Continued exacerba-
tion of the duck curve profile is expected as a result of ongoing rooftop solar installations,
retirement of ageing thermal generation and increasing underlying demand. The resul-
tant steepening ramp rates, operational demand approaching the minimum threshold and
increasing ancillary service requirements all threaten system stability [6].
Operational minimum demand events tend to occur on mild sunny weekends or public hol-
idays in spring or autumn when air conditioning and business loads are less, but rooftop
PV generation is high. An example is shown in the third subplot of fig. 2.3 where opera-
tional demand dropped to a record low of 761 MW on the Sunday 14th November 2021
at around 11am. During this event, solar PV was generating an estimated 1.5 GW, which
was 67% of the underlying demand, as is illustrated in fig. 2.4 [7].

As a result of recent dedicated efforts, the minimum operational demand threshold for
system security has been lowered from 700 MW to 600 MW, below which it is considered
a ”zone of heightened security threat” [8]. However, there are many outstanding actions
which the government plans to implement in its DER roadmap in order to safely manage
the continued evolution of the power system [9].
Another issue is the increasing rate of change being observed as demand swings between
the peaks and troughs. In March 2021, a swing of approximately 300 MW was observed
in a period of 30 minutes due to cloud banks passing over the Perth metro area. This
resulted in a rapid change in behind the meter solar generation and the grid frequency
dropping to 49.5 Hz, outside the normal operating minimum of 49.8 Hz [4]. As the installed
capacity of solar PV increases, the severity of these swings is requiring the ageing thermal
generation fleet to respond in ways in which they were not designed. This increases the
probability of failure in the worst case, and also increases their outages and maintenance
costs.
2.1.2
Electricity Market Operation
While most electricity is traded bilaterally through contracts between generators and off-
takers, the dispatch of generation is determined every 30 minutes by the balancing market
to ensure supply always meets demand. The final balancing market price reflects the spot
price of electricity, not to be confused with the ancillary services market where facilities
provide grid stabilisation services (referred to balancing services in some European mar-
kets). The historical spot price mean daily and yearly profiles are shown in fig. 2.5. On
a daily basis, spot prices are demand driven and follow a similar ‘duck curve’ profile to
operational demand as shown in fig. 2.3.

Along with physical market mechanisms, the WEM operates a capacity market called the
Reserve Capacity Mechanism. This was introduced in 2006 to ensure system reliability
given the geographic isolation and small size of the grid which makes it prone to very
short periods of peak demand. Capacity credits are granted to generators to ensure the
projected capacity requirements are met, for which they receive payment. The largest
consumers during peak demand periods fund these payments.
2.1.3
Brief Overview of Wind Development in WA
WA was the home of Australia’s first wind farm in Salmon Beach near Esperance which
operated for 15 years from 1987-2002. The first grid connected wind farm, located in
Albany, commenced operation in 2001 and is still producing 21 years later. Other early
wind developments include the Walkaway and Emu Downs facilities located northwest of
Perth. The number of new developments has been limited despite a high quality wind
resource, with policy uncertainty and some community opposition delaying or dissuading
investment. The coverage of the transmission lines due to low population density in some
areas also prevents development due to the costs of installing new transmission lines.
In recent years, a number of larger wind farms have commenced operation in the north-
west where the wind resource is highest and the daily wind profile is highly complimentary
to solar generation thereby avoiding lower or negative prices. Unfortunately, the combined
generation from this cluster of wind farms sometimes exceeds the capacity of the network
between their location and the main load centre of the Perth metro area and they are
being instructed to curtail due to access constraints.
Rural towns such as Kalbarri and Bremer Bay have recently instated micro grids which
incorporate wind power from local turbines to reduce their reliability on imported power
from the grid as they are connected via long feeder lines which are subject to frequent
outages which can take time to repair [10] [11].
2.1.4
Renewable Incentive Schemes
The Australian Renewable Energy Target is enshrined in the Renewable Energy (Electric-
ity) Act 2000 [12] which introduced financial incentives for large and small scale renewable
energy producers by way of tradeable certificates. Each certificate represents 1 MWh of
electricity and are able to be purchased or surrendered by users of electricity to demon-
strate its green origin. Credited large scale renewable generators such as wind farms
are able to sell large scale generation certificates along with their electricity to offset the
cost of investment [13]. The target was amended in 2015 from 41000 GWh of renewable
source electricity per year by 2020 to 33000 GWh per year [14]. The Act is in effect until
2030, after which the incentives for renewable generators are not known.
In Australia, renewable energy production currently does not have to occur at the same
time as the load in order for it to be considered green. It is only required that the req-
uisite number of large scale green certificates are procured. Off-takers will use firming
from other generation sources into the grid when variable renewable energy (VRE) is not
available to ensure reliable power supply. In other words, granular supply demand match-
ing, for example on an hourly basis, is not currently mandated. Rather, it is only required
that annualised consumption and green generation are equal. This results in renewable
developments being oversized and excess generation injected into the market. This has
implications for the stability of the grid and does not support decarbonisation of the grid
as a whole. As such, careful planning of incentives is required to encourage behaviour in
the market which supports stable VRE integration.

2.2
Variability of Wind Generation
While being a cost effective source of renewable energy, wind suffers from variability
on both long and short timescales, from yearly fluctuations to those which occur over
periods of minutes. Much of the literature seeks to investigate and quantify the variability
of wind generation, its impact to the power system and how it might be accommodated by
geographical diversity, co-location with solar, compensated using storage or coupled with
flexible loads [15], [16], [17], [18]. A ‘self-cannibalization’ effect is described in [17], which
refers to the erosion of value of VRE during periods of high generation causing lower
prices and therefore lower return on investment the higher the proportion of renewable
generation in the system.
2.2.1
Geographical Diversity
In order to quantify the smoothing benefits of geographical diversity, researchers have
sought to understand the relationship between correlation of pairs of wind farm outputs
and the distance between them, an example in the USA is provided in [19] and the Nordics
in [20]. A correlation length scale is used which describes the spatial correlation relation-
ship in a particular region, whereby the longer the length scale the further apart wind
farms are situated to achieve the same correlation. A summary of such relationships is
presented in [21] where a new method for determining a correlation length is introduced
and is demonstrated on filtered data sets of wind farm production in Canada, the US and
South Australia.
Along with correlation, standard deviation of the power and the power ramps are also used
to quantify the benefits of combined generation from geographically distributed plants. It
is generally observed the standard deviation of these time series decrease with increasing
spacing and number of plants [15].
2.2.2
Demand Response
Traditionally, generation from other sources has been required to balance VRE output.
However, due to the decreasing levels of system demand depicted in fig. 2.3, the avail-
ability and ability of other generators in the WEM to respond is becoming limited. The
Australian Renewable Energy Agency (ARENA) describes this as “no longer sufficient,
as requires more storage and greater infrastructure connectivity, the cost of which can
be significant” [22]. ARENA was established in 2012 to support the transition to net zero
emissions and commits funding to low emission technologies and demand flexibility is
identified as a key focus area.
This position is shared by the IEA [23] who claim the ability to provide demand response
services is required to shift demand and maintain power system stability in order for VRE
resources to displace fossil fuels effectively. This requires new business models, incen-
tives and frameworks which allow loads to participate in the electricity markets.

2.3
Green Hydrogen
Current global hydrogen demand estimated as approximately 90Mt in 2020 [24] and is
predominantly required as feed stock to industrial applications such as hydrocarbon re-
fining and for ammonia to manufacture fertilizer. Furthermore, 80% of global hydrogen
production is from fossil fuels, contributing 900 Mt of carbon dioxide per year [24]. In
order to reach a net zero outcome by 2050, the IEA predicts immense growth in the hy-
drogen industry with a global demand of over 200 Mt required by 2030. Hydrogen can
theoretically be used in a number of applications such as power generation, domestic
heating and cooking, heavy transport and heavy duty industry such as cement and steel
production. Hydrogen can be stored and transported in gaseous form in large salt caverns
and pipelines, or processed into a liquid fuel such as ammonia, where in can be stored
in tanks and transported via ships. Given hydrogen can be made without carbon dioxide
emissions, its many uses, particularly in hard to decarbonize sectors, make it an attractive
solution to meeting the world’s energy needs sustainably.
2.3.1
Flexible Electrolyser Operation
Hydrogen electrolyser operation is based on the electro-chemical separation of water into
hydrogen and oxygen under an electric potential. The two most common and most devel-
oped technologies are proton exchange membrane and alkaline, both of which are able
to be operated flexibly to provide demand response services as demonstrated in [25].
Electrolysers are not yet widely used for hydrogen production, with only 0.03% of all hy-
drogen produced currently from electrolysis [24], due to higher costs. However, the IEA
predicts that by 2030 in the Net Zero Scenario, there will be a significant increase glob-
ally in demand response availability in order to manage increasing generation from VRE.
They predict 150 GW of hydrogen electrolysers would make up the 500 GW of demand
response services required along with buildings, transport and industry [23]. The IEA
also notes Australia’s policies “addressed some of the barriers to widespread demand-
response deployment”.
2.3.2
Australian Hydrogen Industry
The government of WA has recognised the opportunity for the state to develop a renew-
able hydrogen industry and have indicated how they will provide support in the Renewable
Hydrogen Strategy [26]. However, many barriers exist to its development and the primary
challenge is the prohibitive cost to produce green hydrogen compared to traditional meth-
ods of production using reformation of hydrocarbons. This is due in part to the efficiency
losses, as well as the cost and variability of renewable electricity. Flexible electrolyser
loads might achieve a lower electricity cost when used to both take advantage of fluctu-
ations in the price of electricity, provide ancillary services and reduce capacity payment
costs. However this comes with a trade off of reduced utilisation which requires higher
capital cost to maintain hydrogen off-take.

2.4
Wind Generation Simulation Using CorRES
Correlations in Renewable Energy Sources (CorRES) is a tool developed by DTU Wind
Energy to simulate wind generation using atmospheric reanalysis wind data along with
site specific information describing a wind farm such as location and the configuration of
the wind turbines [27]. The methodology has been validated on large scale regional wind
speed and energy generation measurements in Europe using reanalysis data from various
sources. The best performance in simulated generation using traditional error metrics
was observed when using a combination of ERA5 and the Global Wind Atlas release
2 (GWA2). The method can automatically accommodate wake losses by adjusting the
power curve according to parameterisations describing the plant size and turbine layout
[28].
2.4.1
ERA5 Reanalysis Data
The European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) latest reanalysis
data set, ERA5, is produced by “combining vast amounts of historical observations into
global estimates using advanced modelling and data assimilation systems” [29]. Impor-
tantly, it provides wind speed at hourly resolution near modern turbine height at up to 100
m, which makes it a better source for wind power predictions compared to other global
reanalysis data where wind speed is only available up to 50 m height. ERA5 data are
available over a 0.5°(∼31km) grid, which is a higher resolution than the previous ERA-
interim and NASA developed MERRA-2 global reanalysis data. ERA5 data are available
from 1950 to present and updates are available 5 days within realtime, although these
are not quality assured for 3 months.
It is generally acknowledged that most reanalysis data exhibit a bias which requires cor-
recting before use [30]. This correction can be achieved by comparing with actual mea-
surements at the location of interest. The most direct comparison is with actual wind
speed measurements, but as these can be difficult to obtain to due their commercial sen-
sitivity, often other freely available data are used instead. The method of correction can
range in complexity but the simplest form is linear scaling. In [30] it was shown linear scal-
ing derived from comparing aggregate national level capacity factors between measured
and simulated generation was sufficient to significantly improve the simulation, which was
performed using MERRA-2 reanalysis data. However, the calibration factors were shown
to vary significantly over different regions from 66% to 154%.
ERA5 was shown to perform better than MERRA-2 in [31] when used to simulate country
wise generation in Germany, Denmark, Sweden and France and in the northwest USA, it
also performed well on an individual turbine level in Sweden. This study did not involve any
bias correction or wake loss approximation as it purely focused on assessing the accuracy
of each source of reanalysis data. Recently, ERA5 reanalysis data has been shown to
outperform an advanced weather research forecasting (WRF) model using according to
centred RMSE and correlation metrics when compared to measurements up to 200m on
land and offshore in the US [32]. It was however noted that ERA-5 had worse performance
in terms of bias.
The applications for reanalysis data can be limited by its spatial and temporal resolution
which is unable to resolve local changes in topography or over small time scales. There-
fore effects which might alter the wind flow such as speed up over hills or reduction in
speed over forested areas with increased roughness are not entirely accommodated. As
such, methods of down scaling using WRF and the WAsP principle are often used to incor-
porate local effects and improve the accuracy of wind simulations. This however involves
computational effort and is not readily accessible as a time series.

In Australia, a high resolution regional reanalysis has been performed by the Bureau of
Meteorology which covers a 12km grid over Australia, New Zealand and the maritime con-
tinent. Finer scale domains (1.5km) are also available over four regions, which includes
the south west of WA [33]. The Bureau of Meteorology Atmospheric Regional Reanaly-
sis for Australia (BARRA) coverage is from 1990-2019, reported hourly and extends 80
km height into the atmosphere. The boundary conditions are provided by ERA-5 interim.
However, BARRA data are not available to be used within the CorRES tool.
2.4.2
Global Wind Atlas
The Global Wind Atlas is a free to use, online tool which provides high resolution wind
resource estimates around the globe using a method of down-scaling large scale atmo-
spheric reanalysis data with topographical information. It was first released in 2015 and is
currently at the third version, released in 2019. This version, GWA3, uses ERA5 reanal-
ysis data from 2008-2017 as input into the WRF mesoscale model, the output of which
is then used as input into microscale modelling using the DTU WAsP methodology [34].
At each down-scaling step, elevation and roughness length data are used to improve the
accuracy of the modelled wind climate at all locations. The output is a localised wind cli-
mate across a 250 m resolution grid and has been validated in 35 sites in Bangladesh,
Maldives, Pakistan, Papua New Guinea, Vietnam and Zambia. The observed mean wind
speed bias was between ±31% [34].
The wind climate provided by GWA is a statistical representation that may be used to
estimate annual energy production at various sites. While average monthly and diurnal
profiles are available, higher resolution temporal variation as would be described by a
time series is not.
In [28], it was found that using CorRES with ERA5 wind speed time series was improved
when compared to wind speed and generation in Europe if corrected using the mean
wind speed from GWA2 (the previous release). This was also found to be true when sim-
ulated wind generation of aggregated onshore wind power production in twelve European
countries was compared with measurements. ERA5+GWA2 was shown to perform bet-
ter than ERA5 and other European reanalysis data (with and without GWA2) according to
traditional metrics.
2.4.3
Wake Losses
In large plants, it is important to consider the reduced power output which might occur
due to turbulence in the wake of turbines upstream. The magnitude of the wake effect will
vary with the size, spacing and layout of the wind farm as well as the wind characteristics.
In siting assessment, both estimates of net annual energy production and engineering
safety assessment requires that wake losses be determined in order to ensure turbines
are optimally positioned to harness maximum energy while not being exposed to extreme
conditions which might impact their integrity.
Wake losses are not easily modelled due to the complex nature of turbulence, and require
computationally expensive CFD to represent fluid motions. Several engineering models
have been developed and are widely applied to ensure wind farm layouts are designed
in ways which minimise exposure to wake losses. Many of these engineering models are
available through the free to use DTU package PyWake [35] yet require specific details
of the wind farm including layout to compute the losses of individual turbines for different
wind directions before aggregating overall plant losses [36]. A plant specific wake affected
power curve can then be used as input into CorRES or other energy production studies.

2.4.4
Other Losses
Inefficiencies in the transfer of electrical energy through cables, transformers and connec-
tion to the grid substation results in the reported output of a wind farm being lower than
the sum of that generated by individual turbines. Furthermore, unusual conditions such
as sector management, maintenance or outages may result in turbines being removed
from service, or operated at a reduced power output.
2.4.5
CorRES Methodology
To determine a representative wind speed at a plant location and hub height, CorRES
uses horizontal interpolation between ERA5 reporting co-ordinates and the power law to
interpolate vertically. Bias correction of wind speeds is achieved using the ratio of mean
wind speed predicted by the GWA at the plant location compared to the ERA5 mean wind
speed.
A plant representative power curve is developed using the power curves of the turbines
combined with an estimate of the wake losses as described in section 2.4.3. The power
generation for every timestep can then be modelled and adjusted to account for any sys-
tem losses.
The DTU CorRES methodology has been shown to not require bias correction or calibra-
tion using historical European plant generation data in [28]. It is a bottom up approach
which could therefore be applied with more confidence in new locations compared to
other methods, however validation at those locations would still be required to ensure the
method is appropriate.
As CorRES has been developed to simulate large scale renewable energy production,
granularity on a plant level is not always able to be accounted for. For example, the
uncertainty of the wake loss prediction is high given it uses simplified parameterisations
of a wind farm layout. Furthermore, adjacent wind farms which might induce wake affects
on each other can only be accounted for to an extent if the wind farms are modelled as
one. However this requires the same turbine models and heights to be assumed. CorRES
supports a fully detailed wake model such as from PyWake which can overcome both of
these issues, although this requires additional time and information.

2.5
Summary
Western Australia is currently under pressure to transform its electricity system to one
which can handle more renewable generation. This presents a significant challenge, as
increased generation from distributed renewable energy resources, particularly rooftop
solar, is disrupting the operation of the grid and electricity market. Adoption of new tech-
nologies and regulation reform are required to ensure the continued safe and economic
supply of electricity.
In order to support the integration of wind generation, the ability to simulate and under-
stand its variability is an important first step. Introduction of demand response systems
on the grid such as with flexible hydrogen electrolyser loads presents an opportunity to
integrate more renewable generation, decarbonise the grid and support a new energy
industry using hydrogen to displace fossil fuels both domestically and abroad.
CorRES has been shown to effectively model aggregate wind generation in Europe using
reanalysis data and is able to quantify the impact of new developments to power systems.
It has the potential to be used anywhere in the world, but is yet to be tested in the southern
hemisphere. This work applies the CorRES methodology in WA using ERA5 wind speeds
with a bias correction to GWA3 and converted to power with wake affected power curves.
The simulation is validated by comparing to recorded wind generation then applied at new
locations to model potential wind and flexible demand pairing.

3.2.4
Data Aggregation
All WEM data are reported every 30 minute trading interval, and has been aggregated into
hourly resolution to compare with simulation outputs. While generation is reported as total
energy (MWh) over a 30 minute trading interval, this has been converted to the average
power (MW) over an hour by summing the two half hour quantities of energy produced
before and after the hour. Generation data are then standardised by the installed capacity
such that it takes a value between 0 and 1.
3.2.5
Assumptions
It has been assumed all times are reported as standard Perth time; UTC+8hours, including
when daylight savings was trialled from 2007-2009. This was verified by checking there
were no duplicated or missing trading intervals in the data.
3.2.6
Data Masking
To enable a fair comparison with the simulated generation data, masking criteria are used
to ignore data when a facility might not be producing as expected. Some criteria are based
on AEMO data; such as outages and curtailment, while further criteria have been devel-
oped using visual indicators which may be due to upset conditions or sensor malfunction.
These criteria are codified to automatically identify abnormalities and the same rules are
applied to all plants. Initial masking effectiveness has been checked by comparing the
simulation with actual generation. While most masking events do occur when there is a
difference between simulated and actual generation, there are instances of ‘over mask-
ing’ (when generation data are well matched but masked), and ‘under masking’ (when
masking has not captured periods of different generation). A filter is used to mask the
data where a distinct difference is observed between simulated and observed generation
to resolve the latter, however it is not easy to remedy the former and some ‘over mask-
ing’ remains. The following sections describe in further detail the reasons and rules for
masking.
Duplicates
Although duplicate data for a single timestep is rare, where it occurs, all values are re-
moved and the timestep not considered in the analysis.
Curtailment
Wind farm output might be limited due to instruction from the network operator to manage
constraints in the network or ceased during to periods of high negative pricing when gen-
erators might otherwise have to pay to produce. In these cases, the times during which a
plant is curtailed are not used for comparison with the simulation.
Outages
Timing and duration of outages from both the ‘realtime outages’ data set and the ‘outages’
data set have been used to flag when data are suspect and excluded from the analysis.
Flat-line Data
A rule has been used to remove data when generation is not observed to change within a
tolerance of 1% during a period of three hours. The exception to this is when the standard-
ised generation is either less than 10% installed capacity, which might be due to no wind,
or more than 90% installed capacity, which might represent when the facility is producing
at its rated capacity.
Extended output of zero is more likely to be an outage rather than non windy conditions,
therefore nil production for greater than 24 hours is also removed from the data.

High or Low Data
During commissioning or maintenance, a wind farm might operate at a fraction of its max-
imum capacity. This is evident in timeseries of many wind farms. Data are masked if
generation is consistently below 20% of its installed capacity for an extended period of
more than seven days, or consistently 20% for more than five days.
Difference between Observed Data and Simulation
A filter is used to remove periods where observed data are substantially different (more
than 70% installed capacity) from simulated.
3.2.7
Data Statistics and Visualisation
The mean and standard deviation of wind generation quantify average production and
variation. However, better understanding is obtained by visual representation of data
which can highlight patterns or biases, such as those described in this section.
Time Series
The primary visual representation of this data is on a time scale which can be used to
identify any unusual behaviour such as flat lines, however this can result in very long
plots as some data span 14 years. As such, their value is limited to specific windows in
time, and are used sparingly.
Average Diurnal and Yearly Profiles
The average generation for each hour of the day and each month of the year are presented
to illustrate daily and seasonal trends in generation. The range of data about the mean
can be represented by the difference in the 25th to 75th percentiles.
Power Generation Probability Density Function (PDF)
A convenient statistical representation is obtained by plotting generation as a PDF his-
togram which provides an indication of the proportion of time a facility is generating within
various bins of standardised generation.
Power Ramp Probability Density Function
The power ramp is the difference between timesteps and provides an indication of vari-
ability. The power ramp PDF illustrates how often the data changes each timestep for a
range of bins of standardised generation.
Auto-correlation Function (ACF)
The ACF is the correlation of a time series with itself after a time difference or lag. When
plotted against a range of lags the rate of decay in correlation provides an indication of
the temporal variability of the data.
Cross Correlation Function (CCF)
The CCF is the correlation of two time series at different lags which can be used to identify
synchronisation issues which may occur as a result of timezone differences.

indicate how smooth or variable their combined output would be. A high positive value

times and their combined output would be less variable. A negative value indicates the
power sources are complementary and therefore they can counter-balance each other
resulting in a more stable combined output. Correlation matrices are used to summarise
correlation between all pairs of facilities. They are ordered by distance from the furthest
north on each axis and the cell where two locations intersect contains their correlation

If the electrolyser load is reduced when system demand (and price) is high, it can make
generation available when it is required. This would make economic sense if the cost of
reduced hydrogen off-take is offset by the avoidance of high price periods and revenue
from excess wind power sales. Storage such as batteries can perform a similar func-
tion to this by shifting generation from low to high price periods to arbitrage the market,
however in doing so they erode the value of their return on investment. This is because
operating this way reduces the difference between peaks and troughs in price until there
is no financial benefit. Conversely, an electrolyser can shift it’s operational strategy from
initially operating in a way which takes advantage of price fluctuations at the cost of lower
utilisation to operating at higher utilisation if the market stabilises.
An example ‘Set and Forget’ variable load strategy is provided in fig. 4.46, showing a
500 MW electrolyser load which is ramped down from 5-7pm each day during the peak
demand period. It is again primarily powered by the same hypothetical 1 GW installed
wind capacity provides which is now able to provide 113% ‘green’ (i.e. net annualised)
electricity in 2020. This increase is because the utilisation of the electrolyser is reduced
from 100% to 87.5%. Again, the load is maintained at 100% outside the 5-7pm window
and is provided by a combination of wind generation and firming from the grid. Similarly,
when the wind generation exceeds the 500 MW load, the excess is exported to the grid
and sold to the market. The net effect of operating in this way is shown in the second
subplot and compared with the grid’s historical operational demand and price time series.
On a day to day basis, the firming profile is the same as the flat load case described
above, and the demand injection benefit during periods of low demand is maintained.
During periods of high demand, the reduced load from the electrolyser results in all wind
generation becoming available to meet demand. The impact varies due to variations
in generation and demand on a daily basis and the electrolyser load profile remaining
constant. For example, there are days when the electrolyser ramps down where neither
demand nor price are high and the additional supply may not be required nor realise
the same economic benefit as on high demand days. If the load profile were able to be
adjusted on a daily basis, the net effect could be tuned to provide net supply or demand
smoothly according to the daily requirement.

5
Discussion
This section discusses the findings of using the CorRES tool to simulate wind energy
production in WA, how the simulation compares with actual generation and how simulated
generation at new locations can be used to inform siting decisions and model strategies
for powering an electrolyser. Limitations are also discussed and suggestions for further
work are provided.
5.1
Wholesale Electricity Market Data Analysis Findings
Analysis of the WA wholesale electricity market data has provided insights into historical
trends in growth of wind and solar generation over time such as is depicted in fig. 4.3 and
fig. 4.4. How their complementary profiles result in a combined average daily output is
shown in fig. 4.5. In fig. 4.6, this output is represented as a percentage of demand and
is a reasonable stable (roughly 25%) apart from during the afternoon when only approxi-
mately 15% of peak demand is able to be met. This is one illustration of how renewable
generation alone is not sufficient to meet demand, as installing required capacity to meet
peak demand would result in over supply during other times of the day. As generation
varies on a daily basis, additional generation would be required to compensate for low
production periods unless there are other technologies participating in the market that are
able to modulate demand or shift generation.
A negative impact of siting wind developments in close proximity is illustrated in fig. 4.9
where system mandated curtailment has historically been applied to five wind farms and
one solar farm. As these are located on the same transmission line, when the facilities are
producing at their maximum it does not have the capacity to allow all facilities to produce.
Despite this region having the highest resource in WA it is unlikely there will be further
development there until the network is augmented.
An important practical and commercial consideration for siting a new wind development
is whether periods for which facilities might be required to curtail production overlap with
prospective generation profiles. It is evident from the data that six wind farms appear
to be exposed to negative spot prices, while others choose to continue production at all
times, which might reflect the commercial terms of their power purchase agreements.
It is shown in fig. 4.10 that periods of curtailment correspond to times of excess solar
production. It is also shown that the Warradarge wind farm has been most affected by
economic curtailment which has historically occurred up to 1% of the time this has potential
to increase in the future if negative prices become more common. The impact might not be
significant considering the times for which it occurred does not overlap with windy periods.
While the spatial extent of the grid in WA is not as large as other grids, the furthest apart
wind farms are shown to have no correlation in fig. 4.11. This means their outputs can
counterbalance the grid in order manage intermittency. It is also shown the decay in
correlation of power and power ramp is steeper with distance than studies in the US [19]
and the nordics [20], as evidenced by smaller characteristic lengths.

5.2
Simulation of Existing Generation Findings
Simulation of WA wind energy production has been performed using CorRES interpolation
of ERA5 wind speeds with and without GWA3 adjustment and using actual turbine power
curves with a wake loss adjustment. The simulated generation has been compared with
that observed at nine individual wind farms over an analysis period of 14.3 years, during
which time three wind farms were operational over the entire period. The generation data,
which is available every half hour trading interval, has been resampled to hourly frequency
so as to compare with ERA5.
The simulation using GWA adjusted ERA5 wind speeds and wake losses is found to out-
perform that using ERA5 wind speeds only and wake losses, as summarised in table 4.8.
This holds true across all error metrics for the aggregate generation and most individual
locations, with the exception of Mumbida and Mount Barker. The MAE is found to be 12%
for the aggregate generation with a mean of 15% and standard deviation of 3% for individ-
ual locations. The correlation is found to vary between locations with a mean of ρ = 0.84
and a value of ρ = 0.89 for the aggregate generation. These error metrics indicate the
simulation is not as well matched as the aggregate generation in twelve European coun-
tries, which was modelled using ERA5+GWA2. The European simulation was found to
have a mean and standard deviation MAE of 5% of 2% respectively, and a mean ρ = 0.96
[28]. This may be due in part to the significantly larger data sets used in the study.
Examination of the mean diurnal and yearly profiles of the simulation compared with ob-
servations shown in fig. 4.25 and fig. 4.24 indicate the simulation captures daily and yearly
variations at individual locations and on aggregate well. Furthermore, year on year varia-
tion is shown to be well captured in fig. 4.20. The decay in correlation between wind farm
power and hourly power ramp output that occurs with increasing distance is shown to be
represented well by the simulation, although the simulated correlations are slightly higher
than observed.
Small scale fluctuations are not captured as adequately as depicted in ACF plots (fig. 4.28
and fig. 4.29), power ramp plots (fig. 4.30 and fig. 4.31) and spatial correlation plots
(fig. 4.32) where it is observed the simulation have greater correlation in time and space
and have narrower ramp PDF distributions.
5.3
Simulation of New Generation Findings
Once validated, the CorRES simulation was applied at new sites using GWA3 scaling
according to a range of hub heights and locations and a consistent wake affected power
curve. The results of these simulations were used to assess the benefits of geographical
diversity within the grid and test operational strategies of new wind generation paired with
electrolyser loads. The least correlated new locations were found to have a correlation
with existing generation of ρ = 0.3 which increased to ρ = 0.5 when planned generation
was included.

5.4
Limitations
5.4.1
Wind Generation Data and Masking Limitations
While the market data sets provided by AEMO are high quality and readily available, the
are not able to convey the entire status of a wind farm’s operational condition. Periods
of unusual readings such as flat-lines where data may have been lost were evident and
only able to be determined by interrogation. The masking exercise that was undertaken
was able to remove most periods of anomalous data but was a high level screen only, and
where one rule may have required more conservative criteria for one wind farm, it may
have erroneously removed good data for another.
The masking was also not able to use the observed data alone and involved a filter which
compared observed and simulated generation to remove some time steps when they were
significantly different.
Seven wind farms were excluded from validation for various reasons which reduced the
diversity of the data slightly. Despite these minor issues, sufficient data for the purposes of
this study remained, spanning nine wind farms’ operating over a period of 87 cumulative
years.
5.4.2
Wind Speed and GWA3 Adjustment
Unfortunately no wind mast data was available to validate ERA5 wind speeds against
actual conditions at the site location and hub heights assessed. This prevented the ef-
fectiveness of the GWA scaling from being truly assessed in the study, as it is impossible
to know power output without losses from the data available. The closest weather sta-
tion records were not a valid comparison due to geographical separation and only being
available at 10 m above ground level, compared to hub heights around 85 m. Despite
this, correlation between wind speed and direction was observed to exist, with values of
ρ = 0.69 and ρ = 0.73 respectively as summarised in fig. 4.14. While wind direction has
not been used in this analysis, it could be used to improve wake loss predictions if a plant
specific wake curve was available. It was also apparent fluctuations in measured wind
speed and direction exceed those predicted by ERA5.
Ratios between the GWA3 mean wind speed and ERA5 mean wind speed for the existing
locations were generally greater than 1, as shown in fig. 4.15 for the existing locations,
and fig. 4.35 for the new locations. This resulted in the ERA5 wind speeds being scaled
up by 20%. However, there was no apparent geographical pattern to indicate why that
might be the case, and no investigation was undertaken to understand further.
5.4.3
Wake Loss Modelling Limitations
Wake losses for the nine existing wind farms are estimated by the CorRES predictor as
ranging between 9-24% for the ERA5+GWA3 simulation and 6-24% for the ERA5 simu-
lation as summarised in table 4.9. This is considered high, as a plant with wake losses
>20% would generally not be considered economic. The cause is unknown, as the algo-
rithm was not made available as part of this work. It is possible the input data lies outside
the bounds for which the machine learning data set was trained. It is also possible Aus-
tralian conditions are different due to characteristics of the wind or the lower average plant
power densities.
Some wind farms such as Albany and Grasmere, shown in fig. 3.2, have layouts which
would encounter wake losses very much dependant on wind direction. These would not
effectively be captured by the in built wake loss predictor, which takes no consideration
of layout nor wind direction.
Wind Siting in Western Australia for Hydrogen Production
75
==PAGE:86==
Furthermore, wakes induced by adjacent wind farms, which was ignored in this study,
were evident in the case of the Mumbida wind farm when compared with Walkaway in
table 4.7 and fig. 4.24, who’s wind output is clearly reduced compared to simulation and
appears to be affected by the wake of the adjacent Walkaway wind farm upstream which is
much better represented by the simulation. While it would have been possible to represent
both plants as one in the wake loss predictor, this would have involved assuming the same
hub height, turbine and layout characteristics which is not the case. While this may have
resulted in an better aggregate generation, it would have involved a loss of accuracy for
the walkaway wind farm.
As there was a lack of meaningful wind speed measurements to use in the study, the effect
of GWA3 scaling and wake losses were not able to be entirely decoupled and assessed
independently. It is possible the wake losses were over estimated in CorRES by the in
built wake loss predictor and that the GWA3 scaling over corrected the wind speeds such
that net effect was well matched to observations.
5.4.4
Spatiotemporal Limitations of the ERA5 Reanalysis Data
The spatial correlation plot of masked WA wind farm power generation in fig. 4.11 does
indicate an inverse relationship between correlation and distance. It is unusual that it is
not adequately represented by the exponential decay function eq. (3.1) as is observed
in other regions [21] [19] [20]. A linear relationship is instead more evident. Without the
masking, and using a bigger data set of all 16 wind farms the data are less correlated,
and there is some non linearity in the spatial correlation as shown in fig. 4.11, however is
still not entirely represent by an exponential decay function as there is a bias below the
curve at high distances. It is possible the limited number and coverage of the wind farms
is not sufficient as only the furthest apart wind farms reach zero or negative correlation.
It is also possible noise in the data has not been removed sufficiently by masking and is
skewing the correlation. However, since it is observed that the ERA5+GWA3 simulation
spatial correlation also exhibits a similar linear relationship in the first subplot of fig. 4.32,
albeit with slightly higher correlation, it is possible that exponential decay is not an ap-
propriate function to represent the spatial correlation in this region. While this has no
implication for this work, further investigation could be undertaken using the filtering and
parameterisation undertaken in [21].
The spatial correlation of power ramps however are adequately represented by the expo-
nential decay function in both the observed (masked and unmasked) and simulated data
sets. It is also observed the simulated power ramp exhibits higher correlation to a greater
extent (eXP = −0.19 and eX∆P = −0.27), resulting in a longer characteristic length of
53km compared to 10km. Higher spatial correlations in reanalysis data are also observed
in [28] and reflect the smoothing over time and space introduced by the ERA5 hourly
resolution and 30km spacing.
It is observed in the ACF and power ramp PDF plots (fig. 4.29, fig. 4.28 and fig. 4.31)
that the temporal variability of output is not entirely captured by CorRES. This limitation
introduces uncertainty to wind resource assessments and reduces usefulness in power
system studies where system balancing requires the magnitude and frequency of fluctu-
ations to be appropriately captured.

5.4.5
Realities of Developments at New Locations
The criteria for selection of potential new locations in this study was to test the theo-
retical limits of geographical diversity. Consideration of proximity to network, residential
dwellings, land condition and many other facets of actual wind farm development have
not been taken into consideration. It is unfortunate that the least correlated locations used
to simulate a theoretical complementary generation profile in section 4.4.3 are also the
least viable due to their location in popular tourist and wine regions, as is the case for
the southwestern locations, or far from any high voltage transmission lines as with the
locations southeast.
It must also be mentioned that a simulation can only provide insight for energy system
planners to inform the impact of developments at certain locations. Actual measurements
over at least one year are generally required to understand a wind resource in order to
decide if it’s worth committing to financially.
5.5
Areas for Future Work
There are many options to improve, further investigate or build on the ideas presented in
this work, as suggested in this final section.
5.5.1
Australian Wind Speed and Generation Data
Freely available wind speed data from wind masts are available at two locations in the
south east of Australia as part of the Tall Tower data set [46]. Here, it would be possible
to perform a wind speed comparison with reanalysis data and the GWA.
Furthermore, generation data for the Australian National Electricity Market (NEM), which
supports trading of electricity in many other states of Australia, is also made freely avail-
able by AEMO. This data set includes 82 wind facilities totalling 9.2 GW of installed ca-
pacity [47]. Trading intervals in the NEM have also recently been reduced to 5 minutes,
which provides higher resolution data with which the distribution of power ramps at higher
frequencies can be investigated.
5.5.2
Wake Loss Estimation
To better understand the wake losses, an engineering model such as those available in
the Pywake package [35] can be used. Using site specific wake affected power curves is
expected to improve the accuracy of the simulation, particularly where wind directionality
and neighbouring wind plants affect power generation. In order for wake models to be
validated, wind speed measurements are required in order to determine the potential
generation and thereby compare with actual generation to determine wake losses. This
is hard to do, as wind speed measurements are not publicly available. It is recommended
the wake loss predictor be checked on validated wake loss curves, as the current work [36]
compares the capacity factor of simulated generation with wake losses with observations,
and has not performed any verification using wind speed.
As the wake loss predictor available in the CorRES tool is suspected to overestimate the
wakes in this study, it is recommended it is revisited. A study for Australian conditions
could be performed using the 98 grid connected wind farms, however this is unlikely to
provide enough data to train a neural network as has been undertaken using 1000 plants
in [36].

5.5.3
ERA5 Bias Correction
While the GWA has been validated against wind speed measurements at 35 sites in 6
countries, the biases observed (approximately ±31% [34]), are significant as the error
increases three fold when used in a power estimate. Further investigation comparing
actual wind speed data with GWA3 at a particular location would be required to use this
technique with more certainty. If wind speeds are not available, a bias correction using
wind generation data could be used, however this would require confidence in the wake
losses.
With further investigation, more sophisticated statistical bias corrections might be devel-
oped which use more than the mean wind speed to scale ERA5 data. GWA3 also provides
vertical wind speed profiles and wind speed distributions which can be used to adjust re-
analysis data.
Alternative sources and methods of down scaling reanalysis data exist, however they
involve computational effort and are usually not freely available.
5.5.4
Power Fluctuations
In the context of power and system studies, limited temporal variability can be compen-
sated in CorRES using stochastic simulation as is described in [48] and [15]. In these
studies, wind speed fluctuations are added to reanalysis data to achieve the same dis-
tribution of power ramps, however the actual timeseries of generation is not able to be
replicated.
5.5.5
Reduced Variability
There are many options to reduce the variability of onshore wind generation. As dis-
cussed in this study, West Australian wind generation can counter balance solar which is
of benefit considering the significant installed capacity of rooftop PV. Offshore wind devel-
opments are in early stage feasibility in Australia and legislation has recently been passed
to allow renewable generation permits to be obtained in offshore areas. This offers the
opportunity to access increased geographical diversity and higher wind speeds but is not
yet considered economically competitive due to the additional complexities and costs of
installing equipment in the offshore environment and connected it to the mainland. Cor-
RES is able to simulate offshore wind generation using the same principles as described
in this study, however offshore wakes are not covered by the in built predictor, as such
offshore wake affected power curves would be required.
A novel business case for a low specific power turbines is presented in [17] due to their
ability to produce in lower cut-in wind speeds and therefore complement existing gener-
ation and reduce overall variability. It is unlikely this business case would be considered
in the current market compelling given the current low levels of wind penetration, but may
be worth considering if it were to increase.
The long term time series data that is outputted by CorRES could be used to inform the
quantities and combination of both short duration storage such as batteries and long du-
ration storage such as pumped hydro. Storage has an imperative role to play in facilitating
access to renewable energy at times when demand exceeds generation and to provide
ancillary services. However, storage is currently considered a risky investment due to the
current costs and uncertainty of returns and will likely require government incentives and
strategies to ensure they are sized and located optimally.

5.5.6
Green Hydrogen
Wind to load modelling can be greatly extended from the high level examples presented
in this work. A natural next step would be the connection to economic analysis when the
price can be quantified in terms of a levelized cost of green hydrogen ($/kg). This requires
assumptions on the end product, whether it be gaseous hydrogen for domestic purposes
or a hydrogen vector for overseas export. Variables involved in the plant design, sizing,
transport and storage requirements would determine the capital cost and conversion effi-
ciency (kWh/kg). Operational cost would be greatly influenced by the cost to provide the
power to operate the electrolysers. Here, understanding of the electricity markets is re-
quired to understand the benefit of offering a demand side service in reducing the energy
price, and ultimately determine if the reduced utilisation that is associated with that ser-
vice doesn’t compromise the benefit in price. It is likely that collaboration with the market
operator would be required to develop frameworks which support and incentivise this type
of participation, which currently does not exist.
It is acknowledged the examples provided in this work make assumptions on the ability
of electrolysers to ramp from their minimum to maximum loads. In reality, operating in
this way may involve degradation of their performance and may be limited to certain en-
velopes. Greater understanding of electrolyser operation, such as their ability to respond
and ramp in response to controller signals, is currently under investigation such as in
studies conducted by NREL in [25].
Further strategies could be tested which involve using storage to provide firming in order
to achieve more granular green supply to demand matching so that no grid imports are
required. Alternatively, the benefits of connecting a wind generation facility directly to a
hydrogen electrolyser load, which would reduce electrical losses and the delivered cost
of power, could also be investigated.
It is clear that a number of options exist in combating the current challenges to the grid
that present with increasing penetration of VRE resources, all which introduce complex-
ity compared to traditional fossil fuel generation. Ultimately, effective implementation will
require sophisticated and reliable modelling in design and operation in order to maintain
stable and economic generation, and that which is presented in this work is but one ex-
ample.

6
Conclusion
This study has quantified the accuracy of the CorRES tool when used to simulate wind
energy generation in Western Australia for nine individual wind farms connected to the
grid. It has shown individual performance is well matched on a daily and seasonal basis
and performance improves when modelling the aggregate of all locations’ outputs. The
tool can therefore be used to understand generation patterns at new locations and with
known limitations. The ability to capture high frequency power ramps is limited due to
the spatio-temporal resolution of the ERA5 data set being unable to capture small scale
variations.
When the CorRES methodology is used to simulate generation at new locations within
the envelope of the existing grid, new generation at all locations is shown to be comple-
mentary to existing solar and geographically diverse locations can also counter balance
existing wind generation. Development at these locations would better support grid sys-
tem stability enabling a higher VRE penetration compared to continued wind development
at existing locations. To further address the negative impacts of variable wind generation,
demand response can be introduced. Operational strategies for flexible hydrogen elec-
trolyser loads powered by CorRES simulated generation and firming can be considered in
the design of new plants. If the dynamics of this operation are understood, the impact to
the existing market can be optimised in order to support the development of a new hydro-
gen industry in Australia while supporting new renewable generation and decarbonisation
of the grid.
