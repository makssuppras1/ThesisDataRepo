Abstract
A flood risk analysis was completed for the Pottor community in Freetown, Sierra Leone.
Hourly historical rain rate data was retrieved from the ERA5 global reanalysis data from
Copernicus for the data point located at Freetown.
The rain rate data was utilised to calculate design storms for various return periods and
storm durations using frequency analysis with the Extreme Value Type I distribution method.
The design storms were used to derive IDF curves for each return period. Hyetographs
were calculated using the alternating block method to prepare a rainfall timeseries. Iden­
tified dates of historical storms were classified by return period. Additional design storms
and hyetographs were derived for a climate change scenario.
A MIKE21 flood model was prepared for the study area watershed. The 2­year, 12­hour
storm scenario was defined as the base scenario. Additional model scenarios were run
for each return period. A roughness scenario applied a lower roughness than the base
scenario. The results of the model runs were validated using community survey data by
comparing the locations of reported flooding to the water depth results. The results of the
various scenarios were examined to identify the sensitivity of the resulting water depth
and speed due to each parameter. The influence of storm intensity on flooding extent for
more extreme events was limited by the geography of the study area. Storm intensity had
low influence. The roughness coefficient had a high influence on water speed, especially
within observed flow paths.
Mapping of the community was extracted from OpenStreetMap, which creates open­
source GIS data using manual tracing of aerial images by public contributors. The ex­
posure analysis overlaid the flood extent results with GIS data of the community to de­
termine the amount of structures impacted by each storm event. Seven major flow paths
were identified which run through residential areas and divide the low­lying portion of the
community.
The vulnerability analysis identified key factors that increase the probability of extreme
losses for households, including type of structure, being disabled or elderly, and level of
income.
Recommendations were provided for potential objectives for future projects to increase
resilience and mitigate flood risk in the community. These recommendations include
gathering local rainfall data, improving mapping of the community, identifying critical
pathways, reinforcing pathways, monitor the main drainage outlet, mitigate danger from
major flow paths, and identifying vulnerable members of the community.
Flood maps and a guide for downloading the ERA5 rain rate data are provided for use by
stakeholders.

Introduction
Freetown, the capital of Sierra Leone, is located on a peninsula on the Western coast of the
country. It is the largest city in the country with an estimated population of over 1 million.
(The World Bank 2018) Increasing urbanization, especially in Africa, has been driving up
the populations in cities. This leads to increased population density and the development
of additional informal settlements, which are often located in areas near waterways or
around drainage channels. This trend, coupled with the predicted increase of extreme
rainfall events due to climate change (UNDP 2010b), results in an increasingly vulnerable
population of residents in areas with few resources to take preventative action.
The western half of Sierra Leone is relatively flat, but the Freetown peninsula has a dis­
tinct, dramatic geography that leads to complex and varying rainfall patterns in the area.
A tall mountain range runs along the length of the peninsula causing drastic changes in
topography through the city. These geographical features near the shoreline cause a vari­
ance of precipitation in the area between lower and higher elevations, as well as on the
western and eastern sides of the mountain range. The branching ridges of this range add
to the amount of steep slopes near areas of residences. When these areas experience high­
intensity storm events, the steep topography causes rapid moving runoff which results in
flash floods with little time for warning. The hazards of these floods were seen follow­
ing the culmination of a month of high amounts of rainfall on August 14th, 2017, when
the rain and runoff caused a catastrophic landslide that resulted in fatalities and displaced
about 6,000 people. (The World Bank 2017)
Members from Engineers Without Borders – Denmark (EWB­DK) have been operating
within Freetown, Sierra Leone since 2017, when a project was completed to connect new
water taps following the devastating landslide. EWB­DK’s Project 119 continues to work
with the affected community of the August 2017 landslide specifically to build the cli­
mate resilience of the community through efforts to introduce education and installations
to prevent such an extreme level of damages from future storms. Previous measures im­
plemented within Project 119 include community engagement, capacity building, con­
struction of a drainage channel, waste management, tree planting for slope stabilization,
and gathering survey data from residents. This study was completed to contribute to the
information available to the EWB­DK team as they determine future projects to benefit
the community.

1.1
Study Objective
This purpose of this study is to complete a flood risk analysis case study for the Pottor
community in Freetown, Sierra Leone. The study will use hourly rainfall data and explore
the potential of using global datasets for locations with limited historical precipitation
measurements available for hazard assessments. Design storms and hyetographs will be
calculated based on the rainfall data to provide inputs for flood modeling. Open­source
mapping tools will be used to estimate the layout within the community to determine the
potential exposure for future storm events. Classification of previous identified flooding
events will be compared to modelled flood maps and validated using qualitative survey
data. Recommendations will be made of considerations for future risk mitigation efforts.

2.2
Flood Risk Analysis
Disaster risk assessments are a familiar process to international organizations, and their
importance is only increasing as concerns grow over the increasing frequency of extreme
climate events. Humanitarian efforts can be seen annually in the world news to offer aid in
response to devastation in vulnerable communities. However, providing the resources for
communities to understand the risks that are specifically prevalent in their region allows
for the opportunity to prepare through community organizing and implementing infras­
tructure to manage the effects of extreme weather events. (UNDP 2010a) In areas such
as Freetown that experience high amounts of precipitation, a flood risk assessment can
quantify the potential damages from extreme events and understand the expected extent
of flooding to inform decisions made regarding preventative measures. While the main
concepts of risk assessments remain constant, the terminology and specific components
included in the methodology can differ. This study will focus on the three major compo­
nents of a risk assessment: hazard, exposure and vulnerability.
The flood hazard assessment is used to characterize the weather events expected at dif­
ferent probabilities. The assessment requires hydrological analysis that determines the
likelihood and intensity of precipitation in the area. (UNDP 2010a) The analysis utilised
available precipitation data to identify rainfall rates for extreme storm events and eleva­
tion data enabled flood modeling software to calculate the expected runoff during input
rainfall patterns, the likely routes of water flow, and the locations of potential pooling of
runoff. The final outcome of the flood hazard assessment are flood maps, which define
areas of flooding in several zones based on the probability and depth of flooding.
The exposure assessment identifies the assets threatened by the derived hazard. (UNDP
2010a) Risk from a storm hazard comes from movement of water and flooding. Due to the
presence of informal settlement within the study area, it is difficult to obtain official data
of the location of buildings in the area. Therefore, the mapping of buildings and other key
infrastructure relied on the use of open­source mapping tools (mainly derived from aerial
photos) and any available field data from EWB­DK team members. In order to distinguish
which assets qualify within risk exposure, the compiled mapping of community assets
were overlaid with the produced flood maps. The extent of flooding depicted in the flood
maps was validated using survey data reported by residents.
The vulnerability assessment quantifies the potential losses that will result from the haz­
ard. Losses that are included in this analysis can vary from physical, economic, and in­
tangible damages. Physical damages refer to damage done to structures, infrastructure,
or possessions. Economic losses include potential earnings that are lost due to disruption
to businesses and costs of aid provided. Intangible damages can include health effects,
stresses, emotional strife, and inconveniences experienced by the residents of the com­
munity. (World Bank Group 2015) In disaster risk assessments, it is common to equate
expected losses to monetary amounts in order to provide a common unit for comparison
and understanding of the impact of the hazard. However, due to lack of information of
property costs or quantification of intangible losses, the scope of this study was limited to
identifying major factors that increase vulnerability within the population.
The resulting maps and estimations will identify the areas in the community that face the
greatest threat of flooding. This information will be used to provide recommendations on
the prioritization of future project objectives for mitigation efforts.

Hazard
Hourly precipitation data, including extensive historical data, was required for the calcula­
tions that result in the needed rainfall data for flood modeling. Due to a lack of long­term
observed rainfall measurements in the region, a global dataset was used for calculations.
Reanalysis data combines observed data with model data and in order to produce a com­
plete global dataset. It is periodically updated using the most recent observed values.
Although at a poorer resolution, it provides a continuous, hourly dataset for the study lo­
cation that includes several decades in the past. (Copernicus 2018) The retrieved dataset
was compared to available rainfall data for the study area to determine the correlation.
3.1
Copernicus ERA5 Data
ERA5 data was requested from the Copernicus database for Large Scale Rain Rate (LSRR).
LSRR is a parameter used to estimate the rate of rainfall at a location on the Earth’s sur­
face. It utilises the ECMWF Integrated Forecasting System (IFS) to calculate large­scale
precipitation based on changes in atmospheric conditions. The unit for LSRR is equiva­
lent to millimeters per second and reports rates as an average as spread equally over the
area. (Copernicus 2018)
ERA5 data is produced on a reduced Gaussian grid. When it is requested in the NetCDF4
format, it is interpolated into a regular lat/long grid. The grid has a resolution of 0.25°.
Therefore, the data points are centered on latitude and longitude lines every 0.25°. The
approximate coordinates for the Pottor community are 8°26’36.83”N, 13°16’23.80”W.
The data point nearest to the study location is located at 8°25’N, 13°15’W, designated
as point Freetown (F), as shown in Figure 3.1. The point is located just off the northern
coast of Freetown. It is approximately 6.7 km from the study location. The data was
downloaded in a 4x6 grid for point F and further eastward to span over the land. There is
a spatial distance of 28 km between grid points. (Hennerman and Guillory 2021)
Figure 3.1: Grid of ERA5 data points from Copernicus
The challenging topography and variance of rainfall in the peninsula must be taken into
account when modelling with data that is not of high resolution, and it must be determined

3.1.2
Rainfall Patterns
The cumulative annual precipitation for each point was calculated to determine the total
volume of precipitation estimated at each point by the data. The rain rate was first con­
verted to millimeters per hour. Because the data represents the average amount of rainfall,
it was assumed that this rain rate was experienced for the entire hour time step and the data
was multiplied by 1 hour to report total millimeters. The hourly data in millimeters was
then aggregated by sum for each year to result in cumulative annual precipitation.
Figure 3.4: Cumulative annual precipitation values
In Figure 3.4, it can be seen that the pattern of rainfall is quite similar for all four points, but
point F and N fairly consistently have a higher magnitude of cumulative rainfall throughout
the time period.
Figure 3.5: Rain rate plots for each reference historical storm event
In order to determine if the data for point F is appropriately representative of the rainfall
patterns observed in Freetown and Pottor, the rain rate data was plotted for shorter periods
of time that experienced known storm events. These storm events include the disastrous
August 2017 storm that garnered international attention and flooding during July 14th,
2020, which was identified by EWB­DK. When examining the rain rates for these shorter
periods, point F does appear to reflect higher intensity rain patterns during the time period
of these events. This indicated that the data point may be representative of the rainfall
patterns in Pottor.

3.2
Design Storms
Design storms were calculated using the selected ERA5 data in order to estimate represen­
tative design storm events for the Pottor location. These design storms are precipitation
depth values which are needed to calculate resulting rates of flow that are then transformed
into flow routes and volumes by the flood modeling software. These design storms use
rainfall patterns from historical data and the typically seen magnitudes of rainfall for a
location to derive an approximate storm event for each return period. (Chow, Maidment,
and Mays 1988a) A return period, given in units of time, is used to describe the probability
that a storm of the given magnitude would occur on any given year at the location. For
example, a 1­year storm event describes rainfall that would be expected every year in a
location, while a 100­year storm event would represent a much more extreme storm event
that is rarely seen. Design storms were calculated for a typical range of return periods
within the study period of 40 years: 1­ 2­, 5­, 10­, 20­year storm events.
First, the hourly rainfall data (in mm) was sorted to identify the hours during the data
period that had the largest amount of estimated rainfall (Appendix A.2). When using
consistent historical data to derive design storms, a frequency analysis is conducted which
uses statistics to determine the magnitude for the desired probability, or return period. The
maximum hourly rainfall was identified for each year during the data period of 1979 to
2020 (Appendix A.3). These maximum annual values were then used to calculate design
storm values for different return periods using the extreme value distribution method for
Extreme Value Type I (EVI), which is the typical type for use in rainfall frequency analysis
(Chow, Maidment, and Mays 1988b). The mean, ¯x, and standard deviations, s, values used
in the equations were derived from the maximum annual rainfall values. The equations
used in this method are seen below:
Figure 3.10: Frequency analysis equations for Extreme Value Type I distribution (Chow,
Maidment, and Mays 1988b)
Using these equations, each design storm (1­, 2­, 5­, 10­, 20­year) event’s resulting pre­
cipitation depth, xT, was calculated. The equations are able to calculate return periods

3.4
Design Hyetographs
A hyetograph is a graph which depicts rainfall intensity over time increments through the
duration of a storm. A design hyetograph provides an input for flood modeling software
which reflects how the amount of rain falling changes during a storm. (Chow, Maidment,
and Mays 1988a) In order to develop design hyetographs for differing storm frequencies
and durations, the alternating block method was used. For each desired storm, a table
was prepared for each incremental step through the duration. The IDF curve for the given
frequency was used to estimate the intensity for the current duration. Each time step was
treated as a storm of incrementally increasing duration to determine the associated rainfall
intensity. Then, the cumulative rainfall depth was calculated by multiplying the intensity
by the duration. The incremental depth was determined by subtracting the cumulative
rainfall of a time step by that of the previous time step. This determines how much more
rain would be expected for the additional amount of time. Due to the inverse relationship
between intensity and duration as seen by the IDF Curve, the resulting incremental rainfall
depths are ranked in descending order as the storm time progresses. The alternating block
method assumes that the moment of most intense rainfall occurs during the half point of
the design storm event. Therefore, the greatest rainfall intensity is assigned to the middle
time step. Then, each next highest rainfall intensity is assigned to adjacent time steps while
alternating before and after the middle peak. An example calculation table and resulting
hyetograph for the 2­year, 2­hour storm can be seen below.

3.7.3
Time of Concentration
The Time of Concentration is a watershed parameter that estimates the amount of time
it takes for a drop of water to flow the farthest distance from the top of the watershed to
the outlet. This value of time influences when the watershed experiences peak discharge,
because it describes how long it takes for water flow to arrive at the outlet from each area
of the watershed. (Corbitt 1999)
The FAA (U.S. Federal Aviation Administration) equation is a commonly used method for
calculating the Time of Concentration that utilizes a Rational Runoff Coefficient to factor
in ground cover type. The FAA equation is shown below:
t = 1.8(1.1 −c)L1∕2∕100S1∕3
where t = Time of Concentration in minutes, c = Rational Runoff Coefficient, L = length
of the longest flow path of the watershed, and S = average slope of the flow path (deter­
mined using the elevation at the start of the flow path and outlet of the watershed). The
Rational Runoff Coefficient chosen was 0.3 to fit the ranges given for both residential
and unimproved areas to account for the bare slopes of the mountain range as well as the
residential areas at the lower elevations. (Corbitt 1999) The Time of Concentration was
calculated for both the longest flow path in the watershed as well as the longest path which
directly intersects the Pottor community. These flow paths can be seen below in Figure
3.22.

3.7.4
Influence of Return Period
Design storms with longer returns periods input greater cumulative rain depth during a
storm event, including a higher peak rainfall intensity. The higher intensity has the po­
tential of greater flood depth as more water is flowing through the watershed’s drainage
routes and may overwhelm the capacity of these channels.
Additional scenarios of each return period (1­, 5­, 10­, and 20­year) were completed to
determine the influence of return period and a provide a comprehensive flood risk analysis
for the stakeholders.
3.7.5
Influence of Roughness
The model was run with two different values of surface roughness coefficient to deter­
mine the influence of roughness on the flooding extent. The MIKE21 program includes
a roughness parameter using a Manning number. The first value used was a standard
roughness value used for MIKE21 modelling, Manning number (M) = 32, which has cor­
responding Manning n­value of 0.03. The model was then run with M = 20, or n = 0.05.
Both Manning numbers are similar to values assigned in situations with uneven, unim­
proved ground without vegetation. (Engman 1986) The Manning number was lowered
to simulate the roughness caused by obstructions such as buildings, property, and waste
that may be cluttering the drainage ways within the community. A Manning number of
20 is considered the lower limit used for the parameter. Considering the short Time of
Concentration, it is expected that the water flow through the watershed is relatively fast
and a change in roughness may have limited effects on the resulting maximum flood depth
and extent depending on how well the water can drain from the watershed’s outlets.

4
Exposure & Vulnerability
The risk associated with hazards is derived from the presence of people within the affected
area. The space where people intersect with the hazard constitutes the level of exposure
due to a hazardous event, and the ability (or inability) of the people to cope with or mitigate
the damages caused by a hazard determines their vulnerability.
4.1
Exposure
The level of exposure reported by this study was focused on the areas within the study
area that experienced significant impacts from the storm event in the form of flooding and
fast­moving water.
4.1.1
Impacted structures
GIS data of existing structures in the area was exported from OpenStreetMap. Open­
StreetMap is an open­source online mapping platform that builds maps based on data
added by public contributors. (OpenStreetMap 2021) This data can come from existing
sources, gathered GPS data, or manual inputs based on aerial photos. For the Freetown
area, the majority of the information comes from aerial tracing.
There was an estimation of about 600 structures within Pottor. This was likely an un­
derestimation due to the manual input of the data utilising available aerial photos that are
not high­quality. This was especially true when identifying informal structures as they
tend to use similar roofing materials and are clustered closely together, which can make
them difficult to differentiate. It was expected that there are more clustered homes in the
low­lying area, within the area that experiences more flooding.

4.2
Vulnerability
The residents of Pottor experience physical and nonphysical damages following flooding
in the community. These damages include damage to households and businesses, loss of
belongings, losses from the inability to work, sickness, emotional distress, and physical
injury. (Engineers Without Borders ­ Denmark 2020) Various factors and characteristics
of households can increase the vulnerability of residents by influencing the level of im­
pact experienced, ability to prevent damages, or potential to recover after experiencing
damages.
Figure 4.6: Concrete structure in Pottor during July 2020 flooding
4.2.1
Residential Structures
The type of structure where a family lives can influence the minimum depth of water
that is needed to impact a family by infiltrating their home. More permanent structure,
such as those made of concrete or bricks, provide protection from water flow as long as
entrances are secured. More informal structures that do not provide an enclosed barrier
around the entire structure are liable to let in water if they lie at ground level. According to
a Baseline Survey conducted by EWB­DK in 2020, 57% of the 277 residents interviewed
live in homes made from metal sheeting, while 39% live in homes made from concrete or
bricks.

4.2.2
Vulnerable residents
Individual residents in the community can have increased vulnerability if they suffer from
chronic conditions or have difficulty with physical activity. For example, elderly resi­
dents or those with disabilities may not be able to evacuate a residence or react to perform
preventative measures. 11% of the residents in Pottor are over 50 years old, and 27 of
the residents interviewed for the Baseline Survey indicated that they have someone with
a disability in their household.
4.2.3
Low income
According to the Baseline Survey, 30% of the interviewees from Pottor were unemployed,
and 44% either had seasonal or inconsistent employment. Residents with no stable income
source or low income can be vulnerable to damages both due to the inability to purchase
materials to prevent damages and complete repairs promptly after damages have occurred.
If they are unable to recover from one major storm event, they can become even more
vulnerable if another storm event occurs while the residence is damaged. In extreme cases,
some households also lose their belongings, and consequently struggle to recover from the
losses due to a lack of savings or incoming salaries.

5
Discussion
The findings from the hazard, exposure, and vulnerability analyses were examined to pro­
vide the following observations and considerations on the analysis process and possible
recommendations for future project objectives in the community.
5.1
Uncertainties in hazard analysis
There were several uncertainties to be considered within the hazard analysis that were
the result of the data and methods used. The primary uncertainty was derived from the
inability to verify the original rainfall data utilised and disparities seen between estimates
of cumulative rainfall with available literature. However, the same disparity was not seen
when comparing cumulative rainfall for a single known extreme event. Still, the study has
provided a base of knowledge of the impacts of flooding in the area and the influence of
several key parameters in modeling.
The rainfall data used for flood modeling was calculated using IDF curves which were
derived using trendlines. The resulting trendlines did not have a high level of fit and may
have caused disparities in the hyetographs that were derived from them. Similarly, there
was differences observed in the cumulative rainfall of the hyetographs and the design
storms, although the disparity varied for different storm durations.
5.2
Scarcity of rainfall data
The lack of recorded rainfall data in the study region limits the ability to verify the accuracy
of the ERA5 rain rate data. Available information regarding rainfall in Sierra Leone is
limited to estimates on a large spatial scale and there is scarce reporting on local rainfall.
This includes the Freetown Peninsula, which has larger variance in rainfall amounts due
to its geographical features.
The SLMO does gather rainfall data at several weather stations within Freetown, but it
is on a monthly scale and not promptly available. This study and future analysis would
benefit from higher availability of rainfall data for calculation and validation. Building a
working partnership with SLMO may allow for the compilation of a dataset of rainfall data
from Freetown weather stations, even if only at the monthly scale. A basic understanding
of the variance in rainfall within the peninsula could be derived from measurements taken
at different locations during multiple storm events. Additionally, teaching residents to
read a rain gauge and take measurements could allow for a higher availability of data in
the future. Any expansion in the collective resources of observed rainfall in the area could
be used to improve the analysis and model.
5.3
Lack of community data
The ability to assess exposure is currently limited by the lack of mapping within the com­
munity. This has likely caused an underestimate of the amount of structures affected in the
high risk areas of the community. The current information was created manually using
aerial photos. The availability of a high­resolution aerial photo would already improve
48
Flood Risk Modeling and Analysis in Suburban Freetown, Sierra Leone
==PAGE:57==
the level of information, although it is not easy to acquire and would likely need to be
organised by the government or a large international organisation. Similar information
could also be gathered through field work using GPS equipment and further community
surveys.
5.4
Time of concentration
Due to the short amount of time for water to travel through the watershed, there is likely
not enough time to implement a warning system for residents in the low­lying areas. This
indicates that preventative measures that are permanent and do not need to be executed in
the moment are favourable. The speed at which the runoff moves through the system also
suggests the importance of maintenance of the community’s single main outlet through
the large highway culvert and other major flow paths. If the flow paths that experience
high volumes of water are clogged or overwhelmed, it is likely that a backup will cause
overflow and additional flooding quite quickly. Maintenance of these paths can include
organising waste collection and construction that increases capacity.
5.5
Influence of Return Period
The amount of flooding did expand with increasing return period and storm intensity, but
the difference decreases greatly for the more extreme storms. This is likely due to the
topography of the area, which contains the pooling water in the low­lying portion of the
community. Increasing storm intensity also had little effect on the water speed in the study
area.
5.6
Influence of Roughness
Roughness had a greater influence on water speed than the storm intensity. However,the
roughness in the more crowded residential areas is high and would be difficult to change
through project implementation without affecting households. This is a greater concern
along the major drainage flow paths or at higher elevations in the watershed, where a
lower roughness is probable. The largest increase in water speed occurred along major
flow paths. The water speed along these paths could reach dangerous levels.
5.7
Climate Change Scenario
The influence of climate change in this scenario seemed limited, likely due to the lower
sensitivity of storm intensity on flooding extent. The 10­year storm simulated for the sce­
nario reflected the intensity and results of the 20­year event in the present. The increasing
frequency of storm events is likely to have a larger effect on the community if there is not
an increased resilience to flooding.
5.8
Additional storm characteristics
Additional characteristics of storm events may have influence on the risk within the com­
munity but were not considered in this study. These factors include the impact of multiple
storms occurring in a short time period. If a storm event occurs quickly after another, it is
possible that the damages could be even greater because the community has not had time
to recover from the first event. The effects of these storms could also build on each other
to create greater impact. For example, although the storm in August 2017 is known to
Flood Risk Modeling and Analysis in Suburban Freetown, Sierra Leone
49
==PAGE:58==
have caused devastation, it was classified as a 2­year storm event in the analysis while the
storm in July 2020 was classified as a 5­year storm event. However, it was reported that
the region experienced extensive rainfall, estimated at over 1,000 mm, during the month
leading up to the 2017 event. (UNDP 2017) Reoccurring rainfall over a longer period of
time could cause erosion and destabilise slopes along the mountain range, leading to a
landslide.
5.9
Identifying the most vulnerable
In the event of a storm that requires evacuation by residents of the community, it could
be beneficial to identify households and individuals that are especially vulnerable in these
situations. Surveying the community to identify elderly residents and those with physical
disabilities who would have difficulty moving within the community could inform efforts
to prioritise sending aid during extreme events or checking on households following storm
events.
It may also be possible to identify households which have a higher likelihood of damages
due to the type and condition of the residential structure. Knowledge of preventative
measures or the distribution of supplies for those most likely to experience losses could
prioritise efforts for the largest impact.
5.10
Increasing mobility
In the exposure analysis, several main flow paths were identified and it appeared that the
majority of them flow through residential areas and not along established routes. These
flow paths should be investigated to validate their locations and determine whether it is
possible to implement measures to mitigate their effects on the residents. Possible inter­
ventions may include redirecting the flow along a constructed drainage channel or building
barriers to protect effected structures. These flow paths also seem to divide the residential
areas at the lower elevation area of the community. The low­lying portions of Pottor have
limited access points, and therefore these flow paths may prevent residents from evacu­
ating if needed. Outlining ’evacuation plans’ with residents could identify those likely to
be isolated and identify points where crossings of these flow paths could be critical. Mo­
bility in these situations could be improved by reinforcing pathways or installing drainage
culverts to allow for navigation during flooding events.

6
Conclusion
This thesis analysed the flood risk for the Pottor community in Freetown, Sierra Leone.
A global reanalysis dataset was utilised to calculate design storms for flood modeling.
The results of the flood modeling for various scenarios identified the level of influence
of various parameters on water depth and speed during storm events. Flood maps were
created for each return period and varying flooding depths to identify high risk areas. A
guide has been provided for stakeholders to download ERA5 rain rate data.
The study findings resulted in the following observations regarding the hazard analysis
within the study area:
• Increasing storm intensity causes expansion of flooding for 1­, 2­, and 5­year storm
events but influence of larger storm events is limited due to the local topography.
Storm intensity has little influence on maximum water speeds.
• Water is calculated to move through the watershed in under 1 hour due to steep
slopes.
• Roughness has large influence over water speed, especially within identified major
flow paths which can reach hazardous speeds. It has little influence over maximum
water depths.
• Increased intensity of rainfall for the Climate Change Scenario have little impact
for effects of single storm event, but increased frequency could increase damages
due to less time for recovery
• Additional storm characteristics, such as time between consecutive storm events,
should be considered but are not included in this analysis
Furthermore, the following recommendations are provided for future project objectives in
the community:
• Gather rainfall data from local authorities and community measurements
• Improve mapping of community using high­quality aerial or field survey
• Monitor condition of highway culvert outlet and major flow paths to avoid clogging
by organising waste collection and increasing capacity of channels
• Redirect or reinforce major flow paths by constructing barriers or drainage channels
• Survey community to identify structures in poor condition in high risk areas and
provide repairs or preventative measures
• Survey community to identify elderly or disabled residents who are more vulnerable
during extreme events
• Collaborate with residents to outline ’evacuation plan’ to identify areas at risk of
isolation and critical evacuation routes
• Strengthen pathways using drainage culverts or paving to increase mobility

import numpy as np

# Compile time variables from each data file
def comp_nc_time(d1,d2,d3,d4):
t1 = d1.variables["time"]
t2 = d2.variables["time"]
t3 = d3.variables["time"]
t4 = d4.variables["time"]
time1 = np.array(t1[:])
time2 = np.array(t2[:])
time3 = np.array(t3[:])
time4 = np.array(t4[:])
time = np.concatenate((time1,time2,time3,time4))
return time

def extract_nc(d,variable):

return x

# Convert time values from netCDF4 into datetime format
def nc2date(t,unit):
length = np.size(t)
date_nc = nc.num2date(t[:], unit)
startdate_str = date_nc[0].strftime("%Y-%m-%d %H:%M:%S")
startdate =
datetime.datetime.strptime(startdate_str, '%Y-%m-%d
%H:%M:%S')
date = [startdate + datetime.timedelta(hours=h) for h in
range(length)]
return date
# Set dataframe index to datetime
def set_dtindex(df):
datetime_series = pd.to_datetime(df['Date'])
datetime_index = pd.DatetimeIndex(datetime_series.values)
df2 = df.set_index(datetime_index)
return df2
