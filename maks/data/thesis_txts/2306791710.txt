Abstract  
 
An innovative device named passive flux meter (PFM) has been developed at the 
University of Florida for measuring groundwater and contaminant flux 
simultaneously at risky waste sites recent years.  Many studies from experiments 
and field-tests indicates that PFM could work effectively for measurements of 
groundwater flow in different magnitudes, and the evaluation of groundwater 
direction as well. However, the performance of the PFM under different 
hydrological parameters is still unclear. Thus, a PFM model to learn more about 
PFM behaviors is established in the software COMSOL Multiphysics.  In this work, 
the reliability of the model was verified by three model tests to guarantee the 
model can simulate groundwater flow and the tracer transportation is 
trustworthy and accurate.  
 
In this project, not only the basic functionalities of the PFM can be achieved, but 
also some hydrological parameters would be quantified depending on the 
generated relationship Darcy flux graph and the residual mass of tracers. 
 
Moreover, PFMs with a combination of multiple tracers were verified to work 
effectively for measuring groundwater with broad magnitudes and detecting its 
directions as well. Since the functionalities of the PFM might not be guaranteed 
under extreme groundwater flow, thus PFM devices could only be applied under 
some proper circumstances. From the perspective of groundwater flux 
measurement, the valid range of hydraulic conductivity of the aquifer (Kmat) for 
the PFM with multiple tracers is 10-6 [m/s]to 1[m/s], while the working range of 
hydraulic conductivity of the PFM matrix (Kpfm) is 10-5 [m/s] to 10-3 [m/s]. As for 
groundwater direction detection, available Kmat and Kpfm ranges for PFM with a 
similar design are 10-6 – 1 [m/s] and 10-5 - 10-2 [m/s], respectively. Referring to 
the porosity of the aquifer (nmat), its impacts on PFM performance differ a lot in 
diffusion-dominated systems and advection-dominated ones. The remaining 
amount of tracers goes down if a high porosity due to the high molecule diffusion 
in a diffusion-dominated system, but in an advection-dominated system, the 
amount of remained tracers increases under the same conditions. Actually, the 
porosity of PFM sorbent (npfm)has more complicated impacts on PFM, for example, 
with a growing npfm, multiple effects from the retardation, diffusion and advection

could lead to a slightly rising trend to the remaining amount of the tracer methanol 
and ethanol in a diffusion-dominated system, while the residual mass of the tracer 
TBA and DMP falls consistently in advection-dominated systems. In addition, 
regardless of the design of PFM, the PFM with less initial amount of tracers would 
always have a lower working exposure period.

1. Introduction   
 
Over last few decades, the urgency for quantifying contaminant mass flux in 
groundwater has increased significantly owing to the acceleration of soil 
remediation activities. Groundwater and contaminant fluxes measurement were 
frequently utilized for predicting risk, compliance and contaminant attenuation by 
groundwater hydrologists to define the boundary conditions of subsurface 
contaminant plumes and source locations (Einarson, & Mackay, 2001) (Annable , 
et al., 2005). However, spatial variation of both contaminant concentration and 
groundwater flows results in mass flow variations that might range over several 
orders of magnitudes, thus it is difficult to get accurate estimation of subsurface 
contaminant mass fluxes if only with the help of ordinary field data. (Hatfield, et 
al., 2004). Notwithstanding the variability, contaminant mass fluxes could still be 
evaluated by using computed groundwater fluxes and depth-averaged 
concentrations gathered from wells based on recent studies (Hatfield, et al., 2004). 
 
Currently, two primary methods are applied for simultaneously estimating 
contaminant discharge and groundwater fluxes. The first one is applied to derive 
data from spatial integration of local flux-averaged contaminant concentration 
and water flux obtained from transects of a single or multilevel monitoring wells 
(Borden et al,1997) (King et al,1999). This method has been widely used in the 
device of multilevel sampler (MLS) transects. The other method was developed 
for integral groundwater investigations based on research from Holder et al. 
(1998), Schwarz et al. (1998), Teutsch et al. (2000) and Bockelmann et al. (2001, 
2003), which is referred to as “integral groundwater investigation method” (IGIM). 
This approach could help measure the contaminant fluxes by the contaminant 
concentration histories, not only from the vertical cross-section of partial or 
complete captures of contaminant plume, but also from the cross-sectional area of 
the aquifer interrogated by measured ambient groundwater flux and well flow 
rate (Hatfield, et al., 2004). This method is currently applied to the technique 
known as “integral pump test” (IPT). Nowadays, techniques of MLS and IPT are 
the classic standard approaches available for groundwater flux evaluation when 
they are utilized for contaminant treatment in groundwater.  
 
==PAGE:18==
16 
 
However, MLS and IPT are representatives of short-term evaluations as their 
results can only reflect current conditions and not for long-term performances. In 
the absence of continuous monitoring, a concept regarding cumulative 
groundwater flux was proposed and recognized as a compromising technique 
whose results can reflect long-term transport condition (Hatfield, et al., 2004). 
Hereafter, Hatfield and Annable developed and tested a new method which can 
measure the various magnitudes of groundwater and contaminant fluxes in the 
porous media simultaneously (Annable , et al., 2005).  Thus, a new device named 
as Passive Flux Meter (PFM) was introduced to fulfill the purpose of cumulative 
groundwater flux measurements.  
 
The PFM is a self-contained permeable device inserted into a well or borehole. The 
interior of PFM is composed of a matrix of sorbents which can retain the dissolved 
contaminants presented in the groundwater (Kirk et al, 2002). Contaminant 
concentrations intercepted by the sorbent can be integrated to compute the total 
mass of the contaminant afterwards. Meanwhile, the sorbent matrix is 
impregnated with a predefined one or multiple soluble resident tracers. Resident 
tracers are used to quantify the accumulation groundwater flux as the soluble 
tracers are leached from the sorbent matrix at the rates proportional to the fluid 
flux (Kirk et al, 2002). Therefore, PFM achieves the purpose of measuring the 
contaminant and groundwater fluxes simultaneously.  When applying this new 
technique, PFM will be placed into the borehole for a while and get exposure to 
the groundwater required to be monitored or purified. After some exposure time, 
the dissolved contaminants and groundwater fluxes will be quantified through 
experiments by extracting the sorbents of the PFM.   
 
Moreover, PFM has many advantages when compared with other similar devices. 
Primarily, gathering cumulative measures of groundwater flow and contaminant 
mass flow is more cost effective for long-term evaluation. Secondly, PFM is 
identified as the only in situ time-integrating passive sampling device that allows 
the simultaneous measurements of contaminant fluxes and Darcy groundwater 
flux (Verreydt, et al., 2012). Finally, because PFM can directly measure local mass 
fluxes rather than contaminant concentrations, PFM contains less error potential 
than techniques like MLS and IPT (Hatfield, et al., 2004).  
 
 
==PAGE:19==
17 
 
Besides the above advantages that PFM can directly measure contaminate flux and 
different magnitudes of Darcy flux effectively and economically, PFM can also be 
implemented to groundwater direction evaluation.  A Tracer test is typically 
applied as a classic method for groundwater direction evaluation, but at the risk 
of not finding some tracers in the observation wells that become located too far 
away (Thierrin, et al., 1995). Some techniques that are based on the theory of 
thermal perturbation, which are the “heat-pulse flow meter” and the “acoustic 
dropper velocimetry” (ADV), have been introduced recently. However, it has been 
said by the US Department of the Interior that both devices still need more study 
for reliability on directional measurement, because they cannot directly measure 
water direction but instead suspended particles in the water column of a borehole 
(US Department of the Interior., 1999).  Under this condition, PFM is referred to 
as an alternative to the previously mentioned devices and it is developed to 
simultaneously measure the direction and the magnitudes of groundwater by the 
tracer transportation (Harald , et al., 2007).  
 
Although most field-tests and experiments have been applied to study the 
behavior of PFM over the last decades, there is still a lack of sufficient data about 
the behavior of PFM in various hydrography. Thus, a numerical model of PFM, 
which could get results about the performance of PFM quickly and economically, 
is required to simulate the performance of a PFM in different situations. In this 
project, the model was built using the software COMSOL Multiphysics, which is 
developed by the company of COMSOL.  
 
COMSOL Multiphysics is a platform for simulation and physics-based model, with 
many add-on products, the simulation platform can be expanded furtherly with 
dedicated physics interfaces and tools for the application in the fields of electrical, 
mechanical, fluid flow and chemicals. (COMSOL Lnc. , 2016) In the Model builder, 
the model tree gives users accesses to all functionalities, where the geometry, 
physics settings, boundary conditions and studies of the groundwater modeling 
are arranged.

1.1 Aim 
In this project, the tracer leaching from PFM for groundwater flux measurement 
and direction evaluation are assessed.  
 
In order to simulate the behavior of a PFM for groundwater flux measurement, the 
model of a PFM will be built in COMSOL Multiphysics and aims to fulfill several 
functions:  
 
 Establish a reliable model that can simulate the behavior of a PFM device 
precisely in COMSOL Multiphysics.  
 
 Study functionalities of PFM and evaluate how these functionalities could 
be achieved in the model.  
 
 Assessment of the performance of the tracer transportation when different 
PFM designs are applied (e.g. PFM with different sizes of inner PVC tube or 
PFM with different tracers.) 
 
 Assessment of effects of different hydrological factors such as hydraulic 
conductivity and porosity on the aquifer and PFM sorbent. 
 
 Evaluate the relationship of tracer transportation performances under 
different magnitudes of groundwater, as well as the relationship between 
groundwater direction and the tracer distribution after an exposure period.

2. Passive Flux Meter (PFM) Depiction 
 
2.1 Structure and Material of PFM 
Passive flux meter (PFM) is a self-contained permeable tube inserted into a 
monitoring borehole and the structure of PFM is illustrated in Figure 1. In this case, 
the device is assumed to be inserted into a screen borehole to ensure that 
groundwater flows through the borehole easily during the exposure, and the nylon 
mesh of the device is not destroyed when the PFM is pulled out after the exposure. 
Backfill, cement grout, gravel pack and the borehole screen should thus be 
installed into the screen borehole.  Regarding the PFM, from the outside to inside, 
the device consists of nylon mesh, sorbent matrix, rubber washer and inner PVC 
tube. The diameter of the PFM was designed to be 5cm and the end of the inner 
PVC tube is blocked to prevent water from flowing inside.  A cable, which is used 
to pull up the PFM after exposure to the groundwater for a while, is attached at 
the top of the inner PVC tube.   
 
Different parts of the device have various functions. The inner PVC tube of the 
device acts as a supporting backbone for the PFM in order to increase convenience 
of installation and retrieval. In this case, the diameter of the inner PVC tube is 
designed to be 1.5cm. As for the sorbent of the PFM, it is designed to measure the 
different magnitudes of groundwater and to absorb surrounding contaminants 
carried by the groundwater. In order to meet the requirement, the sorbent matrix 
is made of silver-impregnated activated carbon (SIAC), which is prepared by 
equilibrating with an aqueous solution of resident tracers. Here, this aqueous 
solution of tracers was mixed with dry active carbon at a constant volume to mass 
ratio of 2:1 (Annable , et al., 2005). In this case, methanol, ethanol, isopropyl 
alcohol (IPA), tert-butyl alcohol (TBA) and 2,4-dimenthyl -3-pentanol (DMP) are 
used as tracers for evaluating various magnitudes of groundwater. In addition, the 
sorbent matrix of PFM is divided into many 30-cm-long segments by a rubber 
washer for evaluating the differentiation of vertical groundwater flow (Verreydt, 
et al., 2012). In summary, characteristics of PFM are described in following table.

3. Methodology 
 
The last part gives us a general introduction about work theory of the PFM for 1-
D diffusion -advection model. However, how is the performance of the PFM in a 2-
D diffusion-advection system analyzed in this project? For this case, the 2-D model 
is established in COMSOL and how to build the model is described below.  
 
3.1 Model Set Up  
Previous studies of PFM based on experiments and field work have proved that 
PFM can directly measure groundwater flux effectively in all kinds of aquifers. 
However, how parameters of the hydrology, such as porosity and hydraulic 
conductivity of the terrain and the PFM sorbent affect the performance of the PFM 
is still unclear if only consideration of the experiments and field works are 
included.  Therefore, a model which can be applied for analyzing the behavior of 
the PFM device that is installed under different hydrology parameters was built.  
In this project, the 2-D model which consists of the PFM and its surrounding 
terrain is built in the software Multiphysics developed by the company COMSOL 
in the project.  
 
3.1.1 Equations Applied in the Model 
COMSOL is a finite element modeling program applied for solving a broad range 
partial differential equations (PDE), and with the flexibility of COMSOL allowing 
users to applying multiple PDEs within a model domain (e.g. groundwater flow 
and tracer transportation) (Li, et al., 2009) . In this case, in addition to generating 
equations about tracer transportation, relative equations from a basic library of 
predefined PDEs in COMSOL Multiphysics about convection/diffusion are used for 
the groundwater flow and tracer transportation simulation.  
 
The simulation of the model includes two primary steps: groundwater flow 
simulation and the movement of tracers. Since the terrain is assumed as a 
homogeneous domain with constant head gradient (dh/dx), the groundwater flow 
via the domain is simplified as single- phase stationary flow.

scenarios. Then, scenarios with different porosity will be ran later and the 
appropriate ranges of porosity for the aquifer and the PFM are different because 
normally the porosity of the aquifer should be smaller than that of the sorbent. 
Therefore, the range of porosity of aquifer is defined to be 0.2-0.5, but the range of 
porosity of the PFM is defined to be 0.4-0.9 for both the diffusion-dominated and 
advection-dominated systems.  
 
3.2 Uncertainty Analysis of Hydraulic Conductivity  
Random fields with non-constant hydraulic conductivity of aquifer (Kmat) are used 
to analyze the uncertainties generated by the variation of hydraulic conductivity. 
This is encouraged by a default random field generator, which is a package from a 
MATLAB file. In this file, the domain studied is defined to include 100 × 100 grids 
and each grid has a random hydraulic conductivity. Then after the conductivity 
field is generated, MATLAB can write the result into a txt file. Next, the generated 
txt file can be inserted into COMSOL and the hydraulic conductivity will be 
replaced by the generated conductivity fields in the model for further simulation.  
Additionally, the domain is enlarged to be 10× 10[𝑚]  in these simulations to 
ensure a reasonable distribution of different hydraulic conductivities.  
 
After the simulations with a series of conductivity fields are finished, data 
regarding the remaining amount of the tracer after an exposure can be created. 
Then, through this data, the remaining mass of tracers will be varied in a range 
under different realizations of various Kmat, which furtherly reflects the 
uncertainty caused by different conductivity fields. Moreover, in reality, the 
studied field might not have stable hydraulic conductivity. Thus, the simulation 
results can also give us some ideas of the PFM performance a real field.

5.1 Functionalities of the PFM 
In this case, the PFM model was designed to simulate two functions of the PFM, 
which are groundwater flux measurement and water direction detection. The 
groundwater flux measurement was based on the theory that the tracer mass loss 
is linearly proportional to the accumulative water intercepted by the PFM device, 
while the sector section approach is used for water direction detection. The 
simulation results are described in the following two parts separately.  
 
5.1.1 Quantify the Groundwater Flow  
As mentioned previously, the first advantage of the PFM is that it can directly 
measure groundwater flow. However, in this model, with predefined hydraulic 
conductivity of the aquifer and hydraulic gradient in the field, groundwater flow 
can be directly calculated by following the Darcy’s Law.  Hence, in the model, it 
cannot prove how to calculate groundwater flow by measuring the residual 
amount of tracers, but the performance of tracer transportation under different 
groundwater flow can be described. This can help us to quantify the groundwater 
flow in the field once the measured residual amount of tracers is obtained after an 
exposure time. In Figure 11, The relationship between Darcy flux and relative 
mass of remaining tracers is illustrated, and through the relationship graph, log 
value of groundwater flow (q0 [m3/d]) can be directly given by the corresponding 
relative remaining amount of tracers. After the q0 is obtained, hydrological 
parameter-hydraulic conductivity of aquifer (Kmat) can be furtherly quantified 
since Darcy flux can be directly calculated by Kmat.  Here, the simulation result of 
PFM with standard size of inner PVC tube (D = 1.5cm) is taken as the example to 
show how to quantify the value of different hydrology parameters by the 
relationship graph Figure 11, where the hydraulic head gradient is 0.01.

5.2.3 Hydrological Parameters. 
How the hydrological parameters affect the performance of the PFM is studied in 
this section? To meet the purpose, several designed scenarios were simulated by 
applying different hydraulic conductivity of the aquifer (Kmat), hydraulic 
conductivity of the sorbent (Kpfm), porosity of the aquifer (nmat) and porosity of the 
sorbent (npfm) in each case. The parameter studied will be changed based on the 
designing range from Table 4, while other parameters will be kept as the same as 
the values from Table 2.  
 
A. Simulation results under different Kmat.  
Following Darcy’s law, the groundwater flow can be computed, and the hydraulic 
conductivity of the matrix has a linear influence on the flow velocity. When the 
flow velocity is high in the field, the transportation of tracers will be advection-
dominated and the transportation will be the same with the water direction. 
However, if the flow velocity is low, tracer transportation will be diffusion 
dominated and tracers from high concentrations are prone to travel to the area 
with low concentration of the tracers. Moreover, because the hydraulic 
conductivity of the PFM and the aquifer are different, flow velocity in both areas 
will be different as well. The groundwater flow velocity difference will further lead 
to the convergence and divergence of the groundwater streamlines when 
groundwater flows close to the PFM borehole.  
 
Before showing the quantified relationship between the hydraulic conductivity of 
the aquifer and the tracer transportation, tracer distribution under different 
hydraulic conductivity after the fixed exposure time are illustrated in Figure 23.

decrease the flow velocity when the hydraulic conductivity of the aquifer is not 
very high, while secondly it will lead to higher values of diffusivity, which further 
increases the amount of tracers stored in the system. Thus, nmat should have an 
influence on tracer transportation in both advection-dominated system and 
diffusion-dominated system.

6. Discussion  
 
6.1 Tracer Selection  
Based on all the simulation results, behaviors of the PFM with different tracers are 
quite similar under different parameters, which means they have a similar tread 
when increasing the Kmat, Kpfm, nmat and npfm. Even though all tracers have similar 
variation trends, different quantization of the remaining amount of different 
tracers might lead to unsatisfactory behaviors of the tracers under some 
parameters. In this part, how to select the best tracer for a PFM to ensure good 
operation for groundwater measurement is discussed.  
 
6.1.1Hydraulic Conductivity 
Since the tracer transportation is in an advection-dispersion model, when water 
velocity changes due to the variation of Kmat and Kpfm, tracer transportation will be 
converted from diffusion-dominated to advection-dominated if the two hydraulic 
conductivities become larger and vice versa. Therefore, when Kmat or Kpfm are 
fixed, the tracer with the highest effectiveness in the transportation system can 
then could be selected. In order to explain how to choose more appropriate tracers 
when the value of Kmat is certain, the tracers methanol and ethanol are taken as 
examples in Figure 32. In subfigures (a), the tracer methanol works effectively 
when log values of Kmat are −5 and −4, but the transportation gradually changes 
to advection-dominated after log value of Kmat is larger than −3. In subfigures (b), 
transportation of the tracer ethanol is diffusion -dominated when log value of Kmat 
is −5 due to the lower residual amount variation during the exposure time, but it 
is advection-dominated when log value of Kmat is larger than −2, and it works well 
when log value of Kmat is −4 and −3 . 
 
==PAGE:76==
74 
 
 
Figure 32: Relative mass of residual methanol tracer (a) and ethanol tracer (b) under different 
value of Kmat when D is 1.5cm. 
 
The performance for different tracers under various Kmat tells which tracer could 
work the best for a terrain. For example, if hydraulic conductivity of the aquifer is 
assumed to be -4, although both of the tracers could work appropriately in the first 
15-day exposure, only the tracer ethanol can still work for the rest 6 days. Thus, 
the tracer ethanol should be selected for this terrain. However, supposing that Kmat 
of the terrain is -5, then the tracer methanol should be selected in this case since 
it has hugest amounts of the tracer variation. Besides, the tracer methanol and the 
tracer ethanol, the other three tracers can also be selected following the same 
approach which is described in Figure 51 of Appendix (3), and when the 
transportation of other tracers’ change from diffusion-dominated to advection-
dominated is also shown in Figure 51 of Appendix (3).  
 
Moreover, when the initial mass of the tracer decreases, the amount of remaining 
tracers will decrease as expected, and the influences of Kmat on different tracers 
will be different. Here, we would discuss the influences from Kmat on the tracer 
transportation with less initial mass, and the tracer methanol and tracer ethanol 
are taken as examples in Figure 33. It is clear in subfigure (a) that when the initial 
mass of the methanol tracer is 80% lower (D=4.5cm), it almost cannot work under 
all Kmat values. The reason for this situation is that when Kmat is 10-5 or 10-6, the 
remaining amount of methanol tracer is the same after the exposure; Besides, 
==PAGE:77==

tracer transports too fast when Kmat is bigger than 10-4. However, the tracer 
ethanol could work effectively when Kmat is 10-4 or 10-5.

value of Kmat when D is 4.5cm. 
 
To conclude, the transportation of the tracer will transfer from diffusion-
dominated to advection-dominated with an increasing Kmat. The tracer with higher 
retardation factor can work appropriately in high velocity groundwater, but if the 
initial mass of the tracer decreases, workable range of Kmat will be changed for the 
same tracer. Even if the range of Kmat is still workable when the initial mass of 
tracers is reduced, the workable time will be shorter.  
 
Furthermore, when groundwater flows in the borehole with the PFM installed, 
changes of hydraulic conductivity of the sorbent (Kpfm) can also lead the changing 
of tracer transportation condition. Tracer transportation on the PFM sorbent will 
be diffusion-dominated when Kpfm is very low, but transfer to advection-
dominated with high Kpfm. Figure 34 describes the amount of remaining tracer 
methanol and tracer ethanol under different Kpfm when the initial mass of the 
tracers is high, and the same results when initial mass of the tracers is as low as 
illustrated in Figure 35. 
 
In subfigure (a) of Figure 34, the tracer methanol works appropriately when the 
log value of Kpfm are -3, -4 and -5, it cannot work when the log value of Kpfm is -6 
because the movement of the tracer is diffusion–dominated and mass variation 
==PAGE:78==

remains the same when Kpfm is larger than 10-6 [m/s]. However, in subfigure (b) of 
Figure 34, the tracer ethanol can only work effectively when Kpfm is 10-4 [m/s] and 
10-5 [m/s], because when Kpfm is larger than 10-6 [m/s], the mass variation of the 
tracer stays the same. On the contrary, with lower values of Kpfm (≤10-3 [m/s]), 
tracer movement will be advection-dominated so that the tracer is displaced fast 
to work for the whole exposure period.  Therefore, when the sorbent of PFM has 
different hydraulic conductivities, multiple tracers would be used if Kpfm is in the 
range that the selected tracer could work, and the workable range of these tracers 
are described in Figure 35. Also, only the results about the methanol tracer and 
ethanol tracer are shown and the results. 
 
With a lower initial mass of the tracers, the amount of remaining tracers falls as 
expected.  Under this condition, the methanol tracer can only work properly when 
Kpfm is 10-3 [m/s], while the methanol ethanol can work when Kpfm is 10-4 [m/s] 
with the same reason as the PFM with higher initial mass of tracers.  
 
In conclusion, since various kinds of tracers can work for different ranges of Kmat 
and Kpfm, it is important to apply a combination of all the five tracers to ensure the 
PFM can work for geometries with all kinds of Kmat and Kpfm.

6.1.2 Porosity  
With regard to influences from nmat and npfm on the PFM behaviors, their 
performance is not so obvious compared with parameters like Kmat and Kpfm.  
 
In Figure 28, it can be seen that when tracer transportation is diffusion-dominated 
in the aquifer, growth of nmat will lead to the decrease of the amount of remaining 
tracers especially for the tracer methanol and tracer ethanol. Additionally, the 
tracers TBA and DMP are proven again that they cannot work effectively in 
diffusion-dominated systems because their remaining amount of mass is almost 1. 
On the contrary, because of the non-obvious mass variation of the relative tracers 
under different npfm in Figure 29, the PFM behaviors of different tracers are not 
affected by npfm as long as tracer transportation is diffusion-dominated in the PFM.  
 
Tracer transportation behaviors in advection-dominated systems in the aquifer 
and PFM are shown in Figure 28 and Figure 30. With a growing nmat, the tracer 
TBA and DMP increases a bit when their initial mass is small (D=4.5cm), the 
remaining amount of mass of the other tracers become a constant after a 21- day 
exposure.  
 
Therefore, in a diffusion-dominated transportation system, the tracer methanol 
and ethanol might be influenced with the variation of porosity because both 
tracers work more effectively with relatively smaller hydraulic conductivity. 
==PAGE:80==

However, in advection-dominated transportation systems, the tracer TBA and 
DMP will be affected by the porosity change as they work better when the 
groundwater velocity is higher.  
 
6.2 Tracer combination 
Even though the tracer applied for the PFM can be selected based on the 
simulation results under some pre-defined parameters of the field, it is still hard 
to make a decision on applying which tracer when some hydrology parameters 
are unknown since different tracers have different applicable ranges. Thus, in a 
terrain with lots of uncertain parameters, it is necessary to utilize all the five 
tracers instead of only one.  
 
On the one hand, because of the different attributes the five tracers, they will not 
work for the same exposure period in reality, thus it is important to apply more 
than one tracer for the PFM if long-time exposure period. This phenomenon is 
illustrated in Figure 21 and Figure 22. Especially when the initial mass of all the 
tracer’s decreases, corresponding tracers can work for shorter time, which 
requires the combination of all tracers work for the whole exposure periods to 
ensure valuable data is collected.  On the other hand, as some parameters are 
uncertain, it is hard to distinguish whether tracer transportation in the aquifer is 
diffusion-dominated or advection-dominated. Therefore, it is better to combine 
tracers that could work effectively under different conditions. That is also the 
reason why combinations of different tracers can measure different magnitudes 
of groundwater.  
 
As for the second aspects, Figure 36 can be taken as an example to explain how all 
five tracers could facility to measure different magnitudes of groundwater flow. In 
subfigure (a), we can know once log value of groundwater flow is approximately 
larger than -2 or smaller than -1, the tracers IPA, TBA and DMP can work quite 
effectively; but when log value of groundwater flow is bigger than -1, only tracer 
DMP can continually work. So different tracers should be chosen accordingly 
based on the magnitudes of groundwater flow.  
 
In subfigure (b), the initial mass of all five tracers are reduced, which results at 
smaller amounts of the remaining tracers, thus tracers with low initial mass 
cannot work for extremely high groundwater. Similarly, combination of all the five

five tracers could work for more magnitudes of groundwater; and a PFM combing 
five tracers can work for different ranges of groundwater flow by adjusting the

6.5 Pros and Cons of the Model.  
Through the PFM model, the hydrology condition of PFM and its tracer behaviors 
can be simulated. Thus the model can be applied to get enough data of the tracer 
                weight of initial mass 
==PAGE:85==
83 
 
behaviors under all kinds of terrain for in a shorter time in comparison with lab 
work and field tests.  
 
In previous literature, Hatfield and Anneable have already derived equations that 
show how to use a PFM to direct measure groundwater flow for one-dimension 
tracer transportation (Hatfield, et al., 2004), but it is hard to derive equations for 
2-D and 3-D transportation models. However, both 2-D and 3-D fluid 
transportation models can be built for the PFM, which can help us learn more 
about tracer behaviors under different parameters precisely. Next, in existing 
researches, there are many assumptions applied to simplify the calculation. For 
example, as shown in Figure 8, inner PVC tube is ignored which leads to 
streamlines of groundwater inside the terrain to be plotted as straight lines, but 
there should be water convergence when groundwater flow is passing the inner 
PVC tube in practice. Comparatively, the groundwater flow simulations in the 
model is more realistic which could be validated in Figure 15 as the streamlines of 
the groundwater are more practical. Therefore, a PFM model can make tracer 
transportation more similar to the conditions in a real field.  
 
However, in this model, groundwater flow is simulated according to Darcy’s law, 
which is valid for laminar flows through a domain. However, in reality when water 
flows through the coarse-grained sediments, the flow might be turbulent, which 
cannot be simulated in this model. Another uncertainty is that the influences from 
the vertical flow on the PFM performance cannot be simulated in a 2-D model, so 
how the vertical flow affect tracer transportation under different hydraulic 
parameters are unknown.

7. Conclusion  
 
Passive flux meters (PFM) have already been developed for simultaneously and 
directly measuring the groundwater flow and contaminate flux, as well as 
groundwater direction detection.  
 
In this project, a COMSOL Multiphysics model has been used to study the 
performance of the PFM. Before the simulation of designed scenarios, the accuracy 
and reliability of this model are verified by three model tests to make sure the 
model can simulate groundwater flow and tracer transportation precisely. 
Subsequently, the performance of the PFM is based on a model simulation and has 
been analyzed in two aspects: (1) How the functionalities of a PFM device are 
fulfilled in the model. (2) What the behaviors of a PFM device are under different 
influential factors and the impacts of these factors to the performance of PFM 
devices.  For the first aspect, it is clear that the PFM model can not only facilitate 
us to get the groundwater flow by measuring the remaining mass of tracers, but 
also help quantify some hydrological parameters, such as Kmat, Kpfm, npfm and nmat, 
based on the simulation results.  
 
As for the second aspect, the impacts from various factors are studied according 
to the scenarios in Table 4. First of all, PFMs with five different tracers: methanol, 
ethanol, IPA, TBA and DMP are simulated. Then a few differently sized inner PVC 
tubes (D=1.5cm, D=2.5cm, D=3.5cm and D=4.5cm) are designed to identify how 
the initial amount of tracers affect the PFM performance. On one hand, we can 
know that the working exposure time for a tracer relies heavily on its attributes. 
For example, the lower retardation factor of a tracer is, the shorter exposure 
period this PFM would contribute to. On the other hand, with a reduction of the 
initial amount of tracers on the PFM sorbent, a PFM with the same kind of tracers 
will last for less time.  
 
 As for the hydrological factors, we can conclude that with an increasing hydraulic 
conductivity of the aquifer (Kmat), groundwater flow velocity increases, which 
ultimately leads to the reduction of the remaining amount of tracers retained on 
the PFM.  Similarly, the variation of the remaining mass of the tracers is less for a 
higher hydraulic conductivity of PFM sorbent (Kpfm). Furthermore, the PFM would 
==PAGE:88==
86 
 
not work for all Kmat and Kpfm values, since a PFM with different tracers will have 
different valid ranges for Kmat and Kpfm. With low Kmat or Kpfm, groundwater velocity 
in the aquifer or the borehole installed with a PFM is also low, and thus the tracer 
transportation will be diffusion-dominated. In a diffusion-dominated system, the 
tracer methanol might work relatively better because it is the only one that can 
work well with Kmat being 10-6 [m/s]. In an advection-dominated system, the tracer 
DMP works better as it can even work when Kmat is around 1[m/s], but other 
tracers retained in the PFM have been completely displaced by groundwater after 
a 21-day-period exposure. Comparatively, although Kpfm affects the PFM 
performance less, the workable range of Kpfm is larger for the tracer DMP.  
 
The performance of a PFM is also influenced by porosity of the aquifer (nmat) and 
porosity of the PFM sorbent (npfm). Normally, the remaining amount of tracers will 
decrease if a high porosity due to the higher molecule diffusion in diffusion-
dominated systems, but a rise of remaining tracers in advection-dominated 
systems due to less flow velocity. Furthermore, porosity of the PFM sorbent 
(npfm)has more complicated impacts on PFM. with the growth of npfm, multiple 
effects from retardation, diffusion and advection could lead to the remaining 
amount of the tracer methanol and ethanol going up a bit in diffusion-dominated 
systems, while the residual mass of the tracer TBA and DMP falls consistently in 
advection-dominated systems. 
 
Since a PFM with different tracers can work for different hydrological ranges, it is 
necessary to apply a combination of all five kinds of tracers for a PFM device to 
guarantee operation for all kinds of terrains. A PFM with all tracers can work for 
a longer exposure period regardless of the initial mass. Moreover, the applicable 
range of Kmat is 10-6 - 1 [m/s] when the maximum reduction of the initial amount 
is up to 50%, but turns to 10-6 - 10-1 [m/s] when the initial amount of tracers 
reduces by 80%.  Applicable range of Kpfm is 10-5 - 10-3 [m/s] when the maximum 
reduction of the initial amount is 50%, but becomes 10-5 - 10-2 when the initial 
amount of tracers is 80% lower. In addition, the performance of the PFM with all 
tracers combined is furtherly limited by Kpfm than Kmat since the PFM can work in 
a narrower Kpfm range. Besides, when the hydraulic gradient of the field is pre-
defined, we find that a PFM with all tracers can work effectively for 21 days when 
the log value of the groundwater is from -4 to 1. Therefore, a PFM combined with 
all five tracers can work under different magnitudes of groundwater.   
==PAGE:89==
87 
 
However, the computed groundwater flow results based on two different 
approaches, which are the theoretical equation and the model, are slightly 
different. The model result has been proven to be more accurate since the Darcy 
flux in the model increases to the same extend with Kmat, but the theoretical 
equations would not be trustworthy in both extremely diffusion-dominated or 
extremely advection-dominated systems. Furthermore, a PFM is also proven to be 
helpful for direction detection, based on the designed direction detection in PFM 
configuration. Since the greatest mass reduction happened in the section closest 
to the inlet of groundwater to the PFM, the part with the greatest mass reduction 
should be close to the groundwater direction. In order to have an apparent mass 
variation for directional evaluation, the valid range of Kmat should be 10-6 - 10-1 
[m/s], while the valid range of Kpfm is 10-5 - 10-2[m/s].  
 
Although most of the data from the PFM model has been collected to help 
understand more information regarding the behaviors of a PFM, there are still 
some limitations for the PFM model. Firstly, the simulation of the groundwater in 
the model follows the computation based on Darcy’s Flux Law. Thus the model can 
only consider the laminar flow while turbulent flow is not able to be simulated. 
Secondly, the model is more realistic to reflect short-term PFM behavior, because 
the degradation of tracers is ignored in the simulations and there might also exist 
some directional changes of the groundwater when the PFM is exposed to the 
groundwater for a long period. Finally, as the model is 2-D, the effects of vertical 
flow cannot be simulated, but in reality the vertical flow could indeed influence 
the tracer transportation to some extent. However, since most existing researches 
are only based on 1-D tracer transportation model, the 2-D model established in 
this project is already a huge improvement to simulate a PFM’s behavior more 
accurately. 
 
In the future, a 3-D model of the PFM device might be established in COMSOL 
Multiphysics, and through the 3-D model, effects from vertical flow inside the PFM 
sorbent on the performances of a PFM will be analyzed.
