Abstract
The strain gradient plasticity model by Fleck and Willis (2009b) is incorporated in a dynamic fi-
nite strain numerical framework based on the implicit generalized α-method for time integration.
The aim is to increase the solution speed as compared to an existing explicit implementation of
the same plasticity model, which requires significant computational time. An automatic time
stepping algorithm, which dynamically updates the time step size, is implemented alongside the
implicit solver in order to further optimize the solution speed. Results from various test cases
show that the implementation of the implicit solution algorithm on average doubles the solution
speed, but the automatic time stepping algorithm fails to improve further on this.
Numerical results are provided for axially loaded aluminium bars, which are stretched at large
strain rates. The analyses are inspired by the work of N’souglo et al. (2018), which demonstrated
that at large strain rates localization occurs in a necking pattern instead of a single neck. The
current work expands upon the findings by including the effects of strain gradient plasticity,
the aim being to examine the interaction between material inertia and gradient hardening.
The results demonstrate that as the gradient effects grow in strength, the necking patterns are
gradually smoothed until just a single neck is formed.

Introduction
Conventional engineering models of metal plasticity rarely consider the absolute size of a struc-
ture. Once normalized, the structural response of a system remains constant regardless of its
absolute size, since no intrinsic length scale enters the constitutive equations. In most applica-
tions at conventional engineering scales, this linear scalability is perfectly acceptable and indeed
practical. As the system shrinks to the micron-scale however, a strong dependence on absolute
size emerges. If gradients of the plastic strain are present at the micron-scale, ie. if the metal
lattice becomes severely distorted during plastic straining, dislocations are forced to move in
relation to the plastic straining to accommodate the movement of the material (Ashby, 1970).
These dislocations are referred to as geometrically necessary dislocations (GNDs) and their ac-
cumulation leads to increased strain hardening. The result is an apparent increase in material
strength as size decreases.
The GNDs develop alongside the statistically stored dislocations (SSDs), which account for
conventional strain hardening. Unlike the GNDs, the accumulation of SSDs is not tied to the
gradients of plastic strain, but rather results from random encounters and subsequent trapping
of the inherent dislocations in the material.
It is interesting to note that in most cases of
metal plasticity at conventional scales, GNDs will still accumulate in large amounts at grain
boundaries (Ashby, 1970). This explains the Hall-Petch effect, ie. the higher strength of fine
grained metals. However, averaged over the bulk these GNDs give rise to negligible heterogeneity
and their geometric distribution is of little importance. At the micron-scale, this is not the case.
The smaller is stronger effect has been shown experimentally on several occasions (Fleck et al.,
1994; Mu et al., 2014; Xiang and Vlassak, 2006). Fleck et al. (1994) demonstrated the effect by
subjecting thin copper wires (diameters ranging from 12 to 170 µm) to uniaxial tension and to
torsion. In the case of uniaxial tension, where the strain field is homogeneous, the normalized
stress-strain curves show negligible dependence on wire diameter. In the case of torsion however,
where the shear strain varies with the radius and thus strain gradients are present, a strong size
dependence is seen, with the smaller wires strain hardening much more quickly than the larger
wires.
A second source of an intrinsic length scale is material inertia. Parameters such as the wave
propagation speed remain constant regardless of system scaling, and thus dynamic systems can
show strong dependence on absolute size. The purpose of the present work is to examine the
interaction of the length scale determined by material inertia and the length scale introduced
by gradient strengthening. To study the interaction, a numerical experiment by N’souglo et al.

The gradient strengthening effect is modelled using the strain gradient plasticity model by
Fleck and Willis (2009b). The analyses are carried out in a dynamic large-strain finite element
framework, and the numerical implementation leans heavily on the work by Andersen and Nielsen
(2022) and Nielsen and Niordson (2019). The current numerical implementation, as presented
in Andersen and Nielsen (2022), applies an explicit Newmark-method for time integration. This
approach is comparatively simple to implement but requires small time steps to remain stable.
As a consequence, even fairly small analyses (less than 105 degrees of freedom) can take days
or even weeks on a powerful computer. In the current work, the explicit time integration is
substituted for an implicit method which allows much larger time steps when applied to common
elasticity and plasticity models. The hope is that this quality carries over to the strain gradient
plasticity model and allows faster solution times.

2.1
Strain gradient plasticity
Strain hardening is tied to the total dislocation density in a material. Both GNDs and SSDs
contribute to the hardening. While the causes of GND and SSD development differ, there is no
inherent physical difference between the two dislocation categories, and thus it is not possible
to directly measure GND density. Strain gradient plasticity theories instead use the gradient of
plastic strain as an indirect measure of the local density of GNDs (Fleck and Hutchinson, 1997).
As a result both plastic strains and their gradients enter the constitutive equations. The current
work builds upon the phenomenological strain gradient theory formulated by Fleck and Willis
(2009b) which in turn builds on Gudmundson (2004).
In addition to the Cauchy stresses σij, which are work-conjugate to the elastic strain rates ˙εE
ij,

with the microstresses and not with the Cauchy stresses as assumed in conventional plasticity

2.2
Finite strain formulation
In order to extend the analysis to finite strains, the updated Lagrange approach is adopted, in
which the current deformed geometry is used a frame of reference for the equilibrium equations.
Since the current deformed geometry is unknown however, the numerical implementation uses
the most recent displacement solution as a reference, and the displacement increments are then
assumed small. The concept is illustrated in Fig. 2.1. As a consequence of this approach, the
principle of virtual power Eq. (2.25) must be rewritten in the known configuration instead of
the deformed configuration in order to perform the integrations.

system response changes (Bergan and Mollestad, 1985). For these reasons an automatic time
stepping algorithm was implemented, which dynamically updates the step size in response to
the current state of the analysis in order to optimize efficiency. The algorithm is made to be

3.5.2
Instability handling
If the automatic time stepping algorithm increases the step size too much the solution may
become unstable. Several measures of instability are applied at each time step. If an instability
is detected, the solution increments of the current time step are discarded, and the time step is
repeated with a reduced step size. Experience shows that reducing the time step by as much
as 90% for a few time steps is often necessary to regain stability, especially if the instability
occurs when solving for the plastic strain rate increments. To avoid excessive solution times,
the allowable step size change is increased to h = 0.5 for a period after the instability, such that
the time step can quickly recover. The measures of instability applied are:
Instability measure: Non-convergence of displacement iterations
If the displacement iterations do not converge within the allowable number of iterations, the
solution is considered unstable. By default, the allowable number of iterations is 25.
Instability measure: Probable non-convergence of displacement iterations
If the residual r of the displacement solution increases for five subsequent iterations, the dis-
placement iterations are likely to eventually fail, and thus the solution is considered unstable.
Instability measure: Displacement increments are too large
If the largest displacement increment max(∆ui) is more than 100 times larger than the length
or radius (whichever is larger) of the structure, the solution is considered unstable.

Case study: Regular necking patterns
at high strain rates
At low strain rates, the onset of necking in homogenous ductile uniaxially loaded bars occurs
when the true stress equals the tensile strength, as quantified by the Consid`ere criterion. At large
strain rates however, inertia stabilizes the material, which allows the Consid`ere criterion to be
exceeded by a significant margin. This chapter presents the results from repeating the numerical
experiment by N’souglo et al. (2018), which examined uniaxial stretching of aluminium bars at
large strain rates. The current study expands upon their findings by including effects of strain
gradient hardening. Similar studies include Andersen and Nielsen (2022) and Needleman (2018).
4.1
Problem formulation
The study considers cylindrical uniaxially loaded bars, which are stretched at a prescribed strain
rate ˙εB. The bars have initial diameter D0 and initial length L0 as illustrated in Fig. 4.1. In
the finite element model, one end of the bar is constrained axially while an axial velocity ˙uB is
prescribed at the other end. The prescribed velocity is given by ˙uB = ˙εBL0 so as to correspond
to the prescribed strain rate. The analysis is initialized with a linear velocity distribution from
zero at the constrained boundary to ˙uB at the other end such that the axial strain rate ˙ε22 is
initially equal to the prescribed strain rate ˙εB everywhere in the bar.

4.2
Comparison of the applied solution methods
A number of significant differences exist between the present study and the one presented in
N’souglo et al. (2018), the obvious one being the inclusion of gradient effects.
In the case
of LD = 0, where the gradient plasticity model reduces to conventional J2 plasticity, some
significant differences still remain however.
While the present study applies a simple visco-
plastic material model with isotropic hardening, N’souglo et al. (2018) apply the modified Gurson
model, which is vastly more complicated. It models damage development in the material tied to
the development of micro voids as the material deforms. The volume fraction of the micro voids
is known as the porosity. Instead of directly scaling the reference stress as in the present study,
N’souglo et al. (2018) apply the noise distribution to scale the initial porosity of each element.
Thermal effects are also included in the model used by N’souglo et al. (2018), but not in the

present study.
Another significant difference is the finite element discretization. N’souglo et al. (2018) use a full
3d model with corresponding 3d noise distributions, while the present study uses axisymmetric
elements and 2d noise distributions.
For equal element side lengths, all elements in the 3d
model will have approximately the same volume, while in the axisymmetric model the element
volume grows with the radius. As a consequence, an even noise distribution in the 2d mesh will
not result in an even noise distribution in the volume of the model. No attempt was made to
compensate for this however, seeing as the exact noise distribution was shown to be of little
importance.

Consider now the inclusion of gradient effects. The formation of necks necessarily results in
gradients of the plastic strain. Increasing the intrinsic length scale therefore penalizes the for-
mation of necks, since the gradients caused by the necks lead to increased stresses. The effect
of LD is most clearly seen in the case of ˙εB = 1000 s−1. At just LD = 0.025L0 the necking
pattern completely disappears, and a single neck is formed in the middle of the bar. The reason
the neck forms in the middle of the bar and not at one of the ends is likely that inertia still
has a strong stabilizing effect. As LD is increased further however, the gradient effects begin to
overcome the stabilizing effects of inertia. At LD = 0.05L0 a neck begins to form at the end of
the bar and the neck in the middle of the bar is seen to be smoothed out. At LD = 0.1L0 the
neck is pushed completely to the end of the bar. Similar behaviour is seen at the higher strain
rates as well. Seeing however as the inertial forces grow with increasing strain rates, the effect
of gradient strengthening becomes smaller with increasing strain rates. Consider for instance
the analysis at ˙εB = 10000 s−1 for LD = 0.1L0. The neck formation is clearly smoothed out as
compared to LD = 0.1L0 and a neck is beginning to form at the end, but several necks are still
present. The gradient effects are less apparent still for ˙εB = 50000 s−1. Presumably, if LD is
increased sufficiently the response at the higher strain rates will also locate in a single neck, but
this was not examined further.
The amount by which inertia and gradient effects delay the formation of necks can be quantified
by comparison with the Consid`ere criterion. For the assumed power hardening law, the critical
strain εcr which corresponds to the Consid`ere criterion and thus the onset of necking, is simply
found as the strain hardening exponent, ie. εcr = N = 0.1. It is interesting to note that N’souglo
et al. (2018) report εcr = 0.08 for the same power hardening law and material parameters.

stretched further, the gradient strengthening can no longer maintain the gentle curve of the bar
and the neck localizes at the tip of the bar while the rest of the bare unloads.

Concluding remarks
The inclusion of strain gradient effects was shown to have significant impact when included in
dynamic analyses. The interaction between gradient strengthening and inertial forces leads to
complex behaviours not seen at conventional engineering scales. Given the somewhat uncon-
ventional formulation of the underlying strain gradient plasticity model (and resulting criticism
from the community) however, the validity of the results should be considered with a critical
mindset. Experimental verification of the results would provide useful insight, but such an ex-
periment is likely to be very challenging. Generally speaking, although strain gradient plasticity
has been studied for decades at this point, there are still many unknowns, and future work will
undoubtedly improve upon the current theories.
The primary focus of the current project was on increasing the solution speed of the algorithm.
The inclusion of the implicit generalized α-method was seen to provide a noticeable speed up of
the solution, although the increased efficiency did not arise from allowing larger time step sizes
as was otherwise assumed. Unfortunately the implemented automatic time stepping algorithm
failed to further improve on the solution speed, although it does offer significant convenience
when maximum performance is not essential. From the testing on the solution algorithms it was
seen however, that while improving the displacement solution did improve overall efficiency, the
largest potential for increased solution speed lies in the solution of the plastic strain rate field.
Improving the solution process would undoubtedly make for interesting future work.
