Abstract 
In this thesis, the protocols of the ALDREN TAIL-index for offices and hotels were adapted for 
residential buildings to describe the quality of the indoor environment (IEQ) in two examined apartments 
in Copenhagen, Denmark. This thesis aims to contribute to the creation of a market for the evaluation of 
the IEQ by giving the occupants the means to prioritize their comfort and health when selecting their 
homes. Additionally, this will benefit the homeowners by offering an indicator of whether their home is 
meeting the necessary health or comfort levels. Some of the gaps in this research are how and where the 
measurement should be performed in a residential building without mechanical ventilation and what 
decision should be made regarding the uncertain occupation time in a home. A schedule analysis was 
performed to document the changes in the IEQ parameters during different timeframes in the two 
examined apartments. By following the adapted TAIL protocol for residential buildings, the overall IEQ 
of the two apartments was rated as poor – the lowest level. One of the reasons was having a lower quality 
level of daylight due to blocked sunlight from neighboring buildings and a poor thermal environment 
due to open windows, low heating settings, and temperatures between 0-8 degrees outside during the 
measurement. There are still limitations and challenges when using TAIL for residential buildings. To 
make the verification successful, a larger pool of residential buildings is needed to make sure the 
adaptation of TAIL is viable.

1. Introduction 
1.1 The rationale 
The justification of this thesis is to describe and develop a method that would aid the improvement of the 
well-being of occupants in residential buildings. The method uses an indoor climate rating scheme that 
takes its origin in the rating scheme TAIL developed for offices and hotels. The work in the thesis is 
focusing on adapting the scheme to residential buildings and examining its performance in typical homes. 
The purpose of the scheme is to create a demand for better indoor environmental quality (IEQ) by 
providing occupants with the means to prioritize their comfort and health when selecting their homes 
(apartments and houses). In addition, the scheme can benefit homeowners by offering an indicator of 
whether their home is meeting the necessary health and comfort levels and indicating which 
improvements are necessary. Ultimately, this thesis aims to contribute to the creation of a market for 
evaluation of IEQ in residential buildings, which can have long-term benefits for society as a whole. 
1.2 The structure of the thesis 
The thesis is divided into seven sections: introduction, background, introduction to TAIL, methods, 
results, discussion, and conclusion. The introduction outlines the purpose of the thesis and its potential 
benefits. The background section provides an overview of the IEQ rating schemes available and explores 
the adaptation of the TAIL rating scheme for residential buildings. The introduction to TAIL introduces 
the IEQ parameters measured in this thesis. 
 
The methods section explains how the TAIL scheme was adapted to residential buildings and how the 
application was examined in two apartments. The discussion section evaluates the project's limitations, 
compares the results with the existing TAIL protocol, and considers how it could be further improved. 
Finally, the conclusion summarizes the challenges, methods, and results of the thesis, highlighting its 
potential value in increasing awareness of occupant well-being.

2. Background 
The aim of this section is to explain why the TAIL rating scheme is important for residential buildings 
and to provide the basis for choosing it. The first section will provide an overview of what IEQ is, its 
importance in residential buildings, and the need for IEQ labels. The next section will analyze the most 
common IEQ labeling scheme in the market, including BREAM, LEED, DGNB, and WELL. Levels(s) 
and CASBEE, as well as holistic IEQ tools or standards like the IEQ-Compass and DS 3033. The section 
on identifying gaps and limitations will summarize the shortcomings of these schemes. Finally, the TAIL 
rating scheme will be introduced, providing background on the scheme and its potential to address the 
limitations of existing IEQ labeling schemes. 
2.1 Overview of the indoor climate environment and IEQ labeling 
schemes 
 
What is IEQ and why it is important? 
Indoor Environment Quality (IEQ) refers to the quality of the indoor environment and how it affects the 
health, comfort, and well-being of people who occupy indoor spaces, like homes, schools, and offices. 
IEQ includes different factors, like temperature, air quality, lighting, and acoustics. A more elaborate 
description of those factors is given in the background chapter. 
 
The study by Frontczak and Wargocki [1] found that good IEQ makes people feel more satisfied, healthy, 
and productive. To ensure good IEQ, it’s important to consider all the different factors that contribute to 
it and how they interact with each other.  
 
It is also important to keep monitoring and maintaining IEQ over time to make sure it remains at a good 
level. Overall, a comprehensive approach to IEQ is necessary for designing and maintaining a healthy 
and comfortable indoor environment. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:10==
 
 
 
  10
 
Why is IEQ important in residential buildings? 
Most people in developed parts of the world spend almost 90% of their time indoors [2], especially in 
their homes, therefore the indoor environment can have a significant impact on their overall health and 
well-being. Poor IEQ can lead to health problems such as respiratory issues, allergies, and headaches, 
and can also impact their well-being and productivity [3].  
 
The study also highlights that achieving good IEQ in residential buildings is important for energy 
efficiency and sustainability. A well-designed and maintained indoor environment can reduce energy 
consumption and improve the efficiency of the building systems. 
 
What is an IEQ labeling scheme and why is it important? 
An IEQ labeling scheme is a system that rates the quality of the indoor environment in a building. These 
ratings can help the occupant make informed decisions about their health and well-being and can also 
incentivize building owners to improve the quality of the indoor environment in their buildings. The 
scheme provides a clear and transparent rating system for IEQ and can help promote healthy and 
sustainable indoor environments by setting standards for indoor climate quality. Examples of green 
certification labels are BREEAM, LEED, DGNB, WELL, Levels(s), and CASBEE. Examples of specific 
IEQ labeling schemes are TAIL and IEQ-Compass.

2.2 Analysis of existing labeling scheme for commercial and residential 
buildings 
This section contains general information about the most common labeling scheme in the market and the 
existing IEQ labeling scheme in Denmark. 
 
BREEAM 
BREEAM [4] is a global certification scheme that assesses a building's sustainability in areas like energy, 
water, waste, and IEQ. It includes several categories such as indoor air quality, thermal comfort, acoustic 
performance, lighting, and visual comfort, which aim to provide a healthy and comfortable indoor 
environment. BREEAM also considers the building's impact on the surrounding environment, 
encouraging the use of low-emission materials, waste management, and ecological impact. Achieving a 
high IEQ rating can positively impact the health and well-being of building occupants while minimizing 
the building's impact on the environment.  
 
LEED 
LEED [5] is a green building rating system that evaluates environmental performance. IEQ is one of its 
main categories, focusing on creating a healthy and comfortable indoor environment. It includes factors 
such as air quality, lighting, thermal comfort, and acoustics. LEED also considers the building's impact 
on the environment by promoting low-emitting materials and reducing light pollution. LEED promotes 
sustainable design principles to improve environmental performance and occupant comfort in buildings. 
 
DGNB  
DGNB [6] is a certification system that evaluates building sustainability in Germany. IEQ is a key factor 
in DGNB certification, which assesses aspects like air quality, thermal comfort, lighting, and acoustics. 
DGNB emphasizes the importance of creating a healthy and comfortable indoor environment for building 
occupants, and IEQ plays a crucial role in achieving this goal. The certification system promotes 
sustainable building practices and provides a framework for building owners, architects, and designers 
to create healthier and more environmentally friendly buildings 
 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
 
  12
 
WELL 
The WELL Building Standard [7] is a certification system that prioritizes creating healthy and productive 
environments by evaluating various aspects of building design, construction, and operation. The standard 
includes criteria for IEQ, such as air quality, thermal comfort, lighting, acoustic, and water quality. The 
certification process involves assessing the building's performance against pre-defined requirements and 
rewarding points for each criterion achieved. The aim is to encourage buildings to support the health and 
well-being of occupants while being environmentally responsible. A third-party assessment verifies the 
building's performance, ensuring that the standard is being met. 
 
Levels(s) 
Levels(s) [8] is a certification scheme that evaluates and improves IEQ in buildings. Developed by the 
International Living Future Institute, it assesses air quality, thermal comfort, acoustic comfort, and 
lighting quality. It also considers the building location and materials used in construction. Levels(s) have 
performance-based certification levels with a higher level requiring better IEQ performance. It aims to 
promote healthy buildings and the well-being of occupants by providing a high level of IEQ. 
 
CASBEE 
CASBEE [9] is a Japanese tool that evaluates the environmental performance of buildings and 
neighborhoods. It assesses IEQ, including indoor air quality, thermal comfort, lighting, and acoustics. 
The tool ensures healthy indoor air quality by evaluating air pollutants and proper ventilation rates. It 
also considers the temperature, humidity, visual conditions, and sound conditions of the building to assess 
thermal comfort, lighting, and acoustics. CASBEE aims to recognize buildings that provide a healthy 
and comfortable indoor environment for occupants, which is important for the sustainability and overall 
performance of the built environment. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
  13
 
EN16797 
The European standard, EN16798 [10] provides guidelines for the assessment of IEQ in a building. It 
covers several parameters related to IEQ, such as indoor air quality, thermal comfort, lighting, acoustic, 
and more. The standard provides a framework for evaluating the impact of these parameters on the well-
being and health of building occupants. It also provides recommendations on how to improve IEQ in 
buildings, such as through the use of a ventilation system or controlling indoor temperature and humidity. 
EN16798 is important in ensuring that buildings provide a healthy and comfortable indoor environment 
for occupants, and it is widely used in Europe as a base for the evaluation of IEQ in new and existing 
buildings. 
 
IEQ COMPASS  
The IEQ-compass [11] is a new Danish tool for rating the indoor environmental quality of a building. It 
evaluates 16 sub-parameters in four areas of IEQ: thermal, air quality, visual, and acoustic. Each area is 
equally important and has a different weight for each sub-parameter. The result is given a letter from A 
to G and a color from green to red, similar to energy labels. The IEQ-Compass is specifically designed 
for Danish buildings and is currently only available for multifamily social housing.  
 
DS3033 
The Danish voluntary standard, DS3033 [12], is a method of classifying the IEQ in buildings, including 
homes, schools, daycare facilities, and offices. DS3033 provides a framework for evaluating and 
classifying various aspects of indoor environmental quality, including indoor air quality, thermal 
comfort, acoustic, lighting, and visual comfort. It also sets minimum requirements for each parameter 
based on established health and comfort standards.

2.3 Identification of gaps and limitations of existing IEQ labeling 
schemes 
This section contains the gaps and limitations of the schemes mentioned in the previous chapter.  
 
The common labeling schemes 
The most common labeling schemes like BREAM, LEED, DGBN; Levels(s), and CASBEE have made 
a significant contribution towards promoting better IEQ in buildings. However, there are still some gaps 
and limitations in these schemes that need to be addressed. One of the gaps is the lack of a unified 
approach to measuring IEQ, which can result in inconsistencies in results and confusion among 
stakeholders. Another gap is the limited focus on occupant health and well-being, with some schemes 
placing more emphasis on energy efficiency and environmental sustainability. Additionally, some 
schemes may not be suitable for all building types and locations and may not adequately consider the 
specific needs and preferences of occupants. Finally, there is a lack of standardized and transparent 
assessment criteria, which can make it difficult for building owners, designers, and occupants to 
understand and compare the result of different IEQ labeling schemes.  
 
IEQ-Compass 
For the IEQ-Compass, one potential limitation is that it requires a lot of input data as it relies on 
observation and blueprint of the buildings, which may be challenging to collect, especially for existing 
buildings. Another limitation is its lack of clear and measurable criteria, making it difficult to be used as 
a tool for evaluating and improving IEQ in residential buildings. Additionally, the IEQ-Compass does 
not include subjective surveys.  
 
DS 3033 
For DS 3033, one potential limitation is that it only focuses on a limited set of IEQ parameters, such as 
temperature, humidity, and CO2, and does not consider other important factors such as noise, lighting, 
and air quality. Additionally, the classification criteria for each IEQ parameter may be too broad, which 
may not reflect the true impact of occupant health and comfort. The lack of clarity in some of its criteria 
also makes it difficult for building owners and designers to understand the measures needed to achieve a 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
  15
 
specific classification level.  Another limitation is that the classification system is voluntary and does not 
have a requirement for mandatory compliance, which may limit its effectiveness in promoting optimal 
IEQ in buildings.  
 
2.4 The TAIL rating scheme 
The ALDREN (Alliance for Deep Renovation in Buildings) has proposed another way to measure the 
IEQ in buildings, called the TAIL rating scheme [13]. It aims to improve on other labeling schemes by 
measuring 12 different IEQ parameters and no subjective surveys to avoid bias in the rating. The TAIL 
rating scheme focuses on the four components of IEQ: the thermal environment (T), the acoustic 
environment (A), the indoor air quality (I), and the luminous environment (L) in the building. 
 
The quality of each component is shown by four colors: green means it is good, yellow means it needs 
some improvement, orange means it needs more improvement, and red means it is poor. The overall 
quality of the IEQ is given a category rating from I to IV, with I being the highest and IV being the 
lowest. This rating is based on the lowest quality among the four components. The TAIL labeling scheme 
was inspired by major building certification schemes like EN 16798 and Level(S), Well, DGNB, LEED, 
and CASBEE.  
 
The TAIL rating scheme has only been developed for hotels and offices. One of the main benefits of the 
rating scheme is its compliance with existing standards and Green Buildings schemes that address IEQ. 
The parameters are also specific, objective, and measurable, and it focuses on the health and well-being 
of the occupants of the building. The disadvantage is that it lacks attention to occupant behavior and 
consideration of diverse building types, like residential buildings. These are some of the challenges the 
thesis will focus on; examining how to adapt the TAIL rating scheme to residential buildings.

3.2 Acoustic environments (A)

criteria of some spaces and buildings”. There it describes the maximum sound level of different buildings 
and the type of spaces. For measurement of the sound pressure level, the 5th percentile of the working 
hours is used. The criteria for the sound pressure level can be found in Table 3.

Carbon dioxide 
The level of carbon dioxide (CO2) in the indoor air is not directly considered an air pollutant, as it occurs 
naturally in the air. Instead, it gives an indicator of the human presence and ventilation in a room or 
building. High CO2 levels can cause poor air quality and discomfort [14] in terms of tiredness and 
headaches, for instance. The CO2 level is measured in parts per million (ppm). It is defined in the 
standard EN 16798-1, for the Danish version of the standard it can be found in DS/16798-1 in table 
NA.1.2 “Design CO2-concentrations in occupied rooms”. There it describes the maximum concentration 
for different IEQ categories of occupied rooms and bedrooms. To be classified as a quality level, the 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
 
  19
 
measurement must not exceed the range of the lower quality level for more than 5% of the time, and the 
range of the next lowest quality level for more than 1%.  
 
Ventilation rate / Air change rate 
The ventilation rate is a measure of the amount of outdoor air that is brought into a building and circulated 
with the indoor air. It is an essential factor in maintaining good indoor air quality, as it helps to remove 
pollutants and regulate temperature and humidity levels. Ventilation rate is typically measured in cubic 
per minute (CFM) or air change per hours (ACH), with higher value indicating greater amount of fresh 
air being introduced into a space. A lower quality level of ventilation can result in poor indoor air quality 
which can lead to health problems, including fatigue, headaches, and respiratory issues. It is defined in 
the standard EN 16798-1, for the Danish version of the standard it can be found in DS/16798-1 in “NA 
3 Basis criteria for indoor air quality and ventilation rates”. In Table 4 shows the ventilation rate for 
building with mechanical supply or/and exhaust.  
 
Visible mold 
The criteria of the visible mold on based on the size of measured area. For green level there is no visible 
mold, while the rest of the levels depend on the measured area described in Table 4. The TAIL for hotels 
and offices recommends that a qualified expert should do the measurement and inspection of mold.  
 
Benzene 
Benzene is considered an air pollutant and can have outdoor sources from heavy traffic and industrial 
traffic. Sources of indoor benzene can be from building materials, furniture, heating, cooking systems 
and outdoor air. Long term exposure to levels of benzene can pose health problems such as cancer. The 
ranges of the criteria from the parameter are according to World Health Organization (WHO) [15]. Green 
level corresponds to the mandatory guidelines set by the French government of 2 µg/m3.

Formaldehyde 
Formaldehyde is also considered an air pollutant that is commonly used in building materials and 
household products such as insulation. Long exposure to formaldehyde can also increase the risk of 
cancer and other problems such as irritation of eyes, nose, and throat. The ranges of the criteria from the 
parameter are according to WHO [16]. Green level corresponds to the mandatory guidelines set by the 
French public health agency of 10 µg/m3. 
 
Particle PM2.5 
PM2.5 refers to fine particulate matter that is less than 2.5 micrometers in diameter. These particles can 
be emitted by a range of sources, vehicles, cooking, and candles. Exposure to PM2.5 has been linked to 
health problems such as respiratory and cardiovascular diseases. The ranges of the criteria from the 
parameter are according to WHO [16]. Green level corresponds the 24 hours average of PM2.5 not 
exceeded 10 µg/m3. 
 
Radon 
Radon is a naturally occurring radioactive gas that is produced by the decay of uranium in soil and rocks. 
It can enter buildings through cracks in foundation and walls. The exposure of radon has been linked to 
lung cancer. The ranges of the criteria from the parameter are according to WHO[16].  Green level 
corresponds to having less radon concentration of 100 Bq/m3.

4. Method 
This chapter will explain how the adaptation of TAIL for residential buildings was made, and how the 
IEQ for the two apartments was examined by measurement and simulation in order to determine the 
TAIL index [13].  
4.1 Adapting TAIL for residential buildings  
The adaptation of TAIL in residential buildings was done by revising the existing TAIL protocol for 
offices and hotels. The steps necessary to make the existing TAIL were reviewed, to see which 
modifications were needed to be utilized for residential buildings.  The steps made to make the adaptation 
was the following: 
 
● Reviewing the existing twelve IEQ parameters and deciding which parameter should be adjusted 
or removed based on the result from the examination IEQ of the two apartments.  
● Selecting the locations or rooms to conduct the measurement. 
● Adapting the timeframe used for the measurement by performing a schedule analysis. 
● Updating the ranges of criteria for the IEQ parameters to fit the rooms in residential buildings.  
● Determining the method to rate of the overall IEQ in residential buildings.  
 
The IEQ parameters 
The method used to select the IEQ parameter was based on the criteria from the existing TAIL protocol. 
The used criteria for selection were: 
 
1. The parameters have a high chance of changing in the process of a deep energy renovation. 
2. The parameters are included in standard prescribing conditions of IEQ in building, existing 
building certification scheme and recommended by guidelines regarding the comfort and health 
of building occupant.  
3. The parameters can be measured using validated and accessible methods.  
4. The parameters have been demonstrated to have an impact on occupant, comfort, health, well-
being, sleep quality, work performance or sleep quality.  
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
  23
 
The range of criteria  
The range of criteria were found in existing standard for different rooms in a residential building such as 
the standards EN 16798 [10] and EN 17037 [17]. 
 
The measurement location  
The method used to select the measurement location in a residential building was based on the proposed 
principle form the existing TAIL protocol. The used recommendation for selection was: 
 
● Rooms with the highest and lowest occupation density. 
● Rooms with different geographic orientations. 
● Rooms facing both garden and street/road.  
● Rooms that are occupied. 
 
The measurement assessment  
The method used for the measurement assessment was based on the criteria from the existing TAIL 
protocol. The used criteria for selection were: 
 
● Minimum two measurement locations. 
● The measurement location should correspond to about 10% of the total office area in the office 
building and the total guest room floor areas in a hotel. 
● Measured for at least one working week, but for temperature it should be continued for one month, 
and for radon, it should be continued for two months.  
 
 
The measurement timeframe 
In the existing TAIL protocol, the working hours were used in offices and night-time sleeping hours were 
used in hotels. To examine which time frame that should be used for either the whole residential building, 
each room in the residential building, or for the IEQ parameters, an occupant schedule analysis was 
performed. The analysis aimed at presenting an overview of the change of IEQ based on when occupants 
were present and not and using this as one of the factors to determine the time frame. The occupant

The measurement data of the air temperature, relative humidity, CO2 concentration, level, sound pressure

overview of the percentage of time the temperature exceeded by 1℃ or 2℃ in the different room and

The cumulative table and frequency graph were made by first calculating the relative frequency of each 
data point, which is simply the number of times it appears in the dataset divided by the total number of 
data points. Then, the cumulative relative frequency is calculated by adding up all the relative frequencies 
from the smallest data point up to a certain point. The resulting values are plotted on the graph to show 
the cumulative percentage of data points.

5.1.5 Rating of the overall quality of the indoor environment 
A two-step process is used to determine the overall IEQ in a room of the residential building. 
 
1. The first step is to determine the quality level of the twelve parameters based on the ranges 
defined in the subchapter “5.1.4 The ranges of criteria”. 
2. The second step is to determine the quality level of the four major components. It is estimated by 
taking the lowest quality level among the parameters defined in each component of TAIL. Then 
the overall quality level of the room is determined by selecting the lowest quality level among 
the four TAIL components.  
 
These processes had to be done separately for the bedroom, kitchen, and living room. When the TAIL of 
each room had been found with the two-step process, the overall IEQ of the residential building is then 
determined by the room with the worst rating.

6. Discussion 
This chapter will discuss the limitations of the project, the reasoning behind the changes made to the 
existing TAIL protocol and results from the examination. The chapter will also discuss the challenges 
that still need to be addressed. 
6.1 Limitations 
The limitations of this project should be considered when interpreting the results. Due to time and budget 
constraints, the study focused on comparing two apartments instead of two houses and did not measure 
formaldehyde and benzene due to the expensive ordering procedure. The reference apartments were not 
on the ground floor, so radon was not measured. Furthermore, the study is limited to the development 
and adaptation of the TAIL scheme for residential buildings and does not assess its implementation costs. 
Another significant limitation of the study is the lack of an accurate way to track the occupancy period 
in residential buildings, which could impact the results obtained, as IEQ may vary based on the duration 
of occupancy. Additionally, it is important to note that the adaptation of the TAIL protocol for residential 
buildings did not focus on adding more IEQ parameters to the existing twelve, but rather on assessing 
whether the existing twelve parameters could be used and examined. 
 
6.2 Changes made to the existing TAIL protocol 
 
Changing the parameter of “ventilation rate” to “air change rate” 
One of the changes made from the existing protocol from offices and hotels was replacing the parameter 
of ventilation rate with air change rate. In a residential building with a ventilation system, the air change 
rate and ventilation rate are often referred to as the same. The reason is that in buildings like apartments, 
the air change rate would be determined by the natural infiltrations of outdoor air through cracks and 
openings in the building envelope, and the amount of air that enters the building would be the same as 
the amount of air of that ventilated space according to the standard EN 16798. For the adapted protocol, 
the air change rate should be used for residential buildings without ventilation systems as the term can 
==PAGE:75==
 
 
 
  75
 
promote consistency with building regulations, such as the Danish Building Code, where the parameter 
is used to evaluate indoor air quality in residential buildings. Therefore, using the term “air change rate” 
in the labeling scheme would promote consistency and avoid confusion among building owners, 
occupants and professionals.  
 
In the existing TAIL protocol, it was recommended to measure the infiltration rate for buildings without 
mechanical ventilation. To measure infiltration, different methods can be used, like a tracer gas technique 
or the “blower door test”, which is measuring the air permeability of the building's envelope with fan 
pressurization, followed by using a model to estimate the infiltration rate. However, these methods were 
not examined by calculating the air change rate from the measured concentration of CO2, as done in the 
study of ventilation rate in bedrooms of Danish children [27]. This method was tested instead as it was 
more accessible. 
 
It is important to note that in residential buildings without a ventilation system, the air change can be 
highly variable and difficult to control. Factors such as having windows and door open in the room can 
affect the air change rate, leading to an increase in the rate during the measurement [27].  Therefore, the 
result of the air change rate from the examination of the two apartments were not reliable for determining 
the overall IEQ. The estimation was not possible due to insufficient CO2 concentration, and the rate was 
likely too high because the measurement was taken in rooms with open windows and doors. As a result, 
these measurements were not used to determine the IEQ of the apartments. 
 
Removing the parameter of illuminance 
The illuminance parameter, which measures artificial lightning performance and room darkening, was 
removed from the TAIL protocol for residential buildings. This is because it did not meet the criteria for 
selecting an IEQ parameter. One of these criteria is that the parameter must be included in the existing 
building certification scheme and standards. The European standard for workplace lighting, EN12464, 
only applied to workplaces, and residential buildings usually do not have an artificial lightning system. 
In addition, the technical manual of CASBEE states that the artificial light sources operated by the 
occupant should not be considered. Therefore, the illuminance parameter was removed as an indicator 
for artificial light sources.  
==PAGE:76==
 
 
 
  76
 
The illuminance parameter was also used to indicate room darkening in the TAIL for hotels which is 
important for sleep quality. However, this parameter does not meet the criteria for IEQ parameter 
selection because it is not directly related to building performance. Room darkening can be achieved by 
installing curtains, which is an occupant-controlled variable and not related to building design or 
performance. Therefore, the illuminance parameter was removed as an indicator of room darkening.  
 
Selecting the rooms of measurement 
The reason why the bedrooms, living rooms, and kitchens are selected for the adapted TAIL for 
residential buildings is because they are commonly occupied rooms in a home. The bedroom is usually 
occupied by at least one person, while the living room and kitchen are usually occupied by multiple 
people the same time. These rooms also usually have different geographic locations in the buildings 
which fulfills the proposed principle from the existing TAIL protocol.  
 
The existing TAIL protocol for offices and hotels only measured certain rooms, such as office rooms and 
guest rooms, because other rooms like lobbies and meeting rooms were not designed for long-lasting 
occupation. Similarly, in residential buildings, other rooms like entrance halls, bathrooms, storage rooms 
and laundry rooms were not measured because they were not commonly occupied for long periods of 
time.  
 
The chosen timeframe for the measurement 
Based on the occupant schedule analysis, it was observed that the presence of an occupant in a room has 
an impact on the thermal environment. When occupants are present, they tend to open windows and 
doors which can result in extreme temperature values in the data. Therefore, using only the measured 
occupancy hours for rating the thermal environment may not provide an accurate presentation of the 
overall performance during a longer period. Daytime hours should therefore be used for the living rooms 
and kitchens, and night-time hours should be used for bedrooms. This decision was chosen to prove a 
more accurate presentation of the thermal environment during normal use, as it is common for windows 
to be open in naturally ventilated buildings. By considering both occupied and unoccupied hours, the 
thermal environment can be more comprehensively assessed.  
 
==PAGE:77==
 
 
 
  77
 
The selection of the timeframe for the acoustic environment was also based on the occupant schedule 
analysis. Daytime hours should be used for the living room and kitchen, as these are periods when there 
are typically occupants in these rooms. For the bedroom, nighttime hours should be used, as this is when 
occupants typically sleep. The 5th percentile should be used to represent the noise level during occupied 
periods, even though the occupant may not be present for the entire timeframe. This approach allows the 
consideration of the noise generated by sources such as kitchen equipment’s, TV, and laptops during 
times when the occupant is at home.  
 
For the indoor air quality, the occupied hours should be used for the measurement of CO2 concentrations 
since it is directly related to occupancy. For the measurement of relative humidity, it is necessary to use 
the entire measurement time to prevent the growth of allergens like dust mite.  
 
The timeframe of 06:00-18:00 was chosen for representing the daytime hours and 18:00-06:00 for 
representing the nighttime hours. While different people may have different habits and schedules, these 
timeframes were chosen to provide an objective and standardized approach for comparison purposes. By 
using these timeframes, we can analyze the performance of IEQ during periods when the occupants are 
typically active and awake (daytime) and asleep (nighttime). 
 
Range of the acceptable guidelines  
In the adapted TAIL protocol for residential buildings, the kitchen and living room areas may be an open-
concept or connected to each other such as the two examined apartments. It is assumed that the IEQ 
parameters are similar in both areas. Since the guidelines or criteria for the kitchen are not specific, the 
values for the living rooms should also be used for the kitchen. It is not examined in this project what the 
result would be if the kitchen and living room were two separate rooms. The daylight factor is calculated 
for all rooms since the bedrooms potentially are used both as a study place, playroom, and office in 
residential buildings.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:78==
 
 
 
  78
 
Rating of the overall quality of the indoor environment  
The existing protocol used an interim rating to determine the quality level for a parameter in offices and 
hotels, due to the need for multiple measurements across different locations. In the adapted TAIL 
protocol for residential buildings, interim ratings were not necessary as each room was evaluated 
separately. 
 
6.3 Improvement 
 
Examination of the two apartments  
The new TAIL protocol for residential buildings helped to measure the IEQ of two apartments. By 
adjusting the range of acceptable guidelines and rating method, it was possible to determine the quality 
level of the four components of IEQ. The apartments were rated as poor (red category) overall because 
at least one of the components was rated this way, even though other components had higher ratings. 
One reason for this was the daylight factor, which was poor due to blocked sunlight from neighboring 
buildings. The thermal environment component was also poor due to open windows, low heating settings, 
and temperatures between 0-8 degrees outside during the measurement period. The adaptation of the 
TAIL for residential buildings also only considers residential buildings without mechanical ventilation 
system, as that is most common in this type of building.  
 
Challenges  
One of the challenges is to accurately track the time people spend in a residential building without 
violating GDPR rules. In this project, the method of subtracting the time when the measured CO2 
concentration is like the background concentration was tested to determine the occupancy. However, it 
can be difficult to calculate this if the occupant is walking in and out of different rooms. Thus, further 
research is needed to develop an accurate and privacy-preserving method for tracking occupancy time. 
 
Another challenge is the method used in the examination of the two apartments when calculating the air 
change rate in a room. This method can result in higher estimation of the air change rate, which will give 
==PAGE:79==
 
 
 
  79
 
a less accurate description of the indoor air quality (IAQ) of the room. Therefore, it is needed to develop 
a more reliable method to measure the air change rate.  
Additionally, the selection of the parameters for a kitchen might need to be adjusted, as cooking on a gas 
stove can lead to an increase in the level of nitrogen dioxide (NO2), which can affect the IAQ. This 
highlights the importance of considering the different uses of rooms in residentials buildings and their 
potential impact on IEQ parameters.  
 
To ensure the success of the TAIL protocol, it is necessary to test it in a larger sample of residential 
buildings to confirm its effectiveness. This should include other types of residential buildings, not just 
apartments. However, measuring IEQ parameters in these buildings can be challenging, especially if they 
have multiple bedrooms, living rooms and kitchens. In such cases, it may be more appropriate to use the 
interim rating system that considers the overall performance of similar spaces.  
 
Finally, it should be investigated if the new TAIL protocol for residential buildings should be divided 
into weekdays and weekend. The occupant schedule analysis showed little change in results, but there 
were some differences in the thermal environment. Therefore, further research is needed to determine if 
the new TAIL protocol should be adjusted for different days of the week. Overall, addressing these 
challenges will help ensure the accuracy and effectiveness of the TAIL protocol in evaluating IEQ 
parameters in residential buildings.

7. Conclusions 
To describe the Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ) in a residential building using the TAIL rating 
scheme, a revision of TAIL currently existing for offices and hotels was necessary. The following 
modifications were made to the existing TAIL protocol: 
 
• 
In the selection of IEQ parameters, the ventilation rate was changed to the air change rate, and 
the illuminance parameter was removed from the existing twelve parameters of TAIL. 
• 
The measurements in a residential building should be made in the bedroom, kitchen, and living 
room. These rooms fulfill the criteria of the existing TAIL protocol. 
• 
The timeframe used for the measurements was adapted. For the temperature and sound pressure 
level measurements, the daytime hours should be used for the living room and kitchen, and the 
nighttime hours should be used for the bedroom. For the measurement of the CO2 concentration, 
the occupied hours should be used for all rooms. For the measurement of the relative humidity, 
the entire measurement time should be used for all rooms. Lastly, the daylight factor should be 
calculated in all rooms. 
• 
In the ranges of acceptable guideline values, the parameters of sound pressure level, CO2, air 
change rate, and daylight factor were adapted for residential buildings according to the relevant 
European standards. 
• 
In the rating of the overall IEQ of the building, the TAIL was defined separately for each of the 
measured rooms by the parameter with the lowest quality rating. Then, the overall TAIL of the 
building was determined from the room with the worst rating. 
 
Verification of the TAIL rating scheme for residential buildings was carried out through an examination 
of the IEQ in two apartments in Copenhagen, Denmark. The results showed that: 
 
• 
With the newly adapted TAIL protocol for residential buildings, it was possible to determine the 
quality level of the four components used to describe the IEQ of the two residential buildings. 
==PAGE:81==
 
 
 
  81
 
• 
The overall IEQ of the two apartments was rated as poor (red level) because at least one of the 
TAIL components was rated at this quality level, even though other TAIL components reached 
higher quality levels. 
• 
For the two apartments, it is possible to identify which parameters could be improved during a 
deep energy renovation to achieve a higher overall IEQ, in addition to identifying the parameters 
that did not require improvement. 
• 
To make the verification successful, a test in a larger pool of residential buildings is needed to 
make sure the adaptation is viable.  
 
The revision of TAIL for residential buildings had some limitations and challenges: 
• 
The European standards did not have criteria for kitchens, only for living rooms and bedrooms. 
It was assumed that the criteria for kitchens would be the same as for living rooms. 
• 
There is uncertainty regarding when an occupant is present in the different rooms of a residential 
building, as well as the method of accurately tracking their presence without violating GDPR 
rules. 
• 
In the existing TAIL protocol for hotels, the measurement is performed in unoccupied rooms, 
however, in the adapted TAIL, all rooms were measured in occupied rooms. The occupant's 
behavior can affect the IEQ results of the rooms. Potential problems arising from opening 
windows to the outside can create more extreme values in the temperature data and lower the 
rating of the thermal environment. It can also increase the air change rate in the room and lower 
the CO2 concentration, leading to a less accurate representation of the IEQ performance of the 
building. 
 
The adaptation of the TAIL protocol for residential buildings represents a promising step toward 
improving the well-being of occupants in these spaces. However, the examination of the protocol in the 
two apartments has revealed limitations and challenges that must be addressed. While this thesis 
contributes to the development of a method for prioritizing the comfort and health of residential 
occupants, further research and verification is necessary to ensure the effectiveness and reliability of the 
adapted TAIL protocol. Nevertheless, the successful implementation of such a protocol can help create 
a demand for better IEQ in residential buildings, benefiting both occupants and homeowners alike. 
==PAGE:82==
 
 
 
  82
 
Ultimately, this work contributes to the broader goal of establishing a market for evaluating and 
improving IEQ in residential buildings, which has important long-term implications for public health and 
well-being. 
 
 
==PAGE:83==
 
 
 
  83
 
Future study 
For future studies, the challenges that still need to be addressed in adapting the TAIL protocol should be 
investigated. Research is needed to develop an accurate and privacy-persevering method for tracking 
occupancy time. It is also needed to develop a more reliable method for measuring the air change rate. 
Additionally, it must be investigated if other IEQ parameters should be added such as NO2 for the 
kitchen. Lastly, for future studies, the adapted TAIL protocol should be tested in a larger sample and 
different types of residential buildings to make the verification successful.
