Abstract 
Propagation of pH fronts, and Coulombic interactions, govern the geochemical processes and 
contaminant transport in groundwater. The present study focuses on sorption and transport 
behavior of H+ and As in silica media. The investigations were carried out in batch and flow-
through (1-D and 2-D) systems containing different silica media (i.e. quartz sand and glass 
beads), by means of laboratory experiments, surface complexation and multicomponent 
transport modeling. The impact of the ionic strengths on charged species was examined by 
performing the experiments under the influence of different electrolyte background.  The 
triple layer model with charge distribution (CD-TLM), with the Donnan approximation, was 
implemented for batch and 1-D transport to explicitly account for complexation and 
electrostatic interactions; transport and geochemical calculations were performed with 
PHREEQC coupled to Matlab with the IPhreeqc module. A multicomponent steady-state 
transport model was used to simulate 2-D transport of As through a framework that 
considers multi-ionic formulation accounting for charged induced Coulombic interactions 
and comprehensive characterization of the transverse dispersion. The experimental results 
indicate that H+ sorption behavior depends on the porous media and is influenced by the 
alteration of the ionic strength, with major ions exerting a significant control on H+ sorption.  
The models offer a quantitative interpretation of the H+ sorption and of the H+ and major ion 
fronts transport in silica mineral surfaces across a range of ionic strengths. As transport 
appears significantly retarded due to sorption on silica surface sites and greatly dependent 
on pH. Finally, the 2-D model highlights the control of the coupling of dispersive fluxes, due 
to electrostatic effects, on As migration and the influence of pH on As mobility due to change 
in speciation.

Introduction

1 Introduction 
Groundwater contamination has been receiving an increased attention from government 
agencies and from the scientific community in recent years, and the protection of its quality 
is considered as one of the major environmental challenges. Due to the rise of global 
temperature, the increase of human population, the amplified industrial and agricultural 
human activities and geological processes (Appelo & Postma, 2005; Grutzmacher et al., 2013; 
Smedley & Kinniburgh, 2002) the limited water resources around the globe have become 
even scarcer, and their protection is vital for public health. Therefore, understanding and 
predicting contaminants’ propagation is essential for the evaluation and the protection of 
the water quality.  
Contaminant transport in reactive porous media, such as the subsurface, is governed by the 
complex interplay between physical and chemical processes (Rolle et al., 2018). In particular, 
the pH of the groundwater has a fundamental role on (electro-)chemical mechanisms. 
Regarding metal (hydr-)oxide surfaces, pH determines the surface potential of the mineral 
(Davis & Kent, 1990) by the protonation, or deprotonation, of the surface sites and, thus, 
influence the sorption of other charged species. Furthermore, pH influences the speciation 
of a number of heavy metalloids and oxyanion-forming elements (Smedley & Kinniburgh, 
2002) as, for instance, arsenic (As). The change in the speciation alters the charge of the 
compound, and affects its electrostatic interactions, and its dispersion coefficient, resulting 
to an overall change in the compound’s mobility. In addition to pH, major ions, naturally 
present in high concentrations at the subsurface, significantly influence the electrostatic 
interactions between the ions in solution and between the ions and the mineral’s surface 
(Biswas et al., 2014; Davis, 1982; McNeece & Hesse, 2017; Stumm, 1981). Major ions have 
also been shown to compete directly for sorption sites (Hiemstra et al., 1989; Hohl & Stumm, 
1976; Kinniburgh, 1983). Hence, it is of primary importance to offer a correct description of 
the complex interactions between protons, major ions and the mineral surface in order to 
predict the fate and transport of contaminants traveling in the subsurface. 
In the aqueous phase, description of the transport of charged species in multicomponent 
systems often disregard Coulombic interactions between ions. However, recent 
contributions have demonstrated the important impact of these electrostatic interactions on 
the diffusive/dispersive fluxes of ions (Appelo & Wersin, 2007; Lasaga, 1979; Muniruzzaman 
& Rolle, 2014, 2015; Rolle et al., 2013). This is of particular relevance in the presence of 
electrochemical gradients, often observed in natural systems (Liu et al., 2011; 
Muniruzzaman & Rolle, 2016, 2017; Rolle et al., 2018).

Characterizing the mineral – liquid interactions can be a challenging task due to the complex 
processes occurring in the subsurface. The most prevalent method is by model-based 
interpretations; coupling of experimental data with a relevant model (Muniruzzaman & 
Rolle, 2015, 2017; Prigiobbe et al., 2012; Prigiobbe & Bryant, 2014; Rolle et al., 2012).  
Surface complexation models (SCM) aim at offering a mechanistic description of the chemical 
and electrostatic interactions occurring at the mineral – porewater interface during sorption 
processes (Appelo & Wersin, 2007; Davis et al., 1990; Dzombak & Morel, 1990; Hiemstra et 
al., 1989; Hiemstra & Van Riemdsijk, 1996, 1999; McNeece & Hesse, 2016, 2017; Prigiobbe 
& Bryant, 2014; Van Riemsdijk & Hiemstra, 2006). SCMs have primarily been implemented 
for characterizing the sorption mechanisms occurring on the surfaces of iron (hydr-)oxides, 
as the latter are well known for representing the key control of the cycling of trace metals in 
the subsurface (Dzombak and Morel, 1990, Gustafsson, 2003; Rietra et al., 2000, 2001). The 
applications of SCMs for these minerals in batch and flow through systems (Prigiobbe et al., 
2012; Prigiobbe & Bryant, 2014), as well as in single- and complex multi-species 
environment, have demonstrated that this description represents a valuable alternative to 
the traditional empirical sorption modeling approach, due to their ability in capturing non-
linear behavior, resulting from the chemical as well as electrostatic contributions. 
Conversely, recent studies have pointed out the difficulty in reproducing the profiles of 
protons and major ions, during transport in silica porous media, under the influence of 
strong electrolyte background (McNeece & Hesse, 2016, 2017).  
In the subsurface, silica represents the predominant mineral phase (Flörke et al., 2008). 
However, the understanding of the silica-water interface has been very limited compared to 
iron hydroxides, which have been extensively studied (Dzombak & Morel, 1990; Gustafsson, 
2003; Hingston et al., 1972; Rietra et al., 2000, 2001). Therefore, it is important to offer a 
correct description of H+ and major ions transport in silica media in order to interpret the 
geochemical processes occurring in the groundwater system (Lowe et al., 2015). 
Arsenic (As) is recognized as one of the most severe geogenic contaminations of the 
groundwater, as it is ubiquitous in nature (Smedley & Kinniburgh, 2002). It poses a major 
threat to drinking supply due to its toxicity and its elevated concentrations in ground waters 
over large territory. For instance, As contamination has been reported in Vietnam (Berg et 
al., 2001), in the Bengal and Central Myanmar Basin (Acharyya, 2005; BGS & DPHE, 2001; 
Datta et al., 2015; Feroze Ahmed & Ahmed, 2013; Van Geen et al., 2014), in Nepal (Thakur et 
al., 2011), Mexico (Armienta et al., 1995), S.W. Nigeria (Amanambu & Egbinola, 2015), where 
the problem of arsenic contamination of the groundwater aquifers is evident, with 
concentrations exceeding by far the guidelines for drinking water set by WHO (2017) 
(provisional guideline value: 10 μg/L).

Values of As concentration found in literature, cover a large range with maximum values 
under natural conditions up to 5 mg/L (Karim, 2000; Mandal & Suzuki, 2002), and due to 
human intervention, up to 850 mg/L (Nordstrom & Alpers, 1999). Under natural conditions, 
the highest observed concentrations of As are found in groundwater due to the strong 
mineral - water interactions, and due to the great tendency of the groundwater to 
concentrate As. The concentration of As in groundwater varies according to the source of 
arsenic origin and to the geochemical conditions (Smedley & Kinniburgh, 2002).  
To predict As fate in ground water it is essential to comprehend how As travels within an 
aquifer and to consider the processes that limit its mobility. Arsenic is one of the most 
problematic elements due to its mobility in a broad spectrum of pH and redox conditions. 
The latter are the most important factors controlling As sorption kinetics and speciation 
(Smedley & Kinniburgh, 2002). The predominant forms of arsenic in the groundwater are 
arsenites (As(III)) and arsenates (As(V)), which are soluble at pH values typically found in 
groundwater (6.5-8.5) (Smedley & Kinniburgh, 2002). Since As is a reactive chemical 
compound it will not follow a conservative transport. It will travel in reaction to the 
medium’s solid phase and the ions in solution. Its movement will be retarded due to 
electrostatic interactions, adsorption reactions; by forming surface complexes with a variety 
of oxides (Fe-, Al-, etc.) and precipitation (Smedley & Kinniburgh, 2002). The governing 
mechanisms of arsenic’s release in the groundwater are: reductive dissolution of iron oxides 
and oxidation of sulfide minerals; both processes dependent on pH and redox conditions 
(Smedley & Kinniburgh, 2013).  
As transport is widely studied for iron oxides (Dixit & Hering, 2003; Jain et al., 1999; Pierce 
& Moore, 1982; Raven et al., 1998; Zhao & Stanforth, 2001), and its mechanisms are well 
understood. However, knowledge regarding As interactions with silica minerals is limited.

1.1 Objectives 
The overall objective of the present thesis is to strengthen our knowledge and formulate a 
description of proton sorption on silica mineral surfaces, using surface complexation models, 
in order to improve our ability in understanding and predicting charged contaminants’ 
release, and mobility in silica porous media. The incentive in studying proton sorption in 
porous media was raised by its cardinal implications on the geochemical processes occurring 
in the groundwater, and on the contaminant transport mechanisms. The selection of arsenic, 
as the contaminant for study, was due to its severe impact on the environment and the 
human health, and to its elevated concentrations in groundwater around the globe. This 
study focuses on the following key objectives: 
 Study the H+ sorption mechanisms onto silica media and investigate potential 
differences in H+ affinity between different silica materials: glass beads and sand.  
 Explore the importance of ionic strength and the interaction between major ions and 
H+ taking place during sorption.  
 Assess the role of Coulombic interactions between ions in the solution, and on the 
solid – solution interface between ions and charged mineral surfaces. 
 Generate reliable data to implement a surface complexation model for simulating and 
offering quantitative interpretation of the H+ sorption onto silica mineral surfaces 
across a range of ionic strengths. 
 Assess the role of pH on As mobility in flow-through systems through its influence (i) 
on sorption behavior and (ii) on Coulombic and chemical interactions taking place in 
the aqueous phase. 
To that end, a set of batch and flow-through (1-D column) experiments was carried out and 
a surface complexation model was implemented, that explicitly accounts for surface 
complexation and electrostatic interactions. The SCM model simulated batch systems and 1-
D multicomponent transport. In batch modeling, a number of different approaches was 
tested to provide a more solid interpretation of the underlying mechanisms. Moreover, an 
additional model was implemented for 2-D multicomponent transport to study the influence 
of pH on arsenic mobility in the aqueous phase, accounting for Coulombic interactions and 
change in speciation.

2 Materials and Methods 
This section provides information about the experimental set up, the data treatment and the 
modeling approach. The experiments were performed in batch and flow-through setups (1-
D column and 2-D tank), containing silica media. The proton and arsenic sorption dynamics 
were precisely studied in the batch experiments, to provide us with information on the 
proton sorption capacities of the porous media, and to guarantee the accurate 
implementation of the flow-through experiments. Both experimental setups were used to 
optimize batch and transport models and to determine a set of parameters. The proton 
sorption capacities of different grain size media, and the affinity constants of the respective 
surface complexation reactions, were obtained with inverse modeling of both batch and 
column experiments. 
2.1 Experimental Setup 
Laboratory, batch and flow-through (1-D and 2-D), experiments were carried out to study 
the sorption interactions between silica mineral surfaces and charge species. 
Batch experiments were performed to assess the H+ sorption under a variety of experimental 
set ups (background conditions, materials, etc.), and to provide us with information on the 
chemical interaction of the solute-mineral interface, and with an overview of H+ kinetics, 
prior to the column experiments. The latter were implemented so as to examine H+ 
propagation as well as arsenic transport in a flow-through system.  
2.1.1 Materials 
The experiments were systematically carried out using two types of silica materials, sand 
and glass beads, to define the porous matrix. Experiments were also repeated by varying 
the grain size of each medium to acquire observations relative to the solid phase surface 
area. Therefore, two materials of three different grain sizes were used to carry out the 
experiments. Their properties and composition are displayed in Table 2.1.

2.1.4 Analytical Methods 
All samples were analyzed at the Environmental Engineering Department of DTU. 
Concentrations of anions (Cl-, Br-) were determined with Ion Chromatography (IC), 
Inductively Coupled Plasma Atomic Emission Spectroscopy (ICP-OES) for cations (Na+, K+, 
Ca2+, Mg2+), and Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometry (ICP-MS) for As species. All 
three analyses required samples 1% acidified with HNO3. 
pH was measured in line, with a closed KCl refillable pH electrode (PHC805, HACH Denmark), 
with a ceramic porous end. The specific probe was used to minimize KCl leakage in the 
solution. The leakage was monitored during the experiments to assess the effect on low ionic 
strength solutions, which turned out to be negligible.  
Non-invasive high spatial resolution monitoring of the 1D profile of the bulk solution pH was 
carried out, with optical non-invasive pH sensors (preSens Precision Sensing Gmbh, 
Regensburg, Germany) glued on the inner wall of the columns. These measurements allowed 
us to capture the pH front propagation, supporting the design of the experiments, as well as 
offering additional modeling constraints. The operation of the sensors is founded on a 
method called dual lifetime referencing (DLR) (Boniello et al., 2012; Borisov et al., 2010).  
2.2 Modeling Approach  
Modeling of the experiments was performed in order to reproduce the observations and to 
offer a model base quantitative interpretation of the H+ interaction with the silica media, by 
means of surface complexation and electrostatic interactions, mainly considering the triple 
layer model with charge distribution (CD-TLM). Different modeling approaches of the batch 
systems were implemented to examine the surface-solution interactions. However, CD-TLM 
will be considered as the reference model. The key difference of the various surface 
complexation models, is the different perception of the effect of the electrostatic charge that 
directly influences ion sorption. 
The geochemical (i.e. aqueous speciation, surface complexation reactions) and transport 
(advection, dispersion) calculations, were carried out in the geochemical code PHREEQC 
(Parkhurst & Appelo, 2013). PHREEQC was coupled with Matlab through the IPhreeqc 
module (Charlton & Parkhurst, 2011), in order to benefit from the computational capabilities 
of Matlab (i.e. parallelizing simulations, calibration) (Muniruzzaman & Rolle, 2016).

2.2.1 Surface Complexation Model 
Surface complexation models (SCM), besides the aqueous solution chemistry, account for 
charged surface species (Hayes, 1987). More specifically, the SCM explicitly considers (Davis 
& Kent, 1990): 
i. 
Formation of surface complexes due to reactions between surface functional groups 
and ions in solution. 
ii. 
 Electrostatic (Coulombic) interactions due to, basic properties of the functional 
groups, surface charge and electric surface potential. 
iii. 
Mass law equations accounting for chemical equilibria of aqueous and surface 
complexation reactions. 
A number of surface complexation models were considered before concluding to the triple 
layer model (TLM), first introduced by Stern (Westal, 1987). The TLM was often used to 
account for sorption on metal (hydr)oxides (Davis & Kent, 1990; Goldberg, 1992) The main 
advantage of the TLM is that it distinguishes between weakly and strongly bound ions due 
to the two adsorption planes (Hayes, 1987) that allowed the TLM to be used for modeling 
the sorption of ionic species (Davis & Leckie, 1978, 1980). 
2.2.1.1 Triple Layer Model with Charge Distribution 
CD-TLM, is a 2 pK surface protonation model, and its concept is based on the presence of 
three planes of charge accumulation (Davis et al., 1978; Venema et al., 1995). The first plane 
consists of the inner-sphere complexes - ions that are directly sorb on the ligands of the 
mineral surface. On the second plane – Inner Helmholtz Plane (i.e., β-plane) – larger ions 
(Na+, Ca2+, etc.) form outer-sphere complexes constrained by their waters of hydration 
(McNeece & Hesse, 2017). The third plane – Outer Helmholtz Plane (i.e., d-plane) –marks the 
end of the Stern layer and the beginning of the Diffuse Double Layer, where the electrostatic 
contribution of the diffused charged species is being considered. Figure 2.3 represents the 
structure of the electrical double layer of silica mineral. In the DDL, a swarm of counter ions 
equilibrates with the cumulative β-plane surface charge (Hayes, 1987; McNeece & Hesse, 
2016, 2017). Mathematically, this is expressed with the following charge balance equation 
(Davis et al., 1978):

In the first part of the results the focus will be laid on the mineral – solution interactions in

3.1.1 Proton sorption reaction mechanisms  
Batch experiments provided us with understanding on proton sorption dynamics and 
electrostatic interactions at the liquid-mineral interface. Additionally, they facilitated the 
design of the flow-through transport experiments, giving us an overview of the main 
interactions to be accounted. 
3.1.1.1 Proton sorption equilibria 
Acid-base titrations were performed to characterize H+ sorption, determine the capacity of 
different silica porous media and examine the influence of ionic strengths on the electrostatic 
interactions at the mineral/pore-water interface and the electrolyte dependency of proton 
adsorption. Figure 3.1 and Figure 3.2 display the change in pH during acid and base 
potentiometric titrations, respectively. 
Acid titrations (Figure 3.1) gave us an insight on the proton sorption affinity of silica, which 
even though is low compared to other minerals (i.e. clay), it remains significant. Blank 
titrations were performed to highlight the mineral’s effect, and the two media under scrutiny 
are shown to adsorb H+. However, sorption is compound specific, as there are clear 
differences between the studied media.  
The H+ affinity of the glass beads appears to be larger than sand’s. According to Figure 3.1b, 
the initial pH of the glass beads suspension is 10. During the cleaning procedure the 
analogous pH was around 6. When the glass beads dried and again introduced in Milli-Q 
water, the pH rose to 10 overnight. One possible explanation is that the glass beads lead to 
the auto-ionization of water and, subsequently, sorb a large amount of protons. This involves 
that positively charged ions are released from the surface in order to maintain charge 
balance. Another possible explanation of the increase of pH, could be based on the presence 
of (>13%) sodium oxides (Na2O) in glass beads composition. Na2O is a base anhydride of 
sodium hydroxide which in contact with water reacts and produces NaOH.

3.1.1.2 Proton Sorption Kinetics  
Figure 3.3 shows the pH change recorded over time after placing the silica media in contact 
with solutions of different pH. The equilibration time for both sand and glass beads at pH 2, 
seem very short as pH did not dayshift significantly over the time of the experiments. 
However, this might be due to surface site saturation, and excess of H+ in solution.  
On the contrary, sand reacted with increasing time with the addition of solutions 3, 4 and 5 
pH, presenting an increase in the solution’s pH of almost 1 value. The most time dependent 
case was the batch filled with glass beads submerged in an initial solution of pH 3. The 
solution reached a pH 9 value over a period 10 days, showing a large proton affinity. For 
solutions of pH 3, 4 and 5, a kinetic effect in the uptake of protons is apparent as solution pH 
increased by about a unit. The most time dependent case was the batch filled with glass 
beads submerged in an initial solution of pH 3. The solution reached pH 9 after a period of 
ten days, showing a significant proton uptake taking place at low kinetic rate. 
In all cases, for initial pH higher than 3, proton sorption kinetics appeared to be a two-step 
process; a short period of rapid initial uptake followed by a slower sorption process with 
increasing time. The fast step might be credited to adsorption governed by diffusion, 
reaching equilibrium in a short period of time (Davis & Kent, 1990). The second step might 
be due to structural rearrangement, or micro-pore diffusion (Kent, 1983) and in most of the 
cases takes more than 10 days to reach equilibrium.

3.2 pH dependent arsenic transport in silica porous media   
The dependence of As transport, in silica porous media, on pH was studied through a series 
of column experiments, exploring the sorption behavior under various pH and in terms of a 
two dimensional multicomponent transport model, focusing on the influence of Coulombic 
interactions and speciation reactions on As transverse displacement.       
3.2.1 Transport of arsenic in silica porous medium in 1-D  
Column experiments were performed to assess the sorption affinity of As on silica minerals 
as a function of pH and the effect of pH and major cations on As mobility. The normalized 
concentration of As is plotted as a function of pore volumes in Figure 3.15. 
 
Figure 3.15: Breakthrough of As in columns packed with sand under an HCl, Milli-Q water and base injection with 
ionic strength of: a. 10-3 M and b. 10 -1 M. 
For all pH injections, As is significantly retarded as it arrives after the conservative 
breakthrough, at least at 2 PV, indicating sorption onto silica media. In the case of the low 
ionic strength solution, As travels with a longer retardation while being under acidic 
conditions than under neutral or basic regimes, were the fronts appear to have similar 
pattern. This result suggests that, the extent of sorption of As relates to the surface charge of 
the solid; similar to previous observations made for iron oxides (Gustafsson & Bhattacharya, 
2007; Dixit and Hering 2003). Lower pH leads to higher positive charge of the surface (i.e. 
==PAGE:50==
Results and Discussion  
 
38 
 
SiOH+2), and ultimately results in enhanced affinity of As for the surface site, and thus, is 
dependent on pH since OH- and H+ determine the surface charge of silica. On the contrary, at 
large pH the surface is negatively charged (i.e. SiO-), and therefore, As propagates without 
significant retardation. The similarity of the breakthrough curves of As in the circumneutral 
and basic pH, can be explained by the fact that the surface remains deprotonated at that 
range (Dzombak & Morel, 1990). The major ions of the ionic strength seems to substantially 
affect As sorption. The shape of the As breakthrough curve in the case of the acidic and basic 
plume, presents an almost similar trend, with the front occurring faster than the highly 
retarded front of As under neutral pH conditions. An explanation might be, that in sand, 
different surface species, that interact with pH, co-exist, which present significant As 
sorption affinity in different pH ranges.   
As(V) forms complexes with a large number of metal oxides. Silica appears, as expected, to 
have significantly lower affinity compared to hydrous ferric oxides and to other metal oxides 
(Xu et al., 1988; Jain et al., 1999; Hiemstra & Van Riemdsijk, 1999); particularly for two 
reasons. Firstly, the specific surface area of silica is considerably lower, providing less 
binding sites. Moreover, the point of zero charge of Si has been found to be at the range of 
2.5-3 pH while the analogous of goethite and HFO is 7.8 and 7.6 respectively, as indicated by 
some studies (Trivedi et al., 2001; Xu & Axe, 2004). That shows, that the surface of HFO and 
goethite is positively charged for a larger range of pH, attracting the, mainly, negatively 
charged As(V) species. 
At low pH ranges As could be present as As(III). Therefore, since we are only measuring 
As(V) at the outlet, the effect of speciation on the As breakthrough concentration plotted in 
the graphs should not be neglected.  
To interpret satisfactorily the As behavior during transport, would be useful to have an 
insight on if As molecules sorb strongly, and if they form inner- or outer-sphere complexes. 
E.g. As forms strong inner-sphere complexes on iron oxides (Gustafsson & Bhattacharya, 
2007), an information that facilitated understanding the nature of As interaction with the 
surface and the charged species in the solution. A SCM would be a useful tool to reach to that 
information.

4 Conclusions 
The mechanisms of H+ sorption onto silica media and interactions with the major ions were 
assessed through laboratory-scale experiments and geochemical modeling. This analysis 
represented the cornerstone for studying the influence of pH on the As mobility through 
silica porous media via surface interaction, change in aqueous chemical speciation and 
Coulombic interactions. 
The experimental results indicated significant proton affinity for silica mineral surface and, 
therefore, strong retardation of pH fronts when traveling in silica porous media. Transport 
behavior presented high dependence on the physical and electrochemical properties of the 
porous media; with glass beads demonstrating significantly stronger proton affinity, and 
thus, more retarded pH breakthrough than sand. Important finding of the study was that 
major ions exert a significant control on H+ sorption, and vice versa. The extent of silica-
solution interactions clearly depends on ionic strength and silica surface found to release 
major ions with sorption of H+. Indicative example of this interplay, is the fast pulse of H+ 
observed at 1 PV, in the cases of acid and base injection with high electrolyte background, in 
columns packed with quartz sand. The pulse found to correspond to cation (Mg2+, Ca2+, K+) 
release from the mineral.  
Besides the direct interpretation of experimental results, the generation of reliable data 
allowed the implementation of a model aiming at offering a quantitative interpretation of the 
H+ sorption and transport in silica media. In particular, two distinct surface complexation 
models, describing the interactions between aqueous solutes with sand and glass beads, 
were developed and calibrated based on batch and flow-through experiments. An interesting 
aspect of this work is that, even though the system ostensibly appears simple, complex 
modeling was required to satisfyingly reproduce the experimental results. In particular, the 
multicomponent reactive transport was able to capture most of the features of the pH and 
ionic breakthrough curves monitored at the outlet of the columns. 
The study of As transport under variable pH demonstrated its significant retardation due to 
sorption on silica surface sites. The dependence of As sorption on pH was underlined, with 
As breakthrough front at acidic conditions presenting the stronger retardation, in an 
environment of low ionic strength. For further investigation on As mobility, a 
multicomponent steady-state reactive transport model was implemented to simulate 2-D 
transport of As under the influence of pH gradients, through a framework, accounting for 
charged induced Coulombic interactions and aqueous speciation reactions. The model 
indicated that the Coulombic interactions and the coupling of dispersive fluxes, as well as, 
speciation reactions due to spatial variation in pH can significantly influence As mobility 
through a complex interplay. 
==PAGE:57==
Conclusions  
 
45 
 
Overall, the study contributed to strengthen our knowledge on H+ and As transport in silica 
minerals. Surface complexation modeling, and particularly CD-TLM, could be used as a tool, 
to further improve our understanding and for predicting charged contaminant transport in 
silica porous media. 
The continuation of this study could focus on model implementations, with limited 
assumptions-that could be achieved by incorporating higher complexity to the model-, could 
lead in further insights, and an even more comprehensive understanding of the sorption 
mechanisms and electrostatic interactions in the subsurface. Furthermore, serious focus 
should be laid on carrying out 2-D tank experiments with reverse gradients, so as to validate 
the 2-D model, and on implementing surface complexation reactions for As in order to model 
the 1-D column experiments. Finally, more sorption experiments could be performed with 
other species, in order to develop an extensive database for silica surface complexation 
models.

6.3 Appendix C 
Silica Dissolution - Experimental Method 
The dissolution of silica as a function of pH was studied in batch system and under flow-
through conditions. For the batch experiments, sand and glass beads of d= 1mm were 
submerged in solutions of a range of pH (2, 3, 6, 10, 12), additionally a blank batch for each 
pH value as a reference. The solid solution ratio was 1:1 (100g in 100mL) and the solid 
suspensions were let to equilibrate for 24 hours. The column experiment was performed 
only for glass beads by injecting an acidic solution (HCl, pH 2.9). The studied pH range of the 
column experiment was from 10.5 to 8.5. Samples were acidified with 1% HNO3- and 
analyzed for silica. 
Silica dissolution - Results 
The dissolution of silica was studied in batch systems for a range of pH from acidic to basic 
(2, 3, 6, 10, 12) and in flow-through column setup packed with glass beads by injecting an 
HCl solution of pH 2.9. There is a clear influence of the pH on dissolution of silica. In the batch 
systems, glass beads appear to dissolve more within the time provided for equilibration. A 
blank batch was also performed to assess the dissolution of the glass vial containing the 
solution. The maximum dissolved concentration of glass beads (0.27 mM) is observed at pH 
6 and 10 (Figure 6.6a) and at pH 12 it drops, since the majority of the dissolved silica species 
is attributed to the glass vial. Similar trend to silica is observed for Na+ ions which 
compensate the negative charge of silica. Dissolved quartz silica in significant concentration 
was found only at strongly alkaline pH.

Dissolution of silica appears to be dependent on surface charge, as the solubility rises with 
the increase of SiO- sites. Si is dissolved in the in a solution when hydrolysis of the last silicon-
oxygen bond occurs (Mazer & Walther, 1994). That can explain the higher solubility of glass 
beads, which can be credited to the difference in crystalline structure; the Si-O bonds at 
synthetic glasses are weaker than the one in quartz sand, resulting to the faster release of Si 
ions. 
Overall, changes in pH can be attributed to Si dissolution, since cations, including H+, will 
compensate the negative charge generated in the solution. Moreover, silica ions should be 
considered when closing the charge balance to assess the validity of the results.

As Sorption Edge 
A set of sorption edge batch experiments were conducted to quantify arsenic sorption on 
silica oxides as a function of pH. Solid suspensions at a range of pH (3 to 10) were prepared 
in polypropylene flasks. Sand of d=1 mm grain size, was immersed at solutions of different 
concentrations of As(V) with NaCl as electrolyte background. Subsequently, were let to 
equilibrate for 24 hours. The suspensions were continuously mixed by an oscillator orbital 
rotator. The As solution was prepared from reagent grade Na2HAsO4·7H2O. The suspensions 
had a mineral-solution ratio 1:10; 50g of sand and 500mL of solution respecting the surface 
area per volume of solution used by Dixit and Hering (2003) and considering the 
equilibration time reported for As(V)-goethite system (Raven et al., 1998 ; Dixit & Hering, 
2003). After the reaction time, the sample was taken after filtering with 0.2 μm pore size 
cellulose acetate membrane filters, acidified with 1% HNO3- and sent for analysis. The 
experimental conditions are presented in detail in Table 6.1.

As Sorption Edge Results 
The results of the As sorption edge experiments did not provide us with any noteworthy 
information, as there was not a clear trend. That is the reason why they were excluded from 
the main report. However, similar results have been presented in Xu et al. work (1988) for 
the sorption edge of quartz sand, showing no significant effect in pH changes even after a 
very long exposure. The experimental procedure was based on the work of Dixit and Hering 
(2003) which was done on HFO, goethite and magnetite, all of which have a higher specific 
surface area than sand. Thus, the solid solution ratio is presumably very low to highlight the 
sorption capabilities of silica and therefore the experiment failed to give us an insight on As 
sorption under a variety of pH.

6.6 Appendix F 
Tank Experiment in 2D 
The main ideas behind this experiment is the existence of hydro-chemical gradient in the 
subsurface, highlighting the influence of pH on as mobility due to change in speciation and 
to Coulombic interactions. 
The experiment is carried out in a quasi-two-dimensional set up. It is comprised of an acrylic-
glass container with inner dimensions: 100 cm length × 19 cm height × 1 cm width. The tank 
is packed with glass beads of 1 mm grain size, following a wet packing procedure (Haberer 
et al., 2012). The selection of the medium of study is done based on results of the batch and 
the column experiments to minimize interactions with the solid phase (i.e. glass beads were 
found to react the less with H+ and arsenic). The water table level is set at 12 cm, with the 
glass beads placed up to 14-15 cm. The setup of the Tank experiment is illustrated in Figure 
6.14. 
 
Figure 6.14: Schematic representation of the laboratory setup of the tank experiment. 
On both ends, the tank is equipped with 24, 5mm spaced, ports (made of Alltech rubber 
septa) allowing a high resolution sampling. The ports are pierced with hollow needles 
attached to rubber tubing and directly linked to two high precision peristaltic pumps, one at 
the inlet and one at the outlet, establishing a uniform flow, with a seepage velocity of 1 
m/day, ensuring the stability of the water table. For the inlet solution, three tedlar bags, 
stripped of oxygen, are connected to the pump. Two of the bags contain a solution of NaOH 
(pH 11) which is injected at ports 1-8 and 17-24. The third, containing a solution of 1 mg/l 
of As and HCl (pH 3) is introduced in the middle ports (9-16). The specific injection layout 
generates a concentration gradient which creates an environment of spatial variability along 
the vertical profile. Prior to the As injection the porous medium is flushed with NaOH (pH 
11) for the duration of approximately 7 pore volumes. Sampling and pH measurement is
