Abstract
Due to the higher installed capacity for renewable ﬂuctuating energy sources, the need to store
electricity produced by them has increased. Nowadays a lot of research is focused on Power-
to-Gas technology (P2G), which transforms this electricity into fuel that can be stored. The
eﬃciency of this process is however quite small.
In an attempt to increase the eﬃciency from electricity to fuel production, heat can be recovered
from the high temperature processes that take place during the transformation. This thesis
presents the optimization of P2G systems with the implementation of district heating to recover
this heat. Two diﬀerent technologies are studied: a low temperature alkaline electrolyzer and a
high temperature solid oxide electrochemical cell (SOEC). The P2G with alkaline electrolyzer
performs better according to a system eﬃciency of 84.18 % in comparison with the P2G with
SOEC, which has an eﬃciency of 70.73 %. However, a slightly better eﬃciency of 84.29 % is
achieved using a Power-to-Hydrogen (P2H) system with an alkaline electrolyzer that splits the
water molecule into pure hydrogen.
Sensitivity analysis is carried out on the plant size, the system pressure, the temperature of
the fuel out and the eﬀectiveness of the heat exchanger. Also a study on the cell voltage of
the electrolyzers is included, proving that the amount of heat is zero at their correspondent
thermoneutral voltages and increases with voltages above this one. This higher cell voltage
decreases at the same time the total system eﬃciency due to larger power consumption.
In the next step, a techno-economic analysis of the P2G with SOEC and the P2H is done.
First of all, the Purchasing Cost of Equipment (PEC) for the system components is calculated
in order to obtain the initial investment.
After, the main focus is put in the net present
value (NPV) and the payback time of the project. The economic evaluation shows that the
P2G requires a big investment but generates high revenues while operating, which leads to a
positive NPV of 841,573.33 $ after 20 years of lifetime. On the other hand the P2H turns out
to be economically infeasible due to low revenues from low production. When increasing the
hours of operation or the plant size, the two systems follow the same tendency giving more
proﬁt or more losses respectively.
Ultimately this thesis performs a study on danish electricity prices for the past year, aiming to
estimate the price that would make the investments economically feasible and to calculate the
==PAGE:6==
number of hours that had a price below that one during 2015. As expected, it is found that
the likelihood of having electricity prices where the P2H gives proﬁt is very low and on the
contrary very high for the P2G with SOEC, due to the estimated break-even price of 251.87
and 21.50 DKK/MWh respectively.

Chapter 1
Introduction
We live in a world where issues such as energy and environment are very closely related. The
large use of fossil fuels to supply the global energy demand has caused several damages to
the environment such as air pollution, water scarcity, ocean acidiﬁcation or soil contamination
[1]. Moreover several organizations have studied the global average temperature on Earth for
the past years concluding that it has increased at a rate of roughly 0.15-0.20 ◦C per decade
since 1975 [2].
These facts are accompanied by the scarcity factor of fossil fuel resources;
the international reserves of crude oil are declining, and some pessimistic sources refer to an
important detriment in the annual oil availability for 2050.
Another startling factor is the raise in the global population and thus the increase in the
energy supply needed to cover the global demand. The current world population is 7.3 billion
and according to the United Nations it is expected to reach 9.7 billion in 2050 [3].
With such an alarming scenario people have put a lot of eﬀort in ﬁnding new sources of energy
supply and in recent years progress has been made in developing cleaner, more eﬃcient energy
technologies. Indeed, thanks to the progress of the renewable energy sources, economic growth
and energy-related emissions – which have historically moved in the same direction – are starting
to decouple [4].
Renewable Energy technologies have become essential in the Energy transformation to switch

long-term energy goal is to become completely independent of fossil fuels use by 2050 [5].
However, renewable sources are uncertain and ﬂuctuating, which happens to be an issue when

Among all the diﬀerent electricity storage technologies that are being developed nowadays this
report will study a Power-to-Gas System (P2G). Simply speaking, P2G is deﬁned as the con-
==PAGE:19==
5
version of electrical energy into chemical energy by splitting water molecules in an electrolyzer.
The chemical energy or fuel can then be stored in diﬀerent forms and used for many diﬀerent
purposes. The produced hydrogen (H2) can be used directly in applications such as power
generation or vehicle fuel. It can also feed fuel cells to produce electricity from the resultant
chemical reaction [5].
If CO2 is combined with the H2 from the water splitting, synthetic
methane (CH4) can be obtained. This chemical happens to have very similar properties to
natural gas and therefore it can be used in the natural gas infrastructure as its replacement.
The electrical energy to run the electrolyzer can come from renewable sources, hence P2G would
represent one potential tool for managing renewable power intermittency and over-generation.
And at the same time the natural gas infrastructure will become greener and more sustainable
when implementing gas produced from renewable energy.
P2G also contributes to achieve grid stability, a problem that arises when the share of renewable
energy is larger in the system. But apart from adding ﬂexibility to the system, P2G systems can
be used to store the electricity whenever the power prices are low and sell this stored electricity
during the time periods in which the price is high. The commercial use of P2G technology is
primarily being considered in Europe, where it is being reviewed by the European Gas Research
Group as well as the energy departments of several nations [5]. Currently Germany is one of
the most developed countries in this area with projects like ”BioCat”, funded by ForskEL [7].
Another example is Audi’s 6 MW alkaline electrolysis plant in Werlte, Germany, that produces
H2 [8].
Together with CO2, the H2 is chemically converted to CH4, which is subsequently
injected into the gas network. The plant opened in 2013 and via a special certiﬁcation scheme
managed by Audi, owners of Audi’s A3 g-tron gas vehicle can ﬁll up their vehicles with green
CH4 from the natural gas network.
Another example is E.ON’s plant in Falkenhagen, Germany, which is a 2 MW alkaline elec-
trolysis plant where the H2 produced is injected direct into the gas network [9]. Inaugurated
in August 2013, the plant produces 360 Nm3 of H2 per hour. In Denmark last February 2014,
excess wind power was turned into green gas in Avedøre. It was a combination of the already
mentioned BioCat project and another project in Avedøre to convert H2 into eco-friendly gas

for the Danish gas grid.
In the next years sources like GTM (Green Technology Media) foresees rapid growth and pen-
etration of electricity storage solutions accelerating in grid-scale utility markets, residential
markets and commercial building markets [10]. The pathway for longer-term electricity storage
looks more promising in the ﬁeld of battery technology such as sodium-ion and liquid batteries
[11]. However, while battery costs go up in proportion to the quantity of energy stored (dura-
tion), P2G cost is nearly independent of the quantity of energy stored when the existing gas
grid is used as the storage medium [12].
Even though there is a lot of research being carried out within P2G systems, it is still diﬃcult
to achieve high eﬃciencies that would make this technology an attractive option for electricity
storage. In an attempt to improve this process, heat recovery can be applied; the temperature
required for the methanation process (when H2 is combined with CO2 to form CH4) is very
high, around 500 ◦C. Thus, heat can be extracted and utilized for other purpose in order to
improve the total system eﬃciency. This report investigates the eﬀect of implementing heat
recovery by means of district heating water in two diﬀerent P2G systems from a technical and
economic viewpoint.
The structure of this thesis is divided into background, methodology, results, discussion and
conclusion chapters. First of all, the background chapter describes the water electrolysis and
the methanation processes based on the literature review. It also includes information and
references about the Danish gas network and the danish district heating system and ﬁnally an
explanation of the main software used in the thesis.
After this, the methods followed through the project are presented. Firstly, the system designs
are explained including the layout of the cycles and the mathematical model for each type of
electrolyzer. Also the district heating implementation into these systems is described. Follow-
ing, the thermodynamic modelling explains the assumptions to model such systems and it lists
the ﬁxed parameters as well as the ones that are subject to optimization. Lately in this subsec-
tion there is a brief description of the mathematical model for each system component. Next,
the modelling for the techno-economic analysis explains how the costs were calculated for the

The results chapter is divided in two parts. The ﬁrst one makes reference to the thermodynamic

2.1
The Water Electrolysis
The electrolysis of water is a process that uses water (H2O) as a raw material for producing
hydrogen H2 and oxygen gas O2 by means of an electric current. Electrolyzers consist of an
anode and a cathode placed in the water and separated by an electrolyte. Due to the electric
current driving through the electrodes H2 appears at the cathode, the negative electrode where
electrons enter the water, and oxygen appears at the anode, the positive electrode. If Faradaic
eﬃciency is assumed to be ideal, the amount of H2 generated is twice the amount of O2, and
both are proportional to the total electrical charge conducted by the solution [13].

Alkaline water electrolysis has a long history in the chemical industry being the alkaline elec-
trolyzer the most widely used technology for this process. It has been commercially used since
the 1920s and represents the most mature and competitive technology nowadays. Some of its
advantages are the low temperature required (50-90 ◦C) and the high purity of the products
(≥99.8% H2) [17]. The electrolyte is an aqueous alkaline solution of either sodium hydroxide
(NaOH) or potassium hydroxide (KOH) and electrodes are commonly made of nickel-coated
steel [18].
One drawback of this technology can be the limited response to variations in the electrical input
and thus the limited ability to operate with ﬂuctuating electricity sources. Also, the operating

2.1.2
Solid Oxide Electrochemical Cell - SOEC
Fuel cells can be reversed to operate as electrolyzers and produce fuel from electricity and
water. A SOEC consists of a number of single cells, either planar or tubular, forming a stack.
It operates at high temperatures (700-1000 ◦C) [19], and consequently the water coming in is in
the form of steam. As a result the electrolysis process is not liquid water electrolysis anymore
and has higher eﬃciency. Commonly a ceramic electrolyte is used while electrodes can be made
out of nickel cement [18].
This technology is only under research and development use and it has not been commercialized
for industry yet. However it consumes much less electricity than other technologies due to
superior energy conversion eﬃciency. But due to high-temperature operation special materials
are needed and the total system costs result much higher [20].
2.2
Methanation
H2 produced with electrolysis can be combined with CO2 to produce synthetic methane (CH4)
in a methanator. This gas produced can then be used direct and in unlimited volumes in the
natural gas network [21].
The methanation of CO2 can take place either chemically by using catalysts or biologically by
means of microorganisms. It normally happens over a nickel based catalyst at a temperature
range between 250-450 degrees [22]. In the methanation of the H2 the following reaction takes
place:
CO + 3H2 →CH4 + H2O
CO2 + 4H2 →CH4 + 2H2O

2.3
Gas Network in Denmark
The Danish Gas network is characterized by its high operational reliability and its entry-exit
transmission system model. It constitutes a liberalized market for natural gas where all gas
consumers are able to choose their gas supplier since January 2004 [23].
The gas system is developing into a greener system where gas from renewable sources, also
called RE Gas, is getting a lot of importance in the future energy system. Among these ”green
gases”, biogas is the most popular and it has been largely developed during the past years.
According to the danish transmission system operator for natural gas, gas from electrolysis is
not expected to be commercially viable on a major scale until 2030 at the earliest [23].
2.4
District Heating in Denmark
Denmark is one of the most energy eﬃcient countries in the world and one of its main reasons
is the widespread use of District Heating and combined heat and power [24]. During the oil
crisis that hit the winter of 1973 the oil barrel price quadrupled and countries like Denmark,
which was 90% dependent on fossil fuels import, were determined to improve their own energy
supply security.
A District Heating system (DH) is able to distribute heat from a centralized location through
a pipeline network for residential and commercial purposes such as space heating and water
heating. The ﬁrst DH was developed during the 1920’s and 1930’s based on waste heat from
local electricity production. Since then, when DH supplied heat to urban areas accounted for
around 4% of the total heat supply, it has been largely developed and nowadays it has around
60% share of the total heat supply [24].

able fuels for their operation is more and more signiﬁcant. District Heating is considered the

2.5
Dynamic Network Analysis - DNA
In order to simulate and analyze the diﬀerent energy systems, the software Dynamic Network
Analysis (DNA) is used. It allows to choose the diﬀerent system components from an open
source library that can be modiﬁed by the user such as heat exchangers, engines or turbo-
machinery. These components are connected between them by deﬁning the diﬀerent nodes at
their inlets/outlets giving the same numbering to the nodes which represent the same point
(i.e. outlet of a component and inlet of the next one). Moreover with DNA it is also possible
to simulate several networks at the same time.
DNA includes models with the thermodynamic properties of many types of gases (ideal and
non-ideal), solid fuels, ashes and water. Each component is designated as a separate control
volume. The user will deﬁne some parameters for each of these volumes while leaving the rest
free. In DNA the mathematical equations include mass and energy conservation for all compo-
nents [25] and therefore an automatic mass and energy balance will be made for every control
volume. To solve the equation system a modiﬁed Newton convergence method is applied, thus
it is necessary to set some starting values for the unknown parameters.

5.2
Economic Evaluation
The economic results for the two systems studied resulted in a feasible project, the P2G with
SOEC, and another infeasible, the P2H. However, there might be more aspects to consider
when taking such an investment decision. In the ﬁrst place, the P2G with SOEC gives a very
large NPV after a few years of operation, but at the same time the initial investment required
is very high and 15 times larger than the one needed for the P2H. Thus there would be the
need to evaluate whether it is interesting for a company to make this investment based on the
capital available or the economic resources to ﬁnance it.
Another aspect to take into account is the sustainable character of the fuel produced; the
electricity required for its production comes from renewable ﬂuctuating sources and the CO2
can be absorbed from gasiﬁcation plants, which emit this gas at high temperature. Therefore
the CH4 or H2 produced that is going to be injected in the natural gas grid is the so called
”Green gas”. This means that there could be a subsidy according to the legislation in Denmark
for producing such a fuel. If this subsidy is high enough, the P2H could become feasible and
interesting from an economic perspective.
Currently in Denmark there is a subsidy for biogas delivered to the natural gas grid [38]. Since
natural gas from electrolyzers or methanation are not existing technologies on the market so
far, there is no regulation for this gas. But if in the future it becomes subsidized too, the whole
investment decision may change and the P2H can become interesting for an energy company
due to its low investment cost and high thermodynamic eﬃciency.
An investigation of the minimum subsidy needed to turn the investment of the P2H system

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In this thesis, the development of two P2G systems and the posterior implementation of district
heating has been carried out.
In the ﬁrst place the two systems have been designed and
simulated using the software DNA. The higher thermodynamic eﬃciency was achieved with
the P2G with alkaline electrolyzer with a value of 84.18 % whereas the eﬃciency of the P2G
with SOEC had a value of 70.73 %. After this, they have been optimized from a thermodynamic
viewpoint and the impact of several parameters has been studied for each of the systems. At
this point a new energy system was designed which will only produce hydrogen due to the
low district heating and methane production when using an alkaline electrolyzer. The higher
thermodynamic eﬃciency was then achieved with this new P2H, reaching a value of 84.29 %.
Due to sensitivity analysis it was discovered that higher plant size, until a maximum of 1.8 MW,
and higher system pressure had positive impact on the eﬃciency of the P2G with SOEC. These
variations seemed to have a very low impact on the other two systems utilizing an alkaline
electrolyzer. Moreover, it was found that the lower the temperature of the fuel out, the better
eﬃciency is achieved for all the cycles, being this improvement more signiﬁcant in the P2G with
SOEC. It was also discovered that the eﬀectiveness of the heat exchanger plays an important
role on the power consumption and the temperature of the fuel out.
The district heating implementation proved to be more relevant in the case of the P2G with
SOEC. This system had its eﬃciency improved by around 20 % when implementing district

heating water to recover heat while cooling down the fuel. For the P2G with alkaline this
implementation did not seem to be of importance and this is why the P2H system was created.
This thesis also performed a study on the cell voltage of the SOEC and the alkaline electrolyzer
and it was proved that more heat is released from both electrolyzers when the cell voltage is
raised above the thermoneutral for each case. However, running the systems at this higher
voltages would decrease the system eﬃciency as a consequence of higher power consumption.
A techno-economic analysis for the P2G with SOEC and the P2H with alkaline electrolyzer was
also carried out in this thesis. After calculating the cost of each system component, the PTSC
in the P2G with SOEC and the alkaline electrolyzer in the P2H resulted in the higher total
cost. Making some assumptions such as number of operating hours through the year, lifetime
of the project or prices of electricity, heat, natural gas and distilled water, the two systems were
evaluated from an investment decision viewpoint. The P2G with SOEC resulted to be feasible
with a high NPV of $841,573.33 and a payback time of 4.5 years. On the other hand, the
P2H is economically infeasible and the initial investment cannot be paid back with the project
cash-ﬂows due to high OPEX in comparison with the revenues from fuel production. It was
also found that the more operating hours or the bigger the size of the plant, the higher NPV
for the P2G and on the contrary, the more negative NPV for the P2H. Finally, the price of
electricity that would make the investment become feasible was found to be 251.87 and 21.50
DKK/MWh for the P2G and the P2H respectively. These prices were contrasted with market
data and it was concluded that the frequency of having prices below these ones through a year
in Denmark is very high for the P2G and very low for the P2H.

Recommendations for Further Work
P2G is a research area where many interesting projects are still to be carried out. Here, some
suggestions are given on how the work of this thesis can be continued.
• The way the district heating units were implemented depends on the temperatures of
the cycle and the opportunity to recover heat while cooling down the fuel produced by
==PAGE:77==
6.1. Recommendations for Further Work

the P2G. However, it has been mentioned that there are some other points in the cycles
from where heat can be recovered and utilized. When carrying out the study on the heat
that the SOEC and the alkaline would release if the cell voltage is increased above the
thermoneutral, it was mentioned that heat could be extracted from them and one way
of doing so is by means of cooling water. However, SOEC and alkaline eletrolyzers do
not have a cooling water stream as other technologies, like PEM electrolyzers, do [39].
Perhaps another model of electrolyzer including a cooling system would be suitable to
investigate whether district heating could be extracted from it and if that would improve
the eﬃciency of the system while being economically feasible.
• Once the fuel is cooled down and goes out of the district heating unit the scope of this
project has not included further study of the fuel composition or characteristics for its
storage. Investigation on separators, pressure and more detailed temperature could be
carried out according to the storage required for the fuel and the danish natural gas grid.
• This thesis studies the electricity price that would make the break-even of the project,
i.e. will start generating proﬁt, and compares it to real market data. A similar analysis
with heat prices could be carried out. For this project it was not performed due to the
diﬃculty to ﬁnd heat prices and market data because of conﬁdential reasons.
• The model used in DNA for the alkaline electrolyzer splits the water molecule into pure
hydrogen and pure oxygen and, as it was explained in the results section, the mass ﬂow
of hydrogen out of water and electricity is very small. The reason why the P2H resulted
infeasible economically, when actually this system had the highest thermodynamic eﬃ-
ciency, is a consequence of the reduced hydrogen amount that is able to be send to the
natural gas grid in order to generate proﬁt. To increase the hydrogen mass ﬂow a recircu-
lation of the H2 produced into the electrolyzer was studied but it did not work with the
model available for this alkaline since the ﬂuid at the inlet has to be 100 % water. Due
to time constrains a new electrolyzer model was not created, but some research could be
done in improving this alkaline electrolyzer model so that it is capable to increase the
hydrogen production, for the same power and water requirement, in order to see if the

• To perform the economic analysis the prices of power, heat, natural gas and distilled water

and the investment decision may change in accordance.
