Abstract 
New production facilities have dynamic and complex production environments. In an API 
manufacturing process, production planning and scheduling decisions need to be made on what 
to produce, when, and where. Additionally, capacity planning decisions need to be made on 
when to schedule capacity projects given by flowsheet simulation, to meet future production 
targets that reflect increased demand to serve more patients.  
Aiming for a global optimum solution becomes infeasible using heuristic methods and even 
coming to a feasible solution while overseeing all decisions that must be made at every step of 
production. Information from different production steps needs to be iterated back and forth for 
feedback to see if the proposed decisions are feasible for other steps. 
The objective of the thesis is to build a framework using a mathematical programming model 
that can enable production to implement an optimal capacity ramp-up strategy. 
The framework and model are built to be followed by a facility independent of its maturity 
stage, whether it solely needs to make decisions on production planning and scheduling, 
capacity planning, or both. The model is easy to extend depending on the problem and system. 
Initially, a deterministic mixed-integer linear programming model is built as the base model to 
enable simultaneous decision-making in production planning and scheduling, and capacity 
planning. Then, a framework is developed around the base model to establish a systematic 
approach for utilising the model. This framework is then applied to two case studies: a 
conceptual case study and a real-world case study from the industry to demonstrate practical 
application.

1. Introduction 
This introductory chapter will give a brief introduction to Novo Nordisk A/S, including an 
overview of the production landscape and network of active pharmaceutical ingredients (API) 
production in Novo Nordisk. Recent previous work and theses in mathematical programming 
within Novo Nordisk API production will be explored, and the motivation for this thesis will be 
provided. Finally, the chapter will outline the problem scope and definition this thesis aims to 
solve. 
1.1 
Novo Nordisk A/S 
Novo Nordisk A/S (NN) is a worldwide healthcare company established in 1923 with 
headquarters outside Copenhagen, Denmark. With a team of over 64,000 employees spread 
across 80 offices worldwide, with products available in 170 countries. The purpose is to drive 
change against chronic diseases by pioneering scientific discoveries, ensuring wider access to 
medications, and actively working towards preventing and ultimately finding cures for these 
diseases [1]. 
One in every 11 people in the world has diabetes, and those numbers are expected to rise to 
one by 9 before 2045 if no action is taken. Diabetes places a significant burden on the healthcare 
system, and with health authorities and other partners, a commitment is made to prevent and 
treat the disease. New long-term ambitions for social responsibility are preventing the rise of 
type 2 diabetes and obesity, providing access to affordable care for vulnerable patients in every 
country and driving innovation to improve lives [2]. 
1.2 
API Production Landscape 
The local supply chain for API manufacturing contains three main stages at a facility level: 
fermentation, recovery, and purification. In fermentation, the biochemical reaction occurs to 
produce API, and then recovery and purification are both stages where impurities are removed 
for the API to reach the required level of product purity. These stages are executed by one or 
multiple production lines, potentially allocating each production line to multiple products. 
Figure 1 illustrates that each facility receives raw materials (RM) that are transformed into API 
or process waste after going through the three stages. The production needs to be supported 
by departments that are not directly involved in the production. Utility and solvents supply all 
necessary electricity, heating, cooling, water and solvents needed for the production. 
Maintenance is required for the units to be improved and maintained when needed, and the QC 
laboratory is the quality control to test samples to ensure that the product is up to the required 
quality.

Figure 1. A high-level overview of the API production in Novo Nordisk [3]. 
 
Each facility within a production network may include all these stages or a subset of them. 
When combined, the facilities form a production network where some are interconnected, 
enabling them to exchange intermediate materials or utilise shared storage. 
1.3 
Previous Work and Motivation 
This thesis is part of an effort to deploy multiscale modelling across API production facilities 
and areas of Novo Nordisk in so-called Bioprocess Improvement Optimisation (BIO). The focus 
for the last few years regarding operations research (OR) has been to develop rigorous 
production planning (PP) models to optimise production. Various models have been developed 
with different goals and different levels of aggregation. To give examples, A. Madsen 
incorporated and evaluated incorporating agent-based modelling into supply chain 
optimisation to improve decision making in fermentation and recovery for a multiproduct 
facility [3], R. Andreasen applied a robust approach to include sensitivities and uncertainties in 
production planning and scheduling (PPS) using stochastic programming [4], and most 
recently, S. Lindahl did extensive research on production planning and scheduling and capacity 
planning (CP) problems in his Ph.D. thesis [5]. Some of these models and others available in NN 
that have system features and problem features related to the scope of this thesis will be 
explored and compared later in Chapter 4, Model Formulation, to find a good production 
planning and scheduling model as a foundation to build upon. 
The thesis is motivated by the need to address the capacity ramp-up at the facility, which 
requires an increase in capacity over time (the definition of ramp-up will be thoroughly 
addressed in the theoretical background, Chapter 2). In this facility, heuristic methods are 
currently being applied to production planning and scheduling, and capacity planning. 
Production planners and specialists are divided into either fermentation and recovery (F&R) 
or purification and drying (P&D). When making these decisions, information needs to be 
iterated back and forth between planners and specialists to aim for a feasible solution for all 
production steps. When making decisions on when to implement capacity projects, it has 
proven difficult even to find feasible solutions to ensure that production targets are met and 
that intermediate inventories do not run out, leaving later steps in the production process to 
run dry and be unutilised when they could be running production. The projects have been 
tested with what-if analysis in flowsheet simulation but separately for fermentation and 
recovery, and then purification and drying, leading to the analysis not taking into account the 
==PAGE:11==
 
3 
 
entire production process, and it becomes time-consuming to test scenarios and difficult to 
reach an optimal solution.  
Using mathematical programming, a model can be developed where capacity projects can be 
scheduled for the entire production process. It will include all steps in the process and all 
capacity projects that need to be scheduled to reach a global optimum solution for the process, 
ensuring that production targets are met and preventing inventory shortages. The model can 
also be useful in proving infeasibility if the given requirements cannot be met. 
At this point, exploring risks will not be a priority, and therefore, a deterministic model will be 
developed. 
 
This thesis is divided into nine chapters. Chapter 2 will give the theoretical background gained 
from conducting a literature review of the relevant topics needed for this problem, including 
production planning and scheduling, capacity planning and ramp-up definitions. In Chapter 3, 
a value stream mapping (VSM) will be conducted to explore the operations of a matured facility 
and the facility of interest that is undergoing capacity ramp-up. Chapter 4 will give a model 
formulation that shows what decisions are taken for choosing a production planning and 
scheduling foundation model and developing model features needed additionally to build a 
base model. Chapter 5 then gives an outline of how the model from Chapter 4 can be applied 
using a framework going through 8 steps. Chapters 6 and 7 will apply the mathematical model 
and framework to two case studies: a smaller conceptual case study will be conducted first, 
followed by a larger and more complex real-world case study. The first case study will be 
conceptual, built upon a fictional production system and made-up data. The system and data 
will be simpler than those used in the real-world case study. The reasoning behind making a 
conceptual case study is twofold. The first one is that using a simple system, to begin with, 
makes debugging and verifying the model easier. The second reason is that presenting the 
model and sharing results can be done confidently, knowing that confidentiality will not be 
compromised. The second case study is on a real-world existing production facility. The 
production network becomes more complex than in the conceptual case study, and the 
complexity of project scheduling is allowed to increase significantly. The real-world case study, 
therefore, captures the complexity of scheduling capacity projects that follow different 
characteristics. The models are solved using Pyomo and the commercial solver Gurobi 
Optimizer version 11.0.1 on a computer with 16 GB RAM and, an Intel(R) Core(TM) i7 
processor, and 1.80 GHz. Chapter 8 will give a discussion, and finally, Chapter 9 will remark on 
the conclusion and future work. 
1.4 
Problem Scope and Definition 
The main objective in the planning layer of an existing matured API manufacturing facility is to 
determine what to produce, when, and where. In a new facility undergoing a capacity ramp-up, 
the main objective in the planning layer is to determine when and in what sequence to schedule 
capacity projects. This is done while simultaneously considering all parts of the previously 
mentioned production questions. Simulation can generate a set of capacity project options to 
meet future production targets or demand curves. Combining all decisions for scheduling the 
==PAGE:12==
 
4 
 
capacity projects while ensuring a well-operated production becomes a difficult task, and 
finding an optimal solution and even a feasible one becomes difficult using heuristic 
approaches. However, by using operation research and mathematical programming, a definite 
proof can be given if the problem is feasible (even optimal) or not, taking into account all input 
and all considerations. 
 
The problem definition is as follows:  
Given:  
вҖў 
an API manufacturing system consisting of a manufacturing line or multiple lines 
вҖў 
production data (e.g. production times, release times, current state capacities, yields, 
shutdowns (SD), and production ramp-up) 
вҖў 
a demand curve or production targets 
вҖў 
inventory safety levels, inventory limits and initial inventory 
вҖў 
a set of capacity projects, including process improvement and investment projects, 
including their size and durations 
Determine: the optimal capacity ramp-up strategy for an API manufacturing facility that is 
вҖў the allocation of subset, sequence and timings of capacity projects and associated 
shutdowns 
вҖў the allocation of production tasks on the production line, along with inventory levels of 
intermediates and final product 
Subject to: meeting safety inventory levels, demand, and capacity project dependencies 
 
The problem definition will be further extended to match each case study specifically. The 
overall objective is to develop a framework and a flexible mathematical model that can be 
adapted to meet specific requirements. During a facilityвҖҷs capacity ramp-up phase, the model is 
used to generate the optimal capacity ramp-up strategy, including decisions on production 
shutdowns, implementing capacity projects, and ramping up production. Capacity planning 
decisions can be deactivated; in a case where no projects are available or a fixed capacity plan 
is used as input, the model generates production planning and scheduling decisions.  
Alternatively, when a production plan is utilised as input, the model generates decisions related 
to capacity projects, thereby deactivating production planning decisions. 
 
Project scope 
The scope of the project is to develop a systematic framework for production ramp-up and 
apply it to generate the best (optimal) ramp-up strategy for a single-product end-to-end API 
manufacturing facility: 
1. Literature review 
2. Value stream mapping of the facility and site supply chain related to site-wide capacity 
ramp-up 
3. Development of site-wide capacity ramp-up framework 
4. Model development: planning model for site-wide capacity ramp-up 
5. Model analysis 
6. Model application: to generate optimal ramp-up plan(s) for a given facility. 
7. Recommendation of ramp-up plan for an API facility

2.1 
Production Planning and Scheduling  
2.1.1 Planning Problems 
Planning problems of enterprises are generally defined in literature as a two-dimensional 
supply chain matrix at different functions and time scales, as shown in Figure 2, where the focus 
will be on the production function highlighted in blue. The functions of the supply chain are 
overall defined into the procurement of raw materials, production to transform the raw 
materials into products, distribution of the products and sales of the products [6]. The decisions 
for these functions have been divided into general time scales of long-term (strategic) planning, 
usually 5-10 years, down to short-term (operational) planning, often defined around 1-4 weeks 
and medium-term (tactical) planning there in between [7]. Examples of decisions that can be 
made in long-term planning can be to determine the supply chain's structure, such as locations 
of facilities and warehouses. In medium-term planning, decisions in the production planning 
function can be on assigning production targets to the facilities. A short-term planning level in 
production is usually referred to as production scheduling and can involve decisions on 
assigning tasks and their sequence to units to fulfil assigned production targets, and more 
where it depends on the level of detail that the scheduling entails [6].

2.1.3 Production Scheduling 
Production scheduling problems have had a few objectives proposed in the literature to 
summarise, most of them aim to minimise the makespan, lateness, earliness, or total costs. 
Given data from the production facility on processing units, unit availability and connections 
and storage capacities, production recipes on processing time, rates, and utility requirements, 
production costs and production targets. Decisions need to be made on translating demand into 
batches, allocating processing tasks to processing units and utilities and the sequence and 
timing of the tasks to the units, illustrated in Figure 3 [6]. Depending on the facility's required 
level of detail, scheduling can incorporate other crucial decisions regarding materials, utilities, 
personnel, and other resources. Historically, skilled personnel have carried out production 
scheduling manually, often utilising methods such as Kanban or spreadsheets. As production 
volumes increase, product portfolios grow, and the emphasis on reducing production and 
energy costs, manual scheduling becomes increasingly complex, as in time-consuming and 
more likely to contain mistakes. This emphasises the growing importance of optimisation in 
scheduling processes [8]. A MILP formulation has been designed to address various scheduling 
challenges within chemical batch operations in multiproduct/multipurpose facilities, explicitly 
focusing on short-term scheduling. This formulation is known as the State-Task Network (STN), 
further explained in Chapter 2.1.2 [9].

2.1.4 Optimisation and Simulation 
Optimisation and simulation models are type of mathematical models that use constraints to 
describe a system's behaviour. A general comparison will be made between both and finally 
how they can work together. 
 
Simulation involves exploring the solution space to understand system behaviour, compare 
scenarios, and evaluate potential future performance [8, 9]. It provides valuable insights into a 
system's behaviour over time and enables the exploration of "what if" questions about real-
world systems. Additionally, it proves helpful in understanding systems at the design stage, 
==PAGE:16==
 
8 
 
offering the chance to experiment with new designs or policies before implementation, 
therefore preparing for potential outcomes to support decision-making [9]. 
On the other hand, optimisation focuses on using decision variables and an objective function 
to identify the optimal solution within the solution space [8]. The model computes decision 
variables to minimise or maximise an objective function while being within the bounds of 
constraints [8]. Simulation models focus on visualising a system's performance under specific 
assumptions, while optimisation models seek the optimal solution [8, 10]. 
 
Simulation and optimisation can be used together, where one model can be an input to the other 
[10]. For example, optimisation models can be solved to determine the optimal solution, while 
simulation can test the feasibility of the solution and explore uncertainties. Similarly, 
simulation can provide information about the system to the optimisation model, supporting the 
search for the optimal solution based on available information. This combination provides a 
comprehensive approach to support complex decision-making processes without interfering 
with the physical system under consideration [10]. 
 
2.1.5 Production Planning and Scheduling and Solution Methods 
Combining decision making in the supply chain network can be done both over functions and 
time scales. In literature significant attention has been paid to combining production planning 
and production scheduling. Thoughts on if they should be an input to each other of if they 
should be integrated and what level of detail each problem should contain. 
Three solution methods have been proposed in the literature for combining and solving these 
two production planning problems together, illustrated in Figure 4 [6].  
 
 
Figure 4. Three solution methods for solving combined production planning and production scheduling 
problems (inspired by [6]).  
 
The first method is the hierarchical method. The information flow is only from the master 
problem on top down to the subproblem. The master problem consists of a planning model and 
a high-level scheduling model that is solved and gives input down to the subproblem that 
==PAGE:17==
 
9 
 
consists only of a detailed scheduling problem. For instance, the master problem could include 
production planning on a medium-term time scale, giving output on what production targets 
must be reached. Additionally, the master problem could consist of a high-level scheduling 
model that could include decisions on lot-sizing or batching (sizes and number of lots/batches). 
The output from the master problem is then fed to the subproblem, which should make 
decisions on assigning and sequencing tasks to units. It depends on the system in scope and the 
nature of the problem if the decision on lot-sizing / batching is taken in the master problem or 
the subproblem, depending on the level of detail needed [8]. The downside is that infeasible 
production targets can exist, leading to an infeasible schedule, and then a feasible schedule can 
be found in the neighbourhood [6, 7]. 
 
The second method presented is the iterative method, which includes the same master and 
subproblem representation as in the hierarchical approach, but it additionally includes a 
feedback loop going from the subproblem to the master problem. This means that the high-
level scheduling model in the master problem can give an optimistic solution that can then be 
compared to the detailed scheduling problem in the subproblem, where a feasible solution for 
the master problem can be found. The feedback loop from the subproblem can then update the 
master problem with new columns or constraints using methods such as Lagrangian 
decomposition, column generation, and Benders decomposition [6, 7]. 
 
The third method is the full-space method. The problems are not split into master and 
subproblems but integrated into one problem. It is, therefore, called full space as it covers the 
entire solution space for both problems at once. This solution method yields to an optimal 
solution for both problems but might computationally take a long time to solve with larger 
problems. Lagrangian decomposition and Benders decomposition can also be used to solve the 
integrated problem but in a different way since the original model is an integrated formulation. 
The concept involves solving a relaxed master problem that includes only linking variables and 
fixing those variables to resolve decoupled subproblems, eventually using the subproblem 
solutions to improve the master problem [6, 7]. 
 
The solution methods can also be applied to combine other functions and time scales within the 
supply chain. Neither method is fundamentally superior to the other. It depends on the 
problems at hand, such as whether integration is necessary or if one of the other methods is 
more suitable. The hierarchical method, although not always yielding an optimal solution, is a 
widely used approach in companies where one individual creates a solution for another to work 
through. In contrast, the iterative method aims to find a feasible solution but does not guarantee 
an optimal one and can be time-consuming due to repeated iterations of information. On the 
other hand, the integrated method offers a globally optimal solution, but its drawback lies in 
the model's size, leading to longer solving times. 
These three solution methods have been explained and studied in literature with specific 
attention to combining production planning and scheduling. In this thesis, production planning 
and scheduling will be integrated into one PPS MILP formulation to aim for optimal production 
solution.

2.1.6 System Representation 
In short-term scheduling, both resource-task network (RTN) and state-task network (STN) 
have received the most attention in literature for system representation [10]. Significant 
progress has been made, including finding good solutions to industrial-sized problems. 
Especially with increasing complexity in an application, network, product recipes and 
constraints for assigning equipment [11]. Various features can be considered, such as 
switchovers, performance decay, time-varying utility costs [12]. A short comparison of the RTN 
and the STN is as follows: 
 
The RTN representation focuses on the resources with identical treatment and representation. 
The processing and storage tasks consume, at beginning times, and release, at ending times, 
resources such as units, materials and utilities. States and resources are both represented as 
circles required for the processing units as rectangles [11, 12]. The main decisions are the task 
allocation, batch size and resource availability. 
 
The STN is a directed graph consisting of states (circles) that are the feeds, intermediates and 
final products; tasks (rectangles) that are the processing operations that transform material 
from one or more states (consume) to one or more output states (produce) and last the arcs 
connect the states and the units indicating the flow of material [11]. 
 
When choosing the network representation, this thesis focuses not on resources or balancing 
resources where they need to be allocated and assigned, as is done in the RTN. The problem at 
hand does not concern the limitation of available equipment, utilities or workforce [9]. The STN 
is better suited for natural representation, illustrating changes in material or inventory over 
time and demonstrating how materials can transition from one state to another. This 
representation has been more commonly used in similar problem-solving contexts. Therefore, 
the STN has been chosen as the system representation for this thesis. The focus of the system 
representation will be exploring the STN and recent developments. 
 
The State-Task Network was first proposed in 1993 by Kondili et al. [9] to represent batch 
chemical processing networks of any complexity. The STN representation is equally suitable for 
different processing networks, such as continuous, semicontinuous, or batch processes. The 
network can represent processes that include sharing raw materials and intermediates, 
splitting, mixing, recycling materials, and storage.  
The STN framework was considered a breakthrough for batch scheduling in process operations 
because of the importance that it represents a very general framework that goes beyond the 
traditional flow shop and jobs hop model used in Industrial Engineering, where it allows for 
complex and inconsistent network representation with discrete time MILP formulation [13].

Capacity Planning 
Barchi, R.H. in [15] defines the capacity of a production facility as the output in units per time 
when production equipment is run at the maximum efficient rate. The scope of the capacity 
planning is within the production level in the supply chain network described in Figure 2, and 
decisions in capacity planning can be defined as long-term, medium-term, and short-term.  
With increasing customer requirements, production facilities must adjust to dynamic customer 
demands, which may involve introducing new products. Adapting these products requires 
production flexibility to handle modifications in capacity, production processes, and customer 
delivery, all while staying competitive on pricing. Typically, traditional hierarchical decision-
making involves estimating capacity based on demand forecasts. Afterwards, production plans 
in PPS are developed based on available capacities, aiming to find feasible solutions that meet 
these demands [16].  
In the literature, most decisions in capacity planning are categorised as strategic level/long 
term. This is because capacity decisions are typically costly and have a substantial impact, often 
requiring approval from management. Such decisions generally involve determining the size 
and location of facilities. Additionally, decisions regarding specific parts of a production line are 
also deemed strategic, given that the units being purchased are typically expensive and carry 
significant implications for the overall production process [16, 17].  
It is fair to mention that capacity planning can be used across various time scale to provide 
recommendations. However, if capacity projects have costs or have a substantial impact, the 
final decision might rest with the management team. 
The primary decisions in capacity planning are based on capacity sizes, location, and time. 
Where the capacity sizes are continuous variables, discrete increments or a list of possible 
capacity options [18]. The simplest example is when deterministic demand increases linearly 
over time in Figure 8, and when demand reaches capacity, the capacity is expanded by x units.

2.2.1 Integrating PPS and CP 
Traditionally, inventory is built up in production when demand is low to meet the demand in 
periods when the demand outgrows the capacity; in these situations, storage and cost need to 
be considered. Either way, it is not recommended to hold extensive inventories, and if capacities 
are increased, then less inventory is needed [17]. Much focus has been on integrating 
production planning with scheduling and treating capacity as a parameter that requires input 
from capacity planning. Nevertheless, some attention has been on integrating PP and CP, mainly 
focusing on capacity construction or flexible short-term adjustments [18] to improve their 
decision-making [16]. With the integration, projects can be scheduled, taking into account 
inventory levels and demands to avoid stockouts [14]. Figure 10 illustrates how information is 
usually kept separate or combined between PPS an CP problems and when integration of them 
allows for making joint decisions. 
 
==PAGE:26==
 
18 
 
 
Figure 10. An illustration of the relationship between CP and PPS, common input information, and what can be 
determined and decided separately and combined. 
 
Lindahl, S.B., et al. in [18] have summarised and defined two approaches that can be taken in 
capacity planning. The first one is the direct approach, and the second one is the indirect 
approach: 
вҖў Direct approach: input is a set of capacity options for the integrated CP and PPS that are 
evaluated. Capacity options can be generated by using a suitable model, usually 
simulation, to analyse the manufacturing capacity where the options can be to change 
operating parameters, equipment or production recipes and include size, downtime 
until the capacity is available, and location of the option [18]. 
вҖў Indirect approach: the model itself determines and identifies the capacity change, that 
is, size, location and timings needed without having been given options. The capacity 
change is usually limited by a maximum limit within a given time horizon; it could be 
continuously increasing or staircase shape. The capacity output can then be an input to 
the simulation model to find the capacity options required [18]. 
 
The indirect approach involves decisions related to where, when, and how much to increase 
capacity, similar to production planning decisions regarding where, when, and how much to 
produce. In contrast, the direct approach entails fixed size and location, focusing on 
determining the timing of projects, similar to production scheduling tasks to units. Therefore, 
the term "scheduling" will be used for the capacity projects. This thesis will adopt the direct 
approach to identify the optimal schedule from a set of capacity options or projects. 
 
In the literature, different solution methods for capacity planning have been proposed. 
Nonetheless, a gap has been found where the complexity of real-life capacity projects (in 
pharmaceutical manufacturing) has yet to be captured. The complexity of the capacity projects

is in how they affect each other, described as a combination effect in this thesis, where if one 
project is implemented before the other, the increase in capacity is not equal to their individual 
sizes, e.g. Examples of combination effects are: 
вҖў A=0 and B=10 then is A+B=15 not 10 
вҖў A=-5 and B=10 then is A+B=12 not 5 
 
The following definitions will be used for capacity projects: 
вҖў Two types of capacity projects are in scope, those are: 
o Investment projects involve shutdowns and costs. E.g. purchase of standalone 
equipment. 
o Improvement projects do not have shutdowns nor costs. The focus is on 
improving current production, aiming for optimal production. E.g. changes in 
process and operations. 
вҖў Project implementation: 
o Project has predefined capacity increase/decrease, location, and shutdown 
duration. 
o Projects can be implemented one or multiple at a time. 
o Project decisions involve when to schedule them. 
вҖў Project dependencies: 
o Sequence of projects вҖ“ some projects must be scheduled in a specific order due 
to either logical or system restrictions. 
o Combination of projects вҖ“ some projects have combination effects where the 
combination of projects can result in a collective capacity increase less or greater 
than the sum of their individual impacts.

Ramp-up 
Without considering what the term ramp-up can be used for, it can be generally accepted that 
the term fundamentally stands for increasing the activity or the level of something, such as 
increasing speed, power or cost of something, according to the Cambridge Dictionary [20]. 
Figure 11 demonstrates the typical progression of ramp-up over time, starting at point A and 
ending at point B, following a certain pattern or function. 
 
 
Figure 11. Alternative ramp-up curves (inspired by [21]) and staircase curve. 
 
A significant amount of research has been done on a production ramp-up with a focus on new 
products as their lifecycles are becoming shorter and new products are released to the market 
at a higher pace. The ramp-up phase is considered critical for successfully introducing a product 
to a market [22, 23]. It pressures firms to shorten their development times (time-to-market) 
but also reach full capacity utilisation (time-to-volume) quicker to get the product out on the 
market faster than competitors [24]. To summarise, the focus is more on how to ramp-up new 
products on an operational level, focusing on yield management, learning and time pressure 
[25]. Nevertheless, it is known that the definition of ramp-up shows inconsistency across the 
literature, where the main focus is on how to organise a ramp-up [21]. 
To give examples of definitions: 
вҖў The ramp-up phase occurs when a new product is introduced in a factory but also when 
a new  process or a new plant starts up [25].  
вҖў The ramp-up phase is from the start of production until the target production capacity 
is reached, whether it is a new production system, product or organisation [22]. 
вҖў Ramp-up of capacity after a product changeover [18]. 
вҖў Production ramp-up is the period that covers the start of production and the attainment 
of maximum production capacity [26]. 
вҖў C. Terwiesch, R.E. Bohn in [24] point out that ramp-ups can also occur when a new 
process or plan starts.  
 
A production unit needs to do a production ramp-up in a production process with two products 
where a changeover (CO) is inevitable. Even down to a one-product system shutdown, SD needs 
to be done for either maintenance, inspections, or implementing projects. After starting up the 
==PAGE:29==
 
21 
 
production again, a production ramp-up needs to be done to reach a steady-state production 
where the units can be fully utilised. 
The term capacity ramp-up has not been defined clearly nor discussed in much detail in 
literature and is most frequently used to describe production ramp-up corresponding to [27] 
where the period after a capacity increase with no production is described as capacity ramp-
up. 
 
For this thesis, the term ramp-up is clarified and split into two terms: production ramp-up and 
capacity ramp-up, since the literature has not clearly distinguished between those two. 
Production ramp-up is used after an SD or CO on a production unit needs to ramp-up following 
some function over time to steady-state production, as illustrated in Figure 12. Production 
ramp-up has been defined on two planning levels: 
вҖў In short-term (operational) planning, experienced employees in production planning 
staff provide an estimated function on how production throughput increases, 0 to n 
number of batches at each time period (typically in week), from the start of production. 
This estimation is based on their experience and previous production ramp-ups, making 
it a feature of their latest solution or actual production outcomes. This feature can either 
be used as a staircase curve or fitted to some kind of function to be used as an input to 
the medium-term planning. 
вҖў In medium-term (tactical) planning, the knowledge of how the production ramps up 
after an SD or CO from the experienced employees in short-term planning is used as an 
input parameter to the PPS model for each unit to replicate the real-world production 
as much as possible when creating a future production plan. This information is 
obtained from short-term planning and acts as an input to model production ramp-up 
to steady-state production.

medium-term (right) uses the pink fitted line from short-term planning as an input to model production ramp-up to 
steady-state production.

вҖў In tactical (medium-term) planning, the capacity planning model schedules, 0 to рқ‘ҡ

вҖў In strategic (long-term) planning, the capacity ramp-up curve information from tactical

3. Value Stream Mapping 
After introducing the theoretical concepts needed for the problem in the theoretical 
background, an overview of two manufacturing facilities in different maturity stages will be 
provided: one operating in a mature phase and the other in the capacity ramp-up phase. Value 
Stream Mapping (VSM) will be used to map the current state of information flow, from the 
initiation of ideas for increasing capacity to the execution of capacity projects. Finally, 
suggestions for improvements will be proposed. 
VSM is a graphical tool derived from Lean Manufacturing used to map the set of activities, both 
value-adding and non-value-adding, necessary to produce a product from raw material to the 
final product for the customer. The main goal is to discover various types of waste and 
inefficiencies in the current production process and find ways to eliminate them [28]. Waste 
covers anything that doesn't add value to the product, including transportation, inventory, 
motion, waiting, overprocessing, overproduction, and defects. According to lean principles, the 
customer will only be willing to pay for the product's value and not the following waste. Cost 
reductions can be achieved by implementing waste education and continuous improvement 
tools in the manufacturing facility. VSM is used to analyse the current state of the value stream 
of the product, and then a redesign can be proposed for an ideal state that focuses on reducing 
these wastes [29]. VSM has proven to be a valuable tool for redefining and redesigning 
production systems from a Lean perspective to keep up with competitiveness in markets [30]. 
Many organisations have started following lean manufacturing concepts to be able to keep up 
with rapidly changing customersвҖҷ needs and to provide a quality product while keeping costs 
low for customers [29].  
Novo Nordisk started using Lean in 2003, and it has significantly improved efficiency, quality, 
and simplifying processes. Given the increase in demand, it is crucial to use Lean principles to 
ensure that the organisation stays on top of the necessary changes needed to increase capacity 
and serve more patients. Health authorities expect a systematic approach to continuous 
improvements and sharing of best practices across the organisation [31]. 
VSM has primarily been utilised to represent material flow directly within production and the 
associated information flow. Nonetheless, the principles of VSM can extend to mapping 
different scales, including the supply chain, facility, and production line, down to a specific 
process. They can, therefore, be applied to other information flows. VSM will capture the 
information flow related to capacity projects, illustrating how ideas are initiated, how various 
teams need to be involved, including the production, and how they get implemented. 
3.1.1 Application of Value Stream Mapping 
First, VSM will be used to map the current state of the capacity projects process for the matured 
manufacturing facility (MF), providing insights into project initiation, exploration, 
management, and execution within a matured setting. Next, the current state of the capacity 
project process for the capacity ramp-up facility, called target facility (TF), will be mapped to 
gain an understanding of information flow within a development setting. While the focus is on 
TF, the VSM for MF is used to learn how a mature facility operates and the improvements that 
==PAGE:32==

might be needed there. Improvements identified in both facilities will inform the presentation 
of an ideal state, with a future state specifically outlined for TF as an advancement towards 
achieving the ideal state.

4.2 
Base Model Development 
The base model development gives an overview of how the base model is developed to be used 
as a step in the framework presented in the next chapter, Chapter 4. Within BIO, a general 6-
step mathematical programming model development framework called the infinity loop has 
been developed, as shown in Figure 18.  
The infinity loop starts with a model development step, where a problem definition is the input. 
In this step, a model is generated by mapping a network topology, choosing the level of 
aggregation, assumptions, variables, parameters, constraints and some guiding objectives. The 
next step is the data analysis, where data is collected and analysed from the production and 
supply chain, and the parameters are evaluated from uncertainties. In the third step, simulation, 
the model developed in the first step is solved with the data gathered in the previous step. The 
model is tested to find the boundaries of it and test if the constraints hold and if the model gives 
the expected output. In the fourth step, the objective function is developed based on the guiding 
objective from the first step. In the fifth step, the model is validated against production and 
inventory plans, and in the final step, the model is used for application to provide 
recommendations based on the solution from the model. If one of the steps is not applicable, it 
can be skipped, or if some steps need to be revisited, another round is taken. 
 
 
==PAGE:42==
 
34 
 
 
Figure 18. BIO mathematical programming model development framework called the infinity loop. 
 
To develop the base model, two rounds were taken in the infinity loop: first for the PPS model 
and then for the CP model. The model development step receives the problem definition in the 
introduction in Chapter 1 and the medium-term PS API model from Chapter 4.1 as input. The 
model development step then adjusts the network topology as needed from the PS API model. 
No assumptions need to be made in the baseline at this stage. In the data analysis step, data was 
made up from internal knowledge of data types and structures to set some standard values to 
be able to test the model. In the simulation step the made-up data was used to explore and test 
the boundaries of the model to see if it was behaving as expected. In this round, no objective, 
validation, or application was done.

before the SD and run the second period during the SD.

period or the periods before it, equivalent to the length of the startup task. Added as 
constraint (24) in Chapter 4.2.2.

4.2.4 Guiding Objective Function 
An example of an objective function in constraint (32) can be to maximise production and 
minimise startups. The first part of the objective can be what is desired e.g. maximise 
production or minimising inventory for being under inventory safety levels. The second part of 
the objective must be used where the startup tasks are penalised because the model cannot 
differentiate between a steady-state task and a startup task. If the startup task is not penalised, 
then the model will schedule startup tasks when it is unnecessary. In reality, production does 
not randomly start when a steady-state task is running. It is only necessary after shutdowns.

5.  Framework 
The framework provided in this chapter is a systematic step-by-step approach to how the 
model from the model formulation in Chapter 4 can be applied to a production system in 
various stages. The steps can be followed regardless of whether the system is a matured 
production system that only needs to make decisions regarding production with fixed timings 
of capacity projects or a system that is currently growing in a capacity ramp-up phase and needs 
more room for combining decision-making of both PPS and CP or even if system that has a fixed 
production plan and needs to make decisions on when to schedule projects. The framework is 
designed to give a systematic and structured approach to analyse and solve different types of 
problems in PPS and CP that involves an eight-step approach from identifying what the problem 
is to generate results from the model built. Data is gathered to find optimal solutions to complex 
systems, making informed decision-making easier. Figure 19 illustrates the steps and 
connection between them. 
5.1 
Step 1: Problem Formulation 
The first step in the methodology is the problem formulation. The purpose is to scope the 
problem and identify where the difficulties lie in decision-making by aligning with 
stakeholders. There needs to be a clear understanding of why and where the problem exists, 
what is currently being done and what can be done to close the knowledge gap and improve 
decision-making. Information on the production layout, what assumptions need to be made, 
what is considered fixed information and cannot be changed, and what can be determined must 
be precise, along with the limitations and desired objectives. 
Given the system understanding, the outcome is the problem definition that sets the scope of 
the project based on what information is known. Given the input to the model, what needs to 
be determined by the model, subject to the information needed, along with the objective to be 
reached. 
5.2 
Step 2: System Definition 
Given the problem definition and every information gained in the problem formulation, the 
system definition is used first to create an STN that represents the production layout in scope.  
The STN needs to be developed based on a conceptual or an actual physical system. The level 
of aggregation and network topology needs to be decided based on what information needs to 
be captured with the STN, for example, what step connects to another one, where inventories 
are, how many products are being produced on each unit, etc. Next, features need to be listed 
based on the information about the scope of the production process. The information in this 
step is then translated into parameters, decision variables and constraints required to describe 
the system in step 4. The output of the second step results in an STN and the features in words 
needed. 
==PAGE:52==
 
44 
 
5.3 
Step 3: Base Model Selection 
Step 3 is choosing the appropriate base model to use depending on the problem. The step 
consists of choosing between three models, 3a. PPS model, 3b. CP model and 3b. PPS+CP model. 
Depending on what decisions need to be made in the problem according to the problem 
formulation and system identification. The PPS model should be chosen decision on when, what 
and how much to produce are the questions that need to be answered. The CP model should be 
chosen if decisions regarding when, what and how to schedule capacity projects are the 
questions to answer. The third model is a combination of PPS+CP that ensures that all questions 
in both PPS and CP are answered. The core of all models is the STN representation of the 
process. The 3a. PPS model includes capacity projects. If the capacity project timings have been 
decided, the capacity planning variable can be fixed to that timing, and PPS decisions can be 
made taking into account this input. 
If it is desired to make a decision regarding only scheduling capacity projects, then the 3b. CP 
model can be used. Given a fixed production plan, all PPS variables can be fixed according to the 
plan, allowing all capacity projects to be scheduled according to the plan. 
If decisions need to be made for both PPS and CP, then the 3c. PPS+CP can be used, leaving the 
decision variables free to make combined decisions. 
The output of this step is a base model chosen to further be extended in the next step. 
5.4 
Step 4: Model Extension 
In the fourth step, given the base model, additional sets, subsets, parameters, decision variables, 
and constraints need to be modelled to fit the specific problem at hand. A comparison is made 
with the base model to identify any missing features, which are then incorporated as 
constraints. A decision is made regarding the objective function is desired. The output of this 
step is a mathematical translation of the identified features, which are integrated into the base 
model. 
5.5 
Step 5: Data Collection 
Given the mathematical formulation, data must be collected to fill in the input parameters for 
the model, validate it and use it for application in this fifth step as data collection. Data is 
gathered from production data and employees working in production planning, specialists in 
production, and others who might have information needed for the model e.g. warehouse and 
capacity projects. It is essential to analyse the information and evaluate parameters that contain 
uncertainties that might challenge and impact what the optimal choice is. The output of the step 
is data to solve the model, validate it, and apply it. 
5.6 
Step 6: Model Verification 
In the sixth step, the model is solved using the desired software and the data from the previous 
step. Other made-up data can additionally also be used to push the limits of the system and see 
if the model is behaving as expected. Then, verifying different features and constraints of the 
model is essential to test the boundaries of the model and play around with it to see and 
==PAGE:53==
 
45 
 
evaluate if the constraint and the model behaviour represent the actual system and features 
identified in system definition. If the model is not behaving as expected or the results are 
incorrect, then the model extension needs to be revisited. 
5.7 
Step 7: Model Validation 
After the model is verified, it can be benchmarked against a known solution, and to do that, the 
same assumptions and input used in the production plan need to be represented in the model 
if possible. The validation step's purpose is to ensure that the model that has been built and 
assumptions that have been represent the real system in scope. A known solution to benchmark 
against can be a feasible or historical production plan. If the solution is proven to be within the 
solution space of the model, then the model can be validated. There might be that some features 
of the model cannot be validated, but if differences and possible deviations can be explained, 
then it can still be a successful validation. However, suppose the discrepancies cannot be 
explained, or the solution is not within the solution space. In that case, the model extension step 
needs to be revisited to evaluate why there are differences. Sometimes, validation cannot be 
performed if the system of scope is conceptual, if the system is not yet real, or if the data to be 
able to perform a validation is not available. 
5.8 
Step 8: Model Application 
The validated model can be applied to answer the problem definition in the eighth and final 
step. The initial solution can be found as a baseline. From there, scenario analysis can be applied 
based on, e.g. the uncertainties identified in the data collection and create scenarios used to 
challenge the baseline in case more optimal solutions exist in the solution space through, e.g. 
relaxation or testing new aspects of the model.

6. Conceptual Case Study 
The conceptual case study will illustrate the application of the framework. First, the problem 
will be formulated, followed by system definition. Next, the base model from Chapter 4 will be 
used first without scheduling capacity projects to the system given the data to create a baseline 
to evaluate the impact of increasing demand and to ensure that the model works correctly and 
as expected. Then, capacity projects will be added along with additional features needed 
specifically for capacity projects. Three scenarios will be tested where the complexity of the 
project scheduling increases in each scenario. The first scenario only allows capacity projects 
on unit 2, and the second scenario allows capacity projects on both units. The third scenario 
includes project dependencies. 
6.1 
Step 1: Problem Formulation 
The purpose of the conceptual case study is to demonstrate how CP can be integrated with the 
PPS model to schedule projects for a facility that expects a stockout in the near future if no 
action is taken to increase the capacity of the production line. The facility's objective is to meet 
all customer demand; with growing demand, the current state is to minimise inventory 
stockout as much as possible. Balancing the decisions of having just enough inventory before 
scheduling a capacity project to ensure that demand can be met while projects that have SD are 
scheduled. It becomes a difficult problem to find an optimal capacity ramp-up strategy where 
the timings of the projects are the key decision to ensure patients' demand is not neglected. 
Other factors need to be considered, such as production shutdown, where maintenance, visual 
inspection, etc., cannot be carried out when the production is running, along with production 
times, release times, and production ramp-up ratios. 
Considering the information and objectives given above, the following problem definition for 
the conceptual case study is as follows: 
Given: a conceptual single-product API manufacturing system consisting of a single 
manufacturing line, production data (e.g. production times, release times, current state 
capacities, yields, shutdowns, and production ramp-up), time-dependent product demand, 
inventory safety levels and limits and initial inventory, set of capacity projects, including 
process improvement and investment projects, their size and durations. 
Determine: the optimal capacity ramp-up strategy for a single product end-to-end API 
manufacturing facility. The allocation of subsets, sequences, and timings of capacity projects 
and associated shutdowns. The allocation of production tasks on the production line, along with 
inventory levels of intermediates and final product. 
Subject to: meeting safety inventory levels, increasing demand, and project dependencies. 
The output will be the optimal production plan that includes when to produce and how much, 
considering inventory safety level and maximum inventory storage, along with an optimal 
capacity plan for when and in what order to schedule projects, including their SD times. 
Unlimited storage of inventories and unlimited raw materials are assumed. The time horizon 
spans 52 weeks, with a discrete time of 1 week from 1 January until 31 December. During the

summer, there will be a 2-week shutdown due to maintenance. It is assumed that capacity 
projects can be scheduled during the shutdown.

Step 2: System Definition 
The conceptual case study is constructed to resemble an API production process. It captures 
important features from a real production process and additionally capacity project specifics. 
The system is a two-stage production system with two units, inventory in between, and one 
product. It can be seen as part of a production system; for example, fermentation is the first 
task, and recovery is the second task.

вҖў Longer production ramp-ups and using both lower and higher ratios of production 
allowed in each period. 
вҖў Scheduling SD in different places. 
вҖў Initial inventory set to 0. 
вҖў Shorter and longer release times in production. 
вҖў Allowing and not allowing for stockout for the different states 
 
After each test, the following questions and considerations are asked: 
вҖў Is the allocation of steady-sate tasks and startup tasks correct? 
вҖў Is the shutdown working? 
вҖў Is the production ramp-up correct, both when consuming and producing? 
вҖў Is the material balance constraint calculated correctly? Consuming and producing in the 
right periods? Is the received material of intermediate 1 added correctly? 
вҖў Is the capacity increased at the right time and by the correct amount at right the unit?

Since the objective is to minimise the slack variable of S2 from the safety level, it produces as 
much as possible to keep the inventory level above the safety level. The demand outgrows the 
capacities of the units, resulting in a stock out at time 31, as seen on the inventory graph in 
Figure 22. The slack variable enables us to see how much out-of-stock grows over time. 
Because of the capacity imbalance, unit 1 does not need to ramp up again after shutdown until 
week 34 as it does not add value since S1 manages to be well over safety the entire time horizon. 
Due to release time, unit 2 only produces until week 50 of 51, so it doesn't enter inventory and 
thus cannot cause a decrease in out-of-stock. Starting production in week 50 means it takes one 
week to produce and then one week to release; therefore, it is not added to inventory until after

The real-world case study will provide a more comprehensive overview of each step in the 
framework. As in the conceptual case study, first, a problem formulation will be presented, 
followed by a system definition. Then, the PPS+CP model from Chapter 4 will be used as a 
baseline, and additional features will be added and modelled as needed. An overview of the data 
will be presented without going into much detail about confidentiality. The model will then be 
verified, validated, and, in the end, applied. Some model scenarios will be shown in the 
application step based on identified risks and additional environmental constraints. 
7.1 
Step 1: Problem Formulation 
The purpose of a real-world case study is to use the model built in Chapter 4 to verify and 
validate that it can represent a real system and then to apply it to real data to showcase that the 
complexities of scheduling projects with required sequencing and combinations can be taken 
into account to structure and simplify decision making for specialist and planners when a 
facility is in a capacity ramp-up setting.  Due to confidentiality, all numbers have been 
normalised to between 0 and 1. 
The facilityвҖҷs objective is to meet yearly production targets that increase year-by-year in 
parallel with the growth in demand. Until now, decisions on when to SD, implement a project 
and ramp-up the production again have been made using heuristics. To be able to find feasible 
timings and sequence requires a lot of skill and knowledge from employees in all steps of 
production where many decisions need to be taken into account on production volume, 
maintaining inventory above safety levels, knowledge of the inventory levels when shutdowns 
are to be scheduled, knowledge on if the inventory is enough to do an SD without causing 
troubles downstream. To add to this, what project should be scheduled first, then the sequence 
of it, where the complexity of sequencing the projects becomes more difficult as more projects 
can be scheduled and their combinations. Adding to the sequencing and combinations is the 
layer of timing; each project can be scheduled anywhere in 52 weeks; when it should be 
scheduled has been considered the most challenging task of the employees in production. 
Suppose a decision is made to implement a project in the first part of the production. In that 
case, it might lead to a stockout of an intermediate inventory, causing a production stop in later 
steps, which could result in lower production volumes. It isn't easy to see the impact of ad-hoc 
decisions made today on future production volumes. Using mathematical programming, all 
decisions regarding all production steps, inventory levels, and when and in what sequence to 
schedule projects to meet the production targets (to ensure patients' demands can be met) can 
be considered simultaneously. Considering the information given above, the following problem 
definition is as follows: 
Given: a single-product API manufacturing system consisting of a single three-step 
manufacturing line, production data (e.g. production times, release times, current state 
capacities, yields, minimum production volumes, shutdowns, and production ramp-up), yearly 
production targets based on demands, inventory safety levels and limits and initial inventory, 
==PAGE:71==
 
63 
 
incoming shipments of intermediate 1 (I1) material, set of capacity projects, including process 
improvement and investment projects, their size, and durations. 
Determine: the optimal capacity ramp-up strategy for a single product end-to-end API 
manufacturing facility. The allocation of capacity projects, including their sequence, timings, 
and associated shutdowns. The allocation of production tasks on the production units, along 
with inventory levels of intermediates and final product. 
Subject to: meeting safety inventory levels, yearly production targets, and project 
dependencies. 
The output will be the optimal production plan that includes when to produce and how much, 
considering inventory safety level and maximum inventory storage, along with an optimal 
capacity plan for when and in what order to schedule projects, including their SD times. 
Assumptions are made first regarding the storage and supply of raw materials, which are 
unlimited since raw materials supply is not a part of the project scope because it is not a limiting 
factor. A demand plan is not given and, therefore, cannot be used to pull from inventory on the 
final product, and the inventory for it will accumulate over time according to production 
volumes. There are known inventory limits for all states. The storage for the final product will 
be ignored because there is no demand plan pulling from inventory. The time horizon spans 
fifty-two weeks with a discrete time of one week with no planned SD. Within internal planning, 
the release times are often assumed to be zero, where the material produced is available 
immediately in inventory after production. This facility's release times are 2-3 weeks, 
depending on the state. The release times are assumed to be zero to make the same 
assumptions as the production planners. 
7.2 
Step 2: System Definition 
The real-world system under study, given in Figure 31, produces one product that starts as raw 
material that goes to the first step of production fermentation (FER) connected to the second 
step recovery (REC) through pipes and, therefore, has no inventory in between. The third step 
is initial purification (PUR1), and the fourth step is final purification (PUR2), which goes to the 
final step of drying (DRY). Between final purification and drying, there is no inventory, similar 
to fermentation and recovery, where instead of pipes, there are tanks that fill up enough 
material to process in drying.

fact, a solution within the feasible solution space of the model. It shows that if the same

function as in constraint (32) of maximising production but allowing other decisions to be made

7.8.1 Baseline 
The baseline is created based on ideas that specialists and planners have about when projects 
should be scheduled. It runs on the model described in Chapter 7.2, where all the constraints 
and variables are listed. Projects 1 and 2 in F&R are scheduled in Q3, and project 3 is scheduled 
in Q4. For PUR1 and PUR2&DRY, project A needs to be scheduled last November, project B 
needs to be scheduled in Q2, and projects C and D need to be scheduled together after B. 
An example of an optimal solution is presented in Figure 46, with a Gantt chart. The projects 
are scheduled according to Table 16, where the table shows each project рқ‘җ, the project duration 
рқңҸрқ‘җ and at what time рқ‘Ў it is scheduled. Project 1 in F&R is scheduled at times 27 and 28, project 
2 at time 32, and project 3 at time 39. Projects A and B are scheduled in P1 and P2&D at times 
44 and 13,14,15,16, respectively. Projects C and D are scheduled in P1 together at times 17 and 
18.

8. Discussion 
The following will discuss the results and lessons learned from formulating the sequencing and 
combination constraints for the capacity projects. Next, a review of the framework will follow, 
with a discussion of advantages and disadvantages. 
8.1 
Case Studies Results 
The conceptual case study illustrated how projects could be scheduled for simple data with 
simple project dependencies. The model was first solved without scheduling a project to find 
how much the system would stock out. Projects were scheduled first on unit 2, then on both 
units and then on both units with project dependencies. All the scenarios were able to provide 
a solution without seeing a stockout. The scenarios showed symmetric solutions where the 
inventory and production variables would vary slightly, leading to the same objective value. 
Imposing additional constraints or alternating some features in the model could be a solution 
to addressing the symmetry.  
When applying the framework to the real-world case study, it was discovered that the project 
dependencies constraints developed in the conceptual case study would not scale well with the 
complexity of the project in the real-world case study. Constructing a network was then done 
to try to capture the complexity of the project dependencies. It was then discovered in the 
application step that the method does not scale very well as the number of available capacity 
project paths increases. Using 4 capacity projects with all paths allowed reaches a time limit of 
15 hours with a gap of 4.27%. In application in the real-world case study, only 1 year is 
considered at a time with 3-4 capacity projects for each unit. The model can, therefore, give a 
solution, but how the constraints are formulated for creating paths needs to be explored further 
to potentially decrease solution time. Especially if there is a desire to extend the planning 
horizon to more than 1 year, e.g. 3 years ahead, then the model will need to handle more 
projects and the additional decisions that come with extending the time. 
8.2 
Model Development 
In the literature review, a thorough discussion was conducted on capacity planning problems 
to review the various decisions that need to be made regarding the size, location, and timing of 
capacity changes. This included what approaches exist for capacity projects of fixed size and 
location considered as input and where a decision needs to be made for timing and sequencing 
the capacity projects. It was suggested that dynamic programming or constructing a network 
be used to find the shortest path through the network, where the focus is on minimising the 
costs of the projects.  
In the conceptual case study, with the knowledge at that time, it was not considered necessary 
to use either of those methods since the capacity projects had a simple setup, so another method 
with a binary table was explored instead. It was not until the real-world case study that it was 
realised how complex the combination of projects would get, and the method explored in the 
conceptual case study was nearly impossible to use for the real-world case study as a lot of 
==PAGE:97==
 
89 
 
manual work would need to be done. In the real-world case study, the methods in the literature 
review were revisited. The dynamic programming was not considered a good fit since the 
model was already built upon MILP formulation, and, therefore, it was more desirable to use 
MILP. An inspiration was then taken from the network presented both for visualisation and 
allowing for straightforward capacity project combinations constraints. Still, instead of having 
a source and a terminal node, the network was built up as described in the real-world chapter, 
where each edge has a duration and capacity change, where sequencing constraints were used 
to build paths and constraints over each line so the model could only choose one path.   
It would have been ideal to explore the method developed in the real-world case study on the 
conceptual case study. From there then develop more general set of the constraints and sets so 
that it could be used as part of the base model for project dependencies. 
One idea could involve creating a path formulation for each unit, with each edge specifying a 
duration and capacity change as done in the real-world case study, but this formulation should 
additionally be capable of determining the optimal timing. Therefore, the focus is not only on 
identifying the best path based on duration and capacity change but also on ensuring that this 
path is optimal for the PPS, taking into account the timing of the capacity projects. 
When modifying the capacity constraints from [14] to fit capacity projects, it was discovered 
that the letter рқҗ¶ was both used for a set of capacity expansion activities рқ‘җвҲҲрқҗ¶ and for capacity 
variable рқҗ¶рқ‘–рқ‘—рқ‘Ў which can be confusing for the reader, but it was decided to follow the names and 
terms used in the literature. 
8.3 
Framework  
This section presents the discussion on the advantages and disadvantages of the developed 
framework in Chapter 4.  
 Starting with the advantages, the framework first provides a structured and systemic approach 
to applying the model to production systems in various stages regardless of their maturity and 
adapting to any system representation, whether it is a one/multi-product or a one/multi 
production line network. Secondly, it begins with a thorough problem formulation process to 
define scope objectives and constraints, ensuring a clear understanding of the problem and 
aligning with stakeholders. Thirdly, the framework presents a clear process for selecting a base 
model depending on the system and problem in use the framework gives a step for extending 
the model as needed. Lastly, the framework includes steps for model verification and validation 
to ensure that the model accurately represents the system in scope and can answer the 
questions in the problem definition. 
Then, the disadvantages: first, the data collection in the framework might come as a challenge 
for gathering correct data and getting in contact with people needed for the data, especially 
dealing with uncertainties and risks. Secondly, depending on the system, if the base model 
requires a significant extension with additional parameters, decision variables, and constraints, 
it requires careful consideration and may add complexity to the model. Thirdly, in some cases, 
it can be difficult to validate models, especially if data for benchmarking is unavailable. Lastly, 
==PAGE:98==
 
90 
 
some previous steps might need to be visited a couple of times due to the backward loops of 
the framework if the current step encounters unexpected difficulties.

9. Conclusion 
The goal of the project was to develop a systematic framework for production ramp-up and 
apply it to generate the best (optimal) ramp-up strategy for a single-product end-to-end API 
manufacturing facility.  
The framework developed provides future users with a structured approach to applying the 
base model, considering their specific problem definition. A mathematical model was 
formulated using a PPS model, validated and refined to accommodate sequencing and 
combination effects for capacity projects. This model was implemented and solved using 
Pyomo and Gurobi for two case studies, a conceptual case study, and a real-world case study, 
with performance tested in terms of computation time and scalability. 
Future work will focus on improving the scheduling of capacity project combinations to reduce 
solution time and close the solution gap, as discussed in the earlier sections. Additionally, 
capacity projects might require unique production ramp-up curves, which could be included as 
parameters for each project. Capacity projects can also be extended to represent changes in 
yields and not only capacity. 
Furthermore, the framework and model developed now enable Novo Nordisk to make informed 
decisions about the implementation of capacity projects, considering the entire production line, 
instead of relying on heuristic approaches. New future facilities encountering similar challenges 
can utilise the framework for decision-making. This framework, developed in this thesis, 
represents the initial step toward addressing the ramp-up problem. In instances where 
facilities are multi-product, featuring more than one production line and a complex network, 
the framework will support determining the optimal capacity ramp-up strategy.
