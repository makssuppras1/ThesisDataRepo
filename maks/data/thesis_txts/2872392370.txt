Abstract
The production of hydrogen via grid-connected electrolysis introduces significant uncer-
tainty regarding its sustainability, as it depends heavily on the carbon intensity of the
electricity mix. Under current EU certification frameworks, such as the Renewable Fuels
of Non-Biological Origin (RFNBO) criteria, hydrogen produced from grid electricity quali-
fies as renewable only if the grid’s emission intensity remains below defined thresholds.
However, the eligibility of grid-based hydrogen can vary annually due to fluctuations in
weather conditions, which affect the renewable share of electricity generation. This study
explores how simulated weather-year variability influences the emission intensity and fea-
sibility of grid-connected hydrogen production in a fully decarbonising European energy
system. The analysis uses 42 distinct annual production profiles - referred to as weather
years - derived from the CorRES dataset, which simulates hourly wind and solar avail-
ability based on historical weather data from 1980 to 2021. These profiles determine the
temporal distribution of renewable output, while all installed generation, transmission, and
storage capacities are held constant at 2012 levels. No new technologies are introduced
apart from fossil-based backup systems, making all scenario comparisons relative to the
temporal generation pattern of the baseline year 2012.
Using a consistent modelling framework across 41 weather-year scenarios, the study
evaluates how inter-annual variability in renewable output affects electricity and hydro-
gen production, system emissions, and compliance with RFNBO certification thresholds.
Results show that hydrogen production volumes and their associated emissions are highly
sensitive to the alignment between renewable generation and demand. Even years with
abundant renewable output - such as 2011 - do not necessarily result in low-emission
hydrogen, whereas years like 2000 demonstrate that favourable timing and system in-
tegration are more critical than overall renewable volume alone. Only one weather-year
scenario (2000) achieves complete decarbonisation of both electricity and hydrogen pro-
duction by 2050, illustrating the stringent system requirements for robust net-zero out-
comes. Conversely, unfavourable timing, as observed in 1990 and 1996, leads to in-
creased backup reliance, lower hydrogen output, and elevated emissions - despite con-
stant capacity.
Across all scenarios, renewable electricity shares rise sharply by mid-century, reaching
over 90% by 2050. However, all systems still rely on fossil-based backup for heat, and
emissions from this source vary substantially with weather-year conditions. Importantly,
compliance with RFNBO thresholds is not consistently guaranteed in earlier years, even
when average annual renewable shares are high, due to temporal mismatches. This high-
lights the need for certification schemes to incorporate higher temporal resolution and ac-
knowledge inter-annual variability. Additionally, the findings underscore the importance
of system-wide flexibility - such as clean backup capacity and sector-coupled storage - in
ensuring reliable and sustainable hydrogen production under real-world weather dynam-
ics.
In conclusion, while grid-connected hydrogen can be fully decarbonised by 2050, this
outcome is highly dependent on the temporal structure of renewable generation and the
broader energy system configuration. Flexibility measures, particularly those enabling
temporal alignment and backup decarbonisation, play a critical role in enhancing RFNBO
certification robustness and long-term sustainability.

Introduction
The European Union (EU) established a legally binding objective of achieving climate
neutrality by 2050, formalised in the 2019 European Climate Law and forming a core
component of the European Green Deal [1, 2]. This long-term strategy has driven the
development of policies and emission reduction targets at both national and union-wide
levels. At present, as indicated by the International Energy Agency (IEA) [3] and dis-
cussed in the literature review, energy production within the European Union is based
on a combination of fossil fuels and renewable sources, and thus has not yet achieved
climate neutrality. Substantial transformation of the energy system is required to meet the
2050 target .
To support this transformation across these varied systems, the EU promotes cross-
border collaboration through organisations such as the European Network of Transmis-
sion System Operators for Electricity (ENTSO-E) and its gas counterpart ENTSO-G. These
collaborations enables coordinated infrastructure planning and facilitates electricity and
gas trading between member states.
Through these collaborations the emissions reductions are already underway in several
sectors—particularly electricity generation—others remain significantly harder to decar-
bonise. These “hard-to-abate” sectors include heavy industry, freight transport, and avi-
ation, where direct electrification is either technically or economically unfeasible. In such
contexts, hydrogen has emerged as a promising energy carrier.
Hydrogen is widely regarded as a promising energy carrier due to its versatility, zero
emissions at the point of use, and potential to support deep decarbonisation across mul-
tiple sectors. It can be used for power generation, industrial processes, transportation,
and heating, particularly in applications that are difficult to electrify [4, 5]. With a high
energy density by mass and the ability to store energy over long durations, hydrogen is
well-suited to balancing variable renewable energy sources such as wind and solar [6].
Furthermore, it enables sector coupling by allowing surplus renewable electricity to be
converted and utilised across different parts of the energy system [7]. When produced
using renewable electricity via electrolysis, hydrogen can significantly reduce greenhouse
gas emissions and serve as a cleaner alternative to fossil fuels. Its ability to provide long-
duration energy storage also supports energy system flexibility by helping to balance the
intermittency of solar and wind power [8, 9]. In addition, hydrogen contributes to energy
security and enables greater sector integration. However, realising its full potential will re-
quire overcoming key challenges related to production costs, infrastructure development,
and energy conversion efficiency [4, 10].
Recognising hydrogen’s strategic role in achieving climate goals, multiple actors have
outlined future projections for hydrogen supply and demand. In June 2021, the EHB
initiative published the report titled ”Analysing Future Demand, Supply, and Transport of
Hydrogen” [11], presenting anticipated hydrogen supply and demand scenarios as well
as envisioning a dedicated hydrogen transport infrastructure across Europe. Similarly, in
November 2022, the Joint Research Centre (JRC) of the European Commission released
the report Clean Energy Technology Observatory: Water Electrolysis and Hydrogen in
the European Union [12], providing a detailed analysis of technological developments
and demand projections.

To ensure hydrogen production aligns with broader decarbonisation objectives, the Euro-
pean Union has introduced a regulatory framework defining when hydrogen qualifies as a
Renewable Fuel of Non-Biological Origin (RFNBO). In 2023, the European Commission
adopted a set of delegated acts under the Renewable Energy Directive (RED II), estab-
lishing these criteria [13]. They are designed to promote the use of additional renewable
electricity, enforce temporal and geographic correlation, and ultimately reduce the lifecy-
cle emissions of hydrogen. Beyond emissions reduction, the production of RFNBO fuels
can also enhance energy system stability by providing flexible demand that helps bal-
ance fluctuations in renewable electricity supply [14]. In support of these goals, the EU
has committed to producing 10 million tonnes of RFNBO certified hydrogen annually by
2030, supplemented by an equivalent import target [15]. These ambitious hydrogen pro-
duction goals have prompted important questions regarding how the future energy system
should be designed.
Energy system modelling is a valuable tool for assessing pathways to achieve climate
neutrality, enabling policymakers, researchers, and stakeholders to simulate the long-
term evolution of energy systems under varying assumptions about technology availabil-
ity, policy frameworks, and market dynamics. One of the core strengths of such models is
their ability to compare the relative performance and cost-effectiveness of different tech-
nologies, helping to prioritise investments within complex decarbonisation scenarios [16].
This capability is particularly relevant for emerging solutions such as hydrogen, which
introduces additional complexities related to infrastructure development, supply chains,
and temporal balancing of energy demand and supply [12].
However, many existing energy system models assume simplified or static production
profiles—typically expressed through discrete categories such as “low”, “medium”, or
“high” production levels—without fully accounting for the temporal and spatial variability
inherent in renewable energy generation [17]. The output of renewable electricity sources
like solar and wind varies significantly across hours, seasons, and years, driven by fluc-
tuating weather conditions [18]. When this variability is not adequately captured, par-
ticularly across different weather years, it can lead to significant misestimations of the
carbon intensity of hydrogen produced via electrolysis using grid electricity. As hydro-
gen production depends directly on the availability and carbon profile of electricity, this
omission undermines the accuracy of both emissions accounting and techno-economic
assessments [19].
This thesis addresses this gap by integrating weather-year variability into the modelling
framework. Instead of relying on static averages or stylised scenarios, it examines how in-
terannual variations in renewable generation—based on simulated weather data—impact
the emissions intensity of grid-connected hydrogen production [19]. By doing so, the anal-
ysis provides a more realistic and nuanced understanding of hydrogen’s role in future en-
ergy systems and emphasises the importance of incorporating renewable intermittency in
long-term decarbonisation planning [17, 11].
This modelling limitation motivates the central research question of this thesis: What is
the impact of weather-years on the emission intensity of grid-produced hydrogen?
In this context, the term production level refers to a weather-year, since renewable energy
generation is strongly influenced by weather conditions. For instance, periods with lower
solar irradiance or wind speeds directly lead to reduced energy output. This equivalence
between weather variability and production levels forms a central analytical framework for
the study presented.

Accordingly, this thesis explores how the use of different weather-years affects the simu-
lation and modelling of the future European energy systems, particularly with respect to
the production of hydrogen. In this relation this thesis seeks to answer the following sub
research questions:
• How much do hydrogen production emissions change from year to year as wind and
solar profiles vary?
• Is it possible to produce RFNBO hydrogen using grid-produced electricity with vary-
ing weather years?
• What is the relative impact of large-scale battery enforcement versus downsizing
fossil backup generators on residual CO2 emissions?
• How does reliance on fossil-fuel backup (electricity and heat) vary across weather
years, and what are the implications for a net-zero system?
• To what extent can grid-connected hydrogen production meet long-term emissions
reduction targets under varying policy scenarios and demand pathways?
• What is the robustness of the results using different weather years?
This thesis focuses on the EU-27 (hereafter referred to as the EU), as well as the United
Kingdom, Switzerland, and Norway, which participate in various binding and non-binding
EU energy and climate strategies. These countries are included due to their integration
into the European energy market and their alignment with key EU targets. As such this
thesis focuses on modelling the emission intensity of grid-connected hydrogen production
under varying weather-year conditions in a European context.

2.1
Introduction to energy systems
The European Union (EU), consisting of 27 member states, is characterised by consid-
erable diversity in its energy systems, which have been largely developed in response to
national policies, public acceptance of specific technologies, and the availability of natural
resources within each country.
To support integration across these varied systems, the EU promotes cross-border en-
ergy infrastructure development through the Trans-European Networks for Energy TEN-E
framework. This initiative facilitates the planning, financing, and regulatory alignment of
strategically important infrastructure projects, including electricity and gas networks that
span multiple member states [20].
Supporting this policy framework are technical bodies such as the ENTSO-E and its gas
counterpart, ENTSO-G, which coordinate system planning, operational modelling, and
long-term infrastructure development. Looking ahead, these collaborative platforms may
also enable the future cross-border trade of RFNBOs, such as hydrogen, as proposed
in the ”European Hydrogen Backbone” (EHB) report, published in June 2021. The report
outlines projections for future hydrogen supply and demand across the EU and the United
Kingdom (UK) and proposes a dedicated hydrogen transport infrastructure to meet these
needs.
This subsection presents the energy systems of the EU as a whole, along with selected
member and associated states — Denmark (DK), France (FR), and Norway (NO) — to
illustrate the diversity of national energy systems within Europe. The comparison focuses
on the sources of domestic energy supply and electricity generation.
These countries were selected based on their distinct energy profiles:
• Denmark is recognised for its high wind energy potential and advanced wind turbine
expertise, resulting in a significant share of electricity generation originating from
wind power.
• France stands out for its strong reliance on nuclear power, which dominates its en-
ergy mix.
• Norway, although not a EU member, is included due to its close energy cooperation
with the EU, vast hydropower resources, significant oil reserves, extensive storage
capabilities, and key role in Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) and Carbon Cap-
ture, Utilisation and Storage (CCUS) technology innovation.
Energy production is influenced by several factors, including geographical resource avail-
ability, technological development, economic feasibility, and levels of social acceptance.

2.3
Hydrogen
Hydrogen represents a potential solution for decarbonising the hard-to-abate sectors. Be-
yond its end-use applications, hydrogen can also enhance energy system flexibility. By
enabling the storage and temporal shifting of surplus renewable electricity, hydrogen pro-
duction helps mitigate the intermittency challenges posed by high shares of variable re-
newable energy in the European grid.
Hydrogen is currently produced predominantly using fossil fuels as a feed stock e.g. by
the use of a Steam Methane Reforming (SMR) process [22]. SMR is a process that re-
acts methane with steam to generate hydrogen, carbon monoxide, and carbon dioxide.
While widely used due to its cost-effectiveness and established infrastructure, SMR is
energy-intensive and emits significant amounts of greenhouse gases (GHG), making it
environmentally unsustainable. Nevertheless, it remains essential for industries that re-
quire hydrogen or benefit from its by-products.
To fulfil the hydrogen demand, an alternative production method is to produce hydrogen
via electrolysers. Electrolysers uses electricity to split water into hydrogen and oxygen.
In this manner fewer GHG are emitted. As a result thereof the carbon footprint of the pro-
cess is lowered. Electrolysers can be connected either to the electricity grid or directly to

2.4
Weather variability and its implications
Traditional energy system modelling commonly relies on a single representative weather
year to simulate future energy scenarios. While this approach may be sufficient for sys-
tems dominated by stable fossil-based generation, it proves inadequate in systems with
high shares of Variable Renewable Energy (VRE), where weather-driven fluctuations sig-
nificantly influence system dynamics.
Recent research highlights the limitations of single-year modelling. As noted by Hans
Ravn et al. in [17], infrastructure planning based on one weather year risks underesti-
mating variability and overestimating system robustness [17]. In their study, 62 different
weather years were used to optimise capacity layouts for a net-zero European energy
system. These layouts were subsequently tested across other weather years to evalu-
ate performance metrics such as system costs, CO2 emissions, and resource adequacy.
Results revealed a ±10% variation in system costs and showed that designs accounting
for compound weather events achieved 99.9% adequacy and net-negative emissions of
−0.5% relative to 1990 levels.
Similarly, as noted by Seán Collins et al. in [30], it is reported that single-year studies can
deviate by up to ±9% from long-term averages at a continental level, with even larger dis-
crepancies at national scales. These findings underscore the importance of incorporating
inter-annual weather variability into energy system design.
Key implications of multi-year weather modelling include:
• Greater insight into operational variability, [30]
• More accurate estimates of system cost and emissions, [17]
• Improved reliability through resource adequacy analysis, [17]
• Identification of system-critical events (e.g. low-wind winters), [31]
• Better-informed capacity expansion planning, [17]
• Recognition of the risks inherent in single-year analyses, [17, 30]
In response, the modelling community is increasingly adopting multi-decadal weather
datasets, high-resolution spatial and temporal inputs, and advanced sector-coupled frame-
works. These models typically optimise system layouts for select ”design years” and test
performance across multiple ”operational years” using metrics such as loss of load ex-
pectation, marginal costs, and cumulative CO2 emissions.
In summary, capturing the variability introduced by multiple weather years is essential for
developing cost-effective, reliable, and climate-resilient energy systems. As the role of
VRE increases, robust modelling approaches must evolve beyond single-year simulations
to ensure sound infrastructure investment and policy-making.

2.5
Large-Scale Energy Storage Solutions
To support the EU’s energy transition, large-scale storage technologies are essential for
balancing the grid and maximising renewable energy use. The current primary storage
method is pumped hydro storage [32], but emerging solutions include batteries (for short-
term balancing) and hydrogen (for long-term storage).
Storage must evolve for the energy system to be able to phase out fossil fuels, in order
for balancing supply and demand, when both are uncontrollable. Hydrogen and synthetic
fuels can replace natural gas in storage, though challenges like idle losses and conver-
sion inefficiencies persist. By 2030–2050, storage technologies are expected to diversify
significantly [27, 26].
Hydrogen enables long-term, seasonal storage, aligning production with surplus renew-
able electricity and usage with demand. It can be transported via pipelines, the most
cost-effective method [11], though issues like embrittlement, permeation, and leakage
risks complicate deployment [33, 34].
Grid-scale batteries offer flexibility for short-term storage and ancillary services (e.g., peak
shaving, frequency regulation). While currently limited in duration, battery capacity is
projected to grow from 28 GW (2022) to nearly 970 GW by 2030 under a net-zero scenario
[32].
Three battery technologies were evaluated for their suitability in supporting large-scale
energy storage. Lithium-ion (Li-ion) batteries are characterised by high energy density
and widespread deployment; however, they remain costly and heavily dependent on crit-
ical raw materials [35, 36, 37, 38]. Sodium–Sulphur (NaS) batteries, in contrast, offer
high efficiency and utilise more abundant materials, but are constrained by their require-
ment for high operating temperatures [35, 39, 40, 41]. Sodium–Nickel Chloride (NaNiCl2)
batteries present a safer and more durable alternative, although they are currently more
expensive and less technologically mature [42, 43, 33]. Each of these technologies brings
distinct advantages across various time scales and use cases, collectively contributing to
the European Union’s goals for energy resilience and decarbonisation. A more detailed
discussion of these technologies is provided in the Appendix in Section U.1.

3.1
Work flow
In this thesis, the European energy system is simulated using different weather years to
determine how weather variability affects energy and hydrogen production.
The scope of this thesis includes the 27 EU member states, as well as Norway (NO),
Switzerland (CH), and the United Kingdom (UK). A complete list of countries in the simu-

To address the research questions, the Balmorel model is used to simulate the future
European energy system. This thesis utilises the February 2025 version of the Balmorel
framework, developed by DTU Management and obtained from their official GitHub repos-
itory [44]. The model is adapted to fit the specific scope of this study.
In order to simulate realistic VRE production profiles, the model integrates output from
Correlations in Renewable Energy Sources (CorRES)—a time series simulation tool de-
veloped by DTU Wind Energy. CorRES enables the generation of geographically and
technologically differentiated production profiles for wind and solar power, thereby en-

Input data are sourced from GitHub, CorRES, and publicly available scientific publications,
including the Energy Technology Catalogues published by the DEA [45, 33, 34].
Given the high temporal and spatial resolution of the input data, along with the aim of con-
ducting simulations for 42 weather years using daily profiles, the Balmorel model is exe-

of multiple scenario runs, thereby reducing total computation time and enhancing overall
efficiency.
3.2
Balmorel model
The Balmorel model, initially developed in 2001 by Hans Ravn et al.[46], is a well-established
tool for modelling the European energy system.
Since its release, development has
continued under an open-source community, which actively maintains and extends the
model’s capabilities. Balmorel has been applied in a variety of contexts—ranging from
academic research to industry consultancy—and has supported governmental bodies in
developing energy policies, legislation, and future system projections, example of previ-
ous uses of the model are discussed by Ioannis Kountouris et al. [47]
Balmorel is an open-source, deterministic, partial equilibrium model that, under the as-
sumption of perfect markets and rational economic behaviour, optimises energy systems
by maximising social welfare. This welfare is defined as the difference between con-
sumers’ utility and producers’ costs associated with electricity and district heating gener-
ation, storage, transmission, and distribution. It uses a bottom-up approach and operates
on an hourly time resolution (8,736 time steps per year), allowing for detailed simulation
across all technologies and time periods. The model simultaneously optimises both in-
vestment planning and operational dispatch, subject to a wide range of technical, physical,
and regulatory constraints.
Two types of storage are implemented: short-term (electricity and heat) and long-term
(electricity, heat, hydrological reservoirs and gas), all governed by storage dynamics.

3.2.3
Applied approach with capacity run and subsequent dispatch
In this thesis, a particular focus is placed on dual-layer optimisation: a base run is con-
figured as a capacity run, while alternative scenarios use the dispatch outcomes from the
base run as input.
The base run is conducted as a capacity optimisation, where investment decisions de-
termine the installed generation, storage, and transmission capacities under a reference
weather year. For subsequent scenario runs involving different weather years, the invest-

system infrastructure across all scenarios, isolating the effects of weather variability on
system operation. Dispatch variables, however, remain endogenous and are optimised
individually for each weather year, allowing the model to capture how temporal resource
variations influence the operational use of assets.

CorRES (Data)
The department of Wind Energy at DTU has developed a time series simulation tool for
VRE named CorRES, which is an abbreviation for Correlations in Renewable Energy
Sources. The CorRES data provides hourly simulations of installed wind and solar en-
ergy capacities from the 1st of January 1980 to the 31st of December 2021. This dataset
has previously been employed in various wind and solar integration studies, including
large-scale system analyses and plant-level assessments. Notably, it has been utilised
by clients such as ENTSO-E [53].
In this thesis, the CorRES data is used to define the hourly production profiles for wind and
solar across 42 different weather years. These profiles determine the temporal distribution
of renewable output in the model. The objective is to assess whether a fixed base capacity
— using year 2012 as a baseline — can fulfil the RFNBO certification requirements for
grid-connected hydrogen production under varying renewable production profiles.
The production profiles within the CorRES data are provided in two formats: ”existing”
and ”simulated”. The term ”existing” refers to installations present as of 2020. For both
energy technologies, the simulated data are modelled based on site restrictions and are
evaluated across different resource grades. These resource grades support the explo-
ration of different installation possibilities at the examined sites. The highest resource
grade is denoted as RGA and the least as RGC.
Simulated wind capacity includes both onshore and offshore installations, with offshore
further divided into bottom-fixed and floating configurations. Offshore turbines are as-
sumed to have a specific power of 370W/m2 at a hub height of 155 m. Onshore turbines
are modelled with specific powers of 277 and 198 W/m2 at hub heights of 100 m and 150
m, respectively. To simulate an increase in efficiency due to technology development, an
increase of 5% in production profiles for each decade is assumed.
The CorRES database includes three types of solar energy production profiles: ”No Track-
ing Utility”, ”Tracking Utility”, and ”Rooftop”. For simplicity, this thesis focuses exclusively
on the ”No Tracking Utility profiles”, which assume south-facing installations with tilt an-
gles representative of typical system configurations. For the solar datasets, the Balmorel
model includes an “existing” class that is not present in the updated CorRES data. To
address this, it is assumed that the “existing” solar installations correspond in size and
performance to those categorised under the medium resource grade (resource grade B)
in the CorRES dataset.

The following section outlines the configuration of the base and scenario runs used in the
analysis. It describes the modelling approach, key assumptions, and the input data used

4.2
Scenario descriptions
This study distinguishes between model-wide assumptions and scenario-specific settings
to ensure consistency and transparency across all simulations. The scenarios are struc-
tured into two categories: the base scenario, which serves as the foundation for capacity
development, and the alternative scenarios, which are used to assess the robustness of
the energy system under varying weather conditions.
4.2.1
Base scenario
The base scenario is designed to ensure compliance with net-zero emissions targets by
2050. To achieve this, a strict emissions cap of 0 gCO￿/kWh is imposed. Additionally,
a carbon tax is introduced to prevent an over-reliance on biofuels, which, despite being
renewable, may still involve lifecycle emissions and resource constraints. This approach
promotes a more balanced and realistic energy mix while supporting climate neutrality.
The year 2012 is selected as the reference weather year based on findings from both
Collins et al. [30] and DTU’s Balmorel developers, who identify it as representative of
average wind and solar conditions in Europe. This base year is used to simulate electricity
generation from renewables and serves as a benchmark for evaluating all other weather
years.
Decommissioning is enabled for both exogenous and endogenous technologies, with the
constraint that no technology can be reinstated after reaching its end-of-life. Exogenous
decommissioning starts in 2016, while endogenous decommissioning is optimised by the
model from 2025 onwards. All model runs operate at five-year intervals between 2025
and 2050.
In terms of energy storage, the same configuration is maintained across all scenarios. No
reallocation between intra-seasonal and inter-seasonal storage types is permitted. For

sodium–nickel chloride batteries, all assumed to be commercially available.

To prevent the model from allocating unrealistic levels of renewable capacity in pursuit of

the base scenario is developed as a capacity run, and the resulting investment decisions
form the basis for all subsequent alternative scenarios. Hydrogen imports are allowed
across selected non-EU borders (Spain, Italy, and Hungary), reflecting the assumption of
an integrated European hydrogen trade network.
4.2.2
Alternative scenarios
The alternative scenarios use weather years other than 2012 to evaluate the system’s
sensitivity to inter-annual variability in renewable energy availability. These scenarios
import key outputs from the base run to ensure consistency. Specifically, they adopt the
same installed capacities for generation, storage, and transmission, and adds backup
technology to the system configurations.
While demand inputs and infrastructure remain fixed, the production profiles for wind and
solar are updated using CorRES data to reflect the specific weather year of each scenario.
In these scenarios, the strict net-zero constraint is relaxed to test whether the hydrogen
demand for 2050 can still be met under less favourable weather conditions. However, the
carbon tax remains in place, providing continued incentives to minimise emissions.
To maintain feasibility in the model—even in scenarios with extremely low renewable
resource availability—backup technologies are introduced for both electricity and heat.
These include a natural gas-fired condensing power plant for electricity and a gas-fired
boiler for heat. They are not intended as primary sources but act as stress indicators,
only dispatching when needed to prevent infeasibilities. To avoid unsolvable model states,
these backup technologies are assigned exceptionally high capacity limits (e.g., 1,000,000,
000,000 GW). Their actual use is monitored to evaluate the resilience of the system and
to gauge the reliance on fossil-based dispatch in adverse conditions.
Decommissioning is not re-evaluated in the alternative scenarios and remains fixed from
the base scenario. This ensures that only weather-driven variability, rather than changing
technology phase-outs, is being tested in the comparative analysis.

Analysis
This section presents a set of model-based scenarios designed to test the robustness
of the future energy system under varying conditions. It begins with the Base Scenario,
which represents a net-zero pathway under average weather conditions and serves as
the reference for all comparisons. The Scenario Analysis explores inter-annual weather
variability across 42 distinct weather years, assessing its impact on electricity generation,
hydrogen production, storage use, and emissions. A System Configuration Test follows,
focusing on enforced battery deployment to evaluate how different short-term storage
technologies affect system performance and hydrogen-related emissions. Finally, a Sen-
sitivity Analysis examines the influence of key modelling assumptions, such as emission
constraints and backup capacity limits, to identify critical drivers of system behaviour.
6.1
Base Scenario
The base scenario outlines the energy system’s pathway to net-zero emissions by 2050,
serving as a reference for later comparisons. It captures core shifts in emissions, demand,
and supply as renewable technologies and policy interventions reshape the energy land-
scape.
6.1.1
Emissions and Energy Transition
Emissions from energy production decline steadily as fossil fuels are phased out and
renewables expand, more than doubling total energy output while sharply reducing carbon
intensity. By 2050, renewables account for 95% of electricity generation, with remaining
emissions from fossil fuels mitigated through CCS deployment. Hydrogen production
emissions fall to near zero by 2040, driven by a shift from SMR to renewable-powered
electrolysis and imports, though this relies on optimistic assumptions about input purity
and grid carbon content.
6.1.2
Structural Shifts in Demand and Supply
Electricity demand nearly triples by 2050, driven by transport, heating, and hydrogen pro-
duction electrification. Hydrogen demand rises from 106 TWh in 2025 to over 2,000 TWh
by 2050, highlighting its growing role in storage, industry, and synthetic fuels. Heat de-
mand remains stable, possibly reflecting efficiency gains. Significant expansions in stor-
age and cross-border transmission support flexibility needs in a renewables-dominated
system. While fossil fuels still supply 30% of energy by 2050, emissions fall to net-zero
through widespread CCS use and clean hydrogen uptake.
6.1.3
Hydrogen Demand in Context
The model’s hydrogen demand projections exceed external forecasts. Compared to JRC’s
798 TWh in 2050, this scenario estimates over 2,000 TWh, reflecting broader coverage
including power balancing and synthetic fuels. This highlights the need for ambitious hy-
drogen infrastructure development.
6.1.4
Model Assumptions and Limitations
The net-zero pathway rests on key assumptions: electrolysis and hydrogen imports are
considered emissions-free, and CCS is assumed widely available and economically vi-
able. Fossil fuel use persists for balancing, and emissions reduction is gradual. These
caveats emphasize the need for sensitivity testing, especially around electrolysis, CCS
scalability, and policy robustness.

Discussion
This section interprets the key findings from the modelling analysis, focusing on the evolv-
ing role of hydrogen within a decarbonising energy system. It explores how variability in
renewable generation and system flexibility shape hydrogen demand, production, and
emissions outcomes. The implications for policy, infrastructure planning, and future re-
search directions are also examined to provide a comprehensive understanding of the
challenges and opportunities in the energy transition.
7.1
Implications of Electricity System Trends
The dominance of renewables even under relaxed constraints may indicate structural cost
and efficiency advantages that naturally favour a decarbonised mix. However, this out-
come depends on critical model assumptions, including optimistic projections of renew-
able capacity growth, stable investment environments, and idealised system operation. In
practice, political, regulatory, and economic frictions could delay or redirect this transition.
For example, while EU policies support accelerated solar and wind deployment, recent
data show declining installation rates between 2023 and 2024 for both technologies [58,
57, 59, 55], suggesting emerging challenges in maintaining momentum.
The strong influence of wind variability on system outcomes underscores the need for
accurate representation of inter annual weather patterns. In this modelling framework,
generation, transmission, and storage capacities are fixed based on a single represen-
tative weather year—2012—which exhibits higher-than-average FLH. As shown in the
pre-processing analysis (Section 4.1.2), anchoring system design to a year with partic-
ularly favourable renewable output introduces a potential upward bias in projected hy-
drogen production and system performance. Scenarios using less favourable weather
years—with lower renewable output or poor seasonal alignment—show higher emissions
and reduced hydrogen supply by 2050. However, since average FLH is a coarse met-
ric, it cannot fully capture hourly or seasonal mismatches, reinforcing the need for more
nuanced, multi-year sensitivity testing.
This increasing uncertainty demands that flexibility solutions not only improve but also
anticipate sector-specific decarbonisation challenges, such as those seen in the heat sec-
tor. While electricity system flexibility improves over time, the persistent need for thermal
backup—even in scenarios with high renewable penetration—indicates slower progress
in decarbonising the heat sector. This gap may reflect underdeveloped thermal storage
deployment, limited demand-side flexibility, or a lack of integration between electricity,
heat, and industrial systems. Bridging this gap will likely require not only additional infras-
tructure but also smarter sector coupling—such as using waste heat from data centres or
co-locating industrial heat demand with renewable generation.
The increasing divergence in electricity production across weather years by 2050 high-
lights the rising impact of weather-driven uncertainty as systems approach net-zero. Pol-
icymakers and planners must therefore design resilient infrastructure strategies that per-
form under a wide range of climate and demand conditions.
The findings carry significant implications for policymakers and system planners. Fore-
most, there is an urgent need to accelerate investments in electrolyser capacity alongside
renewable generation build-out. This dual approach enhances the likelihood of decarbon-
ising hydrogen production effectively.

The analysis indicates that scenarios with higher emissions in later years often coincide

energy storage technologies. Their performance—like the rest of the system—is strongly
influenced by weather variability across time and space, reinforcing the need for robust,
weather-resilient infrastructure and operational strategies.
7.2
Hydrogen Demand and System Implications
The EHB estimates hydrogen production potential from dedicated renewables at 450
TWh, 2,100 TWh, and 4,000 TWh by 2030, 2040, and 2050 respectively, necessitating
approximately 1,900 GW to 4,500 GW of new renewable capacity. This vastly exceeds
current installed electrolyser capacity, which stood at only 162 MW as of August 2022,
with projections of 1.4 GW by 2025 [54].
This considerable gap highlights the scale and urgency of infrastructure deployment re-
quired to meet green hydrogen targets. Without rapid expansion, the hydrogen transition
risks remaining reliant on fossil fuels far longer than intended.
The results demonstrate that hydrogen demand in the base scenario substantially ex-
ceeds the JRC projections used as inputs. This discrepancy likely arises from the model’s
endogenous treatment of hydrogen demand, which encompasses not only industrial ap-
plications (as reflected in the JRC baseline) but also hydrogen consumption for synthetic
fuel production, energy storage, and electricity system balancing. This underscores hy-
drogen’s evolving role beyond a mere industrial feedstock to a multi-sectoral energy car-
rier. The implications of these expanded uses are further discussed in Section 7.3.
To accommodate rising demand, the model simulates a significant expansion of hydrogen
generation capacity alongside the establishment of large-scale hydrogen transmission in-
frastructure by 2050. In the early years, production remains domestic and locally con-
sumed; however, by 2050 a dedicated hydrogen transmission network emerges, with di-
rectional flows primarily from Southern Europe—particularly Spain—towards Central and

Northern Europe. This shift reflects regional variations in renewable electricity availability

While the model accounts for hydrogen transport via pipelines, alternative transport modes

or sensitivity analyses.
A critical aspect of this analysis is the compliance of grid-produced hydrogen with the

threshold applies at the point of electrolysis, the model employs a simplified system-wide
average emissions profile rather than a spatially disaggregated one to assess RFNBO
compliance.
Under this approach, hydrogen meets RFNBO criteria in 2040 and 2045, when the av-

growth.

Furthermore, a key modelling assumption treats all hydrogen produced via electrolysis
or imported from outside the EU as zero-emission. While reasonable under full renew-

weather years when fossil generation remains active. Consequently, emissions may be
underestimated in scenarios where backup fossil electricity supports hydrogen produc-
tion.
Additionally, the model does not incorporate regional variations in electricity emissions
intensity or renewable resource distribution. This limitation could lead to over- or un-
derestimation of RFNBO eligibility depending on location. Future work should integrate
spatially and temporally explicit modelling to better reflect regulatory requirements and
enhance the realism of hydrogen certification pathways.
7.3
Hydrogen’s Evolving Role in the Energy System
In the model, hydrogen emerges not only as an industrial fuel but also as a strategic
energy vector for seasonal storage, synthetic fuel production, and balancing services.
This broader systemic role significantly increases hydrogen’s value and the associated
infrastructure requirements, distinguishing the modelled pathway from simpler end-use
projections, such as those provided by the JRC.

role expands considerably to support system-level flexibility. The dominant production

and hydrogen-to-storage (H2S)—indicate a clear pivot: hydrogen becomes a strategic

This transition underscores hydrogen’s dual function as both a decarbonisation vector

Importantly, this transformation imposes new demands on transmission infrastructure,

These mismatches could be exacerbated by regional variability in renewable output, mak-

pipeline deployment or electrolyser siting.
Overall, the findings suggest hydrogen acts as a cornerstone in managing the system-
wide energy transition, not only meeting sector-specific decarbonisation targets but also
absorbing, storing, and redistributing renewable electricity at scale. Future research should
investigate the spatial and temporal dynamics of this role, especially under varying weather
years and more realistic infrastructure constraints.
7.3.1
Impacts of Weather Year Variability on Hydrogen Production
A central objective of this thesis is to examine how inter-annual weather variability in-
fluences hydrogen production within a decarbonising energy system. The model results
demonstrate that hydrogen output and usage patterns are highly sensitive to underlying
weather conditions, primarily due to their impact on VRE generation.
In years with abundant solar or wind resources, hydrogen production via electrolysis rises
substantially. Electrolysers absorb surplus renewable electricity, converting what would
otherwise be curtailed power into storable energy. Conversely, during years with lower
VRE yields, hydrogen output is constrained, increasing reliance on stored hydrogen or
alternative energy carriers.
This sensitivity emphasises hydrogen’s critical role as a flexibility and balancing asset in
VRE-dominant systems [62, 63]. However, it also reveals a key operational challenge:
although electrolysis can be dispatched at will, hydrogen production remains indirectly
constrained by weather conditions. Ensuring a reliable hydrogen supply despite weather
variability may necessitate oversizing electrolyser capacity or expanding storage, consis-
tent with findings from Schmidt et al. [64], who emphasise the importance of infrastructure
scale in accommodating variable renewable generation.

Looking ahead, integrating region-specific weather stress tests and probabilistic mod-
elling of renewable supply into policy and investment frameworks will be essential. With-
out these tools, decision-makers risk underestimating the resilience requirements of a
hydrogen-integrated energy system.
7.4
Limitations and Areas for Future Research
This study has investigated the emission impacts of hydrogen production within an evolv-
ing energy system influenced by inter-annual weather variability. The findings reveal sev-
eral critical insights into the role of hydrogen, the robustness of emission reductions, and
implications for energy system planning.
While the Balmorel model provides valuable insights, certain limitations must be acknowl-
edged. The deterministic nature of the simulations per weather year does not capture
stochastic extremes or dynamic feedback loops within the energy system. Additionally,
assumptions regarding technology cost trajectories, fuel availability, and policy instru-
ments such as carbon pricing could influence the projections.
Future research should explore probabilistic approaches to better capture extreme events
and their implications on hydrogen and emissions pathways. Dynamic policy modeling
and sensitivity analyses around key parameters would also strengthen understanding of
hydrogen’s role in the energy transition.
7.5
Hydrogen as Bridge or Barrier
Overall, the study highlights hydrogen’s dual role in the decarbonisation trajectory. While
hydrogen presents a promising pathway to decarbonize hard-to-electrify sectors, the tran-
sition to low-emission hydrogen is not guaranteed. Under weather variability, fossil-based
hydrogen production risks delaying emission reductions, underscoring the need for coor-
dinated technology, infrastructure, and policy strategies.
Designing energy systems that are resilient to weather fluctuations and prioritizing early
deployment of renewable hydrogen technologies will be critical to ensure that hydrogen
acts as a bridge—not a barrier—to a sustainable, low-carbon future.

Conclusion
This study explored the influence of simulated weather-year variability on the emission
intensity and feasibility of grid-connected hydrogen production in a fully decarbonising
European energy system. In this context, the term weather year refers to one of 42 distinct
annual production profiles derived from the CorRES dataset, which simulates hourly wind
and solar generation based on simulated installed capacities from 1980 to 2021. These
profiles determine the temporal distribution of renewable energy output, while holding
installed capacities of generation, transmission and storage fixed at 2012 levels. No new
technologies—aside from backup electricity and heat generators—are introduced in the
model, meaning all comparisons reflect differences in system performance relative to the
temporal generation pattern of the baseline year performance relative to the temporal
generation pattern of the baseline year 2012.
The overarching objective was to answer the question: What is the impact of weather-
years on the emission intensity of grid-produced hydrogen? The results show that
deviations in hourly renewable availability—driven by weather variability—can lead to sub-
stantial differences in both the volume and emission intensity of hydrogen output, particu-
larly when renewable generation is misaligned with demand or system flexibility is limited.
Using a consistent modelling framework across 41 individual weather-year scenarios, the
analysis evaluated how these temporal shifts in renewable generation affect electricity
and hydrogen production, system-wide emissions, and compliance with renewable fuel
certification criteria such as the RFNBO threshold. In doing so, the thesis addressed four
key research questions:
First, how much do hydrogen production emissions change from year to year as
wind and solar profiles vary? Results show that variations in renewable production
timing substantially influence both the volume and emission intensity of hydrogen output,
particularly when renewable availability is misaligned with demand or flexibility is limited.
In weather years such as 1990, reduced hydrogen output and increased backup reliance
stem from fewer hours of production and a temporal mismatch between renewable gen-
eration and system needs, relative to the 2012 baseline. Conversely, weather years like
2011 demonstrate greater alignment with electricity demand and hydrogen production re-
quirements, enabling the highest hydrogen output across all scenarios. However, even
such cases do not always correspond to the lowest emissions, revealing the complexity
of weather-induced variability.
Second, the study examined whether it is possible to produce RFNBO-compliant hy-
drogen using grid electricity under varying weather years. While most scenarios
achieve the 90% renewable electricity threshold by 2050, early years and weather profiles
with unfavourable temporal alignment often fall short. This suggests that the feasibility of
RFNBO compliance is sensitive not only to overall renewable penetration but also to its
hourly distribution. Certification mechanisms that rely on annual averages risk misclas-
sifying hydrogen with hidden fossil contributions, especially in years with poor alignment
between renewable output and hydrogen demand.
Third, the analysis assessed how reliance on fossil-fuel backup—both for electricity
and heat—varies across weather years, and what this means for achieving net-
zero targets. Backup-related emissions persist in nearly all scenarios, particularly from
heat, even when hydrogen production is fully decarbonised. Scenario 1996, for instance,

==PAGE:57==
exhibits the highest electricity-related backup emissions (14.6 MtCO￿), while 1990 has
the highest total backup emissions. Only 1980 eliminates electricity backup emissions
entirely by 2050. These results underscore that decarbonising hydrogen alone is insuf-
ficient; fossil-based backup remains a structural constraint, particularly for heating and
other hard-to-abate sectors.
Fourth, the thesis explored to what extent grid-connected hydrogen production can
meet long-term emissions reduction targets under varying policy scenarios and
demand pathways. The modelling suggests that while a net-zero hydrogen system is
technically feasible by 2050—even under historical weather variability—only one sce-
nario (weather year 2000) consistently achieves complete decarbonisation of both hydro-
gen and electricity. This indicates that robust system design, including high renewable
penetration, strong temporal alignment, sufficient storage, and clean backup capacity, is
essential for resilient and reliable decarbonisation across all conditions.
In conclusion, this thesis finds that weather-year variability plays a decisive role in shaping
the sustainability of grid-connected hydrogen. While it does not prevent full decarbonisa-
tion, it strongly influences the conditions under which it is achieved. Policy and certification
schemes must therefore incorporate temporal dynamics and system integration require-
ments to ensure that hydrogen’s climate benefits are realised under real-world variability.
Achieving net-zero hydrogen requires not only ambitious renewable deployment, but also
careful coordination of flexibility measures, system-wide design, and regulatory frame-
works that reflect the complexity of weather-dependent energy systems.

U.0.0.1
Denmark
As an EU member state, Denmark has long pursued climate action. Since the 1990s,
it has introduced policies and established agencies to reduce GHG emissions. With the
2019 Climate Law, Denmark committed to a 70% GHG reduction by 2030 (compared to
1990) and aims to reach carbon neutrality by 2045, becoming carbon negative by 2050.
To meet these goals, Denmark annually updates its technology catalogues and relies on
oversight from the Danish Energy Agency (DEA), which tracks progress toward a net-zero
future.
According to national projections [26], 73.8% of energy consumption in 2030 is expected
to come from renewables, with electricity production surpassing 100% of domestic de-
mand. Fossil-based heat is set to be electrified, and biogas is expected to fully cover
national gas needs.
Key targets include:
• Quadrupling solar and onshore wind capacity by 2030.
• Offshore wind: 5.3 GW in the North Sea and 7.9 GW in the Baltic Sea by 2030;
expected to provide 49% of electricity by 2031.
• Electrolysis capacity: 4-6 GW for green hydrogen by 2030.
• Bioenergy’s share is projected to drop from 19.5% (2024) to 2.8% by 2040, replaced
mainly by wind and solar. [27]
By 2050, Denmark is expected to become a net energy exporter, driven by its high re-
newable share.
U.0.0.2
France
France aims to become carbon neutral by 2050, a commitment outlined in the Energy and
Climate Law of 2019 and its Stratégie Nationale Bas-Carbone (SNBC). The SNBC sets
carbon budgets and two five-year investment plans, targeting a 27% reduction in GHG
emissions by 2023 and 40% by 2028, relative to 1990 levels.
While nuclear energy remains a cornerstone of the energy mix, France is diversifying
its system by scaling up solar and wind [28]. The expected rise in variable renewables
introduces a need for flexible energy storage solutions, such as batteries and hydrogen,
and may also require energy imports to maintain grid balance.
France is also positioning itself as a hydrogen producer and transit hub.
In 2020, it
launched a national hydrogen strategy with three goals:
• Deploying 6.5 GW of electrolysis capacity by 2030,
• Producing 680,000 tonnes of renewable and low-carbon hydrogen annually,
• Establishing a 500 km hydrogen pipeline network [29].
This plan was updated in April 2025, with revised targets of 4.5 GW by 2030 and 8 GW by
2035. Due to its stable electricity production and strategic location, France is expected to
profitably produce hydrogen and facilitate cross-border transport of imported hydrogen,
playing a pivotal role in the EHB initiative [11].

Norway
Norway’s national goal, enshrined in the Climate Change Act of 2017, is to become a
low-emission society by 2050, corresponding to a 90–95% reduction in GHG emissions
compared to 1990. Under the Paris Agreement, Norway commits to at least a 50–55%

The 2021–2030 Climate Action Plan focuses on sectors outside the EU Emissions Trad-

Currently, Norway’s domestic energy production is dominated by hydropower, oil, and

U.1
Large-Scale Energy Storage Solutions (elaborated)
This section presents potential large-scale energy storage solutions as projected for the
years 2030 and 2050.
Currently, available energy storage technologies utilise resources such as water, electric-
ity, heat, and gas. The primary storage method within the EU is pumped hydro storage
[32]. These technologies are used to balance electricity grids during periods of high de-
mand and low supply. When energy production exceeds demand, renewable energy
generation may be curtailed to stabilise the system; this curtailment emphases the need
for improved storage solutions to maximise the utilisation of renewable energy at all times.
Several emerging options exist for grid balancing, including batteries for short-term bal-
ancing and hydrogen for long-term energy storage.
Achieving net-zero or negative carbon dioxide emissions in energy production necessi-
tates phasing out storage solutions that rely on fossil fuels. This transition can be achieved
through various strategies, such as replacing natural gas storage with biogas or synthetic
fuels like hydrogen or methanol. Gas storage poses complexities because conversion
processes and idle losses vary depending on the type of gas used. Another alternative
is phase conversion of gases, which also incurs energy losses. For simplicity, this thesis
considers hydrogen exclusively in its gaseous form.

81
==PAGE:88==
Projections by the DEA [27, 26] suggest that the role of storage technologies will evolve
between 2030 and 2050. In 2030, the anticipated storage technologies include batteries,
hydrogen, gas caverns, pumped hydro facilities, and CCS/CCUS. By 2050, this portfolio
expands to include synthetic fuels, green gas caverns, and potentially other emerging
technologies, contingent on technological development, cost factors, political incentives,
and energy market dynamics.
The following subsections provide an in-depth analysis of hydrogen, lithium-ion batter-
ies, and sodium-ion batteries as storage solutions. These technologies address energy
regulation over long and short time scales, respectively.
U.1.1
Large scale storage of hydrogen
Storing energy in gaseous form offers the advantage of long-term storage, a benefit that
applies particularly to hydrogen produced from surplus renewable electricity. As noted
by the IEA, hydrogen is an emerging energy carrier with significant potential for seasonal
storage in systems with high shares of Variable Renewable Energy (VRE) [32].
Hydrogen production can be flexibly aligned with periods of electricity surplus and later
reconverted to electricity or utilised in hard-to-abate sectors such as industry and transport
during times of energy scarcity.
The EHB report identifies pipeline transmission as the most cost-effective option com-
pared to transport via shipping or trucking [11]. While hydrogen pipelines are seen as
crucial for an integrated and cost-efficient hydrogen economy, they also present specific
technical challenges due to the unique properties of hydrogen [27].
One significant issue is hydrogen embrittlement, where prolonged exposure to hydrogen
weakens pipeline materials, increasing the risk of cracks and failure [11]. Another concern
is hydrogen permeation, where hydrogen molecules diffuse through pipeline materials,
leading to pressure loss and potential leakage [11, 33]. Moreover, hydrogen is more
prone to leakage than natural gas, raising safety and efficiency concerns [11, 34].
U.1.2
Large-Scale Battery Storage Systems
Currently, the most widespread large-scale energy storage technology globally—and within
the EU—is pumped hydro storage. This method is suitable for intra-seasonal storage but
requires specific geographical features, such as elevation differences and proximity to
water reservoirs, as well as sufficient precipitation levels.
Grid-scale battery storage is emerging as a viable short-term alternative, with fewer spa-
tial constraints and lower operational emissions. Batteries offer additional grid services
such as peak shaving, frequency regulation, congestion relief, and black start capabilities.
However, due to self-discharge and relatively limited energy capacity, batteries are not
yet suitable for long-term energy storage.
By the end of 2022, global grid-scale battery storage capacity approached 28 GW. Ac-

This thesis examines three battery technologies with potential for large-scale energy stor-

Lithium-ion batteries are the most widely deployed, offering high energy density and tech-
nological maturity. However, they are costly and depend on scarce materials like lithium
and cobalt.

Explanation of JRC demand projection
The JRC’s projected hydrogen demand is expressed in mass-based units, specifically
kilotonnes of hydrogen per year (kt H2/year), whereas the EHB data are presented in
energy units (TWh/year). To enable a consistent comparison between the two datasets,
the JRC values are converted into energy units using the lower heating value (LHV) of
hydrogen. A standard LHV of 120 MJ/kg, equivalent to 33.33 kWh/kg, is applied for this
conversion [66]. The resulting figures are subsequently converted from kWh to TWh. As
an illustrative example, Belgium’s hydrogen demand is used to demonstrate the conver-
sion method in Equation (W.1).
