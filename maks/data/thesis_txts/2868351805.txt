Abstract
A cyber range is an environment shaped by a learning management system with a pur-
pose that sets requirements for its capabilities, functionality, and technology. This thesis
aims to propose a new taxonomy that supports recent developments in the growing re-
search papers within the field of cyber ranges. Additionally, this thesis aims to create a
taxonomy toolkit to enable the researcher community to dynamically extend the proposed
taxonomy or build their own cyber range taxonomy. The data for this thesis were collected
from online research databases and systematically verified, analyzed, and synthesized.
A baseline taxonomy (base taxonomy) is developed from a systematic literature review of
all existing taxonomies and systematic literature reviews on cyber ranges. From the base
taxonomy, another systematic literature review process supported the finding towards
an updated taxonomy (a refined taxonomy), highlighting, among others, where existing
research papers converge and diverge. Recent developments and research papers on
cyber range have influenced how we understand cyber ranges and, to that extension, the
classification of objects of interest into an updated taxonomy (a proposed taxonomy). With
the reasoning that the requirements for a technology dimension should also be included
as part of a cyber range taxonomy, the thesis proposes a novel cyber range taxonomy.
These findings indicate the need for resources to create new and refined cyber range
taxonomies dynamically. The proposed cyber range taxonomy contributes to the broader
body of the cyber range field and creates a contemporary taxonomy for cyber ranges.
Keywords: Cyber Security, Cybersecurity, Cyber Range, Taxonomy, Systematic Review,
Technology, Architecture, Infrastructure.

Introduction
Cyber range platforms have emerged as a solution for diverse audiences, offering a multi-
faceted perspective on enhancing the overall state of cybersecurity [1], [2], [3], [4]. Cyber
range platforms have garnered interest from the scientific and private sectors due to their
broad scope. Cyber ranges offer various capabilities and functionalities, including se-
curity research, competence and awareness building, and education. In broad terms, a
cyber range’s role is multifold. First, they can be utilized to raise awareness and train and
educate employees within an organization about cybersecurity best practices. Second,
they act as platforms for identifying vulnerabilities and developing appropriate security
measures to protect the systems and technology under review. Thirdly, they are a hands-
on experience with educational cybersecurity courses that can contain both physical and
virtual components and can be used to represent realistic scenarios for training [5], [6].
Yamin et al. [7] in 2019 conducted a systematic literature review (SLR) focusing on cyber
ranges (and security testbeds). The analysis emphasized architectures and scenarios,
encompassing capabilities like roles, tools, and evaluation criteria, producing a taxon-
omy of the most common cyber range capabilities and functionalities. Similarly, Ukwandu
et al. [8] in 2020 assessed existing cyber ranges and security testbeds based on their
type, employed technologies, threat scenarios, application domains, and training scope.
They also introduced a classification highlighting the key aspects of the identified cyber
ranges and security testbeds, aiming to reduce differentiation across diverse application
domains. Chouliaras et al. [5] in 2021 presents the current state of cyber ranges (and
testbeds within cybersecurity). Using a structured questionnaire, they conducted a sys-
tematic survey of 10 large-scale cyber range platforms. Specifically, they analyzed key
cyber range components, focusing on the tools used for designing, creating, implement-
ing, and operating a cyber range platform. One of the key observations was the variability
in implementation approaches and the operational environments of cyber ranges, which
include physical, virtual, or hybrid tools and equipment, as related to capabilities, func-
tionality, and technology. Lately Kampourakis et al. [4] in November 2024, proposed a
fine-grained reference architecture for cyber ranges, attempting to unify and standardize
cyber range platforms. Other researchers have, with different levels of abstraction and
conformance, adventured into the domain of classifying and organizing cyber range plat-
forms (see Chapter 3 and 5). This dissertation aims to contribute to the growing body of
research literature on cyber ranges by categorizing cyber ranges in a taxonomy, focusing
on proposing a new taxonomy for cyber ranges.
1.1
Background
In a report from January 2006, [9], the Center for Technology and National Security Policy
(CTNSP) at the National Defense University (NDU) – a military institution funded by the
United States Department of Defense (DoD) – the IT activities conducted at the center
and the university on behalf of the defense department are documented [10]. The report
includes recommendations for improving the rapid integration of commercial IT systems
into the defense departments’ IT environments. To address the challenges that hindered
the defense department’s ability to leverage IT capabilities developed outside the tradi-
tional defense acquisition process, the report suggested a six-step approach, where one
of the steps is explicitly to review the testing processes and the need for a cyber range:

”It is important that DoD go beyond performance testing to evaluating the im-
pact of potential commercial IT products on mission effectiveness. To do so,
consideration should be given to expanding existing operational testbeds. As
one step, a review should be conducted on the value of expanding the Ser-
vice battle labs to play this role. In addition, NORTHCOM1 has discussed
the creation of a ”cyber-range” in which candidate commercial IT products
could be evaluated to assess their potential impact on homeland defense and
homeland security effectiveness. Consistent with NORTHCOM’s interest in a
cyber-range, JFCOM2 is in the process of implementing an Information Oper-
ations (IO) Range. The initial version of this capability is to be achieved in the
summer of 2006 by integrating ten existing ranges” (p. 19)
In a brief publication from May 2008 [11], the U.S. Defense Advanced Research Projects
Agency (DARPA) announced the creation of a National Cyber Range (DARPA, 2008).
Later that year, in the fall of 2008, the University of Victoria applied to the Canadian Foun-
dation for Innovation to establish a Canadian At-Scale Emulation Laboratory (CASElab)
[12].
In a booklet titled ”Information Operations Primer” by the U.S. Army War College from Oc-
tober 2008 [13], the section addressing the Army Reserve Information Operations Com-
mand (ARIOC), that conducts Computer Network Operations (CNO) and Information Op-
erations (IO) to support the Army and Joint Warfighting operations, one of the main tasks
are to:
”Operates the secure, stand-alone ARIOC Cyber range. This network is used
for CNO analysis, doctrine development, exercise support, training, certifica-
tion and validation of cyber warrior skill sets. This network facilitates ARIOC
participation in Joint level exercises with the JFCOM Joint 10 Range” (p. 137)
In May 2009, DARPA presented a comprehensive overview of its activities to Congress,
the research community, and various components of the defense department [14]. The
strategy outlined DARPA’s mission, business processes, research priorities, objectives,
and the specific research projects designed to achieve those objectives. DARPA made a
”short list” of ongoing research that promises significant military benefits, ”future icons,”
one of these was a cyber range:
”The National Cyber Range will provide an environment for realistic, qualitative
and quantitative assessment of potentially revolutionary cyber research and
development technologies” (p. 10)
The purpose of the cyber range was to test how systems come together, testbeds for
rigorous experiments and validation, allowing realistic, quantifiable tests and assessments
of cybersecurity scenarios and technology. The range contained thousands of real and
virtual nodes, managed by resource configuration and management suites, to provide
realistic, tailored simulations of large-scale military and government networks. The large-
scale cyber testing was intended to spur progress in making military networks more secure
and reliable in the face of a wide range of cyber challenges.

DARPA operationally developed the National Cyber Range from 2009 to 2012. It tran-
sitioned from DARPA to the defense departments Test Resources Management Center
(TRMC) in October 2012. TRMC was charged with ”operationalizing” the capabilities for
testers, training, and cybersecurity experimentation. By November 2015, they summa-
rized four main points on why to use cyber ranges:
1. Test requirements that cannot be performed on operational networks due to potential
catastrophic risks (e.g., executing malicious threats or releasing self-propagating
malware).
2. Need for testing advanced cyberspace tactics in isolated, complex network environ-
ments (e.g., Internet movement).
3. Requirement to rapidly and realistically simulate operational environments at various
security, fidelity, and scale levels (e.g., Blue, Red, and Gray networks).
4. Need for precise control of the test environment for rapid reconstitution, reconfigu-
ration, and repetition of complex test cases, including quick variation of conditions
for scenario evaluation.
These environments are most commonly referred to as cyber ranges, although other
terms such as testbeds or digital training platforms are also frequently used. Davis and
Magrath [15] conducted a survey of cyber ranges and testbeds in 2013 and found labels
such as attack lab, computer network operations (CNO) testbed, testbed for network war-
fare/cyber war. In the years to come, the term cyber range emerged as a distinct research
field within cybersecurity, setting itself apart from the more traditional testbeds used in IT
security. Some researchers agreed to a cyber range consisting of the four points men-
tioned above, and others diverge by elaborating and extending on these points. With the
growing body of literature on cyber ranges, the need for their categorization and classifi-
cation became apparent. In 2019, the first taxonomy of cyber ranges was published by
Yamin et al. [7], serving as evidence of this development. Since then, researchers have
proposed various methods and frameworks for categorizing and visualizing cyber ranges
(cf. Chapter 3 and 5).

Methodology
The overall research methodology adopted in this dissertation is the Preferred Reporting
Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) framework [16], as updated
in 2020. The framework provides an approach to systematic reviews that allows the reader
to assess the applicability and trustworthiness of the review findings. The methodology
was considered suitable because of its relatively rigorous and detailed process. It can
support a mixed-method systematic review (including both quantitative and qualitative
studies) and address the presentation and synthesis of qualitative data. The framework
consists of multiple phases, including a checklist with items segmented into different sec-
tions. Compared with other frameworks and methods that were considered, such as A
Guide to Conducting a Standalone Systematic Literature Review by Okoli, C. (2015) [17],
we found the PRISMA framework more rigorous and aligned with the research approach
described below.
The PRISMA framework follows a step-by-step approach. It consists of key steps, and
each step is followed with subsequent steps which are detailed in a checklist of a total of
27 items1. The framework follows the following main principles:
1. Developing a research question and protocol: Create clear research questions and
a protocol that outlines the goals, methods, and criteria for the review. This keeps
the review consistent and transparent.
2. Finding and selecting studies: Systematically searching for studies and applying
criteria to decide which ones to include. This ensures a complete and unbiased
review.
3. Collecting and analyzing data: Gather key data from the chosen studies and analyze
it. This includes checking for and reporting any biases, using tables and graphs to
show results, and examining differences between studies to make the findings more
understandable and reliable.
The checklist details each step, explains the ”whys” and ”hows,” and provides examples to
support understanding each step’s context. The structure of the method items contained
in this chapter, centered around the PRISMA framework, consistent with the following:
(1) eligibility criteria, (2) information sources, (3) search strategy, (4) selection process,
(5) data collection process, (6) data items, (7) study risk of bias assessment, (8) effect
measures, (9) synthesis methods, (10) reporting bias assessment and (11) certainty as-
sessment.
Items (1) to (3) cover the Identification phase, items (4) to (9) cover the Screening and In-
cluded phases, and the remaining concludes on the framework. To further strengthen the
literature reviews framework, a systematic snowballing procedure was adopted from the
Guidelines for Snowballing in Systematic Literature Studies and a Replication in Software
Engineering [18].
A huge part of our contribution is understanding, analyzing, and developing a taxonomy,
so a structured approach has been adapted to taxonomy development. To support our

2.4
Selection Process
The previous three sections constitute the initial search process, e.g., the Identification
phase. From this section onward, the Screening and the Included phases are covered.
The selection process is a multi-stage process in which potentially eligible studies are
first identified from screening titles and abstracts, then assessed through full-text review,
and, where necessary, an investigation is conducted to draw a decision on the inclusion
or exclusion of the record in the final set of records.
Step 1
All three team members reviewed titles and abstracts of the first 15 records (out of 217
records) and discussed inconsistencies until consensus was obtained. Then in pairs of
two, the team members screened titles and abstracts of all the remaining records, includ-
ing a keyword search on ”cyber range,” ”taxonomy,” ”review” and ”survey” to grasp the
record in context. In case of any disagreement, the records full-text was screened by
both team members and discussed. If necessary, the third team member was consulted
to make the final decision on including or excluding the record in the poll of papers.
No automation tools were integrated within the overall study selection process in this step.
Step 2 + 3
All three members jointly reviewed the full text of a sample of 35 random records (out of
234). All keywords that directly or indirectly – in a specific or generalized form – related
to ”cyber range,” ”technology,” ”architecture” and ”infrastructure,” were systematically col-
lected. The team members discussed the final collection of keywords and finalized the list.

2.8
Reporting Bias Assessment
Part of our bias assessment is assessing whether the studies conduct a systematic liter-
ature review (SLR). If an SLR is conducted, the risk for bias is low, but if an SLR is not
conducted, the risk of bias is moderate.
The included studies vary in purpose, with several focusing on architecture (e.g., Ex-
ploring the Architectural Composition of Cyber Ranges) and training (e.g., Cybersecurity
Training Acceptance). Most utilized systematic literature reviews, contributing to a lower
risk of overall bias. Three studies can be categorized as having a moderate level of bias
because they did not conduct an SLR. These three studies are ”Enabling Next-Generation
Cyber Ranges” with ”Mobile Security Components,” ”Cyber Taxi: A Taxonomy of Interac-
tive Cyber Training and Education Systems,” and ”Modeling and Executing Cyber Security
Exercise Scenarios in Cyber Ranges.”
Cyber range platforms are in the early literature mentioned with other labels and names,
mainly because the phrase cyber range was not yet coined (see Section 1.1). However,
since around 2015 and onward, there has been a gradual shift to more rigorously view
cyber range as a separate domain compared to, e.g., IT security test-bed. Additionally, a
shift is identified with the separation of test-bed and cyber ranges.
On the bias assessment, it is worth mentioning that even though we have not included the
test bed for earlier papers, the identified cyber range taxonomies and systematic literature
reviews conducted between 2019 and 2024 have covered this, which is included in this
research and thereby extends the coverage.

Related Work
To identify previous and map current research papers on cyber ranges, the study group
will conduct a systematic literature review focusing on papers that have either developed
a taxonomy or performed a systematic literature review on cyber ranges. The aim is to
create a baseline, understand, and acquire domain knowledge on how other researchers
have categorized cyber ranges and utilize the insights into findings to lay the foundation
for developing a baseline taxonomy.
This chapter is structured into two main sections. The first section will describe current
cyber range taxonomies and compare them to identify where they converge and diverge.
The second section explores current cyber range systematic literature reviews, highlight-
ing their findings and contributions to the field. Findings from both sections set the base-
line for the study groups Base Taxonomy (Chapter 4).
As outlined in the methodology (Chapter 2), the study group will use the online research
databases DTU Findit and Google Scholar to identify and collect relevant records. 216
records were identified in the two research databases (24 records from DTU Findit and
216 from Google Scholar). Of the 216 sets of records, 28 were removed as duplicates,
and another 18 were removed due to paywall access limitations. A total of 194 records
were screened, whereas 144 were excluded due to the exclusion criteria. The process
led to 50 records sought for retrieval and the same amount assessed for eligibility. Thirty-
three records were manually removed from this set of records as they did not include
any cyber range systematic literature review or cyber range taxonomy. One record was

The first cyber range taxonomy that was identified in the set of records is titled: ”Cyber

author’s final taxonomy has the following dimensions:
• Scenario describes the life-cycle of the scenario generated. It involves creating, gen-

• Monitoring the data or overall health of a cyber range through different methods,
dashboards, and tools.

Base Taxonomy
Taxonomies play an important role in research and management because the classifi-
cation of objects helps researchers and practitioners understand and analyze complex
domains [19]. Reducing complexity and identifying similarities and differences among ob-
jects are significant advantages provided by taxonomies [44]. Furthermore, taxonomies
enable researchers to study the relationships among objects and, therefore, to hypothe-
size about these relationships.
The study group used the inductive approach by observing empirical and factual cases,
such as currently developed taxonomies, which are then analyzed and studied to de-
termine dimensions and characteristics in our proposed taxonomy. The analysis uses
statistical techniques such as including all the dimensions and characteristics agreed on
by the experts and then evaluating the ones that differ from the deductive approach. The
second approach that was used was the deductive approach, which derives a taxonomy
not from empirical cases but instead from theory or conceptualization. It identifies di-
mensions and characteristics in the taxonomy by a logical process derived from a sound
conceptual or theoretical foundation that the experts or our supervisor describes. This
approach evaluated and modified the taxonomy found during the first step.
As part of the quality assurance, we chose two restrictions [19], [44]. The mutual exclu-
sive restriction means that no object can have two different characteristics in a dimension.
The second restriction is the collectively exhaustive restriction, which means each object
must have one of the characteristics in a dimension. The first principle we address is
that the taxonomy we propose is concise. Our taxonomy shall be designed to contain
a limited number of dimensions and characteristics in each dimension. We understand
that an extensive classification scheme with too many dimensions and characteristics can
exceed our cognitive load as researchers, making it difficult to understand and apply ef-
fectively [19]. The second principle is that our taxonomy should be robust. To ensure
its usefulness, we have included enough dimensions and characteristics to differentiate
the objects of interest, and all the work done by other experts is covered by one taxon-
omy. We recognize that a taxonomy with too few dimensions and characteristics may fail
to distinguish between objects. For example, a framework with only one dimension and
two characteristics within that dimension would not serve its intended purpose effectively.
The third principle that we discussed is that taxonomy should be comprehensive. We
have taken into consideration two interpretations of this attribute. One interpretation is
that our taxonomy should be capable of classifying all known objects within the domain
under consideration, aligning with [45] notion of completeness for empirically developed
taxonomies. The second interpretation emphasizes including all dimensions relevant to
the objects of interest, as implied by [45], who state that ”typologies must provide com-
plete descriptions of each ideal type using the same set of dimensions.” Conceptually
developed taxonomies should reflect this attribute.
The taxonomy design process is designed to allow for the inclusion of additional dimen-
sions and new characteristics within a dimension as new types of objects emerge, ”A non-
extensible taxonomy risks becoming obsolete, while ours is intended to remain dynamic
and responsive to change, as noted by Bailey” (p. 32) [44]. The new proposed taxon-
omy includes dimensions and characteristics that do more than describe every possible
detail of the objects. Instead, they provide helpful explanations about the nature of the
objects under study and any future objects, helping us—and others—better understand

them. Bailey [44] highlights that descriptive typologies (taxonomies), without explana-
tory power, are inherently weak. Our approach allows for identifying where an object fits
within the taxonomy or understanding its characteristics based on its placement without
requiring complete knowledge of every detail [19].
The approach adopted in this dissertation is to use Bailey [46] three-level indicator model
in which we combine both the empirical to deductive approach and the deductive to em-

of the taxonomy. The results of the systematic literature reviews led the study group to

The Environment dimension contains the components that enable the scenario execution.
This dimension consists of the meta-characteristic, Type, and the main characteristic, Gen-
eration. Under Type, we have added Simulation, Emulation, Hybrid, Physical, Hardware, and
Federation. The tables 4.6, 4.7, and 4.8 summarize the number of studies that reference
the Environment dimension and its characteristics. We have identified three types of things
that you can generate in a scenario. The types are User, Traﬀic, and Attack. Those three
types are described in detail in many papers. Some of the reviewed literature only con-
cerns generating traffic, making the scenario more realistic. Some of these papers are
[61], [62], and [63]. The issue of realistic traffic generation is treated in [61]. Here, the
researchers try to emulate the traffic in an organization for the scenario to be more realis-
tic. The traffic is not the concrete attack that the environment is exposed to, but relatively
normal traffic that generally occurs within an organization. Such traffic can, for example,
be emulating human internet requests, FTP requests, and normal background network
noise. In [64], the researchers go through attacks like Stuxnet, Havex, Industroyer, and
Hatman, which they want to emulate in their cyber range. Others like [65] look at re-
producing DDoS attacks, MITM Attacks, and Replay attacks. The attack generation is a
central component of a cyber range because you, as a participant, need to understand
how to avoid cyber attacks. The Generation in the Environment dimension is more about
the tools that enable the scenario than the generation of events itself. This characteristic
supports the Generation characteristic in the management dimension.
The literature reviewed also talks about emulating users in a cyber range. Some of these
literature are [66], [67], [68]. In [66], they discuss a white area in their system where noise
and user traffic are generated. The users are simulated traffic accessing the web server
like normal users would.
As described earlier, the Environment of a cyber range can be split into several types.
Our taxonomy includes Simulation, Emulation, Hybrid, Physical, Hardware, and Federation.
In a simulated environment, you try to recreate a simple version of the environment you
want to test on. In contrast, in an emulated environment, you will try to create a replica

those studies are [67], [70] and [71]. The Hardware characteristic describes another type of

Our taxonomy’s Teaming dimension is a central component of a cyber range. It can broadly
be categorized into two different types of participants. The first one is the agents, which
are a part of the Environment and often programmed entities that simulate participants.
The other type is the actual participants in the cyber range. Those can be categorized
into many sub-types. For readability, we have included the most common ones (Red Team
and Blue Team) and added the others to a category called Others. In this section, we will
explain the roles of the red and blue teams in a scenario execution, and we will also
describe the other types we have found through the literature review. In the tables 4.9,
4.10 and 4.11 are the summarized findings from our literature review for the dimension
Teaming.
In the literature, the different teams are split into different colors, and each color has a
specific role it has to maintain. The different teams and their purpose is listed below:
• Red Team: The red team is the attacking team. It tries to find vulnerabilities in the
system. They are often referred to as the ”hackers”. The red team can either be
played by a user or injected directly into the cyber range[74][7][8].
• Blue Team: The blue team is responsible for defending the environment against
the attackers. It is often the blue team that is the group that is trained in the cyber
range[74][7][8].
• Yellow team: The yellow team is there to improve realism. They are often simulated,
but they can also be played by actual participants[74][7][8].
• White Team: The white team prepares the exercise and observes and leads it[74][7][8].
• Green Team: The green team is the team that maintains the networks efficiency[8].
• Yellow Team: The motivating team during the exercises[8].
• Purple Team: The purple team sets the defensive and offensive strategies during
the exercises[8].
We have identified two types of agents under the agent characteristic. The first is the
AI-based agent, and the second is the autonomous agent.
In [75], they talk about how to create a cyber range that AI supports. They write about
an element called an AI-based infrastructure orchestration controller that generates an
emulated environment according to how the scenario is played out. In the study, the
AI element has multiple usages. They split it up into ”AI-based scenario planner and
reasoner,” ”AI-based event controller,” and the aforementioned ”AI-based infrastructure
orchestration.” The AI-based scenario planner and reasoner do the ”before-action” review.
This AI system helps to plan and reason about the design of training scenarios. At the
same time, the AI-based event controller is responsible for executing the scenario and
keeping track of partial goals and achievements.
An example of an autonomous agent is mentioned in [76]. Here, they describe how Ad-
vanced Persistent Threats (APT) generators are a crucial component of a cyber range.
The study suggests a methodology for automating attacking agents using APT agents.
The technology they suggest is not based on AI but on a predesigned flow based on
known attack patterns.

Econometrics in cyber ranges focuses on understanding the financial impact of cyberse-
curity actions within simulated environments. By applying data-driven methods, econo-
metrics allows organizations to evaluate the costs and benefits of their security strategies.
Table 9.6 summarizes our findings throughout our literature review.
One important characteristic of Econometrics in cyber ranges is risk assessment, which
estimates the potential financial losses associated with various attack scenarios. These
losses include direct costs like system downtime, data breaches, and recovery expenses.
By quantifying these risks, organizations can better understand their exposure and prior-
itize vulnerabilities.
34
Cyber Range for Cyber Security
==PAGE:48==
Another key characteristic is the evaluation of threat forecasting, which uses historical
and simulated data to predict potential cyberattacks and their economic implications. This
helps organizations prepare for future threats by modeling attacks’ likelihood and asso-
ciated costs. Threat forecasting enables proactive planning, ensuring resources are di-
rected toward areas with the highest risk. [8]
The concept of choices and implications is central to Econometrics. Every cybersecurity
decision carries financial consequences, and econometrics helps organizations weigh
these trade-offs. For example, investing in advanced security tools may have high up-
front costs but reduce the long-term expenses of responding to incidents. Understanding
these implications allows organizations to balance short-term expenditures with long-term
savings [84].
Econometrics also examines the mitigation impact of different strategies. Simulations in
cyber ranges test the effectiveness of various cyber attack responses and measure their
financial outcomes. This provides insights into which mitigation efforts yield the best re-
sults, helping organizations implement cost-effective solutions while maintaining robust
defenses [84].
Finally, Econometrics supports the development of assessment models that evaluate the
overall economic health of an organization’s cybersecurity posture. These models in-
tegrate data from risk assessments, threat forecasts, mitigation strategies, and decision
analyses to provide a comprehensive view of an organization’s financial resilience against
cyber threats.
We have not been able to map much literature to the econometrics dimension. This can
be a result of our search strategy, which is focused on infrastructure, architecture, and
technology. We chose to include the Econometrics dimension because we believe it could
prove beneficial for future research.

The Monitoring dimension describes how the participants of a cyber range are monitored.
Among the literature that references this characteristic is [5]. Here, they state that a cyber
range must have the capability of monitoring, whether it is for exercise training, research,
or testing a system. Others that mention the monitoring component in a cyber range are
[85], [86] and [34].
One of the monitoring methods used is in [87]. Here, they describe their monitoring of
the participant in an exercise as ”monitoring CPU usage and file system/process/network
changes.” In [88], they talk about reporting mechanisms that generate reports for the blue
and red teams in order for the white team to evaluate the efforts made by the different
teams. The researchers in [89] take another approach in order to monitor the partici-
pants through an exercise. That has created an event-based system that collects data on
different events occurring through an exercise.
Many monitoring systems provide the user with a dashboard that displays important met-
rics for the participants. In [90], they constructed a Kibana dashboard that collects data
about the attacks and keeps scores for the trainees. In [91], they use the dashboard pro-
vided by OpenStack to give administrators control and help them monitor the activities in
the cyber range.
The Layer characteristic describes which layer the monitoring is happening in. A cyber
range consists of multiple layers, like the network, application, etc. In the case of a social
exercise, the layer can also mean the t social layer [7]. In [92], they have automated the
monitoring process with a SNART tool. Their monitoring is done by looking at the network
traffic during exercises; therefore, it is placed under the network layer. [85] uses Open-
Stack’s dashboard to monitor the network layer. In [86], they monitor their PLC systems
on a hardware level. Instead of splitting up all the layers there could be, we decided only
to include a layer characteristic that gives the reader an idea that the monitoring could
happen at several layers of an application/exercise.
Throughout our review, we found several tools that were used for monitoring purposes. As
mentioned above, OpenStack’s dashboard functionality was one of the tools mentioned
many times. Others used the ELK stack to monitor, and others created their monitoring
components. In [93], they have built their own monitoring component that collects data
from a so-called event chain. [94] suggests that monitoring and monitoring tools are a
must-have in a cyber.
To be able to get an overview of how the cyber range is performing, we have added a
characteristic called Performance. This characteristic performs the logging of the system
and checks the CPU usage, memory usage, etc. A big part of the literature mapped to
performance also includes writing about monitoring the participants’ performance. One
example is [95]. They have an API endpoint for getting the status of the environment and
endpoints for getting the participants’ performance. Therefore, the performance charac-
teristic is both on the user and system levels. Also, in [96], the performance of the trainees
is in focus. Here, the performance of the different trainees across different scenarios is
part of the ”trainee’s profile.”

Inputs, Path, Teams, and Users. As an observer of an exercise, you want to be able to
track the progress of the teams and users participating. You are interested in the paths
they take, their inputs, and their actions so that they can complete specific objectives.

The Management dimension of our taxonomy is one of the dimensions covered in the
better literature. A total of 68 references described the Management aspects of a cyber
range. The summary of our mappings can be seen in the tables 4.17, 4.18 and 4.19.
The Management dimension is mentioned in [102], [103], [104]. In the study [104], they
mention an interesting point when it comes to federated cyber ranges. A federated cyber
range is a composition of multiple cyber ranges, and therefore, the management of such
a cyber range can prove to be more complex because you, as a systems administrator,
have to manage multiple cyber ranges at once. For that, they suggest that the different
cyber ranges remote gateway in the federation has to have a centralized master federation
point that manages the whole federation platform environment.
Under the Management dimension, we have placed the characteristic Resource. The Re-
source characteristic can be split into two types of resources: Data Storage and Meta Data.
In [105], the researchers have a section called Resource Provisioning about how a user
can request data from a cyber range. The data can be information about users, node
availability system logs, etc. In [34], the researchers describe the management object as
being in the cantonment area in the cyber range. One of the capabilities described is the
management of resources, done by the administrator of a cyber range. As [7] describes,
the resource management module can also be used to manage the computational re-
sources in a cyber range like CPU power, etc.
The Data Storage characteristic describes the management of data like scenario models,
attack tools, exercise and experiment rules, and results[7]. In [106], they do a survey
of different cyber ranges, and in one of their architecture overview, they have added a
Training Database where training content can be retrieved. The cyber range they survey
is called CyTrONE. [34] also mentions that scenario Cantonment Data, Scenario Data, and
VM Data/Backups are added to the data storage of a cyber range.
The Metadata characteristic contains information about the scenario, like the difficulty of
an exercise, etc. It also contains information about the system configuration that makes
up the environment. It could provide information about resource allocation and equipment.
For example, in [107], they have a database containing Attack Descriptions. This data is
used to represent the attacks in the cyber range. In [101], they use the metadata to add
it to a packet’s header or payload to identify its source or path.
We have also added the Lifecycle management under the Management dimension. The
Lifecycle dimension covers the different phases of the scenario’s lifecycle, from creation to
recovery after the scenario has been executed. In [108], they state, ”The management of
a CR can be divided into two parts: CR management throughout the CR’s life cycle...”. In
[109], they don’t explicitly mention Lifecycle management but rather explain a scenario’s
lifecycle differently throughout their paper.
The Creation of a scenario involves replicating a corporate infrastructure, creating digital
twin[110]. This means defining and building infrastructure in a virtual environment that
contains the same resources, users, and network traffic as the setup you want to replicate.
Other papers that work with the creation of scenarios are [92], [111] and [112].
Editing has not been covered that well in the literature generally. One of the pieces of
literature that talks about editing is [113]. They have created an open-source cyber range
called Leaf. Each scenario can be started, stopped, deleted, and edited in their interface.

4.10
Discussion
The development of the base taxonomy provides several insights into the strengths of the
existing frameworks and the areas requiring refinement. This section critically evaluates
the decisions made during the development process and explores the implications of the
proposed taxonomy for cyber ranges. We have based our work on the data collected
through observations. From the specific observations in our Base Taxonomy, we identified
patterns and trends. Based on this, we developed a theory that guided our further work
on creating a Refined Taxonomy. To achieve this, we employed the inductive method in
taxonomy development, which included collaborating with a Subject Matter Expert in the
field of cyber ranges.
4.10.1
Strengths and Improvements
Through the systematic literature review process, we identified gaps in the existing tax-
onomies and addressed them in the proposed base taxonomy. One of the most significant
contributions of our taxonomy is its attempt to bridge the gap between capabilities and
underlying technology. Unlike existing taxonomies, which primarily focus on functionality
and capability aspects, our base taxonomy incorporates a dedicated Technology dimen-
sion to ensure a better understanding of the technological foundations. This addition was
motivated by the rapid advancements in areas like virtualization, machine learning, and
cloud-based environments, which were not adequately represented in previous frame-
works.
For example, Ukwandu et al. includes references to specific technologies, such as con-
nections (e.g., 5G, 4G, TCP/IP, and VPN), across multiple sections in his taxonomy. How-
ever, these mentions are scattered and not consolidated into a cohesive representation
of the technological dimension. Similarly, while Yamin et al. discusses the importance
of various technologies in enabling cyber ranges, he deliberately omits them from his
taxonomy, arguing that a technology-agnostic approach ensures relevance over time as
technology evolves.
Our taxonomy acknowledges the importance of adaptability but argues that omitting tech-
nology as a dedicated dimension leaves a critical gap. We introduced technological con-
cepts rather than specific tools or protocols to balance relevance and practicality. By ab-
stracting these technologies into concepts like virtualization, automation, scalability, and
integration, we aim to ensure that the taxonomy remains adaptable and comprehensive.
Furthermore, our taxonomy is designed to be concise, robust, and comprehensive; by
following the quality principles outlined in Bailey [46] and Nickerson et al. [19], we en-
sured that it is practical and flexible. The principles of mutual exclusivity and collective
exhaustiveness were applied to reduce ambiguity, ensuring that objects classified within
the taxonomy are clearly and accurately categorized. This approach enhances the tax-
onomy’s clarity and usability for researchers and practitioners.
The restructuring of specific dimensions, such as the integration of Orchestration into Man-
agement and Data Collection into Monitoring, demonstrates a deliberate effort to streamline
the taxonomy. These decisions were based on overlapping definitions and the need to
avoid redundancy, aligning to create a robust and logical classification system.
4.10.2
Limitations in Frameworks
From our analysis, it became evident that many of the reviewed taxonomies, includ-
ing those by Yamin et al. and Ukwandu et al., focus heavily on the capability of cyber
42
Cyber Range for Cyber Security
==PAGE:56==
ranges while neglecting the underlying technological requirements [48]. For instance,
Ukwandu et al. includes the Econometrics dimension to evaluate the financial implications
of cyber range operations but does not adequately address how these insights integrate
with real-time monitoring or scenario execution. Similarly, while Ukwandu references spe-
cific technologies like 5G, TCP/IP, and VPN in his descriptions, these are not organized
into a unified framework. Yamin takes an entirely different approach by excluding tech-
nology altogether, arguing that taxonomies should remain usable over time and not risk
obsolescence due to technological advancements.
This lack of attention to dynamic and evolving technologies limits the applicability of ex-
isting taxonomies to modern cyber ranges, which is more and more relying on innovative
technology such as artificial intelligence, automated machine learning, and advanced vir-
tualization. The Base Taxonomy (in)directly addresses this gap by integrating technolog-
ical concepts into its structure, ensuring that it reflects a state of a technology dimension.
Another limitation is the inconsistent treatment of objects like Scenario and Environment,
often described in broad terms without clear distinctions between their sub-objects. Our
taxonomy addresses this by introducing well-defined characteristics under each dimen-
sion, ensuring clarity and consistency in classification.
4.10.3
Challenges and Iterations
While the Base Taxonomy addresses the identified limitations, it is not without challenges.
One challenge is the reliance on existing literature, which may not fully capture emerging
trends and technologies as of 2025. To overcome this, the next step in our research will
involve validating this taxonomy based on literature published between 2019 and 2024.
This time frame is critical because the latest comprehensive taxonomy was developed
in 2020 and 2021, and the field of cyber ranges has evolved since then. By mapping
recent research publications to the dimensions and (meta-)characteristics of the Base
Taxonomy, we aim to determine whether the taxonomy aligns with the current state of
cyber ranges or if it diverges, and if so, to which categories and extent.
This validation process will also allow us to identify new dimensions or (meta-)characteristics
introduced into the domain over the past four years. For instance, new technological con-
cepts or operational strategies that were not addressed in prior taxonomies may have
emerged. If such objects are discovered, they will be integrated into the taxonomy to
ensure it remains comprehensive and reflective of the latest developments in the field.
The existing literature provides a structured framework for understanding and analyzing
cyber ranges, addressing key gaps in the Base Taxonomy. It allows researchers to study
the relationships between different dimensions and (meta-)characteristics. For practition-
ers, the taxonomy is a practical tool for designing, implementing, and evaluating cyber
ranges, ensuring they meet specific operational and training needs.
Including the Technology dimension ensures that the taxonomy remains adaptable to fu-
ture advancements, positioning it as a dynamic framework for a rapidly evolving field. By
addressing both theoretical and practical considerations, an revised base taxonomy has
the potential to serve as a valuable resource for advancing research and practice in the
domain of cyber ranges.

Many research papers address the architectural objects of a cyber range. We have identi-
fied that cyber ranges generally are built upon three different architectural models: Mono-
lithic, Microservice based, and Federation based. We discovered that the federation-based
cyber ranges are beginning to be widely used, and they are well covered in the literature.
In the study [221], they are both writing about microservice and monolithic architecture.
They write that monolithic architecture involves building an application as a single self-
contained unit.
In contrast, microservice architecture involves decomposing the application into small in-
dependent services that can be deployed and scaled independently. The pros of the
monolithic approach are that it is a simple architecture on a small scale. It makes it easier
to maintain and operate, but the cons are that the application becomes harder to scale.
The pros of building upon a microservice-based model are the flexibility and the scalability
the model gives. Other literature that mentions the architectural models of microservices
and monoliths, among others, are [158] and [229].

the Base Taxonomy chapter, we discussed the Federation characteristic as an environ-

cyber range. The federated cyber range has been a well-discussed topic in recent lit-

automates testing a designed scenario by utilizing a domain-specific language called Sce-

the designed scenario. Furthermore, the researcher uses an orchestration tool called
CRACK that simulates hardware and software components through the CRACK SDL,
which builds upon TOSCA. In [249], a general-purpose orchestration tool is used. The
researcher uses Kubernetes to orchestrate docker containers that contain the scenarios.

vulnerabilities, user scores, scenario meta-data, etc. [93] uses a Redis database that is a
high-performance NoSQL database to store all stateful information about a cyber range.
In [140], they save historical records in a database for analysis.

Virtualization is central to creating and managing cyber ranges. It provides the flexibility
to simulate real-world systems, networks, and threats in controlled environments. Cyber
ranges use virtualization to create dynamic and scalable cybersecurity training, testing,
and experimentation platforms.
Cloud Virtualization is an important technology in cyber ranges. It allows deploying virtual
machines, networks, and storage on demand [102]. Public cloud platforms, like AWS
or Azure, are popular for their ability to host large-scale simulations that mimic enterprise
environments [250][34]. They are particularly suited for organizations that need scalability
and cost efficiency. Private clouds are often chosen for scenarios requiring strict control
over data and infrastructure, such as military or critical infrastructure simulations [251].
Hybrid cloud setups combine the strengths of public and private clouds, offering flexibility
and ensuring that sensitive data stays within a secure environment while still leveraging
the scalability of public cloud resources [34].
Containerization has become a preferred choice for many cyber range operators. Con-
tainers are managed through platforms like Docker and Kubernetes, allowing lightweight
and efficient environments to be deployed and torn down quickly [84]. This makes con-
tainerization ideal for dynamic scenarios such as red team/blue team exercises or incident
response training [250]. Containers also enable precise isolation of environments, ensur-
ing that simulated attacks or system failures do not affect other parts of the cyber range
[186]. This approach reduces resource overhead compared to traditional virtual machines
and accelerates setup times for training modules and threat simulations.
On-premise Virtualization remains essential in environments where full control over infras-

mation or operating in highly regulated industries, prefer to host their cyber ranges on-site

Traditional Virtualization, using virtual machines, is an important aspect of many cyber

The ability to integrate software-as-a-service (SaaS) tools into cyber ranges has also grown
in importance.
SaaS-based platforms can provide preconfigured environments, auto-

without extensive IT resources [252], [253].

For instance, during an attack simulation, the Environment may need to scale quickly
to handle large traffic volumes. Cloud-based virtualization and containerization excel in
these scenarios, providing the flexibility and performance to replicate such attacks [115]
[1].
By using these virtualization methods, cyber ranges can create realistic environments

organizations to train cybersecurity professionals, test incident response processes, and
evaluate security tools in a safe and controlled setting. Virtualization ensures that cyber
ranges remain adaptable, scalable, and effective in preparing for evolving threats.

6.2
Discussion
The refinement process of the Refined Taxonomy has resulted in notable insights into
the evolving landscape of cyber ranges, especially regarding emerging trends and areas
requiring adjustments. This section critically evaluates these findings and discusses the
implications of the changes made to the taxonomy based on the latest literature.
Following a comprehensive review of the literature, we arrived at a taxonomy with a to-
tal of eight dimensions: Scenario, Environment, Teaming, Learning, Econometrics, Monitor-
ing, Management, and Technology. These dimensions encompass a broad spectrum of
characteristics, ensuring the taxonomy provides a holistic representation of cyber range
components and functionalities. Below, we discuss the key refinements introduced in this
iteration and the rationale behind them.

6.2.1
Purpose and Scenario
During this iteration, we merged the Type and Purpose characteristics into a single Pur-
pose meta-characteristic under the Scenario dimension. This decision was informed by the
realization that these concepts often overlap in practical applications and can be more ef-
fectively represented as a unified characteristic. The updated Purpose characteristic now
includes objectives such as Collaboration, Experimentation, Security Testing, and Aware-
ness. Combining these objects streamlined the taxonomy while preserving its descriptive
depth.
We also reclassified Static and Dynamic scenarios, removing them from the merged char-
acteristic and positioning them directly under the Scenario dimension. This adjustment
reflects their foundational role in shaping cyber range exercises and highlights the impor-
tance of scenario adaptability and structure in cyber range design.
6.2.2
Cyber Range Exercise Design (CXR)
Building on insights from the literature, we introduced Cyber Range Exercise Design as a
new characteristic under the Scenario dimension. This addition acknowledges the iterative
and collaborative processes of creating effective cyber range exercises. Within this char-
acteristic, we relocated the Lifecycle sub-characteristics, which encompasses Creation,
Editing, Deployment, and Execution. This reorganization aligns the taxonomy with how
these processes are referenced in the literature, reflecting the emphasis on structured
design workflows.
6.2.3
Environment
The Environment dimension was expanded with the inclusion of Cyber-Physical Systems
under the Type meta-characteristic. This addition addresses the growing significance of
hybrid environments integrating physical and digital components, such as industrial con-
trol systems and IoT devices. By incorporating this characteristic, the taxonomy better
represents the complexities and challenges of managing cyber-physical systems within
cyber ranges.
6.2.4
Monitoring
The Monitoring dimension was refined by introducing Network and Application character-
istics under the Layer meta-characteristic. The observation drove these additions that
much of the cyber-range monitoring activity focuses on these two layers. This refinement
provides a more granular view of monitoring practices and ensures that the taxonomy
accurately reflects current monitoring methodologies.
6.2.5
Management
The Management dimension was enhanced with the addition of Orchestration, a charac-
teristic widely referenced in the literature. Orchestration tools are critical in managing
and automating complex processes within cyber ranges. By including this characteristic,
the taxonomy acknowledges the importance of orchestration in ensuring seamless and
efficient operations.
6.2.6
Technology
The most significant refinement in this iteration was the enrichment of the Technology
dimension. This dimension was specifically created to address the gap in earlier tax-
onomies, which often overlooked the technological components of cyber ranges. Through
our literature review, we identified key technological objects critical to the design and op-
eration of cyber ranges.
• Connection The taxonomy now includes VPN, TCP/IP, and P2P characteristics un-
der the Connection characteristic. These technologies enable secure and efficient

and system-to-system interactions.
• Layer Under the Layer characteristic, we added Traditional Virtualization, Container-

– Traditional Virtualization focuses on hypervisors and virtual machines, creat-

suggests the need for further exploration in future studies.

By integrating these findings and adjustments, the refined taxonomy captures emerging

Its adaptability and comprehensiveness position it as a valuable tool for researchers and

Proposed Taxonomy
In this chapter, we would like to address the refinements to the refined taxonomy, address-
ing gaps and improving upon previous frameworks for cyber ranges. One key change
involves the emission of specific characteristics deemed redundant or overly general. For
example, the Operating System (OS) characteristic was removed because it is a funda-
mental software system feature and does not require explicit inclusion in the taxonomy.
Similarly, the Connection characteristic, covering technologies such as VPN and TCP/IP,
was omitted as these are standard across networked systems and not unique to cyber
ranges.
Another important adjustment is the restructuring of the Virtualization dimension to re-
flect better the variety of deployment models and technologies in modern cyber ranges.
This dimension now includes Cloud Virtualization (subdivided into public, private, and hy-
brid models), Containerization, and Traditional Virtualization (encompassing virtual ma-
chines and hypervisors). These refinements provide a more transparent and more prac-
tical framework for understanding virtualization within the context of cyber ranges.
We also consolidated all tools into a single meta-characteristic within the Technology di-
mension. This change eliminates overlap between orchestration, monitoring, and security
tools while highlighting their collective importance in cyber range operations. Addition-
ally, while the Architecture meta-characteristic was streamlined by removing monolithic
and microservices characteristics, the Federation characteristic was retained due to its in-
creasing relevance in enabling collaboration across distributed cyber range environments.
Orchestration tools were also moved from the Monitoring dimension to Technology, re-
flecting their broader role in managing cyber range workflows.
7.1
Updating the Framework
The proposed taxonomy builds on existing frameworks while introducing refinements that
address their limitations. One notable addition is the inclusion of Cyber Range Exercise
(CRX) Design as a characteristic. While previous taxonomies often lacked a focus on
the iterative and collaborative processes involved in designing exercises, this taxonomy
highlights their importance. CRX Design emphasizes key aspects such as event-chain
planning, resource configuration, and provisioning, which are increasingly recognized as
essential to effective cyber range operations.
Another area of novelty is the restructuring of the Virtualization dimension. By distinguish-
ing between cloud, container, and traditional virtualization models, the taxonomy provides
a more nuanced view that reflects the diversity of deployment strategies in cyber ranges.
The focus on Federation within the Architecture dimension further distinguishes this taxon-
omy by addressing the growing importance of distributed and collaborative environments
in cybersecurity training and testing.
Additionally, consolidating tools into a single meta-characteristic ensures the taxonomy is
both streamlined and targeted. These changes allow it to focus on dimensions and char-
acteristics specific to cyber ranges, avoiding unnecessary complexity while maintaining
comprehensiveness.

posed several significant challenges. We developed two specialized tools to address
these: the Taxonomy Mapping Tool (TMT) and the Automated Literature Analysis Tool
(ALAT). Both these tools were designed to streamline different aspects of the workflow,
from organizing and visualizing taxonomy structures to automating the identification of
relevant terms in large collections of documents.

1. Base Taxonomy (URL: https://tinyurl.com/4je4kkw8)
2. Refined Taxonomy (URL: https://tinyurl.com/5xxausrs)
3. Proposed Taxonomy (URL: https://tinyurl.com/nnmf6jjt)
The TMT provides an intuitive interface for managing taxonomy hierarchies and mapping
literature to specific elements. It enables collaborative editing, real-time updates, and
structured data exports, making it an essential resource for maintaining consistency and
transparency in taxonomy development.
The ALAT complements this by automating the labor-intensive task of identifying terms
and their frequencies across PDF documents. It generates structured Microsoft Excel
outputs to guide manual reviews, reducing the time and effort required to validate and
refine the taxonomy iteratively.

Taxonomy Mapping Tool (TMT)
TThroughout our mapping and literature review, we encountered several challenges.
Firstly, the study group wanted to be able to map literature to the different elements in
our developed taxonomy to verify the drafted taxonomy against the existing literature.
Secondly, we needed a way to gather and organize data about the various reviewed
categories. Thirdly, we required a shared platform where team members could collabo-
ratively map literature to a taxonomy consistently and standardized. Finally, we wanted
to open up the taxonomy mapping tool to the cyber range community, allowing others to
inspect our mapped literature and create customized versions of the taxonomy.
Initially, we experimented with creating a spreadsheet where the study group could map
the found literature to the developed taxonomy. However, we quickly realized that this
approach would be highly impractical. The taxonomies we wanted to create contained
over 70 elements, and the literature review included over 200 records. This would result
in an extensive and confusing spreadsheet that was difficult to work with and made it
challenging to extract structured data. The data set had too many dimensions to be effec-
tively visualized in a spreadsheet format, and the complexity of managing relationships
and hierarchies between taxonomy elements made the issue even more complex.

During the iterative taxonomy development, the study group encountered instances where
changes required re-mapping the literature to ensure consistency. This process proved
to be both time-consuming and prone to oversight. The need for a tool in the toolkit
to streamline this process led to the creation of the Automated Literature Analysis Tool
(ALAT).
ALAT automates searching for relevant terms in a folder of PDF documents and outputs
the results in a structured Microsoft Excel file. While it does not replace manual validation,
ALAT is a guideline for highlighting relevant terms and linking them to specific documents.
ALAT greatly accelerates the manual review process, allowing researchers to focus on the
most relevant sections of each document.
ALAT was developed with three primary objectives:
1. Automating term identification: To streamline the process of identifying relevant
terms in an extensive collection of PDF documents based on a set of pre-defined
search terms.
2. Summarizing occurrences: To provide a structured and concise summary of the
frequency and distribution of these terms across all analyzed documents, enabling
better insights into their relevance.
3. Guiding manual review: To serve as a guideline for researchers by highlighting key
terms within each record, allowing for faster and more focused manual validation.

9.1
Base Taxonomy
The chapter presents the results of the work in developing a new proposed taxonomy for
cyber ranges. Based on the first iterations of our research, we established Yamin et al. [50]
and Ukwandu et al. [7] (Chapter 3). as the primary foundational taxonomies, which we
took as the baseline taxonomy (Chapter 4). Their contributions helped us establish a
baseline from which we could address any gaps left in previous work and new develop-
ments since these taxonomies were published.
As we progressed, we identified that existing taxonomies often leaned heavily on describ-
ing the capabilities or functionalities of cyber ranges while leaving the underlying technol-
ogy relatively underexplored (Chapter 5). These findings motivated us as a study group
to take a broader approach, merging insights from reviewed taxonomies and introducing
a Technology dimension to capture the technological foundations of cyber ranges (Chap-
ter 6). By iterating over a baseline and a refined taxonomy, we provided a structure that
could be iteratively enriched and validated (Chapter 7).
Our results show how the taxonomy evolved through these iterative steps. From the Base
Taxonomy, we moved to the Refined Taxonomy, addressing gaps and adding dimensions
with characteristics informed by a systematic literature review. Through additional map-
ping and validation processes, the study group contributed with a Proposed Taxonomy,
representing a cyber range taxonomy’s capability (functionality) and technology dimen-
sions.
This chapter is organized around the Base, Refined and Proposed Taxonomies to demon-
strate the contributions of each iteration, showing how each step built on the previous to
address specific research questions, and improve the previous iteration to the last iteration
(and research questions) with a verified proposed taxonomy for cyber ranges.
After conducting the first literature review, we combined the taxonomies found into one
Base Taxonomy. We tried to encompass all the taxonomies into one, but we relied heav-
ily on Yamin et al and Ukwandu et al because their taxonomies stood out to us at the
most comprehensive once. We used the taxonomy in [7] as the core and enriched it with
the others where we identified gaps, such as the econometrics dimension proposed by
[8]. The latest taxonomy we found through the SLR was from the year 2020 [26], and
since the field of Cyber Security and Cyber Ranges is developing rapidly, we decided to
conduct a new literature review and map it to the base taxonomy to verify it. We took
a broad approach to our search strategy, only limiting the findings to contain the key-
words ”Technology,” ”Architecture,” and ”Infrastructure.” This resulted in a set of records
that contained 206 papers and studies. We established a mapping protocol across the
team and started mapping the records to the dimensions and characteristics of the base
taxonomy. Throughout our first SLR, we noticed that the technological aspect of a cy-
ber range was missing or scattered in the current taxonomies, and therefore, we decided
to add a dimension called technology to the Base Taxonomy to be able to collect data
about records that cover the technology part of the cyber range. It is evident to us that
the technology aspect is a key driver in shaping the capabilities of a cyber range, and it
is important to include it in the base taxonomy. An example where the technology has

range” before it was widely used in research papers.
• Taxonomy versus Ontology The choice to develop a taxonomy rather than an
ontology was a deliberate one. Taxonomies offer a hierarchical structure that is
easier to understand and apply, making them more accessible to researchers and
practitioners. While ontologies provide richer relational detail, their complexity can
hinder practical implementation. By opting for a taxonomy, we ensured clarity and
usability, albeit at the cost of reduced depth in representing interrelations. However,
any taxonomy is either inductive (statistical or informal analysis), deductive (may be
followed by empirical verification), or intuitive in approach. This makes taxonomy
development a mixture of exact science and art.
• Technology dimension The decision to include a Technology dimension stemmed
from the realization that either existing taxonomies largely ignored the technical
foundations of cyber ranges or dealt with it spread over different dimensions and
(meta-)characteristics in a non-cohesive way.
Our search strategy emphasized
terms like Technology, Infrastructure, and Architecture to capture these elements.

Discussion
This research aimed to understand, define, scope, and illustrate what a cyber range is and
how it has evolved and to determine whether our work could meaningfully contribute to the
field. To achieve this, we aimed to provide an overview of the domain’s currently published
systematic reviews and taxonomies. Additionally, we sought to discuss, analyze, and
compare existing research on the conceptualization of cyber ranges from these studies.
Building on these efforts, the study group conducted our systematic literature reviews to
incorporate past and present findings. It verified and validated this research to develop a
new taxonomy for cyber ranges.
Through our extensive review of systematic literature reviews and existing taxonomies, it
became evident that much of the current work is heavily influenced by the foundational
efforts of Yamin et al. [7] and Ukwandu et al. [50]. While these taxonomies provide sub-
stantial insights into the capabilities of cyber ranges, they fall short of addressing the tech-
nological and infrastructural elements that underpin these capabilities. Recognizing this,
the study group undertook a systematic approach to developing a taxonomy that captures
both cyber ranges’ functional and technical dimensions. The taxonomy evolved through
three iterations, namely a Base Taxonomy, a Refined Taxonomy, and a Proposed Taxon-
omy. Each iteration built upon the insights gained from literature mapping and systematic
reviews, gradually moving toward a more robust, abstract, and adaptable taxonomy, i.e.,
the proposed taxonomy.
The newly added Technology dimension to the proposed taxonomy marks a shift in fu-
ture cyber range taxonomy development, where the capability and functionality aspects
of all other elements can be reflected or (in)directly linked to one or more elements in the
Technology dimension. The non-technological and Technology dimensions create a rela-
tionship that is interrelated to each other in specific ways. For example, there is a directed
association relationship between the technical AI-Based Agent characteristic and the non-
technical Teaming characteristic. Another example is a composition relationship between
the technical Virtualization characteristic and the non-technical Simulation characteristic.
In the proposed taxonomy, the characteristic Tools occurs in three separate instances,
that is, as the first characteristics under each of the following three dimensions: Scenario,
Monitoring, and Technology. From the interrelated relationship described above, the two
instances where it occurs as a capability or functionality (i.e., in the Scenario and Monitoring
dimensions) could be removed if you strictly wanted to keep the proposed taxonomy free
of any naming reputation that can create confusion (on this point, see Challenges and
Limitations section below).
The toolkit developed as part of the dissertation was partly driven by the study groups’
need to be agile, adaptable, and accurate during the literature mapping process and sup-
port a semi-automatic process as part of the protocol. As mentioned in the previous para-
graph, multiple iterations (and taxonomies) were created before we finalized it in one
proposed taxonomy. The need to iterate over a taxonomy multiple times supports the
idea that developing a cyber range taxonomy is dynamic and can change over time.
Challenges and Limitations
A known limitation of the proposed taxonomy is cyber ranges’ organizational and gov-
ernance dimensions. While this dissertation focuses on the technical and functional as-

pects, future research should also consider the organizational and legal environments
within the cyber ranges. The new European Directive (EU) 2022/2555 (known as NIS2)
and Directive (EU) 2022/2555 (known as the Critical Entities Resilience (CER)) are ex-
amples of these dimensions.1 The inferred compliance and governance characteristic on,
e.g., cyber hygiene and cyber awareness driven by these directives (and Member States
implementations in laws and executive orders) are expected to increase the adoption of
cyber ranges by small and medium-sized businesses and enterprises alike, within the
union’s critical sector and industries. Future work should investigate how the perspective
of these organizations impacts the technological dimensions and adapt the taxonomy to
reflect their needs and constraints.
During the second systematic literature review process, it became clear that it was quite
limited how much the Econometrics dimension was represented in the set of records. This
gap can likely be attributed to the search strategy and its heavy focus on Technology, In-
frastructure, and Architecture. Expanding the search strategy scope to include economic
and cost-related terms could address this limitation.
Additionally, the abstraction of the Technology dimension, while necessary for scalability,
introduced challenges in maintaining interpretability. Striking the right balance between
abstraction and specificity remains an ongoing topic.
The Tools characteristic naming convention can be challenging, as the word tools has a
different meaning under each dimension. In the Scenario dimension, a tool describes the
available tools a participant needs to have access to complete a given scenario. In the
Monitoring dimension, a tool describes the hardware (e.g. a sensor) and software tools
that monitors the participant’s progression, scores, or performance. Furthermore, lastly, in
the Technology dimension, it describes which tools are in the cyber range (e.g., a network
protocol analyzer like Wireshark (see Appendix ?? on Technology Tools). Other cyber
range taxonomies struggle with similar or closely related naming conventions of objects
under different dimensions, e.g., Reporting and Dashboard characteristics. The solution is
either finding better object labels or the taxonomy needs an explanation. Researchers
with domain knowledge or who have conducted an SLR on the subject will be able to
differentiate, but for the more novice reader, it can look confusing.
A list of Technology tools has been identified as mere notes during mapping. To a some-
what limited extent, all the mentioned tools have been validated against a similar list found
in Yamin et al. [7] and [4], and new tools have been added to the list of tools (Appendix ??).
A limitation of the dissertation is that the provided list has not been systematically mapped
up on the set of records. Furthermore, validating from the outset of the newly proposed
Technology dimension up against the proposed taxonomies capabilities and functionalities
(i.e., everything outside the Technology dimension) is something that remains that would
have strengthened the interrelationship between technical and non-technical objects.

Conclusion
The dissertation presents a systematic exploration and iterative refinement of cyber range
taxonomies, resulting in significant theoretical and practical contributions. The research
followed a structured progression through three iterations: a Base Taxonomy (Chapter 4),
a Refined Taxonomy (Chapter 6), and a Proposed Taxonomy (Chapter 7). Each iteration
built upon the previous findings, incorporating insights from systematic literature reviews,
literature mapping, and critical analysis. Alongside these developments, a purpose-built
toolkit (Chapter 8) was created to support the taxonomy development and refinement
process and ensure the transparency and reproducibility of the research:
The research began with the first iteration by developing a Base Taxonomy, which synthe-
sized elements from existing cyber range taxonomies, primarily those by Yamin et al. [7]
and Ukwandu et al. [50]. This initial framework comprised eight dimensions — Scenario,
Environment, Teaming, Learning, Econometrics, Monitoring, Management, and Technology —
and served as the foundation for subsequent refinements.
The second iteration, the Refined Taxonomy, addressed limitations in abstraction and
usability identified during the mapping process. Overlapping characteristics, such as Type
and Purpose, were merged, and new characteristics, such as Cyber-Physical Systems and
Vulnerability Injection, were added to reflect emerging trends. This iteration also focused
more on the Technology dimension, identifying areas for further enrichment.
The final iteration, the Proposed Taxonomy, prioritized abstraction and scalability. Spe-
cific technologies and non-essential characteristics were removed, while broad categories
such as Tools and Virtualization were introduced. This iteration balanced flexibility and
precision, ensuring the taxonomy’s relevance as the field of cyber ranges continues to
evolve.
Each of the three taxonomy chapters (Chapters 4, 6 and 7) concluded with a preliminary
conclusion section (Sections 4.11, 6.3 and 7.4), adding a distinct value to the overall
research:
1. The first preliminary conclusion validated the Base Taxonomy by demonstrating its
ability to synthesize elements from existing taxonomies while highlighting gaps in
the representation of technological components.
2. The second preliminary conclusion guided the refinement process by identifying
specific limitations in abstraction and usability, leading to the development of the
Refined Taxonomy.
3. The third preliminary conclusion validated the refinements, confirming that the Pro-
posed Taxonomy effectively balances abstraction, usability, and representational
depth.
To conclude the proposed taxonomy, the chapter on results (Chapter 9) describes the
Proposed Taxonomy by remapping the existing set of records, ensuring its alignment
with the literature, and identifying any residual gaps.
To this end, this dissertation set out to address a set of research questions (see Sec-
tion 1.2). The first research question (RQ1) on which existing taxonomies and systematic
literature reviews on cyber ranges have been conducted prior to this dissertation, has been

Contribution
The dissertation represents a comprehensive and methodical effort to address the re-
search questions on cyber range taxonomies. By iteratively refining the taxonomy and
leveraging purpose-built tools, the dissertation addresses critical gaps, among others,
the representation of technological and infrastructural elements. The Proposed Taxon-
omy provides a dynamic and scalable framework that captures the multifaceted nature
of cyber ranges while remaining adaptable to future advancements. The research also
underscores the importance of abstraction and systematic validation in taxonomy devel-
opment. By balancing theoretical rigor with practical applicability, the dissertation delivers
a robust contribution to the field, supporting academic research and cyber-range opera-
tional design.
The primary contributions of this dissertation are as follows:
1. An extensive systematic literature review of a set of records covering hundreds of
research papers on the subject of cyber range, leading to the identification of a
missing link in cyber range taxonomies and a Technology dimension.
2. A Taxonomy Mapping Tool (TMT) facilitates an iterative approach to organization, vi-
sualization, and refinement of any cyber range taxonomy. TMT’s adaptive features
and real-time collaboration capabilities support a dynamic approach to systematic
literature mapping, ensuring a simple protocol for teams to map records and en-
abling accuracy and transparency in the dynamic mapping process.
3. A Proposed Taxonomy as a new cyber range taxonomy. The taxonomy is an ad-
vancement in balancing abstraction and specificity across multiple dimensions, char-
acteristics, and meta-characteristics. A novel inclusion of a Technology dimension,
but also addressing critical gaps in earlier taxonomies, i.e., incorporating under-
represented dimensions and characteristics (notable additions include the Cyber-
Physical Systems and Vulnerability Injection characteristics, which among others re-
flect the growing complexity and scope of cyber ranges) by focusing on high-level
concepts such as Tools, Virtualization, and Federation.

Future Work
The taxonomy developed in this dissertation has been verified using a Technology-, Ar-
chitecture-, and Infrastructure-specific search strategy, which effectively captured records
within the focus on these areas. This may have excluded valuable insights relevant to
other dimensions, such as econometrics. The Econometrics dimension was only sparsely
represented in the mapped records, suggesting that the chosen search strategy may not
have been sufficiently broad. Expanding the search strategy to include terms specifically
targeting these dimensions could provide a more comprehensive holistic understanding
of cyber ranges. The omission of social engineering elements is another critical gap that
should be addressed in future work. Another limitation of this study was the restricted ac-
cess to some relevant literature due to paywalls. This constraint may have resulted in the
exclusion of valuable records. Future work should explore strategies to access more di-
verse records to ensure a more exhaustive mapping process. Alternative methods, such
as collaboration with institutions or leveraging public repositories, may help bridge this
gap.
The Taxonomy Mapping Tool (TMT) (Chapter 8) has significantly streamlined the taxon-
omy development and verification process, but several improvements could enhance its
functionality. Implementing a versioning system would allow researchers to track changes
over time, providing a clear history of modifications and their rationale. The ability to
manage multiple versions simultaneously, e.g., through a tabbed interface, would facili-
tate comparative analyses and experimentation with hypothetical changes. Introducing a
change request feature would enhance collaboration by enabling external researchers to
propose modifications and discuss their potential impact. Additionally, integrating the tool
with reference management software like Mendeley (https://www.mendeley.com) would
automate importing literature, reducing manual effort and minimizing errors. While the Au-
tomated Literature Analysis Tool (ALAT) (Chapter 8) proved effective in identifying relevant
terms within the records, its reliance on predefined search terms limited its scope. Future
iterations of the tool could incorporate advanced natural language processing techniques
to detect synonyms and related terms dynamically. This enhancement would reduce re-
liance on semi-manually curated protocols and allow for a more comprehensive analysis.
Expanding the range of alternative terms for each taxonomy element could improve the
capture of nuanced insights, ensuring a more robust and inclusive mapping protocol and
process.
Emerging technologies such as quantum computing are likely to profoundly impact the
technology sector and, more specifically, cyber security, particularly in scenario design,
simulation, and emulation. Future iterations of the taxonomy should anticipate these de-
velopments by including dimensions or nodes that address quantum-safe algorithms and
related advancements. Similarly, the taxonomy should evolve to reflect the increasing
use of artificial intelligence in cyber ranges, particularly in automating threat generation,
performance analysis, and adaptive learning. Even while these technologies are enfold-
ing as this dissertation is being written, the study group has in the proposed taxonomy
future proven for the known-knows and some known-unknowns of these capabilities and
technologies (e.g., in the Technology dimension, and the Simulation and Emulation char-
acteristics). This is the least exclusive of the current considerations for future work.
Further taxonomy validation is necessary to ensure its robustness and relevance across
diverse contexts. Engaging a broader range of researchers and practitioners in, e.g., val-

idation studies through workshops, surveys, and collaborative platforms would provide
valuable feedback and drive iterative improvements. Additionally, applying the taxonomy
to other domains, such as healthcare, finance, and critical infrastructure, could broaden
its impact and reveal opportunities for cross-domain adaptation. The collaborative poten-
tial of the Taxonomy Mapping Tool could be further strengthened by developing features
that support community engagement. This includes mechanisms for users to share cus-
tomized taxonomy versions, contribute to a centralized repository of mapped literature,
and collaboratively develop new dimensions. These enhancements would foster a dy-
namic, evolving resource relevant to the ever-changing cybersecurity landscape. A point
to make on these considerations is that the contribution made in this dissertation was
never meant to be static but a taxonomy that can evolve and adapt to changes within the
research field.
By addressing, among others, the limitations and challenges (Section 10) and the above
considerations, the proposed taxonomy and its supporting toolkit can evolve to meet the
challenges of emerging technologies, regulatory changes, and diverse application con-
texts. As wisely stated by Nickerson et al., ”We want to develop useful taxonomies, but
not necessarily ’best’ or ’correct’ ones, as these cannot be defined and, in fact, maybe
moving targets that could change over time.” (p. 391) [19]. Continued development will
ensure that the taxonomy remains a valuable resource for researchers and practitioners
in the dynamic research fields of cyber range.

Appendix — Thesis Master Data
The Microsoft Excel Spreadset entitled master-thesis-masterdata-27012025.xlsx in-
cludes a list of sheets (tabs) that stores the following data:
Domain Knowledge sheet is a protocol that contains base research and literature relevant
to the cyber range domain and serves as a pre-study for the study group to train the review
process. It categorizes key records based on their contribution, also as a process of
accruing domain knowledge. For each entry, the sheet includes the Authors, Publication
year, Title, categories, Publication (settings), Methodologies, and Significant Findings.
The sheet also provides a summary of key arguments and contextual insights.
Search Strings sheet is used to define the set of records for the systematic literature
reviews (SLR1 and SLR2). The search strings are structured to capture relevant records
from two online research databases as outlined in the Methodology (Chapter 2). The
sheet includes combinations of keywords, Boolean expressions, and specific parameters
for the research focus. This ensures the reproducibility of the literature search process.
SLR1 sheet is the results of the first systematic literature review (SLR1) protocol. The
sheet includes the Titles, Authors, Database Sources, and Review Status for the records
analyzed. The data highlights whether the records contributed to existing taxonomies and
whether they were included in the review, along relevant sector-specific details.
SLR1 Protocol DTUfindit+Scholar sheet describes the methodology and protocol fol-
lowed for SLR1. It outlines how records were selected, criteria for inclusion, and whether
the papers mentioning existing or are new taxonomies to include in the SLR1 review.
SLR2 Pre-Mapping Protocol sheet provides a preliminary mapping of potential records
for the second systematic literature review (SLR2). It lists titles, authors, Publication Date,
and Dimension in the Base Taxonomy, helping to refine the scope of SLR2, but this was
not used as we chose to develop a mapping tool TMT (see Chapter 8).
SLR2 DTUFindit protocol sheet includes the search results obtained from the DTU Findit
online research database during SLR2. It captures Abstract, Scenario, Methodology, and
Environments discussed in the records. Notes on findings and the methodology and pro-
tocol followed for SLR2. The sheet outlines how records were selected, criteria for inclu-
sion.
SLR2 Google Scholar sheet compiles records identified via Google Scholar during SLR2.
It highlights key insights from selected papers, notes on findings and the methodology and
protocol followed for SLR2. It outlines how records were selected, criteria for inclusion.
SLR2 Backward Snowballing Protocol sheet outlines the protocol for conducting SLR2
backward snowballing (see Chapter 2), contains inclusion criteria, and categorization of
papers. It serves as a guide for replicating the systematic approach.
