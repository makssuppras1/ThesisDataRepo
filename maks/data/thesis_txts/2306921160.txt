Abstract 
N-sulfinylimines constitute a versatile class of intermediates in asymmetric synthesis, especially for the preparation 
of amines and their derivatives. Of particular interest is the use of N-tert-butanesulfinylimines (Ellman imines) in 
Reformatsky reactions, which afford a convenient and straightforward method to prepare β-amino esters. Although 
this chemistry has been explored for several years now, the discussion remains lively regarding the stereochemistry 
of the reaction and its products. The first goal of this project was to study the stereochemical outcome of a 
Reformatsky reaction between an Ellman imine of acetophenone and an α-halo-acetate. 
We first performed the synthesis of several N-tert-butanesulfinylimines from diversely substituted acetophenone 
and benzaldehyde reagents, in good yields (62-99%). These were then reacted with ethyl bromofluoroacetate in the 
presence of activated zinc, to afford ethyl 3-amino-3-phenylbutanoate compounds in fair yields (average 65%) and 
moderate diastereoselectivity (71:29 – 91:9 and 67:33 – 87:13). 
 
We then analyzed the products by means of chiral HPLC. It revealed the compounds to be enantiopure molecules, 
and confirmed the role of tert-butyl as a powerful directing group. 
The products were finally deprotected and crystallised to afford the corresponding β-amino esters, whose 
stereochemistry was resolved by single crystal X-Ray crystallography.

1. Introduction 
This project was initially inspired by an article published in Chinese Chemical Letters 1, where Jing et 
al. studied the stereochemical outcome of the synthesis of α-fluoro-β-amino acids performed via 
Reformatsky reactions between diversely substituted chiral N-tert-butylsulfinylimines and 
bromofluoroacetate.  
The article claims these reactions to afford four diastereomers that could be separated one from 
another into diastereomer set I and set II (Figure 2). 
 
Figure 2 - Schematic representation of the stereochemical outcome of the Reformatsky reactions of ethyl 
bromofluoroacetate with N-tert-butylsulfinylimines 1 
This figure draws the structure of each diastereomer set – I and II – which are distinguishable based on 
the conformation of the R1 and R2 substituents on the β-carbon. Each set itself further divides into two 
molecules, based on the configuration of the carbon α carrying the fluorine atom. 
The reactions were performed with various sets of substituents R1 and R2 as reported in Table 1 below. 
This table provides the reader with the I to II ratios that were measured by the authors of the initial 
article. 
Table 1 - List of entries associated with the Reformatsky reaction of ethyl bromofluoroacetate with diverse 
N-tert-butylsulfinylimines 1 
 
As seen above, the ratios reported range from 64:36 to 92:8. However we believe that such ratios 
cannot be observed, and expect them to be greater than 95:5.  
Our hypothesis is based on the bulkiness of the tert-butyl substituants carried by the chiral sulphur 
atom in the N-sulfinylimine. The size of this group should induce a large steric hindrance which would 
prevent the reaction to happen on one face of the molecule, hence directing almost completely the 
addition to the less-hindered face of the sulfinylimine. 
The aim of this project is to study this particular Reformatsky reaction and propose more accurate 
stereochemical results.

2. Theoretical background 
2.1. The sulfinamides 
2.1.1. General introduction to the chemistry of sulfinamides 
Sulfinamides have similar structures to the usual amides (Figure 3) where the electrophilic carbon in 
the carbonyl group has been replaced by a sulphur atom (in yellow). The sulphur atom is doubly 
bonded to an oxygen atom (in red), and linked via a single bond to a nitrogen atom (in blue).  
   
 
Figure 3 - Structure comparison between a sulfinamide (left) and an amide (right) 
2.1.2. Sulphur and its chemistry 
Sulphur (or sulfur) is a non-metal chemical element with symbol S and atomic number 16. It belongs 
to the 3rd period of the periodic table (Figure 4) and stands immediately below oxygen, between 
phosphorus and chlorine. Its electron configuration is the following: [Ne] 3s2 3p4. 
 
Figure 4 - Sulphur in the periodic Table of Elements 
Therefore it is part of the group VI – also known as group 16, the first group of the p-block - in the 
periodic table, whose elements are named Chalcogens (from the Greek word khalkὁs and the Latin 
genes) and also known as the “oxygen family”.  
Sulfur is much less electronegative than oxygen 
2, with a Pauling electronegativity of 2.58 versus 3.44 
for the O-atom. In fact it has more or less the same electronegativity as carbon (2.5). Another main 
difference between oxygen and sulphur atoms is that sulfur can extend its valence shell to hold more 
than eight electrons, but oxygen cannot. As a second row element, sulfur d-orbitals allow it to have 
==PAGE:11==
3 
 
oxidation states 0, 2, 4 or 6, and coordination numbers from 0 to 7. For instance in this project we 
worked with sulfur in oxidation state IV and coordination number 3. 
Furthermore, the double bond S=O is weaker than the auxiliary double bond C=O present in carbonyl 
because of reduced orbital overlap 3; for this reason the S=O bond is less polarised making the S-atom 
less electrophilic than the carbon in a regular carbonyl group, and hence less prone to undergo attack 
from nucleophiles. 
Finally, sulfoxides have the potential for chirality – the tetrahedral sulfur atom being surrounded by 
four different groups (here in Figure 5 Ph, Me, the doubly-bonded O and the lone pair) - and have a 
stable tetrahedral configuration. Inspired from chiral descriptors for carbons, chirality at sulfur atoms 
is described by RS or SS descriptors. 
 
Figure 5 - Enantiomers of a chiral sulfoxide 2 
 
2.1.3. Interesting featuress of sulfinamides versus amides 
Despite their similarities with amides, sulfinamides feature a number of properties that make them 
attractive compounds to perform asymmetric synthesis. Among others we can quote: 
- 
A weaker S=O bond less reactive towards nucleophiles 3, 
- 
Sulphur atoms are chiral centres that can be used as directing group in asymmetric synthetic 
reaction2, 
- 
The sulfinyl group has a weak electron-withdrawing nature (compared to carbonyl groups) 
and activates the imine carbon towards nucleophilic addition, allowing reactions to proceed at 
lower temperatures 4, 
- 
Sulfinyl groups render the N-atom non-nucleophilic such that activation and amide bond 
coupling may be accomplished 5, 
- 
Imines obtained from condensation with ketones and aldehydes are more resistant to 
hydrolysis than other imines, but more reactive towards nucleophiles. 
Sulfinamides interest lies especially in their role as precursor for the synthesis of sulfinimes, which are 
later used to produce amines 6.  
 
To afford a general and straightforward asymmetric synthesis of amines, an ‘appropriate nitrogen 
substituent’ 5 must be used that enables the preparation of stable imines, activates them towards the 
addition of nucleophiles, and if possible directs the stereochemistry of the reaction. Furthermore and 
==PAGE:12==
4 
 
for practical considerations, the nitrogen substituent must also be affordable and easy to remove from 
the product. 
 
The most widely used N-substituents are those developed by the Davis group 6 and the Ellman group 5 
(Figure 6), respectively the N-p-toluenesulfinyl and the N-tert-butanesulfinyl groups. 
 
Figure 6 - Representations of the Davis’ (left) and Ellman’s (right) sulfinimines 7 
Both satisfy the previous criteria, while offering diastereofacial selectivity for nucleophilic addition 
thanks to the configurationally stable stereocenter at sulfur (noted SS or RS).  
 
Limitations in the use of the p-toluenesulfinyl group drove the Ellman group to develop its own N-
substituent. This new group of molecules possesses interesting advantages compared to. p-
toluenesulfinamide (p-tolyl group)5 : its high stability at room temperature makes it fairly easy to 
handle in the fume hood; it is more nucleophilic for direct condensation with aldehydes and ketones 
due to the EDG characteristics of the tert-butyl versus the p-tolyl group and its reduced 
electronegativity; finally, the greater steric hindrance of the tBu group minimizes competitive 
nucleophilic attacks at the S-atom. 
A further interest in the use of the N-sulfinyl group lies in its role as a protecting group 8,9; it not only 
has an ideal chiral directing group allowing asymmetric synthesis of β-amino esters, but it also serves 
as a versatile, low-molecular-weight protecting group analogous to the Boc protecting group. Like 
Boc, it is stable to basic conditions and readily cleaved by acidic treatment.

2.2.3. Chiral auxiliaries 
Inspiration from the synthesis of chiral sulfoxides by Alcudia and co-workers 13 opened the field for 
applications of chiral auxiliaries to the synthesis of sulfinamides. One of the first examples was 
reported by the Ellman group14 using diacetone-D-glucose (DAG) to afford enantiomerically pure tert-
butanesulfinamide (Scheme 4), in which procedure racemic tert-butanesulfinyl chloride is reacted with 
(D)-DAG-OH in the presence of amine base. Dynamic kinetic resolution occurs, providing a 86:14 
mixture of diastereomers that were separated by chromatography. 
 
Later procedures followed, such as the methods from the Senanayake 15,16 involving amino indanol-
based chiral auxiliaries or cinchona alkaloid quinine, and that from Qin, Jiang and co-workers 17 where 
a Cbz-protected norephedrine-based chiral auxiliary was employed.

2.3. Preparation of sulfinylimines 
N-sulfinyl-imines (also sulfinylimines or sulfinimines) represent an exceptional class of imines and 
have led to the development of a widespread array of synthetic methods. 
As discussed earlier, N-tert-butanesulfinyl imines are attractive intermediates for the further 
preparation of amine-containing compounds for various reasons among which their ease of synthesis, 
their hydrolytic stability (most are stable enough to undergo purification via silica gel chromatography 
and can be handled at r.t. under normal atmosphere), their electrophilicity and their stereocontrol. 
Although many routes exist for their synthesis, these can retrosynthetically be grouped into three main 
categories (Figure 9): the oxidation methods; the iminolysis methods; and the condensation methods. 
We will discuss them successively in the following section.

2.3.1. Oxidation 
The Davis group reported in 1973 the first example of synthesis of sulfinimines. The synthesis of N-
arylsulfinimines in racemic form was achieved from the oxidation of their corresponding N-
arylsulfenimines with mCPBA 19 (Scheme 5). 
 
Scheme 5 - Oxidation of sulfenimines with mCPBA 
The group later performed the asymmetric oxidation of sulfenimines to chiral enantiopure sulfinimines 
(Scheme 6) utilizing a methodology that involved asymmetric oxidation with stoichiometric amounts 
of the chiral oxidant N-(phenylsulfonyl)(3,3-dichlorocamphroyl)oxaziridine C (Figure 10) in CCl4 
with a 72 - 95% yield and 88 - 90% enantiomeric excess (ee) 20. The oxidation seems to happen via a 
chiral recognition mechanism 21, where the configuration of the oxaziridine ring (Figure 10) would 
control the absolute stereochemistry of the product: (-)-C would afford nearly exclusively the (SS)-
enantiomer  22,23. 
 
Scheme 6 - Asymmetric oxidation of sulfenimines 20 
 
 
Figure 10 - Representation of (+)-C (left) and (-)-C (right) 
 
The oxidation methodologies can be generally classified into two sub-types: one strategy consists in 
introducing a stereo-inducing chiral centre into the sulfenimine (and then an achiral oxidant can be 
employed); the other technique is based on chiral oxidants that would create asymmetry into achiral 
sulfenimines.

2.3.2. Iminolysis 
In 1977 Cinquini et al.24 reported the synthesis of enantiomerically pure sulfinimines via the reaction 
of metal ketimines with the Andersen reagent (D) menthyl p-toluenesulfinate. The method is based on 
the generation of the corresponding metal ketimine by addition of Grignard reagents to nitriles. 
Unfortunately it revealed limited to ketone-derived N-sulfinyl imines and could not be extended to the 
production of sulfinyl aldimines; it was also found that the method worked only for aromatic nitriles, 
and not with aliphatic ones.  
 
Scheme 7 - Iminolysis of Andersen’s reagent with a metal amide 
Davis et al. later developed a one-pot synthesis of sulfinylimines from D, providing an efficient entry 
to molecules derived from both aromatic and aliphatic aldehydes 10. 
While the iminolysis methodology provides access to a wide range of enantiopure compounds, it has a 
number of drawbacks among which the very practical problem of the isolation of the enantiomerically 
pure sulfinimines from the mixture of reagents and chiral auxiliaries. This in particular, prohibits 
large-scale preparation. 
 
2.3.3. Condensation 
The synthesis of N-tert-butanesulfininimes is most commonly conducted by a straightforward 
condensation reaction between tert-butanesulfinamide and a precursor aldehyde or ketone. Where the 
precursor is an aldehyde, the product will be designated as an aldimine. If it is a ketone it would be 
called a ketimine.

2.3.3.2. 
Synthesis of ketimines 
The synthesis of N-tert-butanesulfinyl ketimines is more challenging than that of aldimines, and 
Ellman et al.10 reported most of the conditions successful for aldimines synthesis to fail when applied

by Ellman and co-workers in 1999 27, in which several titanium(IV) reagents were investigated. Their 
experiments (Table 3) showed that the optimal conditions were to use 1.1 equiv. of ketone and 2 
equiv. of Ti(OEt)4 in THF at elevated temperatures (Scheme 10).

2.4. The Reformatsky reaction 
2.4.1. General remarks 
The Reformatsky reaction (sometimes spelt Reformatskii) was discovered by Sergey Nikolaevich 
Reformatsky in 1887. 
It can be generally defined as the zinc-mediated formation of β-aminoesters from the condensation of 
α-halo-esters with aldehydes or ketones. 
Some would compare this reaction to the Grignard reaction 28 as it involves organozinc reagents that 
can be seen as analogous to the organomagnesium compounds (RMgX) from the Grignard one. In 
==PAGE:20==
12 
 
some respects zinc is chemically similar to magnesium: its ion is of similar size and its commonly 
oxidation state is +2. 
However products from Reformatsky reactions exhibit different reactivity than those from Grignard 
reactions, and are not submitted to elimination and double additions as it would be observed when 
performing a Grignard reaction with an ester of an acyl halide as starting reagent. 
The first example of an addition of Reformatsky reagent to N-tert-butanesulfinyl imines was reported 
in 2002 by Staas and co-workers 29 at Merck in their synthesis of α,α-difluoro β-amino esters.  
The Reformatsky reaction offers the advantage to be conducted at convenient temperatures - 0°C to rt 
in the literature 10 or 60-70°C in our experiments, versus -78°C for titanium enolate additions - and is 
amenable to large scale synthesis. 
 
2.4.2. The Reformatsky Reagent 
The Reformatsky reaction proceeds in two steps (excluding the zinc activation): first, a reaction 
between metal zinc and the α-halo-ester that produces an intermediate; then, the actual addition of the 
intermediate to the aldehyde or ketone. 
The intermediate is usually termed ‘Reformatsky Reagent’ or ‘Reformatsky enolate’, because it is the 
actual chemical entity that will react at the aldehyde/ketone. This organozinc compound is less 
reactive than lithium enolates or Grignard reagents, hence the elimination and second nucleophilic 
addition on the ester group is very rarely observed in the Reformatsky reaction as it would be in the 
Grignard (Figure 11). 
 
Figure 11 – Comparison of outcomes between the Grignard reaction and the Reformatsky reaction 
The lower reactivity of the Reformatsky Reagent can be explained by its structure, which is much 
debated. The intermediate is derived from the zinc and the ester, and can exist as equilibrium between 
two chemical forms (Figure 12). On the left is drawn the C-metallated structure, where the zinc atom 
has inserted into the carbon-bromine bond; on the right structure is represented the enolate form of the 
reagent. It has been proposed 28 based on X-ray crystallography experiments that the solid intermediate 
of t-BuOCOCH2Br and Zn could be a dimeric structure (Figures 13a), exhibiting characteristics from 
==PAGE:21==
13 
 
both the enolate and the carbon-inserted forms. This assumption has been confirmed by Miki et al. at 
Takeda who prepared and isolated the Reformatsky reagent as a Tetrahydrofuran-coordinated dimeric 
crystal. X-ray crystallography experiments could be conducted on the product to sustain their work.

2.4.3. The zinc activation 
Zinc is the chemical element of symbol Zn and atomic number 30. It belongs to the 4th period and to 
the 12th group of the periodic table, and has the configuration [Ar] 3d10 4s2, which makes it suitable for 
oxidation reactions affording Zn(II). 
==PAGE:22==
14 
 
The surface of the pure metal tarnishes quickly, eventually forming a protective passivating oxide 
layer by reaction with atmospheric species 31. Because this layer prevents further reaction of the metal, 
it needs to be removed - reduced - to afford pure zinc. This is called the zinc “activation” 32. 
Activated zinc has the advantage to make possible oxidative metallation of organic halides under very 
mild conditions 33. Several methods for the zinc activation have been reported 33 up to date, and can 
generally be classified in two strategies: either they aim at removing the oxide layer from the metal 
surface, or they tend to achieve a fine distribution of the zinc metal. Regarding this second option, zinc 
is usually used as dust, powder or granules, in order to increase the reactive surface (the reaction rate 
being correlated to the available reactive surface). 
 
Zinc can be activated through chemically by using promoters/additives, or physically. The method 
chosen to activate the zinc can have major impacts on the yield of the reactions performed afterwards 
(Table 4). 
 
The most common physical methods for zinc activation are: 
- 
Irradiation with ultrasound which creates erosion and pitting, hence cleaning the surface of the 
metal 33, 
- 
Cocondensation of zinc metal vapour,

separation and drying), 
-

4.1. Determination of the stereochemistry of the reaction 
As stated in the Introduction, the goal of this project was to study the stereochemistry of the 
Reformatsky reaction between an Ellman imine and a α-halo-acetate. Precisely, we desired to show 
that the reaction affords only two products, diastereomers one to another. 
The following section reports how we proceeded to prove our hypothesis: we first separated the 
diastereomers; then showed their enantiopurity; and finally studied the impact on the stereochemical 
outcome of constraining the number of degree of freedom.

4.1.2. Study of the enantiomeric content of the isolated diastereomers 
Once the two diastereomers separated, a proper study of their enantiopurity could be achieved. 
Analysis was performed with help of chiral columns.  
 
Chiral chromatography is a variant of classical column chromatography in which the stationary phase 
is made of a single chiral enantiomer rather than being achiral. Because the two enantiomers of the 
mixture to analyse have different affinity to the single-enantiomer chiral stationary phase (CSP), they 
therefore exit the column at different times (i.e. have different retention times). The CSP can be 
prepared for instance by attaching a suitable chiral molecule to the surface of an achiral medium such 
as silica gel; the most commonly used chiral materials are oligosaccharides and cyclodextrins 46. 
Analysis of the molecules had to be made with a system - i.e. a column and an eluent mixture - on 
which the enantiomers would properly separate, hence ensuring one’s capacity to distinguish them. On 
such a perfectly running system would be observed two distinct peaks with good baseline separation 
when running a mix of enantiomers through the column. Research of appropriate systems was 
conducted for molecules 14 to 17.

4.1.3. Comparison of results with bromofluoroacetate and bromo-difluoroacetate 
It has been shown in the previous sections that the Reformatsky reaction produces only two molecules, 
which are diastereomers and differ only in their stereochemistry at carbon C2. To verify this 
hypothesis in an indirect fashion, we also tried to observe the impact on the product mixture of 
suppressing the C2-chiral centre. If our hypothesis was right, then we would observe a single product 
when getting rid of the C2-chiral centre. 
To do so, Reformatsky reactions were performed following the same procedures but with reagent 
bromo-difluoroacetate instead of bromo-fluoroacetate. Hence, carbon C2 carried two fluorines instead 
of single fluorine and hydrogen, which made it achiral. For non-understood reasons, coupling 
happened with sulfinylimines derived from acetophenone and 2-fluoro-acetophenone but failed when 
attempted on compounds synthesized from benzaldehyde. 
After purification, the reaction appeared to have produced only one molecule, which corroborates the 
assumption made of a fixed stereochemistry at carbon C3.

Configuration of the sulfinylimines 
The structure of sulfinylimines imposes restrictions on the use of most common NMR experiments to 
resolve their configuration (Figure 36). First, the protons sitting in the tert-butyl are magnetically 
equivalent and integrate in a single peak; then there is no proton in the sulfinamide region other than 
those belonging to the tert-butyl group; finally the number of bonds from the protons in tert-butyl to 
the R-substituent is too high (5) to efficiently use HMBC experiments.

4.2.2.2. 
X-ray Analysis 
X-ray crystallography is used to identify the atomic structure of a crystal. This technique is based on 
the analysis of the diffraction obtained when a finely focused monochromatic beam of incident X-rays 
is diffracted by crystalline atoms into many specific directions. The diffraction pattern can be 
converted into an Electron Density Map, from which the mean positions of the atoms in the crystal are 
then deduced to provide a 3D-structure. X-ray crystallography gives information on the crystal 
structure (unit cell dimensions, bond lengths, bond angles, and site-ordering) as well as the 
stereochemistry of molecules within the crystal 49 . It also offers the advantage of being a non-intrusive 
method. 
A limitation of this tool however consists in its requirements regarding the quality of the crystals: to 
solve a structure with the highest resolution, a very pure and regular single crystal is needed. This 
demand explains the several crystallisation strategies we explored during this project. 
The quality of the first crystals we obtained (compounds 16 and 17) was too low to undergo a full X-
ray analysis as they had grown as needle-shaped crystals. However they could be analysed with XRPD 
which returned Figure 38. It consists in a graph of the measured intensity (in arbitrary counts) of

5. Conclusion 
 
The overall objective of this project was to study the stereochemical outcome of the Reformatsky 
reaction between an Ellman imine of acetophenone and an α-halo-acetate. More specifically we 
wanted to prove that the reaction studied would produce only two molecules, diastereomers each 
other, despite what was stated in reference literature 1. The synthetic steps required in order to prepare 
the reagents from commercially available starting material, and the reaction itself, were successfully 
completed with acceptable yields. The products obtained were purified and analysed via different 
methods (NMR analysis and chiral HPLC), or in one case by single crystal X-ray crystallography. 
Eight different N-tert-butanesulfinylimines were prepared by variation of initial reagents. (S)- and (R)-
tert-butanesulfinamides were coupled to phenyl-derived carbonyls, including which acetophenone. 
The syntheses proceeded without major difficulty and afforded good yields (62 – 100%). 
The Reformatsky reactions revealed more complex to handle and afforded moderate yields (65% on 
average). They were performed on the previously synthesized sulfinylimines using either bromo-
fluoroacetate or bromo-difluoroacetate, and allowed synthesis of 15 different compounds (all isomers 
included) with good to moderate diastereomeric ratios (91:9 / 87:13 – 71:29 / 67:33). These were 
purified and separated for further analysis. 
Chiral HPLC systems were identified and used to show the enantiopurity of the products from the 
Reformatsky reactions, which was the objective of the project. In that respect we can state that the 
particular Reformatsky reaction studied exhibits good diastereoselectivity (enhanced by the choice of 
substituents) and a very high enantioselectivity. 
Analysis of the compounds was taken one step forward by crystallisation and resolution of their 
structure by single crystal X-Ray crystallography. This tool provided us with the full stereochemistry 
of the molecules, and several conclusions can be drawn. It first confirms the potency of tert-butyl as a 
directing group, which determines the stereochemistry of the β-carbon where the largest substituent 
must be trans to the tBu group. Then, it shows that the major product of the reaction is the 
diastereomer where the fluorine on α-carbon is on the same side as the tert-butyl and on the opposite 
side from the largest substituent on the β-carbon. Finally, this result is in agreement with our 
predictions from our suggested transition state, which could be an evidence of the accuracy of our 
hypothesis. 
A more extensive study with many substituents may be necessary to broaden the scope of our 
conclusions.  
Interesting and unsolved features of this project would deserve to be explored, such as: the limitations 
==PAGE:60==
51 
 
encountered when performing the synthesis with bromo-difluoroacetate and sulfinylimines from 
benzaldehyde; improving the crystallisation procedures; isolation and extensive study of the 
Reformatsky reagent associated with computational simulations in order to get a better understanding 
of the mechanistic path and of the probable transition state.

Appendix I - Glossary 
Aliphatic molecule: non-aromatic molecule. 
β-amino acid: amino acid in which the amino group is branched on the β -carbon whereas in a 
“classical” amino acid it would be located on the α-carbon. 
Chiral recognition: specific, reversible interaction between two chiral molecules based on a three-
point contact, the interaction being different for the different diastereomeric pairing. 
Differential Scanning Calorimetry: thermoanalytical technique measuring the difference in the amount 
of heat required to increase the temperature of a sample compared to reference as a function of 
temperature. It can be used to measure characteristic properties of a sample such as transition 
temperatures and enthalpies. 
Electron Density Map: map displaying the electron density in a chemical compound that is the 
probability of electrons to be present at a specific space location. 
Enantiomeric excess (ee): measurement of purity used for chiral species, which reflects the degree to 
which a sample contains one enantiomer in greater amounts than the other (e.g. a racemic mixture has 
a 0% ee). Usually calculated as follows, where R and S are mole fractions of enantiomers: 
       
        
Needle-shaped crystal: crystal habit (characteristic external shape) of a single crystal where one space 
dimension is significantly larger than the others hence this needle geometry. Typical dimension ratios 
are 1:1:100-100050 . 
Rieke zinc 36: zinc specie reported in 1973 by Rieke to show much higher reactivity than commercial 
zinc powder. It consists in metallic zinc generated by reduction of zinc chloride with alkali metal.
