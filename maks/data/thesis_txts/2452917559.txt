Abstract
In many regions of the world such as the Gulf of Mexico, the Mediterranean Sea, the Persian Gulf or the
North Sea it has been denoted the presence of oil and gas reservoirs in the vicinity of salt bodies. Worldwide
experience has shown that drilling adjacent or through salt bodies is rather challenging. The presence of
viscoplastic diapiric material such as salt alters the in-situ rock properties, stresses and geopressures, which
have a direct impact on wellbore stability. This case study investigates stress perturbations occasioned by
the presence of a salt dome in the Zechstein Group in the Oselvar ﬁeld, located in the southern part of the
Norwegian sector in the North Sea. The analysis includes the generation of a 3D structural model of the salt
body and the construction of a regional stress model via petrophysical logs and in-situ measurements. The
regional stress model is further calibrated in the so called “rubble zone” where the largest stress perturbations
are present. A wellbore stability analysis for a well located in the crest of the salt dome is given as an example
for future wells to be drilled adjacent to the salt structure in the ﬁeld. The main objective of this analysis is
to avoid formation related operational problems which jeopardize the technical and economic feasibility of
drilling schemes. The methodology applied for this case study is intended to be extrapolated to any oilﬁeld
containing salt domes in their geological structure.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
The chapter provides an overview of the signiﬁcance of the salt bodies in hydrocarbon basins and illustrates
methodologies conducted by diverse authors in oilﬁelds presenting salt bodies in their geological structure.
1.1
Salt Bodies in Hydrocarbon Basins
Salt bodies provide an ideal environment for structural traps in sedimentary basins due to its
absence of porosity and permeability in conjunction with their naturally occurring visco-plastic properties.
This allows salt structures to mobilize and deform around geological formations. Since the appearance of
prospective seismic methods, salt bodies have proven to represent a reliable indicator of large accumulations
of oil and gas. These structural conditions have been denoted in many of the largest hydrocarbon bearing
basins discovered throughout the 20th century such as the Gulf of Mexico, the Mediterranean Sea, the Persian
Gulf or the North Sea, which resulted in the incorporation and subsequent exploitation of signiﬁcant volumes
of hydrocarbon reserves.
The uncertainties in the geometry of salt diapirs and its interaction with the adjacent sedimentary
material generate uncertainty in the prediction of lithostratigraphic sequences and formation stresses in
Oilﬁelds presenting salt bodies in their geological structure. Various operational problems have been doc-
umented around the world in which the absence of an adequate stress model has led into severe problems
related to wellbore stability while drilling through and in the vicinity of salt bodies which have translated
into Non-Productive Time compromising the operational safety and economic feasibility of drilling schemes
ﬁeld-wide.
Over the last decades, signiﬁcant eﬀorts have been allocated to understand the complexity of the
visco-plastic behaviour of the salt media and the perturbations in shear stress, vertical stress, horizontal

stress with respect to the far-ﬁeld values in the rubble zones adjacent to the salt structures before designing
optimal wellbore trajectories in pre-salt and sub-salt reservoirs in order to augment the eﬃciency of the

The current and future demand of hydrocarbons in conjunction with the absence of new discoveries of
relatively simple structural ﬁelds in the last decades, will require a continuous allocation of human, economic
and technical resources by the Oil and Gas (O&G) industry to improve the understanding and exploitation
techniques in structurally complex reservoirs as the ones containing salt bodies in their geological structure.
1.2
Literature Review
1.2.1
Framework for Oilﬁelds with the presence of Salt Bodies
A methodology to enhance wellbore stability in sub-salt plays via the assessment of geomechanical
stresses in the vicinity of salt bodies is introduced by van der Zee et al., 2012 [28]. The framework established
by the authors introduces an initial seismic interpretation analysis, where it is highlighted the importance of
an adequate structural interpretation of the salt body, due to the impact of the geometry of the salt body has
on the stresses of the adjacent sedimentary material, causing a re-orientation of the principal stresses parallel
and perpendicular to the salt-sediment interfaces. It is, illustrated how recent advances on seismic acquisition
methods such as wide azimuth surveys in conjunction with advanced modelling algorithms have improved
the signal processing yielding in a more accurate seismic imaging of salt bodies over traditional survey logs.
Additionally, it is discussed how geological velocity models are capable to enhance sub-salt predictions on
formation ﬂuid pressure and stress ﬁeld variances.
The second part of the methodology concentrates on the development of the mechanical earth model
composed by the vertical stress, maximum horizontal stress, minimum horizontal stress, formation ﬂuid
pressure and azimuth of the maximum horizontal stress. In this stage, it is highlighted the importance of an
appropriate assessment of the initial stress state surrounding the salt body, which can be performed via the
analysis of well logs, operational drilling events, and wellbore stability problems in wellbores located in the
vicinity of the salt structure.
Conclusively, the frameworks establishes the construction of a structural model valid for the salt
body and generate a solid model incorporating the mechanical behaviour. Results presented by the authors
indicate the framework can be utilized to enhance wellbore stability around salt bodies, resulting in a more
accurate stress prediction.

1.2.2
Initial Stress Model
A methodology to construct mechanical earth models is presented by Fattahpour et al., 2012 [8].
The authors recommend the utilization of geological data, petrophysical logs, drilling data, core laboratory
tests and formation pressure measurements to develop accurate stress models.
The initial stage of the framework establishes the estimation of relevant geomechanical parameters
on a depth basis to be further calibrated via petrophysical logs and laboratory tests. The following step is the
development of principal stresses proﬁles including magnitudes and orientations in conjunction with in-situ
pressure proﬁles for the wells in the ﬁeld. The ﬁnal stage of the methodology is to analyze wellbore stability
with an appropriate failure criteria to generate adequate operational mud windows.
Conclusively, it is denoted that the continuous incorporation of valid data from new constructed
wells enhance the accuracy of the model, reducing the risk of borehole failure in subsequent wells to be
constructed in the ﬁeld.
1.2.3
Perturbation of the Stresses around Salt Bodies
Fredrich et al., 2003 [11] present a geomechanical model for diverse salt geometries occurring in the
Gulf of Mexico. The simulations were performed assuming an idealized spherical salt body, horizontal salt
sheet, columnar salt diapir and a columnar salt diapir with the presence of an overlying tongue. The authors
denoted the variances in shear stress, horizontal stress, vertical stress and principal stress for each geome-
try. Furthermore, an explanation of the stress state in the rubble zones adjacent to the salt bodies is illustrated.
The authors conducted simulations for the spherical structure with three diverse sphere diameters
(500 m, 1000 m and 2000 m). Results indicate normal stresses are required to be continuous through the
salt-non-salt interface, the variations in the vertical and horizontal stress that occur within the equilibrated
salt body, extend to the adjacent sediments. This generates a reduction in the vertical stress above and below
the salt sphere in conjunction with a augmentation of the horizontal stress in the lateral sediments to the salt
wall in an extension of one half to three quarters of the sphere diameter. It is emphasized that the normal
assumption that one of the principal stresses is vertically oriented can not be sustained in the salt-sediment
interface.
A wellbore stability analysis is presented with the results obtained from the simulations correspond-
ing to the 2000 m spherical geometry. The interval of analysis is the location were the stress disturbances were
predicted to occur in the largest extension. At this point, it was estimated a reduction of 10% in the vertical
stress and minimum horizontal stress in conjunction with a 20% increase in the maximum horizontal stress.
Three scenarios were generated, the initial scenario considered the far-ﬁeld stress condition and ignored the

stress perturbations adjacent to the salt body; the second scenario contemplated the stress perturbations
in the vicinity of the salt structure while ignoring the rotation of the principal stress directions and the
last scenario assumed the stress perturbations and principal stress rotations adjacent to the spherical structure.
The analysis revealed that the far-ﬁeld stress model does not accurately accounts the risk of cir-
culation losses as a consequence of the diminished fracture gradient in the adjusted model. Similarly, the
calibrated model indicates feasible well trajectories up to 30 degrees, before requiring a minimum mud weight
exceeding the predicted fracture gradient. The principal stress orientated scenario indicates preference for
determined drilling directions. The minimum required mud weight is enlarged while drilling further away
from the salt direction. The maximum well orientation was found to occur at 30 degrees. Beyond this point
the required mud weight exceeds the minimum horizontal stress.
An investigation of the in-situ stress perturbations around salt bodies diverse salt structure geome-
tries, far-ﬁeld stresses and variances in salt density is presented by Sanz et al., 2010 [25].
The simulation performed for a spherical salt body indicates a perturbation in the initial stress
state in the vicinity of the salt body whilst the deviatoric stress is relaxed. Numerical solutions indicate
a reduction of 20% in the vertical stress and an increase of 14% in the horizontal stress. It is denoted an
augmentation in the radial stress increases towards the salt, while the hoop stress was found to decrease.
Principal stress orientation were investigated for an ellipsoidal salt structure. Results indicate the
isotropic condition inherent to salt bodies causes a re-orientation of the principal stresses tending to align
parallel and perpendicular in the salt-sediment interface.
1.2.4
Wellbore Stability around Salt Bodies
An analysis of the operational problems related to loss of circulation and borehole failure while
drilling near a salt dome was performed by W. Bradley, 1978 [3], in where it is illustrated how the reduction
of in-situ hoop stress in the formations surrounding a salt dome have a signiﬁcant impact on wellbore stability.
An initial investigation of the problems related to loss of circulation was conducted, the study
revealed the events encountered were related to a disturbed in-situ stress state attributed to the salt dome,
the author then considered the fracture stress that led to loss of circulation in the initial well and modeled
the behaviour of this in a subsequent well, to design an eﬃcient mud weight to avoid formation fracturing.
The conclusions of this case study revealed that the inﬂuence of salt domes in the in-situ stresses have
a major repercussion in wellbore stability, speciﬁcally in loss of circulation and borehole collapse problems.

The author demonstrated that the utilization of predictive models for the eﬀective fracturing and collapse
stresses based on well records can be developed to avoid borehole failure in subsequent wells to be constructed
in regions around salt structures.
Diverse geomechanical models were developed by Lou et al., 2012 [19] in order to assess stresses
around salt bodies and design optimal operational windows to maintain wellbore stability for vertical wellbores
in the vicinity of salt structures.
The simulations conducted by the authors assumed salt structures to behave visco-elastically, sedi-
mentary material as elastic, formation ﬂuid pressure was deﬁned as hydrostatic in the sediments and neglected
inside the salt structure while deformation of the sediments is not coupled on a time-ﬂuid ﬂow. The models
assumed two diﬀerent salt geometries, a spherical and an heterogeneous salt sheet structures, to assess the
deviatoric stress and estimate the stress perturbations around the salt bodies.
Based on these results, the authors determined optimal operational windows, assuming two con-
straints. The ﬁrst condition stated that the borehole pressure must be among the formation ﬂuid pressure
and the minimum principal stress, to prevent possible inﬂows derived from insuﬃcient hole pressure and
avoid fractures from a borehole pressure above the minimum principal stress; the second condition required
that the borehole pressure was suﬃcient to avoid wellbore collapse.
The results of the authors denote that in the vicinity of the spherical salt geometry, borehole failure
is more plausible to occur, due to the reduction of the minimum principal stress and an augment of the shear
stress. For the case of the heterogeneous salt sheet geometry it is illustrated that operational windows can be
compromised in the regions of the salt body presenting convex curves when the diﬀerence among the shear
stress and the minimum principal stress is exacerbated.

2.3
Salt Diapirism
Intrusions of underlying visco-plastic material such as salt in overlying sedimentary formations
is often a reliable indicator of large volumes of hydrocarbons in diverse basins around the world (Figure
2.5). The rise of salt diapirs is attributed to the lower density of the salt (ρ ≈2.15) in comparison with
the surrounding sediments (ρ = 2.3 −2.7), this generates a zone of tectonic instability that in conjunction
with exertion of dynamic vertical and horizontal stresses over geological time generates the irruption of the
diapiric material.
11

(Highlighted countries with current E&P projects)
Distinctive drives of diapirism are characterized by the resistance of the overburden in the overlying
sedimentary material. Passive diapirism, the most common form of intrusion, is the result of insuﬃcient
overburden resistance allowing the salt to mobilize through the overlaying sediments and rise, generating
12
==PAGE:27==
Wellbore Stability around Salt Bodies: A Case Study for the Oselvar Field in the North Sea
its characteristic geometric form as a dome. Active diapirism is generated in areas where the overburden
sediments were resistant enough to counteract the rise of the salt but within geological time the vertical
stress was diminished enough to further withstand the buoyancy of the salt as illustrated by Schultz-Ela et
al., 1993 [26]. As a consequence salt strings penetrates the overlying sediments generating radial faults in the
surrounding formations. Reactive diapirism occurs in the cases of extension or contraction of the overburden
due to regional tectonic events allowing the intrusion of the diapiric material.
The diapiric process in the Zechstein Group occurred during the Early Kimmerian until the Jurassic
as proposed by Remmelts, G., 1995 [24]. The mechanism of the salt mobilization is likely a consequence of
tectonic thinning, normal faulting, tensile fracturing and buoyancy eﬀects which diminished the overburden
allowing the generation of salt dome structures throughout the Central Graben as denoted by Davison et al.,
2000 [6].
2.4
Wellbore Stability
During drilling operations maintaining a stable wellbore is crucial in order to avoid potential
operational problems which might jeopardize safety or develop into severe Non-Productive-Time (NPT)
having repercussion on the economical feasibility of drilling schemes. In recent years the increasing complexity
of wells drilled around the world, have triggered the allocation of resources into the development of new
techniques and technologies to understand better the in-situ stresses and the rock mechanical behaviour in
order to avoid mechanical failure of boreholes.
Cheatham J.B, 1993 [5] illustrates the most common wellbore stability issues. Failure under tension
leads to fracturing and lost of circulation events. This occurs when the formation ﬂuid pressure is greater
than the sum of compressive stresses and the tensile strength of the rock. Compression failures lead to hole
size reductions or enlargements, being this eﬀect exacerbated in inclined wellbores due to the contribution of
the vertical stress aﬀecting the hoop stresses around the borehole wall.
Wellbore stability hazards while drilling adjacent to salt bodies have been documented by several
authors. The area of greater hazard is the so called ”rubble zone” where the perturbations in the in-situ
stresses are largely exacerbated. In this a stress transfer occurs before reaching an isotropic stress state inside
the salt structure. These perturbations are largely inﬂuenced by the salt geometry as presented in Section 1.2,
therefore an adequate a structural model of the salt body in conjunction with a estimation of the orientation
of the principal stress is required in order to mitigate stability problems while planning and monitoring real
time drilling operations around salt bodies.

3.2
Methods
3.2.1
Approach
This dissertation aims to study how a salt body aﬀects the in-situ stresses in the surrounding
formations. The research utilized drilling related data, petrophysical logs and geological information from
the Oselvar ﬁeld and surrounding wells in order to investigate the variation of stresses with respect to the
following:
1. How and in which magnitude does the geometry of the salt structure aﬀects the stresses in surrounding
formations?
2. Which is magnitude of the perturbation in the vertical, minimum horizontal and maximum horizontal
stress with respect to far-ﬁeld values?
3. What is the extension of the so called rubble zone adjacent to the salt structure?
4. How is the shear failure pressure aﬀected by the changes in the stresses?
5. How can wellbore stability be maintained while drilling in the vicinity of the salt body?
3.2.2
Fundamentals
The mechanical behaviour of sedimentary material, is based on the principle that a rock responds
to a stress by changing its original size and shape. In the case of elastic deformations the rock will recover its
original size and shape when the exerted stress is removed. If the rock is subjected to a certain stress beyond
its elastic limit, plastic deformation occurs and produces an irreversible change in the original shape of the rock.
In undisturbed in-situ conditions (before drilling operations), it is convenient to assume sedimentary
material is in an equilibrium state. Described by three orthogonal stresses (Figure 3.11) composed by the
vertical stress (σv), the maximum horizontal stress (σH) and the minimum horizontal stress (σh) which rarely
have similar magnitude (e.g inside salt bodies) in addition with the formation ﬂuid pressure (i.e. the pressure
exerted by the ﬂuid ﬁlling the pore spaces).
Sedimentary material deformations are a relation of the diﬀerence between the external stresses and
the support from the formation ﬂuid acting on the rock porous volume. Based on the relation for horizontal
and vertical stresses proposed by Biot, M. 1941 [10], the eﬀective stress can be expressed in terms of total
stresses via Eq.(3.1). Where σieff is the eﬀective stress; σiT otal the total stress, α the Biot’s coeﬃcient and

Structural Modelling of the Salt Body
Salt geometry plays a fundamental role at the time of evaluating stresses inside and in adjacent
sedimentary material as presented in Section 1.2. Structural modelling of salt bodies is rather challenging.
Diapirism creates complex geometrical structures. Often diﬃcult to model realistically via seismic inter-
pretation as denoted by Jones , I., & Davison, I., 2014 [15]. Conversely, surface based models based on
stratigraphic contacts have proven to generate realistic structural models as documented by Caumon et al,
2009 [4] and Jacquemyn et al, 2018 [14].
In the absence of velocity logs for the wells in this case study, the structural model of the salt body
in the Oselvar ﬁeld was generated via a 3D surface based model in ”Petrel 2016 E&P”. The overlying strata
of the Zechstein Group (salt body) was modelled via stratigraphic contacts retrieved from the mud logs and
petrophysical logs introduced in Section 3.1. The salt body model was adjusted via interpreted seismic lines
(Figure 3.22) provided by the Norwegian Petroleum Directorate to overcome uncertainties in the geometry

3.2.8
Stresses around Salt Bodies
Salt media is unable to withstand deviatoric stress due to its visco-plastic characteristics. Fur-
thermore, when diﬀerential loading is exerted it mobilizes and deforms around sedimentary material until
relaxation occurs and reaches an isostatic state as denoted by Nikolinakou et al, 2011 [23].
The isotropic requirement inherent within salt bodies generates a stress transfer eﬀect in the zones
adjacent to the salt bodies where signiﬁcant perturbations with respect to far-ﬁeld values occur in conjunction
with changes in magnitude and re-orientation of the principal stresses governed by the geometry of the
salt body. The normal assumption that the vertical Stress is governed uniquely by the overburden and the
horizontal stress is a fraction of it, is not considered valid as illustrated by Sanz et al., 2010 [25] in the
surroundings of a salt body.
The identiﬁcation of the so called ”rubble zone” plays a fundamental role in the geomechanical
modelling in salt bodies. Adachi et al., 2012 [1] documented the importance of an adequate model of the
salt body geometry and its interactions with the surrounding sediments in conjunction with a reliable stress
model lead to optimize wellbore trajectories, operational drilling windows and consequently avoid wellbore
stability issues that have a repercussion on the well feasibility.

5.1
Well 1/3-4
Operational Resume
Vertical wildcat well 1/3-4 was spudded with the semi-submersible installation Dyvi Alpha on 15
February 1983. The wellbore was constructed with the purpose of evaluating potential hydrocarbon bearing
zones above the domal structure of the Late Permian Zechstein Group, more speciﬁcally in the Danian and
late Cretaceous Shetland Group (Table 5.1). Wellbore 1/3-4 was drilled to TD at 3198 m.
Operational problems (Table 5.2) associated with formation ﬂuids were encountered while drilling in
the base of the Middle Miocene claystones of the Hordaland Group, further geochemical analysis of these gas
occurrences demonstrated the gas was originated by the Mandal formation source rock at the Late Jurassic,
which indicates possible conduit (fault) transferring of formation ﬂuids from Mandal to the Middle Miocene
formations. Repeat formation tester (RFT) measurements indicated the existence of a hydrocarbon bearing
formation at the upper Ekoﬁsk formation in the 2754 to 2780 m interval. The well was abandoned on 8 May
1993 catalogued as a dry well with strong oil shows.

CHAPTER8
Conclusion
The absence of signiﬁcant new discoveries in relatively simple structural ﬁelds over the last decades,
will require the continuous allocation of human, economic and technical resources by the O&G industry in
order to overcome the large uncertainties at the time of developing exploitation schemes in regions of salt uplift.
This case study has conducted an investigation of the stress perturbations due to the presence of a
salt dome in the Zechstein Group in the overlying sedimentary material for the Oselvar ﬁeld, located in the
Central Graben of the Norwegian North Sea.
A good and detailed 3D structural model of the salt body has shown to play a fundamental role at
the time of evaluating the stress perturbations in magnitude and orientation around salt bodies. Orientation
of the principal stress tend to align parallel and perpendicular to the salt structure and salt buoyancy being
a function of the salt body dimensions.
The generation of an adequate regional stress model has proven to play a fundamental role at the
time of evaluating stress perturbations adjacent to salt bodies. The continuous incorporation of valid data
retrieved from previously constructed wells provides the means of overcoming sub-surface uncertainties and
enables a more accurate estimation of geomechanical models.
The required isotropic stress state condition inside salt bodies generates a stress transfer eﬀect in
the salt-sediment interface. The analysis of the degree of perturbation in the salt stress state indicates a
normalization of stresses towards far-ﬁeld values within 2-3 radial distances of the salt wall. This has allowed
the delimitation of the so called rubble zone in the crest and shoulders of the salt domal structure for the
ﬁeld in which potential risk zones of wellbore stability have been denoted.

Wellbore Stability around Salt Bodies: A Case Study for the Oselvar Field in the North Sea
An adjustment of the horizontal regional far-ﬁeld stress have been performed via the assessed
perturbations in the tangential and radial stress. Results indicate a decrease in the estimated minimum
horizontal stress and an augmentation in the maximum horizontal stress. For speciﬁc locations within the
rubble zone the conventional assumption that the vertical stress is the principal stress is not fulﬁlled.
A wellbore stability analysis was performed for well 1/3-4 located at the crest of the dome. Opera-
tional windows were constructed utilizing the regional and adjusted stress model, which included the analysis
of the shear failure gradient. Results indicate perturbations in the rubble zone narrow the operative margins
as a consequence of the increased collapse gradient and reduced fracture gradient.
This case study is intended to serve as a preamble for future wells to be drilled in the Oselvar ﬁeld
and similar post-salt reservoirs. It has been demonstrated the signiﬁcant inﬂuence of salt bodies in the
geomechanical interaction with adjacent sedimentary material. If not accounted, signiﬁcant hazards in terms
of wellbore stability can be encountered during the construction of wellbores jeopardizing the technical and
economical feasibility of drilling schemes.
Findings obtained throughout the conduction of this case study leave areas of opportunity for further
investigation. Being thermal conductivity, pore pressure perturbations, radial fracturing around the crest
dome and drilling hazards interesting aspects to address.

CHAPTER9
Discussion
Structural Model of the Salt Body
The structural model of the salt body in the ﬁeld was constructed assuming the Base of the Shetland
Group as an upper limit for the salt structure around the wellbores which penetrated the salt body to
induce geological continuity to develop the top surface of the Zechstein Group. To constrain the base surface
velocities from seismic lines were converted into depth from the wellbores which penetrated the Zechstein
Group to constrain a base for the salt structure.
The salt structural model and the geometry constructed here shows a very good approximation of
the length and width of the structure as the one presented in various seismic lines found in the Completion
Reports of Well 1/3-4, 1/3-5 and 1/3-1. If more data are available, the model can be calibrated by integrating
velocity proﬁles in order to enhance the accuracy of the salt dome shape and dimension which control the
stress state around the salt-sediment interface as documented by van der Zee et al., 2012 [28].
Far-ﬁeld Stress Model
In the absence of post-mortem information regarding the encountered geopressures for the wells
drilled in the Oselvar ﬁeld. A regional far-ﬁeld stress model valid for the ﬁeld via petrophysical logs, mud
logs and Completion reports from the wells drilled in the Oselvar ﬁeld.
Vertical stress was evaluated via density logs. The formation ﬂuid pressure was estimated uniquely
for the post-salt sediments. Conventional methods to estimate formation ﬂuid pressure are not valid in
carbonate formations, as diverse mechanisms govern overpressure in limestones and dolomites, therefore a
porosity-pore pressure relation can not be assumed. For the estimation of the pore pressure in the Shetland
Group, it was decided to assume as valid formation tester (RFT), drill stem test (DST), and formation multi

==PAGE:125==

tester (FMT) measurements at speciﬁc depths within the Vidar, Ekoﬁsk, Tor, Hor, Blodoks, Hidra and Rodby
formations to be representative of the entire lithological group. The estimation of the formation ﬂuid pressure
inside the Zechstein Group, was not conducted throughout this case study. Values of the pore pressure
gradient within the Zechstein Group were obtained from the uppermost formation pressure measurement and
considered valid. This was decided due that as being the Forties formation and the Shetland Group post-salt
hydrocarbon bearing structures eﬀorts were allocated in the calibration of the stress perturbations in the
rubble zone above the salt structure.
However, in the case of analyzing the rubble zone for sub-salt formations, further calibration of the
formation ﬂuid pressure must be conducted in order to address the excess of pore pressure caused by an
additional not uniaxial load inside the salt as documented by Nikolinakou et al., 2017 [23].
In the case of the estimation of the minimum horizontal stress matrix stress coeﬃcients were adjusted
from available Leak-oﬀtests in conjunction with ﬁeld experience. It is recommended to integrate Leak-oﬀ
tests from additional correlation wells and include them in the model to enhance its accuracy.
Temperature Eﬀect
The impact of temperature in the stress state around the salt body was disregarded during the
conduction of this case study as only two wellbores, located in the remoteness of the salt structure included a
temperature log. Further work needs to be conducted to assess this eﬀect. A preliminary approach indicates
salt media as being a thermal conductor, it is expected to have an augmentation of the in-situ temperature
in the salt-sediment interface more exacerbated in the regions of a larger salt thickness (i.e salt crest). This
will modify the rock elastic parameters and strength in the rubble zone aﬀecting the shear failure pressure.
Additionally, perturbations in the the tangential stress (σθ) and axial stress (σz) are plausible to occur as
illustrated by Fjar et al., 2008 [10].
Orientation of the Principal Stress
The condition that one of the principal stresses is vertically aligned is not fulﬁlled in the salt-sediment
interface. Perturbations in the in-situ stresses with respect to regional values generates an alignment of
the principal stress parallel and perpendicular to the geometry of the salt body. This re-orientation of the
principal stress have a direct repercussion in optimizing the geometry (inclination and orientation) of well
trajectories.
Extension of the Rubble Zone
Due to the complex geometry and the large uncertainties of the salt dome it was only possible to
eﬀectuate the analysis of the extension of the rubble zone in the crest and shoulder area of the dome. The
111
==PAGE:126==
Wellbore Stability around Salt Bodies: A Case Study for the Oselvar Field in the North Sea
extension of the rubble zone was assessed by the degree of perturbation in the stress state around the salt
wall. Results denoted a normalization of the stresses towards far-ﬁeld values within 2-3 radial times from
the salt wall. Potential wellbore stability risk zones were delimited, the high risk zone limit was established
within 1.5 radial distance, the medium risk zone is found at 2 radial distance and the low risk zone boundary
is at 3 radial distance from the salt wall.
It is recommended to realize a ﬁnite-element simulation which will address the complexity of the
salt geometry and would enable the delimitation of the extension of the rubble zone in the ﬂank dome area.
Adjustment of the Regional Far-ﬁeld Stress Model
The salt body was approximated as a vertical cylinder in the crest of the salt dome and estimate
the degree of disturbance in the tangential stress and radial stress inside the salt and consider them as
representative of the perturbations in the maximum and minimum horizontal stress respectively inside the
rubble zone and calibrate the regional stress model. Assessed perturbations in the horizontal stress are in
concordance with diverse ﬁnite-element simulations performed by Fredrich et al., 2003 [11] demonstrating
that for speciﬁc locations within the rubble zone the vertical stress does not represent the maximum stress.
Given the large uncertainties in the salt geometry, hollow cylinder approximation considered non
suitable to estimate perturbations in the vertical stress above the crest of the salt dome. Research performed
by Davison et al., 2000 [6] indicates an attenuation of the overburden in the chalk adjacent to the Zechstein
Group caused by the buoyancy eﬀect of the salt dome. This enables an area of opportunity for further
investigation.
Finite-element simulations are recommended to describe better the salt structure and the evolution
of the salt diapir.
Rock Mechanical Properties
In the absence of laboratory tests and available core samples for the strata analyzed in this case
study, it was decided to estimate the cohesive strength, angle of internal friction and unconﬁned strength based
on sonic log correlations presented by Lal M., 1999 [16] which were assumed valid for the entire sedimentary
material. The Poisson ratio utilized for the carbonate sediments was assumed as valid for the siliciclastic
material. It is recommended to allocate eﬀorts to better constrain the carbonate strength parameters.
Rock Failure Criterion
The Mohr-Coulomb criterion was the one preferred for the shear failure analysis as being considered
the most conservative method to estimate collapse pressure due that it ignores, the eﬀect of the intermediate
112
==PAGE:127==
Wellbore Stability around Salt Bodies: A Case Study for the Oselvar Field in the North Sea
principal stress (σ2) which provides support on the rock. Conversely, the Drucker-Prager criterion accounts
signiﬁcantly this contribution to the rock support. Therefore, it was decided to maintain the most conservative
method in the shear failure analysis to avoid potential instability problems due to overestimation of the
contribution of the intermediate principal stress as documented by McLean, M. & Addis, M., 1990 [21].
An area of opportunity is denoted to analyze failure criterion via diﬀerent methods such as the
modiﬁed Lade criterion to account accurately the contribution of the intermediate stress as illustrated by
Ewy, R., 1999 [7] and optimize the shear failure gradient.
Operational Window
The operational window analysis conducted in this case study indicate a narrowed operational
window in the adjusted stress model as a consequence of the increased estimated shear failure gradient in
conjunction with a reduction in the fracture gradient. Narrowing the mud window and hence hindering well
control.
The reduction of the collapse pressure denotes the necessity of increasing the minimum required
mud weight to avoid compressive formation failure which is a major cause of wellbore instability. From a
drilling engineering standpoint, this generates a reduction in the rate of penetration (ROP), increases the risk
of diﬀerential sticking and formation damage (mud ﬁltration) in permeable formations. Increasing rig time
and consequently increasing the ﬁnal cost of the well.
