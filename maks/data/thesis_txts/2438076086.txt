ABSTRACT 
Since its introduction into the Baltic Sea in the 1990’s, the round goby (Neogobius melanostomus), 
the demersal species has been considered as an invasive alien species. Originating from the Ponto-
Caspian area, the goby can survive in relative wide range of environments and has established 
itself in Danish coastal waters where it poses a risk for the local fish species and reduces income 
of coastal fishermen. Accordingly, strategies for the reduction of the round goby populations are 
ought to be found. One suggestion would be a commercial fishery which would likewise remove 
round goby from the Baltic Sea as well as benefit local fishermen. The potential for this venture 
was assessed by analysing fisheries data regarding length distribution, the condition and the sex of 
round goby. Further, the nutritional quality of round goby fillets was explored. Samples from 
Guldborgsund, Kalvehavesund and Karrebæksminde were taken and three fishermen were 
interviewed. The length of gobies ranged from 0.5 cm to 22 cm with an average of 12.0 cm at all 
locations. The largest length was found at Kalvehave. The length varied between the months, 
acknowledging a pattern with larger length in spring and summer. The condition factor averaged 
at 1.6 and varied only slightly between the areas. However, the condition decreased during the 
summer, possibly due to the spawning season. The samples were male dominated, indicating 
established stocks at all locations. The best months for fishing are April (in Guldborgsund) and 
May in all areas as catches were high. In general, the established stocks as well as the length would 
justify the commercial fisheries. Besides, the best fishing season is outside the eel fishing season. 
Moreover, the high protein content can be valuable in human nutrition as well as the content of 
essential omega-3 fatty acids. One suitable application would be either as fillets or as fish protein 
powder. Nonetheless, the price per kg must be high enough for the fishermen to actually fish this 
species. Moreover, the marketability and customers opinion about round gobies are not known yet 
and are essential for a viable fishery.

1. INTRODUCTION 
In the European Union, around 12 000 species are identified as alien species. Those species live 
in areas where they do not naturally occur. 10 to 15 % of those alien species are being assessed as 
invasive (EU 2014). Invasive alien species (IAS), as they are called, are non-indigenous species 
(NIS), which have been introduced into the new areas by anthropological means. In contrast, a 
biological invasion of species from one area to another happens naturally via migration caused for 
instance by altered condition (e.g. climate change) or density stress. IAS, once they are established, 
have negative effects on the new location. They are key driver for environmental change to the 
ecosystem by various means. They can put pressure on the native flora and fauna by predation or 
occupation of the same niche as native species and thereby outcompete them or reduce their 
numbers substantially (Olenin et al. 2017). Moreover, some species have the potential to alter the 
ecosystem structurally like the Japanese seaweed (Bax et al. 2003; Ojaveer & Kotta 2015). These 
species are often introduced unintentionally by shipping, for instance in the ballast water or 
through hull fouling, but also by import of species meant for mariculture. Furthermore, these 
invasive species have impact on the social and economic use of marine habitats by humans. There 
might be decreases in fishery and aquaculture in regards of employment and production as well as 
touristic drawbacks (Bax et al. 2003).  
Consequently, there is a lot of effort to combat invasive species. First and foremost, the most 
important step of controlling and managing an aquatic alien species is to prevent the species from 
spreading into new areas. This is done by managing the vector by which the species can be 
introduced, such as treating the ballast water as it has been made mandatory by the International 
Maritime Organization (IMO) in 2014 (Ojaveer et al. 2015). A different form of eradication is the 
direct removal of the species by angling (Ojaveer et al. 2015) or in the case of the lion fish with 
spearfishing (Morris & E. Whitfield 2009). Further, some biocontrol agent, a deliberately released 
predator, can be used to eradicate an invasive species (Eilenberg et al. 2001). However, this 
specific method seems to have limited success, as well it can result in unexpected and unintended 
side effects (Ojaveer et al. 2015). One example of this unforeseen side-effect is the event of the 
toxic cane toad in Australia. It was intentionally introduced in 1935 as biocontrol agent for a beetle 
pest of sugar cane (Invasive Animals CRC 2012; Shine 2010). Since then, the cane toad is a pest 
itself.  
==PAGE:11==
2 
 
The toad from central and tropical South America can occupy a wide range of habitats and tolerate 
different environmental and climate conditions (Zug & Zug 1979) and threatens the native flora. 
The cane toad is responsible for the decline of predator species such as crocodiles (Letnic et al. 
2008) and other carnivores (Burnett 1997). Consequently, the attempt of eradicating one pest 
created another which stands out as an example that dealing with non-native species must be 
handled carefully.  
In the Baltic Sea, around 100 – 118 species have been identified as non-indigenous (AquaNIS 
2015; HELCOM 2012). Around 90 species are established, with the south-western Baltic Sea 
being the area with the highest number of NIS. There are three main water bodies serving as source 
for those NIS: the North American east, eastern Asia and the Ponto Caspian region (Olenin et al. 
2017). One of those NIS from the Ponto Caspian region is the round goby (Neogobius 
melanostomus) (Pallas 1814). This fish species originates from the Black Sea and the Sea of Azov 
and has spread into various fresh and brackish water bodies around the globe. It has invaded the 
Laurentian Great Lakes in North America, such as like Lake Erie and connecting streams and 
rivers (Charlebois et al. 2001; Corkum et al. 2004). In the 1990s, the species was observed for the 
first time in the Baltic Sea, more specifically in the Gulf of Gdansk (Figure 1-1) (Sapota and Skóra 
2005), where it established a dense population and later spread into adjacent areas (Azour et al. 
2015; Kotta et al. 2015; Sapota & Skóra 2005). It was suggestively introduced in ballast water in 
ships to the Gulf of Gdansk (Kotta et al. 2015). Nowadays, it is common for small-scale fishermen 
to find round goby in their nets and traps as by-catch in their coastal fishing activities (Ojaveer 
2006). The numbers of round goby are increasing, posing both a risk for the local environment and 
biodiversity. It competes for space with indigenous fish (Balshine et al. 2005; Karlson et al. 2007) 
as well as feeds on local species heavily (Barton et al. 2005). Moreover, local fishermen experience 
a loss in income since fishing their target fish due to enormous amounts of round goby. 
The round goby is commercially harvested around the Black Sea (Jude et al. 1992; Kornis et al. 
2012) and also some Baltic Sea fishermen enjoy eating the species when it enters their gear as by-
catch (Ojaveer 2006). Seeing that the round goby is threatening the Baltic Sea ecosystem and that 
managing it – or any other aquatic invasive species - to eradication is likely deemed to be 
unsuccessful (Ojaveer et al. 2015), this study proposes a different approach to the subject.

This thesis aims to lay down the first steps for a commercial fishery on the round goby to first and 
foremost manage the round goby stock sustainably but also to establish a new fishery for the small-
scale coastal fishermen and thereby introduce a new income source for them.

importance of fish as source of essential nutritional components. Furthermore, examples of already 
commercially exploited invasive species are given and descriptions of small-scale fisheries is 
provided, since one or several of these types of fisheries will most likely be the best way to target 
round goby in the Baltic Sea.

1.1.1. ROUND GOBY  
The round goby belongs to the family of Gobiide, the second largest teleost family after the 
Cyprinida, and it is native to the Caspian Sea and the Black Sea. The fish has a demersal lifestyle 
and can reach a length of up to 29 cm with the distinction of males being larger than females  (Berg 
1965; Sokołowska & Fey 2011). There is a large variety in colour, ranging from brownish to 
greenish or greyish colour (figure 1-3) with males being often darker than their female 
counterparts, especially during spawning (Kornis et al. 2012; Olenin et al. 2017).  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
They are well distinguishable due to their black spots at the base of the first dorsal fin and their 
large head as well as the disk-formed suctorial fin (Olenin et al. 2017). Originating from brackish 
water (Berg 1965), it is also found in freshwater systems (Borcherding et al. 2011; Jude et al. 1992) 
and intermediated salinities (Kotta et al. 2015) and is suspected to have the potential to establish a 
population in the oceanic environment (Behrens et al. 2017). In general the round goby can adapt 
to a wide range of habitats (Charlebois et al. 2001). The round goby prefers stone and gravel as 
substrate but can also live on sandy bottom. For nesting it is essential to have a hide-out place 
hence a rocky substrate is preferred (Charlebois et al. 2001; Sapota & Skóra 2005). The nesting 
habitat is dominated by aggressive males looking after the eggs. One nest can be shared by several 
females (Olenin et al. 2017).

Female round gobies attain maturity at the age of two years while their male counterparts reach it 
at the age of three. Round gobies spawn multiple times a season, depending on the location. The 
spawning season can vary from April (Black Sea) to September (Varna) and the spawning depth 
range from the 0.2 – to 1.5 m (Berg 1965; Charlebois et al. 2001; Meunier et al. 2009; Miller & 
Hureau 1986; Olenin et al. 2017). Male round gobies have an aggressive nesting behaviour, they 
not only prepare the nest but also guard it (Berg 1965; Meunier et al. 2009).  
Further, round gobies are territorial fish with a low home range, 5 ± 1.2 m for example in the Great 
Lakes (Ray & Corkum 2001). After settlement, they stay and reproduce at the same location until 
the reproduction rate surpasses the carrying capacity of that specific area. At that point, 
intraspecific competition emerges, and the small individuals migrate to less populated areas with 
less competition and predation pressure (Ray & Corkum 2001; Thorlacius & Brodin 2018). 
Moreover, asocial behaviour is suggested to respond to the dispersal, not only smaller but asocial 
individuals are the one migrating to other areas (Thorlacius & Brodin 2018). 
Round gobies can be described as benthic and opportunistic feeders, it preys on all benthic 
organisms when available. In the Baltic Sea, their diet is dominated by molluscs, mainly bivalves, 
polychaetes and crustaceans (Järv et al. 2011; Nurkse et al. 2016; S. et al. 2012; Skabeikis & 
Lesutiene 2015; Skora & Rzeznik 2001). A preference in diet can be observed between larger and 
bigger specimens. Smaller round gobies (2 to 5 cm) chose polychaetes and crustaceans over 
molluscs as well as some zooplankton. Medium (5 to 10 cm) and large (10 to 20 cm) individuals 
predominantly prey on molluscs such Mytilus trossulus or Macoma balthica (Skabeikis & 
Lesutiene 2015; Skora & Rzeznik 2001). The feeding rates of this non-native species in the Baltic 
Sea are very high which poses a reasonable risk for the benthic community. Moreover, there is a 
diet overlap between round goby and European flounder (Platichtys flesus) putting this species to 
risk. However, round goby does not only prey on organism, for some species it is food source as 
well. In the Gulf of Gdansk, round gobies are chased by cod (Gadus morhua) and European perch 
(Perca fluviatilis). Moreover, diet of top piscivorous predators, fish and birds such cod or turbot 
(Sapota & Skóra 2005) and as the great cormorant (Phalacrocorax carbo) have shifted and round 
goby is now a substantial part of their prey (Rakauskas et al. 2013).

1.1.2. FISH QUALITY  
Fish and seafood are traditional global resources for nutrition and livelihoods. The global fish 
supply per capita was 20 kg in 2014. In fact, 146.3 million tonnes of fish were produced just for 
human consumption in 2014 by capture fisheries and aquaculture (FAO 2016). It is a source of 
high quality protein and highly valued for its health benefits associated with fish protein, lipids 
and its high content of minerals and vitamins. Moreover, it is an excellent source for minerals such 
as calcium and phosphorus as well as selenium and iodine, if it is a marine fish (Huss 1995; Nunes 
et al. 2010). Proteins make up most of fish meat and the content range from 10 to 25 % 
corresponding to 80 to 90 % of the energy provided by 100 g of a lean fish species (Huss 1995; 
Nunes et al. 2006). Fish protein is of special value for human consumption due to its high 
digestibility and the high amount of essential amino acids. The proteins are of significance for the 
growth and development of the human body as to produce enzymes and hormones (Nunes et al. 
2010). 
Lipids are essential in storage and transportation of energy through the body. Fish generally have 
less fat than meat, ranging from 0.2 to 30 % often stored in the liver and fish lipids is an excellent 
source for the long-chain omega 3 unsaturated fatty acids eicosapentaenoic (EPA, 20:5 n-3) and 
docosahexaenoic acid (DHA, 22:6 n-3). These fatty acids are mainly found in marine fish and were 
accumulated through the marine food web. They are essential for the human body and can prevent 
cardiovascular disease, regulate blood pressure and can have positive effect on cancer when fish 
is regularly eaten. In general, the fattier the fish the higher the amount of those omega-3 fatty acids. 
Fatty species have around 10 % such as the Atlantic Salmon, lean species have fat content below 
1 % (cod or hake) and intermediate species such as trout or sea bass have a range of 5 to 10 % fat 
(Nunes et al. 2010).  
However, the amount and concentration of lipids, proteins, minerals and vitamins can change per 
species, season and sex. Especially the difference in lipids and proteins of lean fish and fatty fish 
is large. Lean fish such as cod and other benthic fish have in general less lipids and store them in 
the liver while fatty fish are often pelagic species and store the lipids in fatty tissues. In addition, 
the lipid content of lean fish is often lower and more stable throughout the year as it changes in 
fatty species. Further, the composition of fish meat is highly depending on the feed intake. Some 
fish might feed less during spawning or migration period or just because of a shortcoming of prey 
resulting in a change of lipid and protein concentration.  
==PAGE:16==
7 
 
During those periods, fish would use proteins or lipids as energy reserves. After a period of 
starvation, protein will be the first component increasing again but very slow. Afterwards, lipid 
will rapidly accumulate in the fish tissue (Huss 1995). Considering the nutritional benefits of fish 
and seafood, it is recommended to consume at least two fish meals a week (Nunes et al. 2010). 
1.1.3. NIS COMMERCIALLY USED  
Per definition, IAS have an adverse effect on the ecosystem they invaded, and eradication is 
often unsuccessful. However, humans are opportunistic and there are examples where these 
intruders became a new valuable resource for economic exploitation. 
One of those species is the red king crab (Paralithodes camtschaticus). In the 1960’s it was 
intentionally introduced to the Bering Sea and the Murmansk area and hoped to become the source 
for successful economical valuable fisheries (Falk-Petersen et al. 2011; Hjelset 2014; Orlov & 
Ivanov 1978). From these points of introduction, it spread further and the first crabs in Norwegian 
waters were caught in Varanger 1977. In 2002, a commercial fishery was established in Norway, 
based on a quota system to benefit those fishermen who suffered economically from the invasive 
species due to by-catch and destroyed gear. The fishery is of high value ranging around 90 million 
Norwegian Kroner (app. 9.3 million Euro) in 2003 and it now seems that local fishermen 
economically depend on this species (Wessel 2004). 
Another example of intentionally introduced alien species for commercial exploitation is the 
pacific oyster (Crassostra gigas) (Thunberg 1793). In Europe, it was introduced on several 
occasions for aquaculture proposes: in France and the Netherlands and later in Scandinavia. From 
the aquaculture facility it spread into the wild and now has established itself in the Danish part of 
the Wadden Sea and Limfjord (Reise 1998), as well as in Sweden and Norway where it was not 
introduced intentionally (Dolmer et al. 2014; Wrange et al. 2010). Hence, the former intentionally 
introduced species has become invasive in new areas. In those areas in Denmark it is legal to 
handpick the pacific oyster which locally can assist in reducing the numbers, but it is not 
commercially exploited as such.  
 
 
==PAGE:17==
8 
 
However, the IAS is adding value to the local community by attracting an increasing number of 
foraging tourists to those locations, who love to pick oyster themselves (Hjalager et al. 2018). In 
the Netherlands, a in 2012 Marine Stewardship Council (MSC) certified fishery has established to 
exploit the commercial value and to control the numbers of the invasive species (Dolmer et al. 
2014). In Sweden, there are raising voices interested in establishing a cost-efficient commercial 
fisheries of the wild oysters (Hanstén 2017).  
The invasion of lionfish (Pterois volitans/miles complex) into the western Atlantic Ocean, 
alongside south Florida, the southeast USA and the Caribbean paints a similar picture as the round 
goby invasion in the Baltic sea. While the round goby was likely introduced by shipping, the 
lionfish, once a valuable ornamental fish, was possible brought to the western Atlantic Ocean by 
aquarium trade and escaped from those aquarium tanks (Hare & Whitfield 2003; Morris Jr & 
Whitfield 2009). The first observations were recorded in south Florida in 1985 (Schofield 2009). 
Lionfish is a highly potential invader due to high fecundity, high initial growth rates and the ability 
to survive periods with low availability of prey (Fishelson 1997; Freshwater et al. 2009; Johnston 
& Purkis 2011). Since its introduction, the management focusses on preventing further spread and 
eradication of this species. Education of locals and culling as well as spearfishing are currently 
used to reduce the number of fish, without having a great impact of abundance (Gallagher 2013; 
Morris Jr & Whitfield 2009). There is no commercial fishery of lionfish at this point, but its 
potential is seen since the fish is regarded as delicious. However, fishing that species is difficult 
and either spears, or nets must be utilized per hand (Karp & Wyatt 2017).  
1.1.4. SMALL-SCALE FISHERIES 
Fisheries in general can be divided into the large industrial fleets, the large-scale fleet (LSF) and 
the small-scale fishing fleet (SSF). A SSF vessel does not exceed the total length of 12 m (EC 
2006) and participates in near shore coastal fishing, often with passive gear. In Europe, 84 % of 
the fishing fleet is considered as SFF with around 100 000 people directly employed in the sector 
(Guyader et al. 2013). Its contribution can therefore not be disregarded and its importance for small 
coastal and rural area is enormous. Regionally SSF as significant in regards of employment and 
locally it is the economical backbone for some communities. 
 
 
==PAGE:18==
9 
 
Moreover, the activity of SSF represents and enhances the attachment of people to the coastal areas 
and promote social stability (Guyader et al. 2013). Compared to LFS the quality of the fish is often 
better. The catch can be caught intact and fishermen can often fish more selective. However, there 
are some infrastructural problems at ports where lack of direct processing facilities and storage 
reduce the quality of the fish. Still, small-scale fisher often generates high marketing values for 
their products than the industrial fleets.  
However, SSF poses a good alternative for the large industrial fleets, which promotes over-
exploitation of the stocks (EC 2009) in regards of environmental friendly fishing since less fuel is 
used, selectivity can be improved and more and diverse fish can be targeted. Therefore, SSF can 
be a lucrative and striking activity in coastal areas in Europe, but competition along the coast and 
uncertainty in stocks poses risks (Guyader et al. 2013).  
In Denmark, there are 1906 vessels categorized as SSF, contributing to 84 % to the Danish fishing 
fleet in total (The Danish AgriFish Agency 2016). But the situation is changing and according to 
the Danish Fishermen’s Association the Danish coastal fishing fleet is forcing challenges. The 
Danish fleet tends to invest in bigger vessel resulting in a reduce in SSF vessels and consequential 
the total number of vessel decreases. The main target species cod and plaice among sole and turbot 
seem to vanish from the Danish coast reducing the number of valuable species. Also, smaller 
coastal harbours are closing, making coastal fisheries impossible in those areas (Lund 2013). 
Consequently, a boost of the SSF in Denmark would be desirable and opening a new resource for 
the market, potentially the round goby, can be beneficial for Danish coastal, rural areas (Lund 
2013).

1.2. RESEARCH AIM  
Seeing that the numbers of round goby in the Baltic Sea are increasing (Azour et al. 2015; Nurkse 
et al. 2016; Rakauskas et al. 2013) and that it is an important commercially exploited species used 
as fresh fish or as canned product in the Bulgarian Sea (Zarev et al. 2013) there might be a potential 
to exploit this species also in Denmark. This would be beneficial for the small-scale fishermen 
along the Danish coast by providing a new income source, even more since the eel and shrimp 
fishery is hampered due to the high numbers or round gobies in some areas of the Danish coastal 
waters (personal communication). Consequently, to assess the potential for commercial round 
goby small-scale fishery the research aims of the present thesis is to (i) investigate the current 
biomass available for a potential fisheries by analysing catch-data from Guldborgsund, Kalvehave 
and Karrebæksminde; (ii) evaluate the quality of round goby meat concerning the chemical 
composition and its variation over the season and (iii) to incorporate the experience of local 
fishermen about round goby fisheries in those particular areas.

2. MATERIALS AND METHODS 
The assessment potential of commercial fisheries on round goby in Danish coastal waters was 
based on four different datasets: (1) survey data from Guldborgsund, Kalvehavesund and 
Karrebæksminde (see figure 2-1), (2) by-catch data from the same areas, (3) fish meat quality data 
from fish sampled in Guldborg and (4) knowledge and experience gathered via interviews with 
local fishermen from the designated areas. In the following, the methodological approaches for 
this study are explained.

2.1. SURVEY DATA 
The survey for collecting round goby started on the first day of April 2017 and lasted until 
December 1st or until no round gobies had be caught and was conducted by selected local fishermen 
on behalf of DTU Aqua. The fishermen were asked to collect fish once a month with a minimum 
sample size of 200 fish per month. After collection the fish were packed in plastic bags, labelled 
with previously prepared labels from DTU, noting down date and location, and were frozen as 
soon as possible. The fish were not sorted, since all sizes were interesting. After the collection of 
fish had been terminated, the samples were analysed at DTU Aqua. In total 3707 fish where caught 
(table 2-1) and all were measured for length to the nearest centimetre after thawing. The fish were 
then sorted into centimetre intervals and 10, or if there were less than 10 all, fish from that interval, 
were prepared for further analysis. Those fish were used for weight assessment to the nearest 0.1 
grams with the help of an electronic scale. Sex was determined by visual inspection of the fish. In 
Guldborg, samples for April, May, June, September, October and November, where caught while 
in Kalvehave all months from April until November had been sampled. In Karrebæksminde, no 
fish in May had been caught.

2.3.3. FATTY ACID EXTRACTION 
The lipid content was assessed by determining the fatty acid methyl esters (FAME), applying gas 
liquid chromatography. All fatty acids in the triglycerides, phospholipids and free fatty acids were 
converted into FAME following the method described in Analysis Instruction no. 122.01 from 
DTU FOOD. Hereby, a one-step transmethylation was used. Via an acid catalysed process (boron 
trifluoride, BF3), bound and free fatty acids were transesterified to FAME in one step. The process 
is accelerated during a short heating using microwaves. A mixture of heptane, methanol and 
toluene is used to ensure solubility and thus conversion of both neutral and polar lipids.  
Afterwards, the fatty acid methyl esters (FAME) had been determined using gas-liquid 
chromatography adhering to the Analysis Instruction no. 105.03 (11.1) from DTU Food (DTU 
Food 2018). Hereby, the FAMEs were separated on a highly polar stationary phase and the fatty 
acids were identified by external standards. The gas chromatography system HP-7890 A from 
Agilent Technologies was used. The samples were then analysed by evaluation of the 
chromatogram and the results were calculated applying the FAME C23 standard. Even if all 
essential fatty acids were analysed and its concentration measured, only Palmitic Acid, 
Eicosapentaenoic Acid (also known as EPA) and Docosahexaenoic Acid (DHA) were used for this 
thesis. EPA and DHA are the commonly known essential polyunsaturated omega-3 fatty acids 
(PUFA n-3) as they are associated with health benefits and a good nutritional value (Nunes et al. 
2010). Palmitic Acid is saturated fatty acid and also associated to health benefits (Nunes et al. 
2010; WHO 2002).

3.5.1. EXPERIENCE  
All three fishermen testified a large amount of round goby at all areas. They experienced round 
goby in their nets and cages as by-catch and two of these fishermen even had to take measures to 
avoid catching round gobies to reach the required numbers of their actual target species. As for 
instance, in Kalvehave, the fisherman had to change his fishing grounds in spring due to massive 
amounts of gobies in his original grounds. However, after June he could return to his native fishing 
areas continue fishing on eels, his target species. At Guldborg, the fish harvester experience round 
gobies in his shrimp and eel traps and installed a net with smaller mesh size in front of those traps 
to avoid gobies swimming into the traps and feeding on the shrimps. Moreover, in Guldborg less 
shrimps have been seen since the appearance of round gobies as well as the reduction of mullet 
numbers for the last four years (2014-2018) until catches decreased to zero.  
Besides this rather negative experience, it was also reported, especially at Kalvehave, that after the 
seeing round gobies for the first time in 2010, larger eels have been caught. Regarding variations 
of round goby numbers throughout the year, all fishermen noticed fewer gobies with warmer 
seawater temperature. One fisherman, in Karrebæksminde, experienced smaller quantity of gobies 
this spring.  
3.5.2. POTENTIAL FOR A COMMERCIAL FISHERY 
The fishermen had a strong opinion on the possibility of fishing round goby commercially. All feel 
that the numbers are strong and that they will be strong in future and that those numbers justify the 
potential for a commercial fishery. In Kalvehave, the fishman indicated that three boats could catch 
up to 500 kg per day or 50 t in a season.  
When asked which gear the fishermen would use for fishing the gobies, all agreed on standard eel 
fyke nets with a mesh size of 13 mm in the front, continuing with 17 mm, and concluding with 10 
mm and 11 mm at the end of the fyke net. They already catch round gobies with these nets easily 
and using already utilized gear would indicate a low investment cost for fish harvester. Further, 
according to their experience, the best time to harvest round gobies is in spring time and in a depth 
of 5 to 7 m.  
 
 
==PAGE:36==

However, the potential for a commercial fishery is to some degree limited according to the 
fishermen. Especially in Karrebæksminde and in Kalvehave the infrastructure possibilities for 
distribution of the catches barely exists. Also, the fishermen do not see the goby as source for 
fillets but rather as a resource used in fish meal production. Further, the fish harvester would like 
a price of 7 to 12 DKK per kilogram of round goby to secure a stable income. 
4. DISCUSSION 
This study provides information on monthly length and condition, catchability, sex ratio, the fish 
meat quality and experience of fishing of the NIS round goby. All information together indicate 
that a commercial exploitation of the species could be viable. In Bulgaria, gobies are an established 
commercial value. They are an important species for commercial fisheries and either used in the 
canning industry but are also sold fresh on the markets. Fishermen do not specify the species hence 
specific numbers for round gobies cannot be given but catches of gobies ranged from 26 to 125 
tonnes per year in 2003 to 2011 (Zarev et al. 2013). Another indication of interest in utilizing round 
gobies for commercial purpose is a study by Melvere et al (2017). They investigated the potential 
of round gobies processing waste in Latvia. This study touches upon the potential of round gobies 
in commercial fisheries as planned by the Latvian government but do not further elaborate on that 
matter. However, they state that catches of round gobies are high, about 1000 tons in total since 
its introduction in the 1990’s (Melvere et al. 2017). Accordingly, round goby is of some interest 
in countries of its origin as well as in Baltic countries were round gobies are invasive. In the 
following, an outlook of the potential for a commercial round goby fishery is discussed considering 
its nutritional value and potential food application as well as fisheries specific information.

4.1. NUTRITIONAL VALUE FOR HUMAN CONSUMPTION OF ROUND 
GOBY  
The quality parameters are linked to the possible application in food production and to the 
nutritional value for human consumption. In general results of this study regarding fat and protein 
content is in line with another which has found mean fat content of 0,67 ± 0,07% and mean protein 
content of 16.6±0.40%  for round gobies in the Baltic Sea caught in April (Melvere et al. 2017). 
The mean values of this study vary slightly (0.81 ± 0.14 fat, 17.28 ± 1.29 protein) since data from 
four months instead of only one were used.  Hence, the round goby is a rather lean fish with a 
higher protein content and a stable concentration of long-chain polyunsaturated fatty acids. The 
protein content is comparable with that from cod as they average protein percentage of cod flesh 
is 16 – 19 %. The lipid content is slightly higher in round gobies compared to cod (0-0.4 % in cod) 
(Waterman 2001). Compared to cod, EPA and DHA content (the most important PUFAs) of gobies 
was low, as a study by Strobel et al. (2012) shows mean value of 48.8 (± 3.4) for EPA and DHA 
from total fatty acids (Strobel et al. 2012). Beef for instance has only a PUFA content of 5% 
(Guyon et al. 2016).  
The chemical composition of fish depends on the food intake and the season (Aidos et al. 2002; 
Huss 1995; Skabeikis & Lesutiene 2015). Especially, the lipid content of fish is varying and 
consequently the content of essential fatty acids such as EPA and DHA also changes as diverse 
studies for different species showed (Aidos et al. 2002; Aro et al. 2000; Gladyshev et al. 2009). 
Skabeikis and Lesutiene (2015) found that the main spawning period for round goby in the SE 
Baltic Sea is in July and August, advanced by pre-spawning time in May and June and followed 
by the post-spawning period stage in September and October. In regards of spawning, fish gather 
energy and use that energy during spawning, which then shows a rapid decline in lipids and 
proteins (Love 1988). Hence, the decline of DHA in August could be in regard of the intense 
spawning season of round goby. This would also explain the decline of proteins and amino acids 
after August, since gobies would have used that energy for spawning. Further, round gobies have 
a lowered food intake during spawning and especially the males do not feed during breeding but 
increase feeding in the post-spawning period (MacInnis & Corkum 2000; Skabeikis & Lesutiene 
2015). 
 
==PAGE:38==
29 
 
In this study, the lipid content is only slightly varying and the PUFA concentration is also very 
stable throughout the season. As EPA and DHA are important fatty acids for human consumptions 
and are related to various health benefits such as cardiovascular diseases, a stable value in PUFAs 
is important. Yet, the PUFA content of gobies cannot reach the content of cod, a highly valuable 
species for human consumption but in regards of proteins both are similar.  
4.1.1. FOOD APPLICATION 
The protein content of round goby could be ideal for people who are eager to obtain a high protein-
diet such as athletes or dietary-interested people. A possible application, besides canned fish as it 
is used already done in the Bulgarian Black Sea (Zarev et al. 2013), could be fish protein powder. 
This product has wide range of possible utilization in food industries as for instance as a binder or 
food supplement to increase the protein content or others (Chung et al. 2000; Pires et al. 2012; 
Ramirez et al. 1999; Shaviklo 2015). White fish with a low lipid content is a good resource to 
produce fish protein powder (Hultin et al. 2005; Park & Lin 2005), classifying round goby as a 
good species for such application. Further, this and other studies showed that round goby are larger 
compared to their relatives in the Black Sea (Mierzwicka 2000; Pliszka 2002; Skora & Stolarski 
1996; Sokołowska & Fey 2011). This holds the potential for actual filleting of round gobies.  
4.2. FISHERIES POTENTIAL 
This study provides information on monthly length and condition, catchability, sex ratio, the fish 
meat quality and experience of fishing of the NIS round goby.  
4.2.1. LENGTH AND CONDITION REGARDING FISHERY  
Kalvehave revealed the largest fish both by length and by weight in this study but with the SE 
Baltic with Melvere et al. (2017), who was assessing the potential use of processing round goby 
residues (waste) for human consumption showed that round gobies were larger there (mean length 
19.5 cm and mean weight 77.5 g) (Melvere et al. 2017). Respectively, as several studies indicates 
that round goby grow larger in marine environments compared to fresh waters systems. These 
studies found a range of maximum length of 18.2 cm to 23.5 cm (Mierzwicka 2000; Pliszka 2002; 
Skora & Stolarski 1996; Sokołowska & Fey 2011). However, if the studies showed smaller length 
than other placed on the Baltic Sea, the maximum length of round gobies was higher than of its 
relatives in the Ponto-Caspian area where they range between 13 cm and 19 cm of maximum length 
(Apanasenko 1973; Macun 2018; Nikolskii 1963).  
==PAGE:39==
30 
 
This can be seen as an opportunity for commercial exploitation in regards of human consumption 
as it might be easier to extract fillets from larger gobies. However, if a fisherman wanted to target 
larger fish within Danish coastal waters, the best season would be the spring and summer months, 
a least for Kalvehavesund and Karrebæksminde as no data for the summer months was available 
for Guldborgsund. 
Moreover, larger lengths and higher weight in round goby is often linked to invasion fronts while 
smaller individuals is connected to establishes sites as several researches have shown in the Baltic 
Sea (Azour et al. 2015; Corkum et al. 2004), the Danube river system (Brandner et al. 2013) and 
the great lakes (Gutowsky & Fox 2011). This could hint at a more established population at all 
three studied sites, which is backed up by the information provided by all interviewed fishermen. 
According to them, round goby has been seen for more than 8 years now. Moreover, Azour et al 
(2015) addressed Guldborg already as an established side with a density of 1.9 round goby m−2 
in 2013.  
The condition factor shows how fit individuals are in regard of length and weight. In this study 
not, much difference between the areas was detected; however, within, months and sex showed 
different conditions. As the condition factor is directly connected to weight and weight is to feeding 
and the breeding cycle, it could be assumed that the decrease of condition is related to the loss in 
weight due to the spawning and breeding season, which is assumed to be peaking in July and 
August (Skabeikis & Lesutiene 2015). During that period of time, male and female round gobies 
concentrate on spawning and breeding and reduce the feeding activity drastically (MacInnis & 
Corkum 2000; Skabeikis & Lesutiene 2015). Hence the weigh decreased in June, July and weight 
and accordingly K declined. After the spawning period, round gobies continue feeding and the 
condition increased again (Skabeikis & Lesutiene 2015). As the highest condition factor was 
detected in Kalvehave in Autumn and the same but less detectable pattern was visible in 
Karrebæksminde. The condition was higher in spring and autumn than in summer and thus it could 
be considered to establish a round goby fishery in spring and autumn. Considering the condition 
factor and the length, fisheries would be most viable in spring. 
4.2.2. GENDER OF ROUND GOBY AND FISHERIES 
All three sides where dominated by male round gobies, which is also an indicator for an established 
round goby stock. This is supported by Brander et al. (2013) and others (Gruľa et al. 2012; 
==PAGE:40==
31 
 
Gutowsky & Fox 2012). This is important as established sites indicate a secure resource for a 
fishing industry. Further, as make were larger than females, a male dominant stock could be an 
asset when fisheries are aiming for larger species for human consumption.  
4.2.3. CATCHES OF ROUND GOBY  
In accordance with the statements of the interview partner, the CPUE was high. The best months 
to catch round goby is May for all areas whereas April shows also high numbers for Guldborgsund. 
The variation in catches depends on the season and spawning times of the round goby. The pre-
spawning period for round goby is in May and June, followed by the peak spawning time in July 
and August and a post-spawning period in September and October. Additionally, round goby 
possibly aggregate around May and June, while males prepare the nest and female swim around 
to find the nests (Meunier et al. 2009). In July and August, round gobies are rather stationary and 
immobile as the males guard the nests and the female spawn (Meunier et al. 2009; Skabeikis & 
Lesutiene 2015).  Besides that phase, a laboratory study has shown that male round goby are more 
active during the spawning season (Marentette et al. 2011) which might explain dominance of 
male round goby in the catches additionally.  
 
 
==PAGE:41==
32 
 
4.3. OUTLOOK ON COMMERCIAL FISHERIES 
As described earlier, fishmen are pressured by the sheer number of round gobies in their eel or 
shrimp fyke nets. A utilization of this by-catch would solve the problem of a financial loss for 
those fishermen and could possibly lift the burden caused by round goby for the ecosystem. The 
quality value of round gobies from the Danish coast show that they have a high protein content 
and low lipid concentration. The little size of the gobies supports the opinion of the fishermen that 
a commercial fishery should focus on other utilization than fish fillets even if round gobies are 
found on Bulgarian fish markets, offered as whole (Zarev et al. 2013) as for instance fish protein 
powder. However, the production of fish protein powder requires a good raw material quality 
which indicates good handling direct after the catch such as freezing (Shaviklo 2015). As 
described, some harbours as for instance Karrebæksminde do neither have access to sophisticated 
storage and freezing facilities nor to means for large transportation since the harbour is used for 
local food production and not globalized food production with large quantities of fish. Further, 
prices for fish protein powder are difficult to anticipate (Shaviklo 2015) which makes a price 
forecast for a kilogram of round goby for the fishermen unreliable. The demand made by fishermen 
in this study to get a price around 7 DKK per kilo might not be met, decreasing the attractivity of 
fishing for round gobies. However, since little investment is necessary it could still be a possible 
side-income. Furthermore, the ecosystem shifts in for example Kalvehave, where round goby has 
intruded a habitat formerly dominated by eel for a certain amount of time. After some time, the 
goby disappears, and eel comes back. This shift can be used to effortlessly catch gobies without 
having a lack of income until the eel reappears. Moreover, the valuable western Baltic cod is 
declining and the stock seem to be at risk (Hamrén 2017). In 2018 and in 2017 a low quota for the 
western stock was issued by the EU (of 5 597 metric tons) (EC 2017), a cut of 56 % compared to 
2016 (Cutlip 2016). This means a drawback for fishermen in the western Baltic and consequently 
also for Danish fishermen.  
 
 
==PAGE:42==
33 
 
This setback could be compensated by round goby fishing, as the effort is relatively low, and the 
catches can be high. Moreover, the eel season, an important fish species for local fishermen does 
only partially overlap with the best time for fishing round gobies. The eel season starts in April 
and runs until October but the peak is in the late summer months (Fiskeridirektoratet 2008). 
Accordingly, round goby fisheries could be done supplementary to a commercial eel fishery.  
 
4.4. LIMITATION AND WEAKNESSES 
This study has been conducted on the basis of sampled data from 2017. This sampling only 
occurred once. For further validation of the findings more sampling of different years should be 
done to also further assess the biomass of round gobies. Especially, data for Guldborg in July and 
August as well as for May in Karrebæksminde should be collected to increase the comparability 
of the season and to complete the year-round picture.  
Moreover, a good knowledge of the Danish language would have been beneficial for the interview 
to effectively talk to the fishermen. It was helpful to have a Danish speaking assistance, but this 
resulted in fragmentation of the interviews. Further, more stakeholder should contribute to this 
topic, to get a broader insight into the situation. Not only fishermen but also processing companies, 
academics in food and fisheries science as well as food producers should have been included to 
fully access the feasibility of a commercial round goby fishing industry.

5. CONCLUSION 
This thesis focusses on a potential commercial exploitation of round goby in Danish coastal waters 
by analysing catch and fish quality data and by incorporating the experience of local fishermen. It 
became clear that round gobies harbour a good potential as fisheries resource as catches can be 
high, especially in Spring time. This would be a benefit for local fishermen who target eels. The 
same nets can be used and the time, just before eel season starts, can be used to fish for gobies in 
high amounts. The available round goby biomass seems to be male dominated and ranges between 
a length from 10 to 15 cm with a maximum of 22 cm, which indicates an established stock. Hence, 
the gobies can stand for long-term resource. This is of importance as the other fish stocks decline, 
for example the western Baltic cod stock. Accordingly, it could be an additional income. However, 
the price for per kilogram gobies should be adequate for the fishermen, as the effort otherwise 
would not be acceptable. Also, a drawback for a commercial fishery is the lacking infrastructure 
in local harbours. As gobies are a coastal species, coastal fishermen would be the ones to establish 
a fishery. Though, often coastal fishermen fish for local demand and do not always transport the 
catch to a larger processing facility. Accordingly, the cool chains are missing in smaller harbours 
which currently decreases the potential of large-scale exploitation of the round goby at the Danish 
Baltic coast. Moreover, the feasibility of marketing the round goby for Danish consumer was not 
assessed in this study. Hence, it is not known if and how the Danish market would react to a new 
species. However, it could be labelled in a way that enhance the benefits of consuming round 
gobies. For instance, it could be promoted as sustainably caught, beneficial for the local 
environment and habitat as the consumption decreases the number of an invasive species and as it 
is locally produced. Further, the high protein content of gobies could be labelled as beneficial for 
people doing sports or being on a diet, such as the low-carb diet. However, for this assessment 
further research must be conducted alongside with research on possible applications in food and 
food processing.
