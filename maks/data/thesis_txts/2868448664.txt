Abstract
This thesis presents a meta-analysis of Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) studies on biobased Sustain-
able Aviation Fuels (SAFs), focusing on the environmental impacts of various production pathways
and feedstocks. The aviation industry’s significant contribution to global greenhouse gas emissions
underscores the urgency of developing SAF to mitigate these impacts, as they offer a promising so-
lution, providing a renewable alternative to conventional jet fuels.
This study compiles and harmonizes Life Cycle Inventory (LCI) data collected after a scientific litera-
ture review, ensuring comparability across studies and characterizing the uncertainty on the harmo-
nized parameters. It provides a detailed analysis of the environmental performance of SAF, consid-
ering multiple impact categories using the ImpactWorld+ method for the assessment. The study also
explores the specific challenges and opportunities associated with implementing these technologies
in different geographical contexts.
Two case studies are presented: one comparing various SAF production pathways in Brazil and an-
other evaluating the impact of geographical variations on the Alcohol-to-Jet (ATJ) pathway. The LCA
studies were conducted using Brightway software, and include sensitivity and uncertainty analyses to
ensure the robustness of the results.
Key findings highlight that while SAF can significantly reduce greenhouse gas emissions, they may
also present higher risks in other environmental areas. The Hydrotreated Esters and Fatty Acids
(HEFA) pathway using oil residues, such as used cooking oil, demonstrates the lowest environmental
impacts. In contrast, pathways utilizing agricultural residues and energy crops exhibit higher burdens
due to significant land transformation and water requirements. Moreover, the findings from the sensi-
tivity on choice of allocation method show it has a significant influence on the results, and could alter
the recommendations of the LCAs.
To conclude, the study emphasizes the importance of optimizing conversion processes and consid-
ering the geographical context of SAF production. The limitations of the study are discussed, includ-
ing the type of feedstocks assessed, lack of characterization factors for non-CO2 impacts of SAF
combustion, assumptions on energy integration, and data availability and representativeness. Rec-
ommendations are derived both for SAF producers and LCA practitioners. Finally, indications for
future research and policy development are provided, highlighting the need for continued exploration
of SAF technologies, integration of environmental and techno-economic assessments, to enhance
the feasibility and adoption of SAF.

Introduction
Commercial aviation is the principal and most rapid worldwide transportation method, and it relies
on fossil fuels for operation. Reportedly, aviation emits 2.4% of all human-induced CO2 emissions
and 4% of total anthropogenic global warming [1]. The traffic forecast expects to have a 3.1% com-
pound annual growth for the period 2019-2050 [2]. If technology developments are conservative and
current rates towards decarbonization remain, this will lead to emissions of 2000 Mt CO2, which is
over double the emissions levels of 2019. Even in future scenarios with aggressive aspirational tech-
nology development to introduce electric, hybrid, and zero-emissions (H2) aircraft in the 2035-2040
timeframe, Sustainable Aviation Fuels (SAFs) still represent over 50% of the emissions reductions to
reach net zero by 2050 [2]. Moreover, the 2022 feasibility report from the International Civil Aviation
Organization (ICAO) for long-term aspirational goals indicates that SAF will be the largest contributor
to CO2 emissions reductions. However, in 2023, SAF represented only 0.2% of the fuel used by the
industry [3], which indicates there are still challenges in the economic and environmental aspects of
these technologies, as well as on the policy and market landscapes [4].
ICAO defines SAF as renewable or waste-derived aviation fuels that meet sustainability criteria.
These criteria cover several categories, such as greenhouse gas (GHG) reductions, ensuring food
security, minimizing impact on biodiversity, and availability of water and land resources. These fuels
are usually referred to as drop-in fuels because no modifications to aircraft systems and engines, or
different airport infrastructure are required.[5]
Biobased SAF entail several production pathways and sources for biogenic carbon, and this portfolio
aspect must be leveraged as no single alternative can cover the total global fuel demand [6]. The
first-generation biofuels are derived from edible materials, and therefore some governments have
ceased their production due to their potential impacts on food safety [7]. Second-generation biofuels
are produced from non-edible crops and lignocellulose biomass, and third-generation aviation fuels
are based on algal feedstock. Even though the latter are viewed as a promising feedstock given
their oil content and low land requirements [8], their technological readiness level is still low and there
are significant challenges to scale up the technology to be able to supply enough feedstock at a
country level [7]. Depending on the type of feedstock, several conversion pathways adopt different
technologies to produce biojet fuel. Due to their potential for scale-up in the near-term, this study
focused on the production of SAF from second generation biomass.
SAF technologies are often labeled as carbon neutral [9], considering the biogenic carbon contained
in the biomass feedstock. However, when the cumulative emissions and environmental impacts are
accounted for over the complete life cycle of the biofuels, these could potentially be unsustainable
[9]. To assess the impact of these technologies, Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is commonly used as
an ISO-standardized decision-support methodology to quantify the human health and environmental
impacts of systems with a life-cycle perspective. The LCA framework consists of four phases: Goal
and scope definition, Inventory analysis, Impact assessment, and Interpretation. These are iterated
on while conducting an LCA. Particularly, during the Inventory Analysis phase of an LCA, the Life Cycle
Inventory (LCI) is produced as a list of elementary flows crossing the system boundaries [10], which
includes resources extracted from the environment (material and energy carriers) and any emissions
and effluents.
Although the reviewed studies of SAF environmental performance follow the LCA methodology to per-
form their environmental assessments, the range in reported LCA results is very large, from -115 g
CO2 eq/MJ to 173 g CO2 eq/MJ for 2nd generation biomass, and even larger for 3rd generation feed-
stocks, from -2400 g co2 eq/MJ to 2880 g CO2 eq/MJ [11]. Differences in assumptions and method-
ological choices contribute to the reported range. Similarly, Kolosz, B. W. et. al [9] and Jeswani,
Harish K. et al. [11] have indicated a lack of clarity on the goal and scope definition of LCA studies
of SAF, for example on the definition of the system boundaries and the functional unit of the assess-
ment. This limits the comparisons and leads to significantly different interpretations of the results and
conclusions. Seber et al. [12] conducted a comparative assessment of several allocation methods,

the HEFA process can vary from 27.1 g CO2 eq/MJ SAF to 60.6 g CO2 eq/MJ SAF when choosing
mass or market allocation methods for the co-products, respectively.
Another factor that contributes to the large range of reported results across the literature is the quan-
tification of Land Use Change (LUC) within the LCI and the system boundaries [13, 14]. The impact
of LUC varies greatly depending on the assumptions made both on the type of land converted to
produce the process feedstock, and on the crop management after conversion [15]. Seber et al. [12]
performed a comparative assessment of LUC impact for five different HEFA feedstocks, assuming
five different types of land converted, and concluded that the conversion of tropical lands to produce
feedstocks for fuel could result in GHG emissions eight times larger compared to the fossil fuel alter-
native.
Furthermore, LCAs of SAF and other biofuels are lacking in assessing environmental impact across
multiple impact categories. The studies are often limited to impacts on Climate Change of the pro-
duced fuels [9, 11, 16], motivated by governmental regulatory frameworks such as the Renewable
Energy Directive (RED) in the EU. However, to determine the environmental effects that these tech-
nologies have, it is crucial to evaluate a comprehensive set of impact categories. SAFs may reduce
GHG emissions, but could have higher impacts on other areas such as air and water pollution, human
health, resource depletion, and more. Moreover, Liu et al. [7] has reported that human toxicity poten-
tial increased by 100% in bio-jet fuel compared to fossil jet fuel due to the extensive use of fertilizers
during feedstock production. Thus, it indicates the relevance of including multiple impact categories
when assessing the sustainability of biobased fuels.
The current state of life cycle assessments of SAF show there are great uncertainties around the en-
vironmental impact of these technologies. From the literature review, it has been found that there is a
lack of harmonization of the LCIs modeling in LCAs. This process aims to align and reconcile data and
methodologies across several LCIs, ensuring consistency and comparability in LCA studies. It helps
minimize variability created by differences in data collection, system boundaries, functional unit, allo-
cation methods and modeling assumptions. Previous efforts to harmonize include the work done by
Cruce et al. [17] which focused biofuel production from algae feedstocks (3rd generation biofuel) by
harmonizing system boundaries, functional units, and parameters such as algae productivity for sev-
eral production pathways. Organizations such as the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO)
and the U.S. Department of Energy’s Argonne National Laboratory have developed comprehensive
schemes and models to assess the environmental impacts of SAF production and use. These are the
Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation (CORSIA) and the Greenhouse
gases, Regulated Emissions, and Energy use in Technologies (GREET) model, respectively. These
initiatives aim to evaluate the environmental performance of SAF pathways while promoting research
and development to achieve sustainability targets. To support these goals, harmonized inventories for
ASTM-approved pathways have been established. This study differentiates itself from these models
in two key areas. First, it focuses on assessing the uncertainties in the harmonized inventories, recog-
nizing the variability in process performance across conversion pathways, to ensure the robustness
of the results and their comparisons to fossil fuels. Second, it expands the scope of environmen-
tal impact assessment to include categories beyond climate change and energy consumption, which
are typically the primary focus of CORSIA and GREET. This broader perspective enables a more
comprehensive evaluation of SAF pathways.
The following research questions are to be addressed throughout the study: what are the harmonized
environmental impacts of SAF pathways in literature? Which are the most impactful processes, from
a life-cycle perspective, of SAF? Furthermore, how sensitive are they and what is the uncertainty of
these processes? By answering these questions, the aim is to derive recommendations for both SAF
stakeholders and LCA practitioners.

Methods
2.1
Overall methodology
The methodology used is presented in Figure 1 and detailed in the following sections; Subsection 2.2
details the systematic literature search conducted to gather relevant Life Cycle Inventory (LCI) data
from studies published between 2018 and 2024. The focus was on articles related to LCA of SAF,
ensuring they met a minimum set of criteria. Consequently, Subsection 2.3 explains the harmonization
process to ensure comparability between studies. It includes defining consistent system boundaries
and functional unit, and detailing the different life cycle stages within these systems. Emissions from
SAF combustion were also standardized, and multi-functionality was handled with energy allocation
for all the studies.
The following sections focus on the methodology used for the impact assessments. Subsection 2.4
describes the use of Impact World+ v2.0.1 Midpoint/Damage as impact assessment method to assess
environmental impacts, and Brightway2 to run the calculations. To show the possible applications of
the harmonized inventories, two case studies are presented; the first one comparing different SAF
production pathways in Brazil, and another evaluating the impact of geographical variations on a
specific pathway across several countries. Finally, the last section describes the uncertainty and
sensitivity methodology used to increase the robustness of the results and the conclusions from the
study, as well as the relevance of different modeling choices.

2.2
Scientific literature review
A systematic literature search was conducted to find representative LCI data across different feed-
stocks and downstream conversion pathways from academic and peer-reviewed studies. Given the
development of emergent technologies and to avoid outdated data, the review and collection of data
focused on articles published between 2018 and 2024.
The following query was made in the search engine Scopus:
((biofuel AND (aviation OR aircraft OR (airplane AND fuel))) OR ”jet biofuel” OR ”renewable jet fuel”
OR ”biokerosene”) AND ((life AND cycle AND assessment) OR lca OR ”environmental impact” OR
lcia OR (”life cycle” AND ”GHG emissions”))
The focus of the literature search was to extract life cycle inventories for different SAF production
pathways for later harmonization. Therefore, the LCA results of these studies were not collected, as
they had different methodological choices for co-product allocation and system boundaries.
Figure 2 shows how the systems were subdivided into life cycle stages and the main inputs collected.
The resulted studies were primarily screened for relevance to LCA of alternative aviation fuels. Conse-
quently, a second screening was conducted considering the minimum requirements listed in Table 1.
In addition, only ASTM approved or close to be approved pathways under D7566 [18] have been
considered in the study, as emerging technologies are under development and the available data is
highly uncertain.

2.3
LCI Harmonization
2.3.1
Scope Definition
To facilitate comparability between LCA studies, it is necessary to establish equal system boundaries
and functional unit for the assessments. All the studies were harmonized to well-to-wake bound-
aries, which includes all life-cycle stages of the SAF: feedstock production, collection and transport,
feedstock pre-treatment and conversion, fuel transportation and distribution and final fuel combus-
tion. Figure 2 shows the activities included under the scope of this study, as well as the relevant
co-products generated in each of them.
Functional Unit
The life-cycle assessment methodology is used to compare the environmental performance of sus-
tainable aviation fuels and fossil based jet fuel. The advantage of SAF over other energy carriers is
that they can work as drop-in fuels. Considering that these fuels would be consumed in the same
aircraft type, hence avoiding performance variations between different aircraft or engines, to use de-
livered energy as a reference value for the functional unit is a common practice [19]. Therefore, the
studies were harmonized to the following functional unit: 1 MJ of 100% Sustainable Aviation Fuel
consumed by a twin-engine (2012) single aisled aircraft in a typical short-haul flight. Single aisled
aircraft is used as reference as it is the most used globally [20, 21]. The year is indicated as the
combustion emissions data was collected from Elgowainy et al.[19], and the engine may evolve over
time.
2.3.2
Primary and generic datasets
Primary data was extracted from the LCI of the collected studies, including feedstock amounts, inputs
during conversion (i.e., electricity, heat, hydrogen, enzymes and solvents) and transport distances.
Background processes were modeled with datasets from Ecoinvent 3.9.1. As the goal is to account for
the potential environmental impacts of the specific SAF production pathways, the LCI data modeling
approach was attributional.
2.3.3
Feedstock Production
The studies were classified depending on the type of feedstock (e.g. forestry or agricultural residue).
For agricultural products and residues, inputs included farming materials and energy like fertilizers and
agricultural machinery use, whereas forestry residues included collection processes and production
of wood chips.

and lack of consensus to set a causal relation between land use and deforestation [22]. There are two
types of land use changes: direct (dLUC) and induced or indirect (iLUC). The former describes the
impact of changing the use of the same land in the analyzed system, whereas the latter includes the

2.3.4
Transport processes
Both for feedstock and fuel transport stages, the mean values from the studies that considered these
stages within their system boundaries were calculated for the harmonized inventories. Studies that
included more than one pathway or inventory were only considered once, unless the transport process
was affected. Moreover, both feedstock and fuel transport means were considered to be by heavy-
truck as it is the most common among the collected studies, and its operation is simpler as the biofuel
infrastructure is under development.
The geometric mean and standard deviation were determined from the available studies. The geo-
metric mean was then considered as the deterministic value for the inventory of the correspondent
transport processes in the LCIs.
2.3.5
Processing and Conversion
The collected studies represented different conversion pathways and different feedstocks. These
were harmonized independently of each other, considering the specific parameters, technological re-
quirements, energy and material inputs and products. Construction and decommissioning processes
and inputs for the SAF plants were not considered in the LCIs. Table 3 presents the harmonized
inventory parameters from each assessed pathway.

2.3.6
SAF combustion emissions
The majority of the studies considered the combustion of biogenic carbon as carbon-neutral as it had
been up-taken during biomass growth. Therefore, the combustion stage is largely unrepresented as
non-CO2 emissions are usually not considered. The goal for the harmonization of this life-cycle stage
was to determine the indexes of all combustion emissions. The flight emission data for CH4, N2O,
CO2, Volatile Organic Carbon (VOC), CO, NOx, PM10 were extracted from Elgowainy et al. [19],
whereas SOx, water vapor, soot and sulfur emissions were extracted from Lee et al. [27].
Elgowainy et al. [19] provide emissions values for the use of 100% SPK fuels in g pollutant/kg of fuel.
These are derived from the fuel-burn-weighted averages for different engines in all the flight stages,
and the emission changes from various SPK fuel blends. All SOx are assumed to be proportional to
fuel composition, and 100% SPK corresponds to 100% reduction in sulfur content. Emissions of CH4,
N2O and VOC are assumed to be unchanged when using SAF.
To determine the inventory of emissions per functional unit, the average emissions values had to
be divided by the Lower Heating Value (LHV) of the fuel. The LHV of SAF can be higher than the
conventional fuels’. Currently, all jet fuels covered by ASTM D7566 have LHVs > 42.8 MJ/kg and
1-2% higher than conventional fossil jet fuel [28]. For each pathway, an average lower heating value
was determined based on the studies collected and the data used by Elgowainy et al. [19], derived
from Carter et al. [29]. These pathway-specific LHV were used to determine the emission inventories

2.3.7
Multi-functionality modeling
Allocation of co-products was handled at feedstock production and conversion stages. In literature,
different allocation methods are normally used [12, 30] and there is a lack of agreement on the appro-
priate method for these systems. Moreover, although regulatory frameworks commonly use energy
allocation (Renewable Energy Directive [31], RenovaBio [32], CORSIA [23]), the Renewable Fuel
Standard (RFS) program in the U.S. utilizes system expansion [33]. In this study, the inventories
were harmonized using energy allocation in both relevant stages. However, this choice was further
explored under a sensitivity analysis described in Section 2.6. The allocation factors used are sum-
marized in Table A.1 in Supplementary Material A.2.
2.4
Life Cycle Impact Assessment Method
The LCA calculations were performed using Brightway2, and Impact World+ v2.0.1 was used as the
impact assessment method. Both midpoint and damage indicators were used to assess the environ-
mental performance of each pathway. 18 midpoint categories are considered in the methodology,
where emissions within the system boundaries are characterized to quantify the impacts. These are
further characterized under the damage assessment, to report the impact to areas of protection (i.e.,
Human Health and Ecosystem Quality). As the environmental indicators are translated to damage
impacts, the endpoint level helps solve trade-offs that can occur at the midpoint level between dif-
ferent environmental issues. Therefore, both levels were viewed as complimentary and supported a
more balanced interpretation of the results [34].
Where short-term (0-100 year time horizon) and long-term (0-500 year time horizon) impact categories
are differentiated by the method, only shorter term categories were included in the assessment. This
is because of the larger uncertainty associated to longer-term characterization factors [35]. This is
particularly relevant to impact categories where the stationary state is reached over time and thus the
impacts remain (e.g., for climate change).

2.6
Uncertainty and Sensitivity Analysis
Uncertainty analysis is required to support decision-making effectively and to provide a comprehen-
sive interpretation of the LCA results. Several authors have categorized uncertainties as parameter,
scenario and model factors [40, 41]. The robustness of the calculated results and the comparison of
the different SAF pathways is dependent on the uncertainty and variability of the LCI data, the impact
assessment model and modeling choices of the practitioner.
On this study, the parameter uncertainty on the LCI was quantified through statistical methods, and
two modeling choices were evaluated (i.e., allocation method for co-products and alternative uses of
feedstocks). Uncertainties from the impact method assessment model and their correspondent input
parameters were not considered in this assessment.
A Monte Carlo simulation was performed on the inventories of the foreground processes, to quantify
the uncertainty related to the gathered LCI data. The analysis uses a sampling method, which consists
of consecutively calculating the impact scores for the system while the inputs are sampled randomly
from a specified distribution for each flow [42]. There is a lack of agreement on the optimum number
of sampling that should be performed in the analysis [43]. Moreover, as the amount of flows in the
dataset increases, the required number of runs is higher [44]. In this study, the number of runs was
optimized to 750 by evaluating the convergence of the mean and standard deviation in the Alcohol-to-
Jet process, which had a larger amount of inventory flows. The results of the optimization are shown
in Figure A.1 in Supplementary Material (SM) A.7.
During the LCI harmonization, the uncertainty distribution parameters for each flow were calculated
from the gathered studies, and fit to a log-normal distribution to model flows that cannot be negative.
The location parameter was calculated as the natural logarithm of the deterministic value, whereas
the scale factor was determined as the standard deviation of the log-transformed data.
For the combustion emissions, where there was only one data set, the parameters were determined
based on a pedigree matrix to specify the log-normal distribution [45]. A score was given to determine
the data quality of the emissions (reliability, completeness, temporal, geographical and technological
correlation). Each of the characteristics was scored between 1 to 5, which has an uncertainty factor
associated that can be found in Table A.2 in SM A.3. The scale parameter of the log-normal distribution
was then calculated with the following formula:

The following modeling choices were assessed, compared to the baseline in Brazil from the case
study:
1. Allocation methods Biofuel processes generally produce several co-products, and if the goal is
to assess the environmental performance of a particular fuel, it is necessary to allocate the total
emissions between all the obtained products. The ISO 14040 and 14044 standards recommend
to avoid allocation when possible by subdivision or system expansion. If this is not possible,
then the impacts can be assigned with allocation factors that are calculated based on physical or
economic relationships. However, in LCA of biofuels, the most common approach is to allocate
through energy-based factors or system expansion [11]. In this study, multi-functionality was
handled in the feedstock production and conversion to fuels stages. On the baseline case,
energy allocation was applied in both stages, and three alternative choices were evaluated:
economic allocation in both stages, economic allocation for feedstock production and energy
allocation for fuel products, and finally, mass allocation for feedstock production while keeping
energy allocation for the fuel products. The allocation factors are presented in SM A.2.
2. Current uses of feedstock Several scenarios were assessed to quantify the impact on results
when considering the available biomass had a current alternative application. For the baseline
case, the feedstock was assumed to be left as waste with no use. The assessment was made
for sugarcane bagasse on the HTL pathway, and the following uses were identified in Brazil:
direct combustion for heat and electricity recovery, production of bioethanol, use as feed for
cattle and use on land as fertilizer [46, 47].
(a) Use for energy recovery: The biomass was assumed to be combusted in a Combined Heat
and Power plant. The net electricity and heat efficiencies were 29% and 69%, respectively
[48].
(b) Use as feed: The conventional animal feed was assumed maize and soybean, and the dis-
placed amounts were calculated based on the digestible crude protein and energy contents
of the residue [49]. The composition for maize, soybean meal and sugarcane bagasse can
be found in SM A.4.
(c) Use for bioethanol production: Mainly the carbohydrates are used to produce bioethanol,
and it was assumed to replace gasoline in the fuel market. The ratio between LHV values
for 100% ethanol and gasoline was considered to calculate the displaced gasoline. Al-
though the engine’s efficiency would vary with a different fuel, the maximum potential was
assumed with a constant efficiency.
(d) Use as fertilizer: Nitrogen, phosporous and potassium contents in the residue were as-
sumed to displace conventional mineral fertilizers with a 100% efficiency.

The coefficient of variation (CV), representing the ratio of the standard deviation to the mean, provides
insight into the relative variability of input parameters across the different pathways. A high CV, as
observed in certain conversion processes (e.g., electricity and heat inputs for Fischer-Tropsch and
hydrothermal liquefaction), highlights significant variability likely stemming from differences in tech-
nology configurations, and levels of energy integration. Conversely, low CV values, particularly for
feedstock amounts across pathways, suggest greater consistency and reliability in these parameters,
potentially reflecting a higher maturity level of the underlying technology (e.g., the HEFA process for
both feedstocks, which had the lowest CVs) or more robust data. However, it is crucial to consider
that low mean values can inflate the CV, making variability appear disproportionately high even if
absolute variations are minimal [63]. These variations underscore the need to cautiously interpret the
results, especially for pathways with high CV, as they highlight areas where additional research and
technological optimization are necessary to reduce uncertainty and improve process predictability.
The boxplots in Figure 3 illustrate the variability in resource consumption (electricity, heat, and hy-
drogen) across the different pathways. FT exhibits the highest variability in electricity and heat con-
sumption, reflecting the variable nature of the process and the different potentials for integration. In
contrast, HEFA shows consistently low and narrow ranges for both electricity and heat demands,
indicating greater process stability and lower energy requirements.
For hydrogen consumption, HTL and FP demonstrate higher median values and variability compared
to other pathways, which could be related to their lower technological readiness level compared to the
other processes, indicating the need for further research. ATJ and HEFA pathways, on the other hand,
have lower hydrogen demands and less variability as well, reinforcing their position as front-runners
as near-term SAF pathways [39].
Overall, the plots highlight significant variability in resource use among SAF pathways, emphasizing
the importance of optimizing processes and understanding the potential for scalability and future de-
ployment of SAF. This variability also underscores the trade-offs between resource consumption and
the technical feasibility of the different technologies.

Damages to human health Climate change, water availability and particulate matter formation were
the main contributors to damage on human health across all pathways (Figure 6). Water availability
was the main contributor to human health damages, surpassing climate change, which is typically
the primary focus for reducing the impacts of aviation fuels. Several factors contribute to the high
scores for water availability. Firstly, significant water consumption may occur during the cultivation of
agricultural feedstocks, especially when irrigation is required, as observed in the case of FP. Secondly,
water is consumed in the conversion processes, including cooling systems and also in the background
processes like hydrogen production via steam methane reforming, where CH4 is cracked using high-
temperature steam. Additionally, electricity generation can further amplify water consumption; for
instance, in Brazil, hydropower accounted for 66% of the electricity supply in 2020 [67].

the feedstock apart from collection and transport. Moreover, this is the only process of the evaluated
ones that resulted in a lower total impact score on human health than fossil kerosene. Nevertheless,
when looking at climate change impacts over human health, all the pathways fall under the fossil
baseline, and only go over it when considering other impact categories.

The only process were feedstock production was the main contributor on climate change was for the
HEFA pathway with energy crops as feedstock, and this is largely because of the LUC emissions, and
secondly to the use of fertilizers. The conversion stage was one of the top contributors for climate
change and water scarcity across all different pathways and types of feedstocks. This is mainly due to
the required process inputs for each pathway; i.e., electricity, heat, hydrogen, and water. Therefore,
the design and optimization of the refining process is crucial for reducing the environmental impact of
these processes. Capaz et al. [33] indicates that for the HEFA pathway, the climate change impacts
can increase by 13% (46.7 gCO2/MJ) with soybean as feedstock and up to 51% (20.0 gCO2/MJ)
when using beef tallow, if natural gas and electricity from the grid are used as external supplies of
heat and electricity, respectively. Their reported values under this conditions are consistent with the
ones found in this study (47 gCO2/MJ and 25 gCO2/MJ). In their assessment, light streams within the
processes were used for internal generation, and thus reducing the total impact.

Comparing climate change impacts with other scientific studies
As most studies focus on the GWP impacts of SAF, the obtained results were compared in terms of
climate change impacts against LCAs performed on the same processes [7, 33, 62, 68–72].
De Jong et al. [69] analyzed various production pathways, including the HEFA process using Jatropha

crops in the U.S. with energy allocation, reporting an impact of 55 gCO2e/MJ. This value is higher than
the 47 gCO2e/MJ estimated in this study. Although land-use change (LUC) effects were excluded,
their findings highlighted low oil yields from Jatropha seeds, which contributed to increased cultivation
impacts. Similarly, Liu et al. [7] observed significant regional variations in Jatropha production across
China. In contrast, results for used cooking oil were more consistent, with their study reporting 27
gCO2e/MJ, closely aligning with the 25 gCO2e/MJ found in this study.
O’Connell et al. [68] presents GHG emissions for FT pathway from forest residues just under 10
gCO2e/MJ, because of internal heat and electricity generation with surplus electricity credited. Simi-
larly, Capaz et al. [33] reported results of 4 gCO2e/MJ for the FT pathway, and the transport stages
were the main contributors to this impacts. In these studies, the feedstock was considered burden-
free, so no impacts were reported for this stages, and the energy requirements were assumed to be
supplied internally. Although there is large potential for energy integration in thermochemical conver-
sions of biomass, an appropriate optimization should be performed to determine the actual require-
ments of external sources. If the energy could be generated or supplied internally, the results from
this study would be reduced from 47 gCO2e/MJ to 11.2 gCO2e/MJ. Swanson et al. [73] performed
a techno-economical assessment of the FT with gasification process, and while it determined that
electricity could be supplied internally, steam and cooling water utilities were still required for the op-
eration of the process plant. Furthermore, the consideration of internal supply of heat and electricity
has led to poor LCI data reported, lacking information on energy requirements, and has hindered the
assessment of other impact categories beyond climate change.
For FP, Tews et al. [70] (34 gCO2e/MJ) indicated 60% of the climate change impacts were produced
during the conversion stage, although they considered the heat requirements were produced internally
by the combustion of the produced char during the pyrolysis process. Moreover, De Jong et al. (41
gCO2e/MJ) indicated the hydrogen requirements were the largest contributor, which is aligned with
the findings from this study. Furthermore, they highlighted the potential for renewable production of
hydrogen to further increase the GHG reduction compared to fossil jet fuel.
ATJ technologies present a large range of results across literature, ranging from 17 gCO2e/MJ (Capaz
et al. [33]) to 75 gCO2e/MJ ([71]), which only accounted for the conversion process emissions but
did not consider energy integration. The results obtained on this study fall within this range, and are
comparable to the results produced by Santos et al. [72] (52 gCO2e/MJ). The main contributions to
the emissions originated from biomass pretreatment and alcohol production, which is aligned with
the findings of this study that indicated the processing and refining stage was the main contributor to
climate change impacts.
The results obtained for the HTL pathway (44 gCO2e/MJ) fall within the range found in literature, with
Zhu et al. [62] and De Jong [69] reporting 51.2 gCO2e/MJ and 18 gCO2e/MJ, respectively. Although
the studies considered the same type of inputs in their inventories, this reflects the variability of the
conversion processes when performing mass and energy balances. Moreover, the former study used
the electricity mix of China, with a higher share of coal and oil in the mix, while the latter uses the U.S.
mix with a larger share of natural gas.

4
Conclusions & recommendations
4.1
Key findings
To conclude, this study highlights the complexity of assessing sustainable aviation fuel production.
While SAF offers significant potential for reducing climate change impacts compared to conventional
fossil fuels, a holistic evaluation of their environmental performance is necessary to avoid burden
shifting to other impact categories. Variability in results underscores the importance of considering
feedstock types, conversion technologies, geographical contexts, and allocation methods in life cycle
assessments. Furthermore, optimizing conversion processes and accounting for the circular use of
resources are critical to ensuring the sustainability of SAF. These insights emphasize the need for
continued research and harmonization efforts to guide the sustainable development of SAF technolo-
gies. To finalize the study, the following conclusions are presented:
I. The harmonization of LCIs is crucial for comparability across studies. However, significant vari-
ability remains due to feedstock types, conversion technologies, and geographical factors. Key
drivers of these impacts include energy inputs during conversion, water usage, and land-use
change (LUC).
II. SAF show potential for reducing climate change impacts compared to fossil fuels. However, other
impact categories such as water scarcity and land transformation need careful consideration, as
they can result in higher damages of these fuel alternatives compared to conventional ones.
III. Among the different feedstocks, oil residues such as used cooking oil and beef tallow offer the
lowest environmental impacts in HEFA pathways due to minimal land-use change and low re-
source demands. Conversely, agricultural and energy crops often result in higher environmental
burdens due to significant cultivation requirements.
IV. The conversion stage was the main contributor to the most damaging categories, such as cli-
mate change and water scarcity. The optimization of this stage has been also highlighted in
literature as crucial to reduce the environmental impacts of these processes. For instance, in
the Fischer-Tropsch process, energy integration and internal electricity generation can minimize
climate change impacts below 10 gCO2e/MJ.
V. The results can be very sensitive to the geographical region where the fuels are produced. Cul-
tivation inputs and land use change effects vary depending on the type of land used, and the
country’s electricity mix has a great impact on the environmental scores.
VI. The choice of allocation method has a great influence on the results. Particularly, it was found that
due to their low economic value, results using market allocation factors for agricultural residues
can vary up to over 90% from the base case with energy allocation.
VII. The scenario analysis of hydrothermal liquefaction (HTL) from sugarcane bagasse demonstrates
the importance of considering current utilization scenarios for biomass residues when assessing
the sustainability of SAF production. Particularly, the largest variations were found when the
biomass was being initially used for energy recovery and feed for cattle, with 122.5% and 86.3%
increase on HH and EQ, respectively, compared to the base case of no utilization. This indicates
the need to perform comprehensive analysis on the circularity of waste systems, to determine
the most sustainable use of it.
4.2
Limitations
The findings of this study are constrained by several limitations and uncertainties that should be
considered when interpreting the results. These include the type of feedstocks assessed, the use of
average market processes for displacement analysis, methodological constraints of the chosen LCIA
method, assumptions on energy integration, data availability and technology readiness, exclusion of
specific life cycle stages and inventories, and the lack of integration of environmental performance
analysis with a techno-economical assessment.

==PAGE:30==
The analysis focuses on second-generation biomass, which may not accurately represent the environ-
mental performance of first- or third-generation feedstocks. For example, first-generation feedstocks
often involve higher land related impacts, while third-generation feedstocks like algae may entail en-
tirely different challenges, like energy consumption for feedstock drying. Consequently, the feedstock
production stage may have higher relevance than presented in this study.
Moreover, the use of average market processes for assessing the impacts on shifting the uses of
biomass residues might underestimate its impacts as they do not account for actual market dynamics.
Although SAF production is not yet deployed at a scale that significantly influences global markets,
this limitation could have important implications when evaluating future scenarios for the aviation
industry. For instance, in countries were there is still a strong reliance on coal for energy production,
the benefits of diverting biomass residues for energy recovery could be more pronounced, potentially
altering the comparative advantages of SAF production in large-scale.
Another limitation emerges from the LCIA method chosen, which does not account for contrail forma-
tion, that can significantly impact climate change scores [20]. This omission could lead to an under-
estimation of the total climate change impacts of SAF, minimizing the contribution of the combustion
stage.
In addition, modeled conversion processes assumed external utility supplies for energy demands,
disregarding the potential for energy integration. This assumption may overestimate the impacts
of the conversion stage, particularly for pathways like Fischer-Tropsch, which have demonstrated
potential for energy self-sufficiency. Although the objective in this study was to determine potential
impacts and hotspots, this should be kept in future assessments as it can significantly enhance the
environmental performance of pathways with high potential of integration.
During the literature review, the different readiness level of technologies limited the amount of path-
ways that could be analyzed. Some are still under development and there is no industrial or pilot
scale data available. However, there is great potential in the early stage of development and this
could impact the results from this study when considered in future assessments.
Regarding the LCI data quality, there are limitations on the geographical representativeness of some
processes, as the reliance on Ecoinvent 3.9.1 sometimes required using broader geographies. This
introduced uncertainty when assessing region-specific impacts.
Furthermore, the burdens associated to equipment manufacturing and plant construction were ex-
cluded, as they typically are minor. However, it is important to keep in mind when performing region-
alized LCAs, as countries with existing infrastructure could reutilize these, while others would require
new equipment. Moreover, catalysts were not included in the assessment as the available data was
limited due to proprietary information not disclosed in literature. However, it has been reported to be
the main contributor to indicators like mineral depletion potential [20].
Finally, this study evaluated and compared the environmental performance of different SAF path-
ways and feedstocks. However, large-scale deployment will entail large investments. Therefore, it
is important to combine this findings with a techno-economical assessment, where the technology
readiness level and economic metrics are determined. Integrating these could enable a more robust
decision-making on the scalability of these technologies.
While these limitations do not undermine the study’s primary conclusions, addressing them could
refine the results and enhance their applicability to broader contexts, particularly in regions or systems
not directly assessed here.

4.4
Future work
This section outlines key lines of future work based on the findings of this study. Some of this recom-
mendations address its main limitations.
Future research should explore energy integration in SAF production processes to reduce external
utility reliance and environmental impacts, focusing on realistic heat integration potential and the use
of internal streams for electricity generation.
In addition, contrail formation impacts should be incorporated into LCIA methods to provide a more
comprehensive evaluation of SAF pathways’ climate change effects. Given the significant contribution
of contrails to aviation-induced climate impacts, including them would enhance the robustness of
environmental assessments.
There is also a need to expand the scope of research by collecting more inventories for underrep-
resented SAF production pathways, such as catalytic hydro-thermolysis (CHT) and synthetic iso-
paraffins (SIP). These pathways currently lack sufficient data to enable detailed assessments. More-
over, harmonization efforts should be extended to include electro-fuels, facilitating consistent evalu-
ations across various fuel types and production technologies.
Integrating environmental LCAs with techno-economic assessments is another critical area for future
work. Evaluating the technology readiness levels, economic viability, and minimum selling prices
of SAF pathways can provide stakeholders with a more holistic understanding, supporting robust
decision-making for large-scale deployment.
The results of environmental assessments can be further enriched by normalizing scores to person-
equivalent metrics. This approach would contextualize the contribution of each indicator to the av-
erage annual impact of a citizen, helping to resolve trade-offs observed between different impact
categories.
Furthermore, the study would benefit from combining the harmonized inventories with future sce-
narios, to understand the possibilities and challenges of reaching net zero by 2050 in the aviation
industry.
Finally, research should adopt a broader perspective, addressing the social and political implications
of large-scale SAF deployment. This includes examining regional differences, exploring regulatory
frameworks, and fostering collaboration among producers, airlines, and policymakers to promote sus-
tainable practices and industry growth.
