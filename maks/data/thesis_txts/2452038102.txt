Abstract
Protein prediction tasks are usually challenging due to limited amounts of annotated
data which leads to prediction models with poor performance or generalization. To alle-
viate some of these issues, most state-of-the-art models rely on external homology and
knowledge database information to carry out their predictions. We seek to address the
issue and improve existing models by adopting natural language processing techniques
that incorporate information from unannotated data. In particular, we seek to incor-
porate amino acid embeddings, trained on unlabelled proteins, in protein prediction
models which we hope will help the models generalize better and possibly remove the
dependency on external information.
In this project we present a framework capable of evaluating pre-trained amino acid
embeddings on various protein prediction tasks, such as subcellular localization and sec-
ondary structure prediction for which the framework is showcased. For each task we
replicated deep learning models with high performance to serve as baselines and demon-
strate the impact of incorporating amino acid embeddings through various architectures.
We find that pre-trained amino acid embedding do capture useful information for protein
prediction tasks. Utilizing amino acid embeddings for subcellular localization on the
DeepLoc dataset with no profile encoding, we achieve 72.77% accuracy which is a slight
improvement of the baseline on the same dataset (72.30%) but short of the baseline
on profile encoded sequences (75.08%). For secondary structure prediction, we achieve
57.99% accuracy on the CB513 dataset with no profile encoding which is a significant
improvement of the baseline on same dataset (56.52%) but far from the baseline on profile
encoded sequences (69.39%). Additionally, we find that the embeddings evaluated in this
project are competitive with SeqVec for subcellular localization.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In this project we introduce a special way to represent amino acids called pre-trained
amino acid embeddings. These pre-trained amino acid embeddings have been created by
and will be evaluated with artificial neural networks, to show if these new representations
can be useful in prediction of protein properties.
Artificial neural networks, as we sometimes will refer to as models, come from a spe-
cial branch of machine learning called deep learning. These artificial neural networks
are capable of solving a large variety of tasks such as language modelling and protein
prediction tasks, as we will see in this project.
In the remainder of this chapter, we motivate pre-trained embeddings and provide an
informal description of the task at hand. Then in chapter 2 the relevant theory and
background knowledge will be presented and a literature review is provided at the end of
chapter 2 where the previous and related work to the project will be considered. Chapter
3 presents the tools used in the implementation and management of the project after
which the implementation details will be presented in chapter 4. The datasets used, the
model architectures and the experimental setups of the different protein prediction tasks
are describe in chapter 5 and chapter 6 presents the results of the various experiments.
Chapter 7 discusses the results along with the future perspectives and finally we conclude
on the findings of this project in chapter 8.
1.1
Motivation
Protein prediction tasks can be challenging as limited amounts of annotated data exist,
however, large amounts of unannotated data is available which makes it desirable to
incorporate this data in protein prediction tasks.
Incorporation of uannotated data has has been done in Natural Language Processing
(NLP) by the use of language models. These language models have been used to create
pre-trained word embeddings which has shown to be highly useful in a number of NLP
problems. If amino acid sequences are thought of as a language where the amino acids
represent words and proteins represent sentences then we might be able to model this
language as we would a natural language. In particular, it might be possible to train a
language model on the large amounts of unannotated protein data and from that model
extract amino acid embeddings. Utilizing these amino acid representations, we might be

able to improve performance on downstream protein prediction tasks just as pre-trained
word embeddings have done for NLP.
Furthermore amino acid profiles are widely used in protein prediction tasks but the
profiles take a long time to generate. With the use of pre-trained amino acid embeddings

1.2
Objective
The main goal of this project is to evaluate the performance of pre-trained amino acid
embeddings in subcellular localization and secondary structure prediction. We hope to
show that incorporating information from unannotated data, in the form of pre-trained
amino acid embeddings, can be beneficial in downstream protein prediction tasks.
To carry out this evaluation the following steps will be taken. First a framework will be
implemented to make it easy to explore different datasets and model architectures for
various protein prediction tasks. Then, the framework will be used to evaluate the pre-
trained amino acid embeddings on the subcellular localization and secondary structure
prediction tasks.
Evaluating the pre-trained amino acid embeddings will be done by first implementing
deep learning models, which are able to perform the two prediction tasks. These models
will be used as baselines and will be replications of existing models from research papers
showing high performance on the two tasks.
With the baselines established, inspiration will be drawn from recent research to best
extract and incorporate the pre-trained amino acid embeddings in the models for each
task.

2.1
Proteins
Responsible: Mikkel Møller Brusen (s134568)
Proteins are large molecules composed of polymers of amino acid residues linked by pep-
tide bonds. They play a vital role in all organisms as they catalyse metabolic reactions,
replicate DNA, provide structure, transport molecules among others. Due to their vi-
tal role, proteins are very well studied and large databases such as UniProt1 contains
millions of protein sequences. It is important to note, that while we have access to
millions of proteins, only a small subset are annotated with information such as subcel-
lular localization and secondary structure. We will be looking to exploit the information
hidden within the millions of un-annotated proteins, to better utilize the small subset of
annotated data. As proteins are sequences of amino acids, it is in fact the composition
of amino acids that we strive to learn more about.
2.1.1
Amino Acids
The amino acids in proteins typically come from a set of 20 universal ones as seen
in Figure 2.1, although a couple more amino acids exist.
Amino acids are typically
mentioned by their abbreviation of 3 or 1 letter rather than their full name, and we shall
do this going forward too. Individual amino acids can be linked together by covalent
amide bonds, called peptide bonds, to form the primary structure of a protein. Short
chains of 20 to 30 amino acids are called peptides and longer chains, typically of 200 to
500 amino acids but sometimes longer, are called polypeptides.
Each amino acid has a unique composition which contributes to the properties of the
protein such as structure, function, polarity among others.

2.1.2
Subcellular Localization
The subcellular location of proteins are vital to the function of a cell, thus it is important
that the proteins target the correct subcellular sites. If a protein targets a different
subcellular site than intended, it may lead to different genetic effects such as cancer
or other genetic diseases (Juan et al., 2011).
To this end, assessing the subcellular
localization becomes crucial, and has been carried out using wet-lab techniques such as
fluorescent microscopy, fluorescent tagging with biomarkers among others (Lodish et al.,
2016, p. 146). However, these techniques are expensive and time consuming, rendering
it infeasible to use them for the millions of proteins we now have in databases such
as UniProt. Thus, methods to computationally predict protein subcellular localization
have had a huge breakthrough in the past decades with very recent techniques utilizing
deep learning now dominating the field.
The delivery of proteins to their proper subcellular location, usually referred to as pro-
tein sorting or targeting, includes two different processes: signal-based targeting and
vesicle-based trafficking. The first process involves the targeting of proteins to an intra-
cellular organelle. Proteins destined for the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), mitochondria,
chloroplasts, peroxisomes and nucleus are sorted by this process. The second process,
known as the secretory pathway, involves all proteins that were originally targeted to
the ER, as the secretory pathway begins in the ER. By entering the secretory pathway,
proteins will be able to reach their final destinations in the Golgi apparatus, lysosome,
vacuole, cell membrane or extracellular enviroment (Lodish et al., 2016, pp. 583–585).
Both processes rely on information encoded within the amino acid sequence, called a
sorting signal (also know as target sequence or signal peptide). The sorting signal is
typically 20-50 amino acids long, and often located at the N-terminus but can some-
times be found in the C-terminus or within the interior of the protein instead. Proteins
destined for the cytoplasm do not have a sorting signal as they are synthesized where
needed, meaning the absence of a sorting signal should classify as cytoplasm. We will be
exploiting the existence and absence of the sorting signals by creating a model capable
of paying special attention to the sorting signals, for which a summary of some of the
most common can be found in Table 2.1.
2.1.3
Secondary Structure
As mention in section 2.1 proteins are composed of linear polymers of amino acids which
in general will fold into three-dimensional shapes called conformations. The conforma-
tion of a protein along with the chemical properties of the amino acid side chains will
determine the function of the protein. Because many distinct shapes and chemical prop-
erties exist for proteins, they can have a huge amount of different functions outside and
inside cells. These functions can either provide a selective evolutionary advantage or be
essential for life itself of the organism or cell that they live in.

To understand all of the functions of proteins and how proteins permit cells to live and
thrive, requires the characterization and identification of all the proteins that a cell uses.
This is one of the reasons why the secondary structure of a protein is interesting.
Proteins fold in a hierarchy of different structure levels that are called primary, sec-
ondary, tertiary, and quaternary.
The primary structure of a protein is simply the
linear sequence or arrangement of the amino acid residues that it is composed of. The
secondary structures, that this project is concerned about, are stable arrangements of
segments in the amino acid chain defined by patterns of hydrogen bonds within the
backbone. The amino acid sequence of a protein determines the propensity of different
segments in the protein to form any given structure. This dependence on the sequence is
what this project will try to take advantage of when predicting the secondary structures
of proteins.
The principle secondary structures are the alpha (α) helix, the beta (β) sheet, and the
U-shaped beta (β) turn which are visualized in Figure 2.2. The parts of the polypeptide
that have a different well-defined, stable shape are said to have irregular structure. There
is also the term random coil which refers to highly flexible parts of the polypeptide chain
with no fixed three-dimensional structure (Lodish et al., 2016, pp. 68–70).

Alpha Helix
The alpha helix is the most common helical form in proteins. In a polypeptide segment
folded into an α-helix, the backbone of the polypeptide forms a spiral structure. This
spiral structure is held together by hydrogen bonds between the backbone carbonyl
oxygen atom of each peptide bond and the amide hydrogen atom of the amino acid four
residues farther along the chain towards the C-terminus. In the alpha helix there is a
complete turn of the spiral every 3.6 residues. A visualization of an alpha helix can

2.1.3.2
Beta Sheet
The beta sheet is another kind of secondary structure. It consists of laterally packed
β strands. Each of the β strands are a short, five to eight residues, nearly extended
polypeptide sequence. In the β sheet hydrogen bonds occur between backbone atoms
in separate but adjacent β strands in contrast to the α helix where hydrogen bonds
occur between the backbone amino and carboxyl groups of nearly adjacent residues. A
visualization of a β sheet is shown in the top figure of Figure 2.2b. Distinct β strands
are indicated as arrows with alternating green and blue segments and they can both be
within single polypeptide chains with long or short loops between the β strands but they
can also be on different polypeptide chains in a protein that are composed of multiple
polypeptides. β-strands have a directionality defined by the orientation of the peptide
bonds.
This means that they can be oriented in alternating opposite, anti-parallel,

2.1.3.3
Beta Turn
The beta turns are composed of four residues and they are located on the surface of
proteins where they form sharp U-shaped bends.
These bends reverse the direction
of the polypeptide backbone. This type of secondary structure is often stabilized by
hydrogen bonds between its end residues. In Figure 2.2c the beta turn is visualized.
Amino acids glycine and proline are often found in beta turns. That information can be
interesting in this project when trying to predict secondary structures of proteins along
with the fact that proline rarely is found in α helices. β turns makes it possible for longer
polypeptides to fold into very compact structures (Lodish et al., 2016, pp. 71–72).

Machine Learning Basics

Deep learning is a sub-field in machine learning and to understand deep learning some
knowledge of basic machine learning is needed. In this section some basic concepts from
machine learning will be presented.
2.2.1
Machine Learning Algorithms
A machine learning algorithm is an algorithm that is able to learn from data. This is
achieved by letting the algorithm experience a dataset and through this experience solve
a given task. It is said that the algorithm learns if a performance measure on the given
task increases with experience of the dataset. Many different tasks can be performed
by machine learning algorithms like classification where an algorithm usually is asked to
produce a function f : Rn →{1, ..., k}. Given an input vector x the model assigns the
input a category by numeric code y between 1 and k. y is the prediction of the model
given the input x. Another type of classification is when the algorithm, f, outputs a
probability distribution of the possible classes. Examples of classification problems could
be object detection in photos or, as is done in this project, subcellular localization and
secondary structure prediction of proteins (Goodfellow et al., 2016; Mitchell, 1997).
2.2.1.1
Unsupervised and Supervised Learning
Machine learning algorithms can broadly be categorized as unsupervised or supervised
by the kind of experience they will get through the learning process. Supervised learning
algorithms are algorithms that learn to associate some input x with some output y. This
means that these tasks need to have a set of training examples and a set of targets or
outputs y. A lot of supervised learning algorithms are based on estimating a probability
==PAGE:19==
2.2 Machine Learning Basics
9
distribution p(y|x). This can be done by optimizing the parameter vector, θ, of the
model to get the best estimation of the distribution.
Unsupervised learning algorithms are algorithms that experience only features but not
any supervised signal or target. Informally, unsupervised learning attempts to learn the
probability distribution, p(x), or interesting properties of that distribution without any
annotation done by humans. An example of unsupervised learning is clustering where
the algorithm tries to group related data into groups (Goodfellow et al., 2016).
2.2.1.2
Training and Test Data
The main challenge in machine learning is that the model should be able to perform well
on the task in general and not just on the data it was trained on. The ability to perform
well on previously unseen data is called generalization. Typically a training dataset will
be made for the model to train on and an error measure called the training error or
training loss will be computed. Then a test dataset of examples not seen during training
will be used to test how the model works on the task in general. The error measure
computed on the test set is called the generalization error or test error and this should
be as small as possible for the model to perform well on the task in general.
If the gap between the training error and the test error becomes large the model is
overfitting.
This means that the model performs well on training data but has low
performance when unseen data is used. The opposite of overfitting is underfitting which
happens when the training error is large.
2.2.1.3
Model Performance and Optimization
When doing machine learning, a way to measure the performance of a model on a given
task is needed to compute training and test errors. For a machine learning model like
linear regression the performance can be measured by computing the mean squared
error on the test data. When the performance measure has been chosen, an algorithm
is needed to improve the parameters, θ, of the model. If mean squared error is used as
performance measure, optimization can be done by simply minimizing the mean squared
error on the training data.
In deep learning, usually a function is chosen for measuring the performance.
This
function is called a loss function but it is also sometimes referred to as the objective
function or error function. The function is minimized usually using gradient descent
where the parameters, θ, of the model are changed based on the gradient of the loss
function. Many different loss functions can be used in deep learning, a popular one is
the negative log-likelihood.

2.2.1.4
Hyperparameters and Validation Data
Often, machine learning algorithms have some parameters that can be used to control
its behavior.
These parameters are called hyperparameters and the values of these
hyperparameters are not set or learned by the machine learning algorithm itself. Often
a parameter is a hyperparameter because it is not appropriate to learn that parameter
on the training dataset. If hyperparameters were learned on the training set they would
always be set to the value giving the best performance on the training data resulting
in overfitting. To pick out the values of the hyperparameters a validation dataset is
needed. This dataset consists, like the test set, of unobserved data. It is important that
the test dataset is not used to make choices about the model and because of this no
examples from the test dataset can be used in the validation dataset. The validation
data is constructed from the training data typically by splitting the training data in a
training set and a validation set. The training set will then be used for training. The
validation set will be used to estimate the test error and after training updating the
hyperparameters accordingly. After all hyperparameter optimizations are complete, the
generalization error may be estimated using the test dataset (Goodfellow et al., 2016).
2.2.1.5
Homology Partitioning
Not directly related to machine learning but nevertheless an important concept when
working with data consisting of protein sequences is homology partitioning. To assess
whether two proteins are homologous, multiple measures can be considered, one of which
is identity. The identity of two aligned protein sequences can be defined as the number
of aligned positions where amino acids are identical divided by the number of aligned
positions including gaps. When generating the training, validation and test splits, it
is important that these splits do not share proteins that are similar above a certain
identity threshold as this could be an indication of homologous proteins. Generally it
is said that an identity above 30% is an indication of homology, however as mentioned
earlier, other measures can be considered too. Nonetheless, it is important to take note of
the threshold used for the partitioning of a certain dataset. Reason being that homology
partitioning is performed during the split creation such that proteins with identity above
the specified threshold are clustered together, and that proteins of each such cluster is
assigned to the same split set. Should the threshold be chosen to e.g. 80%, models using
this dataset would be trained and evaluated on splits that share homologous proteins
which can produce unrealistically high validation accuracy based solely on the models
ability to recognize homologous proteins rather than detecting general features. It is
therefore important to ensure that a reasonable threshold is used such that homologues
proteins are not mixed between splits, thus allowing the model to generalize better (Sikic
and Carugo, 2010; Lodish et al., 2016, p. 80).

Artificial Neural Networks
Responsible: Gustav Madslund (s142800)
Artificial neural networks (ANN) are the core of nearly all applications of deep learning.
Therefore understanding deep learning requires an understanding of artificial neural net-
works. The basic ANN model is called a feedforward neural network (FNN) or multilayer
perceptron (MLP), which attempts to approximate some function f ∗. Given a classifier,
y = f ∗(x), which maps an input x to some category y, the FNN defines the mapping
as y = f(x, θ), where θ is the parameters of the network. The network then learns the
parameters θ that result in the best approximation of the function. These networks are
called feedforward, as information propagates through the function evaluated from x,
through all the intermediate computations that defines the function f, to the the out-
put y. This means that these networks do not include any feedback connections where
outputs of the function are fed back into itself. Feedforward neural networks can be ex-
tended to include these feedback connections (referred to as recurrent neural networks)
which will be described later in section 2.5. Because feedforward neural networks are the
basis of ANNs this type of network is described first to establish the basic understanding
before diving into more complex extensions such as the recurrent neural network.
Feedforward neural networks are often composed of many different functions and can
be visualized as a directed acyclic graph describing how the functions are connected.
For example, given the three functions f (1), f (2), and f (3), connecting them in a chain
will form the function f(x) = f (3)(f (2)(f (1)(x))). Each function represents a linear or
nonlinear transformation and can be seen as a layer in a directed acyclic graph where
f (1) is the first layer, f (2) is the second layer and so on. The last function or layer in a
feedforward neural network is called the output layer as this layer defines what the final
output of the network will be. The layers before the output layer are called hidden layers.
The term hidden comes from the fact that the behavior of these layers are not defined
by the training data. The learning algorithm must decide how to use these layers to
produce the best approximation of the function f. The training data shows the desired
output of the output layer but not the desired output of each of the hidden layers. The
number of layers or the overall length of the chain of functions in the feedforward neural
network defines the depth of the network.
Artificial neural networks are loosely inspired by neuroscience. Each hidden layer in
a network is typically vector valued and the width of the network is defined by the
dimensionality of the hidden layers. Instead of thinking of each layer in the network as
representing a single vector-to-vector function, each layer can be considered consisting
of many units each representing a vector-to-scalar function acting in parallel. This way,
each unit in each layer can be interpreted as a artificial neuron. The structure of these
artificial neurons is described in the following section (Goodfellow et al., 2016).

2.3.3
Gradient-Based Learning
In the previous sections the architecture of the basic feedforward neural network has
been discussed. In this section the methods for training feedforward neural networks
will be described. Many machine learning models use gradient descent in training and
this method can also be used for training deep neural networks. A loss function, also
called cost, objective, or error function, is defined and this function is minimized by
optimizing the weights and biases of the artificial neural network.
One large difference between the classic linear machine learning models and artificial
neural networks is the non-linearity of neural networks that is achieved by applying ac-
tivation functions described in section 2.3.2. This non-linearity of the neural networks
causes a lot of loss functions to become non-convex. Because of this, neural networks
cannot be trained using for example linear equation solvers used to train linear regres-
sion models or the convex optimization algorithms which guarantees global convergence
used to train SVMs or logistic regression models. Instead neural networks are trained
by using iterative gradient-based optimizers driving the loss function to a very low value.
Convex optimization converges but gradient descent applied to non-convex loss func-
tions has no guarantee of convergence and is very sensitive to the initial values of the
parameters.
Different algorithms exists for the iterative gradient-based optimization
method of training feedforward neural networks. Common for most of them is that the
gradient of the loss function is used to descend the value of the loss (Goodfellow et al.,
2016; Nielsen, 2015).
2.3.3.1
Gradient Descent and Stochastic Gradient Descent
A loss function measures the performance of a neural network after computing predic-
tions for the given input. A common understanding when doing deep learning is to
minimize the loss function so that a minimum loss gives the best performing model.
Most neural networks is now trained using maximum likelihood which means that the
loss function used in most neural networks is the negative log-likelihood.
Given the weights and biases of a neural network the loss function determines how these
should be updated in order to minimize the loss of the model. The gradient of the loss
function is computed to find the steepest descent of the function and the weights and
==PAGE:25==
2.3 Artificial Neural Networks
15
biases are updated accordingly. This method is also called gradient descent. The loss
function is defined with respect to a training dataset which means that in every step the
entire training dataset has to be processed in order to compute the gradient of the loss
function. This method however does not work well in training deep neural networks.
Instead of processing the full training dataset before computing the gradient there is
an algorithm for gradient descent which is very useful in practice. This algorithm, also
known as stochastic gradient descent or sequential gradient descent estimates the true
gradient based on a subset of the dataset. Usually this is implemented by dividing the
training data in batches of data points and then updates to weight and biases can be
made based on one batch at a time.
A neural network consists of a number of layers which represent multiple connected
functions. Computing the gradient of a neural network involves computing the gradients
or partial derivatives of each function in the network. This means the gradients has to
be propagated back through each function in the network. The algorithm for computing
the gradients of the neural network and propagating all updates of weights and biases
back through the network is called backpropagation (Goodfellow et al., 2016; Nielsen,
2015).

the weights and biases of the network.

Recurrent Neural Networks
Responsible: Gustav Madslund (s142800)
In section 2.3 the basic feedforward neural network was described where information
was only propagated forward in the network. Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) include
feedback connections such that the output of an intermediate function is fed back into
itself as input in a later time step.
This makes the RNNs capable of preserving a
memory of previous inputs which can influence later outputs. This preserved memory
by using feedback connections make recurrent neural networks specialized for processing
sequential data x(1), ..., x(T) where x(1) indicates the element at the first time step and
x(T) is the sequence element at the last time step. This means that RNNs are useful
when doing sequence learning as they are able to scale to much longer sequences than
networks that are not specialized for sequence-based data. Recurrent neural networks
are also often capable of processing sequences with variable length. To get this behavior
and go from the feedforward neural networks to RNNs, parameters need to be shared
across different parts of the network instead of having separate parameters for each
value of the time index. If the network has separate parameters for each value of the
time index, it is not possible to generalize to variable sequence lengths not seen during
training and different time positions.
Sharing of the parameters are especially important when the needed information can
occur in multiple positions within a sequence. As an example the two sentences ”In 2010,
I went to Denmark” and ”I went to Denmark in 2010” can be considered. If a network
is trained to read each sentence and output the year that someone went to Denmark,
2010 should be considered as the important information, whether the word is positioned
as the second word or the sixth word in the sentence. A traditional feedforward neural
network would have separate parameters for each input feature thus it would need to
learn every rule of the language at each sequence position. By comparison, weights are

2.5.3
Vanishing & Exploding Gradient
The vanishing and exploding gradient problem arises during training of feedforward and
recurrent neural networks. The problem was first described in the 1990’s (Hochreiter,
1991; Bengio et al., 1993, 1994; Hochreiter et al., 2001) and it happens when the gradient
exponentially decreases or increases during training. For feedforward neural networks
the backpropagation error typically decreases or increases exponentially as a function
of the distance from the final layer and for recurrent neural networks the same thing
happens when moving far back in time (Sussillo and Abbott, 2014). This means that the
risk for exploding and vanishing gradients rise with long sequences and deeper neural
networks. For example if the gradient vanishes, where the norm go exponentially fast
towards 0, the network becomes unable to learn as this causes very little change in
weights and the network will not learn correlations between events that are many time
steps apart (Pascanu et al., 2012).

Standard RNNs are good for sequential data but they lack the ability to learn long
term dependencies. Consider the following long sample text which starts with ”I could
not afford a football...” and at some point ends with ”...
Thus, I am not an expert
at playing soccer.”. Say we want to predict the word soccer. A standard RNN would

any context. We need a network that is able to store this context of a football that
was presented early on, in order to guess the word soccer. This is where Long short-
term memory (LSTM) (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber, 1997) can be helpful. They are
capable of learning long-term dependencies due to their memory cell which is regulated
by various so called gates.

as old information.

information. If something is important, like the football or the gender of the current
subject, this special network structure allows us to remember it for later, and can forget
about it when a new sport, or a new subject comes up. The network is mathematically

2.5.6
Encoder-Decoder Architecture
The encoder-decoder (Cho et al., 2014b) or sequence-to-sequence (Sutskever et al., 2014)
architecture was first proposed as a way to map a variable-length sequence to another
variable-length sequence. Previous architectures constrained the input sequence X =
x1, ..., xT and predicted sequence Y = y1, ..., yN to be the same length such that T = N,
which complicates tasks such as neural machine translation (NMT) where languages
might not use same amount of words to express the same sentence.
In its most basic form, an encoder-decoder network consist of an encoder RNN that
learns to encode the variable-length input sequence X into a fixed-length context vector
c = hT (last hidden state) and a decoder RNN that learns to decode C back into a
variable-length output sequence Y where T and N are not necessarily the same. The
encoder RNN works as described in section 2.5 while the decoder will use the context
vector as its initial hidden state and a special ”start-of-string” (SOS) as its initial input
token. The encoder RNN will then use its predicted words as the next input tokens until
a ”end-of-string” (EOS) token is generated.
Note that while c encodes context from the entire sequence, it can be too small to
properly capture the information that lies in a long sequence (Cho et al., 2014a). We will
therefore be using the hidden states h1, ..., hT of the entire sequence rather than just the
last hidden state hT as input to decoders capable of predicting subcellular localization

2.7
Regularization
Responsible: Mikkel Møller Brusen (s134568)
Artificial neural networks can be built of many layers, allowing them to capture subtle
nuances and learn complicated relationships between the inputs and outputs. While
this is what makes them so powerful, it also makes them extremely prone to overfitting,
resulting in networks that perform very well on the training set but generalize poorly
towards the test set. An obvious solution to this problem could be to create less complex
networks, but this might not always be desirable. In order to keep a complex structure
and generalize well, we use so called regularization techniques. In the following, a few
regularization techniques used in this project will be presented.
2.7.1
Dropout
The first regularization technique that we will introduce is dropout. Dropout is a simple
yet efficient technique that randomly sets unit activations to 0 with a probability p
during the training of the network. This forces the network to not rely solely on specific
structures in the input data and thus generalize better (Srivastava et al., 2014).
2.7.2
Gradient Clipping
We mentioned a problem with RNNs in section 2.5.3 referred to as vanishing & exploding
gradients. A simple mechanism to deal with a sudden big change in the gradients is to
clip them whenever they go outside a defined range. This is typically done by either
re-scaling the total norm of the gradient vector or by resetting individual gradient values
(Pascanu et al., 2012; Sutskever et al., 2014).

2.9

2.9.3
Perplexity
When measuring the performance of a language model, we usually do it based on perplex-
ity. Perplexity is a measure of how ”confused” a language model is, that is, how many
words is the language model choosing between on average when predicting a sequence
of words. A language model with perplexity 10 can thus be said to have an average of
10 choices per word, while a language model with perplexity 20 will have an average of
20 choices per word. The higher the perplexity, the more choices we have and the more
random the language model will be, hence we want a language model with low perplexity.

Now that the fundamental concepts used in this project have been covered we can take

Considerable progress has been made in the field of NLP and protein prediction tasks in
recent years thanks to deep learning (Young et al., 2017; Heffernan et al., 2015; Wang
et al., 2016) and it turns out that methods used for NLP can be useful for protein
prediction tasks too. In the following we outline previous work of the two fields which

Statistical NLP has become the primary option when modeling complex natural lan-
guage processing tasks. However, in the beginning the technique suffered from the curse
of dimensionality. For example if one tries to model an n-gram language model that has
a vocabulary with size 10000, there could potentially be 10000n−1 free parameters. This
issue became motivation for the learning of low-dimensional distributed word representa-
tions, also called embeddings, by using a language model (Bengio et al., 2003). The first
to show the use of pre-trained word embeddings for NLP tasks were Collobert and We-
ston (2008) but the use of pre-trained word embeddings first gained real popularity with
the continuous bag-of-words model (CBOW) along with the skip-gram model (Mikolov
et al., 2013a,b). These models efficiently constructs distributed vector representations
of high-quality. Later another word embedding method called Glove (Pennington et al.,
2014) was presented. Glove is essentially a count-based model where the co-occurence
matrix is pre-processed in different ways to obtain lower dimensional word representa-
tions. Since the introduction of CBOW, skip-gram and Glove, pre-trained embeddings
have been essential for NLP.
The word embedding methods such as CBOW and Glove creates a global vector repre-
sentation of a word by considering all the sentences where the word is present. However
a word often has completely different meanings depending on the contexts. Methods
using deeper networks to compute word embeddings as a function of the words contexts
exist. One such method is ELMo or Embeddings from Language Model (Peters et al.,
2018) where different embeddings are produced for a word for each context in which the
word is used. This means that for N different sentences where a word w is used, ELMo
produces N different deep contextualized embeddings. Recently another method for
generating deep contextualized word embeddings was presented. This method is called
BERT or Bidirectional Encoder from Transformers (Devlin et al., 2018). Compared to
ELMo, which simply is a bi-directional language model predicting the next word given
the preceding sequence, BERT instead performs multiple tasks for pre-training the lan-
guage model. One task is to mask a percentage of words and only predict the masked
words and another is to predict the next sentence given a sentence. When BERT was
introduced it reported state-of-the-art performance on eleven NLP tasks.
As seen with ELMo and BERT, language models are essential when producing contextu-
alized word embeddings. The language model mentioned earlier by Bengio et al. (2003)
used a feedforward neural network. Later Mikolov et al. (2010) introduced the use of
RNNs in language models. Since then, RNN based models have been the center for
language model research with the LSTM language model introduced by Sundermeyer
et al. (2012) addressing the difficulty of learning long term dependencies. The following
years LSTMs were extensively used in language models and different ways of regularizing
the networks were presented (Zaremba et al., 2014; Gal and Ghahramani, 2016; Grave
et al., 2016; Melis et al., 2017). The use of some specialized regularization techniques

It was not until

3.4
Pre-trained Amino Acid Embeddings
Responsible: Mikkel Møller Brusen (s134568)
In NLP tasks language models and pre-trained word embeddings are widely used to
improve performance of NLP problems, as described in section 2.4 and section 2.10.
However using word vectors as embeddings does not capture the context, that each
word is in, as the embedding is always the same. In Peters et al. (2018) it was shown
with ELMo that context could be included by using the internal layers of a bi-directional
language model as pre-trained word embeddings. Creating embeddings this way means
that the same word can have different representations depending on the context.
No such pre-trained embeddings exists for protein prediction tasks which is why similar
embeddings for amino acids have been created for this project with inspiration from

ELMo. The pre-trained embeddings have been created by training a language model
on large data sets of amino acid sequences. Using the internal layers of the language
model as embeddings should add context to the amino acid representations, which is
valuable information, for example, for secondary structure prediction. These pre-trained
embeddings will be incorporated into subcellular localization and secondary structure
prediction models to evaluate if they improve the performance on these tasks. In the
following sections the model architecture of the two different language models, which
have been trained to create amino acid embeddings, will be presented. One is a state-
of-the-art language model in NLP and the other is the same architecture but using a
bi-directional LSTM.
3.4.1
AWD-LSTM
The AWD-LSTM (Merity et al., 2017), is a state-of-the-art language model which have
had impressive word level perplexities on the Penn Treebank and WikiText-2 data sets.
The language model is basically just an LSTM with different specialized regularization
techniques.
The name of the language model comes from these regularization tech-
niques. ASGD or average stochastic gradient descent is the same as SGD, described in
section 2.3.3, but instead of returning the last iteration, over a batch of data points, it
returns an average of the iterations past a certain threshold (Polyak and Juditsky, 1992).
In the AWD-LSTM the averaging SGD is triggered when the validation metric has not
improved for several cycles. Another regularization technique used is weight-drop or
DropConnect where dropout is apply on the hidden-to-hidden weight matrices in the
LSTM once before each forward-backward pass (Wan et al., 2013). The AWD-LSTM
also uses other regularization techniques such as variational dropout, where the same
dropout mask is used on repeated connections in the network, and embedding dropout
which means dropped out words will disappear in a full forward backward pass (Gal and
Ghahramani, 2016).
This state-of-the-art langauge model has been trained on amino acid sequences, with-
out modifications, to create pre-trained embeddings which can be evaluated on protein
prediction tasks.
3.4.2
AWD-Bi-LSTM
Unlike the AWD-LSTM, which uses a uni-directional LSTM, ELMo use bi-directional
LSTMs in the language model when training the word embeddings. This was used as
inspiration for adding bi-directionality to the AWD-LSTM architecture. A language
model, with this bi-directional architecture, was trained on amino acid sequences to
create ELMo-like embeddings for amino acids. This way it is possible to evaluate if
additional information can be learned from embeddings created using the bi-directional
architecture.

Encoders
Encoders are found in the models/encoders directory and from here an encoder can be
imported into the model of a configuration. Encoders are general purpose and should

encoders will contain some kind of RNN to compute an output which will be fed to the
chosen decoder. However other neural network layers such as non-linear, convolutional
and embedding-layers can also be part of the context vector computation.

4.3.3.1
Bi-directional Language Model
The bidirectionality was added to the AWD-LSTM by adding three LSTMs for the
backward direction, having six LSTMs in total. This language model was then trained
by providing both the normal input to the three forward directional LSTMs and the
reversed input to the three backward direction LSTMs.
This became an issue, when loading in the pre-trained model, as input used in this
project is not loaded in the reversed direction. In our first implementation, we used the
same architecture as it was trained with and simply reversed the input to the backward
direction LSTMs after which we reversed it back to normal to concatenate the outputs
of the two directions. While this solution was simple, reversing the sequences proved
computationally inefficient as no fast implementation of this reversing exist in PyTorch.
In the final implementation we use normal uni-directional LSTMs to obtain the forward
direction of the bi-directional output. Then for the backward direction, the normal input
was applied to LSTMs by adding a bidirectional=True argument. The forward part
of each of these bi-directional LSTMs was then ignored and only the backward part of
the concatenated outputs, from each LSTM, was used to obtain the backward direction
of the bi-directional output.
With this method it was possible to load the forward
direction parameters from the pre-trained model into the uni-directional LSTMs while
the backward direction parameters were loaded into the backward direction part of the
LSTMs with the bidirectional=True argument. Some renaming of the state dictionary
parameters for the backward direction was needed and carried out with a utility function.

chosen as the best solution as the models are trained on 32GB GPUs.
4.4
Data Loaders
Responsible: Mikkel Møller Brusen (s134568)
Data loaders are important when doing deep learning, and machine learning in general,
as they load data in the format required by the model. Usually data cannot be loaded
straight into the neural network without any prepossessing because it is provided in a
file where features are mixed together. Developing this pre-processing can be challenging
as knowledge about the properties of the dataset is required to make sure it is loaded
in the correct way. The data loader scripts used in this project are based, with some
modifications, on scripts from the known models used as baselines. The properties and
the specifics of each dataset, used in this project, will be presented in section 5.1.
As this project works with distinct protein prediction tasks, different data loader scripts
are needed for each task. These scripts can be found in the directory dataloaders.
Each data loader script is named after the task in which they are used and contain
methods for performing data loading of the datasets for the specific tasks. For each task,
data will be loaded from a file specified in the configuration and then the data will be
pre-processed to prepare it for use in training.
The data is pre-processed in multiple ways for example by splitting the data into training,
validation and test sets and saving it appropriately in memory. This should be done such
that input and targets are stored separately in a way where it is easy to associate an input
with its corresponding target. Additionally the lengths of the different sequences should
be calculated from the provided masks such that padding can be handled appropriately
throughout the models. Pre-processing can also be to remove unusable data points from
the dataset or sorting the data in convenient ways.
The final job of the data loader script is to divide the data into batches. Each data
loader script, in this project, contains a batch generator which will divide a dataset into
batches. This generator will be responsible for doing appropriate shuffling of the batches,
such that the batches change between epochs, making it easier for the loss function to
converge. Then the generator can yield the batches one by one during training.

5.1.1
DeepLoc
The DeepLoc dataset was introduced by Almagro Armenteros et al. (2017) as a result of
a new release of the UniProt database as well as poor homology partitioning in previous
datasets used to train and evaluate models for subcellular localization prediction. The
DeepLoc dataset consists of a total of 13,858 eukaryotic proteins split into 5 sets with
homology partitioning based on 30% identity and with approximately the same number
of proteins for each subcellular location in each set. A total of 10 main locations is rep-
resented in the dataset, with numerous sublocations grouped together under each main
location, resulting in the following 10 main locations: Nucleus, Cytoplasm, Extracellu-

lar, Mitochondrion, Cell membrane, ER, Plastid, Golgi apparatus, Lysosome/Vacuole
and Peroxisome. Furthermore, some of the proteins are tagged as being membrane or
soluble, or unknown if no such information were found.
Not all locations are equally represented, in fact, the most represented location, Nucleus,
consist of 4043 proteins while Peroxisome only consist of 154 proteins. Similar imbal-
ance exist for the membrane/soluble and we must therefore be careful with evaluating
models trained on this set based on their accuracies alone but also consider metrics such
as MCC and Gorodkin.
We used two versions of this dataset, one with raw amino acid sequences and one where
each amino acid had been encoded by profiles as in the official paper. In the dataset
using raw sequences, we found four sequences in the test set that contained amino acid
tokens unknown to the pre-trained language model. We removed these sequences and all
reported test accuracies have been scaled as if those four were wrongly classified. The
removed sequences were two Extracellular and two Plastid.
Additionally the datasets have been pre-processed such that sequences longer than 1000
were truncated, removing the middle to not lose information about the sorting signals
from the N and C-terminus. A total of 9.98% of proteins were truncated using this rule,
and it was carried out to properly replicate and evaluate against the results reported by
Almagro Armenteros et al. (2017), who also performed this truncation.
The simple Uniform, Unigram an Majority class prediction models achieve 10%, 17%
and 29% accuracy respectively when predicting subcellular localization on this dataset.
5.1.2
CullPDB
The CullPDB dataset is a large non-homologous dataset of protein sequences produced
by Zhou and Troyanskaya (2014) using the PISCES Cull PDB server (Wang and Dun-
brack, 2003). PISCES is a public server for culling datasets of protein sequences from
the Protein Data Bank (PDB). The sequences in the dataset has been labeled with
secondary structure through the Dictionary of Protein Secondary Structure (DSSP) pro-
gram (Kabsch and Sander, 1983) which automatically assigns secondary structure of
each sequence into eight classes according to hydrogen bonding. These eight classes are
H (α-helix), E (β-strand), B (β-bridge), T (beta-turn), S (bend), I (π-helix), G (310-
helix), and L (other/loop/irregular). The full dataset consists of 6128 proteins where
no two proteins share more than 30% sequence identity with each other. No proteins
shorter than 50 or longer than 700 amino acids are included in the dataset. The version
of the CullPDB dataset used in this project is a filtered version where sequences with
above 25% identity with the CB513 dataset are removed. The result is a training set
containing 5278 proteins and a validation set containing 256 proteins. These training
and validation sets will be used in secondary structure prediction and the CB513 dataset
will be used for testing.

5.1.3
CB513
The CB513 dataset (Cuff and Barton, 1999) consists of 513 proteins where any two
proteins share less than 25% identity.
One protein sequence in the CB513 dataset
is above 700 amino acids long and this sequence has been split into two overlapping
sequences resulting in a total of 514 protein sequences1 in the version of the dataset

For both the CullPDB and the CB513 datasets features and labels are included where
each sequence position has 52 channels. 22 is for one-hot encoding of the amino acid,
another 22 is the sequence profile, 8 for the secondary structure labels, 2 for N and
C-terminals, and 2 for solvent accessibility labels. In this project when doing secondary
structure prediction using sequence profiles only the one-hot encodings and the sequence
profiles are loaded from the datasets as input features and the secondary structure labels
are loaded as targets.
When doing secondary structure prediction on the raw sequences of amino acid residues
new datasets were extracted from the CullPDB and CB513. In these new datasets the

acid residues, based on the dictionary of the language models used for pre-trained amino
acid embeddings. In these datasets X was replaced by A as X is unknown to the pre-

profiles both using the original datasets and the ones where X had been replaced. These
baselines reached similar performance thus only the datasets where X has been replaced
are used for secondary structure prediction.
The simple Uniform, Unigram and Majority class prediction models achieve 12, 5%, 21%

In this section the different encoders and decoders used in experiments will be presented.
Some additional encoders and decoders can be found in the code base but these have

5.2.1
Encoders
Most of the encoders that will be presented in this section take their general structure
from the encoder used by Almagro Armenteros et al. (2017) which we have replicated in
the encoder deeploc_profiles. Furthermore, most encoders will explore different ways
of incorporating information from the uni and bi-directional AWD-LSTMs, while two
encoders, awd and bi_awd actually consist just of these language models. In order to best
describe all the available encoders we will start out by giving an in depth description
of deeploc_profiles, awd and bi_awd which should serve as a good reference when
we describe the rest. Note that when we refer to the sequence as being 1000 in length,
shorter lengths are possible but 1000 is the maximum sequence length our network will
process thus it will be used to describe the sequence length.
deeploc_profiles The architecture of this encoder is visualized in Figure 5.1a and
takes a sequence of amino acid profiles as input (A). The input sequence is then
fed to 6 convolutional layers (B) that are positioned in parallel, all consisting of 20
filters but with varying kernel sizes to detect motifs of varying lengths. Concate-
nating all the feature maps yields a 1000×120 feature map (C) on which we apply
another convolutional layer (D) with 128 filters and kernel size 3 to get a more
abstract representation of the input, motivated by its success in Sønderby et al.
(2015). The output of this convolutional layer is then passed to the bi-directional
encoder LSTM (E) with 256 hidden units in each direction resulting in a 512-
dimensional representation of each amino acid (F). All convolutional layers use a
ReLU activation function and we employ three dropout layers, two between (A)
and (B) (referred to as input dropout) which is a normal and a channel dropout
(a channel corresponds to a position in the sequence which will be zeroed out)
and one between (D) and (E) which is a normal dropout (referred to as internal
dropout).

awd The architecture of this encoder is visualized in Figure 5.1b with the dotted area
excluded and is an exact replica of the AWD-LSTM architecture (Merity et al.,
2017) which was described in section 3.4.1 with all dropouts set to 10% and weight
tying applied. It takes raw sequences as input (I) and employs an embedding layer
with 320 dimensions (II) on this input. The embeddings are then processed by
three uni-directional LSTMs (III) put in sequence with 1280, 1280 and 320 hidden
units respectively, yielding a 320-dimensional representation of each amino acid
(IV) which can be used for language modelling by using the decoding layer (V)
which produces a sequence of amino acid probabilities. The output of this encoder
is a tuple containing the outputs of all hidden layers of the encoder, allowing us
to explore which layers that contain the best representation of the amino acids.
bi_awd This encoder is similar to awd but with the backward LSTMs in dotted area of
Figure 5.1b included. As such, we get contextualized embeddings that are twice the
size when using this bi-directional encoder rather than its uni-directional version.
While both awd and bi_awd are available as stand-alone encoders we will usually be
using them as part of our other encoders by extracting the hidden states of the LSTM
layers (III-a, III-b and III-c in Figure 5.1b) and use them as pre-trained amino acid
embeddings. In the following we will present deeploc_raw which is a slight modification
to deeploc_profile and various encoders that explore different ways to incorporate the
pre-trained embeddings in the deeploc_raw encoder architecture.
deeploc_raw This encoder is mostly identical to deeploc_profiles but takes raw se-

(A) and (B) to mimic the profiles.
awd_deeploc This encoder extracts hidden states of the first (III-a), second (III-b) or

Common to all the variants is that the hidden states are projected down to 300
dimensions using a linear layer with no bias to keep the dimensions low and prevent
overfitting. The first variant concatenates the projected hidden states between
(D) and (E) allowing the bi-directional LSTM to process the embeddings. The
second variant concatenates the projected hidden states to the output (F) of the
bi-directional LSTM resulting in a larger and possibly richer encoder output. The
third variant combines both of the aforementioned variants. Dropout is performed
similarly to deeploc_raw where the internal dropout between (D) and (E) happens
after the concatenation.
bi_awd_deeploc Similar to awd_deeploc but using the AWD-Bi-LSTM language model
resulting in embeddings of twice the size and hopefully a richer representation, but
still projected down to 300.

soenderby This encoder is a replication of the network described in Sønderby and
Winther (2014), with some slight modifications, and it takes amino acid sequence
profiles as input. The encoder consists of two ReLU activated linear layers, each
with 400 hidden units, and a 3-layer bi-directional LSTM with 500 hidden units
in each direction. In the network described in the paper the linear layers were
inserted between recurrent hidden states. However this was not easily done in
PyTorch so the linear layers were implemented before the LSTM in this encoder.
soenderby_raw Modifications were made to the encoder described above to make a
version which takes raw amino acid sequences as input. An embedding layer of
size 20 were inserted before the first two ReLU activated linear layers to imitate
the sequence profiles.
bi_awd_soenderby_profiles This encoder extracts hidden states of the second (III-b)
or third (III-c) LSTM layer of the Bi-AWD-LSTM and incorporates them in the
soenderby architecture. The hidden states can be concatenated before and after
the 3-layer LSTM. The encoder takes both sequence profiles and the corresponding
raw sequences of amino acid residues as input.
5.2.2
Decoders
The decoders are task specific and we will thus describe the decoders used for each
protein prediction task separately.
5.2.2.1
Subcellular Localization
For the subcellular localization prediction task we have mainly been using a decoder
architecture similar to the one used by Almagro Armenteros et al. (2017) which is im-
plemented in the deeploc_subcel decoder and explained below together with two other
decoders that were explored.
deeploc_subcel This decoder takes a sequence of N-dimensional amino acid represen-
tations as input and employs the attention mechanism to select the representation
or combination of representations that best represents the sequence. It then em-
ploys a linear layer with N hidden units on the attention output followed by two
linear output layers with 1 unit (membrane/soluble) and 10 units (subcellular local-
ization) respectively. Internal dropout is performed after the attention mechanism
and after the linear layer. Note that while Almagro Armenteros et al. (2017) per-
form multi step attention (attention on the attention output repeated for a number
of steps), we only perform a single step as we found it to have the same effect.

attention_subcel This decoder is similar to deeploc_subcel but does not use the
linear layer with N hidden units and instead employ the linear output layers directly
on the attention output to reduce parameters.

seqvec_subcel As mentioned in section 2.10, advancements towards using pre-trained
amino acid embeddings have been carried out concurrently with this project. To
evaluate our embeddings against the results reported by Heinzinger et al. (2019) we
have created this decoder which is similar to their SeqVec decoder for subcellular
localization. This decoder does not utilize attention but requires its input to be
an average of the N-dimensional representations across the sequence dimension.
It then projects the averaged representations down to 32 dimensions with a linear
layer on which 25% dropout, ReLU and batch normalization is applied. The output
layers are similar to those described in the previous decoders.
In the concrete case where we will be using this decoder the input will be the first
and second hidden states from the pre-trained language model where we add the
hidden layers together and average across the sequence length to obtain a input of
the requested shape.
5.2.2.2
Secondary Structure Prediction
The first decoder, mlp2, described has been implemented to complete the architecture
used by Sønderby and Winther (2014). The lstm_mlp2 decoder has been created by
taking incremental steps to find the best performing decoder which will be used for
evaluating performance when pre-trained amino acid embeddings are added.
mlp2 This decoder takes a sequence of N-dimensional amino acid representations as
input and feeds the input to two ReLU activated linear layers, each with 500
hidden units and internal dropout between each layer. A linear layer computes
the final prediction of the decoder.
lstm_mlp2 A bi-directional LSTM with 500 hidden units has been added before the
two ReLU activated linear layers in the mlp2 decoder to see if mixing information
will improve performance.
seqvec_secpred In Heinzinger et al. (2019) they also presented results for secondary
structure prediction and this decoder is a replication of the SeqVec decoder they
used for secondary structure prediction. The input is the same as for the seqvec_subcel
but without averaging across the sequence dimension. The decoder is a ReLU ac-
tivated CNN with 32 filters and a kernel size of 7. The final prediction is made
by a CNN layer with 8 filters and a kernel size of 7. Dropout of 25% is applied
between the two CNN layers.

In the following we describe the experiments that have been carried our for each of the
two protein prediction tasks and the configurations that we have found most relevant in
evaluating the pre-trained amino acid embeddings.
Our experiments have mainly been focused around different architectures rather than hy-
perparameter tuning, as such, most hyperparameters used throughout our experiments
are equivalent to those used in Almagro Armenteros et al. (2017) for subcellular localiza-
tion and Sønderby and Winther (2014) for secondary structure prediction. An extensive
hyperparameter search could have been performed using tools such as SigOpt 2 but was
not feasible within the projects time-frame and remains future work.
In all reported experiments the parameters of the pre-trained language models were
frozen and as such were not updated by the optimizers. We did try fine tuning the
language models to the tasks but that worsened the performance in all cases.
Based on early experiments we made the decision to only carry out experiments on the
second or third layer under the assumption that these layers would capture all the needed
information. This decision was taken to keep the total number of configurations down
as each model takes up to 24 hours to train on a V100 GPU. A few models utilizing
the first hidden layer were however added for comparison but exploring all architectures
with the first layer remains future work.
The usage of ”(bi_)” in the following two subsections indicates that a particular con-
figuration exists in two versions, one utilizing the uni-directional AWD-LSTM language
model and one utilizing the bi-directional AWD-LSTM language model.

and the deeploc_subcel decoder. A baseline for raw sequences is created using the
deeploc_raw encoder instead. With these baselines in place, we seek to evaluate the
use of the second or third (last) hidden layers from both the uni and bi-directional
language models. The experiments that were carried out to do this evaluation include
feeding the hidden states to a simple attention decoder, feeding them to a DeepLoc
architecture without CNNs and lastly concatenating them into various places in the full
DeepLoc architecture. Additionally, the hidden states have been fed to the SeqVec ar-
chitecture from Heinzinger et al. (2019) to see if the pre-trained embeddings from this

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
7.1
Model Selection
Responsible: Gustav Madslund (s142800)
Throughout our experiments we found that the pre-trained amino acid embeddings in
fact do capture information that is exploitable for protein prediction tasks, however,
that information is not much richer than the raw input.
We found that in the simplest architectures such as group (2) in Figure 6.1 and Figure 6.2,
the bi-directionality captured additional information which led to significant increase
in performance compared to the uni-directional embeddings, which was also the case
for our models using the simple SeqVec decoder as seen in Table 6.2 and Table 6.4.
Additionally, we found that utilizing the second hidden layer in the simple group (2)
architectures yielded better results than the first or last hidden layer.
We then saw that introducing a Bi-LSTM layer in either the encoder or decoder almost
completely removed the differences between utilized embedding layers and directionality.
A small tendency towards the bi-directional language models being superior was still
observable, but not at all significant. A noteworthy observation however, was that by
using only a hidden layer as input to a simple Bi-LSTM encoder, (group (3) in subcellular
localization and group (4) in secondary structure prediction), we achieved performances
that were comparable to all of the more complex architectures that followed. This could
indicate that we at least capture information as valuable as the information captured
by the CNNs.
Through our search for optimal places to incorporate the embeddings in the DeepLoc
encoder, we found that placement has no significant effect. As soon as a Bi-LSTM is
used in the encoder on either the raw input or the embeddings, all additional changes
had minor effect. This is especially visible from the top four subcellular localization
models listed in Table 6.1 which contains a mix of all four complex architecture groups,
comparable on both accuracy and ability to handle class imbalance. This could indicate
either that the embeddings does not carry additional information, or that we reached a
limit for what our encoder architecture is able to capture with the chosen hyperparam-
eters. We should however note that we saw a general pattern towards overfitting in all
models utilizing Bi-LSTMs which could call for additional regularization techniques.

7 Discussion
7.2
Final Model
Responsible: Mikkel Møller Brusen (s134568)
In Table 6.6 and Table 6.9 it is seen that our best models have improved performance
in comparison to the baselines on raw sequences. This again indicates that some infor-
mation can be drawn from the pre-trained aimino acid embeddings.
The improvement for subcellular localization was 0.47% which was smaller than expected
as the validation performance showed a larger improvement. Looking at the confusion
matrices in Table 6.7 we saw that prediction performance improved for all locations
except three, which were highly represented locations. Prediction performance increased
for all lower represented locations, which could indicate that embeddings helps the model
handle class imbalance better. The SeqVec comparison in Table 6.8 showed that the
embeddings used in this project performed similarly to the results reported by Heinzinger
et al. (2019) for subcellular localization. This is impressive as the language models used
in this project were only trained on a dataset less than one-hundredth the size of the
dataset used for training the SeqVec language model.
For secondary structure prediction the improvement was 1.47% which was closer to
what was expected from the performance seen on validation. The SeqVec comparison
in Table 6.10 showed that the results reported by Heinzinger et al. (2019) could not
be replicated for secondary structure as performance was 5.63% lower using our embed-
dings. The soenderby_profiles_with_raw model improved performance compared to
the soenderby baseline, although we hoped for a higher improvement when both profiles
and embeddings were utilized.
From the reported results it was clear that profiles performed significantly better than
the pre-trained embeddings on both evaluated tasks.
In subcellular localization the
difference was 2.31% which was small compared to secondary structure where the dif-
ference was 11.4%. The difference seen for subcellular localization could be due to the
fact that 9% of the proteins in the DeepLoc dataset has been shortened to 1000 amino
acids by removing the middle of the sequences.
This modification could potentially
confuse the language model and decrease overall performance. However the difference
could also be a result of the embeddings not holding the same amount of information as
sequence profiles. The big difference seen for secondary structure could be because the
language models used in this project were trained only on eukaryotic proteins and the
CB513 dataset unfortunately also contained prokaryotic proteins. This could also be
the reason why the soenderby_profiles_with_raw configuration did not show a larger
improvement and why it was not possible to replicate the SeqVec results for secondary
structure prediction as the language model used in SecVec was trained on prokaryotic
proteins also.

7.3 Future Work
71
7.3
Future Work
Responsible: Mikkel Møller Brusen (s134568)
To keep the total number of experiments within a feasible scale we made the decision to
explore architectures rather than hyperparameters. Now that we have a good variety of
architectures, it could be interesting to explore hyperparameters to see if tuning these
could help the networks utilize the embeddings better.
Similarly, we only explored the first hidden layer in a couple of our architectures to
demonstrate its poor performance and keep the total amount of models low. It could
however be interesting to see how it performed in all architectures to make a definitive
conclusion about its performance.
The pre-trained language models evaluated in this project were both trained solely on
eukaryotic proteins. It could therefore be interesting to see how our secondary structure
prediction models perform on datasets consisting solely of eukaryotic proteins. Simi-
larly, it could be interesting to see if a language model trained on half prokaryotic, half
eukaryotic proteins could help our models achieve better performance.
Throughout all our experiments, we saw a general tendency for overfitting both in simple
and complex architectures. An exploration of additional regularization techniques could
possibly allow us to generalize better when using the pre-trained embeddings.
The pre-trained language models contain a decoding layer capable of predicting the next
amino acid in the sequence. We did not get the opportunity to experiment with this
layer, however, it could be interesting to see if the predicted amino acid distributions
carry information similar to that of profiles. Maybe using this distribution will allow
models that rely on external tools for profile encoding to use pre-trained embeddings
instead.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
Responsible: Gustav Madslund (s142800)
Through this project, a framework for evaluation of pre-trained amino acid embeddings
in protein prediction tasks have been developed. We have used this framework to evalu-
ate pre-trained embeddings, extracted from a uni- and a bi-directional language model,
on the subcellular localization and secondary structure prediction tasks. We have ex-
plored various ways of incorporating the pre-trained embeddings into existing architec-
tures and have found that by utilizing embeddings, we are able to slightly improve
performance. We are however, not able to reach a level where profiles are no longer
needed to reach state-of-the-art performance.
Through experiments we have evaluated the pre-trained amino acid embeddings in mul-
tiple ways. The first was to evaluate performance of the embeddings in simple models
where no encoder LSTM is used. Secondly embeddings are evaluated in more complex
models utilizing encoder LSTMs. These experiments have been carried out both where
the second layer and where the last layer of the language models have been used as em-
beddings. Finally it has been evaluated if highest performance is reached when adding
embeddings before, after or both before and after the encoder LSTM. Additionally a
few experiments using the first layer has been evaluated.
Through our exploration we found that in simple model architectures the embeddings
extracted from a bi-directional language model significantly outperform the embeddings
from a uni-directional language model. In the more complex architectures, however, we
found uni- and bi-directional embeddings to be equally good.
Similar observations were made in regards to which hidden layer of the language models
the embeddings were extracted from. The second layer was the most performant in
simple architectures and when an encoder LSTM was used in the more complex models
it was difficult to determine a tendency in which layer was best to use as embeddings.
The few experiments utilizing the first layer showed significantly worse performance than
the second and last layer.
For subcellular localization prediction, the best model utilizing embeddings reached an
accuracy of 72.77% which improved the baseline on raw amino acid residue sequences
by 0.47%.
The best model used the last layer of the bi-directional language model
as embeddings and added the embeddings before the encoder LSTM. The baseline for
==PAGE:84==

profile encoded sequences, however, was far superior with an accuracy of 75.08%.
For secondary structure prediction, the best model utilizing embeddings reached a per-
formance of 57.99% which improved the baseline on raw sequences by 1.47%. The best
model used the last layer of the bi-directional language model as embeddings and added
the embeddings both before and after the encoder LSTM. The baseline for profile en-
coded sequences, however, was also here far superior with an accuracy of 69.39%. By
utilizing both profiles and pre-trained embeddings, we were however able to also improve
upon this baseline by 0.39% with a final accuracy of 69.78% but we stress that this result
was achieved by utilizing both profiles and pre-trained embeddings.
In the pursuit of reaching the embeddings full potential and possibly alleviate the need
for profile encoding, future perspectives have been presented which among others include
handling the issue of prokaryotic proteins in the secondary structure data set and new
ways of utilizing the pre-trained language models for protein prediction tasks.
