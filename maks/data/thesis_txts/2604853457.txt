Abstract
The environment is suﬀering severely detrimental eﬀects caused by the ever-increasing heating and
cooling demands of urbanization. The United Nations has highlighted DES (District energy sys-
tems), as a solution to decarbonize the heating and cooling sector to mitigate its environmental
impact by improving energy eﬃciency.
The objective of this study is to develop an assessment
framework that performs a GIS-based Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) to highlight po-
tential areas for urban DES deployment based on energy demand and supply, potential renewable
energy sources, and cost-eﬀectiveness. Building on existing energy mapping and planning processes
for DES, it asks: which criteria should be considered to accelerate the deployment of urban district
energy systems through GIS-based energy mapping using multi-criteria decision analysis and how
can these criteria be assessed in a spatial context?
Based on the literature review and expert knowledge about urban DES deployment, a GIS-based
MCDA model was developed and applied to potential districts in Amsterdam and Lyon. The results
indicate that this GIS-based MCDA approach to land suitability analysis is advocated to technically
identify optimum areas for urban DES deployment according to ten essential feasibility factors. On
this basis, it is recommended that energy planners integrate GIS-based energy mapping as a key
component in designing and targeting strategic energy plans.
Keywords: district energy systems (DES), multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA), geo-
graphic information system (GIS), energy-mapping, energy planning.

1. Introduction
1.1
Urban energy demand
The future of the world’s population is undeniably urban (CPD 2018). In the last few decades,
global demographics have been subject to signiﬁcant population shifts toward urban areas. Since
1950, the global urban population has increased more than 25% and is expected to reach 68% by
2050 (UNDESA 2018). As shown in Figure 1.1, the urban population is projected to grow by 2.5
billion between 2018 and 2050, with nearly 90% of the increase concentrated in Asia and Africa
(UNDESA 2018). These tendencies are also pronounced in developed regions of the world, where
the urban population already accounts for 79% and is expected to reach 86% by 2050 (UNDESA
2018). The number and size of cities will increase in the next decades. This urban tendency will be
driven by multiple factors, including urbanization of formerly rural areas, the surplus of birth rates
over death rates in urban areas, and migrations from rural areas or abroad into urban areas (Lerch
2017).
Figure 1.1: Estimated and projected global urban populations (UNDESA 2018)
This rapid urbanization presents an immense threat and pressure on the environment, just as cur-
rent urban growth and operating patterns induce severely detrimental environmental impacts (Jaeger
et al. 2010). Urban areas have high environmental impacts due, in part, to their high-energy demand
and consumption. Indeed, cities account for around two-thirds of the world’s total energy consump-
tion, which corresponds to more than 70% of global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions (OECD/IEA
2016). Moreover, countries that are more urbanized tend to generate higher levels of GHG emissions.
This phenomenon can be attributed to increased economic activity, especially in emerging and devel-
oping countries, which tend to shift from carbon-neutral to carbon-intensive energy sources, leading
to increasing proportions of GHG emissions from cities (Kamal-Chaoui & Robert 2009, OECD 2008).
Generally, high energy use in urban areas is driven by increased living standards and higher demand
for goods and services, which results from wealth expansion and translates into higher electricity
production and energy usage in buildings as well as for transportation. Indeed, cities use energy for
lighting, heating, cooling, and the operation of machines and appliances but also consume energy
embedded in the manufacturing of consumer goods, foodstuﬀ, and building materials, which often
involves emissions elsewhere (UNECE 2011, Satterhwaite 2011).
1
==PAGE:9==
If actions are not taken, these phenomena will rise as countries develop because the demand for
energy is driven by rapid urbanization and industrialization. However, as much as urban areas are
part of the problem, they can play a role in solutions, because they hold signiﬁcant potential to
reduce GHG emissions. Energy, mobility, building, and innovating planning solutions in cities could
unlock signiﬁcant emission cuts while generating substantial co-beneﬁts in terms of public health
improvements and cost savings through increased energy eﬃciency and security (World Bank 2010).
Moreover, urban areas provide economic activity, population density, and intellectual capacity for
change. Cities could act as pilots and incubators for solutions to climate change since they are
home to institutions that give rise to productive collaboration across sectors and stakeholders. In-
deed, cities could lead by example by promoting green growth through investments in infrastructure
and transport, ﬁnancial and tax incentives, partnerships, regulation of energy suppliers, increased
consumer awareness, and job training (World Bank 2010). Additionally, they can generate strong
markets for eﬃcient energy products and services (World Bank 2010). Therefore, urban density could
create possibilities for lower carbon emissions and a better quality of life through improved planning
and more eﬃcient infrastructure (C40 2012). Thus, in the years to come, developing energy-eﬃcient
solutions to reduce global urban energy consumption can be an essential component to mitigate
climate change for a better and healthier planet in the future.
1.2
Global heating and cooling energy demand
Final energy consumption for heating and cooling in Europe is responsible for roughly half of the
total global energy consumption (EU28 2017). Heating and cooling include a wide range of end-
use technologies and applications in both the building and industry sectors. Even though the global
energy demand for heating currently outweighs the global demand for cooling, it is gradually catching
up, mainly due to increasing demands for air conditioning and refrigeration (IRENA n.d.). As shown
in Figure 1.2, global heat demand is projected to keep increasing until 2030 and will stabilize, whereas
the cooling-demand is expected to rapidly increase and overtake the heating demand by about 2060
(Morna & Vuuren 2009). This trend is anticipated to accelerate as temperatures rise due to climate
change. Currently, indoor cooling devices already account for about one ﬁfth of the total electricity
used in buildings around the world, which amounts to 10% of the total global energy consumption
(IEA 2018). Thus, while heating is currently the most signiﬁcant energy end-use in buildings, cooling
is the leading driver of new energy demand (Ürge Vorsatz et al. 2015).
Figure 1.2: Modeled global heating and cooling energy demand (Morna & Vuuren 2009)
2
==PAGE:10==
Energy consumption for heating and cooling remains heavily based on fossil fuels. Indeed, 66% of
the European thermal energy supply is produced from fossil fuel (EU28 2017). Renewable energy
supply accounts for only about a quarter of the total global heating and cooling demand (REN21
2019). Almost half of the heat demand is consumed in buildings either for space or water heating,
which contributes to more than 40% of carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions (IEA 2019a, EU 2016).
Moreover, CO2 emissions from space cooling are expanding rapidly, and local air pollutant emissions
related to higher cooling demands are increasing (IEA 2019b). Therefore, huge gains could result
from decarbonizing the heating and cooling sector, and improvements could help fulﬁll the growing
demand sustainably during the next decades.
1.3
District energy systems
District Energy Systems (DES) consist of a diversity of technologies that seek to develop synergies
between the production and supply of heat, cooling, domestic hot water, and electricity (UNEP
2015). They have been highlighted by the United Nations as solutions to decarbonize the heating
and cooling sector and mitigate its environmental impact by improving its energy eﬃciency. Indeed,
DES, deﬁned as a public service, are considered as part of cities’ sustainable development action
plans and contributes to the United Nation’s (UN’s) Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) through
saving energy consumption, reducing greenhouse gas emission and air pollution (Chen et al. 2020).
Consequently, 197 nations adopted the "New Urban Agenda" in 2016, which recognizes modern DES
as a critical component to integrate renewables and energy eﬃciency in urban areas. This initiative
contributes to several SDGs, particularly goals 7 and 11, which aim to ensure access to aﬀordable,
reliable, sustainable, and modern energy and make cities and human settlements inclusive, safe,
resilient and sustainable (UN 2015).

1.3.1
District heating
District Heating (DH) uses a distribution network to transport heated water through a centralized
utility, through piping to buildings, primarily in urban areas. The heated water used is either for
space heating or directly used as domestic hot water. A variety of wasted and renewable heat sources
can be used in such systems to generate heat. DH is a well-known and well-documented technology
because it has been around since the late 19th century. However, the future standard for DH is
referred to as "4th-generation" systems, which operate at lower temperatures, resulting in reduced
heat loss, and can utilize diverse low-grade heat sources. The integration of low-grade heat sources
makes it feasible to connect areas with low energy density and allow consumers also to supply energy
(UNEP 2015). Additionally, this generation of DH is designed to integrate high shares of variable
renewable thermal energy such as industrial surplus, solar, and geothermal heat, by providing high
ﬂexibility to the electrical system (Rutz et al. 2019). Thus, these systems should be an integrated
part of smart energy systems (i.e., integrated smart electricity, gas, ﬂuid, and thermal grids) (Lund
et al. 2014).
1.3.2
District cooling
District Cooling (DC) systems distribute cooling capacity in the form of cold water from a central
source to multiple buildings through networks of underground pipes for use in space and process
cooling (Patel 2017). Coldwater can either be retrieved from free cooling sources such as lakes, rivers,
or seas; or from waste heat through steam turbine-driven, absorption, or electric chillers (UNEP
2015). Thus, DC systems are more energy-eﬃcient than conventional decentralized chillers, such as
air-conditioning units, because they signiﬁcantly reduce electricity use during peak demand periods
through reduced power consumption and thermal storage. This technology reduces the consumption
of harmful refrigerants like hydro-chloroﬂuorocarbons (HCFCs) and hydro-ﬂuorocarbons (HFCs),
which deplete the ozone layer.

DES have the potential to rapidly reduce GHG emissions due to fuel switching and decreasing energy
consumption up to 50 %, which improves air quality by reducing both indoor and outdoor air pol-
lution (UNEP 2015). They also increase energy eﬃciency by utilizing locally distributed renewable
energy sources such as biomass, solar and geothermal energy, thereby reducing GHG emissions on a
local level and improving energy resilience by reducing dependency on fossil fuel imports. However,
DES can also recover steam, hot water, or chilled water that otherwise would be wasted or not
used for heating and cooling distribution to oﬀer competitive and high-energy-eﬃcient alternatives
to local energy markets (Chen et al. 2020). The energy eﬃciency advantages of DES result from the
combination of diverse load proﬁles, which allow central energy plant equipment to operate at high
load factors, resulting in higher eﬃciency levels (Chen et al. 2020). This aggregation also beneﬁts
from economies of scale that allows district energy systems to employ high-eﬃciency technologies,
waste heat and free cooling, and industrial-grade equipment, as condensing economizers that would
otherwise not be economically or technically feasible for individual buildings (DOE 2019). DES
also connects and fosters collaboration between diﬀerent sectors, like electricity and transportation
(Lund et al. 2018). Such technologies facilitate electrical demand management, reducing burnout
risks while promoting a green economy (wasted energy recovery, cost savings, and wealth creation).
All these beneﬁts can be considered as main contributions to SDG 7, 9, 11, and 13 (aﬀordable
and clean energy; industry, innovation, and infrastructure; sustainable cities and communities and
climate change) through the project implementation, related incentive policies, business models and
ﬁnancial mechanisms (Chen et al. 2020). Thus, current energetic strategies on diﬀerent levels (na-
tional, EU, UN, etc.) are trying to push DES forward as sustainable solutions to satisfy urban
heating and cooling needs, increase living urban living standards and limit GHG emissions.
However, even though DES are ideal and feasible solutions to fulﬁll the growing heating and cooling
demand in densely populated areas while accelerating eﬀorts by countries and cities in their transi-
tion towards more climate-friendly societies, they still present certain threats and weaknesses. Most
importantly, DES require high investment costs and therefore lack cost-eﬃciency in the short and
middle term (Schweiger et al. 2019). Indeed, the infrastructure required to connect energy demand
and supply through DES are more expensive than district energy production costs (Chen 2019).
Additionally, thermal energy travels poorly, which is why geography plays a signiﬁcant role in DES
planning (Chen 2019). Indeed, DES necessitates extensive and costly planning and design eﬀorts,
especially during initial stages. DES are dependant on the heating and cooling demand in building
and urban densities. Indeed, they are more eﬃcient with buildings that display higher energy de-
mands and districts with higher numbers of connected users. Moreover, alternative low-temperature
heating and cooling options, like heat pumps, are becoming more attractive (Ziemele et al. 2018).
Additionally, existing heat supply contracts could hinder the implementation of DES due to eco-
nomic conﬂicts of interests between diﬀerent sectors and stakeholders.
In sum, because the infrastructure for DES is expensive, their implementation only makes economic
sense under speciﬁc conditions (high heating and cooling demand, elevated urban density, available
excess heat sources, etc.) and in tandem with prudent urban and energy planning. Thus, district
energy planning must explicitly address the spatial dimension as it allows for better understanding
and integration of local energy resources in DES as well as to minimize installation and operation
costs (Chen 2019). Indeed, rigorous spatial planning is required to increase their cost-eﬀectiveness,
by optimally maximizing their strengths and opportunities, while overcoming the barriers to DES
deployment, by minimizing their threats and weaknesses.

1.4
Strategic energy planning and mapping
Strategic Energy planning (SEP) and GIS-based energy mapping are two of the best practices to
identify synergies and opportunities for cost-eﬀective DES and to apply tailored policies or ﬁnancial
incentives (UNEP 2015).
1.4.1
Strategic energy planning
Urban planners develop SEP processes that aim to implement eﬃcient energy use by minimizing
energy consumption and carbon emissions (Usman et al. 2018). SEP plans and prioritizes diﬀerent
development and investment pathways for local energy demand and supply while aligning local
municipal energy planning with national energy objectives (Mathiesen & Drysdale n.d.). It generally
involves collaboration between cities and regions and is often carried out with input from many
stakeholders drawn from government agencies, academia, local utilities, and other interest groups.
Integrated approaches are essential to consider both for the provision of energy supply and for the
role of energy eﬃciency in reducing demands (Wilson & Biewald 2013). Indeed, SEP must consider
the current reality, leverage local resources, and identify local energy challenges and opportunities,
to map out the most eﬃcient pathways to achieve desired energy future all while bringing clean
energy into focus (DOE 2016).

The main output of SEP is strategic energy plans. These plans document and report the strategies
for short and long-term success and deﬁne progress indicators to monitor both progress and success
during the implementation phase. Therefore, as shown in Figure 1.4, SEP consists of four main
stages: energy data collection, data analysis of energy supply and demand, concept development,

1.4.2
GIS-based energy mapping
Energy Mapping is a data analysis method that uses spatial information to develop pathways aimed
at bridging the gap between energy demand and production. It helps planners identify opportunities
for targeting resources and policies to meet energy goals by providing municipalities with detailed
information on both the current and future distribution of energy use and production, as well
as local heat and energy assets, potential renewable energy sources, and distribution structures
(UNEP 2015). Spatial planning is vital for urban adaptation and mitigation measures such as urban
layout, public transit provisions, or DES integration are essential instruments to reduce urban energy
intensity (UNECE 2011). More speciﬁcally, energy mapping processes can be based on Geographic
Information Systems (GIS), which gathers, manages, and analyzes spatial data to organize layers
of information into visualizations using maps. GIS analysis allows for deeper insights into data,
such as patterns, relationships, and situations, which assist planners in making smarter and eﬃcient
decisions (ESRI n.d.).
1.5
Multi-criteria decision analysis
Environmental and energy planning and decision-making rely mainly on conﬂict analysis charac-
terized by sociopolitical, environmental, technical, and economic value judgments (Lahdelma et al.
2001). These processes often include several considered alternatives, which have to be evaluated by
multiple decision-makers in terms of many diﬀerent criteria. Moreover, diﬀerent decision-makers and
stakeholders typically hold conﬂicting preferences. Generally, perfect solutions do not exist, and the
planning processes seek acceptable compromise solutions (Lahdelma et al. 2001). Multi-Criteria De-
cision Analysis (MCDA) methods have been developed to provide decision-makers with systematic
and understandable frameworks to organize the people, processes, and tools for making structured
and defensible decisions (Kiker et al. 2005). Indeed, MCDA evaluates and enables trade-oﬀs between
diﬀerent criteria to elaborate the most suitable solution.
1.5.1
GIS-based MCDA
GIS is commonly applied to environmental planning and decision-making purposes. They are typi-
cally used to inform decision-makers, but they also have potential as process modeling tools, in which
the spatial eﬀects of predicted decision behavior are simulated (Eastman 2001). Furthermore, GIS
analysis is particularly well-suited to facilitate resource allocation decision-making since land evalu-
ation and allocation is one of the most fundamental activities of resource development (FAO 1976).
Thus, MCDA tools are developed to make explicitly reasoned land evaluation processes (Eastman
2001) used for resource allocation to suit speciﬁc objectives (Eastman 2005).
MCDA is based on diﬀerent criteria that consist of a set of objectives and attributes (Malczewski
1999). The diﬀerent criteria must be measurable and evaluable since they constitute the evidence
on which decisions will be made (Eastman 2001). The criteria are either deﬁned as factors that
increase or decrease the suitability of areas or as constraints to limit alternatives (Eastman et al.
1995). Factors are measured on continuous scales, whereas constraints are expressed through Boolean
maps. Constraints are also deﬁned through characteristics that must be processed by the decision
set, which are referred to as goals or targets (Eastman 2001, Ignizio 1985, Rosenthal 1985). If the
criteria are complementary and non-conﬂicting, once they are all aggregated, the solution highlights
the areas that satisfy all the objectives to the maximum degree possible (Eastman 2009). Thus,
MCDA enables the trade-oﬀbetween a set of feasible alternatives.
7
==PAGE:15==
Figure 1.5: Structure for GIS-based Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (Qashqo 2018)
As shown in Figure 1.5, GIS-based MCDA involves 5 main steps. The ﬁrst consists of deriving a
set of criteria and selecting corresponding data sets. The criteria are applied to the data layer using
decision rules to compute constraint or factor maps. The suitability maps are then standardized.
This process measures all the factors quantitatively and orders the alternatives on one common
interval scale (Voogd 1983).
The suitability maps are ﬁnally weighted and aggregated using a
compensatory decision rule. This step aims to combine all maps into one single suitability map by
summing up all the weighted factors.
1.5.2
Using GIS-based MCDA to facilitate the deployment of urban DES
GIS-based MCDA can eﬀectively assist decision-making during energy planning and mapping to
accelerate DES implementation in urban areas. Indeed, urban DES deployment is well suited for
GIS-based MCDA, as its implementation requires certain constraints (e.g., high heating/cooling
demand, excess heat sources, urban density, etc.) to be met and its potential can be augmented by
individual factors (e.g., renewable energy potentials, cost-eﬀectiveness, etc.). Moreover, it can assist
policy and decision-makers to make more consistent and sustainable decisions by considering all
essential factors, including potentially uncertain and conﬂicting criteria (Wang & Lahdelma 2020).
1.6
Research question, aim, and objectives
As outlined above, urban areas are rapidly expanding around the world and are responsible for
a considerable and increasing amount of GHG emissions, which are partly due to growing global
heating and cooling demand. DES are clean, eﬃcient, and sustainable technologies that must be
implemented to meet growing heating and cooling demands while reducing polluting emissions into
the atmosphere. However, energy mapping and planning are critical and essential to overcome the
barriers of DES development. Moreover, GIS-based MCDA could enable trade-oﬀs for DES and help
decision-makers in unlocking their potential. Consequently, this project aims to develop a framework
that will facilitate and accelerates the deployment of urban DES by allowing policy and decision-
makers to visualize potential areas for DES through energy planning and mapping. More speciﬁcally,
this framework will consist of a GIS-based MCDA for site selection in DES implementation in urban
areas focusing on new deployments. It will be designed as a rapid assessment tool used to highlight
high-potential areas for DES, where further analysis of DE could be interesting. Indeed, it is in-
8
==PAGE:16==
tended for the early stage of DES project development before detailed and technical pre-feasibility
studies. Thus, the framework will provide insights into where future resources could strategically be
spent to stimulate and motivate planning and strategic developments. The area selection process
will be based upon (1) energy demand, (2) local energy sources, and (3) cost-eﬀectiveness. These
three focus points correspond to the main criteria developed for the MCDA, which are derived from
the main strengths and weaknesses associated with DES deployment. Thus, the primary research
questions for this thesis remain:
Which criteria should be considered to accelerate the deployment of urban district energy systems
through GIS-based energy mapping using multi-criteria decision analysis? How can these criteria be
assessed in a spatial context?
This research question will be answered and explored through the development and ﬁne-tuning of a
GIS-based MCA model. Thus, the aim of this work is:
Develop an assessment framework which performs a GIS-based Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis to
highlight potential areas for urban DES deployment, based on energy demand and supply; potential
renewable energy sources and cost-eﬀectiveness.
The initial criteria will be set and modeled for a speciﬁc European city based on literature review,
current best-practices, and expert knowledge. By applying the model to another European city, the
criteria will then be evaluated and corrected. Thus, this second iteration will allow for the veriﬁca-
tion and validation of the deﬁned criteria. Indeed, both steps will produce a holistic set of criteria
to determine potential areas for DES installation. Therefore, the investigation of criteria for urban
DES deployment requires that the following objectives are completed:
1. Establish a set of criteria to identify the most suitable areas for urban DES deployment based
on energy demand and supply, potential renewable energy sources, and cost-eﬀectiveness.
2. Compute a GIS model that performs MCDA based on the established criteria to select potential
districts for DES deployment within cities.
3. Calibrate and reﬁne the criteria set through re-iterating and adapting the GIS model using
data sets from two diﬀerent cities.
4. Develop recommendations for future users as well as policy and decision-makers on how to
most eﬀectively integrate energy mapping to the deployment of urban DES and which criteria
are essential to consider when selecting areas with high potential for DES in diﬀerent urban
contexts.

2. Methods
This chapter details the methodology used to develop the assessment tool following six distinctive
steps, the implementation of which leads to cartographic results and relative conclusions. Figure 2.1
illustrates the methodological framework of this study, later explained in this chapter. Moreover,
since the assessment tool applies GIS-based MCDA to identify optimal areas for urban DES deploy-
ment, the methodology utilizes both quantitative and qualitative approaches reliant on secondary
data analysis.
Figure 2.1: Workﬂow diagram
Step one consists of deﬁning a set of criteria implemented into the GIS-based MCDA model. The
criteria presented in Section 2.1 describe the speciﬁc environmental, social, and technical urban
characteristics required for eﬃcient DES deployment and focus on three essential aspects: local
energy demands, local energy sources, and cost-eﬀectiveness. They are identiﬁed based on existing
literature and expert knowledge. Section 2.2, Amsterdam and Lyon are selected as sample cities
to compute and verify the model. The GIS data required for both sample cities to develop the
MCDA model is collected during step two. Both data sets are exclusively comprised of secondary
data that display equivalent quality and accuracy levels. The MCDA model is computed using both
ArcMap and TerrSet software through step three. Here, the criteria are translated into standardized
suitability maps from Amsterdam’s GIS data, and a weighting process is deﬁned to aggregate them
all.
The aggregated results are then used to select potential districts for DES deployment.
In
step four, the model’s sensitivity is analyzed through diﬀerent re-iterations using diﬀerent weight
combinations. This step also tests for any uncertainties deriving from the MCDA model and its
parameters. The model is then adapted to a diﬀerent urban setting using GIS data from Lyon in
step 5. This last re-iteration veriﬁes the model to reveal how sensitive it is to diﬀerent input data
and how successfully the model can be applied to diﬀerent urban contexts. Finally, conclusions are
drawn from the GIS-based MCA model that examines both the model established and the criteria
selection process. Analyzing the diﬀerent results and adaptations provides signiﬁcant insights about
which criteria are the most relevant to accelerate the deployment of urban DES through GIS-based
energy mapping. Recommendations are drawn to explain how to integrate energy mapping to the
deployment of eﬃcient urban DES most and which criteria are essential to future users as well as
policy and decision-makers to consider when developing DES in diﬀerent urban contexts.

2.1.1
Local energy demand
To be most eﬃcient, DES require elevated heating and cooling demands and higher numbers of
connected users.
Thus, DES deployment must consider and aim to manage current energy de-
mands. This helps justify high investment costs and optimizes economic returns. Therefore, the
feasibility conditions required for DES strongly depend on local thermal energy demands. The next
paragraphs deﬁne optimal conditions for DES regarding both heating and cooling demands. Two
distinct feasibility objectives are deﬁned from these conditions and translated into spatial criteria
for the model.
Cooling demand
DC presents inherent advantages in densely populated areas with high cooling density and heavy
demand for air-conditioning. Over the long-term, pooling cold air demands make DC more cost-
eﬃcient than conventional cooling options at the individual building level (Sarraf et al. 2012). DC
costs decrease with increasing cooling density because of lower relative network costs. Thus, the
district’s cooling density load is one of the most critical factors that inﬂuence the success or failure
of a DC scheme. It must be high enough to warrant DES implementation (Olama 2016).
Figure 2.3: Levelized costs versus cooling density for DC schemes (Sarraf et al. 2012)

Speciﬁcally, DC implementations are considered feasible, advantageous, and cost-eﬀective in areas
exhibiting cooling densities above 65-80 W/m2 (Chen, pers. comm.). This value corresponds to the
cutoﬀ-density above, which costs for DC are less than those of conventional cooling (Olama 2016).
This concept is illustrated in Figure 2.3, which displays how levelized costs decrease as the cooling
density increases due to lower relative network costs. Therefore, higher cooling demands lead to
lower investments and better economic returns for DC systems. Consequently, the model prioritizes
areas with cooling energy densities above 650-800 MWh/ha per year for DES deployment. This
spatial criterion is used to compute feasibility objective F1.
Heating demand
DH systems are more proﬁtable in areas with high heating demands. DH costs diminish as the
heating density increases due to lower relative network costs. The cutoﬀdensity for DH systems
is worth 45-70 W/m2 (Chen, pers. comm.). This corresponds to the heat demand that can be
economically served by DH in areas with low heating densities (IEA 2008, QM-Holzheizwerke 2004).
This density is used as the spatial criterion to describe the feasibility objective F2 related to local
heating demands expressed. Therefore, the MCDA model pinpoints areas with heating demands
above 450-700 MWh/ha per year as the most suitable for DES deployment.
2.1.2
Local energy sources
DES must guarantee maximum energy eﬃciency by exploiting local and renewable energy resources.
Utilizing such energy sources optimizes the cost-eﬃciency of DES and minimizes negative envi-
ronmental impacts. Moreover, integrating renewables to district energy schemes increases energy
eﬃciency on both district and city levels (Chen et al. 2020). It also makes DES a more sustainable
option than conventional heating or cooling schemes and promotes fuel switching from traditional
and impactful primary energy sources, like electricity and heavy oil. Thus, the MCDA model aims to
target areas with high potential regarding local and renewable energy exploitation for DES deploy-
ment. Feasibility conditions for DES, therefore, depend on the potentials for local energy sources
consumption. The following paragraphs deﬁne the feasibility objectives and spatial criteria deﬁned
for the MCDA model to determine optimal geographic conditions required for excess heat, free
cooling, and solar thermal exploitation.
Free cooling sources
Low-temperature water bodies can serve as free cooling sources for district cooling. Chilled waters
from oceans, lakes, rivers, and aquifers can be supplied through insulated underground piping net-
works to absorb heat within buildings (UNEP 2015). Once the water has cooled buildings, it is
returned to the free cooling source where it is chilled again and redistributed in a closed loop. This
reduces the strain on the electric grids caused by increasing demands for air conditioning, as well as
GHG emissions and other harmful impacts from conventional cooling. Thus, urban DES deployment
must target areas relatively close to surface water bodies to take advantage of this free cooling and
minimize heat distribution costs. Subsequently, the MCDA model selects locations closest to free
cooling sources for DES deployment. Furthermore, water bodies are required to display temperatures
below 15°C during summer, 3 m beneath sea level to be considered potential free cooling sources
(Chen, pers. comm.). However, corresponding GIS data sets could not be found for the sample
cities to use this speciﬁc criterion. Thus, only water bodies with areas above 0.5 square kilometers
are considered as suitable free cooling sources in the model. Both spatial criteria serve to compute
feasibility objective F3.

==PAGE:21==
Solar thermal energy
Solar thermal energy (STE) systems can be used as complementary heat sources in DES networks
at both building levels and large scales. Indeed, STE increases the eﬃciency of DES networks since
buildings autonomously create useful heat from the energy of the sun (UNEP 2015). STE can also
be stored for times when excess heat recovery is limited, thereby improving energy security in DES
networks. However, solar thermal collectors must be installed close to end-use heat consumers to
keep distributions costs to a minimum and reduce heat losses within networks. DES deployment
should, therefore, favor areas with high potential for solar thermal energy production.
Many spatial criteria contribute to creating optimal conditions for solar thermal energy production.
Speciﬁc climatic and geomorphological features, such as solar irradiation, sunshine duration, av-
erage temperature, slope, and orientation, increase solar thermal potentials. Additionally, certain
building characteristics increase their potential for solar thermal production: suitable rooftop slopes
to facilitate panel installations, adequate heights concerning neighbors to prevent shading eﬀects,
and large enough rooftop areas for solar panels to be installed. However, the model will only take
these criteria into account indirectly. It will use data that estimates the restricted energy poten-
tial using roof-mounted solar thermal collectors throughout Europe to identify the buildings that
display exploitable solar thermal potentials. This map build is based on a statistical approach com-
puted from average annual solar radiation levels on optimal inclined planes and residential heated
gross ﬂoor area based on calculated heating degree-day values (Mueller 2019). It also considers
potential restrictions related to solar collector areas (0.25 m2 per 1 m2 building footprint) and the
collector’s technological eﬃciency (Mueller 2019). Consequently, the model considers buildings that
exhibit higher solar thermal energy potentials above 250-800 MWh/ha as suitable options for DES
deployment. This spatial criterion serves to assess feasibility objective F4.
Excess heat sources
DES use heat produced in central locations that is distributed through insulated underground piping
networks to end-users for space heating. Hence, heat that has no or very low value in one location,
e.g., excess heat, can be transformed to high value in areas with elevated heating demands (Petersen
et al. 2016). A good example could be circulating heat from industrial to residential areas. However,
excess heat can be sourced from many diﬀerent locations: industrial areas, waste incinerators, metro
stations, data centers, etc.
(UNEP 2015).
Recycling otherwise unexploited excess heat allows
DES to maximize energy eﬃciency on a city level as part of a circular economy (UNEP 2015).
Moreover, higher energy eﬃciency translates to lower operating costs even though signiﬁcant capital
investments are required. Waste heat recovery also prevents the burning of fossil fuels to generate
heat, thereby reducing air pollution and GHG emissions. Thus, DES can participate in reducing
dependencies on imported energy as well as fossil fuel price volatility. To that end, the MCDA model
targets areas closest to potential excess heat sources for DES development. This spatial criterion
helps minimize heat losses in the pipelines during heat transport to end-users. It also reduces initial
investments related to the installation of district networks since greater pipe lengths lead to high
capital costs. Moreover, optimizing heat distribution networks is essential to DES deployment since
connecting waste heat sources to networks require capital-intensive equipment and adaptation of the
waste heat supplier’s infrastructure. This criterion is used to evaluate feasibility objective F5.

2.1.3
Cost-eﬀectiveness
Even though DES are relatively cheap providers of heating and cooling that represent cost-competitive
alternatives in many climates and economies, they typically involve high capital expenditures and
relatively low operating expenditures (UNEP 2015). Thus, DES networks must be developed cost-
eﬀectively to optimize municipal investments. Moreover, several spatial conditions, such as compact
or mixed land-use, can be used to assure enhanced cost-eﬀectiveness for DES deployment. The
following paragraphs explain the diﬀerent feasibility objectives and spatial criteria selected and uti-
lized to increase the cost-eﬀectiveness of potential DES deployment projects throughout the MCDA
model.
Existing DES networks
Connecting new districts into existing networks can be an eﬃcient and cost-eﬀective way to develop
DES usage within cities.
Indeed, in some cases, extending existing systems to new or existing
buildings induces relatively lower connection costs than the investments required to install new DES
networks (King & Parks 2012). This is especially the case in dense cities where DES expansion
signiﬁcantly lower the costs and disruption of network development since reduced amounts of roads
need to be dug up in order to install piping networks. Thus, network extension helps cities that
already use DES cost-eﬀectively expand networks, all while guaranteeing investments for continuing
the conversion to low-carbon energy sources and increasing the end-use sectors that could be served
by DES (C40 & UNEP 2018). However, DES must be extended eﬃciently: large consumers should
be identiﬁed, and networks expanded to meet these consumers and connect other consumers along
the new pipe’s route (Riahi 2015). Therefore, the MCDA model targets buildings close to existing
DES networks to minimize connection pipe lengths, which will, in terms, help reduce both investment
and operational costs (UP-RES 2016). This spatial criterion serves to compute feasibility objective
F6.
Compact land-use
Compact land-use maximizes the utility through high densities and energy demand. Since buildings
are located closer together, and less piping is needed to connect them, compact land-use decreases
both costs and energy losses (King & Parks 2012). Thus, compact land-use reduces both invest-
ment and operational costs. It also leads to reduced infrastructure costs and demand, which helps
prevent urban sprawl (UNEP 2015). Urban DES implementation should target areas with closely
compacted buildings, which translates into higher ﬂoor area ratios (FAR) and lower surface area to
volume ratios. Both spatial criteria are implemented in the GIS-based MCDA model.

As implied by its name, the surface area to volume (S/V) ratio corresponds to the surface of a

Capital expenditures
Retroﬁtting existing building stocks to incorporate DES can signiﬁcantly optimize urban plans for
energy eﬃciency (UNEP 2015).
However, minimizing CAPEX is crucial to improving the cost-
eﬃciency of such DES deployment projects. Retroﬁtting buildings from conventional heating and
cooling systems to DES requires large initial investments (Gartland 2018). Thus, targeting energy-
eﬃcient buildings for urban DES implementation is critical in keeping both CAPEX and OPEX low.
Up-grading old or historic ineﬃcient building stocks can prove to be a challenging and costly pro-
cess (Heat Academy Stockholm 2020). Recently constructed buildings are more likely to be energy
eﬃcient due to the recent building regulations and more energy-conscious requirements. Therefore,
the MCDA model prioritizes areas with higher shares of recently constructed building stocks for
DES deployment. It also assigns lower suitability to areas with old or historic building stocks. More
speciﬁcally, areas with high shares of buildings constructed after 1990 are targeted through the
model. This criterion is used to select energy-eﬃcient buildings for feasibility objective F8.
Furthermore, installing piping networks for DES represents signiﬁcant portions of the CAPEX.
Installation costs of pipes generally amount to at least 60 % of the network costs of district energy
(i.e., excluding heat/cold production), as roads need to be dug up to install the infrastructure,
causing costly disruption and the need to replace parts of the road surface (UNEP 2015). Pipes
also need to be installed underground to connect buildings to the central piping network underneath
roads.
Thus, selecting buildings closest to roads reduces CAPEX since less excavation work is
required to connect buildings to central networks. Additionally, proximity to roads reduces pipe
lengths, which minimizes both distribution costs and heat losses. Hence, the MCDA model targets
buildings closest to roads. This spatial criterion is also used to compute feasibility objective F8.
Mixed land-use
DES are most viable in high-density and mixed-use areas, which can be at any scale of development,
from villages and small towns to large urban neighborhoods or even entire cities (UNEP 2015). These
areas congregate diﬀerent types of energy consumers (public buildings, oﬃces, businesses, residential
dwellings), thereby displaying smoother and less-proﬁled energy demands when connected together
through DES networks. Mixed-use zoning oﬀers signiﬁcant economic beneﬁts for DES deployment.
Smoother energy demands lower the unit costs related to infrastructure per square meter of build-
ing due to reduced needs for less-desirable energy-generation capacity, such as boilers and chillers
(UNEP 2015). Even if fossil fuels are used, mixed-use areas are more desirable and eﬃcient because
energy plants do need to shut down and start up frequently. Moreover, anchor loads, like hospitals,
leisure centers, university campuses, or government buildings, are preferable since they help secure
initial build-up for DES. These anchor loads represent signiﬁcantly large heating or cooling demands
that are predictable and nearly continuous and used to guaranty long-term demand (UNEP 2015).
Generally, anchor loads are controlled or owned by public or para-public entities, which encourages
and facilitates initial investments (Pampuri et al. 2019).
16
==PAGE:24==
The model must, therefore, target mixed-use areas and favor those with public or para-public build-
ings. For modeling purposes, mixed-use areas will be deﬁned as building clusters with more than two
types of land use. However, districts that contain anchor loads are more likely to display mixed-use
since they contain public facilities. Indeed, public facilities tend to be integrated into mixed-use
neighborhoods with residential and commercial areas. Therefore, areas with anchor loads will be
considered more suitable for DES deployment in the model. Anchor load inclusion is selected as a
spatial criterion to evaluate feasibility objective F9. It serves both to target mixed land-use and guar-
antees predictable energy demands. Colleges; community centers; courthouses; department stores;
hospitals; ice rinks; libraries; malls; museums; nursing homes; prisons; public buildings; schools;
stadiums; swimming pools; town halls and universities are considered as potential anchor loads for
the model.

2.2.2
Lyon
Lyon is selected as a sample city because it displays attractive attributes for DES deployment within
the context of this study. Politically, the city is actively engaged in becoming more sustainable.
Indeed, it is the ﬁrst urban development in France with an ambitious target of zero increase in
greenhouse gas emissions. Lyon has set a goal to modernize its heat distribution infrastructures to
become more resource and cost-eﬃcient while fostering the use of alternative energies (SMARTER
TOGETHER n.d.). To this end, local authorities have decided to implement an energy framework
scheme aiming to address energy through GIS, combining district heating and gas and electricity
grids, and renewable energy production (ALEC Lyon n.d.). Thus, the model’s results could be used
to help the city transition towards a sustainable future. However, since the GIS framework is still
under development, GIS data regarding the city’s potential residual or waste heat sources could not
yet be found and input into the model. Geographically, Lyon displays diﬀerent characteristics than
Amsterdam. Indeed, it is landlocked and does not have as many water bodies. As shown in Table

population density over six times greater. Thus, running the MCDA model for both cities illustrates
how it can be applied to multiple urban contexts.
2.3
GIS-based MCDA modeling
The GIS-based MCDA model can be broken down into four diﬀerent subsections: (2.3.1) data
preparation, (2.3.2) criteria standardization, (2.3.3) weighted criteria aggregation, (2.3.4) district
selection, and (2.3.5) ﬁnancial and environmental estimations. The data preparation and district
selection parts are implemented in ArcGIS, whereas the criteria standardization and aggregation
are executed in TerrSet. The detailed processes computed in each part of the model are described
within the following subsections.
2.3.1
Data preparation
The initial step for this GIS-based MCDA model consists of preparatory modules that aim to re-
format and resample the diﬀerent data sets used as input. Indeed, all layers must display the same
extent to compute standardized factor and constraint maps in TerrSet to perform the MCDA. Addi-
tionally, all raster data sets must have the same cell size to facilitate computations. Both preparatory
modules are presented in Appendixes A.1 and A.2, where Figures A.1 and A.2 depict the sub-models
applied to input raster and vector ﬁles. Finally, the original input data set for Amsterdam and Lyon
are listed in Tables A.1 and A.2 in Appendix A.3.
2.3.2
Criteria standardization
During the standardization step, all ten feasibility objectives listed in Table 2.1 are transformed
into suitability maps. Each suitability map illustrates a speciﬁc spatial criterion. These maps are
computed from the output ﬁles generated by both preparatory modules.
Spatial criteria are deﬁned as either constraint or factor maps. Constraint maps are Boolean and
serve to exclude or open certain areas for considerations (Gumbricht et al. 2019). On the other hand,
factor maps are non-Boolean and are used to enhance or diminish the suitability of a location for
a particular application, depending on the magnitude of the variable in question (Gumbricht et al.
2019). However, both types of maps must be standardized to transform disparate measurement units
into comparable suitability values. Using speciﬁc membership functions, the standardization process
18
==PAGE:26==
converts continuous variables into desired ranges (Pang 2019). Thus, standardized maps are used to
order alternative locations within the study area on one common scale (Voogd 1983). A suitability
score, ranging from 0 to 1, is assigned to a location on the standardized map. This score corresponds
to a location’s suitability for a speciﬁc feasibility objective and its corresponding spatial criteria. For
example, the standardized suitability map for factor F1 ranks locations according to their cooling
demands: areas exhibiting suﬃcient energy demands for DES deployment are attributed suitability
scores of 1, and those that do not, are assigned suitability scores of 0. Figure 2.4 shows the model
used to compute standardized constraint and factor maps in this study. Speciﬁc explanations on
how each spatial criterion is transformed into constraint or factor maps can be found in Appendix
A.4. It explains each step of the standardization model in detail.
Furthermore, membership functions are used to standardize factors. They transform the value of
each input pixel into a suitability score based on its possibility of being a member of a speciﬁed set:
0 values are assigned to values that do not belong to the speciﬁed set; 1 values are assigned to those
included in the speciﬁed set and the entire range of possibilities between 0 and 1 are assigned to some
level of possible membership (Eastman 1987). Thus, input raster ﬁles are transformed into fuzzy
sets without sharp boundaries, in which elements display diﬀerent degrees of membership, and the
transition between membership and non-membership is gradual (Zadeh 1965). All the membership
functions used for this study are shown in Appendix A.5.
They are all sigmoidal membership
functions produced from cosine functions. The output standardized factor maps are in byte binary
format with standard scaling values ﬂuctuating between 0 and 1, in a maximal range of 256 units
(Pang 2019).

Factor weights are crucial for determining how individual factors trade-oﬀrelative to each other
because the higher the factor weight, the more inﬂuence that factor has on the ﬁnal suitability
scores (Gumbricht et al. 2019). Thus, weights indicate the relative degree of importance each factor
plays in determining the suitability for an objective (Eastman 1987). For computational reasons, the
sum of weights must add to 1, and each weight should be valued between 0 and 1. However, factor
weights also determine how individual factors trade-oﬀrelative to each other. Indeed, the degree of
trade-oﬀis governed by the relative distribution of order weights between the ranking factors: for
example, if the sum of the ordered weights is evenly allocated amongst factors, the trade-oﬀdegree
will be strong (Eastman 1987). On the other hand, if one factor is assigned a relatively substantial
weight, there will be no trade-oﬀ. However, even though the factor weights inﬂuence the trade-oﬀ
level, it can also be user-deﬁned during MCEs.

Along with factor weights and trade-oﬀdegrees, an MCE is also characterized by the decision risk
level. Decision risks are assimilated to the likelihood that the decision made will be wrong. The most
risk-averse combinations use logical conjunction (AND), whereas the most risk-taking ones are per-
formed with logical disjunction (OR) (Eastman 1987). In low-risk combinations, a high aggregated
suitability score can only be given to locations where all factors display high scores. In this case, the
minimum factor score determines the suitability score, and the scores for every other factor are at
least as high (Eastman 1987). Contrarily, in high-risk combinations, high scores for any factor will
result in a high aggregated suitability score (even if all other factors are low). Thus, the suitability
score is determined by the maximum suitability score, and the ﬁnal score only indicates one of the
factor’s suitability (Eastman 1987). However, as shown in Figure 2.5, trade-oﬀdegrees are related
to decision risk levels.
As shown in Figure 2.6, the aggregation process for this study consists of a two-phased process.
During phase I, the factors and constraints for each goal are aggregated through three individual
multi-criteria evaluations. The outcome of these decision processes constitutes another set of criteria
describing how the screened areas satisfy local energy demands (MCE 1), integrate local energy
sources (MCE 2) sources, and enhance cost-eﬀectiveness (MCE 3). Thus, the output map from
MCE 1 determines which buildings have optimal energy demands for DES implementation. The
results from MCE 2 evaluate each building’s suitability in terms of integrating local energy sources
to DES developments. MCE 3 estimates the buildings in which DES can be developed both the most
and the least cost-eﬀectively. The intermediate suitability maps resulting from all three combinations
are presented in Appendix A.6. They each constitute new factor maps, which are then combined
during the fourth and ﬁnal MCE in phase II. Hence, the ﬁnal decision and suitability computation for
each building for urban DES deployment is based on the results from all four weighted aggregations.

District selection
The fourth step for GIS-based modeling selects potential districts for urban DES deployment based
on the aggregated suitability results. This district selection process is executed by the sub-model
depicted in Figure 2.7.
Figure 2.7: District selection sub-model computed in Arcmap
In this sub-model, zonal statistics are used to attribute an individual suitability score to each build-
ing. This value corresponds to the average suitability found within the area of each building. The
buildings with average suitability scores below 0.75 are then excluded from the analysis since they are
22

not considered optimal candidates for DES deployment. Polygon aggregation is performed to com-
bine the buildings with high suitability scores that are positioned close to each other into potential
districts for DES deployment. More speciﬁcally, this step aggregates buildings that are located at
most 200 meters from each other into districts with areas that are at least 0.5 square kilometers. The
average suitability score within each district is then calculated to rank districts against each other.
For analytical purposes, the number of buildings, area, and average suitability scores attributed to
each main objective (energy demand, energy sources, and cost-eﬃciency) are also estimated for each
district.

initial investment prices for DES in terms of euros per MWh, and the CO2 emission factors for

2.4
Sensitivity analysis
The sensitivity analysis is conducted by re-iterating the entire model using two new factor weight
sets but keeping all other input parameters intact. Each weight set is derived according to diﬀerent
motivations behind DES deployment. Indeed, two decision contexts are imagined for this analysis.
They each correspond to a diﬀerent scenario in which the model could assist decision-makers re-
garding DES deployment for speciﬁc municipal development goals. As shown in Table 2.5, scenario
A aims to expand existing district energy networks. Scenario B focuses on developing renewable
energies through DES deployment. Comparing the results from all iterations shows how the model’s
output relies on the decision-making information that is fed into it. This analysis also shows how the
model can be modiﬁed and adapted to accommodate the needs related to diﬀerent energy strategies.

4. Discussion
This last chapter reﬂects on the observations made from the MCDA assessment framework devel-
oped throughout this study. These reﬂections are divided into three sections. Section 4.1 interprets
the results presented in Chapter 3 while presenting speciﬁc improvements that could be made to the
GIS-based MCDA model. Section 4.2 provides an overview of the MCDA assessment framework de-
veloped throughout this study. Finally, Section 4.3 introduces the main limitations and uncertainties
arising from the GIS-based MCDA model.
4.1
Results interpretation
This section interprets the results shown in Chapter 3 and draws conclusions on the MCDA frame-
work and suggests improvements that could be made to it. It is divided into three parts: (1) the
standardized factor and constraint maps interpretation, (2) the building’s suitability maps interpre-
tation, and (3) the potential district evaluation. All three analyses explore diﬀerent aspects of the
MCDA model.
4.1.1
Standardized factor and constraint maps
Figure 3.1 and 3.2 reveals that the feasibility objectives and spatial criteria listed in Table 2.1 can
individually select suitable locations for DES deployment in urban areas. However, analyzing the
standardized factor and constraint maps for both Amsterdam and Lyon also provides deeper insights
into the feasibility objectives and spatial criteria implemented into the MCDA model. Comparing
the classiﬁed building shares in both cities helps to identify trends concerning the steps taken in the
decision-making process as implemented into the standardization model. These insights evaluate
the feasibility objectives deﬁned for eﬃcient DES deployment and therefore provide observations to
coherently and comprehensively reﬁne the spatial criteria set for eﬃcient urban DES deployment.
These observations are used to generate recommendations to help improve the MCDA framework
and ﬁne-tune it for future applications. Four signiﬁcant trends were observed in the data analysis
and interpreted as recommendations for future users:
1. The spatial criterion deﬁned for F1, regarding suﬃcient cooling demands for DES, is nearly im-
possible to fulﬁll in both model iterations. The results indicate that cities located in temperate
climates do not display suﬃcient cooling demands to justify the development of stand-alone
DC schemes. However, this does not mean that DC is not a viable cooling solution for such
urban contexts. It simply illustrates that DC schemes should only be implemented into DES in
such climatic conditions. Thus, F1 should always be assigned a smaller factor weight relative
to F2 for cities with temperate climates during the criteria aggregation process.
2. The spatial criteria set for feasibility objective C1 is nonrestrictive in both iterations. This
criterion is expressed as a constraint in the MCDA framework and classiﬁes areas with FAR
above 0.3 as a suitable alternative for DES development. However, deﬁning this feasibility as
a factor instead of a constraint would be more beneﬁcial to the decision-making process. A
membership function could be applied to the spatial data and attribute higher suitability scores
to buildings that display higher FAR values while excluding FAR below 0.3. This modiﬁcation
would add depth to the spatial information relating to feasibility objective C1 in the MCDA
framework and help to target the most suitable locations for eﬃcient DES deployment.
35
==PAGE:43==
3. In both iterations, the spatial criterion used to express feasibility objective F8 is the most
restrictive regarding cost-eﬀectiveness. Thus, signiﬁcant weight should be assigned F8 during
criteria aggregation not only because reducing CAPEX is one of the most critical parameters
linked to cost-eﬃciency for DES but also because it has a relatively strict spatial criterion.
4. The factor map computed for feasibility objective F5 in Lyon reveals that industrial sites could
not be used to illustrate potential excess heat sources in urban areas. Indeed, all buildings
are considered a suitable alternative for DES regarding F5 for Lyon, as shown in Figure 3.2e.
Therefore, these results are not realistic, and this inclusive spatial criterion provides invaluable
information to the decision-making process. Thus, another solution must be found to eﬀectively
identify potential excess heat sources in cities when corresponding GIS data could not be found.
A potential solution could be to select speciﬁc buildings, such as factories or data centers, to
represent potential excess heat sources for feasibility objective F5.
The comparison between both iteration’s factor and constraint maps also reveal that each spatial
context is unique, and the signiﬁcance of each feasibility objective cannot be generalized.
For
example, access to free cooling sources is the most inclusive parameter for Amsterdam and one
of the most exclusive Lyon cities because of both cities’ geographic settings. Since Amsterdam is
coastal and Lyon landlocked, buildings in both agglomerations cannot exhibit the same proximity
to water bodies for cooling purposes. Similar observations are made for the feasibility objective
linked to solar thermal potentials, which are higher in Lyon than Amsterdam. Again, this is due to
geographic characteristics since Lyon is located further south than Amsterdam; thus, it has better
climatic conditions for solar energy generation. Thus, each city has unique features, and the MCDA
framework must be optimally adapted to each spatial context to most eﬃciently target areas for DES
deployment. Furthermore, model adaptation could result from both changes in factor weights and
redeﬁning spatial criteria. The factor weight assigned to each feasibility objective could be modiﬁed
to fulﬁll the studied city’s speciﬁc needs in terms of energy and DES. Nevertheless, the spatial criteria
used to express each feasibility objective could also be changed to ﬁt a city’s characteristics. For
example, the cutoﬀdensity for solar energy potential can be diminished, or the maximum distance
from water bodies can be extended. However, stakeholder involvement plays a crucial role in model
adaptations because these modiﬁcations should fully reﬂect the energetic strategy implemented in
each city the MCDA framework is adapted to.
4.1.2
Suitability maps
Figures 3.3 and 3.4 show that the feasibility objective and spatial criteria listed in Table 2.1 can
successfully be used and aggregated to construct building suitability maps to assist eﬃcient DES
deployment in Amsterdam and Lyon. These maps comprehensively assess each building’s potential
for DES within the studied urban contexts, combining criteria linked to energy demands, renewable
energy exploitation, and cost-eﬃciency. In the MCDA model, they are used to select potential dis-
tricts for DES development. However, they could also be used in diﬀerent contexts. For example,
they could be used to validate municipal energy plans. If energy planners have already identiﬁed
areas in which they wish to implement DES, the suitability maps can serve to endorse their choices
and show that they are good alternatives.
The suitability maps also validate that the MCDA framework should be adapted to each urban
context either through the spatial criteria or the factor weights. Indeed, both maps are derived
from the factor weight set presented in Table 2.3, but the results obtained reveal signiﬁcantly higher
DES deployment potentials in Amsterdam than for Lyon. Even-though Amsterdam might be better
36
==PAGE:44==
suited to DES deployment than Lyon, Lyon already has a DES network installed. This shows that
DES is a feasible technical solution for the city. Therefore, the model is the problem because it
should reﬂect that and thus be adapted to ﬁt Lyon’s characteristics.
4.1.3
Potential districts for DES deployment
Overall, Figures 3.5 and 3.6 show that the methodology developed for the district selection process in
the MCDA framework is functional. Indeed, the suitability scores derived from the baseline scenario
allowed the selection of six diﬀerent districts in Amsterdam and two in Lyon. According to energy
demands, renewable energy supplies, and cost-eﬃciency, these districts represent optimal groups of
buildings for DES developments. More districts were selected in Amsterdam than in Lyon because
more buildings were assigned higher suitability scores in Amsterdam, as discussed in the section
above.
Furthermore, the MCDA framework developed in this study is designed as a rapid assessment tool
for the early stage of DES project development before detailed and technical pre-feasibility studies
are done. Thus, decision-support information is compiled for each district that aims to provide
insights into where future resources could be spent strategically to stimulate and motivate planning
and strategic developments. The ultimate objective of this data is to help decision-makers choose
the best alternative between the proposed district alternatives according to their energy strategy.
This data shows the district’s various characteristics in terms of energy demand and energy planning
goals. It indicates the number of buildings, area, average and range of suitability scores attributed to
each main objective (energy demand, energy sources, and cost-eﬃciency) estimated for each district
by the model. It also estimates the initial investments required for DES deployment within districts
as well as the reduction in terms of CO2 emissions the potential DES implementation could initiate.
Since this data is only produced to help energy planners make the best informed decisions in terms
of DES deployment strategies, the data is not aimed to provide precise data on the potential DES
developments.
Indeed, the precise data should be determined during further feasibility studies,
once speciﬁc technologies have been decided upon for DES implementation in a potential district.
Nevertheless, examining the decision-support information provided in Table 3.3 and 3.4 also reveals
speciﬁc trends in the data that could be used to assess the framework developed in this study and
derive recommendations for further iterations. The suitability score ranges and initial investment
costs highlight two points on which the MCDA modeling process could be improved.
Suitability score range
Table 3.3 and 3.4 show that the range for building suitability scores within districts are signiﬁcantly
high in both iterations. Indeed, the maximal range in suitability scores is 0.66 in Amsterdam and
0.42 in Lyon. Moreover, even the minimal suitability score range found for building suitability scores
within districts is quite high, as it equates to 0.25 (district 3 in Amsterdam). This means that there
are big disparities in the suitability scores of buildings constituting each district. It results directly
from the strategy behind the district selection process, which identiﬁes highly suitable buildings for
DES deployments and builds potential districts around them. Therefore, the resulting districts could
be composed of some buildings with optimal conditions but also many buildings with low suitability
for DES. To some extent, this strategy uses an idea similar to developing DES around anchor loads,
which help optimize the system’s eﬃciency. However, a diﬀerent strategy could be implemented in
the model. For example, districts could have been built around clusters of highly suitable buildings
for DES implementation. Local Moran’s I statistics could have been used to identify statistically
37
==PAGE:45==
signiﬁcant hot spots, cold spots, and spatial outliers. This district selection process would result
in smoother suitability scores amongst the buildings contained within each district. Nevertheless,
this strategy requires more computation-intensive calculation techniques. Thus, the strategy behind
the district selection process requires trade-oﬀs between data quality and computation times. In
these iterations, the computation time was deemed more important since the MCDA framework is
meant for rapid assessment. However, the model could be adapted to lead to more suitable districts
selection for further iteration depending on the energy planners’ needs.
Initial investment costs
Although the initial investment costs computed by the MCDA model are crucial parameters that
must be taken into consideration by energy planners, they do not reﬂect the entire ﬁnancial reality
of urban DES deployment. Indeed, the ﬁnancial estimation process reveals the costs generated asso-
ciated with DES deployment within the alternative districts, which only corresponds to the CAPEX
associated with DES. In reality, the costs of heating and cooling from district energy to the end-users
are complex and mainly depend on the CAPEX and OPEX of the technologies implemented and
district energy revenues (UNEP 2015). More speciﬁcally, CAPEX includes the development costs
associated with feasibility studies and planning applications, network and connections costs, and
energy center construction costs. Energy centers typically consist of an energy generation plant and
a control center. Thus, construction costs depend on the type of energy produced by the energy
plant. On the other hand, the principal operating cost is fuel (UNEP 2015). Indeed, even if locally
available renewable energy sources are integrated into DES, they still need to be completed by fossil
fuels to fulﬁll energetic demands. Operating costs also include labor for operation, maintenance,
insurance, etc. Finally, district energy revenues are comprised of heating and cooling sales to end-
consumers, ancillary services, and capacity payments and connection charges (UNEP 2015).
Thus, estimating the payback period for each potential DES deployment would be more beneﬁcial
to the decision-making process. According to their budget, these ﬁnancial estimates would allow
decision-makers to pick the most cost-eﬃcient alternative amongst potential districts.
However,
this involves complex estimations and necessitate many input variable parameters. Indeed, prices
for DES vary signiﬁcantly by location and project.
Moreover, DES costs highly depend on the
technology implemented within the network to produce and transport energy. Thus, the estimated
payback periods estimated from this model may not be relevant or realistic since many parameters
are missing for coherent ﬁnancial analysis.
Furthermore, this framework is designed as a rapid
assessment tool meant only for pre-feasibility studies. Thus, a complete ﬁnancial estimate could
be made during feasibility studies, once a speciﬁc district has been selected from the proposed
alternatives, and more information has been collected from stakeholders. This would result in a
more precise ﬁnancial estimate for DES deployment.
4.1.4
Sensitivity analysis
Figure 3.5 shows that all three scenarios implemented into the MCDA model yield diﬀerent results
for potential districts in which to implement DES schemes in Amsterdam. Indeed, this map shows
that scenario A classiﬁes the most land as suitable districts DES deployment throughout the city.
As a reminder, scenario A aims to facilitate DES network expansion, and its weights set attributes
higher suitability scores to locations near existing networks. Figure 3.5 also presents scenario B as
the most restrictive, as it selects the least amount of land for potential districts. The weight set
for this scenario assigns the highest suitability values to buildings with high potentials in terms of
38
==PAGE:46==
renewable energy exploitation for DES. Finally, Figure 3.5 reveals that all the districts selected by
the baseline scenario are also selected either with Scenario A or B. These results show that the
original weight set derived for the model is relatively robust and comprehensive.
Generally, sensitivity analysis is performed in MCDAs to measure the inﬂuence of errors caused
by the criterion maps and the decision maker’s preferences on the decision outcomes (Malczewski
1999). In this case, the sensitivity analysis only reveals how the factor weights attributed to the
diﬀerent criteria aﬀect the results of the MCDA framework. Indeed, it shows that diﬀerent areas are
selected according to the objectives behind the decision making process. Thus, displaying how the
decision maker’s preferences inﬂuence the model’s results. This result is ideal because the assessment
framework is designed to attribute weights to the criteria presented in Table 2.1 from input data
derived from stakeholder’s energy targets and goals. Indeed, even though the weights presented in
Table 2.3 are based on literature review and expert knowledge in these iterations, the framework
aims to serve as a stakeholder interaction tool to facilitate the incorporation of stakeholder input
with the technical input (Thill 1999). Therefore, if the framework is utilized in a practical case, the
factor weights must be derived from the stakeholder’s preferences in terms of energy development.
Many diﬀerent types of stakeholders, such as municipal or public representatives, can be involved in
the weighting process.
4.2
Overview of the MCDA assessment framework
Throughout this study, the assessment framework is developed to show that a GIS-based MCDA
approach in land suitability analysis is advocated to technically identify optimum areas for urban
DES deployment. Thus, it is an eﬃcient tool that could be implemented during concept development
in strategic energy planning to accelerate urban DES development through GIS-based energy map-
ping. Indeed, this assessment framework presents itself as an eﬃcient technical decision-making tool
that oﬀers a solution to facilitate more informed decision-making by enabling the integration of vast
but disparate amounts of readily available, multi-disciplinary data into decision-making information
(Thill 1999). The MCDA assessment framework utilizes ten essential feasibility objectives to spa-
tially assess urban environments and to select suitable districts for eﬃcient urban DES deployment:
1. suﬃcient cooling demand
2. suﬃcient heating demand
3. access to free cooling sources
4. optimal solar thermal energy potential
5. access to excess heat sources
6. DES networks expansion
7. compact land-used
8. minimized heat losses
9. reduced capital expenditures
10. mixed land-use
39
==PAGE:47==
Each of the feasibility objectives mentioned above increases the eﬃciency of district energy schemes
based on three diﬀerent goals essential for urban DES deployment: satisfying local energy demand,
integrating local energy sources, and enhancing cost-eﬃciency. This set of criteria covers all the
crucial, measurable, and meaningful points of interest linked to DES developments that can be
spatially assessed from readily available GIS data sets Lahdelma et al. (2001). However, this list
of feasibility objectives is not exhaustive. Indeed, even though these objectives were selected from
literature review and expert knowledge to best describe the optimal conditions generally required for
eﬃcient DES deployment, other feasibility objectives could be included in the MCDA framework.
For example, spatial air pollution distribution from substances such as CO2, NO2, or CFCs, could
be incorporated into the model to prioritize the most polluted or polluting areas within cities. In-
deed, these criteria could be used to help mitigate air pollution through urban DES implementation.
Social aspects could also be integrated onto the MCDA framework. For example, spatial data could
be incorporated into the model to prioritize DES deployment within energy-poor neighborhoods. It
can also illustrate the public’s willingness to shift from conventional heating and cooling schemes to
DES in diﬀerent neighborhoods. Thus, the MCDA approach implemented into this assessment tool
allows the analysis of alternative locations in terms of multiple multilateral, non-commensurate, and
potentially conﬂicting criteria (Lahdelma et al. 2001).
All ten feasibility objectives are eﬃciently aggregated into a single suitability score aimed to enable
optimal decision-making through GIS-based MCDA. The GIS-based MCDA approach structures
the evaluation of complex issues related to urban DES deployment along cognitive and normative
dimensions, both of which are essential to strategic energy planning (Vatn 2009). Indeed, the frame-
work divides the decision-making process into smaller, more understandable parts with separate
goals: energy demand management, renewable energy incorporation, and cost-eﬃciency enhance-
ment. This process also increases the transparency and robustness of energy planning decisions
because it can incorporate interests, priorities, goals, and preferences from a wide range of stake-
holders. Indeed, decision-makers and stakeholders are crucial to the framework because they must
participate in the weighting of criteria and to the district selection process. Thus, the framework
can facilitate discussions about the subjective elements in energy planning by multiple stakeholders
(Stirling 2006). Therefore, the MCDA framework can serve to survey, review, and evaluate the pri-
orities and objectives from many diﬀerent stakeholders with conﬂicting preferences (Lahdelma et al.
2001). Indeed, one of the advantages of an MCDA approach is the capacity for calling attention to
similarities or potential areas of conﬂict between stakeholders with diﬀerent views, which results in
a more complete understanding of the values held by others (Kiker et al. 2005). Moreover, this can
open energy planning discourse, since all key assumptions must be made visible and be subject to
review (Saarikoski et al. 2016). However, if the framework is misused and turned into an opaque
process in which experts only deliver suitability results, it can also degrade stakeholder involvement
regarding energy planning processes (Stirling 2006).
The framework can also help reduce motivational or cognitive bias from decision-making processes
related to district energy planning. Thus, this decision support aid is designed to help decision-
makers overcome bias and make holistic assessments since it evaluates spatial data sets. However,
the assessment framework is not totally unbiased because the input factor weights and ﬁnal district
selection could be inﬂuenced by biased expert judgment or stakeholders (Stirling 2006). Further-
more, the framework uses a mixed set of quantitative and qualitative data, which makes it easier
to deal with uncertain and incomplete information, which is characteristic in most energy planning
situations (Saarikoski et al. 2016). It can be adapted to ﬁt multiple urban settings with diﬀerent geo-
graphic characteristics according to the quality of the input data, as shown by both model iterations.
40
==PAGE:48==
The ﬁnal outcomes of the assessment framework are the cartographic result of the studied city
presenting potential districts with optimal conditions for DES deployment, coupled with decision-
making information about each area. These districts are decided upon by the feasibility objectives
and factor weights attributed to the GIS-based MCDA model.
This data aims to facilitate the
best-informed strategic decision-making by energy planners and provide additional information to
compare potential districts against each other. Indeed, energy planners can choose to further in-
vestigate the most suitable location for DES deployment during feasibility studies from this data.
Indeed, the average suitability scores, initial investments, and CO2 emission reductions computed
for each district are to facilitate decision-making processes. Thus, the most preferred solution can
incorporate various complementary or conﬂicting objectives, while minimizing decision risks since
the ﬁnal decision requires a choice made by the users between diﬀerent alternatives.
4.3
Limitations and uncertainties
The main limitations in this study are theoretical and computational in nature, whereas its uncer-
tainties occur in the database or decision rules, as shown in the subsections below.
4.3.1
Modeling limitations
At this point, the GIS-based MCDA framework developed throughout this study is a purely theoret-
ical tool computed to help the urban energy planning sector to be more eﬃcient in how it develops
plans for energy use. The sample city selection process was mostly based on the availability of suit-
able GIS data sets. Although the study’s results show that it is technically feasible to select areas for
urban DES deployment using the multi-criteria decision-making framework, it does not show how
useful these results are in a real energy planning contexts. This also means that all the parts in this
study relating to stakeholder involvement during criteria weighting and district selection are solely
conceptual recommendations. This is an important limitation to take into consideration because
the stakeholders involvement plays an essential role for the MCDA framework to be successful for
its intended purposes. Thus, further recommendations for future users would be to test the model’s
utility and functionality in an actual decision-making context. This could include developing new
methods to optimize stakeholders’ involvement in accordance with the MCDA framework through-
out the decision-making process.
Furthermore, the established MCDA framework uses GIS data to model urban contexts and compute
cartographic results. The input data ﬁles simulate the spatial contexts in the sample cities, thereby
simplifying reality to be able to make predictions on which areas are most adapted to accommodate
district energy developments. There are, therefore, bound to be disparities between real conditions
and the modeled ones. Many of the limitations of GIS software revolve around inferences drawn
from concerns about the quality of GIS data (Aneja et al. 2011). Thus, the results are only as
good as the information fed into the model, because input data governs the framework’s output, no
matter if it is correct or not. Moreover, the model cannot be applied to just any urban environment
because it requires speciﬁc data sets to run and generate adequate results. Finally, even though
the MCDA model developed is aimed to facilitate decision-making processes regarding urban DES
deployments, GIS setup remains complex.
The costs of data inputs can be inevitably high in
terms of technology and GIS knowledge. Indeed, the model developed, is not very user-friendly for
inexperienced users because it requires knowledge of speciﬁc ArcMap and TerrSet software. Thus,
41
==PAGE:49==
GIS analysts are needed to assist the decision-making process, making the actual value of utilizing
this GIS framework challenging to establish.
4.3.2
Database and decision rule uncertainties
Information is vital to the decision-making process developed throughout this MCDA framework.
However, since information is rarely perfect, this leads to uncertainty, of which two sources can be
identiﬁed: database and decision rule uncertainty (Eastman 2001).
Database uncertainties reside in the assessments of the criteria enumerated in the decision rules
deﬁned for the feasibility objectives (Eastman 2001). Indeed, the input data sets, for both cities are
exclusively comprised of secondary data. Even though these ﬁles were carefully sourced, their quality
remains uncertain, and they could incorporate errors into the decision-making process. These data
sets may be subject to measurements or computational errors that could falsify the results computed
by the MCDA model. On the other hand, decision rule uncertainties arise from the manner how
criteria are combined and evaluated to reach decisions (Eastman 2001). For example, decision rule
uncertainties could be brought into the assessment framework’s results from the parameters and
thresholds used in the decision rules. Indeed, even though most of the feasibility is based either on
literature review or expert knowledge, they are still potentially prone to errors. More complex issues
can also relate to the very structure of the decision rules themselves, because of uncertainties that
arise in specifying the relationship between criteria (Eastman 2001, Alonso 1968). To some extent,
these decision rule uncertainties can be assimilated to modeling errors during the decision-making
framework’s establishment.

A.4
Standaesdization process
- The monotonically increasing sigmoidal membership function, depicted in Figure A.3a, is applied
to the cooling demand map to select areas with cooling demands above 650-800 MWh/ha2. This
results in a factor map where location with cooling demands below 650 MWh/ha2 are deﬁned
as not suitable, and those with cooling demands above 800 MWh/ha2 are attributed suitability
of 1. The cooling demands between 650-800 MWh/ha2 are given suitability values, which range
between 0 and 1 according to the energy density: the closer the demand is to 800 MWh/ha2 the
higher the suitability. The resulting suitability map illustrates Factor 1.
- The monotonically increasing sigmoidal membership function, shown in Figure A.3b, is applied to
the heating demand map to detect areas with heating energy demands above 450-700 MWh/ha2
and compute their suitability values. The resulting suitability map illustrates Factor 2.
- To determine the suitability map representing the access to free cooling sources, the distance
of each location to a free cooling source is computed. The monotonically decreasing sigmoidal
membership function, shown in Figure A.3c, is then applied to determine the suitability of each
pixel: the most suitable being those closest to free cooling sources and the least suitable those
that are those furthest than 5 km from cooling sources. Free cooling sources are deﬁned as water
bodies with areas higher than 0.5 km2, and the resulting map illustrates Factor 3.
- The suitability map for Factor 4 is created using a layer representing all the buildings located
within the study area and the raster layer illustrating restricted-energy potential using roof-
mounted solar thermal collectors.
A fuzzy membership function, depicted in Figure A.3d, is
then applied to standardize the energy potentials: the most suitable rooftops are those with en-
ergy potentials above 800 MWh/ha2 and the least suitable are those with energy potentials under
250 MWh/ha2. The resulting ﬁle is then overlaid with the building’s coverage to create a layer
that displays the individual solar thermal energy potential of each rooftop.
- The suitability map illustrating access to excess industrial heat is computed in a similar fashion
using the membership function shown in Figure A.3c, which gives the highest suitability values to
areas located near excess heat sources. The lowest suitability is attributed to a location further
than 5 km to industrial areas. The resulting suitability map illustrates Factor 5.
- The distance to the installed DES piping network is computed to establish the suitability map
for Factor 6. The membership function, shown in Figure A.3c, is applied to the distance ﬁle to
attribute the highest suitability values to areas that are the closest to preexisting DES piping
networks. The lowest suitability is attributed to a location further than 5 km to those piping
networks. Constraint map C0 is also created to display the buildings in which DES is already
installed and exclude them from the suitable set.
- The constraint map used to target compact land use is built upon the reclassiﬁcation of the
computed FAR ratios: all locations with FAR ratios above 0.3 are given suitability values of 1
and all others 0. The resulting suitability map illustrates Constraint 1.
- The suitability for minimized heat losses is quantiﬁed using the monotonically decreasing sigmoidal
membership function, shown in Figure A.3e. This function attributes higher suitability values to
areas with a surface to volume ratios under 2. The resulting suitability map illustrates Factor 7.
- The factor map for F8 is computed from the distances to roads to which the membership function
depicted in Figure A.3f is applied.
The resulting suitability map attributes higher suitability

A.7
Factor weight, trade-oﬀ, and risk level assignation process
In MCE 1, a higher factor weight is attributed to areas displaying suﬃcient heating demand than
those with suﬃcient cooling demand. Since Amsterdam is in Northern Europe and has a colder cli-
mate, satisfying heating needs are deemed more relevant, and DES deployment should target areas
with the highest heating demands. Thus, the resulting suitability map attributes higher signiﬁcance
to fulﬁlling heating demands than satisfying the cooling demand. Additionally, the city’s cooling
demands are not suﬃcient for cost-eﬀective DC implementation. In other words, the cut-oﬀdensity
for cooling demand is never exceeded over the studied area. MCE 1 is based on full trade-oﬀand lo-
cates the analysis exactly midway between AND and OR logical operations. Since cooling demands
are not high enough to fulﬁll the spatial criteria linked to F1, trade-oﬀs must be made between both
energy demands. The decision risk level is set to medium, otherwise, the ﬁnal suitability score would
only include the heating demands.
In MCE 2, factor F5 is given a higher weight than F3 and F4, because it is used to assess the build-
ing’s access to excess heat sources. Since suﬃcient heating demands are considered more suitable
for DES deployment, access to excess heat must be given priority relative to free cooling accesses.
Thus, the weight assigned to F5 is a direct consequence of the weights assigned in MCE 1. Addi-
tionally, higher signiﬁcance is given to the free cooling supply over solar thermal potential since the
solar thermal is only used as a complementary energy supply. Furthermore, more buildings display
optimal conditions for solar thermal exploitation than access to free cooling. No trade-oﬀis made
between all three factors in MCE 2. Indeed, this aggregation process selects areas where all the
potential for exploiting all three energy sources are the highest, thereby keeping the decision risk
level low.
In MCE 3, reducing CAPEX is deﬁned as the most signiﬁcant factor. Indeed, as initial investments
are the most prominent ﬁnancial barrier to DES development, factor F8 is assigned the highest
weight. Priority is given to minimizing heat losses over expanding existing networks and targeting
mixed land-use. The objective is to prioritize compact land-use to optimize investments and energy
usage. Finally, factor F9 is given a higher weight than F8 because expanding the existing DES
network should not be given more importance than developing new cost-eﬀective DES. Finally, the
factors with monetary implications, trade-oﬀin MCE 3 to combine all factors and constraints related
to enhancing cost-eﬃciency. Indeed, these factors can balance each other and reduce installation
costs for urban DES. Thus, the risk level is kept intermediate for this MCE.
In phase II, the factor weights are designed to give feasibility objective 3 the most importance as it
is assigned the highest weight. Finding areas where DES can be installed cost-eﬀectively is the main
criterion to select potential districts. Then, potential energy sources are considered more important
than the energy demand considerations for DES deployment. Thus, the weight assigned to O2 is
higher than the one for 01. Moreover, all three feasibility objectives do not trade-oﬀsince they are
all crucial for optimal DES deployment in urban areas. However, a medium decision risk level is
set for analysis exactly midway between logical conjunction (AND) and logical disjunction (OR).
Indeed, the main objective of this GIS-based MCDA model is to highlight several options for urban
planners and allow decision-makers to choose between them, using local knowledge that cannot be
implemented into the model. Thus, a low decision risk level would yield too restrictive results and
allow too little room for interpretation.
