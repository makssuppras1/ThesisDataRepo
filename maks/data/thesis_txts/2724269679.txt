Prediction of membrane protein types using protein language models
Abstract
Membrane proteins are essential for a wide range of cellular function. These functions
ranges from assisting the cell in mediating communication through signalling pathways
and between cells, to facilitating the transportation of macromolecules and maintaining
ion gradients across membranes. For these reasons, membrane proteins often also pose as
targets in drug discovery, which highlights their biological importance. On a high-level
differentiation, membrane proteins can be categorized into three major classes, namely
peripheral membrane proteins, transmembrane proteins and lipid-anchored proteins. The
categorization of a protein into one of these classes often gives a hint to its functionality
or other properties. From a medical and biotechnological standpoint it is highly useful
to have access to tools that can accurately predict the membrane associativity of a protein.
Utilizing the advances made in recent years within protein language modeling we pro-
pose the first multi-label prediction tool for membrane-type predictions building on these
transformer protein language models.
The tool uses four labels to classify a protein,
that includes the three types of membrane proteins along with a class of soluble proteins
to allow the model to distinguish between membrane and non-membrane associativities.
We develop three classes of models, i.e. models trained solely on eukaryotic respectively
prokaryotic sequences along with hybrid models trained on the data from both superking-
doms. The models build on four recently released protein language models of the Evolu-
tionary Scale Modeling and ProtTrans projects. Following the methodology of DeepLoc
2.0 [1], we employ an attention pooling layer on top of the pre-trained protein embeddings
using the Discrete Cosine Transform for prior-based regularization.
Comparing our models to other available tools developed for the task of predicting types of
proteins yields compelling results and we show state-of-the-art performance for all reported
performance metrics. Internal model comparisons between models trained on eukaryotic
and prokaryotic samples, along with the hybrid kingdom models trained on both datasets,
yields similar results for the performance metrics we report, when evaluated pairwise on
the kingdom separated test sets.
As a result of these findings and results, it is evident that using pre-trained protein em-
beddings from unsupervised transformer protein language models is an advantageous ap-
proach for downstream prediction tasks, as opposed to homology-based prediction tools
that require high-quality annotations of protein structures.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Discovering and determining relations between proteins and their subcellular localization
in cells is essential to describe protein properties and function, and is of relevance for most
fields within bioinformatics and biological research. With the ever-growing amounts of
genomic data and unannotated proteins due to the development and exploitation of high
throughput sequencing technologies since the turn of the millennium, developing tools
to accurately predict and describe properties of proteins is of great interest within the
biotechnological and medical industries.
Through the recent decade Deep Learning has been attributed great potential for the tasks
of predicting various protein features [2]. Experimentally analyzing and annotating the
functionality of new proteins is laborious and expensive in most cases, which is part of
the reason why Machine Learning approaches to these tasks have gained a lot of attention
and support in recent years. With the development of highly sophisticated transformer
protein language models (LMs) trained upon millions of unlabeled protein sequences, the
task of predicting unknown properties and features of proteins, by utilizing pre-trained
protein embeddings from a small set of annotated sequences has become fairly simple and
reported with high accuracy for most tasks[3].
In relation to the task of predicting subcellular localization of proteins, the project and
development of DeepLoc 2.0 [1], a multi-label predictor trained upon protein embeddings
from the protein language models of the Evolutionary Scale Modeling (ESM) project [3],
for ten subcellular localization classes and nine protein sorting signals, showed state-of-the-
art performance compared to other available tools for this task at the time of publication.
Another objective with relevance and association to the task of predicting the subcellular
localization of proteins is the task of predicting whether a certain protein belongs to a
subset of membrane proteins. Membrane proteins are of great interest within many fields
of medical research, as they often serve a wide range of fundamental functions for the
cells, e.g. altering signals for communication, leveraging the sharing or transportation of
material between cells or organelles[4]. Due to these properties membrane proteins are
essential to normal cell function, and often pose targets in drug discovery, as knowledge
of the functionality of membrane proteins aids the field of medical research to recognize
and understand the pathology of certain diseases [5].

1.2
Objective
During the lifetime of the subcellular localization predictor DeepLoc1.0 [6], a binary pre-
dictor for distinguishing membrane-bound from non membrane-bound proteins was avail-
able. After the development of the successor DeepLoc 2.0, that reported state-of-the-art
performance for the task of predicting subcellular localization, it was thought that similar
improvements could be made for membrane-type predictions following the same methodol-
ogy. Therefore, throughout this project, we have sought to develop and compare different
multi-label predictors using various pre-trained protein embeddings from the transformer
protein language models of the ESM [3] and ProtTrans projects [7]. More specifically, we
trained three predictors on the pre-trained protein embeddings from each of the ESM2-
650M, ESM2-15B, ESM1b-650M and ProtT5-XL-U50 protein LMs. The three predictors
for each protein LM were trained on eukaryotic respectively prokaryotic samples retrieved
from the UniProtKB database [8], along with a hybrid model trained on the data of both
superkingdoms, yielding a total of 12 models. All of the models were trained with the
same general model architecture, consisting of the following three main stages for infer-
enc: feature representation - an initial layer with normalization of the raw embeddings,
an attention pooling layer - applying prior-based attention weighting using the discrete
cosine transform (DCT) with a regularization loss function to assist the model in learning
which regions of the proteins to focus on when making predictions, and lastly a classifier
head - constituted by one to three fully connected layers, receiving the pooled attentions
as input and doing the final prediction among four different labels related to the type of
membrane association of the protein.
Membrane protein types across the different cellular kingdoms can generally be reduced to
three classes: peripheral membrane proteins, transmembrane proteins and lipid-anchored
proteins [9], which were the three membrane classes the models were trained on. A fourth
class of soluble proteins with no membrane association was also included, to allow the
models to distinguish between membrane bound and soluble proteins.
The aim to include this membrane-type predictor on the server of DeepLoc 2.0 has been
dependent on the performance of the models, which is why part of the project has been
dedicated to comparing performance for the task of membrane protein predictions with
other current and available state-of-the-art models. To assess the performance we have
compared our models to the following three models: 1) The Mem-ADSVM server [10], a
homology based multi-label predictor using adaptive-decision support vector machines to
classify query proteins. 2) We also compare our models to the MemPype model [11], a
multi-class predictor for eukaryotic proteins, which does inference using a pipeline of multi-
ple other prediction tools for various protein features, and 3) the MemType-2L server [12],
a homology-based multi-class predictor employing evolutionary information through the
Pseudo Position-Specific Scoring Matrix (Pse-PSSM) together with an ensemble classifier.

Chapter 2
Theory
Throughout this chapter, the relevant theory with regard to the biology and machine
learning will be covered. More specifically, the structure of cell membranes for eukaryotes
and prokaryotes will be elaborated along with a characterization of the various classes of
membrane proteins. Subsequently, a general introduction to deep neural networks will be
given with a brief overview of the most important algorithms for these networks in asso-
ciation with this project. Additionally, an introduction to transformer protein language
models will also be provided, followed by a section devoted to the project of DeepLoc 2.0
[1], which led to the offspring i.e. this project. The algorithm behind GraphPart (Magnus
G´ıslason, Felix Teufel, Henrik Nielsen, Ole Winther - manuscript in preparation), a novel
tool used for homology partitioning in this project, will also be elaborated. Finally, a
short introduction to homology-based models will be provided, as a few of such have been
the included in this study for model comparison. The performance metrics that we have
assessed for internal and external comparison will be described in the last section of this
chapter.
2.1
Biological membranes and membrane proteins
Membrane proteins are proteins that are associated or directly attached to the membranes
in all cells including the plasma membrane and internal membranes of the organelles,
that carry out the majority of functions associated with the cell membranes [9]. These
proteins provide an extensive range of biological functions to all cells that are central
for cell homeostasis, cell-cell recognition, cell signaling, and communication mediated by
membrane receptors[4].
They also function to allow selective facilitated diffusion and
active transportation of ions and various molecules across the membranes when needed.
On the molecular level biological membranes primarily consist of phospholipids, making
up a lipid bi-layer accounting for appx. half of the mass in the membranes [13], with
membrane proteins constituting the majority of the remaining mass. The remnant part of
the cell membranes consists primarily of glycolipids and cholesterol. Cholesterol functions
to control the fluidity of mainly the cytoplasmic membrane, while glycolipids also facilitate
cell-cell recognition and protein interactions [14]. The lipid-bilayer is made up of two layers
of phospholipids with hydrophilic heads facing the exterior of the membrane either towards
the intracellular cavities or the extracellular space, with hydrophobic tails facing inwards
towards each other. The lipid bi-layer in the cell membranes acts as a barrier between the
cell compartments, where macromolecules and ions are prevented from diffusing across

and between different compartments in the cell or extracellular space. While there exist
structural differences between the membranes of eukaryotes and prokaryotes, this is the
general structure and composition of biological membranes. In the following sections some
of these differences will be explained in a relevant context.
2.2
Classification of membrane proteins
At the highest level, membrane proteins can generally be grouped into two classes of
proteins, i.e peripheral membrane proteins and integral membrane proteins. The integral
membrane proteins can be divided into two major classes of transmembrane proteins and
lipid-anchored proteins, which then both can be further divided into different sub-classes
of the relative protein. The main difference between these classes of proteins is the manner
in which they associate and interact with the cell membranes. Integral membrane proteins
are as the name suggests proteins that are integrated into the lipid bi-layer with strong
binding to the hydrophobic core [9]. On the contrary, the peripheral membrane proteins
are usually only bound temporarily by weaker electrostatic and non-covalent interactions
with the lipid bi-layer or often in the proximity of other membrane proteins that provides
access to such bindings [15]. As non-covalent bindings are prone to changes in pH or the
ionic strength of the solution, these bindings make it possible for peripheral membrane
proteins to dissociate from the membrane [16]. Lipid-anchored membrane proteins are in
contrast proteins covalently bound to the lipid-groups of the cell surface.
2.2.1
Transmembrane proteins
As mentioned, integral membrane proteins can be divided into different classes, one of
which is the transmembrane proteins. Transmembrane proteins caretake many essential
cell processes including but not limited to e.g. acting as receptors for substrates in various
signal transduction pathways. Furthermore, glycosylated transmembrane proteins, known
as glycoproteins, assists in extrinsic cell-cell recognition crucial to the immune response
[14, 17]. This includes the binding of pathogen-associated molecular patterns to pattern
recognition receptors of toll-like receptors, which are single-pass transmembrane proteins
found in phagocytes important for the innate immune response [18]. Other important
functions of transmembrane proteins include ion transportation of primary (ATP-driven)
[19] and secondary (potential energy driven) transporter proteins crucial to the excitation
of nerve cells and transmission of electrical signals etc. [20].
Classification by tertiary structure
The domain of transmembrane proteins is characterized by proteins expressing transmem-
brane domains (TMDs) spanning the entire cell membrane one or multiple times. This
property gives rise to single-pass and multi-pass membrane proteins, that by spanning
across the lipid bi-layer expose stretches of the protein to both sides of the membrane,
consisting predominantly of hydrophilic amino acids [9]. Aside from the number of times
a transmembrane protein spans a membrane, it is also characterized into two classes de-
termining how the protein is bound to the hydrophobic core of the cell membranes, i.e.
α-helical and β-barrel proteins. As the name indicates, β-barrels arise from the secondary
protein structure of β-sheets, with multiple repeats of this secondary structure forming
toroidal structures that are embedded in the membrane. The side chains of the amino
acids of these stretches in the protein are primarily hydrophobic in α-helices, while for β-
barrels each consecutive amino acids of these stretches have alternating hydrophobic and

==PAGE:10==
hydrophilic characteristics depending on whether they are facing the interior of the lipid
bi-layer or inwards towards the pore of the β-barrel [21]. Far most of the transmembrane
proteins that have been characterized belong to the α-helical class. In contrast, β-barrel
membrane proteins are less common and primarily known to exist in the outer membranes
of bacteria, mitochondria and chloroplasts [22].
Classification by topology
Transmembrane proteins are also classified according to the topology on the membrane
of the N and C-terminal of the protein.
As an example, classification by topology of
transmembrane proteins during co-translational translocation at the membrane of the en-
doplasmic reticulum (ER) can be classified as explained hereinafter.
Type I transmembrane proteins are bound to the ER membrane by a stop-transfer se-
quence with their N-terminal region facing the cavity of the ER. The stop-transfer anchor
sequence is one of three topogenic sequences that assists to orient and target the protein
for correct translocation [23, 24]. The other two are the N-terminal signal peptide (signal
sequence) and the signal-anchor sequence. Type I transmembrane proteins also contain a
signal peptide that is cleaved off as soon as the N-terminal of the protein enters the lumen
of the ER. As opposed to regular signal peptides, the N-terminal stop-transfer anchor
sequence stays part of the mature protein. Contrary to Type I transmembrane proteins,
the Type II and III proteins are classified by having signal-anchoring sequences, i.e. the
third type of topogenic sequences. These two types are furthermore differentiated by the
orientation of the N- and C-terminal, with Type II proteins having their C-terminal tar-
geting the lumen of the ER and oppositely Type III having the N-terminus of the protein
facing the inside of the ER. Signal-anchoring sequences are similar to signal peptides,
except they lack the stretch of amino acids that constitutes the cleavage site recognized
by signal peptidases further downstream in the protein [23]. Type IV transmembrane
proteins are also recognised as single-spanning transmembrane proteins and are separated
into two sub-types, namely the Type IV-a and Type IV-b. The subtype of the polytopic
protein is again determined by the orientation of the N- and C-terminal, with Type IV-a
targeting the cytoplasm with the N-terminal region as opposed to Type IV-b that has its
C-terminal facing away from the lumen of the ER. For a deeper introduction to protein
translocation, see section 2.3.
The topology of multi-spanning transmembrane proteins commonly follow the ”positive-
inside” rule, which refers to the membrane-topology of the often found stretches of pos-
itively charged amino acids, like arginine and lysine, in the sequence of multi-spanning
transmembrane proteins. Such protein features and the topological organization of these
stretches have been observed in e.g. gram-negative bacteria.
When these proteins are
inserted into the membrane the positively charged stretches most often end up facing in-
wards towards the cytosol as opposed to the periplasmic region in the inner membrane of
gram-negative bacteria [25].
2.2.2
Lipid-anchored membrane proteins
Like transmembrane proteins, lipid-anchored proteins are also integral proteins that are
embedded into the membrane. Their attachment, structure, and topology do however
differ significantly from other integral proteins. The lipid constituents of the anchor do
9
==PAGE:11==
not span the entire membrane, but are embedded only around the surface of the membrane.
This means that the protein attached to the anchor is also solely reachable from one side
of the membrane, as they do not cross the membrane, making them more simple in terms
of their topology on the membrane.
Four types of association between lipid-anchored proteins and the membranes have been
described, namely through amide-linked myristoyl anchors, thioester-linked fatty acyl an-
chors, thioether-linked prenyl anchors and lastly GPI-linked anchors [26]. These four types
of lipid-anchors will be briefly described in the following.
Amide-linked myristoyl anchors
Myristoylation is typically a co-translational lipid modification, where the fatty acid,
myristic acid, is irreversibly bound to the N-terminal of the first glycine residue of a
protein (usually Gly1/2) facilitated by the enzyme N-myristoyl-transferase during pro-
tein synthesis forming an amide linkage [27]. These lipid-anchored proteins are primarily
involved in signal transduction or protein-protein interactions at the membrane.
Thioester-linked fatty acyl anchors
Lipid-anchored proteins with a thioester fatty acyl anchor undergo palmitoylation, which
in contrast to myristoylation is a reversible addition of palmitic acid to the protein through
a thioester linkage catalyzed by palmitoyltransferases. This post-translational modifica-
tion, at the side chain of Cystein residues, is generally less specific than N-myristoylation
[28], but consensus sequences in prokaryotes have been found, namely the N-terminal
lipobox recognized by signal peptidase II [29]. It has been shown that these types of lipid-
anchor proteins play a role in e.g. protein trafficking (i.e. endo-/exocytosis) and regulating
protein subcellular localization [30]. The ability to reverse and break the linkage between
the palmitoyl group and the protein is unique among lipid-anchors and has been proposed
to allow rapid translocation or trafficking of these proteins between membranes [31].
Thioether-linked prenyl anchors
Prenylation is the post-translational modification of attaching groups of isoprenes, namely
farnesyl or geranylgeranyl, covalently to the side chain of a cysteine residue near the C-
terminal of the protein, creating a thioether linkage [32].
Attachment of farnesyl and
geranylgeranyl is dependent on the surrounding residues in the protein, and requires cys-
teine (in the proximity of the C-terminal) with an aliphatic amino acid next to it, followed
by certain amino acids that allow either prenylation of farnesyl (alanine, methionine, serine
or glutamine) or geranylgeranyl (leucine) [33]. Prenylated proteins at the cell membrane
have been shown to be essential for processes related to cell growth and differentiation.
An example is the Ras family of proteins, which are small G-proteins, involved in cell
signaling related to cellular growth. Mutated forms of Ras have been found expressed in
many types of cancer and act as a predictive biomarker [34].

cal structure than the other lipid-anchors discussed so far. GPI-anchor modification takes
place post-translationally and consists of a protein that is attached to a glycolipid through

2.2.3
Peripheral membrane proteins
The domain of peripheral membrane proteins differ notably from the integral membrane
proteins that have been discussed so far. Peripheral membrane proteins do not bind per-
manently to the membrane through interactions with the hydrophobic core of the lipid
bi-layer, and their ability to attach to and dissociate from the membrane gives rise to differ-
ent functionalities compared to other membrane proteins [4]. Commonly, the membrane-
association of peripheral membrane proteins happens through binding to other integral
membrane proteins [15], but peripheral membrane proteins of amphitrophic character can
also interact and bind to the surface of the membrane alone through hydrophobic inter-
actions. An example of such proteins are amphitropic enzymes that need to interact with
the lipid bi-layer, e.g. phospholipases that hydrolyses phospholipids in the membrane [37].
Variations in the local lipid composition of the bi-layer also play a role in the attachment
of such enzymes [38]. Some peripheral membrane proteins require more specific protein-
lipid binding. Such interactions arise between cytosolic proteins that express structural
surface features that allow the binding of lipid heads to pockets on the protein. These
interactions are usually stabilized by weaker electrostatic interactions, like van der Waals
forces and hydrogen bonds [39, 37].
As seen, the ability to attach and detach allows these proteins to carry out tasks at the
surface of the membrane when needed, which is also why peripheral proteins are often
part of larger cellular pathways, that can be recruited from the cytosol upon signaling.
2.2.4
Soluble proteins
Although soluble proteins in their nature are usually not associated with the membrane,
they still need a short introduction in the context of this project. The main reason for
including these proteins in the study has been to allow the models to distinguish between
membrane and non membrane-bound proteins.
Soluble proteins have a wide range of
structural and functional properties, which will not be explained in detail here but can
generally be described as proteins that can move freely in the cytosol or lumen of the
various eukaryotic organelles, as opposed to the previously described groups of membrane
proteins [4]. They usually fold into a structure where hydrophilic residues of the protein are
exposed to the surrounding environment, while hydrophobic residues usually face inwards
towards the core of the protein and will be held away from the hydrophilic environment
[40].
As seen from section 2.2.3 the ability of peripheral membrane proteins to attach and detach
from the membrane can also lead to this type of membrane protein entering a state of
solubility if it can stay in the cytosol for extended periods. This behavior is in fact observed

membrane over time depending on e.g. external stimuli and activation of signal pathways,
which can lead to recruitment or dissociation of membrane proteins. An example of such

membrane, giving rise to proteins with multiple membrane annotations [41]. Although
the vast majority of proteins placed in the soluble class from the curated dataset are

2.3
Protein translocation
A brief introduction to protein translocation and signal peptides (SPs) should also be given
in this chapter in order to understand how cells translocate various membrane associated
proteins and secreted proteins to their predetermined sites. In eukaryotes, proteins are
primarily translated and synthesized at the cytosol or membrane-bound ribosomes at the
rough ER. Proteins synthesized at free ribosomes can undergo post-translational trans-
portation from the cytosol to the mitochondria, chloroplasts, nucleus or peroxisomes [42].
Conversely, proteins synthesized in the proximity of the ER can undergo co-translational
transport into the ER and further through vesicle transport into the golgi apparatus and
further on for secretion or to other locations in the cell [43]. If a protein possesses a signal
peptide it is usually passed to the lumen or membrane of the ER through co-translational
transport (see section 2.3.1). If the protein does not contain a signal peptide, but instead
possess other target sequences for translocation, it will usually undergo post-translational
translocation to e.g. the mitochondria or nucleus.
It should be mentioned that some
proteins undergoing post-translational translocation also enter the ER through another
pathway than what is observed during co-translational translocation, namely through in-
teractions with the Sec61 translocon [44], so the two types of translocation are not mutually
exclusive with regards to which route of organelles they are targeted for. The following
should therefore not be read as a comprehensible overview of all the pathways and mecha-
nisms related to co- and post-translational translocation, but merely give a brief overview
of some of the main concepts and mechanisms related to protein translocation.
2.3.1
Signal peptide dependent translocation
When the translation of a protein possessing a SP is initiated in the cytosol near the ER
membrane (or cytosol of prokaryotes), a signal recognition particle (SRP) will bind to the
complex between the nascent protein strand and the ribosome thereby impeding transla-
tion [45]. Signal peptides will be described more thoroughly further down, but in brief,
they are short peptide sequences located at the N-terminal of the targeted protein, usually
having a length between 15-25 amino acids with a hydrophobic region that can interact
and bind to transmembrane proteins responsible for translocation, that are translocons.
SPs are the starting point of co-translational translocation targeted for the secretory (Sec)
pathways. After the translation in the ribosomal complex has been stalled by the SRP, the
entire complex is transferred to the rough ER (or the plasma membrane in prokaryotes)
where the SRP is bound to an SRP receptor[46]. Here guanosine triphosphate (GTP)
binds to the SRP and its receptor triggering the release of the nascent protein strand
with the signal peptide. The hydrophobic region of the signal peptide in turn binds to a
translocon, like the Sec61 (present in eukaryotes) or SecYEG (in prokaryotes), embedded
12
==PAGE:14==
in the ER membrane or plasma membrane of prokaryotes, through hydrophobic interac-
tions. Co-translational translocation continues as the protein is translated and pushed
through the translocon [47]. When the full protein has been translated a signal peptidase
cleaves the signal peptide and the protein is then released to the lumen of the ER. If the
protein is destined for other organelles, e.g. the plasma membrane or golgi apparatus it
passes through the ER where it can undergo various modifications before being packaged
into vesicles and transported through the golgi apparatus where it can be further modified
and packaged in new vesicles sorted for different destinations.
As seen, translocons like the Sec61 or SecYEG are important for the translocation of
proteins across membranes, but these translocons are also handling a more specialized task
concerning the integration and translocation of transmembrane proteins. As mentioned
previously, transmembrane proteins have one or multiple TMDs and can be characterized
by their topology on the ER membrane. When a protein is co-translated while bound to
the translocon, it is the translocon that ensures the insertion and right orientation of the
transmembrane protein during synthesis [48]. High resolution crystal structures of Sec61
homologs in prokaryotes have given rise to a proposed mechanism involving a lateral gate
release of the transmembrane domain into the membrane. Although certain parts of the
mechanism have not been disclosed, it is thought that the organization of the TMDs in
the translocon itself forms a lateral gate that can be opened or closed depending on the
biochemical properties of the stretch of the synthesized protein that is interacting with its
hydrophobic cavity and the lipid environment in the membrane around the translocon [49,
50]. When a TMD from the newly translated protein is encountered, the lateral gate opens,
which allows insertion of the full stretch into the lipid bi-layer and in turn exposes the
hydrophilic regions of the protein on the surface of the membrane as translation proceeds,
threading the nascent protein according to the predetermined topology.
Signal peptides
Signal peptides are short peptide sequences, most often present at the N-terminal region
of the mature proteins, destined for the Sec pathway. As briefly explained, the function of
these peptides is to direct the protein towards its target localization [51]. These peptides,
which are usually 15-25 amino acids long, contain a hydrophobic region (h-region) that is
the core of the signal peptide (a stretch of 5-15 AAs), which usually folds into an α-helix. It
is this region that binds to the hydrophobic regions of translocons like the Sec61 located in
the ER membrane, or the SecYEG, a translocon of similar function and structure present
in the cytoplasmic membrane of prokaryotes [52]. Most signal peptides usually contain
two other regions, namely the n- and c-regions surrounding the h-region. The n-region
usually contains positively charged amino acids, while the c-region contains the cleavage
site, which is recognized by signal peptidases, guiding them to where the SP should be
cleaved away from the mature protein [53].
2.3.2
Signal peptide independent translocation
Proteins undergoing post-translational translocation from the cytosol are fully translated
before translocation is initiated, in contrast to the mechanisms during co-translational
translocation. Proteins undergoing SP independent translocation do not show the pres-
ence of signal peptides but instead possess other stretches of sorting signals addressing
them for organelles like the mitochondria or nucleus. Contrary to SPs, these target se-
quences can be located anywhere in the mature protein. e.g. transport of proteins into
the mitochondria can be dependent on a mitochondrial transit peptide. Mitochondrial
13
==PAGE:15==
protein translocation is interesting as the structure of mitochondrial membranes is signif-
icantly different from other membranes present in eukaryotes. Mitochondrial membranes
consist of an outer and an inner membrane separating the mitochondrial matrix from the
cytosol. Transportation across these membranes requires translocons, which are complex
systems consisting primarily of transmembrane proteins, to cooperatively move proteins
across the two membranes [54]. An example of this, important to all eukaryotic life, is the
transportation of enzymes responsible for the electron transport chain that pumps protons
into the intermembrane space and drives the electrochemical gradient that is crucial to
ATP production. The enzymes responsible for the electron transport chain localize at the
inner membrane of mitochondria while translation happens in the cytosol. Immediately
after synthesis the protein is bound by chaperones to prevent it from folding. The mito-
chondrial import sequence, usually an amphiphilic α-helix binds to a translocase of the
outer membrane (TOM) and is then pulled through to the intermembrane space, where
it proceeds to bind to a translocase of the inner membrane, TIM22, which assist the inte-
gration of the enzyme into the inner membrane. Other enzymes needed in the matrix for
the electron transport chain are translocated through the two membranes simultaneously
carried out by the translocons TOM and TIM23[55], assisted by a ratchet system [56],
where chaperons located in the mitochondrial matrix take turns binding to the stretch
of the protein inside the matrix, causing a conformational change of the TIM23 complex
through stepwise hydrolysis of ATP at the translocon leading to the protein being pulled
from the cytosol into the matrix [57].
This procedure is an example of one of many post-translational translocations pathways
that helps us understand how membrane proteins end up where they are supposed to and
illustrates some of the intricate mechanisms evolution has given rise to.
Prokaryotes have similar pathways and mechanisms involved in the translocation of pro-
teins across membranes. For post-translational translocation two translocons are in play
for prokaryotes, the Sec and twin-arginine translocation (Tat) systems.
The TatABC
complex is capable of transporting mature folded proteins across the plasma membrane,
while post-translational translocation through the Sec pathway requires SecB and SecA,
a chaperone and an ATPase, to assist the transport of the nascent protein to the SecYEG
[58] that enables transportation of the protein across the plasma membrane.
2.4
Cell envelope of eukaryotes and prokaryotes
This section is intended to highlight the main differences between eukaryotes and prokary-
otes with regard to the structure and organization of the cellular envelope in the two
cellular types and introduces relevant terminology regarding this. Although the plasma
membranes of eukaryotes and prokaryotes contain the majority of the proteins associated
with the cell envelope, it is still important to consider the functionality and be aware of the
presence of cell walls and bacterial capsules when discussing the function of various mem-
brane proteins. This section aims to give a basic overview of the structure and biological
impacts of more complex cell envelopes.
In opposition to prokaryotic cells, that has no nucleus and only a cell wall with a plasma
membrane and no membrane-bound organelles, eukaryotes have multiple membranes as-
sociated with, and compartmentalizing, the various organelles in the cellular lumen. Both
eukaryotes and prokaryotes are surrounded by a plasma membrane enclosing the cell from
the surrounding environment, but in addition to the plasma membrane bacteria have a
14
==PAGE:16==
more complex cell wall structure [59], and some archaea have a different lipid structure of
the plasma membrane compared to the other kingdoms. Although the plasma membrane
of the different cell types resembles each other in structure and molecular composition,
some key differences should be introduced to clarify and avoid confusion throughout the
following chapters.
Various kingdoms within the eukaryotes and prokaryotes possess cell walls that surrounds
the plasma membrane. Within the eukaryotes, fungi, plants, algae, and some protists
have been shown to have a cell wall. The cell wall of these kingdoms varies in structure
and composition, where e.g. fungi have cell walls made up of chitin, glucans and glycopro-
teins [60] while plant cell walls, in general, contain cellulose, pectin, and hemicellulose [9].
Most bacteria and archaea have been shown to possess cell walls as well, with bacterial
cell walls composed of peptidoglycans while archaeal cell walls primarily contain pseu-
dopeptidoglycans [61]. Although similar in name and functionality, the difference between
the chemical bonds in the backbone of peptidoglycans with N-acetylglucosamine and N-
acetylmuramic acid (bound by β-1,4 linkage) and the β-1,3 linkage in pseudopeptidoglycan
between N-acetylglucosamine and N-acetyltalosaminuronic acid makes a significant differ-
ence biochemically, as pseudopeptidoglycans are not prone to cleavage by e.g. lysozymes
due to this β-1,3 linkage [62]. The functionality of cell walls across the kingdoms appears
to be similar, providing structural support and protection against pathogens.
In addition to the cell wall, gram-negative bacteria have an auxiliary cell capsule surround-
ing the cell wall. This outermost layer of gram negative bacteria can be characterized as
a sticky capsule, consisting of lipopolysaccharides (LPS), with the functional property
to enable attachment to other surfaces and prevent desiccation [63]. This layer will be
referred to as the outer membrane and is not present in gram positive bacteria.
As mentioned, the plasma membrane between most prokaryotes and eukaryotes resemble
each other in structure and functionality made of a lipid bi-layer primarily consisting of
phospholipids. However, a considerable amount of archaea have been found to possess
a lipid mono-layer which has some significant biological impacts on the structure and
functionality of cells possessing this type of plasma membrane, providing protection in
more hostile and extreme environments. The difference in the organization of the lipids
between these membranes lies in the linkage between the head of the phospholipids and
their hydrocarbon chains, where the isoprenoids in lipid mono-layers are bound by an
ether linkage, in contrast to the structure of phospholipids in lipid bi-layers where the
hydrocarbon chains are bound by an ester linkage [64]. The ether bound structure allows
a more tightly packed structure of the membrane, which enables the organism to survive
more extreme conditions with regard to e.g. temperature, pH, and pressure.
2.5
Deep learning
This section will be dedicated to giving an introduction to the field of Deep learning and
present some of the relevant theory and algorithms important for this project. Deep learn-
ing (DL) makes up a sub-branch of artificial intelligence and machine learning algorithms,
that is based on artificial neural networks (ANNs). Contrary to other Machine Learning
(ML) algorithms, deep neural networks are defined by having two or more hidden lay-
ers within the architecture of the network between the input and output neurons. Deep
learning algorithms have gained massive attention during the last decade within almost
all fields of research that involve data and computer science, due to their ability to learn
15
==PAGE:17==
non-linear relationships efficiently and scale to the ever growing amounts of data that are
being produced and can be used by these models.
2.5.1
Supervised and unsupervised learning
The training setup of DL models can be classified into three different types of learning
settings, where training can either be supervised, semi-supervised or unsupervised. Super-
vised training is defined as using labeled data to train the model and guide the learning.
The algorithms in supervised learning use the knowledge of the true labels when comput-
ing the loss and making decisions about which updates to the model it should make during
optimization. Supervised learning can in general be classified into two different classes of
tasks, name classification and regression tasks[65]. These tasks will be explained further
down. In opposition to supervised learning, unsupervised algorithms are not provided
with labels of the data, but instead learn hidden patterns in the data. Common tasks
of unsupervised learning include clustering and dimensionality reduction. Unsupervised
learning does not lead to actual predictions of input, but instead it can e.g. group data
together based on the patterns found, without the intervention of humans and is thus
very applicable in many real-world settings where large amounts of unlabeled data exist
and can be used for various purposes. Unsupervised learning is commonly used in natural
language processing, where e.g. transformer models have gained a lot of attention in the
recent years.
Unsupervised learning is also used in multivariate analysis that through
dimensionality reduction can capture the features that drive the variance observed in a
dataset [66]. Semi-supervised learning combines the two of these learning settings, by
deploying the features of unlabeled data learned by a unsupervised model and combining
this with a small set of labeled data to achieve high performance and generalization that
else would not be possible in a solely supervised learning setting using the same labeled
data. This property is among the reasons why transformer protein language models have
become so popular.
2.5.2
Classification and regression tasks
As mentioned, supervised learning can be divided into classification or regression tasks.
Classification tasks involve the prediction of an input to a number of defined classes. A
common example of this is the classification of images into classes of certain objects, but
expands to all data for which the final prediction can be mapped discretely. The task of pre-
dicting membrane types of proteins is a classification task. Moreover, classification tasks
are usually divided into three types of classification tasks, namely binary-classification,
which as the name suggest is the classification of samples into two classes, multi-class clas-
sification, which is a single-label prediction within a set of classes (meaning only one class
can be predicted positive for a sample) and lastly multi-label classification which is used for
tasks where prediction of multiple positive classes are allowed for a single sample. These
different tasks have implications for the model architecture and more specifically impact
the size and activation function of the output layer. In contrast to classification models,
regression models produce one or multiple continuous output features. Often these output
features can be interpreted as probabilities or estimates of an outcome given various input
features. The learning setting in this project, i.e. the task of predicting membrane types
for proteins, can be categorized as a supervised classification setting, based on data from
unsupervised structural learners that are the pre-trained protein language models.

2.6
Structure of deep neural networks
As previously mentioned, the structure of deep neural networks is distinguishable from
other machine learning models by the property that they contain two or more hidden
layers between the input and output layer. These layers, or types of networks, can have
different properties that make them more suitable for some tasks than others. There exist
various types of neural networks in DL including Feed-Forward Neural Networks (FFNs),
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) [65]. For
these networks different layers for different purposes can be applied, e.g. normalization
layers, pooling layers and attention layers etc.[66]. These different networks and layers are
most often combined to form a model, as they have different areas of application.

connections to other neurons represented as edges of the graph. Given a signal that is sent

2.6.2
Model optimization and loss functions
The fundamental building blocks of neural networks have briefly been described in the
previous sections, but it remains to explain how ANNs learn to solve a given task. To
understand this, a few other terms and notations need an introduction. At the core of every
deep neural network trained in a supervised learning setting, there is an optimizer and a
loss function (also called an objective or cost function). The general purpose of having a
loss function is to get an estimate of how well the model performs in terms of the distance
between the predicted and the expected outputs [66]. There exist various functions to
get such estimates, and the choice of a specific function depends on the task. In turn,
the purpose of the optimizer is to minimize this loss function in a most-often non-convex
landscape, by updating the weights and biases of the connections in the hidden layers.
These non-convex characteristics of the loss function in deep neural networks arise due to
the non-linear activation functions that are applied to the hidden layers of the network. To
update the weights and biases of the network the optimizer uses the negative gradient of the
loss function. This negative gradient is computed through the backpropagation algorithm.
The objective of the backpropagation algorithm is essentially what makes neural networks
able to learn. The mathematical deduction of the backpropagation algorithm will not be
elaborated here, as the purpose of this chapter is to highlight and introduce most of the
relevant theory that is specific and essential to this project. In simple terms, this algorithm
computes this gradient by recursively applying the chain rule with respect to each layer

Optimizers and gradient-based learning
As mentioned previously, the function of an optimizer in a deep neural network is to
minimize the loss function in a non-convex landscape by updating the weights and biases
of the network according to the negative gradient of the loss function calculated at a
point in time. All optimizers can be considered gradient-based as they try to minimize
the loss function based on the gradients they see at any given time [66, 65]. It should
be stated that gradient-based optimizers never guarantee that the global minimum of a
non-convex loss function is reached. An example of a non-convex function is shown in Fig.
2.3, where multiple local minima and maxima can be seen, implying that all gradients
over the landscape of the function do not lead to the same minimum. The black path
represents a random path that an optimizer could choose to reach the global minimum in
this landscape of a loss function.

2.7
Protein language models
Protein language models have arisen from the field of natural language processing (NLP).
The field of NLP has gained massive attention through recent years, also in the wider
population, largely influenced by the introduction of the now commonly known generative
pre-trained transformer (GPT) models [70, 71]. The reason for this lies in these models’
capabilities to accurately decipher language structures and derive meaning from human
languages. For a machine, the processing of protein sequences is no different than human
languages as they can both be represented as long strings containing information and
underlying contextual patterns.
The development of transformer protein language models has given rise to major im-
provements in solving and predicting biochemical features of proteins for a large range of
bioinformatics tasks. These models, like the models of the Evolutionary Scale Modeling
[3] and ProtTrans [7] projects are based on transformers, which represent a class of semi-
supervised models containing layers of encoders and decoders in their architecture, which
will briefly be explained in the following section. The semi-supervised learning setting for
these transformers can be divided into two training phases. Firstly, the transformer is
pre-trained in an unsupervised setting using unlabeled data, where it is trained to pre-
dict missing residues in the protein through masked language modeling. The advantage
of employing masked language modeling during the unsupervised pre-training is that it
allows training on huge amounts of data, which enables the model to retrieve the gen-
eral structures of the language of proteins [72]. As a consequence of the development of
next-generation sequencing technologies and the relatively low cost of high-throughput
sequencing, huge amounts of unannotated genomic and protein data is produced at an
ever-increasing rate [73]. This provides a benefit for these models, as they do not need
annotated data to decipher the language structure of proteins, and learn better with more
data.
This synergetic effect provides part of the reason for the attractability of these
models for solving bioinformatics and biotechnological tasks. Adding to this, experimen-
tal annotation of new protein structures is often expensive and laborious, which is why
these in silico methods have received great interest due to the inexpensiveness of training
a model on a small set of labeled data using the sequence embeddings from a protein
language model to solve a given task.

2.7.1
Encoders and decoders
At the core of a transformer model are an encoder and decoder that contains multiple
self-attention layers[70]. These are the building blocks that make transformers able to
learn the general structures of languages efficiently through masked language modeling.
Here ”efficiently” refers to the property that transformers can process data of strings in
parallel, compared to previous networks used for language modeling, e.g. recurrent neural
networks that are limited to the sequential processing of a string. The purpose of the
encoder is to compute attention vectors of a given string that captures which parts of
the string that are important to the context. The string is first converted to a numerical
vector representation after which the encoder does a positional encoding. The purpose of
the positional encoder is to cluster similar words close to each other in a multidimensional
space by observing each word separately (no-self attention) [70]. The positional encoding
of the sentence is then passed to the actual encoder block containing the attention head
(see Fig. 2.4). The purpose of the self-attention mechanism is to capture which parts of
the input string it should focus on with regard to every single word from the string. From
the self-attentions of these contextual relationships, attention vectors are created. The
structure of the decoder is somewhat similar to the encoder, apart from the first part of
the decoder block, where the attention vectors are generated from masked self-attention.
The masked self-attention means that the decoder does not know which word is the next in
the sentence, it can only compare to previous parts of the sentence that it has encountered
when computing its attention vectors. Attention vectors from the encoder and masked
attention vectors from the decoder are then compared in another self-attention layer of
the decoder. Based on the similarities between these vectors the decoder makes a qualified
guess of what the actual next word is.

2.8
DeepLoc 2.0
DeepLoc 2.0 [1] is the sequel of DeepLoc1.0 [6], a sequence-based web server developed
for multi-label predictions of subcellular locations of eukaryotic proteins. As mentioned
previously, the development and results of DeepLoc 2.0, which takes advantages and em-
ploys pre-trained protein embeddings for this bioinformatics task, is what gave rise to
this project.
The model of DeepLoc 2.0 was built and trained upon protein sequence
embeddings of two of the same protein LMs that have been included in this project,
namely the 33-layer ESM1B model and the ProtT5-XL-UniRef50, using three curated
datasets. The models were trained on a dataset extracted from the UniProtKB database
with labeled annotations of ten subcellular locations for 28,303 accessions, i.e. Cytoplasm,
Nucleus, Extracellular, Cell membrane, Mitochondrion, Plastid, Endoplasmic reticulum,
Lysosome/Vacuole, Golgi apparatus, and Peroxisome. In addition, a dataset including
nine types of sorting signals was curated and compiled mainly from literature. Lastly,
a dataset for testing was constructed from the Human Protein Atlas (HPA) containing
subcellular location labels of 1,717 proteins with strong evidence after filtering.
At the core of the model is an attention head, which function is to guide the model towards
regions of the proteins important for its subcellular location. As described earlier, various
sorting signals are essential for the translocation of proteins to different compartments of
the cell, which is part of the reason why the prediction of sorting signals was included in
DeepLoc 2.0 for better interpretation of predictions. As shown by Tseng et al. [74] feature
attribution in classification tasks of genomic data can be guided by Fourier transforms,
which can lead to better interpretability of models. Reasoning over these findings led to a
belief that a similar attention mechanism could assist in the task of predicting subcellular
locations, aiding to direct the learning of a model towards areas of higher importance in
the sequence. DeepLoc 2.0 builds upon this idea, but applies another transform, namely
the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) that does not rely on the periodicity of signals,
thereby likely leading to better interpretation around the termini of the protein, where
the sorting signals are often present. This attention mechanism is the same that has been
used throughout this project for the attention head of the models and will be explained in
more detail in section 2.8.1. During the training of DeepLoc 2.0, a significant correlation
between sorting signals and the attention mechanism was observed and visualized through
attention plots of the sequence.
DeepLoc 2.0 showed state-of-the-art performance compared to other subcellular location
predictors, including both sequence and homology-based models, i.e. DeepLoc 1.0 [6],
YLoc+ [75], LAProtT5 [76], and Fuel-mLoc [77], after construction of a test setup that
allowed a fair comparison against gene ontology (GO) based models. These results were
contributing to the idea of applying the same methods and principles to the task of clas-
sifying the membrane associativity of proteins. For this reason, along with the fact that
predictions of membrane associativity were also available with DeepLoc 1.0, it has been a
desire to include this feature as part of the DeepLoc 2.0 server.

2.8.1
Attention mechanism
During the development of DeepLoc 2.0, an attention mechanism based on the Discrete
Cosine Transform was implemented in the model architecture, resting on the findings of
Tseng et al. [74], who showed how attribution priors based on Fourier Transforms can
improve interpretability and stability for deep learning models within genomic research.
The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) has long been one of the most important tools for
signal and image processing across various fields of research [78]. The classical example
of an application for Fourier Transforms is the decomposition of frequencies from sound,
which can be decomposed and modeled into the sum of various sinusoidal functions and
e.g. distinguish signal from noise.
Like any type of discrete data that displays these
characteristics and can be modeled as a finite time series, the mathematics of Fourier
Transforms can be applied. Encoded proteins and DNA sequences can also be viewed as
discrete finite time series presenting with noise and underlying signals. There are many
examples of sequence motifs that can act as such signals in genomics and proteomics,
that relate genes and proteins to their function, and in the case of predicting subcellular
locations of proteins, signal peptides or other sorting signals is an example of such.
2.8.2
Discrete cosine transform
The application and implementation of the DFT however has its limits in the task of
predicting subcellular locations.
Since sequences are represented from frequencies and
coefficients made up of sinusoidal functions, this implies that the finite signal can be
represented repeatedly to give an infinite signal. Both the DFT and DCT rely on this
property but handle the discontinuities that can arise around the edges of the finite signal
differently. While the extension of the finite signal does not change the DCT, due to its
continuous properties at a double-length periodic extension, the discontinuities around the
ends of the finite signal can artificially introduce high-frequency components around the
ends of the signal when applying the DFT, caused by the slow decay of Fourier coefficients
[79]. For protein sequences this can lead to the introduction of unwanted high-frequency
artifacts around the termini of the protein. This property is problematic with regards
to the prediction of subcellular locations, but also membrane protein-type predictions, as
sequence motifs that may hint to the localization or membrane-associativity of a protein
are often present around the N- or C-terminus and may therefore be hidden by such signal
artifacts produced around the ends.

2.9
Homology partitioning
When designing a machine learning study, it is important to consider and reduce all factors
that can induce overfitting and negatively impact a model’s ability to generalize. The way
data is organized between training and testing, especially in k-fold cross-validation, is
crucial to enable models to generalize and predict unseen data, which highlights why
caution must be taken when designing the training setup, independently of the model
design and the choice of parameters.
This aspect is even more pronounced for many
deep learning tasks within bioinformatics and proteomics as it is notably rare that data
is randomly distributed, due to the evolution and conservation of genes across organisms.
When constructing datasets for training, validation, and testing it is essential to assess the
similarity between these sets and take measures to ensure that overlaps are not pronounced.
To solve this various algorithms and approaches exist. All of the methods are based on
alignment scores between the sequences in the dataset but apply different methods for
data partitioning which can yield different results and give rise to differences in a model’s
ability to generalize.
The purpose of homology partitioning is to reduce similarity and overlapping homology
between data partitions, based on a sequence identity threshold. This is challenging for
various reasons. For a given task e.g. available data can sometimes be sparse and when
carrying out homology partitioning it is often necessary the reduce the dataset and remove
samples that disobeys the homology constraints set for the study, which can make data
27
==PAGE:29==
even more sparse causing negative effects on the learning. Another challenge to consider
when performing homology partitioning for classification tasks is the distribution and bal-
ancing of labels between the data partitions. If partitions become imbalanced with regard
to the distribution of classes compared to the true distribution of classes in the dataset,
it will in most cases also affect the model’s ability to generalize. When doing homology
partitioning or reduction, a maximum sequence identity threshold is chosen, which defines
a threshold at which the sequence identity between samples of different partitions should
be lower than. A common choice for this threshold is a maximum sequence identity of
30%, which has also been used for homology partitioning in this project.
There exist
various algorithms to compute sequence alignments and calculate sequence identities, but
some of the most common ones for pairwise sequence alignment include the Needleman-
Wunsch (NW) algorithm for global alignment and the Smith-Waterman (SW) algorithm
for local alignment, both based on dynamic programming and substitutions matrices like
the BLOSUM.
Tools that have been popularly used for homology partitioning through recent years include
MMSeqs2 [80] and CD-HIT [81]. These clustering algorithms use the SW algorithm for
computing sequence identities and are fast at doing so.
However, the speed of these
algorithms comes with a limitation, as both of the algorithms only use one representative
sequence from each partition, which is compared to all other samples from other partitions
using the maximum sequence identity threshold. As seen, this inevitably gives rise to the
problem that other sequences within the same cluster of the representative sequence could
potentially have sequence identities that far exceed the chosen threshold leading to a
homology leakage between the partitions. The potential risk of this could lead to poorer
generalization of models if homologous sequences, not captured by these tools, are highly
pronounced across partitions. To circumvent this issue we employ a recently developed
tool for homology partitioning, GraphPart (Magnus G´ıslason, Felix Teufel, Henrik Nielsen,
Ole Winther - manuscript in preparation). This new tool applies more strict criteria for
the homology partitioning while also retaining most of the original samples.
2.9.1
GraphPart
GraphPart is a homology partitioning tool that uses pairwise global NW alignments to
calculate the sequence identities between all pairs of samples leading to a similarity matrix
with sequence identities as the distances between pairwise samples. The algorithm works
by applying a restricted version of the agglomerative hierarchical single linkage clustering
algorithm to the similarity matrix to yield N partitions in contrast to the formal version of
the algorithm that recursively merge clusters by the minimal distance between any pairs
of clusters until every sample is contained in one large cluster. For the task of homology
partitioning the minimum distance refers to the largest sequence identity between any
pairs of samples from different clusters. The restrictions are made up from three criteria,
1. no cluster will be placed in a partition if a homologous sample, based on the threshold,
is already present in any other partitions, 2. no cluster will be placed in a partition if the
expected size of a partition is exceeded by adding the given cluster to the partition and 3.
no cluster will be placed in a partition if the inclusion of the cluster causes the numbers
of the given labels in the cluster to exceed the expected counts of those labels within a
partition. Initially every sample is considered a single cluster. As for the original version
of the agglomerative single linkage clustering algorithm, the restricted version also only
tries to merge pairwise clusters with minimum distance between them, where ”minimum”
for single-linkage algorithms refers to the maximum sequence identity between any pair of
28
==PAGE:30==
samples within two different clusters. If the merging breaks one of the restrictions, then
the algorithm tries to merge two clusters based second largest sequence identity between
any pairs of cross-partition samples etc. After the algorithm has finished running, there
will be usually be some smaller clusters that have not been assigned to any partitions
due to the restrictions. In order to retain most of the samples from the original dataset,
GraphPart then applies a set of heuristics where it recursively removes and adds samples
from the partitions in order to optimize this challenge.
Benchmarking of GraphPart against other commonly used sequence alignment and clus-
tering tools, e.g. CD-HIT and MMSeqs2 previously mentioned, has shown that GraphPart
is superior with regard to not breaking cross-partition homology for all sequences within
all clusters, while also retaining more samples in the final partitions without breaking label
balancing. With these justifications, the use of GraphPart can provide a data setup for
the training that consequently implies a higher validity of the model by creating a data
setup that does not limit the models ability to generalize.
2.10
Homology-based models
To evaluate the competitiveness of the models developed throughout this project the
performance of these has been compared to other currently state-of-the-art tools within
membrane-type predictions for proteins. Various strategies exist to do such classification,
which depends on the data used for the model, but to distinguish the types of models
for this task, models can be deemed either sequence-based or homology-based. To clar-
ify these terms, sequence-based tools are models build solely on sequences, e.g. protein
embeddings encoded from transformer protein language models, but not just limited to
this, as any sequence representation can be considered sequence-based. In contrast to the
sequence-based models, the homology-based models are based on alignment tools to cap-
ture sequence similarities between the unknown protein and annotated sequences from a
database. From the sequence alignment gene ontology (GO-) terms about similar proteins
can be extracted, e.g. annotations of their membrane associativity. Based on these GO-
terms of the membrane annotations of similar proteins the model then makes a qualified
guess about the unknown proteins’ membrane associativity. These homology-based mod-
els both have benefits and disadvantages as their performance is always determined by the
quality and size of the database that they search. Acquiring high-quality experimental
annotations of proteins is often expensive and time requiring which presents a limitation
for these homology based models, especially with the ever-increasing amounts of data that
are produced at a high rate due to the inexpensive access to next-generation sequencing
technologies are opening the gap between undescribed and annotated proteins even more.
Furthermore, the amount of data that these models need to store readily in a database
for fast predictions also presents a challenge for models developed from this approach.
Conversely, transformer protein language models are unsupervised structure learners that
have not been dependent on annotated data during training, which will be shown to make
them more suitable for the task of predicting membrane types of proteins.

For the task of predicting membrane types of proteins, we curate 27,525 unique samples
from UniProtKB[8] based on a set of filtering criteria. To construct a high-quality dataset
all samples were subject to a common set of filtering criteria which included that samples
were required to: 1) have experimental evidence for their annotation, 2) be longer than 40
amino acids and 3) not be composed of fragments. From these general criteria for quality
assurance, the three cellular kingdoms of eukaryotes, bacteria, and archaea were filtered
for several subcellular location terms, which led to the construction of the multi-labeled
data across the four classes of peripheral, transmembrane, and lipid-anchored membrane
proteins along with the negative class of soluble proteins. The retrieved number of samples
from this data filtering can be found in Appendix A.6 in conjunction with the specific
search fields. A short comment should be made with regard to archaeal lipid-anchored
proteins. As seen from the Appendix, very few samples from this class of proteins were
found. We therefore decided to include 18 archaeal lipoproteins expressing Sec/SPII and
Tat/SPII signal peptides which were used for the development of SignalP 6.0 [82].
The distribution of sample lengths for the raw dataset of 28,433 unique sequences can be
seen in Fig. 3.1. The visualization is cut for sequences above 3000 amino acids for better
interpretation.

3.2
Study design
To assess the performance of the various protein language models across the kingdoms the
following experiments were carried out. To begin with, we wanted to compare models based
on the different protein LMs trained on the entire dataset consisting of both eukaryotes
and prokaryotes. These models will be referred to as the hybrid kingdom models. As the
DeepLoc 2.0 server for subcellular localization predictions was built on solely on sequences
from eukaryotes, we also trained a separate model for each of the LMs using only eukaryotic
samples extracted from the partitions. As we know that these partitions can be considered
independent from each other with regard to the chosen sequence identity threshold, subsets
of these partitions will also be independent obeying the same constraints. Similarly, we
trained a model for each LM solely based on prokaryotic data extracted from the partitions.
These models, trained on the two superkingdoms, will be referred to as the eukaryotic
models and prokaryotic models.
We compared the eukaryotic and prokaryotic models
against the hybrid kingdom models by evaluating performance on the respective test sets
for the superkingdoms.
In addition to the internal model comparison, we evaluated performance against three
other state-of-the-art prediction tools for membrane protein predictions, namely the Mem-
ADSVM [10], MemPype [11], and MemType-2L [12] models. As these models were dif-
ferent with regard to their output types, e.g. two of them were multi-class predictors,
while one of these multi-class predictors was also not distinguishing peripheral membrane
proteins from soluble proteins. These model properties meant that we had to construct
a test set that allowed for a fair comparison between the models. The measures taken to
ensure this are explained in section 3.3.
All internal and external comparisons were based on test sets constructed from partition
V that was kept aside as an independent test set.
3.2.1
Training setup
Every model was trained for a maximum of 30 epochs if training had not been ceased by an
early stopping criteria implemented with a patience of 5 epochs. The focal loss, described
in section 2.6.2 was used for the AdamW optimizer. We implemented a learning rate
scheduler for the optimizer, which would halve the learning rate if no improvements in the
35
==PAGE:37==
focal loss were observed for four epochs. A pruner was also implemented in the objective
function for the hyperparameter optimization task, to interrupt unpromising trials caused
by a choice of less optimal hyperparameters during hyperparameter optimization, to reduce
training time [83]. Other aspects of the hyperparameter tuning are described in section
3.2.3. Moreover, dropout was applied to the outputs of the attention head and other hidden
layers between the attention head and the final output layer to improve generalization
and reduce the risk of overfitting. Other aspects of the model architecture is explained in
section 3.2.2
As the training data was split into five partitions during homology partitioning, it was
decided to leave partition V entirely out of the training stage for independent testing.
The training was performed using 4-fold cross-validation on the retained four partitions.
For the experiments where we did hyperparameter optimization, 20 trials were used for
each fold.
The tuned parameters of the hyperparameter optimization are clarified in
section 3.2.3. It is known that hyperparameter optimization can lead to overestimations
of the true generalization of a model if trained in a k-fold cross validation setup, i.e.
non-nested. This overestimation can arise when doing hyperparameter optimization on
the same train and validation split, as the search for better hyperparameters potentially
induces overfitting to the validation data. However, as we held out a completely isolated
test set, namely partition V, we could assess whether the best model from the 4-fold
cross-validation was introducing bias from the hyperparameter optimization and truly
overestimating its generalization. If the validation accuracy of the best model was much
higher than the test accuracy on the independent set, this would be a hint of overfitting
during the hyperparameter optimization. This problematic is discussed in section 4.3.
Training setup for the ESM2-15B
As a sidenote, it should be mentioned that we were not able to train the model based on
embeddings from the largest ESM model, namely the ESM2-15B, for all four folds. We
continued to encounter GPU memory issue, even if we tried to parallelize the training
across multiple GPUs to achieve more VRAM. To attain some performance estimates
of the largest ESM model, we continually lowered the batch size and downscaled other
hyperparameters that could influence the memory consumption, until we were able to finish
training for a number of trials. This condition should be kept in mind when interpreting
the results of the ESM2-15B model that we present throughout the following sections.
The hyperparameter search space we included for the largest ESM model can be found in
section 3.2.3.
3.2.2
Model architecture
The models developed for this project generally follow the same methodology and archi-
tecture as the models of DeepLoc 2.0. A schematic overview of the models can be seen
from Fig. 3.2. The input embeddings, with dimensions depending on the given protein
LMs, are normalized and passed to a linear feed-forward layer that rescales the input
embedding size to the size of the attention head through a fully connected linear layer.
Here the inputs for the attention head are again normalized before being passed from a
linear layer into the convolutional layer that applies the 1-dimensional Gaussian kernel
that serves to smooth the raw attentions (described in section 2.8.2). SoftMax activa-
tion is applied to the output attentions and after this a dropout layer is applied. The
regularization loss based on the Discrete Cosine Transform (section 2.8.2) is computed
from the output attentions. From this point, the attentions are passed into a number of
36
==PAGE:38==
fully connected linear layers (between 0 and 2) depending on the hyperparameter setup
all following the same architecture (green and red blocks in the Figure 3.2). These blocks
first normalize the attention inputs, that are afterwards passed through a fully connected
linear layer with Leaky ReLU activation followed by a dropout layer. The attention out-
puts or transformed attention outputs, depending on the number of hidden layers chosen
by the hyperparameter optimization, are then passed to a fully connected linear layer that
transforms the size of the last hidden layer into the four outputs. From here the focal loss
(section 2.6.2) is computed during the training stage. Sigmoid activation is applied on the
raw output activations to get the label probabilities that are converted into predictions.
Figure 3.2: General model architecture. Dimensions of layers are not noted in the figure due
to variable sizes of embedding inputs and hyperparameter tuning of numbers and sizes of hidden
layers.

3.2.4
Embeddings
From the set of sequences in the entire dataset we extracted embeddings from the follow-
ing protein language models; ESM2-650M, ESM1B-650M (650 million parameters, embed-
ding size 1280), ESM2-15B (15 billion parameters, embedding size 5120) and ProtT5-XL-
Uniref50 (3 billion parameters, embedding size 1024). Since the development of DeepLoc
2.0, which included the pre-trained ESM1B-650M and ProtT5-XL-Uniref50 models for
evaluation, the Meta Fundamental AI Research Protein Team has released a new genera-
tion of ESM models. This led to the inclusion of the ESM2 models in this project, which
had been reported to outperform the previous generation of ESM models on various pre-
diction tasks [84]. While the largest ESM model, i.e. ESM2-15B would likely not prove
feasible for deployment on a potential web server for membrane predictions, we wanted to
assess whether this larger model would give rise to improved inference for this task.
All models, except for the ESM1B-650M, were applicable for any sequence length. The
ESM1B models have a limitation when extracting embeddings for sequences with a length
above 1024 residues. To accommodate this, we applied a strategy to cut the sequences
into chunks of the original sequence, create the embedding for the chunks and stitch them
back together afterward. To avoid getting cuts near the ends of the sequences, which could
potentially lead to some artifacts in the sequence domains expressing potential sorting or
signal sequences, we employed a method to ensure sequences were cut closer to the middle
of the protein. We did this by applying the following steps; the number of cuts was given
by ncuts = ⌊sequence length
1022

then applied along the length of the protein in the variable chunk size. e.g. a protein with
a length of 1050 AAs would be cut at the 525th residue, instead of linearly applying cuts
leading to the example protein being cut in two chunks of size 1024 and 26, resulting in a
cut around the end.

3.3
External model comparison and sample selection
For model comparison, we evaluated the performance of three other state-of-the-art membrane-
type predictors. These included the Mem-ADSVM [10], MemPype [11] and MemType-2L
[12] models. As they were built around a different structure with regards to their predic-
tions we extracted and modified subsets of the test set to allow a fair comparison. The
measures taken to accommodate this will be described in the following sections, where the
models will also be briefly introduced.
3.3.1
Mem-ADSVM
The Mem-ADSVM is a two-layer multi-label homology-based model that uses a support
vector machine (SVM) to make its membrane-type prediction. It does so based on fre-
quencies of occurrences of the associated GO-terms of the sequence. The GO-terms are
retrieved from a compact database, they refer to as the ProSeq-GO database, by searching
for homologous accession numbers. The server discriminates between different types of
transmembrane and lipid-anchored membrane proteins in its predictions, but the outputs
can easily be translated into the four classes of this study. The first layer of the model
aims to distinguish whether the protein is membrane-bound or not. If it is declared non-
membrane, no further predictions are made, while if a protein is predicted to be membrane
bound, it will be passed to the second layer. In the second layer the SVM predicts the
membrane labels of the protein based on the frequencies of occurrences of the extracted
GO-terms. We did not have access to the database that Wan et al. is referring to re-
garding this model [10], so we could not assess which samples from the independent test
set that were possibly present in the database, giving the search engine a huge advantage
when comparing performance. Evaluating the models on the entire test set showed that
the Mem-ADSVM was outperforming our models. To evaluate if the potential homology
overlap between the test set and database was contributing to the high performance, we
removed all samples from the test set that were included in the database of UniProt before
2015. This left us with a subset of 803 independent samples. The new comparison led
to highly different results, yielding a more truthful measure of the generalization of the
Mem-ADSVM. These results are discussed in section 4.2.1.
3.3.2
MemPype
The MemPype server is a multi-class predictor developed for membrane-type predictions
of eukaryotic proteins. The model uses two pipelines for its predictions. The main pipeline,
that we compare our performance to, includes a multi-step prediction stage, that utilizes
other available prediction tools for various tasks, e.g. signal-peptide prediction with the
SPEP server[85] and prediction of GPI-anchor propeptide with PredGPI [86]. For com-
parison we use the specific output under the prediction summary of the server. The output
distinguishes different types of transmembrane and lipid-anchored proteins, but the out-
puts are straightforwardly converted to the classes used for this project. To briefly describe
the pipeline that we compare our models with, which they refer to as sequence profile build-
ing, the sequence of interest is first submitted to the SPEP server for predicting presence
of signal peptides. Afterwards, it is passed to PredGPI for GPI-anchor predictions. After
this stage, the sequence is submitted to the MemLoci server [87], that predicts which of
three membrane classes the query sequence belongs to (cell membrane, organelle mem-
brane or internal membrane). After this sequence profile building the sequence is cleaved
for its predicted signal peptide and/or GPI-propeptide, before it is finally submitted to
39
==PAGE:41==
ENSEMBLE 3.0 [88] which predicts the presence of transmembrane domains. From this
sequence profile building, the MemPype server makes its prediction about the membrane
type of the protein. The details of these other prediction tools will not be elaborated
here, but details can be found in the papers of the references. The other pipeline of the
server is homology-based, which does a BLAST search against the UniprotKB database.
This part of the pipeline is however independent from the pipeline of prediction tools,
that we compare performance to. As the server is built for multi-class predictions and
eukaryotic sequences only, we extracted the eukaryotic accessions from the test set with
single-label annotations for peripheral, transmembrane, and lipid-anchored proteins. In
addition to these properties of the server, the MemPype pipeline does not clearly distin-
guish non-membrane proteins from e.g. peripheral membrane proteins. This implies that
the server always tries to infer some type of membrane where the protein is likely found,
the types being cell membrane, internal membrane or organelle membrane and adds the
term globular to its output. Due to this limitation we encountered with the server, we
decided to also include multi-label samples positive for peripheral and soluble labels, and
merge these with the single-label peripheral class, leading to a multi-class classification of
three membrane types for comparison. This resulted in a test set of 4431 samples. As
a consequence of this class-construction, we merged the multi-label Sigmoid predictions
of samples with a true label for the soluble and peripheral classes, and inferred a correct
prediction if our models had predicted one of the two positive classes.
3.3.3
MemType-2L
MemType-2L is a two-layer multi-class predictor that employs a pseudo position-specific
scoring matrix (Pse-PSSM) and optimized evidence-theoretic K nearest neighbors ensem-
ble classifer (OET-KNN). Through the Pse-PSSM the model captures evolutionary and
sequence-order information that is used to search a database for homologous proteins,
similar to how PSI-Blast works. The retrieved GO-annotations are then assessed by the
OET-KNN, which counts the nearest neighbors and in turn makes membrane-type pre-
dictions for the protein of interest. It does so through a two layer mechanism, wherein
the first layer the protein is classified as membrane-bound or soluble determined by the
nearest neighbors of a balanced set of membrane-bound proteins (7582 annotated samples)
and non membrane-bound proteins (7965 annotated samples). If classified as membrane-
bound, the OET-KNN makes a prediction based on the nearest neighbors in the set of
membrane-bound proteins. Similarly to the other tools that were assessed for comparison,
this model does also distinguish between different types of transmembrane and lipid-
anchored membrane proteins that are conveniently translated into the membrane-classes
of this project. To allow a fair comparison we removed all multi-label samples from the
independent test set along with sequences shorter than 50 AAs which was the shortest
sequence length allowed for submission on the server. This resulted in a test set of 4922
sequences. Furthermore, we applied SoftMax to the raw outputs of the final layer to get
multi-class predictions.

3.4
Tools
Throughout the project various tools were used for the data curation and development of
models. In addition, the computational expense of constructing embeddings of the protein
LMs and training the models of this project, in terms of time and computational resources,
required working on a high-performance computing (HPC) cluster with access to a graphics
processing unit (GPU). In contrast, to working on a regular central processing unit (CPU),
that commonly has four to eight cores, a GPU possesses hundreds of smaller cores that
allow the distribution of tasks and training processes through high parallelization, which
leads to reduced computation times for a task that can be run in parallel. It should be
stated, that GPUs are not more efficient than CPUs for any given computational task.
GPUs are optimized for high throughput, meaning the number of tasks completed per unit
of time, while CPU cores are optimized for latency, which refers to the time of completing a
single task. This means that a CPU is generally faster for tasks that cannot be parallelized,
while GPUs provide much higher throughputs for simple parallelizable tasks due to the
higher number of cores, that each can perform a separate computation [97]. This is what
makes GPU highly suitable for deep learning, where many of the algorithms behind the
networks have a nature that makes them parallelizable, e.g. backpropagation for computing
gradients or computing activations for each neuron of a layer during the feed-forward pass.
In what follows, we will briefly describe the most central tools to the project and explain
the properties and specifications of these tools.

The embeddings of the protein language models were constructed from the publicly avail-

3.4.3
Optuna
For hyperparameter optimization we made use of the Optuna framework6 in combina-
tion with PyTorch Lightning. In contrast to more simple hyperparameter optimization
algorithms, like Grid search or Random search, Optuna utilizes a Tree-Structured Parzen
Estimator (TPE) algorithm for sampling new hyperparameters between each trial. The
main idea behind this algorithm to use the results and history of the chosen hyperpa-
rameters from each trial to recursively narrow down the search space for the optimal
hyperparameters. Initially, a few rounds with random choices of hyperparaters are used
to construct a collection of observations. From these observations two Parzen Estima-
tors for each hyperparameter are constructed from two sets containing the trials of what
are considered good and poor results with regard to the optimization task. From these
density estimators, random choices of new hyperparameters are drawn from the good set
and evaluated on the Parzen estimators. Over time this leads the algorithm towards only
focusing on the hyperparameters that has the largest impact on performance.

figure 4.3, showing the threshold optimization process for the ESM2-650M model, this
yielded different curves for each type of membrane protein, where the optimal threshold
for each class could be found at the threshold maximizing the MCC score for each func-

hybrid kingdom models. As seen, some of the prediction thresholds have changed signifi-

the threshold for predictions of the minority classes has increased radically, i.e. up to 85%
for lipid-anchored proteins and 65% for peripheral membrane proteins. It should be men-
tioned that a change in the prediction threshold does not necessarily lead to a significant
change in performance, independent of the size of the change. To assess which changes
that might cause a significant difference for performance we need to analyze the curvature
of each class. Predictions of classes expressing curves that have a broad flat top, e.g. the
yellow curve in Fig. 4.3 that shows optimization for the transmembrane class, does not
improve significantly even with quite large tweaks to the prediction threshold. On the
other hand, classes exhibiting more narrow curvature around the top indicates that the
performance metrics will be more sensitive to small tweaks in the threshold. Compared
to the standard prediction threshold of 50%, the classes that will usually benefit most
from this optimization are the classes that show the largest change in MCC value between

4.2
Model comparison
The models developed throughout this project were evaluated against three other state-of-
the-art predictors for membrane-type proteins, namely the Mem-ADSVM [10], MemPype
[11] and MemType-2L [12]. The foundation and architecture of these models are described
in section 3.3 along with the construction of test sets that allowed for a fair comparison
against each of these models. As mentioned, efforts were made to include more membrane-
type predictors that were mentioned in the literature, but unfortunately we could not
access them. See section 3.3.4 for details.
4.2.1
Mem-ADSVM
To compare our models with the Mem-ADSVM server a test set was constructed from a
subset of samples from partition V. The subset only contains samples that were included
in the UniProtKB database after 2014. As the Mem-ADSVM server is using a compact
database to retrieve GO-terms from, it was decided to remove all samples from the full
test set that were available in the UniprotKB database before 2015, as the server was
released in 2014. For more details on the model and construction of the test set, we refer
to section 3.3.1.
The results from the comparison can be seen in Table 4.6. All performance metrics indicate
a significant advantage in favor of the protein language models of this project. The ProtT5
model seems to have a small advantage among our internal models scoring slightly higher
for almost all the tracked metrics. The only performance metric where the Mem-ADSVM
server can be considered close to par with the transformer protein language models is for
the subset accuracy. Here the Mem-ADSVM scores 0.77 which is close to the score of the
ESM2-650M that is reported with a subset accuracy of 0.80 on the test set. The reason for
this should probably be found in the decision making mechanism in the architecture of the
Mem-ADSVM server. As described in section 3.3.1, the Mem-ADSVM server consists of
a two-layer mechanism, where the first layer determines whether the protein is membrane
52
==PAGE:54==
bound or not. If a protein is predicted as non-membrane bound in this layer, the model
will not proceed to infer other membrane classes on the sample, leading to soluble proteins
always being single-labeled for this model. As the class of soluble proteins represents the
majority class of the dataset this mechanism appears to give the Mem-ADSVM model an
advantage with regards to the subset accuracy. This advantage arises mainly due to the
overlaps we observe between the soluble and peripheral classes, where our models tend to
infer false positives for the peripheral class when a sample is only truly positive for the

A short section should be dedicated to discussing the hyperparameter optimization that
was employed in the training setup of this project. As explained in section 3.2.1, the hy-

validation. The nested strategy ensures that the best hyperparameters after training, are
less likely a result of overfitting to a particular validation set, while the normal hyper-
parameter optimization with normal k-fold cross-validation can be prone to overfit the
hyperparameters over the different trials. To assess this bias we compared the validation
accuracy that was reported for the specific model during training against the accuracy on
the independent test set for the hybrid kingdom models. The results of this assessment

Chapter 5
Conclusion
Throughout this project, we have assessed four pre-trained transformer protein language
models, for the task of predicting types of membrane proteins, i.e. the ESM2-650, ESM1B-
650M ESM2-15B, and ProtT5-XL-Uniref50 protein language models.
We curate a dataset of 27,461 multi-labeled samples consisting of eukaryotic and prokary-
otic sequences with experimental evidence for three classes of membrane proteins, namely
peripheral, transmembrane, and lipid-anchored membrane proteins, as well as soluble pro-
teins to allow differentiation between membrane-bound and non membrane-bound pro-
teins.
For the task of predicting membrane-type proteins, we trained four eukaryotic, prokary-
otic, and hybrid kingdom predictors on the pre-trained protein embeddings from the four
protein language models by employing 4-fold cross-validation with hyperparameter opti-
mization. We evaluated all models on an independent test, arising from one of the five
partitions following homology partitioning, containing 5378 samples. We evaluated the
hybrid predictors trained on the full dataset against the models trained solely on eukary-
otic or prokaryotic samples to assess any differences between the models generalization for
these two tasks. Furthermore, we compared the models developed throughout this project
to three other state-of-the-art membrane-type predictors described in the literature, i.e.
Mem-ADSVM, MemPype and MemType-2L.
Internal comparison of the four hybrid kingdom models trained on the embeddings from
each of the different protein language models shows only small variations in performance,
with a slight advantage towards the larger protein language models, i.e. ProtT5 and ESM2-
15B. We report an average accuracy on the independent test set of ≈88% across the four
hybrid kingdom models, along with an average MCC score for each type of membrane
associativity of appx. 0.35 for peripheral, 0.94 for transmembrane, 0.66 for lipid-anchored,
and 0.82 for soluble. The main variation appears to be driven by the bias of the model
in terms of the produced number of labels. The ESM2-650M is most affected by this,
predicting ≈7% more labels than true labels in the test, compared to the ProtT5 model
that displays the lowest bias, only producing 2% more labels.
The low MCC score that was reported for the peripheral class was explained by the bio-
chemical overlap with the soluble class, which was appx. eight times higher represented
in the dataset. The assumption of a biochemical overlap was also confirmed by the fact
58
Marius Thrane Ødum
==PAGE:60==
that half of the samples displaying positive labels for the peripheral class in the dataset
did also show co-presence of a positive label for the soluble class, making it challenging
for the models to improve generalization for these classes.
Comparing the performance of the hybrid kingdom models, evaluated on the eukaryotic
subset of the independent test set, to models trained solely on eukaryotic data, did not
reveal any significant advantages in terms of generalization and indicated that employing
hybrid models for eukaryotic predictions might be as well suited. The only observation of
a potential difference arose from the minority class of lipid-anchored proteins, where the
ESM2-650M model trained on eukaryotic data appeared to perform slightly better with
an MCC score of 0.60 in comparison to ≈0.55 for the hybrid kingdom models of ESM2-
650M and ProtT5, partly leading to a higher subset accuracy and F1macro score. However,
this advantage was not supported by the ProtT5 model trained on eukaryotic sequences,
which was reported to have the lowest MCC score for this comparison, with a score of 0.51.
Similarly, we did not observe any pronounced systematic differences in performance be-
tween the hybrid kingdom models and the models trained solely on prokaryotic sequences,
evaluated on the prokaryotic subset of the independent test set. However, the ProtT5
model trained on prokaryotic sequences did display an exceptionally high MCC score for
prokaryotic lipid-anchored sequences of 0.92 which was found intriguing.
In light of this observation, we decided to screen all samples containing lipid-anchor labels
in the prokaryotic test set, as it was hypothesized that the ProtT5 could have picked up
the consensus sequence of bacterial lipoprotein signal peptides.
Predictions from SignalP 6.0 confirmed this hypothesis, as most of the samples positive
for the lipid-anchor label did express the presence of lipoprotein signal peptides. Further-
more it was seen that the average MCC score for prokaryotic lipid-anchors among the
hybrid kingdom models was significantly higher than the average MCC score reported for
eukaryotic lipid-anchors (0.84 vs. 0.62) which was also explained by the strong sequence
motif around the cleavage site of the lipoprotein signal peptide.
A comparison of the models we developed throughout this project were shown to outper-
form all other state-of-the-art membrane-type predictors that we could access, both for
multi-class and multi-label classification. The models we were able to access also displayed
different limitations. The MemPype server did not strictly distinguish membrane-bound
from soluble. The two homology-based models, Mem-ADSVM and MemType-2L showed
limited generalization for unseen data, likely due to their backend database for retrieving
homologs. Furthermore, Mem-ADSVM, which was alledgedly a multi-label predictor, uti-
lized a decision-making mechanism that only allowed soluble proteins to be single-labeled,
making it non-feasible for multi-label predictions due to the widespread evidence of sol-
uble proteins that are capable of interacting with the membrane, giving rise to most of
the multi-label samples among membrane proteins.
The transformer language models
developed throughout this project overcome all of these limitations, while also reporting
significantly higher performance across all performance metrics included in this study.

5.1
Perspectives
The findings during this project emphasize the potential of transformer protein language
models. While homology based models around the turn of the millennium had a major
impact on diminishing the need for experimental assays to classify proteins in the lab,
and still have, the recent advances seen within natural language processing and the de-
velopment of transformer protein language models are now helping to reduce the need for
labeled data, which homology-based models rely on. This makes protein language models
hugely applicable now and for the years to come, considering the ever-growing amounts
of unlabeled data produced from high-throughput sequencing technologies.
As the results of the project have shown the potential of using pre-trained protein em-
beddings for the task of predicting the membrane-type of proteins, outperforming other
state-of-the-art predictors emphatically, it would be of great interest to dive deeper into
the reasons for this.
In order to investigate this, it could be interesting to visualize the raw pre-trained embed-
dings along with the output of an intermediate layer of our models, such as the attention
head, to see if any conclusions can be drawn regarding how the models do inference.
A multivariate analysis like Principal Component Analysis (PCA) or t-distributed Stochas-
tic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) could possibly shed light on some of the properties of
the model, and perhaps also give insights into the performance differences we observed
for the prediction of prokaryotic and eukaryotic lipid-anchored proteins. In addition to
this, it could be interesting to visualize attention plots for a collection of samples, to see
what features and sequence motifs the models use for their predictions when differentiating
between the different types of membrane proteins. Furthermore, nested cross-validation
could be implemented for the hyperparameter optimization to see if it yielded different
results with regards to the choice of hyperparameters and assess if generalization could be
improved.
