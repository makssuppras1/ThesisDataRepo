Abstract
This study holistically assesses building renovation in relation to the measurement areas
environmental sustainability, indoor environment, and energy use. Moreover, the most
influential parameters are determined as well as their optimal solution.
Using the Pearson Correlation Coefficient 12 different renovation parameters are as­
sessed in relation to a set of measures within the indoor environment, energy use, and
environmental sustainability. The 12 assessed parameters in this study are the window
pane constellation, type of window frame, both thickness and type of attic, floor, interior
wall, and exterior wall post­insulation, type of AHU heat exchanger, and type of demand
control ventilation. Moreover, the examined measures include temperature, daylight, rel­
ative humidity, energy consumption, and Global Warming Potential.
The Pearson Correlation Coefficient between the parameters and measures is gradually
assessed as simulations of the different parameter combinations (scenarios) are run. This
is done to estimate when the correlation converges and conclusions can be made. From
the correlation coefficient, the impact of the parameters in relation to the measures is
examined, and the sensitivity is assessed. Prior to the Pearson Correlation Coefficient
simulations in IES­VE, ClimateStudio, and LCA­byg are conducted to address the value
of each measure.
The influential parameters’ optimal solutions in relation to the measures where the pa­
rameters indicate a correlation are then evaluated using two different approaches. An
analytical approach where the parameter solutions are plotted against the values of the
measures where a correlation is found. The optimal parameter solution for each measure
is then discovered and the optimal solution is determined as the best parameter solution
among most measures.
Moreover, the Technique for Order of Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution (TOPSIS)
is used to address the optimal solution. As a result, each parameter solution and its
respective input to the different measures are converted into a performance score, which
indicates how well the solution performs in relation to the ideal solution. This is done by
finding the Euclidean distance between the solutions and the ideal solution.
From this study, it can be concluded that the use of the Pearson Correlation Coefficient
alone is not enough to determine sensitivity. It is necessary to include whether there is
a connection between the parameters and measures before sensitivity can be detected.
Moreover, the optimization approaches addressed in this study can only assess the op­
timal parameter solution and not the optimal scenario. Finally, it can be concluded from
the conditions given in this study that the window pane constellation is the most influen­
tial parameter holistically, and the 3­pane window constellation is found to be the holistic
optimal solution.

Introduction
Growing focus on sustainability impacts the building industry in particular, as buildings
are responsible for around 30% of the total CO2 emissions (Costa et al. 2013). Moreover,
11% of the global energy­ and process­related greenhouse gas emissions originate from
the manufacturing of building materials (Zhong et al. 2021). Consequently, the building
industry is pressured to reduce its environmental impact.
With the 2030 sustainability target, Denmark commits to reduce their overall CO2 emis­
sions by 70% compared to their emissions in 1990. In order to achieve this goal, the
Danish Building Regulations mandated that all new buildings with a gross area greater
than 1,000 m2 must emit no more than 12 kg CO2 ­ eq per year, beginning in 2023 (In­
denrigs og Boligministeriet 2021).
Furthermore, building operation accounts for 40% of the overall energy use, and in de­
veloped countries, 50% of this consumption is used for the HVAC systems (Indenrigs og
Boligministeriet 2021; Costa et al. 2013). Oil, natural gas, and coal still cover roughly
80% of the global energy production (Energistyrelsen 2023). As a consequence, a shift
in the energy sources or a reduction of the overall energy consumption used to operate
buildings would benefit sustainability.
Even though energy savings are often seen as sustainable, it is crucial to account for
embodied energy as well. Embodied energy is the sum of all the energy used for the
production of a material or a product (Hammond et al. 2008). This is important, as energy
savings frequently are facilitated through new implementations or installations in the build­
ing. As a result, it is necessary to ensure that the initiatives taken to save energy are not
causing greater CO2 emissions than the emissions saved (Indenrigs og Boligministeriet
2021).
According to Indenrigs og Boligministeriet (2021) and the Danish national strategy for
sustainable buildings, initiatives aimed at achieving sustainable buildings should support
health and indoor climate as well. This is important since populations in industrialized
countries spend around 90% of their time indoors. Studies show that the indoor environ­
ment affects the population in various ways and that the conditions inside buildings have
a large impact on human well­being (Indenrigs og Boligministeriet 2021). Hence, the in­
door environment, emissions to the atmosphere, and energy consumption should all be
considered together in the design processes for sustainable buildings.
As a result of the requirements concerning energy consumption and indoor environment,
simulations of these topics are frequently included in the documentation related to the
design process in the Danish building industry (Bolig­ og Planstyrelsen 2022c; Bolig­ og
Planstyrelsen 2022d). Up to this point, no regulations regarding emissions to the at­
mosphere or environmental sustainability related to buildings have been included in the
building regulations. Consequently, simulations or documentation of these emissions is
seldom used in the building design. However, incorporating environmental sustainability
in the design of buildings could assist in achieving the emission target of 2030.

In most countries, it is known to be residential buildings that account for the largest share
of primary energy use (Climate Initiative 2009). A large amount of this energy consump­
tion can be related to older buildings, as their use frequently results in high energy con­
sumption. Energy renovation of the older building stock could thereby reduce the overall
energy consumption and the overall impact on the environment (Indenrigs og Boligmin­
isteriet 2021). The optimal design process of the building stock could potentially start off
with renovations of residential buildings.
1.1
Problem statement
The objective of this study is to holistically assess building renovation in relation to the
measurement areas of environmental sustainability, indoor environment, and energy use.
The motivation is to examine how sensitive the different measurement areas are to changes
in renovation parameters and determine the most influential parameters. This will be ad­
dressed by investigating the effects of renovation and determining whether a correlation
can be found between renovation parameters and measurement areas. Furthermore,
the study seeks to holistically identify the optimal solution for the influential renovation
parameters.

Theoretical background
2.1
Indoor environment
In practice, the indoor environment is described through the term Indoor Environmental
Quality (IEQ). This term is affected by multiple factors but is mainly described through
the thermal environment, the atmospheric environment, the visual environment, and the
acoustic environment, as indicated in Figure 2.1 (Vorre et al. 2017). All these four envi­
ronmental areas are of importance regarding the IEQ. However, only the thermal, atmo­
spheric, and visual environment will be addressed in this study.
Figure 2.1: The primary areas which affect IEQ.
2.1.1
Thermal comfort
Thermal comfort is a measure of the thermal environment, which includes the well­being
of the building’s occupants. Thermal comfort can thereby be expressed in terms of how
comfortable and satisfied the occupants are with the thermal conditions i.e. the occupants’
feelings of cold or warmth.
The terms temperature, radiant temperature, and air movement are commonly used to
describe thermal comfort. However, thermal comfort and satisfaction can also be seen as
a product of both personal and thermal factors. As a result, thermal satisfaction can be
defined through the six factors: radiant temperature, air temperature, air velocity, clothing
level, activity level, and humidity. This study will only focus on the building itself and the
quantities of air temperature, radiant temperature, air velocity, and humidity.
The air temperature is known as the temperature of the air in the space where
the occupants are present. The radiant temperature is the temperature of the
surfaces surrounding the occupant. A more commonly used term is the mean
radiant temperature. The mean radiant temperature is defined as a homoge­
neous temperature, where the radiant heat from the human body is equivalent
to the radiant heat from the actual nonuniform enclosure. Thereby a measure of the av­
erage temperature of the surfaces surrounding the occupant. The air velocity is a vector
described through size and direction. The velocity in relation to the thermal environment
is defined as speed, and as a result, described as the magnitude of the velocity vector at
a measuring point (Dansk Standard 2001).

Indoor air quality
The atmospheric environment includes Indoor Air Quality (IAQ). IAQ reflects the con­
centration of pollutants in the air, meaning the quality of the air we breathe in. Multiple
factors have an impact on air pollution including asbestos, radon, lead, particulate organic
materials (POM), total volatile organic compounds (TVOCs), biological pollutants driven
from sources including fungi, bacteria, viruses, mites, and pollen, as well as combustion­
generated contaminates including gases and small particles, which are by­products of

burning (Kubba 2017). Some of these pollution sources can cause eye irritation, throat
irritation, headaches, and dizziness. In contrast, high exposure to others can cause lung
cancer, shortness of breath, and coma (Kubba 2017).
At least 39 different factors can be used to assess the IAQ. However, not all of these
pollution sources are easy to measure or quantify. The most commonly used factors are
the ventilation rate, TVOC, formaldehyde, CO2, CO, PM10, PM2.5, ozone, benzene, and
radon (Wei et al. 2020).
The CO2 concentration is the most common indicator of the IAQ in buildings,
where occupants are the main contributors (Vorre et al. 2017). Like several of
the other gases related to the indoor environment, carbon dioxide is a color­
and odorless gas. High carbon dioxide concentrations indoors may cause dis­
comfort for the occupants in the building. However, before any significant health risks
occur, the CO2 concentration needs to be greater than 30,000 parts per million (ppm)
(Jones 2002).
The CO2 concentration indoors is defined by the CO2 concentration outside and the CO2
emissions related to people’s activities inside. The CO2 concentration in the room can be
determined through the dilution equation (Valbjørn et al. 2000).

(2.3)

2.1.3
Visual comfort
A look outside, adequate glare reduction, and a sufficient amount of natural light are all
indicators of visual comfort (Sok­Paupardin 2020). Visual comfort can be seen as the
comfort of the eye and can be quantified through the amount of either daylight or electrical
lighting, the quality of the light, the view out, and glare (Vorre et al. 2017).
An important element in relation to visual comfort and lighting is illuminance. Illuminance
is measured in lux or lm/m2 and is defined as the total radiated light present on each unit
area of a surface. This concept is used to describe the amount of daylight and electrical
lighting in the building (Jacobs 2004).

Daylight is considered to have an effect on people’s mood, motivation, and
productivity, as well as to reduce stress and exhaustion. As a consequence,
daylight is the most essential source of lighting regarding the well­being of hu­
mans (Dansk Standard 2011). The daylight related to the building is the natural
lighting brought from the outside into the building through its openings. The primary de­
terminants of how much daylight the building receives are the distance to surrounding
buildings, the height of the surrounding buildings, the size of the building openings, the
size of potential shading, the orientation of the building, and the reflectance of the building
materials (Fontoynont et al. 2004).
The amount of daylight in the building can be measured through the daylight factor (DF)
or daylight autonomy (DA). DF is defined as the ratio of illuminance at a given point on
the surface received from the sky (Dansk Standard 2011). DA is defined as:
”the percentage of the year when a minimum illuminance threshold is met by
daylit alone” (Reinhart et al. 2006, p. 16)
According to Bolig­ og Planstyrelsen (2022b), the amount of daylight in residential build­
ings should comply with the following requirement. Where the relevant floor area is de­
fined as the interior floor area.
Min. 300 lux on min. 50% of the relevant area min. 50% of the daylit hours
2.2
Energy consumption
Energy is an essential good in human life, as multiple human necessities are based on
or conducted from energy (Ahmad et al. 2020). The same accounts for the buildings,
where all construction materials, the construction process, the operation of the building,
and the demolition are based on energy. The majority of the energy used in relation to
buildings is consumed during building operation. This energy is mainly used for heating,
ventilation, and air conditioning control (HVAC), domestic hot water (DHW), lighting, and
other electrical­driven technologies within the building (Climate Initiative 2009).

ation (Climate Initiative 2009).

The energy use for the building during its operation varies considerably, as it depends on
multiple interrelated factors in the building. These factors include the climate as well as
the location of the building and its surroundings, the level of energy demand and function
of the building, the design of the building and the construction materials, whether the heat
loss through the building envelope is low or high, and the behavior of the occupants in
the building whether the occupants are active or still. All these factors are affecting the
conditions in the building and thereby the final energy demand of the building (Climate
Initiative 2009).
A more commonly used term for energy use during the building operation is energy con­
sumption. The energy consumption in relation to the building is either described as the
primary energy or delivered energy. Primary energy is the energy associated with fossil
fuels and thus the raw energy that has not been processed by humans. While the de­
livered energy is the energy delivered to the building, and thereby the amount of energy
consumed (Ahmad et al. 2020).
In the Danish building industry, the primary energy consumption of the building
is usually considered through the Energy Frame. The Energy Frame accounts
for the total primary energy needed during the operation of the building (Bolig­
og Planstyrelsen 2022c). In relation to residential buildings, the energy in­
cluded in the Energy Frame is the energy used for heating, ventilation, cooling,
and DHW (Bolig­ og Planstyrelsen 2022c; Kragh et al. 2018).
Energy consumption is determined through factors such as heat losses, heat gains, flow
rates, and solar heating. When the energy consumption in relation to the Energy Frame
is calculated the energy use is multiplied by energy factors to estimate the primary energy
needs. These factors ensure that the energy is converted to primary energy with respect
to the actual energy source. Table 2.1 indicates the present conversion factors.

2.3
Sustainability
Sustainability covers growth, where the present generation can continue to live accord­
ing to their present well­being, without harming the well­being of following generations.
Sustainability is measured through three pillars: social sustainability, economic sustain­
ability, and environmental sustainability (Indenrigs og Boligministeriet 2021). To be able
to address sustainability all three pillars should be documented.
Figure 2.3: The three main pillars of sustainability.
Social sustainability accounts for human welfare and health. Regarding the buildings, so­
cial sustainability is set to revolve around elements such as the health of the occupants,
their comfort, the indoor climate surrounding the occupants, the safety of the building,
and the availability of the building. Economic sustainability ensures that the product is a
balanced solution in relation to investment and quality. Economical sustainability should
ensure value stability, functionality, and flexibility. Environmental sustainability accounts
for the impact on nature, climate, and resources, and for the buildings the energy and
water use is accounted for as well. Environmental sustainability quantifies how a product
or solution impacts the environment (Birgisdottir 2015). This study will however only ac­
count the environmental sustainability.
2.3.1
Life cycle assessment
A life cycle assessment (LCA) is a method, which can be used to address the environ­
mental impact of a product or a solution. The methodology investigates the environmental
impact throughout the entire lifetime of the product or solution. In other words, the product
is examined from cradle­to­grave (Birgisdottir and Rasmussen 2015).
The LCA consists of four main parts: goal and scope, life cycle inventory (LCI), life cycle
impact assessment (LCIA), and interpretation. The goal and scope follow the reason for
carrying out the analysis and the boundaries related to the system. The LCI results in
a detailed list of the quantified physical elementary flows connected to the system. The
LCIA addresses the impact of the elementary flows in the LCI, in relation to a set of impact
categories. This is done through the following equation (Rosenbaum et al. 2018):

2.3.2
Building LCA
Specifically, for the LCA regarding the building industry, the cradle­to­grave investiga­
tion conducted through the LCA includes the production phase (A1­A3), the construction
phase (A4­A5), the use phase (B1­B7), the end­of­life phase (C1­C4), and the potential

The LCA regarding building renovations can be conducted by the use of three different
approaches. These three different approaches, have different degrees of detail and in­

to the building, as the entire building and all the phases should be included. This ap­

The second approach requires less detailed data and is recommended in cases where
the building receives a certificate. The third approach requires data related to the newly
added materials and the use of the building. This approach is recommended when the
design of the building is investigated (Schødt Worm et al. 2017).
When a building LCA is performed, much of the implemented data frequently includes en­
vironmental product declarations. An environmental product declaration (EPD) is a doc­
umentation of the environmental properties of a product or building material. The EPD
provides knowledge of energy consumption, resource consumption, waste generation,
and the environmental impact related to the production, use, and waste handling of the
building material. All EPDs are based on the 4 steps included in the LCA and are mostly
following the European and international standards EN 15804 and ISO 14025. Using an
EPD, especially for a comparative approach should be handled with care. EPDs should
not be compared directly, but should always be used in relation to a building context (EPD
Danmark 2012). Furthermore, if EPDs generated using the new EN 15804:A2 method­
ology are used in a building LCA, that already includes EPDs generated using the old
EN 15804:A1 methodology, should only the GWP impacts be considered (EPD Danmark
2012; Sørensen 2022).

Sensitivity
A sensitivity analysis is a study of how uncertainties in the model output, can be allocated
to different uncertainties in the model input. In other words, the sensitivity shows how
much an alteration to the input parameter affects the result (Saltelli 2002). A sensitivity
analysis can thereby be used to address which parameters have the greatest effect on
the output. Sensitivity can be addressed using multiple methodologies, some common
methodologies are Sobol SA, the Morris method, and Delta moment­independent mea­
sure (DMIM). All these methodologies approach sensitivity differently. However, they all

require an input parameter sample file with inputs of the parameter combinations desired
to address (Van Hove et al. 2022).
Latin hypercube is a methodology that can be used for generating the input parameter
sample file. This specific methodology generates a sample file that includes properties
between random sampling and stratified sampling (Helton et al. 2003). As a result, the
sample file includes a random selection but does still account for the different subcate­
gories indicated in the data.
Sensitivity in relation to this study intends to show if an alteration to the input parameter
affects the result. As a consequence, if there is a correlation between the input parameter
and the results. A correlation indicates whether a change in one variable can predict the
change in another variable (Aarhus Universitet 2019).
The Pearson Correlation Coefficient (PCC) is a measure of the linear relation
between two random variables. Mathematically, it is defined as the covari­
ance of the two variables divided by the product of their standard deviations.
This correlation indicates the strength of the linearity between the assessed
variables. The PCC is ranged from ­1 to 1. As long as PCC is 0, the two vari­
ables are set to be uncorrelated. If the PCC is equal to 1 or any positive number, the two
variables are directly related. While ­1 or any negative number indicates an inverse corre­
lation between the two variables (Zhou et al. 2016). The stronger the correlation between
the two variables, the closer the correlation is to the outer board of the span. The PCC
can be calculated as follows (Zhou et al. 2016):

2.5
Optimization
An optimization is a powerful tool in the holistic approach to design and operation. Opti­
mization entails the selection of the best solution from a set of possible solutions to the
problem. The properties of a specific solution are quantified using an objective function,
which is then sought to minimize or maximize in the search for the best possible solution
(El­Halwagi 2006).
Optimizations can be carried out in a variety of ways. However, most approaches only
account for one criterion, while this study intends to optimize the solution in relation to
multiple criteria. One approach for finding the optimal solution regarding multiple criteria
is the multi­criteria decision analysis methodology TOPSIS.
The Technique for Order of Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution (TOPSIS), is a
method that determines the distance of different alternatives, compared to an ideal so­
lution, among the alternatives. This methodology takes the different solutions and their
respective values to the different criteria and turns them into one specific performance
score, that indicates how well the solution performs in relation to the ideal solution. This

Project boundaries
An eight­story, 6493m2 residential building from 1973 forms the basis of the analysis and
simulations in this study. The building is located in Copenhagen in the neighborhood of
Frederiksberg. The building is an institution building, and as a result, the building consists
of smaller accommodations and multiple common areas and kitchens. The majority of the
accommodations are 1­room apartments, which all have their own bathroom, as well as
a fully functional kitchen or a smaller kitchenette. Each of the accommodations has a
smaller balcony, which is shielded by glass. All accommodations are located from the 1st
to the 7th floor, while the ground floor mostly consists of common areas, hobby rooms, staff
rooms, toilets, and a kitchen. In Appendix A.1 the building floor plans, with their respective
room definitions, can be found. Finally, the building is supplied by district heating and
ventilated through a mechanically balanced central ventilation system.
3.1
Renovation initiatives and parameters
When a building is renovated, a renewal of the building is made. Multiple renewals of a
building are defined as a renovation. This study will, however, not include all the possi­
ble renovations, but only account for a set of chosen renovation initiatives and for each
of these initiatives a set of parameters. This study will only focus on and explore the
renovation initiatives, windows, post­insulation, and ventilation.
According to previous sustainability and energy­saving studies, the window pane con­
stellation and window frames are commonly studied parameters. In the study fsb Hjem
(2021) both 2­ and 3­pane standard or energy­saving windows, with either wood/wood
or wood/alu frames are compared with a sustainable point of view. In the study, both
environmental, economic, and social sustainability are assessed. According to fsb Hjem
(2021) the 3­pane wood/wood framed windows are the most sustainable in relation to
environmental sustainability. However, according to Volf et al. (2019) the 2­pane window
is the best solution regarding health with daylight quality as the health driver. Moreover,
regarding the energy consumption addressed according to Volf et al. (2019), both window
types are of equal benefits. Both of these studies conduct the basis for the parameters
chosen within the window renovation initiative. The window pane constellation and type
of window frame are thus the parameters included in this study.
Post­insulation is a typically used renovation initiative, as it is usually easy to implement.
According to Tommerup (2004), the application of post­insulation in the facade can reduce
the heat transmission loss through the facade by 35%. However, post­insulation can be
applied to the entire building envelope including the roof, the attic, decks, the deck towards
the unheated basement, and both on the interior and exterior side of the exterior walls.
The forms of post­insulation which will be examined in this study are the post­insulation of
the attic, the deck towards the unheated basement, and the post­insulation of the exterior
walls. These areas are selected as they comply with the case building. According to Bojić
et al. (2014) both the thickness of the post­insulation and the insulation type are important
aspects when finding the optimal post­insulation of the building envelope. According to

Bojić et al. (2014), mineral wool results in the lowest primary energy consumption, how­

insulation types. Consequently, the post­insulation comes with the quandary of whether
the thickness matters or not. As a result, both the insulation type and insulation thickness
are defined as important parameters to investigate in this study.
According to Miljøministeriet Miljøstyrelsen (2022), the main contributors to a poor indoor
environment in homes are particles from user products, particles from the occupants,
building materials, pollution from the underground and the air outside, pollution from cook­
ing and cleaning, as well as the moisture generated through for instance cooking. Most
of these contributors can be reduced through the ventilation system. Moreover, the venti­
lation system is often responsible for more than half of the energy consumption taken up
by the building (Laverge et al. 2011). As a result, the ventilation system affects both the
indoor environment and energy consumption and is thereby important in relation to this
study.
From Nicolaisen et al. (2019), a centralized ventilation system is concluded to be the
most sustainable type of system compared to a decentralized system. In this study, the
building already consists of a ventilation system, with ducts and air handling units (AHU).
Thereby would an alteration of the entire installation usually not be prioritized. However,
improvements to the AHU or other alterations can still be cost­effective.
For the AHU, it is for instance possible to substitute the old units with new ones. According
to Bolig­ og Planstyrelsen (2022a), the balanced ventilation system must have a heat
exchanger, which can utilize the heat in the exhaust air. These heat exchangers can come
in multiple forms and solutions. Furthermore, according to Laverge et al. (2011), demand
control ventilation (DCV) is the best approach for energy savings related to ventilation.
Moreover, according to Laverge et al. (2011), the ventilation system can save up to 25%
of its energy consumption by applying DCV, as well as maintain the indoor environment
and the indoor air quality. As a result, the change of AHUs and DCV through sensor

Measures
Since this study intends to investigate the measurement areas of energy use, environmen­
tal sustainability, and indoor environment should all of these be represented through the
measures. However, only a selection of the possible measures within each measurement
area will be included in this study.
No regulations regarding emissions related to building renovations have yet been defined.
However, as mentioned in Section 2.3 all new constructions have to document emissions
for GWP below 12 kg CO2­eq per m2 per year. The GWP and CO2­eq will thereby be­
come common terms for environmental sustainability in the building industry. Therefore,
the impact category GWP has been chosen as the measure regarding environmental sus­
tainability in this study.
Regarding building energy use, the energy consumption in kWh/m2 per year has been
selected as the measure. The energy consumption calculated in this study will comply
with the Energy Frame and thereby be calculated as the primary energy needed for both
heating and electricity. For the energy consumption related to heating, this study will
include the room heating and the heating of the supply temperature in relation to the
ventilation system. The heating related to the DHW will not be included, since the energy

environmental sustainability in building renovation
15
==PAGE:23==
needed for DHW is assumed to be constant and not changing with the parameters in
this study. As for the electrical energy consumption, the energy related to the Ventilation
system will be accounted for.
The indoor environmental quality in this study will be measured through temperature, rel­
ative humidity, CO2 concentration, and daylight. These measures have been chosen to
ensure a minimum of one measure within the three main areas that affect the IEQ: thermal
environment, atmospheric environment, and visual environment. One of the requirements
related to thermal comfort and temperature involves overheating. Here both the overheat­
ing above 27◦C and 28◦C have been selected as measures. Where the number of hours,
where the operative temperature is above both 27◦C and 28◦C, will be collected. Addi­
tionally, the hours when the building is cold have been selected as a measure. As a result,
the number of hours where the minimum temperature is allowed according to the com­
fortable operative temperatures, mentioned in section 2.1.1, will be included. Thereby,
the number of hours where the temperature is 20◦C or less. For the relative humidity,
the hours where the room has less or more than the defined requirements in Section 2.1
have been chosen as measures. As a consequence, the number of hours where the RH
is 25% or less and when the RH is 60% or more.
In relation to the atmospheric environment and IAQ, the CO2 concentration within the
building has been selected as an important measure. As a result, the number of hours
where the CO2 concentration above the outdoor concentration is 500 ppm or above will
be collected. Finally, for the visual environment, the amount of daylight in the building
has been set as an important and interesting measure. The daylight will be measured
as the fraction of the area where the building will have 300 lux or more for a minimum
of 50% of the time. In Figure 3.1 all the measures are illustrated corresponding to their
measurement areas.

Methodology
To address the impact of the renovation initiatives and their respective parameters, the
study has determined the value of each selected measure in relation to a set of different
renovation combinations. Three different simulations were run to define the value of the
measures. The simulation software IES­VE was used to determine the values regarding
energy consumption and IEQ. The simulation tool ClimateStudio was used for the simu­
lations of the IEQ measure daylight. Finally, LCA­byg was used to assess environmental
sustainability.
Two main models of the building were created in the design application software Rhino,
to conduct these simulations. Firstly, a fully built­up model of the building was required
to simulate the daylight. In this study, a fully built­up model is a model where all parts of
the building, except the apertures, are generated as 3­dimensional objects. For the rest
of the indoor environment and energy simulations, a building model with all construction
parts modeled as 2­dimensional elements was required. These two models were gener­
ated and saved for use in the Rhino plugin ClimateStudio and IES­VE. To ensure that the
two building models were consistent, the daylight simulation model was conducted first,
after which the other model was conducted from the daylight model. These two models
were then included in the simulation software, and simulations were run, with respect to
the input parameter sample file.

For the ventilation renovation initiative, the AHU was varied between the old AHU and two
new types of AHUs. Furthermore, the air exchanges were varied between the original

AHUs were replaced by two new AHUs when the alterations were implemented. More­
over, it was assumed that all ducts were maintained, as well as all inlets, outlets, and
cooking hoods. Additionally, it was assumed that both of the new AHUs would be able
to operate with either the estimated continuous flow for the original conditions or the flow
which complies with either of the sensor controls. The existing AHUs were assumed only
to be able to handle the continuous flow. Both of the new AHUs were defined to have a
heating coil. Furthermore, the rotary exchanger was assumed to be able to operate with
no problems when the same AHU was used for rooms with different functions. Finally, it
was assumed that the sensors were only applied in rooms with inlets and not in rooms
with an outlet.

4.2.5
Simulation
With respect to the defined scenarios and their respective changes, the different model
alterations were implemented. The simulations were then run with the use of the 2001­
2010 Danish design reference year weather file and the results were extracted from the
software. All hours for rooms where: the operative temperature was above 27◦C as well
as above 28◦C, the operative temperature was below 20◦C, the RH was less than 25%,
the RH was more than 60%, and the CO2 concentration was greater than 900 ppm were
collected. These results were collected for all the bathrooms, bedrooms, living rooms,
hallways, common areas, common kitchens, kitchens, staff rooms, and depot/storage. In
terms of energy consumption, the total energy used for heating, the total energy used for
heating coils, and the total ventilation rate for each of the rooms over the course of the
year were collected. These results were then used to calculate the total energy consump­
tion of the building, which will be covered in Section 4.5.

4.4
Environmental sustainability simulations
The GWP was chosen as the environmental sustainability measure, and LCA was used
to address the GWP using the software LCA­byg. As this study intends to investigate how
changes in the different renovation parameters affect the measures, it was defined as a
study of renovation design. As a result, the new building materials, the energy consump­
tion regarding the use of the building, and the disposal of removed building materials were
implemented in the LCA.
Furthermore, as this study intends to holistically investigate which renovation parameters
are important in relation to the different measurement areas. The goal of the building LCA
was thereby to compare the impact on GWP for the different renovation solutions (sce­
narios). The functional unit used in this study was thereby defined as 1 ventilated building
envelope providing shelter and housing for approximately 100 residents for 50 years, with
a maximum impact on GWP of 12 kg CO2 ­ eq per m2 per year.

6
Discussion
This study has examined how influential and optimal renovation parameters can be as­
sessed in relation to the indoor environment, energy use, and environmental sustainability
holistically. It was discovered that addressing an optimal renovation parameter in relation
to all three measurement areas was difficult. However, it appears that the window pane
constellation was the most holistically influential renovation parameter, and the 3­pane
window constellation was the optimal solution for this parameter.
In terms of sensitivity, the Pearson Correlation Coefficient was used to determine if the
various parameters had an effect on the measures and whether sensitivity could be de­
tected. This study discovered that some parameters indicated correlation even though no
changes to the parameter were made in the simulations. For instance, the type of window
frame was found to correlate with the daylight measure, although, no changes regarding
the frames were implemented in the daylight simulations. As a consequence, the amount
of daylight in the building should not be affected by the change of window frames. How­
ever, the glazing properties were changed simultaneously with the frame parameters, and
could potentially cause the correlation seen for the frames. As a result, the measure of
daylight in relation to the parameter changes of the window frames was not found to be
sensitive. Hence, it was discovered that the correlation alone was insufficient to identify
sensitivity, and it was necessary to ensure that a connection was found as well. A con­
nection was discovered when parameter changes affect some conditions in the simulation
model, which may affect the value of the specific measures. Adding this additional factor
to the methodology requires a thorough understanding of the parameters and their effects
on the various measures. However, this addition strengthened the robustness of the find­
ings in this study. Furthermore, it could be discussed whether the dependency between
the parameters, causes the correlations to occur when no connections can be detected.
The PCC indicated a correlation for some parameters, where a connection was found as
well. For instance regarding the window pane constellation, where it was seen that for
the energy consumption, the GWP, the daylight, and the overheating hours above 27◦C
and 28◦C a relatively strong inverse correlation was found. As an inverse correlation
was found, the parameter value increased as the area­weighted or specific values for
the measures decreased. As the window pane constellation progressed from the original
conditions to the energy 2­pane constellation and then the 3­pane constellation, an in­
crease in the parameter resulted in a lower g­value, lower light transmittance, and lower
U­value. Thereby an increase in the parameter resulted in less solar heat coming through
the window, less daylight coming through the window, and better insulation of the building
envelope. As a result of these consequences, the parameter changes in relation to the
window pane constellation can be connected to the measures, as less daylight through the
window results in less daylight in the building, better insulation of the building envelope
would decrease the energy consumption, and less solar heat gain through the window
would decrease the amount of overheating. For the GWP, a connection can be difficult
to assess as multiple elements could affect this impact. However, the lower energy con­
sumption could with a high probability result in a lower GWP, as the energy consumption
Holistic assessment of the indoor environment, energy use and
environmental sustainability in building renovation

==PAGE:51==
would decrease and thereby reduce the need for fossil fuels. As a result, the parameter
connection to energy consumption was found to be indirect rather than direct.
By combining correlation and connection as sensitivity indicators, it was discovered that
daylight and the minimum temperature were the most sensitive measures to parameter
changes in general. Furthermore, it was found that the window pane constellation, the
interior insulation thickness, the AHU, and the sensor control were all influential param­
eters. As these parameters had a connection and a strong correlation with one or more
of the following measures: daylight, GWP, energy consumption, overheating above 27◦C
and 28◦C, and the cold temperatures of 20◦C or less. Furthermore, it could be discussed
whether the CO2 concentration was sensitive to changes in the AHU and sensor control.
This measure seems to correlate with the parameter changes, and the PCC seems to
converge aside from the final 4 scenarios included. For these final 4 scenarios, a greater
change in the correlation occurs, which makes the results unreliable. Consequently, the
sensitivity of this measure in relation to these parameters was not further addressed. The
window pane constellation was found to be the most influential parameter overall, as it
indicated a connection and strong correlation with more than one of the measures. Fur­
thermore, the parameter correlated with measures across all measurement areas. The
rest of the measures in relation to the rest of the parameters were not found to be sensitive
either because no connection was found, because the final PCC did not indicate a strong
correlation, or because the PCC did not converge.
The PCC was tracked over time to determine when it converged. Convergence appeared
to occur prior to the simulated 75 scenarios for some of the parameters in relation to
some of the measures, while convergence did not appear to occur within the simulated 75
scenarios for other measures. For most of the cases where the convergence did not occur
the PCC did still seem to head in a specific direction. However, no conclusions should be
drawn for the parameters and measures that did not achieve convergence because the
PCC was not yet stable. Thus, before the correlation and the sensitivity can be addressed
with certainty all the PCCs over time should converge among all the parameters and all the
measures. Despite the fact that PCC did not converge for all measures and parameters,
the approach of using PCC to determine the impact of parameters and the sensitivity of
the measures appears to be useful.
It was also seen that not all the possible solutions to the different parameters were repre­
sented in the 75 simulated scenarios. Here it was found that the 400 mm rock wool attic
post­insulation was not represented in any of the tested 75 scenarios. As a result, it was
possible to discuss whether the study was statistically valid, given that not all parameter
solutions were represented, and others were found to be represented only once. For the
parameters not represented at all, no conclusions regarding these specific parameters
could be made, as no indication of the parameter’s impacts was accounted for. For the
parameters only represented once, no conclusions should be conducted as well. How­
ever, the parameter solution of 150 mm rock wool as exterior post­insulation only occurs
once. Though, both the 100mm rock wool and 200 mm rock wool exterior post­insulation
were represented multiple times. As a result, this parameter solution was embraced, and
conclusions related to this parameter could potentially still be conducted. On the other
hand, the 400 mm wood fiber attic post­insulation constitutes the outer span of the possi­
Holistic assessment of the indoor environment, energy use and
environmental sustainability in building renovation

==PAGE:52==
ble solutions. As a result, this solution was not embraced and conclusions regarding this
solution should not be conducted.
To summarize, the study’s robustness and validity could be improved. This could be
accomplished by simulating more scenarios and ensuring that all parameter solutions
were represented at least twice. Furthermore, it may be advantageous to ensure that the
parameters were independent and not dependent on other parameter solutions.
Two approaches were taken in the search for the optimal solution. Where the analytical
approach appears to provide a good indication of how the results appear and how much of
a difference the parameters have on the measures. With this approach, the optimum was
the solution that was best for some measures, but the worst for others. From the TOPSIS
methodology, scenario 33 was found to be the optimal solution when using the equal
weighting of the different measurement areas. Though, scenario 34 was found to be the
optimal solution when using equal weighting among the different measures. However,
these solutions would not be optimal if a better solution appears among the remaining
464,298 possible solutions. Hence, these optimal scenarios were only the optimal among
the 75 simulated scenarios. To be able to estimate the most optimal scenario from the
TOPSIS methodology all possible scenarios should have been assessed.
However, the methodology appears to be more appropriate for the optimal solution in
terms of the influential parameter. The ten scenarios with the highest performance score
for both weighting approaches evaluated in this study all include the same parameter so­
lution, the 3­pane constellation. These results could potentially differ if a larger amount
of the possible 464,737 solutions were included. Though, from the representativeness
table, all three parameter solutions have been represented a minimum of 18 times. As a
result, even though multiple other scenarios could still be run with multiple other combined
solutions, the 3­pane constellation may still be the best solution in terms of the parame­
ter window pane constellation. Furthermore, this study only addresses two approaches
to weighting the measures in relation to TOPSIS. Another approach accounting for the
two overheating measures as one could have been evaluated as well, and different out­
comes could have occurred. Moreover, both approaches only accounted for the sensitive
measures related to the parameter. Had all the measures been included in the search
for the optimal solution would another result potentially occur. Though the sensitivity ap­
proach did indicate that these measures were not of importance, and as a result, these
should not affect the optimal solution.

were run. These simulations served as the study’s foundation, however, they include
some uncertainties. For example, the electrical energy used on the ventilators was calcu­

Conclusion
This study has holistically assessed building renovation in relation to the measurement
areas of environmental sustainability, indoor environment, and energy use. The most
influential parameters have been assessed as well as their optimal solution.
From the study, it was discovered that multiple parameters indicated a correlation with
some of the measures. The strongest correlation among the parameters was mostly
found in relation to the daylight and the cold temperatures of 20◦C or less. However, it
was discovered that some parameters correlated with the specific measures even though
no connection could be detected. As a result, these measures were not seen as sensitive
in relation to the investigated parameters. Hence, it was discovered that only the window
pane constellation, the interior post­insulation thickness, AHU, and sensor control could
be defined as influential parameters.
The window pane constellation was found to be the most influential parameter regarding
the holistic assessment of the indoor environment, energy use, and environmental sus­
tainability. The GWP, daylight, energy consumption, and overheating hours above both
27◦C and 28◦C were all found to be sensitive to changes in the window pane constel­
lation. Consequently, the parameter was found to be influential in relation to all three
measurement areas.
Furthermore, it could be concluded that the 3­pane constellation was the optimal solution
for this parameter, among the different constellations in relation to the tested scenarios.
However, it could not be determined if this solution was the optimum if the rest of the
464,298 scenarios was accounted for as well. In addition, no conclusion could be drawn
regarding the most optimal scenario using the approaches implemented in this study.
From the Pearson Correlations Coefficient, it was found that not all the measures in rela­
tion to all the different parameters converged. Moreover, it was seen from the representa­
tion of the parameters for the different scenarios, that not all different parameter solutions
were represented in this study. As a result, the study cannot be addressed as statisti­
cally valid. To ensure the statistical validity of the study additional simulations should be
conducted.
However, based on the findings of this study, it is possible to conclude that the approach of
addressing the sensitivity of various parameter changes through the Pearson Correlation
Coefficient can be used. Thus, it is important to account for the connection between the
parameters and the measures, as the parameters and measures need to be connected
as well as correlated to indicate sensitivity.
