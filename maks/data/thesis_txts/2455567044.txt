Summary (English)
The thesis deal with the development of mathematical models for predicting
the heat dynamics in a room where the main heating source comes from district
heating. The purpose of the development of the models is for it to be used in
a Model Predictive Control such that the the heat dynamic of the room can be
controlled.
To help in the development of the models data were collected from Borgerskolen,
that is school building. Each room in the school building has its data collected
and the data was used in the model building.
Based on previous work and
the collected data, the developed models are structured as non-linear grey-box
models.
The models are then developed and a model is chosen to be used in the model
predictive control. To chose which of the models are the best at predicting the
heat dynamics, the model performance and predicting are compared. After the
comparison the model that is chosen is the TiThTe model.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In recent times there has been an increase in the importance of collecting data,
in particular the data relating to consumption of resources such as energy, water
and heat. This utility data is important because by understanding the data it
can reduce the energy consumption and thereby reduce the cost of the resources.
The reduced energy consumption results in a decrease dependents on fossil fuels
and a increase in ﬂexibility. The increase in ﬂexibility means that the use of
renewable energy can more easily be integrated.
One of the ways to collect the utility data is by using smart meters that are
placed in houses and that collect the relevant data. This collected data is then
sent to the utility companies to help them develop new services for the consumers
and help the consumers to reduce their energy consumption.
One of the services that the utility companies can develop is a system to control
the heat dynamics of a consumers residence. The control system will then use
thermostatic set-points that the consumers can set. The system will then control
the heating system will temperature follws the set-points such that the energy
consumption is reduced.
Research into this kind of control, in particular Model Predictive Control(MPC)
has been done for rooms and building that uses electrical heating elements. And
the relationship between the energy consumed by the heaters and the heat that
is generated is known. But in the case where the heating in the residence is not
provided by electrical heating a new model has to be made.
==PAGE:14==
2
Introduction
One of the other forms of heating that is not electrical heating is district heat-
ing. In Denmark 63% of all houses uses this kind of heating and it works by
heating a medium up which in most cases is water and then distributing it out
into consumers residence. After the heated medium has entered the consumers
residence it is further distributed into the appropriate heating elements. These
heating elements would then radiate the heat into the residence and the cooled
medium is returned to the heating facility.
As the eﬀect of the district heating is not known there will be a problem to
control the heating system of the residence using MPC. For MPC to control the
system it requires a good model. A model that models the heat dynamic of the
residence that uses district heating should be developed. As a good model will
make it possible for the MPC to lower the energy consumption, by optimize the
amount of renewable energy that is used. The cost is reduced by controlling
the return temperature of the district heating. If the temperature is not higher
that a certain temperature a cost is added to the consumer.
Such a model is developed on data that was collected from a danish school called
Borgerskolen. In the case of Borgerskolen the medium that the district heating
uses is water and the heating elements are radiators.
The data that was collected was in a period where people are not present in the
building as this could aﬀect the measurements of the data. The period spans 10
days and each room is measured separately, as such the model will be developed
on a single room.
As the model for the heat dynamics is intended to be used to control the heating
system, it should be able to produce reasonable simulations and predictions of
the temperature inside a room.

Chapter 2
Data
To develop the models that describe the heat dynamics of a given room, obser-
vations of the relevant variables are needed. The variables that are collected are
from inside the room and outside the room
The variables that are collected outside the room are variables that aﬀect the
heat dynamic of the room in this case it would be weather data such as the
ambient temperature, wind speed, solar radiation or wind direction. The data
are collected from the area around where the geographical location that the
relevant room is located. This data can be accessed from a third party source
such as The Danish Meteorological Institute(DMI). DMI collects that data for
use as research and for predicting the weather in Denmark.
As for the variables that are collected from inside the room they also aﬀect
the heat dynamisc of the room. The room is chosen based on how complete
the dataset is, meaning the dataset of the room has to have the least amount
of missing observations. This is because when the observations are collected
there will always be missing data and to handle the missing data, techniques
that augment the dataset are required. And if there is a lot of missing data
the augmentation can create a correlation in the dataset that does not exist
in the physical world. This correlation can then make the model development
increasingly harder as the models that result from the model development try
to capture the correlation that is created. This causes the resulting models to
have inaccurate simulation and prediction of the heat dynamics.[10]

Chapter 3
Theory
In this chapter a procedure for identifying models is presented. The procedure
has three main parts model structure, parameter estimation and model selection.
The model structure part of the procedure involves ﬁnding the model structure
and the order of the model.
This is archived using observations and theory
that is relevant to the process that is modelled. In this case the process that
is modelled is the heat dynamics of a room as such the theory and physical
insight that is needed should be about thermodynamics.
This knowledge of
thermodynamics helps in determining the relevant terms that aﬀect the heat
dynamics.
The next part of the model building procedure is the parameter estimation. It
involves ﬁnding the value of the unknown variables that is needed to model the
heat dynamics. Here the knowledge of thermodynamics helps in evaluating if
the estimated parameters are reasonable.
The last part of the procedure is the model selection. The purpose of this part
is to select the model that can best describes the desired process. This is done
by checking if the model can describe given observation and then evaluating the
model on observations that it has not seen before. If the model has adequate
performance in both the checking and evaluation then it is chosen as the ﬁnal
model if not then the model building process is returned to the model structure
part.[3]
Each of the individual parts of the model building procedure is described in
more details of how the parts are accomplished.

3.1
Identiﬁcation
The identiﬁcation of the model structure and order are found using a combi-
nation of the collected data, the information embedded in the data and prior
physical knowledge. And the models that are developed using this combination
are called grey-box models. These grey-box models are used as a base for the
structure of the models since they use all of the information that is available.
With the base of the model structure found a ﬁnal model structure should be
chosen. The structure that is chosen is where the model is the most general as
this makes the models more adaptable.
The case where grey-box models are the most general is when they are non-
linear. This model structure has terms that based on theoretical knowledge and
terms that are unknown but derived from data. In equation (3.1-3.2) the struc-
ture of a non-linear grey-box model is shown and following that the individual
model terms are described.[11]
dxt = f(xt, ut, t, θ)dt + σ(ut, t, θ)dωt
(3.1)
yk = h(xk, uk, tk, θ) + ek
(3.2)
where t ∈R is time, xt ∈X ⊂Rn is a vector of state variables, ut ∈U ⊂Rm
is a vector of input variables, yk ∈Y ⊂Rl is a vector of output variables,
θ ∈Θ ⊂Rp is a vector of parameters, f(·) ∈Rn, σ(·) ∈Rn×n and h(·) ∈Rl
are non-linear functions, {ωt} is an n-dimensional standard Wiener process and
{ek} is an l-dimensional white noise process with ek ∈N (0, S(uk, tk, θ)).
This gives a ﬁnal structure to the models that are found and to complete the
identiﬁcation part of the model building a model order have to be chosen. This
is accomplished by starting with a simple model and then extending the model
with new states and terms. To determine if the models should be the extended
or be chosen as the ﬁnal model it is checked and evaluated as described in section
3.3. In the following section the simple model and extensions to the model are
found for the case study that involves Borgerskolen.

With the model structure and order determine the next step in ﬁnding a model

the parameters that are estimated have a unit that might not make sense in the
physical world as such there is a need to verify that the units of all the variables
in the model can be multiplied such that the output to the model is the correct
unit.

3.3
Model selection, checking and evaluation
With the general model structures found and parameters estimated a selection
process to see which models and which states should be included in the ﬁnal
model that is used to model the heat dynamics in the building. After a model
has been selected the model is checked to see if the observation is adequately
described by the selected model. If the model does not describe the observation
then a new model is selected if it does then the model is tested on data that
the model have not been trained on. This is called cross validation and if the
model does not ﬁt the new data reasonably well, it indicates that the model
can be improved further.
If the model does ﬁt then the model is evaluated
this is done by looking at the estimated parameters to see if the parameters
==PAGE:36==
24
Theory
make physical sense. The predictions from the ﬁtting of the new data and plots
from the inputs, outputs and residuals is also looked at to determine if parts
of the model should be further explained. If the evaluation of the model is not
satisfactory then a new model is selected if it is satisﬁed then the model can be
used to model the heating in the building.
Since the evaluation step of ﬁnding the model is determine by a modeller there
can be bias thus every model that is evaluated is compared to each other to
remove the bias.
3.3.1
Model selection
For the model selection a process that involves starting with a small model and
adding terms that gives most signiﬁcant improvement to that model until it
satisfy some stop criteria. This procedure is similar to forward selection and
to ﬁnd the term that improves the model the probability values (p-values) are
calculated the term that gives the lowest p-value is selected as an extension to
the model. This way of adding terms to the model continues until the extension
to the model does not yield p-values below a deﬁned limit. This limit is usually
deﬁned to be 5 % and this limit is used as the stopping criteria for the model
selection.[14]

be assumed to be white noise. This means that if the residuals fails one test

Chapter 5
Discussion
The model building procedure results in the TiThTe model as the model that
can be used by the MPC. This is because the model has the best predictions of
the models that were developed.
In using the TiThTe model it also model the temperature of all the radiators in
the chosen room.
Figure 4.11 shows the resulting temperature of the radiators when the interior
temperature is modelled. It is seen that when the temperature of the room is
raised by a couple of degrees the temperature of the radiators is raised to a
higher temperature. The interior temperature is changed in the same way as
how the temperature set-points are changed. In the start of the ﬁgure it is seen
that the temperature of the radiators are a lot higher than the set-point this is
caused by the compensation that occurs because of the low ambient temperature
and the rapid change in set-points.
But it is seen in table 4.9 that the model have lower RMSE for the test dataset
than the training dataset. This might be caused by the fact that the training
dataset is in the period where test were performed meanwhile the test dataset
is not in the period. If that is the case then the model is able to handle more
rapid change than what happens in the test dataset. And therefore is the model
better at handling the slow changes that the test dataset has.
To verify this a new dataset where there is rapid changes like the training dataset
==PAGE:60==

should be used. The TiThTe model should then have one-step predictions of
the new dataset and if it still have similar performance to the ﬁt then it can
still be used. If it does not have the same performance then a improved model
should be found.
The model is chosen despite the fact that the model building criteria is not sat-
isﬁed, the reason it is chosen is that the MPC need a model that can predict the
heat dynamic with a reasonable degree of accuracy. This is what the TITHTe
model prediction has, but it does not mean that the model can not be improved.
By suing more of the data and having more observation a improved model can
be found.
One such improvement could be to use the measurement of the interior temper-
ature that the adjacent room has. This could be a new state or a replacement
to the room envelop state as the states resemble each other. The resemblances
comes from the fact that both the states uses the heat that is generated outside
the room and explains the eﬀect it has on the room. The current state does this
by modelling the temperature change that the heat stored in the room envelope
has. And the new state would do this by taking the temperature of the adjacent
elements and then model the change it would have on the interior temperature.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The aim of the thesis was to develop a model that could be used to control
the heat dynamic in a room that used district heating as a heating source.
The model was developed such that it could later be used for MPC as this is
the intended control scheme. For the MPC to work optimally it need a model
that can give accurate predictions. So the model building procedure uses the
prediction to select which of the develop models should be chosen.
To develop such a model, observations of the heat dynamic of a room that
uses district heating were needed. Such observations were acquired with help
from Borgerskolen and the device Danfoss Link. In addition the thermostatic
set-point that a user can change is changed rapidly to help the model be more
robust. This then made the evaluation of the model predictions hard to interpret
as the observations that was not in the period did not have rapid changes in the
thermostatic set-points. This then made the prediction of the chosen model,
the TiThTe model have a lower error compared to the ﬁtting of the training
dataset.
In choosing the model a better model selection criteria could have been used,
since the model are nested model a selection criteria that uses this to its advan-
tage could have give a better indication if the model could be improved upon.
As for improvement to the chosen model, the third state, Te could be replace
with a state that involves the use of data collected from the room that are ad-

on the heat dynamics in the room.
To then further improve on the research that was done in the thesis, more data of

to have an improved model that satisﬁes the model building stopping criteria.
The model that has been developed in the thesis can still be used to control

heat dynamic still has a reasonable degree of accuracy despite the fact that the
TiThTe model does not have p-values that are above 0.05.
