PREFACE
This thesis is written for the Technical University of Denmark as a part of completing the
M.Sc programme in Electrical Engineering. The content is mainly based on the technical
knowledge gained during the four semesters of the programme, but has also been inﬂuenced
by my background as a Bachelor of Science in Mechatronics Engineering. The project
reﬂects upon my interest in power electronics and moreover it shows a great interest in
designing power electronics for commercial use. More speciﬁcally the project includes the
design of a volatile AC-DC converter to charge a set of various devices.
The main motivation for this particular focus, came from investigating existing solutions
and discovering their rather bulky or pricey nature. With these given solutions, I knew
that considerable optimization was possible, which could turn out as a great business case
and that drove me to invest further in the project.
I would like to express my gratitude to my supervisors Ziwei Ouyang and Mingxiao Li
for their guidance and cooperation throughout the project. Also I would like to thank
Wenbo Wang for a great collaboration before and during the project. Moreover I would
to express a great appreciation for the people working in close proximity with me in the
student laboratory for our great discussions and for keeping me motivated.

ABSTRACT
This project includes the design of an AC-DC 60 W converter, operating with an input
mains voltage of 90 V AC - 264 V AC. The converter is designed to be compatible with the
USB-C PD standard, which allows for charging of mobile phones, laptops etc. The project
contains a thorough investigation of commercially available 60 W USB-C PD chargers and
the high-end state of art, which have not been marketed.
The converter is designed around the UCC28780 controller from TI, which incorporates
adaptive deadtime control and ensures Zero Voltage Switching for the Active Clamp
Flyback topology.
The project is divided into diﬀerent design phases such as size
optimization, MOSFET selection, transformer design, controller design and EMI ﬁlter
design. Furthermore an integrated magnetic solution for the common mode choke have
been proposed to increase the leakage inductance and reduce the overall size of the EMI
ﬁlter.
The converter is predicted to be operating with an eﬃciency of 91.46 % and 90.98 % for
an input voltage of 90 V AC and 264 V AC respectively, while the measured eﬃciency is
92.2 % and 90.2 %. Moreover the power density achieved is larger than 25
W
inch3 and the
peak eﬃciency is 93 %, which is signiﬁcantly better than commercially available chargers.
Overall the designed converter have been shown to accommodate most of the requirements
though it still needs certain improvements.

Project Background

billion US$ and is expected to reach 407 billion in 2021 [23]. In order to accommodate
the growing demand on fast charging of the Portable Electronic Devices(PED), USB-C
Power Delivery(PD) was introduced, which provides a charging power capability up to
100 W. USB-C PD includes a set of charging proﬁles(5V, 9V, 15V and 20V) to meet the
speciﬁc voltage requirements of a wide variety of phones, tablets, laptops etc. Furthermore
the PD standard speciﬁes a speciﬁc protocol for communication between the charger and
the PED to negotiate the charging proﬁles. Once a successful charging proﬁle(a speciﬁc
voltage and maximum current) has been agreed upon, the charger will provide power [11].
This protocol oﬀers the capability of a single charger to provide power for a vast variety of
PED.
Many diﬀerent companies have developed AC-DC converters to charge PED with PD
capability for a various of power ratings, though this project tends to focus on the max-
imum power rating of 60 W, which provides enough power to charge many devices but
excludes the need for a Power Factor Correction(PFC) stage [21]. Figure 1.1 illustrates the
comparison among diﬀerent commercially available 60 W USB-C PD compatible chargers.
Clearly the comparison illustrates that the chargers are either relatively bulky and/or
costly and hence allows for the possibility of an comparatively improved charger to occupy

1. Introduction
1.3
Problem formulation
The project focuses on designing a 60 W AC-DC USB-C PD converter with greater power
density compared to commercially available chargers. This is to be achieved without the use
of the relatively expensive Gallium Nitride(GaN) and Silicon Carbide(SiC) semiconductors,
in order to further improve the commerciality of the converter. The main objective of the
project is therefore to improve upon the existing solutions in regards to the power density,
but without compromising eﬃciency and cost.
1.4
Requirements
• Be operational for an input voltage range of 90 V AC - 264 V AC
• Be operational for an input line frequency range of 47 Hz - 63 Hz
• Provide a stable output voltage of 5 V, 9 V, 15 V and 20 V
• Provide a maximum power of 60 W
• Provide a maximum current of 3 A
• Achieve a power density larger than 25
W
inch3 (without casing)
• Keep converter eﬃciency above 90 % during full load
• Keep hotspots below 100 °C
• Comply with the EMC standard EN 55032 Class B
• Be fully compatible with USB-C PD.
• Output over voltage protection
• Output over current protection
1.5
Delimitation
The delimitation list below refers to the limitations regarding the use of speciﬁc components
and relates to the problem formulation in the way of achieving a design, which are more
likely to be recognized for commercial use in the current market.
• No use of GaN or SiC MOSFET devices
• No use of planar transformer

2
CONVERTER DESIGN
2.1
Topology
For low power AC-DC converters, the traditional ﬂyback and Quasi resonant ﬂyback
converter have been widely adopted due to its simplicity and low cost. The drawback of
both typologies are the limitation on the switching frequency due to that the semiconductor
switching losses and snubber losses increases with switching frequency.
Most of the
converters are limited to a switching frequency below 100 kHz, which ineﬀectively sets a
limit on the size of the transformer and EMI ﬁlter. Moreover the voltage ringing and high
dV
dt nature of the switch note, increases the noise generated by the converter and further
increases the required size of the EMI ﬁlter. The ACF oﬀers several signiﬁcant advantages
as stated below [6].
• Recycles the energy stored in the leakage inductance of the transformer
• Achieves ZVS on the primary switches by the use of the energy stored in the
magnetizing and leakage inductance
• Less noise generated from the switch note due to less ringing and lower dV
dt
These advantages signiﬁcantly reduces the dominant switching losses and the topology
therefore oﬀers the possibility to push the switching frequency much higher and at the
same time achieve higher eﬃciency. Therefore in order to satisfy the given eﬃciency and
power density requirements, the ACF topology will be used for the converter design.
A simpliﬁed block diagram of an AC-DC ACF converter is shown in APPENDIX A, which
indicates the content of what is to be designed and addressed in this project. NOTE it is
highly recommended to look at the block diagram before continuing reading.
2.2
Principle of operation
In this section the working principle of the ACF power stage is analyzed. It should be
noted that the operation is investigated in the Discontinuous Conduction Mode(DCM).
An ACF operating in the Continuous Conduction Mode(CCM) with high line voltage and
relatively low output power, requires large leakage inductance to achieve ZVS. This limits
the eﬀective duty cycle and introduces a low frequency and large voltage ringing on the
secondary side. Therefore in these types of wide ranging output voltages, high line voltage
and low output power applications, ACF operating in CCM is less suitable. Furthermore
the secondary MOSFET QS is substituted with a diode DO for simplicity in the analysis.
Region 1: QL is on and the magnetizing current IM increases, while the output capacitor
CO supplies the required load current.

2.3
Main controller IC
Being familiar with the operation principles it then becomes necessary to ﬁnd a proper
controller, which can support the required topology and meet the speciﬁcation requirements.
Both TI and ON Semiconductor have made ASICs for low power ACF AC-DC converters
that supports a switching frequency of up to 1 MHz. The NCP1568 ACF controller from
ON Semiconductor is more limited in maximum duty cycle at larger frequencies, is more
pricey and oﬀers a less descriptive datasheet compared to UCC28780 ACF controller from
TI, and thus the TI controller was used as most suitable for the given application [8][9].
The UCC28780 controller operates in 3 modes dependent on the output power.
Adaptive Amplitude Modulation(AAM): The controller regulates the peak amplitude of
low side switch current, to keep the negative magnetizing current constant and ensures
ZVS under various loads. Hence the switching frequency increases as the load decreases.
Figure 2.9: AAM principle
Adaptive Burst Mode(ABM): As the load decreases to some preset value, the controller
enters burst mode wherein the switching frequency is kept constant for a given input
voltage. The output is now regulated by the number of burst and the burst mode frequency.
Low Power Mode(LPM) and Standby Power Mode(SPM): The number of bursts within
the burst period is reduced to 2 and the controller turns of the high side PWM signal. The
burst oﬀtime is used to regulate the output voltage.

6: Evaluation The design should be evaluated depending on the ratio between the
winding and core loss which averagely should be close to 1. That is during high line the
core loss should be dominant and during low line, the winding loss should be dominant. If
however the design yields signiﬁcant deviation from the above, a few step back must be
taken depending on the speciﬁc scenario. If the winding loss are too dominant, n can be
decreased and checked whether the core loss is still acceptable. If not, the core size must
be increased. However if the core loss is too dominant, NS should be increased. If the
winding loss now becomes dominant n can be decreased until the winding and core loss is
nicely balanced.
The above calculations indicates an overall dominant core loss, though in practise the
winding loss will be larger due to the fringing eﬀect and the proximity eﬀect of each turn
within each layer. Dowell’s equation approximates 1 layer of winding as a single rectangular
conductor and does therefore only include a simpliﬁed analysis of the proximity eﬀect
between each layer. Furthermore there will be an internal proximity eﬀect within the litz
wire bundle which are also not included in the analysis.

2.8
EMI ﬁlter design
In order for switching converters not to expose other electronic grid connected equipment
to excessive noise, strict requirements are speciﬁed on the amount of noise which are
allowed to be supplied into the grid. Hence usually an input ﬁlter becomes necessary to
ﬁlter out the ripple based nature of the current in a switching converter. The noise can be
separated into two distinct categories called Common Mode(CM) noise and Diﬀerential
Mode(DM) noise and their noise path is illustrated on ﬁgure 2.27 and 2.26 respectively [4].
CM noise travels along both line wires with the same direction, while DM noise travels on
opposite direction. DM noise is generated by the nature of the input ripple, while CM
noise is generated by large dV
dt notes. The Line Impedance Stabilization Network(LISN)
illustrated is used to construct a constant impedance of 50 Ωin the frequency span of 150
kHz - 30 MHz, which is then used to measure the current noise by an EMI Test Receiver.

EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
In this chapter the measured performance of the converter will be presented and discussed.
The full load eﬃciency for various loads and input voltage can be seen on ﬁgure 5.1 and
5.2. The converter operates at a peak eﬃciency of 93 % with an input voltage of 120 V AC.
In comparison with the performance analysis, the predicted eﬃciency at VIN = 90 V AC
are about 1% lower than the measured and 0.5 % larger with VIN = 264 V AC. Several
factors can be inﬂuential on the diﬀerence between the predicted and measured eﬃciency.
Firstly, the dissipation factor of the aluminum electrolytic capacitors generally decreases
with temperature and therefore equivalently has lower ESR at higher temperatures [22].
This information were not given in the datasheet and could therefore not be included.
Furthermore the turn oﬀloss are a rough evaluation and are typically overestimated. It
assumes that the crossover region between the current and voltage waveform are purely
due to the current ﬂowing in the resistive part of the MOSFET. Though this is not true,
as the current will also be charging the output capacitor of the MOSFET in a rather non
dissipate manner [18]. The transformer core loss was approximated by the assumption
of sinusoidal ﬂux excitation, which introduces a rather small error as long as the duty
cycle does not approach small and large values. Lastly there are component tolerances,
simulation inaccuracies and small losses associated with passive and IC components, which
were not included in the prediction calculations.

FUTURE PROBLEM IDENTIFICATION
This chapter will present the issues which require further investigation in order for the
converter to be in complete compliance with the requirements. Also ideas for further
improvements which might be addressed in future, will be presented.
Conductive EMI Compliance
As to comply with EN 55032 Class B the CMC needs further improvement. This can be
achieved by slightly increasing the core size such the ﬂux density is reduced. The design
needs to accommodate the asymmetrical magnetizing, such that the ﬂux density does not
exhibit signiﬁcant permeability drop or saturation. Thus the CMC should be manufactured
such that the air gap in the center leg is well controlled and thereby utilizes the maximum
leakage inductance of the design.
Eﬀective shielding solutions can also be implemented to reduce the capacative coupling
to chassic GND and thereby reducing the CM noise. Transformer shielding solutions are
presented in [4].
USB-C PD controller
The USB-C PD source controller needs to tested with a USB-C PD compliant sink device
and herein each voltage speciﬁed in requirement must be tested. Furthermore the source
controller must be programmed to set the maximum advertising current to 3 A.
Further improvements
The bootstrap should either be of a larger package or chosen as SiC diode, to reduce the
hotspot of the highest temperature.
The converter also needs to be tested with an over current condition to ensure that the
OCP is working properly.
Lastly a dedicated heatsink for the MOSETs should be manufactured. A thin plate of
aluminum or copper should be mounted on the MOSFETs and preferably connected to
GND.

CONCLUSION
In this project a complete AC-DC 60 W USB-C PD converter prototype have been
designed and shown improvements compared to commercially available chargers in respect
to power density and eﬃciency. Herein achieving a power density of 25.12
W
inch3 and a peak
eﬃciency of 93 %. This have been achieved without use of expensive GaN and SiC devices.
Furthermore the eﬀective converter dimensions for the prototype are given as 46 mm x 47
mm x 18.1 mm.
The converter topology in use is the ACF operating in DCM with a variable frequency to
ensure ZVS over the whole input voltage range. Moreover the converter achieves burst
mode operation during light load to improve upon the light load eﬃciency. The prototype
utilizes the UCC28780 controller from TI, which incorporates adaptive deadtime control
and includes many other desired features, which makes it suitable for commercial use.
The prototype have shown satisfactory results in regards to converter stability in which
a fast transient response have been achieved and therefore keeps the output voltage well
regulated. The transformer and MOSFET selection have been optimized to yield minimum
power loss and is shown to operate well below 100 °C.
Though the prototype are yet to be fully compliant with the EMC standard EN 55032
Class B, where a maximum attenuation of 15 dB is missing to pass the test. It have been
shown that the designed CMC does not operate as fully intended but a proposed solution
to the problem has been included. Lastly the USB-C PD source controller is still to be
tested with a USB-C PD sink device but is supposedly not an issue due to the availability
of other products with similar controllers.
