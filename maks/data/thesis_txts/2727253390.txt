Abstract
Current methods for microbial drinking water monitoring are based on grab sampling and
samples are not required to be collected with high frequency. The applied microbial anal-
yses have very long response times due to necessary incubation. Danish water suppliers
have expressed the ambition of being more in control of the water quality delivered to the
consumers. In recent years there has been an increased interest in investigating potential
microbial sensor technologies for application in drinking water distribution networks as a
tool for improving drinking water quality monitoring.
This thesis project investigates the potential of on-line sensor technologies for real-time
monitoring of microbial drinking water in distribution networks by developing a sensor
evaluation protocol, and by investigating the applicability of a biofilm sensor in both ex-
perimental laboratory setups and in the field. An evaluation protocol was developed to
achieve a systematic evaluation of sensor technologies, allowing for the comparison of
different technologies before application in a given context. The protocol could be applied
to sensors investigated for use in drinking water distribution networks, while it might be a
source of inspiration for the performance evaluation of sensors applied in other contexts.
The ALVIM biofilm sensor was deployed in two different experimental setups in the lab-
oratory: a flow-through setup and a looped flow setup. Additionally, two biofilm sensors
were installed by Aarhus Vand A/S in the drinking water distribution network in Aarhus.
The investigations demonstrated that the sensor is capable of detecting the initial forma-
tion of biofilm on its sensitive surface. A signal increase indicating biofilm formation above
the threshold value was observed after 2.5 and 4.5 days in the looped flow setup and the
flow-through setup, respectively, and indications of biofilm at the two field locations were
observed after 9 and 10.5 days. It was concluded that the sensor is likely sensitive to
the composition of the water in which it is deployed. Time series analyses indicated that
fluctuations observed in the data series in the field and in the flow-through setup may be
explained as daily variations most likely caused by external factors affecting the condi-
tions within the distribution network. After the initial biofilm indication, it was not possible
to induce an increase in sensor response by the addition of large volumes of surface water
to the looped flow setup. The sensor is therefore incapable of detecting changes in mi-
crobial water quality after the initial formation of biofilm. The ALVIM biofilm sensor did not
meet the requirements listed by Danish water suppliers for the ideal sensor for real-time
monitoring of microbial drinking water quality.

Introduction
Safe drinking water is essential for all human beings and as a result, ensuring safe drinking
water, that is free from harmful contaminants, is of high importance and is included in the
Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) set by the United Nations (UN), which further em-
phasizes the importance (United Nations, 2015). The Danish legislation on drinking water
is based on the European Union’s drinking water directive. The regulations described in
these directives should help to ensure drinking water that is safe for consumption with-
out posing any risk to human health (European Union, 2020; Miljøministeriet, 2023a). In
Denmark, the legislation regarding drinking water quality and monitoring is described in
a number of laws and frameworks, including Drikkevandsbekendtgørelsen. This drinking
water framework defines the responsibilities of drinking water suppliers to comply with
water quality criteria. Furthermore, the framework defines the water quality monitoring
scheme which the water suppliers must comply with (Miljøministeriet, 2023a).
In general, drinking water is of good quality in Denmark (Gunnarsdottir et al., 2020; Kris-
tensen et al., 2017). In Denmark, drinking water is produced entirely from groundwater
(DANVA, 2022) and undergoes only simple treatment processes before distribution to
consumers; aeration and sand filtration (DANVA, 2022; Miljøministeriet, 2023b). In many
countries drinking water is disinfected, e.g., using chlorine, before it is distributed to con-
sumers (Bitton, 2014). The fact that this is not common practice in Denmark leaves the
Danish drinking water vulnerable to deterioration of microbial water quality and microbial
contamination throughout the distribution.
At any point throughout the production and distribution of drinking water, the water quality
can be compromised. For instance, the water may be contaminated already at the source,
as a result of failure in treatment processes, or as a result of the intrusion of contaminants
through breakages in pipes in the distribution network (Lee et al., 2012). Of these poten-
tial risks, failure in the distribution networks is of great concern as there are no barriers
before the water reaches the consumers (Lee et al., 2012). For this reason, it is crucial
to continuously monitor the drinking water quality throughout the network, to ensure that
safe drinking water is distributed to the consumers and thereby protect their health.
Current methods used for microbial drinking water quality monitoring are based on grab
sampling (Naturstyrelsen, 2013), and the regulations require only sampling at the con-
sumer’s tap (Miljøministeriet, 2023a). With respect to microbial water quality, Danish
drinking water is monitored through Heterotrophic Plate Counts (HPC) and analyses of
indicator organisms such as coliform bacteria, including Escherichia coli (E. coli), which is
commonly associated with fecal material. However, these microbial analyses have very
long response times (up to three days) due to necessary incubation. As a result, once the
results from the analyses are ready, the water has most likely already been distributed
and consumed (Tatari et al., 2016).
Besides relying on time-consuming analysis methods the frequency at which these anal-
yses are required is rather low and therefore only a small fraction of the distributed water
is quality controlled. As a result, harmful drinking water might, despite regular testing,
still reach consumers, where it can cause outbreaks of diseases. Furthermore, the likeli-
hood of detecting a contamination incident is also lowered by the fact that most incidents
are short-term, hence almost only long-term incidents and systematic contamination can
be caught using current methods (Stedmon et al., 2011). It has been estimated that the

chance of actually collecting a sample at just the right time and thereby catching an abnor-
mality in microbial water quality or a contamination incident before the water is consumed
is zero (Sorensen, Baker, et al., 2018; Stedmon et al., 2011). It is very likely that the
contamination events that are detected only represent the tip of the iceberg (Boe-Hansen
et al., 2003).
With the current monitoring frequency and methods, the water suppliers do not have a
complete picture of the water quality in the distribution network and cannot guarantee
that the water delivered to the consumers is safe to drink at all times. Grab sampling
can only verify that the water distributed to consumers is of acceptable quality at the time
of sampling and at that particular sampling point. Because of these shortcomings, grab
sampling at the consumer’s tap can be described as ”too little, too late” (Christensen &
Pedersen, 2018).
There is a desire to develop and implement monitoring technologies that will provide better
surveillance of the microbial water quality in drinking water distribution networks. These
technologies could include on-line and real-time sensor monitoring technologies that can
be installed at numerous locations throughout the distribution network, providing informa-
tion on water quality not only at the consumer’s tap. During recent years there has been
an increased interest in investigating potential microbial sensor technologies for applica-
tion in drinking water distribution networks (Christensen & Pedersen, 2018; Tatari et al.,
2016).
When this thesis project was initiated the overall project conducted by Aarhus Vand A/S
was already well under way. As a result, two different sensors had been chosen for in-
vestigations for use in drinking water quality monitoring. One of each type of sensor was
sent to DTU for laboratory investigations, and additional sensors were deployed for inves-
tigations in the field (the drinking water distribution network in Aarhus).
1.1
Aim
The overall objective of this thesis project is to investigate and contribute to the under-
standing of on-line sensor technologies and their potential for real-time monitoring of mi-
crobial water quality in drinking water distribution networks, including investigations of two
sensor technologies: a tryptophan sensor, and a biofilm sensor. Specifically, the objec-
tives of this project are to:
• Review existing technologies for on-line monitoring of microbial drinking water qual-
ity, including a thorough description of the two sensor technologies investigated in
this study.
• Conceptualize a protocol for investigating the suitability of specific sensor technolo-
gies for use in microbial drinking water quality monitoring.
• Investigate the applicability of the two sensors to detect microbial contamination in
drinking water, determine the sensitivity of the measurements, and the limitations of
the technologies by evaluating their performance in laboratory conditions as well as
field deployment.
• Elaborate on the perspectives for on-line monitoring of microbial drinking water qual-
ity and important considerations.

Background
To understand why and how real-time sensor technologies could be implemented to im-
prove microbial drinking water quality monitoring it is important to know the relevant legis-
lation, the parametric values regarding microbial water quality, and the required frequency
and methods applied for current monitoring strategies. This chapter will present a founda-
tion of background knowledge on the topic and will finally provide an in-depth description
of the two sensor technologies investigated in this study.
2.1
Legislation
All EU member states, including Denmark, which is the focus of this study, have to comply
with the EU Drinking Water Directive (Directive (EU) 2020/2184). The directive concerns
the quality of water intended for human consumption as well as access to drinking water
(European Union, 2020), and thereby it aims to protect human health. The directive sets
advised parametric values for a number of parameters for drinking water, e.g., chemical,
physical, and microbial parameters. As a minimum requirement, all member states are
compelled to meet the guidelines set in the EU directive. Though, implementation in the
member states may lead to different regulations and legislation on a national level as
member states are allowed to have more stringent requirements in national frameworks
than those stated in the EU directive (European Union, 2020).
2.1.1
National Legislation
In Denmark, the national guidelines on drinking water are defined in Drikkevandsbekendt-
gørelsen. This national framework applies to all utilities supplying drinking water to more
than one household (Miljøministeriet, 2023a). Not only does this framework state drinking
water quality criteria, but it also defines the water quality monitoring requirements. More
than 50 physical and chemical parameters are being monitored in the Danish drinking
water (Miljøministeriet, 2023a). These include concentrations of metals, ions, pesticides,
and other unwanted chemicals as well as parameters such as temperature, conductivity,
and turbidity. Only a few parameters are directly used to monitor the microbial content
in the drinking water and thereby the microbial drinking water quality. These among oth-
ers include heterotrophic bacteria counts and indicator organisms such as Escherichia
coli. However, some of the physical and chemical parameters are used as indicators of
changes in microbial quality. Among these is turbidity, which is a measure of the over-
all particle content in the water. An increase in turbidity can therefore indicate that, e.g.,
soil or wastewater has entered the drinking water distribution network. In Section 2.3 an
in-depth description of the parameters related to microbial water quality is provided.
Drikkevandsbekendtgørelsen defines the required monitoring scheme. Monitoring water
quality is a very important step in ensuring safe drinking water for consumers. In Den-
mark, the frequency at which water suppliers are required to monitor the water quality is
depending on the produced water volume. Smaller water suppliers need to monitor water
quality less frequently than larger water suppliers. However, common for all water sup-
pliers is the fact that the monitoring is based on a grab-sampling approach, and hence all
parametric values are based on laboratory analyses conducted in certified laboratories.
Drikkevandsbekendtgørelsen states that the water suppliers must ensure that the drink-
ing water is of good quality when it reaches the consumers and not just when it leaves the
waterworks. Therefore, it is required that the monitoring of the drinking water quality is
done by taking samples at the consumer’s tap (Miljøministeriet, 2023a). Previously water

suppliers were required to monitor water quality at the outlet from the waterworks, in the
distribution network, and at the consumer’s tap. The current requirements for drinking

In the Danish drinking water framework and in the European directive, there are no men-
tioning of standardized monitoring methods using on-line sensor technologies. In fact,
the use of on-line sensor technologies is only briefly mentioned, in the Danish legislation,
as a tool for monitoring water quality during contamination events where accredited wa-
ter sampling cannot be awaited (Miljøministeriet, 2023a). Thereby, the current legislation
does not acknowledge sensors for standardized monitoring of drinking water quality.
2.2
Danish Drinking Water
Drinking water can be made from different sources of raw water, e.g., groundwater and
surface water. The source of the raw water used determines the properties, chemistry,
and quality of the water. Compared to surface water, groundwater usually contains fewer
microorganisms and is often less contaminated with both chemicals and bacteria due to
less exposure to contamination sources such as wastewater and chemicals. As a result
groundwater usually requires less treatment than surface water when used for drinking
water (Smith, 1999).
2.2.1
Drinking Water Treatment
In Denmark, drinking water is entirely produced from groundwater, hence only a few sim-
ple treatment processes are needed before the water is distributed to the consumers
(DANVA, 2022). Traditionally the water treatment at the Danish waterworks consists of
only two processes: aeration and biological sand filtration (Arvin, 2002a, 2002b; Miljø-
og Energiministeriet, 2001; Miljøministeriet, 2023b). Without any additional treatment
the water is distributed to the consumers. This is a rather unique situation as additional
treatment such as disinfection is common practice in many other countries (also where
drinking water is produced from groundwater) (Bitton, 2014). Disinfection is in place to
minimize the presence of microbiology in the drinking water and thereby reduce the risk
of pathogens spreading through the drinking water. Disinfection of drinking water can be
done in a number of ways, but the addition of chlorine is the most widely applied method
(Bitton, 2014; Gray, 1994). Disinfection of drinking water, through chlorination, can also
reduce the growth and regrowth of bacteria throughout the distribution network as free
chlorine residuals are present in the water when it leaves the waterworks (Haas, 1999).
2.2.2
Drinking Water Distribution
In Denmark, the drinking water supply system is rather decentralized with more than 2,500
waterworks (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020). Previously the distribution networks have been con-
structed in interconnected loops but today the water suppliers are working towards sec-
tioning the distribution networks. Sectioning water distribution networks have the advan-
tage that it is easier to model these systems than looped systems. This means that it is
easier to control retention times and water flow in the network (Boe-Hansen, 2001). Fur-
thermore, in sectioned networks, it is easier to track down the source of a contamination
event as the water is not looped over a large area spreading potential contamination. Re-
tention time is an important factor for water quality. It applies that longer retention times
of the drinking water in the distribution networks lead to a higher degree of deterioration
of microbial water quality as a result of the microbial activity in the distribution network
(Boe-Hansen, 2001). As the Danish drinking water is not disinfected, and the microbial
activity in the distribution network is not reduced, the retention time plays a significant role
in the deterioration of microbial water quality in Denmark. As a result of not disinfecting
the water biofilm covers the inside of the distribution pipes.

The materials used in the distribution networks play an important role in microbial water
quality. Previously the distribution networks were primarily constructed of cement and
cast iron pipes. Even though these materials are strong they are susceptible to rusting
and leaks. Today, polyethylene (PE) and cross-linked polyethylene (PEX) pipes are re-
placing these materials in drinking water distribution networks (Ryssel et al., 2015). These
materials are less subjected to leakages and corrosion compared to cement and cast iron.
However, organic compounds can migrate from the plastic materials into the drinking wa-
ter resulting in the after-growth of bacteria in the distribution network (Corfitzen, 2004).
Therefore, it is important that plastic materials with only limited migration of organic com-
pounds are used.
2.3
Drinking Water Quality Parameters
Drinking water quality is a wide term covering numerous parameters both physical, chem-
ical, and microbiological. Only when drinking water adheres to all set parametric values in
the EU directive it is deemed safe for consumption purposes (European Union, 2020). The
following sections provide an overview of the parameters that are important with respect
to microbial water quality. These include parameters affecting microbial growth (temper-
ature and the availability of organic carbon), indirect parameters of microbial water quality

2.3.1
Physical-chemical Parameters
Some physical and chemical parameters of drinking water have importance for the growth
of microorganisms in the distribution network. These include temperature and the avail-
ability of ”bacteria feed”. Many organisms have higher growth rates in warmer tempera-
tures. Hence, it is recommended that the temperature of drinking water does not exceed
12 °C at the consumer’s tap, though it is not a requirement. As most pathogenic bacteria
have the optimal conditions of survival around human body temperature (37 °C) keep-
ing the temperature low in the distribution network also reduces the risk of pathogenic
bacteria proliferating and surviving (Boe-Hansen, 2001).
The growth of microorganisms also depends on the availability of ”bacteria feed”, meaning
the organic carbon that the bacteria feed on. It is therefore recommended that the amount
of available carbon in the water is limited (Boe-Hansen, 2001). As a result, the Danish
drinking water framework includes a limit of 4 mg/L NVOC (non-volatile organic carbon)
(Miljøministeriet, 2023a). NVOC concentrations higher than this may result in increased
bacteria growth in the water. A study from 2005 found that bacteria counts were slightly
increasing with higher NVOC concentrations (Ernsten et al., 2005). Besides NVOC, as-
similable organic carbon (AOC) content in the water is also relevant for the after-growth
of bacteria in drinking water distribution networks. AOC consists of organic molecules
with low molecular weight which makes AOC the fraction of carbon that is most easily
consumed by bacteria and thereby facilitating growth (Nescerecka et al., 2014). There
is a positive correlation between the concentration of AOC and the concentration of het-
erotrophic bacteria (Pick et al., 2019).
Besides the above mentioned parameters, the presence of biofilm inside the pipes in
distribution networks can affect the microbial water quality. Biofilms are formed as mi-
croorganisms in the water phase attach to, and start colonizing surfaces in the distribu-
tion network. This is a phenomenon known to occur in Danish drinking water distribution
networks. In fact, more than 90% of the biomass in drinking water distribution networks

Both conductivity and turbidity are important parameters when determining the integrity
of the distribution network. A change in conductivity indicates that the ion content of the
water has changed. This could be due to mixing of different water sources or types and
can therefore indicate leakages of, e.g., surface water or wastewater into the distribution
network. In the same way, an increase in turbidity might indicate contamination with other
water types with higher particle concentrations. Increased turbidity can however also in-
dicate a breakage of pipes where soil particles enter the network. Both parameters are
therefore important when detecting sudden contamination from holes or leakages in the
pipe network. Such contamination events often result in a deterioration of the microbial
water quality and therefore both turbidity and conductivity can be used as proxy parame-
ters or indirect parameters for microbial water quality.
Some of the on-line monitoring technologies which are already used in the water industry
for monitoring physical and chemical parameters include turbidity, pressure, flow, con-
ductivity, pH, and temperature sensors (Tang & Albrechtsen, 2019). These technologies
have been implemented by some Danish water suppliers during the last decades and are
in place to improve the understanding of the dynamics of the distribution networks (Storey
et al., 2011; Tatari et al., 2016). As described, sudden changes in some of these parame-
ters can sometimes be an indirect identification of severe microbial contamination, though
changes do not necessarily mean that there is a microbial contamination incident (Tatari
et al., 2016). Therefore, there might be a potential for using sensors measuring, e.g.,
turbidity and conductivity not only to understand the dynamics of the distribution network
but also as an indirect tool to monitor the microbial water quality.
2.3.2
Microbial Parameters and Indicators
Drinking water in Denmark will naturally contain microorganisms as groundwater con-
tains microorganisms such as bacteria, viruses, and protozoa in varying concentrations
and no disinfectants are used in the water treatment (Roslev, 2014). However, the con-
centrations of microorganisms will usually be low in groundwater as aquifers often are
challenging environments for microorganisms because of low nutrient content as well as
lack of light, and low temperatures (Boe-Hansen, 2001; Ramsay, 2014). When the raw
water is extracted from an aquifer and enters the waterworks, the water is oxygenated
and the temperature also increases. As a result the living conditions for microorganisms
change, which might allow for microbial growth (Ramsay, 2014). For the most part, the
microorganisms present in groundwater aquifers do not present any hazard to human

Coliform bacteria are members of the family Enterobacteriaceae and include among oth-
ers the genera Enterobacter, Erwinia, Escherichia, Klebsiella, and Serratia. The majority
of coliforms do not pose any risk to human health, though some E. coli and Klebsiella
pneumoniae are exceptions (Naturstyrelsen, 2013). Coliform bacteria occur naturally in
soil, decomposing plants, and surface waters. This group of bacteria is therefore used as
an indicator of contamination with soil or surface water. Increased coliform concentration
does not necessarily cause an increased risk to human health (Naturstyrelsen, 2013).
As mentioned, Escherichia coli (E. coli) is a coliform bacteria and some E. coli bacteria
are pathogenic. E. coli is known as a natural component of the microflora in the intestines
of both humans and warm-blooded animals and is hence found in human and animal
faeces (Bitton, 2014). For this reason E. coli is used as an indicator organism for faecal
contamination in drinking water. The presence of E. coli indicates a risk of contamination
with wastewater or other contamination with faeces from animals or humans. Due to the
rather short lifespan of E. coli outside of the intestines the presence of E. coli in drinking
water will usually be an indication of recent faecal contamination (Naturstyrelsen, 2013).
It is known that E. coli is capable of surviving for weeks in drinking water, however growth
of E. coli in distribution networks have not been observed in Danish distribution networks
(Naturstyrelsen, 2013; Roslev et al., 2014). Besides E. coli, both Clostridium perfringens
(Cl. perfringens) and enterococci are also used as faecal contamination indicators. These
are both pathogenic bacteria found in animal and human faeces (Naturstyrelsen, 2013).
Colony Forming Units (CFU) at 22 °C is used as a standardized parameter for microbial
drinking water quality. This analysis provides the number of colony forming bacteria in
the water. These bacteria are mostly not pathogenic, hence this parametric value is used
to control the presence of non-hazardous microorganisms. CFU values are determined
through Heterotrophic Plate Count (HPC) which tests the ability of the bacteria to form
colonies. Increased CFU values might indicate contamination from plants, soil or water,
or be a result of increased after-growth in the water distribution network (Naturstyrelsen,
2013).
Together with coliform bacteria and E. coli, CFU counts are the most commonly used
microbial analysis when determining microbial drinking water quality. However, the incu-

2.4
Current Practices - Surveillance Strategies and Methods
According to the Danish drinking water framework, the responsibility of ensuring safe
drinking water, that adheres to the quality criteria, falls on the water suppliers. For this
reason, the responsibility of monitoring the drinking water quality also falls on the water
suppliers (Miljøministeriet, 2023a). In the following, the required monitoring frequencies
and sampling methods are described, along with a description of the limitations of the
current strategies.

2.4.2
Sampling and Analysis Methods
The methods used for quality monitoring rely on grab sampling, which is followed by later
analyses in certified laboratories. Together with a low sampling frequency, this constitutes
a problem as only a snapshot of the water quality is obtained. Furthermore, the methods
used to monitor microbial water quality are time-consuming. According to Drikkevands-
bekendtgørelsen, the standardized HPC analyses require an incubation time of 68 hours
± 4 hours. For this reason, the result for a given sample is not ready until three days
after sampling. This delay means that the sampled water has, most likely, already been
distributed and consumed once the analysis results are ready. Analyses for coliform bac-
teria and E. coli are also time-consuming. For these parameters, a Colilert-18 test is a
common analysis. This test requires an incubation time of 18 hours.
This also constitutes a problem, as the water suppliers have no chance to circumvent
the consumption of contaminated water or water of deteriorated quality. Furthermore,
short-term incidents of increased microbial content (both pathogenic and non-pathogenic
bacteria included) in the drinking water are not likely to be caught by grab sampling (Sted-
mon et al., 2011; Vang, 2013).
Through grab sampling alone (even at a frequent sampling rate) the water suppliers can-
not obtain the complete picture of the quality of the drinking water distributed to con-
sumers. In fact, only a very small fraction of the distributed water is quality conrtolled.
2.5
Sensor Technologies
To ensure that clean and safe drinking water is distributed to consumers new methods and
strategies for monitoring and surveillance are needed. These new methods should include
technologies with a rapid response or even real-time monitoring of the microbial drinking
water quality. For this purpose, real-time monitoring technologies such as different on-
line sensor technologies might be the solution. Real-time monitoring would significantly
improve the surveillance of the microbial drinking water quality. Also, it could result in
the detection of otherwise undetected changes in microbial water quality caused by, e.g.,
wastewater contamination incidents, as those shown in Figure 2.1.
The theoretical example provided in Figure 2.2 shows how on-line monitoring of, e.g.,
adenosine triphosphate (ATP) could detect contamination events that would otherwise not
be detected during regular grab sampling. Other sensor technologies targeting different
parameters for microbial activity or growth might also be of relevance in this context.

Though on-line sensors provide the possibility of better surveillance of the water quality
in terms of frequency and spatial distribution there are some limitations of sensor tech-
nologies compared to grab sampling and culture based methods. Firstly, where, e.g.,
Colilert-18 tests have a very high sensitivity and thereby a low detection limit (down to
1 per 100 mL) for E. coli, sensors are often less sensitive. Therefore, with respect to
sensitivity the culture-based methods are superior.
Secondly, sensor technologies usually measure proxy parameters or other parameters
than those described in legislation. New sensor technologies for microbial water qual-
ity monitoring also often rely on principles that differ from those described in regulations
(culture-based methods) (Albrechtsen, 2018). As a result, the sensors can not provide
information that can be held up against current drinking water quality regulations. This
constitutes a challenge for the implementation of sensors as there are no parametric val-
ues for the parameters measured, which also means that there are no guidelines for the
use of the technologies nor are there any records of the acceptable values with respect
to human health (Albrechtsen, 2018).
For these two reasons, it is not likely that sensors will completely replace current technolo-
gies. However, implementing sensor technologies as a part of standardized surveillance
strategies would be a significant improvement to current strategies.
As sensors might be a solution to improve microbial water quality monitoring, efforts have
been made in Denmark, in recent years, to boost the development of sensors for moni-
toring microbial drinking water quality. In connection with this, a number of water utilities
were in 2016 asked to describe the ”ideal” sensor for monitoring microbial water quality in
drinking water networks. This resulted in six points that the utilities want the ideal sensor
to fulfill (Tatari et al., 2016):
• A sensor should have on-line installation.
• It should be detecting indicator microorganisms (total coliforms or E. coli).
• It should have a very high sensitivity to detect indicator microorganisms.
• The response from the sensor should be rapid. Meaning that the delay between
measurement and available results should be very short, ideally even real-time.

• The sensor should have a low occurrence of false alarms.
• Very importantly the sensor should also have only low maintenance requirements.
This list sets the overarching goals and requirements for the ideal sensor and provides
some of the important parameters to evaluate when determining the applicability of a
sensor in Danish drinking water networks.
Besides investigating what the ”ideal” sensor for monitoring microbial water quality should
be able to do Tatari et al. (2016) also looked into some of the sensors already available
on the market. Among the technologies investigated in Denmark is the BACMON optical
sensor which provides a measure of the total particles and bacterial cells (Tatari et al.,
2016). This sensor requires no incubation time and aims to detect sudden changes in total
particles and bacterial cells (Tatari et al., 2016). The BACMON sensor has been reported
to have only limited maintenance requirements, which is one of the six points listed above.
On the other hand, this sensor does not fulfill some of the other requirements, including
that it does not give a measurement of indicator microorganisms but just a measurement
of total bacterial cells in the water.
Another principle that has been investigated in Denmark is the use of ATP measurements
as an indication of total bacterial activity. Investigations have demonstrated the potential
of monitoring microbial drinking water quality by means of measuring ATP in the water
(Vang, 2013). It was concluded that this technology does not fulfill the points listed above.
2.5.1
ALVIM Biofilm Sensor
The first on-line sensor investigated in this study is the ALVIM biofilm sensor of the model
A001S3 developed and produced by ALVIM Srl, see Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Illustration of Alvim biofilm sensor, model A001S3, (ALVIM Srl, 2022).
The ALVIM real-time biofilm sensor monitors biofilm growth through the measurement of
a bioelectrochemical signal. The sensor exploits the fact that cathodic depolarization is
affected by biofilm attaching to the sensor surface. The electron transfer from the sen-
sor surface to the oxygen in the water is accelerated as biofilm settles and grows on the
sensitive sensor surface (Di Pippo et al., 2018; Faimali et al., 2010; Pavanello et al.,
2011). As a result, it is possible to indirectly detect the attachment and growth of biofilm
on this surface. Furthermore, the cathodic current increase induced by biofilm formation
is proportional to the number of settled bacteria during the phase of initial biofilm develop-
ment (Faimali et al., 2010). As the sensor can only detect the bacteria interacting directly
with the sensitive surface (the first layer of microorganisms) the proportionality between
cathodic current and settled bacteria can be translated into a proportionality between ca-
thodic current and the area of the sensor surface covered by biofilm. In Figure 2.4 the

principle behind it, the sensor is able to monitor biofilm without mistaking it for other types
of fouling, e.g., mineral deposits.

The ALVIM biofilm sensor has a sensitivity range of 1-100% surface coverage by biofilm,
and thereby it is able to detect the very initial settlement and growth of biofilm (ALVIM Srl,
2022; Pavanello et al., 2011). The sensor is designed based on a threshold approach,
which means that the sensor will indicate biofilm coverage larger than the chosen thresh-
old value by a rapid increase in its signal. According to the manual, biofilm growth above
the chosen threshold is indicated with a signal increase of 150 mV or more over a duration
of hours to days from the beginning of the signal increase (ALVIM Srl, 2022; Pavanello,
2023).
A sensitivity value, related to the fraction of the sensor covered by biofilm, between 1 and
255 is chosen when deploying the sensor. The higher the value the higher the sensitivity.
A sensitivity value of 255 means that coverage of 1% of the surface area is the threshold at
which a rapid signal increase is triggered (ALVIM Srl, 2022; Pavanello, 2023). The sensor
is therefore designed based on a yes/no concept - yes there is biofilm above the threshold
value or no there is not biofilm above the threshold value. This design makes the sensor
useful for applications where cleaning or treatment actions must be taken when biofilm
reaches a certain level.
This technology has been applied in a number of studies already, most of them concern-
ing the monitoring of the effectiveness of disinfection treatments in industrial water lines
(Pavanello et al., 2017). Also, the sensor has been deployed in a water distribution net-
work, distributing chlorinated water. In that study, it proved to be capable of detecting
biofilm growth in distribution networks as well as being a very powerful tool for monitoring
and adjusting sanitation (chlorination) treatments (ALVIM Srl, 2021).
2.5.2
Tryptophan Fluorescence Sensor Technology
The second on-line sensor investigated in this study is the UviLux Tryptophan fluorometer
developed and produced by Chelsea Technologies, see Figure 2.5. This sensor technol-
ogy relies on fluorescence spectroscopy. Fluorescence is the ability of some molecules
(fluorophores) to emit visible light after being excited. The excitation happens when en-
ergy is supplied by an external source, e.g., ultraviolet light, and absorbed by a fluo-
rophore. The fluorophore then remains excited for a very brief time span (typically just a
few nanoseconds), before it emits the absorbed energy in the form of light and returns to
its original state. This emission of light is fluorescence (Lakowicz, 2006). Fluorescence
spectroscopy is a highly sensitive analytical tool and has in recent years been widely ap-
plied in biological, chemical, and environmental science as a tool for the characterization
12
Assessing On-line Sensors for Microbial Drinking Water Quality Monitoring in Distribution Networks
==PAGE:27==
of dissolved organic matter (DOM) (Lakowicz, 2006). Fluorescence spectroscopy has,
among other things, been used to assess the water quality of surface waters by, e.g.,
identifying input of pollution and wastewater (Ward et al., 2021). Also, fluorescence has
proven a useful tool to detect and screen for certain contaminants in groundwater as well
as surface water (Vinther et al., 2023).
Figure 2.5: Uvilux Tryptophan Sensor, (Chelsea Technologies Ltd, n.d.).
Fluorophores emit different fluorescence and it is therefore possible to distinguish fluores-
cence from different fluorophores. One well-known fluorophore is tryptophan. Tryptophan
is an amino acid that is produced by all living organisms and is therefore omnipresent in
the environment (Chelsea Technologies Ltd, 2021). Along with other known fluorophores,
tryptophan is known to emit fluorescence in the ultraviolet region of light at excitation wave-
length 280 nm and emission wavelength 350 nm (Ward et al., 2021). Fluorescence in this
spectrum is hence known as Tryptophan-like fluorescence (TLF).
In recent years, TLF has been applied to assess microbial contamination from wastew-
ater in drinking water sources (mainly surface water and groundwater) as TLF is often
high in human waste, and thereby wastewater, and is furthermore considered to indicate
biological activity (Nowicki et al., 2019; Sorensen et al., 2016; Sorensen, Baker, et al.,
2018; Sorensen et al., 2020). Elevated TLF measurements in water bodies might there-
fore be explained by intrusion or input of wastewater. Tryptophan-like fluorescence has
also been shown to be strongly correlated with total bacterial cell counts and E. coli con-
centrations, advocating for the use of fluorescence spectroscopy in water supply systems,
where it could be used to identify changes in microbial water quality and thereby indicate
the presence of unwanted pathogens (Sorensen, Vivanco, et al., 2018).
The UviLux tryptophan sensor is an in-situ digital UV fluorometer developed to obtain
real-time monitoring of TLF. The sensor has a high sensitivity with a detection range of
tryptophan of 0.02 - 1,200 mg/L (Chelsea Technologies Ltd, 2020), and should accord-
ing to the manufacturer be ”an ideal tool for detecting sewage contamination” (Chelsea
Technologies Ltd, n.d.).

3.1
Laboratory Investigations: Biofilm Sensor
Two different experimental setups were used to investigate the ALVIM biofilm sensor in

In the flow-through setup drinking water from the tap in the laboratory at DTU was led
through a Low-Density Polyethylene (LDPE) tube and into a stainless steel manifold in
which the biofilm sensor was installed (Figure 3.1). The water flow through the system
was set to 1 L/min. The drinking water flowed through the manifold, passed the sensor,

air bubbles on the working electrode of the sensor. Also based on recommendations
in the sensor manual, the highest possible sensitivity value (255) was used. Data was
logged once per hour. The flow-through setup ran for 21 days. Throughout the 21 days,
the temperature of the drinking water was measured regularly and it remained stable at
around 12 °C.

Sensor Evaluation Protocol
As sensor technologies might be one of the solutions for better microbial water quality
monitoring in drinking water networks, many different sensor technologies might be inves-
tigated in the coming years. In relation to this, it would be beneficial to have a standard-
ized protocol for sensor evaluations. Systematically evaluation of sensor technologies
following the same protocol allows for the comparison of different technologies, which is
essential for finding the best solution.
Often, information on performance might be limited for newly developed technologies,
and information received from manufacturers may be biased due to promotional interests
(Tatari et al., 2016). Therefore, it is very important to have an unbiased performance
verification and evaluation of technologies before application. In fact, there are companies
and research institutions that have specialized in conducting performance evaluations, an
example is The Alliance for Coastal Technologies (ACT). ACT is dedicated to fostering
the development and adoption of sensors and platforms in both freshwater and ocean
environments (ACT, 2022).
With inspiration from some of the protocols developed by ACT for performance evalua-
tion, a protocol for the evaluation of sensors for use in drinking water distribution networks
was developed. The protocol presented here is divided into four overarching steps (Fig-
ure 4.1), covering the whole process of finding and evaluating relevant technologies.
Figure 4.1: Overview of the 4 steps in the sensor evaluation protocol.
The four steps included in the evaluation protocol will be described individually in the
following 4 sections.
4.1
Step 1: Defining the Scope
This first step of the protocol covers three aspects:
• Defining the goal of using sensor technology.
• Describing the application case.
• Identifying relevant sensor(s) available on the market.
These three aspects are described separately in the following and the key points are
summarized in Table 4.1.

4.1.1
Goal
It is important to define the goal of deploying any sensor technologies. What type of
information does one want to gain from applying sensors to a system and how should the
information be used? These are both key questions.
In this project, the overall goal of applying sensors in the distribution network is to obtain
a better understanding of the microbial drinking water quality after the water leaves the
waterworks. The desire is to retrieve real-time information on water quality from sensors
which will allow for the development of an early warning system. This presents some
requirements for the applied technology. The sensor has to deliver real-time or close to
real-time data in order to be implemented in an early warning system. There could also
be some requirements for the type of data, e.g., if the data should be incorporated into
existing data platforms. Specifications on the parameter for monitoring and required sen-
sitivity are also an important part of the goal definition. This could include specifications
on the concentration at which the sensor should be able to detect abnormalities in the
system. For the application of sensors in drinking water distribution networks, there are
high requirements for sensor sensitivity as even low concentrations of unwanted bacteria
in drinking water can result in disease spreading. As described in Section 2.5 the Danish
water suppliers want the ideal sensor to have a low occurrence of false alarms. To comply
with this the sensor should have a high sensitivity for the targeted parameter and should
not give high sensor readings as a result of changes in other parameters.
4.1.2
Application Case
Having defined the goal, the next step is to describe the application case. This includes
a description of the environmental conditions, such as pressure and temperature, and
type of media. For the application in drinking water distribution networks, it could, e.g.,
be a requirement that the sensor works within a temperature range of 0-30 °C, and that
the sensor should work within the pressure range found in pipes in distribution networks.
Also, it could be a requirement that the sensor is not affected by a change in flow direction.
There might also be other relevant requirements for a given application case, that could,
e.g., be related to the installation of the sensor. Narrow spaces for installation might
give size requirements. These considerations are all very important when searching for
relevant technologies.

4.1.3
Identifying Relevant Technologies
When having defined the goal and described the application case, relevant sensor tech-

identified when describing the application case and the goal of implementing a sensor

facturers can be biased. A thorough research on relevant technologies is recommended
to have the best possible basis for decision-making and to avoid investing both time and
money on investigating technologies that in previous studies might have shown not to be
suitable for the application case.

• Signal interpretation.
• Detection range and accuracy.
• Precision and reliability.
These parameters are essential to evaluate the applicability of a sensor in a given setup.

4.2.1
Signal Interpretation
To evaluate the performance of a sensor, it is essential to obtain an understanding of
how the signal from the sensor behaves and how the data should be interpreted. For an
on-line sensor that is continuously collecting data, this could include a description of the
”baseline” signal. The baseline signal describes the signal level during normal conditions.
How the sensor signal changes when the water quality changes should also be described.
4.2.2
Detection Range and Accuracy
When baseline signal and signals indicating changes in water quality has been described,
the next step is to determine the detection range and the accuracy of the sensor. These
are key factors in the overall evaluation. The detection range is defined as the range in
which the sensor is able to deliver reasonable results. The detection range tells if the
22
Assessing On-line Sensors for Microbial Drinking Water Quality Monitoring in Distribution Networks
==PAGE:37==
sensor is capable of detecting the targeted parameter in concentrations relevant to the
application case. Often a detection range of a sensor is provided by the manufacturer
of the sensor. However, detection ranges provided by the manufacturer might be in the
optimal working conditions of the sensor, which might be very different from the condi-
tions in which the sensor is deployed. It is therefore important to determine the detection
range under the relevant conditions. The detection range could be determined through
measurements of a dilution series in a laboratory setup simulating the conditions in which
the sensor will be deployed in the field. A simple dilution series could, e.g., be a 10-fold
dilution series (0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100 µg/L or mg/L) of the targeted contaminant or
parameter. The dilution should be made until the sensor is no longer able to detect the
parameter. The lowest detectable concentration is the lower detection limit. The highest
detectable concentration that still provides reasonable sensor signals is the upper de-
tection limit. It applies that the lower the minimum detection limit the higher the sensor
sensitivity.
The detection range of the sensor is highly related to the accuracy of the sensor. The
accuracy of the sensor refers to how close a given sensor measurement is to a true/ac-
cepted value. For some technologies determining the accuracy can be difficult as there
might not be a known true value. This might in fact be the case for many new technologies
as many new technologies target proxy parameters. For this reason, a surrogate for the
accuracy of a sensor might be used instead. One way could be to investigate the relation-
ship between parameter concentration and the sensor signal. The data from the dilution
series could be used to investigate if there is a proportionality between concentration and
signal.
4.2.3
Precision and Reliability
It is also very important to determine the precision of the sensor. The response precision is
a measure of the repeatability of a sensor measurement. High precision is when the sen-
sor gives the same signal when the same sample is repeatedly measured. To investigate
the response precision of a sensor it is therefore suggested to do repeated measurements
of the same sample. From these measurements, the coefficient of variation (CV), which
is commonly used to express the precision or the repeatability of a process or technology,
should be calculated. CV is given as the ratio of the standard deviation (σ) to the mean
(µ) (Brockhoff et al., 2018). The CV should be as low as possible indicating a low extent of
variability in relation to the mean. Usually, CV < 5% or <10 % is accepted in environmental
science. The precision of the sensor is naturally also a very important parameter. High
precision ensures that every measurement is reliable. Also, high precision is essential
to ensure that there are no inconsistencies or at least very few inconsistencies related to
the measurements. Without a high precision of measurements, the results are not very
reliable and as a result, the sensor might not be useful for the desired application.
Finally, it is important to determine the reliability of the sensor. This includes investigating
the stability over time, which will automatically reveal any maintenance requirements. The
ideal sensor would be able to maintain the stability of measurements over time. This is
however not necessarily the case for all sensor technologies as some might have a drift
in signal during deployment time. For this reason, it is important to quantify the possible
drift, especially for sensors that are expected to be deployed over long periods of time. It
is suggested that the reliability over time is determined by measuring controls/references
before and after collecting a series of measurements to see if there are any changes or
drift in rerecorded responses from controls. An alternative to this could be to measure
the controls/references before and after deploying the sensor long-term in the application
case. Either way, if similar responses are observed before and after, it will indicate a low
Assessing On-line Sensors for Microbial Drinking Water Quality Monitoring in Distribution Networks
23
==PAGE:38==
need for maintenance to retain high precision measurements. On the other hand, if a
drift in response is observed it might indicate a need for regular maintenance to ensure
high precision measurements throughout the deployment of the sensor. Some sensor
technologies might require weekly or monthly calibration, reset, or cleaning, while other
technologies might be deployed for numerous months without any maintenance. In Den-
mark, the water utilities have described the wish for sensors that do not need frequent
maintenance, calibration, or reset.

4.3.1
Cost
As already mentioned there are a number of parameters not related to the actual per-
formance of the sensor which are also very important to consider when evaluating the
applicability of a sensor. As described in Section 2.5 the water utilities in Denmark have
expressed that the ideal sensor for on-line monitoring of microbial water quality should
be both affordable and also have low maintenance requirements. Monitoring the micro-

lifespan of the sensor is in the conditions in which it will be deployed.

a large number of sensors, it is essential to determine how user-friendly they are and if

properly use the technology. If some training is necessary, it is important to define who
will provide the training, if maybe the manufacturer offers it, or if it is something that the
water suppliers should do themselves.

Summary and Discussion of Protocol
The protocol presented here suggests one way of evaluating the performance of a sen-
sor with respect to determining the suitability of a sensor for a given application case.
The steps in the sensor evaluation protocol cover different important aspects that can be
summed up in the following.
To evaluate the applicability of a sensor technology one should:
• Determine and describe the purpose and goal of implementing the sensor technol-
ogy, describe the environment and media in which the sensor should be deployed,
and identify relevant technologies based on the defined goal and environment de-
scription.
• Obtain a basic understanding of signal interpretation, including how the signal be-
haves under normal conditions and how the sensor indicates an abnormality in water
quality.
• Determine the sensor detection range and accuracy and thereby also the sensitivity
of the sensor.
• Determine the response precision and the reliability of measurements, as well as
the stability over time.
• Identify maintenance requirements.

==PAGE:40==
• Evaluate cost and installation requirements.
• Evaluate user experience, including user-friendliness.
Following this protocol one should be able to make a final evaluation of the applicability of a
sensor technology to a specific case. For each of the protocol steps, some of the important
considerations regarding application in drinking water distribution networks have been
described. Additional evaluation parameters besides those listed in this protocol might be
relevant in other contexts. The overarching idea is that the protocol presented here could
be applied for sensors investigated for use in drinking water distribution networks, while it
might be a source of inspiration for the performance evaluation of sensors applied in other
contexts such as surface water quality monitoring or monitoring of wastewater treatment
processes.
It is important to bear in mind that sensors may target very different parameters. For
example, the two sensors investigated in this project measure two very different parame-
ters. The ALVIM sensor monitors the changes in the electrochemical signal resulting from
the biofilm growth on the sensor surface. The Chelsea Technologies tryptophan sensor
monitors the fluorescence of the bulk water in the system at a specific wavelength. Be-
cause different sensor technologies target different parameters of water quality a different
approach when evaluating the applicability might be necessary. In this project, the inves-
tigation of the biofilm sensor required a different approach than the one suggested in this
protocol. This technology does not rely on the detection of a parameter in the water phase
and therefore it is not possible to investigate the detection range, accuracy, and precision
following the procedures described in the protocol. As a result, the investigation and eval-
uation approach was more exploratory when trying to determine how and if this sensor
could be applied in Danish drinking water distribution networks.

External Factors Affecting the Sensor Signal
In both the flow-through setup (Figure 5.1) and at Østerbyværket (Figure 5.2a), sensor
readings fluctuated. In the flow-through setup, the fluctuations are occur in the pseudo-

was lowered fluctuations in sensor readings also occured at Østerbyværket.
For both the fluctuations in the flow-through setup and the fluctuations at Østerbyværket,
it was questioned if the fluctuations followed daily patterns and if so, what the reason for
this could be. Statistical analyses of the time series were conducted to see if the observed
fluctuations followed daily patterns. This was done by investigating the autocorrelations
and partial autocorrelations of the data series. Autocorrelation and partial autocorrelation
are measures of the relationship between current and past values in a time series. In
this analysis, the time gap between measurements being considered is called a lag. The
correlation at lag=k is the correlation between values that are k time-steps apart (Madsen,
2007). Together the autocorrelation and the partial autocorrelation can indicate which past
values (if any) in the time series have the highest influence on future values (Madsen,
2007). In this case, autocorrelation and partial autocorrelation will tell something about
the relationship between sensor readings at different steps in the time series and may
indicate whether there are any daily patterns. As data is given at one-hour intervals daily
variations are expected to be indicated with a high positive correlation for lag=24, meaning
that the data point in any given point in the data series is correlated with the data 24 time
steps (in this case 24 hours) prior.

The autocorrelation analysis of the flow-through data (Figure 5.4a) shows that the data
series is non-random since there are numerous autocorrelations that are significantly dif-
ferent from zero. This is indicated by the vertical lines going outside of the blue area that
depicts the 95% confidence interval. There is a slight positive correlation for lag=1 and for
lag=2. This means that there is a correlation between adjacent and near-adjacent data
points in the data series. In other words, the sensor measurement at a given time is pos-

data is collected once per hour it means that the sensor signal next hour is correlated with
the sensor signal both now and an hour ago. Negative correlations is observed for lag=6
and for lag=12. With the strongest correlation being for lag=12. Furthermore, positive
correlations are observed for lag=24, lag=25, and lag=26, where the strongest correlation
is observed for lag=25. With correlations at lag=6, lag=12, and lag=25 it is reasonable to
think that the variations in the fluctuations can be described as daily variations following
a pattern.

Effects of Flow
In the laboratory, it was investigated if increasing the flow from 1 L/min to 2 L/min had
any effects on the sensor signal (Figure 5.6). The flow in the system was changed in the
pseudo-stationary phase where fluctuations of the signal were observed. It appears that
there no noticeable change in signal as a result of the change in flow.
At Østerbyværket the sensor was placed at the outlet of the waterworks. The sensor was,
for regulatory reasons, not placed directly in the water stream leaving the waterworks.
Instead, a water bypass was made in which the sensor was deployed. The flow in the
bypass ranged from 2-5 L/min during the sensor deployment. This is higher than the flow
in the flow-through setup in the laboratory. Higher water velocity past the sensor results
in higher shear forces on the sensor surface which might lower the attachment rate of
bacteria to the sensor surface as well as increase the detachment of bacteria already on
the surface.

5.1.4
Effect of the Sensitivity Value
In the field, the sensor signals reached the upper detection limit of 1200 mV and remained
stable at that level. When the signal reaches the upper detection limit it is no longer
possible to see the full extent of any variations in the data series, nor is it possible to
detect any changes in the biofilm resulting in a signal increase.
With both the sensors in the field and with the sensor in the flow-through setup it was
investigated if lowering the sensitivity value would have any effects on the signal. It was
hypothesized that lowering the sensitivity value would result in a signal plateau at a lower
signal value, which would then leave room within the detection range to observe variations
as well as sudden changes in the biofilm. The sensitivity was lowered to 120 in the flow-
through setup while it was lowered to 150 for both sensors in the field, followed by a further
decrease to a sensitivity value of 1 at Observatoriet.
In the flow-through setup, changing the sensitivity value resulted in an immediate drop in
the sensor signal from 1150 mV to 980 mV (Figure 5.7a). The sudden decrease in sensor
signal was followed by a gradual increase in signal. As measurements were stopped the
following day it is unknown if the signal would have continued to increase to the same
level as before the change or if it would flatten out at a lower signal value.
At Østerbyværket changing the sensitivity value resulted in an immediate decrease in
signal from 1190 mV to 1050 mV. Opposite to the response observed from the flow-through
investigations, the signal remained stable and formed a new plateau at this level for 15
days. After 15 days the signal then increased slightly to 1120 mV and remained stable at
this level until measurements were stopped, see Figure 5.7b.
At Observatoriet the change had a different outcome. When lowering the sensitivity value
to 150 the sensor response dropped slightly from 1200 mV to around 1190 mV and then
it quickly increased and reached the same level as before the change. It was decided to
further decrease the sensor sensitivity to 1 (the least sensitive setting) to see if this would
result in a signal plateau at a lower value. This change resulted in the signal response
dropping from 1200 mV to 480 mV, which was below the initial baseline level of 600 mV.
The signal remained stable at this level until measurements were stopped. It was not
expected that values below the baseline level could be obtained by lowering the sensitiv-
ity value. At Observatoriet the signal became very stable after the change in sensitivity
whereas variations in the signal were observed at Østerbyværket.

5.1.5
Biofilm Analysis
The ALVIM biofilm sensor should be capable of detecting the very initial biofilm on the
sensor surface (ALVIM Srl, 2022), which is most likely not visible to the human eye. When
the sensor was dismantled from the flow-through setup (after 21 days) no biofilm could be
seen on the surface (Figure 3.2a). However, bacteria are very small microorganisms that
can not be seen with the naked eye and therefore the initial biofilm formed on the sensor
can be very thin, down to just a few µm of thickness. With this in mind, there could very
well be a biofilm on the sensor surface. To see if there was in fact biofilm on the sensor,
a sample was collected from the sensor surface. The biofilm analysis was conducted as
an HPC analysis where CFUs were counted. The CFU counts are provided in Table D.1
in Appendix D.
CFUs were only observed in the undiluted samples, and only in very low numbers (6 and 8
CFU/mL) corresponding to approximately 30 and 40 CFU/cm2. In comparison, a previous
Assessing On-line Sensors for Microbial Drinking Water Quality Monitoring in Distribution Networks
33
==PAGE:48==
study found CFUs in the 102 to 105 CFU/cm2 range when collecting a biofilm sample using
a similar swabbing technique (Boe-Hansen, 2001). It should be noted that the samples in
that study were incubated with a different growth media and for 7 days at 15 °C, whereas
the samples in this study were incubated for 3 days at 22 °C.
As the CFUs are very low it is not possible, based on these results alone, to confirm that a
biofilm had formed on the sensor surface resulting in the steep increase in sensor signal.
The low CFU counts may however be explained by a number of different things. First of all,
it is possible that the CFU counts were low because the method used to collect the sample
from the sensor surface was not efficient in removing the biofilm from the sensor surface
or maybe it was efficient in removing the biofilm but not without damaging the bacteria
in such a way that they could not grow. Secondly, the low CFUs could be explained by
the fact that not all bacteria will grow in laboratory conditions or in the chosen growth
media (in this case Yeast Extract Agar). In fact, it has been documented that only 0.1-
10% of the total bacteria in a drinking water sample will grow on agar plates in laboratory
conditions, depending on the substrate (Nedell & Jensen, 2011). The results might have
looked differently had another growth media, such as R2A agar, been used instead of the
yeast extract agar.

5.2.1
Effects of Replacing Tank Water
It was investigated if replacing the water in the looped system, and thereby introducing
new nutrients, would have any effect on the sensor signal which was otherwise stable at
800 mV. The water in the system was replaced twice, see Figure 5.9. When the water in
the tank was replaced with fresh drinking water the first time, the fresh drinking water had
a temperature of 12 °C. A second replacement of the water in the water tank was made
with drinking water at room temperature. This was done to see if the sudden change in
water temperature in the system had any effects on the signal.
Figure 5.9: Effects on sensor signal when water in the system tank was replaced. The first
replacement was made with drinking water directly from the tap (12 °C). The second replacement
was made with drinking water at room temperature (20 °C).
As seen in Figure 5.9 both times the water in the system was replaced the sensor signal
increased. The increase happened immediately after the water replacement. After the
first water replacement, the signal increased from 790 mV to around 880 mV; almost an
increase of 100 mV. After the second water replacement, the signal increased immediately
from 730 mV to around 800 mV; an increase of 70 mV. The signal decreased steadily over
time following both water replacements.
As the increase happened instantaneously it is not likely that the increase in signal is an
indication of the biofilm reacting to the fresh drinking water. Instead, the increase is more
likely an indication of the sensor being sensitive to the composition of the water in which
it is deployed.
5.2.1.1
Temperature
As an immediate increase in signal was observed both the first and the second time the
water in the system was replaced, the sudden increase in sensor signal cannot alone be
explained by the temperature difference between the existing water in the tank and the
replacement water. The increase in sensor signal was however 30 mV lower when the
water in the tank was replaced with water at the same temperature instead of water at a
lower temperature. The temperature of the water might therefore have some influence on
the signal increase observed after the first replacement.

5.6
Overall Applicability Evaluation
The purpose of deploying the ALVIM biofilm sensor in a drinking water distribution network
was to obtain real-time information about microbial water quality.
The investigations conducted in this thesis project show that the sensor is capable of
detecting the initial biofilm formed on the sensor surface and that the sensor is likely
40
Assessing On-line Sensors for Microbial Drinking Water Quality Monitoring in Distribution Networks
==PAGE:55==
sensitive to the composition of the water in which it is deployed. However, the investigation
conducted with the ALVIM biofilm sensor has also shown that it is not capable of detecting
changes in the microbial water quality when the initial biofilm has been formed, and the
sensor has indicated its presence. Therefore, the sensitivity to microbial water quality
changes is very low, if not zero, once the initial biofilm has formed on the sensor. For the
purpose of detecting microbial water quality changes in drinking water, this is of course
not desirable.
The sensor is, however, sensitive to water quality during the initial phases of biofilm for-
mation. Deteriorated water quality results in faster biofilm formation and biofilm detection.
Using the sensor in Danish drinking water distribution networks for water quality monitor-
ing would require regular cleaning of the sensor surface. However, this goes against the
criteria, for an ideal sensor, proposed by the Danish water suppliers, as they want sensors
with low maintenance requirements. Furthermore, it is not possible, based on the sensor
signal, to obtain real-time information about the microbial water quality which is also one
of the criteria for the ”ideal” sensor.
The overall evaluation is therefore that the ALVIM biofilm sensor is not applicable for real-
time monitoring of microbial drinking water quality in Danish drinking water distribution
networks as the sensor can not detect changes in water quality after the initial biofilm
has formed. Though, it might be a useful tool in other places in the world where drinking
water is disinfected using, e.g., chlorine and biofilms are less abundant in the distribution
network.

Perspectives
Even though Danish water suppliers have described that the ideal sensor for microbial wa-
ter quality monitoring should be measuring indicator organisms such as E. coli, it might be
relevant to investigate technologies targeting proxy parameters. The desire to measure
for the indicator organisms is rooted in the fact that there are set parametric values for
these in current drinking water regulations. Technologies targeting indicator organisms
often require an incubation time, and thereby they are unable to deliver real-time infor-
mation on water quality. Targeting parameters without a need for any incubation might
therefore provide better options for real-time or close to real-time monitoring.
As described in Section 2.1.1 the current legislation on drinking water quality and mon-
itoring does not address the use of sensors for monitoring. This might indicate that the
legislation has not kept up with the technological development as some sensors for phys-
ical and chemical water quality parameters, e.g., temperature, conductivity, and turbid-
ity, have already been developed and implemented by some water suppliers. Suppose
sensor technologies should be implemented on a wide scale for microbial water quality
monitoring. In that case, it is a necessity that the relevant legislation addresses the tech-
nologies and provides guidelines for their use. Regulators need to be open-minded to the
use of new parameters for microbial water quality monitoring and in Denmark, this would
mean that Drikkevandsbekendtgørelsen needs to be adapted to include parametric values
and sensor guidelines for some of these proxy parameters measured with sensors.
The results from this project emphasize the importance of thorough research on tech-
nologies to avoid spending time and resources on investigations that could, based on
in-depth research, have been disregarded for the application case. In this project, one
could imagine that the ALVIM biofilm sensor had not been investigated as a monitoring
tool for microbial water quality, had the manufacturer from the beginning been asked about
the abilities and limitations of the sensor.
Also, based on the results of this project, it is recommended that future research on the
topic focus on investigating the potential of sensors targeting parameters within the wa-
ter phase, and thereby parameters immediately affected by the change in water quality.
Sensors targeting such parameters have higher potential as real-time monitoring tools,
compared to sensors targeting parameters where a delayed response to a contamination
event is observed.

Conclusion
A number of investigations were conducted with the ALVIM biofilm sensor to evaluate
whether it would be applicable for monitoring microbial drinking water quality in Danish
drinking water distribution networks, and it was found that:
• Laboratory and field investigations demonstrated that the ALVIM biofilm sensor is
capable of detecting the initial biofilm formation on the sensitive surface of the sen-
sor. A signal increase indicating biofilm formation above the threshold value was
observed after 2.5 and 4.5 days in the looped flow setup and the flow-through setup,
respectively, and after 9 and 10.5 days for the two sensors in the field.
• Time series analyses of both laboratory and field data revealed indications of daily
variations in the sensor signal, indicating that the sensor might be sensitive to daily
variations in water consumption or other external factors affecting the distributed
water.
• A heterotrophic plate count of a sample collected from the sensor surface did not
prove the presence of biofilm on the sensor after 21 days of deployment in a flow-
through setup. However, based on the sensor signal it was deemed very likely that
biofilm was present on the sensor surface.
• Instantaneously changes in the sensor response were observed when replacing
the water in the tank in the looped system. The change in sensor signal was not
alone caused by the change in temperature. Based on this it was concluded that the
sensor is most likely sensitive to the composition of the water in which it is deployed.
• It was not possible to induce an increase in sensor response by the addition of large
volumes of surface water to the looped flow system. It was therefore concluded that
the sensor is not capable of detecting changes in the biofilm after the initial biofilm
has been formed on the sensor surface and thereby it is incapable of detecting
changes in microbial water quality.
• Through direct correspondence with the sensor manufacturer it was confirmed that
the ALVIM biofilm sensor is not developed for the purpose of detecting changes in
the biofilm once it has formed on the sensor. Instead, the sensor is developed to
simply provide a yes/no answer to whether biofilm is present above the threshold
value.
Overall, the ALVIM biofilm sensor did not meet the requirements listed by Danish water
suppliers for the ideal sensor for real-time monitoring of microbial drinking water quality
in distribution networks.
