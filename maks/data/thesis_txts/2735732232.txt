Abstract
This thesis explores the innovative integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI)-driven
Augmented Reality (AR) technology within a museum setting, focusing on the digitization
and interactive presentation of amulets.
Aimed at enhancing the visitor experience,
this project leverages AR to create immersive, interactive narratives that bridge the gap
between historical artifacts and the digital age. By digitizing amulets—objects with rich
cultural and historical significance—the thesis demonstrates how AR can be used to
engage museum visitors in an informative and captivating exploration of cultural heritage.
Central to this exploration is the development of a robust AR application that employs
AI for real-time interaction, including gesture recognition and detailed visualization of the
amulets. This application not only allows visitors to interact with digital replicas of the
amulets in ways that physical limitations previously prohibited but also provides contextual
information, thereby enriching the educational value of museum exhibits.
The methodology encompasses a multi-disciplinary approach, combining 3D scanning
and modeling techniques for digitization, AI for enhancing interaction, and user experience
design principles to ensure accessibility and engagement. The project’s iterative design
process, informed by continuous integration and deployment (CI/CD) practices, highlights
the adaptability and user-centered focus of the development strategy.
This thesis contributes to the field by showcasing the potential of AI and AR technologies
in cultural heritage settings, offering insights into their implementation challenges,
user engagement strategies, and educational impacts.
The findings indicate that
AR can significantly enhance the museum experience, offering new opportunities for
engagement, learning, and accessibility. As museums continue to seek innovative ways
to connect with audiences, the integration of AI-driven AR presents a promising avenue
for enriching cultural education and preserving the legacy of historical artifacts for future
generations.

Introduction
In the dynamic realm of cultural institutions, the National Museum of Denmark
(Nationalmuseet) stands out as a fusion of tradition and exciting new endeavors. As it
embarks on a transformative journey towards a major rebuild and redesign, the museum
is set to redefine its infrastructure for a future-ready experience.
Gone are the days
when museums were merely static displays of historical artefacts within the confines
of brick and mortar. The contemporary vision is a more immersive one, where visitors
can experience and connect with history in new ways. This delicate balance between
innovation and tradition promises to usher in a new era for the museum. To achieve this,
the integration of cutting-edge technologies is essential, yet it must be done in harmony
with the museum’s traditional design and the established visitor behaviors.
At the heart of this project is the development of an interactive feature I have designed
and referred to as the ”magic mirror.” This feature leverages Unity, a powerful game
development engine renowned for its flexibility and capacity for creating immersive
environments.
The magic mirror acts as a bridge, connecting the tangible world of
historical artifacts with the expansive possibilities of digital augmentation.
Employing
sophisticated real-time background segmentation technology, this setup places museum
visitors within an interactive, augmented reality environment where digitized amulets
become focal points of a unique educational narrative, which adds an extra layer to the
existing traditional museum displays. However, the museum does not seek to replace the
more traditional displays.
The concept of the magic mirror is pivotal to this thesis. It’s designed to engage visitors in
a dynamic way, transforming their approach to the display equipped with a high-definition
camera. The moment they step into its view, they’re transported through a visually striking
portal effect into a meticulously crafted augmented space, where they see themselves in
a medieval environment. In this space, the distinction between physical reality and digital
augmentation blurs, inviting visitors into a realm where they can interact with cultural and
historical narratives through the exploration of virtual amulets.
For this project, the museum curators and I selected five amulets from the museum
collection, each with its own distinct cultural significance and backstory. This selection
was driven by a commitment to not only preserve but revitalize the way museum artifacts
are engaged with. Moving beyond the limitations of traditional display methods, digitizing
these amulets for an AR setting opens up new avenues for interaction and exploration of
object details.
Delving into the technicalities, this thesis navigates the confluence of technology and
cultural preservation, addressing the engineering challenges inherent in AR development
within a museum context. Through a comprehensive examination of software engineering
principles, AI algorithms, and the intricacies of Unity-based development, I endeavor to
showcase how AR can be utilised in museum setting.
The magic mirror concept invites visitors on an interactive journey, prompting them to
engage with virtual amulets and delve into the stories they embody.
This interactive
experience seeks not only to educate but also to forge a deeper connection with our
cultural history, demonstrating the compelling power of AR to bridge the gap between
the past and the present. This is different from a traditional experience, where artifacts

are are kept out of reach from museum visitors in glass cases. Instead, the installation
becomes - quite literally - a reinterpretation of a ”hands-on” experience.
This project aspires to redefine museum engagement, illustrating how technology can
serve as a transformative bridge between historical artifacts and contemporary audiences.
It underscores the significant role of AR in creating meaningful connections with our
shared heritage, offering innovative ways for visitors to explore and understand cultural
artifacts.
By contributing to the evolving landscape of museum exhibitions, this initiative highlights
the potential of cutting-edge technologies to foster a more profound understanding of
our shared human history.
As the National Museum of Denmark embraces a period
of significant transformation, this project symbolizes a commitment to modern and
strategic use of technology to craft a new narrative for cultural engagement in the digital
age. Addressing the complexities of technology selection, method experimentation, and
system integration, this thesis underscores the intricate technical challenges involved,
paving the way for future innovations in museum experiences.

Related Work
In this chapter, I examine existing literature and projects relevant to the integration of AI
and AR technologies in museum environments and their application in enhancing visitor
engagement and educational outcomes. This review covers studies on object recognition,
tracking, augmented reality experiences, and user interaction within digital and physical
spaces. The chapter serves to position my work within the current body of research,
identifying gaps my project aims to address and demonstrating how my approach builds
upon and diverges from previous efforts in the field. However, additional to the overview in
this chapter, I will refer to other related work in subfields of Museology, Computer Vision,
Machine Learning and Software Engineering where relevant to the content in the next
chapter on methodologies.
The convergence of Augmented Reality (AR) and Adaptive Augmented Reality (A2R) with
museum experiences heralds a transformative era in visitor engagement, education, and
personalization. This chapter explores seminal contributions to AR and A2R within cultural
heritage, underscoring the potential these technologies hold for redefining museum
visits. Moreover, it delves into advancements in real-world object augmentation in virtual
environments, as demonstrated by Shadman Shahriar Hasan Arko et al. [1], highlighting
the synergistic potential of these technologies to enrich museum experiences further.
The advent of AR in cultural settings has significantly impacted how visitors interact with
and learn from museum exhibits. For instance, the Mirracle system [2] showcases the
potential of AR to superimpose educational content onto physical realities, enriching the
visitor’s learning experience. This ability of AR to blend digital information with the physical
world sets the foundation for more immersive explorations within museum contexts.
Gesture-based interaction within AR interfaces, as highlighted by Billinghurst et al.
[3], introduces an intuitive method for visitors to engage with augmented content.
This approach aligns with contemporary expectations for interactive museum visits,
emphasizing the shift from passive observation to active participation.
Building on these foundations, Adaptive Augmented Reality (A2R), as discussed by
Damala et al. [4], represents a further evolution by tailoring the visitor experience through
dynamic content adjustment based on real-time feedback. This adaptability promises not
only to personalize educational narratives and interactive content but also to foster deeply
personalized and memorable museum experiences.
In addition to these advancements, the recent work by Shadman Shahriar Hasan Arko
et al.
[1] proposes a novel approach for augmenting real-world objects in virtual 3D
environments using deep neural networks and game engines. Their system facilitates
real-time augmentation of real-world objects into a virtual environment, leveraging Unity
and RealSense SDK for acquisition, processing, and visualization with VR. This approach
opens new avenues for creating mixed reality applications that solve real-life problems,
suggesting a potent application in museum contexts for enhancing visitor interactions with
exhibits through accurate and real-time 3D model augmentation.
Integrating the insights from Shadman Shahriar Hasan Arko et al. [1] with AR and A2R
technologies presents a comprehensive framework for enhancing museum experiences.
By leveraging deep learning for skeleton tracking and hybrid hand and body tracking
algorithms, museums can offer visitors a more immersive, engaging, and personalized
3
==PAGE:10==
exploration of exhibits. This fusion of technologies not only enhances the educational
value of museum visits but also cultivates a deeper emotional connection with the exhibits,
promoting a more profound understanding of cultural heritage.
Due to the interdisciplinary aspects of the project, I will also be incorporating the more
general museological discussion on transmedial narratives within the context of museum
experiences, particularly with reference to Moesgaard Museum’s innovative approach, as
this further enriches the understanding of the potential of digital experiments in museum
settings.
Along with The National Museum of Denmark, Moesgaard Museum in Aarhus represents
the Danish cultural history centering around displays of objects central to the fields of
archaeology and anthropology. Moesgaard is an interesting case, because it was able to
experiment with new approaches to the use of technology when the museum re-opened
in a new building in 2014. Using Moesgaard as a case study, Tosca [5] (2016) delve
into the concept of transmedial museum experiences, which encapsulate the aesthetic
interaction between users and a synthesis of historical artifacts, informative labels, and
fictional narratives across diverse media platforms. This approach signifies a shift toward
immersive and multifaceted visitor engagement, underscoring the pivotal role of digital
technologies in crafting compelling narratives around museum exhibits.
The methodologies employed by Moesgaard Museum, as elucidated by Tosca [5],
demonstrate how exhibitions of cultural history artifacts leverage a combination of
tangible objects and digital augmentations to captivate audiences. These transmedial
narratives not only engage visitors on sensory and emotional levels but also highlight the
transformative impact of digital storytelling on the educational value of museum visits.
I have chosen to highlight this more general museological study as related work, because
I was advised by the museum curators of the National Museum of Denmark to visit
Moesgaard Museum in the initial research phase of the project. My direct experience at
Moesgaard Museum allowed for an observational exploration of these narratives analysed
by Tosca [5] in practice. This immersive experience provided deeper insights into how
digital storytelling techniques, when integrated with traditional exhibit displays, create an
enriched, educational, and engaging visitor experience. The strategic combination of
historical fidelity with creative narratives at Moesgaard offered profound insights into the
effectiveness of transmedial experiences in bridging the divide between cultural heritage
and contemporary audience engagement. However, I also learned that the technologies
used were mostly video/audio-based, and that the immersive tactile design of the physical
surroundings - sometimes being non-digital optical illusions - had a huge impact on how
advanced the museum design appeared to the visitors. I learned from the visit, that a fairly
simple construction might still create immense impact. Tosca, [5] thus provide a relevant
framework for understanding the intricate balance between technology, storytelling, and
cultural preservation. It is vital that I understand this museological context, if I wish to
create a properly engineered solution where less might still be more.
Drawing from both the theoretical exploration by Tosca [5] and my experiential
observations, this thesis aims to explore the potential of AI-driven augmented reality
in creating transmedial experiences within museum exhibitions at The National Museum
of Denmark that are contextually similar to those of Moesgaard Museum (but older
in their design).
The model exemplified by Moesgaard Museum is emphasizing the
value of employing digital technologies to forge immersive, educational narratives that
resonate with modern audiences while still preserving the essence of cultural heritage.
Moreover, the incorporation of transmedial narratives, as exemplified by my observations

at Moesgaard Museum and illuminated through the work of Tosca [5], underscores the
profound impact digital storytelling can have in bridging the gap between historical artifacts

To summerize, it is evident that the integration of AI and AR technologies within museum
environments presents a promising avenue for reimagining how visitors interact with
and learn from exhibits. The discussions presented here, spanning from the immersive
capabilities of AR to the personalized experiences offered by A2R, and the creative

rich opportunity for innovation. This thesis contributes to an ongoing dialogue, aiming to
explore and perhaps expand the scope of how museums can utilize technology not just

Methodology and Implementation
In this chapter, I present the process of integrating AI-driven AR technology into a museum
setting for amulet digitization and interaction. The methodology includes 3D scanning and
modeling for artifact digitization, applying AI for interactive enhancements, and employing
user experience design principles for ease of use and accessibility. The chapter details the
conceptual framework, software architecture, background segmentation challenges, and
interaction mechanisms. The focus is on the technical execution and solutions employed
to develop an educational AR experience that connects museum visitors with historical
artifacts through digital means.
3.1
Conceptualization
The conceptualization phase of this project was meticulously guided by the Double
Diamond theory [6], a design thinking framework that promotes a structured approach
to problem-solving through divergent and convergent thinking.
This methodological
framework facilitated the exploration of ideas and the identification of innovative solutions,
particularly in the context of developing an Augmented Reality (AR) experience for the
National Museum of Denmark.
Figure 3.1: Double Diamond Model. [6] ©Wikipedia
Understanding the Problem Space (Discover)
The initiation of the Double Diamond began with a focus on divergent thinking to
thoroughly understand the complexity of the challenge at hand. The initial step involved
immersing myself in the museum’s ecosystem, engaging in extensive discussions with
stakeholders, which mainly involved conducting interviews with the museum experts
through a series of meetings. This collaborative effort aimed to gain diverse perspectives
on the goals, challenges, and aspirations of the museum in the context of modernizing its
exhibition experiences.
By applying divergent thinking in this phase, a comprehensive understanding of the
problem space emerged. It became clear that there was a desire for a more engaging and
contemporary museum experience that leveraged advanced technology while preserving
the essence of cultural artifacts. Museums are traditionally about the objects at display,
and the museum wanted to preserve that, while at the same time incorporating more
playfulness and discovery into the museum experience.
7
==PAGE:14==
Defining the Scope and Objectives (Explore, Define)
Following the divergent understanding of the problem space, the project’s scope and
objectives were defined.
Convergent thinking was employed to narrow down the
possibilities, ensuring that the project aligned with the broader vision of the museum
while addressing specific pain points.
The decision to explore the integration of
AI-driven augmented reality specifically within an amulet exhibition was made during this
phase. This choice sought to strike a balance between technological innovation, cultural
engagement, and educational impact.
Ideation and Exploration (Develop, Test)
The second phase of the Double Diamond introduced another round of divergent thinking,
encouraging the generation of a plethora of ideas. Collaborative brainstorming sessions
and workshops with experts in museum curation fostered an environment of creativity and
exploration. The concept of a ”magic mirror” emerged as a focal point during this phase,
symbolizing the seamless integration of traditional artifacts with cutting-edge technology.
This concept promised an immersive and interactive experience for museum visitors,
aligning with the overarching goals of the project.
To better communicate the idea to the stakeholders and user, a storyboard was created
to show the various phases of the experience.
Refinement and Alignment (Deliver, Listen)
With a wealth of ideas generated during the ideation phase, most quickly scrapped due
to being impractical in a museum setting, ie. AR headsets, the project then moved into
a phase of convergent thinking. Iterative feedback loops and in-depth discussions were
employed to refine and align the proposed solutions. The concept of utilizing Unity[7]
for game development, integrating real-time background segmentation, and employing
AI hand landmark tracking emerged as key components.
This phase of the Double
Diamond ensured that the proposed solutions were not only innovative and engaging
but also technically feasible and aligned with the strategic objectives of the Museum.
The storyboard, representing the user interactions, became a crucial deliverable in this
phase.
It helped refine and align the proposed solutions.
By visualizing each state
of interaction, I, in collaboration with museum representatives, can identify potential
challenges, improvements, and refine the design to ensure it meets the project objectives
and enhances user engagement.
By embracing the Double Diamond theory, the conceptualization phase of this project
unfolded in a systematic, iterative, and thorough manner. The framework provided a
structured pathway to navigate the complexities of the challenge, fostering creativity,
collaboration, and a nuanced understanding of the problem space.
This robust
conceptual foundation laid the groundwork for the subsequent phases of development
and implementation, ensuring a thoughtful and purposeful progression of the project.
3.1.1
Final concept
Since the experience was going to be an AR experience developed in the Unity game
engine, I will from now on refer to the AR experience as the AR game, or simply, the
game. The goals for the AR game were established at the outset in collaboration with a
panel of domain experts, of whom are also representatives of the museum; an Exhibition
Editor with expertise in exhibitions and user experience (UX), a researcher and history
expert, and an expert on digital installations. These goals were informed by the collective
expertise and understanding of the museum’s objectives and are as follows:
• Simplicity and ease of use: Given that museum visitors encompass a wide range
of ages and backgrounds, it was imperative to design the AR game in a way that
does not assume technological proficiency. The goal was to ensure that the AR
8
==PAGE:15==
experience is intuitive and quick to engage with, preventing the formation of queues
and allowing guests ample time to explore the rest of the exhibition.
• Informative: The educational mission of the museum made it essential for the AR
game to be informative. The aim is to engage visitors effectively, preserve and
convey cultural heritage, enhance understanding of the exhibits, shape positive
perceptions, and facilitate a broader educational outreach.
• Focus on museum artifacts: The experts unanimously agreed on the importance
of centering the experience around the museum’s artifacts. This focus is crucial
for preserving the cultural significance of the exhibits, conveying specific historical
contexts and superstitions, and offering a deep, thematic understanding of the
artifacts to the visitors.
• Experimental: Embracing innovation and experimentation was highlighted as key
to modernizing the museum’s offerings. This goal aligns with creating a dynamic
exhibition space that attracts a diverse audience and demonstrates the museum’s
commitment to cutting-edge technology, ensuring its relevance in today’s cultural
landscape.
• Touch-free: In addition, the museum had experienced challenges in the wake of
the pandemic with the use of technological equipment (audio guides, headsets
etc.), especially if it had to be used by many people, and they wanted to avoid that.
Therefore, interaction without actual touch would provide a groundbreaking solution
to such issues.
The collaboration with experts not only validated these goals but also enriched the
conceptualization process of the AR game. Their insights helped shape a comprehensive
approach that blends user-friendly design with educational content, while also leveraging
AR technology to highlight and preserve the uniqueness of the museum’s artifacts. This
collaborative effort has ensured that the AR game aligns closely with the museum’s
mission, paving the way for an engaging, informative, and innovative exhibition
experience.
The final concept attempts to support these points in various ways. The game is to be
interactive with as a mirror with the museum objects ”floating” in the air. Users are to
interact with the objects via hand tracking and gestures. This way, the focus is immediately
on the amulets. However, satisfying the requirement of ease of use, will be a challenge,
so there must be a user experience focus including user tests from the earliest prototype
onwards. Therefore, I will apply the software engineering methodology of continuous
integration and continuous delivery (CI/CD), where prototypes and adjustments are rapid
and user tested often.
The museum also had a wish for the game to teach users about amulets. The installation
will be supplemented with external text-based information close by about the amulets,
and the experience must support that information. In this case there will be 5 amulets
showcased, each with a specific superstition tied to them.
The five amulets are already part of the existing museums archive or exhibition, and they
must be digitized in order for users to interact with them in the experience. Usually objects
are out of reach for museum visitors to preserve the cultural heritage from damage, but
with this technology, users are able to virtually approach and interact with the objects.
I will now walk through the storyboard and explain each step. Each of these steps can be

selected amulet is centered on the screen. Positive visual effects are shown if the

wrong amulet is selected, negative visual effects appear and the game returns to
the previous state after some time. The user can then select a different amulet.

low-power standby state, ready to restart the experience from the first step when a
hand is detected.
The aim is for the whole experience from start to finish will be 60 −90 seconds. One big
challenge from a UX standpoint is that mostly all communication will be done with visual
effects. The museum wanted the game to stay ”magical” with no explicit text or verbal
communication. Any language communication would also be further complicated by a
strict localization requirement of 3 languages. However, physical text communication
will be located close to the monitor to explain the various amulets and corresponding
superstitions, so users will have to chance to know which amulet is the correct poison
curing-amulet before trying the game. In the next sections, I will further concretize these
game states and investigate technologies needed to bring this magical mirror to life.

3.2
Camera Feed and Background Segmentation
Within the realm of augmented reality, the effectiveness of the immersive experience
hinges on robust background segmentation - a fundamental aspect that isolates users
from their surroundings. This section delves into the technical intricacies of background
segmentation, exploring diverse methods that range from traditional computer vision
techniques to contemporary deep learning approaches.
The objective is to evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of each segmentation method,
paving the way for an informed decision on the most suitable approach for real-time
implementation within the Unity framework. This exploration extends beyond theoretical
considerations, involving hands-on experimentation by implementing one of the identified
segmentation methods directly into the Unity environment.

Methodology Overview

Large segmentation model
During my search for a model with higher precision, I found a pre-trained ResNet50 model,
which is used as the encoder with dilated convolutions as the bridge and a simple decoder.
ResNet50, short for Residual Network with 50 layers, is a deep convolutional neural
network architecture designed to address the challenge of training very deep neural
networks. ResNet50 was introduced by Kaiming He et al[15].
ResNet50 is a deep neural network architecture designed to train very deep networks
effectively. It introduces residual blocks with skip connections, enabling the direct flow of
gradients during training. This addresses the vanishing gradient problem and allows the
network to learn hierarchical features. The bottleneck design enhances computational
efficiency, and global average pooling replaces fully connected layers. Pre-training on
ImageNet provides a starting point for tasks.
Overall, ResNet50’s innovation lies in
facilitating the training of deep networks by incorporating skip connections and residual
learning.
The specific ResNet50 model I used for this experiment is accessible from the GitHub
repository by Nikhil Rox Tomar [16], and was specifically chosen for its demonstrated
effectiveness in background removal tasks. Experimenting with this model also serves
as an experiment with semantic and hybrid segmentation, since the model trained
as a machine learning model, but the actual segmentation is done using semantic
segmentation. The core strength of this model lies in its training on a comprehensive
and robust dataset, hosted on Kaggle and also curated by Nikhil Rox Tomar [17]. The
”Person Segmentation” dataset encompasses a wide array of images tailored for the task
of segmenting human figures from diverse backgrounds. The dataset’s variety and depth
have been instrumental in the model’s ability to generalize well across different scenarios,
making it well suited for this task of using it in an AR game.

3.2.3
Depth Segmentation
The concluding segmentation method investigated in this thesis is depth-based using a
depth-map [10]. A typical 2D RGB video feed can undergo cropping in both the x and y
directions, where such cropping alters the frame width or height accordingly. However, in
the presence of frame depth information, the frames may theoretically be cropped in the
z-direction, resulting in a modified foreground or background, contingent upon the chosen
direction.
To undertake this operation, it is imperative to initially employ a methodology for acquiring
depth information.
Several approaches exist for this purpose.
Notably, substantial
advancements have recently transpired in the application of Deep Learning (DL) models
for depth estimation. Nevertheless, it is noteworthy that I did not investigate these DL
estimation methodologies in the context of this project.
Instead, at the midpoint of the project, access was granted access to a depth camera,
specifically the Intel RealSense R435i.
This camera incorporates two RGB cameras
and employs a stereo matching algorithm [18] to compare frames in real-time, thereby
estimating depth.
• RGB Camera 1 and RGB Camera 2: These are two cameras capturing color
information in the RGB spectrum. They are physically placed with a few centimers
in between, allowing for similar images but with a slightly different angle, similar to
human eyes.
• IR Emitter: An Infrared (IR) emitter. The emitter projects an pattern of infrared light

Acquiring Camera Feed
A staight forward method to acquire a webcam feed in Unity is with Unity’s own API
GetWebCamDevice and GetWebCamTexture classes. That handles everything from getting
the frame data, converting the data to a Unity Texture2D object and synchronizing the
camera frames with the engines rendering frame time. However, the API also assumes
a regular RGB device and not a depth camera and as such will not work on the depth
camera.
Fortunately, Intel provides are the RealSense SDK 2.0[19], which includes a wrapper for
Unity, and the framework provides the user low-level control of the camera’s captured
data.
Installing the SDK wrapper in the Unity project is not as straight forward as
installing a regular package from the Unity Asset Store. The proces involves building
custom dependencies using CMAKE with specific parameters depending on the platform,
versions etc.
and setting up a number of scripts.
I followed the readme guide on
RealSense SDK’s Github page. [19]
After installing the SDK, I can finally communicate with the depth camera directly from
Unity. The first step in the data pipeline for the wrapper is RSDevice game object, where
one can set up profiles in accordance to what kind of frame data is needed from the
camera. In my case, I need two profiles, one for the depth channel and one the regular
RGB frames.

1920x1080 for the RGB output and 848x480 for the depth frames. I still type in 1280x720

For the RGB frames, a pixel-wise operation is applied at a specific game state to represent
a poisoning effect on the user. The goal is to apply a green hue that is clearly visible to
the user, while having minimal computational cost. I do this by adding a green scalar to
the image with a varying intensity based on a wave function. This creates a pulsing green
hue effect that hopefully communicates to the user, that the over-saturation of green in
the image is intentional and not just a display or camera error.
The algorithm applies the green hue to the frame, with the intensity of the green hue based
on the sine function of a variable time. The key parts of the algorithm can be described
as follows:
1. Green Hue Intensity Calculation: The intensity of the green hue added to the
image is determined by the absolute value of the sine of a variable time, denoted
as | sin(time)|. This value is then scaled by 100 to determine the intensity of the
green color to be added.
2. Application of Green Hue: The method creates a Scalar representing the color to
be added to the image. This scalar has the form (0, 100 · | sin(time)|, 0), where the
second component (green channel) is modified based on the calculated intensity,
and the other components (red and blue channels) are left unchanged (zero in this
case).
3. Thresholding to Ensure Pixel Values Do Not Exceed Maximum: Due to the
8-bit size of the green channel of the image, the value must be limited to ensure
no overflow occurs. After adding the green hue, the method applies a thresholding
operation to ensure that pixel values do not exceed the maximum allowed value

Segmentation Shader
The custom shader BGSeg.shader is designed for handling background segmentation.
The downside doing the segmentation in a shader is the low level graphics application.
Since the actual segmentation is done on the GPU right before displaying the frames,
there is no way to extract the mask for use anywhere else in the application. However,
this is a very efficient method to perform the segmentation, leaving the very busy CPU to
do other tasks the GPU cannot do.

The shader operates in a transparent rendering queue, indicating it is intended for
rendering translucent objects. It uses a specific rendering pass with custom vertex and
fragment functions. The fragment function computes the final color and transparency for
each pixel.
In the fragment function, it reads the depth value from _MainTex and converts it to meters
using the provided depth scale. It then applies gamma correction to the color information
from _ColorTex. The final output color is a combination of the color information and a
mask value.
The mask is computed based on the depth information, with a smoothstep function
to create a gradual transition between the minimum and maximum depth ranges, with
additional feathering. This mask is then used to control the transparency of the object.
If the object is within the specified depth range, it contributes to the final output with its
color; otherwise, it becomes more transparent.
Unity Raw Image UV scaling
The museum had a preference that fewer people can be part of the AR experience
simultaneously. To that end, we decided to limit the amount of vertical space from the
camera feed. The incoming camera frames, the target monitor are both are in 16 : 9
aspect ratio, so the camera feed perfectly fits the screen naively. The museum wanted
a squared video feed, resulting in a requirement to crop the camera feed in width to a
squared 1 : 1 aspect ratio. There is no direct method to do that in the Unity interface.
When I set the video display rectangle to 1080x1080 and the textures are in 1920x1080,
the textures with logically be squished horizontally when displayed. To undo this image
squishing, I then modify the rect’s UV coordinates.
A theoretically even better alternative would be to request a 1 : 1 video feed directly
from the camera, as the extra width is going through the data pipeline a wasted pixels.
However, the camera does not support such resolutions and setting them will simply result
in no frames received. Nevertheless, this achieves the goal of a cropped video feed when
displayed in the scene for practically no performance penalty.

3.3.1
Hand Tracking Method
The use of hand tracking is integral to the project’s interactive narrative, where the
precise positioning and articulation of a user’s hands play a central role in navigating
the augmented reality environment. I explore the integration of MediaPipe’s[14] (MP)
advanced hand tracking capabilities, emphasizing its significance in augmenting user
interactions and contributing to the overall immersive quality of the museum experience.
During the development phase, I considered various AI tracking methods. However, MP
emerged as the preferred choice due to its exceptional speed, making it the first viable
option capable of running in real-time. This section will not only unravel the technical
aspects of implementing MP hand tracking with landmarks but also shed light on the
decision-making process and the exploration of alternative AI tracking methods. The
successful incorporation of this technology represents a critical milestone in the project,
enabling the development of a responsive and engaging user interface that seamlessly
intertwines the physical and virtual realms.
The process of using MP in Unity involves several steps:
1. Hand Detection:
• The system begins by detecting the presence of a hand in the input image
or video frame.
It utilizes a convolutional neural network (CNN) to identify
potential hand regions.
2. Hand Landmark Localization:
• Once a hand is detected, the system employs another neural network to
precisely localize 21 specific landmarks on the hand.
These landmarks
represent key points such as fingertips, knuckles, and the palm.
3. Neural Network Architecture:
• MP’s hand landmark tracking relies on a deep neural network architecture,
often based on convolutional neural networks (CNNs) or similar structures.
These networks are trained on large datasets to learn the spatial relationships
between different hand landmarks.
4. Landmark Coordinate Estimation:
• The neural network outputs a set of coordinates for each of the 21 hand
landmark in the 2D image space. These coordinates represent the position

Using predicted landmarks in Unity space
When I have the hand landmark positions for each frame inside the Unity development
environment, the next task is to convert MP’s predicted coordinates to Unity space
positions, so the user can interact with the game objects in the Unity scene. To contain
the logic for that, I created the method UpdateHandPoints.
The method takes a list of NormalizedLandmarkList objects, representing the detected
hand landmarks, as an input parameter and performs the following steps:
Iteration Over Detected Hands and Landmarks:
The method iterates over each
detected hand in the handsLandmarks list. For each hand, it updates the last update
time for that hand in the _lastUpdateTimes dictionary.
Unity hand prefab: During the game initialization, I instantiate a X of hand prefabs. X
being the number of hands MP is set to look for. The prefab contains 21 sphere game
objects with colliders, corresponding to the 21 hand landmarks from MP. The er spheres
are connected with line renderers. All renderers for the hand prefab are initially disabled,
but can be enabled to visualize the hands to make debugging easier. The 21 spheres
will then be updated every frame that MP has a new prediction, and the spheres relative
position to each other will then form a virtual hand in Unity, corresponding to the persons
hand in reality.
Updating Hand Landmarks’ Positions: For each landmark in the current hand, it
extracts the normalized coordinates (X, Y, Z). The screen position is calculated in pixels
based on these normalized coordinates.
The Z-coordinate of the screen position is
adjusted based on the normalized Z-coordinate and a depth scale factor. Finally, the
position of the child transform under the corresponding hand game object is updated using
Camera.main.ScreenToWorldPoint to convert the screen position to world coordinates.
SetHandPointDepth Method
I created another method to update the hand prefab with depth data from the depth
camera. Since the user only interacts with the game on a 2D display frome distance,
I only want the virtual hands to move in a plane. As such I do not want to update hand
positions with the depth data. However, I still want to pass on hand depth data to the hand
prefab to be used for other logic in the game.
For this purpose, I created SetHandPointDepth, method, which takes a RealSense
DepthFrame object as an input parameter and performs the following steps:
1. Unity Rect Definition: A variable rectSize is set to 7, representing half the size
of a Unity rect (14x14). This value is used to check if hand points are within the
rectangle.
2. Hand Point Iteration: The method iterates through each point in the _handPoints
array. For each hand point, the x and y coordinates are obtained from its transform
28
==PAGE:35==
position in the 14x14 rect.
3. Rect Bounds Check: The method checks if the hand point is outside the bounds of
the Unity rect (+-7 from the center). If the point is outside, the method returns early.
4. Wrist Point Calculation: The x and y coordinates of the wrist point (assumed to
be the first point in the _wristPoints array) in the 14x14 rect are obtained.
5. Rect Center Calculation: The center coordinates of the rect are calculated as the
average of the hand and wrist coordinates.
6. Origin Shift: The coordinates are shifted so that the origin is at the top-left corner
of the rect.
7. Normalization: The shifted coordinates are normalized to a range between 0 and
1.
8. Pixel Scaling: The normalized coordinates are scaled to the pixel size of the input
frame.
9. Depth Retrieval: The depth value is obtained from the frame at the calculated pixel
coordinates using DepthFrame GetDistance(x, y).
10. Depth Set: If the retrieved depth value is non-zero, it is assigned to the pointDepth
property of the current hand point.
Gesture Recognition
Able to move virtual hands in the Unity scene, the final step to make interaction complete
within my AR game is implementing a method for gesture recognition. I decided to go with
a simple method based on the relative euclidean distance between the hand points. The
first gesture I implemented was a closed hand grabbing gesture. Then after UX testing
the first prototype, I discovered a need for two more gestures, pinching and pointing.
Referencing figure 3.9, the hand points are each labeled with a number. IsGrabbing
method is used to determine if a virtual hand is in a grabbing state.
• First, the method retrieves the positions of various points on the hand: the wrist(0),
the base of the middle finger(9), the tips of the index(8), middle(12), ring(16), and
pinky(20) fingers. These positions are represented as Vector3 objects, which are
3D vectors to represent points in 3D space.
• The method then calculates the distances from the wrist(0) to each of these points.
• The method checks if each finger (except the thumb) is ”close” to the wrist. The
definition of ”close” is based on the distance from the wrist to the base of the middle
finger, multiplied by a threshold and a constant factor. The constant factor is different
for each finger, presumably to account for the fact that different fingers have different
lengths and different ranges of motion.
• If all fingers are ”close” to the hand origin according to these criteria, the method
returns true, indicating that the hand is in a grabbing state. If any finger is not
”close”, it returns false.
• The exact values of the threshold and the constant factors has been tuned based
on UX testing and a desired sensitivity of the grab detection.

Gaussian Splatting
Gaussian Splatting[21], as described in the provided document, is a technique used for
real-time radiance field rendering that leverages 3D Gaussians to represent scenes. This
method combines the desirable properties of continuous volumetric radiance fields for
scene optimization with the efficiency of avoiding unnecessary computations in empty
space. The 3D Gaussians start from sparse points produced during camera calibration
and optimize various properties (e.g., 3D position, opacity, anisotropic covariance, and
spherical harmonic coefficients) in a process interleaved with adaptive density control
steps. This approach facilitates the creation of a compact, precise scene representation
that supports fast, visibility-aware rendering algorithm. This algorithm accommodates
anisotropic splatting, which accelerates both the training process and real-time rendering.
The result is a state-of-the-art visual quality for captured scenes with competitive training
times, enabling high-quality real-time (≥30 fps) novel-view synthesis at 1080p resolution .
More than be read about Gaussian Splatting in their official paper [21].

The Holding State
The holding state manages the behavior of an amulet when it is being grabbed by a hand
in a Unity scene. Upon entering the grab state, it prepares the amulet by disabling certain

movements.

3.5.3
Multithreading and runtime performance
Unity primarily runs on a single thread known as the main thread, which is responsible
for executing game logic, updating the scene, handling user input, and rendering
graphics. This single-threaded architecture is tied to the Monobehavior class and ensures
consistency and predictability in the game’s behavior, simplifying development and
debugging. However, because all these tasks must be completed within a single frame’s
timeframe (usually 16.67 milliseconds for a 60 FPS game), any computationally intensive
or blocking operations executed on the main thread can lead to frame drops, stuttering,
or unresponsive gameplay.
Without multithreading, this game could not run at a high, stable frame-rate on the target
PC, severely deteriorating the user experience. Therefore, it is crucial that I offloaded
high-computational tasks which did not need to run on the main thread. For this game,
apparent tasks to offload are the AR specific tasks such as hand tracking and the
camera feed. By utilizing multithreading or asynchronous operations, such tasks can be
processed concurrently with the main thread’s execution, improving overall performance
and ensuring a smooth player experience without disrupting gameplay or rendering.
These are some of the tasks I parallelised and how I did it:
• Depth camera: A separate thread will be used to raise device frames.
This
functionality is configured through the Realsense SDK.
• Hand tracking: Hand handmark tracking is offloaded to a seperat thread.
• Post processing: The camera post processing is also offloaded.
• RGB to RGBA conversion: Multithreading occurs when the RGB data from the
RealSense camera is converted to RGBA format. This conversion is parallelised
using Parallel.For, which divides the workload among multiple threads to process
the data concurrently.
With this multithreading implemented, the result a smooth and consistent frame-rate on
the target PC, albeit with all threads being very busy at near 100% utilization.

sequences, could offer a more automated and potentially accurate method of capturing
the intricate details of artifacts, reducing the time and expertise required in the manual
modeling process. The benefit for this project would be limited, as amulet objects for this
project were small and with relatively simple geometry. However, had the project called
for digitizing larger and more complex model, using COLMAP or similar to acquire the
model geometry would have been a logical approach.
Another innovative approach that holds potential beyond the initial scope of this project
is the application of Gaussian splatting (GS) for scanning entire scenes, not just specific
artifacts. GS could be remarkably effective for creating immersive AR environments that
encompass the broader context in which artifacts exist.
Employing GS to capture and digitize entire museum settings or historical sites could
revolutionize the AR experience, enabling users to explore not only individual objects but
also their original or reconstructed environments. This method involves projecting 3D
Gaussians into a scene to model its continuous radiance field, capturing both the spatial
and photometric properties of the space. When applied to entire scenes, GS could provide
a highly detailed, photorealistic 3D representation of complex environments, enhancing
the depth and realism of the AR experience.
The adoption of this technique for scene digitization in AR could offer several advantages.
It would allow for a more holistic presentation of artifacts within their cultural or historical
context, offering users a comprehensive understanding of their significance. Moreover, it
could significantly reduce the time and expertise required to model complex environments
manually, offering a more efficient pathway to generating detailed AR content.
However, the application of Gaussian splatting at this scale would also present new
challenges, particularly in terms of computational demands and the management of
large datasets.
Optimizing this technique for efficient processing and integration
into AR platforms would require significant technical innovation, potentially involving
advancements in hardware and software optimization to handle the intricate data derived
from entire scenes.

5.3
Alternative segmentation methodology
The segmentation method chosen, primarily based on depth information from the Intel
RealSense camera, demonstrated effective background isolation for AR integration. Yet,
the exploration of semantic segmentation using deep learning could offer improved
accuracy in distinguishing between the user and complex backgrounds, especially in
scenarios where depth information might be less reliable due to lighting conditions or the
physical environment. Implementing semantic segmentation could, however, introduce
challenges related to computational efficiency and real-time processing, necessitating
further research into optimizing deep learning models for AR applications.
While using the depth cameras data for depth segmentation, has proven effective in
achieving background isolation for augmented reality (AR) integration, there is room
for innovation through the adoption of a hybrid approach. Specifically, the potential of
replacing the depth camera with a machine learning (ML) model to generate depth maps
presents a possibility for simplification and enhancement of the hardware setup. The
recent introduction of a model known as ”Depth Anything” could be instrumental in this
regard, offering the capability to extract depth information using standard webcams. This
advancement could drastically reduce the hardware complexity by eliminating the need
for specialized depth cameras, thereby broadening the applicability of the AR application
to include any camera-equipped device.
The prospect of a hybrid segmentation approach,
combining the strengths of
ML-generated depth maps with semantic segmentation, holds the promise of not only
simplifying the hardware requirements but also enhancing segmentation accuracy. Such
an approach would leverage deep learning to more effectively distinguish between the
user and complex backgrounds across a variety of scenarios, including those where depth
information may be compromised by adverse lighting conditions or intricate environmental
textures.
However, this shift towards an ML-centric model for depth estimation and segmentation
raises considerations regarding computational efficiency and the capacity for real-time
processing. The Depth Anything [27] model, while promising, would require thorough
evaluation to ensure its feasibility for live AR interactions, particularly in terms of its
performance impact and accuracy in diverse conditions. Optimizing such deep learning
models for AR applications would necessitate a focused effort on model efficiency,

Conclusion
This thesis has explored the integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Augmented
Reality (AR) technologies within a museum context, particularly focusing on the
digitization and interactive presentation of amulets. My goal was to enhance the visitor
experience by leveraging AR to create immersion and seamlessly bridge historical
artifacts with new technology.
The development of a robust AR application, utilizing
AI for real-time interaction and detailed visualization of amulets, aimed to allow visitors to
engage with digital replicas of amulets in a playful setting.
The methodology adopted a multidisciplinary approach, integrating 3D scanning, AI,
and user experience design principles to emphasize the project’s adaptability and
user-centered focus.
Employing continuous integration and deployment (CI/CD)
practices, the iterative design process underscored the importance of flexibility and
engagement in technology-driven initiatives.
While this project has made strides in showcasing the potential of AI and AR technologies
within museum settings, it remains an experimental exploration. The final AR experience
will hopefully successfully engage future museum visitors by offering a captivating
exploration of cultural heritage. After the final testing, the application demonstrates high
performance and reliability in real-time interactions, with AI-powered gesture recognition
enabling users to seamlessly interact with the digitized amulets.
Feedback from the
museum staff has been positive so far.
Addressing the challenges encountered throughout the project involved specific
methodologies:
Digitization of Amulets: I employed 3D scanning and modeling techniques to create
accurate digital replicas of the amulets, ensuring the digital models faithfully represented
the physical artifacts.
Real-Time Interaction: AI-driven gesture recognition was integrated to facilitate interactive
experiences, with machine learning models trained on gesture data to recognize and
interpret user actions in the AR environment.
Background Segmentation: Advanced computer vision algorithms were implemented for
real-time user segmentation from their backgrounds, enhancing the immersive quality of
the AR experience.
User Experience Design: Principles of user experience (UX) design were applied to
make the AR application intuitive, engaging, and accessible to a diverse audience,
with continuous user testing guiding iterations to meet visitors’ needs and expectations.
However, changes in the timing of the project by members of the museum staff leaves
further testing to be done at a later stage if possible.
In conclusion, this project underscores the transformative potential of integrating AI and
AR technologies in museum settings, laying a foundation for future innovations in the field.
As an experiment, it highlights the significant role that cutting-edge technologies can play
in fostering a deeper connection with cultural heritage, and furthermore, the final product
creates countless opportunities for further customization of the technology of the ”magic
mirror” for other purposes as well.
