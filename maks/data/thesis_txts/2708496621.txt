Executive Summary
The report aims to evaluate possible suggestions for replacing coal at one of the world’s most efficient
multi-fuel power station - Avedøreværke. Denmark seeks to eradicate the use of coal by 2030 from its district
heating(DH) sector. Upon initial background study, it is noted that Unit 1 at Avedøreværke was recently
converted to a majority wood based co-generation plant(CHP) but coal was still being utilized as and when
required. The four alternatives/scenarios that were suggested are as follow:
1. Conversion of coal based CHP to 100% biogas/biomass
2. Addition of solar thermal unit along with a heat store
3. Adding wind turbines along with heat pump and heat store
4. Replacing coal based unit with waste incineration unit
To visualize the effect of these scenarios an energy system modelling software named EnergyPro was used.
A base case/model was created representing the current DH network, and each scenario was then evaluated
with reference to the base case/model.
Upon evaluation there was a notable decrease in carbon emission in scenarios 1, 2 and 3 showing a net
emission decrease of 0.55%, 0.60% and 2% respectively. In scenario 4, at emission was noted to remain similar
to base case with an increase of 0.03%. Thereby rejecting scenario 4 as a potential solution. On further
analysis scenario 2 was rejected due to large area requirement to establish a solar farm given Denmark’s
solar resource. Also large amount of storage would be required as solar resource is not available throughout
the day. Lastly, on comparing scenario 1 and 3, a conclusion was made naming scenario 3 to be the best
alternative to achieve 0 fossil fuel DH network. The reason favoring scenario 3 were the abundance of wind
resource surrounding Denmark Running large heat pumps powered by green energy showed promising results
with maximum reduction in carbon emission. Furthermore, reasons to reject scenario 1 were in regards to
long term sustainability as burning wood based biomass does not seem to be a sustainable solution as
deforestation has diverse effects such as soil erosion and effect on biodiversity.

Introduction
3.1
Motivation
The worlds first Earth Summit to discuss environmental challenges being faced and to prepare for
future challenges was held in 1992 at Rio de Janerio, Brazil[21]. To mark the summits 20 years, a second
summit was held in 2012 where the panel got together to develop Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)[32].
Furthermore, Kyoto protocol came into to existence focusing on climate change and challenges associated
with it. The Kyoto protocol is an international pact aimed at lowering world’s CO2 emissions[34]. The
Kyoto Protocol was further extended in 2012, but the Paris Agreement completely replaced it in 2015. The
Paris Agreement on Climate Change is a legally binding international agreement on the subject of climate
change. It was accepted by 196 Parties at the United Nations Conference on Climate Change (COP 21) in
Paris on December 12, 2015, and went into effect on November 4, 2016. Its goal is to keep global warming
considerably below 2 degrees Celsius, preferably 1.5, compared to pre-industrial levels[28]. Countries aspire
to reach global peaking of greenhouse gas emissions as soon as possible to produce a climate neutral world
by mid-century in order to meet this long-term temperature objective.
Green Energy has been one of the top priorities of Denmark for decades. The country is a pioneer in solar
and wind energy thereby sharing its experiences and aiding other countries to embark on the green transition.
Despite its small size, Denmark has set one of the most ambitious climate objectives in the world i.e. to
reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 70% by 2030 and reach a net zero emission status by 2050[5].
Figure 1: CO2 emission reduction goals[5]
A step in achieving this goal is eradicating the use of coal/ fossil fuels in various sectors. Currently
Denmark is a world leader in terms of share of renewable energy with around 50% of electricity consumed
being supplied by wind and solar as of 2020[7]. But Denmark is lacking slightly behind in the heating sector,
where coal though in comparatively lower quantity is still being consumed at certain power stations. Thus
the intent of the thesis is to investigate possible replacement of coal at on such power plant - Avedore power

3.2
The Danish District Heating System
Today, district heating(DH) is used in 63% of all private Danish homes, not just for space heating but
also for domestic hot water. In 2014, Denmark had six main central DH zones with a total heat supply of
67PJ, accounting for 56% of the country’s total heat supply. Around 400 small and medium-sized DH areas
with an annual heat supply of around 53 PJ are also present. In 2015, Denmark’s total DH supply was 128
PJ, with 67.4% of all DH produced in co-generation with electricity (CHP). When compared to independent
heating and power generation, this saves a large amount of fuel[12]. In the Danish DH network, the local
DH areas have their own distribution network. from which individuals are supplied heat. A large share of
heat comes from the network itself but a small portion is produced locally.
Figure 2: District heating in Copenhagen area, source: varmelast.dk
In the DH network, four high capacity plants are under the ownership of three different companies namely
Ørsted, HOFOR and VEKS. The four plants together have a net capacity of 2.05 GJ/s. Furthermore there
are 3 waste incineration facilities that have a net capacity of 400 MJ/s[23]. A total capacity of approximately
2 GJ/s comes from various reserve and peak load plants. CTR and VEKs are both owners of a transmission
network. These networks are connected at Damhussøen (a lake) thus forming a continuous network where
heat can be produced and be delivered among the networks[30].

4.2
EnergyPro Model
In order to get an understanding of how the network would react to changes made to it in terms of
new technologies and fuels, a digital tool has been used namely EnergyPro (v4.8). EnergyPro is an industry
leading software tool that enables the users to model complex systems whether its a DH system or an
electricity grid. The software provides a detailed techno-economic analysis of complex system ranging from
a personal home setup to a national setup.
EnergyPro is set to use least-cost approach in for production of heat and electricity. Electricity production
influences the model as revenue is generated from electricity sales. Electricity sales prices are the spot prices
from the day ahead market that have been obtained from Nordpool. Hence, the units are scheduled/ allotted
based on priority.

4.2.4
Scenario 2: Addition of solar thermal unit along with a heat store
In order to achieve a 100% renewable energy driven heating sector scenario 2 was created. As biomass/bio-
gas is a questionable fuel in terms of emissions [19], hence, in this scenario solar thermal was used along with
a heat store.
The setup involves 2 x 10,000m2 solar collectors connected to a heat store. The solar collectors are set up at
30◦inclination with configuration of 10 rows separated by 4m. This is done to account for any array shading
effect. The radiation data was obtained by using EnergyPro’s online time series feature. Figure 9 depicts
scenario 2’s setup in EnergyPro3. The thermal heat store used has a capacity of 95.9 MWh and a utilization
rate of 90%. The heat store has been added for security of supply as the scenario involves a variable energy
source i.e. the solar collectors.

4.2.5
Scenario 3: Adding wind turbines along with heat pump and heat store
With the same intention as scenario 2, scenario 3 was created. Scenario 3 involves the use of wind
turbines connected to an air to water heat pump along with a heat store as a back up. The thought of heat
pump usage arrived from its recent competitiveness. Furthermore, running the pump by wind power aids in
achieving carbon neutrality. The scenario setup includes 2 x 2MW wind turbines connected to a heat pump
which is sized at 50 MW electrical capacity with a COP of 3.1 which feeds a thermal store of 95.9 MWh.
The heat pump chosen is an air to water heat pump with a pump efficiency of 48.7%. The wind speed data
was taken from EnergyPro’s online time series function and the location chosen was a CFSR2 data set point
close to Avedore. Figure 10 shows the scenario 3 set up in EnergyPro4.

5.1
Base model
One of the methods to ensure the designed model is working as intended is to check whether the heat
demand is being met. As per the generated results , it is noted that a total heat demand of around 9.7 TWh
is met by the designed network ,ensuring the working of the model. Furthermore, a max heat demand of
2.35 GW is noted. The Avedore power station accounts for approximately 70% of the total heat demand,
with an annual heat production of 6.8 TWh. AVV is followed by AMV accounting for 21.1% of the heat
demand with an annual production of 2 TWh. The total consumption of coal in the base case is noted to
be 708.25 kilotons, around 85% of which is noted to be from AVV Unit 1. This implies that AVV has an
annual CO2 production of 1.6 Million tons CO2 when an emission factor of 95 kg(CO2)/GJ is considered.

Looking into the economic performance of the base model, the model is generating an annual revenue
of approximately 1.2 Billion DKK from electricity sales.
5.2
Scenario 1
Having made necessary changes to the base model in order to create scenario 1, the first note taken is
that heat demand is being met. Thereby ensuring security of supply. In this scenario, the share of energy
produced by AVV has increased to 72.9% with an annual heat production 7.1 TWh. Figure 14 depicts the
change in share of various producers through a load duration curve. On converting AVV Unit 1 to run on
biogas/biomass instead of coal, the annual coal consumption has dropped to 110.7 kilotons when compared to
base case. This implies the net CO2 emission from coal in this scenario is approximately 251.2 kiloton(CO2)
annually.

The annual revenue generated from electricity sales in noted to remain the same at 1.2 Billion DKK. On

from base case. From DEA technology catalogue for district heating[1], the investment required to convert
AVV Unit 1 (i.e existing boiler) from coal to biogas/biomass is 0.5 M€/ MWe which is approximately 3.7
M.DKK/MWe using an exchnage rate of 7.44. This implies that the total investment would be around 0.3
Billion€which is approximately 2.2 Billion DKK.
5.3
Scenario 2
Similar to the previous scenario, the initial priority is to ensure that heat demand is being met. In
this scenario it is again noted that the heat demand is met but its not by ramping up peak producers or
by increasing production of other units. The addition of solar collectors has increased the share of AVV by
0.1% and decreased the production of peak producers by 0.63% compared to base case. The solar collectors

Conclusion
The continuous increase in CO2 emissions over the past 30 years as well as the rising worldwide de-
mand for steady energy, heating, cooling, and transportation, poses a serious global concern. Each country
faces different kind of energy challenges. Denmark having established security of supply faces the challenge
of producing green energy. Despite the fact that it has no nuclear or hydroelectric power, Denmark con-
sistently is one of the top three countries on the World Energy Council’s Energy Trilemma Index, which
evaluates countries on three essential criterion: energy equity, environmental sustainability, and energy se-
curity (Wyman, 2020). It is also recognized by the IEA as the most advanced country in terms of system
integration of variable renewable energy, and it leads the globe in terms of the share of variable renewable
energy in the grid, with wind and solar providing the equivalent of 50% of gross power consumption in
2020[7]. In Denmark, the transformation has resulted in not only cleaner energy, but also a concentration
on renewable energy as a development area. Denmark further has set an ambitious goal of reaching 70%
emission reduction by 2030 and climate neutrality by 2050. During a political meeting held in June 2020 at
the Danish parliament, majority of the members had declared to end the use of fossil fuel for heating purpose.
A decline in the number of coal fired plants would be seen towards 2030[14]. With the closure of these
plants, the upcoming plants would need to be greener. One such power station in the danish district heating
system is Avedøreværket owned by Orsted. Orsted goal is to say goodbye to coal by 2023. Thus, this thesis
report aims to develop possible recommendations for replacing coal fired unit(s) at Avedøreværket. A total
of 4 scenarios/ alternatives were created as possible recommendation. These alternatives were as follows:
1. Conversion of coal based CHP to 100% biogas/biomass
2. Eradicating the use of coal and adding solar thermal unit(s) along with a heat store
3. Adding wind turbines along with heat pump and heat store
4. Adding a waste incineration unit
In order to determine an optimal alternative among the 4 suggested alternatives, a techno-economic analysis
was conducted. The analysis was done with the aid of an industry leading software for modelling and ana-
lyzing simple and complex energy systems - EnergyPro. Upon initial analysis it was concluded that going
ahead with scenario 4 would not be feasible. Though waste is either free or negatively priced, it was noted
that burning waste instead of coal was equally harmful. Furthermore, Denmark already imports nearly 1
million tons of waste to utilize its current waste incineration capacity. It also came with its fare share of
societal drawbacks such as people rejecting the project due to foul smell.
Alternatives 1,2 and 3 were later further analyzed.
When comparing these alternative options, alterna-
tive 2 looked financially appealing with the lowest investment cost but was later on rejected. One of the key
reasons for rejection being the amount of space that would be required to establish a reasonably sized and
rated plant. For this scenario a total of 20,000m2 of solar collectors were considered which is approximately
the same size as 4 football fields. Furthermore, solar energy is available during the day and hence the storage
33
==PAGE:37==
that would be required to ensure security of supply would be greater.
AVV currently runs majorly on wood pellets and straw. Thus in scenario 1, the use of coal was substi-
tuted by biogas/ biomass. Positive results were noted such at 0.5% overall CO2 reduction in comparison to
base case. But similar to scenario 4 Denmark only produces only 5% of wood based biomass of the total
woody biomass being consumed. Treating biogas/biomass as any other fuel, on the long run importing large
amount of biomass from abroad could deem infeasible due to possible price volatility. If the production of
wood based biomass was shifted to in house, the alternative could be even more attractive. But an issues
with shifting plantation in house are: large land requirement, effect of deforestation on biodiversity, erosion
of soil, etc.
Lastly looking into scenario 3.
Scenario 3 has been one of the most promising scenario backed up by
the CO2 emission reduction. The usage of heat pumps along with wind turbines has shown a CO2 reduc-
