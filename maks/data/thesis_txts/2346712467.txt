Abstract
Brain Computer Interface (BCI) systems enable subjects aﬀected by neuromuscular
disorders to interact with the outside world. A P300 speller uses Event Related Po-
tential (ERP) components, generated in the brain in the presence of a target stimulus,
to extract information about the user’s intent.
Several methods have been proposed
for spatial ﬁltering and classiﬁcation of the P300 components. In this study, xDAWN
algorithm, Independent Component Analysis (ICA) and Principal Component Analysis
(PCA) methods are used and evaluated based on the classiﬁcation performance of two
diﬀerent classiﬁers, namely the Support Vector Machine (SVM) and Fisher’s Linear Dis-
criminant Analysis (FLDA). In addition, an adaptive version of the xDAWN algorithm
(axDAWN), that allows previously estimated spatial ﬁlters to be adapted to new sub-
jects is implemented and evaluated. The ability of adapting previously estimated spatial
ﬁlters can reduce the amount of new EEG signals required to train the spatial ﬁltering
and classiﬁcation models for a new subject. Thus, the adaptive spatial ﬁltering can de-
crease the duration of the training phase without negatively aﬀecting the classiﬁcation
performance. The aforementioned methods are evaluated using two diﬀerent datasets.
The ﬁrst dataset is provided by the BCI Competition III and contains EEG recordings
measured from 2 healthy subjects. The second dataset was obtained in the context of this
thesis and contains EEG recordings measured from 11 healthy subjects. Results indicate
that the combination of xDAWN and SVM algorithms achieves the highest performance.
Regarding the axDAWN, it is shown that it can achieve comparable performance with
xDAWN under the same conditions by incrementally computing the new ﬁlters without
storing data from old subjects.

Preface
This thesis was prepared at the Department of Electrical Engineering in the Biomedical
Engineering Section for the fulﬁlment of the requirements for acquiring the M.Sc. in
Medicine and Technology. The thesis deals with spatial ﬁltering and classiﬁcation tech-
niques for the enchantment and classiﬁcation of P300 components generated in the brain
in the presence of a target stimuli using the BCI P300 speller paradigm. Part of this
work will appear at the IEEE International Conference on Systems, Man, and Cyber-
netics (Spatial Filter Feature Extraction Methods for P300 BCI Speller: A Comparison).

Chapter 1
Introduction
Individuals suﬀering from neuromuscular disorders are deprived of the opportunity to
interact with the external environment.
The most common neuromuscular disorders
include amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, brain stem stroke, spinal cord injury and numerous
other diseases which disrupt the nerves that control muscles [1]. In more severe cases
these disorders can cause total paralysis and loss of voluntarily muscle control. Most
neuromuscular disorders have no cure and aﬀect negatively the quality of patients’ life.
Thus, the development of systems which help the patients to communicate with the
environment is crucial for the improvement of the quality of their lives.
Brain Computer Interface (BCI) systems use brain signals obtained by Electroen-
cephalography (EEG) to provide people with neuromuscular disorders the opportunity
to communicate with the outside world.
Speciﬁcally, EEG signals are processed and
translated into commands allowing individuals to control external devices based on their
wishes.
Diﬀerent neurological rhythms are used depending on the BCI application.
Steady State Visual Evoked Potentials (SSVEPs), Event-Related Potentials (ERPs),
and Event-Related Desynchronization/Synchronization (ERD/ERS) components are the
most commonly exploited types of response in BCI systems. This study focuses on the
processing of P300 components, which are signals generated in the brain as a result of
exposing subjects to certain types (visual/auditory) of stimuli.
Oddball is the most known paradigm where two diﬀerent categories (target/non-
target) of stimulus are presented in a series where the target stimulus occurs relatively
infrequently compared to the non-target stimulus. P300 speller is a BCI system based
on the oddball paradigm. The stimuli are presented on a 6 × 6 matrix containing the
1

1. INTRODUCTION
letters of the alphabet as well as other characters. The user is asked to focus on a letter
of interest while each column of the matrix intensiﬁes in a pseudo-random fashion. The
intensiﬁcation of the column containing the character of interest leads to the elicitation of
a P300 wave in the subject’s brain. Analysing these signals for every possible row/column
intersection can reveal information about the letter of interest as the P300 component
is dominant in responses related to the target stimulus.
However, the low Signal-to-
Noise Ratio (SNR) of the ERPs obtained by measuring the response of a single stimulus
event makes it impossible to accurately discriminate them. Thus, sophisticated signal
processing methods are required to improve the speed of communication in a BCI speller.
Another important challenging aspect of BCI systems is the user and session inde-
pendent analysis of EEG signals. Signal variations observed between diﬀerent users and
diﬀerent sessions require time-to-time and subject speciﬁc calibration using new training
data. The acquisition of a new training data set can be time consuming. Thus, it is
important to develop adaptive algorithms that can reduce the duration of the training
phase.

2.1.1
Nervous System Organization and Units
The nervous system is responsible for the coordination of voluntary and involuntary
actions of the human body and the communication among its parts. The human nervous
system is divided into two main components, the Central Nervous System (CNS) and the
Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) [2]. The CNS consists of the brain and the spinal cord
which lie into the skull and vertebral columns respectively. The PNS mainly consists of
nerve cells that connect the CNS with the other parts of the body.
The main functional unit of the nervous system is the nerve cells called neurons [3].
Neurons consist of three main parts, the cell body, the dendrites and the axons (Fig. 2.1).
The cell body, which is also called soma, contains the nucleus of the neuron and is re-
sponsible for protein synthesis. The dendrites have tree-like structure and constitute
an extension of the cell body. Dendrites are responsible for the conduction of informa-
tion received from other neurons to the cell body. Axons have cable-like structure and
propagate nerve impulses from the cell body to other neurons or muscle cells.
Neurons can be classiﬁed to aﬀerent and eﬀerent based on their function. Aﬀerent
neurons transmit information from the body organs to the CNS, while eﬀerent conduct
information from the CNS to the eﬀector cells.

Neurons Communication
Neurons communicate with each other conducting electrical impulses called action po-
tentials. Action potentials are generated and propagated by ion channels found on the
cell membrane. More speciﬁcally, sodium Na+, potassium K+ and calcium Ca+ channels
allow the generation of action potentials, controlling the ﬂow of the corresponding ions
across the cell membrane.
Action potentials are characterized by three diﬀerent phases, namely the resting, the
depolarization and the repolarization phase (Fig. 2.2). During the resting phase the mem-
brane potential is approximatively −90 mV and the neurons do not transmit electrical
impulses. The voltage diﬀerence across the membrane occurs due to the diﬀerence in ion
concentration inside and outside the cell. More speciﬁcally, there is a high concentration
of K+ ions inside the cell and a high concentration of Na+ and Ca+ outside the cell.
However, the concentration of positive ions in the intracellular space is lower compared

EEG. As mentioned in Section 2.1.2, EEG mainly records the summation of postsynaptic
potentials (EPSP, IPSP) generated by partially aligned populations of neurons located
in the cerebral cortex [4]. The electrical potentials generated by the cortical neurons
propagate along the tissue interposed between the brain source and the scalp and are
recorded by the scalp electrodes. The propagation of the electrical activity depends on
the electrical properties of the interposed tissue which forms a volume conductor and the
orientation of the cortical source relatively to the scalp electrodes. Thus, the recorded
potential at the scalp electrodes is a linear combination of the potentials corresponding

2.3.2
Feature Extraction
In BCI systems the discrimination of source activities generated by cortical sources that
correspond to particular mental states may reveal information about user’s intention.
However, as stated in the previous section, the scalp potentials measured using EEG
consist of a superposition of activities generated by diﬀerent cortical sources. Thus, the
discrimination of source activities considering the measured scalp potentials is compli-
cated due to both the high dimensionality of the input observations and the superposition
of activities from irrelevant cortical sources. For example, EEG data are usually recorded
by multiple electrodes placed across the scalp over relatively long time periods. However,
informative patterns associated with certain mental states are generated only in a subset
of the the cortical sources or in certain time periods. Thus, feature extraction techniques
that reduce the dimensionality of the input observations while simultaneously maintain
the most discriminative features are required [11].
BCI applications that exploit ERPs to discriminate diﬀerent mental states, use spatial
and temporal information. The temporal patterns related to a certain brain activity
are generated in speciﬁc cortical sources. In such cases, feature extraction is usually
performed through spatial ﬁltering.
Spatial ﬁltering techniques linearly combine data from the original set of electrodes
to form a subset of pseudo-electrodes. The pseudo-electrodes retain temporal patterns
corresponding to source activity related of certain mental state. Equation 2.2 gives the
relationship between scalp potentials X and sources activity Sm. The sources activity

Independent Component Analysis (ICA) is a method which projects the data onto a new

of the audio signals emitted by the diﬀerent speakers. Making some assumptions re-
garding the nature of the original audio signals, ICA can separate them considering the
microphone recordings. The separation of the EEG signals is equivalent to the cocktail
party problem as the signals recorded by the diﬀerent electrodes is the superposition of
activities emitted by diﬀerent cortical sources. ICA has been widely used for the analysis
and the visualization of ERPs [18], [19], [20].

Problem Statement
3.1
P300 Detection
The discrimination of ERP responses corresponding to certain types of external stimuli
is a fundamental problem in ERP-based BCI systems. This study focuses on the discrim-
ination of Event-Related Potentials (ERP) and mainly P300 responses, which are signals
generated in the brain as a result of exposing subjects to certain types (visual/auditory)
of stimuli [33]. P300 responses generated in the brain reﬂect a stimulus information.
P300 responses can be generated by a wide variety of stimuli. Oddball is the most known
paradigm where two diﬀerent categories (target/non-target) of stimulus are presented in
a series where the target stimulus occurs relatively infrequently compared to the non-
target stimulus. The subject is instructed to focus on the target stimulus and not on
the frequently presented non-target stimulus. The P300 responses are generated every
time the subject observes the target stimulus thus allowing the detection of it after the
processing of the acquired signals.
Farwell and Donchin in their seminal work [34] introduced the oddball paradigm
in a BCI system (P300 speller). In the context of the P300 speller paradigm a 6 × 6
matrix containing the letters of the latin alphabet as well as other characters (Fig. 3.1)
is available to the user through a computer display [34]. The user is asked to focus on
a letter of interest while each row/column of the matrix intensiﬁes in a pseudo-random
fashion. The intensiﬁcation of the row/column containing the character of interest leads
to the elicitation of a P300 waves in the subject’s brain. Analysing these signals for every
29

possible row/column intersection can reveal information about the letter of interest as
the P300 component is dominant in the responses related to the target stimuli.
The low Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) of the ERPs obtained by measuring the response
of a single stimulus event makes it impossible to accurately discriminate them. Thus,
responses of a series of trials are needed to be recorded and averaged out in order to
improve the SNR of the ERPs. The drawback of this approach is the reduced spelling
rate, which increases the communication time between the user and the environment.
Thus, sophisticated signal processing methods, which allow single-trial analysis of ERP
responses, are required to improve the speed of communication of the P300 speller. Many
methods addressing the problem of single-trial ERP responses discrimination have been
proposed at the diﬀerent stages of the signal processing chain in BCI systems (Fig. 2.6).
Apart from single-trial analysis of ERP responses, elimination of the calibration time
before the diﬀerent sessions is another challenge in the development of real-world P300
speller applications. ERP responses diﬀerentiate between diﬀerent subjects or even dif-
ferent sessions of the same subject. Thus, the acquisition of a certain amount of data
in order to calibrate the BCI is required before each session. However, the acquisition
of calibration data before each session is a time consuming and inconvenient procedure.
Thus, adaptive approaches which oﬀer incremental adaptation of the model parameters

3.2
Related Work
Many methods have been developed to address the processing of the P300 responses
in BCI systems. Below we give an overview of the most notable methods used in BCI
systems based on P300 responses
3.2.1
Preprocessing
Saavedra et al. [35] used a wavelet-based approach to eliminate artifacts. The new ap-
proach, in contrast to other methods, considers the correlation between the available
channels in the wavelet domain. In addition, authors proposed the selection of a time
window which provides discriminative information regarding the classiﬁcation of ERP
responses. The selection of the time window was provided by combining phase and am-
plitude information in the wavelet domain.
The data used for the validation of the method were obtained by 22 healthy subjects
using a P300 BCI speller interface. After the signal de-noising and the selection of the
time window, a SVM classiﬁer was applied for the discrimination between target and
non-target ERP responses. The performance of the proposed method was compared to
the performance of both xDAWN [26] and SureShrink methods [36]. The results indicate
that the method outperforms slightly the other methods achieving an average accuracy of
55.83% compared to 51.03% and 54.80% which were achieved by xDAWN and SureShrink
respectively.
3.2.2
Feature Extraction
Xu et al. [37] used ICA to improve the discrimination between target and non-target
responses. A P300 speller was used to obtain the data. A number of trials were considered
and averaged before the further processing of the data. Initially, PCA was used to reduce
the dimensionality of the raw data, since 64 channels were used to record the brain
activity. After the dimensionality reduction, ICA was applied on the reduced data and
thus, the spatial ﬁlter matrix and the Independent Components (ICs) were obtained.
31
==PAGE:48==
3. PROBLEM STATEMENT
Then, temporal and spatial manipulation of the ICs was performed in order to eliminate
ICs which do not provide valuable information related to the P300 evoked potentials.
After the manipulation, the ICs were back-projected and the enhanced P300 evoked
potentials were obtained. Finally, the area or the peak in a speciﬁed window around the
P300 potential was used for the discrimination between target and non-target responses.
Bertrand et al. [26] proposed an algorithm to enhance P300 evoked potentials, esti-
mating spatial ﬁlters and projecting the raw data onto them. The method was applied
to a P300 speller. The spatial ﬁlters were designed to maximize the SSNR. For the eval-
uation of the proposed method three subjects were used. The proposed algorithm was
applied to estimate the spatial ﬁlters for the three subjects. More speciﬁcally, the SSNR
was maximized for the spatial ﬁlters estimated by the xDAWN algorithm. Also, PCA and
ICA were used to extract spatial ﬁlters. Then, the Binary Linear Discriminant Analysis
(BLDA) method was used for the classiﬁcation of the data obtained by the projection into
the diﬀerent spatial ﬁlters. The classiﬁcation results obtained by xDAWN outperform
the other methods achieving 80% classiﬁcation accuracy. ICA and PCA achieve a classiﬁ-
cation accuracy of 70% and 30% respectively. However, this accuracy was achieved using
the average of ﬁve trials which means that the number of symbols spelled per minute was
decreased.
Onishi et al. [38] investigated a method based on tensor decomposition of P300 re-
sponses. The method extracts an optimal features subspace which consists of temporal,
spatial and other bases which indicate a variant of ERPs. Data obtained by P300 speller
were used for the performance evaluation of the proposed method. The extracted fea-
tures were used to train a LDA classiﬁer. The classiﬁcation performance achieved by the
classiﬁer was between 92% and 95%.
Li et al. [39] investigated a new feature extraction approach to improve the classiﬁca-
tion performance. The new approach was based on a constrained ICA algorithm called
one-unit ICA-with-reference (ICA-R). This method extracts the P300 responses based on
its prior temporal information. The training data were used to train the ICA algorithm.
Then, the the test data were extracted and the SVM classiﬁcation algorithm was applied.
Concluding, the classiﬁcation performance was high, achieving a score of 84%, 94% and
97% for 5, 10 and 15 averaged trials respectively.
Tahmasebzadeh et al. [40] proposed a feature extraction technique to improve the
discriminative information between target and non-target P300 responses. The technique

EEG signals were extracted and the most important coeﬃcients were selected based on a

de-blurring of the ERP responses can increase the classiﬁcation performance.
3.2.3
Classiﬁcation
Hoﬀmann et al. [42] investigated a boosting approach for the detection of single trial P300
responses. Particularly, they proposed an algorithm which maximizes the Bernoulli log-
likelihood of a logistic regression model based on the gradient boosting method. The data
used for the evaluation of the method were obtained by a P300 speller. Two subjects, one
healthy and one unhealthy, participated in the experiments. After the acquisition of the
training dataset and the artefact rejection, the proposed method was applied in order to
boost the performance of the ordinary Least Square (OLS) method. The results indicate
that for single-trial P300 responses the classiﬁcation performance in signiﬁcantly low.
The error rate ranges between 40% and 80% for single-trial P300 responses. However,
as the number of trials increases the classiﬁcation performance increases-for 9 trials the
error rate drops approximately to 5% −10%.
Rakotomamonjy et al. [43] proposed an ensemble classiﬁcation method in order to
improve the classiﬁcation performance of P300 evoked potentials. The dataset which
was used was obtained using a P300 BCI speller matrix. Initially, the training dataset
was divided into 17 partitions and then 17 SVM classiﬁers were trained on them. Each
new observation which corresponds to the P300 evoked potentials obtained by a speciﬁc
row/column was classiﬁed by the SVM classiﬁer and the average score of all the classiﬁers
was calculated. The row/column with the higher average score was supposed to contain

single SVM classiﬁer having a performance of 73.5% compared to 69.5%. However, 5

the number of characters that can be spelled per minute was decreased. In this case,
the number of repetitions was reduced in comparison to other studies but it was still

Lu et al [44] proposed a method for subject independent ERP analysis. More ana-

The classiﬁcation results indicate that the algorithm performs quite well, achieving an

Rivet et al. [45] proposed a method in order to reduce the required calibration time.
More speciﬁcally, the new approach estimates the optimal amount of data which were re-
quired during the calibration phase for the training of the spatial ﬁlters and the classiﬁer.
The results indicate that the classiﬁcation accuracy increases with the number of symbols
spelled during the calibration phase. However, after a certain number of symbols, the
classiﬁcation accuracy remains the same. The proposed method reduces the calibration
time identifying the optimal number of symbols required during the calibration phase.
Blankertz et al. [46] proposed a variation of the LDA method.
In LDA method,
the projection vector is a function of the estimated covariance matrix. Under speciﬁc
conditions the empirical covariance was an unbiased estimator. However, in cases where
the number of channels is high compared to the number of time points, the estimator
can become biased leading to systematic error in the calculation of the covariance matrix
eigenvalues. This systematic error can negatively aﬀect the classiﬁcation performance
of the LDA classiﬁer. Thus, the authors proposed the modiﬁcation of the covariance
matrix through a technique called shrinkage. The modiﬁed LDA was called regularized
LDA. Classiﬁcation results on a dataset of 13 participants were provided. The analysis
was restricted to a binary classiﬁcation problem (target vs non-target). Ordinary LDA,
regularized LDA and stepwise LDA were applied and the classiﬁcation performance of
these algorithms was compared. A varying number of training samples was used for the
estimation of the parameters of the classiﬁers. Then, using the estimated parameters,
the classiﬁers were applied on the test data and the classiﬁcation error was obtained.

Two diﬀerent datasets were used in this study. The ﬁrst dataset [47] is provided by BCI

4.1.1
BCI Competition III Dataset
In order to obrain EEG data containing P300 responses, the P300 speller paradigm
introduced by Farwell and Donchin [34] was used during the data acquisition. The dataset
consists of EEG signals measured using 64 EEG channels. The channels were placed at
scalp according to the Extended 10/20-System. Two healthy subjects participated in the
study. The dataset contains signals from 185 characters. The data were acquired within
5 sessions. Each session consisted of 9 runs and in each run a single word was spelled.
The data was recorded at a sampling rate of 240 Hz using the BCI2000 software.
Initially, the P300 speller matrix remained blank for a period of 2.5s. For each character
12 intensiﬁcations occurred (6 rows/6 columns) randomly and were repeated 15 times.
The intensiﬁcations of each column and row lasted 100 ms and the time between successive
intensiﬁcations was 75 ms. After 15 repetitions, the screen remained blank for a period
of 2.5s before displaying the next character.

4.1.3
Data Analysis
The main purpose of this study is the development of spatial ﬁltering and classiﬁcation
schemes for the enhancement of ERP responses in the context of both user-dependent
and user-independent analysis.
Initially, three widely used spatial ﬁltering techniques described in Section 2.3.2 were
developed and evaluated based on the classiﬁcation performance of two well-known clas-
siﬁers, namely the FLDA and the SVM. Then, the user-independent case was considered
by applying an adaptive spatial ﬁltering algorithm and comparing its performance with
the one obtained using the best performing non-adaptive technique.
Before any further analysis, the raw EEG signals were ﬁltered using a bandpass But-
terworth ﬁlter with cut-oﬀfrequencies set to 1 Hz and 20 Hz respectively. In addition,
epochs were extracted considering samples obtained 800 ms after the stimulus onset.
In order to examine the performance of the spatial ﬁltering and classiﬁcation methods,
the so-called Monte-Calro Cross Validation (MCCV) technique was used [51]. MCCV
randomly selects a certain proportion of the available observations to form the training
set, while the remaining observations form the test set.
The performance is measured in terms of accuracy, sensitivity and speciﬁcity.
In
the case of binary classiﬁcation accuracy is deﬁned as the proportion of the correctly
classiﬁed observations. Sensitivity is deﬁned as the proportion of correctly classiﬁed pos-
itive observations. Speciﬁcity is deﬁned as the proportion of correctly classiﬁed negative
observations.
We study two diﬀerent scenarios, and particularly, a user-dependent (Fig. 4.3) and a
user-independent (Fig. 4.4) scenario. In the user dependent-scenario the spatial ﬁltering
and the classiﬁcation is performed based on observations obtained from the same subject.
In the user-independent scenario, spatial ﬁlters obtained from an old subject subject
were adapted in order to enhance P300 responses corresponding to new subjects. The
classiﬁers were trained from scratch using enhanced P300 responses corresponding to

algorithms. Initially, the sources activity of the test data is estimated and divided in
epochs based on the the stimulus onset. The rows of each epoch are also concatenated
in one vector. At this point let us note that the label of each epoch is predicted based on
the models built during the training stage. This process is repeated 100 times and the
average accuracy, sensitivity and speciﬁcity of the classiﬁcation algorithms are computed.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The purpose of this work was the implementation and evaluation of spatial ﬁltering
and classiﬁcation techniques for the enhancement and classiﬁcation of P300 components.
P300 components are generated in cortical sources after exposing the subject to a simple
discrimination task, the so called oddball paradigm. The discrimination of P300 compo-
nents is useful as it reveals information regarding subjects current mental state. However,
it is hard to discriminate P300 components due to their low SNR. Thus, responses of a
series of trials are needed to be recorded and averaged out in order to improve the SNR.
The drawback of this approach is the reduced spelling rate, which increases the communi-
cation time between the user and the environment. Thus, sophisticated signal processing
methods, which allow single-trial analysis of P300 responses, are required to increase the
speed of communication while maintaining high spelling accuracy.
In this study, we examined 4 diﬀerent spatial ﬁltering techniques, namely PCA, ICA,
xDAWN, axDAWN for the enhancement of P300 responses. In addition, FLDA and SVM
were used for the discrimination of the P300 responses after the spatial ﬁltering. Two
scenarios were considered through this study, a user-dependent and a user-independent
scenario. In the user-dependent-scenario the spatial ﬁltering and classiﬁcation was per-
formed based on observations obtained from the same subject. In the user-independent
scenario, spatial ﬁlters obtained from an old subject were adapted in order to enhance
P300 responses corresponding to a new subject.
In order to examine the aforementioned scenarios we used two diﬀerent datasets. The
datasets were obtained using a P300 speller interface in both cases. The ﬁrst dataset is
provided by the BCI competition III and contains data from 2 healthy subjects. The
59
==PAGE:76==
6. CONCLUSION
second dataset was obtained in the context of this thesis and contains data from 11
healthy subjects.
In the user-dependent scenario xDAWN, ICA, PCA were used for spatial ﬁltering
while SVM and FLDA were used for the discrimination of the P300 responses. The re-
sults indicate that the combination of xDAWN and SVM algorithm achieves the highest
performance (90.16%) in terms of P300 discrimination compared to the other methods
examined in this study. The combination of PCA and FLDA achieves the lowest classiﬁ-
cation performance (80.90%). Furthermore, it is shown that at least 5 trials are required
in order to achieve an acceptable classiﬁcation performance. xDAWN-SVM achieves a
classiﬁcation performance of approximately 85% using 5 trials. As the number of trials
increases the classiﬁcation performance increases (approximately 90% for 15 trials).
In the user-independent scenario the axDAWN algorithm was used for the extraction
of spatial ﬁlters. axDAWN algorithm adapts ﬁlters estimated using data from old subjects
to enhance responses corresponding to new subjects. After the adaptation of the ﬁlters
to the new subject and the enhancement of the P300 responses, SVM and FLDA were
applied for the discrimination of P300 responses. The results indicate that SVM behaves
better compared to FLDA. In addition, we observe that axDAWN has similar or better
performance compared to the xDAWN considering 10 characters during the training
phase. However, the performance and especially the sensitivity remains low and further
investigation of adaptive spatial ﬁltering methods is required. In addition, it will be
interesting to consider data from the same subject corresponding to diﬀerent sessions
and to examine if we can achieve an acceptable classiﬁcation performance considering
few data from a new session. In this case the adaptation of the spatial ﬁlters could lead
to reduced calibration time for the same subject. Thus, we conclude that by exploiting
adaptive techniques we can achieve similar or better performance than the non-adaptive
ones with lower computational complexity.
Regarding the future work, diﬀerent and more sophisticated classiﬁcation algorithms
can be implemented. In the context of this thesis the classiﬁcation was performed using
SVM and FLDA algorithms. However, there also exist adaptive classiﬁcation algorithms
which can be used to further improve the classiﬁcation performance by properly adapting
the classiﬁcation model to new subjects.

C.1.2
xDAWN function
The xDAWN function implemented for the estimation of spatial ﬁlters is depicted below.
function
[U,W,xDawnSignal,A] = xDawnAlg(signal,targetStimulusOnset,epochWind )
% xDAWN tries to construct spatial filters such that the
% signal−to−signal plus noise ratio is maximized. This spatial filter is
% particularly suited for paradigms where classification is based on
% event−related potentials.
% The visualisation is divided into two components.
% First of all each transformation is visualized separately.
% Since the visualization itself may not be so meaningful,
% there exists another combined visualization, which shows
% the filter (u i) with the underlying spatial distribution
% (w i), parameter names taken from paper).
% The number of filters equals the number of original channels.
% Normally only the first channels matter and the rest corresponds to
% different noise components.
% Usage:
%
[U,W,xDawnSignal,A] = xDawnAlg(Signal,StimulusType,window )
%
% Input:
%
signal = recorded EEG signal, dimensions: (#samples,channels).
%
targetStimulusOnset
= contains 1 at the positions corresponding to
%
the onsest of the target stimulus and 0 at the remaining positions,
%
dimensions: (1,#samples).
%
window =
number of sampes in one epoch
%
% Output:
%
U = spatial filter (unmixing matrix), dimension: (channels,channels).
%
W = spatial patterns or spatial distribution over sensors (mixing
%
matrix), dimensions: (channels,channels).
%
signal = recorded EEG signal, dimensions: (#samples,channels).
%
xDawnSignal
= filtered singal, dimensions: (#samples,channels).
%
A =
filtered ERP response, dimensions: (#ERPsamples,channels).
87
==PAGE:104==
C. MATLAB CODE
%Identify the onset of the target stimulus
%toeplitz matrix first row
r=[targetStimulusOnset(1,1);zeros(epochWind−1,1)];
%Generate toeplitz matrix
toeplitzMatrix=toeplitz(targetStimulusOnset,r);
%apply QR decomposition to D and X matrix
[Qx,Rx] = qr(signal,0); %size(Qx)=TxC, %size(Rx)=CxC
[Qd,Rd] = qr(toeplitzMatrix,0);
%size(Qd)=TxN, %size(Rd)=NxN
% Compute SVD for Qd'Qx
QdQx=Qd'*Qx; %size(QdQx)=NxC
[Phi,Lambda,Psi]= svd(QdQx,0) ; %size(F)=NxN, size(L)=NxC, size(PSI)=CxC,
%Select eigenvectors and
U=inv(Rx)*Psi; %spatial filters U −size(U)=CxC
A=inv(Rd)*Phi*Lambda;% sychronous response of reduced dimensions:
%size(A)=NxC
W=Rx'*Psi;
%spatial distribution over sensors
xDawnSignal=signal*U; %Enchansed total signal
end
