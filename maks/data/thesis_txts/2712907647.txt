Abstract
In this project, a CFD simulation was built using a rowing boat’s rigger, hilt, and oar. The
goal is to see how a specific motion changes (and thus influences) the results compared to the
stationary simulation. CFD program Star-CCM+ has been used with the k −ω −SST model.
Four different simulations have been run, applying different motions each time. The motions
that have been used have been described by previous projects [8][7]. It turns out that the
motion of the oar significantly changes the results compared to the stationary simulation. It is
also interesting to look at the contribution of the rigger and the oar on the total drag of the
simulation. The contribution of the oar is approximately three-quarters of the total drag A
preliminary wind tunnel experiment has also been conducted to determine the drag and rigger
contribution to the total drag, where similar results have been observed compared to the CFD
simulations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
An introduction to rowing
Rowing is an Olympic Sport in which oars are used to propel a boat with 1 to 8 crew rowers
(and sometimes a coxswain). The crew sits backwards in the boat, and the boat will move by
moving the oars through the water. During a competition (Regatta), the boat competes with
other equivalent boats over a two-kilometre distance and the first boat to cross the finish line
wins the race. Everything needs to be perfect during a race because races can be won with
minimum margins, for example, the final during the Olympic Games in Rio de Janeiro in 2016.
The winner of the Men’s Single Scull final had to be decided by a finish photo since the number
1 and 2 of the race had the same time of 6.41.34 minutes [11]. A race over 2000 meters was
decided by less than 0.01 seconds.

Since the difference between winning and losing can be so small, every detail is essential. Not
only has the rower to be in top form, but the boat and equipment should also be as good as
possible. There is constant innovation to try to become faster and faster.
Innovation and optimizing equipment to improve the performance even the slightest bit is very
common in (elite) sports. Wind tunnels have been used for ages to improve performances, and
lately, CFD is also becoming an essential part of the performance improvement strategy. In
sports, competitions can be won with the slightest of differences. The sports teams are often
not keen on publishing their results, not to help the competitors out. Therefore, in some sports
fields, not much open literature can be found. This is also the case for rowing. One of the
articles that have been published is research on the effect of applying speed strips on oars to
reduce the drag [3]. Windtunnel experiments were combined with a simplified numerical model
to determine the advantages of applying the speed strips. Speed strips (or other drag-reducing
methods/mechanisms) are used in different sports such as cycling, speed skating, and short
track. Another article in which wind tunnel experiments and CFD simulations are compared
for cycling helmet designs [12] shows a good agreement between the wind tunnel and CFD
results. It is noted that the main limitation to the CFD simulations is that it is hard to achieve
the correct geometries of professional cyclists because a three-dimensional scan is needed to
create an accurate CAD model.
Also, when having limited computational resources, only
static positions can be simulated. Wind tunnels are often used to validate a CFD simulation’s
findings. This has, for example, been done in the research into ”Aerodynamic drag in cycling
team time trials” [2]. They found that the CFD simulation underestimated the drag with 4%
compared to the corresponding wind tunnel results.
This project focuses on building a CFD (Computational Fluid Dynamics) model for a single
scull rowing boat to simulate the drag created by the rigger, oar-lock and oar of the boat. The
model will include several motions, and the CFD model can then, in a later project, be used to
try to reduce this drag. At the moments, the world record for the men’s single scull over 2000
meters is 6 min 30.74 seconds. [10] This time corresponds to an average speed of a little over
5 m/s. Therefore, 5 m/s will be used as the boat’s basic velocity throughout this project.
1.2
Build-up of the project
This project aims to have a simulation that finds the drag created by the rigger, hilt, and
oar of the rowing boat.
The simulation will include three primary motions: heave, surge,
pitch, and it will include one primary oar motion: the horizontal oar motion.
Solidworks
has been used to draw up CAD geometry, and Star-CCM+ has been used to perform the
CFD simulations. To be able to perform simulations on the rigger and oar, CAD geometry is
needed. Unfortunately, there is no official CAD available. Therefore, the geometry is redrawn in
Solidworks by measuring the original components. Chapter 2 will describe this process. Chapter
3 will describe the process of defining the motions. The surge, heave, and pitch motions are
described in a previous project about splash boards of the rowing boat [8]. The oar motion is
described in a project about the resistance of the oars [7]. The defined motions are modified
to suit this project. Also, the oar’s starting position had to be defined by aligning the motions
and positioning the oar in the correct position.
Chapter 4 describes the basic background
of the CFD model, the development of the simulations, the mesh, boundary conditions, and
continua settings. The results of the simulations are described in chapter 5. The results of

1.3
Assumptions and limitations of the project
As with any project, not all details can be correctly covered 100% . Assumptions have therefore
been made. The early-on identified limitations of the project can be seen in this chapter. The
position of different components on the boat can be seen in figure 2.0.1.
• The CFD model is build upon and based on the master thesis project [8] about splash
boards of the rowing boat. The boat’s heave, surge and pitch motion are adapted from
the splash board project. Similar to that project, the yaw, roll and sway are neglected.
• The CFD model is built up using the rigger, hilts, and oar (part of it, see below) but
without the oarlock. The boat and rower are excluded.
• Only starboard of the boat is considered in the CFD simulation, port side is assumed to
be mirrored with a symmetry boundary condition.
• The rigger, oar, hilts and oar sleeve are redrawn by hand using Solidworks. The CFD
model would be better when the parts’ original/official CAD files are used. More infor-
mation in chapter 2.
• The oar sleeve and a small part of the oar are used. The blade and part of the oar are
not included.
• The oar’s horizontal rotation will be considered, while the vertical rotation will be ne-
glected. The horizontal motion is assumed to be a sin-function, which is not the case in
reality. The equation found by a project with regards to oar motion is used. (reference)
• The boat is assumed to be in still water. Waves are neglected.
• The boat will experience a straight headwind, so no side wind is used in this project.
• The boat will be travelling in a straight line.
• The wind tunnel experiments have been very preliminary. The focus of the project was
on the CFD part. Therefore the wind tunnel part has its limitations.

2.1
Rigger
The rigger’s wing shape is measured using a contour duplication gauge. The gauge surrounds
the wing, and the chord length and height of the wing can be read from it (figure 2.1.1). Using
the measurements, the closest NASA airfoil can be downloaded and used in the geometry of
the rigger. Since the airfoil has a standard, non-complex shape, the NACA 4 series are consid-
ered. The NACA 4 series is described by four numbers representing the maximum camber, its
position, and the thickness to chord length ratio [14]. Since the airfoil is symmetric, thus it has
no camber, the 00xx foils are considered. The chord length is approximately 90 mm, and the
height is approximately 30 mm. This results in a thickness to chord ratio of 30. The NACA
airfoil used for the rigger is NACA 0030.
The rigger is one large component that is mounted to the boat. Because it is one piece, it
will serve both sides of the boat. Not the full rigger will be used in the simulations, only the
starboard part. The port side and the mounting part will not be used. (figure 2.1.2) Some
key considerations while drawing was the decreasing chord length over the length of the rigger,

Chapter 3
Motion
During rowing, the boat experiences different motions (figure 3.0.1), among other things caused
by the force input of the oars, change in centre of gravity by sliding the seat, wind and waves.
The motions considered in this project are surge, heave, pitch, and horizontal oar motion.
The neglected motions are yaw, roll, sway, vertical oar motion, and rotation along with the
oar. These are considered to have a relatively small impact on the results while significantly
complicating the simulation and increasing the computational power needed. The boat will
have a basic velocity of 5 m/s under a 0°angle. This velocity will periodically become higher
and lower due to the surge motion.

3.1
Surge, heave and pitch motion
From the 2021 master thesis about splash boards of the rowing boat [8], the heave, surge and
pitch motions are obtained.
In that project, the motions are described by a motion table
(table 3.1) and the two field functions (Appendix). The table describes the motions over time,
and field functions interpolate between the time inputs of the table. The table describes the
motion cycle that has a period of T = 1.682805 seconds. The period exists of two phases; the
underwater phase and the phase above the water. During the underwater phase, the oars are
put into the water (figure 3.2.1b) and pulled to propel the boat. The phase above the water
follows up the underwater phase. The oars are taken out of the water (figure 3.2.1a), the oars
are repositioned, and the rower’s seat is slid forward. The sliding of the seat causes the center
of gravity to change. This change in the center of gravity causes the boat to accelerate. When
pulling on the oars again, the boat decelerates a little. Since the two phases will be repeated
over and over again, the boat will accelerate and decelerate over and over again as well. This
surge motion can be seen in figure 3.1.1.

Application of the motion

The motion is defined in a ‘Superposing structure’. This is a motion defining tool of Star-
CCM+. A motion is defined, and a superposing motion is added. The second motion will move
along with the first, but the second motion does not influence the first. Superposing motion
can be kept added, and a further motion will not influence the previous one(s). When applying
the Xth superposing motion to a region, the region will move with the Xth motion plus all
previous motions. In this case, the translating motion is defined first using the field function
as its input (see Appendix). The added superposing motion is the pitch, which is defined by
the second field function. Lastly, the oar motion is added in a new superposing motion. It is
defined by applying the oars angular velocity of equation 3.2.3. The oar motion is defined by
a different coordinate system. It will rotate around the hilt.
Depending on the wanted applied motion, each region can have applied its own step in the
superposing motion. This means that one region can have all motions while another region has
not all. In chapter 4.6, more information can be found about the applied motions to a specific
simulation.

4.1
CFD - Background information
CFD or Computational Fluid Dynamics, is used to simulate systems involving fluid flow, heat
transfer and associated phenomena such as chemical reactions.[9] In this project, CFD program
Star-CCM+ has been used.
There are three main steps involved with doing a CFD simulation; 1. Pre-prosessing 2. Solving
and 3. Post-processing. During step 1, geometry or computational domain is defined. After-
ward, a mesh will be generated, which divides the domain into cells, and lastly, the physical
phenomena and fluid properties are selected, and boundary conditions are applied. In step 2,
the simulation will be solved using the finite volume method. The governing equations describe
the conservation of mass, momentum, and energy. Since this project will be incompressible and
is an isothermal proces, the equations are in the following form:

These equations combined are the so-called Navier-Stokes equations (normally including the

solver will try to find the ‘true’ solution to the parameters. Once the residuals of the simulation
are converged, the outcome of the simulation is reliable.
4.2
Turbulence model
To resolve the turbulence, the RANS (Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes) model is used. There
are different models that are examples of RANS models, for example; k−ϵ, k−ω and SST−k−ω.

Each model has its strengths and weaknesses. The k−ϵ model has its strength in the turbulence
modeling in the free stream. Its near-wall turbulence modeling is unsatisfactory for boundary
layers with adverse pressure gradients.[9] The k −ω model has the opposite strength and
weaknesses from the k −ϵ model. Its near-wall modeling is very good, while the free stream
modeling is less good. The SST −k −ω model combines the two models. In this project, the
SST −k −ω is used.
4.3
Wall Y+
To be able to resolve the turbulence in the boundary layer correctly, the wall y+ value should
be considered.
A velocity profile has a velocity of 0 m/s at the wall, and free-stream velocity far from the wall.
The gradient becomes steeper and steeper when approaching the wall from the free-stream
velocity (figure 4.3.1).
CFD uses a linear equation to solve for the velocity between different cells in the mesh. Because
of these large gradients, the velocity will not be solved correctly if the boundary layer is not
well defined. Many cells are needed to capture it accurately, with the cells becoming thinner
and thinner when closer to the wall. This is known as the boundary layer. Having the correct
wall distance or wall y+ is essential. In figure 4.3.2, the red line represents a non-linear velocity
function. The black dotted lines are empirical functions (Wall functions) that can be fitted
to the non-linear velocity function. For y+ values of approximately less than 5 (the viscous
sub-layer), the linear function y+ = u+ fits the velocity function very well. For y+ values of
approximately more than 30, the non-linear function u+ = ln(y+)/k + C approximates the
velocity function very well.
In the buffer layer (5 < y+ < 30), both wall functions badly
approximate the real velocity function. It is therefore important to have the correct y+ values
in the simulation.
When it is important to know what is happening in the boundary layer, the y+ values should
be less than 5, while they should be more than 30 if it is only important what is happening in

Windtunnel experiments
Wind tunnels are often used to gain knowledge about the performance of the equipment. A
constant wind (possibly under an angle) moves along the object, and the drag can be calculated
with gauges or wake rakes. However, a rowing boat is not moving at a constant speed. The
CFD simulation results show that the oar’s movement significantly influences the total drag.
Many other variables (or unknowns) can make a difference between results from a wind tunnel
or a CFD simulation.
The main focus of this project has been on CFD. However, a preliminary experiment has been
conducted in the wind tunnel. Part of the rigger and oar are put in the DTU’s Red Wind Tunnel
(figure 6.0.1). The tunnel has a test section cross-section of 50x75 cm. The used rigger and oar
of the CFD model are too big to fit in the tunnel. Therefore, the length of the rigger, drawn
in CAD, is shortened so the oarlock will be in the middle of the cross-section, as seen in figure
6.0.2. The rigger is approximately half of the length of the rigger used in the CFD simulations.
The rigger is 3D printed from the CAD geometry, while the oarlock is an official part. The
oar also used to be an official part, but it has been modified for the project Experimental and
numerical analysis of aerodynamic oar resistance [7]. The length of the oar and the sleeve have
been shortened. The oar now has a length of 50 cm which is half of the length of the oar used
in the CFD simulations. The oar has a diameter of 30 mm (while in the CFD simulation 40
mm was used). The wind tunnel experiment was conducted before the oar angle was decided
upon. Therefore, the oar is positioned differently than it is in the Stationary CFD simulation.
The drag is measured using a gauge in the ceiling of the test section of the wind tunnel. The
drag is measured from 5 to 10 m/s for the rigger without the oar and with the oar. At 5 m/s,
the drag for only the rigger is 0.11 N, and when adding the oar, it is 0.34 N. This means that
the oar is approximately responsible for 0.23 N, which is 67%. The rigger is responsible for
33%. Considering that the rigger and oar have different lengths than in the CFD simulation
and that the oar has a different diameter, the percentages are in the same range as was seen
for the CFD simulations.

Conclusion & discussion
In this project, a CFD simulation was built using a rowing boat’s rigger, hilt, and oar. The
goal is to see how a specific motion changes (and thus influences) the results compared to
the stationary simulation. The simulation has been run four times, applying a different set
of motions each time. The first simulation is stationary, no motion occurs in this simulation.
The translating motions are applied in the second simulation, which is surge and heave. In the
third simulation, the pitch is also applied in addition to the translation motions. In the last
simulation, the horizontal motion of the oar is added to the other motions.
When looking at the results, the mean drag on the rigger, the mean drag on the oar, and the
mean total drag (sum of mean drag on the rigger and oar) are taken into account. The 2nd
to 4th simulations are compared to the stationary simulation to look at the influence of the
motion on the simulation results. Looking at the mean total drag, it could be observed that the
addition of the translation and pitch motions have a relatively small influence on the results
compared to the oar motion. The translation and pitch motion is approximately 4% different
from the stationary simulation, while the oar motion changes the results by almost 16%.
When looking at the mean drag on the rigger, all motions have approximately the same amount
of influence on the results (4-6.7%). On the other hand, when looking at the mean drag on the
oar, the oar motion significantly influences the results. The results differ almost 25% from the
stationary simulation.
Observing the total drag for each simulation, the oar contributes approximately 75% while the
rigger only contributes approximately 25% to the total drag.
Adding motions to the simulation causes a significant increase in demand for computational
power. Therefore the designer/researcher who wants to run these simulations for possible inno-
vation or performance checks should consider whether the differences in results are significant
enough to add motions to the simulation or leave them out.
During the project, a preliminary wind tunnel experiment was conducted. It could be observed
that the contribution of the rigger and oar to the total drag was approximately the same
compared to the CFD simulations, taking into account the differences in the geometry.
The initial idea of this project was to find a way to decrease the drag on the rigger/oar. The
simulation could now be used to reduce the drag by applying, for example, trip wires to the
rigger/oar or designing a streamlined part around the hilt.
The project has its limitations, as discussed in chapter 1.3. The points that possibly could
have the most significant uncertainty on the results are 1. The geometry is redrawn by hand.
Measurements have been made, from which the part is redrawn to CAD. Therefore it is not
100% accurate. 2. The drag on the rigger and oar is relatively small compared to the drag
of the whole boat, but it can be analyzed and help improve (even the slightest) on the drag,
which could be the difference between winning or losing a competition.

Appendix
Field functions are a tool in Star-CCM+, which allows access to fields. They can be used to
visualize the computed fields, specify boundary and region values, or define initial conditions.
In this project, two field functions are used to interpolate the pitch velocity and translation
(surge & heave) velocities between the input fields of the motion table. they are defined as
follows:
translation :
interpolateTable(@Table(”40s trans and rot adjusted”), ”column0”, LINEAR,
”column4”, ”column1”, ”column2”, ${Time})
pitch :
interpolateTable(@Table(”40s trans and rot adjusted”), ”column0”, LINEAR,
”column3”, ${Time})
