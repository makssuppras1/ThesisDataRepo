ABSTRACT 
The aim of this thesis, which is a collaboration between DTU Chemical Engineering and MAN Diesel 
& Turbo, was to investigate the formation and the decomposition of deposits in evaporation of urea in 
maritime urea-SCR processes, in order to obtain fundamental knowledge about the chemistry of the urea 
decomposition process, which can be of support for facilitating a design of an evaporation system that 
secures stable operation and high NO reduction efficiency of the urea-SCR process. 
The impact of heating rate on the urea decomposition process has been investigated, as most previous 
work have been limited to low heating rates. For this purpose, the urea decomposition process was in-
vestigated by the use of thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) at low to medium heating rate (5-500 oC/min), 
and by the use of a fixed bed reactor setup connected to an FTIR analyzer. The investigation by TGA 
indicated that production of CYA, which based on the literature survey is the main precursor for all other 
byproducts formed during the urea decomposition process, is inhibited to some extent for increasing 
heating rate. The results from the FBR setup were in general associated with large uncertainties due to 
issues with the feeding system and limitations of the FTIR. However, there were indications that produc-
tion of CYA is limited when urea is subjected to a high heating rate. 
The long term thermal stability behavior of urea was investigated by TGA. In this investigation, urea 
samples were heated to 280 oC and 380 oC and kept for four days, corresponding to exhaust temperatures 
at low and high load of maritime diesel engines, respectively. It was found that the urea decomposition 
process is not only temperature dependent, but also time dependent. The investigation showed that an 
isothermal temperature of 280 oC, over the course of four days, was insufficient for complete decompo-
sition of the urea sample. However, an isothermal temperature of 380 oC yielded complete decomposition 
of the urea sample after ~55 hours. The results thus implied that solid deposition can be avoided in mar-
itime urea-SCR systems by operating at full engine load over an extended period, e.g. three days. 
A rotary drum setup was used to generate deposits at furnace temperatures of 230, 280, 360, 430, 500 
and 600 oC with exposure times of one hour. It was attempted to identify these deposits by solid sample 
analysis including solid state FTIR, elemental analysis (EA) and TGA. From a combination of solid state 
FTIR and elemental analysis, it was proposed that the deposits generated at 230 oC and 280 oC primarily 
consisted of CYA. In the same manner, it was proposed that the deposit generated at 360 oC primarily 
consisted of a mixture of ammelide and ammeline. The deposits generated at 430 oC and 500 oC were 
proposed to consist of products from melamine self-condensation, such as melem or melon. A TGA 
experiment of the deposit generated at 430 oC showed that the sample was not fully decomposed even at 
1000 oC. Black residue was visually identified in the crucible after the TGA experiment, indicating for-
mation of large and very stable graphitic C3N4 polymer products. However, it was assessed that such 
graphitic polymer products should not appear in deposits formed in maritime urea-SCR systems, as diesel 
exhaust temperatures are rarely above 400 oC, even at full engine load.

PROJECT DESCRIPTION 
BACKGROUND 
Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR) of nitrogen oxides has become a leading technology in flue gas 
cleaning of the exhaust from maritime diesel engines. In the maritime industry, urea is the preferred 
reducing agent in SCR processes. Present mixing chambers have volumes comparable to those of the 
engine itself, and there is a strong incentive to significantly reduce the chamber size. Size reduction is 
expected to increase problems, known from automotive urea-SCR systems, with formation of undesired 
solid byproducts during urea decomposition that may clog the injection nozzles or form deposits down-
stream, causing restrictions in passage as well as enhancing corrosion. A better understanding of the urea 
decomposition chemistry, in particular the formation and decomposition of secondary products consti-
tuting the undesired deposits, is necessary to facilitate a design of an evaporation system that secures 
stable operation and a high NO reduction efficiency for the SCR process.   
OBJECTIVE 
The objective of this project is to characterize experimentally the decomposition process of urea and of 
the heavy molecular byproducts, constituting the undesired deposits. Decomposition of urea will be in-
vestigated over a wide range of temperatures and heating rates in different experimental setups. Based on 
the results, it will be assessed whether byproduct formation during urea decomposition can be minimized 
within the range of investigated process parameters.

INTRODUCTION 
The American Society of Testing Materials (ASTM) has divided diesel fuel into three classifications, 
namely 1D, 2D and 4D, listed in order of increasing density and viscosity. Grade number 4D is the 
heaviest diesel fuel, and is the primary diesel fuel in maritime industry [1,2]. In a diesel engine, the fuel 
is mixed with an excess of highly compressed air inside the combustion chamber of the engine, which 
generates sufficiently high temperatures to allow for spontaneous ignition of the fuel when it is injected 
into the cylinders [3]. For an ideal thermodynamic equilibrium, the only combustion products of a com-
plete combustion process would be CO2 and water. In practical applications, however, the exhaust from 
diesel engines typically contain approximately 1 vol. % pollutant emissions [4]. Five main pollutant emis-
sions from diesel exhaust are CO from incomplete combustion, hydrocarbons from unburned fuel, par-
ticulate matter (mainly soot), SO2 and NOx. The focus of this work will be on the reduction of NOx 
emissions from diesel exhaust. NOx from diesel exhaust typically consists of mostly NO (>90%) and 
remaining NO2 [5ŌĆō8].  
The maritime industry is a major contributor to the global emissions of NOx. In 2007, oceangoing vessels 
emitted ~25 million metric tons of NOx, representing ~15% of global anthropogenic NOx emissions [9]. 
NOx are significant contributors to environmental and health issues such as acidic nitrate deposition and 
lung irritation. The negative environmental and health effects of NOx have led to the introduction of the 
European emission standards. The newest legislation regarding the maritime sector was agreed upon in 
2008 by the Marine Environmental Protection Committee (MEPC) of the International Maritime Organ-
ization (IMO), and is referred to as MARPOL Annex VI. The NOx emission limits in MARPOL Annex 
VI are summarized in Table 1.1 [9].

As apparent from Table 1.1, the newest Tier III emission standards introduces much stricter requirements 
in the NOx limits for maritime engines. Therefore, measures to reduce NOx emissions from maritime 
engines have been of increasing importance. Such measures include optimization of the combustion pro-
cess, like the use of exhaust gas recirculation, and exhaust aftertreatment systems, such as selective cata-
lytic reduction (SCR). This work will only address the secondary aftertreatment of diesel exhaust. Spe-
cifically, it is of interest to investigate reported problems in the maritime industry of deposit formation 
during decomposition of urea, which is the preferred reducing agent in maritime selective catalytic re-
duction systems. Urea derived deposits have been reported to clog injection nozzles and cause restrictions 
in passage, as well as enhance corrosion. The scope of this project is therefore to gain a better understand-
ing of the urea decomposition chemistry, which can be of support for facilitating a design of a system that 
secures stable operation and a high NO reduction efficiency of the SCR process.

2.1 SELECTIVE CATALYTIC REDUCTION SYSTEMS FOR DIESEL EXHAUST 
SCR with NH3 as reducing agent is a well-developed, and the most common, technology in stationary 
sources for controlling emissions of NOx [11ŌĆō14]. The standard SCR reaction, when treating lean exhaust 
where NOx mainly consists of NO, is governed by (R 2.1) [7,14ŌĆō17]:

In practical SCR systems, the NOx reduction efficiency can typically be as high as 80-90%, but highly 
depends on the exhaust temperature, molar ratio of NH3/NOx, and the residence time of the exhaust in 
the catalyst bed [18]. The common SCR catalysts are based on TiO2-WO3-V2O5 [14]. The typical temper-
ature range of SCR is 250-450 oC [19]. (R 2.1) implies a 1:1 stoichiometry for NO and NH3. In stationary 
sources, NH3 is the most commonly used reducing agent and preferentially reacts with NO instead of O2 
due to the selectivity of the catalyst. In mobile DeNOx systems, NH3 may not be the preferred reducing 
agent as it introduces challenges in storage, handling and transportation, as it is corrosive and strongly 
toxic.  
For mobile systems, urea has been widely used as ammonia carrier, as it is inexpensive, nontoxic, soluble 
in water and can be thermally decomposed to yield the reducing agent NH3 [12,13,15]. Urea has a melting 
point of 132.7oC [20] and is thus a solid at room temperature. Dosing of solid urea in mobile systems is 
generally complicated, as it requires spherically formulated urea pellets of defined diameters, which can 
easily agglomerate into larger urea particles and cause transport limitation problems. In order to over-
come the problems of dosing urea into the main exhaust pipe, urea can be dosed as a urea-water-solution 
(UWS) by injection from a spray nozzle into the hot exhaust gas upstream of the catalyst where it then 
thermally decomposes. Urea decomposition is described in more detail in section 2.2. For mobile appli-
cations, a commercial UWS of 32.5 wt. % urea in water (with the trade name AdBlue┬«, commercially 
introduced in 2004 [21]) was chosen as a standardized solution due to the eutectic nature of the solution 
with a melting point of -11 oC [22]. In the maritime industry, a 40 wt. % UWS has been used, as the 
solution is stored in the engine room and is thus not exposed to low temperatures during operation [23]. 
In the automotive diesel industry, however, the relatively high melting point of AdBlue may pose prob-
lems when operating during winter [24].  
As illustrated in Figure 2.1, the SCR unit is typically located downstream of a diesel particulate filter, in 
order to remove the majority of diesel particulates from the exhaust and to avoid deposition of particu-
lates on the SCR catalysts. Often there will also be a diesel oxidation catalyst installed downstream of the 
SCR catalyst to reduce slip of unreacted ammonia.

The byproducts form corrosive deposits and may cause restrictions in operation due to clogging of injec-

2.2.3 EFFECT OF TEMPERATURE 
2.2.3.1 LABORATORY SCALE INVESTIGATION 
Schaber et al. investigated thermal decomposition of urea under laboratory conditions in an open reactor 
vessel by heating urea in a sand bath to certain temperatures and subsequently subjected to rapid cooling 
to room temperature [29]. The heating rate was not reported. The residue were collected and analyzed 
with high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) for urea, biuret, CYA, ammelide, ammeline and 
melamine. From the HPLC analysis, they constructed a HPLC mass plot as presented in Figure 2.3. 
They also performed thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) of urea samples with low heating rates of 10 
oC/min, as shown in Figure 2.4. From their investigation, they proposed a plausible temperature depend-
ent reaction scheme, and argued that urea decomposition in an open reaction vessel can be divided into 
four main reaction regions.  
1. First reaction region, 25-190 oC 
From room temperature to the melting point of urea (132.7 oC [20]), insignificant mass loss is observed. 
As observed from the TGA in Figure 2.4, significant mass loss is first observed at approximately 152 oC. 
Here, decomposition of urea is assumed to begin. At approximately 160 oC, biuret is produced from 
HNCO and intact urea, and is continuously being produced between 160-190 oC. Furthermore, trace 
amounts of CYA and ammelide are produced at approximately 175 oC.  
2. Second reaction region, 190-250 oC 
At approximately 190 oC, biuret reaches its melting point and starts to decompose as reflected in the mass 
maximum of biuret at 190 oC in Figure 2.3, and 1st derivative peak in the TGA near this temperature in 
Figure 2.4. Furthermore, this is also consistent with the TGA curve of pure biuret, as presented in Figure 
2.6, where biuret is seen to first lose mass at approximately 190 oC.  Prior to the decomposition of biuret, 
only small amounts of CYA and ammelide were observed. As biuret begins to decompose at approxi-
mately 190 oC, appreciable amounts of CYA and ammelide are produced. Traces of ammeline and mel-
amine were positively identified around 250 oC. As seen from Figure 2.2, production of CYA is proposed 
to occur through either reaction of biuret with HNCO, or trimerization of HNCO. In this temperature 
==PAGE:19==
Literature Survey 
6 
 
region, Schaber et al. expect the trimerization reaction to become more competitive in the CYA produc-
tion due to the decomposition of biuret and further decomposition of urea, effectively yielding two 
HNCO for every decomposed biuret [29]. They propose that the continued production of CYA and am-
melide is expected to contribute to the NH3 production as well. They reported formation of a white visible 
precipitate at temperatures exceeding 210 oC, and a complete solid ŌĆ£stickyŌĆØ residue, with CYA as the 
major constituent, at 225 oC. At 250 oC, the amounts of urea and biuret have depleted to minor levels 
(only CYA, ammeline and ammelide appears in appreciable amounts), and the residue no longer appears 
ŌĆ£stickyŌĆØ, indicating that the solidification of the matrix has completed. 
Additionally, they observed accumulation of ionic species in the residue in this reaction region. They 
expect that this behavior is due to diffusion limitations, where NH3 and HNCO are trapped inside the 
solid matrix together with other solid products which interacts with each other to form ionic species 
including ammonium cyanate, urea cyanurate and biuret cyanurate.  
3. Third reaction region, 250-360 oC 
From the urea TGA plot in Figure 2.4, a plateau region appears between 250-260 oC where no appreciable 
mass loss is observed, indicating that most of the products in the residue are relatively thermally stable in 
this temperature region. After 260 oC, a significant mass loss is observed in the urea TGA plot. Compar-
ing with the HPLC mass plot in Figure 2.3, the mass loss has been associated mainly with sublimation 
and decomposition of CYA and ammelide. They report that ammeline begins to lose mass at tempera-
tures exceeding 275 oC. At 320 oC, traces of urea and biuret are no longer detectable. A small plateau 
region is again observed in the urea TGA plot at 350-360 oC, which Schaber et al. associates with a 
substantial decrease in rates of decomposition and sublimation due to limited mass amounts [29]. 
4. Fourth reaction region, >360 oC 
In the last reaction region, a slow mass loss is observed, which reflects the continued decomposition and 
sublimation of CYA, ammelide and ammeline. Schaber et al. reports that CYA, ammelide and ammeline 
are completely eliminated from the system at 380 oC, 600 oC and 700 oC, respectively [29].

2.2.4 EFFECT OF HEATING RATE 
As discussed in section 2.2.3, the formation of byproducts during urea decomposition occurs in specific 
temperature intervals. It is likely that the extent of byproduct formation is dependent on the heating rate 
of the UWS, e.g. it may be possible to bypass the side reactions to some extent with a sufficiently high 
heating rate. However, previous investigations in the literature on the effect of heating rate on the deposit 
formation are scarce. Some authors report a negative effect of increasing heating rate due to an increase 
of HNCO concentration in the gas phase leading to enhanced formation of deposits, but have generally 
only studied this effect under low heating rates, e.g. variations of 10-20 oC/min [11]. Brack et al. recently 
reported, from a kinetic modeling study, that the rate of chemical conversion of urea derived species is 
inversely proportional to the heating rate, thus giving a shift towards higher temperatures for higher heat-
ing rates in typical TGA curves [48]. However, experimental studies of the impact of high heating rates 
on the urea decomposition products seem vacant, and is thus of interest to study within this project.  
2.2.5 EFFECT OF WALL WETTING 
Strots et al. performed engine tests of an inline, six-cylinder, 7.6 L, 300 hp MY 2007 Navistar engine with 
a common urea-SCR exhaust system for heavy-duty applications [47]. They investigated the effect of 
exhaust gas temperature, ambient temperature and UWS injection rate on deposit formation. When in-
jecting the UWS into the exhaust gas, they measured a drop in the wall temperature of ~20 oC, which 
may be due to the endothermic nature of the urea decomposition into NH3 and HNCO. The wall tem-
perature was typically ~50 oC lower than the exhaust temperature, which is in good agreement with 
typical automotive SCR systems using UWS, as presented in Table 2.2. They reported a reduction of 
deposit yields (defined as the percentage of the mass of deposit formed to the mass of injected urea on 
dry basis) by almost fifty times when increasing the exhaust temperature from 220 oC to 350 oC (from 
~65% to ~1.5%). Furthermore, they reported that colder temperatures outside the exhaust pipe resulted 
in higher deposit yields. In order to reduce wall wetting, they investigated the effect of installing a 
wiremesh in the exhaust pipe to assist in the interception and UWS droplet evaporation. They reported 
a significant reduction in deposit yields with the installed wiremesh. Additionally, they reported that 
deposit formation could be further reduced by insulation of the exhaust pipe. As a final measure, they 
attempted to coat the wiremesh with a TiO2 catalyst to hydrolyze HNCO but found no clear conclusion 
on the effect. 
From the work of Strots et al., it seems likely that the formation of a stable wall film has a major contri-
bution to the formation of deposits in the exhaust pipe [47]. It is also expected that the effect of wall 
wetting will be enhanced when reducing the size of the system. Therefore, it could be of interest to per-
form more extensive studies on this phenomenon, and develop techniques in order to diminish or com-
pletely avoid wall wetting, but will not be investigated within the scope of this project. 
2.2.6 PHYSICAL EFFECTS 
In order to obtain a better practical understanding of the urea-SCR process, it is important to differentiate 
between chemical effects (such as the effects previously described) and physical effects including mass 
and heat transfer, fluid dynamics and droplet behavior. Geometry factors such as the design of the UWS 
injector mounting and installation of mixers greatly influences the fluid dynamics, and will thus have an 
impact on the deposit formation during urea decomposition in a real urea-SCR application. Spray char-
acteristics such as UWS droplet size, injection rate and spray pattern may also have a significant impact 
on the deposit formation. Zheng et al. performed experimental investigations of a urea-SCR test plant 
(presented in Figure 2.11) capable of simulating conditions similar to typical diesel exhaust systems for 
medium- to heavy duty trucks [49]. They tested the influence of various physical factors on the deposit 
formation, including injector mounting geometry, installation of mixers and injection rate, and the results

are reviewed in the following subsections. However, they performed no byproduct composition analysis 
of the deposits.

2.2.6.1 INFLUENCE OF INJECTOR MOUNTING GEOMETRY 
As described in sections 2.2.3 and 2.2.5, deposit formation occurs at temperatures lower than typical 
diesel exhaust, e.g. from side reactions of HNCO in stable wall films. Thus, an obvious strategy for re-
ducing deposit formation is to develop an injection design that reduces direct impingement on the walls. 
Zheng et al. investigated two different injection designs: A 45 degree injection mounting on a straight 
pipe, and a coaxial injection mounting on an S-shaped pipe [49]. The two designs were compared by 
choosing temperature as the independent variable (as temperature is expected to have the strongest influ-
ence on deposit formation), while all other design parameters and operating conditions were kept con-
stant. From this investigation, they reported a lower amount of deposition at low temperatures using the 
S-shaped pipe geometry, indicating that the S-shaped conformation injects the UWS more directly into 
the exhaust gas than the 45 degree injection mounting. From these observations, the coaxial injection 
mounting on an S-shaped pipe may be the preferred injection design. However, for existing urea-SCR 
systems it may not be a preferred parameter to change, as it can include expensive reconstructions of the 
system. 
2.2.6.2 INFLUENCE OF MIXERS 
Installation of in-pipe mixing devices can be used to promote UWS droplet evaporation and increase the 
turbulence. By increasing the turbulence, the convective heat transfer is also enhanced, and thus in-pipe 
mixing devices are able to distribute heat towards the walls more effectively. From their investigations 
on the pilot test plant, Zheng et al. reported an increase of ~20 oC in the outer pipe wall temperature 
when using a mixer, compared to test runs without a mixer [49]. Considering the rather low thermal 
resistance of the pipe wall in this test plant of 8.82ŌłÖ10-5 m2ŌłÖK/W and the low thickness of the pipe, it may 
be reasonable to assume a negligible temperature change from the outer to the inner surface of the pipe. 
Thus, the reported increase of ~20 oC in outer pipe wall temperature when using a mixer is expected to 
be representative for the surface of the inner wall as well. An increase of inner wall temperature is ex-
pected to reduce the deposit formation. However, an undesired effect of increasing turbulence may be 
increasing cross-sectional mass transfer of UWS towards the wall. The individual effects of the enhanced 
mass and heat transfer from increasing turbulence have not been investigated in this framework. They 
did, however, report a decrease in the deposit formation when applying mixers in the plant design. Again, 
installation of mixers in already existing urea-SCR systems may not be the preferred parameter to change, 
as it can include expensive reconstructions of the system.

2.2.6.3 INFLUENCE OF UREA INJECTION RATE 
The influence of urea injection rate was investigated by mounting a glass window on the exhaust pipe in 
order to visually track formation of deposits and liquid wall films. For different exhaust flow rates and 
temperatures, the urea injection rate was increased until wall wetting occurred. From these experiments, 
Zheng et al. [49]  reported that the urea injection rate could be allowed to be higher without wall-wetting 
effects for increasing exhaust temperature and flow rate. Both these trends seem to agree with the trend 
of reduced wall wetting for increasing heat transfer to the pipe wall. In actual urea-SCR systems, however, 
the urea injection rate is typically chosen in regards of optimizing DeNOx while also facilitating a low 
ammonia slip, whereas less emphasis is given towards the wall-wetting effects. 
 
2.3 MODELLING OF THE EVAPORATION AND THERMAL DECOMPOSITION OF 
UREA-WATER SOLUTION IN SELECTIVE CATALYTIC REDUCTION SYSTEMS 
Development of theoretical and predictive models to describe the behavior of injected UWS in SCR sys-
tems has been of increasing interest, in order to achieve a better understanding of the depletion mecha-
nism of typical UWS droplets in SCR systems. Specifically for this work, it is of interest to be able to 
describe the transient temperature profile and lifetime of the UWS droplets. This section will review and 
discuss some models proposed in the literature. Essentially, it is well agreed upon that the depleting 
mechanism of UWS droplets in an SCR system should be modelled as spherically symmetric UWS drop-
lets (multicomponent mixture) in a stagnant heated environment. The process includes vaporization of 
water to yield highly concentrated or molten urea, followed by thermolysis of urea into HNCO and NH3. 
In most cases, spherical symmetry of the urea particles is assumed, which greatly reduces the complexity 
and computational time of the models. The heated environment is assumed to have uniform temperature, 
pressure and species concentrations. Essentially, heat from the hot exhaust gas is used to heat up the 
UWS droplets and deplete its components. The geometry of the problem to be modeled is illustrated in 
Figure 2.12. As illustrated, a heat flux from the environment towards the droplet drives a mass flux of 
droplet components away from the droplet towards the environment. A thin film region is established 
around the liquid droplet, where the temperature depends on the droplet surface temperature and the 
temperature of the environment. The reference temperature in the film regions is described further in 
section 2.3.1. 
In the following sections 2.3.1 - 2.3.4, the modeling of evaporation of water from UWS droplets and urea 
thermolysis will be covered by reviewing the framework of two different groups, namely Ebrahimian et 
al. and Abu-Ramadan et al. [11,17]. An outline of the model assumptions and equations will be pre-
sented, as well as a comparison and discussion of their simulation results.

In this model, thermodynamic equilibrium is assumed at the gas-liquid interface. However, Ebrahimian 
et al. assume the liquid UWS droplets as a highly non-ideal mixture, whereas the vapor phase is assumed 
to follow ideal behavior [11]. As an ideal vapor phase is generally a good assumption for low-pressure 
systems, the ideal gas equation of state is expected to give a reasonably accurate description of the fugacity 
of the vapor phase in real mobile urea-SCR applications. However, as they describe the liquid phase as 
highly non-ideal, they have introduced an activity coefficient model to modify the fugacity of the species 
in the liquid phase. In this work, it was chosen to implement the non-random two-liquid (NRTL) model, 
which essentially is an excess enthalpy local composition model, without an entropic term, which in-
cludes fitting of six binary interaction parameters against experimental data [53]. In this work, the authors 
collected the binary interaction parameters for the NRTL model for UWS from the literature.

2.3.3 COMPARISON OF MODELS 
The work of the two reviewed author groups both attempts to model the depleting mechanism of urea-
water solution droplets in urea-SCR systems. As reviewed in sections 2.3.1 - 2.3.2, both models include 
evaporation of water followed by thermal decomposition of urea. The two proposed models are basically 
very similar in the overall modeling framework, as reflected in Table 2.5. However, there are multiple 
details which differ in the two modeling frameworks, both in the assumptions and the features of the 
models, which evidently yield two considerably different models. Outlines of the most important features 
of the models developed by the two reviewed author groups are summarized in Table 2.5. 
As it can be seen from Table 2.5, both models have included a gas phase, gas-liquid interface and a liquid 
phase analysis in the water evaporation model. However, Abu-Ramadan et al. [17] includes heat transfer 
by radiation in the energy conservation, whereas Ebrahimian et al. [11] chooses to neglect this term. In

turn, Ebrahimian et al. [11] assumes a non-ideal liquid phase and have thus implemented the NRTL 
activity coefficient model in the gas-liquid interface analysis, whereas Abu-Ramadan et al. [17] assumes 
both ideal vapor and liquid phase. Whether ideality of the liquid phase can be justified or not seems 
unclear from the literature. Some author groups supports the hypothesis of near ideality of urea solutions 
[58ŌĆō60]. Simulation of the vapor-liquid equilibrium (VLE) of urea/water mixture, using an in-house soft-
ware ŌĆ£SPECSŌĆØ, also supports near ideality of the urea/water mixture, as the simulation of the VLE pre-
dicts no azeotrope, and furthermore predicts a large split between the dew point and bubble point curves 
(see simulated VLE in appendix 8.2). Unfortunately, there appears to be no experimental VLE available 
in the literature for validation of the simulated VLE. Other studies suggests a high extent of hydrogen 
bonding between urea and water, which is generally an indication of non-ideality [61].  
A main advantage of Ebrahimian et al. is the capability of predicting byproduct formation from a CSTR 
simulation using kinetic expressions [11]. However, the simulations are to a great extent associated with 
parameter fitting, thus yielding the model less predictive. Abu-Ramadan et al. does not consider byprod-
uct formation, but instead investigated two plausible reaction pathways through vaporization of urea 
followed by decomposition in the gas phase, and an approach where urea decomposition is assumed to 
occur directly from the solid state and governed by kinetic first-order expressions [17].

2.3.4 MODELING RESULTS 
In order to assess the accuracy of the combined models describing water evaporation from multicompo-
nent UWS droplets followed by urea thermolysis, comparison between experimental results and numer-
ical model predictions is required. Unfortunately, experimental data of the depleting mechanisms of 
UWS droplets in heated environments are very limited. It appears that most authors compare their urea 
depletion models with experimental data from Wang et al., who used a high-speed charge-coupled device 
camera to track the transient change of UWS droplets in heated environments [62]. In their experiments, 
heat transfer by radiation was minimized by use of radiative shields. Thus, the models should also neglect 
radiation heat transfer to imitate the experimental results. Furthermore, it is necessary to match the drop-
let and exhaust characteristics of the model to those of the experiments. Unfortunately, it appears that no 
experimental data of the temporal temperature of the droplet are available in the literature. Therefore, 
the models of different authors have only been validated through comparison with experimental data of 
temporal size changes.  
 
2.3.4.1 ABU-RAMADAN ET AL. [17] 
The performance of the model developed by Abu-Ramadan et al. [17] is presented in Figure 2.14, where 
six different simulations have been included in the comparison. As can be seen, these simulations give 
identical results for the first part of the depleting mechanism, namely the evaporation of water from the 
droplet, which is seen to initially follow a linear d2/d02 decrease in time, and thus initially follows the 
well-known d2 Law model [63]. After some time, however, the simulation results of the six different 
models splits up into different depleting rates. This is due to different approaches of modeling urea ther-
molysis. In Figure 2.14, models referred to as ŌĆ£Kinetics-VŌĆØ and ŌĆ£Kinetics-SŌĆØ are volumetric and surface 
area based kinetic first-order models assuming the direct thermolysis of urea from solid or molten phase, 
respectively. Models referred to as ŌĆ£VapŌĆØ assumes vaporization of urea and subsequent decomposition 
in the gas phase. Based on these results, the decomposition of urea from gas phase appears to give the 
most accurate prediction of experimental results from Wang et al. [62], whereas the models based on 
direct thermolysis of urea underestimates the depleting rate of urea. Therefore, the authors concluded 
that the urea depleting process is most accurately described by the vaporization process. However, this 
conclusion does not appear to be widely supported in the literature.  
Additionally, it can be seen from Figure 2.14 that fluctuations appear in the experimental data of Wang 
et al. close to the point where urea thermolysis supposedly takes over [62]. Wang et al. associates these 
fluctuations to microexplosions of the droplets. Microexplosions can occur if the more volatile compo-
nent (in this case water) is trapped inside the droplet due to diffusional resistance and heated beyond its 
boiling point to cause nucleation and thereby internal pressure build-up. This behavior is observed for 
increasing time, which suggests that an increase of urea vapor pressure at the droplet surface over time 
(as water evaporates) causes diffusion limitations of water. This phenomenon is furthermore observed to 
be enhanced at elevated exhaust temperature, due to more aggressive evaporation leading to a faster 
internal pressure build-up. As the microexplosions appear to be random, it is not possible to accurately 
predict the phenomenon. It is, however, possible to predict the mean depleting rate of urea thermolysis, 
which is also of higher interest.

2.4 CONCLUDING REMARKS ON THE LITERATURE SURVEY 
From the literature survey, it can be concluded that urea is the most commonly used ammonia carrier for 
SCR in mobile systems due to advantages in storage and handling. However, many different author 
groups, mainly concerning urea-SCR in the automotive industry, have reported issues of significant de-
posit formation in the injection nozzles, inside the exhaust pipe and on the catalyst during urea decom-
position, and thus cause restrictions in passage as well as enhancing corrosion. From the literature survey, 
it is apparent that urea decomposition is a very complex process, involving evaporation of water from 
liquid urea-water solution droplets, followed by decomposition of urea into NH3 and HNCO. The state 
of urea during decomposition could not be concluded from the literature survey. It seems widely accepted 
that HNCO is kinetically stable in the gas phase, but is highly reactive with respect to byproduct for-
mation in the condensed state.  Byproducts in urea decomposition mainly consists of unreacted urea, 
biuret and CYA, along with more stable triazines such as ammelide, ammeline and melamine.  
Based on TGA experiments, Schaber et al. [29] reported that urea decomposition can be divided into 
four main temperature regions: Between 25-190 oC, urea is heated up and decomposes while biuret is 
produced. Between 190-250 oC, biuret decomposes while CYA and ammelide along with traces of am-
melide and melamine are produced. Between 250-360 oC, CYA and ammelide either decomposes or 
sublimes. CYA, ammelide and ammeline are completely eliminated from the system at 380 oC, 600 oC 
and 700 oC, respectively [29]. 
To the knowledge of the author of the present project, TGA experiments have only been conducted with 
low heating rates of 5-20 oC/min. A kinetic modeling study on urea decomposition by Brack et al. [48] 
indicates an impact of heating rate on the chemical conversion of urea and urea derived deposits. In 
practical urea-SCR systems, UWS droplets are subjected to very high heating rates when injected into a 
heated environment.  
Based on the engine tests performed by Strots et al. [47], it seems that the formation of a stable wall film 
consisting of molten urea can have a major contribution to the formation of deposits in the exhaust pipe. 
Further investigations of this phenomena could be very interesting, e.g. experimental determination of 
UWS wall film temperatures at different operating conditions. 
Multiple author groups, including Ebrahimian et al. [11] and Abu-Ramadan et al. [17], have attempted 
to model the evaporation and thermal decomposition of UWS in SCR. The evaporation process is gen-
erally described by the same governing equations for the gas and liquid phase, while thermodynamic 
equilibrium is assumed for the gas-liquid interface. The models by different author groups mainly differ 
in the model assumptions (see Table 2.5). Modeling of urea decomposition have been treated both as 
evaporation of urea followed by decomposition in the gas phase, and as direct thermal decomposition 
from molten/solid urea. The model of Ebrahimian et al. [11] also considers byproduct formation during 
urea decomposition, but is highly associated with parameter fitting. The two models from Ebrahimian et 
al. [11] and Abu-Ramadan et al. [17] both yield the transient evolution in temperature and droplet size 
of a UWS droplet when injected in a heated environment. However, the models have only been compared 
with experimental data of the transient evolution of droplet size obtained from Wang et al. [62] under 
constant gas temperature. To the knowledge of the author, there is no experimental data available in the 
literature for validating the model predictions of the transient temperature evolution.

3.3.1 EXPERIMENTAL SETUP 
The experimental setup of the TGA-FTIR is shown in Figure 3.3, and consists of: 
1. Coupled TGA-FTIR device. The FTIR is mounted directly on top of the TGA furnace. A high 
accuracy balance is installed in the TGA which measures the change in mass over time during a 
user specified temperature and gas flow program. The chosen gas mixture exit at the gas outlet 
together with all evolved gasses and is transported directly to the FTIR for gas analysis.  
2. Computer with the ŌĆ£ProteusŌĆØ software to program and control the TGA-FTIR, pulse device 
and mass flow controller (MFC). 
3. Pulse device allowing injection of pulses into the TGA-FTIR (optional). 
4. Temperature controller for the transfer line between the TGA and FTIR. 
5. Vacuum pump to empty the TGA-FTIR for gases. 
6. Water bath for TGA balance set to 25 oC. 
Unfortunately, the FTIR was out of order during the period of this project, and has thus not been uti-
lized. Nevertheless, the setup will still be referred to as the TGA-FTIR setup. 
The MFC is located behind the pulse device. Up to three gases can be connected to the MFC, which 
control the flow rate of gases to the TGA-FTIR. One of the connected gases must be pure N2 (purity of 
99.999%), which at all times during experiments must flow at a recommended flow rate of 100 mL/min 
to protect the TGA balance from impurities. The remaining gas connections are user specified and op-
tional. The pressure in the Teflon gas lines from the source is kept at a constant 0.5 barg.

3.3.2 METHODOLOGY 
Initially, the water bath is turned on and set to 23 oC. Additionally, the heating element for the connection 
tube between the TGA and FTIR is turned on and set to 250 oC in order to reduce the likelihood of 
condensation of gases in the FTIR. Prior to a TGA experiment with a specified temperature and flow 
program, a correction run of the exact same conditions must be performed on an empty alumina crucible, 
as also described in section 3.3.1.2. When the correction is obtained, a sample of approximately 50 mg 
will be weighed on a balance with an uncertainty of ┬▒0.01 mg, and placed in the crucible. The crucible 
is placed in the TGA furnace, and gasses are evacuated from the system in order to have a well-defined 
environment during the experiment. It is especially important to evacuate the system if it is desired to use 
the FTIR, as presence of water vapor and CO2 can affect the background measurements of the FTIR. The 
temperature and flow program of a given experiment is programmed in the software ŌĆ£ProteusŌĆØ on the 
computer, as also described in section 3.3.1. When the program of a given experiment has been specified 
in the software, the gas flows are turned, the balance is tared, and the experiment is subsequently started.  
The TGA measurements are shown in the Proteus Analysis software, and can be directly exported to 
Microsoft Excel for further data analysis. The crucible should be properly cleaned after an experiment. 
The TGA may also be used for this purpose, by subjecting the crucible to a high-temperature cleaning 
program, e.g. with a heating rate of 20 oC/min up to 1000 oC , and keep the sample isothermally at 1000 
oC for 5 min. With this temperature program, it should be safe to assume that the urea deposits have all 
been thermally removed from the crucible.

3.5.1 EXPERIMENTAL SETUP 
The layout of the fixed bed reactor used in this project is depicted in Figure 3.7. The reactor is a vertical 
laboratory-scale quartz fixed bed reactor (see dimensions in appendix 8.3). A particle feeder, along with

length of 5.11 m and a volume of 200 mL. The temperature of the gas cell is set to 191 oC. At this tem-
perature, the FTIR has been calibrated to be accurate for NH3 and HNCO in the following concentration 
ranges [69]:

3.5.2 METHODOLOGY 
In order to get reliable results from the FBR experiments with FTIR, several factors need to be addressed, 
including heat tracing of the outlet tube, calibration of gas flows and a viable method for solid sample 
dosage to the reactor. These methods will be described in the following subsections.  
3.5.2.1 HEAT TRACING 
As the purpose of this setup is to quantify the amount of the released NH3 and HNCO during urea de-
composition, it is essential that the evolved NH3 and HNCO enter the FTIR. However, NH3 is known to 
easily adsorb on many surfaces, including Teflon. This introduces a practical problem to the setup, as 
adsorption or even condensation of NH3 on the outlet Teflon tube may yield false results. In order to 
minimize the adsorption of NH3, the outlet tube was heat traced to 180 oC by a heating element, as 
increasing temperature is expected to reduce adsorption [71,72]. The temperature was carefully chosen, 
as it should be as high as possible, yet lower than the gas cell temperature in the FTIR of 191 oC so that 
potential condensation of gasses occur in the outlet tube instead of inside the FTIR gas cell. 
During the project, it was found that the positioning of the thermocouple along the outlet tube was critical 
for the heat tracing, as the temperature along the outlet tube of ~2m could vary up to ~20 oC from inlet 
to outlet. The optimal position of the heating tape was found to be near the middle of the outlet tube 
where the temperature was highest, in order to reduce the risk of melting of the tube. Furthermore, it 
should be guaranteed that the heating tape is positioned in direct contact with the tube, to ensure the 
temperature of the tube is close to the set point temperature. If the heating tape is misplaced, the temper-
ature of the tube can become much higher and risk melting. Therefore, it was chosen not to isolate the 
outlet tube, so that the position of the heating tape could always be visually inspected. 
3.5.2.2 GAS FLOW CALIBRATION 
As the concentration of gases measured in the FTIR is dependent on the gas flow, it is necessary to 
calibrate the inlet gas flow to the reactor, as the gas flow would otherwise have a direct uncertainty on 
the quantification of the evolved gases. The N2 inlet gas flow to the reactor was calibrated with a Gilibra-
tor-2 system from Sensidyne, depicted in Figure 3.8. The principle of this calibration system is to send 
gas flow through a wet cell and manually generate bubble films. The bubble flow rate is then measured 
by an infrared sensor and displayed on the screen.

3.5.2.3 SOLID SAMPLE DOSAGE 
Prior to an experiment, the reactor is heated to a desired temperature by the three electrically heated 
ovens. As a precaution, the nitrogen flow to the top of the system is turned on during heating of the 
reactor (typically ~500 mL/min) to avoid potential melting of the plastic material inherent in the feeding 
device, as the flow will have a cooling effect. Additionally, flow in the top of the reactor will create the 
necessary overpressure for solid sample dosage. As the HNCO calibration of the FTIR is only valid below 
400 ppm, it was necessary to feed very small amounts of urea samples. From initial experiments, it was 
found that urea samples should be dosed in amounts below 1 mg, in order not to exceed the upper limit 
of the HNCO calibration in the FTIR. Thus, the amount of sample that could be fed were very small. 
The balance that was used for weighing the samples is precise down to ┬▒0.01 mg. The uncertainty of the 
sample mass is therefore of considerable magnitude for samples below 1 mg.  
Due to the small amount of sample mass, it was decided to prepare the samples as single particles. When 
only feeding one particle, it was also easier to identify if the entire sample entered the reactor by observing 
the output signals of the FTIR. If the FTIR did show NH3 and HNCO signals immediately after feeding 
urea, it would be safe to assume that the entire sample had entered the system, as only one particle was 
fed. However, if the particle gets stuck at a higher point than the fixed bed, e.g. on the surface of the glass 
tube immediately after passing valve-a (see Figure 3.7), the local temperature of the sample might be 
lower than the intended decomposition temperature. It is a large uncertainty if the exact decomposition 
temperature is unknown, as the release of gaseous substances during urea decomposition is known to be 
temperature dependent, and any further mass balance calculations will be impossible to interpret. Con-
sidering this issue, it was decided to add sand as support when feeding urea samples to the reactor, typi-
cally in the order of 2-3 g. The purpose of the sand is to make it less likely that the urea particle gets stuck 
somewhere on the surface of the glass tube, while also being inert in the urea decomposition process. The 
used sand particles have a diameter of 0.6-0.71 mm, and are thus too large to pass the porous quartz plate 
so that no sand will end up in the FTIR. 
 
3.6 ROTARY DRUM WITH IR CAMERA 
3.6.1 EXPERIMENTAL SETUP 
After carefully considering how to investigate the transient temperature of a UWS wall film, it was de-
cided to use an already constructed high-temperature rotary drum, depicted in Figure 3.9. A PI-diagram 
of the setup is given in appendix 8.4. The chamber furnace is electrically heated by heating elements 
imbedded in the walls. A steel drum of type Sandvik MA253 [74] is positioned in the furnace chamber 
and is supported by a steel tube connected to a motor by means of a roller chain system, thus facilitated 
in order to rotate the drum. Circulation of cooling water is added to the part of the steel tube located 
outside of the furnace, in order to protect the rollers, roller chain and gas sealing from high temperatures. 
Purge gas can be transported to the system through a gas inlet located at the top of the furnace, which 
can be sufficiently pre-heated before entering the furnace. The gas supply can be up to 500 NL/min of 
either air or N2. The gas exits the system and is transported to gas analyzers, which unfortunately for this 
project can only measure O2, CO, CO2, SO2 and NO but not NH3 or HNCO. However, the primary 
purpose of the setup is to measure the transient sample temperature, while less emphasis is given towards 
the gas phase products. For this purpose, the furnace door is equipped with a sapphire glass window for 
visual inspection, onto which an IR camera can be mounted to track the transient sample temperature. 
The implementation of an IR camera mounted on the sapphire glass window is a part of the present 
project. A feeding device extending through the door to the furnace, made of a steel tube and a sample

3.6.2 METHODOLOGY 
In the experiments performed in this project, Adblue┬« (32.5 wt% urea-water solution) and dry urea sam-
ples will be fed to the sample container in the order of 5 g per experiment. The gas flow will be set to 50 
L/min of pure N2. The rotational speed will be set to 1 min-1. The operating temperature, however, will 
be varied from each experiment. Samples are only fed to the chamber at steady state.  
The experiments are conducted in two ways. As the sample container can flipped, the sample can either 
be dropped onto the rotating drum, or it can be kept in another crucible without rotating the sample 
container. The former experiment is expected to give the best imitation of the real urea-SCR system as 
the rotary drum is preheated, whereas the heating rate of the sample in the latter will be lower due to the 
crucible. Thus, for experiments where the main purpose is to investigate the transient temperature of a 
wall film, it may be favorable to drop the sample directly onto the rotating drum. The drum is not build 
to stand still, but can rotate at very low rotational speeds, and the rotation is thus not expected to have a 
great influence on the results. The latter experiment, however, may be used to generate sufficient amounts 
of deposits for identification by solid sample analysis. Available analysis methods in the present project 
for identifying deposits generated in the rotary drum include deposit yield calculations, solid state FTIR 
and elemental analysis. 
3.6.3 RECONSTRUCTION OF THE SETUP 
The main purpose of using the rotary drum setup was to investigate the transient temperature of a UWS 
wall film in a heated environment by IR spectroscopy. In initial experiments, 10 mL distilled water was 
fed to the rotary drum, which was preheated to 380 oC. The purpose of this initial experiment was to 
determine whether or not it was possible to obtain reliable temperatures with the IR camera. As the wet 
bulb temperature and emissivity of water are well known, initial experiments with water could be used 
to validate the results obtained by the IR camera. If the initial tests with distilled water could yield reliable 
results, further experiments with UWS could be performed and assessed with more confidence. 
In the first initial experiment, the furnace of the rotary drum setup was preheated to 160 oC. Then, 10 mL 
distilled water was injected onto the rotating drum, and the process was visually inspected by use of the 
IR camera as presented in Figure 3.10. Approximately 5 s after the water sample came in contact with 
the rotating drum, the picture from the IR camera was completely blocked from condensation of water 
vapor on the sapphire glass. It was assessed that if condensation occurred for a water sample even at 160 
oC, condensation would likely occur for a UWS sample at furnace temperatures of ~380 oC, which are 
the furnace temperatures of interest. Therefore, it was decided to reconstruct the setup by installing a 
purge where a pressurized N2 flow can be sprayed onto the sapphire glass, in order to avoid condensation.

4.1 SOLID STATE FTIR 
The purities of the materials used in this project, provided by the manufacturer Sigma-Aldrich, are pre-
sented in Table 3.1. The high reported purities have been validated within this project by solid state FTIR. 
The resulting IR spectra are compared with literature data from the National Institute of Standards and 
Technology (NIST) database in Figure 4.1 - Figure 4.5. In this comparison, the purities of the byproducts 
are assessed by comparing whether the major peaks of transmittance of the samples are in good agree-
ment with the spectra from NIST. As it can be seen from Figure 4.1 to Figure 4.5, the IR spectra of the 
byproducts are generally in good agreement with the corresponding IR spectra from NIST.  
The IR spectra obtained from NIST were measured in solution in ŌĆ£split solventŌĆØ cells [76]. In this method, 
the solvent must have adequate solubility with the sample, it must not react with the sample, and it must 
be transparent in the infrared spectral region of interest, in order not to have an influence on the IR spectra 
of the sample. Differences in state of sample should be kept in mind when comparing data obtained from 
NIST to those obtained from solid state FTIR. The IR spectrum of a sample in solution can depend on 
its concentration in solution [77]. Therefore, the shifts in transmittance as observed in Figure 4.1 - Figure 
4.5 are expected to be due to the differences in sample conditions, and hence are not interpreted as an 
indication of impurities in the byproducts.  
Thus, it has been assessed that the reported purities of the purchased urea byproducts are reliable.

4.2 TGA 
As described in section 3, two different TGA setups have been used in this project to study the decompo-
sition of urea. The TGA-FTIR setup described in section 3.3 was used to investigate the effect of heating 
rate on the decomposition process. Furthermore, the TGA-FTIR setup was used to study the long term 
thermal stability of urea and urea derived deposits when exposed to temperatures similar to real maritime 
urea-SCR systems over a long period of time. 
The TGA-DSC setup described in section 3.4 was used to investigate the effect of particle size on the 
decomposition process, as well as study the decomposition of the four main urea decomposition byprod-
ucts including biuret, CYA, ammelide and ammeline, which will be used as reference curves in further 
data analysis.  
4.2.1 OVERVIEW OF EXPERIMENTS 
An overview of the performed TGA experiments, with the respective operating conditions, are presented 
in Table 4.1. All experiments listed in Table 4.1, except the long term thermal stability investigations, are 
performed twice to ensure reproducibility. As it can be seen from Table 4.1, there are two different pa-
rameter sweeps including particle size and heating rate. In these experiments, the gas flow was set as 100 
mL/min of pure N2, and the set point temperature were always set to 800 oC, as the literature indicate 
that 800 oC is sufficient to fully decompose urea samples. After any experiment, the crucible was subjected 
to a cleaning program, in order to ensure that no deposits survive from one experiment to another. In the 
cleaning program, the crucible and potential residue were heated at 20 oC/min up to 1000 oC, and kept 
isothermally at 1000 oC for 5 min.  
In the long term thermal stability investigations, the TGA was set to run for four days, as this is a repre-
sentative operating period of a typical maritime urea-SCR system. These experiments were only per-
formed at two different temperatures, representing typical temperatures of diesel exhaust at low and high 
engine load respectively, as they are significantly more time consuming than the other TGA experiments.

4.2.2 IMPACT OF PROCESS PARAMETERS 
The impact of initial particle size and heating rate on the urea decomposition process was studied using 
TGA. All results have been gathered and compared with literature data obtained from Schaber et al. [29] 
in Figure 4.6. The lines with markers are performed in the TGA described in section 3.3, whereas the red 
and blue lines are performed in a different TGA setup. The latter uses an auto-sampler and a reference 
crucible, and is thus also able to generate DSC curves. However, the dimensions of the crucibles for this 
TGA is much smaller. Typical sample masses in this setup was 3-7 mg. As Schaber et al. [29] uses yet 
another TGA setup, Figure 4.6 contains information gathered from three different experimental setups. 
Therefore, a direct comparison between different TGA setups may not be possible, but similar trends in 
general should be observed. The observed trends from the TGA results are seen to be in close agreement 
with results obtained from Schaber et al. [29].  
4.2.2.1 IMPACT OF INITIAL PARTICLE SIZE 
The impact of initial particle size was studied in the TGA-DSC setup. Two experiments (the red and blue 
line in Figure 4.6) were performed with urea particle sizes of 106-180 ┬Ąm and 1180-1400 ┬Ąm respectively, 
prepared as described in section 3.2. In these two experiments, the heating rate was set to 5 K/min, and 
a protective N2 flow of 100 mL/min was used. From the TGA results of these two experiments, it is clear 
that initial particle size has no influence on the urea decomposition process at low heating rates, as the 
two TGA curves lie directly on top of each other. This is also the expected behavior, as differences in 
mass and heat transfer due to increasing particle size should not have a high impact when the heating 
rate of particles is low. At a slow heating rate, it is thus assessed that the urea sample melts and decom-
poses from a molten state equally fast for small and large particles. For high heating rates, there could 
potentially be an impact of initial particle size, as mass and heat transfer limitations for increasing particle 
size might have a greater impact. However, this have not investigated in this project. 
4.2.2.2 IMPACT OF HEATING RATE 
The impact of heating rate on the urea decomposition process was studied in the TGA described in sec-
tion 3.3 with a heating rate sweep of 5, 10, 50 and 500 K/min up to 600 oC, which corresponds to low to 
medium heating rate. Investigations of the impact of a high heating rate have been conducted in the FBR-
FTIR setup as described in section 3.5. An immediate observation from the TGA results is that the TGA 
curves have approximately the same slopes, but are shifted towards higher temperatures for increasing 
heating rate. However, an increase in heating rate is not expected to have an impact on the process itself 
to yield this shift in temperature. This behavior seems more likely to be due to a delayed heating of the 
sample compared to the furnace temperature, or limitations in the reaction kinetics. Whether it is the 
delayed heating of the sample or limitations in the reaction kinetics that has the greatest impact on this 
observation have not been identified within this project. In future work, this might be investigated by 
testing with a material with a very well defined melting point. 
Another trend from Figure 4.6 is that the first large mass loss of the TGA curves, before the first temper-
ature interval of stable mass, is seen to be increasing for increasing heating rate. This behavior indicates 
that for increasing heating rate, less urea decomposition byproducts (e.g. biuret and CYA) are formed 
during the second reaction region, as defined by Schaber et al. [29]. Schaber et al. [29] also describe that 
CYA is the main component in the third reaction region. This is further supported by the TGA curve of 
pure CYA, which is presented in Figure 4.9 in section 4.2.4.2. When assuming that the shift in tempera-
ture for increasing heating rate is primarily due to a delayed heating of the sample, the reduced production 
of CYA for increasing heating rate seems to be the only major impact of the heating rate on urea decom-
position. This is an interesting observation, as it indicates that an increased heating rate will limit the 
extent of secondary reactions responsible for the production of CYA. However, the amount of data gath-
ered in this analysis might not be extensive enough to be able to state a clear conclusion to the impact of 
heating rate on the urea decomposition process.

4.2.3 LONG TERM THERMAL STABILITY INVESTIGATION 
From the literature survey, it was found that many author groups report deposit formation in test facilities 
imitating real urea-SCR systems at exhaust temperatures varying from 180-400 oC [19,44,46,47,49,83,84]. 
However, the maritime industry does not report deposit formation in the urea-SCR systems, even though 
the operating conditions and diesel exhaust properties are very similar (Henrik Christensen, personal 
communication, March, 2017). A plausible explanation to why the maritime industry generally does not 
report any deposit formation could be due to a long term thermal degradation of the deposits, as diesel 
engines in ships often operate for multiple days in a row. Therefore, it has been of interest to investigate 
the long term thermal stability of urea samples at different isothermal temperatures using the TGA-FTIR 
setup. Using the TGA-FTIR setup, the uncertainty is expected to be low, as the balance of the setup is 
very precise. 
Figure 4.7 shows the TGA curves of a 49.54 and 50.05 mg urea sample heated to 280 oC and 380 oC at 
500 oC/min, respectively, and kept isothermally for 96 hours. 100 mL/min N2 is used as protective flow.  
For the experiment at 280 oC, the majority of the mass loss (~70%) occurs within the first minute. After 
the first minute, the TGA mass is seen to decrease further to ~9.5% within a couple of hours. After a 
couple of hours, the mass loss rate is seen to decrease drastically. After 96 hours, the TGA mass has 
decreased to ~6.2% of the initial sample mass. However, the observed trend in Figure 4.7 implies a con-
tinued decrease in TGA mass, corresponding to an exponential decay, even above 96 hours reaction time. 
From these results, exhaust temperatures of 280 oC corresponding to low engine load should yield deposit 
formation in maritime urea-SCR systems, as the TGA mass in Figure 4.7 is not expected to drop to zero 
within a period corresponding to typical operating periods of maritime diesel engines.  
For the experiment at 380 oC, the TGA mass is seen to drop to ~3% within the first minute. The TGA 
mass is seen to be fully decomposed after an exposure time of approximately 55 hours. A temperature of 
380 oC corresponds to a diesel exhaust temperature at close to full engine load. Furthermore, operating 
periods of 55 hours are not unusual for maritime diesel engines. Thus, by only considering Figure 4.7, 
the time behavior of the urea deposits might partially explain why the maritime industry does not expe-
rience the same issues of deposit formation in urea-SCR systems as the automotive industry. However, 
these simple TGA experiments do not completely represent real urea-SCR systems, as real urea-SCR 
systems have continuous injection of UWS into the exhaust pipe. Furthermore, the dimensions of a real 
urea-SCR system is expected to have an impact on mass and heat transfer, which are not captured by 
simple TGA experiments. Nevertheless, this investigation shows that the urea decomposition process 
does exert a time behavior, which can thus be an important factor for controlling deposit formation.

4.2.4 REFERENCE CURVES FOR PURE UREA DECOMPOSITION BYPRODUCTS 
It was decided to perform TGA on the pure urea decomposition byproducts including biuret, CYA, am-
meline and ammelide, in order to obtain reference curves which may be useful for identification of urea 
derived deposits of unknown composition. All materials were subjected to the same temperature pro-
gram, as also presented in Table 4.1. The resulting TGA curves have been compared with literature values 
with the same operating conditions, i.e. heating rate of 10 oC/min and a protective N2 flow of 100 
mL/min.  
4.2.4.1 BIURET TGA CURVE 
The results of the TGA of pure biuret are presented in Figure 4.8. As it can be seen, the TGA results are 
in good agreement with literature data, and lie between the TGA curves from Schaber et al. [29] and 
Stradella et al. [85]. The three temperature regions of stable mass imply formation of more stable decom-
position byproducts such as CYA, ammeline and ammelide. From the reaction mechanisms of urea de-
composition, proposed by Bernhard et al. [23] presented in Figure 2.2, this should only occur if biuret 
first decomposes into urea and HNCO which can then react further to form the more stable decomposi-
tion byproducts. Therefore, it is believed that the first endothermic peak in the DSC curve corresponds 
to decomposition of biuret, and the peak immediately after corresponds to decomposition of urea. 
Around 220-250 oC is seen to be a temperature region of stable mass. From these results it is not possible 
to conclude what species are stable at this temperature region, but is initially, based on Figure 2.2, pro-
posed to be triuret. The third endothermic peak in the DSC curve should then imply decomposition of 
triuret.  Around 275-290 oC is seen to be another temperature region of stable mass, which is expected to 
be due to formation of CYA. The fourth endothermic peak in the DSC curve is thus expected imply 
decomposition of CYA. This is also supported by the TGA curve of pure CYA presented in Figure 4.9, 
which shows a monotonic decrease in mass above 290 oC. Above 400 oC, the TGA mass has dropped 
below 3% of its initial mass, and shows a very slow continued decrease in mass, which suggests formation 
of very thermally resistant species.

4.3.3 MASS BALANCE CALCULATIONS 
As a means of assessing the results obtained from the FTIR, mass balance calculations have been per-
formed on all experiments. The mass balance calculations are based on the assumption that NH3 and 
HNCO are the only gas phase products from urea decomposition. Thus, any deviation from closing a 
mass balance should indicate deposit formation in the respective experiment. According to the TGA 
curve of urea obtained by Schaber et al. [29], presented in Figure 2.4, only trace amounts of solid residue 
should remain at temperatures above 450 oC. Therefore, it is expected that mass balances for all experi-
ments performed at 450 oC or above should close with high accuracy, as there should be full conversion 
of solid sample into gas phase species at temperatures above 450 oC.  
Because the typical sample mass fed to the FBR is very low, and the samples are fed together with sand, 
it was not possible to extract any solid residue that may have formed during an experiment. Therefore, 
the mass balance calculations are based solely on the gas phase.  
In order to quantify the amount of released NH3 and HNCO, the FTIR measurements have been inte-
grated with the software Origin┬« 2017 [88]. As a means of reducing the uncertainty of the area integra-
tions caused by noise in the FTIR signals at very low concentrations, the following integration rule have 
been applied:  
The mean value of the concentration prior to the main peak is subtracted from all values of the original 
FTIR measurements. If any value becomes negative, the value is set to zero.  
Applying this rule effectively means shifting the baseline very close to zero before performing each inte-
gration. Afterwards, the data is integrated in Origin┬« 2017 by setting the integration limits just before the 
main peaks, and at some point when the concentration has declined reasonable close to zero. The choice 
of the latter integration limit introduces some uncertainty to the mass balance calculations, but is expected

4.4 ROTARY DRUM 
This section will present the results obtained from experiments conducted in the rotary drum setup. The 
results include a study of the generation and analysis of urea derived deposits, and a study of the transient 
temperature development of stable urea-water solution wall films when impinged on the wall of the fur-
nace. 
4.4.1 DEPOSIT GENERATION AND ANALYSIS 
The rotary drum setup was used to generate urea derived deposits at varying furnace temperatures, as the 
setup allows for admission of large amounts of sample, and thus allows for aftertreatment and analysis 
of the formed deposits. Samples of 5┬▒0.001 g pure urea were used in all experiments. The samples were 
always injected into the furnace at steady state temperatures in the range of 200-600 oC, with an exposure 
time of 1 hour and a N2 gas flow of 50 L/min. After one hour, the samples were withdrawn from the 
furnace into a water cooled environment, and thus subjected to rapid cooling. The deposits were subse-
quently analyzed by yield calculations, solid state FTIR and elemental analysis (EA). The results from 
the analysis methods will be described in the following subsections. 
4.4.1.1 DEPOSIT YIELD ANALYSIS 
The temperature in the furnace was experienced not to be completely uniform, and thus the temperature 
measured by the thermocouple might not be exactly the same as the sample temperature. However, the 
thermocouple was placed reasonably close to the sample holder, and the uncertainty in the temperature 
measurement is therefore expected to be within ┬▒5 oC.  
The amount of deposit formed at varying furnace temperatures in the rotary drum setup are presented in 
Figure 4.16. As it can be seen, furnace temperatures above 500 oC are required for exposure times of Ōēż1

temperature is seen to be in reasonable agreement with data obtained from urea decomposition in the 
TGA-FTIR setup with a heating rate of 5 K/min. A more extensive comparison is presented in section 
4.5, which include data obtained from Caton and Siebers [40] and results from the rotary drum, FBR-
FTIR and TGA-FTIR setups.

4.4.1.2 SOLID STATE FTIR ANALYSIS 
Collectable urea derived deposits generated in the rotary drum were analyzed by solid state FTIR, as a 
means to qualitatively identify which byproduct species are present in the deposits. The experiment per-
formed in the rotary drum at 600 oC did not yield any deposit, and have thus not been analyzed by solid 
state FTIR. The analysis was conducted by comparing the deposits formed at 230, 280, 360, 430 and 500 
oC, respectively, with the standard curves of the pure byproducts, biuret, CYA, ammeline and ammelide. 
The best fits between IR spectra of the deposits and standard curves are presented in Figure 4.17 - Figure 
4.22, which thus presents the results of the analysis. A complete set of compared IR spectra is given in 
appendix 8.5.  
From Figure 4.17 and Figure 4.18, it can be seen that the IR spectra of the deposits generated at 230 and 
280 oC transmit light very similarly, as their IR spectra are almost identical. Therefore, it is assessed that 
the species present in the deposits at this temperature range are rather stable. This is also the observed 
behavior from the deposit yield analysis, as seen in Figure 4.16. By comparing Figure 4.17 and Figure 
4.18, it is assessed that CYA is the main compound present in the deposits generated at 230 and 280 oC, 
respectively, with a possible low content of biuret. This observation is further supported by the TGA 
curve of pure CYA, as presented in Figure 4.9, where it was seen that CYA is stable up to ~300 oC, 
whereas biuret begins to decompose at ~180 oC as seen from Figure 4.8. Additionally, it is also supported 
by the investigations performed by Schaber et al. [29], who proposes that CYA is the main compound 
responsible for the stable TGA mass in the temperature region between 225-290 oC (see Figure 2.4).

The deposits generated at 430 oC and 500 oC were visually observed to be yellow beige in color as pre-

This is an interesting observation, as it suggests the deposits formed at higher temperatures are most likely 
composed of larger condensation products of melamine, such as melem, melon or larger, as previously 
discussed from the work of Dyjak et al. [43] in section 2.2.3.2. This would also explain why the IR spectra 
of these deposits do not yield reasonable fits to any of the pure byproduct IR spectra.  
Mitoraj and Kisch obtained the FTIR spectra of a melem/melon mixture after heating at 400 oC for one 
hour [42]. They proposed that melon was the major component in the mixture, and assigned the major 
peaks of the FTIR spectra at 805, 1206, 1246, 1325, 1463 and 1616 cm-1 to the heteroaromatic s-triazine 
nucleus as well as the central C-N-C fragment and bridging C-NH-C units [42]. These peaks correspond 
reasonably well with the observed peaks of the deposits generated at 430 oC and 500 oC, as it can be seen 
from Figure 4.22.  
Therefore, it is tentatively proposed that the deposits generated in the rotary drum at 430 oC and 500 oC 
with an exposure time of 1 hour consists of either melem or melon, produced from melamine self-con-
densation.

From Figure 4.23 it can be seen that the sample contained some degree of moisture, even though the 
sample was dried at 110 oC for 5 hours and kept in a desiccator until it was transferred to the TGA. This 
is clearly seen, as the sample loses ~10% mass from room temperature to 100 oC corresponding to evap-

curve shows a monotonic decrease in mass up to ~730 oC, where nearly the entire sample mass have 
decomposed. The monotonic mass loss could be interpreted as deammoniation due to elongation of gra-

the FTIR in the TGA-FTIR setup was out of order over the course of this project. It could have been 
interesting to investigate the gas phase products during decomposition of this deposit, in order to deter-
mine if the proposed deammoniation occurs in practice. 
Above ~730 oC, the remaining 5% sample mass decomposes at a very slow rate, and is not fully decom-
posed even at a temperature of 1000 0C. This indicates that formation of extremely stable species have 
occurred. Based on the study of Dyjak et al. [43], such species are expected to be large graphitic C3N4 
polymer products with melon as precursor. In fact, a black deposit was visually observed in the crucible 
after the TGA experiment, as presented in Figure 4.24, which supports the claim that graphitic C3N4 
polymer products have been formed in the TGA experiment presented in Figure 4.23. 
However, as exhaust temperatures from maritime diesel engines are rarely above 400 oC, even at full 
engine load, it is assessed that such graphitic C3N4 polymer products should not be appear in deposits 
formed in maritime urea-SCR systems.

4.4.1.3 ELEMENTAL ANALYSIS 
In this section, the deposits generated in the rotary drum setup at 230, 280 and 360 oC are analyzed for 
elemental composition. The scope of this investigation is to support the solid state FTIR analysis, in order 
to make more qualified postulations in the identification of deposits. The deposits generated at 430 and 
500 oC have not been included in this analysis, as the generated amounts of deposits at these temperatures 
were insufficient for the analysis.  
The deposit samples were analyzed for C, H and N content by an external lab (FORCE Technology). 
The oxygen content was calculated from a mass balance, by assuming that the samples only contain C, 
H, N and O. The samples were dried at 105 oC prior to the analysis, and the results are thus on dry basis. 
The results have been plotted against theoretical elemental compositions of urea and the main urea de-
composition byproducts, and are presented in Figure 4.25.

drum experiment. This might explain the higher deposit yields observed from the rotary drum at low 
temperatures as compared to the TGA experiment.

consistent with that of the TGA setup, rotary drum setup and data obtained from Caton and Siebers [40]. 
However, it was expected that the results from the FBR-FTIR would show a lower deposit yield than 
that of the TGA and rotary drum setup, as the much larger heating rate of urea in the FBR-FTIR setup 
was expected to limit the extent of secondary reactions leading to the undesired byproduct formation. 
This is not the observed behavior from the comparison presented in Figure 4.27.

High temperatures 
At high temperatures, the deposit yields from the TGA and rotary drum setup are in very good agreement. 
The results from Caton and Siebers [40] are also in reasonable agreement with that of the TGA and rotary 
drum setup, with the exception of the last data point at the highest temperature.  
Caton and Siebers [40] report that the HNCO concentration decreases again at 565 oC, which based on

increase again for increasing temperature. A more likely explanation to the decrease in the measured 
HNCO concentration at 565 oC could be hydrolysis of HNCO into NH3 and CO2. These are merely 
speculations, as Caton and Siebers [40] did not address this data point.  
The results from the FBR-FTIR setup at high temperatures are seen to be in poor agreement with the 
other setups. The estimated deposit yields in the FBR-FTIR are much larger than the expected, by com-

clear conclusions w.r.t. the impact of subjecting urea particles to a very high heating rate. In future work, 
it might be of interest to repeat a selection of the experiments performed with the FBR-FTIR setup in this 
project. However, fundamental changes to the setup are required in order to obtain consistent and reliable 
results.  
One proposal could be to use a porous and inert material and dope it with fine urea powder, instead of 
feeding single urea particles using sand as support material. This is expected to yield a slower, and thus 
more controlled, release of NH3 and HNCO. Furthermore, it might also promote the transport of the 
sample through the reactor, thus yielding the sample less likely to get stuck on the glass of the reactor at 
a higher point that the bed where the temperature might be lower than the intended decomposition tem-
perature.

CONCLUSION 
This project was aimed toward investigating the formation and the decomposition of deposits in evapo-
ration of urea for maritime urea-SCR processes. The objective of the project was to characterize experi-
mentally the decomposition of urea and of the heavy molecular deposits, and to obtain fundamental 
knowledge about the chemistry of the urea decomposition process. 
Based on the literature survey, it was decided to investigate the impact of heating rate on the urea decom-
position process, as it was speculated that a high heating rate of urea might limit the extent of secondary 
reactions. This investigation was conducted in a TGA setup capable of low to medium heating rates (1-
1000 oC/min) and a FBR setup with FTIR analyzer in which urea particles could be subjected to high 
heating rates. The results from the TGA experiments indicated that production of CYA is inhibited to 
some extent for increasing heating rate. The results from the FBR setup was in general associated with 
large uncertainties due to issues with the feeding system and limitations of the FTIR, and thus no clear 
conclusion could be made on the impact of subjecting urea particles to high heating rates. However, 
experiments performed at 250 oC and 330 oC implied that CYA production is limited when subjecting 
urea to a high heating rate. If this happens in practice, operators should focus on heating the UWS drop-
lets at a high rate, as CYA is a precursor for all of the byproducts in the urea decomposition process. 
The long term thermal stability of urea was investigated by TGA. In two experiments, urea samples were 
heated to 280 oC and 380 oC, which corresponds to diesel exhaust temperatures at low and high load of 
a maritime diesel engine, respectively, and kept isothermally for four days. From this investigation, it 
could be concluded that the urea decomposition process is not only temperature dependent, but also time 
dependent, as the decomposition rates were seen to change over time. It was found that a temperature of 
280 oC did not yield complete decomposition of the urea sample over the course of four days. However, 
with a temperature of 380 oC, the urea sample was completely decomposed after ~55 hours. This result 
implies that solid deposition can be avoided in maritime urea-SCR systems by operating at full engine 
load over an extended period, e.g. three days. However, this investigation was performed as batch exper-
iments in a TGA setup, and may not be entirely representative of real urea-SCR systems, where UWS is 
continuously sprayed into the exhaust pipe. 
Lastly, experiments on urea decomposition have been conducted in a rotary drum setup at furnace tem-
peratures of 230, 280, 360, 430, 500 and 600 oC, with exposure times of one hour. The scope of this 
investigation was to gain more insight into the urea decomposition chemistry, by attempting to identify 
the deposits generated at varying furnace temperatures, as the setup allowed for generation of sufficient 
amounts of deposits for further solid sample analysis. From a combination of solid state FTIR and ele-
mental analysis, it was proposed that deposits generated at 230 oC and 280 oC primarily consisted of 
CYA. In the same manner, it was proposed that the deposit generated at 360 oC primarily consisted of a 
mixture of ammelide and ammeline. These proposals were found to be in good agreement with the la-
boratory study of urea decomposition performed by Schaber et al. [29]. The deposits generated at 430 oC 
and 500 oC were, based on a comparison of the physical appearance, and a study of melamine self-con-
densation performed by Dyjak et al. [43], proposed to consist of products from melamine self-condensa-
tion, such as melem or melon. A TGA experiment of the deposit generated at 430 oC showed that the 
sample was not fully decomposed even at 1000 oC. Black residue was visually identified in the crucible 
after the TGA experiment, indicating formation of large and very stable graphitic C3N4 polymer products. 
However, as exhaust temperatures of maritime diesel engines are rarely above 400 oC, even at full engine 
load, the results indicate that such graphitic C3N4 products from melamine self-condensation should not 
appear in urea derived deposits in maritime urea-SCR systems.

Future Work

6 
FUTURE WORK 
In future work concerning the topic of urea decomposition, it could be interesting to repeat experiments 
with the FBR setup with an FTIR analyzer. However, it is necessary to develop a new feeding strategy 
in order to obtain consistent and reliable results from the FTIR. One plausible alternative, as previously 
described, could be to feed a porous inert material doped with urea. This might cause a slower, and thus 
more controlled release of gas phase species, and thus accommodate the limitations of the FTIR. Fur-
thermore, it might also accommodate the transport of the sample to the quartz plate. 
The long term thermal stability investigation by TGA indicated a continued slow rate of decomposition 
over the course of four days. In future work, it might be of interest to obtain sufficient amounts of deposits 
generated under such long term experiments, and subject those to further heating in TGA-FTIR experi-
ments, in order to investigate if the deposits can be easily decomposed upon further heating, and simul-
taneously quantify the gas phase species which are produced during the decomposition. 
From experiments performed in the rotary drum setup, deposits generated at different furnace tempera-
tures were obtained. It was attempted to qualitatively identify the generated deposits by solid state FTIR, 
EA and TGA. Deposits generated at 430 oC and 500 oC were proposed to be composed of products from 
melamine self-condensation such as melem and melon. Further heating of these deposits to 1000 oC in-
dicated formation of very stable and thermally resistant graphitic C3N4 polymer products, which are dif-
ficult to remove by thermal treatment.  In future work, it may be of interest to reproduce such graphitic 
species, and investigate whether they can be removed by gasification or other methods. 
The rotary drum setup was reconstructed within this project, by implementing a purge close to the sap-
phire glass. It was successfully tested that condensation on the glass, due to rapid evaporation of water 
when the sample comes in contact with the preheated rotary drum, could be avoided by applying a high 
purge flow of N2 towards the glass. In future work, it should thus be possible to investigate the transient 
temperature development of a UWS wall film in a heated environment, by monitoring the process with 
an IR camera. However, it may be necessary to obtain information about the emissivity of the UWS 
samples, in order to avoid erroneous interpretations the results from the IR camera. 
It could also be interesting to develop a model for the system of a UWS wall film in a heated environment, 
which could be validated against results obtained from IR measurements of experiments performed in 
the rotary drum setup.  
Finally, it could be interesting to develop a pilot scale facility where urea can be continuously sprayed 
into a preheated exhaust pipe, which would more accurately represent a maritime urea-SCR system than 
the setups utilized within this project. If a window of sapphire glass could be installed on the exhaust pipe 
of such a facility, IR spectroscopy could be used to investigate the temperature of UWS wall films during 
continuous spraying of UWS at steady state conditions, which should yield a better imitation of a mari-
time urea-SCR system during operation.
