Abstract
In recent years, the growing demand for renewable energy sources has led to changes in
the grid condition, affecting the short circuit contribution coming from the grid side in-
fluencing different protections systems in offshore wind farms arrays.
This thesis investigates the impact of changing grid conditions for two array cable protec-
tion systems, distance protection, and islanding detection. It aims to provide complemen-
tary knowledge that supports current research in array cable protection.
Two systems were designed and modeled into a simplified power system based on a lit-
erature review on distance protection and islanding detection. The designed protection
systems were validated by simple testing. From test observation were some of the initial
protection parameters modified before implementing them into the fictive offshore wind
farm model used for the final case studies.
The result of the final case studies showed that distance protection performed well under
strong grid conditions. However, was the protection system affected by the weak grid
conditions, resulting in maloperation in some protection zones. The islanding detection
system also showed well performance in strong grid conditions. It was also impacted by
the weak grid conditions, tipping during the energization period of the wind turbine gen-
erators. These results complement some of the research in this area, stating that the grid
conditions can influence the performance of distance protection and islanding detection
system. The work presented in the study has profound implications for future studies in
protection with changing grid conditions.

There is a worldwide focus on reducing CO2 emissions by increasing electricity generated
from renewable energy sources, driving electric vehicles, and shifting from fossil fuel sources
to renewable energy sources. Offshore wind power has been one of the key methods for
the transition to a higher share of green/renewable electricity generation. With the Paris
agreement COP21, has the build-out of Offshore wind farms (OWFs) increased during
the last decades. Wind farms today tend to grow in capacity, with having Wind turbine
generators (WTGs) being able to produce up to 9.5 MW [3] and even more in the future [4].
The increased penetration of RESs and increased electricity demand has led to a reduction
of the short circuit contribution coming from the gird during short circuit events occurring
in the OWFs.
A short circuit event is an abnormal condition between two conductors with low resistance
in between. Resulting in excessive current flow in the power source through the short
circuit causing potential damage to power equipment and maintenance personnel if not
detected.

Figure 1.2 shows two simple power systems, including a grid side with a voltage source
and a secondary side with a current source being an OWF. During a short circuit is the
current flow coming for the grid (Igird)and the OWF (IOWF ), resulting in the short circuit
current Ifault. A strong grid is represented by having a lower impedance (Zgrid) and vice
visa for a weak grid, meaning that the magnitude of the fault current is dependable on
the current contribution coming from the both sources.
In the early years of OWF construction, the OWFs were often connected to locations
with a strong grid and high fault current contribution.
This was mainly because the
power system traditionally consisted of synchronous generators providing system stability
and inertia resulting in high fault currents coming from the grid side. This meant that
the relays in the OWFs were tripping consistently creating a secure protection system and
making the selectivity high in the OWFs.
But as the trend for increasing the share of renewable energy has led to OWFs being
connected at grid locations where there is a weak grid. Or already operational wind farms
experiencing changing grid conditions due to replacement of generators or outage of trans-
mission lines. This trend raises an important question about how the OWF protection
system performs under future changing grid conditions.
Doing protection in OFWs is the protection system’s setup, affected by the different
short circuit contributions from the wind turbines and the grid. Detecting faults in a
transmission system onshore influenced by synchronous machines is different from OWFs.
The high mechanical mass of the synchronous machines affects the current during short
circuits. When protecting OWFs, the inverter-based wind turbines react differently during
a fault. Only delivering a short circuit current contribution between 110%-130% of the
rated current as an effect of power electronics converter limitation. Creating potential
challenges with current protection methods used for array cable protection [5].

There are multiple ways of defining the grid strength [6] and one method used in the
industry is looking at the Short circuit ratio (SCR) between the OWF and the grid. The
SCR has been used to analyze the stability of a synchronous generator and the connected
grid using the absolute short circuit MVA of the generator and comparing it with the active
rated power of a connected grid. The SCR indicates the robustness of the connected grid
during current injections and faults. A strong grid should be uninfluenced by current
injections at the connected bus and provide a high fault current in a short circuit event.
There is a variety of research papers focusing on strong and weak grid conditions[7],[8],[9]
in terms of stability issues with WTG control. Few of them discuss the influence on the
grid condition during faults in the OWF, which makes it interesting to inspect.

Equation 1.1 shows the calculation of the SCR. Where the SSC,MV A is the absolute short

Another aspect of OWF protection is the demand for having islanding detection at the
array cable level and detecting loss of connection to the grid. Current islanding detection
methods must not unintentionally trip when changes occur at grid level, during faults or
when the grid strength reduces. There is little research done in islanding detection at
the array cable level. Therefore, modern relays are only used as a warning signal in the
protection system and not as a breaking mechanism as intended.

1.2
Motivation
The OWF wind industry must deal with these changing gird conditions in their future
parks, and therefore they must be sure that the intended protection methods in array ca-
bles are reliable. The challenge concerns most actors in the OWF industry, which makes
a good foundation for a deeper investigation of the protection system in OWFs.
Failure of the protection systems leads to a loss in MDKK because of lost production
and possibly the reparation or replacement of medium voltage/ high voltage (MV/HV)
equipment, ultimately endangering the lives of maintenance personnel. Additionally, can
failure of the protection system led to the unintentional disconnection of the OWF. The
loss of connection makes the provision of auxiliary services impossible and creates further
stability challenges at the onshore transmission grid.
Another part of OWF protection is the islanding of the WTG at the array cable level.
Wind turbine protection is a black box where the islanding setup is unknown and might
not detect all islanding conditions. OWF owners need to detect islanding conditions, shut
down the OWF, protect the personnel and equipment and have the islanding protection
at the array level.
1.3
Formulation of the problem
This thesis aims to give theoretical implantation and investigation of protections systems
for OWFs arrays focusing on distance protection and islanding detection. Additionally,
will the two protection systems be evaluated upon how they perform in changing grid
strength scenarios.
• What is the essential method behind array cable distance protection in offshore wind
farms today?
• How can the protection system be implemented in an array cable feeder for an
OWF and modeled in PSCAD? Can the implemented protection system correspond
correctly to single line to ground fault located along with the array?
• How are modern relays used for islanding detection today? What is the methodology
behind, and how can the rate of change of frequency protection technique be assessed
for an array string in an OWF model?
• How does the distance protection system perform under changing grid conditions
(weak/strong)?

1.4
Methodology, Boundary, and Objective
As the main objective of this project is the performance of protection systems in changing
grid conditions, will the state-of-the-art protection in distance protection and islanding
detection be acquired by relevant literature. This gained knowledge is used to develop
a protection model in PSCAD. The designed protection algorithms is tested in a simple
power system including a passive load and modified before implemented in the final OWF.
The protection system is for one 33 kV array cable including 8 WTGs with a nameplate
capacity of 4 MW. The overall OWF model used for the case studies, is based upon a
self-build model with a total capacity of 232 MW using a type-4 wind turbine model from
PSCAD knowledge base[10].
The protection model is evaluated and tested for single line to ground faults placed at
different locations inside a given protection zone. The protection system does not include
instantaneous overcurrent, overcurrent, and differential protection but only focus on dis-
tance protection
1.5
PSCAD
The simulations in this thesis are executed in the simulation software PSCAD [11]. PSCAD
software is widely used for EMT (Electro-magnetic Transient). The software is ideal for

2.3
Trends and potential challenges with distance protection
The following section has been composed from reading through [20],[18],[21],[22],[5].
Distance protection is a well know technology that has been used for transmission pro-
tection for many years and as OWF array feeders has increased with the OWF, has the
detection method also been implemented at array cable level.
Some of the most recent research in array feeder systems includes one additional feature
for distance protection, such as adaptive characteristics settings. Other research shows
solutions for ground fault where the directional element falls into the wrong quadrant. A
detailed description of the two areas is given in the following section.
Evaluation done by Sujo P. George and S. Ashok shows that distance protection with
quadrilateral operation characteristics can maloperate during faults. This phenomenon is
because the source impedance defines the current contribution changes with the number
of WTG in service during the fault event. In a typical setting, the distance relay is the
characteristics fixed and it does not consider the changing source impedance. That is why
there has been an interest in creating a function that can adapt the characteristics by
itself, analyzing the source impedance of the specifically connected feeder.
The answer to the problem is to use an algorithm made by Sujo P. George and S. Ashok
that evaluates a specific time interval and calculates the corresponding fault current con-
tribution coming from each WTG with the present wind speed and power production. A
compensation impedance factor from the wind speed and power production is calculated
and added until the line impedance reaches that the relay is protecting. The compensation
factor changes with the source impedance and reshapes the quadrilateral characteristics
for a particular time interval.
Research in directional elements in array feeders done by [18] shows that distance pro-
tection can poetically maloperate during single line to ground faults. The measured fault
impedance can fall into the fourth quadrant of operation characteristics and causes the
relay not to trip. This phenomenon can happen due to the high inductive current com-
ponents coming from the MV array, the ground system in the OWF, and the power
production by the WTGs. The phase current starts to lead over the phase voltage, re-
sulting in fault impedance in the fourth quadrant. One solution to this problem proposed
by Zoran Gajic[21] is to extend the reach of the quadrilateral characteristics to the fourth
zone, which enables the distance protection possibility to detect single line to ground faults
under these special conditions.
One potential challenge for distance protection is the increase of RES in the grid, which
presents a huge role in the short circuit current contribution coming from the grid. As
RES normally only supply with 110-130% of its nominal operation current, decreasing the
short circuit current magnitude coming from the grid. This change may lead to distance
protection losing accuracy in operation during faults.

2.4
Islanding detection in offshore wind farms
Islanding detection in OWFs relates to conditions occurring outside the OWF, which
courses a disconnection of the farm from the main grid.
There are some main aspects of islanding detection and the reason for having it imple-
mented in an OWF. One important aspect is that running an OWF in an islanding state
can be dangerous for maintenance personnel and potentially create abnormal frequencies
and voltages in the OWF, damaging equipment. Therefore, it is crucial for the OWF to
detect islanding so the operator can safely shut down the WTGs.
The second aspect is that WTGs implemented today are grid following. The converter
tracks the voltage phase angle and tries to synchronize the output waveform to inject the
power into the grid. Therefore is essential to be able to disconnect and sometimes shut
down the wind farm when there are significant changes in the grid affecting the operation
of the WTGs.
A third aspect is that there is usually no dedicated islanding detection for the OWF.
Only the WTG has the function included, but it is not publicly available. This creates
a level of uncertainty, and it has occurred that the internal islanding did not detect the
islanding condition keeping the WTG in operation. The following section goes through
some islanding topologies used for islanding detection today, islanding detection for OWF
array cables, and some of the challenges with islanding detection in OWFs.
2.4.1
Different topologies
Various islanding detection methods can be applied remotely (central) or locally from the
wind farm. The topologies can be divided into three different technique groups for the
local methodology: passive, active, and hybrid[23]. The detection techniques for remote
detection topology can also be separated into different groups: state-, switch monitoring,
and inter tripping[24].
The main difference between local and remote detection is the equipment needed. The
local detection is placed at the specific power component that the relay is protecting, giving
a trip signal to the OWF operator. Whereas remote detection also includes a trip signal
going the grid operator requiring some hardwired or wireless communication between the
site and the grid control room at the utility level.

Voltage and current harmonics detection
In inverter-based generation units like a type 4 turbine, some harmonic voltage and current
distortion are coming from the inverter caused by the switching, semiconductor voltage
drop, dead-time, and some ripple in the inverter DC-link. Marco Liserre explains this area
in [34]. The principle of harmonic detection is to monitor the voltage at the relay location
and compare the harmonic distortion with a certain threshold[24] which is evaluated by a
percentage margin defined as Total harmonic distortion.
There is some harmonic distortion under normal operation but are it is usually neglected
for this detection method or set to THD = 0 [34]. The neglection is because the impedance
seen from the relay reflects the grid impedance and is usually quite low, therefore creating
low voltage harmonics. During an islanding event is, the impedance influenced by the
loads connected to the OWF, resulting in higher current harmonics generated by the
WTG inverter. The grid impedance and current harmonics produce measurable voltage
harmonics compared with thresholds for islanding detection. Furthermore, loads connected
close to the OWF are vital components for this technique to work satisfactorily, which is
not always the case for all OWF connected directly to the HV transmission system.

2.4.3
Active detection techniques
The following section about active islanding methods have been composed by reading
through articles and literature from [34],[35],[25],[36]and, [37]. There are quite a few ac-
tive islanding detection techniques. The more significant part relies on disturbance from
the WTG influencing parameters such as P, Q, frequency, phase, and harmonics in the
power system. One general drawback of active detection techniques is that they inject
disturbances into the grid affecting the power quality and maybe coursing problems com-
plying with grid code requirements. Some of the techniques used for OWF and distributed
generation (DG) are described in the following section.
2.4.3.1
Impedance measurement/estimation
The active impedance measurement/estimation includes a couple of different techniques.
Some such as impedance measurement at the WTG inverter output, harmonic injection,
and grid impedance be reactive power variation. Studies show [38],[39] that the impedance
measurement technique is mainly used for DG or microgrids with few generating inverters,
which is not applicable when looking at OWFs. However, are the two other techniques
relevant for OWFs.
The harmonic injection is characterized by injecting current harmonics at the POC of the
WF and analyzing the resulting voltage harmonics, which depends on the grid impedance
and frequency.
2.4.3.2
Slip-mode frequency shift
Slip-mode frequency shift uses the amplitude, frequency, and phase of the voltage from
the OWF as positive feedback to the PLL of WTG. When an OWF is operating in normal
conditions, the phase current and voltage follow the grid and try to match the changes by
the PLL control. The positive feedback to the PLL causes the phase output of the WTG
to be marginally asymmetrical.
During an islanding condition, the frequency and voltage at the POC are distorted because
of the power system’s mismatch between production and consumption. The event causes
the WTG PLL to increase/decrease the frequency, and the slight asymmetrical difference
enhances the error seen by the PLL and eventually trip Over/Under Frequency protection.
2.4.3.3
Active frequency drift
The active frequency drift is characterized by changing the frequency of the output current
of the WTG by chopping the signal as shown in figure 2.12. Under normal operation,
the WTGs PLL follow the grid frequency at POC, which the current chopping is not
influencing. The reason is that the grid voltage and frequency are clamped at POC [40],
so the inverter frequency current output is stable. During an islanding event, the voltage
frequency change and replicate the current frequency waveform of the WTG at the last
cycle. When the voltage waveform follows the chopped current frequency, the threshold
for over/under frequency crosses, enabling the relay’s braking mechanism.

2.4.4
Islanding detection for array cables
Only a few research articles have been published about implementing the islanding dic-
tation in array cables. However, interestingly does, modern relays such as [41] and [42]
include some of these islanding detection techniques mentioned in the sections above. In-
terviews conducted with Ørsted also verify that they only monitor the islanding signals in
array cables and do not use the method to trip an array cable string. The reason is that
there is currently not enough research and testing data in this area, and having relays
unintentional braking is very costly in terms of lost energy.
One of the main purposes of implementing the islanding detection at the array cable level
is the unknown knowledge about the internal islanding detection system inside the WTG
system. It gives an extra level of security for the OWF operator, knowing that an islanding
event can be detected at the array level.

2.4.5
Challenges with islanding detection
Some of the challenges are the rapid increase in renewable converter based generation,
were the grid have less inertia, thereby lowering the instabilities occurring during faults or
abnormal situations, caused by high currents. This change in the grid conditions makes
some of the islanding techniques described in the chapter, unsustainable to use for OWF
islanding as they are to sensitive [44].
Grid impedance ratio and short circuit current ratio define the robustness of the grid,
and having low grid impedance ratio affects the power quality during changes in the grid
and from the OWF. Resulting in a poorer voltage waveforms influencing the frequency
creating challenges for some islandning techniques.

Compliance of the WTG during faults
The RfG states that a generation plant needs to stay interconnected during faults or
voltage drips in the grid within a certain period of time[54]. The same applies to faults
happening inside the OWF, and the healthy part of the OWF needs to stay in operation
during a fault to supply with reactive power derby feeding the fault with current. The
fault current contribution is important so the protections system can detect the fault and
separate it from the reaming part of the OWF. It can also minimize the generation loss
after the fault has been cleared as there are still WTGs connected to the grid.
Figure 4.6 shows a voltage dip test with 100 WTGs in operation, with a wind speed of
10 m/s. The WTG stays connected and supplies with reactive power during the voltage
dip. The active and reactive power is restored to pre-voltage dip magnitudes when the
voltage level reaches 1 pu. The result in figure 4.6 verifies that WTG models can be used
as generation units for the array system and the OWF. Another important feature of the
WTG is to supply with current during a fault, and the grid-side converter is set to deliver
a maximum current of 1.1 pu[10], which is verified in the appendix , see figure A.4.

5.3
Distance relay performance evaluation
The evaluation method used for all the case studies for the quadrilateral relay is based on
looking at five difference measurements in the OWF located at the breaker and the array
cable model. The measurements are listed below to explain why they have been chosen
for the evaluation.
1. Tripping zone: The first is parameter is in which zone the relay has tripped during
the fault. This factor is important because one may need to know if the relay tripped
at all during the fault and if it tripped for the indented zone set in the relay setup.
2. Tripping time: Distance relays are supposed to be fast at tripping during faults. Is
the tripping time crucial for performance evaluation, and is it often included in how
selective the relay setup. This is because the speed of the relay can reduce damage
happening to components during a fault.
3. Fault voltage level: The fault voltage level indicates how the WTG behaves during
the faults.
It also shows how much the array cable is being stressed at varying
voltages.
4. Fault current level: The current level tells how much the WTG a supplying fault
current to the fault, and the same goes from the grid side. The supplying fault
current is the main factor as it is part of the measured impedance at the relay
location.
5. Impedance measurement during the fault: The foundation of distance protection and
the tripping mechanism are based on impedance measurement, making it a necessary
part of the evaluation.

7
Conclusion
This thesis addresses the performance of two well-known concepts, distance protection
and islanding detection for array protection in an offshore wind farm. Their performance
has been tested during changing grid conditions. First, the distance protection covers
the different cables’ and provides back-up protection for other equipment, such as the
WTG in the array cable. The distance protection is comparing a measured impedance to
the impedance of the desired protection area and using an operation characteristic that
considers the different faults. This method has been verified throughout this thesis by
tests of the modeled distance protection in a simple power system [62]. One of the critical
parts of the distance protections system is calculating the correct ground k compensation
factor. The calibration method used in this thesis was not ideal and a practical idea not
supported by literature, ultimately resulting in some maloperations in the final tests.
Testing the distance protection in a simple power system with a passive load showed
to be a good approach. It presented some setting challenges of the zone reach, confirming
other research articles [50], together with the complexity of setting the zones correctly.
The final distance protection system was only partially implemented in this thesis as it
did not respond correctly all single ground faults. This could be due to the relatively
simplified fictive OWF used in PSCAD, not including all the parameters influencing the
fault impedance.
Second, the islanding protection protects the array cable when the OWF is unintentionally
disconnected from the grid. Based on the literature review, the modern relays, including
multiple different islanding detection methodologies, all serve to detect the islanding of the
OWF. The simple ROCOF relay was with some tuning of the evaluation time successfully
implemented into the OWF. The simple relay configuration can detect islanding in strong
grid conditions, bringing additional data to the real-life implementation. It also revealed
some already known challenges in the setup with having low evaluation periods
The finding related to distance protection complement other research papers [22] con-
cerning overreach in zone 1. This thesis gives additional insight into the research area
focusing on array cables. Some of the results showed the effect of not detecting faults in
the second and third zone and the limiting converter behavior during weak grids compli-
menting findings in other literature. The ROCOF test confirmed that the sensitivity is
dependable on the evaluation period the rate of change, which in this project was config-
ured from the RfG and not specifically designed for an array cable. Altogether, future grid
conditions affect the performance of the two protections system presented and assessed in
this thesis with the need for further investigation in a more accurate OWF model.

7.1
Future work
The following future work can be recommended:
• Single line to ground faults are highly influenced by fault and arc resistance during a
short circuit. Including different fault resistances that are representable in MV cables
faults could substantially impact the distance protection performance. Similar to the
fault resistance, the arc resistance also has an influence on the total fault impedance.
It would be interesting to implement this additional factor and test it for the distance
protection setup.
• All of the test performed in this thesis concerns single ground faults, which raises
the question on how the system performs to other fault types.
Phase to phase
fault are very mush influence by the current contribution comping from the grid and
investigating the impact on changing grid conditions for other fault types could some
interesting insights.
• As both systems were not functioning perfectly, tuning and correcting the setup used
in this thesis could give more accurate results. It is suggested to investigate other
definitions of calculating the ground compensation factor for distance protection.
For the ROCOF it is recommended increase the evaluation time and test scenarios
with weak gird conditions.

A.2
Quadrilateral relay test and unexpected results during Phase
to ground faults in Zone 2
The approach used for setting up the quadrilateral relay characteristics are based on review
of papers from [48] and [50]. The papers go through some technical methods of setting
up the quadrilateral characteristics which is easy to implement in modern distance relays.
They explained how to set the resistive reach in the first quadrant that involves the total
measured resistance during a fault. The measured resistance includes multiple components
such as the following:
Rline = Resistance of line
Rg = Ground resistance
Rarc = Arc resistance during a fault
Rmeasured = Rline + Rg + Rarc
The measured resistance is the real part of the fault impedance and based on the calcula-
tion of the resistive components in equation (A.1), can the reach be set with a gain factor
between 1.1-1.3. The gain factor for the project is set to 1.2 of the line resistance.
