Abstract
This thesis investigates the statistical properties of storm surges along the southeastern
coastline of Denmark. It presents the current method of creating hydrographs in Denmark
alongside four different international methods. By evaluating both the current and inter­
national methods, the thesis introduces a reproducible method for creating hydrographs
based on measured water level data.
By analyzing the measured data, the coastline is divided into groups based on a cross­
correlation analysis, and hydrographs for these groups are constructed. The effects of
using different hydrographs as inputs for a hydrodynamic simulation are then calculated
using a MIKE21 inland flood model for a larger Danish city. The simulation outputs are
evaluated in terms of flooded area, flood depth and duration, and an estimated annual
flooded building calculation.
The findings indicate that it is possible to create a meaningful, reproducible method for
generating hydrographs along the Danish coastline. However, it is important to evaluate
the use case of each hydrograph, as they vary significantly between more frequent occur­
rences and extreme scenarios. Additionally, the results show that the flooded area and
depth are dependent on the duration of the maximum water level.
Given the limited high time­resolution data on storm surges, it is recommended to in­
vestigate the causes of these extreme events, such as wind systems, and evaluate their
statistics. By reconstructing possible future and past scenarios in a high­resolution model,
we can gain better insights into the types of storms that occur rarely.

Introduction
Denmark, though small in size, boasts an extensive coastline. The ten largest cities, as
well as 50 of the 100 largest cities in Denmark, are situated in coastal areas [1].
As global temperatures rise due to climate change, so does the mean sea level. Projec­
tions indicate that the mean sea level around Denmark will increase by 80­120 cm by the
year 2125 [2]. Moreover storm surges, such as those in October 2023 and January 2024,
have caused significant destruction and received considerable media attention. The effect
of storm surges will only be intensified due to climate change.
Future costs from storm surge damage are expected to far exceed those from heavy
rainfall [3], driven by rising sea levels and increasing coastal urbanization. This highlights
the urgent need for effective action.
Water levels along the Danish coast have been measured over periods ranging from 20
to 150 years [4]. However, severe storm surges that cause significant damage are rare,
resulting in limited data for these extreme events. Studies by COWI [5] and DHI [6] have
attempted to incorporate historical sea levels during major storm surges. While these
studies offer valuable insights into return levels, they do not account for the time resolution
of sea level rises during extreme events.
High­time­resolution sea level measurements are limited to a maximum of 40 years, with
most data covering only 12­30 years. This results in a database with significant uncertainty
regarding how sea levels rise during a storm surge, also known as hydrographs. Accurate
cost estimates for storm surges, derived from dynamic simulations, depend on precise
input data, including the hydrograph. Prolonged high water levels during a storm surge
might lead to more extensive inland flooding and greater damage compared to shorter
surges.
This uncertainty can lead to either overestimating or underestimating the costs associated
with storm surges, thus affecting the allocation of funds needed for adaptation measures.
This thesis aims to enhance the understanding of storm surge hydrographs and their
effects using measured data series and hydrodynamic simulations in MIKE21. By gain­
ing this improved insight, a methodology for creating hydrographs will be developed and
compared with existing methods. This comparison will be based on a case study that
examines the extent of flooding in hydrodynamic simulations with different hydrograph
inputs.
1.1
Research questions
To provide valuable knowledge to the field of storm surges along the Danish coast, this
thesis seeks to answer the following research questions:
1. What are the current methods in Denmark for creating hydrographs of storm surges?
2. Do hydrographs vary in space across the Danish coastline?
3. Is it reasonable to create an average hydrograph based on measured data?
4. What effect does the hydrograph have on hydrodynamic simulations?

Theory
2.1
Storm surge definition
A storm surge is an unusual large rise in water level. The surge contribution is defined as
the height above the predicted tidal sea level. According to Naturskaderaadet, a storm
surge in Denmark is defined as a water level that statistically occurs every 20 years [7].
This water level of a given return period is determined by fitting a Generalized Pareto
Distribution model to the events detected in the recorded time series [4]. Unlike a fixed
threshold, the defined sea level for a storm surge in Denmark can change with each
update of the statistics, the most recent of which was in July 2024. If the water level rises
unusually high but does not reach the storm surge threshold, it is classified as an extreme
rise in water level.
2.1.1
Subdivision of sea level rise
Water level changes can be analyzed over different time scales [8] :
• Short­Term Periodic Changes: These include tidal effects detailed in Section 2.3
and seiches, which are oscillations in a body of water caused by a sudden change
in factors like wind or atmospheric pressure changes. This phenomenon can occur
in the Baltic Sea, causing water level rises along Denmark’s southeastern coast.
• Short­Term Non­Periodic Changes: These are primarily driven by wind fields and
low atmospheric pressure. A sustained wind causes a rise in water level towards
the coast, driven by consistent wind speed and direction. The aperiodic component
of a storm surge, distinct from wind fields, is linked to changes in barometric pres­
sure. There is a linear relationship between barometric pressure and water level:
a decrease in pressure by 1 bar results in a corresponding rise in water level of
approximately 1 cm, with a delay.
• Long­Term Changes: These involve factors like rising mean sea level due to ther­
mal expansion, melting glaciers, and geological shifts in land elevation.
Near the coast there are some additional elements to consider during a storm surge. This
being wind generated waves, wave setup and wave run up. In this thesis the rise of the
water level during a storm surge is the measured sea level, detrended and minus the tidal
effect. All elements described above is visualized in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Contributors of an extreme sea level event [9].

2.2
Storm surge processes in Denmark
To understand why storm surges occur in the southeastern coast of Denmark, it’s crucial to
examine the bathymetry of Denmark and the Baltic Sea. The Baltic Sea, a semi­enclosed
sea, has an average depth of 56 m and covers an area of 3.7 × 105 km2 [10]. The Baltic
Sea’s access to Kattegat is constricted by the Great Belt, the straits between Helsingør
and Helsingborg, and the Little Belt, with water flow rates through these channels being 7,
3, and 1, respectively [8]. Meaning that almost all water leaving from the Baltic sea flows
through the Great Belt, some through the straits between Helsingør and Helsingborg and
very limited flow through the Little Belt. The flow described are highlighted in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Bathymetry around Denmark in resolution of 125 m x 125 m with a color palette
ranging from 0 to more than 40 meter of depth. Flow distribution through belt of Denmark
are presented with arrows and fractions of the total flow. Shallow areas are highlighted
with a dotted red line.
2.2.1
Choke point in the bathymetry
The flow of the Baltic Sea towards Kattegat is influenced by two choke points: Drogden,
with a depth of about 8­10 m, and Darsser, where the depth is approximately 18 m see
Figure 2.2. At these points, the cross­section decreases, causing the flow rate to increase.
This increased flow rate raises friction loss, which in turn extracts energy and likely affects
the shape of the hydrograph.

2.2.2
Drivers of sea level rise
Extreme sea levels along the southeastern coast of Denmark, facing the Baltic Sea, can
occur from three primary reasons.
• Strong, continuous easterly wind, which raises the water level along Denmark’s
southeastern coast.
• The wind changes from a southwestern/western wind direction to a northwester­
ly/northern wind, forcing the Baltic Sea’s water into the Kattegat, which also tries to
push its water back into the Baltic Sea, causing a surge.
• Seiches which can be triggered by prolonged strong westerly winds, that elevate the
water level in the northeastern part of the Baltic Sea and lower it in the western part.
When these winds cease, the water level rebounds to its normal state, creating a
large wave that travels towards the southeastern coast of Denmark.
2.2.3
Storm surge amplifiers
Storm surges along the southeastern coast of Denmark can be amplified by specific me­
teorological conditions. All storm surges are affected by the pre­filling of the Baltic Sea,
which is controlled by prior wind and pressure systems. The water level of the Baltic Sea
can vary by up to one meter above or below average levels [11].
Certain wind directions are more severe than others, acting as amplifiers, and the most
severe wind direction varies for each station [12]. In addition to wind direction, the average
wind speed and the size of the wind field also affect the magnitude of the storm surge.
A less intense storm surge may result from a seiche, which can be amplified by the du­
ration, location, and depth of the low­pressure field. Additionally, the location of the shift
from low to high pressure can impact the magnitude of the seiche.
2.2.4
Storm surge characteristics due to the bathymetry
The impact and level of storm surges vary across different Danish locations. The eastern
Zealand experiences significant sea level rises while being heavily influenced by strong
wave action that develops over a long period before striking the coast. Southern Jutland
and Western Funen see the highest water levels during a Baltic Sea storm surge. The
described water flow pattern (7,3,1) indicates that water is trapped in the Little Belt and
therefor continuously rising as it cannot escape. The flow patterns are presented in Fig­
ure 2.2. The stations between Funen and Zealand can experience storm surges due to
the Kattegat tries to flow through the Great Belt while the Baltic Sea tries to flow the op­
posite direction. This lead to a rising flow for those stations. They can also experience
storm surge from strong north western winds, where the water is forced into for instance
Korsør or Karrebæksminde due to the wind. This type of storm surge is only experienced
by the station between Funen and Zealand, as the wind forces the water away from the
other stations.

2.3
Tidal effects
Tides, observable along the coastlines of the entire world, manifest as a periodic sea level
rise with an approximately 12­hour cycle. This phenomenon results from the gravitational
interactions between the Earth, moon, and sun. The moon’s orbit relative to the Earth
primarily drives the daily tidal variations, while the sun influences spring and neap tides
[13]. The velocity of water is squared depending on the water depth, which can alter the
periodic shape of the tide during a storm surge. Additionally, the amplitude of the tide is
significantly influenced by the geometry of the ”basin” in which it occurs.
Neither the North Sea nor the Baltic Sea is significantly affected by tides. The tide along
the west coast of Denmark originates from the Atlantic Ocean entering the North Sea,
creating an interference between waves within the North Sea. This wave interaction pro­
duces tides along the western coast of Jutland, extending into the Skagerrak and Kattegat
north of Skagen. By the time these tides enter the inner waters of Denmark, their ampli­
tude is considerably reduced.
Due to its size and shape, the Baltic Sea exhibits no tidal activity, resulting in an almost
negligible tidal influence along the southeastern coastline of Denmark. Tidal removal is
still included in this thesis as it is considered independent of the sea level rise coursed by
the storm surge.
2.4
Data gathering
Measuring techniques and placement of the stations affects the observations of the storm
surges. This section will outline the main properties of such differences.
Water level monitoring stations in Denmark are managed by Kystdirektoratet and the Dan­
ish Meteorological Institute (DMI). Data from these stations are collected via the website
Vandportalen.dk. Although stations along Sweden’s south coast and those near the north­
ern German border facing the Baltic Sea could be relevant for this study, only Danish
stations are included to simplify the data description. This project specifically focuses on
the southeastern coasts of Denmark and uses the stations located along this coastline.

2.4.1
Measuring techniques
Methods of the data gathering can be categorized into three methods: remote measure­
ment from above the surface, direct measurement at the surface, and measurement from
below the surface. To minimize external influences and wave impacts, all measurements
are conducted within a pipe, which can be made of metal or plastic [14].
Remote Measurement:
• Pros: Offers stable and accurate data.
• Cons: Requires careful calibration with respect to temperature and is sensitive to
obstructions like insects or seaweed within the pipe. It cannot measure water levels
when the surface is covered by ice.
• Technologies: Either radar or ultrasound.
Surface Measurement:
• Pros: The method is straightforward, involving a floating device attached to a wire
with a counterweight.
• Cons: The mechanical parts may wear out, and the presence of a counterweight
can lead to inaccuracies if the density of the water changes. Like remote measure­
ment, it fails to measure water levels under ice.
• Technology: Mechanical device.
Subsurface Measurement:
• Pros: Effective even when there is ice on the surface and relatively easy to install.
• Cons: The measurement accuracy can drift over time and requires periodic cali­
bration. This method involves multiple components, making it more complex than
other techniques.
• Technologies: Includes total pressure measurement combining air and water pres­
sure, relative pressure measurement that compensates for air pressure by connect­
ing to the surface, and a bubble method that uses the pressure required to release
bubbles as a measurement technique

2.4.2
Placement of the station
In addition to the technologies used, the placement of the measurement stations also
affects the values recorded. Stations are typically located inside harbors to protect them
from wave interactions. However, this positioning can experience interference of standing
waves within the harbor basin. In contrast, the Gedser station is situated outside the
harbor, where it is less protected from direct wave action. The surrounding geography
also influences measurements. For example, the station in Korsør, located inside the
harbor, can be affected by the inland waterbody, which is a lake. This lake can potentially
alter the harbor’s water level through wind­induced setup or increased levels due to heavy
rainfall in its watershed. The extent of these effects is not further investigated in this study.

2.5
Hydrograph modelling
Hydrographs are essential inputs for hydrodynamic simulations of storm surges. They
depict how the water level rises, reaches a peak, and then falls again. Hydrodynamic
simulations are commonly used to estimate the costs associated with different scenarios.
By evaluating costs across various return periods and scenarios, an Annual Estimated
Damage curve can be developed. This allows municipalities to discuss and decide on
the appropriate level of investment in storm surge protection. Therefore, the choice of
hydrograph has a significant potential impact on the cost­benefit analysis. It is crucial to
select the most suitable hydrograph for accurate analysis.

2.5.1
Current method of Denmark
Denmark’s current method for storm surge calculations involves using a worst­case sce­
nario hydrograph, either based on a specific measured storm surge hydrograph or an
estimated worst­case scenario.
The most recent damage calculation for Copenhagen was conducted in 2016 [5]. The
hydrograph used for this calculation was trapezoidal in shape, featuring a 12 hour rise in
water levels, followed by a peak duration of 12 hour, and then a 12 hour fall. The report
acknowledges that this peak duration is an overestimation, stating, ”The water level rarely
stays more than 12 hour at the peak.”
In February 2024, the first part of a new storm surge protection plan was revealed, which
included a hydrodynamic calculation of sea level rise [2]. This report utilized a different
hydrograph, specifically one that recreates the sea level rise from 1872. The recreation
was achieved through multiple simulations of weather scenarios similar to the 1872 storm
surge, and it was compared to a hand­drawn hydrograph from 1881.

from the Baltic Sea are as follows. The hydrograph No. 1 is from the COWI report in

There are significant differences between the hydrograph from the COWI report in 2016
and the one from Sund og Bælt in 2024. The key difference lies in the duration of the

ference is the event duration being 36 hour in the COWI hydrograph and 108 hour in the
Sund og Bælt hydrograph. A third notable difference is the return level, though this aspect
is not the focus of this report.

Tidal effects
The tidal effect are either removed or retained. In Method Two, the tidal component is
retained, while in other methods, it is removed initially and reintroduced later. This differs
due to the later methods of hydrograph creation where the first and third method adds the
tide as the sinusoidal shape it has, and fourth method adds the tide as a constant value of
the maximum tidal level along the entire storm surge. While Method Two is a Monte Carlo
approach describing the shape of the sea level rise with everything included. The most
common thing is to remove the tide and add it later on, as it is considered an independent
variable from the storm surge.
Detrending
Measured water level time series must be detrended to account for rising sea levels due to
global warming and the concurrent rise in Denmark’s ground level caused by post­glacial
rebound. Detrending is essential before detecting events using the peak­over­threshold
(POT) method, as this method employ a flat threshold, and without detrending, rising sea
levels could cause past events to be overlooked.
The international methods discussed introduce two detrending techniques: removing a
linear trend and yearly detrending. Both techniques are suitable due to the yearly varia­
tions in sea level alongside an overall linear increasing trend which can be observed in
Figure 2.7.
Figure 2.7: The figure shows the yearly mean sea level with the blue marks and the trend
of rising sea level with the red line [15].
Event detection
Event detection is done using the peak­over­threshold method, which effectively captures
variations in the frequency of events. This method is done by the first three methods with
a percentile threshold. The other event detection method is a variation of block maximum
method which results in 5 events per year. The issue of this method can be that some
years might have 6 large storm surges while other years might have none.
Event boundary
Once the peaks are detected, events must be encapsulated by a boundary, which can
be either time­dependent or water­level dependent.The lowest valley approach ensures
the complete development of the event is captured, another approach is the low limit of
HTWL (Highest Tidal Water Level within a 10­year period) is used and then the high tide
water level is added later in the final hydrograph. This water level boundary might cut the
events short as they might dive shortly beneath the boundary but rises after wards. The
time limit of 36 hour is applied based on the typical duration of storm surges. However, this
approach may include normal water levels or exclude the shape or longer event, affecting

Event analysis
For event analysis, three methods are considered: parameterizing, normalizing the data
and describing events through duration analysis, or fitting an equation to describe a typical
storm surge rise and creating a best­fit curve for the data. Each analysis benefits the next
step of hydrograph creation in its own manner.
Hydrograph method
The first and third article methods scale the storm surge hydrograph from the duration
analysis with a certain return level. Then, tidal effects are added either as peak­on­peak
in method one or station­dependent in method three. The third method, developed for
the English coastline, which experiences large tidal effects, analyzes whether there is a
statistical time delay between the tide and the storm surge peak at each station.
The fourth method is a block method where one block represents the mean sea level, the
second block is the highest tidal value in a ten­year period, and the third block is the fitted
curve times a return level. The second method employs a Monte Carlo analysis of the
determined parameters from each of the detected events. This results in the possibility of
choosing between multiple hydrographs with the same return level.

4.4
Event detection
Each processed time series is analyzed for extreme events using the POT methodology.
To ensure independence of the events, a minimum interval of 80 hours between peaks is
used. If multiple peaks occur within this interval, only the largest peak is considered, and
the others are disregarded as individual events.
The threshold is set to average approximately two events per year per station. Initially the
threshold value is 0.65 m, and then adjusted with steps of 1cm until the yearly average of
detected events is equal to 2. A visual inspection of the method is presented Figure 4.7.
Here the threshold is the red line and events detected are the peaks above the threshold
value.
The target average of two events per year is chosen based on statistical requirements from
Kystdirektoratet, which recommend detecting 0.5 to 2 events per year for extreme event
analysis. The study by [17] employs a POT threshold at the 99.7th percentile, typically
resulting in an average of 1.3 to 5 events per year per station—a frequency considered
excessively high for this study. The idea by selecting the upper limit of the 0.5 to 2 event
range, is that we ensure a sufficiently high but manageable number of events, leading to

Hydrograph modelling
This chapter describes the method used to create hydrographs. This approach combines
methods and insights discussed in the previous chapters to generate a comprehensive
hydrograph.
5.1
Hydrograph modelling method
The data used for hydrograph modeling in this thesis consists of measured water levels.
From this data, tidal effects and long­term trends are removed using Utide. Events are
detected using a Peak Over Threshold (POT) method, where the threshold is iteratively
adjusted by steps of 1 cm to yield an average of two events per year. The event boundaries
are initially set by a 144 hours time limit on each side of the peak, followed by a boundary
condition of if the water level goes below 0, then the event is ended, if not then it is the
time boundary. Almost all events boundary are determined by the below 0 criteria. A
peak is considered individual if there is 80 hours of more to te next detected peak. If there
are two peak within a 80 hours window, the largest peak values is saved. All detected
events are sorted by water level magnitude. The largest 15 events are selected for a
duration analysis, which forms the basis for constructing the hydrograph. This procedure
is presented in Figure 5.1.
Figure 5.1: Step by step hydrograph creation of this thesis.
The selected events for hydrograph modeling are normalized, and for each percentile
on either side of the peak, an average duration is calculated across the events. The
combined hydrograph for a station is then constructed by accumulating the average time
count at each percentile, starting from the peak towards the bottom. An example of this
process is presented in Figure 5.2. All individual hydrographs are plotted in the same
plot in Figure 5.3. The color is chosen by the grouping described in subsection 4.5.2. As
discussed in the section on grouping stations, certain groups exhibit specific similarities.
Therefore, a hydrograph is created for each group and presented in Figure 5.4. These
grouped hydrographs is an attempt to represent hydrographs of storm surge in cities with
a distance to a water level station. A plot of each individual city like the one in Figure 5.2
is found in Appendix E.

7.1
Key results
Throughout the chapters there has been presented key learnings. This section discusses
how some of these learnings can be utilized into use cases.
7.1.1
Denmark divided into sections
By analyzing measured water level data, it becomes evident that the southeastern coast­
line of Denmark can be divided into distinct sections based on the shape of the hydrograph
observed during a storm surge. This classification is crucial for determining appropriate
input data for hydrodynamic simulations of cities situated between water level stations.
The unique characteristics of Kalvehave, which differ from other groups, suggest that ad­
ditional factors, such as the presence of lagoons or channels, may influence the formation
of these subgroups. This grouping is useful as it provides a simplistic overview of which
areas of Denmark are effected by which types of storms, and this can be used for the
broader conversations when planning coastal protection.

==PAGE:62==
7.1.2
The effect of wind systems
Another significant finding is that the storm surges along the southeastern coastline of
Denmark are primarily driven by three wind systems:
• Long­lasting south/southwestern wind shifting to a northwestern/northern wind
• Long­lasting western wind shifting to a northwestern/northern wind
• Long­lasting eastern wind
This information can be leveraged to compile statistics on the frequency and character­
istics of each storm type, particularly the resulting hydrographs. Such data are crucial
for decision­making processes related to urban and coastal protection strategies. Addi­
tionally, understanding these wind systems enhances our comprehension of the driving
forces behind storm surges, thereby improving our ability to predict future storm types.
As demonstrated by the hydrograph of the October 2023 storm, which was notably differ­
ent in terms of duration and shape compared to almost all other recorded storms, most of
which are driven by the first wind scenario. These variations in hydrographs have different
practical applications. For instance, the October storm’s unique characteristics might be
useful for designing the resilience of dikes, while the most common wind scenario could
inform the dimensioning of pumping stations at river outlets to the ocean if a flood gate is
closed or for damage calculations.
7.1.3
Duration at maximum level
Both the flood extension and the flood depth is depending on the duration at the maximum
level. This is important knowledge as it emphasises the necessary of doing statistics
of storm surge hydrographs in order to achieve the most realistic flood scenarios. This
knowledge are particularly relevant when municipalities has to decide how to divide the
expenses to coastal protection. If one municipality lies behind the other, but both are
flooded during a storm scenario, then the extension could be useful for discussion of how
to divide the expenses of coastal protection.
7.2
Limitations
This section presents some of the main limitations of the learning from this thesis.
7.2.1
Measured data of large events
There is a gap in measured storm data with high time resolution, which is necessary to
create meaningful hydrographs based on statistics. The hydrographs in this thesis are
derived partly from small events, and it is essential to examine whether these are repre­
sentative of larger events such as storm surges or if they can be useful for future damage
calculations, especially considering the likelihood of raised sea levels in the future.
7.2.2
Unequal distribution of wind systems
As presented in the key findings, different wind systems generate distinct hydrographs,
with the first wind system—characterized by a southern wind shifting to a northwest­
ern/northern wind—being the most represented in the statistics. This highlights the need
for more data on storms originating from the east or northeast directions to ensure com­
prehensive understanding and accurate modeling of hydrographs. Studies of water levels
with regards to wind systems are already being done as in [12], where the most hazardous
wind direction of different locations in Denmark are defined.

7.2.3
Oceanographic knowledge
The morphology of the Danish coastline, along with the variation in bathymetry, under­
scores the importance of involving oceanographic experts to determine the applicability
of using averaged grouped hydrographs for hydrodynamic simulations in areas lacking
nearby water level stations. For instance, as demonstrated by the example of Kalvehave,
it would not be accurate to use an average of the nearby stations, even if they appear to
experience the same type of storm surges.
7.2.4
Linear dependency of large events
The sub conclusion of a linear dependency between longer storm duration and higher
water levels is limited by the lack of measured data, resulting in significant uncertainty.
From a physical perspective, it is logical that higher water levels require longer duration,
as a larger volume of water is necessary to achieve greater heights. Considering that
most sea level rise during a storm surge is driven by wind force, either increased wind
speed or extended duration is required to transport the necessary water volume.
7.2.5
Choice of city for hydrodynamic simulation
Evaluating the effect of hydrographs based on a single city presents some limitations. In­
undation depends on numerous factors, including local coastal morphology and bathymetry.
Additionally, the slope of the inland area plays a crucial role; flatter or declining areas are
more prone to rapid flooding compared to areas with a slight incline. Therefore, the choice
of testing the hydrograph effect should also consider inland topography to examine differ­
ent scenarios comprehensively.
Studies have assessed the differences in flood extent and damage calculations between
static and dynamic flood simulations [23]. Similar effects are observed when comparing
dynamic simulations against static simulations in two cities in Denmark, resulting in signif­
icant differences in flooded areas. Thus, a comprehensive study of topographic features
before flood simulations is recommended for future investigation.
7.3
Outlook
Future research should investigate the shape of hydrographs influenced by wind systems.
Conducting statistical analyses on wind systems, rather than on hydrographs, could yield
valuable insights, especially if historical wind data extends further back than measured
water level data.
With insights from wind data statistics, hydrographs caused by eastern or northeastern
winds could be recreated using a high­resolution model of the Baltic Sea and the Danish
coastline. Running a series of simulations for these wind systems would provide a clearer
picture of how hydrographs behave under different conditions.
Additionally, applying the methodology of this thesis, with improved event boundary condi­
tions, to the entire coastline of Denmark would help determine if other coastal areas can
also be categorized based on hydrograph shapes. Comparing current coastal flooding
studies with input hydrographs derived from this analysis could indicate whether updates
to flood scenarios are necessary. It is recommended that the event boundary condition for
cross­correlation is the lowest valley method, and for hydrograph creation, a combination
of the lowest valley method to encapsulate the entire event followed by duration analysis
above a water level threshold of zero.
A final recommendation for future research is to establish a national reproducible method
of hydrograph creation for damage calculation of flood scenarios. This would enable the
comparison of hydrographs across the entire coastline of Denmark.

8
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to provide knowledge to the field of storm surges along the Danish coast
by answering the following research questions
1. What are the current methods in Denmark for creating hydrographs of storm surges?
2. Do hydrographs vary in space across the Danish coastline?
3. Is it reasonable to create an average hydrograph based on measured data?
4. What effect does the hydrograph have on hydrodynamic simulations?
The conclusion to these found throughout the thesis are as following.
1. The current method of hydrograph creation in Denmark is not yet standardized.
Recent approaches vary between recreating the worst experienced scenario, such
as the storm of 1872, and using a worst­case scenario with a trapezoidal hydrograph
of 36 hours duration, including 12 hours at peak level. In the flooding directive
of 2019 [21], hydrographs were based on measured data; however, the methods
are not reproducible from the available papers. Additionally, the hydrograph varies
according to the location along the Danish coastline.
2. Hydrographs have unique features based on their geographical location along the
Danish coastline. This thesis presents a method for grouping the coastline based on
hydrograph characteristics. It is recommended to apply this methodology along the
entire Danish coastline to gain a better understanding of how storm surge features
can be categorized.
3. It is reasonable to create average hydrographs based on measured data; however,
the use case of such hydrographs requires further examination. The significant
difference in hydrograph shapes between frequently occurring events and extreme
events impacts their application.
4. The duration at the maximum water level of a hydrograph significantly affects the
extent and depth of flooding in hydrodynamic simulations. Therefore, accurately
implementing this element is crucial for creating realistic flood scenarios for decision­
makers.

B
International methods
This appendix is a more in depth description of each of the four international methods for
creating hydrographs.
Method 1
The first method [16] uses modelled data for the ocean level and then creates an artificial
gauge station for every 50km along the global coastline. Storm surge data are identified
by subtracting the tide from the combined time series. Extreme events are detected using
the peak over threshold (POT) method at the 99th percentile. From the identified peaks,
the time steps 36 hours before and after the peak are extracted to form the identified storm
surges. All storm surges are then normalized, and a general hydrograph is generated by
averaging the durations on each side of the peak, in steps of 0.1 from 0.25 to 1. The total
water level for a storm surge is then calculated as the sum of the average tide and the
storm surge hydrograph times the return period. An interesting finding from this study was
the absence of any delay between the maximum tide and the peak of the storm surge;
thus, the peaks of both are added together to determine the total water level.
Method 2
The second method [17] is developed using measured data from 45 gauges along the
Northern German coastline toward the Baltic Sea. Firstly, the time series is detrended by
averaging the yearly highs of the tides, and then events are detected using POT with a
threshold of the 99.7th percentile. Detrending by yearly averages is performed to account
for the yearly variation in sea level. This is because although sea level rise is often de­
scribed as a linear trend, there is still yearly variation, hence the need for yearly averages.
The POT method results in a larger number of detected events than the annual maximum,
which is important for creating a robust model with relatively short time series and is there­
fore preferred. The threshold of 99.7 is chosen because it yields the most stable return
water level, as shown in further analysis [24]. Once the peaks are detected, the dura­
tions on each side are determined by iterating until the water level either falls below the
high water value for the given year or until there have been five troughs in the water level
curve, with none crossing the high water level; in this case, the lowest of the five troughs
is chosen as the end point. This method of determining duration introduces variation in
the rise and fall of the water level compared to other methods described. The curve of the
rise and fall of the water level is then parameterized and analyzed using a Monte Carlo
simulation, resulting in a hydrograph generation method that provides multiple shapes of
the hydrograph for the same return period.
Method 3
The third method described by [18] is similar to the second and also utilizes measured
values. It differs from the others by using skew­surge water levels instead of just measured
water levels. The skew surge is defined as the difference between the peak of the surge
level and the peak of the tide level. This approach ensures that the tide and surge levels
do not interfere with each other, which is beneficial in the United Kingdom where the
interaction between tide and storm surge varies significantly along the coastline. Skew
surges are isolated using POT with a threshold of the 99.9th percentile and 36 hours
on each side of the peak. The 15 largest events are chosen from each station, and a
hydrograph for each is generated. Firstly, the skew surge is aggregated into 15­minute
time steps, then normalized, and for each 10th percentile, a duration is calculated. The
article describes two methods of calculating the duration. The first and most conservative
method selects the highest duration for each 10th percentile, while the second selects
