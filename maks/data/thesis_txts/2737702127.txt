Abstract
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) has become a crucial tool for optimizing perfor-
mance and equipment design in competitive cycling. This thesis focuses on the develop-
ment and validation of a numerical method capable of simulating the pedaling motion of
a cyclist, which remains a less explored area in the literature on CFD simulations of cy-
cling. The high complexity and resource demands associated with existing methods limits
investigations in this field.
This thesis covers a comprehensive investigation of various turbulence approaches, vis-
cosity models, boundary conditions, and mesh operations aimed at establishing a robust
dynamic model capable of accurately capturing turbulent effects caused by pedaling.
Initial simulations are conducted under static conditions, evaluating turbulence approaches
including RANS, DES, and LES against wind tunnel data. This evaluation aims to iden-
tify the most suitable approach for transient simulations of cyclists, thereby laying the
groundwork for developing the dynamic model.
A methodology is introduced for simulating pedaling motion, employing superposed mo-
tions and multiple overset meshes to eliminate the need for remeshing. Additionally, the
thesis briefly explores the impact of rotating wheels to demonstrate the potential of achiev-
ing fully realistic dynamic simulations. The avoidance of remeshing is shown to increase
sensitivity to stability issues, necessitating further investigation. The introduced method-
ology represents promising advancements in developing a dynamical model for simulating
pedaling. Ongoing research is required to address stability issues, thus obtaining results
of high accuracy aligning with experimental results.

1
Introduction
The aerodynamics in cycling have been intensely researched to optimize performance, as
every second counts in professional cycling. Since aerodynamic drag is the most significant
resistance faced by a cyclist [1, 2], extensive efforts have been made to reduce it.
In
recent years, Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) has become increasingly applied in
studies of sport aerodynamics.[3] However, the impact of leg-motion in CFD simulations
of cyclists, remains a relatively unexplored area. This gap in the research field provided
the motivation for this thesis, which aims to develop a numerical method capable of
simulating this motion. The thesis was conducted in collaboration with Team Danmark,
which works with Danish elite athletes to help them achieve world-class performance.
Since the turbulent effect of leg motion is rather unexplored, there may be opportunities
for unseen improvements, potentially giving Danish cycling athletes a competitive edge.
1.1
Literature review
Until recently, 3D geometries have been modeled by CAD software, often leading to sim-
plified geometries or labor-intensive digital reconstruction from images. [3] With the rising
development of 3D scanners, becoming reliable and more broadly available, the possibility
of obtaining high-accuracy representations of the real-life geometries has become possible.
High-accuracy resolution of the geometry is crucial for conducting CFD simulations, hence
why CAD modeling have been preferred. With modern 3D scanners, higher resolutions
are now possible, enabling realistic and very detailed scans. Widely studied, are rider
positions, bike- and equipment design. [4] Manufacturers of bicycles and equipment as
Specialized have a large interest in producing the best aerodynamic products possible,
where the usage by professional athletes is great marketing. Moreover, professional teams
pursue the best performance of their athletes, whereas the rider’s position on the bike
often is investigated. [5–8] By reducing the drag force, less power is required from the
cyclist to maintain the same speed. Other studies investigate the effect of riding behind
other cyclists or even in the wake from motorbikes as often used in professional cycling.[7,
9, 10] The impact of crosswinds on the flow around a cyclist is also studied [11], as often
measured in wind tunnel tests by performing a yaw sweep, through different angles be-
tween the incoming wind and direction of travel. Defraeye et al. [12] was one of the first
to evaluate the performance of different turbulence approaches. The study investigated
multiple steady RANS turbulence models, along with the LES Smagorinsky model. This
study showed the best performance by the use of the steady RANS k −ω SST model
and the LES model. Since then, computational resources have become less of a limita-
tion, making more complex turbulence modeling approaches possible. This study did not
include a bike and contained only 8M cells, contrary to recent grids commonly ranging
around 30 −50M cells, based on grid sensitivity analysis.[4, 13] A comprehensive study

performed by van Druenen and Blocken [4] was recently (2024) published, investigating
the impact of computational grid parameters and different turbulence models. Blocken
has published a large number of papers regarding CFD simulations of cyclist aerodynam-
ics [7–10, 14], however, the steady RANS turbulence model has been his preferred choice,
as also initially recommended by Defraeye [12]. Occasionally studies have used transient
models, as the more computationally Large Eddy Simulation (LES) model.[11, 12] More
recently hybrid RANS-LES models such as (Improved-Delayed) Detached Eddy Simula-
tion (DES) [11] and Scale-Adaptive-Simulation (SAS) [4, 13, 15] have risen in popularity,
as computational resources are becoming less a limitation. With scale-resolving turbulence
models, it is possible to investigate the occurring turbulent eddies, hence a more accurate
prediction of reality. The study by Fintelman et al. [11] showed a better alignment with
experimental data with the use of LES and DES than RANS. In addition to the often-
used steady-state assumption for simulations, motions have been disregarded in the vast
majority of studies. Partial motions have been studied, such as implementing rotation of
the wheels [16] where rotation of the wheel resulted in a 2.9% drag area difference for the
whole system. The effect of pedaling is rarely studied, where the vast majority of studies
solely focus on steady simulations of static positions. Griﬀith et al. [6, 13] have published
studies on dynamic leg-motion over a full rotation of the crank by time- and phase averag-
ing of a crude mannequin-like CAD model. While the studies have succeeded to attempt
simulating dynamic leg motion, certain crucial limitations of the method exists. A highly
simplified 3D model is used produce 180 different CAD models representing crank angle
positions. This requires a huge workload, as each model had to be meshed individually,
and loaded during the simulation. A succeeder to the approach of producing CAD models
has not been published, until recently, where utilization of digital animation software has
been proposed.[3] In this study, a virtual skeleton methodology is presented, making large
geometry position modifications easy. With this method, realistic geometries can be pro-
duced and modified faster, however, the method does not solve the overlying limitation
that also applies for the work published by Griﬀith et al, as each model has to be meshed
and loaded during simulations. Javadi [17] has proposed a method of a ”semi-pedalling”
cyclist, where velocity components are applied on the thigh and calf, with only minor
effect obtained.
To the best of my knowledge, no method has been published providing a method for dy-
namic leg-motion without the need of importing a high number of meshes or the use of
re-meshing. Therefore the main scope of this thesis is to develop such a method, and
furthermore investigate the use of transient and scale resolving turbulence models.

Aerodynamics theory
2.1
Drag
With the rise of wind tunnel testing and the more recent increase in CFD simulations
within cycling, the standard measure for comparing drag has become drag area denoted
CDA (m2). It is defined as:
CDA =
FD
0.5ρU 2
(2.1)
where FD is the drag force (N), ρ is the density (kg/m3), and U is the flow velocity (m/s)
relative to the object. The isolated drag coeﬀicient CD is diﬀicult to compute when testing
a person in a wind tunnel or at the track because the cross-sectional area is hard to measure
accurately. Instead, the drag area became the standard measure for comparison. This is
also used in CFD simulations, even though the cross-sectional area of the geometries is
easy to compute. The drag force FD acting on the surface consists of two contributions:
pressure forces and shear forces. The pressure force vector that the fluid exerts on the
cyclist’s surface is computed as:
Fpressure = (pf −pref)Af
(2.2)
where pf is the face static pressure (Pa), Af is the face area (m2), and pref is the reference
pressure (Pa). The shear force vector is determined by the stress tensor at the face, Tf
(Pa), multiplied by the face area it is acting on. The direction vector is set to the direction
of travel when determining drag.
Fτ = TfAf
(2.3)
The total drag force is computed by summing these contributions:
FD = Fpressure + Fτ
(2.4)
From the drag force experienced by the cyclist, the required Power (W) to maintain the
velocity U is determined by:
P = FDU
(2.5)
which is a commonly monitored metric by cyclists. The required Power determined by
this is purely based on air resistance, which contributes up to 90% to the total resistance
in road racing [2, 18] and up to 94% in time trial competitions [19]. The remaining resis-
tance primarily consists of rolling resistance [18], which together with minor contributions,
requires additional power.

2.2
Solvers
As stated in Section 1.1 the steady-state time model is often favored in published literature
due to its lower computational cost and fast convergence. However, using a steady-state
model to capture unsteady physical phenomena can be inaccurate, as it does not provide
any prediction of the actual turbulent eddies. Instead, an unsteady model can be utilized,
which introduces time steps to resolve the flow.
STAR-CCM+ offers two options for
unsteady solvers: explicit and implicit. The primary difference between them lies in how
the later state is determined. The implicit solver solves an equation that involves both
the current and previous time-step, whereas the explicit solver relies purely on the current
state.
The explicit solver is mainly used for laminar flow, while the implicit solver is
suitable for any type of flow. For transient simulations of a moving cyclist, the unsteady
implicit solver will be utilized.
To achieve a faster and more accurate solution, high-
accuracy temporal discretization is used. This involves dividing the time into discrete
intervals to solve the system’s equations. The standard Euler implicit scheme uses the
solutions at the current time-step n+1 and the previous time-step n. In contrast to this a
second-order temporal discretization involves the solutions at the current time-step n + 1,
the previous time-step n, and the step before that n −1. For solving the fluid flow, two
flow solvers are available in STAR-CCM+ [20]:
• Segregated Flow Solver: Sequentially solves conservation equations, thereby re-
ducing the memory required.
• Coupled Flow solver: Solves conservation equations in a coupled manner, and is
preferred for strong interactions like compressible flows, shock waves or high Mach
number flows.
As no strong interactions are expected in this project, and constant density is assumed,
the segregated flow solver is used.
2.3
Turbulence modeling approach
As covered in Section 1.1 the most widely used turbulence modeling approach within
CFD simulations in cycling is the Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS). This is due
to low computational requirements and fast convergence, making parameter studies easy
to perform. The RANS model governs the transport of the mean flow quantities instead
of resolving the eddies instantaneously. Siemens recently introduced the Scale-Resolving
Hybrid, which is technically a variation of both URANS and Large Eddy Simulation
(LES), filtering in both time and space. According to Siemens [21] this results in nearly
the same computational requirement as URANS, while being able to resolve some scales
of turbulence.
although this model is not yet widely used in the literature, it will be
tested against the other models in this project. The Detached Eddy Simulation approach
provides a hybrid of URANS-modeled turbulence and the scale-resolving LES model. In
the boundary layer close to the wall, URANS is utilized, while LES is used elsewhere.

Blending functions ensures the transition between the two turbulence models. DES is
implemented in two variations:
• Delayed Detached Eddy Simulations (DDES): Implements a delay factor to
improve the ability to distinguish between RANS and LES regions.
• Improved Delayed Eddy Simulations (IDDES): Further improves the DDES
by adding a dependence between the subgrid length scale and wall distance.
To limit the number of simulations performed in this project, only the IDDES approach
is tested. The LES approach exists for purely scale-resolving simulations. It is by far
the most computationally expensive turbulence model, as it resolves the larger eddies,
providing the most accurate turbulence resolution. However, in addition to the increased
computational resources required, the model also introduces more sensitivity to instability.
This sets higher requirements for the mesh, which has to be more finely resolved to ensure
stability. The LES approach offers three different approaches for modeling the subgrid-
scale stress tensor: [22]
• Smagorinsky Subgrid Scale: The original subgrid-scale model, with multiple
limitations, making it less suitable for complex geometries.
• Dynamic Smagorinsky Subgrid Scale: Replaces the non-universal coeﬀicient
from the basic Smagorinsky model by computing a local time-varying coeﬀicient.
• Wall-Adapting Local-Eddy Viscosity Subgrid Scale (WALE): The most
modern subgrid-scale model implemented and the default recommendation in STAR-
CCM+, shown to be less sensitive to the non-universal coeﬀicient, and able to give
accurate scaling at walls. [22]
2.4
RANS Turbulence models
Several turbulence models are available when selecting the RANS approach.
The five
options with guidelines provided by Siemens [23], are listed below:
• K-Epsilon: Robust and a good compromise between between computational re-
quirements and accuracy.
• K-Omega: Similar to the K-Epsilon model, as two transport equations are solved,
but with a different transport turbulence variable.
• Reynolds Stress: Complex and expensive, recommended for strongly anisotropic
turbulence, such as fast swirling flows.

Synthetic turbulence

2.5.2
Wall treatment
For turbulent simulations, it is crucial to accurately resolve the flow across wall boundary
layers, as walls are a significant source of vorticity in flow problems. The inner region
of the boundary layer is divided into three segments. Closest to the wall is the viscous
sublayer, where the fluid is in contact with the wall, resulting in flow dominated by viscous
effects. Further from the wall is the log-law layer, where the flow is influenced equally by
viscous and turbulent effects. Between these two regions lies the transitional layer buffer
layer. To accurately resolve the flow across wall boundary layers, STAR-CCM+ provides
three wall treatment options:
• Low-y+ Treatment: This approach resolves the viscous sublayer by solving the
transport equations all the way to the wall, requiring suﬀiciently fine near-wall cells
with wall y+ ∼1. This method can be computationally expensive, especially for
high Reynolds numbers problems where the viscous sublayer becomes very thin.
• High-y+ Treatment:, This method does not resolve the viscous sublayer directly.
Instead, it uses wall functions to derive wall shear stress and turbulent dissipation
assuming the near-wall cell lie within the log-law layer at y+ > 30.
• All-y+ Treatment: This is a hybrid approach combining the low- and high-y+
treatments. For fine near-wall cells, the low-y+ treatment is utilized, while for coarser
cells, it uses the high-y+ treatment. A blending function is applied to determine the
turbulence quantities in the intermediate region. The All-y+ Treatment is generally
recommended and will be selected for all simulations in this project.
2.6
Overset mesh
Overset meshes are overlapping meshes often used for moving bodies. A background mesh
is used to enclose the entire domain, and one or multiple overset meshes are created for
separate bodies. When using an overset mesh, the cells are grouped into three types;
Active, Inactive, and Acceptor cells. The active cells are solved, while the inactive cells
are not; however, these can change to become active as the overset region moves. The
Acceptor cells separate the active and inactive cells and ensure interpolation between
these regions. The oversets are arranged in a hierarchy to control the hole-cutting process
and prioritize cell preservation in specific regions. The Alternate Hole Cutting option is
selected for its robustness in cases with close gaps.
Additionally, the Close Proximity
option is activated, allowing more active cells between a moving body and a background
wall.

condition, it is possible to place the bicycle on the road as shown in Figure 3.3, where a
minor portion of the tire goes into the ground boundary. In real-life conditions, the tire
flattens depending on the tire pressure, but this is not expected to significantly impact
any turbulence results. Another widely used method involves using a moving wall for the
ground, imposing a no-slip condition with a velocity equal to that of the inlet, particularly
relevant when using scale-resolving models or incorporating rotation of the wheels [16].
With this method the bike is isolated from the ground by offsetting it, thus hovering above
the ground. As Gerasimov [31] explains, using symmetry planes with LES simulations is
inaccurate because the eddies are reflected in an unphysical manner. For LES simulations,
the symmetry condition for the road is replaced with a moving wall boundary condition.
With the moving wall condition, it is advised to slightly offset the bicycle from the ground,

5.1.2
High and low drag positions
During a crank cycle, the experienced drag forces vary significantly as the legs change po-
sition. The lowest drag position occurs when the pedal arms are approximately horizontal,
while the highest drag position is reached after rotating past the vertical position of the
pedal arms. In this high drag position, one leg is tucked towards the chest while the other
leg is stretched. The stretched leg contributes to an increase in drag, while the tucked-
in leg decreases its contribution. Different positions in the crank cycle are visualized in
Figure 5.2, approximately close to high and low drag positions. Griﬀith et al. [13] have
presented a drag variation throughout a crank cycle, where 180 CAD models representing
different crank angle positions were considered. Their study showed a variation in drag
area ranging from 0.15 m2 to 0.2 m2 throughout the crank cycle, confirming the expected
change in drag based on leg positions.
Figure 5.2: Geometry positions during crank cycle.
To verify the results obtained from the dynamic simulations, two static positions are
simulated corresponding to the low drag and high drag positions. These static simulations
provide a reference for understanding the range within which the dynamic solution is
expected to oscillate. The motion solver is frozen at the high drag position at t = 4.33 s,
and the simulation is extended for an additional duration to obtain quasi-steady state
drag forces.

5.1.3
Pedaling effect from WT data
A study by García-López et al. [38] investigated the impact of pedaling on drag area. Five
different cyclists were measured in a static low-drag position and the same position while
pedaling. The static position resulted in drag areas ranging from 0.237 m2 to 0.291 m2,
while drag area obtained while pedaling ranged from 0.307 m2 to 0.377 m2. The mean static
drag area was 0.260 m2 compared to a mean pedaling drag area of 0.341 m2, indicating a
significant 27% increase in drag area due to pedaling. Static simulations of different crank
positions have been done in several studies as previously mentioned in Section 1.1, and
while this indeed makes it possible to predict a drag area throughout a crank revolution, it
does not take the possible turbulent effects created by the pedaling motion into account.
According to the wind tunnel tests presented by Fitzgerald et al. [24], the turbulence
intensity around the cyclist increases by 0.5% when pedaling. As the turbulence created
by the pedaling motion is expected to depend on the cadence, investigating the turbulence
at sprints i.e. high pedaling frequencies might be worth pursuing in future research.
5.1.4
Superposing motion of legs
Superposing motions are utilized to control the rotation of each part of the leg individually,
while still depending on each other. As covered in Section 5.2, each leg is split into three
parts: thigh, shin, and foot. For all parts of the legs, individual overset meshes are created
as will be described in Section 5.3.1. The methodology of dependently rotating the parts
of each leg is as follows:
• A rotating motion is created for the thigh, with the axis of rotation around the
y-axis, and the origin positioned in the center of the added hip sphere at [x, y, z] =
[0.62, 0.1, 0.73] m. The Rotation Specification is set to Rotation Angle, where the
interpolation function interp_right_thigh is specified.
• To this motion, a superposing rotation is applied for the shin. This allows defining a
separate rotation for the shin using the equivalent interpolating field function, which
now also depends on the rotation caused by the thigh. The axis of origin is set to
the initial position of the knee sphere. However, as the thigh rotates, the coordinate
system managing the rotation of the shin moves accordingly, hence moving the axis
of origin.
• To rotate the foot, a superposing motion is added to the superposed shin motion,
meaning the foot will rotate depending on the rotation of the previous two motions.
By structuring the rotation with this methodology, the motion of each leg can be modeled
to realistically reflect the biomechanics of the motion when pedaling.

Geometry modifications

To enable the motion in the imported geometry, several modifications are required for each

as the main scope is to develop the method.

Overset meshes
Figure 5.4: Split view of created overset meshes for right leg.
When constructing an overset mesh, the process involves creating a geometry encapsulat-

The advantage of the offset operation is the possibility of reducing the size of the overset

moving part, moves on top of the background mesh. The details explaining the working

5.4
Stability optimization
The experienced stability issues consist of diverging values at random timestamps. The
simulation will run for hundreds of time steps, and suddenly a spike in the drag force
and residuals occurs within a few iterations, causing the values to diverge. If the flow is
frozen a few time steps and after the point of the issue, it can continue running without
any diverging values. This means, that only a very few time steps are causing the issues.
However, the randomness of the issue makes it diﬀicult to pinpoint the cause or solve it.
Addressing stability issues of dynamic simulations can be severely complex. While a variety
of measures have been tested to enhance stability, in addition to the mesh improvements,
none of these measures have yielded the desired impact on stability. Covering each measure
in detail would be meaningless, but numerous tests have been conducted, covering different
turbulence approaches, utilizing the coupled solver, the use of adaptive meshing, adaptive
time-stepping, overset behavior, and topology, amongst others. The stability issues appear
to be primarily mesh-related, where the critical factor seems to be the overlapping of the
highly refined overset meshes.

Additional Motions
Including additional motions might be interesting, to provide the most realistic dynamical

5.6
Rotating wheels
Figure 5.8: Direction of rotation of front wheel.
The impact of rotating wheels has previously been investigated by [16] as mentioned in
Section 1.1. The impact on drag area was shown to be low, thus many studies neglect this
motion. The impact in this investigated case is expected to be even lower, as the spokes
have been removed from the scan, to ease meshing. However, to enhance the realism of
the dynamic model, it could be desirable to include the rotation of the wheels, as this
is a straightforward implementation with the given geometry. The wheels are split from
the geometry, creating wall boundaries for both wheels isolated from the geometry. Local
coordinate systems are then defined at the center of each wheel. A tangential velocity is
specified with a local rotation rate of ω = −44.12 rad/s for each wheel boundary, with the
axis of rotation defined. The rotation rate ω (rad/s) is determined by the equation ω = v
r,
where r = 0.34 m is the radius of the wheel, and v = −u = −15 m/s is the tangential
velocity. The negative sign ensures that the wheels rotate in the correct direction relative
to the inlet velocity. Visualizations and the obtained results of this implementation are
discussed in Section 7.3.2.

Rotating pedal arm
The rotation of pedal arms could be implemented by the same method. Creating a single
local coordinate system, located in the center of the crank as the rotation axis, and defining
a local rotation rate. These motions are straightforward to implement, as the motions has
a constant axis of rotation, and follow a simple rotation rate, easily managed by sine
functions. Due to time limitations, this is not pursued as the expected impact on the drag
area is small.

8
Conclusion
This thesis has focused on advancing the capabilities of CFD to simulate the dynamic
leg motion in competitive cycling.
The primary objective was to develop a numerical
method that is capable of capturing the turbulence and obtaining the drag area of a
pedaling cyclist. The ultimate motivation for this development is the potential to identify
previously unseen improvements that could provide a competitive edge for the Danish
cycling athletes working with Team Denmark.
Although the significant impact of leg
positioning has been studied multiple times, as discussed in Section 1.1, accounting for
the turbulent effects from dynamic movement remains a relatively unexplored topic within
CFD simulations. The complexity of the motion has been challenging to simulate, and
substantial computational demands of the existing methods has limited investigations in
this topic. Throughout this thesis, several key contributions towards a realistic and reliable
dynamic model have been made.
A valid methodology was developed to simulate dynamic leg motion using CFD. This
involved superposing motions and using interfaces between multiple overset meshes to
manage the moving boundaries of the legs. This method enables highly realistic leg motion
modeling with the potential for high accuracy. By avoiding the need for remeshing, this
approach significantly enhances computational eﬀiciency but introduces stability issues
that must be resolved.
Different turbulence approaches, including the Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS),
Detached Eddy Simulation (DES), Large Eddy Simulation (LES), and Scale-Resolving
Hybrid (SRH), were compared and evaluated against wind tunnel data to determine their
suitability for transient simulations of cyclists. The findings indicate that the LES and
DES approaches provided more accurate predictions of flow, with similar deviations of
−4.5% from wind tunnel test data. Since wind tunnel data is averaged from pedaling, the
obtained drag results were expected to underpredict this. The improved accuracy of the
LES and DES approaches was expected, given their ability to resolve turbulence scales
rather than model them as with the RANS approach. However, the key finding was that
the mean drag area was predicted more accurately using the LES and DES approaches.
The RANS approach yielded reasonable results, overpredicting wind tunnel data by 4.4%,
making it a valid option for simulations where time or resources are limited. The fast
convergence of the steady RANS approach outweighs the URANS and SRH approaches,
where differences in drag area were found to be 3.9% and 6.7% respectively, but at a
severely larger computational cost compared to the RANS approach.
The effect of refining the wake mesh and prism layers has been investigated, where par-
ticularly the prism layer refinement was shown to be important to accurately obtain the
drag, with differences up to 2.1% found.

Synthetic turbulence imposed on the inlet boundary to match real-life conditions was
investigated. The impact of varying both length scales and turbulence intensity was in-
vestigated, showing minor impacts within the tested range. The key finding is that an
estimation of the length scales is suﬀicient, as the interval of 25 mm to 75 mm only showed
a −0.5% difference at most. Additionally, turbulence intensities of 0.5% and 1.5% resulted
in a difference of less than 0.1%, thus deemed negligible.
The addition of leg motion showed an increase in vorticities in the wake of the cyclist.
While the focus has been on developing and demonstrating the methodology, the isolated
turbulent effect due to the pedaling motion was not quantitatively determined but com-
pared through flow fields. Total Drag areas were obtained for pedaling simulations and
compared to static simulations of high and low drag positions. The isolated effect of pedal-
ing could be pursued by mapping the drag area throughout a crank revolution by multiple
static simulations. With static simulations serving as a reference, the isolated effect of
pedaling could be examined. The obtained dynamic average resulted in an intermediate
solution between the high and low drag positions, as expected. The static high drag posi-
tion resulted in an increased drag area of 16.8%, while the static low drag position showed
a decrease of 9.6% compared to the dynamic averaged simulation. Overall, the obtained
dynamic drag areas are low compared to the static low-drag drag simulations. This is due
to the fact, that mesh refinements have not been performed on the dynamic simulation
which results have been obtained from.
A brief study on the use of rotating wheels showed a significant decrease in drag area.
For the rotating wheel case, a local rotation rate was defined for each wheel, and the
bike was lifted from the ground by 20 mm, while the symmetry condition was replaced
with a moving wall as a road. The DES approach was used for resolving the flow and
showed a significant decrease in drag area, by 4.8%. This underscores the complexity and
impact that motions can have when simulating the unsteady flow of cyclists. As a static
simulation was performed with identical boundary conditions to the simulation of rotating
wheels, the isolated effect of rotating wheels was determined to be the 4.8% decrease.

Future work
• Addressing stability issues: The most vital improvement needed is resolving the
stability issue. The numerical methodology developed is very promising but limited
due to the unresolved stability issues. Resolving this enables reliable simulations
of dynamic leg movement over multiple cycles, offering faster solutions compared to
current methods. Future work consists of resolving these stability issues and refining
the mesh according to the findings from the static simulations.
• Isolated pedaling effect: By mapping multiple leg positions with static simu-
lations, a comparison can be made with the dynamic model, showing the isolated
turbulent effect of the pedaling motion.
• Addition of transition models: The impact of the addition of a transition model
has not been investigated in this project. For future work, this would be an inter-
esting field of study.
