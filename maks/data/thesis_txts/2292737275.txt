Summary (English)
This thesis aims to develop a new method of parameter estimation for the Ra-
diative Transfer Equations. The method is designed to use two images under a
controlled lighting environment to calculate the scattering and absorption coef-
ﬁcients, as well as the mean-scattering cosine. The presented method extends
the inverse adding-doubling method to use a single, diﬀusely lit integrating-
sphere and a camera instead of a light-sensor. This method was designed to be
faster than the inverse adding-doubling method, without sacriﬁcing the accuracy
and without requiring tedious amounts of sample preparation. Furthermore, it
should work with a large range of materials, ranging from optically thick to thin.
The method uses two images, one dominated by reﬂectance, and one with the
addition of transmittance to try and achieve it’s goal.
The method operates by ﬁrst taking two images of the sample, on diﬀerent
backgrounds. The images are then used as an input to the optimizing function,
which uses the adding-doubling method in the back-end to perform its calcu-
lations.
This optimization should ﬁnd the optimal parameters to create the
image. The method is then evaluated against measured and theoretical values,
and it’s accuracy is discussed.

Chapter 1
Introduction
When light is traveling through diﬀerent media (e.g. air, water, milk, skin),
it is often scattered and absorbed by the particles within it. This scattering
can happen multiple times before exiting the medium, and in combination with
absorption is responsible for the appearance of many objects in the world around
us, even though they are interactions on an atomic level.
Much work has gone into modeling this phenomena.
Chandrasekhar [1960]
formulated the Radiative Transfer Equation (RTE), which are currently used in
the analysis and simulation of these phenomena. These equations are based oﬀ
of a set of parameters, and much work has been done in order to measure these
parameters. Speciﬁcally, the scattering and absorption coeﬃcients are used, as
well as the scattering phase function. A brief introduction to the terms is given
below:
σs Known as the "absorption coeﬃcient" or "absorption cross section", this
property describes the amount of light which is absorbed (per unit dis-
tance) as light propagates through a medium.
σs Known as the "scattering coeﬃcient", or "scattering cross section", this
property describes the amount of light which is scattered (per unit dis-
tance) as light propagates through a medium.
==PAGE:14==
2
Introduction
p Known as the "phase function", this property describes the angular distribu-
tion of light when scattered. This is often denoted as p(θ), in which it
can be said to describe the amount of light scattering toward the angle θ
which is relative to the incident direction.
Speciﬁcally, absorption refers to the transformation of radiant energy into other
energy forms as it travels through the medium, while scattering refers to the
direction which the radiant energy is traveling. The phase function describes
the spatial distribution of the radiant energy being scattered. These coeﬃcients
are wavelength dependent.
1.1
Motivation
There are a wide range of methods used to acquire these parameters and work
has been done in many diﬀerent ﬁelds.
Two ﬁelds which have done a large
amount of research into the subject are rendering and medical analysis. Many
methods currently used often rely on matching the parameters through the
use of optimization functions, while some use numerical approaches involving
approximations.
For optimization approaches, there are often strict sample
design procedures which must be adhered to. Furthermore, most approaches
aren’t currently designed with spectral parameters in mind, though they can
possibly be extended to work across spectra.
1.1.1
Problem Statement
Existing methods of measuring the scattering properties of materials are often
limited due to their required time and the range of materials with which they
work. Some rely on multiple images with varied lighting conditions, which can
be time consuming both during acquisition and due to the precision for setting
up samples. Other methods require careful sample design, which means multiple
attempts may need to be made to get the sample to the right dilution needed.
Methods which rely on diﬀusion approximations only work well for optically
thick materials, and lose accuracy when dealing with other media.
Scattering properties are wavelength speciﬁc, and are traditionally recorded as
RGB values. With the onset of spectral-rendering and spectral-imaging tech-
niques, some methods become slow, or cannot handle the high variation of the
coeﬃcients that are presented across the spectrum.
==PAGE:15==
1.2 Goal
3
Because of the problems mentioned above, current methods are not suitable
for lab-work, where one might want to quickly measure the optical properties
of many samples. The amount of preparation and time spent on each sample
quickly becomes cumbersome. Furthermore, some of the methods limit the scope
of materials which can be measured, which means multiple methods might have
to be combined in trial and error in order to acquire a single result. Therefore,
a solution should be designed which aims to solve some of the issue present in
current approaches. The solution should provide a relatively quick method of
multi-wavelength image acquisition for the samples, and should be accurate for
a wide range of materials, i.e., it should not be limited to optically thick or
thin materials. The amount of time which is required to spend on designing the
samples so they ﬁt the method should be minimal in order to help streamline
the processing.
1.2
Goal
The goal of this project is therefore to provide a solution to the problem of
determining optical properties which is both fast, and accurate. The method
should not be restricted to certain types of materials and should require as few
measurements to be made as possible. These requirements have been broken
down to the following broad categories:
Acquisition Speed The method proposed should be fast to use.
Ease of Sample Preparation The method proposed should be easy to pre-
pare.
Accuracy and Precision The method proposed should be accurate across a
wide range of materials.
1.2.1
Acquisition Speed
The sampling process should be relatively eﬃcient. It is preferable to use as
few images as possible when measuring the sample, as certain properties may
change with temperature. For example, the pH may be temperature dependent,
and may have an eﬀect on the proteins in the sample. Furthermore, it would be
preferable to be able to sample a large number of wavelengths in a short period
of time.

The process should aim to allow a large number of samples to be processed
in volume. It should not require a large amount of set-up time per sample.
However, the calculation process may take more time if necessary because it
does not have a direct impact on the sampling process itself.
1.2.2
Ease of Sample Preparation
In line with the speed, the samples should also be relatively easy to prepare and
not require large and complicated amounts of set-up. Sampling should not be a
trial-and-error procedure, and should not require multiple iterations to obtain
a correct sample. Sampling should be easily repeatable and veriﬁable.
1.2.3
Accuracy and Precision
The method should produce accurate results. This can be measured by compar-
ing the samples to known or calculated values. The method should converge and
return the same results given the same input. Furthermore, the method should
not be limited to certain materials. It should not be limited to optically-thick
or -thin media. The method should work for visible wavelengths.

Chapter 2
Related Work
There has been a lot of work done within the ﬁeld of inverting the Radiative
Transfer Equations. Many approaches take vastly diﬀerent methods and use
approximations to aid in their execution.
A sampling of the methods used
within the ﬁeld will be discussed in the sections that follow, in order to give a
brief overview of current works.
One approach is to limit the observed image to only single-scattering events
[Narasimhan et al., 2006]. The term single-scattering refers to the light only
having one scattering event prior to exiting the medium. This is opposed to
multiple-scattering, where the light scatters multiple times prior to exiting.
Therefore, care must be taken when making the samples, as to try and force the
dominance of single scattering events. In practice, this is done by diluting the
sample. This method will be described a more fully in section 2.1.
Another approach is to use multiple lighting setups across multiple images to try
and divide the sample into single- and multiple-scattering events [Mukaigawa
et al., 2010, 2012]. These methods use several images, which are combined in
order to separate between single and multiple scattering events.
The method presented by Gkioulekas et al.
in 2013 which uses single step
propagation and material dictionaries for calculating the light ﬁelds.
These
material dictionaries include specialized phase functions which better represent

the true phase functions than approximations like the Henyey-Greenstein phase
function. This dictionary is used in a minimization problem using Levenberg-
Marquadt for the gradient descent.
The Inverse-Adding-Doubling method [Prahl, 1988, 1995, 2011, Prahl et al.,
1993] uses a set of mathematically derived rules to determine how much light is
reﬂected and transmitted given a set of parameters. This rule is then combined
with the simplex method (as the others are used in combination with optimiza-

Contrary to optimization approaches, several methods use the diﬀusion approxi-
mation [Ishimaru, 1978] in order to numerically calculate a "reduced" scattering
coeﬃcient [Kienle and Steiner, 1994, Farrell, 1992, Forster et al., 2001]. This
method is commonly seen within the medical ﬁeld to derive diﬀerent tissue
properties.
2.1
Dilution Methods
Dilution methods attempt to remove multiple scattering from the equations.
This greatly simpliﬁes the calculations which need to be made. In the article
by Narasimhan et al. from 2006, a large tank with glass sides is ﬁlled with
the diluted liquid. A single glass bulb sits inside the tank on one of the sides.
The idea behind this method is that when the liquid is suﬃciently diluted, only
a single scattering event will happen before the light hits the camera. Using
this information, the distance the light has traveled from the source can be
used to calculate the attenuation, and the inclusion of multiple angles can help
determine the mean scattering angle.

2.3
Material Dictionaries
Gkioulekas et al. converts the Radiative Transfer Equations to an Operator-
Theoretic formulation and uses this formulation to develop a derivative which
can be used by optimization algorithms. In speciﬁc, the Levenberg-Marquadt
algorithm is used to minimize.
This method also adds in the inclusion of a
more complex phase function. This phase function represents a piece-wise linear
function over the angle, θ. This phase function is still assumed to be cylindrically
symmetric, but allows a better ﬁt for cases that Henyey-Greenstein, or more
specialized phase functions are not known. It can be viewed as a generalized,
yet customizable approach to phase functions.
This method requires a precise acquisition setup, and needs accurate calibra-
tion. Furthermore, while it can be used for various geometries, but it produces
highly accurate results, and provides a large improvement for materials not well
approximated by the Henyey-Greenstein phase function.
2.4
Inverse Adding-Doubling
Prahl presented inverse adding-doubling in 1988. This method uses a set of inte-
grating spheres to take accurate measurements of the total transmitted and re-
ﬂected light from a sample. This method then uses the adding-doubling method
which was described earlier in section 3.3. This method was developed for mea-
suring tissue samples, which are sandwiched between two glass slides. It uses the
Nelder-Mead downhill simplex method to ﬁnd the parameters which minimize
the error.
This method is accurate for a wide range of materials, and is not limited to op-
tically thick or thin materials. This method uses carefully calibrated integrating
spheres to collect and infer the total reﬂection and scattering from the sample,
which is illuminated using a collimated beam.
2.5
Diﬀusion Methods
The diﬀusion approximation was used by Jensen and Marschner to calculate the
proposed the Bidirectional Scattering Surface Distribution Function (BSSRDF).
This method assumes that materials are optically-thick, i.e. σs ≪σa Jensen
and Marschner [2001]. By making this assumption, the underlying equations

3.1
Radiative Transfer Equation
The Radiative Transfer Equation comes from the work of Chandrasekhar, and
provides the basis for much of the ﬁeld today.
The equation describes how
electromagnetic radiation is propagated through a medium.
Furthermore, it
characterizes the processes of scattering and absorption. It is often used for
light transport and ss deﬁned below as:

3.3
Adding-Doubling Method
The doubling method was introduced by ?, as a way of solving the radiative
transport equation in slab geometry. The method assumes that you already
know the reﬂection and transmission of a thin slab layer, and then provides a
method of calculating the reﬂection and transmission properties for a slab which
is twice the size. The adding method extends the doubling method by allowing
two dissimilar slabs to be added to each other.
The doubling method begins by operating on a thin layer of homogeneous media.
It assumes that you know the reﬂection and transmission operators for this thin
layer. The layer is then stacked on itself and essentially doubled in size. This
process can be repeated to calculate layers of any arbitrary size. The adding
method is an extension of this, and allows for other arbitrary layers to be added.
These layers can represent boundaries between diﬀerent interfaces, or diﬀerent
media altogether.

3.4
Optimization
The parameters we are searching for are input to the Radiative Transfer Equa-
tion mentioned earlier in section 3.1 as well as to the Henyey-Greenstein phase
function described in section 3.2. The process of ﬁnding these values given the
output is known as inversion.
The search for the parameters going into the
Radiative Transfer Equation, σs, σa, and g, would be the inversion. There are
several methods for solving inversion problems. Optimization tries to solve the
inverse problem by minimizing the error between the predicted values and their
measured results.
Optimization is the applied mathematical process of searching for an optimal
value [Nocedal and Wright, 2006, Nielsen and Madsen, 2010]. It can be used to
ﬁnd the roots of a function, or to ﬁnd the location of local and global minima.
The methods used in this paper employ several diﬀerent forms of optimization.
These boil down to the following methods:
3.4.1
Newton’s Method
Newton’s Method of root-ﬁnding is an iterative approach which improves an
approximation of the root for a real-valued continuous function repeatedly. It
does this by using the functions value and derivative at the guess to identify the
next guess. Speciﬁcally, this is done as:
xn+1 = xn −f(xn)
f ′(xn)
(3.38)
where x0 would be the initial guess, f represents the function, and f ′ represents
the derivative. This method converges quadratically when the initial guess is
suﬃciently close to the root.
The search is terminated when x0 is within a
tolerated range of the root.

3.4.2
Bisection Method
Bisection Method searches for a root of a real-valued continuous function by it-
eratively improving a bracketing interval around the root. A bracketing interval
is an interval [a, b] in which the evaluation f(a) and f(b) have diﬀerent signs.
This method repeatedly searches for the root by selecting a new point at the
center of the bracket, which will be denoted c. If sign(f(c)) = sign(f(a)), the
point a is replaced with the new point c. If this does not hold true, than the
inverse case must hold true, sign(f(c)) = sign(f(b)), and b is replaced with the
value of c. This simple process can be repeated until the bracketing interval
gets suﬃciently small. This method converges linearly towards a root.

Design Outline
The Inverse Adding-Doublign (IAD) method was chosen as a starting point for
this design. The IAD method is considered to have high accuracy and is not
limited to a certain range of materials like some other methods. The AD method
was designed to be a "gold standard" [Prahl, 1995]. However, in its current
state, the IAD process is unwieldy. It requires several images to be taken, and
somewhat complex sampling setups involving a number of integrating spheres

design.

a controlled lighting environment.

the second measurement is made with a black layer beneath the sample. The
material chosen for the black layer was electrical tape.

The inverse scattering problem requires careful planning to enforce uniqueness
of solutions. The design of the samples must take this into account in order
to locate a single solution rather than a set. Given the VideometerLab 3 de-
vice, only diﬀuse lighting is available. Therefore, in order to try and solve the
uniqueness constraint, the method uses two diﬀerent backgrounds underneath
the sample to try and provide varied lighting situations.
The ﬁrst background is a mirror. This background should allow the transmission
of light through the medium to be aimed back towards the camera. Modern
mirrors have high reﬂectivity, and can easily be modeled by specular reﬂection.
This was used in conjunction with a black background. The black background
chosen was electrical tape. This was chosen for ease of use, availability. The
index of refraction for electrical tape is n = 1.52 + i0.01, as described in Liu
and Samuels [2001]. The black background, unlike the mirror, should essentially
limit the sample to reﬂected light. This, in conjunction with the combination
of transmission and reﬂection provided by the mirror, provides the necessary
inputs to the inverse adding-doubling problem.

4.3
Parameter Estimation
The inverse adding-doubling method serves as a base for the solver used in this
method. However, some changes were made to the process. The design decisions
are listed in the following sections.
The ﬁrst section will give a rehash of what the inverse-adding doubling method,
which was brieﬂy mentioned in the section on related work (2). It will present the
basic algorithm followed by details of the implementation steps for the process.
Which quadrature angles should be used during calculations will be discussed,
and then a brief discussion of how to model the boundary layers is presented.
Afterwards, the Nelder-Mead optimization approach to ﬁnding the parameters is
described. The section is concluded with a concise description of the algorithm.
4.3.1
Inverse Adding-Doubling Method
The inverse adding-doubling method is an optimization based approach to ﬁnd-
ing the parameters for the scattering sample. It relies on the Adding-Doubling
process to perform it’s evaluations. The procedure can be described as: using the
Nelder-Mead optimization method in combination with the Adding-Doubling
method for all function evaluations which need to take place.
The inverse adding-doubling method attempts to match calculated total re-
ﬂectance and the calculated total transmittance to the measured total reﬂectance
and measured total transmittance respectively. The Nelder-Mead method only
considers a one-dimensional error-function, so the sum of relative-errors is used
for this calculation.
The measured total transmittance and reﬂectance require many integrating
sphere measurements to acquire, but provide accurate parameters. The method
presented here attempts to match a diﬀerent set of measurements, and hopes to
achieve similar results.
4.3.2
Adding-Doubling of Slabs
The method tries to match the values measured earlier in section 4.2.2. These
measurements that will be matched are the ratio of outgoing radiance for the
black background, and the ratio of outgoing radiance for the mirrored back-
ground.

34

When both samples are of the same thickness and dilution, only one homo-
geneous slab needs to be calculated for the current set of parameters being
evaluated. The adding-doubling method therefore builds a single layer from the
parameters, which it doubles until the proper layer size is reached. This layer is
then used twice for the diﬀerent boundary settings. One set of boundaries will
produce the mirror background’s result, and the other will produce the black
background’s result. The boundary layers used are discussed later, in section
4.3.4. A ﬁnal integration is performed to get the outgoing radiance distribution
as was deﬁned earlier and this value will be used to calculate the error for the
given parameters. This error function and the ﬁnal integration are discussed
later, in section 4.3.5.

4.3.4
Boundary Layers
There are three diﬀerent boundary layers used by this method. Two of these
boundaries are standard and follows the derivations from the works of Prahl
[2011], which was discussed earlier in section 3.3.6. One of the boundaries is
implemented as a custom layer.
The interface between air and the sample is used as the top boundary on both
slabs. The bottom boundary diﬀers to match the mirror and black surfaces. The
black surface was modeled similarly to the top layer, using the Fresnel reﬂectance
found by using the previously stated index of refraction (n = 1.52+i0.01). Any
light which transmits into this layer is ignored, and only specular reﬂectance is
considered.
The mirrored surface is done somewhat diﬀerently. Instead of calculating indi-
vidual Fresnel reﬂectance terms, the mirror is assumed to be a perfect reﬂector,
and a constant Fresnel term of 1 is used.

Summary
The method presented provides an extension of the inverse adding-doubling
method. The inverse adding-doubling method works well for a wide range of
materials, and this method hopes to maintain those beneﬁts. The Videometer-
Lab 3 provides boosts to sampling eﬃciency, but means the experiment design
has to be run diﬀerently. The method can be reduced to the following steps:
Prepare Device: The VideometerLab 3 device needs to be warmed up and

calibrated prior to use, the calibration process takes under a minute and
involves reference targets.Analytik [2015]
Prepare Samples: The samples need to be prepared so that there is a visible
diﬀerence between samples on the mirrored and black backgrounds.
Measure Samples: The samples are placed into the VideometerLab 3 and
measured. Accurate measurements can be achieved in under 6 seconds.
Transform Measurements: The measurements are returned from the Videome-
terLab 3 as image ﬁles. Some work needs to be done to extract the diﬀerent
image intensities from the ﬁle.
Parameter Calculation: The modiﬁed inverse adding-doubling method is run
on the measured data.
Transform Parameters: Optionally, if the sample was diluted, transform the
properties to obtain the correct coeﬃcients for the full concentration.

In order to use the parameters from the results chapter, they need to be scaled
to their proper concentrations in order to be compared to other known readings.
The relationship between values in the charts, as well as the convergence section
does make it appear that the method converges towards a single solution given
the parameters at least.
The inverse adding-doubling method attempts to solve for only two parameters
using the Nelder-Mead method. It uses another measurement to solve for the
third parameter. This method attempted to avoid doing that, but the duplicity
of solutions non-uniqueness can be seen just by looking at the fact that there
are both an isotropic and (relatively) anisotropic solution to the measurements
for milk, both with very low relative errors.

7.3
Possible Sources of Error
There are several possible sources of error in this method. One error could be
that the lighting distribution is not as even as expected. This can be seen in
ﬁgure 6.3. The area towards the center is not lit the same as the other parts of
the liquid. Although these artifacts were sampled around when selecting zones
to average, they still contribute to a loss of lighting, and a breakdown to the
underlying premise of diﬀuse irradiance.
Another possible source of error is the incomplete modeling of the black-tape
boundary. Only specular reﬂection was modeled, and perhaps the full reﬂection
operator would be needed.
The implementation used for the full adding-doubling method was tested against
the original source, and so it is not expected to be a code error. The Nelder-
Mead simplex solver was also tested extensively against the Rosenbrock and
Himmelblau functions, and they do work for that.
Furthermore, the solver
appears to behave well here anyway, as it does minimize the error. The fact
that the method behaves well while converging towards solutions is unfortunate
given that it is not the correct solution. The error may lay with one of the
extensions to the method perhaps.
Several normalizing factors were tried on Iin and Iref on recommendation to
see if they would improve the search, but they did not provide any measurable
beneﬁt.
7.3.1
Conclusion
An extension to the adding doubling method was presented as a faster alterna-
tive. Overall, the method does not appear to work in it’s current form. Drastic
changes would need to be made to get it to a workable point using the equip-
ment. This error may not be insurmountable, but it is large in it’s current state.
It appears that by trying to speed up the measurement process used for inverse
adding-doubling has taken away from the method’s accuracy, which was one of
it’s most touted features.
Going forward, more work needs to be done to try and isolate where exactly
the error lies. The implementation itself appears to be solid, but perhaps an
error was made in derivation along the way which was missed. General improve-
ments could be made by using measured reﬂectance functions for the bottom
boundary layers. There appear to be some issues with the diﬀuse lighting in

Of the original goals, two out of three were met. The method is fast, and easy
to use. However, the very important goal of accuracy and precision, was not
achieved.
