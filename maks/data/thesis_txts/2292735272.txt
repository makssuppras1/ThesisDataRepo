Abstract
In a technological era where all kind of product functions are combined in smaller and more efﬁcient
products, new materials are introduced in the market helping this development. The increasing performance
of semiconductor materials is the driving factor behind these technological innovations. These new semi-
conductor materials can help optimizing an audio chain creating smaller and more efﬁcient products. A
smaller ampliﬁer with the same output power and audio performance together with a higher efﬁciency is the
driving factor for ampliﬁer development. The question arise if a smaller and more efﬁcient ampliﬁers can be
made using new semiconductor materials without sacriﬁcing audio quality in terms of distortion, noise and
bandwidth.
Enhancement mode Gallium-Nitride Field Effect Transistors (eGaN-FETS) are getting more and more
attention in power electronics applications. Their superior switching behaviour, due to a very small gate
capacitance and low on-state resistance, can result in much better performance than MOSFETs. This increase
of switching performance make these devices the next big thing in power electronics applications. This also
includes switch-mode audio power ampliﬁers. Switch-mode audio power ampliﬁers use the same switching
behaviour as switch-mode power electronics to amplify an audio signal to be played over a loudspeaker.
This raises a research question to be answered: "Can eGaN-FETs be used to build a higher power density
ampliﬁer with a higher efﬁciency than MOSFET technology without compromising audio performance?"
The superior performance of eGaN-FET compared to MOSFETs can result in an equally performing
ampliﬁer with a higher power density and a higher efﬁciency. This means a comparable ampliﬁer on audio
speciﬁcations like noise, distortion and bandwidth can be made using eGaN-FETs. At the same time the
total volume of the ampliﬁer decreases and the efﬁciency of the ampliﬁer rises.
The conducted research ﬁnds solutions in eGaN-FET applications in switch-mode audio power ampliﬁers.
An ampliﬁer with a higher switching frequency can be made using eGaN-FETs, resulting in a higher
performance ampliﬁer with smaller physical dimensions. A clocked system and a self-oscillating topology
are used to evaluate an eGaN-FET ampliﬁer performance and efﬁciency. Also, a comparative study is
made between an eGaN-FET power stage and one equipped with MOSFETs. The gain in performance and
efﬁciency of using eGaN-FETs instead of MOSFETs is researched. Tried is to ﬁnd out which material is
preferred when designing a switch-mode audio ampliﬁer power stage. Since the topic of research is quite
new, new research questions arise with the writing of this thesis. Most of them are answered- some require
more research.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Modern technology reaches towards smaller and more efﬁcient electronic designs for all kind of applica-
tions. Audio ampliﬁers have been under huge development towards efﬁciency over the last decades with
the introduction of switch-mode power ampliﬁers [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]. These ampliﬁers beneﬁt from a switching
behaviour as is seen in switch-mode power supplies. Effectively, taking an average of a high frequency
pulsed signal, is the driving technology in such designs.
1.1
Class D ampliﬁcation
Switch-mode audio power ampliﬁcation modulates the audio input signal [6] by comparing it to a high
frequency triangular carrier waveform using a comparator (Fig. 1.1). The two-state output is a Pulse Width
Modulated (PWM) signal, where the width of each pulse in the pulse-train is directly proportional to the
input audio information. This PWM signal is ampliﬁed by switching between the power supply rails of an
ampliﬁer. This switching will be handled by active devices in the designed ampliﬁer. The active devices
operate either in an on- or an off-state. After the switching stage the audio signal is reconstructed by a
demodulation ﬁlter. Ampliﬁers using this switching topology are also known as class D ampliﬁers. The
carrier signal can either be generated externally or using feedback.
The beneﬁt from the on- and off-state of the active elements is that there is either no voltage across the
device in the on state, or no current ﬂowing through the device in the off state. Both result in no dissipated
power in the active element.
Figure 1.1: A topological review on class D ampliﬁcation.
3
==PAGE:30==

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Gallium-Nitride Field Effect Transistors (GaN-FETs) are getting more attention due to their excellent
high-frequency switching behaviour. In a switch-mode power electronics perspective this means that a
power-supply or switch-mode audio ampliﬁer can operate on a higher switching frequency decreasing
switching losses and increasing power density of switch-mode applications. For audio ampliﬁer applications
this means a higher power ampliﬁer can be made measuring lower dimensions. The goal is to achieve
a smaller and more efﬁcient ampliﬁer without sacriﬁcing audio quality in terms of distortion, noise and
bandwidth.
1.2
The Gallium-Nitride Field Effect Transistor (GaN-FET)
Gallium-Nitride (GaN ) material has a higher break-down ﬁeld EgV (measured in mega volts per
centimetre [MV cm≠1]) than a silicon equivalent semiconductor[7]. This means that when two terminals,
with GaN material in between, can be placed 1 cm apart from each other. A higher voltage across these
terminals in this state is required to generate an avalanche. This also means a separation between two
contacts can be made smaller when a voltage difference is required between these terminals. Smaller devices
are directly beneﬁcial towards capacitance included. Smaller parasitic capacitances occur when devices are
getting smaller. This is highly beneﬁcial in switch-mode applications.
Also the electron mobility of GaN is higher than silicon causing electrons to move more freely inside
the material. This is beneﬁcial for designing switch-mode power electronics where the on-state resistance
RDSON will be lower and device dimensions decrease.
To use these characteristics of GaN material for transistor applications, a layer of Aluminium-Gallium-
Nitride (AlGaN) can be added on top of the GaN material as pictured in Figure 1.2. The GaN material can
be applied on top of existing silicon semiconductor material.
Figure 1.2: Cross section of a GaN-FET. Figure from [7]
==PAGE:31==
1.3. THE ENHANCEMENT-MODE GAN-FETS (EGAN-FET)
5
Gallium-Nitride has a piezoelectric nature, which means that when a piece of GaN is strained, a small
voltage is produced. By adding a small layer of AlGaN a large strain over a small distance can be achieved
[8]. By applying an AlGaN layer on top of the GaN material layer, a straining is obtained creating an electric
ﬁeld of electrons at the interface between the two materials. These electrons form a two dimensional electric
gas (2DEG) which can conduct large amounts of current. Adding a negative voltage on the AlGaN layer
depletes the 2DEG where conduction disappears. Using GaN material in such a way, a depletion mode
device is obtained which:
• has a very high voltage blocking capability due to the high break-down ﬁeld,
• low on resistance and due to high electron mobility
• is very fast due to small physical dimensions
1.3
The enhancement-mode GaN-FETs (eGaN-FET)
In 2012 Efﬁcient Power Conversion Corporation introduced the enhancement-mode GaN-FET (eGaN-
FET, [7] pictured in Figure 1.3). These eGaN-FETs can, due to modiﬁcation in its structure towards
GaN-FETs, switch on when a positive gate-source voltage is applied. The device switches off when no
voltage is applied. A ﬁeld plate connected to the source is added to deplete the 2DEG when no voltage
is applied at the gate. A positive voltage at the gate completes the 2DEG layer resulting in a conductive
channel.
Figure 1.3: Cross section of a enhancement mode GaN-FET (eGaN-FET). Figure from [7]
Enhancement-mode GaN-FETs do no longer require a negative turn-off voltage, in which they behave
more alike widely used silicon type Metal Oxide Semiconductor Field Effect Transistor (MOSFET) . Also, in
start-up of a system, no negative voltage is required to deplete the 2DEG layer, resulting in an non-conductive
channel when a application is started. This means no shoot-through events are present when a circuit with
eGaN-FETs is started, since the applied gate voltage is 0 V at start-up.
==PAGE:32==
6
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
However, eGaN-FETs come with some drawbacks that have to be taken in consideration when designing
a eGaN-FET power stage. Care must be taken to prevent damaging a eGaN-FET:
• The sensitive gate of an eGaN-FET break down with a gate-source voltage VGS larger than 6 V. This
means care has to be taken in the eGaN-FET driver to not exceed this driving voltage limit. This
voltage is lower than silicon counterparts.
• A reverse conduction path is available in eGaN-FETs. This path has however a higher forward voltage
drop than is usual for MOSFETs. This higher voltage drop can increase losses during a reverse
conduction time interval.
• Small package makes prototyping and manufacturing more difﬁcult but reduces gate capacitance, stray
inductance and on-state resistance at the same time.
• Fast switching can generate high speed voltage transients which may cause signiﬁcant switching
noises and unwanted transient behaviour. When using eGaN-FETs, layout becomes more important.
1.4
The ampliﬁer
The ampliﬁer can be made using several topologies. For instance a full-bridge or a half-bridge switching
topology in the power stage. The switching PWM signal can be generated using a clocked solution or
with a self-oscillating control loop in the ampliﬁer feedback. Previous research concentrated on a clocked
system using eGaN-FETs [9, 10]. Self-oscillating systems using eGaN-FETs do not require a external clock
source where the carrier is generated using feedback. This can save area on the Printed Circuit Board (PCB)
resulting in a higher power density ampliﬁer. A self-oscillating system can be either a hysteresis-type or a
phase-shift-type oscillator. Either way, a common analysis of the two is available [11].
The goal of this research is to see whether eGaN-FETs allow building efﬁcient and high power density
ampliﬁers. A high power density can be reached by increasing the switching frequency of a switch-mode
power converter. The higher switching frequency allows the use of smaller output ﬁlter component values.
In other words the cut-off frequency of the output ﬁlter can be placed at a higher frequency. This allows
the use of smaller ﬁlter components. Also eGaN-FET packages are much smaller compared to regular
MOSFETs. The performance of these FETs will be evaluated for losses and thermal performance of the
power stage. A high power density self-oscillating ampliﬁer will be designed, build and tested to achieve
high performance and efﬁciency using a high switching frequency. This can be summarized in a research
question: "Can eGaN-FETs be used to build a higher power density ampliﬁer with a higher efﬁciency than
MOSFET technology without compromising audio performance?"

2.2
Delay
Delay in a self-oscillating system can affect the system in two ways. One way in a phase-shift oscillating
type is where the delay is used to push the phase beyond the ≠180° point fulﬁlling the Barkhausen oscillation
criteria. In this way one can beneﬁt from a delay through the modulation chain since it behaves as a linear
phase shift. In a hysteresis type oscillator the delay through the loop can actually introduce a distortion or
offset in the carrier signal making the total ampliﬁer behave non-linear which is discussed in Chapter 4. For
making a versatile power stage, delay is tried to be kept to a minimum. This makes further implementation
of a modulation scheme easier to control without adding severe distortion sources.
The goal set for a high switching frequency demands very fast control over the switching node. It is
desired to have as little delay as possible throughout the power stage module to ensure stable control at all
times. For traditional lower switching frequencies one could get away by having a little more delay in the
loop, where high switching frequency design cannot tolerate this. For modulation purposes explained in
Chapter 4, delay is aimed to be as small as possible to eliminate unwanted behaviour. Also for the control
loop delay td cannot be large. A delay results in a linear phase shift which can cause instability issues when
this is not taken into account. For all selected components, the delay requirements have been set very tight.
This means low propagation delay has ﬁrst priority selecting comparators, logic gates, gate drivers and
others.

3.3.1
The power stage switching cycle
Using ideal switching devices would mean no losses are present during the conductive or non-conductive
state, or the time period in between. However, just as silicon based MOSFETs, eGaN-FETs are not perfect.
This means the eGaN-FETs switching cycle is inﬂuenced by parasitic components [12].
==PAGE:41==
3.3. THE EGAN-FET POWER SWITCHES
15
Figure 3.3: The eGaN-FET power stage schematic, including output capacitance parasitics.
In a half-bridge topology as is used in this research (Fig. 3.3), two eGaN-FETs are stacked in a totem pole
conﬁguration with a common switch-node connection. An inductor is placed in between the switch-node
and the output voltage resulting in a current running through this inductor, ﬂowing into the ﬁlter capacitor.
The inductor and capacitor form the output ﬁlter of the ampliﬁer,which will be discussed in Section 3.4.
A current is ﬂowing through the output ﬁlter inductor, and therefore the eGaN-FETs. This output current
is depended on the output voltage of the ampliﬁer and the timing of the switching cycle. Parasitic output
capacitance of the eGaN-FETs (COSSQ1 and COSSQ2 ) are also depicted.
3.3.2
Switching cycle topologies
The switching can be done in 4 different topologies, where current and voltage waveforms can differ:
• Hard Switching - A generated dead-time interval between the top eGaN-FET (Q1) turning off and
the bottom eGaN-FET turning on (Q2), or the other way around, can be reduced to near 0 s. This
means the voltage across one of the eGaN-FETs is twice the supply rail voltage and the ripple current
is at its highest point. Where both current and voltage are high during the switching transient, energy
is dissipated which can generate high losses. Effectively, in a switching cycle expressed in Fig. 3.4,
hard switching tries to jump from the state in Fig. 3.4a to the state in Fig. 3.4d.
• Reduced voltage hard switching - By introducing a small time interval (dead-time) between the two
eGaN-FET switching transients, the output capacitance of the eGaN-FETs (COSSQ1 and COSSQ2) can
be charged/discharged. This is done by the current ﬂowing in the inductor as depicted in Fig. 3.4b and
Fig. 3.4f. By letting the voltage rise/fall during this state (Fig. 3.4b) a lower voltage will be present
across Q2 when the device is turning on in the next state (Fig. 3.4d). The reverse conduction state
expressed in Fig. 3.4c and Fig. 3.4g is not present in this switching cycle topology.
==PAGE:42==
16
CHAPTER 3. THE POWER STAGE MODULE
• Zero voltage switching - When the generated dead-time is long enough to charge or discharge the
parasitic output capacitances of the eGaN-FETs completely, there is no longer a voltage present
across the eGaN-FET that will turn on. When no voltage is present, no power is dissipated during the
turning on transient of the eGaN-FET so there are no losses. When Zero Voltage Switching (ZVS )
is performed, the states depicted in Fig. 3.4c and Fig. 3.4g are not present. Timing is critical in this
operation. If the generated dead-time is too long, the current will charge/discharge further than the
supply rail voltages where a reverse conduction of the eGaN-FETs is reached (Fig. 3.4c and Fig. 3.4g).
During a reverse conduction of the eGaN-FETs, the peak of the ripple current is ﬂowing in the reverse
direction through the eGaN-FET. This reverse conduction path has a voltage drop of around 1.8 V [13]
which is higher than silicon based MOSFETs. This means that during the reverse conduction time
interval more power is dissipated in the eGaN-FETs and therefore timing of the power stage switching
becomes critical. The eGaN-FET has no reverse recovery charge QRR, meaning that leaving the
reverse conduction state does not involve any losses.
• A forth switching topology is current depended switching where the switching transient is timed to be
performed where the inductor current is 0 A (Zero Current Switching, ZCS). Class D ampliﬁers can
not beneﬁt from this topology where the inductor current might never reach 0 A during a switching
cycle because the output current is dynamic with the audio input signal.
A analysis in an idle state can be made where the output voltage Vout = 0 V. At this operation condition
the duty cycle (or modulation index ) is equal to 50 % (or M = 0). The circuits and waveforms for these

A class D ampliﬁer uses hard switching, reduced voltage hard switching and zero voltage switching
topologies throughout a full sinusoidal output voltage swing. When the ampliﬁer is operating in idle
condition (or output voltage close to 0 V) only the ripple current ﬂows through the eGaN-FETs where idle
losses can be optimized using Zero Voltage Switching. When output current increases with a larger output
voltage, the top eGaN-FET COSS charging/discharging time is reduced. This is due to a larger available
current compared to solely the ripple current. The bottom eGaN-FET will enter a reverse conduction state
before it will turn on. However, when the bottom eGaN-FET is turning off, the COSS capacitances are
not charged/discharged because the output current remains positive. This forces the bottom eGaN-FET in
reverse conduction until the top eGaN-FET is turning on. Throughout a sinusoidal output current, with a
peak current higher than the ripple current, all hard switching, reduced voltage switching and zero voltage
switching topologies are present.

3.3.4
Switching losses
Since an ampliﬁer has a dynamic output current, no static operation point is available. The ampliﬁer
switching losses can only be approximated for idle and full output power, but will vary in between together
with the output current. The ampliﬁer could be optimized after, to generate the lowest losses in idle operation,
but where the output voltage and current are variable, the losses are as well. For an idle operation the
switching frequency is the highest for a self-oscillating system, which will be explained in Chapter 4. In this
case a switching frequency of 2 MHz is chosen. The losses generated by the eGaN-FETs can be calculated
using the same approach as for MOSFETs. Generating the lowest losses in idle operation can be done
by switching the moment where there is no voltage across the eGaN-FET, also known as Zero Voltage
Switching (ZVS).
ZVS can be done by accurate timing of the transient from one eGaN-FET turning off and the other
eGaN-FET turning on. The current in the inductor has a certain inertia which draws charge from (or charges)
the output capacitance of the eGaN-FETs (COSS). The current operates as an integrator of the voltage
applied across is, which results in this inertia. By tuning the inductor ripple current that draws this charge
and the timing between one eGaN-FET turning off and the other turning on ZVS can be achieved. A smaller
output inductance has a higher ripple current that can decrease the required dead-time. However, a higher
ripple current means more dissipation during the conduction time period due to the on-state resistance of
the eGaN-FET. For an idle operation the effect of the inductance and generated dead-time on the switching
losses can be studied.

High frequency behaviour
Since the switching frequency of the ampliﬁer is much higher than the conventional switching frequencies
new effects may occur when selecting an inductor or capacitor for an output ﬁlter. At high frequencies
parasitic components can have a large effect on the component performance, where inductors and capacitors
are no different. An inductor is made by placing windings of copper wire around an iron powder or ferrite
material core, creating a magnetic ﬁeld when a current ﬂows through the windings. This also means a small
capacitance between inductor windings can be present where two conductors are placed in close proximity
with an isolation layer in between. A voltage difference across the two windings/turns inherently means a
capacitance is created as is depicted in Fig. 3.20 and 3.21a [23].

at its self resonance frequency. For frequencies higher than this self-resonant frequency the inductor starts to

the plots only give an indication of the self-resonance effect and do not coincide with actual implemented

3.9
Level shifter
A comparator compares signals with a reference to ground, where also the output voltages of the
comparator will have the same reference. The gate driver will desire a input voltage between ≠40 V and
≠35 V since it is referenced around the negative supply rail. A shift in voltage level has to be made without
losing information in the pulse width modulated signal.
There are several topologies to build a level shifter, where a series capacitance between the two domains is
the easiest solution. However a series capacitance creates a high pass ﬁlter where low frequency information
(original audio signal in the carrier) will be ﬁltered out. Therefore two ways of level shifting will be tested, a
full signal transistor based level shifter and an edge-triggered logic level shifter.
Biasing a small-signal PNP type transistor in its linear region can make the transistor act very fast. As
seen in Figure: 3.23 the transistor Q1 is biased with the base tied to ground and with R1 to the positive low
voltage rail creating an emitter current. This current is modulated by the comparator output in series with
R2 carrying the PWM signal. At the collector of Q1 R3 is placed converting the modulated current into a
voltage again that can be picked up by the logic gates.
Figure 3.23: Implemented PNP-transistor based level-shifter

The PNP-transistor based level shifter consumes an idle current, which generates excessive losses. A
more energy efﬁcient approach can be made using an edge-triggered level shifter which uses logic gates
instead as is depicted Fig. 3.24. The PWM signal from the comparator comes in through capacitor C1
to block DC voltages. The transient will be fast since high frequency content can pass through, but low
frequency content is ﬁltered. This is the reason placing just a capacitor is not sufﬁcient since the high
frequency PWM signal contains low frequency audio information which would be ﬁltered out. Each transient
in the PWM signal generates a spike on node VX, which is biased around the trigger voltage of the used
logic gates. R3 and C2 delay this spike slightly where two different states are present on the XOR gate.
Each time different states are present the XOR gate gives out a high signal. This means the PWM signal is
now translated into a pulsed signal. Translating this pulse signal back to a PWM signal can be done using a
D-type ﬂip-ﬂip.
Figure 3.24: The design of the edge triggered level-shifter.
A level-shifter using the edge-triggered topology as explained was designed and tested but appeared
to be highly unstable due to VS≠rail voltage instability due to switching. Since the voltage node Vx is
biased using the VS≠rail the bias point ﬂuctuates and generates false trigger points. A false trigger point
results in a faulty generated clock pulse generating wrong information at the level-shifter output. This issue
can be resolved redeﬁning the power supply used for the level shifter and generates stable references. An
edge-triggered level shifter would be more efﬁcient compared to the PNP-transistor based level shifter since
the logic gates only consume very little power and can have a lower propagation delay. Future work could
include an optimized level-shifter.
3.10
Dead-time generation
Two eGaN-FETs are placed in a totempole topology in the power stage. If an event occurs in which both
the eGaN-FETs are in the conductive on-state a large current will ﬂow through the eGaN-FETs effectively
shorting the power supply rails. This shout-trough event has to be prevented using a small time delay between
one eGaN-FET switching off and the other switching on.
As explained dead-time can be used to generate Zero Voltage Switching, which is beneﬁcial for efﬁciency
reasons. At the other hand introduces dead-time a distortion to the output signal which is not wanted. The
trade of regarding the timing of this dead-time has been made in Section 3.6.

Modulation
The power stage takes care of the signal processing from comparator input to output voltage node. The
triangular carrier signal can be generated externally or by using feedback. This feedback can be taken either
from the switch-node voltage or the output voltage node to generate the carrier. The aim of the research is
to make a self-oscillating system where the goal of the feedback circuit is set to fulﬁl the self-oscillating
system criteria (the Barkhaussen criteria):
• Create a total phase shift of a complete 360° at the oscillation frequency. ≠180° is made using negative
feedback.
• Have sufﬁcient gain at the oscillating frequency to generate a PWM signal
Figure 4.1: Placement of the modulation loop ﬁlter in the ampliﬁer system.
4.1
Self-oscillation modulators
The Barkhaussen oscillation criteria describes a system will oscillate using negative feedback when
the phase shift reaches ≠180° with a minimum ampliﬁer gain of 0 dB [29]. This criteria can be fulﬁlled
with different topologies. The goal of all self-oscillating topologies is to create a phase shift of 360° at the
oscillation frequency. The oscillation frequency is the same as the switching frequency fSW . To create a
total 360° phase shift, negative feedback can be used. Negative feedback creates an phase shift of ≠180°
due to signal inversion where the other ≠180° can be introduced using feedback.
47
==PAGE:74==
48
CHAPTER 4. MODULATION
There are different topologies in using feedback to create a self-oscillating system [30]:
• AIM Modulator
A very basic modulator introduced in the seventies [2]topology uses the output switching waveform as
a hysteresis feedback. This waveform is compared with the integral of this switching waveform in
the negative feedback. The Astable Integrating Modulator, or AIM modulator, will do so. Taking the
integral of the switching output waveform using a resistor-capacitor (RC) circuit creates a phase-shift
of ≠90°, where the hysteresis window sets a time delay pushing the open-loop phase response beyond
≠180°. Adding this to an inverting input of a comparator a closed loop oscillator is build using very
few components. The beneﬁt of taking the hysteresis and integrating feedback after the power stage
encloses distortion introduced in the power stage and suppresses this in the loop [31, 32]. However
care must be taken that also the power supply rail noise is coupled in the modulator which can increase
noise on the output.
• GLIM modulator
The Global Loop Integrating Modulator (GLIM)[32, 33], introduced by Søren Poulsen in 2004, is a
hysteresis based self-oscillating loop topology. The negative feedback comes from after the output
ﬁlter. By adding a differentiator (i.e. a zero in the frequency domain) the ﬁlter response is shaped to
one to act like an integrator in a open loop for the switching frequency range. This integrating negative
feedback is similar to the one in the AIM modulator. The beneﬁts of the GLIM compared to the
AIM modulator are that the feedback is taken from after the output ﬁlter inductor. Non-linear effects
introduced by the output ﬁlter are suppressed within the modulator loop and therefore a superior
performance can be reached by using a GLIM oscillator compared to using an AIM oscillator. The
topology does not introduce signiﬁcant extra components compared to the AIM type modulator or the
Phase-shift oscillator making it cost effective. Care must be taken on the loop ﬁlter design to maintain
mentioned beneﬁts towards an AIM modulation scheme.
• PSC Phase-shift oscillator
The phase-shift oscillating topology is no different than a hysteresis type oscillator [11]. A phase-
shift oscillator can be seen as a hysteresis type oscillator where the hysteresis window is 0 V. The
delay required to push the phase well beyond the −180° mark can be generated using a frequency
response design method. Feedback to generate the carrier waveform can be taken from after the
output ﬁlter which is beneﬁcial on performance measures. The PSC oscillator has a lower lower
variation of the switching frequency for a change in modulation index. Layout becomes very important
when designing a phase-shift oscillator. Since the comparator triggers on every zero-crossing on the
inverting-input terminal noise and EMI coupling can result in unwanted triggers. This can result in a
highly unstable ampliﬁer.
4.2
Design of modulation scheme
The used modulation topology will be the AIM modulator. The AIM modulator allows a simple approach
and decent performance. A global loop around the AIM modulator stage and output ﬁlter will eventually
boost the performance of the ampliﬁer.

4.4
Hysteresis window
The hysteresis window is taken directly from the switch-node voltage. During the conductive state of
either of the eGaN-FETs, the power supply rail voltage is directly connected to the switch-node. Noise
present of the power supply rails is therefore easily coupled into the output signal, but also in the modulator.
This could introduce noise problems in an environment with noisy power supplies.
The noise on the power supply could introduce wrongly triggered events by the comparator. The noise
can add a variable phase noise to the PWM signal, also known as jitter. For a steady state like idle operation,
noise may add variation in the switching frequency. This variation is directly inﬂuencing the output voltage
and can therefore be kept responsible for output voltage noise. The effect of jitter in a self-oscillating system
as the AIM modulator may be researched in a future project.
A hybrid AIM modulator could be researched in the future where the hysteresis window is taken directly
from the comparator outputs that have a stable power supply. The integrating feedback is taken from the
switch-node to compensate for propagation delay and other non-linear behaviour in the power stage [32].

Summary
An AIM modulator is introduced using the eGaN-FET power stage inside the modulation loop. The
modulator is designed at an idle switching frequency of fSWidle = 2 MHz. The modulator is analysed using
a time domain approach and possible shortcomings are discussed. For this research a single modulator is
used. A follow up research may include different types of modulators like the GLIM of the PSC oscillator.
Especially the PSC oscillator seems promising due to a lower effect of the modulation index towards the
switching frequency. A lower variation in switching frequency may be used to create a more efﬁcient
ampliﬁer, regarding precise timing of the switching transients like dead-time. A better use of the switching
losses low points seen in Fig. 3.10 and 3.11 may be achieved.

5.2
Loop ﬁlter simulation
A global loop will be applied around the power module modulator and output ﬁlter as pictured in Fig.
5.2. The aim of the global loop is to produce an error signal which drives the modulator. This is done by
taking a feedback from the output voltage node and compare it to the input reference voltage. The difference
is the error voltage which drives the modulator. Due to the high gain in the loop the error voltage will be
small.
Figure 5.2: Placement of the global loop in the ampliﬁer.
The global feedback loop will use a negative feedback. The allows the global loop to create the error
voltage. Using negative feedback control is gained of the output voltage behaviour. This means that when an
error is introduced at the output voltage node the global loop processes a new value for the error voltage
to reduce the error on the output. This lowers distortion and output impedance and gains stability and
performance. Also the gain of the complete ampliﬁer can be set using the global loop.
When applying negative feedback one must take care the stability criteria is fulﬁlled to prevent oscillations.
Different than for the modulator, oscillating behaviour is not desired in the global loop. This means a phase-
shift margin and a gain-margin (depicted in Fig. 5.3) need to be present to maintain stable operation. Where
the modulator aims to cross the 0 dB point with sufﬁcient gain for modulation, the global loop is desired
to have a gain well below 0 dB for the ≠180° crossing frequency. The attenuation that global loop has at
this frequency is called the gain-margin expressed in dB. Where the gain reaches 0 dB the phase-shift of the
global loop should be well above ≠180°. The margin created at this frequency is called the phase-margin
expressed in °.

sinking current in the positive rail or sourcing it in the negative rail. This effect is due to the rectiﬁer diodes

no longer change direction as explain in Chapter 3. A positive output current keeps ﬂowing trough the
output ﬁlter inductor, also when the bottom eGaN-FET is on (Fig. 3.6d). This means the negative supply
has to source current instead of sinking. The power supply can only handle a positive output current [39].
The power supply is not capable of handling negative output currents therefore the current ﬂows from the
decoupling capacitor. This discharges the capacitor and therefore the voltage on the rail ﬂuctuates.

CHAPTER 6
Layout
Since eGaN-FETs allow a much higher switching frequency than their respective silicon counterparts.
This also means faster transient slopes in voltage (dv/dt) and current (di/dt) can be introduced. These fast
transients require extra attention in the layout of the power stage. Care has to be taken that the gate-source
voltage does not exceed its limit due to the introduced parasitics, where also accidental turn-on of the device
must be prevented. During these fast switching transients power is dissipated by the eGaN-FETs where
cooling might be necessary.
6.1
The LGA package
eGaN-FETs are very similar to their silicon counterparts, but not when it comes to packaging. The
eGaN-FETs come in a so called Land Grid Array (LGA ) package (Fig. 6.1). This package is similar to
a ﬂip-chip or a Ball Grid Array (BGA ) The substrate of the FET is exposed at the top, where the gate,
source and drain connections are placed underneath the FET. Placing the connections underneath the FET,
the inner FET connections become much shorter. The gate connection is now closer to the actual gate, the
same for drain and source connections. This shorter connection results in a lower stray inductance (depicted
in Fig. 6.2)in the terminal branches, making faster switching transients possible. This stray inductance is a
small parasitic inductance in series with the eGaN-FET connections. This inductance limits the fast di/dt
values required to make fast transients. These inductances are not only found in the eGaN-FET terminals but
also in the copper of the PCB. Therefore layout becomes very important to not compromise the eGaN-FET
possibilities.

Where eGaN-FETs generate very fast switching transients, decoupling capacitors become very important.
Capacitors placed very close to the eGaN-FETs can provide enough energy for these switching transients
locally, depicted with the red arrows in Fig. 6.3. Placing them in close proximity to the eGaN-FETs is a
must. The high currents ﬂowing trough these capacitors at the moment of switching can create voltage spikes
when a series resistance or inductance is present. These currents also generate a magnetic ﬁeld which can
couple unwanted switching noise elsewhere in the circuit.
Figure 6.3: Decoupling capacitance on the eGaN-FETs. The driving current in sketched in blue and the
switching current in red.
Also the gate driving loop has to be kept as short as possible. The gate is supposed to be driven by a
current with a very fast transient generating a fast turn-on event. The gate current loop is elaborated in Fig.
6.3 with the blue arrows.
==PAGE:97==
6.2. DECOUPLING
71
The effect of a insufﬁcient decoupling capacitance near the eGaN-FETs can be directly seen at the
switch-node voltage. Voltage transitions can become slower since not enough energy is available to absorb or
deliver the switching energy. Over- or undershoot might be present when the loops conducting the switching
current are not kept as small as possible. The eGaN-FET package itself is optimized to have a very low stray
inductance. A large switching loop would compromise these advantages using eGaN-FETs.
When decoupling capacitors are placed far from the eGaN-FETs a larger stray inductance is seen in
the switching loop. This inductance limits the current that can ﬂow trough the drain/source and back to
the capacitor. Effectively an LC circuit is created that can generate over-/undershoot and ringing on the
switch-node voltage. Over- and undershoot can dissipate more switching energy and can even damage the
eGaN-FETs. Damaging can occur when the voltage from drain to source exceeds the break-down voltage
BDDSS.
Figure 6.4: Optimized eGaN-FET layout. Figure from [40]
An optimized layout is suggested by (Fig. 6.4, [40]) where the decoupling capacitors are placed very
close to the eGaN-FETs. The return path of the current ﬂows in the second layer of the PCB. The magnetic
ﬁeld generated by the switching current is cancelled out since the return path of the current is directly
underneath the components placed at the top side. Using the inner layer gives a better performance than
using the outside layer, where the switching current loop path becomes longer. When using the outer layer
the path length is increased by the PCB thickness, creating a larger loop area which results in a higher stray
inductance. Also thicker copper of the PCB layers is recommended to lower the loop inductance. A copper
thickness of hcop = 70 µm is used, instead of the standard 35 µm thickness. Also the board thickness is
decreased to 0.8 mm compared to a standard of 1.6 mm. This makes the gate driver current loop smaller
since the gate driver will be places on the bottom side of the PCB. A smaller current loop makes the stray
inductance in this loop lower.

6.3
The Miller capacitance
eGaN-FET have, like any other FET, parasitic capacitances Cdg, Cgs and Cds as depicted in Fig. 6.2.
When a large change in voltage is present at the drain of the eGaN-FET due to a switching transient, a large
current can ﬂow trough these capacitances. There is also a current ﬂowing through the gate of the eGaN-FET.
The gate has a small parasitic resistance Rg. A voltage across this resistance can be introduced by the large
current resulting in a gate driving voltage. If this voltage becomes higher than the eGaN-FET trigger voltage
VGST H the eGaN-FET can turn on accidentally, which is called a Miller turn-on [41]. This would result in a
shoot-through event generating a loss of power. The waveforms that can result in an accidental turn-on are
sketched in Fig. 6.5.
Figure 6.5: Schematic overview of the current ﬂow that can introduce an accidental turn-on in blue. Currents
representing an accidental turn-off are sketched in red.
Also when the bottom eGaN-FET is in reverse conduction as in Fig: 3.6f a fast current is ﬂowing the
moment the bottom eGaN-FET will turn on and enter the conduction stage as in Fig. 3.6g. This large current
ﬂows trough LS and CGS leaving the eGaN-FET trough the gate as in Fig. 6.5. The capacitance and stray
inductance can create a resonance circuit causing the source node to resonate. A resonance may bring the

6.4
Thermal
The eGaN-FETs are very small and likely to cause malfunction when no attention is given to their
thermal behaviour. The thicker copper helps to conduct the heat generated by the eGaN-FETs to a free air
convection. The power supply rails are directly connected to the eGaN-FET terminals and create a heat
conducting path. No additional heat sink is implemented, where cooling is mainly done by the PCB copper.
Two large areas of copper at the top layer are implemented for 2 purposes:
• Low resistance path for rail voltage distribution to the eGaN-FETs
• Large copper convection cooling area to direct heat away from the eGaN-FETs
The total layout as made for the eGaN-FET power stage module can be found in the Appendix E. A
3D plot of the designed power stage module is given in Fig. 6.7. The board dimensions are 35 mm times
70 mm. The PCB thickness is chosen to be 0.8 mm to decrease the switching loop inductance. The volume
of the power stage is estimates at 9.57 cm3 meaning a power density of 15.66 W cm≠3. The layout is not
optimized on power density yet, meaning a improvement to at least 20 W cm≠3 can be made.

Summary
A power stage module design is made including a versatile layout for different modulation strategies.
The eGaN-FETs used in the design have a unique LGA package to minimize the loop inductance and stray

very fast. The module combines a output power of 150 W with a power to voltume density of 15.66 W cm≠3.

the audible bandwidth. This results in an ampliﬁer with a THD of 0.00496% at Pout = 10 W with a 1 kHz

output power. Looking back at the speciﬁcations set in Chapter 1 it can be said most of these speciﬁcation
are made. Possible reasons for the efﬁciency optimization are introduced where the noise of the ampliﬁer

8.2
Ampliﬁer modulation and loop ﬁlter
The ampliﬁers will both run in an AIM modulation scheme with an idle switching frequency of 400 kHz.
Since both modules have the same modulator loop, the global loop can also be identical. This global loop
is different than the one used for the high frequency AIM ampliﬁer as in Chapter 5 and 7. The transition
bandwidth for the loop ﬁlter becomes smaller for a lower switching frequency and output ﬁlter cut-off
frequency. This means a lower gain will be available in the audible bandwidth which is used to reduce output
distortion. Where a comparison of the power stage switching devices is desired, a lower gain in the global
loop ﬁlter can work out positive. A lower loop gain means introduced error are suppressed less, making
easier to measure. This will expose the real differences in performance between the two output stages.

The modulation circuit of the two ampliﬁers differs slightly. The eGaN-FET based power module
operates with very little propagation delay trough from input to output, where the MOSFET based power
module has a longer delay. The delay is mainly build up in the gate driver of the power module. The
delay in the MOSFET driver is 60 ns for the IRS20124S gate-driver, compared to 28 ns for the LM5113

pole. The pole is placed at a much higher frequency than the output ﬁlter cut-off frequency where the open
loop gain is aimed to be 0 dB. The schematic of the test board set up with the applied global loop can be

8.5
The perfect eGaN-FET for a switch-mode audio ampliﬁer
Having the parameters of eGaN-FETs studied throughout this research it can be concluded their behaviour
is superior when compared with MOSFETs. However there are still some limiting factors compromising the
aim of building a perfect ampliﬁer. The eGaN-FETs have a very low on-state resistance, which is desired.
Only when loudspeaker impedance would decrease, the eGaN-FET on-state resistance would have to be
optimized. The ultra low gate charge Qg makes the eGaN devices switch very fast. The switching transient
generating PSW are very short due to a low charge, making eGaN-FETs more efﬁcient. This, however, does
not change there can still be a large left over charge in the eGaN-FET COSS on the moment of switching
which would be dissipated as PCOSS. Therefore a lower COSS would be desired in the future. A complete
half-bridge design like the used MOSFET was equipped with could lower COSS by reducing space. These
devices have been introduced during the timespan of this research [51, 52] and could increase performance
of an eGaN-FET equipped ampliﬁer. To decrease the required PCB area in ampliﬁer applications it is desired
to remove the parallel Schottky diodes as used on the power stage module. A lower voltage drop in the
reverse conduction path would therefore be desired.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
9.1
Conclusions
The eGaN-FETs showed a promising performance towards the use in class D ampliﬁcation. The lower
on-state resistance, combined with the low gate charge and optimized LGA package, result in a strong
competitor for silicon devices.
It was found that the designed eGaN-FET power stage performance is largely depended on the accompa-
nied output ﬁlter inductor. The inductor ripple current is responsible for the behaviour of the switch-node
voltage and therefore also for the ampliﬁer performance. Thermal issues with the eGaN-FETs also lead
back to the output ﬁlter inductor. A comprehensive study on the switching losses was made in Chapter 3
where an optimization can be made. The study showed an optimized point for low power consumption in
idle operation using a low inductance. This low inductance however affects the cut-off frequency of the
output ﬁlter and behaviour towards stability. Lower inductance also means a higher ripple current is present
which has to be kept in mind when evaluating the ampliﬁer losses and thermal behaviour. A trade-off was
introduced where values were chosen accordingly.
A 150 W ampliﬁer was made using eGaN-FETs switching in an idle operation at fSW = 2 MHz. The
ampliﬁer showed very good performance in both THD and efﬁciency measurements. The measured THD
was 0.008% at an output power of Pout = 1 W with a 1 kHz test signal. The ampliﬁer reached an output
power of 150 W. At this output power the efﬁciency was 87%. Most of the speciﬁcations set in Chapter 1
were made.
A comparative study has been made to evaluate the eGaN-FET performance towards the use of traditional
silicon based MOSFETs. Two identical switch-mode audio power ampliﬁers were built with the only
difference in the power stage being the switching stage devices material (and accompanied gate driver). The
ampliﬁer equipped with the eGaN-FETs outperformed the MOSFET equipped ampliﬁer on all measurements.
Only a slightly higher efﬁciency at low output powers was achieved by the MOSFET design due to a lower
output referred capacitance COSS. The eGaN-FET ampliﬁer decreased measured THD by an order of
magnitude and improved the efﬁciency by 4%.

==PAGE:136==

CHAPTER 9. CONCLUSION
Where eGaN-FETs already show their superiorities towards MOSFETs some improvements on eGaN-
FETs are desired. A lower Qg would make faster switching transients and therefore switching losses PSW
would be decreased. A lower voltage drop in the reverse conduction path would decrease losses during
reverse conduction time intervals. Lower output capacitance COSS is desired to decrease dead-time and
lower output charge losses PCOSS. The on-state resistance RDSON is not required to be optimized at this
moment. When loudspeaker impedance would decrease in the future, a lower RDSON would be desired.
The question raised at the beginning of this thesis: "Can eGaN-FETs be used to build a higher power
density ampliﬁer with a higher efﬁciency than MOSFET technology without compromising audio perfor-
mance?" can be answered with a loud yes! eGaN-FETs show that smaller and more powerful ampliﬁers can
be made with comparable performance. Also, when switching frequency is increased, higher performance
ampliﬁers can be made using a simple and straight-forward design technique without using complex feedback
topologies. A wider bandwidth to create loop gain becomes available where massive gain can be created to
lower output distortion.
9.2
Future work
During this research possible follow up research topics are introduced. These can be summarized as
following:
• Optimize the eGaN-FET design on efﬁciency using dead-time and ripple current.
• A more comprehensive study can be made on losses versus output power, also taking the dynamic
output current into account
• The output ﬁlter inductor can be research to ﬁnd an efﬁcient and linear output inductor core material
for a high switching frequency applications
• New available eGaN-FETs which provide a half-bridge circuit in a single package can improve
switching behaviour
• Different modulators can be tested with a dedicated layout. The effect of jitter in hysteresis types can
also be researched.
• More advanced global loop feedback topologies can be tested to optimize the wider transition band-
width
• Research the thermal behaviour of the circuit regarding ﬂuctuation of RDSON and VGST H
• Study the inﬂuence of ultra-low dead-time towards distortion and trade-off towards other distortion
sources
• New faster and more efﬁcient level shifter
• Power supply efﬁciency driving the low side referenced gate driver and logic
In short: eGaN-FET provide a bright future but in switch-mode audio ampliﬁcation other research topics
come up being just as important to build a high performance high efﬁciency ampliﬁer on a very small
footprint.

equipped with eGaN-FETs

ABSTRACT
Where high-frequency clocked system switch-mode audio power ampliﬁers equipped with eGaN-FETs have
been introduced the past years, a novel self-oscillating eGaN-FET equipped ampliﬁer is presented.
A
150 WRMS ampliﬁer has been build and tested with regard to performance and eﬃciency with an idle
switching frequency of 2 MHz. The ampliﬁer consist of a power-stage module with a self-oscillating loop and
an error-reducing global loop. It was found that an eGaN-FET based ampliﬁer shows promising potential
for building high power density audio ampliﬁers with excellent audio performance. However care must be
taken of the e↵ects caused by a higher switching frequency.
1.
INTRODUCTION
Enhancement mode Gallium-Nitride FETs (eGaN-
FETs) are getting more attention these days in
power conversion applications due to their superior
Figure of Merit (FOM) compared to regular silicon
type MOSFETs [1]. eGaN-FETs’ low parasitic ca-
pacitances, low gate-source voltage, small package
and low RDSON make these devices suitable for high
switching frequencies. A high switching frequency
in switch-mode power electronics can lead to smaller
output ﬁlters. Combined with the small eGaN-FET
packages the road opens for high power density con-
verters and ampliﬁers.

Di↵erent topologies of self-oscillating systems with
and without output ﬁlter control have been intro-
duced before [5,6].
This research has been done
using traditional silicon based semiconductor de-
vices.
eGaN-FETs allow a higher switching fre-

eGaN-FETs.
quency which may be used in these existing topolo-
gies. Increasing the switching frequency means that
the output ﬁlter inductor can be reduced in size.
A smaller output inductor can reduce a large cut
of the costs of ampliﬁer modules.
A drawback of
higher switching frequencies may be the increasing
factor of dead-time since the ratio between dead-
time and switching cycle becomes larger. Dead-time
can increase distortion since no direct control over
the switch node is available during this time interval.
3.1.
AIM
To generate a high frequency PWM signal a very
fast comparator is used. A PWM signal can be cre-
ated by applying a hysteresis positive feedback and a
integrating negative feedback. This circuit (Fig. 6)
is called an Astable Integrating Modulator (AIM),
introduced in the 1970’s [7] and has been widely
used.
The AIM modulator can be constructed around the
eGaN-FET output stage as seen in Fig 6.
The
switching-node is used for both the hysteresis and
integrator feedback to run the modulator. The inte-
grator feedback with R1 and C integrates the switch-
node voltage in a triangular waveform until it hits
the hysteresis window set by R2 and R3. This ﬂips
the output signal state and therefore the switch-node
voltage towards the other rail causing the system to
oscillate. The output ﬁlter (or demodulation ﬁlter)
is constructed by a inductor (Lf) and capacitor (Cf)
circuit where the output voltage is reconstructed.
An AIM modulator topology has a larger gain in the
feed back loop if one would compare with a clocked
system. This means that when a global loop is ap-
plied there is more signal gain available for error

The bandwidth of the AIM modulator is depended
on the integrating negative feedback, which is the
only pole in the system [8]. This means the band-
width of the modulator is limited towards the place-
ment of this pole. Since the carrier is generated using
a resistor and capacitor circuit the feedback only be-
haves as an integrator for frequencies above the pole
frequency. For lower carrier frequencies, which are
modulation index depended, carrier distortion may
occur resulting in output signal distortion.
3.2.
Loop ﬁlter
Setting the gain and the bandwidth of the complete
ampliﬁer can be done by a global loop. The global
loop compares the output signal to the referenced
input signal creating a error signal to drive the mod-
ulator.
Placing the output ﬁlter cut-o↵frequency fLC at
a higher frequency, more bandwidth is available for
the transition bandwidth (Fig. 7). In the loop ﬁl-
ter , the bandwidth between the high gain in the
audible bandwidth and the required attenuation at

compared to the audible bandwidth and the switch-

switching frequencies can be used for this transition.
The output ﬁlter cut-o↵frequency can be placed at a
higher frequency because the switching frequency of
the eGaN-FETs is much higher. As a rule of thumb
the ﬁlter cut-o↵freqnecy should be placed a decade
below the switching frequency, in this case around
200 kHz.
This creates a larger bandwidth for the
transition region, which can make room for a higher
gain in the audible bandwidth. A higher gain in the
audible bandwidth means a lower distortion of the
output signal.
In this paper a two stage PI controller global loop
is implemented (Fig. 8 and 9). The ﬁrst PI con-
troller creates the summing node. A zero is added
in the feedback from the output voltage using CZ
around the ﬁlter cut-o↵frequency to create some
phase margin. The placement of the poles can be
optimized using R2 + C2 and R7 + C3 where they
are implemented around 6 kHz and 1.5 kHz.
The
gain of the ampliﬁer is set by R1 and R3 to a total
of approx. 28dB. If improvements should be made
to reduce distortion, the loop gain can be increased
with an higher order loop. Future work could also
conclude an active THD shaping ﬁlter [9].

MEASUREMENTS
The plug in module is used on a test board with the
global feedback added.
Decoupling capacitors are
placed on the test board to provide a stable power
supply and eliminate supply noise as seen in Fig. 14.
The switch node transients are very fast and the de-
lay trough the power stage module very small. The
delay consists of the circuit delay and the dead-time
adding up to a total of 62 ns with a dead time of
14 ns. As can be seen in Fig. 15 the switching-node
transients are very fast. The total rise and fall time
(including dead-time) take approx. 22 ns, where the
maximum dv/dt is reaching 29.7 V/ns.
Total Harmonic Distortion (THD) is measured using
an Audio Precision at 3 di↵erent frequencies. The
plot in Fig. 16 shows that a decent performance can
be achieved at 100 Hz in blue and 1 kHz in red. At
6.6 kHz in yellow the distortion is larger. The min-
imum is found at a test signal frequency of 100 Hz
at 7 W output power where THD is 0.0034%.
To
attenuate unwanted behaviour of the 6.6 kHz har-
monics suﬃcient gain in the global loop is required
at 3 times this frequency (approx.
20 kHz).
The
loop ﬁlter can be adjusted to create a larger gain
up to 20 kHz to decrease output voltage distortion,
however one must keep the stability criteria in mind.
The measured distortion at 1 kHz at 1/8th of the
output power is within the speciﬁcation. Where the

6.
CONCLUSION
Promising results are shown for a self-oscillating sys-
tem using eGaN-FETs.
The performance of the
built ampliﬁer is good with a lowest point mea-
sured distortion of 0.0034% at 7 W of the output
power.
Higher switching frequencies do provide a
design stress relief on the loop ﬁlters where a wider
transition bandwidth is available between gain in the
audible band and required attenuation for switching
frequencies. At the same time these higher switch-
ing frequencies make layout and inductor design very
critical.
The results show excellent performance
when kept in mind only a second-order loop ﬁlter
was used, a higher order would increase the perfor-
mance. Improvements have to be made in optimiz-
ing eﬃciency.
7.
FUTURE WORK
The switching cycle has to be optimized for all
possible output currents and voltages to improve
eﬃciency, optimize dead-time and prevent shoot-
trough.
Also the output ﬁlter inductor has to be
tuned accordingly and thermal behaviour needs to
be optimized. Improving distortion measurements
can be done by optimizing the loop ﬁlter to a higher
order with more gain and a sharper roll-o↵. Also
THD shaping loop ﬁlters can be studied.
