Abstract
Allergic asthma is a chronic Th2­mediated inflammatory disease characterised by dysregulated
B and T cell responses to environmental allergens such as house dust mite (HDM). Murine
models are essential tools for studying asthma pathogenesis. Although both acute and chronic
murine models have been described, direct comparisons focusing on allergen­specific B and T
cell responses remain limited.
This thesis aimed to develop and optimise allergen­specific immunoassays for quantifying both
B and T cell responses in acute and chronic HDM­induced murine asthma models. An in­house
ELISA kit (MY­kit) was established and systematically optimised for total and HDM­specific IgE
and IgG1, demonstrating higher sensitivity at low concentrations compared with a commercial
ELISA kit. Lung homogenates and lymph nodes from acute (two­week exposure) and chronic
(two­month exposure followed by one month of recovery) models were analysed to evaluate
antibody production, T cell proliferation, and cytokine profiles.
The acute model induced significantly higher total IgE levels, reflecting a strong but short­
lived systemic response, whereas the chronic model generated a more strong HDM­specific
IgE and IgG1 responses that persisted after recovery, consistent with immunological mem­
ory. T cell analyses further highlighted these distinctions: the acute model showed minimal
antigen­specific proliferation and inconsistent cytokine secretion, while the chronic model ex­
hibited strong CD4+ T cell proliferation, diverse cytokine production (IL­13, IL­17A, IL­9, IFN­γ),
and an expanded FOXP3+ regulatory T cell population.
Together, these findings validate that allergen­specific immunoassays can sensitively detect
immune responses in both acute and chronic allergic asthma models. The acute protocol pre­
dominantly reflects early sensitisation events, while the chronic protocol more accurately models
persistent allergic inflammation and immune memory. This proof­of­concept work provides a
foundation for future studies, including translational investigations into asthma­associated cog­
nitive impairment.

Theoretical Background
1.1
Asthma ­ An Overview
Asthma is a chronic inflammatory disease of the airways.
It is characterised by recurrent
episodes of wheezing, chest tightness, shortness of breath, and coughing. These symptoms are
often associated with variable airflow obstruction that is usually reversible either spontaneously
or with treatment. The disease is common and affects 1–29% of the population in different
countries, with an estimated 300 million people affected worldwide, posing a significant burden
on healthcare systems, especially in industrialised countries [1], [2].
Asthma is a heterogeneous condition that can be categorised into two types: allergic (atopic)
and non­allergic. Allergic asthma is the most common form and typically has its onset during
childhood. It is characterised by sensitisation to environmental allergens such as house dust
mites (HDM), pollen, or animal dander. In contrast, non­allergic asthma is often triggered by
other factors such as infections, exercise, or cold air, and is less strongly associated with IgE­
mediated mechanisms [3], [4].
The hallmark features of asthma include airway inflammation, airway hyperresponsiveness
(AHR), and remodelling of the airways. Inflammatory cells such as eosinophils, mast cells,
and T helper 2 (Th2) lymphocytes infiltrate the bronchial mucosa and contribute to the chronic
inflammation. This inflammation leads to structural changes in the airway wall, including smooth
muscle hypertrophy, goblet cell hyperplasia, and subepithelial fibrosis, which together result in
long­term changes in airway function [5]. Allergic asthma is primarily driven by a type 2 im­
mune response mediated by Th2 cells and type 2 innate lymphoid cells (ILCs). These cells
produce cytokines such as interleukin (IL)­4, IL­5, and IL­13, which play crucial roles in the al­
lergic asthma immune response. IL­4 promotes B cell class switching to IgE, IL­5 is crucial for
eosinophil survival and recruitment, and IL­13 contributes to mucus secretion and AHR [6].
Mouse models of allergic asthma, particularly those using HDM as an allergen, are widely used
to mimic the immunological mechanisms of the disease. These models reproduce key aspects
such as allergen sensitisation, airway inflammation, and IgE production, making them valuable
tools for research.
1.2
Immunological Basis of Allergic Asthma
1.2.1
Allergen Sensitisation and Challenge
Allergic asthma develops when an individual undergoes the process of allergen sensitisation.
This is the process where an individual is first exposed to an environmental allergen, such as
HDM. Sentisation is the initial phase during which the immune system encounters a harmless

2
==PAGE:11==
allergen and mistakenly identifies it as a threat, leading to the development of allergen­specific
immune memory. This primes the immune system to make a response upon subsequent ex­
posures to the allergen [7]. Upon inhalation, pathogen­associated molecular patterns (PAMPs)
present on HDM, such as β­glucans, chitin and bacterial lipopolysaccharide (LPS), are recog­
nised by pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) on airway epithelial cells. This recognition trig­
gers the release of epithelial­derived cytokines to initiate type 2 immunity. These cytokines
include thymic stromal lymphopoietin (TSLP), IL­25, and IL­33. These epithelial cytokines, also
called alarmins, initiate a local pro­inflammatory environment that recruits and activates den­
dritic cells (DCs), thereby establishing a link between the airway epithelium and mast­cell ac­
tivation without involvement of T2 cells, but results in inflammation of the airways and airway
hyper­responsiveness. These alarmins play a key role in the immune hyper­responsiveness
that support the exacerbations of asthma [8], [9]. DCs play an important role in initiating the
adaptive immune response. They perform the roles of allergen recognition and T cell priming.
In the airways, conventional dendritic cells (cDCs) inhale allergens through different mecha­
nisms, including receptor­mediated endocytosis, macropinocytosis, and uptake facilitated by
the Fc receptor in the presence of IgG or IgE allergen complexes. Receptors on the DCs,
like C­type lectin receptors and Toll­like receptors (TLRs), bind to the carbohydrate or protease
components of the allergen. This will enhance uptake of the antigen and activation [10], [9].
Following antigen uptake, DCs undergo maturation, which is characterised by upregulation of
costimulatory molecules (CD80, CD86) and CCR7 expression. This upregulation guides the
DCs to migrate to the draining mediastinal lymph nodes (mLNs) via the lymphatic vessels. In the
LNs, the mature DCs present the processed peptides on MHC class II molecules to naive CD4+
T cells. The local cytokine environment, induced by IL­4, TSLP, and other signals released from
airway epithelial cells, initiates the naive T cells to differentiate towards the Th2 subset lineage.
This polarisation is reinforced by the ligand OX40 (OX40L) on the DCs to stimulate T cells
by interacting with the co­stimulatory receptor OX40(CD134) and Notch signalling [8], [11], [12].
Once an individual is sensitised, the immune response is primed to react strongly upon exposure
to the same allergen. This reexposure leads to a fast Th2­driven response in the airways. Th2
cells secrete IL­4, IL­5, and IL­13, promoting B cells to produce IgE, eosinophilic inflammation,
and mucus hypersecretion. Additionally, memory T cells and long­lived plasma cells contribute
to the persistence of allergic inflammation [6].
This sensitisation and challenge cycle is a hallmark of allergic asthma and has been success­
fully modelled in mice using HDM and ovalbumin (OVA), where HDM is more relevant since
it naturally contains proteases and PAMPs that reflect real­life exposure scenarios in humans
[13].
1.2.2
Type 2 Immunity and Airway Inflammation
Once an individual is sensitised, subsequent allergen exposures trigger a type 2 immune re­
sponse in the airways, which is central to the pathogenesis of allergic asthma. The type 2

==PAGE:12==
immunity is seen in Figure 1.1. The Airways epithelial cells serve as the first line of defense
between the external environment and internal structures, protecting against pathogens, aller­
gens, and chemical irritants. This defence involves the release of epithelial alarmins, as earlier
mentioned, IL­25, IL­33, and TSLP in response to epithelial damage [14].
The type 2 immune response is driven by adaptive immune cells, more specifically Th2 cells
and innate immune cells such as ILC2s. It is a combination of these cells that coordinates to
induce a cascade of cytokine production and effector mechanisms that lead to airway inflam­
mation, mucus production and hyperresponsiveness.
The classical mediators of type 2 immunity are the Th2 cells that secrete the crucial cytokines
IL­4, IL­5 and IL­13, each playing essential roles in asthma pathogenesis. IL­4 promotes class
switch recombination in B cells. This leads to the production of IgE antibodies that bind to
high­affinity FcϵRI receptors on innate immune cells such as mast cells and basophils thereby
sensitising them to allergens like HDM. Upon subsequent allergen exposure, the variable region
of IgE binds to its specific antigen, resulting in cross­linking of FcϵRI­bound IgE and triggering
mast cell degranulation. This release of inflammatory mediators, like histamine, prostaglandin
and leukotrienes, facilitates the recruitment of additional effector cells, including Th2 cells,
eosinophils, and basophils, amplifying the allergic inflammatory response. IL­5 is fundamental
for activation, survival, and recruitment of eosinophils from the bone marrow to the lungs. IL­13
contributes to airway remodelling by inducing goblet cell hyperplasia and mucus overproduc­
tion, and also promotes AHR, a key feature of allergic asthma [15], [16]. ILC2s can be activated
via epithelial­derived cytokines such as IL­25, IL­33, and TSLP, without the need for antigen
presentation. Upon activation, ILCs produce large amounts of IL­5 and IL­13, just like the Th2
response. Experiments in mouse models have shown that depletion of ILC2s reduces airway
eosinophilia and mucus production, emphasising their non­redundant role in type 2 inflamma­
tion[17]. Moreover, epithelial cells are active participants in orcestrating the immune response.
They sense damage and allergen­associated molecular patterns and respond by releasing cy­
tokines and chemokines that shape immune cell recruitment and activation. This interaction
between immune cells creates a self­reinforcing loop of inflammation and tissue remodelling
[17], [18].

1.3
CD4+ T Cells in Asthma
The population of αβ T cells comprises three main subtypes: CD4+ T cells, CD8+ T cells,
and natural killer T (NKT) cells. They originate from bone marrow–derived precursor cells that
migrate to the thymus, where they develop into thymocytes. These T cell subsets differ in how
they recognise antigens and in their respective effector functions. Throughout this thesis, the
main focus will be on the CD4+ T cells. CD4+ T cells, commonly called T helper cells, play a
central role in coordinating immune responses by enhancing inflammation and supporting other
immune cells, but lack intrinsic cytotoxic activity. The immune system tailors its response to
different pathogens by recruiting CD4+ T cell subsets, each specialised for a specific type of
immune challenge. Currently recognised subsets include Th1, Th2, Th17, Th22, Th9, T follicular
helper (Tfh), and regulatory T (Treg) cells [20]. An overview of CD4+ T­cell subsets, including
their inducing cytokines, key transcription factors, and signature effector cytokines, is provided
in Table 1.1.

1.3.1
Th2 cells
Th2 cells support humoral immunity, contribute to host defence against extracellular parasites
such as helminths, and are a key driver of atopic diseases. Their differentiation is initiated by
cytokines derived from epithelial cells, including IL­15, IL­33 and TSLP [20]. In allergic asthma,
allergen­specific Th2 cells drive disease onset and chronicity through coordinated responses
involving IgE production, eosinophil recruitment and mucus hypersecretion [9], [8].
The differentiation of naive CD4+ T cells into Th2 cells is initiated in the draining LNs and is
driven by the transcription factors STAT6 and GATA3 in a signal transduction pathway. Activa­
tion of STAT6 downstream of the IL­4 receptor leads to upregulation of GATA3, which functions
as the lineage­defining factor for Th2 cells. GATA3 facilitates chromatin remodelling at the IL­4,
IL­5, and IL­13 loci, stabilising the Th2 cytokine profile and reinforcing commitment to the Th2
lineage [21], [12]. Once committed, Th2 cells upregulate chemokine receptors such as CCR4
and CCR8, which respond to chemokines expressed in the lung microenvironment. This en­
ables their migration to the inflamed airway tissue, where they interact with antigen­presenting
and innate immune cells to maintain inflammation. Some Th2 cells persist locally as tissue­
resident memory T cells (Trm), which can rapidly respond to repeated allergen exposure and
contribute to asthma relapses [22], [8]. Th2 cells are functionally defined by their production
of IL­4, IL­5, and IL­13, and in some cases IL­9. In allergic asthma, IL­9 produced by Th9 and
other immune cells enhances mast cell accumulation, supports IgE production, and promotes
mucus secretion and AHR, linking it to persistent and severe disease [23]. These cytokines
serve as key readouts in experimental models and can be detected in various ways. Together,
these features position Th2 cells as a critical bridge between the innate and adaptive immune

downstream effector functions through cytokine release, Th2 cells orchestrate the allergic in­

1.3.2
Other T helper Subsets in Asthma
While Th2 cells are the main drivers in allergic asthma, other CD4+ T cell subsets also play
a role in the disease heterogeneity, severity, and chronicity. These include Th17, regulatory T
cells (Tregs), Th1, and Th9. Each of these subsets is characterised by its own cytokine and
immunological function.
Th1 Cells
Th1 cells are primarily known for making an immune response against intracellular pathogens
and viruses via production of the cytokine interferon­gamma (INF­γ), and are generally consid­
ered to counterbalance a Th2­driven inflammation. INF­γ activated macrophages, CD8+ cells,
NK cells, and enhances DCs antigen presentation, and also induces B cells to class­switch to­
ward IgG production. Th1 differentiation is promoted by IL­12, IL­18 and IFN­γ, which activate
the transcription factors STAT4 and STAT1. These play a role in the expression of the lineage­
determining transcription factor T­bet. Studies have shown that Th1 cytokines can contribute to
airway inflammation and remodelling, especially in severe and corticosteroid­resistant asthma
phenotypes [24], [25]. Murine HDM asthma models have shown that elevated IFN­γ has been
linked to disruption of the airway epithelium and impaired production of IL­10, sustaining chronic
inflammation. Combined Th1/Th17 responses have also been shown to interfere with glucocor­
ticoid receptor translocation in pulmonary macrophages, providing a potential mechanism for
steroid resistance [26].
Th17 Cells
This subset of CD4+ T cells is defined by their production of IL­17A, IL­17F, and TNF­α. Even­
though Th17 cells are known for their role in protection against extracellular bacteria and fungi,
Studies have shown that they are also implicated in the pathogenesis of chronic inflammatory
and autoimmune diseases, including severe asthma. Th17 cytokines induce a neutrophillic
asthma phenotype that is more severe and less responsive to corticosteroids in some cases.
IL­17A and IL­17F also act on structural airway cells, including epithelial and fibroblast popu­
lations, stimulating the release of IL­6 and IL­8 and promoting airway remodelling and tissue
damage [24].
The differentiation of Th17 cells are driven by the cytokines IL­6, IL­1β, and TGF­β, and is
regulated by the transcription factor RORγt, which is the lineage­defining transcription factor
of Th17 cells. Studies, including those on HDM­induced asthma, have shown that a Th17­
mediated response in allergic asthma can enhance airway inflammation, disrupt the epithelial
barrier, and lead to steroid resistance, while IL­17A have been linked to inhibiting Treg activity
and fibroblast proliferation [27], [6], [28].

Regulatory T cells (Tregs)
Regulatory T cells (Tregs) play a central role in maintaining peripheral tolerance, preventing
autoimmune diseases and limiting chronic inflammatory diseases by inhibiting autoreactive
CD4+ T cells that have eluded negative selection in the thymus. Tregs are characterised by
expression of the transcription factor forkhead box P3 (FOXP3), which is required for devel­
opment, maintenance, and function. In asthma a reduced Treg level or impaired function is
associated with uncontrolled disease and increased airway inflammation. Tregs exert their reg­
ulatory function through the production of the cytokines IL­10 and TGF­β, but also through
cell contact­dependent mechanisms that suppress effector T cells, including Th2 and Th17 re­
sponses [29],[30].
The functional balance between these T cell subsets probably influences asthma endotypes, re­
sponse to treatment, and disease progression. Although Th2 cells remain the dominant drivers
of allergic asthma, understanding the interaction between Th17 cells, Tregs, and Th1 cells is es­
sential for a more complete view of asthma immunopathology and may offer new opportunities
for endotype­specific therapies.
1.4
B Cells and Antibody Responses
B cells are central to the immunopathology of allergic asthma through their ability to generate
allergen­specific antibodies and interact with T cells and innate immune cells. Upon exposure
to allergens such as HDM, B cells both initiate and amplify the immune response by undergoing
activation, class switching, and differentiation into plasma cells that secrete immunoglobulins.
In addition, B cells contribute to the orchestration of the adaptive immune system by acting as
APCs, thereby shaping the T helper cell landscape. This dual role positions B cells in a crucial
of allergic sensitisation and chronic inflammation [31].
Activation of B cells typically begins when naive B cells recognise allergenic antigens via their
B cell receptor (BCR). This initial recognition provides the first activation signal and normally
occurs in the context of secondary lymphoid tissues. As illustrated in Figure 1.2, full activation
requires the integration of multiple signals. The second signal is delivered by CD4+ T helper
cells, most often T follicular helper (Tfh) or Th2 cells, via the CD40–CD40L (CD154) interac­
tion, where CD40L (CD154) expressed on activated T cells binds to CD40 on B cells. This
co­stimulatory interaction is critical for initiating germinal center formation, supporting B­cell
proliferation, and enabling class­switch recombination (CSR). The third signal is provided by
cytokines in the microenvironment, particularly IL­4 and IL­13, which activate the STAT6 path­
way and induce expression of activation­induced cytidine deaminase (AID), the key enzyme
required for CSR. These combined inputs drive the differentiation of B cells into IgE­ and IgG1­
producing plasma cells as well as long­lived memory B cells [32], [33].
Within germinal centres, B cells also undergo somatic hypermutation and affinity maturation, en­
suring that the secreted antibodies are of high affinity for their target allergen. These processes

==PAGE:17==
can occur within 4–7 days of initial allergen exposure, underscoring that even short exposure
models are capable of inducing detectable class­switched responses, provided sufficient T cell
help is present [34].
IgE is the hallmark immunoglobulin of allergic asthma and plays a pivotal role in mediating
immediate hypersensitivity responses. IgE mediates type I hypersensitivity reactions, which in­
clude both systemic and localised anaphylaxis. Although IgE has a protective role as a first­line
defence against parasites such as helminths, inappropriate IgE­mediated immune responses
to normally innocuous environmental antigens contribute to the pathogenesis of allergic dis­
eases such as asthma, allergic rhinitis, and atopic dermatitis, and in severe cases may result
in life­threatening anaphylaxis [34].
Once secreted, allergen­specific IgE binds to IgE receptors expressed on immune cells in both
tissues and circulation. Two major IgE receptors have been described: the high­affinity receptor
FcεRI and the low­affinity receptor FcεRII (CD23). FcεRI is primarily expressed on mast cells
and basophils, where its activation mediates rapid degranulation, eicosanoid production, and
cytokine release. In humans, FcεRI is also found on dendritic cells and macrophages, where
it facilitates antigen uptake, processing, and presentation, as well as cytokine secretion that
promotes Th2 differentiation. By contrast, FcεRII is expressed on B cells, where it regulates
IgE production and facilitates antigen processing and presentation, but it is also expressed on
macrophages and epithelial cells, where it mediates uptake of IgE antigen complexes. The
combined activity of FcεRI and FcεRII thus ensures that IgE has both effector functions and
immunomodulatory roles [35], [33].
Local IgE production in the airways has been documented to contribute to persistent allergic
inflammation in chronic asthma. Recent evidence further supports this concept. A study by
Nelson et al. (2025) demonstrated that memory B cells resident in the lung of IgG1+ act as
precursors for plasma cells secreting IgE in the respiratory tract. Their work showed that IgE
production in the lung is independent of systemic IgE levels. Furthermore, peribronchiolar lym­
phoid aggregates containing both B cells and Th2 cells were identified as key sites where IL­4
signalling drives memory B cells IgG1+ to undergo class switching to IgE­producing cells. These
findings highlight the functional interdependence of the responses of IgG1 and IgE in the airways
[36].
At the molecular level, IgE class switching can occur through two distinct pathways: a direct
switch from IgM to IgE, or a sequential pathway where B cells first switch from IgM to IgG1
and subsequently to IgE. Both pathways have been observed in murine models, although their
relative contribution remains debated and appears to vary between experimental systems. Se­
quential class switching via IgG1 may be particularly relevant in chronic allergen exposure,
where prolonged germinal center reactions allow multiple switching events [34]. The pathways
by which IgE is produced and maintained are not yet fully understood. Unlike IgG1, which is
Development of Allergen­specific Immune Assays in Allergic Asthma
9
==PAGE:18==
generated through an early extrafollicular phase and establishes long­lived plasma cells and
memory B cells, IgE production appears less sustained. IgE plasma cells are generally short­
lived, and IgE B cells are found at very low frequencies in vivo, making them difficult to study.
In addition, IgE B cells are difficult to identify, as serum IgE binds to FcϵRII on the surface of
most B cells, complicating phenotypic analysis [37], [38].
In murine models allergen exposure typically induces switching to IgG1. The role of allergen­
specific IgG1 is complex, as it can function both as a protective and pathogenic factor. In one
way, IgG1 can function as a blocking antibody, competing with IgE for allergen binding and
thereby preventing mast cell degranulation. On the other hand, IgG1 can also engage activat­
ing Fcγ receptors on innate immune cells, amplifying inflammatory responses [39], [40]. This
means that high IgG1 concentrations may also interfere with IgE detection in immunoassays.
A study by Zhang et al. (2019) demonstrated that allergen specific IgE ELISAs are limited in
sensitivity when IgG1 levels are high [41].
Beyond antibody production, B cells also play a crucial role as APCs. By internalising allergens
via the BCR and presenting processed peptides through MHC class II, B cells provide help
to CD4+ T cells. Co­stimulatory molecules such as CD80 and CD86 expressed by B cells
further drive the differentiation of naïve CD4+ T cells into Th2 or Tfh subsets [42]. This crosstalk
between B and T cells creates a positive feedback loop, as Tfh/Th2 cells in turn provide the
cytokine help and CD40–CD40L interactions required for sustaining B cell activation [43].
Memory B cells represent an additional layer of complexity. While plasma cells provide imme­
diate antibody­mediated protection, memory B cells ensure rapid and robust recall responses
upon reexposure to allergens. These memory responses are highly relevant for chronic asthma,
where repeated allergen encounters occur over long periods of time [44].
Taken together, B cells function not only as producers of IgE and IgG1 but also as central regu­
lators of adaptive immunity. Their dual role in antibody secretion and T­cell activation explains
why B­cell responses are a defining feature of allergic asthma and why they form the core of
the present project.

1.5
Murine Model of Allergic Asthma
Murine models are widely used and have been of invaluable importance in investigating the
pathophysiology of allergic asthma to evaluate the efficacy of new therapeutic interventions.
These models aim to elucidate key cytokines, cellular networks and pathways in the develop­
ment and effector phases of allergic asthma. This includes mimicking key hallmarks of human
asthma, like eosinophilic inflammation, AHR, elevated IgE levels, and Th2­driven immune re­
sponses. Using allergens such as HDM, the models can be tailored to resemble acute or chronic
disease phenotypes.
1.5.1
HDM as Allergen
HDM is one of the most relevant allergens used in murine models of allergic asthma. This is
because HDMs are one of the most prevalent indoor allergen sources reported to be asso­
ciated with allergic asthma. HDMs can be found almost worldwide, and HDMs such as Der­
matophagoides pteronyssinus allergens contribute to atopic sensitisation in 50–85% of asth­
matics, and are strong inducers [10]. HDM is also effective when it comes to its ability to induce
key immunopathological features of the disease without the use of adjuvants. HDM extracts
derived from D. pteronyssinus contain different bioactive components that act to promote sen­
sitisation and inflammation. An array of at least 30 allergens has been identified in the HDM D.

==PAGE:20==
pteronyssinus. This includes the major allergens like Der p 1, Der p 2, and Der p 23, and the
minor allergens including Der p 5, Der p 7 and Der p 21, each with different bioreactivity. Major
and minor allergens are classified according to their allergenicity and strict IgE reactivity. The
minor and major group of allergens account for 30% and 50% of HDM­specific IgE [10].
Der p 1 is a cysteine protease capable of disrupting tight junctions in the airway epithelium,
thereby enhancing allergen permeability. Der p 2 structurally mimics MD­2, a co­receptor for
TLR4, and can directly activate innate immune pathways. In addition to these major allergens,
HDM extracts also contain proteases that activate protease­activated receptors (PARs), micro­
bial contaminants such as lipopolysaccharide (LPS) and β­glucans that stimulate TLR4 and
Dectin­1, and chitin from mite exoskeletons, which contributes to eosinophil recruitment and in­

and resident innate immune cells, leading to the release of alarmins such as IL­33, IL­25, and
TSLP, which together orchestrate a potent type 2 inflammatory response involving Th2 cells,
ILC2s, eosinophils, mast cells, and basophils [8], [10].
In contrast, the historically dominant model based on OVA relies on sensitisation through in­
traperitoneal injection with alum as adjuvant, followed by aerosol or intranasal challenges. While
the OVA model reliably induces a Th2 response with elevated IgE, eosinophilia, and AHR, it
lacks several key features of human asthma. OVA is not a naturally occurring allergen, and
does not engage innate immune receptors in the same way as HDM. It fails to induce epithelial
barrier disruption or sustained inflammation unless prolonged protocols are used. Therefore, al­
though OVA models are still useful for studying basic immunological mechanisms, HDM­based
models are generally preferred for translational asthma research due to their capacity to reflect
both innate and adaptive features of the human disease [45], [46].
1.5.2
Chronic and Acute Asthma Models
Murine models of allergic asthma can be classified into acute and chronic based on the duration
and frequency of allergen exposure. Acute models are typically established for around two
weeks by repeated intranasal administration of HDM, often in doses ranging from 10 to 25 µg per
day. These models are characterised by marked eosinophilic inflammation, elevated serum IgE
levels, goblet cell metaplasia, and enhanced airway responsiveness to methacholine. Cytokine
profiles in acute models show a strong type 2 immune response, with abundant IL­4, IL­5, and
IL­13. These models are ideal for investigating early events in allergen sensitisation and the
mechanisms driving acute allergic inflammation [46], [47].
In contrast, chronic models extend allergen exposure for several weeks, typically four to eight,
and are used to study long­term inflammation, immune regulation, and structural changes in the
airways. Chronic HDM models reproduce hallmark features of severe and persistent asthma,
including airway remodelling with subepithelial fibrosis, smooth muscle hypertrophy, goblet cell
hyperplasia, and collagen deposition. These structural changes are associated with persistent
AHR and a change in the cellular composition of the inflammatory infiltrate, which may include

==PAGE:21==
neutrophils and monocytes in addition to eosinophils. In addition, chronic exposure leads to
more complex cytokine signatures, sometimes including IL­17 and IFN­γ alongside the canoni­
cal Th2 cytokines. Chronic models also allow the investigation of regulatory mechanisms, such
as the roles of Tregs and IL­10, and have been used to explore systemic effects of long­term
allergic inflammation, including its impact on cognitive and behavioural outcomes [8], [47].
The choice of mouse strain also significantly affects disease expression. BALB/c mice have a
Th2­biased immune system and typically develop strong eosinophilic responses and elevated
IgE, making them suitable for acute modelling. C57BL/6 mice, which have a more balanced
Th1/Th2 profile and a tendency toward neutrophilic inflammation in chronic settings, are often
preferred for long­term studies involving airway remodelling and non­Th2­driven mechanisms
[47].
1.5.3
Model Readouts
A wide range of immunological, histological, and physiological readouts is used to evaluate
murine asthma models. From a humoral perspective, total and HDM­specific IgE and IgG1 are
commonly quantified by ELISA in serum or lung homogenates. These measurements reflect B
cell activation and class­switching under the influence of Th2 cytokines, primarily IL­4 and IL­
13. In addition, cytokine concentrations in supernatants from LNs, lung­draining lymphocytes, or
bronchoalveolar lavage fluid (BAL) can be assessed to evaluate Th2, Th1, Th17, and regulatory
cytokine production. Elevated levels of IL­4, IL­5 and IL­13 are typical of type 2 inflammation,
while IFN­γ and IL­17A can indicate a tendency towards Th1 or Th17 responses. IL­9, secreted
mainly by Th9 cells and ILC2s, plays a role in mucus hypersecretion and mast cell recruitment
and is increasingly recognised as a relevant marker in allergic asthma [8].
Flow cytometry allows for detailed characterisation of immune cell populations in lung tissue and
lymphoid organs. This includes CD4+ T cell subsets (Th2, Th1, Th17, Tregs, Th9), eosinophils,
neutrophils, macrophages, and ILC2s. Specific staining panels can be used to assess intracellu­
lar cytokines, transcription factors (e.g., GATA3, T­bet, RORγt, Foxp3), and activation markers.
These analyses provide insight into the cellular composition and functional state of the immune
infiltrate.
Together, murine models of allergic asthma, particularly those based on HDM, provide a good
platform for mechanistic studies and therapeutic testing. Adjusting variables such as allergen
type, duration of exposure, and mouse strain, researchers can replicate various asthma phe­
notypes and study their underlying immunological and structural correlates in detail.

Aim and Hypothesis of the Project
2.1
Scope
This project focuses on the immunological characterisation and validation of a murine model of
allergic asthma induced by HDM. The primary aim is to evaluate allergen­specific B and T cell
responses in both an acute and a chronic model, to assess the robustness and reproducibility
of the allergic phenotype. Special attention will be given to quantifying HDM­specific IgE and
IgG1 responses, as well as profiling T cell subsets associated with allergic asthma.
Although the model is intended to be used in future studies investigating the link between allergic
asthma and cognitive impairment, this project will not directly assess cognitive function. Instead,
it will provide the foundational immunological data necessary to support the use of the model in
future investigations.
The experimental work will include:
• Sampling of relevant tissues (e.g., lungs, lymph nodes).
• Quantification and characterisation of allergen­specific B and T cell responses.
• Comparative analysis of immune responses across the two murine models to validate the
model’s consistency and applicability.
2.2
Hypothesis
It is hypothesised that allergen­specific immune assays can be developed to quantify both B
cell­derived (IgE/IgG1) and T cell­derived (Th2/Th1/Th17) responses to HDM. Using these as­
says, it is hypothesised that differences in magnitude and phenotype of HDM­specific immune
responses can be assessed between acute and chronic murine models of allergic asthma.
2.3
Aim and Objectives
This project aims to develop and characterise allergen­specific immune assays targeting B and
T cell responses in a murine model of allergic asthma induced by HDM. Furthermore, the project
seeks to explore the potential link between adaptive immune responses and cognitive impair­
ment associated with allergic inflammation. By evaluating these immunological and neurological
parameters in both acute and chronic exposure models, this project aims to validate and deepen
the understanding of the murine allergic asthma model, with particular focus on its translational
relevance.

3.5
Lymph Nodes Preparation
All procedures were performed under sterile conditions in a Class II laminar airflow (LAF) cab­

procedures.

harvested into 96­well plates and stored at ­80°C until cytokine ELISA.

Statistical Analysis
All statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism version 10. Statistical signifi­
cance was defined as p < 0.05. To determine the appropriate statistical test, data were first
evaluated for normality using the Shapiro–Wilk test, followed by an assessment of variance
homogeneity. Based on these outcomes, parametric or non­parametric tests were applied as
described below.

==PAGE:33==
Acute Model
Since the acute model contained only two treatment groups, with a small sample size (n = 3
per group), comparisons were performed using unpaired t­tests. When data were normally
distributed with equal variance (as determined by F­test), an unpaired t­test was applied. In
cases of unequal variance but normal distribution, Welch’s correction was used. If the data did
not meet normality assumptions, a non­parametric Mann–Whitney U test was applied.
Chronic Model
For the chronic model, which contained three treatment groups, comparisons were performed
using one­way ANOVA when the data were normally distributed. If equal variances were ob­
served, this was followed by Tukey’s multiple comparisons test.
In cases of unequal vari­
ance, Welch’s ANOVA was used. When normality assumptions were not met, a non­parametric
Kruskal–Wallis test followed by Dunn’s multiple comparisons test was applied.

Results
4.1
HDM Specific B Cell Response
To investigate allergen­specific B cell responses in a murine asthma model, a series of ELISA­
based assays were developed and optimised. Since allergen­specific IgE is typically present at
low concentrations in samples, a high assay sensitivity was essential. To achieve this, a Total
IgE ELISA was initially established and systematically optimised, serving both as a technical
basis for the development of a HDM­specific IgE/IgG1 assay and as a tool for the evaluation
of Total IgE responses in allergic asthma. Beyond the aim of enhancing assay performance,
the establishment of an in­house ELISA kit (MY­kit) offered the additional benefit of lowering
experimental costs while maintaining performance levels comparable to those of commercial
ELISA kits.
4.1.1
Optimisation of Total IgE ELISA: Titration of Capture and Detection
Antibodies
To optimize the Total IgE ELISA setup, a matrix of four concentrations of both capture and
detection antibodies (0, 0.2, 0.63, and 2 µg/mL) was tested using 25 ng/mL standard in all
experimental wells. Based on the heatmap results seen in Figure 4.1, the combination of 0.63
µg/mL capture antibody and 0.2 µg/mL detection antibody produced a strong and specific signal,
and was therefore selected for further experiments.
Looking at the Figure 4.1, it is seen that increasing the detection antibody concentration above
0.2 µg/mL resulted in a reduced signal. In wells without detection antibody, increasing the
capture antibody concentration still led to an increase in signal, whereas increasing the detection
antibody alone (in the absence of capture antibody) had little effect.
To evaluate background signal and assess nonspecific interactions in the assay, three types
of control wells were included, seen in Table 4.1. Blank wells containing only assay buffer (no
antibodies or standard) showed minimal absorbance (OD450−OD595 ≈0.0035), confirming a low
background signal from the plate and reagents. Wells with no capture, no detection antibody and
no standard, produced a high signal (OD = 1.249), suggesting potential nonspecific interactions
with blocking or coating components. In contrast, wells containing both capture and detection
antibodies at the highest concentration(2 µg/mL each) but no standard gave a lower signal (OD
= 0.9065), indicating that while some nonspecific signal is present, it is not only due to antibody­
plate interactions.

conditions. For consistency with other assays and higher detection potential, the 0.63 µg/mL
detection antibody in combination with 1:10 dilution was selected for future analyses in the
chronic model.

Comparison of B cell Responses in Acute vs. Chronic Asthma Models
To assess potential differences in allergen­specific B cell responses between the acute and
chronic asthma models, ELISA analyses were performed on lung homogenates. The compar­
ison included measurements of total IgE, HDM­specific IgE, and HDM­specific IgG1 (Figure
4.10).
Total IgE concentrations (dilution 1:2) were significantly higher in HDM­treated mice from the
acute model compared to the corresponding group in the chronic model (Post­exposure group)
(***p = 0.0007), suggesting a stronger systemic IgE response during acute allergen exposure
(Figure 4.10.A). When comparing absolute concentrations, HDM­challenged mice in the acute
model displayed a geometric mean of 139.1 ng/mL versus 47 ng/mL in the chronic model, cor­
responding to an approximately three­fold difference. This difference likely reflects the distinct

==PAGE:48==
exposure regimens rather than intrinsic differences in IgE biology. In contrast, no significant
differences were observed between models for HDM­specific IgE levels (dilution 1:2), either in
PBS­treated or HDM­treated mice (Figure 4.10.B). This indicates that while total IgE production
appears more pronounced in the acute setting, the allergen­specific IgE response is comparable
between the two models at this time point.
HDM­specific IgG1 levels (dilution 1:10) were significantly increased in the chronic model com­
pared to the acute model (****p < 0.0001, Figure 4.10.C). This increase may reflect ongoing
class switching and affinity maturation associated with prolonged allergen exposure, which
could contribute to the chronicity of the inflammatory response.
Together, these results show that the acute model elicits a stronger absolute total IgE response,
while the chronic model demonstrates more pronounced allergen­specific differences and en­
hanced IgG1 production. Since antibody production is tightly linked to T cell help, T cell prolif­
eration and cytokine responses were assessed next in both models.

4.2
HDM Specific T Cell Response
T cells are key orchestrators of the adaptive immune response in allergic asthma, mediating
inflammation through cytokine production and cellular proliferation upon allergen recognition.
In this section, HDM­specific T cell responses were assessed in both acute and chronic murine
asthma models using ex vivo restimulation, flow cytometry and Sandwich ELISA analysis to
characterise relevant effector subsets.
4.2.1
HDM Specific T Cell Response in Acute Asthma Model
To characterise the acute immune response, T cells were analysed in mice from the acute
asthma model. Lymph nodes (cLNs and mLNs) were collected and restimulated ex vivo us­
ing either HDM or polyclonal CD3/CD28 stimulation or remained unstimulated to assess the
intensity and phenotype of the antigen­specific T cell response.
Determining Optimal PMA/Ionomycin Stimulation Conditions for Cytokine Assays
Before assessing HDM­specific responses, the sensitivity of the assay was validated by stim­
ulating the lymph nodes of three control mice (PBS). The LNs were stimulated with a matrix
of four different concentrations of PMA and ionomycin, from two different batches of PMA and
Ionomycin: 20 ng/mL PMA + 750 ng/mL Ionomycin, 50 ng/mL PMA + 750 ng/mL Ionomycin,
20 ng/mL PMA + 1500 ng/mL Ionomycin, and 50 ng/mL PMA + 1500 ng/mL Ionomycin. The
supernatants were collected and analysed for IFN­γ concentrations using ELISA. The results
are seen in Figure 4.11.
Mice 1 and 2 were stimulated using PMA/ionomycin batch 1, and tested in two sample dilutions
(1:2 and 1:5). Both mice showed clear dose­dependent increases in IFN­γ production, with the
highest levels observed for the P20/I1500 and P50/P1500 ng/mL combinations.
In contrast, Mouse 3 was tested using PMA/Ionomycin batch 2, samples diluted 1:2 with 20/1500
and 50/750 stimulation combinations. No substantial IFN­γ induction was observed in Mouse 3,
and levels remained comparable to the unstimulated RPMI­only control. Based on these results,
PMA/Ionomycin batch 1 with the concentration 20 ng/mL PMA + 1500 ng/mL Ionomycin was
selected as the optimal stimulation condition for further experiments, as it consistently induced
a strong IFN­γ response across the tested mice and dilutions. Batch 2 was excluded due to
poor stimulation efficiency. PMA/ionomycin stimulated LNs was also included in cytokine ELISA
assays for the acute asthma model to assess the secretion of IFN­γ, IL­13, IL­5, and IL­4.
However, due to technical failure of the assay, the experiment was inconclusive, and results are
therefore not included in this thesis.

4.2.2
HDM Specific T Cell Response in the Chronic Asthma Model
To investigate allergen­induced T cell activation in the chronic asthma model, mLNs and cLNs
were analysed from two subgroups of mice. The post exposure group were sacrificed immedi­
ately after completing the two­month intranasal HDM or PBS challenge period. And the recovery
group, which was kept for an additional month without further allergen exposure, allowing for
the assessment of long­term immune activation and potential reduction of T cell responses.
This experimental design enables comparison between immediate and delayed immune pro­
files, and provides insight into the persistence of T cell activation in the absence of continued
stimulation.
Unlike the acute model, where lymph node cells were stimulated ex vivo with both HDM and
polyclonal CD3/CD28, cells from the chronic model were restimulated only with HDM extract.
T cell activation and proliferation were evaluated exclusively by flow cytometry.
T Cell Proliferation and Intracellular Cytokine Analysis Following HDM Stimulation
Flow cytometry data were analysed using a sequential gating strategy (Figure 4.17) to identify
cytokine­producing and proliferating CD4+ T cells LNs. First, live cells were gated based on
FSC­A and a viability dye (Live/Dead). T cells were then identified as TCRβ+CD4+. Within
this population, proliferation was assessed by dilution of CTV and cytokine­producing subsets
were categorised according to intracellular staining for IL­4, IL­5, IL­13, IL­17A, IFN­γ, and IL­9.
Tregs were identified as CD4+FOXP3+.

5
Discussion
The present study aimed to develop and optimise allergen­specific immunoassays to quantify
both B and T cell responses in a murine model of HDM­induced allergic asthma, with the purpose
of comparing acute and chronic exposure protocols. It was hypothesised that these assays
would allow for the detection of distinct differences in magnitude, intensisty and phenotype of
immune responses between the models. The results confirm the feasibility of this approach
while also revealing important methodological considerations, differences between acute and
chronic responses, and areas for further optimisation.
5.1
Methodological Considerations and Assay Optimisation
Optimisation experiments demonstrated the critical influence of antibody concentrations, block­
ing conditions, and HRP type on achieving high sensitivity and low background noise. The total
IgE ELISA used for optimisation of the capture and detection antibodies (Figure 4.1) showed
that increasing the detection antibody concentration above 0.2 µg/mL reduced the signal. This
suggests the presence of a threshold or plateau, most likely due to steric hindrance or satura­
tion effects [51], [52]. Increasing the concentration of capture antibody in wells without detection
antibody had a more pronounced effect on efficient antigen–antibody binding than increasing
the concentration of detection antibodies in wells without capture antibody. These observa­
tions indicate that when using samples with low antigen concentrations, the results should be
interpreted with caution, as background levels can contribute disproportionately to the observed
signal in the absence of target antigen.
The asymmetry observed in Figure 4.1.B also suggests that nonspecific binding may have con­
tributed to the background signal, potentially arising from interactions with the coating buffer,
blocking buffer, or the sAV–HRP enzyme. This is supported by the higher signals detected
in wells lacking both capture and detection antibodies compared to wells containing high anti­
body concentrations but no standard. Such findings raise the possibility that part of the signal
observed in this experiment was not antigen­dependent, but rather reflected assay artefacts.
Similar challenges have been reported in allergen­specific ELISAs. For instance, Zhang et al.
(2018) demonstrated that high IgG1 titres can interfere with IgE detection, thereby reducing as­
say sensitivity [41]. Although the present study did not directly assess IgG1 interference, this
mechanism could likewise have influenced optimisation outcomes and may partly explain the
nonspecific signals recorded.
Control well and blocking buffer comparisons (Table 4.2, 4.3) further indicated that the back­
ground signal could arise from blocking protein interactions, coating buffer composition, or the
sAV–HRP conjugate. The choice of HRP type was particularly important: HRP type 1 produced
Development of Allergen­specific Immune Assays in Allergic Asthma
58
==PAGE:67==
high background even in the absence of antigen, whereas HRP type 2 yielded a cleaner base­
line. The high background observed in the blocking buffer experiment is therefore likely linked
to both the HRP type (HRP type 1 was used) and the enzyme concentration.
Blocking buffer composition was also a key determinant. PBS supplemented with 2% skimmed
milk performed well, but a study by Gibbs et al. (2017) recommends that lower concentrations
(0.1–0.5%) may further reduce nonspecific interactions. Milk­based blockers are known for their
low cost, broad availability, and effective blocking of hydrophobic sites. However, excessive
protein content can promote weak nonspecific binding [53]. Future assay optimisation should
therefore explore the combination of HRP type 2 with lower blocking protein concentrations to
maximise the signal­to­noise ratio.
Finally, comparison between the optimised in­house ELISA kit (“MY­kit”) and the commercial
BD BioScienceELISA kit (Figure 4.2, Table 4.4 and Table 4.5) demonstrated higher sensitivity
in the MY­kit, particularly at the lower end of the standard curve. This advantage is especially
relevant for allergen­specific IgE/IgG1 detection, where concentrations are often substantially
lower than those of total IgE [41]. Obtaining such sensitivity is essential to quantify allergen­
specific responses and to assess differences between acute and chronic murine models. These
optimisations not only validate the MY­kit as a robust and cost­effective alternative to commer­
cial assays, but also support its application in both acute and chronic protocols due to its high
sensitivity.
Taken together, these methodological refinements provided the foundation for the subsequent
analyses of allergen­specific immune responses. The following section will discuss how the
optimised assays were applied to characterise B cell responses in both acute and chronic HDM­
induced asthma models, highlighting key similarities and differences between the exposure
protocols.
5.2
B Cell Responses in Acute and Chronic Asthma Models
The B cell response represents a central component of allergic asthma pathophysiology through
the production of allergen­specific immunoglobulins. In the acute model, total IgE levels in lung
homogenates (Figure 4.3 and Table 4.6) were elevated in HDM­challenged mice compared with
PBS controls, with an approximate three­fold difference at the 1:2 dilution. When comparing
OD signals between total IgE and HDM­specific IgE within the HDM­challenged group, total
IgE values were on average 1.7­fold higher. This suggests that only a proportion of the total
IgE pool was allergen­specific, which is consistent with previous observations that allergen­
driven IgE typically constitutes only part of the overall IgE repertoire in asthma [54]. However,
considerable inter­individual variability was observed. Some HDM­challenged mice showed IgE
levels that overlapped with PBS controls, while others showed markedly elevated responses,
indicating heterogeneity in sensitisation even under identical exposure conditions.
Development of Allergen­specific Immune Assays in Allergic Asthma
59
==PAGE:68==
HDM­specific IgE (Figure 4.4) and HDM­specific IgG1 (figure 4.6) showed similar trends, with
mean values elevated in HDM groups but with considerable overlap between HDM and PBS
mice. No clear dose–response pattern was observed between 10 µg and 25 µg HDM, consistent
with previous findings by Woo et al. (2018), which demonstrated that increasing allergen dose
above a sensitisation threshold produces a limited increase in responses [55].
The variability observed in the present study suggests that two weeks of exposure may be
insufficient to induce consistent allergen­specific antibody responses, a notion supported by
Woo et al. (2018), who reported that ≥4 weeks of exposure were required to reliably detect
increases in HDM­specific IgE.
In contrast, the chronic model demonstrated a more sustained and stable antibody profile. Total
IgE concentrations (Figure 4.7, Table 4.7) were elevated immediately after the two­month HDM
challenge (post­exposure group) but declined by approximately half following a one­month re­
covery period without further allergen exposure (recovery group). Although this decline sug­
gests that short­lived plasma cells contribute substantially to the acute IgE response, the per­
sistence of detectable levels of both HDM­specific IgE (Figure 4.8) and IgG1 (Figure 4.9) in all
mice after recovery indicates long­term sensitisation and the presence of immunological mem­
ory. This persistence reflects the immunological characteristic of atopy in human asthma, where
allergen­specific IgE remains measurable long after allergen exposure has ceased [56]. A re­
cent studie by Nelson et al. (2025) suggests that lung­resident IgG1+ memory B cells can serve
as precursors for IgE­secreting plasma cells during allergen rechallenge, providing a possible
explanation for the sustained local IgE responses observed even after a recovery phase.
The predominance of IL­13 observed in the chronic model provides a mechanistic link to the
enhanced IgE and IgG1 responses, as IL­13 (together with IL­4) is a key driver of class­switch
recombination in B cells [57]. This interplay between Th2 cytokine production and antibody
responses supports the notion that the chronic model captures both effector functions of allergic
inflammation in a manner highly relevant to human asthma.
Direct statistical comparison of the two models (Figure 4.10) revealed significantly higher total
IgE in the acute asthma model, whereas HDM­specific IgE and IgG1 responses were more
pronounced in the chronic model.
This highlights a key distinction: the acute protocol in­
duces a strong but short­lived IgE response, while the chronic protocol fosters more robust
and persistent allergen­specific responses. These findings strengthen the argument that the
chronic model better reflects the immunological memory and persistence characteristic of hu­
man asthma, whereas the acute model more accurately represents early­phase sensitisation
marked by rapid but less durable antibody production. Together, the data suggest that the
chronic protocol is particularly suitable for studying long­term allergic inflammation and mem­
ory responses, while the acute protocol may be more useful for reflecting the early events of
allergen sensitisation

5.3
T cell Responses in Acute and Chronic Asthma Models
T cell proliferation assays (Figure 4.13) showed stronger responses to polyclonal CD3/CD28
stimulation than to ex vivo antigen­specific HDM restimulation, consistent with the broad activa­
tion of all T cells regardless of antigen specificity. Proliferation was consistently higher in mLNs
(Figure 4.14) compared to cLNS, reflecting the anatomical drainage of antigens from the lung
to the mediastinal compartment. This finding supports prior work from Dullaers et al. (2004),
highlighting mLNs as the principal site of T cell priming in airway allergen exposure [58], [59].
It should also be noted that lymph node extraction for the acute model was performed at an
early stage of technical training, which may have introduced variability in sample quality. In
this model, cLNS were extracted by the author, while mLNs were collected by the supervisor.
Variations in dissection experience and anatomical precision could therefore have contributed
to differences in cell yield and viability between nodes, potentially influencing proliferation read­
outs.
In the acute model, HDM­specific proliferation was minimal (Figure 4.13), which could be due to
the short sensitisation period not allowing for robust clonal expansion of antigen­specific T cells,
or to reduced viability at the time of analysis. This finding is consistent with Jones et al. (2017),
who demonstrated that short exposure periods often fail to induce robust antigen­specific T cell
pools [60]. Moreover, it cannot be definitively confirmed that the proliferating cells represented
T cells alone, since surface markers were not applied and critical controls such as fluorescence­
minus­one (FMO) were lacking. This limitation implies that the observed proliferation could also
include other lymphocyte populations. Consequently, the results are not fully representative of
T cell–specific proliferation.
Similarly, in the chronic model, no FMO or unstimulated controls were included, limiting the
precision of the gating and the ability to distinguish true antigen­specific proliferation from back­
ground activation [61], [62]. Therefore, these methodological limitations should be considered
when interpreting proliferation data in both models.
The chronic model showed an increase in proliferative capacity, consistent with the extended
challenge protocol. Flow cytometry of the mLNs revealed that approximately 99% of the CD4+
T cells were proliferating, confirming that prolonged exposure promotes an increased and sus­
tained pool of responsive T cells. Although the chronic model displayed markedly higher prolif­
eration, it is critical to consider that the acute model exhibited significantly lower live cell counts,
as illustrated in Figure 4.14, with a large fraction of dead cells. Cell viability can be compro­
mised during sample preparation, particularly by technical handling, such as LN dissection,
mechanical stress during centrifugation, and processing delays [63].
Although higher proliferation was observed in the chronic model, it is important to consider dif­
ferences in cell viability between the models. In the acute model, the pool of viable cells was
substantially lower, and Figure 4.14 illustrates that a considerable fraction of cells were dead,
which may have artificially influenced the calculated proliferation percentages. Supporting this
Development of Allergen­specific Immune Assays in Allergic Asthma
61
==PAGE:70==
interpretation, Ma et al. (2010) demonstrated that low initial cell densities, as was the case
in the acute model, can trigger apoptosis and reduce viability within 24 hours [64]. Further­
more, Davis et al. (2024) reported that when using the recommended CellTrace Violet (CTV)
labelling protocol from Thermo Fisher Scientific on murine and human lymphocytes, up to 50%
of the starting cell population could be lost during labelling [65]. Taken together, these findings
suggest that the reduced viability in the acute model, both due to low starting cell density and
potential labelling­related toxicity, could have distorted the calculated proliferation percentages
and thereby complicated the comparison with the chronic model.
Looking into the Cytokine profiling, it emphasised the differences between models. In the acute
model, cytokine secretion was inconsistent, with only isolated outliers (e.g. mouse M6) showing
elevated levels of IL­4, IL­5, IL­13, or IL­17A (Figure 4.16). This could be explained by techni­
cal limitations such as cytokine degradation during storage or insufficient assay sensitivity, as
well as biological timing issues if samples were collected outside peak cytokine production win­
dows. In contrast, the chronic model demonstrated detectable subsets of IL­13, IL­9, IL­17A,
and IFN­γ T cells, along with a substantial FOXP3 regulatory T­cell population for the mLNs
(Figures: 4.20, 4.21, 4.22, 4.23). These findings highlight the interdependence of T and B cell
responses in the chronic model, where sustained Th2 activity promotes allergen­specific IgE
and IgG1 production, while concurrent Th1 and Th17 subsets contribute to the heterogeneity of
the chronic inflammatory environment.
The predominance of IL­13 cells highlights the persistence of a Th2 immune environment,
in agreement with the elevated IgE and IgG1 responses measured in the same mice (post­
exposure group, Figure 4.8, 4.9). This is consistent with the literature describing IL­13 as a
mediator of mucus hypersecretion and airway hyperreactivity in chronic asthma [57], [66]. De­
tection of cells producing IL­9, although at low frequencies, can indicate mast cell accumulation
and eosinophil recruitment associated with a more severe asthma phenotype, which is studied
in allergic asthma in a study in 2022 by Fu et al. [67], [23].
The presence of both Th1 (IFN­γ) and Th17 (IL­17A) populations suggests that the chronic
model does not represent a purely Th2­driven disease, but instead shows the heterogeneity
of chronic allergic inflammation, similar to human asthma [8]. This observation aligns with the
study by Akkoc et. al. (2022), suggesting that Th2 responses dominate during the initiation
phase of asthma, while exacerbation and chronicity are not limited to Th2 pathways, but also
require contributions from Th1 and Th17 responses [68]. Thus, the chronic model may more ac­
curately reflect the later phases of asthma pathogenesis, where mixed Th1/Th2/Th17 responses
drive persistent inflammation and exacerbations.
The relatively high proportion of FOXP3+ regulatory T cells observed in the chronic model may
reflect compensatory regulation aimed at controlling excessive inflammation during prolonged
allergen exposure. Similar increases in Tregs have been reported in chronic allergen mod­

els, where they suppress airway hyperresponsiveness while maintaining low inflammation [69],
[29]. This illustrates the balance between effector and regulatory cells of the immune system in
chronic asthma, where Tregs limit severe pathology but are insufficient to completely suppress
ongoing inflammation.
Differences in T cell proliferation and cytokine profiles were evident between acute and chronic
asthma models. However, it is important to note that cytokines such as IFN­γ, IL­17A, and IL­13
were assessed using two different assay platforms in the acute and chronic experiments. This
methodological difference introduces a limitation when directly comparing cytokine levels across
the two models, as the sensitivity and detection thresholds of the assays may not be equivalent
[70]. Consequently, while the data indicate that prolonged exposure promotes a more robust
T cell response, conclusions regarding quantitative differences in cytokine production between
acute and chronic asthma models should be interpreted with caution.
Taken together, these findings reinforce the distinction between acute and chronic models: while
the acute protocol mainly reflects early priming with weak antigen­specific proliferation, the
chronic model demonstrates both robust proliferation and the establishment of diverse effec­
tor and regulatory T cell subsets. This makes the chronic model more representative of the
complex immunopathology observed in human asthma. From a translational perspective, this
makes the chronic model a valuable platform not only for studying the persistence of allergic
inflammation but also for evaluating therapeutic strategies aimed at modulating Th2­driven an­
tibody responses or restoring the balance between effector and regulatory T cells.
5.4
Strengths, Limitations and Perspectives
A key strength of this work is the direct comparison of acute and chronic HDM­induced allergic
asthma models with simultaneous evaluation of B and T cell responses. The optimisation of an
in­house ELISA (MY­kit) provided high sensitivity at low concentrations. This enabled detection
of antibody responses in the acute asthma model while also offering a cost­efficient alternative
to commercial kits. The combination of humoral and cellular assays provided a broad overview
of allergen­specific immunity and validated the potential of these assays for future studies.
Several limitations must be acknowledged. The small group size reduced the statistical power,
particularly in the acute model, where the variability in IgE responses was high. Cytokines
were measured with different assay platforms across acute and chronic models, preventing
direct comparison. In the T cell assays, reduced viability in the acute model, lack of surface
staining, and absence of FMO/unstimulated controls in the chronic model represent significant
methodological constraints. Furthermore, antibody titres were not normalised to lung weight,
which may have exaggerated the variation between individual samples.
Future studies should prioritise larger group sizes, thereby increasing statistical power and en­
suring that observed trends reflect true biological differences rather than outliers. Standardis­

ation of assays is also important. Future studies should apply the same platforms for cytokine
quantification in both acute and chronic protocols. This would remove a major source of method­
ological bias and allow for more better comparisons of cytokine levels. Multiplex bead­based
assays or high­sensitivity ELISAs could be used based on the literature [71].
Inclusion of additional tissues and sample types, such as serum, would allow a direct compar­
ison of IgE and IgG1 quantification with the broader literature, where serum remains the most
widely used and enables translational comparison to human studies. Bronchoalveolar lavage
(BAL) fluid and mesenteric lymph nodes could further provide insight into local immune re­
sponses within the airway and the gut­lung axis, respectively. As this project also relates to cog­
nitive impairment in asthma, future studies should extend to T cell responses in brain­associated
tissues such as the meninges, in order to investigate whether HDM­specific responses originat­
ing in the lung can also propagate into the central nervous system and contribute to cognitive
dysfunction. This research could be addressed through intracellular cytokine staining and flow
cytometric analysis.
For T cell characterisation, future experiments should include more surface marker staining pan­
els to ensure proliferating cells can be associated with CD4+ or CD8+ T cell subsets. Addition
of markers such as CD45, CD3 for all T cells, CD4, CD8 for subsets, CRTH2, CCR4, and OX40
for TH2 and allergen associated, and a memory marker such as CD62L [62], [72], [73], [74],
[75]. These markers would provide information on activation status, memory differentiation.
Proper gating strategies using FMO controls and inclusion of unstimulated wells are essential
for accurate interpretation. Other analyses could also be interpreted, such as single­cell RNA
sequencing or qPCR arrays of sorted T cells could shed light on transcriptional programmes
underlying Th2, Th1, Th17, and Treg differentiation in acute versus chronic exposure.
In regards to the B cell responses, several opportunities for deeper exploration could be in­
vestigated. While this study demonstrated robust detection of IgE and IgG1, the functional
characteristics of these antibodies remain unexplored. Future work could incorporate basophil
or mast cell activation assays to test the biological activity of allergen­specific IgE. In addition,
qPCR analysis of isolated B cells could investigate the molecular pathways of class switching.
In terms of model optimisation, lengthening the acute exposure protocol to at least four weeks
could result in more consistent sensitisation [55]. Similarly, switching from C57BL/6 to BALB/c
mice may enhance Th2 skewing. Finally, incorporating longitudinal sampling at multiple time
points would provide dynamic insights into the induction, peak, and resolution phases of the
immune response.
In summary, this study provides proof­of­concept that allergen­specific assays can capture dif­
ferences between acute and chronic HDM models. Future refinements, particularly through
standardised assays, expanded tissue analysis, and advanced phenotyping, will improve trans­
latability and ensure a more accurate representation of allergen­driven immunity.

Conclusion
This thesis had the aim to develop and optimise allergen­specific immunoassays for quantifying
both B and T cell responses in murine models of HDM­induced allergic asthma. The overall aim
was to compare the acute and chronic asthma murine models. The hypothesis was that these
assays would enable detection of distinct differences in the intensity and phenotype of immune
responses between the two models.
The work successfully established and optimised an in­house ELISA (MY­kit) with higher sensi­
tivity than a commercial kit, particularly at low concentrations. This allowed for reliable quantifi­
cation of total and HDM­specific IgE and IgG1 in both acute and chronic settings. B cell analy­
ses revealed distinct response patterns across the models: in the acute model, total IgE levels
were significantly higher, reflecting a strong but short­lived systemic IgE response, whereas in
the chronic model HDM­specific IgE and IgG1 responses were more pronounced and persisted
even after a recovery period, indicating long­term sensitisation and immunological memory.
These findings underscore the value of the chronic protocol in modelling persistent allergic re­
sponses, while the acute protocol better reflects early sensitisation events.
T cell analyses further highlighted key differences between the models. The acute model was
characterised by minimal antigen­specific proliferation and inconsistent cytokine secretion, con­
sistent with insufficient exposure duration. In contrast, the chronic model exhibited robust CD4+
T cell proliferation, alongside a heterogeneous cytokine profile including IL­13, IL­17A, IL­9,
IFN­γ, and a substantial FOXP3+ regulatory T cell response. This mixed immune profile closely
reflects the complexity of human asthma, in which Th2 responses dominate sensitisation but
Th1 and Th17 subsets contribute to chronicity and exacerbations.
Despite these achievements, several limitations should be noted, including small group sizes,
methodological differences between assays, and the absence of standardised controls in flow
cytometry. Nevertheless, the study provides proof of concept that allergen­specific immunoas­
says can sensitively quantify both B and T cell responses across different exposure protocols.
Future perspectives include expanding sample sizes, standardising assay platforms, and in­
corporating serum analysis, bronchoalveolar lavage, and advanced flow cytometry panels to
refine immune characterisation. Extending acute exposure or using Th2­biased strains such
as BALB/c mice could enhance sensitisation outcomes. Ultimately, by combining improved
methodological approaches with comparative modelling, future work will strengthen our under­
standing of the mechanisms underlying allergen­specific immunity and provide a more translat­
able framework for studying asthma pathogenesis.

7.1
Original Total IgE ELISA Kit Protocol
Day1: 96­well plates were coated with 50 µL/well with capture antibody diluted 1:250 in 0.1 M
sodium carbonate coating buffer (pH 9.5). Plates were sealed and incubated overnight at 4°C.
Day 2: Plates were washed three times with PBS + 0.05% Tween­20 wash buffer, then blocked
with 250 µL/well assay diluent (PBS + 1% BSA + 0.05% Tween­20) for ≥1 h at room temperature
with shaking. After washing three times with wash buffer, a standard curve was prepared in
assay diluent by twofold serial dilution. Samples were diluted in assay diluent and added in
duplicate at 50 µL/well, along with standards and blanks. Plates were sealed and incubated
overnight at 4°C with shaking. Day 3: Plates were washed six times with wash buffer before
adding 50 µL/well detection antibody diluted 1:500 in assay diluent, containing streptavidin­
HRP–HRP diluted 1:250. Plates were sealed and incubated for ≥1 h at room temperature with
shaking. After seven washes (30 s soak after each), 100 µL/well of prepared substrate solution
(TMB diluted 1:35 in peroxide solution; peroxide diluted 1:35 in distilled water) was added and
incubated for 30 min at room temperature, protected from light. The reaction was stopped with
50 µL/well 0.18 M sulfuric acid. Absorbance was measured at 450 nm with 595 nm as the
reference wavelength within 30 min of stopping the reaction. Concentrations were calculated
from the standard curve generated on each plate. Specifications of the kit is seen in Figure 7.1.
