Executive Summary 
The increase in globalization and tele-communication over the last decades, has given 
businesses access to nearly free instant global communication. The ability to instantly 
communicate with people on the other side of the globe greatly enhances the ability to 
work together over vast geographical distances. This in combination with the rapid 
development of a high-educated workforce in India and China has enabled professional 
services firms to create offshore work centres in low-cost areas to lower the price of 
running their business. An increasing number of businesses have done this over the last 
decade, which increases competitiveness and forces other businesses to follow.  
However, running an offshore office has shown to be a complex task and many 
businesses fail to meet the expected goals, resulting in financial losses. Therefore, this 
paper will investigate how a professional service firm can facilitate the process of 
offshoring. This paper will provide practitioners with a framework that can help achieve 
goals. 
Addressing the Research Gap and Assisting Practitioners 
There is a general lack of guidelines and frameworks to follow by practitioners, when it 
comes to offshoring of engineering tasks. Models exist on how to increase the possibility 
for success of offshoring on a generic level, but none of them provide guidance for an 
engineering company. The provided frame work and set of guidelines are based on a 
longitudinal case study of an engineering company. 
Survey Research, Literature Review and Case Study 
The factors that have impact on offshore outcome have been determine by a thorough 
literature review. From the literature review several factors were found. A case study was 
conducted where the strategies of the company were assessed. A series of semi-structured 
interviews within the case company were carried out to investigate which of these factors 
had impacted the case firm. This resulted in the factors being narrowed down to 7 key 
factors, which have great impact on offshoring success of engineering tasks.   
Practical implications 
The guidelines were developed from the case 
study and literature review. Practitioners are 
advised to consider these guidelines when 
facilitating and offshoring process, to ensure 
the highest possible chance of success.  
Conclusion 
The literature review and the qualitative 
investigation created the foundation for development of a theoretical framework and a set 
of guidelines for practitioners. This frame work and guidelines have not been tested on 
the case company, but were created from the experiences the company made during their 
turnaround. Further research should be conducted to confirm the framework and ensure 
rigidity.

Abstract 
Offshoring is a common method for reducing manufacturing costs, gaining access to 
resources, and establishing the firm in new markets. For professional service firms, 
offshoring creates the need for sharing work across vast geographical and cultural 
distances. The purpose of this paper is to examine how professional service firms can 
facilitate offshoring and work sharing between a headquarter (HQ) and its offshored office. 
By reviewing current literature on offshoring, this paper develops a set of propositions that 
each partially explain how a company succeeds and reaches their strategic goals of running 
an offshore office. These propositions are subsequently validated using an in-depth study 
of a case firm in Denmark with offshored branches in China and India. Results show that 
success depends on the firm’s ability to minimize geographical and cultural distances 
through internet-based communication, standardizing projects, creating incentive for both 
the onshore and offshore office, management’s degree of involvement, the details of the 
implantation plan and not all tasks are equally suited for offshoring or work sharing. This 
paper contributes to the literature of management of offshore offices. For practitioners, this 
paper develops a set of guidelines that management of a professional service firm can 
apply.

1 Introduction 
Business managers are always looking for ways to increase the profit margin of their 
company. This can basically be done in two ways: by reducing the cost of running the 
business or increase the sale price of their product. However, in a modern globalized 
economy, competition is higher than ever before (Gallouj & Djellal; 2011). Therefore, a 
reduction of cost is a more viable option.  
A professional service firm is a company that has a high level of intellectual property, and 
often employs staff with a college degree. It can be any organization that offers 
customized, knowledge-based services to clients as defined by Von Nordenflycht (Von 
Nordenflycht 2010). Before globalization, professional service firms were not under as 
high a pressure from competitors, due to the limitation in the movement of employees 
and customers. In today’s globalized world, competitiveness has increased. Professional 
service firms can now hire employees and bid on projects all over the world. This has 
forced the industry to cut their costs (Lewin & Peeters 2006).  
A company can utilize globalization by creating new offices in other countries where the 
cost of labour is lower than where the HQ of the company is located. It has become possible 
to manage an overseas office from the HQ due to the rapid development in 
telecommunication over the last decades (Kroll & Bardhan 2003).  
Offshoring differs from outsourcing, as outsourcing is defined by the act of buying a service 
or product from a supplier, and offshoring is when “firms relocate their business functions 
… to overseas locations. … while maintaining full ownership and control” (Kedia & 
Debmalya Mukherjee b 2009, p. 250). Work sharing specifically refers to a task where 
work is split between HQ and an offshore office, and employees have to work together as 
one team on solving the task.  
Sharing the workload between the HQ in a high labour-cost country, and an offshore office 
in a low-cost country can decrease the cost of a project. 
Many CEOs have been looking to offshoring and work sharing to reduce their total cost of 
running their business. This can be a necessary step to win essential orders from clients 
(Matthew Heller 2016). Therefore, businesses are willing to invest a large amount of time 
and money in creating new offshore offices and hire new employees. This makes the 
success of an offshore office have utmost importance to a company. However, “In early 
2005, both the Boston Consulting Group and Gartner predicted that 50% of the offshoring 
contracts that companies in North America had signed between 2001 and 2004 would fail 
to meet expectations” (Aron & Singh 2005, p. 3). Levina & Ross states that “about one of 
every three outsourcing contracts targeting cost reductions failed to match expectations” 
(Levina & Ross 2003, p. 332). Other articles point to the risk of failure when offshoring 
(Dibbern et al. 2008; Spohrer et al. 2011; Hutzschenreuter, Lewin & Dresel 2011; S. Kumar 
et al. 2009). 
This gap between perceived goal and realized outcome from work sharing and offshoring 
has resulted in the following research question (RQ): 
How does a global professional service firm best facilitate an offshoring and work 
sharing process?

2 Literature review 
Offshoring is becoming more common than ever before. Traditionally, white collar jobs 
were not being considered at the risk of getting moved to the east (Hutzschenreuter, 
Lewin & Ressler 2011). The last couple of decades many Asian countries have developed 
a work force that is well-educated, trained and willing to work for a much lower salary 
then in Europe. Therefore many companies are looking to reduce costs of running their 
business by moving east to countries like India and China (Larsen et al. 2012; Ellram et 
al. 2008; Hutzschenreuter, Lewin & Dresel 2011; Lewin & Peeters 2006; Tseng et al. 
2016; Spohrer et al. 2011; Alexandre et al. 2006). 
The next sections will focus on reviewing current literature within the area of offshoring, 
to investigate what challenges to be aware of, and what factors can have an effect on the 
outcome of offshoring success.  
2.1 
Challenges 
When moving parts of a business to an overseas location, a company faces many 
challenges that can put the company’s investment at risk (Aron & Singh 2005). The first 
obvious barrier is the language (Gooris & Peeters 2014; S. Kumar et al. 2009; Larsen et 
al. 2012). Having a limited ability to communicate greatly decreases the ability for two 
offices to work together. However there has been an increase in ability to speak English 
in both Europe and China during the last decades (Kroll & Bardhan 2003). India is 
another very common choice of an offshoring country. Here the educated part of the 
population speaks English fluently, which helps to break down the language barrier 
(Blinder & Blinder 2006, Bain 2005). This has made India the popular choice for 
offshoring of tasks that require more information than standardized tasks (Lewin & 
Peeters 2006). Mastering the same language is not the only barrier when communicating. 
Culture plays a big part of what can and cannot be said. This is known as the cultural 
barrier. In China, staff will do anything to save face, which has a negative impact on 
knowledge sharing. (Alexandre et al. 2006). Most Asian countries uses high-context 
communication, where they leave something unsaid, letting the culture explain. In 
northern Europe there is a low-context communication, where a lot of information is 
given and it is excepted that the listener will make the right decision based on the 
information (Hall 1976). This can work in mixed teams complicated. The cultural barrier 
must be broken to achieve success. An increase of social interaction between co-workers 
from the different offices can counter the cultural barrier (Alexandre et al. 2006; 
Hutzschenreuter, Lewin & Dresel 2011). This social interaction and relation building are 
==PAGE:7==
6 
 
known in the literature as boundary spanning. Boundary spanning is defined as “activities 
to foster and improve personal relationships between onshore and offshore team 
members” (Spohrer et al. 2011, p. 2) Henrik Holt Larsen underlines the importance of 
employees: “… see themselves and are seen by others as an intact social entity embedded 
in one or more larger social systems” (Larsen et al. 2016, p. 32) 
The geographical distance also has an impact on how easy it is to manage an office. The 
preferred offshoring locations are India, China, Philippines and Malaysia (Lewin & 
Peeters 2006). These countries are geographically distanced from Europe and are in a 
different time zone. To manage a geographically distant team is called distance 
management, and it is a discipline that requires the manager to be able to set direction, 
goals, facilitate teamwork, motivate and empower staff through telecommunication 
(Larsen et al. 2016). 
The most important factor when offshoring is to ensure a good communication from the 
HQ to the offshore office and back, both between managers and within teams. Failing to 
do so can lead to quality issues, performance issues, power imbalance, lack of motivation, 
high employee turnover and ultimately to the failure of the offshoring investment (K. 
Kumar et al. 2009; Krishna et al. 2004; Gray et al. 2011; Aron & Singh 2005; 
Hutzschenreuter, Lewin & Dresel 2011; Antras et al. 2006). A strong communication can 
help teams achieve their goals even when handling complex tasks. Complex tasks require 
more communication due to an increased level of technical details or customization. This 
is often seen in offshoring of engineering tasks, since engineering tasks have a high level 
of details that require a scientific understanding (Larsen et al. 2012; Ellram et al. 2008; 
Lewin & Peeters 2006; Lewin et al. n.d.). 
There is a strong positive association between a company’s offshoring experience and 
their ability to reach the strategic goal. This is because many companies start their 
offshoring following a learning by doing tactic. This gathering of information and 
experience on the operational level, and the learnings of the failures it brings, can then be 
transferred and formulated into an implementation plan. If a company has an 
implementation plan they are more likely to succeed (Maskell et al. 2017; Larsen et al. 
2012; Larsen et al. 2016; Lewin et al. 2009; Lewin & Peeters 2006; Hutzschenreuter, 
Lewin & Dresel 2011; S. Kumar et al. 2009). 
When offshoring, the general concern is high employee turnover. High turnover of 
knowledgeable workers can cause a loss of intellectual property and put the uniqueness of 
a product at risk. In professional service firms, there is a relatively long training period. 
During this period, an employee will not perform on a high level. Moreover, the 
employees who leave a project will not be able to transfer all their knowledge to the new 
employee. Furthermore, a high turnover can also make it difficult to achieve success, 
because communication and trust builds on social relations. These relations take time to 
build and every time a new employee is introduced, these relations must have time to 
evolve (Dibbern et al. 2008; Taylor & Bain 2005; Ellram et al. 2008; S. Kumar et al. 
2009; Ellram et al. 2008; Lewin & Peeters 2006). 
2.2 
Change management 
When business managers want to implement a change, it is very important that they are 
deeply involved and committed to implement the change. From general theory on change 
==PAGE:8==
7 
 
management it is known, that managers must persist if they want to sustain a change, and 
convert it to a permanent change (Paton & McCalman 2008). From the article 
(Hutzschenreuter, Lewin & Dresel 2011) it is known, that this principle also applies when 
the change is offshoring. If the management does not show commitment, or often change 
the strategy, the employees might think that the implementation could fail 
(Bandyopadhyay & Pathak 2007; Paton & McCalman 2008; Larsen et al. 2012; Lewin & 
Peeters 2006) A method to ensure staff commitment and to avoid internal resistance is to 
empower employees. To give new responsibilities to a staff member can be seen as a trust 
from the manger to the employee. Most employees will respond positively to 
empowerment, and will not let their manager down. Typically, empowerment in an 
offshore situation would mean that the manager partly gives away power to coach others 
and set goals for a team. The purpose of this is to promote an employee’s self-
development, the person’s ability to share knowledge and their ability to involve others 
(Spohrer et al. 2011; Taylor & Bain 2005; Tate & Ellram 2012; Levina & Ross 2003; 
Larsen et al. 2016). 
2.3 
Knowledge sharing 
The ability to involve others and to share knowledge is the crucial point in knowledge 
work, especially when working over vast distances and with different cultures. This is 
even more crucial when working in teams where there is a mix of high- and low-context 
cultures. In these situations there will be a high level of tacit knowledge, and to achieve 
success the knowledge must be extracted (Bain 2005; K. Kumar et al. 2009; Ellram et al. 
2008; Ahmad & Schroeder 2003; Wong et al. 2007). 
Knowledge sharing over vast distances can be challenging, and it can limit transparency, 
because knowledge of projects are not evenly distributed throughout the organisation. 
The HQ can have difficulties gaining insight and understanding who is working on which 
projects, when employees are spread over many different offices. It can also lead to the 
offshore staff failing to understand managerial choices from HQ. It is therefore important 
for management to understand how knowledge is shared, and facilitate the offshoring 
process in a manner, so the knowledge can move from individual, to group, to 
organisation and back to individual (Hansen & Rasmussen 2010).  
2.4 
Risk management 
Managers are advised to be aware of risk factors, and be aware of which risks are most 
likely to hit their business when offshoring. In the literature, six risk factors are defined. 
The two factors that managers can influence after having established an offshore office 
are identified as strategic and operational risks. Operational risks consist of, but are not 
limited to: quality, staff, technology, process and organization. Strategic risks consist of: 
but are not limited to, planning, implementation, decision making and measuring (Gray et 
al. 2011; S. Kumar et al. 2009; Aron & Singh 2005). The risk factors that are 
nation/location dependent are country and compliance risks. These two factors consist of 
socio-economic conditions of the country, which is the wealth, health and education, 
level of a population, the political stability of a nation and the laws and regulations. As 
business managers cannot affect these factors, they have to be considered before choosing 
an offshore location. There is also a risk of reputation, this can affect the overall sales of a 
company. The last factor is the credit risk. The organisation that chooses to offshore must 
fully understand the accounting laws in their home country and offshore country under 
the circumstances of offshoring.

2.6 
Propositions 
The articles reviewed have focused on offshoring in general, by assessing multiple cases 
and developing recommendations that cover many types of businesses (Larsen et al. 
2012; S. Kumar et al. 2009; Hutzschenreuter, Lewin & Dresel 2011; Aron & Singh 
2005). Some articles have investigated specific IT companies (Lacity et al. 2009; Spohrer 
et al. 2011; Levina & Ross 2003). To the best of the researcher's knowledge, there has not 
been a single case study on an engineering service firm.  
In the following, the researcher will develop a set of propositions there are to be tested on 
professional engineering service firm.  
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
A global professional service firm is characterized by having a high level of knowledge 
work, well-educated and trained employees, a high level of customizable products and 
often work is project based. This leads to a high level of complexity. With this and the 
literature review in mind, the following propositions are developed and will be tested: 
P1: That firms with experience in offshoring have a higher possibility to succeed in their 
attempt to offshore new processes 
P2: Companies with an implementation strategy have higher success rate than companies 
without one 
P3: A continuous flow of tasks, with high standardization and repeatable work is better 
suited for offshoring than tasks that vary in content and frequency  
P4: All types of tasks can be work shared 
In addition to these propositions, two more propositions are inductively developed during 
the case study. To the best of the researcher’s knowledge this has not previously been 
described in any literature  
P5: The offshore office must prioritize tasks from the HQ over local clients, to be able to 
deliver work of an acceptable quality 
P6: If decisions regarding which tasks to offshore/work share are made in top 
management rather than at operational level, they yield a higher success rate. 
Proposition will in the following be referred to as (P1), (P2), (P3), (P4), (P5) & (P6) 
3 Methodology 
This section will describe the research, the methods used to gather data and the approach 
of ensuring validity and reliability. 
 
Due to the complexity and explorative nature of the research question, a case study 
approach is selected (Meredith & Mccutcheon 1993). 
The case company is the Danish professional service firm Pharma Engineering (PE). It 
was selected because they have managed to turn around their offshoring and work sharing 
operations by changing their strategy and method of facilitation. The research will be 
conducted from within the department that facilitates the offshoring and work sharing in 
PE. A case study is defined as “… an empirical research that primarily uses contextually 
rich data from bounded real-world settings to investigate a focused phenomenon” 
(Barratt et al. 2011 p. 1).  
 
This case study is improved in its rigidity by the fact of it being a longitudinal case study. 
Prior to this research, Flojgaard investigated a similar problem in 2010 (Flojgaard 2010). 
He conducted a research on how the company work shared and how the employees were 
engaged in the process. His work was conducted by the use of exchange and expectancy 
theory. In 2010 Karjakina & Semtsenko did a report on the offshoring strategy (Karjakina 
& Semtsenko 2010). By assessing this a longitudinal study on how a company can turn 
around and produce good results with offshoring is achieved. A longitudinal single case 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
study will decrease the variance and therefore increase the comparability of the first 
results to the newest results. 
 
Data will be collected by semi-structured interviews and reviewing of internal documents 
from the company. The company has been through two states of offshoring and work 
sharing. The first state ran from 2002 to the beginning of 2016 where top management 
implemented a new collaboration strategy that changed the facilitation of offshoring and 
work sharing. Therefore, this study will investigate the previous state and the current state 
of work sharing and offshoring.  
An unstructured interview was conducted with the vice president of the company. This 
interview served the purpose of getting a historical knowledge of the company and its 
operations and understanding how the organization performed previously. The interview 
was scheduled to take 45 minutes and took 38 minutes. The interview was recorded and 
transcribed (Appendix X). The researcher was present at meetings with the top 
management, when they decided which parts of an order to offshore or work share. These 
meetings were not recorded due to confidentiality, but several important observations 
were made.  
A semi-structured interview schedule was developed following the principals described 
by Barrett and Yin (Barratt et al. 2011; Yin 2003). The questionnaires consisted of 10 
open questions that the respondent should answer to the best of their knowledge (Apendix 
1). The questions were the same for all participants. To ensure an investigation of high 
validity and reliability, the interview was carried out with staff from all organisational 
levels. In collaboration with a HR-specialist, 20 persons were selected and invited. The 
criteria for the selection was that the persons had at least 3 experiences with offshoring or 
work sharing within PE. 13 interviews were conducted in total and were scheduled to last 
between 15 min and 35 min. In the following, persons will be referred to by short form.

The persons who were located in India were interviewed by a Skype connection. 
All interviews conducted were recorded, partially transcribed and confirmed by the 
respondent, following the condensation principle (Malterud 2012). The analysis followed 
4 steps: (1) reading all the material to obtain an overall impression and bracketing 
previous preconceptions (Tufford & Newman 2012); (2) identifying answers that 
represented the attitude of the participant towards offshoring and work sharing, and 
coding for these; (3) condensing and summarizing the contents of each of the coded 
groups; and (4) creating generalizing descriptions of concepts stated by the participant. 
Quotes from the interviews were translated from Danish to English by the researcher in 
the process of writing this article. Essential quotes and result of the coding can be seen in 
section 5.2  
To insure validity of the findings, any results must be triangulated. Thus, any claim made 
in an interviewee must be backed from either another interview or documents, lists or 
observations made within the company.

4 The Case 
This section will contain a detailed description of the case company and show its 
development with work sharing and offshoring.  
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
Pharma Engineering is an alias to respect the company's request for confidentiality. PE is 
a developer of pharmaceutical production equipment and manufacturing facilities. It is a 
service business that does project work and compete on the global market to win projects. 
PE therefore has a high variance in work load, depending on the size of the orders they 
win. Therefore, PE uses many consultants/contract workers (around 30% of their current 
HQ engineers) that they can let go when there is a low work load.  
4.1 
Pre-state  
A timeline of PE’s development can be viewed in figure 1. 
PE was unbundled from a major pharmaceutics developer and producer in the beginning 
of the 90’s. In the late 1990’s PE obtained a major project in Tianjin. The project was 
delivered by Danish engineers who stayed in the area while the factory was being 
developed, build and tested. Upon request from a strategic client, PE was asked to stay in 
Tianjin to provide maintenance and the ability to rapidly execute new projects in China 
for the client. Therefore, PE decided to stay and start a permanent office in Tianjin in 
2000. 
The office consisted of about 20 employees. These employees served the strategic client 
and delivered minor pharma projects to the area. In the 00’ there was growth in western 
pharma manufacturers entering the Chinese marked. This caused increase in the demand 
of PE’s services. HQ middle managers had trouble recruiting enough engineers in the 00’ 
and therefore in 2002 they started to utilize the Chinese office by offshoring task. After 
some mixed results, it got to the attention of the HQ management that the Chinese office 
could be used as a low-cost work centre. They decided to create a strategy for the Chinese 
office. The target of the strategy was two folded: (1) to capture new clients in the rapidly 
growing Chinese market and (2) increase the work sharing and offshoring between the 
HQ in Denmark and China. 
The purpose of the work sharing and offshoring was to lower the costs of projects, since 
Chinese engineers are paid less than Danish engineers. As a result, the Chinese office was 
expanding rapidly from 20 employees in 2000 to 450 employees in 2012. This growth 
was mostly driven by the growth in the Chinese market 
 
Figure 1 Timeline of PE's offshoring development 
The first tasks that were offshored were piping diagrams and 3D sketches. These 
disciplines are characterised by being simple, standardized and repeatable. The Chinese 
office managed to solve these tasks to a satisfying level. The coherency and 
communication was sufficient. Therefore, DK management increased the target for how 
many hours should be work shared or offshored from Denmark to China. This increase in 
target resulted in project managers trying to offshore and work share more advanced 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
tasks. However, these tasks were a major failure and required a large amount of hours 
spend by the Danish office on reworking the projects. 
Simultaneously, with the bad results of the advanced tasks, the quality on the simpler 
tasks were also going down, causing the need for reworking. This caused delays and 
exceedance of project budgets, which gave offshoring a bad reputation in the HQ of PE. 
Project managers would only use it if there were no other alternatives.  
 
These low-quality issues were due to multiple facts: 
The Danish office did not provide a continuous stream of work to the Chinese office. 
Instead they only used work sharing and offshoring when the Danish office was busy.  
There was a very high demand of PE’s services in China. Having the Chinese office 
generate work to themselves meant that they were not dependent on receiving tasks from 
the Danish HQ. Ultimately this resulted in the Chinese management down prioritizing the 
tasks they received from the Danish HQ, meaning that the offshoring tasks were 
secondary to the local Chinese pharma marked. It resulted in lower skilled/less 
experienced engineers being put on the tasks of offshoring. The Chinese engineers fell 
more committed to the local clients, because they would meet them in person and have on 
site visits. The high demand for engineers in China created a high employee turnover of 
about 75%. This in term made the communication, knowledge sharing and training 
difficult. It also led to a low level of boundary spanning.  
 
Prior to 2016 there was no implementation strategy for offshoring and work sharing, there 
was only a goal of how many hours to offshore. This resulted in upper management 
continuously telling the project managers to offshore, without appointing anybody 
responsible for doing it or specifying what to offshore. There was no clear 
communication from management to the organisation on why they should offshore. The 
project managers had a high resistance as they found it too risky. 
 
In 2007 PE bought a competitor who had a small office in India. The Indian office 
consisted of 50 employees, they served as offshoring and work sharing centre for the 
competitor and had only very few local tasks. PE continued that setup, but the poor 
reputation that work sharing had gained in the HQ, led to only a few project managers 
wanting to use the Indian office.  
 
From 2002 until 2012 PE’s top management was not very concerned about the errors 
within work sharing and offshoring and the targets not being met. Since there was not 
much competition on the market, customers tended to have high loyalty to their service 
provider. So when negotiating with clients, PE did not feel a pressure on the price and did 
not react to the poor performance of the offshore offices.  
 
In 2012 PE started to get pressured on price from clients and subsequently got turned 
down by a couple of clients in 2012, 2013 and 2014. Competitors were able to deliver 
similar projects to a lover hourly price than PE. Because of this, the top management 
decided in late 2014 to develop a new strategy on collaboration that should lower the 
average hourly price of their services from 1.050 DKK to 850 DKK

4.2 
Current state 2016 
The contract manager and the Vice-president for sales and business support were 
responsible for developing a new strategy that could reach the goal of lowering the 
average hourly price to gain competitiveness.  
The strategy they developed was implemented by the first of March 2016 and consisted 
firstly of clearly communicating the burning platform to the organisation. “it is important 
that we are competitive on price. If we fail that, then we will all loose our jobs” - VP2 
(table 2) 
Secondly, the purpose of the strategy was communicated. There were two main goals: (1) 
to lower the price and gain competitiveness (2) to have access to flexible resources in the 
form of engineering staff in India and China.  
Clear goals were set up. India must deliver no less than 20.000 hours and China must 
deliver 30.000 hours. Each department must deliver a minimum of 10% of work sharing 
or offshoring on green field A-project (appendix 2)  
India has to expand their staff to no less than 100 full time engineers delivering work to 
the HQ by the end of 2018. There should not be any growth in Chinese offshoring and 
work sharing. They have to continue delivering the few tasks that they have had success 
with so far. So called communication bridges have to be established between the HQ and 
the offshore offices.  
These bridges must follow one of three setups.  
(1) An ambassador of work sharing is placed in a department in Denmark. It is his task to 
gather tasks that are suitable for work sharing from his department and distribute it to the 
offshore engineers. The goal is to have a ratio of 70% in India and 30% in Denmark. 
Meaning that when a Danish engineer spends 1 hour, on a task that is work shared, 2,33 
hours of the work must be provided from an offshore office. 
(2) An Indian engineer is moved to Denmark for the first three weeks and the last three 
weeks of a major project. This is best suited for projects of a longer duration. The goals 
are the same 70/30 cost ratio split. 
(3) The last option of collaboration with offshore offices is the packaged offshoring, 
where all resources that can solve a certain type of task are located in India or China. This 
is already done with PID modelling, where the skill is not present in the HQ and the only 
office who can handle this task is located in China. This is what PE defines as a packaged 
solution and give a split of 100/0. Packages are also called offshore services or products.

6 Analysis 
The data gathered from the interviews, the observations and the listings were analysed. 
The first step was to examine all statements and if any statements contradicted any of the 
propositions. No statement or other data contradicted propositions 1, 2, 3, 5 or 6. Several 
statements confirmed these propositions. However, evidence was found that contradicted 
proposition 4. 
The previous state of offshore facilitation has been described as: inconsistent, 
disorganized, poorly communicated, with a low level of boundary spanning, low level of 
management commitment, poorly formulation of goals and measures and poor planning. 
Hutzschenreuter, Lewin & Dresel point to the importance of keeping a homogenous level, 
both of the flow of tasks, but also in the communication from management and their 
planning (Hutzschenreuter, Lewin & Dresel 2011). They also point out, that not doing so 
greatly increases the risk of failing. Research emphasize the importance of choosing a 
suitable and structured method of facilitating the offshoring process(Aron & Singh 2005; 
S. Kumar et al. 2009). Larsen et al. tells us that to manage staff in an offshore office 
demands extraordinary clear communication and goal setting (Larsen et al. 2016). 
Considering this, there is a clear match between how PE facilitated their offshoring prior 
to the new strategy and their failure to meet their expectations. As seen in table 4 & 5 and  
question 4  
By making a clear implementation strategy, PE stabilized their facilitation of offshoring. 
This led to a clear communication of the goals and the organization could now focus on 
boundary spanning. This has led to improved results which meet the expectations of the 
management as seen from table 6, plot 1 and question 5. However not 5 of 13 persons’ 
respondent no change. Of these there were an overrepresentation of operational staff. 
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
PE started with a spontaneous approach to offshoring and work sharing, which is 
common amongst firms that have global presence. This often leads to a disorganized 
method of facilitating (Lewin & Peeters 2006; Hutzschenreuter, Lewin & Ressler 2011). 
The experience that the organization gathered in this period is crucial to how well they 
can form a new implementation plan. The case study follows the literature and terms of 
success are based on experience, planning and management commitment (Larsen et al. 
2012; Hutzschenreuter, Lewin & Dresel 2011;Aron & Singh 2005). 
7 Discussion 
In the following section, an interpretation of the findings and the analysis will be given. 
This has led to a broadened understanding of the subject and how this study has moved 
the understanding of the field forward. 
Propositions (1), (2) and (3) were deductively developed and have prior to this study been 
tested on a generic level of professional service firms. This study aims to clarify if these 
generic assumptions also apply to engineering.  
From the case study and findings, it has been confirmed that (P1) companies with 
experience in offshoring are more likely to succeed. This has also been confirmed in the 
literature (Maskell et al. 2017; Larsen et al. 2012; Larsen et al. 2016; Lewin et al. 2009; 
Lewin & Peeters 2006; Hutzschenreuter, Lewin & Dresel 2011; S. Kumar et al. 2009). To 
the best of the researcher’s knowledge there was no literature on how to utilize this 
experience. A crucial point is that the experience alone does not yield success, it is the 
management’s ability to evaluate and react to the experiences they make along their way. 
Simply having several years of experience without evaluating and reacting can lead to 
frustration in the whole organisation. This leads to the answering of proposition (P2). By 
comparing the results from before PE had an implementation strategy, in table 4 and 5, to 
after they have implemented their new strategy, in table 6, a higher performance of their 
offshore departments is seen. By assessing the statements given by staff and managers, 
having an implementation strategy has a positive effect on a company’s success rate. This 
confirms earlier research (S. Kumar et al. 2009;  Paton & McCalman 2008; Larsen et al. 
2012; Lewin & Peeters 2006). From the case study and findings, it can be stated that (P3) 
there is an association between having a continues flow of standardized tasks and success. 
This confirms statements in the literature (Bandyopadhyay & Pathak 2007; Paton & 
McCalman 2008; Larsen et al. 2012; Lewin & Peeters 2006; Hutzschenreuter, Lewin & 
Dresel 2011Hutzschenreuter, Lewin & Dresel 2011Hutzschenreuter, Lewin & Dresel 
2011) 
Assessing proposition (P4): Findings showed that if tasks are especially complex, have a 
high degree of customization, demand on going communication with the client and 
require several site visits, then there could be little to no savings by work sharing. Due to 
the fact that achieving an hour split ratio of 70/30 between India and Denmark would be 
impossible. Proposition (P4) was rejected. This has not been stated in any articles 
reviewed, and is therefore considered new knowledge and a contribution to the literature. 
“In engineering companies, tasks that are customized, complex, requires multiple 
customer visit and client communication are not suited for offshoring”  
This needs to be further tested on other case studies to be confirmed.  
==PAGE:23==
22 
 
Findings did also support proposition (P5) by the statements given and the performance 
before and after the new strategy. This proposition was developed inductive and was not 
found in any literature. Therefore, further research is needed on the area of incentive 
structure of offshore offices. From the findings, this research will state that an offshore 
must not have strategic, local clients of their own, to insure a strong incentive to deliver 
quality work to their HQ.  
This statement is extracted from the results of a sample size of one case study with two 
offices, and must therefore be tested on a generic level to be confirmed or rejected.  
There was a positive association between moving decision making of offshoring tasks 
from project managers to top management and offshoring outcome. Proposition (P6) 
confirmed. This proposition was inductively developed. But it was made clear to the 
researcher, that increasing the commitment of management has been showed in multiple 
articles and books to have a positive association with the success of a change 
management operation (Bandyopadhyay & Pathak 2007; Paton & McCalman 2008; 
Larsen et al. 2016; Lewin & Peeters 2006). 
8 Theoretical implications 
The 12 factors form the literature review, the findings, the analysis and discussion are 
comprised into a framework consisting of seven factors which have an influence on the 
outcome of offshoring. When practitioners consider offshoring, they must evaluate these 
seven factors, and how they consider them to impact their business before they develop 
their strategy. If the management has any doubt of how one of these factors will influence 
their business offshoring outcome, it is advised to investigate the area. When developing 
their offshoring strategy, they can ensure that these seven factors are dealt with. Bringing 
these factors into an offshore implementation strategy will decrease the internal 
resistance, increase quality and productivity and increase the chance of success.

9 Practical implications  
From the literature review, the case study and the interviews, a guide on how a 
professional service firm can optimize their offshoring and work sharing is developed. 
This guide can be used by business managers after they have established an offshore 
office.  
9.1 
Offshore facilitation guide 
The guide consists of five steps that every business manager is advised to consider before 
starting their first offshore job. Figure 2 illustrates the five steps. Below each step is 
specified

9.1.1 Definition of purpose & goals 
A professional service firm must define what the purpose of their offshore office is. Most 
common are reduction in sales price, increase in competitiveness, reduction in spending, 
increased profit, access to resources in form of a very large and well educated workforce 
and access to knowledge and skills. The reason why it is very important to define the 
purposes is, that it will help the organisation keep on track and achieve its goals. If the 
purpose is not clearly defined and well-communicated to everyone in the organisation to 
whom it would be relevant, discussions can emerge and employees maybe working in 
different directions.  
When the purpose is defined, the management must define the goals of the outcome. 
When formulating these goals it is important to keep in mind that the performance is 
going to be measured against these goals.

9.1.2 Implementation plan 
In many cases offshoring and work sharing starts as a bottom up approach (Lewin & 
Peeters 2006), because a large company buys small offices elsewhere in the world, as in 
the case with PE. This can lead to a very disorganized and inconsistent effort of 
facilitation. To reduce these variations, an implementation plan must be developed. An 
implementation plan should state who will decide on what tasks to offshore and what 
parts of projects could be work shared, how the work sharing shall be carried out (what 
method to follow), and developing a standardized method of describing the requirements 
of an offshore task. 
Each department are advised to clarify which tasks can be offshored and what tasks can 
be work shared. The only types of tasks that are not suitable of work sharing are tasks that 
demand a high level of communication with the client and a relatively high amount of 
hours spend on site. 
9.1.3 Reduce Distance 
 The largest challenge when offshoring and work sharing is the challenge of distance. 
Both geographical and cultural distance can be very large if a western firm chooses to 
offshore to Asia. Breaking down these distances is crucial to success. Running two or 
more large offices on two difference continents makes it impossible for all employees to 
know each other. Therefore, ambassadors could be appointed in each office. The tasks of 
these ambassadors are to get to know each other very well. This could be done by visiting 
each other’s offices multiple times a year, working on projects together and having social 
interactions. When apart, they should have video conference calls frequently. The 
ambassadors’ task is to collect jobs and send them to the receiving ambassador. The 
receiving ambassador must then distribute the tasks to the workers in their office, collect 
them and send them back once they are done.  
It is important that the two ambassadors have a similar education and understanding of 
the tasks that get send back and forth. The task of being an ambassador demands both 
technical inside, people skill and a strong overview. If the office is large enough and the 
tasks are plenty, several ambassadors can be appointed in each department of the offices. 
9.1.4 Increase Incentive 
It is important that all employees buy into a change towards using offshore offices. To 
ensure this, the right incentive structures must be created by the management. There shall 
be an incentive both from the HQ in a high-cost area and the remote office in a low-cost 
area. The primary goal of the offshore office must be to support the HQ. In the case 
where the Chinese market had enormous growth, the employees in China preferred to 
work on the local projects, thus they did not have an incentive to solve tasks for the HQ. 
Moreover, with the high demand for engineers in the area of Tianjin created high staff 
turnover due to development in salary. Therefore, the location of an offshore low-cost 
work centre must not be in a rapid growing area with multiple clients. Setting up an office 
in such an area would also lead to higher employee turnover and increasing salaries. 
==PAGE:26==
25 
 
The employees must have incentive to follow the strategy of offshoring. Having 
management demanding to utilize the offshore office is one way of doing it. But this 
demand must also be met with the possibilities to do so. The employees must feel that it 
is the best solution. Successfully communicating the burning platform and creating 
budget plans that can be kept by using the low cost offshore office, is a way of making 
sure your employees are satisfied. The management must ensure that the employees in the 
HQ gain empowerment. It is important that the employees in the HQ see this task of 
offshoring and work sharing as a new task they must solve. Having them create plans for 
a project and distributing the tasks to the offshore staff can make them feel that they gain 
managerial power over the employees in the offshore office 
9.1.5 Measuring and evaluating 
There must be established sufficient measurement to determine if the previous set of 
goals are met. These measures make the foundation for evaluating on the process and will 
determine whether the organisation has success or not. Having performance indicators on 
each department for each project will help determine if the organisation is on the right 
path. 
Besides a quantitative measure such as percentage of turnover, hours or projects 
offshored, qualitative measures must be established. Managers must have regular 
meetings with the staff involved with the offshore office, to continuously evaluate the 
process and react to feedback from staff.  
10 Conclusion and notes to further research 
This paper aimed to examine how professional service firms can facilitate an offshoring 
and work sharing process. This was investigated through a longitudinal case study of a 
Danish professional service firm. Six propositions were developed. 
Findings showed that the company had been able to turnaround a failing offshore 
performance to a success. The analysis showed that the failure was a result of a 
disorganized and inconsistent management who poorly communicated goals of the 
offshored tasks. This lead to internal resistance. The management created an 
implementation strategy which led to an increase in management involvement, 
communication and stronger boundary spanning between staff from the HQ and offshore 
offices.  
Proposition 1, 2, 3, 5 and 6 were proven through the findings. Tasks with a high degree of 
communication with client was found not suited for work sharing, hence proposition 4 
was rejected. This indicates that managers should carefully choose which tasks to work 
share on, since the potential savings can be negated by extensive requirements of 
communication.  
A theoretical framework of offshoring impact factors is developed. This framework 
throws light on the core factors that will affect the outcome of an offshoring process. 
These factors can determine if the organisation will have success or failure. The 
framework is robust due to the fact that it is based on a strong theoretical foundation and 
a practical case study, and is a contribution to the literature of offshore facilitation. 
Mangers are advised to consider how these factors can influence their facilitation of 
offshoring and work sharing. A guideline for how to develop an offshoring strategy were 
==PAGE:27==

created. These guidelines can help business managers optimize their offshore 
performance.  
Further research should focus on testing the framework as well as the guidelines in 
companies from different cultural backgrounds and in different industries, to test its 
robustness and general ability. Furthermore, research on the propositions on a generic 
level is needed.
