Abstract
This thesis investigated the efficiency of very large offshore wind farms with a focus on
layout optimisation. The thesis specific site is located south of the coast of Tamil Nadu,
India. This site is made of 1195 15 MW wind turbines spread over 14 separate wind
farm clusters with varying installed capacity between 1 GW to 2.1 GW, totalling 18 GW
total capacity. Each wind farm was separated by a 2.5km buffer from the next one. To
simulate the conditions at the site, mesoscale data from the WRF model for the year
2020 was used.
The goal of this thesis is to maximise the Annual Energy Production through three pa-
rameters: turbine layout, wake modelling, and hub height variation. A series of idealised
and full-site simulations were conducted using the PyWake framework. Layout optimisa-
tion was performed using the NOJ wake model for computational efficiency, while hub
height analysis employed the more detailed Bastankhah Gaussian model to account
for vertical wake expansion. These models were combined with realistic wind fields to
reflect site-specific flow characteristics.
Results show that removing interior boundaries entirely yields the highest AEP increase
of 2.33% but is not operationally feasible. As a practical alternative, wind farm bound-
aries were redefined using a K-Means clustering approach aligned with wind flow fea-
tures, resulting in a 0.25% AEP gain. A further improvement of 0.74% was achieved
by implementing a variable-density layout, which strategically places more turbines in
high-resource areas.
Hub height optimisation demonstrated that energy gains from taller turbines occur only
under vertically sheared wind profiles. However, contrary to expectations, varying hub
heights did not significantly reduce wake losses within the applied modelling framework.
Overall, the findings emphasise that optimal wind farm layouts are highly site-specific.
Adaptive clustering, variable density, and targeted hub height design are effective when
tailored to local conditions.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation and Industry Context
This thesis explores these challenges through the case of a proposed 14 GW offshore
wind farm off the southeastern coast of Tamil Nadu, India. Situated in the Indian Ocean
near the Gulf of Mannar, the project consists of 14 separate clusters forming a single,
large-scale installation. This configuration provides a unique opportunity to investigate
the impact of layout strategies across both intra- and inter-wind farm scales. Specifi-
cally, the study examines how wake modelling approaches, turbine spacing, and het-
erogeneous turbine configurations affect energy performance under realistic offshore
atmospheric conditions.
As the offshore wind sector expands globally, such large-scale projects are no longer
exceptional but increasingly representative of future developments.
Understanding
their design and optimisation challenges is therefore critical within the broader con-
text of a rapidly growing industry. At the end of 2023, global installed offshore wind
capacity reached 75.2 GW, marking a 24% increase from the previous year. China led
this growth with 6.3 GW of new installations, followed by Europe with 3.8 GW. Other
regions, including Taiwan, Vietnam, and the United States, also contributed to this ex-
pansion, reflecting the increasing globalisation of offshore wind development (Global
Wind Energy Council 2024).
Looking ahead, the Global Wind Energy Council (GWEC) projects that global offshore
capacity will rise to approximately 380- 390 GW by 2033, with Asia-Pacific expected
to surpass Europe as the dominant market by the early 2030s. While Europe will re-
main a key player, countries such as South Korea, Japan, India, and the United States
are ramping up development through dedicated offshore wind roadmaps and auctions
(Global Wind Energy Council 2024; WindEurope 2024). As the development in Fig-
ure 1.1 shows, the wind industry is expected to reach a capacity of 1000 GW globally
in 2050.
This growth is not just in capacity, but also in technological scale and spatial footprint.
Modern turbines now exceed 15 MW in rated capacity, with rotor diameters over 230 m
(Vestas Wind Systems A/S 2023; Siemens Gamesa Renewable Energy 2022). Project
footprints are also expanding, often covering hundreds of square kilometres. Addition-
ally, as offshore space becomes more contested, multiple wind farms are increasingly
being developed within shared maritime zones, particularly in the North Sea, the Tai-
Efficiency of very large wind farms
1
==PAGE:19==
wan Strait, and along the coasts of China and the U.S. (Global Wind Energy Council
2024).
These trends introduce new design challenges. As wind farms grow in both size and
proximity, wake interactions become more complex. Wake effects, where upstream
turbines reduce wind speed and increase turbulence for those downstream, can extend
over long distances, especially under stable atmospheric conditions (Frandsen et al.
2006; Allaerts et al. 2015). Such interactions occur not only within individual farms but
also between neighbouring farms, leading to cumulative wake losses at the regional
scale (Fischereit et al. 2022).
These wake interactions directly impact the energy yield and structural loading of down-
stream turbines. Lower inflow velocities reduce power output, while increased turbu-
lence can accelerate fatigue damage. Accurately modelling, mitigating, or exploiting
these interactions is therefore essential to achieving efficient and resilient operation of
very large wind farms and emerging multi-wind farm offshore energy zones.
The rising importance of wake effects is reflected in the growing body of research on
the topic. As illustrated in Figure 1.2, the number of publications on wind farm wake
modelling remained low through the 1980s and 1990s, corresponding with the small tur-
bine sizes (typically below 1 MW) and the absence of offshore deployment. However,
following the commissioning of the first offshore wind farm in Vindeby, Denmark (1991),
and the rollout of utility-scale projects like Horns Rev (2002) and London Array (2012),
research activity began to accelerate. Turbine ratings have increased from 450 kW to
over 15 MW, and rotor diameters have expanded from 35 m to more than 230 m. These
changes have amplified wake effects between inter- and intra-wind farms, particularly
under offshore conditions (Allaerts et al. 2015; Frandsen et al. 2006). Starting around
2008, annual publications on wake modelling rose sharply, surpassing 250 per year by
2024. This trend in research activity reflects growing recognition of the aerodynamic
challenges introduced by large-scale projects, such as the proposed 18 GW wind farm
in this thesis. It emphasises the importance of accurate wake modelling to better un-
derstand and optimise turbine interactions in complex environments (Fischereit et al.
2022; Bastankhah et al. 2014; Meyers et al. 2012).

1.2.2
Wake effect in VLWF
As offshore developments expand, wind farms are increasingly clustered within mar-
itime zones. This introduces inter-farm wake interactions that are not adequately ad-
dressed by traditional layout models. Volker et al. (2017) demonstrated through mesoscale
simulations that distributing wind capacity across multiple smaller wind farms with wide
spacing (10.5 D) significantly reduces cumulative wake losses. In contrast, narrow
spacing (5.25 D) leads to efficiency drops below 50%. For two offshore regions (B
and C), visualised in Figure 1.3, they found that efficiency declines with farm area,
especially under narrow turbine spacing (5.25 D). Wide spacing (10.5 D), however,
maintained efficiency above 70% even for areas up to 105 km2, while narrow spacing
led to efficiencies below 50%.
The results further showed that very large wind farms power density depends on the
resource and the installed capacity density. Showing that wind farms of smaller offshore
wind farms are generally preferable to reduce wake losses and boundary-layer effects;
however, in regions with very strong offshore winds, even very large offshore farms can
operate efficiently. These findings highlight that the viability of VLWFs is highly regional
and must be aligned with local atmospheric and surface conditions.
Fischereit et al. (2022) synthesised results from multiple studies and emphasised that
closely spaced or clustered wind farms can have cumulative impacts on the atmo-
spheric boundary layer, with effects extending tens of kilometres downstream. These

Fischereit et al. (2022) further reviewed the state of wind-farm parameterisations in

Empirical support comes from Schneemann et al. (2020), who found measurable inter-

layout, turbine spacing, and wake modelling are still rare, especially at the scale of multi-
gigawatt VLWFs. Further development of these tools is essential for future offshore

1.2.4
Wake Modelling in Offshore Layouts
Wake modelling is critical in layout optimisation, as the chosen model governs how
turbine interactions and losses are quantified. Offshore environments, characterised
by lower surface roughness and reduced turbulence intensity, generally exhibit slower
wake recovery compared to onshore sites. This effect is particularly pronounced un-
der stable atmospheric conditions, where wakes can persist for extended distances
(Frandsen et al. 2006).
Traditional analytical models, such as the Jensen (or PARK) model (Jensen 1983; Katic
6
Efficiency of very large wind farms
==PAGE:24==
et al. 1986), have been widely used due to their computational efficiency. These models
rely on simplified assumptions about wake expansion and recovery, making them fast
but limited in accuracy. They are particularly prone to underestimating wake losses in
large, dense, or irregular wind farm layouts.
Bastankhah et al. (2014) introduced a Gaussian-based analytical model that more
realistically represents wake shape and velocity deficits. This model improved over
Jensen’s approach, particularly in predicting wake overlap and partial wake effects.
Building on this, Niayifar et al. (2016) developed the Coupled Wake Boundary Layer
(CWBL) model, which integrates turbine wakes with the dynamics of the atmospheric
boundary layer. These models balance computational speed and physical realism, and
they perform better under stable and near-neutral offshore conditions.
Large Eddy Simulations and Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) models (Mey-
ers et al. 2012; Churchfield et al. 2014) offer a much more detailed representation of tur-
bulence, wake meandering, and shear-layer development. LES captures a large spec-
trum of turbulent scales, enabling precise analysis of wake interactions, while RANS
simplifies some turbulence modelling for slightly lower computational costs. These ap-
proaches are used to investigate small-scale dynamics but are too expensive for direct
use in large-scale layout optimisation.
Hybrid frameworks have been developed to make high-fidelity insights more accessible
for practical wind farm design. For instance, Gebraad et al. (2016) and Stevens et al.
(2016) used LES and RANS simulations to calibrate reduced-order models and ma-
chine learning surrogates. These approaches enable faster layout optimisation while
preserving key physical characteristics of more complex models. Similarly, Van Bins-
bergen et al. (2024) demonstrated that calibrating conventional analytical wake models
to site-specific conditions significantly improves their predictive accuracy, underscoring
the value of site-informed tuning in large offshore clusters.
However, recent high-fidelity simulations by Allaerts et al. (2015) show that using sim-
plified wake models in very large wind farms, particularly those organised into clusters,
can significantly underestimate wake losses in inner or downstream sections. This is
due to the inability of traditional models to capture flow blockage, atmospheric feed-
back, and internal boundary layer effects. Their findings suggest that relying solely
on classical models may lead to suboptimal layout choices, especially in farms where
turbine clusters share atmospheric flow fields.
In contrast to turbine-scale models, mesoscale models such as the Weather Research
and Forecasting (WRF) model simulate broader atmospheric phenomena. Volker et
al. (2017) used mesoscale simulations to demonstrate that spacing smaller wind farms

wake effects compared to a single large installation.
Mesoscale models operate at spatial and temporal resolutions sufficient to resolve
boundary layer development, diurnal cycles, synoptic-scale variability, and mesoscale
circulations such as sea breezes and low-level jets. Unlike analytical or high-fidelity
micro-scale models, they can represent the interaction between wind farms and evolv-
ing weather systems.(Fitch et al. 2012; Nunalee et al. 2014)
These simulations have been employed to evaluate regional impacts of wind farms
on wind speed distributions, temperature, and cloud formation. For example, Zhou et
al. (2012) and Abkar et al. (2015) showed that large-scale deployment can cause flow
deceleration and persistent wake zones over distances of tens of kilometres, potentially
influencing downstream sites or entire regional grids.
Despite their detail, mesoscale models are rarely used in optimisation due to high com-
putational cost. Instead, they serve as validation tools and offer strategic insights into
where farms should be located and how large-scale atmospheric effects can be miti-
gated (Churchfield et al. 2014). Integrating their outputs into layout design enhances
physical realism, particularly for multi-wind farm or multi-gigawatt offshore wind devel-
opments.
1.3
Research Gaps
Despite extensive research on offshore wind farm layout optimisation, a closer inspec-
tion of the literature reveals several gaps, particularly when scaling up to the size and
complexity of future projects. Most existing design-guidelines and optimisation frame-
works were developed for small to moderately sized wind farms, often covering areas
of only 2–5 km2 and less than 100 turbines. As wind energy moves toward very large
wind farms, exceeding hundreds of square kilometres and capacities upwards of 10-
18 GW, these conventional layout strategies may no longer sufficiently display these
increased complex wake interactions.
Furthermore, layout recommendations such as fixed downwind and crosswind spacings
fail to account for the spatial heterogeneity in flow conditions that naturally emerge over
such expansive sites.
Another significant shortcoming is the underrepresentation of clustered or nonuniform
layout strategies in the literature. While variable-density designs have been shown to
improve energy capture in certain scenarios, they are rarely explored in the context of
VLWFs. In particular, the potential to strategically vary spacing, hub height, or even
turbine configuration across sub-farm zones remains largely unaddressed.
8
Efficiency of very large wind farms
==PAGE:26==
Wake modelling assumptions further constrain the applicability of existing studies. Sim-
plified models, while computationally efficient, often underestimate wake losses in sta-
ble atmospheric conditions, which is common in offshore environments. Yet, the impact
of wake model choice on layout performance, especially under realistic wind conditions,
is seldom rigorously evaluated. Likewise, wind direction variability and atmospheric sta-
bility are frequently simplified or neglected altogether, despite their known influence on
wake dynamics and farm-scale efficiency.
Finally, much of the foundational literature continues to rely on turbine specifications
that do not reflect the characteristics of modern offshore WTs, such as 15 MW turbines
with rotor diameters exceeding 230 m. This disconnect raises questions about how
applicable prior findings are to contemporary or future installations.
These limitations underscore a clear need for a more integrated, large-scale, and repre-
sentative approach to wind farm layout design. Specifically, there is value in exploring
how layout performance is influenced by the joint optimisation of hub height, turbine
density, and wake model selection, particularly in the context of spatially diverse and
meteorologically complex offshore sites.
1.4
Research Questions
Based on the identified gaps in the literature, this study investigates the following re-
search questions:
1. To what extent can variable-density, cluster-based layouts improve performance
compared to uniform spacing in large-scale offshore wind farms?
2. What is the impact of different wake models on layout optimisation outcomes?
3. How does the variation of hub height affect wake interactions and energy yield?
By addressing these questions, this thesis aims to find more robust and scalable design
strategies for future offshore wind developments.

2.4
Climate
The planned offshore wind farm is located in the Gulf of Mannar, which is a tropical
marine zone in the Indian Ocean. This region falls under the influence of the Indian
monsoon system and exhibits a tropical maritime climate with modest diurnal variabil-
ity, seasonally reversing winds, and high sea surface temperatures (SSTs) throughout
the year. The atmospheric boundary layer here is strongly shaped by air–sea interac-
tions, cloud cover, and seasonal synoptic patterns, all of which influence the stability
regime and wind resource potential (Narayanan et al. 2018; Stull 1988).
The climate in the Gulf of Mannar can be broadly divided into four distinct seasons: Pre-
monsoon (March–May), Southwest Monsoon (June–September), Post-monsoon/Northeast
Monsoon (October–November) and Winter, also called dry season (December–February).
In Figure 2.4, the seasonal changes of wind speed (blue curve) and atmospheric stabil-
ity (red curve) are visualised as monthly mean values, averaged over the entire study
area at a height of 150 m above sea level.
Pre-monsoon season
This period is marked by strong solar radiation, weak to moderate variable winds, and
elevated SSTs. These conditions result in an unstable boundary layer, enhanced verti-
cal mixing, and shallow convective activity (Vissa et al. 2013). It is considered the most
turbulent phase of the year.

As shown in Figure 2.4, average stability values drop to as low as 1.5 in April, indicating
unstable conditions according to the RMOL-based classification. This is consistent with
the convective thermals and shallow cumulus clouds typical of tropical pre-monsoon
environments (Vissa et al. 2013; Stull 1988).
Wind speeds remain moderate during this period (Figure 2.4), with a significant increase
observed in May as the region transitions into the monsoon. While highly turbulent, the
pre-monsoon period is only moderately productive for wind energy due to lower wind
speeds. This season is characterised by atmospheric instability, due to strong surface
heating. This makes the pre-monsoon season highly turbulent but only moderately
productive in terms of wind energy.
Monsoon Season
Dominated by south-western winds, this is the most productive season for wind en-
ergy production. Wind speeds exceed 12 m/s during June (Figure 2.4), driven by the
Findlater Jet (Findlater 1969).
With increasing wind speeds, the atmospheric stability also rises during this period, as
shown in Figure 2.4. This is influenced by deep cloud cover, increased humidity, and
stratification aloft, where solar heating is diminished (Narayanan et al. 2018).
Post-monsoon Season
Winds shift to northeasterly directions, and atmospheric conditions become more vari-
able. While the SSTs remain high, convection weakens, and the stability begins to
decline. (Vissa et al. 2013).
Figure 2.4 shows stability values dropping to around 2.5–3.0, while wind speeds begin
to decrease. This transitional phase is defined by moderate wind speeds and moder-
ately unstable conditions, often associated with the onset of northeast monsoon sys-
tems (Vissa et al. 2013).
Winter season
Finally, the winter season is characterised by calm to moderate northeasterly winds,
reduced convective activity, and slightly cooler SSTs. Wind speeds typically range from
7–9 m/s, and stability values settle around 2.0, indicating near-neutral to weakly stable
boundary-layer conditions (Stull 1988).

Energy source: Wind
A fundamental aspect of wind energy is understanding the amount of kinetic energy
available in the wind and how much of it can realistically be harvested by a wind turbine.
Since the wind acts as the sole fuel for a wind farm, characterising this energy potential
is critical for turbine design, layout planning, and accurate power yield predictions.
The total kinetic power (P) in the wind flows through the rotor’s swept area is given by:
P(U) = 1

where U is the wind speed, ρ the air density and A is the rotor swept area.
The fact that there is a cubic relationship between power and wind speed makes wind
energy so attractive; however, it also implies that small changes in wind speed can
result in large variations in potential power. For example, doubling the wind speed
leads to an eightfold increase in available power.
While Equation 3.1 describes the total power in the wind, a turbine cannot extract all of
it. If a rotor were to capture 100% of the incoming wind energy, it would entirely block
airflow, causing stagnation at the rotor plane and preventing any mass flow through the
system, making full extraction physically impossible.
This is addressed by Betz’s Law, which states that the maximum fraction of wind en-
ergy that can be theoretically extracted by an ideal turbine is approximately 59.26%
(Betz 1926). This upper bound is known as the Betz limit, and the efficiency of energy
extraction is represented by the power coefficient, Cp, defined as:

In practice, modern turbines achieve peak Cp values between 0.35 and 0.50 under op-
timal conditions. Real-world Cp values vary with wind speed, blade pitch, rotor design,
and control strategies.

3.2
Wake Effects
Wind turbines modify the incoming wind by extracting momentum, creating a trailing
region of altered flow known as the wake. Compared to an undisturbed atmosphere,
the wake is characterised by a reduced velocity and increased turbulence. These ef-
fects are closely related to the wind turbine’s thrust coefficient (CT), which measures
how much the rotor extracts energy from the wind and slows it. Figure 3.1 visualised
an idealised turbine with pressure and velocity. These wake characteristics introduce
major challenges for wind farms; lower wind speeds downstream reduce the available
energy for neighbouring turbines, while increased turbulence subjects them to greater
mechanical stress and fatigue.
Figure 3.1: Air flow around idealised turbine with pressure and velocity (González-
Longatt et al. 2012)
The wake effect can be calculated by different models. Different/most common models
are discussed in section 3.3. The development of a wind turbine wake is often divided
into different zones.
Wake formation is typically divided into three zones: the near wake, transitional and
far-wake zone.
Immediately behind the rotor lies the near wake, where the structure of the flow is di-
rectly influenced by the turbine’s operation. According to Burton et al. (2009), this region
typically extends one to three rotor diameters downstream. Here, a cylindrical shear
layer forms around the wake, marking a boundary between the slower core flow and
Efficiency of very large wind farms
17
==PAGE:35==
the faster ambient wind. Embedded within this shear layer are coherent tip vortices, as
described by Vermeer et al. (2003), which play a significant role in promoting turbulence
and initiating the wake’s downstream expansion.
Beyond the near wake, the flow enters a transition zone where turbulence becomes
increasingly dominant as the initial rotor imprint disappears. The shear layer thickens
and gradually encompasses the entire wake cross-section. Eventually, the flow devel-
ops into the far wake, characterised by a more symmetric structure and a velocity deficit
that follows a Gaussian distribution (Barthelmie et al. 2013).
Environmental factors control the rate at which a wake expands and recovers. Turbu-
lence intensity (TI), wind speed and direction, and atmospheric stability play a crucial
role. High turbulence accelerates mixing between the wake and surrounding air, lead-
ing to a quicker recovery of wind speed. Conversely, stable atmospheric conditions
suppress vertical mixing, which can cause wakes to persist over longer distances with
less recovery.
Wake recovery mechanisms rely heavily on turbulent mixing processes. As explained
by Manwell et al. (2009), the interaction between wake turbulence and the ambient
flow gradually replenishes the lost momentum. This “re-energisation” continues down-
stream until the wake velocity closely approaches that of the free stream, though ele-
vated turbulence levels may linger even after the velocity deficit disappears.
Remote sensing technologies have provided new insights into large-scale wake be-
haviour. A study by Christiansen et al. (2005) revealed with satellite imagery that wakes
could extend 5 to 20 kilometres before the wind speed recovers within 2% of its original
value, depending on atmospheric conditions. More recent investigations, such as those
by M. Badger et al. (2015), have documented wakes persisting over distances exceed-
ing 70 kilometres, particularly in offshore environments characterised by low turbulence
and stable stratification.
Summarised, understanding how wind turbine wakes form, develop, and dissipate is
crucial for optimising wind farm layout and performance. Effectively managing wake
interactions is key to maximising energy production and reducing mechanical stress on
downstream turbines.

3.3.1
The Jensen Wake Model
The Jensen wake model, originally introduced by Jensen in 1983 (Jensen 1983), is one
of the earliest and most widely used analytical models for predicting the velocity deficit
in the wake of a wind turbine. It is based on a simplified momentum balance approach
and assumes a uniformly reduced wind speed (i.e., a top-hat profile) within the wake.
Despite its simplicity, the model remains popular in wind energy applications due to its
computational efficiency and ease of implementation, particularly in wind farm layout
optimisation tasks.
A key assumption of the Jensen model is that the wake expands linearly with down-
stream distance. This is characterised by a wake expansion or decay constant k, which
depends on environmental factors such as ambient turbulence intensity and surface
roughness. A higher turbulence level leads to a larger k, resulting in faster wake recov-
ery and broader wake spread. The model also incorporates the thrust coefficient CT,
which quantifies the amount of momentum extracted from the flow by the turbine.

One of the major simplifications of the Jensen model is its assumption of a uniform (top-

wind speed across the wake’s cross-section. In addition, the model does not account
for the turbulent near-wake region directly behind the turbine. As a result, it is only
valid in the far wake, where the flow has developed sufficiently for the linear expansion
assumption to hold.
Despite these limitations, the Jensen model continues to be widely used, particularly
in large-scale wind farm optimisation problems where computational speed is critical.
Its simplicity allows for rapid evaluation of many turbine configurations, making it suit-
able for iterative design processes. However, in scenarios requiring higher accuracy,
such as modelling wake interactions, near-wake behaviour, or yaw misalignment, more
advanced models are typically preferred.

3.6
Efficiency
A central objective in wind farm design is to determine turbine placements and opera-
tional strategies that maximise energy yield while minimising aerodynamic losses and,
where relevant, economic costs. This process, known as wind farm layout optimisation,
involves adjusting design variables such as turbine positions, hub heights, and turbine
types. The optimisation can target different objectives, most notably the Annual Energy
Production (AEP) or the Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE), depending on the scope of
the analysis.
In this study, the focus is on AEP optimisation, which prioritises maximising the total
energy output of the wind farm without directly incorporating financial factors such as
capital expenditure or operational costs. AEP is widely used in academic research
for benchmarking purposes, algorithm development, and aerodynamic performance
studies. It allows for a clearer isolation of wake effects and flow interactions, without the
confounding variables introduced by economic modelling. While LCOE offers a more
holistic perspective for real-world deployment, it requires detailed and often project-
specific financial assumptions that are outside the scope of this study.

PyWake
PyWake is an open-source Python framework developed by the Technical University of
Denmark (DTU) for simulating wind farm performance and wake interactions. It sup-
ports a range of analytical and engineering wake models, allowing users to evaluate
wind farm layouts under varying atmospheric and turbine configurations. PyWake is
particularly well-suited for research and optimisation tasks due to its modular architec-
ture and integration with layout optimisation tools like TopFarm.
The modelling process in PyWake follows a structured sequence, as illustrated in Fig-
ure 4.1a. It begins with the definition of key input parameters, including turbine loca-
tions, wind turbine specifications, and site-specific wind conditions. Wind input data can
be supplied as a discrete distribution (e.g., Weibull parameters per wind direction) or
as time-series data, and can include additional atmospheric parameters such as shear
exponent or turbulence intensity if needed.
Once the site and turbine characteristics are defined, the user selects a wake model to
simulate the wake interactions among turbines. PyWake offers a variety of wake mod-
els, ranging from the simplified NOJ model to more advanced Gaussian-based models
such as Bastankhah and TurboGaussian. These models differ in how they represent
wake shape, vertical resolution, turbulence effects, and wake recovery, allowing for a
balance between computational speed and physical accuracy depending on the appli-
cation.
In this study, multiple wake models within PyWake were tested and applied, with model
selection tailored to the specific optimisation scenario in ideal conditions. For layout
optimisation, where all turbines are placed at the same hub height, the computation-
ally efficient NOJ model was selected. For hub height optimisation, the Bastankhah
Gaussian model was chosen for its ability to resolve vertical wake gradients and shear
interactions. A detailed comparison of wake model behaviour and performance is pro-
vided in Chapter 5.1.

TopFarm is an open-source Python package developed by DTU Wind Energy for the

adjusting turbine positions to reduce wake losses and enhance overall performance.

Mesoscale Modelling
Mesoscale modelling is used in the context of this thesis to simulate the atmospheric
flow processes over large spatial and temporal scales, providing meteorological context
for wind resource assessment and wake behaviour analysis. Specifically, mesoscale
simulations allow for the characterisation of wind patterns, atmospheric stability, and
turbulence intensity at offshore wind farm sites, factors that significantly influence wake
development and recovery (Brower et al. 2004; Jiménez et al. 2016).
Here, the Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) model is employed as the mesoscale
modelling tool. WRF is a widely used, non-hydrostatic numerical weather prediction
system capable of resolving atmospheric processes across scales from a few kilome-
tres to hundreds of kilometres (Skamarock et al. 2008).
WRF solves the governing equations of fluid and thermodynamics on a structured
grid using physical parameterisations for sub-grid processes, including surface fluxes,
boundary layer dynamics, and cloud formation.
In this study, WRF is configured following best practices developed in the New Eu-
ropean Wind Atlas (NEWA) project, with a particular focus on accurately reproducing
near-surface wind climatology relevant to turbine hub heights (Hahmann et al. 2020;

Idealised Experiments
This chapter presents a series of idealised experiments designed to investigate optimi-
sation strategies of offshore wind farm layouts under controlled, simplified conditions.
These experiments serve as a foundational analysis to test key assumptions, evaluate
the influence of modelling choices, and explore the effects of spatial and vertical turbine
configurations on overall wind farm performance.
The primary aim is to isolate and understand the impact of specific layout decisions,
such as inter-turbine spacing, wind farm boundary constraints, wake model selection,
and hub height variation, on wake interactions and energy production. By simplifying
environmental and geometric variables, these idealised setups offer a focused platform
for hypothesis-driven testing without the computational complexity of full-scale simula-
tions.
Idealised experiments are particularly useful in identifying performance trends, validat-
ing model behaviour, and generating design principles that can later be generalised or
refined for real-world scenarios. They also provide a way to benchmark model sensi-
tivity, evaluate the effectiveness of optimisation algorithms, and quantify the influence
of vertical and horizontal design freedoms.
The following hypotheses guide the experiments in this chapter:
• Wind farm layout optimisation is sensitive to the chosen wake model and results
in measurable differences in turbine placement and energy output.
• Vertical staggering of hub heights leads to performance.
• Relaxing spatial constraints (e.g., wind farm boundaries) leads to more efficient
turbine distributions and higher energy capture.
• Incorporating site-specific wind direction variability into the optimisation process
produces layouts that outperform those optimised for a single prevailing direction.
Each case study presented in this chapter is introduced with its methodological setup,
followed by a separate results section. This structure facilitates clarity between what
is being tested and what is observed, laying the groundwork for interpretation in the
subsequent discussion chapter.

NOJ Model:

practical tool for quick, approximate estimates.

5.4
Hub-Height optimisation
The vertical placement of wind turbines plays a critical role in determining wake inter-
actions and overall wind farm performance. While horizontal layout optimisation has
traditionally received the most attention, the optimisation of hub height offers an ad-
ditional and often underutilised degree of freedom. By adjusting the vertical position
of turbines, it becomes possible to reduce wake overlap, improve inflow conditions at
downstream rotors, and increase overall energy production, especially in tightly spaced
arrays or vertically stratified flow conditions.
In this section, hub height optimisation is explored under two configurations: (1) indi-
vidual turbine hub heights, and (2) wind farm-based hub heights. In both cases, the
horizontal positions (x, y) of turbines are fixed, and only the hub height (z) is treated
as a design variable. The vertical range is constrained between 150 m and 200 m.
The lower bound (150 m) corresponds to modern offshore turbine standards and en-
sures adequate clearance between rotor tips and the sea surface (given a 120 m blade
length, this maintains at least 20 m clearance). The upper bound (200 m), while not yet
common in commercial deployments, is being actively explored by manufacturers as
part of next-generation turbine designs. For instance, Nordex recently announced a
200 m tower for its Delta4000 platform, highlighting the industry’s direction toward taller
turbines to harness stronger, more consistent wind resources (Parkinson 2024).
To isolate the aerodynamic effects of vertical staggering, all simulations in this section
are conducted both with and without atmospheric wind shear. This approach helps
distinguish between improvements attributable purely to hub height variation and those
that arise due to the vertical gradient in wind speed. The Bastankhah Gaussian (BG)
wake model is used throughout, as it provides sufficient vertical resolution to capture
wake behaviour associated with hub height differences.
5.4.1

This case investigates the effects of vertical staggering when each turbine can be as-

considered: one with atmospheric wind shear and one without. The goal is to isolate
whether vertical optimisation improves energy capture and under what atmospheric
conditions.

To extend the analysis of vertical wind profile effects, a sensitivity study was performed
on the wind shear exponent α, which controls the vertical distribution of wind speed in
the atmospheric boundary layer. While the previous experiment explored the impact of
changing hub height under a fixed shear exponent (α = 0.10), this section investigates
how varying α influences wind farm power production.
The shear exponents ranging from 0.01 to 0.20 were tested, under a reference wind
speed of 10.54 m/s at 150 m hub height. The vertical wind speed profile was then
computed between 1 m and 320 m using the power-law formulation in Equation 3.12,
which resulted in Figure 5.19.

area of a WT with a hub height of 150m.
Smaller values of α result in steeper wind speed profiles, with minimal variation in wind
speed as height increases or decreases. This is because, as α approaches zero, the
height-dependent term in the power-law (Equation 3.13) expression tends toward 1,
effectively neutralising the influence of height on wind speed.
In contrast, larger values of α amplify the effect of the height term, resulting in greater
variation of wind speeds in those shear profiles. For instance, as shown in Figure 5.19,
the wind speed at 250 m differs by more than 2 m/s between α=0.01 and α=0.20.
This difference has a strong impact on energy yield estimates, as power output in-
creases with the cube of wind speed. Even small variations in shear exponent can

Discussion
5.6.1
Horizontal Layout Flexibility
The results from Cases 0.2 and 0.2.2 (Figures 5.10 and 5.11) demonstrate that relax-
ing spatial constraints significantly enhances wind farm performance. When turbines
are allowed to move beyond wind farm boundaries, the optimiser redistributes them to
reduce wake interference and improve land usage. As shown in Table 5.3, turbines
from wind farm 2 migrate into wind farms 1 and 3, increasing energy capture where
aerodynamic conditions are more favourable. These findings suggest that strict spa-
tial zoning may artificially limit performance, and that boundary flexibility can discover
natural layout patterns during the design phase.
5.6.2
Density as a Design Variable
In Case 0.2.2, the optimiser rebalances turbine density based on the previous cases.
Rather than enforcing uniform capacity across the site, the optimiser favours locating
WT and density increases in upwind and downwind regions while allowing for wake
recovery zones with reduced installations, as seen in Table 5.3. The resulting layout
yields the highest AEP of all idealised cases, with a new capacity factor of 82.80%.
The bar plot in Figure 5.12 highlights this performance shift, particularly the diminished
output of turbines in wind farm 2. This supports the idea that capacity density can be
treated as an active optimisation variable.
5.6.3
Importance of Wind Direction Variability
Figure 5.13 shows the optimised layout for a realistic Tamil Nadu wind rose. In Case 0.3,
the layout presents distinct patterns - rows of WTs perpendicular to the most common
wind direction. These results support incorporating wind roses early in the layout opti-
misation process to better exploit dominant inflow angles and avoid overfitting to single-
direction scenarios.
It should be noted that these results are based on the assumption of a uniform wind
resource across the entire site. While this may be reasonable for small wind farms, it is
unrealistic for VLWF, where spatial variations in wind conditions can significantly affect
turbine performance. Therefore, site-specific modelling and optimisation, accounting
for localised wind resource variation, are essential for maximising wind farm efficiency.
5.6.4
Vertical Dimension and Wind Shear
Cases 2.1 and 2.2 explore the effects of hub height optimisation. Under shear con-
ditions, the optimiser consistently selects the maximum hub height (200 m) to access
stronger winds at higher elevations. As shown in Figure 5.15a, elevated turbines ben-
efit from higher inflow speeds and reduced wake deficits. In contrast, without shear

(Figure 5.16), hub height selections become erratic, and no systematic performance
improvements are observed.
Wind farm-based hub height optimisation (Case 2.2) achieves similar performance
improvements while offering a more practical and implementable design strategy. It
strikes a useful balance between aerodynamic benefit and engineering feasibility. The
heatmaps in Figure 5.17 further confirm that performance gains from vertical staggering
only occur when atmospheric shear is present. This demonstrates that vertical optimi-
sation is effective only in stratified flow environments.
Overall, the results indicate that the applied Pywake framework is primarily sensitive to
wind shear. It does not capture wake mitigation effects from vertical staggering. Under
these conditions, it would be most effective to maximise AEP by targeting the highest
available wind resource. This implies using the tallest permitted hub height instead of
trying to mitigate wakes through vertical staggering.
The effect that the optimisation of hub heights is not caught can be attributed could be
due to the size of WTs. With a hub height of 150 to 200m and a rotor diameter of 240m,
the wake expands at the next turbine position to 500 to 750 m in width. The Gaussian
model was used as it had the vertical variation in the wake.
The limited impact of hub height optimisation observed in the results may be attributed
to two key factors. First, the large physical dimensions of the wind turbines. With
hub heights between 150 and 200 meters and rotor diameters of 240 meters, substan-
tial wake expansion is achieved downstream. Using the Bastankhah Gaussian wake
model, the wake can reach a width of approximately 500 to 750 meters at a downstream
distance typical for turbine spacing (4D). In such cases, the wake already encompasses
the subsequent vertically staggered turbine. Although the Bastankhah Gaussian model
includes vertical variation in the velocity deficit, it was initially assumed that this fea-
ture would capture the potential benefits of vertical staggering. Other papers, such as
Zhang et al. 2019, have shown vertical staggering can be effective; however, these
studies investigated downstream turbines that partially exited the wake and operated
in free-stream wind. However, at this site, this would require WT spacing of smaller
than 2.5D, which is not realistic and therefore not further investigated. As a result, the
model may underestimate the influence of vertical positioning on wake interactions and
power production. Therefore, the observed lack of benefit from hub height optimisation
may reflect a limitation of the wake model rather than an absence of physical benefit.
If smaller, more traditional turbines were used, the impact of hub height variation might
be more significant. In such scenarios, vertically separating turbine rotors could mean-
ingfully reduce wake losses, and the wake model might better capture the benefits of

Across all cases, a recurring pattern emerges: greater degrees of freedom in layout
design enable higher AEP. However, this potential is only realised when grounded in
accurate environmental modelling and careful constraint management.

Building on the observations from the initial WRF-based optimisation, which showed
a subtle redistribution of turbines toward higher wind regions without fully abandoning

AEP.

zones that strategically enhanced the overall AEP. Furthermore, Case 0.3 demon-

6.2.2
Discussion
The mesoscale-optimised layout reveals that the optimiser strongly prioritises regions
with high wind resources, leading to a spatially uneven distribution of turbines across
the site. Rather than applying a uniform strategy, multiple wake mitigation approaches
are observed, several of which reflect patterns identified in the earlier idealised experi-
ments. These behaviours can be grouped into four dominant spatial strategies.
1.
High-density clustering in high-resource zones: Wind farms 1, 2, 3, and 9
emerge as key locations with elevated wind resource availability. These wind farms
show high turbine densities and achieve both high total AEP and capacity factors, indi-
cating that the performance remains strong even under tight spacing conditions when
wind quality is favourable.
2. Wake-aware spacing upstream of productive areas: Western wind farms, lo-
cated upwind of high-performance zones, exhibit structured layouts with turbine rows
aligned perpendicularly to the prevailing wind direction (approximately 262°). This pat-
tern, also seen in Case 0.3, allows turbines to harness undisturbed inflow and establish
wake recovery zones for downstream wind farms. These spatial arrangements mitigate
cumulative wake losses and enhance performance in key production areas.
3. Dense edge placement along southeastern and western boundaries: High tur-
bine concentrations are also observed along the southeastern and western edges of
the site. As these boundaries lie at the downwind edge relative to prevailing winds, tur-
bines placed here cast no wakes onto others. This makes them particularly attractive to
the optimiser, consistent with the behaviour observed in Case 0.2, where edge turbines
were favoured due to the absence of downstream penalties.

6.3
Layout Optimisation on New Proposed Areas
The original wind farms used in earlier experiments Figure 6.1 were based on arbi-
trary or administrative boundaries. Then, by removing the internal boundaries, layout’s
patterns emerge freely due to the wind conditions and wake effects Figure 6.2.
To better understand whether the previously observed AEP gains stemmed from an
expanded layout area or from optimised turbine distribution and wake-mitigating strate-
gies, the wind farm is restructured using a k-means approach based on turbine location
and individual AEP. The objective is to generate wind farms that are both geographically
coherent and performance-consistent, enabling the layout to reflect naturally emerging
patterns, such as wake buffer zones, and align better with wake and resource-driven
behaviour.
To do this, the optimised layout based on K-Means clustering was tested under two
scenarios:
• Variable-density layout: Wind turbines are distributed according to the spatial
density patterns resulting from the optimisation without internal boundaries (sub-
section 6.2.2).
• Fixed-density layout: Wind turbines are distributed almost the same capacity
density for each of the wind farms.
In both optimisations for the new areas the total number of turbines is kept the same
(1195). This setup enables the evaluation of two key aspects: First, whether opti-
mised, non-uniform installed capacity density leads to greater performance gains than
improved clustering alone. Second, how the trade-off between maximising total output
and promoting more balanced turbine distribution affects the relative attractiveness of
individual site areas for development.
6.3.1
K-Means Clustering Approach
To identify spatial patterns and groupings among wind turbines based on both their
geographical locations and performance, a K-Means clustering algorithm was used.
The features used for clustering included the WTs coordinates (utm_x, utm_y) and
the WTs specific AEP value. Before clustering, all features were standardised using
StandardScaler to ensure that differences in scale did not bias the clustering process.
The number of clusters selected was k = 14 as this aligns with the initial number of
predefined wind farms. The clustering was performed using the KMeans implementation
from scikit-learn (a package from Python language), with the following parameters:
random_state=42 and n_init='auto'.
Efficiency of very large wind farms
69
==PAGE:87==
K-Means is an unsupervised learning algorithm that divides the data into k clusters
by minimising the within-cluster sum of squared distances. It begins by initialising k
centroids, either randomly (n_init='auto') or using a heuristic. Each data point is
then assigned to the nearest centroid, forming temporary clusters. The centroids are
recomputed as the mean of all points assigned to each cluster, and the assignment
process is repeated iteratively until the centroids stabilise and the algorithm converges.
In this study, the clustering was repeated 5000 times using different random seeds to
account for the sensitivity of K-Means to initial centroid placement. For each result, the
total AEP within each cluster was compared to a target AEP, defined as the total site-
wide AEP divided by the number of clusters (14). A deviation score was calculated as
the sum of squared differences between each cluster’s AEP and the target, penalising
large imbalances more heavily. The clustering configuration with the lowest total AEP
deviation was selected as the final result. This approach ensured that the resulting
clusters were not only spatially coherent but also roughly similar in energy output.
The resulting cluster assignments were appended to the turbine dataset, and then vi-
sualised on a geographic map. Each turbine is represented as a point, colour-coded
by its assigned wind farm.
The initial distribution can be seen in Figure A.3.6, and the initial boundaries with the
number of WTs in Figure A.3.7. Adjusted boundaries, to cover the entirety of the pro-
posed site, are seen in Figure 6.7. The turbines are grouped into spatially coherent
wind farms that also reflect similarities in AEP. A comparison to the initial boundaries
can be seen in Figure A.3.8.

6.4
Hub-height
This section applies the findings from earlier hub-height experiments to the real-world
offshore wind scenario in Tamil Nadu. The aim is to test the hypothesis developed in
subsection 5.6.5 - Energy gains from vertical wind shear have a greater impact on AEP
than wake mitigation achieved through hub-height staggering. This hypothesis will be
assessed by evaluating the performance and spatial placement of turbines when a mix
of hub heights is introduced into the layout.
To test the hypothesis, the existing wind farm layout is modified so that 50% of the
turbines are assigned a hub height of 150 m, and the other 50% are set at 200 m. Where
the initial allocation is random. The optimisation process allows turbines not to shift hub
heights but relocate, with no interior boundaries, in response to local wind conditions.
This allows the optimisation to reveal whether vertical resource exploitation or wake
avoidance is the dominant driver of the resulting layout.
The wind resource at both hub heights is analysed, and the difference in wind speed
between 150 m and 200 m is visualised in Figure 6.11. Areas with the strongest vertical
gain in wind speed are expected to influence the final positioning of the taller turbines.

Hypothesis and Outcome of Mixed Hub-Height Optimisation
It was hypothesised that a mixed-hub-height configuration would result in taller turbines
(200 m) being preferentially located in areas exhibiting the strongest vertical wind shear,
as this would maximise energy yield. Based on the resource analysis, regions within

and around wind farms 2, 3, 13, and 14 were expected to host most of the taller tur-
bines. In contrast, the 150 m turbines were anticipated to populate the surrounding,
lower-shear regions. The resulting layout was expected to reflect a resource-driven
optimisation pattern rather than one shaped by wake mitigation, possibly forming clus-
tered or concentric formations in high-shear zones.
However, the optimisation process did not complete successfully. The simulation con-
sistently terminated at various iteration steps and failed to converge to a valid or in-

the vertical wind gradient introduced additional complexity that the optimiser could not
resolve.
One possible explanation is that the increased scale and heterogeneity of the wind
resource may have introduced challenges not encountered in previous experiments.
An additional idealised experiment was conducted to investigate the cause of failure
in the original setup. This test used a uniform wind direction and the wind farm-based

the horizontally and vertically heterogeneity of the wind field from the WRF mesoscale
model. However, the resulting layout showed a random distribution of hub heights, with
no evidence of vertical staggering. Since the wind resource was uniform, this outcome
supports the hypothesis that vertical wind shear is a critical factor in driving meaningful
hub-height optimisation and spatial patterns.
Nevertheless, the hypothesis could not be effectively tested under the current setup.
The results highlight the need for further development of the optimisation framework to
accommodate the complexities introduced by vertical wind shear in real-world applica-
tions.
6.5
Synthesis and Implications
The Tamil Nadu case study confirms that spatial optimisation strategies can significantly
enhance overall wind farm performance. However, the gains in AEP come with trade-
offs that extend beyond technical efficiency.
Notably, the variable-density layout produced the highest total AEP (89.01 TWh), but
also introduced greater disparities between wind farms in terms of both turbine density
and capacity factor. Central wind farms such as 1, 2, and 3 exhibited the highest ca-
pacity factors and AEP per installed capacity, making them the most attractive from a
return-on-investment perspective. In contrast, wind farms like 5 or 10 showed lower
76
Efficiency of very large wind farms

performance, raising questions about their commercial viability.
This uneven distribution of performance has implications for large-scale offshore wind
development. In real projects, site attractiveness affects competition in tenders, will-
ingness to invest, and ultimately the success of project deployment. A layout that is
technically optimal but unevenly distributed may struggle to attract balanced developer
interest.
While uniform density layouts reduce this disparity slightly, they do not eliminate it.
The fundamental challenge lies in reconciling physical wind potential with equitable
site design.
This suggests that, beyond layout optimisation, complementary policy
mechanisms, such as differential lease pricing, capacity-based incentives, or wind farm-
specific tariffs, may be necessary to level the playing field across wind farms.
In conclusion, this study highlights that layout design should not only aim to maximise
AEP but must also consider how spatial decisions affect market dynamics and devel-
oper behaviour. Future offshore wind planning should integrate technical, economic,
and policy perspectives to ensure both efficiency and equity in deployment.

Discussion
This thesis aimed to improve the efficiency of a VLWF by exploring various layout strate-
gies, technological parameters, and optimisation approaches. The combined idealised
simulations and site-specific modelling over the Tamil Nadu region, which used Py-
wake, WRF-derived mesoscale wind data and TopFarm. The core focus was on how
wake effects, turbine density, and vertical wind profiles influence the AEP and capacity
factor.
This study highlights that strategic layout design can lead to measurable efficiency im-
provements in the VLWF. It also reveals the complex interplay between atmospheric
conditions and practical constraints.
A key finding is that cluster-based, non-uniform layouts outperform evenly spaced tur-
bines in large, multi-gigawatt projects. By adjusting turbine density to match local wind
conditions, the farm can generate more energy without increasing the number of tur-
bines.
For example, completely removing the internal boundaries between wind farms in-
creased the AEP by about 2.33% compared to the initial optimised layout. However,
this also uses a larger area, as buffer regions were included in the WF design, making
the design not feasible in real world cases.
A more realistic approach maintains wind farms for operational reasons but adjusts
their layout. Using the cluster boundaries as ”wake recovery” buffers resulted in a
0.25% AEP gain. This layout, however, increased the total area used, which may have
contributed to the gain. Additionally, reintroducing variable turbine densities within the
reconfigured wind farms led to a further AEP gain of approximately 0.74
Although these percentage gains may seem small, in an 18 GW wind farm, even a
fraction of a per cent translates to a substantial amount of energy. These improvements
were achieved solely by rearranging turbines more effectively within the same site area,
demonstrating how layout optimisation can significantly reduce wake losses at scale.
The optimisation process produced four distinct layout patterns across the 14 wind
farms, reflecting spatial variations in the wind resource. This highlights that the “optimal”
layout is highly site-specific and cannot be universally applied to other projects. In other
words, each VLWF requires a customised layout tailored to its unique wind conditions,
there is no one-size-fits-all solution.

==PAGE:97==
An important part of the methodology is the use of k-means clustering to generate new
layout designs. In this study, clustering was based on three input features: latitude,
longitude, and AEP, allowing the algorithm to group turbines that are close in both lo-
cation and performance. This offered a practical and computationally efficient way to
reorganise turbines into wind farms that better reflect variations in the wind resource.
To make new layouts more balanced and attractive from an operational or investment
perspective, additional metrics could be considered, such as capacity factor, installed
capacity, or cluster size. Including these factors would allow for the design of wind farms
that are not only efficient but also more balanced in terms of performance, making them
potentially more appealing to different stakeholders or developers.
While this implementation serves as a proof of concept, it demonstrates the potential
of data-driven clustering methods to guide future wind farm segmentation and layout
strategies.
One important aspect of the methodology that warrants reconsideration is the choice of
the wake model used at different stages of the optimisation process. The NOJ model
was selected for the idealised layout experiments because of its computational sim-
plicity and speed. However, in hindsight, it would have been more appropriate to use
the TurboGaussian wake model consistently across all experiments to ensure greater
accuracy and consistency in the results.
Since this thesis focuses on VLWFs, where turbine wakes can extend over distances
greater than 50 km, the NOJ model’s tendency to overestimate wake recovery becomes
a significant limitation. Although the Bastankhah Gaussian model offers improved ac-
curacy over NOJ, it still assumes relatively fast wake dissipation and vertical wind deficit
recovery. This may not accurately capture the prolonged wake interactions that occur
in wind farms of this scale.
In contrast, the TurboGaussian model offers a slower wake recovery, making it likely
better suited to capturing the extended wake effects and vertical wake development that
occur under stratified atmospheric conditions. This makes it particularly appropriate for
evaluating both layout interactions and hub height optimisation. If it had been applied
consistently throughout the study, it might have revealed more significant impacts of
vertical wind shear and wake layering, especially in the hub height analysis.
Using the TurboGaussian model in all experiments was not computationally feasible for
this study. Preliminary tests showed it tripled the processing time, making it impractical
for large-scale parametric runs and iterative optimisation.
While simplified models like NOJ and TurboGaussian were used for resolution and

computational reasons, it is important to reflect on the implications of different modelling
frameworks for wake prediction accuracy. In this study, both the PyWake and WRF
models were used at different stages. These two approaches represent fundamentally
different modelling scales and objectives.
PyWake is a microscale tool, ideal for turbine-level layout optimisation under steady-
state conditions. It provides high-resolution estimates of wake losses and turbine per-
formance but relies on simplified inflow assumptions and user-defined parameters for
turbulence and stability. In contrast, the WRF mesoscale model captures large-scale
atmospheric processes such as boundary layer development, stratification, and synop-
tic variability. When coupled with wind farm parameterisations, WRF can simulate the
cumulative influence of large arrays on atmospheric flow over tens of kilometres.
Together, these tools offer complementary perspectives: PyWake resolves turbine in-
teractions and layout efficiency, while WRF provides broader meteorological context,
including inter-farm wake effects and wind resource distribution. Differences in spatial
resolution and physical processes lead to divergent predictions for wake recovery, tur-
bulence, and energy yield, especially in very large wind farms. These discrepancies
may explain variations in AEP across different layouts and underscore the importance
of model selection in offshore wind design studies.
Finally, the planned analysis of the spatial distribution of hub heights at the Tamil Nadu
site could not be completed due to the probable compatibility issues between the out-
puts from the WRF mesoscale model and the TopFarm optimisation framework. De-
spite this limitation, the idealised experiments offered some insights: when the wind
resource was uniform, varying hub heights did not reduce wake effects. Instead, the
energy gains mainly came from accessing higher wind speeds caused by vertical shear,
making the AEP improvement highly dependent on the shear exponent. As mentioned
earlier, these findings could be affected by the choice of the wake model. The Bas-
tankhah Gaussian model used in these experiments tends to show faster wake recovery
compared to the TurboGaussian model. This may underestimate the benefits of ver-
tical staggering, especially at common turbine spacing distances. As a result, the full
impact of varying hub heights on wake interactions might not have been fully captured.
Despite the modelling limitations, the results suggest that the best hub height strategy
is to maximise the height of all turbines. This recommendation assumes that the extra
energy gained offsets the higher costs of taller structures, an assumption that should
be evaluated through techno-economic analysis in future research.

Future Recommendations
While this thesis focused on wake effects and power production efficiency, several criti-
cal factors remain unaddressed that could affect the practical feasibility of the proposed
layouts:
The optimisation was performed solely with respect to maximising the AEP. However,
capital expenditure (CAPEX), cable routing costs, and substation placement can signif-
icantly affect overall project feasibility. For example, longer inter-turbine distances may
improve energy yield but increase cable lengths and associated costs. Future work
should integrate techno-economic models that jointly optimise energy yield and infras-
tructure cost. In the same context, it is recommended that future planning frameworks
include cut-off performance thresholds, answering the question: “How much does a tur-
bine need to produce (in MWh/year) to remain economically viable in a given location?”
Establishing this threshold would help identify underperforming zones in large farms.
It should also be noted that this study relied exclusively on mesoscale modelling to
represent wind conditions across the entire wind farm domain. While WRF mesoscale
models are well-suited for capturing regional atmospheric patterns, their relatively coarse
spatial resolution (around 2 km for this study) limits their ability to resolve fine-scale flow
features, particularly those affected by local topography, thermal effects, or turbine-level
wake interactions. As a result, the resource inputs used for layout optimisation should
be considered estimations, not high-resolution ground truth. To improve confidence in
these inputs, future studies should incorporate validation against measurement data,
such as lidar, met mast, or floating buoy. This is particularly important for capturing
local shear profiles, turbulence intensity, and wind direction variability, factors that sig-
nificantly influence both wake behaviour and layout performance.
While this study did not consider any marine biodiversity, fisheries, shipping lines, or
seabed disruption, these are critical considerations for large offshore installations. An
environmental impact assessment should be conducted early in the planning phase to
ensure the final layout respects ecological and regulatory constraints.
As some wind farms or offshore zones may offer inherently lower wind speeds or less
favourable shear conditions, governmental or regulatory incentives might be considered
to support development in these areas. This could promote more balanced spatial use,
reduce clustering in high-resource zones, and help unlock less attractive locations for
clean energy expansion.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to explore how layout strategies can enhance the efficiency of very
large offshore wind farms (VLWFs), with a specific application to a 14 wind farm, 18 GW
project off the coast of Tamil Nadu, India. Through a series of idealised experiments
and subsequent full-site applications, three research questions were addressed, each
focusing on a distinct component of wind farm optimisation: spatial layout, wake mod-
elling, and hub height variation. The following summarises the findings in response to
each question.
1. To what extent can variable-density, wind farm-based layouts improve perfor-
mance compared to uniform spacing in large-scale offshore wind farms?
Variable-density layouts can significantly improve the performance of VLWFs by adapt-
ing to local wind resource conditions and implementing strategic wake losses. In this
study, fully removing intra-wind farm boundaries increased AEP by approximately ~2.33%
compared to the optimised initial layout. However, this approach also expanded the
overall layout area, including the buffer zones, and hence is not considered feasible for
real-world projects. A more practical solution is to utilise the wind farm boundaries as
advantages in wake recovery. Modifying wind farm boundaries while maintaining oper-
ational separations still achieved a meaningful AEP gain of ~0.25% without increasing
the number of turbines or installed capacity. Additionally, applying variable turbine den-
sities within wind farms led to an AEP gain of ~0.74%.
During the optimisation process, four distinct turbine layout patterns were observed
across the VLWF, mainly influenced by the local wind resource distribution. This high-
lights that optimal layouts are highly site-specific and cannot be easily generalised to
other locations.
However, the results also show that aligning wind farm boundaries with natural wind
flow features, such as prevailing direction and wake recovery regions, consistently im-
proved performance while supporting operational feasibility. Overall, clustering aligned
to the specific condition at one site combined with a variable-density layout represents
a simple yet effective strategy and should be considered a key design principle in the
planning of VLWFs.
2. What is the impact of different wake models on layout optimisation outcomes?
The choice of wake model has a significant impact on both the resulting wind farm
layout and the estimated performance during optimisation. In this study, the layout
Efficiency of very large wind farms
83
==PAGE:101==
design process was conducted using the PyWake framework, which integrates several
wake models of varying complexity. Simpler models, such as the Jensen (NOJ) model,
were used in idealised experiments due to their computational efficiency. As seen in
Figure 5.6, similar results for layout positioning were achieved using NOJ and Basthang.
However, NOJ tends to underpredict wake effects in complex layouts, especially in
stable atmospheric conditions or when turbine spacing is tight.
More advanced wake models, such as the Bastankhah Gaussian and TurboGaussian
models, present a more accurate representation of wake behaviour. Particularly for
modelling wake expansion vertically. This was especially important when optimising
hub heights, as wake expansion contrary to the NOJ model is not linear. To balance
accuracy and computational cost, this thesis used the NOJ model for layout optimisa-
tions and the Bastankhah Gaussian model for hub height optimisations.
Beyond the wake models themselves, incorporating the WRF mesoscale model was
crucial for capturing spatial variability and large-scale flow structures. The use of a
mesoscale-derived wind field allowed the study to account for wind direction frequen-
cies, vertical shear, and atmospheric stability factors that strongly influence the optimal
placement and height of turbines. These considerations revealed that wake losses and
flow interactions are not uniform across the site, and that layout decisions must reflect
this heterogeneity to be effective.
In conclusion, the accuracy and resolution of the wake model directly affect layout out-
comes, particularly in large-scale farms. While simple models are useful for fast initial
optimisation, more advanced models that capture atmospheric and spatial complexities
are essential for final layout evaluation. Therefore, both model selection and mesoscale
integration should be aligned with the stage of design and the resolution required for
reliable performance forecasting.
3. How does the variation of hub height affect wake interactions and energy
yield?
The variation of hub height plays an important role in influencing wake interactions and
overall energy yield, particularly under stratified atmospheric conditions. In this study,
hub height optimisation was performed using the Bastankhah Gaussian wake model,
which captures both vertical wind shear and wake expansion.
To isolate the effect of shear, experiments were conducted both with and without a
vertically sheared wind profile. It was found that increased hub height only led to im-
provements in energy yield when vertical shear was present. In cases without shear,
such as neutral or unstable atmospheric conditions, wind speed was nearly uniform
with height, and increasing hub height produced no measurable benefit. This indicates
84
Efficiency of very large wind farms
==PAGE:102==
that the performance gain from taller turbines was primarily due to accessing higher
wind resource, not from mitigating wake interactions.
Importantly, the study did not find that varying hub heights contributed meaningfully to
wake mitigation under the applied modelling framework. The observed gains stemmed
from accessing higher wind resources, not from reductions in wake effects on down-
stream turbines. However, it is plausible that under different modelling assumptions,
vertical staggering could influence wake behaviour more significantly.
In summary, this study demonstrates that the strategic optimisation of wind farm layout,
turbine positioning, and hub height can lead to significant gains in AEP. While modelling
uncertainties remain, the use of re-clustering, variable turbine density, and adaptive
hub heights proves to be a scalable and effective strategy for very large offshore wind
farms. Crucially, the results highlight that optimal layouts are highly site-specific and
must be tailored to the unique wind resource distribution and flow conditions of each
location; there is no universal solution. These findings underscore the importance of
full-site layout optimisation that integrates aerodynamic performance and wake mitiga-
tion. Although techno-economic feasibility was not addressed in this study, it remains
a critical factor for practical implementation and should be considered in future work.
