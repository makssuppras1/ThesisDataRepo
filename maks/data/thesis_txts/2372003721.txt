Abstract
High voltage DC energy transmission is increasing the interest of researchers and manu-
facturers as a possible next step in the development of energy transmission systems. One
of the limitations in implementing such systems is the HVDC breakers, which require
more sophisticated approaches to meet the requirement set by today’s standards for energy
systems.
The aim of this project is to design, build and test a scaled down version of a possible solu-
tion - a hybrid breaker. During the process to determine the challenges and shortcomings
of such devices.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
High-voltage direct current (HVDC) systems has been increasing in popularity over the
past decades. That is due to the fact that HVDC in comparison to traditional High Voltage
Alternating Current (HVAC) systems has lower losses, higher power carrying capacity,
better stability characteristics and line length is not limited by line reactances ([1] ch.10).
These are important features when transporting high amounts of energy over long distances
as it is commonly the case in countries like China or India, where a lot of power is
generated up to thousands of kilometers away from load centers ([2] p.31). Furthermore,
HVDC grids are very suitable for energy transmission to and from off-shore equipment,
like wind farms ([2] p.25,26), where highly capacitive cables have to be used.
Up until now, almost all of the HVDC transmission systems have been only two-point
systems used for interconnecting systems or energy transmission in submarine and long
distance applications. The main barrier in the establishment of multi-point HVDC systems
has always been inability to protect the system against faults by isolating only the faulty
part instead of shutting down the whole system. During the last few years, this problem
has been thoroughly analyzed in anticipations of creating a suitable HVDC circuit breaker.
1.1
Problem deﬁnition
In AC systems, even during the fault, current is oscillating between positive and negative
values at the system frequency. Two times in every period, current crosses a zero value,
which allows for the arc between breaker contacts to be extinguished. In DC systems
that is not the case and when the fault current is being disconnected it creates an arc
which is sustained by an almost constant current which does not allow for the dielectric
material between the breaker contacts to recover its insulating properties. Due to this,
traditional breakers used in HVAC systems would not work in HVDC systems and a more

sophisticated solution has to be found.
1.2
Existing solutions
All of the proposed solutions can be classiﬁed into three categories: mechanical, solid-state
and hybrid breakers [3].
Mechanical breakers rely on a resonant circuit connected in parallel to them to artiﬁcially
create a zero crossing this way extinguishing the arc between the breaker contacts. The
main problem of mechanical breakers is long tripping time (approx. 60 ms), which is not
fast enough for DC systems.
Solid state circuit breakers are established using semiconductors and have very fast switch-
ing capabilities (can be less than 1 ms). But unlike mechanical breakers, they suffer from
high power losses during normal operation as the current has to pass through an array of
semiconductor modules (i.e. IGBTs), which, in high voltage applications, can consist of
tens to hundreds of IGBTs in series, adding up to a substantial forward voltage drop.
Hybrid breakers are a combinations of two: a mechanical breaker is connected with a
semiconductor breaker in parallel. During normal operation current passes through the
mechanical breaker and during switching mechanical breaker gets disconnected and the
current goes through the parallel semiconductor branch, which disconnects it without an
arc. Because mechanical breaker is still used it this approach, total disconnection time is
longer than the one of solid state breakers, but it allows the use of much faster and smaller
mechanical breakers as it no longer needs to suppress the arc. This puts them in between
the previously mentioned types of breakers according to the switching speed.
1.3
Approach
The hybrid breaker is the newest, the most promising approach and is receiving a lot of
attention from researchers and even from manufacturers like ABB. In this work, the scaled-
down design of a hybrid circuit breaker will be presented and practical implementation
built and tested.

CHAPTER 2
Overview
The circuit breakers are one of the key components in power systems. The main function
is to connect and disconnect other components of the system, like lines, transformers,
loads, etc. The purposes can be a rearrangement of the layout of a system, disconnection
of underloaded, overloaded or faulty components, as well as for scheduled maintenance
or other planned disconnections. The ﬁrst high voltage oil circuit breaker was patented
more than a hundred years ago ([4] p.58). Over this time many different types of circuit
breaker were developed as standards for effectiveness, longevity and cost-efﬁciency have
increased. Nowadays, even though oil circuit breakers are still quite common, they are
being replaced by their more sophisticated counterparts, like SF6 gas circuit breakers.
Despite many engineers spending countless hours in the development process of these
breakers, their application is limited to the AC systems. The limitation comes from
the fact that all of them are based on zero current crossing when the current crosses
a zero and the dielectric material between the breaker contacts recovers its dielectric
properties. Because of this reason, these breakers are not suitable for HVDC systems,
unless some modiﬁcations are made, like in resonant circuit based mechanical breakers,
which artiﬁcially create a zero crossing [5].
Another important difference between AC and DC systems is the system reactance. In AC
systems, line inductance works as a current limiting reactor as the current is constantly
changing and creating a changing magnetic ﬁeld around the wires, this way inducing a
voltage which opposes to that current change. This inductance, most of the time, exceeds
the resistance and dominates the impedance of the line. In DC systems, on the other
hand, line inductance has no inﬂuence during the steady-state operation and only becomes
apparent during state changes, where it sets the rate of change of the current (di/dt), but
does not actually limit its peak value. This difference makes HVDC short circuit faults
more dangerous as the current for the same voltage level is much higher, thus setting high

requirements for speed.
Nowadays, when semiconductors are so widely used, they seem to overcome all of the
shortcomings of HVAC breakers in this application. Especially suited for this application
are Isolated Gate Bipolar Transistors (IGBT). They are fast - typical turn on/off speed
is in the range of nanoseconds while producing no arcing and having no other problems
disconnecting the current as long as it is within the speciﬁcations. Despite these advantages,
the major disadvantage is their forward voltage drop. In high current applications, typical
for energy transmission, it creates substantial steady-state losses, which are not acceptable
for the component, which might be used only a few times in its entire lifespan.
The possible solution was found by combining both, a mechanical breaker and semicon-
ductors into one breaker - hybrid breaker. The hybrid breaker was ﬁrst introduced by
"ABB" as a solution to the main HVDC energy transmission problem. It combines low
losses of mechanical breakers and fast high current switching capabilities of IGBTs.

CHAPTER 3
Design
In this chapter, the speciﬁcations for the breaker and the design of it will be presented. The
speciﬁcations are arbitrary and the design of the breaker is for the purposes of proving the
concept and analysis of its functionality and limitations rather than presenting an exact
solution to be implemented in existing or emerging power systems.
3.1
Basic design
The main idea behind the hybrid circuit breaker is to combine the advantages of both,
the mechanical breakers and the solid-state semiconductor-based breakers, that are low
losses and short breaking time respectively. The design that is currently being presented,
researched and developed by researchers and manufacturers [6] is a combination of both
connected in parallel as shown in Figure 3.1.
For simplicity reasons, the IGBT connected in series with the mechanical switch (Switch)
will be called switching IGBT (SwIGBT) and the stack of IGBTs connected in parallel
to Switch and SwIGBT will be called disconnecting IGBTs (IGBTs).

Variations in overvoltage protection implementation may occur depending on the differ-
ences in voltage level and protection used. In some cases, the array of IGBTs is divided
into sections, each of which has its own over voltage protection.
For each IGBT there is another one faced in the opposite direction. Because IGBTs can
withstand only low negative voltages across collector and emitter, a freewheeling diode is
added to protect them against it. Due to that, IGBT can only break current in one direction
and an opposing IGBT has to be added in series to allow for bidirectional breaking.
Switch is a mechanical switch, which has to have sufﬁcient current carrying and voltage
withstand capabilities, but does not require to be able to break high current. The switching
time is also one of the main requirement, because it is the slowest component in the hybrid
breaker, requiring at least few milliseconds to operate in comparison to other solid-state
components in the design, which operation time is less than 1µs.
3.2
Operation principle
As shown in Figure 3.2, the disconnection process of the hybrid breaker consists of four
stages. The ﬁrst stage (a) is the current ﬂow (shown in red) during normal operation, when
the current passes mainly through the Switch and SwIGBT, because of the larger voltage
drop across the array of IGBTs. This way, voltage drop across the breaker is very small
and does not produce high losses. When the breaker has to disconnect the current, the
control logic turns off SwIGBT, this forces the current to run through the disconnecting
IGBTs (b). At this point, the total voltage drop is equal to the total forward voltage of
the stack, which should be within the withstand capabilities of the switching IGBT. Now,
because the current does not pass through the switch, it can disconnect without an arc.
When the switch disconnects, the control logic moves to the third stage (c) by closing the
gates of the disconnecting IGBTs. The inductances in the system should resist high di/dt
and increase the voltage to such level at which overvoltage protection starts to conduct the
current, but the voltage level should not exceed the withstand voltage of the whole stack of
IGBTs. The current decreases to zero as the inductances of the system discharge through
the protective device. While the inductor is discharging through the varistor, the parasitic
capacitances in the protective device itself and disconnecting IGBTs charge to the voltage
level set by that device. In the last stage (d), those parasitic capacitances should discharge
as the voltage set by the overvoltage protection is higher than the supply voltage. Those
capacitances together with the inductance L should create another transient process.

3.4
Power component selection
3.4.1
IGBT
According to the established criteria, IXYS Semiconductor IXYX100N120B3 IGBT was
chosen. The datasheet [7] speciﬁes the breakdown voltage of VCES = 1200 V and the
short-term current handling of IC25 = 225 A. For the simplicity reasons, the same IGBTs
will be used as the switching IGBT and in the array of disconnecting IGBTs.
Even though one IGBT can withstand the maximum speciﬁed voltage and lower voltage
IGBTs theoretically could be used in the series stack, due to slight differences in their
switching times, the fastest IGBT, during switching process could possibly be exposed to
the full voltage. The stacking of IGBTs in series is a research area in itself and is not the
topic of this project, therefore the IGBTs of sufﬁcient withstand voltage were chosen to
avoid problems that might arise due to this.
3.4.2
Overvoltage protection
For the overvoltage protection, a varistor was chosen. Selection was done from the TDK
manufacturer catalog [8]. From the graph shown in Figure 3.3, for the required 200 A the
varistor model S20K120 was chosen. For the given current, it should limit the voltage at
around 700 V , which is sufﬁciently higher than the nominal voltage, but much lower than
the breakdown voltage of the IGBTs.

3.4.3
Mechanical switch
In addition to previously speciﬁed requirements, speed is one of the important factors for
the mechanical switch. Limited component selection and pricing lead to the KILOVAC
LEV200 electro-mechanical series contactor, which has dielectric withstand voltage of
2200 V and continuous current carrying capability of 500 A.
The breaking time, provided by the datasheet [9] is given for the most severe conditions
with the arcing included. The test was carried out to see what is the contact separation
time, by applying a small current and measuring with an oscilloscope the time between
disconnection of coil supply voltage and the point when the current stopped. The results
are given in Figure 3.4. From the test, the disconnection time was determined to be
tbreak = 4.7 ms.

4.1
Implications
The results show that the hybrid breaker should work without a zero crossing and the
excessive energy stored in the inductance of the system should be handled by the surge
protection, which, in this case, is a varistor. The mechanical switch opens in a no-load
condition and should not produce any arcs in practice. Therefore, the main parameters to
be considered for it are the switching speed and the current capabilities in closed position.

Test setup
For this test, the setup circuit was designed as shown in Figure 5.1. The current ﬂow
during the different stages is shown in Figure 5.2. In the ﬁrst stage (a), the current ﬂows
through both of the resistors and the magnitude should reach only the nominal value. In
the second stage (b), Control Switch creates a bypass around the R2, this way reducing
the total resistance of the circuit and the current starts increasing. In the third stage (c),
the control picks measures current exceeding the set maximum limit and disconnects the
switching IGBT and that forces the current to ﬂow through the disconnecting IGBTs. At
the same time, the control turns off the mechanical switch and waits for the speciﬁed
amount of time (5 ms), until the breaker opens. In the last stage (d), when the mechanical
switch is open, the control turns off the disconnecting IGBTs. The inductor, due to the
sudden change in current (di/dt) increases the voltage until the varistor starts conducting
the current.

State logic
Figure 5.4: Stateﬂow logic
In Figure 5.4, the Stateﬂow logic is shown. The logic starts at the top left corner and then
propagates according to the conditions set on the connecting arrows. At ﬁrst, logic resides
in "WaitForTurnOn" block and can propagate to the right if the start button (which state is
carried by the StartButton variable) was pressed and the current does not exceed the limit,
and there are no failures in the system. If all of the conditions are met, the logic moves to
the block "TurnOnIGBTs", in which it sets the "IGBTs" output to True, and should raise
corresponding digital output pin to a HIGH state. Variables "ﬂag" and "active" are status
variables which carry the status of the logic to other blocks. The variable "active" activates
the "Data logging" block and "ﬂag" becomes active when the breaker has tripped and is
used to control external LED for the user to see if there is data available for download. It
is reset every time user downloads the data or when the breaker is started again.
After 10 µs, if the current was not exceeded and there are no failures, the logic moves to
the "TurnOnBreaker" state, which turns on the mechanical relay. After 30 ms the switch
should be closed and switching IGBT could be turned on. That is done by moving to the
"TurnOnSwIGBT" state, but only if, once again, the current was not exceeded and no
error occurred. The logic stays in this state until the current is exceeded, the fails occurs
or the stop button is pressed. If any of those events happens, the logic begins turn-off
sequence, which is the opposite to the turn-on sequence, except for different timing due to
the different turn-on and turn-off speeds of the mechanical relay.

5.1.2
Current measurement
The microcontroller can only measure values that are between 0 and 3 V and cannot deal
with current directly, therefore the additional circuit for current measurement had to be
designed.
First, the current measurement is done by the schematic shown in Figure 5.9. Current is
measured by a voltage transducer "LEM LA 125-P", able to handle currents up to 200 A.
The transducer has the ratio of 1000 : 1, so its output current is then passed through four
120 Ωresistors connected in parallel to create 30 Ωload recommended by the datasheet.
The output is driven by the inverting voltage follower schematic based on operational
ampliﬁer "LM7171".
The input to the ADC is handled by the circuit shown in Figure 5.10. Looking from the
right, the input has the impedance of 50 Ω, which together with the output resistor of
current measurement circuit makes up a voltage divider, which decreases the voltage to
a level acceptable for the MCU. To invert back inverted output and add gain and offset
adjustment functionality, a second operational ampliﬁer is used. Because, according to the
datasheet, it becomes unstable with the gain below G = −1 [12], the voltage is divided
again by the R15 and R20 resistors to ensure bidirectional adjustability.

#define GATE1 3
// PortB pin 3 or P11
#define GATE2 4
// PortB pin 4 or P12
#define BUTTON 2
// Button pin
unsigned long previousTime = 0;
// setup code to run once:
void setup() {
// configure gate pins
DDRB |= (1<<GATE1)|(1<<GATE2);
// gates' pins as outputs
PORTB |= (1<<GATE1)|(1<<GATE2);
// both gates off
// create an interrupt for the button press
attachInterrupt(digitalPinToInterrupt(BUTTON), run10ms, RISING);
}
// main loop
void loop() {
}
// interrupt service routine
void run10ms(void)
{
unsigned long now = millis();
// get current time
// if the button was not pressed in the last second
if (now-previousTime > 1000)
{
PORTB &= ~(1<<GATE1);
// turn gate1 on
delayMicroseconds(200);
// wait for 200us
PORTB &= ~(1<<GATE2);
// turn gate2 on
delayMicroseconds(10000-200);
// wait till 10ms mark
PORTB |= (1<<GATE1)|(1<<GATE2);
// turn both gates off
previousTime = now;
// save current timer value
}
}

plummets to zero. At that point voltage (Fig. 6.4) across the breaker increases drastically
to around 660 V . When the varistor starts to conduct the current, the voltage immediately
drops to around 590 V . With the inductor discharging, the current through the varistor
decreases steadily and the varistor voltage declines as well. As soon as the current reaches
zero, the oscillations begin as was observed in the simulation results. The oscillations
of the current have almost negligible magnitude, but by closer observation, it is visible
that the current through the varistor is leading the voltage by approximately 90°, which
conﬁrms the simulation results.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The hybrid breaker design proved to be working not only in simulation but in practice
as well. The tests proved the model to be working as intended without any unexpected
problems.
The design itself is relatively simple and does not require sophisticated control. Apart
from the current measurements, for example, if the control was done by a different device,
like a digital protective relay, the control logic for commutating devices inside the hybrid
breaker does not require sophisticated control algorithms and could be done by a much
simpler microcontroller than the one that was used in this project.
One of the challenges in the design of this type of a breaker is the mechanical switch. It is
the slowest component in the system by a big margin and therefore it is the component
which sets the interruption time for the breaker. Due to this fact, speed for the mechanical
switch is of key importance and should be considered deeply.
Another challenge is the disconnecting IGBTs. IGBTs today can withstand up to couple
kilovolts, but for HVDC transmission applications that is not sufﬁcient. Higher voltage ap-
plications require stacking of the IGBTs in series, which in turn require more sophisticated
control, supplementary circuitry or very thorough selection of IGBTs for each stack.
