Abstract
Due to the global growing demand for food, especially that of animal protein products which
have a large impact on the climate, alternatives such as mealworm larvae food products
are becoming more relevant as a sustainable source of protein. Utilising insects as food in
Denmark is still relatively uncommon, and potential human pathogens which are related to
mealworm larve food products must be investigated and evaluated.
Bacillus cereus strains which produce the emetic toxin cereulide are deemed a human
pathogen, and the strain 21792 (also known as F4810/72) was therefore investigated. Its
ability to produce bacterial spores means that the usual heat treatments in the food pro-
duction industry are ineﬃcient in making the product safe due to the heat stability of the
spores.
The fate of the B. cereus spores in mealworm larvae was investigated by exposure to
four diﬀerent concentrations of spores (0 CFU/g, and 2.0, 4.0 and 6.0 log10(CFU/g)), and
it was found that the higher the exposure, the higher the concentration of spores was found
in the mealworm larvae. It was also found that while sterile dry substrate was used for the
experimental design, the mealworm larvae may have contained enough spores prior to the
exposure study to contaminate the dry substrate and thereby aﬀect the level of exposure.
Means of reducing the level of spores were investigated, speciﬁcally by application of dou-
ble heat treatments which were hypothesised to ﬁrst activate the spores into germination and
outgrowth, followed by elimination the spores once they had outgrown into the signiﬁcantly
less heat stable vegetative cells. However, this method was concluded to be ineﬃcient as
the spores did not germinate, and instead the total aerobic count within mealworm larvae
samples was found to increase suﬃciently to start production and accumulation of even more
spores within the samples.
Based on the results, it was concluded that several factors had to be investigated to
enhance the exposure study and make it more accurate for a better understanding of the
fate of B. cereus spores in the mealworm larvae. Similarly, the potential of a double heat
treatment and the factors that trigger spores into germination should be investigated further.

Introduction
The global demand for food is growing steadily as a result of a continuous growth of the
total human population. It is estimated that the world population will increase with 34% by
2050. In order to feed a population this large, the food production must similarly increase
with at least an estimated 70%. Speciﬁcally the annual meat production must increase with
more than 200 million tonnes to reach a needed 470 million tonnes [FAO (2009)].
With climate changes transforming the planet’s ecosystem, food production must also
take the emissions of CO2 into consideration. Animal proteins such as beef, pig meat and
poultry meat account for a majority of emissions in the livestock sector, contributing 41%,
9% and 8% respectively [Gerber et al. (2013)]. It is therefore clear that more sustainable
and alternative solutions to conventional livestock need to be introduced in order to meet
the continuously growing demand.
Insects such as the yellow mealworm Tenebrio molitor show great potential for food and
feed as a more environmentally friendly choice when it comes to edible animal protein sources
[Akhtar and Isman (2017), Oonincx and de Boer (2012)]. Tenebrio molitor is high in protein,
and has a short life cycle compared to conventional livestock. This insect species can also
eﬃciently bioconvert agricultural co-products, by-products or unutilised waste streams as
feed [Houbraken et al. (2016)].
In Denmark, legislation allows the production and sale of insects for consumption as long
as all risks (for example pathogenic microorganisms in the products and content of potential
toxins) have been evaluated (see Section 4). The insects must be fed with substrate of same
quality and standard as feed for conventional livestock. This means that the substrate must
be safe (free of pathogens), not have direct adverse eﬀect on the environment or animal
welfare, and it must be ﬁt for the purpose. The substrate is also not allowed to contain
remains of for example meat or ﬁsh, kitchen waste, manure or waste from puriﬁcation plants
[Ministry of Environment and Food of Denmark (2018)].
One potential human pathogen in the substrate is an emetic Bacillus cereus strain which
produces endospores and the toxin cereulide in its vegetative state (see Section 3). Therefore,
the overall aim for this thesis is to investigate the fate of B. cereus spores in mealworm larvae,

and how diﬀerent levels of spore exposure aﬀects the concentration in the mealworm larvae.
The focus is on the dry substrate and the presence of spores as the microbiological hazard.
The consequence of mealworm larvae ingesting spores is investigated by studying if an
accumulation occurs in the guts of the mealworm larvae, or if the spores pass through and
potentially disappear completely.
Lastly, means to reduce the level of spores within the
mealworm larvae are investigated.
To observe the fate of the B. cereus spores within the mealworm larvae, an exposure
study was conducted. By introducing groups of mealworm larvae to substrate spiked with
diﬀerent levels of spores, the concentration within the substrate and mealworm larvae could
be analysed over a time period of 14 days.
A method of applying double heat treatments to potentially remove the spores in the
mealworm larvae was developed. This was based on the hypothesis that ingested spores
would germinate and outgrow into vegetative cells (described in Section 3). The method was
tested on mealworm larvae samples incubated at diﬀerent temperatures and durations.
2
Tenebrio molitor
As mentioned previously, the chosen insect for the study is the yellow mealworm Tenebrio
molitor due to its short life cycle and high protein concentration.
When working with insects such as the protein rich yellow mealworm Tenebrio molitor,
it is important to know of and understand the life cycle as it is preferable to harvest them
when the yield is the highest. Similarly, it is important to understand what conditions of the
environment are best for the insects during production, once again leading back to providing
the best conditions for the highest yield.
2.1
The life cycle of mealworms
The yellow mealworm Tenebrio molitor has a short life cycle compared to conventional live-
stock, and it goes through four overall life stages in which metamorphosis occurs; egg to larva
to pupa to beetle. A visualisation of this and the diﬀerent life stages can be seen in Figure
1 [Andersen et al. (2017)].
2

Figure 1: Visualisation of the life cycle of the yellow mealworm Tenebrio molitor, showing the four main
life stages (egg, larva, pupa and beetle). Figure is an adaptation of ﬁgure found in Andersen et al. (2017).
Mealworms start in the egg life stage which lasts a few days up to a week. The second
life stage, the larva stage, lasts several weeks. During this life stage, the mealworm larva
goes through several molts to shed its outer shell to grow. The larva starts out very small
(approximately 3 mm) but eventually grows to be around 25-35 mm long with a weight of
approximately 0.2 g. The larva stage is followed by the pupa stage, and lastly the beetle
stage. This stage is when reproduction occurs, and the female beetle lays approximately 5-8
eggs a day (around 300 eggs over the total adult lifespan) [Andersen et al. (2017), Mariod
et al. (2017)].
The larva stage is the interesting one of the four as it is the primary stage in which the
mealworms experience a biomass increase [Andersen et al. (2017)]. The nutritional value of
mealworms in the larva stage is approximately 20% protein, 13% fat, 62% moisture and 2%
ﬁber [Mariod et al. (2017)]. Vandeweyer et al. (2017a) found the mean pH in mealworm larvae
to 6.7 ± 0.02 and the mean water activity to 0.96 when studying the intrinsic parameters of
mealworm larvae.
2.2
Steps of production
A production of mealworms includes four main steps; reproduction, production, separation
and euthanisation. An overview of these four steps and the overall production can be seen
in Figure 2.

Reproduction
A production of mealworms is started with the mother culture. This consists of adult beetles
and the eggs that they lay. To optimise the production and the overall yield, it is of interest
to optimise the reproduction. It has been found that the overall egg yield is optimised when
the density of beetles is 0.84 beetles per cm2. For the best genetic variety, the ratio between
male and female beetles should be approximately 1:1. This ratio also increases competition
between the male beetles [Andersen et al. (2017)].
The eggs are separated from the mother culture of beetles every three days. To keep track
of the age of separated eggs, the separation can potentially be conducted daily.
Production
To optimise the yield of production, it is of importance to ensure the best living conditions for
the mealworms during production. Factors such as temperature, relative humidity, density
of mealworms, and the composition of the feed play a big part in achieving the best yield.
The usual production temperature is 25-30°C and the optimal humidity is 50-70%. The
density of the mealworms in a production box can be changed over time, and smaller boxes
(20 × 30 cm) are generally used in the ﬁrst few weeks of the mealworms’ life cycle to ensure
high density. In the ﬁrst month, the optimal density is 10 larvae per cm2. Once the mealworm
larvae are moved to bigger production boxes (40 × 60 cm), the density is around 5 larvae
per cm2 [Andersen et al. (2017)].
During the production, the mealworms are fed dry substrate as their primary source of
nutrition and wet substrate (such as brewers’ spent grain or carrots) as their water source.
The larvae have a high feed conversion rate of approximately 2:1 (potentially higher for

optimal yield), and the dry substrate is therefore added in an approximated 2:1 ratio (feed
to larvae) throughout this life stage. The larvae are also given brewers’ spent grain as their
water source. The beetles have a signiﬁcantly lower feed conversion rate, and are fed less dry
substrate and carrots instead of brewers’ spent grain as their water source [Andersen et al.
(2017)].
Separation
Separation of the adult mealworm larvae from substrate residues, faeces, and larvae not ready
for harvesting is done when the adult mealworms larvae are approximately 50-60 days old
with a weight of approximately 170 mg. The separation process is conducted using sieves of
varying mesh sizes. The separated mealworm larvae are euthanised (killed painlessly) while
the residues of substrate and faeces are discarded [Andersen et al. (2017)].
To keep a production going, pupae (or 5-10% of larvae) [Andersen et al. (2017)] can be
separated to start a new mother culture.
Euthanisation
Mealworms are normally euthanised in two steps. First step is a starvation for approximately
24 hours in a 15°C (or lower) room, followed by the ﬁnal euthanisation step in a freezer
(approximately -18°C) for at least 48 hours. During the euthanisation, it is important that
the dead mealworms do not come in contact with living mealworms [Andersen et al. (2017)].
2.3
Gut microbiota
The microbiota of the mealworm larvae’s guts is of importance when following the fate of
B. cereus spores. The background ﬂorae of bacteria and bacterial spores is relevant when
studying exposure of additional B. cereus spores as the microbiota will aﬀect the results of
these experiments.
The conditions within the mealworm larvae gut are of importance for potential microbial
growth. Vinokurov et al. (2006) found that the pH of the midgut of Tenebrio molitor can
be described with a gradient in which the anterior third of the midgut had a pH of 5.2-5.6,
while the middle midgut experienced a slight increase, ranging from 5.9-6.5, and, lastly, a
5

pH of 7.8-8.2 was found in the posterior third of the midgut [Vinokurov et al. (2006)].
A study by Osimani et al. (2018) investigated the bacterial biota of laboratory-reared
edible mealworms with viable microbial counts.
When studying fresh mealworm larvae,
the microbial count of enterobacteriaceae was found to approximately 106-107 CFU/g. The
count of spore forming bacteria after heat treatment at 80°C for 15 minutes was determined
to approximately 103 CFU/g.
In a diﬀerent study by Klunder et al. (2012), the enterobacteriaceae and bacterial spores
counts were found to 106 CFU/g and 102 CFU/g respectively. Similarly, Vandeweyer et al.
(2017b) found the total aerobic count and bacterial spore count to 107-108 CFU/g and 102
CFU/g respectively. The total aerobic count was found to be similar in studies by Stoops
et al. (2016) and Stoops et al. (2017). However, the bacterial spore count in these studies
varied between < 101 CFU/g to 104 CFU/g.
3
Bacillus cereus
Bacillus cereus sensu stricto (referred to as Bacillus cereus) is a gram positive rod-shaped
bacterium which belongs to the group B. cereus (consisting of B. cereus sensu stricto, B.
anthracis, B. thuringiensis, B. mycoides, B. pseudomycoides, B. weihenstephanensis, and B.
cytotoxicus [Savini et al. (2013)]) and the larger genus Bacillus with the ability to sporulate
and produce endospores (referred to as spores). While the majority of species belonging to
the Bacillus genus are rarely associated with diseases in humans, the B. cereus group are an
exception [EFSA (2016)].
B. cereus grows well under aerobic conditions with an optimum temperature for growth
between 28-35°C and within a pH range of 4.3 to approximately 9.3. The minimum water
activity for vegetative growth of B. cereus is 0.912 [Eglezos and Dykes (2014)]. B. cereus also
grows in anaerobic conditions as it is a facultatively anaerobic bacterium [Bottone (2010)].
3.1
Illnesses caused by B. cereus
Depending on the strain of B. cereus, the bacterium is capable of causing two diﬀerent
types of illnesses. The ﬁrst is a diarrhoeal syndrome which is caused by the production of
6

enterotoxins. The diarrhoeal syndrome is caused by ingestion of B. cereus spores in a food
source. The spores are transferred to the small intestine of the host where optimal growth
conditions allow the spores to germinate. The vegetative B. cereus cells then produce the
enterotoxins, usually after an incubation period of 8-16 hours in the intestine. The duration
of the illness is commonly 12-24 hours and it includes symptoms such as abdominal pain and
watery diarrhoea. The foods most frequently implicated in this illness are meat products,
milk products and vegetables, and the dose of cells needed in these to cause the diarrhoeal
syndrome is 105-107 CFU total [Matthews et al. (2017)].
The second illness is an emetic syndrome. This syndrome is caused by a production of
the toxin cereulide in the food product itself before ingestion. Cereulide is produced in a
product by vegetative B. cereus cells. The surviving spores, which are relatively heat resistant
[Batt (2014)], outgrow into vegetative cells and produce cereulide in the food product at
temperatures between 12-37°C (the optimal being 12-15°C) during the early stationary phase
of growth [Griﬃths and Schraft (2017)]. Cereulide is very heat stable (resists 90 minutes at
126°C [Griﬃths and Schraft (2017)]), meaning reheating of a food product is insuﬃcient in
removing the toxin [Rajkovic et al. (2008)]. When ingested, the incubation period is 0.5-5
hours before symptoms like nausea and vomiting appear [Matthews et al. (2017)]. The foods
most frequently implicated are foods high in starch like cooked rice, pasta and pastries, and
the dose causing illness is 105-107 CFU total [Matthews et al. (2017)].
In this study, the focus is on the cereulide producing B. cereus strain 21792 (also known
as strain F4810/72 [Guinebretière et al. (2008)]). This strain is chosen due to the fact that
the temperature for mealworms production lies within the growth temperature for the strain
(15-45°C [Guinebretière et al. (2008)]).
B. cereus is generally widespread in nature and
frequently isolated from soil and plants, and it is easily spread from the environment to
foods. As mentioned, the cereulide producing strains are commonly found in starchy foods
like rice and wheat [Matthews et al. (2017)] which is relevant in regards to the dry substrate
which the mealworms are fed during production.

3.2
Spore sporulation and germination
As previously described, B. cereus’ ability to sporulate and produce highly resistant spores
(which can later germinate) is of importance when talking about foodborne illnesses. While
vegetative B. cereus cells can be killed by heat treatments such as 80°C for 10 minutes, the
spores can survive autoclaving [Matthews et al. (2017)] and pasteurisation [Matthews et al.
(2017)]. The spores of B. cereus are also hydrophobic, enabling them to adhere to surfaces
which makes them both diﬃcult to remove during cleaning and to target for disinfectants
[Matthews et al. (2017)].
3.2.1
Spore structure
The structure of the bacterial spores diﬀer from those of the vegetative cells. The structure
of a typical bacterial spore consists of the exosporium, spore coat, outer membrane, cortex,
germ cell wall, inner membrane and core [Leggett et al. (2012)]. Figure 3 visualises this
structure.

Starting from the outside, the outermost structure (the exosporium) of the spore is composed
of protein, lipids and carbohydrates. It has been suggested that the adherent, hydrophobic
properties of this structure are involved in the pathogenicity of some spores [Leggett et al.
(2012)]. The next structure (the spore coat) is placed within the exosporium (if present),
serving as a initial barrier between the spore cortex and any larger molecules. It consists of
several thin layers and is mainly made up of protein. Following the spore coat lies an outer

next structure. It is composed of peptidoglycan and identiﬁed as possibly being responsible
for maintaining dehydration of the core [Leggett et al. (2012)]. The germ cell wall, which is
the next structure, also contains peptidoglycan, and it becomes the cell wall when the spore
undergoes germination and outgrowth. Second to last is the inner membrane which protects
the core as it has a low permeability [Leggett et al. (2012)]. Lastly, at the centre, lies the
spore core which contains the DNA, RNA, ribosomes and most of the spore’s enzymes. The
core makes up 28-57% of the spore’s wet weight, and the conditions within are linked to the
resistance properties of the spore as these conditions help to protect the spore DNA from
damage [Leggett et al. (2012)].
3.2.2
Sporulation
When vegetative B. cereus cells experience environmental stress or nutrient depletion, the cell
responds by starting the sporulation process [Matthews et al. (2017)]. The process consists
of several steps which are visualised in Figure 4.

The sporulation is initiated with an asymmetric cell division. This process creates a smaller

the mother cell engulﬁng the smaller spore compartment, creating a cell (the smaller spore

in the developing spore as the sporulation proceeds. The spore is eventually encased in a
cortex followed by the spore coat (both previously described). After maturation, the spore
is released as a dormant mature spore [Matthews et al. (2017)].
3.2.3
Germination and outgrowth
Dormant spores can be triggered into activating the germination and outgrowth process.
This is done by exposing the spores to conditions such as heat, pH or chemical exposure
which may activate the process [Leggett et al. (2012)]. This process is of importance when
dealing with B. cereus spores in food products as it is the key factor in the production of
vegetative cells either in food products or a host after ingestion.
Appropriate conditions trigger an activation of the dormant spores which makes it enter
germination. Once the germination has been initiated, the spore is committed to this phase
and can no longer return to its dormant state. The germination is triggered in response to
stimuli such as metabolisable nutrient germinants (for example sugars or amino acids), non-
metabolisable germinants and some physical treatments (for example high pressure) [Leggett
et al. (2012)].
After germination, outgrowth begins. However, the exact transition between these two
processes is not distinct [Matthews et al. (2017)].
The outgrowth process consists of all
developmental events that take place after germination, and it represents a return of the
spore to vegetative cell growth. This includes an initiation of metabolism, swelling of the
spore, emergence from the outer spore layers and growth of the new cell [Leggett et al.
(2012)]. The outgrowth process requires the synthesis of new amino acids, nucleotides, and
other small molecules [Matthews et al. (2017)].

3.3
Cereulide
Under the right conditions, the chosen B. cereus strain produces the emetic toxin cereulide.
Due to the stability of the toxin, cereulide does not lose its biological toxic activity in the
human stomach upon ingestion of contaminated food Delbrassinne et al. (2012).
Jaaskelainen et al. (2003) investigated the production of cereulide by B. cereus in various
bakery products, including a meat pastry with a pH of approximately 6 and a water activ-
ity of 0.97-0.99. The products were inoculated with suspensions of B. cereus F4810/72 at
concentrations of 108, 106, and 0 CFU/g. Focusing on the meat ﬁlling of the meat pastries
due to an assumed high protein content that potentially is comparable to that of mealworm
larvae, Jaaskelainen et al. (2003) found that an increase of 6.7 µg/g and 5.5 µg/g cereulide
occurred in samples inoculated with 108 CFU/g and 106 CFU/g respectively when stored at
21-23°C for 4 days. Meanwhile, Jaaskelainen et al. (2003) also found that samples stored at
4-8°C did not experience any signiﬁcant increases in cereulide contents (≤0.05 µg/g).
While it is uncommon for cereulide to cause deaths, the possibility cannot be ignored.
In October 2008, a 20-year-old man died after ingestion of pasta which had been prepared
5 days prior to his death and left in the kitchen at room temperature. Legal examinations
determined the time of death to be approximately 10 hours after ingestion. Emetic strains
of B. cereus were isolated, and levels of 14.8 µg/g of cereulide were found in the spaghetti
meal [Naranjo et al. (2011)]. Similarly, a 17-year-old boy died after eating spaghetti and
pesto which had been prepared four days prior.
Within two days, the boy died due to
fulminant liver failure. During autopsy, high concentrations of cereulide were found in both
the residue from the pan used to reheat the spaghetti, and in the boy’s liver and bile [Mahler
et al. (1997)]. As a ﬁnal example, the death of a 7-year-old girl in 2003 was also caused by
ingestion of pasta. The pasta had been stored at 14°C for three days, allowing B. cereus to
grow to a count of more than 108 CFU/g. It was concluded that her death was caused by
liver failure as a result of emetic syndrome [Dierick et al. (2005)].
3.3.1
Detection of cereulide
The presence of cereulide in food products can potentially have fatal consequences when
ingested, and it is therefore of interest to be able to detect the toxin in food products.

Food safety
The safety of consumers when consuming food products is of importance. It is up to the
manufacturer to ensure food safety by following set legislation and guidelines, including
evaluation of the risks that chemical and microbial hazards may pose, and upkeep of quality
control during production. The World Health Organisation describe that unsafe food which
contains harmful bacteria, chemical substances, viruses or parasites cause more than 200
diﬀerent diseases, ranging from diarrhoea to cancers. Speciﬁcally foodborne or waterborne
diarrhoeal diseases cause an estimated 2 million deaths annually [WHO (2015)].
Insects are considered a novel food as they have not been consumed to a signiﬁcant degree
in the EU before May 15th 1997, and they are therefore covered by the same regulations as
those set for these foods [Ministry of Environment and Food of Denmark (2018)]. Before an
insect can be approved as a novel food, any risks must be assessed and approved before the
product can be marketed in the EU. It is the marketing company’s responsibility to ensure
that an insect novel food is not unsafe or unﬁt for human consumption. A risk analysis on the
products must be performed in which potentially harmful substances in the food are taken
into account [Ministry of Environment and Food of Denmark (2018)].
In the commission regulation (EC) No 1441/2007 amending Regulation (EC) No 2073/2005
on microbiological criteria for foodstuﬀs, the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) con-
clude that the major control measures when dealing with B. cereus in foodstuﬀs are to control
temperature during production and to establish a general system based on hazard analysis
12
==PAGE:20==

DTU, June 2019
and critical control point (HACCP) principles. Due to the ability to produce pathogenic
spores which survive heat treatments, EFSA specify that for dehydrated or dried foods (for
example dried infant formulae), the numbers of B. cereus spores must be as low as possible
during processing, and a process hygiene criteria should be laid down in addition to good
work practices which are designed to help reduce delay between preparation and consump-
tion. EFSA describe that when testing concentrations of B. cereus in dried products intended
for infants below six months of age, the tests are satisfactory if all observed concentrations
are ≤50 CFU/g and unsatisfactory if one or more values observed are ≥500 CFU/g [EFSA
(2007)].
Diﬀerent quality assurance systems can be applied in the production line to ensure food
safety. As an example, the Ministry of Environment and Food of Denmark suggest that
cooling of a product from 65°C to 10°C should be done within 3 hours when dealing with B.
cereus [Ministry of Environment and Food of Denmark (2011)]. To ensure this occurs during
processing of for example a mealworm product, a HACCP can be applied. The HACCP
consists of seven principles; hazard analysis, identiﬁcation of critical control points (CCPS),
deﬁnition of critical limits for each CCP, establishment of monitoring procedures for each
CCP, establishment of corrective actions when deviation of the limits occur, veriﬁcation of
the procedures established, and record keeping and documenting procedures of the system
[Wallace and Mortimore (2016)]. When applying HACCP to a cooling process, the process
can more easily be controlled and regulated to follow the suggested measures by Ministry of
Environment and Food of Denmark (2011).

Fate of ingested B. cereus spores the mealworm larvae gut
The fate of spores ingested by mealworm larvae is generally unknown. However, studies on
human-ﬂora-associated rats have found that B. cereus spores survived the gastric barrier well,
and in some cases was detected in the gastrointestinal tract up to two weeks after ingestion
[Wilcks et al. (2006)]. While these results are not from experiments conducted on mealworm
larvae samples, they are still relevant in showing that some bacterial spores may survive for
an extended period of time in the gastrointestinal tract of mealworm larvae, indicating that
good food safety is very important when producing food products of mealworm larvae.

To enumerate not only the spore concentration after production but also bacterial and spore

5.4
Spore production on solid nutrient agar with sporulation salts
The B. cereus spores needed for several experiments were produced by following the method
as described by Leguerinel and Mafart (2001).
To make the B. cereus sporulate, nutrient agar plates with sporulation salts were prepared
by mixing nutrient agar powder (CM0003, Oxoid, United Kingdom) with demineralised water
in accordance to the instructions. The sporulation salts, MnSO4 (Sigma Aldrich, USA) and
CaCl2 (Alfa Aesar, USA), were added to the nutrient agar in the concentrations of 40 mg/L
and 100 mg/L respectively before the solution was autoclaved at 121°C for 15 minutes. After
autoclaving, the agar was left to cool brieﬂy before aliquots of approximately 20 mL of the
sporulation agar were poured into sterile petri dishes. The plates were left to solidify and
stored in plastic bags at approximately 4°C.
With the sporulation plates prepared, the cereulide producing B. cereus strain 21792 was
plated on blood agar (Oxoid, United Kingdom) and incubated at 37°C overnight. A colony
from the blood agar plate was then pre-cultivated in 100 mL brain heart infusion broth (BHI,
Oxoid, United Kingdom) at 37°C for approximately 24 hours. The overnight culture of B.
cereus strain 21792 was inoculated onto three sporulation agar plates by spreading 1 mL onto
the plates (333 µL on each) with a drigalski spatula, and the plates were then incubated for
6 days at 37°C.
To harvest the spores, 1 mL of sterile 0.9% saline (Fresenius Kabi, Germany) was added to
each of the three plates. Using a drigalski spatula, the spores were scraped oﬀand the spores
from all three plates were transferred with an autopipette to one Schott bottle containing
100 mL 0.9% saline. After brief mixing by shaking, the suspension was divided into four 50
mL centrifuge tubes (25 mL in each) and centrifuged at 10,000 × g (Eppendorf Centrifuge
5810 R, Germany) for 15 minutes at room temperature (approximately 22°C) followed by a
removal of the supernatant. Afterwards, 10 mL sterile 0.9% saline was added to each tube
to wash and resuspend the B. cereus spores pellet before the centrifugation was repeated.
This washing process was repeated three times in total. Finally, the pellet was resuspended
in 5 mL sterile 0.9% saline. The suspensions from the four centrifuge tubes were pooled in a
single centrifuge tube and stored at approximately 4°C until further use.
The concentrations of spores produced in the suspension was determined several times,

5.8
Statistical Analysis
A statistical analysis was conducted on the observed concentrations of spores in both meal-
worm larvae and dry substrate during the exposure study. The analysis was conducted by
using the plugin data analysis, ANOVA two-factor with replication, in Microsoft Excel 2017.
The ANOVA was run on the triplicate log CFU/g spore concentrations from each box and
each day. To conduct the ANOVA, the signiﬁcance level was set to 5% (α = 0.05) and the
rows per sample was set to 3 (due to the triplicate sampling). The following null hypotheses
were set up with the knowledge that time and spike concentrations were the two variables:
• H1: the average spore concentrations observed in the mealworm larve or substrate
samples are the same in all boxes.
• H2: the average spore concentrations observed over time are the same.
• H3: no interaction between the spike concentration and time is observed.

Once the ANOVA was run, the null hypotheses were either accepted or rejected based on
examination of the F, Fcrit and P (the probability) values. A null hypothesis was rejected if
the following was observed: F > Fcrit and P < 0.05. Likewise, a null hypothesis was accepted
if the following was observed: F < Fcrit and P > 0.05.

5.9.1
Experimental design
Using mealworm larvae from the exposure study, this experiment was designed to investigate
if applying a double heat treatment would lower or remove the concentration of spores found
in the mealworm larvae which had been exposed to 106 CFU/g B. cereus spores for 14
days (see Section 5.7). The design of the experiment was based on the knowledge that the
germination and outgrowth of spores to vegetative cells can be activated by heat treatments
(see Section 3).
The experimental setup consisted of two separate experiment, 1 and 2, which are described
in the following section.
5.9.2
Method of experiment 1
For experiment 1, samples of 1 g mealworm larve were taken from the box with mealworm
larvae previously exposed to 106 CFU/g spores during the exposure study (Section 5.7. To
euthanise the mealworm larvae and apply the ﬁrst heat treatment, the samples were heat
treated for 2 minutes at 100°C in autoclaved Milli-Q water (from on site facilities). For
optimised heat transfer to the mealworm larvae samples, the heat treatment was done by
placing the mealworm larvae in a small ﬁlter which was submerged into the 100°C Milli-Q
water. This allowed for direct mealworm larvae to water contact, resulting in eﬃcient heat
transfer. After ended heat treatment, the ﬁlter was lifted out of the water bath, and the
samples were transferred to 10 mL plastic culture tubes. Visualisation of the method can be
seen in Figure 6.

7
Discussion
7.1
Production of B. cereus spores
During the production of spores, the visual diﬀerence in spore growth on the individual
plates was assumed to correspond to a diﬀerence in spore concentration. However, as the
results in Table 3 of the enumeration of the diﬀerent spore solutions indicate, it is clear that
a visual inspection of the plates is not eﬃcient in quantifying the growth and determining
variations in growth. The two plates that showed minimal growth, S1 and S5, ended up
varying signiﬁcantly. While the concentration of spores in the S1 solution was found to be
2.0 log CFU/mL lower than the solutions of S2, S3 and S4, the concentration in S5 was found
to be less than 1.0 log CFU/mL lower. This indicates that the plate which was discarded
was likely to have contained spores despite showing very little signs of growth.
The pooling of the three of ﬁve spore suspensions (S2, S3 and S5) resulted in an initial
concentration of 7.4 log CFU/mL. A month later, the suspension was enumerated to contain
7.6 log CFU/mL, signifying growth in the suspension. However, in this case growth would
indicate the occurrence of spore germination and outgrowth followed by vegetative growth
until sporulation was triggered again. This is highly unlikely considering the fact that the
spore suspension was stored at approximately 5°C, and the increase of concentration (+ 0.2
log CFU/mL), is most likely caused by uncertainty when enumerating the suspension. This
could be have been examined by taking three diﬀerent samples from the spore suspension
and determined the concentration and standard deviation within the samples.
7.2
Enumeration of background ﬂorae in dry and wet substrates
While using sterile dry substrate for the conducted experiments was an option from the
beginning, the reality is that non sterile dry substrate will most likely be used in an actual
full scale production of mealworm larvae to lower production costs. Therefore, it was of
relevance to investigate what concentration of spores could be found in the dry substrate
despite not being used for the exposure study.
Before the enumeration, it was assumed that minimal concentrations of spores would be

found in the diﬀerent types of substrate. This is why the method of pour plating was used
to decrease the detection limit.
7.2.1
Dry substrate
As described previously, it was not possible to enumerate the concentration of spores with the
pour plating method. With detection limits of 1
2 CFU/g and 5 CFU/g, the plates were too
overgrown to be countable, and the colonies looked diﬀerent from those seen when growing
B. cereus, indicating that spores other than B. cereus are found in the dry substrate. The
concentration could most likely have been determined by diluting the sample to a lower
concentration and using the more common method for enumeration with a detection limit of
2.0 log CFU/g.
The exact composition of the dry substrate used for experiments is unknown. However, it
is known that it consists mainly of wheat ﬂour, and a study by van Broekhoven et al. (2015)
shows that if the substrate contains high concentrations of starch, the mealworms’ growth
rate is increased. In the study by van Broekhoven et al. (2015), it was also determined that
substrate containing maize, bread remains, beer yeast and peels of steamed potatoes was the
most eﬃcient. While this composition seemingly varies greatly from that of the substrate
used in the experiments, the high starch content (28% [van Broekhoven et al. (2015)] is
similar to that of wheat ﬂour (55% of total carbohydrate content DTU Foods (2019)).
It is known that spores are often present in wheat ﬂour. Berghofer et al. (2003) found
up to 3.0 log CFU/g mesophilic aerobic spores in ﬂour and up to 2.0 log CFU/g aerobic
thermophiles. Therefore, a similar composition could be found in the non sterile dry substrate
for the mealworm larvae due to the high content of wheat ﬂour.

2 CFU/g.

2 CFU/g on plates poured with sterile
dry substrate (Figure 9) to the plates of non sterile dry substrate with the corresponding
detection limit, it is clear that the sterilisation process of applying irradiation was very

7.2.2
Wet substrate
The concentration of spores in brewers’ spent grain was enumerated to just below 3.0 log
CFU/g. This concentration was excepted due to the composition of the brewers’ spent grain,
meaning the favourable conditions for microbial growth (high water and sugar content [Lynch
et al. (2016)]). Robertson et al. (2010) found concentrations of aerobic mesophilic bacteria
ranging from 2.0 log CFU/g to 6.0 log, and, similarly, concentrations of aerobic thermophilic
bacteria ranging from 2.0 log CFU/g to 7.0 log CFU/g in brewers’ spent grain from 10
commercial breweries through the EU. The concentration diﬀers signiﬁcantly between the
samples, most likely due to diﬀerent compositions based on what brewing process the spent
grain was from. However, it must be noted that all samples still contained at least 2.0 log
CFU/g of both mesophilic and thermophilic aerobic bacteria. Applying a sterilisation process
like irradiation could potentially make the brewers’ spent grain usable for the exposure study.
The concentration of spores on the peel of organic carrots was found to 1.6 ± 0.1 log CFU/g,
and the growth was determined to look diﬀerent from previously observed B. cereus growth,
indicating that diﬀerent bacteria were found on the carrots.
However, during the heat treatment at 80°C for 10 minutes conducted to kill vegetative
cells, only parts of the stomacher bag containing the sample was submerged in the water
bath. When the sample was transferred from the stomacher bag to a centrifuge tube, it came
in contact with parts of the stomacher bag which had not been heat treated. This means that
the enumerated concentration may not reﬂect just the spore count as there was a potential
for recontamination of vegetative cells.
Comparing the determined spore count of the carrots with data from Aycicek et al. (2006),
it appears likely that the concentration found is mainly that of spores. The study by Aycicek
et al. (2006) found a total aerobic count of 3.8–6.5 log CFU/g in 30 carrot samples which

7.3
Exposure of B. cereus spores to mealworm larvae for 14 days
7.3.1
Setup of the exposure study
The exposure study was set up with ﬁve boxes (see Table 1). Box 3, 4 and 5 were the main
boxes set up to follow the concentration of spores in mealworm larvae fed substrate spiked
with diﬀerent spore concentrations. Box 1 and 2 were set up as control boxes. Box 1, the
positive control, was spiked with the same level of spores as box 4. This was done to observe
if changes such as a natural increase or decrease of the spore concentration occurred without
the presence of mealworm larvae at the set conditions described in Section 5.2.1. If any
signiﬁcant changes occurred, these could be accounted for when evaluating the data from the
other boxes. The negative control, box 2, was set up to compare the concentration of spores in
mealworm larvae fed with sterile dry substrate not spiked with B. cereus spores, and to help
get a better understanding of the data achieved from the other boxes, as the concentration
of naturally occurring spores in the mealworm larvae could be followed throughout the 14
days period. However, this comparison was not utilised due to the unexpected spore count
found in the sterile substrate in the negative control boxes.
As described in Section 2, the usual source of water for the mealworm larvae during produc-
tion is brewers’ spent grain. While it was favourable to mimic the conditions of a bigger scale
production during the exposure study, it was decided that carrots should be used instead
of brewers’ spent grain as the main water source similarly to what is given to mealworm
beetles during production. This was based on the results from both the determination of
spore concentration in the brewers’ spent grain, and the results from applying a double heat
treatment in hopes of lowering the spore count or completely removing the spores. As the
double heat treatment was ineﬃcient, and a concentration of at least 3.0 log CFU/g was
found in the brewers’ spent grain, it was decided that carrots would be a better choice as the
concentration of spores on carrots was assumed to be signiﬁcantly lower.

7.3.2
First replication
During the ﬁrst replication, the concentrations of spores in the dry substrate vary from what
was expected when spiking the substrate. In box 1 and 5, spiked with 4.3 log CFU/g and
6.1 CFU/g respectively, the concentration was observed to be 0.5-1.0 log CFU/g lower. This
diﬀerence may be a result of uncertainty when enumerating the concentration of spores in
the spore solution used to spike the substrate. Another possibility is that the recovery of
spores in the dry substrate is not 100%. During a trial of dry substrate spiking, a sample
was prepared using the same method as described in Section 5.7. After spiking the sample
and enumerating the spores within the sample, a recovery of approximately 40% was found
(results not shown). Assuming that similar recovery is achieved during the exposure study,
the concentration of spores in the dry substrate from box 5 would be 40% of 6.1 log CFU/g,
meaning approximately 5.7 log CFU/g (calculated by converting values to CFU/g). This
value is close to the concentrations of spores found in the samples and possibly explains
the slightly lower concentrations during the exposure study. Similarly, the concentration of
spores on the carrots used as the wet substrate was found to approximately 1.5 log CFU/g
which is not nearly enough reach a contamination of 4.0 log CFU/g. This means that the
unexpectedly high spore count must have been from either a contamination or the addition
of mealworm larvae. While a contamination is an obvious possibility due to the ﬁve boxes
being handled within the same lab, the fact that previous studies such as the ones by Osimani
et al. (2018) and Klunder et al. (2012) observed spore concentrations of < 1.0 log CFU/g
to 4.0 log CFU/g in mealworm larvae suggests that the addition of mealworm larvae could
have increased the concentration of spores in the dry substrate. While the spores found in
mealworm larvae from the on site production were never enumerated, plating of a mealworm
larvae solution (20 g mealworm larvae in 80 mL 0.9% saline) after applying 10 minutes of heat
treatment at 80°C showed that spores were deﬁnitely present within the on site production’s
mealworm larvae (results not shown).
Out of the ﬁve boxes, box 4 spiked with a medium level of B. cereus spores (4.0 log CFU/g)
is the only box in which concentrations of the dry substrate were found to be similar to what
was expected based on the spike level, and this may be a result of the spore contamination
of the mealworm larvae and the 40% recovery of spores in the spiked substrate.

spores in the mealworm larvae seem to reﬂect the expected concentrations of spores instead
of the 4.0 log CFU/g found in all three boxes. The concentrations found in the mealworm

Second replication
The second replication of the exposure study which was conducted to verify the results of the

7.3.4
Comparison of replications
Overall, the two conducted replications of the exposure study returned similar results and
tendencies of how diﬀerent spike levels of B. cereus spores aﬀected the concentration of spores
found in the mealworm larvae. While the second replication was set up to mimic the ﬁrst,
a few diﬀerences in how the study was conducted must be listed as they could have had
an impact on the results. The main diﬀerence to potentially have aﬀected data is that the
detection limit for samples from the negative control, low spike concentration and medium
spike concentration boxes was kept constant at 2.0 log CFU/g during replication 1. For
replication 2, the detection limit was lowered to 1.0 log CFU/g based on the concentrations
observed. This was done to optimise the data by having less samples show zero growth due

7.3.5
Accuracy of results
As mentioned previously, the concentration of spores within the mealworm larvae may have
been the cause of the higher than expected spore concentration found in box 2 and 3 during
both replications of the exposure study. If this is the case, the same contamination will
obviously also have aﬀected the results from box 4 and 5 as these also contained mealworm
larvae. However, in box 4, the spike concentration was similar to what is found in mealworm
larvae not exposed to B. cereus spores, and the total concentration may not have been
aﬀected enough to be visible in the data due to the log CFU/g unit. Similarly, if a recovery
of just 40% is taken into consideration, the concentration when spiking the substrate with
4.3 log CFU/g would result in 3.7 log CFU/g. If the mealworm larvae contaminate with
a similar concentration, this would increase the overall level in the medium spiked box to
what was expected. In box 5 during both replications, the spike level was signiﬁcantly higher
than concentrations of spores found in mealworm larvae even when taking the recovery into
consideration, and the log CFU/g unit makes it so that an increase caused by this is hardly
visible.
The concentration of spores found in the mealworm larvae samples were assumed to quantify
the number of spores found in the gut of the mealworm larvae. However, the mealworm larvae

were mixed in with and sometimes completely covered by dry substrate containing spores,
and chances are that spores may have adhered to the surface of the mealworm larvae. In a
study by Faille et al. (2002), the adherence capability of B. cereus spores was investigated

study showed that B. cereus has great adhering capability with concentrations of up to 7

A possibility to overcome the adherence during experimentation could be to disinfect the
surface of the mealworms before the samples are homogenised with 0.9% saline. However, a
method that is eﬃcient in removing spores from the surface while also not aﬀecting the spores
within the mealworm larvae must be found in order to investigate this. Likewise, if it is found
that a majority of the spores found during the exposure study were actually on the surface
of the mealworm larvae, a method for disinfecting samples that is eﬃcient and also suitable
for food production must be composed. During the experiments, a 1% Virkon S solution
was used to disinfect laboratory equipment. This disinfectant was found to be eﬃcient in
removing both vegetative cells and spores (results not shown). However, it is not suitable
for disinfecting food products and an alternative must therefore be found if disinfection to
remove spores is brought into the production of a mealworm larvae food product.

7.4.1
Double heat treatment on bacterial spores in brewers’ spent grain
The potential of a double heat treatment was initially tested on brewers’ spent grain (as
described in Section 5.6) to investigate if a double heat treatment would be eﬃcient in
lowering the spore count of the background ﬂora to make brewers’ spent grain suitable as a
water source during the exposure study.
Prior to the application of a double heat treatment, the spore count in the brewers’ spent
grain had been determined to 970 CFU/g. After applying the ﬁrst heat treatment, it was
expected to ﬁnd a similar concentration of spores. However, as can be seen on Figure 11
in Section 6.3, the plates with a detection limit of 5 CFU/g were diﬃcult to count and a
concentration was not determined. The diﬀerence in the initial spore count was most likely a
result of the brewers’ spent grain samples coming from diﬀerent portions. As previously dis-
cussed, the background ﬂorae between batches of brewers’ spent grain can vary signiﬁcantly.
While it is unknown if the samples were from diﬀerent batches due to each batch of brewers’
spent grain being divided into several smaller portions, it is certain that samples were taken
from diﬀerent portions. A portion of brewers’ spent grain is used to feed on site production
almost daily, and a single portion lasts up to a few days. However, the time interval between
the two determinations was two weeks.
After applying the second heat treatment, it was only possible to estimate if it had been
successful in lowering the background ﬂora of spores by comparing the growth on the plates
(Figure 12 and Figure 13) with the ones plated after the ﬁrst heat treatment application
(Figure 11). When comparing the two sets of plates poured with detection limits of 5 CFU/g,
a diﬀerence can deﬁnitely be observed before and after the second heat treatment. The main
diﬀerence is the number of colonies on the plates as the count looks signiﬁcantly higher on
the plates prior to the second heat treatment. The diﬀerence could potentially be considered
to simply be a result of variation within the sample and ineﬃcient mixing. However, the
degree of the diﬀerence seems too signiﬁcant for this to be the case.
The conditions within the brewers’ spent grain solution were favourable for germination
and outgrowth to occur. As described in Section 3, activation by heat treatment, and the
presence of for example sugars as stimuli, can activate the dormant spores and trigger the
germination process. Brewers’ spent grain contains appreciable quantities of the monosac-

7.4.2
Double heat treatment on B. cereus spores in mealworm larvae
When investigating the eﬀect of double heat treatment on the total aerobic and spore count
of mealworm larvae samples previously fed dry substrate spiked with 6.0 log CFU/g B. cereus
spores, it was observed that the concentration of both increased signiﬁcantly after incubation
for 7 days at 15°C, 20°C and 25°C (approximately 4.5 log CFU/g and 3.5 log CFU/g increase
in total aerobic count and spore count respectively). However, while it was expected to see
diﬀerent degrees of growth due to the diﬀerence in temperature, samples from the mentioned
temperatures all reached similar levels as shown in Table 9. Meanwhile, both the total aerobic
count and spore count remained the same in the samples stored at 5°C. This may be due to
the fact that the used B. cereus strain, 21792, has been found to have positive growth in the
temperature range 15°C to 45°C [Guinebretière et al. (2008)].
The results of following the total aerobic count and spore count in mealworm larvae samples at
two temperatures (shown in Table 10 and Figure 10) unexpectedly did not show a conjunction
between increase in total aerobic count and decrease in spore count. While the experiment
was conducted without triplicate sampling due to a lack of PCA plates, it is assumed that the
results are similarly precise to those of the ﬁrst experiment in which the triplicate sampling
resulted in standard deviations of no more than ± 0.4 log CFU/g.
An increase in total aerobic count is observed to begin immediately (Figure 19), much

quicker than predicted theoretically. Approximately 24 hours after a concentration of 8.0-9.0
log CFU/g is reached, the concentration of spores starts to increase with 3.0 log CFU/g
total at both temperatures. It is unknown whether the sudden increase from 3.0 log CFU/g
to 5.0 log CFU/g followed by a decrease to approximately 2.0 log CFU/g in the mealworm

Applying a second heat treatment to mealworm larvae samples is seemingly ineﬃcient in
lowering the total spore count as no obvious decrease was observed even though an increase
in vegetative cells count was observed. While the mealworm larvae are no longer alive during
the experiment, the conditions in the gut in which the spores are likely to be found may
aﬀect the lack of germination. Spinosa et al. (2000) describes that it is diﬃcult to envisage
that germination and outgrowth of spores will occur in mammalian intestines due to the
anaerobic environment, and this may also be the case for the mealworm larvae samples if
the gut of these provides the spores with a similar environment. Due to B. cereus being a
facultatively anaerobic bacterium [Bottone (2010)], it may be plausible that a growth in total
aerobic count is observed due to vegetative growth of surviving cells until a concentration
of which spore production begins once more. A study by Abbas et al. (2014) shows that
B. cereus does sporulate under anaerobic conditions, albeit less eﬃciently than at aerobic
conditions.
In anaerobic conditions, the strain tested by Abbas et al. (2014) showed an
average sporulation ability of 4.0 log CFU/mL in an uncontrolled batch culture and 6.0 log
CFU/mL in a controlled batch culture. A lack of the right germinants needed to trigger
germination may also have aﬀected the results.
While the investigation of eﬀect of double heat treatment on B. cereus spores over time
in mealworm larvae did not provide the expected results, it did provide an insight into what
happens with vegetative cells and spores within mealworm larvae, and how 5°C diﬀerence
in storage temperature aﬀects the growth time of vegetative cells. The results also teach
an important lesson in how mealworm larvae products with similar pH and water activity
must be stored after applying an initial heat treatment during production. At both 15°C
and 20°C, the concentrations of spores increased with approximately 3.0 log CFU/g within
a few days, and this may have fatal results for the consumer as described in Section 3.3.

8
Conclusion
In this thesis, the aim was to develop an experimental set-up to address the fate of B. cereus
spores in mealworm larvae as well as investigating means of reducing the level of spores in
relation to food safety of a mealworm larvae food product for consumption.
Prior to setting up the exposure study, it was found that sterile dry substrate would
be preferable as the main feed during the experiment due to the knowledge that only < 1
2
CFU/g bacterial spores were present compared to a signiﬁcantly higher level in non sterile dry
substrate. Likewise, it was determined that brewers’ spent grain, which is usually the choice
for wet substrate in the on site production, could not be used eﬃciently when exposing the
mealworm larvae to bacterial spores as this resource naturally contains a high concentration
of spores.
For the exposure study set up to investigate the fate of B. cereus spores within mealworm
larvae samples, it was ﬁrst and foremost concluded that despite a few diﬀerences in the
ﬁnal experimental designs, the two replications of the study showed similar tendencies of
results. The exposure study was concluded to generally show an interaction between the
concentration of spores of which the dry substrate was spiked with (either 0 CFU/g, 2.0, 4.0
or 6.0 log CFU/g) and the concentration of spores found in the mealworm larvae samples.
Likewise, the mealworm larvae samples’ spore count showed a slight dependency of time,
mainly being a tendency to have the highest spore concentration in the beginning of the
experiment which slowly decreases over time. While it is still unclear exactly which factors
aﬀected the spore concentration in the mealworm larvae samples, it was concluded that the
set-up has great potential in determining the fate of B. cereus spores once several factors
have been investigated and adjusted.
The set-up to investigate means of reducing the concentration of spores was developed
based on the knowledge of bacterial spores’ ability to germinate and outgrow into vegetative
cells. The hypothesis was that an initial heat treatment would make the spores outgrow into
vegetative cells which could then be eliminated with a second heat treatment. However, it
was found that the initial heat treatment did not trigger the spores into germination and
outgrowth as no decrease in the spore concentration was observed in conjunction with an
61
==PAGE:69==

DTU, June 2019
increase in total aerobic count at either of the temperatures (15°C and 20°C). However, the
total spore concentration was found to increase once the total aerobic count reached approx-
imately 8.0-9.0 log CFU/g. While the reason for this observed tendency is unknown, it is
concluded that the initial heat treatment potentially did not trigger germination of spores
potentially due to either anaerobic conditions in the mealworm larvae samples (assuming the
spores were accumulated in the gut) or a lack of germinants needed to start the germina-
tion process, and a growth of vegetative cells occurred instead, resulting in increased spore
concentration once these vegetative cells started a sporulation process.
Based on the conclusions drawn as a result of the various experiments, it is clear that
there is a possibility that a high concentration of spores from the pathogenic B. cereus in the
substrate for feed could accumulate in the mealworm larvae and potentially cause risks for
human health, and that a double heat treatment may not be eﬃcient in lowering the spore
concentration.
8.1
Further investigation
Further research needs to be conducted to really understand the fate of B. cereus spores in
mealworm larvae. To get a better understanding of the results of the exposure study, factors
such as the concentration of spores in the mealworm larvae and the eﬀect of spore count in the
sterile dry substrate under similar conditions without the addition of mealworm larvae should
be investigated. Likewise, the method for spiking the substrate could potentially be improved
to increase the recovery of 40%, and a sterile water source such as irradiated brewers’ spent
grain should be utilised to minimise contamination of unwanted bacterial spores which may
aﬀect results of the study.
Further investigations on the spores found in the mealworm larvae may be needed.
Throughout the experiments, the total spore count in the samples was enumerated. How-
ever, this does not give a proper insight into the exact fate of B. cereus spores as it is likely
that other bacterial spores are present in the gut of mealworm. Investigations to determine
which bacterial spores are present in the sample might be relevant to conduct. Similarly,
it is unknown if the spores determined to be in the mealworm larvae samples prior to the
exposure study were accumulated in the mealworm larvae during the on site production in

which they were fed both dry and wet non sterile substrate. To investigate this further, a
small scale production of mealworm larvae could be set up in which only sterile substrates
are used to give an insight into whether the mealworm larvae accumulate spores in the gut
throughout their entire life cycle, and how much if any.
The potential of a double heat treatment as a mean of lowering the spore concentration
should be investigated, possibly by changing the conditions to those preferable for spores
to enter the germination and outgrowth. Diﬀerent types of samples, such as for example
a homogeneous mixture instead of a whole mealworm larvae, could also be investigated in
case homogenising the samples makes a double heat treatment more eﬃcient. Overall, more
testing should be done to fully get an understanding of the growth tendency of vegetative
cells followed by an increase in spore count which was observed at both test temperatures.
Lastly, an investigation of the toxin cereulide which causes the emetic syndrome in hu-
mans could be conducted. While no germination and outgrowth of the B. cereus spores was
observed during the double heat treatment study, cereulide being present in the mealworm
larvae is still a possibility. By potentially using the detection method of analysing cereulide
extracts by liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry, this could be investigated, and it could
potentially lead to relevant knowledge of whether the toxin is produced in mealworm larvae
during production or incubation/storage.

Microorganisms and chemicals
Bacillus cereus strain 21792
The microorganism B. cereus is responsible for foodborne illnesses. This is caused by spores
surviving heat treatments during food processing. When the conditions are favourable, the
spores may convert back to vegetative cells that produce emetic toxins in the food product
which can later be ingested. The emetic toxin can cause nausea and vomiting.

Precautions taken in the lab
General precautions
Lab coats must always be worn when working in the lab, and diﬀerent lab coats should be
used in diﬀerent labs to avoid cross contamination of microorganisms. Gloves must be worn
when handling the chemicals.
Precautions when handling B. cereus
When handling B. cereus, the work area should be disinfected with 70% ethanol. Gloves
can be worn to avoid coming into contact with the microorganism. However, this is not a
requirement. Any skin that comes in contact with B. cereus should be washed and disinfected
with 70% ethanol suitable for skin contact. Hands should also be washed once work in the
lab is done.
When handling dried spores (e.g. mixed with ﬂour), precautions should be taken as there
is a possibility of spores getting into the air.
If solutions of B. cereus is spilled or comes in contact with equipment, any infected areas
should be cleaned followed by application of 70% ethanol. To kill any potential spores, 1%
Virkon S in water should be applied to the area.
When dealing with equipment that has come in contact with B. cereus, either of the
following should be done: if the equipment is disposable (e.g. pipette tips, centrifuge tubes,
gloves), it should be placed in a plastic bag on the lab table. This bag should be sealed with
a knot and disposed of in a yellow plastic bag in the lab. When this yellow plastic bag is full,
it is sealed with a knot and taken out of the lab room to be disposed of in the red plastic
bag for infected disposable waste out in the area designated for waste. Non-disposables (e.g.
glass waste) should be put in an autoclave metal bucket. When this bucket is full, the lid
should be put on and the bucket is taken out of the lab to be placed in the hallway marked

Appendix B: Calculations done for setting up the expo-
sure study
Dimensioning of exposure study
It is known that the exposure study will go on for 14 days with 8 of these days being sampling
days. It is also decided that triplicates for 1 g sampes will be taken from both the mealworm
larvae and substrate on each sampling day. This means that the needed amount of larvae
and substrate from day 0 is 24 g of each in each box.
The boxes used for the experiment are aluminium foil trays with the dimensions 11 × 16
cm. The number of mealworm larvae which are suitable for this size is calculated by using
the larvae to area ratio in an example in which 10,000 larvae placed in a 60 × 40 cm box to
achieve the preferable density.
nlarvae = (11 cm · 16 cm) · 10, 000 larvae
60 cm · 40 cm = 733 larvae
The weight of larvae to reach the approximated total of 733 larvae is calculated based on the
average size of a full grown mealworm larvae (150-200 mg). To assure that the larvae have
enough room in the box, the lowest average weight of 150 mg is used:
mlarvae = 733 larvae · 0.150
g
larvae = 110 g
Spiking of substrate
The calculations of volume of spore suspension to be added was done by deciding on a
wanted concentration of spores in the 110 g substrate (for example 20,400 CFU/g) that
makes it possible to add approximately 1 mL of a dilution of spore suspension. Knowing
the concentration of spores in a 100 spore suspension dilution, the volume of said spore
suspension to be added can be calculated as shown below:
Vspore suspension mL = msubstrate g · Cspores, wanted CFU/g
Cspore suspension CFU/mL
This gives the volume of the spore suspension to be added in the unit mL. The volume is
divided with the right dilution to get the wanted volume in the unit µL.
