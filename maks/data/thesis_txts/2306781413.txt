Abstract
This project aims in identifying which would be the optimal case for a large scale seawater heat pump
facility placed in Copenhagen area. During this study, a comparison between alternative cycle conﬁgurations
and working ﬂuid domain took place, in order to investigate the performance characteristics of each case.
The comparison focuses mainly in open loop seawater heat pump systems that have to provide identical
heating capacity to a district heating network. As part of this study was to investigate also the optimal case
for the future low temperature district heating network, a comparison of the performance characteristics is
also included for this.
Comparison was made between 1-stage cycles, a cascade system of Ammonia and two cases of serial
connection between different heat pump types. Thermodynamic models were implemented in engineering
software EES in order to derive and illustrate the results.
As it was found, heating in steps with a serial connection between two heat pump units is a case that would
obtain the highest coefﬁcient of performance.
Moreover, as it was observed the case of R717 obtained in all comparisons the best performance characteris-
tics and this study also justiﬁes why it can be the best option when looking for seawater heat pump facilities.
Furthermore, according to this study, for the upcoming low temperature district heating network, the best
option would be a serial conﬁguration that would include also the 1-stage cycle of R152a. Slightly better
performance from the serial scheme of Ammonia heat pumps, however still there are some concerns if that
would be a good reason to swap from a totally Ammonia scheme to another one that would implement
R152a instead.
As this study has shown, the seawater heat pumps have to face a big challenge when coming to the design,
not only due to corrosion but also due to other parameters that have to be maintained such as the avoidance
of freezing the application in a cold climate.
However, it is still a new ﬁeld of study and this means that innovations can happen and this type of technology
to become even more competitive compared to other alternatives used right now for district heating network,
regardless the limitations. As it is concluded from this thesis, seawater heat pumps can be considered as
an advantageous technology and has the potential to integrate in a future low temperature district heating
network.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The depletion of global energy resources has been one of the main concerns and subjects of discussion
among the world’s top economic and political delegates. Denmark is the ﬁrst country to present a speciﬁc
and ambitious strategy for achieving independence from fossil fuels by 2050 [2]. This to be achieved, actions
are needed to be taken not only with respect to the production of electricity and heat, but also with the
consumption. Every nation should beneﬁt from the available technology and obtain both the environmental
target and the national energy security.
Energy savings from alternative sources like the seawater, and integration of larger industrial heat pumps
(HP) to district heating networks is a critical matter and a way to go for a country. In the case of Denmark,
this together with the conversion of district heating (DH) networks to low-temperature (LTDH), could
contribute to the aforementioned goal [5]. As seawater heating and cooling systems have been successfully
implemented and tested worldwide like in Netherlands, UK, Hague [10] and will be more common in the
future, in the case of Denmark several companies involved in district heating like Hofor, have shown interest
and have already started building their own projects.
The potential of the seawater district heating and cooling is signiﬁcant and despite technological constraints
and the main barriers met at each country, is considered to be not only possible but also a feasible case [5].
1.2
This Thesis
The objective of this thesis is to highlight the potential of different examined cycle conﬁgurations of
seawater heat pumps, connected to the district heating network.
The examined case that will challenge the potential of seawater heat pump technology, is a reference case
of a large scale project of Hofor company, placed at Sjællandsbroen. The scale of the test facility is a
prototype of 5 MW heat capacity with two serial connected heat pump units and designed as two-stage
ammonia refrigeration systems for optimal COP performance. The DH water network in the design phase
for all the tested cases in this study is chosen to be Tfor = 90°C (forwarding temperature) and the sea water
temperature proﬁles of Copenhagen. However, as part of this study is also to examine and test the facilities
in connection to a future low temperature district heating network (LTDH), there will presented also results
obtained for Tfor = 45°C.
Thermodynamic models of different heat pump systems, categorized by refrigerant and connections between
the components, will be implemented in Engineering Equation Software (EES)[9], in order to compare their
performance characteristics and more speciﬁc the COP obtained in heating mode operation.
The results of this study will be presented in graphs and will highlight the potential of seawater as heat

1.3
Heat pump technlogy
A heat pump is a refrigeration device, which is used to transfer low-grade heat from a low-temperature
source to a sink at a higher temperature. As every refrigeration device, also heat pump can be used both
for space heating and cooling. One of the main advantages of heat pump technology is that considerable
amount of heat can be composed by consuming a small amount of electricity. The theory behind the working
principle of a heat pump, relies on the vapour compression cycle. The reversed Carnot cycle is known as the
simplest example of this case and can be seen in the ﬁgure 1.1 below.
Figure 1.1: Reversed Carnot cycle
The main components of a heat pump generally are, the condenser, evaporator, compressor and the
expansion valve. A refrigerant is used as a working ﬂuid in order to transfer the heat from the source to
the sink. At the ﬁrst point of the process in ﬁgure 1.1, the refrigerant is at a liquid-vapour mixture phase.
Then, the compressor interferes between the ﬁrst and second point to increase the temperature and pressure
of the liquid in a way that it change state to saturated vapour [16]. A heat exchanger placed between the
next two points 2-3, passes the refrigerant in contact with a sink of lower temperature until this condensates
and heat is released. On that point, the refrigerant becomes saturated liquid. Next is the expander, which
leads the working ﬂuid to initial state of lower temperature Tc as a liquid. At the ﬁnal stage, points 4-1 the
refrigerant starts to vaporise again due to the absorption of heat from the hotter source. Regardless that
this is a theoretical cycle and in practice it cannot be achieved, this illustrates clearly the principle of the
heat pump technology that transfers heat from a colder to a hotter body. The working ﬂuid is important to
have sufﬁcient low evaporation temperature to function effectively. In practice, a heat pump cycle is more
common to that one shown in ﬁgure 1.2.

In general there are three types of heat pumps categorized by their heat source and by the way the heat

usually transfer it to an indoor application. WSHPs, as in the case of this thesis, can utilize heat from water

1.6
Regulations
The European commission is keeping introducing regulations in order to guarantee a brighter future
from environmental point of view. To do this so, several actions has to be taken before the deadline the
commission has established and moreover a general strategy including the phase down of speciﬁc refrigerants
with high global warming potential is already the plan.
Recently the European commission introduced on January 2015, the regulation No 517/2014, in which
special requirements are included for the manufactures, technicians and operators of refrigeration and heat
pump equipments that contain ﬂuorinated greenhouse gases such as hydroﬂuorocarbons [7].
According to this regulation, when buying new equipment, the operators should be aware that from 2010
onwards the servicing of certain heat pumps and refrigeration equipment that have as working ﬂuid ﬂuorinated
greenhouse gases will not be allowed any long [7].
Moreover, the regulations include the ’HFC phase down’, which means that in the near future the HFCs
will be much scarcer. These regulations apply particularly for the HFC’s that have a high global warming
potential. The operators have the incentive from the European commission to invest in equipment that offers
the lowest possible environmental impact solutions. In accordance, hydrocarbons, ammonia and also carbon
dioxide are the main working ﬂuids that are proposed to be used in heat pump applications. To achieve the
goal that the commission has established, every European country will introduce taxes and other incentives
to attract or distract to additional investments [7].
Furthermore, regulations have also been introduced for having a certiﬁed maintenance personnel that is
able to install and service the existed or new systems. Regular checks are also introduced and as only the
certiﬁed personnel will be able to make these checks, the European commission has found the way to track
the unlimited exposure of harming working ﬂuids to the environment. Equipment record at servicing has to
be kept for at least 5 years both from the operator and the contractors in some cases [7].
Operators of seawater heat pumps that are running on banned refrigerants, should seek to invest and having a
guidance on low-GWP refrigerants or technologies for the same purpose. The following ﬁgure 1.6, found
from the published regulation No 517/2014, it is illustrated the obligations of EU regarding the phase down

commonly met synthetic working ﬂuids are chloroﬂuorocarbons (CFCs), hydrochloroﬂuorocarbons (HCFCs)

• CFC

Heat pumps utilizing sea water source
As described in introduction part, water-source heat pumps transfer heat to or from a water body and use
it for heating or cooling applications. This reversible operations of heat pump technology, establish the heat
pumps as a competitive technology from efﬁciency point of view and economic aspect. Seawater heat pumps
are not something new, as they utilize the temperature of the sea water at a certain depth which is almost
stable throughout the year, were used in many cases in small and larger industrial applications and recently
they were introduced to the market of local DH networks [15]. Their advantage rises from the fact that they
can operate efﬁciency as long as their are not big variations at the sea water source [4]. However, there are
many concerns regarding the high cost of such applications (off shore application), the environmental impact

way the ground source heat pumps do.

2.2.1
Open-loop sea-water HP systems
From the terminology, an open-loop system, is a system that extracts water directly from the source
and after extracting the heat it is returned back to the source at a lower temperature as in ﬁgure 2.3. These
type of systems usually require a submersible pump when the depth of the sea bottom is greater than 5 m.
On the other hand, for lower depths, a ground-mounted suction pump could provide appropriate head and
would be easier in installation and maintenance [16]. Further analysis of the pump usually selected for these
applications will be in following chapter. However, the challenge of engineers in such applications is how to
overtake the corrosion in pipes, in the heat exchanger as such the potential for debris and biofouling. The
installation of special ﬁlters on the intake pipes that would prevent these facts should be considered as such
installation of prophylactic even titanium heat exchangers that would be a safer choice and would never foul
the facility [16].
Figure 2.3: Open loop Sea water HP facility connected to DH network
When designing an open loop sea-water system, it is high importance to site the inlet and outlet pipes in
an appropriate distance one to another in order to prevent a failure described as thermal ’short-circuiting’.
By ’short-circuiting’ we describe the decrease in COP of the heat pump when the water from the outtake
pipe, mixes with the water coming from the intake pipe.
Another parameter that should be considered on such systems, is when to operate, as the risk of freezing
in open-loop system is much higher than the tradition systems (closed loop) [4]. As long as there is direct
contact with the system, and the heat is transferred with higher rate to the evaporator, this means that there
should be established ’safe’ operating conditions for the system. To prevent the freezing in open-loop
systems, the sea-water should be pumped into the system only when reaches a speciﬁc temperature that
would prevent the failure in a sudden sea-water ﬂuctuation. The engineers should evaluate the application,
according to the place as such the seasonal temperature proﬁle and other characteristics of the sea (salinity)
and set the optimal operating conditions over the freezing point.

2.2.4
Seawater issues
Seawater facilities are not like every off-shore application. Several things have to be considered in every
aspect as the seawater as a source remains a great challenge for the engineering. According to this and
especially for the seawater HP applications, there is need for stable water temperatures (less temperature
variation of the source) and also considerable good water quality [12]. The water temperature should be
guaranteed at the bottom of the sea or at the surface, depending on the application, and this can only be done
by studying the source geographically, biologically and thermodynamically. The water should be as stable as
it could be for such applications, even considering extreme climate conditions and duration. Seawater has
the advantage of reduced temperature variations seasonal and this is what makes the exceptional. Regardless
the fact that the HP plant can be out of operation for a period of time that even in some occasions could be
more that 1-2 months, in ﬁnancial terms this could lead to a non worth to invest application [12].
Another challenge of the seawater applications is the availability of the source. This means that the source
should be able to supply the amount of heat and have enough water.
Furthermore, the seawater should meet speciﬁc quality requirements regarding the hardness, the chloride to
avoid the failure of the system and the corrosions in piping equipment. In the case where the hardness is
signiﬁcantly high, there is a risk for the heat exchangers to foul, as the heat transfer coefﬁcient is reduced
[12].
Another issue that has to be faced is the water eutrophication. Water eutrophication commonly referred as
’algae’ issue in some cases can cover with a layer the heat exchangers and as a consequence reduce the heat
transfer coefﬁcient [12]. The result is called biofouling and can be seen in ﬁgure 2.8.

One-stage cycles are broadly used both in refrigeration and heat pump technology. Most of the models

Regarding the model developed for the CVHP, initial assumption is that the seawater heat pump is
in steady state, and as such there is no heat loss from both compressor and the expansion valve. In the
evaporator, the working ﬂuid follows both the evaporation and the superheating in order to gain heat. Dur-
ing the evaporation process there are no pressure losses, and the evaporation temperature (Te) is constant.
Superheating on this stage, requires a constant-pressure for the working ﬂuid which is in vapor condition [17].

3.2.3
Serial connected systems
When constructing large seawater heat pump facilities, it is very common to see serial connected heat
pumps or cascade systems [14]. As long as most of these projects are referred as customs, meaning that each
case is different from another, the manufactures of the components have a limited variety in large capacity
options, usually over 5 MW. So the challenge for the engineer that is responsible for the design of the heat
pump plant is to decide the number of seawater heat pumps connected and moreover, how they are connected
in order to reach the high capacity that requires the facility [3]. Also, according to Chang [6], after some
simulations and experiments that he made regarding a serial connected system consisting of 2 heat pump
instead of one of the same size, he concluded that there is an increase in COP around 15 % when smaller
units are connected in order to achieve the same capacity. In the same conclusion also came out another
study from Fouad [3] regarding connection of more heat pumps and splitting the total capacity. Accordingly,
Fouad concluded that to obtain a higher COP could be achieved by connecting more heat pumps in series, if
heat pumps are of the same type of compressor (screw compressor) and for different type of heat pumps
or compression process, a parallel connection of the evaporators between smaller heat pump unit is more
desirable.
A serial connected system, is a system that is usually consisted of two or more heat pumps connected in
series at both condenser and/or evaporator side. The heat demand is divided-split to the smaller heat pump
units respectively. By doing this, the water of the district heating is heated in steps as in Hofor’s case. At
the design phase, an intermediate temperature (Tint) is set as described in a previous section, section 3.2,
and then when this temperature is reached, the next thermodynamic cycle heat the water to the desired
temperature. In theory, the integration of inﬁnite thermodynamic cycles could be achieved by this way.
The mass ﬂow of the source (seawater) when the heat pumps connected in series, is the same for all the
evaporators [4].
At the models developed on EES during this thesis, two cases of serial connected systems were examined.
One case of only Ammonia-R717 working ﬂuid consisting of two 2,5 MW capacity each seawater heat
pump, with a combination of two-stage R717 cascade and one-stage R717 cycle after reaching the Tint. The
total heat capacity is the same as in single-stage seawater heat pump systems, 5 MW .The alternative to this
case was another serial connected system consisting of cascade R717 cycle and one-stage R152a cycle after
reaching the Tint. This system is illustrated in ﬁgure 3.8 is the Ammonia case and is the principle of the
serial connected system. The log p-h diagram for this system can be seen in ﬁgure 3.7. The green doted lines
are illustrating the second circuit of the conﬁguration (3rd compression process), whereas the red dotted
lines the ﬁrst system, the cascade Ammonia cycle.
For both the serial connected and cascade systems, the decision regarding the working ﬂuids that were
ﬁnally implemented in EES thermodynamic models, was according to what was found as the best cases
looking at the COP of the one-stage cycles and also as the working ﬂuids used most in relevant applications.
Another combination of a serial connected system that is close to this, could also be a parallel connection of
the evaporators as an option by each heat pump having half of the mass ﬂow of the source and the evaporator
of each heat pump not in connection with each other, however that is not a case that would obtain a higher
COP so it is not included in the comparison made in next chapters.

Introduction to components
Component selection when designing a seawater heat pump system of this scale is highly importance.
The selected technology has to proof:
• Reliability, due to corrosions, biofouling, complexity as an offshore system.
• Performance, to provide the required heating capacity.
• Low maintenance cost

technology used.

facility, there will be also a discussion regarding the pump and pump calculations.

4.7.5
Avoiding freezing of system
The freezing point of the seawater source is a crucial matter for seawater heat pump. Accordingly the
operation of such an application, depends mainly on the seawater temperature and the quality of the seawater.
The designer has to design the operating conditions, meaning that the facility should be out of operation
even for several months if the temperature of the source is not the desired.
A further investigation for the thermodynamic models of our case, highlighted that in winter operation there
may be a risk of freezing the evaporator side if there is further seawater temperature decrease. The freezing
point of the source was found to be around Tfr = −2°C and according to 4.15 there is no risk of freezing
for the systems as long as the seawater temperature remains over Ts = 1°C. This is challenging the limits of
the application and to guarantee no freezing, the operation has to be controlled by the pumping station and
also a safe operational temperature over this limit to be established, according to temperature variations.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
Despite the fact that the study of this thesis is very relevant to the target of Danish environmental policy
for 2050, there are still some signiﬁcant concerns as currently there are no ofﬁcially objectives for the
heat pumps. However, there is interest in integrating them to biomass, solar and wind, existed or future
technologies. It is soon expected to be an ofﬁcial strategy of heat pump integration, but as Hofor claims,
this will depend on the availability of large scale applications that will operate, gain experience and test the
limitations further for this technology. The same concerns refer also to large seawater heat pump applications
that maybe will be integrated to local DH networks.
One of the main concerns regarding this study, is if the seawater heat pumps will be used to supply a high
temperature to the DH network, or will be used to the future in the low temperature district heating network.
This study is limited to the design parameters regarding the supply temperature. The results obtained refer
only to these temperature differences (current DH and expected LTDH). The design conditions of the LTDH
may differ from the real design conditions that may be applied when the current DH will be converted to
LTDH.
However, this parameter it is more signiﬁcant if someone look at the results. The results obtained for the
LTDH clearly state that the best case regarding the seawater heat pumps differs from the best case of the
current DH network. As long the temperature difference of the network in lower, the R152a shows its
potential compared to Ammonia cases.
Furthermore, if someone imagine that in the UK relevant policy for seawater heat pump integration claims
that the forwarding temperature will be even lower and around to Tfor = 10°C, this means that another case
of refrigerant used would be more suitable for the case. A relevant policy is also discussed for the case of
Copenhagen but this means that individual households should invest or even having the incentives to invest
on installing their own heat pump unit that would raise the temperature coming from the LTDH, maybe from
Tfor = 10°C to 60°C. The study seems to be sensitive in the selection of refrigerant according to desired
temperature differences, so this is a parameter that someone has to pay attention when designing the system.
Another concern regarding the case would be, if actually the single stage cycles that provide the heat capacity
of 5 MW could actually exist. Most manufactures would characterize this project as a custom project so
in reality some of the 1-stage cycles that are evaluated in this study are only for the comparison of their
performance characteristics. Probably components like compressors of this size are not available and this is
one of the reasons why in such applications more than one heat pump units are implemented in the same
system.
Moreover, another parameter that is assumed as free variable but maybe in the future has to be controlled by
a regulation, is the volume ﬂow rate of pumping the seawater to the heat pump units. Till now there are not
clear restrictions (regarding the ﬁlters applied and pumping), however as this is the case that would affect

pipe length of 12 km would affect the performance of seawater heat pump.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
In this thesis, main concerns regarding seawater heat pump technology and integration to the district
heating network were investigated. This study mainly highlights :
• the potential of seawater as a heat source
• the advantageous performance characteristics of alternative cycle conﬁgurations and refrigerants in
seawater heat pump technology with lower GWP
• the potential of heating in steps by a serial connection of seawater heat pumps
• what makes Ammonia as working ﬂuid the most desirable case for seawater
• the advantage of R152a thermodynamic cycle at LTDH networks
The seawater potential as a source, seems to be inﬁnite however there are many challenges for engineering
regarding the seawater conditions such as corrosion, temperature source variation, pumping, ﬁltering and
avoidance of freezing in the pipelines and evaporator.
To examine which is the best case for the seawater source heat pumps, a scenario was made that would test
the limits of the technology. This scenario was based on a similar case of seawater heat pump project of
5 MW that may take place in Copenhagen. Accordingly, the thermodynamic models of 11 different cases
categorized by alternative conﬁgurations and working ﬂuids domain were implemented in the software EES.
The models were compared on full load design phase, seasonal runs, connection to existed DH network and
possible connection to future LTDH. The heating load and design conditions were for all the models the
same.
As it was found, a conﬁguration of 2 heat pumps serial connected (cascade and 1-stage) that would be able
to heat in steps, by splitting the heating load on the two seawater heat pumps performed the maximum COP
compared to 1-stage cycle cases and the cascade system.
In full load design phase, the serial connected scheme of Ammonia working ﬂuid performed a COP = 3,2
when the single stage cycles and the cascade of Ammonia performed more or less, a COP = 3. Both of the
serial connected schemes obtained the best values in this comparison which clearly states that heating in
steps has the potential to obtain a higher performance for the system.
In the seasonal run, only the serial connected sheme of Ammonia seems to be the best option with a seasonal
COPavg = 4,18. Surprisingly second in comparison was the R152a 1-stage case with a COPavg = 3,89
and not the equally serial connected R152a scheme that ﬁnally obtained a seasonal COPavg = 3,58 , a
performance even lower compared to other 1-stage cycles.
In summer and winter operation, the best case still remains the serial connection scheme of Ammonia,

When connecting the seawater heat pump models to LTDH it seems that the best case is the serial connected

CHAPTER 7
Future work
7.1
Optimal operational temperature to avoid freezing
One of the main parameters that have to be further investigated in a future study should be a study for
ﬁnding the optimal operational temperature. To avoid the freezing of the pipelines, or the evaporator side, in
cold climates, one way is to develop the existed technology (insulation) and the other way, the easy way, is
to establish the optimal temperature difference from the freezing point of the temperature of seawater source.
As these facilities are considered large investments it is not affordable to invest on something that would
be out of operation for several months. More studies should take place in order to establish the optimal
temperature difference from freezing point so these seawater heat pump facilities get the maximum ’safe’
operating hours possible.
7.2
Seawater heat pumps and sea life
As seawater is the source further studies should take place in this area regarding the ﬁlters that should
apply. Sea life even when applying the ﬁlters, is affected. It would be relevant to establish a scientiﬁc method
that would give permission only on speciﬁc occasions. Extracting water from the bottom of the sea and as fas
as 12 km is not the same case of extracting the water near the coast and from the surface water. Regardless
the investment costs, higher potential is found when the sea water is extracted from bottom of the ocean,
however it is not the same sea life that is affected.
7.3
Field study of Refrigerants mixing
This ﬁeld of study is undergoing and still more research needs to be done as refrigerants play signiﬁcant
role in the performance characteristics of a seawater heat pump. Mixed refrigerants is not something new in
the ﬁeld, however the game has changed and the challenge is both the performance and the environmental
impact.
7.4
Field study on individual domestic heat pump units and optimization of LTDH
This study is based in the general policy of the introduction of low temperature district heating in the
near future and heat pumps. There should be a research not only regarding the optimal forwarding and
return temperatures, but also the most important, if the best option from ﬁnancial and environmental aspect
is the installation of smaller domestic heat pump units working on an even lower temperature from expected
LTDH. By this way, the seawater heat pump facility could stand and supply a forwarding temperature around
