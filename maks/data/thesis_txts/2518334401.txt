Preface 
 
In recent years, different types of automated facilitators have been studied to compare its 
effectiveness against human facilitation. However, none of these studies explore the effect of 
the facilitators on participants in terms of motivation and engagement. Together with Incept 
Sustainability, this study focuses on research and implementation of motivational elements in 
an automated facilitator to create engagement in participants about sustainability. The study 
has developed a prototype that can be further adapted to Incept Sustainability’s product 
catalogue and a set of elements that can be used as motivators in their applications. This study 
also contribute to current research about automated facilitators and the implementation of 
motivational interventions.

Abstract  
 
 
 
 
 
This master’s thesis describes the design and development of motivational interventions to 
engage participants in workshops, using an automated facilitator prototype. The motivational 
interventions were developed from research in the fields of human-computer interaction, 
sustainability and leadership. 
The prototype was developed in collaboration with Incept Sustainability, a company that 
develops e-learning material for employees in sustainability topics. 
Behavioural, cognitive and affective engagement were measured quantitatively and 
qualitatively in a two conditioned experiment, on the one hand a workshop with motivational 
interventions and, on the other, a workshop with neutral interventions. A total of 48 students 
recruited from Technical University of Denmark participated in the experiment. Results 
showed that there was high engagement in both workshops, with no statistically significant 
difference between the two. However, qualitative data showed that effectiveness increased in 
some team processes. Further research will be needed to determine the impact of the 
motivational interventions in contrast with other factors that might have influenced the 
outcome. 
This thesis represents a contribution to existing research about the application of motivational 
methods in an automated facilitator, as well as how engagement is measured in a workshop. It 
also provides a set of features that can be used as motivational interventions in both automated 
facilitators and Incept Sustainability applications.  
 
Keywords: Automated facilitation, motivation, engagement, HCI, sustainability, case study,

Introduction 
 
1.1 Project background  
 
In recent years there has been an increase in the development of technological tools for virtual 
collaboration. The virtual technologies allow the participants to give their point of view, 
discuss, share knowledge and reflect, which leads to new insights and ideas (Agrifoglio and 
Metallo, 2011). One of these technologies is virtual or automated facilitation of workshops, 
which exists in various forms. A facilitator is someone who helps a group to understand 
common goals and objectives, and assists them to achieve these without taking part in or 
influencing the discussion. This person provides structure and tools for managing a process 
(Bens, 2012). Workshop facilitation often helps to improve the overall team effectiveness and 
have therefore, become increasingly popular.  
 
The virtual and/or automated facilitators which have been developed recently, model the 
behaviours of a human facilitator (Mai et al. 2011). Human facilitation has some limitations 
that can be overcome by the use of an automated facilitator. First, an automated facilitator is 
often cost-saving compared to a human facilitator. Second, there is a potential for the 
automated facilitator to engage with a larger number of people within a bigger geographical 
distance. Third, there is prolonged access to an automated facilitator as the material is online 
(Derrick et al. 2013). These are advantages that were noticed by Incept Sustainability, the 
company this study is collaborating with. 
 
1.2 Company motivation  
 
Incept Sustainability is an online learning and analytics platform that helps companies learn, 
analyse and act on sustainability. Incept Sustainability mainly works with learning, ideation, 
and motivation for the individual employee through e-learning. That said, e-learning has 
shortcomings, as it does not facilitate a discussion between colleagues. Incept Sustainability, 
therefore, seeks to explore a more collaborative online tool in the future as part of the product 
portfolio, in the form of an automated workshop.  
 
==PAGE:13==
 
3 
In addition, part of the vision of Incept Sustainability is to empower employees on contributing 
to make sustainable decisions and challenging status quo, by teaching them and engaging them 
in sustainable topics. Therefore, considering that motivation leads to increased engagement, 
learning and creativity (Ben-Eliyahu et al. 2018), it is key for the company that the automated 
facilitator can motivate participants in the workshops and engage them in their material. 
 
1.3 Aim of the thesis   
 
Together with Incept Sustainability, the aim of this thesis is to investigate the engagement 
effect of using elements from motivational methods in an automated facilitated workshop. 
More specifically, the elements of motivational methods are from the fields of Human-
Computer Interaction (HCI), sustainability practices and leadership which will be investigated 
and adapted as interventions of an automated facilitator.  
 
According to Derrick et al. (2013), research shows that groups in a workshop facilitated by an 
automated facilitator generate better results than non-facilitated groups. Similarly, studies have 
found that workshops facilitated by an automated facilitator can perform equally efficiently as 
workshops facilitated by a human facilitator (Limayem, and Dickson, 1993, Marriott et al. 
2016). However, the specific interventions made by an automated facilitator, like motivational 
interventions, have rarely been studied. This thesis, therefore, contributes to theory on 
automated facilitation and how to develop engaging workshops. By achieving this, we aim to 
give Incept Sustainability a list of potential motivational interventions which can be used either 
in the automated facilitator or in their e-learning modules.  
  
More specifically, the research question that will be answered is as following:  
 
“How can an automated facilitator software motivate employees 
to engage in sustainable topics in a workshop?” 
 
To support the main research question the following questions have been created:  
 
RQ1: Which are the key elements that affect motivation and engagement?  
RQ2: What methods are effective in fostering motivation in human-computer interaction, in 
sustainable practices and in leadership? 
==PAGE:14==
 
4 
RQ3: How can the methods for fostering motivation and engagement be integrated into an 
automated facilitator software? 
RQ4: How can engagement in sustainable practices be measured in a workshop? 
RQ5: How do the motivational intervention perform in a workshop? 
RQ6: What are the key learnings to bring forward when designing engaging automated 
facilitated workshops?  
 
The objective of answering the listed research questions is:  
• Examine the role and effect of motivational interventions designed from motivational 
methods on an automated facilitated workshop.  
• Investigate the engagement dynamics in a group led by an automated facilitator.  
• Understand how motivational interventions can further be designed and improved to 
increase engagement in a group.  
 
1.4 Structure of thesis 
 
In order to answer the main research question and sub-questions, both the research and report 
structure adapts to the Design Research Methodology (DRM) proposed by Blessing and 
Chakrabarti (2009), see Figure 1. The DRM was chosen as it allowed both to develop the 
automated facilitator and set up a research experiment to test our hypotheses. The framework 
proposes that a design project should consist of 4 main stages. First, the Research Clarification 
stage, described in chapter 1 and 2. Here the initial research problem, understanding and 
objectives, were specified. Second, the Description Study 1 stage, described in chapter 3. In 
this stage an in-depth literature review was made to understand the influence team processes 
factors have as well as the success factors for motivating to engage. Third, the Prescriptive 
study, described in chapter 4. Here the automated facilitator was developed and the method for 
conducting and evaluating our experiment was decided. Last, the descriptive study 2 is 
described in chapter 5,6,7,8. In this stage, we conducted the research experiment as well as 
analysed and evaluated the results. For further elaboration see appendix 1.a.

Scope of the project   
 
 
Automated facilitators come in many shapes and forms, this section is therefore used to define 
the scope of automated facilitation this thesis will focus on. In addition, since engagement and 
motivation are described in the literature in various contexts, this section is used to specify the 
context in which engagement and motivation will be used.  
 
 
2.1 Automated Facilitation 
 
There does not seem to be a common definition of automated facilitation, although there are 
various synonyms for it, such as virtual facilitation and e-workshops. As there is no clear 
definition of what an automated facilitator (AF) is, there is also a big variety of what functions 
and applications an automated facilitator may have. It seems that the setting of the workshops 
and the functionalities of the automated facilitator are the two main elements that differentiate 
its design. To help clarify and define what an automated facilitator is in this study, the setting 
and functionalities of other automated facilitators are described in the following subsections.  
 
2.1.1 Set up for Automated facilitation  
Workshops facilitated by humans often have a handful of participants and a facilitator in a 
room. The participants’ number and the room size changes, but the general setting stays the 
same. On the contrary, in workshops facilitated by an automated facilitator, settings change 
more significantly. The following explores the different settings of the automated facilitators. 
Figure 2 shows an overview of the types of automated facilitators we have seen in literature.  
==PAGE:18==
 
8 
 
Figure 2 Types of automated facilitators from literature 
 
Firstly, there is a setting involving only one participant and a screen, participating in a 
workshop with others online. For example, all participants sitting behind their own computers 
in different countries. The participants can either be part of a virtual 3D world with a facilitator, 
an e-meeting with a computer program as a facilitator or look at a human facilitator through a 
webcam (Marriott et. al. 2016, Wong and Aiken 2003, Larach and Cabra 2010). A common 
factor about the workshops mentioned above is that they happen in real-time. Other types of 
virtual workshops that don’t happen in real-time include online courses and meetings. This 
type of virtual workshop is designed in advance, and the participants do not have to interact at 
the same time (Lavolette et. al. 2010). However, the setting is the same, one participant using 
a computer.  
 
==PAGE:19==
 
9 
Secondly, another setting that is more similar to a human-facilitated workshop, is when the 
human-facilitator is replaced by a screen and all the participants are still in the same room. The 
screen can take different formats, for example an agent which looks and talks like a human 
(Derrick et. al. 2013). In addition, some automated facilitators are designed to help a human 
facilitator during the workshop as a virtual assistant (Strohmann 2018). 
 
Overall, the setup of the existing automated facilitators varies quite significantly. Designing an 
automated facilitator will therefore require to decide on the physical setup that is most relevant 
for the experiment and Incept Sustainability’s requirements.  
2.1.2 Functionalities of Automated facilitation  
This section explores the functionalities of already existing automated facilitators, in order to 
identify main functionalities. The functionalities of automated facilitators are closely related to 
the setting in which they are used. However, there seems to exist a common trait, which is that 
the functionalities mimic a human-facilitator. 
 
Most of the automated facilitators are designed to structure the workshop. Wong and Aiken 
(2003) have researched an automated facilitator in e-meetings. The automated facilitator 
includes a system operator, which performs operations like giving technical instructions, taking 
time, etc. Likewise, Marriott et. al. (2016) describes a type of virtual facilitation, where a 
human facilitates a workshop online via a webcam. The designated automated facilitator’s 
main job is to manage time and coordinate discussions in the e-meetings. In the same manner, 
other tools exist to facilitate classrooms and online meetings. These systems include 
possibilities for planning a process of learning, with multimedia recordings, online discussions 
and direct feedback channels (Lavolette et. al. 2010). Also, a virtual workshop assistance has 
been developed, the main functionality of which is to structure the workshop beforehand, and 
help with management during a workshop (Strohmann 2018).  
 
Additional functionalities of automated facilitators also include increasing collaboration, 
creativity and discussion. Among other things, we see automated facilitators support creativity 
by providing information (Larach and Cabra 2010), asking questions to help participants 
discuss (Derrick et. al. 2013) and prompting participants to work as a team.  
 
==PAGE:20==
 
10 
In short, all the researched automated facilitators have the functionality of structuring a 
workshop. In addition, there are possibilities to add other features depending on the nature of 
the desired result. In this study the automated facilitator must be able to structure the workshop. 
Having mapped out the setting and functions of different types of automated facilitators, it 
would be useful to look at the automated facilitator’s effectiveness in facilitating a workshop.  
2.1.3 Effectiveness of Automated facilitation 
Most studies show that automated facilitated workshops are as effective as human facilitated 
workshops, regardless of the type of automated facilitator (Limayem and Dickson 1993). 
Furthermore, studies show that automated facilitated workshops do increase effectiveness 
compared to non facilitated groups (Derrick et al. 2013). For example, in a study done by 
Marriott et. al. (2016), the authors conclude that the effectiveness of their virtual workshops is 
as effective as their human facilitated workshops. They quote participants highlighting this, as 
well as underlining the functionality structuring and the online atmosphere, to mention a 
couple.  
 
However, automated facilitation might involve some risks. For instance, the “human touch” 
might decrease, lowering satisfaction and motivation (Wong and Aiken 2003). The study of 
Marriott et. al. (2016) also has participants indicating that people drop out, a networking 
component is missing and some participants prefer the face-to-face experience. Nonetheless, 
the general conclusion is that the effectiveness does not seem to decrease significantly in 
automated facilitated workshops. To ease the design process moving forward, the next section 
will define what an automated facilitator is in this study based on the previous analysis.

setting and functionalities, we have chosen to define the automated facilitator of this study 
based on these:  
 
Setting: The automated facilitator will be a program on a computer screen facilitating a 
workshop with a smaller group of people that are in the same room, see

2.2 Engagement and Motivation 
 
Engagement and motivation are studied in a number of fields and contexts. This section 
therefore serves to narrow our research and define the relevant fields of motivation and 
engagement. Further analysis of both concepts will be presented in the Chapter 3: literature 
review. 
2.2.1 Engagement  
Nowadays, engagement is described in a variety of ways, depending on the context, e.g. 
Stakeholder, Customer, student or employee engagement. Student engagement is the most 
relevant for this study, considering that it is related to individuals engaged in a learning activity 
such as a workshop.   
2.2.2 Motivation  
There are several psychological theories that study the factors that influence motivation, and, 
in general they can be grouped into two types (Touré-Tillery and Fishbach 2014): “outcome-
focused” motivation (extrinsic motivation, motivation to complete a goal) and “process-
focus” or “means-focused” motivation (intrinsic motivation, focused on the elements that 
relate to the goal pursuit process).  Self-determination theory (SDT) is a theory that has been 
==PAGE:22==
 
12 
thoroughly researched, concerned with exploring intrinsic and extrinsic motivation in depth, 
as well as the factors that cause human behaviour and optimize individuals development, 
performance and well-being (Ryan and Deci 2000). Therefore, this theory is explored in more 
detail in the Chapter 3: literature review. 
2.2.3 Motivational methods  
To design the motivational intervention we will explore current motivational methods from 
other fields. Because of this, it is relevant to choose which fields to investigate further.  
 
As well as there are several phychological theories of motivation, there is also a range of 
methods used to motivate people in different fields. Figure 4 gives an overview of the different 
fields identified for motivational practices. For the purpose of this thesis, the fields HCI, 
Sustainable Practices and Employees within Leadership have been chosen as focus areas.  
 
 
Figure 4 Fields that propose motivational methods 
 
First, motivation and engagement of users in HCI is a recurrent and well-researched topic 
(Doherty, K. and Doherty, G., 2018). It is highly relevant for the development of the automated 
facilitator as the participants will interact with a software application instead of a human and 
therefore the engagement with an application has an influence on the overall engagement of an 
activity (e.g. workshop). 
 
Second, sustainability is used as a case study for this thesis as the content of the automated 
facilitated workshop will revolve around this. Motivational methods from research focusing on 
motivating people to change their behaviours would thus seem relevant.  
 
==PAGE:23==
 
13 
Third, the motivation of employees within leadership seemed relevant for the development of 
the automated facilitator as many of the same motivational elements are used by facilitators in 
workshops to motivate participants.  
 
2.3 Key learnings 
Considering the wide spectrum of literature in the researched areas of this study, it was 
important to scope down the different concepts into more specific ones so they can be used 
through the rest of the study, avoiding misunderstandings and to maintain coherence. 
 
In terms of automated facilitation, we explored existing systems and realized that they can be 
defined by their setting and its functionalities. In our study, the automated facilitator will 
consist of a computer in a room with participants, which will structure the workshop and foster 
motivation.

The term “team” has been defined in a number of different ways with small variations. The

“(a) two or more individuals who (b) socially interact (face-to-face or, increasingly, virtually);

==PAGE:28==

The mediator component of the input-mediator-output framework, is the transformative 
mechanism, and it refers to the emergent processes (activities) and states which explain the 
relationship between inputs and outputs. It is the phase where a team take actions which result 
in achieving a goal (Mathieu et. al. 2008). Processes and states can be classified into three 
mechanisms: behavioural, cognitive and affective. It is important for us to understand how each 
of these mechanisms work, as we thereby can create corresponding interventions that generate 
a desired outcome (engagement). Following, the next sections will dive into each mechanism.  
 
Behavioural mechanisms  
Behavioural mechanisms, in team processes, are what the team members do, their activities 
and interactions, mainly focusing on finishing tasks (Figure 8). Within these mechanisms, 
interventions made by the automated facilitator can affect the teams’ behaviour. Processes that 
occur under this category are transition processes, action processes and interpersonal processes. 
 
Transition processes  
Marks et al. (2001, p 364) have defined transition processes as “Transition periods of time 
when teams focus primarily on evaluation and/or planning activities to guide their 
accomplishment of a team goal or objective.” Activities in the transition process are mission 
analysis, goal specification, strategy formulation and planning, which often occur during the 
time set aside for analysis, evaluation and future direction, see Figure 8 for further details. 
Research has shown a moderate, positive relationship between transition processes, team 
performance and satisfaction (Salas, Rico and Passmore, 2017, p 252).  
 
Action processes  
Action processes is a phase where the team works towards accomplishing its goals and 
objectives. Within this phase, four dimensions are present: monitoring progress toward goals, 
system monitoring, team monitoring and backup responses, and coordination activities (Marks 
et al., 2001, p 366). Studies have observed that action processes have an influence on team 
effectiveness (Salas, Rico, and Passmore, 2017, p 253).   
 
Interpersonal Processes  
Interpersonal processes occur both under transition and action phases and consist of three 
dimensions: conflict management, motivation/confidence building and affect (emotions) 
==PAGE:29==
 
19 
management (Marks et al., 2001, p 368). Interpersonal processes play a fundamental role and 
are the foundation of effectiveness for teams and other processes (Salas, Rico and Passmore, 
2017, p 255).

Learnings 
From the short review above, key findings are that transition, action and interpersonal 
processes relate to the behaviour of a team which have an effect on team efficiency and 
satisfaction. This means that by increasing the quality of these processes within a workshop 
the overall efficiency and satisfaction will increase.  
 
Affective mechanisms 
Affective mechanisms involve relationships, motivational characteristics and affective 
reactions in teams. Interventions made by the automated facilitator in this area can affect the 
teams overall mood and emotions. Mechanisms that are characteristic for effective team 
process are among others; Team cohesion, team efficacy and group potency (affect, mood, 
and emotions) (Salas, Ricoa and Passmore, 2017, p 248).

Group Cohesion 
Group cohesion has been defined in multiple ways by researchers, Festinger (1950) has defined 
cohesiveness as “the result of all the forces acting on the members to remain in the group” 
(Kozlowski and R. Ilgen, 2018, p 88). According to Festinger, the three factors that make up 
cohesion are: attraction, group activities and prestige or group pride, also known as social 
cohesion, task cohesion and group pride (Salas, Rico and Passmore, 2017, p 248), described in 
Figure 9. 
 
Team confidence  
Team confidence includes two related, but distinct categories; Team efficacy and potency. The 
difference between the team efficacy and potency is that efficacy is the teams’ belief that they 
will successfully complete a specific task, whereas potency is the team’s general sense of the 
capabilities to complete various tasks in a different context (Mathieu et al., 2008). Studies that 
have shown that team efficacy and potency have a positive impact on team performance 
(Mathieu et al. 2008, Salas, Rico and Passmore, 2017).  
 
Team trust  
Trust between team members is critical for working together effectively. Teams that are 
operating without trust will often experience lost time, ineffective use of energy, since 
members will be checking and inspecting the other team members instead of collaborating. 
Having trust in teams will impact a number of team processes and outcomes such as group 
participation, contribution, cycle times, product quality and team member retention. 
Additionally, some studies have found that trust fosters a willingness to disseminate 
information more freely within the team (Salas, E. Sims and Burke, 2005).

Cognitive mechanisms refer to the thought processes in a team and how knowledge is 
organised, as well as how new information is acquired. The automated facilitator has a

Team mental models  
Team mental models refer to how knowledge is organised and represented within a team. It 
captures a team’s shared understanding and the mental representation of the knowledge that is 
relevant to the group. Research has shown that cognitive representations can help team 
members to anticipate actions and needs within a team and thereby adjust and coordinate their 
behaviour which leads to improved team effectiveness (Kozlowski and R. Ilgen, 2018, p 83).

3.1.2.1 Motivation 
The concept of motivation has been discussed in depth within the field of psychology since the 
late 1950s (Kleinginna and Kleinginna 1981). The general consensus seems to be that 
motivation is an energizing/arousing mechanism, or the psychological force and desire that 
influence behaviour and enable action (Touré‐Tillery and Fishbach 2014). To be motivated is 
to have the desire of doing something, activated towards an end or a direction. 
 
The Self-determination theory (SDT) has been thoroughly researched and empirically 
validated (Peters, Calvo, Ryan, 2018). The theory posits that there are two main types of 
motivation - intrinsic and extrinsic. Following, both types of motivation will be explored.

chooses to engage in this activity for the pleasure, interest, enjoyment and satisfaction derived 
from performing it. Psychological needs that contribute to the maintenance and enhancement

3.1.2.2 Engagement  
 
Engagement derives from the word “gage”, meaning “pledge”, which can be defined as to tie 
oneself to a course of action (Axelson et. al. 2010). Student engagement has been defined in 
several ways. According to Axelsong et al., some researchers focus on engagement as a 
measurement of involvement with their learning environments and others as a variable in 
research to understand, explain and predict students behaviour in the learning environment. 
 
To broaden the understanding, three more commonly used definitions collected by Trowler 
and Vicki (2010) in a review on student engagement are listed below:  
 
“Participation in educationally effective practices, both inside and outside the classroom, 
which leads to a range of measurable outcomes.” 
“The extent to which students are engaging in activities 
that higher education research has show to be linked with high-quality learning outcomes” 
“The process whereby institutions and sector bodies make deliberate attempts to involve and 
empower students in the process of shaping the learning experience” 
 
These definitions describe students’ engagement as participation, involvement and 
empowerment of students inside and outside a learning environment. For the purpose of this 
study, we will focus only on engagement inside learning environment settings which “are 
indicative of productive involvement with the learning materials” (Ben-Eliyahu et al. 2018). 
This concept can be broken down into three components (dimensions). Behavioural 
engagement in student work is about involvement/participation in academic and social 
activities. Emotional engagement is based on the idea of appeal and cognitive engagement is 
the idea of investment (Axelson et. al. 2010). Dimensions are independent of each other, as a 
student could be behaviourally active but not cognitively or emotionally engaged in an activity 
(Ben-Eliyahu et al., 2018). Below are further descriptions of the 3 components.

3.1.2.3 Motivation is a key element of engagement  
There is an overlap in the literature on motivation and engagement. Some research 
conceptualizes motivation the same way as engagement. In our study, we refer to motivation 
as an instigator of engagement, or as a “pre existing learner characteristic that produces 
engagement” (Ben-Eliyahu et al. 2018). Without the desire or drive to learn, engagement will 
not occur.  
 
The study done by Ben-Eliyahu et al. establishes that specifically, three components of intrinsic 
motivation correlate positively with overall engagement: autonomy, intrinsic goal orientation 
(referring to the identification and integration extrinsic forms of motivation) and self-efficacy 
(competence).

For the purpose of this study, we will, therefore, see motivation as a key driver for engagement

3.2.1 Sustainability practices methods 
Sustainable behaviour has continuously become more important as the state of our planet is 
changing for the worse. Today the news is constantly reporting about the consequences of 
human actions like climate change, decrease in biodiversity, and extreme weather among other 
things. Nevertheless, humans still have a hard time adapting to their behaviours. In more recent 
years, research has therefore started to focus on how to motivate people to change their 
behaviour towards more sustainable practices (Quintal et al. 2013). Two areas that mention

methods to motivate individuals were found in the literature: organisational citizenship

3.2.1.1 Organisational citizenship behaviour towards the environment (OBCE) 
OBCE has been defined as “individual and discretionary social behaviours that are not 
explicitly recognized by the formal reward system and contribute to a more effective 
environmental management by organisations” (Soon-Yew Ju et. al. 2015). OBCE originated 
from the concept of Organisational Citizenship Behaviour (OCB) which is personal and 
voluntary behaviour that goes beyond one’s prescribed job duties without any rewards signed 
to it (Temminck, Mearns, and Fruhen, 2015).  
 
Research suggests that employee engagement in OCBE is one of the key success factors in 
incorporating sustainability into companies (Temminck, Mearns and Fruhen, 2015). 
Employees exceed their job descriptions and enhance environmentally friendly practices and 
assist co-workers in changing their behaviour, ultimately leading to boots the environmental 
effectiveness and efficiency at companies (Soon-Yew Ju et. al. 2015). 
 
There are numerous variables that can lead to OCBE in a companies, however, studies seem to 
agree that personal environmental concern, organisational commitment and perceived 
organisational support is a key factor for employees to engage in OCBE  (Temminck, Mearns, 
and Fruhen, 2015, Sutter, 2007). Three main categories of action that employees make have 
been classified by Boiral and Paillé; eco-initiatives, eco-civic engagement and eco-helping. 
Eco-initiatives refers to discretionary behaviour and ideas to improve environmental 
performance in a company. Eco-civic engagement is when employees voluntarily participate 
in environmental programs or activities provided by the organisation. Last, eco-helping is 
helping colleagues to understand and integrate environmental concerns in the organisation 
(Boiral and Paillé, 2012) 
 
There seems to be a lack of literature focusing on how organisations obtain high OCBE. 
However, studies focusing on developing OCB in an organisation by leadership tools have 
been made. A study made by Boiral and Paillé (2012) concludes that leadership has an effect 
on the level of OCB. More specifically high levels of the following activities will promote

3.2.1.2 Eco-feedback technologies 
Eco-feedback has been defined as “technology that provides feedback on individual or group 
behaviours with a goal of reducing environmental impact” (Quintal et al. 2013). Eco-feedback 
is mainly used for energy saving purposes as there today is a limited understanding of energy 
consumption. The limited understanding is mainly due to disconnection between electricity 
billing (often every 3 months) and the daily behaviour (Miller and Senadeera, 2017). However, 
other applications of eco-feedback are addressing other types of resources or addressing sectors 
of consumptions (Sanguinetti, Dombrovski, and Sikand, 2018). Eco-feedback seems to be a 
promising technology to increase awareness, motivation and reduce resource consumption. 
Studies have shown results where eco-feedback technologies can reduce daily energy 
consumption by 5-12%. (Quintal et al. 2013) 
 
The eco-feedback technologies are both designed to individuals or groups and the medium can 
range from desktop display, mobile devices or ambient interfaces. In addition, the feedback 
information is either given instantly or as a historical backlog, and can be presented in multiple 
ways (Spagnolli, et al. 2011). 
 
Eco-feedback research has mainly been done within behavioural theory or HCI (which will be 
described in the next section). Sanguinetti et al. (2018) have developed a conceptual framework 
to start connecting research within behavioural theory and HCI to develop more effective and 
diverse eco-feedback technologies. The frameworks consist of three dimensions; information, 
timing and display. The information dimension relates to the information presented to the user 
with two subcategories granularity and message. Timing refers to when the information is 
presented in relation to the desired behaviour. The display dimension relates to mechanisms of 
feedback and how assessable that is (Sanguinetti, Dombrovski and Sikand, 2018)). Appendix 
3A shows the details of the three dimensions.

3.2.1.3 Key takeaways from sustainable practices  
Motivational methods within sustainable practices are directed towards changing behaviours 
of people. OBCE describes employee engagement to enhancing environmental friendly 
practices through eco-initiatives, eco-civic engagement and eco-helping. To reach high OCBE 
we have to look for methods within OCB that contain elements like modelling the way, 
inspiring a shared vision and challenging the process. Eco-feedback technologies, on the other 
hand, work with changing behaviour based on giving feedback on the behaviours of individuals 
or groups. The conceptual framework for eco-feedback suggests three key dimensions to keep 
in mind when designing for sustainable behaviour change; information, Display and timing. 
Elements from the motivational methods used in these areas will be applied in the design of 
the automated facilitator.  
 
 
3.2.2 Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) 
Engagement in Human-Computer Interaction derives from the previous concept of student 
engagement, and it refers to the depth of a user's cognitive, temporal, affective and behavioural 
investment when interacting with a digital system (Doherty and Doherty, 2018). It is a recurrent 
research topic in HCI, as part of the need for understanding users and their interaction with 
technology. The research includes understanding of what motivation is and how to use the 
needs that foster motivation and sustained engagement in order to develop technologies that 
motivate individuals into changing their behaviour (Peters, Calvo, Ryan, 2018).  
 
Behaviour change technologies are often based on research about psychological needs and 
behavioural theory, used to design and implement functionalities that motivate and engage 
individuals (Peters, Calvo, Ryan, 2018). Several systems used for behaviour change apply 
frameworks based on self-determination theory. The techniques used can be collected within 
what is called persuasive technologies and gamification. 
 
3.2.2.1 Self-determination theory in HCI 
The research made by Peters, Calvo and Ryan is based on the self-determination motivation 
theory. It is based on the three basic needs of intrinsic motivation: autonomy, competence and

34 
relatedness. In this paper, the authors propose a model that links the psychological needs that 
foster motivation with five spheres representing different levels of user experience, which 
technology can have an impact on (Peters, Calvo, Ryan, 2018) see Figure 13.  
 
Adoption: When individuals become aware of technology and use it for the first time.  
Interface: On this level, sustained engagement can be accomplished through usability and user

Task: The development of features or specific tasks can affect the psychological need for 
satisfaction and create engagement. 
Behaviour: What is the behaviour that should be changed or supported? Even though tasks and 
interface might foster engagement while using technology, users could not be motivated to or

Life: the impact of technology in overall wellbeing and life satisfaction. 
Society: technological impact on social wellbeing.

3.2.2.2 Persuasive technologies 
Persuasive technologies refer to the systems that are created with the purpose of inducing and 
convincing users to perform specific actions in the real world (Carolis and Mazzotta, 2011).

3.2.2.3 Gamification 
Gamification refers to “using game-based mechanics, aesthetics and game thinking to engage 
people, motivate action, promote learning, and solve problems ' (Kapp, 2012, p. 10). It does 
not refer to video games, but to the application of video games features or characteristics, to 
non-game applications. There has been research about gamification considering its influence 
on behaviour, and how it is used to provoke enjoyment, curiosity, frustration, as well as to 
foster engagement in terms of both intrinsic and extrinsic motivation, in both student 
engagement and in the healthcare field (Buckley, Patrick & Doyle, Elaine, 2014). In a study 
made by Buckley and Elaine (2014) they use a gamified online learning system built around a 
==PAGE:47==

future events. On the other hand, James et. al. (2006) developed the social computer game 
Fish'n'Steps, that links the daily footsteps of a participant with the growth and activity of an 
animated virtual character. Another case within the healthcare field is the study provided by 
Nunes and Nedel (2014), where they developed a virtual environment where players can 
interact with others while using a treadmill, a computer and a display for visualization.

3.2.2.4 Key takeaways from HCI 
The study of user engagement is of particular importance to the HCI community due to interest

interface and application tasks.

3.2.3.3 Growth mindset 
Dweck (2006) has proposed that people can have either a fixed mindset or a growth mindset 
which describes the beliefs people have about learning and intelligence. A fixed mindset is 
defined as; “Believing your quantities are carved in stone” and a growth mindset is defined as 
“the belief that you basic quantities are things you can cultivate through your efforts, your 
strategies, and help from others” (Dweck, 2006). The belief creates a mental framework that 
encourages engagement in different behaviours and thereby impacts outcomes such as 
performance and achievement. Growth mindset has been linked with positive outcomes such 
as motivation (Sullivan and Page 2019). Dweck’s theories are based on children in schools and 
how they deal with challenges. However, the method can also be used in terms of organisation, 
employee motivation and leadership. Managers with a growth mindset are in general more 
committed to their own and their employees’ development. It is possible to change people's 
mindsets from a fixed to a growth mindset and from growth to fixed. In the following, we will 
explore different theories and methods to change people's mindset towards a growth mindset 
as it fosters motivation.  
 
In a study done by Sullivan and Page they conclude that developing a growth mindset requires 
self-reflection, choice of mindset and strategy as well as a persistence in action and feedback 
loops from action into awareness of oneself. Likewise, Dweck proposes the same 4 steps to 
achieve a growth mindset, seen in Table 10.

3.2.3.4 Key takeaways from Leadership  
Methods for motivating employees are increasingly becoming more important in literature and 
pratice as research shows it among other things had increased engagement, commitment and 
passion. Empowerment of employees is one tool to motivate people. Empowerment considers 
the employees to be part of the decision-making processes and exert influence in their work. 
The Self-Determination Theory highlight that employees should feel “ownership” in their 
work, have meaningful connections and valuable support. The last leadership theory researched 
in this study is the growth mindset which is a certain type of mindset people can have and 
leaders can foster in employees. Fostering a growth mindset leads to more motivation and 
engagement with activities.  
 
It seems possible that by converting the actions suggested from empowerment, self- 
determination theory and growth mindset leadership to functions of the automated facilitator

4. Hypotheses 
 
After studying what engagement is and the methods that can be used to foster it, the goal of 
this project is to understand if these motivational methods can be used to increase engagement 
in participants in a workshop. We are interested in both individual and total engagement, which 
leads to the first two hypotheses: 
 
1a: Motivational interventions in the AF have a greater total engagement of the team than 
random interventions. 
1b: Motivational interventions in the AF have a greater individual engagement than random 
interventions. 
 
However, as described in the theory, engagement has three dimensions. A person could be 
more behavioural engaged than affectively engaged. Considering this, we are interested in 
comprehending if the motivation methods can foster specific dimensions of engagement: 
 
2a: Motivational interventions in the AF have a greater behavioural engagement of the team 
than random interventions. 
2b: Motivational interventions in the AF have a greater cognitive engagement of the team than 
random interventions. 
2c: Motivational interventions in the AF have a greater affective engagement of the team than 
random interventions. 
 
An outcome of engagement in the process is increased creativity among participants. We are 
therefore also interested in the creative output the students are generating from the workshops:  
 
3a: Motivational interventions will increase the quantity of the ideas generated by the 
participants in the workshop. 
3b: Motivational interventions will increase the variety of the ideas generated by the 
participants in the workshop.

==PAGE:69==

draft of the system. Each wireframe represented a screen and the connection between the 
screens was presented through microinteractions. Essentially, each screen contained a section 
for the content placed in the middle of the screen, a right and/or left arrow to transition between 
them, and a progress indicator at the bottom (see appendix 5H). The reason why this layout 
was chosen is because it was similar to the existing Incept Sustainability e-learning material.  
 
The wireframes were presented to Incept Sustainability, and based on their feedback, 
formulation of instructions and design specifications like color palette, the font family and size 
were modified. 
 
Second iteration 
Second iteration was done by creating a prototype of the software as a web application, 
developed using HTML5, JavaScript and the Bootstrap 4 framework, applying the 
specifications requested from the feedback from Incept Sustainability in the previous iteration. 
After this, four users (students) were recruited and a thinking aloud method was conducted. In 
a ‘thinking aloud’ method, participants are asked to carry out a task, while verbalizing their 
thoughts (Krahmer and Ummelen, 2004). Researchers write down a verbal report and analyze 
them after. In this case, the users were asked to follow the instructions specified by the 
automated facilitator and everytime the user showed some confusion or there was a 
misunderstanding, a note was written and evaluated afterwards. In this way, qualitative data 
was collected. 
 
The main points obtained from the thinking aloud method and its corresponding resolution are 
presented below: 
• There were some content misunderstandings, specifically with the titles of each screen. 
From this, new titles were formulated to add clarification. 
• Some instructions were not clear enough and caused confusion. For example, it was not 
clear if an exercise started on the screen number 5 or on the screen number 6. Similarly, 
for some users it was not clear that they needed to use the mouse and when, to advance 
between screens. Because of this, instructions were modified to be more specific, to 
indicate transition actions or information, for example “Click the next arrow to 
continue” or “Exercise will start on the next slide”. 
==PAGE:70==
 
60 
• The countdown warning was placed just before the first exercise starts instead of 
several screens before, this was due to users being always alert and wondering when 
the countdown would activate. 
• There was confusion about the voting feature. It was difficult for the users to imagine 
how to place the post-its using the image presented on screen. From this, it was decided 
to introduce a physical template in the workshop, as an aid for the automated facilitator 
(Appendix 5I). 
 
Third iteration 
After applying the changes from the feedback collected in the previous iteration,  the last 
iteration consisted of a final version of the prototype and a summative usability test of the 
MVP. The purpose of this was to establish that the developed software was efficient, intuitive, 
easy and satisfying to use, avoiding generating any frustration with the system that might 
influence the overall experience of the motivational interventions. 
This was done by using a System Usability Scale (SUS), which is a tool that provides a measure 
of people’s subjective perception of usability (Brooke, 1996), in other words, how “usable” the 
system is according to the user’s assessment. It is a tool used to test the level of the participant’s 
satisfaction of the product or system, preferably after the participant has used the system being 
evaluated. It consists of a ten-item questionnaire where respondents are given five response 
options, from “Strongly disagree” to “Strongly agree”. The questions are alternate between 
positive and negative statements, preventing any response bias caused by the respondent.

Test conditions 
The experiment was run within-subjects. Within-subjects is also called ‘repeated measures’, 
because the measurements on each test condition are repeated for each participant (MacKenzie, 
2013), or group of participants. One of the advantages of choosing this test condition is that it 
requires fewer participants, which means that it was easier for recruiting and scheduling. 
Additionally, the variance due to participant’s personality or mental condition that might 
influence the performance is approximately the same across test conditions. Similarly, 
balancing is handled, that is making sure test conditions are equal from one team to another.  
 
As mentioned, the test conditions of the experiment consisted of the two types of workshops. 
To counterbalance any order effect or learning effect, half of the teams started with the 
motivational workshops (Condition A) and half of the teams started with the non motivational 
workshops (Condition B). Because the structure of the workshops were similar, the company 
selected as case study also changed in the test conditions (Arla or Carlsberg), and the 
sustainable value creation task was carried out on them. Figure 26 shows the planning for the 
experiment, describing the counterbalancing.

5.2.5 Measures  
In this section, the main methods applied to gather and analyse the data obtained are presented. 
A 4-point Likert scale was used to measure engagement in the workshop. The creativity 
measures were done by a simple mean and a genealogical categorization. These two measures 
were the main analysis to confirm or reject the hypotheses presented in chapter 4. Further, we 
conducted a factor analysis for the engagement scale. To better understand the comments from 
the participants, an affinity diagram was used. Lastly, we conducted a comparative thematic 
analysis in order to evaluate the video recordings.  
Engagement scale: Activity Engagement Questionnaire  
Ben-Eliyahu et al. (2018) have designed a questionnaire to measure engagement processes 
(affective, behavioural and cognitive, and overall engagement) in a learning activity. They 
examine the nature of engagement and the consistency of this structure across contexts and 
activities. Affective, cognitive and behavioural engagement were therefore measured 
according to the items proposed by the mentioned study. The activity engagement survey 
consists of 18 items. The first 15 items are on a 4 point Likert scale and question 16-18 on yes 
or no responses.  
 
Question 1-5 measures affective engagement, where an emotion from each emotion space was 
included in the questions (happiness, relaxation, frustration, tiredness, boredom). Questions 
6,9,10 and 16 measures cognitive engagement, where processes such as thinking and learning 
are emphasized. Lastly, the remaining questions measures behavioural engagement which 
focuses on the involvement in the activity. The study Ben-Eliyahu et al. also measured the 
==PAGE:78==
 
68 
reliability of the dimensions (affective, behavioural, cognitive), using Cronbach’s Alpha, 
which ranged from .64 < ⍺ < .88, providing acceptable consistency. (Appendix 5K contains 
the questionnaire). 
 
Some of the questions had to be adapted to the context of our experiment. Question 16 
articulates “I asked questions to an adult”. The original questionnaire was intended for children 
around the age of 11, however, the study was built to reach all audiences. Considering this, the 
question was changed to “I asked questions/used the help function of the automated facilitator”. 
Another question articulated “I figured out something about science”. Because the main subject 
in the workshops was Sustainable Value creation, the question was changed to “I figured out 
something about sustainable value creation “. 
Creativity measures  
Creativity is measured by using Shah, Smith and Vargas-Hernandez (2003) metrics for 
measuring ideation effectiveness. More specifically, we chose to focus on measuring the 
number of ideas and the variety of ideas. Increasing the number of ideas should indicate a better 
chance of better ideas. Quantity of ideas is calculated by a simple count. However it is 
suggested that this method is assisted by a measure such as variety. Variety measures the 
explored solution space. There is a low variety if a lot of similar ideas are generated and there 
is less probability of finding better ideas in other areas of the solution space. The variety of 
ideas is calculated by the use of a genealogical categorization based on how ideas fulfil each 
design function. As there could be a bias in the categorization part, we decided to have a third 
party to do the categorization that did not know the hypotheses being tested.  
 
2.2.6 Tests performed 
Wilcoxon test  
The method of study presented by Ben-Eliyahu et al. (2018) states that computing both overall 
engagement score, as well as per each dimension (behaviour, affective and cognitive) can be 
done with simple means. Considering that the measurement scale is interval and that the test 
design was within-subjects, the non parametric procedure, Wilcoxon Signed-Rank test, was 
used to compare the two conditions. The tests were conducted using Python 3.7.1 SciPy library. 
 
==PAGE:79==
 
69 
The Wilcoxon signed-rank test aims to compare groups statistically before and after an 
intervention. This is done by calculating the differences between their ranks (Suchmacher and 
Geller, 2012). In a Wilcoxon signed-rank test, the null and alternative hypotheses are as 
follows: 
 
H0: the median difference is zero. 
H1: the median difference is positive. 
Cronbach’s Alpha and Exploratory Factor analysis  
In order to measure the internal consistency between the elements (Brown, 2002) of the 
Activity Engagement Questionnaire, Cronbach’s Alpha was calculated, using IBM SPSS 
Statistics software Version 20. Cronbach’s alpha is used to estimate the proportion of variance 
that is systematic or consistent in a set of test scores, in other words, the strength of the 
relationship between the questions. A high cronbach’s alpha indicates that items have shared 
covariance and they probably measure the same underlying concept. 
 
For the results that indicated a low cronbach’s alpha, an exploratory factor analysis was 
performed. The analysis was conducted using PCA (Principal Component Analysis) as the 
method. An exploratory factor analysis identifies correlated variables, finding relationships, by 
means of factors (Mooi, Sarstedt and Mooi-Reci, n.d.) or dimensions (cognitive, behavioural, 
affective). When doing a Principal Component Analysis, the component matrix is used to see 
the correlations between the item and the dimension. 
Affinity diagram  
The affinity diagram is a method used to organize field data. We used it to organize the answers 
of the students from the last question: “Which one was your favourite part of both workshops? 
why?”. The affinity diagram can help to show the scope of a problem or define performance, 
reliability and more. When building an affinity diagram the notes from all users/participants 
are printed on sticky notes in a random order. Hereafter, the statements are grouped together 
to show patterns (Holtzblatt, Beyer 2017). 
 
==PAGE:80==
 
70 
Comparative thematic analysis  
To analyse the videos of the workshops a comparative thematic analysis was used. A thematic 
analysis identifies, analyses and reports patterns (themes) with data. It is a way of minimize, 
organize and describe data. For this thesis, an adapted version of Braun and Clarke’s (2008) 
six phase thematic analysis was used. Due to the time limit step only phase 2,3,5 and 6 was 
conducted. Phase 2 is generating initial codes, where interesting features of the data are 
collected in a systematic fashion. For this phase we both analysed each video individually, with 
a template to ensure consistency in the data collection, see appendix 5L for an example. Phase 
3 is searching for themes, where the codes from phase 2 are put into potential themes. This was 
done after each video was over and phase 2 had been finalized, see appendix 5M for an 
example. Phase 2 and 3 left us a lot of data, which was organized in phase 4 where themes are 
seen in relation to each other and a thematic ‘map’ is generated of the analysis. This was done 
by first visualizing the data from phase 3, which then was laid out on a table in order to see the 
bigger picture and phase 5 was done by defining the overacting themes. Lastly, phase 6- 
Producing the report, was used in the analysis presented in the next section.

First vs. second workshops  
Generally, we identified higher team cohesion in the second workshop, irrespective of whether 
it was motivational or non motivational. Although it was shown that there was an increase of 
team cohesion throughout the entire workshop, it is not possible to identify a specific moment 
when started to happen. Another pattern is observed was knowledge sharing (transactive 
memory systems) in the second workshops. The participants start sharing experiences and 
knowledge they have had outside of the workshops to help each other. However, this pattern 
is mainly seen in the first two phases of the workshop (introduction and brainstorming).  
 
Finally, it seems that transition processes are more frequent in the second workshops. Here, the 
participants start to strategize about alternative ways to come up with more value points. They 
also talk about the mission and try to figure out if it was reached optimally. The transition 
processes are mainly seen in the first two phases of the workshop.  
 
Non motivational vs. motivational workshops.  
When comparing the non-motivational workshops to the motivational workshops, it seems that 
there is stronger team cohesion in the motivational workshop. Generally, the participants 
==PAGE:92==
 
82 
talked, smiled more and had a better dynamic. In the workshops with motivational 
interventions the team cohesion increased over the three phases, whereas in the non 
motivational workshop the team cohesion increased in the first two phases and decreased again 
in the third phase.  
 
Another observation was that it seemed like the team coordination activities increased, which 
meant that the teams carried out more planning. However, on phases 1 and 3, the team 
coordination activities were higher in the motivational workshop, whereas in phase 2 the level 
of coordination activities was higher in the non motivational workshop.  
 
Lastly, team trust and mental models seemed more present in the motivational workshops, 
during all its phases.  
 
6.5 Individual reactions 
As this study evaluates people’s reactions, it is interesting to analyze individual reactions from 
the participants. This analysis is part of the qualitative study, which supplements the 
quantitative. In the following, the reactions and comments collected from the participants in 
the workshops are described. 
Leadership personalities  
In several workshops, there was often one of the participants that assumed the leadership role. 
In Group 1 for example, during the workshop with motivational interventions, one participant 
took the leadership role very quickly and led the brainstorm exercise. When they arrived at the 
voting exercise, after reading the instructions individually, the participant that assumed the role 
of leadership explained what they needed to do, according to what she understood the 
instructions stated, and the others followed. However, the interpretation of the self-chosen 
”leader” was partly different from what the exercise was about, therefore they ended up 
discussing the right solution again and how to adapt to the exercise.  
Individual reactions to “Gives options on how to brainstorm” intervention 
The exercise where the participants choose which brainstorm method to use generated many 
different individual reactions. For group 3, a participant shows high excitement and stands up 
while saying “we get to choose – cool!”, encouraging the rest of the group to stand up as well. 
==PAGE:93==
 
83 
This positive reaction was also seen in other groups. At the same time, the groups’ approach to 
deciding what method to choose varied. Group 9 had one participant who read the instructions 
and chose one of the methods without asking for the other participants’ input. However, in 
group 6 for example, one of the participants encouraged everyone to provide their input about 
what template to choose.  
Increasing trust 
A participant from workshop 16 said, after the workshop was finished, that automating the 
process was a “cool idea” because it made him feel more at ease and created an environment 
where he felt he could express his ideas more, without having someone judging him (a human 
facilitator). This participant highlighted that an automated facilitator would be a good asset for 
companies where hierarchy sometimes stops the creative process.   
A computer is not a great helper 
A participant from group 4 noted during the learnings section, that “If something goes wrong, 
it’s hard to fix it when it is only a computer and you cannot ask questions”. They were referring 
to the phase of the workshop with motivational interventions that facilitated voting, due to the 
fact that they were confused and unsure of how to proceed. 
 
6.6 Key takeaways  
The quantitative results from this chapter did not provide any evidence to validate the 
hypotheses from chapter 4. To explore the reasons behind this, other tests were performed, 
including validation of the Activity Engagement Questionnaire through factor analysis, video 
analysis of the workshops and an affinity diagram with the participants’ answers to an open 
question. Factor analysis did not provide any significant change in the validation of the 
hypotheses. However, the obtained qualitative data displayed interesting aspects that will be 
discussed further in the next chapter.

7. Discussion  
 
We started this thesis by asking the question “How can an automated facilitator software 
motivate employees to engage in sustainability topics in a workshop?”. This led to research 
about the effects of using motivational interventions to encourage engagement by an automated 
facilitator, as illustrated in Figure 35.  
 
Figure 35 Research model, how motivational interventions effect engagement 
 
After the literature review, a two-conditioned experiment, with two types of automated 
facilitated workshops, was conducted. The first type of workshop was with motivational 
interventions, based on elements of motivational methods from theories researched in the 
literature review. The second type of workshop included interventions designed to keep the 
engagement neutral.  
 
Quantitative data from the Activity Engagement Questionnaire and from creativity 
measurements was collected, followed by qualitative data obtained from an open question at 
the end of the workshops, and a video analysis. The data collected shows that both workshops 
had a high level of individual and group engagement. However, from the quantitative data, no 
significant difference in the engagement scores and in the creativity measurements between the 
two types of workshops was found. On the other hand, the qualitative data registered the 
existence of several team processes and elements from the input that affected, indicating that 
they could constitute some of the main causes of engagement. Taking this into account, we 
suggest a modified research model, presented in Figure 36

7.1 Engagement enablers  
 
The high engagement score in both workshops is an important finding in itself, since it shows 
that automated facilitators have the ability to engage the participants. From the qualitative 
analysis it seems that elements of the input and team processes were the main drivers in creating 
a high level of engagement. It would even seem that these factors reduced the effect of the 
explicit motivational interventions through the facilitator. In the following, we will investigate 
further the elements that led to high engagement and how they have diminished the 
motivational interventions. In addition we will look into why the effect of the motivational 
interventions did not have the expected impact.  
 
It is important to point out that none of the elements mentioned below pertain to the design of 
the software application, since the SUS score was rated as acceptable, stating that the system 
provided satisfactory experience, usability and effectiveness. We therefore conclude that the 
effect of the user experience did not provide an overwhelming positive or negative effect. 
Coordination activities (Team processes)  
As stated in the result section, coordination activities among the participants, as shown in the 
video recordings, was one of the observed team processes. During coordination activities, team 
members orchestrated who and how to perform a task. They are part of the action processes 
Figure 36 Modified research model 
==PAGE:97==
 
87 
within the behavioural processes, and it contributes to high team effectiveness (Marks et al., 
2001, p 365). This process allowed the participants to cooperate between them and have a 
mutual understanding of the exercises. Because the concept of behavioural engagement 
includes the activities of asking questions, as well as interacting and having conversations 
about the activity (Trowler, Vicki, 2010), the presence of this process could be an indicator of 
behavioural engagement within the team. The same conclusion has been made by Torrente et. 
al (2012) which proposes that team coordination and teamwork lead to increased teamwork 
engagement. However, Torrente et. al also recognizes that the main element for engagement is 
motivation within teams.  
 
Team coordination was present every time participants from the groups needed to make sure 
they understood the exercises correctly. In the design of the automated facilitator, we did not 
take into account that not having a human facilitator to facilitate team coordination would 
increase team coordination among team members. However, we do not see any mention of this 
either in previous studies about automated facilitation (Limayem, M. and Dickson, G. W. 1993, 
Marriott et. al. 2016). 
 
Given that team coordination occurred during both types of workshops, it would seem that this 
in itself is an aspect that could have influenced the high engagement score. It is also a factor 
that could explain the reduced importance of the motivational elements in the facilitator. It 
would be interesting to investigate this question further, in particular how to balance the 
number of assigned tasks and challenges that demand team coordination over a period of time, 
without causing frustration and disengagement.  
Learning environment (Team processes) 
Eight participants mentioned in the last open question, that their favourite part of both 
workshops was the fact that they were able to discuss with their peers, about the content of the 
workshops and the tasks. This could be interpreted as a positive effect and enjoyment due to 
the learning environment, which is part of emotional engagement (Trowler, Vicki, 2010). 
Although this was not stated by all groups, it is interesting to note that some teams seem to 
have been more engaged because of a good learning environment. In fact, previously studied 
line team processes show that the input and composition of a team does have an effect on the 
team engagement (Marks et al., 2001, p 365). However, team composition is not mentioned as 
a motivational factor within the motivational theories studied for this thesis. When, in 
==PAGE:98==
 
88 
retrospect, we have looked into research from other fields about team composition and learning 
environment in relation to motivation and engagement, examples of the same relationship can 
be found. For instance a study made by Gomez, Wu and Passerini (2010) investigated team 
contribution and composition in relation to motivation within computer-supported team-based 
learning, and identify the same relationship as we see in this study. We therefore conclude that 
the learning environment is in itself a possible motivational factor in the automated facilitated.  
 
With this in mind, it should be possible for the automated facilitator to create a good learning 
environment in the earliest stages of the workshop, by facilitating introductions, staging team 
building exercises, among other features. However, the challenge of this intervention would be 
adopting these features according to the participants' personalities. This is where an automated 
facilitator has a disadvantage compared to a human facilitator, which has the possibility to 
tailor these types of exercises from the feeling in the room. It is an important factor to have in 
mind, as the selection of wrong team building exercises could create a poor quality learning 
environment.  
Interest in the subject (Input)  
Another factor which could have had an influence on the level of engagement is the participants 
interest in the subject of sustainability. As it was presented in the results, participants overall 
liked the content of the workshops (mean = 2.9/4). Considering that, being interested in the 
subject can lead to a high emotional engagement (Trowler, Vicki, 2010), this aspect could have 
been a motivational element which was present in both workshops. The relationship between 
interest, motivation and engagement is not shown as a motivational method researched for this 
study, however it is investigated in literature about learning methods. Here Flowerday and 
Shell (2015) confirms that situational interest does enhance engagement as well as learning.  
 
However, we would like to argue that an “interest in a subject” motivational intervention is not 
easy to apply as a standard element in the creation of workshops facilitated by an automated 
facilitator, because it is difficult to have a subject that tailors to everyone's interest. In a 
company setting, where the participation in the workshops are mandatory for the employees, it 
might very well be that the case that some participants are not interested in the content of the 
workshop. 
==PAGE:99==
 
89 
Duration of workshops (Input)  
Another factor that could have an influence on high overall engagement is the duration of the 
workshops, reducing the effect of the motivational interventions. Typical workshops last for 
longer than 10-15 min. However, due to the limitations of this study, it was not possible to test 
the influence of the duration of workshops. Nevertheless, it could be that the high level of 
engagement from the participants was due to their realization that their participation would be 
only for a short amount of time, avoiding tediousness. Derrick et. al. (2013) mention automated 
facilitation duration under further research, but in relation to satisfaction, and not in terms of 
the participants’ engagement, with the program. However, in studies of student engagement, 
the common agreement is that the attention span for students is 10-15 min (Bradbury, 2016); 
the duration of the workshops in our experiment could thus have contributed to the high level 
of engagement in both workshops.   
Effect of the motivational interventions 
The results show that there was no significant difference in the engagement scores between the 
workshops with motivational interventions and the ones with neutral interventions. This means 
that we are not able to document an increase in engagement as an effect of the added 
motivational interventions. As shown in Figure 36 the link between team processes, 
motivational interventions and engagement is therefore not the main enabler. In the previous 
section we discussed that a reason for this could be that they were diminished because of the 
high engagement from other processes. Nevertheless, another aspect that needs further 
exploration is why the conversion of the elements from motivational theory into motivational 
interventions in an automated facilitator did not achieve the same effect. 
 
Three of the motivational interventions were mentioned and discussed by the participants 
during the workshops or in the open question section of the questionnaire (“Gives options on 
how to brainstorm”, “Give a template”, “Voting or negotiation”). There were, however other 
motivational interventions that did not seem to provide any reaction from the participants: 
“Specify goal”, “Ask helping questions”, “Common vision” and “Reflect on learning”. The 
question is why these did not solicit any responses.  
 
The intervention “specify goal” and “reflect on learning” were converted from elements of the 
same motivational theory-based methodology; self-determination theory within leadership. 
==PAGE:100==
 
90 
This theory states that self-determination strengthens intrinsic motivation. More specifically, 
specify goal fosters autonomous motivation and reflect on learning fosters competence 
motivation (Kovjanic et al., 2012). However, it does not seem to have this effect in our 
experiment. A reason could be that the motivational elements, which Kovjanic et. al (2012) 
suggested, are only motivational under the circumstances presented in this particular setting 
(employees within the sectors service, education and health care studied). Unfortunately, this 
hypothesis cannot be confirmed, as studies with unsuccessful results are often not published. 
 
Likewise, the interventions “Asking helping questions” and “Common vision”,  based on the 
methods growth mindset (Dweck, 2006) and OBCE (Boiral, O. and Paillé, P. 2012) also 
seemed to have little or no effect. It could be that these types of interventions can only be used 
over a longer time span.  
 
Another reason could be that these elements do not work alone, and that they are more effective 
when applied with the rest of the elements from the motivational methods. The logic behind 
the selection of elements in our design process, was that some of the elements could not be 
applied in an automated facilitator software, at least not in the given time-span. For example, 
the element “Build on employees strengths and develop action plans to improve the problem 
areas” from the leadership theory goes beyond the capabilities of this study. Because of these 
potential limitations the effects of the interventions “specify goal”, “reflect on learning”, 
“asking helping questions” and “common vision” might not have been sufficient.  
 
Key learnings 
The combination of team coordination, learning environment, interest and duration could have 
had an influence on the high level of engagement as shown in Figure 37. In addition, the high 
level of engagement fostered by these factors seem to have reduced the effects of the 
motivational interventions. In the design of the workshop none of the mentioned aspects was 
foreseen as we did not encounter this in the literature about automated facilitation design. One 
==PAGE:101==
 
91 
could therefore argue that the 
current studies on automated 
facilitation does not provide a 
complete 
picture 
of 
the 
processes happening within 
facilitated 
workshops. 
However, in retrospect, we 
would say that the mentioned 
elements are important to 
enable 
engagement, 
as 
is 
shown by research in other fields of teams, student engagement and learning.  
 
From what has been said above, we can identify three key learnings. First learning comes from 
the insights during the team processes in a workshop facilitated by an automated facilitator. 
This can be particularly relevant for further studies and designs of automated facilitation, a 
shortcoming of earlier studies. The second lesson is that engagement can be created through 
some team activities, which are relatively more important than other motivational methods. 
The third lesson is that further studies focusing on automated facilitation would be more 
relevant if research included, among other things, student engagement and learning theory.    
 
7.2 Methodological problems  
 
In this section we will discuss some factors and limitations derived from the process and the 
experiment design that might have played a role in the results obtained.  
Knowledge about the companies as subjects of the workshops 
From the video analysis and the comments in the affinity diagram, we see that the participant’s 
level of knowledge of the companies used in the experiments did seem to have marginable 
impact in the results. Participants that had higher knowledge about either Arla or Carlsberg 
were able to brainstorm more value points, as well as share information about the companies 
to the rest of the participants. We therefore suggest that this could have had an effect on the 
results, as it could be that familiarity with a subject, and knowledge sharing, led to team trust 
and group cohesion, which in turn resulted in engagement. Nevertheless, the effect of this in 
the experiments was minimal, as it was expressed only by a few groups. Still, the reason why 
Figure 37 Modified research model with engagement enablers 
==PAGE:102==
 
92 
this factor was not predicted during the content design of the automated facilitator, is because 
it was assumed that both companies were equally known in Denmark. 
 
Confusion about the voting intervention 
One issue encountered during the experiment was the frustration generated by the voting 
exercise. More than half of the teams expressed confusion about how to proceed, demonstrating 
that perhaps instructions were not clearly formulated. It seems that this might have affected the 
results, as the question “I felt frustrated or annoyed” had a high score (2.85) for the workshops 
with motivational interventions. Yet, removing this question from the statistical comparison 
test does not change the results significantly. And even though it might have affected affective 
engagement, it is unlikely that it had an influence on the other dimensions. An interesting 
observation made during this exercise, by one of the participants from group 4, was that having 
a computer as a facilitator is not helpful, when there are doubts or questions that cannot be 
answered by it. This outcome made us realize instructions have to be refined, and that a help 
function in every section could regulate this issue.  
Background of students  
The automated facilitator was designed for Incept Sustainability, to be used in companies. 
However, due to limitations of resources (time and availability), the experiment was run with 
students. Even though the students were asked to pretend they were part of the case study 
company (Arla or Carlsberg), it could be the case that the interventions from leadership theory 
only work when there is a sense of belonging and identification and a high knowledge of the 
company. None of the researched literature stated these as a prerequisite. 
 
On the contrary, input in the IMOI model suggests that factors like openness, resources 
systems, coordination and top management have effects on teams working in companies. There 
is the possibility that these factors, particularly to employees, might have an effect that cannot 
be perceived in an experiment design with students, and therefore affect engagement levels. 
On the other hand, as stated in the results by a participant from group 16, the substitution of a 
human facilitator by a automated facilitator might increase the trust of the participants, 
considering that there is no one that might “judge” their comments. This particular participant 
felt he could be more open with his peers and generate better ideas. His thought was that this 
is an aspect that could be positive for companies, as some company cultures have a pronounced 
==PAGE:103==
 
93 
hierarchical structure influencing negatively on the factors mentioned above. An automated 
facilitator would allow employees that are on the same level to have a better chance to be open 
and creative. This is an interesting thought that could be studied further. 
Learning effect  
Even though we did account for the learning effect, there seems to be a skill transfer from the 
first to the second workshop in several groups. Results from the video analysis and figure 29 
shows that team cohesion seems to be higher in the second workshop. As stated in the literature 
review, team cohesion refers to the members liking of the group, ideologies that the group 
represent and the degree to which each task allows to obtain a goal (Kozlowski and R. Ilgen, 
2018, p 88), (Salas, Rico, Passmore, 2017, p 248), (Mathieu et al., 2008). High team cohesion 
could be linked to affective engagement, as the participants could have an enjoyable learning 
environment. Furthermore, the second workshop seemed also to show that transition processes 
increased as well. Transition processes are linked to strategizing and the pursuit or more ideas, 
which could be translated into seeking to go beyond requirements, thus as part of cognitive 
engagement. This is something that we did not initially take into account. However for further 
work, a way to offset learning effects is by having a pre-workshop session for the participants 
to get used to the program and each other (MacKenzie, 2013).  
 
Validity of Activity Engagement Questionnaire 
After obtaining the results from the tests, a factor analysis was performed with the intention of 
validating the Activity Engagement Questionnaire. This analysis showed some inconsistencies. 
On the behaviour dimension, it can be seen that questions 11,14,15,17 were not correlated with 
the rest. Further analysis revealed that some possible reasons for this are related to our 
adaptation of the questions to the context of the experiment. For example, the original question 
“I asked questions to an adult” was changed to “I asked questions/used the help function of the 
automated facilitator” which could have changed the underlying meaning. Even so, removing 
these questions did not change the results significantly and furthermore, the cronbach’s alpha 
still showed a low value (0.590). This can also be seen in the cognitive dimension. Question 
16 showed low correlation with the rest of the items. However, removing this question did not 
change the obtained results or increased the cronbach’s alpha in significant terms (0.482). 
These numbers would seem to reflect a low reliability of the questionnaire for measuring 
behaviour and cognitive engagement, questioning the validity of this evaluation instrument, 
==PAGE:104==
 
94 
even though the original study from Ben-Eliyahu et al. (2018) did not provide any conditions 
for using it. 
Summary on validity of the study  
In conclusion, even though, during the design process, many aspects that could affect the 
experiment were considered, there are several factors that could have influenced the results of 
engagement, such as the selection of the evaluation tool to measure engagement. 
 
7.3 Further work 
 
As the study is now, investigating the engagement level for each intervention through coding 
might prove useful in order to show if any of the specific motivational interventions had an 
effect. This could also be an argument for running the same type of experiment, but revised 
with insights gained from this study. In addition, other things might be relevant to measure, 
such as excitement. 
Themes for further investigation  
For future research within the field of engaging automated facilitators, we see a number of 
interesting topics to be explored further.  
Interventions for motivation  
Further research should investigate if the motivational interventions would have had an effect 
on a non-engaged group of participants. Testing and validating such a proposition could give 
a better indication and understanding of and to what degree the motivational interventions do 
work. This would require a different test set up where the participants were part of a longer 
workshop and the motivational intervention are introduced after the 10-15 min of attention 
span. Moreover, other interventions could be tested to see if the non-significant difference in 
this study was specific to the chosen interventions. 
Short term vs. long term engagement  
One limitation of this study was that, it was only possible to measure short term engagement. 
However, for Incept Sustainability, both short term engagement and long term engagement 
are important. Long term engagement would allow employees to use what they have learned 
in the workshop at their work.  
==PAGE:105==
 
95 
It will remain unclear to which degree the participants are engaged in the topic of sustainable 
value creation after the workshop. It would therefore be interesting to investigate long term 
engagement of an automated facilitated workshop.  
Human vs. automated facilitator  
In line with previous studies which have studied automated facilitators vs. human facilitators 
and found the former to be effective, it would be interesting to investigate if an automated 
facilitator could be as engaging as a human facilitator. Of course, much depends on how the 
automated facilitator is designed and for what purpose it is used. On the other hand, a human 
facilitator does not guarantee that the teaching and coaching is successful; it also depends on 
the competence and the pedagogical capacity of the person(s) involved. Our study seems to 
indicate, however, that an automatic facilitator can strengthen the awareness and engagement 
in questions of sustainability. It also has the advantage that it is cost-effective, compared to 
employing teachers/coaches to impart the messages and to encourage activities. Finally, 
through individual devices like portable computers, tablets and mobile devices, the automatic 
facilitator, correctly designed and tested, can be used by all employees at their convenience, 
rather than only during scheduled meetings, workshops and reunions.

8. Conclusion 
 
 
The aim of this thesis was to answer the question “How can an automated facilitator software 
motivate employees to engage in sustainable topics in a workshop?” This led us to investigate 
how teams operate and to elaborate a model and a methodology linking team processes with 
motivation and engagement. The motivational interventions used were derived from Human-
Computer interaction, sustainability and leadership. With this in mind, we hypothesised that 
the application of motivational interventions through an automated facilitator would have a 
positive effect in the behavioural, cognitive and affective mechanisms of a team to create 
behavioural, cognitive, affective and overall engagement in a workshop about sustainable 
topics, as well as foster creativity. 
After an extensive review of existing research, followed by methodological design and 
development process in collaboration with Incept Sustainability, an automated facilitator 
prototype with two contrasting versions was constructed. One version contained motivational 
interventions and another version, neutral interventions. A within-subject experiment was then 
carried out to test initial hypotheses. The results of the experiment did not provide statistically 
significant evidence to validate them. At the same time, however, high engagement scores were 
found in both workshops. The measured qualitative data also demonstrated that there was a 
motivational/engaging effect in different team processes in both workshops, such as team 
cohesion, coordination activities, mental models, transition processes and team learning. 
Our study contributes to research in two ways. First, it assesses the evaluation tool “Activity 
Engagement Questionnaire” that measures engagement, using university students as 
participants. Secondly, it shows elements which does not  seem to work as well as expected in 
fostering motivation to create engagement and stimulate creativity in teams. It also describes 
several factors that should be further investigated to determine their importance for 
engagement. 
Despite the inconclusive results from the quantitative data, it seems reasonable to conclude that 
the developed automated facilitator prototype can be used as a foundation for Incept 
Sustainability products, given that it creates high engagement. This study also provides a list 
of motivational methods that can be applied and tested using their existing material.
