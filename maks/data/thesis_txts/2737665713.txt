Abstract
Heat pumps integrated into district heating networks are an interesting system for reach-
ing net zero emissions, being a low cost and emissions alternative compared to other
technologies.
This project addresses the optimization of a two-stage heat pump system for district heat-
ing with the of minimizing operational costs and CO2 emissions. The system comprises
two heat pumps: Bottom heat pump and HP2 Top heat pump. Bottom heat pump extracts
heat from underground mines, feeding a underground coal mine that works as a thermal
energy storage, which subsequently supplies the Top heat pump.
The modelling of the heat pumps is simulated with EES (Engineering Equation Solver)
and the optimization process was conducted using Pyomo, focusing on two main objec-
tives: minimizing the electricity costs reducing CO2 emissions.
The first model, the Danish case, electricity price and carbon intensity change hourly. It
aims to evaluate the best system configuration in function of the operational or environ-
mental point of view, with the support of the Marginal Abatement Cost Curve (MACC).
The second model, the Scottish case, where electricity prices change only seasonally but
carbon intensity changes hourly, the focus shifted on the minimization of CO2 emissions.
Different configurations of the system are compared to balance the trade-offs between
the best economical and the lowest emissions configuration.
The results indicate that the optimal system configurations are similar between the Dan-
ish and Scottish cases differentiating in electricity pricing. The Danish case achieved the
best economic and environmental performance with the specified sizing and depth, while
the Scottish case highlighted the importance of stable electricity prices in optimizing for
emissions. This study provides a comprehensive framework for optimizing heat pump
systems in district heating networks, emphasizing both economic viability and environ-
mental sustainability.

Introduction
Human activities, through the greenhouse gasses emissions, have undeniably initiate the
phenomenon of global warming, evidenced by the increase in global surface temperature
by 1,1 °C above the levels pre-industrial period in years 2011 to 2020 [1]. The esca-
lation greenhouse gas emissions are persisting due to unsustainable energy utilization,
alterations in land use, as well as prevailing lifestyles and consumption habits. The im-
plications of anthropogenic climate change are already manifesting in the form of height-
ened frequency and intensity of extreme climate events worldwide. Such changes have
precipitated widespread adverse consequences, compromising food and water security,
deteriorating health conditions, and substantial economic and societal setbacks, with con-
sequential losses and to both natural ecosystems and communities. Particularly marginal-
ized populations with minimal responsibility for the current state of climate change, are
disproportionately suffering the effects of these impacts. This will result in the migration of
143 million people by 2050, primarily from South Asia, Latin America, and sub-Saharan
Africa, as they search for more habitable lands [1].

Current World Energy situation

energy markets and the global economy, risking for a further disorder. Even though fossil

ing conflict in Ukraine and the Middle East [5]. Furtherly, the macro-economic outlook is
uncertain, marked by inflation, with high borrowing costs and debt levels.
The world is facing environmental challenges too, with the global average surface tem-

2.1
District heating
District heating refers to the system used for supplying heat to buildings via a network
that delivers thermal energy. This system utilizes a power plant, solar thermal or geother-
mal installation, or a large heat pump to heat water. The heated water is then circulated
through a network of insulated, typically buried underground pipes directly into the build-
ings that utilize this heat.
Figure 2.1: District heating network working principle [13]
Inside these buildings, the water passes through a handover station into the building’s
own heat distribution system, providing both space heating and hot water. Once the wa-
ter cools, it returns to the original heat source to be reheated, creating a continuous cycle.

Thermal Energy Storage
Thermal energy storage represents the temporary containment of thermal energy, either
at higher or reduced temperatures. This concept has been harnessed and refined over
centuries, becoming a pivotal element in multiple applications. The extraction of stored
thermal energy is optimized by efficient systems, ensuring minimal losses and achieving
substantial energy recovery, while maintaining temperature integrity.
These innovative thermal energy storage systems possess the potential to enhance the
efficiency of thermal equipment and grid flexibility, serving as a central solution to over-
come the gap between the availability and heat demand of thermal energy [32]. Well
designed systems not only mitigate initial and maintenance costs but also increase over-
all energy efficiency. By acting as an energy reservoir, these storage systems play a
significant role in meeting society’s increasing demand for environmentally friendly prac-
tices in building space heating and cooling, hot water, power and utility applications.
These storage systems can serve as an energy sink, ready to absorb any surplus energy
not required in the immediate moment. Such storage might not be for smaller residences,
it is suited for large-scale central heating systems. One notable limitation lies in its cyclic,
time-dependent nature, posing a challenge to its extensive applications. This points out
the critical importance of energy storage in ensuring the success of intermittent energy
sources in meeting continuous demand [33].

2.4
Heat extraction
There are two primary methodologies employed for the extraction of heat from the ground
or alternative heat sources, each offering distinct advantages and considerations as elu-
cidated in the following paragraphs [37]:
• Open-loop system: The open-loop system entails the pumping of groundwater to the
Earth’s surface, where it traverses a heat exchanger. Once the heat exchange pro-
cess is complete, the water is directed back to its origin or conveyed to another water
body, such as surface water, a separate set of mine workings linked to the extraction
working, or a designated watercourse. However, compliance with stringent environ-
mental regulations necessitates the implementation of a comprehensive cleaning
and purification process for the discharged water. This adherence to environmental
standards involves acquiring the requisite permits, licenses, and meeting treatment
requirements. It is important to note that the maximum heat supply is subject on the
highest sustainable abstraction rate. The system’s efficiency is closely tied to the
speed at which heat can be supplied to the surface heat exchanger, emphasizing a
direct correlation between energy extraction and heat delivery speed.
• Closed-loop system: In contrast to the open-loop system, the closed-loop configu-
ration involves siting the heat exchanger directly within a borehole. Heat transfer
within the closed-loop system occurs through a circulating media, and this heat-
laden media is subsequently conveyed to the surface. An essential distinction in
this approach is that the circulating media and groundwater remain isolated from
each other throughout the heat exchange process.
The closed-loop system is the most commonly used choice for many applications.
Groundwater, commonly characterized by impurities, presents a potential threat to
the integrity of piping and overall system robustness. This intrinsic drawback in the
open-loop system compromises network efficiency. In contrast, closed-loop sys-
tems offer enhanced reliability and performance, making them the preferred solu-
tion in scenarios where the purity of the circulating media is crucial for sustained
and efficient heat exchange operations.

2.7
Marginal Abatement Cost Curve
A Marginal Abatement Cost Curve (MACC) is a graphical representation that illustrates
the cost and potential of various actions for emissions reduction, typically of greenhouse
gases like CO2. The horizontal axis of the MACC shows the amount of emissions reduc-
tion, or abatement potential, that each technology or measure can achieve. The vertical
axis displays the marginal cost of reducing one additional unit of emissions using each
technology, as show in Figure 2.13.
Figure 2.13: Marginal Abatement Cost Curve [48]
Each bar on the curve represents a specific technology or measure, where the width
of the bar indicates the total abatement potential and the height represents its marginal
cost. Technologies that save money, appear below the x-axis, while those that require a
monetary cost are presented above it. The bars are generally ordered from the lowest to
the highest marginal cost, illustrating which measures are the most cost-effective.
MACCs are good measure for identifying and comparing the cost-effectiveness of different
emission reduction options. They assist the stakeholders in understanding which are
the most suitable measures for the most abatement for the least cost, thus prioritizing
investments in emission reduction strategies [49].

Marginal Abatement Cost curve: operation of the system
After having examined the Danish and Scottish cases, the final part of the Results aims

concept is applied to the Danish case, which has variable electricity prices and variable
carbon intensity values.
The factor Z is introduced, as explained in Section 3.2.3, which is a scalar that shifts the
focus from a completely operational point of view, with the values of 0, to an environmen-
tal point of view, increasing its value.
The configuration chosen is with the Bottom heat pump size ˙QHP,1 = 0, 45 ¨ ˙QHP,2 and a
depth of 1000 meters, as it has the lowest LCOH.
In Figure 4.47, it can be clearly seen what happens when the factor Z is varied: the oper-
ational cost increases and emissions of CO2 decrease. The abatement of emissions has
a constant negative gradient for the Z value from 0 to 5, then from 5 to 10, the slopes de-
crease, while from 10 onward, the pollutant emissions remain constant, since the system
is not able to decrease them any further.
Figure 4.47: Tons of CO2 emitted vs Electricity consumption cost

converges, they are 1917 tons, which is 100 tons less than the worst case.

factor Z. However, after the value of Z = 100, it converges, meaning that no more money
can be spent to achieve lower emissions.

Discussion
From the examination of the results, the following discussion can be conducted:
In the Danish case, the final configuration is chosen with the bottom heat pump sized at
the lowest feasible capacity. This decision is primarily influenced by the total cost of the
system, with the investment in the heat pump playing a crucial role. The total initial cost
increases with the increase of the bottom heat pump capacity, while the costs of the other
components remain fixed. A larger bottom heat pump capacity can increase efficiency in
charging the TES, allowing it to operate when electricity prices are lower, yielding more
benefits. Consequently, the electricity consumption cost decreases as the bottom heat
pump capacity increases. However, the reduction in electricity consumption costs has a
lower impact compared to the investment costs, leading to the most convenient LCOH
being associated with the lower bottom heat pump capacity.
Regarding the depth of the abandoned coal mine shaft, the deepest possible option is
selected, 1000 meters deep. This choice is motivated by the fact that temperature in-
creases with depth, leading to a higher coefficient of performance (COP) of the Bottom
heat pump, leading to a substantial decrease in electricity consumption. Oppositely, the
underground Circulation pump is negatively affected by the longer piping, with the natural
consequence that its capacity needs to be greater and its electricity consumption as well.
Nevertheless, the Bottom heat pump electricity consumption cost has a way greater im-
pact on the overall cost for running the system compared to the Circulation pump related
costs.
The ultimate configuration of the system is determined to have a heat pump capacity of
HP1,capacity = 0, 45 ¨ HP2,capacity and an abandoned coal mine depth of 1000 meters, re-
sulting in a levelized cost of heat (LCOH) of 323 DKK/MWh, equivalent to 46 USD/MWh,
and 2021 tons of CO2 emitted yearly. Comparing this to literature [57], which suggests
typical LCOH values for ground source district heating systems ranging between 40 and
115 USD/MWh, validates the feasibility of this project.
On the other hand, the same analysis was conducted for the Scottish case, where the
optimization aim was minimizing the CO2 emissions. As it is evident the majority of the
total cost of the system is held by the electricity consumption expanses, being however
constant for all the Bottom heat pump capacities, due to the fact that the electricity price is
almost constant throughout the year. What changes with the heat pump size is the LCOH
and the tons of CO2 emitted.
In this case two configuration were taken into consideration. The first one with the configu-
ration with Bottom heat pump of the lowest LCOH, while the second one with less pollutant
emissions. Both configurations appear to give the best results, both from the economical
and the environmental point of view, when the heat is extracted from the deepest point,
1000 meters. The first configuration showed the lowest LCOH of 124 Pounds/MWh, equiv-
alent to 157 USD/MWh, is achived with the depth of 1000 meters, while the yearly tons
of CO2 emitted are 2466. Whereas the second configuration would bring the best LCOH
to be 156 Pounds/MWh, corresponding to 198 USD-MWh, for the depth of 1000 meters,
but with tons of CO2, 2139. From an economic point of view, according to [57], the LCOH
value is above the upper range, making the project not feasible.
This is due to the high electricity prices in the UK, which have increased drastically com-
pared to the pre-pandemic era.
Another aspect to consider is the marginal abatment cost from the operational perspec-
tive in the Danish case. It shows how moving from an operational saving to a diminishing

of CO2 emissions approach, requires few expanses in the first steps of minimizing pollu-
tants, but as the emissions are lowered further, the cost of abatement rises exponentially.
In numbers, the cost for abating the first 37 tons of CO2 is 5275 DKK, and the cost for
abating 102 tons is 196360 DKK. It can be noted that with a larger bottom heat pump
capacity, reducing CO2 emissions would be easier due to higher efficiency in delivering
heat during the most suitable periods. However, a smaller heat pump size was selected

It can be witnessed that the yearly CO2 emissions are lower in the Danish case compared
to the Scottish case in all the instances. This is due to a larger availability of environmen-

The study for both scenarios has been conducted using electricity prices for a specific
year. This explains why the Levelized Cost of Heat (LCOH) may vary significantly be-
tween the two cases. To provide a more comprehensive understanding of technology
feasibility, numerous simulations should be performed using different electricity prices
across various years, comparing them with different values of LCOH.
A more intricate sensitivity analysis could yield diverse outcomes in the simulations, par-
ticularly when varying the upper and lower temperatures of the Thermal Energy Storage
(TES) system for cold and warm periods.
In this study, a fixed size of the TES has been analyzed. The performance of the system,
from both economical and operational standpoints, would likely vary with different capac-
ities of abandoned coal mines, leading to results that would affect the feasibility of the
project. However, the optimal solution for the system is always the largest TES available
in the area, especially considering that its construction is free of charge.
Future works
While this project has provided insights regarding the optimization of geothermal heat
pump systems, there are various ideas for future research. Potential directions for further
investigation and improvement can be indicated from the findings of this thesis.
• Studying cases involving 5th generation district heating. This would imply lower
supply and return district heating temperatures, thereby increasing the system’s ef-
ficiency and reducing costs and thermal losses.
• Simulating the cases for different abandoned coal mine sizes that are present in the
area where the heat pump system will be installed.
• Undertaking an examination and forecast spanning the next decade, evaluating the
anticipated prediction of electricity prices up to 2035 alongside the comparison with
the forecasted Levelized Cost of Heat (LCOH) for relevant technologies.
• Introducing MPC (Model Predictive Control). This control strategy is applied in var-
ious engineering fields, utilizing feedback control to update predictions based on
experience. These tools predict future system behavior based on the current state
of the system.
• Creating a digital twin of the heat pump system to analyze its behavior under varying
environmental conditions
• Integrating LCA (Life Cycle Assessment). Future work could evaluate the environ-
mental impact of the manufacturing, installation, and operation of the system, iden-
tifying opportunities for their reduction.

Conclusions
In conclusion, the thesis project presented a comprehensive understanding of the tech-
nologies utilized for the studied system, such as the heat pump, thermal energy storage,
and the various generations of district heating networks, along with their associated con-
siderations.
The major questions regarding the system configurations have been addressed.
The purpose of the model was to optimize the operation of the two-stage heat pump sys-
tem integrated into a district heating network, that utilizes abandoned coal mines present
in the area as a heat source and and thermal energy storage. Various parameters are
optimized, such as operational costs for running the system and the minimization of CO2
emissions it produces. These simulations were performed for selecting the best capacity
of the first heat pump, the Bottom heat pump, and the optimal depth of the heat source that
gives the best value from the economic or environmental point of view. These differences
were primarily influenced by electricity consumption costs and the CAPEX of the Bottom
heat pump, that was the parameter, along with the Circulation pump, that was varied, with
the depth of the source positively impacting all cases. This contributed to lower emissions
and consumption of the heat pump directly connected to the source. Thus, extracting a
higher temperature from the ground brings more advantages than the costs related to the
more expensive water circulation pump, due to the fact that the consumption of the heat
pump is way greater than the consumption of the circulation pump.
Two major cases have been analyzed: the Danish case, where the electricity price varies
hourly, leading the optimization model to minimize the operational cost of the system, and
the Scottish case, where the optimization focuses on reducing CO2 emissions due to sea-
sonal changes in electricity consumption costs. The conclusions and final configurations
for the two cases are as follows:
• Danish case: The best configuration, selected for its major economic advantages,
features a Bottom heat pump capacity of HP1,capacity = 0, 45 ¨ HP2,capacity and
a depth of 1000 meters for heat extraction.
This system has an LCOH of 323
DKK/MWh, equivalent to 46 USD/MWh, and emits 2021 tons of CO2 yearly.
• Scottish case: Two configurations are proposed, both with the best results for the
energy extraction point at 1000 meters deep. The first, optimal from an economic
standpoint, has an LCOH of 124 Pounds/MWh (157 USD/MWh) and emits 2466 tons
of CO2 pollutants annually. The second configuration, aimed at reducing emissions,
has an LCOH of 156 Pounds/MWh (198 USD/MWh) with yearly emissions of 2139
tons of CO2.
In conclusion, the advantages and the functioning of this system are as follows:
• These kinds of technologies underline the significant capacity of TES to act as a
moderator from both a cost-saving and environmental point of view. Energy can be
stored during periods when electricity prices are low or carbon intensity is reduced.
• Significant effort must be applied both in the phase of designing the system and in
its operation, whether the objective is minimizing operational cost or pollutant emis-
sions. In the studied system, the design phase is crucial. For example, the Scottish
case highlights how the capacity of the bottom heat pump can influence system be-
havior. If the design focus shifts from an economic to an environmental perspective,
the LCOH increases by 21% and 25%, depending on the heat extraction depth, while
