Abstract
The planet Mars features multiple landform types that are believed to hold large quantities of water­
ice deposits. These deposits are identified with the aid of sounding radar measurements from
satellite missions in orbit around Mars, such as the SHARAD radar.
This thesis attempts to build upon the findings and experiences of the [Gupta et al., 2020] case
study, but presents a new approach for the segmentation of water­ice deposits. The landform types
are segmented with an implemented convolutional neural network named U­Net. Moreover, a
strategy for large­scale data localization, acquisition, preparation and annotation is carried out to
manually prepare a data set from scratch to train the neural network.
The relevant glaciology, radar, and deep learning terminology are described in sufficient detail in
the theoretical and method sections. The two sections also describe and argue for all the choices
made regarding the different fields and methods presented, and address any doubts that occurred
during the decision­making process.
The results section presents the best and worst predictions of the network and explains their re­
spective performances. Overall, the network performs well based on the evaluation metrics used
to assess the neural network’s performance. The findings are then discussed in detail in the dis­
cussion section.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The planet Mars, also known as ”the red planet”, has always been of interest to humankind. Under
the right conditions, Mars is visible to the naked eye and becomes the third brightest object in the
night sky, only surpassed by Venus and the moon. For this reason, many superstitions and stories
were made up about its presence. The Greeks named it ”Ares”, and the Romans called it ”Mars”,
after their respective gods of war. More than a thousand years later, in 1609, the red planet was
observed telescopically by the Italian scientist Galileo Galilei, and for the following 400 years, the
scientific interest and study of the planet only grew. However, it is only recently that humans have
begun to seriously consider it as a potential second home for humanity [Snyder, 2002].
Building a human colony on the surface of Mars will prove to be a challenging task, especially
in terms of logistics. Humans need a stable and reliable source of nutrition to survive in terms
of food and, more importantly, water. Hence, if any of the aforementioned logistical problems
can be alleviated in any way, it would significantly ease the problem of building and sustaining
a human colony. Moreover, based on recent satellite observations and scientific studies of the
surface of Mars, it seems that not only does Mars still contain reservoirs of water, it also contains
significantly large and relatively accessible volumes of it [Holt et al., 2008]. Most of it is lying just

Concentric Crater Fill (CCF), the Lineated Valley Fill (LVF) and finally, the Lobate Debris Apron

This thesis aims to document the process of implementing an automatic segmentation strategy to
map the reservoirs of water­ice stored within the LDA type of landform, which is described in
detail later in the report. This is done with the aid of deep learning techniques and sounding radar
measurements from the Mars SHAllow RADar sounder (SHARAD) radar. The deep learning
approach is carried out with the use of a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) named U­Net, to
be able to automatically identify the water­ice deposits and distinguish them from other signals in
the subsurface.
1.2
Ice Sounding Radars
Before a human colony can be established on Mars, an estimation of the water reservoirs must be
made with the tools that are currently available. Considering the current inaccessibility of Mars,
the usual ice investigation methods on Earth, such as ice drilling, ice core analysis, borehole log­
ging, etc., are not possible because those would require in­situ measurements by humans on the
surface of the planet. This motivates the scientific community to make use of remote sensing tech­
niques 1 instead, which are made by payload systems onboard the satellites that are in orbit around
the planet. One of the best payloads currently available for this objective is the sounding radar
system named SHARAD, onboard the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter (MRO). A sounding radar is
a particular type of low­frequency radar operating in the lower MHz frequency region. The low
frequency, which inversely gives a high wavelength, allows the radar to penetrate thousands of
meters beneath the surface. Higher frequencies would not be usable because the absorption of
radar pulses becomes very strong as the frequency is raised, and scattering from inhomogeneities
found in the ice is also increased proportionally. This type of ice sounding from a space­based
radar is rather unique, as implementing such a system would be far more difficult on satellites in

In ice sounding radars, it is important to account for the effects of surface clutter. The term ’clutter’
refers to undesired echoes received in the radar system, i.e. real, physical targets that are not of
interest for the objective at hand. Clutter signals have special implications in ice sounders due
to how the radar wave propagates through the subsurface. In Figure 1.1 it can be seen that the
nadir­part of the radar wave interacts with the surface and starts penetrating through some medium
(ice or otherwise), but then moves at a slower rate, based on the properties of that medium. This
means that the central part of the radar wave is now propagating slower than the outer edges of the
radar wave, which are still propagating through air. This can be seen by the flattened out curve
in the subsurface, as illustrated in Figure 1.1. The surface clutter 2 occurs when radar signals are
received from the Right­Handed Signal (RHS) or Left­Handed Signal (LHS) clutter sources at the
same time as the signal is received from the target subsurface reflector. The clutter signals risk
obscuring the desired signal for several reasons. Most importantly, the obscurities occur because
the subsurface reflection signal has been greatly attenuated during its two­way propagation through
the subsurface, while the surface clutter has much less or close to no attenuation. The difference
of intensity between a signal from a deep subsurface reflection, and a signal originating from the
surface, is on the order of 100 dBs. This is comparable to the light intensity between the sun and a
candle light. For this reason, it is essential to employ clutter suppression techniques in ice sounding
radar data. Clutter suppression techniques for ice sounders will not be elaborated upon further in
this thesis, however, as the radargrams used have already been processed by an internal processor.
This is elaborated upon further in section 3.1. However, the interested reader may refer to [U.
Nielsen, 2011] for further details.

1.5
Problem Formulation
This thesis attempts to build on the experiences and approach of the [Gupta et al., 2020] study
for a new annotation and segmentation strategy. Unlike the study mentioned above, the thesis
is focusing on the annotation of entire LDAs, instead of only the subsurface discontinuities that
separate the LDA from the bedrock below. The LDAs are annotated based on all of the pixels in the
region between the surface reflection and subsurface discontinuity. The subsurface discontinuity
is also known as the subsurface reflection boundary or subsurface reflector, and that will be its
naming convention for the remainder of this thesis.
As a starting point, a similar preprocessing approach as the one that was done in the [Gupta et
al., 2020] study is carried out to build upon a working pipeline. However, the thesis has some no­
table differences, and new strategies are implemented regarding data preparation, the deep learning
approach, data acquisition, and much more. The predicted results are also expanded with new in­
formation, such as measurements of the ground truth and predicted LDA thicknesses, based on the
top­to­bottom pixel distance4, and more.
It should be noted that the approach of this thesis has some advantages, but also some shortcom­
ings, compared to the [Gupta et al., 2020] study. The main advantage is that the proposed approach
annotates a much larger amount of pixels, significantly enlarging the amount of data that the neural
network has to work with and thus reducing the class imbalance problem. This makes the network
more robust and makes it able to segment LDAs with improved performance. The main shortcom­
ing is that the network has a higher risk of segmenting ambiguous subsurface reflectors. This is
problematic if the overall thesis objective is to create a model for 3D­reconstruction of the Martian
subsurface, as it was in the [Gupta et al., 2020] study. However, since the main objective of this
thesis is to identify LDAs, the issues related to this shortcoming are largely negligible.
Being able to segment geological landforms with sufficient accuracy automatically will be a con­
siderable aid in the mapping efforts on Mars, especially for the three types of landforms that contain
water­ice deposits. The technique has the potential to be automated, and in this way, segment geo­
logical features, such as LDAs, every time a radar track is acquired from SHARAD. Furthermore,
it can be expanded to look for other geological features on Mars or even on other extraterrestrial
objects in the future. Having such an automated mapping strategy in place will provide numerous
benefits for the scientific community. One of the benefits is that the time needed for manual inter­
pretation of geological features for scientists is reduced dramatically. Another notable benefit is
that neural networks will reduce the risk of overlooking geological features of scientific interest,
which can easily occur when large amounts of data are examined visually by human eyes. Finally,
the technique provides the means for the automated processing and interpretation of the enormous
amounts of data measured by remote sensors daily, which would not be feasible to cover manually
by scientists.

2.1.1
SAR processing techniques
After enough pulses have illuminated a target to construct an image, it is still of little scientific
use before it has been appropriately postprocessed. The reason for this is that the signal energy
captured in the SAR data is spread out in both the range and azimuth direction. In range, it is
spread out for the duration of the linear frequency modulated transmitted pulse. In azimuth, it is
spread out for the equivalent time the synthetic aperture has illuminated it. An illustration of this
occurrence is illustrated in Figure 2.3, which shows a raw SAR image of a point reflector before
compression. The most common postprocessing method for SAR pulse compression is named the
Range­Doppler algorithm. In short, the Range­Doppler algorithm compresses the unfocused raw
SAR data in both range and azimuth direction, with the use of Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)s, while
preserving the phase information. This allows it to transform the unfocused SAR data into focused
images. This algorithm will now briefly be elaborated upon, but the mathematical equations and
signal theory background are omitted for the sake of the report’s scope.
Figure 2.3 – Raw, uncompressed SAR image featuring a point target [Gundorph et al., 2020].
The Range Compression part is carried out first. Each pulse response captured by the SAR is
compressed individually, as the azimuth position of each signal varies. By using a technique known
as matched filtering, which maximizes the output peak­signal­to­mean­noise ratio, the pulses are
compressed in the frequency domain with an FFT operation to each individual range slice. This
gives a unique filter response for each pulse. After range compression, the point­target will look
like the left part of Figure 2.4. Notice the much smaller amount of rows in this figure. This is due
to a ”throw­away” or ”wrap­around” region that occurs due to the cyclical fashion of the FFT, and
is removed during the compression process.
After Range Compression, it is possible to see targets shifting in range over the length of the syn­
thetic aperture due to range delays to a target being larger than the range sample spacing over
several range bins. As a target is being illuminated by the synthetic aperture, the range and corre­
sponding phase to the target changes over time. This phenomenon is known as squinting and is seen
most often for targets that are far away from the receiver, such as in space­based radar applications

2.2
Glaciology
The main problem formulation of this thesis is the localization of Lobate Debris Apron (LDA)s
for annotation and the detection of LDAs in the neural network. Hence it is essential to have some
geophysical insight into what LDAs look like in a radargram and cluttergram, and also to be able
to distinguish between LDAs and other discontinuities in the Martian subsurface.
LDAs were first discovered by the Viking orbiter satellites from the Viking program (1975­1982)
and were documented to characterize the mid­latitude bands of Mars. Since then, it has been hy­
pothesized that the flow­like characteristics of LDAs suggests a presence of ice. While the origin
of these LDAs is still a subject of an ongoing investigation, strong evidence suggests that Mars
was characterized by large fluctuations in its spin­axis/orbital elements (eccentricity, precession
and obliquity) in its past history [Laskar et al., 2002]. These fluctuations may have been account­
able for a redistribution of the water contained in its polar deposits to lower latitudes, which led
to the creation of ice ages. The LDAs and the other landform types containing water­ice deposits
are believed to be the remnants of these ice ages [Head et al., 2010]. It is important to note, how­
ever, that LDAs only occur at mid­range latitudes. In the equatorial regions of Mars, many steep
slopes, such as massifs (a principal mountain mass) and channel walls, may exhibit taluses, which
is an accumulation of rock fragments found at the base of the massif, that have been transported
downslope either by flowing water, frost­induced surface creep, or by falling off the top. In the

2.3.1
The concept of a neural network
In the present subsection, a very brief overview of the core concepts of a neural network is given,
but the underlying mathematical relations are omitted for the sake of the scope of the thesis.
An arbitrary Neural Network may look as illustrated in Figure 2.11
Figure 2.11 – Simple, neural network [M. A. Nielsen, 2015].
It consists of an input layer, an arbitrary number of hidden layers, and an output layer. Each
layer consists of a number of neurons2, which are connected to each other with a set of weights,
illustrated by the lines connecting the layers. Notice that each node in a subsequent hidden layer
receives input from all the nodes in the previous hidden layer. Networks that employ this strategy
are also known as dense networks.
In concept, the idea is to make a function f that takes a signal in the shape of a single vector x
as input, and transforms it through a series of layers known as hidden layers, until it outputs a
prediction vector y at the final layer, also known as the output layer. For classification problems,
this prediction vector will be a vector of class probabilities or scores. If there are k classes to be
classified, y will be a k­dimensional vector.
The structure of a neural network model consists of a forward model to get the model’s prediction,
followed by the backpropagation algorithm, whose purpose is to tune the model parameters to the
task at hand.
In the forward model of the neural network, the values of nodes between hidden layers are com­
puted with weighted linear combinations of the node values and their corresponding edge weights,

after which an activation function is applied. After the signal has propagated through the network,
the prediction vector y is output in the final layer.
When the prediction vector is acquired, the backpropagation algorithm compares the predictions
with some ground­truth values known beforehand, derived from the training set. By evaluating
how well the predictions match the targets, a loss function is computed, which is a measure of the
algorithms ”badness”. The purpose is to minimize this loss by iteratively adjusting the weights

Each iteration through the entire training data set, consisting of one forward­pass and one back­

optimization strategies available for a user of deep learning, and the choice depends entirely on the
problem at hand.
2.3.2
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
The Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is the current state­of­the­art type of neural network
for image classification and recognition. CNNs make use of several special conventions, which
are remarkably well­adapted for classifying images because they make the CNN fast to train and
also allow for the construction of deeper, multi­layered networks, which would otherwise not be
computationally possible.
A CNN is a type of neural network that takes advantage of the fact that the input comes in the shape
of images. Hence, neurons are arranged in three dimensions (width, height and depth), where depth
refers to the number of color channels in this case. A CNN consists of a sequence of many layers,
where each layer transforms an input 3D volume to an output 3D volume, throughout a series of
node activations and some differentiable function. This difference of layer activations can be seen
illustrated in Figure 2.12.
Figure 2.12 – Left: A regular, dense 3­layer neural network. Right: CNN. The red square is the image
of size (width,height,channels) which is being transformed to a 3D volume layer [Li et al., 2020].
The three most common type of CNN layers are the convolutional layer, the pooling layer and the
fully­connected layer.
The convolutional layer is the backbone of a CNN and is responsible for most of the heavy compu­
tations. It consists of a list of learnable filters, where each filter has a small spatial size but extends
fully throughout the depth of its input volume3. During the forward pass, each filter is convolved
across the height and width of its input volume, and dot products are computed between entries of
the filter and the inputs at any position. As this convolution is carried out, 2­dimensional activation
maps are created that gives responses of that specific filter at every spatial position in the image.
In other words, the learnable filters of the network activate when they encounter a type of visual
feature that it deems informative enough to characterize the image. This could, for example, be a
blotch of some color, an edge of some orientation, or entire features in deeper layers of the network.
At the output of the convolutional layer, a set of filters is obtained, where each of them contains an

dimension in the output volume. A good example of this can be seen illustrated in Figure 2.13 [Li
et al., 2020].
Figure 2.13 – Feature illustration of a convolutional neural network [Zeiler & Fergus, 2014].
An image of a yellow car is input into a CNN, and in the first representation, some low­level fea­

user might risk losing information that the algorithm would otherwise have discovered.
One of the key differences between a CNN and a dense neural network is the concept of local
connectivity. This means that each node in a subsequent hidden layer is only connected with a
small subset of input neurons from the previous hidden layer, known as the receptive field, or
the filter size, of the unit. The depth of the input volume is always equal to the extent of this
connectivity along the depth axis. The reason for using local connectivity in CNNs is that a dense
neural network would not scale well with full images due to the vast size the weight matrix would
have. Consider, for example, a relatively small RGB image of 200x200x3 size. This would lead to
200*200*3 = 120,000 weights for a single fully­connected node in the first hidden layer. Moreover,
considering more than one hidden layer is usually desired, this would quickly lead to an enormous
amount of weights that would both be computationally wasteful and also risk overfitting the data
[Li et al., 2020]. The way local connectivity works for a CNN is illustrated in Figure 2.14.

Another important concept of CNNs is that of parameter sharing, which is a scheme used to ensure
that the number of parameters in each layer does not grow exponentially. Parameter sharing is
based on the assumption that if a feature can be used to compute at one spatial position in an image,
it can also compute at another spatial position in that image. For example, If a specific layer has
an output volume of 55x55x96, and the connected input volume has a filter size of 114, this would
amount to a total of (55 ∗55 ∗96) ∗(11 ∗11 ∗3 + 1) = 105.705.600 unique parameters already
in the first layer of the CNN. This problem is alleviated by parameter sharing, by saying that the
2­dimensional slice of depth, or activation map, in a convolutional layer shares their weights and
biases. The size of an individual activation map would, in this case, be [55x55]. This amounts to
only 96 unique weights in the convolutional layer and would be equal to 96∗11∗11∗3+1 = 34, 848
unique parameters instead, + 96 bias nodes, which are much more manageable. The implication of
this is that now all activation maps can be computed as a convolution of the weights of the neurons
with the input volume, which is what gives rise to the name convolutional layer. It is also for this
reason that each activation map is also known as a filter or a kernel, that is being convolved with
the input, each learning a different feature of the image [Li et al., 2020].

3.1
The CO­SHARPS Processing Boutique
The COlorado SHArad Radar Processing System (CO­SHARPS) is a system hosted by the Plan­
etary Science Institute (PSI). It supplements the Italian­produced standard Mars SHAllow RADar
sounder (SHARAD) products that are archived by PSI. The system allows users easy access to
radargrams and simulated cluttergrams of the Martian subsurface, which are captured by SHARAD.
The access is facilitated through CO­SHARPS Processing Boutique (CPB) and provides the users
with customizable processing parameters to better meet the user’s needs.
In CPB there is a range of different SAR processors available, developed by the US SHARAD team.
The four processor types are: FPB, MDIM only, THEMIS only and QDA. The FPB processor type
is a Smithsonian Focused Processor, and the QDA processor type is a revised focused processor.
The processors carry out a range of Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) processing operations on
the raw radar data, like the ones described in subsection 2.1.1. These include algorithms such as
the Range Migration Correction (RMC), pulse compression, etc., to focus the data into user­ready
data products. For this thesis, the processors FPB and QDA were the two optimal candidates. The
two processors are very similar but have a few detail­oriented differences. The most significant
differences occur in the process of range compression, the way the Doppler centroid is estimated
(i.e. the formation of the synthetic aperture), and the correction for ionospheric distortion. Both
processors allow a range of user­definable parameters, as shown in Figure 3.1, so that processed
data products can be adapted to the users end needs. Furthermore, the QDA and FPB processors
also use different along­track sampling, frame intervals1 and datum. The QDA makes use of the
3396/3376­km ellipsoid reference frame, while the FPB uses the areoid reference frame2 instead.

Some of the parameters shown in Figure 3.1 are restricted to a specific processing type. In CPB,
presumming is the concept of onboard coherent summing of echoes into groups in order to reduce
the data rate. The desired presum value, or factor, depends on the operating scenario. Presum
factors can be chosen in the range of 1 (no presumming), 2, 4, 8, 16, 24, 32, etc. When selecting
an N’th factor of presumming, the samples from N sequential Pulse­Repetition Interval (PRI)s
are summed together sample­by­sample, reducing the data rate by a factor of N but also reducing
the spatial resolution. The resulting sum of N echoes is known as a ’data block’. For the FPB
processor, an aperture length can be chosen in intervals of 256, between the values of 256 and
3072. For the QDA processor, the aperture length is locked at 4096 frames instead. The aperture
length determines the number of presummed pulses used to form each synthetic aperture. Larger
apertures mean a given feature has greater visibility and may also provide additional looks (multi­
looking) for the purpose of speckle reduction3. An aperture length of 4096 has been shown to yield
good results for rugged or sloping terrain, and therefore the QDA processor is recommended by
the CO­SHARPS team for this type of analysis. The QDA processor was chosen for this reason
and chosen due to its available option of selecting a dielectric constant for depth conversion and
selecting a custom presum factor. The QDA was also the processor of choice for earlier works in
the same field, such as the [Gupta et al., 2020] case study.
It should be noted, however, that the aperture length listed in CPB and the presum factor can be the
subject of some confusion, as the terms are used differently than in conventional radar terminology.
In convention, for unfocused SARs the aperture length refers to the length of the synthetic aperture
in the along­track dimension, and the presum factor refers to the summed echoes in this same
dimension, which increases the spatial resolution as the presum factor is raised. Hence, the two

decreases proportionally with dissimilarity.

3.5.1
The Model of choice
The model of choice for the segmentation of LDAs in this thesis is a Convolutional Neural Net­
work (CNN) architecture named U­Net. The U­Net architecture was initially proposed in 2015 by
[Ronneberger et al., 2015] and is a neural network used for image segmentation purposes.
In deep learning applications for image segmentation, there is a large number of available CNN
architectures to choose from. As was elaborated upon in subsection 2.3.2, a typical CNN consists
of stacked layer combinations of convolutional layers, pooling layers and fully connected (dense)
layers. There is no ”right” or ”best” way to stack the layers, as each combination will yield different
results depending on the circumstances of the problem formulation. However, since people are
dedicating their entire PhD’s to inventing new CNN architectures for image segmentation these
days, there is no reason for attempting to invent a new model architecture oneself in this context.
When choosing an existing CNN architecture, there are many considerations to take into account.
These include:
1. The complexity of the model.
2. The time needed to implement the model.
3. The size of the dataset.
4. The difficulty in training on the dataset.
5. The desired accuracy.

(decoding) path, which reveals a U­shaped architecture6. The encoding path is based on the type

The decoding path upsamples the now vast amount of feature maps obtained at the end of the en­

used. This allows a seamless tiling of the output segmentation maps.

simple way to illustrate this process can be seen in Figure 3.16. If the deviation between y_pred

arrow then corresponds to the learning rate.

3.5.4
Data Augmentation
For deep learning applications, it is often said that the network is only as good as the data being
passed to it. Annotating correctly and being critical of which images to use and which not to use
can mean a big difference in the networks ability to reach good performance. Logically, the larger
amount of good and informative images there are in a data set, the better the network will be,
in terms of accuracy and its ability to generalize, since it has been trained on more examples of
the target it is trying to predict. Considering it is desirable to have a data set that is as large and
qualitative as possible, there is a clever trick to synthetically ”enlarge” the data set used in deep
learning10. This trick is named data augmentation and is especially important when working with
a limited data set.
After each epoch has passed in the training phase, the learning parameters will have been updated
based on the information learned during this epoch. However, with the use of data augmentation,
various transformations are applied to the images during each epoch, with some probability, syn­
thesizing new data. In this way, the same images will be slightly different from epoch to epoch,
and the network will treat them as if they were separate images. This makes the network much
better at generalizing and predicting images and will raise the network performance overall. For
image classification tasks, some of the most widely used but simple transformations are 1): Geo­
metric transformations, like rotation, translation, flipping, scaling and cropping. 2): Color space
transformations, which includes noise injection, color casting and varying brightness. An example
of the most common types of data augmentation transforms available can be seen in Figure 3.20.

When working with deep learning of any kind, it is cru­

Several evaluation metrics have been tested during this
thesis before deciding on the best metrics to use. When

matrix and the concepts derived from it.
Figure 3.21 illustrates a simple confusion matrix. The
confusion matrix makes use of the following four con­
cepts. The True Positives (TP) is equal to the number of predicted foreground pixels that are also
foreground pixels in the ground­truth image. The False Positives (FP) is equal to the number of
predicted foreground pixels that are background pixels in the ground­truth image. The False Neg­
atives (FN) is equal to the number of predicted background pixels that are foreground pixels in the
ground­truth image. And the True Negatives (TN) is equal to the number of predicted background
pixels that are also background pixels in the ground­truth image. For example, a TP refers to a
prediction correctly classifying a pixel belonging to an LDA, while a FN refers to the network
predicting a pixel as not belonging to an LDA, even though it did in the ground­truth image.
The training and validation loss
The loss function is based on the BCE loss function, which was
defined in Equation 3.10. Two different values are obtained after each epoch, one for the training
phase and one for the validation phase of the network. While the objective during evaluation is to
get both the validation and training loss to be as low as possible, the profile of the two curves can
inform the user about the quality of the data set and can also lead the user to make better choices.
In general, the following four rules apply when the loss is evaluated in deep learning:
• If validation loss » training loss it can be seen as overfitting.
• If validation loss > training loss it can be seen as some overfitting.
• If validation loss < training loss it can be seen as some underfitting.
• If validation loss « training loss it can be seen as underfitting.
Overfitting means that the model is performing very well regarding the training data but not the
validation data. This means it is not good at generalizing well to new or unseen types of data.
A little bit of overfitting is always standard and expected. Underfitting means that the model is
performing better with regards to the validation data than the training data. This may happen if
too much data augmentation is done, making it hard for the network to predict the training data
compared to the unmodified validation data. The objective is to have both curves as close to a

algorithm works is that it treats the true class and the predicted class as two binary variables and
then computes the correlation coefficient between these two variables. The higher the computed
correlation between the predicted and annotated values are, the better the prediction is. This is
also known as the phi­coefficient ϕ. The MCC is very similar to the more standard F1 metric,
which is more commonly used in deep learning evaluation. However, studies ([Chicco & Jurman,

in evaluations of binary classification problems when compared to the F1 metric and the accuracy
metric. It should also be noted that the MCC metric also accounts for the TN­score, while this
information is not present in the calculation of the F1 metric.

4.1
Training and validation
The data set consists of 204 images in total and is split into 75% training images, 5% validation
images and 20% test images, which amounts to a total of 155 images for training, 10 images for
validation and 39 images for testing. It is important to split the data into a validation and test
set, so the images being tested on do not influence the per­epoch performance evaluation of the
network, or influence when to stop it. In this way, a ”clean subset” of the data is fed to the network
to evaluate how it handles data samples that it has not encountered before. The validation data
is carefully selected manually and consists of 10 diverse images that show the different visual
situations that characterize the overall trends in the data set. The training and test images are
randomly sampled from the remaining 194 images of the data set. The U­Net is run on the training
data for an initial 110 epochs, and evaluated with the validation data after each epoch, with the
spatial hyperparameters, training phase parameters, data augmentations and evaluation metrics
described in section 3.5. The first part of producing the results is determining the optimal amount
of epochs to run the network for before stopping the training phase. This is a concept known as
early stopping and is motivated by the fact that generalization should be avoided in the data set, i.e.
the training is stopped when the training and validation loss values are observed to start diverging
in opposite directions.

evaluation and discarding phase, this image data set was reduced to 204 images in total. The
image data set was split into 75% training images, 20% testing images and 5% validation images,
and then input into a U­Net Convolutional Neural Network architecture implemented in Python,
with a large emphasis on data augmentation techniques. After a careful selection of the different
hyperparameters of the network, the network was run and evaluated, after which an early stopping
point of 75 epochs was determined, based on the per­epoch output of the metrics.
The results shown in chapter 4 indicate that the objective of this thesis has been carried out suc­
cessfully and beyond expectations. All of the metrics are performing very well overall, with a
converging training and validation loss, and with a decent mean score in both the Matthews Cor­
relation Coefficient (MCC) and the Hausdorff object distance metrics across all 39 test images.
In section 4.2, several examples of the individual predictions are shown to demonstrate in which
cases the algorithm is successful and in which cases it is less so. It is seen that there is a clear cor­
relation between the subsurface reflection boundary picked up by the radar and the performance
of the network. This notion is confirmed after an evaluation of both the successful and the non­
successful predictions, as well as the results from the Principal Component Analysis (PCA) done
on the 64 activation maps from the last up­convolutional layer. Furthermore, it is also observed
that the difficulty in segmenting the training images vary significantly from image to image. Some
images are well­defined, and it is straightforward to visually see where an LDA is located, while
other images are much more ambiguous, and in some cases, even beg the question if they should
have been used at all in the data set (see subsection 5.2.1). Nevertheless, as was also described
in section 3.2, finding a good radar track that appropriately covers an LDA is not always easy,
as such ”ideal” radar tracks are scarce. Hence, if only the best and most clearly defined examples
were chosen, the image data set would have been much smaller, and as a consequence, the network
would likely not have been able to generalize well to non­ideal cases.
Regarding the choice of deep learning parameters, different parameters were tried out and tested
for their performance. This does not mean that they were the best parameters possible, however.
Judging from the metric results on the validation set, and especially the loss function curves seen
in Figure 4.1, one could argue that choosing an earlier stopping point than 75 epochs could have
led to better or equally good results. Even though the training loss continues to fall, this is not
important when evaluating an early stopping point because only the validation loss is indicative
of when the model stops learning new information. Hence, by visual examination, perhaps the
training could and should have been stopped already around 50 or 60 epochs.
Another configuration that may have led to better segmentation results was to use a kernel size of
4 and a stride of 2 in the up­convolutional layers of the U­Net. Even though this would have led
to a smaller output size than input size, many scientific papers use this configuration and achieve
better results by doing so.

5.2.1
Ambiguous annotations and annotation errors
In this thesis, more than 200 different radar tracks were downloaded from COlorado SHArad
Radar Processing System (CO­SHARPS) and annotated manually, with the annotation approach
described in section 3.4. Every radar track covered a considerable distance, some of them close to
3000 km, and most of them had several regions of LDAs to annotate, which made it possible to
split the radargram into multiple 512x512 images. In total, almost 250 images of 512x512 were
produced and annotated over two months, and after careful evaluation, reduced to a total of 204 us­
able training images in the end. As have been stated earlier in section 3.4, a neural network is only
as good as the data that are being fed into it, but the process of annotation is both time­consuming
and tedious and highly prone to human error if one is not careful. Even though attempts were made
to be as consistent and careful as possible in the annotation process, wrongly or ambiguously anno­
tated training images are still one of the largest potential error sources of the data set in this thesis.
However, it was also a learning process. This means that as more images were being annotated, the
ability to judge and be critical of where and when to annotate, and when not to, also improved. An­
notating without a clear secondary reflection boundary can lead the algorithm astray. As time went
on, and as the environment of LDA­containing regions on Mars became more familiar, the intu­
ition of when this boundary was clear enough to allow an annotation also changed. Consequently,
this means that some of the earliest annotated images were not annotated in the same consistent
way as some of the last annotated images. After iterating throughout the data set, several training
images and their corresponding annotations initially judged to be good enough were removed from
the data set. Even after this, some ambiguous training images remained. Removing them from the
data set as well might lead the algorithm to better performance, but will also remove the potential
”difficult cases” that differ from the usually more clearly distinguished LDAs present in the data
set. Presenting more ambiguous cases to the network is also an important part of training a neural
network, as this will simulate a more realistic scenario where not every sample is an easy case and
may also lead the network to generalize better. Hence, some ambiguous cases were allowed to
remain in the data set and can be seen illustrated below.

5.3
Research implications and related work
In 2020 the case study published by [Gupta et al., 2020] detailed a segmentation strategy for sub­
surface discontinuities and 3D subsurface reconstruction, with the aid of a U­Net architecture. The
approach of this case study was the first­ever of its kind and managed to produce results, although
with limited performance. The limited performance was mainly due to the fact that the focus was
only on the subsurface discontinuities, i.e. the secondary reflection boundary detected by the radar
but not by the clutter simulator. This implied that the training data they had to work with had
very few pixels to annotate and an enormous class imbalance problem. The workings of this thesis
have addressed a related but different research question and approach. The segmentation of entire
LDAs was the objective of this thesis, and it has been accomplished with good results, with a data
set of only 204 images. With this algorithm, a tool is now in place that can quickly identify LDAs
in radargrams that cover thousands of kilometers on Mars. Although the majority of LDAs have

5.4
Future Work
The objectives of this thesis were for the prediction of LDAs that are bounded by the hard surface
of mountainsides, as found in a small number of radar tracks delivered by CO­SHARPS. The type
of radar track mentioned above is scarce, and this scarcity limits the size of the data set and also
makes the research area of this thesis slightly niche, as elaborated on in section 2.2. First of all,
many more radar tracks that cover LDAs exists, and even more if the criteria to use the well­
defined mountainside as a boundary is dropped. But the dropping of this criteria is also a dropping
of a vital source of information for the network to train on, and will make the predictions much
more ambiguous. Hence, making such an algorithm should be possible but would likely require a
much larger data set to train on before the algorithm would be able to accurately predict all types
of LDAs. Furthermore, the images of this data set would also be more prone to contamination
by more significant amounts of clutter sources, as was explained in section 3.2, due to the radar
wave returning echoes originating from topography at many different levels of elevation. So while
making the algorithm able to generalize across all types of LDAs, regardless of clutter sources, is
a logical and feasible next step, more time and much more data would be needed to accomplish
this.
Another potential next step is to construct an algorithm that can segment Concentric Crater Fill
(CCF)s or Lineated Valley Fill (LVF)s instead, or possibly even be able to segment all three and be
able to differentiate them from each other as unique classes. As was described in section 2.2, CCFs
make out the majority of the ice­deposited landform types on Mars, although the volumes of water
stored in them, as well as the spatial area they cover, are much smaller. Furthermore, contrary to
LDAs, the CCF type of landform contains its water­ice deposit in the bottom of its craters, which
might make it difficult for the radar wave to interact with it. This is due to the geometry of sounding
radars as shown in Figure 1.1, meaning that only a minimum of the radar wave would propagate to
the bottom of the crater, while the majority would interact with the sides of the crater edge instead.
It might be possible to do, however, with large amounts of clutter suppression, but it would likely
require the development of a different SAR processor than the ones currently available in CO­
SHARPS. For LVF­type landforms, this sounding geometry problem is less prevalent, but still
much more an issue than it is for LDAs. LVFs occur only in valleys, which means that potential
clutter sources that could dominate the signal would originate from the cliffs on either side of the
valley. The width of the LVF valleys varies greatly however, similarly to the size­variation seen in
LDAs, so the difficulty of segmenting each LVF would also be case­dependant.
In conclusion, the segmentation of LDAs was the first step and the most straightforward task to
accomplish in the segmentation of the three landform types. Now that this step has been demon­
strated and carried out successfully, the findings of this thesis can be built upon, and the algorithm
can be expanded to segment the other type of landforms as well. It might be challenging, but with
enough time and effort, it should also be feasible. Furthermore, having such a system in place
would make the mapping of potential water sources quick and efficient. And knowing exactly
where the water is on Mars, and how much there is in each region, brings humanity one step closer
to the possibility of building a future, self­sustaining human colony on the red planet.

6
Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to implement a deep learning algorithm that can automatically
identify Lobate Debris Apron (LDA)s in ice­penetrating radar data from Mars. No thresholds
were set in place to determine when the algorithm was ”good enough” for the task at hand, but
based on the results shown in chapter 4, the performance of the network is very satisfactory and
beyond expectations.
Throughout the project work, a large amount of LDAs were located with the combined use of Java
Mission­planning and Analysis for Remote Sensing (JMARS), the shapefile published alongside
the [Levy et al., 2014] study, and the Geosciences Node’s Orbital Data Explorer (ODE) software.
More than 200 radar tracks have been downloaded from COlorado SHArad Radar Processing Sys­
tem (CO­SHARPS) and carefully examined, after which a total of 145 radar tracks were deemed
useful enough to use in the neural network. The radar tracks underwent a large amount of pre­
processing, as described in section 3.3, to prepare them as training images. The preprocessing
included an initial cropping, bilateral filtering, co­registration, the computation of a normalized
difference image, and finally, the construction of the 3­Channel RGB Composite Image, consist­
ing of the radargram, cluttergram and the normalized difference image as separate color channels.
This 3­Channel Composite Image served as the format for the training images. Following the pre­
processing stage, the training images were split into 512x512 regions. Due to the vast size of the
radargrams, of which some were as large as 4096x9000 pixels, up to four 512x512 training images
could be constructed from a single radargram, in rare cases.
After the training images had been prepared, the images were manually annotated in a Python
script named Annotator by drawing on the images. Then, a U­Net convolutional neural network
was implemented in Python, and the images were inserted to start producing results. Great effort
was also spent on fine­tuning the hyperparameters of the U­Net, such as choosing the right batch
size, finding the best training, validation and test splits, choosing the most optimal data augmen­
tations, as well as looking into the learning rate, loss function, optimizer and more to get the best
segmentations possible. In the end, the network was run with the Adam optimizer with a learning
rate of 10−5, a weighted Binary Cross­Entropy loss function, a batch size of 6 for both the training
and test images, and a data set split of 75 % training, 20 % testing and 5% validation images. An
early stopping point of 75 epochs was chosen after examining the per­epoch development of the
different evaluation metrics on the validation set, which included the loss, accuracy, the Matthews
Correlation Coefficient and the Hausdorff object distance score. After the network finished train­
ing, it was tested on the 39 test images of the data set, which produced good results overall. The
testing set had a mean score of 0.615 in the Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) metric and a
mean score of 47.5 in the Hausdorff object distance metric. Several individual images had a MCC
score between 0.8 and 0.9, and a Hausdorff object distance between 10 and 20, which is an excel­
lent result. The results were carefully evaluated, and a subset of the best and worst predictions was
presented, along with arguments for why they performed as they did.
The approach of this thesis and the results produced have demonstrated that it is possible to segment
most LDAs with a good performance by using a U­Net architecture. It is believed that the algorithm
holds great potential and may be further improved by adding more images to its data set, which
will increase its performance when segmenting LDAs. Furthermore, it should also be possible to
improve the algorithm to be able to segment more ambiguous LDA cases, or other types of ice­
deposit landforms such as Concentric Crater Fill (CCF)s and Lineated Valley Fill (LVF)s, or even
other geological features, as described in section 5.4.
