Abstract
The Global Blockage Effect (GBE) has become a hot topic in offshore wind in recent
years. While there is a broad industry consensus that the GBE causes decelerations in
front of offshore wind farms which affect the energy yield, different opinions exist on how
to consider its downstream impacts and how to account for redistribution of energy, for
example. This is commercially relevant as uncertainty in wind farm performance drives
a high cost of capital for offshore sites. The Carbon Trust’s OWA GloBE project tries to
answer how to correctly incorporate GBE in windfarm yield assessment by comparing var-
ious modeling approaches to measurements taken within the Helgoland windfarm cluster.
The GloBE dataset includes, among others, measurements of boundary layer height, Li-
DAR wind speed measurements, and wind turbine SCADA data – an ideal dataset for
model validation. Recent studies on the interaction between atmospheric boundary lay-
ers (ABL) and windfarm clusters, indicate that the atmospheric stability affects the power
production in a wind farm. The aim is to build a fast, accurate analytical model that is cost
effective to calculate the global blockage effect, considering gravity waves. This thesis
will investigate how this effect can be computed with the help of existing yield assess-
ment tools like PyWake by implementing a fast two-layer windfarm model considering
atmospheric boundary layer and comparing predictions with the GloBE dataset. It can be
concluded that the implemented model captures the power generation flow pattern of the
wind turbines well as compared to SCADA data.

1
Introduction
The need to prioritize renewable energy production has become crucial in recent years
due to observed climate change worldwide, which is largely attributed to the use of fossil
fuels. Hence, the wind industry has seen rapid growth in the past few years, supporting
the production of sustainable energy. Even though wind businesses have successfully
flourished, there are many phenomena around wind turbines and farms that are still not
understood and implemented correctly. The interaction of the blockage effect with ABL
and its effect on gravity waves is just one of the phenomena that is not fully understood
(refer subsection 2.1.2). There were other very important and prnounced losses in wind-
farms that had to be understood and mitigated, like wake losses, control losses, etc. In
the recent years as there has been significant understanding of these losses, blockage
loss has gained attention.
The awareness of the blockage effect has increased over time. It was known that, as the
size of the windfarm increased, the energy production is not only dependent on the perfor-
mance of a single wind turbine but also on the arrangement of the windfarm and complex
aerodynamic interactions within and around (atmosphere) the windfarm. Consequently,
there is a need to understand the blockage effect to optimize the windfarm performance.
This thesis will help understand one of the ways to estimate the global blockage effec-
t/losses along with its limitations. The aim is to build a rapid, cost effective model, that
captures the global blockage effects in a windfarm.
1.1
Importance of Blockage Effects
On 29 October 2019, Ørsted declared an underestimation of blockage and wake loss
which would affect their financial targets [5]. This shows that the blockage loss has a
direct impact on the economics of the windfarm. Energy production, windfarm design,
wake loss, control strategies, turbine performance, etc are all affected by blockage effects.
Since then, the topic of the blockage effect gained attention.
The GloBE project aims on understanding how to consider the blockage effect and its
impact due to its importance.
1.2
The GloBE Project
The Global Blockage Effect in Offshore Wind (GloBE) [6] project aims to understand how
blockage should be modeled along with its uncertainty, against the measured data at the
Helgoland windfarm cluster. The objective of the GloBE project is to measure and quantify
the Global Blockage Effect (GBE) to achieve industry consensus [7]. It aims to approach
a standardized GBE quantification [8].

2.1
Blockage Effect
Before delving into the details of the blockage effect, it is important to understand the
factors that influence it. These factors include:
• Windfarm arrangement and layout: The blockage loss is affected by its configu-
ration (staggered and aligned windfarm [11]), turbine distances, windfarm layout,
arrangement, number of turbines, etc [12], [13].
• Turbine characteristics [12]
• Atmospheric conditions: Atmospheric stability, boundary layer characteristics, at-
mospheric stratification, etc [12], [14].
This thesis will especially focus on atmospheric conditions and its effect on blockage.
The blockage effect is observed on a local (single turbine) level and a global (windfarm)
level. In this section, we will understand the difference between local and global blockage
effects while focusing more on the global blockage effect.
2.1.1
Local blockage effect
As the name suggests the local blockage effect is the blockage effect observed at the
turbine level (i.e. from immediate neighboring wind turbines [15].), and the global blockage
effect is observed at the windfarm level [16].
In front of every wind turbine, there is wind speed reduction due to its presence as an
obstacle, which is considered as the induction zone. This is referred to as local blockage
[15].
The accumulation of this local blockage is referred to as Global blockage.
2.1.2
Global Blockage Effect
When the wind is approaching a mountain or a building, it gets obstructed which leads to
an upstream reverse pressure gradient. The building or the mountain acts as an obstruc-
tion to the flow of the wind, and the wind is forced to compress (density increases) and
flow over it. Hence, the wind speed is reduced and pressure is increased.
As windfarms are porous and thrust-producing, the reverse pressure gradient is observed
as pressure decreases upstream and increases downstream of a turbine. This reverse
pressure gradient is referred as global blockage in a windfarm [14].
The inversion layer acts as a lid on the atmosphere that restricts the vertical displacement
of the air. That means vertical movement of air, temperature, pressure, pollutants, etc are
trapped under this lid. The temperature in this layer continuously increases/decreases

and above this layer, the wind speed (air) is not affected by the earth’s surface and is
considered as the ”free atmosphere”.
Unlike the inversion layer, the Atmospheric Boundary Layer (ABL) is not only directly de-
pendent on the temperature profile but is also dependent on the mixing of the air, atmo-
spheric stability, surface roughness, etc. Both the ABL and inversion layers are located in
the lowest layer of the earth’s atmosphere i.e. troposphere and can be at different heights.
If the ABL is below the inversion layer it will lead to a more stable, less turbulent atmo-
sphere (limited vertical mixing), that might reduce the power production of the windfarm
due to slower wake recovery, and vice versa in case the ABL is above the inversion layer.
Types of stability and its classification can be found in section A.1.
Gravity waves are waves present in the air (fluid) due to the presence of gravity. Wind
turbines generate power due to the flow of a fluid (air) through it. The gravity (air) waves
will be excited due to the presence of wind turbines. For simplicity, the whole scenario
can be directly compared to waves in a water body. Just the presence of one wind turbine
might not excite the gravity waves, but in the presence of a huge windfarm, the gravity
waves might get excited. The mass of the displaced fluid in a windfarm will be higher as
compared to one single wind turbine.
Due to the obstacle (wind turbine), upward flow displacement of air is observed. This up-
ward flow displacement excites the atmospheric gravity waves on the overlying inversion
layers in the free atmosphere [17]. The excitement of gravity waves in turn leads to airflow
redistribution within the windfarm.
The excitation of gravity waves is prominent in very large offshore windfarms [6], [11],
[14], [18]. As there is no surface roughness or other obstacles, the turbulence caused by
these natural features is lower. Therefore below the ABL, the effect of the wind turbines
on the airflow is higher. In offshore windfarms, the common stability cycle as on land
cannot be assumed, because the thermal capacity of the water is higher than that of land.
For instance, in spring, water is cold after winter while the air gets warmer quickly, which
generates stable stratification and vice versa.
For large windfarms, to produce power, the kinetic energy must be entrained from the
free atmosphere and not necessarily only from the entrance region. This entrainment of
air from the free atmosphere leads to an increase in the Boundary Layer (BL) depth. The
entrainment and BL growth depend on the following,
1. Static stability of free atmospheric above ABL.
2. Buoyancy fluxes: vertical exchange of heat/energy due to temperature (and density)
differences.
3. Surface momentum
4. Earths rotation, etc. [19]
2.2
Conventionally Neutral ABL (CNBL)
As mentioned before, the gravity waves excitation is dependent on atmospheric conditions
and is observed more in the case of CNBL. Moreover, CNBL is mainly observed offshore
(as compared to onshore), hence the gravity waves excitation is more prominent offshore
than onshore [11], [20], [18].

CNBL can be divided into three vertical layers. The upper layer is a stably stratified free
atmosphere. The lowest layer is the neutral turbulent boundary layer, hence less vertical
mixing of air will be observed. Finally, the thin layer between the upper and lower layers is
considered the inversion layer [21]. This also explains why the gravity waves are mainly
excited in CNBL conditions due to the presence of a large windfarm. Conversely, if the
upper layer is unstable, there is vertical mixing of the air particles, and the inversion layer
(lid), does not force the air particles in the lower layer to enter the upper layer. Hence, in
CNBL conditions, due to the presence of an inversion layer, the gravity waves are excited.
Even though the excitation of gravity waves is not experienced only in huge offshore
windfarms during CNBL conditions, for all the reasons stated above, it can be inferred
that the excitation of gravity waves are most likely to be observed in these conditions.
It is important to note that CNBL is an idealized atmospheric condition. Exact CNBL is
very difficult to find in the atmosphere, which will be further discussed in section 4.2.

2.3
Research History of Blockage Effects
Now there is an attempt to capture a few important moments in the research history of
blockage effect on a windfarm, to get an overall understanding of the topic.
To explain Figure 2.3 in brief, in the early days of windfarm development, the blockage
effect was not understood. In 2009, Smith was one of the first who proposed a theory on
how gravity waves are generated by windfarms and computed, how pressure gradients
are influenced by it [22]. This theory was later developed into a model that will be used in
this thesis [23].
Abkar and Porté-Agel [19], in 2013 were one of the first who demonstrated the ABL and
atmospheric stability effects on the windfarms, with the help of LES followed by Wu and
Porté-Agel [11] in 2017. Later Vortex wake model, commonly known as the Vortex Cylin-
der model was derived by Branlard and Gaunaa [24], which was further extended to as-
sess the induction zone (local blockage effect) in 2015. In the same year, Troldborg and
Forsting [25], derived a simple model called as Self-Similarity model that could describe
the generic features of the induction zone (local blockage effect). In 2018, Allaerts et al
[17] proposed a three-layer model, similar to a two-layer model to assess the impact of
gravity waves. Over this period, various researchers studied blockage effects with the
help of LES/RANS to understand its effects on windfarms and energy production.
Shortly later in 2020, the Vortex dipole model was derived by Branlard and Forsting [26]
along with Bleeg [27] who used Graph Neural Network as a surrogate model for the RANS

2.3.1
Existing Global Blockage Models
The blockage effect in a windfarm has been studied by many researchers, using Compu-
tational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) methods using Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS)
[11], [19], [20], [21]. As it is computationally heavy and very time-consuming/expensive, in
the past few years few regularly used analytical models have been developed to compute
the blockage losses [28].
Some existing models compute the local blockage loss in a windfarm and do not consider
the effect of ABL. A few that can be named are vortex cylinder, vortex dipole, and self-
similar model, whose details can be found in section A.3. The common limitation to these
models is that they do not take into account the effect of atmospheric stability in their
calculations.
Two-Layer Model
The two-layer model by Smith [23], divides the atmosphere into two horizontal layers
that accounts for atmospheric stability. The lower layer constitutes of turbine drag as the
turbine layer, the upper layer is the free atmosphere. These linearized 2D equations are
solved by Fourier transformation to get wake and blockage wind speed deficit.
The two-layer model is further built into the three-layer model. The three-layer model does
not assume the turbine drag layer and the inversion layer are at same height. Hence, it

can be assumed that the three-layer model is more accurate than the two-layer model.
For the thesis purposes, the two-layer model will be used for maintaining simplicity.
Three-Layer Model
The three-layer model by Allaerts et al. [17] divides the atmosphere into three horizontal
layers that accounts for atmospheric stability. The lower layer constitutes of the drag of
wind turbines referred to as windfarm layer. The second layer lies above the windfarm
layer up to the inversion layer referred to as the upper layer. The free atmosphere is the
third layer in the three-layer model. The RANS equation is linearized 2D equations that
gives wake and blockage wind speed deficit. The three-layer model is discretized with a
Fourier-Galerkin method, which forms a linear matrix equation that can be computed by
the LGMRES algorithm.

3.1
Assumptions of the Model
It is difficult to build a model that can simulate exact real-world conditions. Moreover, the
cost and time needed to run such models would be very expensive and time-consuming.
Hence models generally have few underlying assumptions. All the assumptions and lim-
itations of the two-layer Smith model are listed below:
1. The turbine drag has been mixed homogeneously between turbines and vertically
up to the top of the BL. Due to this mixing, the ABL, inversion layer, and turbine
layer are assumed to be at the same height.
10

==PAGE:26==
2. Steady state inflow: Constant inflow wind speed and wind direction with respect to
time. Additionally, constant wind speed reduction factor (β) in the turbine layer.
3. Linear theory: Converting RANS (first-order partial differential equation) to a lin-
earized equation for simplicity. (Negligible viscous dissipation, and Reynolds Stress
perturbations/turbulence). It also implies small amplitude assumption of the gravity
waves.
4. The model considers the shape of the windfarm as a block for its calculations and
not each turbine position.
5. The model does not consider shear.
6. Constant Rayleigh friction coefficient and no-slip condition (wind speed at the earth’s
surface is effectively zero).
7. Two-dimensional flow: The flow of the wind is assumed to be two-dimensional.
8. In-compressible flow: The density of the air does not change with time or space.
9. Hydrostatic balance: The pressure gradient force is balanced by gravitational force
under stationary conditions (no vertical motion).
As mentioned before in subsection 2.1.2, the ABL and inversion layer are not necessarily
at the same height in the atmosphere. Similarly, ABL and the layer that is affected by the
turbine drag do not need to be at the same height. If the ABL is very high and the windfarm
is small in size, the turbine drag will not be mixed homogeneously up to the height of ABL.
But if the ABL is low and the windfarm is large enough, there might be a good mixing of
the turbine drag up to the ABL. As the number of wind turbines increase, the air air mass
displacement due to wind turbines increase. This further leads to increased air mixing in
the turbine layer, and the turbine drag is homogeneously mixed. The thesis aims to study
such a situation. Hence the case study might be close to the assumption that the ABL,
the inversion layer, and the turbine layer are at the same height.
If the turbine layer is unstable, there is high turbulence in the atmosphere. Hence there
is a possibility of high amplitude gravity waves leading to break the wave at crest. As
the thesis is aimed towards examining a CNBL condition, the small amplitude assumption
would stand.

4.2.1
Neutral Stability in the Lower Layer of the Atmosphere using
Measured Data Only
After applying the aforementioned filters, a total of 270 days out of the year are retained
for further analysis.
In CNBL condition, the lower layer of the atmosphere is neutrally stable, which means the
potential temperature (section A.1) is constant along the height. The potential temperature
of the measured wind speed is calculated with the help of Equation A.1. As discussed
before for the Figure 4.7, the temperature is measured at four different heights. For the
lower layer to be neutral, the difference between the potential temperature at these heights
should be zero. In reality, it is very difficult to observe such a situation, as the difference
between the potential temperatures at different heights is never exactly zero. It is not a
practical, but an idealized situation.

5.2
Wake Calculations
Now to calculate wake effects in PyWake after setting up the site, an engineering windfarm
model needs to be set up. The model can be either PropagateDownwind or All2AllItera-
tive.
PropagateDownwind model is a fast windfarm model as it calculates downstream wake
deficits w.r.t free wind stream and upstream turbine wake effects in downstream order
(unidirectional). This approach would be appropriate in cases where only wake effects
from the windfarm are to be calculated quickly.
If one wants to consider the blockage effects, the All2AllIterative model must be used.
Unlike in PropagateDownwind, in All2AllIterative the model iterates until effective wind
speeds converge, hence it is much slower.
wake_deficitModel=ZongGaussianDeficit(use_effective_ws=True) [10]
”ZongGaussianDeficit()” is used to calculate the wake of the windfarm. It is one of the
latest Gaussian deficit models and has the right model constants needed (as per experi-
ence) for this case. The Gaussian deficit is chosen over N.O. Jensen deficit as the wake
propagated downwind does not have sharp edges but has a Gaussian curve. The Gaus-
sian deficit model gives a better prediction of the deficit profile over the entire (far) wake.
Why ”ZongGaussianDeficit()” was chosen over all the other Gaussian Deficits, or other
wake models is out of the scope of this thesis.
superpositionModel=LinearSum() [10]
In combination with ”use_effective_ws=True” in ”ZongGaussianDeficit()”, the superposi-
tion model ”LinearSum()” should be used as the downstream wind turbine will feel the
effective wind speed rather than free wind speed. As opposed to ”SquaredSum()” which
is used in combination with wake models where the downstream wakes are scaled with
ambient free-stream velocity to avoid negative wind speeds.
turbulenceModel=CrespoHernandez() [10]
”CrespoHernandez()” is one of the latest turbulent models, and the reason for it being
chosen over other models is out of the scope of this thesis.
blockagedeficitModel=SelfSimilarityDeficit2020 (groundModel=Mirror()) [10]
As per the discussion in section A.3, the Self-Similar model is one of the most recently
developed analytical blockage models and is faster than VD. VD is a far-field approxima-
tion of VC hence, the SS model is chosen to compute the blockage effect. In this case,
the ground model needs to be added to the PyWake calculations, to capture the effects
of the ground/surface that affect the blockage.
From now on, only wake calculation with PyWake means, with the help of PyWake the
wind speed deficit calculations are performed excluding the blockage effect calculations.
This is performed with the help of PropagateDownwind which calculates the wind speed
deficit using the ZongGaussianDeficit wake model, LinearSum superposition model, and

Summary
Due to climate change, the demand to produce electricity from renewable sources has
increased. Hence the demand to build large wind farms has also increased. It is important
to extract as much power from the wind to produce maximum energy. But there are a few
factors or risks that contribute to the loss of energy. The blockage effect is such a factor.
It is important to understand the loss due to the blockage effect and gravity wave exci-
tation. Hence, an attempt to build a fast, cost-effective windfarm global blockage effect
was made. Initially, through various research, it was concluded that excitation of gravity
waves might be observed mainly in CNBL conditions when the ABL height is low enough
in large offshore windfarms. Therefore, such a situation where all these conditions are
met was chosen as a case study after filtering all the data.
Data from the GloBE project at Helgoland windfarm was available. The Smith two-layer,
linearized 2D model was built in Python. This model was verified with the original Smith
model. Later was coupled with PyWake such that the wind speed deficit due to gravity
wave excitation is captured.
The Smith model, fairly predicts the flow pattern in the wind farm. The assumption that
the turbine drag has been mixed homogeneously up to the ABL in the windfarm highly
limits the model’s usage. Hence, if the turbine drag is not mixed, there is a possibility of
under-predicting the power production in large windfarms. That is also the case here in
one of the windfarms. PyWake overpredicted the power generated in the windfarm as
compared to the SCADA data for the chosen case study. Hence, it is possible that the
gravity waves were excited in this period but the Smith model overpredicted its effects.
It is important to note that the Smith model is coupled with PyWake to calculate the overall
wind speed deficit. Hence, producing the exact SCADA data is difficult. The power pro-
duction is not only affected by the limitations of the Smith model but also by the limitation
of all the other models used within the PyWake.

Further Work
The assumption in the Smith model that the turbine drag has been mixed homogeneously
up to the ABL in the windfarm highly limits the model’s usage. Hence, the two-layer model
should be upgraded to a three-layer model, such that the lower layer is the turbine mixing
layer, and the second layer is ABL height. In this way, the mixing of turbine drag and ABL
height are separated to give better results.
The effect of land (Helgoland island) should be considered in future calculations for in-
creasing the accuracy of the results.
Once the complete data for WRF is available, the period where the CNBL is observed can
be found without the relaxation, to get closer to real conditions in the atmosphere. The
corrected Nacelle yaw direction might give more accurate results.
Understand how Inverse Obukhov length is calculated and analyze the neutral stability
with its help. This will help us understand if the Inverse Obukhov neutral stability fails
because of ASL assumption.
More measurements can always help in reducing the uncertainty of the wind conditions, in
turn being closer to real-life conditions. The met mast can be installed in 2-3 more places
around the wind farm. The pressure measuring device at 92 meters can be corrected.
Moreover, the corrected Nacelle direction would also help in accurate power generation
predictions.
The Smith model can also be compared to LES data that would be available later, for
future references. In cases where SCADA data is not available, these results can be
used as a reference for those sites.
It might be useful to take note of all of the underlying assumptions and limitations of the
PyWake itself as the Smith model is coupled into PyWake.
This model can be developed further to be added in PyWake so that it can be used com-
mercially.

A.3
Existing Local Blockage Models
A.3.1
Vortex Cylinder (VC)
Figure A.6: Vortex Cylinder model 3D view. Figure taken from Branlard and Forsting [24]
62
Boundary layer height, stability, and wind farm efficiency
==PAGE:78==
The VC model by Branlard and Gaunaa [24] prescribes the induced velocity has a wake
geometry to be semi-infinite cylinder of constant tangential vorticity, extending along the z
axis. This model assumes that the wind turbines can be approximated as vortex cylinders
that rotate around their axes and generate a wake behind them. That implies that the
the VC model idealizes the geometry, with homogeneous wind conditions and no wake
interactions. The assumptions of this model are constant uniform circulation, an infinite
number of blades, finite tip-speed ratio, along with vorticity only in longitudinal (radial)
direction and axi-symmetry vortex distribution.
A.3.2
Vortex Dipole (VD)
Figure A.7: Two turbines induction and wake interaction. Figure taken from Branlard and
Forsting [26]
The VD model by Branlard and Forsting [26] corresponds to a far-field approximation of
the VC model, for faster estimation. The model assumes that the wind turbines can be
approximated as two counter-rotating vortices or dipoles that generate a wake behind
them. The VD model has the same assumptions as VC.
A.3.3
Self Similar (SS)
It is a simple induction model of the axial-induced velocity, that is used to compute the local
blockage effect by Troldborg and Forsting [25]. It was observed that for distance beyond
one rotor radius upstream, the induced velocity is self-similar and independent of rotor
geometry. This implies that the SS model assumes that air flow around a wind turbine
remain the same regardless of the size and shape of the turbine, along with homogeneous
wind conditions. It is also important to note that wind turbines do not interact with each
other. SS model has three independent parts to compute: shape, depth, and its width,
with unknowns thrust coefficient and free stream velocity that have to be provided.
The accuracy of all the models (VC, VD, SS) can be improved by tuning the inputs [28].
