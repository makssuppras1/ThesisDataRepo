Abstract 
Tickborne Encephalitis (TBE) is a human infectious viral disease which can cause a serious 
course of disease, affecting the central nervous system. It is caused by Tickborne Encephalitis 
Virus (TBEV) which is spread by ticks, and due to climate change and other factors the vector 
ticks, and hence the virus is spreading in large parts of the world. The most common route of 
infection for humans is through the bite of an infected tick, however infected ruminants can 
shed the virus in their milk and cause TBE in consumers of the milk if not pasteurized. Due to 
the trend showing a spread of TBEV in other nearby European countries, TBEV will likely 
become a larger health concern in Denmark, in the future. Therefore, this project aims to 
develop a quantitative risk assessment model to predict the magnitude of people infected 
with TBE from consumption of unpasteurized milk in Denmark, in the future and identify 
relevant focus areas to reduce the incidence of TBE through this route. 
The model development process had 3 steps. The first was a qualitative approach where the 
different steps in the risk pathway were identified. Secondly a deterministic mathematical 
model was designed for this pathway, and values could be assigned to the different steps, 
yielding an estimate of the number of people infected with TBE through unpasteurized milk. 
Lastly stochasticity was applied to consider the uncertainty of parameters. This was done by 
giving them appropriate distributions instead of the single values, thus integrating the 
uncertainty of the different parameters into the predicted values. The final stochastic model 
started with the prevalence of TBEV in ticks, following the virus from tick to cow, from cow 
into milk, and ended up with the number of people getting TBE from the consumption of 
unpasteurized milk. Data for the model was collected from scientific literature, expert 
opinions and ultimately assumptions were made if information was missing. 
A scenario analysis was performed for both the deterministic and the stochastic model to 
assess how the different parameters influenced the risk estimate. Results gained from the 
deterministic model was not informative enough to yield any valuable output. The 
parameters included were the TBEV prevalence in ticks and the tick abundance. A total of six 
scenarios were analyzed by changing these parameters to imitate certain environmental 
conditions. These scenarios were high and low TBEV prevalence in ticks, high and low 
abundance of ticks and cows grazing outside or kept inside during milking. The scenario 
analysis showed that the tick abundance parameter had the largest impact on the number of 
infected consumers. Due to lack of information, assumptions were made regarding two 
parameters. These parameters were the probability of a cow being infected, if bitten by an 
infected tick and the probability that an infected cow sheds TBEV in the milk. The parameters 
were assumed to be 100% as a safety precaution towards the risk estimate. These 
assumptions resulted in an overestimation of the real risk and therefore it was difficult to 
make reliable predictions of the actual number of future TBE cases through unpasteurized 
milk. The scenario analysis could still be used to assess relevant focus areas for the reduction 
of the risk. 
The area with highest impact on the number of infected people with TBE through 
unpasteurized milk is the abundance of ticks. The largest source of uncertainty comes from

the assumptions made about the probability of a cow being infected with TBEV, if bitten by 
an infected tick, and the probability of an infected cow shedding TBEV in the milk. These 
assumptions however, does not influence the weight of these parameters and thus the 
conclusion is not impacted by these assumptions.

Introduction 
Tick Borne Encephalitis Virus (TBEV) is a virus of the family Flaviviridae. There are three 
subtypes of the virus, namely the European, the far eastern and the Siberian subtype. The 
European subtype is the one considered in this project. If transmitted to humans, the virus 
can cause Tick borne encephalitis (TBE) which most often takes a bi-phasic course of illness, 
starting with flu-like symptoms like fever, nausea and muscle pain. After these have passed 
there is a recovery period before the next phase where the central nervous system is involved. 
At this point, the virus manifests as encephalitis, meningitis, myelitis, or a combination of 
these. This can lead to long term neurological damage or even worse, death.[24] TBEV is 
carried and transmitted by ticks, replication of the virus occurs in the ticks and once infected, 
the tick carries the virus throughout the rest of its life cycle. [6] Ixodes Ricinus is the main 
vector tick of the European subtype and is the tick referred to in this project. The main route 
of human infection is through the bite of an infected tick, while doing outdoor activities. 
However, when an infected tick feeds on a lactating ruminant such as a cow, the virus can be 
transmitted into the bloodstream of the cow and lastly into the milk. [24] TBEV can then cause 
TBE in the consumer after consumption of the unpasteurized milk. TBEVs resistance to low 
pH enables it to survive through the gastrointestinal tract and can then be absorbed into the 
bloodstream and be transported to the brain where it can cause inflammation of the central 
nervous system. [2] 
The presence of TBEV in ticks has been spreading for many years and is expected to continue 
to spread in the future. Probable reason for the spread of the ticks is the climate change, 
resulting in more areas that fit the ticks specific living conditions as well as milder and shorter 
winters resulting in longer periods of high tick activity. [27] When looking for hosts, the ticks 
must climb the vegetation to attach to bypassing hosts and this process causes them to lose 
moisture, and therefore they must recover humidity down in the soil to survive, so a high 
level of humidity in the environment makes it so that the ticks can hunt for hosts more 
efficiently and is thus very beneficial for tick survival. [8]  
The abundance of wild game animal is also increasing in large parts of Europe and is a natural 
host animal for the ticks and play a crucial role in maintaining a high tick population. Since 
these are natural hosts for adult ticks, an increase and spread of these animals is also likely to 
result in an increase and spread of ticks. [6] 
Besides the increase and spread of TBEV and the ticks carrying the virus [8], there are other 
important factors contributing to the importance of TBE infection through unpasteurized 
dairy. Raw foodism is a popular food trend which revolves around consuming food that hasn’t 
been heated to more than 42°C. This means excluding pasteurized products from your diet, 
since pasteurization is carried out at higher temperatures than 42°C. Studies have shown that 
TBEV is not inactivated after heating to 30°C and during long time-low temperature 
pasteurization virus could still be detected after heat treatment at 65°C for 350 seconds. The 
study also showed that the time needed for a 3-log reduction when using temperatures below 
60°C would result in a high increase in the time needed for inactivation [28][9], so heat 
treatment below 42°C is likely not suitable method of inactivation. Besides raw foodism, 
restaurants from around Denmark has taken interest in using and serving dairy products 
==PAGE:7==
7 
 
made from unpasteurized milk in their restaurants. Especially restaurants in the Copenhagen 
area have reached out to the danish veterinary and food administration with the request to 
sell these products in their restaurants, Niels Ladefoged Nielsen from the danish veterinary 
and food administration said (personal communication).  
Based on the spread of TBEV as well as the increase in the abundance of vector ticks for the 
virus as well as an increase in host animals for the ticks, combined with the increasing trends 
of consuming less processed foods and an increasing interest of restaurants using and serving 
these products, it can be expected that TBE through the consumption of unpasteurized milk 
can become a greater health concern in Denmark, in the future. 
As of right now, TBEV is not a major health concern in Denmark, only a few cases have been 
confirmed the last many years and none linked to raw milk consumption but are instead due 
to human tick bites. [29] Small microfocus areas have been identified in Tisvilde and Tokkekøb 
hegn and the island of Bornholm is known as an endemic area. [25][26] Since the low incidence 
of TBE in Denmark, not much has been done to prevent it this far. In other European countries 
however, where TBEV is a larger health concern, usual preventive actions are immunization 
of people living in endemic areas through vaccination.[7] Other than that turning of the soil 
where the animals are kept or other means of keeping the vegetation down is an effective 
way of preventing the ticks from having optimal living conditions.[23] 
If TBEV spreads throughout Denmark in the future, it is beneficial to have an idea of the most 
efficient way of dealing with the virus to decrease its impact on public health. 
 
This project aims to develop a model to predict the future number of TBE cases through 
unpasteurized cow milk in Denmark, identifying relevant focus areas and preventive 
measures to reduce the impact of TBEV on public health.

Method and Materials 
Risk Pathway 
The route of TBEV from tick to consumer was visualized through a risk pathway and is 
illustrated in Figure 1. The starting point is the ticks in the pathway, small rodents are initially 
the natural carriers of the virus and the ticks gets infected when taking a blood meal on the 
rodents in their early life stage. The rodents are however not included in the model developed 
in this project. The virus is found in the salivary glands of the tick and can be transmitted to 
the Ruminant when the tick attaches for a blood meal, the transmission happens very briefly 
after attachment to the host [6]. For this to happen the tick first needs to attach to the 
ruminant which is done by the ambush technique where the tick climbs the vegetation and 
attaches to ruminants passing by. If two ticks are feeding on the same host, one tick carrying 
TBEV and the other is not, TBEV will be transmitted to the other tick as well briefly after 
attachment. This is called non-viremic transmission.[6] The infection of the ruminant relies on 
a couple of parameters. The first is the abundance of ticks in the environment, naturally the 
more ticks that are present the higher the probability of the cow to get infested by a tick, as 
well as the number of ticks on each cow will likely increase, increasing the probability of 
infection further. If the environmental conditions for the ticks is not optimal, the infestation 
rate of the cows are likely to decrease. Since deer and other game animals carry a lot of ticks, 
they will also affect the infestation rate of cows if they are residing in the same areas. 
When a cow has been infected the virus can be transmitted into the milk of the ruminant. It 
is uncertain if the ruminant always shed the virus in its milk if infected and for how long the 
virus is shed. Reports have claimed that shedding of virus in milk can happen for a couple of 
days and up to several weeks, varying between animals.[30] Once the virus is transmitted to 
the milk, consumers can be exposed by consuming the raw milk or cheese made from the 
milk. Studies have shown that the virus is traceable in milk for days and up to several weeks 
at refrigeration temperatures. During cheese making the virus is reduced but studies showed 
that it is not inactivated completely during this process and thus still present in the cheese, 
although at a lower concentration. [10] From here infection of consumers happens through 
the consumption of these products, given the virus is still present in the product at the time 
of consumption.

Model Development 
After the initial qualitative approach with the risk pathway a more quantitative method was 
used to describe the pathway of the virus, and this was done by making a model which should 
calculate the desired output, namely the number of people getting TBE from unpasteurized 
milk based on the different parameters influencing this output. 
The first model that was developed was based on available data from TBEV endemic countries 
like Poland and Slovakia. The goal was to predict the number of TBE cases in Denmark in the 
future, but since there was no cases of TBE due to consumption of unpasteurized milk in 
Denmark, data were used from other countries. 
The initial model development was done by a brainstorming process where parameters and 
variables that influence the different steps in the pathway were identified.  
The model was developed by starting at the end of the pathway, this being the desired output 
of the model, namely the number of ill persons from the consumption of unpasteurized milk. 
The next step was then identifying which steps or parameters this was dependent on. The 
number of ill persons was estimated to be dependent on 2 parameters, the probability of 
getting ill from one consumption and the number of consumptions.

Data Collection 
Since the model was mostly based on prevalence the focus was to find data of tick, cow and 
milk prevalence. Most data were found from scientific articles using DTU-Findit as a search 
engine. To find literature about the virus the search words “TBEV”, “Milk”, “Tickborne 
Encephalitis Virus”, “prevalence”, “cows”, “ruminants”, “ticks”, “Ixodes Ricinus” were mostly 
used and combinations of the above words to narrow in on the desired information. The 
number of articles was rather low when searching for TBEV and milk together. When 
searching for those two phrases together in DTU-Findit it gave 59 hits, and when adding other 
phrases to specify the search more, the search yielded even less results. The amount of 
information regarding TBEV in milk was scarce, and many of the articles stated the same 
things and came to the same conclusion, “the milk should be pasteurized before 
consumption”. It was possible to find prevalence data, but concentration data or dose-
response relationship data of TBEV was not found which limited the complexity of the model.

incidence of TBE through milk. Countries like Slovakia was used because the country having a 
lot of alimentary TBEV cases and outbreaks, and a lot of data from these. When finding 
prevalence data, there were two parameters that the prevalence of TBEV from the different 
sources were based on. The first was virus RNA which was mainly investigated in the milk and 
in ticks. Table 1 shows the prevalence data collected from scientific literature, including origin

Stochasticity 
The next step in the development process was to apply stochasticity to the model parameters 
which had uncertainty in them. The following section goes through the different parameters 
that were made stochastic, explaining the approach and the distributions used for these 
parameters. 
Beta Distribution 
The beta distribution is often used to describe the uncertainty or variation of probability or 
prevalence and is also used for this purpose in several variables in the stochastic model in this 
project. The beta distribution ranges from 0-1 which makes it ideal when dealing with 
probabilities. It is both used for the different prevalence data and the alpha variable. The beta 
distribution is viable for these variables since they are based on prevalence and probability, 
respectively. The beta distribution takes two inputs, α and β. To fit the prevalence data to 
these variables α is the number of successes +1 and β is the number of sampled – the number 
of successes +1 in a trial or dataset. For example, if a study sampled 100 animals and 10 of 
them were TBEV positive, α would be 10+1= 11 and β would be 100-10+1 = 91, so the beta 
distribution would be written as beta(11,91). [11] A nice aspect of the beta distribution is its 
flexibility, since changing the two input parameters α and β, changes the shape of the 
distribution and can therefore easily be used to fit certain parameters.

maximum value(max). The pert distribution is exclusively used for modelling expert opinions 
where expert provide the three parameters. The mean of the pert distribution is calculated

Distribution for Alpha variable 
The Alpha variable describes the probability of getting TBE from a consumption of 
contaminated milk. The value is derived from outbreak data from Slovakia in the period 2012-
2016, a total of 13 outbreaks, see Table 2. However, there was a large difference in size of the 
outbreaks. Some outbreaks involved only 2 persons where another outbreak involved 500 
persons. This difference in size of the outbreak makes it so that not only is there a lot of 
uncertainty tied to the small outbreak cases in terms of infectivity but also, the small 
outbreaks have the same weight on the alpha value as the large outbreaks. To incorporate all 
the outbreaks in the variable, a beta distribution was made for each of the 13 outbreaks. One 
sample was taken from each distribution and the mean of these samples were taken. This 
process was repeated 10 000 times yielding the final distribution for alpha which can be seen 
in Figure 3, and the r-code for the distribution can be seen in Box 3.

Tick Prevalence Distribution 
The tick prevalence distribution was made in the same way as the Alpha value distribution. 
Data of TBEV prevalence in ticks were collected from scientific literature and a beta 
distribution was made for each study, one sample was taken from each distribution, the mean 
of the beta distributions were taken and this was replicated 10 000 times yielding the 
distribution. This was initially done with data from both tick nymphs and adults combined and 
since it is almost exclusively adult ticks that feed on larger animals like cows, the distribution 
was later changed to only include the adult ticks. As mentioned earlier, if a tick is infected, it 
will carry the virus for the rest of its life cycle and therefore adult ticks generally has a higher 
prevalence of TBEV than the younger nymphs. This means that if the tick prevalence were 
estimated based on nymphs and adult combined there would be an underestimation of the 
TBEV prevalence in the model. 
The data for the tick prevalence varied a lot in terms of prevalence, and when making the 
distribution, the data was split into two different distributions because the difference in 
prevalence was so high that there was no overlap in the 95% confidence interval between 
some of the cases. Therefore, two different distribution were made instead of one. The first 
included the low prevalence data and the other included the high prevalence. The data was 
sorted by looking at the 95% confidence interval for each study, and if there was an overlap 
of the intervals between studies, they could be used together. Table 4 shows the data used 
in each distribution. There are zero positive samples in all the data used for the low TBEV tick 
prevalence distribution, but since the shape parameters for each beta distribution is 
calculated by #positive +1 and #sampled - #positive +1 this will give the shape parameters 1 
and 286 respectively, for the first low tick prevalence data. This will yield a very low 
confidence interval, but still greater than zero, which estimates a very low TBEV prevalence 
in ticks. This assumes that there is at least one positive tick in the population, which is correct 
to assume in this case. This assumption is correct, since the areas where the ticks are collected 
are known to have TBEV in its population of ticks and the limited number of ticks collected 
meaning that the prevalence is so low that even after sampling 200-500 ticks as in the used 
data, the probability of finding an infected tick is still very low.  
Having two distribution instead of one is beneficial in this case since one can be used for 
simulating low TBEV prevalence in ticks and the other for high prevalence in a scenario 
analysis.

Tick/cow prevalence model (Final Model) 
One big limitation of the deterministic model was that the ticks were not included in the 
model. To get a good estimate of the risk that TBEV might pose, it was critical to include the 
ticks in the model as well. In the deterministic model they were left out due to the lack of a 
good relation between prevalence in ticks and cows. However, using a model similar to the 
single hit exponential Dose-Response model [32], a relation was made between the two. A 
function for estimating the prevalence of TBEV in cows were made using data and expert 
opinions on tick prevalence and abundance. Using this approach, the data of infected cows 
were not used as input in the model but was calculated from the data on tick prevalence. The 
amount of data on tick prevalence was far greater than the data on cow prevalence making it 
more robust. 
Estimating the prevalence of infected cows started with the ticks infecting the cows. The 
information needed was the mean number of ticks per cow. This data combined with the 
prevalence of infected ticks yielded the number of infected ticks per cow. It was expected 
that the probability that an infected tick infects a cow is close to 1(100%) (Niels Ladefoged 
Nielsen, personal communication). Using this information together with the number of 
infected ticks per cow the probability that 1 cow will be infected can be calculated using the 
modified exponential dose-response model. The approach to implementing this in R is 
described in Box 5.  
prevcowest <- function(n_min, n_mode, n_max)  
{                                      
       sims <- 10000                         
       logni <- rpert(sims, n_min, n_mode, n_max)     
       ntick <- round(10^logni,0)          
       ptick <- replicate(10000,mean(c(rbeta(1,1,519),rbeta(1,1,468), 
     rbeta(1,1,402),rbeta(1,1,371),rbeta(1,1,286)))) 
        ni1 <- rbinom(sims,ntick, ptick)      
        a_par <- rbeta(10000,99,1)     
        Pcowi <- 1-exp(-a_par*ni1)           
        Pcowest <- mean(Pcowi)                
        return(Pcowest)  
} 
 
uncprev <- replicate(10000,prevcowest(0,2.15))   
 
Box 5: R-code for estimating TBEV prevalence in cows 
The estimated prevalence of infected cows was made as a function with three variables, n_ 
min, n_mode and n_max. These are the minimum, most likely and maximum number of tick 
per cow which was acquired from the expert opinions from cattle veterinarians.  
sims is the number of cows. 
logni gives the distribution of ticks on each cow, since the abundance of ticks on cows are 
based on expert opinions a Pert distribution was used for this variable, n_min, n_mode and 
n_max were used in the Pert distribution. 
ntick is rounding logni to whole numbers, removing any decimals since it is ticks which are 
quantified, and it does not make sense to use half a tick for example.  
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
ptick takes the mean of each beta distribution, where each one represents one sample of tick 
prevalence data, then replicates this 10 000 times to yield a distribution of the combined tick 
prevalence, as explained in the Tick Prevalence Distribution section. 
ni1 expresses the number of infected ticks on each cow, using a binomial distribution using 
the values from ntick, ptick and sims.  
a_par is the probability that an infected tick infects a cow, which was said to be very close to 
1, so a beta distribution was made simulating this.  
Pcowi is the exponential dose-response model used to calculate the probability of a cow being 
infected and Pcowest taking the mean of all the values from Pcowi and the whole function 
returns the value of Pcowest. This is the expected prevalence of infected cows. Usually hen 
using the exponential dose-response model an r-value is used instead of a_par and a dose is 
used instead ni1. The r-value describes the probability that a single microorganism passes 
through the gastrointestinal tract and causes disease, and the dose is the amount of food 
ingested. The approach is the same but these variables has been replaced.  
uncprev replicates the prevcowest function several times (10 000) which gives a distribution 
of all the values given from the 10 000 iterations. This distribution describes the uncertainty 
of the prevalence function.

Discussion 
Data Uncertainty 
In this project, the amount of data available was scarce. To the best of my knowledge, there 
are no other quantitative risk assessments made on TBEV through unpasteurized milk. This 
might be because the virus has not been as extensively studied as other viruses, for example 
norovirus. This is likely because TBEV is not as large a foodborne concern as other viruses at 
the moment, but since a spread of the virus is seen in large parts of the world it is important 
to consider, also considering the severe course of disease from TBE infection. Since no 
information regarding a concentration-based dose-response relationship of TBE has been 
found this risk assessment focused on prevalence data and developed a model based on these 
data to predict the probability of getting TBE from consuming unpasteurized milk. Outbreak 
data was used to estimate the probability of getting ill from a consumption of TBEV infected 
dairy product. This was a way of estimating a dose response relationship without using 
concentrations. It gave a probability of illness from a consumption of infected milk, but 
without considering the concentration of virus in the milk. The lack of information regarding 
concentration and dose means that there is uncertainty around this estimate and to reduce 
this, a dose-response relationship including concentrations of virus would be needed. The 
model is also based on prevalence data, which also has its limitations compared to 
concentration data. When using prevalence data, the only knowledge you get is if the virus is 
present or not, but no information of at what concentrations. The model does not consider 
the dose, estimates are calculated based on one consumption of milk, but it is not specified 
how much one consumption actually is, and if implementing a concentration-based dose-
response model the actual dose would also need to be specified. 
The prevalence data was also divided into different types, meaning they based the prevalence 
on either virus RNA in the sample or virus specific antibodies in the sample. Virus RNA was 
common in milk samples where antibodies were analyzed in ruminants. Even though they 
both indicated prevalence of the virus, the RNA was the virus itself meaning that the virus 
was present in the sample at the time, but the antibodies are a response to the virus and can 
be present in the sample long after the virus has been excreted. This means that even if the 
sample is antibody positive it is not certain that the sample contains the virus itself it just says 
that it has at some point and thus gives some uncertainty about the presence of virus. A study 
also investigated if there was any relation between antibody presence and virus RNA in milk, 
but no relation was found.[4] This was only a concern in the deterministic model. In the final 
stochastic model, the prevalence data used was from ticks where virus RNA was measured, 
and it is known that if a tick is infected it will carry the virus for the rest of its lifecycle which 
also makes the stochastic more reliable and robust than the deterministic. [6]

Geography of Data 
The prevalence and outbreak data originated from other European countries like Poland, 
Slovakia, Sweden and Norway. When searching for data countries were chosen that were 
located in central, eastern or northern Europe so that the climate was somewhat similar. It 
should be noted that the environment in these countries is different from Denmark and since 
the ticks carrying TBEV require certain environmental conditions, the abundance of ticks in 
fields with grazing cows are largely dependent on the environment. However, the tick 
abundance estimates used in the model are from danish expert opinions, so these estimates 
are based on the danish environment and not Slovakia or Norway for example. It is uncertain 
how the abundance of ticks in these areas will look in the future, but since the climate changes 
so far have resulted in a spread and increase of ticks in the environment it is likely that there 
will be a further increase in the future. Also, there is a difference in how cows are grazed in 
Denmark compared to other countries. In Denmark milking cows are often permanently kept 
inside while being milked and thus are not exposed to the ticks in the fields during milking. 
(René Bødker, personal communication) This will affect the actual prevalence of TBEV in 
milking cows in Denmark compared to other countries as well. However, these factors will 
likely give an overestimation of the risk estimate. Even though overestimating the risk is not 
desired, since it can result in too strict control measures or preventive actions it is better than 
underestimating the risk. However, organic livestock is kept outside as well, and 
unpasteurized milk being sold is likely mainly from organic milk since they are usually 
considered to follow some of the same trends, like sustainability and health and the tick 
abundance is likely higher for organic cows, compared to conventional milking cows. 
Mixing 
Generally, when dealing with low exposures of a virus or other pathogens, there is a almost 
linear relation between the dose and the response on a dose-response curve. This means that 
if dealing with low exposure, it can be assumed that a certain increase or decrease in dose, 
will result in the same increase, or decrease in response. The same linear relation applies for 
the probability of exposure and the probability of illness (Håkan Vigre, personal 
communication). Milk is generally mixed in large tanks with milk from many cows before 
selling or producing cheese. Since the prevalence of TBEV in cows is typically low, 0.5% for 
low TBEV prevalence in ticks and 3% for high TBEV prevalence in ticks, in areas with low tick 
abundance which is calculated from the cow prevalence function. A tank consisting of milk 
from 100 cows would therefore only have 0-3 cows infected with TBEV and the virus dose in 
the milk would be highly diluted. It can therefore be assumed that the concentration of virus 
will be very low and thus the assumptions stated above are valid here.  
The model calculates the risk of infection from the consumption of milk, based on the 
prevalence of TBEV in one cow, so the mixing step is not included. However, since the mixing 
is not included, the probability of exposure is underestimated but the probability of infection 
is overestimated because the milk is diluted. Since the exposure is low there is a linear relation 
between these two parameters which means that the underestimation of the probability of 
exposure is equal to the overestimation of the probability of infection, meaning they will 
cancel each other out and adding mixing to the model would therefore not make a difference 
on the output. 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
Alpha-variable 
For the alpha variable, the data used to calculate this variable is based on sheep milk cheese 
and not cow milk. Since there is a lack of data from cow milk outbreaks these were used 
instead, and they were estimated to yield a representative estimate of the infectivity. 
However, this extrapolation makes the final risk estimate more uncertain. If the estimate will 
be over or underestimated because of this is hard to say, there is no information about the 
amount of virus shed in milk in sheep compared to cows, there is likely a difference between 
the two animals because of their biology but how large this difference is not known. One thing 
to note is since the variable is based on cheese, it is likely lower than if it was raw milk because 
there is a reduction of the virus concentration during the cheese making process, but 
according to studies made there are still traceable amounts of virus left in the cheese. [9] 
Also, many of the outbreaks reported in the literature are small family outbreaks where only 
a few people were exposed. Because of the size of the outbreaks the data was highly 
uncertain and were discarded due to the uncertainty. As a result, only two outbreaks were 
used that had size large enough to yield a representative estimate. The smaller outbreaks had 
a higher ratio of ill/exposed than the larger outbreaks. Whether this is because the smaller 
family outbreaks shared the same cheese or milk and were exposed to the same dose where 
the larger outbreaks involved many individuals and purchases made over a larger timespan is 
unknown. It could also be that in the smaller outbreaks only the people who got ill reported 
the consumption and therefore people who were exposed but did not have any symptoms 
did not report the consumption and were not included in the reported outbreak. This lack of 
knowledge also contributes to the uncertainty of the estimate, and the large outbreaks were 
in the end used as they had less uncertainty compared to the small outbreaks, because of 
their size. 
Virus Transmission 
In the model, it is assumed that if infected, the cow sheds the virus in the milk. To the best of 
my knowledge there is no information about the likelihood of a cow shedding virus in the milk 
once infected so for safety, this assumption was made. The value of this parameter affects 
the risk estimate from the model proportionally, meaning that if decreasing the value by 20%, 
from 1 to 0.8, the risk estimate received from the model would also be 20% lower. This means 
that this parameter has a large impact on the output of the model and since there is no 
knowledge of this probability, this assumption could skew the estimate from the real value a 
lot. If hypothetically, the real probability was 20% this parameter alone would overestimate 
the risk estimate by 80%. However, since there is no information about this, the probability is 
assumed to be 100% in the model, this is most likely an overestimation of the real value, but 
it is used as a conservative precaution towards the risk, based on the lack of knowledge. This 
assumption does not affect the weight of the parameters investigated in the scenario analysis, 
it only affects the risk estimate itself.

Probability of Infection after Tick Bite  
Another source of uncertainty in the model is that when an infected tick bites a cow, it is 
assumed that the cow gets infected. This is because the virus is present in the salivary glands 
of the tick and therefore the virus can be transmitted briefly after attachment. However, not 
all exposure of virus will lead to infection, the immune defense of the cow will try and repel 
the virus and there is a biological variability between animals which also will influence the 
probability of infection. There is no information about the probability of infection per bite of 
infected tick so the model assumes that if an infected tick bites the animal, the animal will get 
infected as well. This assumption will also result in an overestimation of the risk estimate 
provided by the model compared to the real value. Just like the assumption made regarding 
the virus transmission, this does not affect the weight of the parameters investigated in the 
scenario analysis but only the risk estimate. 
Model Strengths and Weaknesses 
The model itself was rather short and simple in its coding. This was mainly because of the lack 
of information on different parameters like the dose-response. Since there is no 
concentrations-based dose-response relationship available it limits the complexity of the 
model. One option could be to make assumptions about the dose-response and 
concentrations, but this would most likely make the model more uncertain than what 
excluding it would. The model could therefore be made much longer and much more complex 
but based on the information that is available, it was not feasible. One big advantage of the 
simplicity of the model is the risk of typing errors in the code is smaller than it would have 
been if the code were longer and more complex. This also impacts the overall uncertainty of 
the model, because errors in the code can have just as big an impact on the risk estimate as 
other sources of uncertainty. However, due to the simplicity and the size of the code it is 
manageable to search for errors and the amount of error in the code is expected to be low.

The first scenario gave a risk estimate of 0.46 TBEV cases per 1000 consumptions of

going to influence the risk estimate as much as they would if the tick abundance were high. 
Preventive measures 
Even though the model holds a lot of uncertainty and is most likely overestimating the risk of 
TBE through unpasteurized milk because of several parameters, the model and the scenario 
analysis still gave information about what parameters had the highest impact on the risk 
estimate compared to the others. If there is an increase in TBE observed in Denmark through 
unpasteurized milk in the future, this can be useful for determining which areas are worth 
focusing on compared to the others. 
Based on the observations from the scenario analysis the best area to focus on when trying 
to control or reduce the occurrence of TBEV in unpasteurized milk in Denmark, given TBEV 
has become a health concern in the country is the abundance of ticks. This parameter was 
responsible for the largest increase in the risk estimate. Even though the TBEV prevalence in 
ticks also had a large impact on the risk estimate, it is hard to control the spread of TBEV in 
nature since it is spread by the vector ticks which are spreading to new areas due to climate 
change. TBEV is also more common in the neighboring countries to Denmark like Sweden, 
Germany and Poland, so if ticks spread TBEV further in these countries it might spread across 
the borders to Denmark. However, even though it is likely that TBEV will spread to Denmark, 
we cannot know for certain that it will, and if it does, we do not know how long it will take 
and to what extent it will spread. Outbreaks through the consumption of raw milk in Denmark 
is frequently seen due to other pathogens like Shigatoxin-producing E.coli (STEC,VTEC), that 
means there is already a route of exposure to pathogens through raw milk in Denmark right 
now, so if TBEV increases in the future, it will also likely pop up as either sporadic cases or 
outbreaks.[31] If that happens it is likely that there has been an increase of TBEV in Denmark 
and if so, effective and efficient control options for reducing the abundance of ticks and the 
consumption of unpasteurized milk are therefore the parameters that should be focused on 
to reduce the overall risk. 
==PAGE:37==
37 
 
To reduce the number of ticks on each cow it is a matter of removing the cows from areas 
with desirable environmental conditions for ticks, such as meadows, forest edges or other 
areas with high vegetation which benefit the ticks ability to hunt for hosts. It is also important 
to avoid keeping the milking cows in areas where wild animals like deer are living or often 
passing through since they naturally carry a lot of ticks. This method of prevention is cheap to 
implement since it does not require any testing, vaccination or other treatment but is merely 
a geographical change of livestock. It should also be noted that it is only if the livestock is 
grazing in high-risk areas that this is necessary, so not all farms will necessarily be subject to 
change in this area. A challenge with this could be to convince farmers to move or restrict 
their grazing areas if some of it is in the high-risk areas, farmers might not see the problem or 
might not think it is worth the effort. 
Another cheap and possibly effective way of reducing the number of people getting TBE from 
the consumption of unpasteurized milk is communication to the consumers and thus reducing 
the consumption. When it comes to reducing the consumption of unpasteurized milk, it is 
important to reach out to the people who consume these products. Since the consumption 
of unpasteurized milk can be closely tied to certain food trends like raw food, it is crucial to 
find ways to educate about the danger of these products, since one of the big driving forces 
of these trends are that the products are more nutritional when consumed unpasteurized, 
but the possible consequences of consuming them are mentioned less frequently 
communicated by “promoters” of these trends. Therefore, a good option would be 
information campaigns about the risks of these unpasteurized milk products. 
Vaccination of people living in endemic areas have been effective in reducing the number of 
TBE cases in other countries and could also be a viable option if TBEV becomes a larger health 
concern in the future. Austria is a good example that had a large decline in TBE cases after 
mass vaccination resulting in approximately 80% of the population being immunized against 
TBE.[6] However, such an enormous vaccination campaign is very expensive and might not be 
economically feasible compared to other preventive measures. 
Since the project is considering unpasteurized milk, pasteurization is not a viable preventive 
measure. However, it should be noted that it is the most effective way of eliminating TBEV in 
milk and no other measures are as effective. It also eliminates other pathogens like STEC 
where the other preventive measures discussed above only targets TBEV.

Conclusion 
In this project, a model for predicting the risk of TBE infection through the consumption of 
unpasteurized milk in Denmark was developed based on prevalence and outbreak data. 
Valuable information about this risk was obtained through the development and scenario 
analysis of the model. 
The scenario analysis showed that the abundance of ticks had the highest impact on the risk 
estimate. If pasteurization of milk is not used as a preventive measure, reducing the number 
of ticks on the cows, for example by restriction of grazing to areas with low tick abundance is 
an effective method of reducing the number of infected people with TBE through 
unpasteurized milk. It should be noted that ultimately, the best preventive measure for 
dealing with TBEV in milk is pasteurization, and no other preventive measures are more 
effective. If pasteurization is not carried out, the risk of TBE infection will always be higher 
than if pasteurization was used, no matter what other preventive measures were used 
instead. 
The largest source of uncertainty comes from the assumptions made about the parameters 
describing the probability of a cow being infected if bitten by an infected tick and the 
probability of an infected cow shedding the virus in the milk. These assumptions were made 
because of a lack of knowledge regarding these parameters and as a result, the model is most 
likely overestimating the real probability of TBE through unpasteurized milk. Because of this 
lack of knowledge, it is difficult to get a reliable estimate of this risk. To make a more reliable 
prediction about the risk of TBE through unpasteurized milk, more knowledge of these 
parameters are needed. However, these assumptions are not affecting the weight of the 
parameters investigated in the scenario analysis and does not affect the conclusion.

Future Perspectives 
To make a more realistic model in the future, which can predict the expected number of TBE 
cases from unpasteurized milk more accurately, it is necessary to gather more information 
about several parameters. A concentration-based dose-response relationship for TBEV would 
be crucial to achieve this. With this information it would be possible to determine the 
probability of infection based on concentrations instead of prevalence, which is more 
informative and would be a more accurate estimate of the infectivity compared to the 
estimation based on the outbreak data, which was used in the model used in this project. This 
would also require information about the concentration of TBEV in milk which to the best of 
my knowledge, is difficult to find. 
Information regarding the probability that the virus is transmitted to the milk if the cow is 
infected is also missing, therefore it was assumed that the virus always got transmitted to the 
milk. If information regarding this was available, it could be used in the model, which would 
yield a more accurate estimate. 
Information about the probability that the cow gets infected with TBEV, if bitten by an 
infected tick would also reduce the uncertainty in the model and yield a more accurate 
estimate. 
The number of ticks on cows is based on expert opinions from cattle veterinarians in this 
report. These are estimates based on their observations in their daily work. Even though these 
are reliable and realistic estimates, they hold a lot of uncertainty which adds to the 
uncertainty of the model. If sampling of danish cows or other ruminants were made and 
analyzed for number of ticks it could be used in the model and reduce the uncertainty of this 
parameter. 
Looking at environmental parameters and tick abundance to predict abundance of ticks in 
different areas and mapping this data together with raw milk producing farms can be a good 
way of predicting the risk of milk containing TBEV in these farms, using Geographic 
Information Systems for example.

#function estimating TBEV prevalence in cows, using low tick prevalence 
prevcowest_lowtick_prev <- function(n_min, n_mode, n_max) #input variables are expert opinion of 
tick abundance on cow 
{                                      
       sims <- 10000 #sims is number of cows 
       logni <- rpert(sims,n_min,n_mode,n_max) #number of ticks per cow, expert opinion 
       ntick <- round(logni,0) #rounding the tick abundance, removing decimals 
       ptick <- tickprev_low #TBEV prevalence in ticks 
       ni1 <- rbinom(sims,ntick, ptick)  #Number of infected ticks per cow    
       a_par <- rbeta(10000,99,1)   #Probability that 1 infected tick infects cow 
        Pcowi <- 1-exp(-a_par*ni1)   #Exponential dose-response model, using a_par and ni1 instead of 
r-value and dose        
        Pcowest <- mean(Pcowi)       #takes mean of the Pcowi, probability of cow being infected        
        return(Pcowest)  
} 
#function estimating TBEV prevalence in cows, using high tick prevalence 
prevcowest_hightick_prev <- function(n_min, n_mode, n_max)  
{                                      
        sims <- 10000                          
        logni <- rpert(sims,n_min,n_mode,n_max)  
        ntick <- round(logni,0) 
        ptick <- tickprev_unweighted_high 
        ni1 <- rbinom(sims,ntick, ptick)      
        a_par <- rbeta(10000,99,1)     
        Pcowi <- 1-exp(-a_par*ni1)           
        Pcowest <- mean(Pcowi)               
        return(Pcowest)  
}

        prev.cont.milk<-prev.inf.rum*virus.t.milk 
        n.tickbite <- prev.inf.rum/prev.inf.tick 
        prob.ill.cons <- prev.cont.milk*alfa 
        n.ill.cons.year <- prob.ill.cons*n.cons 
        n.ill.cons.year 
}
