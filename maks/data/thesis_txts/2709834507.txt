1. Abstract 
 
The Kara, Laptev and East Siberian seas receive a large amount of dissolved organic 
matter (DOM) originating from Siberian rivers. This study demonstrates the utility of 
DOM fluorescence (FDOM) measured in situ combined with temperature and salinity, 
as a freshwater tracer, for the identification of the origin, modification and pathway of 
water masses in the upper Arctic Ocean. The data originates from the ARKTIKA 2018 
expedition and offers an extensive spatial coverage of the distribution of DOM along 
the Siberian shelf, slope and basin. A robust approach to the calibration of the in situ 
CDOM fluorometer to laboratory measurements was developed and applied. The 
results clearly distinguish FDOM signals associated with different branches of Atlantic 
water entering the Arctic Ocean (~ 0.01 nm-1) and the shelf signals originating from 
Siberian rivers (> 0.05 nm-1), resolving the eastward propagation of river plume due to 
atmospheric forcing. FDOM along with the influence of sea ice annual cycle provides 
a better insight into the formation mechanisms of halocline waters and three possible 
scenarios are proposed; convection in open ocean or influenced by shelf processes and 
an advective mechanism. FDOM is applied to distinguish between these scenarios and 
to decipher complex mixing processes between halocline and Atlantic waters in 
Siberian Arctic. Finally, the present study highlights the potential use of high resolution 
in situ DOM fluorescence for the fractionation of freshwater contributions into the 
Arctic Ocean in lieu of δ18O-H2O measurements.

2. Introduction 
 
2.1 Major Arctic gateways 
Helland-Hansen and Nansen (1909), 113 years ago, investigated for the very first 
time the oceanic exchanges between North Atlantic with the Arctic Ocean through 
subarctic seas. Arctic Ocean including the Nordic and adjacent shelf seas is often 
characterized as Arctic Mediterranean (Aagaard et al., 1985; Rudels, 1987; Tomczak 
and Godfrey, 1994; Rudels et al., 1999) since it is almost entirely surrounded by land 
(North America, Eurasia, Greenland) with four main gateways to the major global 
oceans – the Fram Strait (FS), Barents Sea (BS), Bering Strait and Canadian 
Archipelago (Tomczak and Godfrey, 1994). North Atlantic waters flow into the Arctic 
through FS and BS. FS is the deepest gateway (> 2500 m) that is located between 
Greenland and Svalbard archipelago (Fahrbach et al., 2001) and it is considered to be 
the most significant entrance to the Arctic Ocean (Rudels et al., 2013; Cottier and 
Steele, 2016). Aagaard et al., (1973) reported a northward transport of AW via West 
Spitsbergen Current (WSC) of ~ 8 Sv, whereas estimates of the total AW fluxes through 
BS varies seasonally and have been calculated between 1.2 to 1.8 Sv (Skagseth et al., 
2011; Rudels et al., 2013). Relatively fresh Pacific water flows into the Arctic through 
Bering Strait, which is a relatively narrow and shallow passage (~ 45 m depth, 85 km 
wide) located between North America and Asia (Tomczak and Godfrey, 1994). Main 
outflow of the polar surface and deep waters is passing through the west side of FS 
southwards and the returning modified Atlantic water that has circulated around the 
Arctic Ocean also exits through FS along with almost 90% of ice export (Rudels, 1987). 
A significant part of polar surface waters export also takes place in Canadian 
archipelago flowing through with Baffin current through Davis Strait into the Labrador 
Sea (Rudels, 1987). All four gateways play a key role in controlling and regulating the 
Arctic inflows and outflows with a corresponding impact on thermohaline circulation, 
sea ice and freshwater (FW) export as well as heat content of the Arctic Ocean and the 
adjacent seas  (Karami et al., 2021). 
2.2 Arctic Ocean hydrography 
Arctic Ocean exhibits a special and complex topography (Herman, 1974) and 
consists of four deep basins—Nansen, Amundsen, Makarov and Canada basin—
separated by mid-ocean ridges (Cottier and Steele, 2016) (Fig. 1A). Characteristic of 
the Arctic Ocean is the extensive and shallow continental shelves that cover 
approximately the two-thirds of the Arctic (Herman, 1974). Arctic Ocean is strongly 
stratified with four distinct layers (Fig. 1B); Polar mixed layer (PML), Halocline layer 
(HC), AW and Arctic deep water. PML follows the strong seasonal cycle of ice 
formation and melting, where convective mixing and re-stratification are triggered 
during winter and summer, respectively, thus controlling its depth (Korhonen et al., 
2013; Stedmon et al., 2021). According to Rudels et al., (1996), PML during the 
summer is homogeneous with low salinity and freezing temperatures due to sea ice 
melt, whereas in winter it is removed and stored as sea ice that results in a freezing and 
isohaline layer down to the bottom of the convection. Next summer the remnant of 
previous winter layer (RWL) is indicated by a temperature minimum below the summer 
==PAGE:7==
7 
 
meltwater, which often defines the thickness of the summer PML or when temperature 
minimum is ambiguous, a sharp salinity gradient is used instead (Rudels et al., 1996; 
Korhonen et al., 2013; Stedmon et al., 2021). The lower boundary of the PML varies 
regionally and seasonally and its average thickness in summer for Laptev and EES is 
around 10 m (Tarasenko et al., 2021). Seasonal ice formation and melting is essential 
to maintain PML at low temperatures and salinity, however river runoff into the Arctic 
and low salinity Pacific water also provides an important contribution to the relatively 
fresh PML (Aagaard et al., 1981; Rudels et al., 1996).  
Below PML, there is the Arctic HC layer whose significance lies in the fact that acts 
as a barrier and efficiently insulates the Arctic sea ice and atmosphere from the upward 
release of sensible heat, from the warm Atlantic water at depth (Coachman and 
Aagaard, 1974; Aagaard et al., 1981). The strength, stability and thickness of the Arctic 
halocline has recently been proposed as a new climate change indicator (Pemberton and 
Nilsson, 2016; Polyakov et al., 2018). The halocline layer is considered a distinct water 
mass typically with a depth range of approximately 30 to 300m and it is separated into 
the upper and lower halocline (UHC and LHC) depending on location (Rudels et al., 
2013). The UHC originates from Bering strait inflow and to some extent also riverine 
contribution, and thus is often called Pacific halocline as it only extends over Canada 
and Makarov basins. Similarly, LHC is referred as Atlantic halocline, as constitutes the 
main halocline layer over Nansen and Amundsen basins, however it expands until 
Canada basin, submerged below Pacific halocline (Bauch et al., 2011) (Fig. 1B). LHC 
extends from the ~33 isohaline depth down to 0 °C isotherm (Dmitrenko et al., 2011; 
Korhonen et al., 2013; Bauch et al., 2016), which is therefore considered the boundary 
between LHC and AW. Average temperature within the LHC remains close to the 
freezing point (Cottier et al., 2016), however the upper part of the LHC (~ 30-100m) is 
the coldest part (Polyakov et al., 2018).  
2.2.1 Halocline formation mechanisms 
The formation and maintenance of the halocline layer has been widely investigated 
and discussed already from the 1960s to date (Coachman and Barnes, 1962; Aagaard et 
al., 1981; Rudels et al., 1996; Dmitrenko et al., 2011; Anderson et al., 2013). It is 
reasonable that linear mixing between PML and AW is not possible to produce the near-
freezing temperatures of the LHC (Aagaard et al., 1981). There are two possible 
mechanisms for explaining the origin of the Arctic LHC; the advection and convection 
mechanisms (Aagaard et al., 1981; Rudels et al., 1996; Cottier and Steele et al., 2016). 
Aagaard et al. (1981) proposed the salinization of the shelf water during winter ice 
formation. In autumn, after the ice-free conditions caused by summer heat, ice is 
extensively formed but there is also a continuous sea ice production during winter in 
coastal polynyas, areas of open water created by strong katabatic winds that drive the 
ice away from the coastline (Bauch et al., 2011; Martin, 2019). Winter ice formation 
on the Arctic shelves causes brine to be expelled from the ice crystals and to be 
accumulated in the underlying water column, significantly increasing the shelf water 
salinity (Fig. 1C). Subsequently, these cold and highly saline waters are laterally 
advected off the continental shelf, entering the Arctic basins below PML, forming LHC 
water (Aagaard et al., 1981; Cottier and Steele, 2016). Rudels et al., (2004) suggested 
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
that LHC within Eurasian basin is mostly formed on BS shelf. New ice formation over 
the shelf will induce more brine release and thus, maintain the LHC layer. 
Off the shelf, LHC layer is also maintained with winter convection processes 
(Rudels et al., 1996; Bauch et al., 2011). The convective mechanism includes several 
seasonal cycles of sea ice melt and formation. Initially, warm and saline AW, enters the 
Arctic through FS and when it gets in contact with sea ice, melted water creates a well-
mixed at freezing temperatures surface layer, which then propagates north of BS where 
is subject to winter convection, due to freezing and rejection of brine (Rudels et al., 
1996). Thus, a deeper mixed layer with high salinities and near-freezing temperatures 
is created and retained until next melting season, when the salinity will get lower at the 
very surface, resulting in a seasonal halo- cline (Boyd and Steele, 1998; Cottier and 
Steele et al., 2016). Winter convection due to brine release will follow once again and 
after several seasonal cycles, the convective halocline layer will be well-established. 
However, LHC waters may be result of a combination of these mechanisms (Rudels et 
al., 2004; Dmitrenko et al., 2011). Below Arctic halocline layer there is the pronounced 
AW layer that lies at depth between ~ 300 to 900 m, characterized by relatively high 
temperatures, salinity and oxygen (AW in WSC has ~ 4°C and salinity > 35)(Herman, 
1974; Aagaard et al., 1981). Finally, below AW there is the Arctic deep water that has 
negative temperatures and salinity of ~ 34.9 (Herman, 1974) (Fig. 1B). 
 
2.3 Circulation of the upper Arctic Ocean  
Arctic Circumpolar Boundary Current (ABC) is a topographically driven system of 
currents that flows cyclonically along the continental slope of the Arctic deep basins 
(Aagaard, 1989; Rudels et al., 1999; Aksenov et al., 2011). The largest volume of warm 
AW enters the Arctic Ocean through FS with WSC defined as Fram Strait branch (FSB) 
whereas another branch enters BS with Norwegian Coastal Current as Barents Sea 
branch (BSB) (Rudels et al., 1999). FSB is warmer and more saline than BSB and flows 
eastwards as soon as enters Arctic, along the Eurasian continental slope (Aksenov et 
al., 2011; Rudels et al., 2013). BSB instead has lost heat since winter convection often 
reaches the bottom depth of BS (~250m) (Rudels et al., 1999; Schauer et al., 2002). 
BSB continues eastwards and enters the Nansen basin through St. Anna Trough, where 
joins the FSB into ABC (Aksenov et al., 2011). When the two branches meet north of 
Severnaya Zemlya (SZ) (Fig. 1A) they flow next to one another creating a strong frontal 
zone due to their different hydrographic properties (Rudels et al., 1999). ABC continues 
along the continental slope from SZ to Laptev Sea (LS) and gets significantly modified 
along the slope due to shelf processes (Aksenov et al., 2011; Rudels et al., 2013) until 
it reaches Lomonosov Ridge (LR) where ABC bifurcates due to the physical 
bathymetric barrier (Rudels et al., 1999). Only a small fraction of BSB water as 
boundary current remnant crosses the LR and the rest of the ABC water gets deflected 
into Amundsen basin and gradually is advected towards FS with Transpolar Drift 
(Aagaard, 1989). Even though there is evidence that a part of ABC circulates along the 
slope around the entire Arctic (Rudels et al., 1999), the fate of ABC after crossing LR 
is less studied (Woodgate et al., 2007).

2.4 Biogeochemical oceanographic tracers in Siberian Arctic 
Water mass circulation and chemical characteristics has traditionally been studied in 
the Arctic Ocean based on temperature and salinity properties (Aagaard et al., 1981; 
Rudels, 1989; Rudels et al., 1999). Ostlund and Hut, (1984) were the first to introduce 
the water mass balance in the Arctic Ocean using stable isotopes of water (2H/1H and 
18O/16O). Water chemistry measurements, including inorganic nutrients (nitrogen, 
phosphorus, silicate) (Jones et al., 1998; Ekwurzel et al., 2001; Nitishinsky et al., 2007; 
Zhuang et al., 2020), NO- values (NO- = 9xNO3− + O2) (Broecker, 1974; Alkire et al., 
2010), trace elements such as Barium and Radium (Guay and Falkner, 1997; Cooper et 
al., 2008; Thomas et al., 2011; Kipp et al., 2019) and stable oxygen isotopes (Ostlund 
and Hut, 1984; Grebmeier et al., 1990; Bauch et al., 1995, 2005, 2011) have been used 
for the chemical characterization of Arctic waters, FW and Pacific water contributions, 
for deciphering the biogeochemical processes in Siberian seas as well as the origin and 
influences of LHC waters.  
2.4.1 CDOM properties and use as tracer 
Colored or chromophoric dissolved organic matter (CDOM) is defined as the 
fraction of DOM that absorbs light over a wide range of visible and UV wavelengths 
(Stedmon et al., 2003; Coble, 2007). Fluorescent DOM (FDOM) is the part of CDOM 
that fluoresce at UV and visible wavelengths upon UV excitation (Coble, 1996; 
Stedmon et al., 2003). CDOM is operationally defined as “dissolved” after filtration 
through 0.2 to 0.8 μm pore size filters (Coble, 2007). The laboratory-based excitation-
emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence spectroscopy allows the collection of multiple 
emission scans at numerous excitation wavelengths combined into excitation-emission 
matrices (Coble, 1996) and along with the application of Parallel Factor Analysis 
(PARAFAC) can be separated into independent underlying components (Stedmon and 
Markager, 2003). Visible wavelength fluorescence (referred to as peak A with Ex/Em: 
260/380-460 and peak C with Ex/Em: 350/420-480) are associated with humic-like 
substances, and correlated to lignin-derived phenols near rivers, whereas UV 
fluorescence is often associated with protein-like compounds (Yamashita and Tanoue, 
2004). FDOM measurements can be carried out with in situ optical sensors to map 
DOM distribution in high resolution, mounted to CTD units (Belzile et al., 2006; 
Yamashita et al., 2015), Bio-Argo floats (Xing et al., 2012), Ice Tethered Profilers 
(ITPs) (Dmitrenko et al., 2019; Stedmon et al., 2021) and oceanographic mooring 
arrays (Dmitrenko et al., 2021). Optical properties of DOM and specifically the 
absorption of blue light makes CDOM also a great tool for ocean color remote sensing 
applications (Coble, 2007; Gonçalves-Araujo et al., 2018; Heim et al., 2018). The rapid 
development of these sensors greatly facilitates data collection and provides with high 
resolution profiles, however, the establishment of a universal calibration dataset for 
CDOM in situ fluorometers and account for the necessary corrections still remains a 
challenge within the research community (Yamashita et al., 2015; Stedmon et al., 
2021). 
The optical properties of DOM and specifically the dynamics of FDOM have 
extensively been studied in aquatic environments from lakes to estuaries, coastal seas 
and ocean, and DOM distribution in Arctic has gained special attention over the last 
decade (Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Murphy et al., 2010; Stedmon et al., 2011; 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
Downing et al., 2012; Granskog et al., 2012; Gonçalves-Araujo et al., 2015, 2016; 
Hölemann et al., 2021). Guay et al., (1999) were the first to utilize in situ DOM 
fluorescence measurements as a proxy for dissolved organic carbon (DOC) in the 
Arctic. CDOM contributions to total DOC spans from 20% in open oceans and up to 
70 % in coastal areas where river runoff loaded with organic matter mixes with marine 
waters (Laane and Koole, 1982). Siberian shelf has been characterized as “sensitive 
environmental recorder” and has gained interest as a key region for climate studies and 
for the better comprehension of Arctic marine carbon cycling (Bauch and Kassens, 
2005; Hölemann et al., 2021). DOC in the Arctic Ocean has three main potential 
sources; river runoff, seawater and biological origins (Anderson and Amon, 2015) of 
which the largest concentrations are originated in river waters (Stedmon et al., 2011; 
Amon et al., 2012). Drainage basin of the major Arctic rivers combined—Lena, Ob, 
Yenisei, Kolyma (Siberia) and Yukon, Mackenzie (North America) covers a vast area 
corresponding approximately to the size of Canada (Amon et al., 2012). While Arctic 
only constitutes 1% of the global ocean in volume, it receives approximately 10% of 
global river discharge (Cooper et al., 2008; Anderson and Amon, 2015) and thus Arctic 
Ocean receives large amounts of allochthonous DOM (Anderson and Amon, 2015). 
According to Amon et al., (2012) only the three greatest Siberian rivers (Lena, Yenisey 
and Ob) are responsible for up to 90% of terrestrial DOC entering Arctic Ocean and 
Lena river alone accounts for approximately 5.6 Tg y-1 of DOC drained into LS (Amon 
et al., 2012; Wild et al., 2019).  
Given the great amount of riverine water entering the Arctic Ocean, FDOM 
constitutes an efficient and sensitive tracer for monitoring the distribution and fate of 
DOM in the Arctic, a proxy for DOC supply from Arctic rivers (Guay et al., 1999; 
Stedmon et al., 2011) as well as a great tool for tracing water masses circulation in the 
Arctic Ocean (Stedmon et al., 2021; Williford et al., 2021). Köhler et al., (2003) report 
that DOC entering the Arctic from rivers is mostly refractory due to the conservative 
mixing behavior it exhibits, however more recent studies have indicated that during 
spring, Arctic rivers export DOC to the Arctic, from which up to 40% is labile (Amon 
et al., 2012; Granskog et al., 2012). FSB and BSB also supplies Arctic with lower 
concentration of DOC due to its modification after crossing Greenland-Scotland ridge 
and primary production on the Arctic continental shelves as well as sea ice algae 
production also constitute an important source of biologically produced DOM (Smith 
et al., 1997; Anderson and Amon, 2015). It is expected that VS has a significant impact 
on the FW budget of LS since approximately the half of the annual discharge from Ob 
and Yenisey rivers is flowing through VS (Janout et al., 2015). The complex 
topography, particularly the swift currents, the presence of several water masses, 
shallow depths and the 250 km-long submarine Vilkitsky Trough (VT) that guides the 
Kara Sea waters into the ABC are key characteristics for resolving the riverine export 
into the Arctic shelf seas. High concentrations of CDOM have been found into the 
Arctic halocline in Eurasian basin (specifically carried with Transpolar drift) (Stedmon 
et al., 2021; Williford et al., 2021) as well as in East Greenland Current, suggesting the 
injection of DOM into the LHC along with brine during ice formation 
(Fig.1C)(Stedmon et al., 2011; Granskog et al., 2012), highlighting in this way the 
contribution of shelf processes to the formation of the LHC layer. Laptev and East 
Siberian Seas (LS-EES) are highly influenced by the FW export and the distribution of 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
CDOM in the region reveals conservative mixing with river water and how sea ice 
processes affect the hydrographic properties on the Siberian shelf seas (Hölemann et 
al., 2021).  
2.5 Aim 
The overall aim of the present study was to evaluate the distribution of FDOM along 
the Siberian shelf and its utility in resolving oceanographic processes, using high 
resolution calibrated in situ fluorescence measurements. The first hypothesis was that 
visible wavelength DOM fluorescence, due to its conservative behavior, could possibly 
act as an additional tracer to temperature and salinity for deciphering the origin 
(convective/advective) and pathway of different water masses. Furthermore, it could 
provide a novel approach in tracing modified waters due to sea ice processes and 
riverine input from Siberian rivers. A second hypothesis was that in situ DOM 
fluorescence could be used in lieu of oxygen isotopes to fractionate freshwater 
contributions from sea ice melt and riverine discharge, without the necessity of an 
extensive oxygen isotopes sampling program. 
3. Materials and methods 
 
ARKTIKA-2018 expedition was conducted on board R/V Akademik Tryoshnikov 
from 21st August to 24th September 2018, as a joint cruise between the Nansen and 
Amundsen Basin Observational System (NABOS) and The Changing Arctic 
Transpolar System (CATS) projects. In total, 145 oceanographic stations were 
conducted, organized in 10 transects — nine cross-shelf named by the approximate 
longitude at the slope and one shallower capturing the outflow from Kara Sea through 
VS. In this study, transects are presented from the West to the East (Fig. 2), following 
the inflow of Atlantic water into the Arctic Ocean along the slope. Stations were 
categorized according to the bottom depth to shelf (< 200 m), slope (200-2000 m) and 
basin (> 2000 m). Standard in situ oceanographic parameters were recorded with a 
Seabird SBE911+ conductivity-temperature-depth (CTD) (bottle; Alkire and Rember, 
2019, CTD; Polyakov and Rember, 2019) fitted with double conductivity and 
temperature sensors and equipped with additional biochemical sensors mounted onto 
the rosette. Since the focus of the present study is surface and halocline waters the 
dataset was quality checked and subset to the upper 300 m, whereas potential 
temperature (θ) — calculated with TEOS-10 (Mcdougall et al., 2010) — and practical 
salinity (Sp) were used throughout the analysis. Since this study focuses on the Eurasian 
basin, LHC will be considered for the analysis and thus will be referred as HC.  We 
also refer to the remnant of the previous winter mixed layer (RWL) within the HC, the 
parcel of sub-surface waters that deviates at maximum 0.5 °C from the freezing point. 
In situ FDOM measurements were recorded using a WETlabs Eco-FLCDRTD CDOM 
fluorometer (excitation at 370 nm and emission at 460 nm). The raw data collected by 
the in situ fluorometer are reported in voltage units (Volts [V]).  
Seawater samples for CDOM absorption and fluorescence analysis were collected 
in 50 mL amber glass vials (acid-washed and pre-combusted) from discrete depths 
using the twenty-four 10 L Niskin bottles, mounted on the rosette. CDOM samples were 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
filtered through a 0.2 μm Millipore filter cartridge directly attached to the rosette 
sampler. All CDOM samples were kept in the dark and well-sealed for protection 
against oxidation at ~ + 4 °C to further prevent microbial growth and to avoid 
photodegradation of DOM (Coble, 2007). Oxygen isotope samples were collected 
unfiltered in 20 mL borosilicate glass vials, wrapped with Parafilm to prevent 
evaporation, stored at room temperature and subsequently were shipped to the Oregon 
State Stable Isotope Laboratory for analysis on a Finnegan Mat 251 mass spectrometer 
with the CO2 equilibration method (± 0.02 ‰ uncertainty)(Alkire and Rember, 2019). 
All stable isotope ratios were calibrated with Vienna Standard Mean Ocean Water 
(VSMOW) and are reported with the δ notation (hereafter δ18O). FDOM was measured 
at Technical University of Denmark-National Institute of Aquatic Resources with a 
Horiba Aqualog spectrofluorometer and CDOM absorption on a Shimadzu UVPC2501 
with a 1 cm and 10 cm quartz cuvette, respectively and MilliQ water as blank. An 
absorbance-based algorithm was applied on FDOM data for inner filter effect correction 
(Murphy et al., 2010; Kothawala et al., 2013). The CDOM samples collected were used 
for the calibration of the in situ fluorometer in Raman units (nm-1) (Lawaetz and 
Stedmon, 2009).

4. Results 
 
4.1. FDOM Distribution on Siberian Shelf 
Hydrographic transects 
Overall, the hydrographic sections of θ, Sp and FDOM exhibit distinct patterns as 
we go from the western to the easternmost transects (94°E to 167°E). Vilkitsky Strait 
(VS) is a special case as it is not following the pathway of the AW entering the Siberian 
seas north of SZ (Fig. 2) and it represents a pure BS inflow along with riverine signal 
from Siberian rivers west of LS (Ob and Yenisey). Only the most representative 
transects will be thoroughly presented and discussed in this study, however all the 
hydrographic sections and selected stations depth profiles are available in the Appendix 
(Fig. A1, A2).  
4.1.1. Transects west of Lomonosov Ridge 
The 94°E is the westernmost transect located north of Severnaya Zemlya and has 
four main features. Surface waters (< 20 m) are fresh (Sp < 32.5) and have temperatures 
between -1.52 and +0.84 °C (Fig. 4; 94°E/A-B). DOM fluorescence in surface waters 
is lowest over the basin (< 0.01 nm-1) and highest over the slope. HC layer lies just 
below and expands from 20 to ~70 m at the southernmost part of the section but reaches 
further down to 90m at the northernmost station. These waters have temperatures close 
to freezing point, salinities between 33.8 and 34.8, and low FDOM (< 0.02 nm-1). Below 
this layer for most of the transect there are warmer (θ>0°C) more saline waters (Sp> 
34.8), with comparable low DOM fluorescence. These three distinct water types are 
clearly apparent in the property-property plots for St. 6 (Fig. 4; 94°E/D-E). At the 
southern end of the transect over the slope deeper waters differ, with temperatures 
below zero and elevated DOM fluorescence (St. 9, Fig. 4; 94°E/E). Comparing the 
property-property plots from stations at contrasting ends of the transect, suggest little 
overlap indicating different water column structure and water mass origins. 
The VS transect exhibits unique characteristics as being the gateway of the relative 
fresh Kara Sea outflow to the LS. In the surface waters (< 25 m) salinity ranges between 
27.08 and 34.32 whereas θ between -1.33 and +2.82 °C. The maximum temperature 
coincides with relatively fresh and DOM-rich waters (Sp < 28.5, FDOM > 0.05 nm-1) 
just above the south slope of the Vilkitsky Trough (Fig. 4; Vilkitsky Strait/A-E), 
whereas in the northern part of the transect surface waters show higher salinities and 
lower FDOM (Sp > 30, FDOM < 0.02 nm-1). Below 25m the HC extends down to 250 
m in the south and 150 m in the northern part of the transect, with freezing temperatures 
found at different depths over the section due to advection of RWLs with different 
convection depth from BS. Salinities in HC range between 32.95 and 34.6 and FDOM 
is between 0.02-0.03 nm-1 at the southern part and less than 0.02 mn-1 at the northern 
end. A striking feature here is the apparent absence of the typical warm core of AW at 
depths below the HC, distinguishing the VS’s hydrographic properties from the other 
transects in this study. Below the 34.5 isohaline, θ is gradually increasing, yet remaining 
below 0 °C. Above salinity 34, St. 18 and 19 exhibit a conservative decrease of FDOM 
with increased salinity (Fig. 4; VS/E, Fig. 5; VS) and overlap in FDOM-Sp space 
indicating that these waters originate from the same water mass in the Barents Sea. 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
The surface waters (< 25 m) of the 126°E transect are relatively fresh, warm (θ > +4 
°C, Sp<30) and have high DOM fluorescence (FDOMMAX = 0.07 nm-1), at the 
southernmost end on the shelf (Fig. 4; 126°E/A-C) reflecting the strong riverine plume 
signal. At the northern part of 126°E, surface waters are characterized with near-
freezing temperatures and north of 81°N are relatively fresh (< 31.5) with slightly 
elevated FDOM signal (~0.03 nm-1) compared to immediately below where it gradually 
reduces to a stable minimum value of 0.01 nm-1. HC is present from ~ 25 m to ~100 m 
characterized mostly by near-freezing temperatures, but the negative temperatures 
extend deeper following the continental slope at depth greater than 300 m. Within the 
HC, FDOM follows the same pattern as the surface layer, with higher fluorescence 
(0.02-0.05 nm-1) at the south and northernmost end of the transect whereas oceanic 
signal (0.01 nm-1) is detected at the central area of the transect. 
 Below HC, a well-defined warm and saline core of AW is observed (θ > 0 °C, Sp > 
34.8) and with low FDOM (< 0.02 mn-1). However, a relatively constant FDOM signal 
of ~ 0.02 nm-1 was observed coinciding with the on-slope negative temperatures up to 
220 m. On FDOM-Sp and θ-Sp property plots, the surface FDOM and θ (~20m) have 
clearly separated signals of FDOM and θ, which are following the way from shelf

despite being the northernmost station. Above salinity 34, we observe that St. 31(basin) 
and 45 (slope) significantly overlap (Fig. 4; 126°E/D, Fig. 5;126°E), with St. 31 
exhibiting slightly increased FDOM values and higher θ at Sp compared to St. 45. On 
the contrary, St. 39 and 33, at the middle of 126°E represent pure AW at depth with the 
lowest FDOM values and highest temperatures among the selected stations. 
4.1.2 Transects east of Lomonosov ridge 
The northernmost basin stations of the 143°E transect are located marginally west 
of LR, whereas all the other stations are situated on the shallow continental shelf 
(mainly < 70 m). At the surface 25 m, there is an area of warm (θ > +3 °C), relatively 
fresh water (Sp < 28) with high FDOM (> 0.06 nm-1). This appears in the middle of the 
transect starting above the shelf and expanding to the basin (Fig. 4; 143°E/A-C). A 
second warm and low salinity surface core with relatively high FDOM is observed off 
the slope (θ > +1 °C, Sp < 30, FDOM > 0.04 nm-1). At the southern end of the transect 
surface waters where cold, had the lowest salinities and were depleted in DOM. Below 
them FDOM increased with salinity (St. 69) in waters with near-freezing temperatures 
(Fig. 4; 143°E/D-E). FDOM ranges between 0.01 and 0.06 nm-1 in the HC with the 
higher values overlapping with the sharp salinity gradient over the shelf. St. 65 captures 
an almost conservative behavior of FDOM over a wide range of salinity.  Above a 
salinity of 34 the mixing lines of St. 55 and 63 deviate notably (Fig. 4; 143°E/E, Fig. 
5;143°E). At St. 55 (furthest offshore) FDOM exhibits conservative behavior up to 
34.5, where a kink on FDOM-Sp space is observed and thereafter FDOM remains 
approximately constant with increasing salinity.  
160°E transect patterns resemble those of 143°E, with warm and relatively fresh 
surface waters (< 25 m, θ > 2 °C and Sp < 28.5) (Fig. 4; 160°E/A-C), with the warmest 
core found at the middle of the section over the shelf. HC extends from around 25 m to 
150 m with near-freezing temperatures found almost throughout HC. Below 150m a 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
warm Atlantic layer with θMAX = +1.49 °C, same as observed for AW at 143°E, 
implying same water mass circulating at depth for both transects. FDOM reveals a 
widely spread distribution of high DOM fluorescence signal in surface and sub-surface 
waters above shelf, slope and basin (FDOM > 0.06 nm-1). At the southernmost and 
northernmost end of the section the surface signal decreases to less than 0.04 nm-1. 
FDOM weakens with increasing depth and 0.03 nm-1 contour reaches down to 70 m. 
Below this point, intrusions of water with higher FDOM signal are visible as a kink on 
the FDOM-Sp space of St. 89 and 94 at approx. 34 salinity (Fig. 4; 160°E/D-E, Fig. 5E) 
and St. 96 falls on the same river water mixing line as seen in the other transects. 
The easternmost transect 167°E revealed distinct oceanographic patterns compared 
to the other sections. Negative temperatures cover a thick layer from the surface down 
to approximately 200 m with near-freezing temperatures observed mainly in a thinner 
subsurface layer at ~50 m depth (Fig. 4; 167°E/A-C). At the surface (< 25 m), there is 
a low-salinity core (< 28) with low FDOM values (< 0.02 nm-1) above the shelf, but 
surface FDOM gets higher over the slope and basin (>0.03 nm-1). An intrusion of waters 
with intermediate FDOM signal (0.04 nm-1) is observed just above the slope and 
extends to the surface, whereas FDOM maximum (0.05 nm-1) within the HC at ~ 70 m 
depth is dominated by freezing temperatures and salinities < 34 (Fig. 4; 167°E/D-E). 
The 0 °C isotherm lies at approximately 200 m depth, below which there is a warm core 
of AW with θMAX > +1 °C.  FDOM-Sp property plot of St. 75 (slope) and 80 (basin) 
reveals a positive linear relationship up to 32 salinity with almost constant freezing 
temperatures and a negative correlation for more saline deeper waters above 32 salinity 
(Fig. 4; 167°E/D-E). The close overlap of the two selected stations in FDOM-Sp space 
indicates no difference in the origin of surface, HC and AW core waters.  
Overall, it is apparent that there is a thickening of the HC layer as we proceed from 
the west to the east and past the LR. This is clear as the 33 isohaline extends much 
deeper east of LR (~ 70 m) than west of the ridge (~ 25 m). The same occurs with 0 °C 
isotherm, which deepens as we move east along the shelf. There is also a progression 
in organic matter fluorescence starting at the westernmost transect 94°E, characterized 
by the typical Atlantic derived low FDOM, and a gradually increasing FDOM to 160°E 
to exhibit the most wide-spread and thick FDOM layer observed over the study area. 
At the easternmost transect 167°E the FDOM maxima is then submerged. The cooling 
and freshening of AW on-slope (ABC) and off-slope is observed from the west to the 
east (Fig. A3).

4.2.2. Fractionation based on δ18O and FDOM 
 
δ18O measurements were only available for the transects 126°E, 143°E, 160°E and 
167°E. δ18O was positively and linearly correlated with salinity and greater scattering 
relative to the direct riverine mixing line is observed for waters below 34 salinity. The 
deviation from the AW-river mixing line in the δ18O-Sp property plot (Fig. 8A) reflects 
the processes of net sea ice melting and formation.  During sea ice formation and melt, 
the changes in salinity are sharper than the alteration in δ18O values and it has been 
reported that isotopic enrichment factors are between 1.5 and 2.7 ‰ in Arctic waters 
(Ekwurzel et al., 2001). The datapoints lying above the mixing line are mainly surface 
waters (< 10 m) indicative of excess sea ice melt, whereas the data below the line mostly 
corresponds to sub-surface waters with freezing temperatures, where we would expect 
to find waters that have been through freezing during last winter (RWL) and thus carry 
a signal of excess brine. However, the datapoints below the mixing line reflect not only 
ice formation but also waters mixed with river runoff. The black circles in Fig. 8;A-B 
corresponds to water samples from the surface of 143°E and 160°E transects that reflect 
the signal of riverine plume observed in hydrographic sections in Fig. 4 (143°E and 
160°E; A-E). The observations within the circle in δ18O-Sp plot are the only samples 
significantly deviating from freezing temperatures (with θ > 1.5 °C).

5. Discussion 
 
5.1. Calibration - Factors affecting fluorescence signal in surface 
waters 
In situ fluorometers have been extensively developed and used for more than 20 
years in both freshwater and marine environments to trace DOM, however sensitivity 
limitations compared to benchtop spectrofluorometers and the establishment of 
universal calibration methods are yet some challenges to be overcome (Petrenko, 1997; 
Tedetti et al., 2010; Carstea et al., 2020). Fluorescence signal can be affected by a 
variety of factors such as temperature, turbidity due to particulate organic matter 
(POM), UV radiation or microbial activity that could cause significant deviations of the 
in situ records relative to laboratory-based measurements (Tedetti et al., 2010; Carstea 
et al., 2020; Shigemitsu et al., 2020). Therefore, calibration algorithms and data 
correction of in situ fluorometers have a crucial impact on the final fluorescence data 
quality and comparability among different studies. In this dataset, before the application 
of the depth criterion, the surface waters below 11 m systematically deviate from the 
regression line (Fig. 3C). The deviation was found to be positive (ΔFDOM), meaning that 
FDOMLaboratory overestimated FDOM relative to the in situ fluorescence measurements.  
There are several reasons that could explain the increased FDOMLaboratory signal at 
surface waters. It is well established that intermolecular interactions (thermal 
quenching) caused by an increase in temperature can reduce fluorescence intensity of a 
sample (Cyr et al., 2017; Shigemitsu et al., 2020). However, the temperature range in 
our calibration dataset was considered small (-1.81 to 2.22°C) and thus, no temperature 
correction on fluorescence data was considered necessary. Another factor that could 
have an effect on FDOM in surface waters is light attenuation of both excitation from 
the in situ sensor and also emission of the fluorophore (Downing et al., 2012; Carstea 
et al., 2020). Turbidity due to suspended material, commonly encountered in surface 
waters, could cause the absorption of the emitted light from non-fluorescent material or 
the scattering of either the excitation or emission light. Thus, a light attenuation-induced 
underestimation of FDOMIn situ relative to FDOMLaboratory is expected, as seawater 
samples were filtered prior to the laboratory analysis. Downing et al. (2012) showed 
that light attenuation due to high turbidity in freshwater systems could have a significant 
impact on fluorescence of 80-90% reduction in FDOM if the sample is not filtered, 
whereas open-path in situ fluorometers exhibited a 22% reduction in FDOM in 
relatively low turbidity waters. However, instrument-specific experimental set-ups are 
required in order to obtain reliable correction factors. Similar findings were acquired 
by Saraceno et al. (2009) for closed-path instruments. 
Photodegradation due to UV radiation could also produce deviation between in situ 
and laboratory measurements. Grzybowski et al. (2019) demonstrated that partially 
photodegraded seawater samples (0.8 μm pore size filtered) underwent dark recovery 
of FDOM during storage at room temperature (~ 20 °C). The degree of DOM 
fluorescence regeneration was dependent on wavelength of observation and at those 
relevant to sensor used here, a 16% increase in fluorescence intensity of irradiated water 
from the Baltic Sea was observed, which in absolute values corresponded to 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
approximately 0.01 nm-1 (Grzybowski et al., 2019). In such case, UV induced 
photodegradation of CDOM in surface waters caught by the in situ sensor could 
possibly end up in a dark recovery of the seawater samples after storage, explaining the 
higher fluorescence intensity observed in laboratory measurements. In conclusion, this 
could question the viability of using in situ fluorescence measurements from the very 
surface, especially during the day.  
 
5.2. Fram Strait and Barent Sea branches FDOM signals 
It is well proven that FSB and BSB exhibit distinct oceanographic properties, with 
the core of FSB (~ 250 m) being slightly warmer and more saline than the core of BSB 
(Rudels et al., 2004; Rudels, 2010; Aksenov et al., 2011; Dmitrenko et al., 2011). HC 
waters from these sources also differ but with the opposite pattern.  BSB derived 
halocline waters (BSB HC) (~ 50 m), are warmer and more saline than the colder and 
fresher FSB HC (Rudels et al., 2004; Aksenov et al., 2011; Dmitrenko et al., 2011). 
Dmitrenko et al., (2011), showed consistent differences between on-slope and off-slope 
HC waters, concluding — in agreement with Rudels et al., (2004) — that BSB HC 
gains a significant amount of heat and salt from the underlying AW due to an enhance 
vertical mixing on the slope. In θ-Sp property plots, HC waters are usually recognized 
by a distinct kink close to the freezing point (Aagaard et al., 1981; Rudels et al., 2004; 
Dmitrenko et al., 2011). This kink is often absent from stations over the continental 
slope (Woodgate et al., 2001; Dmitrenko et al., 2011). The results of the present study 
are in accordance with the aforementioned findings, with slope stations exhibiting 
warmer and more saline HC waters whereas colder and fresher AW core waters. Θ-Sp 
property plots of 94°E consists a good example of these differences and it is assumed 
that St. 9 (slope - lacks the sharp kink of the HC) and St. 6 (basin) are representative of 
BSB and FSB waters, respectively (Fig. 4; 94°E/D) both for the HC and AW core. 
These two stations exhibit the same behavior on the θ- Sp space that has previously been 
reported by other authors and associated with contributions from different AW branches 
(Woodgate et al., 2001-Fig. 9; Rudels, 2010-Fig. 4; Rudels, 2004-Fig. 6, Dmitrenko et 
al., 2011-Fig. 7). In other transects, the θ-Sp properties overlap at depth and thus muddle 
the potential distinct signals of the branches. The convergence of both branches into the 
ABC creates a front with large horizontal gradients in θ and Sp making the distinction 
between the FSB and BSB more challenging (Rudels et al., 1999).  
We assume that the difference in oceanographic properties of the two AW branches 
would also be reflected in the DOM concentrations that these two branches may carry, 
given their different pathways into the Arctic Ocean. There has not been any evidence 
in the past that FSB and BSB would have a distinct DOM fluorescence footprint, but it 
is assumed that BSB would have a slightly increased FDOM signal, since it originates 
from the shallow BS and may have come in contact with seafloor sediments. According 
to the findings from 94°E, we consider a threshold of 0.015 nm-1 for the AW HC FDOM 
end-member with the highest value 0.015 nm-1 corresponding to BSB and 0.008 - 
0.01nm-1 being related to FSB. Difference in AW branches reflects also to the AW core 
at depth, with the colder and fresher BSB exhibiting also a slightly higher DOM 
fluorescence (BSB; 0.01 nm-1 and FSB; 0.008 nm-1). The convective origin of the FSB 
HC does not imply any addition of FDOM, thus the FDOM variance between FSB HC 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
and FSB core is expected to be very low. On the contrary, we can expect a slightly 
increased signal within the HC of BSB waters relative to the BSB core, since HC is 
formed due to shelf processes and FDOM is accumulated within the halocline layer 
along with brine. 
5.3 Riverine water export and FDOM distribution 
Vilkitsky Strait  
Observations from the present study agree with oceanographic properties of the 
water masses observed in VS in previous studies (Janout et al., 2015, 2017), confirming 
the presence of low salinity waters at the southern part of the strait and elevated FDOM 
values is the confirmation of the increased riverine export originating from Ob and 
Yenisey into the LS. The co-occurrence of FDOM signals on FDOM-Sp space (Fig. 4; 
VS/E), suggests that the thick cold and saline layer observed in Fig. 4; VS/A originate 
on Kara Sea shelf and is advected through VS towards Eurasian basin. Kara Sea shelf 
is highly influenced by surface plume waters of Ob and Yenisey, and thus during sea 
ice formation, DOM is trapped along with brine in the subsurface. All three selected 
station in VS transect exhibit the same conservative behavior above 34 salinity (parallel 
to the riverine reference mixing line) and their overlapping implies no differences in 
the sources of HC. High resolution ocean model along with in situ observations suggest 
that VSC after exiting VT merges into the ABC and circulates along the continental 
slope (Janout et al., 2015). Janout et al. (2017) support that these dense shelf-
transformed waters at the bottom of VT as they enter Arctic basin, feed the HC layer, 
cool the AW over ABC and potentially ventilate the deeper Arctic waters.  
Wind forcing has a direct impact on surface advected flows and river plumes and 
specifically when N or NE winds prevail in Kara Sea, the FW is constrained on the 
shelf and thus the outflow through VS is minimum (Weingartner et al., 1999; Osadchiev 
et al., 2020). Westerly winds would also restricted the spread of Lena’s river plume 
towards the basin and force the plume to spread over ESS, resulting in increased PML 
salinities at northern LS (Janout et al., 2015). The KS and LS FW export are not directly 
correlated with the Arctic Oscillation (AO) but AO could alternate the summer pressure 
systems predominating in the Siberian Seas (Janout et al., 2015). During July 2018 a 
cyclonic atmospheric circulation pattern was generated over the Siberian Arctic and in 
combination with a positive AO index resulted in an eastward propagation of the 
riverine waters towards ESS, creating a negative salinity anomaly east of New Siberian 
Islands and a positive salinity anomaly north of LS (Dmitrenko et al., 2005; Bauch et 
al., 2016; Bolt, 2021; Tarasenko et al., 2021). Therefore, the river plume from Ob and 
Yenisey as a surface signal coming from VS is expected to have joined Lena river’s 
plume and the mixed riverine signal to have propagated to a northeastern direction. 
Pugach et al., (2018) have reported that main driver of DOM distribution in ESS is 
wind forcing and it is common that during summer Lena’s plume is expanding to the 
western ESS and characterized by low salinity and high DOM waters, agreeing with 
the observed results of this study.  
Special features west of Lomonosov ridge 
The overlapping FDOM signal of HC and AW core for St. 45 (slope) and 31 (basin) 
(Fig. 5; 126°E) implies connectivity between waters with a specific DOM load on the 
==PAGE:36==
36 
 
continental slope at 126°E and waters with an almost identical biogeochemical signal 
of FDOM at the northernmost station of 126°E. Three assumptions are made for the 
explanation of this pattern. The first hypothesis is in accordance with findings of 
Tarasenko et al., (2021) — a study that has been conducted based on the same θ-Sp 
ARKTIKA 2018 CTD data — where they state that katabatic winds pushed further 
offshore the riverine water when Lena river discharge was the highest and the plume 
has propagated up to 81°N below the sea ice. FDOM distribution in Fig. 4; 126°E/C, 
shows low fluorescence at the middle of the section and an increased signal from the 
surface over depth at northernmost St. 31, which possibly rejects this anomaly being 
only a surface event. The second hypothesis assumes a mesoscale eddy-induced 
northward transport of slope waters along with their increased FDOM signal. Woodgate 
et al., (2001) have successfully recorded cold core eddy features with cold and low 
salinity core properties in LS in the upper 300 m, possibly of shelf origin, which 
matches our observations at St. 31. Mesoscale eddies are often responsible for the 
offshore export of biogeochemical tracers, DOM and POM originating from the 
continental shelf (Kamenkovich et al., 2009; Marchal, 2019). Janout et al., (2015) 
declare that the special topographic changes near VT could lead to barotropic and 
baroclinic instabilities and thereafter possibly generate eddies that could carry a 
significant load of Kara Sea freshwater into the Eurasian basin. What’s more, Spall, 
(2013) supports based on models that the halocline is maintained by lateral eddy fluxes 
from the ABC. The third hypothesis suggests the re-circulating of ABC water after 
deflecting into the interior of the basin due to the physical barrier of LR and thereafter 
enters the Amundsen Basin recirculating current (ABRC) (Lenn et al., 2009). ABC 
bifurcation at LR results also in a heat flux divergence that would cool the AW core 
water both passing to the west of LR but also the waters merging with the ABRC 
(Rudels et al., 1994; Lenn et al., 2009). In relation to this hypothesis, we consider that 
waters at the northernmost stations of 126°E are former slope waters that have been 
deflected back to the Amundsen basin.  
East of Lomonosov ridge 
In Fig. 4; 143°E/C the FDOM section exhibits a double core of FDOM surface 
maximum. We assume that the central core could be related to the Lena’s plume export 
eastwards whereas the second core could be a remnant of VS or mixed riverine signal 
export to the basin that has entered ABRC. Tarasenko et al. (2021) identify the second 
core as a previous entrainment of Lena River after series of wind-forced upwelling and 
downwelling events that has subsequently pushed the second core over to the basin.  
Sea ice processes also play a major role in controlling the DOM distribution in LS and 
ESS and Hölemann et al., (2021) especially highlight the significance of land-fast ice 
formation and melting in the fate of DOM in Siberian shelf seas. The transects located 
east of LR (ESS; 143°E, 160°E and 167°E) are the most affected by sea ice meltwater 
as well as ice formation as it is obvious from the FW fractionation results (Fig. 10). 
During August 2018 pack ice was only partly covering 167°E whereas during 
September 2018, transects 160°E, 167°E and the southern part of 143°E were 
completely covered in ice (NSIDC, 2021), however the September ice extent is 
probably related to drifting pack ice as a consequence of enhanced advection following 
the wind patterns (Itkin and Krumpen, 2017). Sea ice growth and melt cycle has an 
==PAGE:37==
37 
 
important impact on the FDOM-Sp relationship and especially DOM that fluoresce at 
visible wavelengths is more likely to be rejected from the ice crystals and concentrate 
in the brine, relative to UV-fluorescent DOM which has been found to accumulate in 
the ice, possibly due to biological activity within the ice (Müller et al., 2013; Granskog 
et al., 2015a). On the contrary, sea ice melting is a low-salinity source that significantly 
dilutes PML and consequently any DOM fluorescence signal that would have been 
present in the surface waters (Granskog et al., 2015b; Hölemann et al., 2021). DOM 
has been trapped within the HC of 167°E during sea ice formation on Siberian shelf and 
the overlap with near-freezing and DOM-depleted water indicates the influence of sea 
ice meltwater. 
5.4 Formation scenarios of previous winter mixed layer remnant 
and non-freezing halocline waters 
 
Freezing temperatures have been associated with convective origin of HC 
(Woodgate et al., 2001; Rudels et al., 2004) and specifically RWL refers to waters that 
represent the conditions of the lower part of previous winter’s PML before stratification 
is initiated due to summer ice melt and river runoff (Granskog et al., 2009). Focusing 
on RWL origin is beneficial since we able in this way to narrow down the parameter of 
time and thus, offer an overview of the spatiotemporal processes along Siberian shelf, 
slope and basin. Striking feature of the RWL analysis is the bimodal distribution of 
FDOM in RWL (Fig. 11). One peak represents high frequency of low FDOM 
observations with salinities above 33 and the second one lower frequency of higher 
FDOM values in fresher waters below 33 salinities. Since RWL is connected to winter 
convection due to brine rejection and has near-freezing temperatures, FDOM provides 
a hint for whether the formation of this RWL has been influenced by shelf processes or 
not. Based on deviation from freezing and FDOM signals, three possible origin 
scenarios are proposed in this study; formation of RWL (deviation from freezing < 0.5 
°C) due to winter convection a) without shelf influence (oceanic convection) with low 
FDOM, b) with shelf influence (shelf convection) with high FDOM and c) formation 
of HC (deviation from freezing > 0.5 °C) on shelf seas with high FDOM and 
subsequently being advected to the sampling region (advection). Figure 11f presents 
the data grouping according to a 10% threshold in FRFDOM and serves as an additional 
proof of the binary origin of RWL. Based on those scenarios, one can assess the origin 
of RWL and non-freezing HC waters along the transects of ARKTIKA 2018. 
94°E slope waters have advective origin from BSB HC whereas basin RWL is 
formed with oceanic winter convection from low FDOM surface waters resulting also 
in DOM-depleted RWL. Transects 126°E and 143°E share a common pattern; RWLs 
above the shelf formed with shelf convection i.e. surface waters mixed with river plume 
(high FDOM) got frozen during previous winter and thus DOM is accumulated in the 
RWL along with brine. Surface waters in Lena estuary have a residence time of 
approximately 2 months (Alling et al., 2010), whereas in LS it has been estimated to be 
minimum one year (Bauch et al., 2013; Tarasenko et al., 2021). The time mis-match 
between peak discharge and propagation of the plume due to atmospheric forcing 
(Weingartner et al., 1999; Pugach et al., 2018) combined with the residence times of 
surface waters on Siberian shelf seas (Dmitrenko et al., 2008), implies that riverine 
==PAGE:38==
38 
 
water could have frozen up in the study area during previous winter. Slope non-freezing 
HC waters of 126°E and 143°E is believed to have been formed possibly on BS shelf 
and have been advected with ABC along the slope, as their relatively high FDOM 
suggests. In 126°E basin RWL exhibits a combination of the convective mechanisms; 
low FDOM at the middle of the section implies oceanic convection whereas high 
FDOM values at the northernmost end could possibly be explained by the re-circulated 
slope waters within Amundsen basin that have undergone another freezing cycle during 
previous winter.  
Basin RWL in 143°E is believed to have been formed by shelf convection justified 
by an extended high FDOM at the surface and subsurface. 143°E is the last transect 
where the slope stations are located west of LR. This is obvious from the absence of 
the non-freezing HC in 160°E and 167°E, since the whole subsurface is dominated by 
freezing temperatures as remnant of previous winter PML. High FDOM in surface and 
sub-surface waters along both transects imply formation of RWL with shelf convection 
processes. Specifically, the bulge spotted on FDOM-Sp plot of St. 89 and 94 at ~34 
salinity (Fig. 4; 160°E/E) could possibly be associated with DOM rejection along with 
brine that has accumulated in RWL due to ice formation. Fractionation results also are 
in accordance with those observations since both FSIMFDOM and FSIMδ18Ο show 
negative values for subsurface waters, serving as an indication for waters that have 
received a brine contribution from ice formation (Fig. 10). In 167°E, shelf convection 
has driven the formation of RWL indicated by the subsurface FDOM maximum. 
However, the origin and formation mechanisms of RWL is subject to interannual 
variability of plume distribution over the shelf, slope and basin, that drives shelf 
convection, even further offshore, when plume has propagated further north due to 
atmospheric forcing. Interannual variability is traded off though due to residence time 
of riverine waters on the shelf (Dmitrenko et al., 2008). Thus, high resolution in situ 
DOM fluorescence offers a great tool to trace and track variability in FW export and 
decipher critical oceanographic processes.

5.5 Theoretical mixing lines 
Archetypical slope profiles of 160°E and 167°E transects exhibited a shift from a 
mixing line with steeper slope to the mixing line defined by the slope profile of 94°E, 
corresponding to BSB waters (Fig. 6E). In response to this observation, a schematic 
was constructed (Fig. 12) to present how DOM fluorescence could provide a better 
insight into the mixing of HC waters with AW core (above 34 salinity). This figure 
summarizes all the potential mixing processes that have been identified over the course 
of this study and it is suggested that mixing processes take place also within the HC 
layer. Three main mixing lines have been identified according to formation scenarios 
of RWL and non-freezing HC waters (see section 5.4). Firstly, oceanic convection 
(black dot, Fig. 12) taking usually place in offshore waters, away from shelf influences, 
produces a RWL (near-freezing) depleted in DOM (< 0.01 nm-1) that mixes 
conservatively with FSB core at depth (St. 6, Fig. 5; 94°E). Secondly, BSB HC waters 
(non-freezing) that have been probably formed in Barents Sea shelf, join the ABC and 
circulate along the continental slope (shelf break) and thus the advected BSB HC (green 
dot, Fig. 12) is mixing conservatively near the slope with the underlying BSB core (St. 
9, Fig. 5; 94°E). Finally, the third scenario represents waters loaded with riverine DOM 
that have fed the RWL (near-freezing) due to shelf convection (orange dot, Fig. 12) that 
are mixing near the slope with the BSB core below (St. 63, Fig. 5; 143°E). However, 
when plume is extending further offshore due to atmospheric forcing, RWL formed by 
shelf convection and loaded with DOM meets at depth the upper boundaries of the 
DOM depleted FSB (0°C isotherm) which defines a strong stratified layer and thus the 
mixing line abruptly shifts to the mixing line defined by the oceanic convection (yellow 
path, Fig. 12) (e.g. St. 55, Fig. 5; 143°E).

5.6 DOM fluorescence for freshwater fractionation 
Pacific water contribution 
Pacific signal within the Arctic is distinguished from Atlantic origin due to its 
oxygen depletion and high nutrient (NO3-, PO43-, SiO44-) concentrations resulting from 
intensified remineralization and denitrification happening when Pacific inflow waters 
interact with the Bering and Chukchi Sea sediments (Jones et al., 1998; Falkner et al., 
2005; Shimada et al., 2005; Alkire et al., 2019). Alkire et al., (2019) assessed Pacific 
Ocean’s contribution to the upper halocline in the Central Arctic Ocean by using NO 
parameter and concluded no Pacific influence existed for the corresponding transects 
of NABOS 2015. However, PW contribution alternates depending on Transpolar Drift 
oscillations and thus Pacific water contribution was also examined in this study by 
looking into the nitrate and phosphate relationships. Denitrification along with high 
nutrient and low oxygen concentrations has been found to occur in Arctic shelf seas, 
and particularly in our study areas in Laptev and East Siberian Sea (Bauch et al., 2011), 
making it more challenging to distinguish between AW and PW contributions within 
the Arctic (Alkire et al., 2019). Based on the Fig. 7, it is concluded that the contribution 
of Pacific water in our dataset is minor or non-existent, and it is not considered for the 
fractionation. 
FDOM-based fractionation 
Fractionation based on high resolution in situ DOM fluorescence represents a state-
of-the-art approach to FW contribution into the Arctic Ocean. Due to methodology 
chosen for the calculation of the FDOM end-members (matrix inversion), it was not 
possible to obtain identical fractions with F-δ18Ο, with a maximum deviation of the 
corresponding fractions 10.5%. A negative DOM fluorescence for SIM end-member 
has mathematically been generated (Table 1), which is inconsequential for physical 
reality since fluorescence consists of non-negative values. However, taking into 
consideration the mass balance equation (FAWFDOMAW + FRFDOMR + FSIMFDOMSIM 
= FDOMM), we consider that a negative FDOMSIM end-member multiplied with an also 
negative FSIM would generate a positive amount of DOM that ice formation has

contributed to the water column. On the other hand, when the negative FDOMSIM end-
member is multiplied with a positive FSIM, this would correspond to an amount of DOM 
that potentially would have been present in the water column but has been removed due 
to sea ice meltwater that caused significant dilution of the signal. Similarly, matrix 
inversion did not generate a FDOMR end-member as it was initially assumed to be an 
extrapolation to zero salinity of the conservative behavior of HC waters  and thus a 
much higher FDOM value > 0.6 nm-1, as it has been previously reported by Gonçalves-
Araujo et al., (2015) and used by Stedmon et al., (2021) (blue reference lines; Fig. 8B). 
The generated FDOMR = 0.2 nm-1 end-member for 0 salinity matches fairly well with 
the generated intercept (0.23) when a linear regression is applied on FDOM-Sp data. 
δ18Ο-Sp plot indicates that roughly a 40% and 60% of all the datapoints would lie above 
(ice melting) and below (ice formation/river runoff) of the theoretical mixing line, 
respectively (Fig. 8A). It is expected that FDOM-Sp plot would follow approximately 
the same pattern and, the arrows indicating the ice formation and melting processes are 
not perpendicular to the riverine mixing line, since according to Müller et al., (2011) 
even though DOM is retained within the sea ice more than salts do, salts are expelled 
more than DOM during brine rejection, meaning sharper changes in salinity than the 
DOM when ice is forming (Fig. 8B). Even though the reference riverine line that we 
initially assumed (Fig. 8B; blue dash-dotted line) greatly represents the conservative 
behavior of FDOM in marine environments, it excludes the surface dynamic processes 
of shelf and basin that we aim to track with the FW fractionation. The choice of FDOM 
end-member to a value close to the linear regression intercept is suggested for FDOM-
based FW fractionation, instead of the extrapolation to zero salinity of the conservative 
riverine mixing line defined by HC waters. FW fractionation is known to be quite 
sensitive to the choice of end-member values, thus many studies have referred to the 
uncertainties of the selected δ18Ο end-members (Bauch et al., 2011, 2013; Alkire et al., 
2015) and Monte Carlo simulations have also been used for δ18Ο-Sp pairs (Alkire et al., 
2015, Bolt 2021). In this study, the negative FDOMSIM end-member was shifted to 0 in 
order to correspond to the minimum realistic fluorescence value and in this case the 
deviation (ΔF) between the δ18Ο and FDOM derived fractions shifted from a maximum 
of 10% to ~15% (Fig. A4). Granskog et al., (2007) used CDOM absorption coefficient 
to fractionate between marine, river and sea ice melt water in Hudson Bay, where they 
also faced an equation system susceptible to uncertainties of the end-member values. 
Greatest deviation between F-δ18Ο and F-FDOM is observed for SIM fraction where 
FSIMFDOM seems to overestimate ice formation whereas FSIMδ18Ο ice melting (Fig. 9). 
ΔF for AW fractions is very small, ranking FDOM equally with δ18Ο, in terms of 
predicting the presence of AW waters. FRFDOM tends to overestimate when riverine 
input is at its peak (Fig. 10). For example, the circle in Fig. 9 represents waters from 
the surface 10 m of 160°E, where riverine plume is widely expanded as the FDOM 
hydrographic section reveals (Fig. 4; 160°E/C). It is possible that these deviations could 
be refined with a more strategic selection of the end-members after an exploratory 
sensitivity analysis. It is important to remember for the correct interpretation of FW 
fractions derived from δ18Ο and FDOM (SIM and R) that the values represent the 
history of the water column (Bauch et al., 1995). These values are the footprint of time-
integrated effects of the water column and do not represent the actual amount of FW 
stored (Bauch et al., 1995, 2016). In situ FDOM constitutes a good substitute of δ18Ο,

43 
 
since at least qualitatively provides a reliable overview of FW contributions and its 
continuous profiles are very promising for the reproduction of high-resolution fractions 
only with few water samples used for the calibration of the fluorometer. 
6. Conclusions 
 
Siberian Arctic is a region of particular interest since many of the climate change 
impacts are noticeable around its margins and due to the significant freshwater export 
from major Siberian rivers constitutes a key area for studying the Arctic marine carbon 
cycle. The results exhibit the utility of optical sensors for providing high resolution 
profiles and underlines the necessity of a universal calibration dataset for CDOM in situ 
fluorometers. This study confirms that in situ DOM fluorescence could successfully be 
utilized to distinguish signals from different Atlantic water branches with distinct 
FDOM footprints and thus track their circulation pathway and modification along the 
continental slope and basin. FDOM could be utilized as a DOC proxy in the Arctic shelf 
seas and thus, due to atmospheric forced DOM distribution in Siberian Arctic, it is 
possible to map the freshwater export and further resolve the pathway and fate of visible 
wavelength DOM fluorescence in Laptev and East Siberian Seas.  
Furthermore, FDOM, due to its conservative behavior, provides a better insight into 
the previously suggested convective and advection halocline formation mechanisms by 
establishing a robust connection of the freshwater export with the strong sea ice 
seasonal cycle in Siberian shelf seas. This study has successfully demonstrated the 
application of these scenarios into further resolving the mixing processes between the 
halocline and underlying Atlantic water. Finally, a promising approach to the water 
type fractionation based on high resolution in situ FDOM is presented here, that could 
possibly substitute the δ18Ο application, since FDOM provided a reliable representation 
of freshwater contributions, despite the uncertainties in the choice of the end-member 
values. The decline of Arctic sea ice poses a serious threat for the alternation of the 
wind forcing system and subsequently the freshwater export pathways. Therefore, it is 
deemed necessary to evaluate and extend the applicability of biogeochemical tracers 
such as DOM fluorescence for deciphering important oceanographic processes and 
monitoring a changing Arctic Ocean.
