Abstract
Surface-Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy (SERS) enables sensitive, label-free chemical de-
tection [1, 2, 3, 4], but conventional substrates often provide limited geometric control and
suffer from poor reproducibility [5, 6].
In this project, an end-to-end workflow was es-
tablished, spanning CAD design to two-photon polymerization (2PP) fabrication of three-
dimensional pillar arrays on silicon, followed by gold metallization and SEM-based character-
ization [7, 8, 9]. An automated metrology tool for quantitative SEM analysis, developed in
Python, was validated by comparison with manual measurements performed in ImageJ [10]
and achieved a relative deviation of 4.98% (below the 5% acceptance criterion), enabling
quantitative high-throughput evaluation. Parameter sweeps in laser power and scan speed
were used to identify the exposure window across multiple pitch designs, where the 1.2 µm
pitch design provided the most robust fabrication process and the best balance between well-
defined spacing and structural quality. The final printing parameter set (1.2H, LP 32%, SS
10,000 µm/s) was subsequently refined by adjusting slicing and hatching distance and scaled
to ∼1 × 1 mm using stitching. External SERS measurements with 4-mercaptobenzoic acid
(MBA) [11] demonstrated measurable on-chip enhancement and a clear performance rank-
ing among the stitched variants (S3 > S2 > S1), with the best-performing variant reaching
a maximum enhancement of 4.2 at 1064 cm−1 and 4.7 at 1580 cm−1. Overall, this work
demonstrates that the workflow enables evidence-based selection of process parameters and
provides a reproducible foundation for further SERS optimization.

Intro
Raman spectroscopy is an optical analysis method that is based on inelastic photon scatter-
ing. [3] It works by a tiny fraction of the incoming light interacting with the vibrations of the
molecules and changing their energy. Using Raman scattering gives a unique spectrum, which
can be used as the molecular fingerprint of a substance. [3] This method is useful because it is
a label-free method that does not need any additives to make the sample measurable or visible.
In addition, it is a non-destructive method and allows identification of chemical compounds in
solid materials, biological test samples, and liquids. [3] However, there is a challenge with this
technique. Raman signals are very weak: only about one out of approximately excited photons
contributes to the signal. This is problematic because it limits the practical use of the method
and the sensitivity for detecting substances at low concentrations.[1]. [3] Surface-Enhanced Ra-
man Spectroscopy (SERS) is a label-free analytical technique with very high accuracy and very
high sensitivity. However, there are some challenges in its use. These challenges center around
three problems.
Control over the nanogeometry is limited in classical 2D SERS substrates,
which are often made from randomly rough metal surfaces. Stable integration of these chip-
based systems or microfluidic systems is also a challenge. In addition, it is difficult to achieve
reproducibility between individual measurement points on the same substrate, and from batch
to batch. These challenges make it difficult to determine a reasonable enhancement factor (EF)
and a limit of detection (LoD) that is stable and reliable.[2] [9, 7] The technology two-photon
polymerization (2PP) overcomes these limitations, because it makes it possible to fabricate con-
trolled 3D nanostructures with extremely high resolution. This means that you can define and
set parameters such as height, radius, pitch, and duty cycle in a repro- ducible and controlled
way, instead of being limited by random nanoroughness. [7, 12] At the same time, 2PP makes
it possible to print directly on relevant technical substrates such as silicon and glass.
This
makes it possible to achieve high reproducibility between batches, large area coverage, and a
stable, direct integration of SERS structures in microfluidic systems. [7, 13] In this thesis, 2PP
is used to fabricate arranged 3D microstructures, which are then metallized with Au in order
to form dense electromagnetic enhancement — that is, hotspots — and therefore high SERS
enhancement. This work builds on the established knowledge of the [9] technical capabilities and
precision level of 2PP. This includes documentation of variance and resolution limits in newer
studies that connect SERS response, for example on optical fiber or microchips, with controlled
2PP geometry. The work is based on existing literature and [7, 14, 9, 15] experimental studies
that have documented the performance, resolution limits, and application areas of 2PP technol-
ogy, supplemented by newer studies of 2PP-fabricated SERS substrates and fiber-based optical
probes. [14, 9, 16] The goal of the project is therefore not to find a new technology, but to
apply the existing knowledge to a chip-based platform with strict process control of parameters,
extended area coverage through stitching, and a robust procedure that contributes to achieving
reproducibility [7, 12] The intention is to establish a coherent chain from design to fabrication
to validation: starting from a parametric geometry library and robust 2PP print strategies,
through controlled metal- lization with low roughness and narrowed nanogaps, and ending with

Theory
2.1
Raman-spektroskopi
Raman spectroscopy is an optical analytical method that uses inelastic scattering of light, mean-
ing that part of the energy from the incident light changes after interacting with a molecule [3,
1]. When a sample is irradiated with a laser in the visible or near-infrared region, scattering of
the light occurs, and this can happen in three different ways [3].
Elastic scattering, also known as Rayleigh scattering, occurs for most photons. Here, the photons
are scattered without any loss of energy, meaning the scattered photons have the same energy
as the incident light. This type of scattering provides no chemical information and accounts for
approximately 99.99% of all photons [3].
Inelastic scattering, also known as Raman scattering, occurs when a very small fraction of the
photons interact with the molecular vibrations, causing a slight change in the photon’s energy
[3, 1].
If the molecule releases energy, the emitted light gains energy (shorter wavelength) — this is
called the anti-Stokes line [1]. If the molecule releases energy, the emitted light gains energy
(shorter wavelength) — this is called the anti-Stokes line [1].
The energy difference between the incident and scattered light is known as the Raman shift
(∆E) and corresponds to the vibrational energy levels of the molecule [1]. Each Raman shift
is unique to specific chemical bonds and can therefore be used as a molecular fingerprint for
substances [3].

This method has several advantages, such as being label-free, meaning that there is no need
for fluorescence, dyes, additives or labeling when analyzing a sample. Here by is also a non-
destructive test, since the sample is also not damaged during measurement. and it can provide
valuable information about the sample’s structure and chemical composition [4].
The limitation of use is that only a very small fraction of the incident light is scattered as the
Raman signal — about one photon out of 108 [2]. It results in a very weak signal, especially
when concentrations is low [2]. This is solved by using Surface-Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy
(SERS), which is an enhancement technique that increases the signal strength through plasmonic
effects [14].
2.2
Surface-Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy (SERS)
Surface-enhanced Raman Spectroscopy (SERS) is an improved version of traditional Raman
spectroscopy, where the technique is based on using nanostructures of noble metals such as gold
or silver, which have plasmonic properties, to amplify the measured Raman signal by several
orders of magnitude [3, 4, 2].
When laser light hits the nanostructures, the electrons in the metal are set into collective vi-
brations (plasmons), which results in the electromagnetic field being strongly concentrated in
specific points on the surface around narrow nanogaps, sharp edges, or nanoparticles [1, 2]. Such
a field concentration is called hotspots, and they can enhance the intensity of the Raman signal
by up to 106–1010 times [3, 4].
The primary source of SERS enhancement occurs due to the increase in the local electromagnetic
field, where the intensity of the local field |E| is amplified around nanogaps and the tips of the
3D-printed microstructures. The total SERS enhancement increases proportionally with the
fourth power of the local field, i.e. |E|4, because the field is affected by both emission and
excitation [1, 2].
There is also an indirect effect, which is chemical enhancement that can arise from charge transfer
between the adsorbed molecules and the metal surface, but its influence is relatively small [4,
3].
How well a SERS substrate performs is largely determined by the geometric design of the
nanostructure, which includes radius, nanogaps, surface roughness, height, and pitch size, all of
which determine both the strength of the field concentration and the spectral position of the
plasmon resonance [5, 20].
Lack of reproducibility and lack of control are central limitations that often occur because of
random rough metal surfaces on traditional 2D SERS substrate [6, 4].
Even small variations in the surface shape of the nanostructure can affect the strength and
change the location of hotspots, which causes variation in the signal from point to point on the
same chip and also between production batches [6, 21].
By using high-resolution fabricated 3D nanostructures, which can be made for example by using
a two-photon printer (2PP), it is possible to reproduce the field distribution and geometry very
precisely [9, 14, 12].

2.3
Two-photon polymerization (2PP)
Two-photon polymerization (2PP) is a 3D-printing method in which light from a pulsed fem-
tosecond laser is focused into a photopolymer—also known as a resist—that contains a photoini-
tiator [22, 7]. The light intensity is especially high at the focal point, and exactly at that focus
the photoinitiator is able to absorb two near-infrared photons at the same time [22]. Because the
probability of this nonlinear absorption process scales with I², activation occurs only inside the
confined focal region, called a voxel, while everything outside the focus remains unchanged [22,
23]. A chain reaction that locally cures the polymer starts as soon as the initiator is activated
[22]. By using this process, one can write solid lines in the material by moving the focal point,
thereby forming a 3D structure inside the photoresist [7]. Finally, the uncured part is removed
by rinsing, leaving the intended geometric structure [7].
Figure 2 illustrates the schematic setup of the 2PP process. A femtosecond laser generates the
beam, which is steered by galvo mirrors and focused by a high–numerical-aperture objective
into the photoresist [24].
The zoom-in shows the writing process on the substrate: the objective is immersed deeply
into the photoresist polymer (IP-Dip), which acts as the immersion medium, enabling direct
writing onto the substrate. This configuration removes the glass–air interface, reduces spherical
aberrations, and increases the usable writing height (Z). The configuration is referred to as DiLL
and is shown more clearly in Figure 3b and the zoom ind view of figure 3
[25, 26].

In 2PP, the femtosecond pulse is important because it produces a very high peak intensity while
keeping the average power low, ensuring that the rest of the sample outside the focal region
does not heat up [22]. Near-infrared light penetrates deeply into the resist and has very weak
linear absorption, which ensures confinement of polymerization [22]. The achievable resolution
depends on the focus size, how close one operates to the polymerization threshold, scan speed
(SS), laser power (LP), and the numerical aperture of the objective [28, 7]. Very small voxels
with sub-micrometer dimensions can be achieved [8].
This technique enables fabrication of
complex 3D structures with very high resolution, although printing is relatively slow and most
suitable for small structures [12].

The voxel is the
smallest printable volume, shaped like an ellipsoid determined by NA and magnification [7, 30].
A 63× objective yields a small voxel, while a 10× objective yields a larger one [30]. Exposure
parameters and resist chemistry also influence the voxel [28].
The hatching distance
(∆xy) The hatching distance is the horizontal spacing between two parallel laser tracks in the
XY-plane[7]. It determines how close the writing paths lie to each other and can be larger or
smaller than the horizontal dimension of the voxel. When the hatching distance is reduced, the
writing paths become closer and overlap more, which makes the printed structure more compact
and continuous[28]. If the distance is larger, the overlap is reduced and the material becomes
more porous[28].
The slicing distance
(∆z) The slicing distance is the spacing between two successive layers of laser tracks along the
Z-direction[7]. It determines how thick the layers of printed tracks are. Just like the hatching
distance, the slicing distance is a process parameter that can be larger or smaller than the
voxel size, but here in the Z-dimension. When the distance is smaller, the writing paths are
closer together and overlap more, which again makes the printed structure more compact and
continuous[28]. If the distance is larger, the overlap is reduced, which makes the material more
porous[28].
Exposure window (LP/SS):
The effective exposure dose is determined by the combination of scanning speed (SS) and laser
power (LP)[7].
A lower SS or higher LP increases the local dose and thereby enlarges the
effective voxel[28]. An increased dose can cause the printed tracks to merge and the surfaces to
become over-exposed or uneven[28]. Conversely, if the dose is reduced, the material is not fully
polymerized, which makes the structure unstable or porous[28].

Stitching (tiles):
When printing an object whose structure exceeds the scan field of the system, the print is divided
into several writing fields, also called tiles[12]. This can introduce stitching artefacts, which are

• small positional shifts between tiles, visible as seams or pitch deviations[12],

By combining 2PP and DiLL, reproducible and controlled 3D nanostructures can be fabricated

When these structures
are metallized (e.g., gold sputtering), they become plasmonically active and generate strong
electromagnetic hot spots, making them highly suitable as SERS substrates [14, 5, 21].
2.4
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is a technique for creating images by using a focused
electron beam to interact with the surface of the examined object.
When the electrons hit
the sample, backscattered electrons, secondary electrons, and characteristic X-ray radiation are
generated, each providing information about compositional contrast, topography, and elemental
analysis [32]. It is possible to achieve a resolution down to 1–2 nm with SEM, although this de-
pends on the machine and the preparation of the sample. This makes the equipment well-suited

for analyzing micro- and nanostructures fabricated by 2PP printing [32, 13]. Non-conductive
polymers such as IP-Dip are coated with a thin layer of noble metal, e.g., gold, in order to make
the sample electrically conductive and prevent charging during SEM imaging [32, 33]. In this
context, SEM is used for verification of dimensions, reproducibility analysis, and quality control
of micro- and nanostructures printed using a 2PP printer [12]. Different areas on a chip surface,
such as a SERS chip, can be compared by using SEM images. It can also be used to evaluate
whether the printed structure matches the CAD design. SEM can further be used to determine
whether there are defects such as overexposure, surface roughness, or collapsing structures [5,
6]. This means that SEM can be used as an assessment instrument to verify design-to-print
reliability in the fabrication process [32].
2.5
Image analysis and validation of pillar diameter
The purpose of using image analysis in this project is to be able to measure the diameter of
micro-pillars in SEM images in an automatic way and then compare these automatic mea-
surements with manual reference measurements that were carried out in ImageJ [10, 34]. The
theoretical foundations behind the method can be divided into three central parts:
(1) preprocessing and contrast optimization of the image,
(2) object description and segmentation
(3) statistical evaluation between automatic and manual measurements [34, 35].
2.5.1
Grayscale images, intensity and contrast enhancement
An SEM image can be described as a two-dimensional function f(x, y), where each pixel (x, y) has
a specific gray value that represents the recorded surface intensity.[32, 36] Dark pixels represent
background, whereas bright pixels typically represent a stronger signal, such as the tops of the
pillars.
In traditional digital image processing, it is often necessary to enhance the contrast before
starting object segmentation. Global histogram equalization is a simple and effective method
for image processing; the method redistributes the gray values of the image so that the intensity
histogram becomes more evenly distributed and the contrast in the image is increased.[37, 10]
The problem with this method is that global contrast equalization can increase the noise level
in regions where the image is already homogeneous.[38]
This can be avoided by using a local variant, CLAHE (Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram
Equalization). This method processes the image by dividing it into small blocks (tiles), where
histogram equalization is performed separately in each block; in this way the contrast stretching
is limited (“contrast limiting”), so that noise does not come to dominate the image.[37, 38]
In this way, the local contrast around the small, bright pillars is increased without the image
appearing excessively sharp or dominated by noise.
After contrast enhancement, a Gaussian low-pass filter (“Gaussian blur”) is applied. A Gaussian
filter is used to compute the weighted mean of each pixel from the surrounding pixels, where the

weights are distributed according to a normal distribution.[39] This can be used to attenuate
high-frequency noise (isolated bright/dark pixels), while preserving larger objects such as the
contours of the pillars. Therefore, Gaussian blur is a standard step prior to thresholding and
edge detection, because it helps make the subsequent segmentation more robust to noise.
2.5.2
Segmentation and binary image formation
To be able to recognize each pillar as an individual object, it is important that the image is
segmented so that it is divided into foreground, representing the pillars, and background.[10]
Segmentation can be achieved by thresholding, where a threshold value T is chosen, and all
pixels with an intensity value above T are classified as foreground, while the rest are classified
as background.[10]
In SEM images, contrast and illumination can vary across the field of view, so a single threshold
value may not be sufficient. Therefore, adaptive (locally computed) thresholding methods are
often used, where the threshold value is computed from the mean intensity in a window around
each pixel, so that the threshold adapts to local variations in contrast and background.[10]
Thresholding results in a binary image, where each pixel is represented as 1 for foreground or
0 for background. The binary image will typically contain small holes and small noise objects
inside the segmented pillar structures. To remove these unwanted elements, morphological oper-
ations are applied to the binary images.[10, 34] Here, high-level functions are used that logically
correspond to erosion and dilation, such as remove small objects and remove small holes
from open-source image analysis libraries (e.g. scikit-image).
• remove small objects: Small, isolated components below a chosen area threshold are
removed as noise.
• remove small holes: Helps remove small holes inside larger objects by filling them in, so
that the pillars appear as compact, white regions.
These operations are based on morphological image analysis, where the shape of binary objects
is modified using the relationship between neighbouring pixels.[10, 34]

When the binary image has been processed and cleaned of noise, the connected white regions
can be identified as connected components. Each component is interpreted as a possible pillar
projection, and geometric properties are computed using so-called region properties.[10, 34]
Important quantities are:
• Area A: the number of pixels belonging to the object.
• Perimeter P: the length of the object boundary.
• Centroid: the center of mass of the object (xc, yc).

irregular are rejected.[41, 34]
In addition to circularity, an expected area interval [Amin, Amax] is used, which represents the
normal size that a pillar is expected to have at the given resolution. Small objects that have
an area smaller than Amin are interpreted as noise, whereas very large objects with area above
Amax are interpreted as artefacts or merged pillars.[34]
Afterwards, components are rejected if their centroid lies close to the ROI edge (a margin of a
few pixels), since these are typically partially cut off and therefore cannot be used for reliable
measurements.[34]

mined from the pixel size given by the SEM image scale (for example 210.9 nm/pixel).[32, 42]

Study overview and experimental workflow

Figure 5 illustrates the workflow for this project, showing the step-by-step process from digital

The process begins with the creation of CAD designs of hexagonal and rectangular patterned

print files [24, 7].
The next phase includes 2PP printing, which is carried out in the Dip-in Laser Lithography
(DiLL) configuration using IP-Dip resist on silicon substrates [25, 26, 43]. In this phase, the
printing parameters — laser power, scanning speed, hatching distance, and slicing distance
— are investigated in relation to the geometric precision of the structures [28, 8]. After the
printing process, the samples are developed in mr-DEV 600, cleaned in isopropanol, and gently
air-dried. This procedure is done to remove unexposed resist without damaging the printed

subsequently sent for external SERS testing
The overall goal is to achieve continuous, precisely printed pillar arrays with less than 5%
dimensional error, minimal structural collapse, and uniform structures across large areas. These
fabricated substrates serve as the foundation for the planned SERS measurements [14, 3, 5].

3.7
Automated image analysis and algorithm validation
To minimise operator bias and achieve reproducible large-scale analysis of pillar diameters, a
Python script was developed to validate the automated SEM image analysis by comparing it
with manual ImageJ measurements [10].
The workflow follows the image-analysis principles
described in Section 2.5 and is illustrated in Figure 9.
Figure 9: Activity diagram for the automated SEM image analysis and validation workflow. The script
loads the SEM image and the manual ImageJ measurements [10], defines a reference ROI, and computes
the relative deviation ∆D%. If the deviation exceeds 5%, the workflow is stopped. If the criterion is met,
additional ROIs are evaluated, classified as PASS (green) or FAIL (red), and displayed as an overlay on
the SEM image.
Manual reference measurements and coordinate mapping
For one selected SEM image, pillar diameters were first measured manually in ImageJ for 16
pillars [10]. The individual measured diameters and the corresponding X and Y coordinates
were exported to a CSV file with values given in micrometres. ImageJ was used to calculate the
statistics (mean, SD, min, max), which were also stored in the CSV file and appear in the bottom
four rows. The Python script imports these reference measurements and their corresponding

coordinates, converts the X and Y positions from micrometres to pixels using the px2µm scaling
[32], and then uses them to determine the reference ROI in the raw SEM image.
Definition of the reference ROI
Using the converted pixel coordinates, the script determines the minimum and maximum X and
Y values for the measured pillars and subsequently adds a small safety margin in all directions.
The bounding box is then adjusted to remain within the image and converted into a square
reference region of interest (reference ROI). The side length of the region of interes (ROI) is
defined such that all 16 pillars are fully contained within it. The reference ROI serves as the
primary validation region for the automated image analysis.
Image preprocessing, segmentation, and object filtering
The SEM image is imported in grayscale [36] and undergoes the same preprocessing steps de-
scribed in Section 2.5: CLAHE [38, 37], Gaussian smoothing [39], adaptive thresholding [36],
and morphological cleaning [40]. Within the reference ROI, the binary mask is used to iden-
tify connected components, after which area, perimeter, centroid, and equivalent diameter are
calculated for each component [40]. Components are filtered based on area interval, circularity
[41], and distance to ROI boundaries to remove partially cut pillars. The validated diameters
are converted from pixels to micrometres using px2µm [32], allowing direct comparison with the
manual ImageJ measurements [10].
Validation in the reference ROI
For all valid objects in the reference ROI, the automatic mean diameter and standard deviation
are calculated. Agreement between the manual and automated measurements is quantified using
the relative deviation ∆D%, defined in Section 2.5. The use of relative deviation follows standard
methodology for comparing measurement methods [35].
If ∆D% ≤5%, the reference ROI is registered as PASS and visualised with green frames in the
output image. Conversely, if ∆D% > 5%, the validation is considered failed, a warning message
is generated, and the image analysis is stopped.
Multi-ROI validation across the full image field
When the reference ROI meets the 5% criterion, the script generates four new ROI regions in
different locations of the image, each with the same side length as the reference ROI. Each of
these new ROIs undergoes the same processing and filtering steps, and the mean pillar diameter
and relative deviation ∆D% are calculated based on the manual reference values. The ROIs
are classified as PASS (green) or FAIL (red) depending on whether ∆D% is ≤or > 5%. This
helps determine whether deviations are local or global across the image field.

Finally, the script generates an overlay image in which the reference ROI and the additional ROIs
are displayed on top of the SEM image with green frames if classified as PASS and red frames

3.8
Parameter optimization parameter sweep experiment
Two separate parameter sweeps were conducted to investigate process stability and optimize
printing performance. Each parameter sweep experiment aimed to identify parameter ranges
that ensured consistent polymerization and high structural fidelity of the printed microstructures
[7, 28].
parameter sweep experiment 1: Exposure window.
Laser power (LP) was varied between 28–34% across five levels, while the scan speed (SS) was
adjusted between 8000–12000 µm/s across three levels. The purpose was to determine the opti-
mal exposure window in which polymerization remained stable enough to produce undamaged,
well-defined structures without collapse or overexposure. The resulting prints were visually eval-
uated using SEM to determine the threshold values for reliable curing and surface integrity [12,
23]. DeScribe GWL script used for this LP/SS parameter sweep is provided in Appendix C.4.
parameter sweep experiment 2: Geometric control.
Geometrical printing parameters were investigated by varying the slicing distance (SD) (∆z)
between 0.1–0.3 µm (three levels) and the hatching distance (HD) (∆xy) between 0.1–0.2 µm
(three levels). The objective was to optimize the balance between voxel overlap, edge definition,
and overall printing time. Combinations of slicing and hatching parameters were analyzed in
SEM images to identify the most efficient settings providing both high resolution and stable
mechanical connectivity between layers [7, 28]. The corresponding DeScribe GWL script for the
SD/HD parameter sweep is listed in Appendix C.3.

4.2.3

For the 1.4H and 1.4R designs (Figures 16–17), the diameter-based classifier marks a large
fraction of the LP/SS combinations as “green”, because the mean pillar diameter frequently lies
within the 0.80–0.90 µm interval or the surrounding slack window. However, the corresponding
SEM images show that many of these nominally acceptable settings are not usable in practice.
At low laser powers and/or very high scan speeds, several tiles show under-developed regions
with many missing or very thin pillars. In the opposite corner of the parameter grid (high LP,
low SS), the pillars become visibly thicker and begin to merge into ridge-like structures, with
local necking and bridges between neighbouring features. In both cases, the automatic diameter
metric still reports values close to the target range, so these conditions appear as green tiles
even though the morphology is clearly unsuitable for SERS.
Only a limited region around intermediate laser powers and relatively high scan speeds produces
arrays where most pillars are present and reasonably well separated. Even there, the larger
pitch makes the gaps wider and the hotspot density lower than for the 1.2 µm designs. Overall,
the 1.4 µm pitch offers a broad numerical exposure window in terms of mean diameter, but
SEM inspection reveals frequent missing or merged pillars, so the effective process window is

5
Discussion
The aim of the project was to establish a reproducible and controlled 2PP-based fabrication
workflow for 3D-printed SERS nanopillar structures and to determine a geometry and exposure
configuration that ensure measurable SERS enhancement and structural robustness. Section 4
presents the results of this process: first through validation of the automated SEM metrology
pipeline (Section 4.1), then by mapping the exposure window for three pitches (Section 4.2),
optimization of the SD/HD geometry (Section 4.3), characterization of stitched prints in a larger
print field (Section 4.4), and finally external measurements to characterize the large stitched print
area of the final fabricated substrates (Section 4.5). In this discussion, an overall quantitative
interpretation of the results is presented.
5.1
Robustness and sensitivity of the automated SEM metrology
An important part of the method is the validation of the automated SEM measurement method
described in Section 4.1. In the reference ROI, the manual ImageJ analysis gave a diameter of
0.96 ± 0.10 µm, whereas the automated algorithm script gave 1.011 ± 0.044 µm, corresponding
to a relative deviation of ∆D = 4.98%.
This value is below the defined threshold of 5%,
which means that the method meets the quantitative precision requirement necessary for high-
throughput evaluation.
44
==PAGE:48==
3D-printed microstructures and patterns for sensor applications
The multi-regional ROI analysis showed deviations of 0.93% (TL), 2.63% (TR), 4.64% (center),
4.18% (BL), and 1.04% (BR); all ROIs were within the defined acceptance criteria. A controlled
stress test was performed to illustrate a worst-case scenario. This was done by changing the
circularity value, which resulted in a deviation above 5% in two regions (6.50% in BL and 6.06%
in C). This highlights that the method is both appropriately sensitive to segmentation choices
and reproducible.
Overall, the results show that the automated metrology pipeline functions as the quantitative
backbone for screening the investigated LP/SS and SD/HD parameter grids in Sections 4.2 and
4.3, and they additionally confirm the necessity of including morphology inspection in cases
where diameter alone can give misleading results.
5.2
Exposure window and choice of pitch
The LP/SS parameter sweep in Section 4.2 was evaluated using an interval of 0.80–0.90 µm,
determined to be the core window, as well as an extended interval of 0.76–0.945 µm, provided
that the morphology remained acceptable.
The results for pitch = 1.0 µm show clear signs of being close to the fabrication limit. Under-
exposed structures occur due to exposure with low doses, e.g., 1.0R at LP 26% and SS 12,000 µm/s
gave 0.72±0.12 µm (FAIL), and 1.0H using the same values gave 0.74±0.14 µm (FAIL). Even the
passing conditions appear unstable, e.g., 1.0R at LP 28% and SS 10,000 µm/s gave 0.77±0.17 µm
(PASS weak). Although a stable point occurred at 1.0H at LP 32% and SS 10,000 µm/s, which
gave 0.89±0.05 µm, the process window is limited and the parameters are sensitive to variation.
This is the reason why the 1.0 µm pitch is not suitable for reliable production under the current
process parameters.
Pitch = 1.2 µm shows the most suitable combination between pillar separation and robustness.
This is seen, among other things, in a stable representative case in the design 1.2R at the setting
LP 34% and SS 8,000 µm/s with 0.86 ± 0.08 µm; however, it was not chosen because SEM
evaluation showed some regions where there were pillars that were collapsed or fused together.
For the chosen final geometry, 1.2H at LP 32% and SS 10,000 µm/s gave 0.88 ± 0.05 µm,
centered in the target interval and showing structural stability and robustness. Section 4.2.4
shows a broad range of acceptable conditions spanning the interval LP 26–34% and SS 8,000–
12,000 µm/s, which suggests good tolerance to experimental variation.
Many parameter combinations with numerically acceptable diameters were also obtained for
pitch = 1.4 µm, but SEM evaluation showed significant morphological problems. For example,
1.4R at LP 28% and SS 12,000 µm/s gave 0.87±0.15 µm even though many pillars were missing,
and 1.4R at LP 30% and SS 12,000 µm/s gave 0.97 ± 0.10 µm with thickened pillar structures
and some collapses. Therefore, diameter-based evaluation alone gives false-positive outcomes
for pitch = 1.4 µm.
Overall, 1.2H at LP 32% and SS 10,000 µm/s is the most morphologically reliable and robust
configuration, which justifies why this configuration was used in the subsequent optimization
process.

5.3
Geometric refinement (SD/HD and contour) and statistics for stitched
prints
In the SD/HD parameter sweep (Section 4.3), the design parameters SD and HD were system-
atically adjusted to restore a reference diameter of µref = 0.88 µm based on the diameter of the
design selected in the previous step (4.2). An acceptance window of 0.836–0.924 µm (≈±5%
around µref) was defined as the criterion.
Measurements for the design without contour gave consistent diameters in the interval 0.865–
0.893 µm, meaning that several different SD/HD combinations gave nearly the same valid di-
ameter. Among these, the combinations (SD, HD) = (0.10, 0.20), (0.30, 0.15), (0.20, 0.20), and
(0.30, 0.10) showed the most promising results, since they all lay within ±5% of the reference
diameter. A version of the design with contour was included at this step to investigate the
effect of the contour. Including a contour systematically increased the diameter and expanded
the interval to 0.878–0.940 µm. Nevertheless, there was only one case with SD = 0.10 µm, HD
= 0.15 µm that gave 0.940 ± 0.088 µm, which lies outside the acceptance window and therefore
failed. This indicates that contours reduce the allowable SD/HD combinations when validating
using diameter.
The stitched print analysis from Section 4.4 confirms these tendencies over an approximately
1 × 1 mm area.
The first stitched substrate S1 (no contour, SD/HD = 0.30/0.10) shows a
mean diameter of 866.94 ± 61.58 nm and pitch of 1224.81 ± 29.01 nm. The second stitched
substrate S2 (no contour, SD/HD = 0.30/0.15) gave a mean diameter of 827.17 ± 36.92 nm and
pitch of 1244.17 ± 61.28 nm. The third and final substrate was a contour variant S3 (SD/HD
= 0.30/0.15) which gave an increased diameter of 946.92 ± 81.27 nm and a reduced pitch of
1192.60 ± 62.68 nm.
Thus, the stitched printed substrates show that adding the contour for identical SD/HD increases
the diameter by about 120 nm, corresponding to approximately 15%, and leads to a reduced
pitch by about 50 nm. The results for these changes show that adding the contour increases the
diameter, reduces the pitch, and simultaneously gives a larger diameter distribution.
5.4
SERS performance and comparison with previous designs
The external SERS measurements were carried out using 4-mercaptobenzoic acid (MBA). The
Raman bands at 1064 cm−1 and 1580 cm−1 were normalized by dividing by the intensity band at
508 cm−1, and measurements were taken both outside the printed area (Raman reference) and
inside the print field (SERS signal) for each chip. The enhancement factor (EF) was calculated
using the following formula:
EF = ISERS,norm

The enhancement factor (EF) for the three substrates (S1), (S2), and (S3) gave values of approx-
imately 2.6/2.8 (S1), 3.8/4.1 (S2), and 4.2/4.7 (S3) for the bands at 1064 cm−1 and 1580 cm−1,
respectively, meaning an overall interval of approximately 2.6–4.7 across the substrates. The
weakest chip (S1) lies around 2.6–2.8, whereas S3 reaches around 4–4.7, giving a clear rank-

ing S3 > S2 > S1. For 1580 cm−1, EF increases from 2.80 (S1) to 4.67 (S3), meaning that
S3 delivers almost 1.7 times higher normalized signal than S1, corresponding to about 67%
higher enhancement. All measurements for these substrates were carried out at a laser power of
0.156 mW.
Here, EF is defined as an on-chip ratio between the normalized SERS signal inside the printed
area and a normalized Raman reference measured outside the printed area on the same chip.
This metric therefore reflects a relative, within-chip enhancement and is not directly comparable
to conventional analytical enhancement factors reported in the SERS literature.
In an earlier experiment in the project, 4-mercaptobenzoic acid (MBA) was measured on P1400
and P2000 pillar arrays.
It was found that P1400 was a better substrate than P2000, and
based on this, the larger design with P1700 was excluded. In this earlier experiment, a higher
laser power of 0.54 mW was used, and the ratio between the signal strength in the center of
the substrate and areas outside the center was examined. For P1400 with sputtered Au, the
4-mercaptobenzoic acid (MBA) peaks in the center were around 1,000–1,300 counts, whereas the
same peaks outside the center were around 220–350 counts, corresponding to a ratio of about
3.7 and 4.5×, for example 1300/350 ≈3.7 and 1000/220 ≈4.5.
In the mentioned SERS screening, there are differences in how the enhancement is calculated
and in the used laser power. In this final test on the substrates (S1), (S2), and (S3), EF is
defined as the SERS signal divided by the Raman signal on the same chip, after normalization
to 508 cm−1. In the earlier P1400 test, the focus was on how much higher the signal was in
the chip center than outside the center, in other words a ratio based on signals from two SERS
measurements. The values for P1400 (about 3.7–4.5×) and for S1–S3 (about 2.6–4.7) therefore
lie in a comparable interval; the difference is, however, that the new chips achieve their 2.6–4.7×
at a lower laser power of 0.156 mW compared to 0.54 mW for the P1400 test, and the new
substrates (S1), (S2), and (S3) have a more controlled method where there is a separate Raman
reference on the same chip. On that basis, it makes sense to say that the new substrates are
better than the earlier P1400 chips, but still far from commercial SERS substrates.
This modest magnitude suggests that nanoscale hotspot generation is still limited by geometry
and/or metallization conditions in the present structures. [6, 5]
5.5
Practical considerations and limitations
In addition to the obtained quantitative EF values for the substrates, there are several practical
factors that are important when weighing the potential of the produced substrates. One impor-
tant observation is that the contour-printed variation (S3) gave a higher SERS response than
(S1) and (S2), which did not have a contour. This seems somewhat contrary to expectations
because the (S3) geometry has thicker pillars, thinner gaps, and local fusion of pillars. Thus,
it was believed that it would be less ideal than the designs that had separated pillars without
contour.
This observation is consistent with hotspot-dominated SERS, where nanometric gaps and junc-
tions are widely reported to dominate the response, meaning that a higher density of narrow
47
==PAGE:51==
3D-printed microstructures and patterns for sensor applications
gaps can outweigh the disadvantage of locally merged pillars. [6, 5]
The result shows that more densely placed pillars and partially fused structures increase the
number of hotspots, which results in a stronger SERS response. This means that for future
work it is a good idea to look more closely at parameters such as pillar diameter, height, gap
width, and the degree of “necking” between the pillars rather than simply describing them as
separate or merged structures.
Another important fact is metallization.
All chips in this project were sputter-coated with
about 14–15 nm gold (20 s), both to make the sample conductive for SEM and to constitute the
plasmonic SERS layer. Looking also at the earlier internal measurements on P1400 chips, one
can note that a thinner Au layer around 10 nm can give a better SERS effect than a thicker
Cr/Au structure, which may indicate that there is still optimization potential in the choice of
metal thickness, roughness, and possibly alternative metals.
In the earlier internal screening, thinner Au coatings appeared to yield stronger response than
thicker Cr/Au stacks under the specific in-house conditions, suggesting that metal thickness and
coating morphology remain an important optimization parameters. [44]
In addition, the reported EF values are based on measurements with (MBA) at one concentration
and one specific measurement setup. They therefore reflect how the substrates perform under
controlled and limited conditions and do not say anything about, for example, robustness, limit
of detection, or batch variation.
6
Conclusion
In conclusion, this project established a scalable and controlled end-to-end workflow for design,
fabrication, and validation of 2PP-produced SERS pillar arrays. It also demonstrated that the
workflow enabled evidence-based process decisions rather than qualitative trial-and-error. The
automated SEM metrology pipeline was validated against manual ImageJ measurements with a
relative deviation of ∆D% = 4.98%, which is below the 5% acceptance limit, and the pipeline
output showed consistent agreement across multiple ROIs. This made it possible to perform
reliable high-throughput screening of large parameter sweeps. Based on quantitative analysis
of the exposure window, using a target diameter interval of 0.80–0.90 µm, 1.2 µm pitch designs
provided the most robust and reproducible process window. The final printing condition was
therefore chosen as 1.2H at LP = 32% and SS = 10,000 µm/s, which gave a typical mean
diameter of about 0.88 ± 0.05 µm.
Subsequently, slicing- and hatching-distance (SD/HD) optimization plus large-area stitching
yielded three final substrates (S1, S2, and S3). Their diameter and pitch distributions were
quantified. An external independent SERS test based on MBA showed clear enhancement and
stable ranking, where S3 delivered the strongest signal strength (≈4.16 ± 0.13 at 1064 cm−1
and ≈4.67 ± 0.18 at 1580 cm−1).
The overall results demonstrate improved performance compared with previous in-house chips.
They also validate the method as a robust platform for further optimization. The remaining gap
to commercial SERS substrates is possibly due to nanoscale hotspot limitations. This may relate

to roughness effects, gaps, and metallization conditions. These limitations may be important.
The next steps should primarily focus on three things: (i) to explore smaller pitch regimes
(≤1.0 µm) more systematically by adjusting voxel and contour parameters to achieve stable
structures. (ii) to optimize metallization, including the thickness and roughness of the gold layer
and possibly investigating whether alternative metals can increase hotspot density. (iii) to carry
out a more targeted geometry study where pillar diameter, height, and gap width are varied in
a controlled manner and linked directly to the measured EF values.
