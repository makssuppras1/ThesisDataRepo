Abstract 
The Sustainable Development Goal 2 “Zero hunger” (SDG-2) has been designed in 2015 by 
the United Nations to decrease pressures exerted by food production on natural ecosystems and 
ensure to all a safe access to nutritious food. One of the key strategies to reach SDG-2 is the 
establishment of a healthy and sustainable diet. So far, studies have mainly focused on high-
income countries while being oriented towards specific dimensions of sustainability (e.g. 
nitrogen footprint, economics). There is thus a further need to investigate healthy and 
sustainable diets, notably by developing numerical models that can simulate the impacts of 
those diets. Informed by such models, policy-makers could suggest specific diets to their 
population and ultimately trigger behavioural change among consumers.   
This study aims at quantitatively defining optimized diets that can be considered as healthy and 
environmentally sustainable, at global scale. To simulate varied regimes and measure their 
repercussions, a model was created with indicators gathered in the light of a large existing 
literature and complemented by the use of Life Cycle Impact Assessment tools. In this model, 
“healthy” was defined through the lens of nutrient intake, while “environmentally sustainable” 
by the effects caused by food production on five impact categories (climate change, water 
consumption, freshwater eutrophication, marine eutrophication, land use and biodiversity loss). 
To refine the sustainability component of the diet, we also discuss qualitative indicators to be 
used by policy-makers or researchers when evaluating the sustainability of diets in terms of 
socio-political and economic aspects.  
Using the model, we obtained healthy and environmentally sustainable diets for five different 
scenarios: omnivorous, pescatarian, lacto-ovo vegetarian, lacto-vegetarian and ovo-vegetarian. 
We then analysed the environmental and health performances of the diets and highlighted 
specific food items which consumption could potentially pave the way to the SDG-2. 
Furthermore, we discuss the importance in our model of combinations of food items and trade-
offs regarding their environmental impacts.  
The collection of indicators presented in this study could be extended to other aspects of 
sustainability upon which policy-makers can establish evidence-informed dietary guidelines. 
Finally, we hope our work to be a starting point for more comprehensive models, encompassing 
more sustainability dimensions and spatially differentiated indicators.

1. Introduction 
In 2015, a total of 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) have been defined by the United 
Nations to achieve a more sustainable future for all, by tackling the global challenges humanity 
currently faces, (e.g. climate change, gender equality, poverty, inequality, hunger, 
environmental degradation, peace and justice). In that setting, one of the SDGs is dedicated to 
food systems, i.e. SDG-2 “End hunger, achieve food security and improved nutrition, and 
promote sustainable agriculture” (United Nations 2015). In fact, as 1.9 billion adults are 
currently overweight and 462 million are underweight, humanity currently faces a global 
nutrition crisis (WHO 2020). Moreover, food insecurity has been prevalent in several regions 
of the world, with 25.9 percent of the world population not having access to nutritious and 
adequate food quantities in 2019 (FAO, et al. 2020). Pressures exerted on natural ecosystems 
have led to natural disasters becoming more frequent and putting human activities at risk, 
notably in the food production sector. Meeting SDG-2 is therefore of crucial importance, as it 
will help reducing inequities in food systems while promoting healthy and sustainable dietary 
lifestyles (Global Nutrition Report 2020). The achievement of SDG-2 by 2030 will require 
changes in dietary habits and agricultural practices to reduce the pressures we exert on natural 
ecosystems (Blesh, et al. 2019). In this context, one of the pathways identified to achieve SDG-
2 lays in the establishment of a healthy and sustainable diet (Blesh, et al. 2019). 
Defining a healthy and sustainable diet is a multi-disciplinary task as different sectors and 
theories are involved. The sustainability aspect encompasses several dimensions (environment, 
affordability, food security, health etc.) (FAO 2010), as does the healthy component which can 
be studied through different perspectives (nutrient requirements, disease causes, life 
expectancy, etc.) (Willet, et al. 2019). The challenge lies in achieving SDG-2 while avoiding 
“burden-shifting” and “rebound effect”, encountered when the scope of a study is too narrow 
and when side effects are not taken into account (Yu, et al. 2016).  
As shown in Table 1, the majority of studies that have investigated sustainable and healthy 
diets concerned developed countries and were conducted at country- or regional-scale. In fact, 
most studies were so far oriented towards specific dimensions of sustainability and health, such 
as nitrogen footprint (e.g. (Costa Leite, et al. 2019)), economics (e.g. (Verly, et al. 2020)), 
health benefits gained through consumption of specific foods (e.g. (Stylianou, Fulgoni III and 
Jolliet 2021); (Sokolow, Kennedy and Attwood 2019)) or towards specific regions (e.g. 
(Jacobi, Mukhovi, et al. 2020). However, Willet, et al.  (2019) and Chaudhary et al. (2018) 
have already conducted global-scale analyses. The former aimed at defining a global 
sustainable and healthy diet and defined quantitative environmental thresholds for different 
Earth system processes and for the food sector. However, Hirvonen, et al. (2020) have shown 
that the diet defined by Willet, et al. (2019) was unaffordable in low-income countries as well 
as the increased production needed to provide the high quantity of treenuts and groundnuts 
involved, being water intensive crops, have been questioned by several studies (e.g. (Vanham, 
Mekonnen and Hoekstra 2020)).  Chaudhary, Gustafson and Mathys (2018) have done a global-
scale analysis to quantify the performance of national food systems of several countries, and 
across sustainability dimensions, notably encompassing nutrition, environment, affordability, 
food waste etc. They however did not take the nitrogen and phosphorus emissions associated 
to food systems. There is thus an urgent need to evaluate as many sustainability dimensions of 
==PAGE:9==
2 
 
the food sector as possible by taking a global perspective and provide concrete examples of 
sustainable and healthy diets, that could be applied to partly bridge the gap until the 
achievement of SDG-2.  
The overall aim of the present study is thus to investigate the sustainability implications of 
meeting SDG-2, through the establishment of a healthy and sustainable diet. The study 
therefore aims at developing a numerical model that could be used to generate different food 
items combinations, i.e. diets, that would be healthy and environmentally sustainable, based on 
a set of indicators collected during literature reviews. Our study focuses on the environmental 
aspect of sustainability and starts with the construction of a model that generates optimized 
environmentally sustainable and healthy diets and we then discuss other aspects of 
sustainability (e.g. socio-political, economics) that could be further included in our model. As 
dietary habits vary among people, limiting the study to the definition of one “diet fits all” may 
lack robustness. Therefore, this project also includes different scenarios, where different diets 
are assessed towards a set of nutritional and environmental constraints while acknowledging 
heterogeneity among dietary patterns (e.g. omnivorous, vegetarian, etc). The methodological 
framework applied as well as the model structure are presented in Section 2. The resulting 
healthy and environmentally sustainable diets as well other sustainability dimensions are 
discussed in Section 3.

2. Methods 
2.1. Overall assessment methodology 
The methodology used to define a healthy and sustainable diet is composed of three main 
phases and is illustrated in Figure 1. In the first phase, we gather health and sustainability 
indicators (e.g. protein needs, water footprint) and we then associate to the retained indicators 
qualitative and quantitative values collected during literature reviews, for both the health and 
sustainable sides. We decide to take on a global perspective by defining indicators valid across 
the world. The second phase is dedicated to the modelling part, where we first collect health 
(e.g. vitamin C content of 1 g of tomatoes) and environmental data (e.g. m³ water consumed to 
produce 1 g of tomatoes) of a set of food items and then use an optimizer to generate healthy 
and environmentally sustainable diets. In phase 3, we evaluate the diets in terms of health and 
environmental performances and finally, we identify and discuss socio-economic and political 
aspects that could be used by policy-makers and researchers when evaluating healthy and 
sustainable diets. 
Even though the health aspect could be considered as being part of the sustainability dimension 
(e.g. Wood, Alam and Dupras, 2019), we decided to keep the two components separated for 
simplicity, likewise several previous studies (e.g. Willet et al., 2019; Trijsburg et al., 2020). 
During the literature review, we collected quantitative and qualitative values for indicators 
related to environmental, socio-economic and political aspects of a sustainable diet. While 
some assessed the healthy component of the diet in terms of required dietary intakes (e.g. Fanzo 
et al., 2020; Lim 2018), others focused instead on assessing health through impacts of diets on 
health diseases such as hypertension, depression or obesity (e.g. Opie et al., 2020, Lelong et 
al., 2019). As more suitable to quantitative modelling, we decided to limit our study to the 
former approach. We also chose to assess the environmental sustainability based on the impacts 
caused by food production on the environment and by the definition of thresholds of maximum 
impact that the environment can tolerate, likewise Willet et al. (2019). The socio-economic and 
political aspects are discussed in a qualitative and semi-quantitative way, in terms of policies, 
economic incentives and food price level that can be used by policy makers when establishing 
dietary guidelines to favour healthy and sustainable diets and are thus not included in our 
model.  
As shown on Figure 1, steps 1a and 1b consist in the collection of health and sustainability 
indicators to characterize a healthy and sustainable diet. Steps 2a and 2b of phase 1 aim at 
defining a set of health and environmental constraints in the form of a system of linear 
equations to be solved in phase 2. Phase 1 therefore leads to a set of linear equations being per 
capita daily nutrient needs and daily per capita maximum levels of impact caused on the 
environment due to food consumption, to quantitatively assess the indicators in terms of health 
and environmental sustainability aspects. Then, phase 2 consists of a data collection on food 
items characteristics (step 4), i.e. nutritional and environmental, and use of optimization 
programming (step 5) to solve the system of linear equations and quantitatively define 
optimized healthy and environmentally sustainable diets, for several scenarios based on 
different dietary-patterns defined in step 4. The performance of the resulting diets (i.e. 
combinations of food items), towards both aspects of the definition, obtained in phase 2 is then 
assessed and discussed in step 7a of phase 3. In addition, socio-economic and political targets 
are identified and discussed in step 7b to qualitatively broaden the definition of the 
sustainability aspect of the diet and match the sustainability indicators defined in step 1b.

diet that minimizes the climate change impact of the diet while fulfilling the other health and 
environmental constraints. To this end, an optimiser was used in Python to solve this system 
of linear equations, for a limited number of food items.  More specifically, a CPLEX solver 
from International Business Machines Corporation (IBM 2016) was used and complemented 
by our constraints.  The full code constituting the model can be found in the Supplementary 
information (Section 2.2 in Supplementary method).

2.3. Definition of a healthy and sustainable diet 
2.3.1. Identification of studies 
In order to tackle both the health and sustainability dimensions of the diet, two literature 
reviews were conducted. The search was performed using “Web of Science” 
(webofknowledge.com) (WOS) and to a lesser extent “Google Scholar” (scholar.google.com). 
The study conducted by the EAT Lancet (Willet, et al. 2019) and aiming at defining a healthy 
==PAGE:14==
7 
 
and sustainable diet was taken as a starting point as it is based on an extensive literature review 
and has a similar scope to ours. As it was published by the beginning of 2019, we decided, for 
both literature reviews, to cover a temporal range going from 2018 to 2021, in a way to cover 
all studies conducted along or after the one from the EAT Lancet (Willet, et al. 2019). Few 
exceptions done before 2018 were also considered when deemed relevant. The focus was 
mostly put on reviewing scientific articles, although national and international dietary 
guidelines reports were also considered.  
Starting with the definition of indicators and key words to search for (in WOS: TS = (("healthy 
food" OR "healthy diet")  AND  ("indicator*"  OR "index"  OR "guideline*"  OR "criter*")) 
and TS= (("sustainable food"  OR  "sustainable  diet"  OR  "food  sustainability")  AND  
("indicator*" OR "index" OR "guideline*" OR "criter*" OR “boundary”)) ), several 
quantitative and qualitative values associated to the indicators were collected from the studies 
reviewed. The indicators searched for in the studies reviewed concern nutritional requirements 
and environmental, economic and socio-political targets and thresholds for a diet to be 
sustainable. A total of 92 studies were reviewed.  A complete list of indicators and studies 
reviewed can be found in Supplementary information (Section 1.1 in Supplementary method). 
2.3.2. Definition of the healthy component 
Indicators targeted during the literature review to define a healthy diet are micro-nutrients (e.g. 
vitamins, minerals) and macro-nutrients (e.g. carbohydrates, proteins) requirements, energy 
need, uptakes from specific food categories (e.g. fruits), physical activity level, age, diet type 
and location.  Table 3 in Section 2.3.6 displays the indicators used in the model.  
While several studies have investigated men and women dietary needs separately (e.g. Jetter, 
et al., 2018), others have instead defined one diet for both women and men.  As some have 
shown that women’s nutrition and physical activity are sometimes affected by gender norms 
(e.g. Spencer, Rehman and Kirk, 2015), we decided, in accordance with the idea of using a 
global scope, to define a single diet valid for both women and men. Therefore, in studies 
providing them separately, an average value was taken. In addition, several studies mentioned 
dietary intakes in terms of number of servings (i.e. food portion sizes). However, the size of 
such servings was not systematically mentioned in the associated study. To compensate for this 
lack, serving sizes for individual foods and averages for food categories were either directly 
taken or an average for a food category was calculated based on the recommendations of the 
National Health and Medical Research Council (Australian Government 2015). The resulting 
serving sizes can be found in Supplementary information (Section 1.3 in Supplementary 
method). Moreover, most studies have defined a healthy diet for a certain age-category. An 
average age range was therefore calculated in order to be associated to our definition of healthy 
diet by taking the average of the minimum and maximum age mentioned in the studies of 
interest. Furthermore, some studies had associated to the healthy diet a level of required 
physical activity, expressed in minutes of effort per day. In those case too, the average was 
taken. The age and physical activity level were however not included in the model and were 
used on the side. 
Food categories are typically described in different ways across studies (i.e. pulses, meat, 
cereals are examples of food categories). Indeed, some would consider a potato being part of 
the category vegetables, but other would not. As every food item had to be assigned to a 
category to be in accordance with the defined constraints, a clear definition of each food 
==PAGE:15==
8 
 
category should be provided. To this end, it was firstly decided that, in order to harmonize the 
data collected, were used in the model only the food categories that had been mentioned in 
more than 3 studies reviewed. Then, food categories were harmonized based on the definitions 
from the FAO (FAO 2014) (FAO 2021).  A complete list of the categories used in the model 
and their associated food items can be found in Supplementary information (Section 1.3 in 
Supplementary method). 
Finally, all data collected was harmonised to compatible units, and the minimum and maximum 
values were identified among studies for each indicator and used to define acceptable nutrient 
uptake ranges and average level of physical activity for a diet to be healthy. The mean and the 
standard deviation were calculated across studies to later discuss the heterogeneity between 
studies. 
2.3.3. Definition of the environmental sustainability component 
The definition of an environmentally sustainable diet can be approached by defining thresholds 
of maximum impact caused by food production that the environment can tolerate and for 
several environmental impact categories (Willet, et al. 2019). An environmental impact 
category can be defined as a category depicting problematic environmental issues caused by 
emissions or resource extractions (Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen 2018). The present 
section describes which indicators, i.e. environmental impact categories, were retained 
following the literature review and Section 2.3.4 details the procedure followed to define 
thresholds for each indicator.  
Different types of indicators were used by the studies reviewed to define an environmentally 
sustainable diet. In fact, some would include food waste or resource depletion while others 
would favour other impact categories (e.g. climate change, water consumption). Data was 
therefore collected for all types of indicators and a harmonization then led to a prioritization of 
impact categories based on, first, their relevance to the food sector, according to scientific 
evidence, second, their redundance across studies, third, the availability of quantitative values 
and fourth, their coverage in Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) method which is used to 
calculate the impacts of the production of a product during its life cycle, from the cradle to the 
grave (Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen 2018). As environmental impact intensities associated 
to the production of each food item will have to be calculated and entered in the model, the 
final choice of environmental impact categories following the literature review should in fact 
be consistent with environmental impact categories depicted in the Life Cycle Impact 
Assessment method. Indeed, the latter will be used to later calculate the so-called 
environmental impact intensities. Further details on the use of the LCIA method are provided 
in Section 2.3.7. Table 2 displays the retained environmental impact categories and emphasises 
the importance of these impact categories based on scientific evidence and previous studies, 
when assessing the environmental sustainability of the diet, being intrinsically linked to food 
production. Some categories were gathered in one box because of similar justifications.

(e.g. Willet et al., 2019). However, others also demonstrate that nuts are water 
intensive crops and that 74% of irrigated nuts are currently cultivated under water 
stress (Vanham, Mekonnen and Hoekstra 2020).  As water is unequally available 
and distributed on Earth, the water consumption of diets is an important 
parameter to consider. 
Marine eutrophication and 
freshwater eutrophication 
Marine and freshwater eutrophication were addressed because highly influenced 
by the fertilizer application in agriculture for the food production. In life cycle 
assessment, freshwater and marine eutrophication are respectively modelled with 
kg P and kg N emitted (Payen, Cosme and H. Elliott 2021).  
An excessive nitrogen and phosphate concentration in water is stemming from 
leaching nutrients following fertilizer application (including manure) (Mateo-
Sagasta, Zadeh and Turral 2017). These excessive nutrient loads in run-off water 
are responsible for eutrophication, a phenomenon where algae start blooming 
excessively in aquatic ecosystems, leading to a decrease of the available oxygen 
and therefore a loss of other aquatic animals and plants (Mateo-Sagasta, Zadeh 
and Turral 2017). We therefore decided to include the categories marine 
eutrophication and freshwater eutrophication in our assessment. 
Land use and biodiversity 
loss 
Earth’s surface was composed of 71% of habitable land (104 million km²) in 
2019. Among these 71%, 50% were dedicated to agriculture (Ritchie and Roser 
2019). Moreover, land grabbing and consolidation are resulting from agrarian 
land use and are threatening land sovereignty (Baker-Smith and Boruss Miklos 
Attila 2016). The extension of the agricultural land has put more pressure on 
natural habitats, notably endangering biodiversity (Ritchie and Roser 2019). The 
later also being at risk due to the spread of chemical pesticides and use of heavy 
machinery (FAO 2019). For all these reasons, the impact categories land use and 
biodiversity loss were also thought to be of crucial importance. 
 
2.3.4. Environmental thresholds to assess the environmental indicators 
Studies have defined thresholds of emissions/pressure caused or maximum levels of impact 
tolerable by natural ecosystems, e.g. max amount of water consumed (e.g. Willet et al., 2019). 
The LCIA method can be complemented by the use of such thresholds (Ryberg, et al. 2018). 
Indeed, the LCIA method allows to quantify the environmental impacts caused by the 
production of food items constituting the diet, on several impact categories. Using thresholds 
then allows to evaluate whether the diet remains in a safe operating space (SOS) in terms of 
environmental sustainability, as defined by the Planetary Boundaries framework introduced by 
(Steffen, et al. 2015). In other words, these thresholds can then serve to define the 
==PAGE:17==
10 
 
environmental sustainability of the diet in absolute terms, by evaluating whether its impacts 
remain below thresholds (Ryberg, et al. 2018), i.e. in the safe zone.  
Two methods were applied for the definition of the thresholds to be used in the definition of 
the environmentally sustainable diet. We decided to use two different ways of proceeding in 
order to be able to later test the sensitivity of the model towards the thresholds. So first, 
quantitative thresholds were collected along the literature review for the environmental impact 
categories retained in Section 2.3.3 and a mean threshold was calculated across studies. Second, 
annual global thresholds from Gebara and Laurent (2021) were downscaled to daily individual 
thresholds for the food sector. The later was done using a sharing principle involving the gross 
value added (GVA). Explanations and details on the two methods used are provided in the 
followings. 
Method 1: literature review 
From the literature review, were extracted thresholds for several of our indicators, i.e. impact 
categories. However, some of the thresholds collected had to be downscaled (e.g. to a specific 
environmental compartment) so as to be compatible with the impact categories of interest of 
this study. This was the case for three impact categories: marine eutrophication, freshwater 
eutrophication and biodiversity loss.  
Marine eutrophication. Thresholds found in the literature review were defined as nitrogen 
loads expressed in kg-N applied on the soil, thus not specific to the emissions of nitrogen (N) 
to the marine water compartment associated to the impact category marine eutrophication. A 
reduction factor therefore had to be applied to the threshold found for N load, to consider the 
actual fraction of nitrogen emitted to the marine water and resulting from the application of 
nitrogen compounds (nitrates, ammonia, etc) on the soil. To do so, had to be considered the 
denitrification and physical processes (leaching, advection, removal) that were happening in 
the soil and the freshwater compartments before the dissolved nitrogen is released in the marine 
water. The denitrification consists of the reduction of nitrate into molecular nitrogen 
(atmospheric nitrogen), which thus leads to a decreased nitrogen concentration in the water 
(Payen, Cosme and H. Elliott 2021) and consequently reduces marine eutrophication level.

compartment is the receiving compartment for marine eutrophication. Nitrogen deposited on

2.3.7. Environmentally-mediated impact scores 
The Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) method is of particular interest when it comes to 
quantify the impacts humans exert on the planet and is therefore used to evaluate environmental 
impacts of food production. The principle behind the LCIA method is to adopt a life cycle 
perspective, assessing all impacts caused by a product on the environment, and through the 
whole life cycle of the considered product, i.e. from the cradle to the grave (Hauschild, 
Rosenbaum and Olsen 2018).  The LCIA method allows to translate elementary flows (i.e. 
material or energy flow that has not been transformed by humans; mainly emissions or 
extracted resources) into impacts on environmental impact categories. Figure 3 illustrates the 
environmental impact categories used in LCIA and displays the linkages midpoint impact 
categories and endpoint areas of protection (AoP). There are indeed two main levels of 
assessment levels in LCIA: midpoint and endpoint. The former being earlier in the cause-effect 
chain, between emission and endpoint category and the later representing Areas of Protection 
(AoP) of interest for society, regarding human health, ecosystem quality and natural resources 
(Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen 2018). All elementary flows will impact a certain 
environmental impact category and will therefore result in an impact score for the considered 
impact category (Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen 2018). 
==PAGE:22==
15 
 
The LCIA method was thus applied to collect impact intensities associated to the production 
of each food item. This was done by collecting unit processes which include input flows 
(materials, energy, resources) and output flows (products, waste, emissions) for the production 
of each food item in the LCA databases. The LCA software SimaPro (PRé Sustainability 2021) 
was then used to quantify the impacts caused by the food items production on the 
environmental impact categories of interest.  
Table 4 shows the different assessment methods that were used in SimaPro to calculate impact 
intensities and their assessment levels (midpoint or endpoint). In order to consider more effects 
in the cause-effect chain following emissions, we decided to calculate some impact intensities 
at endpoint while keeping others at midpoint. For instance, we decided to evaluate the impact 
intensities of the food items production on climate change and water consumption at midpoint 
as quantifying greenhouse gas emissions and water consumption (i.e. carbon and water 
footprints) rather than involving more effects caused by these substance flows is of interest in 
order to evaluate the direct footprints of the food items. We however decided to calculate 
impact intensities for marine eutrophication, phosphorus eutrophication, land use and 
biodiversity loss at endpoint, in order to capture more effects along the cause-effect chain and 
increase the environmental relevance of the assessment (Rosenbaum 2016). As there is no such 
“biodiversity loss” impact category in LCIA, it was decided to use the endpoint damage level 
AoP ecosystem quality as a proxy.   It is considered a proxy as, notably, Woods et al. (2018) 
have shown the need to refine the environmental relevance and comparability of the metrics 
used at damage-level. In addition, the category ecosystem quality represents the proportion of 
all species of an ecosystem that possibly extinct, no matter the cause of the disappearance but 
the category does not take into account the fact that different ecosystem will have different 
species that can be impacted (Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen 2018). Moreover, the use of 
the category ecosystem quality leads to some overlapping with other impact categories as it is 
an aggregation of other impact categories. Despite the uncertainty behind the use of this proxy, 
it still has some relevance, as the AoP ecosystem quality involves several impacts that may 
affect the quality of ecosystems (Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen 2018) which is intrinsically 
linked to biodiversity. 
The assessment was done for the production of 1 kg of each food item. Units of impact 
intensities however vary between environmental impact categories.  For instance, the 
production of 1 kg of beef will release a certain amount of greenhouse gases that will be 
translated via LCIA methods into kg-CO2-eq/kg-beef.  
In order to be in accordance with the fact that impact intensities for marine eutrophication, 
freshwater eutrophication and land use were calculated at endpoint level, the thresholds defined 
in Section 2.3.4 should be converted to endpoint level. This was done using the midpoint to 
endpoint conversion factors from ReCiPe 2016 v1.1. (Huijbregts, et al. 2016) which are 
displayed in Table 5.

2.4. Scenario development 
Several studies have pointed the need to reduce daily consumption of certain types of foods, to 
decrease pressures exerted on the environment. Often, the reduction of animal-sourced-
products is targeted and identified as a key leverage point to transition towards more 
sustainable diets (e.g. Chaudhary, Gustafson and Mathys, 2018; Chalmers et al., 2019; Willet 
et al., 2019).  Future scenarios where the world population would feed on plant-based diets 
have been investigated and revealed a resulting global reduction of agricultural land use from 
4 to 1 billion hectares (Ritchie 2021). While plant-based diets have also been associated to 
lower climate change, some of them may be unhealthy if not carefully managed (Lassen, 
Christensen and Trolle 2020). As more and more people start to avoid the consumption of 
certain animal-based products, it is crucial to investigate potential health deficiencies while 
defining environmentally sustainable diets. We have therefore decided to study different diet 
scenarios based on the inclusion or the exclusion of specific types of animal-sourced products 
to evaluate the results both in terms of nutrient intakes and environmental impacts. The 
definition of scenarios will also allow to partly consider the variety of dietary habits that exist 
around the world. Indeed, dietary habits are of essential interest as they strongly depend on 
personal beliefs, religions and food accessibility.   We therefore defined 6 scenarios, based on 
the presence or not of animal products, by considering different combinations of types of 
animal-based products, namely, meat, fish and seafood, animal-based dairy products, and eggs. 
The definition of the different diet scenarios was done by putting certain health constraints, i.e. 
intake levels, equal to 0 in the model. Table 6 displays the specifications of the scenarios 
assessed.

3. Results and discussion 
The literature reviews and the model led to promising results, which are displayed and 
discussed in the present section. The resulting nutritional intakes and environmental thresholds 
are detailed in Section 3.1 and Section 3.2, while the health and environmental performances 
of the resulting optimized diets are assessed in Section 3.3. 
3.1. Nutritional requirements for a healthy diet 
Out of 108 studies retrieved for the healthy component, 72 were reviewed and values were 
collected for 60 health indicators. The literature review allowed us to grasp values for a high 
number of health indicators and therefore increase the comprehensiveness and accuracy of the 
definition of a healthy diet. Indeed, until now, studies have used approximately 25 indicators 
to define a healthy diet, with few exceptions, e.g. Gómez et al. (2020) who used 42 health 
indicators. Table 7 displays the resulting health indicators, with associated recommended 
uptake ranges, i.e. minimum and maximum amounts of nutrients, minerals, energy and food 
types for a healthy diet. For clarity, the values were rounded before being displayed in Table 
7. These ranges are thus the ones inserted in the model in the form of linear equations (see 
Equation 2.1), i.e. health constraints to be fulfilled by the optimizer. The full set of equations is 
displayed in Supplementary information (Table B.22).  
The minimum, and maximum values were identified among reviewed studies to define a range 
for each indicator, as well as the mean and the standard deviation were calculated across 
studies. The uptake ranges are defined per person and per day. The resulting average age range 
was found to be 16.4-56.1 years while most studies suggested to align a healthy diet with a 
moderate level of physical activity, and we found an average of 38.3 min of effort 
recommended per day across studies. Our definition of a healthy diet is thus limited to adults. 
Further research should investigate whether the nutrient uptake ranges we defined are valid for 
children or if distinct daily nutrient needs should be incorporated. It is likely that daily nutrients 
needs for children in early infancy (i.e. under 5 years old) will be different (e.g. Nuruddin et 
al., 2018). Details on data used to calculate the average age range and recommended minutes 
of effort can be found in Supplementary information (Section 1.1. in Supplementary results). 
As seen in Table 7, the magnitude of the standard deviation varies a lot between indicators. On 
the one side, some have low standard deviation compared to their mean value. This means that 
consensus exists between studies for these indicators and values collected are thus similar. On 
the other side, others have a high standard deviation compared to their mean value, meaning 
that values defined by different studies are dispersed, making the definition of these indicators 
more uncertain. Furthermore, each indicator is defined based on two studies only, one for the 
minimum value and one for the maximum value. For instance, if the study retained for the 
definition of the minimum value is far from the mean calculated across all studies, the defined 
minimum value is relatively uncertain.

3.2. Environmental thresholds to benchmark an environmentally 
sustainable diet 
Table 8 displays the final environmental thresholds derived from both the literature review and 
the global thresholds from Gebara and Laurent (2021) downscaled using the Gross Value 
Added (GVA) sharing principle.  
3.2.1. Use of the literature review 
Out of 138 studies retrieved during the literature review on the sustainable component of the 
diet, 23 studies were reviewed for the environmental side and quantitative values, i.e. 
thresholds, could be collected for 6 indicators (impact categories). The mean threshold was 
calculated across studies for each impact category as well as the standard deviation. The 
standard deviation of the indicators marine eutrophication, freshwater eutrophication and 
biodiversity loss is equal to 0 because the calculation of the mean threshold across studies relies 
on 2 studies only, both involving the thresholds defined by Willet et al. (2019). The latter makes 
the definition of these thresholds uncertain. The indicator water consumption shows a standard 
deviation that is close to its mean value, because this threshold definition relies only on 4 
different studies, showing values of different magnitudes and thus increasing the uncertainty 
of that threshold. The indicators climate change and land use show lower standard deviation 
values compared to their mean. The two latter rely on more studies, 11 for climate change and 
5 for land use and show similar values, i.e. of the same magnitude order. This makes the 
definition of the thresholds for climate change and land use more robust. 
In addition, some thresholds from the literature review had to be downscaled to be applicable 
to a specific environmental compartment (see previous Equations 2.3 and 2.4). The reduction 
factors needed to obtain thresholds for the specific impact categories marine eutrophication 
and freshwater eutrophication were found to be respectively equal to 14% and 10%. The former 
means that from a quantity of nitrogen applied on the soil, 86% is retained in the soil and the 
freshwater compartments before being released in the marine water compartment. The latter 
indicates that from a quantity of phosphorus applied on the soil, 90% is retained in the soil 
before reaching the freshwater compartment. These percentages are however only estimations 
as the fate and characterization factors used are global values, thus disregarding spatial 
differentiation. Ideally, such factors should be based on the location, the meteorological events, 
site-specific conditions, and the agricultural practices in place, ultimately influencing the 
eutrophication levels in both marine and freshwater compartments (Helmes, et al. 2012). 
Hence, if future research involves a spatial differentiation and a definition of regionalised diets, 
site-specific fate and characterisation factors should be introduced.  
3.2.2. Use of the GVA sharing principle  
The use of the Gross Value Added (GVA) (see previous Equation 2.6) sharing principle led to 
a share of the environmental threshold that could be attributed to the food sector of 6.93%. This 
value depicts the utility added by the food sector to all other economic sectors. As shown in 
Table 8, the thresholds calculated using GVA are significantly lower than the mean thresholds 
derived from the literature review. The highest difference happens to be for the category land 
use for which the threshold defined using the literature review is ca. 11 times higher than the 
one defined using the GVA sharing principle. And the lowest difference is observed for the 
category freshwater eutrophication, with a threshold being ca. 2 times higher than the one 
defined using GVA. As thresholds defined using a sharing principle are intrinsically related to 
==PAGE:30==
23 
 
the type of principle used, alternative methods should be applied or researched to test the 
accuracy of the food sector share calculated in this study, which might have been 
underestimated.

3.3. Identification of healthy and environmentally sustainable diets 
3.3.1. Overview of diets 
The model led to a non-null optimized solution for each diet scenario, except for the vegan diet 
for which the model had no solution. This means that it in that case the optimizer was not able 
to fulfil the nutritional requirements while staying below the environmental thresholds. The 
absence of solutions for the vegan diet may be explained by the lack of diversity among food 
items in the list, preventing the provision of the required vitamins and minerals intakes. For 
instance, vitamin B12 intake is often targeted as challenged in a vegan diet as it is not present 
in the majority of plant-based foods (Costa Leite, et al. 2019). However, Spirulina (algae) 
which was not included in our list of food items, has been evaluated as a potential source of 
vitamin B12 (Madhubalaji, et al. 2019). Including Spirulina to the list could potentially have 
been a way to partly resolve the issue of not having solution for the vegan diet.  
Table 9 gives an overview of the solutions, i.e. diet composition, resulting from the model for 
the 5 other scenarios: omnivorous (O), pescatarian (P), lacto-ovo vegetarian (LOV), lacto-
vegetarian (LV) and ovo-vegetarian (OV). The obtained diets can be seen as healthy and 
environmentally sustainable, acknowledging the scope of this study. The diet composition is 
however highly influenced by the optimizing nature of the model, the constraint definition and 
the list of food items used in the model. As it can be seen in Table 9, some food items are 
redundant between the different diets. For instance, dates, walnuts, oats, dry couscous, corn 
oil, soybeans, tofu and cassava are present in all diets but in different quantities. The 
aforementioned food items therefore seem to be interesting, when combined together in a diet, 
both in terms of health and environmental impacts. A deeper analysis of the reasons behind the 
inclusion of these foods in our diets is provided in the following sections.  Interestingly, the O 
diet contains almost the same quantities of fish and seafood than the P diet. The latter is 
therefore not characterized by a higher consumption of fish and seafoods and the difference 
with the O diet happens for other food items. The following sections analyse the composition

3.3.2. Health performances 
Table 11 displays the micro-, macronutrients, energy and food categories intakes of the 5 diet 
scenarios and displays again the mean values derived from the literature review. Most 
nutritional intakes are relatively close to the mean values, but few exceptions happen. Indeed, 
for the omnivorous (O) diet for instance, the following indicators show a difference of a factor 
==PAGE:32==
25 
 
higher or equal to 3 between the value given by the model and the mean value extracted from 
the literature review: Vitamin D, Trans fatty acids, Fruits, Vegetables, Meat, Cow milk and 
alternatives, Cereal products, Whole grains, Processed cereals. In those cases, there is a high 
difference between the value given by the model and the mean extracted from the literature 
review. These differences demonstrate the uncertainty behind the definition of the constraints 
for these indicators and more research should be done to homogenize the dietary requirements 
across studies. 
As all resulting diets have nutritional intakes within ranges defined as healthy, they can all be 
considered healthy, and it makes it hard to evaluate which one would be the most interesting 
nutritionally. However, we decided to evaluate the health performance by focusing on 
identifying food items contributions to energy uptake and the following macronutrient uptakes: 
proteins, carbohydrates, fibers, lipids and sugars. An overview of the results of this analysis, 
i.e. food items that influence the highest number of macro-nutrients, is presented in Table 10 
and Figure 4 and Figure 5 are provided to display the specific cases of sugar and proteins. 
Figures for the food items contribution to energy, carbohydrates, fibers and lipids uptakes can 
be found in Supplementary information (Section 2 of Supplementary results).   
As shown in Figure 4 and Figure 5 (see also Table 9 in previous section), the lacto-ovo 
vegetarian (LOV) and ovo-vegetarian (OV) diets have different food items combinations, but 
the final nutritional uptakes are exactly the same. The later suggests that a diet free of any 
animal-based dairy product (OV) can provide the same nutritional intakes as a diet including 
animal-based dairy products (LOV). However, the LOV diet resulting from this study is also 
free of any animal-based dairy product and therefore makes this conclusion uncertain.

protein, fiber and lipid contents. Stylianou, Fulgoni III and Jolliet (2021) have also identified 
health benefits associated to the consumption of peanuts. In the same food category, walnuts 
are highlighted for the same macronutrients as peanuts. However, Sokolow, Kennedy and 
Attwood (2019) have shown that among nuts and seeds, walnuts have the lowest nutrient 
density, after sunflower seeds, almonds and hazelnuts. As well as they also found that coconuts 
and dates have low nutrient density. The fact that walnuts, dates and coconut were prioritized 
by the optimizer even though they are not the most interesting nutritionally according to 
Sokolow, Kennedy and Attwood (2019) means that they either have a positive effect on the 
environmental performance of the diets or that they contain significant amount of an essential 
micro-nutrient that was not considered by Sokolow, Kennedy and Attwood (2019). This is 
further developed in Sections 3.3.3 and 3.3.4. We also found that millet and oats have 
interesting macro-nutrient content for some of the diets. Sokolow, Kennedy and Attwood 
(2019) have also identified these foods as nutritious. At last, margarine is involved in the O 
and P diets but it has mostly been recommended to decrease or avoid its consumption (e.g. 
Verly et al., 2020) but Costa Leite et al., (2019) have however shown its interest as it contains 
vitamin D. As the same impact intensities were used for cassava and potatoes, the fact that 
cassava is selected shows that it is more interesting nutritionally than potatoes. Interestingly, 
higher quantities of pulses (soybeans in this case) are consumed in the non-vegetarian diets. 
The fewer quantity of soybeans in the vegetarian diets is compensated by higher quantities of 
couscous and whole grain noodles having good protein content. Overall, in terms of protein 
supply, anchovy and soybeans are prioritized. The relevance of these two food items is 
investigated in Sections 3.3 and 3.4.  
Including health-related diseases stemming from diets could help identifying the best option 
among several diets considered as healthy nutrient-wise. For instance, we observed that the 
calcium level drops in the LOV and the OV diets, and at the same time they do not include 
oatmeal beverage and 7 times less spinach than the other diets. These two food items are 
however richer in calcium than coconut milk and kale taken instead. In addition, (Sokolow, 
Kennedy and Attwood 2019) have found spinach to have a very high nutrient density. 
Nonetheless, as the Calcium level of the OV and LOV diets is within the range, both diets can 
be considered as healthy. Future research on the effects of such micro-nutrient decreases in 
some diets could help broadening the definition of healthy diets. 
Moreover, all optimized diets correspond to an energy intake of 1798 kcal, which is the lower 
boundary of the energy constraint. Indeed, a lower energy need implies lower needs of some 
foods and therefore lower environmental impacts on average. However, a person that does 
more sport than the average would probably need a higher energy intake than the one resulting 
from the model (e.g. Esteve-Llorens et al., 2018).

3.3.3. Environmental performances 
The environmental impacts resulting from the model were normalised to the thresholds, and 
for all diets, in order to evaluate the margin left for any increase of the impacts of the diets 
before getting out of the safe operating space, i.e. above thresholds. Figure 6 displays the 
normalised environmental impact results.  
The threshold is reached by all diets for the category water consumption, i.e. normalised result 
is equal to 1. There is thus no margin for an increase of the water footprint of the diets. The 
category freshwater eutrophication shows the second highest normalised scores (between 0.6 
and 0.8), meaning that there is less margin for increases before exceeding the threshold for that 
environmental impact category. More specifically, both the O diet and the LV diet have 
significantly higher normalisation scores (0.8). For the categories climate change, marine 
eutrophication, land use and biodiversity loss, the environmental impact levels of the LV diet 
show the less margin towards reaching the threshold, with normalised results going up to 0.60. 
Impact categories showing higher normalised scores will more likely be exceeded. Indeed, if a 
scenario of increasing population reaching 10 billion people is considered, or the model is 
forced to take food items having higher environmental footprints, the normalisation score 
would likely exceed 1, no longer respecting the definition of environmental sustainability of 
this study. However, Gerten et al. (2020) have identified a maximum population number of 
10.2 billion people that could be sustainably fed within four planetary boundaries, namely 
Biosphere integrity, Land-systems change, Freshwater use and Nitrogen flows. The fact that 
we involve more environmental categories consequently constrains more the safe operating 
space that can be allocated to the diets. This gives an indication of the importance of including 
as many environmental impact categories as possible when evaluating the environmental 
sustainability of diets.

3.3.4. Comparison across diets 
As shown in Table 12, the lacto-vegetarian (LV) diet performs the worst in the most 
environmental impact categories, and the pescatarian (P) diet performs the best in the most 
impact categories, i.e. has the lowest impact scores. More precisely, the LV has the highest 
impact score in the following impact categories: climate change, marine eutrophication, land 
use and biodiversity loss. The P diet instead has the lowest score in the following impact 
categories: climate change, water consumption, freshwater eutrophication, and land use. This 
first observation goes against conclusions brought by several studies (e.g. Chaudhary, 
Gustafson and Mathys, 2018; Chalmers et al., 2019; Willet et al., 2019)  about the need to 
switch to fully plant-based diets. However, the results of the present study should be nuanced. 
Indeed, it is notably worth noting that some differences between impact scores are very small 
and sometimes down to the thousandth. In addition, the solutions given by the model are 
optimized solutions and should not be taken as average diets. For instance, the fact that a 
pescatarian diet performs best is only valid for the specific combination of food items proposed 
by the optimizer (see Table 9). Furthermore, the O diet involves the same quantities of 
anchovies and mussels than the P diet. Hence the need to stress that the P diet is not consuming 
more fish and seafood than the O diet, unlike what might be suggested by its name.

12, the five diets have very similar water consumption and freshwater eutrophication impacts. 
Higher differences happen to be for climate change, biodiversity loss and marine

Climate change 
The pescatarian (P) diet has the lowest impact on climate change (4.10E-01 kgCO2eq/pers/d) 
closely followed by the ovo-vegetarian (OV) diet (4.48E-01 kgCO2eq/pers/d). The lacto-
vegetarian (LV) diet has a climate change impact score approximately 3 times higher than the 
one of the P diet. At the same time, the LV diet is also the only one containing  cow milk-based 
cheeses and cream, having the highest climate change impact score (approx. 7.7 kgCO2 eq/kg) 
of all food items. However, this ranking of diet should be taken cautiously. Indeed, the “fish 
and seafood” component of the pescatarian diet resulting from this study is composed of 
anchovy and mussels. These two food items have lower impacts compared to others and a P 
diet involving other types of fishes (e.g. salmon, tuna) would drop the climate change impact 
score. Indeed, the production of 1 kg of salmon for instance has an impact score of 7 kg CO2 
eq whereas 1 kg of anchovy produces 5.78E-02 kg CO2eq. Besides, the National Oceanic and 
Atmospheric Administration Fisheries have created an online database to help consumers in 
making more sustainable seafood choices and anchovies have been identified as a smart choice 
in terms of sustainability (NOAA 2021). In addition, the P diet includes the highest quantity of 
tofu, having a negative climate change impact score, i.e. -5.26E-01 kgCO2/kg, consequently 
diminishing the resulting climate change impact of the P diet. The fact that tofu has a negative 
impact score for climate change is due to the presence of biowaste treatment processes that can 
eventually influence positively (i.e. decrease) the impact scores by generating reusable 
materials and flows (e.g. biogas, heat). 
Water consumption 
The five diets have very similar water footprints, with differences down to the thousandth. At 
the same time, they all include high quantities of walnuts and date, both having high negative 
water impact intensities of -1.90E-02 and -1.29E+01 m³-world-eq/kg, respectively. The former 
being due to wastewater treatment processes and the later because of the use of renewable 
energies (wind power and anaerobic digestion) during the production. The presence of these 
two food items in the list is therefore crucial to maintain the resulting water footprint just equal 
to the threshold. The O diet has a higher water consumption because it contains chicken which 
has the highest water use intensity (1.07E+01 m³/kg) as well as eggs (9.55E-00 m³/kg), both 
are due to high water intensity irrigation processes.  
Freshwater eutrophication 
The Omnivorous (O) diet has the highest impact score for the category freshwater 
eutrophication whereas the P diet shows the smallest one. Both diets include coconut and tofu 
which both have negative impact scores for that category, but the P diet includes ca. 4 times 
more tofu than the O diet. Tofu has a negative impact score for that environmental impact 
category (-5.73E-10 species.yr/kg) and this higher negative contribution therefore decreases 
the final freshwater eutrophication impact score of the P diet.  In the case of tofu, the negative 
character is mainly due to the production of whey (i.e. the “soy milk” resulting from tofu 
processing). The later might be the cause of chemical or biological reactions that decrease the 
==PAGE:39==
32 
 
overall impact on freshwater eutrophication. Indeed, the negative character of freshwater 
eutrophication impact of coconut production is mainly due to the treatment of spoil from lignite 
mining. The later process releases several compounds to the water compartment of the 
environment, notably iron and sulphate. A possible explanation could be that the two later 
compounds bound to create ferric sulphate which can then precipitate the phosphates contained 
in run-off water and ultimately decrease the freshwater eutrophication level (Ngatia and Taylor 
2018).  
Marine eutrophication 
The LV diet has the highest impact on the category marine eutrophication. The impact score 
of the LV diet on marine eutrophication is 1.5 times higher than the one of the P diet for 
instance. This is also due to higher impact scores associated to the consumption of cow milk-
based dairy products. Indeed, the latter are associated to high releases of urea (nitrogen 
compound) from cow manure towards the environment, consequently increasing nitrogen 
eutrophication. Brizga, Kurppa and Heusala (2021) have instead attributed the marine 
eutrophication impacts caused by milking cows to the production of grass-silage feed. They 
however mentioned the need to refine their study with the inclusion of manure storage practices 
etc. 
Biodiversity impact 
The LV diet has an impact score approximately 1.7 times higher than the other diets, on the 
category biodiversity loss. This is also influenced by the presence of cow milk-based dairy 
products. The latter indeed have higher impact scores than vegetable milks for instance. Indeed, 
the production of 1 kg of brie cheese leads to an impact score on biodiversity loss of 8.17E-08 
species.yr/kg whereas the production of 1 kg of oatmeal beverage and coconut milk 
respectively have impact scores of 4.17E-09 and 3.58E-09 species.yr/kg. We found that this 
impact on biodiversity is related to the production of plant ensilage food to feed the cattle. 
Land use 
The LV diet has the highest impact score for the category land use and the P diet the smallest 
one, closely followed by the O diet. The LV diet is also the only one involving Sunflower seeds 
showing the highest land use impact intensity of all food items (7.21E-13 species.yr/kg ). In 
addition, anchovies have the lowest land use impact (1.81E-17 species.yr/kg) and appear in 
high quantities in both the O and P diets. The latter is since the anchovies considered in this 
study are fished from the sea and not raised in farms on land.

All in all, we cannot conclude from our analysis of the health and environmental performance 
that vegetarian diets (OV, LV, LOV) are better than non-vegetarian ones (O, P), as differences 
are very low. Yet, we show that it is possible to establish optimized healthy and 
environmentally sustainable diets for the five scenarios. The latter are however highly 
influenced by the trade-offs among impact intensities of food items, as some have negative 
values, thus favoured in high quantities by the optimizer. Our optimized results also suggest 
that a lower energy intake (1798 kcal/pers/d for all scenarios) may be a way to decrease 
environmental impacts. The latter was also suggested by Wood, Alam and Dupras (2019) who 
has found that a decrease from 2600 to 2000 kcal/pers/d along with a 50% food waste reduction 
could decrease environmental impacts of diets. We also showed that some food items are 
interesting both in terms of macro-nutrient intakes and environmental impacts, namely: 
anchovy, tofu, coconut, walnuts and dates. Moreover, we found that the consumption of 
cheeses is a good protein source in the LV diet but that it also leads to higher environmental 
impacts. 
 
3.4. Diet patterns and sensitive food items 
The diversity of food items constituting the diets vary between 19 and 22 food items, with the 
omnivorous (O) diet being the most diverse (22 food items) and the pescatarian (P) and lacto-
ovo vegetarian (LOV) diets the less (19 food items). This is intrinsically related to the way the 
scenarios were defined and the optimizing nature of the model. Indeed, the O diet scenario 
includes more health constraints and thus intakes of more food categories (e.g. eggs, fish, etc) 
than the LV diet for instance. 
3.4.1. Consumption of plant-based foods 
Among pulses, soybeans are prioritized in our model. They indeed have relatively lower impact 
intensities compared to other types of pulses. Also, both tofu and soybeans are part of the soy 
products category and therefore involving soybeans instead of other pulses allows the model 
to fulfil both constraints on pulses and soy products intakes. Sokolow, Kennedy and Attwood 
(2019) have also found soybeans to have the highest nutrient density among pulses as well as 
==PAGE:41==
34 
 
the FAO has demonstrated the importance of soybean consumption to fight world hunger (FAO 
2004). 
In terms of vegetables and fruits, pineapples, coconuts, dates, carrots, kale and spinach seem 
to be the most interesting both nutritionally and environmentally. However, pineapples, dates 
and coconuts are produced in certain regions of the world only, showing warmer climate than 
in North Europe for instance. Adding a comprehensive inclusion of all transportation processes 
to this study may lead to a different selection of fruits, as acknowledging the emissions related 
to importation of non-local and non-seasonal foods may change the results. Even though 
studies involving life cycle analyses have already shown that food transportation does not have 
the highest environmental impacts among food sector activities, plant-based diets might 
however show higher impacts notably due to importation of pulses, soy products, fruits etc. 
(Wakeland, Cholette and Venkat 2012). 
Among alternative dairy products, oatmeal milk is the most interesting both nutritionally and 
environmentally while soy milk has environmental impact intensities on average 1000 times 
higher than oatmeal milk. The latter is on the one side due to the fact that the process (from the 
LCA database) used to evaluate the impact intensity of soymilk is a market process and 
therefore includes transport processes and on the other side because the higher footprints are 
due to soy cultivation being more impactful. All three vegetarian diets include the consumption 
of seaweeds, ranging from 10 to 17 grams per day. MacArtain et al. (2007) have indeed shown 
the beneficial nutrients (e.g. fatty acids, fibers) contained in 8 g of seaweeds and their 
contribution to a daily balanced diet. 
3.4.2. Consumption of animal-based products 
The O diet, which is the only one including meat does not include red meat (lamb, beef, mutton) 
nor pork. This is due to their higher environmental impact intensities and to the constraint 
definition. Indeed, as the constraints were defined by taking minimum and maximum values 
from the literature review, some constraints have a minimum at 0 (e.g red meat) while others 
have a non-nul minimum (e.g. poultry, fish), imposing the optimizer the take the minimum 
quantity required.  The model fulfils the requirements in terms of animal products but then 
compensates and/or fills the nutritional gaps with more food items that are less impactful on 
the environment. Furthermore, several studies have identified higher risks of developing heart 
diseases and colorectal cancer due to red meat consumption (e.g. Cena and Calder, 2020) and 
therefore considered it as unhealthy. As chicken was used in the O diet, it is more interesting 
both nutritionally and environmentally than turkey and duck. 
Despite the recommendation of previous studies to fully switch towards plant-based diets, this 
study offers some nuances. For instance, anchovies fished from the ocean have lower impact 
scores in all 5 environmental impact categories than chickpeas. In addition, within animal-
sourced products, more environmentally choices can be done. Indeed, we found for example 
that certain types of cheeses (brie cheese, queso Anejo) were nutritionally and environmentally 
preferable over others (e.g. gouda, parmesan) in the list. The distinction and identification of 
specific foods that can be healthier and more environmentally sustainable among a certain food 
category goes along with the findings of Stylianou, Fulgoni III and Jolliet (2021). They indeed 
prone that small changes in individual food items can lead to significant improvements in terms 
of environmental sustainability and health. 
==PAGE:42==
35 
 
The food items selected by the model to fulfil the constraints could be seen as key leverage 
foods in order to transition towards healthy and more sustainable lifestyles. However, caution 
should be applied as one food item combined with others that are not included in the present 
study might not lead to low environmental footprints. The sensitivity of several food items was 
therefore tested in Section 3.4.3. 
3.4.3. Sensitive food items 
Running the code several times and each time removing either anchovy, dates, soybeans, 
oatmeal, or walnuts led to impact scores that were globally higher than in the base case 
scenario. This is due to the use of the optimizer which is intrinsically related to the choice of 
the most interesting food items both environmentally and nutritionally. Therefore, it was also 
tried to run the code after having removed all the aforementioned food items together, and in 
that case no solution was found for none of the diets. Removing only tofu from the list did not 
lead to any solution either when running the model. This shows the importance of these food 
items in the model. It also shows that more food items should be added in order to increase the 
variety and number of equivalent options among foods. However, the number of food items 
included in the list was limited by their availability in the LCA databases. Further work is 
therefore needed to augment the diversity of foods represented in the LCA databases. The food 
items that we used proxies for because they were missing in the LCA databases are highlighted

Overall, the optimizing character of the model leads to the observation that all food items

included. The negative character can also signify that the production of the food item has a 
positive effect on the environment, e.g. that some chemical reaction happens to decrease the 
final flow of emissions to the environment. 
3.4.4. Threshold definition 
In order to test the robustness of the model towards the thresholds, it was decided to run the 
code with the second set of thresholds defined using the GVA. The latter led to 0 solution for 
all diets, meaning that the set of thresholds is a sensitive parameter in this study. In fact, the 
GVA-based thresholds are smaller than the ones from the literature review, making the 
resolution of the optimization problem more challenging. Other sharing principle should be 
applied to refine the definition of these thresholds.

The optimized solutions obtained for each diet of this study could be seen as ways to get closer 
to the achievement of SDG-2. However, the scope and limitations of this study prevent a 
complete achievement of the goal. In fact, SDG-2 is made of several targets, being described 
in brief terms in Table 14 (United Nations 2015):

Our diets also show environmental impacts below thresholds, meaning that they are within a

decreasing pressures exerted and allowing more agroecosystem resilience (Blesh, et al. 2019).

Yet, targets 2.1, 2.3, 2.4, 2.5, 2.A, 2.B, 2.C involve other aspects not taken into account in our 
model. Indeed, in order to incorporate more sustainability dimensions to the model, future 
studies should include food accessibility indicators (e.g. food prices, indicators of access to 
grocery shops, etc), crop productivity scenarios, minimum income level, different agricultural 
practices, and geopolitical and international trade aspects. The challenge lies in finding 
quantitative ways to model these aspects to include them in our model. For instance, the 
economic aspect could be tackled by collecting prices associated to each food item along with 
a definition of a food price threshold not to exceed to be economically sustainable. In Section 
3.6 we identify and discuss socio-economic and political aspects for several socio-economic 
and political indicators for which quantitative values should be associated to be entered in the 
model in later studies.  
3.6. Socio-economic dimension of a sustainable diet 
Several socio-political and economic targets/actions were collected during the literature review 
conducted for the sustainable component of the diet and are discussed in the present section, to 
gather more sustainability aspects that should be taken into account by policy-makers when 
establishing dietary guidelines or by model developers in future studies on the topic.  
3.6.1. Creating conscious and transparent buying environments 
Empowering the sustainability of the diet can also be done through educational means. There 
is a need to raise the awareness of the consumer to direct his choices towards more sustainable 
foods. Indeed, Costa Leite et al. (2019) emphasised the need to give access to evidence-based 
education, to trigger to customers’ appetite for plant-based diets. In the same vein, Fanzo et al. 
(2020) pointed the need to use Front of Package labels (FOP) to create selling/buying 
environments understood and accessible by/to all, regardless the level of education. This idea 
of bettering product information and improve transparency was also highlighted by Downs, et 
al. (2020). More transparent buying environments may trigger significant behavioural changes 
towards more sustainable and healthy diets. 
==PAGE:45==
38 
 
3.6.2. Adaptation of social norms and empowerment of local food systems  
Sustainability can also be enhanced through the support of fair local food systems and the 
adaptation of social norms (Jacobi, Mukhovi, et al. 2020). Indeed, the latter can lead to 
substantial changes in consumption patterns and notably increase the demand for plant-based 
diet (Leite, et al. 2019). Favouring local food systems has mostly been seen as a positive aspect 
to generate local jobs, money and develop local territories (Seconda, et al. 2019) (Moragues-
Faus and Marceau 2018). Indeed, local stakeholders can regain the value of their work more 
easily while increasing their revenues and sharing the responsibility of food provision 
(Seconda, et al. 2019). Nutritional quality, safe labour conditions and stakeholders should be 
reinforced by local food system (Downs, et al. 2020). However, as mentioned by Allen et al. 
(2018) there is also risk of higher vulnerability associated to more local diets, due to higher 
local biodiversity losses. It is therefore important to find the right balance between boosting 
short-supply chains and avoiding putting more pressure on local biodiversity. 
3.6.3. Agricultural changes, reduction of food waste and animal welfare 
Fanzo et al. (2020) evaluated the reference diet defined by the EAT Lancet (Willet, et al. 2019) 
and emphasized the need to reconsider current agricultural practices in order to be able to feed 
the global population with the EAT Lancet diet. This implies the establishment of agro-
ecological techniques, climate-smart agriculture, and sustainable intensification (Fanzo, et al. 
2020). 
Several studies also highlighted the need to reduce food waste in order to be able to sustainably 
feed the global population (e.g. Leite et al., 2019; Downs et al., 2020). Wood, Alam and Dupras 
(2019) also reported that food losses were mainly composed of meats, poultry and fish (30%), 
perishable vegetables (19%) and dairy products (17%). Focusing on reducing food waste 
stemming from these categories would help avoiding unnecessary production and increasing 
the efficiency of the food system. 
Moreover, as part of the definition of sustainability should be included the protection of animal 
welfare. Animals should have access to safe and comfortable nutritional conditions (Downs, et 
al. 2020).  
3.6.4. Affordability of the diet 
Healthy diets sometimes cost more because of higher prices of healthier nutrient sources 
(Verly, et al. 2020). Therefore, some studies have set price levels not to exceed for the diet to 
be affordable and economically sustainable. Until now, studies on diet prices have been 
oriented towards specific countries (e.g. Verly et al., 2020; Wrieden et al., 2019), and have 
been focusing on high or middle income countries. In fact, as the budget allocated to the diet 
is intrinsically related to the income, and therefore to the economic situation of a certain 
country, it is hard to define an average affordable diet price for all.  
It is thus important to distinguish low- from middle- and high-income countries. The literature 
review led to numerical data for middle-income (Verly, et al. 2020) and high-income (e.g. Jetter 
et al., 2018; Faksová et al., 2019; Wrieden et al., 2019) countries. Table 15 displays the diet 
prices used to make the calculations. Raw prices collected during the literature review can be 
found in Supplementary information (Section 1.2 in Supplementary method). Values for USA 
and UK are significantly higher than the values attributed to Brazil and Czech Republic. This 
is expected since the average salary of these two countries is also higher (see Table 15).

3.6.5. Preservation and enhancement of food security 
Establishing a healthy diet may lead to several positive health-related impacts, but it may also 
put some populations at risk, threatening food security, notably in low-income regions (Leite, 
et al. 2019). The adoption of a broad perspective is therefore crucial. In fact, when defining 
healthy guidelines, it should be made sure that socio-cultural and economic contexts are 
adequate and ready for such transition (Leite, et al. 2019). Food security should be ensured 
notably through the development of more types of distribution channels (Mora, et al. 2020), 
access to water and capacity to store and process food, and affordability of food in terms of 
prices (Jacobi, Mukhovi, et al. 2020). Furthermore, food storage technology should be 
upgraded (Wang, et al. 2020) to facilitate food accessibility.  
==PAGE:47==
40 
 
Moreover, while for some a healthy diet would imply a reduction of the consumption of animal-
sourced products and a transition towards a plant-based diet, some countries such as India or 
South Africa in the world would still consider that the consumption of more animal-sourced 
elements is healthier (Mora, et al. 2020). Such differences should therefore also be taken into 
account when assessing diet sustainability. 
3.7. Political dimension of a sustainable diet 
Political aspects to define a sustainable diet were collected in terms of key concepts to pay 
attention to when establishing a healthy diet, for the following indicators: trade, 
governance/rights and geopolitical stakes.  
3.7.1. Appropriate geopolitics and inclusive governance environments 
Several tools can be used and put in place by governors and states in order to favour and 
smoothen the establishment of a healthy and sustainable diet. For instance, food procurement 
standards, environmental policies, economic incentives (e.g. taxation of high-environmental-
impacts-foods) and discouragement policies (e.g. media campaigns), should be introduced to 
influence consumers choices (e.g. Downs et al., 2020; Fanzo et al., 2020). At lower scale (e.g. 
retailers), actions can also be taken. For example, the use of labelling, specific food disposition 
in shops and reduction of plate size can trigger behavioural change at the consumer level as 
well as at the industry level (e.g. Wang et al., 2020). Some of these tools (e.g. labelling) have 
mainly been used in high-income countries (Fanzo, et al. 2020) and they should therefore be 
applied in low-income countries too. 
Some shifts should also happen among geopolitical stakes to reach more efficient ways of 
dealing with resources. In ideal cases, the integrity of the environment should be kept through 
better management of surpluses and deficits among holders (Willet, et al. 2019). However, 
sometimes political and economic reasons get in the way and prevent these exchanges, putting 
some regions more at risk (Willet, et al. 2019). Changes in trade management could lead to 
better repartition of food produced between countries while avoiding pressure put on high 
biodiversity-level-regions (Willet, et al. 2019). 
Moreover, policies should include more aspects aiming at solving environmental issues for 
future generations. Indeed, as mentioned by Moberg et al. (2020), there is a need to define 
environmental health goals and associated metrics and include these in politics. 
3.7.2. Multi-level partnerships and rights to food 
Moragues-Faus and Marceau (2018) have highlighted the need to gather forces across sectors 
and strengthen food partnerships in order to establish food programmes and strategies at the 
city scale for instance. Mora et al. (2020) has instead accentuated the need of partnerships at 
international level, to address problems from different perspectives/sectors. This idea of 
embracing expertise was also mentioned by Jetter et al. (2018), emphasizing the use of 
collaborative research basics, daily objectives, and technical assistance. All in all, there is a 
need to work together and across levels, allowing synergies and interlinkages, notably between 
the rural and the urban sectors (Mora, et al. 2020) to achieve greater goals, while reducing 
losses and inefficiencies throughout food supply chain.  
Finally, rights to food should include water and seeds accessibility, availability of diverse and 
nutritional food and finally food-related knowledge should be shared (Jacobi, Mukhovi, et al. 
2020). Furthermore, right to land should be emphasised. As potential agricultural land 
==PAGE:48==
41 
 
expansions might stem from the adoption of healthier diets, appropriate management should 
be done to preserve forests and land areas notably needed for energy production (Mora, et al. 
2020). 
3.8. Uncertainties and limitations 
The present section presents the main sources of uncertainty identified throughout the study as 
well as the limitations, mainly related to the choice of model, data collection and constraint 
definition and the lack of inclusion of further aspects to tackle SDG-2. 
3.8.1. Choice of model and LCIA data collection 
Instead of using an optimizer minimizing global warming while also fulfilling all constraints, 
another type of model could be used. Indeed, using a model that would find a set of solutions 
fulfilling the health and environmental constraints (in the form of linear equations) without 
optimizing would potentially lead to more solutions and a higher diversity among the diets. 
This would also have helped in defining an average diet instead of an optimized diet specific 
to a certain set of food items. In addition, as it was decided to minimize the climate change, a 
certain number of possible solutions having a higher carbon footprint but still under the 
threshold were bypassed. Future studies could therefore try to use the same set of data and 
constraints but in another type of model.  
Moreover, as some food items were not available in the LCA databases, impact scores were 
collected in the LCA literature or proxies were used for food items missing in the LCA 
databases consequently increasing the uncertainty of the values collected as some of them 
might have been under- or over-estimated. Besides, processes defined at global scale were 
prioritized when available in LCA databases, yet this was not always the case. Some impact 
scores are therefore uncertain because proxies corresponding to a specific region (e.g. Rest of 
the World or specific country) were used instead. More details on the proxies and data collected 
from the literature can be found in Supplementary information (Table A.21).  
A thorough inclusion of transport processes was not pursued during this study. The majority of 
LCA processes used for the collection of impact scores were “market processes”, named so 
because they represent the market for products of a certain region, and they include transport 
processes. However, few exceptions used were transformation processes, that do not include 
transport. In addition, market processes defined for a certain region include an average 
transportation scenario for the given region. Nonetheless, this study was pursued at global 
scale, some processes used are thus defined for smaller regions and might have misestimate 
some impact intensities. 
Agricultural practices used in food production were not acknowledged either when collecting 
processes from the LCA databases. This might have led to some inconsistencies and 
overestimations of the impacts of the diets. Indeed, different types of agriculture (e.g. organic, 
conventional) have different impacts on the environment  (Blesh, et al. 2019).  
3.8.2. Constraint definition and health-related issues 
The definition of the healthy diet of the present study has been based on nutrient profiles and 
intakes from specific food categories. Health-related diseases such as diabetes, high coronary 
disease and such have not been taken into account. Considering these aspects could broaden 
the definition of the healthy diet and strengthen the outcomes of the study. 
==PAGE:49==
42 
 
Moreover, the definition of nutrient intake ranges was done by taking the minimum and 
maximum values from all values collected during the literature review. As a result, each 
obtained range depends on two studies only which makes it quite uncertain. Some nutrient 
intakes brought by the model are indeed close to the minimum or maximum defined and thus 
far from the mean value calculated across studies reviewed. In addition, the serving sizes used 
to calculate some of the constraints might have been overestimated, leading to maximum value 
being far from mean values across studies. Furthermore, the definition of the environmental 
thresholds should also be further studied, notably by investigating other sharing principles to 
downscale global thresholds to the food sector. 
3.8.3. Missing aspects to meet SDG-2 
In this study we decided to take on a global perspective as global average diets across regions 
have some relevance, notably when conducting large-scale studies, where differentiation is not 
always possible. However, the variety of climates and site-specific conditions existing all over 
the world makes these “one diet fits all” diets very uncertain when it comes to consider site 
specific conditions and reduce inequities in poorest regions (Thorkildsen and Reksnes 2020). 
A regionalisation should therefore be performed, considering local conditions in several 
regions, in terms of, notably, economic systems, dietary habits, population number, 
meteorological conditions, farming system, soil fertility, imports and exports in order to 
consider as much as possible the variety of existing conditions. This will allow to tackle more 
targets of SDG-2, notably by considering the situation in some developing countries. The 
definition of spatially differentiated healthy and sustainable diets could help taking site-specific 
conditions into account. As well as it should be verified whether the healthy and 
environmentally sustainable diets defined in this study are suitable to children. Indeed, the latter 
is of importance as for instance, of 534 million people living in West and Central Africa, half 
of them are children (UNICEF n.d.). Meeting SDG-2 will only be possible if a comprehensive 
perspective is adopted, considering people from all age. 
Moreover, our quantitative definition of the sustainability component of the diet integrated in 
the model is limited to the environmental sustainability and to certain categories of impacts. 
On the one side, more environmental impact categories (e.g. resource depletion) should be 
taken into account in order to strengthen the definition of the environmental sustainability and 
avoid burden shifting. On the other side, more aspects of sustainability should be quantitatively 
defined to be added in models like the one used in this study. Overall, there is a need to 
quantitatively define more aspects of sustainability notably by defining quantitative targets for 
socio-economic and political indicators that should be acknowledged when defining healthy 
and sustainable diets.

4. Conclusions and Recommendations 
The objective of this study was to define healthy and sustainable diets as a pathway to tackle 
SDG-2 “Zero Hunger”.  To do so, we first used a numerical model to define healthy and 
environmentally sustainable diets in quantitative terms and we then identified socio-economic 
and political aspects that should be acknowledged to broaden the sustainability component of 
diets.  
The thorough literature review pursued during this study allowed us to associate quantitative 
values and constraints to 60 indicators to be used to define healthy diets. We also set a safe 
operating space for the diets to be environmentally sustainable, by defining thresholds of 
maximum impact tolerable by natural ecosystems, for several categories (climate change, water 
consumption, freshwater eutrophication, marine eutrophication, land use and biodiversity loss). 
We found that five different diet scenarios, i.e. omnivorous, pescatarian, lacto-ovo vegetarian, 
lacto-vegetarian and ovo-vegetarian could fulfil our health constraints while being below 
environmental thresholds. Based on these results, we analysed the health and environmental 
performance of the diets and showed the importance of combinations of food items and trade-
offs among environmental impact intensities of foods.  In addition, we found that among food 
categories, more environmentally sustainable choices can be made, as well as the consumption 
of specific food items allows to partly cover several macronutrient intakes at a time.  
We anticipate our numerical model to serve as a first step for the development of more 
comprehensive models, that could then be used to draw pathways towards the achievement of 
SDG-2. Our model could indeed be further developed to do a spatial differentiation and pursue 
a regionalisation, considering more indicators, such as transportation, site-specific conditions, 
agricultural practices, economic systems, and cultures to name but a few, and ultimately 
acknowledging the heterogeneity among food production systems around the world. 
Finally, we identified the limitations and uncertainties of our study and suggest additional 
aspects that should be included in future studies to increase the comprehensiveness of the 
definition of healthy and sustainable diets in a way to get closer to meeting SDG-2.
