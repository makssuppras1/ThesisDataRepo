Abstract
A combined measurement of 3D shape and spectral characteristics is of great interest
in various fields of science and technology, such as agriculture, food production, art
conservation and medical analysis. In this thesis we present a novel method for ac-
quiring this data, using a hyperspectral projection system that has been constructed
by modifying a commercially available DLP projector and replacing its light source
with a tunable supercontinuum laser. By using the modified projector in conjunc-
tion with a stereo camera pair, 3D measurements are gathered with sub-millimeter
precision, utilizing structured light patterns. This can be done over the system’s
entire operating range of 504-735 nm, while simultaneously recording the reflected
spectrum, resulting in data with high spectral and spatial resolution.
A number of possible applications for the system have been briefly investigated.
For instance has the measured spectrum been used to distinguish a real plant from
a fake one, and multiple translucent test objects have been scanned to evaluate the
wavelength dependant effect on 3D measurement accuracy.

Introduction
Hyperspectral imaging is an expanding field, which enables information to be gath-
ered for a target and its material properties, by examining it across multiple light
spectra. This is of interests in many different areas of science and technology and
for that reason more and more relevant use cases continue to be discovered. Among
the more prevalent are analysis of food by structure, composition and quality [3, 32],
various medical applications such as disease diagnosis and surgical guidance [33],
analysis of plant disease [9, 35] and analysis of objects of artistic and historic value
[5].
1.1
Problem statement
Many of the shortcomings of the different hyperspectral imaging techniques are
caused by the intrusiveness of these measurements. In many cases the material in
question has to be either dissected or dissolved in order to achieve good results. Of-
ten this is not an option, for example when dealing with quality control or studying
non-replaceable objects such as artwork.
In these cases the technique of reflectance spectroscopy is often utilized, where a
white light source illuminates the target and the reflected spectrum is recorded. This
is relatively simple when measuring on flat surfaces of small uniform areas, but when
the topography and shape become more abstract, problems arise by artefacts in the
measurement introduced by the change in the light incidence angles. It is possible to
correct for some of these geometry-related artefacts if the 3D shape of the target is
known [7]. This implies that gathering information about the 3D shape of the target
will yield better results.
The access to spatial data gathered at multiple wavelengths also opens up for
new possibilities in precision 3D scanning. When scanning, the different optical
properties of materials can have a negative impact on the precision of 3D scans.
For instance, translucent objects are problematic due to light penetration, causing
points to be measured below the actual surface of the object. This issue can, to some
extent, be mitigated by scanning at shorter wavelengths, which generally exhibit less
penetration depth [51]. This can be seen illustrated in Figure 1.1.

1.2
Project formulation
The suggested solution is to develop a hyperspectral projection system, which can be
used to gather both spectral and spatial data, using structured light. Structured light
is the process of projecting a known pattern onto a target and then inferring the 3D
shape of the target from how the pattern is deformed by the target’s surface, based
on acquired images. By varying the wavelength of the projected pattern between
acquisitions, a hyperspectral 3D model of the target can be measured. This system is
constructed by modifying a DLP projector, so that its light source can be substituted
with a supercontinuum laser, that is fed through a controllable filter. An illustra-
tion of this setup can be seen in Figure 1.2, where a computer serves as a common
interface between the different parts.

Generally, when using structured light, the 3D reconstruction is performed using
a projector combined with either a stereo camera pair or a single camera.
A two camera system has been chosen to ensure the quality of the 3D recon-
struction. This is due to the necessary modifications of the projector throughout the
project, which might diminish its precision. If a single camera setup is used, the
quality of the 3D reconstruction highly depends on the precision of both the camera
and the projector calibration.
The system’s purpose is foremost to act as a general proof of concept and is not
designed towards a specific use case. Therefore there are no clearly defined demands
for the system’s specifications. However, some of the system’s specifications, such as
scanning volume, scanning speed, spectral range, and both spectral and spatial reso-
lution, are limited by the resources available for the project. The available equipment
used to construct the setup will be elaborated in Section 5.2.

2
Project management
The project was divided into three iterations. Each iteration was designed to end
with a standalone product, each adding functionality to the previous iteration. Using
a MoSCoW-inspired approach [37], the first iteration contains the minimal function-
ality which must be fulfilled. The second iteration adds functionality that should be
realized. Lastly, the third iteration adds functionality that could be implemented if
time permits.
In the first iteration, we focused on modifying the projector so that it could be
coupled with the laser light source, to perform simple experiments. In the second
iteration, a comprehensive framework was developed in order to combine the pro-
jector with the laser, filter and cameras in more advanced experiments. The third
iteration experiments with data generated by the developed equipment. This itera-
tive structure ensures that the most important features are developed first and helps
decrease the impact of a potential delay in the time plan, since it will then affect the
lower priority features in the end of the plan.
The developed time plan can be seen on page 4. Each iteration has been split
into a number of tasks, listed below it. To each task a responsible person has been
assigned together with an estimate of the number of workdays needed to complete
that specific task. The estimates are based on intuition and, where possible, prior
knowledge. Due to the uncertainties these kinds of estimates bring to a time plan,
one week of buffer time has been allocated in the end of the time plan.
In an attempt to ensure an even distribution of workload throughout the project
the estimated needed workdays for tasks have been distributed over the assigned
work spans for each task. This is shown using white numbers on the allocated work
spans in the plan, shown in shades of blue. All of these white numbers have been
summed in the bottom row to show the estimated needed workdays, in a resolution
of half week periods.
In conjunction with the time plan, a risk analysis was also carried out. This can
be seen as the ’Risk index’ in the time plan. Each task has been evaluated on a scale
from one to five, in both likelihood and impact, where a score of one means very
unlikely and negligible and a score of five means very likely and severe, respectively.
The overall risk is evaluated as the average of the likelihood and the impact. In
Appendix A the high risk tasks are discussed in further detail, along with suggested
measures for avoiding and handling possible issues.
During each week, a weekly report has been fashioned based on the work done,
discussing topics such as methods, problems and results. These reports forms the
foundation for the thesis.
To help ease the project management, a range of tools have been selected for
various tasks. A short description and reasoning for the choices can be found in
Appendix B.

Related work
In hyperspectral imaging, the electromagnetic spectrum must be segmented into
multiple narrow bands, so they can be individually measured. For ease and flex-
ibility it is often desired to use a broad spectrum light source, accompanied with
some sort of optical band-pass filter. Alternatively, the spectral segmentation could
be achieved, by dividing the spectrum spatially over an angle, using a diffraction
grating as illustrated in Figure 3.2. Hyperspectral images can then be acquired by
either applying spectral segmentation at the light source directly, or at the sensor
input (see Figure 3.1) - the former being the method of choice in this project. Both
approaches have their advantages and disadvantages and have been developed in a
variety of ways using vastly different technologies.

This section gives a short introduction to some recently developed approaches,
their techniques, strengths and weaknesses.
Zhang et al. (2017) [51] use a stereo camera setup in combination with a GOBO
projector (GOBO = GOes Before Optics), which produces a sinusoidal pattern to en-
code the scene, by moving a stencil in front of the light source (i.e. the stencil GOes
Before the Optics). The spectral content is filtered using a rotating fixed filter wheel
in front of each camera. This allows for a relatively simple design, but with the lim-
itation that the number of spectral bands is restricted by the number of filters in the
rotating wheel.
A similar system has been developed by Sitnik (2012) [43]. To this system an
additional grid of light sources has been added to enable it to also determine BRDF
characteristics of the target.
Heist et al. (2018) [25] construct a hyperspectral 3D scanner using a high speed
GOBO projector and a pair of commercially available hyperspectral snapshot cam-
eras. The cameras provide 25 spectral channels in the VIS-NIR range with a reso-
lution of 409 × 216 px. The system can be operated in two spectral ranges: 600-875
nm and 675-975 nm where it can with a rate of 17 Hz output 25 3D point clouds,
corresponding to each of the spectral channels in the cameras. The relatively high
measurement rate makes the system well suitable for dynamic scenes. However, the
low resolution of the images, in comparison to conventional cameras, restricts spa-
tial resolution and scan volume heavily.

Total internal reflection prism
Total internal reflection prism (TIR prism) is a specific type of a prism that is often
used in DLP projectors to seperate the illumination from the projection path [15].
This can also be achieved by using mirrors, but prisms allow for a more compact
design and better efficiency [34].
A TIR prism is a combination of two prisms, made from optical materials with
different refractive indices as illustrated in Figure 4.3. At the border of two optical
materials, an incident ray is split into a refracted (transmitted) and a reflected compo-
nent, where the intensity of each component is dependant on the angle of incidence,
with the reflected component being largest at a tangential incidence angle and the
refracted component at a normal incidence angle. However, if the incidence angle
is larger then a certain threshold, called the critical angle (𝜃𝑐𝑟𝑖𝑡), all light is reflected
[50]. This situation is called total internal reflection and is illustrated in Figure 4.4.

A collimated light beam, is a beam where the rays are parallel and do therefore not

magnification that cannot be adjusted.

Beam homogenization
In order to get a uniformly distributed laser beam, some form of beam shaping must
occur, as laser beams usually have a Gaussian intensity distribution. Beam shaping is
the process of redistributing the irradiance and phase of a beam of optical radiation
[14]. Laser beam shaping techniques can be divided into three main categories.
Firstly, there is aperturing of the beam as illustrated in Figure 4.7. The input beam
is expanded and an aperture is used to select a part of the beam that is sufficiently flat.
The main drawback of this method is the considerable loss of energy. Furthermore,
the irradiance distribution of the input beam might not be even enough to yield
satisfying results.
Secondly, there is field mapping as illustrated in Figure 4.8. Field mappers can
have near 100% efficiency but do require a specifically designed lens to transform the
input field into a uniform field. Furthermore they are only applicable to well defined

4.7
Structured light
Structured light is used to add the information needed to solve the correspondence
problem that can not be fully solved using only epipolar geometry. This is done by
projecting a coded pattern onto the scene, usually with a projector. Depending on
the scene, target and system requirements, several different types of coded patterns
can be used. A simplified illustration of one such pattern can be seen in Figure 4.14,
where a colored rainbow pattern is used to encode the scene. With the encoded
scene, the epipolar lines in the second image can be searched for points that match
the encoded value of the original point in the first image. This illustrated pattern is a
’single shot’ pattern meaning that the 3D shape of the scene can be estimated using
this single static pattern. In cases where the scene is stationary, ’multi shot’ patterns
can be employed. These consists of a series of coded patterns that are sequentially
projected on to the scene, one at a time. This allows to measure the scene in greater
detail and accuracy, at the cost of longer measurement times. Examples of these are
’binary gray coding’ and ’phase shifting’.[22]

To filter the broad-spectrum laser output from the laser, into a specific narrow spec-

The SuperK Select is controlled via its connection through the laser, and can be
controlled using the same interface options (SDK and Control software). The filter
output is channeled through a SuperK Connect, which enables fine adjustment of
the beam alignment with the optical fiber, with attached collimator. The alignment
is performed manually using the four alignment screws which adjust a set of mirrors.
The collimated outputs have full width at half maximum (FWHM) bandwidth
between 3.5–14 nm and 6.4–19.8 nm for the two outputs, respectively.

5.3
Feasibility study
This section serves to study the illumination and subsequent capture of light to eval-
uate the necessary exposure times for the cameras. To argue feasibility we make the
assumptions that:
• The system enclosure is completely isolated from external illumination
• The target has a perfectly Lambertian surface
• The target is a flat surface perpendicular to the projector
• There is no power loss in the optical path from filter to projector output
The main limiting factor is the output power from the laser light source, in com-
bination with the QE of the cameras. Another limiting factor for the overall design,
is the size of the system enclosure, which is to be built on an optical breadboard

Power per wavelength estimate
As stated in Section 5.2, the output power of the SuperK Extreme is 4 W, that is the
total power for the entire spectrum of 400 - 2400 nm. Due to the spectral limitations
of the system elements, discussed in the same section, we will only consider the
spectral range 500 - 800 nm. By looking at Figure 5.5 on page 20, it is evident that
the spectral power density varies quite much in this region. The same can be said
for the cameras’ QE curves in Figure 5.11 on page 24. For simplicity, we will only
consider two wavelengths, representing a best and worst case combination of laser
spectral power density and camera QE. For the same reason, only the QE of the
grayscale camera will be considered - thereby avoiding to integrate over the three
color channel QE curves. The two wavelengths of choice are 550 nm, where both the
camera’s QE and the laser’s power density are high, and 800 nm, where there laser
power is at a local minimum and the camera is also at its minimum QE, within the
chosen range.
To estimate the power output at these wavelengths we will assume that the fil-
tered output will have a Gaussian spectral power distribution with a peak value cor-
responding to the curve in Figure 5.5. The total power is then given as the integral of
the Gaussian spectral distribution curve, for the given wavelength. This integral can
be roughly estimated by multiplying the FWHM with the peak value. The filtered
output has a FWHM of 3.5 - 14 nm in the range 500 - 900 nm (see Figure 5.2). How
the FWHM varies with wavelength is not described in the documentation and we
will therefore assume an average value of 8.75 nm.

Projector
This section covers the exploratory examination and modifications made to the sys-
tems projector in order to make it compatible with the laser system.
At the start of the project two different DLP projectors were available, a Wintech
PRO4500 and a Keynote Photonics LC4500, which both can be seen in Figure 5.7.
The electronics in both of these are based on the Lightcrafter 4500 design from Texas
Instruments[17], and therefore use essentially the same electronics, with only minor
adjustments for placement of the components. The optics, on the other hand, are
more different in their design.
The light path of both projector is shown in Figure 6.1. The light path of the blue
LED is the most appealing, as it has a more direct path towards the DMD. In this
project the main focus will be on the Wintech PRO4500 since it has seemingly one
advantage over the Keynote Photonics LC4500. The blue LED is placed at the back
of the projector, unlike Keynote Photonics LC4500, which has its blue LED placed at
the front.
The placement of a LED in the back-side of the projector is important since, if it
were to be removed it would allow for easy line of sight access trough the projector,
to the DMD without blocking the projector’s output lens. The Keynote Photonics
LC4500 would require additional modification.
6.1
Initial projector examination
This subsection covers the initial discoveries made when examining the Wintech
PRO4500 projector. The goal of the examination was to achieve a general overview
of the projector’s different constituents and study the optical assembly.
This examination was done in an order that maximized the knowledge gained,
while trying to minimize the risk of making irreparable damage to the projector.
The examination started by connecting its HDMI connector to a computer. It
started flashing seemingly random patterns, and not showing the HDMI output. The
control software was there after installed. This enabled to set the projector into
’Video mode’, after which it functioned via the HDMI output from the computer
as intended, albeit with a unfocused output at the test distance of 40cm. It was dis-
covered that the projection lens assembly could be turned to focus the output. The
thread is quite loose, which makes the focus adjustment sensitive and a focus lock
was therefore devised. After concluding that the software was functioning correctly,
the examination moved on to the hardware elements.
The projector consists of a mounting plate with a case on top, containing the
optics. On the top and side of this case are two PCBs, as can be seen in Figure 6.2.
Through testing and referencing the data sheets it was determined that the side-
mounted PCB handles the LED power management, while the top PCB handles the
remaining features, such as DMD control and USB interface.
After removing the PCBs, a top plate covering the optics casing became accessible
revealing the optics seen sketched in Figure 6.3. It consists of three different LEDs
(i,l,r); red, green and blue, which are mounted on the outside of the case. The red
and the green LED share a cooling plate (k), while the blue has its own that can be
separately removed. The DMD has its own separate cooling plate and a designated
fan. Following each LED comes a system of three lenses, to collimate its light (n,p,q).

The projector was then connected to a computer’s HDMI port to control its out-

output was no longer controllable over the HDMI, and it showed a completely blue
output, even though the HDMI output was set to black on half the screen.
To examine the course of the error the projector was turned off and assembled

suspicious, it was reassembled.
On next start up the output started to flicker. The projector was quickly turned
off, the flickering was identified to be caused by a damaged mounting bolt on the
flex cables connector on the PCB. After the screw was replaced, the situation seemed
to have worsened. When turned on, the projector did not turn on its LED’s and
connection could no longer be established over USB. From this it was concluded that
either the PCB or the DMD, possible both had sustained permanent damage from
this bad connection.
To isolate the damaged part, the DMD was removed from the Wintech projector

it was decided not to use the also possible faulty PCBs from the Wintech projector,
and instead combine the Wintech optics assembly with the working PCB originally
mounted in the Keynote projector. After consulting the schematics for both pro-
jectors, to ensure compatibility, this setup was tested using a temporary mounting
mock-up. To great relief the projector was once again fully operational.
To later fully complete this fusion, a custom mount was designed for permanent
mounting of the Keynote PCB onto the Wintech optics assembly. This is covered in
Section 6.4.

6.6
Structured light encoding patterns
By using the control software discussed in chapter 12, any grayscale pattern can be
projected for structured light scanning by loading images from a folder. In addition
to that, two types of patterns can be generated by the application; binary gray coding
and phase shifting.
The binary gray coding was implemented first due to its simplicity and prior
experience with using it. Later on in the project it was decided to implement phase
shifting primarily for one reason: each 3D point is generated from a single camera
pixel pair, making it easier to find corresponding 3D points between spectral 3D
scans. This is discussed in further detail in Figure 11.1.
Binary gray coding
An illustration of binary gray encoding can be seen in Figure 6.13, where each depth
layer represents a pattern to be projected onto an object that should be 3D scanned.
As the depth increases the pattern gets finer. With each layer the pattern width
gets halved and shifted half its width, so that the border between white and black
is always in the middle of next depth layer’s pattern. For a complete 3D scan the
inverse patterns are also required along with a completely white and black output.
The pattern can be generated for a screen of an arbitrary size, though only to a
depth where the pattern width is an even number - to avoid rounding errors. In the
case of a DLP4500 based projector which has a resolution of 912×1140 px, it is, for
instance, possible to generate a horizontal pattern with a depth = 6 and a minimum
width of 14 pixels as 896 = 26 ⋅14. By going any deeper the next layer would have a
width = 7 and shifted 3.5 pixels, therefore giving a rounding error.
If a finer pattern is required it is also possible to dismiss a larger portion of the
DMD. A 768 px wide pattern yields 6 px wide stripes while a 512 px wide pattern
yields 2 px wide stripes. The finer patterns result in a higher resolution pointcloud
but they do also require more images to be taken. Eventually the possible focus of the
projector and cameras will also limit how thin that can be distinguished. In the end
it is therefore a trade-off between speed, scan area, focus and pointcloud resolution.
An example of the generated pattern, using the constructed projection system,
can be seen in Figure 6.14.
Phase shifting
The phase shifting pattern sequence consists of two sets of sinusoidal patterns; one
primary pattern, and a second cue pattern, which is used for unwrapping the absolute
phase of pixels, as discussed in Figure 11.1. Each of these patterns are then shifted
in phase over 1 period in a number of steps. To generate the pattern sequence, three
parameters are required:
• Periods: Number of sinusoidal periods to repeat over the width of a pattern.
• Primary steps: Number of steps used for shifting the phase 1 period.
• Cue steps: Number of steps used for shifting the phase 1 period.

Optical adapter
This section describes the design process for the optical path needed to adapt the
laser beam output, from the optical fiber connected to the NKT Select filter, to the
projector’s DMD. The output beam poses two challenges: firstly, the beam has a much
smaller diameter than that of the DMD; and secondly, the beam has an Gaussian
intensity distribution [45].
The first challenge requires the beam to be expanded so that it can cover the
entire DMD and the second requires some form of homogenization - in order to get
uniform intensity from the projector’s output.
7.1
Choice of methods
The considered methods for both beam expansion and homogenization are discussed
in Section 4.2, including the advantages and disadvantages of each approach. Due
to limited resources and the experimental nature of this project, it has been tried to
keep costs as low as possible while maintaining flexibility.
Beam expansion
For beam expansion it has been chosen to use a manually con-
structed beam expander using optical lenses. The main reason for this choice is the
flexibility this approach has, making it possible to drastically change the magnifi-
cation by replacing a single lens, but also the possibility to select lenses with the
desired characteristics. Furthermore, this is a much more cost-effective approach
as compared to using mirror-based magnification or a commercial lens-based beam
expander.
Homogenization
As discussed in Section 6.3, we wanted to assess if the projec-
tor’s existing homogenization optics would be sufficient for our use. As a fallback,
we chose the simplest method, homogenization by aperture, as this only requires an
extra set of lenses with larger magnification - thereby combining the beam expansion
and homogenization with only two optical elements.

wavelength, where the diameter is given in terms of a 1/𝑒2 width at three wave-

Camera
This section elaborates on the work needed to enable the system’s cameras to perform
reliable and precise measurements.
8.1
Camera selection
Initially in the project, only color cameras where available, which introduced some
restrictions and challenges. The used color camera filters colors by the use of a Bayer
filter placed in front of the camera sensor. This is a filter consisting of three different
kinds of filter elements, each transmitting either red, green or blue, while blocking
other colors. This is illustrated in Figure 8.1, where white light is filtered trough
a Bayer filter. This results in each pixel only receiving either red, green or blue
light information, while lacking any information about the other two. This missing
information is artificially made, trough an interpolation scheme to form a complete
image for all the three color channels.
Figure 8.1: Bayer RGB color filtering. In front of each pixel is a colored filter which
only passes a part of the visible spectrum.
By splitting the light spectrum into three, partly overlapping sub-spectra, a layer
of unnecessary complexity is introduced, as each sub-spectra has to be individually
calibrated, to achieve reliable spectroscopy measurements. As only a set of color
cameras were available at the start of the project, this individual calibration was ini-
tially attempted. The results of this can be seen in Appendix D.10. Well into the
project, a set of monochrome cameras became available, allowing us to circumvent
this problem. These therefore replaced the color cameras in the setup. This change
also allowed the system to scan at higher wavelengths, due to a wider quantum effi-
ciency graph, shown previously in Figure 5.11.
As the color cameras were used trough roughly the first half of the project, some
of the results and figures presented will be for color cameras.

Adjustable aperture
During testing it was discovered that the cameras initially had a rather poor depth of
field. As a result it proved difficult to acquire sufficiently sharp images for calibration
and measurements. To mitigate this problem the aperture on the camera lenses was
decreased by adjusting the f-stop value from the minimum setting of f/1.8 to a value
of f/2.8. This will improve the depth of field [27], but will also decrease the amount of
light entering the camera, resulting in a need for longer exposure times. This trade-
off between depth of field and image intensity is illustrated in Figure 8.6. The selected
aperture setting was chosen as working compromise and due to the lens having an
indicator mark at this point, thereby making it easier to adjust both lenses to the
same value.

Variable exposure profile: grayscale cameras
Measurements can be taken over a much wider range with the grayscale cameras,
as compared to RGB, due to their wide quantum efficiency curve seen in Figure 5.11.
However, the curve has a descending efficiency towards the near infrared end of the
spectrum, requiring increasing exposure time in order to get meaningful measure-
ments. Due to this the intensity correction profile must be calculated in intervals
over the spectrum with increasing exposure time.
Through empirical testing the spectrum has been split into 5 intervals that can
give meaningful measurements with a constant exposure time:
• 500-700 nm
• 700-735 nm
• 735-770 nm
• 770-800 nm
• 800-900 nm (maximum exposure time)
In this order the correction profile for each interval is calculated using an exposure
time corresponding to the correction calculated for the last wavelength in the previ-
ous interval (the first wavelength in the current interval) except for the last interval
where it exceeded the maximum exposure time of the camera (30s). Each profile is
then normalized to its first value and scaled with the last correction factor in the
previous interval. The combined profiles can be seen in Figure 8.11.
Validity of grayscale correction profile

wavelength dependant abnormalities in the intensity homogeneity that occur above
∼735 nm. This was first noticed after taking images with variable exposure on the
grayscale cameras. This is illustrated in Figure 8.12. This does effectively restrict the
upper range of the system to ∼735 nm. The reason for these abnormalities has not
been found, but for a more detailed analysis see Appendix F.

Laser
This section describes the work done in order to effectively use the laser sub-system,
consisting of the SuperK Extreme laser (Section 5.2), the SuperK Select filter (Fig-
ure 5.2), and an optical fiber. The most crucial parts have been safety concerns, solv-
ing how to properly calibrate the filter, and designing a rugged mount for the optical
fiber probe, to make sure that the laser beam’s alignment with the beam expander
would be stable.
9.1
Probe mount
After initial testing of the beam expander setup (Section 7.5), it was determined that
the existing probe mount was not sufficiently stable. The mount consists of a simple
tube in which the probe is inserted and tightened with a screw on the side. This only
gives the probe one point of contact and therefore it can pivot slightly around this
point. A second mount was therefore designed to help distribute the load over mul-
tiple points. This new mount is designed to fit the existing cage system. It features
screw holes to fasten it to the four cage rods to lock its position, and four screws to
fix the probe in the center hole. The design can be seen in figure 9.1.
The mount is 3D printed in PLA material, and it was first designed so that the
thread needed in the set screw holes where carved in the plastic the first time the
screw was inserted. This is sometimes strong enough, but in this case it proved not
to be, as the plastic thread failed when the screws where tightened. The mount was
therefore redesigned to feature insert holes for metal nuts. These insert holes can be
seen in figure 9.1 marked in green.
The laser probe can be seen mounted in Figure 9.2.

Filter re-calibration
As discussed in Section 9.2, the laser filter could not be calibrated successfully in
conjunction with the fiber delivery system. After acquiring NKT’s alignment tool,
specifically designed for this purpose, another calibration was performed with the
assistance of a specialist from NKT. The procedure is the same as in Section 9.2, but
the alignment tool is made with much higher precision than the 3D printed apertures,
leading to more precise calibration and a successful alignment with the fiber delivery
system.
It was observed that there was considerable power loss introduced by using the
fiber delivery system as opposed to free-space operation. This can partially be ex-
plained by the efficiency of the fiber which is approximately 70-85% within the op-
erating range according to measurements from NKT for the specific unit at our dis-
posal, as can be seen in Figure 9.5. A part of the observed power-loss might also
be due to the small aperture of the fiber coupling but in the free-space setting a 3D
printed aperture with a diameter of 2mm was used - which is far from optimal and
does allow for a great amount of stray light of other wavelengths, to come through.
Power/Intensity calibration
The significant power loss observed in Section 9.3, underlines the importance of cal-
ibrating the output, to ensure maximal power output. The previous calibrations had
only been made by visual observation. This was sufficient with the relatively large
aperture (2mm) used in the free-space setting, but proved to be much more chal-
lenging when using the fiber. Therefore, the output of the fiber delivery system was
measured with a spectrometer, to make it easier to fine-tune the filter to its inten-
sity maxima. This resulted in more then double the peak intensity as compared to
calibration by visual observation.

Safety concerns
As the SuperK Extreme is a Class 4 laser, it must be handled with the utmost care.
Class 4 laser products pose fire hazards, are capable of causing injury to both the eye
and skin from direct exposure, and reflections may also be hazardous [30]. Safety
glasses have therefore been employed during the development stage. Additional
safety measures were taken when constructing an enclosure for the system, where a
large lamp indicates when the laser is emitting light, and an interlock is used to turn
off the laser emission when the enclosure is open. This is described in more detail in
Section 10.1.

11.1
3D reconstruction
This section describes the process of reconstructing a 3D model using structured light
image sequences acquired by the system, and the measured stereo camera calibration
parameters.
The overall process includes the following steps, which will be further elaborated
in the following subsections:
1. Correcting for distortion artifacts in the images
2. Image rectification
3. Image encoding
4. Matching point correspondences
5. Point triangulation from the found correspondences
As described in Section 6.6 binary gray coding was initially used for image en-
coding but was later replaced with phase shifting. This section describe the use of
both methods.
Another change is the way the undistortion and rectification of the acquired im-
ages have been implemented. Initially this was done in Matlab in two separate step,
but due to some difficulties this was later done using OpenCV, where it is performed
in a combined method. This will be further elaborated in Figure 11.1.
Radial distortion compensation
When doing 3D reconstruction from a stereo setup, radial image distortion presents
itself as a 3D estimation error term, that increases on 3D points stemming from pixel
coordinates towards the edges of the images. This is clearly visible when doing a
reconstruction of a flat surface. This effect can be seen in Figure 11.1.
As a part of the stereo camera calibration, the radial distortion is also estimated.
Radial distortion is usually described with 1 - 3 coefficients, which map how much
a given pixel in an image should be shifted based on its distance from the center of
the image. This can be expressed as: [1]

Rectifying images
When searching for the point correspondences in the image pairs (described in Fig-
ure 11.1), it is a clear advantage if the images are warped in a way so the same vertical
indices can be used to search in both image sets. This process, know as image recti-
fication, uses epipolar geometry to calculate and warp images.
Matlab implmentation
Initially it was attempted to implement the rectification in Matlab using the function
imwarp and later using rectifyStereoImages. Both methods use homographys to rec-
tify the given images. These homographys have been calculated based on the method
described in [2](p. 181)
After evaluating this rectification technique it was discovered that the used func-
tion introduced a varying offset in the y-direction between the two images. This
evaluation and several attempts to fix the problem is covered in Appendix G. As no
satisfying solution was found to this problem, it was decided to implement the image
rectification in OpenCV instead, where a suitable solution was found.
OpenCV implementation
In OpenCV a slightly different approach is used for image rectification. The recti-
fication and distortion are combined using transformation maps that are calculated
during camera calibration, as discussed in Section 8.3. These transformation maps
are then simply applied to the images that should be rectified, using the remap func-
tion.
Image encoding
Based on the multiple, now rectified, images a single pair of encoded images must
be formed. These two resulting encoded images will later be used in the search for
point correspondences, between the two cameras.
The process of forming these encoded images varies greatly depending if binary gray
coding or phase shifting is used.
Binary gray encoding
In this implementation a pattern depth of eight increasingly finer patterns have been
used (Shown in Figure 6.13 and Figure 6.14). This process starts with subtracting the
negative image from the positive in each image pair. This step will ensure grater
tolerance to illumination variance in the scene. The result of this subtraction is then
blurred slightly using a Gaussian blur to reduce image noise, and is here after thresh-
olded around zero, to form eight binary images.
This results in two times eight binary images, of values 0 and 1, with increasingly
thinner lines. These then needs to be coded down to one image per camera. This is
done by multiplying the eight images with an increasing weight of 2𝑞, starting at q
= 0. This will give the first image weight ’1’, the second weight ’2’ and third weight
’4’ and so on. All these eighth weighted images is then summed to form the final
encoded image.
This can be seen illustrated in Figure 11.3, which shows that values of a single
row of pixels for each pattern, and their summed value. For illustration purposes
only four of the eight patterns are used in the illustration.

Phase shift encoding
In this implementation 16 primary and 8 cue images, of 16 and 17 sinusoidal peri-
ods accordingly (Illustrated in Figure 6.15), are acquired from each camera. From
each camera these stacks are compiled into a single image for each stack using a
Fourier transform, creating two wrapped phase images. These phase images from
the primary and cue stack are then combined into a single unwrapped phase image
using the heterodyne principle [41], which calculates the difference between two sin
waves. This process is illustrated in Figure 11.4. This yields a continuous image with
a coded value for each pixel in the generated phase image, wheres is the binary gray
coding method only gives information along the line transitions. The advantage of
this one-to-one correspondence between 2D images points and 3D world is further
discussed in Section 11.2.
Finding point correspondences
Now that the images have been rectified and later encoded, it is merely a matter of
searching each row in the encoded images and noting each time a value is found for
the first time in that row in both images. This results in a list of paired x,y coordinates
from both images, which then can be further pressed into 3D points. For added
precision, sub pixel correspondences are found in both methods. This is achieved by
first finding the closest pixel correspondence. Here after the nearest neighbors in the
x direction is inspected, based on which the sub pixel x values is estimated trough
interpolation.

Point triangulation
The list of 2D pixel correspondences between the two encoded images is then con-
verted into a list of 3D points, using triangulation. This can be seen as calculating
the crossing of two lines in 3D, each originating in the principal point of on of the
cameras going trough the image plane in the found pixel location. This is illustrated
in Figure 11.5. In an ideal case this could be perceived as a problem with a linear
solution, but due to the cameras quantization of the world onto a regular image grid,
the two lines will likely not cross exactly. This must therefore instead be solved as
a minimization of a least squares problem. This will find the 3D point at which the
two lines are closest to intersection.

Conclusion
During the course of this project a hyperspectral 3D scanning system has been suc-
cessfully constructed, by modifying a commercially available DLP projector. The
projector’s RGB LED light source has been removed and replaced with a tunable
supercontinuum laser source, allowing for light projection of narrow spectral band
with a FWHM of 3.5-14 nm. To accomplish this, the projector’s RGB specific optical
elements have been removed, and an optical adapter, consisting of a Galilean beam
expander, has been integrated, to interface the laser system with the projector.
This hyperspectral projector is used in conjunction with a stereo camera setup,
utilizing structured light to scan the spatial and spectral characteristics of a given ob-
ject. The structured light patterns ’binary gray coding’ and ’phase shifting’ have been
used to encode the scanning targets, making it possible to do a 3D reconstruction of
the targets.
To achieve an even homogenization of the modified projector’s output, a software
solution has been developed, that based on sample images acquired with the system,
iteratively adjusts the output to an uniform intensity distribution, with a standard
deviation of less than 4%.
The laser output power and the camera sensitivity vary across the spectral range,
variable exposure time compensation curves have been measured and applied to en-
sure evenly exposed images across the system’s wavelength range. However, as the
power of the laser is relatively week in comparison to projectors ordinarily used in
structured light systems, rather long exposure times are needed for acquiring images,
ranging from 0.85s 3.7s per single image.
The system’s spectroscopy and 3D reconstruction performance has been eval-
uated by scanning various calibration objects. These tests have shown promising
results; the system can on calibration objects, scan with a sub-millimeter spatial ac-
curacy and with a range of 504 - 735 nm, with 1 nm spectral resolution. The lower
end of the spectral range is limited by the laser’s filter; but the higher end, however,
is limited by intensity abnormalities that were observed above 735 nm.
The developed system have briefly been tested in a variety of different use-cases,
scanning organic as well as inorganic materials, including skin, orchids and plastics.
In these test it has been confirmed that the system can measure a wavelength depen-
dant difference in penetration depth in for example translucent materials caused by
subsurface scattering.
As the system uses a Class 4 laser, special care have been taken to build an en-
closure with inbuilt safety mechanisms, so the laser can only be operated while the
enclosure is closed. Additionally, the enclosure is equipped with lamp indicators,
signaling to surrounding personnel when the laser is active and when the system is
performing measurements.
For ease of use, a dedicated GUI has been developed for the system. This enables
the operator to specify a wide selection of scanning settings to suit specific measure-
ment purposes. In order to achieve high quality measurements, a series of calibration
algorithms are also accessible in the GUI, simplifying the calibration of the camera
and projector setup.

Future work
The projects main focus has been to develop a system that can acquire 3D hyperspec-
tral data. However, there has not been done much work on specific applications, and
where this has been done (chapter 14), it has only been a proof of concept. For fur-
ther work within these applications some limitations of the current system might
have to be addressed, depending on the desired application.
Long exposure times.
As discussed in Section 8.6 the system is much slower then
initially estimated. Although this has not been an issue for the measurements done
in this project, this can be a problem, for instance, if a dynamic scene were to by
scanned. If a faster system is needed, it can essentially be sped up in two ways:
• Increase the projector’s optical power throughput by minimizing loss or using
a more powerful light source.
• Use a more sensitive camera sensor.
Limited spectral range
The spectral resolution of the system is limited some-
where along the light path from the laser probe to the output of the projector. It can
possibly be expanded by replacing optical elements inside the projector that have
unknown specifications. If this limitation is overcome, the spectral resolution might
be further expanded by the use of a camera with a wider spectral response curve.
Restrictive view angels
In the equipment’s current configuration, the scanning
is limited to only viewing the target from one orientation. To address this, a rotation
stage for the target could be added, allowing to stitch several scans together with
different orientations to form a more complete pointcloud. Furthermore, this would
open up a possibility to compensate for specular highlights, as described above.
Modular design
The data acquisition can only be done through the control soft-
ware running on a Windows computer that is connected to the system via USB and
HDMI. To make the system more flexible a computer could be embedded in the sys-
tem that would handle direct control of all hardware and decouple the data acquisi-
tion from the rest of the system’s software. This computer could then be controlled
through a simple SDK and/or a network API. This new layer of abstraction would
make it simple to integrate the system into future projects and could also make it
platform-independent.
Data fusion
When a spectral 3D scan is performed, a point cloud is generated
for each wavelength. For scans with high spectral resolution this results in a large
amount of data. This could be merged into a single pointcloud with spectral infor-
mation for each point using the fact that the there is a direct correlation between a
camera pixel and a generated 3D point, when using phase shifting for 3D scans. That
is, each camera pixel can only result in a single 3D point for each wavelength. How-
ever, for translucent materials, the 3D position changes, depending on wavelength,
so in order to merge the pointclouds in a meaningful way, some algorithm must be
implemented that can estimate the true surface position. This could for example be
done by extrapolating if the scanned material is known beforehand.

Project Management Tools
To help ease the project management, a range of tools have been selected for various
tasks. Common for many of the tools is that they allow for real-time collaboration
between multiple people. Since both project members will frequently have to work
on the same part of the project, the real-time collaboration was deemed a highly

A small presentation of the different tools, their uses and the reason for choosing
them will here be presented.
B.1
Axosoft - Task/Time management
In order to keep good track of work progress, the task management tool Axosoft has
been chosen for this project. Axosoft, which is based on the Scrum framework, has
a large set of functionalities which allows for easier overview of project progress,
including a projected finish date, a burndown chart and comparison of estimated
versus actual time spent on development.
One of the main reasons for using Axosoft is the possibility to define a task workflow.
The workflow for each task is divided into five stages:
• New: Task has been defined but no work is being done.
• In progress: Work is being done.
• Ready for testing: Work finished, assigned to peer for testing.
• Completed: Testing successful.
• Rejected: Testing unsuccessful. Re-assigned to developer for further work.
Using a workflow like this helps ensuring a certain level of quality control for the
project as each task is always assigned to a peer for testing before it can be marked
as complete. For this to work effectively, it is essential to have a clear specification
for each task, along with a list of test scenarios to evaluate - when applicable.
B.2
Overleaf - Documentation
To ensure real-time collaboration on the documentation, the online LATEXeditor Over-

What equipment is used for the test, and how is the result measured.

Eliminating exposure time as the source of the effect
As the shadow effect, described in Figure 8.5, was only experienced at wavelengths
that require a rather long exposure time to be measured (approx. 4-30s), a couple
of experiments have been conducted to eliminate the exposure time as the source of
the shadow. Two scenarios have been examined:
1. At a constant wavelength, use the same exposure times as used over a wave-
length range where the shadow effect is evident (in variable exposure mode).
2. Use constant exposure time over a wavelength range where the shadow effect
is evident.
For each of the tests the wavelength range 735-770nm is used for reference as this
is where the shadow effect is first observed and where the shortest exposure times
must be used - thereby shortening the total time it takes to perform each test. For
the constant exposure scenario the exposure time equivalent to the variable exposure
value at 758.5nm has been chosen (8.2s) as it lies in the center of the 735-770nm range,
minimizing the amount of under- and overexposure. For the constant wavelength
scenario 758nm has been chosen for comparison, as well as 630nm as it lies within
the visible range of the light spectrum. All the measurements are compared with
