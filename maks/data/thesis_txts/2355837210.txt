Abstract
In a context of rising renewable production, harder to dispatch and whose output is
difficult to forecast, the problem of committing traditional thermal units has become
more difficult for system operators. A branch of optimization, stochastic programming,
can be used to mitigate the uncertainty brought by increasing renewable production. One
of the way to model such uncertainties is to create scenarios, and devise a stochastic
model whose aim is to minimize the average objective function over all scenarios.
However, a large number of renewable facilities implies a more complex uncertainty,
requiring more scenarios to model correctly. This creates a challenge in terms of time
necessary to solve the corresponding stochastic model. As a result, efforts have been
made in scenario reduction, defined as selecting a limited number of scenarios out of a
large initial set while preserving the relevant information.
In this thesis, scenario reduction techniques based on probability metrics, well-known,
are applied in the context of stochastic unit commitment.
To do so, a stochastic
optimization model for unit commitment has been formulated, implemented in Python,
and applied to a test case inspired from the IEEE 1996 Reliability Test System. An
initial scenario set has been reduced with reduction algorithms dubbed the simultaneous
backward reduction and fast forward selection. Then, the decision obtained with the
reduced set of scenario is compared to the optimal decision to evaluate the optimality gap
subsequent to the loss of information caused by reduction. A computation time study
is also performed. Finally, the choice of distance between scenario is also considered.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Context
Power system daily operation relies on two main stages and their associated markets: the
day-ahead dispatch schedule, and the re-balancing operations. Day-ahead dispatching
consists of assigning a scheduled production setting to each production unit in the system
the day before, and re-balancing occurs during the day to clear discrepancies between
scheduled and actual operation. Traditional power systems feature generation units that
are both reliable and easy to dispatch, and a well-known demand. These characteristics
make scheduling units a fairly straightforward optimization problem, thoroughly studied
and put into application by system operators all around the world. Grid robustness
to failures or unexpected events is ensured by production reserve requirements and
contingency analysis.
However, the apparition of renewable energies, and their increasing penetration,
challenge the characteristics of traditional power systems. For large renewable production
unit, output uncertainty and difficulty to dispatch makes the day-ahead scheduling task
more demanding. For small-scale renewable production, located in the distribution grid,
their impact on the system is effectively to make the demand more volatile. Increasing
reserve requirements is one way to deal with this new reality, but the higher the share
of renewable production is, the less practical this approach becomes. As an example,
Denmark already gets more than 30% of its electricity from wind power, and it has
already seen days where the whole demand is supplied by wind power [1].
This uncertainty can be dealt with by a particular branch of optimization: stochastic
optimization. A wide variety of techniques have been studied to model uncertainty in
power system economic dispatch [2]. Among them is the use of scenarios: considering a
stochastic process - e.g. wind power output - one can generate an array of scenarios, each
being a realization of this stochastic process. The optimization problem now consists
of finding the decision which best suits the most scenarios, typically by optimizing
the average objective function. Although effective, this technique may requires a very
high number of scenarios if the stochastic process is complex enough, rendering the
optimization unpractical to compute on a reasonable time frame This springs the need
for scenario reduction methods: considering an set of scenarios, is it possible to retain a
smaller set of most relevant scenarios that will still yield a quality decision.

1.2
Contribution and outline
In this thesis, it is proposed to evaluate the gains of scenario reduction in a practical,
standard unit commitment test case. To do so, a stochastic optimization model for a
typical unit commitment problem was formulated and implemented in Python. A test
case based on the IEEE 1996 Reliability test system [19] and a set of scenario based
on Western Denmark historical data was used as a practical case. Scenario reduction
algorithms based on methods inspired from probability metrics have been implemented
in Python as well, and applied in various configuration in this standard test case. The
efficiency of these algorithms from a theoretical and practical standpoint have been
evaluated, and their gains in computation speed measured. Additionally, a sensitivity
analysis of these algorithms to the size of the uncertainty vector and the total renewable
capacity has also been conducted.
In chapter 2, the approaches to unit commitment as an optimization problem will be
presented, and the model used for this thesis will be formulated. Chapter 3 will focus on
the state of the art for scenario generation and reduction, as well as present the test case
used to evaluate scenario reduction method. Finally, chapter 4 will present in details
scenario reduction algorithms that have proven consequential, and show how they fare
in the standard test case.

CHAPTER 2
Unit Commitment and
Optimization
Unit commitment is a problem born from the technical constraint associated with
power generation and markets. It is well known that electrical power cannot be stored
efficiently on a large scale, and as such, demand has to be met by supply in real time.
Power generating units, however, have technical constraints such as minimum uptime or
downtime, scheduled maintenance. Those constraints imply that the decision to commit
them to produce has to be made ahead of time and carefully. This chapter will first
introduce the historical approaches to the unit commitment problem,
2.1
Historical approaches to Unit Commitment
The unit commitment problem is often coupled with another decision-making problem:
economic dispatch of power plants. Economic dispatch consists on deciding how each
committed plant will produce to satisfy the demand, given technical constraints of the
plants themselves, transmission constraints, and demand characteristics. Similarly to
the unit commitment problem, economic dispatch is also a scheduling problem. For the
rest of the thesis, the term “unit commitment” (UC) will stand for the coupled general
problem of committing units and dispatching them.
2.1.1
Linear programming applied to Unit Commitment
Power system operators have as objective to meet the demand as well as planning for
contingencies, at the best possible cost; as such, unit commitment is of high interest
to them. Logically, the unit commitment problem has been amply studied. It can be
formulated as a constrained optimization problem [3]–[6], that is proven to be NP-hard
[2]. Linear Programming (LP) and Mixed-Integer-Linear Programming (MILP) have
been used to find optimal solution to unit commitment problems [3]–[7].
MILP is
especially adapted for unit commitment, as it allows to represent discrete states of
thermal units. Commitment of units is represented as binary variables for each plant at
each time period. Dispatch is represented as continuous variables, adequately representing
power outputs of units, and easier to handle than integer variables. Pure LP programming
has efficient algorithms to find optimal solution, such as the well-know simplex algorithm
[8]. MILP is generally much harder to solve, but plenty of progress has been made

and a number of algorithms have been developed [9], [10] and used to good effect on
unit commitment problems.
As examples, branch-and-bound [9] has been employed
on a large-scale unit commitment problems [5], Lagrangian relaxation and Benders
decomposition have also been applied [11], [12]. More evolved model can have multiple
binary or integer variable, in order to model complex and realistic plant states and
cost functions [13], but those additional variables add to the computational difficulties:
typically, in a branch-and-bound algorithm, more discrete variables widen the search
tree used by the algorithm, increasing computation time.
2.1.2
Stochastic Unit Commitment
Unit commitment without any uncertainty on production or demand characteristic is
called deterministic unit commitment. It is best suited for systems where the demand
is well know and can be forecast with good confidence, and units are reliable enough.
For contingencies, system operators include reserve requirements, i.e., units ready to
adjust their production from to what they are scheduled on short notice. However, the
ever-increasing renewable energy penetration challenges those assumptions [2]. Indeed,
on the production side, large renewable units cannot predict their output with the
same degree of confidence than traditional thermal units. On the demand side, small
renewable energy-producing facilities - e.g.
roof-mounted solar panels - are usually
connected on the low-voltage distribution grid, and as such, effectively lower the demand
and make it more volatile.
System operators have typically been increasing reserve
requirements to accommodate for those uncertainties [14].
Another approach is to
include uncertainties into the unit commitment decision-making process, by applying
stochastic optimization to unit commitment.
Stochastic optimization is a branch of
optimization aiming at determining optimal decision under uncertainties [15], used
effectively on many fields [16]. Instead of relying on a single value, assumed perfect,
for inherently uncertain variables, it includes the uncertainty itself into the model in
order to come at an adequate solution for the highest share possible of realization of
those uncertain variables. Significant research has gone into modeling uncertainty of
power system to effectively take into account renewable energies into unit commitment
decisions, and three main approaches have been considered: scenarios; uncertainty sets,
and probability constraints [2].
With these approaches, the model objective is the
expected objective function over all stochastic process realizations. Other approaches
than expected objective function - e.g. robust unit commitment, aiming to optimize
worst case scenario [2], [17] - also have been developed , although they will not be
considered in this thesis.
One of the way to represent uncertainty is to generate a set of scenarios, each of
which representing a particular realization of the vector of uncertain variables [2], [18].
These scenarios can be bundled into a tree structure, where variables at early periods
can be shared between subset of scenarios [12], [18]. If the system operator aims at
minimizing its expected objective e.g. costs or emissions, the model objective function

is simply the weighted sum of costs over all scenarios. As a result, LP and MILP are
particularly suitable for those approaches. In such a case, the MILP model will be a two-
(or multi-) stage model, i.e., variables and constraints will be separated into separate
stage. The first stage will represent decisions and constraints relevant ahead of time,
when uncertainty is maximum, and the subsequent stages will represent decisions and
constraints relevant when uncertainty is starting to unravel.
For UC problems, the
first stage will naturally represent a day-ahead commitment, and subsequent stages will
represent decisions made during the day to re-balance the grid as forecasts become more
precise and parts of the scenario set can be ruled out. As a result, the optimal first-stage
variables will take into account the future decisions to take during re-balancing, in order
to come up with the best possible decision.

2.3
Stochastic programming
Stochastic programming can be introduced based on this formulation. A typical stochastic
programming modeling approach is the two-stage stochastic programming problem, which
can be generalized to multiple stages. In this formulation, uncertainty is modeled as a
set of scenarios. Each scenario is associated with a probability of realization, and each
imply different constraints for the model when they do realize. The model is still solved
in a single optimization instance, but it is possible to separate variables and constraints
into two categories, or stages.
The first stage consists of variables and constraints that are not scenario-dependent.
Intuitively, this represent the point where decisions have to be made ahead of time,
when scenario realization is still unknown. The optimized value of these variables are
ultimately the most important output of the model.
The second stage consists of variables and constraints that are scenario-dependent.
Again, intuitively, these reflects the point in time where scenario realization is known,
and decisions have to be made in order to adapt for this particular scenario.
The objective function is then a sum of first-stage variable terms and second-stage
variable terms. Typically, this is expressed as the sum of two expressions: a first stage
objective function, and the expected value of a second-stage objective function.

2.4
Unit commitment problem formulation
2.4.1
Model presentation
Two stochastic optimization models have been developed in Python for this thesis.
Inspired from the deterministic unit commitment presented in [7], they are intended
to represent the decision-making process of a power system operator dispatching units
for the following day. The first model, named Single-Bus Stochastic Unit Commitment
(SBSUC), represents a system with only a single node: all thermal units, renewable
energy plants, and loads are connected to a single bus, without any consideration of
transmission or power flow.
This simplified model has been only used as a step to
the second model, named Network-Constrained Stochastic Unit Commitment (NCSUC),
which represents a system with an actual transmission grid, with buses, transmission
lines, and production/consumption defined at given buses. These models are a decent
representation of a UC problem seen by a system operator, yet are simple enough to
be run in a reasonable timeframe ( 1h) on a personal computer with around 100 scenarios.
Both models are defined as stochastic optimization models. As a result, the model
variables are separated into first-stage and second-stage variables. The second stage
variables are specific to each scenario; they model the system, configured initially with
the first-stage variables, adapting to each scenario.
The first stage variables consists of:
• Thermal unit commitment decision - is the plant online or not at this period
• Thermal unit operation setpoint - scheduled power output for this period.
The second stage variables consists of:
• Reserves of thermal plants at each period - the difference between the scheduled
setpoint and the actual power output of the plant.
• Load shedding variables - amount of load discarded. Those variables are here to
garantee feasibility of the model: if the system cannot cope with the load, some
load will be shed, albeit at a high cost.
• Renewable curtailing variables - amount of renewable generation discarded. Similarly
to load shedding, those variables are here to garantee feasibility if the system has
too much production at a given time. There is no penalty to curtail renewable
production except from the obvious downside of wasting energy.

• Power angle variables, specific to NCSUC - power angle at each bus, to model power
flow physical constraints. Power transfered through transmission lines depend on
power angles.
The constraints are:
• Minimum uptime and downtime of thermal plants
• Ramp-up and ramp-down limits of thermal plants
• Minimum and maximum power limits for a committed thermal plant
• Power balance on the whole model, specific to SBSUC - the sum of all energy
produced must equate the sum of all energy consumed.
• Line capacity limits, specific to NCSUC - the flow of energy through each line is
dictated by the power angles at both extremities and the susceptance of the line,
to model power flow constraints.
• Power balance at each bus, specific to NCSUC - this constraints states that the
sum of all power inbound and outbound of a bus must be null. A bus can receive
power from connected thermal units, connected renewable plants, or other buses
via transmission lines. It can give power to the connected loads and to other buses
via transmission lines.
The objective model in both models is the expected value of the total costs over the
whole period, i.e. , the sum of costs over all scenarios weighted by the probability of
each scenario. Costs include thermal plant starting costs, fuel costs, and loss of load
costs.

3.1
Context
The number of scenario is, intuitively, a determining factor on how well uncertainty is
represented. The more complex the uncertain process is, the more scenarios it requires
to come to a decent representation and thus a decent solution. With more and more
renewable units being connected to the grid, the number of location increases, each of
them having slightly different weather and production forecast, which in turn augments
the complexity of the stochastic process expected to model it. If the system is small
enough and transmission constraints are not very tight, this can be reduced to a simple
aggregated forecast; or if renewable production are concentrated geographically, the
variable production might be sufficiently correlated to be reduced to a single variable
common to all units. On large systems over a wide area, variable production at different
locations, with meteorological forecast more loosely correlated, a larger set of variable
might be needed, which in turns increases the number of scenarios required. However,
a large scenario set implies a higher complexity in the model, leading to a longer
computational time to come up with the optimal solution. This prompts the need for
scenario reduction techniques: how to capture most of the relevant information contained
in a large set of scenario into a smaller, more manageable set that the SUC model will
be able to compute a solution for. Intuitively, that corresponds to finding and keeping
the most relevant scenarios to the decision-making process.
3.2
Scenario generation
Before going into scenario reduction, the process of scenario generation is relevant to
be discussed. Creating a scenario set is often done with Monte-Carlo simulations of
probability functions, themselves infered from historical data. For multistage stochastic
programs, scenario trees can be created with random path from an underlying stochastic
process. Other research has focused on creating scenarios from forecasting, respecting
dependencies in space and time for wind power generation [22], [23].
A review of
scenario-generating process can be found in [2].
Although being an active research field, the scenario-generation process is outside
the scope of this work. As a result, the initial scenarios and their number is a given, and

3.3
Approaches to scenario reduction
Various methods for scenario reduction have been studied. Simple clustering algorithms
such as k-means and derivatives [27] has been applied to scenario reduction [28]. A
consequential work has been proposed using probability metrics [29] and expanded [30],
[31]. Considering the space of all scenario possibles Ω, one can define a set of scenarios as
a probability measure P on this space. Between two probability measures, it is possible
to obtain a distance, using probability metrics. Finding a smaller set of n scenarios Q
approximating the original large scenario set P, of cardinality N, means minimizing this

distance. As will be further developed in 4, this is a problem proven to be NP-hard.
It is however possible to use heuristic algorithms giving good results. Those heuristics
have been refined to be more computationally effective [30] and expanded to scenario
trees [32], applied and compared with other scenario-reduction techniques [28] or with
different parameters [33]. Those same heuristics have been applied during scenario tree
generation [34], or used to select scenarios from clusters obtained via a different method
[35].
However, these methods have been criticized for not preserving the moments of the
original probability distribution [36], a property preserved in other methods such as
importance sampling [36]. Importance sampling is a statistical technique used to obtain
the expected value of a stochastic process with fewer Monte-Carlo simulations, within a
certain accuracy. Other clustering algorithms has been developed, among which EPSO,
an Evolutionary algorithm inspired by Particle Swarm Optimization [37].
It is also worth mentioning that clustering (k-means) and probability metrics-based
algorithms rely on a distance between scenarios to execute their selection. This distance
can be chosen generically, e.g. a simple norm on the scenario space considered; but
this can have downsides. As an example, let us consider scenario consisting of simple
generation profiles of different localized renewable plants w ∈W over time t ∈T, defined
on R|W||T|. If two wind farms are close spatially, difference in their generation profiles
in two different scenarios can cancel out from the power flow point of view; but a norm
on R|W||T| will not pick up on that subtlety. Tailoring the distance between scenario, to
fit their actual impact on the problem at hand, thus has merits. Obviously, knowing
the exact impact of each initial scenario without solving the actual model is a difficult
exercise, but can yield good results [35].
3.4
Scenario set or scenario tree
3.4.1
Multi-stage programming context
As explained in 2.1, two-stage programs consider independent scenarios, with the re-balancing
stage happening on the whole duration considered. This way of modeling uncertainty
is somewhat inefficient to represent typical weather forecasts - and thus, renewable
production forecasts. The closer to present the time period is, the more precise the
forecast is, and the less important the number of scenario need to be to model uncertainty.
Similarly, the farther away to present, the more scenarios are required. This does not
reconcile well with a two-stage stochastic program, where the number of scenario is fixed
and the same for all time periods. Multi-stage programs consider a scenario tree, where
each tree depth representing a time step. Early time steps of scenarios are bundled
together, and scenarios are branching out as time steps advance. This has two benefits.
First, a scenario tree allows to tailor the modeling to the stochastic process considered.
==PAGE:28==
16
3 Scenario reduction framework
In a problem considering multiple time steps, if random events can happen at every
period, a complete modeling of the stochastic process over the whole period will have
scenarios identical over the first steps, then branching out to model every outcome of
each random events. Similarly, in a weather forecast where prediction accuracy decreases
over time, more scenarios are needed for the latest time steps. As explained in 2.1, the
number of constraints and binary variables is an important factor in the difficulty to
solve the model, which means that, for a given model the number of scenario nodes will
be a limiting factor; it is therefore better to spend those scenario nodes where it is the
most needed.
Second, it corrects a flaw of two-stage stochastic programs: in those, the rebalancing
stage can be done over the whole considered period, which, translated to real-life operation,
would mean that at the first hour of the day, uncertainty realize and a single perfect
forecast is known for the rest of the day. What would actually happen during power
system operation is: as operating hours pass, some forecasts are not possible anymore,
others get more probable - but no single scenario stands out. The operator then makes
adjustments to future operations with the set of still-possible scenarios in mind. In a
multi-stage stochastic program, the re-balancing at early stages has to consider the fact
that scenarios branch out afterwards. The program can thus exploit forecast but not
hindsight on what the future decisions have to be, which is much closer to reality.
3.4.2
Scenario tree application and reduction
Scenario trees have been used in numerous application: water management with flows
forecast [33], [38], financial optimization [39], as well as unit commitment [34], [40].
Some scenario reduction methods created for scenario sets have been generalized to
trees [32], [41]:
starting from a scenario tree directly However, where scenario set
reduction methods can aim at a fixed final number of scenarios dictated by computational
requirements, one then has to decide how many tree nodes to eliminate at each step. It
is possible to use a very hand’s on approach, by setting the maximum number of nodes
at each step; the risk is to reduce the tree too aggressively and lose valuable information.
In [32], it was chosen to use distance thresholds for each step t to determine whether or
not branches could be eliminated between step t0 and t −1, which guarantees that the
reduction cannot be too aggressive; however the final number of nodes is then unknown.
Another approach has been to use reduction algorithms directly during the scenario
tree generation process. In [34], scenario trees are generated time step after time step,
where a number of children are created for each node, then the fast-forward algorithm
is applied to select a few of these nodes.
In this thesis, scenario tree generation and reduction has not been considered. However,
the NCSUC model has been adapted to run on a scenario tree; the python code for this
tree model is available with the rest of the implementation used in this thesis.

3.5
Evaluation model presentation
As will be developed in chapter 4, the purpose of this thesis is to present scenario
reduction techniques, apply them in a standard test case. To do so, a SUC optimization
model is run to get a decision, defined as the set of first stage variables outputted by the
model, from the reduced set of scenarios. Then, the decision thus obtained is evaluated
against the optimal decision, obtained from the full initial set of scenario. To do so,
evaluation models have been derived from the SBSUC and NCSUC models described in
2.4. Those evaluation models are essentially identical to the model they are derived from,
except the first stage variables are now fixed and given as input, and the model only
has to derive the second stage variables and the objective function. For a given number
of scenario, this evaluation model is, unsurprisingly, much faster to solve than the full
model.
Indeed, there are no binary variables in the second stage, which eliminates
a major contributor to complexity. Additionally, when first-stage variables are fixed,
the model is effectively partitioned in smaller, separate models each corresponding to a
scenario, which contributes to ease of computation as well.
==PAGE:30==
18
3 Scenario reduction framework
As a result, evaluating a scenario-reduction method has been done as follows:
1. From the initial set of scenarios, run the full model to obtain the optimal decision
on the full uncertainty, as well as the optimal objective value.
2. Run the reduction method to obtain a reduced set of scenarios
3. Run the selected SUC model on the reduced set to obtain an actual decision
4. With the given decision, run the evaluation model with the initial set of scenario
to obtain an actual objective value. The difference between the optimal objective
value, obtained at step 1, and the actual objective value gives the optimality gap
of the considered decision.
Additionally, one can infer the ”prediction gap”.
When running the SUC model
on the reduced set, an objective function value is infered along with a decision, the
”predicted” objective value. One can then compare this predicted value to the actual
objective value. A system operator might take interest in the prediction gap, to know
how much the actual value can differ from what is computed initially.

As exposed in 3.3, a particularly consequential approach has been to consider a scenario
set as a discrete probability measure on the space of all possible scenarios, and exploit
properties on probability metrics to find smaller sets of scenarios. This chapter will
present this approach in a detailed mathematical formulation and apply it to the standard
test case.
4.1
Formulation
In [29], further expanded in [30] and commented in [31], a formulation of general scenario
reduction in stochastic programming was introduced and showed how to implement two
techniques for scenario reduction based on two different heuristic approaches. First of
all, as explained in 3.3, a scenario set can be seen as a discrete probability measure
P on the space of all possible scenario Ω. Naturally, the space of all possible scenario
is a continuous one, since the underlying stochastic processes are continuous. Generating
scenarios thus acts as a sampling process, transforming a continuous probability distribution
into a discrete one.
As a result, scenario reduction is equivalent to finding another
discrete probability distribution, of smaller support, that would adequately approximate
the initial one.
Ultimately, what is interesting for scenario reduction is the distance between the
decision taken with probability P and with probability distribution Q, with Q being
ideally with a much smaller support, i.e., with much less scenarios. It is possible to
define a distance ζc(P, Q) between P and Q that will allows us to quantify the difference
between those 2 probability measures, and allow us to bound the difference in the 2
decisions taken with P and Q.

4.3.1
Impact of norm choice on optimality gap and

For this part, the system considered was 6 wind farms of 200 MW each, as suggested in

As explained in 4.2, using the optimal redistribution rule on a reduced set of scenario
to get a reduced probability measure Q allows to compute ˆµc(P, Q) easily. Additionally,
this has the added benefit to demonstrate which part of reduction heuristics is the most
important : is it the selection of scenario, or the rebalancing of scenario weights after a
selection is made.

a quite small optimality gap and significantly better than random selection, even with
weights redistributed. It is worthy of note that some random selections perform quite
well; but it is also possible to get wild cards which get a significantly worse decision.
In average, using reduction heuristics appears to be a significant improvement over
randomly selecting the scenarios.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion and future work
The paradigm of scenario reduction is, in essence, to capture the most essential information
contained in the initial scenarios, but in a smaller, more manageable structure.
As
presented in chapter 4, it is possible to use simple norms between scenarios and methods
inspired by probability metrics to achieve interesting results. In particular, the gains in
computation time coupled with the minimal losses in optimality gap can prove very
effective.
It is to note, however, that the higher the wind power penetration, the
higher the penalty is to reduce scenarios: the loss of information caused by the scenario
selection process will have the most consequences when renewable production is high
and concentrated.
To go beyond the reduction heuristics presented in 4.2, two main axes of progress
can be identified. The first is to use distance between scenarios closer to their impact on
the actual stochastic model used, rather than generic norms on mathbbRn. As discussed
in 4.3.2, this is a task presenting theoretical difficulties, if one simply want to swap the
distance c used in backward reduction and forward selection. Obviously, as done in [35],
it is possible to use different clustering algorithms. However, the framework provided
by probability metrics does provides a powerful result in terms of model stability, which
could be interesting to exploit better with a more suitable distance.
A second axis of progress is to go outside scenario sets, to instead build a scenario
tree. The advantages are numerous: a multi-stage stochastic program closer to real-life
operation, the ability to tailor the uncertainty representation by allocating increasing
node numbers at various depths. It does present a theoretical challenge: determining how
many nodes to allocate at each depth, depending on the initial uncertainty representation.
Multi-stage stochastic optimization is a rather active field with numerous practical
applications, and unit commitment will surely benefit from the theoretical advances
in that field.

• Solving times: time spent solving the model on the reduced set of scenarios.
