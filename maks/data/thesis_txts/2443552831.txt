Abstract
This thesis presents an experimental campaign of 16 push-oﬀtests of keyed shear connections. The
specimens consists of two L-shaped prefabricated elements grouted together and are all tested in a
combination of shear and tension load. Of the 16 specimens, 12 are grouted with mortar and 4 with
concrete. The keyed shear connections are all reinforced the conventional way with overlapping
U-bars and a locking bar. The ultimate shear load capacities are compared to both upper and
lower bound ﬁrst order rigid plastic calculations.
The purpose of the test campaign is twofold. First to investigate the behaviour of the keyed shear
connection in a combination of shear and tension load. More speciﬁcally, how the deformation
capacity of the connection is inﬂuenced by the tension load level. This is done with the use of
Digital Image Correlation (DIC) and Linear Variable Diﬀerential Transducers (LVDT’s). Secondly,
to asses whether the applied models accurately predicts the combined capacity of the keyed shear
connections.
The tests indicate that both the ductility and the shear capacity of the specimens decrease with an
increase in tension load. A tension load above 50% of the maximum tension capacity also leads to
a change in failure mechanism not predicted by the upper or lower bound models. This failure is
an anchorage failure. The upper bound model predicts the capacity of specimens loaded with low
tension loads well. The lower bound models can be used to predict the capacity of the specimens
tested with a high tension load with suﬃcient accuracy. The tests show that the specimens grouted
with mortar and concrete have roughly the same failure pattern and the same load-deformation
curve.

Sammenfatning
Dette speciale præsenterer en forsøgsserie med 16 push-oﬀforsøg af fortandede forskydningssam-
linger. De testede emner består af to L-formede præfabrikerede elementer, som er støbt sammen og
testet i en kombination af forskydning og træk. Af de i alt 16 elementer er 12 af dem udstøbt med
mørtel og de sidste 4 af dem er udstøbt med beton. De fortandede forskydningssamlinger er alle
armeret på konventionel vis med overlappende U-bøjler og en låsestang. Forskydningskapaciteten
sammenlignes med både øvre- og nedreværdi førsteordens stift-plastiske beregninger.
Formålet med forsøgsserien er todelt. For det første undersøges opførslen af de fortandede forskyd-
ningssamlinger i en kombination af forskydnings- og træklast, altså hvordan deformationska-
paciteten af samlingerne er påvirket af træklastens intensitet. Dette gøres ved hjælp af Digital
Image Correlation (DIC) (Dansk: digital billede korrelation) samt ﬂytningsmålere. Det andet for-
mål består af at undersøge de benyttede modellers evne til at forudse kapaciteten af samlingerne
i de kombinerede lasttilfælde.
Forsøgene viser, at både duktiliteten og forskydningskapaciteten mindskes med en forøgelse af
træklast. En træklast på over 50% af den samlede kapacitet af samlingerne medfører desuden et
skift i brudmekanisme, som hverken er bestemt med øvre- eller nedreværdimodellerne. Denne æn-
dring i brudmekanisme skyldes et forankringsbrud. På trods af dette forudser øvreværdimodellerne
kapaciteten af samlingerne godt for forsøgene med lave værdier af træk. Nedrevædimodellerne kan
bruges til at forudse kapaciteten af samlingerne testet med høje værdier af træk med tilfredsstil-
lende præcision. Forsøgene viser, at samlingerne udstøbt med henholdsvis mørtel og beton har
omtrent de samme brudﬁgure og arbejdskurver.

Introduction
Pre-fabricated reinforced concrete (RC) is widely used in the construction industry and for good
reason. It is easier to perform quality control on pre-fabricated elements. It is also often a cheaper,
more eﬃcient and less time-consuming solution than casting in situ. However, an important as-
pect of a pre-cast structure is the joints between the diﬀerent pre-cast parts. The overall structural
behaviour of the structure will depend on the behaviour of the connections.
It has long been the ambition to use computational methods to model the overall ultimate load
bearing capacity of RC structures. These more detailed calculations require an in-depth knowledge
of the behaviour and deformation capacity of the joints between the pre-fabricated parts of the
structure.
When designing a pre-cast structure, the wind load is often carried by a system of stabilizing walls.
Between these walls are the keyed shear connections that ensures that the load is transferred be-
tween the diﬀerent panels. These connections should be able to transfer both shear and normal
forces between the panels.
Today, empirical calculation methods and simpliﬁed assumptions dominate the ﬁeld of connection
design. If more precise models are wanted for elasto-plastic computations, it is important to know
the behaviour of the keyed shear connection in detail with a variation of diﬀerent load types.
Recent research has been conducted with an unconventional reinforcement layout of the keyed
shear connections.
Instead of having a locking bar in the middle of the connection along the
length, the U-bars were turned 90° and had individual double T-headed bars between them. The
results from that research showed an improvement in both the capacity and the ductility of the
connections.
Other research has tested keyed shear connections with wire ropes instead of using U-bars. The
reason for this approach is that the wire ropes has no bending stiﬀness which makes it easier to
build on the site. Tests shows that the wire ropes makes the failure of the connection more prone
to failure in the grout material between the wire ropes. Models are therefore also created to take
the strength of the grout material into account together with eﬀects of conﬁnement.
In the spring of 2018 a separate master’s thesis was conducted to test the inﬂuence of diﬀerent
combinations of shear load and conﬁnement due to normal forces. In that thesis it is concluded
that the normal force has great impact on the shear capacity of the keyed shear connections.
Meaning that an increase in conﬁnement of the connection leads to an increase in shear capacity.
The ductility of the connection was not impacted by the increase in conﬁnement.
This thesis examines how the shear capacity of keyed shear connections between pre-fabricated
RC panels are reduced when loaded with a combination of tension and shear. The study involves
an experimental campaign covering the range of pure shear with a combination of tension and
shear with the pure tensile capacity of the keyed shear connection. The results complement the
previous master’s thesis covering the combination of conﬁnement and shear.
For the theoretical modelling, plastic upper bound solutions are used, covering both shear and
tension capacity and the combination of these loads. A focal point for the thesis is also to precisely
describe the load deformation curves for shear load with increasing tension load levels. These
curves can then be used for more detailed elasto-plastic calculations.
The test setup used is based on a test setup that has previously been used to test the combination
of conﬁnement and shear. Displacement data is collected during the tests using Digital Image
Correlation (DIC) in order to describe the load-deformation curves of the connections.

Theory of Plasticity
The keyed shear connections are analysed using ﬁrst order rigid plastic analysis. A short introduc-
tion to the relevant theories and equations is therefore presented below.
A rigid plastic material is deﬁned as a material where no strains occur, until the stress reaches a
certain value, called the yield stress (fY ), where arbitrarily large deformations can occur without
a change in loading. An example of a stress-strain curve for such a material, in uni-axial tension
and compression can be seen in Figure 2.1.

Such a material does not exist in reality, but as an assumption a lot of structural materials can be
treated as perfectly rigid plastic materials. An example of such a material is reinforcement steel,
but also concrete given an appropriate eﬀectiveness factor.
The analysis of structures at their peak load using plastic theory is called limit analysis and makes
use of three concepts. A yield condition that describes the combination of stresses that causes the
material to yield. The ﬂow rule that describes the connection between plastic strain increments
and stresses. Finally the extremum principles can be used to determine the load carrying capacity
of a structure.

2.4 |
Extremum Principles
The extremum principles are incredibly useful when determining the yield load of a given structure.
The principles assumes that the yield surface is convex and diﬀerentiable. The mathematical proof
for the extremum principles can be found in (Nielsen et al., 2011).
2.4.1
Lower Bound Theorem
The lower bound theorem states that a given safe and statically admissible stress distribution will
correspond to an applied external load that is equal to or below the yield load. A safe stress
distribution is a stress distribution that is on or inside the yield surface i.e. f (Q1, Q2, ..., Qn) ≤0.
A statically admissible stress distribution satisﬁes the boundary and equilibrium conditions.
This means that if it is possible to ﬁnd a stress distribution that is admissible and safe, the ultimate
load will be larger than the load found.
2.4.2
Upper Bound Theorem
A geometrically possible yield mechanism, where the strains have been determined by the ﬂow
rule, will have a corresponding load that is equal to or larger than the yield load. A geometrically
possible yield mechanism fulﬁls the geometrical boundary conditions and comparability conditions.
Note that this means that the corresponding stress ﬁeld does not have to fulﬁl the statical condi-
tions.

2.4.3
Uniqueness Theorem
According to the lower and upper bound theorem only one load can be found where the stress
ﬁeld is safe and statically admissible and the strain ﬁeld is geometrically admissible. This load will
be equal to the yield load of the structure and it is uniquely determined. However it should be
remembered that for a rigid plastic structure only the yield load is uniquely determined, not the
stress or strain ﬁeld.

Lower Bound Models
The theoretical capacity of the keyed shear connection can also be modelled with lower bound
methods.
One such method is the strut and tie method described in (Nielsen et al., 2011). This solution
yields a very conservative result. By considering a more advanced stress distribution, taking the
friction in the interface layer and biaxial stress state in the nodes into account, it is possible to get
a much less conservative estimate of the capacity of the keyed shear connection. This solution is
developed in (Jesper Harrild Sørensen, 2017c).
In the lower bound solutions, the keyed shear connection is assumed to be in plane stress.
5.1 |
Lower Bound Modelling of Keyed Shear Connections
The stress distributions in these more advanced lower bound models are inspired by FELA (Finite
Element Limit Analysis) results. They diﬀer from a traditional strut and tie model by taking
the friction in the nodal zones into account and considering them as plane stress discs instead of
hydrostatic zones.
The transverse reinforcement is assumed to be distributed evenly across the joint and are as such
not connected to the struts in the nodal zones as usual for strut and tie solutions. The tensile load
is taken only by the transverse reinforcement and in eﬀect, an increase in tensile loading means a
decrease in eﬀective U-bar reinforcement, much like the upper bound solution. The locking bar is
not taken into account in these lower bound models.

7.3.1
Used Data Acquisition Devices
The deformations of the connections are measured using two diﬀerent technologies. Linear Variable
Diﬀerential Transducers (LVDT’s) are placed on one side of the test specimens at each side of the
connection where the longitudinal and vertical deformations are measured. The connections are
also measured using Digital Image Correlation (DIC). This method is applied by spraying the
connections with spray paint which can be used to create a speckle pattern in DIC software.
During the tests, pictures are taken of the connection and the speckle pattern on the specimens
can be used to measure the strains and displacements on the surface of the specimens.
The advantage of using LVDT’s is that the LVDT data is ready to be used immediately after the
test is ﬁnished and it is easy to compare between specimens. The data that is collected by the use
of DIC has to be processed with software ﬁrst but it grants the possibility of measuring strains
and deformations everywhere on the surface that has the speckle pattern. It should also be noted
that the LVDT data is sensitive to rotation of the LVDT itself as the data is only a displacement
which does not take the angle of the LVDT into account. Virtual extensometers with DIC, on the
other hand, can measure a deformation in any direction (i.e. vertical or horizontal) regardless of
the rotation of the speckle pattern.

7.3.5
Testing Procedure
A total of 16 specimens are tested in this thesis. 12 specimens are grouted with mortar and 4
specimens are grouted with concrete. The amount of tension load that is applied in the tests with
a combination of shear and tension load is done in intervals of approximately 12.5% of the maxi-
mum tensile capacity. In order to do tests with a combination of shear and tension, it is necessary
to know the actual capacities of the connection in pure shear and tension. A total of 2 specimens
grouted with mortar are tested in pure shear and a total of 3 specimens grouted with mortar are
tested in pure tension in order to know the shear and tensile capacity of the specimens respectively.
Non of the specimens grouted with concrete are tested in pure tension as it is assumed that the
tensile capacity is the same regardless of the grouting material. That is because it is assumed that
failure of the specimens in pure tension is due to the U-bars. The specimens grouted with concrete
are tested in pure shear and in combination tests with tensile loads of approximately 12.5%, 25%
and 50%.
The shear force is applied using displacement control with a displacement rate of 2 mm/min
in all of the tests except the pure tension tests which of course does not have any shear force
applied. The tests in pure tension are tested using displacement control with a displacement rate
of approximately 1 mm/min (exact input in the machine is 0.017 mm/sec). The tests with a com-
bination of shear and tension are ﬁrst loaded with tension to the desired tension load using load
control. The load rate for this is based on the mean time it took to reach the maximum tension
force in the pure tension tests. The tension load rate is then 0.2 kN/sec which is used for the time
it takes to reach the desired load. When the tension load has reached the desired value, the tension
force is kept steady at that value for the rest of the test or until the connection loses the ability to
carry both the shear and tension forces. Since the tension force is load controlled, it is important
to set displacement limits in order to prevent the connection to be torn apart when the tension
force can no longer be obtained.
The loads from the actuators and the displacements of the LVDT’s are logged throughout the
tests using a data logger. The LVDT’s are calibrated together with the logging equipment and
the calibration can be seen in Appendix M. The logging rate is set to be as fast as the equipment
can handle which, for the amount of data inputs used in this setup, is approximately 0.52 seconds
per data log. The pictures used for DIC are taken manually. The rate of the pictures increases as
the test approaches the maximum capacity and then decreases again a while after the maximum
capacity has been reached. The load associated with each picture is collected by using a load
display placed on the test specimen. A calibration of the load display can be seen in Appendix N.
The quality of the pictures is checked before the beginning of each test to see if the sprayed pat-
tern can be recognised by the DIC software. After the quality check, a picture of the connection
together with a ruler is taken which is later used to let the software know the physical distance per
pixel. Then a picture is taken just before the test is started which is used as a reference picture in
the DIC software. The stop criteria for the tests are as follows. The tension force can no longer
be sustained, the load is much lower than the ultimate capacity or it is estimated that continued
testing will render it very diﬃcult to remove the test specimen from the test setup.
When all the push-oﬀtests are done, the grouting material is removed from the connection with the
use of a demolition hammer in order to explain some of the drops in load and to get a better idea
of the failure mechanism that occurred in the test. The main things that can be seen by removing
the grouting material is the state of the reinforcement and local key corner cut-oﬀs. Pictures of
the connections after the removal of the grouting material can be seen in Appendix S.

9
Experimental Analysis
This section contains an analysis and a discussion of the test results. The following will be anal-
ysed
• Discussion of the load deﬂection curve for all specimens, considering both DIC, LVDT’s and
the state of the reinforcement after test.
• Reﬁnement of the capacity calculations by taking the grout’s ability to transfer the tension
force into account.
• Comparison between the predicted capacity and the experimental results for all specimens.
• Comparison between the connections grouted with mortar and concrete.
• Investigation of the inﬂuence of eﬀectiveness factor and friction angle.
• Discussion of the inﬂuence of the scale and other factors that can inﬂuence the yield mecha-
nism of the keyed shear connection.
The specimens are split into four separate categories
• Mortar grouted specimens tested in shear with a tension force of less than approximately
50% of the tensile capacity.
• Mortar grouted specimens tested in shear with a tension force of approximately 50% of the
tensile capacity or more.
• Mortar grouted specimens tested in pure tension.
• Concrete grouted specimens all tested in shear with tension of up to approximately 50% of
the tensile capacity.

experimentally observed pure tension capacity and one for the specimens with a tension load level
at or above 50%.
The specimens tested in pure tension will be discussed on their own. At the end, all the specimens
grouted with mortar will be compared.
The elements grouted with concrete are investigated on their own. Later the mortar specimens
will be compared with the concrete specimens.

because the shear force is dominating the failure mechanism and that the reinforcement is gov-
erning the capacity. The tension force can be carried in the mortar to the U-bars despite the
shear load. That means that when one part of the shear transfer mechanism fails, the load is
redistributed and carried in another way, i.e when the failure mechanism shifts from the one at
ﬁrst peak to the one at global peak or when the locking bar ruptures and the load is taken in the
U-bars and grout instead.

9.1.4
Comparison of Connections Grouted With Mortar
There are two failure patterns for connections grouted with mortar and loaded in a combination of
shear and tension. The specimens with a tension load below 50 % of the capacity have an observed
failure mechanism that closely resembles that of the modelled yield mechanism D, see Figure 4.3b.
These specimens have reasonable ductilities until a rupture in the threaded part of the locking
bar which leads to a sudden decrease in shear capacity. The results from the specimens tested in
pure shear, A1 and A2, are very similar to the previously tested specimens in pure shear found in
(Wind, 2018). These specimens showed clear signs of yield mechanism D as well.
The specimens with a tension load at or above 50 % have another failure mechanism.
They
are heavily inﬂuenced by the tension load in the connection. This manifests itself as anchorage
problems of the locking bar and failure in the grout that transmits the tension load between the
U-bars. It could be that the lower force in the locking bar, due to anchorage problems, causes
the conﬁnement in the mortar between the U-bars to drop, weakening the connection’s capacity
to carry the tension force. This leads to a sudden drop in shear capacity after the global peak
load. The failure mechanism for these specimens is not a part of the yield mechanisms modelled in
Section 4. Even though signs of failure mechanism D can be observed in some of these specimens,
it does not fully develop and the load deformation curve is dominated by the eﬀects described
above.
The specimens tested in pure tension does not develop the predicted yield mechanism. Instead
there are signs of failure of the mortar between the U-bars. The failure mechanism for the pure
tension specimens is similar to the specimens tested at or above 50% tension load.
Both test
categories seem to have a failure in the grout at the U-bars. The failures of the specimens tested in
pure tension are rather brittle and the specimens are prone to rotation after the global peak load
is reached. It is therefore not relevant to analyse the tension specimens load deformation curves
further.

Comparison Between Specimens Grouted With Mortar and Concrete
When comparing the specimens grouted with concrete with the specimens grouted with mortar,
two apparent diﬀerences in behaviour becomes clear. First of all the specimens seem to have the
same failure mechanism but are best described by two diﬀerent upper bound capacities. Second
that the specimens grouted with concrete seems to have a much better ductility. This will be
discussed further below.
The capacities of the specimens grouted with concrete seem to be best described with yield mech-
anism D with a reduced area of the locking bar. The mortar specimens with comparable tension
loads, on the other hand, are best described with yield mechanism D without a reduction of the
locking bar area. This diﬀerence in behaviour can be explained by anchorage problems of the
locking bar for the specimens grouted with concrete, see Figure 9.25. The higher strength of the
mortar ensures that a higher tension force can be carried by the locking bar. As discussed, the
limit for the threaded part of the locking bar might not be reached due to the transfer of force from
the locking bar to the grout, see Figure 9.34. This mechanism is not relevant for the specimens
grouted with concrete because they cannot anchor the reduced force of the bar.
The specimens grouted with concrete seem to have much larger deformations before total failure
compared to the specimens grouted with mortar. But as described, the specimens grouted with
77 of 82
==PAGE:90==
Master’s Thesis
January 2019
concrete have problems with the anchorage of the locking bar. This means that the mechanism in
the two types of specimens that causes the ductility is diﬀerent. For the specimens grouted with
mortar, the ductility is caused by activation of the locking bar. When the locking bar ruptures, the
ductility is caused by second order eﬀects in the U-bars. For the specimens grouted with concrete,
the anchorage problem manifests itself soon after the global peak load. This means that the large
plateau after the peak load is caused by second order eﬀects in the U-bars. The shear interlocking
eﬀect in the aggregates for the concrete may also have an eﬀect however it cannot explain the large
ductility.
9.5 |
Discussion of the Deviation Between the Tests and the Expected Results
In Subsection 9.3 it is discussed how some of the test results deviates from the expected behaviour.
It is observed that the failure mechanisms that occur in the push-oﬀtests are diﬀerent from the
ones expected. Most of the tested specimens with low levels of tension, developed yield mechanism
D while the expected yield mechanism is C. In the push-oﬀtests with high levels of tension, the
expected yield mechanism is either A or C. The observed yield mechanisms was, however, D or a
mechanism that has not been modelled. There are several aspects of the tests that can explain
these deviations. These aspects are discussed below.
9.5.1
Failure Between the U-bars
Some of the deviations in both the specimens with high as well as low levels of tension can be
explained by anchorage problems of the locking bar. The specimens tested with high levels of
tension had another failure mechanism than the ones modelled. The failure mechanism observed
includes failure in the grout between the U-bars which occurs, as the tension force has to be carried
in the U-bars and this force in the U-bars has to be transferred between them in the grout. In
Subsection 9.2 a model is introduced that can be used to calculate the capacity in the grout when
conﬁnement from the locking bar is taken into account.
With this model, it is seen that the
capacity of the mortar is lower than the yield strength of the U-bars for specimens tested in pure
tension. The result of this is that the grout will fail before the reinforcement starts to yield which
leads to a brittle failure of the connection instead of a more ductile behaviour which is seen in the
tensile tests of the reinforcement.
9.5.2
Scaling of the Tested Specimens
The design of the tested keyed shear connections is shorter than the keyed shear connections that
are made for full size structures. This gives less keys in the connection which, according to the
theory, makes yield mechanism C more likely to occur. With an increase of the keys, the capacity
of yield mechanism D will get closer to yield mechanism C. This is because the inﬂuence of the
locking bar diminishes.
Another problem with the size of the tested specimens is the anchorage length of the locking bar.
The inﬂuence of the anchorage problems becomes more signiﬁcant as the connections are relatively
short. The result of this is that the locking bar can not be fully utilised. It is attempted to avoid
this anchorage problem by placing a washer at each end of the locking bar, however, this results
in a much lower capacity of the locking bar at the threaded part. All of this makes it diﬃcult to
predict the stresses in the locking bar and the connection.

9.5.3
Procedure of the Application of the Loads
Some of the deviations between the expected results and the tests can also be due to the procedure
of the load application of the tests. The connections tested in a combination of tension and shear
are ﬁrst gradually loaded with tension until they reach the intended steady tension load and then
the shear load is applied. For specimens tested with high tension loads, it is observed that the
tension load itself results in cracks in the interface before the shear load is applied. An example
of this can be seen in Figure 9.49 for the specimen tested with the highest level of tension in
combination with shear.
Figure 9.49: Major strains before the shear load is applied for specimen C2
By looking at the interface cracks in Figure 9.49 it can be seen that the amount of interface friction
must be limited. This has an inﬂuence of the capacity calculated in the advanced lower limit model
which takes the interface friction into account. The size of the interface cracks can also result in
second order eﬀects before the the peak loads are reached and the modelled capacities in this
thesis do not account for second order eﬀects. An illustration of this stepwise loading can be seen
in Figure 9.50.
(a) Assumed mechanism
(b) Pure tension applied
(c) Shear load applied
Figure 9.50: Illustration of the stepwise loading
The stepwise loading shown in Figure 9.50 shows that the dissipation of the grout is lower, as the
local key corner cut-oﬀis smaller. This also inﬂuences the lower limit solution as the interface
friction is reduced or removed completely. This can be a part the explanation of the lower capacity
of the specimens tested with high levels of tension.
9.5.4
Assumption of Yield Mechanism In Locking Bar
When the specimens are loaded with tension loads close to the maximum tension load, the trans-
verse deformation will be a big part of the relative displacement ﬁeld.
This means that the
assumption of only axial stresses in the locking bar becomes questionable. It is likely that some
shear stresses will be present in the bar.

Conclusion
The specimens grouted with mortar and loaded with low levels of tension loads have relatively
ductile behaviour showing a steady state after the global peak load. When the tension load level
increases, the level of ductility decreases. The decrease in ductility for these specimens is rather
large, however this is due to a change in failure mechanism. Whereas the specimens with low levels
of tension fail as a result of rupture in the locking bar, the specimens loaded with high levels of
tension fail as a result of anchorage failure of the locking bar or crushing of the grout material
between the U-bars. Naturally, the latter failure mechanisms are much less ductile compared to
yielding in the locking bar. If a design was carried out so that these failure mechanisms were
avoided, it could be expected that the ductility would be improved for the specimens with a high
level of tension as the failure could be governed by yielding in the reinforcement. There would still
be a decrease in ductility with an increase in tension load, however it would probably not be as
dramatic as for the specimens tested in this thesis. Specimens grouted with concrete show a better
ductility but this is probably due to the catenary eﬀect of the U-bars which is a second order eﬀect.
The proposed upper bound solutions predict the capacity with suﬃcient accuracy as long as the
assumptions for the models ﬁt with the failures in the tested specimens. The proposed upper
bound models can not account for neither grout failure between the U-bars nor anchorage failure
in the locking bar. It therefore seems clear that the models can not be expected to predict these
capacities with good accuracy.
For specimens tested in pure tension, both the upper bound solution as well as the lower bound
solution from (Jesper Harrild Sørensen, 2017c) corresponds well with the test results. It should
be noted that the failure in the specimens tested in pure tension fail due to crushing failure of the
grout between the U-bars. A model based on (Henrik B. Joergensen, 2016) is used to analyse this
behaviour which also predicts the test results very well.
The eﬀectiveness factor used in this thesis predicts the capacity of the connections suﬃciently.
Variations of the eﬀectiveness factor is checked to see if the models ﬁts better with another eﬀec-
tiveness factor but no improvement of the precision of the models can be observed. Changes of
the friction angle is also checked but the results from this analysis gives the same result as the
changes in eﬀectiveness factor. No improvement of the precision of the models is seen by changing
the friction angle.

Suggestions to Future Research
The primary focus of this thesis is the investigation of keyed shear connections loaded in shear and
tension. For high tension loads, the load-deformation curve seems to be heavily inﬂuenced by the
anchorage problems of the locking bar and grout failure in the grout between the U-bars. A test
series with a more ductile failure mechanism, such as yielding of the U-bars could be interesting
and achieved with the following modiﬁcations.
• Better anchorage of the locking bar.
• Increase the leg width of the U-bars to increase the capacity of the grout transferring the
tension stress between opposing U-bars.
The results of the investigation have shown that the following observations could be researched fur-
ther. Especially that the observed yield mechanism does not match the theoretical yield mechanism
for the test campaign merits further investigation.
• A test campaign with another key geometry to check the inﬂuence of key depth and investigate
the eﬀect it has on the failure mechanism.
• Specimens with more keys as it is suspected that the length of the connection inﬂuences the
behaviour
Finally, it is suggested that a better model is developed for the conﬁnement stress in the grout
between the U-bars to ensure that the failure of the keys is governed by a ductile failure regardless
of the combination of tension and shear load.
