Abstract
For the time being the tracking radar is a rather expensive conﬁguration and mostly used by the
industry. doing the course of the project, tracking radar will be built under following restrains:
The price, the transmission power, the bandwidth of transmission, simplicity, and the learning ex-
perience for students.
Diﬀerent methods of tracking will be evaluated such that a qualiﬁed choice can be made.
The sequential scan tracking method will be evaluated to see if this method would be preferable
compared to other methods of tracking. Arguments will be provided for the Sequential lobbing,
Amplitude comparison Monopulse and the Phase comparison Monopulse.
The Phase comparison method will be chosen at the end. The choice is made based on its
simplicity, cost, learning experience, and performance.
The three wave forms dual frequency continuous wave, frequency modulation continuous wave,
and simple pulse will be assessed with the prospective of being used in a Phase comparison
Monopulse.
The three wave forms are acccesed based on criteria such that a qualiﬁed choice can be made
about of which type of wave form to select. This accesment gives the result that the frequency
modulation form will be the most suitable for the application because of it having best chances of
succes under the presented criteria.
An overview of the design of each component, the set up of the test, and results of the test will
be evaluated in a pass/fail set up, where passing criteria are presented.
An ideal performance of the Phase comparison Monopulse in the sense of gain, losses and noise
will be shown and explained in detail.
The operation of performance for this method will be
explained. This will also explanation about which components are critical for the design.
This design of the ideal will be set up against the non-ideal radar which is shown with the losses
from the components design section. An overview of the gain, the losses, and the noise is shown in
a table of which the most critical components will be discussed in the discussion section.

Chapter 1
Introduction
For many years the tracking radar was based on the pulse radar concept. Advance in technology
have made it possible to build diﬀerent kind of modulation forms for the CW radar.
For present time several companies have made, a business out of making a tracking radar of this
type (CW radar). With the aid of the Fast Fourier Transform it is made possible to archive good
resolution and fast response. Two of these companies are Trackman and Weibel.
For Trackman, the tracking radar is used to track balls such as a golf ball, in mid air. The
ground breaking is that they can process the data real time.
Weibel uses the tracking radar to track projectiles while mid air. This is used to aid defense in
the military to shoot down incoming projectiles.
For these companies the price of a tracking radar is, rather high. This project was made to see
if it is was possible to make a tracking radar. The goal was to make it relatively cheap to aid in
educational perspective.
The educational use of the radar was to show students in the course 30400 what a tracking radar
can do, the restraints, a tracking radar has and what components is it made of.
The purpose of the tracking radar made some restrictions from the start of the project. A
civilian or non-proﬁt radar had some statutory requirements. Which frequency band it was allowed
to transmit. The power which it was legal to transmit where it was measured in Eﬀective Isotropic
Radiated Power. The cost of the project was a crucial factor. The budget of the project was limited.
The cost was therefore to be held at a minimum, without being a problem for the performance of
the tracking.
At the moment, 3 types of tracking radar are possible. The conical scan, the sequential scan
and the Monopulse design. Each of these will be discussed. They will be evaluated such that a
decision can be made of the type which will be worked with further on.
A type of radar will be chosen and arguments for this choice will be presented and what com-
ponents there will be used for the type of radar. Each type of radar design will be set up against

2.1.
PULSE RADAR
DTU
22. February 2020
The pulse radar transmits a signal which in most cases are a series of pulses, also called a pulse
train. These are enveloped in a square wave form where the signals carrier is a sine wave. The
signal is then transmitted in a certain amount of time, where after it stops for the rest of the duty
cycle. The on time of the transmitted signal is also called the pulse width of the transmitted wave
τs.
The signal are transmitted in time interval this implies that only one antenna is required. It
can be in the transmitting mode in one interval and in the receiving mode in the other part of the
signal duty cycle time.[1]
The simple pulse radar measure the distance to a object by transmitting a signal, stops trans-
mitting then measure the time it takes the signal to travel fourth and back. The wave itself travels
with the speed of light[1].
R = c · ∆t

where c is the speed of light, R is range and ∆t is the time it takes to travel fourth and back.[1]
Since the signal is transmitted in a pulse, which is transmitted in a short amount of time, the av-
erage power of the pulse has to be high. This makes the pulse radar excellent for detecting far away.
The range resolution is the ability for the radar to distinguish between two targets which lies

2.11
Range compensation
As shown in the radar equation, power is mainly lost in the range, to compensate two methods are
used, one for the pulse, and one for FMCW radar.
The idea is to maintain a steady amplitude of received signal so that the dynamic range is mini-
mized at any given time this done for each of the two radars
Pulse - range compensation to compensate for the range in fourth in the radar equation an
Automatic Gain Control (AGC) is used.
The measured time and therefore the measured range, can be compensated by a gain modiﬁcation
such that the longer the signal is away, the higher the gain is to the IF gain stage.
The AGC is controlled by a feedback loop where the feedback loop takes the time it takes for
the return of the signal and converts this into a gain modiﬁcation this modiﬁcation then sets the
ampliﬁcation for the IF-gain stage
This compensation eliminate the 1/R4 dependency from the radar equation, and therefore it is
possible to detect a object further away as well will the dynamic range be constant.
since the R power of four gives a large dynamic power range.
The AGC maintain the DC level of the receiver output constant to smooth or eliminate as much
of the noise like amplitude ﬂuctuations as possible[1].
The AGC also makes it possible to make sure that the receiver is not saturated where this would
make it impossible to extract the information of the targets position.
FMCW - range compensation For the compensation in the FMCW radar for the range in

Antenna
An antenna is the most important part of a radar, since the antenna, is the radiating part of the
radar as well as the receiving part.
It acts as a transducer for the traveling wave and transducer for the guide wave in the transmission
lines.
It concentrate, the wave in a direction. It receives the echo power from the target and it ampliﬁes
a signal with a certain gain.
Gain is a measure of how much the antenna can concentrate the transmitted power in a direction
which is called the directivity.
Power gain is where the loss of the antenna is taken into account.
The antennas decide how sensitive the radar is to the phase diﬀerence.
The closer the antennas is, placed to each other. The more sensitive is radar to small changes
while if they are placed far from each other. The phase diﬀerence is less important meant in the
matter of sensitivity.
The compromise which is made is between the sensitivity and the unambiguous range of the
angle measurement the radar can track.

Amplﬁers in general
An ampliﬁer, is a device which ampliﬁes a signal amplitude, with a certain gain.
The ampliﬁers are used in various applications such as the Audio, RF power ampliﬁers, LNA (Low
noise ampliﬁers) and IF ampliﬁers.
An ampliﬁer is a non-linear device since it consist, of a transistor which is matched to the input
and output for the application of the ampliﬁer.
Matching is out of scope for this project but will be covered slightly.
The matching is done so that either the power gain is in focus, the noise or both of them.
For a RF power ampliﬁer of IF ampliﬁer the power is in focus while for the LNA the noise is in
focus.
Designing an ampliﬁer can be read more carefully and detailed in [2].
Most RF and microwave ampliﬁers are contructed around a single transistor, and is, operated in
class A mode [2].
For an ampliﬁer following parameters are crucial, to determine the ampliﬁers performance.

A weak signal is received at the antenna from the target in the range of -99 to -35 dBm. The
signal has to be ampliﬁed by a low noise ampliﬁer (LNA) with as much gain as possible, with a low
noise ﬁgure (Friis formula). To achieve as good as possible signal to noise ratio.
After the ampliﬁcation of the signal a band pass ﬁlter is provided to the system. The band pass ﬁlter
application is to get rid of the mirrors and unwanted signals, as well as selection of the bandwidth
of the overall system. This makes the noise of the system go lower which is related to Pn = KTB.
For this application of X-band tracking radar the band pass ﬁlter will have a bandwidth of 400
MHz, and goes from 9.5 GHz to 9.9 MHz, where the center frequency will be of 9.7 GHz. The
selection of this band pass frequency is provided in section 3.1. Ideally the band pass ﬁlter has a
loss of 0 dB but a 3 dB ripple.
From the ﬁlter the signal needs to be subtracted or added between the diﬀerent channels as ex-
plained in section 3.4.3, this is done by the rat race. Each rat race as ideally a loss of 3dB per rat
race where the signal goes through two of these, this introduces a loss of 6 dB
A mixer is placed after the rat race subtraction and addition. The mixer downconvertes the signal
to base band plus a beat frequency. The signal at which the beat frequency lies between for the 40
m is later calculated but is in the audio spectrum.
At audio frequencies an active ﬁlter is possible to realise. Since it removes noise as well as have the
ability to generate ampliﬁcation. For the ideal scenario no use of an ampliﬁcation before the ﬁlter
is needed.
For the FMCW the power spectrum can be lowered, by using what is called range compensa-
tion. The range compensation is that a high pass ﬁlter is used to attenuate all the low frequencies,
where the power will be high, and with a higher frequency lower and lower attenuation of the signal.
The high pass ﬁlter have this probability have the ability to remove all the DC such that clutter
and stationary targets.
An ampliﬁcation is then made to make sure that the signal is above the noise ﬂoor as well
as being able to be detected. This state will be called the IF-gain stage. In this stage a huge
ampliﬁcation of the signal will be presented and will often have a gain of up to 60 dB, which is
hard to accomplish for a transistor based ampliﬁer, but rather easy for an OpAmp circuit. After
the IF-gain stage a low pass ﬁlter provides the ﬁltering of the mixer products, and the higher order
harmonics. The mixer products as well as the higher order harmonics are in the MHz spectrum.
The last component which is added for, the system is the phase detector. This detector has two
inputs, a reference input (range branch) and either the elevation angle diﬀerence or the azimuth
angle diﬀerence. The phase detector has to both register the diﬀerence in angle as well as the
lead/lag of the angles compared to the reference, such that it is possible to distinguish between the
azimuth (side to side) and the elevation (up and down). The diﬀerence compared to reference make
an error voltage which only provides information about the magnitude of the angle error.
All of this information is provided into the ADC/micro controller.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
In conclusion the project of building an object tracking radar was not made. It was shown it would
be possible to make a working radar within the low price of the project, The reasons for the radar
not being functioning are the following:
1. Diﬀerent components not working as expected.
2. No back end was provided.
3. No phase detector was made
4. The statutory aspects of the project
5. Equipment not available in house
Since the radar was not built, only a theoretical understanding would be possible in the course
30400. The students could get an idea of the system components and diﬃcult part of designing
a radar. The components were not working as planned which gave a higher loss than expected
this made the non-ideal test of the system fail. An ampliﬁer was not amplifying in the frequency
spectrum. Did therefore not pass the test.
Three types of tracking were discussed through the project. The phase comparison method was
chosen. This method had the most pros compared to others. Being the cheapest and the most
simple of the three types.
Each of the radars and their modulation forms were compared and set up against each other, to
make a suitable choice of these.
First the dual frequency modulation form was not chosen since this type had the crucial fact that
it needed two constant and stable oscillators.
The pulse radar wave form was not chosen since this form of wave form had a too high transmission
power and a too short a transmission pulse, with the equipment in house.
The ideal circuit was shown and a power budget and a noise budget was made to show it would
work ideally. The overall circuit with actual losses from the test was, made in theory and shows a
too high loss in front of the IF-ampliﬁer.
