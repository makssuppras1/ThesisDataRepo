Abstract
Liquid In situ holder for electron microscopy and x-ray tomography is a powerful tool
for characterizing and analyzing electrochemical reaction on a fundamental level. In situ
studies have within the recent years exploded in numbers and type of charactering tech-
niques. This project have been looking into the possibility of creating a system that could
combine electron microscopy and x-ray tomography, within the same reference system.
Commercial system are available, like the Poseidon Select liquid holder from Protochips,
but only have 3 electrical contact and is diﬃcult to mount in a computer tomography
machine. The investigation of a more versatile in situ system also called for the devel-
opment of techniques to mount materials in theses systems. Speciﬁcally, electroplating of
dendrite free tin and electrophoresis of lithium iron phosphate particles, was investigated
as methods for creating battery like systems for characterization and analysis.
The project found that a small holder fabricated in the polymer Polyether ether ketone,
is compatible with STEM, SEM, CT and possible TKD techniques. The system allows
for ﬁve electrical contacts on chips used to enclose the liquid system. This have allowed
for 4-point measurement of dendrite free tin layers, successfully deposited with diﬀerent
thickness measured in therms of coulombs. The electrophoresis process however has shown
inconclusive results, with diﬃculties in characterizing the particles with EELS in liquid
TEM holder or EDX in SEM. The chip transfer of sample between holder for diﬀerent
characterization proves the beneﬁts of similar design and reference system.
This project have given a prove of concepts for many aspects of in situ studies and suggests
variable solution for future systems.

Outline
The outline section is meant to give the reader an overview of the content within this thesis.
Part I, will state the motivation for the project along with basic introduction to concepts
used throughout this report. Scientiﬁc ﬁelds like electro chemistry, electron microscopy and
computer-tomography are the main focus of the introduction.
Part II, describes the development and fabrication of the main product of this study, a new
in situ holder for electro chemistry studies. Here the technical aspects of the holder will be
described along with the analytical methods that can be used in conjunction with this holder.
Part III, diﬀerent methods for depositing materials to be analyzed has been researched and
explored, which are presented in this part. Here concepts of electroplating and electrophoresis
are described and tested for the use in the in situ holders.
Part IV, focuses on 4-point measurements of electroplated Tin in one of the holders and proves
the functionality of this method. Diﬀerent characterization methods for future experiments are
tested and introduced in this part as well.
Part V, Summarises the the ﬁnding of the thesis and gives an outlook for future improvements
and studies.

Motivation
Today the world is still highly depending on fossil fuels both for transportation, grid power
and in the creation of electricity to charge any electrical energy storage. Many of the methods
for converting fossil fuel to electricity or motion have since the industrialization been reﬁned
and ever higher eﬃciency of cars, ships and power plans have been developed throughout this
period. However, scaling a power plants output to accommodate the energy demand is highly
ineﬃcient and has a high amount of out let of green house gases [Manthiram, 2016]. This release
of carbon dioxides from fossil fuels when burned, is of great concern today and is contributing to
the process of global warming along with air pollutions with a mix of compounds. Furthermore
fossil fuels is a ﬁnite fuel source and the energy demands of the world are ever rising.
The last few decades many renewable energy sources like wind, solar, geothermal, water turbines
and biomass have been developed and are becoming more widely available even on larger scales.
These technologies solves the problem of pollution and limited source of energy, though in many
cases the storage of the energy is still not eﬃcient enough or economically sound yet. Faster
emerging technologies as Electrochemical Energy Systems (ECES) shows great promise since
a range of devices would meet diﬀerent needs for very diﬀerent applications: vehicles, portable
electronics and large scale industrial equipment. The major technologies to utilize ECES is fuel
cells, supercapacitors and batteries. Each on show some potential but batteries are the most
viable near future option for grid storage, transportation and small portable devices. However,
the technology still face many major challenges if it is to becomes widespread for all application
areas. Cost, life cycle, energy density, safety and environmental impact are all problems that
need to be solved [Nasef et al., 2016]. These problems are all linked to the materials in use in
the ECES.
Modern batteries are designed for speciﬁc tasks and can diﬀer vastly in voltage output, current
density and recharge ability, which is highly dependent on the materials used in the batteries.
Characterizing of battery performance has over time been standardized for various techniques
and can give some information about the material being used. Though as the operation limit
of the materials are being pushed, for high and longer lasting energy output, so to do we need
to push the understanding of the materials during the electrochemical processes. Intercalation,
lithiation, forming of the Solid Electrolyte Interface (SEI) and pulverization are some of the
eﬀects that needs to be understood on a fundamental level.
This is where in situ electron
microscopy and x-ray tomography show great potential and have already given some insights
into the workings of materials used for cathodes and anodes in batteries. For not only batteries
materials but also for entire battery systems. The studies of this reports aim to further develop
methods for material characterization using the latest techniques within Scanning Electron Mi-
croscopy (SEM), Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM), Nano Computer-axial Tomography
(nanoCT) and Scanning Transmission Electron Microscopy (STEM) and solving the challenges
with building and operating the in situ Electro Chemical System (ECS) holders needed for this.

2.1
Electrochemistry
Deﬁnition of electrochemistry: “Electrochemistry is the branch of physical chemistry that stud-
ies the relationship between electricity, as a measurable and quantitative phenomenon, and iden-
tiﬁable chemical change, with either electricity considered an outcome of a particular chemical
change or vice-versa. These reactions involve electric charges moving between electrodes and
an electrolyte (or ionic species in a solution). Thus electrochemistry deals with the interaction
between electrical energy and chemical change” [Wikipedia®, 2016b]. These reactions can be
a current running a process to produce ions or a chemical reaction creating electricity like in
a battery. The overall system is supplied with an external power source and a chemical liquid
called the electrolyte, containing the reactants that will react with is other or the materials on
the electrodes supplying the current. Figure 2.1 shows a sketch of a simple Electro Chemical
Cell (ECC).
Figure 2.1: Sketch shows an electrochemical cell, with two electrodes in a bath of electrolyte.
The transport of charges between two electrodes is more complex than just using Ohm’s law
for a conducting material, since there is not a continuum of free electrons to carry the charges
3
==PAGE:13==
through the electrolyte. Instead electrochemical reactions should be seen as transfer of charges
from a metal to a chemical solution or vice-visa, which could be seen as a heterogeneous charge
transfer reaction. Seeing the ECC as a closed system where the net charges must remain the
same, then running a current through the electrodes must create opposing reactions to keep this
net sum. Depending on the potential of the electrodes the reaction will either produce electrons
in an oxidation of the electrode material or consume the electrons in a reduction of the species
involved. This is called anodic and cathodic reactions, respectively. Hence it is critical to the
reactions that the electrolyte can conduct the current. Thus the electrolyte must be able to
dissolving salt which will dissociate into negative and positive ion’s that can carry the current,
thus increase the conductivity with increase in concentration of ions. Deﬁning the conductivity
of the electrolyte is however a bit more complicated than ﬁnding the concentration of salts and
will also depend on hydration, dissociation and interaction of ions.
Hydration of metal ions dissolved in a watery solution will be positive charges creating an
attractive force between the negative side of the bipolar water molecule. This will very often
lead to the association of the water molecules with the metal ions and will make the ion grow
in size as it is now hydrated. Consequentially the mobility of the hydrated ion is lower due to
the larger dimensional cross-section.
Dissociation describes how dispersed the salt, acid or bases are in the electrolyte. High level
of dissociation is said to have a higher conductivity as the charge carriers are split into smaller
molecules or ions, giving a better net distribution. The opposite reactions, association and
recombination can be said to be and equilibrium which is often reversible but will eﬀect the
conductivity.
Interaction of Ions in high concentration electrolytes may be seen as a shielding eﬀect. This
happens when the negative and positive ions attract each other and ions of same charges repel
each other creating a net reduction in conductivity.
These eﬀects contribute to the conductivity of the electrolyte when heat is created in the bath
as an eﬀect of the current passing through it, where the heating increases the conductivity due
to the dissociation. Ohm’s law can’t be said to give full understanding of conductivity however
using it as a simple analog of a normal resistor, the electrolyte between electrodes can be viewed
in the same way and the resistance would be formulated

2.2.1
2- Vs 3-electrode Sytem
The electrochemical cell can have many diﬀerent setups depending on the components and
reaction needed to be studied. Normally when running a cyclic voltammetry (CV) it is done
with a 3 electrode setup consisting of a working-, counter- and reference-electrode, sketched
in ﬁgure 2.3(a). The working electrode is where the current is measured, the counter is also
called the auxiliary electrode, and is the other side of reaction, thus passing the current. The
reference electrode passes no current and is there measure the actual electrical potential. Since
no current is being passed in the reference it can be used to control the potential for the working
electrode. If the system only has two electrodes keeping the potential stable will be extremely
diﬃcult since the current will be modiﬁed to counter the reduction taking place at the working
electrode. This modiﬁcation of current due to the chemical reaction on the electrode, will have
an unstable and ﬂuctuating potential to counter the ongoing interface changes on the electrode.
Thus in most cases the 3 electrode system is preferred.

3.1
Electron Microscopy
EM is a versatile ﬁeld of characterization tools and can give a lot of information about a sample.
SEM, TEM and STEM are with modern microscopes all capable of imaging materials with a
few nanometer resolution or less, under ideal conditions. By probing a sample with an electron
beam with accelerated electrons at 30 or 300 [keV], the de Broglie wavelength of the probe
becomes λe = 2.23 or 7.08 [pm], which can therefore interact with sub atomic features. The
300 [keV] and 30 [keV] are examples of the acceleration voltages used in TEM and SEM for
this report, where STEM can be utilized in both cases. SEM uses the reﬂected electrons, TEM
and STEM uses the transmitted electrons and both covers several diﬀerent interactions from
the samples as indicated by ﬁgure 3.1.

The electron beam needs to be generated, accelerated and focused onto a sample through a set
of lenses, then detected in diﬀerent ways to form an image or signal. The following section will
give a basic understanding of the operations of a SEM and TEM. STEM will be touched more
upon in section 7.
3.2
Microscopes and Interactions
The scanning electron microscope is usually build in a vertical column and the top houses the
electron gun, which has a usual energy span of 0.5 −30 [keV] that accelerate and emits an
electron beam by thermionic or ﬁeld emission. The ﬁeld emission gives the most coherent and
more mono chromatic electron beam, due to the thin cold needle they are extracted from, via
the electric ﬁeld. The beam is then condensed by a system of lenses, called the condenser
lenses, which forms a focused beam of the accelerated electrons. After the condenser lenses
an aperture can be inserted, which is a small hole that cuts oﬀthe outer part of the beam,
see ﬁgure 3.2(b). These make the beam thinner and removes some of the aberrations in the
beam, which stems from the imperfect lenses and chromatic spread of the emission from the
gun. Next the deﬂection lenses will move the beam in a raster scan across the sample before
the ﬁnal lenses focus the beam into a spot. The image is then created by collection intensity
8
==PAGE:18==
measurements point by point of the electrons being reﬂected and emitted by the sample, and
a computer then renders a gray scale image by correlating the position of each measurement,
see ﬁgure 3.2(a).
The incident electrons will have a length of travel in the material being
analyzed, which is described by the mean free path, further described in section 3.2.2. These
electrons interact and change path depending on the events the electron experiences in the
sample and creating what is called the interaction volume. This volume usually extend to a
couple of µm in depth depending on the mean free path, which depend on the atomic number Z
and incident energy E0 [keV]. The detectable interaction from this volume are Back Scattered
Electrons (BSE), Secondary Electrons (SE) and x-rays, where ﬁgure 3.3(b) shows at what depth
the diﬀerent interaction can be detected from. BSE and SE are the two signals mainly used
for imaging and x-ray are used for elemental analysis. SE are electrons knocked out from an
atomic shell by a incident electron and have a shorter mean free path than BSE, due to energy
transferred is much lower than the incident energy. Therefore they can only escape from the
top most layer, which makes the SE more sensitive to surface structures. The BSE are the
incident electrons scattered back through the surface via nucleus interaction and have much
longer mean free path than SE due to the low energy loss doing the interactions, which allows
the BSE to probe the sample deeper and can reveal relative densities of matter depending on
atomic number Z. Characteristic x-rays are detectable from the whole interaction volume due
to the small absorption cross-section of x-rays, section 3.3. When an electron is knocked out of
a core shell a higher energy electron might drop in energy level and the excess energy is released
as a characteristic x-ray photon.

Elemental characterization Techniques
EDX:Energy dispersive x-ray spectroscopy measures the energy of characteristic x-ray photon
hitting the detector located at an angle above the surface of the sample, as indicated by ﬁgure

measurements are carried out in a raster scan it can create a elemental map of the sample and
calculate a weight percent of elements based on the proportions of peak of count with respect
to incident energy. The technique is limited by angle and space where the detector must be
placed. Figure 3.4(a) shows and example of ad Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDX)
spectrum.
EELS:Electron energy loss spectroscopy measure the lost energy of an electron by inelastic
scattering, where the detector is located at the bottom of a TEM. The electrons are passed
through a series of lenses so they hit a detector along a 1D axis, depending on their energy [eV]
loss and outputs the count of electrons. Many electrons being inelastic scattered by the same
atom will create and edge of high count in the spectrum. This will reveal elemental composition
of the area in view and an elemental map can be created if the microscope is running in STEM
mode.
This technique usually works best with elements that give a sharp loss edge in the
Electron Energy Loss Spectroscopy (EELS) spectrum, seen in ﬁgure 3.4(b).

Lithiation experiments
This section focuses on the diﬃculties with creating a lithium system in the in situ holder.
ECS experiments in the TEM is a complex process and just in preparation and assembly the
smallest of errors can lead to failed experiments. Aligning of chips, cleaning and drying, tweezer
handling of chips, liquid dropping on microchips and clamping with the lid, which all require ﬁne
handling and skill. This becomes a great deal more diﬃcult when Li rich organic electrolytes
are to be introduces into the system, as they strictly require to be handled in a glove box. The
very thick gloves and limited movement range of the arms have proven to make it extremely
hard to assemble and have resulted in broken membranes on the chips every time. This could
be solved be assembly outside glow box and ﬂowing the electrolyte into the holder. The organic
electrolyte have a concentration of 2 [M] LiPF6 in a Dimethyl Carbonate (DMC)/Ethylmethyl
Carbonate (EC) solution, which reacts strongly with both air and water. Even trace amount of
water and air will make the solution develop Hydroﬂuoric, with a pH of a value around 1. This
demands that the holder is very dry before the electrolyte is ﬂowed into the holder. By keeping
the holder under a constant stream of N gas, while assembling the holder, most moist from the
air should be avoided. Keeping all channels open when evacuating the airlock and leaving it
for some time in the glove box before ﬁlling, gives the system to suﬃcient time to dry, before
introducing the electrolyte. The procedure of getting the electrolyte into the system was tested
with the XEM holder, showing a picture form the procedure in ﬁgure 13.1. The syringe with
the valve and teﬂon tube is need to extra the electrolyte from Eppendorf©tubes, where small
portions have been stored. The teﬂon tube was then remove and valve screwed on to the XEM
holder and the electrolyte was introduce into the cavity in the holder.

Part V
Summary and Outlook
14
Summary
This project has been a multi disciplinary eﬀort, in which the main product has been the
XEM holder. This system makes it possible to perform SEM, STEM, and possible TKD, on a
ECS within the same dimensional reference system. Diﬀerent imaging, elemental and density
techniques and analysis is thus possible with the XEM.
The system is yet to be tested with the electroplating method, where solution of 0.2M Sn2SO4,
0.5M H2SO4 with and without additive SAT 35 1, is proven to work well for the ECSEM
system. This in situ deposition technique has delivered smooth layers of Sn for conductivity
measurements, with successful data collection on diﬀerent deposited layers on the same chip.
The lack of a dedicated counter- and reference-electrode in the XEM holder makes for an
unstable electroplating. Hence the system needs further development before multiple platings
and subsequent measurements, can be performed in the same stable manner as proven with
the ECSEM holder. LFP depositing for the aqueous battery system of Zn vs LFP, has proven
to be a delicate and still not fully investigated operation. While the processes are working for
larger electrodes, further work and development is needed for the micro electrode deposition.
XEM and TEM holders have produced liquid layers which can be electron transparent at both
30 [keV] and 300 [keV], with STEM and TEM respectively.
Though spectroscopy method
have been unable to conﬁrm LFP particles on the electrodes, but have been able to detect
the thin layers of TiO2 and Au, in dry state.The XEM holder is furthermore compatible with
the assembly and ﬂushing with LiPF6 rich solution in a glove box for later anode/cathode
lithiation experiments. Where this has shown to be a very delicate and very time consuming
process just for setting up the system for analysis. The in situ holders operation is complex
and must be conducted with the utmost care to detail, but has great potential for researching
the fundamentals in batteries or other ECS.
15
Outlook
15.1
XEM
The XEM has proven that this design is usable, but several aspects could be improved. The
holder milled out in PEEK would beneﬁt from being longer, since this would allow the stage
position further away from the pole piece in SEM. From ﬁgure 7.1(a), it can bee seen that
space is already limited and by making the holder longer the detector could be moved closer
to the pole piece, without the risk of hitting the single stub holder on the stage. The material
PEEK should be more than strong enough to make it considerably longer, and thinner at the
same time. Where the thinner dimension could lead to closer position of detector but more
importantly, possible less shadows for the TKD signal. Less material would also beneﬁt the
nanoCT, where transmission would be eﬀected by the volume of material in large part of the
rotating angle space.The central transmission could possibly have angled slopes so that larger
amount of transmitted beam could hit a detector which would be placed close to the holder.
Same goes for the lid, where the narrow hole could lead to smaller amount of the beam hitting
the sample. Contacting the chips with the pogo pins as in the ECSEM and XEM, is being
developed for the TEM holder. However, this will be done with much smaller pogo pins and
PCB or wires will not need to go past the central transmission hole. This should be build into
46
==PAGE:56==
the next XEM as well as less shadow eﬀect from metal and holder parts, in both TKD and CT,
will be visible in the respective techniques.
15.2
TKD, nanoCT and EELS
The TKD signal has proven to have either lots of shadows from holder or the Kikuchi lines have
been very spread out, due to the large detector distance. Thus a parameter study should be
performed, where the eﬀect of liquid on the holder should be a key point of the investigation.
This will be best studied in a new version of the XEM holder, with angled slops on the lid and
transmission hole. The nanoCT is a must for future development, if the density analysis on the
micro electrodes is to be realized as the microCT can’t resolve on the dimensions needed. The
nanoCT source is working and have been characterized, though motor control for optics and
stage need a function power supply. Hence it could be a simple matter of a new power supply
and repeating the characterization, before the nanoCT is operational.Furthermore, an EELS
camera could possible be attached to a SEM [Luo and Khursheed, 2006], allowing for EELS
on the XEM holder. This would give the XEM system many of the properties that have been
limited to the TEM system.
15.3
Electrophoresis
Getting better adhesion and selective deposition of LFP will be a key factor, this could still
be possible with electrophoresis. However, this method needs further development. Where
a parameter study could ﬁnd the setting for speciﬁc electrode design to be used. Though it
has been observed that diﬀerent micro electrodes would melt or otherwise be damage, when
inspected after processing. This could also indicate that the electrodes simply are too small
to handle the process, and the whole process should also be reevaluated, and other solutions
should be looked into.
15.4
Plating and Flow
Plating was shown to have a very low eﬃciency, compared with plating on large areas outside
the window region. This could be solved by fabricating the chips with a isolating layer of SU8
or some other material, that would make the window region and/or other electrodes conductive
in speciﬁc areas. This will give control of the area of deposition and less material will need
to be present in the solution.
This could solve the problem with eﬃciency and competing
reactions taking place due to the local depletion. The depletion could also be solved by adding
a ﬂow motor to the system, driving a steady ﬂow of solution through the holder. Depending
on reaction speed this could provide the reaction volume in XEM and ECSEM with a near
constant concentration. The ﬂow could potential make the system more robust for hydrogen
and oxygen evolution, allowing for larger potential area of operation for a given ECS. The need
for a plasma cleaning the chips before use under an electron beam could potential be less critical
or removed by the ﬂow. The ﬂow would continuously push liquid, possibly with a suﬃcient
force overcome the surface tension making the water reseed to the edge of the window region.
15.5
Extra electrode and conductivity
The XEM and TEM holder lacks the dedicated counter- and reference-electrode of the ECSEM
holder. This could be helped by redesigning the holder and chips with and extra contact/elec-
trode. With six electrodes in the holder the 4-point chips could have a counter and reference,
materials plated on the chips creating a real 3-electrode system for potential control during
47
==PAGE:57==
plating, CV and other electro chemical processes. The extra contact would require some fab-
rication and machining, but would make for more electrochemical stable system. The extra
contact could make the XEM very useful for 4-point measurements, as the stable system could
be analyzed with TKD, STEM, CT and possibly EELS, without moving the chip from holder
to holder.
15.6
Process and Handling
The multiple ﬁelds of engineering and science, need to work perfectly together if these systems
are going to work and produce meaningful data. This promote the development of strict SOP’s
for all aspects of handling and operation, for all holder systems, with the in situ capabilities.
This can be further complicated by materials, electrolytes etc. with diﬃculties in handling or
demands for cleanly environments etc. Where in the case of LiPF6, a SOP should be developed
for the assembly and inﬂow of the of the electrolyte in a glove box. This again promotes the
need for proper ﬂow in all holder systems, as assembly in a glove box is close to impossible,
without destroying the chips or ECS.
The strict and thorough handling and methods in following developed SOP’s, should be the
mantra for all future work with the in situ systems.

3.0 
PROCESS  SEQUENCE 
 
3.1 
Pre-treatment of steel- or copper parts 
 
Steel or copper parts are pre-treated as usual and are put into Matt Tin SAT 30-1 after 
dipping in diluted sulphuric acid. Please note that the pre-treatment process does  
not contain any hydrochloric acidic solutions. Chloride impurities > 0.3 g/l will lead to an 
irreparable decrease of the covering power. 
 
3.2 
Pre-treatment of non-ferrous metal alloys containing zinc 
 
Non-ferrous metal alloys containing zinc may not be tin plated directly, as the zinc will diffuse 
from the base material into the tin layers above even at room temperature. An intermediate 
copper or nickel layer approx. 3 - 5 µm thick is set for diffusion barrier. Intermediate layers 
made of copper should be deposited from acidic or cyanidic top quality baths. After heat 
aging, the adhesion of the copper layers deposited from additive-free cyanidic pre-copper 
electrolytes may be reduced.

3.3 
Pre-treatment of Printed Circuit Boards for tin plating 
 
The tin-plating process of printed circuit boards requires a immediate rinsing after 
electroplating with copper, followed by dipping into diluted sulphuric acid (chemically pure,  
50 ml/l) and subsequent transferring into Matt Tin SAT 30-1. 
Please note that the tin deposition may be impaired by resist bleeding of alkali-soluble resists 
in Matt Tin SAT 30-1. Therefore we recommended to check the compatibility of the individual 
resists with Matt Tin SAT 30-1 prior to operating the electrolyte. 
 
Long-term experience has proven that this system can be used as a metal resist layer 
because of its excellent metal distribution and the development of new photo resists. 
 
 
4.0 
MAINTENANCE  AND  FUNCTION  OF  THE  BATH  COMPONENTS 
 
4.1 
Tin 
 
The tin concentration should range between 15 - 30 g/l with an optimum of 20 g/l. Lower tin 
concentration will reduce the achievable cathodic current density. Higher metal 
concentrations will impair the metal distribution in the electrolyte. Lack of tin is replenished 
with Tin Salt SU (55 % tin). If a excellent metal distribution is required, we recommend to 
operate the electrolyte with a metal concentration of 10 g/l tin. The acid concentration must 
then be increased to 210 g/l (114 ml/l) sulphuric acid. The maximum achievable current 
density is approx. 1 A/dm². 
 
4.2 
Acid concentration 
 
The concentration of sulphuric acid should range between 80 and 110 ml/l with an optimum 
of approx. 100 ml/l. Lower acid concentrations would impair the good metal distribution while 
higher concentrations could lead to anode passivation in extreme cases. 
 
4.3 
Tin Additive SAT 31 1 
 
Tin Additive SAT 31 1 contains grain refining agents and is used when coarse crystalline 
depositions occur. The reasons for coarse crystalline depositions may also be caused by a 
low metal concentration, a low operating temperature or a too high cathodic current density 
and must therefore be checked first. Tin Additive SAT 31 1 is added in increments of  
2 - 5 ml/l up to approx. 10 ml/l. Overdosing is not very critical. Tin Additive SAT 31 1 is mainly 
consumed due to local drag-out losses and varies between 1.5 and 2 ml per m² surface 
throughput.

4.4 
Tin Additive SAT 32 
 
Tin Additive SAT 32 is responsible for the excellent covering power of the electrolyte.  If the 
covering power decreases though the tin and acid concentration is correctly adjusted, add 
Tin Additive SAT 32 in increments of 2 ml/l.  Overdosing is not very critical but should still not 
exceed 20 ml/l. Should the covering power be insufficient despite correct electrolyte 
composition a chloride contamination could be possible. The consumption of Tin Additive 
SAT 32 is determined by local drag-out losses and varies between approx. 0.7 - 1.0 ml  
per m² of surface throughput. Tin Additive SAT 32 is absorbed by activated carbon. Cleaning 
of resist impurities in the electrolyte with activated carbon in batch quantities only. 
Continuous filtration in bypass operation with activated carbon would cause tremendous 
changes to Matt Tin SAT 30-1.  
 
4.5 
Tin Additive SAT 26 
 
Tin Additive SAT 26 is a methanol free variety of Tin Additive SAT 32. It can be used 
alternatively with the same parameters. 
 
4.6 
Tin Additive SAT 35 1 
 
Tin Additive SAT 35 1 is a mixture of Tin Additive SAT 31 1 and SAT 32 and combines the 
characteristics of both. The electrolyte can be operated with either SAT 35 1 or with the 
single additives Tin Additive SAT 31 1 or SAT 32.   
 
4.7 
Operating temperature 
 
The operating temperature should range between 15 - 30 °C. Although, temperatures above 
give the possibility to apply higher current densities but they will decrease the covering power 
at the same time. 
 
4.8 
Current density range 
 
The applicable current density range is 0.5 - 2 A/dm². Higher current densities will lead to a 
rough-crystalline deposition. 
 
4.9 
Electrolyte agitation 
 
Sufficient electrolyte flow around the parts must be provided. Additional electrolyte circulation 
by continuous filtration is advantageous.
