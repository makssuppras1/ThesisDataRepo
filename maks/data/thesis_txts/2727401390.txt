Abstract
Travel demand predictions play a key role in the success of transport-sharing systems of
any kind. Successful predictions allow the services to utilize the reallocation of available
transportation resources by allocating them to the areas with the highest demand pre-
diction at a given time.[1] However, during data collection, the number of users served
depends on the availability of bikes and their placements. Therefore private bike data is
utilized, where every need can be considered met.[2] The goal is to predict bike traveling
patterns using machine learning methods. Contextual information, such as weather and
public holidays has been shown to improve prediction for bike-sharing systems.[3] Similar
has been shown for the incorporation of domain knowledge.[4] Now that the data in hand
is private bike data it is of interest if the same improvement will be shown. Two ways
of constraining the models with domain knowledge were defined, predicted value penalty
term and true value penalty term. Experiments were set up and conducted which in-
cluded parametric models, NNs, and LSTMs. Overall it was concluded that contextual
data did not improve prediction results. Domain knowledge improved baseline models
and multivariate models, but did not show significant improvements for univariate mod-
els. Suitable machine learning approaches and deep learning architectures demonstrated
to achieve accurate bike travel demand prediction accuracy.
Keywords: Machine Learning, LSTM, Performance Metrics, Domain Knowl-
edge, Contextual Data, Time Series Analysis, Bike Travel Demand Prediction,
Proportional Data Patterns

Chapter 1
Introduction
Living in a big city, like Copenhagen, offers multiple transport options in day-to-day life,
and these options can easily get heavily crowded during rush hours. It is hard to avoid
crowds regardless of if you prefer using your car, your bike, or public transport. A recent
popular choice in our fast evolving community is shared transport such as bike sharing
services.[5] Being able to foresee heavy periods of bike demand can be a key factor in opti-
mizing the bike distribution so there is enough for everyone in the busiest locations as well
as monitoring the eﬀiciency of the overall service.[6] Accurate travel demand predictions
are also crucial when it comes to accident prevention and traﬀic management. This is
especially important under irregular circumstances such as public holidays, social events,
or great weather days that can impact the usual travel demand.[7]
Travel demand predictions play a key role in the success of transport-sharing systems of
any kind. Successful predictions allow the services to utilize the reallocation of available
transportation resources by allocating to the areas with the highest demand prediction at
a given time. This way the customers can rely on enough supplies, even in unforeseeable
circumstances.[1] Travel demand indicates potential transportation demands that could be
fulfilled. For a taxi company, for instance, every fare request counts toward the demand
even though only some of the requests may actually be fulfilled. The number of users
served depends on a number of variables, including the availability of drivers and cars.
This emphasizes the importance of precise travel demand forecasts that take potential
demand into account.[2] The diﬀicult part of this issue is that the supply capacity of the
service controls the data for the transport-sharing service. This limits the capacity to
predict travel demand since, depending on how the data is gathered, the current data may
underestimate the actual demand for the transportation option.[1][8] The specific problem
of interest in this thesis is bike travel behavior and how to obtain a predictive model
that accurately predicts behavior. The goal is to utilize private bike data to predict bike
traveling patterns using machine learning models. The purpose of which is to construct
a model that in the future could be used to integrate into bike-sharing system demand
predictions.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
The following section explores the existing body of research on the topics of time series
prediction, more specifically within the domain of travel demand forecasting. It includes
a review of literature on parametric models and machine learning models for time series
predictions, with a focus on Long-Short-Term-Memory(LSTM) models for travel demand
forecasting. Finally, it includes a review of the literature on the inclusion of contextual
data and incorporating domain knowledge by constraining neural networks. This review
aims to provide an overview of the current state of knowledge and establish the relevance
of this research.
As addressed in the introduction, a problem that bike-sharing travel demand prediction
opposes is that the predictions are limited by the data collected. The data may or may
not represent the full demand, as the availability of bikes can be less than the actual
demand for bikes. This can cause the demand to be underestimated.[1] A topic of interest
is whether additional knowledge enhances time series-based model predictions. This data
will be referred to as contextual data.
One of the research questions of this thesis is
whether incorporating data on typical bike travel behavior in the area of data collection
into our model, which is trained on bike travel patterns, can lead to improved predictions
of bike travel demand, this will from now on be referred to as domain knowledge. That
way the model obtains domain knowledge of the usual bike travel behavior at each time
and can adjust the prediction based on those insights. The first challenge of this thesis
is establishing a base model that can successfully predict bike travel demand for a 24-
hour period. Travel demand predictions are a well-known problem that has been solved
in various ways. Even though the data used in this thesis is private bike travel data, all
timeseries prediction models based on historical patterns can be utilized.
2.1
Parametric Models
Parametric models such as Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average(ARIMA) and Sea-
sonal Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average(SARIMA) models have successfully been
applied to solve time series prediction tasks. Taghreed Alghamdi et al.[17] built several
ARIMA models using the different trends in the travel data, grasped using the autocorre-
lation function(ACF) and the partial autocorrelation function(PACF). The data used in
that specific research is non-stationary and non-normally distributed traﬀic data, recorded
at hourly intervals. The final model obtained excellent performance at a 95% confidence
interval, successfully grasping the temporal aspect of the traﬀic flows.[17] The limitation
of ARIMA models is that they are not able to capture seasonal cycles in the data nor are
they able to detect the dependencies of multiple variables. However, SARIMAX models

utilize the structure of ARIMA models to allow the model to grasp seasonality and external
variables to improve the predictions of the model.[18]. Naveen Kumar Chikkakrishna et
al.[19] successfully developed a SARIMAX model that predicts short-term traﬀic volume
using the previous week of hourly captured traﬀic flow data.[19].
2.2
Machine Learning Models
The recent rapid development of machine learning algorithms, methods such as deep learn-
ing, and improvement of computational resources have advanced the possibilities of ac-
curate time series forecasting immensely, especially when data is limited.[20] The latest
progress in the field of time series prediction also introduces effective models that are able
to train on significantly larger data sets and forecast time series patterns with greater
precision than traditional time series models. An example of such a model is Long-Short-
Term-Memory(LSTM) neural network. LSTM models are instances of Recurrent Neural
Networks(RNNs) which over RNNs can better learn the long-term dependencies and ef-
ficiently model complex sequential patterns due to their abilities to learn high-level rep-
resentation that reflects the structure of the data. Finally, LSTM neural networks aren’t
as prone to the exploding/vanishing gradient problem as RNNs, this is a consequence of
their LSTM cell that they use as activation. The LSTM cell allows the network to re-
tain information over extensive sequences.[21] Sima Siami-Namini et al.[22] explored how
LSTM compares to ARIMA in a time series prediction problems using financial time series
data recorded monthly over 33 years. The result shows that the Rounded Mean Square
Error(RMSE) is reduced by 84-87% by using the LSTM model over ARIMA, strongly
representing the superiority of the deep learning network.[22] LSTM models are also a
convenient choice for this thesis since it looks to solve univariate predictions, looking at the
entire Copenhagen area, as well as multivariate predictions, looking at 12 different zones
within the Copenhagen area. Univariate refers to one feature being fed into and predicted
by the model while multivariate refers to multiple features being fed into and predicted by
the model in parallel.[23] LSTMs can eﬀiciently model both uni and -multivariate data.
Shaheen Alhirmizy and Banaz Qader, used LSTM for forecasting Spain’s capital Madrid
Air Quality using a dataset that reports on the weather and the level of pollution through
multiple input features, utilizing the multivariate capabilities of LSTMs.[24]
The application of LSTM models in the specific case of traﬀic-related predictions has
become popular in recent years. Zheng Zhao et al.[25] trained an LSTM neural network
for solving the task of short-term traﬀic forecast using historic traﬀic data collected every
5 min from over 500 observation stations. The result shows that the LSTM network is
better than other traditional models used for the task; SAE, RBF, SVM, and ARIMA
model, especially when the forecast horizon is extended.[25] Rose Yu et al. developed
a deep LSTM and applied it to predict peak-hour traﬀic. Additionally, they improved
the model for post-accident forecasting with a Mixture of Deep LSTM, considering both
normal traﬀic and accident patterns. Evaluation on a real-world dataset from Los Angeles
shows a 30% to 50% improvement over baselines. The study demonstrates the eﬀicacy of
LSTM in addressing extreme traﬀic conditions.[26]

2.3
Contextual Data
One of the research question of this thesis is whether or not the incorporation of contextual
data into the model training improves the predictions. Centelmo et al. used historical de-
mand data along with weather data to build a model for bike-sharing demand forecasting.
They obtained that the errors in predictions were significantly reduced for the majority of
the clusters in the data, while the precision of the model remained the same, above 75%,
by introducing the weather data to the model.[3] Useful context to give to a model in the
field of travel behavior is e.g. weather and holiday data.[14][15]
2.4
Constraining Models with Domain Knowledge
Another challenge of this thesis is to incorporate the domain knowledge of bike travel
behavior into the model and see if it improves the predictions in terms of accuracy. The
previously listed research all support that LSTM neural networks are eﬀicient tools when
it comes to time series prediction, no matter which genre the data is. However, incorpo-
rating domain knowledge in the model needs to be integrated into other aspects than the
model architecture. Andrea Borghesi et al.[27] conducted a survey on ways to constrain
neural networks with domain knowledge, finding the five main approaches to inject do-
main knowledge are: acting on the features space, modifications to the hypothesis space,
data augmentation, regularization schemes, and constrained learning.[27] Regularization
schemes are widely used and allow the model to be penalized in regard to domain knowl-
edge through the loss function during training. Fischer et al.[28] developed a system, called
DL2, for training and querying neural networks with logical constraints, the final outcome
is a system that can for a number of queries find the desired logical inputs in seconds. Do-
main knowledge was incorporated into the training process of a neural network encoded as
logical constraints transferred to the loss function. Each logic constraint was interpreted
specifically into the loss function as an individual term.[28] Similarly, Mohammad Amin
Nabian and Hadi Meidani[29] incorporated domain knowledge of governing laws for the
response of a physical or biological system into deep neural networks(DNN) by involving
a penalty term to the training loss function that penalizes for divergence in predictions
form the governing laws.
This approach has significantly smaller generalization errors
than common regularization methods and provides surrogates that are physically inter-
pretive.[29] Using penalties in the loss function during training is nothing recent and has
in addition to the listed research been used as regularization for standard linear regression
models like Ridge Regression[30] and Lasso Regression[31] where the regression coeﬀicients
are shrunk towards zero by penalizing the model using the L2-norm and L1-norm penalty
terms during training within the loss function.[30][31]
2.5
Conclusion
This literature review synthesizes the main findings of the focus area of this thesis from
recent years. It discusses how parametric models have set the scene for time series fore-
casting and how machine learning models have advanced the possibilities of accurate time
series forecasting, especially when data is limited. The possibilities of different models for
time series forecasting were discussed and concluded how LSTM is suitable for prediction
tasks where the focus is on temporal patterns as well as the possibilities it offers with
regard to multivariate problems. Two possibilities to advance predictions by utilizing con-
textual data and model constraining with domain knowledge through loss functions were
discussed. This will serve as a foundation for the design and applications of the thesis and
aid in decision processes. Ultimately, the main findings of this literature review will build
the grounds for carrying out comprehensive research which aims to conclude the stated
research questions of this thesis.

This section will discuss the theoretical background of the benchmark model used in this
thesis, which is a Seasonal AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average (SARIMA) model.
Time series forecasting is split into two broad categories, parametric and non-parametric
models.[37] SARIMA is a parametric model, in general, parametric models are statistical
models that make assumptions about the underlying distribution of the training data and
estimate a fixed set of parameters to seize the relation between the predictors and the
response variable to optimize prediction for periods that reach further than the data in
hand.[38]. The backbone of the SARIMA model is the AutoRegressive Integrated Moving
Average (ARIMA) model which is one of the most frequently used time series modeling
tools, SARIMA additionally allows for integrating seasonality in the data into the modeling
aspect.[39]

4.2.1
ANN
The study of the human brain has a long history, and with the rise of modern technolo-
gies, attempts were made to replicate its thinking process. In 1943, Warren McCulloch
and Walter Pitts created a simple neural network model using electrical circuits, marking
the first step toward artificial neural networks(ANNs). Neural networks(NNs) have the re-
markable ability to extract patterns and detect complex trends in data, surpassing human
and traditional computer techniques.[46] They are composed of a densely interconnected
group of simple neuron-like threshold switching units, where each unit produced a single
real-value output from a number of inputs. By adjusting the connectivity between the
units, intricate behaviors can be modeled.[47] That is, ANNs can be viewed as weighted
directed graphs, where nodes represent artificial neurons and edges represent the outputs
and inputs. They can be classified into two categories based on their connection archi-
tecture: feed-forward networks without loops and recurrent networks with loops due to
feedback connections.[48]
There are several types of neurons that have been developed, but the most basic type of
an artificial neuron is the perceptron. As can be seen in Figure 4.1, the perceptron is
made up of a number of external inputs, these can either be the original input values or
values from the previous layer. The perceptron takes the input values and sums them up,
after weighting them with a multiplier. The weights are initialized randomly while the
perceptron learns and adjusts them based on the training data. These weights determine
the importance of the connection.
Additionally, the perceptron has an internal input
called a bias. Finally, we have the activation function, which in the perceptron is a unit
step (threshold). That is, if the output from the weighted sum and bias exceeds 0, the
neuron is considered to have fired, resulting in an output value of 1. Single perceptrons
are limited to learning only functions that are linearly separable.[47] They are described
here as a basis for understanding more complex neurons which will be described later in
the chapter.
In order to lay the groundwork for understanding more complex neural network architec-
tures, it is essential to start with the fundamentals. One such fundamental architecture
is the feed-forward neural network (FFNN). The FFNN provides a solid starting point to
comprehend the concepts of neural networks, such as layers and the connections between
them. In it the neurons are organized into layers: one input layer, one output layer, and
at least one intermediate hidden layer.
A network diagram for a two-layer feed-forward neural network, with M hidden units,
can be seen in Figure 4.2. Nodes represent the input, hidden, and output variables while
the weights are represented by the links between the nodes. The weight, or adaptable
parameters are denoted with ω = ω(1), ω(2). The information flow through the network
during forward propagation is represented with green arrows.[49]

desired performance or optimal solution. Convergence is typically measured by a loss func-
tion. A loss function, also called a cost or error function, is a measure of the discrepancy
between the network’s predicted output and the true values. It quantifies the error of the
predictions and the network works to minimize said error during training. The choice of
loss function depends on the specific problem and the nature of the data. A common loss
function for example is the mean square error (MSE), as can be seen in Equation 4.9.

• Number of LSTM layers: Increasing the number of layers can allow the model to
learn more complex patterns and temporal dependencies. On the contrary, it can
also drain computational resources and risk overfitting.[70]
• Number of hidden units: Increasing the number of units has the same gains and

in correspondence to the dimensionality of the input so that the model has a chance
to grasp the trends and dependencies in the data.[71]
• Consider bidirectional LSTM layers: Bidirectional LSTM layers process the input
sequence(s) in both forward and backward directions, allowing the model to grasp
trends both in past and future data points.[72]
• Regularization methods: Utilize regularization such as dropouts to prevent the model
from overfitting and enhances its ability to generalize well on new data.[73]
• Network tapering: Decreasing the number of units along the LSTM layers so that
each layer has fewer units than the one before. This promotes regularization by
decaying the number of parameters in the model as well as does it increases compu-
tational eﬀiciency.[71]

Chapter 4.2.7 introduces how domain knowledge is constrained into the models in this

of the domain knowledge utilized in this thesis. Domain knowledge refers to information
about the data which is already acknowledged either through some other discovery process,
experience or by an expert in the specific domain.[16] The domain knowledge incorporated
into the models in this thesis reflects on typical travel behavior and is extracted from
the Hövding data. This typical travel behavior is formulated as a weighted average of
proportionally departed trips over a fixed 24 hours for each hour of the week, using the
train split of the data. This information is then utilized to inform the models’ of the
typical proportional departure value at each hour of the week during the training of the

hour of the week. If there are X values recorded at hour Y of the week there will be X
values to make up for the CDF used to calculate the weighted average of proportional
trips at hour Y of the week. The CDF is used to describe the probability distribution
of a random variable and supplies information on the probability of the random variable
taking on a value less than or equal to a given value.

5.2.1
Initial Model
An initial LSTM model serves as the foundation for the proposed framework. The Method-
ology chapter provides a comprehensive overview of LSTM models, including its architec-
ture, functionality, and relevant details. For the implementation in this thesis, a common
approach was adopted, as discussed in the Methodology chapter, namely a simple stacked
LSTM model with dropout layers inserted between the LSTM layers. The first dropout
layer was set to 0.2, while the subsequent dropout layers were set to 0.25. The LSTM
model consists of four LSTM layers, each containing 50 hidden units. The first layer of
the model is the input layer, which was designed to accommodate a week’s worth of data
in hours, resulting in 168 units.
The final layer of the model is a dense layer, consisting of 24 units, corresponding to the
number of hours the model is tasked with predicting. In a dense layer, each neuron is
connected to every neuron in the previous layer.[50] The activation function used for this
dense layer is the custom ReLU discussed in the Methodology chapter. During training,
an Adam optimizer is employed, with a learning rate of 10−3, facilitating the optimization
process and allowing the model to update its parameters based on the gradient of the loss
function. The default activation functions provided by the machine learning framework,
Keras, are utilized to govern the activation patterns within the model. The loss function,
which the optimizer aims to minimize, measures the discrepancy between the true and
predicted values, the mean squared error (MSE) loss function is used in the univariate
section that is without constraining with domain knowledge.

Domain Knowledge
In previous chapters, domain knowledge was defined as the weighted average of the pro-
portion data and will be used to constrain the model. A more detailed explanation of this
concept can be found in the Methodology chapter, specifically in Equation 4.27, and the
methods of constraining in Equations 4.24 and 4.25. In this section, the application of
domain knowledge within the univariate LSTM model is explored.
The structure of the univariate LSTM model remains unchanged from the visualization
of the univariate model in Figure 5.14, as the constraining with the domain knowledge is
incorporated solely in the loss function and not as an input to the model itself. The λ
values chosen for experimentation are 0.0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, and 1.0. As mentioned in the
domain knowledge section of the Methodology chapter, two different forms of constraining
are to be tested; predicted value penalty and true value penalty. To recall, predicted value
penalty refers to calculating the penalty loss to encompass the discrepancy between the
predicted value and the domain knowledge while true value penalty calculates the penalty
loss to encompass the discrepancy between the true value and domain knowledge combined
with the predicted value.
The results for the constraining with domain knowledge and predicted values can be seen
in Table 5.10 and for constraining with domain knowledge and true values in Table 5.11.

yielded an average accuracy score of 0.837, while the best performing predicted value
penalty model yielded 0.868. While, on average, λ = 0.25 resulted in the best overall
performance for true value penalty with an accuracy score of 0.875. For other λ value the
true value penalty did not exceed the unconstrained performance.
However, it is noteworthy that when considering the individual models, not averages of

observations per zone in each prediction. Figure 5.34 visualizes the poor performance of

model is a two-layer LSTM model with dropout regularization, dense layers, and reshape
functionalities: The input layer expects 168 observations for 12 features, which equals one
week’s worth of data for each zone. The first LSTM layer has 64 units and is applied to

LSTM layer with 32 units which returns sequences is applied to the 24,32 output from the
repeat layer followed by a 0.2 dropout regularization. Finally, the output layer is a dense
layer with 12 units, reducing the output dimensionality to 24,12, 24 hours for each zone,

5.4
Performance Validation
Previous sections have explored the effects of constraining LSTM models, a univariate
model as well as a multivariate model, with domain knowledge. Incorporating the domain
knowledge has yielded good results, with λ = 0.25 for the univariate and λ = 1.0 for
the multivariate models, respectively. It has also proven to be more eﬀicient to constrain
the model through the true value penalty. As touched upon earlier in the chapter, the
values that the models are working with are proportional, and on a very small scale. The
errors, in turn, are therefore also small scale, and the performance enhancements as well.
This, along with the fact that the models already have quite a good performance without

6.1
Findings
This section will summarize the highlights of the findings for the uni- and multivariate
models and discuss how they compare to the benchmark models: unconstrained and con-
strained SARIMA and NN for the univariate model and SARIMA for the multivariate
model.
The model structure for the univariate model was obtained by tuning the number of hidden
units, the number of layers, and the learning rate size. Once the structure was finalized the
model was trained five times, standalone, with the selected contextual data, and with the
single most correlated contextual data feature. The first result for the univariate models
is that the model’s accuracy in prediction is not improved by integrating contextual data
into the model. The hypothesis is that, due to the fact that the data stems from a device
owned by bike owners, instead of bike renters, the weather isn’t having the same effect
as it otherwise would have. To support this, one can reflect on the results of Cantelmo
et al.(2020), discussed in the literature review, where feeding contextual data into the
model made improvements. There the model was trained with bike-sharing data.[3] It is
reasonable to conclude that people that already have a bike and were planning on using
it, are less affected by the weather than those that are choosing to rent it. This is also
supported by Figure 5.17, where the travel behavior in different weather conditions is
quantified by summing all trips taken in certain weather conditions and divided by the
number of instances the weather condition appears in the data. Note that the weather
conditions can be a combination of many. Looking at Figure 5.17 the travel behavior is
most negatively affected when there is only snow listed as a weather condition, however,

Overall, the users of the data aren’t too affected by extreme conditions.

Once
that was clear, that the contextual data did not aid in the predictions for the univariate
model, we moved towards exploring if constraining the model with domain knowledge
would improve the model’s performance.
The model was again run five times, this time for every λ parameter value and penalty term
combinations. Thus obtaining the average best performance, and therefore the optimal
model for the univariate data, which was a constrained model with λ parameter value
0.25 and a true value penalty term in the loss function. The model was enhanced from an
average accuracy score of 0.873 to 0.875 by constraining it with domain knowledge using
λ = 0.25 through a true value penalty term during training. Though the results can be
deemed marginal. However, the predicted value penalty decreased model performance as
did the true value penalty for other λ values than 0.25. Similarly, the MAE and MSE
were reduced by introducing this constraining element. Overall the model does a great job,
especially during the busy period later in the day where the distribution of the absolute
error is relatively tightly spread considering the scale of the values at that time.
Comparing the optimal univariate model to the SARIMA benchmark, the univariate model
performs significantly better, and even compared to the SARIMAX model we see improve-
ment as well. There is less improvement compared to the true value-constrained NN which
achieves an accuracy score of 0.014 lower than the optimal univariate model. Overall for
the univariate models, they are all improved in performance by constraining them with
true value penalties, though some only marginally. However, the model manages very well
in terms of performance without any constraining, indicating that the machine learning
approaches and deep learning architectures utilized for the raw model grasp the data well.
Looking at the multivariate or zonal predictions result. The first finding is that the model
structure obtained from the tuning process for the univariate model was not suﬀicient
for 12 input features and that an iterative parameter testing process and the knowledge
gained through the tuning process for the univariate model had to be utilized to build a
suﬀicient model for the multivariate data. Once the final model structure was obtained a
comprehensive comparison of the models’ average performances over three training runs
was conducted, revealing that the optimal model based on performance was the zone-
wise(ZW) constrained model, using λ = 1.0 for the weight of the penalty term and true
value penalty, with an accuracy score of 0.776 on the aggregated level. The model achieved
the highest accuracy score for zone 2, 0.806, and the lowest for zone 11, 0.710. Overall,
the models improve their accuracy by constraining them with domain knowledge, and ZW
constraining scores higher for all λ parameter values than the AVG constraining for both
the predicted value penalty and the true value penalty. Lastly, comparing the predicted
value penalty and the true value penalty the latter one performs measurably better for
both constraining methods and all λ parameters.

6.2
Limitations
While this thesis effectively explores predicting bike travel behavior patterns it’s important
to acknowledge the challenges associated with the research. This section discusses the
limitations of the thesis.
Firstly, the number of users of bike-sharing systems depends on the number of bikes
available and their placement. Since Hövding helmets are owned by individuals it allows
for a hypothetical situation where every demand is being met. It, however, might also
affect various contextual data like weather diminish. While people might still be prone
to go out biking on their own bike in bad weather they might be less inclined to rent
one. Secondly, as the number of Hövding owners is limited the data is more sparse than
would be optimal. This can affect the training of the model especially on the multivariate
level. In Figure 3.12b one can see the standard deviations from the mean of true values
for the univariate level and zone 1 and 9 from the multivariate analysis. The univariate
level and zone 1 have a lower standard deviation than zone 9. Comparing that Figure
to Figure 3.7a one can see that zone 1 has significantly more trips than zone 9. This
indicates that data density allows for more reliable predictions. This is especially true in
the case where domain knowledge is being applied. Lastly, computational resources and
time posed a limitation to the study. As could be seen in Chapter 5 the performance of the

allow for even better model structuring through extended parameter tuning.
Acknowledging these limitations is crucial for the transparency and validity of the research

6.3
Future Work
The work done so far has demonstrated the effectiveness of various models when it comes
to predicting bike travel patterns and behaviors. There are, however, some limitations and
therefore opportunities for future work. This section explores potential opportunities to
improve the predictions further as well as incorporate a different dimension of information.
The models that have been explored so far are parametric models, NNs, and LSTMs.
One avenue of future work can be exploring different models such as the Transformer
model. Transformers are self-attention mechanisms and do not rely on recurrent connec-
tions and allow for more parallelization, which is interesting, especially for multivariate
prediction.[88]
A logical first step with the model in hand is to quantify the performance of the model
by multiplying the proportional departure prediction with real numbers of daily travel
demand and assessing the discrepancies in predictions during peak hours. To improve the
approach in regard to bike sharing and how bikes can be properly reallocated, a thing
worth trying in future research would be proportioning the multivariate data so that it
retains information about proportions between the zones at each hour of the day. That
is, instead of each zone being proportioned, adding up to one, the whole area would be
proportioned and the sum of all the zone data would add up to one. There is, however,
the risk that this would leave the data too sparse and the values too small, with the
data already being small scale. If this would be fruitful it would, however, capture the
relationships between the zones even better, and allow for suﬀicient bike reallocation for
bike-sharing services.
As of now, the data provided is the Hövding data. It would, however, be of interest to try
training the model with bike-sharing data. By exploring the data in a different domain
valuable insights might be acquired even though others could be lost. Following that, it
might be valuable to re-try using contextual data, or even, adding different aspects of
contextual data. Traﬀic data, accident reports, and such might for example be effective.
Another intriguing area to explore would be to use different domain knowledge. This could
include intentionally overestimating peak hours and underestimating low traﬀic hours. It
could also include introducing departure time choices. Departure time choice refers to the
decision-making process by which individuals or travelers select the specific time at which
they will begin a journey or activity.[89] This would allow for a more detailed recognition
of traﬀic behavior than the domain knowledge utilized in this thesis.
Additionally, focusing more on the NN models combined with the true value penalty
could be especially interesting. It would be intriguing to investigate whether enhancing
the domain knowledge aspect of NN could elevate their performance even closer to the level
of the more advanced models, such as LSTM. This would be highly desirable considering
that neural networks require significantly lower computing power. Lastly, it would be

Chapter 7
Conclusion
Travel demand predictions play a crucial role when it comes to the effectiveness of transport-
sharing systems.
Making successful predictions allows for optimized allocation of the
transportation resource. This approach ensures that customer service can be improved by
directing resources to the time or area of the anticipated demand. Travel demand indi-
cates potential transportation demands that could be fulfilled. However, the number of
users served can depend on the availability of the transportation resource. Consequently,
the actual demand can be underestimated.[1]
The goal of this thesis was to utilize bike travel demand data, from individual users that
own their own bikes, to predict patterns using machine learning models.
The private
bike travel demand data has been proportioned over 24-hour intervals. The data is then
aggregated onto a city-level, univariate, and then zone-level, multivariate. The hope is
that predicting the private bike travel patterns accurately can be utilized when making
decisions within bike-sharing systems. Additional contextual information, such as weather
and public holidays were used in an effort to refine predictions, giving a deeper insight
into the effects of these parameters on bike usage. To constrain the model during training
domain knowledge was incorporated. Domain knowledge refers to information about the
data in hand that is already acknowledged either through some other discovery process,
experience or by an expert in the specific domain. Two approaches to constraining the
model with domain knowledge were established; true value penalty and predicted value
penalty, both based on Ridge regression.
As stated earlier, this led to the following
questions:
• Can accurate predictions of private bike travel demand be achieved by determining
suitable machine learning approaches and deep learning architectures?
• Does the integration of contextual data into the model contribute to the enhancement
of private bike travel demand accuracy?
• Does the inclusion of domain knowledge within the model impact the improvement
of private bike travel demand accuracy?
By answering these questions and consequently improving the model predictions we allow
for better leveraging of the model to redistribute bikes within the bike-sharing system,
aiming to adequately meet the user base’s demands. This should translate into better
system utilization and customer satisfaction. The predictions could further be used for
traﬀic management and accident prevention.
Analyzing the predictions long-term and
more granular might lead to the identification of pressure points in bicycle highways. By

units and a learning rate of 10−3.
The resulting model was modified to incorporate
contextual data. The results from which were that the contextual data did not improve
the model predictions. The accuracy score dropped from 0.878 to 0.867 by incorporating
all selected contextual data and to 0.871 by incorporating only the most correlated feature.
The next thing to discuss is the benchmark model, the SARIMA models, and the NNs.
Applying domain knowledge to the SARIMA model, resulting in a SARIMAX model
improved the accuracy score by 12%. The NN constrained with the true value penalty of
λ = 0.75 improved the accuracy score by 2% from the unconstrained NN. The next step
was exploring if constraining the univariate model with domain knowledge would improve
the model’s performance. Using a constrained model with λ parameter value 0.25 and a
true value penalty term in the loss function gave the best average results. This improved
the univariate model from 0.873 to 0.875, or by 0.2%. An improvement of 0.2% is very
minor and the unconstrained model can be concluded to have the same performance as
a constrained model on the univariate level. Looking at the multivariate level, or zonal
predictions reveals that the optimal model based on performance was the zone-wise(ZW)
constrained model, using λ = 1.0 for the weight of the penalty term and true value penalty,
with an accuracy score of 0.776 on the aggregated level. The unconstrained multivariate
model yielded an accuracy score of 0.769, which means that the improvement is
1%.
Again, these improvements are slight at this level, but improvements nonetheless. What
is also interesting here is the constant improvement in performance with increased λ value.
The model achieved the highest accuracy score for zone 2, 0.806, and the lowest for zone
8, 0.710. Overall, the models improve their accuracy by constraining them with domain
knowledge, and ZW constraining scores higher for all λ parameter values than the AVG
constraining for both the predicted value penalty and the true value penalty.
Lastly,
comparing the predicted value penalty and the true value penalty the latter one performs
slightly better for both constraining methods and all λ parameters.
The more advanced models(uni- and multivariate) are gaining less from constraining than
the benchmark models(SARIMA and NN). What we call the true value penalty is always
either slightly or significantly outperforming the unconstrained model. Even though nu-
merical differences are sometimes marginal, it can not be considered a coincidence that the
true value penalty is continuously yielding the best performance across models. The com-
plexity of the models and their data density clearly affects how much the regularization
through constraining is helping. The univariate LSTM gains the least from the constrain-
ing methods, to the point that the performance is comparable to the unconstrained one,
here the variance over the training data is also very little. The benchmark and multivari-
ate model, however, show improvements. The SARIMAX performs significantly better
than the SARIMA. The NN model improves with both the predicted value penalty and
true value penalty, more so for the true value penalty. The multivariate model also shows
improvement. Again, for both penalty terms but more so for the true value penalty. The
hypothesis is that the benchmark models benefit from the constraining of domain knowl-
edge due to the models not being complex enough to grasp the patterns as well as LSTM
models on their own. The multivariate data suffers from the suburb zones having greater
variance in the training data than the central zones and the univariate, the zones higher
in variance over the training data seem to gain more from the constraining.

Appendix F
Overview of Notebooks
This chapter provides an overview of the different Jupyter notebooks and python scripts
that contain the source code used to generate the figures, plots, and tables upon which this
thesis is built. These notebooks serve as the foundation for the analysis and interpretation
of the research findings. By presenting this overview, we aim to provide transparency and
reproducibility.
However, the thesis itself is oriented towards the project being repro-
ducible with the progression and information presented.
Zones
Notebook ”1. Create Zones” includes acquiring geo-data for later matching each trip a
zone in Copenhagen, as well as plotting the bike lanes. It additionally produces a plot
illustrating the zones.
Exploratory Data Analysis
Notebook ”2. Exploratory Data Analysis” includes matching each trip to a zone as well
as finding the geographical area of each zone. It also produces plots where each trip has
been plotted onto each corresponding zone. It then continues to produce various temporal
plots with trip count information.
P- & T-Tests
Notebook ”P+T_tests” includes the implementation and analysis of the aggregated level
p and t-tests which conclude if there is a significant statistical difference in the means of
fixed hourly departures across the years of data collection.
Data Preparation
Notebook ”3. Data Preparation” takes the trip information and aggregates them by zone
and hours. It creates data rows of 0 values for when no trips are taken, to make sure that
there is a row for each hour in the time span on the data set. This notebook also includes
the addition of the weather and holiday data.
Parametric Model
Notebook ”SARIMAX-PropData” covers the time-series analysis of the training data using
PACF, and ACF and validates the parameter selection with residual analysis. Secondly, it
fits a parametric model(SARIMA) for the uni-and multivariate data using the proportioned
data and obtained parameters from iterative parameter search based on ACF and PACF
alog with seasonality observed. This notebook also conducts the SARIMAX model which
includes the domain knowledge for the univariate data.
Tuning & Tuning Analysis
Notebooks ”5.1 Hidden Unit Tuning”, ”5.2 LSTM Layer Tuning” and ”5.3 Learning Rate
Tuning”, cover the process of tuning the parameters for the univariate model. They use
the LSTM model class found in ”lstm.py” as well, which includes the actual model setup,
