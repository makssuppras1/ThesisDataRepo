Abstract
A supercontinuum is a wide continuous optical spectrum, which is gener-
ated from a spectrally narrow laser pulse, through the interaction of a wide
variaty of linear and non-linear processes. This process is known as Super-
continuum Generation (SCG), and we will in this thesis investigate how the
shot-to-shot noise can be reduced, at speciﬁc wavelengths, for a supercon-
tinuum generated by a picosecond pulse in a Photonic Crystal Fiber (PCF).
To do this we will go through some of the most important theory for SCG.
We will present the Generalized Nonlinear Schr¨odinger Equation (GNLSE),
PCF, diﬀerent linear and non-linear eﬀects, and a discussion of solitons and
solitonic eﬀects, which are in some sense the workhorse of SCG. It is then
explained how tapering can be used to enhance soliton trapping, and the
SCG eﬀects mutual interaction is summed up. The numerical fouth-order
Runge-Kutta interaction picture method (RK4IPM) is then applied to the
GNLSE, together with an adaptive step-size scheme and Graphics Process-
ing Unit (GPU) computing, to create a code for numerical modelling. This
code is then used to investigate, how a reduction of noise can be achieved at
speciﬁc wavelengths, using tapering to conﬁne solitons in balance between
spectral recoil and Soliton Self-Frequency Shift (SSFS), at the second Zero
Dispersion Wavelength (ZDW).

Introduction
SCG is a phenomenon, where linear and nonlinear eﬀects together in sym-
phony creates a broad continuos spectrum from a narrow band light source
[3]. First discovered around 1970 [1], the ﬁeld of SCG is today a mature
technology, with most processes explained, and a wide variety of practical
applications [3].
Among these applications is Optical Coherence Tomog-
raphy (OCT), where the axial resolution scales inversely with the optical
bandwidth.
For traditional OCT based on superluminescent diode light
sources this leads to a resolution of 10 −15µm, where the resolution for
broadband sources can go as low as 1 −5µm [21]. A low amplitude noise of
the pulse is of great importance, as it is the limiting factor in OCT sensi-
tivity. In practice image scattering of the relative transparent tissues of the
eye are done using wavelengths in the 800nm range, and 1300nm are used
for tissues other than the eye [21]. And these wavelengths are therefore of
special interest, when noise reduction is considered.
Outline
The aim of this thesis is to get an insight into the noise properties of tapered
ﬁbers, with picosecond generated supercontinuum. The starting point of
this project was to develop a code, using a previous code as a template.
The results from this code was then to be matched, with those from the
simultanous bachelor project carried out by Mikkel Jensen for veriﬁcation.
With the code veriﬁed the code was then to be used to investigate the
noise properties, of picosecond pumped supercontinuum lasers, with tapered
ﬁbers.
More speciﬁcally we were interrested in the investigation of how
tapering of ﬁbers could be used to reduce noise, at speciﬁc wavelengths,
with main interrest in the wavelengths 800nm and 1300nm.

Supercontinuum Generation Theory
A supercontinuum can be generated by propagating a narrow pulse through
a non-linear ﬁber. The propagation of Electromagnetic (EM) waves are gov-
erned by Maxwell’s equations, and depends entirely on the material which it
propagates in. This is due to the light-matter interaction, where the EM ﬁeld
interacts with the atoms of the material, which leads to a material response.
The material response of pulse propagation through an optical ﬁber, can
be divided into linear and non-linear response. Linear response is loss and
dispersion, where loss is a combination of diﬀerent scattering and absorption
eﬀects, and dispersion is a consequence of the frequency dependant refrac-
tive index, which makes light at diﬀerent wavelengths travel at diﬀerent
velocities. The non-linear material response only become observable, when
the intensity is high enough. The two most important non-linear eﬀects are
the intensity-dependant refractive index, also known as the Kerr eﬀect, and
the photon-phonon interaction, known as the Raman eﬀect, where photon
of the pulse interacts with the material to create phonon and a red-shifted
photon [2] [1]. These eﬀects form the basis of many spectral broadening
mechanisms, which we will investigate in this report. In this chapter we
start in 4.1 by introducing the GNLSE, which we will later use to model
the pulse propagation through an optical ﬁber. In 4.2 we will introduce
the PCF, and brieﬂy describe the guiding properties of this ﬁber. We will
then discuss the linear eﬀects, starting in 4.3, where the loss of our PCF
will be discussed, and a model for loss, which we will use in our numeri-
cal modelling, will be presented. In 4.4 we will give a brief introduction to
linear dispersion, and present the dispersion proﬁles for our PCF. Then we
will move on to the non-linear eﬀects, starting with the intensity dependent
refractive index, known as the Kerr eﬀect, which will be presented together
with Cross Phase Modulation (XPM) and Self Phase Modulation (SPM) in
4.5. In 4.6 the Raman eﬀect will be presented, and a simple model, for the
Raman response will be introduced. We will then move on to the workhorse
of SCG solitons and solitonic eﬀects, which will be presented in 4.8. This
will be followed by a brief run-through of the most important SCF eﬀects
from ﬁber tapering in 4.9.
And ﬁnally we will discuss how these eﬀects
together create the supercontinuum in 4.10.

optical ﬁber and modelling of the noise.

4.8.3
Spectral Recoil
Solitons losing energy to dispersive waves generated in the normal disper-
sion regime, will recoil into the anomalous regime.
This eﬀect is known
as spectral recoil [12].
This eﬀect is caused by the soliton losing energy
slowly with increasing propagation distance, z, transferring it to the disper-
sive wave. Due to overall conservation of photon energy, the soliton gets
frequency shifted in the opposite direction of the generated dispersive wave.
For a soliton undergoing a red-shift towards a higher ZDW, due to SSFS,
the frequency of dispersive wave generation approach the central wavelength
of the soliton, as the soliton move closer to the higher ZDW. The energy
exchange between the soliton and the dispersive wave will reach maximum,
when it is transferred from the central part of the soliton spectrum. This
radiation will grow exponentially, as the soliton is red-shifted, but saturate,
as increasing recoil shifts the soliton back towards the blue spectrum. This
leads to a balance between the SSFS and spectral recoil, creating a frequency
conﬁned soliton [13].
4.8.4
Soliton Trapping
Soliton trapping can occur across an ZDW, where an optical pulse in the
normal dispersion regime, is trapped by a soliton in the anomalous disper-
sion regime. The trapped pulse will be shifted to satisfy the GV-matching
resulting co-propagation of soliton and trapped pulse [14]. As the trapped
wave in the normal dispersion regime is blue-shifted, it will be slowed down.
For the soliton, the Raman red-shift moves the soliton to longer wavelengths,
which in the anomalous dispersion regime will decrease the GV, leading to a
continuous trapping of the dispersive wave. For propagation lengths longer
than z > 1.2m interpulse FWM kicks in, and the blue peaks are dragged
further to the blue and the soliton is red-shifted further [15]. To demon-
strate soliton trapping we have in Fig. 10 plotted the spectrogram of the
ﬁeld at L = 1.5m for a single seed calculation of a 10kW sech pulse, with
a pulse width TF W HM = 10ps. This pulse has propagated in a ﬁber with
relative hole size d/Λ = 0.52, and pitch, Λ = 3.3µm. It can be seen that at
approximately λ = 2010nm four solitons are propagating at diﬀerent time
delays, and that four corresponding dispersive waves are propagating at ap-
proximately same time delays, at λ = 550nm. Further the GV proﬁle of the
ﬁber, which has been plotted below, shows that these four soliton-dispersive
wave couples are travelling at same GV.

5.2
Grid
So far we have treated ω0 as the expansion frequency of the calculation
domain, and as the center frequency of the pulse. This is also the approach
in both [3] and [1]. However, using this deﬁnition does not always give a
usable computation domain, which can easily be shown, also is the case for
28
==PAGE:30==
our computations. If we require the frequencies to be positive, one can ﬁnd
that
ω0 = ωmax −ωmin
2
⇒ωmax < 2ω0.
(46)
For our computations our intention is to pump a ﬁber, with a pulse centered
at the ZDW for a PCF with relative hole size d/Λ = 0.52 and pitch Λ =
3.3µm, which has ZDW at 1064nm.
Using this as pump frequency and
the relation ωmax < 2ω0, one can ﬁnd that ωmax, would translate into a
minimum wavelength of approximately λmin = 532nm. As we will see later,
this wavelength is not suﬃciently low to contain our supercontinuum, and a
diﬀerent approach must be used. Another approach, which we have used in
our computations, is to deﬁne a minimum and maximum wavelength for the
computation domain λmin, and λmax respectively, and from these calculate
a maximum, ωmax, and minimum angular frequency , ωmin, and deﬁne the
expansion frequency , ω0, as the mean frequency, and use a separate angular
frequency for the pulse ωp. However, as the pulse angular frequency is no
longer the expansion frequency, the retarded frame for the GNLSE needs to
be redeﬁned so it moves at the group velocity at the pulse center frequency,
instead of the grid center frequency. This leads to a change in the dispersive
operator, which is now given as
ˆD = i [β(ω) −β0(ω0) −β1(ωp) [ω −ω0]] .
(47)
The beneﬁts of this approach is, that as these are selected as positive real
wavelengths, they will yield correspondingly real positive angular frequencies
ωmax and ωmin, and that it is more intuitive to choose a wavelength range,
which is limited to the wavelengths, for which the ﬁber characteristics are
known [26].

5.4
Adaptive step-size
To improve the accuracy of our computation, we have applied an adaptive
step-size. Adaptive step-size schemes are widely used for numerical diﬀer-
ential equation solvers, and we will in our project adapt the “local-error
method” [17]. As it can be seen from the ﬂowchart in Fig. 13, the propa-
gation loop starts with the calculation of a ﬁne and a coarse step solution.
The ﬁne solution is calculated by two ﬁne steps of h/2, and the coarse step
is a single step with step size h. From these two solutions a relative local
error is calculated as
δ = ||Af −Ac||
||Af||
,
(51)
where ||Af|| =
qR
|Af(t)|2dt. This local error is then used to adjust the
step-size in four diﬀerent cases according to a deﬁned local error goal δG.
1) If δ is in the range 1/2δG to δG the step size is kept, and the solution is
accepted. 2) If δ > 2δG the step-size is halved and the solution is discarded.
3) If δ is in the range δG to 2δG the step size is divided by 21/3, and the
solution is accepted. 4) If δ < 1/2δG the step-size is multiplied by 21/3, and
the solution is accepted. For the accepted solutions the propagated step is
calculated as a weighted linear combination of the ﬁne and coarse solution
A(t) = 4/3Af(t) −1/3Ac(t),
(52)
which yields a fourth-order accurate result [17]. The drawback of using this
adaptive step-size is that the computation time is aﬀected by an increasing
number of Fourier transforms, which leads to an increased computation time.
However compared to other adaptive step-size schemes this method is best
performing for modeling solitons, which is central in SCG.

since the data amount for the individual calculations in this project have

temporarily stored in the memory, and then in the end of the code written to
the harddrive, saving writing time. This is in some way a small optimization,

5.5.1
GPU computing
Using the RK4IPM the computation time is linear dependent on the number
of Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) used in the computation [9]. To optimize
the code it therefore makes sense to start by shorting down the time used for
each individual FFT operation. The built-in Matlab function fft, supports
gpuArray variables, which is a part of Matlabs parallel computing toolbox,
which for cuda-enabled GPU allows one to utilize the computing power of
the GPU [22] [23]. Utilization of GPU computing in Matlab is fairly simple.
Using the built-in function
gpuArray(X)
the array X is sent to the memory of the GPU, and from then on processed
on the GPU. The array can be transfered back using
gather(X)
and further computations involving the array, is then carried out from the
CPU. Or in other words; fft is a Matlab function, which can be paral-
lelized, making it ideal for GPU computing. Parallel computing is in many
cases ideal for speeding up a computation, but a regular Central Processing
Unit (CPU) has in the range of 2 −8 cores, where a GPU has hundreds
to thousands of cores. As an example my personal computer has 8 cores,
and runs at a clock frequency of 4GHz. For comparison my GPU has 640
cores, which runs at a clock frequency of 1.2GHz, making the GPU far su-
perior for parallel computing. Further the GPU has a power consumption
of 60W compared to the processors 125W making the CPU computing far
more costly for large scale computations. To quantify this a bit 4 diﬀerent
hardware setups has been compared for 1.5m ﬁbers, where the number of

5.6
Tapering
As the ﬁber is tapered, the changing ﬁber geometry leads to changing ﬁber
properties. The ﬁber therefore needs to change ﬁber properties during the
propagation.
This has in this project been done by applying a staircase
method, where the ﬁber properties are changed every time the ﬁber pitch
has changed by ∆Λ. The provided datasets for the ﬁber properties are cal-
culated for a discrete number of diﬀerent pitch values Λ. These datasets are
at best separated by ∆Λ = 0.1µm, which we have found insuﬃcient for the
staircase approach, we have applied to the tapering proﬁle update routine.
This means that we either need to calculate more datasets, or we need a
way to approximate the ﬁber properties for pitch values between those of
two neighboring datasets. This has been done using two-dimensional inter-
polation, where a ﬁtting matrix has been created for the diﬀerent param-
eters important to our calculations, β(λ), AEff(λ), nEff(λ) and αleak(λ).
The two-dimensional interpolation has been carried out using the build-in
Matlab function interp2.

The RIN is measured as variance in diﬀerent wavelength bands ∆λ.

6.3
Changes in RIN due to tapering length
In 6.2 we investigated how tapering can be used to induce a low noise region
as solitons are conﬁned due to balancing SSFS and spectral recoil. This was
all done for a ﬁxed downtapering length of 1m. Using the same parameters,
but now keeping the tapering end pitch to Λt = 1.6µm, and varying the
tapering length from 1−3m, we will investigate, which changes there might
be from the increased tapering length. In Fig. 27 we have plotted a surface
density plot of the PSD and RIN in the same fashion as in Fig. 23 and 24,
but where the tapering length has been varied. Further we have plotted the
PSD and RIN proﬁles at tapering lengths 1m, 1.5m, 2m, 2.5m and 3m, aswell
as the integrated energy of the PSD below 500nm. Here we can see that as
the taper length is increased, the loss moves the red-edge of the spectrum
in as some of the longer wavelength dispersive waves die out. Further it can
be seen as the tapering length is increased, a larger part of the spectrum is
shifted to the blue-edge, which can be seen by the increased power density in
the sub 500nm region. It can also be seen that for longer tapering lengths,
the noise is increased at the blue-edge, but decreased in the red dispersive
wave region.

7
Discussion and Perspective
It is clear from the results in this report, that tapering can be used to tune
a noise reduction to speciﬁc wavelengths. This shows great potential for
utilization in OCT, where noise is essential for the accuracy. However, there
is still much more optimization to be done, before the eﬀect can be fully
utilized. A few immediate steps, which could have been taken given more
time, is proposed.
7.1
800nm noise
In this project no conclusive result was found for the noise reduction at
800nm. However, running the same computations in a better tapering res-
olution, might give a more conclusive answer, but in general it seems that
the noise reduction of the conﬁned solitons, are transferred better to the
dispersive waves beyond the second ZDW. And that a noise reduction at
800nm might require more optimization of the ﬁber properties.
7.2
Numerical optimization
GPU computing has great potential for improvements to the computation
time, and cost of numerical computations, but as the implementation in
this project was only a ﬁrst attempt, there are surely more optimization
to be done. In this project only the build-in Matlab functions for GPU
computing has been used, but calling mex functions and using the CUDA
based cuFFT, could maybe be next step in further improvements of the
computational speed. Further the optimal condition in this projects, was
found to be a multicore CPU running parallel jobs on two GPUs. As modern
stock motherboards can host 6-7 GPUs, with the limiting factor being the
number of pcie ports, more GPUs could be added to the system, for an
immediate performance improvement.
7.3
Loss
The model used to model loss in this report, is based on a measurement of
a ﬁber, in a narrow wavelength regime, at optical transmission wavelengths,
an ideal model including the conﬁnement ratio between silica and air could
be used instead. This could maybe change the loss properties at long wave-
lengths, and it might be worth investigating, if there could be found a better
model.

8
Conclusion
In 6.1 we saw that the wider the band, in which the noise was measured,
the lower the ﬂuctuations, and thereby noise. However, it was concluded,
that for physical eﬀects on a small length scale, a more narrow band could
be neccessary to investigate the noise. From 6.2 we saw, that using taper-
ing to include a second ZDW, the supercontinuum could be extended, and
that the conﬁnement of solitons approximately 50nm from second ZDW,
could lead to a reduction of the shot-to-shot noise. This reduction was also
found for the dispersive waves, in the normal dispersion regime beyond the
second ZDW, but could not conclusively be linked to a noise reduction in
the normal dispersion regime below the ﬁrst ZDW. The wavelength of the
noise reduction could be controlled, with a certain degree of precission by
controlling the tapering. The noise reduction was also found to be greater
away from the well deﬁned center of the pulse, i.e., the noise reduction, was
greater for longer wavelengths. From 6.3, where we instead of controlling
the tapering pitch, tried to vary the tapering length, we saw that the greater
the tapering length, the more power is transferred to the blue-edge of the
spectrum, and the red-edge is moved in due to the increasing accumulated
loss. Further we saw that the long tapering length increased the noise at the
blue-edge, but decreased the noise for the normal dispersion regime, beyond
the second ZDW.
Outlook
It is clear to see, that using tapers to reduce noise at speciﬁc wavelengths,
has great potential, and that the subject is surely worth looking into. Given
more time, it would have been interesting to investigate the noise reduction
for shorter wavelengths more thoroughly.
However, this would ﬁrst and
foremost require more seeds for the ensemble, and higher resolution of the
tapering proﬁle update scheme to ﬁnd anything conclusive.
