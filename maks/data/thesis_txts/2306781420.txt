ABSTRACT
4.	 Abstract
The thesis assesses the development in the Danish construction industry 
from a socio-technical perspective at a time when digital tools are 
implemented in an expectation to improve the stagnant performance of 
the construction industry. Consultants and contractors have identified the 
potential of digitalization and use of various ICT tools, but clients still seems 
reluctant in relation to demand for ICT in construction projects.
The objective of the project is to contribute constructively to the 
development of the construction industry. The report constitutes empirical 
data consisting of qualitative semi-structured interviews of ICT specialists 
and a representative quantitative survey among AlmenNets members, the 
majority of the Danish social housing organizations.
The thesis is designed with an initiating problem that examines and 
describes how ICT tools create value for clients generally. The challenges 
and opportunities in the construction industry are also examined in the 
light of technological developments. The problem lies among other things 
in the implied value creation that is not clear to clients. The social housing 
organizations are identified as a professional and relatively homogeneous 
client type, thus, selected to form the basis for further study. Next, the 
problem is specified to:
How are the challenges with digitalization evaluated by social housing 
organizations in a change management perspective?
The social housing organizations contribute by answering a survey with a 
response rate of 74%. The study shows not all clients live by the ICT-Act, but 
that there generally is a positive perception of ICT tools specified in the ICT-
Act. The social housing organizations will states lacking ICT skills, high initial 
costs for ICT, inadequate ICT-tools and change management being the 
essential barriers to the implementation of ICT. The driving forces related 
to the process, product and monetary value is not enough to overcome the 
barriers. Finally, I call to initiate a discussion of buildings importance and 
application in the future “Smart Cities” where production and recycling of 
resources are decentralized and undertaken in the city’s existing buildings. 
This leads to new requirements for management of the buildings and 
their installations, made possible by Industry 4.0 and BIM-based Facility 
Management systems.

INTRODUCTION
6.	 Introduction
This thesis is based on genuine problem in all industries. Implementation 
of digital systems and tools. The construction industry is no exception on 
the contrary, the construction industry lack behind when compared to the 
manufacturing industry where robotic technology and 3D design are the norm. 
The difference might be explained by the nature of the construction industry 
with ever changing temporary project organizations, locations and different 
educational backgrounds. Nevertheless, the industry suffer from stagnating 
productivity. Digitalization in general is appointed as a mean for optimization 
and increase of efficiency. The digitalization process has been ongoing for 
many years parallel with the technological evolution where process power 
doubles faster and faster. As of 2016, there is sufficient processor power to 
handle the extensive amount of data included in construction projects. Thus, 
technology is a minor barrier. There are different opportunities to approach 
digitalization in the construction industry: Optimization of a specific tool 
or process, development of new digital tools, case studies of projects with 
high degree of digitalization, evaluation of sociotechnical perspectives and 
system thinking of the industry. 
This thesis will evaluate the sociotechnical perspectives of digitalization 
of the Danish construction industry in a context of client organizations, 
more specifically the social housing organizations. Development of ICT 
competencies in client organizations are currently of high interest within the 
Danish construction industry. There are limited research on social housing 
organizations and client organizations in general, thus the thesis contribute 
to the documentation and perception of social housing organizations in 
Denmark. The empirical data acquisition consist of qualitative interviews 
and a quantitative survey.
6.1	
Objective
I have a background within civil engineering with a strong focus on BIM, 
digitalization and management throughout my education. My interest in 
digitalization was sharpened when I did my internship at the Danish ICT-
specialist company, Exigo Consult. During my time in Exigo, I worked with 
state-of-art ICT-tools for 4D and 5D simulations on real projects and I was 
part of a team developing mobile applications for quality assurance. During 
my internship, I became aware of the major opportunities for enhancing 
productivity that lies ahead for the construction industry. 
My motivation to write a thesis within this area is to be a part of the 
continuous development of the construction industry. Denmark is one of 
the leading countries to apply BIM (Malleson & Watson 2016) and there 
is still untapped potential in digitalization. The objective of the thesis 
is to contribute to the development of the Danish industry and push the

6.2	
Initial problem
Based on the development within digitalization of the construction industry 
the initial innovation and implementation of digital tools came from 
the architects and engineers who started to design in 3D using building 
information modelling (BIM). Recently the contractor have realized that 
there are significant commercial benefits by implementing digital tools for 
construction management purposes. What is remarkable is that there is 
limited interest and focus on digitalization on the client side. The clients 
does not utilize BIM or VDC tools themselves, but since the clients pays for 
the processes they should benefit from the digital processes. Client benefits 
could be better and cheaper projects with lower risks, better decision-making 
and increased collaboration. There seems to be some unidentified barriers 
that prevent the construction client to make digitalization a matter of course 
and manifest an untapped potential for better construction projects. 
This thesis is written with co-supervisor Rolf Simonsen from The Danish 
Association of Construction Clients (DACC). The research is done in 
continuation of DACCs Digitalization Committees strategy for 2016-17 
where three focus areas are presented (Aagren et al. 2016). It is relevant 
for DACC to get a better understanding of the reluctant implementation 
or application of digitalization by the construction clients in the Danish 
construction industry. During the thesis process, AlmenNet has played an 
important role and shown great interest in the thesis as well. 
6.3	
Limitations
Doing research on construction clients, you can go two ways. Either 
construction or operation and maintenance of buildings. This thesis 
consider the construction process from programming through design 
and tender to construction of the building. The construction process was 
chosen from the rationale that the construction clients must be able to 
define their requirements in the programming phase before they are able

to reap the benefits in the operation phase. Construction and operation 
and maintenance of buildings is fundamentally different in their nature. The 
construction process is within a defined period and include many different 
stakeholders with different objectives where the operation phase is an

people are engaged in the daily FM routines. It is not possible to cover both

them very interesting and relevant. 
Along the way, some limited and overall descriptions of concepts like value

METHOD
7.	 Method
This section cover the considerations related to thesis design and 
methodological approach to the empirical data acquisition. Articulating 
the thesis design principles prepare the reader for the structure of the 
thesis. Knowing the structure makes it easier to follow the argumentation 
throughout the thesis (Reinecker & Jørgensen 2004). The methodological 
approach is described to enable the reader to duplicate the data collection 
and come up with identical results. There is no distinction made between 
considerations made initially and during the thesis process.
7.1	
Thesis design
Articulation of thesis design has the purpose to create a clear framework for 
the research and the content within the report. There are several methods 
to design a research. Andersson & Borgbrandt (1998) introduce four types 
of research: change oriented, evaluating, theory and modeling as well as 
testing. Change oriented research has the purpose to study or contribute 
to changes in processes. Evaluating research has the purpose to describe 
objectively a phenomenon after which needs and opportunities for changes 
are analyzed. Theory and modeling research focus to enhance knowledge 
within a highly specialized field. The fourth research type, testing, has the 
purpose to test, investigate and explain methods or functions through 
organized experiments.
 
This thesis is characterized as an evaluating research because it strives 
to clarify the client value creation by digitalization and ICT tools after 
which needs for change and recommendations are presented. In Table 
1 by Andersson & Borgbrandt (1998) the standard research process for 
evaluating research is described. This thesis follows the described standard 
process and apply both interviews and surveys for data acquisition. Note 
that the thesis is structured with an initial problem, which are being studied 
to identify the core problems of the topic before specifying the problem 
statement to investigate and evaluate the issues given by the specified 
problem statement. The results might as well give more insight into the 
current change process within digitalization of the construction industry.

7.2	
Qualitative methods - Interviews
The digitalization process in the construction industry has both a 
technological and societal history that is important to understand in order 
to answer the problem statement. Qualitative methods have been adopted 
to establish an understanding of the past development and the current 
digitalization situation in the Danish construction industry. 
Qualitative methods offer many choices of technique i.e. participant 
observation, case studies, action research, focus groups, interviews etc. The 
qualitative interview was chosen to collect information on digitalization 
in the industry. Kvale (1997) characterize the interview as suitable for 
investigating people’s understanding of the meanings in their lifeworld, 
describe their experiences and personal insights and clarify and deepen 
their own perspective of their lifeworld. The respondents was chosen to be 
specialists within digitalization in the Danish construction industry covering 
different organizations from public clients to leading contractors.

Prior to the qualitative study, literature on design of qualitative research was 
consulted. For this research, Kvales guide to a qualitative research interview 
and Yin’s “Qualitative research from start to finish” have been applied.

Design of interview guides
The interviews were designed as semi-structured qualitative interviews where 
interview guides were developed to encourage to an open dialogue within 
a relaxed atmosphere. This structure allows theoretically the respondent to 
elaborate on topics outside of the interviewers’ awareness. The definition 
of research questions follows an iterative and recursive pattern allowing 
the study to evolve throughout the study. The interviews were recorded as 
audio files when possible. During the interviews, notes were taken and later 
detailed further via the audio files. The interview guides are attached in 
Appendix 1 to 5 including notes from the interviews. The interview language 
and notes are done in Danish for the ease of the respondents and to 
accommodate the business language. 
Selecting respondents
Alvesson(2011) describes two principles applying when choosing the 
respondents. The first principle, representativeness, emphasize breadth and 
variation among the respondents so that they are able to cover the category 
one aims to address. Here a holistic coverage and avoidance of bias in the 
respondents studied is vital. The other principle is to aim for quality with the 
interview respondents. The ambition is then to select or pay considerable 
attention to what is being assessed to be rich, perceptive and insightful 
accounts. Within this principle, one is less worried about representation and 
bias and are more eager to use the resources. 
Selecting respondents was initially a combination of both principles where 
10-12 quality respondents were contacted to obtain representativeness 
in the gathered data. Five interviews were conducted and hereby the 
representativeness is questioned due to the limited number of respondents.

In return, the respondents are very qualified with significant engagement

THEORETICAL CONCEPTS
9.	 Theoretical concepts
This section cover theoretical concepts that will be used in the thesis both 
to describe challenges within the construction process and to establish a 
common understanding of the intangible term, value.
9.1	
Defining problems
To characterize a problem we can either define it as tame or wicked problems. 
Tame problems are those that are amenable to mathematical modelling in 
some way or another because the bounds of a problem can be set and the 
solution is rule-bound. A tame problem in a construction process could be 
structural design of a specific beam. In this example, the geometry is locked 
and the design is bound by the building codes. Whereas wicked problems 
could be the entire design process of a building. The people included in the 
process determine the process and the design is subjectively evaluated. No 
rules apply for the designers in the process and a construction program 
can result in many different proposals of the given task. Not a single 
solution exists and wicked problems are often characterized by iterative 
problem solving. Construction projects become more and more complex 
due to innovative designs, new materials and new focus areas such as 
sustainability. The purpose of digitalization is to minimize the wickedness of 
the construction process by applying structure and transparent information 
flows in the project coalition.  
9.2	
Uncertainties
Construction projects is recognized by a high degree of uncertainty. Winch 
(2010) define uncertainty as “the absence of information required for the 
decision that needs to be taken at the point of time”. Reasons for the missing 
information is exhaustive and here it will be treated in general terms. This 
section will describe the pitfalls in decision-making and establish a common 
terminology of problem definitions, risk concept and information flows as 
well as a description of the concept of value.
An interesting feature of the dynamic uncertainty in the project lifecycle 
is that problems shift from being wicked to being tame as the amount of 
information becomes available and the problem definition becomes more 
precise. This phenomenon is highly relevant in the relationship between the 
client and the designers as the client rely on the designers to turn ideas and 
needs into the desired asset given the technical and regulatory constraints. 
The designers also rely on the client to have fully articulated the project 
mission and that the client is aware of its own needs and opportunities. 
One cannot select an option, which they do not know exists. The Johari 
Window, Figure 1, capture this problem, known as the briefing problem.

9.3	
Value concept
One of the principal ways in which modern societies generate new value is 
through projects which create physical assets that can then be exploited to 
achieve social and economic ends (Winch 2010). Nevertheless, the concept 
of value is rather intangible and theories on value has been subject of 
several studies. This thesis has different purpose thus the value concept will 
be treated in a general manner. The value concept is described according to 
Graham Winch, Søren Wandahl and Mikael Thyssen to establish a common 
terminology and understanding of the value concept that is applied in this 
thesis. Value is context and time dependent(Thyssen 2015) and within this 
research value is addressed in the context of construction clients working 
within construction projects. This section will introduce the general value 
concept and serve as a basis for an identification of values from ICT-tools in 
the construction process. 
Winch (2010) explains the development of value perception from Adam 
Smith’s general distinction between “value in use” to “value in exchange” 
to the development of neoclassical economics with emphasis on price 
formation through equalization of price and demand. Adam Smith’s concepts 
are exemplified by water, which has no value in exchange, but considerable 
value in use while a diamond has no value in use but considerable value 
in exchange. Neoclassical economics does not have the same distinction 
between exchange and use values because utility (value) was determined by 
what the market was prepared to pay to satisfy its wants. These economical

10.1	 Industry characteristics
A construction industry can be characterized as an 
engineering system with a high degree of complexity. The 
construction industry has a high complexity due to ever-
changing projects coalitions, unique designs and on-site 
production resulting in a process vulnerable to human 
behavior and uncertainties. On the other hand, there is 
a relatively low technical complexity, since construction 
materials often are limited to concrete, steel, sand and 
glass. Furthermore, construction projects often have many 
stakeholders and low profitability on projects that can lead 
to adversarial relations between stakeholders. 
National business system
Winch (2010) introduce three basic types of advanced 
economy business systems: Anglo-Saxon, Corporatist and 
State-led. The Danish construction industry is shaped by 
the Danish national business system, a corporatist type system with more 
negotiated coordination between social partners, greater willingness to 
intervene in the market to protect social values, greater reliance on banks 
for industrial finance and relatively high levels on worker protection. The 
corporatist system is constituted in the tripartite system incorporating 
workers, employers and government.

Productivity challenges
The construction industry has long been accused for low productivity 
development compared to other industries and is often mentioned as a 
conservative industry that does not implement new technology or adapt 
new organizational structures or management concepts. It is very difficult to 
measure the productivity of construction projects and the author concur with 
BAT Kartellet (2010) and IDA (2014) saying that productivity has increased

technical stakeholders consist of architects, engineers, contractors and 
their suppliers. Furthermore, all construction projects must relate to the 
surroundings represented by authorities and neighbors. In the following, the 
main stakeholders, also known as the project coalition, marked in Figure 2,

Client
The client is the juridical person that initiate the construction project and 
typically the person who pays for the project. The client role is decision 
maker. The client has the final decision on what is built and is responsible 
for compliance of the authorities’ requirements in the finished building. The 
significant tasks for the client are:
• Programming: Define specifications for the building i.e. price, quality, 
design, size etc.
• Tender: Manage tender process and  contracting
• Attend decision processes
• Pay for the building
• Manage operation & maintenance
Client advisor
The client advisor is the clients’ impartial consultant in the building 
project. The client advisor bring experience and capabilities in regard to 
the construction process and make sure that the client has the relevant 
information to make the right decisions at the right time. Services can 
include guidance on contracting, tender, development of cost plans etc. 
Hence, the client advisor guide the client through the construction process.
Architect
The architect is responsible for design and project design of non-structural 
elements, which means everything else than what affect the structural 
stability of the building. This could be design of exterior and interior, selection 
of materials, surfaces, colors etc. Sustainability aspects and indoor climate 
have also become important aspects of the architects because the strict 
building code prescribe low energy houses where passive energy design has 
become increasing important. 
Engineer
The engineer perform the structural design of the building, determining the 
structural principle as well as designing the building services. The engineer 
determines the dimensions from calculations based on building codes, in 
Denmark Eurocodes and Danish Standards. Energy and sustainability 
calculations are of increasing interest and are typically the responsibility of 
the engineers. 
Contractor
The contractor is constructing the building based on the project material 
from engineers and architects. A contractor can undertake tasks in different 
organizational roles i.e. turnkey contractor, general contractor and trade 
contractor. The rate of responsibility is decreasing from turnkey contractor 
with responsibility for design and construction to the trade contractor where 
the contractor is responsible for specific trades.

10.3	 The construction process
All larger construction projects follow the same process with few adjustments 
according to procurement type. In Denmark, the “Phase model” describes 
the process that provide a common framework for collaboration in complex 
projects. The phase model, shown in Figure 3, will also serve as framework 
for the construction process in this thesis, as it is a convenient and well-
known process plan. The construction process is presumed known by the 
reader; however, each phase will be described briefly. Phases where the 
client has an active role is described more thorough than phases where the 
clients is less active.
Programming
In the programming phase, the client’s requirements for the building are 
defined. The “Idea” is a presentation of the general requirements such 
as areas, functionality etc. The “Idea” also contains an assessment of 
the economic conditions. The “Idea” is developed by the client eventually 
with help from the client advisor in a process where needs and visions are 
discussed.
 
With the construction program, the client requirements and needs are 
defined unambiguously so they can serve as basis for contracting. The 
construction program usually include:

•	
Specific regulatory requirements such as local plans and restrictive 
covenants.
•	
Existing connections to sewer, water, heating and electricity
•	
Preliminary examinations of geotechnical conditions and contamination
•	
Description of room requirements i.e. kitchen, shower, offices etc.
•	
Net areas to determine minimum area requirements
•	
Short description of contract type
•	
Requirements to operation and maintenance
•	
Rough schedule

A budget is also included in the programming phase. The budget start roughly

Design
Design is the process where programming is transferred from ideas, 
requirements and needs to become clear project material that allows 
the contractor to build the project. The design phase consist of different 
sub phases of different detailing levels. The final design is at a detail level 
where it serve as basis for procurement, contracting and construction. The 
subsequent detailing levels are not described here because of low relevance 
for the thesis.
Procurement
Procurement is undertaken before, during or after the design phase according 
to desired contracting type. With procurement based on the construction 
program a turnkey contract is typically selected because the contractor 
and his suppliers make the design. The turnkey contractor is responsible 
for the architectural and structural design as well as construction costs and 
management. Partnering is an alternative to the turnkey contract. With 
partnering the client team up with contractor, engineers and architects early 
in the process and jointly strive towards the best possible project within 
the given budget. Partnering include strong incentives for collaboration and 
often the profit is shared among the project coalition. 
Further detailed project design can serve as basis for other procurement 
methods such a general and trade contracts. The favored procurement 
method for the thesis client type is the turnkey contract. Trade and general 
contracts will not be elaborated further. 
Construction
During the construction phase, the physical asset is realized through a 
process where materials are assembled in a coordinated, planned and 
stringent order. The contractor turns construction materials into valuable 
assets. At the finalization of the construction project, a handover process is 
initiated. The contractor notifies the client of the completion. In the period 
from the notification of completion to the actual handover, the client review 
defects on the building that have to be repaired before the actual handover 
is completed. According to AB92 and ABT93, handover also include a 1-year 
review and a 5-year review.

Operation and maintenance
After the handover, the client takes on the full responsibility of the buildings 
operation and maintenance. O&M is based on the operating manuals 
delivered from the contractors. Operation cover the daily tasks necessary 
to maintain the functionality of the building as well as inspection of building 
services. Maintenance cover periodic maintenance to sustain a certain 
quality level.
10.4	 Client role and responsibilities
The client’s tasks are outlined in previous sections, but the responsibility of 
the client is still somehow vague. In general, the client mobilize a coalition of 
firms to deliver the project mission. The contractual relationships between 
the firms define the project organization structure and charter the project 
coalition. The client has important responsibilities towards the project 
coalition throughout the project lifecycle and is obliged to participate actively 
in the project management. Winch (2010) describe these responsibilities to 
act as:
•	
Promoter – Defining the need for the project and ensuring that it meets 
that need.
•	
Financier – Obtaining the capital required to finance the project.
•	
Decision-maker – Making those decisions required to push the project 
through the life cycle.
•	
Recruiter – Mobilizing the most appropriate and capable firms to realize 
the project.
These responsibilities are genuine and apply through the project lifecycle 
covering both the construction and operation processes. Digitalization and 
ICT-tools generally strive to improve communication and decision-making 
by mitigating some of the risks within the early phases of construction 
projects. Both communication and decision-making are highly dependent 
on the amount of information available at a given time.

THE DISRUPTIVE TRANSFORMATION IN CONSTRUCTION
11.	The disruptive transformation 
in construction
Many industries experience a disruptive transformation where traditional 
and conventional business models are being disrupted by new digital 
exponential business models. The exponential development occur within all 
technologies when they become information based. We all know Airbnb who 
became the largest provider in overnight accommodation in just 5 years 
and Uber who has achieved a market value of 40 billion dollars in 7-8 years 
now causing large discussions in the Danish taxi industry. 
11.1	 Disruption
Many industries fear to be disrupted as numerous new business models 
are invented every month. Traditional organization structures have evolved 
mainly to manage scarcity of people, money and assets/resources and the 
concept of ownership has proved to work well for scarcity. Accessing or 
sharing works better in an abundant, information-based world (Ismail et al. 
2014). What all exponential organizations (ExO) have in common is they all 
rely on information technology which is easy scalable. Flexibility is crucial 
and ExO’s prefer to rent assets, people and resources. 
The construction industry is like many other industries under a certain 
pressure due to the disruptive nature of the rapid technological 
development. The product, buildings, will continuously be constructed 
from bricks, steel, concrete, class and other known construction materials. 
Perhaps construction materials become more sustainable, but disruption 
of the construction industry is much more processes oriented. Construction 
has traditionally been an experience-based industry where skilled craftsmen 
have been the most recognized knowledge resources. The contractor turns 
construction materials into valuable assets in a process where materials 
are assembled in a coordinated, planned and stringent order. Over time 
construction complexity has increased and the high demand for buildings 
has put the industry under pressure to build faster, better and cheaper. 
Industrial manufacturing methods and philosophies such as modularization 
and Lean are implemented to increase efficiency and effectiveness. Applying 
processing power to planning and design processes give numerous new 
optimizing opportunities. However, smartphones, tablets and the increased 
availability of computer chips connected to the internet unleash completely 
new opportunities in construction and O&M.
 
Industries will be very much data driven and the vast amount of data will 
generate new opportunities for better decision-making, management, 
analysis and simulations. Construction clients must embrace the new

contractors with automatic quantity take-offs, simulations of construction 
processes and successive calculations based on the BIM model. The tools 
create an important transparency in complex projects and the 3D visual

assembled of many standardized construction materials and products. By 
increased standardization, pre-fabrication and lean-management principles 
such as Just-in-Time, construction can be moving away from laboriously 
created one-offs towards professionally manufactured products. Combining 
digitalization and modularization, it becomes very interesting as construction 
materials can be digital with barcodes or RFID-technology, which provides 
exact location of any building element and update the BIM model of the 
current construction progress. The RFID-technology is part of the fourth 
industrial revelation, named industry 4.0. The first industrial revolution 
improved efficiency by hydropower, the increasing use of steam power and 
the development of machine tools. The second brought electricity and mass 
production, and the third and most recent further accelerated automation 
using electronics and IT. By the fourth industrial revolution, physical objects 
are being seamlessly integrated into the information network. The Internet 
is combining with intelligent machines, systems production and processes 
to form a sophisticated network. The real world is turning into a huge 
information system (Blanchet et al. 2014).
Smart Cities
The vision of Smart Cities widens the scope of reflection into a system 
perspective. Buildings characterized as micro engineering systems are 
part of the built environment that is characterized as macro engineering 
system. The complexity increase from micro to macro engineering systems. 
Industry 4.0 is going to evolve and transform our cities to smart cities and 
change the way we live. There is still no recognized definition of a smart 
city by it is characterized as a city that has digital technology embedded 
across all city functions. We will see digitized mobility management with 
electric autonomous cars, digitized energy management and airborne 
drone delivery services. Everything is connected to the internet acting as 
one huge information system. Buildings must have a digital system i.e. an 
intelligent FM system based on the BIM model to interact in the smart city. 
Therefore, there are great potentials in digitalization of the construction 
industry optimizing the design- and construction process, creating data for 
intelligent FM-systems and preparing buildings to be part of the smart city.

The construction industry can improve its productivity but it is a traditional

14.	Social housing Organizations
This section introduce the social housing organizations in a historical context 
to explain the complex legal framework in which they operate. It explain 
how they through the years have been predecessor for technological leaps 
and initiatives. The present situation for the social housing organizations 
is analyzed within McKinseys’ 7S model to describe the social housing 
organizations by the seven elements and hereby perform a diagnosis of 
misalignments between the organizational elements.
14.1	 Historical context (KAB 2011)
Social housing in Denmark has existed since 1887, where the state enforced 
restricted loans for associations and municipal constructions. The political 
interest in housing was of low interest but through the years, the political 
system modelled the loans and social housing organizations emerged after 
the First World War. Liberal parties prioritized the private housing market 
thus the housing problem generally was perceived as a private cause, which 
must be solved through the free market forces. Therefore, the loan schemes 
had an insignificant effect on the housing market. 
Housing committee
Social democratic forces established in 1916 the housing committee, which 
created the foundation for the networks across different organizations that 
have affected the politics on housing and the position of social housing 
until today. The housing committee showed the complexity of the housing 
issues. Labor market policy, social policy, health policy, municipal economy, 
national economy, financial system, urban planning, architecture, material 
economy, technological development and politics are all part of the political 
housing issue. The numerous stakeholders results in a complex legal 
regulation. Finally, in 1947 the ministry of housing was established where 
it marked a change in mind-set for the housing issues. At that time, it was 
found necessary to apply some sort of command economy on housing to 
accommodate housing and employment needs. An active regulation of the 
housing market became a political project.
Housing shortage
After the Second World War, housing shortage was a latent problem. The 
later prime minister, Jens Otto Krag formed the post-war program “Future 
Denmark” where the housing shortage was termed as one of the most 
important responsibilities.  The problem should be solved with governmental 
management under the headline “More housing, better housing, cheaper 
housing”. The goal was 25-30.000 houses a year.

SOCIAL HOUSING ORGANIZATIONS
Social housing was a primary strategy with the purpose to increase the 
standard of living to help people of limited means and improve social 
equality. The strategy required lowered construction costs with an increase 
in quality. The recipe was chosen to be large-scale operation and effective 
production methods. With a clear strategy of state management, the social 
housing organizations was primarily considered for allowances of national 
financing. This mechanism consolidated the social housing organizations in 
the fifties to the seventies. 
Industrialization
In the meantime, the industrialization allowed new production methods and 
the social housing organizations was at that time recognized as having the 
most comprehensive construction experience in terms of efficient use of 
materials and human resources. Therefore, the social housing organizations 
took lead to increase efficiency of house building. Prefabricated concrete 
structures became the solution to the hungered need for housing and houses 
rose at impressive pace. It was a question of speed rather than quality. The 
standardized houses settled the housing problem with a significant increase 
in standard of living.
Up through the seventies and eighties private housing became popular and 
the social houses was questioned upon their quality and lack of social life. By 
decentralization of the decisions on housing requirements, the responsibility 
moved from the state to the municipalities. Parallel quality and social 
aspects became more and more important. The perceived role as provider 
for housing was changed to severe image problems where social housing 
was related to social problems. Poor quality of the buildings combined with 
low-income residents resulted in an adverse image. A balanced demography 
in the social houses became a political goal. 
Social role
To strengthen the social life in the social housings the tenants’ influence 
was strengthened. Tenant democracy gave more influence to the tenants 
in view of more competences on the local plan would result in larger 
responsibility and more political engagement. Today social housing has a 
strong empathy on their social responsibility and role in the welfare system. 
It is very apparent in the book “How to house – Architecture and welfare” 
(BL - Danish Social Housing 2014) where it is stated that the social housing 
model is built on principles like room for everyone, sustainability, innovation, 
community, societal development as well as contributing to public health. 
Reality is that housing has a minor role on the political agenda and the social 
housing organizations are far from the glorious strength they possessed 
in the sixties. Social housing are competing with private housing and are 
under pressure from the state to lower costs in a period where many of the

14.2	 Analysis of social housing organizations 
with McKinsey 7S model
Social housing is strictly regulated and based upon social values. Every 
aspect from the financing of new construction and renovation of the 
existing building stock, to the size of apartments and the activities the 
housing organization may engage in is regulated. As a generalization, the 
social housing organizations are analyzed as one within McKinsey’s 7S 
framework. The McKinsey 7S model highlights seven interrelated elements 
of organizations, which, when aligned, make an important contribution to 
organizational effectiveness (Pascale and Athos 1981, cited in Hayes 2014). 
McKinsey’s 7S model is used for diagnosis of misalignments between the 
elements thus; it can provide useful insights on the dynamics in social 
housing organizations. This section will generally describe the social housing 
organizations by the seven elements introduced in the model. 
Strategy: 
The strategy of social housing organizations is to provide affordable, sound 
housing for everyone also elderly and people with special needs. A balanced 
demography should decrease social inequity when resourceful tenants 
support tenants with fewer resources. The competitive advantage of social 
housing organization is that they provide more than just roof above your 
head. They provide a social community where good architecture, room for 
everyone and social life are the pillars of a community where a volunteer 
framework support tenant democracy. The tenant democracy allows 
everyone to take part in relevant decisions. They furthermore strive to 
develop smart construction through their renovation of existing buildings 
where cost-effective and sustainable solutions are pursued.
Structure: 
There is a high degree of tenant democracy in social housing organizations 
where the tenants have the opportunity to volunteer for the different 
positions in the tenant democracy. Figure 8 visualize the tenant democracy 
together with the organizational chart of a social housing organization. The 
arrows represent the directional path for decisions. The tenant democracy 
provide great influence for the tenants. Within the housing organization the 
administrative task are somewhat divided into departments i.e. financial 
and construction departments. According to Klaus Kramshøj, the employees 
support each other if necessary and therefore the departments are visualized 
in Figure 8 in one box representing the overlap of administrative functions 
within the organization.

Systems: 
Social housing organizations are non-profit organizations that allows 
no rewards other than a fair salary. The work in terms of annual reports 
are reviewed and approved at department meetings by tenants prior to 
municipal approval. Communication between tenants and the social housing 
organization is rather bureaucratic with long notice requirements.
The social housing organization work with Excel based IT-systems. They 
have advanced IT-systems for rent collection, tenant waiting lists etc. Some 
organizations benchmark performance on facility management.
Staff: 
The staff in social housing organizations consist primarily of employees with 
a technical background within craftsmen, architecture and engineering 
and employees with an administrative background such as accounting or 
economics.
Style:
The social housing organizations work in a highly regulated sector and 
everyone in the organization must obey the regulations. Punctuality and 
clear communication is very important on all levels in the organization.

Room for everyone, sustainability, innovation, community, societal

16.5	 Burning platform
The term “Burning Platform” emphasizes immediate and radical change 
due to dire circumstances. After more than three years with the ICT-Act, the 
social housing organization still have much to learn about the opportunities 
with ICT. The absent change in social housing organizations makes it 
inevitable not to question if there exist a burning platform for the social 
housing clients. Definitively the ICT-Act has not caused a radical change 
of the social housing organizations, rather an ongoing incremental change 
process. 
The respondents were asked if they experienced a political pressure for

governmental initiative that aims to decrease governmental funding from 
efficiency improvements in operations by 10%, corresponding to 1.5 billion 
kroners by 2020.

16.8	 Survey summary
Summarizing the findings from the survey, it has been found that the social 
housing organizations apply the ICT-tools enacted by the ICT-Act to some 
extent. However there is room for improvements since up to one third of 
the clients never ask for sharing of BIM models in IFC-format, classification 
of BIM models and clash detection which can be categorized as some of 
the fundamentals for a successful implementation of ICT on construction 
projects. The results reveal a change process where some social housing 
organizations are left behind where others are changing. This is the cold 
numbers despite of high recognition of the value creation of the ICT-tools 
within the ICT-Act. The reluctant change process must be explained by a 
set of barriers overruling the acknowledged value creation. The primary 
identified barriers differ from lack of ICT-competences and understanding 
of the value creation from ICT-tools to barriers related to new working 
procedures, high investment costs and poor ICT-tools. The barriers are 
identified three years after the institution of the ICT-Act and this can be 
interpreted as the social housing organizations are relative change averse 
since they have not overcome the barriers yet. 
The social housing organizations prefer to outsource the ICT-management 
as only 35% have an internal ICT-responsible employee. The preferred 
external consultants are the technical consultant (44-50%) followed by the 
independent ICT consultant (32-33%). The lack of internal BIM competencies 
leave the social housing clients in an blind spot (Johari window) where the 
engineers, designers and contractors understand the potentials of ICT but 
the clients does not. 
Despite a relative convincing positive evaluation of the value creation of 
the ICT-tools in the ICT-Act, many respondents demand more focus on the 
value creation in practice. A higher focus on employee development and 
education can increase the knowledge of BIM within the organizations. 
However, the ICT related value creation must be more explicit i.e. through

17.1	 Burning platform
Highlighted in the presentation of the survey results, the political burning 
platform has diminished with the political deal maintaining the social 
housing organizations in their current state until the next election in 
2018. In general the liberal parties are pro privately owned property thus 
against social housing and the socialistic parties generally are pro social 
housing. It is important that the social housing organizations maintain lean 
and competitive organizations if the political landscape change and their 
financial structure are reformed. They still have their competitive advantage 
by their emphatic and social values providing affordable housing and welfare 
for low costs. 
To maintain low rent the social housing organizations are in competition 
with other housing developers thus they must follow the development and 
optimize processes to obtain savings. The ICT tools enacted by the ICT-Act 
provide monetary, process and product value. To be more specific they can:
•	
Increase transparency and communication in the construction project 
with 3D models
•	
Increase quality due to coordinated project material
•	
Reuse data from the design phase in O&M
If the other ICT-tools are considered as well, they can:
•	
Mitigate risks by 4D and 5D tools.
•	
Reduce time for design and construction through better planning tools 
and automation
•	
Increase user involvement by visualizations
This is all known to anyone who have heard a BIM sales pitch. However, it is 
not enough to convince all the social housing organizations and to initiate a 
change process. As mentioned earlier some social housing organizations are 
implementing ICT tools and capabilities within their organization but there 
is more to come. The driving and restraining forces are visualized in Lewins 
force field, Figure 27. The restraining forces are still stronger than the driving 
forces for many social housing organizations as the current political burning 
platform is somewhat vague. To unfreeze the current situation and convince 
the remaining social housing organizations of the unexploited potentials of

17.2	 Boost motivation
To boost the motivation or increase the driving forces is possible with the 
concept of appreciative enquiry. Appreciative enquiry is a process that 
involves exploring the best of what is or has been and amplifying this best 
practice. It seeks to accentuate the positive rather than eliminate the 
negative. It focuses attention on what is good and working rather than 
what is wrong and not working (Hayes 2014). The remaining clients need to 
hear the positive examples of projects where ICT-tools have improved the 
process, product or increased the monetary values. However, when seeking 
the good examples, critics can always question the results due to the high 
degree of uncertainties in construction. One of the few organizations that 
give explicit examples on savings is the IT-specialist company, Exigo. Exigo 
act as independent ICT-consultant and provide services within 3D, 4D and 
5D BIM where they optimize Gantt charts by location-based scheduling (LBS) 
methods saving 10% of the construction period. They have developed a 
systematized clash detection process resulting in 20% savings in unexpected 
costs. Moreover, these numbers are based on real projects.
Obviously sales pitches are of no good convincing social housing 
organizations. According to the survey responses, they prefer to receive 
information on new development opportunities by demonstration projects.

17.3	 Break down barriers
The alternative to increase of the driving forces is to decrease the restraining 
forces to unfreeze the situation. Lacking competencies has proven to be 
one of the most frequently stated barriers to implementation of ICT-tools. 
Competencies is an interesting issue as the solution is straightforward: hire 
or educate. However, the underlying problematics are worth to discuss. 
New competences can be taught all life for the open-minded employees 
and age does not restrict employees to learn about ICT. However educating 
the existing staff require excess time away from the core business as well as 
the right educational program for the right price. 
More interesting is the solution to hire the right competencies. This 
problematics touch upon the educational system, new ICT-consultant roles 
and paradigm of in-house or out-sourcing. Starting with the educational 
system, their primary task is to educate qualified graduates for the labor 
market. The client role is quite interesting because there is not an education 
that educate construction clients. Construction client is something you 
become if you are going to build something or develop the competencies to 
hold such a position. Nevertheless ICT-specialists needs a different skillset 
that exist within the university educations. In Denmark, a master in building 
informatics is available at Aalborg University and other universities offer 
courses in BIM, ICT and change management. The new students have the 
opportunity to specialize within ICT in the construction industry. It is up to 
the companies to attract the new graduates and here the social housing 
organizations can face problems because of their non-profit politic limiting 
the salaries compared to other ICT job positions. Not attracting employees 
with ICT qualifications can force the social housing organizations to hire 
expensive external consultants. 
Outsourcing is typically applied for technical services such as engineering and 
architectural design but ICT becomes of increasing importance in all social 
housing organizations. To accommodate the disruptive transformation of 
the construction industry the social housing organizations can benefit from 
acquiring in-house competencies securing an appropriate ICT-management 
and development as the FM-process becomes digital and linked with the 
BIM model from the construction process. In-house ICT competencies allows 
the organization to act more proactive in a time of rapid technological 
development. In-house competencies are often cheaper in situations 
where the workload is sufficient for the job position. Smaller social housing 
organizations could share an ICT-responsible to be able to offer a full-time 
position.

17.4	 Strategic challenges
The Digital Construction (DA: Det Digitale Byggeri) was a governmental 
initiative resulting in formulation of the ICT-Acts enacted in 2013. By this 
initiative, the government forced consultants to apply ICT-tools on all public 
projects above 5 million kroners and 20 million for social housing projects. The 
consultants have traditionally run digitalization in the Danish construction 
industry but the recent years we have seen large contractors such as MT 
Højgaard and NCC take the lead as BIM and VDC has become a commercial 
driver for these companies. ICT is now perceived as a competitive edge that 
results in better and cheaper projects. So how should we proceed now? 
The ICT-Act has laid the foundation for digitalization of the construction 
industry, by defining the elementary aspects of ICT-tools. Denmark is 
among the world leaders in ICT in construction(Malleson & Watson 2016). 
However, what is the political vision for the Danish construction and real 
estate industry? The politicians say we need more houses. According to 
Copenhagen municipality’s Municipality Plan 2015, 45,000 new residential 
units must be built before 2027(Københavns Kommune 2015). However, 
how are the buildings going to interact in the Smart City where resources, 
energy, climate, mobility, infrastructure, financials, digitalization and society 
all form the future cities? How are we going to keep expanding cities and 
still decrease our energy consumption? How are we going to adapt the 
construction and real estate industries to the disruptive development? 
In my opinion the government has kick-started the digitalization of the 
construction industry with the ICT-act where interoperability, communication, 
contracting and hand-over forms the basis for object-based digitalization. 
From now and onwards the digital development should be commercial, 
as ICT has become a competitive edge for many organizations. It is still 
important with international and national organizations developing and 
sharing best practices because the smaller organizations might not have 
the resources to develop ICT competencies internally. Note that best 
practices tend to consolidate learnings instead of driving the development 
and thus the smaller organizations cannot drive the development. Among 
the interviewees, there were strong consensus on commercial development 
of ICT as well, with an emphasis on the necessity of open-source and 
international standards. 
With a pragmatic, some might argue dull, engineering perspective there 
are great potentials in modularization and industry 4.0 in context of the 
smart city. Modularization can optimize the construction process with solid 
standard solutions and eventually in the future automate the design process 
like Google Flux. Strategy consultants, Roland Berger assess Denmark to 
have great potential to utilize industry 4.0 assessed by and visualized in 
Figure 30.

rely on political stability. A long-term political vision and strategy for the real

energy and water generating and collecting assets. Photovoltaics, earth

18.	Conclusion 
Answering the specified problem statement the social housing organizations 
still are challenged by the requirements in the ICT-Act, as 25% of the 
questioned organizations never demand 3D models to be shared with IFC 
nor to apply classification of the BIM model. 30% rarely demand digital 
hand-over at the completion of the project. Tools closer to the social 
housing organizations responsibilities generally are implemented better in 
the construction process.
The social housing organizations are generally found to be change averse 
and the current driving forces for change are counterbalanced by the 
restraining forces resulting in little progress. Generally, the social housing 
organizations have a positive perception of value creation due to ICT-tools 
but their resources and competencies does not allow them to establish the 
in-house competencies within ICT thus the service is typically outsourced to 
the technical consultant.  
There is an increasing awareness of the untapped potentials given by ICT 
and the social housing organizations welcome new initiatives. They prefer 
receiving information by demonstration projects or education instead of 
developing internal competencies. Demonstration projects are assessed to 
be ideal to quantify and communicate the value creation of ICT even though 
there are challenges related to quantifying monetary, process and product 
value due to general uncertainties in the construction process.
Commercialization of BIM based FM can serve as a considerable driving 
force for the social housing organizations in the implementation of ICT. 
It is time to initiate a strategic discussion on the role of the construction 
and real estate industry in the context of industry 4.0 and the smart city. 
Agreeing on a common strategy does require time and effort, but it may 
provide security and incentives to utilize the potentials of the disruptive 
technological development to enable BIM based facility management. 
The thesis has contributed to the knowledge on social housing organizations 
in Denmark. The contribution primarily is constituted by the survey answered 
by a representative group of member in AlmenNet. The survey confirmed 
the expectations of AlmenNet thus did not bring radical results but valuable 
qualitative data. 
The qualitative interviews and other empirical studies are justified by 
establishing a comprehensive appreciation of the current trends and 
development of digitalization in the construction industry. The interviews

FURTHER INVESTIGATIONS
19.	Further investigations
Coherence between quality in BIM material and design 
quality 
As stated by Sara Asmussen experience indicates that there is a coherence 
between solid BIM material and high design quality. A statistical investigation 
of different projects done with BIM could clarify the proportions of the 
correlation and by combining list prices with actual construction costs on 
the projects.
COBie / BIM based FM
The COBie-format was described by many of the interviewees as an ideal 
format for FM-systems. As most of the existing FM-systems are excel-
based the BIM-data must be exported as such. It would be very interesting 
to investigate the utilization of COBie and explore the possibility for links 
between the BIM-model and the FM data.
Modularization / Industry 4.0
Modularization is an analogue optimization process inspired by the 
manufacturing industry reforming the construction process to a become 
leaner and systematized. Combining with the concepts in Industry 4.0, 
construction has the potential to become even more automated and 
systematized. Studies about RFID chips are already undertaken when the 
technology was immature. Chips have become cheaper, smaller and are now 
of a different commercal interest. I find it highly relevant to investigate the 
opportunities of Industry 4.0 to further develop the construction industry.  
Buildings importance and application in the Smart city
The “Smart City” concept is rather new, but introduce a terminology for a 
city with a high degree of interconnectedness between the city’s assets. 
Eletricity, heating, water and other resources will countinously be more scarce 
as population grows globally and we become more aware of sustainability. 
It will be very interesting to investigate how the building stock can take an 
active role in the “Smart City.
