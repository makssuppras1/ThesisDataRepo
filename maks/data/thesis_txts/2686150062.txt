Abstract
With the increasing global demand for energy and the resulting increase of climate problems, the focus
is shifting towards solutions where renewable energy sources are exploited at their maximum potential.
Therefore, the EU is aiming towards communities with high penetration of renewables with policies to
support it. Hence, PV-based Citizen Energy Communities (CEC) such as the one proposed in the present
work, will play an important role.
In this work, a study of the impact of a demand-side management strategy in a CEC has been carried
out. The proposed CEC, located in the depopulated municipality of Allueva, Spain, aims to develop
rural areas and tackle energy poverty. The CEC consists of 5 identical households with diﬀerent energy
consumptions habits. A ﬂexibility strategy based on the control of the heat pumps inside each house-
hold and their thermal capacitance has been conducted. This work investigates the eﬀect of diﬀerent
heat pump controller conﬁgurations, attending to load and/or price signal on diﬀerent indicators: PV
self-consumption, avoided emissions and monetary savings while maintaining thermal comfort.
Data scarcity pushed this work to arrange several interviews with Spanish companies like SomEnergia
and Lumio to obtain real household data. To compare the proposed controllers, this work built a base
case scenario with a thermostatic response where the heat pump consumption is added to the obtained
data.
The impact of these controllers is studied in detail with diverse methodologies that relay on standards
and other investigated literature cited in this work. It is revealed that most of the proposed controllers
presented improvements with respect to the base case. The most favourable controller regarding the men-
tioned indicators is the load controller (Net Load controller), with a 2.21% increase in the self-consumed
PV energy and a 1.15% increase in monetary savings, while maintaining thermal comfort.
This project also studies the Spanish legal framework regarding self-consumption and energy communities,
as well as the latest European directives. It is concluded that the energy community deﬁnition that better
ﬁts the Spanish legislation is the CEC and recommendations on how to improve the legal framework are
given, like implementing a dynamic sharing coeﬃcient and virtual net metering within the CEC .

Introduction
The power system has been traditionally scheduled for operation based on generation resources. This is
because most of its loads cannot be measured nor controlled at the desired time resolution. European
Technical System Operators (TSOs) are moving towards enhancing active participation in demand re-
sponse actions by consumers based on new pricing policies and the installation of smart meters, where
consumers can be accountable for their hourly consumption and choose the most suitable price tariﬀ.
Smart grid technologies allow consumers to schedule loads and therefore potentially reduce emissions,
helping the grid and making noticeable economic savings. However, expecting consumers to create an
optimal load schedule can be fanciful strategy. Automatic demand response action, on the other hand,
may result a more practical solution.
The main purpose of this work is to develop an automatic demand response action within energy com-
munities, based on diﬀerent inputs explained in sections below.
1.a
Motivation
Residential sector is a key factor in the actual energy policy, since the growing signiﬁcance of their energy
demand. Residential consumption in Spain during 2016 amounted to 18.3 % of the total energy con-
sumption, and around 30 % of the electricity consumption [6]. Similar values were observed in European
households, with a total consumption of 25.4% and an electric consumption of 29% [7]. This growing
demand lead to an increase in CO2 emissions, especially since the proliferation of all-electric houses. At
the end of 2019 in Spain, renewable energies represented 49.3 % of the installed capacity, producing 38.17
% of the total electricity, as illustrated in ﬁgure 1. Additionally, 58.6 % of the electricity generated was
free of CO2 emissions [1].
Figure 1: Generation structure for 2019. Source [1]
The current work also aims to tackle the depopulation and economic decline of rural areas in Spain.
One of the regions that suﬀers these consequences the most is Arag´on, located in the northeast of Spain.

The project developed in the current work is located in Allueva, a small village in the region of Arag´on.
This region is a strongly unbalanced territory, as 53.1 % of the GDP and 73.3 % of the population is
concentrated in the regional capital city of Zaragoza. This territorial imbalance is especially noticeable
in rural areas inside the region, like in the province Teruel, representing 10 % of the total population [8].
Allueva’s population in 2020 was 24 inhabitants, according to the National Statistical Institute (INE) [9].
Since 2017, the population of this village was reduced by 7 people and, as many areas around this region,
it is undergoing an exodus to nearby cities. Although the economy in that region is based on agriculture,
renewable energy projects are becoming more appealing to investors in this area, specially wind energy
projects. In 2017, Enel Green Power Espa˜na installed 167 MW of wind energy in the municipality of
Allueva, with an investment of 165 million EUR. One of the purposes of the current work is to encourage
more investment through renewable energies, and hence more economic interest in the area. Facts like
the forced telework due to the COVID pandemic, could make this environment even more attractive.
Electricity is generated in various power plants unknown to the ﬁnal consumer and has to travel long dis-
tances through transmission and distribution lines. Consequently, the energy sector is ruled by a limited
number of participants, with four main associated businesses: generation, transmission, distribution and
supply. Electricity travelling long distances also carries losses.
The centralization of the energy sector also reﬂects on the electricity price. During the ﬁrst half of 2020,
the European average was EUR 0.2126 per kWh. This price depends on a range of diﬀerent conditions
such as the level of supply and demand, energy mix, network costs, geopolitical situation, import diver-
siﬁcation, environmental protection costs or weather conditions, that accounts, on an average European
level, to approximately 60 % of the price. The remaining 40 % is accounted by taxes, levies and VAT.
The European country with the highest share of taxes in the electricity price during the ﬁrst half of 2020
was Denmark, with 66.4 %. In Spain these taxes represented 47.37 % [10].
Disruptive energy technologies such as photovoltaic (PV) systems are proposed as a counter measure
against this problem. Forecast on PV installations has been constantly surpassed over the years due to
innovation and incentives, ﬁgure 2. PV module prices fell by about 99.5% from 1975 to 2016 due to scale
economies, R&D and lowered proﬁt margins through increasing competition 2, making PV systems an
attractive mean for both investors and consumers to reduce their CO2 emissions as well as lowering their
electricity bill. Thus, it would relieve the consumers’ dependency on the grid, especially noticeable in
rural areas, by making them active participants in the energy sector.

Investing on large scale PV plants is usually out of reach for the general public, as big sums of money are
required. On the other hand, new alternatives like PV-based Citizen Energy Communities (CEC), where
price is shared between many consumers, are a more feasible method of involving the general public into
renewable energy technologies.
In this regard, PV-based CEC are bound to be, according to one of the main European strategies on Low-
Carbon society, one of the main actors in achieving the EU greenhouse gas emissions target by 2050 [11].
While a higher proportion of renewable disruptive energy technologies like PV in the low voltage grid
would aid to reduce energy losses due to transport and distribution, it could also impact grid stability,
power quality and the direction of the power ﬂow when a high share of that energy is being exported to
the grid.
PV-based CEC could be a good local solution to this problem. Controlling the load of the CEC would
reduce imports and exports of energy to the grid.
This would avoid other more costly options like
strengthening the current grid or making new local regulations to reduce the load. Economic savings by
the costumer perspective are also considered, as the CEC would be able to reduce their contracted power
to the grid operator.
1.b
Energy Community deﬁnition
Since the ﬁrst renewable energy communities appeared in Denmark in 1978, their deﬁnition has been
ambiguous, as it recognises diﬀerent scenarios and schemes around it (economic and legal model, disposi-
3
==PAGE:13==
tion, number of consumers). For instance, energy communities under the Clean Energy for All Europeans
legislative package (CEP), have diﬀerent deﬁnitions [12]:
• “Citizen Energy Communities (CEC)” – deﬁned in the IEMD (Recast Electricity Market Directive).
• “Renewable Energy Community (REC)” – deﬁned in the REDII (Renewable Energy Directive).
Both deﬁnitions have a common purpose, which is to provide environmental, economic or social commu-
nity beneﬁts for its shareholders or members, rather than ﬁnancial proﬁts. They both must be subject
to a legal framework and a set of criteria (ownership, governance, collaboration). Both deﬁnitions have
major commonalities: The requirement of a legal entity as a community umbrella, must be voluntary and
open, should be driven by thr common purpose above mentioned, require a speciﬁc governance, should
be collective actions.
Despite their commonalities, their major diﬀerential characteristics are:
• Geographic limitation. A CEC is not subject to geographic limitation, whereas REC requires
proximity (to be deﬁned by each national law). A CEC considers electricity consumption and pro-
duction with all kinds of energy sources, while REC is just open to sources of renewables producing
and consuming both heat and electricity [13].
• Participation. As for autonomy, a REC “should be capable of remaining autonomous from indi-
vidual members and other traditional market actors that participate in the community as members
or shareholders, or who cooperate through other means such as investment [14]”. A deﬁnition for
autonomy is not included for CECs; nevertheless, the decision-making actors should not participate
in any large-scale commercial activity within the energy sector. Therefore, participation in CEC is
limited to natural persons, local authorities and micro, small, medium or large enterprises whose
main economic activity is not related to the energy sector. On the other hand, RECs have a more re-
strictive participation: natural persons, SMEs (Small and Medium Enterprises) or local authorities
(including municipalities) whose main economic activity is not related to the energy sector [14].
• Eﬀective control. The eﬀective control also varies between CEC and REC. From a deﬁnition
of eﬀective control as the power over management decisions, RECs may exercise it by members
who are in the proximity of the renewable energy project. Each member state should decide this
proximity criteria. CECs are not limited by proximity, thus, IEMD restricts eﬀective control to
“natural persons, local authorities, including municipalities, or small enterprises.” [15]

Literature review
This section elaborates on previous works on scheduling load control strategies including heat pumps,

Figure 3: Thesis Scope
2.a
Scheduling loads
This subsection describes the main research that was considered when studying demand side management
strategies in both individual household and community.
2.a.1
Household level
Studies regarding the impact of the demand-side management control strategies like [16], considered a
ﬂexibility strategy based on the set-point modulation that depended on the energy price signal. This study
was carried out in a Mediterranean nZEB (nearly Zero Energy Building) residence with the objective of
providing monetary savings by shifting the heat pump utilization towards periods of lower prices. The
methodology followed in this cited work consisted in assumptions regarding the physics of the building,
the simulation boundaries based on the comfort criteria set by ISO 7730 [17], and the diﬀerent control
strategies performed with their corresponding simulation boundaries and accounting for day and night
schedules. The occupancy proﬁle estimated for the simulation period was equivalent to a typical week
according to the family habits. This work concluded that by up and down-modulating the heat pump
consumption according to the price signal, monetary savings were achieved at the expense of higher losses
and an increased electricity consumption. Therefore, authors of this work did not consider any energy
saving parameters.
Other studies regarding demand side management like [18], scheduled thermostatically controlled loads
based on forecasted price and consumption. The aim of the cited paper was to minimize the energy bill
subject to comfort constraints set by the consumers. Up until the date when this paper was published,
literature barely treated the comfort constraints inside the house, while this paper introduced a novel
appliance commitment algorithm with a detailed thermal model of an electric water heater that was nec-
essary in order to consider the comfort constraints of the consumers. Energy eﬃciency parameters were
not considered in this paper.
6
==PAGE:16==
The authors in research [19], studied diﬀerent residential energy management systems aiming to reduce
peak load demand while making monetary savings. Enhancements by the introduction of renewable en-
ergies as well as a storage system and a power sharing algorithm were concluded. The methodology used
in this research used predicted generation and consumption data, that fed the power sharing algorithm.
This work considered the electricity consumption on an hourly basis without accounting for the occupancy
schedules of the houses nor diﬀerent parameters that might aﬀect the ﬁnal energy consumption (outside
temperature or appliance modelling).
More complex control strategies like Economic Model Predictive Control (EMPC) were followed in [20].
The cited research used the operation of a heat pump inside a household combined with the thermal
capacitance of the building to add ﬂexibility to the consumption, aiming to achieve monetary savings.
This paper presented both the EMPC formulation, based on the thermal model, and the heat pump
modelling. The results of this paper concluded in monetary savings of around 25-35% depending on the
hardness of the EMPC constraints. However, this model did not account for any energy or environmental
considerations.
2.a.2
Community level
Other authors [21] proposed an optimal energy sharing algorithm in energy communities with PV pro-
duction. The model proposed in this paper applied the concept of energy community to the individual
prosumer, where prosumers are enabled to exchange their excessive PV production with other prosumers
by means of IoT (Internet of Things) devices and communication network. The algorithm set as an in-
put energy tariﬀs, energy consumption needs and energy production forecast. The model computes the
scheduling of electric loads and the amount of energy exchange to minimize the overall global energy cost
on a community level. In this research storage system units were considered as well as forecasted consump-
tion and PV production. The optimization problem was solved by the implementation of the Branch and
Bound algorithm provided by CPLEX library that provided signiﬁcant monetary savings with high com-
putational eﬀort. In this paper, schedulable loads did not consider the consumer comfort inside the house.
Regarding demand ﬂexibility modelling, the current work contacted some of the authors and partners of
a European project called FlexCoop [22]. Interviews with active participants in this project like professor
Peder Bacher from DTU and SomEnergia [23] (Spanish energy cooperative) were conducted. FlexCoop
project aims to introduce Distributed Energy Resource (DER) device models for residential consumers.
These DER models considered demand, storage, and generation.
On the demand side, this project
aimed to implement demand-side ﬂexibility, that would enable costumers to: consume energy when it’s
available and cheap, help stabilise the grid by matching energy demand to production, and increase self-
consumption. Demand models considered appliances with a signiﬁcantly big load with the capacity to
aﬀect building demand. Heating, Ventilation and Air Condition (HVAC) loads and lighting were consid-
ered. In order to stablish a DER model, these loads were modelled, and the energy production forecasted.
Speciﬁcally, heat pumps were modelled in accordance to the data sheet provided by the manufacturer, and
considering the inside and outside temperature and the output temperature of the heat pumps’ transfer
ﬂuid. A Model Predictive Controller (MPC) was used to model these loads with signiﬁcant monetary and
energy savings while maintaining thermal comfort. As a result from the interviews, FlexCoop shared the
results they had obtained in a research they had carried out in Spain. These results considered diﬀer-

2.b
Thermal model
Research on scheduling thermal appliances inside households considered both the thermal model inside
the household and the modelling of these thermal appliances. Samuel J. G. Cooper [25] presented a build-
ing thermal model based on the selection of the building parameters and the heat ﬂows in the building.
Building heat ﬂows were discretized from the steady-stated with the Euler approximation method.
Based on Cooper’s research, the thermal model proposed in [26], added more complexity by consider-
ing one more thermal capacitance. Hence, three diﬀerent thermal capacitances (thermal masses of the
building, indoor air, and heat emitters) were considered. This work used an electrical circuit analogy
(where temperature was analogous to voltage and heat ﬂow to the current) to better describe the ther-
mal model. This thermal model also included casual thermal gains based on an “active-occupancy“ model.
Other authors implemented diﬀerent, more complex models to describe their plant model for an EMPC
(Economic Model Predictive Controller) [27]. For a three-ﬂoor household subject to study, with plenty of
data available, PowerFlexHouse3, was used to estimate the plant model. In this thermal model, building
envelope temperatures, resistances and capacitances were considered. Building ﬂoors and envelope heat
dynamics were represented by stochastic diﬀerential equations that were fed into the EMPC to perform
a dynamic economic optimization with the objective of minimizing costs. The mentioned study account
for the thermal mass of the building to provide ﬂexible consumption.
2.c
Energy Communities and Self-consumption
Recent papers like [28] studied the proﬁtability of self-consumption solar PV systems under the latest
Spanish legislation depending on the number of household members and compared it with diﬀerent Eu-
ropean support policies. The paper concluded that with tthese European policies scaled to the Spanish
legislation, resulted in better proﬁtability in the studied European countries than in Spain. This conclu-
sion, however, did not consider the compensation tariﬀfor the exported energy to the grid, while it did
consider diﬀerent Fit-in tariﬀ(FIT) schemes of the other countries.
On the other hand, research [29] studied the proﬁtability of household PV self-consumption in Spain under
the recent legislation. It concluded that under the new regulation, grid-connected PV self-consumption
is cost-eﬀective for the average Spanish dwelling. It also suggested that a way to improve the economic
proﬁtability of the PV self-production project was to introduce a demand management by load-shifting
and collective self-consumption.
A recent research [30] provided a general comparison of the recent regulatory frameworks of 9 European
countries, including Spain. Although Spanish legislation is not the most favourable for energy communi-
ties, set ups like CEC as virtual network are a possibility. Energy cooperatives like SomEnergia, mentioned
earlier, was given as an example of these possible set-ups. The research concluded that collective self-
consumption laws are not enough to provide a robust legal framework for RECs and encouraged the EU

3
Methodology
One of the main purposes of this work is to study the possible technological potentials of the CEC. The
technological potential proposed by this work is to build an automatic demand response (DR) controller
aiming at maximising the PV self-consumption within the CEC. The goal of this DR controller is to
analyse how the scheduling of electrical heat pumps could beneﬁt maximising the PV self-consumption
within the CEC, always aspiring to beneﬁt the consumers and to reduce emissions.
The methodology consists of three parts. The ﬁrst one is related to how the data has been treated as well
as the assumptions made regarding the available data. The second part corresponds to the PV installation
sizing for the CEC and its annual generation, as well as the energy demand and how it was forecasted.
Part three describes the thermal model inside the houses. For PV sizing and its annual generation, tools
like the System Advisor Model (SAM) and Typical Meteorological Year (TMY) from the Photovoltaic
Geographical Information System [31] have been used; for the data treatment, Excel and MATLAB have
been used.
3.a
Data acquisition
This subsection details how the data was obtained from both demand and generation side, naming diﬀerent
companies that provided it and describing the methodologies followed to estimate the ﬁnal PV generation.
3.a.1
Demand side
During the ﬁrst steps of this work, data availability was limited. Lumio [32], a small enterprise from
Spain, provided 10 min data from PV production and consumption of one household in Madrid from
week 5 to 39 of 2019.
Due to the scarcity of data, during the ﬁrst week of October, another Spanish company was contacted.
SOMEnergia [23] provided two-year hourly consumption data from ten unknown and randomly chosen
households in Spain.
Consumption from these 10 houses varied from 8315.5 to 1704.4 kWh a year.
According to the Spanish TSO, Red Electrica de Espa˜na (REE) [6], residential electricity consumption
represents approximately 25 % of the total energy consumption. The energy consumed by a household
varies depending mainly on size, location and number of occupants, with an average consumption of
around 3272 kWh a year. For this reason, ﬁve out of ten houses were selected, with annual consumption
between 8315.5 and 3592.0 kWh a year. Resulting in an annual CEC electricity consumption of 23090.9
kWh.
3.a.2
Generation side
This subsection describes the sizing of the PV and inverter installation for the CEC. The sizing method-
ology for diﬀerent percentages of self-consumption is the same. As an example, the chosen amount of
self-consumption was 30 %. Solar resource data was obtained from Photovoltaic Geographical Information
System (PVGIS) and was generated using the TMY generator [31]. To estimate the PV production, a
free techno-economic software model from NREL called System Advisor Model (SAM) was used.

The amount of Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) received in Allueva, was the ﬁrst output from SAM
considered. GHI is the total solar radiation incident on a horizontal surface measured in W/m2, and will

Tilt and orientation of the PV installation
The PV system is assumed to be installed on a ﬂat ground area and therefore the tilt can be optimised
with respect to the latitude. The SAM parametric simulation was used to optimise the tilt of the PV,
resulting in a tilt of 36 ◦.
Losses
Since the PV system is installed in an area with no buildings or trees around it, the shadowing losses are
not considered. Self-shadowing is also not considered, since the area where the PV installation is set is
vast enough for arrays to be as separate as possible. Regarding electrical the losses of the system, default
values given by SAM are assumed:
• Module mismatch = 2%
• Diodes and connections = 0.5%
• DC wiring = 2%
• AC wiring = 1%
Losses like tracking error, nameplate, DC power optimizer loss and transformer losses were set to 0 %.
The average annual soiling loss was set to 5.7% as an average value [34]. Soiling losses refer to power

losses from snow, dust, dirt and other particles that cover the PV module. These losses vary depending
on location and environment. Soiling by dust can vary depending on the season: the Iberian Peninsula is
aﬀected by Saharas sand storms and this could raise the soiling losses up to 0.14 % a day during the dry
season [35].
Sizing of the PV array
As mentioned above, as an illustrative example, the PV installation was design to cover the 30 % of the
yearly consumption. Considering that level of self-consumption, it can be estimated that the PV system
should cover, as an average, 18.98 kWh/day.

The selected module is an AXITEC AC-370/72V. This module consist in 72 polycrystalline high eﬃciency

Number of panels in an array
In order to size the PV installation, there are some parameters that needed to be calculated. These
parameters are related to the amount of radiation received at the location and correction factors that are
also location dependent. Figure 5 illustrates, on a schematic way, the steps that were followed.

First, to calculate Hcorrected, the daily average horizontal insolation, HG, was multiplied by the Slant
coeﬃcient. The Slant coeﬃcient is a correction factor that accounts for the slant path scattered radiation
of the Sun and it is location dependent. The closest Slant coeﬃcient found in the literature [37] was for
the city of Sevilla, Spain with a tilt of 45◦and an orientation of 0◦. To calculate the normalised reference
yield, YR, Hcorrected has to be multiplied by the shading factor kh. As the system has no shading, these
shading factors were estimated to be 1 during the year. This decision could make the system slightly
undersized if in the following years new constructions are built around the installation.
The normalized reference yield was calculated by dividing the plane-of-array insolation by the reference
irradiance under standard conditions, 1 kWh/m2. Following corrected yields (YT and YA) are calculated

3.c
Forecasted data
As mentioned, one of the objectives of this work was to develop a controller that turns on and oﬀheat
pumps inside households within a CEC. This controller bases its actions in the near future energy con-
sumption and therefore, forecasting was necessary. The presented forecast methodology uses the processed
10-min data estimated in section 3.a.
The method used to forecast this data was a kind of recurrent neural network (RNN) known as Long
Short-Term Memory (LSTM). This method has been broadly used and it has been proven suitable for
the application of time series modelling and forecasting. Results, like the one shown in [40], prove a bet-
ter electric load forecast with reduced percentage errors than other traditional methods such as ARMA,
SARIMA, and ARMAX. Since this forecast method has been used in the controller simulation as an input,
a brief explanation on LSTM principles was highlighted.
LSTM adds or subtracts information to the cell state, regulated by three steps: input gate, output gate
and forget gate.
The ﬁrst step in a LSTM, is the decision of whether the new information input is useful or not. This
decision is made by a sigmoid function in a layer called forget gate layer. This gate takes ht−1 and xt as
inputs and outputs ft, which should be a value between 0 and 1, to the state cell Ct−1.
In the second step, the LSTM decides what new information is going to be stored in the cell state. This
decision is taken in two pieces: the ﬁrst one, called the input gate layer, is a sigmoid function, it, deciding
which of the two input values, ht−1 or xt, is going to be updated. The second is a hyperbolic tangent
function that creates an activation vector, Ct. Combining both, updates the cell state Ct−1 into the new
cell state Ct.
In the ﬁnal step the decision on the output is made. This output is based on a sigmoid function over the
inputs ht−1 and xt multiplied by the cell state Ct through a hyperbolic tangent function. An explanatory
ﬁgure for the three basic steps of a LSTM network can be found in the Appendix, 39.
This work aimed to forecast the NL proﬁle of each household inside the CEC for the next day. Since the
NL depends on both generation and consumption proﬁles, two diﬀerent strategies were followed:
20
==PAGE:30==
• Strategy 1: Forecasting CEC consumption and PV production separately and then subtract both,
likewise 12.
• Strategy 2: Forecasting NL directly from the result given in equation 12.
To decide which strategy should be followed, several test simulations were conducted aiming to reduce
the computational time. After these simulations, it was decided to select three days: two as train data
and the third one to be compared and validated with the predicted output and to follow the listed layer
speciﬁcations:
• Solver: SGDM
• MaxEpoch: 200
• Gradient threshold: 0.5
• Initial learn rate: 0.05
• Lear rate schedule: piecewise
• Num. of hidden units: 100
To forecast the values of future time-steps of the data sequence, the train-data must be divided into
predictors and responses. Both predictor and response sequences values are shifted by one time-step.
Therefore, the LSTM network learns how to predict the values of the next time step at each time-step.
To forecast the values of future time-steps of the data sequence, the “train data” must be divided into
predictors and responses. Both predictors and responses sequences values are shifted by one time-step.
Therefore, the LSTM network learns how to predict the values of the next time step at each time-step.
The chosen strategy was selected considering the one with the lowest Root Mean Square Error (RMSE).

• Heat losses are calculated assuming a steady state.
• There are no unheated spaces in the house.

4.a
Legal framework
This section describes the current legal framework regarding self-consumption in Spain. This work studies
diﬀerent schemes of self-consumption gathered in Spanish Royal Decree 244/2019 (RD 244/2019). Al-
though the RD 244/2019 considers other sources of generation, it is worth mentioning that this work only
studies PV installations for domestic self-consumption.
The RD 244/2019 describes self-consumption as the consumption, by one or various consumers, of elec-
tricity originated in generation sources that are close to and associated with consumption installations.
It diﬀerentiates two types of self-consumption: “self-consumption with and without surplus”. In the case
where surpluses are considered, prosumer can discharge the surpluses to the grid when their demand is
satisﬁed. Out of these two possible cases, the current work is focused on self-consumption with surpluses.
Within this case, the RD 244/2019 deﬁnes two diﬀerent cases:
• With economic compensation. Consumers can beneﬁt from this self-consumption scheme if they
fulﬁll certain requirements, like the prime energy source being renewable and the installation size is
below 100 kW.
• Without economic compensation. Consumers that do not fulﬁl one of the requirements mentioned
in RD 244/2019 Art. 4a.
Article 14 of the RD 244/2019 deﬁnes the Simpliﬁed Compensation Mechanism as a balance in
economic terms of the consumed energy during a speciﬁc billing period.
Two diﬀerent scenarios are
diﬀerentiated:
• Contract with a free retailer: Where both the hourly consumed electricity from the grid and the
hourly electricity surplus would have an agreed price between consumer and retailer.
• Contract with a reference retailer: Where the hourly consumed electricity from the grid would
be charged at the regulated price PVPC (Voluntary Price of Small Consumers) each hour. The
electricity surplus would be priced at the mean hourly price (Pmh) obtained from the daily and
intraday market at that exact time, minus the cost of deviation, CDSVh. The ﬁnal price can be
found in the transparency portal of REE (Spanish Electricity TSO - Red El´ectrica Espa˜nola) [51]
and it is updated every hour.
This balance mechanism states that in no case may the economic value of the hourly electricity surplus
be greater than the economic value of the hourly consumed electricity from the grid during the billing
period, which may not exceed one month.

On April 1, 2004 a new regulaltion that governned the new pricing policy came into eﬀect, the PVPC

This price is applied to small consumers with a contracted power below 10 kW. According to the Royal

• Default Tariﬀ- No hourly discrimination

• Electric Vehicle Tariﬀ. Hourly discrimination. This tariﬀestablishes 3 diﬀerent periods: peak

Communal self-consumption
Communal self-consumption, as deﬁned by RD 244/2019 is when a consumer participates in a group of

sources that are close to and associated with consumption installations. This provision also states that

is done within installations close to the interior grid.
Consumers can participate from communal self-consumption under the following requirements between
generation sources and consumers issued by 244/2019:
• Are connected through an interior grid to the generation source or directly connected.

ownership of both consumption and generation installations, the consumer and the owner of the generation
installation can be diﬀerent natural or juridical persons.

4.a.1
Energy Communities under the Spanish legislation
The legal structure around Energy Communities in Spain is yet to be made, despite certain aspects of the
EU Directive regarding these communities have been transponed to he Spanish legislation through RD
23/2020 (Royal Decree, law 23/2020 June 23rd).
According to the RD 23/2020, Sustainable Energy Communities are deﬁned as juridical entities based
on open and voluntary participation, as well as independent and eﬀectively controlled by members or
shareholders located near the renewable energy projects. These projects must be the property of such
juridical entities. The members or shareholders can be physical persons, SMEs or local authorities, in-
cluding municipalities. The main purpose of this Sustainable Energy Communities must be to provide
environmental, economic or social beneﬁts to the members or shareholders or to the local areas where
they operate, instead of ﬁnancial beneﬁts. Therefore, these communities are open to installations of any
kind of energy vector, i.e. thermal, electric, provided that they are coming from renewable sources. This
deﬁnition is based on European Directive 2019/244 [15], that urges Member States to provide a favourable
regulatory framework for these energy communities.

According to Art 17.5 of the RD 244/2019, the energy transfer from the generation sources to the self-

EUR for connections and generation points connected to the low voltage grid. Right now, there is an open

Study cases
The present work aims to develop, moved by the deﬁnitions of energy communities set forth by the
Clean Energy for All Europeans legislative package (CEP), an automatic demand response system within
a Citizen Energy Community (CEC), located behind the meter.
Three cases were studied based on
diﬀerent control strategies. Figure 18 illustrates a logical architecture, rather than a physical one, of the
CEC.
Figure 18: Logical architecture of the CEC
Houses in the CEC are geographically close, within the same land registry reference. The ﬁve houses

The objective of the automatic demand response (DR) system developed in this work is to turn on/oﬀthe
heat pump inside each household based on diﬀerent inputs. In order to better analyse how the automatic
DR system could potentially reduce emissions and electricity bills, three diﬀerent controllers have been
compared to a common thermostatic controller as the reference case. This comparison study can be seen
as a progressive study where each case is built on top of the previous one by adding a new input to the
system. These study cases are listed below and detailed later on this section.

• Net Load Controller (NL controller).
• Price Signal Controller.

The strategy followed by this work was:
• Simulate a thermostatic response inside each house form the CEC.
• Based on the thermostatic response, considered two diﬀerent inputs to add: Net Load from each

• Finally, applied the sharing algorithm to those three control strategies (NL, price signal, NL + price

4.b.1
Study case A: Thermostatic response controller
To simulate a thermostat, the only input parameter that was considered was the inside temperature. The
thermostat was built around a relay, that will switch output between two given constants, i.e thermal
comfort band. The output is 1 or 0, representing the on and oﬀof the heat pump respectively.
A relay allows its output to switch between two given values. When the relay is on, it will remain on
until the input value decreases under a certain value (the switch oﬀpoint). Similarly, when the relay is
oﬀ, it will remain oﬀuntil the switch on value is reached. A relay accepts one input and generates one
output. Heat pump would be on until it reaches the upper thermal comfort limit and oﬀwhen it reaches
the lower thermal comfort limit.
4.b.2
Study case B: Heat Pump Controller
As mentioned, each controller was built around the thermostatic response control. Three diﬀerent strate-
gies based on two inputs added to the thermostatic controller were considered:
• Net Load (NL) from individual houses. Based on their forecasted consumption and their share of
PV production, when the NL was below a certain value, the house would export electricity to the
grid, else it would import from the grid. This value was set to 100 W. To anticipate sudden changes
in the NL produced by intermittent PV production due to occasional shadowing, i.e clouds, this
added control strategy considered the sum of the NL for the next 30 min. Hence, it was considered
that when the sum of the next 3 time-steps is below 300 W, the household was exporting energy to
the grid. When this condition is fulﬁlled, the controller would send an on signal to the HP, else, the
household would be importing energy from the grid, and thus the controller would work as just a
thermostat.

This work also considered a sharing algorithm within the consumers on the CEC. This algorithm shares

with higher consumption. The method followed to build this algorithm is illustrated in the following
ﬁgure:

• Consider the NL that was calculated after the HP controller, NLHPcontroller
• Calculate, at every time-step, when and how much energy were the whole system (CEC) exporting

5
Results & Discussion
The following section addresses the results of 7 diﬀerent control strategies considering every price signal
and NL signal, under the scope of building thermal response, energy Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)
and economic results based on the actual Spanish pricing legislation.
Simulations were done during the heating season. This work assumed as a representative period for the
heating season the months of January and February since both temperature and sun hours were similar
during the heating season. Spanish heating season was assumed to be equivalent to the winter season
(from December to March). Data provided by the TMY tool suggested that during the assumed heating
period, temperatures vary between 16.9 and -7.37◦C.
Indoor temperatures were studied to verify that temperatures varied within the limits established by the
comfort limits and determine which model performed better in terms of thermal comfort.
The aim behind evaluating the energy KPIs was to demonstrate which controller contributed the most to
the CEC in terms of optimal energy consumption, which is evaluated by several energy parameters listed
in Section 5.b.
The economic results are a reﬂection of the energy KPIs. Monetary savings can be expected with an
increase in PV utilization.

5.c
Economic Results
In this subsection results for the economic study are given and discussed.
The economic study considered the 6 controllers and the 3 diﬀerent price tariﬀs. The parameters this
feasibility case took into account were:
• Costs related to the investment of the installation: PV panels, BOS, and labour force. Balance
of System, BOS, costs include every element of a PV system but the PV panels, i.e.
wiring,
switches, mounting system and inverters. This work did not consider other additional equipment like
measurement components, solar trackers, sensors, or any other equipment to enhance its production.
• Self-consumption: Savings in the electricity bill by the amount of energy consumed from the PV
installation with respect to what would have been consumed under the three price tariﬀs.
• Exported energy. The economic gains that were received by exporting energy to the grid regulated
by the Simpliﬁed Compensation Mechanism.
• Regional incentives. Depending on the region and municipality, incentives in the form of tax reduc-
tion were considered.

panels, as mentioned in previous section, was of 0.49 EUR/Wp. The selected inverter cost was 1,449.59

the PV, i.e. installing PV panels on an incline roof is more technically challenge that on ground level.

costs represented 0.32 EUR/Wp.

Discussion
Given the results and their corresponding analysis, this section aims to discuss the results by outlining
the key ﬁndings and referencing both research questions. The discussion will consider all the studied con-
trollers under the three scopes: thermal response, energy KPIs and economic results based on regulation
and installed capacity.
5.d.1
Thermal Response
The aim of this scope is to evaluate if the inside temperature boundaries were satisﬁed by each controller
and whether these controllers increased or decrease the average temperatures with respect to the thermo-
static response.
Figure 28 shows the mean inside temperature was kept within the limits set in Section 3.d.6 for every
controller. Figure 29 and Table 9, also show the frequency the upper bound comfort temperature was sur-
passed and by how much. The maximum temperature reached was 25.59◦C in house 5 by the NL+Default
Price controller. The maximum frequency the upper limit comfort temperature was surpassed was 4.2%
of the time, accounted again for the house 5 by the NL+Default Price controller.
Surpassing the upper limit comfort temperature by 0.59◦C, specially at noontime, when the highest PV
generation and temperatures are typically reached, could cause some degree of discomfort to consumers.
Nevertheless, these high temperatures were reached on rare instances and for a short period of time.
Therefore, all the controllers fulﬁlled the thermal comfort.
64
==PAGE:74==
Results regarding the rise and decrease of temperature inside each house reﬂected accurate methodology
and assumptions. Assumptions like heat capacitance and envelope materials of each house were very
sensitive parameters, where an increase or decrease of them could greatly aﬀect the results.
5.d.2
Energy KPIs
Energy KPIs aimed to establish the most favourable controller in terms of energy savings that would
also translate into electricity bill savings by several indicators. It also evaluated the performance of the
sharing algorithm and its impact to these indicators.
Tables 12, 14 and 17 show that almost every controller improved results from the thermostatic response
for every price tariﬀ. The only controller providing an inferior performance was the 2 Period Price Signal
controller. The fact that this controller was the only price-based controller that provided an inferior
performance, indicated that the assumed theta value for the 2P price was not as accurate as those chosen
for the Default and EV price.
Results regarding the energy KPIs determined the NL controller as the most favourable and provided
enhancements with respect to the thermostatic response controller. Additionally, the sharing algorithm
also provided an important enhancement of every indicator of around 12%.
5.d.3
Economic Results
Economic results aimed to evaluate if the controllers provided an economic improvement with respect
to the thermostatic response. They also aimed to study which price tariﬀwas the most economically
favourable.
As shown in Table 21 and further explained in Figure 31, the tariﬀthat provided the lowest electricity
bill was the 2 Period price tariﬀ. This was a reﬂection of the consumption behaviour of the CEC, showing
that consumers usually consumed energy during “valley hours”, as illustrated in Figure 31.
Considering PV installation and controllers, Table 22 shows that with the installation of the PV, economic
savings were achieved. Once the PV installation was considered, the base-line case was the thermostatic
response. Results displayed in Table 22 reﬂected that not every controller provided economic savings with
every tariﬀ. For ease of explanation, discussion on the results provided by Table 22 were divided in price
tariﬀs, as follows:
• Default tariﬀ. The only controller that provided economic savings with respect to the thermostatic
response, was the price controller. This was due to the proﬁle of the default price signal and because
the price controller was a thermostatic response controller with just the price signal as an input.
The default tariﬀoﬀers almost a constant price thought the day. Hence, by assuming a theta value
that sets a lower bound sensitive enough to determine if the price was low, and therefore sending
an on signal to the heat pump, economic savings could be expected.
• 2 Period tariﬀ. Although greater savings were given by the NL controller, every controller provided
economic savings with respect to the thermostatic response. These savings were due to both price
and consumption signals. As observed in Figure 17, 2 Period price signal peaked from 12 to 21h.
65
==PAGE:75==
On the other hand, PV production during this period, according to the simulations performed in
SAM, went from 9 to 16h. Therefore, the NL signal was usually negative (exporting to the grid)
during half of the peaking period of the 2 Period price signal. Since the NL controller was only
driven by the NL proﬁle, heat pump on signal was sent during the period when the 2 Period price
signal was peaking, and thus, more savings were achieved. This was the reason why the NL+2P
controller achieved slightly higher savings in the electricity price compared to the price controller.
• EV tariﬀ. Similarly to the 2 Period tariﬀ, every controller provided economic savings with respect
to the thermostatic response, but in this case the price controller provided the highest savings. As
observed in Figure 17, EV tariﬀprovided a similar tariﬀto the 2 Period, were the “valley hours”
where shifted one hour: covering from 22 to 12h. This one-hour shift and the theta value set for
the price controller were the main reasons of that slight increase in economic savings between the
NL and price controller (0.02 EUR).
It can be observed too that when the PV was installed, diﬀerences in economic savings between the hourly
discriminatory prices were broader than when no PV was installed. This was mainly because the imported
energy from the grid during the EV price “valley hours” was higher than the energy imported from the
grid during the 2 Period price “valley hours”. To illustrate this argument, ﬁgure 33 provided price curves
from a randomly picked days and the mean CEC Net Load curve.

Sensitivity Analysis
In this section a sensitivity analysis was performed in the NL+2P and NL controller. The aim of this
section was to test the robustness of the model and study how the modiﬁcation of certain parameters could
have an impact on the results provided in the section above. It is also aimed to increase the understanding
of the relationship between these parameters input to the model and the output.
Three diﬀerent scenarios were studied in this section:
• Firstly, to further understand the diﬀerences between the NL+2P and the NL controller, and how
the price signal aﬀected the results given by the NL+2P controller, the theta parameter was subject
to study. Theta parameter 6 was considered a sensitive parameter, as by changing it, it would
modify the lower bound and hence the HP utilization.
• The second scenario studied was set to investigate the results of a possible controller that did not
account for the higher temperature comfort limit. In this case, the NL was considered since raising
the temperature based also on the price signal would increase the price, whereas if it was just
considered the net load proﬁle, the energy received and used to raise the temperature would be
free. The aim of this controller was to use the houses as thermal storage. Considering the thermal
comfort and the frequency that very high temperatures were reached determined the feasibility of
the stated controller.
• The third scenario studied the impact the size of the PV installation had on the system and ul-
timately indicating the optimal PV size with respect to the CEC. There were 6 diﬀerent cases
considered, where the PV size is reduced to 10% and 20% and increased to 40%, 50%, 70% and
80%.
6.a
Theta sensitivity
As previously stated, the sensitivity of the theta parameter was studied. Since NL+2P controller was a
partially price-driven controller, the variation of this parameter resulted in a modiﬁcation of the lower
bound, as stated in Equation 32, and hence determined how frequently the HP was activated.

bound. The lower bound sets the threshold from which price the HP should be activated. As explained in
above sections, the logic behind it was to use the HP when prices were low and thus reduce the electricity
bill. The lower the bound, the less sensitive to price variations and hence less HP usage driven by the
price. It can be expected that by increasing the lower bound to a certain level, NL+2P results would tend

Conclusion and Recommendations
In the present work, diﬀerent demand-side management controllers within a CEC, were studied. These
controllers aimed to increase self-consumed energy from the PV system with respect to the thermostatic
response controller, while maintaining thermal comfort inside each household, resulting in monetary sav-
ings, and emission reduction. A sharing algorithm was also implemented with the objective of enhancing
these results.
Results showed that every controller provided these aimed results except for the 2 Period price signal
controller (2P). The controller with the most favourable results was the NL controller which increasing
the self-consumed energy from the PV system, and hence increased the avoided emissions by 2.21%. This
controller also enhanced the results of diﬀerent energy indicators related to the self-consumption (SC and
SS) in a similar proportion. On the other hand, 2P controller underperformed by a 0.15% with respect to
the thermostatic response. As discussed earlier, this was caused by inaccurate assumptions. The sharing
algorithm provided an enhancement when implemented to every controller, resulting in an increase of
13.12% in the self-consumed energy with respect to the thermostatic response, when implemented in the
NL controller. NL controller also provided a 0.16% in the peak power consumption with respect to the
thermostatic response.
These energy indicator results translated into economic savings, where the controllers were compared
against the scenario were no PV had been installed. Similarly to the energy indicators, the NL controller
provided the higher monetary savings. With the installation of the PV system and the implementation
of the controllers, monetary savings rose during the selected period up to 23.94% by the NL controller.
Implementing the sharing algorithm, these savings rose up to 24.67%. When the PV installation was
considered, the thermostatic response was compared to the controllers, resulting in 0.26%.
The sensitivity analysis results illustrated the high sensitivity of the price controllers to the theta value,
concluding that up to a 150% increase on theta value, NL+2P controller would deliver diﬀerent results
than the NL controller. The importance of an upper temperature limit on the controllers under consid-
eration was also adressed, since their absence resulted in thermal discomfort for the household members.
Finally, the sensitivity analysis concluded that the most suitable PV-sizing regarding the self-consumption
to imported-energy ratio was the one covering the 38.56% of the demand. This percentage was calculated
for this speciﬁc project. Diﬀerent characteristics may vary this ﬁgure.
It can therefore be concluded that the proposed controllers and sharing algorithm within the CEC pro-
vided economic savings and self-consumption enhancements while maintaining the thermal comfort in
every household.
Deﬁnitions on sustainable energy communities are not verry accurately deﬁned within Spanish legislation.
The main Spanish laws that govern sustainable energy communities are Royal Decree (RD) 23/2020. Al-
though a more speciﬁc legal structure is yet to be made, technical and economical aspects relevant for
collective self-consumption are regulated under the RD 244/2019. The energy community deﬁnition given
by EU Clean Energy for All Europeans legislative package (CEP) that could better ﬁt into the Spanish
legislation is the CEC. Since they are not limited by proximity, CECs can work as RE virtual networks
73
==PAGE:83==
under Spanish legislation. Energy cooperatives like SomEnergia can own generation plants for collective
self-consumption, avoiding distance and capacity limitations set by RD 244/2019, 4.a.
Delocalization provided by the CECs over RECs could tackle energy poverty for low-income households
that do not have the possibility of participating in place-based energy communities (RECs). It would also
open up the opportunity for rural areas like the one described in this project, to become more attractive
for renewable energy investments, and to counterbalance the eﬀects of rural exodus.
Spain needs to develop a more comprehensive legal framework to withstand the complexity of the up-
coming energy communities, i.e.
demand-side management, new business models, participation, etc.
Therefore, this work provides few regulatory recommendations.
7.a
Recommendations
Virtual net metering would broaden the sharing options of PV installation owners that export part of
their energy to the grid. Virtual net metering would also allow other households that do not have space
or enough incomes to invest in PV, to participate in energy communities without the proximity constraint
set in RD 244/2019.
Enabling a peer to peer market on a national or community level would also facilitate the participation of
more consumers on renewable energy projects. Peer to peer market would open new market opportunities
for both consumers and prosumers as well as new business models with new agents (aggregator and third
parties). Prosumers would be able to sell their excessive energy to others at an agreed price, increasing
the utilization of the energy generated by the PV system and providing monetary savings for both parties.
Diﬀerent incentives to encourage investments, like tax-reduction on the electricity bill for a limited period
of time, increasing the pay-back time. New speciﬁc tariﬀs for prosumers, who only use the LV grid, aimed
at reducing the grid utilization fees.
The cited recommendations require careful consideration and study when implementing them into the
legal framework. Another recommendation that could be made with much background study and would
be easy to implement into the current legal framework is the dynamic sharing coeﬃcient. This work
proposed a dynamic sharing coeﬃcient based on the hourly forecasted consumption for the next day for
each household (24), instead of on the contracted power.
