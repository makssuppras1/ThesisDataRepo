Abstract
Improving the wake trajectory description may bene’¼üt the prediction of required yaw o’¼Ćsets
for turbines in wind farms when applying wake steering control. The desired yaw o’¼Ćset is
potentially smaller when initial wake de’¼éection is accounted for, which reduces the predicted
power loss and loading e’¼Ćects on the yawed turbine itself. This could further lead to more
accurate predictions of the annual energy production.
This thesis attempts to increase the robustness of the Gaussian engineering wake model by
quantifying the initial horizontal wake de’¼éection under non-yawed and yawed conditions. The
initial wake de’¼éection is caused by momentum transport of air parcels in the wake due to
wake rotation and vertical shear. This causes an asymmetry in the lateral wake de’¼ücit and
induces an initial de’¼éection in the horizontal and vertical direction, even for a non-yawed turbine.
Several parameters have been identi’¼üed that could help in parametrizing the initial wake
de’¼éection: vertical shear, turbulence intensity, torque, power operation CP /CT , and yaw angle.
A parametric study is performed employing computational ’¼éuid dynamics Reynolds-Averaged
Navier Stokes simulations. The k-╬Ą-fP turbulence model is chosen to simulate the ’¼éow around
the NREL-5MW reference turbine, which is modelled by an actuator disc based on a Joukowsky
rotor.
Shear and wake rotation, induced by torque, are essential for initial wake de’¼éection to occur.
An increase of either leads to an increase in the initial de’¼éection because momentum transport
is enhanced. An increase in CP /CT would mean that the rotation e’¼Ćect on the wake becomes
more dominating compared to the e’¼Ćect of CT . For positive yaw, the lateral thrust force
component enhances the wake rotation at the top of the wake, which increases the momentum
transport of high-momentum parcels, thus strengthening the initial de’¼éection. For negative
yaw, the opposite is true.
Two parametrizations have been found that reduce the velocity error of the model, especially
for positive yaw angles. For negative yaw, the extended models perform similar to the original
model. The parametrizations also improve the wake trajectory prediction. For this reason,
the wake asymmetry due to shear and wake rotation is able to be modelled successfully by
including these parametrizations in the Gaussian wake model.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The demand for new sustainable methods to produce electricity has driven wind energy to
become one of the main renewable energy resources. The global energy consumption has been
continuously increasing [19] and will continue to do so as the energy grid is still expanding
in developing countries [21]. With this increase in energy consumption, an increase in CO2
emissions of 1.7% was observed in 2018 [19] contributing to climate change. In order to meet
the future energy demand, while also mitigating the e’¼Ćects on climate change, the shift to
renewable energy is inevitable. Furthermore, renewable energy resources in Europe such as
solar and wind energy have become cheaper than fossil fuels. This can be seen in Figure 1.1,
where the levelized cost of energy of several energy resources in Europe are compared for the
year 2018. Additionally, as opposed to fossil fuels, wind energy is not a depletable resource.
Figure 1.1: Levelized cost of energy of several energy resources in Europe in 2018. Source: IEA
[20].
Wind turbines are able to extract kinetic energy from the incoming air’¼éow and convert it
into mechanical energy. Due to the extraction of energy from the air’¼éow, the ’¼éow downstream
of a turbine decelerates. Furthermore, this ’¼éow is characterized by an increase in turbulence.
These characteristics of the ’¼éow downstream of a turbine are what is called the wake e’¼Ćect.
Wind turbines can be clustered together in a wind farm in areas where the wind resource
is favourable in order to produce as much power as possible and lower costs. However, the
wake e’¼Ćect negatively a’¼Ćect downstream turbines. Due to reduced wind speed in the wake,
a downstream turbine will generate less power. This can lead to losses in annual energy

production (AEP) of more than 10% [6, 5]. Furthermore, the lifetime of downstream tur-
bines is a’¼Ćected, because of the increase in fatigue loading caused by the more turbulent ’¼éow [41].
Several wind farm control strategies can be implemented to potentially reduce the wake
e’¼Ćect. One of these strategies is wake steering by yaw misalignment [16, 49]. Wake steering
control ’¼ünds an optimal combination of yaw o’¼Ćsets for each turbine in a wind farm to optimize
AEP production or to decrease fatigue loading on downstream turbines. This yaw o’¼Ćset causes
the wake to be steered to the left or right of a downstream turbine, depending on the preferred
direction. Do note that yawing a turbine reduces its power output and increases undesirable
loading e’¼Ćects on the turbine itself.
A control strategy such as wake steering needs to be computationally e’¼ācient for implementation,
since they are required to react to highly variable ambient conditions. For this reason, analytical
wake engineering models are used to describe how the wake propagates downstream in terms of
wake velocity and wake trajectory. These models contain expressions derived, both analytically
and empirically, from computational ’¼éuid dynamics (CFD) simulations and wind tunnel
experiments. Below, several wake engineering models are brie’¼éy described:
ŌĆó the Jensen model [23, 25], which describes the wake with a simple ŌĆśtop-hatŌĆÖ distribution
that expands linearly downstream.
ŌĆó the Frandsen model [14], which introduces descriptions for merging wakes in a wind farm
as well as the interaction with the atmospheric boundary layer (ABL).
ŌĆó the Gaussian wake model from the ├ēcole polytechnique f├®d├®rale de Lausanne (EPFL)
group [7], which uses the self-similarity characteristic of a Gaussian pro’¼üle to describe
the wake velocity de’¼ücit distribution, thus assuming the wake to be axisymmetric. The
Gaussian wake model allows for wake steering by yaw misalignment.
Although, assuming the velocity de’¼ücit in the wake is distributed Gaussian is a good approx-
imation, studies using CFD and wind tunnel experiments have shown the wake is actually
non-axisymmetric due to various reasons discussed in detail in Section 1.2.2.
This thesis investigates the non-axisymmetric nature of the wake by employing CFD
simulations. In particular, the emphasis lies on the initial wake de’¼éection caused by momentum
transport which can be attributed to wake rotation and vertical shear.
This momentum
transport was also observed in the work of Zahle and S├Ėrensen [60]. In the work of Fleming
et al. [13], an initial o’¼Ćset of the wake de’¼éection in the horizontal and vertical direction for
a non-yawed turbine is observed. Section 1.2 will further discuss the cause of initial wake
de’¼éection.
The thesis contributes to the improvement of the Gaussian wake model by quantifying this
initial horizontal wake de’¼éection under non-yawed and yawed conditions, which is typically
tuned with additional model parameters, in an attempt to increase the robustness of the
Gaussian wake model. Furthermore, improving the description of the wake trajectory could
bene’¼üt the prediction of required yaw o’¼Ćsets of turbines in wind farms when applying wake
steering control. The needed yaw o’¼Ćset could potentially be smaller when initial wake de’¼éection
is accounted for, which reduces the power loss and loading e’¼Ćects on the yawed turbine itself.
This could further lead to more accurate AEP predictions.

these are out of the scope of this thesis. The shape of the velocity de’¼ücit in the near-wake can
most accurately be described as a double bell curve. However, in the work of Krogstad and
Adaramola [27] it is shown the shape of the near-wake is dependent on the tip speed ratio ╬╗;
for optimal ╬╗ the shape is almost uniform, while for higher ╬╗ the double bell curve is much
more profound.

bell curve slowly mix together to an axisymmetric wake de’¼ücit. The shape of the far-wake is

1.2.2
Wake asymmetry
The approximation of an axisymmetric wake, as is done in the Gaussian wake model [7], is in
fact not the case. There are several e’¼Ćects that cause the wake to be non-axisymmetric:
ŌĆó Atmospheric stability: Abkar and Port├®-Agel [1] performed a large-eddy simulation (LES)
study to show that during unstable conditions the wake becomes more elliptical in shape
due to di’¼Ćerent turbulence intensities, which causes the wake to have di’¼Ćerent recovery
rates in the lateral and vertical direction. This e’¼Ćect is explained by thermal strati’¼ücation,
which adds to the turbulent mixing of air near the ground for unstable conditions, whereas
for stable conditions it dampens turbulent mixing. Therefore, it is stated that the total
turbulence intensity should be considered when determining the impact of atmospheric
stability.
ŌĆó Wind veer: Abkar et al. [2] simulated the wake employing LES in an ABL under stable
atmospheric conditions taking into account the Coriolis force. In a stable ABL the Coriolis
force causes the wind direction to change over height, creating directional shear, i.e., wind
veer. This e’¼Ćect causes the wake to be skewed in a diagonal, i.e, rotated elliptical shape.
ŌĆó Ground e’¼Ćects: Xie and Archer [59] simulated the wake downstream of a turbine in
neutral conditions. It was found that the wake expanded linearly in the lateral direction,
while expanding slower than linear in the vertical. This anisotropic behaviour is caused
by vertical shear, given the elliptical shape of the wake, but mainly by the wake colliding
with the ground hindering the wake to expand vertically. This results in the wake to
spread laterally, which is why self-similarity is not valid anymore in the lower part of the
wake.
ŌĆó Yaw misalignment: Howland et al. [18] presented that intentionally yawing a turbine
deforms the wake into a, non-axisymmetric, ŌĆśkidney-likeŌĆÖ shape. Moreover, Bastankhah
and Port├®-Agel [8] demonstrates that under highly yawed conditions the wake consists
out of a counter-rotating vortex pair (CVP), which in’¼éuence is signi’¼ücant for the in’¼éow
for downstream turbines.
Furthermore, a di’¼Ćerent case of wake asymmetry was found employing LES in the work of
Fleming et al. [13], where an absolute o’¼Ćset is observed in the horizontal direction x/D = 0.08
and vertical direction z/D = 0.03 at 7.5D downstream for a non-yawed turbine. This asymme-
try, which is the emphasis of this thesis, causes a slight initial horizontal de’¼éection of the wake
due to momentum transport which is attributed to vertical shear and wake rotation [60].
When considering a turbine that rotates clockwise, the wake will rotate counter-clockwise.
Low-momentum air parcels at the surface are transported upwards and to the right, while
high-momentum air parcels at the top of the wake are transported downwards and to the

In this thesis several series of CFD simulations are performed on a single non-yawed wind

2.1
Choice of ’¼éow solver
In CFD, several levels of ’¼üdelity exist for models that calculate a turbulent ’¼éow. The model
with the highest ’¼üdelity is called direct numerical simulation (DNS) and solves all turbulent
structures, i.e., eddies, present in the ’¼éow up until the smallest turbulent length scale. This is
computationally very expensive, thus not feasible considering the large number of cells needed
to investigate initial wake de’¼éection in the far-wake. Secondly, there is LES, which solves up
until a certain length scale, while parametrizing eddies smaller than the grid size. Lastly, RANS
models, which have the lowest ’¼üdelity of the three, calculate a representation of the mean
steady-state ’¼éow by directly modelling all turbulent length scales. This means that RANS is not
able to resolve the turbulent eddies, but rather attempts to give a time-averaged representation
of the ’¼éow. However, RANS is computationally much more e’¼ācient than LES, which is desirable
if multiple parameters are to be investigated in the parametric study. Furthermore, the initial
wake de’¼éection is expected to be observed in the mean steady-state ’¼éow. Since LES represents
a non-stationary ’¼éow, a time series is needed from which the average is computed. If this
time series is not large enough, this could result in wake asymmetry due to non-stationarity,
potentially obscuring the e’¼Ćect of initial wake de’¼éection. Therefore, RANS is chosen as the
preferred method to simulate the turbulent ’¼éow.
For the purpose of this thesis the in-house DTU ’¼éow solver for incompressible ’¼éuids, EllipSys3D,
is used to perform RANS simulations. EllipSys3D is developed in the works of Michelsen [31,
32] and S├Ėrensen [46]. In this work, the Navier-Stokes equations are solved using the SIMPLE1
algorithm, which solves the momentum equation to estimate the velocity ’¼üeld, updates the pres-
sure distribution, and then corrects the velocity ’¼üeld in an iterative procedure until convergence
is reached. The partial di’¼Ćerential equations are discretized using a ’¼ünite volume method called
QUICK2 scheme for the convective terms and central di’¼Ćerencing scheme. EllipSys3D can be
used with Python due to the framework of pyellipsys and the front end Python-interface
pywake_rans.

3.1

In the work of Bastankhah and Port├®-Agel [7] the framework of this Gaussian wake model
to predict the wake velocity de’¼ücit and wake width, i.e., wake recovery is described. The
most important aspects of the model are summarized below. Most notably the characteristic

far-wake region. In the near-wake a ŌĆśtop-hatŌĆÖ distribution has been chosen in line with the work

visualization of the Gaussian wake model is given in Figure 3.1.

e’¼Ćects and shear. This anisotropic behaviour in the wake growth rates was also found in
the work of Xie and Archer [59], which performed an LES study in a neutral ABL. The
non-axisymmetry was attributed to ground e’¼Ćects and shear. The Gaussian wake model was
modi’¼üed to account for an elliptical wake shape by applying di’¼Ćerent wake growth rates in
vertical and lateral direction. The estimation of the wake growth rates is improved by having

include wake de’¼éection due to yaw misalignment and the far-wake onset is analytically de’¼üned.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusions & Future research
The wake of a turbine is often approximated to be axisymmetric. In reality however, this is
rarely the case. Initial wake de’¼éection is a form of wake asymmetry often neglected or modelled
insu’¼āciently. To understand the mechanism of initial wake de’¼éection RQ-1 is answered by
employing RANS simulations. Subsequently, the Gaussian wake model is extended to include
the initial horizontal wake de’¼éection, thereby answering RQ-2. The most important conclusions
are summarized in Section 5.1. Some suggestions for future research on related topics are
provided Section 5.2.
5.1
Conclusions
Relevant parameters that in’¼éuence the initial wake de’¼éection together with the underlying
mechanisms are listed in Table 5.1. These mechanisms are supported by the results from the
RANS simulations. In the CFD set-up employed, vertical shear is coupled with turbulence
intensity. Therefore, the e’¼Ćect these two parameters have on the initial wake de’¼éection are
combined and explained as one.
When investigating wake rotation, it is found that for increasing ╬▒, IH,Ōł×the di’¼Ćerence between
the maximum and minimum cross-stream velocity increases as well. This supports that there is
more momentum transport, thus a larger initial wake de’¼éection.
The contribution of rotational wake e’¼Ćects to the streamwise velocity pro’¼üle is found to be
asymmetric for low IH,Ōł×. Additionally, the results show that for low IH,Ōł×wake rotation adds
to the entrainment of the wake.
A potential collapse is shown of cases with similar CP /CT . Therefore, di’¼Ćerent cases of turbine
operation can lead to similar initial wake de’¼éection, which could be useful when applying wake
control strategies.
Including yaw to the parametrization is proven essential so that the performance of the Gaussian
model is also increased for negative yaw. Results of simulating yaw with and without wake
rotation show that the lateral thrust force component interacts with the wake rotation, thereby
in’¼éuencing the initial wake de’¼éection.
The performance of three data-driven modi’¼ücations to the Gaussian model and one modi’¼ücation
based on literature are compared to the original Gaussian model and RANS simulations.
When examining the approaches for non-yawed conditions, Approach II and III perform worse
than the original Gaussian model for x/D > 10, but perform better than the original model
and Approach I and IV for x/D < 10. Nevertheless, Approach I and IV are determined to be
more robust, since they performed better overall. Moreover, the velocity error of the approaches
is sensitive to the tuning process. Approach II is non-physical, since it assumes the wake
continually de’¼éects linearly downstream. Approach III requires ’¼üve coe’¼ācients, which is the
most compared to other parametrizations.
When comparing the performance Approach I and IV for yawed conditions, both are observed

5.2
Future research
On the basis of the work done in this thesis, several suggestions are provided for future research.
Continued research on initial wake de’¼éection
Some suggestions are made below on how to improve the parametrization of initial wake
de’¼éection.
ŌĆó Investigate e’¼Ćect of vertical shear ╬▒ and turbulence intensity IŌł×separately
A di’¼Ćerent turbulence model could be employed to investigate the e’¼Ćect of ╬▒ and IŌł×
separately. It is expected that in reality ╬▒ and IŌł×have an opposite e’¼Ćect on the initial
wake de’¼éection. An increase in the mixing of air is believed to be related to a quicker

dissipation of wake rotation. By including atmospheric stability, the relation between ╬▒
and IŌł×changes, which could make it possible to compare di’¼Ćerent cases of IŌł×where ╬▒
is constant.
ŌĆó Investigation of vertical wake de’¼éection
This thesis only focuses on the wake pro’¼üle at hub height. Considering the ground e’¼Ćect,
wake rotation, anisotropic wake growth rates and yaw, the streamwise velocity de’¼ücit
pro’¼üle in the vertical direction will be asymmetric. This causes the wake to also de’¼éect
vertically. Partial wake overlap on the in’¼éow of downstream wind turbine in wind farms
could also cause a vertical wake de’¼éection. To investigate this, it is essential a valid wake
centre estimation method is applied that considers 2D-e’¼Ćects.
ŌĆó Adding the e’¼Ćect of tip speed ratio ╬╗ to CP /CT
To improve the description of wake rotation in the parametrization of initial wake de’¼éec-
tion ╬╗ should be included. Adding ╬╗ to CP /CT will capture more of the possible turbine
operation conditions. A similar approach as in Section 4.3 can be followed.
Extension of the Gaussian engineering wake model
The proposed extensions of the Gaussian wake model, Approach I and IV, require to be validated
to conclude which one is the most robust.
ŌĆó Validation of Approach I and Approach IV
It is desirable that Approach I and IV are further tested by employing LES simulations
or measurements, even though these extensions of the Gaussian model improve the wake
trajectory and reduce the velocity error compared to RANS.
Furthermore, the e’¼Ćect on the yaw o’¼Ćsets and power output in the context of wind farm
control could be investigated.
Physical e’¼Ćects in the wake
Several observations in the wake are made that are either left unexplained or require more
research.
ŌĆó Added entrainment due to wake rotation
In Section 4.2, the results indicate that wake rotation contributes to the wake recovery
for low IŌł×, especially for 5 Ōēżx/D Ōēż10. In the context of wind farm control, accounting
for this e’¼Ćect in the Gaussian model could potentially improve power predictions for low
IŌł×. This can be achieved by extending the description of the velocity de’¼ücit.
ŌĆó Realignment of the wake with the main ’¼éow direction
The initial horizontal wake de’¼éection displayed in Figure 4.2 is shown to have a near-linear
trend up until at least x/D = 20. Furthermore, the wake de’¼éection induced by yaw
for IH,Ōł×= 0.1 and IH,Ōł×= 0.15, displayed in Figure 4.9, illustrates an increase rate of
de’¼éection. This is unexpected behaviour and could be investigated further to see whether
it is caused by the RANS simulation set-up, or whether it is a physical e’¼Ćect unaccounted
for.
