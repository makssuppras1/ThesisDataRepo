Abstract
Two test methods, EN 12370 and RILEM MS-A.2, are used to investigate four types of ﬁred
clay bricks durability towards salt deterioration. The bricks were chosen based on experimentally
determined material properties, exposure class and ﬁring temperature. Uncertainties were found
while performing the two test procedures and the assessment methods of the results were mainly
dependent on observations in photographic documentations. The deterioration atlas was used to
evaluate the deterioration patterns. Since both test methods relied on photographic documentation,
considerations when performing the photographic documentation was made and suggestions were
given to a consistent documentation.
The need for a new assessment is identiﬁed, and a suggestion is given. By initially coloring the test
surface before the test specimen is exposed to salt deterioration, a clear division is made between
the deteriorated area and the non-deteriorated area on the test surface. Different approaches to
determine the deteriorated surface area is given in the thesis. The coloring was performed with a
permanent marker and the water vapour transmission properties for the surface were investigated.
A cup test was performed, and it was statistically proven that the water vapour permeability and
the water vapour permeance were not affected by the coloring of the surface.

1 Introduction
The introduction presents knowledge regarding salt deterioration divided into relevant theoretical
statements, existing test methods and how the results are assessed. Thereafter an overview and
introduction of the work performed in the present thesis are given and at last a description on a new
test method, which is being currently developed by the RILEM Technical Committee 271-ASC.
1.1
Current state of knowledge
It is well known, that salt crystallization in porous materials is the cause of deterioration, yet
uncertainties regarding salt deterioration are still to be resolved. A summary is given on three
areas that are relevant to the aim of the present thesis.
• Theoretical models describing salt crystallization deterioration
• Reliable and realistic test methods
• Assessment of test result and validation through comparison with ﬁeld data.
Theoretical models
Due to the complexity of salt deterioration, theoretical models are not yet capable of describing the
full deterioration process of a porous material. The deterioration process can not be explained by
a single mechanism but consists of different processes where the transport kinetics, environmental
exposure, materials chemistry and poromechanics are central elements where some of them have
been theoretical described, (Flatt et al., 2017).
Theoretical models have been developed and well established for the transport mechanisms of
soluble salt in liquid and moisture into the porous material. The accumulation and distribution
of the salt ions have been explained by equations for advection and diffusion where the dominant
mechanism can be deﬁned by the Peclet number.
Several models regarding the deterioration of the material have been developed. The current un-
derstanding is the concept of crystallization pressure where pressure exerted by a growing crystal
in a supersaturated solution can be calculated by a theoretical model. A limitation of the model is
to describe deterioration in pores larger than 50 nm, where ﬁred clay brick would typically have a

Test methods
Several test methods have been developed to investigate the durability of the porous material
towards salt deterioration. EN 12370, The procedure of the tests consists of cycles of wetting
and drying, where the salt will dissolve and precipitate a number of times. To accelerate the
deterioration, the specimens are often exposed to a high concentration of salt and/or drying at
high temperatures. The critic of the methods consists of the unrealistic conditions, which the
test specimens undergo, will result in unrealistic deterioration patterns. Besides, uncertainties in
the test procedure and biased evaluation of the results contribute to an incomparability between
different laboratories, which leads to a reluctance to use the methods.
Assessment of results
Different assessment methods can be used to evaluate the results of a test method, where the most
common is to make visual and photographic observations, to document the mass change or to
count the cycles of wetting and drying. The mentioned assessment methods each have their weak-
nesses. A visual documentation is biased by subjective considerations, the mass change is affected
by the mass increase of the accumulated salt induced by several salt contaminations and the count
of cycles depending on failure criterion, which is speciﬁed in the individual cases.
To support the visual and photographic observations, deterioration atlases have been developed to
describe different deterioration patterns, (ICOMOS-ISCS, 2008), (MDCS, 2019).
1.2
The present thesis
This thesis is an investigation of the current existing test methods ability to evaluate deterioration
caused by salt in the porous building material, ﬁred clay brick. The results from the two existing
test methods are intended to be compared with results from a to be developed test method within
the framework of RILEM TC 271-ASC. A new assessment method is suggested as an addition to
the current assessment methods for evaluation of results.
The thesis is constructed as follows to achieve the aim of investigating current test methods. Ini-
tially, a characterization of the different types of ﬁred clay bricks was made. The test specimens
for further investigation were chosen based on material properties, which were experimentally
found. The material properties are the porosity, density, initial rate of capillary absorption, and
water absorption. An introduction to the types of ﬁred clay brick is given in Chapter 2 and the

Different issues with the current assessment methods are established in Chapter 11. A suggestion

and contributing to a quantiﬁable value. The concept is, in short, to initially color the test sur-

clear division is made on the surface area into deteriorated surface, non-deteriorated surface and
efﬂorescence. The deteriorated surface area can then be found. To evaluate the reliability of the
method, a cup test was performed and a surface grind was made.
1.3
Development of a new test method by the Technical Committee
271-ASC
The uncertainties mentioned previous regarding salt deterioration is being met by the RILEM
Technical Committee 271-ASC. The Technical Committee 271-ASC is working on a project titled
"Accelerated laboratory test for the assessment of the durability of materials with respect to salt
crystallization.". They held a meeting from the 12th to the 13th 2019 of September, which the au-
thor participated in. The committee meets every six months and discusses results obtained since
the last meeting and plan which experiments to perform to the next. The committee initiated their
work in 2016 and the estimated time frame is 4 to 5 years.
The new method is divided into an initial accumulation phase and a propagation phase, see Figure
1.1. The test materials are two types of limestone, Maastricht and Migne limestone, which have
a unimodal pore size distribution with different porosity and pore size, to obtain results indepen-
dently from variations in the material properties. A preliminary explanation of the two phases is
given as they were at the time of the meeting.
Accumulation phase
During the initial accumulation phase, the porous material is contaminated with a salt solution.
Two salts were used, sodium sulfate and sodium chloride, yet the salt concentration was still dis-
cussed and varied depending on the salt. The aim is to position a thin layer of salt right below the

solved salt and afterward drying it at 60 ◦C to constant mass. Another possibility is to induce the
solution into the test specimen by capillary absorption followed by a drying period of evaporation
at 20 ◦C and at RH of 10 %. The last contamination procedure was tested which resulted in a
time-consuming drying process, see Appendix ?? for further information regarding the procedure
and result. The preliminary drying conditions were altered at the meeting to test an increased tem-
perature of 40 ◦C and a 20% RH. A third contamination procedure was precluded at the meeting
due to being too time-consuming.
The location of the salt in the test specimen was examined after the different methods to assess
the contamination procedures by performing micro x-ray ﬂuorescence mapping.
Propagation phase
The propagation phase starts after the salt has been positioned right under the surface and deteri-
oration of the material begins due to cycles of re-wetting and drying, see Figure 1.1. The aim is
to develop a realistic type of damage in a short period by dissolution and precipitation of the salt.
Depending on the salt used in the accumulation phase, the propagation phase will initiate with
a dissolution of sodium sulfate by capillary absorption to activate both the hydrated and anhy-
drous phases of sodium sulfate at a low temperature. For sodium chloride, the propagation phase
initiates with the crystals dissolving due to a 95% RH at 20 ◦C, which is above the point of deli-
quescence at 75.4% RH. The time for the salt to dissolve is further investigated, to see if it is too
time-consuming, and in that case, if it is only necessary to dissolve part of crystals to save time.
After the re-wetting, the sodium chloride induced test specimens are dried to a constant mass at
40 ◦C and 20% RH.

2 Characterisation of test material
This section describes how the properties of bricks are deﬁned during the processing of the raw
material. The concept of porosity is explained and different types of ﬁred clay bricks, which are
the test specimens, are introduced by appearance and brickyard.
2.1
The making of ﬁred clay bricks
The properties of bricks are determined by the making of it and what it consist of.
The raw material
Clay is found in the soil around Denmark in abundant amounts. It was formed during the ice age,
where weathered rocks decayed into clay minerals. The composition of clay minerals depends
on the depths of the area where it is extracted from. The clay obtains its plastic properties from
the clay minerals, which are shaped as very ﬁne plates. Due to the plasticity, it can gain a tensile
strength when exposed to moisture, which is necessary to maintain the shape after forming. The
amount of water needed to reach plasticity depends on the clay composition. (Bjerge, 2016).
When the clay is extracted from the soil, it is found in layers. Digging techniques have been
developed to mix the clay already while extracting it from the soil. When the clay is at the man-
ufacturing plant, it is further mixed to ensure a homogeneous mixture. This is done to ensure the
properties of the bricks are with as little variation as possible and to improve the formability of
the clay. (Bjerge, 2016)
The color of the brick is deﬁned by the amount of different oxides in the clay minerals. Red stones
have a lime content of up to 3% while pink-colored stones have up to 10% lime content. If the
amount of lime is three times higher than the iron content, the stone will be yellow. The brick is
more light in the color if the amount of lime is high. A yellow brick is known to be more resistant
towards salt deterioration than a red brick.(Dam et al., 2008)
Manufacturing
The modern manufacturing process consists of an overview of extracting clay from the soil, where-
after it is grounded and mixed with water. The clay is molded into the desired size and afterward,
it is dried and ﬁred as shown in Figure 2.1.

Figure 2.1: Production of brick
The raw material can be molded by three different techniques which are either handmade, slop-
molded or wire-cut bricks. Handmade bricks are made by throwing a lump of clay into a pre-
moistened wooden frame that usually has room for 2 stones. The excess clay is removed with a
ruler, the frame is turned on a board and the stones are ready for drying. The slopmolded bricks
are produced to imitate the handmade bricks, but the process is performed by machines instead.
A lump of clay is pushed into a frame, placed on a board and transported to its place for drying.
Clay formed by wire-cut are pressed against a nozzle which has an opening corresponding to the
length and width of the stone. The continuous clay extrusion is then cut into slices whose thick-
ness corresponds to the height of the stone. The cutting is performed by stretched, steel wires and
several stones can be cut at the same time. (Bjerge, 2016) When molding with the three methods,
the frames have the dimensions of the ﬁnal brick plus an addition to make up for the shrinkage
that happens during the drying and burning. The molding of the clay has an impact on the prop-
erties of the brick. A wire-cut brick usually has higher compressive strength and lower porosity
than a handmade and slopmolded brick because less water is necessary during production. The
handmade brick has a more irregular surface than the two other types, which will have a more
uniform surface.(Bjerge, 2016)
After the clay is molded, it is dried for usually 2-3 days to remove some of the water used in the
molding process after which it is ﬁred in a kiln. The typical kiln is a tunnel kiln, where the clay is
transported through zones of different temperatures depending on the type of brick. Most types of
Danish clay has a ﬁring temperature of 950-1050◦C, which is just below the melting point of the
clay. The melting point is around 1000-1100◦C, but it depends on the clay composition. (Bjerge,
2016)
Depending on the ﬁring temperature, different material parameters are deﬁned. A low ﬁring tem-
perature will result in a higher porosity and a lower compressive strength in the ﬁnal brick, where a
high ﬁring temperature will result in a lower porosity and a higher compressive strength. (Bjerge,
2016) The porosity and the compressive strength will affect the density, water absorption, initial
water absorption per minute and elastic modulus of the material. (Dam et al., 2008). Studies have
been made on a high ﬁring temperature and the effect on the porosity and the pore size distribu-
tion. A high ﬁring temperature showed a decrease in the porosity and an increase in the diameters
of the modal pore size in the material. (Sosim et al., 1985) The temperature also affects the color
of the bricks. If the brick is ﬁred at higher temperatures, red bricks will turn towards brown or
black and yellow bricks will turn towards green or grey. Another way to affect the surface of the

A porous material consists of solid matter and systems of cavities called pores. To characterize
the amount of a material which is pores, the porosity can be found and it is given in a number
from 0 to 1, where 0 is a non-porous material. Information regarding the porosity of a material is
necessary to understand the transport of liquid and moisture through it.

The connectivity between the pores is called the porous system, and a distinction is made between
an open or a closed porous system. In the case of bricks, only the open porous system is relevant

The pore size distribution describes the size of the pores in the porous material. It is relevant to
know due to the capillarity of the material. The size distribution of pores for ﬁred clay brick is

pore size distribution is with a mercury intrusion porosimetry. Mercury is induced into the porous
material with speciﬁc pressures that correlates to a speciﬁc size of pore.

3 Material properties and their test
methods
To decide which types of ﬁred clay brick to use as test specimens for the accelerated salt dete-
rioration tests, initial tests for material properties are performed. The properties investigated are
the porosity and density [kg/m3], the initial water absorption [kg/m2] and the water absorption
[%]. These parameters are chosen as being signiﬁcant in the material characterization because
they contain pieces of information regarding the structure and the water transport in the porous
material which have an impact on the durability towards the salt deterioration of the bricks. A
brick which is durable towards salt and a brick which deteriorate are requested as test materials
for further investigation.
The standard deviation is calculated as samples for the experimentally obtained results. The stan-
dard deviation describes how far the experimentally obtained values for each test specimens are
from the mean of their type of brick. It is calculated as one standard deviation from the mean
where, assuming a normal distribution, 68.2% of the experimentally obtained values fall within
the standard deviation.

absorption is found as an addition.

The exposure class
The exposure class is not calculated based on a test method, but assigned from the brickyard based

suitable for a saline environment such as salt saturated air, seawater or de-icing salts. Figure 3.3
shows a map where zone 2-4 is recommended to use bricks with exposure class MX 4. Besides
the zone in Figure 3.3, special consideration should be made when building close to roads, where
de-icing salt is used regularly at winter.(DTI, 2019)

masonry is protected by other buildings or otherwise sheltered. (MURO, 2016, p.13)

4 Salt
4.1
General about salt
Salt is a chemical compound formed by cations and anions. A cation is a positive charged ion, eg.
sodium, and anions are negatively charged ions, eg. chloride or sulfate. Salts can be formed by a
chemical reaction between a base and an acid or a metal and an acid.
In dry solid form, salt is found as crystals. The crystal is built by ions placed in ﬁrm ion bondings
in a crystal grid. Salt is a hygroscopic material which means it can obtain water molecules and
bind the molecules between the ions bonding. When water molecules are present in the crystal
grid, it is called water of hydration and the salt is a hydrated salt, eg. sodium sulfate decahydrate.
In dry air at a low RH or exposed to heat, the hydrated salt will release the water of hydration,
and an un-hydrated salt will be able to obtain water molecules from the water vapor in high rel-
ative humidity, so the salt remains in equilibrium with the ambient environment. Salt can obtain
enough moisture from the ambient air to dissolves into a solution, this point is called deliques-
cence. Different salts deliquescences at different relative humidities and temperatures. Due to the
hygroscopic ability of the salt, materials containing salt does, in general, contain more moisture.
(A. Elena Charola and Wendler, 2015, p. 59)
A salt solution precipitates to crystals when a supersaturated solution is present. A supersaturated
solution contains more of the solute than in a saturated solution, which creates an unstable system.
To reach equilibrium the system will precipitate into solid crystals and a saturated solution as a
result. A solution can reach supersaturation when a saturated solution is cooled. This is because
salt is readily soluble at higher temperatures, so when the temperature is lowered, the concentra-
tion of salt stays the same as it was at the higher temperature. (Hartmann-Petersen and S. Nielsen,
1998, p. 100)
When salt dissolves into a solution, it dissociates into anionic and cationic components. It becomes
an electrolyte, which means it can conduct electricity. This is because the crystal structure is
decomposed and the ions can move freely. (Hartmann-Petersen and S. Nielsen, 1998)
Regarding salt deterioration, the loaction of the precipitated salt is important where either efﬂo-
rescence or subﬂorescence are discussed. Efﬂorescence is the formation of soluble salt crystals on
the surface and does not damage the porous material since the precipitation happens externally and

5 Transportation mechanisms
For salt to move in or enter a porous media, it must be dissolved in water, that is either in the state
of liquid or vapor. The fact that salt can deliquescence on its own by absorbing moisture from the
ambient air contributes to its mobility in the porous material. The transportation mechanisms of
how water moves are therefore fundamental to understand the distribution of salt leading to the
deterioration.
5.1
Water movement
Water can enter a porous material either as a liquid or in a vapor state, and the water movement is
dependent on the size of the pores. The most relevant transport mechanism is capillarity in case
of salt inﬂuence in ﬁred clay brick. To mention other transport mechanisms, inﬁltration is the
downward movement of liquid water (e.g. rain), and moisture in vapor state can be transported by
the hygroscopicity of the material. Hygroscopicity is the ability of the porous materials to attract
and absorb moisture from the ambient air due to polar interactions between the surface of the
material and the water molecules via hydrogen bonding. (A. E. Charola, 2000)
In general, the movement of water is strongly dependent on temperature and relative humidity.
Capillarity
When a porous building material is in contact with liquid water, water will enter the pore system
of the material as a result of capillarity, also known as capillary rise, capillary action or capillary
suction. The water initial enters the porous material due to the hygroscopicity of the material and
the water and the pore wall will form a meniscus, where the angle of the curve, Θ is an expression
of the attraction between the surface and the water. For material with Θ < 90◦, the adhesive forces
between the surface and water exceed the cohesive forces within the water molecules resulting in
capillary absorption. See Figure 5.1 illustrating capillary rise. The attraction happens due to polar
interactions between the surface of the material and the water molecules via hydrogen bonding.

5.3
Drying movements
When a porous material is exposed to drying it will undergo three stages. (A. Elena Charola and
Wendler, 2015, p. 60-61)
Initially, the porous material is saturated and the moisture evaporates from the surface. As long
as the rate of water supply to the surface is equal to the rate of evaporation from the surface, the
==PAGE:46==
30

evaporation will continue from the surface and the drying rate is constant. The drying rate of the
evaporation depends on the temperature, the RH and if circulating air is present to remove the
water from the surface, but it is also affected by material properties such as a higher porosity will
lead to a higher removal rate due to the capillary transport.
When the liquid water transport to the surface no longer can be sustained, the critical moisture
content is reached and the drying rate starts to decrease. The water supply to the surface is slower
than the evaporation rate and the evaporation surface moves below the surface.
At last, there is almost no liquid left in the porous material. The drying rate will be very slow and
almost constant, and the porous material tends towards equilibrium with the ambient environment,
in particular, the RH.
Drying of the porous material is, compared to wetting with capillarity, a slow process due to the
attraction of water molecules to the mineral surface of the porous material. It is further compli-
cated by the presence of salt, which complicates the drying rate, due to its hygroscopic properties.
The location of the evaporation zone is signiﬁcant towards a full understanding of the formation
of efﬂorescence and subﬂorescence.

6 Deterioration mechanisms
There is a general agreement that deterioration happens due to the presence of salt in a porous ma-
terial, but the mechanisms causing the mechanical work of deterioration are yet to be developed.
Why deterioration happens and how the damage occurs can not yet be established. The most es-
tablished theory is the concept of crystallization pressure, which is presented in the present thesis.
6.1
Crystallization pressure
The concept of crystallization pressure is the pressure exerted by a salt crystal that is growing in
a supersaturated solution. It causes stresses inside the porous material leading to deterioration.
(Scherer et al., 2001, p 45)
For a crystal to grow in a supersaturated solution, a liquid ﬁlm must exist around the entire grow-
ing crystal and thus between the crystal and the adjacent surface of the pore wall. If the ﬁlm is
incoherent at any point, the growth of the crystal would stop. The growth of the crystal happens
from the bottom surface of the crystal through a diffusion of ions from the surrounding liquid ﬁlm.
The liquid ﬁlm exists due to a repulsive force between the crystal and the surface wall, which act
across the entire ﬁlm of liquid as long as the crystal grows. (Scherer et al., 2001, p 46)
In a supersaturated solution, the pressure, Pc required to prevent the growth of a crystal is shown
in equation 6.1, (Flatt et al., 2017)

8 Execution of existing test methods
The two test methods, EN 12370 and MS-A.2, are executed to investigate the test specimens
resistance towards salt deterioration. Both test methods consist of cycles of wetting and drying,
where the salt will dissolve and precipitate several times. To accelerate the deterioration, the
specimens are exposed to a high concentration of salt and/or drying at high temperatures.

Before the specimens are placed in the oven, the oven is prepared by placing a tray with 300ml
water in the oven 30 minutes before. The oven is set to room temperature with an increasing
temperature to 105◦C in not less than 10 hours. According to the test method, the tray is placed
to ensure an initial high RH in the early stages of drying. A high RH at a low temperature will
result in the sodium sulfate precipitating into decahydrate. When the temperature increases above
32.4◦C and the RH remains high due to the tray of water in the oven, the sodium sulfate will
precipitate to the anhydrous phase.
The slow increase in temperature is set to ensure a slow evaporation from the surface of the test
specimens which should make the right conditions for subﬂorescence to form. The slow evapora-
tion will result in a supersaturated solution in the entire test specimen since diffusion of the ions
is the dominating transport mechanism of ions. Due to the large concentration of the salt solution,
the precipitation is more likely to happen deeper in the material rather than just below the surface
due to the increased viscosity in the salt solution, (Lubelli et al., 2018, p.8)
Before the test specimens are re-immersed into the sodium sulfate solution, they are cooled to
room temperature in a desiccator with silica gel. When being immersed, the test specimen will
go from a low RH to 100% RH at a temperature around 20◦C. A transition happens between the
anhydrous phase to the decahydrate phase before the salt dissolves in the water.
Unspeciﬁed parts in the test method
While performing the test, (EN 12370, 1999), some uncertainties appeared in the absence of an

results are not considered to be comparable with results obtained at other laboratories.
Initially when the test specimens were cut, as many as possible of the original ﬁred surfaces were
maintained. One test surface from each test specimen was chosen to document the deterioration,
and the chosen surface was a ﬁred surface in all cases. The properties of the cut surface and the
ﬁred surface vary and it can be assumed the deterioration patterns are different for the two types
of surfaces. To obtain the most realistic result, the ﬁred surface should be speciﬁed.

8.2
Test method MS-A.2
The test was developed as part of a larger investigation, ”RILEM TC 127-MS: Test for masonry
materials and structures.”, (MS-A.2, 2006). The aim of this test is to get an indication of a mate-
rials resistance towards subﬂorescence caused by soluble salts.
The method is supposed to simulate the moisture dynamic in a wall, where a salt-containing solu-
tion is transported by capillarity from the bottom surface and evaporates from the top which is the
test surface. The test is performed by exposing the bottom surface to a saturated sodium sulfate
solution, where after the test specimen is dried at 20◦C in 50% RH. Sodium sulfate anhydrous is
used for the test method. The cycle of wetting and drying is continued until damage occurs. The
results are the number of cycles the specimen is exposed to and a photographic documentation.
A deviation from the prescribed test method was made. Epoxy was used instead of a rubber sheet
to cover the sides. The epoxy was painted on the test specimen and seal the surfaces to ensure
evaporation would only occur from the test surface. The rubber sheet was not used to prevent
migration on the external surface on the sides of the test specimens.
Theoretical deterioration in this test
The test specimen is contaminated with the saturated sodium sulfate solution from the bottom and
the solution is transported by capillarity to the test surface. During the two hours the test spec-
imen is in contact with the solution, most of the pores in the test specimens are fully saturated
according to the previously performed capillarity experiment in Chapter 3. Since the test surface
is uncovered, evaporation occurs, but since the rate of water supply to the test surface is equal to
the rate of evaporation, the evaporation will remain at the surface. Due to the ﬂow towards the test
==PAGE:53==
8.2. TEST METHOD MS-A.2
37
surface, the advection of ions is accumulating and a large possibility of efﬂorescence is present.
During drying, evaporation will continue from the top surface until the rate of water supply to
the test surface no longer can be sustained. The critical moisture content will be reached and the
evaporation zone will move below the surface leading to the formation of subﬂorescence when the
salt precipitate. When the water begins to evaporate in the climate chamber at 20◦C, the RH will
decrease from the 100% RH for the saturated test specimen to the 50% RH, which is the drying
environment in the climate chamber. When the RH is between approximately between 75-95%,
the sodium sulfate will precipitate to decahydrate, and when the RH is below 75%, the sodium
sulfate will transition to the anhydrous phase. The test specimen are dried for a period of 4 hours
in the climate chamber.
Unspeciﬁed parts in the test method
While performing the test, (MS-A.2, 2006), some uncertainties appeared in the absence of an ex-
plicit test procedure. Due to the interpretations of the procedure made by the performer, the test
results are not considered to be comparable with results obtained at other laboratories.
After a cycle of a total of six hours was performed, no information was given on the intervening
period between cycles. The test specimens were in the present thesis placed in a desiccator with
silica gel to maintain a constant dry environment for the time period. The silica gel absorbs mois-
ture from the surroundings which creates an environment with a constant, low RH. A development
in the surface and the location of efﬂorescence were seen during the intervening period, which in-
dicates a transportation of solution was still active as a result of the specimen were not fully dry.
According to the test method, the cycles are continued until the ﬁrst damage occur. During drying
in the climate chamber and the intervening time, a large amount of efﬂorescence and salt crust
precipitated on the test surface. The efﬂorescence and the salt crust was tried to be removed by
a brush to increase the visibility of the surface to establish the sign of damage. No instruction is
given in the case of large efﬂorescence formation and how to interfere with the test surface in an
appropriate manner. A large amount of efﬂorescence was also formed at the bottom surface of
the test specimen. Since the bottom surface was not sealed evaporation caused large amounts of
efﬂorescence to form. The efﬂorescence was removed by a brush to reduce clogging of the pores.

10 Evaluation of results from the two
test methods
10.1
Aim of the test methods
The results are evaluated based on their aim. They are both standards that are investigating the
resistance of a porous material towards salt crystallization.
The scope from MS-A.2 is to specify a method of indicating the resistance of individual masonry
units to damage caused by sub-surface crystallization of water-soluble salts (crypto efﬂorescence),
MS-A.2, 2006.
The aim from EN 12370 is (..) to assess the relative resistance of natural stones with an open
porosity of greater than 5% (..) to damage caused by the crystallization of salts, (EN 12370,
1999).
Both methods have succeeded in their aim of causing deterioration in the porous material due to
exposure to a salt solution and the relative resistance of different bricks has been established. The
evaluation is not a comparison of the two test methods, but an evaluation of the results when using
two different methods on the same types of ﬁred clay brick.
10.2
Results of the test specimens
A comparison is made between the test samples within the individual test methods and the results
are evaluated based on deterioration pattern as prescribed by the test methods. In addition, the
parameters of a degree of severity and the extension of the deterioration are made from the authors’
point of view. The degree of severity is given as a number from one to four on a scale of not severe
to very severe and the extension is an expression of the deteriorated surface area in percent. The
parameters are by inspiration from Rob van Hees.

11 Evaluation of the current assessment
methods
The results in EN 12370 and MS-A.2 is recorded as the mass change, the number of cycles and a
photographic documentation. The advantages and disadvantages of the current assessment meth-
ods are evaluated according to their usability and the objectiveness.
It is, in general, found that the current assessment methods are more or less inadequate on their
own and a comparison between laboratories is impossible. Guidelines for more consistent photo-
graphic documentation is suggested. The need for a new quantitative method is considered, which
can be supplemented by a photographic documentation.
11.1
Recording the mass change of the test specimens
As part of the results for test method EN 12370, a relative change in mass was recorded. Weighing
the test specimen before and after the test method is a "simple" approach which only requires a
weight as equipment. A single value is obtained for each test specimen which can be gathered as
quantitative data for a sample size of test specimens. The data can be compared across laboratories
without any biased inﬂuence from the performer of the experiment.
When evaluating the deterioration based on the mass change, chromatic changes, cracks and other
kind of deterioration which does not detach from the test specimen are not recorded in the mass
change.
It has been proven to be problematic to interpret the value of the relative change of mass. The value
consists of the mass loss of the material and the accumulated salt from the continuous exposure of
a high salt concentration. The method has therefore proven to be insufﬁcient.
11.2
Count of cycles
As a part of the results for test method MS-A.2, a count of cycles is reported. No special equipment
is needed for the evaluation of the result. The test method is terminated when the ﬁrst damage

appears. No deﬁnition of the damage or the extent of it is given, so the termination of the test is

11.3
Photographic documentation
The photographic documentation was required for both test methods EN 12370 and MS-A.2. Per-
forming a photographic documentation is a qualitative assessment method where all deterioration
on the test surface is documented including chromatic changes, extent and location of the deteri-
oration as well as the type of deterioration. The type of deterioration can be described with the
use of a deterioration atlas, as described in Chapter 7. The deterioration at the surface is of main
interest because the exterior appearance of the ﬁred clay brick exposed to salt deterioration is the
cause of the materials loss in value which is fully illustrated with a photographic documentation.
But to evaluate the deterioration based on photographic documentation is unintentionally biased
by the knowledge and experience within the subject of the performer.
In the case of EN 12370, the deterioration is seen on the entire test specimen and not just on the
test surface. Only the test surface is recorded to minimize the amount of photographic documenta-
tion, and it is initially assumed all surfaces are equally exposed to deterioration. It was conﬁrmed
at the end of the test method because there was no signiﬁcant variations in deterioration between
the 6 surfaces of one test specimen.
During the evaluation of results in Chapter 10, the two parameters severity and degree of dete-
rioration was added to the evaluation of the photographic documentation. Both terms are based
on the observations and are affected by the performer, but a value is given which increases the
possibility of comparison.
To gain the most knowledge from the photographic documentation, some considerations are made.
It is suggested to consider the view of photographic documentation, the equipment settings and
setup of the camera arrangement. It is also suggested to consider the possible size of the ﬁnal
documentation according to the level of detail and to evaluate when to perform the photographic
documentation. No instructions were given in the two test methods on how to perform the photo-
graphic documentation. A common guideline on how to perform photographic documentation is
suggested to ensure comparability between different laboratories.
View of photographic documentation
In the present thesis, it is chosen to document a top view of the test specimens. This is to show
the entire surface equally and to be able to recreate the same view for comparison between the
initial and ﬁnal pictures. When minimizing the uncertainties in the view more information can be
obtained from the comparison. When performing the top view, the depth of the deterioration is
not shown and certain types of damage is not visible. An example of damage could be ﬂakes that
are only partly detached which was seen on the test specimens from MS-A.2. Another example
was seen during the test method EN 12370, where the depth of the deteriorated corners was not

12 Suggestion to an assessment method:
Coloring of the test surface
The need for a development within assessment methods is established in the previous chapter.
The idea for a new assessment method was developed in the middle of the thesis period while per-
forming and evaluating the two test methods, EN 12370 and MS-A.2, and is used as an addition
to visual observations.
One of the issues with the current assessment methods is a large degree of subjectivity when eval-
uating the results by visual observation. The new assessment method aims to have an objective
method that clearly shows the extent and distribution of the deterioration. The deteriorated surface
area can be quantiﬁed and compared across laboratories. The method is easily performed and can
be used with little equipment.
The idea is to color the initial test surface black. When performing the accelerated salt deteriora-
tion method, the color will deteriorate along with the surface of the porous material, and a clear
distinction is seen between the deteriorated surface and non-deteriorated surface. Two different
approaches are discussed to calculate the surface area.
Depending on, which results are required to be obtained, the new assessment method also has some
limitations. The evaluation is made without taking the depth of the deterioration into account and
only determines whether or not a deterioration has occurred. The deterioration must be deeper
than the color absorbed by the porous material, otherwise, it is not shown as a color change on the
ﬁnal surface. The depth of the absorbed color is estimated to be less than 1 millimeter but can be
investigated by further work.

13 Conclusion
The present thesis is an investigation of the current existing test methods and their evaluation of
deterioration caused by salt in four types of ﬁred clay brick. An initial characterization of the ma-
terial properties for different types of ﬁred clay brick was made to be able to decide which bricks
to perform the salt deterioration tests on. The material properties which were experimentally in-
vestigated were the open porosity, bulk density, water absorption and initial rate of absorption.
The bricks, that have been under investigation, are Red Neutral, Black Beauty, DTI 960 and DTI
1060. Red Neutral and Black Beauty were received from Egernsund brickyard. Little variation
is seen between Black Beauty and Red Neutral in the investigated material properties, yet Black
Beauty is in exposure class 4, which is recommended for use in saline environments, whereas Red
Neutral is not. The bricks DTI 960 and DTI 1060 are received from the Danish Technological
Institute. They have been ﬁred at respectively 960◦C and 1060◦C which is their only deviation
from each other. The ﬁring has affected all the investigated material properties and the effect of
the ﬁring temperature is investigated in the test methods.
The test method EN 12370, (EN 12370, 1999), and the RILEM test method MS-A.2, (MS-A.2,
2006), was performed on the bricks. Some uncertainties appeared for both test methods while
they were performed. The test results prescribed for EN 12370 was the relative mass change
and a photographic documentation for all test specimens. The relative mass change resulted in
an increase in mass due to accumulated salt within the test specimens and the evaluation of the
test specimens’ resistance towards salt was based on the photographic documentation. The photo-
graphic documentation was evaluated based on a simpliﬁed deterioration atlas. Red Neutral and
DTI 960 were evaluated to be the least resistance towards salt deterioration, and Black Beauty and
DTI 1060 were the most resistance towards salt deterioration.
The results gained from the test method MS-A.2 were a count of cycles until the test was termi-
nated at sight of initial damage and a photographic documentation. Red Neutral and DTI 960 were
exposed to 8 cycles and Black Beauty and DTI 1060 were exposed to 11 cycles. The photographic
documentation was made for all the test surfaces and evaluated based on a simpliﬁed deterioration
atlases. Red Neutral and DTI 960 were exposed to fewer cycles, are deteriorated at large parts
of the surface and are evaluated as the least resistant bricks. Black Beauty and DTI 1060 were
exposed to more cycles, show little deterioration on the surface and are therefore the most resistant
bricks towards salt deterioration.

CHAPTER 13. CONCLUSION
According to the photographic documentation of the two test methods and the number of cycles
according to MS-A.2, a correlation is seen between the results which both deﬁne Red Neutral
and DTI 960 as the least resistant and Black Beauty and DTI 1060 as the most resistance towards
salt deterioration. The results correlate with the exposure class of Red Neutral and Black Beauty.
For DTI 960 and DTI 1060, the ﬁring at a higher temperature increased the resistance of the ma-
terial towards salt deterioration. An evaluation of the current assessment method is made with
the objective of usability and objectivity. The relative mass change is found less useful due to
the accumulated salt within the material and the count of cycles is found to be biased due to the
lack of deﬁnition of when the initial damage appear and the test method should be terminated. A
guideline for photographic documentation is suggested where the view of the picture, the setup of
the camera and the size of the picture is considered.
Based on the evaluated assessment method, the need for a new quantitative method is identiﬁed.
Therefore in the present thesis, it is suggested to color the initial test surface to make an unbi-
ased division between deteriorated surfaces and non-deteriorated surfaces on the test specimen.
Further, coloring of the surface was to be made with the aim to inﬂuence the test surface as little
as possible. A permanent marker was chosen to color the surface because it was considered the
least sealing option. Two test specimens of the type DTI 960 were colored on the test surface
and were exposed to the test method of EN 12370, and two test specimens of the type DTI 960
were colored on the test surface and were exposed to the test method MS-A.2. The test speci-
mens were compared to their equivalent non-colored type of brick for each test method, and the
deterioration patterns were found to correlate between the colored and the non-colored test speci-
men. The deteriorated surface area was then calculated and two different methods were suggested
which previously have been used in another context. One method is to either cut the areas of the
specimens circumference and deteriorated surface in paper, ﬁnd the mass of the pieces of paper
and calculate the percentage of surface area, or to measure the surface area with a planimeter. The
other method is to calculate the deteriorated area by node count. A mesh with a hundred nodes are
illustrated on the test surface, and the nodes which are in the deteriorated area are counted. The
examination of the surface area found by the mass of paper showed a larger deteriorated surface
area for the test specimens exposed EN 12370 than the area found by node count, which instead
showed a larger deterioration area for the test specimens exposed to MS-A.2. The planimeter
method was not performed due to missing equipment. The most appropriate method to ﬁnd the
surface area is still to be identiﬁed and a larger sample size would be necessary.
It was investigated if the coloring of the surface with a permanent marker affects the water vapour
properties of the test surface or in any way changes its properties by performing a permeability
test. A test setup was made with six non-colored surfaces and six colored surfaces. The water
vapour permeability for the non-colored surface was found to be 11.4E−12 kg/(m·s·Pa) and for
the colored surface to be 11.3E−12 kg/(m·s·Pa). According to the t-test, there is no signiﬁcant
difference between the two permeability averages for the colored and non-colored test specimens.
