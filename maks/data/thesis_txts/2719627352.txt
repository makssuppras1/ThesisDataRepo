Abstract
The reliability of hearing aid devices under various climatic conditions is one of the crit-
ical factors for manufacturers. Field failure analysis of a large number of hearing aids
from different markets revealed that failures were caused due to exposure to various en-
vironmental conditions such as humidity, temperature, and human sweat contaminants,
which caused the degradation of conformal coating, corrosion of the hearing aid circuitry
components, hand solder joints, microphones, battery contacts, Bluetooth antennas, and
many other components. Although different corrosion test methods are used for hearing
aids and their components, they are insufficient to reproduce failures seen in the field.
Therefore, a new test method was investigated and developed to create field exposure
conditions and reproduce field corrosion failures at an accelerated rate.
Chapter 1 gives insight into the climatic reliability of hearing aids, current corrosion test
methods, and the motivation behind the current project. Chapter 2 provides a compre-
hensive literature review discussing the factors that affect the reliability of hearing aids
during the service in the field and the manufacturing process. Furthermore, the various
corrosion failure mechanism commonly occurs in electronics are presented in this chap-
ter, followed by corrosion protection techniques by conformal coatings. This chapter also
gives an overview of hearing aid failures from the field with a brief description of corrosion
failure mechanisms and factors that caused those failures. The commonly used corrosion
test methods for testing electronics are also described in Chapter 2.
The research methodology in the study, divided into four parts, is described in Chapters 3,
4, 5, and 6. Chapter 3 describes the investigation of currently used corrosion test methods
by comparing the test results to the field failures. The tests were performed on hearing
aid metal and plated metal components. The results revealed that the sweat spray test
method is the most suitable for replicating corrosion observed in the field. Subsequent
Chapter 4 presents the investigation conducted to evaluate the performance of liquid-
based and gas-phase conformal coating protection applied on PCBA. The test method
consisting of sweet spray test, humidity/temperature cycle in climatic chamber combined
with DC leak current test was used for the study. The high leakage current and amount of
corrosion were observed on PCBAs protected with a liquid-based conformal coating. The
SEM/EDS analysis showed that a high percentage of failures was caused due to poor
conformal coating coverage and galvanic corrosion due to increased porosity in the Au-
layer. Similar results were observed on corroded PCBA in the field, confirming the validity
of the used test method. Chapter 5 describes the investigation conducted to test hearing
aids at the device level. The test methodology was validated from the PCBA level testing.
The test was performed on three different hearing aid types to identify the weak points in
HA design towards corrosion. Device A showed the highest failure rate due to capillary
areas, poor conformal coating coverage, and galvanic corrosion. The components which
failed the most were microphones, hand-soldered joints, and ENIG-plated copper pads.
Finally, Chapter 6 describes the method used to investigate and formulate a new artificial
sweat solution that can produce all corrosion product colors seen in the field. The new
artificial sweat solution was developed using the Design of Experiments approach to find
how pH value, NaCl, lactic acid, and acetic acid content affect the corrosion behavior of
hearing aid components. The sweat spray test was used in the investigation, and the
tests were performed on a Bluetooth antenna, a component made of Cu-Ni-Zn alloy. As
a result, the sweat solution with a neutral pH value, low NaCl content, and high lactic and
acetic acid content resulted in corrosion similar to the field. The overall conclusion of the
study is described in Chapter 7, and suggestions for future work are described in Chapter
8.

Introduction
As the level of technology increases, hearing aids (HAs) become more automatic and
have various features which make their design miniaturized and more complex. As a re-
sult, the demand for highly reliable products that can resist multiple service conditions
has increased, posing an extensive research challenge for manufacturers. Reliability
testing helps to identify issues that may degrade a product’s field performance and char-
acterize the risk of failure under various conditions. Furthermore, reliability testing pro-
vides unique insights into product failures and enables engineers to make suggestions
for improving reliability through design, material selection, and corrosion protection strate-
gies.
Figure 1.1: Example of HA miniature and complex design
Hearing aids are frequently exposed to extreme climatic conditions such as high humidity
and temperature, as well as the combination of human sweat and ambient conditions, all of
which impose stress on HA components and trigger various corrosion processes in the de-
vices. Corrosion is the deterioration of materials by chemical or electrochemical reactions
with their environment. Numerous published papers on the corrosion reliability of elec-
tronics demonstrate that miniaturization, in combination with unfavorable material com-
binations, potential bias, humidity levels, and various service and manufacturing-related
contamination, can cause significant corrosion reliability issues [1, 2, 3]. The greatest
influence on the chances of HA surviving during use without serious corrosion can be
created by including the corrosion causes during the product planning and testing phase.
Hearing aid corrosion resistance is evaluated using various accelerated corrosion test
methods. These test methods are specifically designed to study the corrosion resistance
of electronics intended for usage in the automotive, military, and other industries. As
a result, when it comes to hearing aids, the majority of these methodologies can lack
correlation to the field results.
The study aimed to investigate and create a new laboratory corrosion test method capable
of simulating hearing aid failures from the field and thereby predicting product reliability
under various environmental conditions. The test method was designed using electronic
reliability standards and considering the environmental conditions present to the device
in the field.

1.1
Background
Hearing aids are miniature, high-tech electronic devices designed to provide a better life
for people suffering from hearing loss. They are worn daily and are exposed to various
environmental conditions. The plastic housing with all the critical HA components hangs
behind the ear in a region that tends to trap sweat and moisture. Moisture and sweat
enter the device through small openings/gaps and accumulate on the surface of electronic
components, initiating the electrochemical corrosion process. Furthermore, condensation
and evaporation during temperature cycling, as well as the presence of contaminants in
the atmosphere such as chlorides and sulfur-containing compounds, can greatly increase
the corrosion rate of electronic components.
The demand for micro-design has resulted in a tremendous reduction in the size of elec-
tronic components and assemblies, which has significantly reduced corrosion reliability for
HAs. A HA device’s main component is a flexible printed circuit board (FPCB) on which in-
tegrated circuits (ICs) and other electrical components are installed and connected. The
distance between various components on FPCBA is reduced down to the micrometer
scale, thereby increasing the electric field and increasing the formation of corrosion cells
between bias points during local condensation in humid conditions [4]. If two dissimilar
metals remain electrically connected and bridged with an electrolyte, galvanic corrosion
occurs, resulting in the dissolution of metal with lower electrochemical potential [5]. More-
over, HA manufacturers have moved to rechargeable Li-ion batteries that use power sup-
ply modules (PSM) consisting of charging and voltage step-down circuity which has a
huge effect on the corrosion reliability of HA devices [6].
The synergetic effect of various metal/alloys combination, miniaturization, a potential dif-
ference between biased points on PCBA, and humidity conditions enhance the formation
of an electrochemical cell locally on the PCBA surface and cause a variety of corrosion
failure mechanisms such as galvanic corrosion, creep corrosion, electrochemical migra-
tion (ECM), surface insulation resistance (SIR) reduction and other types of bias induced
corrosion [5, 6, 7].
The water layer formed on the PCBA surface is not a good electrolyte due to its low con-
ductivity (high resistivity), but the presence of diverse contamination, either from service
(chlorine from human perspiration, SO2, dust) or introduced to the PCBA surface during
the manufacturing process (flux residues), may increase water conductivity and cause a
drop in SIR due to leak current [1, 6]. Most of these ionic residues can increase water
absorption due to their hygroscopic nature and, in solution, they will increase leakage
current and corrosion, such as ECM [5]. Conformal coatings are applied on PCBA during
the manufacturing process in order to protect the electronic components from an exter-
nal corrosive environment. Conformal coating is a thin polymeric film used to protect
PCBA, soldered joints, and other electronic components from environmental conditions.
Sometimes, the service conditions are severe, which causes adhesion loss at the inter-
face between the coating and the surface so water can reach the metal surfaces and
initiate corrosion. Corrosion can cause intermittent malfunction or permanent failures of
an electrical system by creating unintended electrical breaks, leaks, or shorts between
conductive paths [1, 6, 8].
Various test methods are used for the corrosion testing of electronics. Single component-
level testing of metal and plated metal components is undeniably important in ensuring
that the material meets all requirements for specific applications prior to component in-
stallation in a sub-component or product. PCB-level testing introduces much more vari-
ability due to the introduction of multiple components, laminates, solders, etc. PCBA-level

testing is typically performed using various electrochemical corrosion techniques under
accelerated exposure conditions to better understand corrosion reactions and mecha-
nisms and their correlation with exposure conditions [6]. This test is generally combined

performed under accelerated field exposure conditions to assess the corrosion reliability

The current corrosion test methods can not account for all corrosion mechanisms involved

problems to manufacturers in terms of high-cost repairs and affect their market value due
to customer dissatisfaction. Root cause analysis of hearing aid device failure from the
field can provide useful information about field conditions that cause corrosion [6]. Such
findings can be used as a basis for the design of a new corrosion test method that can
mimic field failures by creating field exposure conditions.
1.2
Motivation for the project
This project was inspired by interesting findings reported in [6], investigations of corrosion
failures in hearing aid devices under environmental conditions [10, 11], and in general due
to the lack of methods for testing the corrosion reliability of hearing aid devices. Analysis
of field failures revealed that hearing aid components such as battery contacts, FPCBA,
hand solderings, LED, and Bluetooth antenna were attacked severely by various corro-
sion processes during service. Since severe corrosion of hearing aid devices were seen
in the field, it was understood that the current test methods are insufficient in addressing
these failures and finding solutions to prevent them. Thus, it was of utmost importance
to do a detailed root cause failure analysis to identify the corrosion failure mechanism
and its causes. The cause of corrosion can reveal the nature of exposure conditions and
corrosion-causing factors. In addition, the analysis of corrosion products can reveal the
concentration and types of corrosion-causing residues, corrosion modes, and their effect.
This information can be used to compare the current corrosion test methods and develop
a new and reliable lab-scale corrosion test method that can accelerate the corrosion con-
ditions to produce field failures within a short period.
Analysis of corroded areas from the field failed samples conducted using a light optical
microscope and scanning electron microscope (SEM) [6] revealed that large amounts of
NaCl residues were present within devices in all analyzed markets. The corrosion product
had primarily mud-crack and porous morphology. The prevalent mechanism for corrosion
failures of various HA components was delamination and degradation of conformal coat-
ing caused by wet conditions on the surface, possibly due to moisture layer formation
due to temperature change and human sweat intrusion. Based on these findings, the ef-
fect of three factors is essential to investigate and introduce in the development of a new
corrosion test method:
1. Sweat application
2. Humidity and temperature exposure
3. Chemical composition of the sweat
Based on how the sweat is applied to the components, there are different tests currently
used to test hearing aids and their components: immersion, sweat spray, accelerated
corrosion, atmospheric, and humidity & heat tests. However, some of these methods

lactic acid cause corrosion of Cu, Ni, and Sn materials commonly used in electronics [15,

compounds on the corrosion of hearing aid components and to propose a new artificial
sweat solution.
1.3
Research Aims & Objectives
The aim of this research was to develop and validate a corrosion test method that can
mimic field failures of hearing aid devices and their components with correct acceleration
factors applied. The sweat residues discovered inside field-failed devices, as well as the
humidity/temperature exposure conditions, were identified as the corrosion acceleration
factors. The research was based on two tasks:
1. Development of sweat corrosion setup based on sweat application and humidi-
ty/temperature exposure
2. Finding the correct chemistry of artificial sweat based on investigation of pH and
different elements from the sweat
During the formulation of the study methodology, it was stated that the testing would be
carried out at three levels: the single component, PCBA, and device level. This separation
would allow the study to gain a comprehensive understanding of the behavior of each com-
ponent when individually exposed to corrosive conditions. Single-component or material-
level testing was needed to understand corrosion reactions and mechanisms happening
when the component is in contact with sweat. Since the PCBA consists of multiple compo-
nents, solderings, and conformal coating, PCBA-level testing was needed to investigate
the synergetic effect of the corrosion-inducing factors (sweat, humidity, temperature, bias)
and PCBA design parameters on electrochemical corrosion failures. Device-level testing
was required to check the overall performance of the electronic design under acceler-
ated field exposure conditions. The main advantage of device-level testing is that the
weak points in the design can be identified such as capillary areas, the effect of dissimilar

2.1
Conditions affecting the reliability of hearing aids
Exposure to various environmental conditions has a significant impact on the climatic re-
liability of electronics. Humidity and temperature variations caused by weather changes
and day-night cycling can result in the formation of a water layer on electronic components
that will initiate the electrochemical process [6, 19]. Electrochemical corrosion occurs on
electronic components in a locally created aqueous environment due to the combination
of four factors: (i) humid conditions, (ii) various metal/alloys, (iii) applied potential bias,
and (iv) ionic contamination on the PCBA surface, whether introduced during the manu-
facturing process or field operation [5, 20]. Figure 2.1 shows how the synergetic effect
of ionic contamination on the PCBA surface, moisture, potential bias, and various metal
combinations, leads to galvanic and electrolytic corrosion of electronic components.
Figure 2.1: Main factors causing corrosion development in electronics [6]
The amount of water on the surface is determined by the amount of moisture in the air as
well as the surface morphology and surface energy of the components where the water
is retained [21]. An electronic device is composed of a variety of materials with differ-
ent surface energies. Metals and ceramics are examples of hydrophilic surfaces with
high-surface energy that attracts water. On the contrary, polymers and PCB laminates
are examples of low-surface energy materials with hydrophobic surfaces that repeal wa-
ter from the surface. However, high roughness, surface defects (cracks), and filler type
can increase the surface energy of the polymers and PCB laminates and make them hy-
drophilic surfaces. All kinds of narrow cracks on PCB are very harmful with regard to the
corrosion of electronic devices. The capillary force draws liquids into the cracks and since
the vapor pressure of liquids in the capillaries is lower than in an open space, even thin
layers of water remain in capillary conditions.
Ionic contaminants present on the PCBA surface can increase the conductivity of water
and cause high corrosion rates. These contaminants are usually hygroscopic in nature
and can increase water absorption and cause high leakage current and ECM failures
[3, 22, 23]. Furthermore, the temperature profile of the electrical components inside the

Moisture is a liquid (water) found in trace amounts in the environment, such as condensed

are related to moisture. Because colder air is less able to hold water vapor than warm air,

humidity has a higher dew point temperature compared to the lower relative humidity at
the same temperature.
Human perspiration is also affected by the dew point. Sweat evaporates quickly when the
dew point is low because there is more room in the air for more water molecules. When
the dew point is high, sweat accumulates on the skin and cannot evaporate because the
air is already saturated with water vapor molecules [25].

2.1.2
Contamination effects on electronics
Contamination is any unwanted impurity that is present on or in electronic systems, cir-
cuits, or components but was not intended to be there. Unwanted impurities can be solid
(dust, dirt) and liquid (lubricants, cleaning agents, polymer additives, etching chemicals,
sweat, others), and gasses (sulfur, carbon, nitrogen, others). Depending on the contam-
ination characteristics, amount, and location, they can have a destructive effect on the
reliability of electronics [21]. There are two basic categories in which all potential sources
of contamination can be classified:
1. Contamination induced by the manufacturing process
2. Contamination from the user environment
The primary sources of contamination in the manufacturing process are those that are
introduced during the fabrication of bare PCBs and later during the PCB assembly pro-
cess, including etching, soldering, storage, etc. Additionally, since electronic products like
hearing aids are used in various environments, there are numerous potential sources of
contamination, such as particles in the air, environmental pollutants, and body fluids [6, 7,
21]. The potential sources of contamination that could have an impact on the performance
of electronics are briefly shown in Figure 2.3.

PCB fabrication processes, during PCB assembly, and general handling during packag-
ing, storage, or testing. A blank PCB board is the basic component of the PCB on which
other electrical components are placed and connected. The substrate layer of PCB is
made of resin (usually glass fibers and epoxy) in which a thin layer of conductive (copper)
foil is laminated using heat. The copper layer is protected with a solder mask which is
usually green epoxy coating.
Polymer additives such as lubricants, stabilizers, and flame retardants are added to resins
in order to improve the physical and chemical properties of the material. Those addi-
tives together with air can be trapped in the PCB substrate during resin preparation and

2.2
Corrosion failure mechanisms
Humidity and various contaminants introduced during manufacturing or service can cause
a variety of corrosion failure mechanisms. This section discusses the most common cor-
rosion failures in hearing aids that are relevant to this research.
2.2.1
Uniform corrosion
Uniform corrosion results from metal’s direct contact with liquid and gasses. The chemical
reaction includes direct electron transfer from the metal to the cations of the contacting
media [48]. If electrical components are not protected, they can suffer from direct corrosive
attacks from the environment. Copper can be the best example of a material used in
electronic devices. When copper is oxidized, red cuprous oxide (Cu2O) or black cupric
oxide (CuO) will form. Cu2O is the initial corrosion product that forms on copper in an
atmospheric environment. When Cl−ions are present in the solution, the passive cuprous
oxide film breaks, resulting in the formation of copper chloride complexes such as CuCl2,
which have a turquoise blue color  [49]. The formed chemical compounds will decrease
the conductivity of copper and its alloy. Ammonia can corrode copper and its alloys,
particularly when other ions in solution, such as Cl−, are present, resulting in green to
blue corrosion deposits [48].

Nickel has a high resistance to uniform corrosion because it readily forms a thin but pro-
tective oxide film on its surface. However, Ni can be attacked in acidic conditions in the
presence of strong oxidants, forming black nickel oxide or green nickel salts [47, 48].
Silver is another metal that can oxidize if the environment contains sulfur compounds.
The brown/black silver sulfide corrosion products can form [48, 50]. In NaCl solutions,
the precipitation of AgCl will occur due to higher anodic overpotential [51, 52, 53].
Other metals commonly used in electronics (Sn, Au, Al, stainless steel) have good resis-

2.2.2
Reduction in Surface insulation resistance (SIR) and leakage current
When electronic devices are operated in a humid environment, moisture can adsorb onto
and diffuse into the PCBA surface, reducing the resistance of insulation material and
promoting leakage current flow. The amount of adsorbed water depends primarily on hu-
midity, temperature, surface morphology, and the type and amount of ionic contamination
present on the surface [6, 54, 55]. The leakage current is a term for the unwanted flow of
current from one conductor to the opposite conductor along various conductive paths [20].
The leakage current between two conducting points can flow through the PCB laminate,
on the PCB surface, or through the adsorbed water layer [20]. When contaminations ei-
ther from the manufacturing process or environment are introduced in/on PCB substrate
material, there will be an increase in leakage current because most of these contami-
nations are polar in nature, e.g. salt from human sweat and flux residues. The failures
caused by SIR reduction are difficult to detect and analysis of root failure causes requires
knowledge about manufacturing, field exposure conditions, and PCBA design [20].
Verdingovas et al. [22] investigated the effect of NaCl on a single chip capacitor on PCBA
with 5 V potential bias under humid conditions. As shown in Figure 2.9(A) and Figure
2.9(B), the leakage current was more pronounced at higher humidity levels (90, 95, and
98 % RH). The samples contaminated with µg · cm−2 NaCl showed intermittent leak-
age current fluctuations compared to uncontaminated samples. Figure 2.9(C) depicts the
change in leakage current measured on capacitors that were uncontaminated and con-
taminated with varying NaCl concentrations throughout the transition from dry (25 ◦C, 60
% RH) to fully condensing conditions achieved by cooling the PCBA from 25 ◦C to 17.5
◦C. Increases in NaCl concentrations increased not just the magnitude of the current
but also the time (representing temperature) at which it occurred. The increase in current
was observed in the noncontaminated samples at the very last step when the tempera-
ture was around 17.5 ◦C. However, with increased contamination, a smaller temperature
difference was necessary to achieve the dew-point and increase leakage current [20, 22].
SIR testing is frequently used to investigate the effect of contaminants along with temper-
ature and humidity on the amount of leakage current generated. Previous studies used
SIR testing to estimate conformal coating protection on coated SIR PCBs by measuring
leakage current, where the degree of the leakage current indicates coating degradation
[12, 56, 57].

2.2.4
Conductive Anodic Filament Growth (CAF)
Conductive anodic filament (CAF) is a standard electrochemical failure mode within the
PCB laminate that occurs between two biased conductors, one being anodic with exposed
copper. Moisture ingress into the PCB laminate and cyclic temperature exposure will
increase the residual stresses within PCB laminate which eventually leads to epoxy-glass
fiber debonding and exposing copper conductors. In a presence of an electrolyte, the
anode dissolves, and copper ions migrate along the glass/resin interface to the cathode
forming a conductive copper salt filament. These filaments have usually black or dark
brown color which indicates the presence of copper oxidation products, such as cupric
oxide [6, 63]. CAF failures can manifest in high leakage current, intermittent electrical
shorts, and dielectric breakdown between conductors in PCB [64].
In essence, four prerequisites for the occurrence of CAF are conductors with exposed
copper, an electrolyte to bridge the conductors, voltage bias to drive the reaction, and a
pathway for the ions to move from the anode to the cathode [63, 64].
Figure 2.12 (a) illustrates the formation of CAF between adjacent vias (i.e. copper plated
holes), and Figure 2.12 (b) shows the nature of CAF formation around the fibers in PCB
laminate.

Many other factors can accelerate CAF formation, including high humidity, contaminants,
high temperature, repeated thermal cycles, high potential bias, the distance between elec-
trodes, materials with high moisture absorption, porosity in PCB laminate, and more [6,
63]. Conformal coatings are commonly used to suppress CAF growth and prevent the
bridging of biased conductors by water films [63].
2.2.5
Galvanic corrosion
Galvanic corrosion is the most common electrochemical corrosion encountered on PCBA
due to the dissimilar selection of metals/alloys. The mechanism of galvanic corrosion is
illustrated in Figure 2.13. When two metals with different standard electrode potentials
are bridged with an electrolyte, the less noble metal will start to dissolve and undergo a
corrosion attack. The nobility of metal is inversely proportional to its propensity to form
ions. Because of its resistance to ion formation, gold is considered a noble metal, whereas
magnesium and zinc form ions efficiently and are thus considered very reactive or less
noble metals [48].
The presence of an aqueous liquid on the surface is the trigger for the occurrence of
galvanic corrosion and the following factors will intensify the formation of galvanic cells
within electronic systems [66]:

2.3
Corrosion protection techniques
Applying either conformal coatings (thin polymer layer) or potting (thick polymer layer)
to the PCBA surface is a typical method for protecting electronic components from envi-
ronmental conditions. PCBA protection using these strategies is briefly explained in the
sections that follow.
2.3.1
Conformal coating
A thin film of a polymer called conformal coating is used to shield electronic components,
circuit boards, and other devices from harsh environmental factors like moisture, contam-
ination, temperature changes, and mechanical stresses. Conformal coatings can be ap-
plied onto PCBA surface either in liquid form or by vacuum deposition. The standard IPC-
CC-830, ”Qualification and Performance of Electrical Insulating Compound for Printed
Board Assemblies” classifies conformal coating as the following types: acrylic, epoxy,
silicone, polyurethane, and fluoropolymer. The conformal coating’s performance is deter-
mined by the coating’s adhesion to the substrate surface [6]. Therefore, prior to applying
the coating, the PCBA surface must be thoroughly cleaned of production residues. The
sections that follow provide a brief overview of various types of conformal coating, their
properties, and application methods.
Acrylic
Acrylic conformal coatings are thermoplastic acrylic resins dissolved in a suitable solvent.
These coatings offer good all-around protection, are simple to apply and repair, and are
reasonably inexpensive. Moreover, acrylic coatings have excellent dielectric properties,
and decent moisture and abrasion resistance. Additionally, they are easily applied on
PCB surfaces by dipping or spraying. A variety of solvents can promptly and efficiently
remove acrylic coatings, frequently without the need for agitation. This makes field repairs,
including rework, practicable and cost-effective. Acrylic coatings, on the other hand, are
a bad choice when the working environment demands resistance to chemical solvents or
acidic or alkaline solutions [9, 70, 71].
Epoxy
Epoxy coatings are rigid coatings that have good adhesion to the PCBA surface and pro-
vide very high resistance to humidity. All epoxy resins have oxirane functional groups,
which have a strained three-member C-O-C rigid ring structure. They provide strong

abrasion resistance and very good mechanical strength. These coatings are exceed-
ingly challenging to remove off the surface due to their chemical resistance to different
solvents. Moreover, due to the rigidity, epoxy coatings are not suitable for flexible printed
circuit boards [9].
Silicone
Silicone coatings are made of elastomeric silicone polymers. Because of their internal
structure [9], these coating types are flexible and offer a high degree of heat resistance,
making them an ideal candidate for use at high temperatures. Silicone coatings may be
applied by spray, dip, or brush method. The disadvantage of using silicone coatings is
related to the high permeation rate for atmospheric constituents such as moisture, oxy-
gen, and other gaseous elements due to the flexible Si-O bond angle [9]. Furthermore,
because of its rubbery nature, silicone conformal coating is not abrasion resistant, but this
property makes it resilient to vibrational stresses. In high-humidity environments, silicone
coatings are commonly used for applications such as outdoor signage. Removal can be
difficult, requiring specialized solvents, long soak times, and agitation with a brush or an
ultrasonic bath [71].
Urethane (Polyurethane)
Polyurethane coating is the outcome of a chemical reaction between a polyol resin (known
as the base) and a poly-isocyanate curing agent (known as the hardener). The chemical
structure of polyurethane is highly cross-linked and has a very high molecular weight.
They require a high curing temperature and can take up to 24 hours to reach 90% of
their end properties and 7 days to complete the entire cross-linking process. Coating
adhesion to the substrate surface is excellent, but surfaces with poor bondability require
adequate surface preparation [70]. Urethane conformal coatings are well-known for their
high resistance to moisture and chemicals. They are both abrasion- and solvent-resistant,
resulting in coatings that are extremely difficult to remove.
Fluoropolymer
Fluoropolymer coating is made of fluorocarbons and applied onto PCBA surface by a
dipping process. There are known for very good dielectric properties, and provide excel-
lent protection against humidity due to their high hydrophobic properties. Fluoropolymer
coatings, on the other hand, have low solvent resistance, making them unsuitable for
applications requiring high chemical resistance [9].
Parylene
Parylene coatings are solid and applied via vacuum deposition, in contrast to conformal
coatings that have previously been discussed and are applied in a liquid form. The vac-
uum deposition of the polymer results in a pinhole-free coating film that truly conforms to
the assembly contour. Film thickness is the same on sharp edges as on flat surfaces,
unlike for liquid coatings where edges become exposed due to polymer shrinkage (Fig-
ure 2.15). The penetration characteristics are exceptional, ensuring complete coverage in
crevices [70]. The parylene coatings have excellent dieletric properties and offer excellent
resistance to moisture, chemicals, and mechanical stresses. However, only a few pary-
lene coatings, such as Parylene D and Parylene HD, are suitable for use at temperatures
above 100 ◦C [9].

lating polymer such as liquid epoxies, urethanes, silicones, and polysulfides. To remove
voids in a polymer that can later trap moisture, the potting process requires de-airing a
mixed liquid in vacuum conditions prior to pouring [70]. The thickness of the potting layer

2.4.2
Analysis of field failures of hearing aid devices
The failure analysis of HA devices from different markets representing various climatic
conditions was conducted by [6, 10, 11] in order to understand the root cause of corrosion
in hearing aid components. Although there are various types of hearing aids, this failure
analysis covered ninety typical Behind-The-Ear types of hearing aid collected from differ-
ent markets. The selection of corrosion failures from the field was based on three criteria:
i) high current consumption, ii) visual observation of corrosion products inside the device,
and iii) performance degradation of microphones. Initial failure analysis of HA compo-
nents was carried out using visual inspection (with a light optical microscope) and a device
performance test (DPT). The failure mechanisms and causes were investigated using a
scanning electron microscope (SEM) with energy dispersive x-ray spectroscopy (EDS).
Furthermore, statistical analysis was used to summarize and conclude on the identified
failure parts of HAs, failure probability and percentage for different device components,
and types of contamination present inside the device.
The following was concluded after summarizing the findings of two hearing aid failure
research studies, one from tropical markets [10] and one from three markets (Europe,
Japan, and the United States) [11]:
• High failure percentage of microphones due to the deposition of corrosion products
and contaminants.
• High amount of corrosion was observed on FPCB, hand solderings, battery contacts,
and SMT components. The major corrosion mechanism identified was galvanic cor-
rosion due to a combination of various metals/alloys and localized corrosion caused
by conformal coating failure.
• Failures were synergistically affected by moisture, temperature, and human sweat.
A high amount of sweat residues in form of sodium chloride, and especially chlorine
(Cl) entwined in corrosion products were found in all the corroded areas inside the
HA device. Humidity/temperature can cause a high rate perspiration rate in humans
and creates a water layer inside the device.
A recent analysis of failed devices in the field [74] also reported failures of Bluetooth
antennas and MEMS microphones due to corrosion.

2.5
Testing climatic reliability of electronics
Electronic technology is rapidly progressing in both miniaturization and complexity. A
smaller design means higher-density packed components and more opportunity for er-
ror due to corrosion. Standard reliability testing copes with tackling all of the new design
emerging challenges. New product designs require adapted corrosion testing to ensure
that products meet desired reliability goals. The Physics of Failure (PoF) based root cause
failure analysis technique can identify potential corrosion causes by analyzing the failed
corroded parts and components [6]. The cause of corrosion can reveal the nature of ex-
posure conditions, and flaws in the design. The information from field failure analysis can
help better select and optimize parameters during climatic reliability testing. The climatic
reliability testing is done at the single component (material level), PCBA, and device level.

Single component level testing is done to evaluate material quality in contact with the en-

2.5.1
Single component level testing
Single-component level testing is used mainly to investigate the corrosion resistance of
metal and plated metal materials used in electronics in contact with various chemicals and
under exposure to atmospheric conditions. Immersion and potentiodynamic polarization
testing is used for the investigation of corrosion resistance of metal and plated metal
parts, while accelerated atmospheric, and salt spray tests can be used for both device
and component level testing.
Immersion test
Immersion tests are frequently used to quickly assess the corrosion resistance of metals
in aqueous solutions. The metal sample is fully or partially immersed in a defined solution
30
Development of corrosion test methods for replicating field failures of hearing aids
==PAGE:44==
(salt water, acid, etc.) under ambient conditions or at elevated temperatures and pres-
sures. After the tests, the samples are examined for signs of corrosion including coating
degradation, cracking, and adhesion loss. Examples of standard immersion tests are EN
ISO 11130, ISO 17025, ASTM D 6943-03.
Potentiodynamic polarization testing
Potentiodynamic polarization testing is a DC electrochemical test method widely used to
investigate the sensitivity of metals and alloys to localized and pitting corrosion in a cer-
tain media. It is a very useful method to study the corrosion behavior of solder alloys,
battery contacts, ENIG, and other metallic and plated materials used for electronic com-
ponents. The test is performed using a potentiostat in a three-electrode setup including
a working electrode (WE), reference electrode (RE), and auxiliary electrode (AE). Three
important parameters in this test are potential, current, and time. The potential is applied
to the test electrode where the reduction takes place resulting in the generation of current.
The test result is a polarization curve represented as the plot of potential versus current
density. Critical current density, passive potential, and passive current density can be
extracted from this test. Different standards related to the working principle and practice
methodology for potentiodynamic polarization can be found online.
Accelerated atmospheric corrosion test
The accelerated atmospheric corrosion test is used to evaluate the performance of metal-
lic materials under various environmental conditions. The test is carried out by introducing
a corrosive environment in a controlled chamber at an elevated temperature for several
days. The test results provide information that can be used to select suitable materials
and coating for a particular application. Standards for conducting atmospheric corrosion
tests on metals include ISO 8565 and ASTM-G50.
Salt spray test
The salt spray test is a corrosion test method that is used to assess the corrosion re-
sistance of materials and surface coatings in a salt environment. The testing equipment
consists of a closed test chamber in which a saltwater solution is vaporized using a spray
nozzle and pressurized air. During the test, the samples are exposed to continuous salt
mist creation, which some electronics do not require. As a result, salt tests are frequently
combined with climatic testing and are referred to as modified salt spray tests. The mod-
ified salt spray test is designed to mimic and accelerate the effects of naturally occurring
corrosion. ASTM G85 is a test standard that includes numerous modified salt spray tests
that are variants of the original salt spray test.
2.5.2
PCBA level testing
Compared to the single components level testing, testing at the PCB level introduces
much more reliability due to the introduction of multiple components (materials), PCB
laminate, solderings, and conformal coatings. Component-level testing for PCBA com-
bines the use of device-level test methods (such as salt spray and temperature & humid-
ity tests) and electrochemical measurement techniques, which can measure SIR, leakage
current, surface potential, and impedance of the surface (electrode-electrolyte interface).
The tests are performed on test boards that represent the actual PCBA with similar SM
components, SIR pattern, manufacturing process, and coating protection. The intention
of PCB level testing is to investigate corrosion mechanisms such as ECM, the cleanliness
of the flux residue on the PCBA, and the corrosion resistance of PCBA to atmospheric
contaminants under cyclic climatic conditions.
Temperature & Humidity test
Temperature and humidity tests are performed to evaluate the performance of materials,
electrical components, and devices in a humid environment. These tests are usually per-
formed at constant or cycling temperature and humidity, and it is also common practice
Development of corrosion test methods for replicating field failures of hearing aids
31
==PAGE:45==
to combine temperature and humidity with voltage bias. Temperature and humidity bias
(THB) tests are used to assess the likelihood of electrochemical-induced failures in elec-
tronic devices in humidity conditions. The aim is to create a condensing environment and
activate water penetration through the external materials (encapsulant), or the protective
coating to metallic conductors and produce degradation or failures [9]. Electrical mea-
surements are taken during the test. The device is considered as failed if the parametric
limitations (leakage current) are exceeded. Furthermore, the appearance of corrosion
on the parts under test, as well as the appearance of visual defects, can be a sufficient
reason for rejecting the parts.
Surface Insulation Resistance (SIR) testing of PCBA surfaces
A commonly used technique for studying the effect of humidity and temperature on PCBA
is a measurement of surface insulation resistance (SIR). The principle of this technique
is similar to the potentiostatic method, where the DC voltage is applied to the system
and the resulting current from the system is monitored [9]. Hence, the resistance in an
electrical circuit can be calculated from Ohm’s law, R=V/I. SIR testing is usually carried
out in the climatic chamber under constant or cyclic temperature/humidity conditions. In
general, this testing is performed to evaluate the risk of ECM, and leakage current values
indicate the performance of the conformal coating [6]. The testing is usually performed
on industry standard test boards or patterns specially designed for process testing pur-
poses. Various IPC standard test methods provide information about test conditions and
failure evaluation criteria for PCBA [79]. The use of standard test patterns can be very
challenging in practice because PCBA layout differs for each product. Therefore, in order
to obtain reliable results, the test boards should be similar to the ones used in the field in
terms of material and circuitry patterns [9].
Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) testing
Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) is an AC technique that measures the
electrical resistance (impedance) of a metal/solution interface over a wide frequency range
with a small amplitude voltage [80]. Unlike DC test methods, EIS can distinguish various
components of the electrochemical system under test and determine polarization resis-
tance (low-frequency region), the solution resistance (high-frequency-region) and capaci-
tance of double layer [9, 80]. The principle of impedance measurement and test procedure
can be found elsewhere online.
EIS was also proven to be useful for studying the performance of conformal coatings
under humid conditions and in presence of hygroscopic processes and service-related
contaminations [12, 22]. EIS measures the impedance of the coating at various frequen-
cies. The impedance value is an indication of the barrier effect of the coating. In corrosive
environments, the barrier coating will have a high impedance.
2.5.3
Device level testing
There are various corrosion test methods available for assessing the reliability of elec-
tronic products, as well as for identifying weak points in electronic device design in simu-
lated corrosive environments that electronic devices are expected to be exposed to during
field operation. Mixed flowing gas, dust, salt spray, and high humidity and temperature
tests are examples of the commonly used accelerated corrosion tests for electronics.
These tests are carried out in a sealed chamber where the specific contaminant is de-
posited on the samples.
Corrosion in the field is caused by the synergistic effect of contaminants and environmen-
tal conditions [6]. These parameters can be combined in the accelerated test to simulate
the field and operational conditions. Accelerated corrosion tests for electronics are often
combined with voltage bias to accelerate the occurrence of corrosion or ECM [9]. It is

presence and quantity of corrosion product phases can help identify the conditions that
caused corrosion, such as temperature, pH, potential, and electrolyte chemistry.

methods are available for testing HA devices and their components in contact with hu-
man sweat and exposure to humidity/temperature conditions. These test methods are
based on different electronic test standards (heat and humidity and salt spray tests) and
metal/alloy test standards in different environmental conditions (accelerated atmospheric
tests and immersion tests).
Based on how sweat is applied to the components, testing is generally classified as testing
in direct contact or indirect contact with sweat. In immersion tests and accelerated tests,
samples are in direct contact with sweat during the test. On the other hand, during atmo-
spheric and salt spray, sweat is introduced into the samples through the atmosphere or in
spraying cycles, so it can be said that these testings have indirect contact. The aim was to
analyze these corrosion test methods and find out which method has field-representative
results. The samples used in the corrosion test were Bluetooth antennas (Cu-Ni-Zn alloy)
and battery contacts (Au-plated stainless steel).

3.3
Conclusion
The study examined different corrosion test methods used to test hearing aid components
in sweat solution. Tests were performed on metal components (Bluetooth antenna) and
plated metal components (battery contacts). The goal was to find which corrosion test
can replicate corrosion mechanisms observed in the field.
The results of the Bluetooth antenna showed that the sweat spray method produced cor-
rosion results similar to the HA device antennas corroded in the field in terms of corrosion
occurrence, corrosion product color, and morphology and composition of the corrosion
product. The corrosion product consisted mainly of Cu, O, C, and Cl, followed by Ni and
Zn. The XRD analysis of the blue and green corrosion products showed the presence
of the same phases, of which the highest intensity was for CuCl2, followed by ZnCl2,
CuCl2(H2O) and ZnCl2nH2O. The red corrosion product was mainly a mix of CuO and
Cu2O. Similar corrosion products were observed on the field samples. The results of
the immersion and accelerated corrosion tests were not comparable to field corrosion.
The samples from atmospheric and heat and humidity tests showed no signs of corro-
sion on the metal surface. This research confirmed that the sweat spray test is the most
suitable method for introducing sweat into HA devices/components, thus replicating field
conditions.

4
Development and evaluation of the
corrosion test method for PCBA
The investigation in the previous chapter showed that the sweat spray test is the most
suitable method for replicating the corrosion observed in the field. If the sweat spray can
cause field corrosion on metal parts, it should also be applicable for PCBA testing and
evaluation of the conformal coating. In order to evaluate the effectiveness of conformal
coating on PCBA, the sweat spray test was combined with the heat and humidity test.
The humidity/temperature cycle is crucial in determining the coating’s adhesion strength
and resistance to water absorption. Temperature cycling can stress conformal coatings
by allowing water to diffuse into and out of the coating. The formation of a water layer
(condensation) on PCBA creates a conductivity path for current to pass, establishing an
electrochemical cell between two points on PCBA. The moisture barrier performance of
the coating is affected by factors such as the conformal material type, coating thickness,
conformity, and cleanliness of the board [12]. The adhesion of the coating is highly in-
fluenced by PCB cleanliness, and various studies have observed conformal coating ad-
hesion loss due to the presence of flux residues [12, 19, 56, 62]. The water layer can
dissolve residues on the PCBA surface, leading to surface insulation resistance reduc-
tion between biased points on PCBA, causing ECM or other corrosion failure modes [56].
Also, in the presence of a strong electrolyte, galvanic corrosion can potentially develop
on PCBA due to dissimilar combinations of metals [3, 18].
In this study, the sweat spray test was used to introduce sweat into samples, and exposure
to condensing conditions was performed in a climatic chamber with constant humidity
while cycling temperature to obtain condensation on the PCBA surface. DC voltage was
applied to samples during the test in order to include bias conditions. The synergetic
effect of sweat, humidity/temperature, and bias voltage affect PCBA reliability by causing
corrosion on its components. The potential bias serves as a thermodynamic driving force
for the corrosion cell. The leakage current was measured during the test. The damage of
the coating (adhesion loss) and corrosion failures in form of ECM shows an increase in
the leakage current.
The results were compared to the field failures of PCBA, and such information reveals the
validity of the applied corrosion testing methodology.

corroded the most were hand solder joints on the receiver and tact switches, ENIG-plated

As shown in Figure 5.4, Device A samples had the highest leakage current in the test

leakage current remained high throughout the test. Sample A 3 failed after 144 hours,
and sample A 5 did not fail the test.
Device B samples did not exhibit a high leakage current. After 168 hours, sample C4
(Device C) showed a high leakage current, indicating the possible formation of ECM, and
the current was stabilized the next day. The production of a large amount of corrosion
products provides greater resistance to the flow of leakage current, which is why the
leakage current drops or stabilizes after its high level. Leakage current measurements

6.1
Introduction
Analysis of hearing aid device failures showed that many components have failed due to
corrosion caused by human sweat and exposure to humidity conditions [6]. The corro-
sion products found on the antennas, battery contacts, and microphones contain a high
amount of Cl, C, and O [6]. These elements can potentially come from various chemical
compounds in human sweat such as sodium chloride (NaCl), potassium chloride (KCl),
lactic acid (C3H6O3), and acetic acid (CH2COOH).
The findings of the investigation of various corrosion test methods in Chapter 3 showed
that, although the sweat spray test is a very efficient method for replicating the corro-
sion mechanism seen in the field, it does not reproduce all corrosion colors (products)
detected on the field corroded samples. Red corrosion products on corroded Bluetooth
antennas, for example, and green corrosion products on battery contacts were regularly
observed in the field but not during the sweat spray test. This led to the conclusion that
the artificial sweat solution used in the tests was insufficient to produce all of the corrosion
mechanisms and products observed in the field. Therefore, it was necessary to investi-
gate the current artificial sweat chemistry to assess how pH and other sweat elements
affect corrosion and, based on the results, propose new sweat chemistry that can mimic
all the corrosion mechanisms and products detected on metal parts in the field. Previ-
ous research on metals in artificial sweat served as a basis for determining which sweat
compounds should be considered as factors during testing.
Several studies on the corrosion behavior of metals in artificial sweat found that Cu cor-
rodes when Cl−ions are present in solution [15, 17, 42, 43], whereas lactic acid in sweat
solution can corrode Cu, Ni, and Zn [16, 44]. A few studies reported that Cu can also
corrode in the presence of acetic acid vapor and form CuO and Cu2O [93, 102]. How-
ever, no investigation has reported metal corrosion in urea. Pourbaix diagrams can be
used to predict the stability of a metal in a corrosive environment as a function of pH and
potential. Figure 6.1 (a) shows Pourbaix for CuO in pure water and seawater. These
diagrams can be used to predict the direction of electrochemical reactions and to iden-
tify corrosion product formation. As can be seen from Figure 6.1 (b) the corrosion area
for Cu is below pH 4 and above 300 mV. In seawater, solid complexes such CuCl and
CuCl2 · 3Cu(OH)2 will appear, producing an increase in total copper dissolution [103].
Similar corrosion products can appear when Cu is in contact with human sweat.
Several researchers used the Design of Experiments and factorial design to investigate
the corrosion of metals in aggressive media [104, 105, 106]. They showed that DoE was
an efficient method to study the effect of pH, acid content, temperature, and time on the
corrosion rate of various metals, and helped them to reduce the work required to achieve
the results. Although no research has yet been reported on exploring different artificial
sweat solutions on the corrosion behavior of metals using the DoE approach, this method
can be used to efficiently optimize the artificial sweat solution by investigating the effect
of various sweat parameters on the corrosion behavior of metals. DoE is a statistical

Results and Discussion
6.3.1
Results from pH value and NaCl content investigation
Figure 6.7 shows the results of the investigation of pH and NaCl content on corrosion.
From the results, it can be seen that pH value affected corrosion on metals more than
NaCl content. Sweat spray tests with low pH and low NaCl content resulted in dense
green and blue-green corrosion products uniformly distributed over the entire surface.
Similar results were obtained for artificial sweat with low pH and high NaCl content. In
contrast, artificial sweat solution with a high pH value only showed slight corrosion spots
on the surface, and the results were not field representative. Artificial sweat solution with
neutral pH resulted in blue-green and brownish corrosion products whose intensity is not
equal over the entire surface. Corrosion starts at several points and gradually spreads
over the surface. The results of this test are very similar to the field samples in terms of
how corrosion appears on the surface and the corrosion product color.
SEM analysis of corroded areas performed on all samples showed that the corrosion prod-
uct had mud-crack and porous morphology, which was also reported in the field analysis.
That means the corrosion was caused by the same factors, chlorine ions and humidi-
ty/temperature exposure. The EDS analysis of corroded areas of low pH (low and high
NaCl) and neutral pH (low and high NaCl) showed that the corrosion product consists
predominately of C, O, Cu, Cl, and the Zn and Ni content was 3-5 times lower compared
to Cu. Some research reported that Cl ions form complexes with Cu rather than Ni and
Zn in solutions that contain NaCl.

6.5
Conclusion
Different artificial sweat solutions were investigated using the standard sweat spray test.
The tests were performed on the Bluetooth antenna. The aim of the investigation was to
propose a new sweat solution that can replicate all corrosion colors/products observed
in the field. An attempt was made using the DoE method to investigate the effect of pH
value, NaCl, acetic, and lactic acid content on the corrosion behavior of Cu-Ni-Zn alloy
and compare results to the field. The investigation was divided into two experimental
procedures, one was to investigate the mutual effect of NaCl content and pH value using
full factorial design, and the second experiment investigated the impact of lactic and acetic
acid content using the fractional factorial method.
The results from the first experiment showed that the artificial sweat solution with a neutral
pH value and low NaCl content had the most similar results to the field in terms of corrosion
product color, corrosion type, and composition of the corrosion product. The results from
the second experiment showed that artificial sweat solutions with high acetic acid and
lactic acid resulted in corrosion similar to the field. The corrosion product had all colors,
blue, green, red, and brown. Therefore, a new artificial sweat solution that contains low
NaCl content and a high amount of acetic and lactic acid content, with a neutral pH value
was proposed. The result of the sweat spray test with the proposed artificial sweat solution
performed on the Bluetooth antenna and battery contacts showed all corrosion colors as
seen on the field corroded samples.

Overall conclusion
Based on the results and discussion of the research and development of the new corrosion
sweat setup described in Chapters 3, 4, and 5, as well as the research and formulation of
the new artificial solution described in Chapter 6, the following conclusions can be drawn:
1. Among immersion, artificial sweat, accelerated corrosion, sweat spray test, and hu-
midity & heat corrosion tests conducted on the Bluetooth antenna, it was found that the
sweat spray test can mimic the corrosion mechanisms observed in the field. The corro-
sion product formed on the surface was blue and green with few dark brown spots and
similar corrosion products were observed in the field. The corrosion product morphology
was found to be mud-crack and porous. The porous morphology resulted from a Cl−
ions ingress in the metal oxide layer, causing micro pits. If the sweat spray test was the
most suitable method for replicating field corrosion on a Bluetooth antenna, then it is also
applicable for testing other hearing aid components.
2. The evaluation of conformal coating applied on FPCBA was done using the test method
that consisted of a sweat spray test, high humidity, and cyclic temperature exposure in
a climatic chamber combined with DC voltage bias. Leakage current measurements,
SEM/EDS analysis were used to evaluate the performance of conformal coating on spec-
imens. Among the three tested conformal coatings, gas phase coating Type 1(PE CVD)
showed fewer failures due to high leakage current compared to the liquid-based coatings
Type 2 (fluorinated polymer) and Type 3 (acrylate urethane). However, all Type 3 samples
showed high leakage current, and this was due to the high voltage components that this
FPCB contains. A high amount of corrosion was observed on SMT components, hand
solder joints, and ENIG-plated copper pads on all failed FPCBs in the test. The corro-
sion failure mechanism that occurred on the tested FPCBs is the same as in the field,
confirming that the climatic reliability of the FPCB can be tested using this method.
3. There types of hearing aids were tested using the corrosion test method validated
by FPCB testing. Device A showed the highest number of failures identified by leakage
current measurements. Components that were susceptible to corrosion were hand solder
joints on the tactile switches and the receiver, the microphone, and ENIG-plated copper
pads. The presence of capillary gaps increased corrosion of the plated area beneath
the microphone gasket and on the receiver. On the contrary, Device B did not show any
failures due to high leakage current, but all Device B samples in the test showed a high
amount of corrosion on the solderings around the microphone cans and the microphone
sound inlet. The corrosion of these components was accelerated due to galvanic coupling
with the Au-layer.
4. The Design of the Experiment method was used to investigate the effect of pH value,
NaCl, lactic, and acetic acid content on the corrosion behavior of hearing aid metal compo-
nents. The investigation results showed that the new artificial sweat solution should con-
tain a high amount of lactic and acetic acid in addition to other compounds from standard
neutral pH sweat solution. The addition of the acids increases the red corrosion product
on the surface that is commonly seen in the field and which corresponds to Cu2O. Further-
more, the sweat spray test results performed with the proposed sweat solution showed
efficient replication of field corrosion products observed on Bluetooth antenna and battery
contacts.

Future work
8.1
Ideas to improve the Current study
Analyzing the entire work done during this project, some ideas emerged that could be
applied to improve the current study.
1. Preparation of artificial sweat solution and sweat spray chamber
The artificial sweat solution should be prepared carefully. After sweat ingredients are
added to the container with DI water, an Electric Lab Stirrer Mixer should be used in order
to dissolve all ingredients. The presence of any solid matter in the solution might block
the nozzle of the spraying device. According to the ISO 9227 standard, the solution has
to be preheated to 35 ±2 ◦C. All the components in contact with artificial sweat solution
such as supports for the test specimen shall be made of non-metallic material. Also, it
should be avoided that the drops of the sprayed solution do not fall from supporters down
to the samples. During the test, it was observed that not all samples in the test received
the same amount of sweat solution. The samples which were placed directly under the
spray nozzle received more solution compared to samples that were placed closer to the
sides of the cabinets. This can affect the reliability of the results if more samples are used
in the test. The recommendation is to redesign the sweat spray chamber and ensure that
all surfaces being tested are exposed to the free circulation of the spray.
2. Investigation of both sides of PCBA during testing
During the test, the PCBA side with more electronic components/circuits was turned up-
wards and directly exposed to sweat deposition and condensation. As a result, no or much
less corrosion was observed on the lower side of the PCBA. Thus it is recommended to
test both sides of PCBA facing up during the test with an equal number of samples.
3. Preheat the samples before the climatic test
The samples should be preheated before placing them in the climatic chamber to avoid a
large amount of condensation and cause additional stress, which may affect the correct
evaluation of the experimental results. For example, if the temperature of the surface of
the test specimen is lower than the dew point of the air in the climate chamber, conden-
sation will occur on the surface of the specimen at the beginning of the test.
4. PCBA-level and Device-level testing with the proposed artificial sweat solution.
8.2
New ideas based on the Current study
1. A large amount of corrosion was observed on the microphone components mounted
on the amplifier because the soldered joints were not protected with a conformal coating.
Therefore, it is recommended to protect the microphone components with a conformal
coating.
2. Galvanic corrosion caused by coupling with the Au-layer can be avoided by using
another interlayer instead of Ni (for example Ni-Sn) or by increasing the thickness of the
Au-layer.
3. Many failures have occurred in hand-soldered joints due to the large amount of flux
contained in the solder wire. Flux residue reduces the effectiveness of conformal coatings,
so that some cleaning may be recommended.
