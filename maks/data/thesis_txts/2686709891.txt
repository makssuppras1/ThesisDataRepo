Summary
Sandwich composites have a signiﬁcant importance for several industries due to their
strength-to-weight ratio and their improved structural properties by the combination
of diﬀerent materials.
Ensuring structural integrity and characterisation of failure
mechanisms is crucial for the industry.
This project focuses in wind turbine industry, speciﬁcally in wind turbine blades, and
aims to provide crack propagation failure mechanism for debonded sandwich composites.
Experiments and numerical simulations will be developed to address this goal.
There is not any similar reference for four point bending testing with sandwich debond
propagation. Thus, a testing methodology will be provided in this project. Experiments
aim to validate numerical simulations.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Wind energy is currently a priority regarding energy production globally, especially in
Denmark. Denmark fulﬁlled 47% of its electricity consumption with wind power in 2019
[1], being by far the top country in Europe, in terms of percentage.
This wind energy production has being accompanied by a parallel technology development
and investment, in blade manufacturing and maintenance costs.
1.1
CORTIR Project
This thesis is part of the CORTIR (Cost and Risk Tool for Interim and Preventive
Repair) project, in collaboration with Bladena ApS. CORTIR aims to provide a Cost
and Reliability Tool, CAR Tool, to facilitate the decision-making in order to minimise
risks, operation and maintenance costs [2]. The Danish Energy Agency funds CORTIR
through the Energy Technology Development and Demonstration Program (EUDP).
EUDP public programs stimulate the development of new technologies in the ﬁeld of
energy, driving Denmark towards its climate and energy objectives [3]. EUDP was
launched in 2007 and has provided ﬁnancial support to 870 projects with 3.9 billion
DKK, equivalent to more than 500 million Euros [4].
CORTIR spans across several organisations, from world leading energy companies, Ørsted
or Acciona, and blade manufacturers, LM Wind Power or Vestas, to danish universities,
Aalborg University and Technical University of Denmark, DTU. Figure 1.1 shows all the
partners involved, being Bladena the Project Manager.
DTU participates in the project by performing diﬀerent scale structural testing and
analysis on sandwich composite materials. This thesis covers the Sub-Structural and
Structural levels, as shown in Figure 1.2.
1.2
Sandwich Composites
Sandwich composite materials are widely used in the diﬀerent industries because of their
improved structural properties by the combination of diﬀerent materials bonded to each
other so as to utilise the properties of each separate component to the structural advantage
of the whole assembly, as described by ASTM [5]. This combination of materials often
follows the following architecture: two thin faces of high performance material, both

core is placed between the faces and bonded with adhesive. By doing so, face sheets are

solid from the microscopic point of view, allowing easier bonding with the face sheets,
in comparison with honeycombs. Balsa cores are very humidity sensitive to and have
higher mechanical properties than foams.

1.3
Objectives and Achievements
Debond damage in sandwich composite materials is a failure mechanism that must
be addressed to ensure structural integrity. In comparison with other failure modes,
Figure 2.4, debonds are located below the outer surface, meaning that it is possible not
to detect them before a critical debond size is reached, leading to a sudden failure of the
structure.
==PAGE:17==
1.3 Objectives and Achievements
5
The primary objective of this thesis is to show how crack growth propagation behaves
for debonded sandwich composite materials while subjected to fatigue loads. Four Point
Bending, FPB, experimental set-up will be used for this purpose. Sub-Structural scale,
referred also as Beam in this thesis, and Structural scale, referred also as Plate, levels
from Figure 1.2, will be the components to study.
Additionally, parametric FEM models for both conﬁgurations, Beam and Plate levels,
replicating experimental results have been developed. Static simulations were carried
out to deﬁne the experimental set-up analysis, and a comparison with the experimental
response is covered.
For both scales there is a debond in between the top face sheet of the sandwich composite
material and its core, while the bottom face sheet remains bonded to the core material.
This debond will propagate along the length of the specimens. At the same time, a
buckling opening of the top face sheet will occur driving the crack growth.
Experimental fatigue results will be used to validate fatigue FE simulations, which are
not covered in this thesis.
Additionally, there is not any speciﬁc standard testing for FPB debond propagation.
A methodology will be proposed based on learning and experience, aiming to provide
guidance for future similar testing.

2.1
Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics
In 1920, Griﬃth observed that the theoretical stresses needed to fracture crystal (brittle
material), referred to the atomic bonds, are signiﬁcantly higher than the actual stress
values to make it fail [12]. Internal material cracks reduce signiﬁcantly the theoretical
strength of the material. This occurs because these cracks act as stress concentration
points. The elastic potential of a cracked body can be deﬁned as follows:
Π = W −U
(2.1)
where Π is the potential energy of the system, W is the work performed by the external
forces and U is the elastic strain energy stored in the body.
Later, Irwin [13] extended Griﬃth’s theory considering plastic deformations around the
crack tips, and proposed an energy approach for fracture, deﬁning the energy release rate,
G. G measures the energy dissipated per unit area when an increment of crack extension:
G = −dΠ
dA
(2.2)
where dA the incremented crack area. Fracture criteria, or crack propagation, happens
when:
G ≥Gc
(2.3)
where Gc is the fracture toughness or critical strain energy release of the material.
The stress ﬁeld at the crack tip can be characterised by the combination of the three
diﬀerent fracture modes, Figure 2.1

2.1.1
Bi-Material Interfacial Cracks
In structures where there are more two or more diﬀerent materials, failure initiation often
happens in the interface between them. Fracture mechanics for bi-materials is diﬀerent as
for single homogeneous materials. Cracks for homogeneous materials propagate following
the path that allows them to have fracture Mode I at the crack tip, despite the global
loading condition. However, interface crack propagation for dissimilar materials is the
result of mode-mixity fracture at the crack tip [17].
Sandwich composite materials have notable material properties’ diﬀerence between face
sheets and core. Thus, critical energy release rate, Gc, at the interface is strongly
inﬂuenced by mode-mixity [18].

2.4
Four Point Bending Test
This test method aims to subject samples to bending. The applied loads/moments
produce curvature of the ﬂat specimens [5]. Flexure tests are usually performed to
determine ﬂexural and shear stiﬀness of sandwich structure, shear modulus and shear
strength of cores, or compressive or tensile strength of face sheets. In addition, FPB
tests aim to evaluate bond between face sheets and core. For this purpose, core shear
stresses must be lower than the actual core shear strength. Core shear failure must be
avoided for this study.
This thesis aims to propagate debond crack while, simultaneously, face sheet’s buckling
happens in FPB test.

Stage 1 refers to the loading of the specimen. This part of the test exhibits a linear
elastic response. At stage 2 buckling occurs, with an associated buckling load of 869
N. Buckling opening starts growing from this stage henceforth. Stage 3 corresponds
with the maximum load during the test. So far, energy release rate at the crack tip has
been increasing, having always a value lower than the critical energy release rate of the
interface, Gc. It is at stage 4 where Gc is exceeded and sudden crack propagation occurs,
with the consequent associated drop in force.
After stage 4, crack keeps propagating smoothly, represented by stage 5, until the beam
reaches a steady state, stage 6. Top face sheet deformed shape and its interaction with
the overall geometry of the beam is extreme enough that G at the crack tip is lower than
Gc and no more crack propagation takes place.
Based on this analysis displacement control must be chosen over force control for fatigue
loading. If force control is chosen, crack will drastically propagate as for stage 4, meaning
that only one cycle fatigue test will be obtained, which must be avoided.

3.2.5.1
Fatigue Methodology
All fatigue tests carried out in this chapter have been displacement controlled.
Methodology proposed in Figure 3.18 is based on experience and learning during this
thesis. It has been proven the best way to proceed for crack propagation and buckling
opening analysis. From static test, buckling load and buckling displacement is obtained.
This buckling displacement will be taken as reference for the fatigue test.
Parameters needed to be chosen for dynamic testing are:
• Maximum displacement ∆UMax
• minimum displacement ∆Umin or Ratio R between maximum and minimum dis-
placement
• Frequency f
For ∆UMax is recommended to select the buckling displacement determined from static
test plus a certain % of it. It was conﬁrmed that simply with the buckling displacement
was not enough to have debond propagation. % values between 10% and 30% gave stable
crack propagation results.
Ratio R for all tests was constant and equal to 0.1.
Tests with diﬀerent frequencies f were carried out, however, it was proven that 1 Hz gave
the best results. This is due to the fact that FPB loading ﬁxture, shown in Figure 3.4, is
relatively heavy. When the system is excited at high frequency, the load cell captures as
force the inertia introduced by the loading ﬁxture when moving at high speed, which
results in inaccurate results because of the false load cell reading.
Likewise as for static tests, 50 N loading was taken as initial position for fatigue tests to
ensure contact during the whole experiment. All displacements are taken relative to the
position of the actuator at this initial position.
Testing pictures for beams have been taken every 500 cycles, following with Figure 3.19
procedure. Pictures are captured at %90 ∆Umax, avoiding any crack propagation due to
high constant loading. This procedure is repeated until no more debond is noticed.

3.5.1
Initial Crack Manufacturing
Beam’s debond was manufactured by conventional methods. Once square panels for the
beams were cut and, with the help of a hand saw, debond was perform in between the
top face sheet and the core, with an initial crack length of 100 mm. Later, crack tips
were sharped with the help of a blade.
This method does not create a perfect debond, with a smooth crack tip at the interface; on
the contrary, the transition from debond crack tip to non-debonded zone may be abrupt,
leading to a higher G needed at the crack tip in the ﬁrst stage of debond propagation,
which can result in sudden crack growth.

4.1
FEM Models Objectives
The following 3D Plate FEM model has been the basis for further studies within DTU’s
research group. Experimental tests include debond crack propagation in fatigue loading,
however, FEM models only cover static simulations, as fatigue simulations are not part
of this thesis’ scope.
Two diﬀerent modelling approaches have been used for beams and for plates. For beams,
a 2D model has been developed, whereas for plates it has been a 3D model. This decision
relies on the diﬀerence in debond between both conﬁgurations, having beams a linear
debond with only one direction available for propagation, while for plates the circular
debond has inﬁnite directions available for propagation.
The goal of these numerical simulations is to provide a proposal FPB experimental set-up
for plates and validate previously proposed FPB set-up for beams.
Both models are based on parametric Python scripts, allowing fast and easy customisation,
which has facilitated diﬀerent studies for FEM models. Displacement or force control
can be chosen to drive non-linear simulations.
Abaqus/CAE 6.22 FEA software, Abaqus/Standard has been used for this purpose.

4.2.1
Linear Buckling Analysis
In this ﬁrst linear analysis the objective is to know the magnitude of the buckling load.
For this purpose, a normalised uniformly distributed pressure is applied to the model
with the same direction as in the tests, simulating the load eﬀect of the loading rollers,
as this load can not be modelled with any contact for linear perturbation analysis.
In an eigenvalue buckling problem, it is needed to solve for which loads the model stiﬀness
matrix becomes singular, hence [23]:
Kv = 0
(4.1)
has nontrivial solutions. K is the tangent stiﬀness matrix when the speciﬁed loads are
applied, whereas v are nontrivial displacement solutions.
The base state of the structure is considered as the state before the linear analysis is
performed. However, if the linear analysis is preceded by any other step, the base state is
the current state of the model at the end of the last general analysis step. This base state
can include preloads, P. These preloads are often zero in classical eigenvalue buckling
problems.
To do this, an incremental loading pattern Q is deﬁned in the eigenvalue buckling
prediction step, which will be scaled by the load multipliers λi, and the eigenvalue
problem is considered as [23]:
(K0 + λiK∆)vi = 0
(4.2)
where K0 is the stiﬀness matrix corresponding to the base state or, in this case, initial
==PAGE:55==
4.3 Material Properties
43
conditions, K∆is the diﬀerential initial stress and load stiﬀness matrix due to the
incremental loading pattern Q, λi are the eigenvalues, vi are the buckling mode shapes
or eigenvectors and i refers to the ith buckling mode.
The critical buckling loads are then P N + λiQ. For this thesis’ goal, only the ﬁrst value
of λi is of interest. Additionally, there is not any preload pattern, P, before the linear
analysis is carried out. The buckling mode shapes, vi, are normalized vectors, but they do
not represent the actual magnitudes of deformation for those critical loads. The largest
displacement component in the mode has a magnitude of 1.0.
Abaqus/Standard allows Lanczos or Subspace iteration eigenvalue extraction methods.
In this case, Subspace is used, as Subspace is recommended for less than 20 eigenmodes
calculation.
From the linear analysis, buckling modes and nodal variables can be extracted for the
speciﬁc conﬁguration, by using *NODE FILE option in Abaqus/Standard, and requesting
U, displacement, as the nodal output request.

4.4.1
Mesh
For this purpose, plain strain assumption is considered, since all loads are in the x-y
plane, and thickness in z direction is larger than for the in-plane x-y directions. Actual
beams’ width, z direction, is 60 mm, which compared to y direction, width, is considered
thick body assumption. Plane strain condition assumes no strain in the z direction, as
follows:
εz = 0
σz ̸= 0
(4.4)
For meshing purposes, CPE8R 8-node biquadratic reduced integration plain strain
elements are considered all over the model. At the crack tip, CPE4R 4-node bilinear
reduced integration with hourglass control plain strain are implemented. Hourglassing is
a zero energy deformation mode in FEA where elements are deformed, while the overall
mesh section remains undeformed [25].
Figure 4.3 shows the comparison between ﬁne and coarse mesh at the crack tip. Fine
mesh at the crack tip allows the application of Crack Surface Displacement Extrapolation
method [26], CSDE, to compute Mode I and Mode II energy release rate, GI and GII,
as well as the mode mixity Ψ. CSDE method computes G and Ψ by using the relative
crack ﬂank displacements, δx and δy. Although CSDE is not considered in this project,
it is necessary to adapt FEM models so CSDE can be easily implemented.
Having such a ﬁne mesh at the crack tip does not allow large loading displacements for
non-linear static analysis, as mesh elements collapse before desired, not giving a full
non-linear response to be compared with experimental results. Therefore, coarse mesh at
the crack tip has been implemented for this purpose, see Figure 4.3.

experimental buckling load determined for these specimens would be higher than the
actual one.

4.5.7.1
Failure modes
The most common failures modes in sandwich composite materials are core shear and
face wrinkling [28], among others, Figure 2.4. Any failure mode other than debond
propagation must be avoided when testing plates, thus, a comparison against FEM model
results has been done.
Core Shear
Core shear failure takes place if the core reaches to its maximum shear strength value.
Based on supplier’s data sheet regarding cores, Foam has a maximum shear strength of
1.21 MPa.
Figure 4.42 shows S13 shear stresses at the core, which are the most critical. The most
critical zone is at the debond crack, where these stresses are 0.48 MPa. However, this
stresses may not be considered as crack propagation at the debond is desired and mesh
might have inﬂuence on this stress levels.
Core shear must be considered in between the loading and support rollers, which can be
seen in Figure 4.42 having a look at their corresponding mesh partitions. In this plate
zone, stress levels reach 0.17 MPa, being much lower than the shear strength of the core.
According to FEM model, there will be a safety factor of 7.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
Sandwich composite materials have outstanding mechanical properties concerning their
strength-to-weight ratio. Structural integrity analysis is necessary to characterise failure
mechanisms which can take place during blade operation. This thesis has covered debond
crack propagation along the sandwich material’s interface, which is a very common type
of failure for wind turbine blades.
Testing methodology for FPB debond propagation and buckling opening for sandwich
composite materials has been proposed and successfully validated. Crack propagation
rates have been provided for speciﬁc loading conditions.
It has been proven that displacement controlled testing is the only way-to-go. This is
against typical fatigue tests, where force control is the most common testing methodology.
Force controlled tests would not allow fatigue testing due to sudden and critical debond
propagation.
It was not possible to nucleate any transverse crack during fatigue testing, which was
one initial matter of interest. Load levels may have been too low to allow this crack
initiation, or FPB test might not be the most suitable test. Blades in operation exhibit
transverse cracks on their surfaces, which usually lead to turbine stop and repair.
Resin/adhesive within slots for Slotted Foam core inﬂuences the mechanical response of
the sandwich composite. An alternative core modelling has been proposed and validated
for SF-L beams, by including NY130 PVC core material properties instead of the actual
NY75 PVC core properties.
Considering numerical modelling, good analysis about how Imperfection size inﬂuences
simulations has been performed for both conﬁgurations, beams and plates models.
Minimum imperfection scaling factor is recommended to be included in the geome-
try. Additionally, buckling opening is overpredicted by simulations, whereas load vs.
displacement response for non-linear analysis is well captured.

CHAPTER 6
Future Work
Plate testing is the ﬁrst future work to be done. Despite size and debond geometry
diﬀerences, similar sandwich composite behaviour is expected. Force & buckling opening
vs. displacement correlation with FEM models proposed within this thesis is possible
only for static loading.
To have a complete face sheet characterisation, it would be needed to know mainly which
are the shear material properties of the face sheets, as these might have a strong inﬂuence
on the sandwich behaviour.
It is suggested to do a sensitivity analysis varying all face sheet material properties, to see
how the global sandwich material behaviour depends on them. This thesis only covered
this analysis for Ex and Ey GFRP properties.
Sandwich composites, such as balsa, can be analyse in a similar manner as in this project.
Balsa cores are widely used in wind industry, and a comparison between Foam and Balsa
core’s debond propagation is encouraged to be done.
