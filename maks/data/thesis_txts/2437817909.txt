Summary (English) 
Summary (English) 
History has shown that with building structures follows structural failures. A part of these 
failures is the product of consequences related to unforeseen external circumstances, whereas 
another part is the result of inadequate knowledge or misjudgments by the designer, engineer, or 
constructor. To reduce both the risks and costs of failures, several guidelines and some 
requirements are defined by both international and more local organizations. The intention is 
primarily to negate or limit structural collapses, often by making the structure insensitive to 
accidental circumstances and local failures. 
     The current European and Danish building code states such a requirement of ensuring 
robustness, however, no regulatory methods or approaches are prescribed. This leaves room for 
the engineers and designers to choose between the use of basic approaches, or to interpret their 
own methods, resulting in a broad variety of unique solutions. Depending on the method(s) 
chosen, the results thereof may lead to overestimated designs, which entail a use of excessive 
material, or it may lead to underestimation of structural capability, which ultimately entails 
structural collapses. 
     The aim of this thesis is to study the possibilities of developing a computational method 
capable of automating robustness analyses. The underlying motivation is to create a 
computational method able to assess any and all possible instances of local failures in a 
structural manner, from which the essential or key elements can be located. The intention of 
such a tool is to potentially provide both designers, engineers, and contractors the insights to the 
consequences related to the removal of certain elements, ultimately in the hope of negating 
structural misjudgments and failures.  
     The outcome of this thesis is a tool made for models in Revit through the use of FEM-Design 
and Dynamo. The tool consists of several components, which together forms what is—by the 
writers—known as the first computationally automated robustness analyses workflow, herein 
denoted ARA. The tool is to be used on an analytically acceptable 3D BIM Revit model, as 
defined by Hejnfelt (2016). The tool automatically simulates all possible local element-based 
structural failures for the model, based upon knowledge gained from the study done by Madsen 
& Andersen (2018). The failure scenarios are then—based upon the assumption of the 
structure’s ability to create alternative load paths—analyzed structurally in FEM-Design. The 
tool finally provides a colorization of the individual elements in the original 3D Revit model 
according to the number of other elements, when removed, are assessed to fail. 
     From this study, it seems ambiguous if a computational method may replace (all) the manual 
method(s) for assessing the robustness of building structures. However, this study has provided 
a computational method that may help in the process of assessing it by locating the key 
elements.

Summary (Danish) 
Summary (Danish) 
Lige så længe som der har eksisteret bygningskonstruktioner, har der været tilfælde af 
strukturelle kollaps. En del af disse kollaps skyldes uforudsete udefrakommende påvirkninger, 
hvorimod andre skyldes utilstrækkelig viden eller fejlvurderinger lavet af designere, ingeniører 
eller konstruktører. For at reducere både risiko og pris i forbindelse med strukturelle kollaps er 
der defineret adskillige retningslinjer og enkelte krav af både internationale og lokale 
organisationer. Formålet med disse retningslinjer og krav er primært at undgå eller begrænse de 
kollapsomfang—som kan opstå—ved at udvikle konstruktioner, der er mindre sensitive overfor 
utilsigtede omstændigheder og lokale brud. 
     I de nuværende europæiske og danske bygningsreglementer er sådanne retningslinjer og krav 
formuleret. Der er dog ikke fremlagt noget krav til hvilke konkrete metoder eller 
fremgangsmåder, som skal benyttes. Dette giver plads til, at ingeniører og designere kan vælge 
imellem forskellige metoder, som er alt fra basale fremgangsmåder til egne udtænkte 
undersøgelser. Resultatet heraf er en bred vifte af forskellige løsninger. Den valgte metode har 
dog en afgørende betydning for, om bygningselementerne bliver overdimensioneret, hvilket kan 
lede til et overforbrug af materialer, eller om bygningens strukturelle egenskaber undervurderes, 
hvorved et kollaps kan opstå.  
     Formålet med denne afhandling er at undersøge mulighederne for at lave en automatisk 
robusthedsanalyse ved brug af computerbaserede metoder. Den bagvedliggende idé er at skabe 
en computerbaseret metode, som er i stand til at vurdere alle tænkelige muligheder af lokale 
strukturelle fejl på en måde, hvorfra de væsentlige eller centrale elementer kan bestemmes. 
Formålet med et sådant værktøj er potentielt at give både designere, ingeniører og entreprenører 
indsigt i konsekvenserne ved at fjerne visse elementer, og dermed forhåbentlig udelukke 
strukturelle overestimeringer og fejlvurderinger. 
     Resultatet af denne afhandling er et værktøj, som kan bruges til modeller i Revit ved brug af 
FEM-Design og Dynamo. Værktøjet består af flere komponenter, som tilsammen danner, ud fra 
forfatternes viden, det første computerbaserede, automatiske robusthedsanalyseprogram, som 
her i afhandlingen betegnes ARA. Værktøjet skal bruges på en analytisk acceptabel Revit 3D-
model, som defineret af Hejnfelt (2016). Værktøjet simulerer automatisk alle tænkelige 
bortfaldsscenarier for modellen, baseret på den opnåede viden ud fra undersøgelserne lavet af 
Madsen & Andersen (2018). Bortfaldsscenarierne er da baseret på antagelsen om strukturens 
evne til at skabe alternative belastningsveje, som analyseres strukturelt i FEM-Design. 
Værktøjet farver til sidst i den oprindelige 3D-model, de elementer—som ved bortfald—er 
skyld i, at flest mulige af de andre elementer bryder. 
     Ud fra denne afhandling kan det ikke entydigt konkluderes, om en computerbaseret metode 
kan erstatte samtlige manuelle metoder, som i dag benyttes til at vurdere robustheden af 
bygningskonstruktioner. Denne afhandling har imidlertid givet en computerbaseret metode, som 
muligvis kan hjælpe med at vurdere robustheden af konstruktioner ved at lokalisere 
nøgleelementerne.

1 Introduction & background 
Definition 1.1 (Robustness). 
“The quality or condition of being strong and in good 
condition or the ability to withstand or overcome 
adverse conditions or rigorous testing” (Oxford 
University Press, 2018). 
 
“(Of a person or animal) strong and healthy, or (of an 
object or system) strong and unlikely to break or fail” 
(Cambridge University Press, 2018). 
1.1 Historical motivation 
The deciphering of the oldest discovered building code attributed to the Babylonian King 
Hammurabi (c. 1754 BC) states that “If a builder has built a house for some one and does not 
construct it properly, and the house which he built has fallen and killed its owner, then that 
builder shall be put to death” (King, 2008). This deciphering shows that the happening of 
structural failures—and attempts of negating them—can be traced back thousands of years. 
Since the time of King Hammurabi, many new building codes and regulations have been 
implemented. The European codes such as EN 1990 (2002) provides—in addition to local 
regulations—requirements for designers and engineers with the means of ensuring structural 
reliability and to prevent collapses. Even with these prescriptions failures however still do 
happen and a cautious statement could be that they might continue to do so as long as there are 
human errors, design flaws, material flaws, extreme and unforeseen conditions or environments, 
and combinations of the mentioned (Petroski, 1992). 
A study was done in 1976 by the Swiss Federal Institute of Technology assessing 800 cases of 
different structural failures caused by faulty engineering in which 504 people were killed and 
592 people were injured. An overview of the results of the study can be found in the following 
Table 1-1.

1.2 What is Robustness? 
Definition 1.1 (Structural 
                          robustness). 
Robustness is the ability of a structure to withstand 
events like fire, explosions, impact or the consequences 
of human errors, without being damaged to an extent 
disproportionate to the original cause. (EN 1991-1-7, 
2006) 
 
After the incident at Ronan Point, an inquiry report was produced by Griffiths (1968) wherein 
the following description involving redundant structures is provided: 
 
“It is a common aim of structural engineers so to design their structures that is 
one or two component parts or members fail due to any cause, the remaining 
structure shall be able to provide alternative paths to resist the loads 
previously borne by the failed parts, even though with a reduced margin of 
safety. This is one of the well-known features of what is called by engineers 
‘redundant structures’—that is, structures in which not all the members are 
absolutely essential, and so some can be termed redundant. In the aeronautical 
world, this line of approach to design is particularly common, and airplane 
designer aim, as they say, to make their structures such that they can ‘fail-
safe’; that is, crack (as by fatigue) for example, and so fail locally without 
precipitating general collapse.” (Griffiths, 1968, pp. 39-40) 
 
 
As said by Griffiths and as seen from the pictures in Figure 1-1 it is crucial for the structural 
engineer to ensure a robust building in the event of an element or two failing. The consequences 
==PAGE:18==

Page 4 of 154 
 
of not analysing the robustness of a building can be fatal, as the building may collapse and 
thereby lead to both economic and human costs. To prevent this from happening the engineer 
must analyse the robustness of the building structure according to the set regulations  (EN 1991-
1-7, 2006), in which both strategies for identified and unidentified accidental actions are 
provided. Such a strategy provides e.g. a design based upon tensions ties, which was also noted 
by Griffiths as being a possible solution to counteract the progressive collapse at Ronan Point: 
 
“Progressive collapse is not an inevitable feature of high system-built blocks. It 
can be avoided by the introduction of sufficient steel reinforcement to give 
continuity at the joints, and the adoption of a plan-form which provides for the 
arrangement of the load-bearing walls in such a way that the load is carried by 
alternative load paths if part of the structure falls.” (Griffiths, 1968, p. 63) 
 
 
In addition to and in elongation strategies in the European regulations, one might draw 
connections to terms such as safety, risk, and redundancy (see e.g. (Giuliani, 2012)) or solidity1 
and ductility2. 
Despite the use of norms regarding constructional robustness in Denmark, there is—according 
to Danish Association of Precast Concrete’s BEF Bulletin no. 3 (Jensen J. F., 2016)—no real 
rules of best practice regarding the requirements for methodologies or the documentation 
thereof. Annex E in DS/EN 1990 DK NA:2010-05, however, states that a construction is robust 
when; 
A. the crucial parts of the construction in terms of safety are only slightly sensitive 
regarding unintended or accidental impacts or defects, or 
B. there is no extensive failure of the design if a limited part of the construction fails. 
1.3 State-of-the-art: BIM and FEM 
This section is based upon a digital interview answered by the structural engineering 
department of Sweco Danmark A/S, which can be found in Appendix A. 
In a survey conducted by the National Building Specification (NBS) on professional architects, 
engineers, and contractors in 2017, an increase of confidence related to the use of BIM in the 
UK was recorded as following (National Building Specification, 2017); 
- 
55% of the respondents describe themselves as “confident in BIM”, whereas the number 
was 35% in 2012. Furthermore, 
- 
60% of the respondents think that “BIM will help bring time efficiencies, reducing time 
from inception to completion” (no previous comparison), and  
- 
70% believe that “BIM will realize a cost reduction in the design/build/maintain 
lifecycle” (no previous comparison).

Despite the positive responses towards BIM in general, it was however also noted that 90% of 
the respondents answered that they “believe BIM adoption requires changes in workflow, 
practices, and procedures.” Similar surveys have not been found done in the Danish 
construction industry. 
The department of structural engineering at the company of Sweco Danmark A/S (later denoted 
‘Sweco’) answered some questions regarding their use of 3D models, FEM software, and 
robustness analysis methods for their projects. These questions have provided the base of the 
study of state-of-the-art in regards to BIM and FEM within the contemporary construction 
industry, from which the most interesting parts are presented in the following.   
At Sweco they create 3D (BIM) models for almost all projects. The models are described as one 
of the most important parts of their products, why they generally spend a lot of time to ensure 
models of high quality. The models are used from the early design phases to the end of the 
projects to more easily (and digitally) connect different disciplinary areas, extract geometries, 
and to provide a more tangible product for their clients. Despite the seemingly extensive use and 
large amounts of time spent on 3D models, the extent of the use of these models for structural 
calculations are described as it being used in some projects. 
The assessment of structural robustness is part of all construction projects at Sweco. The choice 
of method is however not governed by a single best practice but depends on the project and the 
respective project teams. A typical assessment may consist of locating the key elements 
manually—using Microsoft’s Excel or PTC’s Mathcad—or of enhancing redundancy and 
structural coherence using tension ties (see the method presented in Section 2.3). Both methods 
are however described as being very time-consuming. 
The use and awareness of FEM software are generally present in the construction department of 
Sweco, however, the actual integration between analytically correct 3D models and the FEM 
software is limited to very few people. Regarding analyses of robustness, FEM software is only 
being used as a tool to analyse a few different scenarios subject to notional removal of elements, 
to locate whether the structure may provide alternative load paths. This is, however, a process 
that most often is done manually.

1.4 Motivation 
There have been multiple motivational factors for why it has been deemed interesting to study 
the area of robustness analyses, and more so in relation to the use of structural 3D models and 
FEM software: 
• 
The assumption of Petroski (1992) being that the world is unpredictable and that the 
nature of architectural renewability will continue to provide unprecedented designs—
thus inevitably providing risks for structural failures—incentivizes a constant 
improvement for robustness assessments.  
• 
The conclusion of Matosouek & Schneider (1976) being that engineers do make 
mistakes and that a rather large portion of these mistakes could have been avoided using 
better-standardized practices. 
• 
The Danish Association of Precast Concrete (Jensen J. F., 2016) and the questionnaire 
with Sweco (Appendix A) revealed an absence of a single best practice regarding 
robustness analyses. Such a practice could possibly help reduce the amount or extent of 
structural failures. 
• 
The elaborate use of 3D models in Sweco without consistent use of these for structural 
analyses. The development of an automatic computational method for robustness 
analyses may save time as well as enhance the value of the models and the quality of 
the projects. 
• 
An automatic computational method may ease or help the process of defining or 
assessing the robustness of more complex structures. Furthermore, such a method may 
envision other possibilities for computational workflows, enhancing the overall utility 
potential of the 3D models. 
• 
Personal experience related to the use of 3D modelling software and BIM in general as

2 Theory 
Definition 2.1 (Key element). 
A key element is a load carrying element that is a 
central and crucial part of the whole construction or a 
substantial part of the constructions integrity. 
 
This chapter will briefly cover the basic theory found relevant for the study on robustness 
analyses. It includes highlights and excerpts of the contemporary European and local—in this 
case Danish—building regulations, two qualitatively prescribed methods of how to approach the 
concept of robustness, and finally a state-of-the-art robustness analysis provided by a large 
Danish consulting engineering company. 
2.1 European and Danish Standards on robustness 
A more thorough study of the European and Danish regulations of DS/EN 1990:2007 and 
DS/EN 1991-1-7:2007 regarding building structures can be found in Appendix B. 
A structure must be designed with adequate structural resistance and durability—thus not being 
critically damaged in events such as explosions, collisional impacts or consequences of human 
error—to an extent disproportionate to the original cause (DS/EN 1990:2007, p. 23). 
With buildings having different functions—thus providing various risks and probabilities e.g. 
regarding the loss of structural stability and human lives—the scale of required assessments for 
building structures appertaining the robustness are determined to correlate with the defined 
consequence classes, whereas the strictest rules must be met for a building of consequence class 
3 (CC3) (DS/EN 1991-1-7:2007, p. 18). 
Furthermore, it is for construction works classified as CC2 or CC3 recommended that key 
elements of the structure should be located and designed to resist actions by either using an 
analysis based upon equivalent static load models or by applying prescriptive design/detailing 
rules (DS/EN 1991-1-7:2007, p. 31).

According to DS/EN 1991-1-7 (2007) it is recommended for buildings of CC2b and CC3 that

All structures are however set to be designed to resist progressive collapses caused by an 
internal explosion. In addition to this, it is recommended that the structural design may permit 
failures of a limited part of the structure, provided this does not include key elements upon 
which the stability of the whole structure depends (DS/EN 1991-1-7:2007, p. 32). 
According to DS/EN 1990:2007 (2007, p. 27) the relevant design situation must be selected 
depending on the circumstances, whereas the design situation in the case of e.g. a fire, 
explosion, impact, or the consequences of localized failure is specified as an accidental design 
situation. 
According to the European code DS/EN 1991-1-7 (2007) the assessment of accidental design 
situations can be divided into two: 
1. Strategies based on identified accidental actions. 
 
This strategy is based upon a specific pre-defined cause of action rated (relatively) 
plausible. It may lead to local design choices with the purpose of preventing or reducing 
the action most efficiently. 
 
2. Strategies based on limiting the extent of localized failure. 
 
This strategy is not based upon a specific pre-defined cause, but rather on a more 
general view of structural mitigation. It may lead to both local and global design 
choices with the purpose of enhancing redundancy, integrity, and ductility.

In the design, the potential failure of the structure arising from a specified cause shall be

a) “Preventing the action from occurring or reducing the probability and/or magnitude of

b) Protecting the structure against the effects of an accidental action by reducing the

c) Ensuring that the structure has sufficient robustness by adopting one or more of the 
following approaches; 
a. by designing certain components of the structure upon which stability depends 
as key elements to increase the likelihood of the structure’s survival following 
an accidental event, 
b. designing structural members and selecting materials to have sufficient 
ductility capable of absorbing significant strain energy without rupture, 
c. incorporating sufficient redundancy in the structure to facilitate the transfer of 
actions to alternative load paths following an accidental event.” 
In the design, the potential failure of the structure arising from an unspecified cause shall be 
mitigated. The accidental design situation may consist of the three following adoptive 
mitigations (EN 1991-1-7, 2006, s. 17); 
a) “designing key elements, on which the stability of the structure depends, to sustain the 
effects of a model of accidental action 𝐴𝑑;

c) applying prescriptive design/detailing rules that provide acceptable robustness for the 
structure (e.g. three-dimensional tying for additional integrity, or a minimum level of 
ductility of structural members subject to impact).”

2.2 General prescribed methods for ensuring robustness 
In 2016 the Danish Association of Precast Concrete (Danish: Betonelement-foreningen) 
published their 3rd edition of their bulletin called “Betonelementbyggeriers robusthed” (English: 
“Robustness of buildings of prefabricated concrete elements”) (Jensen J. F., 2016). 
According to the bulletin the idea behind the bulletin itself was partly; 
- 
to form a connection between the requirements for robustness analyses according to the 
European and Danish Standards and the design of the constructions, and partly 
- 
to outline a similar framework for the documentation of robustness analyses as to the 
documentation of the static analyses of contemporary Danish buildings. 
This was done by presenting the two methods A and B—presented in the following 
subsections—which deal with: 
- 
Key elements 
- 
Key connections between elements 
- 
Key details inside elements 
It is through the bulletin recommended that it is the one responsible for the construction work 
(Danish: Bygværksansvarlige), who determines which elements and connections are the key 
elements and connections, while the key details inside the elements are to be determined by the 
party responsible for the static documentation for the relevant design section. 
According to Jensen J. F (2016) the static documentation is presumed to follow the “SBI-
anvisning 223: Dokumentation af Bærende Konstruktioner” (English title: “Documentation of 
loadbearing constructions”).  
2.2.1 Method A: Verification of the safety of the key elements 
This method is based upon the ideas of either; 
1. enhancing the safety of the key elements using a partial factor of 1.2, or 
2. document the verifications for the key elements showing only a light sensitivity 
regarding unintended or accidental impacts or defects, such as; 
• 
unforeseen loads or load actions, 
• 
unintended deviations between the used calculations models and the actual 
functioning of the structure, 
• 
unintended deviations between the execution of the project and the project 
materials, 
• 
unforeseen geometric imperfections, 
• 
unforeseen settlements, or 
• 
unforeseen degradation. 
==PAGE:28==

The first solution above can not necessarily be used as a standalone due to its inadequate 
handling of connections and element details. Furthermore, the unforeseen effects may have too 
large of an impact on more sensitive elements, thus requiring specific considerations. 
2.2.2 Method B: Loss of load-bearing structural components 
This method is based upon the idea of a local loss/disappearance of either a structural 
component or a part of a main structural component and the ability of the structure to generate 
alternative stress distributions. 
The basis for the presumption of the remaining structure’s ability to retransfer forces into 
alternative structural parts and thereby generate alternative stress distributions could be verified 
by a purely plastic model, such as a lattice model of the remaining structure shown to be able to 
uphold equilibrium, i.e. using reinforcement and ties in the slabs and walls. 
The verifications of the load-bearing capacities of the remaining structural elements are based 
upon the loads and material strengths used for accidental scenarios. 
2.3 Manual state of the art robustness analysis: Overturning

systems, based upon the assessment of static equilibrium. This stability analysis is also used to 
study certain local failure scenarios. From this, it can be assessed whether the structure is prone 
to overturn, thus indicating the need for e.g. tensile ties. This approach is used in the consulting 
engineering company of Sweco Danmark A/S.

3 Methodology 
In this chapter the initial thought approach on how to develop a workflow that automatically 
analyses the robustness of a structure is presented. Furthermore, some of the conclusions 
regarding possibilities and requirements for the use of software—and the restrictions thereof—
are identified. Lastly, the choices regarding the use of software and the resulting workflow are 
presented. 
3.1 Overall methodology 
The chosen method for developing a computational robustness analysis method (ARA) is to 
generate notional removals of elements in a pre-existing structural 3D BIM model, thus 
simulating local failure scenarios. These—many—failure scenarios are to be analysed statically, 
after which the carrying capacity of all the remaining elements are to be calculated. From this, it 
is thought to estimate which elements that—when removed—leads the most other elements to 
fail, thus providing an overview of the most critical removal scenario for a single element, 
which hopefully provides an estimate of which elements are to be assessed as being key 
elements. 
 
Figure 3-1: Illustration of some of the local failure scenarios for a building structure. Which ones are the 
most critical?

The chosen local failure scenarios consist of; 
- 
a 3 m wall section (an assessment done by the estimation of typical precast concrete 
wall elements having a length between 4 and 15 ft (Precast/Prestressed Concrete 
Institute, 2018)), 
- 
a column, or 
- 
a beam. 
In order to make use of pre-existing 3D models for structural calculations, the developed 
workflow must either: 
1. Include calculations of static systems, internal forces (e.g. FEM), and carrying 
capacities of elements. 
2. Make use of pre-existing software. 
The 2nd option above is—especially due to the existence of already developed and recognized 
FEM and analysis software—the choice of method for developing the ARA. 
3.2 Initially intended workflow 
The initially considered workflow for the ARA would result in the user only needing to interact 
with the 3D modelling software, thus not having to interact with the FEM or structural analysis 
software at all. The idea of the workflow process consisted of 5 steps—in which a single failure 
scenario would be designed and analysed—and a 6th step repeating the process with a new 
failure scenario designed. 
The originally intended workflow would look as follows: 
i. 
Remove a single element (3 meters of wall, a column, or a beam) in the 
3D model (in the 3D modelling software). 
 
ii. 
Export the model—with a removed element—into the FEM and 
structural analysis software. 
 
iii. 
Run the FEM and structural analysis on the model (in the FEM and 
structural analysis software). 
 
iv. 
Extract the data of the overutilized/non-acceptable elements into a 
readable and data-light format. 
 
v. 
Put the removed element back into the model (in the 3D modelling 
software). 
 
vi. 
Run steps i-v with a new element removed. 
 
The above process stops when all possible scenarios have been evaluated. 
 
As mentioned above, a fully automatic computational method based entirely on the above 
workflow would eliminate any need of interaction with the FEM software itself and would only 
require for the user to ensure a correct analytical model in the 3D modelling software.

3.3 Requirements for software 
To develop and enable the functioning of the originally considered workflow, some 
requirements regarding both the compatibility and functioning of the software must be met. 
These requirements will be explained in the following. 
3.3.1 3D BIM software 
In 3D BIM software—such as Autodesk’s Revit, McNeel’s Rhinoceros (denoted as Rhino), or 
Trimble’s Tekla—three types of main elements are typically present: 
• 
Architectural elements such as façade coverings, floorings, ceilings, doors, windows, and 
interior. These elements may have any kind of properties or data associated, however they 
typically do not provide any structural insight. 
• 
System-associated elements such as water pipes, ventilation ducts, plumbing and 
electrical systems. These are often connected with some sort of mechanical installation 
and do typically not provide any structural insight besides the holes they make in the 
structural elements. 
• 
Structural elements such as slabs, walls, columns, and beams. Structural elements 
typically include sub-elements called analytical elements. These analytical elements 
typically provide data with structural insights—e.g. material properties, connections, 
strengths, and dimensions—but will be further explained in Chapter 4. 
In terms of transferring the necessary data from the 3D BIM software to the FEM and/or 
structural analysis software, the analytical elements (the sub-elements of the structural 
elements) are the only ones of relevance. The analytical elements will be described further in 
the following Chapter 4.

3.3.2 Programming software 
The computational method is—due to its compatibility with the 3D BIM software of Revit— 
been chosen to be developed in the visual programming tool of Autodesk’s Dynamo. 
Furthermore, previous experiences with Dynamo have provided notional proposals for 
developing the planned computational robustness analysis tool. 
Other visual programming software capable of working with 3D BIM software are i.a. 
McNeel’s Grasshopper 3D—which is directly associated with and may access the API of 
Rhino—Trimble’s PUPPI designed for Tekla, or Bentley Systems’ GenerativeComponents 
originally intended for Bentley Systems’ MicroStation, but with the capability of working with 
other CAD tools as well, such as Autodesk’s AutoCAD (GenerativeComponents, 2017). 
The term “visual programming” may be understood with a dual meaning, since it (in 
architectural communities) is used in reference to programming related to the design of graphic,

3.5 Developed workflow 
The thought workflow—as described in Section 3.2—requires access to the export functions 
provided in the Revit ribbon (StruSoft, StruXML Revit Add-In, 2015) as well as the functions 
provided inside the FEM software. With FEM-Design, however, lacking an application 
programming interface (API (The Tech Terms Dictionary, 2016)), the automatic of exporting 
through the Revit-to-FEM-Design add-in isn’t an option. Furthermore, an export function of the 
reinforcement data from Revit to FEM-Design isn’t provided. These limitations have hindered 
an automation of the initially considered workflow, why some moderations have been made. 
==PAGE:38==
 
3. Methodology 
 
Page 24 of 154 
 
FEM-Design and Revit exhibit syntactic interoperability (Casrai, 2015), meaning that they are 
capable of transferring data bi-directionally through a specified data format – in this case, the 
.struxml file formats. With all the data-transferring happening through these files alone, a 
modification of these would induce an alteration in the respective model. 
With the inclusion of reinforced concrete elements being of a high priority, the workflow is 
altered to—instead of demanding an analytically correct 3D Revit model that is meant to be 
exported to FEM-Design—demand an analytically correct 3D FEM-Design model (see the 
requirements for an analytically correct model in Chapter 4). This means, that the 3D FEM-
Design model must be based off an already existing Revit 3D model for the results 
(visualization by the use of colours, see Section 5.5) to be displayed properly in Revit. 
The actually developed workflow consists of the following five steps, wherein the ith step—for 
now—is a semi-manual step. The four steps ii-v are however steps for which automated 
computational methods have been developed, which all in which can be found in Chapter 5: 
i. 
“Step 0”: Prepare the analytically correct FEM-Design model based off 
on existing Revit model. This FEM-Design model file will be denoted 
‘mother .struxml file’ or just ‘mother file’. See Section 5.1. 
 
ii. 
“Step 1”: Copy the mother file into as many duplicates as there are 
structural elements in the mother file. These copies will be denoted 
‘children .struxml files’ or just ‘children files’. See Section 5.2.  
 
iii. 
“Step 2”: Modify every one of the children files to simulate all possible 
local element-based failure scenarios (according to Section 3.1). See 
Section 5.3. 
 
iv. 
“Step 3”: Run the FEM and structural analyses for each and every one of 
the children files/failure scenarios. Save the results in lists in .csv files. 
See Section 5.4. 
 
v. 
“Step 4”: Evaluate and visualize the results from the results in the .csv 
files. See section 5.5. 
 
It should be noted that the actually developed workflow doesn’t run as one wholesome cyclic 
operation—as the case is with the originally intended workflow—but instead does a single run-
through with the individual steps containing cyclic operations. One may say that the large cyclic 
operation—presented in the originally intended workflow (operating a single failure scenario 
from start to finish before continuing to the next failure scenario)—in this case, has been 
divided into five steps each handling their appertaining operations for all the failure scenarios at 
once. The coding and visual programming behind each step can be found in Chapter 5.  
With this method, the workflow is—in addition to steel and wooden elements—able to include 
analyses of reinforced concrete elements. The only requirement for this to happen is for the user 
to insert the reinforcement into the original analytical concrete model elements from inside 
FEM-Design.

4.8 Reinforcement 
The software of Revit and FEM-Design is not directly compatible in regards to reinforcement in 
the sense that an insertion of it in either program won’t be retrievable in the other after a model 
export from one to the other. 
Consider a 3D Revit model containing concrete elements—in which reinforcement is wanted—
that is wished analysed in regards to robustness by using the ARA. Firstly, one must export the 
model to the FEM-Design format. Any reinforcement data will be lost through this transfer. 
First after the model has been imported into FEM-Design one may define the reinforcement, 
which will be considered in the ARA. 
The lack of compatibility of reinforcement between Revit and FEM-Design may, however, be 
overcome e.g. through the development of Dynamo graphs and standardizing the reinforcement 
data in Revit, from which it can be added to the .struxml file for the model. A workflow based 
upon this way of thinking is what provided the developed workflow for assigning edge releases 
of structural walls elements (Madsen & Andersen, 2018). In the same manner, a workflow 
defining a predetermined syntax of reinforcement data may be made, possibly enabling an 
automatic transfer of reinforcement defined in Revit to FEM-Design.

5.1.1 Splitting of walls 
A prerequisite for achieving the goal of simulating all possible failure scenarios consisting of a 
3 meter wall removal (see Section 3.1 and Section 3.5) is to be able to remove any arbitrary 3 
meter wall section. Typical 3D BIM objects (hereby walls) are modelled as entire coherent 
elements, hence a partial removal is impossible. Therefore—to achieve the desired objective—
Algorithm 1 has been developed. Through using the Dynamo graph 1 to run this algorithm, all 
wall elements in the Revit model will be split into wall sections with a width of ~500 mm. 
The desire to acquire exactly 500 mm wall sections are disrupted by the general lack of wall 
lengths represented by integers. Therefore, a Python modulus operator (symbol ‘%’) is used to 
retrieve the remaining value of the total wall length, measured in mm, divided by 500 mm. This 
remainder is split into even amounts onto all the 500 mm wall sections, thus resulting in ~500 m 
wall sections with a guaranteed width ≥500 mm.

5.2 Step 1: Copying of mother file into children files 
From the initial step 0, the mother .struxml file is produced. Through this step 1 of the ARA, a

done by searching for the strings of ‘slab’ and ‘bar’ in the mother file (see the categorization of

failure scenario. As described in Section 3.1 and Section 3.5, a failure scenario of a wall-bearing

With the lack of an available API for FEM-Design, the assistance of FEM-Design developers 
has been proven a necessity in acquiring a code for automating any FEM and structural analysis 
(for the various available FEM-Design analyses methods, see Appendix D). The team of 
StruSoft therefore provided a “FEM-Design script” (filename extension: .fdscript) from which 
the desired analysis can be initiated to automatically run for a single .struxml file. 
The FEM-Design script—which is a text-based (XML) arrangement of the necessary function 
calls—contains the function calls from which FEM-Design may open and structurally analyse a

script is ‘ana.fdscript’ (see Appendix M). 
The ‘ana.fdscript’ is built upon four steps: 
1. Launching a .struxml file in FEM-Design 
2. Running the desired analysis 
3. Extracting the wanted analysis results in a .csv format 
4. Closing the current .struxml file 
With all the different failure scenarios created in the previous step 2, the FEM and structural 
analysis are now to be initiated for all the different failure scenarios (all the .struxml children 
files). However, the provided ‘ana.fdscript’ is merely a code to run one analysis of a single 
.struxml file. Therefore, a script has been developed to modify the provided ‘ana.fdscript’ in 
such a way that it multiplies the code—used to run a single analysis of a single file—as many 
times as there are children .struxml files. This means that the modified ‘ana.fdscript’, instead of 
being able to analyse merely a single .struxml file, can analyse as many .struxml files as needed.

5.5 Step 4: Visualization of results 
Two methods for categorizing the results of the FEM-Design analyses have been thought out. 
Both are based upon the idea of colouring the element(s) depending on how many remaining 
elements—when removed—becomes overutilized (utility ratio > 100 %). 
In both methods, four categories have been defined for the elements in order to visualize the 
results of the analyses; 
- 
1st category: Elements that—when removed—causes the most other elements to become 
critical. Colourization: Red. 
- 
2nd category: Elements that—when removed—causes the second-most other elements to 
become critical. Colourization: Orange. 
- 
3rd category: Elements that—when removed—causes the third-most other elements to 
become critical. Colourization: Yellow. 
- 
4th category: Elements that—when removed—causes the least other elements to become 
critical. Colourization: None, however 50% transparent. 
The terms of “most”, “second-most”, “third-most” and “least” varies in the two proposed 
methods, why these are defined in the following subsections. 
It must be noted that the above categories are made separately for column, beam and wall 
elements. This is due to the possibility of one of the element types causing a relatively large 
number of other elements to fail, thus not giving regard to the elements of the other types. 
Without the separation, there would be a probability of critical elements—of an element type 
other than of the critical one—to be overlooked. 
5.5.1 Colourize according to set parameters 
The first method is based upon the suggested limit of admissible failure according to DS/EN 
1991-1-7 (2007) (see Section 2.1). This limit is set to be 15 % or 100 m2 of the floor area for 
two adjacent storeys, whichever is smaller. 
The developed workflow does however not include such an assessment of the slab parts, 
which—in the same way as for the wall parts—would include a necessity of splitting the slabs 
into smaller sections. Furthermore, separate slab analyses would be needed in FEM-Design, 
drastically increasing the sampling size of the data. Therefore, an alternative parameter, 𝑥1, that 
is meant to resemble a ~15% loss of floor area, based upon the already retrieved data for the 
other elements, has been defined as 
 
𝑥1 = number of elements
number of storeys · 0.15 
(5-3) 
 
The above parameter equals 15% of the average number of elements on each storey. This can be 
used as a lower limit in order to define/colour the elements causing the most other elements to 
become critical when removed. These walls are assigned to the 1st category. 
==PAGE:63==

The 2nd and 3rd category can respectively be defined by 
 
𝑥2 = number of elements
number of storeys · 0.10 
(5-4) 
 
 
𝑥3 = number of elements
number of storeys · 0.05 
(5-5) 
 
The remaining non-categorized elements will be assigned to the 4th category. The resulting 
colorization will be as follows: 
1. Colourize according to the value of a calculated parameter: 
a. Red if 𝑥1 remaining elements are overutilized. 
b. Orange if 𝑥2 remaining elements are overutilized. 
c. Yellow if 𝑥3 remaining elements are overutilized. 
d. No colour to the remaining elements, however, makes them 50% transparent. 
5.5.2 Colourize according to percentiles 
This is the method of choice for the developed ARA. 
The second method is—similar to the first method—based on a series of set lower values 
dividing the elements into different categories. What differs from the first method is, however, 
the way these lower values are defined. 
In the first method, parameters were calculated in order to divide the elements that—when 
removed—caused more than respectively 15%, 10%, and 5% of the average number of elements 
on each floor to fail. The second method, however, bases its intervals on percentiles defined by 
the scenario that results in most of the other elements to be overutilized. 
The division of the colour categorization of the elements will be as follows: 
2. Colourize percentiles of elements: 
a. Colour the 0-10% of removed elements that causes the most other elements to 
become overutilized red. 
b. Colour the 10-25% of removed elements that causes the second-most other 
elements to become overutilized orange. 
c. Colour the 25-50% of removed elements that causes the third-most other 
elements to become overutilized yellow. 
d. No colour to the remaining elements, however, makes them 50% transparent. 
5.5.3 Discussion of colorization methods 
The first method (colorization according to set parameters) provides a visualization of the 
results based on a percentage of the total amount of elements in the model. This was made to 
approach the suggested admissible failure of 15% of the floor area recommended by DS/EN 
==PAGE:64==
 
5. Workflow of the ARA 
 
Page 50 of 154 
 
1991-1-7 (2007), as mentioned in Section 2.1. This means that the more elements present in the 
model, the more elements need to be proven overutilized for the removed element to be 
assigned to the 1st, 2nd or 3rd category. The second factor being the number of storeys was added 
to minimize the lower limits of the categories for multiple storeyed buildings, thus rating a 
structural failure in high-rise buildings more severe than a similar structural failure in a single-
storeyed building with the same total number of structural elements. 
For the first method to show any results/colours, the lower limits must be met, meaning that a 
certain number of elements must be found overutilized. This however also means that if no 
scenarios result in a failure of 15% of the average number of elements per floor, no elements are 
assigned with the red colour. The same for the orange and yellow colours defined by the lower 
limits of 10% and 5%, respectively. 
In the case of the second method, the lower limits in each of the categories are defined by the 
single scenario with the highest number of overutilized elements. This results in the ARA itself 
having no pre-determined intervals but instead defines the intervals depending on the structural 
model. In other words, it merely visualizes the scenarios in a scale from; “element(s) causing 
the highest number of overutilized elements when removed” to “element(s) causing the least 
number of overutilized elements when removed”. Thereby, a colouring scale of red, orange and 
yellow will always be produced, despite there being no failure scenarios at all with a realistic 
critical amount of overutilized elements. On the other hand, this method may also prove 
inadequate in cases with too many critical elements, e.g. for a badly dimensioned structure or a 
very large 3D model. Furthermore, such inadequacies might also be present for models in which 
a single element is assessed overly critical, thus leaving out other less yet critical elements. 
These elements might have required extra attention, but due to it simply not being assessed as 
critical as the upper 10%, 25%, or 50% (defined by the single most critical scenario), it won’t be 
visualized. 
The fact that elements, through the secondly explained visualization strategy, are colourized no 
matter what, in a way “forcing” the engineer to rethink or reapprove certain elements, is the 
reasoning behind this strategy being the choice of colorization method. It is deemed the most 
conservative choice. Furthermore, this second method is more general than the first in a sense 
that it isn’t pre-defined or limited by a recommended percentage of failure—such as it is 
recommended in DS/EN 1991-1-7 (2007)—thus making it generally applicable no matter the 
specific local recommendations. In the case of having a single extraordinarily critical element—
thus resulting in the ARA only colouring that single element—it is recommended to either run 
several ARA processes iteratively alongside the development and improvement of the static 
design or to run the ARA on limited parts of the modelled structure. This last approach might 
also help reduce the amount of data for the ARA to run, thus enabling the ARA to analyse larger 
data heavy models (process times of ARA tests can be found in Section 7.3, whereas the 
discussion on computational limits can be found in Section 8.2). 
For both methods, there will be very specific outcomes for models having no critical elements. 
This is worth noting, since the accidental load combination (which is the prescribed 
==PAGE:65==
 
5. Workflow of the ARA 
Page 51 of 154 
 
combination for structures subject to accidental actions, see Section 2.1) often isn’t the most 
critical (dimensional) load combination, as it disregards both wind and snow loads. The first 
method would provide no coloured elements at all, whereas the second (and chosen) method 
will show all elements as being coloured red (all elements are “the most critical element”). 
As presented in Subsection 2.3, the state-of-the-art manual robustness method is available 
through the software of FEM-Design. This method could theoretically provide the user with 
knowledge of which elements are prone to overturn, thus possibly requiring tension ties. Since 
the structural elements may be prone to overturn despite not being overutilized, this method 
could be a possible choice of method for a secondary run of the ARA. The method of 
overturning is however only considering (and relevant for) the wall elements. 
One must note that whichever visualization method was chosen, this developed ARA and the 
defined colour categories doesn’t conclusively deduct which elements are insufficiently 
dimensioned. It merely shows which elements—according to the software of FEM-Design and 
the analysis in question (see Appendix D)—causes the most other elements to fail if removed, 
thus suggesting which elements the engineer might want to consider being particularly attentive 
towards or even perform supplementary analyses on.

In the previous Chapter 5, the actual developed Dynamo graphs and coding making up the ARA

6.1 Description of the simple 3D building model 
An illustration of the “test case model” can be found in Figure 6-1. The building is designed to 
resemble a typical residential or office building consisting of prefabricated concrete elements. 
The building model itself doesn’t represent the design of a building intended for usage—e.g.

structural system. 
The building itself consists exclusively of concrete walls except from a single section on the

model to—in addition to several storeys of concrete wall elements—include both windows, 
columns, and beams, why it was assessed likely that errors would occur. Furthermore, the 
model was through qualitative reflections assessed to provide at least one relatively foreseeable 
critical section, being the opening provided by the mentioned column and beams. This section 
was—in the case of an element being removed—deemed likely to cause a major collapse.

6.7 Remarks regarding the case study 
In this section, the most notable unforeseen behaviours of the chosen workflow for the ARA is 
presented. For each behaviour, an assessment of its importance for the end results as well as 
possible solutions is discussed. 
6.7.1 Problems with windows when splitting walls 
When splitting the walls, two unexpected outcomes were noticed – both involving the windows 
in the model. 
The 1st of these two outcomes was noted after splitting the walls in the case study model 
(Subsection 6.2.1). After splitting the walls, all wall openings (doors, windows, etc.) were 
“removed” (filled out), leaving behind wall sections with no openings. This was found to be 
caused by Algorithm 1 (Subsection 5.1.2), which is developed to split the walls by entirely 
removing the initial walls prior to modelling the (seemingly) identical—yet split—wall sections. 
All the wall properties except the start and end locations are transferred from the old wall 
elements to the new walls elements. However, since the wall openings aren’t defined as a wall 
property, these haven’t been found transferable onto the new sections. Therefore, the windows 
must either manually be remodelled after splitting the walls or be collectively copied from the 
model prior splitting to the model after the splitting. For the case of the case study model, the 
windows were collectively copied from the original model to the model with split wall sections. 
This study has shown a possible solution to the 1st unexpected outcome. The solution could 
comprise of enabling Algorithm 1 to automatically copy the windows’ properties including their 
global coordinates, from which the exact same windows could be modelled in the wall sections 
after the split. However, due to the window elements typically—as well as in this case—
belonging to hosted families (Autodesk, 2018), thus not behaving like non-hosted (more “free”) 
objects, the implementation thereof hasn’t at this point been successful. 
The 2nd of these two unexpected outcomes was for the case study model noted after exporting 
and opening the mother .struxml file in FEM-Design after the preparations (hereby splitting of 
walls) done in Revit. It was—prior to testing—known that since window elements in Revit don’t 
have any corresponding analytical models, these aren’t included in the analytical FEM-Design 
.struxml model, but that the corresponding holes would still be present. Window holes in walls 
are commonly not a problem for structural 3D models since these typically entails more realistic 
results. However, it was noted that the window holes—which in this case spanned 915 mm 
horizontally—would span across the whole 500 mm width of some of the wall sections—or 
extend beyond it—and cause FEM-Design to not be able to generate the wall section, why it 
was removed. This was caused by the window holes providing a gap in-between two parts of a 
single wall section, which—in theory—would result in having two separate wall sections 
sharing a single element’s properties, which isn’t analytically suitable. 
==PAGE:84==
 
6. Case study with the ARA 
Page 70 of 154 
 
     
     
 
Figure 6-18: Visualization of how wall sections in FEM-Design are removed if not coherent. 
The gaps in the model shown in the above Figure 6-18 are—in other words—caused by splitting 
the walls containing windows and exporting the analytical model to FEM-Design. It is thought 
to reduce the structural cohesion of the model, thus disabling potential alternative load paths. 
Ultimately, this could provide much more conservative results for the individual failure 
scenarios, however, it could also result in unrealistically large or untransferable section forces, 
possibly lead to faulty conclusions of which elements—when removed—causes the most other 
elements to fail. 
However, according to the qualitative study of the results of the test study case (see Figure 
6-17), the provided resulting visualisation fits the idea of the area of the façade opening, 
provided by the single column and the two beams—when removed—are among the elements 
that—together with the surrounding wall sections—are causing the most other elements to 
collapse. 
A reason why the vertical wall gaps not being of greater importance regarding the provided 
ARA test results might be due to the lack of external lateral loads, which is to comply with the 
accidental load combination defined in the Eurocodes (see Section 2.1). A presence of such 
horizontal loads is however estimated to potentially provide majorly or even critically faulty 
errors since some building models might lack the ability to transfer horizontal loads throughout. 
A way to bypass the abovementioned 2nd unexpected outcome could be to manually model 
separate wall sections above and below all the windows. In this way, the windows aren’t 
creating lateral gaps through entire wall sections, despite being split vertically. A possible 
automatic solution incorporated in the ARA itself has also been thought of: This solution could 
e.g. consist of an extraction of the windows’ coordinates—local or global—through Algorithm 
1 (Subsection 5.1.2) from which the splitting process would skip splitting the walls inside a 
certain perimeter or horizontal margin of the windows. Such an implementation has however 
not been developed for the workflow yet.

6.7.2 Cover distances of reinforcement in split walls 
It is important to note that any reinforcement data in the Revit model will get lost when the wall 
elements are split into smaller wall elements, similar to the reinforcement data from the Revit 
model being lost through a transfer to FEM-Design. This means, the all reinforcement data must 
be added from inside FEM-Design after the export. When adding the reinforcement to the—
now split—walls, the user must take note of the chosen cover distances. If the cover distances to 
the side edges are chosen to be less than half of the distance between the bars, the amount of 
reinforcement will be increased compared to the reinforcement in a none-split wall section. The 
opposite goes for a cover distance larger than half of the distance between the bars, as this will 
decrease the amount of reinforcement compared to the reinforcement in a none splitting wall 
section. However, despite not setting the cover distances properly (in accordance with reality), 
the set amount of reinforcement isn’t an issue, since the generic nature of the chosen 
visualization method is indifferent as long as the reinforcement properties are the same for all of 
the walls. 
6.7.3 Base constraints of wall elements 
It was noted that—in order to split the walls correctly by the use of the Dynamo graph running 
Algorithm 1 (Subsection 5.1.2)—all wall sections must belong to their respective levels (their 
actual intended base level, ‘Base Constraint’). This means that no walls can be offset from any 
level (‘Base Offset’ must equal zero). This is due to the choice of running a “locked” node—
which most likely is due to it calling functions from the API of Revit—from inside Algorithm 1 
called “Wall.ByCurveAndHeight”. This node generates the new wall sections based on four 
inputs, being; ‘curve’, ‘height’, ‘level’, and ‘wallType’. The reason for using this node in the 
Python code—instead of coding a custom one—is simply due to it being deemed the most cost-
effective option in regards to the development of the code. However, in order for the user to 
more freely design and model their walls, a custom node may be developed for future versions 
of the ARA. 
==PAGE:86==

the wall, whereas the ‘Base Offset’ must be set to the value of 0. 
In case the walls are modelled with a base offset, and the Dynamo graph containing Algorithm 1 
is run to split the walls, the generated 500 mm wall sections will still all be modelled at the 
selected base constraint level with an offset of 0. This would, in other words, induce a stacking 
of wall elements at the set level of ‘Base Constraint’. 
6.7.4 Continuous wall removal 
When modifying the children .struxml files by the use of Algorithm 3 (Subsection 5.3.1) to 
simulate all the different wall-based failure scenarios, the algorithm runs through each of the 
children .struxml files and removes 6 wall elements. For each .struxml file, the 6 walls removed 
are offset with a single wall element from the prior .struxml file. An illustration of this can be 
observed in the following Figure 6-20. 
==PAGE:87==

Figure 6-20: Illustration of which elements are removed in the children .struxml files for the 16 first local 
failure scenarios of the test case model. 
As it can be observed from Figure 6-20 above, Algorithm 3 doesn’t—due to the increase in the 
amount of data it would cause—read any data or properties of the walls prior to removing them. 
This means that Algorithm 3 simulates all failure scenarios merely by removing whichever 6 
walls are adjacent in the .struxml file. When looking at a single level, the adjacent .struxml 
walls are—due to them being systematically created by Algorithm 1—also adjacent in the actual 
Revit model. However, this isn’t the case for walls on different levels. This might create 
discontinuous failure scenarios, meaning that for more complex structures, wall elements may 
be removed at completely differing locations for a single failure scenario.

For the test case, the abovementioned discontinuities haven’t been found problematic. If 
anything, it has been found to create additionally diverse failure scenarios. For certain building 
models, it may however, in theory, create unrealistic critical scenarios, which may provide 
unexpected results. This could e.g. be a section of a wall being visualized as though it is

critical—despite it not being—due to a single section being part of a failure scenario with other 
wall sections located in a completely different location. The models in which such unrealistic 
critical scenarios could be created have however been assessed to be a very small portion of 
relatively complex building structures. Furthermore, the consequences of such failures haven’t 
been studied. 
A way to completely bypass the above attribute could be to—instead of splitting the wall 
elements into sections of 500 mm—design and model the (pre-fabricated) concrete elements as 
they are in reality, from which the individual wall elements—instead of 6 wall elements—could 
be removed. An automatic bypassing through the ARA has also been thought of, which could 
consist of reading the vertical coordinates in order to determine if the wall elements at least are 
located on the same level. In this way, cross-level wall failures can be avoided. This has 
however not been implemented due to the following additional reading of data, which would 
induce a significant increase of time spent on creating the failure scenarios (process times can 
be found in Section 7.3, whereas the discussion on process times can be found in Section 8.2). 
6.8 Conclusions 
From this case study it has been observed that the developed ARA offers a method for 
determining the criticalness of individual elements of a 3D model. The criticalness is defined by 
how many other elements structurally rely upon them, which is obtained from analysing all 
possible local failure scenarios statically and structurally. This provides a tool to possibly help 
qualitatively assess the importance of the elements, thus providing an incentive for enhancing 
the carrying capacity or safety of certain key elements. 
Several noticeable behaviours of the ARA workflow have been observed and discussed, 
however, none of them have been deemed to lead too severe consequences invalidating the final 
result.

7 Results of the ARA 
To further test the developed workflow eight different models were designed. The intention of 
having several models to test the workflow on was to study the behaviour of the ARA on 
buildings consisting of a diverse combination of structural elements, thus possibly identifying 
flaws or improvements of the workflow or code. 
In this chapter, the results of a single smaller, a medium wall-based structure (test case model, 
see the previous Chapter 6), a larger high-rise building, and two façade model (later denoted 
“2D sections”) are presented. The tested models which aren’t presented, are due to them merely 
being designed as wall-based “box structures”. These models were developed to provide a base

the previous Chapter 6. 
7.1 Results of 3D structure tests 
The 3D models that have been tested with the ARA consist of a single smaller frame structure,

to the Eurocodes (see Section 2.1). Before running the ARA, this load combination was used to 
design the elements, thereby making sure that none of the elements were overutilized before the 
start of the ARA process.

8 Discussion 
This chapter provides reflections upon the chosen workflow of the ARA and the results 
obtained from some of the analyses done throughout this study. Furthermore, it provides a 
comparison of the developed automated computational robustness analysis and the manual 
method presented in Section 2.3. Finally, the found limitations specific to the computational 
method are discussed. 
8.1 Discussion of workflows 
The originally intended workflow presented in Section 3.2 would not require for the user to at 
any point interact with the FEM software. This means that the user would be able to prepare a 
3D Revit model, after which the whole process of the ARA could be run directly from Revit – 
without the need for copying and modifying all of the .struxml files at once. However, this 
would require for StruSoft to implement and release an API to gain access to the functions e.g. 
related to exporting models from Revit to FEM-Design. This was not a possibility during the 
time frame of this study. Furthermore, a method of transferring the reinforcement data from 
Revit to FEM-Design must be implemented. 
The implemented workflow is—due to the lack of an API for FEM-Design and the above-
mentioned constraints for exporting reinforcement data—based upon a pre-prepared analytical 
FEM model. This was found to be the most straightforward (and customizable) method of 
ensuring that e.g. the reinforcement data was properly applied to all elements. In case the 
specific model only consists of non-composite materials (e.g. unreinforced concrete, steel or 
timber elements), the user may prepare the full model from inside Revit, thus requiring minimal 
interaction with the actual FEM software. However, the Revit .rvt file must be converted into a 
FEM-Design .struxml file format for the ARA to process it. 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:101==
 
8. Discussion  
 
Page 87 of 154 
 
The originally intended workflow has been assessed to—in theory—be the most optimal 
workflow due to three primary attributes: 
1. The original workflow could—in theory—bring an ARA that would require no 
knowledge or interaction with the chosen FEM software. 
 
This would enable any user of the chosen 3D modelling software to be able to run the 
ARA directly from there, as long as the user knows how to successfully prepare the 3D 
model for structural analyses. 
 
2. The original workflow could—in theory—entail a more straight-forward solution that—
relative to the implemented workflow—would require less effort regarding the 
implementation. 
 
Furthermore, this has been assessed a more logical workflow that could ease future 
improvements or enhance the development of workflows based on similar principles. 
  
3. The original workflow could—in theory—enable flushing (Rouse, 2005) of data after 
each failure scenario run-through. 
 
This third point points out that data flushing isn’t provided in the implemented 
workflow, thus creating rather large amounts of data stored onto the RAM (Fisher, 
2018), hence leading to exponentially growing process times, thus setting up stricter 
requirements for the 3D models and hardware in use. 
The points above provide several theoretic benefits of running the initially considered 
workflow. 
What must be considered—regarding the 3rd point above about potentially reducing the usage of 
RAM through flushing—is that the use of flushing indeed might be able to hinder the overuse of 
RAM. However, the initial thought workflow would—due to the use of the same analyses in 
FEM-Design—provide the same results at almost the same speed as the actual developed ARA. 
As it can be seen in Figure 7-9 in section Section 7.3, the process times for each of the four 
steps are relatively similarly divided for all 8 different tested models. From this, it can be noted 
that the by far largest step in terms of time consummation is the 3rd step (step 3, analyzing the 
children files, Section 5.4), which—based on the 8 test models—use an average of 𝑡8,step3 =
89.11% of the total time spend on the workflows with a standard deviation of 𝑠𝑑8,step3 =
±5.75%. It is worth noting that the two largest models couldn’t run the final 4th step due to an 
overuse of RAM, why the time spent on the 3rd step for these two cases are underestimated. The 
average time used on the 3rd step for the 4 smallest models are therefore found to be 𝑡6,step3 =
87,34% ± 5,33%.

8.2 Computational limitations of the developed ARA 
The process times may depend on the hardware used. The specifications of the computer used 
for the test case models presented throughout this thesis can be found in Appendix O. 
What was especially noted during the testing of the developed ARA was the time spent on 
running it. With the chosen workflow making use of the .xml file format and the data-extraction 
thereof, numerous cyclic run-throughs of the element trees are required in order to e.g. count the 
numbers of different structural elements in each file, read through resulting data in the .csv files, 
count the number of overutilized elements, and read and connect element IDs (and results) from 
the .xml files (and .csv files) with the 3D model in Revit. The times spent on these processes 
were seen to not only depend on the number of .xml files but of the sizes of the individual files 
as well - both which are increased as the size of the model (the number of elements) increases. 
As it can be seen in Figure 7-8 in Section 7.3 a sample of process times has been recorded for 
the tests of the ARA show an approachable exponential growth in run-time as a function of the 
number of elements in the initial 3D model. 
As mentioned in the previous Section 8.1, the developed workflow doesn’t allow for flushing of 
processed data in each of the process steps, thus causing a momentary build-up of stored data. 
Tests done on the largest of tested 3D models (≥ 1000 elements) have shown that these 
processes may cause unforeseen failures in the software of Dynamo, causing it to become 
inoperative and unresponsive. By studying the computer’s performance in Windows Task 
Manager5 the freezing was evaluated and assessed to be caused by a restriction of the use of 
RAM, meaning that the storage of data currently in use is of a too large amount for the 
computer to handle. This can be e.g. be seen by the lack of process times for step 4 for the 
models with 1359 and 1986 elements (Figure 7-8 and Figure 7-9 in Section 7.3), which was 
caused by the unresponsive behaviour from Dynamo. These process times are therefore noted 
with a hollow circle instead of a dot, to indicate the lack of process times recorded for the final 
step.

Despite the extensive use of RAMs being caused by the ARA processes, the ARA process itself

is run in Dynamo, the software itself has not been proven able to flush any of the previously 
processed data, which could have negated the constant accumulation of RAM in use. The 
limitations regarding the time spent on the processes—and the use of RAM—is therefore 
assessed to be a discrepancy between three main factors: 
• 
Amount of data caused by the number and sizes of the .struxml files caused by the size 
and complexity of the 3D model 
• 
Hardware (RAM) 
• 
Software (Dynamo) 
Several solutions have been suggested for how to possibly help bypass a large portion of the 
restrictions caused by the accumulation of the RAM, none of which has been tested: 
• 
Acquire computers with a larger RAM storage. Increasing the RAM may, however—
with the use of the RAM exponentially increasing for the larger sized models (see 
Figure 7-8)—not be a viable solution on its own. 
• 
Change the complete workflow to support flushing e.g. after analysing each failure 
scenario. This could e.g. be achieved by; 
o the use of an API for FEM-Design (not released at the time of writing), or 
o the use of another FEM software like Autodesk’s Robot Structural Analysis 
Software, which has an API developed. 
• 
Change to another programming environment than Dynamo6. Other (or future) 
environments may provide the capability of flushing unwanted data, thus enabling 
running of—in theory—models of any size, as long as the flushing occurs before the 
RAMs are fully utilized. 
• 
Optimization of the appertaining code which determines what—and how much—data is 
extracted and stored. Certain data-sorting algorithms may reduce the current time 
needed for read-throughs of the data, ultimately potentially reducing both the speed of 
and the RAM required to run the ARA. 
Even if the codes get optimized and times required to run step 1, 2 and 4 decreased drastically, 
the main process time of the ARA still consists of the 3rd step. The time spent on analysing a 
model is determined by the FEM and analysis software in question.

8.3.2 Walls to columns 
A short summary of the theory regarding reinforced concrete walls found relevant can be found 
in Appendix C. 
Columns are typically only considered as being columns if the following two criteria apply  
(Jensen B. C., 2012): 
• 
The length of the column (the wall height) is at least 3 times the height of the cross-
section (the wall thickness). 
• 
The height of the cross-section (the wall thickness) and the width of the cross-section (the 
wall width) do not exceed a difference equal to a factor of 4.  
Through the ARA the walls are split up into wall sections with a width of 500 mm, meaning that 
failures scenarios are simulated for each 500 mm of wall, thus in some cases “leaving behind” a 
single 500 mm wall element.

failure scenarios leaving a stand-alone wall element intact, the difference between the width and 
the height of the cross-section becomes

300 mm = 1.67 < 4, thus suggesting that the wall 
section (or even two adjacent) could be considered as a column with a possibility of biaxial 
deformations. This is however not available through the current ARA since the type of the

insignificant for the tested models since no lateral loads or eccentricities are present. 
A method for the ARA to change the type of the element could theoretically be possible by the

theoretically possible—would result in a large amount of additional data merely from checking 
if the wall sections are standing alone.

determine whether different structural sections will experience an overturning, thus indicating a 
need for e.g. counter-effective vertical ties. The method is based upon basic static 
considerations, thus forming a relatively quick manual method. 
In contrast to the manual method, the developed ARA is assessed to work the best when used on 
wholesome 3D models. This—however—results in a method that isn’t as flexible as the manual 
one, meaning that it isn’t as easily “customizable” in regard to checking specific structural

in the way they both operate, and in the results obtained. 
 
The manual method: 
 
 
 
 
An approach based upon basic static considerations. 
 
The end goal of this approach is to assess the stability of 
the structure, by examining whether overturning occurs. 
 
This approach may lead to the dimensioning of tensile 
ties (either horizontal or vertical), which is to counteract 
any overturning, e.g. caused by a specific notional 
removal of a single element or wall section. 
 
The computational method: An approach based upon basic static considerations, 
internal forces, and load carrying capacity of elements. 
 
The end goal of this approach is to assess the criticalness 
of all the single elements or wall sections, by examining 
how many other elements are critically influenced by a 
notional removal thereof. 
 
This approach may lead to the localization of the most 
critical (key) elements, which are to be dimensioned 
according to the increased risks involved with a potential 
removal thereof. 
 
In addition to the distinct operations and results obtained from the two methods, the advantages 
and disadvantages of these on a more general note has also been studied. Both methods require 
some sort of knowledge and skill; 
- 
the manual method requires for the user to have knowledge of static systems and be 
observant regarding the specific critical structural areas either specified by the 
individual or company-based routines, whereas 
- 
the computational method requires for the user to being able to prepare the analytical 
3D model. Furthermore, it is recommended that the user understands the processes and 
whether the results are valid or relevant in scenarios depending on the analyses.

The advantages and disadvantages listed both prior to and in Table 8-2 may be considered a

use of one of the methods doesn’t necessarily exclude the use of the other. 
In short, the computational method is evaluated to have a potential use for localizing the—
objectively speaking—most critical key elements for larger 3D models. The method doesn’t 
require any notable extra time, as a structural engineer might need when analysing and

3D models may limit the potential.

and haven’t been found suited for stability analysis has maintained the usefulness of the manual 
method. The manual method is—in addition to the qualitative results of the critical elements 
provided by the computational method—furthermore used in quantitative analyses to determine 
the need and design of tensile ties.

9 Conclusions 
This thesis has aimed to study whether it’s possible to develop a computational method to help 
assess the robustness of a pre-modelled 3D building structure through simulation of all possible 
local failure scenarios. To answer this, a computational method has been developed and tested 
on pre-modelled 3D building structures. The method consists of four automated processes, 
which together forms what is denoted as—to the knowledge of the authors—the first Automated 
Robustness Analysis (ARA) workflow. 
The ARA enables an automatic simulation of all possible local element-based failure scenarios 
of an existing 3D model and a visualization of which structural elements or sections—when 
removed—cause the most other elements to fail. This provides the user with an overview of the 
scenarios that are deemed the most critical. 
The workflow of the ARA is—by use of IronPython 2.7.3—developed in Autodesk’s visual 
programming tool of Dynamo, their 3D BIM software of Revit, and StruSoft’s FEM and 
analysis software of FEM-Design. 
To use the ARA, the 3D model must be analytically correct, meaning that the cross-section, 
material properties, loads and load combinations, boundary conditions, and—in some cases—
reinforcement all need to be properly defined. Furthermore, any wrongly connected elements 
and element collisions may provide faulty results. 
To test the functioning and outcome of the ARA a variety of 2D and 3D models were designed. 
For the 3D models, these tests provided results in compliance with the expected outcome. The 
expected outcome was based upon assessments of how many adjacent elements structurally 
relied on the particular elements. The results of the 2D models were more unclear. This was 
assessed most likely to be partly caused by the small number of elements and partly caused by 
the lack of consideration for the elements in the 3rd dimension. 
A few unexpected behaviours were detected during the testing of the ARA. The most 
noteworthy involved a problem with the window apertures spanning the whole width of a 500 
mm wall section, thus splitting a single wall element into two. However—since the two 
elements shared the same parameters (including GUID)—the software of FEM-Design couldn’t 
==PAGE:112==
 
9. Conclusions 
 
Page 98 of 154 
 
model them, resulting in vertical gaps throughout the model. This behaviour was—like the 
others—assessed to not having a vital impact on the outcome of tests done in this study.  
However, limitations were observed regarding the hardware in use. Tests done on the largest of 
designed models were unable to be completed due to the high use of RAM by the ARA. This 
meant that the tests were restricted to models of a certain size. The largest model—for which the 
ARA was completed—was for this study a 23-storey high-rise building consisting of 1359 
failure scenarios. 
A comparison has been made between the developed ARA and a state-of-the-art manual 
method. The two methods have been deemed distinct in both operation and results; the manual 
method assessing the stability of the structure, thus examining the proneness of overturning—
thus the need for tensile ties—whereas the ARA provides a qualitative measure of which 
elements—when removed—cause the most other elements to fail, thus indicating the 
criticalness or importance of the elements. Furthermore, the ARA doesn’t provide any regard 
towards the joints of the structure. 
From this study, it seems ambiguous if a computational method may replace (all) the manual 
method(s) for assessing the robustness of building structures. However, this study has provided 
a computational method that may help in the process of assessing it.

10 
Future improvements 
This final chapter presents some of the many considered ideas for improving or utilizing the 
developed workflow. The improvements consist of the altering of or adding of functions to the 
developed ARA. The ideas for utilizing the workflow are based upon the idea of using the 
cyclic nature of the workflow, which enables automatic repetition of a great number of different 
models, to develop other tools for structural (or other) assessments. Only ideas, whose 
development has been assessed practically possible, are presented. 
10.1 Improving the existing workflow 
10.1.1 Change of workflow (API) 
The change of workflow from the currently developed to the originally intended would 
eliminate the need for reading through and editing the .struxml files, thus resulting in a more 
direct process regarding the exportation from Revit to the FEM software. This elimination—in 
the addition of the enabling of consistent flushing—would reduce the total process time by 
reducing a substantial part of the heavy memory-reliant processes. 
10.1.2 Make reinforcement transferrable from Revit to FEM-Design 
As the ARA works right now, the preparation (step 0) may be done partly in Revit. However, it 
can always only partly be done in Revit in the case of there being any reinforced concrete 
elements. This is due to the fact that FEM-Design is not supporting reading of reinforcement 
data from Revit. In the same manner, as Madsen & Andersen (2018) did for wall edge releases, 
the data for reinforcement in elements could, however—by using a standardized input-form for 
reinforcement in Revit—be transferred from Revit to FEM-Design. This could potentially result 
in the user of the current ARA not having to interact with the FEM software at all, thus 
providing a larger number of potential users to gain value thereof. 
 
==PAGE:114==
 
10. Future improvements 
 
Page 100 of 154 
 
10.1.3 Reduce data amount and process times by not splitting walls 
A large part of the .struxml files—that are read through—consists of the data appertaining the 
analytical wall models. However, due to the splitting of the wall elements into 0.5 m sections, a 
single wall with a length of 5.0 m entails 10 times as much data than if the wall element wasn’t 
split. The splitting of the walls was, however—for now—the only found method of removing 3 
m wall segments capable of overlapping one another. The consequences of splitting the wall 
elements into larger sections with lengths of e.g. 1.0 m could also be examined, in this case 
(wall sections lengths from 0.5 m to 1.0 m) potentially reducing the amounts of data 
appertaining the wall elements—thus the process time of the ARA—by 50%. 
10.1.4 Optimization through algorithms 
Due to a large amount of raw data—especially in the .csv files containing the results from the 
analyses—algorithms could possibly be implemented through the code to reduce the processing 
time and power required. An example of this could be the algorithm of a binary search tree 
(Sedgewick & Wayne, 2011, pp. 396-414). The idea behind this algorithm is to initially sort out 
the data in such a way that future readings of it will be much less time and memory consuming. 
This is done by organizing the data in a tree structure according to set criteria, e.g. individual 
elements according to utilization percentages or the individual .csv files according to the 
number of overutilized elements per failure scenario. 
10.1.5 Closer localization of critical areas beforehand 
As it has been provided in Section 7.3, the process time of the ARA increases drastically when 
the number of modelled elements crosses a certain limit (depending on the hardware). For larger 
models, the current form of the ARA might even fully utilize the RAM before the process is 
completed. This can potentially result in practically infinite process times, why sizes of models 
within a certain limit are recommended. One of the reasons for this happening is that the current 
ARA analyses all failure scenarios – even the ones that—through experience—quite obviously 
aren’t critical. A way to reduce the use of RAM and potentially assess larger structure models 
using the ARA could be to limit the extent of analyses. This could e.g. be done by developing a 
pre-analysis system to prompt the user to manually select the part(s) of the structure, in which 
the removal of elements is wished studied. 
10.1.6 Collapse of slabs 
As mentioned in section Section 2.1 it is in DS/EN 1991-1-7 (2007) recommended using the 
area of the floor collapse as an indicator for when the collapse extends beyond what is assessed 
an admissible local failure (15% of the relevant floor area). This has—despite it not being the 
chosen solution in this case—been assessed possible with FEM-Design as the chosen software. 
It could—in a similar way as for the walls—be done by finding the number of floor slabs that 
are being overutilized in each failure scenario. Beneficial to estimating a collapsed floor area, 
one may split the floor sections into squares, similar to the walls being split into vertical 
==PAGE:115==
 
10. Future improvements 
Page 101 of 154 
 
rectangles. This would result in a finer mesh of floor panels than the larger slab elements 
(typically modelled as wholesome floor slabs), possibly leading to a closer-to-realistic floor 
collapse. Such a split of slab elements would, however, increase the number of calculations as 
well as the data output. Furthermore, it would require some reflection regarding the boundary 
conditions of the slab elements. 
10.1.7 In-depth quantitative analysis 
The current state of the ARA only visualizes the results of the number of highest found utility 
ratios of the elements, which—simply put—is a qualitative output. However, the utilization 
ratio is merely a maximum of the calculation results (see Table D-1 and Table D-2 in Appendix 
D) provided by FEM-Design. This, however, means that the current .csv files—which are 
created after analysing each of the failure scenarios—contains much more in-depth results. 
These results could—for the sake of developing a greater customized design-tool—be extracted. 
An extraction or maybe even visualization thereof could lead to wide ranges of views or 
visualizations containing the more specific results of every one of the calculations or areas of 
interest, meaning that the designer—instead of just having visualized which elements are the 
most critical key elements—may be provided with the structural aspect of why the element is 
assessed unviable. 
10.1.8 Visualization depending on failure criteria 
The current workflow of the ARA provides a more general visualization of which elements—
when removed—entails the most other elements to fail. This could, however, be improved with 
an additional workflow function, consisting of a method for the user to manually select an 
element to visualize which elements were concluded to fail by the removal thereof. This could 
provide the user with a clearer idea of the more specific consequences of the actions, and 
potentially contribute to a better understanding thereof – which might lead to better design 
choices based on the failure. A way to do this could e.g. be to bundle the ARA workflow into a 
user interface (UI, e.g. a “menu”), from which the workflow could be run, and such post-
functions can be accessed. A choice in the UI could also additionally be to create new viewports 
in which the specific failure scenario and the consequences thereof are visualized. 
10.1.9 Update workflow according to software versions 
The names and version numbers of the software used to develop the workflow are presented in 
Table 3-1. Despite developing the workflow on an as-“general”-as-possible level (such as 
coding the necessary Dynamo nodes instead of using packages relying on 3rd parties), updates 
for the software in use (Revit, Dynamo, and FEM-Design) may result in the current workflow 
not being compatible with the updated software versions. This means that it is assessed likely 
that a future update of the workflow must be done in order for it to be compatible with future 
versions of the software in mention. It is also likely that future updates of the software in 
mention may cause a potential improvement to the workflow or the speed thereof.

10.2 Utilizing the workflow for other tasks 
10.2.1 Specific checking for progressive collapses 
The cyclic workflow developed for this computational automated robustness analysis could be 
used for other examinations, such as to inspect structures regarding the likelihood of progressive 
collapses. This could be done by—after running the 1st ARA process as it is provided in this 
report—running a 2nd or even xth amount of analyses for each failure scenario, for each analysis 
removing all members being overutilized due to other being removed. This would provide a 
clear understanding of how a collapse would happen as a result of an initial removal of 
elements. Due to a large amount of data provided and processing power required to run the 
currently used workflow, this has not been a reachable goal within the provided time frame. 
10.2.2 Use of workflow to assess joints 
The ARA is—at its current state—only analysing the structural elements without giving regard 
to the strength of the connections or joints of the structure. By the use of the workflow 
developed for the ARA, a similar workflow could be developed in which—instead of analysing 
the carrying capacity of the elements—the carrying capacity of the connections could be 
assessed. This would—in addition to the current requirements set for the 3D models—however, 
set additional requirements for the design and modelling of such connections. Whether this is 
possible directly through Revit, or the design thereof must be designed in the FEM and analysis 
software, haven’t at this point been examined. 
10.2.3 Check of possible load paths 
For the analyses of the structural system using the ARA, the alternative load paths are assumed 
in FEM-Design according to the pre-defined connections. However, no regard is giving towards 
the checking or confirmation of these connections being viable for transferring the present 
internal forces. In reality, when a local failure occurs in a vertical load-carrying section, the 
internal forces must find alternative load paths towards the bearing component, which (in 
concrete structures) is often done by the use of both vertical and horizontal tension ties. A 
system may possibly be developed to examine these ties in a building throughout, in order to 
verify the assumed load paths. 
10.2.4 Analysis of dynamic actions 
The workflow could be utilized to provide a structural understanding of dynamic actions such as 
seismic effects on building structures according to Annex C in DS/EN 1991-1-7 (2007, p. 52). 
By using the cyclic workflow—and the ability to process a large variety of design solutions 
through Dynamo—these different designs could be examined at once. From this, optimization 
of designs could be streamlined.

Appendix B - European and Danish regulations on robustness 
In this appendix, the requirements and principles regarding the robustness of building structures 
and the analysis thereof that are found most relevant are presented. The primary sources are 
DS/EN 1990:2007 and DS/EN 1991-1-7:2007. It is worth noting that the Eurocodes and Danish 
standards, e.g. EN 1990, differentiates between principles (read: legal requirements) and 
application rules (read: suggested methods) (Danish: ‘principper’ and ‘anvendelsesregler’, 
respectively). The understanding of the European and local standards—and the differentiation 
between what is legal requirements and what is suggestive requirements—might proof crucial in 
the understanding and execution of how to address robustness analyses with any predefined 
method or in general. 
The principles comprise of general statements and definitions with no alternative rules or 
calculation methods unless stated so. These are marked with a “P” after a number in parenthesis. 
In addition to these principles, the application rules are commonly acclaimed rules in 
compliance with the principles and their requirements, they just consist of a number in a 
parenthesis. 
B.1 Basic requirements of structural designs 
According to DS/EN 1990:2007 (2007) a structure must be both designed and executed with 
adequate structural resistance and durability, thus not being critically damaged in events such as 
explosions, collisional impacts or consequences due to human errors disproportionate to the 
cause of the building. The events to be considered are to be agreed upon with the client and the 
relevant authority for each individual project. 
 
“(4)P A structure shall be designed and executed in such a way that it 
will not be damaged by events such as: 
- 
explosion, 
- 
impact, and 
- 
the consequences of human errors, 
to an extent disproportionate to the original cause. 
 
NOTE 1 The events to be taken into account are those agreed for an individual project 
with the client and the relevant authority.” (DS/EN 1990:2007, p. 23)

accidental situations are dependent on the classification of consequence classes, thus dependent 
on the assessment of the probability or the magnitude of loss of human lives. 
 
“(1) The strategies for accidental design may be based on the following

B.3 Acceptable and non-acceptable damage 
In addition to the recommended criteria of the collapse not exceeding a certain limit of floor 
area presented in the previous subsection, an incontestable principle is also provided by DS/EN 
1991-1-7 (2007): 
 
“(1)P Structures shall be designed to resist progressive collapse 
resulting from an internal explosion. 
 
(2) The design may permit failure of a limited part of the structure 
provided this does not include key elements upon which the stability of 
the whole structure depends.” (DS/EN 1991-1-7:2007, p. 32) 
 
 
According to the above DS/EN 1991-1-7 (2007) states that structures shall be designed to resist 
progressive collapses, thus accepting failure of a limited part of the structure as long as no key 
elements are included in the collapse. 
 
“(2) A localized failure due to accidental actions may be acceptable, 
provided it will not endanger the stability of the whole structure, and 
that the overall load-bearing capacity of the structure is maintained 
and allows necessary emergency measure to be taken.” (DS/EN 1991-
1-7:2007, pp. 16-17) 
 
 
B.4 Design approach 
In prolongation of the criteria and principles from the previous subsections, some of the 
recommended approaches in evaluating the building structures are prescribed in DS/EN 1991-1-
7 (2007): 
 
“(3) Measures should be taken to mitigate the risk of accidental 
actions and these measures should include, as appropriate, one or 
more of the following strategies: 
a) preventing the action from occurring (e.g. in the case of 
bridges, by providing adequate clearances between the 
trafficked lanes and the structures) or reducing the probability 
and/or magnitude of the action to an acceptable level through 
the structural design process (e.g. in the case of buildings 
providing sacrificial venting components with a low mass and 
strength to reduce the effect of explosions); 
b) protecting the structure against the effects of an accidental 
action by reducing the effects of the actions on the structure 
(e.g. by protective bollards or safety barriers); 
c) ensuring that the structure has sufficient robustness by 
adopting one or more of the following approaches: 
 
==PAGE:135==
 
Appendix B - European and Danish regulations on robustness 
 
Page 121 of 154 
 
1) by designing certain components of the structure upon 
which stability depends as key elements to increase the 
likelihood of the structure’s survival following an 
accidental event. 
2) designing structural members, and selecting materials, to 
have sufficient ductility capable of absorbing significant 
strain energy without rupture. 
3) incorporating sufficient redundancy in the structure to 
facilitate the transfer of actions to alternative load paths 
following an accidental event. 
 
(4) Accidental actions shall, where appropriate, be applied 
simultaneously in combination with permanent and other variable 
actions in accordance with EN 1990, 6.4.3.311. 
 
(5) The safety of the structure immediately following the occurrence of 
the accidental action shall be taken into account.” (DS/EN 1991-1-
7:2007, pp. 16-17) 
 
The above application rules (3), (4) and (5) refers to measurement strategies recommended 
taken to reduce the probability and/or magnitude of the action. In order to establish such a base, 
both the actions and the structural design process must be defined.  
According to the Danish Standards (DS/EN 1990:2007) a distinction should be made between 
ultimate limit states and serviceability limit states, whereas each of the limit state design 
situations must be related to any of the four design classifications presented in the following 2nd 
principle: 
 
“(1)P The relevant design situations shall be selected taking into 
account the circumstances under which the structure is required to 
fulfil its function. 
 
(2)P Design situations shall be classified as follows: 
- persistent design situations, which refer to the condition of normal 
use; 
- transient design situations, which refer to temporary conditions 
applicable to the structure, e.g. during execution or repair;

Appendix E - What is XML and how is it relevant? 
Extensible Markup Language or XML is a structured text-based system that is designed to store 
and transport data. Markup languages and file types written on the base of it such as .xml are 
meant to be self-descriptive, meaning that it’s meant to be readable by both humans and 
machines (w3schools, n.d.), why they in general often combine text and descriptions of the text, 
being of e.g. the structure or presentation of the text (Merriam-Webster, n.d.). This means that 
.xml files themselves do not directly include program code, but rather a well-structured set of 
data, thus making consistent readings of the data possible. This enables a source code or a 
program to process different tasks based on the readings thereof (Cover, 2002) (Korpela, 2005). 
When exporting a model from e.g. Revit into a FEM-Design readable format through the Revit 
Add-In (StruSoft, What's new with the latest StruXML Revit Add-In, 2014), the add-in creates a 
.xml file containing all the data necessary for FEM-Design to re-model the analytical Revit 
model. The particular .xml files used in the XML reading system of FEM-Design is denoted 
.struxml. These are .xml files structured in a very specific way for FEM-Design to read and 
interpret (StruSoft, How to use the StruXML Revit Add-In?, 2015). 
 
The .struxml files of FEM-Design 
The structure of XML files is often referred to as being a tree structure. The tree consists of 
folders and subfolders with elements and subelements as seen on the Figures E-1, E-2, and E-3. 
Whenever a 3D model is either saved in FEM-Design or exported to the FEM-Design readable 
format, the format is merely a well-organized XML file. The structure of the XML file is 
specifically designed for the software to interpret, why the XML files associated with or 
readable by FEM-Design are denoted with the file type of .struxml. 
All the elements in the FEM-Design model are presented by a folder in the .struxml file. Each 
element is specified by giving it a unique identification label called GUID, which is consistent 
in both the Revit model and the FEM-Design model, thus ensuring that the two models are 
identical. 
If a .struxml file gets modified by using a text reading program, such as XML Notepad, the 
change will also appear when opening the model in FEM-Design. A change can e.g. be to delete 
a folder of an element in the .struxml file, thus deleting the element from the model.

Appendix F - What is CSV and how is it relevant?

Appendix F - What is CSV and how is it relevant? 
Comma-Separated Values or CSV is a text-based system that uses commas to separate values or 
data such as text or numbers. Files created with the CSV format (.csv files) can—due to the 
commas indicating the separation of the data—be read or interpreted as a table, which allows 
for spreadsheet programs such as Microsoft Excel or Google Spreadsheets to read and sort the 
data. Worth noting is that the .csv files are of a much simpler structure than the other 
spreadsheet file types as they cannot save cell, column, or row styling as well as not being able 
to interpret formulas (BigCommerce, 2018). The simplicity ensures a relatively small file size as 
the file format only consists of text, numbers, and commas, thus making it a relevant candidate 
to be used to exchange large amounts of data between different applications (Fisher, 2018). 
It is possible to retrieve the results of any structural analysis in FEM-Design in a .csv file format 
(StruSoft, 2010). This further means that it is possible to retrieve the results of all the structural 
analyses done by all of the failure scenarios in a .csv file appertaining the individual scenarios. 
The results of these analyses are wished visualized on the 3D model, why all of these .csv files 
must be read and interpreted. With these large amounts of files with resulting data, it has been 
proven necessary to use a relatively small and simple file type, i.e. the .csv file format.

The time spent on modifying each child .struxml file of the biggest model with 1986 failure 
scenarios can be found on figure N-1 and N-2 below. Figure N-1 shows the time of the day in 
which the file was modified, whereas figure N-2 shows the time spent on modifying each file.  
On Figure N-1 the space between each point (file) indicates the time spent on the file, which 
means that the more space there is between the points and the steeper the graph becomes, the 
slower the process went. Therefore, the times where the computer’s RAM capacity was full can 
be observed, indicating when the process needed to be restarted. E.g. around file no. 1550, 
where the points get further and further away from each other until the process was manually 
restarted just prior to the processing of file no. 1600.   
 
Figure N-1: A graph showing the time of modification of each file from the biggest model. 
The same can be observed on figure N-2, where the time spent on each file is shown. Most of 
the files took less than half an hour to do, but a few took more than one hour – and one even 
seven hours. This also illustrates how the computer’s RAM capacity was full and that the 
process needed to be restarted. When aware of it, the 1st, 2nd and 3rd step were manually 
“flushed” by restarting the processes after a certain number of processed files. However, the 
RAM filling was a serious problem in regard to the 4th step, where the building is being 
coloured, since a restart at a custom file no. isn’t possible with this process. The two figures are 
therefore a good example of why the coding should be optimized in a way that doesn’t get the 
computer to fill up the RAM, and allow the processes to be run without supervision even for 
larger building structures.
