Preface
This thesis is part of the requirements to achieve a Master’s degree from the Technical University
of Denmark. This thesis were preformed as a collaboration between the center of Intelligent Drug
Delivery and Sensing Using Microcontainers and Nanomechanics (IDUN) at the Department
of Micro-and Nanotechnology, Technical University of Denmark and the Faculty of Pharmacy,
University of Helsinki.
The thesis was conducted from January 2017 to June 2017, at the Technical University of
Denmark, including two stays at the University of Helsinki. The experimental work started in
Denmark, where sample preparations were made, followed by two weeks at the University of
Helsinki where pilot studies with CARS microscopy were initiated. After the pilot studies, more
experiments and sample preparations were preformed at the Technical University of Denmark.
Followed by one month of studies at the University of Helsinki, with use of multimodal non-linear
microscopy, kindly provided by the Faculty of Pharmacy, University of Helsinki.

Abstract
The aim of the study was to explore the potential use of Coherent Anti-Stokes Raman Scattering
(CARS) as a tool for in vitro investigations of oral drug delivery systems and interaction between
cells and drug particles.
The oral drug delivery system used in this study consisted of lipid-based nanoparticles (cubosomes)
and a micro carrier system (microcontainers). In this study, spray drying was used to prepare
cubosomes encapsulating an antigen (ovalbumin) and an adjuvant (Quil-A), with a stabilizer
of dextran. For protection of the cubosomes, through the stomach, SU-8 microcontainers were
utilized. For ensuring a controlled release of the cubosomes from the SU-8 microcontainers in
the small intestine, a pH-sensitive lid of the polymer Eudragit L100 were coated on the cavity
of the SU-8 microcontainers after loading of the cubosomes.
CARS microscopy was used simultaneously with other non-linear optical methods such as second
harmonic generation (SHG), two-photon excited ﬂuorescence (TPEF) and Raman scattering
for imaging the drug delivery system of cubosomes and microcontainers. Raman scattering
was used for characterization of vibrational bands of molecules for use in CARS microscopy.
Release studies prior to CARS microscopy was conducted on a µDiss proﬁler to interpret
when the cubosomes were released from the microcontainers.
Furthermore, X-ray powder
diﬀraction (XRPD) was used for internal characterization of cubosomes.
During this work
it was possible to obtain clear images of microcontainers made of SU-8 (model system) and
polycaprolactone (biodegradable). Cubosomes were visualized by CARS microscopy, where the
non-linear phenomena SHG was detected with a strong signal. The cubosomes release from the
microcontainers were preformed over a time period of 30 min, but no release were detected in
CARS microscopy, even though the initial studies showed promising results. The dissolution
of the pH-sensitive Eudragit L100 lid was possible with a lid thickness of 28±18 µm. A piece
of small intestine from a pig was fused with SU-8 microcontainers loaded with furosemide, and
was used for investigation of the attachment of SU-microcontainers. Few SU-8 microcontainers
were found in the piece of small intestine, where they were covered in mucus, preventing the
determination of the orientation of the cavity of the SU-8 microcontainers. The loaded drug
furosemide, was due to low concentration, not able to be detected by CARS. The interaction
between cubosomes and macrophages were visualized by CARS microscopy, were activation of
the macrophages and possible cubosome up-take was detected.
In conclusion CARS microscopy was found to be promising tool for in vitro investigation of
oral drug delivery systems, and for investigation of the interactions between cells and drug
particulates.

Introduction
Oral drug delivery is an area within the pharmaceutical industry expected to show signiﬁcant
growth in the upcoming years. Oral products represent 70% of the values on the US pharmaceutical
market. Where the oral drug delivery products are 60% of the drug delivery market [1]. The
main driving force of growth in this area is the patients preference for a non-invasive route
of administration [2]. Oral drug delivery provide products with high patient compliance, low
production cost and eﬀortless administration [3]. Despite these beneﬁts, oral delivery has some
challenges such as immediate release causing toxicity and lower the penetration across the
gastrointestinal (GI) wall. The main obstacles are the multiple barriers presented through the
GI tract, such as pH changes, enzymatic and chemical degradation [3]. These barrier have to be
overcome in order for successful delivery of drug to a speciﬁc target. Nanotechnology presents
some solutions in this ﬁeld, such as using polymer or lipid based nanoparticles as a carrier
system [4]. These carrier systems permit delivery of poorly soluble drugs and can protect drugs
against changes in the pH value and enzyme degradation in the GI tract [5].
1.1
Gastrointestinal Tract
Oral delivered drugs travel through the GI tract consisting of the mouth, throat, stomach and
the upper and lower intestine (Figure 1).

The GI tract controls and regulates uptake and absorption of nutrient and microorganisms,
working as a protective barrier against pathogens and other foreign substances. Challenges for

oral delivered drugs in the GI tract are the broad pH span from pH 1 up to pH 8, the presence
of digestive enzymes and the intestinal mucosal layers [6]. The stomach is a harsh environment
for most oral administrated macromolecules such as peptides and proteins. In fasted state the
stomach has pH 1 to pH 3 and can reach up to pH 5 in fed state [7]. This low pH value in fasted

changes in pH value together with secreted digestive enzyme such as bile and hydrochloric acids
[8], can result in hydrolysis, oxidation, deamidation and can lead to deactivation of the drug
[9]. Drug components such as peptides and proteins have to be absorbed in the small intestine,
due to degradation in the stomach where little to no absorption occur [10].
1.2
Small Intestine
In the GI tract the highest percentage of absorption occur in the small intestine, and is the best
target for drug absorption. The small intestine are divided in three regions; the duodenum (pH
6-7), jejunum (pH 6-7.7), and ileum (pH 6.5-8.0) [11]. Most absorption and uptake of nutrients
and particulates occur in the jejunum and some in the ileum [12]. The intestinal surface are
arranged into crypt and villi, which enhances the absorptive surface area to 200-300 m2 [12].
Villi consists of a layer of cells; the intestinal epithelium, these cells are connected by junctions
and forms the surface of the small intestine and colon. The epithelial cell layer is part of the
mucosa layer, consisting of three layers, the lamina propria and the muscularis mucosae. The
surface facing the lumen are covered with microvilli and two layer of mucus an attached layer
and a overlaying mucus layer (Figure2)[13].
Figure 2: Schematic of the structure of the small intestinal wall.
Villi are covered with micro villi and surrounded with mucus layers.
The intestinal epithelium consists mainly of absorptive enterocytes and goblet cells secreting
mucus. Less abundant cells are paneth cells, enteroendocrine cells and microfold-cells (M-cells).

The M-cells are located at the follicle-associated epithelium of the intestinal Peyer’s patches,
serving as portals for diﬀerent particles. The M-cells transport particulates from the lumen into
the lamina propria where a large population of immune cells such as dendritic cells, lymphocytes
and macrophages are present [17]. M-cells can take up antigens, proteins and microorganisms
from the lumen by phago-,endo-,pino- or transcytosis [18]. The M-cell’s capability to absorb
antigens and microorganisms, are used as potential targets for oral administrated drugs, due to
the low coverage by mucus compared to other GALT cells [13]. Since only 5% of the epithelial
cells in the intestinal tract are M-cells, speciﬁc target for M-cells have been investigated to
enhance the binding speciﬁcity and drug uptake. Few M-cell targets are known as the toll like
receptor (TLR-4), platelet-activating factor receptor (PAPR) and the integrin (α5β1) [14].

1.3
Mucus
Mucus covers the GI epithelium, and works as a facilitator of food and chyme by lubricating the
epithelium membrane. Other functions are the protection of the epithelium from pathogens,
toxins and endogenous substances such as digestive enzymes [6].
Mucus is a hydrogel composed of proteins, mainly mucins accounting for 2-5% of the total
volume, the rest is water and a small fraction are lipids and carbohydrates. Mucins are O-linked
glycoproteins, controlling the gel structure of the mucus layer [19]. There are at least twenty
proteins encode genes from the mucin gene family (MUC). Genes such as MUC2, MUC5AC and
MUC6 are the most abundant types in GI tract [20]. The visoelastic properties of mucus are
essential for the lubricating and protective functions in the GI tract. The viscosity of mucus is
balanced to trap pathogens and prevent uptake by epithelium, simultaneously with continuous
ﬂow of nutrient uptake. Studies are showing an increase in viscosity at decreasing pH [21],
which makes the viscosity in the stomach signiﬁcant higher than in the small intestine.
The thickness of mucus varies though the GI tract, depending on uptake and removal needs of
the diﬀerent regions in the GI tract. The mucus layer can be up to 200 µm in the stomach which
is the thickest layer in the GI tract followed by the colon (110-160 µm). In the small intestine
the mucus layer varies depending on the on diet, and the thickness has been measured from 7
to 30 µm [6]. Two mucus layer covers the epithelial cells in the GI tract. A ﬁrmly adherent
mucus layer and a loosely overlying layer. In the small intestine mucus layer are renewed by
the mucus secreting goblet cells [13]. The mucus layer in the small intestine are thin compared
to the stomach and colon, to allow a higher nutrient uptake. The thin layer of mucus and the
high uptake of nutrient and other substance makes the small intestine the best target for oral
delivered drug [22].
Diﬀerent approaches has been suggested in order to overcome the mucus layer and improve
the bioavailability of drugs. Increasing the mucoadhesion of the delivery system, could provide
improved residence time for the system and increase the local drug concentration for an increased
uptake from the mucosal surface. Another approach is to use mucus-penetrating particles, by
exploiting neutral charge particles with lack of hydrophobic regions, since they seem to have an
increase in penetration of mucus [23].

1.4
Vaccines
Vaccine development have expanded and are one of the most eﬀective methods for prevention
of diseases and are an important contribution to the public health world wide [24]. A Vaccine
is a biological preparation for improving immunity towards a speciﬁc disease.
There are diﬀerent types of vaccines, live attenuated, inactivated and subunit vaccines [25].
The live attenuated and inactivated vaccines consist of live microorganisms weakened or killed
before use as a vaccine to induce an immune response. These vaccines are very eﬀective and
elicit long-lasting immunity, but are accompanied with some risks. The risks associated with the
microorganisms are the capacity to replicate and the reversion of microorganisms into virulent
form, such as the polio vaccine in 1970 [26].
Other vaccines have been developed with limiting side eﬀects.
These vaccines are subunit
vaccines consisting of small pieces of microorganisms, selected to induce a appropriate immune
response with less side-eﬀects [27]. Microbial components in subunit vaccines are peptides and
proteins with the ability to induce a immune response once absorbed and represented by antigen
presenting cells (APC). The proteins and peptides do not have the ability to replicate and induce
virulent responses. The subunit vaccine can be designed speciﬁcally for any given disease and
are not as sensitive as other vaccines which makes them easier for production and up-scaling [28].
Subunit vaccine is an ideal way of inducing a immune response with low risk, but have the
disadvantage of a low immunogenicity [29]. Improving the immune response induced by the
peptide and proteins, immunostimulatory agents such as adjuvants are needed to increase the
immune response [24]. The use of adjuvants are very useful in development of subunit vaccines.
An adjuvant is a substance with the ability to enhance the immune response against the
antigen. Ajuvant can be categorized into two groups; delivery systems and immunopotentiating
compounds. Delivery systems work by deliver of antigens to APC. The immunopotentiating
compounds are often used to amplify immune responses against the antigen and maintain long
term memory responses [28]. Today many vaccines use aluminum salts, prolonging the release
time of antigens [30]. Cytokines, chemokines, bacterial, and natural components have in the
last years gained more interest, due to the adjuvant eﬀects of enhancing the immune response
aganist antigens [31].

1.4.1
Oral Vaccine Delivery
Most vaccines are given by injection, due to the direct route to the blood stream.
Patient
compliance and production coat has force the pharmaceutical companies to work with in the
ﬁeld of oral delivery instead of the traditional injection method. Orally administrated vaccines
have been investigated due to the high patient compliance and few vaccines have already reached
the market against rotavirus and typhoid fever [32]. The oral delivery of vaccines can have a
huge eﬀect on mass vaccination, especially in developing countries [33]. Where vaccines often
have great risks related to non-sterility by contaminated needles and syringes [34].
The main advantages of oral vaccines are the ability to induce a response in both the mucosal
and systemic immune system. This enable oral vaccines to activate both the innate mucosal
immune response simultaneously with the adaptive response. Oral delivery of antigens have a
high risk of degradation in the stomach of the GI tract. For antigens to travel safely through
the stomach, to the small intestine, a protective layer is needed. Micro- and nanoparticles are
good potential carriers and can have the ability to induce a particulate uptake of the M-cells
[35]. These particles serve as delivery vesicles having properties to increase the interaction with
the intestinal mucosal surface and enhance the loading capacity [14]. Nanoparticles at about
300 nm have shown to be absorbed in signiﬁcant amounts in the small intestine [36, 15]. As
previous mentioned ( Section 1.2) the M-cells are often target for drug absorption, the use of
the vaccine carrier system to speciﬁcally target the M-cell could increase the drug uptake [37].
1.5
Particulate System
Micro- and nanoparticles are promising delivery vesicles for drugs, due to low toxic side eﬀects
and enhanced therapeutic eﬀects [38].
An example of a micro particulate are chitosan particles. Chitosan are microparticles derived

1.6
Immune Cells
Vaccines delivered orally are taken up by M-cells over the intestinal wall, into the lamina propria,
where immune eﬀector cells such as dendritic cells and macrophages are located. Macrophages
are the most abundant immune cells present in the intestine [60]. Macrophages are phagocytic
cells and are a part of the ﬁrst-in-line innate immune system. They have the ability to engulf and
digest microorganisms and other foreign substances [28]. Macrophages with their mononuclear
shape are derive from precursors called monocytes originating from the myeloid progenitor cells
in the bone marrow [61]. Macrophages have many functions one of the primary functions are
maintenance and recycle of cells in the host, such as the recycle hemoglobin and iron [62].
Macrophages clean the host for cell debris and cells undergone apoptosis in order to maintain
the balance of the host.
Other important functions of macrophages is the involvement in the immune system. Macrophages
can function as a host defence mechanism against pathogens and play a important role in wound
healing due to the inﬂammation regulatory eﬀects they induce [63]. Macrophages respond to
environmental signals and alteration in the innate or adaptive immune system.
In respond
to these alterations the macrophages can change their phenotype and physiology to become
more aggressive towards foreign subjects. The macrophages secrete chemokines and cytokines
to induce inﬂammation, activating the immune system. Macrophages can act as an APC, by
digesting a pathogen or particle and present a speciﬁc antigen on the MHC class II, which
initiate the Helper T-cells to start an immune response [64]. Macrophages are mostly located
in the tissue, with a high concentration in the intestine. Here some are located in the lumen
where direct absorption of particulates and pathogens occur. Most macrophages are present
in the lamina propria, where M-cells transfer pathogen and other subjects for interaction with
immune cells.

Non-linear optical imaging has shown great potential as a method for pharmaceutical analysis.
Non-linear imaging is an optical technique with two or more photons interacting with a sample.
The method consists of a series of optical phenomena such as Raman scattering, Coherent
Anti-Stokes Raman Scattering (CARS), Two-Photon Excited Fluorescence (TPEF) and Second
Harmonic Generation (SHG). These techniques have shown improved and more detailed optic
properties compared to linear imaging [99]. Non-linear imaging provide better chemical speciﬁcity

biological environments. By the use of ultra short laser pulses the intensity of the incident
light is much higher than linear eﬀects. The laser pulses duration are in the range of pico-
to femtoseconds, which limits the time the laser interact with the sample. This reduces the
risk of photo damage to the sample and make the non-linear technique fast enough for bio
imaging [92]. Non-linear optics are beneﬁcial for imaging and investigation of surface interfaces
and structures, particle size and chemical characterization. SHG and TPEF have been used
for drug formulation analyses, where SHG signal is easily detected in powders with crystalline
structures and TPEF detection works well for auto-ﬂuorescent drugs. The application of these
optic phenomena are well suited for imaging of drug delivery in cells and tissue, when used
simultaneously [100].

1.9
Raman Spectroscopy
Raman spectroscopy is a two-photon inelastic light-scattering method. The scattering technique
can be used for characterization of chemical composition of molecules without the need for
labeling. Every molecule has a unique composition of atoms. The atoms in molecules can be
transferred between diﬀerent energy levels, when excited by light. The excited light can induce
a dipole moment due to the molecule’s unique vibrational energy. In Raman scattering the
vibrational energies are used to create a spectrum characteristic for molecules [101].
When focusing a incident laser light on a molecule, diﬀerent optical interactions can occur
between the photons and the molecule, depending on the properties of the sample and the
wavelength of the photons from the incident light.
Absorption, ﬂuorescence and scattering
are some of the interactions occurring. In Raman spectroscopy scattering process is used. A
monochromatic light is directed to a sample, and scattered photons is emitted by the sample.
Two types of scattered emission exists; elastic and inelastic scattering. The elastic scattering
called Rayleigh scattering, are when the scattered photon has the energy as the incident photons.
No change in energy means no polarization change occur in the molecule [102]. The other type
of scattering is inelastic scattering and is utilized in Raman applications. Here the scattered
photon is at a diﬀerent energy level than the incident photon, due to an induced dipole moment
causing a change in polarization [71].
The incident photon in Raman scattering interact with vibrational or rotational vectors in the
molecule. These vibrational or rotational vectors causes a energy transfer of the molecule [103].
This process generate a scattered photon having a diﬀerent energy than the incident photon,
also called a shift in frequency, the Raman eﬀect. Depending on the molecule’s vibrational
state, the scattered photon can have less or more energy than the incident photon [102].
1.9.1

made possible by applying energy to the system, this phenomena is utilized in Raman scattering
[102].

state, which is higher than the ground state by emission of a photon [104]. The frequency of
the emitted photon is called the stokes frequency, because the scattered photon has less energy
than the incident photon. If the scattered photon has more energy than the incident photon
the molecule needs to be in a start position of a higher vibrational state. Here the incident

1.9.3
Raman Scattering for Pharmaceutical Characterization
Raman scattering is already used for chemical characterization of diﬀerent medical compounds.
The use of Raman for pharmaceutical compounds have been explored and show great advantages,
due to the non-destructive and label free properties which reduce the preparation time of the
samples [107]. Raman is a relatively fast method for gathering data with reduced photo damage
of the samples.
The technique also has a low sensitivity for water, and can be applied on
biological samples with high water content [108]. There are some limitations to the Raman
scattering technique. When using spontaneous Raman scattering, the signal can sometimes be
very weak and in order to get a proper signal the use of higher laser power and longer acquisition
time is needed, which is a major disadvantaged for biological samples [101].
1.9.4
Time Gated Raman
In normal Raman scattering the stokes signal provides a strong signal (Section 1.9.1). Spontaneous
Raman scattering provid a good technique for characterization of substances and materials with
no ﬂuorescence, but most biological samples are to some degree ﬂuorescent. This is a problem
because the stokes frequency is a red-shifted signal (low frequency signal), with the same shift
in frequency as ﬂuorescence [109]. This is a problem when detecting stokes-signals, since the
ﬂuorescence intensity is orders of magnitudes higher than the stokes intensity. The Raman
peaks will be overlapped by the ﬂuorescence and the separation of the two signals are nearly
impossible, due to the signal to noise ratio arising when separating a weak signal from a very
strong overlapping signal [106, 110]. Overcoming this problem a technique has been developed,
Time Gated Raman. Time Gated Raman scattering takes advantage of the time of emission
for Raman active samples and ﬂuorescence materials. Raman active molecules emits scattered
photons picoseconds after excitation, whereas the ﬂuorescence emission is slower and emits a
signal nanoseconds after the excitation [111]. Exploiting this opportunity the two signals can
be separated, by the use of short intensive laser pulses. The laser excites the samples with short
laser pulses, the sample emit photons detected picoseconds after excitation [112]. The Time
Gated Raman technique has been able to get high quality spectra of biological samples, without
photo damage and interference of the ﬂuorescence frequency [113].

1.10
Coherent Anti-Stokes Raman Scattering
Raman scattering is a useful technique for chemical characterization and investigation of biological
systems such as tumor diagnostics and DNA detection [114]. Raman scattering has some major
limitations due to low Raman eﬀects and long acquisition times. Coherent Anti-stokes Raman
Scattering (CARS) microscopy is a non-linear Raman based technique used for imaging and
characterization of diﬀerent chemical and biological systems [115]. CARS exploits the same
unique vibrational signature of molecules as spontaneous Raman scattering. CARS has a 100
times faster imaging speed and yield a larger signal, makeing CARS 6 orders of magnitude
more sensitive than Raman spectrometry.
The sensitivity permits imaging at a video-rate
speed, which is highly beneﬁcial for living biological systems [116].
CARS was ﬁrst discovered by Marker and Terhune in 1965 [117], where they studied non-linear
crystal structures. In 1982 Duncan and co-workers used CARS for imaging onion cells [118].
Since then the technique has been attractive as a non-destructive label free imaging method.
CARS is a promising method for investigation of thick tissue, cellular uptake and for drug
delivery purposes. The ability to visualize various biological systems, are a huge advantage
especially in drug delivery, where it is critical to understand diﬀerent mechanisms and the
changes during drug release in order to design an eﬀective and optimized system [115].
1.10.1
CARS Principle
The CARS process excite molecules into a speciﬁc vibrational mode, where molecules emits
strong anti-stokes signals [115].
CARS is a four wave-mixing process, where three lasers
interact with the sample to generate a fourth wavelength at a diﬀerent frequency [119]. CARS
is a coherent process where all the lasers are connected to yield a higher signal. The three
lasers consist of a pump laser (ωp), stokes laser (ωs) and a probe laser (ω′
p) [120]. The pump
laser emits a photon towards the sample, this photon transfers the molecules from the energy

When the lasers are focused on a sample, a CARS image can be generated. CARS has two

1.10.2
CARS Microscopy
In CARS microscopy picosecond pulsed laser systems in the near-infrared (NIR) excitation
wavelength are preferred.
These NIR lasers provide higher penetration depths, optimized
signal-to-noise ratios and generate only low multiphoton absorption-induced photo damage
[122]. The CARS microscope consists of forward and backward directed lasers. The forward
light (F-CARS) is collected by an objective lens. In order to separate the excited light from
the forward detected light a band pass ﬁlter is used (Figure 13). The backward detected light
(Epi-CARS) is collected with the same objective lens as the one focusing the laser beams. Here
a band pass ﬁlter is used to separate the excitation beam and the Epi-beam, as well as a dichroic
mirror having the function to transmit the generated signals. Both the sample and the laser
beam are scanned to record and generate images in the CARS microscope [124].

1.10.3
Advantages of CARS
There are many advantage for using CARS instead for Raman or linear methods. One advantage
is the anti-stokes signal a blue-shifted signal, which eliminates the overlapping inference of
auto-ﬂuorescence from biological samples seen in Raman scattering, since the auto-ﬂuorescence
will naturally separate from the CARS signal [109]. CARS oﬀers imaging without the need for
straining or labeling of samples, this is beneﬁcial for samples where composition and properties
alter due to labeling [102]. CARS as mentioned above examines the samples with a video-rate
speed which provides lower acquisition time and reduce the risk of photo damaging [123]. One
of the main advantages of CARS microscopy is the possibility of simultaneously use other
non-linear optics. This provides opportunity to detecting many components at the same time,
as needed for complex biological systems such as drug delivery systems and drug uptake in cells
and tissue [114].
1.10.4
Disadvantages of CARS
A major limitation by using CARS is the non-resonant background, occurring due to the third
order susceptibility.
The non-resonant signal is mixed with the resonant signal which limit
the detection of the weak Raman bands, this reduces image contrast and chemical selectivity
[125].
The Surface-Enhanced Raman Scattering could be a better method since it has a
background-free and readily interpretative chemical contrast. But the signal in SERS depend
very much on the concentration of the material, eliminating certain samples [107].

1.11
Second Harmonic Generation
Another non-linear phenomena is the second harmonic generation (SHG). The SHG frequency
is generated when two photons with the same energy excites a molecule to a virtual energy
level, from which the molecule emit a photon with twice the energy of the incident photon, and
return to the ground energy state (Figure 14)[126].
Figure 14: Diagram of the energy transition in optical SHG. Two photon interact with a sample molecule,
which excite the molecule to a virtual energy level, here the photon emit a photon with twice the energy
of the incident photon[99]
In order for a molecule to emit a photon with SHG frequency, laser light needs to be focused
on a highly polarizable material with a noncentrosymmetric molecular structure [127].
Noncentrosymmetric molecules have chiral centers and are often samples having a crystalline
structure. Besides many pharmaceutical crystalline drugs, tissue such as collagen is known for
the ability to emit SHG signals [128].
The SHG rely on the polarization and structure of sample materials, and is a beneﬁcial technique
to investigate and determine the orientation and composition of particles.
The planar and
colloidal surface interfaces of particles are good for SHG signal investigation, since bulk samples
do not emit SHG signals due to symmetry [129].
One of the main advantages of SHG is how easily separated the signal is from other signals such
as auto-ﬂuorescence and CARS, which provides good optical imaging of compound structures.
SHG is not always limited to noncentrosymmetric structures, some SHG signals have been
25

Master Thesis
June 2017
detected, at the boundary between two optically diﬀerent materials due to breakage in the
central symmetry [130].
Some studies suggest that SHG can be emitted from the interface
between two centrosymmetric materials [131].
SHG detection has a deep penetration due to the optical radiation and it is very sensitive
to symmetry changes.
This can be applied to increase precision and get a more detailed
characterization of biological systems [132].

5.4
In vitro Release of Cubosomes from SU-8 Microcontainers
Release time is essential when designing an oral drug delivery system, to ensure a controlled
target speciﬁc release. The aim of this study was to investigate the release of drug formulation
from SU-8 microcontainers by CARS. In order to improve imaging of the release in CARS, the
release time was investigated, to determine a time range for expected drug release.
The in vitro time of release for cubosomes loaded in SU-8 microcontainers were measured by
a µDiss proﬁler, a solubility assessment predicting the time estimate expected for cubosomes
to release. A 20mM PBS buﬀer pH 6.8 was used to simulate the pH environment of the small
intestine, where the model system are designed to release cubosomes. The release occurred
under constant rotation of the chip with loaded microcontainers, to mimic disturbance in the
GI tract. Release of cubosomes were detected immediately after addition of 20 mM PBS buﬀer
pH 6.8. The cubosomes had a rapid release from the SU-8 microcontainers for 30 minutes,
where 32±11% of the total load of cubosomes were released (Figure 20).

5.7

Pilot studies with CARS, revealed a problem during visualization SU-8 microcontainers. The
problem came from the non-transparent silicon wafer the microcontainers were placed on.
Imaging of the microcontainers were not possible with the forward directed CARS detector
due to the lack of transparency, instead the backward reﬂected CARS detector was used. In
order to use the reﬂected Epi-CARS detector the chip with microcontainers had to be turned
up-side down on a glass slide used for imaging of samples in CARS microscopy. The induced
release of cubosomes from microcontainers, was problematic because of blockage by the glass
slide. Overcoming this problem, a 3D printed device was created in order to elevate the chip,
making the cubosomes able to transfer out of the microcontainers when solution was added.

intended. The system was tested with MQ water (Appendix 6) and 20 mM PBS buﬀer pH 6.8 to
initiate the cubosomes release. Chips with microcontainers were placed in the device on a glass
slide and solution was forced under the chip at the beginning of the experiment, where samples
were taken after 5, 10 and 30 minutes. SEM images were obtained before the experiment was
initiated and after the time-points (Figure 25).

The drug delivery system with microcontainers, is designed to deliver the cubosomes in the
small intestine, where the cubosomes should be released once the coated pH-sensitive lid has
dissolved.
A PhD study had tried to mimic the attachment of the microcontainers in the
in the small intestine of a pig (Unpublished work). The experiment was preformed in situ,
where a pig intestine was place with the opening on a tube from where loaded microcontainers
was ﬂush through the intestine. A ﬂuorescence microscope was when used for locating of the
microcontainers in the small intestine and the orientation of the microcontainers opening was
investigated, to ensure correct drug release into the intestinal membrane. During the study
with ﬂuorescence microscopy the orientation of the opening was diﬃcult to determine. The
released drug from the microcontainers was furosemide a ﬂuorescence drug and the location of
the drug was detected with both ﬂuorescence microscopy and Raman, but the concentration
seemed to low for detection.
Previous publication propose CARS as a good and label-free
analytic technique for direct visualization of tissue [142]. For this reason pieces of small intestine
samples with attach SU-8 microcontainers were investigated by CARS. The hypotheses was that
the high resolution in CARS would provide a clear image of the microcontainer and the released
furosemide, providing a easy method for determination of the location and orientation of the
compounds. Raman spectrum of tissue and furosemide was not possible to be recorded by Time
Gated Raman, properly due to high ﬂuorescence of the samples that the Time gated Raman
could not separate from potential Raman peak or because no strong Raman signal was within
the CH-stretching region. The Raman shift used for detection was set to 2845 cm−1, generating
a strong signal from the protein and lipids found in tissue [143]. Small pieces of intestine was
cut and placed on a glass slide. The tissue was too thick for forward directed detection, instead
the backward reﬂected detectors were used. A strong signal from the tissue was observed by
both the Epi-CARS and Epi-SHG detectors (Figure 45).

Conclusion
The aim of the study was to explore the ﬁeld of non-linear imaging for visualization of an
oral drug delivery system. The non-linear techniques exploited were CARS, raman scattering,
SHG and TPEF. Raman scattering was used as a characterization tool of the vibrational bands
detected from diﬀerent components prior to CARS imaging. Non-linear imaging with CARS
microscopy holds many advantages for in vitro study of biological and pharmaceutical systems,
due to fast acquisition and label free imaging.
During this study in vitro cubosome release from SU-8 microcontainers were investigated by
CARS. A small release was visualized from the SU-8 microcontainers but no complete release
was detected. Cubosomes tend to obtain poor powder quality due to moisture together with no
added stirring during release, this could prevent the cubosome from initiating release from the
SU-8 microcontainers.
Characterization of the powder precursors of cubosomes by CARS revealed a surprising phenomena
of non-linear signals. The cubosomes had a tendency to emit a SHG signal, when excited with
light. These structures are not normally generated from amorphous centrosymmetic particles
such as cubosomes. The origin of this SHG signal could be from the monoolein components of
cubosomes. During XRPD investigation of the cubosomes both monoolein and some cubosomes
revealed a crystalline structure, which could be the reason of the SHG signal, common in most
crystalline materials.
Dissolution of spray coated Eudragit L100 lids, were possible for imaging with CARS microscopy,
but no drug release was detected afterward.
Biodegradable PCL microcontainers propose good quality for CARS imaging, with easy detectable
and distinguishable CARS spectra of both the biodegradable material and the loaded cubosomes.
Detection of the location of SU-8 microcontainers in the small intestine was made possible in
CARS, even with some diﬃculties due to overlapping signals. The orientation of the microcontainers
were not possible to be determine.
In this study macrophages were incubated with cubosomes for 6 hours prior to imaging. Visible
particles or droplets surrounding the cell membrane were observed during CARS and provided
high quality images of the incubated cells. Particle or droplets was not observed in macrophages
incubated with antigen solution, indicting the need for nanoparticles for antigen absorption by
macrophages
Throughout this thesis it was establish that CARS could be applied on various biological samples
of a oral drug delivery system.

Future perspectives
CARS microscopy has proven to be a good tool for visualization of a variety of biological samples
during this project. PCL microcontainer showed beneﬁcial properties in CARS imaging, and
future studies of drug release could yield better results with this material. Imaging of drug
release from microcontainers, should be made possible by manufacturing a new experimental
set-up and the use of other drugs for release. The lid dissolution study seem to work for the
50 layer lid, but due to some equipment diﬃculties, a second study would be preferable. An
interesting study could be to ﬁnd the origin of the SHG signal observed in cubosomes. This
could include a design of experiment study where parameters such as moisture and the period
between production and imaging, should be investigate for there role in generating the SHG
signal. Other measurement within the ﬁeld of optic imaging could be used for gaining of more
information of the SHG signal.
The internal structure of cubosomes by SAXS or Circular Dihroism could be used for determination
of a phase transition of hydrated cubosomes over time. The location of the microcontainers and
the loaded drug in the small intestine such be optimized, by imaging other materials with
distinct non-linear signal from those abundantly found in the small intestine.
A thin layer
of the small intestine for visualization could provide better imaging an easier location of the
microcontainers, but could provide a risk of removal of the microcontainers and drugs from the
surface.
The cell study showed good properties for future imaging. The particle or droplets should be
investigated, by staining of the cubosomes and three dimensional imaging could be detected to
provide a quantitative analysis of the samples.
