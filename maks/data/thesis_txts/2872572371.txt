Abstract
With the introduction of Maritime Autonomous Surface Ships (MASS) and the goal of
enhanced safety, efficiency, and environmental performance, the maritime sector is ex­
periencing a significant transition. For this transition to be feasible, a Remote Operat­
ing Center (ROC) is essential to support and oversee the operation of MASS. However,
this introduces new obstacles. This study investigates the concept of ROC, its role in
facilitating autonomous vessel operations, and proposes a structured conceptual frame­
work for its implementation from a business­oriented perspective. The study analyzes
the key regulatory, technical, operational, and human factors that determine the success­
ful implementation of ROC through a combination of literature review, industry real­life
examples, expert interviews, and risk assessment. The risk assessment was conducted
using a semi­quantitative method and identified cybersecurity threats, regulatory uncer­
tainty, high initial investment, cross­border operations, and situational awareness loss as
the most critical risks. These were further examined using bowtie analysis to explore
causes, consequences, and potential mitigation strategies. The conceptual framework
presented in the study is developed from a business perspective and is divided in several
sections, such as technological setup, technology onboard, communication and connec­
tivity systems, fallback systems and operator training. The proposed framework is based
on current regulations and industry standards. Its intended purpose is to support maritime
companies, technology providers, seafarers and policymakers in understanding the oper­
ational and strategic challenges of ROC implementation, and consequently contributing
to the worldwide adoption of autonomous shipping solutions.

Introduction
The maritime sector has experienced a significant shift since the introduction of autonomous
vessels due to the enhanced safety, effectiveness, and sustainability they provide [1] .
As environmental concerns are rising globally, the need to minimize carbon emissions is
becoming a priority in maritime transportation [2]. Autonomous vessels help overcome
this challenge by optimizing operational efficiency. Additionally, the maritime industry is
now facing a lack of qualified seafarers, which creates operational difficulties that au­
tonomous systems can help resolve [3].
While autonomous vessels reduce the need for onboard crew, human oversight remains
essential. Remote Operating Center (ROC) fills this gap by enabling centralized monitor­
ing and real­time decision­making from shore­based facilities [4].
Given the importance of supporting the growth of ROC in the maritime industry, this
study introduces a conceptual framework to guide companies in their implementation.
The framework brings together regulatory analysis, technical requirements, and risk con­
siderations into a structured approach that can be adapted by other organizations in the
future. To ensure the framework reflects real­world conditions, the research was devel­
oped in close collaboration with TUCO Marine Group, a company actively engaged in the
maritime sector.
The conceptual framework is needed for several reasons. Firstly, ROC represents an
emerging technology with limited academic research, despite growing interest from com­
panies in the maritime sector. Secondly, the shift towards remote operations is driven by
the need to enhance safety, as almost 80% of maritime accidents are caused by human
error [5]. Finally, ROC has the potential to reduce operational costs by minimizing the
need for crewed vessels [6], and as the industry is facing a declining interest in seafaring
careers [7], remote operations could become a strategic solution to workforce shortages.
Given these factors, the findings presented in this study may be particularly valuable to
maritime companies looking to implement an ROC, technology providers such as sys­
tem integrators, seafarers who want to explore future roles in remote operations, and
researchers working on the topic of ROC.
Following the motivation of the study, a problem statement was defined, leading to the
formulation of the key research questions of this study presented below.
• How can an ROC be effectively established in the maritime industry?
• What are the technical and regulatory requirements for setting up an ROC?
• What are the financial, operational, and human related risks associated with devel­
oping and running an ROC?
This study investigates the development and implementation of ROC within the maritime
sector. In addressing the current challenges in regulation, technology, and operations,
the contributions of this study are as follows:
1. Synthesizing regulatory and classification insights – By reviewing regulations
and guidelines from maritime authorities, classification societies, and flag states, this
paper identifies key requirements in the regulatory landscape, while also identifying
regulatory gaps

2. Technical and operational recommendations – This paper outlines technical and
organizational elements of ROC, including infrastructure, safety systems, worksta­
tion layout, operator interfaces, situational awareness tools, and human resource
requirements. These recommendations are based on both literature and expert in­
puts.
3. State­of­the­art review ­ This paper includes a state­of­the­art review that show­
cases the most important ROC projects and their characteristics.
4. Conducting a structured risk analysis for ROC – This study presents one of the
first structured semi­quantitative risk assessments for ROC, involving identification
of risks in four categories: technical/infrastructure, economic, regulatory/legal, and
human factors. The risks are scored based on stakeholder interviews and expert
evaluation, forming the basis for a risk matrix.
5. Applying bowtie analysis to critical risks – High and critical risks identified in
the matrix are analyzed in detail through bowtie diagrams. This method highlights
causes, consequences, preventive measures, and mitigation strategies, offering
practical tools for risk management in ROC projects.
6. Incorporating industry perspectives through stakeholder interviews – The pa­
per bridges the gap between academic research and industry practice, since insights
from classification societies, maritime authorities, training institutions, and private
companies were collected via interviews. These perspectives are used to validate
the framework and enrich the technical and regulatory discussion with real­world
challenges and solutions.
7. Developing a conceptual framework for ROC – This paper presents a structured
framework for the establishment and operation of ROC that addresses the research
questions. Designed for a global scale, the framework is a guideline that stake­
holders can follow to build and operate an ROC. It brings together relevant existing
information, including:
• regulations and guidelines from classification societies and authorities;
• recommendations of the technical setup of an onshore ROC, including human
roles, infrastructure, training, and safety requirements;
• risks that may occur while building, operating, and maintaining the ROC;
• feasibility of integrating an ROC based on the scalability of the vessels.
This paper is structured to reflect the key components involved in ROC implementation.
Section 4 examines the current regulatory landscape, focusing on international maritime
laws and classification society requirements. Identifying regulatory gaps is the key to un­
derstanding the compliance challenges faced by companies. In Section 5, the technical
requirements for setting up and operating an ROC are analyzed, including infrastructure,
communication systems, and supporting technologies. This is complemented by exam­
ples of existing ROCs to illustrate real­world applications. Then, in Section 6.1, a risk
assessment is performed to highlight the main regulatory, technical, and business risks,
along with strategies to mitigate them, followed in Section 6.2 by a conceptual framework
to link the research concepts, which can guide other companies in implementing their own
ROC solutions.

Literature Review
This chapter investigates the methodology used for identifying and analyzing relevant
literature on ROCs and Autonomous Ships. It also provides a primary overview of these
two core topics and outlines the primary challenges related to ROC implementation from
a human, technical, and legal perspective.
2.1
Review Methodology
To identify relevant literature, an iterative search process was conducted using various
keyword combinations. These keywords were carefully selected and applied to both the
DTU Findit and Google Scholar platforms to refine the search results. The keywords were
selected based on the research questions and included ”Remote Operating Center”, ”Au­
tonomous vessel”, ”Maritime”, ”Flag State”, ”Regulations”, and ”Classification Societies”.
Multiple iterations were performed, testing various keyword combinations in both DTU
Findit and Google Scholar. Additionally, ”Remote Operating Center” was identified as the
primary keyword for the objective of this research. Through the initial search process, it
became evident that the technology is referred to by various names across the literature,
beyond just Remote Operating Center. These include Remote Operation Center, Shore
Control Center, Remote Control Center, Shore Operations Center, and Base Control Sta­
tion. Therefore, due to the limited number of search results for the primary term, some
of these synonymous terms, such as ”Remote Operation Center”, were incorporated as
supplementary keywords to broaden the scope of the research.
The initial search string included all keywords separated by ”OR” which resulted in a high
number of papers. Therefore, to focus on the state of the art, the search string was nar­
rowed down to always include ”Remote Operation Center” combined with other keywords,
such as: ”Remote Operation Center” AND ”Maritime” with 39 hits, ”Remote Opera­
tion Center” AND ”Autonomous Vessels” with 79 hits, ”Remote Operation Center”
AND ”Regulations” AND ”Maritime” with 19 hits, ”Remote Operation Center” AND
”Flag State” with 20 hits, and ”Remote Operation Center” AND ”Classification So­
cieties” with 30 hits. To identify relevant risk assessment methods for new technologies
for ROC, the same process was followed with a search of the keywords ”Quantitative
Risk Assessment” AND ”New Technology” with 800 hits, while the search ”Quantita­
tive Risk Assessment” AND ”Remote Operation Center” only had 2 hits. In addition
to that, a new search was made to identify examples of conceptual frameworks for new
technologies like the ROC. The keywords ”Conceptual Framework” AND ”New Tech­
nology” were used, with multiple hits, while the search ”Conceptual Framework” AND
”Remote Operation Center”, only had 11 hits, none of which were relevant.
To reduce bias and maintain consistency, only the most recent available articles, pub­
lished from 2019 to 2025, were considered, and all were written in English.
To enhance the depth and reliability of the literature review, in addition to the methodology
described above, backward research was also conducted to identify additional material.
Following this method, the reference list of some relevant articles was analyzed to locate
other significant publications that contributed to the research.
2.2
Autonomous Ships
Autonomous ships, over the last years, have experienced significant growth in the mar­
itime sector. Consequently, the International Maritime Organization (IMO) has been work­
Developing a Remote Operating Center Framework for Autonomous Vessels: A Business Perspective
3
==PAGE:14==
ing to incorporate these vessels into its regulatory framework. The IMO defines Maritime
Autonomous Surface Ships (MASS) as vessels capable of operating with different levels
of independence from human interaction [8]. Additionally, the IMO has defined four de­
grees of autonomy for MASS. The first degree involves ships with automated processes
and decision support, where seafarers remain on board to take control if needed. The
second degree refers to remotely controlled ships with crew still present onboard, ready
to take control and operate if needed. In the third degree, ships are remotely controlled
without any seafarers onboard. Finally, the fourth degree describes fully autonomous
ships, capable of independently making decisions and executing actions without human
intervention [8].
When discussing autonomous ships, it is essential to define a clear vocabulary. MASS
refers to any autonomous vessel. However, “unmanned”, in some research articles, is
often used as a synonym of the term “autonomous”, which can lead to confusion. Au­
tonomous means that the ship can carry out a set of defined procedures with little or no
supervision from the bridge crew. This does not necessarily mean that there is no human
present. Unmanned, on the other hand, means that the vessel operates without a crew
onboard, but it is still remotely controlled by humans [9]. Additionally, it is important to
differentiate between ”Automation” and ”Autonomy”. An autonomous ship is a ship that
is characterized by some level of automation and self­governance. Automation refers to
the systems, which are frequently computerized, that allow the ship to perform specific
tasks and routines without human input. In the maritime industry, examples include au­
topilot systems, dynamic positioning, and automatic pump control. Autonomy, however,
involves self­governance, where systems independently execute all stages of decision­
making, such as monitoring, analyzing, deciding, and acting, without the need for human
intervention. Therefore autonomy is the outcome of applying advanced automation to­
gether with some form of self­governance to a ship [10].
The feasibility of autonomous maritime operations has been demonstrated through sev­
eral relevant projects and initiatives, leading the way for further advancements in this field.

2.3
Remote Operating Center
The successful implementation of autonomous vessels is highly dependent on ROCs,
shore­based facilities responsible for monitoring, controlling, and managing these ships.
The IMO defines the ROC as a site located away from the MASS that is capable of man­
aging some or all operations remotely [16]. Additionally, the IMO has adopted an official
definition of the Remote Operator as a qualified individual responsible for managing cer­
tain or all functions of an autonomous vessel from a ROC [16].
One of the main responsibilities of the ROC is the planning of the voyage, which includes
setting the navigation waypoints and setting up the vessel machinery. Throughout the
voyage, the center monitors the vessel’s progress and guarantees constant situational
awareness. It is also responsible for the health monitoring of onboard machinery and the
vessel’s hull. In the event of anomalies or emergencies, the operator must be prepared
to respond promptly and appropriately [17].
In addition to internal vessel management, the ROC has a crucial communication role. It
interacts with ports and coastal states, such as ports’ Vessel Traffic Service (VTS) system.
To ensure navigational safety, it also facilitates communication with nearby vessels. Fur­
thermore, the station is also in charge of controlling transitions between different modes
of operation, ensuring seamless shifts [17].

onshore or installed on another ship, and in some cases, a single ROC can be designed

2.4
Risk Assessment
Risk is typically defined as the combination of the likelihood of an event occurring and its
consequences, as shown in Equation 2.1.
Risk = Likelihood of the event occurrence × Consequence the event brings
(2.1)
Risk assessment is generally characterized by three types of data: qualitative, semi­
quantitative, and quantitative. Each type corresponds to a specific scale and method
of assessment. Qualitative methods use descriptive categories, and quantitative assess­
ments rely on measurable, data­driven metrics. Semi­quantitative scales represent an
intermediate approach between qualitative and quantitative methods. They provide nu­
meric metrics based on qualitative judgment, allowing for more structured comparison
[21].
When measurable, objective data is unavailable or scarce, subjective expert judgment is
often used to estimate risk factors [25]. The quality of definition, structure, and interpreta­
tion of the input data within the chosen methodology largely affects the accuracy of a risk
assessment.
When working with new technologies, such as ROC, risk assessment becomes particu­
larly complex due to the novelty of both the technology and the operational conditions, as
well as the presence of a multi­stakeholder environment [26]. When the technology and
the conditions are new and unfamiliar, the risk analysis must take into account a higher
number of unknowns and data gaps in order to be effective [27].
A common tool for risk assessment, recommended by the American Bureau of Shipping
(ABS), is the Risk Matrix. This matrix helps visualize and organize risks by plotting an
event’s likelihood against its consequences’ severity in a grid of M x N cells. Risk matrices
classify risk in levels, such as low, medium, and high, based on color codes or numerical
scales. They are often chosen for their simplicity and intuitiveness in the communication of
risks [10] [25]. There are several types of risk matrices based on the type of data provided:
qualitative matrices assign both likelihood and consequence with descriptive levels (e.g.,
low, medium, high), and additive rules evaluate risks; semi­quantitative matrices assign
a numeric score to each category, and calculate risks by multiplying them, allowing more
consistent comparisons, though subjectivity is crucial; and quantitative matrices define
risks by numerical intervals (e.g., “likely” = [1/3, 2/3]) [25]. Risk matrices help determine
which risks require intervention. Mitigation strategies depend on risk thresholds within
the matrix; when the predefined acceptance boundaries are exceeded, action is needed.
Literature also points out some limitations of these matrices, such as the variability of
results depending on how the categories and the cutoff boundaries are designed [28].
Another important method, recognized by the classification society Det Norske Veritas
(DNV), is the Bow­Tie Analysis. This method combines top­down, used for example in
the Fault Tree Analysis (FTA) method, and bottom­up approaches, used in the Event Tree
Analysis (ETA). The top­down layer starts from a potential failure and works backward to
identify the causes. This is represented on the left side of the Bow­Tie diagram, together
Developing a Remote Operating Center Framework for Autonomous Vessels: A Business Perspective
7
==PAGE:18==
with the preventive barriers that aim to stop the event from happening. The bottom­up
layer starts from the same event and explores its consequences. This is shown on the right
side of the diagram, along with mitigative barriers designed to reduce their impact. The
Bow­Tie Analysis helps understand both the causes and the effects of a risk event and to
identify where preventive and mitigative barriers can be applied. It can be used in complex
socio­technical systems where it is important to have a comprehensive understanding of
both functional failures and the operational interactions [29] [30] [31].
The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) 27005 standard offers general
principles for risk assessment but does not require the use of specific methods. It supports
both qualitative and quantitative approaches. For qualitative assessments, it recommends
using simple ordinal scales like ’low­medium­high’. For quantitative models, it suggests
that risks should be calculated by multiplying the probability and the consequence of an
event. However, ISO 27005 also warns about false precision when numerical models are
used without reliable data [32].
2.5
Gaps in literature
To the best of the author’s knowledge, this research is the first to develop both a con­
ceptual framework and a semi­quantitative risk assessment. Although there is growing
academic and industrial interest in autonomous maritime operations, the current literature
reveals several notable gaps:
• Absence of ROC­specific risk assessment methodologies: Existing studies on
autonomous ships primarily address onboard systems or general risk management
approaches. There is a lack of risk assessment frameworks that focus specifically
on the operational and infrastructural complexities of ROCs.
• Lack of a structured conceptual framework for ROC implementation: Despite
the emerging role of ROCs in supporting MASS, no comprehensive framework cur­
rently exists that defines their key components, functional architecture, technology
integration requirements, or training.
• Limited connection between regulatory developments and current technolo­
gies of ROC: Few academic contributions attempt to bridge the gap between inter­
national regulations, and current shore­based technologies.
This study seeks to address the identified research gaps by proposing an original con­
ceptual framework for the design and operation of ROCs. In addition, it conducts a semi­
quantitative risk assessment that will identify and evaluate key risk domains, including
infrastructure, economic viability, regulatory uncertainty, and human performance. By fill­
ing these gaps, this study offers a new perspective on remote operations in the maritime
industry and provides a foundation for future research and practical use in the field.

ROC can be used for different vessel types, each with particular risk characteristics and
mission priorities. In some of the cases, such as for commercial cargo vessels or passen­
ger ferries, it involves regulatory complexity, higher safety expectations, and high costs of
implementation and maintenance, making those cases less feasible for ROC implemen­

tation in the current stage of development. Sometimes big companies rely on external
funding, as they are not yet profitable in the remote operation sector, because the costs
outweigh the potential savings.
In contrast, USVs used for missions such as wind measurement at offshore wind farms,
hydrographic surveying, surveying of deep sea cables, critical infrastructure, or collection
of waste in harbors etc. [34], are a more practical and active case of ROC. These vessels

reflects the reality faced by the industry stakeholders of today.
3.2.1
Risk Identification Approach
Given the lack of sufficient quantitative data in the current state of ROC development,
a semi­quantitative risk assessment approach was selected [21]. This method enables
structured evaluation using comparative scoring and allows for more consistent interpre­
tation than qualitative assessments, without relying on the detailed empirical data required
by fully quantitative models. Such methods are particularly valuable for new technologies,
where uncertainty is high and historical data are limited. Translating expert judgment into
structured numerical scores provides a way to evaluate risks in early­stage or new sys­
tems [27]. Therefore, the use of a semi­structured risk matrix aligns well with the unique
characteristics of ROC, which, unlike mature MASS platforms, requires a broader frame­
work to address regulatory, organizational, cyber, and economic vulnerabilities beyond
just technical failure modes.

4.1
Regulations

4.1.1
IMO
The IMO is a specialized United Nations agency that is responsible for establishing reg­
ulatory frameworks for shipping worldwide. As the rise of autonomous ships challenges
current regulations, the IMO is crucial to shaping new standards. Although there are no
mandatory regulations from the IMO, many groups are actively working to achieve this as
a result. The work is primarily focused on Autonomous Ships but it is expected to include
requirements for ROC as well.
4.1.1.1
Regulatory Scoping Exercise
At the 98th session in 2017, the IMO’s Maritime Safety Committee meeting (MSC 98)
approved a proposal to undertake a Regulatory Scoping Exercise (RSE) to determine
how the safe, secure, and environmentally sound operation of MASS might be introduced
in IMO instruments [17].
The RSE was conducted in two stages. The first stage, completed in 2021, evaluated
how existing IMO instruments might apply to ships with varying levels of automation. To
facilitate this process, the degrees of autonomy were defined as presented in Chapter 2.2.
The second stage of RSE analyzed and determined whether existing instruments were
required to be amended or new instruments needed to be developed to address MASS
operations [17].
For this RSE to have results, some IMO committees collaborated, and more specifically,
the MSC completed the RSE for safety treaties during the 103rd session in May 2021,

while the Legal Committee (LEG) finalized its RSE at its 108th session in July 2021. The
Facilitation Committee (FAL) approved the outcome of the RSE for treaties under its juris­
diction during FAL 46 in May 2022. Although the FAL and LEG concluded that only minor
changes were required to accommodate MASS operations within their frameworks, the
MSC identified various gaps and challenges [36].
MSC identified several key challenges, including the need to redefine traditional mar­
itime roles such as master and crew, establish functional and operational requirements for
ROC, determine the classification and responsibilities of remote operators, and address
concerns related to connectivity, cybersecurity, and emergency response protocols. To
address these findings, the development of a dedicated MASS Code is recommended,
rather than the modification of each regulation individually. This is supported by interim
guidelines to ensure safety, security, and environmental protection during the transition
[8].
4.1.1.2
MASS Code
Following the completion of the RSE and discussions initiated at MSC 105, the IMO has
been developing a goal­based MASS Code to regulate the operation of autonomous ships.
MSC 108 (May 2024) approved the report of the third session of the Joint MSC­LEG­FAL
Working Group on MASS (MASS­JWG 3) and MSC 109 (December 2024) agreed on a
revised road map for its development.
The timeplan is that by May 2026, a non­mandatory MASS Code will be finalized and
adopted, and in December 2026, an Experience­Building Phase will be conducted to
collect operational insights. Based on these findings, the development of a mandatory
MASS Code will begin in 2028, with potential revisions to the International Convention for
the Safety of Life at Sea (SOLAS). The IMO aims to adopt the mandatory Code by July
2030, with entry into force by January 2032 [36].
4.1.1.3
Interim guidelines for MASS trials
To facilitate implementation in the early stages, at its 101st session (June 2019), the MSC
approved Interim Guidelines for MASS trials. These guidelines are intended to support
flag States and other stakeholders in MASS trials, ensuring they are conducted safely,
securely, and with due regard to the protection of the environment [37].
4.1.2
Other existing regulations
In addition to the development of the IMO MASS Code, the integration of MASS into ex­
isting international maritime regulations presents significant challenges, particularly with
regard to the International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea (COLREGs), the
Standards of Training, Certification, and Watchkeeping for Seafarers (STCW), and SO­
LAS, which were created for vessels with crew in mind.
The COLREGs, originally designed for human­operated vessels, raise concerns about
how MASS can comply with rules such as keeping a proper lookout (Rule 5) and exercising
good seamanship (Rule 2) in collision avoidance scenarios.
However, according to an analysis by the Danish Maritime Authority (DMA), remote­
controlled ships may still meet COLREGs requirements for human control if real­time
navigation decisions are made remotely. If this gains international support, there is a
suggestion that no amendments to COLREGs would be needed for these levels [38].
Similarly, the STCW will need substantial changes to describe the competencies required
for remote operators and to address the shifting roles of seafarers in autonomous opera­
tions.

4.2
Documentation
The first step in establishing an ROC is to determine which operational tasks that are
traditionally performed by the onboard crew will now be reallocated to either a remote
operator, a decision support system, or an autonomous system. To do so, several key
documents need to be prepared, including but not limited to the Concept of Operations
(ConOps). A ConOps is a structured, high­level document that describes how a vessel,
system, or operation is intended to function under real­world scenarios and is a required
submission in many approval processes.
A draft ConOps should be submitted at the beginning of the project, while a finalized
version should be submitted for approval by the completion of the verification and vali­
dation phase. The document should be reevaluated when there are hardware, software,
operational, or management changes to the ship or ROC [10].
In the context of remote vessel operations, a ConOps is usually mandatory in the following
cases:
• When applying for a class notation related to autonomy or remote operation (e.g.,
DNV AROS, ABS AUTONOMOUS/REMOTE­CON);
• When the vessel is unmanned or partially unmanned;
• When an ROC is involved.
For ROC­related operations, this document should include, among others [29], [10]:
• Description of the intended operation of the vessel, distinguishing between which
tasks will become remote or autonomous and which will remain manual;
• Description of the fallback states and their effectiveness during the different opera­
tions;
• Description of the expected mode of operation and the location of control;
• Connectivity specifications, ensuring the communication link between the ship and
an ROC is available, secure, and capable of supporting the operational needs. The
more responsibility the ROC has, the more resilient the connectivity link needs to
be;
• Qualifications and training requirements of the ROC personnel, who should be ap­
propriately qualified and experienced to conduct the operations, agreed upon with
the Flag Administration;
• Description of interactions between the ROC and external systems and entities,
such as Vessel Traffic Systems (VTS) or other vessels;
• Cybersecurity and resilience precautions for the ROC that affect the location security
and that will prevent potential corruption of the autonomous function;
• The limitations and operating limits of the ROC operations.

that can be transmitted per second, although there is the geographic limitation;
• Satellite network: Suitable for long range or deep ocean operations, having global
coverage, with high latency and costs.

• RC3 (Full Remote Control): Operators continuously monitor and control the ship
remotely.

vironmental conditions to ensure operator performance and safety and can also be
implemented on ROCs [43];
• Communication links between the ROC and the ship, such as Global System for
Mobile Communications, Worldwide Interoperability for Microwave Access, Very
High Frequency (VHF), or satellite technologies, and should support interactions
with VTS, to the port authorities, or the vessel operator.
To support complex operations, the ROC should be split into several groups of functions
covering essential services, such as voyage, navigation and positioning, detection sys­
tems (radars, sensors, etc.), communication systems, ship integrity and machinery moni­
toring, cargo and passenger management, remote control and intervention, security, and
emergency response. [44] [42].
4.3.4
ClassNK
ClassNK has established a guideline for autonomous operations on ships in order to cre­
ate a structured approach, evaluate autonomous ship technologies, and bridge the differ­
ences between conventional and autonomous technologies. The aim of this guideline is
to create a standard understanding of safety requirements and human intervention [45].
4.3.4.1
Remote control levels
ClassNK’s framework categorizes systems based on some key factors: the scope of au­
tomation, the scope of remote operation, and the fallback executor. The factors presented
below explain how tasks are handled and where intervention takes place in the remote
facility [46].
Scope of Remote Control and the Fallback Executor: [46]
• Level 0: All operations are performed by crew onboard, including the fallback exe­
cution;
• Level I: Some decision­making subtasks are executed remotely, while the fallback
execution is shared between the onboard crew and remote operators in ROC;
• Level II: All decision­making is carried out remotely from the ROC, including the
fallback execution.
4.3.4.2
Recommendations for Remote Control Functions
Class NK divides remote operation into two types: remote monitoring and remote control.
For both functions, it is required that remote operators have the necessary knowledge,
experience, and abilities to appropriately perform their assigned tasks.
4.3.5
DNV
DNV is having a leading role in shaping the frameworks for autonomous and remote op­

and Remotely Operated Ships (AROS) notation family. These guidelines apply to both
autonomous vessels and those operating under partial control, and include requirements
for ROCs [47].

4.3.5.2
Recommendations for Remote Control Functions
Workstations
The ROC must be a dedicated physical area solely intended for the tasks necessary to
remotely operate the vessel. Each vessel function controlled or supervised by the ROC
should be performed from a dedicated physical location within the ROC, referred to as a
remote workstation.
A remote operator should be able to perform all the assigned tasks from a single remote
workstation. Additionally, the ROC should have at least one spare remote workstation
available and capable of taking over control and monitoring in case of failures or planned
maintenance.
These workstations should support full functionality, with layouts designed to enable the
simultaneous performance of all tasks. The design should account not only for normal
operational conditions but also for emergency situations and demanding operational sce­
narios.
Operators must have a constant overview displayed in such a way that they will have a
full understanding of the status of all the functions, which may be presented in three levels
for each function:
• Green: Full operational with all redundancies and health conditions of supporting
systems;
• Yellow: Operational, with limited functionality or redundancy, but with easy access
for the operator to detailed information about the current situation;
• Red: The function or system is unable to fulfill its intended purpose.
Equipment and Power Supply
ROC infrastructure should ensure that possible failures of systems and components will
not result in loss of normal control, supervision, and situational awareness.
Power supply in the ROC should be redundant, with two main feeders from the grid and
a backup through a UPS in case of power grid blackout to ensure uninterrupted opera­
tions. Also, sufficient emergency lighting should be installed in the ROC to maintain the
operation of vessel functions.
Real­time situational awareness and Senses substitution
Operation in the ROC should be based on real­time situational awareness for the remote

Human senses must be substituted by sensor technology:
• Visual inputs: Range from single readings to continuous streaming of high­definition
images with zoom possibilities covering a wide sector with an update frequency and

the operation of the function;
• Sound inputs: Microphones with sufficient noise cancellation properties, without the

The sensor information should be continuously recorded and support playback, covering
a sufficient elapsed period. Alerts must be triggered by distinctive sounds and visuals
relevant to the operation.
Alerts
Remote operators should be provided pre­warnings and caution alerts so they can be
informed about hazards and developing conditions on the related workstation in time to
analyze the situation, plan appropriate control actions, and intervene before a situation
becomes critical. Common alerts may be given on several workstations when necessary
but should be minimized.
Tests
Systems and components supporting functions under remote supervision should undergo
an initial test prior to each voyage, including switching between redundant systems and
components in order to identify hidden failures. Similar tests should be available through­
out the voyage, for instance before high risk operations [29].
4.4
National Maritime Authorities
ROCs must be approved by the vessel’s Flag State Administration and other relevant
authorities. For this reason, the Maritime Authorities play a critical role in setting national
requirements for these operations. All autonomous and remote operations must comply
with the applicable flag, coastal, or port State regulations and early coordination with these
regulatory bodies is essential to ensure smooth project implementation [10].
Additionally, according to the UNCLOS, remote operators may be considered as ”masters”
of the vessel, meaning they will fall under flag state legal jurisdiction [49]. In this section,
some impactful examples of maritime authorities will be presented.

levels, they will have simultaneous decision­making authority.
Denmark has adapted its regulations to support remote operations, including [50]:

5
Technical Setup
This chapter will examine recommendations and real­world examples related to ROCs,
focusing on the roles of human operators, required training, and the specific technical
infrastructure needed to support remote vessel operations.
5.1
Recommendations for Human Roles
With the increased development of these new technologies over the decades, some sea­
farers are being transferred from onboard to a shore­based position in the ROC [55]. For
instance, crew members responsible for navigation and control are being moved from the
vessel’s bridge to these remote facilities [9].
When staffing the ROC, usually there are two different strategies:
Maritime Professionals Learning Technology:
Many organizations aim to employ experienced mariners, usually master mariners, and
train them in ROC technology. Countries like Belgium explicitly mandate certified mar­
itime operators for ROC roles, reflecting the trend among maritime authorities to require
equivalent training and certification as onboard crew members, at least in the short term.
Technicians Learning Maritime Operations:
Some companies hire technically skilled professionals, such as electrical or systems en­
gineers, training them in fundamental maritime competencies. This approach is common
in Denmark, where no standardized training exists for remote operators, but companies
collaborate with seafarers’ schools to provide them with training tailored specifically to
their system and crew requirements.
In general, remote operators must develop a diverse set of competencies. Future op­
erators should include system understanding, technical and communication skills, and
maritime competencies, supported by a strong foundation in regulatory knowledge, nav­
igation, and engineering. To ensure adequate preparation on ROC usage, there should
be a training trial with intensive use of simulators [56].
5.1.1
Trainings
Training programs differ based on the two categories mentioned before.
Maritime Professionals Learning Technology:
They must learn to distance themselves psychologically from being physically onboard.
Simulator training allows operators to adapt to the remote operating environment, specif­
ically addressing the loss of senses problem, such as vessel vibrations and motions typ­
ically experienced onboard [56]. These sessions familiarize operators with various ROC
interfaces, highlighting system capabilities and limitations. Techniques borrowed from avi­
ation are often used, particularly to train situational awareness through instrument scan­
ning techniques. Additional training on the extended use of the ROC, including data anal­
ysis, must be implemented [17].
Technicians Learning Maritime Operations:
They face challenges including misconceptions of vessel handling, often equating boats
to cars, and difficulties in key maritime concepts such as interaction with wind, COLREGs
etc. For this reason, practical maritime experiences should be provided, such as spending
one or two days sailing a vessel. Furthermore, radio communication training is essential

==PAGE:36==
due to the typically poorer audio quality and delays in maritime radio systems compared
to mobile phones, which most of these people are used to.
The STCW Code recommends the Advanced Training Program (15 weeks plus 8 weeks of
in­service training) that prepares senior navigators and engineers for management­level
tasks or the Basic Training Program (15 weeks) that equips operational­level professionals
with fundamental nautical and technical abilities [57].
In addition, these trainings also include practical hands­on training, often conducted dock­
side to provide direct exposure to vessels and integrated ROC systems. Sessions typi­
cally last several days, with additional in­depth troubleshooting training, such as camera
or communication system failures, provided to supervisory personnel.
Long­term view is that the operator will not need to be a seafarer or to have experience in
the water but to be a graduate of a maritime school. In the short term, however, most mar­
itime authorities request an equivalent training and certification level to people operating
on the vessel.
5.1.2
Operational Roles
It is essential that the roles of the ROC are designed with the workload of each task in
mind to guarantee stability in operations, and the operators should be assigned to specific
tasks within a specific area [57]. The number of people needed in the ROC often varies
according to vessel size and complexity:
Small Vessels (5 to 15 meters) are typically managed entirely by a single operator. Eco­
nomically, this approach is essential since staffing multiple people for small­scale opera­
tions would decrease the cost advantages of remote operations.
Larger Vessels (Commercial and Service­Scale Operations) include more complex opera­
tions that require a structured hierarchy. At its core is the remote operator who is handling
navigation and direct operational control, typically managing one or two vessels simulta­
neously, depending on operational complexity. Supporting this primary operator are spe­
cialized roles such as communication specialists and technology specialists. Additionally,
an experienced supervisor oversees multiple stations, providing expert assistance, man­
aging emergencies, and ensuring operational continuity when operators need breaks or
experience difficulties. It is suggested that one operator be present per vessel for larger
vessels, with clearly defined supervisory roles available for immediate assistance and
operational takeover during critical situations.
Today, in most projects, in addition to the remote operator, a captain is on board and is
responsible for everything that is happening. If something goes wrong, the captain can
take manual control and resolve the situation.
5.2
Recommendations for Infrastructure
Regarding the infrastructure of the ROC, some specifications were found in addition to
the classification and regulation recommendations in Chapter 4. As a basis the ROC
should look according to ISO 11064 – Ergonomic design of control centres, which refers,
in general, to control centers.
5.2.1
Interface
The workstation of the ROC should have easy and intuitive user interfaces as well as vi­
sual, auditory, and haptic perception to compensate for the absence of physical presence
on the vessel [58]. The interface has no standard setup and can vary depending on the
type of vessel. However, it typically includes a minimum of three live video displays: one

ship bridge and show different camera angles from the onboard cameras, including for­

A bridge­replica multi­display screen is also present and typically includes key vessel data

horns, or alarms, helping to maintain environmental awareness. This type of audio aware­

5.2.2
Connectivity and communication
A secure communication link between the vessel and the ROC is essential, with support­
ing infrastructure at both ends. This system must include robust cybersecurity measures
to ensure fast and secure data transfer between the vessel and the operator.
Connectivity options depend on the mission’s level of criticality. For low­criticality mis­
sions, 4G and 5G solutions are commonly used due to their low latency and cost­effectiveness,
although they offer limited coverage. While 5G is slightly more expensive than 4G, it
provides lower latency, making it suitable for applications that require quick response
times. Both technologies are used in coastal operations and short­range USVs. For high­
criticality missions, such as long­range or offshore operations, satellite communication
systems such as AV SAT (medium bandwidth) or Starlink (high bandwidth) are preferred
due to their global coverage, despite higher costs and increased latency.
The ROC must also be equipped with a VHF radio to support short­range voice communi­
cation and ensure compliance with maritime regulations. This is essential for interaction
with nearby vessels, VTS, and port authorities.
5.2.3
Fallback System
When talking about fallback systems, it’s important to differentiate between critical and
non­critical situations. In less serious scenarios, simple, low­cost fallbacks may be enough.
However, in more complex operations, such as those occurring in busy ports or involving
passenger ferries, more complex and expensive fallbacks are required to ensure safety
and continuity.
As a fallback for connectivity, different options should be considered depending on the
technology in use. For 4G/5G networks, maintaining contracts with multiple providers
enables seamless switching in case of network failure. Specifically, if the primary 5G
connection fails, the system should switch to 4G, and if that is also unavailable, fall back
to a low­bandwidth satellite connection. If using 4G as the primary network, switching
to an alternative 4G provider is recommended, followed by a fallback to low­bandwidth
satellite if necessary. For vessels relying on high­bandwidth satellite connections, the
fallback should also be a low­bandwidth satellite system.
In case of system failure or cybersecurity incidents, a common fallback strategy is tran­
sitioning the vessel to a ”minimum risk condition”, where it stops and maintains position
using station­keeping or dynamic positioning systems.
Ensuring reliable power is another critical aspect of ROC design. The ROC should be
equipped with emergency generators to handle power grid failures. To further increase
reliability, battery backups and UPS are used throughout the system. The control in­
terface itself should have a dedicated battery, allowing essential operations to continue
uninterrupted during power outages.
Finally, to enhance traceability and compliance, all VHF radio communications should be
automatically recorded and stored within the system for monitoring, analysis, and regula­
tory review.

5.3.7
Sea Machines ­ Boston
Sea Machines has developed the SM300 Autonomous Command and Control, a cutting­
edge wireless ROC, as seen in Figure 5.3, designed for autonomous self­piloting and
off­boat remote command. The system provides operators with real­time situational and
operational awareness through an intuitive chart­based user interface, enabling advanced
navigation and seamless vessel control from virtually anywhere. By integrating the SM300,
the goal is to enhance safety and support for fatigued crews, particularly during repetitive
or low­stimulation tasks such as overnight watches [70].
The SM300 includes a box installed in the vessel that functions as an edge computing
system. The selected mission is executed autonomously by the SM300 onboard the ves­
sel, even if the connection to the control center is lost. During the mission, the system
transmits live data back to the operators, including camera feeds, position tracking, speed,
and heading. The acceptable timeout duration for operations without a connection is cus­
tomizable. For example, commercial vessels typically allow 1 to 5 minutes, while naval
applications may permit indefinite disconnection to prioritize autonomy. A load balancer
is also installed onboard to manage multiple data channels effectively.
The SM300 laptop interface is equipped with the main operational data required to con­
trol and monitor a vessel, including status readings such as temperature, RPM, speed,
alarms, and control of navigation lights. While it does not yet offer the complete function­
ality of a full ship bridge system, it provides a solid foundation for remote and autonomous
operations.
The SM300 is used daily, logging approximately 750 test hours annually, and more than
200 client units are in operation globally. Each system undergoes rigorous in­house and
on­water testing before delivery, and software updates are deployed only upon request
to prevent mission disruption.
The SM300 system is currently being installed on a 90­meter freighter, which will remain
manned but use autonomy as a backup to reduce crew fatigue. Sea Machines Robotics
gained BV Type Approval for its commercial wireless remote­control helm systems [71].

Business Case
This chapter will address the business feasibility of operating and building an ROC. This

6.1
Risk assessment
This chapter presents a structured risk assessment of ROC to identify, prioritize, and ana­
lyze potential threats associated with its implementation and operation. The assessment
integrates evidence­based insights derived from expert interviews, combined with find­
ings from the literature, and was validated by the collaborating company, confirming its
relevance and applicability to real­world operations.
Firstly, a literature search was conducted, and some important risks were identified, which
were categorized into four main categories: technical and infrastructure, economic, reg­
ulatory and legal, and human factor risks. During the interviews, the group collected the
main risks for each category, and the interviewees were asked to compare them qualita­
tively, taking into consideration likelihood and consequence. Then the data was translated
to a scale from 1­5 based on interview inputs and the group’s expertise within the topic.
Plotting this information, the risk matrix was formulated. The interviewees were asked
to identify potential causes and consequences of the risks they had previously classified
as high. They were also invited to provide recommendations for recovery and mitigation
strategies. All collected data, including the groups’ experiences, were used to develop
bowtie diagrams. The approach that will be followed is described in Figure 6.1.

6.1.1
Risk identification
The main risks of building and operating an ROC are identified below. The risks were
defined based on the data collected from interviews.
Technical and Infrastructure Risks
• Communication Delays: Small signal delays can lead to critical situations or colli­
sions.
• Bandwidth Limitations: Bandwidth affects video feeds from the vessel, sensor data,
and remote commands sent to or from the vessel. Insufficient bandwidth can cause
delays or loss of critical data, affecting decision­making.
• System Reliability: It’s challenging to ensure consistent and uninterrupted system
performance, which can lead to unexpected failure.
Developing a Remote Operating Center Framework for Autonomous Vessels: A Business Perspective
33
==PAGE:44==
• Security Threats: Unauthorized access to the ROC, whether through physical intru­
sion or cyberattacks, poses significant threats to operational control, data security,
and overall system integrity [29].
• Equipment Failure Onboard: Failure of any equipment or structure on the vessel,
such as engines, steering components on the bridge, or navigational sensors, can
be critical for the navigational safety of the vessel [10].
Economic Risks
• High Initial Investment: Setting up systems like an ROC requires a significant upfront
cost, which can be a barrier for companies.
• Delayed Return on Investment (ROI): The ROI might only be seen after 2–3 years,
making it difficult to convince stakeholders or secure funding in the short term.
• Operational Cost: Crew salaries are currently one of the biggest operational ex­
penses, which affects profitability, especially in industries with tight margins. There
is a lack of skilled captains and operators, making recruitment difficult and costly.
Regulatory and Legal Risks
• Communication Barrier in Port State Control/Inspections: Authorities often require
direct communication with the captain during inspections, which is problematic when
the captain is not physically on board.
• Cross­Border Operations: Moving vessels across national waters poses legal and
operational challenges due to differing regulations. For this reason, some com­
panies plan to control a vessel from multiple ROCs located in different countries,
meaning that when the vessel crosses the border, another ROC in a different loca­
tion will be responsible for it, which complicates even simple tasks like operator shift
changes.
• Lack of Regulations: Maritime laws are not yet adapted to remote operations, creat­
ing uncertainty around liability, traditionally assigned to the onboard captain, which
becomes especially complex across international borders.
Human Factors and Situational Awareness
• Insufficient situational awareness: It’s difficult for ROC operators to stay alert and
avoid distractions, leading to a risk of losing focus, which can affect situational
awareness on the ROC if they stay for long periods without physical presence on­
board. This is called out­of­the­loop syndrome, and it happens when a human oper­
ator becomes too disconnected from a system, usually because the system is highly
automated [20].
• Experience Gap: Judgment of vessel behavior by individuals without seafarer ex­
perience can be difficult in an ROC [29].
• Human error: Oversight during remote monitoring or mistake during remote control
[49].

Conceptual Framework
The conceptual framework developed in Figure 6.8, outlines the key elements needed for
the implementation of an ROC. The framework provides an organized overview of how to
set up an ROC by determining the essential needs, such as essential regulations, commu­
nication systems, operator qualifications, and workstation configuration. The framework
can be used for any type of vessel, although for those that are not USVs, additional at­
tention must be given to safety protocols and mission criticality of the operations.
The best way to navigate the conceptual framework is to follow it step by step in the direc­
tion of the arrows. When an arrow has two heads, it indicates a two­way process, which
means that the steps can go back and forth if needed. The framework uses sections
highlighted in different colors to represent different components of the ROC system. Grey
areas show the initial and final phases, green highlights the ROC interface at its physical
location, purple indicates the onboard technologies required on the vessel, orange covers
essential support systems like power supply, security, communication, and connectivity,
blue represents fallback systems designed to prevent loss of vessel control and ensure
ROC stability, and finally, yellow refers to operator training elements. Throughout the
conceptual framework, small light bulb icons followed by text indicate specific recommen­
dations that should be considered at each stage of the ROC development process.
In addition to the recommendation of the conceptual framework, the following recom­
mended actions are highlighted and presented below:
• Strong Cybersecurity Measures: Apply data encryption, software updates, multi­
factor authentication protocols, and regular system updates. IACS has addressed
cybersecurity risk through UR E26 “Cyber Resilience of Ships” and UR E27 “Cyber
Resilience of On­Board Systems and Equipment” [74].
• Optimize operators’ schedules: To prevent fatigue, it is recommended to schedule
shifts according to rest hour regulations [75].
• Interactive System: Enhance system interactivity through features in order to im­
prove situational awareness and maintain operator engagement.
• Active Participation in MASS Working Groups: Maintain contact with national au­
thorities to stay informed and provide input on regulatory developments.
• Number of operators: For USVs, operate with one operator in the ROC and one
standby to ensure effective backup in case of an emergency. As for commercial and
passenger ships, the ROC should include more than one operator and a supervisor,
depending on the vessel’s size.

Conclusion
This study presents, for the first time, a conceptual framework and a semi­quantitative risk
assessment specifically tailored for the implementation and operation of ROCs in the con­
text of MASS, with a novel approach that focuses particularly on Unmanned Surface Ves­
sels (USVs). The research was guided by three central research questions and answered
through a combination of desk research and interviews with industry professionals.
To ensure that the ROC meets industry standards and complies with relevant maritime
regulations, the legislative, regulatory, and technical requirements for establishing an
ROC were first examined. Furthermore, by incorporating the gained knowledge from the
research, the main risks associated with building and operating an ROC were analyzed,
with a particular focus on infrastructure, economic, regulatory, and human factor risks.
The conceptual framework defines the system architecture with the main technologies
needed for the ROC interface, including workstation design, communication and connec­
tivity systems, sensor and radar integration, and power supply options. Generally, to
maintain stable operations, fallback systems are developed to handle critical situations.
In addition, physical and cyber security should be addressed by ensuring redundancy
in the different systems. Before a system is fully implemented, various tests should be
conducted to identify hidden issues and confirm its dependability.
The successful operation of the ROC depends on human factors, and according to the
framework, the operator of the ROC should be familiar with maritime operations and re­
ceive qualifications such as STCW, GMDSS, and remote operation training. The human
element is essential for the success of the ROC, with operator certifications being cru­
cial to ensure safe and efficient operations. To improve operator situational awareness, it
is recommended to have a user­friendly interface that allows for monitoring vessels and
effectively responding to emergencies by combining PTZ cameras, alarm­handling sys­
tems, and customized displays. This interactivity enhances both operator concentration
and the overall safety of vessel operations.
By addressing these aspects, the main findings from this paper highlight the key risks
in implementing an ROC and present a conceptual framework for their integration into
the maritime industry. The regulatory analysis shows that the current legal state is not
yet ready for broad ROC adoption, as the IMO’s MASS RSE is still under development.
However, in the short term, the ROC is expected to function as land­based extensions of
the bridge operations, which require human oversight for safe operations.
This research has several limitations, including its focus primarily on the implementation
of ROCs for USVs, which limits the generalizability of the findings to other maritime op­
erations. Additionally, the risk assessment is subjective, relying on qualitative data from
interviews, which creates a gap between the perspectives of the interviewees and the
researchers’ interpretations, potentially contributing to subjectivity in the findings. Fur­
thermore, the conceptual framework has not been validated through real­life pilot imple­
mentations, meaning that its practical applicability has not yet been tested.
To conclude, the recommendations for further research should focus on validating the
conceptual framework through pilot tests in a real environment to identify potential gaps
and refine the framework based on operational challenges. Finally, as new IMO guidelines
for autonomous vessels and ROC are being developed, it would be valuable to integrate
Developing a Remote Operating Center Framework for Autonomous Vessels: A Business Perspective
41

these regulations into the framework to enhance the robustness and relevance of the
framework in the context of future maritime developments.

Interview Question Framework
This appendix contains the guiding questions used in stakeholder interviews for the the­
sis. The questions were adapted for each stakeholder group to explore perspectives on
regulations, technical setup, human factors, risk, and economics in relation to Remote
Operating Centers (ROCs).
General Questions (All Stakeholders)
Introductory Questions
• What contact did you have before with Remote Operating Centers (ROCs)?
• Do you currently operate or plan to implement an ROC? If not, why?
Interview Questions for Companies
Technical Setup
• Describe your current ROC setup (or planned setup).
• What hardware/software platforms do you use?
• How many vessels are monitored per operator/workstation?
• What communication methods are preferred (satellite, 5G, etc.) and why?
• What were the key design challenges?
Operational Experience
• What is your current operational use of the ROC?
• Are you using it only for your own vessels, or also providing services to others?
Economic Questions
• What are the startup and ongoing costs of your ROC?
Human Roles
• What tasks are assigned to ROC operators?
• What training do operators receive before beginning their duties?
Regulatory Approach
• What regulations did you follow to implement your ROC?
• Did you work with any classification societies or flag states for certification?
Interview Questions for Classification Societies and Flag States
Regulations and Legal Framework
• Do you have any standards or guidance specific to ROCs?
• Are you working with IMO, other flag states, or class societies on ROC regulation?
• What documents or testing are required to certify an ROC?
Technical and Safety Requirements
• What technical setup requirements are there for ROCs?
• How are issues like safety, cybersecurity, and fallback operations handled?
• Do you provide training or competency requirements for ROC operators?
• In the case of an accident, who is legally responsible—the ROC, operator, com­
pany?

South Korea
2024
HD Hyundai has received AiP from the Korean
Register and the Liberian International Ship &
Corporate Registry. The demonstration was con­
ducted within the regulatory framework of Ko­
rea’s Advanced Industry Regulatory Sandbox
Project, overseen by the Ministry of Trade, Indus­
try, and Energy 31.
A Remote Control Service is being commercial­
ized by HD Hyundai in collaboration with Avikus,
a subsidiary specializing in autonomous naviga­
tion, utilizing its HiNAS Control autonomous navi­
gation solution, along with Pont.OS, the in­house
remote control solution developed by HD Korea
Shipbuilding & Offshore Engineering. With this,
HD Hyundai successfully demonstrated its inte­
grated autonomous navigation and remote con­
trol capabilities on a large commercial vessel,
specifically an 8,000 TEU containership.
The remote control service enables precise steering
and speed control while being specifically designed
to tackle the unique challenges of a vessel’s operat­
ing environment. It is equipped to manage communi­
cation delays, respond to unexpected situations, and
mitigate cyber threats that may arise during naviga­
tion.
Additionally, the technology allows seamless
control transfers between multiple ROCs on large
vessels during long­distance voyages, ensuring un­
interrupted remote operations.

2017
Sea Machines Robotics gained BV Type Ap­
proval for its commercial wireless remote­control
helm systems. Sea Machines is the first com­
pany to receive BV’s Type Approval certification
for this specific form of technology 40.
Sea Machines has developed the SM300, a
cutting­edge wireless remote helm control sys­
tem designed for autonomous self­piloting. Al­
ways on watch, the SM300 enables off­boat re­
mote command, providing operators with real­
time situational awareness, advanced navigation
capabilities, and seamless vessel control from
virtually anywhere 41.
SM300 provides real­time operational domain aware­
ness, telemetry, and vessel attitude to remote com­
manders through an intuitive chart­based user inter­
face. The system integrates data from onboard and
offboard cameras, sensors, and other equipment, of­
fering tracked targets, video feeds, and warnings to
ensure safe and efficient operations.
Additionally,
onboard machinery, instrumentation, and payload
data are streamed directly to the user interface for
seamless monitoring and control. It allows mariners
to break free from fixed control stations. Offering line­
of­sight wireless helm and propulsion control with an
impressive range of up to 1,000 meters, the system
also supports remote operation of auxiliary and pay­
load equipment via a wearable belt­pack device 42
43.

cial product.
