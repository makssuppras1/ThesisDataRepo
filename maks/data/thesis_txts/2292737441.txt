Abstract
Inorganic arsenic is a well known toxic element and has been a eld of interest for some
years.
Studies have revealed that some foodstu has higher levels of inorganic arsenic
than others. Especially some types of seafood can have very high levels of total arsenic
where the primary arsenic species is arsenobetaine, which is innocuous, whereas other
types, such as seaweed can have very high levels of inorganic arsenic compared to the total
arsenic content. Because of these dierences, speciation analysis for arsenic is important.
The aim of the present project was to determine the content of inorganic arsenic in Italian
mussels and optimise the selectivity using hydride generation.
The Italian mussels analysed in this project were from seven dierent locations scattered
across the Italian coastline. They were extracted in a water bath and analysed by HPLC-
ICP-MS. The levels of inorganic arsenic was in the range of 0.0014 - 2.029 mg · kg−1w.w
across the dierent locations while the LOD was 0.0014 mg ·kg−1w.w. Hydride generation
was applied on some of the Italian mussels as they displayed additional peaks overlapping
the inorganic arsenic peak, when analysed using HPLC-ICP-MS.
The stability of the inorganic arsenic content in freeze dried materials was investigated
using an isochronous stability study at three dierent temperatures. The study was con-
ducted using a certied reference material TORT-3 and Danish mussels (Mytilus Edulis).
The results showed a tendency in the content of inorganic arsenic in both the TORT-3
samples and the Danish mussels that if the samples were stored at 60◦C they would have
a higher content of inorganic arsenic than samples stored at -20◦C.

Introduction
Arsenic is the 20th most abundant element in the earth's crust [1]. Arsenic is a well
known element and has been used for many applications throughout time, from the Roman
empire to the present. The use of arsenic as pigments [2] for paint and glassware and as
poison are all well known. Arsenic is emitted into nature from a number of dierent natural
sources, such as volcanic activity and erosion of arsenic containing minerals. Furthermore
there are anthropogenic sources of arsenic emission, such as the use of arsenic containing
pesticides, mining industry and combustion of fossil fuels [35]. In 1990 more than half
of the arsenic used in industrial production, was used for timber preservatives and 22% of
the arsenic was used in agricultural chemistry, such as pesticides [6].
Arsenic is known to have toxic eects on humans when consumed. If large quantities
are ingested or inhaled the symptoms can be acute poisoning with death as the endpoint.
Studies show that the lethal dose (LD50) for rats is between 15 and 145 mg · (kg · b.w.)−1
for arsenite [4]. Other eects of long term arsenic exposure are dierent kinds of cancer
(e.g. lung, kidney and skin cancer) and skin lesions, these eects are usually referred to
as chronic eects [4,7]. Long term exposure to arsenic can have dierent sources, either it
can be a work related problem or it can be due to exposure through drinking water and
diet.
Arsenic is not the only element which is harmful to the human body. Other chemical
contaminants in the food supply such as heavy metals and organic molecules, which are
believed to be harmful, has been an area of interest for many years. Especially the content
of heavy metals in the most commonly consumed sh has been a widely discussed topic
in Denmark. Recently it has been debated whether or not pregnant women and children
should eat tuna because of the high content of methylmercury [8].
Rice as well as seafood has been subject to many investigations concerning the content
of arsenic in recent years and the results indicate that rice has a high arsenic content.
Hence, the levels of arsenic are especially threatening in countries where the primary diet
consists of rice. In some of these countries the levels of arsenic in the ground water is very
high (> 50µg · L−1) compared to the average value of arsenic (0.1 −2µg · L−1) [4]. This
may have an enhancing eect on the arsenic content in rice due to the ability of the plants
to absorb arsenic from the water and accumulate it in the grains [9,10].
With all the contaminants in our food supply it is important to keep a close watch
on the food safety in order to reduce the number of people, who are exposed to harmful
contaminants by eating contaminated food. These contaminants could be chemicals that
cause endocrine disruptions or elements which accumulate in the human body and cause
dierent organ failures [11]. The European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) and The Joint
FDA/WHO Expert Committee on Food Additives (JEFCA) have published health based
guidance values that indicate levels of ingestion, where humans will have an increased risk
of experiencing toxic eects caused by exposure [4,11,12]. Some toxic elements are assessed
dierently depending on the species. An example of this is mercury, where methyl mercury
is more harmful than inorganic mercury [13]. The same applies to arsenic, which is divided
into two dierent species, the organic arsenic and the inorganic arsenic, where inorganic
arsenic is considered to be the most toxic.
Since there is a dierence in whether the exposure of arsenic comes from organic or
inorganic arsenic, it is important to develop methods for speciation analysis. This need
for speciation analysis is especially important in seafood where there is a high content of
arsenic, but where the majority is organic arsenic in the form of arsenobetaine, which is
innocuous [14]. In comparison, the predominant form of arsenic in rice is inorganic arsenic.

The present project will focus on inorganic arsenic in mussels. Mussels, oysters and

Theory
4.1
Arsenic
Arsenic (As) is an ubiquitous element. It is released into the environment from various
sources, both natural and anthropogenic. The earth's crust contains arsenic-containing
minerals and due to erosion and volcanic activity arsenic is released into nature. Other
sources that contribute to the emission of arsenic into nature are combustion of fossil fuels
and the use of arsenic-containing pesticides [9, 11]. Arsenic compounds are divided into
two groups, the inorganic arsenic (iAs) and the organic arsenic (oAs), where the organic
arsenic is identied as compounds containing at least one arsenic-carbon bond.
Inorganic arsenic is present in mainly 3 oxidation states: -3 (AsH3, arsine),+3 (H3AsO3,
arsenite) and +5 (H3AsO4, arsenate) [5, 16]. The organic arsenic species are numerous,
some publications state that over 100 dierent naturally occurring species have been iden-
tied in marine samples of which 50 are newly discovered arsenolipids [4, 14, 17, 18]. In
gure 4.1 a selection of dierent arsenic species is shown.
Figure 4.1: A list of some arsenic species, from top left corner As(III), As(V), MMA
(monomethylarsonate), DMA (dimethylarsinate), AB (arsenobetaine), arsenosugars and two
dierent arsenolipids at the bottom [14].
4.1.1
Arsenic toxicity
Arsenic has varying toxicity, depending on the species. Inorganic arsenic has proved
to have toxic eects on the human body. Among the inorganic species AsH3 (As3) is
considered more toxic than both As+3 and As+5. In contrast most of the organic species
such as arsenobetaine are considered harmless. There is, however, a large group of other
organic compounds, which have unknown toxicity, including arsenosugars and arsenolipids.
Arsenosugars consists of a sugar scaold with an arsenic moiety attached (see gure 4.1),
whereas arsenolipids have long hydrocarbon chains. Both can be found in many types of
seafood [5,19,20].

Arsenosugars and arsenolipids have not been studied as much as other arsenic com-
pounds and therefore they are considered to be potentially toxic. Studies show that some
arsenosugars and thio-arsenicals have toxic eects [19].
A study conducted by R. Raml et al. [21] assessing the bioaccumulation/excretion
of a synthetic made arsenosugar, resulted in a 4 to 95% excretion within dierent test
subjects [21]. This result indicates an accumulation of arsenosugars in the human body,
although the levels accumulated clearly depends on the individual metabolism of the test
subject [21].
Arsenolipids are the primary arsenic species in marine oils, but even so the toxic prop-
erties have not been investigated as much as inorganic arsenic [20].
Inorganic arsenic has been proved to be carcinogenic, especially skin and lung cancer
are common eects [9, 22]. Other symptoms of elevated arsenic exposure are skin lesions
and to some extent neurological damage, such as numbness in extremities [4].
Due to
the fact that it is primarily inorganic arsenic which is toxic, there has been an increased
interest to perform arsenic speciation on various foodstus [7,17,23].
The health eects of arsenic are being investigated continuously to discover other ad-
verse eects which the arsenic consumption may have on the human body [24].
Some
research links arsenic exposure to diabetes in areas where the drinking water has a high
content of arsenic. There is, however, not enough data to prove the connection [25].
4.2
Seafood safety
4.2.1
Arsenic in seafood
According to EFSA there is a 1% increased risk of getting certain cancer types if hu-
mans ingest inorganic arsenic in quantities in a range of 0.3-8 µg · (kg · b.w.)−1 per day [4].
This range is referred to as a Benchmark Dose Level 1% (BMDL01). Another expert group
appointed by JECFA refers to a dierent health based guidance value BMDL0.5, which
corresponds to a 0.5% increased risk. In table 4.1 both EFSA's and JEFCA's BMDL val-
ues for inorganic arsenic are listed [4,12].
In some countries the primary source of inorganic arsenic exposure is contaminated
drinking water, especially in some developing countries such as India and Bangladesh,
where high levels of inorganic arsenic have been reported in the water supply [4].
In
Denmark, the main source of exposure is also the water in beverages but in much lower
levels than in Bangladesh. In addition to beverages the main source of exposure in Denmark
is cereal products. In gure 4.2 a graphical representation of the distribution of inorganic
arsenic exposure in Denmark is shown [11].

4.2.2
Exposure
Seafood generally has a high content of arsenic, but the ratio between inorganic arsenic
and organic arsenic ( iAs
oAs) is low in comparison with rice. A study showed that blue mussels
(Mytilus edulis) contained on average 0.042 mg · kg−1 inorganic arsenic and 1.12 mg · kg−1
total arsenic [11], and in the same study rice contained on average 0.10 mg·kg−1 of inorganic
arsenic and 0.25 mg · kg−1 total arsenic, this data shows that in the rice samples around
40% of the arsenic was inorganic whereas the percentages in the mussels were around 4%.
This shows the importance of speciation when assessing food safety regarding arsenic, as
some foodstu might be classied as harmful even though the content of inorganic arsenic
is very low.
Due to the fact that inorganic arsenic is more toxic than organic arsenic species, it would
be more relevant to conduct speciation analysis when analysing for arsenic in seafood and
other food stu. Because of this, EFSA has underlined the need for speciation analysis to
determine the content of dierent arsenic species [4].
It has been reported by Amlund et al. [28] that adults have an intake of 0.7 - 32
µg per day of inorganic arsenic [28], and since the consumption of rice and seafood varies,
countries with a high consumption of either rice or seafood are more likely to ingest levels
exceeding the BMDL values in table 4.1. While countries with a low consumption are less
prone to exceed the recommended values.

Analytical theory
4.3.1
Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometry
Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometry (ICP-MS) can measure a wide array
of elements in the same sample, it can measure single elements or it can scan a range of
mass to charge (m/z) ratio. This can be very useful if several elements are required to
be analysed, for instance in water samples analysed for heavy metals along with benecial
elements as well.
In addition to the ability to measure many elements simultaneously it has a very low
limit of detection (LOD), in some cases as low as parts per trillion (ppt) and it has a long
linear range, which enables the ICP-MS to measure a sample with very dierent concen-
trations of analytes [36]. Furthermore, the ICP-MS can have a high sample throughput
depending on how many elements that needs to be targeted.
An overview of an ICP-MS instrument is shown in gure 4.3. Prior to the mass separ-
ation the sample is introduced into the apparatus. When a liquid sample is injected into
the ICP-MS a nebuliser transforms the sample into a ne aerosol, which is carried to the
spray chamber by an inert carrier gas, usually argon (Ar). The spray chamber eliminates
larger droplets and the smallest droplets with a diameter of 1 −7 µm are transferred to
the torch [37].

polyatomic interferences or a reactive gas can shift the target analyte to a higher m/z ratio
to avoid certain interferences. Examples of these interferences could be 40Ar35Cl+ with
the same m/z ratio as 75As+ or 40Ar16O+ which has a similar m/z as 56Fe+ [38,39]. The
40Ar35Cl+ interference can be avoided or diminished by shifting the 75As+ to another m/z
by reaction with a reactive gas.

4.3.2
High Performance Liquid Chromatography
High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) is a method for separating analytes
in solution. The way the analytes are separated is by an equilibrium with the mobile phase
and the stationary phase, where the stationary phase is the column material and the mobile
phase is the eluent.
Depending on the column material the chromatography can have dierent properties,
if the material is polar the column retains polar analytes, whereas if the column is apolar
it retains apolar analytes.
Usually the column is packed with either silica or polymers as the backbone, where
groups with the desired functionalities are attached. Most silica based columns have the
disadvantage that if the mobile phase has a high pH (> pH 8) the columns are destroyed.
This is because the silica backbone begins to dissolve. The functional groups attached to
the backbone can be apolar hydrocarbon chains with 18 carbon (C-18) or positively charged
amine groups depending on the column type, in gure 4.7 the two types of column material
are displayed. The time analytes are retained on a column is referred to as the retention
time (tr), which is equal to the time from injection to the analyte is detected. Analytes
in a solution must have dierent properties to be separated via HPLC, otherwise they
will have the same retention time and it will be dicult to dierentiate between them [36].
These properties can be polarity, charge or ionic strength which gives the analytes dierent
anity for the column and thereby dierent retention time.

Dierent HPLC methods have dierent properties, but the most widely used is reversed

charged at pH above 9.2, see table 4.3. When using anion-ion exchange chromatography
the analytes must be negatively charged in order to be retained on the column.
Ion exchange columns can be used either for cations or anions depending on the func-

are in the samples and if they are positively or negatively charged in order to choose the
right column.

pros and cons. For instance, a wide short column can have a higher ow rate, which results
in a short analysis time for each sample, compared to longer columns with the same width.

4.3.3
Hydride generation
Hydride generation (HG) is an acidic reductive reaction converting hydride active ele-
ments to hydrides. This reaction has been known for many years, but has been rediscovered
and applied for arsenic speciation [40,41]. The reaction works by mixing the arsenic spe-
cies with sodium borohydrate (NaBH4) and hydrochloric acid (HCl) to form the hydrides,
which can be analysed afterwards.
HG is used to increase the sensitivity as the hydride generation reaction produces volat-
ile arsenic hydride species and thereby diminishes co-eluting arsenic species or interferences
from some organic arsenic species [41,42]. Some organic arsenic species such as MMA and
DMA are also hydride active species and cannot be eliminated this way. But as they elute
earlier than As(V) when using HPLC the interferences are not critical. The reaction con-
ditions can however be adjusted to gain a lower conversion of MMA and DMA by changing
the pH and the concentration of NaBH4 [41].
Studies have shown that arsenosugars can form volatile compounds [42]. This can be
a potential problem as arsenosugars and As(V) have a tendency to have similar retention
time under some HPLC conditions [43]. The hydride generation of arsenosugars has a
lower eciency than hydride generation of inorganic arsenic [17].
Since the reaction requires two reactants (NaBH4,HCl) the ow rates need to be op-
timised to the amount of sample introduced. In gure 4.9 the HG-system is displayed.

5.3.1
Stability study
The stability study was planned using the principles described by Lamberty et. al
(1998) [47], where an isochronous stability study is described. Since the available time was
limited it was chosen to run the study for a shorter period of time than the six months
mentioned in the article. The total duration of the stability study was therefore set to
eight weeks. The samples were stored at four dierent temperature gradients, all the tem-
perature gradients are displayed in gure 5.3. The study was an isochronous study, which
allowed all the samples to be analysed on the same day to avoid day to day deviations in
the analytical method.
Three samples of CRM-TORT-3 and three samples of Danish mussels (Mytilus Edulis)
were stored at each temperature gradient. Before homogenisation of the Danish mussels a
decision was made to only use the edible parts. The edible parts were homogenised in a
blender and transferred to circular aluminium foil trays so that the homogenised mussels
were around 1 cm deep. These trays were placed in a -20◦C freezer prior to freeze drying
(CHRIST BETA 1-8, ∼42 h, 0.520 mbar).
5.3.2
Sample preparation and extraction
The method of extraction was chosen based on the master thesis written by Y. Qian
in 2011 [48], in which she concluded that an extraction with 0.1 M HNO3 in 3% H2O2 and
60 minutes in a 90◦C water bath gave the same results as the previously applied method
using a microwave assisted extraction. Based on this knowledge the water bath extraction
method was used in the present study.
0.3 g of each sample was weighed into a round bottomed 13 mL centrifuge tube
(101x16.5 mm, PP from Sarstedt) and 10 mL of the freshly prepared extraction solu-
tion was added (0.1 M HNO3 in 3% H2O2, see section 5.1). The tubes were placed in
a water bath at 90◦C for 60 minutes. During the 60 minutes the samples were shaken
3 times to ensure that all sample material came in contact with the extraction solution.
After the water bath the samples were centrifuged at 4000 RPM for 10 minutes using a
Sigma 3-18K centrifuge. The supernatant was decanted and 300 µL of each sample were
ltered in single use 45 µm lter vials (Mini-UniPrep, 45 µm).

6.3.1
Seafood safety
Based on the data from the analysis and data found in literature (table 4.2), it seams
that the Italian mussels analysed in the present project have a content of inorganic arsenic
that in general is within the levels found by others in similar sample types. All the content
of the mussels has been plotted in gure 6.8. This shows that all the samples except four
have a content below 0.4 mg · kg−1w.w of inorganic arsenic.
Figure 6.8: The content of inorganic arsenic in the Italian mussels separated by location. The
two that goes out of the frame have values of respectively 2.0 and 1.6 mg · kg−1 w.w.
Comparing these ndings to the BMDL01 of 0.3 - 8 µg · kg bw−1 per day of iAs it is
necessary to calculate an exposure scenario to estimate the intake of iAs if the diet contains
mussels.
For the exposure scenario a person of 70 kg eats 250 g of mussels (only the edible parts)
containing on average 0.118 mg·kg−1 (1180 µg·kg−1) of iAs or in a worst case scenario the
mussels contain 2.029 mg · kg−1 iAs. Then the meal of 250 g of average mussels contains
29.6 µg of iAs and divided with the body mass of the person this is equal to a consumption
of 0.42 µg · kg bw−1, which is within the BMDL01 range. In the worst case scenario, the
intake would be 7.2 µg ·kg bw−1 iAs, which is in the high end of the range. Based on these
calculations a person of 70 kg should eat no more than 177 g of mussels (assuming they
contain 0.118 mg·kg−1) to stay below the BMDL01. In the worst case scenario the mussels
contain 2.029 mg · kg−1 and therefore no more than 10 g of mussels should be ingested.

Conclusion
The results of the HPLC-ICP-MS analysis showed that the Italian mussels on average
across all locations contained 0.118 ± 0.56 mg · kg−1 w.w of inorganic arsenic with a range
from 0.0014 to 2.03 mg·kg−1 w.w, where the LOD for the method was 0.0014 mg·kg−1 w.w.
The content of each sample is listed in appendix A.5 and a summary for each location is
viewed in table 6.6. The chromatography of some samples showed additional peaks in the
area of As(V), which were investigated using hydride generation.
The post column hydride generation reaction did show potential to increase the se-
lectivity for inorganic arsenic, and the HPLC-HG-ICP-MS results showed that some ar-
senic compounds were co-eluting with the As(V) species as the HPLC-ICP-MS analyses
proposed. Nevertheless, more work needs to be done in order to get the hydride generation
to function properly.
The seafood safety was assessed by an exposure scenario in section 6.3.1 which showed
that the Italian mussels does possess an actual risk of exceeding the range of the BMDL01
if ingested in quantities of more than 177 g per day of average mussels. In a worst case
scenario 10 g of mussel is enough to exceed the lower bound in the BMDL01 range.
The stability conducted showed that freeze dried samples stored at 60◦C for three weeks
or more had a signicantly higher level of inorganic arsenic compared to samples stored at
-20◦C. The results varied depending on the type of statistical method used, but the AN-
OVA and Fisher's LSD, which are the strongest methods, showed a signicant dierence
between the control samples and the samples stored at 60◦C.
Finally, the data from the Italian mussels were compared using a principal components
analysis to see if there was any correlation between the geographical origin of the mussels;
however, there was no clear pattern in the data-set.

Perspectives
In future work it would be interesting to investigate the nature of the arsenic com-
pounds that apparently co-elute with As(V) in the chromatograms. In addition to the
identication of the co-eluting compounds a method to avoid this interference when meas-
uring inorganic arsenic should be developed. A possible method could be the post-column
hydride generation. This method could be of use when evaluating the seafood safety more
accurately in the future. Furthermore, the Italian mussels should be analysed again to
conrm the ndings, especially the ndings in the few samples with very high contents of
inorganic arsenic and the samples that were beyond the levels of the calibration curves.
Another project that would be interesting is to examine whether it is possible to identify
