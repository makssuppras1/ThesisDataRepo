Introduction 
 
This chapter describes the introduction of the project which includes a short section about reinforced 
concrete in constructions as a motivator for this project, a description of the project scope and the end of 
this chapter will include the structure of this project report. 
1.1 Motivation 
In centuries, concrete has been used for structural purposes as for instance in the Egyptian pyramids around 
3000 BCE. The Egyptian produced bricks of mud and straw, the mortars was created with gypsum and lime 
which ended up being an early form of concrete. Around 300 BCE the Romans used concrete close to modern 
concrete in their buildings such as Colosseum and Pantheon. At that time, the concrete structures were not 
reinforced, but later reinforcement were used in e.g. the first bridge Alvord Lake Bridge in the US in 1889.  
Knowing that concrete has been used in centuries, it is not a surprise that concrete is the most used structural 
material in the world. An estimation of the concrete expenditure in the world is of the order 33 billion metric 
tons each year (Metha and Monteiro, 2013). There is three main reasons, that concrete is the most widely 
used material in structures. First, concrete has a remarkable water resistance without severe deterioration, 
which is the reason concrete is used as dams, canals, pavements and bridge piers. The second reason is about 
the cost and location of concrete. Concrete is commonly the cheapest and simplest material, since the 
material is inexpensive and usually available in the most places. The third reason regards concrete as 
structural element, which has the potential to be formed into different sizes and shapes in prefabricated 
formwork, which is illustrated in figure 1.1. Figure 1.1 illustrates a bridge that is assembled by prefabricated 
concrete elements, which shows the potential of size and shape. 
 
Figure 1.1: Bridge consisting prefabricated concrete elements. 
==PAGE:13==
 
2 
 
 
Concrete is often used in combination of pre-stressed concrete or reinforced concrete. Reinforced concrete 
is basically concrete with steel bars that collaborates to resist the tensile force. Pre-stressed concrete is pre-
compressed by the steel tendons to resist the tensile stresses, when concrete structures are loaded. This 
method entails deflection control, long span elements and prevents cracking in concrete. Concrete is a 
composite material with high compressive strength and a low tensile strength, and crack development is 
possible when concrete is exposed to various influences.  
Handling cracked reinforced concrete elements different ways of thinking develops, for instance according 
to the bearing capacity and the lifetime of a given element. When cracks occur in load carrying reinforced 
concrete structures then crack size and location have an influence on the structural bearing capacity of the 
structure. When size and location of cracks are determined, the structural lifetime can be computed 
according to the environmental impact. Another way of thinking is the change of stiffness and stress path in 
the element when cracks occur. Locations where cracks develop are reducing the stiffness of the element in 
that area, which provides the forces in the element to change flow paths. A third way of thinking is about the 
directions of the crack growth, when the forces changes flow paths according to the existing crack condition. 
Each of these ways of thinking are interesting according to statically loaded systems as well as for dynamically 
loaded systems, but this project is only considering statically load.  
According to the thoughts about crack growth, this project concerns crack occurrence when a reinforced 
concrete disk is affected by in-plane external load. 
1.2 Purpose of Project  
This project aims to investigate in-plane loaded reinforced concrete disks, when crack formation and crack 
growth occur, likewise how internal forces change flow path during crack development. 
The main focus of this project is numerical modelling, where it is necessary to establish an easy and simple 
finite element program to determine a response of an in-plane loaded reinforced concrete disk with an 
acceptable accuracy. The MATLAB program is a numerical tool based on the principals of the fiber model, 
Newton-Raphson method and two types of elements. The two elements are a bar element (B21) and a 
bilinear quadrilateral plane element (Isoparametric CPS4). 
The numerical modelling are compared with experimental studies to confirm or reject the assumptions and 
results produced with the MATLAB program. 
1.3 Numerical Modelling Tool - ConCrack 
ConCrack is the name of the numerical modelling tool built and used in this Master’s thesis, while the purpose 
of the numerical tool is to compute the behavior of an in-plane loaded reinforced concrete disk according to 
fracture mechanics. The name ConCrack abbreviation of ‘concrete’ and ‘crack’ which refers to the purpose 
of the tool. The numerical tool is only operation on the MATLAB-platform, since MATLAB is able to solve 
complex numerical problems, got an effective data processing and it is possible to make a user friendly and 
visual tool as ConCrack. The numerical tool is built on the fundamental principles in fracture mechanics, the 
fiber model, Newton-Raphson method and two different element types as described in chapter 1.2.  
In order to achieve a user friendly and visual illustration of a given structure used to investigate, three 
different scripts were gained from respectively course 41525 and 11305 at DTU. The scripts gained from the

Numerical Modelling of Fracture Mechanics 
 
This chapter describes fracture mechanics that is the fundamental principal in the numerical model, which 
describes the behavior of crack growth and crack propagation in in-plane loaded reinforced concrete disks. 
First part concerns the basics of fracture mechanics as well as the fictitious crack model in ordinary concrete. 
The last part comprises usage of the fictitious crack model and the fiber model describing reinforced concrete 
behavior. 
2.1 Basic Fracture Modes and Crack Concepts 
Fracture mechanics contains three primary fracture modes, which is defined as mode I, mode II and mode III 
– see figure 2.1. These fracture modes are describing how cracks occur relatively to element behavior.  
 
Figure 2.1: Three fundamental modes of fracture propagation. [Modification of (Björnström, et al, 2006)]. 
Mode I indicates how the parts of the element are torn apart, in the y-direction, according to tension in the 
element. Mode II shows how the element parts are sliding in-plane in opposed direction of each other, 
according to in-plane shear. Mode III illustrates how the element parts are sliding out-of-plane in opposed 
direction of each other, according to out-of-plane shear. 
For unreinforced- and reinforced concrete as well as similar material types, mode I is the most common crack 
propagation, since mode II and mode III unusually occur likewise. A combination of the different modes does 
occur whereas the combination of mode I and mode II is the most common mix for concrete (Malm, 2006). 
The numerical finite element model is operating with mode I and mode II, since the focus area is in-plane 
loaded disk elements, which neglects mode III. 
Two types of crack concepts are concerned in this chapter – the discrete concept and the smeared concept. 
The discrete crack concept models a crack as a geometrical discontinuity while the smeared crack concept 
models a crack as a continuum. 
The discrete crack concept is similar to the natural notion of crack propagation. Some of the disadvantages 
about the discrete concept concern the crack limitation according to the defined path along the element 
sides. Another disadvantage is about the finite element method, which does not coincide with the varying 
reversal in the nodal connectivity that occurs in the fracture. These disadvantages disappears, if the location 
of fracture development is known similar to the yield line mechanism. When modelling a discrete crack the 
==PAGE:16==
 
5 
 
 
initial stiffness is equal to a large value in relation to describe the un-cracked state at the predefined discrete 
crack (Rots and Blaauwendraad, 1989). 
The smeared crack concept is used in this project as a notion in the numerical program, ConCrack, since a 
smeared crack conserves the indigenous element mesh and there is no limits according to the crack 
orientation. Considering micro-cracking in the concrete the smeared crack gives a more realistic approach 
(Rots and Blaauwendraad, 1989).

strengt and stiffness of the concrete structure. When concrete is loaded a non-linear conduct occurs 
according to qrowth of microcracks and propagration of cracks, which is shown in figure 2.2.b. Figure 2.2 
illustrates the response of the concrete when exposed to tension.

To describe non-linear fracture mechanics in numerical modelling, the fictitious crack model is well-known 
and accepted to use in concrete fracture – accordingly a model to implement in the numerical model.  
2.2.1 Hillerborg’s Fictitious Crack Model 
The fictitious crack model (Hillerborg’s fictitious crack model) is a material model proposed by (Hillerborg, et 
al, 1976), and it is able to describe all types of tensile fractures in general, but it is primary used for concrete, 
since concrete is a non-yielding material and contains micro-cracks (Hillerborg, 1991). The “fictitious” part of 
the “Fictitious Crack Model” refers to the zone without full seperation, which is able to transfer stresses 
(Bazant, 1992). This model enables analyzing crack growth of existing cracks, propagation and formation of 
new cracks. 
The fictitious crack model is described with figure 2.3 which is an illustration of the crack propagation (mode 
I – similar applied for mode II and III), and figure 2.4 that is plots describing the deformation caracteristics. 
The major assumptions of the fictitious crack model is illustrated with figure 2.3, figure 2.4 and mentioned in 
e.g. (Hillerborg, 1978).  
 
Figure 2.3: Fictitious Crack Model (DTU Course: 11347). 
Figure 2.3 shows that a crack propagates, when tension stress reaches the tensile strength of concrete (ft). 
The parameter w is the width of the fictitious crack, which is the zone that transfers stresses across the 
narrow crak opening. When w = wc then σ(w) = 0, which causes the actual crack to occur. When w = 0 and ε 
= εct then σ(w) = ft , this means the stress at the fictitious crack tip is equal to the tensile strength of the 
concrete. εct is the limiting strain where the fracture zone starts. Basicallly, the stress is decreasing with an 
increasing fictitius crack width at a given point until w = wc. The length of the stress free crack is denoted a0, 
and the length of the fictitious crack is denoted lp. This means figure 2.3 is very much similar to figure 2.2.a. 
According to the theory of elasticity, a stress concentration occurs at the tip of a crack, which is infinitely 
large when concrete is exposd to tension. Therefore the fictitious crack model is more qualified in finite 
element analysis, since it assumes finite stresses (Hillerborg, 1978). 
The illustration of the fracture performance in concrete (see figure 2.2.b) is explained with two curves (see 
figure 2.4). Two curves are necessary because the stress-deformation curve is unable to describe the general 
behavior of the specimen, when the fracture process zone develops. The development of the fracture process

2.3 Modelling Assumptions of Reinforced Concrete 
The fundamental principles of the reinforced concrete model are defined with the fiber model [(Olesen and 
Poulsen, 2016), (Olesen and Poulsen, 2011)] and the fictitious crack (chapter 2.3.1). The principle of the idea 
behind the fiber model concerns a larger element or structure which behavior is described by a finite amount 
of fibers. These fibers are given some material parameters, which define the behavior of the larger element 
or structure when loaded. 
An illustration of the fiber model is shown in figure 2.5, which is a slender post crack cylindrical specimen of 
reinforced concrete in tension. 
 
Figure 2.5: Slender post crack cylindrical specimen (Olesen and Poulsen, 2016). 
Figure 2.5 shows a shaded fiber, which defines the cylindrical specimen. The fiber is defined as figure 2.5 
indicates, where the parameter s is the width of the fiber. The variable w is the fictitious crack width (see 
chapter 2.2.1) and u is the fiber elongation, which is equal to the overall elongation of each material. N is the 
tensile force applied to each end of the specimen, Dc and Ds are respectively the concrete diameter and 
reinforcement diameter. Following assumptions are applied for the fiber model (Olesen and Poulsen, 2016). 
The displacement at the fiber boundaries are equally identically, where the boundaries are defined as the 
midpoints between each crack in the specimen. The reinforcement is assumed linear elastic, Ec until ε = εy 
then the reinforcement is assumed behaving perfectly plastic (Nielsen, 2010). Concrete is assumed behaving 
linear elastic in tension when ε ≤ εct and σ(w) ≤ ft as illustrated in figure 2.4 and figure 2.6. The behavior of 
reinforced concrete exposed to tension when ε ≥ εct is described later in figure 2.6.a. The shear stress is 
assumed constant regardless of the relative slip magnitude between concrete and reinforcement, because 
the concrete deviates from the reinforcement allowing the materials to slips against each other.  
The fiber model is used for simple mechanics approach as shown in figure 2.6. Figure 2.6.a illustrates the 
tensile behavior of a fiber exposed to tensile behavior, and figure 2.6.b illustrates a bilinear work-curve for 
concrete in compression. This signifies the reinforced concrete fiber to contain a compressive behavior (see 
figure 2.6.b) and a tensile behavior (see figure 2.6.a).

Isoparametric CPS4 Element in Finite Element Modelling 
 
This chapter describes the implementation of the fiber model in the isoparametric CPS4 element. The first 
part of the chapter defines the CPS4 element according to advantages and defects as well as the crack 
propagation, and finite element expressions. In the end, the CPS4 element verification is described. 
4.1 Element Definition 
The 4-node plane stress element with isoparametric properties contains 8 degree of freedom. The nodes in 
the element are locally numbered as visualized in figure 4.1 – the nodes are numbered counter clockwise 
starting with lower left node. The bar element was only capable of obtaining axial forces while the 4-node 
continuum element is able to obtain shear force and in-plane force in two directions. The stresses and strains 
are varying linearly across each direction of the element. 
 
Figure 4.1: Definition of isoparametric 4-node plane stress element having 8 d.o.f. The d.o.f at each node are pointing 
in the same direction as the global coordinate system (x,y). [Reproduction of (Cook, et. al, 2001)]. 
Figure 4.1 illustrates that isoparametric element contains a local coordinate system with reference 
coordinates ξ and η, where −1 ≤𝜉≤1 and −1 ≤𝜂≤1. The geometrical element design is an advantage 
of using isoparametric elements, since the elements are able to differ from the rectangular shape into other 
shapes with four nodes. This way, the response of complicated structures are possible to describe – chapter 
4.3 is going into details about the isoparametric 4-node element. 
The CPS4 element is simple and easy to use, but the shear locking behavior is a disadvantage. Shear locking 
occurs because the CPS4 element is not capable of exhibit pure bending (Appendix F). Increasing the mesh 
of isoparametric CPS4 elements in a structure is decreasing the error according to shear locking.

5.1 Experiment of Shear in Reinforced Concrete Disk 
This part concerns experiments of reinforced concrete disks fulfilled in (Vecchio and Collins, 1986) and 
(Vecchio, et. al, 1994) – tests of 30 specimens in (Vecchio and Collins, 1986) and tests of 12 specimens in 
(Vecchio, et. al, 1994). The two articles investigate the response of quadratic reinforced concrete element 
subjected to in-plane shear and normal loads with an experimental test setup shown in figure 5.2. The main 
difference between the articles is the concrete strength while (Vecchio, et. al, 1994) is experimenting with 
high strength concrete. The article (Vecchio and Collins, 1986) is based on an international competition 
according to the response of reinforced concrete panels (Collins, et. al, 1985). The interest of investigating 
the two-dimensional reinforced concrete disk response corresponds to the interest of knowing the response 
of large-scale and more complex civil engineering structures as high-rise buildings and long-span bridges. To 
achieve this knowledge, it is necessary to know the response of a simpler element, which is able to describe 
the behavior of larger structures. 
5.1.1 Specimen Details and Experimental Test Setup 
The plane dimensions of the test specimens are 890x890 mm and the thickness is 70 mm. Only in-plane 
reinforcement is used, which means the reinforcement is located in xy-plane parallel with the edges of the 
specimen, which means it is located as a grid in x-direction and y-direction. For some specimen, the 
reinforcement is orthogonally and for others specimens, the reinforcement is isotropic. A sketch of the used 
specimens are illustrated in figure 5.1.

6.1 Newton-Raphson Method 
Newton-Raphson method is a non-linear solution method used to compute the load and response of a given 
element used in ConCrack. Basically, the Newton-Raphson method is all about iterating until convergence for 
each increasing load level to achieve a response of a load-displacement curve. Large load steps entails a 
coarse load-displacement response, which means an unprecise response while small load steps entails an 
accurate load-displacement response. For each iteration a tangent stiffness matrix is computed to describe 
the slope at that specific point on the curve (table 6.1). The accepted iteration accuracy to obtain static 
equilibrium is described in subchapter 6.1.1. To describe the Newton-Raphson implementation table 6.1 is 
used for this purpose while it describes the setup in MATLAB code.

Further Work 
 
This project concerns numerical modelling with bar-element and a bilinear quadrilateral plane element, 
which is compared to experimental studies, but this investigation can be extended beyond the work done in 
this thesis by e.g. using higher order elements to achieve accuracy. Another way of improving the numerical 
tool can be done by including Poisson’s ratio after cracking as well as the difference between the amounts of 
reinforcement in x- and y-axis in a disk. 
This project only considers statically loading which provides a structural behavior, but it is interesting to 
investigate the same structures with dynamic loading to see how the structural behavior has changed or not.  
This project is operating with in-plane loaded disks, but shell elements would be of same interest according 
to crack pattern, flow path of stresses and how the different crack modes occur. 
This thesis investigates 2D structures, but a 3D structure is interesting according to crack growth, flow path 
of stresses, stiffness behavior and much more.

CPS4 Element Defects 
 
This appendix concerns defects of a CPS4 element by using information from (Cook, et. al, 2001). The defect 
concerns pure bending, since the CPS4 element does not bent properly according to a rectangular block, 
which is shown in figure F.1. 
 
Figure F.1: a) Illustrates a pure bending deformation of a rectangular block, b) Illustrates bending of a CPS4 element 
loaded with bending load (Cook, et. al, 2001). 
Figure F.1.a illustrates pure bending, which is not the case in figure F.1.b. The element nodes in figure F.1.b 
is only moving along the x-axis and not along the y-axis which is the case in figure F.1.a – therefore the top 
and bottom sides remain straight. 
The pure bending in figure F.1.a develops horizontal- and vertical strains while the shear strain is zero. For 
CPS4 element in figure F.1.b, the vertical strain remain zero, the horizontal strain is a nonzero variable similar 
to the pure bending in figure F.1.a, and the shear strain is as well a nonzero variable though it should be zero 
in bending. The shear strain that occurs in the CPS4 element is identified as the parasitic shear (Cook, et. al, 
2001). Basically, the CPS4 element generates shear locking behavior since the applied bending moment to 
produce pure bending of the rectangle is larger than the actual value of the bending moment, while the 
parasitic shear is absorbing strain energy. This can be minimized by increasing the amount of CPS4 element 
in the mesh.
