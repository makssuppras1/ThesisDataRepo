A B S T R A C T
In the last few years, especially in the northwest countries of Europe,
the wind energy market has been oriented towards offshore turbine
sites [1]. The higher costs connected to offshore sites brought to a
larger incentive to increase the power production of a single turbine
[2]. Higher output turbines can be achieved by using larger rotor di-
ameters, which in turn can be achieved by using stiffer and stronger
blades [2]. Thus, hybrid ﬁber composites, made of both carbon ﬁbers
and glass ﬁbers, have recently been investigated and employed.
By analyzing the results obtained from the experimental work, the
project aims at evaluating and validating three theoretical models
(Model for Volumetric Composition of Hybrid Composites, Model
for Elastic Modulus of Hybrid Composites, and Model for Tensile
Strength of Hybrid Composites) recently developed at DTU Wind
Energy. With regards to the experimental work, it consists of: com-
posite specimen preparation, microscopy analysis, ﬁber content and
porosity analysis, and tensile test. With regards to three theoretical
models concerning the hybrid carbon/glass ﬁber composites, the ﬁrst
model refers to their volumetric composition, the second model refers
to their elastic modulus, and the third model refers to their tensile
strength.
Generally speaking, it has been observed that the experimental data
points are in good agreement with the model curves of the three the-
oretical models. In particular, with regards to the third model, the
so called "hybrid effect" included in it, has been able to explain the
obtained failure stresses and failure strains, which are increased with
respect to the predictions made by simpler rule of mixtures models.

The introductory chapter is divided into ﬁve sections. Section 1.1
gives the motivation. Section 1.2 describes the objective. Scope and
limitations are discussed in section 1.3. Methodology is illustrated in
section 1.4. The structure of the report is summed up in section 1.5.
1.1
motivation
According to the Renewable 2016 Global Status Report, Wind power
was the leading source of new power generating capacity in Europe
and the United States in 2015, and the second largest in China [3].
Wind power played an important role in meeting electricity demand
particularly in Denmark (42% of demand in 2015), Germany (more
than 60% in four states), and Uruguay (15.5%) [3]. Still in Denmark,
the two world’s biggest wind turbines, with a height of 200 meters,
wingspan of 164 meters, and an 8MW production capacity, were in-
augurated last April [4].
So far, the majority of the existing wind farms have been built on land,
but in the last few years, especially in the northwest countries of Eu-
rope, the wind energy market has been oriented towards offshore
turbine sites [1]. In offshore sites the winds are stronger and steadier
than on land, making wind farms more productive with higher ca-
pacity factors [1]. The higher costs connected to offshore sites, and in
particular to foundations, towers, and grid connections, brought to a
larger incentive to increase the power production of a single turbine
[2].
Higher output turbines can be achieved by using larger rotor diam-
eters, which in turn can be achieved by using stiffer and stronger
blades [2]. By adding material to the main laminates in the blade,
the bending stiffness of the blade is going to increase, as well as the
overall weight of the blade, which in turn puts further stress on the
structure [2]. In the light of these considerations, a material with lower
density and higher stiffness has to be used [2]. Indeed, composites with
carbon ﬁbers, instead of glass ﬁbers, would represent the best choice
if it were not for the higher costs connected to carbon ﬁbers. Thus, hy-
brid composites, made of carbon and glass ﬁbers, have recently been
investigated and employed.
Historically speaking, glass ﬁbers have represented the most used
ﬁber material for composites in wind turbine blades [5]. Nowadays,

even if the use of glass ﬁbers is still dominant, carbon ﬁbers are slowly
becoming a necessity due to their better performance in terms of
low weight and high stiffness. The limit of using carbon ﬁbers mainly
concerns the cost. Thus, the main challenges for composites for wind
turbine blades regard the perfect combination of low weight, hybrid
carbon/glass composition, and materials costs. In order to better un-
derstand hybrid composites, the development of theoretical models
able to describe themselves, e.g. in terms of performances and not
only, is necessary.
Recently, three hybrid composite models have been developed at DTU
Wind Energy. The ﬁrst model concerns the volumetric composition
and the micro-structural uniformity of hybrid carbon/glass ﬁber com-
posites. A way to establish interrelations among ﬁber and matrix vol-
ume fractions has been found [6]. The second and the third model
concern the mechanical performances of hybrid composites using
micro-mechanical considerations. In particular, with regards to the
third model, a synergistic hybrid effect of combining glass and carbon
ﬁbers has been observed for both composite strength and composite
failure strain [2]. Both mechanical properties are increased compared
to predictions made by simpler rule of mixtures models [2].
1.2
objective
The objective of this project is to evaluate and experimentally validate
the three micro-structural related models. With regards to the hybrid
carbon/glass ﬁber composites, while the ﬁrst model refers to their
volumetric composition, the second and the third model refer to their
mechanical performances. A better understanding of these three mod-
els will deﬁnitely lead to a deeper knowledge of hybrid carbon/glass
ﬁber composites.
1.3
scope and limitations
This project deals with hybrid carbon/glass ﬁber reinforced com-
posites. The carbon/glass ﬁbers, together with the epoxy resin, are
manufactured into rectangular shaped plates. The unidirectionality of
these plates is achieved by laying unidirectional carbon/glass ﬁbers
through the ﬁlament winding process. In addition to that, a vacuum
resin infusion process is used to impregnate the ﬁbers. During this
process, resin is forced into the laminate via vacuum tubing. The
manufacturing part is followed by three different tests. In particular,
microscopic examinations, ﬁber content and porosity analyses, and
tensile tests are carried out. The ﬁnal results are compared to the
three micro-structural related models [2, 6].

2.1
fiber reinforced composite materials
Composite materials are macroscopic combinations of two, or more,
distinct materials with importantly different physical and/or chem-
ical properties [7]. The constituent materials are combined in such
a way that "the resulting composite material has superior properties
compared to the single constituent material" [8]. We can also deﬁne
a composite material as "a multiphase material from a combination
of materials, which differ in composition and/or form and remain
bonded together, but preserve their identities and properties, with-
out going into any chemical reactions" [8]. And still, "the components
do not dissolve or completely merge and the synergistic effect is not
obtainable by any of the original components acting singly" [8].
The constituent materials are represented by reinforcements and ma-
trix. Usually, the reinforcements stand for long ﬁbers and they have
a carrying load responsibility. Noteworthy is the fact that, most of
reinforcements used in composites have a ﬁbrous form because mate-
rials in the ﬁbrous form, rather than in any other form, are stronger
and stiffer [9], conferring to the composite higher values of strength
and stiffness. In addition to long ﬁbers, it is also possible to have re-
inforcements in the form of particles, ﬂakes, whiskers, short ﬁbers,
continuous ﬁbers, or sheets [9]. On the contrary, the matrix maintains
the ﬁbers aligned and protects them from probable accidents.
The type of matrix determines the type of composites. Indeed, we
have: polymer matrix composites (PMCs), metal matrix composites
(MMCs), and ceramic matrix composites (CMCs). The polymer ma-
trix composites use synthetic resins as their matrix, the metal matrix
composites use metals as their matrix, and the ceramic matrix com-
posites use ceramics as their matrix. Several manufacturing methods,
depending on the type of composite, can be processed.

2.1.2
Carbon Fibers
The carbon element has a density of about 2.268 g/cm3 and a multi-
plicity of crystalline forms. In the graphitic form it shows an empha-
sized anisotropic behavior. Its theoretical Young’s modulus, which
depends on the bond strength, goes from about 1,000 GPa along the
layer plane to about 35 GPa along the c-axis [9], as shown in Figure 3.
Figure 3: (a) Graphitic layer structure. (b) The hexagonal lattice structure of
graphite [9].
Carbon ﬁbers are produced from organic precursor ﬁbers and poly-
acrylonitrile (PAN) represents the most common one. The majority
of carbon ﬁbers are made through a series of steps consisting of: a
ﬁberization procedure to produce the precursor ﬁbers, a stabilization
treatment to avoid that ﬁbers melt in the following thermal treatment,
==PAGE:31==
2.1 fiber reinforced composite materials
9
a carbonization treatment to take out all the non-carbon elements,
and a graphitization treatment to improve the mechanical properties.
Depending on the type of the organic precursor ﬁbers, it is possible
to produce: ex-PAN carbon ﬁbers, ex-cellulose carbon ﬁbers, and ex-
pitch carbon ﬁbers, where the preﬁx "ex-" stands for "coming from".
Figure 4 represents a schematic of PAN-based carbon ﬁber produc-
tion. The PAN ﬁbers are ﬁrstly produced through the ﬁberization
procedure consisting of wet-, dry-, or melt-spinning followed by some
drawing or stretching [9]. Successively, in the stabilization treatment,
the PAN ﬁbers are stabilized in air for few hours at a temperature
of about 250 °C; during this oxidation treatment the ﬁbers become
black and the applied tension prevents them from contracting [9]. It
follows another thermal treatment called the carbonization treatment
in which the black ﬁbers are heated up slowly to a temperature of
about 1,000 - 1,500 °C. Because of the slow heating the molecular
order in the ﬁbers does not change even though the ﬁbers are los-
ing non-carbon elements. However, during this treatment the ﬁbers
lose weight and see a decreasing in diameter dimensions. The ﬁnal
treatment, which is carried out at a temperature of about 3,000 °C
for a very short period of time, is called the graphitization treat-
ment and it heavily affects the mechanical properties of the resultant
carbon ﬁbers; the Young’s modulus increases with the increasing of
the graphitization temperature since it changes the orientation of the
graphitic crystals making them with a higher degree of order [9].
Figure 4: Representation of all the steps necessary to produce PAN-based
carbon ﬁbers [9].
In the case of ex-cellulose carbon ﬁbers and ex-pitch carbon ﬁbers, the
cellulose, or sometimes a thermosetting polymer called Rayon, and
the pitch, respectively, represent the ﬁbers to be carbonized. Note-
worthy is the fact that, during the production of carbon ﬁbers the
formation of some defects in the ﬁbers can occur. The defects are
intended as misoriented crystallites and represent a weak point that

The polymerization process consists of joining small molecules, called
monomers, in order to form large molecules, called polymers. When
monomers join producing a molecule of a simple compound as by-
product, then the process is called condensation polymerization; when
they do not produce any by-product, then the process is called addi-
tion polymerization [9]. Depending on their behavior, polymers can
be classiﬁed into two main categories: thermosetting and thermoplas-
tic polymers. Thermosetting polymers are like concrete. When their
polymeric chains, through a curing process, develop into cross-linked
chains they become set in their shape [14]. On the contrary, when
thermoplastic polymers are heated up they soften and become plas-
tic, when they are cooled down they hardens [14]. The glass transition
temperature Tg is one of the most important properties for polymers,
as well as for every other amorphous material.
Figure 6 describes the behavior of an amorphous and a semicrys-
talline polymer in terms of speciﬁc volume (volume/unit mass) and
temperature. When the temperature decreases, the polymer contracts.
This happens because the thermal energy of the molecules decreases
(e.g. they vibrate less). Tm corresponds to the melting point. The amor-
phous polymer does not have any ﬁxed melting point; on the con-
trary, the semicrystalline polymer, which consists of a crystalline and
an amorphous phase, posses it. In particular, the crystalline phase
undergoes a thermodynamic phase transformation thanks to which
crystals form [9]. The polymer continues to contract until it reaches
the glass transition temperature where it becomes a supercooled liq-
uid polymer. Supercooled liquids are deﬁned as "liquids that, when
cooled, apparently solidify but actually continue to ﬂow very slowly
under the inﬂuence of gravity" [15]. Therefore, because of its high vis-
cosity, the polymer is quite rigid, solidlike and highly disordered.
In Figure 7 it is described the behavior of an amorphous polymer
in an elastic modulus vs. temperature graph. Below Tg the polymer
is hard. Above Tg the polymer becomes like rubber, indeed the elas-
tic modulus drops importantly. Above Tm the elastic modulus drops
sharply. The polymer is processed above the melting point [9].
As already said, polymers can be either amorphous or semicrystalline.
Generally speaking, the amount of the crystalline phase determines
the degree of crystallinity; in a polymer it varies from 30 to 90 %.
The mechanical properties of a polymer are strictly dependent on the
degree of crystallinity. Indeed, the higher the degree of crystallinity
is, the higher the stiffness and the strength of the polymer are [9]. It is
worth saying that, in order to understand the behavior of a material
is very important to know where the glass transition temperature

2.2
manufacturing of thermoset matrix composites
Thermoset matrix composites can be manufactured in several ways.
Here, the most widely used manufacturing methods are described as
follows.
In hand layup and spray techniques, ﬁbers, by hand, are placed onto
a mold and resin is, afterwards, sprayed on it [9]. Successively, the
deposited layers are made more compact through the use of a roller.
Curing at room or moderate temperature follows. In ﬁlament winding,
a continuous roving or tow is, ﬁrstly, immersed into a resin impreg-
nation bath and, secondly, wound over a rotating or ﬁxed mandrel
[17, 18]. It is, successively, cured at elevated temperatures. Filament
winding can be either wet or prepreg. In wet ﬁlament winding ﬁbers
are immersed into a low-viscosity resin; in prepreg ﬁlament wind-
ing ﬁbers are preimpregnate by means of a hot-melt process [17, 18].
Pultrusion is a continuous molding process in which ﬁbers are, si-
multaneously, passed through a resin impregnation bath and pulled
through a heated die [19]. The latter gives the shape of the part to
be produce. In resin transfer molding (RTM), ﬁbers are placed into a
mold, and resin is injected through a pump [9]. RTM is classiﬁed
as a closed-mold and low-pressure process. In order to have a solid
composite, resin is afterwards cured. In vacuum infusion process (VIP),
resin is driven into the enclosed cavity because of a difference in pres-
sure between the inlet and the outlet. Reinforcements are laid dry into
the enclosed cavity, which is connected to an inlet and to an outlet.
Once the enclosed cavity is completely under vacuum the infusion

can start [20]. The two following methods are completely automated
and allow to manufacture rather large structures. The ﬁrst process is
called automated tape placement (ATP). Fibers are preimpregnated with
resin in the form of a tape, which is then placed in order to get the
desired shape [9]. This process is very common in the aerospace in-
dustry. The second process is called automated ﬁber placement (AFP).
This time, ﬁber tows preimpregnated with resin are placed onto a

prepreg ﬁbers are subjected to the application of high temperature
and pressure [21]. Thus, the laminate consolidates and the entrapped
air is easily removed.
In the present study, the ﬁlament winding and the vacuum infusion
are used. The two processes will be described more in detail later on.

2.5
hybrid composites
Hybrid composites contain more than one type of reinforcement into
a single matrix. The use of hybrid composites allows to balance the
disadvantages of one type of reinforcement with the advantages of
the other one, and conversely. Hybrid composites are categorized as:
tow-by-tow, in which "the different types of ﬁbers are mixed in a reg-
ular or random manner"; sandwich hybrids, in which "one material is
sandwiched between two layers of another"; laminated, in which "al-
ternate layers of the reinforcements are stacked in a regular manner";
intimately mixed hybrids, in which "the constituent ﬁbers are mixed
as randomly as possible" [25].

Infusion
Once the wound plate has been placed into its bagging, the infusion
process can start. Infusion is meant to draw the air out of the bagging
system and to impregnate ﬁbers with resin. If some air is entrapped
into the laminate, it will, indeed, turn into porosity.
The resin, before impregnating the ﬁbers, is sucked by the bagging
system under vacuum. Noteworthy is the fact that, it does not travel
through the ﬁbers, but through the infusion aid materials. As a mat-

secondly, addressed to the ﬁbers, passing trough the perforated re-

3.3
fiber content and porosity analysis
The ﬁber content and porosity analysis aims at determining the global
volumetric composition of hybrid carbon/glass ﬁber composites (see
Subsection 2.5.1). It is based on weight and temperature measure-
ments and knowledge of composite constituents densities [29] and
provide us with:
• Weight fraction of carbon ﬁbers, glass ﬁbers, and matrix (%).
• Density of composites (g/cm3).
• Volume fraction of carbon ﬁbers, glass ﬁbers, matrix, and poros-
ity (%).
The analysis consists of many steps. The most important ones are
described right away:
1. Cutting of samples. Per each analysis 5 samples of 10 mm x 20
mm x the thickness of the composite plate are cut out (see Fig-
ure 58) [29].
2. Annealing of crucibles. Crucibles are annealed overnight in a muf-
ﬂe furnace at 625 °C [29].
3. Weighing of samples. The samples are weighted on an analytical
balance (see Figure 62) [29].
4. Weighing of samples in water. The samples are weighted on an
analytical balance while they are immersed into water. Its tem-
perature is measured (see Figure 63) [29].
==PAGE:83==
3.3 fiber content and porosity analysis

5. Weighing of empty crucibles. The empty crucibles are weighted on
an analytical balance (see Figure 64) [29].
6. Burning off the matrix material. Each sample is placed in the cor-
responding crucible and put into a preheated mufﬂe furnace at
450 °C for 2 hours (see Figure 65a) [29].
7. Weighing of crucibles containing carbon and glass ﬁbers. The cru-

C O N C L U S I O N S
The concluding remarks with reference to this study are presented in
this second to last chapter.
The ﬁlament winding, the bagging, and the infusion were used to
manufacture the composite plates. In particular, the ﬁlament wind-
ing was utilized to wind glass rovings and carbon tows over an alu-
minum plate, the bagging was utilized to provide the wound plate
with a vacuum bag, some infusion aid materials, an inlet, and an out-
let, and the infusion was utilized to draw the air out of the bagging
system and to impregnate ﬁbers with resin. It is important to remem-
ber that the composite plates were designed in such a way that each
specimen differed from the others in terms of hybrid ﬁber weight
mixing ratio β.
The microscopy analysis was used to investigate the microstructure
of hybrid carbon/glass ﬁber composites. At a macroscopic level, the
microstructure presents: no evidence of big defects (also called macro-
scopic defects), a easily recognizable layered structure, and a non-
homogeneous thickness. At a microscopic level, the microstructure
presents: a few small defects (also called microscopic defects) repre-
sented by voids, unwetted ﬁbers, and particles of dust, a quite homo-
geneous distribution between ﬁbers and matrix, and a few layers of
ﬁbers separated by thin layers of matrix.
The ﬁber content and porosity analysis was used to determine the
volumetric composition of hybrid carbon/glass ﬁber composites. In
particular, the analysis provided us with densities, weight fractions,
and volume fractions of hybrid carbon/glass ﬁber composites. In ad-
dition to that, the weight fractions were used to calculate the hybrid
ﬁber weight mixing ratios corresponding to the various composite
plates.

F U T U R E W O R K
In order to conclude this study, a few recommendations for a future
work are provided:
• Strain gauges, instead of extensometers, may be used during
the tensile tests. As a matter of fact, strain gauges, remaining
on the samples for the whole duration of the tensile tests, allow
to acquire more accurate measurements.
• Not in this study, it has been observed that the mechanical per-
formances of the hybrid carbon/glass ﬁber composites are im-
proved when the tensile grips of the tensile testing machines
do not touch the layers of carbon ﬁbers. Therefore, new layered
structures, showing layers of glass ﬁbers on the outside, may be
taken into account.
• In order to better understand the behavior of the hybrid car-
bon/glass ﬁber composites in proximity to the threshold carbon
ﬁber composite volume fraction Vc carbon (T), other β values may
be used.
• The same study may be repeated with different kind of carbon
ﬁbers and glass ﬁbers. It may also be repeated with different
kind of ﬁbers, e.g. boron ﬁbers and organic ﬁbers.
