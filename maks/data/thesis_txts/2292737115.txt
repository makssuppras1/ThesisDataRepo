Abstract  
Protein misfolding diseases, with emphasis on neurodegenerative diseases, constitutes a major 
health problem worldwide. Some neurodegenerative diseases involve formation of the same type 
of disease-related aggregate known as amyloid fibrils. It is of interest to discover compounds that 
are able to selectively interfere with the formation of amyloid fibrils. This could have therapeutic 
potential with a future perspective of changing or altering the course of neurodegenerative 
diseases. The aim of this master’s thesis was to investigate the effect of different thiourea-
modified dendrimers with increasing hydrophobicity on the formation of amyloid fibrils. 
Additionally, the aim was to investigate if the thiourea-modified dendrimers had a denaturing 
effect on the conformational stability of natively folded proteins. The fibril perturbing effect was 
investigated in a Thioflavin T binding assay monitoring fibrillation of PrP106-126. It was found that 
methylthiourea- and ethylthiourea-modified dendrimers at 100 μg/mL were able to accelerate the 
formation of PrP106-126 fibrils. An IgG denaturation analysis was carried out using anti-PAGP mAb as 
a model for a natively folded protein. The thiourea-modfied dendrimers were incubated with anti-
PAGP mAb and analysed in a conformation-dependent immunoassay in an ELISA set-up. It was 
shown that the thiourea-modified dendrimers at 20 μg/mL and 100 μg/mL did not have a 
denaturing effect on anti-PAGP mAb.

Protein misfoldede sygdomme, med fokus på neurodegenerative sygdomme, udgør et stort 
sundhedsmæssigt problem i verden. Nogle protein misfoldede sygdomme er karakteriseret ved 
dannelse af den samme type sygdoms-relaterede aggregat bestående af amyloid fibriller. Det er af 
interesse af undersøge og identificere agenter der har en specifik effekt på dannelsen af amyloid 
fibriller. Dette kan have terapeutisk potentiale i forhold til at ændre forløbet og udviklingen af 
neurodegenerative sygdomme. Formålet med dette speciale var at undersøge effekten af 
forskellige thiourinstof-modificerede dendrimerer med stigende hydrofobicitet på protein og 
peptid fibrillering. Formålet var endvidere at undersøge, om thiourinstof-modificerede 
dendrimerer havde en denaturerende effekt på konformationen af nativt foldede proteiner. 
Effekten på fibrillering af PrP106-126 blev undersøgt i et Thioflavin T assay. Her blev det observeret at 
methylthiourea- og ethylthiourea-modificerede dendrimerer ved 100 μg/mL var i stand til at 
fremskynde fibrilleringsprocessen af PrP106-126. Derefter blev dendrimerernes effekt på nativt

protein for et nativt foldet protein. De thiourinstof-modificerede dendrimerer blev inkuberet med 
anti-PAGP mAb og analyseret i et konformationsafhængigt immunoassay i form af en ELISA. Der 
blev ikke observeret nogen denaturerende effekt på anti-PAGP mAb af de thiourea-modificerede 
dendrimerer ved hverken 20 μg/ mL eller 100 μg/mL.

1 INTRODUCTION 
To date, more than 40 different diseases have been recognized to be caused by misfolding of 
proteins and peptides. Some protein misfolding diseases are rare, whereas others are more well-
known, such as Alzheimer’s Disease and Parkinson’s Disease [1] [2] [3]. Protein misfolding diseases 
are caused by accumulation of misfolded proteins or peptides assembling into disease-related 
aggregates, however, the factors initiating this process are currently not fully understood. Disease-
related aggregates are often insoluble, highly toxic and accumulate in tissues and organs [4].  
1.1 NEURODEGENERATIVE DISEASES 
Neurodegenerative diseases constitute a substantial part of protein misfolding diseases and are 
characterized as progressive disorders - often with a late onset- deteriorating the brain and central 
nervous system [5]. Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) is the most prevalent neurodegenerative disease [6]. 
The cause of AD is believed to be associated with cleavage of the Amyloid Precursor Protein (APP) 
in the brain into misfolded amyloid-β (Aβ) peptide fragments [7]. One of the main symptoms in 
patients suffering from AD is dementia which affects thinking, memory and cognitive behaviour [5] 
[8]. According to the World Alzheimer Report from 2014, around 44 million suffer from dementia 
worldwide and the number is anticipated to triple by 2050 [5].  Consequently, the total cost of 
dementia treatment worldwide is predicted to increase from US$604 billion in 2010 to US$1,117 
billion in 2030 [9].  
Parkinson’s Disease (PD) is the second most prevalent neurodegenerative disease [6]. One of the 
main symptoms of PD involve movement deficiency, such as slowing of movements, tremor and 
rigidity [10]. The development of PD is linked with degeneration of neurons in multiple parts of the 
brain [6] [11].. The cause of the degeneration is often unknown but is generally related to the 
aggregation and accumulation of misfolded α-Synuclein (αSyn): a small presynaptic protein 
expressed in neurons [12] [13].   
Rarer neurodegenerative diseases are also known. Creutzfeldt-Jakob Disease (CJD) is a fatal and 
rare disease affecting one in every million people each year worldwide [14].  Symptoms include 
dementia, movement deficiency and visual disturbances [14]. The progression of the disease is 
fast and it is estimated that around 90% of people diagnosed with CJD die within a year [14]. The 
cause of CJD is related to misfolding of the prion protein into a disease-related conformation. CJD 
belong to a group of diseases called Transmissible Spongiform Encephalopathies (TSEs), which can 
be acquired by transmission of the disease-related conformation of the prion protein [15]. A form 
of TSEs in cattle is known as Bovine Spongiform Encephalopathies (BSEs) or Mad Cow Disease. In 
1996, it was suspected that variant CJD, a human prion disease, was caused by consumption of 
meat from cattle ‘infected’ with Mad Cow Disease [16]. An outbreak of Mad Cow Diseases was 
seen in Europe during the 1980s and 1990s and has later been suspected to be associated with 
numerous cases of variant CJD in humans [15] [16].

1.1.1 AMYLOID AGGREGATES 
Some protein misfolding diseases, in particular neurodegenerative diseases, involve formation of 
the same type of aggregates [5]. This type of aggregates is known as amyloid or amyloid fibrils and 
has characteristics that include dye specific binding properties and a highly ordered β-sheet rich 
conformation [17]. Not all protein misfolding diseases are associated with the formation of 
amyloid aggregates. Other types of ordered and disordered aggregates are associated with disease 
as well [18] [19]. A simplified illustration of different proteins and peptides misfolding into the 
amyloid structure is seen in Figure 1. 
 
 
Figure 1. Misfolding into the amyloid structure is illustrated for prion protein, αSyn and Aβ1-28 associated with CJD and 
BSE, PD and AD, respectively. Figure adapted from [20]. 
Neurodegenerative diseases will continue to constitute a substantial health problem worldwide 
taking into consideration the general increase in life expectancy [8] [9]. It is of high interest to 
discover compounds that are able to interact with amyloid fibrils, as this could potentially change 
or alter the course of neurodegenerative diseases [21] [22]. Common therapeutic strategies 
against amyloid diseases have been investigated in Module 2 – Protein and peptide fibrillation: 
effect of dendrimers [23]. The strategies investigated included, but are not limited to, β-sheet 
breaking peptides, antibodies and small molecules, with emphasis on a specific class of molecules 
called dendrimers. It is of interest to further investigate dendrimer-fibril interactions, as this could 
potentially open up for rational drug design of e.g. fibril solubilizing compounds.

1.2 DENDRIMER-FIBRIL INTERACTIONS 
Dendrimer-fibril interactions are of importance in this master’s thesis. It was previously shown by 
Heegaard et al. [24], that highly charged cationic guanidino surface groups on dendrimers could 
decrease fibril formation of the synthetic prion protein fragment PrP106-126, which was used as a 
model peptide of the full length prion protein [24]. Guanidinium hydrochloride (GuHCl) is 
commonly used as a control for protein denaturation [25]. The effect of the guanidino dendrimer 
was shown to be similar to that of GuHCl, emphasizing the importance of cationic surface groups 
in relation to fibril perturbing effect [24]. However, other types of dendrimers have shown to have 
a fibril enhancing effect on PrP106-126 fibrillation as shown in Figure 2 [24].  
 
Figure 2. Results from a study by Heegaard et. al, where the effect of dendrimers on the formation of PrP106-126 
amyloid fibrils were investigated in a Thioflavin T binding assay. Dendrimer 1: Underivatized dendrimer with amino 
surface groups. Dendrimer 2: cationic guanidino surface groups [24].  
Similar fibril perturbing effects was observed by Klajnert et. al where it was shown that 
dendrimers with cationic surface groups could decrease fibril formation of PrP185-208 [26]. Similarly, 
another study by Klajnert et al. found that cationic dendrimers could perturb already formed 
PrP185-208 fibrils [27]. The study also suggested that higher generations and concentrations of 
dendrimers led to higher potency in terms of fibril perturbing effect [27]. These studies emphasize 
the importance of cationic surface groups in dendrimer-fibril interactions. However, cationic 
surface charges are considered toxic due to interactions with negatively charged membranes in 
vivo [28]. Consequently, research has moved towards design and synthesis of more biocompatible 
dendrimers such as dendrimers with urea- and thiourea-modified surface groups.

1.3 PROJECT AIM 
The aim of this master’s thesis is to investigate the in vitro effect of different thiourea-modified 
dendrimers with increasing hydrophobicity on the formation of PrP106-126 amyloid fibrils. 
Furthermore, the aim is also to investigate if the thiourea-modified dendrimers affect the 
conformational stability of natively folded proteins. 
 The hypothesis of the master’s thesis is: 
“Dendrimers may perturb amyloid fibril structure, however they do not affect the conformation of 
natively folded proteins”

2 THEORETICAL BACKGROUND  
2.1 PROTEIN FOLDING AND MISFOLDING  
Proteins exert essential functions in living organisms [29]. The biological functions of proteins are 
dependent on their three-dimensional conformation; consequently correct protein folding is of 
high importance [29]. Correct folding of proteins and elimination or refolding of misfolded 
proteins is managed by a Protein Quality Control (PQC) system. A specific class of enzymes in the 
PQC system known as chaperones are of particular importance in facilitating and managing correct 
protein folding [30]. The driving force of protein folding can be explained by a ‘funnel energy 
landscape theory’. According to this theory, the most attractive conformation of proteins result in 
a lower level of free energy [30]. When proteins are in a folding intermediate, the most attractive 
conformation is usually the native conformation. However, folding intermediates can also take a 
different path leading to a misfolded conformation that is also of minimal free energy [29] [30]. A 
simplified illustration of the funnel energy landscape theory is depicted in Figure 3. 
 
Figure 3. Illustration of the funnel energy landscape theory. Proteins at the top are in an unfolded intermediate state 
and seek towards a conformation of low free energy.  Two pathways can be taken, leading to either the native state 
conformation or a misfolded conformation. The misfolded conformation can eventually lead to amyloid aggregates 
[30].

2.2 AMYLOID FIBRILS 
The word ‘amyloid’ is a derivation of the Latin word ‘amylum’ and the Greek word ‘amylon’, both 
meaning ‘starch-like’ [31]. The word was introduced in the middle of the 17th century by the 
German scientist Virchow when performing a staining technique used for identification of starch in 
tissue from a patient that had suffered from an unknown brain disease. The discovery of a positive 
staining reaction in a large tissue abnormality led to the assumption that it was starch-like [31]. In 
the 1920s, it was discovered that the chemical dye Congo Red, at the time commonly used for 
staining purposes in the textile industry, was able to bind to amyloid thus resulting in a bright 
green colour when viewed under polarized light [32]. In the 1950s, the dyes Thioflavin T and 
Thioflavin S were found to selectively bind amyloid in histological staining [33]. Additional 
investigations in the 1950s and 1960s, including electron microscopic studies and X-ray diffraction 
techniques, led to further insight into the structure and properties of amyloid [34] [35]. These 
studies revealed that amyloid was composed long and unbranched fibrils with thread-like 
morphology [36]. Furthermore it was discovered that amyloid fibrils were not made up from 
starch but from proteins and peptides with a high amount of β-sheet content [37]. The β-sheet 
content was found to be arranged in a cross-β-structure, meaning that multiple β-sheets were 
stacked on top of each other in a direction perpendicular to the length of the fibril axis [38]. 
Additionally, it was discovered that the amyloid fibril was made up from units called 
protofilaments. In Figure 4 is shown an amyloid fibril of insulin.  
 
Figure 4. Cryo-Electron Microscopic image of an insulin amyloid fibril [36]. The amyloid fibril is made up of four 
protofilaments that twist around each other. Each protofilament is represented in a different colour and is made up 
from the characteristic β-sheets that are stacked on top of each other in a direction perpendicular to the fibril length, 
also known as a cross-β-structure [36].

The β-sheets within a protofilament are stabilised by hydrogen bonding between adjacent 
strands, leading to parallel or anti-parallel conformations. The β-sheets between two 
protofilaments are stabilised by e.g. Van der Waals forces, hydrophobic effects and π-effects [37]. 
In relation to this, studies have shown that stacking of the aromatic groups in Phenylalanine 
residues in aβ fragments is important for retaining amyloid structure [37] [39]. 
2.2.1 FUNCTIONAL AMYLOID 
The ability to adopt the amyloid structure is independent of the amino acid sequence of proteins 
and peptides [36] [40]. Furthermore, the amyloid structure is not always associated with disease. 
The term ‘functional amyloid’ refers non-disease-related amyloid structures [38]. Examples of 
functional amyloid include bacteria that take advantage of the mechanical strength of amyloid 
fibrils in relation to surface adhesion properties [41]. In humans, it has been shown that fibrin, the 
main component of blood clot, is able to bind Congo red under certain conditions and has a high 
β-sheet content [41]. Additionally peptide fragments of fibrin are able to form amyloid fibrils in 
vitro [41].  
2.3 PROTEIN AND PEPTIDE FIBRILLATION 
Amyloid fibrils can reach up to 10 nm in diameter and can be of indeterminate lengths often 
ranging between 0.1 to 10 μm [40]. As illustrated in Figure 4, amyloid fibrils are made up of units 
called protofilaments. Protofilaments can reach up to 2-5 nm in diameter and around 2-6 
protofilaments usually assemble to form an amyloid fibril [2] [42] [43]. A simplified illustration of 
the assembly of protofilaments into amyloid fibrils is shown in Figure 5. 
 
   
Figure 5. Simplified illustration of the assembly of protofilaments combining and twisting around each other to form 
aggregates of amyloid fibrils. 
Protofilaments are made up of smaller prefibrillar species and oligomers. The process leading to 
the formation of amyloid fibrils is referred to as protein and peptide fibrillation. The fibrillation 
process is more complex than illustrated in Figure 5 and will be introduced in more detail in 2.3.1.

2.3.1 NUCLEATION-POLYMERIZATION AS A MODEL FOR PROTEIN AND PEPTIDE FIBRILLATION  
One model explaining the process of protein and peptide fibrillation is known as the ‘Nucleation-
polymerization model’. The kinetics of the Nucleation-polymerization model leading to the 
formation of amyloid fibrils is characterized by a sigmoidal-shaped curve as shown in Figure 6. 
 
Figure 6. Nucleation-polymerization model of protein and peptide fibrillation [44]. An initial lag phase is observed 
where misfolded protein or peptide monomers (M) begin to assemble. This phase is known as the nucleation phase. 
The assembly of protein and peptide monomers (M) lead to the formation of dimers and oligomers (O), also known as 
prefibrillar species. Then follows the elongation phase, where protofilaments (PF) are formed and assemble into 
amyloid fibrils (F) [44].  
The S-shaped curve consist of an initial lag phase where misfolded protein or peptide monomers 
(M) combine and begin to assemble. This phase is known as the nucleation phase. The assembly of 
protein and peptide monomers (M) leads to the formation of dimers and oligomer (O) and other 
prefibrillar species. After the nucleation phase follows the elongation phase where protofilaments 
(PF) are formed. Finally, protofilaments assemble to form amyloid fibrils (F) [44]. A way of 
reducing the initial lag phase and speed up the fibrillation process is to perform seeded 
aggregation [45]. Seeded aggregation is carried out by the addition of preformed fibrils to the 
protein and peptide monomers [45]. Other ways of reducing the lag phase is to add glass beads to 
the wells [46]. 
The nucleation-polymerization process is highly sensitive and thereby easily affected by factors 
such as solvent, ionic strength, salt, pH, temperature, denaturants, detergents, and agitation [45]. 
Furthermore, different proteins and peptides may require different conditions in order to adopt a 
misfolded state and initiate the fibrillation process [47].

2.4 PRION PROTEIN  
Prion protein (PrP) is a protein expressed on membranes, in particular on neuronal cells, in both 
humans and animals [24]. The protein is natively unfolded from residues 1-120 and has an α-
helical conformation from residues 121-230 [48]. PrP can exist in two isoforms with distinct 
conformations and properties. Consequently, different nomenclature is used to distinguish these

2.5 DENDRIMERS 
Dendrimers are well-defined monodisperse synthetic polymers with distinct structures and 
properties that will be introduced in the following. Dendrimers first appeared in the 1970s under 
the name ‘cascade molecules’ and further developed in the 1980s and 1990s [19]. Today, the word 
‘dendrimer’ is the most commonly used [19]. The word is a contraction of the Greek word 
‘dendron’ and ‘meros’ meaning ’tree’ and ‘part’, respectively [54]. This word represents the highly 
branched structure of dendrimers resembling branches on a tree, which will be further explained 
in 2.5.1. 
2.5.1 STRUCTURE AND PROPERTIES OF DENDRIMERS  
Dendrimers are made up from a central core to which repeating synthetic layers are peripherally 
attached [55]. The core can be bifunctional, meaning that there are two reactive sites from where 
branching can occur. After addition of the first layer to the core, the dendrimer is said to be of a 
specific generation. For polypropyleneimine (PPI) dendrimers, the addition of the first layer to the 
core leads to the first generation, also denoted G-1, with 4 end-groups on the surface [55] (Figure 
8). These end-groups can serve as branching points for further growth of the structure. Addition of 
the next layer leads to 8 end-groups and an increase in generation number to G-2 (Figure 8). Each 
layer can be viewed as a shell. The attachment of an additional shell leads to a doubling in end-
groups on the surface and an increase in generation number as shown in Figure 8 [55]. 
 
Figure 8. Illustration of polypropyleneimine (PPI) and polyamidoamine (PAMAM) dendrimers of different generations. 
An increase in generation number is illustrated by a decrease in colour intensity of the shells going from the centre to 
the periphery. Figure  adapted from [55]. 
It is noticed that for PPI dendrimers, G-1 has 4 end-groups, G-2 has 8 end-groups and so forth. For 
PAMAM dendrimers, G-1 has 8 end-groups, G-2 has 16 end-groups and so forth. 
Going from the central core to the periphery leads to highly symmetrical and globular structures 
that are compartmentalized into an exterior and an interior. The exterior of a dendrimer is 
composed of the multiple end-groups located on the surface, thus constituting the outer shell. The 
interior of a dendrimer is the space beneath the outer shell. Increasing the numbers of shells, and

as polypropyleneimine (PPI) dendrimer and was introduced by Vögtle et. al in 1978 [59]. Both

2.5.3 IN VIVO EFFECT OF DENDRIMERS ON AMYLOID FIBRILS  
The first study investigating the effect of dendrimers on amyloid fibrils were in carried out by 
Supattapone et. al in 1999. This study showed that PAMAM dendrimers of G-0 to G-4 and PPI 
dendrimers of G-2 to G-4, were able to clear PrPSc aggregates in a neuroblastoma ScN2a cell line, 
with the highest concentrations and generations showing highest potency [60]. Furthermore, G-4 
PAMAM was able to completely clear the cell line of PrPSc aggregates at low concentrations of 1.5 
μg/mL [60]. A study by Supattapone et. al from 2001 showed that PAMAM and PPI dendrimers 
were able to completely inhibit and transmission of PrPSc [61]. A study by Solassol et. al in 2004 
showed that G-3 to G-5 phosphorous dendrimers with cationic end-groups on the surface were 
able to clear PrPSc aggregates in the neruoblastoma ScN2a cell line [47]. Furthermore, in 2005, 
Yudovin et. al showed that glycodendrimers, composed of polysaccharide units coupled to cationic 
end-groups, showed similar anti-prion effect on clearing PrPSc aggregates in the same cell line [62]. 
A study by study by Eaglestone et. al in 1999 investigated the importance of charged cationic 
groups on fibril interactions. This study showed that millimolar concentrations of GuHCl was able 
to revert prion positive phenotypic cells into prion negative phenotypic cells, thus suggesting an 
anti-prion effect of the charged cationic groups[63].  
Several studies emphasize the importance of cationic groups on dendrimer-fibril interactions. 
However, cationic surface charges are considered toxic due to interactions with negatively 
charged membranes in vivo [28]. Consequently, research has moved towards design and synthesis 
of more biocompatible dendrimers such as glycodendrimers where the surface groups are 
modified with oligosaccharide units [64] [65] [44]. Other types of biocompatible dendrimers 
include urea- and thiourea-modified surface groups.  
2.5.3.1 In vivo effect of urea- and thiourea-modified dendrimers  
The effect of urea-modified dendrimers on PrPSc in a mouse brain cell line have been investigated 
by Cordes et. al in 2007 [66]. It was shown that the PrPSc clearing effect was dependent on both 
the generation of dendrimers and the surface groups. Dendrimers of higher generations were 
most efficient in clearing PrPSc aggregates, with amino and guanidino surface groups being most 
potent from G-3 to G-5. For the uncharged urea surface groups, only G-5 was able to clear PrPSc 
aggregates, showing less efficiency [66]. Additionally, it was recently shown in 2014 by Laumann 
et. al that urea- and methylthiourea-modified dendrimers are able to clear intracellular aggregates 
of αSyn fibrils in a human cell line [67].

2.7.2 INFRARED SPECTROSCOPY  
Almost any compound have the ability to absorb energy within the infrared region of the 
electromagnetic spectrum. The mid-infrared region extends from 400-4000 cm-1 [73]. IR 
spectroscopy can therefore be viewed as a fingerprint of a compound [73].  The absorbance of 
infrared light is dependent on the vibrations of the functional groups present in a compound [74]. 
Factors that affect these vibrations include the type of vibration, the strength of the bonds and the 
mass of the atoms. Common types of vibrations include bending, denoted δ, and symmetrical and 
asymmetrical stretching, denoted νS and νAS, respectively [73] [75]. Usually, vibrations that require 
a lot of energy will absorb infrared light at higher wavenumbers. In relation to this, stronger bonds 
and heavier atoms will absorb infrared light at higher wavenumbers [75]. This makes infrared (IR) 
spectroscopy a suitable technique for studying changes in molecular vibrations [73].  
2.7.2.1 Attenuated Total Reflectance FTIR  
Attenuated Total Reflectance (ATR) is a sampling technique that can be used in FTIR Spectroscopy. 
The main advantage of ATR is that samples can be analysed without prior treatment. This means 
that liquid and solid samples can be analysed directly in their respective states [76].  
The output from IR spectroscopy is a spectrum, where the horizontal axis expresses the 
frequencies at which infrared light is absorbed [73]. A common used expression for frequency in IR 
spectroscopy is wavenumber, which is expressed as the reciprocal of the wavelength in 
centimetres (cm-1) [73]. On the vertical axis of the spectrum is indicated the percentage of infrared 
light absorbed by the compound expressed as % Transmittance. The principle of FTIR spectroscopy 
is illustrated in Figure 12, with a protein or peptide serving as an example of a compound.

also referred to as the amide I band region (marked in red).

2.7.2.2 Amide I band region and secondary structure elements in proteins and peptides 
Amide bonds constitute repetitive structural units in proteins and peptides and are known to 
contribute with stretching and bending vibrations within different regions of the infrared 
spectrum [77]. These vibrations are referred to as amide bands and 9 amide bands has been 
identified, with the most well-studied being the amide I band and amide II band [77].  
The vibrations of the amide I band are located at 1600–1700 cm-1. This region is consequently 
known as the amide I band region. Vibrations within this region derives mainly from stretching of 
the C=O group, but are also coupled to bending of N-H and stretching of C-N [78].  
The stretching and bending vibrations of the amide I band are correlated to the conformation of 
protein and peptides [78]. Studies have investigated the correlation between secondary structure 
elements in proteins and peptides and the localization of the amide I band [79] [80]. This was 
investigated by examining the localization of the amide I band of various synthetic polypeptides in 
different conformations to which empirical correlations and mathematical operations were 
applied [79] [80] . The correlation between the localization of the amide I band and secondary 
structure elements in proteins and peptides are presented in Table 1.

2.7.2.3 Amide I band region of amyloid fibrils 
In the 1970s, FTIR Spectroscopic studies emerged investigating the amide I band region of amyloid

literature survey study examined spectral differences of the localization of the amide I band in 
amyloid fibrils compared to the localization in natively folded proteins with a high content of β-
sheets [85]. From this study, vibrations in the amide I band region of amyloid fibrils were shown to 
cluster between 1615-1630 cm-1. However, for natively folded proteins with high content of β-
sheets, the vibrations were shown to cluster between 1630-1643 cm-1 [85].

2.7.2.4 Amide II band  
The vibrations of the amide II band give rise to absorbance around 1550 cm-1 and this region is 
consequently referred to as the amide II band region [81]. Vibrations within this region derive 
mainly from bending of N-H and stretching of C-N.  
2.7.2.5 Amide II band and hydrogen-deuterium exchange 
Hydrogens bonded to C and N in proteins and peptides are able to exchange with hydrogens from 
solvents [86] [87].  The hydrogens involved in this exchange can be those in the amide bonds and 
those associated with the polar groups on the side chains of proteins and peptides [86] [87]. 
Deuterium in D2O is able to exchange with hydrogen in e.g. proteins and peptides. Deuterium is a 
stable isotope of hydrogen with a molar mass double to that of hydrogen [88]. The rate of 
hydrogen-deuterium exchange is dependent on factors such including pH, salt, solvent and 
temperature [87]. A simplified illustration of the reaction scheme of hydrogen-deuterium

2.7.3.1 Protein denaturants  
Denaturation refers to the unfolding of proteins upon which the three-dimensional structure is 
abrupted. Denaturation can be induced by e.g. temperature, pH, salt, solvent and chemical 
denaturants. The most common chemical denaturants include urea and GuHCl. Usually, strong 
concentrations of around 8M of urea and GuHCl are used to effectively denature proteins [90]. 
The denaturing effect of urea and GuHCl is not fully understood but is suggested to occur through 
interactions water and with the protein [25] [56]. Urea and GuHCl are referred to as chaotropes 
due to their interference with the hydrogen properties of water which reduces entropy and order 
[56]. 
Other protein denaturants include non-ionic and ionic detergents. An example of an anionic 
detergent is Sodium dodecyl sulphate (SDS), which denature protein by weakening non-covalent 
interaction [90].

4.3.1.1 Deuteration of PrP106-126 with DCl 
In order to exchange TFA with HCl, a 5mM HCl solution was prepared by diluting 4M HCl in D2O. It 
was decided to use a HCl solution prepared from D2O, as it has been reported that fibrils formed in 
D2O results in higher quality spectra than those formed in H2O [93]. Lyophilization of PrP106-126 was 
carried out in a round-bottomed flask where 9.7 mg PrP106-126 was dissolved in 10 mL DCl (5mM). 
The solution was freeze-dried by swirling the round-bottomed flask gently in an acetone bath with 
dry ice. The flask was then kept on vacuum pump overnight. This was repeated four times. Next, 
PrP106.-126 was dissolved in 200 μL D2O, and freeze-dried and kept on vacuum pump overnight. 
PrP106-126 was kept at -80° until further use. This sample will from now on be referred to as PrP106-
126(Deuterated).

4.5 FTIR SPECTROSCOPY  
FTIR spectroscopy was performed using a MIRacle 10 single reflection ATR accessory from 
Shimadzu with a scanning range between 600–3600 cm-1. Initially, the ATR crystal was cleaned 
with MilliQ-water followed by EtOH and acetone until complete dryness. Then a background scan 
was carried out by pushing down the high-pressure clamp onto the ATR crystal, ensuring good 
contact. 
 
The ATR FTIR spectroscopy allowed acquirement of spectra of samples in both liquid and solid 
states:  
 
- 
Liquid samples were applied onto the ATR crystal in a total volume of 3 μL. 
 
- 
Solid samples were applied onto the ATR crystal ensuring that the entire area of ATR 
crystal was covered. 
 
- 
Solid samples that had been dissolved in PBS followed by freeze-drying were added in a 
small amount to the ATR crystal and suspended in 3 μL D2O.  
 
After addition of a sample to the ATR crystal, the high-pressure clamp was pushed down and a 
spectrum was obtained. This procedure is referred to as ‘FTIR Spectroscopy Protocol’ and is found 
in the Appendix section 10.2.

4.6.1 ENZYME-LINKED IMMUNOSORBENT ASSAY (ELISA) 
Anti-PAGP mAb 1.62 (2 mg/mL) was diluted (1:1000) in coating buffer (sodium carbonate 0.1M, pH 
9.6) to a concentration of 2 μg/mL. Next, 100 μL was added to each well on a 96-well Maxisorp 
NUNC plate. The plate was covered with sealing tape at kept at 4°C overnight. The plate was 
washed 4 times with washing buffer (250 mL PBS with 6 μL 20% Tween 20). Next, 200 μL blocking 
buffer (2.5 g Bovine Serum Albumin and 625 μL 20% Tween 20 in 250 mL PBS) was added to each 
well and the plate was incubated at room temperature on shake (200 rpm) for 30 minutes. The 
plate was washed 4 times in washing buffer and Biotin-PAGP (1.58 mg/mL) was diluted (1:1580) in 
blocking buffer to a concentration of 1 μg/mL. Two-fold vertical dilution series of Biotin-PAGP in 
blocking buffer starting from 1 μg/mL was carried out. The plate was covered with sealing tape 
and incubated at room temperature on shake (200 rpm) for 1 hour. The plate was washed and 
Streptavidin-HRP (DAKO P397) was diluted (1:2000) in blocking buffer and 100 μL was added to 
each well. The plate was covered with sealing tape and incubated at room temperature on shake 
(200 rpm) for 1 hour. Next, the plate was washed followed by the addition of 100 μL TMB-plus to 
each well. The plate was kept in dark during the colour development reaction where a blue colour 
was observed. The reaction was stopped after 2 minutes and 20 seconds by addition of 100 μL 
0.5M sulphuric acid to each well resulting in a bright yellow colour. The OD of each well was read 
using an automatic plate reader (Thermo Multiscan Ex spectrophotometer, Thermo Scientific). The 
OD was read at 450 nm with subtraction of unspecific coloration at 650 nm.

6 DISCUSSION 
 
6.1 INITIAL SET-UP OF THIOFLAVIN T BINDING ASSAY 
Various factors are known to influence protein and peptide fibrillation and interfere with the 
reproducibility of ThT binding assays. These factors include, but are not limited to, pH, 
temperature, solvent, salt, agitation mode and agitation speed [45] [47] [46]. A study by Giehm & 
Otzen investigated the reproducibility of αSyn fibrillation in plate readers [46]. This study showed 
that the fibrillation process is highly sensitive.  For instance, different time profiles of replicates 
were observed depending on the different brand types of plates used, e.g. Nunc, Corning and 
Greiner. The optimal conditions in terms of reproducibility were found at a sample volume 
between 150–225 μL in a 96-well plate from Nunc with orbital shaking at 300 rpm. This was in 
contrast to other conditions investigated which included a sample volume of 60 μL in a 384-well 
plate. In this master’s thesis a sample volume of 200 μL was used in a 96-well plate from Nunc 
with linear shake at 200 rpm, which resulted in reasonable reproducibility between the duplicates. 
Giehm & Otzen additionally showed that reproducibility was dependent on the ratio of the sample 
volume to the test tube volume. For example, a high sample volume will result in a low degree of 
shaking compared to a low sample volume in the same test tube volume. In this master’s thesis, 
the test tube volumes differed between samples incubated in plate and in tubes, with the test 
tube volume being substantially higher for samples incubated in tubes. It is speculated that 
samples incubated in tubes were subjected to non-sufficient mixing due to the large test tube 
volume of the Eppendorf tubes compared to that of the wells in the plate. Insufficient mixing 
could lead to ThT not being able to bind efficiently to the fibrils formed. This may in turn explain 
why the time profiles in the ThT binding assay do not increase as steadily compared to samples 
incubated in tubes. Insufficient mixing is speculated to not only affect the binding of ThT to fibrils 
but also affect the fibrillation process itself.  
6.1.1 CONCENTRATION-DEPENDENCY OF THT  
A study by Heegaard et. al investigated the concentration-dependency of PrP106-126 on ThT 
fluorescence [24]. It was showed that higher concentrations of PrP106-126 lead to higher levels of 
ThT fluorescence [24]. In this master’s thesis, a similar concentration-dependency was observed 
with higher concentrations of PrP106-126(Deuterated) leading to higher levels of ThT fluorescence. 
In this master’s thesis it was shown that samples incubated in tubes of PrP106-216(Deuterated) at 
0.5 mg/mL had a level of ThT fluorescence around 200 after 68 hours of incubation, which was 
similar to the level observed for the negative control of PBS incubated with ThT. In contrast, the 
sample of PrP106-216(Deuterated) at 2 mg/mL showed a ThT level around 1000. In relation to this, 
the AFM images revealed a substantial difference in size and aggregation of the structures, with 
the sample at 2 mg/mL showing larger structures clumped together in large aggregates compared 
to the structures observed for the sample at 0.5 mg/mL. Because the structures observed at 0.5 
mg/mL did not seem to have significant ThT binding properties, it can be speculated if the 
==PAGE:63==

structures observed from the AFM images are prefibrillar species rather than amyloid fibrils. In 
contrast, the larger aggregates at 2 mg/mL are able to bind ThT more readily and the images may 
represent mature amyloid fibrils.  
6.1.2 VERIFICATION OF THE PRESENCE OF AMYLOID FIBRILS 
Even though rare, it should be mentioned that ThT does not always bind specifically to all types of 
fibrils [72]. Cases are known where an increase in ThT fluorescence of several orders of magnitude 
does not correspond to the formation of amyloid fibrils, as ThT in some cases can bind to other 
ordered and disordered aggregate [72]. Therefore, it can be necessary to include additional 
techniques for verifying the presence of amyloid fibrils [23]. In this master’s thesis, ThT binding 
assay and FTIR spectroscopy was complemented with AFM images. Further details of additional 
techniques for investigation and verification of amyloid fibril structure were previously discussed 
in Module 2 – Protein and peptide fibrillation: effect of dendrimers [23]. 
6.1.3 EFFECT OF SOLVENT ON PROTEIN AND PEPTIDE FIBRILLATION 
As previously discussed, the fibrillation process is highly sensitive towards various factors including 
solvent, salt concentration and pH [45] [46]. In relation to this, it has been shown that different 
types of aggregates with different morphologies can be formed using different solvent conditions 
[94] [45] [95].  It should also be considered that solvents affect the dendrimer conformation, as it 
has been shown by Gurtovenko et. al that charged dendrimers decreases in size with increasing 
salt concentrations [96]. In relation to this, a study by Heegaard et. al from 2007 investigated how 
solvents affect dendrimers and the fibrillation process PrP106-126 in a ThT binding assay [19]. In this 
study, G-1 PPI dendrimers with amino, guanidino, and urea surface groups at a concentration at 
20 μg/mL were incubated in MilliQ-water and PBS, respectively. A fibril decreasing effect was 
observed for dendrimers with guanidino and urea surface groups in both MilliQ-water and PBS. 
The underivatized dendrimer with amino surface groups showed a fibril increasing effect in PBS. 
However, in MilliQ-water, no effect was observed compared to the control of PrP106-126 incubated 
without dendrimers [19]. This may be related to the different conformation of the underivatized 
dendrimer in PBS, where salt is present, compared to in MilliQ-water. It can therefore be 
necessary to consider the effect of solvents in relation to both the fibrillation process as well as 
the dendrimer conformation and size, as this may affect dendrimer-fibril interactions [19]. 
6.2 HYDROGEN-DEUTERIUM EXCHANGE OF PRP106-126  
As previously mentioned in 2.7.2.5, hydrogens in proteins and peptides are able to exchange with 
deuterium in D2O [86] [87].  In this master’s thesis, PrP106-126 was subjected to multiple rounds of 
lyophilization from D2O, which mean that hydrogens have most likely been exchanged with 
deuterium from D2O [77]. In this master’s thesis it was observed that PrP106-126(Deuterated) 
showed a higher absorbance of the amide II’ band region at 1456 cm-1 compared to the amide II 
band region at 1526 cm-1. For PrP106-126(Control), the opposite was observed, meaning that the 
absorbance was higher in the amide II band region compared to the amide II’ band region.  
==PAGE:64==

63
A higher absorbance in the amide II’ band region for PrP106-126(Deuterated) could indicate that 
deuterium has been incorporated into PrP106-126(Deuterated). As deuterium is a heavier isotope 
than hydrogen, the stretching vibrations of C-D are slower than those of C-H which could explain 
the high absorbance in the amide II’ band region. Other vibrations, such as e.g. C-H deformation, 
are known to absorb within the amide II’ band region [74]. Additionally, it is commonly known that 
the isotopically mixed D-O-H specie give rise to an additional bending mode, δD-O-H, which 
introduces a band around 1450 cm-1 interfering with the amide II’ band region [75]. In this 
master’s thesis, the FTIR spectrum of D2O revealed a small peak around 1490 cm-1 with 6% 
absorbance, possibly deriving from δD-O-H. Therefore, it is not accurate to examine if deuterium has 
been incorporated into the peptide based on an increase in the amide II’ band. Instead, it could be 
suggested to examine hydrogen-deuterium exchange based on a decrease in the amide I band for 
PrP106-126(Deuterated) compared to that of PrP106-126(Control). 
6.2.1 EFFECT OF HYDROGEN-DEUTERIUM EXCHANGE ON PRP106-126 FIBRILLATION 
Assuming that deuterium has been incorporated, to some extent, into PrP106-126(Deuterium), it is 
speculated that the molecular weight of PrP106-126(Deuterated) has increased compared to that of 
PrP106-126(Control). As previously mentioned, the molecular weight of deuterium is double 
compared to that of hydrogen [88]. Therefore, a sample of 2 mg/mL of PrP106-126(Deuterated) 
would contain less moles compared to a sample of 2 mg/mL of PrP106-126(Control).  
In relation to this, it was shown in this master’s thesis, that the level of ThT fluorescence is 
substantially lower for PrP106-126(Deuterated) compared to that of PrP106-126(Control). A possible 
explanation for this could be that a concentration of 2 mg/mL of PrP106-126(Deuterated) contains 
less moles compared to PrP106-126(Control), and it could be speculated that fewer amyloid fibrils 
are formed. The concentration-dependency of PrP106-126(Deuterated) on ThT fluorescence was 
previously discussed in 6.1.1, where larger concentration of PrP106-126(Deuterated) were shown to 
result in higher ThT fluorescence. In relation to this, the AFM images of PrP106-126(Deuterated) at 
0.5 mg/mL revealed fewer and smaller structures compared to those observed at higher 
concentrations of PrP106-126(Deuterated) at 2 mg/mL.  
6.3 EFFECT OF DENDRIMERS ON PROTEIN AND PEPTIDE FIBRILLATION 
It has been suggested that high concentrations of dendrimers block the growth of fibril ends, 
thereby preventing further fibril growth. In contrast, it has been suggested that low 
concentrations of dendrimers are not sufficient in terms of blocking the fibril ends, and may 
enhance protein and peptide fibrillation by facilitating the assembly of protein and peptides into 
fibrils [27].  
In relation to this, it was showed by Klajnert et al. that G-3 to G-5 PAMAM dendrimers are able to 
decrease PrP185-208 fibrillation at high concentrations at 0.1 μM and 1 μM, while enhancing 
fibrillation at lower concentrations at 0.01 μM [27] [97].

The proposed mechanism, that dendrimers enhance fibrillation at lower concentrations while 
decreasing fibrillation at higher concentrations does not fit the results observed in this master’s 
thesis. In this master’s thesis it was found that MTU, and in particular ETU, at 100 μg/mL showed 
an enhancing effect on PrP106-126 fibrillation. However, at lower concentrations of dendrimers at 2 
μg/mL and 20 μg/mL, no fibril enhancing effect was seen, except for perhaps iTU at 2 μg/mL. 
However, it seems rather unexpected that an effect is observed at 100 μg/mL and 2 μg/mL but not 
at 20 μg/mL. It is not certain if the increase in ThT fluorescence observed for iTU at 2 μg/mL is 
caused by variation or if it indicates a small fibril enhancing effect. For the underivatized 
dendrimer at 100 μg/mL it was difficult to interpret a fibril decreasing or increasing effect due to 
variation between the duplicates. The molar weight of Underivatized G-4 PPI is 3514 g/mol, while 
that of Underivatized G-2 PPI is 773 g/mol [98]. It can therefore be estimated that 20 μg/mL of 
underivatized G-2 PPI dendrimer correlates roughly to 100 μg/mL Underivatized G-4 PPI 
dendrimer in terms of molarity. This could provide basis for comparison between the effects 
previously seen for underivatized G-2 PPI dendrimer at 20 μg/mL by Heegaard et al. [24], and the 
effects observed in this master’s thesis for the underivatized G-4 PPI dendrimer at 100 μg/mL. 
However, the time profile of ThT fluorescence for the underivatized dendrimer at 100 μg/mL in 
this master’s thesis show variation between the duplicates, which makes it difficult to interpret a 
fibril decreasing or increasing effect.  
6.3.1 FIBRIL ENHANCING EFFECT OF DENDRIMERS ON PROTEIN AND PEPTIDE FIBRILLATION  
As previously discussed, a fibril enhancing effect was observed in this master’s thesis at 100 μg/mL 
for MTU and ETU, while lowering the concentration of dendrimers to 2 μg/mL and 20 μg/mL did 
not reveal any effect.  
In relation to this, a study by Klajnert et al. investigating the effect of PPI dendrimers on PrP185-208 
fibrillation at different pH showed that underivatized G-3 PPI dendrimers are able to enhance 
PrP185-208 fibrillation at pH 5.9 and 5.4 at 1 μM [99]. However, increasing the concentration of 
dendrimers to 1.5, 2 and 5 μM showed a fibril decreasing effect [99]. 
Another study by Klajnert et. al from 2012 found that a specific type of dendrimers showed a fibril 
enhancing effect on Aβ 1-28 [100]. In this study, a ThT binding assay was set-up to monitor 
fibrillation of Aβ 1-28 upon incubation with a gallic acid-triethylene glycol dendrimer modified 
with morpholine surface groups [100]. Interestingly, the fibrillation process was accelerated at 0.1 
μM and 1 μM, with the highest concentration leading to the most significant fibril enhancing 
effect. In contrast, the lowest concentration of dendrimer at 0.01 μM showed almost no effect on 
the fibrillation process. Increasing the dendrimer concentration 100 times from 0.01 μM to 1 μM 
in this study led to a significant increase acceleration of the fibrillation process [100]. In relation to 
this, it was shown in this master’s thesis, that increasing the concentration of ETU and MTU 50 
times from 2 μg/mL to 100 μg/mL led to a substantial fibril enhancing effect of PrP106-126.

Another interesting finding in the study by Klajnert et. al was that the toxicity of Aβ 1-28 was 
significantly reduced in the presence of dendrimers, with the higher concentrations of dendrimer 
resulting in the lowest cytoxocitiy [100]. Thus, it is speculated that speeding up the fibrillation 
process can lower the amount of toxic oligomers and prefibrillar species of Aβ 1-28 [100]. In 
relation to this, studies have shown that the oligomer and prefibrillar species formed during 
fibrillation of Aβ fragments are more toxic than the mature Aβ amyloid fibrils [81]. A fibril 
enhancing effect observed in this master’s thesis may therefore be an interesting property of MTU 
and ETU, in terms of overcoming and circumventing toxic prefibrillar species faster.  
In relation to toxicity, it was shown in vivo study by Laumann et. al that G-2 to G-5 urea and MTU 
PPI dendrimers are able to clear intracellular αSyn aggregates in a human cell line with the highest 
generation of MTU showing the highest potency, but also the highest cytotoxicity [67]. It can be 
speculated that a fibril solubilizing effect in vivo may be associated with high cytotoxicity due to 
the fragmentation of αSyn fibrils into smaller oligomer and prefibrillar species.  
6.4 AMIDE I BAND REGION OF PRP106-126 AND DENDRIMERS 
A study by Caughey et. al  investigated changes in the amide I band region of PrP27-30 before and 
after fibrillation [101]. Initially, PrP27-30 was washed with D2O and incubated for 7 days in D2O after 
which a spectrum was recorded. In the study by Caughey et. al, second derivative analysis was 
carried out in order to quantify the amount of secondary structure content. Caughey et. al did not 
observe any changes in wavenumber of the bands after incubation of PrP27-30  for 7 days. The 
bands were localized at 1627, 1636 and 1657 cm-1. According to Caughey et. al, an explanation for 
the lack of changes in wavenumber in the amide I band region could be that portions of the 
secondary structure had already, to some extent, adopted β-sheet structure [101]. Perhaps this 
was the reason why it was not possible to observe a shift from e.g. α-helical structure to β-sheet 
structure, meaning a shift from higher to lower wavenumber. This may explain why in this 
master’s thesis, no changes in secondary structure elements were observed after incubation of 
PrP106-126(Control) for one week. Additionally, a band at 1622 cm-1 was observed prior to carrying 
out the fibrillation process, which may indicate that PrP106-126(Control), to some extent, had 
already adopted β-sheet conformation.  
Other studies have succeeded in demonstrating changes in the amide I band region upon 
incubation of prion peptide fragments with dendrimers. A study by Klajnert et. al investigated 
changes in the amide I band region of PrP185-208 over time upon incubation with a G-4 phosphorous 
dendrimer. Prior to commencing the experiment, a spectrum of PrP185-208 revealed helical and 
unordered structure. The fibrillation process was carried out and a spectrum revealed the 
appearance of a band at 1613 cm.1 increasing in absorbance over time, indicating formation of β-
sheet structure. The absorption of this band was found to decrease over time upon incubation 
with dendrimers compared to the control without dendrimers. Klajnert et. al concluded that the

amount of β-sheet-content was decreasing over time upon incubation with the G-4 
phosphorous, which fit with the fibril decreasing effect observed in a ThT binding assay [26].  
Investigation of PrP106-126 fibrillation using FRIT spectroscopy in this master’s thesis did not show 
any significant differences in spectral position of the amide I band region. A small shift was 
observed for PrP106-126 incubated for one week in PBS compared to PrP106-126 that was not 
incubated. According to Table 1, the peak at 1622 cm-1 and 1624 cm-1 indicate the presence of β-

decrease in wavenumber after fibrillation. Upon incubation with dendrimers, the peak at 1624 cm-

In order to further investigate secondary structure elements, it would perhaps have been 
necessary to complement FTIR spectroscopy with other techniques in order to verify the presence 
of β-sheet content. For example, the samples investigated by FTIR spectroscopy could have been 
complemented by fluorescence measurements in the ThT binding assay. However, due to low 
quantities of PrP106-126 available, this was not possible. Furthermore, it is known that the lines 
broadening a peak in the amide I band region sometimes overlaps other lines and give rise to false 
interpretations [78]. Additionally, as shown in the study by Klajnert et. al  [26], band 
decomposition and curve fitting analysis should be considered in order to separate the bands and 
make more accurate comparison between the individual secondary structure elements [78] [102] 
[103]. It should also be considered that the incubations for FTIR spectroscopy of PrP106-126 with 
dendrimers were carried out in a 12-well plate, in contrast to the ThT binding assay, where 
incubations took place in a 96-well plate. As previously discussed in 6.1, the fibrillation process is 
highly sensitive and affected by various factors such as the different brand types of plates. 
6.5 AMIDE I BAND OF L-ALANYL-GLYCINE AND DENDRIMERS 
In this master’s thesis, FTIR spectroscopy showed that MTU and ETU at 10 mg/mL are able to 
substantially reduce absorbance in the amide I band region of L-alanyl-glycine from 30% to 10%. 
Additionally, the appearance of a band of strong absorbance around 40% at 1500 cm-1 appeared in 
both spectra. The shift of the amide I band to lower wavenumbers is possibly caused by 
interactions of the hydrogens in the thiourea groups with the oxygen in the C=O of the amide 
bond. This may cause a decrease in strength of the C=O bond, which would slow down the 
vibrations of C=O and shifting the absorption of infrared light to lower wavenumbers. For iTU at 10 
mg/mL, the decrease in the amide I band from 80% to 70% was not as substantial as for MTU and 
ETU. Furthermore there was no appearance of a band at 1500 cm-1. The peaks of the spectrum of 
iTU was shown to be identical to those observed for the spectrum of ITU incubated with L-alanyl-
glycine. This could suggest that no interactions or weakening of the C=O bond of L-alanyl-glycine 
has occurred, as this would have been seen by a change in position of any of the bands. It is 
speculated that the C=O in the amide bond of L-alanyl-glycine are not capable of approaching the 
hydrogens on the thiourea group due to steric hindrance of the large isopropyl thiourea groups.

6.6 DENATURING EFFECT OF DENDRIMERS ON NATIVELY FOLDED PROTEIN  
A study by Heegaard et. al investigated the denaturing effect of underivatized G-5 PPI dendrimers 
in a conformation-dependent immunoassay [19]. Different concentrations of the underivatized G-
5 PPI dendrimer (10, 50, 100 and 200 μg/mL) were incubated with edema disease verotoxin type II 
(VT2e), serving as a model for a normal protein. The denaturing effect was analyzed in a two-fold 
dilution series in a sandwich ELISA and showed that the underivatized G-5 PPI dendrimer did not 
have any denaturing effect on the titration curves and therefor did not alter the conformational 
stability of VT2e[19]. 
In this master’s thesis dendrimers were incubated at 20 μg/mL and 100 μg/mL with anti-PAGP 
mAb, serving as a model protein for natively folded protein. The effect of the dendrimers were 
also investigated in a two-fold dilution series in an ELISA. No effect in the titration curves were 
observed for neither of the dendrimers compared to the control of anti-PAGP mAb incubated 
without dendrimers. Therefore the dendrimers did not have any denaturing effect on the 
conformation of natively folded anti-PAGP mAb.  
The molecular weight of the underivatized G-5 PPI dendrimer is 7168 g/mol and that of the 
underivatized G-4 PPI dendrimer is 3514 g/mol [98]. It can be estimated that 200 μg/mL of 
underivatized G-5 PPI dendrimer correlates roughly to 100 μg/mL underivatized G-4 PPI dendrimer 
in terms of molarity. This may give rise to comparison in terms of molarity between the two 
dendrimers, where no denaturing effect on the conformational stability of either VT2e or anti-
PAGP mAb was observed.

7 CONCLUSION 
A Thioflavin T binding assay of PrP106-126 fibrillation did not reveal any fibril perturbing effect of the 
dendrimers included in this master’s thesis. Instead, a fibril enhancing effect was observed for 
MTU and ETU at 100 μg/mL, with ETU showing the highest fibril enhancing effect. At 2 μg/mL and 
20 μg/mL no effect was observed for neither of the dendrimers. A trend in hydrophobicity was not 
observed in terms of fibril enhancing effect. An IgG denaturation analysis was set up and it was 
verified that the chosen concentrations of dendrimers and denaturants did not affect the ELISA 
itself. Furthermore, it was verified that a denaturing effect of urea and GuHCl could be seen from 
the ELISA after 1 hour, 3 hours and 4 days of incubation. It was found that G-4 PPI MTU, ETU, iTU 
and Underivatized at 20 μg/mL and 100 μg/mL did not affect the titration curves in the ELISA 
compared to the control of anti-PAGP mAb incubated without dendrimers. Thus the 
conformational stability of anti-PAGP mAb was therefore not affect by the presence of 
dendrimers.  
In relation to the hypothesis outlined in 1.3, it was concluded that ETU and MTU at 100 μg/mL 
were able specifically perturb amyloid fibrils of PrP106-126 in terms of enhancing the fibrillation 
process, without exerting a denaturing effect on the conformational stability of anti-PAGP mAb.  
Investigations of dendrimers effect on PrP106-126 fibrillation using FRIT spectroscopy did not show 
any significant differences in spectral position of the amide I band region. A small shift to higher 
wavenumbers from 1668 cm-1 to 1674 cm-1 and from 1622 cm-1 to 1624 cm-1 was observed for 
PrP106-126 after incubation with and without dendrimers. However, this does not fit the expected 
decrease in wavenumber upon fibrillation for PrP106-126. From FTIR spectroscopy it was not 
possible to observe changes in secondary structure elements to β-sheet content. This was possibly 
due to already adopted β-sheet structure prior to carrying out the incubations. Furthermore, it 
may have been necessary to consider band decomposition and curve fitting analysis in order to 
separate the bands and make comparison between the individual secondary structure elements. 
FTIR spectroscopic Investigations of dendrimers interaction with L-alanyl-glycine showed that MTU 
and ETU at 10 mg/mL showed a substantial reduction in the amide I band of L-alanyl-glycine from 
30% to 10 %. Additionally, for MTU and ETU, the appearance of a band with absorbance around 
40% at 1500 cm-1 was observed. The shift of the amide I band to lower wavenumbers is possibly 
due to the interaction of the hydrogens in the thiourea groups of MTU and ETU with the oxygen in 
the C=O group in amide bond.  This may lead to a weakening of the C=O bond, resulting in slower 
vibrations that absorb infrared light at lower wavenumbers. For iTU at 10 mg/mL, no shift in the 
amide I band of L-alanyl-glycine was observed. Furthermore, the peaks in the spectrum of iTU 
were identical to those observed in the spectrum of ITU incubated with L-alanyl-glycine. This may 
suggest that the hydrogens in iTU did not affect the C=O in the amide bond of L-alanyl-glycine, 
probably due to steric hindrance of iTU.

8 FUTURE ASPECT 
It could be of interest to further investigate the therapeutic potential of dendrimers on amyloid 
fibrils. An example of a set-up could include investigation of the effect of different generations of 
thiourea-modified PPI dendrimers in a Thioflavin T binding assay, in order see if a generation-
dependent effect is observed. It could also be of interest to include other types of dendrimers as 
well as different proteins and peptides capable of forming amyloid fibrils e.g. αSyn and Aβ1-28.  In 
relation to this, the fibril enhancing potential of dendrimers could be investigated in relation to 
the formation of Aβ1-28 fibrils combined with cytotoxicity studies. 
It could also be interesting to further investigate if the thiourea-modified dendrimers have a 
stabilizing effect on anti-PAGP mAb. In order to investigate this, the experimental set up could 
consist of two-fold titration of 8M denaturants, while keeping the concentration of dendrimers 
and anti-PAGP mAb constant. The ELISA would otherwise be carried out as previously described in 
10.3. It would be expected that higher concentrations of denaturant result in lowering of the OD 
signal due to denaturation of anti-PAGP mAb. However, if dendrimers have a stabilizing effect on 
anti-PAGP mAb, it could be hypothesized that specific concentrations of denaturants do not result 
in a decrease in OD signal. A stabilizing effect of thiourea-modified dendrimers could be 
interesting to investigate further in relation to biomedical applications such as design of drug 
delivery systems and stabilization of protein drug formulations.

10.1 THIOFLAVIN T PROTOCOL 
A stock solution of Thioflavin T (1 mM) was freshly prepared by dissolving 3.18 mg ThT in 10 mL 
MilliQ-water.  The solution was immediately covered with tin foil and kept at 4° C until further use. 
Negative controls were prepared in advance consisting of PBS alone and PBS with ThT. In brief, 
ThT was diluted 1:50 in PBS yielding a final ThT concentration of 20 μM by adding 28 μL ThT (1 
mM) to 1372 μL PBS. Different concentrations and samples of PrP106-126 were prepared according 
to  4.3.1 and 4.3.2 . 
All solutions were covered with tin foil and added in duplicates onto a black optical bottom 96-
well plate (Thermo Scientific) with a total volume of 200 μL per well. The plate was covered with 
sealing tape and tin foil. The fluorescence was measured each day on a BioTek® Synergy HT Multi-
Detection Microplate Reader using an emission wavelength of 485 nm and an excitation 
wavelength of 440 nm. Detection mode: fluorescence, read type: end point, optics position: 
bottom, sensitivity: 35.  
The plate was kept on gentle linear agitation (200 rpm) at room temperature in between each 
measurement. This procedure will from now on be referred to in the Appendix in 10.1 as Thioflavin 
T protocol.

10.2 FTIR SPECTROSCOPY PROTOCOL  
FTIR spectroscopy was performed using a MIRacle 10 single reflection ATR accessory from 
Shimadzu with a scanning range between 600–3600 cm-1. Initially, the ATR crystal was cleaned 
with MilliQ-water followed by EtOH and acetone until complete dryness. Then a background scan 
was carried out by pushing down the high-pressure clamp onto the ATR crystal, ensuring good 
contact. 
 
The ATR FTIR spectroscopy allowed acquirement of spectra of samples in both liquid and solid 
states:  
 
- 
Liquid samples were applied onto the ATR crystal in a total volume of 3 μL. 
 
- 
Solid samples were applied onto the ATR crystal ensuring that the entire area of ATR 
crystal was covered. 
 
- 
Solid samples that had been dissolved in PBS followed by freeze-drying were added in a 
small amount to the ATR crystal and suspended in 3 μL D2O.  
 
After addition of a sample to the ATR crystal, the high-pressure clamp was pushed down and a 
spectrum was obtained. This procedure is referred to as ‘FTIR Spectroscopy Protocol’ and is found 
in the Appendix section 10.2.

10.3 ELISA PROTOCOL  
Anti-PAGP mAb 1.62 (2 mg/mL) was diluted (1:1000) in coating buffer (sodium carbonate 0.1M, pH 
9.6) to a concentration of 2 μg/mL. Next, 100 μL was added to each well on a 96-well Maxisorp 
NUNC plate. The plate was covered with sealing tape at kept at 4°C overnight.  
The plate was washed 4 times with washing buffer (250 mL PBS with 6 μL 20% Tween 20). Next, 
200 μL blocking buffer (2.5 g Bovine Serum Albumin and 625 μL 20% Tween 20 in 250 mL PBS) was 
added to each well and the plate was incubated at room temperature on shake (200 rpm) for 30 
minutes.  
The plate was washed 4 times in washing buffer and Biotin-PAGP (1.58 mg/mL) was diluted (1:158) 
in blocking buffer to a concentration of 10 μg/mL. Two-fold vertical dilution series of Biotin-PAGP 
in blocking buffer starting from 10 μg/mL was carried out. The plate was covered with sealing tape 
and incubated at room temperature on shake (200 rpm) for 1 hour.  
The plate was washed and Streptavidin-HRP (DAKO P397) was diluted (1:2000) in blocking buffer 
and 100 μL was added to each well. The plate was covered with sealing tape and incubated at 
room temperature on shake (200 rpm) for 1 hour.  
Next, the plate was washed followed by the addition of 100 μL TMB-plus to each well. The plate 
was kept in dark during the colour development reaction where a blue colour was observed. The 
reaction was stopped after 2 minutes and 20 seconds by addition of 100 μL 0.5M sulphuric acid to 
each well resulting in a bright yellow colour. The OD of each well was read using an automatic 
plate reader (Thermo Multiscan Ex spectrophotometer, Thermo Scientific). The OD was read at 
450 nm with subtraction of unspecific coloration at 650 nm.
