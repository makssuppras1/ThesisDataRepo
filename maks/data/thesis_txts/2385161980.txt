Abstract
In this thesis prevention of metabolic shifts has been the main objective. The metabolic shift has been
reported as an indication of rest in microbial growth, resulting in an increase in the dissolved oxygen
(DO). The metabolic shift could be predicted by the Frequency content analysis value (FCA) which has
been found to decrease around two hours before a metabolic shift. And this was exploited by developing
monitoring methods to detect decreases in the FCA.
The theory of the influence of the microbial growth was investigated through model simulation, where
the FCA signal and the metabolic shift was simulated. The simulation of the metabolic shift confirmed
the theory of the influence of the growth rate. However the factors that influenced the growth rate was
not fully investigated.
The monitoring detection methods were developed from offline data and tested online in three pilot plant
experiments resulting in 11 batches. First experiments validating the online implementation, second
experiments testing the robustness of the methods and finally the last experiments testing suitable
control actions at the time of detection.
Conclusively two robust detection methods were developed which predicted metabolic shifts 8 out of 8
times with detection times 1.5 to 3.3 hours before the occurrence of the metabolic shift. And it was found
that addition of phosphate at the time of detection could prevent a metabolic shift. Furthermore, pH was
found to contain information about the metabolic shift in the same manner as DO did. This potentially
expanded the application of the detection methods to fermentations without DO measurements.

Formålet i denne opgave har været at finde en metode der kunne afværge metaboliske skift. Ved at
bruge den information givet ved at kvantificere reaktionen af ilt overførslen til cellerne for små ændringer
i substrat tilførslen kaldet (FCA) kan man observere et fald i denne værdi ca. 2-3 timer før celle vækst
stop. To metoder blev udviklet, der kunne forudse skift i den mikrobielle vækst under fermentering
processer ved at analysere FCA signalet.
Metoderne blev udviklet på baggrund af offline data. De to metoder blev implementeret online og tre
eksperimenter blev udført svarende til 11 batch forsøg. I 8 ud af de 11 batch forsøg kom der metaboliske
skift, der indikerer ophør i den mikrobielle vækst. 8 ud af 8 gange detekterede metoderne faldet i FCA
og dermed forudså det metaboliske skift. I et enkelt forsøg blev et metabolisk skift afværget ved tilfø-
jelse a fosfat buffer ved detekteringstidspunktet, dette viste, at detekteringsmetoderne har potentiale
for at kunne afværge metaboliske skift.
For at bekræfte den mikrobe væksts indflydelse på de observerede metaboliske skift blev processen
simuleret. Tidligere er den brugte model kun blevet brugt til kontinuerlig simulering. Derfor måtte der
udvikles en metode, hvorved den manipulerede substrattilførsel blev sendt til systemet som impulser,
for at reproducere de forhold som fermenteringerne er udført ved. Efter at have efterprøvet forskellige
pulseringsmetoder, blev det metaboliske skift simuleret ved at tilføje et tredje led til vækst rate ligningen
i modellen. Dette tredje led symboliserede de begrænsende faktorer, der fremprovokerer det metaboliske
skift. Det blev observeret, at når dette led kom under en bestemt værdi så resulterede det i et skift,
som det også var observeret i det eksperimentelle arbejde.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The metabolic pathways of microorganisms have been investigated for many years. The aim has been
to map the metabolic paths and find triggers that control which metabolic pathway that are followed.
The aim in industry is the control the pathway to achieve high production rates. This thesis proposes
a control method to prevent metabolic shifts in industrial fermentations.
1.1
Background
1.1.1
Industrial fermentation process
Fermentation may be referred to as any process that produces a useful product by the mass culture
of microorganisms.[1] In 1850s Louis Pasteur described as a process from living cells, contrary to the
previous theory from Liebig which described the fermentation as a result of contact with decaying
matter.[2] Today fermentation technology is used in a broader term by the industry and no longer only
assess the fermentation part of the microbial behavior but fully exploit all the metabolic pathways.
The industrial fermentation processes originated in 1849, where industrial production of bakers yeast
started, fermentation and biotechnology since then have rapidly developed. Multiple utilization has
been discovered and utilized, moving away from traditional fermentation and towards the industrial fer-
mentation processes known today.[3] Today, the industry exploit the microorganisms ability to convert
sugar and organic compounds to a broad range of useful products. Fermentation technology aims to
fully exploit this diversity and the range of application is already wide. The microorganisms are used
for the production of fine chemicals, organic solvents, dairy products and enzymes etc.[4]
Industrial fermentation takes place in bioreactors or so called fermentors. The most commonly used
reactor type for industrial fermentation processes is the stirred tank reactor (STR). It can be run in
three different modes: [5]
• Batch mode: Fin(t) = Fout(t) = 0. No stream in or out during the process.
• Fed-batch mode: Fin(t) > 0, Fout(t) = 0. Feed is added throughout the process, while no product
is taken out.
• Continuous mode (CSTR): Fin(t) = Fout(t) ̸= 0. During the process feed is added and medium
is simultaneously taken out for a steady state process.
Fed-batch cultivation is the main operation mode in the industry, due to the flexibility and smaller
risk of mutation during the process.[5] Operation with fed-batch mode ensure a good control of the
system since the feed can be added throughout the batch to maintain product formation and biomass
growth.[6]
Monitoring and control
Monitoring and control of the process is essential to maintain high production rate, which is one of
the main objectives in production. The variables that are measured are most often temperature, pH,
feed rate, oxygen consumption and carbon dioxide formation.[7] These influence the process in term of

In fed-batch operated processes one of the main manipulated variable is the feed rate. Other process
variables as pressure, agitation, aeration, temperature and pH are most often controlled after predeter-

concentration, by-product formation, process yield, productivity and controlling an oxygen concentra-
tion profile.[8] To achieve high productivity and product concentration the feed should be increased.
However, overfeeding could also result in by-product formation as a result of overflow metabolism.[5]
Lee et al. [9] reviewed different fed-batch control strategies, in which some will be presented in the
following paragraphs.

The open-loop control is a control where the feed rate for the whole process is predetermined by the
process conditions. For example it can be held constant during the whole process. Another way is to
define an exponential changing feed flow rate, which could be determined by (1.1).[9]

Simple feedback control
A commonly used control method is simple feedback control. The principle of the feedback control is
that the feed rate is manipulated based on measurements of the process. Lee et al. [9] suggest feedback
control of pH or dissolved oxygen (DO). When the process is limited of sugar the microbes starts to
excrete ammonia ions and thereby increasing the ammonia concentration and the pH of the broth.
Similar with the dissolved oxygen which increase when the substrate is depleted.[9] The increase in DO
occur when the substrate is decreased, since growth is limiting and the oxygen is thereby in excess and
this is expressed.[10] This feedback controller will feed more substrate to the system if the controlled
variable (DO or pH) increase and thereby maintaining the fixed set point. A block diagram of a feedback
control is presented in Figure 1.1.[9]
Figure 1.1: Block diagran of indirect feed (single-loop) method. (Drawing based on [9]).
Probing control
The last control method which will be presented is probing control, also called perturbation-based
control. The concept of perturbation-based control is to add pulses in the manipulated variable e.g.
the feed rate and evaluate the response in the controlled variable e.g. DO. Based on the response of
the feed pulse in the DO signal can the control decision be made.[6] The response in DO is dependent
on the glucose concentration in the broth, see Figure 1.2. So an up pulse in the feed can result in two
outcomes; a pulse decrease in DO or no response in the signal. A reaction in the dissolved oxygen
concentration is indicating that the glucose is limiting growth and the feed should be increased. No
response in the DO could be a result of accumulating glucose or glucose could be at the optimum level.
When the feed is reduced in a short pulse the output can be an increase in DO or no response. A
reaction in DO is interpreted as two things; the glucose is limiting growth or glucose is at optimum

Frequency content analysis (FCA) and it quantified the response in the DO signal and thereby estimated
the feed demand.[11]

1.1.2
Frequency Content Analysis (FCA) to detect metabolic changes during

With the development of the FCA method it was found that the FCA signal decreased hours before a
metabolic shift. The quantification of the DO response therefore revealed the a change in the metabolic
state before a metabolic shift occurred, see Figure 1.5.
Figure 1.5: Results from a batch experiments showing a decrease in FCA before the metabolic shift.[13].
The metabolic shift was reported as a stop in microbial growth causing the DO to increase.[13] The
current feed rate controller could not prevent the metabolic shift with the control manipulating the
substrate feed rate to maintain the DO set point. The idea is to switch from the current DO control
to a metabolic shift preventing control at the time close to the metabolic shift indicated by the FCA
signal. The aim is to prevent metabolic shift in batches with high affinity to metabolic shifts.
1.1.3
Metabolic shifts
In this, the focus will be mainly on the microbial growth and the conditions which influence the growth
rate. The Monod model suggest that microbial growth is dependent on one limiting substrate and
thereby implies that no other substance effects the microbial growth.[14] However, growth in general
is not limited by one substance, for instance looking at the Liebig’s law of the minimum developed in
agricultural science. Liebig’s states that growth is controlled by the limited resource. This resource
could be any of the multiple resources required for growth as shown in Figure 1.6.[15]
Figure 1.6: Liebig’s barrel illustrating Liebig’s law of the minimum.
==PAGE:16==
6
1 Introduction
Egli and Zinn [16] reviewed the concept of multiple-nutrient-limited growth where a the microbial
cultures simultaneously can be limited by two or more nutrients, which differs from Liebig’s law that
only depend on one nutrient at the time. In the article the ratio between nitrogen and carbon was
evaluated and a transition zone where both carbon and nitrogen was limiting factors was reported. But
also phosphate has been reported to be co-limiting with glucose and nitrogen.[16]
When growth stops a shift in the oxygen consumption is expressed, this phenomena was investigated
in the thesis of Rey [13]. Different conditions was tested to provoke a metabolic shift in a culture and
thereby get an understanding of why the metabolic shifts take place. The initial hypothesis was that
acetate build-up would result in a metabolic shift. Four condition that all can result in acetate formation
were investigated; oxygen limitation and glucose excess, nutrient limitation, low pH and osmotic stress.
From the study it was found that the production of higher level of acetate did not start before after
the reported metabolic shift. Rey [13] reported metabolic shift when phosphate went below a certain
threshold along with low pH and low DO process conditions, as seen in Figure 1.7.[13] These three
factors all contributed to the metabolic shift implying the multi variable dependence of the growth.
Figure 1.7: Phosphate concentration and dissolved oxygen tension during fermentation .[13].
The shift in the dissolved oxygen seen in Figure 1.7 indicates the stop in growth. Since the process
stops consuming oxygen leaving more dissolved oxygen in the broth. Dauner et al. [17] investigated the
metabolism during nutrient limiting conditions in Bacillus subtilis. Under phosphate limitation the rel-
ative flux in the TCA cycle was reported remarkably low compared to the flux under nitrogen limiting
conditions. So under phosphate limited conditions overflow metabolism and production of metabolites
such as acetate became the primary metabolic path.[17]
Hoi et al. [18] investigated the phosphate starvation response in the transcriptional and translational
level. Hoi et al. reported down-regulation of amino acid biosynthesis which are keystone in protein
production and thereby enzyme production under phosphate limiting conditions. Most of the TCA cy-
cle enzymes were not affected by the phosphate starvation, but there was reported great repress of the
gene for fumarate hydratase.[18] The enzyme fumarate hydratase catalyzes the hydration of fumarate
to malate, known as a step in the TCA cycle.[19] Emphasizing that the relative metabolic flux in the
TCA cycle is reduced during phosphate starvation, since the cycle can not be completed.[18]
Marzan and Shimizu [20] investigated multiple factors of the metabolic regulation including pH, phos-
phate and nitrogen for E. coli. For nitrogen starvation it was reported that cell concentration decreased
under these conditions. Some TCA cycle genes were down regulated as well, as reported for the phos-
phate limitation, resulting in repression of the TCA cycle. These effects were enhanced under phosphate
limiting conditions again indicating the network of variables that affect the metabolism.[20] At low pH

and phosphate limitation it was also reported that the growth was reduced as found from Rey [13].
Paczia et al. [21] reported immediately stop in biomass formation after ammonia depletion in a study
with four different cultures; E. coli, B. licheniformis, S. cerevisiae and C. glutamicum.
All of the above emphasize the complexity of the metabolic shift, where multiple factors affects the
growth rate and thereby induce a metabolic shift. The aim of this study will not be in investigating
the cause of the metabolic shift but to find a method to predict and prevent it from occurring.
1.2
Aim
Probing control, such as FCA (Frequency Content Analysis) control, is not only an interesting way of
controlling feed rate in fermentation systems, but it can also be used to provide increased understanding
of the metabolic state of the cells during the fermentation process. Since the FCA value contains this
information, it could be of interest to combine/incorporate the FCA signal into other feed rate control
systems, e.g. DO control, as a way to monitor and potentially react to metabolic changes during the
fermentation. Given the fact that the FCA value changes dynamically during a fermentation, a main
challenge will be to develop a generic method, that in a reliable and robust way, automatically could
detect indications to metabolic changes while then shifting to a suitable other control option.
Project milestones:
(a) Define a good way to make use of changes in the FCA signal so that it can be used as a reliable
indicator of metabolic changes.
(b) Develop and evaluate strategies to optimize how to shift between different control strategies (DO
vs. FCA control) based on the FCA signal.
Possible next steps
(c) Evaluate/challenge the top control candidates during online monitoring of pilot scale fermentations
at Novozymes.
(d) Evaluate suitable controller actions based on metabolic changes in the fermentations.
(e) define relevant PID settings (potentially with gain scheduling or adaptation) for the controllers
output based on the signal.
1.3
Approach
To accomplish the project milestones the work was divided into three parts. Offline data analysis, ex-
perimental work and simulation.
The offline data analysis was used to get knowledge of the FCA signal for batches with and without
metabolic shifts. From this three methods were defined and developed answering milestone (a) and (b).
Milestone (c) and (d) was reached with experimental work. Pilot scale experiments were run to evalu-
ate the defined monitoring methods and finding method limitations by changing key process variables.
Suitable control action were tested to find whether the detection methods could prevent a metabolic
shift.
The last milestone was not reached. Instead time was used on simulating the process and evaluating if
a given model could reproduce the experimental FCA signal.

CHAPTER 2
Material and Methods
The work in this thesis is separated into three parts. The first part constitutes the development of
detection methods, that automatically can detect changes in the FCA signal. The second part is the
experimental work, where the detection methods were implemented for online testing and evaluation.
In the third part the process is simulated. In this chapter the material and method for each of the parts
is presented.
2.1
Development of monitoring methods
In this section, the objective is to specify a method that can detect decreases in the FCA signal, since
it has been found to be an indication of the metabolic state and thereby a predictor of metabolic shifts.
To design a detection method that detects changes in the FCA signal offline data from 11 batches
provided by Novozymes Fermentation Pilot Plant were examined. The provided batches had been run
under different conditions which were expected to provoke a metabolic shift.[13] The 11 batches were
categorized in four categories, the first two regarding the metabolic shift.
• 4 with metabolic shift.
• 7 without metabolic shift.
Regarding the robustness of the detection methods the batches were also categorized into the following
groups.
• 7 without significant disturbances introduced by the feed rate.
• 4 with significant disturbances introduced by the feed rate.
Figure 2.1 shows representative plots of the FCA and DO signal for the different categories. In Ap-
pendix A.1 the data from all 11 batches is presented.

2.1.2
Evaluation of the methods
The methods will be evaluated by two measures; the action time and the accuracy of the method.
Action time
The action time is the time from detection to the time of the metabolic shift. The longer the action
time, the more time there will be to prevent the metabolic shift. For the 7 batches with metabolic shift
an average action time (¯taction) will be calculated. The average action time is the average of the action
times of all correct detections for the 7 batches. For incorrect detections the action time is set to zero,
since no control action should be taken.
Accuracy
The measure of accuracy is given by the errors that the methods can constitute. The list of possible
error outcomes are the following:
• Type I Error (False positive)
• Type II Error (False negative)
• Late detection
Preliminary for description of statistical type I and II errors must the null hypothesis (H0) of the test
be stated.
H0 : The batch has metabolic shifts.
The aim is to find the abnormal batches with metabolic instabilities, which accepts the null hypothesis.
If the batch is normal, the null hypothesis is rejected and no action will be taken. In this a type I error,
a false positive, will be a detection of a change in the FCA signal, but with no shift in the metabolic
state 2-3h later, often caused by disturbances in the feed rate. An example of a type I error can be seen
in Figure 2.5, where a large deviation occurs in the FCA signal.

2.2
Experimental
During the project period, three sets of experiments with different objectives were performed giving a
total of 11 pilot plant fermentations. The aim of each experiment was:
• Online detection of metabolic shifts, using the developed detection methods.
• Test of detection methods robustness.
• Test of control actions at metabolic shifts.
2.2.1
Fermentation
Each fermentation takes place in a pilot scale bioreactor. The fermention is operated in fed-batch mode
where the substrate is added throughout the process to control the level of dissolved oxygen (DO) in the
system. Multiple system variables are controlled in the process; temperature is controlled by cooling
water jacket, pH is controlled by adding acid or base, the broth is aerated from the bottom of the tank
with air, the broth is kept at constant stirrer speed and the head space pressure is kept constant. Albaek
et al. [22] gives a description of the used pilot scale bioreactor. The fermentations were performed by
Novozymes Fermentation Pilot Plant operators.
2.2.2
Strains and growth conditions
In this study the bacterium Bacillus Licheniformis was used. The bacterium was chosen since the work
is in continuation of the study made by Rey [13] where a correlation between the FCA signal and a
change in metabolic state during fermentation was found. The specie is closely related to other species
and it is assumed that the results of this study can be used with fermentations of similar species. The
process condition are based on the study from Rey [13] where a recipe to provoke metabolic shifts where
developed.
2.2.3
Measurements
The DO and feed rate were sampled in all experiments and from this the FCA signal was calculated
simultaneously. In Experiment 2 the process variables pressure, agitation, aeration and temperature
were sampled. For model validation and process understanding, the following process parameters whee
sampled for all experiments: base addition rate, pH, OUR, RQ and CER.

Frequency Content Analysis
The Frequency Content Analysis method was developed by Johnsson [11] for perturbation-based control
of industrial fed-batch bioprocesses, this is described in Section 1.1.1. In this project the FCA value is
used for its measure of the metabolic state, where the aim is to make a system that reacts to changes
in the metabolic state. The FCA is calculated online during the batch experiments based on the work
from Johnsson [11] and further developments done by the Fermentation Pilot Plant department (FPP)
at Novozymes A/S.
To determine the FCA signal the DO signal is first filtered by a filter designed by FPP. A Fast Fourier
Transformation was used over an interval of four cycle times to find the power spectral density (PSD) of
the perturbations in the DO signal, using the Fast Fourier Transform (fft) function in MATLAB. The
FCA is found by taking average of m numbers of PSD around the frequency of interest (ωreli) in the
span (d), which is set to 11 data points. The frequency of interest is given by the cycle time frequency,
the cycle time is defined by the length of a feed pulse, which varies slightly throughout the process. The
number of sample points m for determining the average PSD is the length of 4 cycles.

• polyfit(x,y,1) with median filter of the data.

Summary of method development
Method 1 was found to be the best method from this evaluation. The method had the best average
action time and smallest variance in the achieved action times. The variance can be a measure of the
methods reliability for example if the taction is between 2 minutes and 3.4 hours as found in one of the
variants of Method 2, it would not be certain if the method detect 2 hours before every batch. If the
minimum and maximum taction is 2 and 2.5 h then the method is reliable because it is expected that

is not achieved, it is more desirable to have a more consistent detection.

1 and 3 were tested online, since they both had good detection times and their own benefit; Method 1
with low variance and longest average action time and Method 3 with the highest achieved action time
for single batches. By implementing both methods the reliability from Method 1 can assure detection
every time, while Method 3 in some cases will have the earliest detection, and the main objective is
early detection.

From the analysis it was found that smoothing of the data was essential for the rejection of disturbances.
The median filter was chosen since it is a more robust operation for outliers compared to using a moving
average. If the signal contains large variations, the variations will appear in the average while the
variation will not appear in the median. However smoothing and filtering data can be unreliable, since
important information from the data can be lost.[25] For the purpose of filtering data there are some
common data smoothing methods that could have been used e.g. Savitzky Golay filtering or Local
regression smoothing.[26]
Savitzky Golay filter
is an unweighted linear least-squares fit using a polynomial of a given degree.
For higher polynomial degree the smoothing happens without weakening the signal.[26] This filter is
often used for spectroscopic data where the aim is to cancel out noise and maintain the height of the
peaks. In this study, the filter should reduce the peaks in the data caused by disturbances, while the
decrease over 2 hours should be preserved. Therefore the Savitzky Golay filter would not have the
desired effect.
Local regression smoothing
use locally weighted linear regression to smooth data. In this the
regression weight for each data point in the span is first computed.
Then the linear least-square
regression is computed resulting in the value for the smoothed data.[26] By using this smoothing method
outliers are rejected, this smoothing is implemented in the robustfit function of MATLAB which showed
potential in rejecting disturbances. However a build-in smoothing function with this theory was not
available for this study and requires more computational time compared to the simple median filter.

Data point resolution discussion
In the analysis from Section 3.1, Method 1 was found as the method with the best results. It was
therefore anticipated that this would be applicable for also for the online experiments. A reason for the
difference could originate from the data resolution. The data from the 11 offline batches was provided
with a sampling frequency of around 50 seconds, while the online implementation had a sampling rate
of 10 seconds. This evidently has an effect on the tuning, since the resolution is bigger for the online
data. The effect could potentially affect all the tuned parameters, but for this matter the effect on the
filter time, which is a measure used in both methods, was investigated to find the influence of sampling
frequency. Figure 3.22 shows the difference between filtering data with different sampling time. It can
be seen that for higher resolution (e.g. samples every 10 s) the disturbances is more expressed in the
filtered data. This indicates that the determined filter time of the methods can be too short for the
online implementation. A longer filter time could potentially counteract the difference that occur when
switching from 50 to 10 second sampling time. This could be the reason why Method 3 turned out
better than Method 1 in the online implementation. For method 3 it was found that decreasing the filter
time would increase the action time, so the higher resolution could potentially result in earlier detection
with Method 3. For Method 1 a decreasing filter time would result in increase in action time for tshort
and a decrease in action time for tlong. The two could perhaps outbalance each other explaining why
Method 1 decreased the action time compared to Method 3. If the development of the methods had
been on data with 5 minute sampling the tuning results could have been vastly different and the results
could have been misleading.

3.3.2
Summary of disturbance experiment
From Experiment 2 it was found that shut down of agitation, aeration and cooling water will result in
changes in the FCA signal so big that the detection methods will incorrectly detect. If automatic control
will be implemented a control action will be carried out automatically at detection. Therefore if the
detection is a false positive, the control action will occur at a time where the control action is not needed.
This can be avoided by adding a supervisory level where the process variables are monitored. If the
process variables deviate x% from their set point then the supervisory level will suppress the detection
and thereby prevent a control action under larger deviations in the process conditions. These suggested
rejection limits could for example be -10% for agitation and aeration, since agitation and aeration affects
the oxygen transfer rate quite much. For pressure a change of -30% was rejected and therefore this could
be the suggested rejection limit and for the temperature a rejection limit of +10% could potentially be
enough to reject disturbances in temperature. However, to confirm that the proposed rejection limits
are enough additional experiments must be carried out.

Rey [13] investigated which process conditions that caused metabolic shifts and investigated the decrease
in the FCA signal that was visible hours before the metabolic shift. In the study it was hypothesized
that phosphate depletion was one of the factors for metabolic shift occurrence. However the conclusion
of the study was that many factors were involved as it also has been suggested in this study. In Batch

[28] described four decoupling phases under phosphate limiting conditions for E. coli. In the first phase
increased substrate demand and alleviated respiratory activity was reported. The second phase was a
respiratory chain decoupling, corresponding to down regulation of the TCA cycle. Followed by step 3
where the production of overflow metabolites initiate. The final step is the metabolic breakdown, which
reportedly occurs when the cells enter total phosphate depletion.[28] Acetate formation and increased
flux of overflow metabolism had also been reported for the Bacillus species under phosphate limiting
conditions.[13, 17, 18] An increased substrate demand should in the theory be visible as an increase
in the FCA signal if B. Licheniformis has similar reaction at phosphate limiting conditions as E. coli,
which has seemed to be the case for most of the reported results. However, the increased substrate
demand was not visible in sampled data.

3.5
FCA on other signals
The DO signal has been used in reported perturbation control methods.[11, 10, 29] However the dis-
solved oxygen tension is not the only measure that can give an indication of the substrate demand.
When pH increase it is an indication that the process is low on carbon sources and therefore this signal
should in theory result in a similar FCA trend.[9] The hypothesis is therefore that the response of feed
pulses in pH can reveal the state of the cells as the DO. In the following section frequency analysis of
the measured pH was done to confirm this hypothesis.
Figure 3.36(a) shows the frequency content analysis of the pH signal (FCA(pH)), for all FCA(pH) results
see Appendix A.5. The figure shows that FCA(pH) has the same decreasing tendency as FCA(DO)
hours before the metabolic shift. The main difference between FCA(DO) and FCA(pH) is that FCA(pH)
starts increasing midway through the fermentation period. This rise could potentially correspond to
the ATP decoupling resulting in increased substrate demand reported by Schuhmacher et al. [28]. But
the most likely cause to the rise is found in the feed rate which is correspondingly decreasing during the
batch. Therefore the increase is most likely not linked to the phosphate depletion. But the decrease in
FCA(pH) can be used to detection for batches that do not have DO measurements.

3.6
Simulation
The last part of the project focused on the ability to simulate the process and reproduce the FCA
signal. The focus of the simulation is not to develop a model, since the existing model from Albaek
et al. [23] and Lundquist [24] is used. Previously the model has mainly been used to simulate the process
continuously. The aim of the simulation is to simulate the discrete response in the DO, achieved by
adding the feed discrete in pulses. First the model is validated by simulating without pulses and see
if the model could follow the same trend as observed in the experiments. The input variables sampled
values from Experiment 2 were available so these were used as inputs to the model to include the natural
variance that occur in the process variables. Experiment 2 was therefore used as a validation batch.
3.6.1
Simulation of the process with disturbance in process variables
The disturbance data from Experiment 2 and the model was implemented into Simulink as presented
in Section 2.3. All simulation results of Experiment 2 are presented in Appendix C.2. Simulation of
the response of large changes in the agitation and aeration failed at the point of the first disturbance,
revealing limitations of the model. This could be expected since the system itself came out of balance
after the addition of the disturbances in the aeration and agitation. Figure 3.37 shows the simulation
output for the disturbances in the head space pressure which shows a close fit between the model and
the experimental data. Some simulations with smaller step changes applied in the four process variables
was run to confirm that changes in agitation and aeration could be simulated without a system failure,
see Appendix C.2. The results showed that changes larger than -20% in agitation resulted in the DO
going to zero and staying there throughout the simulation after the disturbance. For changes in aeration
the disturbances smaller than -50% did not cause the system to fail. This results implies that the model
can simulate most conditions of the process. However, whether the small step change simulation results
are close to the true can only be validated by experimental results.

With a confirmation that the process can be simulated with the given model, the application of a pulsed

a continuous flow rate and add additional pulses to this main feed (as [10]).

a result the pulse must be higher to achieve the same amount of feed added. The used pulse function
is given by (3.2).

In a real process the phosphate and other nutrients are known to decrease during the batch, therefore

of the simulation, after that none of the four model variables are clearly affected before the metabolic
shift. At the point of the metabolic shift the substrate concentration increase rapidly, since it is no
longer consumed, volume increases due to increased feed rate and product and biomass decreases, since
growth is stopped, see Appendix C.2. Further development could be to formulate and include model
equations for the phosphate or other nutrients.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
The scope of this project was to a develop a method to detect changes in the FCA signal that was
previously found to be an precursor for metabolic shifts. Two working detection methods were
developed and implemented to monitor online experiments. The experimental work resulted in
11 batches with online monitoring. 8 out of the 11 batches had a metabolic shift and all 8 metabolic
shift were predicted by the detection methods, and the observed detection were 1.5 to 3.3 h
before the metabolic shift. One experiment was carried out to find limitation in the detection methods.
Experiment 2 had changes in the process variables; temperature, agitation and aeration and pressure.
The first three resulted in large changes in the process states and resulted in false detections and thereby
exposed the limitations of the detection methods. To prevent control actions at false detections a su-
pervisory layer was suggested to reject detections if the process variables should be deviating from
their given set point. With the supervisory layer two robust detection methods to predict
metabolic shift had been established.
By applying the FCA method on the measured pH the same decreasing trend as observed in FCA(DO)
appeared for FCA(pH). With this discovery a new metabolic shift ’predictor’ was found and
FCA(pH) could potentially be used for detection in batches without dissolved oxygen measurements
thereby increasing the applicability of the detection methods.
The next step was to test the proposed control action; addition of phosphate at the time of detection.
For one batch it was possible to add phosphate buffer at the time of detection. Because of the addition
of phosphate buffer, no metabolic shift occurred during the remaining batch period. This demon-
strated that addition of phosphate buffer as the control action could prevent metabolic
shifts. If the phosphate is added after the metabolic shift it has little effect. This was concluded an
experiments where the addition of phosphate after the metabolic shift could make the batch return to
DO set point 24 h after the addition of phosphate buffer. However, this was far from satisfactory and
emphasized the success of the detection methods and the proposed control action.
In the last part of the thesis, simulation of the FCA signal was done. It was achieved to resemble the
experimental data by using a split feed where small pulses were added to a main continuous
flow. However the simulation did have shortcomings when it came to simulating the applied changes
in the process variables. The disturbances in agitation and aeration caused the simulation to fail at
the point of the first disturbance period.
The closest fit was obtained for disturbances in pressure
where the simulated and experimental DO followed the same main trend. Simulation with temperature
changes revealed limitation in the used model, since the temperature changes caused larger changes in
the experimental data than for the simulation. This implies that some of the assumptions in the model
do not accommodate changes in temperature, such as assuming constant maximum specific growth rate
µmax and temperature dependent oxygen saturation DO∗could be the reason for the small response to
changes in the temperature.
To investigate the models possibility to simulate metabolic shifts an additional term was added to the
growth expression in the model.
By decreasing this term a metabolic shift was simulated and the
decrease in the FCA signal was expressed in the simulated data. This emphasized the growth rate
influence on the metabolic shift and the DO response corresponding to the FCA signal.
A model was developed that could simulate the decreasing behavior of FCA before a
metabolic shift.

CHAPTER 5
Future Works
From this study two working detection method has been formulated and tested. The next step would
be to test if less phosphate addition would prevent the metabolic shift as well or if it could be possible
to control phosphate addition by the value of the FCA, for example adding phosphate as a continuous
feed until the FCA increases again and stabilizes. The following step would be implementing this as
automatic control with either the continuous addition of phosphate based on the FCA signal or a sep-
arate phosphate boost.
In term of modeling it could be interesting to further develop the model by adding the term of phos-
phate or other nutrient concentration and thereby improve the simulation of the metabolic shifts. Also
additional experiments could look into the other factors of the metabolic shift, for example at sudden
dilution as it was experienced in one of the experiments.
