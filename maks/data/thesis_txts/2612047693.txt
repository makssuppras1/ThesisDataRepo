Abstract 
The increasing awareness regarding the dramatic environmental impact of fossil fuel consumption 
and the limited remaining resources of the latter is driving the development of alternative energy 
conversion and storage technologies. Their ability to resort to environmentally friendly energy vectors 
such as hydrogen would profoundly reduce the impact of anthropogenic activities on the ecosystem. 
Among the aforementioned technologies, electrochemical devices able to produce hydrogen with 
high efficiency and low environmental impact have been widely investigated in the recent past. 
In the last years, particular interest has been paid towards the development of protonic ceramic 
electrolysis cells due to their high efficiency, ability to produce pure and electrochemically 
compressible hydrogen and the possibility of coupling them with waste-heat resources. However, 
their commercialization is hindered by multiple materials challenges such as low sinterability and 
high grain boundary resistance of electrolyte materials, poor chemical stability and electrochemical 
performance of steam/oxygen electrode and others. In this context, this Master Thesis investigated 
two of the main challenges hindering the commercialization of high-temperature water electrolysis 
technologies. Specifically, the preparation of a stable and well-performing composite electrocatalyst 
for oxygen electrode application for protonic ceramic electrolysis cells was attempted to overcome 
the low stability and high polarization resistances of the state-of-the-art materials. Moreover, the 
development of an alternative fuel electrode preparation technique was investigated to achieve higher 
electronic-conducting phase loading in yttria-stabilized zirconia backbones compared to state-of-the-
art methodologies to improve fuel electrode performance in solid oxide electrolysis cells through 
generation of larger electrochemically active surface area and improved microstructure, while 
reducing the costs of electrode manufacturing. 
A composite steam/oxygen electrode based on a doped layered perovskite, able to transport protons, 
oxide ions and electrons simultaneously, infiltrated into a porous proton-conducting backbone was 
successfully manufactured. The parameters affecting the achievement of the desired chemical 
composition and microstructure of the electrocatalyst were investigated, such as the chemical 
composition of the precursor solution, its concentration and viscosity, calcination procedure 
parameters and the number of infiltration cycles. The electrochemical performance of the developed 
catalyst was then analysed by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy. Moreover, the insertion of a 
surface decoration of ex-situ synthesized gadolinium-doped ceria and praseodymium oxide 
nanoparticles was investigated as a suitable technique to improve the oxygen evolution reaction 
kinetics of the composite electrode. The effect of the surface modification on the electrocatalyst 
microstructure and electrochemical performance were investigated. 
Symmetrical cells presenting composite electrodes BaZr0.2Y0.2O3-δ-PrBa0.5Sr0.5Co1.5Fe0.5O5+δ-
Ce0.8Gd0.2O2-δ-Pr6O11 demonstrated promising performance, comparable to that of state-of-the-art 
materials, and represent an encouraging starting point towards the development of microstructurally 
optimised oxygen electrodes via infiltration technique and tailoring of the surface composition of the 
electrocatalyst through the introduction of ex-situ synthesized nanoparticles. 
Simultaneously, the development of an alternative preparation technique for composite fuel 
electrodes for solid oxide electrolysis cells was attempted. The infiltration of ex-situ synthesized 
nickel nanoparticles was considered a possible alternative to nickel precursor solution infiltration to 
maximise the electrochemically active surface area of the fuel electrode and the parameters affecting 
the procedure were investigated.

1. Introduction 
The increasing consumption of the world’s finite fossil fuels resources is generating a large variety 
of environmental challenges and leading to a progressively more complex energetic crisis. In the last 
decades, different alternatives have been proposed and developed to overcome such difficulties, 
involving the development and commercialisation of sustainable devices for energy conversion and 
storage.[1][2][3][4][5] 
Energy production systems such as solar and wind energy technologies have been increasingly 
applied as an alternative to non-renewable technologies in the last decade. Still, they are intrinsically 
dependent on the geographical position and the climate conditions, resulting in a lack of continuity 
and consistency in the power supply.[6][7][8][9] 
Different energy vectors, as substitutes of carbon-based fuels, and technologies have been suggested 
as suitable ways to stabilise the power provision from renewable sources and ensure reduced harmful 
emissions. Using hydrogen as an energy carrier and its derivatives and adopting electrochemical 
devices for its production and conversion represent one of the most viable and promising 
alternatives.[10][11] 
Hydrogen can be easily stored and transported while ensuring minimal undesired emissions when 
converted to generate electricity in electrochemical devices, resulting only in water production as a 
by-product. 
Traditionally, the production of hydrogen takes place through thermochemical processes[12][13], such 
as steam reforming of natural gas and coal gasification. These processes are energy-intensive and 
fraught by challenges like the need for noble metal catalysts, low energetic efficiency, low purity of 
the final product requiring extensive adoption of purification and compression systems. Moreover, 
these technologies rely mainly on the conversion of fossil fuels resulting in broad emissions of 
environmental contaminants and the necessity of applying suitable raw material purification 
processes not to inhibit the catalyst irreversibly.[12][14] 
Water electrolysis represents one of the most viable alternatives for hydrogen production able to 
overcome the previously mentioned challenges and has been going through technological 
breakthroughs in the recent past. Currently, the adoption of this electrochemical technology is 
minimal, representing only 4% of the global hydrogen annual production.[15] 
 
Figure 1: Percentage distribution of hydrogen by production technique (2018) [15] 
The energy efficiency of an electrolyser is not restrained by the Carnot cycle, and harmful emissions 
are drastically reduced. 
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
 
Water electrolysis technologies can be classified according to their principle of operation and charge 
transport species. Among them, the most widely diffused are Alkaline Electrolysis Cells (AECs), 
followed by Proton Exchange Membrane Electrolysis Cells (PEMECs). 
Thanks to extensive research effort for the development and commercialization of well-performing 
and stable Solid Oxide Fuel cells (SOFCs), Solid Oxide Electrolysis cells (SOECs) are currently 
arising as a feasible alternative to low temperature electrolysis technologies.[16] Differently from 
AECs and PEMECs, SOECs are generally based on oxide ion transporting ceramic materials 
operating typically at temperatures higher than 650°C. The high operating temperature not only 
allows to avoid the use of noble metal catalysts, but it also ensures a lower electrical energy demand. 
According to the equation ∆𝐻= ∆𝐺+ 𝑇∆𝑆 where the terms correspond respectively to the total 
energy, the electrical energy and the heat energy demand, at a steam pressure of 1 atm, the total energy 
demand is reduced dramatically at 100°C because of the water phase transition from liquid to gas 
form, as shown in figure 2.[17][18] 
 
Figure 2: Total energy demand for water electrolysis as a function of temperature.[19] 
Additionally, as temperature increases, so does the thermal energy, thus reducing the required 
electrical energy supply. If the thermal energy supply comes from industrial waste heat, operational 
costs are reduced compared to low-temperature electrolysis technologies, while ensuring a high 
hydrogen formation rate.[17][20][21][22][23][24]  
The high operating temperature, unfortunately, corresponds also to several different issues that are 
currently hindering the commercialisation of such technology, such as [4]: 
• significant material degradation due to corrosion, reactions between components and thermal 
stress; 
• complex and expensive complementary equipment; 
• necessary application of expensive sealing materials; 
• limited utilisation in portable applications; 
• slow start-up and shut-down operations. 
While different research groups are carrying out the development of oxide-ion-conducting materials 
able to operate at lower temperatures, the adoption of proton-conducting ceramics as electrolyte 
materials represents a promising alternative to reduce the challenges presented by the high operating 
temperature of oxide ion conducting SOECs.[25] Thanks to their protonic conductivity and the lower 
activation energy for proton transport if compared to that of the oxygen ion[26][27][28], these materials 
can operate at low and intermediate temperature ranges, respectively under 500°C and between 500 
==PAGE:12==
 
12 
 
and 750°C.[29] In turn, this leads to lower plant costs, expanded range of suitable materials[30] and 
removal of the primary degradation mechanism shown by oxide ion transporting electrolysers, thanks 
to the limitation of radiative transfer of heat and material inter-diffusion.[31] Moreover, operating 
temperatures lower than 600°C enhances the efficiency of the electrochemical system, due to lower 
electronic leakage through the bulk of the electrolyte.[32][33][34][35][36][37]  
While the cathode of a protonic ceramic electrolysis cell (PCEC) is generally based on a Ni/proton-
conducting ceramic cermet, the anode is usually composed by perovskite or perovskite-related 
materials. 
 
Figure 3: Representation of PCEC working principles.[39] 
In a PCEC, water splitting and oxygen evolution reaction (OER) take place on the positive electrode, 
which ensures reduced risk of Ni oxidation and coarsening, also resulting in the production of dry 
hydrogen, which could be electrochemically compressed in the cell reducing system cost and 
complexity, while producing diluted oxygen, thus reducing safety concerns.[9] Moreover, the absence 
of water from the fuel electrode ensures the possibility of achieving higher open-circuit voltages 
compared to oxide ion conducting SOECs and reducing the dependency on porosity of the cathode.[39] 
The lower operating temperature of a PCEC ensures reduced thermal stress on the membrane-
electrode assemblies and interconnects, minimizing heat management and ensuring high energy 
efficiency. On the other hand, this corresponds to a limited range of possible current densities when 
operating at the thermoneutral voltage. [9] 
Proton conducting electrolysis cells represent a promising alternative among the power-to-fuel 
technologies as well as for electrochemical ammonia synthesis and natural gas upgrading.[9][40] 
Despite the previously mentioned advantages over other technologies, PCECs still require further 
developments to overcome several challenges that are currently hindering scaling up and 
commercialization, among which[9]: 
• Low chemical stability of anode and electrolyte materials; 
• Device failure caused be chemical expansion due to proton incorporation into the lattice 
of the oxides; 
• Low sinterability and blocking nature of grain boundaries of electrolyte materials; 
• High electrode polarization of anodes; 
• Lack of reliable and cost-effective manufacturing techniques for anode fabrication.

1.1 Electrolyte materials and proton conduction mechanism  
Among the oxides demonstrating proton conduction capabilities, simple perovskites (with general 
formula ABO3) show the highest proton conductivities.[10] This characteristic is generally attributed 
to the minimal distortion from the ideal cubic structure and to their high crystallographic unit cell 
volume, both beneficial to proton mobility upon hydration of the material.[9] The two main classes of 
proton conducting electrolyte materials are yttrium-doped barium zirconates (BZY) and barium 
cerates (BCY). 
Hydration of perovskite materials ensures incorporation of protons as positively charged hydroxide 
defects through the dissociative adsorption of water, described in Krӧger-Vink notation as: 
𝐻2𝑂+ 𝑉𝑂
.. + 𝑂𝑂
𝑥→2𝑂𝐻𝑂
∙ 
where 𝑉𝑂
.. is an oxygen vacancy, 𝑂𝑂
𝑥 is a lattice oxygen and 𝑂𝐻𝑂
∙ is a hydroxide defect at a lattice 
oxygen site.[9][39]   
Proton transport in perovskite oxides can be described as a 2-steps process, illustrated in figure 4 for 
BaZrO3, and known as the Grotthuss mechanism: 
 
Figure 4: Schematic representation of proton transport mechanism in BaZrO3.[39] 
In the first step, a hydroxide defect bends towards the adjacent lattice oxygen ion, and the bond length 
ensures the reduction of the energetic barrier for proton transfer. In the second step, the proton is 
transferred through breaking and formation of weak hydrogen bonds; a process also called proton 
hopping.[29][41] 
A sufficient amount of oxygen vacancies in the lattice is required to achieve a significant proton 
concentration. Generally, doping of a cation with lower oxidation state than the host cation is usually 
carried out to create the necessary vacancies.[9] 
The mobility and incorporation of protons depend on different parameters such as chemical nature 
and characteristics of host cation and acceptor dopant, the relative quantity of doping agent[42] and the 
crystal structure of the material.[9]  
The most commonly used dopant is Y. Its insertion in the structure of the perovskite oxide generates 
a lattice distortion due to the different ionic radii of the cations and can potentially lead to a lower 
symmetry. The lower distortion caused by Y to the lattice of the oxide if compared to other possible 
B-site dopants (e.g. Yb, Gd, Nd or Dy) yields reduced strain energy and increasing defects mobility 
and is the main reason for its broad application. Moreover, Y shows the lowest variation of hydration 
enthalpy of BaZrO3 among all doping candidates, implying that Y and Zr have similar interactions 
with hydroxide ions and oxygen vacancies of the lattice.[9] Y seems to ensure the lowest effect among 
the proposed dopants in terms of interaction with proton defects, also called proton trapping, which 
strongly hinders proton transport.[43] 
==PAGE:14==
 
14 
 
While barium zirconates show high chemical and thermo-mechanical stabilities, they exhibit lower 
conductivity compared to barium cerates and poor sinterability, generally yielding membranes with 
low densities and small grains. The high grain boundary resistance of zirconates, together with their 
poor sinterability, also results in high grain boundary densities.[9]  
The use of an acceptor dopant generates basic oxygen sites that favour proton incorporation and 
improve solubility and stability of protonic defects in the lattice of the perovskite oxide. On the other 
hand, oxides with a more basic character tend to react with H2O and acidic gases such as CO2 
generating respectively undesired hydroxide and carbonate phases, explaining the lower stability of 
cerates compared to zirconates. [9] 
The strong metal-oxygen bond of zirconates not only justifies their high stability but is also expected 
to hinder the mobility of defects and consequently proton conductivity.  
A large variety of material modification and innovative manufacturing techniques have been 
suggested in the recent past to overcome the issues presented by both cerates and zirconates. For 
example, better sinterability of BaZrO3 has been achieved by introducing sintering aids such as LiNO3 
and NiO.[39][44] Alternatively, materials based on solid solutions of cerates and zirconates (BZCY) have 
been developed and nowadays represent the majority of the used materials for the fabrication of 
electrolytes. Optimal conductivity, sinterability and stability are achieved as a result of a trade-off 
between the relative quantities of cerium and zirconium in the material.[9][39] Doping strategies were 
also proposed as suitable ways to achieve the desired characteristics.[36][45][46][47] Encouraging results 
have also been achieved through the adoption of solid-state reactive sintering which, through a single 
high-temperature sintering step, simultaneously allows to achieve phase formation, densification and 
grain growth.[48] 
One of the main challenges presented by PCEC is the low faradaic efficiency due to parasitic transport 
losses and reactions of defects in the bulk of the electrolyte and at its interface with the anode, the 
most important of which is probably represented by p-type electronic conduction across the 
electrolyte.[38][49] Both barium cerate and zirconates are mixed proton, oxygen and electron hole 
conductors. The transference number of each charge carrier depends on the composition of the gas 
atmosphere, the temperature, the chemical composition of the material and the current density of 
operation.[42] It was observed that, in reducing conditions, the total conductivity of BaZr0.8Y0.2O3-δ 
(BZY20) increases with anode and cathode humidification due to improved proton conductivity. In 
dry atmospheres oxide ion transport is favoured, while protonic transport progressively becomes the 
main mechanism at higher steam partial pressures. Higher steam partial pressures also ensure lower 
oxygen and hydrogen partial pressure gradients, favourable for electrolysis operation because of the 
reduced open-circuit voltage achieved.[50] 
Among the various strategies proposed to improve the energy efficiency of PCEC, reduction of the 
ohmic resistance has been achieved by the fabrication of thin-film electrolytes by a variety of 
techniques and by carefully tailoring the heat treatment parameters, with encouraging results in terms 
of improved proton conductivity and efficiency.[51][52]  
The encouraging results obtained for the development and fabrication of well-performing electrolytes 
have moved the focus of research on the development of stable and well-performing anode materials, 
now representing the main barrier towards the spread application of PCEC. 
As mentioned previously, the introduction of sintering aids and alternative processing methodologies 
allows overcoming difficulties related to the low sinterability, small grain size and density and high 
grain boundary resistance typical of barium zirconates. The ability to produce electrolyte-supported 
==PAGE:15==
 
15 
 
cells with porous backbones of proton-conducting material opens up the possibility of achieving 
higher control on the microstructure of the cell and thus better performance. The infiltration of 
electrocatalyst on pre-sintered backbones allows splitting the high-temperature annealing in a two-
step procedure, ensuring optimization of the characteristics of both electrolyte and electrode phases, 
with the first sintering process producing a dense, large-grained electrolyte, and the second 
calcination step yielding nanometer-sized catalyst particles with the desired morphology, coverage 
and adhesion between electrolyte and electrocatalyst phases.[53] Moreover, the adoption of composite 
electrode constituted by the proton-conducting electrolyte phase and electrocatalyst can alleviate the 
issues related to the thermal stability of many catalysts due to their high thermal expansion 
coefficients (TECs). 
To develop a suitable electrocatalyst, the work of this project has been carried out on symmetrical 
BZY20 electrolyte-supported cells, supplied by Sandrine Ricote from Colorado School of Mines. The 
cells were composed by a thick 800 μm BZY20 electrolyte obtained by the uniaxial pressing of 
BZY20 powder mixture with 1 wt% NiO as a sintering aid, followed by sintering, and by symmetrical 
porous BZY20 backbones (≈ 60 - 80 μm thickness) obtained by brush-painting of a BZY20 slurry 
with NiO 4 wt.% and 3 wt.% ethyl cellulose in alpha terpineol, followed by annealing.  
1.2 Fuel electrode structure and preparation by impregnation 
The cathode of a SOEC is formed by a Ni/oxide-ion-conducting ceramic cermet, fabricated through 
a variety of methodologies. Among the possible techniques, infiltration of a nickel precursor solution 
has been proposed as an effective and cost-efficient strategy. Multiple sources[54][55][56] claim that 
infiltrated electrode ensure higher triple phase boundary (TPB) density and the possibility of carefully 
controlling the particle size of the infiltrated phase and porosity of the electrode by tuning the sintering 
and infiltration parameters. Moreover, infiltration of a nickel precursor solution lowers the 
percolation threshold, reducing production costs relative to raw materials, and less energy-intensive 
procedures are required to attain the desired structure.[55] 
Traditionally, fuel electrodes are prepared by mechanically mixing electrolyte and conducting phase. 
The reduction of NiO precursor used in this case not only generates the Ni conducting phase but is 
also fundamental to create the required porosity. In infiltrated electrodes, instead, the porous structure 
of the backbone is generated through the use of different pore-formers. The conducting phase is 
subsequently obtained through the infiltration of a Ni(NO3)2 solution followed by annealing to 
decompose the nitrate to the oxide phase and high-temperature reduction to convert the oxide phase 
to metallic Ni. [54][55][56] 
It is evident that the infiltration procedure still requires the application of high-temperature steps 
(≥350°C for NiO formation and ≥ 800°C for reduction of NiO), consequently affecting not only the 
production cost of the cells but also the environmental impact of their manufacturing process. Further 
reducing the number of energy-intensive procedures would be beneficial for the commercialization 
of SOECs and SOFCs. 
In the recent past, extensive focus has been paid towards the development of methodologies able to 
produce metallic nanoparticles.[57][58][59] The proposed techniques can vary from wet chemical methods 
to biochemical synthesis and have shown the opportunity of creating well-dispersed Ni nanoparticle 
suspensions, although generally of low concentrations of approximately 0.035 M through the 
insertion of mixed surfactant systems.[59][60] A well-established procedure is the modified polyol 
==PAGE:16==
 
16 
 
method, which shows consistency and possibility to scale-up although requiring harmful 
chemicals.[57][58] 
As demonstrated in other applications, the infiltration of as-prepared nanoparticles allows a further 
degree of control on the microstructure of the electrode.[61] 
Manufacturing of symmetrical cells by tape-casting, lamination and co-sintering is generally easier 
than producing asymmetric cells, especially when air/oxygen electrode materials with high thermal 
expansion coefficient are adopted. One of the main challenges is the possibility of infiltrating high 
amounts of electron-conducting phase. 
To the author’s knowledge, the preparation of fuel electrode for SOECs by infiltration of dispersed 
Ni nanoparticles suspensions has never been attempted. 
As part of this project, the infiltration of Ni nanoparticles produced by modified polyol method on 
porous yttria-stabilized zirconia (YSZ) half cells has been attempted for SOEC fuel electrode 
preparation. This procedure aims to propose an alternative technique for the infiltration of high 
amounts of electron-conducting phase. Simultaneously, it is expected to be a less energy-demanding 
alternative for fuel electrode manufacturing while ensuring a higher catalytically active surface area 
and tunable electrode morphology. Moreover, this methodology could be further improved by 
adopting less harmful reagents.  
1.3 Strategies and materials for anode development 
In the presence of a well-performing electrolyte, the electrode polarization overpotential becomes the 
dominating factor affecting PCEC performance due to a sluggish catalyst kinetics. 
Steam electrodes are generally fabricated from oxides belonging to three main classes of materials: 
simple perovskites (ABO3-δ), double (or layered) perovskites (A’AB2O5+δ) and Ruddlesden-Popper 
oxides (A2BO4+δ). 
The materials traditionally adopted for the fabrication of steam electrodes for PCEC application are 
generally derived from the research for oxide-ion transporting SOECs. These materials are mixed 
ionic-electronic conductors (MIECs) with high conductivities for electrons and oxide ions and high 
catalytic activity for the OER. Due to their low or absent proton conductivity, their application in 
PCEC limits the electrochemically active surface to the TPB, the interface between proton conducting 
electrolyte, electrode and gas phase, thus virtually to the area of contact between electrode and 
electrolyte. 
For a PCEC to be operated efficiently, it is of fundamental importance that the electrode would show 
not only conduction capabilities for oxide ion and electrons but also protons to extend the catalytically 
active surface area.[62][63] This kind of materials is generally defined as triple conducting oxides 
(TCOs). It can overcome the issues due to relatively slow electrode kinetics, especially if it shows a 
wide operating temperature range.[62][64] Different studies have highlighted that the conductivity of 
TCOs is strongly affected by their hydration and their temperature of operation, showing that these 
materials tend to behave as triple conductors in the intermediate temperature range and as mixed 
oxide ion and electrons conductors at higher temperatures.[65][66]  An electrode with sufficient

When considering MIECs that might show proton transport behaviours, layered perovskites 
LnBaCo2O5+δ (with Ln = Pr, Gd, Y, Sm, Nd) gave interesting results due to their high activity towards 
OER and reactions involving protonic species. A large variety of doping strategies and electrode 
fabrication techniques have been attempted to improve their performance with encouraging results, 
for their application in both PCECs and O-SOECs.[38][70][79][80][81][82][83][84][85][86][87][88] 
Grimaud et al.[89] found that layered perovskite oxides could show high proton transport capabilities 
thanks to the high achievable hydration level due to their extensive oxygen deficiency. When these 
oxides were compared with low oxygen-deficient perovskite such as La0.6Sr0.4Co0.2Fe0.8O3-δ (LSCF), 
the tests showed that improved electrode performance was obtained with increasing steam partial 
pressure, indicating that reactions involving both O2- and H+ take place on the surface of the 
catalyst.[89]  
These materials show an ordering of the A-site cations, with alternated Ln-O and Ba-O layers along 
the c-axis, generating oxygen vacancies due to the different valence of the metal cations. These 
vacancies are generally localized on the Ln-O plane because the large Ba2+ cations tendentially 
acquire 12-fold oxygen coordination, differently from Ln.   
The presence of oxygen vacancies δ can vary largely between 0 and 1 and are subject to an ordering 
around δ = 0.5 yielding an orthorhombic crystal with space group Pmmm with lower symmetry if 
compared to the ideal cubic structure of perovskite, in which CoO5 square pyramids and CoO6 
octahedra alternate along the b-axis.[70][81] When δ is lower or higher than 0.5, the crystal tends to 
acquire a higher symmetry tetragonal structure with a P4/mmm space group.  
The amount of oxygen vacancies increases with decreasing size of Ln3+, strongly affecting the crystal 
structure.[70][81] For instance, La gives a cubic structure because no oxygen deficiency is generated 
while Pr and Nd give highly oxygen deficient systems with tetragonal crystals.  
Oxygen vacancies reversibly vary as a function of oxygen partial pressure and temperature and 
depending on the parameters of the high-temperature treatment during the synthesis of the catalyst, 
due to reduction/oxidation of the Co cation. [70][81]

LnBaCo2O5+δ oxides go through structural transitions upon heating due to changes in the spin-state

Spin-state transitions are commonly observed for oxides with intermediate levels of oxygen vacancies 
in a temperature range between 0 and 200°C and correspond to a considerable improvement in 
electronic conductivity especially for Ln = La and Pr for which small polarons conducting behaviours 
have been suggested.[81] 
For higher temperatures, oxygen vacancies disordering yields the random localization of the latter in 
the Ln-O plane, causing a change in the crystal structure of the compound without affecting lattice

transition takes place decreases with decreasing oxygen partial pressures.[81] This transition towards 
disordered oxygen vacancies improves the bulk oxygen diffusion property of the material.[81] 
Further increment in temperature can yield oxygen loss and deterioration of electronic conductivity.[81] 
Among the possible ways of controlling the oxygen content of the layered perovskite, insertion of Fe 
in place of Co increases the average oxidation state of (Co,Fe) and the average oxygen content. 
Moreover, as already mentioned, the thermodynamic stability of the compound is improved because 
of the higher (Co,Fe)-O bonding energy. However, the electronic and oxide ion conductivities and 
electrochemical properties, in general, are penalized. [70][81] 
The high TEC of Co-based perovskites is known to be related mainly to chemical expansion caused 
by changes in the chemical composition of the compound in terms of oxygen loss. Moreover, the high 
TECs of these compounds has also been related to the spin-state transition taking place with 
increasing temperatures.[81] LnBaCo2O5+δ with Ln of smaller sizes seems to show lower TEC values. 
For example, it has been demonstrated that structures based on Pr and Nd tend to show anisotropic 
lattice expansion along the a,b-axes with low oxygen partial pressure while contracting along the c-
axis due to the increasing concentration of oxygen vacancies on the Ln-O plane and that the chemical 
expansion of Pr-structures with careful doping of Fe in place of Co is negligible if compared to 
LSCF.[81] On the other hand, Sr-doping appears deleterious for TEC values and area-specific 
resistance (ASR). [70][81] 
Employing composite electrodes of LnBaCo2O5+δ and electrolyte material would not only improve 
performance due to extended catalytic surface area, but it would also reduce the thermochemical 
stress on the electrode, minimizing the negative impact of the high TECs of layered cobaltites. 
Unfortunately the obtained lower ASR also corresponds to lower electronic conductivity. [70] 
The substitution of Sr for Ba has been attempted to modify the physical characteristics of the systems. 
The insertion of a cation with a smaller radius, if compared to Ba, decreases the size difference with 
Ln3+, thus reducing unit cell volume and cell parameters.[81] Sr also seems to stabilize phases at high 
temperatures.

Sr-substitution increases the oxygen content yielding structural transitions to more symmetric 
systems and resulting in an increase in charge carrier (electronic holes) and in the relaxation of 
compressive stress on the O-Co-O bond which hinders electronic conductivity in compounds with 
relatively small Ln cations. These modifications ensure better electronic conductivity through the 
bulk of the perovskite. Electronic conductivity in perovskite-related oxides occurs through electron 
hopping along the Co4+-O-Co3+ bonds; thus an increase in oxygen vacancies impedes such pathway 
and consequently hinders electronic conductivity. Sr-doping counteracts this phenomenon. [70][90] 
The oxygen bulk diffusion is thought to occur by a vacancy transport mechanism. At a given 
temperature, the oxygen permeation flux decreases with decreasing size of the lanthanide cation 
because of lattice strain. The oxygen transport occurs through movement from the vacancy rich Ln-
O plane to the closest neighbouring site in the Co-O plane.[81] 
Pr-based compounds, as well as those based on Nd and Gd, appear to ensure a good trade-off between 
electrochemical performance and TEC. Among them, one of the best performing and stable state-of-
the-art catalysts can be identified, PrBa0.5Sr0.5Co1.5Fe0.5O5+δ (PBSCF).[38] 
Haile et al. [38] achieved an astonishing current density of -1.8 A/cm2 and an operating voltage of 1.3V 
at 600°C by developing a microstructurally optimized electrolysis cell. The creation of a catalyst 
functional layer by pulsed laser deposition (PLD) was carried out previous to depositing the electrode 
by drop-casting, and the cell was cathode-supported with the cathode made of a Ni-BCZYYb4411 
cermet. A significantly reduced ohmic resistance was obtained thanks to the improved contact 
between anode and electrolyte, ensuring an exceptional faradaic efficiency of 76%, stable cyclic 
operation between electrolysis and fuel cell modes and minimal loss of electrochemical 
characteristics during a 500 hours continuous operation in electrolysis mode. 
It appears evident that the creation of a suitable interface between electrode and electrolyte is 
fundamental to obtain a well-performing cell. The differences in terms of TEC and chemical 
expansion coefficient between electrode and electrolyte can result in poor adhesion and consequently 
lead to poor electrochemical behaviour and short lifetime of the device due to delamination of the 
electrode.[40] Applying an interfacial layer between the two phases by adopting PLD helps to overcome 
such an issue.[9] Still, it is not feasible at a larger scale due to the high cost of the latter technology. 
Dubois et al.[91] showed that material manufacturing accounts for 30 % of the total production cost of 
an electrochemical cell. Adopting manufacturing techniques and chemical precursors able to reduce 
costs would make the production of electrolysis cell more cost-competitive if compared to traditional 
hydrogen production processes.  
Among the possible methodologies able to ensure contained manufacturing costs and appreciably 
increased catalyst performance and stability, infiltration appears particularly attractive because it 
could be easily scaled-up through inkjet printing, and it has been widely applied.[84][85][86][92] 
Infiltration of catalyst nanoparticles onto a sintered porous backbone of electrolyte material ensures 
the possibility of tailoring the electrode microstructure by varying parameters such as the composition 
of the catalyst precursor solution and calcination parameters. The formation in situ of nanometer size 
catalyst particles allows the creation of an electrode with a wide electrochemically active surface area 
and extended interfacial connection with the electrolyte, resulting in improved performance compared 
to the electrode prepared by mechanical mixing of electrode and electrolyte phases.[84][85][86][92][93] 
Different groups[84][85][86][92] demonstrated the possibility of producing PrBaCo2O5+δ and related 
compounds by infiltration for both SOECs and PCECs, showing encouraging results[92], although not 
comparable to those obtained by Haile et al.[38]

A commonly adopted strategy to ensure improved electrochemical characteristics of electrodes is the 
insertion of a surface modification by the infiltration of nanoparticles of specific materials showing 
the desired features in terms of conduction and reaction kinetics. This strategy has been widely 
applied in both SOEC and PCEC development.[61][94] 
Saqib et al. showed that the infiltration of Sm0.2Ce0.8O2-δ (SDC) nanoparticles through a precursor 
solution could enhance the electrochemical performance and stability of a well known TCO such as 
BaCo0.4Zr0.1Fe0.4Y0.1O3-δ (BCZFY), remarkably reducing the polarization losses of the electrode. The 
insertion of SDC nanoparticles extends the electrochemically active surface area and expands the 
number of possible conduction pathways. [94]  
The strategy proposed by Saqib et al. successfully achieved a better performance because oxygen 
migration is considered to be the rate-determining step (RDS) of the OER on BCZFY. [94]  
Although this is not the case in layered perovskites such as PBSCF, the insertion of doping agents in 
the structure of the oxide was demonstrated to affect negatively oxide ion and electron transport 
through the bulk of the catalyst. The infiltration of nanometer-size MIEC like SDC could then 
improve the performance of the catalyst analogously, by supplying alternative conduction pathways 
and improved OER activity. 
Similarly, Tong et al.[61] demonstrated that the activity and stability of a La0.5Sr0.5FeO3-δ electrode of 
a SOEC could be strongly enhanced by infiltration of a solution mixture of Pr(NO3)3 and colloidal 
Ce0.85Gd0.15O2-δ (CGO15) nanoparticles. It is claimed that not only the composition of the coating but 
also its morphology can be carefully controlled by infiltration of as-prepared nanoparticles. The two 
infiltrated species not only ensure better surface oxygen exchange kinetics, but the insertion of 
CGO15 nanoparticles can avoid Pr6O11 nanoparticle coarsening, leading to improved long term 
stability. 
To the best of my knowledge, no attempt to prepare by infiltration the doubly Sr- and Fe-doped 
PrBaCo2O5+δ, PBSCF, has been carried out for its application in PCECs. This project focused on the 
preparation of  PBSCF composite anodes through the infiltration of a precursor solution into the 
aforementioned BZY20 cells. In an attempt to improve catalytic performance, the infiltration of 
Ce0.8Gd0.2O2-δ (CGO20) and PrOx nanoparticles has been carried out. The surface modification by the 
insertion of MIEC nanoparticles is supposed to counterbalance the adverse effects of Sr and Fe 
dopants on the catalyst electrochemical behaviour by boosting electronic and oxide ion conductivity 
and OER kinetics.  
1.4 Project objective and challenges 
The main focus of this project was to develop a well-performing and stable composite anode for 
PCEC application. As previously mentioned, the main issues of state-of-the-art electrode materials 
are the high polarization resistance at the anode, hindering the faradaic efficiency of the system, and 
the low stability of the catalytic system when exposed to high steam partial pressure. Among the best 
performing materials, PBSCF has shown encouraging electrochemical performance and appreciable 
stability.[38] On the other hand, to achieve this result, complex and non-scalable techniques have been 
applied. 
This project aims to produce competitive electrochemical performance by applying cost-effective 
methodologies for cell components manufacture while investigating the possibility of enhancing the 
electrochemical behaviour of a triple conducting catalyst by carefully tailoring the electrode 
microstructure through the infiltration of MIECs nanoparticles synthesized ex-situ. 
==PAGE:23==
 
23 
 
While the preparation of porous backbones is proposed to overcome the issues related to sinterability, 
conductivity and stability of the barium zirconate electrolyte and porous backbone, infiltration is 
presented as a valuable alternative for the fabrication of composite electrocatalyst because of the high 
control on the final microstructure, good adhesion between the PBSCF and zirconate phases and 
improved resistance to thermal stress of the composite electrode. An intermediate value of the 
polarization resistance, of approximately 0.1 Ω cm2, is expected compared to those obtained by Wu 
et al.[77] through a self-architectured ultra-porous 3D electrode and those achieved by Choi et al.[34] by 
adopting PLD. 
Moreover, the infiltration of MIECs nanoparticles is expected to improve the OER kinetics by the 
creation of favourable pathways for oxide ion transport, overcoming the limitations introduced in 
PBSCF by the Sr- and Fe-doping, further reducing the polarization resistance of the electrode. The 
synthesis of nearly monodispersed CGO20 and Pr2O3 nanoparticles is supposed to ensure a higher 
control on the surface area and morphology[61] compared with the formation in situ of these species 
by the infiltration of chemical precursors. 
Similarly, this project investigated an alternative procedure for the production of cathodes by 
infiltration of Nickel nanoparticles yielding larger electrochemically active surface area due to 
reduced and controllable particle size[58], easily tailored morphology and, thus, improved 
performance. The study was carried out on YSZ porous backbones due to the limited availability of 
symmetrical BZY20 electrolyte-supported cells. 
This technique could be a feasible alternative to nickel nitrate infiltration for large scale 
manufacturing. Using tape casting to produce symmetric backbones that could be infiltrated with 
large amounts nickel nanoparticles would reduce process complexity and ensure higher control on 
the microstructure of the cells compared to the preparation of asymmetric cells. Moreover, this 
methodology could lead to a reduction of production costs thanks to the lower energy requirements 
of the high-temperature processing and the lower amounts of Nickel precursor necessary for the 
creation of a percolating conductive network. This eventuality could be assessed only by evaluating 
the economical impact of the various sources of expenditure, such as raw materials and energy 
requirements of processing. This analysis is out of the scope of this project.

2.1 Electrocatalyst synthesis 
2.1.1 PrBa0.5Sr0.5Co1.5Fe0.5O5+δ precursor solutions preparation 
For the preparation of the precursor solution of the electrocatalyst, the use of different types of 
precursors was attempted, following similar methodologies to those found in the available 
literature.[38][82][83][84][85][86][87] The composition of the precursor solution strongly affects the chemical 
nature of the infiltrated electrode and its morphology. Thus, the careful choice of suitable precursors, 
chelating agent and calcination parameters, is of fundamental importance for the electrochemical 
behaviour of the catalyst.[85] 
In the first place, the preparation of a nitrate precursors solution was attempted. Stoichiometric 
amounts of the metal nitrates (Pr(NO3)3*6H2O, Ba(NO3)2, Sr(NO3)2, Co(NO3)3*6H2O, 
Fe(NO3)3*9H2O) were dissolved in distilled water together with citric acid monohydrate in molar 
ratio to the metal cations of 1,5:1. Thereafter, EDTA in 1:1 molar ratio to the metal cations was added, 
together with suitable amounts of aqueous NH3 32 wt.% to bring the pH to 8. A 0.1 M and a 0.25 M 
solutions were obtained. An aliquot of the 0.1 M solution was heated at 80°C under magnetic stirring 
until gelation. Subsequently, the gel was dried at 200°C for 12 hours and calcined at various 
temperatures (700, 800 and 900°C) to determine the minimum temperature required to obtain the 
==PAGE:26==
 
26 
 
desired PBSCF phase. Part of the remaining solution was infiltrated on CGO20 and 
BaCe0.2Zr0.7Y0.1O3-δ (BCZY27) powders by dripping a small aliquot of the solution on the powders 
and mixing to study the eventual reaction between the various phases during calcination at different 
temperatures, respectively 700, 800 and 900°C.  
To the remaining part of the solution, Triton X-100 equivalent to 10 wt.% the mass of the metal 
cations was added to improve the wetting properties of the solution.    
To achieve a higher concentration of the precursor solution, acetate salts of the metals were dissolved 
in distilled water in stoichiometric amount to obtain a concentration of 0.5 M, with 10 wt.% Triton 
X-100 as a wetting agent. The preparation of an equivalent solution containing glycine (molar ratio 
2:1 to metal cations) as a chelating agent was also attempted. Part of it was brought to gelation by 
heating at 80°C followed by drying at 350°C for 5 hours and calcination at 800°C for 3 hours. The 
solution containing glycine resulted unstable, yielding a precipitate after 24 hours. On the other hand, 
the solution containing acetates appeared super-saturated. Thus, to ensure its homogeneity, before its 
use, the solution was heated to 60°C, and its volume was carefully followed to provide a stable 
concentration value. 
The acetate solution was infiltrated on BCZY27 powder and annealed at 800°C for 3 hours to study 
phase stability and possible undesired reactions. 
2.1.2 PrBa0.5Sr0.5Co1.5Fe0.5O5+δ precursor solutions infiltration 
Both the nitrates and the acetates solutions were infiltrated on YSZ half-cells from which nickel was 
etched and BZY20 half-cells to study the obtained microstructure and the required number of 
infiltration cycles. Ni was etched by reducing the half-cells for 6 hours at 600°C in 3% H2/Ar, 
subsequently immersing the reduced half cells in a suitable volume of 3 M HNO3  aqueous solution 
for 2 hours. 
Infiltration was generally carried out by applying a small aliquot of the solution on the surface of the 
cell and left to wet the porous support for a few minutes. Subsequently, the superficial excess was 
removed by lightly dabbing with a tissue, and the half-cell was placed in a furnace at 350°C for 30 
minutes to remove the solvent and decompose the precursor salts. The procedure was repeated 
multiple times previous to a final calcination step at the appropriate temperature.  
Once determined the required number of infiltration cycles to obtain the desired coverage, the same 
infiltration technique was adopted for two sets of symmetrical BZY20 cells. The six porous cells were 
infiltrated with a solution 0.5 M of acetate precursors through 8 cycles, equivalent to approximately 
2.75 wt.% of PBSCF on the porous backbone.  
2.2 Ex-situ nanoparticles synthesis 
2.2.1 Nickel nanoparticles synthesis 
Nickel nanoparticles were synthesised by applying a modified polyol method following the 
procedures proposed by Banik et al.[58] and Couto and coworkers[57]. This procedure ensures the 
possibility of carefully controlling the size of the nanoparticles by varying the composition of the 
precursor solution in terms of the amount of reducing agent, solvent composition and pH of the 
solution. These parameters also affect the required heating time and the maximum temperature 
attained during the synthesis. According to Banik et al.[58] and Couto and coworkers[57], nickel 
nanoparticles of approximately 10 nm of average diameter and composed by pure metallic face-
centred-cubic nickel are obtained. Moreover, the particles should show non-ideal super-
paramagnetism due to dipolar magnetic coupling between nanoparticles.  
==PAGE:27==
 
27 
 
The reducing agent adopted was N2H4*H2O, as suggested by Banik et al. [58]. Aqueous NaOH was 
used to control pH, and the introduction of a capping agent was necessary to avoid irreversible particle 
agglomeration. Similarly to Couto et al.[57], polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP) was adopted as the capping 
agent among the large variety of possible alternatives.[95] 
In this procedure, nickel nanoparticles are synthesized by reducing a Ni2+ precursor according to the 
reaction: 
2𝑁𝑖2+ + 𝑁2𝐻4 + 4𝑂𝐻−→2𝑁𝑖+ 𝑁2 + 4𝐻2𝑂 
Keeping the pH of the reaction mixture at the desired value between 12 and 13 is of fundamental 
importance to avoid the formation of undesired products such as Ni(OH)2 and Ni2O2OH.  
In the typical procedure, a 0.1M aqueous solution of NiCl2*6H2O was added to ethylene glycol by 
gentle stirring yielding a clear green solution, followed by the addition of an equimolar amount of 
PVP. Subsequently, the reducing agent, hydrazine monohydrate, was added in large excess, and the 
solution turned blue, indicating the formation of the [Ni(N2H4)2]+2 complex. After continuous stirring, 
the solution turned into a turbid blue-violet colour. At that point, appropriate amounts of 0.5M 
aqueous NaOH solution were added to maintain the pH value between 12 and 13. The reaction 
mixture was then heated in a hot silicone oil bath under vigorous magnetic stirring. The change in 
colour of the solution to black after heating for 15 minutes up to 60°C was observed, indicating the 
formation of Ni nanoparticles. The changes in colour mentioned above correspond to the formation 
of different chemical species in the reaction system, as shown in figure 7. 
 
Figure 7: Colour change and corresponding chemical species in Nickel nanoparticles synthesis procedure. 
At that point, the heating was stopped, and the reaction mixture was left for 10 more minutes in the 
hot oil bath. The solution was subsequently cooled to room temperature, and the nanoparticles were 
separated by centrifugation and washed multiple times with distilled water. Finally, the particles were 
dried in a vacuum desiccator.  
Known amounts of nanoparticles were dispersed in various solvents to understand which one would 
give a stable and sufficiently concentrated solution to be used in the infiltration of half cells. The four 
solvent systems investigated were distilled water, ethanol, MEKET and toluene. The choice of 
toluene was dictated by the non-availability of less harmful non-polar solvents. Neither of the four 
ensured the possibility of creating a concentrated stable dispersion. It could be easily observed that 
the previously mentioned magnetic behaviour of the nanoparticles leads to the creation of dispersible 
soft agglomerates and consequently to the precipitation of the metallic aggregates. 
The most stable dispersion was obtained by using MEKET and a concentration of Ni nanoparticles 
equivalent to approximately 0.2 M, which showed complete precipitation of the nanoparticles after 4 
minutes. Less stable dispersions were obtained through the adoption of ethanol. Toluene and water 
dispersions showed respectively no dispersion capability (due to the solubility of PVP in polar 
solvents) and aggregation of the nanoparticles on the walls of the vessel, probably due to the 
displacement of the capping agent from the surface of the nanoparticles.

2.2.2 Ce0.8Gd0.2O2-δ and Pr2O3 nanoparticles synthesis 
The synthesis of CGO20 and Pr2O3 nanoparticles was carried out according to the procedures 
proposed by Taniguchi et al.[96] and Jung and coworkers[97], respectively. 
Taniguchi and coworkers[96] claimed the possibility of synthesizing CGO20 spherical nanoparticles 
with an average size of 3,2 nm by the hydrolytic condensation of the cerium-oleate complex by the 
addition of aqueous ammonia. The product can be easily dispersed in non-polar solvents. 
In a typical synthesis of 1.2 g of CGO20 nanoparticles, 0.0056 mol of (NH4)2Ce(NO3)6 and 0.0014 
mol GdCl3*6H2O (molar ratio Ce:Gd = 4:1) were dissolved in 30 mL of distilled water. Sodium oleate 
was synthesized by dissolving oleic acid and NaOH in equimolar amounts in ethanol 70 wt.% and 
left stirring overnight at room temperature. The obtained sodium oleate was dried in vacuum at room 
temperature and formed a white solid. 
At the same time, 0.007 mol of sodium oleate were subsequently dissolved in 30 mL of water, and 
the solution was slowly added under vigorous stirring to the salt precursors solution. Thereafter, 
suitable amounts of aqueous NH3 25 wt.% were added to the reaction mixture which was placed in 
an autoclave and brought to 200°C for 6 hours under autogenous pressure. The products were 
subsequently separated by centrifugation, washed in water twice and dried at 150°C for 6 hours. 
Toluene was used to create a nanoparticles suspension, and non-dispersible agglomerates were 
separated by centrifugation, dried and weighted to determine the effective concentration of the 
dispersion. The volume of the dispersion was subsequently adjusted to obtain a concentration of 
approximately 0.15 M. 
Due to the lack of suitable synthetic routes for the synthesis of the dispersible Pr6O11, the synthesis 
of Pr2O3 nanoplates by thermal decomposition of the acetate precursor in the presence of oleylamine 
and oleic acid was attempted. The less thermodynamically stable Pr2O3 phase is converted to Pr6O11 
when exposed to a temperature above 600°C. Through this procedure, Jung et al. [97] demonstrated the 
possibility of creating ultra-thin nanoplates with a thickness of approximately 1,1 nm and a width of 
about 20 nm, readily dispersible in non-polar solvents. 
Conventionally, 0.004 mol of praseodymium acetate hydrate are dissolved in a solvent mixture 
composed by 0.12 mol of oleylamine and 0.036 mol of oleic acid by placing the solution in a vacuum 
oven at 90°C and 200 mbar until the hydration water is removed and a clear yellow solution is 
obtained. Subsequently, the solution is placed in a furnace at 320°C for 2 hours in Ar atmosphere to 
decompose the acetate and get the oxide phase. The nanoplates are then precipitated by adding 200 
mL of ethanol to the reaction mixture and separated by centrifugation. The products are then dried in 
a vacuum desiccator and dispersed in an adequate volume of toluene to obtain a 0.076 M dispersion 
of Pr2O3, equivalent to a concentration of 0.025 M of Pr6O11. 
The solution adopted for the infiltration of CGO20 and Pr2O3 was obtained by dispersing both species 
in the same volume of toluene to optimize the time of the infiltration procedure, yielding a single 
dispersion with concentrations of CGO20 and Pr2O3 equal to 0.15 M and 0.076 M, respectively. 
2.2.3 Nanoparticles infiltration 
Two kinds of transitorily stable nickel nanoparticles dispersions were adopted to carry out infiltration 
tests, respectively a dispersion in ethanol and dispersions in MEKET at three different concentrations. 
The solutions were placed on a shaking table to ensure their homogeneity during the infiltration 
process. Infiltration tests were conducted onto etched YSZ half cells. 
Different infiltration techniques have been attempted to maximise the achievable nickel coverage of 
the porous backbone through multiple cycles: submerging the cells in the dispersion followed by 
drying on the hotplate, carrying out the infiltration on a strong neodymium magnet to try to force the 
==PAGE:29==
 
29 
 
particles to enter the backbone due to their magnetic properties or by applying a small aliquot of the 
dispersion on the cell followed by removal of the superficial excess and drying on a hotplate. 
The half-cells were subsequently heat-treated at 350°C for 1 hour to remove the capping agent and 
reduced for 2 hours at 600°C to ensure no NiO was present. 
The infiltration of CGO20 and Pr2O3 nanoparticles followed a procedure similar to that adopted for 
PBSCF, with intermediate drying steps in a furnace at 350°C and a final calcination step at 600°C for 
1 hour. The surface modification accounted for approximately 0.02 wt.% of the weights of the cells, 
with a molar ratio CGO20/Pr6O11 equal to 6.

3. Results and Discussion 
As mentioned in section 1.4, this project had the objectives of developing a stable and well-
performing composite anode for PCEC application via infiltration technique and investigating the 
possibility of preparing fuel electrodes for SOECs through the infiltration of nickel nanoparticles 
synthesized ex-situ. 
The first step of this study regarded the characterization of the non-infiltrated YSZ and BZY20 
backbones adopted for the infiltration and electrochemical tests. 
The fuel electrode investigation required the characterization of the synthesized Ni nanoparticles. 
Subsequently, the effect of different parameters on the possibility of generating the desired electrode, 
such as the adopted solvent and infiltration procedure, were investigated. The various factors affecting 
the infiltration process were taken into account to determine the feasibility of the proposed 
methodology. 
Simultaneously, the preparation of the composite anode required to establish the chemical 
compatibility between the electrode components. Subsequently, to obtain the desired chemical 
composition and microstructure of the infiltrated electrocatalyst, the optimal composition of the 
catalyst precursor solution and suitable calcination and infiltration procedures parameters were 
determined. 
The introduction of a surface decoration composed of ex-situ synthesized nanoparticles of CGO20 
and Pr2O3 was investigated as a strategy to improve the electrocatalyst activity for the OER. The 
synthesized products were characterized, and their effect on the microstructure of the electrode was 
studied. 
To determine whether the prepared composite electrode showed the desired electrochemical 
characteristics, two sets of symmetrical cells were tested by EIS. The first group of samples was 
composed of symmetrical cells infiltrated with PBSCF electrocatalyst. In contrast, the second set of 
cells were prepared by infiltrating a surface decoration of MIECs nanoparticles on the composite 
PBSCF electrode. The EIS characterization was carried out to study the electrochemical behaviour 
of the PBSCF composite electrode and to establish whether the insertion of a surface modification 
affects the electrochemical performance of the catalyst. 
An investigation of the microstructure and chemical composition of the cells presenting the PBSCF 
composite electrode but lacking the surface decoration was carried out to determine the effects of the 
exposure to the electrochemical testing conditions.     
3.1 Characterization of non-infiltrated backbones 
The tests carried out in this project required the use of two different kinds of porous backbones. 
BZY20 backbones were supplied by Sandrine Ricote (Colorado School of Mines). The received 
samples were composed by both symmetrical and half cells, respectively 6 and 4 samples. Due to the 
limited amount of BZY20 backbones, they were adopted exclusively for the composite anode 
development. In contrast, the infiltration tests for the preparation of fuel electrodes through the 
infiltration of nickel nanoparticles were carried out on largely available YSZ backbones. 
Knowledge of the microstructure and morphology of the porous supports allows for easier 
identification of the infiltrated phase.

dictated by the capping agent and the low stability of the suspension. 
In order to assess the effect of the wetting ability of the suspension and the steric limitation dictated 
by the capping agent, a different infiltration technique was evaluated. The half cells were submerged 
in the nanoparticles suspension for a few minutes. Subsequently, the cells were heat-treated at 350°C 
for 1 hour before repeating the infiltration cycle. At the end of the infiltration procedure, the typical 
reduction treatment was carried out. Different numbers of infiltration cycles were investigated: 3, 5 
and 10 cycles corresponding respectively to samples Ni-S4, Ni-S5 and Ni-S6. 
As for the previous samples, the half cells obtained by immersion were studied by SEM, and the 
resulting morphologies and coverages are presented in figure 12.

and c) Ni-S6 (10 cycles). 
The increasing amount of nanoparticles deposited on the backbone with the growing number of 
infiltration cycles suggests that wetting ability and steric hindrance of the capping agent are not the

main factors affecting the possibility of creating a percolated network of electron-conducting phase. 
These results support the hypothesis that the low concentration of the suspension is the main limiting 
factor. Furthermore, the adopted infiltration technique was time-consuming and not effective. 
The multiple heat treatments at 350°C did not affect particle size, with the average diameter of the 
particles remaining in the order of 20 nm.  
To assess the effect of the concentration of the suspension on the coverage of the backbone, the 
nanoparticles were separated by centrifugation, dried and dispersed in MEKET to obtain three 
suspensions at different concentrations: 0.25 M, 0.5 M and 1 M. Lower transitory stability was 
observed for higher concentrations, requiring the placement of the dispersion on a shaking plate to 
ensure homogeneity. These suspensions were respectively used for carrying out procedures of 10, 20 
and 50 cycles in which a small aliquot was applied on the cell and left to percolate the backbone for 
a short time, followed by removal of superficial excess, drying of the solvent on a heating plate at 
60°C and heat treatment at 350°C for 30 minutes between each cycle. Finally, the cells were reduced 
at 600°C for 1 hour as the previous samples. The resulting samples are respectively indicated by the 
names Ni-S7, Ni-S8 and Ni-S9.

Ni-S7 appears to be the sample showing the highest Ni nanoparticles coverage, although still not

By comparing figures 13, 14 and 15, a progressive decrease in Ni coverage is observed with 
increasing concentration of nanoparticles dispersion. However, a higher number of cycles have been 
carried out with solutions at higher concentration, and similar average particle size among the three 
samples is observed, supporting the idea that the reduced coverage is not linked to nanoparticle 
coarsening. 
The lower presence of the electron-conducting phase is further supported by the increasing charging 
phenomena observed in the images related to Ni-S8 and Ni-S9 compared to Ni-S7. 
According to these results, an optimum concentration value at around 0.25 M can be expected. At 
lower concentration, a high number of infiltration cycles are necessary to achieve the desired 
coverage. In contrast, at higher concentrations, the ability of the nanoparticles to enter the porous 
backbone is hindered. 
This phenomenon may be attributed to the non-ideal super-paramagnetic character of the Ni 
nanoparticles. The sufficient amount of solvent molecules can transitorily stabilize the dispersion to 
==PAGE:38==
 
38 
 
ensure nanoparticles deposition on the porous support at lower concentrations. Lower amounts of 
solvent would tendentially lead to an incomplete extension of the polymer capping agent, which in 
turn would not ensure the transitory magnetic isolation of the nanoparticles due to the reduced steric 
hindrance provided by the PVP chains. Consequently, large magnetic soft agglomerates would form 
on the surface of the half cell, not allowing the nanoparticles to access the pore of the YSZ backbone. 
Further studies are necessary to demonstrate this hypothesis, showing the lower steric hindrance 
ability of the capping agent in solutions of higher concentrations. Moreover, alternative capping 
agents and solvent systems should be investigated to develop stable dispersions at higher 
concentrations. 
Among the possible solutions to obtain more stable dispersions at higher concentrations, the use of 
capping agents with longer non-polar chains could be adopted, leading to stronger steric repulsion 
between nanoparticles. The use of PVP with higher average molecular weight, such as 50000 or 
360000 Da, is a possible alternative but could also lead to other challenges in terms of removal of 
capping agent, percolation and creation of a conducting network. Moreover, carbon deposition on the 
surface of the nickel nanoparticles may arise. 
According to the obtained data, the infiltration of sintered backbones with as-synthesized Ni 
nanoparticles does not appear as a suitable strategy for the preparation of fuel electrodes. A vast 
number of factors has to be taken into account to ensure the creation of a percolating network, among 
which the concentration of the nanoparticles dispersion, the nature of the solvent system, the choice 
of the capping agent and the characteristics of the adopted infiltration technique. On the other hand, 
this procedure appears to be a suitable alternative to expand the catalytically active surface area of 
electrocatalysts prepared by both standard ceramic methods and infiltration of nickel precursors. 
Infiltration of nearly monodispersed nickel nanoparticles, whose size and shape can be easily 
controlled by varying the synthesis parameters can extend the TPBs of the fuel electrode more than 
what is generally achieved by the already mentioned techniques. Further tests should be carried out 
to assess this possibility. 
Furthermore, a possible alternative could be the infiltration of a metallic nickel precursor without 
resorting to the use of a high-temperature reduction step for the conversion of NiO to the metallic 
phase. The infiltration of the [Ni(N2H4)3]+2(EG) precursor complex onto pre-sintered backbones could 
be a suitable alternative for the formation in situ of nickel nanoparticles without resorting to the 
adoption of high-temperature processing. This technique would not only allow overcoming the 
aforementioned challenges related to the infiltration of as-synthesized nanoparticles but may also 
ensure the possibility of tailoring size, shape and distribution of nanoparticles on the porous backbone 
consequently offering the opportunity of producing highly optimized fuel electrode structures with 
lower economical and environmental impact due to the reduced temperatures of the process.

concentration of the precursor solution.  
Further investigations are necessary to determine the optimal choice of chelating agent and the

3.5 Electrochemical performance of infiltrated cells 
Electrochemical testing through impedance spectroscopy was carried out on both sets of symmetrical 
cells composed respectively by C1, C2 and C3, prepared by infiltration of PBSCF precursor solution, 
and C4, C5 and C6 obtained by infiltration of PBSCF precursor solution followed by infiltration of 
CGO20 and Pr2O3 nanoparticles. The objective of the electrochemical testing was to assess the 
electrochemical performance of the samples and the effect of the surface modification.  
The electrochemical testing was considered merely as a proof of concept. Consequently, the fitting 
of the spectra was not carried out, and the necessary electrochemical data was extrapolated from the 
raw spectra of the samples. The ohmic resistance (Rs), attributed to the electrolyte, was determined 
as the high-frequency intercepts of the impedance spectra with the x-axis. Similarly, the polarization 
resistance (Rp), attributable to the electrode and due to overpotentials dictated by chemical and 
physical processes taking place on the electrode, has been determined as the difference between the 
low and high-frequency intercepts of the impedance spectra with the x-axis.  
Qingjie Wang carried out the electrochemical investigation, and the performance of the 
electrocatalyst was investigated as a function of different parameters such as temperature and steam 
and oxygen partial pressures. Moreover, the short-term durability at 650°C in humidified air (3% 
steam) of samples C1, C2 and C3 was investigated.  
The dependency of the electrochemical performance on the temperature for samples C1, C2 and C3 
in wet air (3 % H2O) is shown in figure 34.

dependence. 
The low-frequency data of the three cells does not intersect the x-axis of the Nyquist plot, 
consequently not allowing the determination of the correct Rp and Rs values if not through curve 
fitting, which was not carried out due to the high variability and poor overall performance. However,

values, although the increase for C2 and C3 is more evident.

3.6 Post-mortem analysis of infiltrated cells 
In order to investigate the reasons behind the poor and not homogeneous electrochemical performance 
of samples C1, C2 and C3, the samples were analyzed by SEM, EDS and XRD. 
During the removal of C1 from the testing housing, it was observed that the cell presented a fracture 
perpendicular to the surface of the electrodes. No visible defects could be observed for samples C2 
and C3.  
The first step of the post-mortem analysis was an investigation of the morphology and microstructure 
of the samples through SEM.

and steam partial pressure adopted for electrochemical testing, as demonstrated by the probable 
undesired presence of BaCO3. Is not clear whether barium carbonate was originated from the

prepared by applying an electrocatalyst functional layer followed by drop-casting.[38] Considering that 
the method adopted for the preparation of the aforementioned samples resulted in extremely variable 
performances, possible effects on the stability of the electrocatalyst cannot be excluded.  
Further studies are necessary to establish whether the low stability and poor performance of C1, C2 
and C3 can be attributed to the preparation methodology or the characteristics electrocatalyst. Still, 
they could not be carried out due to the limited availability of symmetrical BZY20 cells. 
Further studies are also necessary to analyse the crystal structure and morphology of samples C4, C5 
and C6 to determine whether the infiltration of CGO20 and Pr2O3 not only affects the electrochemical 
performance of the electrode but also its stability.

4. Conclusion and Outlook 
The work carried out in this project produced encouraging results and provided useful information 
for the development of electrodes via infiltration for PCEC and SOEC applications. 
The first part of this project focused on the development of an alternative preparation methodology 
for fuel electrodes via the infiltration of ex-situ synthesized nickel nanoparticles. Dispersible 
nanoparticles were successfully synthesized through modified polyol method and characterized. 
Infiltration tests were carried out using nanoparticle suspensions with different concentrations and 
solvents, and the obtained samples were characterized through SEM. Several infiltration procedures 
were applied to evaluate the parameters affecting the creation of a percolated network of an electronic 
conducting phase in YSZ backbones. It was demonstrated that various factors strongly influence the 
maximum achievable loading of Ni and that a suitable trade-off has to be found. Among them, the 
main ones are the concentration of the dispersion and the choice of solvent and capping agent.  
Due to the non-ideal super-paramagnetic character of the nickel nanoparticles, it was impossible to 
generate stable and concentrated dispersions. At lower concentrations, the number of cycles necessary 
to achieve the desired coverage makes the technique not feasible, considering that insufficient loading 
was achieved after 20 cycles. At higher concentrations, the progressively lower transitory stability of 
the suspensions hinders the deposition of the nanoparticles into the backbone. It is believed that the 
lower amount of solvent molecules in more concentrated solutions results in the creation of soft 
magnetic agglomerates due to the reduced transitory steric stabilization imparted by the capping 
agent, limiting nanoparticles deposition. Further studies are necessary to confirm this hypothesis. 
Possible strategies to overcome the aforementioned challenges include the adoption of alternative 
solvents and capping agents and the use of surfactants to achieve more stable suspensions. On the 
other hand, the extensive use of surfactants and the adoption of bulkier capping agents may result in 
new challenges in terms of their removal and excessive repulsion between the deposited nanoparticles 
hindering the creation of an electronic conducting network. 
In conclusion, the investigated technique was considered not feasible, and alternative methods should 
be investigated. A promising alternative is the infiltration of metallic nickel precursors for the 
generation in situ of nickel nanoparticles. The use of this technique would not require the use of 
surfactants and capping agents. Moreover, sufficiently concentrated and stable solutions can be easily 
prepared, ensuring a contained number of infiltration cycles. This technique should reduce the 
percolation threshold due to the small and controllable particle size, reducing the required amount of 
nickel precursor. Moreover, a higher degree of control on the final microstructure of the electrode 
could be achieved. Furthermore, this technique should not require any high-temperature processing 
due to the facile conversion of the precursors to the metallic phase at temperatures around 100°C, 
reducing the environmental and economical impact of fuel electrode preparation. 
The main objective of this project was the development of a stable and well-performing composite 
oxygen electrode for PCEC application. PBSCF was chosen as an electrocatalyst because of the 
promising results achieved through its fabrication with other methodologies.[38][87] Moreover, the 
introduction of a surface decoration through the infiltration of ex-situ synthesized CGO20 and Pr2O3 
nanoparticles was investigated as a suitable strategy to improve the catalyst activity for the OER. 
Initially, the development of the composite electrode was attempted through the adoption of a 0.1 M 
nitrate precursor solution. The solution ensured the formation of phase pure PBSCF by decomposing 
the precursors at 350°C in the presence of citric acid and EDTA as chelating agents and further 
calcination step at 800°C. On the other hand, the low concentration of the solution required a high 
number of infiltration cycles to achieve the desired catalyst loading. A 0.25 M solution of nitrate 
==PAGE:62==
 
62 
 
precursors was prepared to optimise the infiltration procedure, and infiltration tests were carried out 
on YSZ backbones. The high viscosity of the latter solution strongly hindered its ability to access the 
pore structure and, thus, the possibility of generating the desired composite structure.  
A suitable trade-off between sufficient concentration, wetting ability, desired microstructure and 
phase purity of the electrode was found by adopting a 0.5 M acetate precursor solution and a 
calcination temperature of 800°C. The required number of infiltration cycles yielding sufficient 
electrocatalyst loading was found by infiltrating the catalyst precursor solution into YSZ and BZY20 
half cells, and for the latter backbones corresponded to 8 cycles. Subsequently, a set of three BZY20 
symmetrical cells was prepared through an 8-cycle infiltration procedure. The electrochemical 
performance of the cells was evaluated by EIS. Non-comparable results were obtained, probably due 
to a poor infiltration and calcination procedure. To explain these unsatisfactory results, the effects of 
the exposition to the testing conditions on the catalyst composition and microstructure were 
investigated by XRD, EDS and SEM. The analysis revealed significant damages to the electrolyte of 
one of the cells. Moreover, a drastic change in the chemical composition and microstructure of the 
electrode due to the probable formation of BaCO3 in the shape of spike-like structures was observed. 
Further analyses are required to explain the poor performance and instability of the developed cells 
and whether the creation of the undesired carbonate phase can be related to the instability of PBSCF, 
formation of secondary phases or chemical reaction with the electrolyte material. Unfortunately, the 
required work could not be carried out due to the limited availability of BZY20 cells. 
As a next step, symmetrical cells with a surface decoration of MIEC nanoparticles were developed. 
CGO20 and Pr2O3 nanoparticles were synthesized, characterized by XRD and their impact on the 
microstructure of the electrode was investigated by SEM and EDS. A second set of symmetrical cells 
with BZY20-PBSCF-CGO20-Pr6O11 composite electrode was prepared with a similar procedure to 
that used for the first set of symmetrical cells. The second set of cells was subjected to EIS analysis. 
Promising results were obtained, and the catalyst showed a performance comparable to that achieved 
by the microstructurally optimized PBSCF electrode developed by Wu et al.[87]  
The obtained results support the hypothesis that improved performance can be achieved by surface 
modification of the PBSCF catalyst. Moreover, establishing the performance-limiting processes 
taking place on the catalyst can provide useful information to improve its characteristics. A new set 
of symmetrical cells with BZY20-PBSCF composite electrode is required to assess the effect of the 
surface modification on the catalyst activity.  
The adoption of proton-conducting backbones with higher porosity may ensure a further reduction of 
the Rp of the electrode due to improved gas diffusion. Similarly, the adoption of electrolytes with 
lower thickness and tailored composition are expected to reduce the ohmic resistance.  
Further effort should be paid towards the investigation of alternative precursor solutions. Adopting 
alternative precursor systems of salts might yield a more concentrated and homogeneous solution, 
ensuring a lower number of infiltration cycles. Among the possible alternatives, the use of a hybrid 
system of nitrates, chlorides, and hydroxides should overcome the difficulties related to the 
solubilities of the salts. Moreover, the careful choice of the chelating agent can yield a better adhesion 
between catalyst and electrolyte phases, reducing the Rs, and favourable phase purity and optimized 
morphology that would positively affect the Rp of the catalyst and its stability.  
The results and the knowledge acquired through this project are an encouraging starting point for 
developing microstructurally optimized electrodes for PCECs and SOECs through the adoption of 
cost-effective manufacturing techniques.
