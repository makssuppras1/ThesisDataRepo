Abstract
Bitcoin is a digital asset that allows peer­to­peer transactions without the need for a cen­
tral bank to act as an intermediary. Bitcoin is a highly volatile asset and therefore offers
the possibility for attractive returns. This thesis aims to develop a deep learning model
to predict Bitcoin returns and thus assist a trading strategy that outperforms the ’buy
and hold strategy’ and a simple baseline based on a moving average. We tested three
deep­learning models for time­series prediction: the Long­Short term Memory (LSTM),
the Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) and an Autoencoder. We consider features including
historical prices, attention on social media, sentiment data, technical indicators and data
from the blockchain network. This work contributes to the existing literature by conducting
comprehensive research in the choice of parameters, bringing together data from tradi­
tional and alternative data sources and comparing results from three different models.
We find the combination of features and parameters, including the length of the input se­
quence (window), that lead to a small prediction error. We found that different models
and choices of parameters gave different combinations of features, and thus we cannot
conclude which features are the best for a general purpose. We conclude the analysis by
implementing a ’buy low’ and ’sell high’ trading strategy based on the predictions of the
deep­learning models. We found that the Autoencoder yielded better profitability than the
simple moving average baseline and the ’buy and hold strategy’ for a window of 5 days.
The LSTM achieved similar results for a window of 20 days.

Introduction
The prediction of the financial world has been an exciting venture for researchers since the
1900s. The market capitalization of equity­backed securities have grown from 2.5 trillion
USD in 1980 to 70.75 trillion USD in late 2019 [1]. Apart from the traditional stock mar­
ket, the cryptocurrency market emerged in 2010 [2]. A cryptocurrency is a form of digital
currency backed by cryptography and hard to copy or double spend. Cryptocurrencies
are built on blockchain, which is a ledger supported by a network of computers. Cryp­
tocurrencies are decentralized, which means a central authority does not issue them, but

Many researchers have attempted to forecast the behaviour of economic markets [5].
During earlier times, the central belief was that the market is efficient and that the stock
market’s movement resembles a random walk. However, the research of [6] who did
empirical studies in investment portfolio found evidence that historical data can be help­

evidence that financial markets are not random but behave chaotically.
Since the emergence of the cryptocurrency market and Bitcoin, there has been signifi­

potential returns. The paper of [8] suggests that the the efficiency of the market is also
sensitive to various external events such as hacks. The paper advocates that the market

mid 2018. The research of [9]–[13] has focused on Bitcoin price prediction using both
statistical and Machine Learning(ML) methods. The statistical methods vary from Moving

This continuous output can either be the Bitcoin price itself or the daily Bitcoin returns.
The researchers have employed methods to achieve those tasks such as Support Vector

Related work on the stock market
Predicting prices and movements of stock market, indices and other emerging markets
has been a topic of interest for many researchers. An accurate prediction could yield
attractive profits for traders and investors. Th literature has utilized machine learning
methods as an approach to attack the problem. Below, we briefly present some interesting
findings and conclusions for the prediction of the stock market.
[25] made an early attempt to forecast the stock market. The paper discusses a buying
and selling timing prediction system for Tokyo Stock Exchange Price Indexes (TOPIX) and
an analysis of internal representation based on modular neural networks. They developed
a system made up of several feed­forward Neural Networks that learned the relationships
between various technical and economic indexes and when to buy and sell stocks. The
model consists of three layers: the input layer, the hidden layer, and the output layer. All
layers are entirely connected to form a hierarchical network. The learning of the model
is based on back backpropagation. The model’s input are interest rates, turnover, New
York Dow­Jones average, and Foreign Exchange rate among others. The model’s output
is an analogue value between [0, 1] signifying when to buy and sell. The timing for when
to buy and sell used is the weighted sum of weekly returns. Output values larger than
0.5 corresponds to a buying signal, and output values smaller than 0.5 signifies a selling
signal. The backtesting of the prediction system showed that taking action according to the
buying and selling signal yields greater profits than buying and holding strategy. Namely,
in January 1987, the TOPIX index was considered as 1.00. A the end of September 1989,
the TOPIX index was 1.67 following the buy and hold strategy and 1.89 following the buying
and selling signal from the prediction system.
In their paper [26] trained a Neural Network for predicting the weekly price of the FAZ­
Index, which is calculated based on 100 major German stocks using the backpropagation
learning. Apart from using previous prices as input to the model, they also use fundamen­
tal and technical analysis elements in their approach. The paper investigates the use of
activation functions other than the usual sigmoid function, the addition of a small positive
offset to the derivative of the sigmoid function to avoid saturation at the extremes, and a
momentum term in the equation for the weight changes. The input data consists of the in­
dex of FAZ Index, received orders, the daily published US­Dollar exchange rate, the bond
market index, and the 5, 10, 90 day moving average. The model’s hyperparameters such
as learning rate, number of hidden layers and, hidden size are calibrated through trial and
error. The experiments showed that neural networks with one hidden layer perform bet­
ter. The use of the sigmoid function (plus offset) achieved the best results in the shortest
time. The sine function or a combination of sigmoid and sine is inferior to the sigmoid
function. They observed the best results when the learning rate parameter was between
0.7−0.9 and the momentum parameter at 0.7. The papers infer that the prediction quality
of neural approaches to the stock price prediction problem is generally better than the
results obtained with conventional statistical techniques. Regression methods applied to
the time series used in the present paper predict the trend between two successive values
in the test set in 51% to 62% of the cases correctly. In contrast, the range of correct trend
predictions in the test set with the neural networks used lies between 66%and 76%.

4.1.2
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN)
A recurrent neural network (RNN) is a class of artificial neural networks and are based on
the work of [57]. RNNs are appropriate for modelling sequential or time­series data and
are used for temporal problems, such as language translation, natural language process­
ing (NLP) etc. Those networks utilize training data to learn. A characteristic of RNNs is
their “memory”, as they can use information from prior older inputs and affect the output
of the current input and output. This means that their utility depends on older data points
within the training sequence. Recurrent neural networks utilize Backpropagation Through
Time (BPTT) [58] algorithm to determine the gradients which is specific to sequence data.
BPTT works similar to traditional Backpropagation (BP) [56], where the model trains itself
by calculating errors and updating weights from its output layer to its input layer. The
BPTT network differs from the traditional BT as the former sums errors at each time step
whilst the later do not.

gradients keep multiplying as they are flowing through the different layers due to the chain
rule, which causes the gradients to either shrink or explode. Small gradients prevent the
model from learning, and large gradients are causing instability during training.

To tackle the shortcomings of RNN, [61] introduced the Long­Short Term Memory (LSTM).
LSTMs are artificial RNN suitable for solving tasks using sequential data (speech recog­
nition, time series forecasting, anomaly detection). They are set apart from the traditional
RNN due to their gating mechanism, which addresses the ’short term memory’ drawback
of RNN. Figure 4.4 illustrates the fundamental differences between an RNN and an LSTM.
The different gating mechanisms allow the network to capture long term dependencies
and use them in its current output.

Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU)
The Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) [64] is a later version of the LSTM and a type of RNN.
They are also suitable for modelling long term dependencies in sequential data and cope
with the short term memory problem of the RNN. The GRU controls the flow of information
through its gating units but fundamentally works like an RNN. The input data are fed into
the GRU cell at each time step along with the hidden state. The hidden state is then
consumed again into the RNN cell together with the subsequent input data. The process
continues iteratively, producing the output. While the LSTM process the data through two
states, the hidden state and the cell state, the GRU have only one state passing down
through the steps. Figure 4.6 illustrates the differences between an LSTM and a GRU on

where N is the batch size, L is the length of the sliding window 4.2 and Hin in the num­

or hidden state and an output. We discard the output and we preserve only the hidden
state which encapsulates the information of the input elements. The decoder has its state
initialized with the final state of the encoder. This means, that the output hidden state of
the encoder is the input to the GRU cell of the decoder. The output of the GRU cell is
given as an input to a linear layer which gives a one step ahead prediction.

4.4
Time Series Evaluation (Hurst Exponent)
The Hurst exponent measures the long­term memory and fractality of a time series and
was initially proposed by [67]. This measure has been used previously by the financial
community [68]–[70]. The Hurst exponent is robust with few assumptions on the under­
lying system, which has raised its applicability for time series analysis. The values of
the measures range between 0 and 1, and a time series can fall within three categories.
When the Hurst exponent H takes the value, H = 0.5, it indicates a time series that acts
as a random walk. When 0 < H < 0.5, the time series is defined as anti­persistent.
An anti­persistent time series is the ’mean reverting’, which means that a movement of a
value upwards is more likely to be followed by a movement of the value downwards and
vice versa. This effect is strengthened as H moves closer to 0. Values of H between
0.5 < H < 1 indicates a persistent time series. A persistent time series is trend reinforc­
ing, which means that the direction of the next value is a time series that will likely be the
same as the direction of the previous. This phenomenon is also increasing as H moves
towards 1. The study of [71] shows that time series with higher H is more predictable. We
estimate the Hurst exponent by rescaled range analysis (R/S analysis). The idea behind
the Hurst estimation is that we can utilize the variance of log prices to assess the rate of
diffusive behaviour. For a time lag τ the variance is given by eq. (4.25)
V ar(τ) = |log(t + τ) −log(t)|2
(4.25)
We are comparing the rate of diffusion with that of the Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM),
and we assume that at large τ the variance is proportional to tau. This yields eq. (4.26)
τ ∼|log(t + τ) −log(t)|2
(4.26)
If any auto correlation exist(sequential price movements have non zero correlation) then
eq. (4.26) is not valid and can be modified to include an exponent value as eq. (4.27) such
that it introduces the Hurst exponent:
τ 2H ∼|log(t + τ) −log(t)|2
(4.27)

Hyper parameter optimization
The optimization of the hyper parameters is done with Optuna framework [75]. Optuna is
an open source hyperparameter optimization framework that automate hyper parameter
search. Optuna needs an objective function to minimine. In our case, the objective func­
tion is the minimization of the validation loss during training. Secondly, we need to define
a set of hyper parameters that the framework should be searching. Optuna is trying to
find a combination of those hyper parameters than minimize the validation loss during
training. Optuna uses a history record of trials to determine which hyperparameter values
to try next. Using this data, it estimates a promising area and tries values in that area.
Optuna then estimates an even more promising region based on the new result. It repeats
this process using the history data of trials completed thus far. Specifically, it employs a
Bayesian optimization algorithm called Tree­structured Parzen Estimator[76].

Selecting suitable period for Bitcoin prediction
In this section, we want to examine whether some periods are more predictable than
others. To check this assumption, we calculate the Hurst exponent, which we describe
in section 4.4. After that, we create forecasts for particular periods, and we measure
the performance on distinct periods for the GRU and the LSTM. We also want to check
if making predictions to individuals periods will eliminate the straight­line predictions we
saw in previous sections.
5.4.1
Hurst Exponent
Figure 5.18 illustrates the Hurst exponent with the red line and the temporal evolution of
the Bitcoin price with the blue line. The Hurst exponent is calculated for a whole time
period and not for single points. That means, that the first red value in the plot is the
Hurst value for the first six months of 2016. As a reminder, 0 < H < 0.5 signifies that the
time series is mean reverting, H = 0.5 signifies that the time series is a random walk and
H > 0.5 defines a persistent time series. From the figure we see that most of the spikes
revolve around H = 0.5 but the majority of the Hurst values are below 0.5. This translates
that the majority of the six month time series that each one of those Hurst values represent
are mean reverting. Due to the high fluctuation of the Hurst values in this time period, we
will calculate the Hurst exponent on a twelve month rolling window.

5.5
Feature Selection
In this part of the thesis we describe the feature selection process. We make the feature
selection as follows. Initially, there is only one feature in the model, which is the PriceUSD
(If the model forecast the returns then the first feature will be the returns). We train the
model, and then we measure the VP USD. As a reminder, we measure the VP USD
by calculating the RMSE of the predicted values with the actual values. Thus, we have a
minimum benchmark value for the RMSE of VP USD for one feature. Next, we are looping
through all the features. Suppose we find a feature whose combination with the PriceUSD
features gives a lower VP USD. So after the first iteration, we have two features and a new
benchmark VP USD. For the sake of the example, we assume that the second feature
whose combination with the PriceUSD gives a lower RMSE VP USD is the volume. In the
third iteration, we have as main features the PriceUSD and volume. We loop through all
the remaining feature one by one until we find another feature whose combination with
PriceUSD and volume gives a lower RMSE VP USD than the second iteration. We do
that until we cannot find a combination of features that does not reduce the VP USD.

Discussion
This section will focus on the interpretation of the results and what they mean. We attempt
to explain what went wrong what went well and give a reasoning behind it.
6.1
Drawback and pitfalls
We have created two variations of GRU and LSTM models in an attempt to forecast the
returns. The first variation is with a single linear layer before the output and the second
variation is with two linear layers before the output. For the first variation, we see from
figures 5.2 and 5.9 but also section 5.4.2 that the models were unable to model the returns.
By that, we mean that the prediction output for both the training and validation set was a
straight line. The predicted values were not the same, but they had minimal variation, and
in every case, they were predicting positive values. Eventually, we solved this issued by
creating the second variation of the model with the two linear layers before the output. The
first variation of the model was not complex enough to capture the pattern of the returns,
whilst the second, more complex architecture. Initially, we experimented a lot with the
hyper parameters of the first variation of the model. We experimented with the learning
rate, the number of layers the hidden size, and none solved the issue. On the other hand,
the second variation of the model, due to its increased complexity, managed to capture
the complete structure of the data. For this model, we also experimented a lot with the
hyper parameters.
While GRU/LSTM with the one linear layer cannot produce good results for the returns, it
gives reasonable results when forecasting the prices. How can the same model with the
same hyper parameters give such different results? The same question has been asked
by [81]. The answer comes with the kind of data that we model. On the one hand, we
have the prices to model and, on the other hand, the returns. The time series of price is
non­stationary, which means that there is some trend. If we see the temporal evolution of
Bitcoin from figure 3.1, we see that there is an increasing trend. Moreover, we can check
the non­stationarity by measuring the mean and variance at different periods. With a visual
inspection, we can see that the period between mid 2017 to mid 2018 has a different mean
and variance than period 2019 to 2020. On the other hand, we see from figure 3.3(a) that
the returns is a stationary time series. A stationary time series is one whose fluctuations
appear random but often with the same type of stochastic behaviour from one time period
to the next[82]. The model takes advantage of the non­stationarity of the price time series
to assume a value for a future prediction. On the other hand, a stationary time series,
because of the lack of dependences, the past values of a white noise process, do not
contain information that can be used to predict future values. Therefore, the best predictor
of any future value of the process would be the mean[82]. Figure 5.2(a) illustrates the
training data with the blue line and the predictions with the yellow line. We see that the
training data oscillates around zero and that the prediction we make is also very close to
zero. Therefore, we assume that the simple variations of the LSTM behave in the same
manner, which is to assume the time series as white noise and predict the mean value of
the time series as its forecast.
The LSTM / GRU models are appropriate for sequential data. However, in the experiments
we also feed the neural networks single data points. The single data points are the sliding
windows of 1 days. We see a common thing from figures 5.5(b), 5.12(b) and 5.17. For
a sliding window of 1 day, they all have the highest F1 score from the rest of the sliding

We see interesting behaviour when we attempt to forecast the prices. Figures 5.7(b) and

starters, we focus on the period from 2020 −01 to 2020 −11. At first sight, the forecast

marginal value. Now we shift our focus from the period of 2020−11 until the end. We notice
that the forecast is no longer following the actual values closely. The forecast reaches a
maximum value of 20000USD whilst the actual values reach a maximum of 32000USD.
We hypothesize that this happens for two reasons. Firstly, when we see figure 5.7(a) we
notice that the maximum value of the time series is 20000USD which is the same value
as the maximum value in the validation forecast. Secondly, we do not see an upwards
trend in the training set; therefore, the model does not learn that the prices increase with
time. Thus, a potential limitation is that we do not have enough data to capture a general
trend in the training set.
6.2
Performance comparison
In this thesis, we try to perform a regression task which means that we predict either the
returns or the prices. Therefore, the metric that the models are optimizing for is the RMSE.
The F1 score is a metric derived from the forecast, but it is not what the model is trying
to optimize. With that being said, we note down that if a model is having a lower RMSE
than another model, it does not necessarily mean that the model with the small RMSE
will also have a low F1 score. For example, if we have a time series with ten values, the
model with the low RMSE can make five predictions with the correct sign and have their
value close to the actual value. The model with the higher RMSE can predict the sign of

the time series correctly eight times, but their value is not close to the actual value. For
the remaining of this analysis, we will not discuss any further results from the GRU and
LSTM models where we forecast returns with one linear layer in the output. Moreover, we
will also refrain from commenting on results from sliding windows of one day, since the
the models predicted everything to be on the positive side.
We will now start discussing the performance of the three models, GRU / LSTM and Au­
toencoder, for the returns forecast. In terms of RMSE on the validation set, all three
models agree that the smaller values are at a sliding window of five days. For the RSME
of the training set, the GRU and LSTM agree on the sliding window of 10 days whist the
Autoencoder finds the smallest error on the 15 days windows size. Regarding the F1
score, the LSTM has the highest value on window size 40, the GRU on a window of 15
days and the Autoencoder on a window size of 45. The F1 scores do not have a consis­
tent pattern across the three models, meaning their highs and lows are different. Thus,
based on the validation RMSE, the sliding window of five days gives the best results. The
research paper of [47] found the smallest RMSE for Bitcoin on a sliding window of 7 days,
so our results agree with the ones in the literature. When comparing the models them­
selves, we find that the LSTM is performing best of all. It is consistent with having the
smallest RMSE in the validation set in most of the sliding windows. The worst performing
is the Autoencoder, since it has the highest values in the validation set. However, the Au­
toencoder is more consistent with high values of the F1 score across the sliding windows.
In the literature research papers, we have not found that compares the LSTM and GRU
on Bitcoin returns but only on Bitcoin prices.
The next part of this discussion is to compare the performance of the LSTM and the GRU
when forecasting the Bitcoin prices. Tables 5.1 and 5.2 provides the data for the LSTM and
GRU comparison respectively. For the LSTM, we get the smallest RMSE on the prices
for a window of 60 days for both the training and the validation set. The GRU is having
the smallest training RMSE on a sliding window of 20 days whilst for the validation set
we identify the smallest RMSE on the 10 days lookback period. The experiments of [84]
suggests that for an input sequence of 10 or 20 days, they obtain the best performance for
Bitcoin price prediction with an LSTM. This time sequence agrees with the results that we
obtained from the GRU but not the LSTM. What we see from the results of this thesis, and
the results from the literature is the the length of the input sequence is situational. Two
different sources from the literature have found different optimal sliding windows. We also
have have found optimal sliding window which is dependable of whether we try to forecast
returns of prices. This evidence tells us that choice of the sliding window is dependable
of the specific problem at hand and needs to be chosen through experimentation in every
case. In regards to the F1 score, both LSTM and GRU has a similar pattern and is thus
difficult to distinguish over a window size. Since we are interested in predicting Bitcoin
returns, we convert the prices and measure again the performance. For the GRU we get
the smallest values for sliding window 15 whilst for the LSTM we get the smallest values
for window size 15 and 30 on the training and validation set respectively. In terms of
performance comparison between the models, we found the GRU to be superior. The
RMSE of the prices in the training and validation set are smaller in the GRU than in the
LSTM. Those results agree with the findings of [85], [86].
From the performance comparison, we found out that when predicting returns, the LSTM
is doing better, however when forecasting prices, the GRU is outperforming the LSTM
which is also confirmed by the literature.

6.3
Examining the market efficiency
In section 5.4, we attempted to examine whether some are periods are more predictable
than others. We produced results both for the LSTM / GRU models that forecast returns
and the LSTM / GRU model that forecast prices. In terms of the F1 score, all the models
performed worse in 2018 and 2019 than the whole period. When we take into consideration
the returns forecast, for the validation set in tables 5.5 and 5.4, both the LSTM and GRU

also agree with the SMA baseline, where it follows the same pattern as the predictions.
On a side note, the GRU performed better than the LSTM. Tables 5.6 and 5.7 shows the
performance results of GRU and LSTM model that forecasts prices. We see something
interesting. In years 2017 and 2020 we record the worst performance in the validation set,
both for the predictions and the baseline, for the GRU and LSTM model. This, however,
does not come as a surprise. If we look in figure 3.1 we see that 2017 and 2020 had a
huge surge in price. The validation set of the individual years contains 73 days. On those
73 days, the price achieved new maximum values in comparison to the training set. This
translates that the models could not predict those all­time high values since they have not
seen something like that before. Therefore they did a poor job in the validation set. This

Conclusion & Future Work
This thesis aimed to develop a deep learning model to forecast Bitcoin. The primary
motivation is to profit from the high volatility of digital asset. In this study, we attempted to
both forecast the daily returns of Bitcoin and the prices. We wanted to see if there are any
benefits in forecasting one over the other or making no difference. We created four model
variations. A GRU/LSTM with a single linear layer before the output to forecast returns. A
GRU/LSTM with a single linear layer before the output to forecast prices. A GRU/LSTM
with two linear layers before the output to forecast returns and a GRU based Autoencoder.
Due to the lack of complexity, the GRU/LSTM with a single linear layer to forecast the
returns did not fit the task. Despite trying out various hyper parameters, the models could
not capture the data structure of the returns and output only positive predictions with small
variability. Therefore, we did not use the model as we dove deeper into the analysis.
A big part of the analysis was determining whether there is an input sequence length
(sliding window) that produces better results than other sequences. We experimented
with sliding windows of 1, 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45, 50, 55, 60 days. We measured the
performance of the models in terms of the RMSE and the F1 score. Our dataset consists
of five years, spanning from 2016 to 2020. We examined the possibility to work only in
individual years and not the whole dataset. However, the analysis showed that selectively
choosing individual years to work with could introduce biases to the results. We carried
on the analysis with a feature selection process based on two different occasions; when
forecasting returns and when forecasting prices. For the case of price forecasting, we
also perform hyper parameter optimization. Finally, we close the thesis by implementing
a ’Buy Low’ and ’Sell High’ strategy for the models that we created, and we measured
their performance against a SMA baseline and a buy and hold strategy.
When forecasting the Bitcoin returns, we found that the GRU performs better in overall
performance, including computational speed. It took GRU 39 seconds to train a model,
43 seconds for the LSTM and 1 minute and 37 seconds for the Autoencoder. All three
models found the smallest RMSE value on the validation set for a sliding window of five
days. The models did not find common ground in determining the best value for a sliding
window when it comes to the F1 score. When forecasting the Bitcoin prices, the LSTM
found the smallest validation RMSE of the prices on a sliding window of 60 days, whilst
the GRU has the smallest validation RMSE on a sliding window of 20 days. In terms of
the F1 score, the results were similar between the LSTM and the GRU and thus makes it
difficult to differentiate. Even though the GRU is faster, the analysis shows that the LSTM
is better in forecasting returns, but the GRU is better in forecasting prices. The initial
analysis showed that it is not easy to determine a sliding window for a general­purpose.
Our experiments showed that when predicting returns, a good sliding window is five days.
When predicting prices, the sliding window is model dependent.
We analysed the Bitcoin on individual years and recorded the results. When forecasting
the returns, we obtained smaller RMSE on the validation set for 2016, 2019 and 2020 for
both GRU and LSTM. We compared the results from the price forecast with the Hurst
exponent values that we have calculated. We found that our results agreed with the
predictability that it was suggested by the Hurst exponent for each individual year.
The feature selection section showed that different model and different sliding windows
could pick a different feature combination. We had 60 features in total. When we forecast
the price, the model selected as a feature the network’s difficulty to find a hash, which
Development of an AI Trading Bot for the Cryptocurrency Market
65
==PAGE:78==
other researchers also selected. The model that forecasts returns picked the ROI1yr
and FeeToNtv three out of six times. We have not seen these features in some other re­
search, potentially serving as a new feature for modelling in future studies. Unfortunately,
none of the models picked as a feature the sentiment data from Reddit and therefore we
could not prove one of our initial hypothesis. We learned from this section that feature
selection and hyper parameter optimization are model­specific and must be selected with
care.
In the profitability analysis, we found that the Autoencoder with just the returns as an input
feature produces more cumulative returns than both the SMA and the ’Buy and Hold’
strategy. That is for a sliding window of five days. Moreover, the LSTM that forecasts the
prices does better than the SMA and the ’Buy and Hold’ for a sliding window for 20 days.
This thesis has contributed to the literature by providing a comprehensive research for
the selection of an appropriate sliding window. We showed that a small RMSE does not
necessarily mean that we forecast the market movement correctly. We provided an ex­
tensive performance comparison for different models variations and contributed to the
predictability hypothesis of individual years. We found two features that showed of ev­
idence of predictive power and can be used in future research. Finally, we set a basic
framework that future researchers can use to build on.
To conclude, there is plenty of space for future work. The cryptocurrency market is still
young and needs to mature. There is a need for more data in order for a model to capture
the time series and increasing trend of bitcoin effectively. Due to time constraints, we did
not examine in depth the Autoencoder nor estimated the cumulative returns of the GRU /
LSTM model that forecasts returns. It would give us a complete picture of whether prices
or returns is better to forecast. There is limited research in forecasting Bitcoin returns,
so that is something that could be explored further. Researchers can also treat the prob­
lem as a classification task. When trading just one coin, what matters is to predict the
directional market movement correctly. In that regards, the same work can be repeated,
but instead of focusing on a regression task, we implement a classification task that pre­
dicts whether the market will go up or down. We can take it one step further and create
a multi­class classification problem, where each class will represent a range of returns.
Therefore, we will predict the directional movement and a sense of the magnitude of the
returns. That work would be specifically helpful if we want to create a portfolio that trades
many cryptocurrencies and allocates resources according to the magnitude of the pre­
dicted returns. There is also limited research and space for future work in forecasting
shorter time intervals, for example, 1 minute or 5­minute intervals. The problem is the
scarcity of data aggregates for such short periods. Nevertheless, forecasting in such a
short period is realistic due to the high volatility of Bitcoin within the day.
