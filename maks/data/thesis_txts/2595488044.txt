Abstract
Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) of civil engineering structures are important to en-
sure a safely functioning structure throughout its lifetime. A vital part of SHM is the
detection of structural changes. In this numerically based study, a small steel structure has
been investigated with the scope of determining a structural change based on Operational
Modal Analysis (OMA).
A scenario based approach has been used to introduce the structural change through
multiple cases with a point mass of diﬀerent magnitudes and at diﬀerent positions. The
Time Domain Poly Reference identiﬁcation techniques has been used on the responses to
determine the modal parameters. Based on Multi Variate Linear Regression (MVLR) a
ﬁnal model has been developed, to detect the structural change and determine the magni-
tude and location of the point mass. The selection of predictors for the MVLR and criteria
to select the best model for prediction of the structural change has been presented.
Various predictors was considered, but the ﬁnal model predicting the structural change
did only include the identiﬁed natural frequencies. The performance of the ﬁnal model
showed that the magnitude of the added point mass could be predicted with an acceptable
precision. Also the model was used to indicate an area where the point mass was placed.
The methodology presented in this thesis showed that a structural change could be
detected by a model based on OMA identiﬁcations techniques combined with a MVLR
analysis. Further research are needed to fully prove the potential of the presented method-
ology for the purpose of SHM.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This project deals with the detection of structural changes on a simple small scale steel
structure modelled by a Finite Element (FE) software. Analysis is based on Operational
Modal Analysis (OMA), and focuses on two identiﬁcation techniques, namely Time Do-
main Poly Reference (TDPR) and Frequency Domain Decomposition (FDD). The scope
is to determine the magnitude and location of a structural change introduced to the system
by an added point mass. Numerous simulations on diﬀerent load cases are used to perform
Multi Variate Linear Regression (MVLR) and determine a ﬁnal model to predict the state
of the structure based on outputs of the OMA identiﬁcation.
The subject of this thesis is related to the topic Structural Health Monitoring (SHM)
of civil engineering structures. An introduction to SHM, including examples of already
implemented SHM systems, was given by C. Bastien [13]. The aim of SHM is to imple-
ment a system that monitors the state of the stucture and detects when damage occurs.
A robust and reliable monitoring system is essential to ensure that the structure is safely
functioning throughout its lifetime. While the applicability of OMA for the purpose of
SHM has been demonstrated in multiple articles, some of which will be presented in this
chapter, the implementation of a continuous OMA monitoring system is time consuming
and challenging. External parameters inﬂuencing the modal parameters of a structure
for example temperature and humidity alongside other factors, complicates the process
of detecting a structural change. A comprehensive study on the eﬀect of external factors
must be made over a representative reference period to create a model that determines if
the change in modal parameters are due to a structural change or due to a change in the
environment of the structure.
An example of an OMA based SHM system monitoring a large structure, is given in
the paper: Vibration based structural health monitoring of an arch bridge by Magalhães
et al. [17]. In this paper the Infante D. Henrique Bridge in Portugal has been measured

The analysis presented in the paper by Magalhães et al. [17] utilizes three diﬀer-

the tutorial presents that evaluating the state of the structure can be automated based on
preliminary research and a model removing environmental eﬀects. Continuous recordings
of the response can be collected since the ambient vibration on large structures is always
present [18].
Several articles highlight that an aspect of OMA and the identiﬁcation techniques,
that makes it suitable for SHM, is the possibility of automating the process of damage
detection. The paper by Diord et al. [14] investigates automated modal tracking with the
purpose of determining when a structural change has occurred based on the 푇2 method.
An extensive 4 years of monitoring in 1 hour time series are used to describe the time
evolution of the modal parameters. To establish a model accounting for the environmental
eﬀects PCA is used. The article explains how it is diﬃcult to know beforehand the number
of independent factors to include in the PCA. To avoid over ﬁtting of the PCA, the cho-
sen number of independent factors should be chosen carefully. The article furthermore
presents how it is necessary to have multiple measurements exceeding control chart limits
to declare that the exceeding measurements are a result of a damage.

on how added mass aﬀects the modal parameters of a small steel frame was made by G.
Bernagozzi in 2017 [4]. The test structure in the paper is a small scale representation of
a 2-story building. Investigations include evaluating the performance of the identiﬁcation
methods: Natural Excitation Technique Eigensystem Realization Algorithm (NExT-ERA)
and the enhanced FDD, also known as EFDD. Besides the performance of the identiﬁcation

Theoretical Background
2.1
Operational Modal Analysis
OMA deviates from the experimental modal analysis (EMA) where the inputs are known
and often very well deﬁned. In OMA the excitation is unknown and either in the form
of natural excitation: wind, waves, traﬃc, unknown signal, etc. or artiﬁcial excitation:
scratching device, air ﬂow, acoustic emissions etc. In testing of large civil engineering
structures the natural excitation is often suﬃcient. This means that no artiﬁcial excitation
is needed and the structure is monitored in operational conditions. However for small
scale test structures artiﬁcial excitation may be necessary to increase the response of the
structure. Ensuring that the artiﬁcial excitation is random in time and space, is essential
in order to acquire useful measurements [11]. As presented in [11] a key assumption
of OMA is that the input causing motions have nearly, but not necessarily exactly, white
noise characteristics. In the ﬁgure below the system is loaded by white noise, turning into
unknown loads when it passes through an imaginary loading ﬁlter. As only the measured
response is known, this will contain information on both the structural system and the
loading ﬁlter. A ﬁgure similar to the one presented below can be found in Brincker and
Ventura [11].

the structural system by unknown loads. In OMA only the measured response is known.

The theory of OMA applies to both continuous times series and discrete time series.
When performing tests the data output is always in the form of a discrete time series. Most
commonly the response of the structure is measured in units of acceleration, velocity or
displacement. An inﬁnite number of degrees of freedom (DOF) is found in any ﬂexible
structure which means an inﬁnite number of modes exists. The lower modes are often of
most interest as they are generally more dominant than modes of higher order. Measure-
ments on large scale structures are collected during operational conditions which means
modes of higher order might not get excited.

3.1
Design of the Structure
The following guidelines and requirements served as framework during the design of the
test structure.
• Only the 5 ﬁrst modes are of interest. Mode swap will occur when mass of reasonable
magnitude is added to the plate.
• The possibility of adding mass at diﬀerent positions must be incorporated in the
design.
• Small, simple, and stable model representing a real structure.
According to the ﬁrst requirement the test structure must be asymmetric to distinguish
between the bending modes in the x- and y-direction. Furthermore making the structure
asymmetric the requirement of experiencing a mode swap, when mass is added, could also
be fulﬁlled. The magnitude of the added mass resulting in a mode swap must be below
structural capacity, see Appendix A.2. Preferably the design should avoid signiﬁcant

Last requirement is made to ensure that the design and simulations are somehow rep-

the change in magnitude and position of the point mass. A general tendency and a low
variance, within the 10 simulations of each cases was found. This indicates that the 5
frequencies are potentially good predictors. It is expected that the addressed issue regard-
ing the discontinuity on location 31 and adjacent points are going to have some inﬂuence
on the prediction when mass is added in this area. However for the remaining locations
a continuous tendency of the frequency ﬁtting a polynomial of ﬁrst or second order was
observed.

corresponding to a second order polynomial can be observed in some of the plots. To

this however is not always as straight forward. Because of this it was chosen to include

7.5
Quadrant Determination
Only cases in the ﬁrst quadrant of the Cartesian coordinate system, placed centrally on the
plate, has been investigated as described earlier. Due to double symmetry of the structure
the identiﬁed modal parameters will be equivalent for a given point mass added in the
following positions (x,y) = (-x,y) = (-x,-y) = (x,-y). In other words the frequencies and
the MLVR does not reveal in which of the quadrants the mass is located. The analysis
regarding the mode shape showed that the placement of the mass did provoke a detectable
rotation of the top plate as addressed in section 6.4.
An analysis of adding a point mass at locations illustrated in Figure 7.19, was carried
out to validate if the angle of rotation can be used to determine in which of the quadrants
the point mass is added. The red dots indicate the locations included in this analysis.
The corresponding plot in Figure 7.20 shows the rotation angle of the second mode for
these positions. The second mode is selected because as illustrated in Figure 6.14 a point
masses added at 푦= 0 m does not result in signiﬁcant rotation of the top plate for mode 1.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
This analysis aims to determine a model to predict the mass and location of a point mass
added to the top plate of the structure. In the previous sections the ﬁnal model has been
presented along with an analysis on the performance of the selected model. The results
presented show that a structural change can be detected, and that the precision of the model
is at an acceptable level. In section 7.4 it is shown that a point mass with a magnitude of
0.5 kg can be found with a mean error of approximately 5% while for the remaining mass
cases the error is between 1% −3%. In terms of predicting the location the MVLR does
not give the exact position but the output can be used to indicate an area where the point
mass is located. For the 0.5 kg case the model has diﬃculty determining the location close
to the main axis. Also the model struggles to estimate the location for 2 kg in the area
where a discontinuity was found in the frequency plots.
The discontinuity was described in section 6.2. It was explained that when adding
the point mass the modes shifted from bending modes in the xy-direction to diagonal
bending modes. This mode shift introduced a diﬀerence between the analytical calculated
frequencies with no mode shift and the obtained frequencies including the change in the
mode shape. A maximum diﬀerence of 1 Hz was observed for mode 4 and 5 with an added
point mass of 2 kg. It is assumed that the discontinuity in the identiﬁed frequencies had
an impact on the overall MVLR performance to some extend. This discontinuity was not
intended when the structure was designed, and the mode shape change could be avoided
for a similar structure by increasing the cross sectional dimensions of the column.
Both the frequencies and the angle of rotation was taken into consideration in the
MVLR analysis, however the ﬁnal model did not include the angle of rotation. Other
predictors such as the damping ratio could have been included in the MVLR. However
for this simulation based study the damping ratio was assumed in order to generate the
response. Other structural parameters, that has not been investigated in this thesis, could

the MVLR.
The issue regarding available computational power limited the number of predictors
to be included in the MVLR, this issue is addressed in section 7.2. It is not known how
much including all the predictors would inﬂuence the performance of the ﬁnal model.
As presented in the paper by M. Vigsø[21] the drawback of a scenario based approach
is, that the model might experience diﬃculty in predicting scenarios that has not been used
to develop the ﬁnal model. This means that the ﬁnal MVLR model determined in this
thesis has some limitations. The performance of the model when the point mass exceeds 2
kg is to this state unknown. Furthermore the model can not be used to ﬁnd multiple point
masses added simultaneously. Lastly the point mass must be located on the plate, since
this is what has been investigated in this thesis.
In this study the mass was only placed in one quadrant of the top plate due to double
symmetry. The MVLR model determined in this thesis was not able to determine in
which quadrant the mass was placed. This is nonetheless an important information to
have in order to reduce the area where the added mass is expected to be located. It was
established that the rotational angle of the plate could be used to determine which quadrant
the mass was placed. This methodology used to get around the issue regarding quadrant
determination of the mass might not be applicable for all structures. For large scale and
more complex double symmetric structures the issue might have to be solved using another
indicator than the rotation angle.
The ﬁnal model presented in this study is not a general model that can be used for
structures of diﬀerent form and size. The beta coeﬃcients and included predictors has
been determined for this speciﬁc case and based on these speciﬁc point mass scenarios
included in the analysis. However it is expected that the methodology presented in this
thesis could be implemented for other structures and show a similarly good performance
in terms of predicting the mass and location of a structural change. It should be noted
that this analysis is based on a numerical study of a small and simple structure, and it is
at this point unknown how the methodology will perform for large scale structures where
the complexity of the structure is higher. For the purpose of SHM it should be noted that
no environmental eﬀect was included in this study.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
The results in this thesis show that the ﬁnal model successfully determines that a structural
change has occurred, and estimates the location and magnitude of the added point mass
with an acceptable precision. The ﬁnal model is based on a MVLR analysis. While other
parameters was considers as predictors in the MVLR, the ﬁnal model did only include the
identiﬁed frequencies to predict the state of the structure.
Two identiﬁcation techniques was used, EFDD and TDPR with condensation, to iden-
tify the model parameters of the simulated cases. However only the outputs of TDPR
with condensation was used to determine the ﬁnal model. TDPR with condensation was
preferred over the EFDD since it performed better in determining the mode shapes.
A general tendency of the ﬁnal model shows that the most diﬃculties are found for
the lowest mass magnitudes considered in this analysis. For the structure analyzed in this
thesis a discontinuity in the frequencies was found, which was not intended in the design
phase. Investigations showed that the discontinuity was a result of mode shapes changing
from xy bending modes to diagonal bending modes. A larger prediction error was found
in the area where the discontinuity occurred.
A methodology of a scenario based detection of a structural change has been presented
in this thesis.
An important part of this methodology is the selection of predictors
along with evaluating and selection of the best model. The ﬁnal model is not a general
model for all structures, but the procedure of this scenario based approach for detecting a
structural change can be applied to other structures. Further investigations, and especially
experimental studies, are needed to fully prove the potential of this methodology for SHM.

CHAPTER 10
Future Perspectives
In this study it was shown how MVLR could be used to ﬁnd the location and magnitude
of a structural change. However this study was based on a simple test structure and was
purely numerical. In this chapter a few considerations for future studies will be introduced.
An essential future study is to investigate if this method can be used for SHM for
real life civil engineering structures. Before moving into experiments of large scale and
high complexity structures, various other studies using the methodology presented in this
thesis must be made. An initial study could be to investigate if the results obtained in this
thesis could be reproduced from a physical test of a similar structure. Furthermore a new
numerical and/or real structure with a higher complexity could be analysed to verify the
methodology. Another study could be to introduce other kinds of structural changes, for
example reducing the cross section or cutting into the structure.
Due to computational limits it was not possible to include all the predictors in a single
MVLR analysis. Based on this a future study could be to investigate how much the MVLR
would improve if a higher number of predictors could be included in the MVLR. Also
investigations could be carried out to see if other structural parameters could be included
to improve the MVLR.
