Abstract
During the last three decades an increased occurrence of multi-drug resistant pathogens has
been observed. This has become an very important public health challenge and due to this
an investigation of the diﬀerent resistance mechanisms and resistance proﬁles are of very big
importance and interest.
In this study a program named Mutational Resistance Finder (MRF) was made in order
to ﬁnd chromosomal mutations causing antimicrobial resistance. These mutations were ver-
iﬁed by minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) determination.
The program was also
compared with PointFinder-3.0.py (PF) in order to see if the same mutations were found by
both programs. Furthermore, sequences of Klebsiella pneumoniae was collected and received
from The European Nucleotide Archive (ENA) and Statens Serum Institut (SSI). The se-
quences from SSI were used to detect chromosomal mutations using the two above mentioned
programs. Sequences from bot ENA and SSI were used for phylogenetic analysis.
The algorithm of MRF was found not to perform as well as PF, which was observed to be due
to the algorithm not calculating the insertion and/or deletions correctly when the sequence
was reverse complemented. Furthermore, the results from MIC did not match the mutational
ﬁndings both programs found to cause antimicrobial resistance, which could indicate that
K. pneumoniae uses multi-factorial mechanisms for antimicrobial resistance.
Keywords: Klebsiella pneumoniae, chromosomal mutations, Mutational Resis-
tance Finder, PointFinder MIC determination, phylogenetic analysis, BEAST

Purpose and Aims
Multi-drug resistant pathogens such as Klebsiella pneumoniae has increased in occurrences
during the last three decades and has developed multiple diﬀerent mechanisms to become
resistant to antimicrobials.
The aim of this study was divided into three objectives. The ﬁrst objective was to use a
program, which was made by me in spring 2017 to detect chromosomal mutations in K.
pneumoniae and implement ﬁndings of insertions and deletions in whole genome sequences
of Klebsiella pneumoniae. The new program is called Mutational Resistance Finder (MRF).
The previous program contained a database of known mutations of K. pneumoniae to cause
antimicrobial resistance from literature study and this database should be extended to in-
clude more chromosomal mutations. This database should be used in two diﬀerent programs;
PointFinder and MRF. The MRF should afterwards be compared with PointFinder, in order
to check if the two programs ﬁnds the same chromosomal mutations.
The second objective was to collect the whole database of Klebsiella pneumoniae from The
European Nucleotide Archive (ENA) for phylogenetic analysis. Together with the resistance
proﬁle and phylogenetic characterization statistics of time and geography would be calcu-
lated.
The third objective was to verify if the mutations found by MRF did cause antimicrobial
resistance. This was done by determining the minimum inhibitory concentrations (MICs) of
each antimicrobial drug for some of the isolates.

1. Background
1
Background
1.1
Klebsiella pneumoniae
Multi-drug resistant (MDR) pathogens has during the last three decades increased in oc-
currences and are becoming an important public health challenge worldwide. There are two
ways of which bacteria can acquire antimicrobial resistance; either by transferring plasmids
by horizontal transfer, containing antibiotic resistance gene(s) or by mutation(s) in their
DNA due to errors occurred during transcription, translation or replication[16].
Klebsiella pneumoniae is a Gram-negative, facultative anaerobic, encapsulated, and rod-
shaped bacterium, belonging to the family of Enterobacteriaceae. Usually, it is found in
the intestines, the normal ﬂora of the skin, and the mouth. It can very fast colonize the
human mucosal surfaces, especially the gastrointestinal tract. Furthermore, it also invades
other sites, causing severe nosocomial infections, such as pneumonia, urinary tract infections,
septicemia, and meningitis[25]. It has been observed that there is an increased risk of worse
clinical outcomes and even death if infected by a resistant K. pneumoniae strain, compared
to patients infected with a non-resistant Klebsiella pneumoniae strain. Due to this increas-
ing progress, infections caused by K. pneumoniae has developed to be very community and
hospital acquired[16][15][11].
During the years K. pneumoniae has evolved to become a MDR microorganism, acquir-
ing high resistance towards extended spectrum of β-lactams (ESBLs), carbapenems, ﬂuo-
roquinolones, aminoglycosides, and tigecycline, resulting in almost all available treatment
options being ineﬀective. This has caused the World Health Organization to identify K.
pneumoniae as an urgent threat to human health[32][37]. When trying to explain the cause
of antimicrobial resistance the knowledge of which mechanisms of action and how the mech-
anisms in antibiotic resistance of K. pneumoniae works have become interesting.
In this study, isolates from The European Nucleotide Archive (ENA) and Statens Serum
Institut (SSI) were collected an received. The sequences were either in fasta or fastq for-
mat (sequenced or as raw reads). The raw reads were analyzed by St. Petersburg genome
assembler (SPAdes) with the aim of using the assembled sequences for phylogenetic charac-
terization and identiﬁcation of mutations in the chromosomal genes of K. pneumoniae.
1.2
Mechanisms of antibiotic resistance in Klebsiella pneumoniae
Antimicrobial agents such as β-lactams (including carbapenems and cephalosporins), ﬂuo-
roquinolones, tetracyclines, and aminoglycosides are some of the most clinically important
classes of antibiotics used to treat K. pneumoniae infections. This is mainly due to their
safety and relative eﬃcacy[31]. The usage of these antimicrobial drugs has emerged due to
selection pressure resulting in evolutionary adaptation in Gram-negative bacteria such as
K. pneumoniae[24]. The mechanisms of how this development of antimicrobial resistance

1.2.1
Antimicrobial Drugs
Carbapenems (imipenem, biapenem, ertapenem, meropenem, and doripenem) are antibi-
otics used to treat severe infections caused by multi-resistant Enterobacteriaceae, such as
K. pneumoniae.
Most of the genes important for mediating carbapenem resistance are
present in K. pneumoniae and is the main cause of infections caused by carbapenem-resistant
worldwide[10].
Cephalosproins is a class of extended spectrum β-lactamases (ESBLs) and are most com-
monly used in treating K. pneumoniae infections. However, treatment with this class of
antibiotic has also been complicated, due to the increased resistance by the production of
ESBL or AmpC-type β-lactamase by K. pneumoniae[16][26][8].
Fluoroquinolones are broad-spectrum antibiotics, which have been used for treatment of re-
sistant or severe infections. Because of their spectrum of eﬃcacy against a wide range of
Gram-negative and Gram-positive pathogens it has been used world wide[10][24].
Tigecycline are extended broad-spectrum antibiotics belonging to a class called glycocyclines.
It is often used against carbapenemase- and ESBL-producing K. pneumoniae. Tigecycline
is one of the antimicrobial drugs used as the last solution in treatment of MDR Enterobac-
teriaceae[28].
1.2.2
Membrane permeability
1.2.3
Resistance Genes Mediating Quinolone and Fluoroquinolone Resistance
Resistance to quinolones and ﬂuoroquinolones in K. pneumoniae is multifactorial and is
known to be a combination of mutations in target-site genes, modifying enzymes, and
increased production of MDR eﬄux pumps[21].
Encoded genes on plasmids leading to
quinolone resistance are known as plasmid-mediated quinolone resistance (PMQR) genes.
One of PMQR gene is a variant of aminoglycoside acetyl transferase aac(6’)-lb-cr and has
the ability of degrading certain ﬂuoroquinolones[27][10].
Another mechanism is the double-membrane structure of Gram-negative bacteria cell wall
presents a barrier for hydrophilic molecules which can only cross the membrane via porin
proteins. Mutations that results in downregulation of these porins increase the MIC and are
commonly seen in ﬂuoroquinolone resistant isolates[27].

Furthermore, chromosomal multidrug eﬄux pumps has the ability of actively remove ﬂu-
oroquinolones and other drugs from the bacterial cell.
Mutations in these results in an
over-expression of this system[27][34]. One of the genes mediating this over-expression is
acrR, which is a part of the acrRAB eﬄux pump operon regulating the acrAB eﬄux system

1.2.4
Resistance Genes Mediating Tigecycline Resistance
Known resistance mechanisms to cause tigecycline resistance includes modiﬁcations of both
the 16S and the 30S ribosomal unit targets of the antibiotic along with changes in the cell
permeability[21][24].
1.2.5
Resistance Genes Mediating β-lactamase Resistance
AmpC β-lactamase are important cephalosporinases, encoded in the chromosomes in many
Enterobacteriaceae, where they mediate resistance to most antimicrobial drugs such as peni-
cillins, cefazolin, cefoxitin, and cephalothin by breaking down their structures. When muta-
tions occur, the AmpC-lactamase enzymes can be overexpressed leading to resistance to a
broad-spectrum of cephalosporins[16][26].
1.2.6
Point Mutations in gyrA and parC
One or more mutations in gyrA, gyrB, parC, and parE is one of the most common mechnisms
of high-level ﬂuoroquinolone resistance.
The region where mutations occur is known as
the quinolone resistance-determing reion (QRDR). Mutations in this region of these genes
alter the target protein and ﬂuoroquinolone-binding aﬃnity due to amino acid substitution
leading to drug resistance. Highly resistant Gram-negative organisms will typically carry a
combination of mutations within both gyrA and parC[27][30].
1.2.7
Point Mutations in Outer Memebrane Proteins
The two largest known porins of K. pneumoniae are ompK35 and ompK36.
Important
amino acids in the ompK36 structure and function has been studied to observe if mutations
of these has an impact on antimicrobial resistance. The study showed that mutations in the
L3 (highly conserved motif PEFGGD), L4, L5, and L6 regions of the porin have a major
impact on cephalosporin and carbapenem resistance. Especially mutations in L4 and L5
regions have been shown to cause major functional changes in the bacterial porin fuction,

1.3
Antimicrobial determination
1.3.1
Whole Genome Sequencing
The ability to detect and track outbreaks of infectious diseases and identify antimicrobial re-
sistance genes are important for understanding the underlying mechanisms of action and the
epidemiology of antimicrobial resistance. As the ﬁnancial burden of whole-genome sequenc-
ing (WGS) continue to decline, WGS has become increasingly available in routine diagnostic
laboratories and is anticipated to substitute traditional methods for resistance gene identi-
ﬁcation. WGS has therefore become very important in surveillance, infection control, and
clinical diagnostics. Especially for slow-growing bacteria, WGS-based resistance prediction
has been considered faster than phenotypic testing, where time has become an important
factor for the treatment strategy[38][36].
1.3.2
Phylogeny
Phylogeny, is a diagrammatic depiction of biological entities, which are connected through
e.g. common species and is known as an evolutionary tree. By using phylogenetic analysis
the relatedness between either diﬀerent or same species can be observed[7].
In this study both Nucleotide Diﬀerence Tree (NDtree) and Bayesian Evolutionary Analysis
Sampling Trees (BEAST) were used for phylogenetic analysis. NDtree was used to identify
the nucleotide diﬀerences in sequences of K. pneumoniae. BEAST was afterwards used to
estimate the substitution rates from alignment of SNPs identiﬁed by NDtree to interpret the
relation between the diﬀerent K. pneumoniae isolates from ENA and SSI.
1.3.3
State of the Art
Studies show that detection of some chromosomal mutations in K. pneumoniae causing
antimicrobial resistance has previously been observed[29][33][30]. The literature also reveals
that the biggest interest is on plasmid-mediated antibiotic resistance, which plays a role in
acquiring and disseminating resistance[19].
One literature study had a very detailed and in depth details of both chromosomal and
plasmid-mediated antibiotic resistance genes and the mechanisms behind in K. pneumoniae.
This is to my knowledge one of the most detailed studies only concerning K. pneumoniae[21].
This study concerns detection of chromosomal mutations and phylogenetic analysis of K.
pneumoniae, which can be seen as a new approach.

2. Materials and Methods
2
Materials and Methods
2.1
Data
In this study 379 isolates were collected from ENA as raw read in fastq format together with
a json ﬁle containing all the metadata. Furthermore, 37 isolate whole genome sequenced
sequences in fasta format were received from SSI and 35 of the 37 isolate sequences was also
received as raw reads in fastq format.
Information for the chromosomal mutation database used in MRF were all found by literature
study.
All data, including the metadata was used in this study.
2.2
Metadata
Isolates of K.pneumoniae was received from SSI, from the Carbapenemase-producing organ-
ism (CPO) surveillance. Furthermore, K. pneumoniae isolates from ENA was collected using
an in house script from the Evergreen project on Computerome. In total 37 isolate sequences
of K. pneumoniae were received from SSI and 379 were collected from ENA. The metadata
of the total collected data includes names of the isolates, number of isolates, collection date,
location note, strain, host disease, isolation source, host health state, and sequence type.
This is shown in appendix A.
For all analysis the metadata from the json ﬁle were used. Later it was observed that the
json ﬁles were currupted and due to this the metadata table was made manually.
2.3
The European Nucleotide Archive (ENA)
ENA stores and presents information regarding experimental work-ﬂows based on nucleotide
sequencing. ENA contains data from a variety of sources. These include submissions of raw
data, assembled sequences and annotation from small-scale sequencing eﬀorts, data provision
from the major European sequencing centers and routine[2].
K. pneumoniae isolates were extracted from the ENA database by using the script from
the Evergreen project on Computerome at Risoe. In total 379 isolates were extracted. The
collected data consisted of fastq ﬁles together with json ﬁles. The json ﬁles were used further

2.4
St. Petersburg genome assembler (SPAdes)
St. Petersburg genome assembler (SPAdes) is an assembler using k-mers for building the
initial de Bruijn graph. Afterwards the exact distances between the k-mers in the genome
is calculated and a paired assembly graph is constructed.
SPAdes constructs the DNA
sequences of contigs to map the reads by backtracking the graph simpliﬁcations to the
contigs[3].
SPAdes was used on raw reads from the 379 isolates of K. pneumoniae collected from ENA
and on 35 out of the 37 isolates gained from SSI, because they were the only ones in fastq
format. The results were used for further phylogenetic analysis.
2.5
Multi Locus Sequence Typing(MLST)
Multi Locus Sequence Typing(MLST) is a tool used to understand the genetic diversity of
pathogens and to understanding the evolutionary dynamics[22]. MLST is used to identify
nucleotide sequences (alleles) of 400 to 500 bp in multiple housekeeping genes in bacteria
or fungi. The alleles are assigned a random integer number and an allelic proﬁle based on
a unique combination of alleles at each locus. These together speciﬁes the sequence type.
MLST is considered as the ’gold standard’ of typing bacteria and fungi. The allelic proﬁles
can be determined by uploading the assembled genome at https://cge.cbs.dtu.dk/services/MLST/[17]
Many of the json ﬁles gained from ENA were corrupted, thus MLST was used for sequence
typing (The tool is available on https://cge.cbs.dtu.dk/services/MLST/).
MLST was used on the 37 isolates from SSI and the 379 isolates from ENA with default
identity of 95%. The analysis of the isolates were used to get an overview of their sequence
types enabling to determine which isolates should be used with Nucleotide Diﬀerence Tree
(NDtree) and to see if there were some correlation between the sequence types and diversity.

genome. It splits the reads into 17-mers by default and map these to a reference whole
genome. When all reads have been mapped, the signiﬁcance of nucleotide base call at each
position are evaluated by using a Z-score, that is deﬁned by this equation[18][12][14]:

2.6.2
Bayesian Evolutionary Analysis Sampling Trees (BEAST)
Bayesian Evolutionary Analysis Utility (BEAUti) was used on the output gained from NDtree
of the data from ENA. BEAUti is a software package that converts the input to a XML ﬁle
for a number of diﬀerent model combinations[7]. In this project the multiple alignment gen-
erated from NDtree were stripped at all unknown and identical positions by using Stripali,
which is an in house script. This gave an output of a multiple alignment only containing the
known nucleotide diﬀerences. Afterwards it was used as input ﬁle to BEAUti. BEAUti was
used two times. First time when only using it on isolates from ENA separated into world
areas and second time when using it on the isolates from both ENA and SSI, but with three
speciﬁc ST-types. The ﬁrst time the models for BEAUti was set were as follows: Tips was
set to Guess Dates, Clock was set to uncorrelated relaxed clock with lognormal as relaxed
distribution, four diﬀerent Tree priors was chosen: Coalescent: Constant size, Coalescent:
Exponential growth, Coalescent: Logistic growth, Coalescent: Expansion growth, MCMC
was set to run for 100.000.000 steps and sampled every 10.000 steps. The second time all
diﬀerent clocks and Coalescent tree priors were used, resulting in 32 diﬀerent models for all
three ST-types. The other parameters were set as previously. After this step the generated
xml ﬁles were analyzed by Bayesian Evolutionary Analysis Sampling Trees (BEAST).

BEAST is a program used for Bayesian Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) analysis of
bacteria and virus sequences. It can be used to test evolutionary hypothesis and for recon-
structing evolutionary phylogenies[39][7].
The equation for Bayes theorem, used by BEAST for model comparison is:
P(model|data) = P(data|model)P(model)
P(data)
(3)
P(model—data) is the posterior probability which is the probability of the model after the
data is observed. P(model) is the prior probability which is an estimation of the probability
of the model before the data is observed. P(data) is the observed data.
In this study BEAUti v1.8.4 and BEAST v1.8.4 were used to estimate the substitution rates
from alignment of SNPs identiﬁed by NDtree (variable sites). The data was separated into
their collection country, to determine how the samples were distributed around the world.
This was used for BEAST analysis to generate an overview of the time and geography of the
isolates.
The results were afterwards analyzed by Tracer v.1.6, which is a softeware package providing
visualization of the analysis of MCMC output giving a presentation of how well the analysis
was.
The most important parameters to look at in the Tracer are Posterior, Prior, and
Likelihood. All these parameters have to gain an Eﬀective Sample Sizes (ESS) at minimum
200. If they are below 200 Tracer will show it by colouring the numbers red and thereby
indicating that the trace contained a lot of correlated samples. This means that the speciﬁc
analysis not yielded a suﬃcient number of independent samples from the posterior for that
exact parameter and then the Chain length, Echo state, and Log parameters has to be
changed to higher numbers[4].
Treeannotator was then used to submit all the trees into one target tree. It takes the set of
trees and ﬁnds the most reliable clade tree. Then it will annotate the summary tree with
the mean ages of all nodes and the highest posterior density (HPD) ranges. Furthermore,
it calculates the mean rate of each lineage, the posterior clade posibility for each node, and
the HPD ranges[5].
Tracer was further used for comparing which model was the best for this exact data set.
Finally, Figtree was used to illustrate the results graphically.

BLAST was used as a part of the Mutational Resistance Finder (MRF) to identify the best

2.8
Identiﬁcation of point mutations in Klebsiella pneumoniae
2.8.1
PointFinder
PointFinder consists of a python script PointFinder-3.0.py. It comprises of two chromoso-
mal mutation databases; One based on chromosomal mutations with information of codon
positions and substitutions, and one based on chromosomal genes, containing all reference
sequences in fasta format.
The program uses BLASTn from NCBI to identify the best
macth for each gene in the chromosomal gene database. Only the matches with an identity
above 90% and coverage of 60% are further analyzed. It goes through each saved alignment
comparing each position of the query. All mismatches are compared with the chromosomal
mutation database. The user can select whether they want to only see known mutations
or all mismatches that are found. PointFinder is able to use raw reads in fastq format and
whole genome sequenced data in fasta format. The user can specify which input is needed by
typing ”blastn” for sequenced data and ”kma” for raw reads. The PointFinder outputs three
ﬁles, two include the found mutations and the third lists the resistance phenotype predicted
by the program [38][13]. PointFinder was used to detect chromosomal mutations in all 37
isolates from SSI, which have not yet been identiﬁed previously.
2.9
Phenotypic determination
The ResFinder at CGE (Center for Genomic Epidemiology) website was used to identify
antimicrobial resistance genes of Klebsiella pneumoniae. The results were compared with
the results from MRF in order to identify what type(s) of resistance that could be made
MIC determination on to verify the ﬁndings of the program. This identiﬁcation was made
on the 37 isolates from SSI.
MIC determination is a tool to determine in vitro activity of antimicrobials. It is deﬁned
as the lowest concentration of an antimicrobial drug that will inhibit the visible growth
of an organism after overnight incubation. MICs are considered as the ’gold standard’ for
determininng the susceptibility and resistance of organisms to antimicrobial drugs[1].
MIC determination was conducted on 8 isolates from SSI, determining tigecycline resistance.
One isolate from SSI (CPO20150082) and an Escherichia coli (ATCC 25922) strain were
used as control strains. Each bacteria strain was put on an agar plate overnight. Next day
a suspension of the isolates were prepared to a McFarland standard with an absorbance of
0.120 - 0.140. 10 mL of saltwater containing bacteria was added to a Mueller-Hinton broth
with a concentration of 105. This was done for each of the 10 bacterial strains. 21 mL was

3. Results
3.5
Mutational Resistance Finder (MRF)
In spring 2017, as a part of a pilot study, I made a program to ﬁnd chromosomal mutations
in K. pneumoniae. However, this program was very limited, because it was not able to ﬁnd
insertions or deletions (indels) among other things. Due to this the idea was to implement
ﬁndings of indels into the existing program. The program was very locked and thus it was
very hard to implement. This resulted in the evolving of a new program. The new pro-
gram is named MRF. MRF is used to ﬁnd mutations on DNA level of chromosomal genes
(ompK35, ompK36, ompK37, acrR, ramR, gyrA, and parC) in K. pneumoniae. Besides from
the python3 script, the MRF consists of a database of chromosomal genes of K. pneumoniae
in fasta format, which in literature has been studied regarding chromosomal mutations. Fur-
thermore, it includes a mutation database containing observed nucleotide mutations causing
antibiotic resistance, based on observations from literature studies[33][29][30]. The mutation
database was created in the pilot study, but during this project the database was further
developed and improved. An excerpt of the VCF ﬁle is shown in ﬁgure 16. Some of the
codons needs to have two or three mutations to cause resistance and are assigned with an
”2” or ”3” in the third column of the mutation ﬁle. MRF uses whole genome sequences for
detection of chromosomal mutations. It uses Blastn from NCBI with the chromosomal gene
database as a reference and the unidentiﬁed sequence as the query to identify the best hit of
each gene. Only matches with an identity at or above 90% and coverage of 60% are saved as
query for further analysis. Each nucleotide position in each query is compared to the saved
alignment. The mismatches are saved and used to be compared with the chromosomal mu-
tation database to identify if any of the mutations observed causes antimicrobial resistance.
The output contains ﬁndings of which mutations in the query has chromosomal mutations
that has been identiﬁed to cause antimicrobial resistance, which mutations are wobble mu-
tations, known indels, and mutations that has too few mutations to cause resistance. The
output includes furthermore unknown mutations and unknown indels. The positions in the
output are the nucloetide position of where a mutations was observed. The thresholds are
set by default, but the user can change these if needed.

4. Discussion
4
Discussion
4.1
Comparison of the program
The PointFinder and MRF was expected to ﬁnd the same number of mutations and at the
same nucleotide positions. Only 72% of the total number of mutations were found by both
of the programs. When manually looking at the positions where PointFinder only predicted
a mutation, it was observed that all these were actual mutations. However, mutations pre-
dicted only by MRF were not correct. It was observed that the counting of deletions and/or
insertions for some of the nucleotide positions were wrong. This is caused by MRF not count-
ing insertions and/or deletions correctly after a sequence has been reverse complemented.
Therefore, MRF needs to be debugged to correct this. When not taking the mutations MRF
only found into account, PointFinder found 100% of the mutations, where MRF found 75%
of all mutations.
When comparing the computational time in average MRF was 3.5 times faster than PointFinder,
when running on Computerome. This could be due to PointFinder calling a second ﬁle, which
is used to save the best alignment generated from BLASTn and then PointFinder uses this
for comparison. This could require some extra computational time, whereas MRF only re-
quires one ﬁle.
PointFinder was the only program enabling to ﬁnd all mutations in all isolates from SSI. If
MRF will be debugged and changed in that manner that it will calculate the insertions and
deletions correctly, then it could be as eﬃcient as PointFinder.
4.2
Laboratory results
The results from MIC determination showed that only one of the eight isolates was resis-
tant to tigecycline. On the CARD (The Comprehensive Antibiotic Resistance Database)
webpage[6] this mutation (ramR gene at nucleotide position 56) in K. pneumoniae was de-
scribed as a known mutation causing tigecycline resistance. This could indicate that other
mechanisms besides chromosomal mutations has an inﬂuence on the resistance proﬁle. When
comparing the results from ResFinder the main observation was that the isolate, Hil3, lacked
the aac(6)-Ib-cr gene, whereas the others had this gene. Because this gene is involved in
ﬂuoroquinolone resistance it was interesting to see if the MIC values of ciproﬂoxacin and
levoﬂoxacin decreased when lacing the aac(6)-Ib-cr gene. The result was that Hil3 had one
of the highest MIC values in ﬂuoroquinolone resistance. This could indicate that more mech-
anisms have to be in to play simultaneously before showing resistance to ﬂuoroquinolones.
34
==PAGE:48==
4. Discussion
4.3
BEAST
Unfortunately, BEAST results did not give any reliable phylogenetic tree. One explanation
of why the results did not ﬁt any of the models could be because BEAST was used on posi-
tions where SNPs were found and not the whole genome. It could be that if running BEAST
with all positions of the genome and not only SNP positions then the model would ﬁt the
data better. If all positions always should be included it would take much longer time, be
more expensive and usually it is not needed.
To summarize, PointFinder was in overall more precise in predicting mutations leading to
antimicrobial resistance.
Furthermore, from the MIC determination it was seen that K.
pneumoniae uses many diﬀerent mechanisms to escape antimicrobial drugs.

5. Future perspectives
5
Future perspectives
Future perspectives of this study would be to change the counting of insertions and deletions
for reverse complement sequences in MRF, enabling it to be more precise. Implementation
of KMA would make MRF to be able to handle raw reads in fastq format which also would
improve the program.
Furthermore, the chromosomal database could be further improved, as well as the gene
database.
If this study should be made again, the BEAST parameters should be changed and try
to make a new analysis.
Furthermore, to get a better overview of the metadata of K.
pneumoniae the whole database from ENA should be collected.
