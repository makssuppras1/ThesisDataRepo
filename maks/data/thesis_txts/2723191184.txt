Abstract
Lipid nanoparticles loaded with messenger RNA (LNP-mRNA) and the presence of ionizable
lipids have risen in popularity as drug delivery systems and non-viral gene therapy carriers
after the massive success of Moderna/BioNTech and Pfizer’s vaccine against COVID-19.
These systems can encapsulate and protect different types of molecules and grant significant
cellular uptake and a successful endosomal escape. Amongst other advantages, LNPs possess
excellent biocompatibility and encapsulation efficiency. To assess a particle’s encapsulation
efficiency is crucial to compare a sample of intact mRNA-loaded lipid nanoparticles with
another where the LNPs are disrupted and the mRNA is free.
The RiboGreen assay is
one of the most popular assays for mRNA quantification and the most utilized to evaluate
encapsulation efficiency. However, many discussions surround the assay’s performance due
to difficulties on mRNA quantification in presence of lipids, both inside of intact LNPs and
after disruption. There also needs to be more explanation about the best surfactant and
concentration to achieve the highest particle dissociation. Other questions regarding the
interaction of the surfactant with the particle loading and the possible shielding of the free
mRNA by the formation of micelles are recurrent when discussing the effects of the assay. In
this project, we will bring some light to the topic and study the correlation between several
variables and the assay’s performance.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Aim
The RiboGreen assay was mentioned in a study in 1998 [1]. Since then, an extensive range
of scientists in the drug delivery community have used the RiboGreen assay to assess their
LNPs encapsulation efficiencies.
This project aims to find correlations between different
variables like the surfactant and its concentration, as well as the incubation method, and
the assay’s performance to improve the assay and get better and more consistent data with
a better insight into how the RiboGreen assay works.

Chapter 2
Scientific Background
2.1
Drug Delivery
Drug delivery, fundamentally the administration of a pharmaceutical ingredient to generate
a therapeutic effect, is gaining attention to avoid the general organism effect and achieve
a more targeted one. In other words, the main goal of drug delivery systems (DDS) is to
deliver drugs more efficiently [2]. For that reason, the route of administration and the char-
acteristics of the drug delivery system are crucial issues that require thorough investigation.
Drugs are often delivered by intravenous, oral, nasal, pulmonary, buccal, and transdermal
routes. Any device that allows the body to absorb a medicinal substance qualifies as a drug
delivery system [3]. DDS, which include particulate carriers predominantly made of lipids
and polymers, can enhance many of the pharmacological characteristics of traditional med-
ications. These DDS are made with the intention of changing the pharmacokinetics (PK)
and biodistribution (BD), enhancing blood circulation and avoidinc clearance of the drugs
they are paired with [4].
Pharmaceuticals were administered via traditional techniques in the early days of medi-
cal DDS, such as liquid solutions, lotions, creams, ointments, powders, injectables, pills, and
3
==PAGE:15==
capsules. Liposomes, micelles, transdermal patches, oral controlled release systems, aerosols,
rapidly dispersing dosage forms, taste-masking systems, and site-specific delivery systems are
examples of modern drug delivery systems with significantly increased therapeutic poten-
tial. One of the biggest problems was for big molecules to reach the intracellular space. DDS
overcome that limitation due to their ability to transport any cargo to the cytoplasm of the
cells. Most DDS licensed for parenteral administration are therapeutic compounds derived
from polyethylene glycol (PEG), liposomal or lipid-based formulations [4].
2.2
Messenger RNA (mRNA)
In eukaryotic cells, RNA polymerase transcription of DNA in the nucleus produces naturally
occurring mRNA. The mRNA molecules are carried from the nucleus to the cytoplasm, where
the ribosomes use them as templates to translate into certain proteins. Therapeutics made
of mRNA have numerous advantages as well as several challenges compared to other modali-
ties like small molecules, DNA, oligonucleotides, and antibodies. Messenger RNA only needs
access to the cytoplasmic ribosomal translation machinery, not the nucleus like the DNA,
and does not risk genomic interaction. Besides the protein for which it is coding, ribosomes
cannot produce other proteins by translating this mRNA. For a more efficient translation
by the ribosomes, different chemical alterations can be made to the mRNA molecule when
it is employed as a therapeutic drug (e.g., nucleoside modification and codon optimization).
One disadvantage of mRNA as a therapeutic drug is the activation of the immune system.
Toll-like receptors (TLR) 3, 7, and 8 facilitate foreign mRNA to activate innate immune
cells. This TLR ligation will cause cytokine synthesis, developing adaptative T and B cell
responses [5].
Another risk is mRNA degradation. Naked mRNA is susceptible to enzymatic degrada-
tion by RNAses. After injection, the predicted half-life of mRNA is 8 to 10 hours before it
4
==PAGE:16==
rapidly deteriorates and is destroyed. mRNA also has almost no levels of cell uptake. For
this reason, the encapsulation of mRNA in nanoparticles protects and supports the intra-
cellular delivery. Besides that, drug carriers also ensure an adequate cellular uptake and
an efficient endosomal escape that allows the mRNA to be translated by the ribosomes.
This project will focus on mRNA-loaded lipid nanoparticles (mRNA-LNPs), nanospherical
structures composed of lipids that protect the mRNA and serve as a DDS. This type of drug
carrier is used in fields like gene therapy, and vaccine development [6].
2.3
Lipid Nanoparticles and Gene Delivery
Nucleic acids can barely permeate tissues and enter cells like tiny molecules. That is also
because they are also highly charged, which limits absorption due to the negative charge of
the membranes. LNPs are spherical vesicles made of different types of lipids: an ionizable
lipid that is protonated at low pH and neutral at physiological pH, a helper lipid with no
charge, a packing lipid that affects the rigidity of the LNP membrane, and a PEGylated lipid,
which improves drug stability, solubility and decreases immunogenecity. LNPs are used to
transport molecules or drugs across membranes. These carriers are considered safe to employ
for the patient due to their no toxicity and can encapsulate vast volumes of material. This
is effective and simple compared to other modes of therapy, like viral vectors.
Lipid nanoparticle delivery techniques are still gaining more and more importance in science.
The ideal illustration is the widely accepted use of lipidic nanocarriers in Moderna/BioNTech
and Pfizer COVID-19 mRNA vaccines. A very important advantage of these formulations is
that part of the lipids do not have a charge, and one of the components is an ionizable lipid,
which can gain a positive charge depending on the pH of the environment. Non-charged
or neutral carriers are very important to avoid non-wanted bindings to other molecules in
the organism, like proteins. On the contrary, if the LNPs are too positively charged, they
5
==PAGE:17==
might attract other proteins in the organism, which will attach to them and increase their
size, making them prone to elimination [7]. Additionally, Cheng et al., 2020 found that the
surface charge of the LNPs influences their biodistribution. Neutral LNPs target mainly the
liver, slightly positive LNPs target the lungs, and slightly negative LNPs mainly target the
spleen [8]. Therefore, depending on the charge of the LNPs, different diseases in different
organs can be treated.
Due to their excellent stability and biocompatibility, lipids have
been employed in varieties of nanocarrier systems, such as liposomes. LNPs are capable of
encasing nucleic acids for gene delivery [9].
mRNA-LNPs are used in COVID-19 vaccines and offer safe and effective means to protect
mRNA drug substances to avoid degradation through encapsulation via electrostatic interac-
tion and mediate drug delivery into the cytoplasm, where it is active. mRNA is formulated
into LNPs by mixing mRNA and acidic aqueous buffer with lipids in ethanol to initiate
self-assembly. In the most common formulations, ethanol contains ionizable cationic lipid,
cholesterol, distearoyl PC (DSPC), and PEG lipid at molar ratios of 40-50/40-30/10/10-
1, respectively. The formulation is normally mixed at a lipid-to-oligonucleotide phosphate
(N:P) ratio of 6:1 [10]. Typically, the aqueous medium buffer and the ethanol are mixed in a
3:1 ratio. After mixing, the LNP formulation is dialyzed against a pH 7.4 buffer in order to
increase the pH to physiological levels causing the ionizable lipid to lose the positive charge
and become neutral, and eliminate any remaining ethanol. Spin-filtration is another type of
buffer exchange method [11].
The self-assembly of LNPs requires the coordination of millions of molecules and a well-
controlled environment promoting rapid and consistent mixing. This is crucial to ensure
that particles meet critical quality attributes that dictate the safety and efficacy of the drug
product. Precision Nanosystems NxGenTM NanoAssemblr®mixers employ proprietary mi-
crofluidics technology to rapidly bring fluids together in a laminar and controlled manner.
6
==PAGE:18==
In theory, every particle is manufactured under the same localized conditions. As the fluids
mix on first contact, the cationic lipids complex with the negatively charged nucleic acids to
form the beginnings of the particle core. The helper lipids aggregate further as they come in
contact with the aqueous solution. Finally, the PEG lipids orient themselves on the surface
with hydrophobic tails pointing inwards. This process creates uniform particles and is highly
reproducible (Figure 2.1)[12].

2.3.1
Endosomal escape
LNPs are mainly internalized into cells through the endocytosis pathway [13]. This route
involves endocytic vesicle internalization, fusion into the early endosomal compartment, mat-
uration into a late endosome, and finally, accumulation in the lysosome. During the endo-
7
==PAGE:19==
somal maturation phase, the pH first decreases from physiological pH (7.4) to pH 6.5 in the
early endosome, then to pH 6 in the late endosome, and finally, it drops all the way to pH
5 in the lysosome. Several studies indicate the importance of a successful endosomal escape
in the delivery of therapeutics because a failure in this matter often results in entrapment
and potential particle degradation in the lysosome [13].
As figure 2.2 depicts, there are several theories about the mechanism of endosomal escape of
DDS: endosomal escape via membrane fusion, using osmotic pressure, nanoparticle swelling,
and through membrane destabilization. However, mRNA-LNPs mainly escape the endo-
some through electrostatic interaction between the positively charged particles and nega-
tively charged endosomal membrane by fusing with the endosomal membrane or inducing
an osmotic shock [13]. Several factors, including the composition of the LNPs, the type of
target cell, and the dosage of the LNPs, can affect how well mRNA-LNPs promote endoso-
mal escape. However, it has been demonstrated that mRNA-loaded LNPs generally achieve
a high level of effectiveness in delivering mRNA to the cytoplasm of cells[13].
2.4
Lipids Used for Lipid Nanoparticle Formulation
Lipids are three-domain amphiphilic molecules with a polar head group, a hydrophobic tail
area, and a linker connecting the two domains. Cationic lipids, ionizable lipids, and other
lipid types have been investigated for mRNA transport. This will be elaborated further in
the report.
2.4.1
Cationic Lipids
Cationic lipids possess a head group with a permanent positive charge. A few examples of
cationic lipids used alone or with other materials in mRNA delivery could be 1,2-dioleoyl-
sn-glycero-3-phospoethanolamine (DOPE) and 1,2-dioleoyl-3-trimethylammonium-propane
8
==PAGE:20==
Figure 2.2: Schematic representation of different endosomal escape methods. (a) Membrane fusion
is generated between the LNPs and the endosomal membrane. (b) Osmotic Rupture, the proton
sponge mechanism where the influx of chloride increases the osmotic pressure. (c) Particle Swelling
of the pH-responsive nanogel ruptures the endosomal membrane. (d) Membrane Destabilization,

(DOTAP). Cationic lipids are the precursors of ionizable lipids, which have grown consider-
ably in popularity in LNPs formulations. This is because cationic lipids can cause toxicity
and immunogenicity issues due to their positive charge, leading to interactions with nega-
tively charged biomolecules such as proteins and cell membranes [14].
2.4.2
Ionizable Lipids
Acid dissociation constant (pKa) values for ionizable lipids are generally lower than seven.
The amine groups on the ionizable lipid get protonated and positively charged at low pH, en-
abling the assembly with the phosphate groups on the mRNA, which are negatively charged.
For the same reason, they remain neutral at physiological pH. The LNP formulation has
demonstrated benefits for complexation, encapsulation, and delivery efficacy when an ion-
izable lipid is included in the formulation. Moreover, the positive charge of the lipid may
9
==PAGE:21==
stimulate endosomal membrane instability and allow nanoparticles to escape them (Figure
2.2) [14].
In the acidic environment of the endosome, zwitterionic lipids, made up of a pH-switchable
zwitterion and three hydrophobic tails, can assemble into a cone, allowing for membrane
hexagonal transformation and evacuation from the endosome.
SM–102
SM-102 is an amino lipid that can be used in the formulation of lipid nanoparticles and can
be used for the development of mRNA-based vaccines. SM-102 is part of the DDS in the
Moderna COVID-19 vaccine. Moderna developed new classes of ionizable lipids intending
to increase potency by enabling more significant branching. As seen in figure 2.2, this new
sub-type of lipids has an ethanolamine ionizable head group connected to a single saturated
tail with a primary degradable ester and a second saturated tail that splits into two satu-
rated C8 tails using a less degradable second ester. SM-102 was shown to be the best for
intramuscular delivery (IM), even though several of these lipids are ideal for intravenous (IV)

2.5
mRNA Quantification
Many molecular biology techniques must be carried out in order to identify and quantify
nucleic acids. Accurate RNA quantification is crucial in applying sophisticated molecular
biology methods to get effective findings. High-Performance Liquid Chromatography can
be used in order to quantify the concentration of mRNA in an LNP sample. In addition
to that, there is The Stunner. The main advantage of the Stunner combines both UV/Vis
spectroscopy and DLS and can measure LNP size, PdI, and mRNA concentration without
having to use different instruments.
Nucleic acid concentrations can be determined using fluorescent nucleic acid stains, which
offer higher sensitivity and fewer contaminant interference than 260-nm absorbance assays.
An assay was developed for detecting picogram levels of double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) us-
ing the PicoGreen reagent. Few dyes exhibit the fluorescence increase, intensity, and linear
response necessary for a sensitive RNA solution test. As a result, a small variety of dyes
may be used for sensitive fluorescence-based RNA detection. Ethidium bromide has been
widely used to quantify RNA in solution. Even though the test is only effective for detecting
RNA quantities over 200 ng/mL since the dye has a strong intrinsic fluorescence. Another
well-known stain for RNA solution quantitation is the SYBR Green II, which has a detec-
tion limit of 2 ng/mL of RNA in a fluorometer cuvette. However, this stain was initially
13
==PAGE:25==
developed as a sensitive gel stain for RNA and, therefore, lacks the desirable fluorescence
intensity for this kind of assays [1].
The main issue is that these dyes have low quantum yields and fluorescence enhancements
upon RNA binding. For that reason, there is a lack of dyes or stains capable of effectively
measuring little RNA concentrations in the solution. A more sensitive fluorescence-based
RNA test is intended to be achieved with the RiboGreen RNA quantification reagent.
2.5.1
RiboGreen assay
The RiboGreen dye binds to RNA in solution to provide the highest quantum yield, ex-
tinction coefficient, broadest linear response range, detection sensitivity, and fluorescence
intensification of any dye.
Using a fluorescent solution test, the spectrum characteristics of the RiboGreen reagent
contrasted with those of ethidium bromide, the industry-standard reagent for RNA quanti-
tation, in 1998. The unbound RiboGreen reagent has an absorbance maximum of 482 nm,
the same as unbound ethidium bromide. It was found that the unbound molar extinction
coefficient of the RiboGreen reagent was more than ten times higher than the absorption
maximum of ethidium bromide [1].
The fluorescence of the RiboGreen reagent was also assessed to increase RNA binding. It
was shown to be greater than 1,000-fold, rendering the signal gathered from the unbound
dye, insignificant. Compared to the difference made using ethidium bromide, this difference
is more than three times bigger[1].
The fluorescence response in the case of RiboGreen depends on both the nucleic acid and
the dye. Additionally dependent on dye concentration is the range of the linear fluorescence
14
==PAGE:26==
response. The RiboGreen RNA quantification reagent may be used to quantify two distinct
ranges of nucleic acid content. Only 75 nM of the RiboGreen reagent is required for RNA
concentrations between 1 ng/mL and 50 ng/mL, whereas 750 nM of the dye can be used
to measure ribonucleic acid concentrations between 20 ng/mL and 1 µg/mL. Higher dye
concentrations result in a rise in background fluorescence, followed by a potential decrease
in fluorescence response due to quenching. The lowest detection sensitivity of RiboGreen is
200 times greater than that of ethidium bromide. The sensitivity of the RiboGreen test is
two times greater than that of SYBR Green II[1].
2.6
Surfactants
Surfactants are chemicals that decrease surface tension when added to a liquid, improving
the spreadability and wetting qualities. The surface-active molecule has to have both hy-
drophilic and lipophilic properties. As an emulsifying or foaming agent, it concentrates at
the interfaces of water bodies or droplets and lipids, such as oil. Defoamers and demulsifiers
can be made from additional surfactants that are more lipophilic and less hydrophilic. A
few surfactants are insecticides, fungicides, and antiseptics [2].
The phospholipid molecules that make up the lipid bilayer of the membrane are strikingly
similar to those of surfactants. Surfactants alter the natural structure of the lipid bilayer by
adhering to the cell membrane, which lowers surface tension. The result of this interaction
depends on the concentration and tends to solubilize cell membranes at sufficiently high
concentrations, causing cell lysis. Similarly, surfactants can also dissociate LNPs due to the
presence of a lipid phase in their membrane [19].
This section will focus on three surfactants used throughout the project in the RiboGreen
assay. Also, a key concept will be introduced, the CMC. The critical micelle concentration
15
==PAGE:27==
(CMC) is the surfactant concentration at which micelle production occurs in a solution. The
CMC is related to the molecular structure, exposing the hydrophobic parts to the center and
the hydrophilic parts to the outside, making them water-soluble. Surfactant is added to the
solution to reduce interfacial energy and remove hydrophobic groups from the surfactant
surface in contact with the water. Instead, the free energy of the system decreases. The
molecules of the surfactant group together and form micelles as the concentration of the sur-
factant in the solution rises. By reducing the interaction between the hydrophobic groups
and the surfactant, the creation of micelles significantly reduces the free energy of the solu-
tion. More micelles form when the surfactant concentration is raised above the CMC value,
but the free energy of the solution is scarcely decreased [20].
2.6.1
Triton X-100
Triton X-100 (Figure 2.6) is a non-ionic surfactant with an aromatic hydrocarbon lipophilic
group and a hydrophilic polyethylene oxide chain. The hydrocarbon group is 4-(1,1,3,3-
tetramethylbutyl)-phenyl and contains an average of 9.5 ethylene oxide units. Triton X-100
is soluble in water at a temperature of 25 °C. Among many other things, this detergent is
frequently used in the lab to permeabilize unfixed eukaryotic cell membranes and inactivate
viruses with lipid envelopes [21].
Its critical micelle concentration (CMC) in water is 0.22 mM or 0.0137 %, and it has a
molecular weight of 625 g/mol. When performing the RiboGreen assay, one concentration
below and one above the CMC of this surfactant was used: 0.01 % and 2 %.

Buffer Exchange
The ethanol contained in the LNPs sample has to be removed and the pH of the formulation

Dialysis
The final LNPs, collected from the NanoAssemblr Ignite®, are transferred to a dialysis
chamber (Slide-A-Lyzer 0.5-3 mL, 1000 MW cut-off) using a 3 mL syringe. The dialysis
chamber is placed inside a 600 mL beaker with 400 mL Tris buffer 20 mM at pH 7.4 for 5
minutes to wet the membranes. Hereafter, the particles are transferred to the chamber using
the syringe, making sure not to break the dialysis membrane with the needle. All the air
is sucked out of the chamber so the whole sample is in contact with the membrane so the
whole membrane is used for the buffer exchange process. This process takes up to 16 hours
at 4 °C and with minor stirring. Finally, the sample is extracted from the chamber using
a syringe and stored in an eppendorf tube at 4 °C. It is important to weigh the Eppendorf
tube before and after to have a clear idea of how much sample volume there is so the LNPs
concentration can be calculated accurately.
4.2
Dynamic Light Scattering
Depending on the light source and detector, particular characteristics of molecules may be
examined using the useful technique of light scattering. A sample is subjected to a monochro-
matic light wave in a typical light-scattering experiment, and a suitable detector picks up
23
==PAGE:35==
the signal.
The Brownian motion of dispersed particles is the basis of dynamic light scattering. When
disseminated in a solution, particles travel unevenly in all directions. The essential principle
behind Brownian motion is that solvent molecules and particle collisions are continuous.
The energy that is transferred during these encounters causes particle movement. Due to
the more or less constant energy transfer, smaller particles are impacted more. Therefore,
smaller particles are moving faster than bigger ones. Knowing all factors that influence parti-
cle movement, the hydrodynamic diameter can be computed by measuring particle speed[22].
The link between particle speed and size is given by the Stokes-Einstein equation.
The
speed of the particle is determined by the translational diffusion coefficient D. Additionally
since temperature and dispersant viscosity directly affect particle mobility, they are also
taken into account in the calculation. In order to use the Stokes-Einstein equation, the
particle motion must only depend on Brownian motion. Sedimentation can cause larger
particles to settle to the bottom of the sample, making the instrument only measure the
smaller particles. Hence, the resulting value is an underestimation of the true particle size
[22].
The success of the self-assembly of nanoparticles is determined by the measurement of
their size. Through dynamic light scattering, the size of the nanoparticles was determined
(Malvern Panalytical, DLS Zetasizer Nano series- Nano ZS). The test samples were diluted
by taking 10 µL of the test sample and diluting it in 990 µL of MQ water. The dilution
was necessary to reach the detection limit of the Malvern Panalytical DLS apparatus. The
desired size of LNPs was 80 nm to 100 nm to achieve the best balance between efficient drug
delivery, prolonged circulation times, and enhanced stability[23].

4.3
Zeta-Potential
Zeta-Potential in colloidal systems is the name given to electrokinetic potential. The electric
potential in the double layer of an interface between a dispersed particle or droplet and a
point in the continuous phase distant from the interface is known as the "Zeta-Potential"
theoretically. It is the difference in potential between the fixed layer of the dispersion medium
that is connected to the dispersed particle and the mobile layer of the dispersion medium,
and it is mostly influenced by the pH of the medium. Additional factors include temperature,
the concentration of any additional components, and ionic strength.
Zeta-Potential is crucial because its value may be used to forecast both the short- and
long-term stability of emulsions known as LNP formulations. Positive or negative emulsions
with a high Zeta-Potential are electrically stabilized, whereas emulsions with a neutral Zeta-
Potential have a tendency to coagulate, which may jeopardize their physical stability. The
repulsive forces often surpass the attractive forces when the Zeta-Potential of an emulsion is
high, resulting in a system that is rather stable[24]. The existence of charge impacting the
lipid nanoparticles is determined by measuring the charge of the synthesized nanoparticles.
In the end, determining the charge of the formulation can aid in determining whether or not
lipid nanoparticle self-assembly was successful and provide an overview of its stability. The
Malvern Panalytical Zetasizer Nano ZS was used to assess the charge of the nanoparticles.
Identically to the size measurements, the test samples were diluted by taking 10 µL of the
test sample and diluting it in 990 µL of MQ water. The dilution was necessary to reach the
detection limit of the Malvern Panalytical DLS apparatus. Moreover, the Zeta-Potential of
the lipid nanoparticles was expected to be close to zero due to the non-protonated ionizable
lipid and the neutral charge of the rest of the lipids in the formulation.

6.1
An effective surfactant must be able to dissociate the
LNPs efficiently
As mentioned in section 5.3.1, the principle behind the RiboGreen assay is to get the most
mRNA possible out of the LNPs. Dissociated LNPs are compared to intact LNPs to de-
termine the EE. Literature often reports high EE, but these data do not consider the total
mRNA concentration[30][31]. As shown in table 6.1, the EE is high in both formulations,
even though one case depicts a 50 % DE and the other 100 % DE. This is an example of how
to show high EE data without considering the actual mRNA concentration measured by the
RiboGreen assay since both values are high, but one scenario lost 50 % of the postulated
mRNA concentration of the LNPs. Hence, the EE and DE must be included when discussing
the efficacy of the LNPs manufacturing process. Otherwise, a high concentration of mRNA
can be encapsulated while most of the postulated mRNA total concentration of the LNPs
is not recovered. The DE obtained with the RiboGreen assay was compared with the total
mRNA concentration given by the HPLC to ensure that the maximum mRNA is extracted
with the disruption of the LNPs.

As mentioned in section 5.3.1 and shown in figures 5.3 and 5.4, Triton X-100 achieves low DE
regardless of the concentration used (below or above the CMC), Tween 20 dissociates LNPs in
a more efficient way when used in low concentrations, and Zwittergent 3-14 gives higher DE
values in high concentrations. This tendency is observed in M1 and M2. Since Triton X-100
is an amphipathic single-chain molecule, it has the predisposition to self-assemble in aqueous
solutions, which can influence its membrane-disruptive properties [21]. Gooran et al., 2022
compare the ability of Triton X-100 and Simusol SL 11W (SL-11W) to dissociate solid-lipid
bi-layer structures using the QCM-D technique to track the biophysical interactions between
the detergents and the bi-layer. In the aforementioned study, they show that high Triton
X-100 concentrations (above 250 µM) cause complete membrane solubilization that occurs
through a brief membrane perturbation period. On the other hand, lower concentrations of
Triton X-100 do not dissociate solid-lipid bi-layer structures. The data represented in figures
5.3 and 5.4 is inconsistent with Gooran et al., 2022 findings. The inability of Triton X-100
to disrupt could be attributed to another finding from the same article [21] since SL-11W
is another amphipathic, single-chain, and non-ionic molecule. Both detergents interact with
the phospholipid membranes and cause disruption in different ways. SL-11W induced mor-
phological changes to the membrane, but the interaction process occurred more gradually
and did not cause extensive membrane solubilization, suggesting reversible membrane inter-
action. One possible explanation of the Triton X-100 data on DE (Figures 5.3 and 5.4) is
51
==PAGE:63==
that a similar interaction to the one of SL-11W in the article [21] took place with the LNPs
due to a different environment (pH, buffer, and lipid composition). Additionally, figure 5.8
shows no disruption of the LNPs when in contact with Triton X-100 at high concentrations.
According to the data collected in figures 5.3 and 5.4 and as mentioned in the paragraph be-
fore, Tween 20 achieves higher DE when used in low concentrations (below CMC). The data
is consistent for M1 and M2, as well as the highly significant difference between the effect
of high and low Tween 20 concentrations in disrupting LNPs. A research article looking for
GFP extraction via cell disruption using aqueous solutions of surface-active compounds [32]
affirms that some amphiphilic compounds above their CMC, disrupt cell membranes due to
their interaction with lipid bi-layers. The same article also shows how 250 mM Tween 20
(above CMC) is unable to permeabilize the cell membrane. On the other hand, cationic
surfactants with alkyl chains larger than eight carbon atoms allow the disruption of cell
membranes [32]. The inability of Tween 20 in concentrations above its CMC to dissociate
the membrane of the LNPs correlates with the data shown in this report (Figures 5.3 and
5.4). On the other hand, Heerklotz et al., 2008 report that surfactants may induce membrane
leakage at higher concentrations [33]. A reason for the low DE values achieved by a high
concentration of Tween 20 can be the formation of micelles. The concentration of Tween
20 used in this project is almost four times more than its CMC (0,06 mM), leading to the
formation of micelles that could shield the free mRNA released from the dissociated particles
or could interact with the RiboGreen reagent. In both cases, there is no interaction between
mRNA and RiboGreen. Hence, no fluorescence is measured, reporting low free mRNA and
low DE. The high DE that Tween 20 achieves in low concentrations could be attributed to
the lack of micelle formation, resulting in normal binding between the RiboGreen dye and
the free mRNA and a consequent detection of fluorescent signal.
In terms of the Zwittergent 3-14, also shown in figures 5.3 and 5.4, a different result from
52
==PAGE:64==
Triton X-100 and Tween 20 is observed. Zwittergent 3-14 achieves better DE in high con-
centration, which shows a significant difference compared to the lower concentration. As
mentioned in section 2.6.3, Zwittergent 3-14 is a single-chain amphipathic molecule. For
that reason, a similar effect as Triton X-100 on the particles should occur based on their
chemical structure. The significant DE difference between high and low concentrations of
Zwittergent 3-14 could be due to the formation of micelles, which are able to solubilize the
LNPs more efficiently than individual detergent molecules. As Cheng et al., 2015 report,
at high concentrations, more molecules of Zwittergent 3-14 are available to interact with
the membrane of the LNPs, increasing the probability of disruption. Zwittergent 3-14 is
also more soluble at higher concentrations, which may allow it to penetrate the LNPs more
efficiently, resulting in membrane dissociation [34]. The different effects that Tween 20 and
Zwittergent 3-14 achieve in DE when above their CMC can be supported by Tiefenbach
et al., 1993, which report that there are problems with micellar detergents like Triton X-
100, Tween 20, and Zwittergent 3-14, amongst others. Frequently, these detergents contain
impurities and are heterogeneous, and need appropriate experimental conditions related to
temperature, and ionic strength, amongst others, to guarantee micellar formation. Micelles
in the detergents are also highly influenced by environmental conditions [35].
6.2
The encapsulation efficiency is not influenced by the
incubation method
As mentioned in section 5.3.2, to get high encapsulation efficiency values using the Ribo-
Green assay, the total mRNA concentration defined by the dissociation efficiency needs to be
high, and, on the other hand, the outside mRNA concentration must be low. Even though
some differences are observed in the EE from M1 and M2, the method does not show a
difference in the EE regardless of the surfactant used.
53
==PAGE:65==
The difference between M1 and M2 are the temperature and the shaking. It can be ar-
gued that temperature and shaking do not impact the outside mRNA concentration based
on the data in figure 5.7, hence, on EE. Results in figure 5.7 support that temperature and
shaking do not harm the LNPs since the size, PdI, and Zeta-Potential were maintained for
the incubated and non-incubated LNPs. Since the size of the LNPs determined in figure 5.7
showed no significant difference when comparing the non-incubated LNPs with the incubated
ones, it could be discussed that no aggregation due to particle debris is observed, suggesting
that the particles do not undergo physical disruption provoked by the temperature or shak-
ing. Regarding PdI, the values are similar and consistently below 0.2. Lastly, the similarity
between the incubated and non-incubated Zeta-Potential data in figure 5.7 can explain that
no mRNA is being released from the interior of the LNPs without adding the surfactant to
the sample. If there was some mRNA exposure, the surface charge of the sample would be
more negative, showing a statistical difference between the Zeta-Potential measurements of
the incubated and non-incubated LNPs. Since temperature and shaking do not explain the
high outside concentration of the samples non-incubated with a surfactant, the manufactur-
ing process of the LNPs could be the variable determining the poor encapsulation shown in
figures 5.5 and 5.6, which will be discussed further in section 6.4.
6.3
Time and temperature can influence LNP stability
Although lipid and mRNA interaction results in robust LNPs, time and storage conditions
are significant for the stability of a DDS. Therefore, preparing a new batch of LNPs for every
experiment is needed, which can be inconvenient and expensive. Using low temperatures for
nanoparticle storage is the most popular method to overcome the issue of carrier stability [31].
Suzuki et al., 2015 reports mRNA-LNPs to be stable for 18 months when stored at 4 °C
with no change in size or PdI. The LNPs kept at 4 °C showed the same behavior as in the
54
==PAGE:66==
study from 2015, retaining size and PdI. Another study [31] determined that mRNA-LNPs
were stable for seven days when stored at 4 and 25 °C. However, there is an observable
difference between the size of the control LNPS and the LNPs stored at 25 °C. Even though
the difference between the size mean of the control LNPs and the LNPs stored at 25 C after
one week is almost 8 nm, it is considered significant enough and could be due to a mistake
in the measurement. Interestingly, the LNPs stored at 37 °C were stable, and their size and
PdI were retained for seven days, as the ones kept at 4 °C. Moreover, the LNPs stored at
4 °C were also stable after 21 days, supporting the finsings from Suzuki et al., 2015. The
significant difference in size between the LNPs stored at 25, and 37 °C and the control LNPs
can be attributed to aggregation, and fusion [36][30].
Considering the Zeta-Potential, surprisingly, only the LNPs stored at 37 °C for seven days
had no significant difference compared to the control LNPs. However, Kamiya et al., 2022
collect Zeta-Potential values between -5 and -8, which are considered normal, hence, stable.
The LNPs stored at 4 and 25 °C showed surface charge values of -7.37 and -7.68, respectively.
Even though a statistical difference of p<0.05 was determined for these two samples when
compared to the control, their Zeta-Potential values can be considered normal, and LNP
stability can be argued [31]. After 21 days, the difference between the particles increases
regardless of the storage temperature. Supporting the size data measured with the DLS,
the decrease in surface charge can be attributed to mRNA release due to LNP aggregation
or disruption after exposure to high temperatures for a prolonged time. Some differences
observed between the literature and the data collected might be explained by the difference
in the ionized lipid used in the formulations. For instance, Akinc et al., 2019 use DLin-
MC3-DMA in the LNP formulation, which contains two double bonds and is susceptible to
oxidation. On the other hand, SM-102 does not have unsaturated bonds in its structure and
is expected to be less susceptible to oxidation.

As mentioned in section 5.4.3, when measuring the variation in the mRNA concentration of
the LNPs for the stability study, only significant differences can be observed in the LNPs
stored at 25 °C for three weeks (Figure 5.9). In an LNPs encapsulation study, Ball et al.,
2016 reported an entrapment pattern irrespective of storage condition. Encapsulation levels
decreased from days 0 to 14, increased from days 14 to 23, then steadily decreased for the
remainder of the study. They argue that the increasing levels observed from days 14 to 23
may have been due to the degradation of unencapsulated siRNA during this time period.
In contrast to room temperature conditions, LNPs stored in the freezer retained most of
their efficacy over 156 days while experiencing an increase in size and PdI. Therefore, time
and high temperature might lead to mRNA release. High temperature might degrade the
mRNA, decreasing the measured mRNA concentration [37]. Based on the data collected,
which shows the opposite (no degradation of the mRNA), may lead to questioning the ac-
curacy of the Stunner.
Kamiya et al., 2022 also studied the effect of vortex mixing on mRNA-LNPs stability. Using
the vortex was recurrent during this project since sample homogeneity must be continuously
ensured. In this study, mRNA-LNPs tolerated exposure for 30 seconds; however, after 5 min-
utes of continuous exposure, an evident collapse of the nanoparticle structure was observed.
Strong mechanical stress, such as vortexing, leads to LNP aggregation and degradation of
the enclosed mRNA. Every time the LNPs were vortexed during this project, the duration
was a maximum of 5 seconds, which should not alter the integrity of the LNPs.
6.4
LNP manufacturing could influence the outside con-
centration
Figure 5.7 demonstrates that the method does not influence the outside mRNA concentra-
tion and, hence, the encapsulation efficiency of the particles. Therefore, it is probable that
56
==PAGE:68==
the manufacturing process of the LNPs is responsible for how much mRNA is not encapsu-
lated.
The parameters that influence LNP formation and mRNA encapsulation are: aqueous to or-
ganic phase ratio (Aq:EtOH), nitrogen to phosphorus ratio (N:P), the microfluidics flowrate
at which the particles are mixed, the lipid composition, and the buffer exchange after par-
ticles formation [38]. Aq:EtOH and N:P ratio [10] is widely studied in the literature, but
there is no specific optimal value for the microfluidics flow rate (Q) at which the mRNA
and lipids are mixed. A report by Ripoll et al., 2022 shows how larger particles and lower
EE are obtained using a flow rate below 1 mL/min. Between flow rates of 4 to 20 mL/min,
the PdI decreases as the flow rate increases. Additionally, particles formed at Q higher than
4 mL/min had improved encapsulation efficiency compared to results collected using lower
flow rates [11]. Another study reported the suitability of microfluidics manufacturing as a
high throughput method with constant liposome characteristics while increasing the system’s
total flow rate. Generally, two or more inlet streams, like lipids in a solvent and an aqueous
phase, are rapidly mixed together. Flow profiles in the chamber itself are of low Reynolds
numbers and categorized as laminar [39]. Therefore, higher flow rates (>4 mL/min) cause
laminar flow rates, which is optimal for manufacturing small LNPs capable of successfully
encapsulating mRNA. The flow rate used during this project was 9 mL/min, which has been
proven to achieve positive results in manufacturing mRNA-LNPs.
On the other hand, the buffer exchange method could also influence the mRNA loading
of the particles due to an important observation that the LNP size and structure continue
to evolve after mixing. That happens when the suspension is diluted or dialyzed against an
aqueous buffer. Buffer exchange methods aim to neutralize the pH and eliminate the excess
ethanol in the formulation. As the ionizable lipid becomes neutral, it becomes less soluble,
leading to larger hydrophobic lipid domains that drive the fission process. As a result, the
57
==PAGE:69==
size of the particles increases, and their core transforms into an amorphous electron-dense
phase primarily containing the ionizable lipid bound to the mRNA [12]. The method used in
this project was overnight dialysis. As observed in the Cryo-EM image of the control LNPs
(Figures 5.1b and 8.1), besides LNPs, liposomes and "bleb" structures or acorns can also be
observed in the sample. As the literature mentions [12], these structures are the precursors of
the dense-core LNPs. Performing a different buffer exchange method could ensure achieving
more LNPs and might decrease the presence of liposomes and acorns, which would probably
result in a higher concentration of mRNA inside the particles rather than on the outside. As
mentioned in section 5.3.2, a higher mRNA concentration inside the particles and a lower
concentration outside would increase the EE of the mRNA-LNPs.
Regarding the lipid composition of the LNPs formulation, Leung et al., 2015 report that
LNPs prepared with DSPC show a âblebâ structure appearing on approximately 20 % of
LNPâmRNA systems seen with the Cryo-TEM. Contrary, when the same formulation was
prepared using DOPE instead of DSPC, the protrusions disappeared, and the LNP became
uniformly spherical, solid core particles. It is possible that DSPC can be separated from other
lipid components in formulations having plasmid or mRNA because the protrusion is only
visible when DSPC is present. This separation could result in forming a bilayer membrane
bleb with an aqueous compartment. This leaves the ionizable lipid and cholesterol, which are
more compatible with inverted micellar or related structures, to create the electron-dense
portion of the particle in conjunction with the mRNA [29]. Another article [6] suggests
that, occasionally, the mRNA could be encapsulated inside the "bleb" compartment, and a
charge-driven migration of mRNA from the bleb into the body of the LNP occurs when the
mRNA-LNP sample of was dialyzed into pH 5 buffer. Since some "bleb" structures can be
seen in the control sample of LNPs (Figure 8.1 in the Appendices), it can be discussed that
the formation of these structures might influence the proper encapsulation of the mRNA
and, therefore, increase the mRNA concentration outside the LNPs.

Future Work
Further tasks could be focused on finally answering the question: "How can it be ensured
that the assay is performed as best as possible?". The main focus in the future would be
repeating the project, including another type of surfactant on top of Triton X-100, Tween 20,
and Zwittergent. Since the methods are already developed, a wider range of concentrations
could also be considered. The RiboGreen data could still be supported by more Cryo-EM
data, including control LNPs and LNPs in contact with all the different concentrations of
the surfactants in the study. The aim would be to establish the best surfactant and the
lowest concentration at which high DE is achieved. Additionally, future tasks could focus
on understanding deeper how different surfactants interact with the lipids in the membrane
of LNPs, how the CMC influences the dissociation of LNPs, and if the formation of micelles
shields the free mRNA or interacts with the RiboGreen dye, affecting the fluorescence signal.
Moreover, an additional method could be included in the study, in which only the standard
curves containing surfactant would be incubated rather than all the standard curves. In
addition, measuring the fluorescence signal could be done under different temperature con-
ditions to investigate if that parameter would influence the performance of the RiboGreen
dye. As mentioned in section 6.4, various parameters in the LNPs manufacturing process
could be further investigated to achieve low outside mRNA concentration resulting in high

Chapter 8
Conclusion
Even though Triton X-100 is the most used surfactant in the RiboGreen assay for EE deter-
mination, it did not achieve as high values as typically reported in the literature. The DE
is often not shown in DDS encapsulation publications using the RiboGreen assay, which is
essential to consider when reporting reliable data. Moreover, Triton X-100 did not achieve
high DE regardless of its concentration, despite being the golden standard surfactant in the
RiboGreen assay. Regarding Tween 20 and Zwittergent 3-14, the EE data collected during
this project was significantly lower than generally reported in the literature. Future work
is needed to determine an optimal surfactant and method for dissociating the LNPs. The
different behavior of Tween 20 and Zwittergent 3-14 in the RiboGreen assay regarding the
formation of micelles would need further investigation. An important matter to consider is
that for a project like this, where different surfactants and concentrations are used, doing
a concentration screening is crucial to determine the best surfactant and the lowest and
best concentration for particle disruption. The mRNA concentration outside the particles
is a critical matter to consider and keep as low as possible to ensure a high encapsulation
efficiency. Cryo-EM data showed the presence of liposomes and acorns ("bleb" structures)
in the LNPs sample, which can explain the high mRNA concentration outside the LNPs.
The presence of these LNPs precursors can be attributed to the process of manufacturing.

Despite needing images of the LNPs incubated with different concentrations of the surfac-

surfactant that achieved high DE data in the RiboGreen assay. In addition, data shows that
LNPs are stable when stored at 4 °C for at least 21 days, whereas they tend to increase in
size and release mRNA when kept at higher temperatures for a long time.
