Preface
This project aims at assessing the static and dynamic voltage support capabilities
of renewable generating plants.
First, the main concepts related to voltage stability and voltage collapse are
discussed and the most recent grid codes concerning voltage support from wind and
photovoltaic plants are listed.
The system used for the simulations is composed making use of a simpliﬁed
transmission system and wind/photovoltaic models available in the simulation tool
PowerFactory.
In the static analysis,voltage collapse is induced by increasing the active power
demand of the power network.
The PV-curves at a representative bus are used
to compare the contribution of a wind farm at diﬀerent penetration levels and for
diﬀerent control schemes.
In the dynamic analysis, a severe short circuit causes the voltage at the nearby
terminals to drop: time-domain dynamic simulations are carried out to compare the
contribution that wind and photovoltaic plants bring in sustaining the voltage in
diﬀerent plant conﬁgurations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
A power system, in order to function in a stable and reliable way, needs to be pro-
vided some services, called ancillary services (or grid support services). A proper
functioning of the power system will result in a better service (electricity) to the
customers. Provision of voltage support is one of these ancillary services.
A certain system topology, comprising of transmission lines, transformers, protec-
tion equipment, etc. . . needs to operate at some speciﬁc voltage levels. There are
two main reasons for this. The ﬁrst reason is element wise: a piece of equipment
working outside its operational range for too long will lose in performance and even-
tually incur in failure. The second reason is system wise: even one fault event like
the collapse of a terminal voltage can trigger a chain reaction that endangers the
overall stability of the system, which can ultimately result in blackouts.
Voltage stability is deﬁned as the “ability of a power system to maintain accept-
able voltages at all buses in the system under normal conditions and after being
subject to a disturbance” [5]. It also called load stability, because it is usually re-
lated to a small-disturbance situation of gradually increasing active load demand
that results in the network being highly loaded, thus increasing its reactive power
needs and causing voltages to drop. Voltage instability has caused several blackouts
in the past 40 years, the last aﬀecting India in 2012.[6]
The reactive power needs of a transmission system are fulﬁlled mainly by the large
conventional synchronous generators. Due to the fact that voltage support is mainly
a local issue [7], compensating devices spread all over the system are also used, as
well as on load tap changer (OLTC) transformers. Introduction of grid codes are
making renewable generators (ReGen) more and more responsible with regard to
provision of ancillary services.[8][9]
According to several studies [10],[11] the penetration of renewable generation will
increase in the near future. Political targets are going in that direction and the
countries with availability of resources (Ireland, Denmark, Germany, UK, Spain and

1.2
Problem Formulation
When the penetration of ReGen increases, the provision of voltage support becomes
more diﬃcult: the phasing out of conventional generators means losing the main
suppliers of ancillary services, thus making the burden on ReGen and other equip-
ment heavier.
Apart from the power system loading, demand of ancillary services from ReGen is
also depending on its active power dispatch (itself depending on weather conditions):
diﬀerent loading of the ReGen feeder equipment (transformers and cables) means
diﬀerent need of reactive power injection.
Power plants can operate at diﬀerent control schemes, meaning that they can be
set to work according to certain setpoints, which are usually deﬁned by the trans-
mission system operator (TSO). When it comes to voltage control, a generator can
be set to provide, for instance, a constant value of reactive power or to inject reac-
tive power trying to keep the voltage at his PCC to a certain voltage setpoint, etc.
Using diﬀerent schemes leads to diﬀerent impacts on system voltage stability.
It is thus of great interest to assess the contribution that ReGen has in supporting
voltage of a highly loaded transmission system. More speciﬁcally, the impact that
diﬀerent penetration and control schemes have on voltage stability is analyzed in
this report.
Critically low voltages can occur in case of large-disturbance event, like a trans-
mission line short-circuit. In these cases, conventional generation usually sustains
the voltages by injecting fast reactive current. The contribution of ReGen on such
situation will be addressed.
Because of conventional power generation phasing out in favor of renewable gen-
eration, it is very important to understand how ReGen can contribute to voltage
stability both in a small-disturbance situation (like the slow increase in active load
demand) and a large-disturbance one (like a major short-circuit in order to support
the planning of future power systems decision process.

1.4
Scope and Limitations
The main focus of this study is on the voltage behavior of a representative bus, as-
sumed to be an acceptable mirror of the voltage stability of the whole power system.
In general, the compliance of the ReGen to the grid codes (which is deﬁned at the
PCC) will not be assessed and assumed fulﬁlled. Thus, the analysis carried out is a
comparison among the diﬀerent scenarios of the voltage levels at the representative
bus. Nonetheless, the relevant grid codes will be listed and explained to show the
kind of support that ReGen are required to provide.
The analyses are performed making use DIgSILENT PowerFactory simulation tool.
Static analysis makes use of the load-ﬂow based “PV curves” tool, while dynamic
analysis is performed with the “RMS/EMT Simulation” toolbox.
The types of ReGen analyzed are fully decoupled from the power system, namely
full-scale converter WTs (type 4) and photovoltaic systems (inverter connected).
The system design in PowerFactory is made according to available models: the
grid model is provided by the supervisor and the wind and PV systems are available
in the software library.
Both wind and PV plants are modeled as static generators injecting a certain
amount of active power. Their power output is not calculated as dependent on any
wind/solar resource measurement. Thus, it can be assumed that constant whether
conditions occur within each scenario.
The network used is representative of a transmission network (high voltage grid).
The behavior of a distribution network (low voltage grid) with regard to voltage
control might be signiﬁcantly diﬀerent but is outside the scope of this report.
Voltage stability is usually tightly related to rotor angle stability.
(***kundur)
Nonetheless, the latter will not be object of study in this report.
The following limitations can be identiﬁed:
 In order to appreciate the contribution of the ReGen systems, some com-
ponents or strategies that are generally used to provide voltage support are
deactivated: namely, the OLTC transformers are not allowed to change tap
position and any compensating device (STATCOM, SVC) is turned oﬀ. With

Chapter 2
State of The Art
This chapter introduces the reader to the concept of voltage stability, the condi-
tions that bring the system to critically low situations, what measures can be taken
to improve the operating state and some indexes to quantify these improvements.
Considerations will be made about the transmission system on why it is important
to implement voltage support at high voltage levels and for increasing penetration
of renewable generation.
The grid codes regarding voltage/reactive power support will be listed both for
wind and PV systems.
Finally, the current capabilities of wind and PV systems will be depicted and the
results of the relevant literature concerning ReGen voltage support will be shown.

Decreasing load angle has quite an impact on the PV curve: increased voltage sta-

dealing with high load condition, the shape of the PV curve needs to be changed

is not present. A voltage collapse is mainly due to the inability of the system to
provide enough reactive power: this is mainly provided by the generators and the
compensating equipment (FACTS). A consequence of this is that voltage stability is
more a planning problem than an operating problem. Even when issues are recog-
nized, they may be very diﬃcult to mitigate without reducing power transfer, unless
provision has been made for local voltage support. [16]

2.2
Transmission System and Voltage Support
Traditionally, voltage support is provided by large centralized synchronous gener-
ators from the transmission system down to the distribution system (according to
the radial ﬂow concept: active and reactive power from producers to consumers).
Moreover, FACTS (Flexible Alternating Current Transmission System) devices are
used where needed; these are electronic-based equipment that improve transmission
quality by optimizing the power factor, decreasing voltage or compensate for exces-
sive inductive reactance (all actions that are somehow related).
With the increasing penetration of distributed generation, it will possibly be ex-
pected that these small generators also contribute to voltage stability by inject-
ing reactive power; one reason above all: the current method used (On-Load Tap
Changer transformers) is a cumbersome one [23]. Thus, FACTS at distribution level
will also be necessary. It is also true that the power system is shifting from a radial
(or one directional) network to a grid-like network, in which power is transmitted
according to optimization and resource availability (particularly relevant for ReGen)
also at local level.
Nonetheless, large renewables plants (connected at transmission level) still need
to be built when an area is found to be suitable (especially resource-wise). Conse-
quently, conventional plant will be phased out: indeed this is the main target of the
policy started by several countries [12] [24]. Voltage support burden will then be on
the new renewable plants [7].
This is even more true considering that suitable areas are often distant from the
load centers, or even from the network itself (think about oﬀshore wind farms).
This means longer cables or overhead lines, which in turn means higher impedances,
thus higher need for voltage support, as explained in subsection 2.1.5
Giving priority of voltage control to plants connected to the transmission rather
than the distribution network might also be justiﬁed by the diﬀerence in the X/R
ratio. Distribution systems are usually made of underground cables (with low X/R
ratio), while transmission systems are usually made of overhead lines (higher X/R
ratio). This means that the latter are more suitable to be voltage regulated by in-
jecting reactive power, because the voltage diﬀerence in the lines is more dependent
on Q ﬂows than it is in distribution systems [23]. This last consideration is not
valid for oﬀshore wind farms, which are connected through undersea cables: in fact,
ReGen very often need (actually, ReGen developers are required to install) some
sort of compensating device (FACTS) to ensure proper Voltage control. This will
be later explained in subsubsection 2.3.1.3.

The plant can provide automatic reactive power regulation by operating at three
diﬀerent control modes: Voltage Control mode, Reactive Power Control mode or
Power Factor Control mode
2.3.1.3.4.1
Voltage Control Mode
The plant tries to keep the voltage at the connection point as close as possible to a
reference Voltage Vref (or voltage setpoint) by providing reactive power. The set-
point has to be at least in the range 0.95-1.05 pu (the Danish code deﬁnes a range
from Vmin to Vmax , which is the typical voltage levels in Table 2.1), namely from
0.9 to 1.1 pu (for a 132 kV Nominal Voltage). The setpoint must be deﬁned in steps
smaller than 0.01 pu (it is 0.002 pu in the Danish codes)
The plant will inject capacitive Q when the voltage at the PCC goes below the
setpoint and on the other hand it will inject inductive Q when it goes above the
setpoint. When the voltage at the PCC is the same as the setpoint, the plant will
inject no Q. The injected amount of Q is deﬁned according to a droop, i.e. the ratio
between the change in Voltage (in pu) and the change in injected Q (in pu). As the
Figure 2.11 shows.
The plant needs to be able to set a droop within a range of at least 2-7%. Once
the droop is set, the operating point will move along that droop.
This is also called control by Q(U) characteristic, because the reactive power in-
jected is a function of the Voltage at the PCC.

are composed by a large amount of relatively small modules. In fact, the small na-
ture of PV panels gave way to the development of PV systems at distribution level.
The increase in household size penetration was also due to the favorable framework
that took form in high incentives in countries like Italy and Spain.
While the distribution system saw its PV penetration increased, the same cannot
be said for transmission systems: only around 1% of HV transmission system is
supplied by photovoltaic. As a consequence, most international TSOs did not ﬁnd
necessary to develop speciﬁc grid requirements for PV plants feeding their PCC at
HV. [26]
This is not true for Germany: in 2007 a Transmission Code [15] was released con-
cerning the requirements of systems connected to the high voltage grid (110kV):
a photovoltaic system needs to fulﬁll some requirements both during static and
dynamic operation.

2.4.1.1
Literature review (wind)
Keeping in mind that most of the analyzed literature refers to DFIG (or Type 3)
WTs, the results from a selection of studies are brieﬂy shown here. Also, it is worth
noticing that some of the results were achieved on Distribution Systems.
Implementation of Voltage Support capabilities in Wind Farms increases the load
stability margin (as deﬁned in subsection 2.1.4) according to [28] [18] [29] [30] [31].
Consequently, the same studies show that a higher loading of the system is pos-
sible. This is even more true for increasing penetration of wind farms with Voltage
control capabilities [30] [32].
Weather conditions aﬀect the capability to provide this ancillary service, accord-
ing to [29] and [32]. This is an aspect which this report is expected to show an
improvement of, given the new requirement and capabilities of Q provision at low
P.
Implementation of FACTS devices in combination of Wind generators has shown
overall improvements in terms of Voltage stability. [18] [29]
Reactive Power injection also helps in reducing Active Power losses. [31][29] [33].
In particular, [31] found that the dispersion of penetration highly aﬀects loadability
and power losses, namely a more dispersed conﬁguration is preferable. Moreover,
power losses decrease at ﬁrst, but then they increase, although they remain below
the level of base case (no wind penetration) at least within the considered penetra-
tion range (0-30%).
With regard to short-circuit contribution, [34] concludes that type 4 have limited
functionality (at least compared to conventional synchronous generator) as the cur-
rent is limited to 110% of rated current for a limited amount of time.

The local wind turbines (Local WTs) are modeled as a lumped equivalent generator.
They are equipped with induction generators and lack any kind of control system:
they inject active power and absorb reactive power (for the magnetization of the
induction machines). They are poorly damped, meaning that during undervoltages
they might overspeed and cause heavy oscillations; this is why they are disconnected
from the grid by the protection system. The oﬀshore wind farm is equipped with
ﬁxed-speed, active stall wind turbines (it is thus called Active Stall Wind Farm, or
ASWF). The active-stall control is applied to improve the ride-through capability of
the wind farm. The control model is simpliﬁed by controlling directly the mechanical
power if a voltage below a certain value for a certain time is detected (instead of
modelling the blade pitching system). Both the local WTs and the ASWF have their
own compensation equipment (shunt capacitors) that supplies part of their reactive
needs.
Literature [41] speciﬁes that any kind of wind turbine models can be used in the
grid. Photovoltaics models are not mentioned in the literature, but it is assumed
that no contraindications are in place.

Static Analysis
The impact of diﬀerent ReGen penetrations and control schemes on the system is
assessed in terms of steady-state voltage stability. Steady-state refers to the fact
that the power system either is in unperturbed condition either is subject to very
small-disturbance. Thus, it can be studied making use of load ﬂow based analysis.
[2] [5]
As seen in subsection 2.1.1, a typical case that can lead to critically low voltages
is when the system load (intended as active power demand) increases. Thus, an
increase in all the loads of the system is simulated making use of the PV curves

It is the bus to be used as reference to compare results of the diﬀerent scenarios. In

critical bus for Q control were found to be in the southern part of the grid. In case
more than one critical bus is found for diﬀerent scenarios, a representative bus is

3.3.1.2.2
Loadability and VSM
Loadability is the amount of active power demand that can be satisﬁed before the
system collapses, as explained in subsection 2.1.4. It is of interest because litera-
ture (***54) shows that in general there is a correspondence between a more stable
system and a high loadability. It is thus desirable that this value is high, meaning
that the system has enough reactive reserves to sustain the low voltages that occur
at such high loading levels.
Another interesting index is Voltage Stability Margin (VSM) calculated as deﬁned
by Equation 2.1.7 in Section subsection 2.1.4. It is somehow equivalent to load-
ability, just seen from another perspective: how far is the system from its critical
point?
The fact that VSM is a number between 0 and 1 (while loadability goes from
100% to theoretically inﬁnite) makes it more suitable to compare diﬀerent scenarios.
3.3.1.2.3
Voltage at Reference Bus
The voltage levels in a grid depend on the grid impedance and power and the re-
active requirements of the grid components (transformers/lines), so it is reasonable
to expect diﬀerent voltage levels for diﬀerent scenarios. As explained in subsec-
tion 2.1.5, reactive power absorption from a component with equivalent reactance
X increases with the square of the current I2, thus leading to lower voltages. There-
fore, the voltage levels at the reference bus are related to the reactive requirements
of the grid components. Special attention is given to the parallel lines connecting
the renewable bus to the northern part of the grid: increasing wind penetration will
necessarily result in higher loading of those lines, thus higher reactive losses and
lower voltages.
The voltage levels is compared with the load ﬂow sensitivities ∂Vref/∂PWF in
order to verify their consistency.
Moreover, the fulﬁllment of the admissible voltage levels deﬁned in subsec-
tion 2.3.3 are assessed, namely whether the voltage ever falls below Vmin=0.9pu
and for what system load P(Vmin=0.9pu)
3.3.1.2.4
Voltage Gradient at Reference Bus
In a situation of increasing load like the study case under analysis, it is preferable
that the voltage decreases as slowly as possible, in order to allow higher loadability
before reaching the lower voltage limits deﬁned in subsection 2.3.3. When charac-
terizing a PV curve by its initial voltage level and the gradient of the stable part
(the ﬁrst part), the latter is probably the most important, because it reﬂects the
ability of the power system to modify its reactive power injection in order to keep
up the voltages.

3.3.3.1
Methodology
Three aspects are of main interest in the voltage dynamic response of a system
subject to a perturbation (like a line short-circuit): the steady-state voltage before
and after the perturbation, the transient and the settling time.
3.3.3.1.1
Steady-State Voltages
When a fault is addressed by changing the topology of a grid, it is reasonable to
expect diﬀerences in the steady-state voltage of a bus before and after the fault.
This should apply also to case under study, in which the local wind turbines are
disconnected after the fault. Thus, the steady-state voltage before the fault is com-
pared to the voltage after the fault and diﬀerences among scenarios discussed. More
speciﬁcally, the entity of the diﬀerence is discussed along with compliance with the
voltage operational limits deﬁned in subsection 2.3.3
3.3.3.1.2
Transient
The transient dynamic response of the power system during a fault depends on the
control scheme used by the ReGen plants, thus the focus is on comparing scenarios
WT V, WT Q 42 and PV droop.
3.3.3.1.3
Settling Time
The settling time is deﬁned as the time needed to settle to the new steady-state
voltage, using the fault occurrence time as t=0. More precisely, it is the time from
which the result lies between its ﬁnal steady state value ±0.001pu. This time is
preferred to be as short as possible, meaning that the system can quickly stabilize
to a new steady-state situation.

4.3
Summary
In this chapter, the results of the static and dynamic analyses have been presented.
Increasing wind penetration has proved to have a negative impact on the system
stability: in a situation of increasing active power demand, the system will experi-
ence lower voltage levels and reduced loadability with increasing wind penetrations
due to the increasing reactive losses and the quicker saturation of the generators.
An improvement, using the base scenario as reference, could be observed for low
penetration scenarios (when in V control). Throughout the whole static analysis,
the wind farm set on V control has proved to better support the voltage at the
reference bus, compared to when set on Q control.
V control is preferable to Q control also in case of short-circuit event. Although
Q control yields smaller oscillations at the reference bus voltage, V control not only
keeps the post-fault voltage closer to rated values, but also reaches the new steady-
state condition much faster than Q control and base scenario. The PV plant does
not seem to have a relevant impact in the voltage stability, both when used as the
only ReGenplant and when used in combination with a wind farm.

Chapter 5
Conclusions
The project aims at assessing the impact that renewable generating plants (wind
and PV) have in supporting the grid voltage in a steady-state (static) situation and
in a fault situation.
The theory of voltage stability is introduced in chapter 2 using a simpliﬁed represen-
tation of a transmission system: the mechanism that leads to voltage collapse, the
impact that the power system’ parameters and equipment have on voltage collapse
and the deﬁnition of concepts and indexes that would be used in the Assessment
Studies. The Danish and German grid codes are referenced to list the kind of sup-
port that it is expected from wind and PV plant at their point of connection. The
acceptable voltage levels are also deﬁned. chapter 2 is concluded with the state of
the art of the current ReGen capabilities in providing steady-state voltage support
and reactive power injection during fault, along with a brief review of the available
literature regarding voltage support from wind and PV plants.
The ﬁrst part of chapter 3 describes the network and ReGen model used in the
simulations, with focus on the control models used by the ReGen.
The last part of chapter 3 deﬁnes the methodology and is divided in two parts,
one for the static analysis (performed to assess the steady-state voltage support)
and one for the dynamic analysis (performed to assess the voltage support in a fault
situation).
In the static analysis the case of a continuous increase in active power demand
is studied. The static voltage stability is assessed for diﬀerent penetrations of wind
power and for two diﬀerent control schemes (Q and V control). The main product
of this analysis is a set of PV curves that indicate how voltage collapse takes place
in the diﬀerent scenarios: the voltage level, gradient and critical point of the curves
are the main assessment criteria.
In the dynamic analysis a short-circuit is simulated at the end of a critical trans-
mission line in the proximity of the renewable bus. The dynamic voltage stability is
assessed for the same control schemes used in the static analysis (Q and V control)
and for diﬀerent reactive power injection levels (when in Q control). A scenario
is reserved to a PV plant, connected to the same bus the wind farm is connected
to, and another scenario assesses the combined support provided by the wind and

PV plants. The voltage proﬁle of the bus across the fault is monitored, namely the
voltage levels before and after the fault, the transient and the time it takes for the
voltage to settle at the new steady state voltage.
In chapter 4 the voltage stability assessment studies are performed.
The static analysis shows that the increasing penetration of wind has a negative
contribution on the voltage stability of the power system. Increasing penetrations
result ﬁrst of all in lower voltage levels: this due mainly to the increased reactive
losses (because of the higher active power transferred) at the parallel transmission
lines connecting the load centers to the renewable generators. When in V control, re-
active losses increase at a lower rate (compared to Q control) because of the reduced
amount of reactive power that needs to be transferred (because it is dispatched by
the wind farm). The second consequence of increasing wind penetration is the re-
duced loadability, meaning that the power system collapses at smaller system loads.
It was shown how these results are related to the ability of the generators to supply
reactive power and the fact that higher penetrations yield higher reactive power
dispatched from the generators that thus saturate more quickly. When in V control,
the wind farm injects reactive power, thus helping the conventional generators that
saturate less quickly (compared to Q control scenarios).
When compared to the base scenario, V control scenarios with low wind penetration
yield better results in terms of voltage levels, while all the V control scenarios yield
an increased loadability. Considering that all voltage levels are within acceptable
operating range, it can be stated that all the considered V control scenario show an
improvement, compared to the base scenario. Nonetheless, it can be deducted from
Figure 4.3 that a scenario with higher penetration (¿200 MW) would most likely
yield a lower loadability than the base scenario.
When set in Q control, the wind farm does not bring any improvement, compared
to the base scenario, except for the 0 MW scenario. This does not mean that the
results yielded by Q control are completely unacceptable: the voltage lies within
acceptable voltage range for most of the operating points and an increasing reactive
power injection would deﬁnitely result in higher voltage levels. The issue with Q
control is that it is cumbersome, meaning that an injection might be adequate for a
certain operating condition but not for another.
In conclusion, V control allows a more ﬂexible behavior from the wind farm,
which, by injecting increasing amount of reactive power for increasing power de-
mand, makes the PV curve less steep (compared to Q control and base scenarios)
and yields to a slower depletion of reactive power reserves (the conventional gener-
ators) thus increasing the loadability of the power system.
The dynamic analysis shows that, overall, a wind farm set on V control can support
the voltage - in case of short circuit - better than when it is set on Q control: the
steady-state voltage of the reference bus after the fault is closer to its rated voltage
and the time it takes to settle to the new steady-state condition is less than half it
takes when in Q control or in the base scenario. Although V control performs worse
during the transient, because it yields higher voltage oscillations, the voltage lies

within acceptable operating range.
The connection of the PV plant does not yield any substantial impact, albeit
a small contribution can be observed in terms of reduced voltage oscillations and
settling time (when connected together with the wind farm).

Future Work
The selected scenarios were chosen as representative and worthy of analysis. Nonethe-
less, more solid patterns and results could be achieved by analyzing scenarios with
closer penetrations to one another, but most of all scenarios with higher penetra-
tions. Also, concerning the reactive power injection, it would be interesting to study
how that inﬂuences the loadability of the system.
The obtained results can be considered conservative, the reason being that, in all the
simulations, the SVC at the renewable bus was deactivated and the transformers
were not allowed to change tap position. In the operation of a real transmission
system, transformers can change tap position and FACTS devices (among others:
SVS, STATCOM) are installed in strategic points of the network (usually the weak-
est points) and inject/absorb reactive power when needed. Thus, a more realistic
study could be performed considering these two aspects.
The transmission network used in this report is a simpliﬁed network, aimed at
simulating the behavior of a real transmission grid. It would be interesting to per-
form the exact same studies on a more realistic network and see if (and how much)
the results diﬀer.
All the ReGen plants where connected to the same bus in the southern (weaker)
area of the network. A diﬀerent positioning and/or a more scattered distribution
of renewable resources could show the impact that diﬀerent plants have on voltage
stability and the diﬀerent contribution required by the plants.
The model of the wind turbines used does not contain any mechanical/aerodynamic
part, thus no power oscillations is transferred to the grid. Although a type 4 wind
turbine is decoupled from the grid, meaning that most of the oscillations should be
erased by the converter, a more realistic analysis would require the use of a complete
wind turbine model.
Constant active power injection from ReGen has been used in the report. It is like
assuming constant whether conditions (in terms of wind speed and sun irradiance).
In order to assess the static and dynamic voltage stability, resource measurement
77
==PAGE:78==

time series should be assigned to the ReGen plants.
The wind and PV plants where modeled in a simpliﬁed way as though they were
a huge wind turbine and PV panel. A properly modeled wind farm or PV plant
(with realistic simulation of weather conditions) would probably yield more realistic
results.
A distribution network has diﬀerent characteristics than a transmission network
and thus requires its own voltage stability assessment.
Although technical feasibility is an inherent requirement, what drives the decisions
regarding power systems’ planning and operation is the economical factor. It is
recommended to take this into consideration in order assess the best solution both
technically and ﬁnancially.

The remaining generators increase their reactive power injection to keep up with
the increasing loading of the system (the loading increases because more active cur-
rent is transferred when the power demand increases), until they saturate, meaning
they reach their maximum Q (for the speciﬁc P they dispatch) and the genera-
tors terminal voltages will start to decrease as happened previously for generator
G SE. The reactive power injected by G NW and G SW is shown in Figure B.3 and
Figure B.4 respectively.
As Figure B.5 shows, G NE does not get to saturation in any of the scenarios.
Its reactive power injection gradient can be observed to change in two moments: the
ﬁrst, barely visible, takes place when G NW gets to saturation; the second is the
clearly visible sudden increase, due to saturation of G SW.
A reason for this diﬀerence in the two changes might be that:
1. 1. for G NE it is easier to take the burden of G NW reactive injection (which
sustains the voltages of the northern part of the grid) than it is to take the
burden of G SW, located further and thus implying a high reactive injection
required from G NE to sustain the voltages of the far away terminals;
2. 2. after the ﬁrst saturation G NE is sharing the reactive balancing with an-
other generator, after the second saturation G NE sustains the voltages all by
itself.
