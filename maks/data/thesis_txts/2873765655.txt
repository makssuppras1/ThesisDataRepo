Abstract 
 
Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy has transformed the treatment of blood cancers but 
remains limited in solid tumors due to the lack of highly specific targets. Most current CAR-T designs 
focus on tumor-associated antigens, which are also expressed in healthy tissues, leading to off-target 
toxicity. A more precise and safer alternative lies in targeting tumor-specific neoantigens, peptides 
derived from somatic mutations and presented by MHC class I molecules. 
This thesis presents a fully computational pipeline for designing TCR-mimic CAR T cells capable of 
recognizing such neoantigens with high structural precision. Two complementary strategies were applied 
to identify targetable peptides: one based on recurrent somatic mutations, and another focused on tumor-
specific gene overexpression. Genomic and expression data from six solid tumor types were integrated 
to shortlist tumor-exclusive peptides with strong MHC binding potential. These peptide–MHC 
complexes were structurally modeled and evaluated, and immune recognition was simulated through 
docking with engineered receptor fragments. 
The findings demonstrate that structure-based approaches can support the rational design of neoantigen-
targeted CAR T cells. Moreover, targeting shared neoantigens across multiple tumor types may offer a 
path to broader therapeutic applications while preserving tumor specificity.

Aim of the Project 
 
The aim of this project is to develop a structure-based computational approach for designing TCR-mimic 
CAR T cells that can selectively target tumor-specific antigens (TSAs) in solid tumors. By focusing on 
neoantigens mutated peptides presented by MHC class I molecules this work seeks to identify tumor-
exclusive targets and evaluate their potential for safe and precise CAR-T cell therapy. The goal is to 
contribute toward more selective immunotherapy by integrating genomic analysis, antigen prediction, 
and structural modeling into a unified design pipeline.

1.1 Cancer – main concern of nowadays healthcare 
Cancer is increasingly becoming one of the main causes of death in the world, being responsible for one 
out of six death (16.8%) worldwide, causing three in ten global premature death, mainly, from 
noncommunicable diseases NDCs – diseases that are not caused by an invading organism such as viruses 
and bacteria (Bray, F., et. al., 2024). Through unhealthy diet, exposure to dangerous chemicals and 
unhealthy habits, such as smoking – cases of lung, breast, prostate, and colon cancer has been 
significantly increasing, especially in 21st century (Trichopoulos, D., et. al., 1996). By World Health 
Organization predictions, by year 2050, estimate of 35 million new cancer cases worldwide will be 
registered, which is 77% percent increase compared to 20 million cases in registered in 2022 (World Health 
Organization, 2024).

on Cancer, 2024) 
 
Cancer – a genetic disorder that results from genetic or epigenetic alterations in the somatic cells and has 
abnormal cell growth which may be spread to other body parts. This unregulated growth of altered 
somatic cells starts forming neoplasm, also known as tumor. Despite many growth factors that play a 
significant role in cancer formation, one of the key factors is the genetics of the patient itself (Ponder, B.

A., 2001). When it comes to cancer formation, it is all dependent on DNA, it’s structure and changes 
caused to it - mutations. Mutation is a change in the DNA sequence in which normal base pair are 
substituted, added, deleted, or rearranged. The DNA sequence containing a mutation still consists of 
standard base pairs, and this DNA sequence is still eligible to be copied and replicated, causing an 
increase of mutated DNA containing cells. These mutations can turn off some of the viable genes, 
necessary for coding a protein with correct structure, making it abnormal in structure and in function. 
Nevertheless,3 mutations in genome can also activate oncogenes, inactivate tumors suppressor genes or 
cause genomic instability in afore mentioned replicating cells to whom progenitor cell is a mutant 
(Bernstein, C.,et. al., 2013).  
Main cause of cancer being damage of DNA, which can be divided into two categories: Endogenous 
(events happening inside the cell) and Exogenous (events that are of outer environment origin). 
Endogenous sources could be alkylation (addition of alkyl group to specific bases, which produces 
products such as O2-alkylthymine or O6-methylguanine, resulting in the turn off of the encoded gene 
(Grady, W. M., 2007)), hydrolysis, oxidation, or mismatch of DNA bases during replication. Exogenous 
sources include ionization, due to radiation, ultraviolet radiation and various, cancerogenic chemical 
agents. Exogenous sources are causing damage in DNA structure by breaking bonds between the base 
pairs, damaged DNA is then inaccurately repaired, which leads to genome encoding abnormal protein 
(Hakem, R., 2008).  
Cancer has countless variations, these types can be divided into seven major groups, usually 
differentiated by their location in the body, where malignant cells are proliferating (Saini, A.,et. al., 2020). 
1. Carcinomas – Neoplasm formation starts in the tissue or the skin, which covers the glands and 
internal organs. This type of cancer produces solid tumors. Typically found in patients, suffering 
from breast, prostate, lung colorectal cancers. 
2. Sarcomas – Cancer cell proliferation starts at connective and supportive tissues of the body, this 
type of cancer is located in nerves, joints, blood vessels, bone, lymph nodes, muscles, cartilages, 
and other connective tissues. 
3. Leukemia – Happens when blood cells, are starting to mutate, can be divided into 4 subgroups: 
Acute myeloid leukemia (cancer is quickly affecting bone marrow, which starts producing abnor-
mal myeloblasts and red blood cells), Acute lymphocytic leukemia (bone marrow is quickly pro-
ducing abnormal lymphocytes), and 2 other groups which happen slower and takes longer dura-
tion to develop – Chronic myeloid leukemia and Chronic lymphocytic leukemia. 
==PAGE:9==

4. Lymphomas – Cancer cells proliferation start at lymphatic system, including its network of glands 
and vessels. 
5. CNS cancers – Cancer that starts in brain tissue and/or spinal cord.  
6. Multiple Myeloma – This type of cancer begins in plasma cells and other types of immune cells. 
Malignant plasma cells build up in bone marrow, causing neoplasms found in bones. 
7. Melanoma – Affected by cells, responsible for producing melanin. In most of the cases this affects 
skin cells, but there are cases where development of cancer took place in a other pigmented tissues 
e.g. eye. 
Development of cancer is highly complicated process, there exist numerous factors that are responsible 
for causing the disease. Since the first discovery of cancer, around year 1600 BC, humanity was fighting 
cancer, since then many ways of treatment were invented and put in to use, nevertheless the research is 
still ongoing and clinical trials are being performed, especially now, that the growth of cancer cases has 
significantly increased (Sudhakar, A. et. a., 2009). 
 
1.2 Start of cancer therapy – detection of disease using

Treatment of cancer has always been a highly complex procedure. There have been many methods 
implemented, starting surgery, chemotherapy or radiotherapy and many more that are still being 
researched, such as stem cell therapy, targeted therapy (including T-cell or drug therapies) and many 
more (Debela, D. T., et. a., 2021). Before treatment can be selected and started for specific patient, 
biomarkers are needed to be discovered in patients. Biomarkers are biological phenomenon that can be 
difficult to find yet indicate a clinically significant outcome or interim consequence. Biomarkers can not 
only be used to determine current disease that is affecting the body, but as well indicate first signs of 
disease’s formation. Specific antigens can be considered as specific biomarkers, that can be linked to a 
specific type of cancer. As of now, there are many methods to help detecting the biomarkers – DNA 
sequencing, during which DNA structure and its base pair arrangement is compared to the ones most 
commonly found in cancer-affected patients, Electron microscopy, which allows us to get close view of 
the cell and mutations it could be going through, these and many more (Fig. 2) (Zhou, Y., et. al., 2024).

1.3 Chimeric Receptor Antigen T-Cell therapy – the answer to 
cancer treatment mystery 
 
One of the main issues with most of the novel drug therapies is the development drug resistance of most 
of the diseases. This has been a leading cause of treatment failure in conventional therapies, including 
cancer and many of its types. One of the most promising solutions to this issue is Chimeric Receptor 
Antigen T-Cell (CAR-T-cell) therapy. For this approach, T-Cells are genetically altered, to express 
chimeric antigen receptor, which can recognize tumor-specific antigens, without the involvement of 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
major histocompatibility complex (MHC). This results in immediate T-Cell activation and robust anti-
tumor response (Majumder, A., 2023). Starting with Chimeric Antigen Receptors – these are recombinant 
receptors for antigen, which redirect the specificity and function of immune cells, such as T lymphocytes. 
Construction of CAR-T-cells require patients T-Cells to be genetically modified allowing CAR 
expression. In theory, any surface molecule can be targeted through CAR, which increases the selectivity 
of the targets, but at the same time, recognized molecules need to be present only on the target cells as a 
mistake in choosing the recognizable molecule could cause harm to healthy cells as well. Depending on 
chosen antigen, different signaling paths, CAR-T-cell expression in tumor environment, and the 
expansion can be modulated, giving them a title of “living drugs” (Sadelain, M., et.al., 2023). Before 
discussing the structure of CAR, it is important to mention, that there are 3 generations of CARs. With 
each new generation, the CAR becomes more selective and more complex, as more Co-stimulatory 
molecules are introduced, that will be discussed in more detail in the following part of this section. 
The basic concept of CAR includes extracellular ligand recognition domain, usually it is a single chain 
variable fragment (scFv) which consists of Variable heavy (VH) and Variable light (VL) chains linked 
(Ectodomain) to intracellular signaling module which could be CD4, CD8α, CD3ζ or CD28 
(Transmembrane domain). Transmembrane domain and Ectodomain are usually conjugated by a linker, 
also known as hinge, its length usually dictates the CAR expression, signaling and activation strength. 
Transmembrane domain then is connected to signaling domain proteins, or co-stimulatory molecules if 
it is CAR of 2nd or 3rd generation, this is Endodomain. Endodomain usually consists of derivatives from 
CD28 or 4-1BB. Each of the domains are responsible for different function of the receptor, Ectodomain 
is the antigen binding domain, it is responsible for selectivity of target, Transmembrane domain allows 
the receptor to be stable and also has a role in the expression level of CAR and in assembly, activation 
and clustering (process during which surface receptors that bind to ligands, aggregate into localized 
groups it is crucial for signal strength durability and duration). Finally, Endodomain is responsible for 
sustaining the signaling cascade (Srivastava, S., et.al., 2015). Endodomain, also known as intracellular 
signaling domain (ICD), Intracellular signaling domain usually contains a chain of CD3ζ connected to 
one more signaling domain, these could be CD28, 4-1BB (Also known as CD137, highly expressed in T 
and Natural killer cells), OX40 (also known as CD134, part of tumor necrosis factor receptor family) 
CD27 or ICOS (also known as Inducible T cell Costimulator), first two being used most commonly in 
CAR-T cell therapies as of date. Studies show that CD28 intracellular domain stimulates greater CAR-T 
cell functionality (How well receptor performs), while 4-1BB promotes greater persistence (How long 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
does activation lasts) (Guedan, S., et.al., 2018). The three generations of CAR receptors are shown in a

While CAR-T cells are usually made from various types of T cells, preclinical studies indicate that some 
of T-cell subtypes show distinct properties in vivo, these properties could be enhanced proliferative 
capacity or increased antitumor effects (Gattinoni, L., et.al., 2011). It is worth mentioning that 1st generation 
CARs are not commonly used in therapies as they lack in both functionalities, even causing toxicity due 
to low selectivity, and persistence (Thistlethwaite, F. C., et.al., 2017). Going back to the Costimulators CD28 
and 4-1BB, these usually play aa role as a 2nd or 3rd signal in the cascade, first being connection of peptide 
antigen, presented by major histocompatibility complex (MHC), on the surface of membrane, and T cell 
receptor (TCR) in the Ectodomain. As single signal alone is not sufficient enough, single signaled T-
Cells become anergic and do not proliferate in responses to the antigen and even if the response comes, 
it is not modulated enough for an efficient activation of T cell (Thistlethwaite, F. C., et.al., 2017). Despite 
that, adding and additional costimulatory domain in to the signaling cascade, enhances the signal 
transduction greatly.

Other therapy method worth mentioning is engineered T-Cell Receptor (TCR) therapy. Similarly, to 
CAR-T therapy, it is focused on recognizing the cancer cells, but instead of using the antigens presented 
on the outer side of the cell, Major Histocompatibility complex (MHC) plays a role in recognizing the 
cancerous properties of the cell (Baulu, E., et.al., 2023). During TCR therapy, when T cell approaches other 
cell, MHC class I complex presents a protein for recognition. These proteins could potentially be Tumor-
associated antigens (TAAs) or Tumor-specific antigens (TSAs). As there are many factors preventing 
tumor from being recognized by T-cells, including the downregulation of presentable antigens, making 
the connection weaker between cells (Kim, S. K., & Cho, S. W., 2022). Engineering of TCR begins by 
sequencing sample, collected from a tumor and identifying possible antigens. After identification and 
sequencing of the antigen, epitope prediction is performed, using data-driven bioinformatic tools, such 
as NetMHC (Lundegaard, C., et.al., 2008), the result after prediction are validated and finally, identification 
of epitope-specific TCR is concluded (Fig. 5).

Whether it is TCR or CAR-T approach, both therapies have high potential in treatment of patient-specific 
cancers. As cancer develops, due to suppression of recognition by T-Cells, more efficient epitopes are 
needed to be engineered. For therapy to be operating at the highest efficiency, cancer-origin antigens 
need to be recognized. As most of these antigens are not innately recognizable by T-Cells, they need to 
be identified and have specific epitopes engineered for them. These novel antigens are also known as 
Neoantigens (Schumacher, T. N., et.al., 2019). 
 
1.4 Neoantigens – a vital part in development of precision 
medicine 
 
Neoantigens are self-antigens (non-targetable, by immune system, surfaces molecules of organism’s own 
cells) generated by tumor cells because of genomic mutations. Besides, neoantigens can also derive from 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
unique proteins or peptides produced by dysregulated RNA splicing and disordered post-translational 
protein modification in non-virus-associated malignancies (Baulu, E., et.al., 2023). Tumor-specific antigens 
(TSA) are synonymous with neoantigens. By using, before mentioned, engineered TCRs or CARs, 
neoantigens can be identified as alien to organism molecules, most of the time proteins, that are 
considered unusual or absent in the healthy patient’s tissue. These abnormal proteins can appear due to 
genomic mutations, aberrant transcriptomic variants, post-translational modifications (PTMs) or viral 
open reading frames (ORFs) (Wang, Z., & Cao, Y. J., 2020).  
Before any treatment can be applied, identification of targetable neoantigens is needed. The identification 
can be performed using whole-exome or RNA sequencing (Borden, E. S. et.al., 2022) followed by 
proteomic data that is available at TCGA (Tomczak, K., et. al., 2015). Together with RNA-seq, HLA 
typing must be performed. Human Leukocyte Antigen – HLA is a part of MHC system, it can be divided 
into 2 classes. Class I is found in all of the cells of the body, that have nucleus, they are responsible for 
presenting intracellular peptides to CD8+ T-cells, and Class 2 are mainly expressed in antigen-presenting 
cells (APCs), such as B cells, macrophages and dendritic cells which present extracellular pathogens and 
proteins, which are recognized by CD4+ T-cells. This difference is important as beforementioned TSAs 
and TAAs are only presented by HLA class I and not by class II (Klein, J. A. N., & Sato, A., 2000). Once all 
the required information is gathered, these results then can later be used for precision medicine or patient-
specific therapy development. Once data is analyzed, simulation of binding can be done, during which 
the engineered TCR binds to neoantigen (TAA or TSA), presented by HLA on the tumor cell. Simplified 
workflow of this procedure is shown below (Fig. 6).

2. Materials & Methods 
 
This chapter presents the structure-based computational pipeline developed to identify tumor-specific 
neoantigens and evaluate their potential as targets for TCR-mimic CAR T-cell therapy. The pipeline 
combines two complementary strategies: 
Mutation-based approach: Identifies neoantigens derived from somatic missense mutations, prioritizing 
peptides with strong predicted binding to MHC class I molecules. 
Expression-based approach: Targets neoantigens derived from genes that are significantly overexpressed 
in tumors but minimally expressed in normal tissues. 
Each approach involved distinct data sources and preprocessing steps, followed by a shared workflow 
for MHC binding prediction, structural modeling of peptide–MHC (pMHC) complexes, and docking 
simulations with TCR-mimic single-chain variable fragments (scFvs). 
All analyses were performed using a combination of Python, R, and Bash, with Conda environments 
used to ensure reproducibility. Full code, including scripts for data preprocessing, peptide extraction, 
MHC binding predictions, modeling, and docking, is available at: 
GitHub Repository: https://github.com/novaforbes/tcrm-thesis-code

2.1 Dataset Selection and Preprocessing 
2.1.1 Overview of Data Sources 
To support both the mutation-based and expression-based pipelines, genomic and transcriptomic datasets 
were collected from the following sources: 
• 
TCGA (The Cancer Genome Atlas): Used for both mutation data and tumor gene expression pro-
files. Datasets were obtained via cBioPortal using the 2018 TCGA PanCancer Atlas release, 
which includes harmonized multi-omics data for ~11,000 tumors across 33 cancer types. 
• 
GTEx (Genotype-Tissue Expression Project, v8 release) [https://gtexportal.org]: Provided normal 
tissue gene expression profiles used for differential gene expression (DGE) analysis and tumor-
specificity filtering in the expression-based pipeline. 
• 
GTEx is a reference dataset of gene expression in healthy human tissues, enabling comparison 
with tumor transcriptomes to identify cancer-specific overexpression patterns. 
2.1.2 Tumor Type Selection 
The study proceeded in two phases: 
Mutation-based feasibility phase: Focused on four tumor types: 
1. Breast invasive carcinoma (BRCA) 
 
2. Colon adenocarcinoma (COAD) 
 
3. Lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) 
 
4. Skin cutaneous melanoma (SKCM) 
 
These were selected for their high mutational burden and detailed mutation annotations available in 
TCGA. 
 
Expression-based refinement phase: Focused on six epithelial tumor types: 
 
1. Breast invasive carcinoma (BRCA) 
 
2. Colon adenocarcinoma (COAD) 
 
3. Lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD)

2.1.3 Mutation Filtering and Format Validation 
Somatic mutation data were extracted from the data_mutations.txt files available via cBioPortal for each 
TCGA dataset. These files are part of the 2018 TCGA PanCancer Atlas release and contain standardized 
mutation calls derived from MutSig pipelines. 
A custom Python script (filter_tcga_mutations.py) was used to perform the following steps: 
• Filter for missense mutations: Only non-synonymous single nucleotide variants (SNVs) classified 
as "Missense_Mutation" were retained, as these are the most likely to result in altered peptides 
presented by MHC class I molecules. 
• Retain essential columns: 
The script extracted the following fields required for downstream processing: 
o Hugo_Symbol (gene name) 
o HGVSp_Short (amino acid change, e.g., p.G12D) 
o Tumor_Sample_Barcode (sample identifier) 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
• Validate mutation format: 
Only mutations following the standard amino acid substitution format (p.[A-Z][0-9]+[A-Z], 
e.g., p.G12D) were kept. This format clearly defines: 
o The wild-type amino acid (e.g., G) 
o The residue position (e.g., 12) 
o The mutant amino acid (e.g., D) 
This standardization enabled automated parsing and accurate mapping to reference protein sequences 
during peptide extraction. 
• Export cleaned output: 
Mutations that passed all filters were saved in .csv format 
(filtered_missense_<CANCER_TYPE>.csv) and used as input for the next step in the pipeline. 
Mutations with ambiguous formats (e.g., insertions, deletions, frameshifts, or malformed entries) were 
excluded from further analysis, as they could not be reliably mapped to reference sequences. 
2.2 Neoantigen Generation and Filtering 
This step focused on generating 9-mer peptide sequences from validated somatic missense mutations, 
selected as the preferred source of tumor-specific neoantigens. These single-residue substitutions are 
structurally accessible and immunologically meaningful, making them well-suited for pMHC modeling 
and immunogenicity assessment. 
The goal was to extract peptides that: 
• Center the mutated residue, 
• Are structurally complete (full-length 9-mers), 
• And are traceable back to the originating gene, mutation, and sample. 
Only peptides fulfilling all three criteria were advanced to MHC binding prediction and 3D structure 
preparation. 
2.2.1 Protein Sequence Mapping and Peptide Extraction 
To generate structurally valid neoantigen targets from somatic missense mutations, a Python-based 
pipeline was implemented to automate protein mapping, mutation validation, and peptide extraction. This 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
step was executed using a cancer-specific Python script named generate_peptide_<CANCER 
_TYPE>.py, where <CANCER_TYPE> was replaced with the relevant tumor abbreviation (e.g., 
generate_peptide_BRCA.py).All script versions are available in the project GitHub repository. For each 
mutation entry, the script performed the following operations: 
1. Protein sequence retrieval: 
The script queried the UniProtKB REST API using the gene symbol to retrieve the corresponding 
canonical protein sequence from the Swiss-Prot database (organism: Homo sapiens, ID: 9606). 
In cases where multiple isoforms existed, the first listed canonical isoform was consistently 
selected to maintain uniformity across samples. 
2. Mutation parsing and validation: 
Mutation annotations (e.g., p.G12D) were parsed to extract the wild-type amino acid (G), 
mutation position (12), and mutant residue (D). The mutation was then mapped to the retrieved 
protein sequence, and the wild-type residue was checked against the reference amino acid at the 
specified position.  
If the amino acid at the reported position did not match the expected wild-type residue (due to 
annotation mismatches or isoform inconsistencies), the mutation was excluded from further 
analysis. 
3. Peptide extraction: 
For each verified mutation, a 9-mer peptide was constructed by extracting four amino acids 
upstream and four downstream of the mutated residue. The central wild-type residue was replaced 
with the mutant amino acid, simulating the altered epitope as it would be presented by MHC class 
I. 
Mutations occurring near the N- or C-terminus of the protein, where a full 9-mer could not be 
formed, were also excluded.

2.2.2 HLA Allele Selection 
A fixed panel of ten common HLA class I alleles was selected for consistent use across all peptide–MHC 
binding predictions. This panel was chosen to ensure broad population coverage and compatibility with 
structural modeling tools: 
HLA-A: A01:01, A02:01, A03:01, A11:01, A24:02 
HLA-B: B07:02, B08:01, B35:01, B44:02, B51:01 
These alleles were selected based on their global prevalence, frequent appearance in immunopeptidomics 
datasets, and availability of solved or modelable MHC structures. Using a consistent HLA set across both 
mutation-based and expression-based pipelines enabled direct comparison between neoantigens derived 
from different sources. 
2.3 MHC Binding Prediction and Neoantigen Prioritization 
To identify tumor-specific neoantigens suitable for TCR-mimic CAR T-cell therapy, two complementary 
strategies were implemented: 
• Mutation-based strategy, focusing on 9-mer peptides derived from somatic missense mutations 
with high predicted affinity for MHC class I molecules. 
• Expression-based strategy, focusing on peptides derived from genes with elevated expression in 
tumors and minimal expression in normal tissues. 
Both strategies shared the same downstream workflow: NetMHCpan v4.1 was used to predict peptide–
MHC binding strength, and only peptides with strong binding predictions were advanced to structural 
modeling and further analysis. 
2.3.1 The Mutation-Based Strategy 
This strategy focused on identifying strong MHC class I binders derived from somatic missense 
mutations in four tumor types: BRCA, COAD, LUAD, and SKCM. Peptides used for binding prediction 
were generated as described in Section 2.2, ensuring that each 9-mer was centered on the mutated residue, 
structurally valid, and traceable to a specific gene and sample. 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
NetMHCpan Input Configuration 
Peptide binding predictions were performed using NetMHCpan v4.1. The following settings were 
applied consistently across all tumor types: 
• Peptide Input: FASTA files generated in Section 2.2.1. Due to web server size limitations, these 
were manually split into smaller batches for submission. 
• Peptide Length: Fixed at 9 amino acids. 
• HLA Alleles: The panel of 10 HLA class I alleles defined in Section 2.2.2 was applied uniformly 
across all predictions. 
• Filtering Threshold: Set to -99 to retain all peptides and enable downstream filtering outside the 
server. 
Binding Classification: 
• Peptides were categorized based on their eluted ligand (EL) rank, which estimates the likelihood 
of a peptide being naturally presented on the cell surface: 
• 
Threshold for strong binder: % Rank < 0.5 
• 
Threshold for weak binder: % Rank < 2 
These thresholds are based on well-established benchmarks comparing EL rank to experimentally 
validated peptide–MHC interactions and T-cell recognition. The <0.5% cutoff is widely used to enrich 
for peptides with the highest immunogenic potential. 
Output: Prediction results were downloaded as .xls files and processed using a custom Python script for 
parsing, merging, and post-hoc filtering. 
Peptide Filtering and Prioritization for Structural Modeling 
Following MHC class I binding predictions using NetMHCpan v4.1, all peptides with EL rank < 0.5% 
were classified as strong binders. From each tumor type (BRCA, COAD, LUAD, and SKCM), peptides 
with the lowest EL ranks were shortlisted along with their predicted HLA alleles. These peptides were 
derived from missense mutations mapped to recurrent or oncogenic genes and were prioritized for 
downstream structural modeling. 
Tumor Specificity Assessment 
To evaluate tumor specificity, each mutant peptide was paired with a wild-type counterpart by reverting 
the central amino acid of the 9-mer to its original residue. 
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
 For example, FSEDSTRFY (AKT3_p.R247S) was reverted to FSEDRTRFY. 
Wild-type peptides were assessed using NetMHCpan under identical parameters to their mutant versions, 
allowing direct comparison of binding affinity across the same HLA alleles. 
Only peptides where the mutant sequence exhibited significantly stronger binding (i.e., lower EL rank) 
than the wild-type were retained. This filtering yielded ten high-affinity neoantigen–HLA pairs across 
the four tumor types, all showing favorable fold-change differences in binding. These pairs were 
considered both tumor-specific and immunologically distinct, supporting their selection for pMHC 
modeling and TCRm-CAR docking simulations. 
 
Shared Neoantigen Identification 
As an exploratory analysis, the filtered list of strong binders was screened for recurrent peptides across 
tumor types. One such peptide, VVGADGVGK, derived from the KRAS G12D mutation, was found in 
both LUAD and COAD datasets. This peptide exhibited strong binding to multiple HLA alleles (e.g., 
HLA-A11:01), indicating its potential as a shared neoantigen for pan-cancer targeting. 
TP53-Specific Neoantigen Pipeline 
Following this, an extended analysis was performed focusing on TP53, the most frequently mutated gene 
across six selected epithelial tumor types: BRCA, COAD, LUAD, PAAD, ESCA, and STAD. These 
cancers were chosen based on their epithelial origin and the availability of paired mutation and expression 
data. SKCM was excluded due to its non-epithelial origin and limited normal tissue expression data in 
GTEx, which limited tumor specificity assessment in later steps. 
For TP53: 
• All somatic missense mutations in TP53 were extracted from the six selected epithelial cancers 
(BRCA, COAD, LUAD, PAAD, ESCA, and STAD). 
• Each mutation was used to generate a 9-mer peptide centered on the altered residue, following 
the procedure outlined in Section 2.2. 
• These mutant peptides were submitted to NetMHCpan v4.1 for MHC binding prediction. Peptides 
with EL rank < 0.5% were classified as strong binders. From this set, the 10 peptides with the 
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
lowest EL ranks were selected for further comparison with their corresponding wild-type 
sequences. 
• For each selected strong binder, a corresponding wild-type peptide was reconstructed by reverting 
the central residue and re-analyzed using NetMHCpan under the same settings. 
• Only those mutant peptides that showed significantly stronger binding than their wild-type 
counterparts were retained. 
This extended TP53-focused analysis allowed the identification of neoantigens derived from the most 
commonly mutated gene across the selected tumor types, broadening the mutation-based pipeline beyond 
recurrence-based filtering alone. 
Pan-Cancer Mutation Frequency Analysis 
To identify genes that are recurrently mutated across multiple epithelial tumor types, mutation data were 
retrieved from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) for six solid tumors: breast invasive carcinoma 
(BRCA), colon adenocarcinoma (COAD), lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD), pancreatic adenocarcinoma 
(PAAD), stomach adenocarcinoma (STAD), and esophageal carcinoma (ESCA). For each cancer type, 
the top 20 most frequently mutated genes were extracted using the cBioPortal platform, and mutation 
frequency plots were generated to visualize the distribution of mutations within each cohort. 
2.3.2 The Expression-Based Strategy: 
To expand the neoantigen discovery pipeline beyond mutation-centric strategies, an expression-based 
approach was implemented focusing on highly tumor-specific overexpression profiles. This was 
particularly aimed at identifying neoantigens from genes upregulated in tumors with low mutational 
burden or greater heterogeneity. 
Tumor Type Expansion and Expression Profiling 
This analysis included six epithelial tumor types from TCGA: BRCA, COAD, LUAD, PAAD, ESCA, 
and STAD selected for their shared tissue origin and comprehensive data availability. Skin cancer 
(SKCM) was excluded in this phase to focus on epithelial tumors. 
==PAGE:27==
27 
 
Differential gene expression (DGE) analysis was conducted using DESeq2 in RStudio. Tumor RNA-seq 
data from TCGA were compared against normal tissue expression from GTEx (v8 release) accessed via 
the GTEx Portal. Genes were considered significantly overexpressed if they met the following criteria: 
Log₂ fold change > 2 
Indicates that the gene is expressed at least 4-fold higher in tumor tissue compared to 
normal tissue, reflecting biologically relevant upregulation. 
Adjusted p-value < 0.01 
Ensures statistical significance after multiple testing correction (e.g., Benjamini–
Hochberg), reducing the likelihood of false positives in differential expression. 
Median TPM < 1 in GTEx 
Filters out genes with baseline expression in normal tissues, helping to identify targets that 
are tumor-restricted and minimizing off-tumor risks. 
Mutation Filtering and Neoantigen Prediction 
The filtered list of tumor-specific genes was then cross-referenced with TCGA somatic mutation data to 
identify genes carrying protein-altering mutations. For these genes, the same neoantigen prediction 
pipeline described in earlier sections was applied: mutant 9-mer peptides were generated, MHC class I 
binding was predicted using NetMHCpan v4.1, and peptides with EL rank < 0.5% were retained as strong 
binders. 
While the mutation-based approach screened all mutated genes, this expression-guided strategy narrowed 
the scope to only those genes that were both highly tumor-specific and mutation-bearing. 
Tumor Specificity Validation 
To further assess tumor specificity and minimize off-target risks, each mutant peptide–HLA pair was re-
evaluated alongside its corresponding wild-type version, as described in the mutation-based strategy. 
Wild-type sequences were reconstructed by reverting the central residue of each 9-mer back to its 
reference (non-mutated) amino acid. These wild-type peptides were submitted to NetMHCpan using the 
same HLA alleles and binding parameters to enable direct comparison. 
==PAGE:28==
28 
 
For each mutant–wild-type pair, EL ranks were compared across all HLA alleles. Peptides were 
shortlisted only when the mutant version demonstrated clearly stronger binding than its wild-type 
counterpart, typically indicated by a lower EL rank and substantial fold-change. This step prioritized 
immunogenic, tumor-specific peptides 
This final set of expression-derived neoantigens was combined with those from the mutation-based 
pipeline for downstream structural modeling and docking analyses.

2.4 Structural Modeling of Peptide–MHC Complexes 
To assess the structural feasibility of neoantigen presentation, 3D models of peptide–MHC class I 
(pMHC) complexes were generated for shortlisted neoantigen–HLA pairs derived from both mutation-
based and expression-based strategies. The goal was to confirm stable peptide accommodation within 
the MHC binding groove and prepare validated complexes for receptor docking simulations. 
2.4.1 Input Preparation and Model Assembly 
Each pMHC complex was modeled using AlphaFold2-Multimer via ColabFold. For every neoantigen–
HLA pair, a three-sequence FASTA file was constructed containing the HLA class I heavy chain (from 
the IMGT/HLA Database), β2-microglobulin (β2M, from UniProt), and the 9-mer neoantigen peptide 
sequence identified from NetMHCpan predictions. Models were generated without templates to allow 
unbiased pMHC prediction. Resulting structures were evaluated in PyMOL to verify correct peptide 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
insertion, MHC groove compatibility, and absence of structural errors. Only stable and plausible 
complexes were retained for downstream receptor interaction analysis. 
2.4.2 TCRm-CAR Design and scFv Modeling 
With validated pMHC models in place, the next step was to evaluate whether these complexes could 
structurally engage immune receptors either natural TCRs or engineered TCR-mimic scFvs. Public 
databases (VDJdb, McPAS-TCR) were queried for TCR sequences with matching HLA restrictions. 
However, most entries lacked full α/β chain pairing or were not specific to the neoantigens of interest. 
As a workaround, well-characterized TCRs like LC13 (HLA-B*08:01-restricted) were selected for 
structural modeling due to their compatibility with the target MHC allele. 
TCR sequences were annotated using ANARCI to define CDR regions, and full TCR models were built 
using ABodyBuilder2. Structural validation was conducted in PyMOL and PDBsum to ensure correct 
folding and surface accessibility of the antigen-binding site. In cases where native TCRs were unavailable 
or incomplete, antibody-based scFv frameworks were considered for TCRm-CAR design, leveraging 
existing scaffold structures and re-engineering their variable domains for MHC-peptide targeting. 
 
2.4.3 Molecular Docking and Structural Modeling 
To simulate receptor engagement, molecular docking was performed using HADDOCK 2.4. Each pMHC 
structure served as the receptor, while the modeled TCR or scFv fragment was defined as the ligand. 
Docking was guided by CDR regions identified in ANARCI and refined using semi-flexible modeling 
around the predicted interface. Several docking attempts, particularly those involving antibody-derived 
scFvs, failed to yield stable or biologically plausible complexes, likely due to structural incompatibilities. 
To address these limitations, AlphaFold-Multimer was used as an alternative docking approach. By 
modeling the pMHC–receptor complex as a co-folded unit directly from sequence, this method bypassed 
the need for predefined contact residues or rigid docking constraints. The resulting models were inspected 
in PyMOL to evaluate peptide engagement, interface complementarity, and overall structural plausibility. 
Together, these modeling efforts formed the foundation for identifying neoantigens capable of stable 
MHC presentation and potential recognition by engineered immune receptors, supporting the design of 
structure-informed TCRm-CAR constructs.

3. Results 
This section presents the key findings of the neoantigen discovery pipeline, organized around two main 
strategies, mutation-based and expression-based, and two focused observations: (1) the recurrence of 
TP53 mutations across cancers and (2) the identification of KRAS G12D as a shared neoantigen across 
tumor types. Each subsection includes both computational prediction results and structural validation 
through TCR–pMHC modeling. 
 
To evaluate peptide presentation and TCR interaction, AlphaFold-Multimer was used to model peptide–
MHC class I complexes, combining 9-mer peptides with the appropriate HLA allele and β2-
microglobulin. For TCR–pMHC ternary complex modeling, known TCR structures were selected based 
on HLA compatibility: LC13 TCR (PDB: 1MI5) for HLA-B08:01 and OW6 TCR (PDB: 7OW6) for 
HLA-A11:01. CDR loops were annotated using ANARCI and visualized in PyMOL to assess spatial 
proximity to mutation sites and potential structural compatibility for TCR mimic CAR design.

these, top-ranking peptides were prioritized based on their improved binding compared to wild-type 
counterparts, as measured by fold-change in EL rank.

– BRCA: ERCC1 (P178R)

– LUAD: AGO2 (S226F), SESN (R264H) 
– SKCM: AFP (T142M), AKT3 (G476R)

3.3 Structural Validation of Peptide–MHC Models 
To ensure that the modeled peptide–MHC (pMHC) structures were suitable for downstream docking 
simulations and interaction analyses, each complex was evaluated using PROCHECK as part of the 
PDBsum server and LigPlot+. These tools assess the overall geometry of the protein structure, identify 
any structural distortions, and evaluate how well the peptide fits within the MHC binding groove. 
The four models examined were derived from different target selection strategies: 
1. Mutation-based neoantigen 
2. Expression-based neoantigen 
3. Shared peptide found in multiple tumor types 
4. TP53-derived hotspot neoantigen 
Each structure was evaluated in terms of backbone conformation, side-chain geometry, and peptide 
anchoring within the MHC groove. 
3.3.1 Ramachandran Plot Analysis 
The Ramachandran plot checks the angles of the protein backbone to determine if they are in stable, 
allowed positions based on known protein structures. A high percentage of residues in the “most 
favoured” regions indicates a well-folded and realistic structure, while any residues in disallowed regions

3.3.2 Bond Geometry and Side-Chain Stability 
 
In all four pMHC models, mutation-based, expression-based, shared neoantigen, and TP53, the lengths 
and angles between backbone atoms were within the normal range for high-quality protein structures. 
Most bonds differed from ideal values by less than 0.05 Å, and the slightly larger deviations (0.10–0.20 
Å) were observed only in flexible loop or terminal regions outside the peptide–MHC binding site. This 
indicates that the structural core, where the peptide is bound, is not distorted.  
Side-chain orientations were also assessed by PROCHECK, which compares torsion angles to those 
found in experimentally solved protein structures. In the expression-based model, the Chi-1 rotation 
variation was 16.8° compared to a typical value of 18.2°, which falls within the expected range. The Chi-
2 rotation variation was 14.4° compared to the typical 20.4°, a result classified by PROCHECK as “better 
than average” due to lower variability. The mutation-based, shared neoantigen, and TP53 models showed 
similar or stronger results, all remaining within expected ranges or better than average. 
These findings confirm that the side chains adopt realistic, strain-free orientations, particularly in and 
around the peptide-binding groove, supporting the stability of the models and their suitability for 
downstream docking and interaction studies.

4. Summary 
 
Across all four pMHC complexes: 
• Backbone geometry met high-quality structural benchmarks. 
• Side chains were correctly oriented, with no abnormal torsions. 
• Peptides were anchored by multiple hydrogen bonds (2.40–3.09 Å) and complementary 
hydrophobic interactions. 
• Shared neoantigen and TP53 models achieved the highest stereochemical quality, with no 
disallowed Ramachandran residues. 
These findings confirm that all models are structurally robust and meet the quality requirements for 
reliable docking simulations and downstream TCR-mimic CAR interaction studies. Building on this 
foundation, the next stage focuses on docking these validated pMHC models with TCR-mimic scFvs to 
evaluate binding geometry, interface complementarity, and predicted interaction strength. Complexes 
showing optimal orientation and contact profiles will then be subjected to molecular dynamics 
simulations to assess stability and refine the predicted binding interfaces under near-physiological 
conditions.

Future Work 
The next stage in translating this computational work into a therapeutic application involves progressing 
from in silico–validated pMHC–scFv interactions to the design and assembly of complete TCR-mimic 
CAR constructs. This begins with scFv generation, in which the variable heavy (VH) and variable light 
(VL) domains from the top-performing TCR models identified in this study are formatted into single-
chain variable fragments. These scFvs must retain high structural compatibility with the target HLA–
peptide context to ensure selective binding. Once optimized, they are incorporated into a CAR 
architecture, which includes a hinge domain for flexibility, a transmembrane domain for anchoring in the 
T-cell membrane, and intracellular signaling domains such as CD3ζ in combination with co-stimulatory 
modules (e.g., CD28 or 4-1BB) to trigger robust T-cell activation upon antigen engagement. 
In parallel, there is an opportunity to explore tandem TCRm-CAR designs, in which two distinct scFvs 
are linked within the same CAR framework. This configuration could target two independent peptide–
MHC complexes, such as COL11A1_G110R and KRAS G12D, thereby broadening tumor coverage and 
reducing the risk of immune escape through antigen loss. The final stage would be experimental 
validation, involving the cloning of these constructs into suitable expression vectors, transduction into 
primary T cells, and systematic testing for specificity, binding affinity, and cytotoxic activity against 
target-expressing cells, both in vitro and in vivo. 
By building directly on the structural and binding feasibility demonstrated in this study, these steps would 
advance the pipeline toward a clinically viable, tumor-specific TCRm-CAR therapy. 
5. Discussion 
This study presents a computational strategy for identifying tumor-specific neoantigens suitable for TCR-
mimic CAR T-cell therapy. By combining mutation-derived and expression-based discovery approaches, 
the pipeline successfully prioritized peptides with high predicted MHC binding affinity, tumor specificity, 
and plausible structural compatibility for scFv-based targeting. The strength of this approach lies in its 
integrative nature: it leverages both genomic mutations and transcriptomic dysregulation to uncover a 
broader spectrum of targets, which is particularly valuable in tumors with heterogeneous or low 
mutational burden. 
One of the key observations was the identification of multiple strong-binding peptides restricted to a 
single HLA allele (e.g., HLA-B*08:01). This pattern enabled the formulation of a tandem CAR design 
==PAGE:44==
44 
 
strategy where a single CAR construct could incorporate two distinct scFvs targeting two different 
peptide–MHC complexes presented on the same tumor. Although speculative at this stage, such a strategy 
could significantly improve antigen coverage and reduce immune escape, especially in solid tumors 
known for their intratumoral diversity. From a structural perspective, docking results support the 
feasibility of this design, particularly using antibody-derived scFv scaffolds rather than canonical TCR 
frameworks, which showed limited docking success in some cases. 
 
Despite these strengths, the study remains limited by its purely in silico nature. Computational tools like 
NetMHCpan and AlphaFold offer powerful predictions, but actual antigen processing, peptide 
abundance, and immunogenicity cannot be confirmed without experimental validation. Similarly, 
docking results provide only a static snapshot of potential interactions, and lack the dynamic insight 
offered by molecular dynamics simulations or cellular assays. Additionally, the HLA coverage was 
restricted to a selected subset of alleles, and expanding this to more diverse or underrepresented 
populations would increase clinical relevance. 
 
Taken together, the results of this study suggest that neoantigen-based TCRm-CARs represent a 
promising direction for personalized or tumor-type-specific immunotherapies. By expanding beyond 
conventional tumor-associated antigens and leveraging tumor-exclusive peptide–MHC targets, it may be 
possible to design CARs with improved precision and safety profiles. The identification of structurally 
viable, shared neoantigens also raises the possibility of creating semi-personalized therapies that are still 
broadly applicable across patients with common HLA types. While speculative, the tandem TCRm-CAR 
approach proposed here could contribute to overcoming antigen escape in solid tumors, if validated 
experimentally. 
 
In conclusion, this work provides a scalable, modular pipeline for neoantigen prioritization and structural 
modeling, and introduces a tandem targeting strategy based on shared HLA restriction. These findings 
lay the foundation for future experimental studies and offer a path forward for structure-informed design 
of next-generation CAR T-cell therapies tailored to the unique immunogenic landscape of solid tumors.
