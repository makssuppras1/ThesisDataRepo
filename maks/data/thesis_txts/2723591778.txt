Abstract 
In this thesis, the potential of using additive manufacturing to produce PEEK dies for 
polymer extrusion is explored. The study includes experimental testing, flow 
simulation, and optimization of die geometry for the MEK03 extruding PP and ABS. 
The investigation provides valuable insights into the performance of PEEK dies and 
their potential to improve the quality and functionality of extruded products. 
The experimental testing section collects critical data on the performance of the 
MEK03 die, providing a basis for the validation of the flow simulation results and the 
development of an optimized die geometry. The flow simulation section uses 
computational fluid dynamics (CFD) to model the flow of the polymer melt in the die, 
resulting in a deeper understanding of the flow behavior of the polymer melt in the die. 
The flow simulation results facilitate the identification of areas for improvement in the 
MEK03 die design, leading to an optimized die geometry that enhances the flow 
characteristics of the die. 
An additional significant achievement of this thesis is the analysis of surface 
roughness of the extruded parts collected during the experiments. The analysis 
reveals a direct correlation between the surface roughness of the extrudates and the 
screw speed. The finding is particularly noteworthy, as surface roughness is a critical 
aspect of the quality of extruded products, affecting their functionality, appearance, 
and durability. The correlation between screw speed and surface roughness highlights 
the importance of controlling the screw speed during the extrusion process and 
optimizing it to achieve a smoother surface finish for the final product. 
The findings of this study have significant implications for the extrusion industry, 
suggesting that manufacturers must carefully consider the impact of screw speed on 
product quality and incorporate the appropriate controls and parameters to optimize 
surface finish. The reproducibility of the FDM process is a notable achievement, with 
the potential to enable manufacturers to produce highly reliable and functional 
products at a lower cost. 
Overall, this thesis contributes to the field of polymer extrusion by providing valuable 
insights into the performance of PEEK dies and the importance of optimizing the screw 
speed to achieve a smoother surface finish. The findings have significant implications 
for the extrusion industry and the broader field of additive manufacturing, indicating 
that continued research and development could lead to further improvements in the 
reproducibility and quality of 3D-printed parts.

2 Introduction 
2.1 Background and Motivation 
Polymer extrusion is a widely used manufacturing technique that accounts for 
approximately 30% of plastic products worldwide. One of the key benefits of plastic 
extrusion is the ability to mass-produce products with complex cross-sections. 
However, achieving this level of production efficiency requires multiple process design 
changes before the die is finalized, and the costs associated with the development of 
the die can be prohibitively high for small batches and highly customized products. 
Additive Manufacturing (AM) has experienced remarkable growth in recent years due 
to technological advancements that have reduced costs and improved final product 
quality. The high flexibility and design freedom of AM products provide the foundation 
for optimized extrusion dies with complex geometries that are difficult or impossible to 
manufacture using conventional subtractive manufacturing methods. This flexibility 
and responsiveness have not yet been achieved in the polymer extrusion industry, 
where different die designs and specific polymer materials can result in different 
yielded products. The design of the extrusion die is the most challenging part of the 
process, due to issues such as melt pressure, melt temperature, and polymer swelling 
that can impede uniform melt flow. AM techniques such as Stereolithography (SLA) 
and Continuous Liquid Interface Production (CLIP) have been implemented to create 
soft tools for the die. However, the SLA manufacturing technique creates an evident 
layer on the fillet edges that can make it challenging to measure the back features of 
the profile. CLIP, an evolution of the Digital Light Processing (DLP) technique, is an 
improvement over SLA and can produce parts without an evident layer, although some 
inaccuracy is still detected. In addition to the additive manufacturing techniques used, 
the material used for the die also plays a crucial role in the design. Soft tools are 
significantly affected by the thermal and mechanical properties of the polymer and are 
subject to heat deflection and relatively rapid wear. Recent developments, such as the 
introduction of glass fiber and carbon reinforcements, have made AM materials 
increasingly popular due to their ability to improve mechanical and thermal properties. 
Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF) or Fused Deposition Modelling (FDM) is the 
preferred process for printing composite materials, as the melted polymer with 
reinforcement material is uniformly extruded through the nozzle and deposited into the 
desired cross-section of the printed object. However, FFF has the limitation of reduced 
surface quality due to the layering effect of filament. In a previous study, a series of 
carbon fiber reinforced Polyether-ether-ketone (PEEK) dies were manufactured using 
a Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF) AM process for polymer extrusion. The dies were 
characterized and experimentally tested in profile extrusion, and the results indicated 
that they were able to withstand the combination of high pressure and high 
temperature of the extrusion process. The study tested three profile designs of carbon 
fiber reinforced PEEK dies, and each design was produced in five samples. 
Experimental testing was performed with profile MEK01 and propylene (PP) as the 
==PAGE:11==
P a g e  11 | 146 
 
 
extrudates material, yielding extrudates of 330 meters in length, running for 82 minutes 
and 15 seconds. The use of AM in the plastic extrusion industry has the potential to 
disrupt and improve the process chain, as the high flexibility and design freedom of 
AM products allow for optimized extrusion dies with complex geometries that were 
previously impossible to manufacture using conventional methods. The material 
selection, as well as the improved die geometry achieved through AM, are expected 
to mitigate the issues associated with soft tooling. As AM continues to advance, it is 
likely that it will play an increasingly important role in the plastic extrusion industry and 
other manufacturing sectors.

2.2 Objectives 
The objectives of this project are summarized as follows: 
1. To perform experimental testing for the remaining dies designs (MEK02 and 
MEK03) extruding PP and ABS, to obtain relevant experimental data i.e., mass flow 
and die head pressure by varying the screw speed. 
2. To perform flow simulation and their validation by comparison with experimental 
testing data. 
3. To optimize the geometry of die based on flow simulation results. 
4. To redesign the die and die adapter considering the thermal expansion of the die 
after experimental testing.

3 Polymer Extrusion 
3.1 Introduction 
Polymer extrusion is a process used to create continuous shapes from polymer 
materials, such as plastic or rubber. It involves melting the polymer and forcing it 
through a die or shape to create a desired shape, such as a tube, sheet, or profile. 
The history of polymer extrusion dates back to the 1930s, when the first extruders 
were developed for producing plastic pipes. Since then, extrusion has become a 
widely used process in various industries, including construction, automotive, 
packaging, and consumer products. 
There are several types of extruders, including single screw, twin screw, and ram. 
Single screw extruders are the most common and are used for a wide range of 
applications. Twin screw extruders are used for more complex shapes and can also 
be used for mixing and compounding. Ram extruders are used for high-pressure 
applications, such as extrusion of metal or wood composites. 
There are several parameters that can affect the quality and efficiency of the extrusion 
process, including temperature, pressure, screw speed, and die design. Proper control 
of these parameters is essential to produce consistent, high-quality products. 
There is a vast literature on polymer extrusion, covering various aspects of the 
process, including design and optimization of extruders, modeling and simulation of 
the extrusion process, and the influence of various parameters on the quality and 
properties of the extruded products. 
3.2 Extrusion Process 
Polymer extrusion is a continuous process that involves melting a polymer material 
and forcing it through a die or shape to create a continuous product. The extruder is 
just one component of the entire production line, which may include numerous 
operations and require good communication and collaboration among operators.  
The extrusion process can be affected by various factors, such as the temperature 
profile of the extruder, the formulation of the product ingredients, the melt temperature, 
and the cooling bath temperature, among others. If any of these conditions are not set 
correctly, the product may not meet the desired specifications.  
Before being extruded, some polymer materials may need to be dried to prevent 
degradation due to moisture. Once the polymer is properly dried and formulated, it is 
fed into the extruder, where it is melted and mixed before being shaped in the die. 
After exiting the die, the product is cooled and solidified before being pulled away from 
the extruder at a constant velocity. Secondary operations, such as flame treatment, 
printing, cutting, and annealing, may be performed on the product before it is 
inspected, packaged, and shipped. (Harold F. Giles, 2005) 
==PAGE:14==
P a g e  14 | 146 
 
 
 
Figure 1: Basic extrusion process schematic 
Profile extrusion is a widely used process that involves creating continuous shapes 
from polymer materials, such as plastic or rubber. This process is often used to 
produce a wide range of product shapes in high volume. Profile die has a fixed opening 
requiring a specific draw down ratio, with only one speed ratio between the extruder 
and puller or haul-off that provides the correct product dimensions. Profiles can be 
produced with any resin system, the most common resins are: 
• 
PVC 
• 
Polyethylene 
• 
Polypropylene 
• 
ABS 
• 
High impact polystyrene 
The profile extrusion process typically involves several pieces of equipment, as shown 
in Figure 2. These may include an extruder, a cooling tank, a puller, a movable saw 
for cutting the product, and a product packaging system. The type of cooling tank used 
in the process can vary, with some systems using a vacuum calibration tank and others 
using a cooling tank without vacuum. The movable saw can be replaced with other 
cutting equipment, such as a rotary cutter or guillotine cutter, depending on the size 
and material of the profile being produced. 
 
Figure 2: Schematic of equipment used in profile extrusion.

3.2.1 Extruder 
The single screw extruder is depicted in a cut-away drawing in Figure 3. This 
illustration shows the various components of the machine, including the screw and 
barrel, which work together to convey, melt, and shape the plastic material. The 
electric motor drive unit and gearbox are responsible for rotating the screw at a 
predetermined speed. Temperature controllers are also present to regulate the 
temperature of the barrel by controlling heating and cooling elements. These 
components interact to produce a continuous shape from the polymer material by 
melting it and forcing it through a die. The proper operation of these parts is essential 
for efficient and consistent extrusion of high-quality products. 
 
Figure 3: Single screw extruder. 
3.2.2 Feeding Fase 
During the feeding phase of the extrusion process, the plastic material typically flows 
into the extruder barrel under the force of gravity. However, depending on the type 
and nature of the material, some flow issues may arise. To ensure a steady flow and 
prevent material bridging or wall sticking in the hopper, additional devices such as 
vibrating pads and mixing stirrers may be needed. It is important to avoid entrapping 
air during the feeding process, as it can affect the final quality of the extrudate by 
causing surface imperfections and defects. One effective method for preventing air 
entrapment is the use of vacuum feed hoppers. (Rauwendaal, 2014) 
3.2.3 Screw 
Throughout the history of extrusion, a variety of screw designs have been developed 
for processing thermoplastic materials. Some screws are designed to improve mixing, 
while others are tailored to specific materials or processes. With the increasing 
availability of computing power and a deeper understanding of the extrusion process, 
computer-aided design of extrusion screws has become a common practice. These 
advances have helped to optimize the performance and efficiency of extrusion 
processes for a wide range of applications. 
 
==PAGE:16==
P a g e  16 | 146 
 
 
 
Figure 4: Standard single flighted screw. 
The feed section of a plastic extrusion process is responsible for conveying solid resin 
and heating it up. As the resin is forced against the barrel wall in the transition zone, 
sliding friction and conductive heating create a melt film on the barrel surface. The 
polymer is then divided into three distinct areas: a compacted solid bed, a melt film on 
the barrel wall, and a polymer melt pool. Near the end of the transition zone, the solid 
bed size decreases and there is a risk of solid bed breakup. In the metering section, 
the plastic is pushed through a shallow channel to the discharge end. Good material 
compression is essential to a correct functioning of the extruder and to a good final 
quality of the extrudate However, if unmelted pellets are floating in the melt due to 
solid bed breakup, it can lead to nonuniform melt, poor mixing of additives, and inferior 
product properties. (Whelan) 
3.2.4 Die 
The die is a part of the plastic extrusion process that is located at the end of the 
extruder and contains an orifice used to shape the molten plastic. It consists of a block 
of metal with an internal flow channel that can be divided into three parts: the adapter 
section that connects to the output channel of the extruder, the distribution section that 
spreads the melt into the desired shape, and the land, which maintains a constant 
cross section of the flow channel until it reaches the exit lips of the die. 
There are three types of die designs that can be used in the production process, 
depending on the length of the production run and the type of polymer being 
processed: flat back, semi-streamlined, and streamlined.  
Flat back dies are suitable for low volume or prototype runs that are four to six hours 
long. They consist of a flat plate with the desired profile cut into it, mounted onto a 
round tube that conveys the molten polymer from the extruder. The die plate is flat on 
the side facing the extruder and is directly attached to the tube that makes up the body 
of the die.  
A semi-streamlined die has a tapered channel in the die body that compresses the 
polymer flow as it approaches the die plate, reducing the areas where polymer 
degradation can occur.  
Streamlined dies, on the other hand, have a streamlined polymer melt channel that 
leads directly to the land area with no dead spots for polymer stagnation and 
degradation. They are used in long production runs where polymer degradation must 
be avoided.  
==PAGE:17==
P a g e  17 | 146 
 
 
Semi-streamlined dies fall in between the two extremes and can be used in situations 
where a flat back die is not sufficient, but a streamlined die is not necessary. Some 
dies also have inserts that allow for different shapes or slight modifications of the basic 
profile to be made more easily. 
 
Figure 5: Semi-streamlined die. 
The desired cross section of the profile can be used to design the required die cross 
section using computer programs. These programs can also predict the pressure 
drops, melt temperature, and flow profiles within the die based on the rheological 
parameters (viscosity versus shear rate, extension modulus and flow modulus) of the 
resin and the desired throughput rate. 
One of the main challenges in designing a profile extrusion die is achieving a uniform 
velocity profile at the exit face. If the velocity is non-uniform across the exit face, it can 
cause the extrudate to deform after it leaves the die. The polymer melt tends to flow 
more readily through areas with less flow restriction, leading to higher velocities and 
more flow in the thicker regions of the die and lower velocities and less flow in the 
thinner regions. To address this issue, the design of the extrusion die begins with the 
dimensions of the extrudate and the geometry of the exit face and progresses 
upstream to determine other features of the die. The primary strategy used is to reduce 
the thickness of the regions that feed the wider sections of the exit profile and increase 
the thickness of the regions that feed the thinner sections. This helps to balance the 
flow and achieve a uniform velocity profile at the exit. (Pradeep, 2016) 
 
Figure 6: Comparison of profiles with versus nonuniform cross sections. 
Incorrectly designed dies can cause significant warping issues in the profiles due to a 
variety of other factors. When there is a higher flow rate of polymer in one area of the 
die compared to another, it can result in different molecular orientation and uneven 
==PAGE:18==
P a g e  18 | 146 
 
 
shrinkage characteristics, leading to differential cooling and shrinkage outside the die. 
Similarly, if the melt temperature is not consistent throughout all channels of the die, it 
can cause one section of the profile to be hotter than the others, leading to warping. 
Additionally, if there is a difference in molecular packing in different parts of the die 
due to uneven die pressure, it can also contribute to warping in the final part. 
For more complex profiles, including those with internal partitions and hollow 
chambers, and where higher line speeds are required, streamlined dies are used. 
Here, transitions in the flow channel from extruder to die exit are smooth and gradual; 
they are usually made up of four zones along the flow length: die inlet, pre-forming, 
adapter, and parallel zone. Depending upon the complexity of the profile, the die will 
consist of several adapter plates, starting from the circular section at the extruder and 
successively introducing more detail of the profile. The final plate(s) will carry the 
parallel land zone, where the flow channels are prismatic in form. The intention of this 
is to allow stresses resulting from extensional deformations in converging flow further 
back in the die to relax and to provide a well-controlled flow at the exit.  
Since a large part of the total flow resistance through the die is due to the narrow 
channels in the land region, adjustments to the dimensions here have a significant 
influence on the flow distribution across the profile and are often a key part of the 
design development. (Pittman, 1996) 
 
Figure 7: Die Channels 
Another options to change the flow resistance in different legs, the land length is 
modified, making it longer in thicker profile sections and shorter in thinner profile 
sections to produce equivalent pressure drops. The best way to establish the land 
length is to provide sufficient land to generate 1000 to 1500 psi across the land. 
(Harold F. Giles, 2005)

3.2.5 Calibration System 
To ensure the correct dimensions of a part and prevent warping or twisting, cooling 
and sizing or calibration is often employed. This process involves cooling the part as 
it exits the die, either through the use of air or water spray, flooding with water, or 
passing it through a metal calibrator with circulating water and a heat exchanger. The 
cooling helps to solidify the part to its final dimensions. 
To prevent warping and twisting caused by differential shrinkage, it is important to 
ensure uniform cooling. This can be achieved by ensuring that the flow from the die is 
uniform and that the profile is not dragged across a surface during the cooling process. 
Nonuniform cooling can often lead to bowing or twisting of the profile. 
Cooling techniques for shaping and sizing plastic parts include air cooling, water 
cooling, and vacuum sizing or calibration. 
Air Cooling 
Air cooling is often used for simple shapes with relatively loose tolerances. It is a 
relatively slow process, which gives time to adjust the profile with jigs to freeze it in the 
desired configuration. The most important considerations for this method are a uniform 
melt temperature, good melt strength, and uniform cooling around the entire part. Air 
is directed onto the profile through adjustable nozzles as it exits the die. 
Water Cooling 
Water cooling involves submerging the profile in a bath below the water level and using 
a fixture to size and hold the profile during cooling. The profile may pass through a 
ring that sprays water on all sides simultaneously, or through water nozzles before 
entering the tank. 
Vacuum Sizing 
A more advanced cooling system is vacuum sizing or calibration under water, where 
a hollow profile, whether round or square, exits the die and enters a sizing or 
calibration unit in an underwater vacuum tank. The water acts as a lubricant in the 
calibrator, which may have multiple compartments with adjustable vacuum levels and 
the option to surround or spray the profile with water at different temperatures. The 
highest vacuum is typically in the first compartment, where the polymer is being 
expanded the most as the surface skin forms. 
Dry Vacuum Calibration 
Dry vacuum calibration is used for complex, rigid shapes that require high dimensional 
precision and accuracy, such as window profiles. It involves one or more calibrators 
or sizing fixtures and a secondary spray cooling tank. Calibrators can be made of 
aluminum, steel, or stainless steel and have highly polished or chrome or nickel-plated 
surfaces to improve the profile's surface finish. The calibrator length must be sufficient 
to provide enough cooling to hold the dimensions while running at high throughput 
==PAGE:20==
P a g e  20 | 146 
 
 
rates. After exiting the calibrator, the profile may pass through an additional cooling 
tank. 
 
Figure 8: Example of the dry vacuum calibration system 
3.2.6 Puller 
The puller, or haul-off unit, is responsible for pulling the product from the die through 
the calibrator or sizing tank at a constant rate with the correct draw down ratio to 
achieve the desired dimensions. The puller must apply pressure evenly and at a 
uniform speed to avoid crushing or distorting the profile, while also taking into account 
the tension created during the calibration process. Belts with different levels of 
hardness, abrasion resistance, and friction can be used for this purpose. Longer puller 
lengths allow the profile to be gripped with less pressure, minimizing distortion while 
still providing sufficient pulling power. The puller may be powered by AC or DC motors, 
with digital controls including start and stop buttons.

3.3 Extrusion Problems and Defects 
There are several extrusion problems and defects that can occur during the 
extrusion process. Some common ones include: 
Die swell: Die swell is a phenomenon that occurs when the cross-sectional area of 
the extruded product becomes larger than the die opening. This is caused by shearing 
of the polymer melt as it passes through the die, which causes the molecules to 
become extended, with the greatest orientation near the die wall. When the melt 
emerges from the die, the molecules tend to coil up, resulting in a contraction in the 
flow direction and an expansion in directions perpendicular to the flow. If an extrudate 
is cut at the die face, it will have a convex leading edge, which indicates that the 
greatest contraction in the flow direction is nearest to the wall where the shear has 
been greatest. 
 
Figure 10: Die swell 
To reduce die swell in a given material, the following strategies can be employed: 
• Decreasing the extrusion rate 
• Increasing the melt temperature 
• Increasing the length of the die parallel or lands 
• Streamlining and/or reducing the die entry angle 
• Increasing the draw down ratio 
 
 
==PAGE:22==
P a g e  22 | 146 
 
 
In addition, the use of a lubricant can help to reduce die swell by causing slip at the 
wall and easing the flow of polymer through the die. Using a low molecular weight 
polymer as a lubricant, or broadening the molecular weight distribution of the material, 
can also help to reduce die swell. 
 
Figure 11: Extrudate distortion due to die swell 
Draw Down: The draw down depends on the puller speed relative to the extruder 
output. Draw ratio is directly related to molecular orientation, resulting in higher tensile 
and flexural properties in the machine direction compared to the transverse position. 
If the extruder throughput is increased, the puller speed must be increased 
proportionally to maintain the same finished product dimensions. Likewise, if the 
throughput is decreased, the puller speed must be decreased proportionally to 
maintain the same finished product cross sectional area. The draw ratio and molecular 
orientation can only be increased or decreased by changing the die cross sectional 
area relative to the puller speed, assuming the final product dimensions are kept 
constant. Dimensions of the profile are increased due to extrudate swell phenomenon 
but are decreased by drawdown and shrinkage. (MICHEL A. HUNEAULT) 
 
Figure 12: Die swell and draw down 
The draw down ratio is defined as the die cross section divided by the final product 
cross section. If the die opening is twice as large as the final product, the draw ratio is 
2:1. Higher draw ratios produce higher molecular orientation, resulting in greater 
differences between machine and transverse direction properties. (Harold F. Giles, 
2005)

Die lines or streaks: Die lines or streaks are visible lines or streaks on the surface of 
the extruded product that are caused by variations in the flow of the polymer melt as 
it passes through the die. These variations can be caused by a number of factors, 
including variations in the melt temperature, variations in the viscosity of the polymer 
melt, and variations in the pressure of the polymer melt. 
Blisters: Blisters are small, raised areas on the surface of the extruded product that 
are caused by pockets of air or gas that become trapped in the polymer melt as it is 
extruded. Blisters can be caused by several factors, including poor venting of the die, 
insufficient back pressure, and excessive moisture in the polymer melt. 
Sink marks: Sink marks are depression or indentations on the surface of the extruded 
product that are caused by the polymer melt not being able to flow uniformly into the 
die cavity. Sink marks can be caused by a few factors, including poor material flow, 
variations in the melt temperature, and variations in the viscosity of the polymer melt. 
Warping: Warping is the distortion or deformation of the extruded product, which can 
be caused by a variety of factors, including uneven cooling, poor material flow, and 
improper processing conditions. Warping can be caused by the polymer melt not being 
able to flow uniformly into the die cavity, which can lead to uneven cooling and 
distortion of the extruded product. 
Burn marks: Burn marks are black or discolored areas on the surface of the extruded 
product that are caused by the polymer melt being overheated or exposed to too much 
heat for an extended period of time. Burn marks can be caused by several factors, 
including high melt temperature, high extrusion pressure, and poor material flow. 
Poor surface finish: A poor surface finish on the extruded product can be caused by 
a variety of factors, including poor die design, poor material flow, and improper 
processing conditions. A poor surface finish can result in rough or uneven surfaces, 
or surfaces that are too smooth or too rough. It can also result in surface imperfections, 
such as cracks, wrinkles, or voids.

4 Die Fabrication 
4.1 Introduction 
Additive Manufacturing, is a rapidly growing field that offers numerous advantages 
over traditional manufacturing methods. The technology involves creating 3D objects 
directly from a CAD model by depositing material layer by layer, without the need for 
specific molds. This layer-based approach offers a high degree of flexibility, both in 
terms of shape and material choice. By simply changing the CAD file inputs, it's 
possible to change the entire production process, offering great versatility. One of the 
most significant benefits of AM is the reduction in costs and plant space. This is due 
to the lack of a need for specific molds and the flexibility of the process. The high 
degree of customization also means that complex shapes, which may be difficult to 
achieve with traditional manufacturing methods, can be created with ease. 
Additionally, the technology results in less waste of material compared to traditional 
processes, making it more environmentally friendly. Another advantage of AM is that 
it offers a more streamlined production process. Unlike traditional manufacturing 
methods, the production procedures do not change based on the complexity of the 
piece, making it easier to manage the supply chain. Additionally, the process is 
relatively constant, regardless of the size of the production run, which results in cost 
savings on a small scale. In conclusion, Additive Manufacturing offers a range of 
advantages over traditional manufacturing methods, making it an increasingly popular 
choice for many industries. The technology offers high flexibility, reduced costs and 
plant space, and a more streamlined production process, making it a smart investment 
for any company looking to stay ahead of the curve. 
According to the American Society for Testing and Materials standard terminology for 
AM technology, AM can be divided into the following seven categories: (1) vat 
photopolymerization; (2) material jetting; (3) material extrusion; (4) binder jetting; (5) 
powder bed fusion (PBF); (6) sheet lamination; and (7) directed energy deposition 
(DED). Figure 13 summarizes the classification of AM processes and the 
characteristics of each process.

4.2 FDM 
FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling) is a 3D printing technology that involves extruding 
a thermoplastic polymer through a heated nozzle and depositing it on a building stage. 
It is a form of additive manufacturing, which creates objects from 3D model data by 
joining materials layer upon layer. In the process, a plastic filament is fed from a spool 
to the nozzle where it is liquefied and "drawn" onto the platform. As the filament 
touches the build stage, it hardens and is gradually deposited to create the final 3D 
print. The platform then lowers by one layer thickness to allow the printer to start on 
the next layer. FDM can use different materials such as ABS, PLA, Nylon, or exotic 
blends like plastic and wood or carbon. It is often used for non-functional prototypes, 
low-volume production, aerospace, medical anatomical models, and rapid prototyping 
of biomedical microdevices. Home 3D printers typically use FDM and plastic filament. 
However, FDM does not have the same level of accuracy as other technologies like 
Stereolithography, and its quality and resolution depend on various factors such as 
the size of the nozzle, extruder movements, and the bonding force between layers. (F. 
Kunz, 2017) 
FDM technology is widely utilized for various purposes such as creating non-functional 
prototypes, concept parts, functional models, manufacturing tooling, end-use parts, 
and low-volume production prototypes for form, fit, and function testing. It is commonly 
used in the aerospace industry to make wind turbines and anatomical models for 
medical purposes. Additionally, FDM has been making rapid prototyping of biomedical 
micro-devices possible, which are crucial and widely used in hospitals. This

technology is considered cost-effective and safe. (Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM): 
3D printing with filaments, u.d.) 
Regarding the cost of 3D printing technology, one initial concern is the long-term 
expense of materials. However, those who choose to use Fused Deposition Modeling 
(FDM) have a cost advantage as FDM printing machines are among the most 
affordable and cost-effective, particularly for those who wish to use it at home.  
When it comes to precision, Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) 3D prints may not 
reach the same level as those produced using Stereolithography. The quality of the 
print varies depending on the application. Nozzle size plays a major role in determining 
the resolution, but other factors such as the accuracy of the machine's extruder 
movements and the strength of the bond between the layers also affect the outcome. 
However, the weight of the layers may cause lower layers to compress, which could 
negatively impact the final quality of the 3D print. (Tong Cao, 9 Jul 2004)

In the first test session, cooling failures have been experienced, for a variety of

water level caused surface defects and variation in the section as a result of improper 
heat dissipation from the extruded part.

Commercial plastics don't conduct heat well. Additionally, they usually exhibit poor

thermoplastics' weak heat transfer capabilities.  
Therefore, it's crucial to implement proper cooling, to do that could be an idea to

improve the processes over time. 
DIE DROOL PHENOMENON 
An issue that frequently arises during extrusion but whose origins are still unclear is 
the creation of die drool. Die drool formation is the term for deposits surrounding the 
nozzle exit that first seem as a mild soiling but have the potential to spread. The 
likelihood that individual particles will dissolve increases with the size of the die drool 
at the die, which can subsequently dramatically lower the surface quality of the 
extruded product and even result in waste output or process failures. 
 
==PAGE:39==
P a g e  39 | 146 
 
 
 
Figure 27: Die droll formation first experiment. 
Although the basic mechanism for how drool forms is still not entirely known, there are 
numerous causes and cures for this undesirable extrusion phenomena documented 
in accessible literature. They can generally be split into three categories. First, 
adjustments to the design inside or outside of the extrusion die, followed by 
modifications to the polymer material, and last, a much smaller set of modifications to 
the inside or outside of the processing conditions. To avoid the formation of a die drool, 
it is therefore advisable that the molecular chain degradation during processing is kept 
as low as possible. In addition it is helpful to be very careful with the process 
parameters and to avoid high temperatures (also in the history e.g. with recycling 
material) or pressure fluctuations as well as high mechanical forces on the melt. 
Moreover, a good homogeneity of the melt (material and thermal) should be ensured. 
Alternatively the use of additives can help prevent chain degradation. 
(SHS_Extrusion_Training, 2017). Thanks to the recent redesign of the die, the flow of 
polymer has been balanced and as a result, there has been a noticeable reduction in 
die drool formation.

LEAK OF POLYMER 
The last issue that was encountered during the experimentation process, in both of 
session, was related to a polymer leakage that occurred while extruding ABS. in the 
first test session the problem stemmed from an imbalance in the speed of the extrusion 
process, which resulted in a high mass flow that the flange of the melt pot was unable 
to counteract. The resulting pressure caused the polymer to leak out of the system, 
leading to significant disruptions to the experiment.  
Unfortunately, the problem was compounded when several screws failed to hold and 
detached during the extrusion process, preventing the die from being locked properly. 
This malfunction led to further polymer leaks and exacerbated the issue, making it 
even more difficult to control the flow of ABS through the system. 
The polymer leaks caused by these issues had a significant impact on the accuracy 
and reliability of the experimental results. This was particularly problematic because 
the ABS extrusion was a critical part of the experiment, and the data gathered from it 
was essential for achieving the research objectives. The polymer leaks caused 
inconsistencies in the results, making it difficult to draw meaningful conclusions from 
the data.

6 Polymer Extrusion Simulation 
6.1 Introduction 
Extrusion processes are widely used in industries, before the introduction in the use 
of numerical simulation the production was being performed based on empirical 
experience and trial-and-error approaches. For simulation of a physical process, we 
mean the representation by solving equations of a mathematical model with the 
computer. The numerical method is a tool that allows you to achieve results not 
achievable by other means. The use of numerical simulation is becoming increasingly 
important with the increase in computing power available.  
Among them, the finite difference method (FDM) and the finite element method (FEM) 
and the finite volume method (FVM) are the most commonly used. The FDM method 
gives a point-wise approximation to a problem with an array of grid points subdividing 
the geometry along each coordinate axis, while the FEM model gives a piece-wise 
approximation to a problem with an assemblage of elements subdividing the geometry 
along the boundaries. The so-called finite-element mesh is made up of all of these 
basic shapes. 
The finite-volume method (FVM) is similar to the finite-element method in that the CAD 
model is first divided into very small but finite-sized elements of geometrically simple 
shapes. Aside from this, the finite-volume technique differs greatly from the finite-
element method, starting with the notion of elements, which are now known as cells. 
The finite-volume method is based on the fact that many physical laws are 
conservation laws—what goes into one cell on one side needs to leave the same cell 
on another side. Following this idea, you end up with a formulation that consists of flux 
conservation equations defined in an averaged sense over the cells. Historically, this 
method has been very successful in solving fluid flow problems, but COMSOL used 
finite element methods for CFD. (Sjodin, 2016) 
The current state of the art in die design for profile extrusion is based on experience 
and requires time-consuming running-in experiments to be performed for each new 
die. This process can be both costly and time-consuming, and there is a need for a 
more efficient and reliable method for designing dies.  
Nonuniform wall thickness in extrusion can lead to unbalanced flow through different 
sections of the process, resulting in diversion of the flow towards thicker sections due 
to greater resistance in thinner sections. This can have negative impacts on the final 
product's dimensions, surface quality, and mechanical properties, causing increased 
costs and decreased productivity. Nonuniform wall thickness can also pose challenges 
in downstream processing such as welding, painting, and assembly.

To address these issues, the main goal of this study is to find a model that accurately 
represents the extrusion process and balance the mass flow due to experimental 
failure. By achieving this goal, it will be possible to improve productivity, reduce costs, 
and ensure better quality control in downstream processing. Following are some 
literature examples: 
For instance, a study on balancing the flow in each part of the extrusion die by 
adjusting the land length and die entry angle used analytical flow simulations and trial-
and-error methods. (W., 1984) 
A design process for complex profile extrusion dies based on a combination of the 
Flow Analysis Network technique was established through research. This technique 
was used to balance the flow via several channel dies, enabling the creation of a 
channel topology that ensures a consistent flow distribution at the flow channel outlet. 
For all of the examples taken into consideration, the suggested strategy produced 
excellent outcomes. (Hurez, Tanguy, & Blouin, 1983) 
Elgeti (Elgeti, 2012) described a revolutionary design process that consists of two 
steps: systematic design of the initial geometry and numerical optimization of the 
shape of the flow channel. A slit profile and a floor skirting are the examples used in 
their study. Additionally, the study examined the conclusions drawn from two distinct 
rheological models, namely Newtonian fluid and non-Newtonian inelastic fluid 
represented by the Carreau equation. The findings reveal that the Carreau model has 
a considerable impact on the precision of the forecasts. 
This article (An Innovative Method of Die Design and Evaluation of Flow Balance for 
Thermoplastics Extrusion Profiles, 2009) presents an experimental and analytical 
method for flow balancing a U-shaped die profile with nonuniform thicknesses. The 
technique involved putting in place a flow restriction apparatus along the melt flow 
channel. For the purpose of arriving at a preliminary optimal design, a parametric 
research based on the restrictor dimensions was conducted. Fluent software was used 
to do simulations in order to examine the flow velocity at the die exit. In order to achieve 
a desired flow balance, experimental study was then conducted at various restrictor 
positions. 
In the article (Carneiro, 2001) the rheological design of profile extrusion dies is 
proposed using a global technique. This methodology takes into consideration three 
different aspects of post-extrusion phenomena: shrinkage upon cooling, draw-down 
induced by pulling, and swelling after die exit; as well as, flow balancing and flow 
defects caused by maximum allowable stresses, pressure drop, and melt temperature 
increase.

In summary, using simulation software and CFD can provide a more efficient and 
reliable method for designing dies that can produce uniform wall thickness in profile 
extrusion, ultimately leading to improved quality of the final product, increased 
productivity and reduced costs. 
6.2 Material Models 
To effectively model a process, it is crucial to select a simulation tool that can analyze 
the operation in a practical and realistic manner. When it comes to polymer composite 
extrusion, the simulation tool should be able to handle all aspects essential to the 
process. However, it can be challenging to achieve a first-time-right design through 
extrusion simulation alone, prior to physical validation experiments. Fortunately, 
COMSOL Multiphysics provides a powerful and interactive simulation environment for 
modeling and solving a wide range of scientific and engineering problems. With this 
software, it is easy to extend conventional models for one type of physics into 
Multiphysics models that solve coupled physics phenomena simultaneously. This 
capability makes it an ideal choice for polymer extrusion simulations, where multiple 
physics need to be considered. 
The simulation work presented in the following chapters has been developed using 
COMSOL Multiphysics, which offers a high degree of control and customization, and 
an intuitive approach to numerical modeling. It allows users to analyze different 
physics, even in fully coupled problems, such as those encountered in polymer 
extrusion simulations. Furthermore, the software provides many rheological and 
viscoelastic models that are already implemented, along with various customization 
options, which are essential for accurate polymer flow analysis. 
6.2.1 Laminar Flow 
The Laminar Flow interface is a tool used to calculate the velocity and pressure fields 
for the flow of a single-phase fluid in the laminar regime. A flow is considered laminar 
when the Reynolds number is below a specific critical value. However, as the 
Reynolds number increases, disturbances may occur and lead to turbulent flow. The 
critical Reynolds number varies depending on the model, for example, in pipe flow, it 
is around 2000. 
This interface supports analyzing both incompressible and compressible flows at low 
Mach numbers and non-Newtonian fluids. It solves the Navier-Stokes equations for 
momentum conservation and the continuity equation for mass conservation. 
It can be used for both stationary and time-dependent analyses. However, for higher 
Reynolds numbers, the flow becomes time-dependent and three-dimensional, making 
time-dependent studies necessary.

6.2.2 Non-isothermal Laminar Flow 
The Non-isothermal Laminar Flow interface is a powerful tool for simulating the 
complex interaction between heat transfer and fluid flow. It is a combination of the 
Heat Transfer in Fluids and Laminar Flow interfaces, with the added capability of the 
Non-isothermal Flow multiphysics coupling. This allows for the inclusion of flow 
heating in the model and the ability to consider fluid properties that vary with 
temperature. The interface also has the capability to incorporate heat transfer in solids, 
as well as surface-to-surface radiation and radiation in participating media, when used 
in conjunction with the Heat Transfer Module. 
The Non-isothermal Laminar Flow interface is designed to handle flows at low Mach 
numbers, typically less than 0.3, and it is also capable of handling non-Newtonian 
fluids. The Laminar Flow interface includes a Fluid Properties node that is active by 
default on all the interface selections. Additionally, the flow interface domain selection 
can be edited to include solid domains if the model requires it. 
This interface provides a comprehensive and powerful solution for solving complex 
problems involving both heat transfer and fluid flow, enabling engineers and 
researchers to gain a deep understanding of the underlying physics and optimize the 
performance of their designs. 
6.2.3 Heat Transfer in Fluids 
The Heat Transfer in Fluids interface is a powerful tool that allows engineers and 
researchers to model and simulate heat transfer by conduction, convection, and 
radiation in fluid systems. By default, a fluid model is active on all domains, but the 
interface also provides functionality for including other types of domains, such as solid 
domains. 
The temperature equation defined in fluid domains corresponds to the convection-
diffusion equation and can include additional contributions such as heat sources. This 
equation is a fundamental aspect of heat transfer in fluids and is essential for 
understanding and predicting the behavior of complex systems. 
The Heat Transfer in Fluids interface supports a wide range of modeling capabilities, 
including stationary, time-domain, and frequency-domain modeling in all space 
dimensions. This makes it an ideal tool for solving a wide range of heat transfer 
problems, from simple steady-state systems to more complex dynamic systems. 
In addition, the interface allows for the consideration of various boundary conditions 
such as convective, radiative, and conductive heat transfer on the surface of the fluid 
domains. It also allows for the modeling of heat exchange with other domains, such 
as solid domains or surfaces. 
Overall, the Heat Transfer in Fluids interface is a powerful and versatile tool that 
provides a comprehensive solution for solving complex heat transfer problems in fluid

systems. It allows engineers and researchers to gain a deep understanding of the 
underlying physics and optimize the performance of their designs. 
6.2.4 Stationary study 
The Stationary study is a type of analysis that is used when the field variables of a 
system do not change over time. It is a valuable tool for understanding and predicting 
the behavior of systems that are in a steady-state condition. 
One of the primary advantages of using a Stationary study is the ability to compute 
multiple solutions, such as a number of load cases or the nonlinear response to a 
slowly varying load. This allows to gain a deeper understanding of the system's 
behavior and make more informed decisions about design and optimization. 
For example, in the case of a mechanical system, a Stationary study can be used to 
determine the stresses and strains on a structure under different loading conditions. 
In thermal systems, a Stationary study can be used to determine the temperature 
distribution and heat transfer within the system. 
Additionally, the Stationary study can be used in conjunction with other types of 
analysis, such as time-domain or frequency-domain analysis, to gain a more 
comprehensive understanding of the system's behavior. 
Overall, the Stationary study is a powerful and versatile tool that provides valuable 
insights into the behavior of systems that are in a steady-state condition. It allows 
engineers and researchers to gain a deeper understanding of the underlying physics 
and make more informed decisions about design and optimization. 
6.2.5 Share rate 
In extrusion, the term "share rate" refers to the speed at which a material is pushed 
through a die or other type of opening. This is typically expressed in terms of the length 
of the extruded material per unit of time, such as feet per minute or meters per minute. 
The share rate is an important factor in the extrusion process, affecting the quality and 
consistency of the finished product. The share rate can be controlled by adjusting the 
speed of the extrusion machine, the size and shape of the die, and the properties of 
the material being extruded. 
In addition to the impact on dimensions, shape, mechanical properties and surface 
finish, the share rate also affect the output of the extrusion process. A higher share 
rate will increase the production rate, while a lower share rate will decrease it. 
Moreover, the share rate can also have an impact on the energy consumption of the 
extrusion process, a higher share rate will require more energy to push the material 
through the die while a lower share rate will require less energy. 
To summarize, the share rate is a critical parameter in the extrusion process and it's 
crucial to find the optimal share rate that will result in the desired properties of the final 
product while keeping in mind the production rate and energy consumption.

7 Die redesign procedure 
7.1 Literature 
Designing a profile extrusion die involves addressing two main challenges: achieving 
uniform flow distribution and anticipating post-extrusion effects. Literature provides 
practical guidelines and accumulated experience for improved die design, sometimes 
combining empirical rules with analytical calculations to give designers a better 
understanding of the involved phenomena. With the advancement of computational 
fluid dynamics and access to accurate numerical codes, die designers can now utilize 
computers as a powerful design tool. Modeling the flow of molten polymers in extrusion 
dies can now be done accurately using 3D modeling codes, mostly based on finite 
element method. Maintaining a balanced flow is crucial as unbalanced flow leads to 
distorted products with residual stresses. 
When optimizing the flow channel of profile extrusion dies, there are two main 
approaches. Some suggest making changes to the parallel zone cross-section (or die 
land), while others propose leaving it untouched and making modifications in upstream 
regions. Techniques that adjust the parallel zone cross-section have been shown to 
create more robust dies, resulting in flows less affected by variations in process 
conditions or material properties. However, complete redesigns may be necessary, 
which can be a costly and time-consuming process in a competitive market. To reduce 
this trial-and-error cycle, computational fluid dynamics (CFD) is now being utilized, 
though designer experience remains crucial for optimal results. 
Using either designer experience or simulation tools, the process of flow balancing is 
an iterative one. The cost of the preliminary tests and corrections of a profile die may 
be as much as 10–50% of the total cost (Abbas Zolfaghari, An Innovative Method of 
Die Design and Evaluation of Flow Balance for Thermoplastics Extrusion Profiles, 
2009). 
7.2  Parametric design 
Parametric design is a design method in which the design is defined by a set of 
parameters or variables that can be adjusted to explore different design options. In 
this method, the design is defined by a set of mathematical equations or algorithms 
that describe the relationships between the different parameters. These equations can 
then be used to generate different design options based on different input values for 
the parameters. This approach allows for a more efficient and flexible design process, 
as it allows for the exploration of a wide range of design options without the need for 
manual adjustments or rework. It is often used in fields such as architecture, 
engineering, and manufacturing, where complex shapes and forms need to be 
generated or optimized. 
 
==PAGE:52==
P a g e  52 | 146 
 
 
Parametric design allows for the exploration of a wide range of design options based 
on different input values for the parameters. This enables the designer to find the 
optimal design solution for a specific extrusion process. This approach allows for 
automated design changes and updates. This can save time and reduce the need for 
manual adjustments or rework, which can be especially useful for complex shapes 
and forms. It is also possible to optimize a design by varying the input values for the 
parameters and evaluating the results. This can help to improve the performance of 
the die and increase the quality of the final product. Parametric design can be easily 
integrated with other tools such as computational fluid dynamics (CFD) to analyze the 
flow of molten polymers in the extrusion dies, which can provide a better insight on the 
phenomena involved and improve the die design. By allowing for the exploration of 
different design options, parametric design can help to reduce the costs associated 
with trial-and-error design approaches, as well as allowing for more efficient use of 
materials. 
On the other side the parametric design process can be complex and requires a certain 
level of expertise to implement effectively. This can make it difficult for designers who 
are not familiar with the software or the method to use it effectively. The process of 
creating a parametric model can be time-consuming, especially if the design is 
complex. This can increase the overall design time and add to the project schedule. 
(Aung, s.d.) 
The die design includes four distinct components: C1, which is the die land section 
that remains unchanged; C2 and C3, which are the lower and upper halves of the 
transition zone; and C4, which is the final section. The value of C4 is calculated by 
subtracting the sum of C1, C2, and C3 from the total length of the die, ensuring that 
the die remains a constant length of 35mm, even when adjusting one of the 
parameters.

7.3 Use of restriction bar 
A potential solution to improve flow balance in a die design is to implement a restrictor 
bar at a specific location along the flow in the thicker section. This restrictor can be 
designed to impose resistance in order to compensate for the differences in resistance 
at the different sections. By doing this, it can help to balance the flow of material 
through the die. To optimize the design, it is important to determine the correct height 
of the restrictor bar, which can be used as an indicator of the resistance against the 
flow at the middle segment. This will ensure that the restrictor bar is providing the 
necessary resistance to balance the flow and ultimately improve the performance of 
the die. (Abbas Zolfaghari, An Innovative Method of Die Design and Evaluation of Flow 
Balance for Thermoplastics Extrusion Profiles, 2009) 
It is expected that at an appropriate height, a balance of flow will be achievable. To 
attain an optimum design, in addition to the height of the restrictor bar, some other

As mentioned before an important factor in extrusion is the die land, which serves to 
create a uniform cross-section passage that is typically 10 times longer than the 
thickness of the extrudate. This length allows for the relaxation of viscoelastic stresses 
in the melt before it exits the die, reducing die swell and shaping the extrudate. The 
profile of the die land is designed to compensate for extrudate deformation after the 
die (die swell and drawdown) and produce the desired shape downstream. (Design of 
Extrusion Dies) 
The required length of the die land depends not only on the type and temperature of 
the thermoplastic melt, but also on the flow rate. In the entry section of the die, the 
deformation of the melt causes strain, which only gradually decreases over time 
through relaxation. To ensure consistent product dimensions and mechanical 
properties, it is crucial to allow the melt to fully relax before reaching the die. Failure 
to do so can result in variations in product dimensions and properties, especially with 
rapid cooling. (V, 1998)

8.1 Introduction 
Metrology is the scientific study of measurement, is concerned with the application of 
measurement to manufacturing and other processes and their use in society, ensuring 
the suitability of measurement instruments, their calibration and quality control. 
Producing good measurements is important in industry as it has an impact on the value 
and quality of the end product. Thanks to the help of the use of computers for quality 
control is it possible to increase the productivity in the inspection process and reduce 
the lead times in manufacturing, this technique is called computer aided quality control 
or CAQC. One of the techniques that is possible to use is the coordinate measuring 
systems that are divided in cartesian and non-cartesian, which in turn are divided into 
contact and non-contact probing. 
The contact technique is called coordinate measuring machine (CMM) that is the most 
accurate on the market; the structured light, laser triangulation and photogrammetry 
are non-contact probing.

Manufacturing inaccuracies are not only related to the dimensions of the components 
but also to their geometry. Planes are not perfect surfaces just as cylinders are not, 
axes are not straight and the conditions of parallelism or angle are therefore not always 
ensured. The lack of these "certainties" might endanger the correct functioning of the 
assembly, so it is necessary to define geometric tolerances within which correct 
operation remains guaranteed. The industrial repercussions linked to these tolerance 
problems are extremely important: if the pieces don’t assemble correctly or work badly, 
the costs associated with rejecting the part increase, and vice versa if you set too tight 
tolerances manufacturing costs increase. Each type of prescribed tolerance requires 
different measurement processes whose process must be logged and standardized to 
avoid incorrectly measuring the effective tolerance, which would increase even more 
manufacturing costs.

8.1.2 Laser Measuring Microscope 
The OLYMPUS LEXT 3D is a confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM), the 
technology is based on a conventional optical microscope but instead of a lamp, a 
laser beam is focused onto the sample. The intensity of the laser light is adjusted by 
neutral density filters and brought to a set of scanning mirrors that can move them very 
precisely and quickly. One mirror tilts the beam in the X direction, the other in the Y 
direction. The beam is then brought to the back focal plane of the objective lens which 
focuses it onto your sample. This light travels backwards through the same path that 
the laser travels. This light then passes through a semi-transparent mirror which 
reflects it away from the laser and toward the detection system. The first object in the 
detection system is the pinhole aperture, which is in the intermediate image plane of 
the microscope. This allows only a small central portion of the light through to the light 
detectors. If this is fluorescent light, it will be a different color from the laser light, and 
emission filters are used to separate it from laser light that has been reflected from 
your sample. If reflected light is being examined, it will be passed through a polarizer 
that will allow only laser light with a different polarization angle from the initial laser 
light to pass. 
 
==PAGE:64==
P a g e  64 | 146 
 
 
Any light that emerges from the CLSM’s optical system may have a very low intensity, 
and so the photomultiplier tube (PMT) is used to detect and amplify this light signal. 
Photomultipliers are capable of amplifying a faint signal around one million times 
without introducing noise. The output from the PMT is an electrical signal with an 
amplitude that is proportional to the initial light signal. This analog electrical signal is 
converted to a series of digital numbers by an analog to digital converter in the 
computer. As the laser beam moves along the specimen, the detection system 
constantly samples and converts the PMT output and displays them on the computer 
monitor in the correct order. (Bitesizebio, 2014)

diameter, flatness, and cylindricity of a die. To obtain the most accurate data, each of

a 2D line was also created to cross these points.  
Once the component was properly defined and oriented, the measurement strategy 
was applied. The same strategy was used for each measurement, although the 
strategy used for the Prismo was different from the one used for the Duramax for 
diameter measurements.

surface area, which was measured on a CMM by placing the stylus at several probing 
points or following a polyline path defined across the surface. The smaller the 
deviations, the higher the flatness of the object. 
Cylindricity verifies the roundness of cylindrical. CMM checked the cylindricity by 
putting several probing points in the cylindrical form. The smaller the deviations, the 
higher the cylindricity of the object.

standard deviation indicates that the values are more spread out. The reference value

To interpret the results of the t-test, is it necessary to set a significance level (e.g. 0.05)

significant difference between the samples. 
8.4.1 Sa “Surface Average” 
Sa, acronym for "Surface Average", is a surface roughness metric that expresses the 
average deviation of the surface from its mean line (also known as "mean profile"). In 
other words, Sa measures the arithmetic average of the differences between the 
height of the surface and the mean line within a specific area. 
To calculate Sa, the mean line of the surface is determined and the height differences 
between the surface and the mean line are measured at each point within the area of 
interest. Then, the arithmetic mean of these height differences is calculated to obtain 
the value of Sa. 
Sa is a common metric used to characterize surface roughness and is often used in 
conjunction with other metrics such as Rq (root-mean-square roughness) and Ra 
(arithmetic average roughness). (https://www.keyence.eu/, s.d.). The value of Sa 
related to ABS are classified according to the screw speed. For each extruded part the 
Sa was measured in 9 points Figure48 four horizontal, four Vertical and one in the

8.4.2 Sq “Root-Mean-Square Roughness” 
Sq, or "Root-Mean-Square Roughness", is a surface roughness metric that quantifies 
the root-mean-square deviation of the surface height from the mean line over a given 
sampling length. In other words, Sq measures the average roughness of the surface 
over a specific distance. 
To calculate Sq, the surface profile is divided into small intervals or sampling lengths, 
and the height deviations from the mean line are calculated within each interval. These 
deviations are then squared, averaged, and the square root is taken to obtain the root-
mean-square roughness. 
Sq is a commonly used metric for characterizing surface roughness, and it provides 
information about the small-scale variations in the surface. It is often used in 
conjunction with other surface roughness metrics, such as Sa (Surface Average 
Roughness), to provide a more comprehensive description of the surface topography. 
(https://www.keyence.eu/, s.d.)

8.4.3 Sp “Maximum Peak Height” 
Surface roughness Sp is a parameter used to describe the peak-to-valley height of a 
surface profile. It is a measure of the average height difference between the highest 
peaks and the lowest valleys of the surface over a specified length. 
In other words, Sp represents the average vertical distance between the highest point 
and the lowest point within a given sampling length. This parameter is commonly used 
in surface metrology to quantify the surface roughness of machined or finished parts. 
The Sp value is calculated as the arithmetic average of the highest peak-to-valley 
distances measured over the sampling length. This parameter is expressed in the 
same units as the surface profile, such as micrometers or inches. 
The Sp value is useful in characterizing the surface texture of machined parts and 
predicting their performance in various applications. Higher Sp values indicate a 
rougher surface with greater peaks and valleys, while lower Sp values indicate a 
smoother surface with less variation in height. (https://www.keyence.eu/, u.d.)

8.4.4 Sv “Maximum Pit Height” 
Surface roughness Sv is a parameter used to describe the vertical distance between 
the highest peak and the lowest valley of the surface profile over a specific sampling 
length. In other words, Sv represents the average height deviation of the surface 
profile from the mean line over a specified sampling length. It is a more comprehensive 
measure of the surface roughness than some other parameters, such as Ra or Rz, as 
it considers all the height deviations from the mean line, including the high peaks and 
low valleys. 
The Sv value is calculated as the square root of the average of the squared height 
deviations of the surface profile from the mean line over the sampling length. This 
parameter is expressed in the same units as the surface profile, such as micrometers 
or inches. 
The Sv value is useful in characterizing the surface texture of machined parts and 
predicting their performance in various applications, such as friction, wear, and 
corrosion resistance. Higher Sv values indicate a rougher surface with greater height 
deviations from the mean line, while lower Sv values indicate a smoother surface with 
less variation in height. (https://www.keyence.eu/, u.d.)

8.4.5 Sz “Maximum Height of the Profile” 
Sz, or "Maximum Height of the Profile", is a surface roughness metric that measures 
the maximum height of the surface profile within a given sampling length. In other 
words, Sz represents the vertical distance between the highest peak and the lowest 
valley of the surface over a specific distance. 
To calculate Sz, the surface profile is divided into small intervals or sampling lengths, 
and the maximum height difference between the highest peak and the lowest valley 
within each interval is calculated. The maximum value obtained from all the intervals 
is the value of Sz. 
Sz is a useful metric for characterizing the overall roughness of a surface, as it 
provides information about the highest peaks and deepest valleys on the surface. 
However, it does not provide information about the smaller-scale variations in the 
surface, which can be captured by other surface roughness metrics such as Sa 
(Surface Average Roughness). (https://www.keyence.eu/, s.d.)

about 0,262 the highest value is at n=0,6 for PP.  
The only factor that can influence the length is the draw down phenomena, during the 
experimental tests is difficult to control the speed of the puller and the speed of 
extrusion, in order to have the correct value of length this two values of speeds should 
be perfectly set. If the not the length of the extruded part result to be thinner due to a 
stretch caused by the high speed of the puller.

can significantly impact the properties and quality of the resulting product. We carefully 
selected the appropriate speed values. It is worth noting that we used the same speeds

due to other factors. By maintaining consistency in the extrusion speeds, we aimed to 
obtain accurate and reliable data that could be used to make informed decisions about 
the extrusion process. 
However, it was challenging to maintain precise control over the extrusion speed 
during the process, which resulted in some variability in the recorded data. This 
variability made it difficult to obtain highly accurate results that could be relied upon to 
make critical decisions about the extrusion process. Despite this challenge, every

To address this issue and improve the dimensional accuracy of the product, it is 
recommended to either reduce the speed of the puller or, if possible, implement an 
automatic speed control system. This will ensure that the speed of the puller remains 
higher than the extrusion speed and vice versa. This strategy could help prevent any 
unnecessary stretching of the material and minimize the deviation from the desired 
specifications. By ensuring that the product meets the expected dimensions, we can 
enhance its performance, functionality, and overall quality, making it more suitable for 
various applications.

10 Conclusion 
In this thesis, the potential of using additive manufacturing to produce PEEK dies for 
polymer extrusion was investigated. The research objectives included experimental 
testing, flow simulation, and optimization of die geometry for the remaining die design 
MEK03 extruding PP and ABS. Valuable insights into the performance of PEEK dies 
were gained through these investigations. 
The experimental testing section provided us with important data on the performance 
of the MEK03 die in extruding PP and ABS. By varying the screw speed, we were able 
to collect mass flow and die head pressure data that allowed us to evaluate the 
performance of the die. This data was crucial for validating the flow simulation results 
and for developing the optimized die geometry. 
The flow simulation section provided a deeper understanding of the flow behavior of 
the polymer melt in the die. Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) was used to model 
the flow of the polymer melt in the die and simulate the flow characteristics of the 
MEK03 die. The simulation results were validated by comparing them with the 
experimental data, which demonstrated the accuracy of the CFD model in predicting 
the flow behavior of the polymer melt. The flow simulation results also facilitated the 
identification of areas for improvement in the MEK03 die design and the development 
of an optimized die geometry that has the potential to enhance the flow characteristics 
of the die. 
The optimization of die geometry section built upon the flow simulation results to 
develop a new design for the MEK03 die. Modifying the die geometry enhanced the 
flow characteristics of the die. The design was based on the principles of uniform flow 
distribution and reduced pressure drop, both crucial for improving the performance of 
polymer extrusion dies. The optimized die geometry was developed using the flow 
simulation results and further validated through testing, which confirmed its improved 
performance in comparison to the original MEK03 die. 
The die design was also optimized by considering the thermal expansion of the die 
after experimental testing. To ensure accuracy and precision in the extrusion process, 
it was crucial to redesign the die. Therefore, the diameter of the die was increased 
from 55.4mm to 55.8mm, and this modification proved to be highly effective in reducing 
the dilatation after extrusion. Through careful observation and analysis, it was possible 
to observe that the dilatation after extrusion was significantly smaller when compared 
to a smaller diameter 
An additional significant achievement of this thesis was the thorough analysis of 
surface roughness of the extruded parts collected during the experiments. This 
analysis revealed a direct correlation between the surface roughness of the extrudates 
and the screw speed. It was observed that higher screw speeds led to smoother 
==PAGE:107==
P a g e  107 | 146 
 
 
extrudates. This finding is particularly noteworthy, as surface roughness is a critical 
aspect of the quality of extruded products, affecting their functionality, appearance, 
and durability. 
The direct correlation between screw speed and surface roughness highlights the 
importance of controlling the screw speed during the extrusion process. By optimizing 
the screw speed, it is possible to achieve a smoother surface finish for the final 
product. Moreover, a smooth surface is often desirable to minimize friction, reduce 
drag, and improve the appearance of the extruded product. 
The finding that screw speed affects surface roughness could have significant 
implications for the extrusion industry. It suggests that manufacturers must carefully 
consider the impact of screw speed on product quality and incorporate the appropriate 
controls and parameters to optimize surface finish. Overall, the observation of a direct 
correlation between screw speed and surface roughness is an essential contribution 
to the field of polymer extrusion, with the potential to enhance the performance and 
aesthetics of extruded products. 
In conclusion, the reproducibility of the FDM process is a significant achievement that 
has important implications for the field of additive manufacturing. With continued 
research and development, it is likely that we will see further improvements in the 
reproducibility and quality of 3D-printed parts, enabling manufacturers to produce 
highly reliable and functional products at a lower cost.
