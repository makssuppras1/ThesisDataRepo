A B S T R A C T
Individuals spend over 90% of their time indoors and have limited exposure to the outdoors (EPA, 1989).
This shortage of outdoor stimulation has heightened the significance of designing buildings that enhance
indoor health. Yet, the integration of shading systems into building design still requires improvement,
and their effect on human health needs to be more thoroughly understood.
The absence of a tool to compute spectral lighting using BSDF files calls for creating a new simulation
setup utilizing the reliable simulation engine, Radiance. This study proposes a novel workflow that
uses advanced simulation techniques to predict the spectral distribution of shading systems, allowing
for the assessment of their health potential. The tool prioritizes fast calculation and is written in the
flexible Python programming language without needing a graphical user interface. Multi-phase simulation
techniques are employed to evaluate the health potential of different Complex Fenestration Systems (CFS)
using BSDF files to determine the daily illumination of glazing and shading systems.
The tool’s capabilities are demonstrated through a case study at DTU SMART Library, which aims
to assess the impact of various CFSs on circadian entrainment in two selected zones at the library. A
range of commonly used shading systems, including different glazing constructions and coatings, were
selected with variations in parameters such as color, angle, and structure to evaluate their effect on health
potentials.
The study calculated the performance of eleven glazing systems with differing constructions and coatings,
revealing that the two-layer LowE PXN glazing system had the best results. Furthermore, was six different
shading systems analyzed for their effect on health potentials, where an expanded metal type was found
best. Finally, to understand the shading systems’ health potential over the whole space, the ”spatial health
potential” for each system is calculated based on the readvised spatial daylight autonomy (sDA) method
denoted spatial melanopic lux (sML).
The developed tool and the proposed evaluation method can be an entry point for analyzing health
potentials in the regular daylight analysis strategy.

I N T R O D U C T I O N
People spend more than 90 percent of their time indoors with limited access to the outdoors (EPA, 1989).
The lack of outdoor stimuli has increased the importance of designing buildings that promote healthy
indoor environments. However, a healthy indoor environment is a complex subject that consists of many
subjects which, in alignment, create a healthy indoor environment. Therefore understanding how the
dynamics work to create a healthy indoor environment is crucial for sustainable building that promotes
individual human health.
The balance between thermal and visual comfort, in a physical sense, has long been the measurement for a
healthy indoor environment. One clear challenge has been maintaining and controlling indoor conditions
in an ever-changing climate for enhanced human well-being and building performance. Climate change
and uncertain indoor climate in the near future, (Dobrovolszky, 2022), calls for a detailed understanding
of systems.
However, the understanding of the building systems has been done with a major focus on energy efficiency
aspects with a large focus on heating and cooling systems. However, the appropriate use of solar gains
can enhance energy consumption related to cooling. Due to inappropriate daylighting principles, cooling
demands increased by 6.5 % globally in 2021, expecting an increase in electricity demand by as much as
40 percent by 2030 (IEA, 2022).
Moreover, daylight can offset the need for electric light energy, especially in working environments with
high electricity savings potential. Artificial lighting makes up 20 percent of the electricity consumption,
constituting approximately 30 percent of the total energy consumption in commercial buildings.

2.1
H E A LT H P OT E N T I A L S I N B U I L D I N G S
The spectral properties in daylight exceed all other light sources. The full spectrum and the change in the
intensity in the daylight lay the ground for the entertainment of the image-forming (IF) and non-image-
Forming (NIF) effects. The image-forming IF or visual response refers to the eye’s ability to transmit light
and form images on the retina, which creates a visual perception. The non-image-forming effects refer
to the physiological responses to light exposure that do not involve visual perception. These responses
are mediated by non-visual photoreceptors, such as the intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells
(ipRGCs) found in the eye’s retina (Do, 2019). The cells send signals to the brain to help regulate the
body’s internal clock and other physiological processes sensitive to light, such as the sleep-wake cycle.
The ipRGCs cells are most sensitive to the blue part of the spectrum, with wavelengths of light between
480 and 500 nanometers. When exposed to light, is melanopsin, a type of photopigment, activated
inside the ipRGCs, which triggers an action potential. This process helps regulate various physiological
processes, such as the body’s internal clock, performance, and mood. In addition, they also play a role in
adjusting the sensitivity of the retina to different levels of light, which helps to improve visual acuity in
different lighting conditions(Do, 2019).
Imaging effects of shading devices have several assessment methods, Section 2.3, non-imaging assessment
method for shading devices has yet to be defined. However, simulation with NIF responses has enhanced
rapidly in recent years. The n-step simulation methods Section, 2.4.2 and spectral lighting simulation
methods Section 2.4.1, have created the basis for the NIF assessment of spectral glazing. However, there
is not yet a method for calculating NIF responses with shading devices. Therefore, the current thesis is a
novel attempt to introduce NIF assessment methods and calculating methods for better understanding the
health potentials of shading devices.

2.2
S H A D I N G S Y S T E M S
Shading systems devices or techniques are used to control the amount of sunlight and heat that enters a
building through windows or other openings. Various types of shading systems are available, including
exterior shading systems, such as awnings and overhangs, and interior shading systems, such as blinds,
shades, and curtains. Shading systems can be manually operated or automated and controlled by sensors
or a building management system (Kuhn, 2017).
2.2.1
Complex Fenestration Systems classification
Modern buildings often feature some complex fenestration system (CFS) to control the amount of light,
heat, and glare that enters the space, as well as to improve the energy efficiency of the building. A complex
fenestration system is described as a type of window or glazing system that consists of multiple layers or
components that work together to provide various functions (Bueno et al., 2015). The terminology can be
understood as different elements but is often considered as a type of shading device. Shading devices
refer to several products, such as blinds, screens, electrochromic glazing, and overhangs, with different
advantages and disadvantages. Therefore is necessary to make some classifications for the different types
of shading devices.
A method for classifying shading systems is placing a shading device in relation to the glazing. The
shading systems can be external, internal, or between glazings (Tsangrassoulis, 2015)(Kuhn, 2017). The
placement of the shading systems highly affects the transmitted energy, as external shading systems block
direct sunlight from entering the space while internal and between glazing is mainly used to block the
direct light or diffuse it. In relation, can the shading systems also be classified as passive or dynamic,
which refers to the moveability of the shading systems (Kuhn, 2017). Dynamic shading systems use
sensors and control systems to automatically adjust the amount of shading based on the sun’s position, the
time of day, and the weather conditions. Passive shading systems do not use any mechanical or electrical
components. An example is solar control glazing or a fixed overhang.
Another classification of shading systems is based on their angular selectivity, which refers to their ability
to block direct radiation while transmitting diffuse radiation (Tsangrassoulis, 2015). The classification
has three main classes of shading systems. The first classification is the non-selective shading systems,
which do not have angular selectivity and can not block or transmit light at all angles equally—for
example, solid screens. The second classification is the partially-selective shading system. These systems
have some level of angular selectivity and can block or transmit light at different angles to varying
degrees—for example, louvers and perforated screens. The last classification class is the fully-selective
shading systems. These systems have high levels of angular selectivity. They can block or transmit light
at specific angles, allowing it to pass through at others—for example, Venetian blinds and roller shades.

2.3.1
General assessment of shading devices
Shading devices can be assessed based on European Norm (EN), a set of technical specifications and
guidelines created by the European Committee for Standardization (CEN). EN 14501 (DS/EN 14501,
2021), covers the properties and classifications of shading devices and windows concerning thermal
and visual comfort. The standard contains guidelines concerning energy transmittance, secondary heat
transfer, direct solar transmittance, darkening performance, glare control, night privacy, visual contact
with the outside, daylight utilization, and color rendering.
The shading device is assessed by three factors that can affect thermal comfort within the room. (DS/EN
14501, 2021)
• Solar gains: The solar gains represent the amount of solar energy that passes through the glazing or
the shading device. The value is directly proportional to the total solar energy transmittance gtot,
which determines the system’s ability to reduce heat gain.
• Secondary heat gains: The secondary heat gains are the total amount of solar energy transmitted
through the fac¸ade. The secondary heat transfer factor is qi,tot.
• Protection from direct transmission: Protection from direct transmission describes the system’s
ability to protect from direct irradiation. The direct solar transmittance factor is τe,n−n.
The shading device is assessed by six factors that can affect visual comfort within the room. (DS/EN
14501, 2021)
• Darkening Performance: The darkening performance represents the shading device’s ability to
reduce light penetration into the room. The performance is measured by illuminance measured.
Then no light penetrates the shading device.
• Glare Control: Glare is a discomfort caused by light intensity in the field of vision that is greater
than the light intensity to which the eye is adapted. The function between diffuse light transmittance
normal – diffuse (τv,n−di f ) and light transmittance normal – normal (τv,n−n) classifies the glare
control for a shading device.
• Night Privacy: Night privacy is the shading device’s ability in a fully extended state to shield the
occupant from outside view and light. The function between diffuse light transmittance normal –
diffuse (τv,n−di f ) and light transmittance normal – normal (τv,n−n) classifies the night privacy for a
shading device.
• Visual Contact with the Outside: The visual contact with the outside represents the shading
device’s ability to allow outside viewing when fully extended. The function between diffuse light
transmittance normal – diffuse (τv,n−di f ) and light transmittance normal – normal (τv,n−n) classifies
the visual contact for a shading device.
• Daylight Utilization: Daylight utilization represents the shading device’s ability to provide natural
light and reduce the need for artificial lighting. The light transmittance diffuse–hemispherical
(τv,di f −h) classifies the daylight utilization for a shading device.
• Rendering of Color: Rendering of color represents the shading device’s ability to render colors
accurately. The color rendering index Ra is calculated for either the glazing or the glazing and
fenestration.

2.3.2
Dynamic assessment of shading devices
In available simulation tools, we have different possibilities and several advances for dynamic shading
assessments, such as dynamic daylight performance metrics. Daylight performance metrics have evolved
from static metrics, such as Daylight Factor (DF), an illuminance ratio between the interior and exterior,
unshaded to more complex dynamic metrics to asses daylight performance, such as Daylight Autonomy
(DA), Useful Daylight Illuminances (UDI), Continuous Daylight Autonomy (DAcon). The dynamic
daylight performance metrics asses the daylight over a time series of illuminance within the building
(Reinhart et al., 2006). A later proposed method was the Spatially Daylight Autonomy (sDA) which
assessed the daylight based on the area of the zone. The sDA method has been interpreted by different
organizations, such as European Committee for Standardization (CEN) and Leadership in Energy and
Environmental Design (LEED).
The dynamic performance metrics developed allowed shading devices to be included in the assessment.
However, the new matrices meant that the geometry had to be assessed three-dimensionally, which meant
that the space had to be described three-dimensionally. In addition, the shading device should be included
in the geometry, which was achievable through the bidirectional scattering distribution function (BSDF),
further explained in Section 2.3.4. The use of such methods adds to the complexity of the simulation;
hence the simulation time and the processing power needed for the computation will rise. In order to
remedy the most common and validated methods available is a matrix-based method, Section 2.3.3, which
allows the simulation to be done in different phases. Also, this method has the advantage of defining
different resolutions in different phases and gradual development of the simulation workflow for higher
accuracy. For the aforementioned reasons, such methods are suitable for developing a workflow where
the health potentials of the shading systems can be calculated.

daylighting simulation. In this method, dividing the simulation into phases decreases the simulation time.

2.3.4
Bidirectional distribution scattering function (BSDF)
The bidirectional scattering distribution function (BSDF) is a mathematical function that describes how
light is scattered by surfaces (Bartell et al., 1981). It comprises a description of the reflection and
transmitted light, which are described as separate functions. The reflection is described in a BRDF
(bidirectional reflectance distribution function), which stands for the characterization of the surface’s
reflection. The transmitted light is described in BTDF (bidirectional transmittance distribution function),
12
==PAGE:24==
which stands for the characterization transmission of the surface. The concept of a BSDF is to explain
how an incoming ray of light and an outgoing ray of light for all angles of incidence is reflected and
transmitted.
Figure 3: Visualization of BRDF and BTDF. Credit: Wikipedia, 2022
The LBNL BSDF uses the XML file format to encode information. The BSDF file describes the intensity
and direction of light leaving the system for each incident angle. The file includes attribute information
such as manufacture and builds information. Furthermore, the scattering data included, which is the
wavelength data (visible and solar), direction (transmission front, reflection front, transmission back,
reflection back), and the angle basis (Klems full, Klem’s half, Klems Quarter, or tensor tree) (Mcneil,
2015).
The full scattering data contain the front and back transmission and the front and back reflection. The
scattering data blocks for a Full Klems BSDF file contain 145x145 values. The BSDF Full Klems
describes the value for each outgoing angle for the 145 incident angles. Each column in the BSDF
describes a single incidence path where each of the 145 values represents the percentage of incident flux
that leaves through an outgoing patch divided by the solid angle of the patch and the integral of cosine
theta over the patch area.
The Impact of varying resolutions of the BSDF was tested in the (Pedersen et al., 2019) to analyze how
resolution can impact luminance- and illuminance-based daylight simulations. The resolution of the
Klems can be modified to increase or decrease the number of patches. The resolution in the Half and the
Quarter Klems basis denotes 73x73 (5329 values) and 41x41 (1681 values), respectively. Each patch can
be subdivided n times to improve the accuracy. Where for 2x Klems will, each patch be subdivided by 2,
resulting in a 580x580 matrix. For further subdivision, can tensor tree BSDF be considered as the variable
resolution may be more beneficial as not all values might be equally important. The study (Pedersen
et al., 2019) found that the BSDF resolution had a negligible effect on annual climate-based simulations.
Therefore it was concluded that using Klems BSDF is the best solution for combining resolution and
13
==PAGE:25==
time-saving for annual climate based simulations. However, the study also found that in point-in-time
climate-based simulations, there is a noticeable difference in cases where the solar penetration of direct
light is high. In cases of low solar penetration of direct light, the difference is minor. The recommended
BSDF resolution for point-in-time simulations is a tensor tree BSDF with n = 5.

2.4.2
N-Step Algorithm
The lighting and visualization platform Radiance (Ward & Shakespeare, 1998) was validated in (Rup-
pertsberg & Bloj, 2006) for color and luminance accuracy using an N-step algorithm, based on (Yang &
Maloney, 2001), to perform multispectral simulations. The principle of the N-Step algorithm is related
to hyperspectral imaging, where one image is taken for each waveband, resulting in N images for a
scene. Together these N images carry the complete spectral information for each pixel in the image. The
spectrum can be subdivided based on the wavelength step size. If the data is comprised of 5 nm step size
is the maximum subdivision 81. The spectrum is generally divided into N=3,9,27,81 wave bands. The
average value for each waveband and used as the color descriptor. To make N=9 in Radiance is three
images rendered, each accounting for a different part of the spectrum.
The validation study of Radiance (Ruppertsberg & Bloj, 2006) used N-step algorithm with a subdivision
of N = 3,9,27,81 to evaluate color accuracy. The study found that spectral rendering with more than
9-channels led only to minor improvements in accuracy. The same effect for the color differences where
the increasing number of channels did not lead to any noticeable improvement in accuracy. However,
it was concluded that under CIE D65 sky the error in the accuracy decreased quicker than under a
fluorescent sky, meaning that for 9-Channel, the accuracy would only mildly increase if the channels
were increased. Under a fluorescent light will, the number of channels more significantly improve the
simulation accuracy.

energy white light source. However, the light in the physical world is represented as the entire visible
spectrum (380nm – 780nm). The spectral power distribution represents the entire spectrum but has only
been measured in a few locations. More common are the measurements of the CCT, where the spectral

M E T H O D O L O G Y
There is currently no tool for calculating spectral lighting through BSDF files. Therefore, a new simulation
setup that can handle BSDF files is needed. The simulation tool should use the simulation engine Radiance,
as Radiance is a highly validated lighting simulation tool. It was chosen to create the tool directly in
Python to achieve higher flexibility without relying on a visual interface.
Since the code is not dependent on programs other than Radiance, the program can be designed more
specifically to examine a variety of glazing and shading systems quickly. The choice to use the coding
language Python (Van Rossum & Drake Jr, 1995) allows for a more flexible design and the use of the
many packages that have been developed for this programming language. In addition, it will allow
other developers to easily understand the code and help develop the tool, as python is a widely known
programming language.
The primary purpose of the tool is to create a platform to examine circadian lighting through BSDF files
for glazing and shading systems. Furthermore, it is constructed with a particular focus on optimizing the
calculation speed, as the tool is intended to quickly examine the health potentials of many different types
of CFSs. The list below summarizes the objectives of the tool:
• Spectral lighting simulation through BSDF files.
• Creation of BSDF files weighted to RGB.
• Spectral lighting simulation with glazing systems.
• Simulation tool with a focus on calculation speed.
A simulation study is formulated to demonstrate how the tool works and to introduce the possibilities that
the tool provides. In addition, the study will provide a preliminary study of how several different CFS
affects circadian lighting.
In order to show how CFS affects circadian lighting, several different glazing and shading systems were
selected. For glazing systems, different glazing constructions and coatings were investigated. The selected
shading systems represent a varied section of commonly seen shading device solutions on the market.
Furthermore, it was chosen to vary parameters such as color, angle, and structure in order to investigate
how these parameters affect health potentials.
The objectives for the simulation study are:
• Demonstrate the possibilities of the tool.
• Demonstrate how CFSs affect health potentials.

The MBE shows the average error between the base layer glazing and the CFS. A positive MBE indicates

RMSE indicates that the CFS is less accurate relative to the base layer glazing.

6.3
S PAT I A L LY A NA LY Z I N G M E T H O D
Non-spectral daylight calculations are often analyzed spatially. The most used method for analyzing
daylight spatial is EN17037 spatial daylight autonomy (sDA) (DS/EN 17037, 2018). The standard
recommend a minimum of 300 lux in half of the room in half of the daylight hours (DS/EN 17037,
2018).The indoor illuminance is calculated on a horizontal analysis plane at the height of 0.85 m and
with some predetermined material values. In addition, it requires climate data with illuminance for a
63
==PAGE:75==
year. However, the method can still be used if the predetermined rules are changed. Since only four
days have been calculated, it is necessary to find the number of daylight hours for these days, instead of
the whole year. In addition, the predetermined material values are disregarded, and the requirement for
indoor illuminance is changed to 250 M-EDI according to the CIE standard (CIE S 026/E, 2018). The
method for calculating the modified sDA metric will be called spatial melanopic lux (sML).
For the previously calculated values, a vertical analysis plane has been constructed at the height of 1.2 m,
describing the amount of daylight the eye receives. Whereas in sDA, a horizontal plane describes the
daylight level in the room. Therefore, it must be assessed how a horizontal analysis plan in 0.85 m will
work against a vertical in 1.2 m. To do this, sML is calculated for both a vertical and horizontal analysis
plane. The average for the vertical analysis plane in the four orientations is taken. In addition, a shading
schedule of 100 w/m2 is used for the dynamic shadings.
Figure 44a shows the difference between the calculated sML for a vertical and horizontal analysis plane.
The R-squared for linear regression over the data is 0.6017, indicating some correlation between the two
methods. However, the vertical analysis plane yields lower results than the horizontal plane. This is
because there are view orientations that do not receive any light, while for the horizontal analysis plane,
all points will always receive light.
It can be concluded that a horizontal analysis plan can be used. However, attention must be paid to the
fact that the values generally will be higher when using a horizontal analysis plan.

D I S C U S S I O N
The study has focused on two points, developing a tool to calculate spectral light simulations and
demonstrating the tool by studying different CFS types. Each point has been divided into several
objectives described in Section 3. The study will be assessed and discussed based on its objectives’
success and its implementation’s quality.
7.1
S I M U L AT I O N TO O L
The objectives for the simulation tool:
• Spectral lighting simulation through BSDF files.
• Creation of BSDF files weighted to RGB.
• Spectral lighting simulation with glazing systems.
• Simulation tool with a focus on calculation speed.
The created simulation tool was designed solely through Python. The tool was designed with the latest
methods for calculating spectral daylight, where Radiance functions were selected according to purpose.
In addition, freeing the tool from other programs made opportunities for designing the code significantly
more open, which has led to a tool that can quickly adapt to new methods. However, creating a tool from
scratch has also meant that areas of the tool could be advantageously improved. Due to lack of time and a
need for more knowledge, this has not been possible. Since methods for calculating spectral light are
still under development, and the fact that the used daylight calculation engine Radiance is not designed
to calculate spectral light. Especially color in simulations was found difficult since the color element is
usually unimportant in lighting simulations. Therefore, it is advantageous to investigate and develop how
material and sky colors are correctly used in simulations.
The sky model and the sky’s coloring have been discussed frequently within the spectral lightning
community, as there is no model yet or approach that mimics the color of the sky correctly yet. The
majority of weather stations do not measure the spectral power distribution of the sky. The approach
used in this tool was to use the Perez sky model, which is colored with a CIE D illuminant. In addition,
could real CCT measurements also be included to calculate a D illuminant from the measured CCT,
which is only applicable in point-in-time simulations. However, more methods to achieve this have been
developed, so it could be interesting to implement more options to compare the methods in this tool. A
new method that would have been interesting to compare to is the gendaylit, colored sky option (Wienold
et al., 2018), which is embedded in Radiance. The method creates the sky based on sky luminance models,
67
==PAGE:79==
which have been created based on the data collected in Berlin (Diakite & Knoop, 2019), see Section 2.4.3.
The reason the model was omitted was that the model could not calculate a sky under an intermediate sky,
which is determined by Perez’s sky clearness (Perez et al., 1993). However, it could have been interesting
to compare the utilized method and the proposed method as it could possibly have influenced the final
result.
The developed tool allowed spectral light simulations through the BSDF file, which has made it possible
to calculate with CFS in spectral light simulations. A success criterion was to develop a workflow that
could create spectrally weighted BSDF files. It was intended that this process should be part of the tool.
However, no method was found to generate spectral-weighted BSDF files easily. Therefore, the BSDF
files were generated through commonly known methods where the material and glass files included were
spectrally weighted, meaning three BSDF files were made for each CFS type. However, the method to do
so is not verified by real-life measurement, which raises many questions regarding the method’s accuracy.
The method used is based on the N-step method, which splits up the RGB to make it more precise.
However, this has previously only been done for spectral files. Therefore, it can be discussed whether the
method is correct or how it can be verified and, concerning this, how the color information is transferred
and which Radiance parameters should be used for the creation of spectral-weighted BSDF files. They are
all critical questions in legitimizing the method and should investigate further. Nevertheless, all require
more studies to be evaluated, but the method still follows the otherwise verified N-step method to generate
the files used in the creation of the BSDF files. Therefore, the inputs for generating the BSDF files must
be correct, while it is the translation to the BSDF format itself that is most critical. It is therefore how
color is translated correctly from materials to the BSDF file that is the concrete uncertainty. Therefore, it
is assessed that the method can be used for generating BSDF files.
The tool’s inability to calculate the BSDF file means that the user must spend time preparing these files
themselves. The inability means that the tool’s usability is impaired, and only people with an initial
knowledge of BSDF files can use the tool. However, it can be debatable whether people who use the
tool not often also have prior knowledge within Radiance and BSDF generation. Nevertheless, the tool
has become more reserved for users with this knowledge. The original thought was a system that could
also be used by people with a limited knowledge of daylight simulations, but in reality the system at this
early stage is more targeted at the more advanced users. The reason for developing the tool was an idea
to normalize the inclusion of health potentials when assessing the impact of daylight. It will require an
easier understanding and use of the tool. In all development, however, it is often the case that you start
with a tool that is more difficult to access and then work on user friendliness.
The simulation strategies in the workflow are based on Subramaniam, 2017, where the two-phase and the
three-phase method are utilized. However, both methods lack in accurately calculating the sun’s location,
see Section 2.3.3, as they both overestimate the sun. The DDS method can more accurately calculate
the sun’s location. The method is time-consuming but would increase the accuracy of the simulation.
It can be debated whether this will be beneficial since better accuracy is always desired, but not if the
simulation time is significantly increased while the difference in results is minimal. It should therefore be
investigated what implementation of this would do for both parameters. In addition, the tool’s purpose
must also be included in the assessment, which opens up the tool’s future and target group for the tool.
As this helps to assess how vital accuracy is compared to speed. For now the focus have primary been on
calculation speed.
The tool demonstrates a new method for calculating spectral light, but not without the user’s ability
to complement the tool with prior knowledge that enables the user to create some essential input files.
68
==PAGE:80==
However, the tool has still created an opportunity to calculate with BSDF files in spectral simulations in a
time-optimized tool.
7.2
S I M U L AT I O N S T U DY
The objectives for the simulation study:
• Demonstrate the possibilities of the tool.
• Demonstrate how CFS affects health potentials.
In the simulation study, calculations were made for eleven glazing systems with different constructions
and coatings, which showed that the two-layer glazing system, LowE PXN, performed best. This system
also has the highest transmittance indicating that it is an essential factor. Furthermore, the spectral
transmittance for three CLX and SKN couples with the same average transmittance was analyzed. The
analysis showed that the spectral distribution of transmittance affects simulation results as expected.
How high transmittance in the blue it of the spectrum will result in higher M-EDI results. However,
the difference is still small in the study. Therefore, to make a more significant difference, colored glass
should be included. This would have more significantly indicated whether what we see is correct. In
continuation of this, the glazing systems could also be made as BSDF files to see how results are affected
by the two simulation methods. Evaluating the two fenestration methods was not found interesting in
the project description. At the same time, the simulation part of the study has proven to be interesting to
cross-verify the methods.
To assess how the color of the CFS materials affected M-EDI, six different material colors were calculated.
Not as expected, only a slight difference was found between the colors. One of the reasons may be that
the presented results are averaged over all states for the shading device. However, mainly the diffuse part
of the light will be affected by the material color. In contrast, the direct light that stands for the most
significant part of the illumination in the zone is not affected by the material color. Therefore, it could be
investigated how the material color affects the result when the shading device is more closed.
Furthermore, as previously discussed, the M-EDI results are more sensitive to the blue spectrum. The
material chosen due not present a material where the reflectance is mainly in the blue part of the spectrum.
Therefore is hard to distinguish between if the reflectance alone affects the M-EDI in the zone or if the
material’s color has an influence when calculating with BSDFs. This problem was first found in the data
handling, but materials must be selected more specifically in future studies to demonstrate that material
color has an impact. Where material with spectral reflectance, which dominant lies within each part of
the spectrum, should be selected.
In addition, a general assessment of how the N-step method is used to calculate the spectrally weighted
BSDFs must also be further investigated. The method is generally used on spectral files but has not,
before this thesis, been used on BSDFs files. Therefore to validate the method should physical studies
and simulations be conducted.
The distribution shows a significant difference between the CFS types. The CFS types, exterior, interior,
between blinds, and expand, were found to have the highest M-EDI values. While in the study of MBE
and RMSE, where all states of the blinds were included, it was found that expand had the best agreement
with base layer glazing, thus the highest M-EDI values. In addition, MS was also found to have a similar
performance to generic blinds. Based on these results, it could be determined that the ranking of CFS
69
==PAGE:81==
had to be in the following order, expand, exterior blind, interior blind, between blind, MS, blind 1, and
blind 2. A shading schedule was introduced to analyze the CFS dynamically, with three threshold values
50, 100, and 150 w/m2. Considering the blinds as dynamic, these particular CFS options perform best.
However, this required that the threshold value was 50 w/m2 or above. These assessments have only
been based on the criterion that the best shade type is based on the highest M-EDI values. Results are,
however, highly influenced by the analysis points closest to the facade, which imbalance how the room is
assessed as a whole.
Another proposed method uses a modified version of the EN17037 spatial daylight autonomy method
called sML. For this to be possible, a few criteria set up for the EN17037 method had to be changed.
The most significant change was using a horizontal analysis plane, and the threshold value changed to
250 M-EDI. In addition, EN17037 is used for annual simulations, which were not calculated in this
study. In the future, however, it will be advantageous to use annual simulations, as the four days selected
can only provide an estimated estimation of daylight conditions. When comparing the different CFS
types calculated with the sML method, it was found that the difference between static and dynamic CFS
systems was significantly reduced when a shading schedule was used. This also means that the ranking of
CFSs does not change when using a shading schedule 100 w/m2. However, the proposed sML must be
further verified to assess whether the method is sufficient for spectral lighting assessments. In addition,
the changes made to the original EN17037 sDA model should also be further investigated to determine
each parameter’s influence. The proposed sML model gives a good insight into how the zone performs
and can give the zone a concrete score.
In this study, the CFSs were assessed solely on which CFS performed best in relation to M-EDI. However,
M-EDI is only one photoreceptor of five, affecting the NIF responses. It would therefore be advantageous
also to evaluate the other photoreceptors. In addition, to provide a fair assessment of the best CFS,
the analysis must also address other factors, such as visual and thermal comfort, to provide a holistic
assessment of the CFS. The focus of this simulation study was to investigate how shading systems’ health
potential was affected; therefore, visual and thermal comfort were not considered at this stage.
The study showed how the simulation tool could be used. In this study, 622 different solutions have been
calculated for each date, which means 1866 simulations due to calculation in 9 channels. A simulation
time study was set up to give an insight into the simulation speed, which showed how long a simulation
with the tool took and which parameters affected the simulation time. The most critical parameter was
the choice of Radiance parameters, with a significant time difference between low and high Radiance
parameter settings, as expected. The low calculation time in the geometry simulation, shading simulation,
and result handling is a product of the multiprocessing tools implemented. By implementing this, the
tool can utilize the full potential of a computer. However, it can be discussed if multiprocessing is
best, especially for data handling, where it is expected that large quantities of data will flow. Therefore,
threading could be more beneficial as the process might be input/output bound (I/O) when more data is
processed, which means that process speed is determined by wait time for input/output operations.

C O N C L U S I O N
The developed workflow has demonstrated a new method for calculating glazing systems and shading
systems in spectral lighting simulations. As something unique, a method has been proposed to create
spectrally weighted BSDF files that can be used for multi-spectral simulations. Uniquely, a method is
proposed to create spectrally weighted BSDF files that can be used for spectral light simulations. The
method to generate BSDF files was not implemented in the tool, which therefore requires the files to be
created beforehand. In addition, the tool can also calculate for glazing systems that are based on spectral
data.
The design tool has allowed many multi-spectral simulations to proceed quickly in a simple workflow.
Simulation options can easily change to study different parameters concerning health potentials. A
simulation study is conducted to evaluate the developed tool and provides a preliminary study of how
several different CFS affects health potentials.
In the simulation study, eleven glazing systems and seven different CFS were evaluated, three of which
were calculated with six different material colors. The glazing study showed that the LOWE PXN glazing
performed best, which correlates with the glazing systems having the highest transmittance. Furthermore,
the color of the glazing systems was analyzed. How high spectral transmittance in the blue part of the
spectrum was shown to affect the resulting M-EDI.
Six different shading materials were simulated to see if color affected the M-EDI results and then
calculated with BSDFs. The study showed that there was some effect of color. However, the effect was
insignificant to concluding how material color affects health potentials.
The CFS types were evaluated in three ways. First, it was evaluated based on the highest M-EDI, where
all CFS was assumed passive. Secondly, a shading schedule was implemented, allowing CFS to be
evaluated as dynamic. Last, the CFSs were evaluated spatially, where the EN17037 sDA method was
modified to fit spectral simulations. Each method provides a method to evaluate the CFS system for
spectral light, with different advantages and disadvantages. Evaluating a system on the highest M-EDI
alone can be misleading, as average results will significantly influence the entire assessment. However,
it still assesses which CFS type will generally perform best concerning light exposure in the zone. A
shading scheme was created to assess better passive and dynamic CFS types, which meant the dynamic
CFS types could be assessed more fairly to the passive systems. Finally, CFS was assessed with the
sML method created in this study and implemented to understand better how the zone performs with
the different CFS types. In addition, the analysis method can be the cornerstone for creating an analysis
method that people within the daylighting area can easily adopt into their workflow due to its origins.

9.1
V E R I F I C AT I O N
The method used to calculate spectral lighting shall be validated. The method in question is:
• N-Step BSDF generation
• Material color in Radiance
To verify BSDF generation through the N-step method, it is necessary to make some physical tests to
compare the calculated BSDFs. The implementation of material color should be further investigated to
ensure that the spectral data gathered from measurements are correctly inputted in Radiance. This can be
done through extensive literature surveys on the subject and physical testing.
9.2
C O L O R I N R A D I A N C E
Color and how it is perceived and used correctly in simulations is a complex subject. In the development
of the tool, it was important that methods regarding translating measured spectral reflectance to reflectance
values that are perceived correctly in Radiance in terms of correct color representation. The method
chosen was to use the measured spectral reflectance directly in Radiance without any conversion. This
means that after the spectral reflectance was measured, the average for the nine divisions of visible light
was calculated and used to make the materials.
However, there are still uncertainties with this simple method. Although several presentations referred to
using this method, inaccuracies could still be found in whether Radiance perceives the correct color. When
referring to renderings using Radiance, it is generally advised to convert the found spectral reflectance
data to CIE XYZ and then convert this to Radiance RGB. This may not necessarily be the case for
grid-based calculations as other Radiance functions are used here, which weigh the calculated irradiance.
However, more research in the Radiance documentation is needed to ensure that the methods applied are
correct. This verification will be essential to explain whether the fundamental element has been calculated
correctly, as colors impact precisely this type of simulation.
9.3
S K Y M O D E L
The sky model is a complex subject, especially when trying to recreate skies in a virtual environment.
Section 2.4.3 presents several cloud models suitable for spectral daylight calculations. The tool uses
73
==PAGE:85==
Gendaymtx and Gendaylit to create a Perez sky, which is assigned a color triplet calculated from CIE
Illuminant D.
Other sky models could be beneficial to integrate into the tool to allow the user to choose between
different methods to generate a sky. An obvious candidate is the -C function in Gendaylit, which will
generate a colored sky description based on the model from A. Diakite, TU-Berlin. The extension of
Gendaylit to create a colored sky was presented in the 2018 Radiance workshop (Wienold et al., 2018).
Implementation was attempted at the creation of the tool but was omitted as color information is only
generated for overcast skies and clear skies. This meant that the function could only be used if the
clearness index is less than 1.065 or more than 4.5. Therefore, it is necessary to calculate the clearness
index in the early steps of the tool to warn the user that the clearness index is within the limit before the
simulation starts. Otherwise, implementing the Gendaylit function should wait until a new version that
also generates color information for intermediate skies.
9.4
C O D I N G
9.4.1
User-friendliness
The Python-based simulation tools have several parts where user-friendliness could be improved. One of
these areas is the interface which consists of an excel sheet and a txt file. The interface was designed
in this manner due to easy implementation. However, there are some problems with this solution. The
biggest problem is keyword changes in the two files. Then triggering the tool will, the interface file be
searched for inputs on lines with a specific keyword. The given inputs will then trigger several events in
the code. However, if the keywords are changed, the tool will report missing input. Therefore shall, the
user is careful not to delete such keywords. Therefore it could be advantageous to create a new interface
where the user cannot alter the keywords. Furthermore could, drop-down menus also be implemented for
the different simulation settings.
Another element is the creation of geometry and simulation points. In the version in question, this is
up to the user to make and insert correctly. This can easily create problems such as windows not facing
correctly or missing model elements. This could be solved by developing a tool that could write the
geometry and points file for some geometry information. The most common way to create these files is
through Ladybug/Honeybee in Rhino/Grasshopper. Therefore could it be beneficial to create a plugin in
Rhino/Grasshopper that uses some of the functionality that Ladybug/Honeybee provides to create a tool
that could create the geometry and points file for this tool. This would also ensure that the user still gets a
visual element when inspecting the geometry. A geometry tool would also solve the issue of naming and
connecting the surfaces to the material name. In the current version, the user should manually type in the
surface name under the specified materials to ensure the material connects to the surface. By making a
tool that had the function of writing the model, the names could be passed on more intuitively.
9.4.2
Coding valdiation
The Python-based simulation tool must be verified to be usable in a more public or industrial context.
Verification of the tool must be done in several different contexts, such as general code verification to
verification of the methods applied in the tool. Code verification has several elements. First, it must
be ensured that the compiled code in each function delivers the correct output. In order to provide this,
74
==PAGE:86==
several tests must be carried out that demonstrate that the function will report back with an error in the
event of missing or faulty input in a function. This ensures that the output will be correct if the inputs are
within the permitted limits. In addition, the various outputs must be verified for correctness, ensuring that
the code in the function delivers the desired output.
Regarding error and exception handling is a general error handling set up in the main script. Thus, errors
that occur in the individual tasks will be sent back to this error handling and stop the code. However,
expanding the error and exception handling to individual tasks could be advantageous. Thus the individual
code segments could handle the exceptions and errors without triggering a full code stop.
As mentioned earlier, a logging function has also been set up in the code, which logs everything that
takes place in the code at strategic times. This function must be expanded so that logging becomes more
intuitive and more widespread. In this way, the user can find the location of the error. It will also help the
developer to find errors in the code.
9.5
F U RT H E R D E V E L O P M E N T O F T H E TO O L
The developed spectral lighting tool provides a workflow to investigate circadian light in buildings with
complex fenestration surfaces. However, to further help architects, lighting designers, and researchers
examine circadian lighting in buildings, the tool must be expanded so that several elements of daylight
design can be examined through the same tool. Therefore, several improvements and new methods are
proposed to strengthen the tool.
• NVRD-model
• Python package Frads
• Daylight calculation
NVRD-model
For the annual or sub-annual simulation, could it be beneficial to implement the Non-visual direct-
response model (nvRD). The model outputs altering responses representing the potential of a space to
induce alertness in the occupants. The model takes into consideration past exposure which directly affects
the current response. It could therefore be beneficial to implement this in the annual simulation. However,
this was implemented in this development process as the tool was mainly designed to calculate office
buildings where past exposure cannot be trusted, as occupants will not be in the zone after working hours.
Radiance function replacement
The tool uses the 3-Phase and 2-Phase methods to calculate daylight in Radiance as described in
Subramaniam, 2017. This method uses the Dctimestep function at the end of each calculation to add the
calculated matrices together. Although this calculation is not particularly time-consuming, it could be
a valuable place to improve the code—an article written by Nathaniel L. Jones (Jones, 2022) presents
a new Python-based tool called Frads to merge matrices through the Python package Numpy. It was
demonstrated that a two-order-of-magnitude speedup in the matrix multiplication step could be achieved
by using NumPy vectorization instead of native Radiance functions. In addition to the immediate
75
==PAGE:87==
improvement in calculation speed, the data will also be loaded into Python during this process, which
will mean that the data will not have to be loaded later. Therefore, will the improvement affect the result
calculation time and the matrix multiplication.
Daylight calculations
In many aspects, the calculation method to calculate spectral lighting is the same as in standard daylight
calculation. Therefore could, daylight calculation also be achieved through the tool with only minor
changes. Furthermore, glare could also be implemented if the image-based simulation was implemented.
