Abstract  
 
The purpose of this project was to use a network analysis approach to study complex human 
diseases using genomics and transcriptomics data with the goal of detecting biological networks 
that are regulated different in diseased individuals compared to healthy individuals. The diseases 
selected for analysis were Crohnãs disease, which is an inflammatory bowel disease, and two 
neuropsychiatric diseases, which were schizophrenia and depression defined as Major Depressive 
Disorder.  
 
The network analysis was carried out using Intomicsã network analysis pipeline together with 
Intomicsã command line and web programs. Each data set was analysed separately followed by all 
data sets from each disease being integrated and analysed in order to detect perturbated biological 
networks across the data sets. 
 
Several biological networks were identified as being regulated differently in diseased individuals 
compared to healthy individuals. The most successful results were obtained from Crohnãs disease 
data sets. Here several signalling pathways were identified, which regulate biology that is 
implicated in Crohnãs disease. These were the JAK-STAT signalling pathway, the MAPK cascade 
and signalling through NFkB transcription factors.  
 
The results from the depression data sets identified the following signalling pathways as being 
perturbated in depressed individuals compared to healthy individuals: The notch signalling 
pathway, signalling through the NFkB transcription factors and G-protein coupled receptors. 
Compared to the evidence which connected the signalling pathways identified among Crohnãs 
disease perturbated networks to Crohnãs disease, there was less evidence supporting the implication 
of the signalling pathways identified among the depressed perturbated networks with depression 
defined as Major Depressive Disorder.  
 
The least successful results were obtained from the schizophrenia data sets, which could be due to 
several factors.

Introduction 
 
The vast majority of human disease burden is made up of complex diseases, which are caused by a 
combination of genetic, environmental and lifestyle factors. Despite massive research in complex 
diseases, the precise aetiology of many still remains vaguely known. Improvements in analysis 
methodologies in biological research can aid the study of complex diseases. Emphasis is put on 
systems biology network analysis methods to help the study of complex diseases. Systems biology 
is a more holistic approach to studying biology that views it not as single entities but as complex 
systems made up of biological networks. Disease is viewed as perturbations in the networks and by 
analysing the perturbations in several systems and integrating the results it is possible to infer a 
deeper understanding of the biology involved in the disease.  
 
In this project the systems biology network approach is tested on three complex diseases. These 
disease are Crohnãs disease, which is an inflammatory bowel disease, depression which is a mood 
disorder and schizophrenia which is a neurodevelopmental disease. For each disease 4-5 data sets, 
are obtained through online databases. Each data set is first analysed independently, and then all 
data sets belonging to one disease are then integrated and analysed, as to detect perturbated 
networks across all the datasets. The biology associated with the perturbated networks are then 
compared to what is already known in the literature as to validate the results from the network 
analysis. To help identify networks that are more likely to be involved in the diseases, the networks 
are combined with information of well-known disease associated proteins identified through text-
mining.   
Scope of the project  
The scope of this project is to conduct a network biology based study of transcriptomics and 
potentially other types of omics type data from case/control studies of self-chosen human diseases 
with the aim of identifying biological systems that are differentially regulated in healthy vs. 
diseased individuals.

Theory 
 
Complex diseases 
Human disease can occur due to many different causes: they can be caused by exogenous factors 
like microorganisms or intake of poisonous substances, or they can be caused by endogenous 
factors like genetic mutations. In a simple model of human disease, there is one specific factor that 
results in the disease. This factor can therefore easily be identified as it will be present in all 
diseased individuals but would be absent from all non-diseased individuals. Many diseases caused 
by exogenous factors fall under this simple model of human disease. The factorãs disease-causing 
ability can be experimentally proven by the induction of disease upon giving the exogenous factor 
to healthy individuals. Some diseases caused by endogenous factors fall under this category, these 
are termed single-gene or monogenic diseases and although rare, they are associated with a heavy 
loss of life1. Monogenic diseases are easy to identify as they follow standard Mendelian patterns of 
inheritance2.  
 
A vast majority of human genetic diseases are not monogenic but instead polygenic, meaning they 
are caused by mutations in more than one gene. Polygenic diseases are caused by a number of 
independently acting or interacting mutated genes. The more genes influencing a polygenic disease, 
the smaller the effect of each gene becomes3. The smaller the effect of a gene is on a disease, the 
harder it is to identify, which has to be proven problematic in the study of polygenic diseases4.  
 
Polygenic diseases can be observed to aggregate in families, but they do not follow standard 
Mendelian patterns of inheritance5. There is also not always a direct correlation between having 
genes associated with a polygenic disease and developing said disease, in some cases the 
development of the disease further depends on environmental and lifestyle factors6. These 
polygenic diseases that further depend on environmental factors are termed complex diseases and 
they make up the vast majority of human disease burden with some examples being: cardiovascular 
disease, asthma, diabetes, cancers, and psychiatric diseases7.     
 
Despite massive investment and funding of research into complex 
diseases, the aetiology of many of these still remains vaguely 
known7. This is due to several factors that contribute to the 
complexity of determining factors influencing complex diseases:

All of these above-mentioned factors make the isolation and characterization of genes and genetic 
mutations that influence complex diseases especially difficult, as the contribution of single genes to 
the disease may be confounded or obscured. There have been problems with genetic associations 
studies not being able to be replicated in a new cohort or subsequent studies on the same complex 
disease implicating different genetic loci and/or environmental factors7. Improvements in high 
throughput sequencing techniques and in the field of genome-wide association studies (GWAS) 
have made some important discoveries regarding complex diseases. It has been discovered that 
complex diseases are driven mainly by an accumulation of weak effects on key genes and 
regulatory pathways. Furthermore it has been discovered that complex diseases are largely driven 
by variations in the noncoding regions that is assumed 
to affect gene regulation, where monogenic diseases are 
usually caused by mutations in the protein coding 
region9.  
 
Solely identifying disease-associated genes and loci has 
its shortcomings, in part due to the above-mentioned 
factors, but also because it relies solely on the statistical 
significance of the association and leaves out the 
biology involved in the disease. Genes and their protein 
products rarely work on their own, instead they work 
together to form complex interaction networks that 
maintains a specific biological function. These 
networks are fairly robust to perturbations in inputs, but 
perturbations in one or more genes can be propagated 
throughout the network via the interactions of the 
perturbated gene products with other gene products in 
the network10. This could be the answer as to why 
different genetic mutations can lead to the same disease 
type, because the genetic mutations affect genes whose 
products participate in the same network and perturbations in any of the networkãs components will 
lead to a dysregulation of the system thereby affecting the same biology4.  
Systems biology 
At the turn of the century biological research underwent a paradigm shift caused by the 
technological developments in the field itself and those in the field of computer science. Before the 
shift biological researchers mainly took a reductionist approach to studying biological systems, 
where the systems were reduced to their minimum components with each component being studied 
separately. The reductionist approach was based on the circumstance, that biology was in the past 
mainly a low-throughput science, where data was mostly qualitative and not quantitative, thus could 
be interpreted by humans without the need for complicated mathematical techniques or 
computational power11.  
 
The reductionist approach has its limitations, which are best exemplified in the radio analogy 
presented in the paper ãCan a biologist fix a radio?ã by Yuri Lazbenik12, where the reductionist 
approach is applied to studying a broken radio. As it turns out, studying a complex electronical 
system by solely looking at its single parts and what happens when these single parts are 
ãremovedã, does in the end not give much information about the system ã just as studying single 
significantly disease-associated genes/loci does not confer much information about the underlying

Systems biology offers a more holistic approach to studying biology than the reductionist approach. 
Instead of studying single components, systems biology views biology as an information science 
and looks at biology as a result of group interactions between components in a biological 
system11,13. This view of biology came in part with the finalization of the Human Genome Project, 
which made way for biology to be viewed more as an informational science. The information stored 
in biological systems can be viewed as a hierarchical layering of information. As an example, a cell 
has a bottom layer which is its genome, which is a sort of code. On top of the genome lies the 
informational layer which represents the process of going from the genome to RNA (often denoted 
the transcriptome). On top of the transcriptome is the proteome, which is the information stored in 
all the proteins that are present in a cell. Systems biology views each of these informational layers 
and other types of biological informational layers, see Table 1 for more information, as containing 
part of the full information regarding a biological system. By analysing each layer and integrating 
them with one another one can gain a full understanding of the biological system14, this approach is 
exemplified in Figure 5. The study of individual layers of information are defined by the suffix 
omics, so the study of the genome is in the field of genomics and the study of the transcriptome is in 
the field of transcriptomics and so on, the integration of several omics data is known as multi-
omics.

A typical systems biology approach in any omics field usually goes over the following steps: 
perturbate a system, monitor said system, analyse the result from the monitoring and formulate a 
hypothesis or model that describes the system and the response to the perturbations15. Contrary to 
the reductionist approach where an initial hypothesis is formed and then tested, the systemsã 
biology approach generates a hypothesis based on data analysis. In biology the system being 
analysed is most often cells and the perturbation could be a gene-knockout, an environmental 
stimulus, or a disease. The cellular response to the perturbation is identified based on a comparison 
of the results of the perturbated system with a control system, that has not been perturbated. The 
monitoring methods depends on the omics field. A genome-wide association study is a widely used 
approach in genomics to detect disease associated loci. The data for a GWAS is obtained using 
either whole genome sequencing or more often Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP) arrays, as 
these are much cheaper. In transcriptomics data is obtained using either microarrays or RNA 
sequencing, and the goal is to detect differences in mRNA levels between the perturbated system 
and the control system. To infer the significance of the difference between the two systems various 
statistical test can be applied, the choice depending on the analysis methods that the researchers 
employ16.

This too has its limitations, as it does not confer a deeper biological understanding of how the 
differences observed are related to the disease. One approach to infer a deeper biological meaning

consequence of perturbations in biological networks. All processes taking place within cells are 
thought to be network-based, these networks being groups of genes, gene products or metabolites 
that are functionally coordinated, co-regulated or physically interacting17. Protein interaction 
networks derived from high-throughput protein-protein interaction analyses have proven especially 
useful in analysing the underlying biology of complex diseases. The use of protein-protein 
interaction networks is based on the theory of ãguilt by associationã, meaning that it is believed that 
proteins which interact share a common molecular function or biological process18.  
 
A network model consists of nodes connected to other nodes via edges as exemplified in Figure 6. 
In the case of protein-protein interaction networks the nodes represents proteins and the edges 
represents a physical interaction19. The information regarding the interaction between proteins is 
derived from high-throughput analyses usually performed under a single experimental condition, 
which is available on online databases. Overlaying the network models with the list of components 
that differ significantly in the perturbated system (this will require to uphold the genomic and/or 
transcriptomic data to proteomic level), it is possible to view if the components directly interact 
with each other or are present in the vicinity of each other on a protein level17 as also exemplified in 
Figure 6. When studying diseases, it can then be useful to further overlay the system with known 
proteins or genes associated with the disease being investigated, as it will be possible to view if the 
perturbated components are interacting with the disease-associated ones and thus also be involved 
in the disease-biology by the ãguilt by associationã theory.  
 
Several methods exist to gain knowledge of the biology underlying a network, most commonly used 
are pathway analysis and gene ontology (GO) analysis methods. Pathway analysis use knowledge 
stored in pathway databases and when combined with the list of components that differ in the 
perturbated system it is possible to gain knowledge about which pathways are affected by the 
perturbation20. GO analysis on the other hand makes use of the Gene Ontology project, which is a 
collaboration among model organism databases to create a well-defined and structured vocabulary 
to describe biological processes, molecular functions and cellular components of gene products in a 
language that can be read by both computers and humans. Through functional profiling it is then 
possible to determine which processes e.g.  pathways or GO terms are present significantly more in 
the list of components or in the networks being investigated. The easiest way to determine the 
significant pathways or GO terms is to calculate the enrichment of each pathway or GO term. This 
is done by calculating the fraction of observed components in the list or network that is associated 
with the pathway or the GO term versus what is expected. The expected is calculated as the fraction 
of all proteins that are associated with the pathway or GO term in the network based on the fraction 
of all proteins that associated with the pathway or GO term in the cell. The enrichment is then 
further backed up by an appropriate statistical test21.

Crohnãs disease 
Crohnãs disease is a chronic inflammatory disease of the gastrointestinal tract characterised by 
periods of remission alternating with periods of recurrence22. It is a progressive disease, that often 
begins gradually and then becomes worse over time23. All parts of the gastrointestinal tract from 
mouth to anus can become affected by the disease, but it most often manifests itself in the terminal 
ileum and colon. The location of the disease tends to be stable over time, but the behaviour of the 
disease often changes22. Crohnãs disease together with ulcerative colitis and other inflammatory 
bowels diseases are grouped together under the umbrella term Inflammatory Bowel Diseases (IBD), 
as they share many of the same symptoms and manifestations.  
 
The incidence of Crohnãs disease varies a lot regarding to geographic area with rates between 0.1 
and 16 per 100,000 inhabitants. The highest incidence is found in Northern and Western Europe, 
North America and Oceania and the lowest rates are found in Africa, South America and Asia24. 
The highest prevalence of Crohnãs disease is also found in also found in Europe and North America 
with 322 and 319 per 100,000 inhabitants respectively25. Generally there seems to be a correlation 
between a countryãs development and an increase in Crohnãs disease prevalence22.  
 
The most common symptoms of the disease are22,23:  
- 
Diarrhoea 
- 
Cramping and pain in the abdomen area 
- 
Weight loss 
- 
Fatigue 
- 
Anorexia  
 
Other symptoms include: anaemia, fever and inflammation in other areas of the body like joints, 
eyes and skin23.  
 
Crohnãs disease can lead to serious complications that may require surgery and/or more intervening 
treatments and can ultimately be fatal. These complications include23:  
- 
Intestinal obstruction i.e. blockage of either the small intestines or the large intestines 
- 
Fistulas i.e. abnormal passages between two organs 
- 
Abscesses i.e. painful, swollen, pus-filled pockets of infection  
- 
Ulcers i.e. inflamed areas that can occur anywhere along the digestive tract. 
 
Diagnosing Crohnãs disease is complicated as presenting symptoms can be heterogenous and 
insidious, depending on the precise disease location in the gastrointestinal tract and the severity of 
inflammation22. The diagnosis is based on a combination of a physical exam, diagnostic tests e.g. 
blood test and examination of stool sample, intestinal endoscopy and a histological examination of a 
biopsy of the affected area ã emphasis is put on the results of the endoscopy23. There might also be 
performed other tests to rule out other diseases that present similar symptoms like ulcerative colitis 
or colon cancer22,23.  
 
The leading theory regarding the cause of Crohnãs disease is, that a complex interplay of genetic 
and environmental factors together with an altered gut microbiome results in a dysregulated innate 
and adaptive immune response that is directed towards the intestines22,26. 
 
Several environmental factors have been assumed to contribute to the disease risk, but for many of 
these a causative association remains to be proven. The best studied environmental factor, which is 
==PAGE:14==
 
14 
known to contribute to disease risk is cigarette smoking, which is associated with a 2-fold increase 
in risk22. Environmental factors are however not equally contributing to disease risk across 
populations e.g. the prevalence in Asia and Africa is lower than the prevalence in Europe, despite 
Africa and Asia having higher smoking rates than Europe. This instead correlate with another 
known risk factor, which is living in an urban area24. 
 
Alterations in the faecal microbiota have been reported in many patients suffering from Crohnãs 
disease. Different studies have pointed towards different bacteria classes influencing the disease, 
but overall there is a reduced diversity in the gut microbiota of disease individuals, compared to 
healthy individuals22,24,27.No single specific microbe have been found to be significantly associated 
with the disease28. The role of the altered gut microbiome in Crohnãs disease is further supported by 
the finding, that some of the genes e.g. Interleukin 23 receptor (IL23R) associated with the disease 
play a role in bacterial sensing, innate immunity and T helper 17 (Th17) cell regulation22.  
 
The immune system processes that are most strongly believed to be associated with Crohnãs disease 
are: defective bacterial killing, innate immunity, autophagy and de-regulated adaptive immune 
responses especially the IL-23 and T helper 17 Th17 cell pathway22,29.  
 
Findings suggest that genetics play a significant role in the development of Crohnãs disease, as up 
to 15% of patients report a family history of the disease and for monozygotic twins this is around 
20-50%. Several genes and loci have been found to be significantly associated with the disease, but 
so far only three genes: NOD2, IL23R and ATG16L1 have been found to have a very strong 
association that has been proven in several studies24,27.  
 
There is currently no cure for Crohnãs disease and no single treatment that works for everybody 
suffering from Crohnãs disease. Current treatment aims at decreasing the inflammation of affected 
areas, prevent eventual flare-ups and keep patients in remission23. Often a combination of different 
types of medicine will be used, these include among others23. In some cases surgery might be 
needed to treat certain complications like fistulas and intestinal obstructions23. 
 
Major Depressive Disorder 
Depression is a complicated mood disorder30, that is estimated to affect more than 264 mio. people 
around the world31, thus regarded as a major global cause of disease burden and human suffering. It 
affects both physical and mental health and can ultimately lead to the death of the patient by 
suicide30. Depression can be graded based on severity and categorised based on whether it arises 
due to specific circumstances e.g. postpartum depression and also whether it is a recurrent or a 
single episode32,33. 
 
The focus of this study has been on the form of depression known as Major Depressive Disorder 
(MDD) specified under the codes F33.0 ã F33.9 in the International Classification of Diseases 
version 10 (ICD-10), where it also goes under the name Recurrent Depressive Disorder33. In the 
other widely used manual for diagnosing psychological diseases, Diagnostic and Statistical Manual 
of Mental Disorders version 5 (DSM-5) it is specified under the codes 296.20 ã 296.36.  
 
Diagnosis of MDD is currently based on clinical examination and a subjective evaluation of 
symptoms by the psychiatrist following guidelines from one of two main diagnostic systems: ICD-
10 or DSM-534. Central for both diagnostic systems are, that a number of symptoms should be

15 
present for at least 2 weeks and that the patient has experienced repeated episodes of depression. 
These symptoms fall under the following broader categories30,32,33. 
 
- 
Depressed mood 
- 
Abnormal judgements about oneself 
- 
Neurovegetative dysfunction (appetite and/or sleep disturbances, fatigue and inattention) 
- 
Cognitive dissonance (feelings of guilt and worthlessness) 
-

- 
Elevated suicide and self-harm risk  
 
Not everyone who is depressed experiences every single symptom, some people will only 
experience a few symptoms while others may experience many32. In some cases, the symptoms may 
be masked by added features such as irritability, excessive consumption of alcohol, attention-
seeking behaviour, hypochondriacal preoccupations and an increase in severity of pre-existing 
phobic or obsessional symptoms33. The prevalence of comorbid diseases with depression is high 
and the most common mental diseases to comorbid with depression are anxiety, personality 
disorders, obsessive-compulsory disorder and substance-abuse disorders35.  
 
Depression results from a complex interaction of genetic, biological, environmental, and 
psychological factors31,32. No single cause is known, but there exist several theories all which have 
both supporting and contradicting evidence36. The lack of definite aetiology could likely be due to 
the heterogeneity of the disease37.  
 
Twin studies have revealed that genetics account for around 40% of disease heritability and several 
loci have been identified which are significantly associated with MDD through GWAS studies38. 
Newer research has also pointed towards epigenetic mechanisms like miRNA that may influence 
MDD by inducing alterations in the expression of genes39.  
 
A simple, but popular theory regarding the aetiology of MDD is that it arises due to a chemical 
imbalance in the brain37, especially the lack of monoamine neurotransmitters like serotonin, 
norepinephrine and dopamine have been presented as a likely cause36.  Another popular theory 
implicates the neuroprotective protein Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF) in the aetiology 
of MDD. The protein regulates neuronal survival, growth, and differentiation. It is further 
associated with mood, and findings have showed that BDNF levels are reduced in serum, the 
hippocampus and the prefrontal cortex of MDD patients36,37. Dysregulation of the Hypothalamic-
Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) axis is also a popular theory and comes from the observation that there is a 
correlation between stressful events and the development of MDD. The HPA axis covers a complex 
set of interactions between the hypothalamus, the pituitary gland and the adrenal glands ã it is 
considered a major neuroendocrine system that controls reactions to stress and regulates many other 
processes like the immune system, mood and emotions36,37.  
 
Several environmental and psychological factors have also been implicated in the cause of MDD, 
some examples being36:  
 
- 
A family history of depression, suicide, or substance abuse. 
- 
Low socioeconomic status  
- 
Chronic medical conditions or disabilities 
- 
Stressful life events 
==PAGE:16==
 
16 
- 
Presence of other psychiatric conditions e.g. anxiety 
- 
Hormonal changes e.g. pregnancy, intake of p-pills40, menopause, and andropause  
 
MDD is treatable and the earlier the treatment commences the more effective it is. It can be treated 
with medication, psychotherapy, a combination of the two or if none of the two work, then 
electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) can be an option32,36. The heterogenous nature of MDD is also 
reflected in the treatment of it ã as there is no recommended first-line treatment, patients should in 
collaboration with their physician and/or psychologist find the treatment or combination of 
treatments that works the best for them32.  
Schizophrenia  
Schizophrenia is a severe complex mental disorder that usually manifest itself in the second or third 
decade of life41,42. It is associated with considerable morbidity, mortality as well as disability, and 
individuals suffering from schizophrenia are in many places subjected to stigma and 
discrimination41,43. Schizophrenia is a chronic disease but can be treated well enough so that 
affected individuals are able to live a normal life41,42.  
 
Schizophrenia is a highly heterogenous disease and there exist no single defining symptom44. 
Diagnosis is made by a medical professional according to criteria stated in either the ICD-10 or the 
DSM-5 manuals. Central for the disease is a profound disruption in thinking that affects language, 
perception and the sense of self41. Further symptoms include, but are not limited to: hallucinations, 
delusions, abnormal behaviour, disorganised speech and disturbances of emotions41,42.  
 
The aetiology of schizophrenia is not well understood, but it is believed that the disease is caused 
by complex interactions of genetic and environmental factors45ã47.  
 
Environmental factors that pose a well-known risk for developing schizophrenia is complications 
during pregnancy. These complications include: fetal malnutrition, premature birth, hypoxic-
ischemic events and maternal infections during pregnancy and/or birth. Other environmental factors 
include childhood and/or adulthood trauma, advanced parental age, migration, growing up in an 
urban area and cannabis and substance use46.  
 
Several genomics studies have added to the growing list of genetic factors associated with 
schizophrenia43ã45,47,48. These genetic factors can be divided in three broad classes: SNPs, rare 
highly penetrant copy number variations (CNV), and the last and newest class is: microRNA47,48. 
The alterations are found in the mechanisms regulating brain neurodevelopment and 
neuroplasticity, dopamine and glutamate neurotransmission, neuronal myelination, the immune 
response system, cell adhesion and G-protein coupled or cAMP-mediated signalling45.  
 
No environmental or genetic factor has been found, that by itself is either necessary or sufficient for 
the development of schizophrenia43,46. 
 
Treatment for schizophrenia remains symptomatic, as the underlying cause remains unknown42,44. 
The first line of treatment consists of antipsychotics. There exist two generations of antipsychotics 
that differ in their mode of action. The majority of the first-generation antipsychotics acts as 
dopamine D2 receptor inhibitors, while second generation acts by additional blockage of the 
serotonin 5HT2A receptor44. Doctors and patients work together to determine which type of 
antipsychotics and which dosage works the best for the patient42.

Methods 
Introduction  
This project is based on large quantities of data generated by high-througput techniques and made 
available online by the researcher who produced it. This means that there are potential sources of 
error that are outside the power of this project. The researchers who produced the data are entrusted 
with having done their best and followed common standard of operation practices when generating 
the data, performing quality control measures and having made available all needed information 
regarding the data. 
 
In the following sections the methods used in the various steps in the overall analysis are described. 
For each of the initial data analysis steps and the network analysis then each method is first 
introduced, the programs used are presented and any consideration regarding the data is noted. The 
workflow for the initial data analysis steps that prepare the data for the network analysis and the 
network analysis itself are illustrated in Figure 7. 
Text-mining 
In order to gain an overview over proteins and genes that are already known to be associated with 
the three diseases an initial text-mining analysis was performed using the online tool InBio KnowTM 
developed by Intomics. InBio KnowTM searches the literature for all instances of pairwise 
associations between all diseases, all proteins/genes and all anatomical entities and computes a p-
value for how often a pairwise association is found in the literature.  
 
In this project all pairwise associations between the three diseases and all genes/proteins were 
searched and associations with a p-value below 1 ÞË 10%&' were selected as being significant and 
added to the list of known associations. These lists were then used later in the network analysis, as 
benchmark list, when integrating all data sets within each disease. When doing the final part of the 
network analysis, the lists were then used to identify networks containing known disease-associated 
genes/proteins. 
Data research 
For this study it was decided to use genetic data and expression data.  
 
A literature search was performed to find suitable genomics data, the focus being on GWAS meta-
analysis studies as to increase the sample size. Expression data was found searching the two online 
databases:  
 
- 
ArrayExpress, which is an archive of functional genomics data from microarray and 
sequencing platforms50. 
- 
Gene Expression Omnibus, which is a public functional genomics repository for array- and 
sequence-based data51.  
 
An overview of the data sets included in this study can be found in Table 2. Appendix I shows an 
overview of the extraction and sequencing methodologies used to produce the expression data. 
 
The selection of data was based on the following criteria:  
==PAGE:19==

1. The data sets had to include samples from both diseased individuals and healthy individuals 
and the overall composition of these two groups should ideally be quite similar with regard 
to sex, age and race as to avoid population stratification.  
 
2. Regarding expression data, the tissue sampled from should be relevant for the disease, 
preferably a biopsy of the diseased organ or other disease-relevant organs. 
 
3. The sample size of the data set should preferably be as high as possible as to increase the 
statistical power of the analysis.  
 
4. Lastly the data sets were evaluated based on their age. Optimally the data had to be 
produced within the last 5-7 years so the methodologies used werenãt outdated and could be 
used in the analysis pipeline. Older data sets were included if they scored well on other 
parameters.

GWAS Analysis 
 
Annotation of SNPs to genes and subsequent computation of gene p-values were performed using 
the command line program MAGMA61 according to the MAGMA manual version 1.07 guidelines 
for annotation and gene analysis. The precise commands can be found in supplementary materials 
[magma.sh].  
 
Before the actual gene analysis, in which gene p-values were computed, SNPs were mapped to 
genes if the genomic location of the SNP fell within the genomic location of the gene defined as the 
sequence between transcription start and stop. By recommendations in the MAGMA manual a 10 
kb window upstream of the transcription start site was included in the gene genomic locations as to 
include SNPs in the promoter regions of genes. Gene positions were specified according to a 
reference genome, for all three GWAS data sets in this study this was the Genome Reference 
Consortium Human Build 37 (GRCh37) which is available on the official MAGMA webpage. The 
genomic location of SNPs was specified according to the information in the GWAS summary 
statistics files.  
 
Computation of gene p-values was performed using the ãtopã gene analysis model, which utilizes a 
permutation procedure to obtain an empirical gene p-value. To account for linkage disequilibrium 
among SNPs a reference data was included. This was also available on the official MAGMA 
webpage and was selected based on the ethnicity of the participants in the GWAS studies. For all 
three data sets the subjects were of European descend.

Micro array data  
This study included six micro array data sets, four of which were made available as raw data files, 
these were: 4MDD, 3SZ, 7SZ and 1CD, and two were made available only as already processed 
files, these were: 6CD and 11CD.  
 
The two data sets 4MDD and 7SZ consisted of samples from two parts of the brain and it was 
decided to analyse the samples arising from the two different brain parts independently of each 
other as a PCA plot of the data showed that the biggest source of variation in the data was the tissue 
samples, see Figure 8. The data set 6CD consisted of samples from four different parts of the 
intestines: three from the colon and one from the ileum. Based on a PCA plot of the data, see Figure 
8, it was decided to pool the data as the tissue sampled from was not the biggest source of variation.  
 
The aim of the initial analysis of the micro array data was to identify genes, that were transcribed 
significantly more or less in the diseased individuals than in the control group and calculate a p-
value for the significance of the differential expression. 
 
Microarray data analysis  
 
Microarray raw data files were downloaded from the NCBI Sequence Read Achieve using the 
command line tool fasterq-dump from the SRA Toolkit version 1.20.562 and then imported in to R 
using the oligo package version 1.52.063. If data was not available as raw files then the already pre-
processed data was imported directly from the Gene expression Omnibus database using the 
GEOquery package version 2.56.064.  
 
The entire differential expression analysis of the microarray data sets was done in R and the script 
used can be found in the supplementary materials [microarray.R].  
 
Quality control of data was performed using the ArrayQualityMetrics package version 3.44.065. 
The package produces a detailed report of quality metrics of a microarray data set, which was used 
to assess the quality of the data set, identify outliers and batch effects. Outliers are evaluated in 
ArrayQualityMetrics based on three detection methods: Distance between arrays, Boxplots and MA 
plots ã if a sample was detected as being an outlier in all three detection methods, it was removed 
from the data set. During initial quality control five outliers were removed from the data set 6CD 
and one from the data set 4MDD.  
 
A batch effect was identified in the data set 3SZ based on a PCA plot where data was clearly 
grouped based on the year it was produced in, see Figure 9. This was corrected for using the 
package sva version 3.36.066, which employs an empirical Bayesian framework to adjust for known 
batch effects. Upon batch correction, the data set was analysed again using the package 
ArrayQualityMetrics to determine the quality of the batch correction.  
==PAGE:24==
 
24 
 
If information was lacking on which samples were from the case group or control group, this was 
then manually added to the data object, where the samples were stored.  
 
Raw data was then processed using the RMA algorithm from the package oligo version 1.52.063, 
which performs background subtraction, quantile normalization and summarization on the data set.  
 
Finally, differential expression analysis was performed using the package limma version 3.44.167 
according to the limma user guide chapter 9: single-channel experimental designs. The results were 
exported as a text file for the network analysis.

Network analysis   
The network analysis was done using Intomicsã command line programs. Here follows an overview 
of the specific programs used and their function.  
 
VirtualPulldown is a program that can generate protein interaction networks based on protein-
protein interaction data from the inBio MapTM database, which is an IntomicsôÛ database of high-
confidence, experimentally derived protein-protein interactions that covers more than 700000 
physical protein-protein interactions from more than 17500 human reviewed UniProt proteins. The 
program takes a list of seed proteins as input and generates a 1st order protein interaction network 
around the seed proteins, meaning that the resulting network will contain the seed proteins and the 
proteins that the seed proteins directly interact with.  
 
SystemSignificance is a program that computes the significance of a biological system given a list 
of p-values associated to proteins in that system. The significance of the system is computed 
through a permutation-based method, where the significance is calculated as the fraction of all 
permutated systems that have a more extreme p-value than the original calculated p-value for the 
system. The output of SystemSignificance is table of networks ranked by their significance. The 
table further includes information on the size of the networks and the integrated p-value for the 
system. The networks are identified by their central protein, which is the seed protein used to 
generate the network in VirtualPulldown.  
 
SystemMerger is a program that merges overlapping biological systems, the calculation of overlap 
is done using the Szymkiewicz-Simpson coefficient, which is stated below.

ListIntegrator is a program that takes as input several scored/ranked lists e.g. list of proteins

In step two all the networks were passed along to the program SystemSignificance, that based on 
the proteinsã associated p-values generated from the differential expression analysis and gene 
analysis calculated the significance of the protein interaction networks. Integration of p-values to 
generate a network p-value was done using Edgingtonãs method, which was also used for handling 
duplicate genes and their p-values. Networks with a significance above 1e-04 were eliminated from 
the further network analysis. From the output from SystemSignificance a list of significant protein-
protein interaction networks was compiled 
 
In the third step protein interaction networks were generated based on the list of significant protein-
protein interaction networks, this was also done using VirtualPulldown. The output from 
VirtualPulldown was then passed along to the program SystemMerger, which based on the overlap 
between protein networks merges networks that overlap with more than 75%.  
 
In step four the output from SystemMerger was passed to SystemSignificance and again a list of 
significant protein-protein interaction networks was created based on the output from 
SystemSignificance with the same significance threshold as before. If too many (>30) were 
significant, then only the top 20 networks were selected for further analysis.  
 
In step five of the network analysis a GO term overrepresentation analysis was performed on the 
significant protein networks to identify the underlying biology of the networks, this analysis was 
performed using the program SystemAnnotater. For each network the output from SystemAnnotater 
was then manually inspected and based on the GO terms which were significantly associated with 
the network a short overview of the network biology was composed. The annotation analysis 
workflow is compiled in a bash script, which can be found in supplementary materials 
[annotate_networks.sh].  
 
The networks were then subsequently imported in to Cytoscape, which is an open source platform 
for visualizing molecular interaction networks and biological pathways and integrating these 
networks with annotations, gene express profiles and other state data76. Once imported in to 
Cytoscape the networks were then overlapped with the text-mining list containing known disease-
associated proteins/genes, to identify networks with already disease-associated proteins/genes. The 
visualization of networks for this project was done using InBio DiscoverTM, which is a web 
program developed by Intomics for creating and visualization of protein-protein interaction 
network. InBio DiscoverTM.  
 
After all data sets were individually analysed through this above described network analysis 
workflow, then all data sets within the same disease were integrated using the tool ListIntegrator 
(step six), so that each protein found in one or more of the data sets were assigned one integrated p-
value based on the individual p-values from the various data sets from the same disease ã the 
integrated p-value was calculated using Edgingtonãs method. This list of proteins and integrated p-
values was then run through the network analysis pipeline and annotation pipeline i.e. the two 
scripts network_creator.sh and annotate.sh.

Several of the networks across different data sets represent immune system biology, which fits the 
knowledge that a hallmark of Crohnãs disease is a dysregulated innate and adaptive immune

system processes, see Table 4 and Appendix II, both innate immune system and adaptive immune 
system processes are represented in the networks. Innate immune system processes represented in 
the different networks are: the complement system, opsonization, toll-like receptor signalling and  
phagocytosis. The processes from the adaptive immune system present in the networks are: Fc 
receptor signalling, regulation of T-cell mediated immunity and antigen processing and 
presentation, which fits the known knowledge of immune system processes involved in Crohnãs 
disease.

- 
Mitogen-activated protein kinases (MAPK), which are intracellular serine/threonine kinases

They are involved in controlling a wide range of important cellular functions such as: cell 
growth, proliferation and differentiation, inflammation and production of proinflammatory 
cytokines, and survival77,78. 
 
- 
JAK-STAT signalling pathway, which is a complex signalling pathway involved in many 
important cellular processes like immune system regulatory processes, cell growth and 
differentiation79,80.

Vitamin D3 receptor (VDR), which is a Crohnãs disease associated protein found in several 
of the annotated networks across Crohnãs disease data sets. Vitamin D functions in 
regulating calcium homeostasis and bone metabolism, but has also been found to act as an 
immune-modulator25,28.  
 
- 
Nuclear Factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells (NFkB), which is a family of 
transcription factors that mainly regulates transcription of genes involved in inflammation 
and in the adaptive and innate immune system81.

The benchmark plot in Figure 14 shows the comparison of the different schizophrenia data sets and 
the integration of all the data sets to a benchmark list composed of well-known disease associated 
genes/proteins from text-mining. From the plot it is evident that especially two data 7SZ_HPC and

caused by the two 7SZ data sets. Attempts were made in order improve the integration of data while 
still keeping the two data sets, these included only integrating genes that were present in two or 
more data sets and to use a rank-based integration method, but it was ultimately decided to discard 
the two data sets. 
 
 Figure 15 shows the benchmark plot comparing the results from the four remaining schizophrenia 
data sets and the integrated data to the benchmark list. It can be seen that discarding the two data 
sets improved the quality of the integrated data as it now has a slightly stronger signal compared to 
the benchmark list. From the plot if can further be seen that the data set 10SZ also performs slightly 
under the benchmark list, but it was decided to keep it in the analysis anyways as the integrated data 
still performed better than the benchmark list.

Discussion of results  
Crohnãs disease 
MAPK cascade  
The network around P17252, see Figure 16, contains only two proteins known to be associated with 
Crohnãs disease, but was still selected as interesting as it was one of two networks that overlapped 
between Crohnãs diseaseãs data sets. Due to the size of the network, is was not significantly 
enrichment for proteins associated with Crohnãs disease, as can be seen by the p-value for the 
enrichment. The network is however significantly enriched for proteins that are associated with 
IBD. From the annotation analysis, see Table 4, it is known that the network is involved in 
neurogenesis, programmed cell death and signalling through the MAPK cascade. The proteins that 
are associated with the MAPK cascade are highlighted in green in Figure 16. 
 
The MAPKs are intracellular serine/threonine kinases involved in converting extracellular signals 
and stimuli to changes in transcription activity. They are involved in controlling a wide range of 
important cellular functions such as: cell growth, proliferation and differentiation, inflammation and 
production of proinflammatory cytokines, and survival77,78. Perturbations in the MAPK cascade has 
been implicated in several diseases e.g. Alzheimerãs disease, inflammatory diseases and various 
types of cancers78,86, which makes them interesting regarding Crohnãs disease.  
 
The cascade consists of the following components: extracellular signal-regulated kinases (ERK), 
p38 and c-Jun NH2-terminal kinase (JNK). Once activated the final step in the cascade is the 
phosphorylation of transcription factors such as p53, STAT1/3, NFkB and others77,86.  
 
There is evidence that implicates the MAPK cascade in IBD, as it was discovered that IBD patients 
have increased expression of MAPKs78,87 and that some of the transcription factors activated by 
MAPKs control the expression of genes involved in IBD78. There is little research on the 
implications of MAPKs specifically in Crohnãs disease, so this can so far only inferred from the 
implications of MAPKs in IBD.  
 
There have been a few clinical trials on the usage of MAPK inhibitors to treat IBD with mixed 
results77,78. A preliminary clinical trial of the MAPK inhibiting drug Semapimod on patients with 
moderate to severe Crohnãs disease found improvements in the majority of patients receiving the 
drug 88, thus potentially linking the MAPK cascade and Crohnãs disease.

The JAK-STAT signalling pathway is a complex signalling pathway that coordinates signalling 
from more than 50 different ligands mostly cytokines and growth factors, and it is involved in many 
important cellular processes like cell growth and differentiation, and immune system regulatory 
processes79,80. This corresponds to the results from the annotation analysis of the network.  
 
The signalling pathway consists of three parts: a variety of membrane-receptors, receptor-associated 
proteins of the JAK-family, which there are four of in total (JAK1, JAK2, JAK3 and TYK2), and 
transcription factors of the STAT family, which there are seven of in total (STAT1-2, STAT5a/b, 
and STAT6).  
 
The importance of the pathway is evident from its evolutionary conservation, and that loss-of-
function or gain-of-function mutations in proteins involved in the pathway are known to cause 
detrimental effects e.g. embryonic lethality, immune deficiency and cancer80. Mutations outside of 
the two categories, but which cause the JAK-STAT pathway to be constitutively active or fail to 
regulate it is known to cause inflammatory diseases, different types of leukaemia and other 
disease89. GWAS studies have identified polymorphisms in specific genes involved in the JAK-
STAT pathway that are associated with an increased risk of developing IBD. Especially interesting 
is polymorphisms in the gene IL-23R, which functions as a membrane-receptor in the pathway and 
is also highly associated with Crohnãs disease 79. The JAK-STAT signalling pathway is also known 
to interact with other signalling pathways, the most well-known being with the MAPK pathway.  
This interaction between the two pathways could explain why precisely these two networks P17252 
and P42224 are found significant in the two different data sets.  
 
Another significant network which is involved in the JAK-STAT signalling pathway is the network 
around P23458, see Figure 18. The network is largely enriched for proteins associated with Crohnãs 
disease and also proteins associated with IBD. The transcription factor STAT3, see red arrow in 
Figure 18, couples the JAK-STAT signalling pathway to the immune system processes that are 
perturbated in Crohnãs disease patient, as STAT3 is known to be critical for differentiation of Th17 
cells79. Activated Th17 cells are increasingly present in the gut of Crohnãs disease patients and 
Th17 cytokines are found in high levels too. With the knowledge that constitutively activated JAK-
STAT signalling leads to inflammatory diseases, this indicates that signalling through STAT3 could 
be constitutively active in Crohnãs disease patients.

Vitamin D deficiency is common in patients suffering with Crohnãs disease, but it is not known if it 
is a casual relationship or a caused relationship. Measured serum 25(OH)D, which is the inactive 
precursor, is found in lower amounts in the blood of patients with active Crohnãs disease compared 
to those who are in disease remission. The inactive 25(OH)D is converted to active 1,25(OH)D, 
which binds to VDR and activates it, this leads to binding of the VDR-response elements in the 
cellãs genome and results in gene transcription90.  
 
Vitamin D functions in regulating calcium homeostasis and bone metabolism but has also been 
found to function as an immuno-modulator with potent anti-inflammatory abilities. This has led to 
the discovery of vitamin D deficiency in inflammatory diseases like IBD and hereunder also in 
Crohnãs disease.  
 
Several studies have indicated that vitamin D exerts its immuno-modulatory effects by affecting gut 
epithelial integrity, innate immune system barrier functions, and the development and function of T-
cells, which are all affected in patients suffering from Crohnãs disease28,91. Several studies have 
identified an altered intestinal permeability among Crohnãs disease patients, more specifically it 
was found that there was an increased permeability for large-size molecules and an increase in 
epithelial apoptosis28. It has also been found that active vitamin D is capable of supressing Th17 
cytokine production and thereby also directly inhibit Th17 differentiation92, which links the vitamin 
D deficiency and uncontrolled Th17 response in Crohnãs disease patients.

Several of the significant networks from the individual Crohnãs disease data sets are involved in 
regulation of IkB kinase/NFkB signalling, see Table 4 and Appendix II. It is known that chronic 
expression of the pathway is found in many inflammatory diseases hereunder also IBD93, so it could 
potentially also be implicated in Crohnãs disease. The IkB kinase is a master regulator of the NFkB

NFkB transcription factors regulate the expression of a wide array of genes whose gene product are 
essential for processes like inflammation, adaptive and innate immune system processes, and cell 
survival 81 and have thereby been identified as associated with IBD94. The transcription factors 
belonging to the NFkB family are involved in responses to stimuli such as stress, cytokines and 
bacterial and viral antigens95.

Major Depressive Disorder 
Notch signalling  
An interesting network that is found among the significant results in both 4MDD data sets and 
seems to convey disease-associated biology is the network around P49768, see Figure 22. Based on 
the annotation analysis, see Table 8, the network is involved in programmed cell death, 
neurotransmitter secretion and notch signalling.  
 
Notch signalling regulates neural stem cells and neural development, but is also implicated in the 
regulation of migration, morphology, synaptic plasticity and survival of neurons in the adult brain82.  
 
Only a few studies have implicated any association between notch signalling and depression97,98. 
But theoretically notch signalling could be linked to MDD through its implications in synaptic 
plasticity and survival of neurons in the adult brain. Synaptic plasticity is the ability of oneãs own 
experiences to modify neural networks and thereby influence future thoughts, behaviour and 
feelings. This occurs by strengthening or weakening synaptic transmission at pre-existing synapses 
through e.g. alterations in neurotransmitter release99,100, which the network P49768 is involved in. 
Synaptic plasticity has been implicated in several neurodegenerative diseases like Alzheimerãs and 
Parkinsonãs, but also in neuropsychiatric disorders such as autism99 and could thereby also 
potentially be implicated in MDD.  
 
Programmed cell death is an important process used to regulate tissue homeostasis and prevent 
cancerous growth101. Excessive apoptosis of neurons led to neurodegeneration and some studies 
have implicated the loss of neurons by apoptosis to MDD, which is supported by the findings of 
morphological changes in the brain of MDD patients e.g. a decrease in hippocampal volume102.  
 
More research is however needed to fully conclude if the signalling pathway is involved in MDD. 
As notch signalling is mainly active during embryogenesis, it could potentially be very difficult to 
discover any implication of it in MDD, as the effects of it would be non-detectable once the patient 
is dead and it is possible to sample from the brain.

Glutamate  
The network around Q9NUP9 from the data set 10MDD is especially interesting in regard to MDD. 
Based on the results from the annotation analysis the network is involved in nervous systemsã 
processes like: neurotransmitter secretion specifically glutamate secretion, neuron cell-cell adhesion 
and regulation of the NMDA receptor, which is a glutamate receptor.  
 
Glutamate is a major excitatory neurotransmitter found throughout the central nervous system and it 
has critical roles in multiple brain functions and synaptic plasticity83,84. Is as long been of interest in 
regard to MDD as it was discovered that NMDR antagonists have antidepressant-like effects in 
mice109. Several studies have found perturbations in the levels of glutamate in various regions of the 
brain in studies of the post-mortem brain of depressed individuals compared to non-depressed 
individuals. These findings have further been backed up by the use of newer technologies like 
proton magnetic resonance spectroscopy which allows for in vivo measuring83.  
 
Excessive glutamate release is known to be toxic for the brain and glutamate toxicity has been 
associated with exposure to severe stress, which is a known risk factor for MDD110. The potential 
role of glutamate toxicity in MDD can coupled to the popular theory that dysregulation of the HPA 
axis is involved in MDD, as glutamate is an important regulator of the HPA axis111. This 
strengthens the potential involvement of this network in MDD.

Schizophrenia  
The overlap of significant networks  
Among the significant networks from the data set 2SZ, see Table 8, are a total of five networks 
shared with other data sets. One network, the one around O14980, is shared with a depression data 
set, which is not surprising as the two diseases both are neuropsychiatric diseases. The network is 
involved in nucleocytoplasmic transport and gene silencing by miRNA. Gene silencing by miRNAs 
have been implicated in both schizophrenia and MDD39,114. miRNAs are important regulator of 
brain development, and dysfunction of miRNAs have been implicated in many neuropsychiatric 
diseases115.  
 
The other four datasets are shared with two Crohnãs disease data sets. The biology represented by 
these four networks is mainly basic cellular processes like cell division, chromosome organization, 
DNA repair and regulation of gene expression. The network around Q13618 is also involved in 
immune system processes, which is interesting as the link between the immune system and 
schizophrenia has been discussed for decades and have been established in several studies116ã119.  
Traditionally the brain was viewed as a system separate from the immune system, but it has now 
been discovered that there exist complex interactions between the immune system, inflammation 
and the brain, which can lead to changes in mood and behaviour. It is believed that inflammation 
could play a role in schizophrenia, which would explain the overlap of significant networks 
between Crohnãs disease and schizophrenia120.  
 
Among the significant networks from the data set PGC-SZ, see Table 7, are two networks that are 
found among the significant networks from the other two diseases. The data sets which share these 
two networks are the GWAS data sets. It was discovered that the two networks, which were 
involved in immune system processes, consisted of a majority of HLA proteins, which are known to 
be highly polymorphic and as several HLA genes can map to the same protein identifier, it is 
difficult to infer if it is the same HLA genes that are mutated across the three data sets or different 
ones.

in individuals suffering from schizophrenia compared to healthy individuals121,123. The role of 
alternative splicing in psychiatric disorders is still very much undefined, but it is known to play a 
role in many genetic diseases123 and thus could be a subject for more research investigating its link 
with schizophrenia.  
Ribosomes and their potential implication in schizophrenia 
There is some evidence that points to a potential role of ribosomes and the translationary machinery 
in schizophrenia. Based on a few studies that have discovered a dysregulation in the copy number 
of ribosomal genes present in the genomes of patients suffering from schizophrenia124,125. It has 
further been discovered that perturbations in ribosomal biogenesis plays a role in 
neurodevelopmental disorders like schizophrenia with various pathways being suggested as the 
mechanisms by which the perturbations in ribosomal biogenesis leads to neurodevelopmental 
disorders126. In relation to schizophrenia it is known that perturbations in ribosome biogenesis leads 
to ribosomal stress which activates the p53 signalling pathway127, which is one of the GO terms that 
is enriched in the ribosome networks, see Table 8. Mutations in the gene TP53 which encodes the 
tumour suppressor protein p53 has been associated with schizophrenia together with mutations in 
other tumour suppressor genes. The link between tumour suppressor genes and schizophrenia was 
made based on a study which found a lowered risk of cancer in people suffering from schizophrenia 
and their immediate relatives128.  
 
However, based on the current research on the link between schizophrenia and ribosomes nothing 
definitive can be stated that strongly supports the findings in this project. It is interesting that the 
ribosomal networks are so significant in two independent of each other data sets arising from two 
different types of tissues and that two ribosomal networks are among the significant networks from 
the data set 2SZ. But further analyses of the data sets would definitely be needed to determine if this 
was indeed a significant result or if there are some mistakes made or underlying biases in the 
analysis pipelines that could have caused it.  
The lack of significant schizophrenia networks 
There are not many networks enriched for proteins known to be associated with schizophrenia and 
the biology represented by the significant networks do not match what is widely accepted as being 
associated with schizophrenia. One possible answer to this could be the untraditional choice of 
tissue sampled from in the data sets. MDD and Crohnãs disease data sets are sampled from the 
diseased organ, while none of the schizophrenia data sets are sampled from the diseased organ. 
They are instead sampled from alternative tissues like fibroblasts and induced pluripotent stem 
cells. Although they have originally been sampled from individuals suffering from schizophrenia, 
then the environment they are kept under during the experiments is most likely nowhere near what 
they originally came from. With the knowledge that inflammation and the immune system 
potentially plays a role in schizophrenia, would explain the lack of results.  
The use of alternative tissues instead of brain tissue is to circumvent the difficulties of getting 
samples and the difficulties examining post-mortem samples.  
 
Schizophrenia is highly heritable and it is believed that its aetiology must be largely influenced by 
genetic factors129,130. Recent reviews on historical candidate genes for schizophrenia have yielded 
==PAGE:61==
 
61 
no evidence for the association of these candidate genes with schizophrenia and they are also not 
well supported by GWAS results130,131. Despite many successes in GWAS studies to identify 
significant loci associated with schizophrenia, much of the heritability of the disease still remains 
unknown130. Epistasis and epigenetics have been proposed as potential mechanisms to explain the 
problem of ãmissing heritabilityã132. However, as schizophrenia is a highly heterogenous disorder 
with no single defining symptom, it might be worth rethinking if schizophrenia is indeed a single 
defined disease or if it is actually more of an umbrella term covering several diseases with 
overlapping symptoms. This could explain the lack of significant results not only in this study but 
also more general.

Conclusion  
 
Based on this projectãs results and finding, it can be concluded that systems biology network 
analysis approaches offer a great tool for investigating the underlying perturbated biology of 
complex diseases. For both Crohnãs disease and MDD the identified perturbated networks were 
involved in biology that from literature was known or hypothesized to be implicated in the diseases. 
For schizophrenia the results were not as successful and it is hypothesized that this could be due to 
either the types of tissue sampled from, the heterogeneity of the disease or potential bias in the 
analysis pipeline.   
 
As the approach is mainly a hypothesis generating method, it must be supported by literature 
studies of the biology represented by the significant networks in order to identify how and if the 
network biology and the disease are connected. It is thus limited by current knowledge of the 
disease and the network biology. The results from a network analysis does also not confer 
information about whether the network biology is causing the disease or simply affected by the 
disease, which is a main factor of interest in the research of complex diseases.  
 
From the results of the network and annotation analysis of Crohnãs disease data it can further be 
concluded, that the systems biology method of integration of several data sets across omics type 
data has potentials for gaining even more knowledge of the perturbated disease biology. This is 
concluded as the integration of all Crohnãs disease data sets resulted in networks that were highly 
associated with Crohnãs disease based on the number of known disease associated proteins in the 
networks that the significance of those present, compared to the networks from the independent data 
sets.

Future perspectives  
 
For detecting more of the disease perturbated biology, it would be desirable to include more omics 
type data in the analysis. In this project only genomic and transcriptomic data was used, a potential 
expansion here could be to additionally include proteomic data, as to infer if the disease perturbated 
biology identified at genomic and transcriptomic level is also reflected at proteomic level. As 
complex diseases tend to vary in severity and manifestations it could be useful to sample the 
different omics data from the same individuals and take more care to select individuals with the 
same severity of disease. This approach focusing on subgroups of the complex disease could 
potentially circumvent the problem of disease heterogeneity in complex diseases.  
 
The results from a network analysis of a complex disease is a snapshot of the biology that was 
perturbated at the time of sampling. With progressive diseases like Crohnãs disease, it could be 
interesting to expand a network analysis with time series data and perform a network analysis on 
the data at each time point. This would investigate if the disease perturbated biology changes over 
time e.g. what biology is perturbated when the patient is in remission versus when the disease is 
active. This would also be interesting with regards to MDD for detecting which biology is 
perturbated when the patient is in a depressive episode versus when the patient is not in a depressive 
episode. However, the implementation of time series data is especially difficult with regards to 
neuropsychiatric diseases, as it is for obvious reasons not possible to take time series samples of the 
diseased organ. For ethical reasons it might also be problematic to take time series data of the 
infected organ in Crohnãs disease patients as it would require an invasive sampling method. Further 
research in to alternative tissues to sample when examining complex diseases, especially 
neuropsychiatric diseases, would be beneficial for the implementation of time series data in network 
analysis methodologies
