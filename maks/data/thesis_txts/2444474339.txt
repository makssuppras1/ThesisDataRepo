Abstract
This master thesis was curried out on the investigation of the eﬀect of oxygen on the
corrosion behaviour of 13Cr stainless steel in seawater. Due to its high strength and
corrosion resistance, at relatively low cost, 13Cr martensitic stainless steel is widely
used in injection wells in oil and gas industries, but essentially under oxygen-free
working conditions. However, the presence of dissolved oxygen traces, which is diﬃcult
to completely avoid, may have detrimental eﬀects on the corrosion performance of the
13Cr stainless steel.
Therefore the focus of the present thesis has been given to a deeper understanding
of the impact of oxygen on the nature and stability of the protective oxide ﬁlm on
the 13Cr stainless steel in NaCl solution.
In this connection, the electrochemical
techniques (cyclic polarization and long term open circuit potential tests) were cur-
ried out in 1 wt% NaCl, under various dissolved oxygen concentrations. For deeper
understanding, the surface and oxide ﬁlms were characterized by means of scanning
electron microscopy (SEM), transmission electron microscopy (TEM), electron energy
loss spectroscopy (EELS) and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS).
The most evident feature revealed by the electrochemical tests was the increase of
the corrosion potential with increasing dissolved oxygen, which resulted in reducing
the corrosion resistance of the material. The results from the surface and oxide ﬁlm
characterization revealed that the susceptibility of the 13Cr stainless steel to pitting
corrosion increased with increasing dissolved oxygen concentration.

Introduction 1
1.1 Corrosion Problems in the Oil & Gas production
One important source of oil is the subsurface reservoir of hydrocarbon trapped in
porous or fractured rock formations. These reservoirs form and are sealed in geological
faults and have an internal pressure due to the weight of the earth covering them.
When extracting oil in these reservoirs, the internal pressure, causing free ﬂow of oil
to production facilities, gradually decreases. This mechanism is known as primary
recovery [1, 2]. However, as the pressure decreases, the ﬂow of oil decreases as well.
In order to maintain the pressure of the reservoir at its optimum production pressure,
gas (such as carbon dioxide or nitrogen) or water is injected through an injection
well to replace the extracted oil [2]. Figure 1.1 shows an illustration of the injection
system. For oﬀshore sites, seawater is typically injected through steel pipe in the
injection well. In order to achieve the most eﬃcient recovery system, selection of
proper material for the well is very crucial, as corrosion attack can compromise the
performance of the utilised materials.
Figure 1.1: Schematic of the enhanced oil recovery system [3].

1.2 Corrosion Problems During Oil & Gas Production
Corrosion is the destructive attack of a material by reaction with its environment.
Nearly any aqueous environment can stimulate corrosion, which can occur under var-
ious complex conditions. The oil and gas wells contain highly corrosive media such as
carbon dioxide (CO2), hydrogen sulﬁde (H2S), free water and chloride [4].These corro-
sive media can with time make the internal surface of the pipes used in the wells suﬀer
from corrosion eﬀects. The common types of corrosion encountered in the oil and gas
facilities include CO2 corrosion, H2S corrosion, galvanic corrosion, erosion corrosion,
microbiologically induced corrosion, stress cracking corrosion, crevice corrosion and
oxygen corrosion [4].
Among the mentioned types of corrosion, this project will solely focused on the oxygen
corrosion, as dissolved oxygen(DO) in injection wells is one of the major source of steel
pipe corrosion. Acting as electron acceptor and depolarizer during cathodic reactions,
oxygen accelerates the anodic degradation of the metal [4, 5]. In general, deaeration
systems are designed to remove DO from water used in the injection wells.
The
methods commonly used for the deaeration include chemical scavengers, gas stripping
or liquid extraction [6]. These systems designed to reduced the DO concentration to
less than 10 part per billion (ppb), do not achieve this performance consistently. This
lead to traces of DO suﬃcient to aﬀect the metal surface corrosion reaction [7].
Depending on the exposure conditions and used material, the corrosion related to
oxygen is either uniform or localized (i.e. pitting or crevice corrosion). The corrosion
attack on the materials results in the loss of mechanical properties like strength,
ductility, impact strength, which may lead to ultimate failures. These failures lead
to serious consequences in terms of economical losses, as production will be stopped
to replace the damaged materials with new ones.
Corrosion may not only cause
economical losses, but also bring with it problems related to safety and protection
resources. Therefore the selection of materials with a high corrosion resistance is of
utmost importance.
Due to its high strength and corrosion resistance at relatively low price, 13Cr marten-
sitic stainless steels (12-14 wt%) have been extensively used in the oil and gas injection
as well as production wells. For instance, this steel type has been used as an alter-
native to carbon steel (1 wt% Cr) and inhibitors, which were mostly used for the
production tubing in the North sea [8]. The corrosion resistance 13Cr stainless steel
results from the passivation chromium oxide ﬁlm formed on its surface, which serves
as a protective layer [9]. In the presence of DO in the surrounding media, even at
low concentration, the 13Cr steels may suﬀer from the severe corrosion. Despite their
self-passivation properties, a minimal level of DO has been found to alter their pas-
sivation properties, thus aﬀecting their corrosion resistance [10, 7].
Therefore the
present study has been focused on a deeper understanding of the eﬀect of DO on the
corrosion behavior of the 13Cr stainless steel.

1.3 Aim of the Project
The aim of this project is to investigate the eﬀect of oxygen on the corrosion behaviour
of the 13Cr stainless steel, AISI 410, in NaCl solutions. Focus has been given to the
nature of the protective oxide ﬁlm of the steel, how it is formed and aﬀected in
this chemistry. In this regard, cyclic polarization and free corrosion tests have be
performed in fully aerated and fully de-aerated 1 wt% NaCl solutions. In addition,

For deeper understanding, the surface and oxide ﬁlms were characterized by means of
scanning electron microscopy (SEM), transmission electron microscopy (TEM), elec-
tron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS) and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS).

Background 2
2.1 Corrosion Behaviour of 13 Cr Stainless Steel
Stainless steels exhibit high resistance to corrosion in a variety of environments, par-
ticularly the ambient atmosphere. The high corrosion resistance requires an addition
of chromium with a concentration of at least 11 wt%. The corrosion resistance of
stainless steels is due to their passivation by a surface ﬁlm of Cr2O3 [11, 12]. Corro-
sion resistance may also be improved by addition of nickel and molybdenum [13, 14].
It is very important that the formed oxide ﬁlm is dense. The oxide ﬁlm is rapidly
formed on clean and smooth surfaces. However, if the surface is rough or contam-
inated with deposits, mill scale, welding slag, the passivation may be hindered and
the surface becomes more prone to pitting or deposit corrosion [9]. Based on their
microstructures, stainless steels can be divided into ﬁve groups: ferritic, austenistic,
duplex and martensitic and precipitation hardening grades [14, 13].
Among the groups of steels, the 13Cr martensitic stainless steel grade is one of the
most popular steel grade produced. This steel type can be hardened and tempered.
Therefore it is commonly used in applications where combined hardness and high
strength with moderate corrosion resistance is required [15, 13]. It has also excellent
corrosion resistance compared to carbon steels up to around 150◦C and are typically
used for various wellhead, tubular and downhole components [16]. In the oil and gas
it is typically used in the injection wells and also in the production wells [4].
According to recent studies [10, 11] regarding the eﬀect of oxygen on the surface
passivation of 13Cr stainless steels, it has been found that the stability of the pas-
sivation ﬁlm of stainless steels diﬀers in aerated and deaerated Cl−ions containing
solutions. I was also found that the combined action of oxygen and chloride in water
that accelerates the corrosion rate of stainless steels.
It has also been reported that pitting and repassivation potential of 13Cr stainless
steel increase with increasing pH and decreasing temperature [15] in HCl solution
with diﬀerent concentration. in addition, synergistic eﬀect was found for hydrogen
and chloride ions in the pitting mechanism.
Furthermore, various literatures suggest that air saturated chloride containing solu-
tions raise the corrosion potential of stainless steels [14, 8, 9], which results in reducing
the corrosion resistance. The eﬀect of oxygen was found to be an accelerating factor
of the cathodic reaction rate, which lead to an increase in the potential.
In addition, another study [10, 17] performed on 13Cr stainless steel revealed that

2.2 Fundamentals of Corrosion
2.2.1 Electrochemical Reactions
Electrochemical reaction is the reaction in which electrons are transferred from one
specie to another. The corrosion process for metallic materials is typically an elec-
trochemical reaction that consists of a cathodic (reduction) and anodic (oxidation)
reaction. At the anodic site, oxidation reaction takes place, where the metal atoms re-
lease electrons. The electrons released by the anodic reaction are conducted through
the metal to the cathodic site, where they are consumed in the cathodic reaction
[9, 13]. This corrosion process requires a conduction environment (e.g. electrolyte).
An example of anodic and cathodic reactions is shown in Equations (2.1) and (2.2),
respectively.

2.2.2 Pourbaix Diagrams
As pH is an important variable of aqueous solutions and aﬀects the equilibrium poten-
tials of majority of the possible reactions, Marcel Pourbaix derived his pH - potential
diagrams, known as Pourbaix diagrams. From the Pourbaix diagrams, corrosion be-
haviour can be predicted, when the potential, pH and an estimate of the metal ion
concentration are known. Pourbaix diagram of iron is depicted in Figure 2.1. The
Pourbaix diagram consist of three main zones including: 1) the immune zone, where
the material does not react, 2) passive zones, where the material forms a passive ﬁlm
serving as protecting layer, and 3) corrosion zones. This diagram is very important
for corrosion engineers, as the potential or pH can be adjusted to take the material
structure to the region of interest, namely the immunity or passivation.
Pourbaix diagrams can be used for the design of cathodic protection systems, which
consist of bringing the potential of a material to regions, where its corrosion rate is
reduced. Furthermore, the Pourbaix diagrams can be related to the anodic polariza-
tion curve. However, attention must be paid to the fact that the pH near the surface
can signiﬁcantly diﬀer from the bulk pH of the material. This is especially crucial for
localized corrosion such as pitting and crevice corrosion. Therefore it is important
to notice that recorded polarization curves are not always completely in accordance
with the theoretically calculated Pourbaix diagrams.

2.2 Fundamentals of Corrosion
2.2.3 Passivity
The passive state of an oxide ﬁlm is characterized by low ionic conductivity and low
solubility. These properties allow the oxide ﬁlm for preventing the transfer of ions
from the metal to the electrolyte, to a large extent. However, slow dissolution, known
as the passive current density ipass is still allowed. This is due to the fact that the
formed oxide ﬁlms are very thin (∼3nm thick), and thereby allowing electrochemical
reactions on the external side of the oxide. There are diﬀerent types of surface ﬁlms,
which can be classed in three main groups based on their characteristics [9, 11]:
• Films that prevent the anodic dissolution eﬃciently, but not the cathodic reac-
tion. These types of oxide ﬁlm typically form on Fe, Ni, Cr and their alloys.
Examples of cathodic reactions that may occur are oxygen reduction, as electron
transfer through the oxide ﬁlm is allowed.
• Films that prevent both anodic and cathodic to a large extent. The passivating
oxide ﬁlm of aluminium can be taken as an example. However, defects can allow
some electron transfer and cathodic activities.
• Deposits that can decrease both anodic and cathodic activities, but not enough
to provide eﬀective passivity. This type of ﬁlms consist of porous surface layers,
such as rust and salts deposited from the electrolyte, when the actual solubility
limit is exceeded.
When there is an oxidizer an electrolyte and the concentration of this is increased,
the reversible potential for the cathodic reaction is increased, thus increasing in the
corrosion rate [9]. The oxygen reduction typically dominates over the hydrogen reac-
tion in aerated aqueous solution when the pH exceeds roughly 3. Therefore in most
environment with presence of air, such as the atmosphere, fresh waters and seawater,
the oxygen reduction is the essential cathodic reaction [9].
2.2.4 Pitting Corrosion
Stainless steel and other types of metals forming passive oxide ﬁlms on their surface
are susceptible to pitting and crevice corrosion, in environments containing chloride,
sulphate, or bromide ions, when their potential exceeds a critical value.
Pitting
corrosion is an electrochemical oxidation-reduction (redox) process, which takes place
inside localized holes or cells on the surface of passivated metals. The pitting corrosion
is characterized by narrow pits with a radius of the same order of magnitude as, or less
than, the depth. Pits may have distinct shape, but their typical feature is the sharp
boundary. Pitting is considered as a dangerous form of corrosion, since the material
often may be penetrated without any clear warning. Furthermore, the growth of the
pit is also diﬃcult to predict. This is due to the fact that the extent and the intensity
of pitting corrosion is diﬃcult to determine, since the number and size of pits change
from region and within each region [13, 9].

3.1 Corrosion Monitoring Methods
3.1.1 Open Circuit Potential (OCP)
OCP is the measure of the potential between a corroding metal (also called working
electrode) and a reference electrode. When immersed into an electrolyte, the surface
of the working electrode needs to adjust to the new environment by undergoing various
stages of adsorption, hydration, and possibly formation of corrosion products [13]. As
the potential is measured with respect to a reference electrode, a change in time can
be observed. Initially the potential changes and then tends to stabilize after several
minutes or hours. At this stage, it can be assumed that the working electrode is in
equilibrium with its environment, and the OCP has reached the corrosion potential
Ecorr.
The OCP monitoring is a technique generally used in corrosion engineering. The
OCP is used as a reference potential, when investigating the corrosion behaviour of
materials. Potentials below the OCP value go in the cathodic regime, while potentials
above the OCP value fall in the anodic regime, where degradation of the metal can
be expected.
3.1.2 Potentiodynamic Polarization
Potentiodynamic polarization is the scanning of potential at a constant scan rate,
while the electrical current is recorded. The measurement setup requires two elec-
trodes (reference and counter electrodes) in addition to the working electrode. The
counter electrode serves as a conductor, therefore closes the electrical circuit. On the
other hand, the reference electrode does not conduct current but is used as a refer-
ence to the potentiostat that performs the polarization by controlling the potential
diﬀerence between the working and the reference electrode.
From the recorded data, the potential is plotted as function of the absolute value of
the current density in a logarithmic scale. A schematic illustration of an anodic and
cathodic polarization curve is depicted in Figure 3.1. As indicated by the term, the
anodic polarization is dominated by the anodic reaction. The anodic branch consists
of three characteristic regions, as shown in Figure 3.1(a). The region starting from

3.2.1 X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS)
The XPS technique consists of irradiating the sample with an X-ray beam gener-
ated by heating a target material with an electron beam. The working principal is
illustrated in Figure 3.2(a).
Figure 3.2: Schematics depicting the working principle of the XPS system. (A): Conventional XPS system. (B): XPS
system with addition of ion gun allowing depth proﬁling analysis. Adjusted from [19]
Aluminium is typically used for the source of the X-ray beam. The X-rays are focused
by mean of an ellipsodial mirror, which produces a monochromatic source of X-rays.
Those X-rays interact with the atoms of the sample to produce photoelectrons. The
photoelectrons are then collected by lenses and concentrated by energy analyser to
separate them by characteristic energy levels. Thus a spectrum, relating the intensity
and the binding energy, can be generated [19].
For the study of the chemical composition and physical structure of the oxide layer
formed on the surface of the samples, the XPS technique has been used. This tech-
nique is widely used for surface analysis, as it is very sensitive to the top few atomic
layers (0.5 - 3 nm) of solid samples. Figure 3.3 illustrates a schematic diagram of the
diﬀerent types surface layer of solid samples. The surface can be deﬁned as the region
where atoms and molecules from the environment (whether gas, liquid or solids) can
interact with the atoms of the sample. Going deeper, we may have a region deﬁned
as ultra thin ﬁlm with a thickness of around 10 nm. In the two above-mentioned top
layers, techniques such as SIMS, AES and XPS can be used, see Figure 3.3. As we go

Finally as we get micrometer deeper onto the sample, bulk analysis techniques are
the governing techniques [19, 20].
Surfaces can have very diﬀerent composition than the bulk of the sample, there-
fore speciﬁcally XPS is a useful technique for the surface analysis. However, when

In order to inspect the surface of the tested sam-

SEM
can produce high resolution image of a sample by

As illustrated in Figure 3.4, electrons are gener-

These electrons are then accelerated down the

which keep the beam electrons from interacting
with the gas molecules in the chamber to ensure

utilised to control and focus the electron beam,
while scanning coils are used to scan the beam

the surface topography.
On the other hand backscattered electrons are generated from elastic interactions from
deeper region from the surface. The backscattered electron signal provide contrast
information of areas with diﬀerent chemical composition, as regions with high atomic
number appear brighter.
In this project the secondary imaging mode has been used, as the main purpose was
to characterize the expoosed surfaces for pits and eventually corrosion products.
3.3.1 Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)
TEM is an indispensable tool in terms of characterizing features of materials, such
as crystal structures and structural features including among others dislocations and
grain boundaries, and chemical analysis as well.
Furthermore, TEM can be used
to investigate the growth of atomic scale layers, their composition and also their
corresponding defects at resolution down to atomic level [23].
The fundamental principle of operation behind TEM can be compared to that of light
microscope, except that TEM uses electron and electromagnetic lenses.
In TEM,
electrons are emitted from a thermionic or ﬁeld emission source and then accelerated
at high voltage, in order to have transmission of these electrons through the specimen.

Hereof, an electron beam is formed and shaped by various lenses placed along the
electron beam column. The transmitted electrons through the specimen are used to
generate the ﬁnal image by means of an image forming system.
Scanning Transmission Electron Microscopy (STEM)

within STEM. These consist of high angle annular

Within the transmission imaging system,
scanning transmission electron microscopy
(STEM) mode can be performed. The STEM
mode combines the principle of TEM and
SEM, and can be performed on either type
of these instruments. Like SEM, the STEM
technique allows a ﬁnely electron beam to be
scanned across the specimen in a raster pat-
tern. The interactions between the electrons
of the beam and specimen atoms create a se-
rial signal stream. This stream of signals is
correlated to the position of the beam and
used to construct an image of the specimen.
Figure 3.5 shows a schematic of the imaging
mode of STEM. The most commonly used
detectors are the annular bright-ﬁeld (ABF)
detector and the high angle annular dark-ﬁeld (HAADF) detector. The bright-ﬁeld
detector includes the transmitted beam, consequently the holes appear to be bright.
Contrarily, the dark-ﬁeld detector excludes the transmitted beam thus holes appear
dark. The STEM mode allows for the collection of both bright and dark ﬁeld images
at the same time, and the easy control of the dynamic range of detectors such as EDS
and EELS, during the specimen analysis[22, 25].
Contrary to SEM in which bulk specimens can be investigated, STEM and TEM re-
quire a thin electron transparent specimen (with thickness of 50 - 150 nm) that enables
transmission mode of imaging [22]. As the STEM mode requires thin specimens, the
probe size becomes a crucial aspect to be considered. The spatial resolution is deﬁned
by the probe size, therefore the highest resolution is achieved by the smallest and most
intense probe. The primary advantage of the use of STEM mode in TEM over the
conventional SEM is the improvement in the spatial resolution down to atomic level.
This advantage is very useful for the characterization of the oxide ﬁlm formed during
corrosion tests.

Electron Energy Loss Spectroscopy (EELS)
Figure 3.6: Schematic diagram of the working princi-

Electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS) is
a very useful technique to inspect formed ox-
ide on the surface of corrosion tested samples.
This technique analyses the energy distribu-
tion of transmitted electrons across the speci-
men. The transmitted electrons may have ex-
perienced an energy loss or no loss in energy
stemming from inelastic collisions, commonly
from electron - electron interaction.
In EELS the transmitted electrons are passed
through a magnetic prism, where those elasti-
cally scattered or those with zero energy loss
pass across the prism with least deﬂection.
Electrons suﬀering higher lost in energy deﬂect more compared those having zero lost
by the magnetic ﬁeld. Hereof a spectrum is generated in a dispersion plane, where
a distribution of electron intensity is plotted as function of energy loss. Figure 3.6
shows a schematic illustration of the trajectory of the electrons across the magnetic
prism spectrometer.
The energy loss phenomena reveals chemical information and the electronic structure
of the specimen atoms. It also provide details about their bonding or valence state,
the nearest-neighbour atomic structure, their electronic response, the free electron
density, the band gap and the thickness of the specimen [26]. Therefore EELS is a
great technique for elemental identiﬁcation and is very useful for detection of lighter
elements, which are very challenging when investigated by the energy dispersive X-ray
spectroscopy (EDS).
Diﬀering from EDS, the EELS technique can reveal and quantify all elements in the
periodic system. In addition, EELS yields better spatial resolution and analytically
sensitivity. However it requires really thin specimens to achieve the most information.
As a complementary technique to XPS, EELS has been used in this project to inves-
tigate the present phases in the formed oxide layer on the surface of the specimens.
For this purpose thin lamella cross-section samples were prepared, where line-scan
were performed from the steel substrate and across the oxide layer. From the EELS
line-scan, the thickness of the oxide layer can be estimated.

Preparing high quality electron transparent samples is of utmost importance in TEM

semi-conductors, metals, polymers and biological materials as well. When performing

ion mill. A single ion gun generates a well-deﬁned beam of Ga+ ions, with a radius

4.5.4 TEM Sample Preparation
The applied procedure for the TEM sample preparation was the "lift-out" technique.
The procedure consists of 5 main stages, as depicted in Figure 4.8.
a) : A platinum layer was deposited to protect the topmost surface of the sample.
b) : A rough cut was curried out using a relatively high current ion beam of 9 - 20
nA. The cut was applied on both side of the region of interest. Subsequently,
both surfaces of the produced lamella were cleaned with lower ion beam current
of 0.9 nA.
c) : The bottom of the lamella was cut and welded on the tip of an omniprobe, so
that the lamella could be lifted out.
d) : The lamella was mounted on a STEM sample holder by welding it with platinum
deposition.
e) : Finally, the sample was gradually thinned to the desired thickness and cleaned
with very low beam current to remove eventual residues.

The results from the XPS analysis were evaluated by qualitative
comparison of the characteristic peaks of the diﬀerent chemical compounds in the
oxide ﬁlm.
The analysis was solely focused on the Fe2p and Cr2p core spectra,
particularly their oxide states, since these elements are the dominating constituents
in the 13Cr stainless steel used in this project.

Discussion 6
In order to investigate the impact of DO on the corrosion behaviour of 13Cr stainless
steel, various electrochemcial tests were conducted under diﬀerent DO concentrations.
These included anodic (experiments 1, 3 and 4) and cathodic (experiment 5) CPP
tests, as well as free corrosion tests (experiment 2).
The features exhibited by the recorded CPP curves in experiment 1 (Figure 5.1), re-
vealed that the passivation performance of the 13Cr stainless steel was signiﬁcantly
reduced with the presence of DO. This was clearly indicated by the potential shift
toward higher values, when the DO concentration was increased. As a consequence,
the passive region as well as the corresponding slope were reduced, thus poorer cor-
rosion resistance. This was in agreement with our expectation, since an increased
concentration of oxidizer in aqueous solutions generally leads to an increase in the
potential [9, 11]. The reason for the poorer corrosion resistance can also be explained
by the Pourbaix for Fe and Cr, shown in Figure 6.1. The potential Ecorr in the deaer-
ated case in experiment 1 was recorded at -613 mV which falls in the immune region
according to the Pourbaix diagram for Fe (Figure 6.1a). In addition, the passivation
event observed in the deaerated case is also in agreement with the prediction of the
Cr Pourbaix diagram, see Figure 6.1b.

The OCP curves from the free corrosion tests showed that the equilibrium potential
of the material was nearly unchanged under the diﬀerent DO concentrations, see

Figure 5.7. Theoretically [9, 10] and according to the CPP curves in experiment 1
(Table 5.1), these potential values were expected to follow a gradual increase in the
potential Ecorr with the DO content. Although the nearly similar levels of potential
after 72 hours exposure, the material tested under deaerated condition revealed the
best corrosion resistance. This was indicated by the least metastable pitting events
compared to the other cases with presence of DO (Figure 5.7). This could also be
conformed by the SEM images shown in Figure 5.8.
Experiment 3 revealed that the exposure of the sample to atmospheric air for two
days before immersion, particularly did aﬀect the recorded CPP curve for the deaer-
ated case.
This could be due to the fact that the passivation mechanism during
the polarization was delayed by the pre-formed passive ﬁlm in air. Therefore it was
important to give better attention to the time between the sample preparation and
the immersion. Nevertheless, the presence of DO appeared to reduce the passivation
performance of the material, resulting in poorer corrosion resistance.
To record CPP curves that could provide better reﬂection of the corrosion behaviour
of the 13Cr stainless steel, the material was allow to reach better equilibrium poten-
tial before the CPP measurements. The resulting CPP curves (Figure 5.4) revealed
signiﬁcant changes, both in the potentials and the current densities compared to those
recorded in experiment 1. The Ecorr shifted from -613 to -200 mV for the deaerated
case while that of the aerated case remained unchanged. Furthermore the icorr moved
to lower values and no re-passivation even could be seen. These reduced current den-
sities suggested that a more stable passive ﬁlm was formed on the material compared
to that formed during experiment 1. In addition, the results from experiment 4 could
be said to exhibit better reﬂection of the corrosion behaviour of the material, since
these are in better agreement with the results from the free corrosion tests.
As a consequence of the the increased levels of Ecorr, the passivation performance of
the material was found to be signiﬁcantly poor, even at the lowest achieved DO con-
tent, i.e. deaerated to ∼10 ppb. These results are similar to those previoulsly reported
by Lei Zhang [10], who found that even 10 ppb could signiﬁcantly reduce the range
and slope of the passive region in the CPP curve, which led to poorer passivation
performance.
To have a better understanding of the relatively high Ecorr values recorded in the
CPP curves (Figure 5.4), cathodic CPP tests were conducted as well.
The most
evident feature revealed by the cathodic tests, in both deaerated and aerated cases,
was the potential shift to higher value. This showed that the level of Ecorr was mostly
dominated by the cathodic process, particularly for the deaerated case, see Figure 5.23.
In general, the cathodic process is predominated by two main types of reactions [9,
13]: hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and oxygen reduction reaction (ORR), as
shown in Equations (6.1) and (6.2), respectively.

The HER dominates in solution with high concentration of H+ and in absence of
oxygen.
However, in solution containing DO, the cathodic process may involve a
combination of HER and ORR, but the ORR would be promoted upon increasing the
concentration of DO in the solution [10, 9]. This would therefore have a major impact
on the passivation performance of the material. With that in might, the presence of
DO in the solution can be though to be a dominating factor controlling the Ecorr.
Similar results were reported in Trond’s previous study [8], who suggested that in-
creased cathodic reaction rate results in higher corrosion rate of 13Cr stainless steels.
Another study by Paul [15] on the same steel, found that the presence of DO was
able to raise the potential Ecorr to a level where the material becomes more prone to
pitting corrosion.
The XPS analysis revealed that the passive ﬁlm formed on the 13Cr stainless steel,
essentially consisted of Fe and Cr both in metallic and oxide states. The oxide state
of Cr could assigned to Cr2O3 and that of Fe matched with Fe2O3. These are well
consistent with previous study [11, 30] and the Pourbaix diagrams of Fe and Cr shown
in Figure 6.1.
The higher intensity of Cr-oxide observed from the CPP sample exposed in deaerated
solution (Figure 5.10), could be related to a higher Cr-oxide content, compared to
the aerated case. Therefore, the relative Cr content of the oxide ﬁlm was believed to
increase at reduced DO content. These results were in agreement with those reported
by Xinqiang [17].
The higher Cr-oxide content in the deaerated case suggested a
more stable passive oxide ﬁlm, therefore more resistant to corrosion. This is also in
agreement with their corresponding recorded CPP curves (Figure 5.1), from which
the material presented better passivation performance in the deaerated case.
Comparison of the XPS depth proﬁles showed decreasing peak intensity of Fe-oxide
and Cr-oxide with longer etching time for the deaerated and aerated cases. The bulk
of the stainless steel was considered to be reached, at spectra where the oxide peaks
(both Fe and Cr) no longer were visible. In this connection, the bulk seemed to be
reached after 30 s etching time for the aerated case, while the deaerated case required
more than 50 s etching time before reaching the bulk. This indicated that the oxide
ﬁlm formed under the deaerated condition may be thicker than that of the aerated
condition.
On the other hand, the STEM-EELS analysis revealed no major diﬀerence in the
oxide thicknesses (about 3-4 nm) for both cases.
The challenging part connected
to the oxide ﬁlm investigation is its small dimension, which makes it diﬃcult to
observed any inﬂuencing aﬀect stemming from the presence of DO. Therefore further
investigations need to be conducted to clarify whether the presence DO has an impact
on the oxide thickness or not. Proposal of future studies are elaborated in Chapter 8.
Overall, based on the results of the diﬀerent investigations performed during this
project, the presence of DO in 1 wt% NaCl solution revealed a detrimental eﬀect on
==PAGE:59==
49
the corrosion resistance of the 13Cr stainless steel. This eﬀect was mostly pronounced
by the reduced passivation performance revealed by the recorded CPP curves and also
by the pitting events which increased with the DO content during the free corrosion
tests. The cathodic process was found to be the dominant factors controlling the
Ecorr, which led to delayed passivation, thus reduced corrosion resistance.
The small dimension of the oxide ﬁlm limited the possibilities for deeper analysis, such
as quantiﬁcation of its elemental composition and analysis of its physical structure.
Nevertheless the 13Cr stainless steel can be said to have very poorer passivation
performance, with presence of DO, even at very low level.

Conclusion 7
In this project, the eﬀect of DO in 1 wt% NaCl solution on the nature and stability
of the passive oxide ﬁlm formed on 13Cr stainless steel were investigated. For this
purpose, various electrochemical and characterization techniques were applied.
• The results from the anodic CPP tests revealed that the passivation performance
of the material was signiﬁcantly reduced by the presence of DO. This was mostly
evident on the potential (Ecorr) raise when the DO concentration was increased.
The cathodic process was found to be the dominating factor controlling the
potential raise.
• The results from the Long term OCP tests also showed that the corrosion resis-
tance of the material was reduced by the presence of DO. This was pronounced
by occurrence of pitting corrosion.
• The XPS and EELS analysis revealed that the passive ﬁlm on the samples,
tested under deaerated and aerated conditions, was essentially composed of
Fe2O3 and Cr2O3. The diﬀering feature was that the sample from the deaerated
case suggested relatively higher content of Cr-oxide, which indicated more stable
passive ﬁlm.
• Regarding the dimension of the oxide ﬁlm from both cases, no major diﬀerence
could be seen in their thickness, which were estimated to 3-4 nm from the STEM
images. Contrarily, the XPS depth proﬁles suggested thicker oxide ﬁlm for the
deaerated case, since it required longer etching time before reaching the bulk of
the material. The small dimension of the oxide ﬁlm was a limiting factor for
deeper analysis that could reveal its formation mechanism.
Considering the overall corrosion performance of the 13Cr stainless steel, its corro-
sion resistance appeared to be very poor in the NaCl solution, regardless the DO
concentration.

Issues & Future Work 8
8.1 Experimental Issues
During this project, many issues have been encountered, which limited the amount of
conducted experiments. First of all, the access to the corrosion laboratory was limited
only 3 days per week, which particularly limited the possibilities of conducting the
long term OCP tests. Secondly, the XPS equipment was damaged after performing
the two measurement on the samples from deaerated and aerated conditions. Finally,
as the TEM and especially EELS are techniques that use very advance equipments,
getting access to them required long waiting time.
8.2 Future Works
Regarding the thickness of the passive oxide ﬁlm, controversial results were revealed
by the XPS and STEM analyses. The XPS results suggested thicker oxide ﬁlm, while
no thickness diﬀerence could be observed from the STEM results.
Few aspects to consider for future investigations could be the following:
• STEM investigation could be performed on the polarized samples, as the ma-
terial under polarization may grow thicker layer, from which better diﬀerences
could be detected. The oxide of these samples could be compared to that of a
reference sample, i.e. not exposed sample.
• Since the depth proﬁles showed better diﬀerences regarding the thickness of
the samples from the long term OCP tests, same depth proﬁle analysis could
be applied on the polarized samples, to eventually show clearer diﬀerences. In
addition quantitative comparing of the present phases could be performed.
• Potentiostatic hold at predetermined potential value could be done, in order to
investigate the oxide ﬁlm development using the STEM-EELS technique.
