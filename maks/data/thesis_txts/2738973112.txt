THESIS
Optimization of tuned mass damper for resilient structures
Abstract
In the present thesis, it is demonstrated utilizing numerical optimization approach using Cuckoo Search (CS)
algorithm, inspired by the breeding behaviour of Cuckoo birds, to find the optimal tuning of tuned mass
dampers (TMDs). A proposed solution is created within the framework of single-objective and multi-objective
Cuckoo Search, where the excitation applied on the system is independent on the tuning. Firstly, a
benchmark is constructed as a SDOF system equipped with a TMD, here the proposed tuning principle is
designed and compared with two other existing optimal choice of parameters for TMDs given as Den Hartog
(DH) and Steen Krenk (SK), based on the closed-form expression with harmonic excitation. The results from
the proposed CS tuning compared to the existing approaches, demonstrated that the proposed solution is
reliably with similar response. From the benchmark, the proposed solution will be actively used on a MDOF
system defined as the balanced cantilever during construction phase of the Queensferry Crossing cable-stayed
bridge, for which is the case study of this thesis. The balanced cantilever is excited by a buffeting, and two
TMDs with identical properties are attatched, where each TMD is located at the tip of the structure. Here,
the results show-cased that the TMD is most effective in reducing the system’s response, when having a low
damping level of the system. The case study was compared with the Steen Krenk’s closed-form expressions,
and show-cased very similar results in reducing the response of the system, as the CS tuning. It was
discovered that when the damping level of the system increases, the relative difference between the frequency
ratios ν of CS and SK increases, due to the SK closed-form expressions assume zero inherent damping. This
discrepancy difference further increases with increasing mass ratio, µ. This indicates that the optimal tuning
frequency of the TMD is relatively sensitive to the damping level of the damped system.
Keywords: Balanced Cantilever, Queensferry Crossing Bridge, Single objective, MOCS, Pareto front,
NSGA-II, TMD, MTMD, Cuckoo Search, Numerical Optimization.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
The economics of modern design and construction dictate an efficiency in material volume for structural design.
This has increasingly led to the desirability to create slender and flexible structures[1]. Structures such as slen-
der bridges, wind turbines, chimneys, and tall buildings are typically designed to withstand various vibrations
through their overall stiffness, geometric shape and inherent structural damping properties. However, these
structures typically oscillate with long natural periods, which makes them particularly vulnerable to dynamic
resonance when exposed to low frequency excitation. Excitation loads are usually wind, seismic, -and human-
induced vibrations, and low frequency structures are especially susceptible to dynamic resonance in wind, as
wind energy is highest at the low frequencies[51]. Increasing the lowest frequencies is difficult, as by increasing
the stiffness this will also require an increase of the mass[2].
Thus, the role of damping becomes a crucial aspect of structural design. To address this challenge, supplemental
externally mounted damping systems - such as passive, active or an intermediate form, semi-active dampers,
are therefore often implemented. These systems can be retrofitted to existing structures without any further
modifications, for which offers a convenient “plan B” for both designers and clients.
The definition of the
standard passive tuned mass damper (TMD) is given by three key parameters: mass, stiffness, and damping,
which are the ”tuning” parameters to calibrate the TMD in order to reach optimal peak vibration amplitude
reduction around resonance. This is typically defined within the dynamic amplification factor (DAF) or the
frequency response function (FRF), where the goal is to minimize the peak response |Max(X)|, where X is the
complex response amplitude vector.
In this study, multiple tuned mass dampers (MTMD) will be utilized as passive type dampers.
The case
study is the Queensferry Crossing cable-stayed road composite bridge in Scotland, and the focus area is during
construction phase given the balanced cantilever, as shown in Figure 1.1. Two symmetrically placed TMDs,
one at each end of the tip, with identical properties, will be used.
1.2
Aim and Objectives
This study aims to calibrate and optimize the three critical tuning parameters of tuned mass dampers: mass,
stiffness, and damping, to achieve a balance that is both economically viable and practical. Specifically, the tun-
ing will be justified for the construction phase with two temporary TMDs of the case study; Queensferry Crossing
cable-stayed bridge. The calibration and optimization will be carried out using numerical simulations that em-
ploy evolutionary computation techniques, specifically single-objective and multi-objective Cuckoo Search (CS).
This approach aims to tune the mass, stiffness, and damping of the TMDs under the MOCS framework, and
damping and stiffness for the single-objective CS, to effectively mitigate structural responses, particularly to
wind-induced buffeting loads. The critical phase for a structure due to wind effects is the construction phase,

The study will compare the numerical optimization approach with the classic design method with closed-form

2.1
Cuckoos’ Behaviour and Idealization
Cuckoo Search (CS) is a metaheuristic optimization algorithm introduced by Yang and Deb [8]. The meaning of
”Meta” in metaheuristic is ”higher-level” and ”heuristic” means ”method of discovery”, which combined means
”higher-level of heuristics”[24],[23]. Metaheuristic algorithms are frameworks that are used to find the optimal
solution for different complex problems that cannot be solved using traditional methods. These algorithms
are nature-inspired in such examples given as genetics (Genetic Algorithms, GA), swarm behaviour (Particle
Swarm Optimization, PSO) and breeding behaviour (Cuckoo Search, CS), which are used to identify the global
optimum of a problem by exploring the total search space. Although, there is no guarantee that the algorithm
will find the best solution due to the random nature of the algorithm. Thus, they may not always find the
global optimum. The benefit of metaheuristic is that it can handle complex and quite large search spaces, and
they do not require an initial starting point for the optimization problem, which is in favour of solving complex
problems, where the initial conditions are unknown[20].
The CS idea is inspired by the breeding behaviour of cuckoo birds, they have an aggressive reproduction strategy,
and combined with an efficient random walk behaviour that is exhibited by many animals and insects - defined
as L´evy Flight[10]. Some cuckoo species given as Ani and Guira cuckoos lay their eggs in communal nests,
for which they may also remove other’s eggs to increase the hatching probability of their own eggs. There are
quite a number of species, for which practice obligate brood parasitism by laying their eggs in the nests of other
host birds, but it is often of different species. The new brood-parasitic Tapera cuckoo species have evolved, for
which specialize them in mimicry in colour and pattern of the eggs of some host species. This will reduce the
probability of their eggs being discovered and thereby increase their reproductivity for the next generations[18].

1. ”Each cuckoo bird lays only one egg at a time and dumps it in a randomly chosen nest.”
2. ”The best nests with high-quality eggs will be carried over to the next generations.”
3. ”The number of available host nests is always fixed, and the egg laid by a cuckoo bird is discovered by

The idealized rules are defined for an artificial cuckoo, for which only one egg can be laid at a time. Given in

a mature cuckoo. High-quality eggs are equivalent to the best solutions, and is the case where the cuckoo egg
is not detected by the host bird. The second rule uses an elitism strategy, in which it passes the best solutions
onto the next generation. Lastly, the third rule indicates that the number of host nests are fixed. If the host
bird discovers the cuckoo egg, it means that it is a worse solution that deviates from the best solution value, and
the cuckoo egg get killed, or the nest will be abandoned. Furthermore, for the last rule, this last assumption
can be approximated by the fraction pa of the population of nests which are replaced by new nests given by new
random solutions[8]. pa is probability of discovery of alien eggs, also called switching probability. If pa = 0.25,

more detail in section 2.2.
The aim is to use the new and potentially better solutions (cuckoos, eggs, nests) in order to replace a not so
good solution in the nests[9].

2.4
Multi-objective Cuckoo Search
The focus of this thesis is the use of two objective functions (bi-objective) both as a minimization problem,
for which provide a pareto curve with optimal solutions, also known as non-dominated solutions, representing
the possible tradeoffs between objectives. The theory for multi-objective cuckoo search (MOCS) use the exact
same formulas defined in section 2.2, specifically when generating new solutions. The idealized rules defined
in section 2.1 will also change to accommodate multiple-objective needs.
The multi-objective optimization
framework extends beyond a standard single-objective problem, as outlined in section 2.3 for CS. Multi-objective
optimization requires a sorting process, which is independent of the algorithm used, because the sorting purpose
is to organize the non-dominated solutions. This process is efficiently handled by the Non-Dominated Sorting
Genetic Algorithms (NSGA-II)[19], which operate with two goals that are governing of solving multi-objective
optimization based on pareto based methods - convergence to pareto front and diversity among the non-
dominated front.
A multi-objective optimization problem consists of a number of single-objective optimization problems, that
requires simultaneous optimization of these single objectives. Multi-objective problems comes with the trade-off
law, where improving one objective may compromise another. This means that in a multi-objective problem,
different objective functions can be in conflict with each other and this yields in a set of optimal solutions, also
named pareto optimal solutions - compared to single-objective problems only a single optimal solution will be
provided.
The approach in section 2.3, including the steps to solve the single objective problem for CS given in Table 2.1,
can not be directly used in solving multi-objective problems - only as generating new solutions based on Cuckoo
Search algorithm.
The most common way to solve multi-objective problems is by pareto based methods,
which use a dominance concept to address conflicting multi-objectives.
Solving a multi-objective problem
using pareto dominance concept consists of identifying the set of non-dominated solutions, that represents the
possible tradeoffs between the objectives[17]. Therefore, for solving multi-objective optimization problems based
on pareto based methods two goals are important, and can be visually observed in Figure 2.5:
1. Convergence to the pareto-optimal front
2. Diversity among the non-dominated front.
It can be seen in the second goal that the solutions are converged into a very small region of the pareto front,
which prevent to give the overall idea of what the pareto front is of the given problem. It should be noted that
the achievement of one goal does not necessarily achieve the other goal[15].

2.4.1
Dominance-based Ranking: First Goal
The first goal of multi-objective optimization is to converge to the pareto-optimal front, which the dominance
concept can help with. The concept of the dominance are given below[15]:
A solution x(1) dominates solution x(2), if
1. x(1) is no worse than x(2) in all objectives, and
2. x(1) is strictly better than x(2) in at least one objective.
If x(1) ⪯x(2), then:
• x(2) is dominated by x(1),
• x(1) is non-dominated by x(2)
Computationally implementing the dominance concept utilizes three dominance features, for which are defined
as the following[15]:
1. Dominance Rank: How many solutions is a solution dominated by (plus 1)?
2. Dominance Count: How many solutions does a solution dominate?
3. Dominance Depth: At which ”front” is a solution located?
The dominance rank utilize the principles for the concept of dominance as given earlier for how a solution
x(1) dominates solution x(2), that depends on their objective values. This can be easily visualized using only
a minimization problem. Consider a population size of 12, as shown in Figure 2.6. By looking towards the
coordinate (0,0), one can check for solutions within a specific solution’s rectangular area (blue), defined by
vertical and horizontal dashed lines around that specific solution (green circle). Solutions where 1 is written
are the non-dominated solutions, because no other solutions fall within their blue rectangular space. For the
dominance count, as also depicted in Figure 2.6, the goal is to look towards the top right corner. By drawing
vertical and horizontal dashed lines for each solution, one can determine how many the specific solution is
dominating. The pink rectangulars indicates that three solutions are not dominating anyone at all and are
therefore assigned the value 0.

2.4.3
Modification on MOCS
To extend the single-objective to the multi-objective frameworks, contributions to efficiency in convergence and
diversity are essential[17]. The three idealized rules in section 2.1 must be modified for MOCS in multi-objective
optimization problems with K different objectives. Here, the first and last rules shall be modified in order to
incorporate the multi-objective needs[18]:
1. Modified: ”Each cuckoo lays K eggs at a time, and dumps them in a randomly chosen nest.
Egg k corresponds to the solution to the kth objective.”
2. ”The best nests with high-quality eggs will be carried over to the next generations.”
3. Modified: ”Each nest will be abandoned with a probability pa and a new nest with K eggs will be
built, according to the similarities/differences of the eggs. Some random mixing can be used to generate
diversity.”
The initial study on the original multi-objective Cuckoo Search (MOCS) was introduced by Yang and Deb[18].
According to another study[17] the primary issues with the original MOCS approach include the following: 1)
High computational complexity due to considering multiple eggs for each cuckoo, equivalent to the number
of objective functions in the multi-objective problem. 2) The absence of a leader selection strategy. In the
standard CS, the best solutions are used to globally update the current generation. An efficient and simplified
variant of the MOCS is proposed by A. Kaveh and T. Bakhshpoori [17], for which includes: 1) By utilizing
an archive consisting of primary and secondary populations to select and keep the non-dominated solutions
at each generation, this approach avoids the pairwise analogy used in both CS and the original MOCS. This
approach shows that low computational complexity can enable CS to satisfy the multi-objective needs without
considering multiple eggs for cuckoos. 2) Achieving a good spread of solutions in the pareto front, which is
crucial in the multi-objective optimization process, along with convergence to the pareto-optimal set. These two
aspects are key features of the Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II), a leading algorithm
known for its fast non-dominated sorting approach and diversity preservation[19]. The leader election strategy
will be explored by considering the cuckoo belonging to the first optimal front with less number of neighbouring
cuckoos will be selected as the nestbest[17]. (In the code that I will be using, only the first solution given with
non-dominated front-1 sorting will be the best solution selected.)

2.5
Why CS is more efficient
The benefit of selecting Cuckoo Search compared to other algorithms is its simplicity of few user-defined
parameters, the random walk given as L´evy flight, low sensitivity of user-defined parameters[10], and guaranteed
global convergence[27]:
1. Few user-defined parameters: The CS algorithm is easy to implement due to its few user-defined
parameters. This simplicity makes it accessible for users who are not specialized within numerical op-
timization to simply use it. In contrast, many other algorithms require quite more user-defined tuning
parameters, for which need to be adjusted for each new problem.
2. L´evy flight = random walk: The CS algorithm employs a random walk given as the L´evy flight,
which consists of many small steps and occasional large-steps, which provides an efficient search pattern
- enhancing the algorithm’s exploratory capabilities.
3. Low sensitivity to the choice of user-defined parameters: The performance of the CS algorithm
only have a small effect on the performance by the choice of user-defined parameters. This low sensitivity
means that an “off-the-shelf” implementation of the CS is likely to yield good results immediately[10].
4. Guaranteed global convergence: It has been proved that CS can satisfy the global convergence
requirements and thereby has guaranteed global convergence properties[27],[9].

the performance metric Dg (y-axis) proposed by Yang and Deb[18], which defines the distance or error between

3.3
Tuned mass damper
In modern structural design, there is an increasing focus on minimizing material usage, yielding to the construc-
tion of lighter and more slender structures. Consequently, these structures are more susceptible to environmental
loads such as strong winds and earthquakes. The dynamic responses induced by these loads can become signifi-
cant, potentially yielding to structural failure if not properly controlled[32]. A particularly effective solution for
mitigating high vibrations is the implementation of passive tuned mass damper (TMD), and will be elaborated
here in this section 3.3. A TMD is a device that consists of a mass, a spring and a viscous damper, for which
is attached to a vibrating main system to dampen unwanted vibrations. The damper’s natural frequency is
tuned to closely match the main system’s natural frequency, the vibration of the main system causes the TMD
to vibrate in resonance and dissipate energy[30]. TMDs are especially effective when the structure is excited at
its resonance frequency, for which corresponds to when an excitation load exactly excites one of the vibration
modes of a given structure.
The concept of using passive TMDs for controlling and reducing vibrations in dynamic systems was first proposed
by Frahm in 1909[33]. Passive TMDs are advantageous because they do not require an external energy source,
making them an attractive option for vibration control. Many high-rise buildings and major bridges worldwide,
such as the Taipei 101 skyscraper, which employs a pendulum damper (approx. 700 tons), and the Normandy
Bridge, which used large temporary TMD to suppress lateral oscillations due to wind, are equipped with TMDs.
The tuning parameters of TMDs are critical as they directly influence the response of the main structural system,
and must carefully be determined. Typically, these tuning parameters are the mass mTMD, stiffness kTMD and
damping coefficient cTMD of the TMD. To assist in this process, several researchers have developed closed-
form expressions for optimal TMD tuning under harmonic and white noise random excitation[6],[71]. In this
study, a numerical search procedure, with the help from Cuckoo Search CS, is employed to find the optimal
tuning parameters for TMDs, applied to both a benchmark single-degree-of-freedom (SDOF) system and a case
study involving a balanced cantilever. The study includes a comparison with the closed-form tuning calibration
proposed by Steen Krenk[6]. No correction factors to any closed-form expression will be used in this study due
to the inherent damping of the system are not accounted for in the expressions - only the simple under harmonic
excitation from Krenk[6] will be utilized.

3.3.3
Closed-form expressions - Steen Krenk
According to Steen Krenk’s tuning procedure, the primary objective is to minimize the peak vibration amplitude
|x| using a TMD. A secondary objective is to limit the relative motion |xd| of the TMD, due to limitation of
space. Thus, optimal TMD characteristics should aim to minimize both |x| and |xd| around the resonance. The
mass and stiffness of the TMD are crucial, as the TMD must be exact resonance synchronized with the structure
to achieve optimal interaction between the main system m and the TMD mass mTMD[37]. By formulating the
equation of motion for a SDOF-system equipped with a TMD, where the structural damping is considered

3.3.4
Damped systems and numerical search
Optimal damping can be characterized either as the damping that results in a ’flat’ maximum in the structural
amplification, or a ’flat’ maximum in relative frequency motion. This yields to balanced amplitudes, preventing
the structural response from being dominated by any particular peak[37]. In reality the main concern is around
the resonance, therefore, with a much flatter DAF curve from Steen Krenk’s method it is more desirable, as
the amplitude is more constant in a broader frequency band. It has been demonstrated that optimal frequency
tuning can be determined through frequency analysis as defined previously, particularly by identifying the two
neutral points A and B. However, when the optimal frequency ωTMD is correctly tuned, it leads to an identical
damping ratio for the two coupled modes of vibration in the interaction of the structure and TMD, as shown for
the undamped system under harmonic excitation, and is approximately ζp ≈1
2ζd. If the SDOF system possesses
inherent damping, ζp, the aforementioned equations do not account for it, and correction factors or numerical
optimization tools, such as Cuckoo Search, may be necessary for accurate tuning. In damped systems, there

5.2
Excitation Wind Load used on Case Study
Wind-induced vibrations of bridge girders can be defined into the following types: Buffeting, vortex-induced
and self-excited vibrations. Buffeting results from fluctuating forces induced by turbulence and occurs over
a wide range of wind speeds, and the dynamic response due to buffeting increases monotonically with higher
wind speeds. It is important to note that buffeting does not lead to catastrophic failure[50]. Although, it can
cause fatigue issues and affect the bridge’s functionality. This study will focus exclusively on buffeting as the
wind-induced vibration affecting the bridge girder.
During the construction phase, given in the state of the balanced cantilever; partially completed bridge girders
are especially vulnerable to wind-induced vibrations due to the lack of torsional continuity before the whole
structure’s completion. In most cable-supported bridges, the vertical frequencies remain relatively constant
during and after construction, as the vertical stiffness of the girder is primarily derived from the cable system.
In contrast, a significant portion of the torsional stiffness originates from the girder itself. Consequently, the
torsional stiffness of the girder may increase by a factor of two or more from the start of construction to its
completion, for which often results in vertical and torsional frequencies being quite close during construction[50].
For this study only the first two modes, which the first mode is tilting vertically of the deck and bending of the
pylon, and second mode is lateral movement of the deck and torsion of the pylon.
Slender bridges are typically designed to withstand wind-induced vibrations through their irregular shape design
of the deck, which helps to reduce vortex shedding. Another approach for vibration control is stiffening the
structure to move the natural frequency to where the vibration level is more acceptable, but this also adds
more mass to the structure[51]. Additionally, one can also add supplementary damping, such as Tuned Mass
Dampers (TMDs) together interacting with the inherent structural damping to control the vibrations.
Slender bridges typically oscillate with long natural periods (low-frequencies), which makes them particularly
vulnerable to dynamic resonance when exposed to low-frequency excitations.
Low-frequency structures are
especially susceptible to dynamic resonance by wind, as wind energy is highest at the low-frequencies[51]. In
this thesis, the structure will be subjected to wind load excitation, which will be detailed further in chapter 6.
The analysis will focus only on the fluctuating wind load. In the actual Queensferry Crossing, temporary tie-
down stays were installed as the cantilevers extend to stabilize against buffeting[53]. In this case study, two
temporary tuned mass dampers will be utilized to stabilize against buffeting.
5.3
Criteria for the Case Study
The primary objective is to reduce the vertical deflections in the deck and the bending moment of the pylon
base by utilizing the dynamic loading given with turbulence (u and w) in the oncoming flow to excite the first
mode of the system. This reduction will be achieved through the use of two Tuned Mass Dampers (TMDs) with
identical properties, placed at each cantilever end. These TMDs shall be designed to reduce at least 20% of
the bending moment of the pylon base, which is a pre-defined criterion for this study. In some cases, this wind
load may govern the design of the pylon base, allowing for the attachment of temporary tuned mass dampers,
which can result in reduction of the pylon’s cross-sectional dimensions.

Chapter 6
Finite Element Model & Force Setup
In this chapter 6, the setup of the finite element (FE) model for the balanced cantilever is presented, along
with the definition and outline of the objective wind load excitation. The FE-model is constructed as a 3D
finite element model, where all structural components are treated as 3D beams using the MaxiFrame tool.
MaxiFrame, originally developed by Simon Mathias Gren at DTU[55], has been further extended and utilized
in various thesis papers, such as [56] -[58], to be used in dynamic analysis.
The FE-model in this study
is therefore established by using MaxiFrame Dynamics, which is provided by the author, Andreas Grøndahl

6.4
The Excitation Wind Load on the structure
The excitation load objective of the case study is to stabilize against buffeting of the balanced cantilever during
construction phase. Buffeting is a phenomenon where fluctuations in natural wind cause the bridge to vibrate
due to the wind’s turbulent nature[50]. These vibrations result in the bridge structure responding randomly
in one or more of its natural modes[64]. The total wind load on a bridge deck, qtot(x, t), is the sum of the
time-averaged mean wind load, ¯q(x) the fluctuating wind load, q(x, t), and the motion-induced wind load,
qae(x, t), given in the Equation 6.7[65]:
qtot(x, t) = ¯q(x) + q(x, t) + qae(x, t)
(6.7)
Wind turbulence induces fluctuating wind loads on the structure, causing it to vibrate, and when these vibrations
are significant, a dynamic response analysis is necessary. This study focuses on the dynamic part of the wind
force. For simplification, the time-invariant mean wind load, ¯q(x), defined as the static part, and the motion-
induced wind given as the dynamic part, qae(x, t), caused by the interaction between flow and structural motion,
will be neglected. Thus, the emphasis is on the dynamic part caused by turbulence-induced buffeting forces,
q(x, t) [66]. This simplification aims to provide a somewhat accurate approximation of the wind load to be used
in the dynamic analysis, and evaluate the performance by the tuning of the tuned mass dampers given in its
effectiveness of the CS algorithm in providing an optimal tuning solution.

1. The airflow component and mean wind speed vector in the longitudinal direction U(z) + u(x, y, z, t).
2. The airflow component in the lateral direction v(x, y, z, t).
3. The airflow component in the vertical direction w(x, y, z, t).

The dynamic response of a slender bridge structure due to buffeting increases monotonically with higher wind
speeds. While buffeting does not lead to catastrophic failure, it can cause fatigue issues and affect the bridge’s
functionality. The fluctuations in the oncoming wind can be defined in the frequency domain by the power
spectral density (PSD) of each velocity component relative to the frequency of fluctuation, often referred to as
the turbulence spectrum. The turbulence spectrum illustrates that the energy distribution of the fluctuating
wind varies as a function of frequency[50].
Turbulent flow is viewed as a composition of different types of vortices, which can be described through the
spectrum. Vortices move and rotate, with larger vortices rotating slowly and smaller vortices rotating faster.
The average size of the vortices within the flow is referred to as the integral length scale and can be visualized
as a three-dimensional structure, simplified to a football shape. According to Taylor’s hypothesis of ”Frozen
Page 63 of 117
==PAGE:78==

Turbulence”; the motion within the flow follows the wind and, due to rotation, moves up and down, forming
the vertical component, w. Short vortices result in high frequencies, while large vortices correspond to low
frequencies. The frequency is connected to the size of the vortex, and the Fourier transformation of the velocity
turbulent u or w component provide a spectrum, which is the fingerprint of the turbulent flow. This energy
distribution can be modelled using different proposed spectral density forms for the longitudinal turbulence
component, such as the von K´arm´an and Eurocode models, which describe the composition of vortices in the
flow. This turbulent energy is inside the flow, and will thereby impact the structure[68].

Structural and Aerodynamic Damping
The aerodynamic damping is approximated using Rayleigh damping as described in chapter 3. This includes the
inherent damping defined by the Eurocode for composite structure, combined with the aerodynamic damping
specified in the modal Rayleigh damping ratio.
The interest of damping given for mode 1, which is our
target mode, is to use an aerodynamic damping given for the vertical mode.
It is important to note that
this aerodynamic damping is specific to this case study, though similar cases have been seen for the vertical
mode to reach up to 5-6% documented by Ramboll. The value of 6% is for the total damping of structural and
aerodynamic damping. This value is a rough approximation for special cases that is achieved by experience from
Ramboll’s projects. This is considered high but within realistic bounds. This higher damping was necessary
to address the excessive vertical deflections (up to +/- 7 m) observed when wind loads were applied to the
bridge girder. By adjusting the vertical derivative lift coefficient to 3 (from a more realistic value of 5), and
incorporating high aerodynamic damping, vertical deck deflections were reduced to under +/- 3 m. This is still
quite high compared to an expectation of +/- 1.5–2 m, but this adjustment now provides a more realistic range
in order to work with the dampers on.

Chapter 7
Optimal TMD Tuning
In this chapter 7, an idealized design of two dampers on the balanced cantilever case study is presented, and
will be tuned to dampen the target mode 1, for which the structure is excited by a buffeting load as described
in chapter 6. Target mode 1 is the dominant mode in the vertical response, which involves the vertical tilting of
the deck and bending of the central pylon, similar to the first mode of a typical cantilever. In this case study,
the multiple tuned mass dampers (MTMDs) can be considered as a single TMD because the two TMDs have
identical properties and are symmetrically placed at each tip. Therefore, the same secondary system property
can be represented by taking half of a larger single TMD, where each TMD has each identical halves. The
response with MTMDs is carried by the definition of the EOM in Equation 3.11 with augmented matrices given
in Equation 3.15 - Equation 3.17, and will be computed by Newmark time integration given in section 3.4.
The optimal tuning of the MTMD will be executed by a single-objective Cuckoo Search, based on the principles
presented in chapter 3, by minimizing the |Max(X)|, which represents the magnitude of the complex response
amplitude vector for a given DOF. Furthermore, to find both a practical and economical TMD tuning, the multi-
objective Cuckoo Search will be utilized. Its pareto front outcome will help the designer selecting a feasible
solution that achieves at least 20% reduction of the base bending moment My,base. Additionally, to check the
reliability of the numerical search by Cuckoo Search, the classic closed-form expressions from subsection 3.3.3,
are utilized and compared with the results from CS.
Two different masses of the TMDs will be presented and equivalent to mTMD = 150E+03 kg and another mass
specified by the MOCS numerical search, referred to as mTMD,A and mTMD,B, respectively. The relatively high
mass is used to mitigate the very high vertical dynamic response from the structure due to the buffeting load.
The total damping of the primary structural system is 6% for the first two modes, as defined in Table 6.10
for the design of the two TMDs given for vertical vibrations. Additionally, an investigation of a lower total
damping of 2% for the first two modes will be presented, showing that the response performance increases with
lower inherent damping when operating with TMDs.
It should be noted that the idealized design of the TMDs is to target mode 1, although the response for the drag
modes, is also initially presented given only for the uncontrolled case response with 2% total modal damping
for the first two modes.

7.2
Tuning procedure of the MTMDs
To mitigate the high vertical vibrations, two TMDs will be attached to the balanced cantilever, each placed
at the tip with identical properties. The target mode is mode 1, which is defined by the tilting of the deck
and bending of the pylon, typical of a fixed cantilever. As shown in Figure 7.3, the dominant vertical response
corresponds to mode 1. These two TMDs can effectively be represented as a single TMD due to their identical
properties, using two damper devices to emulate one damper.
The design of the two TMDs is specifically
located at the tip, where the largest amplitude occurs, and where mode 1 is normalized to unity at TMD
location. TMDs are in particularly effective when the structure is excited at resonance, which is where the
largest amplitudes occurs. The effectiveness of the TMD diminishes when it is located further from the node of
maximum amplitude. If not placed at the idealized location at the tip, the mass of the TMDs would need to be
increased to achieve the same effectiveness. Therefore, is the tip the idealized (optimal) location of the TMDs.
The TMDs need to be exact resonance, synchronized with the structure, where the tuning of the spring-mass
creates a resonance for which interacts with the structural resonance[37].
The classical closed-form expressions from Steen Krenk, defined in subsection 3.3.3, will be used to calculate
the two calibration parameters for the TMDs: cTMD and kTMD. These expressions are based on a harmonic
excitation, with the mass of the secondary system, mTMD, being preselected. The kTMD will be obtained by
Equation 3.20, and cTMD is determined by Equation 3.23. The results from these classical expressions, which
do not account for inherent damping, are provided in section 7.3. The essential of the classical expressions, is to
demonstrate the reliability of the proposed method given by the numerical search framework by using Cuckoo
Search.
To dampen the first vibration mode, a simplification is employed by using a fictive load that exactly excites
this target mode 1. This approach serves as a calibration method for the TMDs independent of the actual
dynamic load, such as the buffeting load. The goal is to reduce the dynamic response caused by the buffeting
load, thereby decreasing the base bending moment of the pylon. In order to do so, the TMDs needs to be
tuned correctly, and this is done by an idealized environment working within one mode given as the target
mode 1. This approach outlines the main idea behind the proposed numerical search executed with the help
of the Cuckoo Search algorithm. The optimal tuning of the MTMD will be determined using a single-objective
Cuckoo Search, following the principles presented in chapter 3. The objective is to minimize |Max(X)|, for
which represents the maximum magnitude of the complex response amplitude vector for a given DOF, which is
the TMD location where the largest amplitudes occur. To design the fictive load the definition from Equation 3.4
has the force amplitude F, and will be used to create an equivalent mode shape targeting specifically mode 1.
Once the target mode shape is normalized at the TMD location, a unit value at DOF 3 and 483 for mode 1
is achieved, given as -1 and 1, respectively. Thus, the force amplitude will excite the targeted mode 1, given
as a tilting of the deck by defining a unit value at DOF 3 with -1 and DOF 483 with 1. Additionally, to
find a practical and economical TMD tuning, the multi-objective Cuckoo Search will be utilized. Its pareto
front outcome will help the designer in selecting a feasible solution that achieves at least 20% reduction in the
base bending moment My,base. A detailed overview of the choices for tuning within the single-objective and
multi-objective Cuckoo Search numerical optimization procedures is provided below in subsection 7.2.1 and
subsection 7.2.2, respectively, before presenting the results given in section 7.3.
The ”Preselected” TMD Masses
It should be noted that the preselected mass of the TMDs is mTMD = 150E+03kg for each TMD, chosen
to provide a good and noticeable performance due to the high total damping ratio of 6%. These are quite
large TMDs, typically seen in applications for the lateral movement of bridges, such as the Normandy Bridge
during its construction phase, which may had utilized 50 tons TMDs at each tip of the balanced cantilever[72].
However, the TMD masses used in this study are considered as a special case due to the high vertical responses
induced by the buffeting load. By employing the MOCS algorithm, it is possible to find both economical and
practical solutions. The pareto front generated by MOCS allows for the selection of a feasible solution that

The primary goal of the parametric optimization using the single-objective CS algorithm is to maximize the
effectiveness of the TMDs. The objective function is therefore designed to maximize TMD effectiveness in
reducing high vertical vibrations. However, one could consider creating a second objective function, for which
multi-objective optimization will be utilized, that specifically targets the robustness of the TMD by achieving
a flatter curve in the dynamic amplification factor (DAF) or a non-dimensional magnitude quantity curve.
This will not be pursued in this study, as the focus is on maximizing TMD effectiveness by minimizing the
maximum magnitude of the complex response amplitude given for a specific DOF, defined as |Max(X)|. The
robustness objective function is detailed in the work of Houng Cao, as described in the papers [31] and [32].
Cao’s approach involves using the drift in the structural stiffness, for which is defined in discretizing the DAF
curve equal to [-χi%, +χi%], where χi represents the drift in the structural stiffness. His optimization process
includes two steps: the first step is similar to the objective function used in this study, which aims to minimize
the peak vibration amplitude. The second step focuses on the robustness of the device, results in a decrease
in effectiveness due to the trade-off law in multi-objective optimization. For a comprehensive understanding of
this multi-objective optimization problem with robustness, please refer to Cao’s papers.
Additionally, another paper given in [35], focuses on maximizing the economical and practical aspects of the
TMD design by reducing all three tuning parameters mTMD, cTMD and kTMD. In this study, the authors,
Sadegh Etedali & Hojjat Rakhshani, successfully reduced seismic excitations on two different benchmark build-
ing while maintaining performance effectiveness. Drawing inspiration from this approach, the case study aims
to deliver practical and economical solutions for the TMD design by generating different solutions for selection,
particularly when a specific criterion is defined, indicating a higher-level information of the choice of selection.
The specific criterion in the case study is to at least minimize the base bending moment with 20%. By using
the MOCS algorithm, the pareto front can be created and offers a number of optimal trade-off solutions for
selection. This approach helps quickly identify tuned optimal TMD parameters to meet the criterion for My,base,
as each numerical optimization can be time-consuming if generated by single-objective alone. Therefore, MOCS
will be utilized to provide feasible solutions for the designer to choose from, ensuring the defined criteria are
met while offering practical and economical TMD solutions. The size of the TMD mass can be directly related
to the cost, and optimizing for lower mass can significantly reduce expenses.

Discussion
A TMD for which is tuned to the most dominant structural mode is most effective. This perspective is crucial
because the wind excitation frequency content is not a single frequency. Thus, if the frequency content of the
excitation deviates from the structure’s natural frequency, then the proposed numerical optimization approach
may not cause sufficient response reduction. Therefore, the proposed solution, which involves creating a fictive
load, for which exactly excites the target mode, may not be effective if the target mode is not the dominant
structural mode. This limitation is also evident if multiple natural frequencies are closely spaced within the same
vertical or horizontal modes, depending on whether vertical or horizontal TMDs are being designed. However,
the case study demonstrated that both vertical and horizontal modes had a dominant mode in their direction
of mode motion, and the TMD worked effectively to reduce the system’s response. Another perspective of the
numerical search is the time to find the optimal tuning, which can almost take a day for a such MDOF system
given for the case study.
The proposed solution is independent of the dynamic load, offering a simple and accurate method for targeting
a dominant mode, for which needs to specifically to be dampen.
In the presence of damping level of the
system, the optimal parameters obtained through the numerical search provided good results, with performance
comparable to Steen Krenk’s closed-form expressions. The proposed solution provides exactly equal peaks in the
non-dimensional magnitude quantity by utilizing the transfer function Equation 3.4, and approximately equal
effective damping ratios in the combined effect at both investigations of damping level of 6% and 2%, for which
satisfy the system property. The comparability of the two approaches’ reduction of response show-cased similar
results, which makes the proposed CS tuning a reliable solution. Although, this highly similarity, where Steen
Krenk’s closed-form expressions, which are based on a harmonic load, may rely on the projection of considering
an idealized environment as the CS solution, where a specific mode is targeted, and the wind excitation response
is not a white noise. Both methods can be said to work sufficiently well for the random loading of the wind
excitation, especially for the CS tuning results, though the SK method with 6% damping level of the system,
requires a correction.
Initially, a different numerical approach were attempted to be depended on the dynamic wind excitation.
However, challenges arose, particularly with the definition of the objective function being dependent on the
dynamic load. The initial approach involved minimizing the peak displacement of the response, normalized to
the uncontrolled response, such as
xmax,dis,con
xmax,dis,uncon , as a simple single-objective function, originally inspired by the
study in the paper [35]. It was advised to work only within the time-domain. However, difficulties emerged in
reducing the amplitude or magnitude of the response within the time-domain, particularly with wind excitation,
which exhibit a relatively stationary dynamic load response. Formulating an expression to characterize the
stationary random load, can best be executed by using the spectral density function to minimize the response
variance of the structure, and is within the frequency domain, which is executed in several studies such as [42]
and [71].
Other objective functions that were attempted and being depended on the random loading, were to minimize
the Root Mean Square (RMS) of the displacement for the combined system with TMDs, defined as an effective
magnitude. This objective function did reduce the dynamics response sufficiently, but provided a low damping
coefficient cTMD, leading to high peaks in the magnitude curve and a resonance point far away from the two
peaks. This also increased the relative motion of the TMD, xd, which were then managed by creating a constraint

when tested in the non-dimensional magnitude curve, the peaks were not balanced, and the system property of
equal damping ratios was not satisfied. Working within the time-domain can be challenging for wind excitation,

Chapter 8
Conclusion and further work
In this thesis, a numerical optimization approach utilizing Cuckoo Search algorithm, inspired by the breeding
behaviour of Cuckoo birds, was shown to be an accessible and effective algorithm, even for those with little
experience in metaheurisric algorithms. Specific features of the Cuckoo Search that makes it one of the lead
metaheurisric algorithms is its L´evy flight, defined as the random walk, which can simply be defined by a power
law distribution. Additionally, the algorithm’s insensitivity to user-defined parameters, as demonstrated in the
sensitivity analysis of the benchmark. A benchmark given as a SDOF system equipped with a TMD was created,
with the proposed tuning principle of minimizing the maximum complex response amplitude, |Max(X)|, and
compared with both closed-form expressions by Den Hartog and Steen Krenk (based on harmonic excitations).
The results show-cased that the optimal tuning nature by the proposed solution using CS, produced comparable
optimal tuning outcomes, demonstrating its reliability and applicability. The validated proposed solution was
then applied to the case study of the balanced cantilever during the construction phase of the Queensferry
Crossing cable-stayed bridge. By using MaxiFrame Dynamics the FE-model was created by 3D elastic beams
with different simplifications assumed, and thereby able to achieve the correct first two modes of the global
system of the actual balanced cantilever. The buffeting forces were designed by achieving wind speeds from
Ramboll’s internal tool, and transform them to forces acting directly on the bridge girder from mode 1-81.
The mitigation of vertical deflections and high bending base moment were achieved by placing two symmetrical
TMDs with identical properties.
A problem statement was defined specifically to use the numerical optimization by Cuckoo Search (CS) to min-
imize the overall vertical deflections of the deck, which leads to high bending moment acting on the base pylon.
The objective was to achieve at least 20% reduction in the base bending moment My,base. This was addressed
using the tuning capabilities of the CS algorithm for both single-objective and multi-objective optimization
problems. The single-objective design aimed to maximize the effectiveness of the TMD, for which a proposed
objective function was created. To achieve both practical and economical solutions, MOCS was employed to
identify the best solution to reach our goal of a 20% reduction in My,base. The MOCS utilized a pareto front,
which provided optimal set of solutions to select from. Two masses were proposed: mTMD,A = 150E+03kg,
which was a pre-selected TMD mass, and mTMD,B = 80.443E+03kg selected by the pareto front as a feasible
solution. The feasible solution from MOCS provided a reduction of 20.43%, satisfying the problem statement
in this thesis.
The performance of the tuning by CS with the established objective function, show-cased that the TMD is
most effective when having a low damping level of the system. The proposed solution by CS achieved exactly
equal peaks in the non-dimensional magnitude curve with sufficient damping, and approximately equal effective
damping ratios for the combined effect, thereby satisfying the system property. The case study was compared
with the Steen Krenk’s closed-form expressions, and show-cased quite similar results in reducing the response
of the system, as the CS tuning. The results in the case study concluded, that when the inherent damping
increases, the relative difference between the frequency ratios ν of CS and SK increases, due to the SK closed-

mass ratio µ. This highlights the influence of inherent damping on the tuning process, and indicates that the
optimal tuning frequency of the TMD is relatively sensitive to the damping level of the primary system.
The proposed solution operates within an idealized environment to dampen specifically the target mode, which
effectively works best when the mode is the dominant mode in the excitation frequency content. It provides an
easy method for achieving optimal damping and frequency of TMDs, particularly in damped systems. This is
why numerical search methods are widely used, as they are the best and easiest way to account for the presence
of inherent damping. Steen Krenk has developed different expressions of tuning formulas for TMDs, and here
in this thesis only harmonic excitation are used, because other formulas given for white noise would not work
effectively for the buffeting load. The harmonic excitation tuning did show-case some satisfying results similar
performance in reducing the response as CS, but there were some discrepancies in the frequency tuning given
for the system property of equal damping ratios, especially when the damping level was high with 6% with
noticeable unbalanced peaks.
The work presented in this thesis does not conclude here, as numerical search optimization is an efficient,
accurate and straightforward method for tuning TMDs with inherent damping. Unlike closed-form expressions,
such as Den Hartog and Steen Krenk, which assume low damping levels and do not account for inherent damping,
and thereby provide an approximate solution (unless corrections are added), numerical search allows you to
tailor your goal specifically to minimize responses that need to be reduced, while incorporating the inherent
damping. As demonstrated, the optimal frequency tuning of the TMD is sensitive to the damping level. The
solution from the SK showed a conservative optimal tuning than the CS, with both larger TMD stiffness and
damping, kTMD and cTMD. Therefore, for practical and economical tuning of TMDs, the proposed solution in
this thesis offers a simple and accurate approach.
The following further works are included below:
• Two-Step Objective Function: Investigate the creation of a two-step objective function using a multi-
objective approach to maximize both the effectiveness and robustness of the TMD. This robustness is
similar to the closed-expression by Steen Krenk (detailed in subsection 3.3.3, based on harmonic excita-
tion), where the DAF curve would appear flatter across a larger frequency band - like a flat plateau.
• Dependency on the excitation: This thesis created an efficient tuning principle without dependency
on the wind excitation load, for which is a random loading. Therefore, it will be interesting to explore
the use of Cuckoo Search directly within the frequency domain, utilizing the spectral density function to
minimize the response variance of the structure, while imposing constraints on the TMD stroke.
• Comparison with Other Algorithms: It is also interesting to investigate other algorithms than Cuckoo
Search, and compare them against a benchmark with the proposed solution tuning, in order to assess the
performance of Cuckoo Search relative to other algorithms.
• Application to Different Loading Types: Further investigation into other loading types, such as
earthquakes or pedestrian loads, to see how well the proposed solution can reduce the response, and still
satisfy the system property of the optimal frequency tuning.
