Abstract
Future deep space mission to explore icy moons will want to have the ability to autonomously
navigate by the aid of optical and inertia sensors and obtain high resolution surface images
in certain locations. To enable this, the right optical navigation instruments and methods are
needed. This thesis describes how such an optical system can be used to image the surface
of Europa, and what the needed speciﬁcations are to provide such images. It also deals with
the navigational methods that the system can use in order to better estimate the trajectory in
space and ﬁnally how it can be used to track surface patches for obtaining images at diﬀerent
angles. This enables the creation of 3D elevation maps, which are are of great use for future
lander missions.
The optical instrument of choice is a telescope with the ability to change its viewing direc-
tion, and also obtain the pointing direction in space from the aid of star trackers. The telescope
must have a resolution of 0.5m/pixel at 50 km altitude and the image sensor is 4 mega pixels
with a FOV of 1.17◦. The length of the telescope can be made to only 20 cm long even with
a focal length of 1.35 m. A design of a telescope which can have these requirements is proposed.
The navigation system can visually ﬁnd a targeted moon in the ﬁeld of view at relatively
long distances, even with multiple objects in frame. From the size of the observed object and
the aid of ephemeris data the spacecraft can ﬁnd the position in space and correct its trajectory
data for better navigation. Close to the surface it is possible to use feature tracking algorithms
to locate certain surface patches, but the SIFT tracker proposed did not perform well on the
icy landscape.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
“All truths are easy to understand once they are discovered; the point is to discover them.”
- Galileo Galilei 1
Are we alone in the Universe? Possibly mankind’s biggest question. The universe is a very
big place and space travel between star systems is still just a part of science ﬁction. Meanwhile
we are actually capable of sending spacecraft to all the planets in our own solar system. The
search for life starts within our own solar system. In the early days of space exploration Venus
and Mars were the top candidates, but after the discovery the ﬁrst visits by space probes,
disappointment followed and life was not thought to exists in the solar system outside Earth.
Anywhere else it was either too hot or too cold for life to exist as we know it. This was until the
icy moons in the outer solar system got a heightened status as potential life harboring worlds
following discoveries made by the Voyager, Cassini and Galileo probes and the famous Hubble
space telescope. Some of these icy moons are even called water world’s, because there exists
evidence that they contain large oceans of liquid water underneath their icy surfaces. Knowing
the importance of liquid water to life on Earth, the possibility of ﬁnding life is more relevant
than it ever has been. This time it is based on scientiﬁc evidence.
To know for sure we have to go there and gather the evidence, by unlocking the secrets
that these icy moons hold.t he large distances and hazards involved means robotic 2 explo-
ration is the only way to make these discoveries happen by today’s technology. One of the the
major challenges with deep space exploration missions is that the spacecraft have to operate

CHAPTER 2
Problem at hand
2.1
Problem Formulation
The goal is to propose a initial design of an optical telescope that can be used as a part of a
navigation system, in order to obtain high quality surface images of icy moons in deep space
missions. These missions are of highly interest as proposed and planned by the major space
agencies like NASA and ESA.
The optical telescope which is being addressed here will be part of a system of existing
navigational sensors in order to know the absolute attitude and position of the spacecraft. By
using these sensors the aim is to use the telescope for tracking icy moons from afar and au-
tonomously recognize and track multiple surface patches close to the surface during a ﬂy-by.
The requirement for imaging multiple patches not lying adjacent to each other demands rapid
re pointing of the camera during the ﬂy-by, this means the telescope needs to have a mechanism
that allows it to steer the incident beam of light without moving the spacecraft. Imaging of
the surface patches from several incident angles enables reconstruction of 3D elevation maps.
The existing sensors that aid in the navigation are for example the micro Advanced Stellar
Compass (µASC) to obtain absolute attitude pointing of the telescope, while inertia sensor and
ground support on Earth (the Deep Space Network) are responsible in providing the position
of the spacecraft in space. Unfortunately there are uncertainties in the position in deep space,
there are for example no Global Positioning System (GPS), and the masses of Jupiter and its
moons have still quite large uncertainties compared to Earth. The optical telescope can aid
in improving the position and get a more accurate trajectory estimation. For surface tracking
there exist diﬀerent methods, but a generalized one does not exist for every purpose. The

2.2
Limitations
There are many icy moons out there, but the one of highest scientiﬁc interest at the moment is
Europa. It is chosen as the icy moon of interest and it is the one that will be considered further.
Many missions are requested of and wanted by scientists, and many of the icy moons are
of interest. The Europa Clipper mission proposed by NASA/JPL is considered as the base
platform spacecraft for the optical telescopic navigation system.
The spacecraft is assumed to know its approximate position in space and have access to a
ephemeris look-up table for its targets. A completely lost in space scenario, where the spacecraft
and targets are at unknown positions in space will not be dealt with, since the topic of this
ﬁeld is large on its own. This is however touched upon and referred to later.
2.3
Mission Proﬁle
The mission is going to be a multiple ﬂy-by deep space mission to Jupiter’s icy moons, where
the target is Europa. The platform is a yet unmade spacecraft, but will heavily be inspired by
the Europa Clipper proposal. The scientiﬁc instrument in question, is a telescope which can
use the aid of existing star trackers to ﬁgure out the direction it looks in space. The spacecraft
position is known from both Earth tracking stations and inertia navigation sensors on-board the
spacecraft. The unique thing about this telescope is not just the ability to know the pointing
direction, but also to steer the incoming beam of photons, thereby changing the view direction
of the telescope.
9
==PAGE:11==
With these aids it is possible to have an independent camera system that can obtain pictures
of the surface of Europa with a large coverage area. It can obtain images of surface patches
that are not adjacent to each other and at diﬃcult angles to the orientation of the spacecraft.
By this the camera system is not restricted to the orientation of the rest of the spacecraft
and the scientiﬁc output will be greater. In a ﬂy-by mission where there are only a limited
number of passes of the icy moon, the possibility to select and image certain areas of inter-
est is the key driver. In order to take images, the knowledge behind is the target ephemeris,
position of spacecraft and pointing direction of the telescope. From this the telescope knows
where to point, but as a ﬁnal step visual conﬁrmation is needed to ﬁnd the right surface fea-
ture. When an area is imaged, the surface features are stored in memory. Figure 2.1 shows
the basic sketch of the mission. From far distance the telescope observes the target and can
improve the pointing and trajectory coordinates. At near distance the telescope can take im-
ages at diﬀerent locations and also ﬁnd the same surface patches for imaging at diﬀerent angles.
To date only a handful of high resolution images of the surface of Europa exist. The surface
is still considered alien to us. In previous missions there were made swaths of lower resolution
images only where the spacecraft ﬂew by. The problems with higher resolution and narrow
ﬁeld of view is the accuracies required to acquire these images. It becomes an issue to point
the camera in the right direction and ﬁnding areas that are wanted, but due to the apparent
likeness of the surface it could be hard to recognize similar areas from diﬀerent observations.
The high resolution images that are possible to obtain from a narrow ﬁeld telescope, could make
it possible to ﬁnd future landing sites and also increase the understanding of Europa’s surface.
With much higher resolution images the only limitation would be the amount of storage space
and bandwidth, for transferring images back to Earth.

CHAPTER 3
Theory
3.1
Icy Moons
The outer solar system is ﬁlled with icy moons orbiting the gas giants like Jupiter and Saturn.
The reason these icy moons became interesting is the possibility to ﬁnd some sort of life. Many
of these moons are thought to have vast oceans of liquid water beneath their icy surfaces.
Recent observations of Enceladus by the Cassini probe have observed liquid water shooting out
from cracks in the surface. Another observation is of huge water plumes from Europa observed
by Hubble space telescope. The most recent study also done with the Hubble is showing that
Ganymede also have a liquid ocean under the surface.
Combining these observations with
orbital imagery of surface geology, and data obtained from magnetometer measurements, there
is enough evidence that icy moons are now considered as extremely good candidates for ﬁnding
life elsewhere in our own solar system. If life is found on icy moons, then life truly can exist
almost everywhere in the universe far from the warmth of a star. For more information about
the search for life, the reader can look in appendix 7.1.
3.2
Target Discussion: Europa
This book will focus on Jupiter’s moon Europa as the icy moon of interest. Europa is of highly
interest by international space agencies. NASA and JPL have in their plans to send an orbiter
to Europa to study its surface and determine the ice-shell thickness and presence of water below
to determine its habitability. This NASA/JPL mission has been named the Europa Clipper and
is described in more detail section 3.3. ESA on the other hand is also planning an orbiter mis-
sion where it will explore the icy moons of Jupiter including Ganymede, Europa and Callisto.
This mission is presently called JUICE for “Jupiter’s Icy Moons Explorer”. A stunning global

3.2.1
Europa Information Desk
Europa was initially one of the four Medici moons discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610 aided
by his telescope invention. He initially named Europa with the Roman numeral of II. The name
was later changed to Europa to avoid confusion in the numerals when more moons were dis-
covered. In ancient Greek Mythology Europa is the daughter of Agenor. Europa was abducted
by Zeus in the form of a spotless white bull. He brought her to Crete where she had many
children of his, including Minos. Unfortunately the Europa in interest is not located on Crete
and is slightly more complicated to visit.
Europa is the fourth largest moon of Jupiter after Io, Ganymede and Callisto. It is slightly
smaller than Earth’s moon with a radius of about 1560 km. Europa is orbiting Jupiter each
3.551 Earth days in s synchronous orbit like the most moons in the solar system. A synchronous
orbit means always the same side of the moon is facing Jupiter. Indications are however that
Europa might have a non-synchronous orbit and that the whole surface slips around every 50
000-250 000 years due to torques on the equatorial caused by Jupiter’s gravity. This is a very
13
==PAGE:15==
long time and Europa can be considered synchronous for the duration of a space mission. [22]
Europa has one of the smoothest and brightest surfaces of all the bodies in the solar system.
It is covered with frozen water ice and has very few craters telling us that the surface is young.
The most distinct feature of the surface of Europa is large globally stretching cracks in its
surface. The color in some places especially around cracks is red-orange and the composition
of this material is unknown. Just looking at this planet is intriguing and screams for more
exploration.
Earlier Exploration of Europa
Most of the things we know about Europa is obtained from mainly the Galileo and Voyager
spacecraft. In total eight spacecraft have visited Europa, but only one, the Galileo spacecraft
has returned for multiple ﬂy-bys. Only 10% of the surface has been imaged by the Galileo
spacecraft with a resolution of 250 meter per pixel or better. 75% of the surface is covered in 500-
2000 meter per pixel resolution. More data could have already been acquired, but unfortunately
Galileo had a problem with the high gain antenna. The antenna failed to deploy and prevented
a global image map of Europa. Galileo spent 4 years in storage after the Challenger accident and
the lubricants for the deployment mechanism had evaporated, no one thought of lubricating
it again before it was launched. The low gain antenna had to be used instead limiting the
bandwidth considerably and eﬀectively reduced the amount of transmission it could send back
to Earth. The storage of data before transmission was therefore even more critical, back then
the storage was on a 114 megabyte tape recorder. Before the Jupiter insertion a command
made the tape recorder rewind for 15 hours before they could shut it oﬀ, because of the risk of
tearing the tape from stresses caused at rewind, the end of reel section was declared oﬀlimits
and all the Io and Europa data from the insertion phase was lost. This eliminated storage of
25 layers of tape. [40]
The last spacecraft to visit the Jupiter system was New Horizon on route to Pluto and
the next spacecraft that will go in Jupiter orbit is the Juno spacecraft. Although Juno is not
intended to study its moons, it mission might even provide some more details about Europa.
Meanwhile we have to wait until the Europa Clipper or similar spacecraft is sent there.
Tidal Eﬀects and Evidence for Subsurface Ocean
Tidal eﬀects describes the reasoning for why Europa has a subsurface ocean even though the
surface is as cold as 100 Kelvin. Without the tidal heating the liquid water would have been
frozen long ago. The eccentricity in the orbit around Jupiter is 0.0101 and this is enough to
cause diurnal tides in Europa, heating its core due to frictional stress. If an ocean exists, and
there are strong indicators that it does, the stretching of the surface of Europa should be around
30 m due to the diurnal tides. But if the ice is frozen solid throughout, the stretching should
14
==PAGE:16==
only be one meter. Europa is also in orbital resonance with Io and Ganymede. This resonance
was ﬁrst understood by Laplace and it shows that it enhances their mutual gravitational eﬀect
making the tides more powerful. It might explain the huge water geysers observed by the Hub-
ble space telescope. [22]
Libration is the slightly shifting of the face in a synchronous orbit. Libration around the
equilibria point in the synchronous orbit around Jupiter is expected to be around 100m if the
ice shell is rigidly attached, if not the libration are expected to be 3 times more. Also if Eu-
ropa has an obliquity of non-zero, the surface would drift around slowly changing the facing to
Jupiter over time which is thought to be the case, but the movement would be so slow that it
would be diﬃcult to detect during a short time span like a space mission.
Europa does not create its own magnetic ﬁeld, which indicates either a frozen core or a ﬂuid
but non convecting core. Europa interacts with Jupiter’s magnetic ﬁeld through eddy currents
for shielding its interior from changing external electric or magnetic ﬁelds.
These currents
create an induction ﬁeld. The measurements made by the Galileo spacecraft’s magnetometer,

Environment
The environment on the surface of Europa is deﬁnitely inhospitable on the surface. There
is deadly radiation from Jupiter’s radiation belts, that will destroy organic molecules on the
surface. The thin (near non-existent) atmosphere which has a pressure of just 0.1µPa doesn’t
warm the surface much and the average temperature is only 100 Kelvin. The ice here will be
rock hard. An astronaut without protection would be frozen to death, have boiling blood and
sudden expansion of organs due to the vacuum and if that is not enough, exposure to lethal
ionizing radiation. The radiation dose on Europa is roughly around 5.4 Sievert per 24 hours.
A manned mission to Europa will demand advanced radiation protection for the astronauts.
Electronics in an unmanned spacecraft is also in need for protection although they can with-
stand higher doses than cells in the human body. The radiation exposure can be minimized

by applying thick layers of dense metals, but the most eﬀective way is for the spacecraft to
spend as much time outside the radiation belt as possible and orbit in a wide ellipse around
Jupiter. [19]
A more friendly environment is underneath the ice, the ice stops the radiation after just
a meter and the further we travel down the ice the warmer it gets until we reach the ocean.
However there is complete darkness down there, no sunlight can penetrate the thick ice. The
heat present is created by the tidal friction inside the planet.

3.2.3
Terrain Morphology
The geology on Europa is complex and relatively new compared to the history of the solar
system. The surface is thought to be renewed every 60 million years or so, making Europa a
very geologically active planet. It is much more active than its sister planets Ganymede and
Callisto, that have much geological older surfaces in comparison. It looks that Europa is even
more active geologically than Earth which has much slower moving plate tectonics.
Understanding the surface and interior of Europa will also aid in the understanding of other
icy moons such as Ganymede, Enceladus of Saturn, Triton of Neptune and Miranda of Uranus.
The surface of Europa is covered in features such as linear ridges and cracks, bands, chaotic
terrain, mountains, plains and impact craters.
Linear Features and Ridges
Linear features can be seen all the way from globally to the best resolution images obtained.
Every part of Europa that has been imaged has in fact ridges in them. It is the most common
morphological feature on Europa. The ridges on Europa can range from 0.1 km to over 500 km
long. They can be up to 2 km wide and several hundred meters tall. They come in diﬀerent
forms classiﬁed as; troughs, raised-ﬂank troughs, double ridges, ridge complexes.
Troughs are linear grooves that can be hundreds of kilometers in length and hundreds of
meters wide and few hundred meters deep. They have a v-shaped proﬁle, but the wider ver-
sions have the bottom ﬂat. They are individually structures probably made from tension in
the surface. Although they occur frequently they are not dominant in the surface topography.
An example of a trough can be seen in ﬁgure 3.2.

Raised-ﬂank troughs, sometimes called single ridges have one of the edges raised above the

The most common ridge form by far, and the terrain feature that Europa is known for, is
the double ridge. It is fundamental in shaping the landscape. They consist of two raised crests

The ridges that come in pairs have an inner and an outer slope, the inner slopes are rel-

ridge and has a smaller drop than the outer. The inner slopes suﬀer from typical mass wasting.

Bands
Bands are a common feature on Europa. They can be seen as old features compared to many
of the other features, due to the fact that they have other features breaking into them and
transforming the bands. Europa’s bands can be described from plate tectonics such as the pull-
apart bands, and another type called bright bands. It is not clear if the bands have common
origin or are related somehow. The bands do not seem to have formed recently in the geologic
history.

Pull-apart bands
The bands resemble somewhat the sea-ﬂoor spreading on Earth, where the ice is pulled apart
by some force, could be ocean forces or tidal forces. The surface in these bands can be relatively
smooth, to very rough and fractured with lines running along track. The younger bands are
darker in color than the older ones as they seem to brighten with time. Some bands seems to
have more than one episode of deformation and the preexisting band has been reactivated in
the ventral spreading axis.
Pull-apart bands are often called dilation bands. The typicality of bands is that the surface
terrain that the bands interrupts or margins can be reconstructed to ﬁt extremely well together
if the bands are removed. Three main types of pull-apart bands can be diﬀerentiated; Smooth
bands, ridged bands and lineated bands.

Chaotic Landscape
This terrain as the name suggest is very diverse and uneven. It covers around one fourth of
Europa’s surface. This landscape consists of mostly matrix landscape and plates. The chaos
landscape can cover from kilometer sized to 1300 km across.
The preexisting surface is disrupted into a lumpy and rugged matrix landscape that contains
old surface plates, that are cracked into blocks, that sometimes are elevated and tilted above the
surrounding terrain. They are also shifted in position from previous locations. The matrix is
the rugged materials between the ice blocks. The jigsaw puzzle of the chaotic terrain can often
be placed to ﬁt together with surrounding plates. The process making the chaotic terrain is
unknown, but may be due to fully or partial melt through areas in the ice surface. Typically for
these areas is the distinct reddish colored substance seen in images, still no one knows what this
substance is or where it comes from. The theory is that it is deposits from the ocean. The typi-
cal chaos terrain is shown in ﬁgure 3.10, these chaotic areas have typically the reddish color in it.

Description of Spacecraft Platform: Europa Clipper
The Europa Clipper is a proposed orbiter mission by Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) and
California Institute of Technology (CALTECH) in collaboration with NASA, to study Jupiter’s
moon Europa. The mission will conduct detailed reconnaissance of the icy surface of Europa
by multiple ﬂy-bys in orbit around Jupiter. The mission’s key goal is to explore Europa by
investigate its habitability. The habitability will be determined by conﬁrming the existence
of an ocean, characterizing the composition of the water in and beneath the ice and evaluate
the geological processes of the surface. Including ﬁnding high science interest localities, such
as sites with recent geologic activity and characterize hazards and potential places for future
landing sites. [33]
The mission got its oﬃcially funding early in 2015 when NASA received enough support
by the US government to make a concrete formulation of the Europa Clipper mission. By now
the mission is in its early stages, but the concept has already come a long way [10]. A concept
strawman of the Clipper is shown in ﬁgure 3.21.
Figure 3.21. Europa Clipper strawman concept [33]
The reconnaissance objectives will be in two parts. One deals with the engineering objec-
tive of site characterization, where the goals are to ﬁnd the distribution of surface hazards,
ﬁnding the load bearing capacity, structure of subsurface and the regolith thickness. The goal
is to ﬁnd potential good candidates for future landings. Second is of scientiﬁc value, where the
composition of surface materials, potential of geological activity, the presence of near surface
water and if there exists active upwelling ocean material. A telescopic reconnaissance camera
will be needed for both purposes, where stereo imaging is wanted for 3D mapping of the surface

of interest.
The Europa Clipper is planned for launch in the early 2020s on board the Space Launch
System (SLS), and the trajectory can be a direct Hohman transfer. If instead the Atlas V-551

Europa is tidally locked with Jupiter and the position of Europa in the orbit will determine

is determined by the direction towards Jupiter, the Anti-Jovian point or the 180◦meridian is

Radiometry
Radiometry is the study of electromagnetic radiation, in this case visible light. The main goal
is to characterize the brightness of objects in space.
3.4.1
Brightness
The amount of light falling on to a surface is called the irradiance, it is the power per unit
area. The image system measures the irradiance, this is the brightness we are interested in.
The viewing geometry can be seen in ﬁgure 3.23.
To know the scene brightness of features on Europa will depend on how the surface is
illuminated, its shape and how it reﬂects light. In deep space the Sun can be considered the
only signiﬁcant source of light, although Jupiter will reﬂect massive amounts of light making
it clearly visible. The amount of light radiated from a surface is called radiance and is the
energy ﬂux emitted from the surface. A three dimensional object’s projection on an image are
determined on the position of the object relative to the image system and the attitude of the
object. For determining the radiance we must know how strong the Sun irradiance is on the
surface we want to observe.

3.4.5
Photometric models
Photometry is the quantitative measurement of reﬂected and emitted radiation from an object,
here it is planets which is measured. The goal is to extract information of the surface properties
from the variation in the intensity of sunlight on diﬀerent viewing angles. A wide variety of
viewing and illumination angles is required and needs to be obtained from spacecraft when
observing planets in the outer solar system.
From these observations photometric models can be made to predict the radiance of a
planetary body from diﬀerent viewing geometry. The common for all these models is that
they not are fully accurate, but can be used as approximations for what has been tested under
laboratory conditions.
The simpler models relate the reﬂectance from incidence angle, emission angle and solar
phase angle. More complex analytical models incorporate these with modeling radiative trans-

of mathematical models that together describes the reﬂectance from the surface. The main
components in such models are a description about the albedo and scattered light from the
regolith, how photons are scattered in a volume of grains on the surface, a light scattering
model that describes light scattering on macroscopic roughness scale and that also described
the opposition eﬀect. The opposition eﬀect is a sudden linear increase in the brightness when
observed from very small phase angles. [37]
The complexity of light interactions makes these models life time studies and even today
after many decades these remain incomplete. They are regularly updated as new corrections
are made when set to the test in laboratory. One model that is seen as one of the best is the
Hapke model it has a wide application to both photometric and spectroscopic data, not at least
to icy surfaces which is most important when considering Europa.

3.5.4
Charged-Coupled Device (CCD)
A CCD image sensor converts the incoming photons into electron charges at the semiconductor-
oxide interface. Each pixel is represented by a p-doped MOS (Metal Oxide Semiconductor)
capacitor. The capacitor accumulates electric charge proportional to the incoming light in-
tensity, the irradiance, from the interaction with photons over the capacitor. When incident
photons hit the photosensitive area, holes and electrons are generated in the depletion region
of the MOS capacitor. The electrons generated migrate into the potential well associated to
each pixel and stored before readout. This charge is linearly proportional to the photon ﬂux
incident up to the capacity of the well.
The full well capacity determines the upper amount of charge it can store. Over exposure
images have reached the level of the full well capacity. The greater the full well limit, the
better dynamic range and SNR. Dynamic range is the ratio between brightest and faintest
object that can be discriminated in the image. Larger pixel size usually means a higher charge
well capacity.
The dynamic range of a CCD is given by the full well capacity over the read noise, shown

Mirrors and Lenses
Pinhole projection has disadvantages such as diﬀraction issues, instead incoming light is led
through either lenses or mirrors. They gather ﬁnite amount of light onto the imaging sensor.
The arrangement of mirrors and lenses in a camera make up the optical part of the telescope.
The ﬁrst astronomical telescope made by Galileo Galilei was a simple refractor telescope
using a set of lenses. With this invention he was able to discover the moons of Jupiter and
question the world view of the time and get in trouble with the Catholic Church. Modern
astronomical telescopes mostly use mirrors instead of lenses as the primary focusing aperture.
Lenses suﬀer from chromatic aberration which are avoided when using mirrors due to the same
refractive index. The mirrors can be made with very high accuracy by polishing only a single
side of a quartz surface and then coating the surface with a very thin layer of reﬂective metal.

Mirror coating

reﬂectivity of the mirror if dielectric materials are stacked in a special way, where the layers
have alternating high and low indices of refraction [23]. Important things to consider for coating
material for a telescope for space is reﬂectivity of the material, tarnishing, radiation resistance
and degradation rate. There is a need for a protective layer of anti-reﬂective coating on top, for
protection and prevent sublimation. Lenses are usually used along with the mirrors to decrease
aberration eﬀects. Dielectrics made of rad-hard silica as the BK7 or similar can be used for

3.6
Computer Vision and Tracking
Computer vision is the ﬁeld of vision sensing for a computer. The visual sense for humans
is the strongest sense, and gives us huge amounts of information about the world around us,
and makes us able to interact with it. The aim for computers is to be able to do the same
things with the aid of vision. Since vision is a very complicated sense for humans, the ﬁeld of
computer vision is also far from trivial. The computer should be able to make three tasks with
its vision; obtain image and process it, detect objects of interests and classify them, and in the
end be able to use the information from the objects in the image to perform a task. [25]
3.6.1
Feature Selection and tracking
A major challenge for a tracking system is to be able to track surface features as the viewing
angle and orientation is changed. A tracking system that relies on surface features of Europa’s
terrain needs a selection of good features that can be successfully tracked frame to frame. Too
much dissimilarity in a feature between images will make the feature unusable for the tracker.
A good feature will have little dissimilarity and thus have little change in brightness, rotation
and scale. Good features are repeatable, e.g. they can be found if the images are slightly
changed from another. Good feature trackers are rotationally and scale invariant, also eﬃcient
and easy to implement.
Hough Transform
Hough transform deals with recognizing predetermined shapes in the image and is great when
you know what to look for. A shape can be parametrized and found in the Hough space image.
The Hough space often consists of several dimensions due to the shape of the object. The
47
==PAGE:49==
hough can be made very fast and achieve near real time performance in even large images. The
downside with Hough is the need for clear edges and smoothing ﬁltering of images to enhance
its edges is necessary. [5]
Corner Detectors and feature descriptors
Extracting feature points are often considered more robust and much less complex than ex-
tracting other features such as lines with the Hough transform. Feature selection might be the
most computationally heavy task, and when the location of the features are known in each
frame, the correspondence problem can be solved relatively easy. The classic feature selector
is based on corner response function over the image. Shi and Tomasi described the impor-
tance of corners in their paper [28]. This can be obtained from the image gradients which is
a measure of how an image is changing over the 2D image area. The magnitude will tell how
fast the image is changing and the direction will tell on which direction the image is changing
most rapidly. The Harris corner detector is a popular one because it is simple to implement
and gives a relatively good result, but a weakness is that is not scale invariant. Another is
the corner detector made by Shi, Tomasi and Kanade [4] which has been an interest for use
in autonomous landing [8], but fail when the rotation is too large. Similarly other methods
use the same approach to compute the corner strength, but they all are relatively computa-
tionally heavy. Real time processing puts a lot of pressure on the feature selector algorithms
and other methods needs to be used. A problem with increasing the speed is the accuracy in
the tracking might go down. This is a consideration that needs to be made. The literature
on feature trackers is huge, and improvements are continuously made. Good trackers today
might get outdated in a short timespan. The downside is that each tracker are more or less
good at one particular task and not general. Tracking algorithms are especially sought after in
Space, defense and the automobile industry. For space it is in special interest when it deals with
navigational purposes for landing vehicles and autonomous navigation for rovers and spacecraft.
A descriptor of a feature is usually present to enhance the robustness of the tracking. It
makes the features distinguishable such that it is possible to match correct features. The de-
scriptor can for example tell about the size and orientation of the feature. Image correspondence
to match features can be used with aid of feature descriptors, where they try to describe each
feature. The descriptor uses image gradients that will rotate with the image. The correspon-
dence can then found by scoring the similarity between each feature in the images. This is
computed for each feature to describe the particular feature, and is based on a small patch
around each feature. The Scale invariant feature transform (SIFT) descriptor is considered as
one of the best and will be considered further in 4.5.4 [35]. The SIFT is able to recognize
features when the illumination, scale and orientation changes. As most feature detectors SIFT
rely on high contrast objects such as edges. The features are extracted by key features from the
result of Diﬀerence of Gaussians (DoG) that are applied in scale space, where a local maximum
48
==PAGE:50==
is found. DoG blurred two images at diﬀerent scales and subtracts them. It is believed that
the eye use this to extract details from images in neural processing. The orientation of the
feature is to look at the nearby area of a future and align it to the local gradient. Finally
descriptors are used to describe the feature even more by dividing the patch over the area
and making orientation histograms. The matching or correspondence of descriptors is to ﬁnd
the descriptors that matches best between the images and have a match within a certain criteria.
Other feature detectors have been made that also are scale invariant to some point. The
segmented corner detector like the Features from Accelerated Segment Test (FAST) [16] can be
used to ﬁnd corners by looking at pixels values around points instead of ﬁnding the gradients,
which can be a speed beneﬁt, although it is not as advanced as the SIFT.
Feature tracking is done on the consecutive image to ﬁnd the same features found in the
reference image with known coordinates. This is the classical correspondence problem. False
matches can occur when matching features, but a ﬁlter that rejects these can be applied that
rejects the false matches. Block matching is often used where portions of the image are checked
for the matching feature. Searching the entire image for the feature is necessary if the motion
is not known, however if the motion of the image platform is known, the search for features
can be improved, by using epipolar geometry the search for matching features can be narrowed
down into a small line over the image.

4.1
Scene Analysis
The optical camera will operate within the visible range and the near infrared region. The
chosen operational bandwidth is 400-800 nm. In order to predict the amount of light gathered
by a camera system from an icy moon such as Europa, an analysis is done where the scene
radiance is established. The scene radiance is needed to know the irradiance on the image and
has an impact when choosing the aperture size of the camera.

certain degree. The problem is that no one knows how the surface material is composed and
how it scatters light in a physical way. The only certain way to know this is to go there and
do surface sampling, which an accurate brightness model can be established upon. Since we
cannot go there yet and do surface samples, one have to use BRDF models and try ﬁtting
the parameters to the measured brightness from the mentioned space missions. And then use
laboratory studies with materials that can match the measured brightness. But none of these

4.2.2
Focal Length and Pixel Size
The choice of pixel size will directly determine the focal length of the telescope when the res-
olution is decided. Large pixels in the CCD are good because they hold a greater number of
electrons in the pixel well, the full well capacity. The larger the full well capacity, the larger
the dynamic range and SNR. The dynamic range and SNR plays a huge role in the quality of
the images. With larger full well capacity the signal to noise ratio (SNR) gets better. However
larger pixel sizes means also the focal length will get large pretty fast. As seen in ﬁgure 4.4
three resolution requirements are shown with the pixel size on the vertical axis and the focal
length on the horizontal axis. At lover resolutions it is possible to obtain larger pixel sizes at

mission to Deep Space.

4.2.3
Choosing a CCD
So what kind of pixel size are out there and what can one expect from their corresponding full
well capacity? Looking at manufacturers of high end scientiﬁc CCDs, the range in pixel sizes
are usually from around 6µm up to 15µm. This is the range which will be considered. Also the
size of CCD in terms of how many pixels it can have needs to be considered. Many pixels will
mean a larger FOV. A larger FOV will cover a larger part of the sky or the surface in one image
and reduces the need for stitching many images together when mapping, this however might
not be too big of a problem. Since we are dealing with deep space and limited bandwidth in
transmissions, large focal plane arrays will take up massive amounts of storage space that will
take forever to send back to Earth. Also the large focal plane arrays are slow to read out, such
that using the telescope as a navigational tool will be diﬃcult.
Considering the Cassini mission as inspiration, its Narrow Angle Camera (NAC) have 12µm
pixels where the CCD has 1024 x 1024 pixels. This might not seem impressive for todays stan-
dard, but back in the 1980’s when the Cassini was starting to be designed, this was very
good [18]. Its pictures of Saturn and its moons still impresses and new discoveries are being
made as the mission keeps on going. Although the Cassini is 20 years old, the bandwidth prob-
lem is much of the same when going into deep space. Today it is possible to attain scientiﬁc
CCDs with 85 mega pixels, and if more are needed the convention is to stack them side by
side. This is done with the ground based telescopes and a couple of the space telescopes close
to Earth. Such large CCDs require lots of cooling, very large storage space for the images and
long read out times. The storage space for a 85M pixel image with 15 bits each is a tremendous
156MB. A CCD closer to Cassini’s is more realistic in a deep space mission. In the table (ﬁgure

4.3
Design of Telescope assembly
Now the parameters are at hand to start designing the telescope itself. What type of telescope,
how large does it need to be, and how can it obtain 2 degrees of freedom (2DOF)? The 2DOF
ensures independence of the telescope somewhat from the rest of the spacecraft. The 2DOF
function will enable the telescope to navigate to search for targets and take multiple images at
diﬀerent location of the surface at varying angles during a single ﬂy-by. Thus increasing the
scientiﬁc output considerably. First the optical part of the telescope will be designed.
4.3.1
Optical Design
As known the focal length is 1.35 m. This length can be problematic to mount on the spacecraft
and a common method to reduce the overall length of a camera is to use a Cassegrain type
design. By increasing the secondary mirror one can reduce the length between the mirrors and
shorten the assembly. Other tricks can be used to compact the assembly such to use mirrors
and place the CCD in such a way that the overall assembly comes in a little package. Previous
missions with very long focal lengths packed into a small package is for example the High Res-
olution Camera (HRC) onboard the Proba-1. The HRC has a very long 2298mm focal length
and a mass of just 2.1kg in a miniaturized Cassegrain type design. [17]
The downside with Cassegrain is the high demands for a secondary mirrored surface which
needs to be made very accurate. The obstruction of the secondary mirror will also reduce the
amount of light coming onto the primary mirror.
Cassegrain
The Cassegrain parameters are chosen where the most important factor is the distance between
the primary and secondary mirror and the magniﬁcation of the secondary mirror. The secondary
mirror slows the convergence of the primary mirror and expands the focal length, where the
magniﬁcation factor determines how much the primary mirror focal length is expanded. If the
magniﬁcation factor is increased the overall length of the telescope will decrease, but at the cost
of larger secondary mirror and sensitivity to changes in the secondary mirror position which
will oﬀset the focal point.
The distance between the mirrors is the ﬁrst parameters that has to be chosen. The formu-
las used to ﬁnd the speciﬁcations for the Cassegrain are found in appendix 7.5, only the results
will be discussed here. [34]

Mirror coatings
Coatings will degrade in the form of sublimation where the solid coating material will go straight
to gas form without being in the liquid phase. Sublimation happens if the temperature, air
pressure and type of material under certain conditions. For coatings in space this will happen
due to the low pressure in vacuum and low temperatures that the metal will simply skip the
liquid phase. Silver is the most reﬂective material for visible wavelengths, however it tarnishes
easily when exposed to ozone, hydrogen sulphide and air containing sulphor. It is then highly
critical to coat a protective dielectric layer before exposing it to the atmosphere. This makes
silver more diﬃcult to work with. Silver also tarnishes over time which can be a problem for
long duration space missions.
A study has been on the degradation rate for silver and aluminum mirrors used on Earth [21].
Where both mirrors are coated with a protective layer of SiO2. The test batches were exposed
to the atmosphere at nighttime to resemble observations. The degradation curves are presented
in 4.21. On the left is the silver coating, it quickly degrades after 6 months when exposed to
the atmosphere. The aluminum mirror degrades much more slowly and reaches the same level
of degradation after 6000 days.
Although it was tested on Earth within the atmosphere, the study still shows how much
longer aluminum coatings can last, and if the Clipper mission will have to take the long route

coating can be as thick as 40µm.
A protective coating needs to be added on top of the coating, to protect the coating from
the environment, such as humidity in the atmosphere during making of the spacecraft, and also
oﬀer protection from radiation and prevent sublimation. But introducing another layer which
the light can reﬂect back into the coating and produce blurring due to refraction of passing
from one material to another is unwanted. The protective layer has to be anti-reﬂective to
reduce the reﬂections from about 3% to 0.01%. A broad band anti-reﬂective coating is needed
that cover 400-800nm.

4.3.3
Material Considerations
The material part is not a focus point of the book, but will be mentioned. Mass is always the
restriction when making spacecraft for deep space and materials that are strong enough and
still do the job are needed. With optics it is crucial that the materials will be stable within a
temperature range and have low outgassing. There is also the stresses and vibrations at launch,
and it is crucial that the pan-tilt mirror will have to survive this. Radiation is also a big factor
around Jupiter, where the CCD needs to be encased in a radiation encapsulation and these
involves the use of dense metals which is problematic. For the structure titanium can be used
since it is strong and lightweight. If aluminum is used the structure will need to be thicker to
compensate. The mirrors are made from high quality quartz glass.
4.3.4
Radiation Mitigation
One of the major challenges with the Jupiter system is the intense radiation from its trapped
particles in the radiation belts. This is harmful for the electronics and has unwanted eﬀects
on other materials since the radiation is ionizing i.e. it will change the molecular structure by
dislocating electrons. The radiation exposure needs to be mitigated and this can be done in
several ways. Shielding, radiation hardening and limiting the exposure time.
Shielding is the ﬁrst step in protecting the delicate CCD, it is placed within vault that is
made to sit as close as possible to the sensor. Gold can be used for this purpose since it is
very dense. The second step is to use radiation hardened electronics and sensors, these are very
expensive components that are tested thoroughly before use. The second step is to spend most
of the time outside the radiation belts. This can be done by using highly elliptical orbits or
elliptical polar orbits planned for the Juno spacecraft. A spacecraft going to Europa will need
to have more equatorial elliptical orbits around Jupiter in order to survey the surface.

Lost in Space Mode (LSM)
LSM has the purpose to ﬁnd the spacecraft position, if the position is lost or completely wrong

To make a long story short, the lost in space scenario is where the spacecraft needs to
ﬁnd its position in space, this can be done with or without aid from Earth tracking stations.
Without aid from Earth the spacecraft must use on-board equipment to ﬁnd the position. This
can be done by using stellar compasses to ﬁnd suitable targets such as moons or planets and
then triangulate the spacecraft position from the angles obtained by the stellar compass. The
chances for detecting objects of interests are greater with a larger FOV in star trackers than
the surface imaging telescope. Once two or more objects have been found with their directions,
the position of the spacecraft can be determined with vector analysis. The method of how to
obtain position in space is explained in more detail in [41].
Once the position of the spacecraft is settled within some uncertainty, the coarse tracking

Obtaining Pointing Direction from Pixel Coordinates
The celestial coordinates are along the celestial sphere and the projected image is on a ﬂat
plane. However due to the narrow angle telescope the celestial sphere appears ﬂat and can be
considered a plane when the astrometric coordinates are converted from pixel coordinates. The
warping of the sphere projected onto a plane gets larger when looking near the poles of the
celestial sphere, but the telescope will mostly look at near the equator of the sphere when using
J2000 coordinates. The orbits of the spacecraft around Jupiter will have small inclinations.
When passing close to Europa the camera can point more near the poles of the celestial sphere
than at larger distances.
The bore sight i.e. the center pixel has the coordinate of the determined pointing vector
from the star tracker vector. The angle of the camera is hardly ever arranged such that the
horizontal pixels are aligned with the plane of the celestial sphere. To convert the pixel posi-

Approximate known spacecraft position: It is assumed that the position of the spacecraft

Gibb’s method of orbit determination
Orbit determination is an old problem that goes all the way back to the ancient times. Due
to the diﬃculties of grasping the motion of the planets people thought Earth was the center of
the solar system, even in the entire universe until Kepler successfully could describe the orbits
of the planets around the Sun. The method of predicting the motion of bodies did not became
really accurate until Carl Friedrich Gauss came up with his method that uses angles only to
determine the orbit of Ceres. Gauss is considered as one of the best mathematician that ever
lived. Since then other methods have been made such as Gibb’s method and Lambert’s problem.
The problem with Gauss’ method is that it is very mathematical heavy and needs three or
more observational points. Where Gibb’s method is more straight forward to implement and
only needs in total three points, but the position vectors needs to be coplanar. Usually the
orbit can be considered on a plane within the measurement interval and Gibb’s method can be
used. Although Gibb’s method is used here, Gauss’ method can be implemented in the future
for better accuracy. [15]

ond measurement position can be established, and with a position and velocity, the orbital
elements of the spacecraft can be established and updated. The observation to the gravita-

Fine Tracking Mode (FTM)
The ﬁne tracking mode has the purpose to fully geo-localize the absolute longitude and latitude
of the target for surface mapping.
By using the improved coordinates the latitude and longitude position of the surface can
be found, but not necessarily in the accuracy required without using surface tracking. The
thought scenario is that many surface patches have been imaged in the ﬁrst few ﬂy-bys with
approximately known latitude and longitude coordinates. These are areas chosen by scientists
as areas of interest in terms of science related and future landing sites. Using surface tracking to
ﬁnd these initial images will be problematic since the surface in this resolution is still unknown.
Only much lower quality images exists. It is however possible to use surface feature extraction
and save the areas with their respective coordinates, such that in the next ﬂy-bys it is possible
to recognize them with surface correspondence, and take more images at diﬀerent angles for
3D elevation mapping.
The camera is pointing towards the surface and the area is within an error ellipse which the
camera needs to perform a zig-zag pattern to ﬁnd. When the target is found the image can be
obtained.

4.5.5
Motion Compensation
A spacecraft in a ﬂy-by trajectory past a planet or moon will have a high relative velocity. The
purpose of motion compensation is to reduce smearing without reducing the integration time
of the picture and to obtain the high resolution needed. Since less irradiance will fall on one
pixel and spread out over several pixels by the smearing eﬀect, it will also reduce the tracking
ability in the image, smearing will be a noise source. By changing the direction of the incoming
photons to counteract for the apparent motion of the target, the target will appear still in the
image during exposure.
To compensate for the apparent motion of the target, is to use the already implemented pan-tilt
mirror. This pan-tilt mirror will also have the ability to move rapidly to change the direction
of view as well to image other areas without creating large inertia disturbances to the space-
craft. At this point the motion compensation is not implemented, but is necessary to obtain a
resolution of 0.5m/pixel.
The relative velocity of the spacecraft to Europa is over 3 km/s and when the integration time
is 5 ms, it means the spacecraft would have moved 15 meters relative to the surface. When one
pixel covers 0.5m at 50 km altitude, this will make smearing over 30 pixels.

5.1.6
Feature Detection
To test the feature detector a series of images of the surface of Europa is checked with the SIFT
in order to see how well it performs. This is where the terrain morphology comes in and how
well the feature tracker will respond to the surface. Up close the Europa surface has plenty of
sharp edges and criss-crossing lines in its cracked surface and it is expected that the tracker will
ﬁnd many features. As mentioned earlier a lander needs a ﬂat surface in order to land safely
and it the so called subdued-plains are of special interest.
Images from the same areas on Europa, but at diﬀerent angles are hard to obtain. Therefore
the same images will be used twice for comparison, but the imaged is altered with rotation and
skewing the perspective by making one edge of the image smaller than the other. Most of the
prominent surface types were tried from plains to the archetype double ridges. The threshold
for matching were set to 1.5 in VLFeat, this controls the strictness of matching. The lower the
number the more matches are obtained, but at less probability they are in fact true matches.
1.5 is the default value in this program. The reference image was altered by using paint.net
and the image rotated and skewed in perspective with values, 0.79, 1.26 and 1. The result for
feature detection and matching is summarized in table 6.
The overall matching was quite poor with the threshold setting, if this was set lower, the
matching number got much higher, this however made many false matches. Some of the im-
ages were also in poor resolution since good resolution up close surface images are few. The
images that had many similarities in features was for example the boulder image and ridge
complexes. They had few hits on the matching and many were wrong. The one that stood
out was the double ridge image, however most of the matches was not on the ridge itself but
on the surrounding landscape. It is noted that the quality of the double ridge image also was
high compared to many of the other, in terms of contrast and pixels. An image that shows
good features in the double ridge image is shown in 5.10. The highest resolution image which
is a perspective view over Europa did not get that many matches and this is a bit surprising
since the image resolution is high. It could be that many ambiguities arise and the threshold
removed the matches. The subdued plains image, which is interesting since it is so ﬂat, had
many features found, this could be because of the quality in the image, but the matches were
not many on the ﬂat landscape. Most of the matches happened near cracks and imperfections
in the landscape.
The results show that the SIFT feature tracker might not be up to the task for the job
in this case. It could need a better correspondence method and having supporting ﬁlters and
statistical methods to make the matching more reliable. Making a dedicated surface tracker
could be challenging for the surface of Europa since much of the landscape seemingly has many

idea to use diﬀerent feature trackers for diﬀerent parts of the landscape. Another aspect is to
make it fast enough and still reliable, a topic not explored here.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The main requirement of the telescope is to obtain surface resolution of 0.5m/pixel from 50 km
altitude, this while avoiding a large physical size of the telescope. The CCD size chosen was
4MP with 13.5µ squared pixels which can read out an image in under 0.7 seconds in normal
operation. The required focal length is 1.35 m with a FOV of 1.17◦. By using Hapke reﬂectance
model it was found that the average radiance from Europa was almost 7W/m2sr. The diameter
of the aperture was chosen to be 13.5 cm from the diﬀraction limit, CoC and SNR. The large
contributor to noise is the dark current and the shot noise. It was found that the SNR is 343
when imaging Europa up close with an integration time of 5 ms. The maximum obtainable
dynamic range is 80dB which requires 14 bits ADC.
The telescope type was chosen to be a Cassegrain type that is miniaturized with only 20
cm length. A pan-tilt mirror is chosen to be used as the mechanism to steer the viewing di-
rection with the possibility of having a ﬁeld of regard of ±70◦in the along-track direction and
±20◦in the cross-track direction. The mirror coating was chosen to be enhanced aluminum for
durability reasons.
The navigation system is made up of three main components, the LSM, CTM and the
FTM. Only the two latter were explored. CTM can calculate the pointing towards the icy
moon and recognize it visually. The pointing direction had a large oﬀset in the Right Ascen-
sion of 0.3 degrees at 680000km, from comparing it to observer tables from JPL. From the
visual conﬁrmation the Hough transform was able to identify the right target, even with mul-
tiple objects in the frame. With this also update the direction coordinates when the target
is not the center of the image frame. Due to images used with diﬀerent resolution than the
intended system generated by Celestia, the distance measurement had up to a few thousand
116
==PAGE:118==
km error. By using Gibb’s method there was possible to obtain a velocity within 0.6m/s of the
true value. However the method of obtaining the orbital parameters are not accurate enough by
just using Keplerian methods, where especially the inclination angle is deviating more than 1%.
In the FTM, by using geodetic coordinates and transforming it to J2000, and deﬁning an
equatorial plane of Europa. It was possible to ﬁnd the pointing to speciﬁc longitude and latitude
coordinates on the surface from the spacecraft. Of future trackers, the SIFT was considered
due to its scale and rotational invariance. From testing the ability to recognize surface features
on Europa the results had poor matching capability. Another method of matching needs to be
considered and the ﬁeld further explored. The feature detection itself was capable of ﬁnding
many features in the landscape. The result of ﬁnding matches was better for high quality and
high contrast images.
6.1
Future works
In the future to improve results the pointing diﬀerence obtained compared to JPL observational
date needs to be resolved. Using more realistic images with from known camera parameters
to avoid scaling errors that happened when using Celestia, will have to be done.
It need
consideration if Gibb’s is the right method of orbit determination, or that another method
should be used. However the bigger issue is getting the right orbital elements from the position
and velocity.
A better model to align the equatorial plane of Europa to use the geodetic coordinates
correctly are needed that takes into consideration the axial tilt, libration, and precession and
nutation for making the coordinate transform more accurate.
The method of searching for targets near the surface has to be developed and a full case
study into feature trackers that can ﬁt for the Europa landscape has to be done. More complex
matching algorithms that can get rid of most of the false matches and provide a robust feature
tracking is key.
The optimization of algorithms to make them run as fast as possible was
not focused on. Existing feature tracking algorithms was used and the development of more
specialized tracking methods will do better results.
In the hardware part, a more detailed design has to be reviewed where the components,
electronics and materials can be established before building the telescope. Since the spacecraft
is not yet made therefore the mass is yet unknown.
Mass from the telescope and inertia
disturbances from the telescope was not taken into consideration. It was not focused on methods
to deal with radiation protection of equipment. Also obstruction of the spacecraft body for the
star tracker and telescope ﬁeld of view was not considered.

7.1
Search for Life
The question whether life can exist elsewhere than Earth, is one of humanity’s big mysteries,
and will have fundamental impact on our world view, religion, politics and philosophy if this is
the case. In terms of mathematics, the probability that we are completely alone in the universe
is not realistic. We might be alone in our own solar system, but this has not been conﬁrmed.
The possibility for life to exist in oceans beneath many of the icy moons in the outer solar
system dramatically increases the probability of ﬁnding life even in our own “backyard”. The
frozen outer solar system suddenly become a whole lot more interesting. It is true that Earth
like planets needs to be placed in a certain distance from its star, but if the icy moons are
habitable then life might not be so picky about the location of a planet. Space agencies like
NASA and ESA have as one of their main goals in exploration to ﬁnd life, and a big part of
that involves sending spacecraft to other planets and moons.
The search for life on other worlds is not easy.
We need to send spacecraft billions of
kilometers away that can operate autonomously and withstand the harsh space environment.
Often just escaping Earth’s gravity in a rocket is the most risky part, the rocket industry is not
nearly as mature as the aircraft industry, and launch failures are a risk. Sending a spacecraft
to the outer solar system can cost up to several billion US dollars, but rewards are often huge
and the value given back in terms of research and technology innovations and not forgetting
all the jobs this industry creates, then space travel might not be that expensive at all. The
122
==PAGE:124==
cost of space travel cannot even measure up to military spendings of nations, this topic is of
course controversial and one can debate forever over this on what is the right spending. There
is however no doubt that investing in space gives many beneﬁts to a nation and should be
emphasized more in the future.
The search for life starts with assessing the habitability of a planet. The habitability is
the environment’s ability to sustain life. We only know of life as we have here on Earth which
is carbon based and require three main criteria, liquid water, energy and organic molecules.
Other types of lifeforms may exist, but the likely requirements is that life needs some type of
liquid to mix chemical compounds and energy easily accessible. In the search for life we have
to start searching for life as we know it, carbon based life made up from amino acids. The
best icy moon candidates are thought to have liquid water containing organic compounds and
energy available for life to have developed.
Determining what life actually is, is a non trivial task, even scientists have diﬃculties coming
up with a uniﬁed deﬁnition of what life actually is. One deﬁnition is that life needs to be able to
reproduce itself, this deﬁnition is problematic because it does not includes viruses which needs
a host that can provide a cellular structure and metabolism to help reproduce the virus. One
normally consider virus as life since it is composed of genetic material and can interact with
other lifeforms. Throwing into the mix other types of life that might exists such as life based
on silicates instead of carbon, then life can become an abstract thing. The ﬁeld of astrobiology
is still immature and in its early stages of modern science. [19]

7.3
Optical Aberration
Optical aberration is blurring of images caused by the non convergence of light at a single point.
The aberration increases as the angle of the light ray increases from the optical axis.
Spherical aberration is an optical eﬀect that increases the refraction of light rays as they are
closer to the edge of a mirror or lens. This will make create out of focus points for the light at
the edges compared to the optical axis. This is unavoidable without using a perfect lens which
does not exist, however this eﬀect can be minimized using a combination of lenses.
Chromatic aberration is the eﬀect of the light rays change in refractive index at diﬀerent
wavelengths through a lens. This is important for color photography where a halo will form
around the object. Chromatic aberration can be made minimal with special lenses or avoided
completely by using mirrors.
Comatic aberration explains variation in magniﬁcation over the entrance aperture, this is
especially a problem for telescopes using a refractive mirror. When the light rays strike the
mirror at an angle, individual rays are not reﬂected into a single point, this will happen when
objects are not aligned with the optical axis.
The name comatic is due to the comet like
image distortion obtained as a result. The use of special lenses will minimize this eﬀect. More
aberration eﬀects exists, but will not be discussed here.

7.6
Orbital Mechanics
The classical orbital mechanics is known from Kepler’s equations and is one of the most impor-
tant works in astronomy and led to a revolution of understanding of how the planets move in
ellipses around the Sun. Newton built up on Kepler’s legacy and created modern math along
with the fundamental laws of gravity from Kepler’s work.
Still to this day we are using orbital mechanics from Kepler and Newton to calculate the
orbits of planets and spacecraft. Even after Einstein have proven that Newtonian physics is
wrong when approaching 1/10 of the speed of light, the simplicity of usage wins over more
complicated relativistic mechanics when low relative speeds of spacecraft compared to light
speed is used. We might wait a long time until Newtonian mechanics no longer are valid in
space travel in terms of mathematically language.

7.6.1
Orbital parameters
To describe an orbit a reference frame has to be selected, this depends on the application. For
example the planets motion around the Sun the solar system barycenter is a natural coordinate
origin, while a satellite in Earth orbit the coordinate origin would be natural to place at Earth’s
center. In astronomy the most common inertia frame used is the J2000.0 reference frame, where
the coordinate axis is pointing towards vernal equinox at a deﬁned time and the plane is either
aligned with the ecliptic or equatorial plane of Earth.
To fully describe an orbit in three dimensions one need at least 6 orbital parameters. The
orbital geometry can be seen in ﬁgure 7.1. The 6 classical orbital elements that describe an
orbit are described below. [15,44]
 Semi-major axis a. This is the length of the long end of the ellipse to the center of it.
 Eccentricity e, which describes how elliptical the ellipse is. Elliptical orbits always have
eccentricity below one.
 Inclination i, the angle measured from the plane to the orbital plane at the line of nodes.
 Right ascension of the ascending node Ω. This is measured as the eastward angle from the
vernal equinox to where the object crosses the plane from south to north at the ascending
node. This is sometimes also called longitude of the ascending node since it will give the
longitude over where the orbit crosses from south to north from the vernal equinox.
 Argument of periapse ω. Measured in angles from the ascending line of nodes along the
orbital plane to the periapse point in the orbit. Periapse is the point which the orbit is
closest to the orbiting body.
 True anomaly θ. The true anomaly is the angle from the periapse to the position of the
spacecraft along the ellipse.
The last element that must be included is the time. The time could be deﬁned as time since
start a deﬁned position or the time since periapse passage.
To begin with an initial position vector and a velocity vector for the object is known. All
the orbital elements can be found from these vectors and some knowledge about the orbiting
body. For planets and moons the ephemeris can be obtained from JPL in very high precision
using the Horizons service. The ﬁrst step is to ﬁnd the distance from the object to the orbiting
body using the position vectors for the object and mass body in 7.22.Then the magnitude of the
velocity is found for the orbiting object which is found by similar manner. it is then possible
to ﬁnd the radial velocity in that point for the object. The radial velocity is the velocity of
