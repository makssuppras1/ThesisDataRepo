Abstract
The industry demand for turbo blowers is shifting from integrally geared machines with journal bearings to high-speed
direct drive blowers with Active Magnetic Bearings (AMB). Commonly, these blowers operate at speeds exceeding the
first critical speed. The AMBs are superior in terms of efficiency and do not require lubricants which otherwise might
contaminate the medium. In order for for the AMBs to provide damping, thus stabilisation the rotor, accurate models of
the system are required. Hence, a finite element (FE) model of the rotor assembly, which is capable of representing the
real rotor at frequencies well above the first critical speed must be developed.
In this work, a rotor is designed for a high-speed direct drive turbo blower with AMBs, but design constraints prohibited
a conventional rotor design. In brief, the rotor shaft consist of one center part with two extensions which are tied to
the center part with long central screws, referred to as tie-rods. The resulting complex rotor-shaft assembly might be
referred to as a built-up rod-fastened-rotor, which turbomachinery designers recently have turned to. Such a design is
not commonly imposed by constraints, but rather motivated by advantages, and has recently been introduced into the
governing application standards.
A simple continuous rotor model of the shaft-rotor assembly is developed, but it inadequately represents the rotor when
validated with an Experimental Modal Analysis (EMA). Thus, a multibody rotor model including the tie-rods, axial pre-
tightening forces and contacts, as suggested by the latest research, is developed.
The refined model is capable of modelling the rotor characteristics, but fails to match the natural frequencies with suffi-
cient accuracy. As the coupling parameters required for the refined model are based on surface roughness parameters only
available prior to assembly, the couplings in the multibody model were based on interpolated values from other applica-
tions. To mitigate the parameter uncertainty the results from the EMA are used to perform an optimisation of the coupling
parameters, which is capable of adjusting the model to yield adequate results.

Preface
This master’s thesis is submitted as the final assignment of the two-year Master of Science (M.Sc) in Mechanical Engineer-
ing at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The master’s thesis was carried out in collaboration with GL-Turbo.
Albeit, the start- and end dates are listed as the 4th of September 2018 and 28th of April 2019, respectively, the vast ma-
jority of the work has been done in the period between the 1st of January and the submission date. One week after the start
of the project I crashed on my bike and suffered a severe concussion, which I struggled to recover from, and for which an
extension of the project deadline was granted.
The project has been supervised by Professor, Dr.-Ing., Dr.techn. Ilmar Ferreira Santos and the industry-supervisors Ph.D.
Jonas Skjødt Lauridsen and Martin Carlsen from GL-Turbo International.
Initially, the scope of the project was to simulate the rotordynamics of a high-speed turbo blower featuring active magnetic
bearings, including the control system, permanent magnet motor, and aerodynamic forces from both the seals and impellers.
However, when validating the rotor model it became evident that a simple model would not suffice. The rotor model,
which initially should have been a minor section in the report, then required immense modelling and validation effort,
and eventually became the entire project. In the end, the work and care put into the rotor model, albeit at times being
frustrating, was rewarding when the final results were made.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This project was carried out in collaboration with GL-Turbo and is part of the research collaboration between DTU Me-
chanical Engineering and GL-Turbo. It is part of the development of the GL-Turbo DT3 High-Speed Direct Turbo Blower
with Active Magnetic Bearings prototype. The DT3 is the direct successor of the DT1 test rig [4][12], the first machine
developed by the collaboration. An overview of the DT3 is shown in figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: 3D rendering of the test rig. 1 Volute, 2 Labyrinth seal, 3 Backup bearing, 4 Radial AMB, 5 Motor,
6 Water cooling channels, 7 Air cooling, 8 Axial AMB, 9 Axial position sensor, 10 Radial position sensor, 11
Tachometer.
Both machines developed at DTU feature Active Magnetic Bearings (AMB). The DT3 will use AMBs for both radial and
axial levitation, unlike the DT1 which used Passive Magnetic Bearings (PMB) for axial stabilisation. The use of AMBs
==PAGE:21==
2
require good models of both the mechanical and electrical system. The models are used for synthesising digital controllers
for the bearings which govern the stability and performance of the rotordynamic system. Damping of higher order modes,
above the operation frequency range, is not inherent in the AMB characteristics, contrary to journal bearings commonly
used for turbo blowers. Instead, the control engineer designs the controller characteristic to damp the relevant modes,
based on the rotor model. Thus, the mechanical engineer must provide accurate models of the rotor. A brief overview of
the AMB specifications is given in table 1.1.

Several challenges and issues faced during the commission of the DT1 test rig, especially with the rotor design, practical-
ities related to the desire to test with aerodynamic load, and GL-Turbo’s interest in the development of a larger prototype,

Chapter 2
Initial Rotor Model
2.1
Method and setup
A finite element (FE) model of the rotor was carried out. Timoshenko beam elements were used as given in [11]. The beam
elements have consistent mass and include gyroscopic effects, and stress-stiffening. A MATLAB code implementing the
element is given in appendix B.1.
An automated MATLAB routine for automatically meshing/discretizing the shaft based on a target element length was
written and used for modelling the shaft-rotor assembly. The routine supported manually selecting the location of desired
nodes, as it is often desired that certain node locations are fixed e.g. for adding discs bearing seals, unbalance force,
monitoring deflection at specific locations and adding overlap elements. Furthermore, it supports overlaying FE element
matrices with secondary overlapping element matrices e.g. for adding discs with distributed mass or adding shrink fit parts
to the FE model. Because, the shaft-rotor assembly is rather complex both functions were necessary. Node locations were
fixed at the discs’ center of gravity (COG), the back-up bearings’ centers, AMBs centers, and at the beginning, and end,
of any overlapping elements added. Overlap elements were added to represent the RAMB rotor lamination stacks and the
PM magnet assembly on the motor rotor. Later, in the more advanced models, overlap elements were also used to model
the impeller as described in [15].

was used to check if any of the sensor or bearing locations coincide with any of the relevant modes’ nodes. If any of the

For the purpose of this work an analysis of frequencies up to at least 1.400 Hz was deemed necessary. To be conservative,
and accounting for gyroscopic effects, the frequency range for analysis was extended to 2.000 Hz
Assessing the higher order modes it is clear that some of the modes will have poorer observabillity and controlabillity as
nodes coincide with sensor and or bearing locations e.g. the left bearing for mode 3 and left sensor at mode 6. Fortunately,
the bearing force is not lumped to a single node in reality and hence some energy can still be applied to the given mode.
Moreover, some modes nodes are located between the bearing and sensor locations. This means that vibration at the
bearing and sensor will be in counter phase, and thus that actuation has to be in the direction of, not opposite the direction
of, the measured vibration. This is inherent as the bearing and sensor are not co-located, and a model based controller is
most likely required for controlling the mode.

3.2
Experimental setup
3.2.1
Support structure
Typically support types can be categorised as either ’grounded’ or ’free’. While some consideration have to be made
regarding where and how to add the supports in order not to constrain the structure, or add unwanted damping to it, it
can generally be said that the ’free’ condition is ideal [5]. In this case the structure is not huge and an overhead crane
available in the laboratory makes it easy to suspend the rotor mid air. Furthermore, the ’free’ condition mimics the actual
conditions supporting the rotor when installed in the machine, as the stiffness of the bearings is not sufficiently high to
alter the natural frequencies of the rotor. The support structure for the EMA is shown in figure 3.2.
When measuring lateral vibration and supporting the rotor as shown in figure 3.2a the excitation direction is perpendicular
to the direction of the support which ensures that the support structure has minimal influence on the stiffness associated
with the flexible modes. However, the connection between the shaft and the rotor, shown in figure 3.2b, does add mass,
and might add damping too. While the mass is inevitable, damping, in this case, is primarily caused by friction between
moving/vibrating parts. Tightening the nut and eye nut tightly together minimises friction and hence damping which
otherwise might distort the obtained frequency response function.

eters is with a threaded stud. However, this requires modification of the test structure, which is far from always possible.
On ferromagnetic materials the accelerometers might be attached to the structure with a permanent magnet base, but better
and easier mounting can be achieved by applying a thin layer of wax to the structure and or accelerometer, which works as
an adhesive. For a curved structure, such as a rotor, it is important to balance the amount of wax in the interface between
the structure and accelerometer. Too much, and the signal quality will deteriorate, because the wax damps vibration. On
the other hand, using too little wax increases the risk of the accelerometer detaching from the structure. This concern is
exaggerated for structures with low radius of curvature.
Moreover, considerations have to be put into where to mount the accelerometer. Mounting the accelerometer at, or close
to a node will make it very hard to measure the associated mode effectively. This concern is less important for the sensor
locations when using an ’rowing output’ measuring strategy, and vice versa. Hence, it is always advisable to check whether
or not the fixed input point or output point location coincide with a node of a mode, which it is desired to measure. Prior
knowledge of the node locations from e.g. a FE analysis is an effective guide for avoiding coinciding node and fixed point
locations, provided that the analysis can be trusted. Moreover, it provides insight into how many FRFs it is required to
properly resolve the desired mode shapes. On the rotor-shaft assembly 30 points were spread out over the length of the
rotor and marked with numerated ’dymos’, as shown in figure 3.3.
Marks were first placed at important locations such as bearing and sensor locations and thereafter spread out evenly

3.3.1
Hammer configuration
A medium stiffness tip was used on the Brüel & Kjær 8202 impact hammer used for the EMA. No additional weight was
added to the hammer head. The hammer configuration is shown in figure 3.4.
Prior to carrying out any extensive measurement series an investigation of the frequency spectrum excited by the hammer
blow was carried out, by computing the auto-spectrum of the input and output signals. The auto spectrums were calculated
with the MATLAB command pwelch with the options 'onesided','psd','mean'. An example of the responses and
their frequency input are given in figure 3.5.
There is a direct link between the impulse width and the hammer/structure interface resonance, governed by the contact
stiffness and the impact hammer mass. At frequencies higher than the resonance, the force, F(ω), delivered declines
and the signal to noise ratio deteriorates. For this case the resonance frequency lies in between 800-900 Hz. Excitation at
frequencies above the resonance will be ineffective compared to frequencies below [5]. Increasing the resonance frequency
can be done by increasing the contact stiffness or decreasing the impact hammer mass. Increasing the contact stiffness
can be done by hitting a different part of the structure with a higher elastic modulus. This is not feasible, as this is usually
not possible while still obtaining the desired measuring points. Instead, tips made from different materials are provided
together with the impact hammer, so the operator can change the interface stiffness. Decreasing the mass of the impact
hammer mass can be achieved by choosing a smaller ’screw-on’ load commonly attached to the backside of the impact
hammer head.

Windowing
Windows can be applied to both the input and output signal to reduce leakage and noise. For different excitation methods,
different windows are applied. Using impact excitation it is not strictly necessary to window the signal unless the signal
quality is poor. A ’force window’ can be applied around the impact impulse if the signal is contaminated by noise. As the
only part of the signal containing useful information is the part around the impulse, the window has to preserve this part
and remove all other parts. Essentially, this can be done by using a narrow rectangular window around the impulse, but this
approach is likely to cause leakage if the window is made to narrow. A better approach is using a smoothed rectangular
window, but attention has to be paid not to loose any of the actual signal.
Windows can also be applied to the output signal when using impact excitation. This is often used for very lightly damped
structures and in the past when the signal analysis memory was limited to 12 or even 8 bit. Applying an exponential
window to structures with very light damping to artificially add additional damping can be very effective. If transients
take more then a couple of seconds to die out, the added damping can reduce the measurement time, and the amount of
data stored which otherwise would prolong post-processing time and increase memory requirements.
For the EMA of the shaft-rotor assembly windows were not applied. It was assessed that the signal quality and the available
computing power was sufficient.

Aliasing
To avoid aliasing, analogue filters are placed between the transducers and their respective signal conditioner, i.e. between
the accelerometer and the output signal conditioner and between the force transducer and the input signal conditioner. The
signal conditioners are shown in figure 3.9.
The signal conditioners include both a high-pass and a low-pass filter each. The low-pass filter may also be referred to as
an anti-aliasing filter. It is essentially a low-pass filter with a sharp cut-off at a frequency which can be set by turning the
knob on the front of the signal conditioner. The cut-off frequency has to be set lower than the Nyquist frequency, ωs/2,
as frequencies above it are reflected into the frequencies below. However, it is common to set the cut-off frequency lower
than the Nyquist frequency, as the filter has a finite cut-off rate [5].
For the EMA of the rotor the low-pass filter cut-off frequency was set to 10 kHz, which is 0.5 · (ωs/2) for the experiments
carried out with ωs = 40kHz, rendering it very unlikely that the measurements are influenced by aliasing.

It is evident that the experimentally-measured eigenvectors is only a subset of the full eigenvectors of the system. In fact
even a very fine FE model with many DOF will have less DOF than the physical system which is continuous and thus has
an unlimited amount of DOF. Hence, when computing the AutoMAC it isn’t really an orthogonality check, but rather a
validation of the amount, or the choice of DOF [1].
From figure 3.16 it is clear that for some sets of mode shapes the MAC numbers between them are large i.e. mode 1-5,
mode 6-8, mode 9-10 and mode 12-13. This is really a quantification of what is readily visible from the mode shapes
themselves i.e. that they in sets resemble each other. But at the same time it is also a figment of using too few or the wrong
degrees of freedom. The structure of the AutoMAC resembles an example given in [5](page 429), where a reduced set
of degrees of freedom is used for computing the AutoMac. In this case, it seems that the resolution of the modeshapes
across the shaft itself is high enough, but there are parts of the structure which were not measured. The vibration of
the overhanging tie-rod, covered by the impeller and its auxiliary parts when mounted, were not measured. Neither, for
obvious practical reasons, were the parts of the tie-rods internal in the rotor-shaft assembly. Thus even though the mode
shapes for the parts of the rotor-shaft assembly measured resemble each other, it is likely that the parts not measured were
vibrating in phase-phase or counter phase-phase positions for the mode shapes resembling each other.
Furthermore, as discussed in the previous section, exciting the structure demands precision from the operator. One of
the pitfalls often faced is that some of the impacts are not exactly parallel to the measurement direction, and hence the
structure is not vibrating exactly parallel to the accelerometer’s measurement direction. Albeit, SDOF accelerometers
are less sensitive to their secondary axis than the axis belonging to their measurement direction, vibrations normal to the
measurement direction distorts the measurement. This is usually not a problem if the structure in symmetric, but if the
structure, to some degree, is asymmetric either in terms of mass, stiffness, or damping then the natural frequencies are
shifted from those of the measurement axis.
It can be hard to tell whether a rotor can be considered symmetric, or not, as often some components which are not nec-
essarily symmetric are mounted on the rotor. For the rotor-shaft assembly analysed here several components are threaded
and thus are not strictly axis symmetric in every plane. Furthermore, balancing has been carried out prior to the EMA and
hence material has been removed in some balancing planes altering the mass and stiffness of the rotor rendering it slightly
asymmetric. Therefore, the split peaks i.e. 548/554 Hz, 831/840/849 Hz and 1298/1312 Hz and perhaps 1725/1759 Hz,

4.1
Introduction
It is common to use tie-rods to fasten discs on aircraft engine rotors, gas turbines, turbomachinery and centrifugal compres-
sors. Such built-up rotors typically consist of many parts and several sub-assemblies and hence being able to disassemble
the different components for maintenance is an advantage. For others, the shear size of the rotors render it impossible or at
least unfeasible to manufacture the complex geometries in once piece[14]. The design is thus recognised by the governing
standards e.g. API 617 or the ISO equivalent 10439-1:2015 [3], and if designed properly comparable to solid rotors [10].
Several researchers have contributed to the modelling of such rotors and point to that accurate modelling requires including
the tie-rods, the axial rotor assembly contact interfaces and stress stiffening effects in the model.
One modelling approach is to use a full 3D solid FE model of the shaft including the contacts as contact element pairs,
and defining the contact conditions as either sliding or bonded contacts depending on the characteristics of the contact.
Moreover, engagement of the contacts, and accurate modelling of the tie-rods require that pre-tightening forces and stress
stiffening effects are included [9]. The stress stiffening effects have a minuscule near-zero effect on the shaft parts them-
selves, but have a large impact on the tie-rods’ natural frequencies, as the stress is much higher in the tie-rods due to their
lower cross-sectional area.
Attempts were made to model the shaft-rotor assembly in 3D with solid elements in ANSYS, but the endeavours were
unsuccessful. Setting up the model, solving and post-processing was found to be very cumbersome, and the computational
time required is immense.
Yuan et. al. 2008 [16], proposed a simplified 2D model as a less time consuming and computational heavy alternative to
the full 3D models. The axial contacts are modelled as hinges with torsional springs as shown in figure 4.1b.

4.4. OPTIMISATION
4.4
Optimisation
Because the parameters used to estimate the spring element stiffness are merely interpolated values from other studies, and
the results were not entirely satisfactory, an optimisation algorithm was used to optimise the model parameters. In brief
the idea was to use the experimental results as a reference for minimising model error. Practically, the natural frequencies
and mode shapes from the experimental and theoretical results were compared. Then model parameters were updated and
the results were evaluated once again.
Optimisation is widely used and hence several advanced algorithms are readily available. The ’Optimisation Toolbox’ and
’Global Optimisation Toolbox’, in MATLAB are two entire toolboxes with many different tools for the users disposal. The
’Global Optimisation Toolbox’ as the name suggests is a toolbox with global optimisation routines, which intelligently
searches for a global minimum function within a set of parameter constraints. On the other hand, if searching for a local
minimum, the ’Optimisation Toolbox’ can be used. In this case the objective function has several local minima, and hence
the ’Global Optimisation Toolbox’ was used. One of the tools, and the one Mathworks’ documentation recommends to use
first, is an algorithm called ’GlobalSearch’. In brief, GlobalSearch initially scatters a grid of trial points over the parameter
space, and then intelligently refines the following search for minima. Then a local optimisation algorithm is used to find
local minima for trial points ’likely’ to be the global minimum. Naturally, the ’likely’ and ’intelligently’ are algorithms
which may or may not be intelligent when applied. The function was set up as shown in figure 4.11.

First of all, the parameters used for optimisation must be chosen. Ideally, all the parameters used in the model would be
included in the optimisation, which would yield the very best result. However, considering the additional time required
when additional parameters are added, a balance between optimality and computation time must be made. For instance,
running the optimisation with 11 parameters and 251 nodes, as done for the rotor, takes approximately 2 hours on a
regular desktop computer. Because, the computation time is that high, and several combinations of weight functions and
optimisation parameters were used, a rather small number of optimisation parameters were used. If a more elaborate
optimisation had to be carried out it would most likely be feasible to parralise the objective function and use another
optimisation function suitable for parallel optimisation e.g. ’MultiStart’. In the end the optimisation parameters used were
the spring element torsional stiffness and tie-rod pre-tightening forces, additionally, the density and Young’s modulus for
the 3 major shaft parts and 2 tie-rods were used in the optimisation.
An overview of the optimisation flow is given in figure 4.12.
The steps in the flow chart are just headlines and are elaborated in the following.
1. A set of initial values for the optimisation parameters must be supplied to GlobalSearch. In this case an approxi-

upper bounds were supplied with the initial values. The lower bound for the kc values was found be trial and error.
If the value is set too low, the solution behave as if the system was decoupled. A lower bound of 10.000 Nm/rad
yielded consistent stable results. The upper bound was set equal to the largest shaft sections’ rotational stiffness,
approximately 1000 GNm/rad.
The lower- and upper bounds for the tie-rod pre-tighening force were set to -120 kN and 0 N, respectively. The
lower bound is equal to a pre-stress of 766 MPa, or approximately 80% of the proof strength of the tie-rods, and is

5. The eigenvectors from the model have more DOF than the experimental eigenvectors, and moreover some DOF are
at locations not measured in the experiment. Hence, to make a comparison between the two, the normalisation of the
theoretical mode shapes had to carried out with respect to the experimental mode shapes. To do so, the nodes within
the span of the experimental measurement locations were identified and the theoretical mode shapes’ maximum
value within the span was used for normalisation. Hence, the parts of the mode shapes coinciding can be compared.
This also meant that the amplitude of the mode shape outside the general structure is exaggerated, in the case where
the general structure of the rotor does not participate much in the mode.
6. When sorting the modes it is not possible to sort them in a way which accounts for phase of the mode nor the modes
plane i.e. y or z direction deflection. To account for this, a small sub-routine turning and flipping the mode was
implemented. Had the objective function been implemented with only in plane rotation and position at each node
the computation time would have been greatly reduced, but the code would lose generality.
7. As the location of the nodes in the theoretical model is automatically generated their locations do not coincide exactly
with the measurements points locations. Hence, to avoid error, another small sub-routine interpolates between the
theoretical mode locations in order to create an exact match.
8. To quantify the similarity between the theoretical and experimental mode shapes in a single number, an RMS value
of the residuals are computed. As the modes are already normalised with respect to themselves yielding a maximum
value of 1 or -1 the maximum residual is 2, and thus the maximum value of the quantified error is 2.
9. The error between the eigenvalues are computed as (ωi−fi)/fi, where ωi and fi are the theoretical and experimental
natural frequencies of mode i. Usually, the error between the two is initially not larger than 25%, yielding an estimate
maximum error of 0.25. To quantify all the modes error in terms of natural frequency another RMS value is computed
from them. Finally, to make the two error values comparable a 10x weight is added to the frequencies’ RMS value.
A single objective function value is required for GlobalSearch, and hence lastly a sum of the two numbers is passed
to GlobalSearch.

Optimised results
To assess the influence uncertain parameters a comparison of the optimised and the un-optimised multibody model is
made. Hence, the optimisation is run without including the material parameters. The resulting natural frequencies for the
rotor with and without discs are show in table 4.6 and table 4.7 respectively.
The improvements are minor for the natural frequencies associated with the general structure, and are even worsening
by small amounts in some cases. These seem to be the cost for the large improvement made for the natural frequencies
associated with the tie-rods which in many cases are lowered to less than 1%. Some improvements are made for the higher
order modes associated with the general structures.
Table 4.8 and table 4.9 shows a comparison of the initial and optimised model parameters.
From these is is quite evident that substantial changes to the model parameters are made in order to improve the model.
In general the torsional spring stiffinesses are increased by several orders of magnitude, while the pre-tightening forces
are approximately halved. It is like that the improvements in terms of natural frequency made for the modes associated
with the tie-rods can be attributed to the lowered pre-tightening forces. A plot of the natural frequencies when varying the
pre-tightening force mutually set is shown in figure 4.13.
Thus, the natural frequencies are in general controlled by the torsional spring stiffnesses but the natural frequencies of the
tie-rod modes can be controlled with the pre-tightening force. The change in pre-tightening force is however so large that
it cannot be attributed to the pressure uncertainty when assembling the rotor.

The large torsional spring stiffness values found when optimising are in the range of 10e13 to 10e16 which is too large
compared to the values found in [6] and [16], albeit those applications are much larger in size. However, the contact
pressures are lower than those presented in table 4.1. [13] investigated the effect of rod-fastened-rotors with pilot fits. The
results do not indicate that the pilot fits have any significant effect on the torsional stiffness. However as the geometry
presented in the article differs from the shaft-rotor assembly presented, here it cannot unanimously be concluded that the

increases. If the latter is true, the estimations used to determine kN in this study underestimates kc. Lastly, the large kc
values found by optimisation can be a mean to compensate for too flexible rotor components. It is uncertain whether or not,
the effects from the overlapping radial AMB rotor parts, lock nuts, PM magnets etc., have been modelled correctly. Had
an experimental modal analysis been done before assembling the rotor of each part, the individual sub-assembly models
could have been validated. Naturally, such investigation was desired only after final assembly, grinding and balancing had
been carried out, and thus it was very cumbersome to disassemble the shaft-rotor assembly.

Conclusion
A complete rotor design for use with Active Magnetic Bearings (AMB) was complete in the beginning of the project. A
continuous rotor finite element (FE) model was developed during the design phase, to estimate the critical speeds and node
locations. Construction and assembly of the rotor at the beginning of the project allowed carrying out an experimental
modal analysis (EMA) early in the project. The initial EMA revealed that the rotor did not behave as a conventional solid
rotor, and that the continuous model developed during the design phase failed to fit experimental results. Thus a more
refined model and an elaborate EMA was required.
An extensive EMA was carried out, revealing that the shaft-rotor assembly dynamics was strongly influenced by the central
screws, referred to as tie-rods. As the tie-rods are not accessible, they could not be measured and included in the EMA, but
the elaborate analysis revealed the effect of them. The experimental analysis is well described, documented, and includes
references for further study. It is perhaps a trifle superfluous, but future bachelor and master’s thesis students could benefit
from reading it prior to performing their own EMAs.
A literature study was carried out to find references describing how to model such rotors. It was found that the turboma-
chinery designers lately had turned to rotor designs similar to the rotor design in this study. Those rotors are referred to
as built-up rod-fastened rotors, and are characterised by being built-up of multiple components tied together with screws.
To model such rotors either a full 3-dimensional solid FE, or lumped beam element models are used. Modelling the rotor in
a commercial FE software rendered infeasible as the setup and size of the model was too complex, especially considering
that the model structure would have to be adapted and reduced before synthesising controllers based on it. Therefore,
modelling the complex rotor was approached with a multibody model, including the tie-rods, axial pre-tightening forces
and contacts. The contacts coupling the bodies were modelled as hinges with torsional springs. The torsional spring
stiffness was estimated by using presented data from other applications and later optimised with an elaborate optimisation
procedure. The final model adequately modelled the rotor’s first 6 flexible modes and additional auxiliary modes associated
with the tie-rods, in order for synthesising controllers for the AMBs.
Future work
Firstly, a run-up or similar test will have to be carried out to validate the model in terms of gyroscopic effect and it’s
influence on the critical speeds. Depending on the complexity of the controller used, a model of the rotor-bearing system
including the controllers will have to be developed to carry out the comparison. However, it may suffice to use a fixed
linear stiffness to model the bearings for the comparison.
Secondly, it would also be interesting to carry out a full 3D solid FE model of the rotor to replicate the results from the beam
FE model. The solid FE model may reveal why, or where the discrepancies between the beam model and experimental
results originate from.
Finally, a full stability analysis of the system will have to be carried out. The system will have to include the bearings,
high-speed permanent magnet motor and additionally the aerodynamic forces from both the seal and impeller. It is unlikely
that a similar rotor design will be employed for the eventual commercial machine, but some of the results may be useful
as contacts and thus their representing couplings in the rotor model will be present in future machines.
