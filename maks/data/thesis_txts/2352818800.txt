2 Abstract 
Immunotherapy is important for the treatment of many types of leukemias, as it is the only responding solution 
for many of the high-risk hematologic malignancies. The adverse effects of this class of therapies, albeit not 
as serious as the ones of other treatments, are still a problem for the health of the patients and for the overall 
outcome of the cure. In this thesis, we will study T lymphocyte (T-cell) therapies by analyzing and proposing 
possible structural modifications to T-cell receptors (TCRs) and chimeric antigen receptors (CARs).  
When engineering a T-cell to express an exogenous, cancer-targeting TCR, and transfusing them to patients, 
there is the potential risk of expressing mispaired TCRs, containing an endogenous and an exogenous chain, 
thus reducing the number of effective molecules and triggering the possibility that such molecules could target 
healthy cells. We performed an in silico analysis of the interface of a TCR recognizing the SLL antigen of the 
PRAME gene, to find the appropriate mutations that would impair the improperly paired dimers by yielding a 
negative energy contribution for the mutated TCR and a positive one for mixed TCR complexes. Two different 
software tools, FoldX and Rosetta, were used for the interface analysis. Rosetta was not able to predict any 
stabilizing mutations. FoldX predicted ten stabilizing mutations on the variable and constant domain. Based 
on sequential and structural analyses of the ten mutations, four mutations on the constant domain of the TCR 
were selected as candidates able to stabilize it, making it more favorable to pair correctly and fold, compared 
to the wildtype TCR. 
The second immunotherapy molecule investigated was a CAR molecule, they are engineered proteins that 
carry the variable domain of an antibody and the effector regions of other molecules in the T cell activation 
pathway. The antibodies, used to target tumoral antigens, are often partly or completely of murine origin, 
which can yield immunogenicity upon infusion into patients. We performed an in silico analysis of a CAR 
recognizing CD19 aiming to reduce the immunogenic risk while retaining the original specificity. We propose 
a possible CAR structure which framework consists of human antibody sequences with incorporated murine 
parts specific for the binding specificity.

3 Resume 
Immunterapi er for leukæmi en vigtig behandlingsmetode da det er den eneste metode der har virkning imod 
flere typer af blodkræft. Dog er immunterapi behandling ikke helt uden problemer for behandlede patienter, 
men disse er dog i en mindre grad end for andre behandlingsmetoder. Problemerne der kan opstå ved 
behandling kan både have helbredsmæssig indvirkning, men også behandlingsmæssigt. Vi vil i denne tese 
studere T lymfocyt (T-celle) terapi ved at analysere og herefter foreslå strukturelle modifikationer af en T-
celle receptor (TCR) og en kimær antigen receptor (CAR).   
Når man overfører strukturelt kræft modificerede TCR til patienter under transfusion, er der en potentiel risiko 
for at opnå et miks mellem de eksogene og modificerede TCR. Dette uønskede miks vil reducere antallet af de 
kræft effektive TCR samt medføre muligheden for at disse vil angribe raske celler. Vi har foretaget in silico 
analyser af kontaktfladen for en TCR, der genkender SLL antigenet fra PRAME genet. Vores mål var at finde 
potentielle mutationer der kunne forringe muligheden for parringen af den uønskede miksede TCR. For at opnå 
dette, skulle mutationerne kunne opnå et negativt energi bidrag, og dermed en stabiliserende effekt, for de 
muterede TCR og et positivt bidrag, og dermed destabiliserende, til de miksede. Vi anvendte to forskellige 
programmer til at udføre TCR kontaktflade analysen: Rosetta og FoldX. Rosetta var ikke i stand til at forudsige 
nogen af mutationerne som stabiliserende, hvorom FoldX fandt ti stabiliserende mutationer. De ti mutationer 
blev efterfølgende analyseret sekventielt og strukturelt hvoraf vi fandt fire mutationer på det konstante TCR 
domæne til at kunne stabilisere den muterede TCR, og dermed opnå en øget chance for, i forhold til den 
originale TCR, at blive parret og foldet rigtigt. 
Foruden en TCR, analyserede vi også en anden type immunterapi struktur nemlig en CAR, som er et strukturelt 
modificeret protein der til dels består af den variable del af et antistof samt effekter regioner forbundet med T-
celle aktivering. De antistoffer der bruges til at genkende antigener fra kræft, stammer ofte helt eller delvist 
fra mus hvilket kan medføre et immungent respons efter patient transfusion. Vi foretog in silico analyse af en 
CAR som genkender CD19, for at opnå en reducering af det immungene respons uden at miste antigen 
specificiteten fra mus strukturen. Vi foreslår en CAR struktur hvis ramme består af humane antigen sekvenser 
hvori dele af mus antigenet er integreret for at implementere bindings specificiteten.

4 Introduction  
Cancers are among the most life-threatening diseases [1]. Surgery, radiation, and chemotherapy are the 
traditional treatment options for cancer. However, these forms of treatment often fail to fully eradicate the 
cancer while potentially damaging healthy tissue. These treatments lack specificity and are prone to degrading 
the patient’s immune system [1], [2]. Therapies consisting in combinations of more traditional treatments can 
often effectively treat primary tumors, but are not very efficient in preventing the spread of the disease. Cancer 
immunotherapy is an alternative treatment method that has proven to be effective in reducing or eradicating 
the cancer with less side effect when compared to other therapies, thus improving the  patient’s life expectancy 
and quality [1].  Immunotherapy is a cancer treatment where the immune system is manipulated or immune 
components are utilized (e.g. cytokines, monoclonal antibodies, lymphocytes) [3].  
Leukemias are among the cancer types for which immunotherapy has shown great promise. The focus of 
leukemia immunotherapy has been on boosting the immune system of the patient or give immunity using 
dendritic cells, monoclonal antibodies, natural killer cells, or T cells [4]. Leukemias are the result of 
hematopoietic stem cells not differentiating into normal mature blood cells. Acute leukemias originate from 
immature hematopoietic stem cells undergoing self-renewal, whereas chronic leukemias originate from 
hematopoietic stem cells that are more mature [5]. 
Immunotherapy has developed a lot during the last decades and two of the T lymphocyte (T-cell) approaches 
will be covered in this study: engineering of T-cell receptors (TCRs) and chimeric antigen receptors (CARs).   
The introduction is divided into three sections. First, an introduction to the basic molecules used in this study, 
T and B lymphocyte receptors, and the engineered chimeric antigen receptors which is a mix hereof, followed 
by an introduction to the immunotherapies that apply these molecules. Lastly, we will introduce the protocols 
and principles we use for the structural modification of these molecules. The introduction is rounded off with 
an outline of the aim of this study.

4.1 Lymphocyte receptors 
Lymphocytes are a family of white blood cells with similar morphological appearance but diverse in function. 
Lymphocytes comprise B-, T-, and natural killer lymphocytes. T-cells are part of the adaptive immune 
response and are able to assist by recognizing and fighting intracellular pathogens. The antigen-specific 
receptor expressed by T lymphocytes is called T-cell antigen receptor (TCR) and are expressed on the cell 
surface (Figure 1). Antibodies are Y-shaped proteins (Figure 4) that are produced by B-lymphocytes (B-cells) 
as part of the immune system. They are found in blood and fluids circulating in the body. Antibodies (or 
immunoglobulins) recognize antigens from e.g. pathogens and are often sufficient to eliminate the antigen 
upon binding. However, sometimes the antibody-antigen complex activates additional effector mechanisms 
that will assist in the pathogen elimination [6]. 
 
 Introducing the T-cell  
T-cells recognize antigens presented via the major histocompatibility complex (MHC), also known as human 
leukocyte antigen (HLA) in humans. A specific TCR is only able to recognize a specific antigen-MHC complex 
and elicit the appropriate immune response thereafter  [6]. T-cells that have not encountered an antigen are 
called naïve T-cells. When a T-cell recognizes its specific antigen-MHC complex the T-cell proliferate and 
differentiate into an effector T-cells. Effector T-cells can be divided into different subsets, CD8+ T-cells and 
CD4+ T-cells, which are based on a cell surface marker [7]. CD8+ effector T-cells mature into cytotoxic T-
cells which are able to kill pathogen infected cells. CD4+ effector T-cells mature into helper T-cells, either 
Th1 or Th2, or regulatory T-cells. T helper cells enhance the adaptive response toward the antigen, whereas 
regulatory T-cells are able to regulate (suppress) T-cell responses and are thus able to prevent autoimmunity 
[6]–[8]. CD8+ interact with MHC class I and CD4+ interacts with MHC class II [7]. 
 
4.1.1.1 
The T-cell receptor 
The specificity and diversity of the B and T lymphocyte are gained during the formation of the receptors [7]. 
The TCR portray a similar structure as antibodies [6] and also have highly diverse receptors due to somatic 
recombination of a limited set of genes [9]. The receptor is determined by the somatic recombination of 
variable (V), diversity (D), and joining (J) genes [7]. The TCR is a heterodimer composed of an α-chain and a 
β-chain and is divided into two domains: the variable and constant. The variable domain is composed of Vα 
and Vβ and the constant domain is composed of Cα and Cβ. The variable and constant domains are connected 
by a short region. There is another class of TCR structures, composed of γ-and δ-chains instead of the α- and 
β-chains [10]. However, this study will focus on the αβ-TCRs.

During the development of the T-cell, the β-chain of the TCR is assembled starting with a D to J recombination 
followed by a V to DJ recombination. Hereafter are the V to J recombination of the α-chain initiated [12]. The 
relative diversity of the TCR is encoded by the complementarity-determining regions (CDR) 1, 2, and 3 which 
are location on the variable domain of the TCR on both chains [10], [13], [14]. 
The CDR1-3 comprises the epitope binding site of the TCR, the CDR3 region make the primary contact with 
the epitope that are presented by the MHC molecule. The CDR1+2 make contact in a stereotypically manner 
with the MHC [15]. The CDR1 and CDR2 of both the α- and β-chain are encoded in the V-gene, whereas the 
CDR3, of both chains, are formed contributions of the D, J, and N regions (Figure 2).  Thus, CDR3 portray 
more diversity than CDR1+2. The CDRs are flaked by four framework regions [14]–[16].  
From a structural point of view, the CDRs of the TCR are hypervariable (HV) loops that comprises a high 
level of variability, compared to the remaining parts of the structure. The first and second HV-loop of both the 
α- and β-chain in the variable domain (Vα, Vβ) are built from a small repertoire of main-chain conformations 
named canonical structures. The variable domain of the TCR portray similar secondary structures to the 
variable domain of antibodies. Both domains are constructed of two face to face packed antiparallel β-sheets. 
They are made from nine β-strands, typically arranged as G, F, C, C’, and C’’ for Vα and A, B, E, and D for 
Vβ (Figure 3). The HV-loops are those connecting the B-C, C’-C’’, and F-G β-strands (Figure 3). As for the 
CDR3 of the α- and β-chain the third HV loops portray great variability, in size and sequence, thus canonical 
structures cannot be assigned. Specific residues of the HV region are responsible for the given canonical 
structure conformation along with the size of the loop [17].

 Introducing the chimeric antigen receptor  
Chimeric antigen receptors (CARs) are partly constructed of antibody and T-cell domains.  
4.1.2.1 
Antibody structure 
Antibodies are composed of two heavy chains (H) and two light chains (L). Each chain is composed of a 
variable domain (V) and one, three, or four constant domains (C), all of which have a similar fold. The number 
of constant domains is dependent on the heavy chain isotype (α, γ, δ, ε, and µ) [18]. Light chains only have 
two isotypes (κ and λ) which can associate with any of heavy chain isotopes. The heavy chain isotype also 
determines class of the antibody (i.e. IgA, IgG, IgD, IgE, and IgM) [6]. 
The fold adopted by the constant and variable domains is called immunoglobulin fold and it consists of seven 
or nine β-strands for C- and V-domains, respectively [19]. The antibody consists of two two-domain L-chains 
and two four-domain H-chains (Figure 4) which each follow the immunoglobulin fold. The antibody can be 
divided into three dimer segments, two Fab fragments and one FC fragment (Figure 4).  The Fab fragment is 
a mixed light-heavy chain dimer consisting of two L-chain domains (VL and CL) and two H-chain domains 
(VH and CH1). The Fc fragment is a H-chain dimer consisting of four H-chain domains (two CH2 and two CH3). 
The Fab and Fc fragments are joined by a flexible hinge that allows it to be cleaved by proteases into separate 
fragments [20]. 
 
 
Figure 4 – Schematic presentation of the antibody structure. The antibody structure consists of two L-chains (blue) and two 
H-chains (green). The structure can be divided in three fragments, two Fab fragments and one Fc fragment each consisting 
four domains. Each domain adopts the immunoglobulin fold which are held together by disulphide bridges. The antigen 
binding site is located on both Fab fragments on the variable domain.  (Figure inspired from [11].)

The V domains each comprise two β-sheets and loops that are connecting the β-strands. Three of these loops 
are considered hypervariable (HV) as they are highly diverse in length and/or amino acid composition. The 
remaining loops and the two β-sheets are part of the conserved framework regions (FRs) [21]. The variation 
of the variable region (binding site) of the antibody is generated by the recombination of either the heavy chain 
(V(D)J genes) or light chain (VJ genes). The recombination of the genes can lead to different antibody 
sequence combinations and thus the ability of the antibody to bind to different antigens [22]. The binding site 
is located on the top of the Fab fragment and is composed of six CDRs  [23] – which are more or less the same 
as the HV-loops. The definition of the CDRs is based on the sequence of the antibody, whereas the HV refers 
to the structure of the antibody [24]. The three HV-loops located on the light chain is denoted L1, L2, and L3 
and the three on the heavy chain is denoted H1, H2, and H3 [22], [24]. 
As for the TCR, the antibody portrays canonical structures. Five of the HV-loops (L1-3 and H1-2) display a 
small number of main-chain conformations. They are determined by the HV length and conserved residues in 
both the HV and the FRs. The surface of the HV-loops is complementary to the surface of the antigen the 
antibody is recognizing. As for the TCR, the variable domain of the H- and L-chains contains the face to face 
packed β-sheet (Figure 5). Six of the connecting β-strand loops constitute the six HV-loops (Figure 5) [25].

4.1.2.2 
Chimeric antigen receptors  
CARs aim to put together the high specificity of B-cells towards their antigen, achieved by their antibodies, 
with the efficacy of the T-cell response in targeting and killing pathogenic cells. CARs are recombinant 
receptors often targeting surface molecules [27]. They are composed of an extracellular and an intracellular 
domain linked via a hinge and transmembrane domains (Figure 6). Typically, the extracellular domain 
comprises an antigen-recognition moiety, while the intracellular domain comprises T-cell activation of 
==PAGE:15==
Katrine Bolvig Hansen  
Master Thesis 
February 1st 2017 
Page 15 of 132 
 
costimulatory domain moieties. The hinge region has a huge impact on the flexibility of the extracellular 
domain and the overall function. Thus CARs, unlike TCRs, are able to recognize unprocessed antigens that 
are not presented via MHC molecules [28].  
The extracellular domain of the CAR is often a single-chain fragment variable (scFv) derived from an antibody 
[28]. scFvs are recombinant molecules comprised of the variable domain of an antibody linked together into a 
single polypeptide by a flexible linker [29]. The intracellular domain is derived from signal-initiating 
molecules of lymphocytes. The hinge region, separating the extracellular and intracellular domain, is often 
derived from immunoglobulins. The CAR structures have developed over time, aiming to elicit the appropriate 
response. Unlike first-generation CARs, second- and third-generation CARs combines signaling domains in 
addition to the cytoplasmic domain (Figure 6) [28].

4.2 T-cell immunotherapy 
There are different approaches for cancer immunotherapy: active immunization, adoptive cell transfer, and 
nonspecific immune stimulation [30]. Therapy applying adoptive cell transfer approaches have shown 
promising results. 
 
 Adoptive therapy 
Adoptive T-cell therapy aims to genetically engineer tumor specific T-cells that are to be infused into the 
patient to elicit an immune response that recognizes and targets tumor cells. Adoptive cell transfer therapy 
==PAGE:16==
Katrine Bolvig Hansen  
Master Thesis 
February 1st 2017 
Page 16 of 132 
 
uses cancer antigen-specific T-cells, such as peptide-induced T-cells, tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes, and 
engineered T-cells (TCR and CAR). The use of genetically engineered T-cells overcomes the problem of 
individualized treatment which requires surgical removal of tumor tissue and their following in vitro 
expansion, which can be difficult [30].  
 
4.2.1.1 
Gene transfer 
Gene transfer of TCRs is a method enabling the production of antigen specific T-cells used for adoptive 
therapy. TCR gene transfer can be a useful approach to avoid the tissue removal and in vitro expansion 
difficulties, where the TCR genes are transferred into e.g. primary T-cells [31]. The basic approach of this has 
already been shown possible as antigen specificity have been transferred into mouse T cells by TCR gene 
transfer [32]. So, transferring the TCRα and TCRβ chain genes into the patient’s own T-cells can be an 
alternative approach to gain large quantities of the desired T-cells [31].   
However, transferring TCR genes leads to T-cells which have acquired additional rearranged TCR chains. This 
can potentially lead to the expression of four different TCR complexes: the exogenous and the endogenous 
receptors, and two mixed receptors consisting of exogenous and endogenous chains (Figure 7) [31]. However, 
expression of a mixed TCR can reduce the TCR expression level [31] and can lead to autoimmunity due to the 
potential gain of new antigen specificities [33].  
 
 
Figure 7 - One of the challenges for TCR immunotherapy is the expression of the endogenous TCR and the mixed (mispaired). 
The expression of the mixed complexes might lead to new unwanted antigen specificity. 
 
Sommermeyer et al. [31] investigated if some TCRs can be expressed better than other TCRs by performing 
TCR gene transfer of two TCRs into T-cells. They demonstrated in a murine T-cell line that upon TCR gene 
transfer they obtained a new antigen specificity while eliminating the original specificity. Sommermeyer 
explained the situation had to be due to weak and strong TCR in regards to cell surface expression, and that 
strong TCR are expressed over the weak. Furthermore, they did not observe any mixed TCR heterodimers

unless the cells were forced. After this, likewise experiments were performed on human T-cells with human 
TCR transfer. They demonstrated the same effects as observed in the murine T-cell line. However, they did

demonstrated that TCR are co-expressed in the presence of two strong TCRs on human cells, leading to T-
cells with both antigen specificities [31]. 
 
 Allogeneic therapy 
Allogeneic hematopoietic cell transplantation (HCT) is the only curative treatment method for many of the 
high-risk hematologic malignancies (blood cancers). The HCT manage to reconstruct the immune system of 
the donor yielding graft-versus-leukemia (GVL) immune reactions. However, the development of graft-versus-
host disease (GVHD) is a major risk after HCT. GVHD are caused due to donor immune cells attacking non-
cancer recipient cells [34]. The T-cells are thought to be responsible for GVL but also for GVHD [35], [36]. 
However, by depleting the T-cells from the HCT the frequency and severity of GVHD decreased while the 
risk of relapse increased [37]. It is thus important to figure out how to treat leukemia and obtain GVL without 
GVHD.  
 
4.2.2.1 
Graft-versus-host disease 
GVHD causes morbidity and mortality, increased severity is observed when donor and recipient are not fully 
HLA-matching [38]. GVHD are divided in two categories, acute GVHD and chronic GVHD each with two 
subcategories. Acute GVHD is divided into classic acute GVHD, associated with skin, liver, and 
gastrointestinal symptoms, and recurrent/persistent/late-onset acute GVHD, which occurs +100 days after 
HCT. Chronic GVHD is associated with skin, mouth, eyes, lung, nails and hair, liver, musculoskeletal, 
gastrointestinal, and hematopoietic symptoms, among others. Chronic GVHD are divided in classical chronical 
GVHD and overlap syndrome, that show sign of acute GVHD as well [39]. 
Adoptive therapy using transfusion of donor lymphocytes is used for treatment for bone marrow transplanted 
patients with recurrent leukemia [35].  Kolb et al. [35] observed strong effect of the lymphocyte transfusion, 
from the same marrow donor, against different forms of myeloid leukemia. Complete remission was observed 
for patients with chronic myeloid leukemia (CML), polycythemia vera with osteomyelofibrosis (PCV), acute 
myeloid leukemia (AML), and myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS). However, they did not observe any response 
for patients with acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL). However, Kolb et al. [35] did observe development of 
myelosuppression and GVHD in  60 % of the patients, more frequently for patients previously treated with a 
T-cell depleted HCT than non-depleted. They conclude that the lymphocyte fusion is acceptable, despite the 
risks, compared to the alternative second HCT [35].

Several leukemia patients do not undergo HCT due to lack of a HLA-matching donor. Many patients are treated 
with T-cell depleted HCT to lower the risk and severity of GVHD. However, Zheng et al. [38] have 
demonstrated GVL without GVHD in murine MHC class II mismatched models of CML. By adding effector 
memory CD4+ T-cells to T-cell depleted and MHC-mismatched HCT, they observed a promotion of both GVL 
and reconstitution of the patients’ immune system with less GVHD. They reason their observation due to the 
fact that effector memory CD4+ T-cell are able to retain key cytolytic functions while simultaneously lacking 
pivotal features for GVHD initiation, and suggest this approach could be safer than lymphocyte transfusion 
[38].  
 
4.2.2.2 
Gene transfer into allogeneic T-cells  
Holler et al. [40] investigated whether the transduction of a dominant TCR both could enhance tumor 
protection, by achieving new antigen specificity, and also reduce toxicity and thereby the risk of GVHDs.  As 
mentioned earlier, with TCR gene transfer there are some safety concerns regarding the mispairing of the 
transduced TCR chains with the endogenous chains expressed by the T-cell to be modified. Holler et al. 
introduced additional cysteine bonds and codon optimized the TCR, to prevent mixing of TCR complexes. 
They were able to reduce expression of the potentially allogeneic reactive endogenous TCR in vitro by 
selecting T-cell with high-level expression of the correctly paired TCR. The TCRs were transferred in vivo to 
mice and T-cells expressing high levels of the engineered TCR were purified. These T-cells were able to 
suppress expression of the endogenous TCR. However, exposure of the allogeneic antigen in vivo provided a 
strong selection for endogenous TCR expression. Nevertheless, when the T-cells with the engineered TCR 
were able to expand, they effectively facilitated control over the tumors expressing the given allogeneic 
antigen. Over time they did observe GVHD for mice with persistently high expression of engineered TCRs, 
due to an enrichment of mixed TCRs. The GVHD was not observed for mice with a limiting expression of the 
engineered TCRs. Holler et al. concluded that gene transferring a dominant TCR can suppress the expression 
of endogenous TCRs, but long-term persistence will lead to T-cells no longer expressing the dominant TCR 
[40]. Thus, in order for the implement of dominant TCRs to work, it is important to avoid increase of allogeneic 
specificities, by fully depleting the expression of the endogenous TCRs.  
 
 The PRAME gene 
A major focus of immunotherapy is to identify suitable cancer antigens which can be used to generate cancer 
vaccines or antigen-specific T-cells [30]. The PRAME gene is expressed by a variety of tumor cells, including 
acute leukemia, and is only expressed in some normal tissues at lower level. PRAME antigens are recognized 
by cytotoxic T-cells [41]. Expression levels of the PRAME gene show a correlations with remission and relapse 
of leukemia, thus making it a useful detector [42].

Furthermore, the PRAME gene is a good immunotherapy antigen target candidate as it is recognized by 
cytotoxic T-cells. Rezvani et al. [43] studied the CD8+ T-cell response towards the PRAME gene in patients 
with ALL, CML, and AML and healthy donors. They observed natural occurrence of memory CD8+ T-cells

 Chimeric antigen receptors 
CARs were constructed with the aim of redirecting the T-cell specificity towards tumor-associated antigens 
expressed on cell surface. The extracellular domain of the CAR is able to bind tumor-associated antigens while 
the intracellular domain gets triggered, by the antigen binding, and activates T-cells and effector functions 
[44].  
 
4.2.4.1 
CD19 
CD19 is the most investigated CAR target. It is a B-cell lineage specific transmembrane glycoprotein, it works 
as a response regulator that lowers the threshold for the necessary antigen receptor dependent stimulation 
needed for B-cell activation [45]. It is an attractive CAR target as it is present in most B-cells of leukemia and 
lymphomas and not found in normal tissue other than the B-cell lineage [46].  
Kalos et al. [47] engineered a second-generation CAR recognizing CD19. They were able to induce a CD19-
specific memory response that retained their effector functions for months in vivo in the marrow and blood of 
CLL patients [47]. They have previously observed GVL effects and complete remission of the CD19-CAR in 
the tested CLL patients. However, they did observe a delayed tumor lysis syndrome and lymphopenia from 
day one [48]. The ability of the CARs to persist in long-term could potentially lead to sustained tumor control 
and thus avoid continuous treatments [47]. In a different research, they were also able to treat relapsed and 
refractory ALL using a CAR targeting CD19 and observed remission up to 24 months. Cytokine-release 
syndrome was observed for all patients, which developed into severe cytokine-release syndrome for 
approximately one third of the patients and was treated [49].

4.3 Principles and tools for protein engineering 
We have already established the importance of generating tumor specific T-cells. However, it is important not 
to disturb the stability of the protein with the incorporated changes.  
 
 Protein stability and thermodynamics 
The biological function and activity of the protein is dependent on its specific three-dimensional conformation 
[50] – the native structure of the protein. Even though the conformational space that any given protein can 
potentially adopt is huge, proteins can fold into their native structure rapidly [51]. It is hypothesized that the 
native conformation of the folded protein has the lowest Gibbs free energy [52]. This has also been described 
as the “thermodynamic principle” [53], [54].  
The protein stability is a good measure for comparing wildtype proteins to mutated proteins as the specific 
identity of each amino acid will affect the stability. Thus, it is possible to introduce mutations that will have 
the ability to either stabilize or destabilize the protein. 
 
 Protein stabilizing forces 
Different forces contribute to the stabilization and folding of protein structures. Proteins bury around 86% on 
average of their hydrophobic (non-polar) sidechains [55]. The interior of the protein is tightly packed which is 
important for the stability [56], Klapper [57] even concluded that the interior of proteins is closer to a solid 
than to a liquid due to the little spacing [57].  
Besides the stability contribution from the tight packing of the proteins are also the hydrophobic interactions, 
which can be divided into two types of contributions. Approximately 80 % of the hydrophobic effect is due to 
hydrophobicity, usually denoted as the hydrophobic bonds [58]. Hydrophobic residues repel water, this forces 
the buried hydrophobic groups to hold together to avoid contract between them and water [59]. The remaining 
20 % is due to the previously mentioned packing of the hydrophobic sidechains [58].  Experiments by Pace et 
al. [59], where a buried hydrophobic group is removed, showed that proteins lose stability for each -CH2- that 
is removed (e.g. from Isoleucine to Valine) [59]. This demonstrates that the hydrophobic interaction also 
contributes to the protein stability. Matthew et al. [60] further showed that the bigger the cavity left after amino 
acid mutation (mutating to a smaller amino acid), the bigger the contribution to instability of the protein [60].  
Hydrogen bonds also make a favorable contribution to the stability of the proteins, but are not the major driving 
force [56]. In a folded protein 1.1 hydrogen bonds are formed per residue. From these, 65% are bonds between 
peptide groups, 23% are bonds between sidechains and peptide groups, and the remaining 12% are bonds 
between sidechains [61]. Pace et al. [62] did also investigate the impact of hydrogen bonds by mutating e.g. 
serine to alanine when the -OH group of serine is not and is hydrogen bonded.  They observed that the loss of 
an -OH group of amino acids that are not hydrogen bonded yield a small unfavorable contribution to the overall 
==PAGE:21==
Katrine Bolvig Hansen  
Master Thesis 
February 1st 2017 
Page 21 of 132 
 
stability, and bigger if hydrogen bonded. Thus, indicating the stabilizing contribution of the hydrogen bond. 
Their results also showed that the contribution of a hydrogen bond or interactions of -OH groups can be more 
favorable than the interaction with water in the unfolded protein. Also hydrogen bonds between sidechains and 
peptide groups make a similar stability contribution as the -OH groups [62].  
Disfulphide bridges, charge-charge interactions, and the formation of salt bridges also contribute to the folding 
of proteins [56]. From these different stabilizing interactions, the disulphide bridge has the highest 
contribution, followed by salt-bridges, hydrogen bonds, and hydrophobic bonds. Clashes (Van der Waals 
forces) of amino acid sidechains constitute the biggest destabilizing interactions, followed by exposed 
hydrophobic residues, cavities, and unsatisfied hydrogen bond acceptors and donors [56], [58], [59], [62]. 
By solving the three-dimensional structure of a protein, using e.g. X-ray crystallography or NMR, atomic 
details on the architecture is obtained, whereas no information regarding the forces is revealed [63]. Protein 
forces are studied by introducing mutations and subsequently measuring the energetic consequence thereof 
[56], [64]. 
 
 Protein thermodynamics 
Gibbs free energy is a measure, as the name implies, of the free energy of the system. The Gibbs free energy 
function is composed of enthalpy (H), temperature (T), and entropy (S): ∆𝐺°(𝑇) = ∆𝐻°(𝑇) −𝑇∆𝑆°(𝑇). The 
free energy is generally measured as the difference between the energy of the system when it changes from 
absolute zero to a certain temperature. However, this standard is not usually adopted for protein systems, where 
the thermodynamic properties are considered in relation to the stable state of the protein. Thus, the change in 
the thermodynamic properties is measured during folding and unfolding of the protein [65]. 
The ∆𝐺 for a protein is thus a measure of the free energy of stability. This is calculated based on all the energy 
contributions mentioned in the previous section. It is possible to evaluate how and how much the 
implementation of changes in the engineered TCR have on the stability of the protein, by calculating the 
difference in the free energy of the wildtype protein and the mutated (engineered) protein: 
∆∆𝐺= ∆𝐺𝑚𝑢𝑡𝑎𝑛𝑡−∆𝐺𝑤𝑖𝑙𝑑𝑡𝑦𝑝𝑒 
A ∆∆𝐺> 0 indicates that the mutations have destabilizing properties, whereas a ∆∆𝐺<  0 indicates that the 
mutations have stabilized the protein more than the wildtype.   
 
 Energy estimation approaches  
Different approaches exist for estimating protein energetics, that are divided into different classes: approaches 
based on statistical analysis of already known proteins (statistical effective energy function, SEEF), approaches

based on properties that are physical (physical effective energy function, PEEF) [66], and approaches mainly 
based on empirical experimental protein data (empirical effective energy function, EEEF) [67].  
FoldX is an EEEF tool that was developed with the aim of estimating the free energy change, on protein 
stability when mutating, fast and accurate. The free energy (in kcal/mol) is calculated using different empirical 
terms, which are important for protein stability [68]. 
Rosetta is a complex software that offers a long list of protocols suitable for protein modelling and analysis. 
ddg_monomer is a Rosetta application that predicts the stability change (∆∆𝐺) due to mutations [69]. A new

 Modelling of lymphocyte receptors 
It is important to have good models of the protein structure (e.g. TCR, CAR) prior to any structural analysis. 
 
4.3.5.1 
Homology modelling 
Homology modelling is based on the identification of protein with solved structure that are homologous of the 
protein to be modeled, and from which the structure of the latter can be derived. The framework regions of the 
antibody and TCR structures are highly conserved, and the structural differences are confined to the HV loops, 
as explained earlier. A structure with a high sequence similarity is chosen for the modelling of the framework 
regions. If only one structure is chosen to represent the entire framework regions, then the final model will 
inherit the V(L)-V(H) domain orientation, which is not necessarily the most appropriate packing for the 
structure to be modelled [71]. As the packing of the domains influence the conformation of the CDRs [72], 
hence the binding site, it is important to choose appropriate structures for each chain and for the packing. The 
hypervariable loops are each modeled according to their canonical structures [71]. So, the basic principle of 
homology modelling is to use known structures to copy the conformation from. After modelling, it is essential 
to validate the model to investigate the overall quality, via e.g. a Ramachandran plot [73]. 
LYRA is an online webserver for lymphocyte receptor structural modeling. It builds structural models of TCRs 
and BCRs based on their amino acid sequence. The modeling is based on the canonical structure method that 
yield high accuracy models [74]. Several other modelling tools are available for antibody modelling [75]–[78], 
but LYRA were the first also to model TCR structures [74]. LYRA is still, too our knowledge, the only fully 
automatic tool available for TCR modelling.

 Humanization  
As murine structures are often used for the extracellular domain of the CAR structure, it is important to 
humanize them to avoid immunogenicity while still preserving the antigen specificity. The general idea of 
humanizing is to make the non-human structure more human like, by combining human and non-human parts, 
without losing the wildtype function.  
Antibodies show great promise for therapeutic applications in humans. However, humanization of antibodies 
plays an important role in regard of that.  When non-human antibodies are to be used for human application, 
it is common practice to humanize the antibody. This will alter the antibody to generate a molecule with 
minimal immunogenicity while still preserving the specificity and affinity of the parental antibody [24].  
There are two different approaches to humanize antibodies: the rational and empiric approach. Rational 
approaches are characterized by only engineering few different variants of the antibody per time. If the result 
does not meet the expectations, another cycle with few variants are engineered until the desired result is 
obtained. Empiric approaches are characterized by generating large libraries containing different variants of 
the humanized antibody and selecting the best by using high throughput screening techniques or using 
enrichment technologies [24].  
 
4.3.6.1 
CDR-grafting 
CDR-grafting is the paradigm of rational approaches and consists of three main steps which are to be reviewed 
during the planning and decision-making: 
1. Defining specificity regions of the donor antibody to be targeted for CDR-grafting. 
2. Finding human sequences to be used as FR donors. 
3. Defining residues in non-specificity regions appropriate for backmutations in order to achieve 
improvement or restore the affinity of the humanized antibody [24]. 
So, firstly the specificity regions of the donor antibody are defined. If the experimental structure of the antigen 
and parental antibody complex is available, it will provide a detailed insight of the location of the residues, 
which are in contact with the antigen. These residues determine the specificity of the antibody. However, the 
experimental structure of the complex is rarely available [24]. If that on the other hand is the case, then CDRs 
often have been grafted as specificity defining regions [79]. Another way to graft without the experimental 
structure is to use a combination of the HV loops and the CDRs [24].  
After the specificity regions of the donor antibody have been defined, the next step is then to find human 
sequences, which are to be used as FR donors. Different strategies can be applied when searching for the 
human FR donors. For example, the “fixed FR” strategy does not take homology toward the non-human 
antibody into account when choosing, but simply chooses human antibodies FRs with known structures. 
Whereas the “best fit” strategy uses human FRs with the highest similarity toward the non-human donor. When 
==PAGE:24==
Katrine Bolvig Hansen  
Master Thesis 
February 1st 2017 
Page 24 of 132 
 
the desired strategy has been chosen, then the source of the human FR sequence has to be chosen. Often 
germline gene sequences are being used as it show several advantages, but also mature sequences have been 
applied [24]. 
The last step in CDR-grafting is then to define residues that are appropriate for backmutations. This is often 
necessary as the affinity might decrease upon the CDR grafting. The potential decrease is a consequence of 
the incompatibilities between the non-human donor CDRs and the human FRs it has been grafted to. It is of 
great importance to find residues that can increase the affinity upon backmutation to compensate for the affinity 
decrease. However, it must be done with great care as the backmutations also might increase the 
immunogenicity of the humanized antibody [24]. 
Other rational approaches include resurfacing, superhumanization, and human string content optimization [24]. 
 
4.3.6.2 
Tool for humanization using CDR grafting 
Olimpieri et al. [80] have developed Tabhu which is an online tool for humanization of antibodies. It provides 
tools for all the critical humanization steps: template selection of the human antibody, grafting, evaluation of 
backmutations, antibody modelling, and structural analysis. Tabhu requires the VH and VL of the non-human 
antibody as input information along with the antigen volume. Firstly, it finds suitable human FR donor that 
have the highest similarity with the V region of the non-human antibody. The user can choose the desired FR 
donor to be grafted with the non-human CDRs. Hereafter, the humanization procedure begins with the CDR 
grafting followed by a binding profile similarity estimate, where the binding mode of the native non-human 
antibody is compared to the binding mode of the humanized antibody. Then residues suitable for 
backmutations are chosen and subsequently the binding mode is evaluated again. The binding mode profile is 
presented as a plot showing the predicted probabilities for each residue to be involved in the recognition of the 
antigen, for both the humanized and mouse antibody [81]. Residues predicted to yield are more similar 
probability will be an important residue to backmutate, indicated by a high mutation score in TabHu. The 
process of introducing backmutations into the humanized antibody can be iteratively repeated. If the user 
however has introduced backmutations that generate clashes or cavities Tabhu generates an alert and allows 
the user to ignore or remove the troubling backmutations. The user can finalize the model when the desired 
binding mode of the humanized antibody has been achieved and retrieve sequences and three-dimensional 
structures [80].

4.3.6.3 
T-cell epitope prediction 
As murine parts are added to the human antibody during the CDR grafting and backmutation potential 
introduced immunogenicity can be studied by predicting T-cell epitopes of the given humanized antibody 
sequence. 
The human MHC, HLA, is located on chromosome 6. The classic MHC I molecules are encoded by HLA-A, 
HLA-B, and HLA-C genes. MHC II are expressed as either HLA-DR, HLA-DQ, or HLA-DP isotypes [82]. 
TCRs exhibit cross-reactivity enabling them to engage with multiple peptide-MHC complexes. Cross-
reactivity is achieved by the ability of the TCR to tolerate differences in the residues outside of the peptide 
core [83]. MHC class I molecules present 8-10 amino acid long peptides to CD8+ T-cells, whereas MHC class 
II molecules presents longer peptides. The binding pocket of the MHC class II molecules is open, limiting the 
constraints on the length of class II epitopes [84]. 
It is possible to predict peptides binding to MHC molecules. As they play a crucial role in the immune response 
outcome, predicting MHC binding epitopes is of great importance. Peptides presented on MHC class II 
molecules derive from the extracellular environment. They can stimulate cellular and humoral immunity, via 
CD4+ T helper cells, towards pathogens [85]. 
Different computational methods are available for peptide predictions [86]–[90]. NetMHCpan is an online 
server which can predict MHC class I binding epitopes with data sets containing multiple receptors and peptide 
lengths, based on neural network trained, from multiple MHC class I, data. NetMHCpan is able to handle a 
varying length profile (8-14mer peptides) along with a simple alignment step allowing deletions/insertions in 
the peptide data [90]. NetMHCIIpan is also an online server able to predict MHC class II binding peptides, 
based on neural network training of MHC class II peptide binding data [89]. Both servers predicts whether the 
peptides are strong binders, weak binders, or not binding,  together with the identification of the nine amino 
acid long peptide core [89], [90]. Both NetMHCpan and NetMHCIIpan use the term “strong binder” to describe 
epitopes that are confidently predicted to actually bind the MHC molecule.

4.4 Aim of this study 
Immunotherapy using T-cells and TCRs have shown great potential [31], [35], [38], [40] but are also facing 
challenges with HVGDs [34], [38], [39] and the risk of expressing mispaired TCR complexes containing an 
endogenous and an exogenous chain [31], [33]. During this study, we try to overcome this problem by 
performing in silico analysis of the interface of the TCR with the aim of introducing paired mutations that can 
stabilize the TCR complex, making it more favorable than the wildtype TCR. We are analyzing the variable 
domain a human αβ TCR recognizing the SLL antigen from the PRAME gene, and the constant domain of the 
human TCR from PDB entry 1OGA.  
The interface analyses are executed using two different software tools, FoldX and Rosetta, to compare the 
software and the results thereof to each other. The FoldX AnalyseComplex and Rosetta ddg_monomer 
applications are used to estimate the protein energetics (ΔG and ΔΔG) as a result of the proposed mutations. 
We wanted to identify mutations that would make it unfavorable for an endogenous and an exogenous chain 
to pair together. While obtaining an energy of the exogenous-exogenous complex lower or at least the same as 
wildtype. This would ensure that the pairing of the exogenous-exogenous complex is not unfavorable. Lastly, 
the exogenous monomers should more or less have the same individual energy as wildtype. This would make 
sure that the mutation on the exogenous chain would not be so unfavorable that it would impact the folding of 
the chain. 
Another T-cell immunotherapy approach, that also have shown potential, is the use of CARs [47], [49]. The 
CARs are engineered to consist of an extracellular domain of an antigen-recognition moiety, and an 
intracellular domain composed of costimulatory domains. But, one of the issues that are faced with CARs are 
the potential immunogenicity when using murine structures as the extracellular domain. In this study, we aim 
to perform in silico humanization of the variable domain of a murine antibody recognizing CD19 to avoid 
immunogenicity. The humanization is conducted in humanization tool TabHu using CDR grafting and 
backmutations.

5 Methods 
For the in silico interface analysis, two different software tools, FoldX and Rosetta, were used to analyze the 
interface of the αβ TCR that binds to the SLL antigen of the PRAME protein, which was found in different 
tumors including leukemia [41]. The α and β TCR chain sequences were provided by Concetta Quintarelli and 
Biagio De Angelis from Ospedale Pediatrico Bambino Gesù. The constant domain of the human TCR from 
PDB entry 1OGA [91] and the murine TCR from the PDB entry 2Q86 [92] were also used during the interface 
analysis. 
In the second part of the project, we created a humanized version of the scFV CAR molecule targeting CD19. 
The CAR sequence was as well provided by Concetta Quintarelli and Biagio De Angelis.  
 
5.1 Modelling of lymphocyte receptors 
The TCR was modelled using LYRA 1.0 (http://www.cbs.dtu.dk/services/LYRA/) [74] with manual template 
selection. The α- and β-chains were given as input and no PDB IDs were blacklisted. After the results were 
generated, the templates were inspected by clicking “Manual Template Selection” on the navigation bar. 
Templates with an Identity Score above 85% were chosen, for both the α- and β-chain, and combined in all 
possible combinations. Models were built for all combinations and the PDB files were downloaded. the best 
model was chosen based on the resolution and R-values of the α and β templates obtained from 
http://www.rcsb.org/pdb. 
The CAR related sequences were modelled likewise using LYRA with manual template selection. Templates 
were selected based on a high Blosum score and high %ID, with the Blosum score weighing higher than %ID. 
The resolution, R-free, and R-work from the PDB entry were also considered. And lastly, visual inspection of 
the different template suggestions. The templates were selected based on the above mentioned factors. The 
unrefined modelled structures were downloaded for the models.

mean of the top-3 most accurate energies were used to calculate the most accurate ΔΔG [69]:

Eventually, the best model is chosen based on the quality retrieved from the template structures (Table 2). A 
good protein structure has a high resolution (small Å values), the higher the resolution the more information 
you have. High resolution data makes it more easy to produce a precise and accurate model [108]. As the 
resolution is approximately the same for the α-chain templates, then the choice is based on the R-values. The 
R value is an indicator of the quality of the data and is obtained during the refinement of the structure. The R 
free value gives an indication of whether the data has been overfitted during the refinement, as the R free is 
not based on data used during the refinement (cross-validation). Low resolution data are more prone to overfit, 
as few data is available. If good data is available and the structure fits well with the electron density data, then 
it is expected to observe a good correlation between the data used to refine (R) and the date used to cross-
validate (R free). Thus, if the R-values are similar, with a difference of approximately 0.05, it indicates that 
the data is of good quality [108]. The 1J8H template has the lowest difference in R-values with 0.035 (Table 
2). The β-chain is chosen based on the same approach. The 3MV7 and 3MB8 have approximately the same 
resolution, but 3MV8 has the smallest R-value difference with 0.047 (Table 2). Thus, the 1J8H-3MV8 model 
is chosen for further analysis.

6.3 Alanine scanning 
An Alanine Scanning is a useful and rather quick analysis that gives an indication of important residues in the 
structure. The FoldX AlaScan yields an output file which contains the Gibbs free energy in kcal/mol for the 
mutation to alanine for each residue in the structure. The ΔΔG(bind) values, extracted from the Robetta output, 
gives the predicted change upon alanine mutation in free energy (kcal/mol). The energies from both the FoldX 
and Robetta analysis for the variable and constant interface residues can be found in Appendix A7.  
The FoldX and Robetta energies for both the variable and constant domain show a positive correlation between 
the FoldX and Robetta data (Figure 10) with a Pearson correlation = 0.35. So even though the energies are not 
calculated to be the same using FoldX and Robetta, there is some correlation between the results.

6.8 Evaluation and selection of mutations 
At this point we have found ten mutations to be favorable and all meet the three criteria (equation 1-5). Four 
mutations on the variable domain, Mut01, Mut16, Mut21, and Mut38, and six mutations on the constant 
domain, Mut_AC01, Mut_AC03_5, Mut_ACM04_2, Mut_P01, Mut_P02, and Mut_P05. We want to take a 
closer look at each mutation, on the sequence and structural level, to select the best candidates. 
 
 Sequence analysis 
The variable domain portray variability, hence the name. Therefore, we will do a sequence logo to get an 
indication of how much variety the specific positions of the mutations portray. If the residue is too variable it 
might be a risky residue to mutate as it varies, thus the endogenous sequence might not have the same residue 
from which we designed the destabilizing mutation from. If the endogenous thus have another residue, the 
outcome of mispaired pairing is a bit unsure, as the mutation could be destabilizing, stabilizing, or have a 
neural effect.  
The sequence logo for the α- (Figure 18 & Figure 20) and β-chain (Figure 19 & Figure 21) gives a visual 
representation of residue patterns found in the submitted multiple alignment of both the variable and constant 
domain. The entire sequence logo can be found in Appendix A12. The variable domain is represented as the 
first part of the sequence logo with a varying sequence frequency, whereas the latter part of the logo portrays 
the conserved domain with highly conserved residues. This is seen for both the α- and β-chain (Appendix 
A12). 
For the variable mutations on the α-chain, only Mut01 has the residue it is mutated to represented on the 
sequence logo (Figure 18). Whereas none of the four mutations has it mutant residue represented on the β-
chain (Figure 19). But the wildtype phenylalanine is quite conserved, which could indicate that the residue is 
important for functionality. However, the tryptophan is found at a lower frequency, supporting Mut01 on the 
α-chain. Of Mut16, Mut21, and Mut38 Mut16 show the least variability on both chains, which is favorable as 
explain before. Mut21 and Mut38 portray some variability on both chains, which make the actual outcome of 
the mutation unknown, as the residue of the endogenous TCR will vary a lot. However, for the constant 
mutations on both the α- and β-chain are all mutations, except for Mut_ACM04_2, fully conserved (Figure 20 
& Figure 21). This is not surprising though, as the sequence of the conserved domain is conserved.  
Mut_ACM04_2 portray a low sequence frequency for cysteine on both the α- and β-chain (Figure 20 & Figure 
21).

 Structural analysis 
Each of the ten mutations was visually inspected using PyMOL to investigate if any of the mutations are to be 
discarded as candidates. We will look at the FoldX AnalyseComplex output structures as we based our 
selection of the ten mutation on the FoldX analysis.   
When we look at Mut01 we see that there is quite a lot of space between the valine and tryptophan (Figure 
22A). Thus, we suspect that there is enough space for the mixed complex of the exogenous α-chain and the 
endogenous β-chain to pair. By mutating the valine back to phenylalanine, we see that it is possible to have 
the mixed complex with the two big amino acids (Appendix A14). When mutating the tryptophan back to 
phenylalanine, we see that there are no space issues either (Appendix A14). Thus, neither of the two mixed 
complexes are impaired by serious clashes as we expected. 
The alanine and tryptophan of Mut16 has a good and close interaction in the output structure (Figure 22B). 
When we mutate each residue back to wildtype we see that the pairing in each of the mixed complexes is 
impaired. When we mutate the alanine back to leucine, obtaining a complex of the exogenous β-chain and 
endogenous α-chain, we see that the leucine is too big and conflict with the tryptophan (Appendix A14). When 
we mutate the tryptophan back to leucine we see that there now is too much space and a deep pocked is formed 
between the two residues (Appendix A14). However, even though the mixed complexes fit with the desired 
criteria, we fear that mutating leucine to tryptophan might be too drastic. As the tryptophan is so much bigger 
it might be able to change the interaction of the α- and β-chain and thus changing the orientation of the CDRs. 
By changing the CDRs orientation we might lose binding specificity.  
When we look at the rotamers of the Mut21 residues in PyMOL we find that the residues are either too close 
or too far from one another to interact. According the FoldX analysis the residues of Mut21 do not interact 
with one another either (Figure 22C). If we mutate the residues and look at the mixed complexes we see that 
there are no space issues for the complex with the exogenous α-chain and endogenous β -chain (Appendix 
A14). Whereas the residues of the complex with the endogenous α-chain and exogenous β-chain clash 
(Appendix A14). Given that these residues are solvent exposed, the energy gap due to the salt bridge formation 
energy is lowered by the solvation.

 Selecting mutations 
We choose to discard Mut01 as neither of the mixed complexes are unfavorable (Appendix A14). Additionally, 
the residues of Mut01 are found in FR4, close to the CDR3 which might be risky (Appendix A13). We do this 
even though the mutation is supported by the sequence logo of the α-chain (Figure 18) and the interaction 
energies (Table 8). We also choose to discard Mut16 even though both mixed complexes are unfavorable 
(Appendix A14), the residues are found in FR2 (Appendix A13), and the interaction energies are good (Table 
8). We are concerned that the mutation might be too drastic and thus influence the binding. Additionally, we 
choose to discard Mut21, despite the favorable interaction energy (Table 8). The residues are too close to 
CDR2 (α) and in CDR3 (β), which might be risky locations (Appendix A13). The mixed complex of the 
exogenous α-chain and endogenous β -chain is not unfavorable as well (Appendix A14). Lastly, we also choose 
to discard Mut38. Similar to Mut16, we find the residues in the CDR3 (Appendix A13), and the mixed complex 
of the exogenous α-chain and endogenous β-chain is not unfavorable (Appendix A14). Thus, we end up with 
suggesting none of the 38 proposed mutations on the variable domain of the TCR.  
Previously we found that Mut_AC01 and Mut_AC03_5 neutralize each other’s effect, if they are analyzed 
together (Table 13). As we did not find the mixed complex of Mut_AC03_5 unfavorable (Appendix A15), and 
the fact that the ΔΔG of Mut_AC01 is somewhat better (Table 11), we choose to discard Mut_AC03_5. Even 
though FoldX was not able to introduce the disfulphide bridge of Mut_ACM04_2 (Figure 17A) yielding bad 
unfavorable interaction energies (Table 17), we believe that this mutation has potential. The mutations of 
Mut_P01, -P02, and -P05 are similar. We choose to discard Mut_P02 as Mut_P01 and -P05 have better 
interaction energies (Table 15) and the residues of the mutations interact better and are fully satisfied in bonds 
(Figure 23D-F). 
Thus, we end up proposing no mutations on the variable domain and four mutations on the constant domain: 
Mut_AC01, Mut_ACM04_2, Mut_P01, and Mut_P05. These four mutations follow the three criteria we set 
(equation 1-5) and thus have a more favorable interaction energy for our exogenous-exogenous complex than 
for the mixed endogenous-exogenous complexes.

6.9 Humanization 
The murine CAR structure targets CD19, but as it is non-human it can cause an immunogenic reaction when 
injected in humans. By humanizing the structure, we remove the majority of the murine sequence while still 
preserving the binding specificity of the murine structure.  
The single chain sequence is separated into the L and H chain by removing the joining linker region from the 
scFV.   
 
The variable domain of the CAR structure is humanized using TabHu with germline genes. As the mature 
framework sequences only have a sequence similarity of 53.51%, for the highest match, we choose only to 
look at the germline sequences. After submission to TabHu the human templates are selected. We see that the 
top ten entries for the LV sequence are from the same gene family (IGKV1) and therefore we choose the 
template with the highest sequence similarity (Table 18). Whereas for the HV chain are the top ten entries 
composed of two gene families (IGHV4 and IGHV2). Thus, we choose a template from each family. To make 
sure we do not select a heavy chain gene that are never found to pair with our selected IGKV1 family, we look 
at the VH/VL pairing heatmap generated by TabHu (Figure 24). The heatmap show that the pairing of IGKV1 
to IGHV4 or IGHV2 is not unlikely. Therefor we choose the template for the two families based on the 
sequence similarity (Table 18). The heatmap reveals that pairing to IGKV1 is more likely with either IGHV1 
or IGHV3 (Figure 24). However, we do not choose them as they have a lower sequence similarity of 40.82 
and 53.06 % for IGHV1 and IGHV3 respectively. The TabHu distance is a measure of the distance between 
the humanized and murine structure in respect to the binding mode. We see that the G2 structure is slightly 
more similar to the murine than G1 (Table 18).

6.9.1.2 
Inspection of structures 
The unrefined LYRA models of the murine, G1, and G2 are minimized using FoldX prior to inspection of the 
structures (Table 20). 
We do the following analyses to check if the human framework sequences are happy to accept the murine 
CDRs, an to see how well the CDRs were grafted onto it. Based on the following analysis we will chose either 
G1 or G2 to move on with the humanization on.  
To this aim, we validated the murine, G1, and G2 structures with PDBsum and inspected the Ramachandram 
plot of the FoldX minimized structures. As the FoldX minimization does not interfere with the backbone, we 
would not see a difference between the minimized and non-minimized. For any residue in an out-of-
Ramachandran conformation, we checked the Ramachandran plots of the templates used in LYRA, to see if 
the problems are due to the CDR grafting or already were present in the template structure. The Ramachandran 
plots for murine, G1, and G2 can be seen in Appendix B1. For the murine structure, 87.6 and 9.8 % residues 
are located in the most favored and additional allowed regions. Four L-chain and one H-chain residues are 
found in the generously allowed regions.  Two of the residues are also found in the template Ramachandran 
plots, and the three remaining are part of the L-CDR2, so suspect them to be bad due to a not so good CDR 
grafting.  
For G1 86.3 and 12.1 % are in the most favored and additionally allowed regions, respectively. Two residues 
are in the disallowed regions (H-Glu55 and L-Thr51). For G2 86.4 and 12.6 % are in the most favored and 
additionally allowed regions, respectively. Only one residue is found in the disallowed regions (L-Thr51). The 
residue of L51, which is part of L-CDR2, is however often found in the disallowed regions [110]–[112]. The 
L51 residue of the 3NCJ template is also found in the disallowed region. The other disallowed residue of G1, 
H-Glu55, is part of the H-CDR2, which could be an indication that the human structure is not fully happy with 
the grafting of H-CDR2.

looking for bumps. A bump is an indication of amino acids clashing due to lack of space. The python module 
“show_pumps” is applied to the G1 and G2 models of both the unrefined LYRA model and the FoldX 
minimized one.

 Backmutations 
By now we have grafted our murine CDRs onto a suitable human framework. However, there is a chance that 
during the grafting process we have lost the binding affinity. This can be restored by backmutating some of 
the residues of human origin to the corresponding murine residue [80]. TabHu proposes backmutations with a 
given score. The higher the mutation score the higher the probability that the residue is involved in recognizing 
the antigen [81], and thus the more closer the binding profiles of the human and murine will be. We want to 
obtain a close binding profile without gaining immunogenicity. 
The binding profile of the H-chain differ mostly around position 34, 56, and 100. The L-chain binding profile 
are quite similar, but differ mostly around 48 and 89 (Appendix B5). The first backmutation proposed by 
TabHu, with the highest score, on both the H- and L-chain are implemented in Round 1 (Table 23). We see 
that the humanized H-chain profile is more similar to the mouse around position 35, 93, 100, 104 (Appendix 
B5). The binding profile of the L-chain are now more like the mouse around position 35 and 89 (Appendix 
B5). In Round 2 we introduce a backmutation only on the H-chain (Table 23). The second backmutation on 
the L-chain TabHu proposes are on L42. As the binding profile is already quite similar we do not find it 
necessary to introduce this backmutation. However, as this residue is important for the interface type (Figure 
26), we keep this in mind, as this potential backmutation could be suggested if the first in vitro humanization 
does not work. The third backmutation proposed on the H-chain is on position 5. However, H5 have been 
suggested to affect the expression of the antibody [117], and for that reason we choose not to go further with 
backmutating the H-chain.  
As we found earlier, our hum-G2 and murine structure do not share the same type of interface. This was 
identified by the region of L39-44 having a high RMSD (Appendix B2). We will try to compensate for this by 
mutating L41 and L44 back to murine as they are the most important positions, in order to resemble interface

7 Discussion 
Immunotherapies show great promise as cancer treatment and their development is an important field of 
research as the traditional treatment options are not always capable of curing. This is especially observed for 
leukemia, as immunotherapy is the only solution for some of the leukemias.  
During this study, we have investigated two immunotherapy approaches: engineering of TCRs and CARs. 
 
7.1 T-cell receptor 
 Evaluation of FoldX and Rosetta 
FoldX predicted only four, out of the 38 proposed mutations (Table 7) in the variable domain to be favorable: 
Mut01, Mut16, Mut21, and Mut38 (Table 8). 12 of the 38 mutations, with the best predictions, were hereafter 
analyzed in Rosetta. According to Rosetta neither of the 12 mutations satisfied the three criteria, and none of 
the mutations were favorable. Of the 29 mutations proposed on the constant domain (Table 10) three were 
found favorable (Table 11) in the FoldX analysis and whereas none of the 10 mutations analyzed with Rosetta 
was found favorable (Table 9 & Table 12). This was also observed for the additional mutations proposed based 
on the Mut_AC01 and the murine comparison. Thus, Rosetta did not predict any mutations to be favorable, 
and did not produce consistent results (Appendix A9). Therefore, we do not believe that Rosetta produces 
trustworthy results, and we choose to evaluate good or bad mutations based on the FoldX predictions.  
However, as Rosetta did not predict any mutations to be favorable we decided to trust in the FoldX results. As 
this is an in silico analysis we can still not know whether the FoldX results are trustworthy, as we do not have 
any experimental data of energy prediction of our proposed mutations to compare to. Nevertheless, based on 
the varying FoldX results, the fact that some mutations are predicted to be both good and bad, we decide to 
trust FoldX to give an indication of good or bad – favorable or non-favorable mutations.  
Our Rosetta results are however supported by previous findings by student Kristian Davidsen (unpublished 
data), he compared experimental results of both stabilizing and destabilizing mutations to the Rosetta 
prediction. He found that Rosetta rather poorly determined stabilizing mutations, whereas it did a good job on 
determining destabilizing mutations. As our aim was to find stabilizing mutations, Rosetta might simply not 
be the correct prediction software for our purpose.  
 
 Evaluating our approach 
FoldX predicted four mutations on the variable domain and six mutations on the constant domain to be 
favorable. However, by a sequential and structural analyses, we discarded six mutations ending up with 
Mut_AC01, Mut_ACM04_2, Mut_P01, and Mut_P05 on the constant domain. Most of the mutations were 
discarded based on the structural analysis in PyMOL, where we saw no major conflicts on a structural level of 
the mixed complex (Appendix A14 & A15). Thus, it is important to not only consider the energy predictions,

After the FoldX, sequential, and structural analysis we end up with four mutations on the constant domain of 
the human αβ PRAME TCR that we believe are, individually, capable of stabilizing the TCR complex while 
destabilizing the mispaired complex. This stabilization should make the expression of the TCR more favorable 
over the expression of the mispaired complex. The stabilization will hopefully lead to the higher expression of 
the exogenous TCR which will lead to a better treatment. By decreasing the chance of having the mispaired 
complex we also decrease the chance of gaining unfavorable effects such as new antigen specificity or GVHDs. 
Thus, making TCRs with the mutations a candidate for immunotherapeutic leukemia treatment. 
Finding and analyzing stabilizing mutations is a fairly simple approach. It is simple to execute the energy 
predictions using the FoldX software, and it seem to be a good way of identifying mutations to be further 
analyzed in the lab. As lab experiments, can be time consuming and very expensive, it is important to limit the 
number of experiments. However, the method used in this study to find and propose mutations was done 
manually and was thus also time consuming. It could be beneficial to find another approach for the search and 
generation of mutations.  
 
 Other approches 
Sommermeyer et al. [31] were able to overcome the problem of mispaired complexes differently. By using 
TCR gene transfer in a murine T-cell line they were able to eliminate the potential risk along with elimination 
of the original TCR antigen specificity on the endogenous TCR. They only observed mispaired complexes if 
the T-cells were forced to, by experiencing the absence of their respective chain partner. They reasoned their 
observation to be because of the gene transferred TCR being strong, in terms of surface expression, and thus 
being expressed over the others. They were able to detect the same phenomenon in human T-cells as well [31].  
Knies et al. [118] were able to demonstrate a significant reduction of mispairing to any TCRα using a single 
chain TCR in human T-cells. They used a 3-domain single chain TCR, composed of murinized Cβ, Vα, and 
Vβ, to avid mispairing because of sterical hindrance along with the autonomously expressed murine Cα [118].  
Another approach to solve this problem could be to remove the endogenous TCR by gene editing approaches 
(e.g. CRISPR), but this adds an additional complex step in the engineering of the T-cell and risks to reduce the 
vitality of the lymphocytes, thus reducing the efficacy of the treatment [119]. 
Beside Sommermeyer [31] and Knies [118] approaches are also others e.g. murinization of TCRs [120] or 
introducing dominant TCRs intro murine T-cells [40].

7.2 Chimeric antigen receptors 
The fact that having the interface type B which are only found in mouse, on the antibody to be humanize, can 
potentially lead to an ineffective humanization [114]. This might complicate things a bit for our humanization 
of the CAR structure. However, we tried to overcome this problem by backmutation two of the interface 
determining residues (Table 22 & Figure 26). We backmutated L41 and L44 (Table 23) as these are the two 
most important residues for the interface type (Figure 26). TabHu did not detect these to mutations as important 
as they only had a mutation score of 0.056 and 0.000 for L44 and L41, respectively (Table 23). Even though 
these residues are important for the interface, and the interface of the TCR can impact the antigen binding sites. 
However, TabHu did predict L42 as an important residue, which are also an interface important residue (Table 
22 & Figure 26). This residue could be considered to be backmutated in case our proposed humanization does 
not work in the in vitro analysis. 
It could be a good solution to find another CD19 recognizing antibody which have a type A interface. This 
would, compared to our CAR mean that the L41 and L44 back mutations is not necessary. Thus, limiting the 
amount of back mutations.  
The backmutation on L80 (P  Q) led to the formation of six new strong binder epitopes (Table 26 & Table 
27). As the human binding profile of the L-chain are acceptably similar to the murine, it might not be worth 
including the backmutation to risk the potential immunogenicity due to the new epitopes. 
Humanization is the common solution to tackle the potential immunogenicity related to murine structures. 
Qian et al. [121] humanized a murine scFV-based CAR recognizing CD19 successfully. They were also able 
to detect CARs expressing the humanized scFV domain in vitro and in vivo. They demonstrated that the 
humanization did not have an impact on in vitro and in vivo function, as it was comparable to the non-
humanized CAR [121].

8 Conclusion 
We demonstrated that four mutations (Mut_AC02, Mut_ACM04_2, Mut_P01, Mut_P05) on the constant 
domain of a αβ PRAME TCR individually were able to yield favorable ΔΔG that followed our three criteria. 
The mutations give rise to a stabilization of the exogenous TCR complex and destabilization of the mispaired 
complexes due to clashes or the formation of cavities. This should enable a more favorable expression of the 
exogenous TCR complex over the endogenous and mispaired complexes.  
Furthermore, we demonstrated that it was possible to humanize the CD19 CAR by grafting the CDR regions 
of the murine structure onto a human framework sequence, and backmutating four residues: two mutations on 
the H-chain and two on the L-chain. The H-chain backmutation generated are more similar human binding 
profile for the H-chain and the L-chain backmutations compensated for the different interface types between 
the human framework and murine sequence.

9 Future perspectives 
The four mutations we proposed to circumvent the challenges of expressing mispaired TCRs are at the moment 
being tested in the lab at Ospedale Pediatrico Bambino Gesù in Italy by our collaborators. They are testing to 
see if any of the four mutations would work in practice and thus observe the expressing of the exogenous TCR 
in vitro. 
To investigate if any potential favorable mutations were missed, we would like to do a more thorough and 
organized approach when looking for mutations to propose, e.g. by building a pipeline to automate the 
generation of mutations, the FoldX prediction, and the evaluation of the ΔGs.  This could be done by mutating 
each residue of the defined interface to a set of amino acids, based on the same set of rules we more or less 
used during this study. Mutating charged residues to the opposite charge, mutating polar residues no 
hydrophobic, and the other way around. And lastly, designing a protocol able to automatically analyze the 
huge amount of data and selecting favorable mutations based on the ΔΔGs.  
The results from the humanization of the CAR structures are also to be tested in the lab in Italy. Before we 
hand over the humanized sequences we would like to investigate the humanization of the framework sequence 
of mature genes. These were quickly set aside, because of a lower sequence similarity compared to the germline 
sequence, and because of the time limitation of this thesis.
