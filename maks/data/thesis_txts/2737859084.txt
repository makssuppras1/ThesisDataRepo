Abstract
Offshore wind energy is one of the fastest growing sources of renewable energy. As most
of the wind resource around the world is in regions with water depth of more than 60m,
floating foundations are being researched and in the future, a huge portion of offshore
wind energy will be comprised of floating wind turbines.
Floating wind turbines undergo large tilt angles due to the wave and wind loads. Due to
this they are subject to higher fatigue loads and hence the towers of these turbines need
to be made stiffer. This causes the 1st Tower bending mode to be closer to 3P frequency
which poses the risk of resonance. To cope with this problem, a Tuned Mass Damper
(TMD) can be used. The TMD can be represented as a heavy body with a spring and
dashpot and is installed high up in the turbine i.e. in the either in the Nacelle or the Tower.
The TMD is used to dampen the 1st Tower bending mode by installing it high up in the
turbine i.e. either in the Nacelle or the Tower.
In this report, initial groundwork of tuning the TMD is established on a single degree of
freedom system which is basically a single mass oscillator with a spring and dashpot
where the optimal TMD tuning parameters are set and the effect of the TMD is analysed.
Frequency analysis for different damping ratios is carried out and it was established that
extra damping is needed in the TMD in order to keep the stroke in check. Modelling of
the endstop is also carried out so that the oscillations of the TMD are further reduced and
the TMD does not collide with the structure.
The turbine that is being analysed is the IEA­15­240 RWT Offshore reference turbine with
the UMaine Volturn US­S Floating semi­submersible platform developed by the University
of Maine. A reduced order model of two degrees of freedom is formulated which include
the tower fore­aft motion and the platform pitch. The equations of motion for this model
are derived and then the response of the system is analyzed with and without the TMD.
The single mass oscillator system is initially solved using a Newmark time integration
scheme which is a simulation method used to solve linear systems. The end­stop non­
linearity is taken care of by employing the ode45 function in MATLAB which makes use of
an adaptive time step and solves non­linear second order differential equations by using
the Runge­Kutta integration scheme. The reduced order model is subjected three types
of load cases­ a harmonic load 5% under the tower natural frequency , a stochastic load
simulating the 3P frequency and an impulse load at the 3P frequency. The model is
analysed with and without the TMD along with the endstop. The TMD shows promising
results with the displacement due to tower bending being reduced.

1
Introduction
Wind Energy is one of the main leaders in the growing Renewable energy sector with
around 1TW of installed capacity around the world and with policies in place to upgrade
the production to 2TW by the end of 2030 [1].Offshore locations offer enhanced wind
energy potential due to reduced surface roughness, leading to elevated average wind
speeds at lower heights.
Traditionally, offshore wind farms have employed bottom­fixed foundations. Monopile
foundations have been the preferred type of foundations with accounting for over 80%
of all existing installed offshore foundations in Europe [2] ,where a single column is driven
into the seabed on top of which the turbine is mounted. However, such foundations limit
the deployment of such wind farms to areas with a water depth of about 60 metres. With
80% of the world’s offshore potential in areas with a greater water depth, massive devel­
opment in floating offshore wind energy is expected with a forecast to reach an installed
capacity of 16.5 GW by 2030 [1].
1.1
Floating Offshore Wind Turbines
Several floating foundations are being researched and the choice of the foundation is
dependent on the water depth, cost, and complexity of transportation. The different types
of floating foundations currently in the market are as follows:
1.1.1
Spar type Floater
Spar type foundations typically consist of a cylinder which is kept in position by mooring
lines with drag or suction anchors. These types of floaters are typically ballast­stabilized
by adjusting the centre of gravity of the floater below the centre of buoyancy. Spar foun­
dations have the advantage of a simpler design and lower installed mooring cost. How­
ever, they need deeper waters of over 100m water depth and more heavy­lift vessels are
needed than the other concepts to be applicable. Currently they can only be installed in
relatively sheltered deep waters.[3]

1.1.2
Tension Leg Platform
The Tension Leg Platform (TLP) is a highly buoyant platform with taut mooring lines.
These mooring lines, called Tendons or Tethers, restrict the vertical and rotational mo­
tions of the offshore wind turbines i.e heave, pitch, and roll. The platform is compliant
with surge and sway motions.[5]. TLPs are particularly good for preserving the advan­
tages of a fixed platform while reducing the costs of production for greater water depths
with assembly possible onshore. However, a special­purpose vessel may be needed
depending on the design and also they are harder to keep stable during transport and
installation.
Figure 1.2: Tension Leg Platform type floater on Offshore Wind Turbine [6]
1.1.3
Semi­submersible type Floater
Semi­submersibles are multi­legged structures with a large deck. The legs are intercon­
nected underwater with horizontal members known as pontoons. These types of floaters
are water­plane stabilized where water­plane area and moment of inertia play a major role
in vertical and rotational stability.
These floaters can be operated in water depths as low as 40 meters. They are also eas­
ier to transport using conventional tugs and can be constructed onshore. However, they
tend to use more material and are larger structures compared to other concepts. The
fabrication is also more complex compared to a spar­type foundation.

1.2
Dynamic problems in Floating Wind Turbines
Floating turbines, however, are subject to large static tilts and slow rigid body motions.
These motions cause high fatigue loading on the turbine; hence, to cope with this prob­
lem, the turbine’s tower needs to be made stiffer compared to the bottom­fixed offshore
turbines. Due to increased stiffness, the 1st tower bending mode frequency is higher
compared to its bottom­fixed counterpart and is closer to the 3P frequency (3 times the
rotational frequency of the blades, since there are three blades) when the turbine is pro­
ducing full power. This poses the risk of resonance and causes increased fatigue loads.
Hence, reducing the amplitude of vibrations of the first bending mode is essential. This can
be done by increasing the total damping of the structure. One can increase the structural
damping of the tower but that is more complex and expensive. A much cheaper option is
to use a passive or semi­active Tuned mass damper (TMD) that is mounted either in the
Nacelle or the turbine tower.
The primary objective of this thesis is to analyse the performance of a TMD on floating wind
turbines and work on mitigating the risk of resonance and reduce the vibrations in the 1st
tower bending mode. The mounting of TMDs are done in tight spaces and hence it is also
important to contain the stroke of the TMD within set limits. Therefore, implementation of
endstop with suitable parameters is essential.
1.3
Tuned Mass Dampers in Offshore Wind Turbines
In offshore wind turbines, Tuned mass dampers are considered the best form of reducing
vibrations because of the advantages of tuning the natural frequency as will be explained
in Chapter 2. Different types of TMDs exist in the industry and a few examples are given
here as follows:
Tune Liquid Column Damper: The conventional liquid column damper is a U­shaped
liquid column tube as shown in Figure 1.4. When the tube moves with the structure, the
liquid moves inside the tube, and with properly selected damper parameters, the relative
motion of the liquid can dampen the vibration of the structure. The natural frequency of
such a damper is determined by the length of the liquid column [8]. Typically, water is the
liquid used. However, the damping properties can be used enhanced by using a higher
viscosity liquid.

1.6
Floating Platform
The platform is made of three cuboidal pontoons at 120◦angle with each other and are
filled with water. Further, the platform consists of three cylindrical buoyant columns radially
spaced on the pontoons and the tower rests on a fourth buoyant column at the center of
the platform. The three radial buoyant columns have an iron­core ballast situated at the
bottom.
The platform mass is 17854 tonnes of which the structural steel is 3914 tonnes. The radial
columns have fixed iron­core ballasts with a total weight of 2540 tonnes. This weight
is distributed between the three radial columns. The cuboidal pontoons are filled with
seawater which amounts to 11300 tonnes. An additional point mass of 100 tonnes was
assumed at the top of the central buoyant column as the tower­platform interface.

system at time tn+1 =tn + h given the values (xn, ˙xn) at a previous time step tn [12].
The time integration procedure calculates this state vector by introducing two weighting
parameters γ and β, which collectively determine the algorithm’s stability along with the

2.4
End­stop design for the Tuned Mass Damper
The Tuned Mass damper on a wind turbine is typically installed in the tower or the nacelle
where there are very tight space constraints. Hence, the TMD’s total stroke must be
limited based on the space available. Typically, for wind turbines, this is done by opting
for a higher damping than the optimal value explained in section 2.3. This is not an issue
for harmonic loads which are typically at a single frequency. However, for realistic loads
with a spectrum of frequencies, solely choosing a higher damping ratio is not enough.
On the off chance the damper exceeds the space limits, a different method needs to be
employed to limit the stroke of the damper.

5
Conclusion and Future Outlook
5.1
Conclusion
The aim of this work was as defined in Section 1.2
’ to analyse the performance of a TMD on floating wind turbines and work on mitigating the
risk of resonance and reduce the vibrations in the 1st tower bending mode. The mounting
of TMDs are done in tight spaces and hence it is also important to contain the stroke of
the TMD within set limits. Therefore, implementation of endstop with suitable parameters
is essential.’
The reduced order model was subject to two load cases which were targeted the 3P
frequency of the turbine. The TMD applied to the model was successful in reducing the
amplitude of the vibrations due to tower bending. In the case of impact load, a large
reduction in the RNA displacement was observed, proving the performance of the TMD
Endstop modelling came with added non­linearity in the system which was solved uing
the ode45 function in MATLAB. The endstop was successful in limiting the stroke limits
and preserved the performance of the TMD.
5.2
Future Outlook
Extending the DOFs :The analysis presented in this thesis is based off a 2 DOF model
with only the tower fore­aft bending mode and platform pitch mode accounted for. Addi­
tional DOFs can be incorporated and the reduced order model can be expanded.
Christian et.al. [20] have developed a 17 DOF model that was developed using the Eu­
ler Lagrange method which showcases that implementing a higher DOF model helps in
understanding the coupling of all the different DOFs and can also help in gaining a fairly
accurate assessment.
More Relevant turbine data: The stochastic loading conditions used in this thesis were
derived from a Vestas Turbine. The IEA 15 MW turbine was used whose stiffness and
damping parameters were tweaked in order to solve the issues faced by Vestas. Real
turbine data and response can improve the quality of work by enhancing it and making it
more aligned to Vestas’s use case.
More complex TMD: The TMD used in this work has been a 1 DOF block mass with a
linear spring and damper. A more complex TMD system such as a PTMD would be of
interest and could make a stronger viewpoint in terms of design.
HAWC2 simulation and controller tuning: A full scale load simulation could be made
in HAWC2 with controller tuning. The tower properties of the turbine in the model were
changed which would require a re­tuning of the turbine controller. Also, an assessment
of the reduction in the Design Equivalent Load could also be made to better asses the
fatigue load reduction of the TMD.
Alternate Solution Strategies: Other Solution strategies could be implemented to nodel
the endstop nonlinearity. Van TIl et. al. [15] explores Harmonic Balancing as a frequency
domain alternative to time integration used in this thesis and a significant amount of com­
putational speed was gained.
