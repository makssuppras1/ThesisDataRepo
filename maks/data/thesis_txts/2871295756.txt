Abstract
Concerns exist regarding hearing risks from video gaming due to sound exposure. This
thesis investigated sound levels and hearing thresholds in 27 adult gamers. Using Ex-
tended High Frequency Békésy Audiometry (EHFBA) before and after a ≥2-hour gaming
session, and measuring gameplay sound levels (LAeq) via a calibrated loopback record-
ing setup, the study looked at potential Temporary Threshold Shifts (TTS) and typical
exposure. Mean LAeq was found to be approximately 48 dB(A), well below recommended
safety limits and no significant TTS link to gaming was found. Analysis of Annual Noise
Exposure (ANE) indicated gaming contributed little to overall risk for most participants,
with high ANE levels primarily coming from non-gaming noise sources and inconsistent
use of hearing protection. Within the controlled environment of the tests, typical gaming
sessions posed a low risk of leading to NIHL, but highlights the importance of address-
ing overall noise exposure and promoting safe listening practices, particularly regarding
non-gaming sources. The lower-than-anticipated LAeq warrants further investigation into
factors influencing preferred listening levels.

Introduction
With each passing year, the total number of video gamers is increasing. Looking at data
from the largest gaming platform Steam, the total ”in-game” i.e. concurrent player count,
the number has been increasing every year for the past 6 years and the total number of
active users has been increasing since the service first launched in 2003 (Djundik 2025).
While the number of video gamers continues to increase, so does the competitive scene.
In the past decade, the competitive gaming scene has exploded in terms of number of
tournaments and the size of the price pools (Charts 2024). Similar to traditional sporting
events, these tournaments sell out entire arenas every year (Spivak 2019). Typically
these events are also being broadcast live to the internet via the livestreaming service
Twitch where it is possible for people to sign up and broadcast their own gaming sessions
(Twitch 2024).
This increased access to video games and gaming via the internet has also helped drive
the desire for young people to become a person livestreaming (streamer) or professional
gamer (Graeme 2021). In Denmark, DGI (Danish Gymnastics and Sports Associations)
has since 2017 offered the opportunity to practice competitive gaming in the form of esport
in many of their sports clubs (Esport 2024). Unlike many other competitive sports there
is no inherent need for protective equipment that can be found in an array of sports like
in football via shin pads, in sailing via safety vests or in ice hockey via their whole outfit.
In gaming and esport, the potential danger is also not an immediately noticeable one.
While it does not include any potential physical altercations with a competitor or equip-
ment, there is the chance for lasting damage to the video gamers’ hearing. A report from
the World Health Organization (WHO) on safe listening in video gamers and esports found
that there is not enough evidence to conclude that this is the case (Mulas 2023).
Thus this thesis aims to investigating these concerns shared by WHO to get better insight
into the habits of gamers and any potential risks they may expose themselves to.
1.1
Project goals
• Establish an accurate measurement method for conducting non-intrusive sound
level measurements of video gamers.
• Establish an accurate and portable measurement method for conducting extended
high-frequency audiometry to investigate potential temporary threshold shifts that
can occur after a gaming session.
• Gain a better understanding of the typical sound levels and exposure lengths of
video gamers.
1.2
Research questions
• Are video gamers playing video games at safe levels in accordance with EU Direc-
tive 2003/10/EC on equivalent time-intensity levels or equivalent standard?
• Is there a detectable hearing threshold difference after a gaming session?
• Are there any safety considerations already in place to help reduce the risk of po-
tential hearing damage?

Literature review
Looking at the growth of the largest gaming platform on computers, Steam, they had a
growth of 21.44% year over year from 2.4M to 36.6M weekly users between 2010 and
2024 (Djundik 2025). At the same time the world had a population growth of 1.08% year
over year (UN 2025). In an article written by L. O. Mendes, Cunha, and R. S. Mendes 2022
about the popularity of video games, they too see this increase and even an exponential
increase in number of games released.
In the report made in collaboration with WHO (Mulas 2023), it is stated that there is limited
publication available on the topic of hearing damage in gaming but the research that does
exist indicates that gaming could be a source of unsafe listening that could lead to NIHL.
In an article by Dillard et al. 2024, is it concluded that gaming is a likely source of unsafe
listening and suggests the need for intervention education and spreading of awareness
about the potential harmful effects of gaming.
2.1
Noise-Induced Hearing Loss
While a single loud sound event can lead to lasting hearing damage, these are typically
called acoustic trauma and caused by impulsive sounds. Meanwhile Noise-Induced Hear-
ing Loss is introduced over a long period of exposure. So while a single sound event would
not cause lasting damage or acoustic trauma, the accumulation of exposure is what intro-
duces the NIHL. NIHL is typically present at 3-6 kHz and comes from damage to the outer
hair cells or the auditory nerve. Luckily it is a type of hearing loss that is preventable if the
proper steps are taken and safe listening level recommendations are followed (Gelfand
2016). An example of this type of hearing loss can be seen in Figure 2.1.

2.2
Noise exposure evaluation
In order to evaluate if a person had been exposed to a loud sound event, there are several
methods that can be utilized. Some more direct than others but each helping to provide
a full image of the acoustic event that is being investigated. Temporary Threshold Shift
looks at the changes in auditory threshold after exposure to noise. This can be done using
Extended High Frequency Audiometry. Using a Noise Exposure Questionnaire, an esti-
mation of the annual noise exposure can be derived. Finally in order to directly measure
the noise exposure, a recording can be performed using various methods depending on
the setup required.
2.2.1
Temporary Threshold Shift
Temporary Threshold Shift is a type of hearing loss defined by a temporary increase of the
auditory threshold. For a Threshold Shift to be temporary it has to return to the auditory
threshold levels before the exposure to noise occured. It is a direct result of exposure to
a loud sound event. The recovery time for a TTS can vary from minutes to weeks with
an upper limit of 30 days. Several factors affect the recovery time such as the duration,
intensity and stimulus type. Additionally, age and prior noise exposure can have an effect
(Ryan et al. 2016).
As the high-frequency regions of the cochlea are most sensitive to noise damage, the
TTS will be most apparent around 4-6 kHz. Despite the TTS only being temporary, it is
important to note that sound exposure accumulates over time and what may be temporary
can potentially become permanent if exposed repeatedly. While being temporary, it is an
indication that the auditory system has been stressed beyond normal exposure ranges.
Despite TTS being detectable primarily around 4-6 kHz, some studies show an associa-
tion between noise exposure and EHF auditory threshold shifts. This association can be
observed even in the case where a standard clinical range hearing test results in normal
hearing. While this effect requires further investigation, looking at TTS at EHF could po-
tentially show if any of the participants have any early signs of increased noise exposure
(Lough and Plack 2022).
2.2.2
Extended High Frequency Audiometry
In traditional clinical audiometry, hearing thresholds are assessed in the range from 125 Hz
to 8000 Hz. While these frequencies are the most crucial for understanding speech, nor-
mal audiometry frequencies do not extend high enough into the upper frequencies of
human hearing where potential early hearing loss can be detected. In order to investi-
gate the potential for early hearing loss, EHFA can be utilized (Mehrparvar et al. 2011).
The typical frequency range of EHFA is from 9 kHz up to 16k kHz, often at 1/6th Octave
intervals but sometimes also extends to 20 kHz.
To perform the EHFA there are several methods that can be deployed. One of these is
pure-tone based Békésy audiometry. This method is automatic and performed in such a
way that the threshold can be estimated based on the participant’s response. In Békésy
audiometry, the tone is continuously presented but the participant changes the presenta-
tion level of the tone. Starting at an audible level, the participant holds a button to lower
the level until no longer audible. Once the participant lets go, the tone gradually increases
again. When the participant once more hears the tone, they press the button to lower it
again. This will create an oscillation around the threshold of the specific frequency. This
is then repeated for each frequency of the audiometric range (Békésy 1947).

EHFBA has been recommended as an ”early warning” procedure for use with people in
high risk categories (Gauz and Smith 1987). A capstone research project into EHFBA has
also shown it is consistent with hearing thresholds obtained in other literature (Ranshaw
2019).
Once the result of the Békésy tracking is obtained, a threshold in dB SPL is achieved. For
this to be expressed in dB HL, a conversion factor needs to be applied. This conversion
factor is called the Reference Equivalent Threshold Sound Pressure Levels (RETSPL).
These values are specified as part of ISO 389 on calibration of audiometric equipment.
ISO 389-8 (ISO 2004) specifies the RETSPL values needed to convert between SPL and
HL for the Sennheiser HDA 200 model of earphones from 125 Hz to 8000 Hz. To correct
for EHFA, ISO 389-5 (ISO 2006) can be used as it also specifies the RETSPL values for
the Sennheiser HDA 200 earphones but from 8000 Hz to 16000 Hz.
2.2.3
Recording
In order to more accurately quantify the exposure of a sound event, a recording can be
made of the sound level. Using a calibrated Sound Level Meter, it is possible to directly
record the exposure to a sound from a given position. This can be used to record the total
sound exposure level of a concert, construction work and so on. While this works well
for sound sources coming from a distance, if the sound source is coming through a pair
of headphones, the microphone placement needs to change. One of the ways to record
headphone sound exposure is through binaural microphones placed in the ear while the
pair of headphones are worn. This will give a good indication of the sound level being
present from the headphones (Silwanowicz 2024).
2.2.4
Noise Exposure Questionnaire
While a TTS or EHFA can provide an indication of whether a person was exposed to too
much noise or not, it doesn’t give an accurate indication of the level of exposure. With
a recording of the actual sound event, a more accurate indication can be obtained and it
can be referenced with respect to safe listening levels to get an estimation of the amount
of potential hearing loss that has incurred. Both methods only provide information from a
single sound event.
In order to understand a person’s exposure to sound a task-based questionnaire called
a Noise Exposure Questionnaire (NEQ) has been developed to assess the Annual Noise
Exposure (ANE). The NEQ can then be used in identifying people who are at risk of
developing a NIHL. It also makes it possible to assess how one event such as playing
games can affect the ANE and if any preventative measures should be taken.
In the paper by Johnson et al. 2017, a list of various activities are described as well as
the LAeq levels gathered from literature. Using these with questions about exposure fre-
quency, the ANE is calculated. In order to include exposure that does not come only from
occupational noise, the level of 85 dBA from the NIOSH recommended exposure limit is
expanded to cover the equivalent of a full year’s exposure. This means the recommen-
dation of 85 LAeq2000h extrapolates to 78.6 LAeq8760h. In order to determine who is at the
highest risk of exposure, any person with a NEQ calculated ANE of more than 79 LAeq8760h
was considered at risk in the study by Johnson et al. 2017.

The main drawback for the NEQ is the exclusion of impulse-based noise and the exclusion
of Hearing Protective Devices (HPD). Understandably, the impulse-based noise expo-
sures cannot be included in the LAeq calculations. In cases such as when using firearms,
this activity is already classified as causing hearing damage and its inclusion is therefore
not needed. However, using a HPD can potentially change the exposure level depending
on how often it is used. To investigate this, the questionnaire in Silwanowicz 2024 also
questions the use of HPD during the various activities. While there is no investigation into
NEQ values using HPD, it can help explain some of the ANE values better. In order to
get a better estimation of the performance of ear plugs, NIOSH has a recommendation
for estimating the performance of HPDs using the NRR rating (CDC 1998). The actual
reduction in dB(A) is (NRR −7) · 50% for formable ear plugs, so a 20 dB NRR rated ear
plug would reduce noise by (20 −7) · 50% = 6.5 dB(A).
2.3
Noise exposure recommendations
In order to ensure safe sound exposure levels, various standards and recommendations
have been set for sound dosages. These vary between country and governing body but
the recommendation is generally set at a limit around 80-85 dB(A) for occupational noise.
2.3.1
Occupational Noise Exposure
The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) has a recommendation known as
NIOSH. This recommendation puts the limit for safe occupational sound exposure at an
equivalent continuous level of 85 dB(A) for a 40 hour work week (CDC 2024). Similarly,
The Danish Working Environment Authority (Arbejdstilsynet) puts their limit at 85 dB(A)
as well (Arbejdstilsynet 2023). The EU has similar regulations; however, they split these
into 2 different levels, a lower limit of 80 dB(A) and an upper limit of 85 dB(A). In the case
of an 80 dB(A) limit, the EU directive states that hearing protection must be provided. For
85 dB(A), hearing protection is required (SCENIHR 2008). In all cases, an increment by
3 dB results in a halving of exposure times as it corresponds to a doubling of the sound
pressure.
2.3.2
Safe listening for video gameplay, esports and personal listening
devices
While the standards for occupational noise exposure help prevent hearing loss caused by
a workplace, other regulations try to protect against hearing loss from non-occupational
sound exposure. The World Health Organization has a recommendation for sound ex-
posure through personal audio devices on 80 dB(A) for 40 hours a week for adults and
75 dB(A) for children; these are known as Mode 1 and Mode 2 (WHO 2019). This ex-
posure is thought of as exposure that happens during leisurely activities and therefore
outside the occupational noise exposure. While this recommendation from WHO is pri-
marily based on safe listening in personal audio devices, it was the precursor to the de-
velopment of the ITU recommendation on safe listening for video gameplay and esports
(ITU 2024). The ITU recommendations use the same Mode 1 and Mode 2 for safe sound
levels as WHO has proposed. While 80 dB(A) refers to a 40 hour a week exposure, other
values can be extrapolated from the same 3 dB increase per halving of duration as well
as WHO’s allowance of unlimited exposure of 60 dB per week (WHO 2025). In Table
2.1, a comparison of the different exposure limits are shown. Anything above 168 hours
will mean you can be exposed to more than a week’s worth of the level, within a week,
meaning exposure is unlimited at that level. The WHO weekly level can be thought of as
recreational exposure, the NIOSH as occupational exposure and NEQ the total weekly
exposure extrapolated from NIOSH values.

2.4
Digital SPL calculations
Using a Sound Level Meter, you can get an LAeq value calculated with various different
weightings when recording a sound event. Many Sound Level Meters also have the option
to save a digital audio recording of the measurement along with the data, like the Nor139
Sound Level Meter. These audio recordings can be used along with the data collected
simultaneously to convert real SPL into a digital representation.
2.4.1
dBFS, WAV and PCM
The Waveform Audio File Format (WAV) is an audio file format capable of storing audio
data uncompressed with great control over sample rate, bit depth and encoding type.
When using an encoding type known as Pulse-Coded Modulation (PCM) the data is stored
at fixed intervals and the amplitude of the signal is saved as a point at each of the intervals.
For the Nor139 Sound Level Meter, the interval known as sampling rate can be set at up
to 48 kHz meaning frequencies up to 24 kHz can be captured and represented reliably,
this frequency is also known as the Nyquist frequency.
In order to also capture the amplitude accurately of the signal, WAV files use a bit depth
that determines the amplitude resolution. The more bits, the higher the dynamic range of
the WAV file, the typical bit depths are 16, 24 and 32. When capturing 16 bits of amplitude
information, the dynamic range can be calculated as 20 · log10(216) ≈96 dB and 24 bit
20 · log10(224) ≈144 dB. This range in amplitude is in PCM encoded WAV files known as
decibel relative to full scale (dBFS) and encodes the data from 0 dBFS to -144 dBFS in
the case for a 24 bit WAV file (IEC 2009 & Kabal 2022). Using this encoding, real SPL
values can be represented digitally while still maintaining the dB scale.

If a recording of an 85 dB SPL 1 kHz sine wave is made, that could end up encoded
as -10 dBFS depending on microphone gain. When the reference for one recording is
known it becomes possible to calculate any other dB level within the dynamic range of
the WAV File. So a 10 dB gain to the audio file would produce a 0 dBFS WAV file that
would correspond to a 95 dB SPL 1 kHz sine wave recording on the same microphone.
This process can also be used in reverse for playing audio out of a speaker. When a WAV
file with 0 dBFS amplitude is played through a speaker and recorded as 95 dB on a SLM,
that same SLM would show 85 dB when the same WAV file was played back but with a
-10 dBFS gain.
2.4.2
Headphone response curves
Calculating the SPL value of a pure tone signal in a pair of headphones is straightforward
when measured with a sound level meter. Matching that SPL value to a different pair
of headphones is also easy once the same signal is played back and measured. Once
the difference in dB SPL is calculated, the difference can be calculated in dBFS values as
well. However, as you introduce more frequencies or more complex sounds, a headphone
calibration is needed. This is typically done via a chirp and recorded on a Head And Torso
Simulator (HATS). While differences in sensitivity are ignored, the frequency response
over the audible range can differentiate a lot between headphones; additionally no single
frequency response is preferred across all people (Ravizza et al. 2023).
The response of a pair of headphones when compared to another can vary widely, even
up to tens of dB for a single frequency (Lelièvre 2024). While headphone calibration is
specified at a 94 dB SPL in IEC 2011, there is no requirement for a specific response
curve. However, some headphones may target certain curves. It is therefore important to
record the differences in the case where any headphone Leq needs to be obtained with
reference to a flat or other headphone curve.

3
Methods
3.1
Overview
This study had varying planned procedures that changed over the course of several
months. The procedure always consisted of 3 parts but with varying methods for data
collection that will be described in this chapter. The basic structure contained a section
before, during, and after a gaming session. The goal was to keep the same physical
location of the entire procedure throughout each participants participation.
3.1.1
Before gaming session
As part of the introduction to the procedure, an introduction session was mandatory for
every participant. This introduction contained all the required procedure information, con-
sent forms, rights documents and so on. It also allowed the participant to ask any clarifying
questions should they have any. Following the introduction, a first part of a 3-part ques-
tionnaire was presented, the questionnaire is almost identical to the one presented in the
previous work of Silwanowicz 2024. This questionnaire is based on the NEQ from Section
2.2.4. This questionnaire will be used to compare with the participants in this study in Sec-
tion 5.1. Once the questionnaire was filled out, the last remaining part was an Extended
High Frequency Audiometry hearing test.
3.1.2
During gaming session
The changes in procedure for changed primarily for this section throughout the method
planning process. In the final method used for the study, the entire audio output from
the computer was captured internally in the computer the participant played on. As part

In order to ensure the right method for data collection was chosen, a few different methods

3.4.1
LAeq matching using two headphones
The previous work of Silwanowicz 2024 utilized a DPA 4560 binaural headset microphone
worn under the headset during gameplay. As described in their work, some participants
found the microphones uncomfortable to wear for prolonged use. In order to remove the
need for binaural microphones and make a more comfortable setup when participants
were playing, a different method was devised using an additional headphone. While the
participant was playing games using their headphones of choice, the other headphones
would be placed on an artificial ear at the same time. Using a Y-splitter cable, the same
audio signal would be presented to both headphones. In the case were the headphones
are identical, the measured response on the artificial ear was assumed to be good enough
for use in determining the sound pressure level in the participants headphones.
Sound levels and hearing thresholds among video gamers
13
==PAGE:22==
While this method would be ideal in the case where the headphones are identical, a more
likely scenario was that the headphones did not match. The headphones would likely
have a different frequency response, different sensitivity and even different impedance.
In the case for two headphones with identical frequency response and only difference in
sensitivity and impedance, the resulting SPL value would only need to be corrected by
a gain factor. In order to obtain such a value, each headphones could be measured on
the artificial ear playing the same tone such as a 1 kHz reference tone. With the artificial
ear connected to a SLM, the two headphones would be placed over the artificial ear while
playing the reference tone. A gain offset could then be calculated using the recorded SPL
values from the SLM.
As most headphones do not share frequency response the method was expanded to take
that effect into account as well. Similar to using the 1 kHz reference tone to calculate a
gain offset for the headphones with identical responses, this method is used but across
the entire frequency range (20 Hz to 20 kHz). With the SLM, a 1 kHz reference tone was
once again recorded and then followed with a frequency sweep. From the SPL value on
the SLM at 1 kHz, the SPL values versus frequency can be extrapolated.
In order to ensure proper driving of multiple headphones, a dedicated DAC was used.
To drive both headphones an RME Fireface UCX was chosen. A series of recordings
were then made for each headphones while still being connected using the Y-splitter.
All recordings were done using a Norsonic Nor139 Sound Level Meter that saves the
recordings as 24-bit 48 kHz WAV files with the headphones placed on a GRAS 43AC-
S1 ear simulator. This made it possible to use the dBFS units of PCM encoded files to
calculate dB differences during post-processing.
For each headphone, the following signals were recorded on the SLM.
• A 114 dB SPL at 1 kHz calibration tone from a Norsonic Sound Calibrator type 1251
• A 10 s logarithmic chirp from 20 Hz to 20 kHz generated in MATLAB with chirp and
played at 100% Windows volume
• A 1 minute gameplay recording from Battlefield 3 at 100% Windows volume
Using the chirp recordings, a filter was reconstructed from the differences in MATLAB.
Using the difference in the frequency domain of each chirp, a transfer function is created
using fft. To make sure that this method is able to correct for the differences in frequency
response, it was tested on 3 different headphones. Using a Sennheiser HD 280 Pro as
the reference headphone, a Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro 80 Ohm and AIAIAI TMA-2 S10-
H04-E04-C15 headphones.

3.4.2
Headphone SPL matching using external audio recording
A second method was also devised and tested for an even simpler setup when recording.
Using this method, a second pair of headphones would not be needed. Instead the chirp,
reference tone and gameplay would be recorded in one WAV file on a portable recorder
connected to a Y-splitter with a headphone. To match the SPL value, the same 1 kHz
reference tone from the recording would be played through the headphone placed on the
GRAS 43AC-S1 ear simulator connected to the Nor139 SLM. From the SLM, the SPL
value of the reference tone would be noted down so that it could be matched later. To
match the frequency response, the chirp would be played over the headphone while on
the ear simulator and again recorded with the SLM.
This method reduced the complication of needing the ear simulator and SLM set up close
to the participant throughout the gameplay session while also ensuring no additional hard-
ware or software needed to be installed on a participants computer. A potential drawback
of this method meant that it completely eliminated background noise as an additional fac-
tor in the recording and also required extra hardware in the form of an audio recorder.
However as background noise would also be different based on each headphones pas-
sive and active noise cancelling capabilities, completely eliminating this factor turned out
to be ideal. By eliminating the background noise, the resulting Leq calculation will be
purely based on the sounds produced through the computer while playing.
Similar to the method of matching a headphone to another headphone, this method matches
the flat recorder chirp and reference tone and a filter based on the difference between the
flat and headphone response is created. From this method, similar results were gathered
to see how close a recording could be to the headphone. For each headphone in Table
3.3 there is always a slightly higher LAeq compared to the value calculated in MATLAB but
as it is always within 1 dB, this difference was deemed insignificant due to measurement
uncertainty as well as human perception for Just Noticeable Differences.

3.4.4
EHFA using Békésy
In standard audiometry, the test is typically performed in an noise insulated listening booth
to give the best results. However, for the fixed setup in this thesis, in order to minimize
the need for transporting people to different buildings before and after their play session,
various test were run to see how much the ambient noise would affect the results. The
goal was to be able to perform the hearing test immediately after the gaming session
concluded.
In order to run Békésy audiometry from a normal computer, a MATLAB implementation
for Békésy Audiometry was used. The implementation used the spacebar as an input
to control the presentation level and is decreased while holding spacebar and increases
once spacebar is let go. The method starts with the presentation of a pulsed pure tone
at the lowest frequency, presented in the left ear at an audible level. The pulsed tone
is pulsed at 200 ms with 200 ms silent intervals. Once the participant holds spacebar,
the level decreased at a steady rate until a point is reached where the participant could
not hear the tone. Once spacebar was let go, the tone would steadily increase until the
participant once again could audibly detect it, in which they once more held spacebar.
From this oscillation between undetectable and barely detectable, an average threshold
was found after 4 reversals after which the next frequency was presented at the hearing
level found for the previous frequency. This was repeated for all desired frequencies and
then the process repeated for the right ear.
In order to calibrate the equipment, the output levels at the different frequencies were
measured in a noise insulated listening booth so the SPL at each frequency was known.
An additional correction factor, the RETSPL from ISO 2004 and ISO 2006 were used to
convert the SPL values to HL as the Sennheiser HDA 200 headphones were used.

3.5
Finalized procedure
With all methods tested and investigated, the final procedure was in place and participants
were invited.
The procedure started with a 30 minute information session to get the participant famil-
iarised with the required documentation, risks and experiment approval. This also allowed
for any questions and to ensure the participant was comfortable and willing to partake.
After the information session was concluded, the participation agreement was filled out
and locked away as to protect the personal data present on the agreement. After the in-
formation session, the first part of the questionnaire was filled out by the participant. The
first part of the questionnaire was regarding noise exposure, habits around gaming and
demographic information. Following the questionnaire, the first hearing test was done by
the participant while alone in the room. With the conclusion of the hearing test, the first
part was done.
The second part of the procedure was the gaming session. The audio on the computer
was turned down so the participant had to increase and set it to their preferred level when
audio started playing. The audio recording was started whereafter the participant was left
alone in the room to play what they preferred. Each participant was checked up on after
2 hours to see if they were interested in playing further or end their gaming session. If
the participant wanted to keep playing, a new check-up time was agreed upon. After the
session concluded, the audio recording was saved to the computer.

The final part of the procedure began with immediately performing the second hearing
test. Once complete, the second part of the questionnaire was filled out by the partici-
pant. This part of the questionnaire was about their gaming session with questions about
ANL, the type of game they played, breaks and the primary game of their session. Lastly,
the participants were asked if they experienced any ringing, buzzing, reduced- or muffled
hearing. If they experienced nothing, they were asked to fill out the last part of the ques-
tionnaire, if they did, they were tasked with filling it out after these effects had stopped.
As a post-processing step, the audio recording was cut into a 30 minute recording. Based
on the response in the questionnaire, the 30 minute recording would be placed in the
middle of the play session of the game they played the most during their session. This
was done manually in Audacity. The resulting 30 minute recording was then processed
using the headphone filtering method from Section 3.4.3 and then ran through MATLAB’s
splMeter. The resulting values was how each persons LAeq and Lpeak was obtained.

An analysis of the normal distribution of LAeq and Lpeak for the full group and RR group
was done in MATLAB. This was done via a Lilliefors test using lillietest with Monte Carlo
simulation and vartestn. The results revealed a normal distributions with equal variance

found.

Shortcomings of NEQ in relation to HPD
Among TS03, TS06, TS14, TS17 and TS27, the only participant that reported not using
any HPD for their largest source of noise was TS14. TS06 on the other hand reported
always using a minimum of 20 dB Noise Reduction Rating (NRR) earplugs any time they
were at a concerts/nighclubs. Using the NIOSH derating formula from CDC 1998, 20 dB
NRR earplugs would reduce levels by 6.5 dB(A). While the derating is specified for NRR
ratings, in Europe, Single Number Ratings (SNR) are used and typically rated 3 dB higher.
However in Europe, the SNR value is designed to be subtracted from the measured dB(C)
value. So using the same -7 dB correction factor to convert from dB(C) to dB(A) from CDC
1998 along with a -3 dB correction factor from NRR vs. SNR ratings, a 20 dB SNR rated
would also reduce the levels by 10 dB(A).
TS06 reported primarily using 37 dB SNR ear plugs which would give an equivalent 27 dB(A)
reduction. Using this ear plug as a reference and always wearing it at concerts/nighclubs,
the ANE would drop to 80.57 after gaming. For the use of power tools, TS06 reported
using earplugs only sometimes. However in order to fall below the 78.6 ANE threshold,
the same earplugs would need to be used about 50% of the time. This highlights a very
important fact about using HPD. It is much more important to be consistent using hearing
protection than having a high SNR rating. Using a pair of earplugs with a fictional 100
dB SNR for only 20% of the time results in an effective attenuation of only 1 dB, as seen
in the calculation from (4.1) to (4.4) which shows the resulting energy factor is close to 1

In order to verify that the distributions are normal, each ANE column put through a Lil-

Discussion
In this chapter, the results from this thesis will be compared to various different sources.
Primarily the comparison with Silwanowicz 2024 which this work is mostly based on. Addi-
tional comparisons to the recommendations from the ITU 2024 recommendation for safe
listening in video games.
5.1
Comparison with previous work
The original intent for this thesis was to further investigate the areas in which it was not
possible for Silwanowicz 2024 to cover, specifically in esport and at gaming events. How-
ever like in their thesis, no clubs, events or unions returned on any enquiries despite per-
sonal contact information being acquired through contacts in the esport industry. Instead
the focus of this thesis pivoted to focus on eliminating as much variability as possible from
the gaming procedure. As part of this this, the intent was to conduct the entire procedure
in one room. Additionally it was noted that the binaural microphone needed for recording
the LAeq value was uncomfortable to wear and was changed to a novel method of as-
sessing LAeq. In the study by Silwanowicz 2024, hearing tests were conducted based on
Bayesian Active learning (BA) (Casolani et al. 2024).
When EHFBA is compared to the performance for other tests seen in Table 5.1, EHFBA
follows closely with the BA (Bayesian) method up until 12.5 kHz where EHFBA performs
better and at the same time performs worse at 16 kHz. Considering the two hearing tests
are so close it is highly likely that any differences in results between this thesis and Sil-
wanowicz 2024 is not caused by the audiometry method employed. Furthermore, the
results gathered from hearing tests are similar across all of the results with only minor
differences.

EHFA in non-clinical room
While proper hearing tests should ideally be performed in a noise insulated hearing booth
the results from this study showed that even in a quiet room the EHFA results would
overall not differ much between the two tests. However due to the way the procedure
was done it led to some influence on the results in the form of learning effects primarily on
the presentation of the first frequency and the first presentation of the high frequencies.
A familiarization phase would have helped on the results as well as a random selection
between which ear is the starting ear. From the data there was an indication that the
left ear performed worse than the right across all participants which could very well be
explained by the order at which the tones were presented.
As seen in Figure 5.1, the performance of the hearing results were close to the BA
(Bayesian) method used in Silwanowicz 2024. The hearing tests were also done on par-
ticipant groups with similar mean age. Across the full group no case was found were
there was an indication of TTS which is also expected based on the LAeq values and ex-
posure duration for all participants. The only effect found was a very slight increase in
performance for the hearing test done after gaming however this could be attributed to
measurement error and learning effects.
5.3
Limitations and future work
As it was important to gather enough samples for a significant sample size, multiple peo-
ple had to have their gaming sessions measured fairly close to each other which put a
constraint on the duration of playtime. Therefore the mean playtime found in this study
should not be taken as fact but rather the result of not having much variance could help
isolate other potential issues.
The gaming location for this thesis had an ANL of about 40 dB(A) and was made com-
fortable with curtains and a carpet to reduced as reverberation and background noise as
much as possible. This room may however not be representative of the typical room the
participants would be gaming in. In relation to gaming events and esport, this room could
potentially be much quieter by comparison.
Getting responses from the esports and gaming industry also proved difficult both in this
thesis and in Silwanowicz 2024. For future investigations it is important to highly prioritize
this fact. With no response from the esports and gaming industry it is also difficult to asses
the reason for this. However, as it is an unregulated industry when it comes to hearing
requirements and a relatively new industry, any investigation into getting it regulated may
lead to a decrease in responses as it could harm the industry depending on the findings.
The final limitation for this thesis was the requirement to only include adults as partici-
pants. From conversations with gaming industry coaches, the majority of esport clubs as
recreational activities are teenagers. In order to include this demographic, more compli-
cated procedures need to be used and it was not within the time frame of this study to get
ethical approval for an investigation of that demographic and then collecting the data. For
future work it is highly suggested that this demographic is investigated taking into account
the recommendations from ITU 2024 also sets a lower exposure limit for children than that
of adults. As they are also the primary demographic for esport clubs and gaming events,
it should be the main focus on any future studies that tries to asses the most critical group
for gaming noise exposure.

6
Conclusion
This study investigated the typical sound exposure levels experienced during gaming.
This was done to asses if there were any immediate effects such as Temporary Threshold
Shifts that could be an early indicator of whether gaming leads to Noise Induced Hearing
Loss (NIHL). This was done via a measurement of the participants hearing thresholds
using Extended High Frequency Békésy Audiometry (EHFBA) as well as through a Noise
Exposure Questionnaire (NEQ) and measurement of LAeq exposure during gaming. The
EHFBA were performed in a non-clinical but quiet environment immediately before and
after gaming. The study employed a novel method for measuring LAeq using loopback
recordings of the audio output as well as frequency response curves of the headphones
used.
The primary findings indicate sound exposure level measured across a 30-minute sample
of a gaming session was approximately 48 dB(A) for the full participant group (n=27).
The levels measured are considerably lower than those in Silwanowicz 2024 and fall well
below the recommended limits of 80 dB(A) for 40 hours a week recommended by WHO
and ITU. While the type of game participants played showed a statistically significant
influence on LAeq, even the highest exposure remained within the safe recommended
limits. Games like Role-playing, Shooters and Rogue-like showed a trend towards higher
levels than Adventure and Strategy. It was also shown that similar to Silwanowicz 2024,
Role-playing games showed the highest sound levels for a given game type.
Consistent with the relatively low sound exposure levels measured, the EHFBA showed
no evidence of Temporary Threshold Shifts (TTS) after gaming. While a statistically sig-
nificant median improvement of 0.63 dB was observed in hearing thresholds post-gaming,
it was withing measurement variability of ±5 dB and likely caused by learning effects. The
study showed that use of EHFBA is feasible outside a clinical sound booth for the pur-
poses of detecting changes in hearing thresholds. However, learning effects particularly
found at the start- and higher frequencies should be addressed in future studies either
through randomization of ear or a familiarization round.
Analysis of the Annual Noise Exposure (ANE) revealed that for most participants in this
study, gaming contributed minimally to their ANE. Only one participant’s ANE crossed the
risk threshold due to the addition of their gaming exposure, however the primary source
of exposure was still from concerts/nighclubs. Five participants already exceeded the
78.6 dB(A) ANE threshold based on other activities, primarily use of power tools and at-
tendance at concerts/nighclubs. The investigation highlighted the limitations of the NEQ
in accounting for Hearing Protective Device (HPD) usage. In the case of consistent use of
even moderate protection, the ANE values could have fallen below the 78.6 dB(A) thresh-
old. Since the study by Silwanowicz 2024, a new recommendation by ITU has also been
published, ITU 2024, titled ”Safe listening for video gameplay and esports”. This rec-
ommendation specifically targets the safety considerations for gaming in an attempt to
prevent sound-induced hearing loss in gaming.

In summary, based on the measured sound levels and hearing thresholds within this spe-
cific participant group and controlled setting, the typical video gaming sessions did not
demonstrate sound exposures likely to cause a TTS or exceed safe daily/weekly dosages.
The measured LAeq levels were substantially lower than anticipated based on prior studies
and the most significant risks for NIHL identified among the participants stemmed from
non-gaming activities. However, these findings must be interpreted within the study’s
limitations. The controlled environment, fixed equipment, young adult demographic, and
constrained session durations will likely not represent the diverse habits and environ-
ments of the entire gamer demographic. The significant discrepancy in measured LAeq
compared to Silwanowicz 2024 warrants further investigation.
Future work should prioritize understanding the factors contributing to varying preferred
listening levels among gamers, potentially exploring different headphone types and home
listening environments.
Most importantly, future research should aim at investigating
teenage gamers as they are a main demographic of esports. While requiring more com-
plex ethical considerations, it is essential for assessing risk in potentially more vulnerable
groups. Engaging directly with the gaming and esports industry remains a challenge but
is play an important role in accessing the representative demographic.
