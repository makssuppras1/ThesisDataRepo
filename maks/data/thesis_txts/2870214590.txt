Abstract
Hard X­ray focusing optics represent a poorly explored field in high­energy astrophysics,
crucial for study the universe’s most energetic and enigmatic phenomena. This thesis ex­
plores the feasibility of using a balloon flight as a vessel to test hard X­ray optics, aiming
to determine whether a balloon­based platform can offer a viable, cost­effective alterna­
tive to space­based testing. By focusing on hard X­rays up to 200 keV, this instrument
opens up new research on cosmic events: observations of radioactive decay lines from
56Ni and 57Ni will offer new insights into the velocity and structure of supernova ejecta,
advancing our understanding of detonation models and our cosmological interpretation of
Type Ia supernovae. Similarly, detecting 44Ti allows astrophysicists to probe alpha­rich
freeze­out, revealing the dynamics of ejecta and jet formation in core­collapse supernovae
in relatively later stages after the explosion, where some of the universe’s heaviest ele­
ments are born. The design will also enable the study of rare isotopic emissions, such as
126Sn, tracing how heavy elements are forged and distributed across the universe. Be­
yond supernova remnants, the optics can capture critical features in X­ray binaries, such
as hard X­ray cutoHs in LMXBs, which shed light on the temperature and motion of high­
energy plasma, and cyclotron resonance scattering features (CRSF) in HMXBs, revealing
the magnetic fields of young neutron stars. This research uniquely combines high­energy
astrophysics and elements of space systems engineering, advancing the observations
possibilities given by NuSTAR. The payload of the probe is studied and a selection for de­
tector and mirror material is done to ensure optimal performance. Furthermore, an anal­
ysis of the geometrical design of the probe is held and reflective coatings are designed in
IMD to increase the reflectance of the substrate to energies up to 200 keV. Eventually the
performance of the observatory is evaluated by means of its continuum and narrow line
sensitivity, giving estimates of the expected counts for a calibration target like the pulsar
in the Crab Nebula.

1
Introduction
The study of the Universe is a fundamental pillar to unveil the physical principles that
rule our understanding of nature. Collecting electromagnetic radiation from astrophysical
objects is what allows astronomers to study their behavior and the physical processes that
take place inside and around them. The collection of this signal is more and more difficult
for increasing energies of the radiation, becoming extremely challenging for energies in
the hard X­ray and γ­ray range. This is because at these energies the photon fluxes are
rare and the background radiation tends to be more significant. Here comes the need
to adopt light concentrators such as focusing optics to increase the Signal to Noise ratio
of the detected electromagnetic radiation. The problem with this instrumentation is that
photons at high energies tend to either pass through or be absorbed by the reflective
surface used to focus the light into the detector. Nowadays, focusing optics have been
used to reflect hard x­rays up to 79 keV with the NuSTAR mission: this project aims to
extend the energy range up to 200 keV to close part of the sensitivity MeV gap.[1][2] This
will enable the study of high energetic phenomena that take place in supernovae, neutron
stars and X­ray binaries. In the first case, the study of the radioactive decay of 56Ni at 158
keV that powers the optical light curve of type Ia supernovae can lead to the understanding
of energy recycling in the envelope of the object, allowing for a physical and yet empirical
calibration of a standard candle.[3][4] Additionally, mapping angle and radial velocity of
44Ti in core collapsing SN will help clarify ejecta dynamics and jets formation and studying
isotopes like 126Sn and 60Fe will provide information over cosmic evolution and heavy
elements formation. Our knowledge on neutron stars, on the other hand, can be expanded
by improving the study of their Cyclotron Resonant Scattering Feature that expresses as
an absorption line, which energy is related to the magnetic field of the compact object.[5]
A leap in the focusing capabilities of high energies will also improve the understanding
of power law cutoff for LMXB and furnish focused and detailed images of the observed
objects to correlate spectral and spatial data. In order to achieve that, it is necessary to
both improve on the sensitivity of the instrument but also improving the collection rate
of the photons, and therefore the effective area of the telescope, as radiation at these
energies is rare and difficult to catch. This project, therefore, aims to assess the feasibility
of manufacturing and testing a probe with revolutionary optics to reflect hard x­rays by
means of a stratospheric balloon or, in case this is found to be unsuitable, by means
of other space options such as sounding rockets or small spacecrafts. A probe can be
defined as a small scale, low cost scientific mission designed to validate new technology or
instrumentation. An X­ray optics probe will most likely focus on testing the performances
of the optics such as efficiency and resolving power, validation of the technology like new
materials and design by focusing their attention on fewer targets, specifically selected
to test the technology employed. A probe mission will also have a shorter duration and
smaller size with respect to a full mission, making them useful precursors of much larger
space missions.[6] The aim of the project is to set a valid baseline to further develop the
mission concept and manufacture the probe possibly keeping all the processes within
Danish borders and industries. In the following chapters, there will be firstly an analysis
of previous hard x­rays space missions to set constraints on feasibility and the state of the
art technology. Then, an analysis of the different platforms that can be used to test the
probe is carried out setting important parameters as location and observing plan to define
the capabilities of the telescope. The optics are then studied and designed following the
constraints and the trade offs performed for different materials, geometries and coatings.

Theory
Telescopes for high energy particles like X­ and γ­ rays are more complex than their op­
tical or radio counterparts. This is because a canonical parabolic dish used to collect
photons at low energies would absorb the whole incident radiation at these higher ener­
gies. This leads to the need of grazing incidence angles between the incoming photon
and the reflecting surface, much longer focal lengths and specifically designed detectors
to collect the focused radiation. This is because, although the x­rays are easily absorbed
by the atmosphere, they also become more and more difficult to absorb in a detector as
the needed stopping power becomes larger.
2.1
Physics of the optics
As mentioned above, high energy telescopes have different working principles with re­
spect to radio, infrared, optical, and UV telescopes.
X­rays, as a form of electromagnetic waves, can exhibit refraction at interfaces between
different media. To analyze such refractive effects, the media are typically assumed to
be uniform, with distinct boundaries and characterized by their respective refractive index
n. By definition, the refractive index in vacuum is unitary. For visible light, the refractive

so focusing X­rays is more challenging. This necessitates longer focal lengths and spe­

2.1.1
Optics geometry
As mentioned before, the reflection of x­rays is possible when the incident radiation comes
at a grazing angle with respect to the mirrors. Wolter­type telescopes are widely used
in astrophysics applications as they allow reasonable focal lengths and effective areas.
These telescopes base their principles around two reflections: one performed with a
parabolic mirror and another one with a hyperbolic mirror. These two reflections are made
so that they share the same focal point. This combination allows for shorter focal lengths
with respect to other geometries such as Kirkpatrick­Baez, which would require much
more space to focus the radiation. These geometries can be built in different ways, but

HEFT
The High Energy Focusing Telescope is one of the major references taken into account
for the development of this project as it was the precursor of the NuSTAR space telescope
(sec 2.2). The mission flew on a stratospheric balloon from Fort Sumner (USA) and of­
fered a total observable time of around 20 hours, of which almost 7 were spent on targets
(see sec.3.5.1).[21] The balloon used for the flight was a 39.57 MCF balloon and the total
weight of the gondola was 1270 kg.[22] This mission accounts for three different optics
tested on a balloon to assess the performance of reflecting mirrors for energies up to 70
keV using conical approximations of Wolter type I geometry.[21] The three telescopes
are all arranged in a cloverleaf pattern and each of them has its own CZT detector. The
substrate used for the three optics is D265 borosilicate glass: this material offers good
thermal and density properties and it’s suitable for space applications. For this mission,
the substrates were 10 cm long and 0.21 mm thick. The length of the substrate allows
it to cover up to 72°, so only 5 substrates are required to complete a full revolution of
the optics, 10 for a conical section and 20 for an entire shell.[21] The importance of the
number of substrates necessary to populate the telescope will be discussed later in chap­
ter4 Graphite is used to separate the different shells and some intermediate mandrels
are placed instead of mirrors to enhance the stiffness of the structure. The substrates are
coated with W/Si multilayers which ensure the operational energy band to be up to 69 keV
where the tungsten absorption edge is located. The detector used for the mission was
of semiconductor type, surrounded by Z­graded plastic to shield from background noise.

Mission requirements definition
The analysis of space missions starts with the definition of the requirements that need
to be met in order to accomplish the mission objectives. Firstly, the observation of as­
tronomical x­rays and soft γ −rays is extremely difficult and their observation from the
ground is impossible, given the opacity of the atmosphere to this radiation. This happens
because of resonance effects between the incoming photons and materials like oxygen,
ozone, and nitrogen that turn the atmosphere into an opaque medium that absorbs all the
radiation, as shown in figure 3.1.[35] The observations of such energies therefore must

send any kind of instrument into space. Moreover, in the case of cutting­edge technolo­

Scientific requirements and objectives
The goals of studying new technologies for X­ray telescopes is to enable observations of
phenomena that are currently poorly resolved: the definition of the energy range and of
the type of phenomena within this range are the first step to develop any kind of telescope
and its instrumentation. The observation of the X­ray sky, spanning the energy range from
keV to TeV, involves various telescope technologies: in the 1­100 keV range, space tele­
scopes can either employ Wolter­1 focusing optics or coded­aperture mask optics for light
collection as shown in sec.2.2. For the 50 MeV­1 TeV range, different techniques are re­
quired due to the reduced interaction of these high­energy photons with matter. Instead
of focusing or absorbing photons, telescopes such as Fermi­LAT rely on bare detectors,
where pair production is the dominant interaction mechanism.[36] The 100 keV to 50 MeV
range represents a large lack of sensitivity in astrophysics, primarily being covered by the
INTEGRAL­SPI instrument and COMPTEL [37]. The former of these two telescopes is a
BGO detector with a coded­aperture mask. The lack of focusing optics is detrimental to its
performance, as its high large resolution of 2.5◦vastly increases the background observed
for each source as discussed in section 3.4.[38]. Currently operational telescopes, such

a high instrumental background. In general, the smaller the sensitivity of an instrument,

observed by HEAO­3, COMPTEL, RHESSI, SPI and NuSTAR. [1] A tenfold improvement

Low Mass X­ray binaries
Low mass X­ray binaries (LMXB) are astronomical systems
made by two objects: a compact object (usually NS or BH) and an accretor (usually a
main phase star). A high energies, these systems can exhibit a spectral cutoff that re­
sults from processes such as thermal Comptonization, non­thermal electron interactions,
and disk­corona interplay. The spectral cutoff represents the energy beyond which the
photon flux decreases rapidly due to photon upscattering in the hot corona. In LMXBs,
the primary mechanism driving the cutoff is thermal comptonization, where soft X­ray pho­
tons from the accretion disk are upscattered by high­energy electrons in the corona. The
energy at which this cutoff occurs is directly related to the electron temperature in the

emission sources.[52] Moreover, the combination of imaging and spectroscopy is essen­
tial for studying the variability of spectral properties across different regions of an object.

shock physics can vary dramatically across the remnant, and high­resolution imaging can

project is to assess whether or not a stratospheric balloon is the best option as a techno­

One of the fundamental parameters to achieve is the focal length. This parameter is of
utmost importance because it allows for the use of much smaller incidence angles com­
pared to those required with a short focal length. This is pivotal since the ability of mirrors
to reflect photons at energies up to 200 keV is highly dependent on the grazing angle
of incidence. By keeping this angle very small, the mirror’s reflective capability can be
significantly improved. Closely tied to the focal length is the observation time. As previ­
ously mentioned, the idea of using a sounding rocket might prove ineffective because this
platform does not allow for sufficiently long integration times. Furthermore, as outlined
in section 3.1.1, the collection of a large number of photons within an energy range is
heavily influenced by the duration for which the sensor is exposed to the radiation. This,
in turn, is separate from the collecting area, which is not dependent on the platform used
to test the probe. The next parameter in order of importance is cost. These types of mis­
sions typically operate on very tight budgets, which could hinder the proper development
of the optical bench, diverting funds from the study and construction of the payload in
favor of a more expensive platform. For this reason, cost has been assigned the third
position in terms of importance. Closely following cost is sky availability. The objective
here is to have access to as many X­ray sources as possible, enabling the observation
of diverse targets during a single flight. As previously mentioned, a satellite would pro­
vide the best performance in this regard, followed by the other two platforms. However,
given that the mission in question is designed for a test flight of a prototype instrument,
the criterion used to evaluate this parameter is the platform’s ability to offer decent sky
access with moderate flexibility in the selection of objects to observe. Another important
parameter is the capability to carry large masses to the required altitudes. All three plat­
forms considered—satellites, sounding rockets, and stratospheric balloons—are capable
of reaching the desired altitudes, which is above 35 km. However, differences arise in
the amount of mass these platforms can transport. Satellites are subject to very stringent
mass requirements, as previously mentioned, and the cost per kilogram of payload is sig­
nificantly higher compared to sounding rockets and balloons. Additionally, using a satellite
would impose strict conditions in terms of volume, potentially escalating the mission from
a simple prototype test to a medium or large­scale mission. Sounding rockets, on the
other hand, have somewhat less restrictive weight requirements, as they can carry up to
800 kg of payload into orbit. However, a significant drawback of this platform is the lim­
ited time in orbit, approximately 500 seconds (see figure 3.5). This duration is insufficient
even for the proper calibration and alignment of the instrument, let alone for meaningful
observations.

weight to the desired altitudes and they also offer observation times that can extend to

next parameter in order of importance is pointing capability. While it is not the highest
priority, it remains crucial for stable and precise observations. Accurate pointing, in con­
junction with the angular resolution of the telescope, ensures the proper identification of
the observed source, avoiding errors caused by misalignment. It is essential to remem­

are visible to the naked eye, making precise pointing critical. In orbiting satellites, point­
ing can be achieved relatively easily using instruments like reaction wheels, gyroscopes,
magnetometers, and other devices capable of applying external torques to the satellite.
These tools enable fine adjustments for accurate alignment. Sounding rockets, on the
other hand, primarily rely on gyroscopes to correct for roll. Although this method allows
for relatively accurate pointing within the visible field, it limits the degree of freedom to a
single axis, significantly restricting the number of observable targets.
Furthermore, when it comes to data transmission, especially for X­ray measurements that
generate large volumes of data, sounding rockets are the least suitable platform. They
can accommodate only small antennas, and their functionality is highly dependent on the
limited time in flight and specific orbital conditions. Satellites offer a better alternative,
with scheduled communication windows for downlinking data to Earth. However, these
windows are still time­limited and can be disrupted by adverse atmospheric conditions
or misalignment of the satellite’s antenna during observations. Stratospheric balloons,
on the other hand, are equipped with dedicated antennas that enable more continuous
communication. However, this is still influenced by the launch location and geographical
constraints. A significant advantage of balloons is their ability to recover the payload after
the mission, allowing for the retrieval of any missing or corrupted data. This capability
also enables post­flight hardware analysis, providing insights for future improvements.

the annual number of launches has increased significantly. However, these launches typ­

this mission. Sounding rocket launches have become increasingly rare in recent years,

The definition of the thermal subsystem derives from the thermal
conditions that the balloon will face once in the stratosphere. The temperature of the en­

is designed to maintain the payload at its optimal operating condition and temperature
throughout the entire flight to the stratosphere and back to Earth. Meteorological data
indicates that the temperature between sea level and an altitude of 20 km ranges from
−60 to 30 °C. Based on the payload specifications, an operating temperature range has to
be defined to make sure the system is built ad­hoc to maintain it within the working range.

3.4.2
Detector
Another component that is fundamental to obtain any kind of data from a telescope is the
detector. This component is responsible for detecting the photons by converting them
into a measurable electronic signal. The probability of detection for a photon within the
detector depends on the energy of the photon and on the material the detector is made of.
The accuracy of a detector’s measurements is determined by its resolution, defined by its
imaging capability, which is the ability to measure the position of each interaction (Spatial
resolution), its energy sensitivity, which measures the energy deposited by each inter­
action (Spectral resolution), and its timing capability, which refers to measuring the time
of the interactions. Detection of photons at high energies may be challenging because
of the interactions between photons and matter. Figure 3.10 shows that for elements
up to Z=50, at 200 keV the interaction is a combination of photoelectric absorption and
Compton scattering. The first and most critical detector design decision is that of the type.
Figure 3.10: Photon interaction processes as a function of stopping element and energy
of radiation [65]
In addition to requiring a high sensitivity and spectral resolution to fulfill the science re­
quirements, the difficulty in shielding high energy X­rays provides unique challenges for
a telescope focusing up to 200 keV. At the same time, the detector should have good
accuracy in determining the position of each interaction within the sensor, in order to ac­
count for high energy particles with large interaction length. Proportional counters are not
suitable for this application, as the gas tends to become transparent to the incident radi­
ation for energies above 20 keV.[66] On the other hand, scintillator detectors have been
widely used for balloon applications in the past [66], however, they may suffer from poorer
energy resolution than solid state detectors, because of their intrinsic non­proportionality
of the light yield. So far, high 3D position accuracy has only been demonstrated using
solid­state detectors and large liquid or gas time projection chambers.[67] Unfortunately,
these chambers suffer from relatively low energy resolutions, and a large volume is nec­
essary to compensate for the low stopping power of these materials. Regardless, given
the recent advancements in the development of solid­state detectors and their ability to
achieve excellent energy and spatial resolutions, they will be the only type of sensors dis­

3.5
Launching site
The selection of the launching place will play a determining role in the observation time al­
located to observe sources, and in the selection of the specific sources that will have to be
visible from that location. In the selection of the launching place, there are two consider­
ations to be done regarding the latitude and the longitude of the site that is intended to be
used. The longitude will play a marginal role, as it takes part in the calculation to identify
the recollection point of the balloon and payload after the mission, but it’s not fundamental
for the target definition. The latitude, on the other hand, will play a fundamental role with
regards to the accessibility of the sky and objects. In fact, objects located below the hori­
zon won’t be observable by the probe as they are hidden behind the Earth. The latitude is
also very important in the determination of the length of the flight; in fact, at higher latitudes
such as in Antarctica and in the Arctic Circle, circumpolar currents allow the stratospheric
balloon to be launched from one site and be collected more or less in the same position
after a week or two. However, this may limit the amount of observable targets and may
affect the precision of the pointing, given the polar days and the polar nights that will affect
the star camera on board. On the other hand, balloons launched from closer to the equa­
tor can face harsh weather conditions due to the tropical environment present there.[22]
Following what was done for HEFT, a flight time of at least one day is necessary (see
section 3.5.1), but a longer flight would be preferred, given the rarity of photons at these
energies. This does not rule out the use of bases at high latitudes, but it may complicate
hardware recovery after the mission. Additionally, utilizing high­latitude locations requires
careful consideration of logistical challenges. Specifically, the optics and detector must
be transported to the launch site, assembled, aligned, and calibrated. This process is not
feasible during the winter, particularly for Antarctic flights, and must be scheduled for the
end of the summer season. Assuming the optics are assembled in Europe, most likely
in Denmark, the ESRANGE facility in Sweden emerges as the most logistically favorable
option.[83] Transporting the payload to this site via land or sea minimizes stress on the
structure and mirrors, as these methods generate relatively low vibrations. In contrast,
for launch sites in the United States or Latin America, the payload would either require
a longer transportation time using the same low­vibration methods or faster delivery by
air. However, air transport introduces the risk of vibrations or unconventional motions that
could potentially damage the structure or misalign the mirrors.

jects are intended to be observed. Before doing that, it is necessary to understand the

4.1
Optics
The first thing to account for is the population of the different mirror shells. Different pack­

this application, measuring its performances in terms of grazing angles, shell spacing,
and collecting area. First of all, the selection of the substrate has to be carried on. The
two choices mentioned in sec.3.4 are the 0.5 mm thick Si substrates and the borosilicate
slumped glass. The second one can be produced in Denmark from companies such as

will have in terms of telescope performance.
4.1.1
Geometrical design
Assuming the same inner and outer radius, the same gap between the first and second
reflection, and the same focal length, two telescope apertures have been populated with
mirrors following a geometry that varies the spacing between mirrors to maintain their
length constant across the aperture. Note that maintaining the same mirror length across
the aperture implies that, in order to avoid mirror shadowing, it is necessary to vary the
spacing between consecutive shells to account for the increasing grazing angle. The
obtained results are presented in figure 4.1. Here, it’s immediate that the first thing that
changes as a consequence of the choice of the material is the number of shells, going
from 26 for the Si substrates to 34 in the borosilicate case. This is because a thicker
substrate occupies more space and therefore allows for fewer shells in a defined aperture.
As noticeable, the minimum and maximum grazing angles are the same between the two
configurations, as they are dependent only on focal length and radii considered, which
are the same for both shells population. A direct consequence of the reduction in the
number of shells is the decrease of the integrated collecting area. The figure, in fact,
shows that the configuration with thinner substrates is able to achieve a collecting area
of 208 cm2, while the other goes only up to 169 cm2. The graph also shows that, for the
same number of shells, thicker substrates result in a higher integrated collecting area.
This, however, is related to the fact that a thicker substrate leads to a different geometric
positioning compared to a thinner substrate, primarily in terms of radius, which will be
larger for thicker substrates with the same shell number, thereby increasing the collecting
area. This effect, however, is offset by the greater number of shells achievable with thinner
substrates, which significantly increases the overall collecting area. The challenge in
the adoption of slumped borosilicate glass as substrate comes with the alignment of the
mirrors and with their position in the telescope aperture. The minimum spacing between
mirrors will be in fact of only 0.99 mm while for the Si substrate would have been of 1.29
mm. However, NuSTAR had spacing in the sub­millimeter range and therefore the relative
position of the different shells between each other won’t constitute an issue for the probe.

shadowing of the mirrors and stray light. Moreover, for these configurations, different mir­
ror lengths have been tested. Specifically, mirrors of 225 mm (same as for NuSTAR), 500
mm mirrors and 1 meter mirrors. The NuSTAR mirrors have been tested and are capa­
ble of maintaining their shape and alignment under vibrations and temperature gradients.
Note that HEX­P is planned to have 400 mm long mirrors.[27] As for longer mirrors, such
as those measuring half a meter (the maximum size coatable at DTU Space) and one
meter, they should be considered as an assembly of multiple shorter mirrors, properly
assembled and aligned. Due to the inherent challenges in reflecting X­rays, the graz­
ing incidence angle must be minimized. However, given the constraints on focal length,
adopting maximum radii similar to those used in HEX­P or NuSTAR would not yield com­
parable observational quality. Furthermore, these two observatories were not designed
to reach the same energy range as the probe under consideration. A maximum radius of
100 mm has been therefore selected for further analysis, with the opportunity of assem­
bling multiple telescopes together to increase the collecting area if necessary. With these
constraints, the maximum grazing angle that the mirrors will have is of 0.140 degrees.
Figure 4.2 shows the comparison of the three configurations with the three mirror lengths
in terms of integrated collecting area. The figure shows that, for the same mirror length,
the configuration with variable spacing that keeps the mirror length fixed is the most effi­
cient, followed by the configuration with variable length, and finally by the one with fixed
length and spacing throughout the telescope aperture. As it appears, there is not an a­
priori geometry that performs better than the other, but they are subordinate to the length
of the mirrors. Table 4.1 collects the values of the total integrated collecting area to clarify
this aspect. The adoption of variable spacing with fixed mirror length seems to be the
most performing one for all the three lengths selected; however, depending on the size of
the mirror, the second best configuration can vary, being the variable length one for the 1
meter mirror length and the fixed spacing and length for the other two mirror sizes. Note
that to build these models, a first run with variable spacing and fixed length has been run.
The maximum spacing found has been used for the fixed spacing and length configuration
as it ensures no shadowing of the mirrors, while for the variable length one, the minimum
spacing found has been used, as it ensures a tight shell population with no shadowing
at bigger radii. A consequence of the variable spacing with fixed length configuration is
the high number of shells that have to be nested, larger than the configuration with fixed
spacing, which has to leave more room between different shells.

Detector
As mentioned in section 3.4, the materials that look the most suitable for the probe de­
signed in this project are Ge and CdZnTe. The major problem of Germanium is the need
of cryocooling the instrument in order to exploit all its power, while CZT detectors can
achieve high performance at room temperature. This would add an extra challenge to the
project but this is not necessary, as a 3D CZT drift strip detector has been developed in
DTU Space in the last years[92], bringing it to TRL 5. Consequently, the idea of keeping
the entire payload design in­house at DTU Space is certainly appealing, as it would allow
for a more streamlined development of the probe while ensuring exceptional quality. This
detector uses the drift strip method to detect and locate photons[92]. It is based on solid
state detector principles, where the atoms in the sensor are hit by the incident radiation
creating a cascade of electrons. The setup presents some anode readout strips separated
with negatively biased strips placed to ensure the electron created by the interaction is
directed towards an anode. This also ensures that the positive charges created in the
interaction won’t affect the measure.[93]. On the opposite side, cathode electrodes are
present. The energy carried by the photon is therefore proportional to the signal mea­
Mission Concept Analysis for Developing a Hard X­ray Focusing Optics Probe for Astronomical
Observations
69
==PAGE:82==
sured in the anode strip.[94] The combination of cathode and anode signal allows for the
3D positioning.[92] Figure 4.35 shows the concept of drift strips detectors, the disposition
of bias strips, anode ones and cathode.
Figure 4.35: Schematic of the 3D CZT detector[95][96]
This revolutionary geometry allows this detector to achieve sub­millimeter spatial reso­
lution. It was demonstrated in fact that it can achieve a spatial resolution <0.4 mm in
the detector plane for some isolated cases.[72][97] The spatial resolution, which for most
pixel detectors is similar to the pixel size, together with the focal length of the instrument
defines the angular resolution of the detector. It has to be noted that this angular res­
olution is different from the angular resolution derived from the optics, that depends on
the wavelength and on the aperture of the telescope. This means that for imaging appli­
cations, even if the optics could provide higher angular resolution than the one given by
the detector, that wouldn’t change its response. The angular resolution is defined as the
capability of the instrument to distinguish fine spatial details: the term angular refers to
the separation of two features in a target, with the observer placed at the vertex. When
observing a single point of light in the sky, a telescope does not produce a perfect dot
on the detector but rather a spread­out pattern called an Airy disk, consisting of a cen­
tral disk surrounded by rings of light. If the angular separation between two points is too
small, their respective Airy disks overlap, making them indistinguishable. The detector
has been previously studied in the 2x2 centimeters configuration but a 4x4 centimeters
configuration is currently under evaluation. A bigger detector would lead to higher back­
ground rates but also high collecting area. The choice of the size is mainly led by the size
of the focal spot, which is difficult to estimate without proper simulations.
This detector is designed to perform up to 2000 keV and might suffer from poorer spectral
resolution at the energies that are studied with the payload discussed here. The spectral
resolution refers to the capability of the detector to distinguish different lines at a specified
energy. This detector has been tested at a temperature of ­15◦C and with operation volt­
ages of Vd=­120 V and Vp= ­300 V for the drift strips and cathodes. Different calibration

This chapter collects all the results and analysis derived from the selection of the plat­

5.1
Systems engineering and platform selection
Following the discussion held in chapter 3, the best platform that can be used to test the
probe designed is the stratospheric balloon. Different design parameters are considered
for the platform trade­off analysis, considering performance and feasibility of the project
as the main design parameters. Within those, it’s important to highlight the importance of
a long focal length, the cost, and the available observing time, which are the parameters
that can potentially have a heavier impact on the success of the mission. The choice of
the focal length will define the grazing incident angles of the shells of the optics, and there­
fore it’s important to keep it as long as possible. The choice of a 10 meters focal length
instead of 12 is due to some safety margins to include some structure to mount the optics
and some electronics for the detector that may have to be applied in close proximity of the
sensor itself. However, although the choice of the parameters has been done following
previous missions’ priorities and scientific requirements, the selection definition and the
different scores assigned for the trade­off may be biased by the author’s knowledge and
ideas. Within the stratospheric balloons, a Zero Pressure balloon is found to be a suitable
option, as it combines good flight time with optimal lift capacity. Following previous mis­
sions such as HEFT, a flight time between 1 and 2 days should be enough to prove the
efficacy of the optics and the feasibility of the project. The mission is expected to have
a maximum weight comparable with that of the HEFT mission, and consequently, the
choice of the same kind of balloon, a 39.57 MCF, ensures both high altitudes achievable
and some mass margin. The gondola will have to withstand a vertical load up to 10g and
a lateral one up to 7.5 g and will have to be specifically designed for the probe presented
in this work. It will most likely be an open structure to reduce wind effects on the probe
during the observations while maintaining a sturdy structure to limit thermal deformations.
The structure will be attached to the stratospheric balloon by a rotator to desaturate the
rotation of the balloon with respect to the gondola. Moreover, between the junction and
the balloon itself, the parachute will be placed to increase the odds of recovering the pay­
load.
The gondola will house a truss to keep the optics and the detector aligned. The optics will
be placed on one side of the mast, while the detector will occupy the other side: assum­
ing the truss can provide the same performance as the one used in X and XL­Calibur, a
deviation in the position of the focal spot smaller than 3mm is expected due to thermal
gradients in the structure. However, this needs to be verified by including in the model
the instrumentation expected to be carried for this probe as they will modify the weight
distribution on the truss. The gondola has to include a variety of sensors useful to monitor
the operational state of the observatory and to point the telescope in the right direction.

background measurements have to be taken into account more precisely before defining
the exact observing time. Moreover, the discussed observing plan is extremely depen­
dent on the date set for the observations, and therefore will vary depending on when the
launch will be executed.

Geometrical configuration design
The geometry selected for this observatory is the widely used in X­ray astronomy Wolter­
Type­I geometry that includes two reflections to focus the radiation. The two reflections
share the same focus as one of them is made with parabolic mirrors and the other one with
hyperbolic mirrors. The manufacture of conical substrates with small radii is challenging
and therefore a linear approximation of the true Wolter­Type­I geometry is assumed for the
design discussed here. This doesn’t affect all the considerations held here but will play an
important role during the ray­tracing simulations of the optics, as a linear approximation
will increase the HPD of the optics. The grazing incidence angle at which every mirror has

smaller aperture, the FOV for the probe discussed here is expected to be smaller than the
NuSTAR one. Eventually, the definition of the focal length allows to define the plate scale

on the focal plane as a function of the focal length. Consequently, it’s possible to find that
one arcsecond in the sky corresponds to 48µm on the detector. This is exactly the same
plane scale as the CHANDRA telescope.[99] As a consequence, an object like the Crab
Nebula with an extension of ∼7 arcminutes will be of ∼20 mm on the focal plane, which
suggests the detector has to be bigger than this. However, this is difficult to estimate

ray­tracing simulations are required for this purpose.

The performance of the telescope is often computed in terms of its sensitivity. This is
an expression of the total signal­to­noise ratio that the telescope achieves while looking
at the sky. This is usually reported as continuum sensitivity and line sensitivity in order
to distinguish the ability to catch the spectrum of the target or its features in terms of
emission and absorption lines. Ideally, the higher the sensitivity, the dimmer objects can
be observed with the telescope. The evaluation of the sensitivity of an observatory is not a
straightforward process and requires ray­tracing simulations and complex analysis of the
telescope response at different energies for background and signal. Following equation

Future works
The project is intended to be a baseline to develop a high energy x­ray probe and there­
fore multiple aspects need to be validated and further detailed. First of all, a review of
the science case is necessary, to give more stringent requirements and increase the ob­
servable objects. A careful design of the balloon gondola has to be done according to
the needs and requirements of the mission. The instrumentation required to operate the
telescope has to be defined once the payload design is complete to make sure the power,
thermal, and attitude requirements are fulfilled throughout the whole mission. The obser­
vation plan will need reviews once the launch window is set, to make sure all the targets
are available within the operation time. Similarly, the launch site has to be evaluated again
on the basis of the fragility of the assembled structure and on the availability of the site
itself. Regarding the optics, more mirror lengths can be studied to find the best collecting
area­to­weight balance to achieve the best performance possible at the minimum price.
An investigation over the manufacture of small radii substrates has to be carried to under­
stand if a minimum radius of 30 mm is a feasible constraint. All the coatings designed can
be improved by optimizing them for the specific shell they are intended to be placed on.
Moreover, other hybrid coating recipes can be studied to further improve the reflectance
in the 100­200 keV range and in the 50­100 keV range. Moreover, a new optimization
should be held changing the optimized parameters of the first and second round in or­
der to constrain from the beginning the total thickness of the stack. The coating designs
will also have to be validated with high energy X­ray measurements, testing the mirror
reflectance up to 200 keV. The integration with the detector has to be investigated with
ray­tracing simulations, to derive the expected PSF and FOV. The same has to be done
with the background flux: simulations in GEANT4 and SRIM will help determine more pre­
cisely the background on the detector and therefore will allow for the design of an active

6
Conclusion
In this thesis, an investigation over high­energy X­ray focusing optics is carried on. The
aim is to study the feasibility of developing a new focusing optic able to reflect photons of
energies up to 200 keV, with an eye on the advancements and applications of this kind of
optics in this energy range. The scope of the thesis is to deliver a valid baseline (phase
0­A) that improves on the performance offered by existing x­ray observatories such as
NuSTAR, INTEGRAL, CHANDRA and XMM, filling part of the MeV sensitivity gap. This
leads to new studies of supernovae, detecting radioactive decay of isotopes such as 56Ni
and 44Ti, useful to infer the processes that power the expansion of the supernova in later
stages after the explosion. Moreover, this new observatory aims to open to new studies
of the cyclotron resonance scattering feature: an absorption line present in young neutron
stars that can be used to infer the intensity of their strong magnetic field. Furthermore, this
telescope will enable the study of energy cut­off in systems like Low­mass X­ray binaries,
which is related to the accretion disk state of the system. Additionally, the selection of
a detector able to correlate spectral and imaging capabilities will allow multi­dimensional
analysis combining spectral resolved and spatial resolved data. Scientific and physical re­
quirements are set in order to improve on the state­of­the­art performance while keeping
in mind the feasibility of such an instrument according to existing and planned missions
such as HEFT, NuSTAR and HEX­P. Consequently, the baseline for the minimum radius
is set at 30 mm, while the maximum one is set to 100 mm given the difficulty to reflect high
energies at high grazing incidence angles. This work also aims to define the best platform
that has to be employed to test and validate the developed technology: small satellites,
sounding rockets and stratospheric balloons are analyzed following parameters set on
the basis of the scientific and physical requirements. Eventually, a stratospheric balloon
is chosen to bring the probe over 38 km altitude to limit atmospheric absorption at the
minimum possible without going to space. A high­level analysis of the required subsys­
tems is held in this work to define what will be required and why, but a precise definition of
all the elements and subsystems requires specific knowledge of every subject and a fully
defined payload. Regardless, the telescope will have a baseline of 10 meters focal length
and the attitude control will be managed by the WASP system developed by NASA. The
ESRANGE facility (Sweden), the WALLOPS facility (USA) and Cachoeira Paulista (BRA)
have been considered to host the launch of the probe and an observation plan has been
created for all of them. The optical bench is based on a Wolter­Type­I geometry with two
reflections that share the same focal point. This allows nesting multiple shells one into
another, with great benefits to the total effective area at the price of major difficulties in the
assembly, aligning and achieving low angular resolutions. Different substrates have been
considered for the mirrors and the best one has been found in borosilicate glass, which
is thin enough to allow high nesting, light­weight and with heritage given by the NuSTAR
mission and DTU Space. Eventually, 500 mm long mirrors have been selected as they
offer the best balance between collecting area and mass of the optics. Different detector
materials have been investigated for their stopping power, and CdZnTe has been found
to be the best option, given the recent improvements on their spectral and spatial resolu­
tion. The selection fell on the 3D CZT Drift Strip detector developed at DTU Space and
although it doesn’t provide the required energy resolution at 158 keV, its efficiency and
margins of improvements make it the best choice. Various coating configurations have
been analyzed to identify the optimal design that maximizes the integrated effective area
within the mission’s energy range of interest. A brute­force optimization is performed us­
86
Mission Concept Analysis for Developing a Hard X­ray Focusing Optics Probe for Astronomical
Observations
==PAGE:99==
ing IMD, leading to the selection of W/Si power­law depth­graded multilayers due to their
enhanced broadband performance and the suitability of these materials for fabricating
coating layers as thin as 10Åwhile maintaining favorable surface properties. A maximum
thickness of the stack has been set to limit stress effects induced by the coating onto
the substrate. The contribution of each shell to the effective area has been computed
and the resulting total effective area has been used for the sensitivity and counts esti­
mation. The continuum sensitivity of the probe designed shows small improvements on
the performance of high energy telescopes like NuSTAR and INTEGRAL, with margins
of improvements derived from the possibility to stack multiple optics together to increase
the total SNR. The line sensitivity shows good resutls, comparable with NuSTAR and IBIS
and better than JEM­X. Although not all the mission requirements are achieved yet, big
margins of improvement are expected deriving from future works like ray­tracing simula­
tions for optics and detector performance. The mission designed in this work constitutes
a valid baseline to build a new x­ray focusing optic to fill the MeV­Gap and providing new
data to study the high energy universe.

Appendix B: Coating optimization
results
The following tables show the parameters retrieved from the two optimization iterations
carried on to design the coatings of the probe. Every group of coatings have its own
table with the 8 designs studied. All the designs are made of W/Si and assumed a rough­
