Abstract
The hybrid membrane aerated bioﬁlm reactor (HMABR) combines the activated sludge
process with a bioﬁlm reactor. This novel technology aims to sustain nitriﬁcation in the
bioﬁlm (Membrane Aerated Bioﬁlm Reactor, MABR) and denitriﬁcation in the suspended
phase.
Hollow ﬁber membranes (HFM) supply air into an activated sludge reactor developing
an oxic bioﬁlm and anoxic bulk liquid. The heterotrophic bacteria in suspension oxidize COD
through nitrogen removal and possibly aerobic COD oxidation in the bioﬁlm.
The present study consists on the design, construction and operation of two HMABRs
as well as the development of a mechanistic process model (Aquasim 2.1).
This novel HMABR design integrates a lamella settler next to the CSTR without the
need of an external clariﬁer. The settling efﬁciency of this unit was above 97% and required
only 12% of the systems space optimizing space and resources.
Complete mixing of the HMABR posed a challenge and despite using two magnetic
stir bars, complete mixing was not achieve. The theoretical HRT calculated was 16 hours
however experimental results showed an HRT of 24 hours.
An autotrophic and a heterotrophic nitriﬁcation/denitriﬁcation start-up strategies
demonstrated that the total nitrogen removal in the HMABR was affected by the wastewater
COD/N ratio if the bioﬁlm was not completely formed.
The reactors were operated at HRT of 16 hours, and 45 mgNH4 −N/L, while the COD
loading was gradually varied (C/N = 0, 3, 5). The ammonium surface loading for both reactors
during this study was 0.89 gN/m2.d
Reactor 1 started up with a C/N loading of 3 and its ratio was increased up to 5 with

a CSTR next to a MABR with an advective link. This model can be optimized and be further
used for future star-up strategies on HMABRs or to see conditions affecting the performance
in the process.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
1.1
Wastewater
Wastewater is known as the water that has been supplied by the waterworks to a community or
industry and that has been used in diverse applications therefore treatment is needed before it is
discharged into the different water bodies. Untreated wastewater contains many pathogenic or-
ganisms, nutrients (C, N, P, etc), toxic compounds or even mutagenic/carcinogenic compounds.
Therefore to protect the public health and environment, the removal of these pollutants is
essential.
In many ﬁrst world countries, wastewater has a safe network of distribution to the
WWTP keeping it away from households however this is not the case in third world countries
where the lack of a sewage system is a reality.
Sewage has been considered a risk for health for many centuries. Ancient Greeks (300
BC to 500 AD) collected wastewater and stormwater from their community into basins to use
them as fertilizer for their agricultural ﬁelds. Romans in the 800 BC built the Cloaca Maxima
which was a central sewer system well protected by the roman empire. The Cloaca Maxima
collapsed after the fall of the roman empire leaving the romans on a period denominated the
”Sanitary dark ages” (Henze, 2008).
During the dark ages wastewater was dealt by throwing it in the streets or recovered
using sewage carts where ﬁnally they would end up in water bodies nearby. Hence, spread
of diseases such as the bubonic plague or typhoid fever were converted into epidemics in
crowded communities. Scientists, started correlating the importance of wastewater treatment
and disposal to these epidemics (Salgot and Folch, 2018).

The constant increase of the population in cities making it difﬁcult to collect wastewater,
the spread of diseases and the feeling of bad odours was pushing the people for higher
investments and efforts to put an end to these problems. It was not until 1914 where in the UK
the activated sludge process was invented.
Despite having discovered the activated sludge process scientists realized that rivers
could not manage with all the organic loads being discharged to the water bodies as the
population kept constantly expanding therefore innovation in the wastewater treatment ﬁeld
has been going on for many years.
Wastewater treatment has evolved in many ways, that now it is being treated at many
levels.The levels of wastewater treatment are as follow and in order of nutrients/pollutants
removal: preliminary, primary, advanced primary, secondary, secondary with nutrient removal
and advanced treatment (Metcalf and Eddy, 2014).
1.1.1
Wastewater characteristics
The characteristics of wastewater rely on the origin of its source. It is important to comprehend
wastewater characteristics so that further improvements can be done in terms of its collection,
treatment and reuse. The most common sources of wastewater are stormwater, domestic and
industrial wastewater. Wastewater constituents are classiﬁed for their chemical, biological and
physical properties. These three properties are interrelated between each other in wastewater
(Metcalf and Eddy, 2014).
Tables 1.1, 1.2, and 1.3 show the main chemical and physical properties present in
domestic wastewater as well as important microorganisms. Concentration of these parameters
will depend on the usage that the water has had. The parameters mentioned will deﬁne the
quality of the sewage (Sperling, 2007).

1.1.1.1
Carbonaceous organic matter
Organic carbon present in wastewater is one of the main causes of water pollution prob-
lems. Microorganisms will consume dissolve oxygen and organic carbon for their metabolic
processes and thus decrease the amount of dissolved oxygen in the water.
These carbonaceous constituents can be measured by COD and BOD laboratory
analyses. Analyses of these compounds in wastewater will determine for an activated sludge
process the following: the design of the aeration tank volume, the required oxygen transfer
rates and the rates of sludge production (Metcalf and Eddy, 2014).
BOD is totally biodegradable, however COD has parts which are not biodegradable.
As it can be seen on ﬁgure 1.1 COD is splitted into biodegradable and nonbiodegradable
COD. The part of soluble COD which is not biodegradable will be found in the efﬂuent of
an activated sludge process while the particulate nonbiodegrabale portion will form part of
the biomass. A high level of interest takes the biodegradable COD part which is divided into
soluble biodegradable COD and slowly biodegradable COD present.
Figure 1.1: Fractionation of COD
It is important to determine the soluble biodegradable portion of COD since is the part
which will be quickly assimilated by the biomass at high rates while the particulate part will
ﬁrst have to be dissolved by extracellular enzymes at slower rates (Metcalf and Eddy, 2014).

1.1.1.2
Nitrogen compounds
Nitrogen reactive compounds in aquatic medium can be found as NH+
4 , NO−
2 , NO−
3 , N2,
and organic nitrogen. Nitrogen constitutes an essential nutrient for microorganisms growth
and therefore information of its presence in wastewater is needed to check the treatability of
wastewater.
The main sources of nitrogen compounds come from: plants and animals origin,
atmospheric nitrogen (nitrogen ﬁxation), and sodium nitrate (natural deposits) (Metcalf and
Eddy, 2014). Lately humans have intervene in the natural nitrogen cycle adding new sources
of nitrogen compounds, a clear example is the industrial production of urea as fertilizer.
Nitrogen coming from wastewater is combined with proteinaceous matter and urea.
Decomposition changes the organic nitrogen to ammonia. Ammonia oxidation to nitrite and
nitrate in water bodies can lead to oxygen depletion also as Nitrogen is an essential nutrient
for algae it can lead to eutrophication in lakes and rivers (Sperling, 2007). Hence a worldwide
interest is taken on Nitrogen reactive compounds associating it to water pollution.
Total Kjedahl nitrogen (TKN) is a measure of the amount of ammonia and organic
nitrogen, where 60-70% of the wastewater’s inﬂuent will be composed of NH+
4 −N ready
for nitriﬁcation. Organic nitrogen can be particulate or soluble and as both forms it can be
biodegradable or nonbiodegradable (Metcalf and Eddy, 2014).
Particulate organic biodegradable nitrogen will have to undergo hydrolysis before its
ready to be degraded by bacteria. As well as with COD, the nonbiodegradable particulate
organic Nitrogen will stick into the biomass while the nonbiodegradable soluble portion will
leave in the water efﬂuent.

1.1.2
Wastewater environmental impacts
Wastewater either treated or untreated can have several impacts in the quality of water and
sediments of the water bodies where it is discharged.
Despite the different techniques and technologies used to treat wastewater, the dis-
charges from the WWTP still holds diversal microbial communities, nutrients, and organic/i-
norganic contaminants (Shon et al., 2006) (Bundschuh, 2014).
Once macro and micronutrients as well as trace metals are discharged from treated
or untreated wastewater, microorganisms uptake them for their metabolism in the aquatic
ecosystem. An excessive release of these nutrients (mainly N and P) leads to eutrophication
and ecotoxicity (Hilton et al., 2006).
Eutrophication is deﬁned as ”the enrichment of water by nutrients causing an acceler-
ated growth of algae and higher forms of plant life to produce undesirable disturbance to the
balance of organisms present in the water and to the quality of the water concerned” (OSPAR,
2003).
In order to control eutrophication and ecotoxicity in waters, management and control
of the efﬂuents discharged in aquatic ecosystems are needed. Managing the P loading to the
aquatic ecosystems decreases the uptake of N by algae but it also increases the loading of N to
coastal waters producing eutrophication (Conley et al., 2009). The main focus on reducing P
discharge load in the environment has served to tackle this problem however researchers point
at elevated N loads as an equally cause of eutrophication (Paerl and Scott, 2010).
The process of eutrophication brings with it several economic consequences. As a
result of excessive algae in rivers and lakes, they become aesthetically unattractive and have a
negative impact on tourism and prices of waterfront houses. Proliferation of algae in waters
will also increase the cost of water treatment at the water supply facilities (Hilton et al., 2006).
Wastewater treatment is known for releasing greenhouse gases (GHGs), its energy
consumption, and nutrients discharge (Hospido et al., 2004). In the European Union (EU),
there is a capacity of nearly 400 million population equivalents (Massara et al., 2017) therefore,
a big interest is centered on minimizing its energy consumption and decreasing its emissions.
WWTP release N2O, CO2, and CH4 during nutrient removal. Methane is released in

It is clear the environmental consequences of discharging and treating wastewater,

or chemoheterotrophs respectively (Metcalf and Eddy, 2014). Chemoheterotrophs oxidize

1.3 Biological treatment processes

1.3
Biological treatment processes
Biological treatment processes are methods used for the removal of colloidal or dissolved
biodegradable organic substances. These substances are turned into gases which can go to
the atmosphere and biomass which can be settled and removed using a physical separation
process. Biological treatment is widely used for the removal of nutrients (C, N, and P). If
further treatment is needed, chemical treatment processes can be used.
Biological treatment processes use microorganisms to treat wastewater. These microor-
ganisms usually use carbon as energy source and can be: strictly aerobic, strictly anaerobic or
facultative. The metabolisms which provides them the major energy-yielding are as followed
(Jørgensen, 1979):
• Fermentation: Energy-yielding redox reaction where organic compounds serve as ﬁnal
electron acceptors.
• Respiration (aerobic): Biological oxidation process where molecular oxygen is the ﬁnal
electron acceptor.
• Respiration (anaerobic): Biological oxidation process where inorganic compounds are
the ﬁnal electron acceptor.
1.3.1
Suspended growth processes
In suspended growth processes, the microbial communities who degrade organic matter are
kept in suspension by using effective mixing methods. Most of the suspended growth processes
are known for mostly being aerobic processes (Ghangrekar and Behera, 2014). Figure 1.3
shows the compiled different suspended growth process which can be aerobic or anaerobic.
Activated sludge is the suspended growth process most used for wastewater treatment.
Activated sludge is a mixture of microbial communities such as fungi, protozoa, bacteria and
rotifers. Bacteria are the ones in charge of degrading the organic carbon present in wastewater,
while rotifers and protozoa aid removing the dispersed bacteria without letting it go through
the reactor efﬂuent (Ghangrekar and Behera, 2014).

The conventional activated sludge system is composed of an aeration tank, a secondary

1.3.2
Attached growth processes
Attached growth processes are deﬁned as a type of biological treatment where microbial
communities are attached to inert mediums (substratum) and convert the organic matter and
other constituents of wastewater into gases and cell tissue. Attached growth processes are also
known as ﬁxed ﬁlm processes (Metcalf and Eddy, 2014).
In attached growth systems bioﬁlms are formed. Bioﬁlms are microbial communities
adhered between each other and to the surface. These microorganisms produce extracellular
polymeric substances (EPS) to be adhered (Vayenas, 2011).
In aqueous environments, bioﬁlms are formed when bacteria present in the medium
contacts the substratum by transport mechanisms. Cells adsorb in the substratum and giving
them enough time, they will produce EPS and attach to it. Substrates (carbon, oxygen, reactive
nitrogen, etc) transport to and within the bioﬁlm where the attached cells will start metabolizing
these substrates, growing, replicating , producing more EPS and ﬁnally establishing a bioﬁlm
community (Bryers, 1993).
Substrates are transported through the bioﬁlm-liquid boundary layer, moving towards
the bioﬁlm and reaching microbial cells. This mechanism of transport in bioﬁlms is diffusion.
The substrate penetration will depend on its concentration in the bulk liquid, bioﬁlm’s porosity,
and mass transfer in the interface. Because of the concentration gradients of the substrates, a
growth rate gradient also exists in the bioﬁlm (Vayenas, 2011).
In a system with diverse microbial communities, the microorganisms with slower
growth rate will be found inside the bioﬁlm whereas the ones with higher growth rates will be
found outside of it. Due to this organization, the microorganisms with slower growth rates are

1.4 Membrane Aerated Bioﬁlm Reactors (MABR)

1.4
Membrane Aerated Bioﬁlm
Reactors (MABR)
In wastewater treatment various dissolved gases can be used as electron acceptors or donors
depending on the microbial coomunities present. Despite the efﬁciency of these gases to
oxidize or reduce contaminants, they are limited by their poor solubility in practical uses.
Activated sludge provides continuous aeration in wastewater treatment to transfer
oxygen however it is considered to be a process with low oxygen transfer rates (OTR), energy
demanding, and for stripping GHG back to the atmosphere. Fortunately a more energy
efﬁcient way of dissolving gases into aqueous medium has been discovered and it is done with
membranes (Martin and Nerenberg, 2012).
Membranes’s lumen are pressurized with a gas which will diffuse from the membrane
wall to the bioﬁlm attached to it, when this gas is air they are called Membrane Aerated
Bioﬁlm Reactors.
MABRs are bubbleless gas-transfer membranes with bioﬁlm growth where oxygen
diffusing from the lumen is consumed by bacteria attached to the membrane. The rest of
substrates come from the bulk liquid and overcome the liquid boundary layer to diffuse inside
the bioﬁlm (Pellicer i Nacher, 2013).
Due to the diffusion of substrates from the bulk liquid to the bioﬁlm and the diffusion
of oxygen from the lumen to the bioﬁlm, these bioﬁlm systems are attributed to be counter-
diffusion bioﬁlms (Syron and Casey, 2008).
In MABRs, the highest relative activity will depend on the concentrations of the
electron acceptor and the electron donor. When the bulk liquid is limited by the electron
acceptor (e.g. O2) and the membrane side by the donor (e.g. NH4 –N), then the highest
relative activity will occur in the middle of the bioﬁlm (Essila et al., 2000).
The microbial activities within the bioﬁlm in MABRs show higher resistance to toxic
substances since the main activities are carried out in the inner portions of the bioﬁlm leading
to the growth of unique microbial communities (Misiak et al., 2011)

The HMABR is a novel technology used for carbon oxidation and TN removal. The HMABR
like the MABR is composed of hollow-ﬁbre membranes (HFM) from where air is supplied,

In the HMABR an aerobic nitrifying bioﬁlm is developed closed to the membrane
surface, from where NOx diffuse to the bulk liquid media. Once the HMABR is installed,
aeration from the activated sludge system is stopped and heterotrophic denitriﬁcation is
performed by the bacteria present in the bulk liquid. Denitriﬁcation is possible due to the
nitrite and nitrate exported from the bioﬁlm.

and heterotrophs proliferation in the bioﬁlm is limited (L.S. Downing and Nerenberg, 2007).

2.1.1
CSTR design
A continuous ﬂow stirred tank reactor (CSTR) was designed. This reactor consists on a volume
continuously mixed and where no gradients of state variables arise. In a CSTR the inﬂuent
and efﬂuent ﬂows are equal and as a consequence the reactor has a constant volume (Gujer,
2008). Hence, no gradients of intensive properties leads to a simple solution for volumetric
accumulation and reaction. In a CSTR the concentrations in the bulk and the efﬂuent must be
the same (Gujer, 2008).

2.1.2
Lamella settler design
The lamella settler is characterized for being composed of inclined plates. This type of settler
belongs to the multicurrent units. Lamellas settlers distinguish from conventional settlers
because the suspended solids are distributed into many streams ﬂowing separately through
parallel channels (Ziolo, 1996). The sedimentation of solids in the lamella settler will depend
on the following physical parameters:
• Particle size distribution.
• Flow patterns.
• Dimension of the plates in the lamella settler.
==PAGE:42==
30
2 Reactor design
• Solid’s density.
• Liquid’s density and viscosity.
One of the goals when designing the HMBR was to retain biomass and to sediment it
without the external aid of a larger conventional gravity settler, the countercurrent ﬂow as the
ﬂow pattern was chosen for the lamella settler. The countercurrent ﬂow is the ﬂow pattern
where suspended solids settle opposite to the liquid ﬂow (Ziolo, 1996) and the feeding of the
wastewater occurs from the lower part of the reactor, with water moving upwards through the
channels while solids collide between each other through the plates and sediment.
Water ﬂows in laminar regime through the inclined plates reducing the distance
particles need to travel before settling (Figure 2.3). The inclined plates suppress unstable
ﬂow patterns and mixing currents increasing the settler’s settling efﬁciency (Tarpagkou and
Pantokratoras, 2014).
Figure 2.3: Particle behavior in a conventional settler and a lamella settler.
(Tarpagkou and Pantokratoras, 2014)
The system geometry of the plates is crucial since it will have inﬂuence over the
sedimentation effectiveness. To design the plates four parameters are considered: Angle of
inclination (α), width (b), length (l), and the distance between each plate (h) (Ziolo, 1996).
For the construction of countercurrent plates, (Ziolo, 1996) suggests a ratio of l/h=
15-20, an angle of inclination between 45-60°. The angle of construction is one of the most

projecting settling area. Once lamella plates were designed in Autocad, before sending them

Process design
The process design comprehends design variables driving Carbon and Nitrogen removal. HRT,
N-load, C-load, Membrane surface, Ammonia Oxidizing Rate (AOR), and the number of
HFM are deﬁned.
For a 16 hours HRT the inﬂuent ﬂow rate is 270 mL/h in a reactor of 4.3 L, connected
to the settler. The combined HRT is of 18 hours, an the initial bSRT is 8 days to mimic a
conventional activated sludge system. The bSRT would be controlled/varied during this project.
Carbon dosing is calculated based on complete denitriﬁcation requirements. From literature it
is established that one electron equivalent (eqq) corresponds to 8 g of COD (Henze, 2008),
the COD equivalent will be 40 gCOD/molHNO3 which is equal to 2.86 gCOD/molNO3 –N
(Equation 2.1). The inﬂuent NH4 –N is set at 50 mg/L and since full nitriﬁcation to nitrate is
expected, the COD concentration needed will be 150 mgCOD/L.
HNO3 +5H+ +5e−−−→0.5N2 +3H2O
(2.1)
NH4-N inﬂuent is set at 50 mgN/L as a typical value of domestic wastewater and
maintained constant. The reactor will operate at different C:N ratios (3 and 5) as (Lin et al.,
2016) found that a COD/N ratio of 5 enhances the oxygen uptake rate (OUR) of AOB and
NOB in MABRs.
Ammonia oxidation rate (AOR) ranged between 0.85 and 1.3 gN/(m2.d) in previous
MABRs studies (L.S. Downing and Nerenberg, 2008; L.S. Downing and Nerenberg, 2007;
Shanahan and Semmens, 2015). Assuming an AOR of 1 gN/(m2.d) the total number of
membranes was calculated based on the characteristics of the HFM. The theoretical calculated
number of membranes needed to nitrify the ammonia loading per day corresponded to 8
bundles, each bundle constituted of 85 hollow-ﬁber membranes (Table 2.5) . The extra
membranes installed gave a 10% of speciﬁc surface excess to assure all ammonia would be

Reactor setup
Two lab-scale HMABRs were operated for TN removal and carbon oxidation (Figure 3.1).
Inﬂuent ammonia and sodium bicarbonate are pumped from the substrate tank 1 while the
trace elements and secondary substrates are pumped from the substrate tank 2.
Air is supplied to the HMABR system from an air compressor, through a globe valve,
a pressure gauge, ﬂow meter, and ﬁnally to the HFM. A pressure gauge and ﬂow meter in the
air outlet allows for pressure control inside the membrane. Air is delivered to the bulk liquid
via stone diffusers intermittently, controlled with a solenoid valve which is plugged to a timer
(Electromatic S-System): every two hours the valve opens six seconds delivering air to the
bulk. This procedure allows for better mixing conditions and forces bioﬁlm detachment to
prevent clogging.
Reactors 1 and 2 are fed with inﬂuent using a peristaltic pump (Ole Dich Instrument-
makers, ApS) (Figure 3.2). Complete bulk mixing is achieved by using two magnetic stirrers
(Nuova stirrer, Thermo Scientiﬁc). In Reactor 1, 2 magnetic stir bars of length 35 and 25 mm
long each at 600 rpm and 400 rpm respectively. In Reactor 2 uses two stir bars identical to
Reactor 1 at 350 rpm. In both reactors the largest stir bar is close to the inﬂuent zone for better
mixing while the smallest is located by the end of the reactor where mixing is weaker. The
smallest stir bar was set at low rpm to not disturb the settling of particles.

3.1.3
Tracer test
A tracer test was conducted to studty the non-ideal hydraulic behaviour of the reactor
(CSTR+settler). The tracer test is based on marking water molecules with substances which
are easy to measure in sufﬁcient temporal resolution. The residence time distribution will
describe the transport and mixing processes present in the reactor, thus a tracer which does
not interact with the media and that is dependant on the transport processes of water must be
chosen (Gujer, 2008) such as (NaCl):
• Does not react or adsorb to the reactor walls.
• Highly soluble, cheap, easy to measure.
• Does not affect mixing conditions or transport processes.
Procedure: The reactor was ﬁlled with distilled water. Air ﬂowed continuously through
the HFM and 6 seconds every two hours from the diffusers. Distilled water was pumped to the
HMABR at a ﬂow rate of 270 mL/h. The tracer test was performed by the addition of a pulse
(Dirac pulse) of a concentrated NaCl solution. Conductivity was measured with an electrode
(WTW, Germany) and stored every 10 minutes. The test ﬁnished when the conductivity
reached background levels.

Discussion
In general R1-COD successfully achieved simultaneous nitriﬁcation/denitriﬁcation neverthe-
less this is not the case for R2 whose performance decreased with the increase of COD loading,
suggesting that a different microbial community had developed.
R1-COD performed short-cut denitriﬁcation via nitrite despite R2 performing full
nitriﬁcation to nitrate when no COD was loaded. This can be explained by nitrite being the
==PAGE:73==
3.2 Results
61
main product of nitriﬁcation and thus being immediately reduced to nitrogen by heterotrophs
in the presence of COD whereas when no COD was being loaded, nitrite could be oxidized by
NOB.
R1-COD short-cut denitriﬁcation via nitrite agrees with the results presented in other
studies for MABR and HMABR (Hibiya et al., 2003; L. Downing and Nerenberg, 2008;
L.S. Downing and Nerenberg, 2008). This suggests that mainly nitrite is being exported as
nitriﬁcation product from the bioﬁlm.
With a nitrifying bioﬁlm formed in R1-COD at a low C/N ratio (3), the increased
ratio of 5 enhanced nitriﬁcation as it was shown in previous MABR studies (Lin et al., 2016).
The increase of COD loading did not affect R1-COD performance since the COD was being
consumed for denitriﬁcation in the bulk and was less probable to diffuse into the bioﬁlm.
Both reactors showed that at low C/N ratios incomplete denitriﬁcation occurs, with
nitrate as the main product in the efﬂuent, suggesting COD limitation or aerobic COD oxidation
in the bioﬁlm, as the bulk remained anoxic.
R2 high COD efﬂuent concentrations allow greater COD penetration into the bioﬁlm
and thus increases the population of heterotrophs (L.S. Downing and Nerenberg, 2008),
whereas low COD efﬂuent concentrations as the present in R1-COD denitriﬁes in the bulk
liquid keeping heterotrophs in suspension rather than in the bioﬁlm. R2 experienced the
competition between autotrophs and heterotrophs by decreasing its nitriﬁcation rate due to the
rapid proliferation of heterotrophs compiting with autotrophs for oxygen in the presence of
COD.
The OTE of the system must be improved to enhance nitriﬁcation (abundance of
autotrophs), therefore better aeration techniques as the ones explained in the OUR section
can be implemented. Due to the leak of air from the system in the connections, air ﬂow and
pressure can be reduced to avoid unnecessary energy misuse. Membranes arrangement shall
be also improved since in operation membranes tend to stick to each other and form a bundle
together limiting the substrates diffusion into the bioﬁlm and the effective bioﬁlm surface
(Tierra et al., 2015).

4.1
Model concept
Mathematical modeling offers a way to describe and analyze multiple processes that occur
simultaneously in time and space in bioﬁlm systems, therefore is a useful tool before starting
experimental procedures. Modeling can save time and optimize resources.
A model for a CSTR, MABR, and HMABR are developed using the software Aquasim
2.0. The model calculates the reactor performance under different substrate loading conditions,
abundance of bacterial groups, and bioﬁlm thicknesses.
Two nitrifying communities, AOB and NOB, and heterotrophic bacteria are considered
in the model. Heterotrophs grow on carbon removal as electron donor and aerobic/anoxic
electron acceptors (oxygen, nitrite and nitrate).
Processes included consist of: aerobic and anoxic growth of heterotrophs, aerobic
AOB growth on ammonia, aerobic growth of NOB on nitrite, microbial death and lysis, and
hydrolysis of organic particulate and organic nitrogen. Consumption of alkalinity is attributed
to AOB growth and the generation of alkalinity occurs denitriﬁcation.

Adaptations to the conceptual model can be done to simulate a real HMABR. An
MABR connects through an advective link to a CSTR. This link is based on that when having
a cascade of two CSTR and the recirculation ﬂow tends to inﬁnity, the performance of the
cascade CSTR will lie to the one of a single CSTR (Gujer, 2008).
The model consists of 3 completed mixed reactor compartments (CSTR, Aeration,
and Clariﬁer), one bioﬁlm reactor compartment (MABR) (Figure 4.5). This model includes a
clariﬁer next to the CSTR for the control of the SRT.
The following assumptions are considered: The maximum bioﬁlm thickness was set to
100 µm. The bioﬁlm reactor volume was reduced to 0.1 Liters as the liquid reaction occurs in
the CSTR. The CSTR considers periodic aeration for 12 seconds once per day to to simulate
the aeration purge to limit bioﬁlm growth in the MABR.

4.4
Discussion
Overall the model conﬁguration validation and evaluation comply with the main processes of
nitriﬁcation, denitriﬁcation, and carbon oxidation. The model describes nitriﬁcation taking
place in the MABR and denitriﬁcation in the CSTR, resulting in a hybrid MABR process.
So far, no process models have been developed before for a HMABR however (Aybar
et al., 2014) developed an energy-efﬁcient HMABR model which is a cost-operation model to
decide whether the HMABR was to be implemented in a conventional WWTP.
The developement of this process model is a great advantage for future HMABR
studies. New HMABR operation strategies can be tested where different parameters can be
changed in order to see how the HMABR performance would be affected.

4.4 Discussion
77
The model proved that an oxic nitrifying bioﬁlm could be possible to simulate however
it also indicated that a minimum bioﬁlm thickness shall be present for autotrophs to be present
in the bioﬁlm.
The shifts on microbial communities when changing the COD loading conﬁrms that
microbial communities will shift depending on the substrates supplied. In other words if the
start-up of the HMABR R1 starts with a C/N ratio of 3 and R2 starts with a ratio of 0 and then
this ratio is increased to 5, when reaching steady state both reactors will have the same bioﬁlm
composition.
Further improvements to the model can be done, implementations to approach a more
realistic simulations are listed below:
• Implementation of four step denitriﬁcation.
• N2O production by AOB.
• Diffusive transfer of all gaseous species such as N2O, NO, CO2.
• Inhibition factors in the growth of AOB, NOB and HB.
• Calculate pH variations in the bioﬁlm (Ma et al., 2017).

CHAPTER5
Conclusion
A Hybrid MABR with a lamella settler included was successfully designed, built and operated.
The lamella settler proved to have a high settling capacity due to the lamella plates inclination
and the contact surface provided for settling. Less space was needed than other conventional
clariﬁers used in other HMABR studies.
The HMABR proved not to be affected by the COD loading. At a C/N ratio of 5
(R1-COD) the nitriﬁcation rate increased and thus autotrophic population. The COD loading
did not affect the performance of Reactor 1 because inﬂuent COD was being uptake by
heterotrophs in suspension which decreased the COD concentration in the bulk liquid and thus
did not allow high COD concentrations diffuse into the bioﬁlm keeping heterotrophs at lower
concentration in the bioﬁlm. Short SRT (5 days) also kept the right amount of heterotrophic
bateria for denitriﬁcation avoiding them to proliferate in excess and be transported to the
bioﬁlm.
Reactor 1 (heterotrophic start-up) presents a higher OUR for C/N 3 because ammonia
is being fully nitriﬁed to nitrate due to limited Carbon conditions while at a C/N ratio of 5
OUR decreases despite the nitriﬁcation rate increase, this can be explained because nitrogen
is being removed via nitrite and therefore less oxygen is being used.
Heterotrophs present in the start-up of the reactor aided autotrophs to proliferate in the
membranes by providing them protection from shear stress, depredation or wash out.
The failure of a bioﬁlm formation (R2 case) affects nitriﬁcation and thus denitriﬁcation,
probably more than 21 days shall have been waited before inserting COD in the inﬂuent due
to the autotrophs reproduction rate.
The Aquasim model suggests that despite using different C/N ratio as start-up strategies

Future work/suggestions
Uncomplete mixing (experimental HRT 24 hours) must be adressed to increase the perfor-
mance of the HMABR and thus the magnetic stir bars can be changed for an axial mixer.
The membranes arrangement can always be improved for a better diffusion of sub-
strates into the HFM as well as air distribution into the HFM. Periodic venting in closed-ended
HFM have proved to increase OTRs in previous studies and thus can be implemented in the
HMABR.
Characterization of the bioﬁlm such as thickness or microbial communities present in
the bioﬁlm (qPCR or FISH techniques) can be performed to conﬁrm the experimental results.
Batch tests on the suspended biomass and membranes, separately, to test for speciﬁc
nitriﬁcation and denitriﬁcation rates.
