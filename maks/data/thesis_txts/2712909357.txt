ABSTRACT 
Gaussia luciferase (GLuc) is a small enzyme secreted by the marine copepod Gaussia princeps, 
that produces very intense bioluminescence when it reacts with the luciferin coelenterazine. 
The focus of the scientific research on this protein has been mainly placed on its applications as 
a reporter system and not that much on the basic characterization of this enzyme. For this 
reason, not much information is available in the literature about the mechanism of light 
production of this luciferase and the catalytic residues of the reaction to convert the substrate 
coelenterazine to coelenteramide.  
This Master thesis is separated into two studies, both of them directed towards the 
characterization of this enzyme. In the first part of the study, two arginine residues were studied 
as putative catalytic residues, as indicated by the results of a previous work in the JRW research 
group. To this end, the bioluminescence properties of two single alanine to arginine substitution 
variants of GLuc were analyzed and compared with GLuc wild type. In the second part, several 
C-terminal truncated variants of GLuc were analyzed, in order to study the relevance of the C-
terminal of the protein for the production of bioluminescence.

1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1. Bioluminescence 
Bioluminescence is a phenomenon that implies the emission of light as a result of a chemical 
reaction that occurs in some organisms (Kahlke et al., 2016). The enzymes that catalyze 
bioluminescence reactions are called luciferases and their substrate luciferins. The enzymatic 
reaction consists of the oxidation of the luciferin by the luciferase. For some types of luciferases, 
the luciferin has to be activated using ATP before it can be oxidized (Kaskova et al., 2016), 
although this is not the case for the GLuc luciferase that is the luciferase of interest in this study. 
The oxidized form of the luciferin relaxes from its excited state in a process that releases a 
photon, and therefore this reaction results in the production of light (Kotlobay et al., 2020). The 
production of bioluminescence has been observed in some organisms of different evolutionary 
lineages, such as bacteria, arthropods, molluscs, annelids, and vertebrates (only in fish) (Delroise 
et al., 2021). 
The scientific research about GLuc that can be found in the literature is more focused on the 
application of this enzyme as a reporter system, and there is not so much information in the 
literature about the basic characterization of GLuc. Surprisingly, its three-dimensional structure 
was only recently published and its catalytic mechanism remains unknown up to date. 
 
1.2. Gaussia luciferase 
Gaussia luciferase (GLuc) is a small enzyme that produces a bright quick flash of light in the 
presence of its preferred substrate, named coelenterazine. In nature, it is produced by the 
copepod Gaussia princeps, which is a crustacean of about 10 mm long that inhabits in the 
mesopelagic aquatic layer of the ocean, at about 350-800 meters below the ocean surface.  
Gaussia princeps secretes the luciferase GLuc together with its substrate to the marine 
environment probably as a mechanism of defense from predators, as the emitted burst of light 
can act as a distraction mechanism to allow the copepod to escape from them (Barnes et al., 
1972).  
GLuc is composed by 185 amino acids and a molecular weight of 19.9 kDa (Rathnayaka et al., 
2010). In nature, it contains a secretion signalling peptide of 17 amino acids in the N-terminal of 
the protein sequence, but due to the lack of purpose in bacterial expression, this part has been 
truncated in the protein being researched at Jakob R. Winther’s (JRW) group. Therefore, the 
protein length of the GLuc version used in this Master thesis is 168 residues (with a molecular 
weight of 18.2 kDa), and the numbering of the amino acids used in this report will be from -17 
to 168. GLuc comprises two structural domains that include the residues 27-97 and 98-168 
respectively, and a very variable N-terminal region. The two domains exhibit a sequence 
similarity of 28%, as they contain around 20 conserved amino acids each (Inouye and Sahara, 
2008a). 
The natural protein contains eleven cysteines, one of them in the pro-sequence, whose function 
has been hypothesized to be the contribution to the folding of the protein by reshuffling non-
native disulfide bonds in vivo. (Wu et al., 2015; Weissman et al., 1992). In the protein sequence 
without the secretion peptide there are ten cysteine residues, which have been proved to be 
oxidized (Rathnayaka et al., 2010; Larionova et al., 2018).  There are five cysteines in each 
domain, with four of them in conserved positions. They are believed to form two sets of 
intradomain disulfide bonds and one interdomain disulfide bonds, although it has not been

elucidated yet which specific cysteines are linked together (Wu et al., 2015). In fact, in the JRW 
group, several attempts to determine the disulfide bond pattern using proteomics have given 
inconclusive or even contradictory information. All of the cysteines except for the cysteine in 
position 59 have been described to be essential for activity (Goerke et al., 2008). 
 
1.3. Coelenterazine luciferin 
Similarly to other marine luciferases such as Oplophorus luciferase (OLuc), Renilla luciferase 
(RLuc) and Metridia longa luciferase (MLuc), GLuc uses the luciferin coelenterazine as a 
substrate (Haddock et al., 2010). Coelenterazine (CTZ) consists of a central aromatic 
imidazopyrazinone that is derivatized with three moieties: p-hydroxy-phenyl, benzyl and 
phydroxy-benzyl (Woo et al., 2008). GLuc has a fairly narrow substrate specificity with CTZ being 
its preferred substrate (Innouye et al., 2013). 
Deep sea copepods are one of the few groups of organisms that have been found to 
biosynthesize coelenterazine, unlike other organisms that use this luciferin, which acquire it 
through their diet (Lau et al., 2021; Oba et al., 2009). The biosynthesis of GLuc is believed to 
occur as a result of the cyclisation of the backbone of three amino acids: one phenylalanine and 
two tyrosines (Thomson et al., 1995). 
Coelenterazine is less stable and prone to auto-oxidation than other luciferins (Adams et al., 
2014), and this entails taking certain measures when handling it in the laboratory to do 
experiments, as it should be kept cold and not exposed to light and oxygen as much as possible. 
 
1.4. Coelenterazine bioluminescence reaction 
The reaction catalyzed by GLuc can be described in two steps, the oxidation of the CTZ substrate 
followed by a decarboxylation, which results in the emission of blue light at 480 nm (Tannous et 
al., 2005). In the first reaction step, molecular oxygen is incorporated to the molecule leading to 
the formation of a peroxide intermediate. This intermediate is unstable and closes into a 
dioxetanone ring structure of high energy. The subsequent descarboxilation of this molecule 
results in coelenteramide in an excited state, that finally relaxes to the ground state releasing a 
photon that can be detected as blue bioluminescence. (Syed et al., 2021; Lourenço et al., 2018; 
Wu et al., 2015). This reaction can also occur spontaneously in solution, due to the auto-
oxidation of CTZ, but the quantum yield in this case is much lower (Shimomura et al., 2011).

1.5. Characteristics of GLuc bioluminescence  
As it has been mentioned before, the catalytic mechanism of GLuc has not been described yet 
in the literature, nor the key catalytic residues involved in the reaction to convert coelenterazine 
to coelenteramide in the case of this luciferase.   
The research on this enzyme has been more focused on the development of new applications, 
and only a few groups have been doing basic research on this enzyme, so there are many aspects 
that still remain to be elucidated in order to have a clear picture of the mechanism of light 
production and the flash properties of the bioluminescence produced by Gaussia luciferase.  
In a recent paper published by the JRW group (Dijkema et al., 2021) several breakthroughs about 
the reaction of GLuc with its substrate CTZ were reported, contributing to the understanding of 
this peculiar enzyme. 
Very importantly, they describe that the quick decay in the luminescence produced by GLuc 
shortly after the burst of light characteristic of the flash properties of the luminescence of this 
enzyme  is due to the inactivation of the enzyme and not due to depletion of the substrate. This 
is a very relevant fact about this enzyme that surprisingly had not been described yet in the 
literature. 
The experiment conducted to prove this consisted of two luminescence assays, in which CTZ was 
injected to a sample containing GLuc, followed by a second injection into the sample of either 
CTZ or of GLuc. The second injection was performed 5 minutes after the first injection, when the 
light intensity had dropped to the baseline and was stable. The luminescence spectra of this 
experiments are depicted on Figure 11, as a picture taken from the paper Dijkema et al., 2021.

The hypothesis to test in this experiment was that, if the decay in light signal was produced by 
the depletion of substrate, the second injection of CTZ would lead to a second peak in the light 
intensity recorded. Conversely, it was observed that the second CTZ injection did not lead to a 
high peak in light intensity, but just a very low increase in light intensity, that can be attributed 
to the luminescence of the auto-oxidation of the substrate. This indicates that the enzyme 
present in the sample was not able to react with the fresh CTZ added anymore. In the assay in 
which more enzyme was injected to the sample, a second peak almost as high as the first one 
was indeed observed upon the injection of the enzyme, so it was concluded that there was still 
substrate left in the sample. Thus, the decay in the first case was not due to substrate depletion 
but rather caused by the inactivation of GLuc after the reaction with the substrate. In further 
experiments, it was determined that this inactivation occurred after 10 to 200 reaction cycles, 
depending on the substrate concentration.  
Another important finding reported in this paper is that the inactivated form of the enzyme has 
a larger apparent size, and they concluded that the flash properties of GLuc are caused by an

1.6. GLuc structure 
The three-dimension structure of GLuc was recently determined by NMR (Wu et al., 2020) (see 
Appendix). However, an ongoing structural study of GLuc by NMR at the JRW group have led to 
the determination of a structure with some differences in comparison to the published 
structure. It is important to note that the figures depicted in this Master thesis (except if 
otherwise indicated) represent the structure predicted from data collected by NMR at the JRW 
group, which is not final nor published, as more data is still being collected to better determine 
the structure. I decided to use this structure as it is the main structure of reference in the 
laboratory at the moment of writing this thesis. 
As it is shown in figure 3, the structure consists of a variable and flexible N-terminal region, 
followed by the two domains of GLuc. Both of the domains contain two large alpha-helices 
forming a “V” shape, that are connected by a small loop, located in the bottom part of the 
structure as viewed in the picture on the left. The first amino acid of the second domain is 
marked with a red arrow in the picture on the left. The C-terminal of the protein, which is the 
last part of the second domain, is predicted to be an unstructured tail that unlike the N-terminal 
contains several highly conserved residues. 
In all the pictures of this structure included in this report, the structure has been colored 
attending to the degree of conservation of the sequence of GLuc at each position, based on a 
multiple sequence alignment of a small set of homologues of GLuc. The highly conserved 
residues are depicted in pink, while residues that are less conserved are colored in orange, 
yellow and green.

1.7. This study 
1.7.1. Aim of the project 
The objective of this Master Thesis was to advance in the characterization of the mechanism of 
luminescence production by Gaussia luciferase. With this main objective in common, two 
different subprojects were carried out in this thesis. The first one consisted in the analysis of the 
relevance of two arginine residues as potential catalytic amino acids of the enzyme. In the 
second one, the objective was to study the effect of the truncation of the C-terminal of the 
protein on the ability to produce bioluminescence by the enzyme. 
 
1.7.2. Previous work 
In a previous project in the JRW research group, a mutational study of GLuc was carried out, in 
which 31 single alanine substitution mutants were analyzed in terms of their ability to produce 
luminescence in comparison to the wild type GLuc. The residues studied in this alanine scanning 
of GLuc were conserved residues that could possibly form hydrogen bonds (Nordentoft, 2020). 
This study was not performed with the purified proteins, which would have been too much time 
consuming. Instead, it was decided to use a more efficient method to identify promising mutants 
based on their luminescence that they display, which could be investigated later by doing a more 
fine-tune analysis of their activity with the purified protein. E.coli BL21 (DE3) that expressed the 
GLuc variants were spotted on a plate, and the luminescence displayed by the bacteria colonies 
was measured by taking pictures with a charged coupled device (CCD) camera immediately after 
spraying the plates with the luciferin coelenterazine. The analysis of this pictures revealed the 
presence of some GLuc variants that lead to a slightly prolonged luminescence, a few in which 
the bioluminescence intensity was very reduced and some others that were not very different 
from the GLuc WT. The focus of this previous project was to study the GLuc variants with 
prolonged bioluminescence, and those were the ones that were further investigated. However, 
the results obtained in the bioluminescent colony screening unveiled some GLuc variants that 
exhibited very low or even any detectable luminescence which would also be interesting to 
study. This previous work at the JRW group led to the proposal of the first subproject of the 
present Master Thesis.

1.7.3 GLuc variants in this study 
 
1.7.3.1. GLuc arginine mutants 
First of all, an important point to clarify is that throughout this thesis the term “arginine 
mutants” (and not “alanine mutants”) is used to refer to the arginine to alanine mutants of GLuc 
that were generated in this project. Specifically, these are mutants of GLuc that contain an 
alanine residue in the place of an arginine in the positions 76 and 147 of the protein sequence. 
In the previous study in the JRW group (Nordentoft, 2020), two arginines were identified as 
potential key catalytic residues of GLuc, as the single mutants of GLuc in which this amino acid 
position had been substituted by an alanine did not display any detectable luminescence in 
comparison to the negative control in an assay carried out in plates with colonies that expressed 
these mutants. 
If these arginines were confirmed to be catalytic residues, this would be a great advance towards 
the discovery of the catalytic mechanism of this luciferase. 
Besides, in the case that these residues were found to be essential for the activity but were not 
involved in the binding of the substrate, the mutants GLuc R76A and GLuc R147A would be 
inactive variants of GLuc that are able to bind the substrate, luckily in the same conformation as 
the WT. Then, the structure of these GLuc mutants bound to the substrate might be easier to be 
solved by NMR, as the substrate hopefully would help to stabilize the protein. 
With these expectations, in this project it was decided to follow this research line by purifying 
the mutants GLuc R76A and GLuc R147A and to test their activity. In a preliminary study 
conducted just before this project started, another student from the JRW group had purified the 
arginine mutants and had found a little activity in the samples, but these results were not totally 
trusted and had to be verified, due to contamination problems with GLuc WT that had been 
observed in other projects in the laboratory at that time. This will be further explained at the 
beginning of the results section. 
As a last remark on the arginine mutants, it has to be mentioned in this introduction that the 
initial plan of this thesis was to study the arginine mutants GLuc R76A and GLuc R147A. However, 
as the project advanced and the first results on this single arginine mutants were obtained, it 
was considered interesting to generate a double arginine mutant in which those two arginines 
were substituted by alanine residues (named GLuc R76A-R147A), as it will be explained in the 
results section. The figure below depicts the position of the arginines of interest for this study.

1.7.3.2 GLuc truncated variants 
In a different attempt to obtain more knowledge about this enzyme and to help determine 
which parts of the protein are essential for the activity, it was decided to study the implication 
of the C-terminal of the protein sequence in the production of luminescence. 
A former student at the JRW group had studied the effect of the truncation of the N-terminal of 
GLuc and had described that the removal of this end of the protein sequence up to the residue 
28 did not cause a major decrease on the production of luminescence (Madsen, 2019). However, 
the same study on the C-terminal had never been done, and this was one of the objectives of 
this Master Thesis.

The truncation points to generate the mutants were decided based on a multiple sequence 
alignment of homologues of GLuc. Four truncated variants of GLuc were designed. GLuc del. 1-
164, GLuc del. 1-155 and GLuc 1-151. In GLuc del. 1-164 the deletion was very short, it only lacks 
four amino acids and none of them are highly conserved, so it was expected that this variant 
would display a very similar activity to GLuc wild type. In this truncated variant, the glycine 164 
was not deleted, as it is highly conserved and thus it could be important for the activity. In GLuc 
del. 1-155 the deletion comprised 13 amino acids, including the highly conserved G164 and a 
glutamine and an isoleucine that were mildly conserved. In GLuc del. 1-151 there were 17 
residues deleted, including two additional conserved amino acids, a lysine and an isoleucine. It 
was decided not to delete the highly conserved phenylalanine 151, although further truncations 
could be tested if this third variant proved to have a similar activity to the wild type. 
A fourth variant was generated, named GLuc del. 1-135, in which the truncation was much larger 
than in the other cases. The idea behind this variant was to check if it was possible to obtain a 
very small version of GLuc that still conserved a similar level of luciferase activity to the wild 
type. It has to be admitted that this attempt to create a still active variant was not very likely to 
succeed, because due to the extensive deletion it is very probable that this variant will not be 
able to fold in a conformation that allows it to be active. Beside this, the truncated region 
includes several residues that were described to reduce the bioluminescence production in the 
alanine scanning of the project conducted by Nordentoft, including the arginine 147. However, 
these residues are only a few of the many amino acids that have been found to alter the 
luminescence emitted by GLuc in the mentioned alanine scanning and in other mutational 
studies published in the literature, so it was hypothesized that this variant might still be able to 
produce luminescence and it was decided that it could be worth to generate it and test its 
activity. 
To select the truncation point, it was observed that the cysteine C148 was located in the upper 
part of the helix to be removed, and therefore it was considered necessary to remove the 
cysteine that is closest to it (C136), both in the sequence and in the three dimensional structure, 
so that all of the cysteines present in the truncated variant could potentially form a disulfide 
bond and there was not an odd number of cysteines in the protein. This reasoning eventually 
lead to decide to create the truncated variant GLuc del. 1-135. The last amino acid of this variant, 
glutamine Q135, is located in a loop between two small alpha-helices at the end of the second 
domain of GLuc, that are positioned just before the C-terminal tail.

1.8. Special features for the expression of GLuc 
1.8.1. Solubility enhancing peptide (SEP) tag 
The single arginine mutants were designed in a previous study. At that time, all the mutants 
generated in the JRW included a solubility enhancing peptide (SEP) tag. The reason for this is 
that it had been described in the literature that the addition of this tag to the C-terminal of the 
protein increased the amount of active GLuc protein, folded and with the correct disulfide bond 
pattern, that was collected from the expression in E. coli BL21 (DE3) (Rathnayaka et al., 2011). 
However, previous students at the JRW group compared the expression yield of GLuc WT with 
and without the SEP tag using the expression conditions that are used in the JRW laboratory, 
and they found that the expression of active GLuc was not too much influenced by the presence 
of the SEP tag. Remarkably, in later projects at the JRW group and also in this thesis, the activity 
of the enzyme has been found to be affected by the presence of the SEP tag. The two versions 
of the WT protein have been compared in luminescence assays on the same day, and it has been 
determined that the luminescence signal exhibited by GLuc WT without the SEP tag is 
approximately 3-fold higher than that of the WT protein that included the SEP tag. 
As it was not considered an advantage to include the SEP tag in the C-terminal of the enzyme, it 
was decided that the GLuc variants generated from this point forward would not include the SEP 
tag. Moreover, as one of the lines of work in the JRW laboratory was to determine the three-
dimension structure of GLuc by NMR, it was preferred that the studied protein was as similar as 
possible to the protein in the native state in nature, not including any tags unless they were 
essential for the expression or purification of the protein.

1.8.2. CyDisCo expression system 
GLuc is a protein that contains ten cysteine residues, and therefore, to be correctly folded and 
be able to display activity, it has to contain the correct disulfide bond pattern. This poses a 
problem for the recombinant expression of the protein in BL21 E. coli in the laboratory, as it 
reduces the yield of monomeric and correctly folded protein that is obtained from the 
expression. 
To by-pass this issue, at the JRW group the CyDisCo system (Matos et al., 2014) is used. The BL21 
E. coli cells that are used to express GLuc and its variants also contain a cytoplasmic disulfide 
bond formation in E. coli (CyDisCo) plasmid. This plasmid contains a gene that encodes the 
enzyme ERV1p, which is a sulfhydryl oxidase from yeast mitochondria, which is able to catalyze 
the formation of disulfide bonds, and a human protein disulfide isomerase (hPDI), that can help 
in the isomerization of the disulfide bonds. Therefore, the transformation of the cells with this 
helper plasmid has been found to be useful for the cytosolic expression of proteins rich in 
cysteines. 
In a previous study at the JRW group (Nordentoft, 2020), it was determined that the use of the 
CyDisCo expression system for the expression of GLuc allowed to increase the purification yield 
of this protein by 7-fold, due to the reduction of the amount of GLuc forming aggregates and an 
increase of the soluble monomeric protein, and apparently in the native conformation. For this 
reason, this expression system was used in this thesis as well, for all the GLuc variants and GLuc 
wild type that were expressed throughout the project. 
 
 
 
2. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
2.1. Arginine mutants of Gaussia luciferase 
As it was explained in the introduction of this thesis, a previous study in the JRW group allowed 
to identify the arginines R76 and R147 as important residues for the production of light by Gluc, 
and concluded that it would be interesting to analyze the catalytic activity of the two mutants 
==PAGE:16==
13 
 
more thoroughly using a more sensitive method, by conducting luminescence assays with the 
purified enzymes. This analysis would allow to determine if they were completely inactive, as 
indicated by the previous study, or if the mutants displayed residual activity that was too little 
to be detected by the method used.  
Shortly before this project started, some preliminary luminescence assays were carried out with 
the purified arginine mutants, in which the mutants displayed 1,000-fold less activity than GLuc 
WT. More specifically, the activities measured were 0.17% and 0.09% of the activity of the wild 
type for GLuc R76A and GLuc R147A respectively. This was so little activity that it was suspected 
that it might have come from a contamination of the mutant samples with the wild type sample 
at some point during the purification process. Thus, it was decided to repeat the purification and 
the assays again to confirm or refute the observed results. From this point, this Master Thesis 
continues the research on this line. The first part of this project was dedicated to the 
characterization of these two arginine mutants with a focus on their activity. 
 
2.1.1. Analysis of the activity of GLuc R76A and GLuc 147A 
In order to measure the activity of the purified mutants GLuc R76A and GLuc R76A, luminescence 
assays were conducted following the method described in the Methods section. As it has been 
explained in the introduction, the interaction of GLuc WT with CTZ results in a flash of blue light 
that rapidly decays over time, and this is recorded in the spectrometer as an initial peak in the 
light signal immediately after the injection of CTZ in the sample, followed by an exponential 
decrease of the signal. 
In the case of the arginine mutants, a much higher concentration of enzyme had to be used in 
order to be able to detect if they displayed any luminescence. The value of the light intensity of 
the peak in the spectra of GLuc R76A and GLuc R147A was compared to that of GLuc WT, after 
normalizing by enzyme concentration. As it is explained in the Methods section, this allowed to 
quantify the percentage of activity of these mutants in comparison to the WT. The values of light 
intensity of the peaks measured in these assays and the percentage of activity calculated are

2.1.1.1. Possible explanations for the extremely low activity observed for GLuc R76A and 
GLuc R147A  
Although it is indubitable that there is luciferase activity in the samples assayed in this project, 
the explanation for this remaining small amount of activity is not so clear. This section will 
discuss different hypothesis on this matter. 
 
Differences in catalytic efficiency 
First of all, one explanation for the little activity observed would be that there is another 
mechanism that is able to lead to the production of luminescence and that is totally independent 
of the arginine residues, so it works even when the arginines are not present in the arginine 
mutants. This hypothetical different route to produce light would be much less efficient to 
match the results from the last section. Another possibility could be that there is only one 
mechanism for the production of light in the reaction of GLuc with CTZ, and that this mechanism 
is highly but not totally dependent on the arginines, and it works to some extent even with the 
lack of the role performed by the arginines. A third slightly different explanation would be that 
there could be some functional replacement of the arginines, if some residues close to those 
positions in the three-dimension structure could perform the role of the arginine residue in the 
enzymatic reaction to some extent, leading to a much less efficient but still active enzyme. 
 
Differences in structure in comparison to GLuc WT  
Another reason that could explain the lack of activity in an enzyme is that it might not be folded 
in the correct conformation to display activity. So, it could be that the mutation of an arginine 
to an alanine in those specific positions lead to the incorrect folding of the enzyme and caused 
==PAGE:18==
15 
 
the large decrease in activity observed. Even if in this project it was not checked if the arginine 
mutants conserved the same structure as the WT, it is not very likely that a single mutation has 
such a big impact on the folding of the whole protein to cause the loss of the activity of the 
enzyme. In any case, this explanation alone does not match the results observed, as it would not 
be able to explain the residual amount of activity observed (unless it was the case that a small 
percentage of the proteins present were able to fold in the correct structure and maintain the 
normal activity exhibited by the wild type, which is not likely). 
Other explanation could be that the structure of the binding pocket in the mutants was different 
to the one in the wild type, resulting in a decreased affinity of the substrate for the enzyme. 
None of this hypothesis were investigated in this project, although in the last part of the results 
section of the arginine mutants it is analyzed if they are able to bind the substrate even if they 
are not able to produce almost any light. 
 
Possible presence of GLuc WT in a small proportion due to mistranslation 
In addition to this, considering the magnitude of the percentage of activity measured, a different 
explanation was contemplated. The hypothesis was that the activity observed in the samples 
could be attributed to the presence of traces of GLuc WT, not due to contamination, but due to 
the mistranslation of the mRNA encoding GLuc R76A and GLuc R147A. In other words, as a result 
of an error in the translation of the protein, which can occur although in a very low frequency, 
an arginine residue could be incorporated in the positions 76 or 147 of the mutants respectively, 
leading to the production of a small portion of proteins with the sequence of GLuc WT. 
Therefore, it was decided to search in the literature the error rate related to the translation 
process in bacteria, to check if it matched the results that had been obtained for the arginine 
mutants in our experiments. 
In E. coli, it has been calculated that the mutation rate associated to the replication of the 
genome is around 10-9 - 10-10 per nucleotide per genome replication; the error linked to the 
transcription is significantly higher, around 10-4- 10-5 per position; lastly, the missense error rate 
in translation has been estimated to be 10-3 - 10-4 per codon, so it is the one that accounts for 
the highest error rate (Mordret, 2019; Kramer, 2007). The errors that occur during the 
mechanism of translation are mainly caused by either the charging of a tRNA with an incorrect 
amino acid by the tRNA synthetase, or the mispairing of the codon in the mRNA sequence and 
the anticodon in the aminoacyl-tRNA by the ribosome, which leads to the incorporation of an 
incorrect amino acid (Mordret, 2019). 
As the frequency of the mistranslation found in the literature was in the same range as the 
percentage of activity found in our assays, it was decided to investigate further this hypothesis. 
To this end, a double arginine mutant, namely GLuc R76A-R147A, was generated. The probability 
that a mistranslation to the wild type amino acid occurs in both positions in the same protein 
leading to a protein GLuc wild type is so small that the signal would be impracticable to measure. 
In other words, if the hypothesis of the mistranslation was true, GLuc R76A-R147A would be 
expected to display no measurable activity. 
In addition, the double mutant would give valuable information regarding the other mentioned 
explanations for the residual activity of the arginine mutants. For example, in the case that the 
arginine mutants conserve a little bit of enzymatic activity by themselves although much lower 
than the wild type, the double mutant would allow to distinguish if the effect of the two arginine 
mutations is additive or not.  
==PAGE:19==
16 
 
 
Possible presence of GLuc WT in a small proportion due to contamination 
As a last explanation for the low activity observed, the hypothesis of the contamination of the 
samples with GLuc WT during the purification process can never be totally discarded, but it is 
very unlikely, as this was the second time that these results had been obtained. As the possible 
contamination with the wild type was a concern, the purification process was performed 
especially carefully and no GLuc WT was purified at the same time as the arginine mutants.

for the single arginine mutants. However, in this case the percentage of activity was calculated

Discussion on the GLuc R76A-R147A variant 
In connection to the previous section of this manuscript, the presence of activity in the double 
arginine mutant refutes the hypothesis of the misincorporation of the wild type residue in the 
mutated position due to the low frequency errors that occur in the translation process, which 
would lead to the presence of a small portion of GLuc WT proteins in the single arginine mutant 
samples responsible for the very low activity found in these samples. This translation error that 
has been described in the literature probably occurs in our samples but in a lower frequency, 
and it is not the main contributing factor for the residual activity in the single mutant samples.  
From the dicrease in activity observed in the single arginine mutants, it is clear that both 
arginines are essential for the activity of the enzyme. However, the observation of activity in the 
double arginine mutant refutes the hypothesis that the residual activity present in the single 
arginine mutants comes from a slight functional complementation of one arginine for the other, 
that is able to replace the lack of the other arginine by performing its role to a small extent. As 
the effect of the lack of the two arginines is not additive, because the activity observed for the 
double mutant is similar to that of the single mutants, it can be concluded that the arginines R76 
and R147 play an independent role in the contribution to the production of luminescence.  
The double arginine mutant does not provide an explanation for the residual activity observed 
in the single arginine mutants, so the question of what causes the production of this little 
amount of light in the samples, not only in the single arginine mutants but also in the double 
mutant sample, remains unknown. There was no more time to continue this line of investigation 
in this project, but some possible explanations about the role of the arginines 76 and 147 in GLuc 
will be presented in the further discussion section of the results.  
 
2.2. Modification of GLuc R76A and GLuc R147A by a derivative of the 
substrate 
The activity assays with the single arginine mutants were carried out at the beginning of the 
project, but the double arginine mutant was not purified and tested in the assays until the end 
of the project. The results that will be explained in this section were obtained in the meantime 
and only include the single arginine mutants.  
As mentioned in the introduction, an interesting finding described in a paper from a previous 
study at JRW laboratory (Dijkema et al., 2021) is that GLuc presents a slightly larger size after it

has reacted with its substrate CTZ, measured both by size exclusion chromatography and in SDS-
PAGE. In this paper, it was concluded that this increase in molecular weight is due to a covalent 
binding of GLuc to a derivative of the substrate during the reaction, and that it is the cause of 
the characteristic inactivation of GLuc.  
Based on this result, and in order to gain more insight into the role of the arginines 76 and 147 
in the function of GLuc, it was decided to analyze the molecular weight of the two arginine 
mutants after the incubation with the substrate in SDS-PAGE gels. Thus, the aim of this 
experiment was to determine if the single arginine mutants still interact with the substrate even 
if their ability to produce light is very much reduced. In this experiment, not only coelenterazine 
but also furimazine was tested as a substrate to study the difference in size upon incubation. 
Furimazine (Fz) is a substrate analogue to coelenterazine, which was developed simultaneously 
as the engineered luciferase NanoLuc (Hall et al., 2012). NanoLuc is more efficient in the 
production of light when it uses Fz as a substrate than with CTZ. However, in previous studies at 
JRW group, furimazine was tested as a substrate for GLuc WT and no luminescence was 
detected, at least in the conditions and with the parameters of emission wavelength used in 
these experiments. Interestingly, in previous analysis of shift in the bands in SDS-PAGE, it was 
observed that GLuc WT gets retarded with Fz as well as with CTZ. Thus, it was decided to include 
the incubation with this substrate as one of the conditions in the experiment with the single 
arginine mutants. 
The experiment was conducted with both of the single arginine mutants (GLuc R76A and GLuc 
R147A) and GLuc WT without SEP tag as a control. For each of them, four conditions were tested: 
a control sample taken from the protein stock to which nothing was added, a sample incubated 
with isopropanol (which is the solvent in the CTZ solution), a sample incubated with a 20-fold 
excess of CTZ, and a sample incubated with Furimazine, also in a 20-fold excess. A control for 
the solvent in the case of Furimazine (15% glycerol and 85% ethanol) was not included because 
of space limitations in the SDS-PAGE gel, but like isopropanol, these reagents are not expected 
to affect the mobility of the sample.  
The samples were prepared as described in Materials and Methods. Based on preliminary data, 
it was decided to analyze the samples at two time points, namely after one hour of incubation 
and after overnight incubation (approximately 20 hours), to be able to analyze the progress of 
the process. The results are shown in the figure .  
The samples of GLuc WT did not behave accordingly to what was observed in a previous 
experiment carried out in this project (shown in Appendix) and also reported in Dijkema et al. 
2021. The sample of GLuc WT incubated with CTZ was expected to present a larger size than the 
control samples, but conversely, the corresponding band is at the same level in the gel. This fact

this region of the gel to warn the reader to not be misled by the picture. The sample of GLuc WT 
incubated with Fz does present a larger size than the controls as expected.

2.2.2. Previous evidence that links the increase in size with the covalent binding of a CTZ 
derivative to GLuc 
In the study published by Dijkema et al. 2021, it was concluded that the increase in size observed 
for GLuc WT after the incubation with the substrate is probably caused by an irreversible 
covalent binding of a derivative of CTZ to GLuc during the enzymatic reaction. In this paper, it 
was checked that the shift persisted when running the samples in a reducing SDS-PAGE gel. This 
discards the hypothesis that GLuc could present a smaller size when it has not been incubated 
with CTZ due to the fact that it conserves its disulphide bonds and presents a more compact 
conformation, in comparison to the sample run under reducing conditions, in which the protein 
does not maintain the disulphide bonds and would run a smaller distance in the gel (as explained 
in the Methods section).

This had also been suggested before in the paper by Dijkema et al. 2021, where the increase in

molecules of derivatives of coelenterazine are bound to GLuc as, for example, the size of 
coelenteramide is 411 kDa.  
Moreover, it was found that the fluorescence spectra upon excitation at 333 nm of a sample of 
GLuc that had been incubated with CTZ was not the same as the spectra obtained from a sample

although it was more similar to the second one. As a control, it was also measured that a sample 
of GLuc that has not reacted with CTZ did not emit fluorescence at that excitation wavelength. 
This data suggests as well that GLuc is bound by some derivative of CTZ after the incubation with 
this substrate. 
 
2.2.3. Discussion on the increase in size of GLuc R76A and GLuc R147A  
In connection with the section above, it is reasonable to affirm that the shift in the bands of the 
samples of GLuc R76A and GLuc R147A is due to the covalent binding of a derivative of CTZ as in 
the case of GLuc WT.  
This results from this experiment are very interesting and unexpected as, even if the results 
obtained in the luminescence assays indicated that the single arginine mutants were almost 
inactive, they do interact with the substrate, as a change in their size can be detected by SDS-
PAGE after the incubation with CTZ and Fz, and they do this to the same extent as GLuc WT.  
An explanation for this could be that these mutants might be as active as the wild type to convert 
coelenterazine to its derivatives, but they have a lower quantum yield, so they produce less 
luminescence as a result of the reaction. 
This would also explain why the samples incubated with furimazine are shifted in the gel, even 
if it has been checked that GLuc does not produce luminescence with this substrate, at least in 
the conditions tested. As in the case of the arginine mutants, the explanation could be that GLuc 
does interact with furimazine and react with it, but this reaction does not lead to the efficient 
generation of photons. This will be further developed in the discussion. 
 
Further discussion on the arginine mutants 
Possible role of arginines 76 and 147.  
In this Master thesis it has been evidenced the importance of the arginines R76 and R147 for the 
production of luminescence by GLuc. In this section, a possible explanation for this will be 
discussed based on supporting information found in the literature. In a nutshell, the hypothesis 
==PAGE:25==
22 
 
is that the role of the arginines R76 and R147 might be to establish a cation-π interaction with 
the aromatic rings of coelenterazine and that this interaction could help to maintain the 
substrate molecule in a flat and rigid structure, so that the quantum yield of the reaction to 
convert CTZ to CTD is higher. 
It is a well-known fact that a positively charged cation, such as arginine in a solution with a pH 
below 12, and a negatively charged π-electron cloud, like the one found in extensive conjugated 
system in CTZ, can generate a cation-π interaction (Dougherty, 2013). The interaction of the 
arginine side-chain guanidinium and aromatic groups has also been described many times in the 
context of protein structure (Kumar et al., 2018). 
In the fields of study of luminescence and fluorescence, it has been determined that maintaining 
a molecule in a rigid state minimizes the rate of relaxation from the excited states by non-
radiative emission, such as intramolecular thermal motion, and favors radiative emission. (Mori, 
2020; Clark, 2011). Several studies report that the restriction of molecular vibration, for example 
by the intermolecular π-π stacking aggregation or by using a low temperature, enhances the 
production of luminescence (Cai et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2019). It has also been described that the 
formation of cation- π interactions can trigger luminescence in synthetic molecules containing a 
pyridinium cation and an aromatic ring and when they are positioned stacked forming 
aggregates (Zhong, 2018). 
Taking this information into account, it could be hypothesized that the key function of the 
arginines 76 and 147 in the production of luminescence by GLuc during the reaction with CTZ 
could be the establishment of a cation-π interaction with the substrate in the excited state, 
possibly locking it in a rigid conformation and favoring the relaxation through radiative emission, 
leading to a higher quantum yield of the reaction. Conversely, the lack of these arginines in the 
GLuc arginine mutants studied in this thesis, impedes the enzyme to keep the intermediate of 
the reaction in a less rigid state, and therefore in most of the cases this molecule will relax from 
its excited state through non-radiative emission and not by releasing a photon.  
The results obtained in this thesis about the arginine mutants have shown that the lack of one 
of the arginines reduces the luminescence production as much as the lack of both of them. In 
line with what was explained in the previous paragraph, maybe one of the arginines its not able 
to maintain the substrate in its rigid position on its own. Then, it could be further hypothesized 
that the two arginines are implied in locking the substrate in this rigid conformation in a 
collaborative manner, each of them binding from one of the sides of the planar structure of CTZ. 
Then, the lack of one of the arginines from one of the sides would impede to hold the substrate 
in this rigid conformation, leading to a great decrease in the quantum yield of the reaction. 
It can be expected that other residues in the protein sequence of GLuc probably contribute as 
well to the maintenance of the substrate in a rigid conformation, even though in the alanine 
scanning performed in previous work, the single arginine mutants GLuc R76A and GLuc R147A 
were the ones that presented the most drastic reduction in luminescence production in 
comparison to the wild type. Thus, this hypothesis also provides an explanation for the decrease 
in luminescence caused by the alanine mutation of some residues of GLuc close in the three-
dimensional structure to those arginines, as these amino acids might also form cation-π 
interaction or π-π interactions, in the case of aromatic residues, with the substrate.

2.3. Truncated variants of Gaussia luciferase 
In addition to the arginine mutants, other variants of GLuc were also studied in this project, 
namely the C-terminal truncated variants GLuc del. 1-151, GLuc del. 1-155 and GLuc del. 1-164, 
in an effort to gain more insight about this peculiar enzyme. These parts of the project are 
therefore not too much interconnected, and this second part is less extensive due to time 
limitations. 
The aim of this second part of the project was to determine if the C-terminal of GLuc is essential 
for the enzymatic activity, or alternatively, if it can be removed without reducing the activity as 
it is the case for the N-terminal. To study this, four variants of GLuc were designed with a 
truncation at different points in the amino acid sequence of GLuc. The variant GLuc del. 1-155 
lacks the last 13 amino acid of the GLuc sequence, among which a glutamine, a isoleucine and a 
glycine are highly conserved in other luciferase sequences with a quite high similarity. In the 
GLuc del. 1-151 the last 17 amino acids of the GLuc sequence are truncated, which removes four 
amino acids more in comparison to the other variant, including a conserved lysine and 
isoleucine. The variant GLuc del. 1-164 only lacks four amino acids that are not conserved, while 
including as a last aminoacid a glycine that is highly conserved. A last truncated variant was 
generated, in which the deletion was much longer than in the other cases, named GLuc del. 1-
135. 
 
2.3.1. Analysis of the activity of GLuc del. 1-151, GLuc del. 1-155 and GLuc del. 1-
164 
The activity of these truncated variants of GLuc was first tested in cell extracts obtained from 
the sonication of E. coli BL21 (DE3) that expressed these variants. In this preliminary experiment, 
the cell extracts from all of the variants displayed luciferase activity except for GLuc del. 1-135, 
even when using a higher volume of cell extract than for the other variants.  Thus, it was decided 
that it was not worth to investigate this variant further. 
The rest of the variants (GLuc del. 1-151, GLuc del. 1-155 and GLuc del. 1-164) were purified by 
affinity chromatography to be able to estimate their activity more precisely. As it was explained 
in the introduction, these variants did not contain the SEP tag and, accordingly, they were 
compared with GLuc WT without SEP tag. The results of the analysis of the luminescence assays 
carried out in the spectrometer for these truncated variants are shown in the table below.

Discussion on the GLuc truncated variants 
In view of the data shown in the table above, it can be concluded that truncation of the C-
terminal of GLuc diminishes the ability of the enzyme to produce light. It is interesting to see 
that the magnitude of this decrease varies depending on the position of the truncation point in 
the variants: The removal of only the last four amino acids in the sequence of GLuc already 
causes a 5-fold decrease in activity in comparison to GLuc WT, the elimination of the last 13 
amino acids causes a 100-fold decrease, and the deletion of the last 17 amino acids causes 
approximately a 1,000-fold drop in activity. 
In this case, an SDS-PAGE experiment to determine the size of the variants after the incubation 
with the substrate was not performed due to time limitations, so it cannot be concluded if the 
decrease in the light production can be attributed to a decrease in the enzymatic activity of the 
variants or a reduced ability to interact with the substrate. Another hypothesis that was not 
tested is if the truncation of the C-terminal hinders the correct folding in the majority of these 
GLuc variants and only a small portion of them display the structure appropriate to exhibit 
activity. This explanation could be plausible in the case of GLuc del. 1-151 (although not in the 
case of the others, specially for GLuc del. 1-164), given the fact that the truncation point is very 
close to one of the ten cysteines present in GLuc, so maybe the disulfide bond pattern in the 
truncated variants is different to the one in GLuc WT, leading to a different three-dimensional 
structure that is catalytically less active. 
As it has been explained in the introduction, GLuc has a very dynamic and difficult to determine 
structure. From preliminary NMR data that has been obtained at JRW group, there is not much 
information about the position of the C-terminal relative to the central region of GLuc, but it is 
predicted to be a flexible tail that sticks out of the main part of the protein, and it does not 
contain any secondary structure. Therefore, it is also not expected that the lack of this tail would 
affect the proper folding, so that the GLuc variants would present a very different structure in 
comparison to GLuc WT, although this could be checked in the future. And for the same reason, 
it was not expected that the removal of this tail could have a large impact on activity, and yet 
the results of this project have proved that it does. However, it is difficult to suggest what could 
be the role that the C-terminal could play in the contribution to the emission of light of GLuc 
after reacting with CTZ.   
The results obtained in this thesis about the truncated variants are in accordance with the results 
of the mutational screening of a previous study in the JRW group (Nordentoft, 2020), in which it 
==PAGE:28==
25 
 
was determined that the mutation of several residues in the C-terminal to alanine produced a 
decrease in activity. Specifically, the single mutation of Q156 or K163 to alanine produced a 
slight drop in luminescence production, but interestingly the single mutation of G167A produced 
a larger drop in light production, which could explain the results found for GLuc del. 1-164, in 
which this residue is missing. Although these experiments were not conducted with the purified 
enzymes, and therefore light production cannot be accurately related to activity of the GLuc 
mutants, those results also support the statement that the C-terminal is not irrelevant for the 
luminescence production by GLuc. 
It is also noteworthy to comment the 10-fold difference in activity of GLuc del. 1-151 (0,13% in 
comparison to the WT) and GLuc del. 1-155 (1,35%). The effect of the lack of four additional 
amino acids in the case of GLuc del. 1-151 could be explained by the importance of the 
conserved lysine and isoleucine in positions 154 and 155 of the GLuc sequence, or by the 
shortening of the protein and maybe the approaching of the truncated point to a cysteine and 
maybe interfering with the normal disulfide bond pattern. A way to test this could be to 
mutagenize the residues at positions 154 and 155 to alanine in GLuc del. 1-155 and to check if 
the activity is reduced to a similar level to that of GLuc del. 1-151. In the case of the K154, which 
is located close to the arginine 147, it could be hypothesized that it could have this small 
contribution to the production of light, as it has been shown in this thesis that the R147 has a 
great impact in the light production, and both are positively charged aminoacids. However, the 
study of Nordentoft discards this hypothesis, as the mutation of K154 to alanine was not found 
to have a negative impact on light production.  
 
3. MATERIALS AND METHODS 
3.1.Materials 
GLuc Plasmids 
Some of the plasmids used in this Master thesis had been designed by former students in JRW 
group (Nordentoft and Dijkema), while others were designed at the beginning of this project. 
The plasmids that were already available in the laboratory were those of GLuc WT with SEP tag 
(pMKN101), GLuc WT without SEP tag (pFMD01) and the single arginine mutants GLuc R76A 
(pFMD07) and GLuc R147A (pFMD10). 
The other plasmids were ordered to TWIST biosience. These were the double arginine mutant 
GLuc R76A-R147A (pMIE15), GLuc del. 1-135 (pMIE9), GLuc del. 1-151 (pMIE11), GLuc del. 1-155 
(pMIE13) and GLuc del. 1-164 (pMIE14).  
All of these plasmids were pET-29b+ plasmids that contain a kanamycin resistance gene. The 
appropriate version of the GLuc gene in each case was located under the control of a lac 
operator and the T7 promoter, and the lacI gene under control of the lacI promoter. This allows 
to induce the expression of the protein upon addition of IPTG to the culture media.  
Regarding the sequence of GLuc encoded in the plasmid, all of the variants of GLuc used in this 
thesis contained a polyhistidine tag (His tag) fused to the N-terminal of the protein to allow to 
purify the protein by affinity chromatography. In the case of GLuc WT with SEP tag, that was 
designed in a different project, the His tag was separated from the GLuc protein sequence by a 
Tobacco Etch Virus (TEV) protease cleavage site. In addition, as it has been explained in the 
==PAGE:29==
26 
 
introduction, GLuc WT with SEP tag and GLuc R76A-R147A included a solubility enhancing 
peptide (SEP) tag attached to the N-terminal of the protein sequence, separated from it by a 
TEVp site, that would allow to remove this tag from the protein if it was needed. This SEP tag 
consists of three repeats of the amino acid sequence “DDDG”.  
 
CyDisCo plasmid 
The cytoplasmic disulfide bond formation in E. coli (CyDisCo) plasmid was used in this project as 
a helper plasmid, to improve the yield of monomeric and soluble protein obtained in the 
expression of GLuc WT and the GLuc variants in E. coli BL21 (DE3). 
The CyDisCo plasmid used (pMSJ205) was provided by Lars Ellgaard group at the University of 
Copenhagen, who previously received it from Lloyd W. Ruddock (Gaciarz et al., 2016). It contains 
a chloramphenicol resistance gene and the codon optimized genes of the human protein 
disulphide isomerase (hPDI) and the sulfhydryl oxidase ERV1p from yeast mitochondria. 
 
Glycerol stocks of GLuc WT and GLuc arginine mutants 
GLuc WT with SEP tag, GLuc without SEP tag and the single arginine mutants (GLuc R76A and 
GLuc R147A) had already been transformed into E. coli BL21 (DE3) by former students in the 
JRW group. A sample from these cells had been stored as a glycerol stock at -80ºC.  
In this project, these glycerol stocks were streaked in LB-plates, and a single colony was picked 
to prepare a preculture which was expanded to a 1L expression culture the following day, to 
express the desired protein.  
 
E. coli BL21 (DE3) competent cells containing the CyDisCo plasmid 
In a first attempt to obtain E. coli BL21 (DE3) that contained both the CyDisCo plasmid and a 
GLuc variant plasmid, it was tried to transform the two plasmids simultaneously, as this had 
been recommended by the co-workers that provided the CyDisCo plasmid. After several trials, 
in which different concentrations of the plasmids and different miniprep samples were used, it 
was decided to use an alternative method to the co-transformation. Instead, the two plasmids 
were transformed in two subsequent steps, and the E. coli cells were made competent again in 
between, to be able to do the second transformation. These competent cells were prepared by 
suspending the E. coli cells in a CaCl2 solution. 
To this end, E. coli BL21 (DE3) were first transformed with the CyDisCo plasmid and glycerol 
stocks were stored at -80ºC. Then, these cells were transformed with one of the GLuc plasmids 
that were designed in this project: GLuc R76A-R147A, GLuc del. 1-135, GLuc del. 1-151, GLuc del. 
1-155 and GLuc del. 1-164. 
 
3.2.Methods 
Chemical transformation 
The five GLuc plasmids that were designed in this project were transformed into E. coli 
competent cells that already contained the CyDisCo pMSJ205 plasmid. The competent cells were 
==PAGE:30==
27 
 
thawed on ice for 30 minutes and 2uL of the GLuc plasmids were added to the samples. After a 
30 minutes incubation on ice, the cells were treated with a heat shock at 42ºC for 45 seconds 
and then left on ice for 5 minutes. Next, 200 uL of LB was added to the samples and they were 
incubated while shaking at 37ºC for 1 hour. LB plates were prepared containing Kanamycin (50 
ug/mL), Chloramphenicol (50 ug/mL) and glucose (0,2%). Finally, the cells were plated under a 
laminar flow bench and incubated ON at 37ºC. 
 
Protein expression in E.coli BL21 cells 
A preculture of E. coli BL21 (DE3) containing the desired GLuc variant or WT was prepared by 
picking one colony from the plates mentioned before and adding it to a tube of 10 mL of LB 
media containing Kanamycin (50 ug/mL), Chloramphenicol (50 ug/mL) and glucose (0,2%). The 
precultures were grown ON at at 37ºC. 
The expression culture was prepared by adding 10 mL of the preculture to 900 mL of LB-B media 
(which is LB with supplements of 2 mM MgCl2, 0.1 mM CaCl2, 3 µM FeCl3), 100 mL of A-media 
(which contains 20g (NH4)SO4, 150g Na2HPO4∙12H2O, 30g KH2PO4 and 30g NaCl in miliQ water), 
Kanamycin (50 ug/mL), Chloramphenicol (50 ug/mL) and glucose (0,2%). The culture was grown 
shaking at 37ºC for approximately 3 hours, until the OD600 had reached 0.6-0.7. The expression 
of the target protein was induced by adding IPTG and the culture was incubated at 30ºC shaking 
overnight. 
To obtain cell extracts from the cultures, the cultures were harvested at 8,000g and 4ºC for 15 
minutes. The cell pellet was resuspended in lysis buffer (Triton X-100, 150 NaCl, 50 mM Tris pH 
8.0, 10 mM Imidazole) and sonicated on ice using three sonication cycles of 8 times 30 seconds 
on and 30 seconds off. Next, 1% streptomycin was added to the sample to precipitate the DNA 
and then it was centrifuged 30 minutes at 15,000g and 4ºC. Before purifying the samples, it was 
checked that the expression and sonication steps had been successful by SDS-PAGE. 
 
Protein purification by IMAC 
As it has been explained previously, the GLuc proteins were designed with a His tag fused to the 
N-terminal of the protein sequence. Therefore, they can be purified from the cell extracts by 
immobilized metal affinity chromatography (IMAC), using a nickel nitrilotriacetic acid (Ni-NTA) 
resin. The purification is achieved by the retention of the target protein, due to the interaction 
of the imidazole rings present in the six histidines in the His tag with the nickel ions in the resin, 
while the majority of the rest of the proteins in the cell extracts elute without binding the 
column. A wash step was performed using 30 ml of buffer (150 NaCl, 50 mM Tris pH 8.0, 10 mM 
Imidazole) and then the target protein is eluted by adding a higher imidazole concentration 
buffer (150 NaCl, 50 mM Tris pH 8.0, 300 mM Imidazole) and collecting elution fractions of 1 ml. 
These fractions are analyzed in a SDS-PAGE gel under reducing conditions to determine which 
of them contain the majority of the target protein, and these fractions are mixed together.  
 
Differential ammonium sulphate precipitation 
To further purify GLuc in its monomeric form a differential ammonium sulphate precipitation 
step is carried out. This procedure is necessary to get rid of other proteins that are still be 
present in the sample after the affinity chromatography, such as the sulfhydryl oxidase ERV1p, 
==PAGE:31==
28 
 
and aggregates of GLuc that are not of interest for this study. First, ammonium sulphate (AMS) 
powder is added to the protein sample to a final AMS saturation of 50% and is equilibrated on 
ice and stirred with a magnet for 30 minutes. Subsequently, the sample is centrifuged at 14,000g 
and 4ºC for 15 minutes. The pellet is discarded, as the monomeric GLuc will not precipitate at 
50% AMS concentration, unlike others contaminants. Some more ammonium sulphate is added 
to a final saturation of 75% and let equilibrate as before. The centrifugation step is repeated, 
and this time the supernatant is discarded, as the monomeric GLuc will be present in the pellet.  
The pellet is then solubilized in 1 mL of dialysis buffer (50 mM Tris pH 8.0) and it is dialyzed 
against 2L of dialysis buffer for one hour two times. The final protein stock of each variant and 
GLuc WT is stored in the fridge at 4ºC. 
 
SDS-PAGE in non-reducing conditions 
The majority of the samples run on SDS-PAGE gels in this project were prepared by adding 
sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) but did not contain a reducing agent such as dithiothreitol (DTT).  
In an SDS-PAGE under reducing conditions, GLuc will be denatured and will not maintain its 
intramolecular nor intermolecular disulfide bonds, thus, all the GLuc present in the sample will 
be in a unique band at the apparent molecular weight of monomeric GLuc. On the contrary, in 
an SDS-PAGE in non-reducing conditions, the disulfide bonds are maintained, so the monomeric 
protein is slightly more compact and runs a bit further in the gel, and bands of aggregates of 
GLuc are observed at high molecular weights. 
The reason to use SDS-PAGE in non-reducing conditions is to be able to observe the real amount 
of monomeric GLuc that is present in the samples, which is the form of GLuc that is of interest 
for this study, and to distinguish it from the GLuc proteins that are forming aggregates.  
 
Luminescence assays with purified protein 
The samples measured in these luminescence assays were prepared by doing serial dilutions of 
the protein stock in order to obtain the desired concentration in the 2 ml volume of sample 
measured in the assay. The concentration of enzyme used in the samples of the GLuc variants 
was much higher than in the case of GLuc WT, in order to be able to detect the low luminescence 
that they displayed.  
The assays were carried out using a spectrometer to record the intensity of the luminescence 
emitted by the samples. The light intensity (measured in relative light units (RLUs)) was recorded 
at 480 nm, which is the wavelength of the emission peak of the light emitted by coelenterazine 
when it is converted to coelenteramide by a luciferase (Tannous et al., 2005).  
To conduct the assay, the measurement was started before injecting the CTZ to be able to record 
the peak of the signal. A small volume of CTZ from a concentrated stock (typically around 15-20) 
was injected into the sample to a final concentration of 5 uM in all the assays conducted in this 
project. The sample was stirred using a magnet to mix injected CTZ in the sample. The 
measurement finished after 180 s, when the light intensity signal had almost reached the 
baseline, as the interesting time points for these results were the first seconds of the reaction, 
when the high peak of light intensity is produced. 
Several samples of Gluc WT and the different GLuc variants were measured in order to have 
replicates from the assays (except in some cases, as explained in the Results section). The mean 
==PAGE:32==
29 
 
and standard deviation of the peak height of the spectra of these replicates was calculated for 
each of the mutants, and this value was compared to the one calculated for the WT, after 
normalizing by enzyme concentration. This normalization is valid, as it has been shown in 
previous studies at the JRW group (Dijkema et al. 2021) that the luminescence signal correlates 
linearly to the concentration of enzyme used in the assay.  
The mean and the standard deviation (SD) of the maximum light intensity values were calculated 
from non-independent replicates, as the measured samples were prepared from the same 
diluted stock of the main highly concentrated stock and measured on the same day, so in this 
experiment n=1. Thus, it is important to note that the standard deviation shown in the tables of 
the results section accounts mainly for variations in the pipetting while preparing the replicate 
samples to be measured in the assay (although not for variations while preparing the diluted 
stock from the concentrated stock) and for variations in the volume of CTZ injected.  
 
SDS-PAGE analysis of change in size after incubation of GLuc with substrate 
In this experiment, four samples of GLuc wild type without SEP tag (GLuc WT) and the two single 
arginine mutants (GLuc R76A and GLuc R147A) were prepared, containing a protein 
concentration of 8.33 uM. Among the four samples of each type, nothing was added to the first 
sample, so it just contained the protein in buffer 50 mM Tris pH 8 (Buf); the second was mixed 
with 4.55 uL of coelenterazine to a final concentration of 166 uM, which is a 20-fold excess (CTZ); 
then 4.15 uL of furimazine were added to the third one to a final concentration of 166 uM (Fz); 
the volume of isopropanol added to the forth one was the same as the volume of CTZ solution 
added to the samples incubated with CTZ (4.55 uL in a total volume of 125 uL). These samples 
were incubated at 4ºC and not exposed to light. An aliquot was taken from these samples after 
1 hour of incubation and after overnight incubation, mixed with SDS and boiled at 90ºC for 2 
minutes to be analysed by SDS-PAGE. It is not known if the reaction stops immediately after 
mixing with SDS, but the SDS-PAGE was run no more than one hour after taking the aliquot 
samples.  
 
 
 
 
Concluding remarks 
This Master Thesis contributes to the investigation of Gaussia luciferase through two 
independent studies. 
In the first part of this report, it was confirmed that the arginines in positions 76 and 147 are 
essential for the production of luminescence in GLuc WT, and an explanation of the role of these 
residues is suggested. In addition, the data presented indicates that the reaction of GLuc with 
coelenterazine can occur independently of the light production, as GLuc variants that displayed 
very reduced luminescence were found to react with substrate to the same extent as GLuc WT. 
In the second study, the relevance of the C-terminal of the protein was evidenced, as it was not 
possible to generate a truncated variant of GLuc that retained a similar activity to GLuc WT. It is 
interesting to note that even a variant with a small truncation presented a quite reduced 
==PAGE:33==
30 
 
luminescence production ability, and this decrease in activity was successively higher in the 
variants with longer truncations. 
It is very peculiar that in an enzyme that is used for so many applications, there is so little known 
about its basic characteristics of an enzyme. There has not been much research done to 
elucidate these aspects of GLuc in the past, and it is not being a straight forward and easy 
investigation in the present, but recent studies, including this thesis, are uncovering many 
unexpected and interesting information about this protein. This project helped to expand the 
current knowledge on Gaussia luciferase, that will have to be continued in the future to find an 
answer to the many important questions that are still left unresolved.
