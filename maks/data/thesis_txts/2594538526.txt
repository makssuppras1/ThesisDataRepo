Summary
In this project the two middlewares MobotWare and ROS have been compared. Mobot-
Ware was developed by the Technical University of Denmark and is only used there.
ROS on the other hand is open source and is used all over the world. It would there-
fore be interesting to look into the possibility of ROS taking over from MobotWare. In
order to do this comparison a small mobile robot had to complete a series of common
challenges on two different tracks.
MobotWare already had a series of commands that could be used to complete the
first tracks, where the robot had to follow lines on the floor and drive through a series
of gates. However ROS did not. Therefore a series of action servers have been made in
ROS, in order to implement the commands that were available in MobotWare. For the
other track the robot had to navigate using a map, read a series of guidemarks, find the
location of a box and detect a number of red and blue balls. Again MobotWare already
had several plug-ins that could handle this and were ready to use on the robot. ROS
had similar plug-ins for two of the challenges but they needed to be configured for the
robot in question.
Once the needed commands and functions were made available in ROS and the re-
spective maps were made, the two middlewares could be tested. In the tests MobotWare
scored higher points than ROS even though both were able to complete all the challenges.
MobotWare were generally faster and more robust than ROS, but ROS was not that
far behind. The conclusion of this should not be that MobotWare is better than ROS.
It should be remembered that the solutions made for MobotWare have been tested and
improved over the years, whereas the solutions made for ROS were made and tested
during this project.
It is impossible to give a definitive answer to which middleware that is the best. It
depends entirely on what the user wants to use it for. The features in MobotWare are,
as of now, more robust than the ones in ROS. However ROS have better documentation
and more flexible features. With further testing, the solution made for ROS could be
improved.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Mobile robots are being used more and more for a variety of tasks in different fields. Al-
though robots cannot replace human labour completely, they can replace simple, repet-
itive or dangerous tasks and thereby allowing humans to do the more complex tasks.
However robots are only as smart as they are made, so if they have to move around in a
certain area, they will need specific instructions of how to do that. Since a robot is just
some software that interprets inputs from sensors and controls some hardware, a soft-
ware platform or middleware is needed. Automation and Control at Technical University
of Denmark (DTU) have developed a middleware called MobotWare almost two decades
ago. MobotWare is being used by most of the robots in Automation and Control and
is most prominently used be the Small Mobile Robots (SMR) that are used in different
courses.
Over the years several other middelwares such as CLARAty, OROCOS, Player and
ROS have seen the day. Each have a different take on how to tackle the challenges of
controlling a robot, mobile or not. ROS in particular is being used by several robotics
companies in Denmark, whereas MobotWare is only used at DTU. Since ROS is open
source and have a large network of users that can provide examples and help each other,
it has become interesting to explore its possibilities within Automation and Control
further. However it would be a large task and a big risk to change everything from
MobotWare to ROS. So one might ask:
Will ROS be able to take over from MobotWare without compromising the
current stability and usability?
In order to compare the two middlewares a series of tasks will have to be performed
by a SMR. These tasks will cover several common challenges for mobile robots. For this
purpose two different tracks or obstacle courses will be used. Both of which are used
to teach autonomous robots at DTU. Since MobotWare is used as an educational tool
that is fairly easy to use, even for people with no programming skills, the ROS solution
should be similar easy to use and have the same basic functionality, without compromis-
ing the strengths of ROS. MobotWare can be used as it is, since it have a large number
of commands and custom made features. These commands and features only have to be
put in the right sequence in order to complete the tracks. These commands and features
will be adapted to and implemented with ROS. The software which makes it possible
to control the SMR using ROS is already available and is ready to use (see Appendix A
for more information). It is therefore only the commands and features, along with the

CHAPTER 2
Framework
The following chapter describes the SMR and the basic parts of MobotWare and ROS
that will necessary to know in order to properly understand how to control the SMR.
Lastly the challenges the SMR will have to perform will be explained.
2.1
Small Mobile Robot
The SMR (see Figure 2.1) is a differential drive robot, with two powered wheels in the
back and two castor wheels in the front. It has a laser scanner placed in the front with
a 180 degree field of view, five IR sensors (three in the front and one on each side), a
line sensor (reflectance sensor) under it and a Xbox 360 Kinect mounted on top of it.
Everything is controlled by an Intel NUC i3 with Linux Ubuntu.
Figure 2.1: Small Mobile Robot (SMR)
2.2
MobotWare
MobotWare was created at DTU about two decades ago. It consist of three main mod-
ules, a Robot Hardware Daemon (RHD), a Mobile Robot Controller (MRC) and the
==PAGE:16==
4
2 Framework
Automation Robot Servers (AURS). It has its own scripting language called Small Mo-
bile Robot Control Language (SMR-CL). These will be described in the following sections.
Figure 2.2 shows the framework for MobotWare[Bec+10]. The architecture of Mobot-
Ware is very modular which makes it easier to develop new features. The modules are
configured through XML-files and the components are connected through TCP/IP con-
nection. Furthermore new features are made as plug-ins, all of which are done to make
it simple to work with. [Bec+10].
Figure 2.2: Overview of the MobotWare framework
2.2.1
RHD
The Robot Hardware Daemon is the primary real-time control scheduler in MobotWare
and it runs on Linux. It handles the protocol interaction and has simple security func-
tions. The RHD consist of a several hardware driver plug-ins which manages hardware
I/O, pre- and post-processing and the database interface. It also consist of some core
components which are the basis for the variable database, the TCP/IP server and the
real-time scheduler [Bec+10].
2.2.2
MRC
The Mobile Robot Controller handles the low level control of the SMR and uses the RHD
as an interface to the robot hardware. Some of the features it handles are the odometry,
the motion controller, calibration, SMR-CL interpreter and the socket interface for the
sensor servers. It also handles the calibration of the odometry, line sensor and IR-sensors.
==PAGE:17==
2.2 MobotWare
5
The calibration of these are explained in Appendix A. The MRC also provides the basic
control for the SMR-CL commands (which will be explained below) [Bec+10].
2.2.3
AURS
Data processing of large data sets takes time and might not be possible in the real-
time setting, so this is instead done by the Automation Robot Servers. AURS normally
consists of a laser scanner server, a camera server and a mission management server. A
series of plug-ins have been made in order to process the data. Several of the plug-ins
uses the same sensor data (e.g. the laser scanner is for obstacle detection, localization
and to follow walls) [Bec+10].
2.2.4
SMR-CL
The Small Mobile Robot Control Language has a series of different commands that can
be used to control the SMR. Among these are:
• fwd drive a specified distance
• turn turn around centre
• turnr turn with a radius
• followline follow a black or white line on the floor
• followwall follow a wall at a specific distance
• drive drive continuous
• drivew drive using localization
• stop stops the program
Common for the first three commands is that the SMR will stop once it has driven/turned
the specified distance. For the next four a stop condition will have to be set. This can be
if the laser scanner reads a specific distance, the SMR is within a certain distance of its
target, it has crossed a black line or much more. SMR-CL also has access to the sensor
data, so it can be used to control/stop the SMR. Furthermore it can handle the plug-ins
from AURS for navigation, localization and vision [Bec+10]. Further explanation can
be found in [AC].

2.3
ROS
ROS resembles MobotWare in many ways. It was also developed around two decades
ago, it can have several plug-ins (although the appropriate name for it would be Nodes),
sensor data can be shared by several nodes and it also runs on Linux. However ROS is
not a real-time framework.
The underlying functions that makes it possible to call commands in SMR-CL are
programmed in C. These functions will have to be somewhat replicated in either python
or C++ in order to get the same commands for ROS. Furthermore the different sensor
data are handled quite differently in ROS but they are, in this case, still handled by the
RHD on the SMR. The following sections describe the parts of ROS that are necessary
to know in order to implement these functionalities. If the reader wishes to know more
about ROS please visit https://wiki.ros.org/ or see one of the books listed in the
Bibliography.
2.3.1
roscore
Before anything can be started in ROS, the roscore has to be started. roscore will start
the ROS Master, which provides names for all the nodes and tracks them, so they can
communicate to one another. roscore also starts the ROS Parameter Server where all
parameters are stored and a rosout logging node which logs logging messages such as
warnings. The roscore can either be started manually in a terminal or by roslaunch
which will start a launch file.
2.3.2
Launch Files
The easiest way to start several nodes is to use Launch files. In the launch files one have
to specify which node to start, where it is located and the name of the file containing
the code (either a .py or .cpp file). In addition to this it is also possible to start other
launch files and set up parameters used by the various launch files or nodes that are
being started. If there are any variables that the user should be able to set, it can be
done in the launch file and thereby avoiding that the user has to look through a lot of
code, that they might not have written themselves.
2.3.3
Topics and Messages
Nodes are, as mentioned, the ROS equivalent of plug-ins but they also the equivalent of
functions. One node might handle the laser scanner, one the odometry and one combines
the two and makes the robot move, based on the received data. The data passed between
nodes is done so with messages, which is basically a data structure consisting of integers,
floats, strings etc. Topics are used to publish and subscribe to messages.
==PAGE:19==
2.3 ROS
7
For instance one node can publish the odometry into a topic called /odom containing
the message type Odometry. Then the nodes that requires the data from the odometry
can subscribe to that topic and use whatever data from the message that is needed.
Figure 2.3 shows how the Odometry message looks. The Odometry message is part of
nav_msgs (navigation messages) and uses messages from geometry_msgs for the pose of
the robot and the velocity (twist). All messages have a .msg file with information about
that particular message.
Figure 2.3: Example of how a message can look
There are a large number of ”build-in” messages that are used for the majority of
cases, but if none of them should suffice, then it is possible to make custom messages.
2.3.4
Services
Although the publish/subscribe model, given by topics, is used in many cases, it is not
always appropriate to use, since it is one-way communication. An alternative is the
synchronous request/response model given by Services. One node will request another
node to do something and will wait for a response in return. Services also use messages
to communicate with each other. Services are often used for things that only needs to
happen once in a while or to set/get a variable. As with messages, services have a .srv
file with information about the service.

2.3.5
Actions
Services are not the best option when longer running tasks are needed. Since they only
give a reply when they are done, the user will have no information about how far it
has come. Instead Actions can be used as they send feedback with updates and can be
cancelled should the situation call for it. The Action needs a goal (similar to the request
in Services) and will return a result when it is done. The goal, result and feedback are
specified in a .action file. Actions are normally used for long running operations such as
driving to a specific point. Furthermore Actions does allow other tasks to be performed
while it runs. Figure 2.4 shows the interface for actions1. The client sets the goal and
the server does whatever it is programmed to do with that goal.
Figure 2.4: Action interface
2.3.6
rviz
ROS has a visualization tool called rviz (ROS visualization). It can be used to visualize
the laser scanner, the map, planned trajectories, the robot itself and much more. This
is done by rviz subscribing to the different topics and thereby allows 2D and 3D data to
be shown so humans can understand it. The input from the camera can also be shown,
so the user can see what the robot sees. The different things that should be visualized
can be added ”on the fly” or saved as a configuration so the same setup can be used
again later.

2.4
Tracks
As mentioned in Chapter 1 the SMR will have to perform a series of tasks in order to
compare MobotWare and ROS. Since the SMRs and MobotWare are being used to teach
the different aspects of autonomous mobile robots at DTU, two tracks with different
challenges are available. One is used for the course 31385 Autonomous Robot Systems and
the other for its successor 31388 Advanced Autonomous Robots. The different challenges
for these two tracks will be explained below. In addition to the challenges directly given
by the tracks, another common challenge should also be addressed, namely recovery.
More precise what should the SMR do if it cannot reach its goal, looses the line it is
following or cannot complete its current task.
2.4.1
Track 385
Track 385 is used for the course 31385 and is shown on Figure 2.5. The main goal is
to follow black and white lines on the floor in order to drive through as many gates as
possible and end up in the goal box. However the track can be broken down into six
challenges as described below. Although the order in which the challenges are completed
is unimportant the order below is suggested as it is the most straight forward.
1. The x-distance from start to the box is measured
2. Push box away and drive through gates
3. Find the ”gate on the loose” and drive through it
4. Follow the wall and drive through gates on each end of the wall
5. Follow the white line and drive through the two gates
6. Open door and drive into goal box
Completing the track should take under three minutes. Note that the floor is neither
white nor black but light oak. Furthermore the blue lines are walls, the green line marks
the area where the gate (3) can be and the yellow lines are gates. The brown boxes are
cardboard boxes.

2.4.2
Track 388
The main challenge of track 388, which is used for the course 31388, is to navigate
correctly within a map, while completing four different tasks as described below. In
contrary to track 385 there is no straight forward order to complete the challenges.
However one order might be more time consuming than others.
1. Drive from guidemark to guidemark until number 98 is found
2. Detect the number of red and blue balls
3. Find x- and y-coordinates of the box
4. Drive “home”
The track is shown on Figure 2.6 where the blue lines are wall, the guidemarks are shown
as green lines on the blue walls and the red stars represent the balls. The green box
with the blue circle is the start position and ”home” of the robot. The location of the
guidemarks and the balls are known in advance and the box can only be within the
dashed green lines. The black numbers next to the guidemarks are the numbers of them,
but not the number that the robot will read at that position. At guidemark 2 the robot
might read ”7” and should then drive to guidemark 7 and read a new number. The area
with guidemark 13 and 14 is called the maze.

CHAPTER 3
How to Reach the
Comparison
As mentioned before the SMR should perform a series of tasks using MobotWare and
ROS. MobotWare already has working commands in SMR-CL for most of the given
challenges and these will not be improved. However the ball finder plug-in is deliberately
not as good as it could be, since it is up to the students taking the course 31388 to
improve it.
Therefore it will have to be improved.
Chapter 4 will analyse how the
different subsystems could be made.
This includes both how the different SMR-CL
commands could be implemented in python to be used in ROS and how to solve the

4.2.1
Image Analysis for Ball Detection
The MobotWare plug-in that can find the balls uses OpenCV (Open Source Computer
Vision). Since OpenCV works for both Python and C++ along on Linux, Windows and
MacOS systems, it is ideal for the image analysis. There are several options as how to
find the balls. The approach used in the plug-in made for MobotWare uses Canny edge
detection on a channel in a HSV image (the channel is specified by the user). After this
it uses Hough Circle transform to find circles, checks the colour of the found circles and
counts them. This approach is definitely fine but it could use some improvements, since
initial testing showed that it found a large number of false positives.
One improvement is to make a binary image, either by filtering out all other colours
than red and blue (colour of the balls) on the HSV image or by applying a threshold to
one of the channels from the HSV image. After this either Canny Edge Detection and
Hough Circles can be used or the build-in OpenCV function FindContours(). However
this function will find all the contours in a binary image and not just circles.
In order to determine the colour of the ball, the centre is found and if the colour of
the centre pixel is within a specified threshold, the ball is assumed to be that colour.
The centre can be found in several ways as well. If Hough Circles are used, one simply
has to check the centre of the found circle (the Hough Circle Transform returns the
coordinate of the centre of the circle along with the radius). If FindCountours() is used
it is recommended to use moments() instead. FindContours() returns an array of points
describing the contour which moments() can use to find the approximate centre.
Testing showed that all four approaches works and are able to find the balls. Fig-
ure 4.1 shows the four approaches used on the same image. The combination of thresh-
olding the saturation channel and using Findcontours() is not a good combination as
it can be seen on Figure 4.1a. The reason for this is that the two ball are connected
on the binary image. Findcontours() cannot separate them and sees them as one. This
is not a problem with Hough Circles, since it finds both circles even though they are
connected. The other three combinations can find all four balls correctly without any
false positives.

4.2.2
Centre of Box
The coordinates for the centre of the box can be found in several different ways. Either
by using the laser scanner and measuring the distance from the SMR to the box in the
x-direction and the y-direction from two different points and simply adding the ”radius”
of the box along with the respective coordinate of the robots position when the measure-

The approach is almost the same except that three distances are measured (Chapter 5
explains the theory behind).
Yet another way could be to use computer vision. This approach has not been tested
but it should be possible to find the box in an image (the corner can be found using
Harris Corner Detection). Two images would be needed and if the same 2D point can
be identified in each both (feature matching), the point can be found in 3D using point
triangulation. This requires knowledge of the camera parameters and the position of the
SMR in order to work. However since the box does not have that many features and a
corner can be found many places, it is expected that it would not be as precise as using
the laser scanner.

4.3
Structure of Code
The code developed for ROS can be structured in two different ways. The most straight
forward is to simply have a file with functions and use flags to decide when to do what.
Another option is to use state machines. In ROS state machines are made using the
smach-package. smach is normally used when the robot has to do a set of well defined
tasks where each task have one or more outcomes. It can also be used to drive between
different points and is pretty straight forward to implement. However the transitions
from one state to another have to be hard-coded (i.e. which sequence to drive between
guidemarks) and since this is not known, smach is not suited for this specific case. It
could be used for track 385 but since each task only has one outcome it will only make
the code more complex. Furthermore, since each transition needs to be hard-coded, none
of the states could be used more than once. If the SMR had to repeat the exact same
task over and over, smach could have been used. In order to implement the different
recovery methods, it would be necessary to go back and forward between two states, e.g.
current state and fail state, since the fail state could be used multiple times but this is
not possible. It would therefore be necessary to have several fails states and then the
simplicity is gone.

5.2
Localization and Navigation
The navigation can be separated into three parts. A map of the area to navigate in, a
localizer so the robot knows where it is in the map and a planner that tells the robot
where to go and which route it should take.
5.2.1
Mapping in MobotWare
The map used by MobotWare is handled by the Mapbase plug-in and has to be made
manually. Each wall/obstacle are defined by a starting point and an end point given
by x- and y-coordinates in either the file that starts the laser server or in the SMR-CL
script that is to be run.
5.2.2
Localization in MobotWare
The plug-in aulocalize uses Kalman localization to localize within the map. That is, it
matches detected features with features in the given map and based on this gives an
estimation of its position. Every time the command localize is called a transformation is
returned which, along with the odometry, can be used to calculate the correct position
in world coordinates. The position in world coordinates is used by the path planner.
The plug-in first predicts the robots position based on odometry and the previous
estimation. Then it extract lines from the laser scanner with RANSAC (Random Sample
Consensus) and finds the best lines. using LSQ (least-squares estimation) with Cartesian
coordinates. Afterwards it predicts the expected lines based on the map and the robots
predicted position and match the extracted and predicted lines. Lastly the position
estimation can be computed by a combination of the robots position based on position
prediction and the observations. The target position in odometry coordinates is then
found so the robot can drive to that position [SNS11].
5.2.3
Navigation in MobotWare
The path planning is done by the plug-in auplan. It uses Graphplan to make a graph
and Dijkstra’s algorithm to search for a route through that graph. The plug-in needs a
series of points scattered around the map, along with a definition of which points that
are connected. Based on this it calculates the cost of going from one point to another.
If there is more than one route, the one with the lowest cost will be selected [SNS11]. If
a point is not connected to any other points it can not be reached and if a part of the
map does not have any points it can not be reached either.
The combination of the two plug-ins (auplan and aulocalize) and the command drivew
will make the robot navigate. The drivew command need a x- and y-coordinate and an
angle all in world coordinates (the same as map coordinates). It will then transform the
coordinates using the transformation from the localizer plug-in.

5.2.5
Localization in ROS
The localization in ROS is handled with a set of probabilistic algorithms called Adaptive
Monte Carlo Localization (amcl) which is described in the book Probabilistic Robotics
by Sebsastian Thrun, Wolfram Burgard, and Dieter Fox [TBF06]. amcl looks at the
laser scanner, the odometry, the map and the initial pose of the robot. Based on this
it calculates an estimate of where the robot might be. This estimate have a probability
attached and the higher the probability the more likely it is to be correct. As the robot
moves around the laser readings are compared to what would be expected from the map
and the estimated position and its associated probability is updated accordingly.
As mentioned before a series of algorithms are used to perform the localization. These
are all explained in [TBF06] and will only be explained briefly here. One algorithm is
Sample odometry model which uses the odometry to calculate the robots motion over
time. The difference from the initial position to the final position is modelled as a rota-
tion, a translation and a final rotation. This, along with four error parameters (α1, α2, α3
and α4) gives the position of the robot. These error parameters represent the expected
noise in the odometrys rotation/translation estimate from the rotational/translation of
the robots motion. There is a bug in the implementation of the odometry model called
diff in amcl which implements the Sample odometry model. Due to this bug a now model
called diff-corrected have been made to avoid that old systems stop working and this
==PAGE:39==
5.2 Localization and Navigation
27
model should be used instead. However when this model is used the default values (0.2)
for the four alphas does not fit. Instead much lower values (around 0.005) are needed.
To model the laser two different models can be used; Beam model and Likelihood
field model. The Beam model is an approximate physical model of a range finder (laser
scanner). The range is measured along a beam, therefore the name. It uses four types of
measurement errors, ”small measurement noise, errors due to unexpected objects, errors
due to failures to detect objects, and random unexplained noise”[TBF06] to make the
model. These four types of error are noted as phit, pshort, pmax and prand. It can however
be quite computational expensive and is prone to errors when there are many small
objects.
The Likelihood field model does not have those limitations. It is not based on a physical
explanation, but it works well in practice. The algorithm maps the end point of each
sensor readings into the global coordinate system but only if an obstacle is detected. In
other words it calculates the measurement probability by finding the likelihood of an
obstacle detection. It too uses phit, pmax and prand to model measurement noise, failures
and random measurements. To do this, it uses the parameters z_hit and z_rand which
is a weight for how much to trust the laser and how much to trust random readings
respectively. Setting z_hit to 0.9 and z_rand to 0.1 gave good results.
For the actual localization an adaptive algorithm called Augmented_MCL is used. It
uses the Sample odometry model to sample new poses from old particles and one of the
laser models to set a weight for that pose. Based on this, an estimate of the position is
found. To make the algorithm more robust a set of random particles are added to the
particle sets. This reduces the chance of mistaking sensor noise for a bad localization
result. The parameters used to configure the amcl node can be found in Appendix B.
5.2.6
Navigation in ROS
In ROS the path planning and navigation is done by the move_base package, that makes
a global plan and a local plan. It uses information from the map, the localization system,
sensors and the odometry to make a global plan and local plan [Qui16].
5.2.6.1
Move Base
The global plan is the shortest route from the current position to the goal. The local
plan tries to follow the global plan but it can make changes if an unknown obstacle

5.2.6.2
Recovery
There are two different types of recovery, clear costmap recovery and rotate recovery.
Clear costmap recovery resets the local costmap back to the static (original) map within
a specified radius to remove any obstacles that might have disappeared. rotate recovery
also tries to clear the costmap but does so by rotating 360 degrees [Jos15].
5.2.6.3
Global Costmap
The global costmap looks like the original map but contains information about how
good/bad it is for the robot to be in a certain place. Walls are bad, free space is good.
There are too many parameters to explain here, but the inflation radius and cost scaling
factor are the most important. They define how close to an obstacle the robot can get.
All the obstacles in the map are inflated to the size of the inflation radius and the cost
of moving into a certain area of the map is then calculated with (5.2). The closer to an
obstacle the robot is, the higher the cost will be. The inflation radius should be so large
that the robot only have a small free path in the narrow spaces (e.g in the maze). This
will keep it in the middle of the free space and thereby avoiding getting too close to the
walls.
cost(x) = exp(−1.0 · cost_scaling_factor · (x −inscribed_radius)) · (254 −1) (5.2)
where cost scaling factor is set by the user, the inscribed radius is the size of the robot, x
is the distance to the nearest object in the map (that is further way than the inscribed
radius and closer than the inflation radius away from an obstacle). The number 254 is
a variable called INSCRIBED_INFLATED_OBSTACLE which cannot be changed.
5.2.6.4
Local Costmap
The local costmap is quite similar to the global but where the global costmap is static
the local is not. It uses a rolling window that is updated as the robot moves around, so

scaling factor are quite important. For the local costmap the inflation radius should be
smaller than for the global costmap. Otherwise the robot will not be able to turn around
in narrow spaces, since the local planner will think that it is in risk of hitting a wall.

5.2.6.5
Global Plan
As mentioned above the global planner will compute a trajectory from the current posi-
tion of the robot to the goal, using the global costmap to avoid obstacles. The default
global planner is navfn which uses Dijkstra’s algorithm which was explained earlier. The
planner uses the cost calculated in (5.2) in order to find the best path. There are not
that many parameters for the global planner, which means that it relies a lot on the
global costmap.
5.2.6.6
Local Plan
Where the global planner finds the optimal route for the robot, the local planner will
find the optimal way of driving that route. While the global plan stays the same, the
==PAGE:43==
5.2 Localization and Navigation
31
local plan can change several times as the robots moves. The local plan is handled by
dwa local planner (Dynamic Window Approach). It was developed by Sebsastian Thrun,
Wolfram Burgard, and Dieter Fox in 1997 and takes the robots dynamics and inertia
into account when making a new path. It samples the robots velocity and performs
forward simulation to predict what would happen if the sampled velocity was applied
for a short amount of time, while only allowing trajectories where the robot will be able
to stop before hitting an obstacle. Then it evaluates each trajectory and chooses the
highest-scoring and sends the associated velocity to the robot [FBT97]. (5.3) shows the
cost function used to evaluate the trajectories.
cost = path_distance_bias · α + goal_distance_bias · β + occdist_scale · γ
(5.3)
where α is the distance to the path from the endpoint of the (simulated) trajectory, β
is the distance to the local goal from the endpoint of the (simulated) trajectory and γ
is the maximum obstacle cost along the (simulated) trajectory2. path_distance_bias,
goal_distance_bias and occdist_scale are parameters that the user can set.
path_distance_bias specifies how close to the given path the controller should stay. It
have been set to the default value (32.0). goal_distance_bias specifies how close to the
goal the controller should reach. It have been set to 20.0 (instead of the default 24.0).
Lastly occdist_scale specifies how much the controller should avoid obstacles. In this
specific environment it is quite important, since it defines how close to the end of wall
the robot can go. Changing it from the default 0.01 to 0.1 improved the performance
significantly.

plan (see Figure 5.5 for the full error message). This error originates in the function

planner. The cause for it seems to be a transformation with the global plan where the
time does not always fit. When this error happens the estimated position of the SMR
will jump even though it has not moved that much. As of now there are no solution as
to how to avoid this, since the problem is in the source code for the local planner.

5.6
Recovery
In addition to the other challenges, the SMR should also be able to recover from a fault
should one happen. A fault could be that it cannot read a guidemark, cannot detect
any balls, looses a line or cannot reach its goal.
Guidemarks
If the SMR cannot read a guidemark in MobotWare it will back up and
read again, turn to one side, read again and then turn to the other side and read again.
This is repeated twice and if there is still no luck it will proceed to the next challenge.
The QR-reader for ROS have a very wide range and is capable of reading QR-codes that
are not directly in front of it. Therefore it has not been necessary to implement this
approach in ROS.
Balls
It is assumed that there will be at least one ball of each colour, so if only one
colour of balls are detected the SMR will drive a little forward and try again. If there
are still not enough balls, the challenge will be skipped. This is implemented in both
MobotWare and ROS. Another option could be to change the parameters used for the
image analysis, but this would require knowledge about why the SMR could not detect
any balls.
Path planning
If the SMR cannot reach its goal or gets stuck while localizing in ROS,
the parameters can be changed using Dynamic reconfigure. If the planner fails it will

Follow line
It can be checked if the SMR looses the line by checking the input from

drive 15 cm backwards and try to follow the line again. Usually the line is lost because
the SMR is crooked on top of the line and by stopping as soon as the line is lost it should
be sufficient to just drive a little backwards, so that the line sensor will be on top of the
line again. Another approach, which have neither been implemented nor tested, is to
log the odometry and drive to a specific ”check point” if the line is lost (or if any of the
other challenges fails). Once the ”check point” is reached the SMR should continue the
challenge from there at a lower speed. This has not been implemented partly due to the
complexity and partly due to the fact that is was sufficient to drive a little backwards
when the line is lost. Furthermore it would still be necessary to check if the line is lost.
Due to the layout of track 385 a specific route to each ”check point” would have to be
followed, to ensure that the SMR would not hit any of the other challenges.

6.3
Comparison
The comparison of the two middlewares should be done partly based on the conducted
tests and partly based on their functionalities.
Test
Both MobotWare and ROS were able to complete all the challenges from the two
tracks although MobotWare was generally faster and more robust than ROS. Especially
the robustness of the line follower in ROS should and can be improved.
Documentation
The documentation of the different functionalities and plug-ins in
MobotWare are pretty poor. There is no document which explains all of it, although
[AC] explains everything needed to complete track 385. There is also a website1 but it
is not complete and explanations to some of the plug-ins are missing. ROS on the other
hand have a big community which updates the website2 with examples and there are
several books and papers available.
Installation
Once MobotWare is installed all of the plug-ins and functionalities are
available whereas ROS will need several packages to be installed afterwards if they are

2http://wiki.ros.org/

needed. Among these are the gmapping, map server and teleop twist keyboard which
are used to make the map and move base, amcl and dwa local planner which are used
for the planning and navigation. The installation of packages only requires an internet
connection and can be done with the command sudo apt install ros-distribution-package*
where distribution should be the name of the used ROS distribution and package the
name of the package to be instatted..
Maps
A big difference between MobotWare and ROS is the way the maps are made.
In MobotWare the coordinates for the start and end of each line in the map has to be
defined. This require a lot of measurements but a line can easily be removed if the
scenery changes. It is however not suited for environments with a lot of odd shaped
things, since it would be difficult to map properly. ROS on the other hand uses the
laser scanner to map an area. The shape of things does not matter and the map can be
altered in an image editor (by removing or adding lines) if needed.
Localization and Navigation
The localization and navigation in MobotWare does
only require a series of points to navigate between to be specified. Once this is done the
SMR can navigate. This is in contrast to ROS where a lot of different parameters can
be set (see Appendix B for parameters). However once suitable parameters for the SMR
are found they do not need to be changed. Some of the parameters for the costmaps and
local plan depends on the environment and should be changed when the environment
changes.
Calibration
It is (as of now) easier to calibrate the IR sensor, line sensor and odometry
in MobotWare since a plug-in with a menu have been made specifically for this. The
calibration of the line sensor is done within the action server in ROS as long as a file
with the expected values for black and white are provided. The calibration of the IR
sensor and odometry will either have to be made with MobotWare or a suited math
program. The files containing the result of the calibration can then be read by ROS.
Logging
In MobotWare logging of data can done simply by adding log ”$variable” to
the program. This saves the data to a text file which can then be plotted using a suited
program. ROS have a similar option. By writing rostopic echo /topic »data.txt in a
terminal the data from the topic will be saved in the text file. There are however several
other options depending on what the user needs. One is rosbag which can be used either
from the code or the terminal and will record the specified topic. The data can be played
back to the robot so it will do exactly as the file tells it, or it can be displayed using for
example rqt_bag.
Debugging
Another important feature to consider when choosing a middleware is
the options for debugging. In MobotWare this can be done by using the simulator (and
thereby eliminating noise) and by using qclient or auclient both of which can monitor
and visualize data from the laser, map and camera. ROS have similar options with
==PAGE:57==
6.3 Comparison
45
Gazebo for the simulation and rviz for the visualization. MobotWare is more static
in the visualization (in auclient the view cannot be changed and qclient only have the
option to zoom/pan) and simulation (view cannot be changed). Gazebo and rviz on the
other hand offers the possibility of zoom/pan/tilt the view and thereby allowing for a
better understanding of what is happening. ROS also have a series of plug-ins called
rgt_name where name changes depending on the plug-in. Common for all of them is that
they can visualize different parts of ROS (actions, messages etc.) or change different
parameters. Finally there are log messages which will display information of varying
severity such as warnings and errors.
Setup
Lastly, even though it requires the same in both MobotWare and ROS to get
the SMR for instance to follow a line, ROS requires a bit more setup in the main file.
This setup will be the same every time, so by having a template were the different actions
are set up should make it as easy to use as a SMR-CL script in MobotWare.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
It is impossible to give a single answer to the question ”Can ROS take over from Mobot-
Ware”. Both middlewares are capable of completing the same challenges even though
they do it differently. Provided that the entirety of MobotWare and ROS are available
along with the smr_control_node.cpp (or a similar node for the robot in question)
then it depends on how the system is to be used and what features the user wants. If
the basics of autonomous robots it to be taught then MobotWare is the way to go. It
is easy to use and does not require much setup, so everybody can use it after a short
presentation. If time is an issue, then MobotWare also seems to be the way to go.
However if the user wants to get a better understanding of how the different func-
tions work, e.g. how the line follower works, then ROS is better. Each functionality is
kept in its own file to make it easier to find and change. In MobotWare the different
functionalities are scattered in several files. This makes it difficult to get a good overview
of how the different parts work and interact. In addition to this, ROS is used all over
the world, so if the goal is to prepare the students for something that they might end up
working with then one should go with ROS. Another thing to consider it that, once the
people who developed MobotWare leaves DTU, a lot of knowledge is bound to be lost.
Lastly, ROS have more options for logging and debugging along with a more interac-
tive simulation and visualization tool. If these features are important then ROS is the
better choice.
7.1
Future Work
There are several things that could or should be added to make ROS as robust and user
friendly as MobotWare. Most importantly the line follower should be made more robust,
so it does not loose either the black or white lines.
Although the parameters used for the localization and navigation does give satisfying
results they could be better, so that the SMR can get both in and out of the maze.
Furthermore if ROS was to take completely over from MobotWare a node that can
calibrate the odometry, IR sensor and line sensor would be necessary.
A feature that could be nice to have in both middlewares is a debug mode for the
ball finder plug-in. In this debug mode a trackbar should be added in order to find the
best possible parameters for the thresholding that makes the binary image. Since the

have for future tracks.

Reset motor
If the SMR drives into an obstacle the motor drivers will shut down so
they do not burn out. If this happens they will need to be reset otherwise the motors
will not run. MobotWare resets the motors every time a new program is run, but ROS
does not. Therefore a service which resets the motors have been implemented. The
service can either be added in the beginning of a node or be run by itself by running
reset_motor_service.py.
