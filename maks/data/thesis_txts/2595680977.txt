Abstract
The aim of this master thesis was to investigate the possibilities and the limitations of an
underﬂoor heating and cooling system combined with a heat pump system in a low energy
single-family house which is located in Sisimiut, Greenland. A simulation case study was
conducted to investigate how the desirable thermal comfort can be achieved and how the
potential overheating could be eliminated in summer. Moreover, energy consumption and
technical solution were examined. Three basic scenarios for heating were studied in order
to ﬁnd the optimal solution considering energy consumption and thermal comfort. The
ﬁrst scenario was a stand-alone air to water the heat pump with heating capacity 15kW.
Secondly, a lower capacity heat pump, 7kW combined with a top up heater, an electric
boiler, as a supplementary system. The last case was a 7kW heat pump combined with
electric radiators. This system has an extra boiler for providing DHW. Regarding cooling
system, two cases were examined in order to encounter the overheating which may occur
in summer. The ﬁrst one was a reversible heat pump system and the second one was a
dry cooler system which would provide free cooling to the house. Both heat pump and
dry cooler are connected with the ﬂoor heating and cooling system.
The simulations demonstrated that the ambient temperature plays a crucial role in the
operation of the heat pump. The heating capacity that the air source should have, in or-
der to overcome the operating problems that low ambient temperature creates, is at least
15kW in order to be able to operate without any other supplementary system. Hence a
15kW HP can be used as an independent system with annual consumed energy 4604.7
kWh. The 7kW HP with the electric boiler proved a pretty eﬀective system combination
regarding both thermal comfort and energy consumption. The combination of the 7kW
HP and electric radiators achieved to keep the operative temperature at the desirable lev-
els with annual energy consumption 5525.7 kWh. Concerning cooling, both systems had
similar results as they manage to reduce the overheating at the acceptable levels.
In general, the use of an air source heat pump in such low outdoor temperatures, like
Greenland can signiﬁcantly aﬀect the eﬀectiveness of the heat pump, as the energy con-
sumption is increased.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The building technology has made a great progress the last few decades and the building
regulations are updated every few years. European countries have set building codes which
follow the model of energy eﬃcient and airtight residential buildings in order to limit the
energy consumption for heating and cooling. High insulation and balanced mechanical
ventilation are the main characteristics of such dwellings. The last few years Greenland
adopted this route and aims to implement at new dwelling constructions or to improve
the already existed. Greenland, due to harsh weather conditions, has a great need of such
improvement. Nowadays, the most signiﬁcant problem of Greenlandic dwellings is the high
energy use for heating. The detached and semi-detached houses have an average energy
consumption only for heating about 416 kWh/m2, while Canadian houses, which have
similar weather conditions, consume 213kWh/m2 [1]. That shows that current houses are
not well insulated and there is possibility of residential building upgrades.
By increasing the insulation and the air tightness, the heating demand is decreased. How-
ever, the air tightness may create problems of overheating during summer. In this case a
cooling system is needed. A potential solution to deal with low temperatures in winter and
high in summer could be ﬂoor heating and cooling, as the same installation can be used
for both cases. Floor heating and cooling systems are generally known as suﬃcient tech-
niques, as they can provide uniform temperature distribution and hence, thermal comfort
environment. Moreover, underﬂoor systems can be combined with several energy sources
even with renewable sources. In this case, an air source heat pump will supply the water
to the ﬂoor heating and cooling system that is required.
This project investigates and compares diﬀerent heating and cooling system techniques
between air source heat pump and underﬂoor heating and cooling systems. The simula-
tions are based on an actual single-family house which is located in Sisimiut. Moreover,
the possibility of a cooling system will be required due to high solar loads and internal
gains during summer is investigated.

2.2
Underﬂoor heating and cooling systems
The underﬂoor heating and cooling system is a distinctive approach of central heating and
cooling techniques. It can provide decent thermal comfort and indoor climate control, in
combination with reliability, low maintenance and energy eﬃciency.
Figure 2.4: Vertical distribution of temperature [7]
The thermal distribution of the conditioned air is quite smooth and the temperature can
be kept constant up to two meters from the ﬂoor. The desirable temperature at the eye
level is about 20oC and the feet need a temperature approximately 4oC higher. If a room
is heated from the bottom, the mean temperature in the whole area of the room should be
able to be lower as the feet is the primary sensory part of the human body for temperature,
otherwise speaking, if feet feel warm the entire body feels warm as well. Consequently,
that allows the required temperature to be lower 2-3oC and save roughly 10-15% compar-
ing to a convectional radiators heating system. [8] [9]
Moreover, regarding thermal comfort, occupants diﬀer in the perception of which is the
most comfortable temperature on ﬂoor surface. Therefore, it is impossible to determine
an optimal ﬂoor temperature. Suggestions only can be provided for ﬂoor temperatures
to ensure that a minimum number of people are not satisﬁed. Thus, aiming to thermal
comfort, the surface temperature for an occupied zone should be between 19 and 30oC.

2.3
Heat pump
Heat pumps are considered as a low carbon technology as the heat transfer can be three
to four times higher than the electrical power consumed. In other words, a heat pump
requires 1 kW of electrical power to provide 3 to 4 kW of heat. One of the most common
type of heat pumps is the air source heat pump (ASHP). There have been many studies
conducted regarding ASHPs and the majority of them showed that these systems have a
remarkable drawback comparing to the rest types of heat pumps, which is the reduced
COP in extreme low ambient temperature. Additionally, another problem which aﬀects
the proper operation is frost formation that may occur on the outdoor heat exchanger and
reduce the ability to absorb heat from the outdoor environment. As a result, a perfor-
mance degradation is occurred.[13]
Many researchers suggest several solutions, however, the most dominant is the reverse cycle
defrosting which can worsen the indoor thermal comfort and increase the energy consump-
tion for achieving the required conditions, as Arif Hepbasli and Yildiz Kalinci support.[13]
Likewise, a research, conducted by J. Zhang, R.Z. Wang and J.Y. Wu claims that the
COP of an air source heat pump can be doubled during the summer, in cooling mode.
Their investigation demonstrated, the COP, for Tamb = 0oC in winter, can be approxi-
mately 2.61 while for Tamb = 35oC in summer EER can rise up to 5.66. In intermediate
conditions, for Tamb = 25oC is 4.817.[14]
Furthermore, there has been a lot of investigations regarding the COP of a variety of
heat pump technologies. Especially, comparing ASHP and GSHP according refrigerant
temperature, in terms of COP, Brendan Fox and Muhammad Akmal (2016) [15] state in
their research, that after an investigation of over 100 diﬀerent models of heat pumps, they
end up that the temperature diﬀerence between the supplied and the returned water of
the system is also a signiﬁcant factor which aﬀects the COP of the heat pumps. While
the temperature diﬀerence is increased, the COP is decreased both in GSHP and ASHP
systems as it is demonstrated in ﬁgure 2.6.
According to Robbin L Garber-Slaght et al.(2017) [16], in 2013 in Fairbanks of Alaska, the
Cold Climate Housing Research Center (CCHC) replaced a wood ﬁred masonry heater
and a 22.3 kW oil ﬁred condensing boiler which were the basic heat source of a building
with a GSHP. The heat pump was a residential size 12kW water to water unit. It was
connected to an underﬂoor heating system with a 303 liters water tank.
The objective of the installation was to monitor and examine the system for a ten-year

2.3.1
Free cooling
The summer in Greenland is quite mild with temperatures that rarely exceed 15oC, as the
average high temperature in July is 9.9 oC and the average low is 3.3 oC [17]. Considering
that potentially mechanical cooling is needed, especially during periods with high solar
loads, the underﬂoor cooling system will be supplied with cool water by the dry cooler. A
free cooling system will be tested taking advantage of the low ambient temperature. Free
cooling is a cost-eﬀective method which use the low air temperature to assist in chilling
water. Free cooling is mostly used in commercial buildings and data centers where the
need of cooling is extremely high.
The cool outdoor air is used as a cooling source when the ambient temperature is lower
than the required indoor temperature. During hot periods, the heat pump works in a
reversible mode to provide the required cooling to the house. If the outdoor temperature
is lower than the indoor, there is no need to operate the heat pump. Taking advantage
of the outdoor unit, dry cooler, the antifreeze water mixture in the system is recirculated
and absorbs the heat from the space. Afterwords, the heat is emitted to the outdoor
environment through the dry cooler. The only mechanical part that is in use is the water
pump which circulates the antifreeze water mixture into the system.

2.4
Objectives
The aim of this master’s thesis was to investigate if implementing an underﬂoor heating
and cooling system which would be supplied from a heat pump could be a suﬃcient so-
lution for the heating and cooling of a modern low-energy Greenlandic house. Especially,
ﬁrst goal was to see if a ﬂoor heating system can confront the Greenlandic climate as the
winter has tremendously low temperature and in many cases there are abrupt weather
changes which burdens the condition, as the underﬂoor systems do not have such an im-
mediate response. On the other hand, another challenge is to test if the ﬂoor cooling can
deal with the overheating problems of the high solar gains.
Moreover, the second goal was to determine if the domestic air source heat pump can be
negatively aﬀected by the low ambient temperature. If operating problems occur from the
particularly cold air, which are the potential solutions in order to provide the needed heat
to house. The ﬁnal goal was to ﬁnd the optimal heating system or, if that is not possible,
a combination of diﬀerent heating systems which could ensure the thermal comfort both
for winter and summer, according to the Greenlandic energy framework.

3.1
Case description
The extreme weather conditions that prevail in the Greenlandic region make the dwelling
design very demanding. Houses should be highly insulated in order to cope with such se-
vere climate and can keep the thermal losses at low levels. Insulation is a very signiﬁcant
part of a house design and plays a critical role in the total building energy consumption.
As a result, a heating/cooling system is closely related to the energy demand of the build-
ing, as the higher the insulation is the lower energy is needed. Consequently, the capacity
of the heating/cooling system is deﬁned from these indicators. In this project the basic
investigation was conducted regarding to the implementation of an air source heat pump.
This type of heat pump was used and compared with diﬀerent system combinations, to
ﬁgure out the advantages and disadvantages of each method, while operating under hard
circumstances.
IDA ICE was used in order to run simulations and test diﬀerent scenarios for diﬀerent
cases. A parameter analysis was carried out to evaluate the eﬃciency for each system
combination and to investigate which are the possibilities and the limitations for heat
pumps to provide heating and cooling with an underﬂoor system in Greenlandic region.
Due to the fact that IDA ICE does not have a reversible heat pump unit, simulations were
conducted in separate models. A heat pump was used for heating and a chiller for cooling,
Taking into consideration that they cannot operate simultaneously.

3.2.4
Ventilation
As far as ventilation is concerned, it is worth noting that it is one of the most signiﬁcant
elements for fulﬁlling the indoor air quality requirements.
Humidity, Carbon dioxide
concentration and Fanger’s indices (PMV,PPD) can be adjusted and set at the desired
level for a comfort residence. Especially, there are two diﬀerent methods of ventilation,
natural and mechanical ventilation. In this project, both types will be investigated focusing
more on the mechanical one. Cogitating that Greenland has ﬁerce weather conditions, it
is obvious that it cannot be always possible to use natural ventilation.
Using mechanical ventilation, the inlet air can be preheated with a view to reduce
the excessive heat transfer and the draught problems causing discomfort. This system
can provide a constant air ﬂow for a certain time period so that venting is completely
controlled. However, mechanical ventilation includes extra cost and increase of energy
consumption [21]. There are two distinct systems of mechanical ventilation, the constant
air volume system (CAV) and the variable air volume system (VAV). The CAV system
has a constant air ﬂow which is regulated by turning the system on or oﬀ. On the other
hand, the VAV system, has an adjusted air ﬂow which is regulated according to the room
needs and it is considered as a more eﬃcient system for buildings with variable occupant
loads. For residential buildings CAV is preferred.

Chapter 5
Discussion
In this chapter, the possibilities and limitations of the investigated heating and cooling
systems will be discussed according to the output of the reference building simulations.
5.1
Air source heat pump
5.1.1
Heating function
The air source heat pumps rely on weather conditions which assists the heat transfer be-
tween the heat exchanger and the outdoor environment. It is an undeniable fact that the
eﬀectiveness of the heat pump is closely related to the ambient air temperature, the lower
the air temperature is, the higher is the limitation of the heat pump. As Greenlandic
weather is characterized by extremely low temperature, the functionality is negatively af-
fected.
Firstly, simulations showed that the stand-alone 7kW HP could not meet the requirements
about indoor temperature, as during the periods when the ambient temperature was very
low, the heat pump was operating with very low eﬀectiveness or completely stopped op-
erating. Since the operation of the heat pump is aﬀected in high-level, this system cannot
be considered as a proper solution for the building heating needs. Furthermore, a stand-
alone 15kW heat pump and a 7kW HP with an EB can potentially respond to the building
demands and provide the needed hot water to the storage tank. However a comparison
between the two of them showed that the 15kW HP has a remarkable disadvantage re-
garding COP. In the ﬁrst case, the COP of 15kW HP, ﬂuctuates from 2.36 to 2.87, while
in the second, 7kW HP with EB, it is slightly higher, from 2.55 to 3.06. The annual mean
COP is 2.66 and 2.84 respectively, which shows that even if the 15kW HP can meet the
needs, it uses more energy in comparison to 7kW HP. This happens because the ambient
temperature creates operating issues. In order for the HP to absorb heat from outside, the
evaporator must operate at lower temperature than the ambient temperature. This very
low temperature allow ice or frost to accumulate on the evaporator. Thus, the operation
of heat pump stops. On the other side, a 15kW HP, due to its high capacity, can overcome
this issue and continue working but the COP is signiﬁcantly reduced. The HP from the
15kW capacity uses maximum 6 the days that there is risk of ice or frost accumulation
while the rest operating period only 3 to 4 kW. Therefore, instead of using a 6kW HP to
cover the demand of the building, a 15 kW is used in order to not to stop operating. This
is the main problem of using an air source heat pump in very cold regions. Moreover,
the 7kW HP with EB system responded very well in diﬀerent heating setpoints without
any temperature issues in zones. Therefore, a 7kW hp and an EB system is considered
as a eligible solution, since both the consumed energy and the investment cost are lower

compared to a 15kW HP system.
From another point of view, a conventional residential heat pump, that operates under
standard weather condition may have higher COP, which often varies from 3 to 4. The
reduced COP, in both cases, is due to the low air temperature which constrains the heat
pump to absorb the necessary heat. It is apparent that using an air source heat under
these weather conditions, it is impossible to take entirely advantage of its capabilities.
However, as the results proved, an air source heat pump combined with a supplementary
heating system, can perform at an adequate level and provide the required hot water to
the connected heating system. This indicates that the issues created by the low ambient
temperature can be overcome. Hence, air source heat pumps can be conditionally imple-
mented in regions with such weather patterns.
Moreover, the alternative system combination with the 7kW HP and electric radiators was
tested regarding any potential advantages according to thermal comfort, comparing to the
rest systems, as it could provide heat from an extra source. A combination of a ﬂoor
heating system and electric radiators had not any remarkable advantages, except from
lower vertical diﬀerence in speciﬁc cases. It might be considered as an alternative solution
if the DHW was excluded of the heating system. However, the investment cost is higher
than the 7 kW HP system, as 6 extra units of radiators are needed to be implemented in
the house. The total energy consumption is signiﬁcantly higher as well, than all the rest
systems.
5.1.2
Cooling function
Despite the low ambient temperature that prevail in summer, Greenlandic dwelling may
suﬀer in some occasions from overheating.
The solar gains and the internal loads, as
simulations showed, can create overheating issues. The combined system of a 7kW HP
with an EB, which was used also for cooling and as it is proved, can constrain the high
operative temperatures that occur. The heat pump operating in reversible mode achieved
eﬀortlessly to provide the tank and the ﬂoor cooling system with 20oC water, with almost
minor energy as the SEER was approximately 5. Moreover, the system responded quite
well in a potential setpoint change, keeping the overheating hours below the maximum
levels.
Lastly, the most important fact for using a 7kW reversible heat pump is that
cooling and heating can suﬃciently be accomplished with the same equipment and same
installation.
5.2
Free cooling - Dry cooler
The free cooling system had approximately the same eﬀectiveness with the 7kW HP re-
garding the cooling function. The dry cooler achieved to supply water to the tank and to
the ﬂoor cooling system at the required temperature, 20oC. The dry cooler uses energy
only for the AHX fan and the water pump in order to recirculate the water and cool it
down with the low ambient air temperature. Thereby, the annual energy consumption is
60% lower than the heat pump. The low ambient temperature gives a great advantage to
the dry cooler comparing to the heat pump. However, taking into account the insigniﬁcant
amount of energy consumption for cooling comparing to the total annual energy consump-
tion, the beneﬁts will not be so substantial. Moreover, the dry cooler is an independent
equipment which should be installed apart from the heating system, which needs extra
supply network to be connected with the tank. Considering the aforementioned, it is a
possible solution if a heat pump for heating was not needed.

5.3
Floor heating and cooling system
5.3.1
Floor heating
The ﬂoor heating system was examined regarding if it can provide the required heat to the
zones and about the variation of surface temperature, in order to avoid thermal discom-
fort due to very high surface temperature. As regards the indoor temperature, it could be
mentioned that it achieved to control completely the operative temperature and to keep
the zones close to the setpoint without letting the temperature drop. Concerning surface
temperature, the system was very eﬀective as there was no case when the temperature
exceeded 29oC . Additionally, the vertical temperature diﬀerence, was in general, at ac-
ceptable levels except for some short periods that slightly exceed 3oC, but it was only for
some hours. Generally, the ﬂoor heating system had a very eﬀective operation without
creating thermal comfort issues.
5.3.2
Floor cooling
The ﬂoor cooling system was examined for the same subjects, as ﬂoor heating and the
results were pretty much the same. Taking into account that the cooling demand cannot
be compared to heating demand the outcome concerns the days that overheating occurred.
The ﬂoor cooling system managed to keep properly the operative temperature below 26o
and reduce the overheating hours. However, when setpoints were changed, the unmet hours
were slightly increased especially with 24oC, but in general the operative temperature was
close to the setpoint. The surface temperature was, as well, at the desirable levels without
dropping below 20oC and there was no danger of condensation on the ﬂoor surface. In
general, it can be considered that the ﬂoor cooling and heating system responded very
well to the reference house under high heating needs and high internal and solar loads.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Completing the study regarding the possibilities and limitations of ﬂoor heating and cool-
ing in Greenland, it may be said that Greenland needs a special handling about the
designing of heating and cooling system.
The extraordinary, for European standards,
prevailing weather conditions showed that can signiﬁcantly aﬀect the way that a heating
and cooling system will be designed and potentially set limitations to some of them. As
simulations showed, even if the total heating demand of the reference building could be
covered from a stand-alone 7kW HP, the extremely low ambient temperature prevents
the regular HP operation. The most signiﬁcant limitation about heat pump is the low
ambient temperature and the risk of ice creation on the evaporator. This is the reason for
the need of a supplementary system. Therefore, it can be said that air source heat pumps
have a limited range of use in regions with extremely low ambient temperatures such as
Greenland.
Furthermore, if the ﬂoor heating system is combined with a system that can provide prop-
erly the ﬂoor pipes with water at the required temperature, it is very eﬀective and it can
respond eﬀortlessly at the building’s needs. That is documented also, by the fact that the
heating setpoints were increased up to 23oC and the system could provide the necessary
heat to keep the operative temperature at the level of the setpoint. The ﬂoor heating sys-
tems do not aﬀect at all during the whole year, except for the cases that the supplied water
might not be at the recommenced temperature. Hence, it seems that ﬂoor heating systems
are very promising and can stand in modern houses with similar construction in Greenland.
As far as ﬂoor cooling is concerned, it could be mentioned that, in general, it reacted
properly when it was required.
In Greenland there is no remarkable need for cooling
during summer, as the ambient temperature is quite low. However, the daylight in summer
lasts from 18 to 21 hours per day,[32] which can signiﬁcantly aﬀect the solar radiation a
house receives. Taking into consideration that most of the current residential buildings
in Greenland are quite old, they may be not aﬀected that much. But the well-insulated
modern houses probably will have a great impact from solar radiation which can lead to
increased indoor temperature.
