Introduction
Historically, the earliest implication of privacy rights was seen in the form of a law
review article written by Samuel Warren and Louis Brandeis in 1890 known as ”The
right to privacy” also know as ”The right to be left alone”. This was seen as one of
the most inﬂuential essays in the history of American law [18]. Since then, there has
been a development on privacy rights throughout history [19]. In the recent years,
the Snowden revelations about National Security Agency surveillance and facebook
battling the Cambridge Analytica scandal are one of the famous incidents showing
the importance of protecting personal data.
Hence, the emergence of GDPR in EU enforced in May 2018 emerged ensuring a sys-
tematic way of monitoring, maintaining and documenting privacy rights on personal
data throughout Europe. This has aﬀected the way of gathering and processing per-
sonal data for many organizations and businesses. Thus, there is a constant need
for all types of organizations to comply with the GDPR in the future.
In this thesis, we will be exploring the GDPR legal document to design an access
control model that will comply with all the requirements of the GDPR i.e. A GDPR
Compliant Access Control Model. We have decided to work with AERBAC [13] due
to well known limitations of other access control models. This is explained in section
1.3.
To begin with, GDPR legal document with a focus on all concerns related to access
control will be analyzed. In this way, a set of requirements will be developed that
reﬂects the GDPR Regulation associated with the access control of personal. The
designing process will be initiated with the help of abstracting the requirement
set, modelling them mathematically using set theory and then data modelling from
software engineering.
Based on the mentioned analysis and modelling techniques, we will develop a Java
implementation of AERBAC that implies the practical use of our model. The Java
implementation will demonstrate a system of how AERBAC can be used by users
deduced from GDPR system role terminology to access personal data satisfying
GDPR requirements. Thus, a GDPR compliant AERBAC design an implementation
will be tested by demonstrating use cases and test cases.
Finally, a discussion of our work and its implications for future use and research will

be presented. In addition, we will conclude the thesis showing the learning outcome
of our work as a student specializing in Computer Security.

1.3
Background of Access Control
Access control models are crucial in the ﬁeld of computer security and since the
appearance of multi-user computing, access control has been used as an important
security mechanism. The function of access control is to regulate who can have access
to resources. Access control matrix was ﬁrst introduced in 1960’s and as technology
advanced, several other access control models were introduced. The most popular
types of access control models are as follows:
6
==PAGE:8==
• Discretionary access control (DAC) In this method, administrator set the
rules or policies for who can access the resource.
• Mandatory Access Control (MAC) This method is often used in gov-
ernment or military environments. In this method, multiple constraint are
applied which are beyond the control of the owner.
• Role-based Access Control RBAC are used to enforce the MAC and DAC
models. It was ﬁrst introduced in 1990’s by Ferraiolo et al. [1] and further im-
provements resulted in RBAC been approved as NIST standard ANSI/INCITS
[2].
We will now look into two of the most dominant access control paradigms, RBAC
and ABAC.
1.3.1
Role-Based Access Control(RBAC)
For a detailed understanding of RBAC please refer to [1]. Here, we only want to
introduce the access control paradigm. In this section we will name some of the
components of RBAC:
• Users A user is a person represented by either a program or a process
• Roles Role represents a job role with a set of tasks
• Operation Operation allows a user to execute a task (e.g. read, write etc.)
• Object An object represents information or a system resource
• Permission A permission is a regulation that permits an operation on an
object
There are several advantages associated with using RBAC. They are mentioned as
below:
• It is easy to manage the roles and permissions
• Easier policy administration
• Role based approach ﬁts nicely with the organizational structures
However, RBAC is known to be limited for pervasive computing. Pervasive comput-
ing means that computing can appear anywhere and at any time in our everyday
life. Hence, in such cases, authorization control becomes complex as they might be
based on context. RBAC does not provide ﬂexibility to entail context awareness
and therefore this is one of limitations of RBAC model.
Another issue is the role-permission explosion. In cases of a large organization re-
quiring thousands of diﬀerent roles. Many of these roles are closely related and
hence managing them becomes a very hectic task and this leads to a role explosion.
Moreover, we know that permissions are deﬁned in terms of object identiﬁers (re-
ferring to every single object). In cases where a role requires access to thousands of
diﬀerent object, a permission will need to be deﬁned for each object and this leads
to permission explosion.
7
==PAGE:9==
Even though RBAC is seen to have essential advantages, another paradigm of access
control was identiﬁed known as Attribute-Based Access Control [3]. This approach
was meant to help with overcoming the limitations of RBAC and provide another
way of providing access control [4].
1.3.2
Attribute-Based Access Control(ABAC)
An alternative approach to RBAC is ABAC. As mentioned earlier, ABAC is used to
overcome the limitations of RBAC, such as computing of contextual information in
making access control decisions [5]. Entities information can be represented by an
attribute. Access control policy of ABAC is created using attributes; user, object
and environment.
Although ABAC was ﬁrst introduced in 2005 [3], the use of
attributes for users and objects was mentioned already by late 90s in the literature
[6][7][8].
Due to its easily accessible contextual attributes, it is considered more ﬂexible than
RBAC. The features of RBAC and ABAC are discussed in the next section.
1.3.3
Integration of RBAC and ABAC
Both RBAC and ABAC have advantages and disadvantages. Both of the access
control model have features that complement each other thus, the combination of
RBAC and ABAC could result in a more eﬀective access control system. Hence,
NIST identiﬁed three possible ways in order to integrate RBAC with ABAC to
make use of advantages of both mechanisms. These three possibilities initiated by
NIST are as below:
• Role-centric In this approach the roles regulate the maximum number of
permissions available to a user, and attributes are used to constrain these
permissions. This approach is regarded as a direction for future research by
Kuhl et al. [9].
• Attribute-centric In this approach, roles are not related to permissions and
are treated as just one attribute. This approach does not inherit any advantage
from RBAC.
• Dynamic roles In this approach, the roles are determined by the attributes.
Kern et al. [10] applied this approach to large organizational scenarios. This
approach does not address the issue of role and permission explosion
1.3.4
Our choice
Our choice of access control model for this thesis is the Attribute Enhanced Role
Based Access Control Model (AERBAC). AERBAC makes use of the role-centric
approach and hence utlizing beneﬁts from the advantages of the RBAC and the
ABAC models. AERBAC implementation is explained in more detail in section 4
and [13].

General Data Protection
Regulation (GDPR)
The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is a European Union “Regulation”
that has a goal to protect the personal data of every individual in the EU. The
Regulation does not diﬀerentiate between EU citizens or people who are temporarily
located in the EU. The regulation applies to the personal data of every individual
within EU.
The GDPR regulation was published in May 2016. A 2 year transition period was
provided for all the organizations to understand this new regulation and ensure
compliance. On May 25th 2018, the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)
was enforced and every organization working with personal data was required to
strictly comply with it. If no compliance is found, the organization will be seriously
ﬁned.
Thus, the GDPR compliance has become a very relevant topic for all the organi-
zations dealing with the personal data of anyone located in the EU. Organizations
have taken diﬀerent measures to integrate their existing systems to comply with
GDPR regulations. To understand the GDPR requirements, there is no tool or of-
ﬁcial (EU) scientiﬁc work for engineers to use directly. Hence, we have decided to
go through the GDPR legal document and present our ﬁndings. In this thesis, we
will look deep into GDPR regulations and construct a way to handle personal data
complying with all the regulations provided in the GDPR.
In this section, we will look into GDPR chapter-wise chronologically. The purpose of
this chapter is to extract all the relevant requirement data that fall under the scope
of this thesis. This ensures that our ﬁnal access control implementation encompasses
all the scenarios and requirements mentioned in the GDPR Regulation document.
We will be collecting the most important mentions by deﬁning a ﬁndings section
for every chapter of the GDPR. These ﬁndings will later be used to deﬁne the
requirement set of our system model 3. This will help to provide with domain and
the requirements of our system design in later stages of the thesis.

2.1
GDPR deﬁnitions
There are multiple terms used in the GDPR regulation that need to be explained
before we can start our analysis of the GDPR articles. The most important terms
are deﬁned as follows:
• Data Subject A data subject is any individual whose personal data is being
collected or processed and that, he or she can be directly or indirectly identiﬁed
by a that personal data.
• Data controller A data controller is a natural or legal person or an organi-
zation that determines the purpose of processing personal data.
• Data processor A data processor is someone who will only be allowed to
process personal data on behalf of the controller.
• Data Protection Oﬃcer (DPO) A DPO is a security leadership role re-
quired by GDPR. A DPO is responsible to monitor the data protection strat-
egy and implementation within an organization to ensure compliance with the
GDPR requirements.
• Commission The Commission is the European Commission (EC) which is an
institute of the European Council. The Commission is responsible to legislate
and manage the everyday business task of the EU.
• Member State The Member State is every country that is part of the EU.
The GDPR regulation applies to each of these countries. The country list is
provided in appendix B.
• Supervisory Authority This is an independent public authority established
by each Member State. The purpose of establishing this authority is to ensure
GDPR compliance in a Member State.
• European Data Protection Board This is an independent European body
that is responsible for consistent application of GDPR throughout EU. It also
promotes the cooperation between EU’s supervisory authorities.
2.2
Chronological view of Final legal text of the
Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (EU GDPR)
In this section we will go through GDPR chapters one by one, analyzing all the
relevant requirements before we can start with the design of our solution.
2.2.1
Chapter 1 - General provisions
This chapter includes article 1-4. Article 1 deals with subject-matter and objectives
of the GDPR. This article describes that the regulation is to protect natural persons
with regards to the processing of their personal data and the free movement of
personal data within an organization or outside an organization.
12
==PAGE:14==
Article 2 deals with the material scope fo the GDPR. The article states that this
regulation applies to processing data through automation and without automation.
Moreover, that the regulation does not apply to any activity performed outside the
scope of European Union law.
Article 3 deals with the territorial scope describing that this regulation also applies
to a controller or processor established outside the Union, processing the personal
data of data subjects who are in the Union. Article 4 provides deﬁnitions to all the
terms used in the GDPR regulation.
In this chapter, we learn the scope and background of the GDPR regulation. We
know that GDPR is enforced to protect the privacy right of individuals. GDPR
regulation does not support any business activities or any other beneﬁcial activity
for organizations. Thus, complying with GDPR is purely due to individual privacy
rights. We also learn about the scope of GDPR.
Findings:
• Purpose of protecting privacy rights
• Applies to all types of data processing
• Applies to processor or controller outside EU
2.2.2
Chapter 2 - Principles
Article 5 characterizes the principles relating to processing of personal data. It lays
down 7 very important points that will be mentioned in the ﬁndings list below.
Article 6, 7 and 8 describes conditions for the lawfulness, consent and a child’s
consent. Article 9, 10 and 11 describes special categories of personal data, personal
data related to criminals and processing where no identiﬁcation is required.
Findings:
• data is processed lawfully, fairly and in a transparent manner in relation to
the data subject
• data is collected for speciﬁed, explicit and legitimate purposes and not fur-
ther processed in a manner that is incompatible with those purposes (purpose
limitation)
• data is adequate, relevant and limited to what is necessary in relation to the
purposes for which they are processed (data minimization)
• accurate and, where necessary, kept up to date (accuracy)
• data is kept in a form which permits identiﬁcation of data subjects for no longer
than is necessary for the purposes for which the personal data is processed. If
data is to be stored for longer periods that it needs to implement article 89
(storage limitation)
• data is processed in a manner that ensures appropriate security of the personal
data (integrity and conﬁdentiality)

We will not investigate how each requirement is to be fulﬁlled. Hence, we assume
that each requirement can be satisﬁed and thus can be represented as a requirement
set R where r1, r2, r3 . . . rn are the number of all the requirements.
R = {r1, r2, r3 . . . rn}
In the same way, we deﬁne a set D of personal data d1, d2, d3 ... dn:
D = {d1, d2, d3 . . . dn}
We will introduce a new set G which is a set of GDPR compliant data:
G = {g1, g2, g3 . . . gn} where a contains d ∈D and g ⊇R
From here we can substantiate that for data d ∈D to exist in a set G (GDPR
compliant), all the requirement attributes r ∈R need to be fulﬁlled. Thus, we can
write this in a set notation:
G = {d1 ∪R, d2 ∪R, d3 ∪R . . . dn ∪R}
This can also be written as:
G = {(d1, r1, r2, r3 . . . rn), (d2, r1, r2, r3 . . . rn), (d3, r1, r2, r3 . . . rn) . . . (dn, r1,
r2, r3 . . . rn)}
Now, we deﬁne the users U that will require access to the data D as:
U = {u1, u2, u3 . . . un}
32
==PAGE:34==
Based on our analysis in the section 3.2 we have already established that one or more
users will be required to access the data to perform their required tasks. The set G
contains the data with the GDPR requirements. Hence, we will further develop our
set G to integrate one or more users. This can be done as follows:
G = {U ∪d1 ∪R, U ∪d2 ∪R, U ∪d3 ∪R . . . U ∪dn ∪R}
Thus, set G can be expanded as:
G = {(u1, u2, u3 . . . un, d1, r1, r2, r3 . . . rn), (u1, u2, u3 . . . un, d2, r1, r2, r3 . . .
rn), (u1, u2, u3 . . . un, d3, r1, r2, r3 . . . rn), (u1, u2, u3 . . . un, dn r1, r2, r3 . . . rn)}
This shows the general case of sets R, U and D unifying to form set G. However, we
assumed that all the users of the set U will be allowed to have access to personal
data. Note that, the sets R, D and U have speciﬁc type R, D and U respectively.
However, the set G does not have any speciﬁc type and hence can hold elements of
all types.

Logical relation between sets
We know that there could be cases where not all the users are validated to have
access to the personal data. Hence, the user access could be limited to only one user
for instance. The following diagram shows this relationship between the sets U, R,

Thus, the set G represented by grey color will be:
G = (G ∩D) ∪(G ∩U) ∪R ∪(G \ (R ∪U ∪D))
This shows that the sets D and U are disjoint sets and they are uniﬁed using set
G. Moreover, set G contains the set R and therefore, knowing that G ⊃R we can
simplify the formulation of G as:
G = (G ∩D) ∪(G ∩U) ∪(G \ (U ∪D))

3.3.2
Relationship between our data sets
Through our use of set theory and venn diagram 3.8 we have established that our
data sets share a speciﬁc relationship. We will describe this in terms of G in the
following way:
• G ⊃R: This relationship shows that the set G is the proper superset of R. This
means that G contains all the elements of set R and more. This indicates that
our GDPR complaint set contains all the requirements previously mentioned
in R. Thus, R is contained in G.
• G ∩D: This relationship shows that the set G intersects with the set D. This
means that there are one or more elements of D that are in set G. Hence, the
personal data in D can be part of the GDPR complaint data set G.
• G ∩U: This relationship shows that the set G intersects with the set U. This
means that there are one or more elements of U that are in set G. Hence, the
users in D can be part of the GDPR complaint data set G.
The results here can be stated as that for set D to be uniﬁed with set U there needs
to exist a set G (set R is contained in set G). This can further be interpreted as set
G to be an access control model. Thus, an access control containing requirement set
needs to exist to grant users access to data. Based on this, we will start our system
design in the next sections by creating a conceptual and logical data model.

Application in the AERBAC
architecture
To be able to grasp big data and pervasive computing, and to achieve enhanced
strategic insight and informed decision making, a systematic and ﬂexible access
control mechanism is needed. Attribute Based Access Control and Role Based Ac-
cess Control (RBAC) are highly used access control mechanisms. The ABAC system
is proved to be very complex in terms of policy reviews but easy to mold with chang-
ing circumstances. RBAC provided a stable policy management system but lacks
ﬂexibility to adapt changes in user’s object.
Attribute Enhanced Role Based Access Control is one example of role-centric access
control which provides ﬂexibility. Moreover, it provides the capacity to represent dif-
ferent access control models, as well as application in diﬀerent technology domains.
Internet of Things (IoT) is of the best examples. In the context of AERBAC, at-
tributes allow an easy approach in specifying the conditions under which users and
permissions can be assigned. For more details, please refer to this research [13].
We have implemented an AERBAC model in a Document Management System
on the basis of our requirement set, organizational models, set theory and data
modelling. As this section involves system integration and implementation, we would
be using more relevant terms for the AERBAC model. We translate the terms from
our analysis to AERBAC terms as follows:
• All the roles from the section 3.2 requiring access to the personal data will be
referred to as User
• Personal Data will be referred to as Object (i.e. a Document in our imple-
mentation)
• The requirement set will be referred to as Permission. This will be explained
ongoing.
The main goal for this project is to show how we can grant a user access to documents
with the help of permissions based on attributes attached to users, environment and
document (object) itself. In short, the user can perform diﬀerent actions for example
download, share and view document after clearly passing through the permission
check stage.
After passing the permission check, if the attributes of query user

Conclusion
Due to the huge integrated systems, authorization control has become complex as
access decisions may depend on the context in which access requests are made. The
contextual information consists of such information which will be associated with
a user, object and environment. For example, an access control permission may
depend on the user’s current geographical location, the object being currently in
a speciﬁc state, and the time of day when the access is requested. AERBAC is
most adequate for such applications, as access to data is more naturally described
in terms of its semantic context, for example, in a rating system of movies, Horror
movies are restricted to users above a certain age, based on the movie contents.”
Using the same beneﬁts of AERBAC, we have been able to implement a document
access system which can be explored to implement GDPR requirements. The system
architects and programmers can work together on this implementation as a template
to develop their speciﬁc application to ensure GDPR compliance.
From the perspective of this thesis, the requirement set can be used to deﬁne at-
tributes for the user (GDPR role) and the document (GDPR personal data) and
easily implement this using this program as a template.

Discussion
In the beginning of the thesis, we provided a summary of the background of access
control model and the developments that we have seen in the recent years within this
ﬁeld 1.3. However, AERBAC solves the issues of context awareness, role explosion
and permission explosion []. Thus, in this section, we will discuss the challenges
and limitations related to the practical implementation of our GDPR compliant
AERBAC model.
Scope: The purpose of designing our solution is not to provide a model that can
be readily applied to any organization and attain a GDPR compliant access control.
This thesis is relevant for developers who are researching diﬀerent approaches to
develop a GDPR compliant access control system.
Flexibility: The implementation of the AERBAC has used some speciﬁcations
with regards to the users, objects and permissions. These speciﬁcations were used for
illustrative purposes. This thesis does not promote any speciﬁc way of implementing
the GDPR compliant AERBAC. Our intent is to show one way of implementing
it and at the same time, provide the basis for other ways of implementing the
ﬁnal solution. In this way, this thesis provides ﬂexibility for researchers to use the
analysis, data modelling and domain model from this thesis to develop their own
ﬁnal solutions.
Performance: In this thesis, we have not taken consideration to the performance
of AERBAC algorithms. Thus, we do not herewith claim that we have adopted the
best performing algorithm. To measure the performance, a reference implementation
of other models with suitable policies would be required [15].
GDPR validity: Our solution is based on a theoretical study and academic re-
search of the topic i.e. GDPR. We have not taken the business parameters into
consideration during the analysis, modelling and implementation phase of this the-
sis. Moreover, our solution is not certiﬁed or tested by any GDPR authority hence,
we do not claim that this is an oﬃcial way of performing GDPR compliance as this
is not the aim of this thesis. We propose a solution following an academic approach
which can be referred to for future projects. More details on this can be found in
section 6.

This thesis has been a long journey with a blend of interest, complications and hard
work. As a student specializing in Computer Security, the distinctive methods of
access control has always been of interest especially the recent research on AERBAC
which we have had the opportunity to study in detail for this thesis.
On the other hand, the emerging discussions on GDPR in the mainstream media,
from a human as well as business perspective, has also been of high interest and
relevance. In this thesis, we have managed to combine academic research with a
real world topic. Moreover, we have successfully used academic research to address
a method of solving a real world issue concerning many organizations and businesses
today.
GDPR is a legal document written for an audience having a background in law and
order. It is an utmost challenging task for a computer science engineering student
to read, comprehend, analyze and use this GDPR regulation document as a basis
to create a GDPR compliant AERBAC model design and implementation. In this
thesis, we have shown endeavour to achieve this task with its implications[5][6].
We started this thesis by theoretically studying AERBAC segments, decision making
algorithms and its ﬂexibility of use. Then we began on a thorough study of the
GDPR document and its requirements with a focus on how an access control model
can be designed accordingly.
We did not leave out on any detail of the GDPR
and worked on an AERBAC model that can encompass all the requirements of the
GDPR. We have achieved this by the process of abstraction and data modelling the
most important components of the GDPR with regards to an access control model.
The next step was to try and implement AERBAC, and show how our theoretical
design can be programmed and implemented as a basis for a practical solution. This
was done in Java, using one of our previously deﬁned template during the study of
AERBAC. Some adjustments were made to incorporate the GDPR analysis and
model into the context of Java.
All in all, it has been a good learning process. We have learned substantially about
the GDPR and its implications on access control. In the future, we hope that this
thesis can be helpful for others who are researching the topic of access control and
GDPR.

GDPR Personal Data
Requirements
The following notes were made during the analysis phase of the GDPR Regulation
documents. These points represent the ﬁndings made by keeping the personal data
as a dependent variable:
• Purpose of protecting privacy rights
• Applies to all types of data processing
• Applies to processor or controller outside EU
• Data is processed lawfully, fairly and in a transparent manner in relation to
the data subject
• Data is collected for speciﬁed, explicit and legitimate purposes and not fur-
ther processed in a manner that is incompatible with those purposes (purpose
limitation)
• Data is adequate, relevant and limited to what is necessary in relation to the
purposes for which they are processed (data minimization)
• Accurate and up to date (accuracy)
• Data is kept in a form which permits identiﬁcation of data subjects for no
longer than is necessary for the purposes for which the personal data is pro-
cessed. If data is to be stored for longer periods that it needs to implement
article 89 (storage limitation)
• data is processed in a manner that ensures appropriate security of the personal
data (integrity and conﬁdentiality)
• Data controller shall be responsible and be able to demonstrate compliance
with paragraph 1 - all these mentioned (accountability)
• Right to information: The data subject can ask an organization about what
personal data (about him or her) is being processed and the rationale for such
processing.
