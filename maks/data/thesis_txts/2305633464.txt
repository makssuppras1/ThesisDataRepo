1 Abstract 
 
This report is focusing on the primary school timetabling problem. By introducing the 
teacher group parameter a model is developed that consider all constraints that are 
required by a primary school. The teacher group parameter is created as a response to 
the wish from a group of teachers to be able to be more flexible in the teaching.  
The reports main focus is on the development of the full model that can create a 
timetable for any primary school that could be accepted in a real life situation. This 
means that many constraints are included that all have an influence on how the 
timetable will look like. 
In this report mixed integer programming will be used when solving the problem. The 
obtained results received by the use of a Gurobi solver shows that the problem can be 
solved with an acceptable timetable as output, however, the running time may be too 
long in practices using only MIP to solve the problem. The constraint for minimizing the 
number of idle timeslots in the schedule for teachers is definitely making the model hard 
to solve and different formulations of this constraint, along with other constraints, have 
been tested in the effort of optimizing the model. 
The results are based on two different datasets from two different primary schools in 
Denmark. The complexity of the datasets is different – one is very detailed and the other 
one is simpler. In the conclusion, different suggestions are given that could help speed 
up the running time for the solver.  
 
Keywords: Operation Research, Mixed Integer Programming, Primary School Timetabling, 
Gurobi, Idle Timeslot.

4 Introduction 
 
Scheduling is an important task in the primary school. Teachers need to know what 
subjects and classes to teach and when and where to do so. Likewise, it is important for 
the children to know which subjects to take and when.  
 
Today it is up to the individual school to decide how the schedule should look like for 
the teachers and the students.  
The government introduced some new rules for the schools to comply in the school 
reform of 2014. These rules dictate the number of teaching lessons that have to be 
obtained for both teachers and students. When creating the schedule for the school all 
these rules need to be considered.  
 
Because of the school reform, among other things, a need and wish has evolved among 
some teachers to have more influence and freedom to plan their own teaching time. 
Instead of having told exactly when to teach which class in which subject the teachers 
want to control when to teach the classes what, as long as they in the end can prove to 
have met the numbers of teaching lessons. 
 
In Danish primary school it is often seen that the teachers follow a specific class in a 
specific subject through several years. This has of course some benefits in the way that 
the connection between the teacher and the students are close. In some cases and 
especially for some subjects, however, it is not necessary for the students to absolutely 
have the same teacher all the time. It could even be beneficial to switch teachers 
according to specific topics covered by the subject. 
 
To hand over some responsibility of the planning to the teachers can help doing the 
overall scheduling for the school more flexible and in the end possibly more optimal. 
Following problem formulations arises from this: 
 
Is it possible to develop a simple model that can easily solve the primary school 
timetabling problem by introducing the new teacher group parameter? 
 
This project is intended to try to solve the scheduling for primary schools. By detaching 
the teacher from the class the idea is to introduce the teacher group parameter. A 
teacher group will be able to cover not only the class but also all students from the same 
year. When doing the schedule, a teacher group can be assigned to multiple classes at 
the same level and then it is up to the teachers in that group to arrange the specific 
teaching for the students.  
 
The problem is to find the optimal timetable while still having to comply all constraints 
dictated by the government, the school board, the teachers and the students.

5 Literature Study 
 
The Class-Teacher Timetabling Problem with Compactness Requirements (CTTPCR) has 
been research by multiple people since the 60s. 
There are many approaches used to successfully solve the problem and a lot of studies 
describing how.  
 
Branch-and-Cut 
Branch and cut involves running a branch and bound algorithm and using cutting planes 
to tighten the linear programming relaxations and reduce the problem. 
Burke et al. (2012) uses Branch-and-Cut to solve the Udine Course Timetabling Problem. 
Two different types of cuts are introduced. Type 1 is the necessary cuts that are cuts 
from events/free period patterns, while type 2 cuts are problem specific cuts that are 
not necessary to reach optimality but will improve performance, when added on 
violation. 
 
Column Generation 
Column generation can in some cases be preferred as the approach for solving 
timetabling problems. Qualizza et al. (2005) are using column generation to find a 
solution and use a branch-and-price procedure for improving it. Two main advantages 
are stated. One is the producing of a better bound for the branch-and-bound procedure 
and the other is the possibility to better control the schedule. 
Santos et al. (2010) is also exploring column generation with a cut and column 
generation algorithm that results in a very good obtained lower bound.  
 
Heuristics 
Heuristics techniques have been the most commonly used technique when solving 
timetabling problems. There are different heuristics that can be applied when trying to 
solve a problem. 
MirHassani et al. (2013) is discussing different solution methods used on the university 
timetabling problem. Without presenting any data they are presenting and discussing 
the benefits of different methods. Especially decomposing the problem into smaller 
problems and hybridizing the heuristics method to produce advanced search methods 
will improve the solution time and create quality timetables in general. 
The article discusses different Meta-heuristics that all have shown very good results. 
Genetic & Memetic Algorithms, Graph Colouring Algorithm and Simulated Annealing are 
three of those along with Tabu Search. 
 
Tabu Search 
Tabu search is a well-known meta-heuristic used to improve a solution. Souza et al. 
(2003) are using Tabu search when trying to optimize the solution with good result. 
Starting from the initial solution the algorithm goes the neighbour that produces the 
best solution. The movement is then stored in a Tabu list so the algorithm does not go 
back to the already explored solution. Being able to worsen the solution makes the Tabu 
search able to continue the search outside local minimum/optimum.  
 
Local Branch 
The university timetabling problem is a NP-hard problem and even trying to solve this 
with mixed-integer programming can be hard in real-world scenarios due to the size of 
the problem. The aim with Local Branching is to integrate local search and meta-
heuristics ideas within Mixed Integer Programming. 
Fischetti et al. (2003) are exploring a new solution framework for general MIPs that 
proved effective when solving hard MIPs.

5.1 Timetabling in Primary School 
 
Timetabling is a well-known and highly researched area within universities and high 
schools. There are many studies describing the different problems when having to 
create a complete schedule for these schools. The studies are using different operational 
research techniques to solve these problems within a reasonable timeframe and are 
showing good results. 
 
Primary school timetabling is not a problem that is discussed as common as the others 
and there can be different reasons for that. Many results and techniques, however, can 
be applied to the primary school timetabling problem. The basic parameters are 
identical and therefore it is relevant to look at the results from University and High 
School timetabling and which techniques that are used, when solving this problem for 
Primary Schools. 
 
5.1.1 
Tabulex 
There are many different tools in the market for helping primary schools creating and 
optimizing their timetable. One of the largest players is Tabulex which is a tool 
developed to create and optimize a timetable based on input from the user. 
Tabulex is built on the initial idea that a class is tied together with a specific subject and 
a specific teacher. When creating the schedule this combination cannot be broken.  
 
 
Figure 1 
Figure 1 shows a representation of the bricks that are the keystone of the way that 
Tabulex is handling the scheduling. Each brick fits a timeslot in the schedule. When all 
bricks are scheduled without breaking any constraints, the full timetable is created. The 
example in Figure 1 shows the schedule for the class 7C where some of the bricks have 
been scheduled. The rest of the bricks are still waiting to be scheduled along with the 
bricks for the classes 7A and 7B. Every class has their own timetable where their bricks 
have to be scheduled. 
This way of scheduling is traditional in the way that the same process was used even 
before the computer was developed to help. At that time the same bricks were created 
manually in the form of post-its and were spread out on a table and then put up on the 
board that represented the timetable. Tabulex is basically a tool helping the process by 
doing it automatically. 
==PAGE:9==
Page 9 of 54 
 
 
Tabulex can be used in different ways. When having imported data on classes, subjects, 
teachers etc. the solver can be started and stopped at any time. When stopping the 
solver, it is possible to look at the timetable that has provided the best solution at the 
time. The user can then make changes to the data and to the model to search for specific 
improvements in the timetable. When started the user can see a graphical presentation 
on how good the currently found solution is and whether all constraints are satisfied.  
 
5.1.2 
Metaheuristic vs. MIP 
 
Tabulex is using meta-heuristics to solve the timetabling problem. As describe meta-
heuristic is a good method to use to get results quickly. However, it is often seen that the 
objective is not as low as wished. 
In this report the mixed integer programming (MIP) method will be used when solving 
the problem. MIP generally returns better solutions or at least if optimality is not 
reached, bounds are returned, which is very useful when evaluating the performance 
compared to meta-heuristics that has to rely on external information. Also MIP gives an 
insight into which constraint and variables that are causing any problems to be hard to 
solve – both when it comes to running time and when it comes to the value of the 
bounds.  
In practical settings the size of data can be huge and typically meta-heuristics are less 
sensitive to this compared to MIP solutions. This would indicate that when solving a 
practical problem, a good meta-heuristic would be to prefer if the resources like time 
limit are scarce.  
 
As the report is based on solving the problem in an academic sense, MIP will be used in 
order to search for optimality with the ability to evaluate any results by having 
necessary information.

6 Model 
 
To formalize the problem a MIP is formulated. The key part of the model will still be the 
bricks which all have to be scheduled. Compared to other models, like Tabulex, a brick 
will no longer be bound together with a specific teacher. Instead the Teacher Group 
parameter will be introduced. 
A Teacher Group is a group of teachers in which all teachers are allowed to teach a class 
in a subject. For example, a teacher group could consist of three teachers that are all 
qualified to teach 7th grade in the subject Math.  Instead of appointing a specific teacher 
to the class from the start, the data will contain a list of all the possible teachers that 
could teach the subject. From that list, different teacher groups will be created and the 
solver can then choose between these groups, who to appoint to the class. Hence, the 
class will be appointed a teacher group and not a specific teacher. 
For this to make sense another change to the initial part of the model has to be 
mentioned. This change is the alignment of all schemas for classes from the same year. 
All 7th grade classes will have identical schemas, all 8th grade classes will have identical 
schemas and so forth. In addition to teaching a specific class the teachers can by this mix 
the students from the same year across the classes to be able to do specialized teaching 
or other kind of workshops or even big lectures for all students at the same time. 
Aligning the timetables for classes on the same year will make the use of teacher groups 
more evident. A teacher group contains multiple teachers and will therefore be assigned 
multiple classes.

Figure 2 is illustrating part of how a schedule would look like. The image is showing the 
schedule for all classes at 7th grade. Monday morning the classes are scheduled to have 
the subject math with the teacher group number 1. Teacher group 1 consists of three 
teachers – in this case number 1, 2 and 3. This teacher group has been selected by the 
solver to be the one to teach 7th grade in math. The solver had the opportunity to select 
other teacher groups like teacher group 2 and teacher group 3. These groups all have a 
different combination of teachers which of can affect the overall timetable for the school. 
Likewise, in the example the teacher group 10 has been selected as the group to teach 
7th grade in Danish and teacher group 14 has been selected to teach 7th grade in English. 
How many teachers added to a teacher group depends on the number of existing classes 
on the same year. In the example there are three teachers added to each teacher group, 
which indicates that there exist three classes on 7th grade level, however, this is not 
definite. It is up to the single primary school to decide how many teachers is needed to 
cover the different grades and also it can vary according to the different subjects.  
 
The model described in this report will incorporate these parameters and requirements 
to solve the problem that is present in the primary schools. The formulations of the 
different constraints have been selected to be able to find the best solution the fastest.

6.1.3 
Constraints 
 
The following constraints have to be obeyed in order for a timetable to be feasible. 
 
§ 
Events: All events have to be scheduled exactly one time. 
 
§ 
Teacher Conflict: A teacher can only teach one class in each period. 
 
§ 
Class Conflict: A class can only be taught one subject in each time slot. 
 
§ 
Same Teacher Group: The same teacher group has to teach all events from the 
same event group. 
 
§ 
Allowed Teacher Group: Only a teacher group that are allowed can teach a 
specific event. 
 
§ 
Max Periods in Subject: A teacher can teach no more than a specific number of 
periods in a specific subject each week, given by TS_MAX.  
 
§ 
Min Periods in Subject: A teacher can teach no less than a specific number of 
periods in a specific subject each week, given by TS_MIN.  
 
§ 
Max Period: A teacher can teach no more than a given number of periods in a 
week, given by M_MAX. 
 
§ 
Min Period: A teacher has to teach at least a given number of periods in a week, 
given by M_MIN. 
 
§ 
Group Equal Events: Events that are part of the same event group will have to 
be scheduled according to a certain distribution.  
 
§ 
Outer Events: Some events, given by OE, should be scheduled in outer periods 
so that no other events will follow or precede it. 
 
§ 
Distribute Events: A maximum, given by maxSubOnDay, of events that are part 
of the same event group can be scheduled on the same day. 
 
§ 
Include Breaks: By including breaks in the plan we ensure that two equal 
events that have to be scheduled adjacently will not be interrupted by a break. 
 
§ 
Class Start: All classes should have scheduled lessons from the first period of 
the day. 
 
§ 
Idle Period Class: There should be no idle timeslots scheduled for all teachers. 
 
§ 
Idle Period Teacher: There should be no idle timeslots scheduled for all 
teachers. 
 
§ 
Preparation Period: Some active teacher groups, given by PEG, should have 
scheduled a preparation period where all teachers part of the group, can 
participate.

Objective Description: 
The solution to the problem is the most optimal feasible timetable that assigns all 
classes to a timeslot and with a subject and a teacher group, while complying all 
constraints. 
Following is a list of the objectives that will be used for optimizing the timetable. These 
are based on the soft constraints from Table 1: 
 
1. Minimize the number of empty periods between filled periods for teachers. 
If a teacher is teaching in periods 1 and 2, and also in periods 5 and 6, then periods 
3 and 4 can’t be free. 
Importance: Medium 
Penalty: 3 
 
2. Maximize the free periods for teachers in same teacher groups that is at the 
same time. 
Teachers from the same teacher group have to have empty periods at the same 
time.  
Importance: Low 
Penalty: 1 
 
3. Maximum of teaching hours for teachers 
A teacher should teach no more than a given number of periods each week. 
Importance: Low 
Penalty: 1 
 
4. Minimum of teaching hours for teachers. 
A teacher should teach at least a given number of periods each week. 
Importance: Low 
Penalty: 1 
 
5. Maximum of teaching hours for teachers in specific subject 
A teacher should teach no more than a given number of periods in a specific 
subject each week. 
Importance: Low 
Penalty: 1 
 
6. Minimum of teaching hours for teachers in specific subject = 1 
A teacher should teach no more than a given number of periods in a specific 
subject each week. 
Importance: Low 
Penalty: 1 
 
7. Minimize the number of free periods in the start of each day so that all classes 
optimally would start in period 1. 
A teacher should teach no more than a given number of periods in a specific 
subject each week. 
Importance: High 
Penalty: 5

6.3 Objective analysis 
 
The first part of the objective is a compactness requirement for the teachers. This will be 
thoroughly analysed and discussed in the following section about idle timeslots.  
 
6.3.1 
Preparation objective 
 
The second part of the objective focuses on the parameter preparation-period.  
 
When scheduling the events with teacher group instead of a specific teacher there is set 
for collaboration between the teachers being part of that teacher group. To have the best 
teamwork there has to be time to plan the work together. Being able to schedule a 
period for each teacher group in the timetable to get together if needed to discuss and 
plan the following weeks lessons would for most groups be beneficial. 
 
In the optimal timetable all teacher groups that are active (scheduled to teach an event 
group) should have a scheduled preparation period. The means that if a teacher is part 
of three teacher groups, then the teacher should have scheduled three preparation 
periods – one for each group. This secures that all teachers, part of the same group, will 
have time to participate in the preparation meeting.

6.5 Optimizing the Model 
 
Optimizing the model is an important job. Having the algorithm finding the optimal 
solution the fastest is depending on having the optimal model. There are different 
optimization tricks that can be done which then can be tested to check for better 
runtime of the model. 
 
It is hard to predict how the solver reacts to these changes in the model. By changing the 
number of variables, constraint, non-zeros etc. you can affect the optimization but how 
the solver is implemented and constructed to run is not something that can be changed.  
 
Trying to optimize the model is a matter of adding extra constraints that could help in 
making some constraints easier for the solver to handle. 
 
6.5.1 
Symmetry Constraint 
 
As an effort to optimize the solving time a Symmetry constraint was introduced to break 
the symmetry of the model meaning that the events from the same event group would 
be constrained so that they would have to be scheduled in a certain order. E.g. has 6th 
grade 7 Danish lessons which is represented by 7 identical events. The solver may try to 
switch two of these identical events to search for a better solution but will have the 
same results after the switch. In the end the solver may have wasted a lot of time 
switching events that are identical. The symmetry constraint was intended to create an 
order in which the events from the same event group were scheduled to eliminate the 
switching-of-identical-events time waste. The 7 Danish lessons would then have a 
number added from 1 to 7. The solver would then always know that the event with the 
order number 2 always had to be scheduled in a ‘later’ module than number 1. Likewise, 
event with order number 3 should be scheduled later than event with order number 2 
and so forth. The solver would then not have the opportunity to switch the identical 
events due the order number. 
 
The new constant ENG was created telling which number in the order the event should 
have.

6.6 Room Constraint 
 
The allocation of a specific room to each event is an important part of the model when 
dealing with University timetabling and High School timetabling. At universities the 
room has to be at the right size so that every student can fit into it.  
 
In Primary Schools in Denmark all classes have their own allocated classroom. The 
government has a set a maximum of the number of students in each class. Having this 
fixed number, the schools have designed their rooms to fit that exact number of students 
and by this the problem with class size does not matter.  
The room constraint for Primary Schools, however, is available for some subjects. A 
class will always have the opportunity to be in their own classroom because no other 
class is using it but in some cases the class has to be taught outside their classroom. 
Subjects like sport and science will often have to be held in specific rooms like a gym 
and a laboratory and then the schedule has to constrain these rooms to have no more 
than the maximum number of classes allowed.

The Room Constraint is considered elsewhere in the model as well. In some cases, the 
school will have specific rooms for specific subjects that do not fit an entire class. In 
these cases, the introduction of the outer events constraints can be used as part of the 
room constraint. 
Being able to fix certain events to be scheduled in outer periods brings the opportunity 
to schedule events that have to be held in these small rooms, so that the class can be 
split into two. One half can go home and the other half will have the lesson. The number 
of lesson for that specific subject will then have to be doubled so that there will be 
scheduled another set of events where the other half of the class can have the lesson.

7.2 Gurobi Solver and PC specifications 
 
Gurobi is using a linear-programming based branch-and-bound algorithm to solve a 
problem. This is done by removing all integrality restrictions from the MIP to get the LP 
relaxation. The LP relaxation is then solved and the result is analysed. If the result 
satisfies all integrality restrictions the we have the optimal solution. If not, the solver 
selects an integer-restricted variable to branch into two sub-MIPs. The same procedure 
is then used on these sub-MIPs to find a solution. The better solution will in the end be 
the optimal solution for the model. Gurobi is using different techniques when solving a 
problem. As described in the literature section there are several solutions that can be 
applied with different results. When solving a MIP, Gurobi uses presolve, cutting planes, 
heuristics and parallelism. Further discussion on some of these techniques is found 
under Literature Study, section 5. 
 
Gurobi has recently been updated to version 6.5 which should have increased the 
performance of the solver. Furthermore, the test has been performed on an Intel Core i7 
3.07 GHz machine with 12 GB RAM running 64-bit Windows 10 operating system.

8 Reflection & Discussion 
 
Looking at the results, the first thing that is noticed is that optimality is not reached 
within the defined timeframe. This indicates that the problem is not an easy problem to 
solve. The gap is above 75% for all the tests, which could indicate that the solution is far 
from the optimal. 
Analysing the results from the three different tests it is worth noticing that the solver is 
quicker to find a good solution when the preparation-objective is included in the model 
than when it is only the no-idle-timeslots-for-teachers-objective. 
For dataset 1 (Skovgårdsskolen) the solution found after 2.5 days when all objectives 
are included has an objective value of 188 and a gap of 77.7%, while the best solution 
after 2.5 days with only the no-idle-timeslots-for-teachers-objective included has an 
objective value of 221 and a gap of 87.8%. For dataset 2 (Borup Skole) the best solution 
found after 1.5 days has an objective value of 52 with a gap of 78.8% with the 
preparation-objective included compared to an objective value of 26 and a gap of 92.3% 
without it. The gap can be used here to indicate how good the solution is in relation to 
the lower bound found in LP-relaxation. A reason for this could be that the preparation 
periods can be used in the schema to fill an idle timeslot for a teacher. 
 
Also worth noticing is that the time for finding the first feasible solution is much better 
when having all objectives included compared to the test where the preparation-
objective was left out, for dataset 1 (Skovgårdsskolen).  
 
Real Life problem 
Looking at the problem as a real life problem, which it actually is, then optimality does 
not have to be found to actually have an acceptable timetable. The model is trying to 
minimize the number of idle periods for all teachers. This means that the solver is 
searching for a solution where there are no idle periods at all for any teacher. Even 
though it would be optimal to have a timetable like this the management of the school 
will accept a timetable where there in average is two or three idle periods per teacher. 
Another situation alike is present when looking at the number of hours that each 
teacher has scheduled. The model will search for a solution where the number will be in 
the defined interval but in fact the management of the school will accept an average 
deviation from defined interval for each teacher.

Running time 
 
There are many different constraints in the model and most of them could have different 
formulations. In this report some formulations have been tested and some of them have 
influence on the running time as shown in examination of the no-idle-timeslot-for-
teachers-constraint. In the model many specific constraints have been added to ensure 
that the result fulfills all requirements. Being able to exclude some of these constraint 
would of course have an influence on the running time. 
 
The aim is to have an algorithm that can solve the problem and return an acceptable 
timetable within a reasonable timeframe. Using MIP to solve the problem shows that a 
feasible solution can be found that is accepted as good enough. However, the time for 
the solver to find the solution could be improved. This points to the fact that MIP will 
have to be helped in the search for a solution. 
Using matheuristics could also improve the running time. Matheuristics (a contraction of 
mathematic programming MP and heuristic) is a combination of exact methods 
(methods capable of finding an optimal solution) and heuristics. This kind of methods is 
based on hybridization where a problem is divided into sub-problems to be solved. 
There is generally two ways of using matheuristics according to Boschetti et al. (2009). 
MP can be used to improve or design matheuristics and matheuristics can be used for 
improving known MP techniques.   
Using matheuristics could improve the running time by guiding the MIP to only search 
in a neighbourhood that consist of a small part of the solution space and then having a 
metaheuristic to control how what neighbourhood to search. When defining the suitable 
neighbourhood only a number of decision variables are left free and the neighbourhood 
is composed by all possible value combination of these free variables.

9 Conclusion 
 
The final conclusion is that the created model yields correct and useful results but have 
a too long running time that has to be optimized for the model to be used in real life 
cases. 
 
Some of the constraints created in the model may be formulated differently, which may 
make it more compact. However, the model is including all necessary constraint that is 
needed to create a timetable that will be accepted in a real-life situation. 
 
The report has a focus on the construction of a model that fulfil all requirements for 
making a solution that will give the best result. Different formulations of constraints and 
inclusion of extra constraint has been tested in the effort of trying to optimize the 
running time.  
Optimality was only found when excluding the constraint on idle timeslots for teachers, 
which is an indication of that this constraint is making the problem hard to solve.  
 
MIP has been used in the hope that the problem could be rather easily solved. The 
argument for using this is the ability to evaluate on solutions and by this always 
knowing how good the found solution is compared to optimality.  
 
The results have been compared to existing timetables where optimality does not need 
to be achieved and from this it can be concluded that the model is creating a good 
timetable. The size of the data has obviously a great impact on the running time and this 
may have to be improved for the model to be used in practice. 
 
It is relevant to bring the initial problem formulation to mind again: 
Is it possible to develop a simple model that can easily solve the primary school timetabling 
problem by introducing the new teacher group parameter? 
 
As shown and tested through the report, it is possible to create a timetable for primary 
schools that solves all problems and creates a fully acceptable timetable. The new 
parameter teacher group is making the problem more flexible and by this helping in 
easing the problem. However, the model cannot be said to be simple. There are many 
constraints in the problem that have very specific but also important tasks. These 
constraints could be left out to have a even more general model but the probability for 
the problem to generate an unacceptable timetable would increase. Having included a 
lot of constraints makes the problem harder to solve but will also ensure that the found 
solution can be accepted from the schools.

9.1 Future Research 
 
Making the model useful in real life situations would imply a reduction in the running 
time of the solver. In this report the Gurobi solver has been used to solve the problem 
but it may that another solver like a standard CPLEX solver would return better and 
faster solutions. Using a heuristic like matheuristics to find a feasibly solution faster 
would also be an obvious next step.
