Abstract
Size effect, which refers to the change in a material’s strength or behavior as its dimensions
change, has been observed in deep and slender reinforced concrete members without
shear reinforcement over an extensive period of time.
This research investigates the
capability of non-linear finite element modeling, specifically utilizing the DIANA software,
to accurately capture the shear size effect in such members.
It further compares the
effectiveness of different concrete constitutive models, encompassing a spectrum of brittle
to ductile behavior, while taking into account the influence of concrete softening.
In the initial phase, the study commenced with an in-depth analysis of a compressive
cylinder, employing various compression models to examine the phenomenon of concrete
softening.
Subsequently, to ensure the credibility of the program and methodology
employed, a comprehensive comparative analysis was conducted by revisiting the
renowned beam experiment conducted by Collins and Kuchma.
This involved a
comparison of the author’s findings, results derived from DIANA’s Verification Report,
and experimental tests. Moreover, earlier experimental studies on shear tests on slender
beams performed by Bentz and Buckley, and on deep beams by Walraven and Lehwalter,
were compared to the numerical results.
Finally, a parametric analysis study was
conducted, systematically increasing the height of the deep beam from a baseline size
to an unprecedented height of 10 meters, while keeping a consistent and geometrically
identical cross-section.
The main findings of this study, obtained through the analysis of over 140 final versions of
FE models, indicate that the parabolic compressive behavior model in DIANA effectively
captures the shear size effect, while the application of other compressive models does not
exhibit a size effect for both slender and deep beams. Moreover, the orientation of cracks
in the beams was found to significantly impact their behavior. Rotating crack orientations
were found to benefit slender beams, whereas fixed crack orientations were suggested
for deep beams exhibiting brittle shear behavior.
However, the analysis also revealed
significant discrepancies between DIANA’s predictions and the experimental data, raising
concerns about the software’s reliability in accurately simulating the shear size effect.
Based on the identified gaps and limitations in the existing knowledge, several future
research directions are proposed. Firstly, investigating pile caps capable of experiencing
transverse cracking would enhance our understanding of the behavior and shear size
effect in squat concrete members. Secondly, exploring alternative shear retention models,
such as aggregate-based, could offer a new approach for accurately capturing the shear
size effect.
Lastly, studying the influence of different shear slenderness ratios on the
shear size effect would contribute to how various parameters and shear spans impact this
phenomenon.
Keywords:
shear size effect, squat reinforced concrete members, non-linear finite
element modeling, DIANA FEA, concrete constitutive models, concrete softening, crack
orientation.

1. Introduction
1
Introduction
Concrete structures form the backbone of modern civil engineering, providing durable and
adaptable solutions for a wide range of applications. From towering skyscrapers to bridges
spanning vast distances, concrete structures are integral to our built environment. Within
this realm, a specific focus is placed on squat reinforced concrete (RC) members, which are
characterized by their relatively large depth compared to their span.
Understanding and studying squat RC members is of paramount importance due to their
widespread usage and critical role in supporting various infrastructure systems.
These
members are essential components of high-rise buildings, bridges, parking garages, and
stadiums, among other structures.
Deep beams are often employed in high-rise frame
structures with basements or underground parking areas to support the floors above and
transfer the loads to the columns, as shown in Fig. 1.1. In existing bridges, deep beams are
commonly employed in the bridge pier cap above the columns. The bridge pier cap plays
a crucial role in transferring the load from the superstructure to the bridge piers. However,
in some cases, particularly in older structures, these elements are classified as "without
shear reinforcement" due to insufficient reinforcement compared to the minimum shear
reinforcement requirements stipulated by modern design codes. Consequently, accurately
assessing the capacity of these structures and predicting their behavior becomes essential to
ensure their continued safe operation.
Figure 1.1. Buildings with deep and slender beams [1].
Non-linear finite element analysis (NLFEA) outperforms standard hand calculations and
linear structural analysis methods when studying and analyzing reinforced concrete.
The
ability of NLFEA to incorporate non-linear material behavior, geometric non-linearity, and
other essential aspects characterizes it. Using NLFEA, researchers are able to obtain precise
estimations and predictions of structural response, as well as identify potential failure modes
and crack patterns. The remaining sections in this chapter explain the study’s motivations,
research areas, key questions, and provides a concise outline of the thesis structure and
objectives.

1.1. Background
1.1
Background
The purpose of this study is to compare the DIANA FEA 10.6 (hereinafter referred to as
DIANA) program’s ability to accurately capture the shear size effect in both deep and slender
beams without the presence of shear reinforcement.
This includes, among other things,
investigating several non-linear compression softening models with properties ranging from
brittle to ductile, as well as the effect of crack orientations on the shear size effect. The objective
of this research is to identify which concrete constitutive model in DIANA best relates to the size
effect based on linear elastic fracture mechanics principles.
The importance of this research area stems from the extensive use of squat members, such
as deep beams and pile caps, in large-scale structures. As a result, it is critical to determine
whether modern finite element analysis (FEA) software, such as DIANA, can reliably capture
the size effect phenomenon. Numerous experimental studies undertaken over the years have
consistently proven that the nominal shear strength of beams without shear reinforcement
reduces as their size increases. Understanding the ability of non-linear finite element analysis
(NLFEA) software to effectively simulate and analyze this size effect is critical for guaranteeing
the structural integrity and safety of such components.
The choice of this research topic was motivated by the author’s university professor, who

This idea emerged from an ongoing pile cap

foundation for this research. Extensive laboratory studies on both deep and slender beams

understanding into the behavior of concrete members subjected to shear forces and size effect,
laying the framework for future research in this field.

Method

2. Concrete Modeling in DIANA
2
Concrete Modeling in DIANA
This chapter delves into the key parameters associated with concrete modeling in DIANA.
It examines crucial aspects, including various crack orientation models, behavior curves for
concrete softening in tension and compression, the impact of lateral cracking models on
compression strength reduction, and a shear retention model. Additionally, a comparative
analysis is conducted at the end of the chapter, investigating the variation in concrete softening
through testing a standard size compressive cylinder with different compressive behaviors.
2.1
Crack modeling
Discrete and smeared cracking are the two basic methodologies for modeling in plain concrete.
Discrete cracking is directly represented by an interface as a discontinuity between two pieces.
The crack’s behavior is then defined by the material model. Smeared cracking, on the other
hand, describes the cracked material as a continuous, anisotropic medium, i.e. no interface
elements. When the locations of the cracks are known, such as potential cracks in a notched
beam, a discrete model should be considered. However, smeared cracking is used when the
location of the cracks is unclear. This is the most typically used methodology, specially in
beams that don’t have stirrups [6].
2.1.1
Total strain based crack model for smeared cracking: Rotating vs Fixed
Total Strain-Based Crack Models is the major model for RC cracking issue in DIANA [11]. In
total strain (smeared) based crack models, there are two widely used models to analyze and
understand the behavior of concrete cracks, namely rotating crack models and fixed crack
models [12].
Rotating crack models consider that the principal stress directions change continuously as the
loading value increases. As a result, the pre-defined cracking directions also adjust accordingly
to align with the principal stress direction, resembling a rotating crack [11]. This approach
accounts for the rotation of the crack tip, allowing the crack to continue rotating until it aligns
with the principal direction of the strain vector.
Whereas in the fixed crack orientation, crack appears when tensile stress reaches fracture
strength and it is assumed that cracking direction no longer changes along with the direction of
principal stress once it appears [11]. Thus, it assumes the crack maintains in the same position
and orientation, and contains a shear retention factor.
In other words, The rotating crack model is employed when the crack propagation is implicit
and not readily known. On the other hand, the fixed crack model is used when the crack
propagation is evident and can be clearly determined. In DIANA, an alternative model option
called the "rotating-to-fixed" crack model exists [13].
This model operates in such a way
that when a specific user-defined strain is attained, the crack ceases to rotate and adopts the
characteristics of a fixed crack model.

2.1.2
Reduction due to shear behavior of cracks
Shear stiffness is generally reduced as the concrete cracks. As a result, in fixed crack orientation
models, the shear behavior must be modeled for smeared cracks in plain concrete with a
suitable shear retention model [6]. The shear retention model could either be constant or
variable depending on the crack width, where the shear stiffness gradually reduces to zero for
a crack width of half the average aggregate size [6]. Though the use of constant shear retention
is seen to be discouraged [6].
In Putter’s recent study [7], a damage-based shear retention model was found to be the most
suited for beams without stirrups. Though it must be noted that this shear retention behavior
does not take aggregate interlock into account. The damage based recalculation of the shear
modulus (Gcr ) is based on the reduced Young’s modulus (Ecr ) and reduced Poisson’s ratio
(νcr ) as per [7]. The decrease of Poisson’s ratio due to cracking refers to the reduction in
lateral expansion or contraction that occurs when concrete is subjected to axial compression
or tension. Under compression, concrete shortens axially but expands laterally due to Poisson’s
effect. Similarly, under tension, concrete elongates axially while contracting laterally.

Tensile behavior
An exponential-type softening diagram such as the Hordijk relationship or the exponential
softening, as shown in Fig. 2.1 is suggested for NLFEA of concrete structures [6]. The argument
is that an exponential-type diagram will result in more localized cracks and consequently will
avoid large areas of diffuse cracking, where cracks spread over a wide area. [6].
Figure 2.1. Non-linear tension softening curves of concrete [13].
The exponential softening curve shows a nonlinear relationship between the applied stress and
strain. The curve is generally flat in the first elastic area, showing that the material resists the
given stress with high stiffness and strength. As the applied stress exceeds the tensile strength
of the concrete, a crack starts to form in the material. At this point, the tensile curve enters the
softening area, where the concrete’s resistance to further deformation decreases exponentially
as the strain increases. This tensile behavior represents the material’s decreased stiffness and

2.2.2
Crack bandwidth
The amount of energy dissipated by the crack is determined by the element size. The crack
bandwidth hcr is one solution to this problem. Crack bandwidth is an important parameter in
the modeling of crack propagation in FEA [15]. It is the width of a small region, or "band"
around the crack path that is presumed to be cracked.
As shown in Fig.
2.1, the crack
bandwidth in RC structures can have a substantial impact on the exponential softening curve,
which represents the decline in material stiffness as cracks grow and propagate [16].
The crack bandwidth in FEA is determined by the mesh size and desired accuracy, impacting
stress distribution and model accuracy [17]. Smaller crack bandwidths yield more accurate
results but require increased computational effort, as concluded by many researchers such as
[17]–[19]. In DIANA, the determination of crack bandwidth, also known as equivalent length,
can be done automatically through two methods or by direct user input [13]. The two available
automatic methods are Rots’ element-based method [20] and Govindjee’s projection method
[21].
While Rots’ method takes into account the shape and other aspects of the element,
Govindjee’s method also considers the element aspect ratio and crack orientation [7], [8], [13].

Introduction
The aim of this chapter is to validate DIANA by reevaluating Collins and Kuchma’s beam tests
from 1999 [10]. The primary goal is to compare the findings in order to determine DIANA’s
accuracy and reliability through a comparative analysis of the results.
By replicating the
experimental tests and adhering to the guidelines provided in the DIANA’s software manual
[29] and online tutorial [30], the chapter intends to establish the validity and consistency of
the adopted methodology. The subsequent sections of the chapter are structured as follows:
an overview of the experimental setup and the test parameters utilized by Collins and Kuchma
is presented. This is followed by an explanation of the methodology employed to reproduce
the beam tests using the DIANA. Finally, a thorough comparative analysis of the findings is
provided, highlighting any discrepancies or similarities between the current study and the
original investigation. It is a four-point bending test of a shear-failing reinforced concrete beam
[29]. The beam is 5 m long and has a rectangular cross-section that is 500 mm in height and
169 mm in width. The beam has two top loading points and two bottom support points. The
beam has sixteen reinforcement bars.

4. Repeating a Classic Set of Experiments on Slender Beams
4
Repeating a Classic Set of Experiments on Slender Beams
4.1
Introduction
This chapter presents a comparative analysis of the results obtained from NLFEA of RC slender
beams without stirrups with those reported in experiments conducted by Bentz and Buckley
(2005) [4]. The study by Bentz and Buckley repeated a classic set of experiments originally
conducted by Bazant and Kazemi (1991) [2] on the size effect in shear of members without
stirrups. Bentz and Buckley aimed to investigate the size effect phenomenon using modern
experimental techniques and to compare the results with those of Bazant and Kazemi. The
study found that the shear strength of beams without stirrups decreased with increasing size,
highlighting the significance of the size effect. The studies are well-established references in
the field of shear size effect in reinforced concrete and provides a valuable benchmark for the
comparative analysis. The objective of this study is to evaluate the accuracy and applicability of
the NLFEA approach in predicting the maximum failure load and size effect of such beams and
to identify the key factors that affect the size effect phenomenon. The comparative analysis
aims to assess the performance of the NLFEA approach by comparing the modeling results
with the experimental data reported by Bentz and Buckley, which can inform the design and
analysis of reinforced concrete structures without stirrups in practice.
4.2
Beam properties and test results
In Bentz’s experiment, three beam sizes with specific cross-sectional properties were tested, as
depicted in Fig. 4.1 and Tab. 4.1. To create the finite element (FE) models, the two support
bearings and the loading plate were simulated as steel plates with a thickness of 30mm, length
of 50mm, and the width was kept the same beam’s. It should be noted that in both the FE
models and experimental tests, the dimensions of the bearing plates remained constant and
were not increased, which may not be a suitable approach for studying size effect in shear,
as the beams have geometrically dissimilar shapes that can influence the shear size effect
[31]. To address this issue, many researchers including Hasegawa (2001) [32] suggest keeping
the bearing plate size-to-beam height ratio constant for all specimens, with the bearing plate
dimensions increasing proportionally to the beam height.

4.3.2
Boundary conditions and loading
This shear size effect study disregards the self-weight of the beams. The restraint in the y-
direction is located at the midpoint of the two support bearing plates beneath the beam.
In order to maintain symmetry and equilibrium, a horizontal restraint in the x-direction is
positioned at the midpoint of the loading plate at the top, despite the absence of reactions
in that support. During the experimental tests, the beams were loaded in a displacement-
controlled testing frame, with a constant stroke rate [4]. Therefore, in this analysis, loading
is also applied through a prescribed deformation with increasing load factor in each step of
the structural nonlinear analysis. For this purpose, arbitrary supports are placed in the same
nodes as the loads. Technically, the reactions of these supports at the top are the sum of the
two reactions at the bottom and the total applied load. The loading is applied on the entire
top edge of the plate, i.e. a line load, with prescribed deformation of -0.05 mm. The boundary
conditions and loading of the supports can be observed in Fig. 4.2.

4.3.3
Mesh sensitivity analysis
The size of the mesh elements has a significant impact on the accuracy and computing
efficiency of a finite element analysis. Larger element sizes can produce erroneous results
since they are incapable of capturing local behavior and phenomena like stress concentrations
and crack patterns. Using a finer mesh with smaller element sizes, on the other hand, can
dramatically raise the computational cost of the study. As a result, a balance must be struck
between the element size and the needed level of accuracy.
In the present study, it has
been found that areas with large stress concentrations or potential crack initiation, especially
near the bearing plates where high compression and shear stresses occur, require smaller
element sizes to capture the behavior and produce reliable results. As suggested by [5], a mesh
sensitivity analysis was performed on all beam sizes utilizing the same concrete constitutive
model, specifically rotating crack models with GC = 10, wherein the results were compared
across three different element sizes: h/10, h/20, and h/30, where h represents the height
of the elements. The analysis aimed to assess the impact of mesh refinement on the beam
capacity. Interestingly, the results in Fig. 4.3 reveals that the capacities obtained with the
three different element sizes were relatively similar to each other, with minimal fluctuations
observed. Consequently, it was concluded that further mesh refinement did not significantly
affect the beam capacities. Thus, a mesh discretization was adopted as discussed in Sec. 4.3.4.

4.3.4
Mesh discretization
Mesh discretization was employed in order to strike a balance between computational time
and accuracy, allowing for efficient analysis without compromising the reliability of the
results.
This approach involved refining the mesh in regions characterized by high stress
concentrations or potential crack initiation, while using larger element sizes in areas with lower
stress concentrations or less likelihood of crack initiation. By implementing this optimization
method, a computationally efficient analysis was achieved without compromising result
accuracy. As shown in Fig. 4.3, the chosen mesh discretization strategy had a noticeable impact
on the capacity results, with a slight increase observed. This can be attributed to the higher
number of elements employed, resulting in a finer mesh in areas where cracks and stresses
are anticipated. Notably, the automatic meshing of reinforcements and bearing plates was
performed by DIANA. The specific mesh element sizes and discretization for the beams can be
observed in Fig. 4.4 and Fig. 4.5.

SBB3 - 1.0(40) 0.5(250). It is important to note that no stop criteria were employed, resulting in

4.6
Shear size effect - results and discussions
The shear size effect is expressed in terms of the variance of dimensionless shear strength
V/(bdfc) with effective depth d. Here, V represents the shear strength, i.e., half of the total
failure load, b is the beam width, and fc is the compressive strength of the concrete, which is
used to normalize the shear strength due to the shear-compression failure mode of deep beams
[3], [5], [31], [33], [34].
The shear size effect of Bentz and Buckley’s slender beams experiments is plotted using half of
the failure loads from the tests given in Tab. 4.2, and this is shown in Fig. 4.10 as a red line.
This indicates a significant reduction in the shear strength as the depth of the cross-section
increases, which is consistent with the size effect phenomenon. These results are compared
with the results obtained from DIANA’s models. For each of the three beam specimens, six
different FE models were analyzed.
In this study, it was found that the only concrete constitutive model exhibiting a shear size

the consistent and pronounced size effect observed in such beams. Conversely, for members
characterized by relatively smaller shear span-to-depth ratios, such as deep beams, fixed crack

5.1
Introduction
Walraven and Lehwalter [3] undertook a comprehensive research study into the behavior
of short beams loaded in shear through multiple laboratory experiments.
The study was
conducted across three distinct series of tests. The initial series of tests involved members
with constant dimensions but varying aggregate size. The subsequent series of tests were
conducted on members with varying dimensions, while maintaining all other parameters at
constant levels. Lastly, the third series of tests examined the effect of shear reinforcement on
the occurrence of size effect. The current chapter is focused on the second series of tests, which
evaluated the shear size effect of short beams loaded in shear. The results of this series were
analyzed and compared using NLFEM in DIANA. The selection of these five specimens in the
second series of tests allowed for a comprehensive investigation of the shear size effect across
a range of different beam dimensions.
5.2
Beam properties and test results
The second series of tests comprised five different specimens, the dimensions of which are
shown in Fig. 5.1 and Tab. 5.1. To ensure uniformity, a shear span-to-depth ratio of 1.0 was
maintained in all specimens, which ensured that the influence of beam size on shear behavior
could be isolated and analyzed effectively.
Notably, contrary to Bazant and Kazemi (1991) [2] and Bentz and Buckley (2005) [4], Walraven
chose to scale up the dimensions of the bearing plate in proportion to the beam height. The
ratio of the bearing plate length (k) to the effective depth of the specimens (d) was constantly
kept at k/d=0.25, indicating that the length of the loading plate was k=0.25*d. Furthermore, the
reinforcement’s center line was 40mm above the bottom side of the smallest beam and 70mm
above the bottom side of the largest beam.

Cracks
The predominant failure mode observed in all beams was primarily attributed to shear-
compression failure, characterized by the rapid propagation of a diagonal main crack from

5.5. Shear size effect - results and discussions
5.5
Shear size effect - results and discussions
The shear size effect results obtained from Walraven and Lehwalter’s deep beams experiments
are extracted from Fig. 10 in their work [3]. The data is carefully collected and plotted, resulting
in the representation shown in Fig. 5.6 where it is depicted as a red line. A comparative analysis
is conducted between the experimental data and the outcomes obtained from NLFEA from
DIANA.
Similar to the discussions on the size effect of slender beams presented in Sec. 4.6, the present
study revealed that the only concrete constitutive model demonstrating a notable shear size
effect is the one based on parabolic compressive behavior. Accordingly, for each of the five
beam specimens, four distinct FE models incorporating the parabolic compression behavior
were analyzed. The differentiating factors among these models lie in the assigned compressive
fracture energy values (10 and 33) and crack orientations, namely fixed or rotating.
The outcomes depicted in Fig. 5.6 and Fig. 5.7 demonstrate a remarkable similarity between
the parabolic constitutive models with GC magnitudes of 10 and 33. This observation indicates
that, unlike in the case of slender beams, the magnitude of GC does not exert a significant
influence on the size effect exhibited by deep beams. Instead, the orientation of cracks and
shear retention factor emerge as critical factors.
Notably, the models characterized by fixed crack orientations, illustrated by dashed lines,
exhibit a more pronounced and consistent size effect in comparison to the rotating crack
models, depicted by solid lines, which display fluctuations. This suggests that, particularly for
members characterized by a relatively small shear span-to-depth ratio (a/d=1), the adoption
of fixed crack models is more suitable, which is the opposite of the findings from the slender
beams where rotating crack models were more appropriate.
Both the outcomes obtained from the NLFEA and the experimental data corroborate the
presence of a size effect for the first four beam sizes.
However, a deviation from the
expected trend is observed for the largest beam size (h=1000), as the nominal shear strength
demonstrates an increase rather than a decrease.

6. Parametric Analysis of Deep Beams
6
Parametric Analysis of Deep Beams
In the context of analyzing the shear size effect in deep beams, this research encompasses
a comprehensive parametric analysis.
The analysis entails a systematic increase in the
dimensions of the beams proportionally, starting from a baseline size and progressing towards
an unprecedented height of 10 meters.
This surpasses the realistic limits of laboratory
experiments, considering that the largest slender beam previously tested in a laboratory was a
4-meter deep beam conducted by Collins and Bentz in 2015 [35]. However, this raises intriguing
questions: How will NLFEM respond to even larger short members loaded in shear? Can
advanced software tools, such as DIANA, predict the shear size effect in these significantly
larger deep beams? While the construction and experimental testing of such immense deep
beams may be impractical in reality, the primary objective of this study is to assess the
capability of NLFEA in DIANA to simulate and analyze the size effect under these extreme
scenarios. To ensure accurate and proportional scaling of the beam sizes, a reference beam
with dimensions of (hre f = 100) was selected. All other beam dimensions were scaled up using
a ratio of (h/hre f ). A total of 7 beam sizes were generated, each with a shear span-to-depth

to the center of the bearing support plate. Additionally, the distance between the center of the

6.1. FE-modeling
6.1
FE-modeling
To investigate the shear size effect of the beams under various concrete compression
constitutive models, a total of 98 FE models were generated and analyzed. These models
encompassed beams of different sizes, crack orientations, variations in concrete compressive
behavior, compressive fracture energy, and utilized different lateral cracking reduction models
proposed by V&C, as described in Sec. 2.3.4. The material models for concrete, reinforcement,
and steel plate used in DIANA are summarized in Tab. 6.2. For the concrete constitutive
model, an exponential softening tensile behavior was employed, as shown in Fig. 2.1. Various
curves, including parabolic, Hognestad, Thorenfeldt, EN 1992-1-1, and fib Model Code 2010,
were utilized to represent the compressive softening behavior, and their performances were
compared.
The boundary conditions, loading conditions and magnitude, mesh element
sizes, and mesh discretization were the same as previously described for the Walraven and
Lehwalter’s beams, depicted in Fig. 5.2, Fig. 5.3, and Fig. 5.4, respectively. For the structural
nonlinear analysis, a tangential regular Newton-Raphson iteration with a maximum of 100
iterations was employed. Line search was enabled to improve convergence. Load steps of
0.20(300) were used for the first five beam sizes, 0.25(400) for the 6th beam size, and 0.50(400)
for the final 10m beam. It should be noted that no stop criterion was applied, allowing for
early failure in the load steps. The analysis was manually terminated upon observing failure.
The energy convergence norm was set at 0.003. No divergences were observed in any of the
models. While a few non-converged steps occurred around the peak load, they were deemed
acceptable since the relative energy variation was closely monitored and managed to ensure
accurate results.

6.2. Cracks
6.2
Cracks
The cracking patterns of the deep beams analyzed in this study were similar to those observed
in the NLFEA results of Walraven and Lehwalter (Fig. B.2). Shear-compression failure was the
primary mode of failure in all beams. Concrete crushing near the loading plate was observed
in the 200mm and 500mm beam sizes, just before failure, after reaching the peak load.
6.3
Reinforcement stresses
Deep beams, as they increase in size, exhibit larger deformations and internal forces, leading
to wider crack widths. These wider cracks have a negative impact on the shear capacity of the
beams. The reduction in shear capacity arises from two main factors: diminished aggregate
interlock and decreased concrete contribution to shear resistance. The widening cracks in
larger beams result in a reduced ability of aggregate interlock to transfer shear forces, thereby
decreasing the overall shear capacity. However, it is important to note that in the FE model
and results, the effect of aggregate interlock is not considered in the shear retention model.
This omission means that the actual influence of aggregate interlock on the shear capacity
of deep beams may not be accurately reflected in the analysis. Consequently, the reduced
shear capacity in larger deep beams makes them more susceptible to experiencing shear failure
at lower load levels compared to smaller beams. As shear failure occurs in the beam, the
activation of longitudinal reinforcement in resisting shear forces becomes limited. As crack
widths widen and shear capacity decreases, the contribution of the reinforcement in resisting
shear forces becomes less significant.
This is primarily because the diameter of the steel
reinforcement is typically larger than the crack width, which means that the crack width is
insufficient to fully engage the steel before the concrete fails in shear. In other words, the
shear failure occurs before the reinforcement can provide significant resistance. As a result,
the longitudinal reinforcement experiences a decrease in maximum stress. This relationship
is demonstrated by the exponential decay observed between reinforcement stress (σs) and the
depth-to-reference depth ratio (d/d0) in Fig. 6.2. The analysis of larger beams suggests that
reinforcement stresses are typically minimal and may not be activated before failure. However,
in one model, specifically the smallest beam size (200mm) with GC = 35, the reinforcement
stress exceeds the yield strength. It is important to consider that this overestimation may be
specific to the FE model used in this scenario.

6.4. Shear size effect - results and discussions
6.4
Shear size effect - results and discussions
This study’s methodology was identical to that used in previous chapters to evaluate the shear
size effect in DIANA. Effects of crack orientation (rotating vs fixed) as well as several concrete
compression constitutive models (parabolic, Hognestad, Thorenfeldt) were investigated.
Furthermore, the compressive curves of the EN 1992-1-1 and fib Model Code 2010 models were
examined to see whether or not they captured the size effect. Furthermore, the study expanded
its scope by investigating the effect on different lateral cracking reduction models, specifically
the V&C models from 1986 and 1993.
Some of the results shown in Fig. 6.3 support earlier chapter findings, demonstrating that the
Hognestad and Thorenfeldt compressive models do not show any shear size effect. Similarly,
there is no size effect for the compressive models based on EN 1992-1-1 and fib Model Code
2010. As a result, these models are not suitable for investigating the shear size effect in deep
beams.
Whereas the parabolic compressive model was capable of capturing the shear size effect, which
was consistent with previous findings in both slender and deep beams. The investigation found
that a model with a high GC of 35 and rotating crack orientation significantly overestimated
shear capacity when compared to a model with a low GC of 10. Notably, the difference in shear
capacity between GC values of 10 and 35 was greater in the rotating than in the fixed crack
model.
Both the rotating and fixed crack models with GC = 10 demonstrated a satisfactory size
effect. The rotating crack model with GC = 10, on the other hand, showed significantly more
consistent behavior than the fixed crack model, which displayed small fluctuations in the shear
strength curve, particularly in the first three beam sizes. This finding contradicts the findings
from Walraven and Lehwalter’s deep beams, where the fixed crack model performed better.

7. Discussions
7
Discussions
Main Findings:
The application of the deformation load to a single central node of the loading plate caused
plate rotations and deformations, which led to early beam failure due to compression failure
beneath the loading plate. To prevent this, the loading was applied as a line load along the
entire top edge of the plate.
The parametric analysis conducted on very large beams highlights the importance of mesh
refinement and discretization.
Accurate estimation of the reduction in tensile strength is
crucial, especially for materials with exponential softening tensile curves.
Decreasing the
element size near the crack region effectively reduces the crack band width, improving the
accuracy of the analysis. Implementing mesh refinement strategies is essential for capturing
the behavior of such beams and ensuring reliable predictions.
It was found that the only concrete compressive constitutive model exhibiting a shear size
effect is the one based on parabolic compressive behavior. Other compressive models such
as Thorenfeldt, Hognestad Parabola, EN 1992-1- and fib Model Code 2010 do not show any size
effect for both slender and deep beams.
A crucial aspect of this study was the assessment of compressive fracture energy (GC) and its
impact on the results. A thorough investigation of the size effect and resulting changes was
carried out by including both small and high values of GC. Previous studies and experimental
tests cited in DIANA’s manual suggest that GC tends to be 50 to 100 times the tensile fracture
energy (GF), although modern guidelines suggest a value of up to 250 times the GF.
The selection of crack orientations in the total strain based crack model requires careful
consideration. Slender beams, in general, benefit from rotating crack orientation, whereas
deep beams, due to their brittle shear behavior, benefit from fixed crack orientation. When
the structural response starts with bending-like behavior (distributed cracks) and progresses
to a noticeable and localized shear failure, such as beams with a low shear reinforcement ratio,
rotating-to-fixed may be applied.
From Bentz and Buckley’s slender beam test study: Rotating crack models demonstrated
better alignment with the line of Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics (LEFM) in slender beams,
indicating their suitability for analyzing shear size effects in such beams. The influence of GC
on the size effect when using rotating crack orientation was observed. As the dimensions of
the beams increased, the magnitude of GC became a significant contributing factor, indicating
a stronger size effect for the series of beams with the lower GC magnitudes.
From Walraven and Lehwalter’s deep beams test study: Fixed crack models displayed a more
pronounced and consistent size effect compared to rotating crack models. The magnitude of
GC did not significantly influence the size effect when using fixed crack orientation.
Parametric analysis of deep beams: Models incorporating rotating crack orientation with high
GC did not exhibit any size effect for beam sizes ranging from 200 to 2000mm, which is
consistent with Walraven and Lehwalter’s findings. The best model among the series was based
on fixed crack orientation with low GC and a shear retention factor.

7. Discussions
Comparison with Existing Literature:
DIANA’s Verification Report used the Thorenfeldt model for the compressive behavior to
replicate the experimental tests on Collins and Kuchma’s slender beam [10]. However, the
present study revealed that the Thorenfeldt model failed to capture the shear size effect
under any circumstances.
This raises concerns about the suitability and methodology
used in DIANA’s work and questions why DIANA used it in their verification report instead
of the parabolic compressive behavior model, which effectively captures the size effect.
Consequently, one would question whether replicating and scaling their work using the same
method would capture the shear size effect.
In the deep beam example provided in DIANA’s Verification Report, namely Deep Beam B2.0-1
by Foster and Gilbert (1998) [36], DIANA utilized rotating crack models with high GC. However,
the present study’s results on deep beams concluded that such models did not capture the
shear size effect. This raises the same concerns again about the methodology used in DIANA’s
work and questions whether the shear size effect would be captured if one replicates and scales
their work using the same method.
Chen’s recent study [5] highlighted that beams with fixed bearing plate size exhibited a stronger
size effect compared to beams with proportionally varied plate sizes. His finding aligns with
the results of this study. However, some researchers argue that using geometrically dissimilar
shapes may not be suitable for studying the shear size effect, as it can influence the results [32].
In this study, the size effect observed based on Bentz and Buckley’s beams (fixed bearing plate
size) was greater than that based on Walraven and Lehwalter’s study and the parametric beams
with proportionally varied plate sizes.
Future Research Directions:
While considerable progress has been made in understanding the shear size effect in deep and
slender beams, gaps and limitations in the existing knowledge still remain. Future research
endeavors should explore the behavior of pile caps, as they share similarities with deep beams
but differ in their three-dimensional nature.
Investigating the effectiveness of DIANA in
capturing the size effect in pile caps would contribute to a more comprehensive understanding
of the shear size effect phenomenon in squat reinforced concrete members.
Future research should look into alternative shear retention models, such as aggregate-based
models, to gain a better understanding of the shear size effect. By conducting a parametric
analysis to examine the impact of these models on the shear size effect, researchers can gain
a better understanding of how shear retention models influence the analysis outcomes when
utilizing fixed crack orientation.
Additionally, future studies could explore different shear slenderness ratios, such as a/d = 1.5,
2.0, 2.5, and 3.0, to investigate their impact on the shear size effect in the parametric analysis.
This would provide further information about the influence of shear span-to-depth ratio on
the shear size effect.

8. Conclusion
8
Conclusion
In this study, the non-linear finite element modeling of size effect in deep and slender
reinforced concrete beams without shear reinforcement was investigated. The thesis aimed
to assess the ability of DIANA to accurately capture shear size effect. The research objectives
were achieved through a systematic analysis that involved comparing experimental data
with numerical simulations using DIANA, considering various concrete constitutive models
and crack orientations. The findings provide valuable information for structural engineers
and researchers involved in this field, emphasizing the importance of selecting appropriate
concrete constitutive models for accurate predictions.
The main findings of this study indicate that the parabolic compressive behavior model in
DIANA effectively captures the shear size effect, while other compressive models do not
exhibit a size effect for both slender and deep beams. Crack orientation must be carefully
studied. Rotating crack orientations benefit slender beams, whereas fixed crack orientations
are required for deep beams with brittle shear behavior.
The findings of the investigation revealed challenges in accurately capturing the shear size
effect in such concrete members using DIANA. Significant discrepancies between DIANA’s
predictions and experimental data highlight limitations in the software’s capability to simulate
this phenomenon effectively. These disparities raise concerns about the reliability of DIANA
in terms of shear size effect. Future research directions were identified based on the gaps
and limitations in the existing knowledge. These include investigating the shear size effect in
pile caps, exploring alternative shear retention models, and studying the influence of different
shear slenderness ratios on the shear size effect.
