ABSTRACT 
Despite the step forward that recirculating aquaculture systems have represented for the 
aquaculture industry, both in terms of production and environmental impact, they also entail 
some risks. Given the high organic loading and the high retention time of the water, they shape 
the perfect conditions for algae blooms to take place. Harmful algae blooms are especially 
critical because of their impact both in the production and in the environment. In this thesis we 
have explored the potential of different zooplankton species to control harmful algae blooms 
with the aim of reducing chemical usage in aquaculture. Moreover, we have studied the 
feasibility of implement this type of method in recirculating aquaculture systems. In order to do 
that, grazing experiments with two different zooplankton species, Daphnia magna and a species 
of cyclopoid, were carried out to determine the ability of these two species in controlling toxic 
algae growth. To determine the feasibility, the cladoceran was also exposed at different current 
velocities to determine its resistance and to check the effects of the water current on the grazing 
activity. In addition, algae were also exposed to peracetic acid in short and long-term 
experiments to study the effect of the chemical in the algae population and to compare it with 
the biological control. Finally, grazing experiments were also done in the presence of peracetic 
acid to test the effect on algae growth of combining both methods. Our results show that the 
two zooplankton species tested are indeed potential candidates for biological control. In fact, 
despite the toxicity of the algae both Daphnia magna and the cyclopoid species were actively 
grazing on the different algae and were able to reduce the abundance by inducing negative 
growth. They also show that while challenging in terms of water velocity, it is also possible to 
implement this method in RAS and it is indeed feasible in terms of required individuals. In 
addition, this thesis proves also that biological control is not only feasible but that it might also 
be more adequate, especially when algae blooms are caused by cyst-forming species.

BACKGROUND: Introduction to recirculating aquaculture systems 
Origin and development of aquaculture  
Aquaculture can be defined as farming of aquatic organisms both in coastal and inland waters, 
involving interventions in the rearing process to enhance production. Despite this relatively 
modern definition, aquaculture is believed to have started in China around 4-5000 years ago 
(Milne, 1972). It may have originated as a consequence of fishermen temporarily keeping their 
surplus catch alive in baskets, submerged into rivers or ponds, or from ancient practices of 
trapping fish that later on evolved from the classical “trapping-holding” to a “trapping-holding-
growing” process. 
Despite being an old method for producing fish, it was not until the second half of the last 
century that aquaculture started to gain importance. There are several historical circumstances 
that can explain this: 1) an increase in world’s population with the consequent increase in food 
demand, 2) an initial deceleration in catches with resulting stagnation since 1980s and 3) an 
increased concern for sustainability. World’s population has increased from a bit less than 3 
billion people in 1950 to around 7 in 2011 (FAO 2014), while fish consumption has 
simultaneously increased from 9.9kg per person in 1960 to 19.2 kg in 2010. Taking into 
consideration that the annual increase in fisheries since 1980 has been around 1%, it seems 
obvious that aquaculture has been, and still is, responsible for this increase in fish consumption. 
In fact, FAO (2014) estimated that aquaculture is and has been the fastest growing sector in food 
industry, increasing ca. 8.8% year-1 for the last 20 years (Fig. 1). Fish production is markedly 
heterogeneous country-wise. According to FAO (2014), Asia – and more specifically China – is 
leading the world production with around 89% of the fish and shellfish being produced in 2010 
(Table 1).

Aquaculture research and changes in production method  
The rise in fish consumption is probably the factor that has contributed most to the increase of 
the aquaculture industry. Nevertheless, this increase would have been impossible without a 
great advance in research during the last decades.  
First, improvements in fish nutrition have allowed for higher growth rates, optimized protein 
use and retention and enhanced condition of the fish (Davis et al., 2009; Oliva-Teles, 2012). This 
has led to a significant improvement in terms of quality standards of the farmed fish, as well as 
to the rearing of new species (Dalsgaard et al., 2013). 
Second, advances in genetics have greatly contributed to the increase in aquaculture. By 
studying the relations between genes and specific traits of interest it has been possible to 
establish successful breeding programs (Gjerdem, 1992). Although selection of traits through 
breeding has taken place since farming started thousands of years ago, the recent advances in 
genetics have boosted the possibilities in animal production. This knowledge does not only 
ameliorate many relevant characteristics – such as growth, tissue composition and colour, 
disease resistance or spawning time, to cite some – but also helps, through the improved 
breeding programs, to reduce the risks derived from the loss of genetic variation (Okazaki et al., 
2001; O’Malley et al., 2003; Reid et al., 2005). 
Third, advances in production methods have been extremely important for aquaculture 
industry. As for any type of food, there are two types of production methods: extensive and 
intensive and, as it is also in most of the cases, well-developed countries tend to have more 
intensive production methods. 
In aquaculture there has recently been a significant shift in the production methods, mostly 
taking place in Europe and North America but to some extent also in developing countries in 
Asia. The industry is going from flow-through methods to the modern recirculating aquaculture 
systems (RAS). Research on RAS technology has also been increasing in the recent years. In fact, 
Denmark is one of the leading countries in recirculation technology, especially in freshwater.

Recirculating aquaculture systems: Advantages and disadvantages  
The principle of RAS is the re-use of the system water after a number of treatments are applied 
(Rosenthal et al., 1986). After leaving the rearing tanks the water is first mechanically filtered in 
order to remove as many suspended solids as possible. Then, it undergoes a biological 
treatment: ammonia excreted by the fish is converted into nitrite and then nitrate in the bio-
filters. This is a key process because it converts a toxic compound into a relatively harmless 
product. Finally, part of the water can be treated with disinfectants or with UV light to help keep 
under control the pathogenic loading of the water. After this, the water re-enters the feeding 
tank (Fig. 2).  
 
 
 
Figure 2. Schematic representation of a RAS. Tanks are followed by the treatment units. It is important to 
note that the order of the treatment units does not necessarily have to be one presented in the image. 
Image by AKVA Group. 
 
RAS have some great advantages compared to the old production systems. To begin with, they 
do not rely on the seasonality of the natural water supply and, consequently, they do not depend 
on potential regulations under which natural water bodies are. Instead of deriving water from a 
creek or flooding a pond, groundwater is usually used as a water source. Many studies show that 
groundwater tends to have less pathogenic organisms (Madsen et al., 2008; Jørgensen et al., 
2009) and a constant temperature. The advantages are obvious: constant and controlled water 
supply throughout the year and less risk of importing a pathogenic organism (Lekang, 2007). 
The different types of treatments that system water undergoes allow and ensure a better 
control of the water quality. This is not a minor issue: higher water quality means less stress for 
the fish and better growing conditions (Bardach, 1987; Zhang et al., 2011). Consequently, the 
==PAGE:12==
 
4 
 
stocking densities have significantly increased compared to the old flow-through systems. This 
is one of the main factors that can explain the increase in fish production. 
Also, given the high degree of water re-use, in terms of water consumption, RAS are more 
environmentally friendly than the older systems. It is estimated that to produce one kilogram of 
fish, 30-60 m3 of water are required in flow-through systems, whereas in RAS, down to 3-4m3/kg 
or even less are needed. Moreover, because of the treatment units the effluent water is also 
cleaner (Lekang, 2007; Martins et al., 2010; Timmons and Ebeling, 2010). Further, the number 
of fish that escape from fish farms is lower in RAS than in the conventional systems (Boyd et al., 
2005; Buschmann et al., 2006), which decreases the genetic contamination of the natural stocks 
and prevents them to lose genetic variation. 
However, despite the step forward that RAS represent, they also entail some drawbacks and 
risks. One of the main issues with RAS is the high capital and running costs that they require 
(Schneider et al., 2006; Dalsgaard et al., 2013). Moreover, due to the elevated complexity of 
such systems, they are also very complicated to run, as opposed to the simpler flow-through 
systems, and usually require more experienced and skilled labour force. 
Furthermore, because of the water re-use, there is very little dilution of the system water, which 
leads to accumulation of waste products, despite of the treatment units (Lekang, 2007; Timmons 
and Ebeling, 2010; Fig. 3). These waste products are usually nitrogenous and phosphoric 
compounds that, together with other dissolved and suspended solids, create a high nutrient 
environment that benefits diverse microorganisms (Jeney et al., 1995; Ortega et al., 1995; 
Michaud et al., 2006). This high degree of recirculation results in a high retention time of the 
water in the system which, in combination with the high nutrient loading, creates perfect 
conditions for the growth of microorganisms, which in extreme cases can result in their mass-
occurrence (Guo et al., 1996; Madsen et al., 2008; Rodgers, 2008; Moestrup et al., 2014)

Mass-occurrence of microorganisms in RAS  
There is a number of studies which have documented a mass-occurrence of microorganisms in 
RAS. For instance, episodes with toxic algae have been described in two recirculating fish farms 
==PAGE:13==
 
5 
 
in Denmark using brackish and marine water. The species responsible for the blooms were 
Pfiesteria shumwaye and Luciella masanensis. Serious fish kills were reported in both cases and 
despite the treatment, one of the facilities had to be shut down (Moestrup et al., 2014).  
Another example is a study carried out by Oosterveld et al. (submitted manuscript) in which they 
monitored the algae abundance and composition in an outdoors tank, with rainbow trout 
(Oncorhynchus mykiss). Oosterveld et al. determined that temperature, ammonium, nitrate and 
the amount of feed supplied to the fish could explain 80 and 87% of the variation in the 
abundance of the two algae groups present in the tank (pennate and centric diatoms, 
respectively). Moreover, they suggested a differential and species-specific utilization of the 
different forms of nitrogen, which suggests that depending on the exact composition of the 
nitrogenous loading, some species will be favoured over others. This fact, that has also been 
pointed out for other authors (Loureiro et al., 2009; Domingues et al., 2011) as a mechanism 
controlling algae population dynamics in open water systems, may have important implications 
in the characterization and treatment of algae blooms in recirculating systems. 
Other documented cases of algae blooms in recirculating systems include shrimp farms where a 
non-toxic bloom was reported by Otoshi et al. (2003) and copepod cultures in research facilities 
(Zilliouz, 1969). 
A significant number of the pathogenic outbreaks are caused by heterotrophic or mixotrophic 
organisms. These types of organisms are in general more efficient at carbon, nitrogen and 
phosphorus uptake than autotrophic organisms under aquaculture relevant conditions (nutrient 
rich and potentially light limiting) (Zheng et al., 2012; Kim et al., 2013). This feature is key to 
understand their capacity to bloom or to cause an outbreak in aquaculture facilities and 
especially in RAS.  
Technical causes of microorganism outbreaks and their implications 
What triggers an outbreak of a microorganism is the combination of high nutrient loading of the 
system water and the high retention time of the water (Timmons and Ebeling, 2010). Thus, it is 
important to consider what causes this nutrient accumulation.  
This high nutrient loading observed in recirculating fish farms can be attributed to different 
reasons that more often than expected are related to bad managing strategies with an 
overfeeding of the fish and subsequent accumulation of uneaten food pellets (Dalsgaard et al., 
2015). Diet formulation of the feed can also have a big impact in the concentration of both 
dissolved and particulate nitrogen and phosphorus as well as in the particle size distribution and 
the carbon bioavailability in the make-up water (Bureau et al., 2010; Meriac et al., 2015). 
Consequently, mechanical filtration plays a key role when it comes to keeping the nutrient 
loading as low as possible.  
The drum-filter is the most common compound used for this purpose and its location should be 
a key decision when designing recirculating facilities: it should be located right after the rearing 
units to ensure maximum filtration of the suspended solids as there is a breakage of suspended 
particles into smaller particles that can then avoid the mechanical filtration unit. In fact, RAS are 
systems already dominated by small particles, typically Ø < 20µm (Chen et al., 1993; Fernandes 
et al., 2014) and an increase in the proportion of small-sized particles would thus increase the 
problem. Furthermore, the particles, mostly originating from uneaten food pellets and faeces, 
leak nitrogenous and phosphoric compounds (Dalsgaard et al., 2011; Dalsgaard et al., 2015) that 
can dissolve into the water and will therefore be available for microorganisms. Removing 
==PAGE:14==
 
6 
 
suspended particles as soon as possible diminishes the amount of nitrogen and phosphorus that 
will be released into the water.  
Further consequences of incorrectly placing the mechanical filtration unit and inefficiently 
removing suspended particles are that the latter can directly be used for microorganisms as a 
food source and that fish gills may partially clog or irritate, which represents an important source 
of stress for fish (Chapman et al., 1987). On top of that, organic matter also contributes to 
oxygen depletion, which can negatively affect fish and the bio-filter causing a decrease in water 
quality (Hanaki et al., 1990; Micahud et al., 2006; Pedersen et al., 2012; Rurangwa et al., 2015). 
Furthermore, having high levels of organic matter in the bio-filter can favour heterotrophic 
bacteria over the nitrifying species (Michaud et al., 2006). 
Location of the mechanical filtration unit is not the only technical detail influencing the number 
of suspended particles. It is equally important that the piping connecting the rearing units to the 
drum-filter helps minimizing particle breakage. If that is not achieved, pipe and pump 
malfunctioning as a consequence of blocking might occur, in addition to increased nutrient 
loading.  
Harold et al., (2010) suggest that it is not only that the high organic loading favours the outbreak 
of microorganisms that can potentially be pathogenic, but also that some of the components of 
a RAS such as bio-filter, or any other component where microorganisms can establish a biofilm, 
might offer shelter to some of these opportunistic agents (King et al., 2004 and 2006).  
Because of its nature, bio-filter is colonized by different species of nitrifying bacteria that convert 
ammonia to nitrate in a two-step process. These colonies of nitrifying bacteria are found under 
the form of biofilms, that is thin layers of microorganisms that cover themselves with an 
extracellular matrix mainly composed of some polysaccharides and proteins (Donlan, 2002; 
Harold et al., 2010) in order to create a microenvironment with optimal conditions that allow 
them to have higher metabolic and reproduction rates and that also serves as protection in front 
of potential competitors and external agents. If any potentially pathogenic organism succeeds 
in sheltering in these protective structures, biofilms could act as a potential reservoir of 
pathogens that would be extremely difficult to detect and eliminate (King et al., 2004; King et 
al., 2006; Yanong et al., 2010). Nonetheless, others (Micahud et al., 2006) suggest that having a 
certain abundance of non-pathogenic heterotrophic bacteria might increase the resilience of the 
bio-filter unit against pathogenic organisms.  
Similarly, another component that some authors identify as a contributor to pathogenic 
outbreaks are the rearing units and, especially, the dead areas in the inappropriate designed 
tanks, as well as the area of the tank right above the water level (Yanong et al., 2010). A well-
designed stocking unit should be to a great extent self-cleaning. That means that there should 
not be any dead corners where uneaten food pellets, faeces or any other kind of organic material 
can accumulate since this could create favourable conditions for heterotrophic organisms. 
In short, recirculating systems represent a key step forward in aquaculture. They are more 
efficient in terms of water re-use and more environmentally friendly than conventional systems. 
Further, RAS ensure almost a total control over the water quality and production cycle allowing 
for higher stocking densities. Nevertheless, due to their inherent characteristics they also 
present some disadvantages, such as creating favourable conditions for blooms of 
microorganism.

1. INTRODUCTION 
This thesis deals with alternative ways of treating harmful algae blooms in RAS. Aquaculture is 
increasing and so are doing its associated problems. Typically, the industry has used chemical 
products to fight blooms of all types of microorganisms. Nevertheless, environmental concerns 
are increasing all over and issues like worker safety are also in the agenda. In the present thesis 
an alternative to chemical products is presented and studied in terms of its biological and 
technical applicability in RAS. 
1.1. Harmful Algae Blooms (HABs) in aquaculture 
When talking about potentially pathogenic microorganisms, the most common organisms that 
are considered are bacteria and viruses. Less attention has been paid to algae blooms and 
particularly harmful algae blooms (HABs), despite them representing an important problem, 
both in fresh and saltwater and in natural and man-made systems. 
HABs have caused problems at a global scale and are known to cause severe impacts on aquatic 
living resources, both wild and farmed (Anderson et al., 2000; Sellner et al., 2003). HABs 
normally result in increased mortality of fish and shellfish, which leads to very significant 
economic losses for the aquaculture sector (Haigh et al., 2014: Itakura et al., 2014). Besides 
these effects on fisheries and aquaculture, HABs also have negative consequences for tourism 
and can even be toxic to humans, posing therefore a risk to human health. Consequently, 
increasing concern and interest have been arising both within the affected industries but also at 
a governmental scale (Anderson et al., 2002; Guo et al., 2014). 
In marine land-based aquaculture, problems caused by HABs have also been recently 
encountered (Moestrup et al., 2014; Oosterveld et al. submitted manuscript) but, despite the 
problems that HABs have caused in coastal environments, they have not been fully studied. 
Given the rapidly growing fish production, the risk of experiencing HABs in RAS can only increase. 
When it comes to the freshwater aquaculture industry, HABs have also caused many economic 
losses and RAS have not been an exception to the problem (Zimba et al., 2003; Rodgers, 2008). 
There are many recirculating freshwater fish farms that have suffered the consequences of a 
wide range of toxic algae species (Robin et al., 2006; Zhong et al., 2011; Houle et al., 2011)  
Increased mortality with the resultant economic losses is the main problem associated with 
HABs in land-based aquaculture. Nonetheless, not all the associated risks are so straightforward.  
Due to the presence of disinfection units in land-based systems, algae populations, as well as 
other microorganisms, are kept normally under control and they do not cause the problems 
typically associated with blooms (Harold et al., 2010). Despite this, and especially if the algae are 
toxic, they can still cause negative sub-lethal effects on fish (Brunson et al., 1994; Rodgers, 
2008), making them much more vulnerable and exposed to other pathogenic agents or stress 
factors. These negative sub-lethal effects of HABs can act e.g., through decreased visibility, 
changes in pH and, in general, making fish more vulnerable. 
It is also common that HABs – or even non-toxic algae blooms – cause diurnal fluctuations in the 
oxygen concentrations. The consequences of these variations are a source of stress to fish 
(Brunson et al., 1994) and may also be potentially problematic for the correct performance of 
the bio-filter. The resulting increased ammonia – or nitrite – levels if the bio-filter performance 
==PAGE:16==
 
8 
 
is compromised would lead to an increased mortality or, at least, a reduced growth as well as to 
more vulnerable fish (Svobodová et al., 2005; Barbieri et al., 2012). 
Besides the consequences for the production, the occurrence of HABs in RAS can have 
associated risk for the environment. RAS provides the perfect environment for the algae blooms 
to occur. Therefore, some species that can commonly be found in the wild in low or in non-
blooming abundances, in a RAS have the opportunity to bloom. Given the small but still existent 
degree of dilution, these algae can then return to the environment in higher concentrations. It 
is arguable that once the favourable conditions disappear, so will the bloom. Nonetheless, even 
a temporarily high abundance of toxic species can have an effect on the ecosystem, altering the 
natural community in terms of both species composition and ecosystem function (Brunson et 
al., 1994; Anderson et al., 2002). 
1.2. Current methods for water disinfection 
Recirculating systems are usually equipped with disinfection units, components of the system 
that treat part of the circulating water to reduce the pathogenic loading of the system. In up-to-
date RAS, there are three state-of-the-art methods: 
1) UV-light systems treat only a fraction of the total water volume. UV-light is harmful for 
living organisms because it destroys the genetic material and therefore kills the 
pathogenic agent (Summerfelt, 2003; Lekang, 2007; Timmons and Ebeling, 2010). Its 
two main drawbacks are the high operational costs and the low efficacy in treating 
waters with high organic loading as light cannot penetrate in turbid water (Timmons and 
Ebeling, 2010). 
2) Ozonation units also treat only part of the total water flow. Ozone is a very oxidant and 
reactive molecule and is effective in killing most bacteria and viruses and, in general, all 
kinds of living organisms (Summerfelt, 2003; Lekang, 2007; Timmons and Ebeling, 2010). 
Nevertheless, ozone needs to be generated – usually on site – and efficiently transferred 
into the water, which has high operational costs. Another problem with this method is 
that for the ozone to act it requires some contact time, which depends on the organic 
loading of the system and the concentration added. Given the high flows required in 
aquaculture, being able to provide enough contact time can be complicated 
(Summerfelt et al., 1997). Finally, ozone is not a target-specific product. This implies that 
the generated and transferred concentration is not the final effective concentration 
because part of the ozone is not reacting with pathogenic organisms but with the 
organic matter present in the water. On the other hand, oxidizing organic material also 
prevents microorganisms from using it as a food source (Bullock et al., 1997). 
3) Chemicals are the most used products in aquaculture, not only in RAS, as a method to 
disinfect the water. The most widely used chemicals are formalin, peracetic acid (PAA) 
and hydrogen peroxide. These products do not have a specific target. Instead, they react 
with any organic material present in the water, so same problem than with ozone is 
encountered in terms of effective concentrations. Other chemicals with algicidal 
properties that are sometimes being used to specifically treat HABs include ammonium 
sulfate (Barkoh et al., 2004), copper sulfate, and potassium permanganate (Dorzab et 
al., 2005; Barkoh et al., 2011). However, the required concentrations of ammonium 
sulphate to control certain HABs are likely to produce un-ionized ammonia in 
concentrations that can be toxic to fish (Barkoh et al., 2004). Moreover, despite the 
ability of copper sulphate in reducing the abundance of algal cells, it has no effect on 
their toxicity (Sarger et al., 2007) and it can itself be toxic to some crustaceans, further 
==PAGE:17==
 
9 
 
increasing the environmental risk (Boyd, 1990). Finally, the dynamics governing the 
decay in concentration of the different chemical products are very complex and depend 
on a range of factors, such as added concentration, time, concentration of organic 
matter in the water and salinity.  
The increasing trend in aquaculture production leads to a situation where more and more 
chemicals are being used. Besides the technical challenges explained above, the rising use of 
chemicals, sometimes in a very uncontrolled manner, poses a threat to the environment and 
counteracts the sustainable philosophy that recirculating systems inherently present.   
1.3. Alternatives to reduce chemical usage in aquaculture 
There is a need to find alternative methods to avoid the undesired effects of pathogenic agents 
while still reducing the amounts of chemicals being used in aquaculture. 
It is important that these alternative methods are able to accomplish the same function as 
chemicals: control the pathogenic loading and reduce the consequences of these hazardous 
organisms. Some of these alternatives are: 
1) Vaccination: consists of boosting and preparing the immune system of the fish so that 
when it encounters a given pathogenic agent the fish can fight it. This method is mostly 
aimed at rising resistance against bacterial and viral diseases. Better vaccines would not 
only help decrease the amount of chemicals being used to treat a disease outbreak, but 
also the amount of antibiotics. 
2) Breeding programs: researchers have already found additive genetic variation for 
resistance against almost all of the most common diseases suggesting that it is possible 
to breed more resistant fish (Okazaki et al., 2001; O’Malley et al., 2003; Reid et al., 2005). 
This would allow farmers to use lower amounts of chemicals to control disease 
outbreaks. 
3) Re-designing RAS:  RAS is a relatively recent and it is expected that improvements will 
be done in accordance with the increasing knowledge and environmental restrictions. 
For instance, location and type of the mechanical filtration, correct pipping and the 
needs of the bio-filter are key issues in RAS design. In the future, another factor to 
consider should be building smaller and more isolated units flow-wise, so that if an 
outbreak were to take place in a given tank, it would be possible to isolate the affected 
tank and the associated filtration units while keeping the rest of the farm operating.  
4) Biological control methods: in principal it should be possible to use natural predators to 
control a given pathogenic organism. Biological control is inspired by the bioremediation 
tools to restore natural water bodies, but it has never been investigated in aquaculture. 
Unlike the two first methods, it seems more likely to be used to control HABs than 
bacterial or viral outbreaks. 
This thesis focuses on biological control methods. There is a justified need to explore alternative 
solutions to chemical use that make RAS an even more environmentally friendly production 
method. Unlike vaccination and breeding programs, biological control methods have been until 
now overlooked as a tool that can help reducing the amount of chemicals used, while efficiently 
controlling HABs. 
In order to study the potential of biological control methods, a number of algae species 
belonging to different taxonomic groups that are known to have caused problems in aquaculture 
==PAGE:18==
 
10 
 
were selected (Zimba et al., 2003; Vogelbein et al., 2002; Barkoh et al., 2004), as well as two 
zooplankton species that could have the potential of controlling algae blooms.  
The potential of the zooplankton grazers and the feasibility of using such methods in 
recirculating systems have been studied in this thesis as a potential method to reduce chemical 
loading. This work contributes both to a healthier environment and to safer and more profitable 
working conditions for the aquaculture sector. 
1.4. Objectives of the Thesis 
The general aim of the thesis is to explore the potential of different zooplankton species to 
control HABs in recirculating systems. Additionally, the feasibility of using these zooplankton 
species as alternative to chemical disinfectants is tested with the aim of reducing the use of 
chemical disinfectants to control algae blooms in RAS by applying biological control methods. 
Apart from this overall aim, three separate and specific goals have been set: 
1) Test the potential of different zooplankton species to control algae growth by 
performing grazing trials. 
2) Compare the efficiency of biological (zooplankton) and chemical (peracetic acid) control 
of algae blooms. 
3)  Test some aspects of the practical feasibility of using zooplankton in a small-scale 
aquaculture system, for instance, the effect of flow rate on zooplankton survival and 
grazing.

2.1. Organisms

1) Microcystis aeruginosa belongs to the so called “blue-green algae”. It has been 
described to be a strict freshwater organism, mostly dependent on light and oxygenic 
conditions to carry out its metabolic activities. Nevertheless, it has also been found in 
eutrophic lakes living under lightless and anoxia conditions for periods of time.

vacuoles that allow them to stay close to the surface where light and oxygen conditions 
are typically better. It is known to form colonies that can contain millions of individual

caused problems in freshwater aquaculture. Already in 1993, Andersen et al. identified 
microcystin, toxin produced by the Microcystis genus, as the causative agent of a liver 
disease in salmon. Later on, Zimba et al. (2003) carried out a study in some catfish farms

2) Prymnesium parvum is a unicellular flagellate with a narrowly oval shape that ranges 
between 8-11µm in length. It is an euryhaline and eurythermal organism that has been 
described as facultative heterotrophic algae with preference for autotrophic conditions. 
In lightless conditions, Prymnesium parvum can utilize organic matter as a carbon food 
source and it can even graze on bacteria and smaller flagellates if phosphorus is limiting 
(Nygaard et al., 1993; Skovgaard et al., 2003). There is, nowadays, a certain degree of 
agreement in the fact that the toxic effect produced by Prymnesium parvum is likely to 
be caused by more than one compound. In fact, the produced toxins, prymnesims, have 
been described to have many different adverse effects on fish (Ulitzur et al., 1966; 
Ulitzur et al., 1970; Ulitzur 1973). Interestingly, some authors (Baker et al., 2007) 
suggested that toxicity might be increased with decreasing salinities as they represent 
sub-optimal conditions for growth, while others did not find any correlation between 
these two features; to the present date, no clear consensus has been reached with this 
respect. Furthermore, this species of golden-brown algae has also been identified as the 
responsible for the increased mortality of many different fish species (Guo et al., 1996; 
Barkoh et al., 2004).  
3) Pfiesteria piscicida has also been used in these experiments. It is a putatively toxic 
dinoflagellate with both flagellate and cysts stages in its life cycle, which has been 
described as very complex (Steidinger et al., 1996; Burkholder et al., 1997; Litaker et al., 
2002; Burkholder et al., 2002). Given the high number of stages that this species 
presents, the range of sizes is quite broad, going from 5µm the smallest zoospore to 
around 60µm for the largest tri-flagellate planozygote. In the present study, bi-
flagellated zoospores were around 7-8µm. The different life stages have different 
nutrional modes, being mixotrophy the dominant, especially in flagellated stages. It has 
been reported that to a great extent, the life-stage is dependent on the type and 
abundance of food source (Glasgow et al., 1995; Burkholder et al., 1997) and that the 
feeding method is based on the use of a peduncle with which Pfiesteria sucks up its prey. 
The feeding behaviour is thought to be governed by chemosensory cues that can trigger 
the changes in life-cycle from resting cysts to active “hunting” flagellated forms. This 
species is particularly difficult to control and to completely eradicate from the water, 
precisely because of its life-cycle complexity, and especially due to its capacity to form 
resistance cysts under unfavourable conditions. Furthermore, there is abundant 
literature reporting the toxicity of this species and other close relatives of the same 
genus, both in the wild and in fish farms (Glasgow et al., 1995; Burkholder et al., 1997; 
Vogelbein et al., 2002; Moestrup et al., 2014). To the present day, the exact compound 
or compounds of the toxin remain unknown and there is not an agreement on how 
exactly the toxin works: some authors suggest that Pfiesteria cells release a toxin that 
preys somehow uptake, others think that the toxin is released into the prey when the 
peduncle is injected. Nevertheless, it seems clear that its toxicity depends on the life-
stage and that densities as low as 250-300 cells/mL are enough to cause massive 
mortalities (Burkholder et al., 1997).

4) Scenedesmus oblicus has been used as a control food source for Daphnia magna. It 
belongs to the class of chlorophyceae and it is a freshwater, non-toxic, autotrophic 
organism that can nevertheless survive for periods of time in lightless conditions. 
Although it can be found as individual cells of around 3-5µm, it is very commonly found 
forming colonies of 4 or 8 cells, also known as coenobiums. There are many factors that 
can trigger the formation of this coenobia, being the presence of predators the most 
common. Moreover, some species also have the capacity to produce spine-like 
structures aimed at further reducing the grazing pressure.  
5) Rhodomonas salina has been used as a control food source for the harpicticid copepod 
used in this study as well as to feed Pfiesteria piscicida. It is a full-salinity species that, 
as many other species of the class of chryptophyceae, is considered mixotrophic given 
its ability to feed on bacteria if nitrogen or phosphorus are limiting. They tend to be 
around 6-7µm in size and to have an oval shape and two flagella.  
6) Daphnia magna: freshwater cladoceran also known as water flea. It is a filter feeder that 
has a size of 4-5mm once it reaches the adult size. This species has been widely use in 
the laboratory experiments because of the ease of culture and due to the fact that it 
presents a relatively short life-span and displays high reproduction rates. Nevertheless, 
it has been chosen based on one main reason: it has been described to feed on a vast 
variety of algae including some that are highly toxic (Kersting et al., 1976; Barros et al., 
2001; Degans et al., 2002). Their feeding activity relies on the phylopods, some flattened 
leaf-like legs that produce a small water current. This current passes through some 
gathering apparatus that brings the food particles into the food groove. This type of 
feeding activity is highly convenient for the biological control studied in this thesis 
because it means that Daphnia magna is not very selective with the food. If it was, then 
it could easily refuse to eat certain types of toxic algae. In fact, this species displays the 
classical type 2 functional response where the feeding rate is proportional to the food 
concentration below a certain threshold. Above this threshold, feeding rate decreases 
until it becomes constant because filtering rate decreases, also to some extent, with 
increasing food concentration (Porter et al., 1982). 
7) Cyclopoid copepod species: as explained in the Method section below, some copepods 
were found living in the bio-filter of some of the recirculating systems that DTU AQUA 
has in Hirtshals. Although the copepods resembled Oncaea sp., no taxonomical 
characterization was carried out. Consequently, the species has been treated as a 
member of order Cyclopoid.

2.3. Grazing Experiments  
This type of experiments has been the basis of the work carried out in this thesis (Table 4). In 
order to be able to compare between zooplankton species and food sources, the starting algae 
concentration was always 400µg C·L-1, which is slightly above the saturation point of ingestion 
of most zooplankton species (Kersting et al., 1976; Lampert et al., 1980; Porter et al., 1982). The 
number of zooplankton individuals was set to obtain a reduction of ca. 30% from the start 
concentration. This was done to ensure that the food would not be limiting during the 
experiments, while at the same time a 30% reduction would be sufficient to detect the grazing 
signal from the background noise. Both zooplankton species were assumed to eat 50% of their 
carbon content on a daily basis. 
All grazing experiments followed the same principal set up, based on measuring the 
disappearance of algae cells in experiments containing zooplankton compared to controls 
containing only algae (Frost, 1972). At the start of each experiment a solution was prepared with 
a cell concentration giving 400µg C·L-1. Once the solution was ready, the bottles were filled to 
the top, the zooplankton was added and the bottles were sealed with parafilm and with the lit. 
In the experiments with Daphnia, the bottles were then brought to a plankton wheel and left 
rotating for 24 hours. When the cyclopoid was used, the bottles were left standing. In both cases 
experiments were carried out in the dark. Volumes of the bottles and number of replicates can 
be seen in Table 4. After 24 hours, the zooplankton was sieved out and their condition was 
checked. Then, 2% Acid Lugol solution was used to fix the samples that were afterwards counted 
in an inverted microscope using a Sedgewick chamber. In all cases, a minimum of 250 cells were 
counted. Furthermore, the body length of some Daphnia individuals was measured. Average 
body length was ca. 4.5mm.

2.4.1. Staining  
Unlike normal grazing experiments, where by cell counting it is possible to estimate the grazing 
activity of a given zooplankton species, in peracetic acid exposure tests dead cells remain in the 
sample. Therefore, it is not possible to count cells at the end of the experiment because that 
would account for both dead and living cells. Consequently, a staining method to differentiate 
living from dead individuals was developed and tested. 
In this regard, a dye exclusion method was used. The selected stain was Trypan Blue solution 
0.4% from Sigma-Aldrich. This stain is normally used in cell proliferation assays and cytotoxicity 
tests and it only stains the dead and significantly damaged cells without affecting the living ones.  
However, this stain is typically used in animal cells and despite the fact that there are some 
studies that have used it with dead vegetal cells (Lebour 1925; Jones et al., 1977; Keogh et al., 
1980; Koch et al., 1990), there was no certainty that it would also work with the species used in 
the present study. Therefore, tests were carried out to ensure the quality of the method.  In 
these tests, Pfiesteria cells, in a concentration giving 400µg C·L-1, were exposed to increasing 
levels of peracetic acid. The stain was added after ca. 30 minutes of the addition of the chemical, 
to avoid interaction between the stain and the acid. In order to add the stain, the following 
developed protocol was used: a 6mL subsample of the experimental bottle was taken and 
Trypan Blue 0.4% in a 1/10 dilution (final volume = 1mL) was added. After gently mixing and 
once a dark blue colour was obtained, the counting chamber was loaded (1mL) and let it stand 
for 1-2 minutes before counting. Number of dead cells was then checked in an inverted 
microscope using. 
Staining appeared to work well, and it was possible to visually distinguish between living and 
dead cells (Fig. 10 and pictures below). Moreover, a conservative approach was taken by only 
including cells which were clearly stained as dead ones and by individually checking each cell to 
rule out that any damaged but still living cell could be counted as dead.

2.5. Flow Tests  
A special set-up was made for these types of experiments, simulating a small-scale recirculating 
system. As can be seen in Figure 4, the set-up consisted of a cylindrical 0.135m diameter tank of 
6.5L from the bottom to the overflow. From the overflow located at the top of the tank the 
water was going through a 6L reservoir to be pumped back into the tank through an adjustable 
recirculation pump (TUNZE 1073.008). A mesh was added in the outlet of the tank to prevent 
Daphnia from entering the reservoir and the pump. Two types of experiments were carried out: 
1) Short-term exposure to determine the maximum water velocity Daphnia magna can resist 
without getting flushed out (Table 8) and 2) 24-hour grazing experiment to study the effect of 
water velocity on the grazing activity of Daphnia magna (Table 9). 
In both cases, to begin the experiments both the tank and the reservoir were filled to the top 
and the system was started to check for normal functioning. If no malfunctioning was detected, 
the system was then stopped. In the short-term exposure, 10 individuals of Daphnia of ca. 
4.5mm were added in the middle of the tank. In order to minimize potential stress from the 
handling, the pump was turned on after ca. 10min after the addition of the animals. The pump 
==PAGE:30==
 
22 
 
was turned off after 1 hour and the individuals were sieved out of the tank and the mesh and 
counted. They were also checked for condition. 
In the 24-hour grazing experiment, after checking for malfunctioning of the system, the solution 
with algae (to give 400µg C·L-1) was prepared and added to the tank – previously a volume like 
that of the algae solution had been removed from the tank –. Once the algae were in the tank, 
the pump was turned on for ca. 10min to get a uniform distribution of the algae in the system 
and was then turned off. After 5min Daphnia were added in the middle of the tank and the pump

2.6. Peracetic acid degradation tests 
These tests were performed with the aim of building a decay curve that would allow for a better 
understanding about how the peracetic acid reacts and therefore disappears in freshwater and 
5 psu water given an organic loading of 400µg C·L-1. This analysis relies on the formation of an 
unstable colour complex between peracetic acid and two reagents, N,N-diethyl-p-
phenylenediaminesulphate salt and potassium iodide buffer solution (Falsanini et al., 2006). 
2.5mL of sterile-filtered water sample are added into a 10mL cuvette prior to the addition of 
reagent 1 and 2. After gently mixing, the colour intensity is measured using a spectrophotometer 
at 550nm for 20 seconds. The algae used was Scendesmus sp. and the peracetic acid 
concentration tested was 1.5mg PAA·L-1. The procedure to start the experiment is very similar 
to the 24-hour exposure to peracetic acid: a solution containing 400µg C·L-1 was prepared for 
both salinities and added into 3 bottles of 10mL each. Then the peracetic acid was added and 
the samples were taken as explained above. 
Nevertheless, before the decay curves were constructed, tests to make the standard curves 
were carried out. In these tests, known concentrations of peracetic acid were added to MiliQ 
water and a mix of MiliQ water with sterile-filtered seawater giving a salinity of 5psu. Following 
the same procedure described above, absorbance was measured and the standard curve 
constructed as shown in Figure 5. The equations for the below presented standard curves are: y 
= 0.3299x + 0.0457 with and R² = 0.9985 for freshwater and y = 0.2859x - 0.0232 and R² = 0.961 
for the water at 5psu.

average values for each concentration.

2.7. Data processing and statistical analysis  
The data from the experiments has been processed to obtain all the relevant parameters as 
suggested by Frost (1972). To process the data, produce the plots and carry out the statistical 
analysis RStudio Version 0.98.501 has been used.  
Regarding the statistical analysis, one-way ANOVA tests have been used to detect significant 
differences between the different levels of a factor with a significance level of α = 0.05. The 
residuals appeared to follow standard normal distribution. 
When ANOVA results showed significant differences, multiple comparison Tukey Tests were 
performed with the same significance level as for the ANOVA to detect where were the 
difference in the means of the levels of a given factor. By using the Tukey Test, the increasing 
probability of declaring a significant difference when it is not in fact present (Error Type 1) that 
would arise from performing multiple t-tests is avoided.  
To compare between means and means to numbers, usual t-tests for comparing samples of 
unequal sizes and variances – which also contains the case of equal sizes and variances – were 
performed. The test statistic t is compared to the T distribution with the respective degrees of 
freedom with the usual level of significance (α = 0.05).

3. RESULTS 
3.1. Testing the potential of Daphnia magna and a cyclopoid species to control HABs 
Both Daphnia magna and the copepod species were able to reduce the concentration of the 
different algae sufficiently to inflict negative growth rate of the algae, displaying therefore 
potential to control algae population dynamics (Fig. 6). For the experiments done with Daphnia 
magna, there was a significant difference in the growth rates of algae in the presence of Daphnia 
(1-way ANOVA, F4 = 0.00213, p < 0.05), although the growth rate of the control food 
Scenedesmus showed no significant differences from any of the other food sources. However, 
Prymnesium displayed a significantly lower growth rate than Pfiesteria, Microcystis and 
Scenedesmus at 5psu (Tukey HSD; p < 0.05, Table 1 – Appendix 1). Experiments with the 
cyclopoid indicated that the copepod caused a significantly higher reduction in growth when 
feeding on the control food Rhodomonas and on Prymnesium than when feeding on Microcystis 
and Pfiesteria (1-way ANOVA, F3= 0.00304, p < 0.05 Tukey HSD; p < 0.05, Table 1 – Appendix 1).  
The two controls, Scenedesmus in freshwater and at 5psu, showed small but significant 
differences in terms of clearance rate (Tukey HSD; p < 0.05, Table 1 – Appendix 1) indicating a 
potential (but small) effect of the salinity increase in feeding rate of Daphnia (Fig. 6). 
Clearance rate for Daphnia did not seem to follow the same trend than the algae growth: there 
were differences depending on the food source (1-way ANOVA, F4 = 4.09e-13, p < 0.05). The 
clearance rate of Daphnia feeding on Microcystis (2.65 mL ind.-1 h-1) was significantly higher than 
the other diets (Tukey HSD; p < 0.05, Table 1 – Appendix 1). Scendesmus in freshwater and 
Prymnesium caused similar and intermediate feeding rates (2.07 and 1.93 ml ind-1 h-1, 
respectively) with the lowest clearance rate observed for Daphnia feeding on Scenedesmus at 
5psu (1.62 mL ind.-1 h-1) and Pfiesteria (1.48 mL ind.-1 h-1) showing no significant differences from 
each other (Fig. 6).  
Clearance rate of the cyclopoid also followed a different trend than algae growth. The clearance 
rate of every food source was significantly different from others, Rhodomonas exceeding 
Microcystis, exceeding Prymnesium, with the lowest rate observed when feeding on Pfiesteria 
(1-way ANOVA, F3 = 1.83e-14, p < 0.05; Tukey HSD; p < 0.05, Table 1 – Appendix 1). The clearance 
rate with Pfiesteria was ca. 40% from that on Rhodomonas (Fig. 1). The clearance rate of the 
cyclopod varied from 0.48 to 0.2, being on average ca. 15 % from that of Daphnia. 
Figure 6 also shows the weight-specific carbon ingestion. For both zooplankton species, the 
weight-specific ingestion followed the same trend than the clearance rate: when feeding on 
Microcystis, Daphnia displayed the significantly highest value for carbon ingestion (0.012 (µg C) 
(µg C)-1h-1). Following Microcystis there was Prymnesium and Scenedesmus in freshwater – not 
significantly different from each other, 0.0091 and 0.0084 (µg C) (µg C)-1h-1, respectively (1-way 
ANOVA, F4 < 2e-16, p < 0.05; Tukey HSD; p < 0.05, Table 1 – Appendix 1) – and finally Scenedesmus 
at 5psu, that was significantly lower than ingestion on freshwater Scenedesmus but not 
significantly lower than ingestion on Pfiesteria (0.0075 and 0.0067, respectively). Typically, 
Daphnia ingested ca. 25% of its body weight day1 (Fig. 6). 
For the cyclopoid, carbon ingestion was the highest for Rhodomonas (0.079 (µg C) (µg C)-1h-1), 
followed by Microcystis, Prymnesium and Pfiesteria (0.063, 0.046 and 0.033 µg C (µg C)-1 h-1, 
respectively; 1-way ANOVA, F3 < 2e-16, p < 0.05; Tukey HSD; p < 0.05, Table 1 – Appendix 1). In

3.2.3. Long-term experiments with peracetic acid 
Three long-term (5 days) experiments were carried out to test the effect of PAA on algae growth. 
The difference in these experiments was the dose of peracetic acid used. In the first, the 
Pfiesteria solution was spiked with 1.5mg PAA·L-1 at the beginning of the experiment (Pulse-1.5), 
in the second one, the same dose was added on a daily basis (Repeated daily-1.5) and finally a 
knock-out experiment was carried out with an initial dose 5x higher than the minimum 
concentration identified in the previous experiment (Pulse-7.5). During the experiment, 
numbers of living and dead cells were monitored together with the number of cysts (Fig. 11). 
In all the treatments the number of living cells tended to decrease with time whereas the 
number of dead cells consequently increased. The cysts followed a similar pattern as the dead 
cells (Fig. 11). 
The Pulse-1.5 treatment was not enough to kill all the algae cells. In this treatment, the major 
decrease in the number of living cells took place in the first two hours after the addition of the 
peracetic acid; in fact, the number of living cells at 24h was higher than at 2h. After 24h, the 
number of living cells started to decrease again, probably as a result of starvation. The opposite 
trend was observed for dead cells: an important increase in the number of dead cells in the first 
two hours was followed by a levelling off at 24h. After this point, the number of dead cells 
remained constant. Cysts followed a similar pattern than dead cells for the first 24h. After this 
point, they continued to increase, which is consistent with the trend observed for the living cells. 
Between 2 and 24h, there was a decrease in the number of cysts that could explain the increase 
observed in the living cells. 
After 5 days, the experiment was stopped and Rhodomonas salina was added into the bottles 
to mimic a change from unfavourable to favourable conditions and to see whether or not the 
algae could increase again. After 2 days of the addition of Rhodomonas there was an increase of 
around 65% in the number of living cells and a similar decrease was observed for cysts (Fig. 11). 
When peracetic acid was added daily, the same pattern than for Pulse-1.5 was observed with 
no significant differences in cell numbers after 24 hours. However, after the third addition, all 
cells appear to be dead (t = 72.5h).

4. DISCUSSION 
My results showed that the two zooplankton species tested have the potential to control 
harmful algae blooms. They could actively graze on the different algae species despite their 
different toxins. Grazing alone was in the same range of efficiency than commonly used 
concentrations of peracetic acid in terms of inflicting negative growth rates. In the case of 
Pfiesteria, peracetic acid treatment did not fully eliminate the algae population, which might 
bloom again after the peracetic acid has degraded. Therefore, using a biological control might 
more suitable since zooplankton could also eliminate the cysts. 
In terms of feasibility, I showed that using Daphnia magna’s grazing activity as a control 
mechanism of algae growth is a plausible alternative given the relatively low Daphnia densities 
required to inflict negative algal growth. Regarding the technical applicability, I identified a 
threshold concerning water velocity as higher water velocities lead to decreasing grazing 
activities. This will help to improve the implementation of biological control as a solution to 
control algal growth in aquaculture. 
In this section I discuss these results with the aim of providing a deeper understanding of the 
methodological, biological and technical mechanisms underlying the reported findings. 
4.1. Biological Issues 
4.1.1. Feeding of toxic algae  
The first aim of the thesis was to investigate the biological interactions between the different 
algae and zooplankton species with the final objective to determine the potential of the tested 
zooplankton species to control harmful algae blooms. 
Results show that both zooplankton species were able to supress growth to all the different 
algae (Fig. 6).  
4.1.1.1. Daphnia magna 
Regarding Daphnia, the results showed that there had been grazing activity with all the food 
sources (Fig. 6). Daphnia typically had highest clearance rate on Microcystis, irrespective of the 
toxin production, which is dependent on culture conditions (Song et al., 1998) and increased 
when the algae is exposed to grazers (Jang et al., 2003). This high clearance rate may be due to 
the fact that this species was the fastest grower (ca. 2x faster than the rest). Consequently, it is 
also logic that carbon ingestion was also the highest and growth reduction the lowest. 
There is one important consideration to make regarding Microcystis: in natural occurring algae 
blooms it has been reported to form colonies (Xie et al., 2001; Ma et al., 2009). Nevertheless, in 
culture, it has never been observed to form these colonies (Dr. Gert Hansen, personal 
communication – SCCAP). During the experiments it was never seen to form colonies either. This 
is important to take into account because colony formation might impair or complicate 
Daphnia’s grazing activity (DeMott et al., 2001). There is no information on whether or not this 
species forms colonies in aquaculture systems. 
No mortality was observed in the experiments with Microcystis (Fig. 7), which is in agreement 
with other studies such as the one carried out by Thostrup et al. (1999), where the authors 
report no mortality in in vitro studies although high mortality in in situ experiments. Barros et 
al. (2001) also reported very low sensitivity of Daphnia magna to the toxins of Microcystis. 
==PAGE:50==
 
42 
 
However, the results are in contrast with others like Ferrãro-Filho et al. (2000) where they report 
a significant decrease in survival of other species of the same genus Daphnia when exposed to 
moderate and high levels of the microcystin. The results are hard to compare given the fact that 
other species were used and that the authors relate survival to toxin concentration, which has 
not been measured in our experiments. Some studies have shown that toxin production can 
change depending on conditions (Plumely, 1997), which further difficults the comparison with 
other studies.  
There appear to be three reasons that could explain the high feeding and low mortality in my 
experiments. It could be that the Daphnia culture used was, for one or another reason, 
particularly resistant or tolerant against the toxin (see Chislock et al., 2013). Another reason is 
that Daphnia in experiments had optimal conditions (e.g., high food concentration), and was 
thus better able to resist toxins. Finally, another reason could be the exposure time. Jang et al. 
(2003) reported that survival was related to exposure time. In their study, no mortality was 
observed in the first 48 hours, which is in agreement with my findings, Nevertheless, mortality 
increased as time progressed, up to 100% after 4 days. 
Daphnia feeding on Prymnesium did not display a significantly different clearance rate compared 
to the control food at 5psu (Fig. 6). The strain of Prymnesium was one of the most toxic strains 
in the culture collection (Dr. Gert Hansen, personal communication – SCCAP). Some studies have 
shown that Daphnia have certain degree of selectivity (DeMott, 1999) and it is therefore 
unexpected that no differences in clearance rate were detected compared to the control food 
source at 5psu. 
Prymnesium displayed a low growth rate that could partly explain the reduction in growth 
induced by Daphnia. In fact, the growth rates observed (0.19 d-1 ± 0.02) were below the values 
reported in the literature (Larsen et al., 1998; Baker et al., 2007) for the given rearing conditions 
(ca. 0.35 d-1 in both studies) and clearly below the values obtained in optimal conditions, 
suggesting that the temperature and maybe light conditions where not optimal for the growth 
of this species. 
The observed mortality was ca. 35% (Fig. 7). Little information is available on the effects of 
Prymnesium being grazed by Daphnia. Vasas et al. (2012) reported a 100% mortality after only 
3 hours of exposure to the algae. Nevertheless, they do not report culture and experimental 
conditions, size of grazers nor algae concentration used (presumably very high), which are likely 
to affect survival of the zooplankton. Schwierzke et al. (2010) reported a decrease in 
reproduction of Daphnia when exposed to water containing Prymnesium, but no mortality was 
reported. As for Vasas et al. (2012), not much information of the cultures is provided. 
I consider that the results obtained regarding mortality are reasonable, taking into account the 
toxicity of the strain, the algae concentration used (ca. 50000 cells·mL-1) and the size of the 
grazers, as well as the fact that at the sub-optimal salinity of 5psu Daphnia might not have been 
able to resist the effects of the toxin. Moreover, the salinity at which the culture was kept 
(10psu) was higher than the one used in the experiment, which could have boosted the toxicity 
of the algae (Shilo et al., 1953). The study made by Baker et al. (2007) reported that at low 
salinities the LC50 value was also lower than at medium and high salinities, implying that the 
toxicity displayed by the algae in our experimental conditions was probably high. 
 Pfiesteria caused the significantly lowest clearance rate compared to all the other food sources 
(Fig. 6). This dinoflagellate has been reported to be very toxic (Burkholder et al., 1992; 
==PAGE:51==
 
43 
 
Burkholder et al., 1995) which can explain the decreased grazing activity (Huntley et al., 1986; 
Barreiro et al., 2006) and the consequently low reduction in growth.  
Regarding the mortality, 40% of the individuals used were found dead (Fig. 7) and some of the 
living were displaying non-normal behaviour. This was evident in lack of activity: some Daphnia 
were found alive but laying at the bottom of the bottles and barely moving. This confirms the 
high toxicity of this dinoflagellate species. 
4.1.1.2. Cyclopoid 
The cyclopoid used could not be identified to a species level. It appeared to be able to withstand 
fresh- and brackish water, in accordance with the plasticity that some cyclopoid copepod species 
show in this regard (Thompson, 1991). Moreover, the cyclopoid was found in the bio-filter of a 
recirculating system, making it already a potentially good candidate for biological control. 
When feeding on Microcystis, the cyclopoid displayed the second highest clearance rate, right 
after the control food source (Fig. 6). Unlike Daphnia, the cyclopoid seem to be more affected 
by the toxin produced by the algae. In fact, a 10% mortality was observed (Fig. 7). 
Also, Prymnesium toxicity was likely to have impacted clearance rate, which was significantly 
lower compared to the control (Fig. 6). The same mortality (10%) was observed than for 
Microcystis (Fig. 7). Given the high toxicity of the strain used and the low salinity conditions 
probably leading to further increased toxicity (Shilo et al., 1953; Baker et al., 2007), the observed 
mortality is relatively low. This could be explained by a toxin tolerance or by copepods ability to 
detoxify themselves (Teegarden et al., 1996). Willis et al. (2004) found cyclopoids to have the 
lowest detoxification capacity, compared to other orders of the same subclass, when exposed 
to a component of a commercial disinfectant used in aquaculture. Nevertheless, the 
detoxification capacity of copepods might partly explain the high survival compared to Daphnia. 
Lastly, the case of Pfiesteria is similar to Prymnesium. The very high toxicity of this dinoflagellate 
is likely the cause for the significantly lowest clearance rate observed. In this case, the mortality 
was 20%, that is twice the mortality observed for Prymnesium. Same factors though can explain 
the response of the copepod species.  
4.1.1.3. General considerations  
Clearance rate depends on both temperature (Kersting et al., 1976) and food type and 
concentration (Porter et al., 1982). In general, the clearance rates obtained for Daphnia magna 
are a bit lower, although in the same range, than those reported by Kersting et al. (1976) and 
Porter et al. (1982), taking into account temperature and food concentration used in the 
experiment (ca. 2 mL·ind-1·hour-1 for the experiments and ca. 2.5 mL·ind-1·hour-1, for the above 
mentioned studies). The carbon ingestion reported in this study for Daphnia feeding on 
Microcystis was ca. 0.3 mg C·mg C-1·d-1, which is slightly inferior than the reported by DeMott 
(1999), ca. 0.5 mg C·mg C-1·d-1. Nevertheless, DeMott (1999) used juvenile Daphnia that were 
fed on a diet consisting of 80% of Microcystis. Therefore, the values obtained are in the range of 
what has been previously reported. 
For cyclopoids, the observed clearance rates are higher, although in the range, of those reported 
by Nakamura et al. (1997). In that study, a cyclopoid species was fed ciliates and heterotrophic 
dinoflagellates. The reported value for the latter is 0.13 ± 0.03 mL·ind-1·hour-1 and the observed 
values for Pfiesteria were ca. 0.19 mL·ind-1·hour-1. If looking for instance at the observed 
clearance rate for Microcystis, the average reported in this study (ca. 0.3 mL·ind-1·hour-1) is one 
==PAGE:52==
 
44 
 
order of magnitude higher than the value reported by DeMott et al. (1991) (0.02 mL·ind-1·hour1). 
In the same study, the authors report a significantly higher clearance rate (0.92 mL·ind-1·hour-1) 
when Microcystis was dead.  
In general, results from grazing experiments with toxic algae are surprising taking into account 
that a number of studies have reported that both Daphnia (DeMott, 1999; DeMott et al., 2001) 
and especially copepods (DeMott, 1986 and 1989; DeMott et al., 1989) are selective with the 
type of food they ingest, commonly refusing to eat toxic phytoplankton. 
Cyclopoids are apparently more efficient than Daphnia in terms of grazing. Nevertheless, as an 
adult Daphnia individual is significantly larger than a cyclopoid, its individual feeding rates are 
also much higher. Therefore, Daphnia might be more suitable to use in biological control, 
especially taking into consideration the less-selective feeding behaviour compared to copepods 
(DeMott, 1986 and 1989).  
My aim here was to check if the zooplankton candidate species had potential to control harmful 
algae blooms. Biological control methods are likely to be to some extent “tailor-made” solutions 
that will depend on the operating conditions of each farm as well as on the species responsible 
for the bloom. For this reason, both species are considered good potential candidates to use as 
biological control in aquaculture. 
Apparently, neither Daphnia nor the cyclopoid have died from toxic exudates, but from ingestion 
of the toxins, which, in the case of Pfiesteria, is in agreement with the results reported by 
Vogelbein et al. (2002) but contrary to what Burkholder et al. (1992 and 1995), Glasgow et al. 
(1995) and Noga et al. (1996) suggested. The implication of this is that both predators can graze 
despite the toxicity originating from feeding on the algae. It remains unknown what happens to 
the toxin: if it is bioaccumulated, this could pose a threat to the fish in case they would eat the 
zooplankton. The alternative to the bioaccumulation is that zooplankton species metabolize the 
toxins. As mentioned above, copepods can to some extent detoxify themselves but the 
biological processes underlying these mechanisms are not fully known. 
4.1.2. Exposure to PAA: decay and effect on algae and zooplankton 
The usage of peracetic acid in aquaculture has considerably increased in the recent years, given 
ease of use and the promising results, especially regarding parasite infections, reported in some 
in vitro studies (Meinelt et al., 2009; Picón-Camacho et al., 2012). Results from in vivo 
experiments are less clear as water quality and rearing conditions differ (Rintamäki-Kinnunen et 
al., 2005; Sudová et al., 2010; Pedersen et al., 2011). 
First of all, regarding the decay of peracetic acid, it should be noted standard curves document 
limited difference in peracetic acid degradation rate between freshwater and 5psu water (Fig. 
5, Methods). This is in agreement with the findings reported by Liu et al. (2014) who did not 
observe differences between their control (distilled water) and 3psu water. The coefficients for 
the equation reported in this thesis are higher than those of Liu et al., (2014), which can be 
explained by methodological techniques such as the duration of the mixing time after the 
addition of the reagents or the accuracy in the determination of the stock solution.  
The main factors affecting the decay of peracetic acid are the nominal PAA concentration 
(depending on the product formulation and the added dose), the stability of the product itself 
and the specific consumption rate of peracetic acid, which depends on the organic loading of 
the water (Pedersen et al., 2009; Pedersen et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2014). In the experiment 
==PAGE:53==
 
45 
 
carried out to study the decay, all these factors were held constant, increasing the 
reproducibility of the methods applied.  
For the first 2 hours, the curves followed the classical exponential decay (Antonelli et al., 2006; 
Falsanisi et al., 2006). After 24 hours, measureable residuals were found, corresponding to ca. 
one third of the initially added concentration. This points to an incomplete peracetic acid decay, 
which is very likely due to limited organic matter content used in our experiments. In the 
previous studies where all peracetic acid was eventually degraded (Pedersen et al., 2009; 
Pedersen et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2014), organic loading was significantly higher (3 orders of 
magnitude) than in our experiment, where the carbon concentration in the water was set to 
400µg C/L.  
Two different experiments were carried out to determine the effect of peracetic acid on 
Pfiesteria and Daphnia magna. The two 24-hour experiments are related to other experiments 
performed later on: the one with Pfiesteria was done with the aim of identifying minimum 
concentrations of peracetic acid giving negative growth, which allowed us to better design the 
experiment of the 5-day exposure to the chemical. In the case of Daphnia, the 24-hour exposure 
was also carried out previous to the combined grazing experiment with the grazer and the 
chemical with the hope that the information obtained in it could help us better define the 
concentrations range to be tested in the combined grazing experiment. 
4.1.2.1. 24-hour exposure for Pfiesteria 
Figure 9 provides evidence for the hypothesis that increasing concentrations of peracetic acid 
effectively lead to a reduction in algae growth. However, the variation in the response of the 
algae increased with increasing concentrations, especially in the last two concentrations. There 
are two reasons that can explain this trend. First, high concentrations of peracetic acid could 
cause cell disintegration, which could have led to under-estimation of the dead cells. 
Nevertheless, given the below discussed methodology (see chapter 4.1.2.3) it seems unlikely. 
Second, it is possible that above a certain threshold, the exposure to peracetic acid has a 
differential effect on the different individual cells. Potentially, what is observed here might be 
the result of different cells having different sensitivity to peracetic acid. Consequently, the 
numbers of positively identified dead cells – and therefore the growth – would vary more as the 
concentration is increased. 
4.1.2.2. 5-day exposure to PAA 
The 24-hour experiment allowed us to identify the lowest peracetic acid concentration giving 
negative growth. This was key to design the 5-day experiment (Fig. 11). The reason for using 
Pfiesteria in these experiments was due to its ability to form resistant cysts (Litaker et al., 2002), 
which increases the difficulty of successfully eliminating a bloom, as shown by Moestrup et al. 
(2014). 
As hypothesised, some differences in the way Pfiesteria reacts to the different doses were 
observed. In the first treatment, the dose applied was not enough to kill all the algae. In fact, 
the number of living cells at 24 hours was higher than at 2 hours, indicating that the major effect 
took place in the first hours after the PAA addition. This coincides with the results both from the 
decay curves and the 24-hour exposure: from the decay curves one can see that most of the 
peracetic acid reacts in the first hours after the addition and also that there was a remaining 
concentration of 0.5mg PAA·L-1 after 24 hours. These levels of peracetic acid were not enough 
==PAGE:54==
 
46 
 
to cause negative growth in Pfiesteria, which probably indicates that some of the remaining 
living cells could reproduce and that some of the formed cysts hatched again. 
After the first 24 hours, there was a decrease in abundance that can only be explained as an 
effect of starvation. The rest of the results support this idea: number of dead cells remained 
stable after 24 hours and number of cysts increased. Moreover, if looking at Figure 12, where 
the control for the 5-day experiment is shown, the same pattern can be seen both for living cells 
and cysts after 24 hours. 
In the second treatment, the repeated daily addition of 1.5mg PAA·L-1, the first 24 hours are 
identical to the first treatment. This confirms that the set-up and the methodology were good 
in terms of experimental noise. It should also be pointed out that after the 3rd addition (time 
points 72.5 and until 120), the majority of cells were dead or in the form of cysts.  
Finally, the initial pulse of 7.5mg PAA·L-1 had the expected effects: all living cells were killed 
before the first 2 hours after the addition and consequently all the numbers remained stable 
until a food source was added. After the 120 hours, I added a food source to mimic a shift to 
favourable conditions (no chemical products and high food concentration). 
In all cases, there was an increase – although very small in the second and third treatments – in 
the number of living cells. This was expected in the first treatment: there were still living cells at 
the end of the experiment that could start reproducing. Similarly to the control, some of the 
cysts hatched, further increasing the number of living cells. 
This increase is especially important in the second and third treatments. There were no living 
cells at time 120. Consequently, the increase in living cells after the addition of a food source 
could only be due to the fact that some cysts had been able to resist long exposure to medium 
and very high concentrations of peracetic acid, respectively. This is central to understand the 
effect of peracetic acid treatment as well as the implications in aquaculture. 
There is extremely little information concerning the effect of peracetic acid on algae growth. 
There is number of studies testing the potential of this product as a ballast water treatment. For 
instance, Fuchs et al. (2004) report 100% mortality of all microorganisms tested (different 
zooplankton species and one algae species) in 48 hours. Nevertheless, the concentrations used 
were between 50 and 1600 mg PAA·L-1, significantly higher than those used in this study. Baldry 
(1983) also reported good efficiency of peracetic acid in killing microorganisms and spores. 
Finally, Gregg et al. (2006) also tested the efficacy of peracetic acid in microalgae and in 
dinoflagellate cysts. Their results are similar to those from Fuchs et al. (2004) in terms of 
concentrations required to kill the tested organisms. Moreover, commercial products used in 
Fuchs et al. (2004) and Gregg et al. (2006) differ in formulation (14% g/100mL of PAA) from the 
one used in this study (35% g/100mL of PAA), which further complicates comparison of results. 
The results document that despite a long-term treatment, the algae population can still survive 
and might bloom back, which has implications for aquaculture management. Moreover, this 
highlights the need for alternative methods in controlling algae blooms, especially in the cases 
caused by cyst-forming species. In this sense, biological control methods can play an important 
role. 
4.1.2.3. Further considerations: control and staining method 
The data from the control without peracetic acid addition confirms that there was indeed an 
effect of starvation in the first treatment. The magnitude of this effect was very similar in the 
==PAGE:55==
 
47 
 
control and in the first treatment. If looking at the numbers, the effect of starvation in the 
control caused a decrease of ca. 50% in the number of living cells, from time 24 to 120 hours. In 
the same period of time, when the effect of peracetic acid was presumably negligible, in the first 
treatment there was also a decrease of ca. 50% of the remaining living cells (from ca. 1000 
cells·mL-1 to 500 cells mL-1). This further confirms that the set-up and measurements were 
robust. 
The results of this long-term test confirm my hypothesis that increasing concentrations of 
peracetic acid cause a decrease in growth and enhance cyst formation in Pfiesteria. Regarding 
the first treatment, the hypothesis was partly confirmed: there was an initial decrease in growth 
that was nevertheless not followed by an increase once peracetic acid had degraded, given the 
starvation effect that was not originally considered. Still, once the conditions shifted to totally 
favourable conditions, Pfiesteria population increased again. 
For the second treatment, the hypothesis was also partly confirmed: there was a reduction in 
growth and the abundance decreased with progressing time but again no real increase was 
observed after the decrease in peracetic acid. The fact that Pfiesteria did not increase is primarily 
due to the daily addition of peracetic acid. In this case, starvation was not the main driver of the 
observed trend. In addition, in aquaculture facilities, and especially in recirculating systems, it is 
unlikely that starvation can take place, due to the high organic loading of the water. 
In the knock-out treatment, the hypothesis was totally confirmed. A high and rapid mortality 
was observed with minor cyst formation. Also most of the cysts were dead due to the small 
increase in the number of living cells after the addition of the food source. This high degree of 
cysts mortality may be due to the ability of peracetic acid to act as a protein denaturant, 
displaying a high disinfection success even with spores and spore-like structures (Lefevre et al., 
1992; Liberti et al., 1999). Still, and despite this disinfection success, the results indicate that 
even very high concentrations are not enough to kill all the cysts, with the resulting risk of a 
second bloom. 
The staining method was developed to allow discrimination of cysts and living and dead cells 
when evaluating the effects of peracetic acid on Pfiesteria. As explained in the Methods section, 
in the 24-hour exposure and in the 5-day experiment with peracetic acid, the monitoring of the 
algae concentration could not be done in the same way than in grazing experiments, because 
counting cells would have included both dead and living and no effect would have been 
identified. Consequently, another method had to be developed.  
Staining methods using Trypan Blue have been previously used in vegetal cells in order to 
morphologically characterize organisms and to more precisely see particular structures such 
plate patterns (Lebour 1925; Koch et al., 1990; Licea et al., 2002). In these type of studies, cells 
were always killed by heat treatment which typically denatures biological structures, such as 
membranes and cell walls (Lepock, 1982), and there was no certainty that Trypan Blue would 
also work in cells killed by acid, despite some studies showing the effect of peracetic acid on 
cells walls and cell membranes as a consequence of its ability as protein denaturant (Lefevre et 
al., 1992; Liberti et al., 1999). 
In order to ensure that this method would work, Pfiesteria cells were exposed to increasing 
concentrations of peracetic acid (Fig. 10, Results). The aim was to identify if this increasing 
exposure would lead to an increasing number of positively identified dead cells. Figure 10 
confirms that this was the case, suggesting that the staining method could be used. To further 
confirm it, I summed up the numbers of living cells, dead cells and cysts from the 5-day 
==PAGE:56==
 
48 
 
experiment for each time interval. I assumed that the total amount of cells should be relatively 
constant since it was unlikely that cells would have divided under the unfavourable conditions. 
As expected, the total numbers of cells did not differ much between the days nor the treatments 
(Table 9). Values at time 0 were the lowest in all cases, indicating an initial underestimation of 
both the number of dead cells (incorrectly assumed to be 0) and the number of cysts present in 
the culture. An explanation for that underestimation could be that for the initial check of the 
culture Lugol was used, not Trypan Blue, which complicates the identification of the cysts, that 
are more easily identified when using the stain. From time 0.5 hours and until 120 hours, the 
numbers showed limited variance. In the trial with Pulse-1.5, values ranged from 3225 to 3495 
(≈ 7 % variance); data from the Repeated daily-1.5 goes from 3275 to 3470 (≈ 6%) whereas in 
Pulse-7.5 the variation was 3.3%, with values ranging from 3246 to 3358. Given these numbers, 
the method seems to be robust. 
The numbers at time 168 hours were in the first two treatments higher than the rest of the 
values indicating that Pfiesteria cells could have started to recover when a food source was 
added. The increase was higher in the Pulse-1.5 treatment compared to the two other trials. 
To confirm if this observation is a real recovery, longer experiments should be carried out where 
the algae concentration is monitored over a longer period after the shift towards favourable 
conditions. Moestrup et al. (2014) observed a reappearance of a toxic dinoflagellate in the water 
of a recirculating fish farm after the end of the disinfection treatment. Our findings are therefore 
in agreement with their observations and could indicate that peracetic acid is not the most 
suitable product to treat blooms caused by cyst-forming species in RAS.  
4.1.2.4. The effect of PAA on Daphnia: mortality and grazing 
Another experiment that involved exposure to peracetic acid was the one carried out with 
Daphnia (Fig. 13A). The results clearly show that there was a threshold concentration (1mg 
PAA·L-1) above which Daphnia started dying. These findings refute the suggested hypothesis 
concerning Daphnia’s exposure to peracetic acid. 
These results differ from those reported by Liu et al. (2015), where the authors exposed Daphnia 
magna to different commercial products with different formulations all containing peracetic 
acid. The authors reported that, at a concentration of 1mg PAA·L-1 of the commercial product 
E400 (the most similar to the one used in this experiment, containing 40% m/v peracetic acid 
and 12% m/v hydrogen peroxide) the mortality was above 50% and reached 100% at 1.5mg 
PAA·L-1. There are some differences between the observed values in the present experiments 
compared to those of Liu et al. (2015). For instance, the authors used small individuals that were 
6-24 hours old whereas I used adults of ca. 4.5mm in size.  
Liu et al. also reported mortalities of ca. 25% for concentrations below 1mg PAA·L-1. Taking into 
account the size of the individuals used in our experiments it is reasonable that the threshold in 
the present study is also significantly higher.  
 An experiment was carried out with Daphnia feeding on Scenedesmus and on Pfiesteria while 
exposed to increasing concentration of peracetic acid. 
From the results depicted in Figure 13B it seems clear that there was a reduction in growth and 
abundance in both algae species for all the concentrations tested. Nevertheless, it is also clear 
that increasing peracetic acid concentrations led to a decrease in the grazing activity with both 
species. 
==PAGE:57==
 
49 
 
Mortality results concerning Daphnia for the combined experiments were surprising (Table 10). 
No mortality was observed at 0.5mg PAA·L-1 neither with Scenedesmus nor with Pfiesteria. In 
the case of the dinoflagellate this is especially remarkable given the high (ca. 40%) mortality 
observed in the grazing experiment without PAA addition (Fig. 7). The same appears to happen 
for the other two peracetic acid concentrations, which also resulted in a low mortality. At 1mg 
PAA·L-1, the mortality was higher in the experiment performed with Scenedesmus than in the 
one with Pfiesteria, which indicates that the observed mortality was due to the effect of 
peracetic acid rather than the dinoflagellates’ toxin. 
It is possible that peracetic acid addition somehow impairs toxin production in Pfiesteria but this 
has never been reported nor observed in any of the consulted studies (Burkholder et al., 1992 
and 1995; Glasgow et al., 1995; Noga et al., 1996 and Vogelbein et al., 2002). An implication of 
this result could be, at least with Pfiesteria, that it is possible to find an optimal combination 
where the addition of peracetic acid could benefit the grazing activity of the grazer and therefore 
the removal of the algae. 
4.2. Feasibility Issues: Towards practical implications 
The second aim of the thesis was to study the feasibility of implementing the biological control 
methods in recirculating aquaculture systems as well as to evaluate the challenges concerning 
flow rate and hydraulics. 
Several factors can affect the ability of grazers to feed on harmful algae in recirculating systems. 
In this thesis, I have focused in two key aspects concerning feasibility RAS:  
1) identifying minimum density of zooplankton required to achieve negative growth of algae and 
2) challenges related to flow rate. All types of aquaculture involve economic concerns, based on 
the profit margin of the fish farms. This is important to keep in mind, considering the practical 
aspects of cultivation of zooplankton, such as the technical challenges of maintaining dense 
zooplankton cultures. Moreover, in recirculating systems flow is a key parameter that (among 
other factors) determines the oxygen availability and the self-cleaning of the tank. 
Consequently, the margin for changing the flow is very limited. Studying the effects that flow 
rate has in Daphnia is thus the key to understand how biological control methods can be 
implemented in RAS. 
4.2.1. Zooplankton density 
Figure 14 shows the relation between density of zooplankton and growth rate of the two toxic 
species tested in this thesis.  
Based on the data points, two different types of fittings were added: a linear fitting and a 2nd 
order polynomic fitting. The linear fitting indicates that a minimum of 5-6 individuals would be 
required to cause negative growth in the algae population. The quadratic fitting indicates that a 
minimum of 8 individuals would be needed to cause the same effect (Table 11). Based on the 
data, the polynomic function gives a better fit. 
Although it is not clear why the effect of grazer density on algae growth would not be linear, a 
possibility could be that there is an increase in toxicity or an increased toxin production of the 
algae with increasing predator densities, as shown by Selander et al. (2006) and Bergkvist et al. 
(2008). In the latter, the authors also reported that this grazer-induced toxicity could be grazer-
specific.  
==PAGE:58==
 
50 
 
In any case, taking into account that Daphnia can be mass-cultured in sustained densities that 
range between 500 – 3000 ind.·L-1 (De Pauw et al., 1981; FAO, 1996), a concentration of 6-8 
ind·L-1 is completely feasible to produce at a commercial scale. Assuming the lowest density of 
the range (500 ind·L-1), 8 ind·L-1 required and a volume of 26m3 (see chapter 4.2.3. and Fig. 19), 
a tank of 0.42m3 would be needed to grow enough Daphnia individuals. If a safety factor of 2 
can be applied to account for mortality events, then the required volume of the tank would be 
of 0.8 m3. 
4.2.2. Flow related challenges: 1h-exposure, grazing and technical implications 
Flow experiments involved adjustment of hydraulics by the use of a recirculating pump thereby 
changing the velocity at which Daphnia was exposed. This was done to test different velocities 
provided that, in a RAS, the velocity of the water current is not constant throughout the system. 
By doing so, the objective was to identify Daphnia’s resistance to the velocity as well as to detect 
the maximum velocity not affecting the cladoceran’s grazing activity. 
Figure 15 depicts the number of individuals flushed out of the tank after 1-hour exposure to 
different water velocities. This test showed a threshold velocity of 7 m·h-1, where after only 1 
hour 10% of Daphnia where found outside of the tank. At the highest velocity, 10.5 m·h-1, an 
average of 20% of the individuals were flushed out of the tank. 
Identifying what is the threshold in terms of current velocity that the cladocerans can withstand 
without getting flushed out of the tank was important to see in which component or part of the 
system the zooplankton could potentially be added. Given that 10% of the individuals get flushed 
out in only 1 hour already at 7 m·h-1, it seems unfeasible to just add the zooplankton in a 
component of the RAS. The consequences of that would be that in less than half a day the grazer 
would have spread throughout the system with unknown but potentially negative 
consequences, such as malfunctioning of the bio-filter due to grazing activity, clogging of pumps 
or pipes or having fish eating them with the risk of toxin bioaccumulation. 
Furthermore, experiments were conducted to test the influence of different water velocities in 
the grazing activity of Daphnia when feeding on Scenedesmus. The results from Figure 11 
support the threshold identified with the short-term exposure. In fact, two groups can clearly 
be made in terms of velocities: no velocity and 3.5m m·h-1 showed similar values as in previous 
grazing experiments in terms of growth reduction. At 7 and 10.5m m·h-1 there was a very 
significant decrease in grazing activity, showing that from a certain threshold, increasing 
velocities negatively impact the grazing activity. Further, there seemed to be a decrease in 
grazing and increase in the number of animals flushed out with time, already at 3.5 m·h-1 (Fig. 
18). This suggests an exhaustion phenomenon of the cladoceran when being exposed to currents 
for long periods of time. 
Taking all the information regarding Daphnia’s response to water velocity confirms the technical 
challenges to implement biological control methods in RAS. One of the components with the 
lowest velocity is the bio-filter. There are many different types of bio-filters, but a reasonable 
average velocity in bio-filters can be around 25 cm·min-1. It is a velocity applied to ensure optimal 
substrate exchange from the bulk water phase into the biofilm where the nitrifying bacteria 
metabolize ammonia and nitrite. The maximum velocity that did not influence Daphnia grazing 
in my experiments was 3.5 m·h-1, that is to say a bit less than 6 cm·min-1, around four times less 
than in the bio-filter. 
==PAGE:59==
 
51 
 
Thus, there is no component in the RAS where zooplankton can be added and expected to graze 
uninfluenced, regardless of whether or not they would get flushed out from the system.  
All this has clear implications in the way this method has to be implemented in RAS: another 
unit has to be designed in order to be able to implement biological control methods when using 
Daphnia magna. 
4.2.3. Considerations for the design 
When designing this potentially new unit for a RAS there are some criteria that need to be met: 
1) the unit has to be designed according to its utility 2) the unit has to be integrated in an already 
existing and working RAS farm and 3) the unit needs to exploit all the advantages of the 
biological control methods over the currently used methods. 
Regarding the first criteria, the unit has to be able to guarantee the right conditions for Daphnia 
to graze on the algae, that is to say the adequate velocity (maximum of 3.5 m·h-1). Regarding the 
second criteria, the unit has to be large enough to work in a commercial scale. However, current 
designs of RAS fish farm, while accounting for operational and working space, do not include 
spaces for having extra units. Finally, the third criteria relates to the functionality but also to the 
attractiveness of the product: farmers have to see it as a good alternative to their conventional 
methods. 
Taking all this into account, a sketch of a potential biological control unit is suggested (Fig. 19). 
It was decided that the entire system volume should go through the biological treatment unit at 
least once a day, which corresponds to ca. 63 m3·h-1 (1 m3·min-1) for a 1500 m3 RAS. Moreover, 
the unit has been designed and dimensioned to have a maximum velocity of 3.5 m·h-1 (Criteria 
1), which means that a radius of 1.35m (or bigger) is required. Length of the unit in Fig. 19 has 
been set to 4.5m, only for picturing purposes. The length (or height) of a real unit would 
determine the volume of whole cylinder, which would determine hydraulic retention time in the 
cylinder. 
The suggested unit includes three horizontally placed cylinders mounted on a rolling table 
(Criteria 2). In the sketch, pumps are added as well as inlet and outlet pipes.  
In addition, the unit has been designed as a modular unit that can be adjusted by 
adding/removing cylinders and through adjustable pumps. Consequently, it is relatively easy to 
use both in terms of operation – fill up the units, add the zooplankton and turn on the pumps – 
and work labour required to operate it – one single person can start the unit in relatively short 
time –. Also, it is highly adaptable due to the wheeled-table as well as the pipes in the inlet and 
outlet that could be extended to reach the water source. Further, the costs for building the 
suggested unit would not be very high – plastic cylinders, piping, the table itself and the pumps. 
Running costs would come from the power required by the pumps – the fact that the cylinders 
are in a horizontal position decreases the height at which the pump needs to lift the water and 
which reduces energy consumption – and the cost of the zooplankton.

4.2.4. General considerations for applicability: scenarios and solutions 
So far, the biological and technical implications of biological control have been discussed. These 
implications are central for biological controls in aquaculture as a tool to decrease and even 
substitute the use of disinfectants, i.e. Chloramine-T or peracetic acid, to treat algae-related 
problems. 
Nevertheless, putting biological control into perspective, not only in biological and technical 
terms, but also with regard to the whole industry is of key importance to assess the real 
applicability that this method could have. 
In order to do this, three different solutions to treat algae problems have been evaluated: 1) 
usage of peracetic acid 2) usage of biological control and 3) a combination of both methods. The 
first solution represents the currently used method. The second one is the biological control as 
it has been presented in this thesis, based on the usage of Daphnia magna as a grazer and using 
the above suggested unit as the way to implement it in recirculating fish farms. The third solution 
corresponds to a combination of both methods. 
Five criteria have been chosen to evaluate the solutions under the three different scenarios. 
Each criteria grants a certain amount of points (from 1 to 5) depending on how important for 
the aquaculture industry the criteria is. The ranking of the criteria changes depending on the 
different scenario conditions. The criteria used are: 
==PAGE:61==
 
53 
 
1) Efficacy of the method: defined as the potential of totally eliminating a bloom and the 
risk of successive blooms. 
2) Costs: understood as both the capital costs required to acquire the solution and the 
running costs.  
3) Ease of use: regarded as the complexity in terms of technical challenges and work labour 
required to put into practice each solution. 
4) Worker safety: defined as the risk that the solution poses to the workers. 
5) Environmental impact: understood as the consequences of the solution in terms of 
impact on the surrounding ecosystem of the fish farm. 
Each solution has been given a score with respect to each criteria and taking into account the 
conditions from each scenario. The scores range from 0 to 2 and they indicate: 0 – deficient 
degree of accomplishment of the criteria, 1 – average degree of accomplishment of the criteria 
and 2 – high degree of accomplishment of the criteria.  
Justification for the ranking of the criteria – and therefore the points granted – as well as for the 
scoring of each solution can be found in Table 1 – Appendix 2. 
Moreover, three different scenarios have been defined. Scenario 1 represents a situation similar 
to the current one in Denmark in terms of economic conditions as well as environmental 
legislation. It has been assumed that the major focus of the current environmental legislation is 
on discharge of nitrogenous and phosphoric compounds and not that much in chemical and 
especially peracetic acid discharge. Scenario 2 corresponds to a situation where EU and/or 
Danish policies become more restrictive in terms of the allowed environmental impacts, 
including chemicals as potential responsible for increased environmental impacts. Finally, 
Scenario 3 corresponds to a situation of economic melting down, where cost reduction is the 
main concern of the industry. The different scenarios present differences in the ranking of the 
above described criteria to account for the varying conditions that they represent (Appendix 2). 
The ranking of the criteria in the different scenarios can be seen in Tables 13, 14 and 15. 
Furthermore, two sub-scenarios are suggested for each scenario. Scenario A assumes a bloom 
of cysts-forming species and scenario B assumes a bloom of a non-cyst-forming species. 
The final evaluation is obtained by adding up the result of timing the points that each criteria 
grants by the score of the solution (Stevens et al., 1998). The interpretation is therefore simple: 
the highest score is the recommended solution for a given scenario.

5. CONCLUSIONS AND FURTHER WORK 
Two main objectives were defined at the beginning of this thesis: 1) Test the potential of 
different zooplankton species in controlling algae growth and 2) Study the feasibility of 
implementing biological control in RAS, with particular focus on the flow-related challenges.  
My results show that 1) the two zooplankton species tested can be considered good candidates 
for biological control provided that they have controlled the different algae species, despite their 
toxicity, 2) while challenging in terms of water velocity, it is in fact possible to implement this 
method in RAS and it is indeed feasible in terms of required individuals. 
From the evaluation made in the discussion, I suggest that biological control is particularly 
suitable when the algae blooms is caused by cyst-forming species and with increasingly 
restrictive policies regarding environmental impact. 
5.1. Further work 
This thesis is, to my knowledge, the first scientific approach to test usage of biological control in 
aquaculture and especially in recirculating systems with the aim of reducing the amount of 
chemicals being used in the industry. Although my results show the potential of the biological 
control, there are a number of new questions to address. 
A better understanding of the aquaculture related HAB species in terms of their tolerance ranges 
and their toxicity as well as the effects of the toxin on the zooplankton would allow for better 
management strategies of these blooms and to optimise biological control. Also, understanding 
of the potential toxin bioaccumulation in the grazers is important to further improve the 
implementation. 
It would also be interesting to study the genetic basis of the zooplankton in terms of toxin 
resistance and detoxification capacity, to see if it is exploitable. This would clearly boost the 
efficacy of biological control. 
From a technical perspective there are also unsolved questions. Research on how to implement 
biological control in a commercial and pragmatic manner should be carried out: unit design, 
study of costs and comparison with other commonly used chemicals are necessary. The present 
thesis has proved that biological controls are feasible at a small scale, nevertheless, further 
experiments should also be carried out mimicking realistic farm conditions, especially in terms 
of organic loading of the water.

APPENDIX 2: JUSTIFICATION FOR THE POINTS GRANTED BY EACH CRITERIA AND OF THE 
SCORING IN THE SCENARIO ANALYSIS 
In this appendix a justification for the points granted by each criteria and for the scores given to 
each solution in each scenario are presented. 
Regarding the points granted by each criteria, in Scenario 1 it was considered that the most 
important criteria (5 points) for a fish farmer would be Efficacy of the method. It has been 
assumed that above any other thing, a farmer would like the solution to work. As second criteria 
(4 points) there is Costs. As for any business, costs have to be kept as low as possible. Fish 
farmers in addition tend to have low profitability margins. The third criteria (3 points) was Ease 
of Use. For a method to be implemented it has to be relatively easy to use and it should not add 
a lot of complications to an already complex system. Fourth and fifth criteria (Worker Safety and 
Environmental Impact) both grant 2 points. The reason being that in Denmark there is a concern 
for both topics. 
In Scenario 2, the first criteria was still Efficacy. It was considered that despite the increased 
restriction in environmental legislations, a fish farmer would still want above any other thing 
that the solution works. In this scenario, Environmental Impacts grants 4 points, given the 
assumed changes in the legislation. Right after it, Costs would still be one of the major concerns 
(3 points). Ease of use would be fourth granting the same points than the fifth, Worker Safety. 
Finally, in Scenario 3, the ranking of the criteria was: 1st for Costs as in a situation of economic 
melting down they would be prioritized. 2nd for Efficacy, 3rd for Ease of Use and 4th for Worker 
Safety. In this scenario, and to make it more informative, the points granted for Environmental 
Impacts, in the fifth place, were reduced to 1, the reason being that in situations of severe 
economic crisis, the environmental concerns usually decrease. 
Regarding the scoring for PAA, a general comment should be made concerning the Efficacy in 
scenarios A to B. In the latter, Efficacy is increased given that the literature consulted and the 
experiments carried out showed good results in eliminating non-cysts forming algae species. For 
the rest of the solutions, and PAA in all the other criteria, scoring is the same in both scenarios.
