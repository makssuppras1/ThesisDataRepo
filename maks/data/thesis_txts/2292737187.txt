Abstract
In this thesis the main goal was to produce a large scale tunnel segment from textile reinforced
concrete by using an inﬂatable formwork.
To get the right concrete mix, 17.2mm and 70mm thick beams samples were produced. The beam
specimens are tested for ﬂexural strength in a 3 point bend test in accordance to ASTM D790.
The 17.2mm beams tests resulted in loads of 0, 514kN to 0, 694kN and a bending strength of
26.06MPa.
The larger beams, 70mm, resulted in max loads of 3.84kN to 4.68kN and a bend
strength of 7.23MPa.
Once the concrete mix design was found, a large scale concrete cylinder is cast. This is done by
creating a cylindrical segment from a combination of ﬁbre reinforcement and textile ﬁbre lining.
A Textile ﬁbre lining is made to act as the outer shell of the cylinder and engineered cementitious
concrete (ECC) is poured inside the shell. An inﬂatable cylinder made from PVC fabric is made
to act as the formwork of the tunnel segment.
Keywords
Tunnel, ﬁbre, reinforced concrete, ECC, textile, inﬂatables, segment

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Framework
The worlds population is growing everyday and the cities are getting bigger and bigger. There is
an increased demand on CO2 and cars are taking over the cities. Many cities are leaning towards
building underground roadways and railways to connect the city and reduce road traﬃc.
Underground tunnels are built to improve city infrastructures. Tunnels primarily function are for
roadways, railways, public utilities and telecommunications. They leave a very small footprint
when compared to bridges and can directly connect two areas with a straight line thus saving
time, which is a large factor in large populated cities.
The main disadvantage in building tunnels is that the cost overruns during the building phase.
The formwork or mold, that holds the concrete till it hardens is an expensive process and having
precast segments also require heavy machinery which also has a signiﬁcant costs attached to it.
Reducing this cost can greatly aﬀect the future of tunnel building.
The focus of this thesis is to explore alternate and more cost eﬀective ways of building tunnels.
1.2
Tunnel Building
The deﬁnition of a tunnel can vary signiﬁcantly from country to country, but in general it can be
described as a long and narrow underground passageway. In United Kingdom it is deﬁned as ”a
subsurface highway structure enclosed for a length of 150m or more” [3].
Tunnels are constructed by digging or boring though various rocks and clays. The excavation
needs to be protected from constant moving rock formations which can destabilize and collapse
them. A method of involves using a tunnel lining to stabilize the rock/earth around the tunnel
and provide protection and safety. These linings can be either temporary or permanent. Figure
2.1.1 illustrates an example of the forwork for casting temporary or permanent tunnel linings.

Temporary methods include spraying the tunnel walls with shotcrete, setting up steel arches,
bolts and meshes.
The typical concrete used for shotcrete is ECC (Engineered Cementitious
Composites), which is a special mortar based concrete made with small plastic ﬁbres as reinforced.
These ﬁbres make the concrete very ductile and therefore perfect for dissipating the amount of
energy coming from the tunnel walls.
Permanent tunnel linings (the tunnel walls) are made from either precast concrete segments or
cast in place concrete. These are made in a ring or arch formation to provide maximum support.
Cast in place concrete is the most time consuming as it requires a lot of man hours. The required
formwork needs to be set up and reinforcement needs to be bound before the concrete can be
poured, once hardened, the formwork has to be removed. Overall this is a very lengthy process.
Precast segments are made at a factory beforehand and are transported to the excavation when
required. Assembly of the segments is usually done with a tunnel segment erector, which places
the segments to form a concrete ring. The erector can be part of a tunnel boring machine or as a

1.3
Objective
The motivation of this thesis is to explore the possibility of creating a tunnel segment from textile
ﬁbre lining and concrete by using an inﬂatable formwork. Answering questions of variability and
sustainability of the method, as well as its applicability.
To achieve this goal smaller scale beam specimens of textile reinforced concrete will be created to
achieve the right concrete mix. These specimens will than be tested for their ﬂexural strength.
The main goal of this thesis is to successfully produce a proof of concept by designing a large scale
tunnel section made entirely from textile reinforced concrete and using and inﬂatable cylinder and
formwork. This will be designed and cast at the concrete lab at DTU BYG.

Chapter 2

2.1
Textiles
Textiles are ﬁbrous materials which have started seeing use in concrete structures as reinforcement.
The most commonly used ﬁbres are carbon ﬁbres and alkali resistant glass (AR glass) ﬁbres 2.1.1a.
These ﬁbres have a very high tensile strength and therefore a good substitute as reinforcement in
concrete elements.
To create the textile fabric, the ﬁbres are turned into yarns by interlocking the ﬁlaments which is
done by either twisting or grouping them together. This determines the bearing capacity of the
yarns due to the inﬂuence of ﬁbre interaction. Figure 2.1.1b shows ﬁlaments of yarn and ﬁbre
mesh.
(a) Filaments grouped together.
(b) AR glass ﬁbre yarns in a mesh.
Figure 2.1.1: Figures of a yarn and a ﬁbre mesh.
The yarns are than turned into a textile mesh, where the mesh size determines the concrete used
for the element. Usual methods of textile reinforcement allows for layering the mesh inside a beam
with a given height. For this, the mesh has to be open for the concrete to be able to penetrate
and bond.
For this project the textile mesh used is almost closed and allows very little concrete to penetrate
the outer mesh shell. The textile will be used as a textile mold, that will be part of the ﬁnished
structure.

2.2
ECC
Traditional concrete is reinforced with steel bars to provide tensile strength. Using steel bars has
an eﬀect on the cross section of the concrete element, as steel reinforced needs a concrete cover to
protect from corrosion, this puts some restraints on how thin an element can be made.
An alternative to traditional concrete is ﬁbre reinforced concrete. In this project ﬁbre reinforced
concrete is used to ﬁll the textile shell. There are numerous types of ﬁbre reinforced concrete which
can include diﬀerent ﬁbres like glass, steel,carbon etc [4]. One of the recent developed concrete is
called Engineered Cementitious Composites (ECC). It is a high strength mortar based composite
with randomly dispersed polymer ﬁbres [4]. Unlike conventional concrete which is very brittle,
ECC is a highly ductile and behaves more like metals when exposed to strain. ECC can reach a
strain capacity as high as 3 −5% [4]. The physical properties of ECC can be seen in Table 2.1.

3.1
Mix design
Figure 3.1.1: Measured materials for a 200l concrete mix.
The mixing and casting of the concrete was done at DTU’s concrete lab. The whole tunnel segment
was cast from a single mix. All the materials were measured separately and than transported to
the mixer on a pallet 3.1.1.
The base ECC mix design was provided by Gregor Fischer, this mix has been used earlier to create
various types of elements. It was important that the concrete was viscous enough to be able to get
pumped inside the textile lining. The design had to be adjusted and be highly ﬂowable in order
to be able to get inside the seams of the textile shell.
Multiple trials were done in order to get a good workable concrete. The trial mixes where done in
a 15l mixer and a few of them were done in a 40 l mixer, while the mix for the cylinder segment
were done in a 200 l mixer.
The casting procedure consisted of mixing the dry materials which included cement, ﬂy ash, sand,
superplasticiser and viscosity agent together until it was fully mixed. Water was than added to
the mix and mixed again for 3 min. Once a good ﬂowability was acquired the ﬁbres are added
fresh concrete and mixed for 5 min.
The ﬁrst trial mixes were very dry and required a lot of added water. It was found that the ﬂyash
had a diﬀerent composition, which resulted in the dry concrete. This had made it impossible to
pour inside the forms, as the ﬁbres kept lumping together. In the trial mixes to come the water
amount was increased gradually by 5, 10, 15 and than 20%. The best result ended up to be with
15% of added water.
The viscosity agent amount, tylose, was also increased in order to reduce concrete bleeding, as the

3.2. BEAM SAMPLES
3.2.2
Casting
The casting procedure of the small beams 17.2mm were troublesome. Due to the very narrow
opening in the beams, it was very hard to pour the concrete inside the molds. A funnel was used
to ease the pouring of the concrete but the ﬁbres would clog the funnel and stop the ﬂow of ECC.
A method to force the concrete was to use a thin metal bar and insert it inside the funnel to get
the concrete following. This methods worked but was very tedious and time consuming, as each
beam took 30 minutes to cast, see Figure 3.2.2 and by than the concrete started to harden and
made the task more diﬃcult. This method is not optimal as the PVA ﬁbres will start to be aligned
in the direction of the rebar and thus the ECC will no longer be a homogeneous material.
Figure 3.2.2: 17.2mm beam casting.
Due to the casting problems with the beams, bigger beams were designed with a much larger cross
section and proved to be much easier to cast as the 70mm opening made the concrete ﬂow without
any diﬃculties. The larger beams were cast in the same way as the smaller ones. Care was taken
to make sure that the concrete mix was homogeneous throughout the casting period.
3.2.3
Flexural test
A ﬂexural test is a 3 point bending test, Figure 3.2.3a, where a specimen is supported at 2 ends
and a load is applied in the middle. The supports allow for free rotation. While the test is easy in
terms of preparation and testing, it is sensitive to specimen geometry and strain rate. This test
provides the ﬂexural strength of the sample beam.
The ﬂexural tests were done on a Instron 5500R, Figure 3.2.3b, which is capable of applying up
to 100kN of force. The machine was calibrated and datalogger was used to collect the data. The
method involved loading the specimen inside the machine and adjusting the span length, Figure
3.2.4a. The testing machine then started to apply a given displacement per minute, Figure 3.2.4b.
The beam tests were done with the machine displacing the sample by 1mm per minute. Due to
the varying height and length of the sample beams, the tests ran between 20 −45 minutes or were
deemed unsafe. As the beams were not all geometrically straight, it was important to make sure
that the 3 point of contacts were relatively even. This was done to avoid load on the thinnest
cross section or an already deformed area of the beam.
Figures 3.2.4c and 3.2.4d shows the two diﬀerent beams in bending.

Chapter 5
Discussion and Perspectives
5.1
Mixing and casting
The ECC trails gave interesting results. The was mainly due to minor changes in the materials
and ﬁbre distribution.
The concrete mixtures for the beam specimens were very dissimilar. It took many trails to get
the right water ratio but even than the results kept varying. This was especially diﬃcult for the
smaller beams, as the concrete was not ﬂowable enough to ﬂow naturally inside the mold but had
to be forced though.
Using diﬀerent mixers also proved to be problematic, as diﬀerent amount of concrete was made
in diﬀerent mixers and there were areas that did not get mixed due to the rotating mixing arm.
This was rectiﬁed by opening the mixer and manually trying scrap the walls of the mixer to make
sure there are not dry material pockets left. This was one of the reasons that led to adding the
ﬂyash and cement last, as to not having the heavier materials holding them down. The ﬁbres were
distributed by sprinkling them instead of just dumping, this was done to make sure that they were
evenly distributed.

(b) ECC during second cylinder cast

The ECC mix for the ﬁrst tunnel segment casting was lumpy and very hard to work with, see
Figure 5.1.1a. The ﬁbres kept collecting inside the nozzle of the concrete pump, which ended

in a complete jam and the pump stopped working. When the pumpline was cleaned some older
concrete pieces was found inside the pump and added pump jamming up. Had the pump not
stopped, a successful concrete cylinder might have been cast.
The insert for the pump of the ﬁrst textile lining was found out to be too narrow. This problem
was mainly due to the pump, the makeshift pump nozzle, that narrowed the pumpline was not
a very good solution. The best thing would have been to replace the complete pumpline with a
smaller one and connect the pumpline directly to the textile lining, as was the case with the 2nd
cast.
The second cylinder ECC mix was successful. It was very easy to work with and was very ﬂowable,
Figure 5.1.1b, which meant that the mixing procedure was also without any problems. There were
some usual problems with the ﬂyash but extra water was added to make sure that the ECC was
ﬂowable.
The ﬁbre amount was also reduced in the second cylinder casting, this precaution was taken to
stop the ﬁbres from lumping together and maybe cause another jam inside the pumpline. This
proved to work quite well as the end product turned out to be very homogeneous and without any
lumps. The concrete was very workable and was pumped without any diﬃculties.
The inﬂatable PVC cylinder started to sag a little due to the weight of the concrete. This was to
be expect and an air gun was ready to provide air when needed. But it seemed that more air was
needed and that was not possible with the current setup and design. If the air supply could have
been increased the concrete cylinder should have been able to hold a more more circle form.

Beam testing
The 3 point ﬂexural test went as planned. The textile ﬁbres could not be tested as it was deemed
unsafe due to their elastic nature. The tests had to be stopped before the textile ﬁbres reached
their ultimate strength, see Figure 5.2.1.

strength then the concrete. This is not optimal, as it will result in a sudden collapse without
any warnings. Either the beam cross section has to be increased or the textile lining thickness be
reduced. Due to this the ﬂexural strength is very high when compared to the 70mm beams.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and Future Research
6.1
Conclusion
The beam samples proved to be problematic with smaller dimensions but this was immediately
corrected when the beam sizes were increased.
Testing the smaller beams did not perform as hoped. The tests provided good results but was
diﬃcult to see when the concrete had cracked. The larger beams on the other hand gave much
better results, where it is clear when the concrete starts cracking.
Casting of the tunnel segment was a success. Optimization of the ECC mixtures resulted in ﬁnding
the perfect composition for casting of the cylinder tunnel segment.
The textile lining design worked as planned. The concrete spread around in the lining as predicted
and formed an almost perfect cylinder. This was especially true with the second casting with the
increased width of the cross section.
While the ﬁrst inﬂated cylinder made by the tailor proved to be a failure, the second cylinder was
also successful. It was able hold under the casting and the hardening phase with minor sagging.
With some optimizations, this method of creating a cylinder could prove to be an alternativ
method in tunnel making design.
6.2
Future Research
It seems that producing ECC concrete is almost streamlined, but there are still some problems with
material consistency like the ﬂyash. This is especially problematic when casting large amounts of
ECC, as materials tend to stick at the sides of the mixers.
The textile lining could also be iterated further open. This could be patterning of lining for better
distribution of the concrete while casting, since some ribs observed were getting blocked during
casting which had to be manually forced though.
The established textile ECC cylinder was not tested due to limited time. This method could be
replicated and cast another cylinder while stopping the formwork to sag and end with a better
cylinder. Tests could be a mere point/line load test or also real world simulation where the cylinder
segment is cast outside underground. This could be done by digging a hole in the ground, putting
a oil drum inside and casting the cylinder inside the drum. Once covered up, the cylinder could
be tested for soil interaction.
Futhermore it could be interesting to see how the formwork will hold when going full scale with a
much larger tube segment with diameters of 3 −4m.
