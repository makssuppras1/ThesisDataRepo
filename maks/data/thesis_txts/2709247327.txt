Abstract
The report investigates a cyclone separator experimentally and through a simple CFD
analysis. Elements to accommodate injection and collection of particle matter was added
to the experimental setup described in Shared Project Report: Design of a Cyclone
Separator Experiment by L.Scou & T.Thomsen[2]. The injection of particle matter still
needs improvement, and minor problems regarding the collection mechanism should be
remedied.
The setup and data collection methodology from the Shared Project Report:...[2] is used
to make pressure drop measurements. The attained pressure drop measurements are
validated against the data from Experimental Investigation of Flow in a Gas Cyclone by
L. Schou[1], showing good accordance. Following the validation of the results, the effect
of changing the outlet design is investigated, showing an increased pressure drop and
reduced swirl.
Visualisation of the flow within the experimental cyclone separator setup is presented,
and several literature-described phenomena are observed; multiple rows of helical particle
strands, lib-leakage, dip-leg ”puﬀing”, and ’Target Zone’ erosion. A flow phenomenon
not described in the literature, a vortex under the incoming flow to the cyclone, is also
documented. The documented vortex is also shown through CFD, though the pressure
drop results from the simulation are not following those measured.
The effects of solids loading on cyclone pressure drop are estimated using three different
models (Smolik [1981], Briggs [1946] and Baskakov [1990])[3] and compared to the
results obtained from running the setup at three different settings. The experimental
results confirm the solids loading effect, which lowers the pressure drop of the cyclone.
The model that most closely represents the data is that of Baskakov (1990).

Introduction
Even though cyclone separators as we know them today have existed since the 1920s[3],
the exact nature of all the flow phenomena within such devices remain a mystery.
Through the years, many authors have tried to create models and ways of estimat-
ing the pressure drop, performance and effective cut-size of these devices, and models
continue to improve to this very day. Even with computational fluid dynamics and its
ability to predict the flow of very many cases to a high degree of accuracy, the flow in
cyclones poses a problem due to the complex nature of turbulent and swirling flow with
solids laden particles.
The simple design and functional principles behind cyclones makes them a highly regarded
invention, even today, where cyclones continue to expand from one industry into another
and even into the household.
With the intent of creating a basis from which further research in cyclone separators
continues, Aerotak ApS teamed up with the Technical University of Denmark to build an
experimental setup. The setup is ideal for experimentation focused on validating current
theories and pushing the development of cyclone design towards greater eﬀiciency and
prediction models towards higher accuracy.
The project initially started in the summer/fall of 2021. The construction of the setup
began, and a study of literature was performed. The primary source of knowledge and
the theoretical foundation for the project comes from the book Gas Cyclones and Swirl
Tubes; Principles, Design and Operation (Second Edition) by Alex C. Hoffmann & Louis
E. Stein[3], which will be cited several times. Besides this comprehensive work, relevant
papers were found if additional information was needed.
The construction of the cyclone is documented in the report Shared Project Report:
Design of a Cyclone Separator Experiment by L.Scou & T.Thomsen[2], concluding a
well-documented construction and design process with a comprehensive guide to taking
measurements on said setup.
It was followed by the work of L. Schou, with a report titled Experimental Investigation
of Flow in a Gas Cyclone[1], which covered a wide range of the fundamental theories
within the field to estimate and predict the performance of cyclones. The theoretical

3.1
Smoke Machine
The author wanted to investigate the flow in the cyclone with a smoke machine. Af-
ter discussion with the project supervisors, it was made clear that the existing smoke
machines were of such poor quality that this would present itself as a challenge since
the smoke output of these devices was rather pitiful. The discussion then turned to the
somewhat recent popularity of Electronic-Cigarettes(e-cig from here on), which in some
instances can produce a substantial amount of smoke in a short while with a seemingly
very high density.
The topic was further investigated.
The liquid used in e-cigs is propylene glycol or
glycerin[4]. From this information, it was found that the glycerine is the part of the e-cig
liquid which quote ’helps create much vapour with a thick and full-bodied consistency
’[5]. The other possible ingredients in e-liquid were dismissed on this background, and
pure glycerine was ordered and used and smoke-liquid.
After asking several different shops/vendors which sell these e-cig devices for inputs,
be it with varied results and enthusiasm, the characteristics of the needed device was
somewhat determined, and a suitable model was found.
After contacting different vendors, a ”SMOK R-KISS KIT WITH TFV-MINI V2”, shown
in fig. (3.1) was ordered. This unit is delivered with a mesh-coil, or ”hyper engine” as it
is marketed. Which allegedly has a longer lifespan from the vendor’s experiences.
After a thorough inspection of the unit and its functions, two adaptors were made to
transform the e-cig into a portable and handheld smoke machine.

3.2
Different Outlet Design
To test the effect of outlet configuration, and see the effect of a ’bad’ outlet compared to
a ’good’ outlet configuration, a mirrored outlet of the same design was 3D-printed. The
initial outlet followed the natural flow path of the existing swirl, as the flow would go up
and out through the vortex finder. It presents the slightest hindrance to the flow, aiding
its turn and straightening out, recovering some of the pressure stored in the dynamic
motion of the swirl. An outlet having the same dimensions but being ’poorly designed’
and not following the direction of the swirling flow would pose as a restriction to the
flow. A picture of the mirrored outlet is shown below, in fig. (3.5a).

Particle Injection & Collection
To investigate the effects of particle loading on the cyclone, several factors needed to
be considered.
• Measurement of particulate matter going in and coming out; validate eﬀiciency
• Knowing and being able to control mass-flow rate; controlled loading
• Mixing of particles in the flow; resemblance of industry loading
Each of these points contains several issues in a practical design that will be addressed.
Validating eﬀiciency: To validate the eﬀiciency of the cyclone at least two of the fol-
lowing needs to be known; the feed, the captured or the emitted particles. As described
earlier, in section (2.2). If the particles were to be ”poured” into the blower, which
supplies the system with airflow, several problems could arise;
• At the blower:
1. Caked material already in the blower could dislodge and contaminate the
sample
2. Material could exit directly at the blower due to its housing not being fully
sealed
3. Particles impacting the blower blades could reduce in size, affecting the ex-
pected density distribution of the loaded particles
4. Particles impacting the blower blades could cake on the blades
• In the ducting
1. Because the piping is not smooth, particles could deposit within them or in

Controlled Loading: Being able to control and know the loading of the cyclone is
crucial to measure its performance accurately and to be able to compare with existing
literature. Controlling the loading is not a trivial task. Several solutions to this have been

are mentioned below:
• A cylinder going ”up” a tube and particles being ”swept up” by a rotating broom.
This method was used in similar experiments. It should be placed at the inlet to
the blower or within the air-stream. Additionally, it was noted that as the piston
goes up, it could result in the packing of the particle matter at the bottom of the
tube as it moves up towards the rotating broom and thereby effectively changing
the mass-loading while the experiment is running.
• A ”sandblasting” type device. The ”siphon type” sandblaster uses the venturi
effect to suck up blasting media and blow it out through the nozzle. Doing so
requires a suﬀiciently high flow rate to work properly, which would add to the mass
flow rate of gas entering the cyclone, and therefore need to be placed before the
flow meter (a problem described earlier).
• ”Conveyor” type of device, ”unloading” particles into the air-stream. Particles
enter the air-stream via a ”conveyor” and get dispersed as it ”falls” in from the
top. Depending on the ”conveying” type, particle packing would again/still be a
problem, primarily if a ”screw” type or actual belt conveyor is used. However, it
could be placed at any point in the system if the flow is of suﬀicient magnitude.
• A fluid bed type of device could be used to suspend particles in the flow, but the
mixing with the flow stream coming from the blower and controlling of flow rate
to this device would pose a problem.
Some of the discussed options and methods are mentioned here, but the discussion
was ongoing. Until someone does a specific study on this topic, the classification and
pros/cons for different methods remains diﬀicult to compare directly.
Resemblance of industry loading: Cyclones in laboratories usually aims to give an
insight into the workings of cyclones used within the industry. Therefore having a loading
effect representing actual usage can be of great value. It is desired to have the particles
entirely distributed within the flow coming to the inlet, not affected by how the particles
are introduced into the air stream.

4.1.1
Description of operation
The lid is removed by removing the sealing tape around the edge; then, the four retaining
screws are removed. After that, the lid is free to be screwed off; friction holds it in place.
A measured amount of particle matter can be poured into the container, and it can be
closed and sealed, reversing the steps. A silicon tube is connected to the top of the lid,
which supplies a set static pressure, which forces particles into the flow; a back pressure
of ≈5900[Pa] was used. On the front of the funnel is access to a screw that regulates
the size of the particle outlet and can close it off altogether when needed.
The screw is completely closed as the blower is turned on, and desired initial flow is
achieved stably. Making sure the back-pressure is turned OFF the screw is turned out
to the set depth, though opening for the particle flow, the pressure from within the flow
moving below does not allow particles to flow. After sampling is turned on, the flow
to the container is opened. Particle matter is forced into the flow. As the container is
emptied from particle matter, the sampling is ended, and the blower is turned off. The
loading time should be noted from the sampling time or an external timer. The author
used an external timer.

Particle Collection Bucket
A bucket assembly was added to the particle outlet of the cyclone to collect separated
particle matter. When researching the design of cyclones, the importance of a ’vortex
stabiliser’[3] became apparent. Initially, the concern was to avoid the vortex core ex-
tending down into the dust bucket, as this would allow captured material to reenter into
the vortex. Upon further examination of the literature, if correctly designed, a vortex
stabiliser provides the vortex with a surface to centre itself; this will improve separation
eﬀiciency by avoiding vortex instability and ’wandering’, which can entrain dust off the
walls of the separator cone or dip-leg. Additionally, terminating the vortex above the
dust-bucket creates an increase in static pressure below the vortex stabiliser, which is
usually desirable[3].
The ’vortex stabiliser’ is a simple ’Chinese hat’ type. The angle of the ’hat’ should be
between 90◦and 120◦[3], an angel of 108◦was chosen from intuition and its personal
significance. It should be noted that in the Van Tongeren AC configuration, the solids
outlet is ≈3/4Dx. Therefore the clearance between the vortex stabiliser and the dip-leg
should be ≈1/3Dx[3]. Part of the entire dust-bucket assembly design was the ability
to adjust the height of the vortex stabiliser, as this will allow for future optimisation and
experimentation on related matters such as clearing margin, submergence into the dip-

6.2
Sampling Time
Before discussing experiments involving data collection, the sampling time for such ex-
periments will be determined.
Determining a sampling rate and period was done to make a consistent measure with
as least variance as possible. It is mentioned by Hoffmann & Stein[3] that the typical
particle residence time within a cyclone lies between 20-50 milliseconds(ms) for small
high-velocity cyclones and about 1-2 seconds for large industrial units. Based on this
remark, an adequate sampling rate of 2 seconds is assumed and using a factor of 10
to have a stable range over which to mean the sample, equating to a sampling time
Experimental Investigation of a Cyclone Separator
31
==PAGE:43==
of about 20 seconds, containing 10 data points. The particle residence time is not the
only factor to consider, as the flow and sensors’ stability should also be considered.
As observed by L. Schou[1], though the flow rate had stabilised, the mean pressure
(over 2 seconds) was still moving (up/down) on a larger time scale(within the 1-minute
sampling). Because of the instability, she opted to narrow the used part to the last 30
seconds of the 1-minute sample. Therefore, a raw data set of a prolonged period is
crucial to evaluate when the actual sampling time should start. Additionally, to make
samples 2-3 times the length of the desired sampling time, to allow for inspection of
the data to avoid inconsistencies. When combining these precautions, the conclusion
becomes that a sampling time of 1 minute, after conditions have stabilised (referring
to the flow rate), allows for an adequate space within which to make a sample of a
minimum of 20 seconds. The sampling length of an unloaded cyclone has now been
covered, whereas the loaded cyclone differs.

Not knowing the powder’s particle size distribution, it is assumed that the cyclone could
run at a 99.5% eﬀiciency at lower speeds. If running at 99.5% eﬀiciency, 0.5g would end
up as particle overflow per 100g of particle matter introduced to the cyclone. Taking
precautionary measures and designing the system to hold 500g would result in 2.5g
in overflow, allowing for some particle deposit within the outlet pipes and weighting
insecurities, assumingly without significant influence to the final measure.

6.3.1
Effect of non-optimal outlet conditions
As earlier described in the section about Additional Setup3.2, another outlet was made
to test the effect of a ”bad” outlet design, holding the dimensions of the outlet equal
(form/design, cross-sectional areas, radii). A plot comparing the pressure drop over the
cyclone is shown in the figure below, fig. (6.12), where it is evident that the experienced
pressure drop over the cyclone is greater at every given point at the Industrial outlet,
whereas the pressure drop is lower at the point in the vortex finder.
The opposite
reactions indicate the expected effect a non-optimised outlet would have. It reduces
the swirl in the cyclone and increases the flow resistance by forcing the rotating gas
in the vortex finder to ’turn against’ its momentum of inertia.
Less swirl results in
a reduced pressure drop in the vortex finder and thereby lower dynamic pressure, and
correspondingly a larger pressure drop at the industrial outlet where the flow is in essence
restricted by the poor outlet design. The increase in total pressure drop is also evident by
the reduced reachable ’top speed’, with an inlet velocity of only 20,44 m/s. Additionally,
while running the tests, the author noticed that the fan required more power (controlled
in engine Hz), and the cyclone emitted a noticeably different sound than the original
outlet configuration.

Discussion
7.0.1
Particle Injection
The dispersion of particles into the flow does not fully live up to the desired specification
of particles being fully dispersed and mixed into the flow. As also shown, in fig. (6.5), the
particles are not seeded in a single strain but are chaotically shifting around, effectively
covering the majority of the space of the inlet region.
It was observed that as the
string of particles shifted to the upper part of the inlet, it highlighted more clearly the
dynamics which appear when the particles enter the flow region along the roof of the
cyclone, where they travel around in the upper half of the cyclone and re-encounter the
flow from the inlet. Therefore, it is worth acknowledging that the particle introduction is
sub-optimal and that another solution, resulting in better particle dispersal, is needed to
show better the entire range of particle motion within the cyclone. Another observation
is the sporadically appearing second particles strain due to lack of particles entering into
the far-side region causing its formation.
Not only does this hint at poor particle dispersion/mixing, but it also shows that the flow
coming into the cyclone is not uniform but contains some turbulence, most likely due to
either the 90◦bend up-streams of the setup or residual turbulence from the blower.
Besides particle dispersing, the particle injection experienced frequent clogging or run-
out. While running a test and as the container slowly emptied, particle matter would
stack up on the walls of the container funnel and, when collapsing, cause the outlet
to block. As already mentioned earlier, this was mitigated by tapping the container
and slightly moving the adjustment screw. The funnel would need to be redesigned
to avoid clogging due to particle matter packing, t. Another reason for clogging was
also experienced, as larger particles than the opening of the injection outlet would block
particle flow. These larger particles could be crystallised particle matter, likely due to
humidity and fragments that contaminated the sample.
The particle matter should
be poured through a sieve before being put into the container to avoid fragments or
crystallised particle matter influencing the testing.

7.0.2
Particle Collection - The Bucket
The design of the particle collection bucket was based on polymer construction, the
bucket in PMMA and the mount in POM. Because of the material choice, it was assumed
that a second guide was needed to stabilise the Vortex Stabiliser. It was assumed that
the intense swirl of the vortex core would cause vibration or a considerable strain to be
exerted on the vortex stabiliser axle, which could, in turn, cause the PMMA of the bucket
itself to fracture. The author hypothesised that the lack of material rigidity would cause
problems, which was discussed with the mechanic at the DTU machine shop, which led
to the second guide. The practical implication of having a second guide at the bottom
of the cyclone was not initially considered. It made the measure of the particle matter
collected in the bucket extremely uncertain due to a considerable amount of residue
being caked on the bucket mount and horizontal surfaces of the triad legs going to the
guide. Caked particle matter would lead to imprecise measurements of collected matter
and wrongly estimated eﬀiciency. The second guide also poses additional diﬀiculty in
disassembly. It should be investigated if the second guide is necessary or if the bucket and
vortex stabiliser could take the strain if any is present at all. The vortex stabiliser could
also be mounted with a shorter axle on the bucket mount. However, particle matter
would continue to build up/cake on the axle shaft and bucket mount inner surfaces,
causing the same measuring insecurity.
7.0.3
Flow Sensor
The flow sensor’s data output is filtered somehow, given its lack of sensitivity to imme-
diate changes in flow conditions. Significant flow changes cause large changes to the
flow seen and reported by the sensor. However, it appears to do some internal data
processing or signal filtering, using a mean of some seconds, filtering out a relative de-
viation to the mean to stabilise the output signal. If precise and deviation sensitive data
is to be collected, the current flow measuring setup is not well suited. Additionally, the
flow meter has not yet been verified by a calibration device, such as an aperture pressure
difference device, thereby still making its accuracy questionable.
7.0.4
Particle size
From the literature, particle size could lie within the range of 39.4-289.2µ[6]. In deter-
mining the particle size, they tested maltodextrin from three different suppliers resulting
in three different particle size ranges; A ranging from 59.9-109.2µ, B from 79.3-289.2µ
and C from 39.4-71.3µ. The actual particle size distribution of the used maltodextrin
remains unknown, which raises problems concerning the evaluation of cyclone eﬀiciency
and its cut size. The lack of knowledge regarding the particle size distribution renders
it impossible to test whether or not the estimated cut size is accurate. Some particle
matter did make it to the vortex finder, showing that some particles within the used
powder are smaller than the cut-size of the cyclone, though not a significant weight
fraction as shown by the weighting of the Vortex Finder tube.

Conclusion
The project was undertaken in several stages, and it concludes on some of those stages
in this report, as future work is laid out.
Together with L. Schou, the initial construction resulted in the founding work for this
project. The additions to the experimental setup were manufactured and implemented
from that work. Neither the particle injection nor the particle collection bucket worked
entirely as intended. Would this have been the case, it would have been a pure strike of
divine grace. The Particle injection and collection bucket did work to a function to an
extended degree, leading to several insights into the dynamics of cyclone behaviour and
flow. Only minor adjustments are needed to the bucket to make it work as seamlessly
as intended, whereas a different approach might be needed to the particle injection/dis-
persion method.
The setup exhibited the same behaviour as the results from L. Schou[1] showed, thereby
validating the method used, even when separated by time and additions to the setup.
As expected, the change in outlet to a non-optimal design, which does not follow inertia
of the flow, proved itself to cause a higher pressure drop and presumably a lower swirl,
judging from the lowered pressure difference in the vortex finder and higher pressure
difference to the industrial outlet.
Smoke proves to have very limited usability to illustrate the flow in cyclones due to a
large amount of swirl and turbulence present, even at very low inlet speeds. The large
swirl and turbulence cause the smoke to dilute quickly, rendering it useless for practical
purposes, such as characterising flow patterns at industry comparable inlet velocities.
Outside of practical comparison, the use of smoke did prove the presence of cyclonic
swirl even at very low inlet velocities, 1m/s, which was unexpected when looking at
the measured pressure drop at said velocities. The use of smoke and a laser plane also
revealed a flow phenomenon which the author initially did not know off or had seen in
the literature, but which, on closer inspection, was present in the CFD model at high
inlet speeds; also explaining the additional erosion which did not coincide with the inlet
’Target Zone’.
The introduction of particles to the cyclone led to prove several literature-described
Experimental Investigation of a Cyclone Separator

phenomena; multiple rows of helical particle strands, lib-leakage, dip-leg ”puﬀing” and
’Target Zone’ erosion, as well as the loading effect of lowering the cyclone pressure drop.
In comparing the experimental pressure drop with the described models for predicting the
effect of solids loading, the model by Baskakov came close. From Baskakov’s paper[7],
the cyclone design seems to differ significantly; the inlet is angled, outlet diameter is large
compared to the body diameter and uses a hot carrier gas with a temperature of 250◦C,
making its usability rather impressive as other authors have noted it also predicting their
observed pressure drop well[3].

Future Work
The future work is divided into; practical work on the setup itself and theoretical work,
testing and experiments.
9.1
Practical tasks
The practical tasks start from the blower, piping system, and surrounding factors before
going to the cyclone.
• Installation of a flow straightener, five diameter lengths before the flow measuring
device, to ensure that neither bends in the system piping nor the blower, leaves
traces of vorticity, which could influence the flow measure or particle dispersion.
• Mounting an aperture, two diameters after the flow measuring device and doing
a series of tests to establish the flow meter’s accuracy.
• Redesign of particle injection to ensure better mixing of particle matter in the flow.
• Redesign the particle injection method to ensure that particles are dispersed into
the flow uniformly. Good particle dispersion is important to utilise the experimental
setup’s potential fully.
• Redesign of particle bucket, by testing, if the second guide is needed at all, by
changing the mounting flange. Alternatively, consider if the vortex finder should
be mounted on the current mounting flange.
• Sealing of cyclone, to avoid particle matter loss, through cracks and holes. From
the author’s experience, any small opening, hole or surfaces not mating properly
will make themselves apparent by the appearance of particle matter outside the
cyclone within which it should have stayed. Leaks become especially important
when using a particle matter which could pose a health risk, such as soda-lime-glass
dust, fly-ash, chalk, and the list goes on.

9.2
Theoretical work, Testing and Experiments
Interesting theoretical work remains.
• A CFD model which can accurately predict the pressure drop of the experimental
cyclone should be made. When pressure drop predictions and measurements are
in accordance, then it is that the effect of particle matter in the simulation can be
investigated.
• Determination of cut-size, through experiments, using particle matter with a
known particle-size distribution and collecting and testing the particle matter emit-
ted out the vortex finder.
• Characterisation of the flow pattern within the cyclone, using hot-wire and LDA
methods to establish the flow characteristics of the cyclone to validate theoretical
models/estimation and CFD calculations.
• Effect/Optimisation of vortex finder design and its outlet configurations. Hoff-
mann & Stein have proposed/documented several different outlet configurations
and their ability to recover pressure, referring to Vortex-tube geometries Fig.
15.1.12[3]. It would be worthwhile to peer-review their findings and conjure up
more effective outlet designs for pressure recovery and swirl intensification.
• Optimisation of vortex stabiliser, to characterise span of angels, clearance require-
ments and effect of height/submergence into dip-leg.
• Data analysis of raw pressure measurements. The swirling flow in cyclones emit
sound and have a specific pressure behaviour that the author believes can be
used to validate if a production line is running optimally or if specific problems
are developing, as this cause the ’note’ of the cyclone to change audibly. The
changing sound of the cyclone was experienced when varying inlet speeds/flow
rate, particle loading, outlet designs and vortex stabiliser height.
These are areas that the author believes needs further investigation.

H = 1098 mm
