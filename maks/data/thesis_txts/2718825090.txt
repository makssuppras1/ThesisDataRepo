Abstract 
The pressure on the world’s resources is more significant than ever before, 
emphasising a broad agreement that society needs to shift from a linear to a circular 
economy (CE). Policy interventions are necessary to accelerate transitions; however, 
the positive outcomes of such policies are often taken for granted. Despite well-
meaning intentions, political interventions often fail to solve or even create new 
problems due to unintended consequences and rebound effects. Methods for 
investigating unintended consequences frequently focus on quantifying scales and 
magnitudes. Still, a more explorative ex-ante tool is rarely adopted before 
implementing policies.  
This thesis proposes a tool to support decision-makers in investigating policies' 
potential intended and unintended consequences on complex systems, utilising a Rich 
Picture modelling approach. The tool is developed and tested through several case 
studies. The analysis is targeted at the Circular Economy Action Plan (CEAP) 
measures, focusing on smartphones as a product group. The case studies are 
conducted through iterative cycles with stakeholder engagement, shedding light on a 
broad spectrum of possible outcomes and making the iterative development of the 
tool explicit. 
The tool is composed of three main components: (i) the life cycle perspective, (ii) the 
stakeholders’ perspective, and (iii) the consequences tree – which allows a broad 
exploration of potential intended and unintended outcomes of policies. The analyses 
of the CEAP policy measures indicate that a complex set of unintended consequences 
can potentially occur due to the influence with product design, consumption patterns, 
and waste management.  
The qualitative and exploratory aspect of the tool lays the ground for a more in-depth 
assessment of selected areas where unintended consequences seem severe.  
Ultimately, the proposed step-by-step procedure enables decision-makers to 
recognise the potential unintended consequences of policy design for them to be 
minimised.

1. Introduction 
For more than a century, industrial evolution has been dominated by a linear 
economy in which materials are extracted from the earth, treated into products, 
consumed, and finally disposed of. As the world’s population is growing, so is the 
demand for raw materials. The extraction and processing of raw materials increase 
energy consumption and emissions, negatively impacting the environment (European 
Parliament, 2015). 
A circular economic system rethinks how the world consumes and produces goods 
and services and is radically different from a linear economy. By transitioning to a 
circular economy (CE), the challenges with resource and energy use can be 
addressed. A CE is restorative and regenerative and aims at keeping the resources in 
the loop and to the highest possible value and utility (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 
2015). The transition to a CE will significantly contribute to climate neutrality by 2050 
and decouple economic growth from resource use (European Commission, 2020).  
Key elements in the circular economy transition are eco-design, circular business 
models, sustainable consumption patterns, and increased reuse and recycling. 
However, enabling conditions such as policies and regulations need to be in place to 
encourage them. Such policies need to target the whole life cycle to address the 
challenges of the circular economy. 
The European Commission adopted the Circular Economy Action Plan (CEAP) in 
2020 to achieve a cleaner and more competitive Europe in co-creation with 
economic actors, consumers, citizens, and civil society organisations. It builds upon 
the European Green Deal and the circular economy actions implemented since 2015 
to accelerate transformational change (European Commission, 2019). The new plan 
seeks to streamline the regulatory framework for a sustainable future, maximising 
opportunities while minimising burdens on people and businesses (European 
Commission, 2020). 
An essential aspect of the CEAP concerns electronics and ICT (information and 
communications technology). Electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) continues to 
be one of the fastest-growing waste streams in the EU (European Parliament, 2022). 
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
 
Reasons for this are many, such as products not being repairable, the software cannot 
be updated, or materials not being recovered.  It is estimated that less than 40% of 
electronic waste is recycled in the EU (European Commission, 2020), while the 
average number of electronic devices per EU capita is estimated to grow from 5.6 in 
2018 to 9.4 in 2023 (Cisco, 2020). This, combined with shorter lifetimes than desired 
and increasing obsolescence of electronic products emphasises the urgency to 
rethink the way smartphones are treated in the economy. E.g., a smartphone has an 
actual lifetime of 1.8 years, and a designed lifetime of 2, while the desired lifetime is 
5.2 years (European Environment Agency, 2023). 
The CEAP targets the challenges by introducing new regulatory measures, mobilising 
existing initiatives, and implementing them in the coming years. These count 
regulatory measures for the design of electronics and ICT for improved circularity, 
strengthening consumers’ right to repair, regulations of common chargers, improving 
waste collection and treatments, and reviewing the rules on hazardous substances in 
electronics (European Commission, 2020). 
However, implementing alternative CE initiatives might be more complex than it 
seems in the actions presented in the plan. Policies can have unintended 
consequences or side effects due to shifts in production and consumption (Niero et 
al., 2021). Such consequences can be both indirect, undesirable, and unanticipated, 
and occur because they intervene with highly complex social, economic, and 
environmental systems.  
A previously known instance is the unintended consequence of subsidies for more 
energy-efficient appliances, such as refrigerators. Studies show that while this type of 
policy has encouraged many consumers to purchase new refrigerators, as the 
policymakers intended, almost half of the consumers decided not to discard their old 
products, and either kept them, gave them away or sold them (Eisen & Green, 2010). 
The result was an increase in overall GHG emissions, despite the opposite intention.  
This phenomenon can also be explained by the term rebound effect (RE), which is a 
certain type of unintended consequence. It is often described as an improvement in 
efficiency that generates a counteracting effect, which reduces or entirely offsets the 
==PAGE:9==
9 
 
 
improvements (Sonnberger & Gross, 2018). The same type of RE is illustrated within 
the EU. While the energy efficiency of household appliances has improved 
significantly over the past two decades, these improvements have been mitigated by 
an increase in the amount and use of appliances. As a result, overall electricity 
consumption per household for appliances has gone up, demonstrating the RE 
(Maxwell, Owen, et al., 2011). It thus seems crucial that efficiency improvement (and 
thus saved resources and money) gained by the CE transition is directed to 
sustainable consumption practices (Korhonen et al., 2018).  
It is essential to take a systemic perspective when assessing policies introduced to 
accelerate sustainable development. It is necessary not only to think about the 
technical solutions but also how they will affect the consumer, producer, and society 
as a whole. By doing so, it is possible to achieve more of the intended benefits and 
minimise possible unintended consequences. 
1.1. 
Research goals 
Unintended consequences can occur as a result of the diffusion of electronics and 
ICT, just as CE policy initiatives can bring unforeseen outcomes and REs. The policies 
and regulations in place for the design of new products play an essential role in 
determining these. Both policymakers and designers have the responsibility to deal 
with the long-term sustainability of incremental improvements.  
As some designers and policymakers do not have a practice of considering 
unintended consequences, they can benefit from having a tool that can make them 
visible and actionable.  
This leads to the formulation of the overall goals of this thesis. The first goal is rather 
descriptive and focuses on the policy, whereas the second goal is prescriptive to 
building a tool. 
1. To investigate the potential intended and unintended consequences of the 
Circular Economy Action Plan in the life cycle of electronic products  
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
 
2. To develop a support to help companies and decision-makers investigate the 
potential intended and unintended consequences of policies 
Researchers have been concerned with this issue of unintended consequences and 
REs, particularly regarding energy efficiency. Methods in the field to investigate the 
effects are dominated by mathematical models with economic data or simulations of 
system behaviour. The aim is to propose a tool that addresses a gap for a more 
explorative design-based tool. It takes its starting point in a Rich Picture approach to 
capture the situation’s complexity, including socio-technical aspects.

2.1. 
Design Research Methodology 
This thesis follows the Design Research Methodology (DRM) to fulfil the two research 
goals, being a research methodology introduced by Blessing & Chakrabarti (2009) to 
address design research issues. The methodology aims to achieve more rigorous 
research for efficient and effective design. The DRM framework consists of four 
stages, RC, DS I, PS and DS II, as outlined in the following (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 
2009). 
Research Clarification (RC) 
The objectives of the RC stage are to identify the expected research goals and the 
project’s focus. This includes the research problems, questions, and the areas in which 
contributions are expected. This will lay the ground for the focus of Descriptive Study 
I.  
Descriptive Study I (DS I) 
The DS I stage aims to increase the understanding of design by reviewing the 
literature and undertaking empirical research. The objective of this stage is to obtain 
a better understanding of the existing situation. Factors that might be suitable to 
address in the prescriptive study to improve the current state are suggested. 
Prescriptive Study (PS) 
The overall aim of design research is to develop support for improving design, which 
is done in the PS. The support developed can take any form and medium. According 
to DRM, a systematic way of creating design support in a research project includes 
empirical data and the development of a model of the desired situation.  
Descriptive Study II (DS II) 
The DS II stage focuses on the evaluation of the support. The evaluation should 
involve, besides the researcher, the users to reveal the real issues of using the support 
in practice. A detailed evaluation makes it possible to determine whether the goals 
have been achieved and improve the support.

2.2.1. Literature review – Q1 and Q2 
The literature reviews are mainly used in the DS I stage to obtain a better 
understanding of the existing situation. A literature review summarises and provides 
a critical assessment of the available literature on a specific subject (Hart, 1998).  
The three-step approach provided by Biolchini et al. (2005) for systematic reviews 
conduction was used for the literature reviews in this thesis. The steps are planning, 
execution, and result analysis. In the planning phase, research objectives and central 
research questions are listed as well as the methods that will be used to do the review. 
The execution stage involves studies identification, selection and evaluation of the 
studies. In the result analysis phase, data from the articles are synthesised and 
presented.  
 
Figure 3: Literature review conduction process. Simplified model inspired by (Biolchini et al., 2005) 
The research questions and the execution are explained in the following paragraphs. 
The results are critically described and analysed in section 3. 
Q1: What are the characteristics of policy-induced CE transitions that might lead to 
unintended consequences?  
The first question relates to DS1, as the goal is to understand the current situation. It 
seeks to shed light on the characteristics of policy-induced CE transitions that might 
lead to unintended consequences.  
In this study, the type of literature review known as overview review (Grant & Booth, 
2009) was utilised to give a summary of the literature on the area and describe its 
characteristics. This overview review was done with an explorative approach, 
providing a broad summation of the topics.  
The outcome is an analysis of the role and consequences of policies in CE transitions, 
presented in section 3.1.

Q2: What system tools can help map the (un)intended consequences of CE policies? 
The second question also contributes to the DS1, as it implies an investigation of 
existing system tools that can help map the (un)intended consequences of CE policies. 
It was investigated through a mapping review/systematic map (Grant & Booth, 2009), 
which can be used to map and categorise existing literature on a particular topic, 
identifying gaps in research literature from which to commission further research. The 
goal was to obtain a tabular mapping of which kinds of methods and tools have 
frequently been used in the assessment of unintended consequences. The table of 
existing methods indicates relevant methods or tools that are suitable for investigating 
unintended consequences and the gaps to further guide policy and business decision-
makers.

The outcome of the search was a total of 6196 results. Some subject areas were 
excluded to reduce the number of results. Results related to side effects in medical 
studies were not of interest; hence subject areas such as medicine, pharmacy, 
immunology, dentistry, and veterinary were excluded, which left 1245 documents. 
The abstracts of the 100 most cited papers were read through and sorted by their 
relevance to the topic. Relevance was determined by if the document concerned a

or policy interventions. Additional papers were also identified by rolling the snowball, 
i.e. by following references in the papers from the search.

The tools and methods obtained in the literature review follow the categorisation 
proposed by Little et al. (2016), who categorise the environmental sustainability 
assessment approaches into design-based or computational frameworks and 
indicators. Design-based means that they follow principles and guidelines, enabling 
sustainability thinking to be a part of the design process – especially in the determining 
early stages – to identify the relationships between the parts of the system and 
improve the whole. On the other hand, the computational frameworks and indicators 
range from pure indicators, which are categorised and quantified impact, to 
computational frameworks, which mathematically model system behaviour. (Little et 
al., 2016).  
The outcome of this research question is the description and critical analysis of the 
methods and tools found in the literature review. The findings are documented in 
section 3.2.  
Based on the knowledge gained from Q1 and Q2, requirements for a tool were 
identified throughout the section, and DS I is concluded by presenting a list of 
requirements for a tool in section 3.3. 
2.2.2. Case studies: Q3, Q4, and Q5 
Case studies were used in the PS and the DS II to respond to research questions 3, 4, 
and 5. The case study as a research method is used to build theory by using cases to 
create theoretical constructs or propositions from empirical evidence (Eisenhardt, 
1989). Yin (2015) considers the definition of a case study to be an empirical inquiry 
that closely examines a contemporary phenomenon (the case) within its real-world 
context.  
The case studies performed in this thesis consist of three cases where the potential 
unintended consequences of three measures in the CEAP associated with 
smartphones were investigated.

According to Eisenhardt & Graebner (2007), the central idea is to use cases to develop 
theory inductively, i.e., observing, making patterns and building theory. The theory is

cases, and to evaluate the tool (Q3, 4, and 5)

as there was an iterative movement between the questions. This means that the tool 
building, analysis, and internal evaluation were done in subsequent cycles. The reason 
for doing it in cycles was mainly that the CEAP concerning electronics suggests five

Q3: How should a support look like to help understand (un)intended consequences 
in policy-induced CE transitions? 
The third question opens the prescriptive study, which contains the tool building and 
testing. It seeks to identify how a support should look to help understand (un)intended 
consequences in policy-induced CE transitions.  
Tools and methods were sought to find a tool that fitted the requirements and the 
gaps. A Rich Picture model was chosen as it responds well to the requirements. It is 
a system visualisation method used to depict complicated situations (The Open 
University, 2000). Rich Pictures help gain an overview of the bigger picture and the 
maze of structures and processes operative in a context (Bell & Morse, 2013). 
Examples in the literature show that such a tool can be used to expose underlying 
issues and unintended consequences (e.g., Bell & Morse, 2013; Conte & Davidson, 
2020), however empirical evidence on the use of Rich Pictures are limited. 
The Rich Picture tool was adapted according to the identified characteristics of 
systems under circular economy transitions, as presented in section 4.1 Moreover, a 
step-by-step approach was developed based on the different elements of the tool, 
guiding using the tool. Hence, the tool was adapted to have more guidelines than 
originally prescribed by Checkland et al. (2020). The step-by-step tool is presented in 
section 4.1.1.  
A particular part of the tool was built by gathering available knowledge on the existing 
consequences of electronics, ICT and CE. This is the so-called catalogue of unintended 
consequences, presented in section 4.1.2. The development of this catalogue involved 
a literature review to identify earlier discussed unintended consequences and REs in 
research. The catalogue was a necessary addition for the tool to be able to provide 
the knowledge-based background information which could be utilised in the analysis 
of the CEAP.

3. Literature Review – Descriptive Study 1  
This chapter presents the outcomes of the two first research questions. It starts by 
outlining the role and consequences of policies in the CE transition. This includes a 
description of the CE and how policies are positioned and contributes to CE 
transitions. It is found that it requires a new type of holistic policy-making with 
instruments focusing on product design, use patterns, and end-of-life. Moreover, 
synergies and trade-offs across different dimensions should be considered.  
It is investigated how policies have been used so far to implement CE strategies in 
the EU, and the newly adopted CEAP is presented in continuation of this. The 
concepts of unintended consequences of policies and innovation as well as REs are 
unfolded.  
Moreover, already existing methods and tools for assessing unintended 
consequences are mapped. It is found that there is a gap for a design-based, 
explorative tool. The chapter concludes by identifying the requirements for such a 
tool, which can help assess the intended and unintended consequences of policies.

3.1. 
The role and consequences of policies in Circular Economy 
transitions 
3.1.1. 
Circular economy 
The CE approach puts an end to the traditional linear process of production of take-
make-use-dispose and a system largely reliant on fossil fuels. According to the (Ellen 
MacArthur Foundation, 2023b), the CE is based on three principles driven by design: 
• 
Eliminate waste and pollution 
• 
Circulate products and materials (at their highest value) 
• 
Regenerate nature 
These three principles are supported by the transition to renewable energy and 
materials. Ultimately, the goal is to decouple economic growth from the use of 
resources.  
Despite the school of thought of the circular economy can vary, the core refers to 
three activities: reuse at the product level (repair and refurbish), reuse at the 
component level (remanufacture), and reuse at the material level (recycling) (Zink & 
Geyer, 2017). 
At a product design- and business model level, Bocken et al. (2016) suggest 
minimising resource use and emissions by slowing, closing, and narrowing. To slow 
resource loops, products need to be designed for product-life extension, e.g., through 
repairability, which means that the utilisation period of the product is prolonged, 
resulting in slowing down the resource flow. Closing resource loops, on the other 
hand, refers to the recycling of materials by creating a connection between post-use 
and new production, creating a circular flow of resources. Lastly, the principle of 
narrowing encourages using fewer resources per product and thus limit the resource 
flow (Bocken et al., 2016). The CE strategies that can be used to transition to a CE 
address different stages in the life cycle, creating various dynamics and resulting in 
different outcomes. It is thus essential to establish a systemic perspective that takes 
the whole life cycle into account when assessing the outcomes of CE strategies. Two 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
 
main cycles constitute a CE system, a technical and a biological cycle (Braungart et 
al., 2007; Ellen MacArthur Foundation, n.d.-a).  
The Ellen MacArthur Foundation visualises the flow of materials in a CE systems 
diagram, also called the Butterfly diagram. In the technical cycle, products and 
materials are circulated through processes such as reuse, repair, remanufacture and 
recycling, in line with slowing, closing, and narrowing principles. The right side 
consists of several loops, with the inner loops being the highest level of value 
maintenance (sharing, repairing, reusing). The outer circles are related to 
refurbishment, remanufacturing, and recycling of materials and products and are thus 
the ‘last resort’ when it comes to resource management in the CE. In the biological 
cycle, the nutrients from biodegradable materials are returned to the earth to 
regenerate nature by, e.g., employing farming practices that allow nature to rebuild 
soils.  
 
Figure 8 Butterfly Diagram. Source: Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019 
The Butterfly diagram is a simple model of how the CE works and the flows between 
actors. Zink & Geyer (2017) argue that the butterfly model represents the economy 
as a simplified engineering model. Material flows travel directly from the consumer 
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
 
to the collector, to the secondary processor and back to the consumer. They 
underline that the actual CE is more complex because, between every step, there is a 
market in which secondary goods compete with primary goods. This competition is 
difficult to predict and visualise in an engineering scheme.   
3.1.2. Policies in circular economy transitions 
Despite somehow agreement for the transition to a CE, the actual transition is lagging. 
The economy of the world is only 8.6% circular, meaning that more than 91% of the 
materials used globally go to waste (Circle Economy, 2022).  
It is essential to think systemic when working with CE transitions and policies. 
Kirchherr et al. (2017) argue that CE systems can be interpreted at three levels,  
micro-, meso-, and macro level. The micro-system perspective considers products, 
individuals, consumers, and what needs to happen to increase the circularity. The 
mesosystem perspective – also called the regional level - usually focuses on actors 
who can benefit from symbiotic relations, e.g., eco-industrial parks as systems, where 
different system entities rely on sharing resources. The macro-system level highlights 
the need to adjust the industrial composition and structure of the entire economy and 
areas that affect society. Among others, Jackson et al., (2014) argue that to 
successfully transition to a CE, it must occur on all three levels of the CE system.  
According to Wasserbaur et al. (2022), who build upon the work of Planing (2015), a 
systemic transition to a CE includes four fundamental building blocks. The first block 
implies materials and product design, such as the adoption of eco-design principles, 
careful material selection, and a product life extension mindset to keep the products 
and materials in the loop at their highest possibly value. Secondly, circular business 
models with offerings based on capturing and remaining value in products, 
encouraging take-back systems, leasing, sharing, and pay-per-use should be utilised. 
The third building block implies reverse supply networks. i.e., making the recovery of 
materials from used products profitable in supply chains.  
To enable these three aspects to come into play, the fourth building block entails the 
enabling conditions. These can be enabling policies, regulations, financing, support for 
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
 
secondary materials and products, and raising consumer awareness (Wasserbaur et 
al., 2022).  
Winans et al. (2017) refer to policy instruments as being regulatory and economic 
instruments implemented to achieve an effect that will not occur in the absence of 
governmental intervention. This view suggests that society will not be able to regulate 
itself and make sustainable changes; political interference is necessary. Benoy & 
Lehne (2018) divide policy instruments into four categories; regulatory, financial, 
institutional, or private sector-led. Regulatory policies refer to law and administrative 
rules, while financial instruments include fees, taxes and subsidies. Institutional 
instruments are the provision of public services and other direct actions by the 
government, and private-sector led can be any policies, targets, or commitments set 
by private-sector.  
Wasserbaur et al. (2022) consider governmental policies and business models as key 
elements for a transition to the CE and are occupied with the interplay between them. 
By reviewing the literature, they found recurring themes in the interaction between 
business models and policies. First, business models adapt to policies, and they can 
either emerge in a given policy framework or change accordingly. Policies can 
‘mould’ business models. Moreover, policies and business models can co-evolve and 
affect each other over time. Regulatory support is important for business models, such 
as subsidies or other support mechanisms for niches. They furthermore emphasise 
the importance of public-private partnerships for the diffusion of large-scale 
environmental technologies and innovation. Lastly, business models with new 
technologies can exploit gaps in legislation, forcing policy makers to adapt the 
legislation.  
Wasserbaur et al. (2022) point out that economic instruments can act as a boost for 
the initial stages of business model transformations; however, they should not be 
necessary for the long-term circular business model to survive.  On the other hand, 
long-term stable regulatory frameworks can create the needed environment to 
establish the business model. They highlight the importance of policy sequencing, 
referring to the idea that the adoption of some policies enables or supports the 
==PAGE:27==
27 
 
 
subsequent adoption of other policies (Linsenmeier et al., 2022). Depending on the 
stage of the business model, different policies can be contributing to its needs.  
It should be noted that legislation is not always suitable for regulating emerging trends 
that are yet unseen, which can result in businesses seeking loopholes in the 
regulations. Effective control mechanisms can ensure the effectiveness of applied 
policies. However, they emphasise that SMEs can find it harder to comply with 
environmental legislation than larger organisations, partially because they are 
embedded in large value chains (Wasserbaur et al., 2022).  
During the last twenty years, the resource policy direction of the European Union has 
turned towards more sustainable use of natural resources, increasing resource 
efficiency, recycling, and prevention of waste while aiming at sustainable levels of 
economic growth (Domenech & Bahn-Walkowiak, 2019). 
Domenech & Bahn-Walkowiak (2019) moreover find that policies, targets, and key 
instruments at EU-level directed towards the CE are part of a complex landscape of 
policies that serve a variety of purposes. These might vary from supporting the free 
internal market to competitiveness, growth, and wellbeing. They notice that below 
these overall policy objectives are a variety of power structures and lobbyers who 
influence the policy-making process. Priorities and instruments are negotiated with 
several different actors that possess different sets of interests and practices, 
emphasising that the complexity is further shaped by the division of abilities between 
the European level and the member state level. 
Milios (2018) argues that the complexity of CE policies requires a new approach to 
policy development. It should prescribe a holistic policy view at the systems level. 
Therefore, CE policies are required to identify and intervene in all relevant life-cycle 
stages of products.  
By outlining different policies relevant to the CE in the EU, he finds a high 
concentration of EU legislation towards the end of the life cycle with the primary aim 
to limit resource loss and increase the circulation of materials mainly through 
recycling. Moreover, there is a gap in policies that can push resource efficiency in the 
distribution and use stage. There are no policies that target the consumption and use 
==PAGE:28==
28 
 
 
of products and services, neither in companies, the individual consumer, or public 
actors (Milios, 2018). Domenech & Bahn-Walkowiak (2019) also argue that most 
policy efforts in the EU have concentrated on increasing efficiency and enhancing 
secondary market materials.  
Calisto Friant et al. (2021) show a contrast between words and actions in the EU in 
their analysis, indicating that the discourse is rather holistic, while actual policies focus 
on end-of-life solutions and do not address the many socio-ecological implications of 
a circularity transition. Laws and legislation related to CE have aimed to enhance safe 
waste management through sorting waste, landfill diversion, charges and taxes and 
extended producer responsibility (EPR) (Berg et al., 2018). 
More holistic long-term thinking is needed to ensure that EU policies are not too 
focused on the end of life. There is a great need for policy instruments focusing on 
upstream phases such as product design and service development. In general, 
carefully prepared, consistent, coherent, and credible policy mixes are needed (Berg 
et al., 2018). No single policy can promote the complementary benefits across sectors, 
industries, and policy domains needed for CE transition (Hartley et al., 2020).  
There is a need to be aware of both synergies and trade-offs across different 
dimensions of resource efficiency when developing policies. Domenech & Bahn-
Walkowiak (2019) describes this with the example that a synergy of increasing waste 
recycling could reduce the consumption of primary raw materials and the associated 
greenhouse gas emissions, both in Europe and potentially in exporting countries. 
However, it is not evident whether increasing levels of recycling have contributed to 
substituting the demand for primary resources anywhere in the world. Milios (2018) 
states that while policies can have individual desired outcomes across different stages 
of a product’s life cycle, they might have synergistic effects if combined with other 
policies at another life cycle stage. E.g., it can be a benefit to combine policies 
targeting the end of life, such as extended producer responsibility, with eco-design 
requirements, to ultimately increase circularity.  
According to Ekvall et al. (2016), a policy mix goes beyond combining loosely 
connected policy instruments. A policy mix combines long-term qualitative 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
 
objectives and shorter quantitative targets, which are fulfilled by linking them to an 
instrument set applied in a sequential process. Creating a coherent policy mix requires 
a future-oriented road mapping, aligning synergetic effects while minimising 
unintended negative side-effects.  
3.1.3. Consequences of innovation and policy in the circular economy 
Different CE strategies can lead to different outcomes, why there is a need to assess 
the impact of implemented strategies (Castro et al., 2022). Authors working in the CE 
literature are concerned with the unintended consequences that the different 
strategies towards the transition can have. Despite some agreement on the benefits 
connected with the CE strategies, there are still issues and open questions regarding 
the implementation (Schröder et al., 2019).  
Laurenti et al. (2016) underline that decoupling efforts such as CE strategies fall into 
a much larger complex socio-technical system, consisting of interactions and 
feedback loops from social, technical, and economic aspects over time. They argue 
that the interventions are often rebounded by unanticipated responses. Schröder et 
al. (2019) further express that another unanswered question is how it will affect the 
social dimension, inequality, power relations, the role and rights of consumers, users 
and citizens and the distribution of resources and exploitation of labour. In addition, 
how to deal with trade-offs and avoid lock-ins is unclear. As also emphasised by 
Domenech & Bahn-Walkowiak (2019), there can occur synergies as well as trade-
offs, which are often complex and difficult to foresee. 
Thus, it is essential to make an effort to understand the consequences of CE activities, 
both intended and unintended. The phenomenon of unintended consequences of 
sustainability improvements has been discussed from both policy and innovation 
perspectives. 
The idea of unintended consequences was first introduced by (Merton, 1936) despite 
he calls it unanticipated consequences. The words unintended and unanticipated are 
later used interchangeably in the literature, even though they are not entirely 
synonyms (Sveiby et al., 2009). Merton defines consequences of purposive action as both 
being exclusively the outcome of the action and those that are the consequences of 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
 
the interplay between the action and the objective situation (Merton, 1936b). He 
identified five factors that limit the anticipation of consequences, the first being lack 
of knowledge, such as not knowing what might follow an action. The second factor is 
an error in assumptions, including assuming that things will lead to the same desirable 
outcomes as previously. The third source of unintended consequences concerns the 
desire for immediate benefits to an extent where the short-term is prioritised above 
long-term considerations. The fourth factor is when unintended consequences are not 
even considered due to the perceived necessity of the actions due to a certain value 
set. The fifth and last factor is the so-called self-defeating prophecy, which refers to 
when a prediction is announced, it can change the course of actions in itself (Merton, 
1936; Sveiby et al., 2009) 
Rogers (2003) argues that, when introducing an innovation, change agents often 
assume that the adoption will produce primarily benefits, while little concern is given 
to the potential negative consequences of the innovation. A change agent is defined 
as someone who influences clients' innovation decisions in a direction deemed 
desirable(Rogers, 2003), hence, they should be aware of their responsibility for the 
measures they propose. Ideally, it should be possible to predict the consequences of 
an innovation, both intended and unintended, before introducing it in a context. This 
is rarely the practice and often difficult to do, partly because they occur over an 
extended period. Moreover, consequences are not easily measured, and people 
exposed to them might not be fully aware of the influence. It can also be challenging 
to assess whether an occurrence of an effect is due to one innovation or another 
factor.  
Rogers distinguishes between three dimensions of consequences; desirable versus 
undesirable, direct versus indirect, and anticipated versus unanticipated. Desirable 
consequences are described as the functional effects of innovation for an individual 
or a social system, while undesirable consequences are dysfunctional. In most 
instances, innovation will cause desirable- and undesirable consequences, and it is 
impossible to separate them.  
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
 
Interrelationships in a system mean that a change in one part often results in indirect 
consequences through a chain reaction from the direct consequence. Direct 
consequences are the changes to an individual or a social system that occur as an 
immediate response to the adoption of the innovation. Indirect consequences can be 
described as the consequences of consequences and are the changes that occur due 
to the direct consequences. This notion relates to Merton’s definition of a 
consequence of a purposive action leading to an actual outcome and consequences 
of interplay with the situation.  
The last dimension that Rogers discuss is anticipated and unanticipated, which is the 
dimension that corresponds closest to the term (un)intended. Anticipated 
consequences are changes due to an innovation that are recognised and intended by 
the members of a social system, while unanticipated consequences are changes due 
to an innovation that are neither intended nor recognised. He concludes that the 
undesirable, indirect, and unanticipated consequences of innovation usually go 
together, as do the desirable, direct, and anticipated consequences (Rogers, 2003). 
3.1.4. Rebound effects 
A term used for a certain type of unintended consequence is rebound effect. The 
concept was early described by the economist William Stanley Jevons during the 
industrial revolution, who claimed that ‘‘technological efficiency gains (…) actually 
increased the overall consumption of coal, iron, and other resources, rather than saving 
them’’, which led to the formulation of Jevons Paradox. More than a century later, 
energy economists called this phenomenon “rebound effects”, which characterises 
the adverse side effects of efficiency policies and strategies that ended up taking back 
the environmental gains they had permitted (Gossart, 2015). The RE can be defined 
as “a behaviour or other systemic response to a measure taken to reduce environmental 
impacts that offsets the effect of the measure” (Hertwich, 2005). 
It can also be expressed as the gap between the engineering assessments of a 
potential energy saving (PES) and actual energy savings (AES) (Guerra & Sancho, 
2010; Thomas & Azevedo, 2013):

32

REs are categorised in slightly inconsistent ways but are often divided into direct, 
indirect/secondary, and economy-wide effects (Sorrell & Dimitropoulos, 2008). 
Sometimes a fourth category, transformational REs, is added (Greening et al., 2000). 
In the case of direct effects, the higher efficiency leads to lower cost, which induces 
price reductions that trigger an increase in the demand for the same, cheaper goods. 
Indirect REs occur when a resource is used more efficiently, leading to its price going 
down, which results in the consumption of other products or services. The third 
category concerns economy-wide REs, which can be an aggregation of direct and 
indirect REs that cause structural changes in production patterns and consumption 
habits (Gossart, 2015; Metic & Pigosso, 2022). The last type of RE is the 
transformational effect that occurs when energy efficiency increases, change 
consumers’ preference and societal institutions or results in other large-scale effects 
(Greening et al., 2000). 
3.1.5. The Circular Economy Action Plan 
The new Circular Economy Action Plan (CEAP) for a cleaner and more competitive 
Europe was adopted by the European Commission in March 2020, following the first 
action plan introduced in 2015.  
The CEAP is a comprehensive policy proposal, entailing several measures and 
instruments. It affects a deeply complex system which is composed of many sectors, 
stakeholders, and industries. Moreover, it addresses the whole life cycle of products. 
This means that it targets how products are designed, promotes CE processes, 
encourages sustainable consumption, and aims to ensure that waste is prevented and 
that the resources used are kept in the EU economy for as long as possible (European 
Commission, 2020). The focus is thus shifted from waste management to maintaining 
resources in the loop and preventing waste in the first place.  
The new action plan announces interrelated measures - legislative and non-
legislative. It consists of measures with six overall objectives (European Commission, 
2020): 
==PAGE:33==
33 
 
 
• Make sustainable products the norm in the EU 
• Empower consumers and public buyers 
• Focus on the sectors that use the most resources and where the potential 
for circularity is high such as electronics and ICT, batteries and vehicles, 
packaging, plastics, textiles, construction and buildings, food, water, and 
nutrients  
• Ensure less waste 
• Make circularity work for people, regions, and cities  
• Lead global efforts on CE 
The measures cover 35 actions related to the objectives. Due to the broad focus on 
the whole life cycle as well as the multiple, interrelated measures contained in the 
plan, one can argue that the new CEAP can be termed as a policy mix rather than 
several independent instruments.   
Approximately two years after the action plan was adopted, the package Making 
sustainable products the norm as presented on 30 March 2022. The package extends 
the existing Eco-design Directive from 2009 (The European Parliament and The 
Council of the European Union, 2009) in two ways. From only covering energy-
related products in the current directive, it will cover the broadest possible range of 
products in the future, such as expanding to textiles and construction products. 
Secondly, the requirements with which the products are to comply with are 
broadened. This means not only energy efficiency, but circularity is to be included to 
make products more durable, reliable, reusable, upgradable, reparable, easier to 
maintain, refurbish and recycle, and energy- and resource-efficient. The proposal will 
set a framework and a process for Eco-design for Sustainable Product Regulation 
(ESPR) through which the Commission will set out requirements for each product or 
group of products on a running basis.  
As CE strategies are highly systemic and affect different stages of the life cycle, it is 
important to bear in mind the unintended consequences, so that the overall aim of 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
 
transitioning to more sustainable production- and consumption patterns are reached, 
and the problems are not worsened.  
3.2. 
Methods and tools for investigating (un)intended consequences 
In the following section, the discovered variety of existing system tools that are used 
to map the (un)intended consequences of CE policies are presented. The methods 
and tools are listed in Table 3 with an indication of the orientation towards the 
qualitative/ design-based or quantitative methodology (Little et al., 2016). A short 
description and their references are mentioned as well.   
A typical quantitative model is the General equilibrium model that uses data to estimate 
how an economy might react to changes in policy and technology. It consists of 
equations with variables that require economic data to calculate a scale. It has mainly 
been used regarding energy efficiency and to estimate the scale on a macro level, i.e., 
of an economy-wide RE.  
Another quantitative method is life cycle assessment, which is a modelling approach 
for assessing the potential impacts of a product or system over its full life cycle. It 
allows for the identification of the biggest contributions to the environmental impact 
as well as a comparison between alternative scenarios (ISO 14040, 2006).  
Niero et al. (2021) suggest combining environmental assessment models like LCA 
with practice theory or actor-network theory to supplement the assessment with 
knowledge about practices and the interests of actors, making the assessment method 
a combination of qualitative and quantitative aspects. 
The causal loop diagrams and system dynamics also combine qualitative and 
quantitative approaches. It is a system modelling technique that explores the 
variables and interrelationships of a system. The diagram often serves as the first step 
in the direction of evaluating a problem, whereas simulating the system through a 
system dynamics model can be used to expose consequences and develop strategies 
(Laurenti et al., 2016).

Several researchers propose frameworks to identify REs, such as Font Vivanco et al., 
(2016) and Metic & Pigosso (2022). These frameworks are categorisations of REs that 
can help researchers and decision-makers in identifying specific effects. Metic & 
Pigosso propose a conceptual framework regarding the CE and investigate the 
evolution of the RE across disciplines (energy economics, sustainable & industrial 
ecology, and circular economy). They distinguish between the direct, indirect and 
economy-wide RE types, just as they consider the three levels: micro, meso and 
macro. Lastly, the framework also indicates the involved stakeholder, the producer, 
or the consumer. The type of frameworks identified seems very interesting and 
helpful, as they can be used as a background catalogue and an overview of what types 
of REs can occur in different situations.  
The table consists of tools and methods that are established in the field of assessing 
the environmental impacts of measures or products on systems. Common for the 
different approaches presented is that they are mainly quantitatively oriented, 
meaning that they either build on mathematical models, data, or indicators, that help 
to assess the unintended consequences in a quantifiable way. This implies that a scale 
or magnitude of the effects will be determined when using the model. While many of 
these methods are good at quantifying impact, they have a quite narrow focus. E.g., 
LCA focuses mainly on the product and only to a limited degree on the surrounding 
system (Niero et al., 2021). The data that the models are fed with will only give results 
concerning that exact effect studied within the chosen system boundaries. The 
literature review shows that only a few design-based or qualitative-oriented 
approaches are mentioned. As one of the overall goals of the thesis is to develop a 
support that can help designers and decision-makers understand the unintended 
consequences of their actions, it is relevant to go beyond the quantitative methods 
and think in more qualitative and design-oriented approaches. There is a research 
gap regarding these types of methods. 
A benefit of having a more qualitative approach is that such a method can include 
factors that are not quantifiable. Many quantifiable approaches are more rigidly 
configurated and do not consider socio-technical aspects of a system. This could 
==PAGE:38==
38 
 
 
include new emergent behaviour of actors, which can result in unintended 
consequences, or different worldviews throughout the system. The socio-technical 
aspects that take place across different life cycle stages are crucial to consider in the 
transition to a CE. E.g., an assessment methodology such as LCA usually does not 
consider that the object under investigation is embedded in a large, complex 
sociotechnical system with dynamic interactions between actors. (Laurenti et al., 
2014).  
The ability to address unintended consequences is thus limited to the specific system 
boundaries or dataset under investigation. The argument posed is that to fully be able 
to benefit from the quantitative tools, a design-oriented tool that can qualify the 
understanding of unintended consequences should go before. The breath of such a 
design-oriented tool can enable sustainability thinking to be a part of the determining 
early stages of the design of policies. This is needed, as the complexity of CE 
transitions requires, as already described in 23 3.1, to think more holistically about 
the policies, as they intervene in all life cycle stages and involves all the actors within 
society. 
Thus, it can be advantageous to adopt a design-oriented, qualitative mapping 
perspective concerning CE transitions, as it encourages those involved to look at a 
situation as a whole and consider multiple factors. Moreover, it can utilise 
relationships between different parts of the problem instead of addressing one aspect 
at a time. Such a qualitative analysis could act as a prior assessment which can inform 
later, more in-depth quantitative models. The design-based method could point to 
areas of investigation for the use of LCA or system dynamics. I.e., the initial, visual 
system mapping can ensure the focus of the quantitative analysis is put on the critical 
aspects of the system, not overlooking any important facets. 
The target audience of an initial, broader qualitative analysis is designers or 
policymakers seeking to map out the consequences of a design or policy measure 
and thus identify the best possible direction based on this. A socio-technical mapping 
speaks their language and can expose the potential intended and unintended 
consequences of a decision, design, or policy measure.

3.3. 
Requirements for a tool 
Based on the literature reviews and considerations discussed above, the third 
outcome of the DS, i.e., the requirements for the design-based tool are identified and 
described below. 
1. 
Draw a picture of the different outcomes of a policy 
The literature review on the consequences of innovation and policies (section 3.1.3) 
and the extensive evidence of RE (section 3.1.4) indicate that policies can bring with 
them unintended consequences and REs. These can be desirable, undesirable, 
anticipated, and unanticipated consequences and are often underestimated or 
overlooked. Thus, the tool must show a broad picture of potential outcomes in a way 
that is manageable and understandable for the user.  
2. Indicate the potential intended and unintended consequences of policy 
measures (including RE) throughout the lifecycle of electronics   
The introduction of the CE concept in section 3.1.1 presented the life of cycle stages 
and how they are interconnected. The material- and energy loops are connected and 
changes in one part of the system can result in changes in other parts, and the material 
loops are rarely as smooth and unproblematic as they appear on engineering 
schemes. The literature review on policies in CE transitions (section 3.1.2) indicates 
that when working with policy interventions in CE, a life cycle perspective is needed 
to accommodate all the potential dynamics. CE policies intervene in all life-cycle 
stages of products and can create synergies and trade-offs in other stages than the 
one where it directly intervenes. Thus, it is this essential to establish a systemic 
perspective that takes the whole life cycle into account when assessing the 
consequences of CE strategies. 
3. Consider the different worldviews of the stakeholders involved in policy 
implementation  
The literature review in section 3.1.2 furthermore exemplified that the transition to a 
CE is complex and involves a broad range of stakeholders, who will have differing 
interests and practices, which can result in different acceptance of policies. The 
==PAGE:40==
40 
 
 
instruments and priorities are often a product of a negotiation process between 
several actors in the EU at different levels. To create a coherent policy and increase 
the synergies while minimising the unintended side effects, the different actors and 
their worldviews should be represented. Policy makers are responsible for designing 
policies and implementing them, however, the stakeholders that will translate these 
policies into action, such as actual product design, services, and business models, are 
the business decision-makers and designers. It is therefore important to have a 
thorough understanding of all stakeholders.  
4. Work as an engaging tool for dialogue about potential outcomes  
The many potential outcomes of a policy can be complex for one stakeholder to 
predict. Thus, making the support an engaging tool that can be used in a dialogue with 
different stakeholders will improve the tool’s outcomes. Participation has been 
encouraged to evaluate policies, through the involvement of stakeholders in a 
dialogue where the different types of knowledge are taken into consideration. Making 
a tool engaging would require that it is recognisable, easy to decipher and build upon 
for people with various backgrounds. 
3.4. 
Sub-conclusion of Descriptive Study I 
In this chapter, the characteristics of policy-induced CE transitions that might lead to 
unintended consequences were investigated. We often see simplified representations 
of the CE as engineering schemes, where material flows go directly from primary 
loops to secondary loops in a smooth movement. The transition to a CE is more 
complex than this. It requires holistic long-term policy making with instruments 
focusing on product design, use patterns, and end-of-life. Moreover, synergies and 
trade-offs across different dimensions should be considered. It is found that 
combining policy instruments can have synergistic effects, which can ultimately 
increase the circularity.  
The CEAP can be seen as such a policy mix, as it announces interrelated measures 
that address the whole life cycle of products. However, unintended consequences 
and REs can also occur. Interrelationships in the system mean that intervention in 
==PAGE:41==
41 
 
 
one part can result in indirect consequences through a chain reaction elsewhere, 
according to the theories about unintended consequences posed by Merton and 
Rogers (Merton, 1936; Rogers, 2003). The complexity of sectors, material loops, and 
production chains in the CE make it even more challenging.   
Furthermore, this chapter provided an overview of system tools that can help map 
the (un)intended consequences of CE policies. Despite the need for a holistic 
approach, the methods and tools used to investigate unintended consequences are 
found to be primarily quantitative, e.g., by applying a mathematical model or 
simulating the system behaviour. A benefit of having a more design-oriented, 
qualitative tool with CE transition is that it can include unquantifiable elements such 
as different stakeholders’ views to get a wider picture of the possible unintended 
consequences.  
Finally, requirements for a design-based tool were stated. Based on the insights 
gained in the literature reviews, the tool should 1) Draw a picture of the different 
outcomes of a policy, 2) Indicate the potential intended and unintended consequences of 
policy measures (including RE) throughout the lifecycle of electronics, 3) Consider the 
different worldviews of the stakeholders involved in policy implementation, and 4) Work as 
an engaging tool for dialogue about potential outcomes.

In this chapter, a Rich Picture tool for investigating unintended consequences is 
presented. The tool is created based on the knowledge obtained in the DS-I and the 
requirements that arose from it. The step-by-step approach consisting of eight steps 
is explained. Subsequently, results from the application of the tool on the three

on the analyses.

4.1. 
 Presentation of a Rich Picture Tool for investigating unintended 
consequences 
The proposed tool builds on the concept of A Rich Picture system first introduced by 
Checkland in 1981. A Rich picture is a system visualisation method used to depict 
complicated situations (The Open University, 2000). It helps gain an overview of the 
bigger picture and the maze of structures and processes operative in a context in a 
unified format (Bell & Morse, 2013).  
A Rich Picture modelling technique responds well to the requirements because it 
deals with human and social complexity. This means that it does not view systems as 
a strict configuration of entities but as something that can consist of different 
viewpoints from different people involved.  
It aims to capture the situation’s main entities, structures, viewpoints, and processes. 
Moreover, it can help depict the current recognised and potential emerging issues 
(Checkland et al., 2020). Thus, the notion is that a Rich Picture can function as a 
system tool to identify intended consequences based on the current processes and 
structures and the unintended consequences that might occur from policy 
intervention.  
A Rich Picture can express crucial relations between elements in the situation and 
work as an object of discussion on how the system is perceived and how to improve 
the situation. When accepting that different worldviews about the situation exist, the 
Rich Picture can be used in a learning process to identify desirable and feasible 
“actions to improve” (Checkland & Poulter, 2020). The Rich Picture must encapsulate 
objective matters and subjective perceptions of situations and interests (The Open 
University, 2000). 
The tool developed includes the following essential elements of a Rich Picture (The 
Open University, 2000): 
• Elements of structure. The parts of the situation that change relatively slowly 
over time and are quite stable, such as the people, the setups, and the 
==PAGE:44==
44 
 
 
hierarchies. In the proposed tool, the elements of structure are the stakeholder 
setup in the different life cycle stages. 
• Elements of process. The things in a state of change, such as the activities 
going on. These are the life cycle process and the CE activities. 
• Factual as well as subjective information. In the suggested tool, factual and 
subjective information comes from the CEAP, the legislative and non-
legislative documents, as well as interview statements and information from 
newspapers. Lastly, a catalogue of intended and unintended consequences 
from literature also adds to the information that is used in the tool. 
The proposed modelling tool seeks to combine the policy measure with a life cycle 
view – where the different stages of the product are depicted – with a consequence 
tree, and a stakeholder analysis.

how to address them. This can potentially be done through an in-depth analysis. 
4.1.2. Catalogue of unintended consequences of ICT, electronics and CE   
As described in step 2, the catalogue of unintended consequences is used to provide 
a research-based background for the analysis. It is inspired by some of the 
frameworks for unintended consequences that were discovered in section 3.2, such 
as the ones by Font Vivanco et al. (2016) and Metic & Pigosso (2022). The catalogue 
created in this research includes unintended consequences that appear in the ICT 
and electrical and electronic equipment literature (marked with EEE). In addition, it 
contains unintended consequences and REs of CE initiatives and strategies (marked

Table 4 has emerged based on the types of effects that are repeated in several articles. 
They are categorised into the following types: direct effects and consequences by design, 
direct rebound effects, indirect effects, and economy-wide effects. It includes a combination 
of rebound effects and other types of consequences. 
==PAGE:55==

Table 4 shows that the potential unintended consequences and REs are manifold both 
concerning electronics, ICT and CE. They touch upon economic effects, material 
efficiency, consumer preferences, practices, and social aspects such as citizens' health 
and rights. Below follow some examples from the table. 
One of the direct, unintended environmental consequences of design, described by 
(Laurenti et al., 2015) concerning ICT and electronics, is the obsolescence and 
redundancy by design. It occurs when consumers replace their products due to 
obsolescence. The word obsolete can be defined as “no longer in use or no longer 
useful”1. Most people agree that obsolescence concerns the technical and mechanical 
aspects, but the idea of psychological obsolescence is increasingly recognised. 
Aspects like style preferences, new product features and marketing campaigns can 
affect the desire for a replacement, despite the old product still being functional, which 
can have environmental consequences (Laurenti et al., 2015). 
Another example of an unintended consequence is the economic REs, which exist in 
two forms. It is when an increase in material- or energy efficiency results in prices 
going down, which then increases the consumption of resources or energy. The direct 
effect entails that the efficiency leads to an increase in consumption of the same 
product (i.e., more efficient cars lead to more driving), while the indirect results in 
another product or service being consumed (Börjesson Rivera et al., 2014; Gossart, 
2015; Sorrell & Dimitropoulos, 2008).  
Some of the REs experienced within CE innovations rely on the exact mechanisms 
as the types known from energy economics, such as the economic RE. The term CE 
rebound (CER) is used when the eco-efficiency of a system is partially or wholly offset 
by an increase in production or consumption instead of energy efficiency. It refers to 
when circular strategies or activities fail to reduce consumption, increase production 
levels, and reduce the expected decoupling benefits (Zink & Geyer, 2017).

Despite most of the effects being environmental, a few of the consequences also 
touch upon the social aspect. Concerning electronics and ICT, Laurenti et al. (2015) 
described the embodied impact by design, meaning that products have effects 
downstream of the life cycle from the point of design. An example is the smartphone, 
which, when ending its life in developing countries can go through informal recycling 
processes, leading to consequences for both the local environment and the people. 
Chen (2021) talks about the social rebound, which refers to damaging workers’ and 
citizens’ health and rights, creating social inequality. 
Figge & Thorpe (2019) introduced the term symbiotic RE, a type of RE specific to a 
CE. While the ‘conventional’ rebound literature is highly focused on demand as the 
driving force (i.e., resource efficiency leads to higher demand, which generates more 
consumption and thus resource use), the symbiotic RE is a result of changes in 
resource flows in production that affects other producers. The driving force is 
opportunity costs, which relates to the fact that choosing one circular strategy comes 
at an opportunity cost of not doing another. When a system is circular instead of 
linear, all resource flows are interconnected. The input for one producer could be a 
waste by-product for another. Changing a resource flow by e.g., reusing instead of 
recycling, could generate implications for using the resource at another point in the 
system. The important point is that resource efficiency can only be assessed as a part 
of the overall system and not as an isolated use of the resource (Figge & Thorpe, 
2019).

4.2.1. Policy overview: The Circular Economy Action Plan and electronics 
The analysis is based on the Circular Electronics Initiative, which refers to the part of 
the CEAP that concerns electronics and intends to mobilise existing and new 
instruments. The initiative is composed of the following five overall measures 
(European Commission, 2020), some of which will lay the ground for the analysis of 
consequences: 
• Eco-design requirements. Regulatory measures for electronics and ICT 
including mobile phones, tablets and laptops, under the Eco-design Directive 
so that devices are designed for energy efficiency and durability, reparability, 
upgradability, maintenance, reuse and recycling.  
• Right to repair. Focus on electronics and ICT as a priority sector for 
implementing the ‘right to repair’, including a right to update obsolete 
software. Measure of interest in cycle 2. 
• Common charger. Regulatory measures on chargers for mobile phones and 
similar devices, including the introduction of a common charger, improving 
the durability of charging cables, and incentives to decouple the purchase of 
chargers from the purchase of new devices. Measure of interest in cycle 1.  
• Take back scheme. Improving the collection and treatment of waste from 
EEE, including exploring options for an EU-wide take-back scheme to return 
or sell old mobile phones, tablets, and chargers. Measure of interest in cycle 3. 
• Hazardous substances. Review of EU rules on restrictions of hazardous 
substances in EEE and provide guidance to improve coherence with relevant 
legislation, including REACH and Ecodesign.

dropping. Nevertheless, the amount of money saved is not big enough to make a 
difference in consumption in the developed part of the world (Informant C, Appendix 
2).   
(2) Motivation for innovation. Some stakeholders, such as smartphone companies, 
are not as positive towards the measure and expect some adverse side effects. They 
argue that common chargers will narrow the design space and hinder innovation, 
removing the incentives to create new solutions (Dahlberg et al., 2019; Innocenti & 
Peitz, 2022a). This could in the last instance hinder innovators introduce more 
sustainable solutions, such as more material- or energy-efficient chargers or batteries.  
The consequences related to whether the freedom of design is narrowed is a question 
that divides the informants involved in the case study, with the industry on one side 
and more environmentally concerned stakeholders on the other side. Informant B, 
Appendix 2, stated that the directive might not just narrow the freedom of design, but 
also lead to killing the incentives to innovate.  
“One area where I find it may have a negative influence is the entire motivation for 
innovation and also differentiation. Companies would like to create a competitive 
advantage through innovation and differentiation. When you are harmonising it, I think a 
==PAGE:66==
66 
 
 
very important prerequisite is how you still allow innovation. Which to me is a big 
challenge at the moment.“ – Informant B, Appendix 2 
This opinion is backed by Informant A, who agrees that the whole design process 
would change radically. Normally, the innovation is driven by the companies, but 
when making political constraints for the design, new dynamics will occur. There 
might be a risk that the development will stagnate, because of the bureaucratic 
process it requires to make changes. However, informant E, Appendix 2, points out 
that this is already the case in many other areas.  
“With chemical substances, for example, there are long processes where you must 
document everything. So, it’s more in an ideal world that you just find something new and 
smart in an instant, and then it can hit the market right away. That’s rarely the case. “ – 
Informant E 
With this statement, she contradicts the argument and further adds that it might be 
an excuse for some companies to try to affect policy-making. As Informant C says, it 
is important to beware of who is the sender of the message. The reasons that 
stakeholders might be against a common charger initiative are because of their 
financial interests, more than they are concerned with consumer convenience and 
the environment. 
(2.1b) (2.1.1b) Lock-in on existing solutions. As pointed out by Informant D, there is 
a risk that it will hinder innovation and create a lock-in on solutions, that are not 
optimal, in favour of more efficient technologies.  
“European legislators should not get too fixated on what we are selling now. I fear that in 
three years when the common charger is implemented, we will probably be charging 
wirelessly. And it is the interplay between technology and politics when its most wonderful. 
Sometimes we arrive at something, but the time has passed since then” – Informant D, 
Appendix 2 
Some stakeholders even suggest that the development of wireless chargers is 
intensified, as an attempt to bypass regulation. As the area of wireless chargers is not 
(yet) regulated, manufacturers can develop the charger and its communication 
==PAGE:67==
67 
 
 
interfaces to only fit a specific brand, potentially creating the same problem as the 
Common charger seeks to solve with the wired solutions.  
(3) The overall effect of the measure. There is an agreement that the common charger 
will have some degree of positive effect. Informant C believes the Common Charger 
is a step in the right direction, but will not make radical changes to consumption or e-
waste. Other initiatives, such as the eco-design requirements for smartphones, would 
have a bigger effect. 
Informant A believes that some parts of the measure will overall have a positive effect 
on the environment, such as the unbundling and the information requirements. 
However, according to the study commissioned by Apple, the requirement for 
harmonising charging interface will not have a positive effect, more likely the opposite 
(Basalisco et al., 2022). Corresponding to the report, the harmonisation of interfaces 
will not improve consumer convenience and ultimately not reduce the e-waste 
stemming from chargers. This is because the number of chargers is not determined 
by the type of charger but by the number of devices and the locations where the 
consumer needs a charger (e.g. one in the living room, the car, etc.). 
According to Informant B, the overall effect of the common charger will to a wide 
extent be determined by the amount and the complexity of products.  
“The amount of equipment that requires charging, from a laptop to an iPad, to a notepad, 
to a phone, to a watch – that’s also undergoing a lot of changes and very fast. It’s going to 
be influenced by that as well, and of course by the convenience. I would say it’s very difficult 
to assume beyond a certain point.” – Informant B 
(4) Ripple effect. However, informant B also points to an overlooked aspect in the 
pre-analysis and emphasises an expected positive consequence of the directive: the 
ripple effect. The ripple effect can occur when large, global companies decide to make 
the charger the same across the continents, and not differentiate between the EU and 
the rest of the world. This will make the positive consequences even more 
widespread.

4.2.2.2. 
Recommendations for the Common Charger  
5. Find the right balance between standardisation and innovation 
The introduction of the common charger is a step towards standardisation, which is 
positive regarding ensuring consumer convenience and reducing the number of 
chargers needed, and thus the amount of e-waste produced. A focus on only 
developing one type of charger and interface can lead to fine-tuning and 
improvement of the exact solution, including more efficient versions. However, 
standardisation can tend to discourage innovation and suffocate new solutions even 
before they appear. It is essential to find a balance where the standardisation does 
not kill the innovation but instead serves as a foundation for innovation that reaches 
above and beyond the standard.  
However, to ensure that there is room for new solutions, it would be a 
recommendation to implement a standardisation organ that can select the right 
solution in the future. This organ should on an ongoing basis consider whether there 
is a need for changes to the standard, considering the landscape if new solutions 
emerge. To ensure the benefits of a common charger, it is essential that the chosen 
solution is the standard on the market for a certain time, to gain positive 
consequences. However, the development of more efficient solutions should still be 
incentivised, which is maybe not the case, if it requires going through a long, 
bureaucratic process of changing the policy. Instead, if a company develops a 
promising new way to charge phones, it should be possible to apply for the adoption 
of a new standard at the standardisation organ. When the common charger was 
discussed 12 years ago, it was the micro-USB that was on the table (European 
Commission, 2009). The technologically superior USB-C was not yet invented, so 
believing that it has reached its final level of innovation now might be naïve. 
Introducing a decision-making organ could avoid delays in updates of the current 
USB-C standard due to the long legislative process, as well as the introduction of 
entirely new versions if needed.  
 
 
==PAGE:69==
69 
 
 
6. Handle the upcoming wireless solutions with care  
The European Commission should be careful not to regulate something that will 
already be outdated once it is implemented. Apple and other companies are already 
working on wireless solutions, and it could be tempting for them to introduce products 
that only use these options (Innocenti & Peitz, 2022b). As the EU already stated, it is 
ready to implement delegated acts once the wireless technologies are more 
developed. On the one hand, the EU should ensure that other charging solutions that 
are not wired will make the regulation redundant and that the directive is not already 
outdated when it enters into force. On the other hand, the standardisation 
requirements for wireless solutions should not be introduced too early, to avoid the 
wireless technology is not developed to its full potential. The development of 
sufficient technology could need some time and require some room for 
experimentation. 
7. Investigate the indirect economic rebound effect  
There is no doubt that the measure will have a positive effect, leading to a benefit of 
annual savings of 180 ktCO2e, 1000 tonnes of e-waste, and 250 million euros for 
European consumers (European Commission, 2021b). However, the indirect RE 
associated with economic savings should be investigated further. If there even is a 
risk of a ‘re-spending effect’, the size of it should be assessed to determine to what 
extent the increased income will be redirected to new forms of consumption, and if 
this increased consumption offsets some of the saved GHG emissions.  
8. Consider the countereffect of emerging product trends  
The number of products per capita is increasing across the world, as various new 
devices in different forms with increased capabilities and features are introduced in 
the market. It is predicted that the average number of devices and connections per 
person will rise from 5.6 to 9.4 in Western Europe (Cisco, 2020).  It should thus be 
considered that the reasons consumers buy chargers might be that the number of 
devices that need charging is increasing. If the consumer desires to charge several 
devices at once, and possibly in different locations, the number of chargers will not 
be significantly reduced. This is not a consequence of the measure per se, but a 
==PAGE:70==
70 
 
 
consequence of trends in society. The impacts of these emerging consumption- and 
use practices based on the introduction of new electronics and ICT should be 
investigated through further modelling and accounted for in the calculations for 
economic savings and waste reduction of the common charger measure.  
8.1.1. Policy analysis Cycle 2 – The right to repair 
The right to repair refers to a combination of different intentions, such as empowering 
consumers, expanding the guarantee, making spare parts available, and products 
more repairable. However, there is not yet a specific directive or legislative proposal 
covering the right to repair. The European Commission has announced its plans to 
introduce the initiative in the last quarter of 2022, which will be a legislative change 
to the Sale of Goods directive, but so far this has not been published. Moreover, the 
proposals for requirements for eco-design also include actions on how to ensure that 
products are designed for repairability. 
The actions listed below are the actions that are understood as a part of the Right to 
Repair initiative, described in a policy briefing by the European Parliament Research 
Service (Sajn, 2022): 
- 
Ensure devices are more durable, easier to repair and include removable and 
replaceable parts 
- 
Oblige manufacturers to grant free access to repair and maintenance 
information and guarantee software updates for a minimum period 
- 
Extend guarantees 
- 
Offer better consumer information regarding the reparability of devices 
- 
Make repairs more attractive to consumers, like bonuses for repairing a faulty 
device or receiving a replacement device for the duration of the repair

(1.1.3) (1.1.3.1) The risk of the economic rebound. If consumers save money either 
due to their phones lasting longer, or saving money on repairs instead of buying a new 
one, the direct and indirect economic rebound found in Table 4 can occur because 
people will have a bigger financial leeway.  
Whether there is a risk of an economic rebound is something that raises a dilemma. 
Literature suggests that to minimise the RE, secondary production must not affect the 
overall prices. This means that the overall price of a smartphone should not be 
lowered by the right to repair. On the other hand, several stakeholders address the 
price as a key problem for people not repairing their devices, arguing that the price 
of repair should be lowered.

It is a matter of finding a balance between incentivising people to get their 
smartphones repaired by making it cheaper, but not creating an economic RE, where 
the overall prices are lowered, resulting in more consumption.  
(2) Choosing repair can compromise other circular strategies. Most people agree that 
repair and lifetime extension are preferred over recycling due to their higher rank in 
the waste hierarchy. However, it should be kept in mind that it can have derivative 
effects when focusing on one particular CE strategy. The symbiotic RE known from 
the catalogue of unintended consequences could potentially occur (Table 4). This is 
because choosing one circular strategy can come with the cost of not doing the other. 
It might compromise other circularity aspects otherwhere in the system. I.e., when 
choosing the strategy of repair, it could potentially have the unintended consequence 
of reducing recyclability properties or the durability of products. 
This trade-off was acknowledged by some of the informants. Different aspects were 
emphasised. Informant E highlighted that some industry members were concerned 
with this.

“A company can change their product so it consists of modules that can be taken apart, but

It is thus important to make a product-specific assessment in each product group so

repair sectors, while it might result in higher costs in other sectors, such as the 
traditional manufacturers. This can impact employment in the repair sector and 
create jobs.  
(3.1.2) Shifts in service and repair. Depending on how the Right to repair will be 
designed, a shift can happen in who is performing the repairs. On one hand, it is 
preferred to have high-quality spare parts and certified repairs to ensure quality. It is 
an essential action to make the repair sector, which now widely consists of 
volunteering and smaller repairers, more professionalised.

of the knowledge getting lost from the experience amongst the many repair initiatives 
that already exist. This knowledge includes which products are easy to repair, which 
are durable and not, etc. As emphasised by Informant C, this value should be 
sustained in the right to repair implementation.

8.1.1.2. Recommendations for the Right to Repair 
1. 
Investigate the rate of displacement, i.e., the imperfect substitution effect 
As suggested by researchers within the CER field, it is not realistic to expect that the 
number of reused phones will replace the production of new phones on a 1:1 basis. 
When calculating the expected avoided production from the right to repair measure, 
the displacement rate should be considered. 
2. Support the right incentives for companies 
There are different alternatives for how companies can respond to this measure. An 
undesirable option is that they intensify their marketing by emphasising new features, 
appealing to the consumers’ psychological perception of obsolescence, leading to the 
sale of new products to continue at a high rate. Another more desirable option is that 
they turn to circular business models which include product service systems, e.g., that 
a repair service is included in the purchase of a smartphone. It is important to support 
the right incentives. This can be done by ensuring that companies can earn revenue 
==PAGE:78==
78 
 
 
on these types of business models, e.g. by making a service agreement with the 
consumer, so they maintain the relationship with the company throughout the life 
cycle of the product. Calculations of different business models could be an idea to 
illustrate the opportunities for business model transition. Moreover, to support these 
kinds of business models, there need to be requirements on the component level for 
standards and how long they should be on the market. Consumer rights should also 
be strengthened, such as an expansion of the warranty. 
3. Ensure preservation and utilisation of knowledge and experience in 
repair networks 
It should be considered who should take care of the repairs when introducing the right 
to repair. There is a benefit of professionalising the existing initiatives such as repair-
cafés. However, there is a significant amount of knowledge in the repair networks 
about which products can be repaired easily, which are less durable, and what should 
the requirements for product repairability be, etc. It is worth considering that the right 
to repair should be implemented in a way where extensive networks with knowledge 
about products such as repairability and durability are preserved and utilised. From 
this perspective, it would be an advantage for manufacturers and repair organisations 
could create cross-industrial partnerships to enhance and preserve their expertise.  
4. Pay attention to price balance to avoid the economic rebound effect 
It should be considered how the prices can be lowered to make repairs more 
attractive because it is found to be a crucial barrier to reparation.  This could for 
example be done by providing a tax deduction on repairs. However, the lower the 
repair prices are, the bigger the risk of the economic RE. Thus, further investigation 
should be made on the right balance between price reduction and REs. 
5. Mitigate social rebound effects 
The problem of illegal waste export should be acknowledged and measures to 
prevent it should be taken to make sure that it does not create social REs in countries 
outside of the EU. This will also increase the possibility of reusing more products 
directly and recovering more materials at the end of life. 
==PAGE:79==
79 
 
 
6. Recognise trade-offs by design and the symbiotic rebound effect 
Policymakers should be aware of the trade-offs when implementing repair initiatives. 
Prioritising one CE strategy often leads to trade-off situations, meaning compromises 
and sacrifices are made in one area to obtain benefits in another (Berlin & Feldmann, 
2022; Byggeth & Hochschorner, 2006). It is usually impossible to optimise all the 
strategies at once (i.e. narrowing, slowing, and closing). It is thus essential to think 
through the entire life cycle and value chain and consider what effects it may have 
elsewhere, such as on the durability and recyclability of products.  
Repairability vs. durability. Despite reparation and durability both being about long-life 
products and lifetime extension (i.e. slowing), the product durability can potentially 
be compromised by designing for repairability. An example of this could be due to 
removing a protective layer of material (which protects from water), to be able to 
separate components (informant H). Another example could be to not mould 
components in one piece to favour separability, knowing that the durability of the 
product is deprioritised (informant E).  
Repairability vs. recyclability. Opening up for repair will also open up a market for 
components produced by many different types of manufacturers with varying 
standards. When products reach the end of life and should be recycled, the variety of 
materials inside the phone could complicate the process. Most components are made 
of metals or plastic, but they can vary in quality, grade, and additives. The variation 
can make it more challenging to treat the products right and raise the recycling rates.  
Symbiotic rebound effect. It is important to bear in mind that the symbiotic RE can 
occur when one strategy is chosen before another: When there is an increase in 
reparations, it might lead to an increase in re-use, however, this might have a negative 
on the recycling processes as the input is reduced.  This could be the need for primary 
raw materials in production. As found by Figge & Thorpe (2019), higher reuse and 
higher recycling have a positive impact on the circularity of resource use in isolation. 
The positive effect we expect from changes in the repair is however at least partially 
offset by the negative impact on recycling. Thus, it should be investigated to what 
==PAGE:80==
80 
 
 
extent the positive effects expected from the repairability, will be offset by the 
negative impacts of not recycling.  
8.1.2. Policy analysis cycle 3: Take back schemes 
The EU-wide take-back scheme is a measure proposed by the EU and is relevant to 
investigate as it especially targets the end-of-life of electronics. However, the measure 
is very limitedly described. At this point, no clear combination of instruments has been 
suggested, but several have been discussed. The measure under investigation in this 
analysis consists of the instruments recommended in the “Study on options for return 
schemes of mobile phones, tablets and other small electrical and electronic equipment in the 
EU” (Romagnoli et al., 2022). This study identifies and conceptualises policy measures 
for action at the EU level to increase the return and take-back of small used 
electronics to ensure maximum collection rates and re-use, repair, refurbishment, and 
recovery. The actions recommended as “quick wins” and “key enablers” for return in 
the study are the following:  
- 
Drop-off points & database (search tools for available collection points) 
- 
Financial incentives (return the device and get a financial reward) 
- 
Personalised EoL information (the user gets information at an optimal time 
about value, collection points, etc.) 
- 
Door-to-door and postal services 
- 
Data privacy certification scheme (guaranteeing proper deletion of data after 
return)

9. Investigate the mechanisms for the export of used phones 
An EU-wide take-back scheme must be constructed properly. The EU should make 
sure that the proper conditions are in place so that the right incentives are supported. 
This includes making sure that when products are returned, they are as far as possible 
directly reused in the best way possible. Up to 70% of functional, used products are

directly exported. There is a dilemma between the opportunities and threats that 
these secondhand products impose on the local communities. On the one hand, in 
terms of access to high-quality products and job creation, and on the other hand, the 
issues of improper waste management harm people and the environment.  
One way of dealing with this problem is to ensure that there is a proper market for 
reuse within the EU, supporting the right initiatives so that the products are kept in 
the EU-economy and do not end up in the countries ‘out-of-sight, out-of-mind.  
Another way of dealing with waste export is to increase collaboration with the 
receiving countries. These collaborations could ensure the refurbishment of 
smartphones in the receiving countries, which would improve access to quality 
products in these countries. However, an important part of such collaboration should 
be to set a plan for how to do proper waste management of the products.  
It is essential to ensure that the problem of waste management is not transferred to 
countries outside the EU, where the requirements for waste management and 
treatment of toxic materials are less stringent. Failing to include the dilemmas with 
electronics export will have the risk of social and environmental REs.  It should be 
investigated to what extent these REs will apply, and how to mitigate them in the best 
way possible. 
10. Counteract the tendency to hibernate 
It is essential to investigate the barriers holding people back from engaging with take-
back systems. Research shows that the reasons for hibernation are many. One is the 
lack of knowledge of what to do about the phone and the perception of the phone as 
not having a high value. Another barrier is the fear of data security issues because 
smartphones may contain sensitive information and personal data. As mentioned by 
informant F+G, theft from drop-off points can be an issue, which can further 
contribute to the reluctance towards return. An EU-wide take-back scheme should 
ensure that both data and resources are safe from theft. It is thus important to 
investigate who should have the responsibilities for collection, storage and resell, and 
which requirements should be for this.  
==PAGE:87==
87 
 
 
11. Investigate the economic rebound effect 
As also stated in the recommendations for the other two analyses (sections 4.2.2 and 
8.1.1), the economic RE should be investigated. The ‘reward’-aspect of the take-back 
schemes encourage is a delicate balance between encouraging consumers to return 
their phones and encouraging them to replace products too early and consume more. 
The magnitude of the effect should be considered and further modelled, to account 
for how much of the consumption will be avoided. Moreover, putting an effort into 
communicating the facts to the consumers, to avoid misconceptions, could 
potentially refrain some people from replacing their products early due to 
misinformation. A solution to mitigate the RE could be to encourage business models 
where services are included in the price. I.e., instead of the consumer receiving money 
when buying a phone (e.g. through rewards for returning another phone), the 
consumer could pay a fee (for the repair service throughout the whole lifetime of the 
product), and then the incentives for re-spending are decreased.   
The risk of an economic direct and indirect RE was proposed in all three measures. 
When discussing the economic RE, the claim is that the consumer will spend more 
money because they get a higher net income. However, it is well known that the 
global society stands in economically uncertain times with inflation meaning higher 
prices on goods and necessities. Building on the logic of the RE, these economic 
mechanisms could mitigate the RE, as the economic leeway would be eaten either by 
price increases in general or by consumers’ reluctance towards spending money. It is 
thus another reason to investigate further how the consumption patterns will be 
affected in the three measures now and the coming years.  
4.3. 
Sub-conclusion of Prescriptive Study 
In this chapter, the elements of a Rich Picture tool to help understand the (un)intended 
consequences were presented. The proposed tool seeks to assess policy measures 
by combining a life cycle view, with a consequence tree and a stakeholder analysis. 
The Rich Picture tool can be applied using a step-by-step approach consisting of 8 
steps from initially defining the measure and finally providing recommendations.  
==PAGE:88==
88 
 
 
Moreover, the chapter analyses the potential (un)intended consequences of the 
measures contained in the CEAP in the electronics sector. A catalogue of unintended 
consequences was developed to provide a research-based background for the 
analysis. It includes unintended consequences and REs that appear concerning the 
ICT and electronics and of CE initiatives and strategies. The three measures under 
analysis are the common charger, right to repair, and take-back schemes.  
The common charger is said to lead to savings and more convenience for the consumer 
while decreasing e-waste. Critics argue that the directive will hinder innovation, 
removing the incentives to create new solutions. The recommendation in this regard 
is to find the right balance between standardisation and innovation. Moreover, new 
technologies such as wireless charging can enter the market, which can make the 
directive redundant. It is suggested to make room for these innovations to be 
sufficiently developed before legislating on the area. A last recommendation for the 
common charger is to consider the countereffect stemming from the increasing 
number of devices per capita across Europe, which also means more chargers. This 
should be accounted for when calculating economic savings and waste reductions.   
The right to repair empowers consumers and makes products more repairable and 
durable, which could decrease the number of new phones consumed in the EU. 
However, it is not realistic to expect that the number of repaired phones will replace 
the production of new phones on a 1:1 basis, and the displacement rate should be 
investigated and included in predictions of benefits. Moreover, the right incentives 
should be supported for companies to avoid psychological obsolescence through 
marketing. Instead, product service systems should be encouraged, with repairs as 
the service. It is furthermore important that the extensive knowledge within the 
current repair networks is sustained through partnerships when implementing the 
measure. In addition, policymakers should be aware of the trade-offs, when 
implementing repair initiatives, such as repairability vs. recyclability.  
Take-back schemes provide a unique opportunity for companies to increase their 
knowledge about their products in terms of their durability and weaknesses, and it 
also opens new business models that foster reuse and recycling. However, many 
==PAGE:89==
89 
 
 
barriers exist for consumers to hold back their phones, such as data safety. It is 
important to carefully consider the construction and roles of such systems, to 
encourage consumers to avoid hibernation.  With that said, more than half of the 
current reused phones in the EU are sold to developing countries. This poses benefits 
for the receiving countries but also dilemmas such as fewer requirements for waste 
management, potentially resulting in social REs at the end of life. It is recommended 
to investigate these dynamics more in detail.

6.3. 
Discussion of the results 
From the initial literature review, some expectations were built towards what would 
be found in the analysis of the CEAP. The CEAP showed signs of a new way of doing 
policy making in the EU, approaching the life cycle more holistically, and utilising 
different kinds of policy instruments to complement each other, also called a policy 
mix. Policies would ideally be designed to reinforce the positive feedback loops until 
reaching the desired goal of the CE.  
Despite policies being designed to minimise the unintended side effects that can 
occur, individual policies as well as policy mixes need to be evaluated for potential 
unintended consequences and REs. A concern is the question of the most optimal 
way to combine policy instruments so that the consequences throughout the whole 
value chain and implementation process are acknowledged. 
However, the analysis of the CEAP showed that policymaking concerning the CE is 
challenging. A challenge can be related to the fact that a material is used in many 
different applications, and there is an interdependency of resource streams between 
actors, sectors, and national boundaries. It is nearly impossible to consider all the 
conditions and applications but neglecting to do so increases the risk of policies 
shifting the resource use to other applications or parts of the world. Moreover, 
efficiency improvements in resource use may lead to greater consumption of the 
same or other products, which can cause unintended consequences or REs.  
Research methods influence on results  
The results from the literature reviews have been determining the focus of the study, 
as they provided direction and gave information for the analysis. It can be said that 
the literature review provided the lenses through which the situation was observed. 
When reviewing the literature for existing tools and methods, the gap identified 
provided the direction for the study and shaped the tool development. When 
searching for already identified unintended consequences and REs for the catalogue, 
the researcher went through a prioritisation process, selecting the relevant studies to 
lay the ground for the work. The results could have ended up differently if the 
literature review had been conducted with other search words. Moreover, it cannot 
==PAGE:100==
100 
 
 
be excluded that the researcher possessed a degree of observer bias, where the 
prejudice or expectations affect what is found in the study (Bhandari, 2022). This has 
been avoided to the greatest extent possible, by not letting the results from the 
literature review limit the study but looking to capture the whole spectrum of 
consequences by including stakeholders.   
The tool evolved throughout the cycles to be more and more stakeholder centric. 
This means that the stakeholders have come to play a central role, and their varying, 
and sometimes contradicting, worldviews are all present in the same model. Thus, it 
is essential to be aware that the results that the model provides are also a reflection 
of the different stakeholders’ opinions and are coloured by their backgrounds and 
standing points.  Different results could have been found if other types of stakeholders 
had been involved, such as consumers, who were not directly included (except that 
most people are, besides their professional role, also consumers).  
Another point about the stakeholder interaction can be made on the common 
understanding of the research goals. The researcher experienced that now and then 
the participants did not understand the purpose of the study, especially concerning 
the unintended consequences. For example, one person replied by email:  
“When you write consequences, what do you mean? I read consequences as a negative word, 
but I may be misunderstanding you. Initiatives towards the CE should have positive effects 
on resource consumption, the environment, and the economy. We want to help you, but I 
just need to get to an understanding of what you really want to know.” 
The response implied that the person did not acknowledge the true essence of the 
study, which was further elaborated. The encounter made it clear that the term 
“unintended consequence” could bring out negative feelings in some people, leading 
the researcher to slightly alter the wording throughout the study when communicating 
with certain people. The concept of consequences was widened to also include the 
desirable and intended consequences, to make sure that the people did not feel 
threatened or like their professionalism was disputed. Changing the wording to 
include both positive and negative notions helped the understanding acceptance 
==PAGE:101==
101 
 
 
amongst some participants, especially the ones that were not familiar with the 
phenomena of unintended consequences and REs.  
Except for requiring further elaboration, it was also found that asking directly for the 
participants to suggest unintended consequences from a measure was not fruitful. 
Despite being a specialist in CE, it does not mean that people have the level of 
reflexivity to express themselves from the top of their heads.  
As mentioned by one of the informants, when asked directly: “It's just always hard to 
think of the unintended, because how can you uncover it in advance? If you could, then 
something would already have been done about it”.  
Asking for unintended consequences was not a straightforward task. This insight led 
to casting a broad net of questions to track down the specific knowledge of the 
participant and capture relevant data about their ideas of consequences. Moreover, 
using other wordings like asking what “challenges” they might experience or if they 
have knowledge of any “trade-offs” helped avoid blocking the participants' thoughts. 
This led to a great amount of data which was not always relevant for the analysis, but 
sometimes essential points emerged from the dialogues.  
The stakeholders’ different disciplines and backgrounds led to each interview taking 
different shapes. Every interview was sought to be directed to the specific stakeholder 
and their expected knowledge areas; however, this was difficult to predict in each 
case. The questions had to be adjusted slightly during the interview to fit the 
knowledge and worldview of the participant. This slightly unstructured approach had 
the benefit of flexibility, enabling new topics and ideas to flow. However, it also led to 
the analysis stage being more challenging. It was not always possible to compare 
responses as the participants did not always get the same questions. The process of 
analysing and extracting information thus became lengthier. Moreover, one could 
argue that the analysis became more subjective and less reproducible and replicable, 
as the results depended on the judgement and interpretation of the researcher.  
It can moreover be discussed when in the process it is wisest to involve stakeholders 
and when the most useful knowledge is generated. Throughout the cycles, different 
points in time were tested, as not all interviews could be placed exactly 
==PAGE:102==
102 
 
 
simultaneously in the analysis process. Some interviews were planned earlier, while 
others were held towards the end of the analysis.  
The informants that were included early in the analysis contributed by giving 
direction. The researcher was less biased and did not have a great amount of 
background knowledge about the measure, such as different perspectives on 
consequences. Hence, the informant’s statements became more steering and played 
a greater role in the final analysis, whether this was conscious or not. 
In the cases where extensive analytical work had preceded the involvement of 
informants, the informant's statements were challenged more, as the researcher had 
a larger knowledge base from the start. It was easier to ask critical follow-up questions 
and discuss the pros and cons. However, one can also argue that the researcher was 
less open and more biased, which may have limited the topics of conversation, and 
ultimately excluded some unintended consequences of becoming part of the model. 
With the above taken into consideration, it was assessed that the best time to involve 
stakeholders is when the researcher is well acquainted with the measure, knows the 
arguments for and against it, and has an idea of  the unintended consequences, from 
which the conversation can start.  
6.4. 
Discussion of the tool 
The Rich Picture tool is created from the insight that there were no consequence 
mappings that could serve as an initial tool to understand a broad spectrum of 
unintended consequences. To the researcher’s knowledge, no tool that takes an 
explorative approach to the consequences of policies exists. The tool fills a gap for a 
holistic tool, that provides a step-by-step guide to make explicit both the intended and 
unintended consequences throughout the life cycle and how they affect stakeholders. 
However, the Rich Picture tool applied in this study has some deviations from the 
prescribed method by (Checkland & Poulter, 2020). As explained, the Rich Picture is 
a very loosely configurated system modelling tool which does not set up many rules 
for the modelling. Thus, it is close to other explorative tools such as mind map 
==PAGE:103==
103 
 
 
diagramming. As seen in other studies, the Rich Picture method is used as a way of 
cocreating a common diagram from scratch on blank paper (Bell & Morse, 2013).  
The application in this study introduces a new way of using the method. Here, the 
researcher has created the tool based on the principles suggested by the Rich Picture 
method, but through iterations, more stringent structures have been created. These 
count the life cycle view, the stakeholder view, and the consequence tree. These 
structures and processes are expected to be followed when applying the step-by-step 
tool. The reason for creating the structures is because there is a need to follow certain 
procedures to use the tool in different contexts, such as by different stakeholders or 
on different measures to ensure consistency of the tool. 
Despite it having its deviations, the tool still seeks to include openness and falls well 
under the Rich Picture method to explore complex problems and facilitate change 
among people who hold different views and agendas. The tool allows for differences 
of interpretation to be identified, and most importantly, it is a source of inspiration as 
to what aspects of the Rich Picture should be analysed further to seek out 
opportunities for change. Moreover, it serves as an effective tool to invite participants 
into the process and enable them to think in different roles, viewpoints and life cycle 
stages, and the dynamics between the different elements. Another benefit of the 
developed tool is that it enables the entire system to be seen at once, with individual 
views in relation to one another. 
(Un)intended, (in)direct and (un)desirable consequences 
An important cornerstone of the tool is the phenomenon of unintended consequences 
as known from the work of Rogers (2003) and Merton (1936). The tool partially 
accounts for the three dimensions suggested by Rogers, being the (un)anticipated, 
(un)desirable, and (in)direct. The (un)anticipated, or (un)intended, are reflected in the 
tool by using different colours, whereas the direct and indirect consequences are 
depicted by making them interdependent in the consequence tree, showing the 
derivative effects. Thus, the notion of these two aspects of Roger’s perception fits well 
with the one adopted in the model. However, the factor of desirability is not reflected 
in the tool. Despite that unintended consequences are often undesirable; this is not 
==PAGE:104==
104 
 
 
always the case. Consequences can certainly be unintended yet desirable. Moreover, 
the perception of whether a consequence leads to desirable or undesirable 
consequences can also vary across stakeholders in the system. These nuances are 
not depicted in the tool, where the undesirable and unintended are almost treated 
likewise, with orange indicating negative (unintended, undesirable) and green being 
positive (intended, desirable).  
Time dimension  
As further emphasised by Rogers (2003), the study of consequences is complicated 
by the time dimension. They occur over an extended period of time, making the usual 
investigation methods difficult to use. People cannot when asked, foresee all 
consequences. In this respect, explorative methods known from anthropology can 
come handy (e.g., observations over time), as they can expose hidden behaviours. As 
Rogers points out, to study unintended consequences, the researcher should emerge 
in the field for a longer period.  
“An innovation’s consequences cannot be understood simply by adding an additional 
question or two to a survey instrument […]. Instead, a long-range research approach must 
be taken in which consequences are analysed as they unfold over a period of time, which 
may be several years” (Rogers, 2003).  
This aspect is relevant to consider when using the tool. The results obtained from 
using the tool will give a snapshot of the point in time, based on the opinions proposed 
by the public and the people involved. It will only give a potential prediction of the 
consequences. To validate the results and their correctness, the researcher should 
spend more time in the field or dive deeper into the consequences that are uncertain 
and lacks evidence. 
The time aspect is relevant to include when considering the unpredictability of an 
innovation’s consequences. The longer the time horizon, the more the uncertainty 
grows. The model does partially include the time dimension, suggesting that the 
further to the right (in the lifecycle and the consequence tree, see Figure 9), the further 
forward in time the consequences will occur. The time horizon is interesting to 
==PAGE:105==
105 
 
 
consider, partially because of the increasing uncertainty as time goes, but also the 
correlation with time and severeness. As also indicated by Informant B:  
“The influence of the policy today may be different from how it influences tomorrow. Short 
medium and long term the influences could be very different.” - Informant B, appendix 2 
Application in policy and company contexts 
The tool is intended to be applied by companies, designers, and policy and business 
decision-makers to understand the consequences of a policy, regarding business, 
social and environmental aspects. It is more accessible to practitioners than other 
tools, as there is no need to be directly familiar with calculations of the impacts of 
policies. Transferring the concern about unintended consequences and REs from the 
academic world to actual political action is sometimes lacking, as Font Vivanco, 
Kemp, et al. (2016) suggest. They raise the issue that the RE has generally not been 
translated into tangible policy action. Positively, this tool can make the work with REs 
and unintended consequences more accessible to a broader field of professionals, 
making it an issue to include in decision-making.  
Policymakers are responsible for designing policies and implementing them, 
however, companies have the responsibility to translate these policies into action, in 
the form of product design, services, and business models. For companies, the step-
by-step tool can help ensure that the whole value chain is covered. It can contribute 
to decision-makers understanding how their business will be affected, avoiding 
unintended consequences that will harm the business, the environment, or social 
actors down- or upstream in its value chain. Moreover, it can help make strategies 
for future product and service design. Potentially, the tool could be incorporated into 
CSR or ESG processes to account for the positive and negative consequences of 
policies on their company and the value chain and develop strategies for how their 
business should evolve. The tool places the companies in a bigger legislative context 
and the insights enable them to interact and negotiate with policymakers.  
“It could be an input to how we get each link covered? If you are in a company, you have to 
listen to your value chain and your customers, but also subcontractors and all parts.” – 
Informant E, Appendix 2

From a policy perspective, the tool for investigation of unintended consequences can 
be utilised in the initial phase of the impact assessment of a policy. The EU 
Commission carries out impact assessments on initiatives expected to have 
significant economic, social or environmental impacts (European Commission, n.d.). 
The EU lays out an initial analysis of the problem, policy objectives and different 
solutions as well as their likely impacts, to which stakeholders can give feedback to. 
The tool could contribute to making an early-stage mapping of the social, 
environmental and economic consequences, as well as the affected stakeholders, and 
their interests, which could lay the ground for a public consultation and feedback 
process with the stakeholders. An informant from an industry association working 
with EU policy emphasised that the tool could be useful when working with the 
legislative processes and making sure that the various industries are represented.  
“That is part of what I try to do by asking the various associations. The waste industry, for 
example, sits at the other end, and they want everything to be separated to make it easier 
for them and to ensure good quality waste streams. The manufacturers want something 
else. Maybe this is the framework that we use to talk to our members.” – Informant E 
Application in other contexts 
The tool is developed based on the investigation of the CE transition, and 
consequences of policy concerning electronics and ICT. Hence, the tool is 
characterised by its life cycle perspective and stakeholder view, which reflects well 
the outcomes of policy interventions in CE. Due to this, it can in principle be 
transferred to other value chains within the CE transition, such as textiles, food, or 
construction, where the life cycle stages are also strongly interconnected and 
interdependent. However, when transferring the tool to other product chains, it is 
important to bear in mind that the catalogue of unintended consequences should be 
evolved to fit the topic under investigation. The catalogue is a central aspect of the 
tool that serves as an initial list of potential effects. Literature should be reviewed for 
consequences documented or discussed in research in the specific field to ensure that 
the analysis takes into consideration already observed effects. This is to ensure a 
scientifically sound basis, where the predictions reflect the existing research.

Acknowledge synergies and trade-offs 
As described in the literature review section 3.1.2 about policies in CE transitions the 
complexity of CE transitions requires a new approach to policy development (Milios 
2018). The policies should, among others, acknowledge the different trade-offs and 
synergies between measures. For example, eco-design requirements can have 
synergies with measures targeting the end of life, such as take-back schemes.  As 
argued, the CEAP can be seen as a policy mix that seeks to enhance the synergies 
between measures and minimise the negative side effects.  
The tool proposed in this thesis investigates the measures separately. Instead of 
looking at the interdependencies between measures, it focuses on the individual 
effects of the measures. This is a conscious choice, as it makes it easier and more 
manageable to handle the initiatives. However, it can be seen as a shortcoming of the 
tool, as it might overlook some of the positive synergies between measures, leading 
to environmental and social benefits. Just as importantly, it can potentially make 
some of the trade-offs between objectives and measures invisible, which in the worst 
case can lead to decisions made on an undisclosed basis.  
To be further added to the discussion about trade-offs and synergies are the power 
structures and negotiations taking place beneath the explicit policy objectives. The 
nature of policymaking in the EU is extremely affected by lobbyers and various 
interests, that influence the policy-making process.  
“The number of lobbyists in the EU is enormous. You must know that all regulation, 
including EU regulation, exists based on tough negotiations and lobbying.” - Informant C, 
Appendix 2 
As an outcome of this, many policies operate in synergies, while others pursue 
conflicting goals. The conflicting goals are traditionally outspoken between industry 
and environmental organisations. As also mentioned by one of the informants:  
“The fight is on because the industry will always say that ‘We must have the free market, 
you must not interfere too much’[…] and from the NGO side saying ‘This is not enough’ 
==PAGE:108==
108 
 
 
[…] and the Commission is always sitting between the two chairs”. – Informant I, 
Appendix 2 
These underlying structures are important to be aware of, and how it influences policy 
making. Everything is a product of extensive negotiation processes between 
stakeholders and their interests, and the perception of end goals is not mutually 
agreed upon.  
Analysis paralysis  
The tool provides an opportunity to consider a very broad spectrum of consequences, 
spanning from social, environmental, and economic. Throughout the step-by-step 
application of the tool, more and more nuances will occur and reveal an ever-
increasing number of potential consequences and REs. When you start looking for 
unintended outcomes, it will always be possible to find them.  
The term analysis paralysis is used for the inability to decide due to overthinking a 
problem(J. Chen et al., 2022). An individual or a group can have too much 
information, which results in the endless weighing of the upsides and downsides of 
each option, making it difficult to pick one. As one informant stated, when asked 
about how to deal with the trade-offs and unintended consequences by design:  
“We can also just sit and discuss this for 100 years, and then nothing ever happens.” – 
Informant H, Appendix 2 
Over-discussing and over-analysing can have the opposite effect than expected. This 
also goes for unintended consequences and REs in CE transitions. A decision for a 
purposeful act will always imply unforeseen events. Thus, it is crucial not to get 
paralysed by the fact that they will occur and therefore refrain from taking any 
decisions at all. It is important to understand the basic condition that every policy 
decision will imply rather unforeseen or unpredictable events. 
Instead, as encountered throughout the analysis and assessment, it is important to 
use the information gained correctly. The practitioner should not get paralysed by the 
information, instead, they should ask themselves what can be done to minimise them. 
As one informant stated:  
==PAGE:109==
109 
 
 
“There are many of those rebound effects I think are, what should I say, a little 
demotivating, right? Because so what? Should we just not do anything about it? Instead, 
you should ask, what can we do to remedy it? – Informant C, Appendix 2 
The tool must not act as an obstacle to making decisions, because it is thought that 
no decision must have negative consequences for any stakeholder group, or the 
slightest degree of REs on the environment. Instead, it must be used for ensuring 
holistic policy-making that deals with the entire life cycle and not only targets one life 
cycle stage, such as waste management, as has been the case up until now. It should 
ensure an informed and nuanced basis for how to work with the unintended 
consequences going forward, whether it be additional investigations or adding 
mitigating measures.  
6.5. 
Limitations and suggestions for further research 
The nine informants were chosen out of their specialist knowledge to cover a broad 
spectrum of the life cycle from design to end-of-life and being both from academia 
and industry. 
Despite the involvement of stakeholders throughout the cycles, the evaluation of the 
tool has to this point been carried out by the researcher based on the input from the 
interactions. Future studies could thus include an actual test of the tool in practice. 
As Rich Pictures are indeed highly participatory tools, it would be insightful to test the 
following aspects:  
- 
How does the tool work as a co-creating tool? Do the participants gain an 
understanding of each other's views, and do they reach a form of agreement? 
- 
Is it easy to understand the steps considering the experience level of the 
testers? 
- 
Are the results related to i) consequences and ii) recommendations obtained 
in the testing similar or do they vary greatly from the ones found in the 
research study? Why?

7. Conclusion 
This thesis aimed to investigate the potential intended and unintended consequences 
of the EU’s circular economy action plan in the life cycle of electronic products 
focusing on smartphones as the product group. Furthermore, it intended at 
developing a support to help companies and decision-makers do similarly with 
additional policies. As a result, an explorative, design-oriented Rich Picture tool was 
developed and used to analyse three measures contained in the action plan. By 
applying the tool to the measures, a wide range of potential consequences and 
rebound effects were discovered. 
The project followed a Design Research Methodology, covering four stages from the 
research clarification to the descriptive study I (understanding and describing the 
situation), prescriptive study (tool building and tool application), and descriptive study II 
(evaluation of the tool).  
In descriptive study I, it was found that policy-induced CE transitions are complex 
and require a special approach to policymaking to minimise unintended 
consequences of interventions. Particularly, there is a need to be aware of both 
synergies and trade-offs. Policy interventions in one life cycle stage might lead to 
unintended consequences elsewhere, due to the interdependency between resource 
streams, stakeholders, and sectors. Thus, the policies are required to identify and 
intervene in all relevant life-cycle stages, focusing on product design as well as end-
of-life, while acknowledging all stakeholders involved.    
A literature review on existing tools and methods for assessing the impact and 
consequences of sustainability actions was conducted to seek inspiration for an 
appropriate basis for tool-building. Many existing tools were quantitatively oriented, 
using indicators and mathematical models, while only a few design-based or 
qualitative approaches were described, leaving a gap for research. It was theorised 
that by using such an approach when investigating CE policies, it is possible to include 
processes and stakeholders and eventually get a broad picture of potential 
unintended consequences. Such a qualitative mapping can act as an initial 
assessment, to inform later quantitative in-depth modelling.  
==PAGE:114==
114 
 
 
The proposed Rich Picture tool suggests an explorative, design-oriented approach, 
consisting of three main perspectives, i) the life cycle view, ii) the stakeholder view, and 
iii) the consequence tree. The (un)intended consequences of the measures can be 
indicated throughout the lifecycle of electronics to capture the potential synergies and 
trade-offs. The different worldviews of the stakeholders involved in the policy 
implementation are reflected to show differing interests and influence, which can 
result in various levels of acceptance of policies.  
In the analyses of the CEAP, it was found that it consists of a comprehensive mix of 
measures that address the whole life cycle of products, from design to consumption 
and end-of-life. The three measures, i.e., the common charger, the right to repair and 
take-back schemes were the objects of analysis for the developed tool. Despite the 
measures driving the transition in the right direction, the analyses indicate that a 
variety of unintended consequences can occur due to the impact on product design, 
business models, usage patterns, and waste treatment. 
The common charger exposed the dilemma between standardisation and innovation, 
which can potentially prevent the implementation of more efficient solutions. A 
recommendation for adopting a decision-making organ for standardisation was given 
to mitigate this consequence. It is suggested to leave room for experimentation for 
new solutions, such as wireless chargers, for them to be fully developed before 
legislating on the area. It was also found that the emerging product trends will have a 
big impact on the number of chargers per capita, and this should be investigated 
further and accounted for in the calculations of positive benefits.  
The analysis of the right to repair showed that psychological obsolescence by design 
can become even more prominent and lead to the replacement of smartphones. The 
imperfect substitution rebound effect raised the question of the displacement of 
repaired, used smartphones over newly produced ones, and why it is recommended 
to estimate the displacement rate. Moreover, considerations were made about the 
necessity of making trade-offs between CE strategies in the design stage. Detailed 
investigations about the consequences of trade-offs between repairability and 
durability or recyclability should be made to make the right design requirements. 
==PAGE:115==
115 
 
 
Additionally, recommendations were given about implementing the right to repair so 
the current networks with repairability and durability knowledge are preserved and 
utilised.  
The analysis of take-back schemes showed that while they provide a unique 
opportunity for companies to transition to sustainable business models, they also 
create a risk of encouraging early replacement and unnecessary consumption. More 
than half of the current reused phones in the EU are sold to developing countries, 
which could be increased by introducing take-back schemes. This can impose 
dilemmas in the receiving countries due to fewer requirements for waste 
management. It was thus recommended to consider impacts occurring outside of the 
EU’s borders.   
The research methods followed in the thesis are a combination of literature reviews 
and case studies. Literature reviews have been used in the initial part of the study to 
investigate the characteristics of policy-induced CE transitions and existing tools for 
assessing consequences. The case studies have been used to build the tool and 
analyse the measures by interviewing informants. They have been carried out in three 
cycles, enabling the insights from the first cycle to improve the tool and analyse the 
second measure in the next cycle. Throughout the cycles, the tool was developed to 
include more elements or adjust the existing ones.  
From a policy perspective, the exploratory Rich Picture tool can contribute to an 
early-stage impact assessment of the unintended consequences as well as mapping 
the affected stakeholders and their interests. Companies and designers have the 
responsibility to translate policies into action in the form of product design, services, 
and business models. The tool can contribute to business decision-makers 
understanding how their business will be affected and improve their product and 
service design. Ultimately, the proposed step-by-step tool enables decision-makers 
to recognise the potential unintended consequences of policy design for them to be 
minimised.
