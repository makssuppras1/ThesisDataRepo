Abstract
This project aims to illustrate damage detection on a monopile-like, medium sized, naturally
loaded experimental model. Damage detection is realised through implementation of a vibration
based structural health monitoring (SHM) system utilizing simple, existing tools. The monitored
parameters are the natural frequencies and mode shapes of the structure. These are implemented in
the SHM system as damage indicators in the form of natural frequencies corrected for environmen-
tal conditions and modal assurance criteria (MAC) values respectively. Successful damage detec-
tion relies on the SHM system being both robust and reliable. Obtaining this is the main focus
when designing the system with regard to the measurement system, signal processing, applied
identiﬁcation method, and correction for environmental conditions.
In the proposed SHM system, the structural response is measured by three triaxial geophones,
chosen based on their high sensitivity and low noise ﬂoor. Signal processing includes appropriate
correction and quality assessment of measurements. The OMA-based time domain poly reference
identiﬁcation technique is applied due to its performance regarding stability and consistency.
Finally, correction for the effect of environmental conditions is based on a least square multivariate
linear regression model including i) air temperature, ii) relative humidity of air, iii) wind speed,
and iv) wind direction.
Ultimately the proposed SHM system is evaluated as damage is induced to the experimental model
in six depth steps. Indication of damage is deﬁned as continuous outliers. The mode shape based
damage indicator, the MAC values, show no continuous outliers. Thus, damage is not indicated.
It is however, seen that a large amount of damage affects the deviation of the MAC values. The
frequency based damage indicator, the corrected natural frequencies, indicates damage already at
the ﬁrst damage step for the ﬁrst and second mode, eventually all modes do. It is thus concluded
from the project, that damage detection is possible when the right measures are taken in design of
the SHM system.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Structural health monitoring (SHM) is of interest to ensure structural integrity of large public
structures such as bridges, thus ensuring human safety. Additionally, SHM is attractive for offshore
structures such as oil platforms and wind turbines, which are difﬁcult to inspect manually. The
opportunity of successful implementation of SHM has been increasing over the last decades, due
to recent technology development of sensors and computational methods. The main purpose of
SHM is to monitor structures continuously in order to detect possible damage while the structures
are in normal operation. Vibration based monitoring can be used for this purpose, as the dynamic
properties of a structure provide information about the physical system. For this reason operational
modal analysis (OMA) has become a widely used tool in SHM, as it facilitates identiﬁcation of
the dynamic properties of the structure under ambient vibrations, thus in operation.
Damage detection covers several aspects and for clariﬁcation the deﬁnition of damage detection
given by Rytter [1] is utilised. Rytter describes four levels of damage detection, which are deﬁned
by
Level 1 Detection: Qualitative identiﬁcation of possible damage somewhere in the structure.
Level 2 Localisation: Includes additional information of the location of the the damage.
Level 3 Assessment: Includes quantiﬁcation of the damage.
Level 4 Consequences: Includes analysis of the structural safety and remaining life of structure.
When considering the ﬁrst level of damage detection, changes in the modal properties (natural
frequencies, mode shapes and damping) can be applied as damage indicators (DI). Changes in the
natural frequencies can reﬂect stiffness changes, as these are related. The stiffness changes can for
example be caused by changes of boundary conditions, cracking, and reduced material stiffness
[2]. Two examples of experimental studies showing the possibility to detect damage (level 1)
based on changes of natural frequencies are conducted on a concrete bridge by Brincker et al. [2]
and on a steel mast by Andersen [3]. Mode shapes have formerly been used as DI by evaluating
the modal assurance criterion (MAC), which compares mode shapes of the healthy structure and
damaged structure. However, some studies have shown the success of using MAC values as a
DI highly depends of the geometry of the structural system as well as location of the damage

[1]. Damping estimates can as well be applied as a DI since structural damping is dependent on
friction, which is increased in small cracks [1]. There are other, not as physically meaningful,
parameters that can be used as DIs. Some of these are presented in [2, 4] and will not be treated
further.
Research shows that the estimated modal parameters are highly dependent on environmental
and operational conditions, which complicates damage detection as health changes need to be
distinguished from environmental and operational effects. This means changes of non structural
mass, temperature, humidity and wind loading among others will cause changes of the modal
parameters, making them variate with time. This time variation is presented in studies by Diord et
al. [5] and Magalhães et al. [6]. Here the natural frequencies of a stadium suspension roof and an
arch bridge are seen to have a daily and an annual pattern mainly due to temperature variations.
The dependency between frequencies and temperature has not deﬁnitively been described, as this
dependency has shown to differ between structural systems and between the modes of each system
[5, 7]. It is known that the total damping of a structure is dependent on structural components,
underlying soil and loading.
For example increasing wind speed increases the aerodynamic
damping [8]. This makes it challenging to detect changes in structural damping, as other damping
components are predominant and there are high uncertainties when estimating ligth damping [1].
Mode shapes are generally seen to be less sensitive to environmental and operational conditions
than damping and natural frequencies [9].
Based on the above, it is clear that there are challenges in performing damage detection by
monitoring modal parameters, as they are obscured by operational and environmental conditions.
However, their sensitivity implies good relation to physics making them trustworthy candidates
for DIs. Keeping this in mind the, the project pursues to answer the main question:
Can environmental/operational conditions as well as damages be robustly detected by applying
OMA-based identiﬁcation techniques in monitoring of a simple structural system?
It is the purpose of this project to answer this question using a simple approach. Focus is on
reliability and robustness of the process, not on precision. Reliability and robustness is to be
understood as designing the measurement system such that measurements of a high quality are
passed on to identiﬁcation with a good success rate. The applied identiﬁcation must be robust
such that ﬂuctuation or outliers does not occur from it. Correction for environmental conditions
is kept simple, to focus on the importance of this correction rather than to optimize it. This leaks
into damage detection, where the aim is to illustrate that damage detection (level 1) is possible.
To obtain this, a SHM campaign is designed for a medium sized monopile-like structure subjected
to natural loading. This SHM campaign is structured so that it follows the scheme presented in
Figure 1.1. Both the environmental conditions and the structural response are measured for a
period of 32 days, split into 27 days of monitoring the healthy structure and 5 days of monitoring
the structure with induced damages. Measuring the environmental conditions is done with an

CHAPTER 2
Operational Modal Analysis
In this project the monitoring system is based on OMA, where the physical properties of the
experimental model are estimated from operational responses of the considered system. In this
chapter the basics of the OMA technique is outlined and the identiﬁcation methods applied within
this project are introduced.
2.1
Basic Concept of OMA
The idea behind OMA is that modal properties such as natural frequencies, fn, modal damping
ratio, ⇣n and mode shapes, bn or an1, can be determined from the response of the system, when
the system is subjected to random loading. A random load has similar characteristics as white
noise with an even distribution of energy over a large range of frequencies. Most structures are
excited by loads that can be considered random, such as wind. According to Brincker and Ventura
[10] the load does not necessarily need to have white noise characteristics for OMA to succeed, as
the load just needs to have a wide frequency range that covers the modes of interest, ensuring that
the modes are excited and contributes to the measured response. In this case, the concept of OMA
can be illustrated as in Figure 2.1. The ﬁgure shows that the broadband loading is a result of an
imaginary ﬁlter, which is loaded by white noise. This means that the OMA-based identiﬁcation
not only contains physical information about the structure but also information about the loading
ﬁlter. However, this is not a problem as the loading ﬁlter does not change the properties of the
structural system [10].
Additionally, it is illustrated in Figure 2.1 that the structural system acts as a linear ﬁlter to the
unknown load. This linear ﬁlter can be expressed by the equation of motion of a multiple degree
of freedom system as

a size of N ⇥N. Damping in the system is considered to be viscous. Furthermore a small amount

From the measured response of a structural system, the physical information about the system

of the structure must be described.
In the time domain this is accomplished by considering
correlation functions as free decays, where the physical properties are concentrated as long as
the response is Gaussian [10]. Spectral densities which are equivalent to the correlation functions
are used for identiﬁcation in the frequency domain.
It has to be kept in mind that information about both the structural system and the loading are
present in the OMA-based identiﬁcation results, therefore a challenge remains in differentiating
the physically meaningful modes with respect to the structural system from the modes originating
from the loading ﬁlter or noise. Additionally, the technique should be used carefully when the
bandwidth of the loading is not wide enough or it is acting on the structure in a way that does not
excite all modes of interest, thus not visible in the analysis.

2.2
Identiﬁcation Methods
To extract estimates of the modal parameters from the condensed information in the CF and SD
matrices, a variety of OMA-based identiﬁcation methods can be applied. These identiﬁcation
methods are generally split into two different types; time domain identiﬁcation and frequency
domain identiﬁcation. The main difference between these two is that the time domain identiﬁcation
techniques work by ﬁtting to the CF whereas the frequency domain identiﬁcation techniques
work by ﬁtting to the SD function. For the work connected to the present report three different
identiﬁcation methods are used, one in the time domain, time domain poly reference (TDPR), and
two in the frequency domain, frequency domain poly reference (FDPR) and frequency domain
decomposition (FDD).

OMA-based Identiﬁcation
To investigate if the choice of sensor conﬁguration is appropriate for identifying the ﬁrst ﬁve
modes, OMA-based identiﬁcation is performed to estimate the modal parameters of the FE model
from the simulated responses. It is chosen to use the FDD identiﬁcation method presented in
Section 2.2. Appropriate signal processing techniques are applied before the actual identiﬁcation.
For the simulated responses this consist of decimation of the signal and construction of the SD
matrix from the signal.
When following good signal processing practice the natural frequencies of the modes has to
lay within 10%-90% of the Nyquist frequency. Since this is not possible for all of the modes
considered here at the same time, it is chosen to perform the identiﬁcation of the ﬁrst three modes
and the two last modes in two separate analyses. In the analysis of the ﬁrst three modes the
simulated signals are decimated by a factor of 8. This reduces the Nyquist frequency from 50 Hz
to 6.25 Hz. For the analysis of the last two modes no decimation is applied. The SD matrix is
estimated by applying the Welch averaging technique using a Hanning window with 50% overlap.
The number of data points in each segment is set to N = 512 for both analyses. The singular
value plots of the SD matrices are presented in Figure 4.5.

Identiﬁcation and Robustness
The robustness of the applied identiﬁcation technique is crucial for SHM systems, as health
changes need to be distinguished from any other sources of change in the modal parameters
(natural frequencies and mode shapes). On this basis the robustness of the three proposed OMA-
based identiﬁcation techniques (FDD, TDPR, and FDPR) is evaluated on the 528 valid measure-
ments from the structural healthy state. The robustness of a technique is evaluated on its stability
and consistency. Stability of a technique covers the ability to identify all ﬁve modes of interest
when having constant input parameters. While consistency deals with the uncertainty of the
identiﬁed modal parameters. At last a sensitivity analysis is carried out for the technique with
best performance regarding robustness.
7.1
Identiﬁcation
To achieve robust identiﬁcation preparation of the measurements and application of the methods
must be well-chosen. Identiﬁcation is performed on the structural response represented by the six
DOFs in the centroid, ycentroid, of the top site. However, these DOFs have different units why
identiﬁcation should not be performed on these measurements directly. The translational DOFs
are measured in m, whereas the rotational DOFs are unit-less. To overcome this issue and obtain
the right relative magnitude between signals, it is decided to multiply all rotational DOFs by half
the height of the structure, thus a factor of 2 m. From this it follows that the rotational DOFs of
the identiﬁed mode shapes need to be divided by a factor of 2 m.
Next the measured signals must be decimated to meet the conditions of keeping the sought frequen-
cies in the band of 10-90% of the Nyquist frequency, when applying the three identiﬁcation
techniques; TDPR, FDPR, and FDD. Thus, the decimation performed varies between the identiﬁ-
cation of mode 1, 2, and 3 and the identiﬁcation of mode 4 and 5. The performed decimations are
presented in Table 7.1. All applications of the decimation are applying a Chebyshev ﬁlter. When
estimating the SD matrix, the methods start to differ. For the FDD and TDPR, the SD matrix is

Environmental Model
As presented in the previous chapter, the natural frequencies are subject to change when environ-
mental conditions change. In order to perform damage detection these changes must be distinguish-
ed from the changes due to structural health degradation. Therefore the aim in this chapter is to
construct a model that can predict the natural frequencies as a function of environmental conditions
and since correct the identiﬁed frequencies accordingly. The model is based on the four parameters
i) temperature, ii) relative humidity of the air, iii) wind speed, and iv) wind direction. Thus, these
parameters are measured while measuring the structural response. No correction is performed of
the mode shapes, as the estimates of these are found to be rather constant in the previous section.

CHAPTER 9
Damage Detection
Design of the SHM system as presented in the previous chapters is focused around robustness and
reliability. Ultimately, these are the criteria for successful damage detection. Thus, in this chapter
the SHM system will be applied while the experimental model is damaged. Successful damage
detection in this case is considered as the SHM system’s ability to illustrate that structural change
has occurred. For the present study this is deﬁned as one or both of the DIs (natural frequencies
and mode shapes) showing continues outliers. Continuity is necessary as outliers with no physical
explanation can appear randomly within the statistical margin but are unlikely to be in immediate
succession of each other. On the other hand, damage causes permanent change of the structural
system, thus outliers due to damage are expected to be continuous. An outlier is deﬁned as a value
that deviates more than three MAD values from the median of the DI based on measurements in
the healthy structural state.
Damage to the experimental model is induced as eleven cuts on one side of the wooden pile. The
cuts are made with a circular saw. These cuts are distributed over 200 mm, effectively leaving
20 mm between each cut. The lowest cut is located at a height of 231 mm above the top of the
foundation mounts, see Figure 9.1. Damage is induced stepwise and for each step the damage
depth is measured as the average depth of all eleven cuts. An overview of the damage induction
can be seen in Table 9.1.

CHAPTER 10
Discussion
The SHM system proposed in this project does successfully illustrate damage, it is considered
vital for this success that the applied SHM system is robust and reliable. The applied measures to
obtain robustness and reliability of the system are discussed in this chapter.
To be truly robust, the SHM system must provide information on the structural health every time
it is prompted. Thus the 99.11% success rate for obtaining quality measurements, presented in
Section 6.2, could be improved. One approach could be to investigate and develop the redundancy
of the monitoring system. A few measurements are discarded due to false ampliﬁcation of a
single signal. For increased redundancy of the system, this signal could simply be discarded and
the remaining still used for transformation to the centroid DOFs. Furthermore, as it was also
mentioned in Section 6.2, the criteria for identifying false ampliﬁcation is incomplete. Besides
this, the quality assessment of measurements should include detection of clipping, drop outs
and spikes. For this project, a portion of measurements is manually inspected, thus building
some degree of trust that these concepts are not occurring. However, detectable criteria should
be implemented such that all measurements are checked. Criteria for these as well as further
developed criteria for false ampliﬁcation could be developed from simulation studies. In addition,
the large amount of planned measurements that are simply not performed or stored by the measure-
ment system must be attended to. A large effort is already put into minimising this problem,
approaching automation of the measurement system, though inadequate. Obtaining full automation
of the measurement system would thus add to the robustness, avoiding periods of time where no
structural health information is provided.
For the SHM system to be reliable, indications of change should occur solely from structural
degradation. In this project this is treated through robustness of the applied identiﬁcation method
and the correction for the inﬂuence of environmental change. As presented, the TDPR is the
applied method for identiﬁcation. This choice is based on it being the more robust of the three
proposed methods when compared on measures of both stability and consistency. With consistency
playing an important part, the formulation applied here could be further developed. First it is
evaluated on the identiﬁed frequencies without any form for correction, despite knowing that

the frequencies are inﬂuenced from environmental changes. Thus, deviation from the mean not
necessarily normally distributed, as assumed for determining MoE. Why it could be considered to
evaluate either in a different way or on a different parameter. Additionally, it could be investigated
if a certain precision level is necessary for a general SHM system to robustly detect damage.
Differentiating change in the frequencies from structural health degradation and changes in environ-
mental parameters is for the proposed SHM system based on a least square multivariate linear
ﬁt. This model is chosen, as simplicity is also an aim for the presented project. While it does

Another improvement is to utilise the observed change in mode shape behaviour, seen as the
change in deviation of the MAC value. Reformulating the criteria for damage detection to be
deviation based might allow the MAC value to also be a successful damage indicator. If so, the
importance of alignment of the identiﬁed mode shapes should be noticed, as presented in Section
4.3. Having multiple DIs would increase reliability of the SHM system and potentially assist in
raising the damage detection to level 2 (localisation).
Overall, it is seen that even though damage is successfully illustrated by the corrected natural
frequencies, the SHM system contains multiple aspects that can be improved. Thus, it must be
considered that damage being indicated by the system at a quite small reduction of the cross
section might also be caused by other factors than robustness and reliability. First the simplicity
of the experimental structure yields a correspondingly simple dynamic system, governed by the
stiffness of the wooden pile and mass of the top site. This means that any damage induced to the
wooden pile will affect the global behaviour, thus be detectable through OMA. Furthermore the
location of the induced damage is considered to be in a quite critical part of the pile, potentially
making the effect in global behaviour greater, causing early damage detection. The damage level
that can be illustrated in the presented project can therefore not necessarily be considered a realistic
expectation for real, more complex, structures. For example, more locally induced damage on a
mast is investigated by Andersen [3]. Here damage is ﬁrst detected when a local cross section is
reduced by 27%. This is found using natural frequencies with no correction for environmental
changes, thus comparable to the detection at 6.9% reduction presented here.
The presented
damage detection does however, with its high sensitivity, create good basis for investigating at
what level damage can be detected in real structures. Especially since sensitivity is expected to
increase when improving robustness and reliability according to the above discussion.

CHAPTER 11
Conclusion
This study includes design and application of a SHM system, with the aim to illustrate damage
detection while monitoring an experimental model. The experimental model is designed to be a
monopile-like structure consisting of a wooden pile and a steel girder top site. The measurement
system is designed based on FE analysis and simulations, thus the system should describe the
three translational and three rotational DOFs in the centroid of the top site. Nine translational
sensors are placed on the top site and transformed into the six DOFs of the centroid, this level
of over-determination is considered a balance between redundancy and simplicity. The applied
sensors are triaxial geophones, chosen due to their high sensitivity and low noise level.
Robustness and reliability of the SHM system are obtained through quality assessment and choice
of the applied identiﬁcation method, thus avoiding false identiﬁcation of damage. Quality assess-
ment is evaluated on two parameters; the signal to noise ratio and the residual of transformation.
These criteria improve robustness of the system but are not fully sufﬁcient. The applied OMA-ba-
sed identiﬁcation method, TDPR, is chosen as the most robust out of the three candidates (TDPR,
FDPR, and FDD) when evaluated on stability and consistency.
From the identiﬁed modal parameters it is found that the natural frequencies are inﬂuenced by
environmental conditions. The identiﬁed mode shapes are not seen to be inﬂuenced by environmen-
tal conditions. Reliability of the SHM system is thus dependent on the ability to differentiate
changes in the modal parameters from the inﬂuence of environmental conditions and structural
changes. This is done by correcting the natural frequencies with an environmental model, designed
by multivariate linear regression based on four parameters, i) air temperature, ii) relative humidity
of the air, iii) wind speed, and iv) wind direction.
The most suitable model for each mode
considerably decreases the deviation of the natural frequencies, allowing more sensitive and reli-
able damage detection. The proposed model does however not remove all ﬂuctuation caused by
environmental changes.
Induced damage is detected from the corrected frequencies of the ﬁrst and second modes already
at the ﬁrst damage step (1.1% cross sectional reduction). All modes eventually indicate damage.

No indication of damage is observed from the MAC values. However, a change in deviation of the
MAC values is seen in the ﬁnal damage step for the ﬁrst two modes. Overall it is concluded that
damage detection based on natural frequencies is indeed possible with implementation of simple,
existing tools.
11.1
Future Perspectives
Even though the present study shows that damage detection is possible through SHM, there are a
number of ways in which the work presented here can be improved. Some of these improvements
would be necessary before the SHM system can be up-scaled and successfully be implemented on
real structures.
As robustness and reliability are essential for a successful SHM system, it is important that
damage is only indicated when it actually is present. Thus improving the environmental model
of the natural frequencies would be very beneﬁcial.
This could be done by monitoring the
considered parameters more locally or considering other methods for ﬁtting, such as principal
component analysis (PCA). Implementing continuous updating of the environmental model when
measurements are declared healthy is essential to maintain and potentially increase precision of
the model. This is especially relevant for long term monitoring campaigns of real structures.
Furthermore the correction process should be expanded to include other non-structural parameters
such as changes in mass and other operational conditions. Additionally, ﬁnding a way to identify
damage from the observed changes in MAC values, would allow for damage detection on an
additional parameter. This is expected to be beneﬁcial for implementing localisation (level 2
damage detection).
In this study it is only illustrated that damage can be detected, but for the SHM system to be
applicable for real structures, the deﬁnition of when damage is detected should be clearly deﬁned.
This could be done based on statistical considerations. Furthermore, the SHM system proposed
here is to some extend running automatically, however issues, such as failures of the measurement
system, causes manual labour and investigation of errors. To make SHM attractive, these practical
issues should be solved and full automation applied.
Another interesting investigation, is to increase complexity of the monitored experimental model.
This could include a study of different locations of damage. Thus, increasing the chance of
successful and consistent damage detection on real, complex structures.

APPENDIX B
Experimental Model
Additional to the initial introduction of the experimental model in Chapter 3, this appendix contains
a more detailed description of the model including technical as built drawings and information
about material properties.
B.1
Material Speciﬁcations
The structure, as mentioned previously, consists of three main elements, the steel girder top site,
the wooden mast, and the concrete block foundation. The three components are mounted together
with different steel mounts and bolts.
Steel
The top girder is made of S235 steel and is welded together. All bolts used are made of A4
steel. All connection pieces are made from S235 steel as well. The steel is painted for corrosion
protection.
Wood
The wood is construction wood of type C18. Each mast is ordered as 12 feet long and 4 inch
⇥4 inch cross section.
However, the precision of the wood is not prioritized and so these
measurements are merely estimates, thus the measurements used for calculations through out the
report are estimates and the length and cross section dimensions are set to 3.6 m and 100 mm ⇥
100 mm respectively.
Concrete
The foundation is a concrete block measuring 100 cm ⇥100 cm ⇥80 cm and cast as a 35 MPa
aggressive concrete. The cement used is White Portland Cement. As aggregates there is a 1 - 5 -
3 split of cement, stone and sand. 4-8 mm and 8-16 mm blue-green stones and 0-2 mm Great Belt
sand are used. It has a total volume of 0.8 m3 and is estimated to weigh 1.9 t. No reinforcement is
added.

In order to perform proper monitoring, the measurements must be performed automatically. For
this purpose the MATLAB script MainScript_AutoMeasurement.m is used, see Annex.
Within this script, a conﬁguration ﬁle (config.m in Annex) is loaded with speciﬁcations of the
measurement. The script then prompts the base station to perform each measurement. The script
takes into account some of the most critical issues with the SenseNet system. First the system
==PAGE:92==
D.3. TEST SET-UP
83
sometimes fails to run the measurement for the full length of time, it most often fails within the
ﬁrst minute. This is registered in the script, and if it is the case, the amputated ﬁle is then deleted,
the system is paused for 60 seconds and a new measurement is prompted. Most often this solves
the problem, once in a while the second measurement also fails, this then results in an actually
failed measurement. Secondly the SenseNet system has an issue with the set time and date, and it
is not possible to change it. This means that the automatic time stamp must be corrected in order
to match the correct environmental data. For this, the time and date from the control PC is used
for when the measurement prompt is sent to the base station, thus deviating with a few second to
when the measurement is actually started. Finally, when the measurement is completed the ﬁle
is identiﬁed in the base station and moved to a location speciﬁed in the conﬁguration ﬁle. This
serves two purposes, it sorts the data ﬁles where the user wants it and it keeps the rather limited
space on the base station free. In order to run every hour this script is run on the control PC by the
in-build Windows software Scheduled Task. This is done by running a command line in Windows.

APPENDIX G
Environmental Model
The environmental model is constructed by performing multivariate linear regression on the identi-
ﬁed natural frequencies and a set of eight possible predictors as presented in Chapter 8.
In
order to avoid collinearity, the correlations between the predictors are presented in Figure G.1.
Correlation is present between predictors originating from the same environmental parameter;
the linear component and second order component of the parameter. Thus, both predictors will
not be included in the model at the same time. The threshold for collinearity is chosen as a
correlation above 0.8. The wind direction parameter compared to its own second order parameter
is an exception since the found correlation is very low, but there seem to be a visual correlation.
Thus, this combination is also excluded.
Figure G.1: Correlation analysis of predictors. Predictors showing correlation are crossed with a purple
cross to show that the combination must not be included in the model.
