Abstract
This thesis investigates the production of liquid oxygen and liquid methane on the surface
of Mars using only the resources that be found on Mars. Two versions of this production
plant are establishedëƒ one of these uses the Martian waterôÙice as its hydrogen source,
whereas the other plant uses hydrogen brought from Earth. Both of the plants use the CO2ôÙ
rich Martian atmosphere as their carbon source. In establishing this plant, the SpaceX
Starship launch system is used as a foundation, and the production target is set in order
to fill the propellant tanks of this ship during a 22 month period. The energy demands
of both plants are found, including the cost of expelling excess heat, and this is used to
size support systems like power generation and mining equipment in order to establish an
approximate mass budget. The EarthôÙbased hydrogen system quickly exceeds the 100
ton mass budget of the Starship rocket, whereas the Martian waterôÙice system seems to
allow for ample headroom in comparison.

Introduction
Since the space race of the 1960ãs, Mars has been of key interest as the next frontier
of human space exploration. In our solar system, Mars is together with Venus the most
EarthôÙlike planets, and current research and speculation is pointing to the possibility of
Marsã once having had a thick atmosphere and liquid water present on the surface. While
Venus has a much denser atmosphere than Mars of approx. 90 atm, high concentrations
of sulphuric acid and high surface temperatures of more than 400ô¯C make Venus a more
difficult choice than Mars when it comes to human space exploration. Next to the Moon,
Mars is recognised as one of the first bodies in the solar system that will see colonisaôÙ
tion, and recent development projects from private companies, like SpaceX, are investing
heavily in Marsã future colonisation. Its atmosphere is rather thin at around 1/100 the presôÙ
sure of Earthãs atmosphere, and the temperature on the surface swings wildly between a
freezing ôÙ100ô¯C in the night and a warm 20ô¯C in the day. With an atmosphere consisting
almost purely of carbon dioxide and small amounts of nitrogen and argon, the conditions
on Mars are almost invitingly hospitable compared to other places in the solar system.

projects. At its core, the idea is simply to utilise the available resources around you in
order to produce what is needed for a certain mission to remove the need for transporting
resources from Earth. For colonisation projects of the Moon and of Mars, ISRU has to
be thought into the projects as the expense of continuous shipping of resources may
make the projects economically unfeasible, and the risk of disrupted supply chains might
endanger human life. The International Space Station is an example of a project that is
EarthôÙdependent because it cannot sustain itself independently.

rocket fuel, with this thesis focusing on the latter. Moon dust and Mars dust have been

2.1.1
Process using CO2ôÙelectrolysis
Just like in the original NASA proposal from fig. 2.1, CO2ôÙelectrolysis could still be impleôÙ
mented in a coôÙelectrolysis process when using a solid oxide electrolysis cell. This allows
for electrolysis of steam and carbon dioxide to occur simultaneously in the same compoôÙ
nent. In the context of methane production, there will always be a water product which
has to be electrolysed, so a steam electrolysis always needs to be present.
If an internal hydrogen source is used, it is not possible to produce enough oxygen from
the H2O electrolysis process to obtain the correct oxygenôÙtoôÙfuel ratio needed in a rocket.
Therefore, CO2 electrolysis is needed to increase the oxygen production. This becomes
clear when looking at the overall reaction scheme of the entire process. If oxygen is
created through water electrolysis, and the only water source is the CO2 methanation
then in order to increase water production, methane production has to increase as well.
Seeing how methane production demands hydrogen to occur, this will also increase the
amount of needed hydrogen, and at the end result will be an overproduction of methane
to meet the oxygen production demand. Thus methane will need to be discarded when
the storage is filled ahead of time. The proper fix to this problem is instead to employ
dual electrolysis of carbon dioxide as well to produce more oxygen. By increasing CO2
intake, oxygen production can be increased, and the methanation will switch to be carbon
monoxide based instead of carbon dioxide. This will lead to an overproduction of carbon
monoxide which will have to be discarded, however, it is still less expensive than bringing
excess hydrogen from Earth. Put in short, the waterôÙice plant will have to discard O2
whereas the hydrogenôÙbased plant will have to discard CO.

2.3.2
Water purification
Generally true for all electrolysis systems is that they need very pure water in order to
operate under stable conditions for prolonged amounts of time. On Mars, the ice will be
dug out of the soil, which will inevitably introduce contamination to the water. The soil will
need to be filtered from the liquid water, but any ions that dissolved into the water will also
need to be removed. The contaminants that may be found in the dirt is covered more
inôÙdepth in section 3.2. On Earth, reverse osmosis is often used to strip water of its ions,
but it requires a separate sacrificial water stream which is discarded after absorbing the
ions from the product water stream. Since on Mars, the water will be a precious resource,
it seems like a poor idea to use a reverse osmosis process. Another option is distillation
which entails boiling the water such that it leaves the ionic compounds behind. Boiling
water is a very energy intensive process, but due to the presence of methanation reactor,
plenty of heat will be available to perform this process. In boiling the water, the heat can
also be used to pressurize the steam to the desired operating pressure, in a similar fashion
to how a pressure cooker works, which removes the need for a compression system and
thus saving energy.
Any purification system will need occasional cleaning procedures to get rid of the contamôÙ
inant material, and this would be no different. An option to enable this would be to have
two identical systems running side by side, such that when one distillation boiler needs a
cleaning cycle, the other will be ready to take over. Additionally, some sort of hatch sysôÙ
tem will be needed for a pressurized distillation system to enable the continuous adding

Cryogenic liquefaction is the process of taking a substance which would otherwise be a
gas at ambient conditions (1 atm, 20ô¯C) and cooling it until it liquefies. The term cryogenic
is not particularly wellôÙdefined, but it is generally accepted to be anything liquid processing
system that is below ôÙ50ô¯C. At its core, liquefaction is no different from any other cooling
system. It uses one or more working fluids to move heat from a process stream by changôÙ
ing the pressures of the working fluids to change their boiling points and thus allow heat
removal at certain temperatures. The systems can be very simple or very complex and
the efficiency of these systems generally scale with size and complexity. This makes it
an interesting tradeôÙoff to consider in a Mars plant. Energy might be difficult to come by
which would argue for the need of a more efficient system, but on the other hand getting
equipment to Mars is expensive and complicated, so the less equipment the better. It will
ultimately be an engineering decision to figure out how to balance these two parameters
against each other.

2.5.3
Geothermal generation
While not likely to be immediately useful to Mars operations, it is an interesting exercise to
assess to feasibility of Martian geothermal power generation. It is no sprawling research
topic, but serious considerations have been made towards this within the past 25 years

crust. The consutruction of a geothermal power plant is more difficult than the setup of
solar panels or wind generation, however, the production of power should be continuous

effective geothermal plants would be used directly for the production of heat [23], but in
this case electrical energy would be needed which introduces the need for heat rejection

subsurface temperature profile, but a mean thermal gradient of 4.5 K/km is assumed [24]
for the first 50 km. With a depth of, for example 5 km, and a mean surface temperature
of ôÙ63ô¯C, the temperature would be ôÙ40.5ô¯C. It becomes clear that site selection would be
especially critical and it would be desired to select one which is on the upper range of the
proposed thermal gradients at 25 K/km. For a 5 km depth, this would yield a temperature
of 62ô¯C, which is more likely to be usable. This would likely also mean that water would
be a poor choice for the working fluid seeing as the hydrostatic pressure of 5 km water
column on Mars would yield a boiling point of 360ô¯C and thus not allow for a phase change.
Generally, a phase change would be desired as the greatly increases the heat carrying
capabilities of whatever working fluid is used. For the case of water, a 125ô¯C temperature
change without phase change would allow for 0.5 MJ/kg, while the phase change alone
at 62ô¯C would allow for 2.3 MJ/kg to be absorbed [5]. Thus without the phase change, a
factor of almost 5 of increase in water flow would be needed to move the same amount
of energy. This does not account for that fact that at ôÙ63ô¯C, water would be close to its
triple point, furthering the case against not using water as the working fluid. A benefit of
the low atmospheric pressure on Mars is that pressurized systems can be run at lower
absolute pressures while still maintaining positive gauge pressures. Since geothermal
energy generation probably will not see use for the first methalox production plants on
Mars, it will not be further considered.

4.1
A note on optimization
Normally in process plants, many of the design variables like number compression stages
and their intermediate pressures, reactor temperatures, etc., are arbitrarily chosen based
on previous experiences. Due to a large amount of degreesôÙofôÙfreedom (DoF) to consider,
this is usually the accepted approach for nonôÙcritical, nonôÙoptimized systems. Ideally, for
an n DoF system, an n dimensional design space would be mapped out in order to pick
the most ideal temperatures and pressures for operation, and this is called parametric
sweeping where the full design space is swept across. This problem however scales to
the power of n, so if 100 sampling points are desired for each DoF, 100n simulations would
be required. This approach is typically feasible for no more than 4ôÙ5 DoFs depending on
the number of samples that are required to map out the design space. Algorithmic and
scoring approaches are normally applied in these types of situations to reduce the total
number simulations. In this thesis, the degreesôÙofôÙfreedom are set according to available
literature or otherwise reasoned for in the specific case, and as such no computations are
spent optimizing for overall plant performance.

4.3
Water pretreatment
The water enters the system in solid form at Martian conditions, very close to its triple point.
The melting process and subsequent pressure buildup occurs through a heat exchange
process with the methanation system. From the methanation reactor, ample heat will be
available at, and in Aspen, the water will be heated in two steps. The first step is a heating
step to raise the temperature to boiling, and the second step is a boiling step to raise the
vapor quality to 1. No superheating is implemented, but superheating by a few degrees
would not change anything in terms of heat management in the plant, so the vapor quality
of 1 is selected as a service to the reader.
In actuality, the Aspen model only deals with the heating and boiling of liquid water and
water vapor. It does not model the melting of the ice or the pressure buildôÙup, however,
the melting of ice is accounted for and deemed possible by looking at the heat available
from the methanation system. Likewise, the Aspen model does not handle any particulate
matter that may be in the process stream on Mars or subsequent filtration and purification.
These are very real engineering problems that will need to be solved eventually. The
pretreatment could involve a filtration or settling step, and a distillation or reverse osmosis
step for the initial purification. Melting the ice would like just involved a continuous process
where ice is a fed into a tub of water. For now, however, the model only accounts for the
thermodynamics of going from cold water to steam. A diagram of the boiler unit is shown
on fig. 4.2.

almost pure methane a the bottom of the distillation column and leaving the other comôÙ

is used for this. The plant will byôÙdefault produce too much oxygen seeing how methane

condenser of the column needs low temperature cooling, and this is found in the already

is used to cool the condenser at around ôÙ195ô¯C.

Heat management
Heat rejection of the plant is not modelled as described in section 2.6. Instead, heater
units are used in Aspen to set the temperature to 3ô¯C when needed, and this is assumed
to be handled by the heat management system. Generally, the plant produces an excess
of heat, and while most of this has to be discarded somehow, some of it usable in the
water pretreatment step or hydrogen storage. Boiling of water requires large amounts of
energy, and while the heat transfer itself is not modelled, it is assumed possible, and it
is confirmed in the results section that the heat for this can be found in the methanation
setup. Thus, it must be expected that there would be multiple heat management systems
on the plant, both for internally moving heat and for the expulsion of excess heat. Internal
heat management could be handled by a waterôÙbased system, and heat expulsion could
be handled by an ammonia system as described in section 2.6.

Given that the complexity of launching a rocket to Mars, having it gather ice and CO2,
synthesizing its own fuel from the gathered resources, and then storing this fuel for almost

effort would be made to reduce the complexity of this mission. A way to do this is to bring
a hydrogen store from Earth ôÙ at least for the first proofôÙofôÙconcept mission. The idea of

bring parts of the mining infrastructure with it while not having to rely on it. Regardless,
the idea is worth investigating in the context of Starship as it would allow for a fully selfôÙ

6.5
Mass budget
Recall that the plant design in this report bases itself on the SpaceX Starship interplaneôÙ
tary launch system as shown on fig. 5.1. This system is intended to bring between 100
ton to 150 ton to the surface of Mars, which puts a hard line on the mission mass budget.
Normally in the design of chemical processing plants, mass budget is not a concern, howôÙ
ever, since this plant needs to be packed inside a massive space ship and sent to Mars it
is of major concern. While some effort has been made throughout this report to quantify
the mass of some of the required subsystems, no effort has been made to quantify the
mass of the plant itself. Instead, the other subsystems can be used to indicate how much
headroom is left for a plant designer in the final rocket.

7
Discussion
The discussion chapter covers some questions that can be difficult to numerically analyse
and tries to highlight key points for some of these topics.
7.1
Earth hydrogen or Mars ice?
One of the biggest questions is whether or not the first ship to Mars should bring its own
infrastructure for ice mining. The mass implications of not doing so are significant, as
approximately 70 tons of hydrogen would otherwise need to be hauled to Mars in a StarôÙ
ship context. Starship is currently supposed to be able to handle 100 tons to Mars, so
a significant portion of the mass budget would be absorbed by the hydrogen. The other
option is, as should be clear by now, to mine the required ice inôÙsitu and electrolyse it.
This significantly slacks the mass budget, but also introduces a toôÙbeôÙproven technology
which is autonomous mining robots. While currently in development, fully autonomous
mining robots have not been industrially proven, and this could have a significant impact
on the mission risk assessment as well as the realistic time horizon. The difference in
energy demands between the two plants are relatively insignificant in this scope.
Since we cannot know the weight of the remaining systems easily without a more inôÙdepth
analysis, we cannot say how much margin is actually needed for the plant itself. It is also
likely that the 100 ton is not a hard cap, and the actual value may differ with some ton
to either side. One of the best way to proceed with finding the answer to this question
would be to establish close communications (or even work for/with) SpaceX to understand
the limitations of the Starship system, which admittedly is constantly being refined at the
time of writing August 4th, 2021. An assumption made by the author of this thesis is that
it is desirable to pack the entire production stack into a single ship, which may not be
true. Using two or more ships will give considerable amounts of freedom to the mission
designers, while also adding to the mission complexity of having to land multiple ships in
close vicinity with whatever implications that might have.
7.2
Unaddressed plant issues
This section will cover some of the issues that have crept up during the theoretical develôÙ
opment but which were not directly addressed during the thesis. It is very likely that the
list is infinitely long, however, only a subset of entries to said list is covered here.
7.2.1
Ice inlet
The ice inlet of the ISRU version of the plant will need some careful consideration. Since
the plant and melting process operate at pressures higher than the ambient pressure on
Mars, an admission system that allows the feeding of ice to proceed without depressurisôÙ
ing the plant. This will likely have to be done using some sort of hatchôÙbased system.
Excavated iceôÙsand mixture could be admitted into a volume which is then sealed off and
pressurised before being moved to a melting tub, which could also be used for collecting
regolith sedimentation. This would need to somehow be discarded.
7.2.2
Water removal
The final water removal step of the methanation process is now just assumed to remove
all water from the stream, but no consideration is given to the technology used. This could
be desiccant based where the product stream is run across a bed of moisture absorbing
pellets. Once these are saturated, they would need to be heated to release their moisture
InôÙsitu resource utilisation for production of rocket fuel for methane oxygen propulsion systems on Mars 69
==PAGE:78==
content, which entails the use of at least two desiccant chambers. It could also be temôÙ
perature based, where the gas stream is cooled far below freezing such that the water
freezes out. When the freezer system is saturated, a thawing cycle would need to be run
where the main product stream is cycled to another freezer system.
7.2.3
CO removal
Currently, a carbon monoxide removal step is implemented for the EarthôÙbased hydrogen
version of plant after the electrolysis process. More carbon dioxide is admitted to the sysôÙ
tem from the atmosphere is to ensure a sufficient production of oxygen, but this produces
an excess of carbon monoxide which would overload the methanation process if not disôÙ
carded. The actual removal step is currently not modelled, and the carbon monoxide is
just simply taken out in the correct proportion.
Realistically, the setup would likely not use coôÙelectrolysis. There would be two electrolôÙ
ysis unitsëƒ one for the water, and one for the carbon dioxide. In doing so, a valve could
control that a certain amount of the product stream after the electrolysis unit is readmitted
to the atmosphere, thus removing both remnant carbon dioxide as well as carbon monoxôÙ
ide. This would have very minor implications on the energy consumption of the plant (but
it would probably put them even closer to each other), and the EarthôÙbased hydrogen
plant would run with less lower carbon dioxide to carbon monoxide ratio.
7.2.4
Flue gas handling
The waste product of the distillation process is currently being burnt off with an offôÙtap
from the oxygen production in the electrolysis unit. This is mostly to avoid atmospheric
contamination of Mars, so the hydrogen and methane in the offôÙgas is converted to carbon
dioxide and water, which Mars has ample of. The plant is run with a slight hydrogen
excess in order to reduce CO2 in the final product, which causes hydrogen to end up in
the methane product stream. Given that a hydrogen combustion is extremely energetic,
the outlet temperature of the exhaust is almost 2800ô¯C in the ISRU waterôÙice case, which
would demand some sort of cooling or exotic material use in the combustor. The observant
reader might have noticed that the temperature of the exhaust in the hydrogen plant is only
around 1200ô¯C due to an oxygen deficiency meaning that not all the hydrogen is burnt
off. One might question whether or not combustion is required, but it was implemented to
reduce the atmospheric impact a plant like this might have.
7.2.5
Hydrogen pumping
Currently the hydrogen is introduced to the system using thermal pressurisation, but this
would require a sort of batch system, similarly to the ice admission process discussed
above. This might be unnecessarily difficult in practice. There is no reason why there
couldnãt be a liquid hydrogen pump pumping the liquid. Energy costs from pumping are
generally significantly lower than compression costs as liquids are relatively incompressôÙ
ible. Due to time, this was not changed in the simulation, however, the implications are
miniscule on the big picture regardless.
7.2.6
Hydrogen recycling
In the current setup, the methane is refined through a distillation process with the intent
of removing hydrogen, argon and nitrogen from the product stream. This is then burnt
as discussed in section 7.2.4. This is wasteful, especially in the EarthôÙbased hydrogen
plant, so it would be desired that this hydrogen is recycled back into the methanation
system somehow. A good solution to this would be a hydrogen membrane that allows
only the hydrogen to pass back into the system. Given that we need to expel excess
nitrogen and argon to avoid a buildôÙup of these, the whole stream cannot be admitted
back. Another solution would be an additional cryocooling step where the nitrogen and
70 InôÙsitu resource utilisation for production of rocket fuel for methane oxygen propulsion systems on Mars
==PAGE:79==
argon are liquefied out of the overhead stream from the methane distillation and leaves a
hydrogenôÙrich stream behind to be recycled.
7.3
Local environment
Another question that deserves some thought is the sensitivity of a methalox production
plant to environmental changes. As shown in table 3.1, the Martian atmosphere changes
during a Martian year, and depending on the chosen landing site, the local ambient temôÙ
perature varies wildly ôÙ somewhat similarly to Earth. Changes in atmospheric composition,
specifically in CO2, might warrant minor adjustments to some of the flows in the plant but
should not matter drastically. Changes in temperature will only have effect on the 1st comôÙ
pression stage, where the outlet temperature of the stage will follow the inlet temperature.
At ôÙ70ô¯C input, the output of the first stage is 117ô¯C warm, and at 0ô¯C input, the output
is 221ô¯C warm. Sand loading in the local ice resources will impact the number of trips
needed per kg of ice delivered, which needs to be considered as well.
For later development of a stationary plant, it might be possible to use the local topology to
oneãs advantage. For example, the liquefied storage and radiator setup could be placed
in crater shadows to limit sunlight exposure or to create a well of cold air, thus reducing
boilôÙoff.

8
Conclusion
In this thesis, two examples of a chemical plant architecture which produce liquid methane
and liquid oxygen were presented. One version uses local ice deposits as its hydrogen
source while the other uses hydrogen which has been brought from Earth. Both use
atmospheric CO2 as their carbon source. The feasibility of methane oxygen rocket fuel
production is confirmed in this thesis, and a potential landing site is suggested as well
for the waterôÙice version of the plant, recalling that the hydrogen version could essentially
land anywhere on the planet.
The SpaceX Starship interplanetary launch system is used as a concrete example throughôÙ
out the report to enable the sizing of an example plant, however, the plant size should
scale linearly on all parameters as long as all ratios are kept constant. Based on this,
the production rate of liquid methane and liquid oxygen is determined with some marôÙ
gin, and using the lowerôÙbound of the mass budget of the Starship system, the two plant
versions are evaluated against each other. While the mass of the production plant itself
is unknown, the mass of the liquid hydrogen alone seriously strains the mass budget of
Starship when assuming a lowerôÙbound of 100 ton. The mission complexity of the hydroôÙ
gen plant is assumed to be lower than that of the waterôÙice mining plant, which would be
the benefit that outweighs the mass implications. The hydrogen plant would also not be
sustainable in the sense once it runs out of onboard hydrogen, it loses the capacity to proôÙ
duce methane. Only the waterôÙice plant can sustain a continuous production of methane
and oxygen given it uses inôÙsitu resources for this. Regardless, both plants are intended
as proofôÙofôÙconcept pilot plants, that showcase the possibility of methalox production in a
Martian environment. In the long term, an ISRU approach will need to be adopted, and
this speaks in favour of the waterôÙice plant. Potentially, the waterôÙice plant could stay on
the surface of the planet and be used to produce fuel for other rockets than itself.
In terms of energy, both plant versions consume approximately the same, however, the
hydrogen plant needs to dispose of more heat than the waterôÙice plant. This is mostly due
to less heat being absorbed by the hydrogen evaporation process in the hydrogen plant
compared to the ice evaporation process in the waterôÙice plant. For the hydrogen plant
to produce the correct ratio of methane to oxygen as required by the rocket, atmospheric
CO2 needs to be electrolysed. For both plants, part of their products needs to be discarded
during production to avoid an overproduction of oxygen or methane for the waterôÙice plant
and the hydrogen plant, respectively. The waterôÙice plant will need to discard oxygen
back into the atmosphere, and the hydrogen plant will need to discard a mixture of carbon
dioxide and carbon monoxide into the atmosphere.
Conclusively, both plants seem feasible, but they have each their own difficulties. The
waterôÙice system depends on a yetôÙtoôÙbeôÙdeveloped autonomous fleet of mining robots,
and the hydrogen system is pushing the mass constraints of the Starship launch system.
It is only a matter of time until it becomes public knowledge which of these methods will
be pursued by either a private or governmental space program. Until then, this thesis will
serve as the only holistic work available to the public that details not only some of the inner
workings of a Mars methanation plant, but also covers some of the associated issues with
the supporting systems around such a production facility.
