Abstract 
This thesis provides a comprehensive review of smart city initiatives and related governance issues within 
the transport sector. Making a city ‘smart’ is emerging as a strategy to mitigate the difficulties generated by 
urban population growth, the rapid urbanization and increasing prosperity that create new challenges for 
urban systems and services. Many cities have strategies and visions focusing on smart evolution, but few 
have explicit thoughts on what this actually means or how they can transform into a smart city. The aim of 
this thesis is to clarify the overall definition of smart cities, and investigate existing smart city initiatives 
within the field of transport, and how these initiatives have been planned, evaluated and selected.  
The thesis discovered that there is no universally shared definition of smart cities. Instead, researchers and 
cities have over the last decades developed their own definitions. Many agree that smart cities make use of 
information and communication technology (ICT) to optimize the use of resources and make urban systems 
and services more efficient. However, most definitions also agree that ICT is not the only aspect of smart 
cities, though they disagree on other aspects. Yet, the lack of a shared definition has not stopped 
organizations ranking cities in terms of smartness. This has resulted in the creation of several top ten 
rankings arguing for different cities being the smartest in the world. These rankings were explored, and this 
formed the basis for discussing some of the general challenges that the smart city concept needs to 
overcome to avoid turning into yet another buzz-word. A particular problem in this context is the lack of a 
shared definition of the smart city concept.  
The thesis explore the governance characteristics of smart cities, and of how this aspect has been neglected 
in favour of the technologies related to smart cities. Some argue that the concept is merely born and 
controlled by technology companies seeing this as a marketing opportunity. However, ICT products cannot 
solve deep-rooted urban structural problems and inappropriate habits and norms. To overcome deep-
rooted urban challenges, and to get the most out of ICT products, smart city transformations processes 
require governance considerations and changes. These challenging tasks call for strong leadership, clear 
visions, but most of all, it requires a comprehensive understanding and insight into the local capabilities, 
involving local actors and stakeholders in robust partnerships that develop and exploit their ambitions and 
competences. 
From smart governance characteristics, the thesis moves towards the area of existing smart transportation 
initiatives installed or utilized in urban areas. The use of technologies was a common theme for the smart 
transportation initiatives examined. These initiatives are known as intelligent transport systems (ITS), 
where ITS relates to the use of ICT in transport. In general, ITS solutions collect and process data and use 
communication technologies in vehicles and devices across the transportation infrastructures to optimize 
traffic flow, traffic safety, environment and/or the economic feasibility of the transport system. The thesis 
presents a list of the most well-known ITS applications installed across urban infrastructures within the field 
of road transport (cars, trucks, buses, bikes, pedestrians, etc.), and it presents the initiative’s effect on 
urban society. A comprehensive network of integrated ITS solutions can improve the quality of life for 
citizens in a broad sense, whereas ITS applications directed towards bikes and pedestrians mainly focus on 
traffic safety, while applications directed towards bus traffic mainly focus on economic savings.  
Finally, smart governance and smart transportation are combined to study how ITS projects are planned, 
evaluated and selected in Denmark. The study showed that in Denmark, ITS projects rarely exceed the pilot 
phase, and therefore, professionals within the area express that Denmark suffers from “pilot project 
illness” when it comes to smart city applications. The study has not been able to find any specific 
==PAGE:6==
iv 
 
recommendations or requirements that explicitly stress procedures for how to plan, evaluate and select 
Danish ITS projects. This might be due to the absence of previously completed large-scale ITS projects.  
The extended use of technology compared with traditional transport projects and the requirements for 
increased cooperation between multiple technical disciplines create substantial challenges for managers of 
ITS projects. This has resulted in cost and schedule overruns for several ITS projects around the world. As a 
result, the US Department of Transportation recommends certain ITS projects to follow a specific 
procedure that ensures careful considerations of citizen’s needs and problems, to ensure that the final 
execution and performance accords with the initially defined needs and requirements. The procedure helps 
project managers establish an overview of the final outcomes at an early stage, minimizing the risk of 
schedule and cost overruns. A case study, concerning the development of an ITS project in Copenhagen is 
used to demonstrate how this procedure can be beneficial in Danish settings. 
In summary, the thesis provides a general introduction to the challenges of smart city development. It 
emphasizes how governance structures affect a city’s ability to grow smart, and it presents an overview of 
existing smart initiatives within the transport sector. It argues that governance changes and clear 
procedures directed towards ITS applications can be beneficial for the urban society, even though the 
procedures are more resource demanding in the development phase than traditional transport procedures. 
It is concluded that the procedures recommended in the United States for ITS projects could benefit Danish 
ITS projects with high risk characteristics, such as projects relying on new technologies or projects relying 
on multiple agencies for their implementation and operation. A national ITS architecture is a fundamental 
prerequisite for applying the recommended procedures, and currently Denmark does not have one. The 
national ITS architecture should ensure interoperability of ITS applications between cities and regions, it 
should prioritize ITS projects, and it should outline a framework of standards and best practices.

Abstract (in Danish) 
Dette kandidatspeciale har til formål analysere smarte byudviklingsprojekter (smart cities) og 
styringsprocedurer inden for transportsektoren. Ønsket om at udvikle ’smarte’ byer fremgår af flere 
strategier rundt om i verden. Disse byer håber på, at koncept kan afhjælpe nogle af de vanskeligheder som 
de står overfor i forhold til befolkningsvækst, urbanisering og stigende velstand. På trods af at mange 
strategier og visioner udtrykker et ønske om smart byudvikling, er der ingen der reelt kan redegøre for, 
hvad konceptet betyder, eller hvad der kræves for at udvikle smart byer. Formålet med dette speciale er 
derfor at præcisere definitionen af en smart by, at undersøge hvilke smarte transportløsninger der er 
blevet gennemført i eksisterende byområder, og at undersøge hvordan disse løsninger er planlagt, 
evalueret og udvalgt.  
Gennem specialeforløbet stod det hurtigt klart, at der ikke findes en universel accepteret definition af en 
smart by. I stedet har forskere og forvaltninger i byer rundt om i verden de sidste årtier lavet deres egne 
definitioner af konceptet. Flere af definitionerne peger på, at smarte byer anvender intelligente 
kommunikationsteknologier (IKT) til at optimere ressourceanvendelsen for dermed at gøre byernes 
systemer og tjenester mere effektive. De fleste af definitionerne antyder også, at IKT ikke er det eneste 
aspekt af det smarte byudviklingskoncept. Der er dog ikke en entydig opfattelse af, hvilke andre aspekter 
konceptet omfatter. Manglen på en fælles definition har imidlertid ikke stoppet organisationer fra at 
vurdere, hvor smarte forskellige byer rundt om i verden er. Dette har resulteret i offentliggørelsen af en 
række top-ti ranglister, der argumenterer for, hvilke byer der er de smarteste i verden. Ingen af dem er 
kommet frem til samme konklusion. Dette speciale analyserer ranglisterne, og diskuterer nogle af de 
udfordringer som det smarte bykoncept er nødt til at adressere hvis det ikke skal ende sine dage som 
endnu et buzz-ord. Dette omfatter bl.a. de udfordringer, der er forbundet med ikke at have en fælles 
definition af smarte byer. 
Specialet undersøger styringsmekanismerne bag bykonceptet, og hvordan disse er blevet forsømt til fordel 
for det teknologiske aspekt af en smart by. Nogle forskere hævder, at konceptet udelukkende er styret af 
teknologivirksomheder, der ønsker at markedsføre deres produkter. IKT-produkter kan imidlertid ikke løse 
strukturelle problemer, der er dybt forankrede i byerne, og ej heller gennemtvinge ændringer af 
uhensigtsmæssige vaner og normer. For at overvinde disse udfordringer, og for at få mest muligt ud af IKT-
produkterne, kræves det, at vores styringsmekanismer og ledelsesstrategier optimeres. Disse udfordrende 
opgaver kræver et stærkt lederskab, klare visioner, men mest af alt kræver de en grundlæggende forståelse 
og indsigt i lokale behov og problemer. Dette kan opnås gennem interessentinddragelse. For at adressere 
udfordringerne, er ambitioner og et robust partnerskab mellem forskellige afdelinger i byforvaltningen, 
men også mellem andre byer, lande og virksomheder, en nødvendighed.  
Fra smarte styringsmekanismer blev fokus flyttet over på transportområdet, og på de eksisterende smarte 
transportløsninger, som anvendes i byområder rundtom i verden. Fælles for de smarte transportløsninger, 
der blev undersøgt i specialet, er at de alle anvender avancerede teknologier, og de falder derfor alle ind 
under kategorien intelligente transportsystemer (ITS). ITS er en underkategori af IKT, som anvendes inden 
for transportområdet. Overordnet set indsamler og behandler ITS-løsninger forskellige former for data, 
som kommunikeres mellem køretøjer og enheder på tværs af transportinfrastrukturen, med det formål at 
optimere eller forbedre trafik-flow, trafiksikkerhed, miljø og/eller den økonomiske rentabilitet af transport 
systemet. Specialet præsenter en liste over de mest kendte ITS-systemer, der er installeret på tværs af 
byernes infrastruktur inden for vejområdet (biler, lastbiler, cykler, fodgængere osv.). Listen præsenter 
hvordan disse løsninger hver især påvirker vores bysamfund. Et samlet netværk af integrerede ITS-systemer 
kan forbedre livskvaliteten for byboere i en bred forstand, hvorimod ITS-løsninger rettet mod cyklister og 
==PAGE:8==
vi 
 
fodgængere primært fokuserer på trafiksikkerhed, mens ITS-løsninger rettet mod bustrafik primært 
fokuserer på økonomiske besparelser. 
Endelig undersøges det hvordan ITS projekter planlægges, evalueres og udvælges i Danmark. Studiet viste, 
at de fleste ITS-projekter i Danmark aldrig kommer ud over pilotstadiet. Nogle fagfolk indenfor området 
udtrykker, at Danmark lider af ’pilot-syge’, når det drejer sig om smarte byløsninger. Danmark har ingen 
specifikke anbefalinger eller krav, der udstikker procedurer for planlægningen af ITS projekter. Dette 
skyldes formentlig manglen på udførelse af større ITS-projekter. 
Undersøgelser har vist at de høje teknologiske krav til ITS projekter sammenlignet med almindelige 
transportprojekter, samt de mange krav til tæt samarbejd mellem flere tekniske discipliner, skaber 
udfordringer for projektledere (af ITS projekter). Udfordringer der ofte har medført, at budgetter og 
tidsplaner skrider. Det amerikanske transportministerium anbefaler derfor visse ITS-projekter at følge en 
bestemt procedure, der sikrer omhyggelig overvejelse af borgernes behov og problemer, og som sikrer 
løbende kontrol af processen og beslutningerne. Det drejer sig om procedurer der verificerer, at den 
endelige gennemførelse og ydeevne er i overensstemmelse med de oprindeligt definerede behov og krav. 
Proceduren hjælper projektlederen med at få et overblik over de endelige resultater på et tidligt stadium af 
processen, hvilket minimerer risikoen for at budgetter og tidsplaner skrider. I specialet gennemføres et 
casestudie, omhandlende udviklingen af et ITS-projekt i København, som har til formål at afklare, om den 
amerikanske procedure kan være gavnlig for Danmark.  
Dette speciale giver dermed en generel introduktion til de forskellige udfordringer, der er forbundet med 
smart byudvikling. Specialet beskriver hvordan styringsstrukturer påvirker en bys evne til at udvikle sig til en 
smart by, og det giver et overblik over hvilke smarte transportinitiativer, der er blevet gennemført rundtom 
i verden. Specialet argumenterer for, at styringsændringer og klare procedurer rettet mod ITS kan være 
gavnlige for et bysamfund, også selvom procedurerne er mere ressourcekrævende under udviklingen af 
systemet end traditionelle transportprocesser. På baggrund af et litteratur- og casestudie konkluderes det, 
at de procedurer der anbefales i USA i forbindelse med planlægning og design af ITS-projekter, med fordel 
kan anvendes i Danmark i tilfælde af, at ITS-projekterne opfylder visse højrisikokriterier. Det kan bl.a. være 
projekter, der anvender hidtil uprøvede teknologier, eller projekter, der er afhængige af flere instanser for 
at blive implementeret og drevet på den ønskede måde. Specialet konkluderer dog også, at Danmark 
mangler en vigtig forudsætning for at anvende den amerikanske procedure - en national ITS arkitektur. En 
national ITS arkitektur skal sikre interoperabilitet mellem ITS-løsninger på tværs af byer og regioner. Den 
skal sikre et fælles fodfæste for ITS-projekter, den skal udpege hvilke ITS projekter der bør prioriteres og 
udvælges, og den skal skitsere rammer og standarder for, hvordan ITS projekter skal udføres.

1 Introduction 
During the past 50 years, cities and their populations have increased significantly in size in all parts of the 
world. Scientists predict that by 2050 70 % of the world population will live in cities. The size of the 
population makes cities a place of immense opportunities. It creates the chance for people to meet and 
learn from one another, it creates opportunities for companies to grow, and it attract experts to the area, 
which also have a positive impact on the educational system. However, increasing urbanization also poses 
new challenges to the structure of cities. The traffic congestion increases, the pollution increases, and so 
does waste production along with costs of living. At the same time, the consequences of cyberattacks and 
terrorism become immeasurable (Benevolo et al. 2016). 
In 2015, 190 world leaders got together to create 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) to overcome 
the global challenges, and in cooperation create a more prosperous, equitable and sustainable world 
(GlobalLiving 2015). The 17 goals are shown in Figure 1. The SDGs creates a broader view of sustainability 
than previously achieved by covering aspects such as energy, economy, education, innovation, climate, and 
production.  
 
Figure 1 - The 17 global Sustainable Development Goals (illustration from GlobalLiving (2015)) 
The SDG number 11 emphasizes the development of sustainable cities and communities with living 
environments and public health. It focuses on the creation of well-planned urban environments with 
effective transport systems and reliable municipal services, with increased attention to traffic safety and 
accessibility to green and safe public spaces (PwC 2016). It aims at improving the attractiveness and 
competitiveness of cities by engaging in initiatives to improve urban infrastructure and facilities, and to 
improve environmental, social and economic conditions. These efforts and ideas were actually brought up 
by the concept of intelligent cities in the late 1990s (Neirotti et al. 2014), which was the predecessor of 
smart cities (Trindade et al. 2017). 
Smart cities are often seen as a tool to overcome urban challenges in a progressively urbanised world. It is 
an umbrella concept that contains quite a lot of subthemes such as urbanism, economy, environment, 
==PAGE:14==
2 
 
sustainability, mobility, and governance. The many subthemes creates confusion concerning the concept of 
smart cities. Cities use the concept in different ways to cover their unique needs and contextual conditions. 
This has created a lack of consensus as to the definition of smart cities. It is widely understood and 
accepted as a concept making benefit of information and communication technologies (ICT) to help cities 
make better use of resources and create a competitive advantage. Hence, it does not only cover elements 
from the SDG 11, but it also contains elements from other SDGs such as goal number 9 emphasising on 
innovation and infrastructure.  
ICT uses a variety of information technologies for continuous data collection and communication between 
different components, systems and people. They utilize internet-based features, or other means of 
technologies such as sensors, satellites or radio-waves (Neirotti et al. 2014). ICT is used in many sectors to 
optimize the way we are living. The latest Climate Action in Megacities report found that more than half of 
the climate actions taken by cities came from the ICT sector, indicating an increasing interest in the smart 
city agenda (Cosgrave & Doody 2014). 
Innovation, digitalization, automation, and ICT have in the past 10-20 years revolutionized the transport 
sector by the use of intelligent transport systems (ITS), which is a category of ICT concentrating on 
transportation (Kristensen et al. 2018). At the same time, the always-on culture with smartphones and 
applications have created a new way of communicating, organizing and buying goods. The urban 
transformations and technological developments require new business models. Business models that have 
the capacity to respond to, and to some degree lead, the technological and social changes that are 
currently happening, and help shape the sustainable, healthy and recreational urban areas that we desire.  
Cities around the world have already put up strategies indicating their ambitions to develop into a smart 
city, but with the confusion of the concept of smart cities, the target might be difficult to achieve for most 
cities. There is in all case clear focus on the fast evolving technological dimension, leaving the political and 
social dimension of smart cities behind. However, cities will only be truly smart when the benefits and 
opportunities they offer can be implemented, controlled, organized, and mobilized to everyone (Cosgrave 
& Doody 2014), and when the city addresses deep-rooted structural problems and needs (Kitchin 2014).  
Based on a literature study, this thesis will explore the definitions of smart cities and a selection of 
associated conceptual models. Not having a shared definition of the concept has created considerable 
confusion for urban planners and city administrations when forming new strategies and deciding which 
initiatives to support. This is not only caused by the different perceptions of the concept, it is also related to 
the many different rankings arguing for different cities being the smartest in the world. Thus, a benchmark 
is needed to ensure a sustainable development of city strategies. The thesis explores literature concerning 
smart cities, and examine if the world’s smartest cities also are the ones with the smartest governance. It 
discusses some of the general challenges that the smart city concept needs to overcome, and the overall 
characteristics of smart governance. Furthermore, the thesis explores the transport aspect of smart cities. It 
describes the concept of ITS, and it addresses the problem of not having catalogues of ITS initiatives by 
presenting a list of the most well-known ITS solutions and their impact on the environment, society and 
economy. Based on the smart governance characteristics and the current actions in the smart mobility 
area, the governance aspect within the transport sector is investigated. How are ITS projects planned, 
evaluated, selected and implemented in Denmark? Are there any regulations or best practices on the 
matter? And should different procedures than the traditional ones be implemented when evaluating and 
designing ITS solutions? These are some of the questions that the thesis tries to answer.

1.1 Scope 
Smart cities, sustainable cities and smart sustainable cities are all popular phrases in urban planning and 
city governance, and several academic papers (Bibri & Krogstie 2017; Kramers et al. 2014; Trindade et al. 
2017; Pinna et al. 2017) explore the difference between these concepts. This thesis is limited to smart city 
initiatives, and it will not explicitly explore the sustainability aspects of solutions and interventions. It will 
not consider if certain technologies, solutions and/or interventions fulfil the requirements of being 
sustainable. Furthermore, the last part of the thesis is limited to the domain of transport within the road 
sector, and it concentrates on technologies within city transport.  
1.2 Structure of the report 
The report consists of nine chapters, structured so as to reflect and address the above presented problems. 
The nine chapters are divided into three different themes as illustrated in Figure 2. The first theme focuses 
on the overall concepts of smart cities and governance structures. The second theme is about smart 
mobility and the transport aspect of smart cities. The third theme combines the previous two. It reflects on 
the transport aspect in relation to smart governance structures and procedures, and this is explored in a 
case study. Figure 2 illustrates the themes by colours, and it illustrate interactions between chapters in the 
report with arrows. The content of each chapter is specified below. 
 
Figure 2 – Structure of the thesis 
Chapter 1 introduces the thesis and presents the overall purpose and outline of the report.  
Chapter 2 reports the results of a literature study on smart cities. A selection of definitions and conceptual 
models of smart cities are described and compared for illustration of the complexity and inconsistency 
inherent in the concept. It presents the most recent rankings of the world’s smartest cities, and it describes 
how some of the ranked cities have achieved their grade. Finally, it summarizes the key challenges that the 
==PAGE:16==
4 
 
smart city concept needs to overcome to avoid turning into yet another overused buzz-word without 
substance. 
Chapter 3 describes the overall concept of smart governance, and it defines seven steps that the smart city 
council should follow before and after the development and investment in smart city projects. 
Furthermore, it focuses on five elementary efforts that needs attention when implementing smart cities. 
Chapter 4 defines smart mobility and ITS. It presents and categorizes existing technologies used in ITS 
applications. 
Chapter 5 describes how the technologies are combined in different ways and attached to a variety of 
transport elements. It presents a list of existing, well-known ITS applications. Each ITS application is 
described, and their impact on the environment, economy, and social life is evaluated.  
Chapter 6 presents the existing Danish procedures for planning and evaluating transport projects. It 
describes how the traditional way of governing transport projects might not be ideal for ITS projects. It 
looks to the United States of America for inspiration on different ways of planning and evaluating ITS 
projects. Based on the concept of system engineering, the chapter presents an proposed ideal conceptual 
framework for ITS projects.  
Chapter 7 tests the conceptual framework from Chapter 6 and the governance steps from Chapter 3 in a 
case study focusing on an ITS application from Chapter 5 that uses technologies from Chapter 4. The 
theoretical case study focuses on the governance processes and functionalities. The intention is to 
demonstrate governance concepts and underline why traditional transport methods might be insufficient 
for ITS projects. 
Chapter 8 discusses the benefits, drawbacks and limitations of the findings, including issues related to 
previously presented results and analyses. 
Chapter 9 presents the findings, conclusions and main contributions of the thesis, and it presents 
recommendations for future research.

2 Smart cities 
‘Smart city’ is a notion that frequently has appeared in the debate during the past decade as one of the 
answers to the growing problems and concerns of urban living, highly related to the urbanization trend. 
Underpinning the notion is a multitude of old, radical, and new concepts and ideas of how to utilize data 
and information to improve the quality of life in cities and communities worldwide (Palmer 2014). This 
chapter introduces the overall evolution of the concept of smart cities, and it presents a selection of the 
definitions and conceptual models of smart cities, and compares these in order to illustrate the complexity 
and inconsistency inherent in the concept. 
In general, smart cities are understood as cities that make use of information and communication 
technology (ICT) to help cities build their competitive advantage, e.g. by using big data or traffic 
management, energy consumption statistics, security, optimisation, and customer services (Trindade et al. 
2017; Kramers et al. 2014). ICT systems can briefly be described as systems that ‘sense and act’, and ensure 
that excessive volumes of real-time data is processed, communicated and integrated across other 
processes, systems or operations in order to inform authorities of a problem or to optimise systems or 
processes (Neirotti et al. 2014). However, according to Neirotti et al. (2014) the deployment of ICT should 
not be branded as the only element of smart cities - investments in human capital and changes in urban 
living conditions are equally important. Smart cities are often envisioned as a development trend to follow 
when creating a better and more sustainable city where the quality of life is high, the environment is 
healthy and the traffic runs smoothly. The increased attention on smart cities makes it appear as a new 
paradigm of intelligent urban development and sustainable socio-economic growth (Neirotti et al. 2014). 
2.1 The evolution of smart cities 
The concept of smart cities is relatively new (Trindade et al. 2017), though the notion was first introduced 
decades ago when Tatsuno used it as a metaphor for ‘intelligent cities’ in Gibson et al. (1992). An intelligent 
city, also referred to as an informational city, was first introduced by Batty in 1990 (Marsal-Llacuna & Segal 
2016). According to Gabrys (2014), the concept of smart cities can even be traced back to ideas and 
discussions in the 1960s about informational or cybernetically planned cities with autonomous cars, and to 
the proposals for networked or computable city plans from the 1980s. Common for the intelligent, 
informational, and computable cities are their focus on ICT as the key driver for delivering services and 
information to the public, city administration and businesses. Thus, intelligent cities, informational cities, 
cyber cities, computable cities, networked cities, digital cities, knowledge cities, etc. are the predecessors of 
smart cities (Lee et al. 2014; Bibri & Krogstie 2017; Trindade et al. 2017).  
Since the first introduction of the smart city concept, the notion has been used differently depending on 
the context. Some smart cities represent green cities, but for others it means improved services, more jobs 
or more efficient transportation (Palmer 2014). 
In association with economy, a smart city has been defined as a city focusing on smart industry and growth 
using ICT. Economists defines a smart city as a city that could attract human capital and activate capital by 
the use of ICT (Anthopoulos 2017). In the aspect of education, a smart city would host smart inhabitants, 
and it would be a city focusing on schooling and the level of educational grades. In other literature, the 
term refers to city governance and the smartness of the administration. In this context, a smart city uses 
new channels of communications to reach the citizens, e.g. e-governance1 or e-democracy2. In the

After the recent adaptation of the concept in city planning (Batty et al. 2012), resulting in an extensive 
number of publications about smart cities peaking in 2015 (Trindade et al. 2017), the concept has evolved 
to be more holistic, and it now goes beyond the definitions of intelligent cities, informational cities, 
knowledge cities, etc.  (Trindade et al. 2017; Bibri & Krogstie 2017). Today, a smart city is an umbrella 
concept that contains many subthemes such as urbanism, economy, environment, technology, energy, 
mobility and health (Trindade et al. 2017).   
2.2 Definitions and conceptual models 
Even though the smart city concept is widely used in many sectors, it still has no shared or definite 
definition (Trindade et al. 2017; Bibri & Krogstie 2017; Angelidou 2015; Fernandez-Anez et al. 2017) nor a 
one-size-fits-all definition (Nam & Pardo 2011). Instead, a wide range of definitions are available (Bibri & 
Krogstie 2017). ISO TMB Smart Cities Strategic Advisory Group has made an attempt to develop an 
internationally standardized definition of smart cities covering most city functions. The definition is not yet 
complete (ISO/IEC 2014; Deloitte 2015), and other definitions are at the time of writing still being 
formulated. Table 1 presents a selection of the many definitions of smart cities.

The definitions focus on various elements from the importance of technology for the creation of economic 
growth, to environmental concerns and improved quality of life, and they have been formulated by both 
scholars, governmental and non-governmental institutions. Based on the definitions, several conceptual 
approaches have been developed, all resulting in different interpretations of initiatives and developments 
(De Andrade Guerra et al. 2016; Anthopoulos 2017). 
The most cited definition, defined by Caragliu et al. (2009) and inspired by Giffinger et al. (2007), focuses on 
six axes (or distinct dimensions) founded on theories of regional competitiveness. The axes are: smart 
economy, smart governance, smart people, smart mobility, smart living, and smart environment. Together 
they represent a conceptual model for analysing and assessing smart city performances, which is shown in 
Table 2 (taken directly from Giffinger et al. 2007). The model has been highly used e.g. by Helfert et al. 
(2015) and United Nations Commission on Science and Technology for Development (2016). Smart 
Economy covers elements such as innovation, entrepreneurship and productivity. Smart governance 
includes participation in decision making, strategy formulation and perspectives, transparency, and social 
services. The Smart people domain covers e.g. lifelong learning, creativity, and open-mindedness. Smart 
mobility includes elements such as accessibility, ICT-infrastructure, traffic safety and sustainablity. The 
smart living (or quality of life) domain covers culture, health, housing quality, education, and safety. Finally, 
smart environment focuses on the attractivity of natural conditions, pollution, protection, and sustainable 
resouce management.

Lee et al. (2014) and Nam & Pardo (2011) argue that the concept embraces three core factors, creating a 
simple conceptual model for smart cities: Technology (infrastructures of hardware and software), People 
(creativity, diversity, education), and Institutions (governance and policy). According to Nam & Pardo 
(2011), the core factors embrace several of the previously used phrases of cities that have been used as 
synonyms for smart cities. The technological dimension covers the phrases: digital city, intelligent city, 
ubiquitous city, wired city, hybrid city and information city. The people dimension covers the phrases: 
creative city, learning city, human city and knowledge city. Finally, the institutional dimension covers the 
phrases: smart community and smart growth. One could argue that this approach defines the actors and 
activities generating smartness, whereas Anthopoulos (2017) defines the services delivered by smart cities.

Overall, there seems to be consensus that smart cities are characterised by the use of ICT to help cities 
make better use of resources, while in the meantime creating a more efficient and productive system that 
will generate economic growth. ICT is important in a smart city context, but it cannot stand-alone. 
According to most definitions, it should be seen as complementary to investments in social and 
==PAGE:22==
10 
 
environmental sustainability that will lead to increased quality of life for citizens. Furthermore, it is obvious 
that the city governance plays a central role in paving the way for cities to become smarter. ICT can help 
powering decision-making, planning and control activities, and therefore, smart governance is an important 
tool for the smart city.  
Even though it is not explicitly stated in the definitions, it is commonly acknowledged that a smart city is to 
optimise the use of both physical facilities (transport infrastructures, energy networks, pollution controls, 
waste management systems, etc.) and intangible assets (social health and welfare, culture, human and 
intellectual capital, etc.) (Neirotti et al. 2014). For the physical activities, ICT is a key player to attain the 
desired improvements, along with introductions of suitable policy interventions and deliberate urban 
planning. For the intangible assets, ICT has a more limited role, and in this domain, it is often unnecessary 
to process and integrate real-time data (Neirotti et al. 2014).

Ranking the smartest cities in the world has been a popular activity among several organizations for the 
past years, and different cities are often elected as the smartest. This causes confusion as regards to which 
city to use as benchmark for development strategies in other cities.

2.3.1 Copenhagen 
According to the ranking by the organisation Easy Park in 2017, the news website Business Insider in 2017, 
the American business magazine Fast Company in 2014, and the European project partnership Cross 
Innovation in 2013, Copenhagen deserves a top spot in the lists of the smartest city in the world or in 
Europe (Easy Park 2017; Garfield 2017; Cohen 2014; Cross Innovation 2013). In 2014, the city was also 
rewarded the greenest city in Europe by Siemens (Siemens 2014) 3, and the same year, it won the 
prestigious World Smart Cities Award for best smart city projects for the initiatives: Connecting 
Copenhagen and The Copenhagen Climate Plan (Jakobsen 2014). So far the city has focused on 
environmental issues related to the ambitious carbon reduction plan. The smart city initiatives are highly 
concerned about energy efficiency and renewable objectives, building standards, but also the widespread 
bike culture and the smart urban laboratory (Copenhagen Solution Lab). The latter tests a variety of 
innovative projects. Furthermore, Copenhagen (and Denmark in general) has a leading position in 
technologies such as smart grid, wind turbines, and the digital infrastructure (especially focusing on bikes) 
is well developed. However, several barriers are still in the way for further smart growth. These are 
especially linked to the governance of the city. The city could benefit from a closer partnership with the 
private sector (Copenhagen Cleantech Cluster 2013), a closer network and more cooperation between 
Danish municipalities (Arup & CEDI 2016), and they need a national strategy for the smart city development 
(Jung et al. 2016). See further discussions in Chapter 3.4.1. 
2.3.2 Barcelona 
Barcelona was an early player in the smart city transformation, and despite the downturn during the 
financial crisis, Barcelona managed to maintain its position as one of the smartest European cities (Bakici et 
al. 2012). According to the rankings, Barcelona has been outpaced by many other cities since its peak in 
2015 and 2016. Barcelona was one of the first cities to test e-mobility and bike-sharing projects, and it is 
now an urban laboratory for piloting projects and services intending to make the city more open, efficient 
and friendly, and attract local and international entrepreneurs to the city (European Commission 2017a). 
The city has noise and air quality sensors along major roads, they have smart streetlights, smart parking 
facilities, and e-government solutions (Ferrer 2017). However, many of the smart initiatives in Barcelona 
are directed towards tourists, and citizens do not have the impression of living in a smart city. This has

2.3.3 Smart city development outside Europe 
European cities were front-runners at the start of smart development, but cities in the United States and 
Asia are now catching up with the leading European cities, and to some extent they have overtaken cities in 
Europe. New York, Singapore, and San Francisco are high on the lists of smartest cities in the world. New 
York has some of the best car sharing services, education facilities, Wi-Fi hotspots, innovative economies 
and high internet speeds. San Francisco also has some of the best car sharing services, and high internet 
speed. Besides that, the city is a leader in smart parking, they have many public charging spaces for electric 
vehicles, and it is awarded as one of the best cities to live in (Luciano 2017). The high density, island city-
state, Singapore, has during the past two years raised through the ranks of smart cities in the world after 
the introduction of their ‘Smart Nation’ program aiming at pushing the city and the government into the 
digital age. It is now on the top of the lists due to its flourishing business environment, urban planning 
ambitions, high internet quality and speed, their quality of public transport, and their efforts towards clean 
energy (Hynes 2017).  
A study made by Government Technology, an American magazine, shows that Asia is most likely to emerge 
as the leading global region for smart city development within few years (Power 2017). Currently, Asia is 
transforming rapidly towards top positions of smart city rankings due to the many governmental 
investments. In India, about 100 new smart cities are developed from scratch and about 500 urban areas 
are converting to smart city, and in China, 285 smart city related projects are planned (Power 2017; Smart 
City Hub 2017). In the United Arab Emirates, new smart and sustainable cities are flourishing as well. One of 
them is Masdar City just outside of Abu Dhabi. This city goal is to be the first zero carbon city in the world, 
using technology to improve the environmental quality and deliver better services for its citizens. The focus 
is on high quality of life, energy efficiency, water management, CO2 emission reductions and waste 
management. Internet of Things (IoT) is a reality in Masdar, where subsystems related to the environment, 
transport system, security, governance, and public utilities as power, water, gas, toilets and educational 
systems are connected to the internet (Madakam & Ramaswamy 2016).  
2.4 Key challenges facing the smart city development 
The digital technology evolution stands as the primary driver of smart city transformations. Sometimes, 
however, the wheels on the technological train runs so fast that it leaves several stakeholders behind. The 
technological development cannot control the evolution by itself; it is dependent on political agreements, 
ethical considerations, and international communication and determination. 
The smart city development is associated with high complexity as an endless row of elements comes into 
play, creating a smart urban development that pulls in different directions. There are several major 
obstacles to the realisation of smart cities, including the development of an interdisciplinary conceptual 
model and a shared definition of smart cities, creation of international comparative knowledge about smart 
cities, research on the social and political consequences when implementing smart city solutions, 
investigations on how to avoid a hackable city, and how to prevent a culture of control. Other issues are the 
financial challenges that force cities to use their resources on basic services rather than innovative, 
untested solutions, and challenges concerning uncontrolled development and implementation of smart 
technologies as technology companies often fail to collaborate with city planners (Osborne Clarke 2015). 
The following discusses the challenges and consequences of having multiple definitions and

conceptualizations of smart cities, the importance of knowledge sharing and extended focus on smart 
governance, on how to avoid a hackable city and prevent a culture of control. 
2.4.1 Lack of a shared definition and conceptual models, and the need for international 
knowledge sharing and benchmark studies 
According to Neirotti et al. (2014), the lack of a shared definition is caused by the diffusion of smart city 
initiatives happening in countries that all have different needs and contextual conditions, which makes it 
difficult to identify global common trends. The general perception of smart cities is only based on single city 
case studies, and it is fragmented along isolated disciplinary lines, which means that we only have limited 
understanding of the opportunities, challenges, and implications connected with smart development 
(Luque-Ayala & Marvin 2015). Lee et al. (2014) state: “despite the rapid growth of smart city development, 
few academic studies address smart cities from a holistic or typological viewpoint.” Madner et al. (2012) 
supports this statement: “at first glance, the term smart city is a catchy phrase that people like to use as a 
label to emphasise innovation, sustainability and competitiveness […] A look at the relevant literature 
reinforces the impression that there is no clear explanation of the smart city as a term or as a concept. 
We may within a few decades not need a definition of the concept when the ‘smart-parts’ are just natural 
parts of a city (Brynskov 2017), but until then it is essential to have a shared idea, and especially common 
standards, to act from. The different perceptions of the concept, the many ideas of how to obtain a smart 
city, and the shallow, unstructured and inadequately maintained catalogues of initiatives makes it difficult 
for politicians to find out where and how to begin their smart city interventions (Cledou et al. 2017). At the 
same time, mayors continue to compete to declare that their city is ‘smarter’ than other cities (Palmer 
2014), and organizations keep ranking cities according to which are the smartest in the world (see Table 6 
and 7). The claims and rankings complicate matters because who can prove their statement when there is 
no final definition of the concept, and which cities should then inspire us? Without a clear definition comes 
also a risk of politicians and other stakeholders getting confused and despaired over yet another ‘empty’ 
buzz-word that suddenly embrace most of the new technology and development. Yet, developments and 
improvements of procedures are not necessarily linked to smart cities (Dienhart & Seeberg 2016), but 
nobody really knows what should fall into the box labelled ‘smart city’. The fast evolving digital evolution 
has also given birth to the feeling that smart cities are a techno-utopian progress fantasized by technology 
companies marketing smart products (Cosgrave & Doody 2014). 
Currently, start-up companies with a good idea about how to improve procedures in a city find it incredible 
difficult to get hold of needed data. Almost any utility-company, local authority, or organisation can be in 
possession of the data (or maybe no one has yet managed to get hold on the required information). A 
company might need funds to start an initiative, but external investors or the mayor can easily be reluctant 
in aiding when the outcome is uncertain. At the same time, citizens raise questions about data security and 
privacy (Palmer 2014; Osborne Clarke 2015). This is not a tenable situation and it means that nobody dare 
take the first big step (Brynskov 2017). Thus, it is clear that there is a need for standards and basic 
frameworks that can help all involved and give some best practice examples (Palmer 2014). 
Obtaining a shared definition of a smart city, or at least developing a common understanding, is essential to 
move the discussion and research forward unaffected by the biased needs of technology companies. A 
common understanding or a shared definition will allow researchers, industries, organisations, 
governments, etc. to make more comparative evaluations supported by the same conceptual 
understandings. Hopefully this will provide a more homogeneous picture when attempts are made to rank 
the smartest cities and thereby provide benchmarks for other cities to follow. It will also allow for a shared 
focus, which will ease the confusion of the concept, permit a more goal-oriented development, and 
==PAGE:26==
14 
 
hopefully the concept will not die out as an overused buzz-word (Cosgrave & Doody 2014). This important 
task is needed in order for people to really know what the concept can offer, innovators can explain the 
benefits of their ideas, and city authorities can spend public funds with confidence in the outcome (Palmer 
2014).  
Many city governments have realised that there is an opportunity around being smart, but they are not 
sure what it means for them. They need to understand what they are investing in, and what smart 
technologies can do for their city (Cosgrave & Doody 2014). The important work of creating a shared 
understanding and framework for smart city development has slowly started. A UN inter-agency team is 
currently developing comprehensive Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) for smart cities, that they hope to 
turn into a Global Smart Sustainable Cities Index (United Nations Commission on Science and Technology 
for Development 2016). ISO standards are made for how to use different ICT technologies, and in the 
United States, they have regulations for the planning process. However, many standards, regulations, basic 
frameworks, etc. are not international acknowledged, making the situation bewildering. For a reliable, 
homogeneous, and less confusing evolution of smart city developments, it is essential to ensure 
international knowledge sharing, to agree on international standards, and by the use of comparison 
studies, to find good and bad practices to ensure that other cities do not make the same mistakes (Luque-
Ayala & Marvin 2015). 
2.4.2 Politics of the implementation of smart cities 
Most research focus on technology, engineering science and economy in the context of smart city 
development. Researchers focus on solving current problems using ICT, and on achieving optimal outcomes 
under certain technical and market conditions (Batty et al. 2012; Luque-Ayala & Marvin 2015). However, 
several scholars are now beginning to question the problem-solving oriented focus, and instead calling for 
increased focus on political and social dimensions of smart cities (Luque-Ayala & Marvin 2015); dimensions 
which according to most definitions and conceptual models of smart city are inevitable elements of the 
concept.  
When talking about smart governance, many refer to a transformation of city governance that uses 
information and analytic software to measure, monitor and address city problems (Kitchin 2014). With this 
comes the assumption that if only we were able to define more parameters and measure them all, we 
could predict and respond perfectly (Haque 2012), and by having enough algorithm processes, we could 
ensure a city governance that always make rational, logical, and impartial decisions (Kitchin 2014). Should 
concerns arrive anyway, the politicians may want to say “It’s not me, it’s the data” (Haque 2012). The 
technocratic type of governance is reductionistic and way too narrow in scope to handle the many 
governance tasks, as technological solutions on their own cannot solve deep-rooted structural difficulties as 
they rarely address their root cause. The root causes are often associated with human habits, norms, and 
ethical persuasions that need to be handled politically, usually over a long time. Thus, big data, real time 
analytics and ICT solutions are not necessarily the only approach to address the development of smart 
cities. 
In general, there is a need for more examinations of the social and political implications of the smart 
technology implementations, and of how specific urban conditions, socially or politically, can affect smart 
city developments (Luque-Ayala & Marvin 2015). There is also a need for an increased realisation of 
technological development not being the only driver of smart cities, but that the foundation of cities - the 
governance structures - are equally important to realise the ambitions of smart cities. The next chapter will 
focus on the governance issues that should be addressed before realising ICT ideas and other smart 
initiatives. 
==PAGE:27==
15 
 
2.4.3 Data security  
With the increased use of technologies, data collection, databases, and online platforms, comes the 
unavoidable risk of hacking and system lock-down (Arup & CEDI 2016). The use of technologies can make 
cities extremely vulnerable. For example, if the software used to control the metro system crashes, the 
trains cannot run, and if the supermarket’s checkout tills fails, customers cannot shop their goods. Even 
worse was the cyber-attack in Israel in 2012, were the traffic management system was hacked causing 
traffic jams for hours (Kitchin 2014). When more and more ICT networks are placed on top of existing 
systems, and we at the same time are removing analogue alternatives, we create cities that are exposed to 
viruses, crashes, and security hacks. Furthermore, many cities do not have a clear cybersecurity leadership, 
which makes professionals claim that it is just a matter of time before the first city crash, and the question 
will then be when smart cities fail (Peters 2015; Townsend 2013).  
2.4.4 The society of surveillance and dataveillance 
The digital technologies increase the concern over the growing level of surveillance, which in many cities 
makes it possible to track individual’s actions, interactions and transactions at work, travel, and shopping. 
The culture of control, or the big brother society, with surveillance and dataveillance that together ensure 
that citizen’s life are captured and never forgotten, is a scenario that most people fear. It is therefore 
essential to discuss how to balance the benefits of data collection and analytics with individual privacy 
rights in order to maintain trust in governments and in the smart city development.

3 Smart governance 
City governments are faced with several daily challenges. They have to ensure economic return on their 
investments, consider how to communicate and involve stakeholders, decipher funding options, and 
support public welfare. On top of this, they must respect the changing environment in which they operate, 
as the government should reflect and be responsive to the changes in patterns and behaviours of the 
overall population. They need to react with integrity to the shifting environment and consider when the 
citizen’s needs can no longer be served by traditional means. If the city governance does not respond on 
the changing context, they risk being unable to effectively deliver on their responsibilities of ensuring 
economic return and appropriate public welfare. These challenging tasks call for strong leadership, clear 
visions, but most of all, it requires a comprehensive understanding and insight into the local capabilities 
using stakeholder involvement, ambitions and a robust partnership across the city and beyond (Cosgrave & 
Doody 2014).   
According to Cosgrave & Doody (2014) smart governance “is a means to achieve a vision, rather than the 
vision itself”. They highlight that smart cities, and smart governance, are not utopian dreams or political 
ideas about the ideal future civilisations and their way of living, and it is not about buying state-of-the-art 
technologies to look flashy. It is about identifying and understanding the evident movements, challenges, 
and opportunities that ICT and other smart city ideas can be used for, that create a more efficient city with 
increased quality of life for its citizens.  
This chapter presents the different interpretations of smart governance. Based on one of the 
interpretations, the chapter presents an overall smart governance model with key aspects that should be 
considered when creating smart governance. The chapter discusses governance issues related to the 
government of Copenhagen that smoulder behind the scenes of the apparent success of Copenhagen, and 
it presents the governance procedures that have led to some success stories in Barcelona.    
3.1 The various interpretations of smart governance 
The fragmented understanding of smart cities explained in Chapter 2 is reproduced in the interpretation of 
smart governance. In an extensive literature review of smart governance, Meijer & Bolívar (2016) arrive at 
four different conceptualizations of smart governance:  
1. Government of a smart city, 
2. Smart decision-making,  
3. Smart administration, 
4. Smart urban collaboration. 
The first conceptualization suggests that smart governance is simply the governance of a smart city, and 
thus, the job of a smart governance is to make the right policy choices and to implement these in an 
effective and efficient way. The second conceptualization underlines the need to rearrange decision-
making processes and the process by which decisions are implemented (or not implemented), and it 
emphasizes that technologies and the increased collection of data can be used to strengthen the rationale 
behind decisions, and make the decision-making process more accessible. The third conceptualization 
stresses that smart governance is a new arrangement of electronic governance that utilizes ICT to increase 
the service level for citizens, e.g. using e-governance. Finally, the fourth conceptualization focuses on urban 
collaboration between the many actors in the city, e.g. across departments and with communities. The 
literature review showed that there is a dominant acceptance of smart governance being connected with 
the fourth conceptualization: Smart urban collaboration, which is also the interpretation that requires the 
highest level of transformation of the existing governance structure as it not only involves transformation 
==PAGE:29==
17 
 
of the internal organization but also the outside society (Meijer & Bolívar 2016). This report focuses on the 
Smart urban collaboration interpretation of smart governance, though it also touches upon aspects from 
the other conceptualizations.  
3.2 The 7 steps of a smart governance 
Different governance models exists for smart city operations. Overall, these stands out as using either a 
bottom-up or a top-down approach. The bottom-up approach is based on the local needs, behaviours, and 
priorities, where the needs and remarks from citizen’s feeds the future processes, and these are essential 
for deciding on problems and solutions. With this approach, the solutions can be assisted by technology, 
but the implementation of new ICT applications is not decided in advance. In the top-down approach, large 
software vendors often offer off-the-shelf systems that are not necessarily based on specific local needs, 
but which can be implemented in the hope of creating a good business and create an impression of smart 
development. In practice, the latter can seem appealing to a local government with expertise and resource 
limitations. The two approaches basically highlight the difference between ‘being’ a smart city and ‘buying’ 
a smart city (Hilton & Marsh 2017). 
Based on a research of governance challenges in cities around the world4, Cosgrave & Doody (2014) have 
suggested seven principles that the city council of a smart city should follow before and/or after 
development and investment in smart city projects (Figure 3). The seven steps follows a bottom-up 
approach and they are based on the smart urban collaboration conceptualization model by Meijer & Bolívar 
(2016). Note that it is not expected that cities should follow the steps as a linear methodology but use 
these in an iterative approach.  
 
Figure 3 – The 7 principles of governing a smart city (Cosgrave & Doody 2014) 
3.2.1 Clarify the opportunities 
Before deciding on projects to invest in, it is necessary to understand the city and its surroundings. All cities 
have different priorities, needs, and social and economic drivers, and thus, smart solutions and 
technologies should not play the same role in all cities. To make the right investments, it is important to 
clarify the drivers of change, and to identify how ICT can contribute to the movement. Here, the strengths, 
weaknesses, opportunities, and threats for the city should be clarified in a SWOT analysis, and the planned 
activities and projects should be mapped to avoid undesirable interferences and overlaps with other 
projects. Furthermore, it is important to understand the value proposition (short and long term values) for 
both citizens and politicians to clarify the city’s needs and desires.  
3.2.2 Take ownership 
The value proposition, the SWOT analysis, and the drivers of change are translated into a smart city vision. 
A strategy and programme for how to obtain the vision is created in cooperation with the relevant 
departments in the city administration and relevant city stakeholders (see Chapter 3.3.3). The vision and 
strategy lay out the purpose and expectations of the investments in a clear and simple way, which can be 
                                                          
4 Based on the following cities: Barcelona, Boston, Bristol, Chicago, Hong Kong, London, Rio de Janeiro and Stockholm 
==PAGE:30==
18 
 
directly used for investment decisions. This will act as commitment for the citizens, and it will provide some 
level of security to local businesses considering development of ICT applications. Finally, it is important to 
ensure broad political endorsement in the vision and leadership. Without adequate endorsement and 
leadership, the development will be slow and incremental as the willingness to invest resources will be 
lacking. 
3.2.3 Engage 
Ensuring full engagement and endorsement from the municipality and city stakeholders (see Chapter 3.3.3) 
is essential, partly to make sure that the interventions always reflect the needs, and partly to ensure that 
the visions can be financed. To overcome this challenge, the city council should consider mechanisms that 
continually consider funding opportunities, look for advice and support, and conduct workshops with the 
relevant departments in the city administration. Mechanisms that repeatedly ensure full understanding of 
needs, roles, partnerships, and capabilities of city stakeholders are equally important.  
3.2.4 Prepare 
This step focuses on the preparation of financial agreements, procurement strategies, and the acquisition 
of the needed human skills. In this step, the city council develops a pre-commercial model that, among 
other things, specifies: the engagement with suppliers, how to avoid vendor lock-in, organisational 
structures, budgets and financial options such as PPPs, internal investment, or external funding. The council 
should consider if they have the needed human resources in the municipality. They may also employ new 
staff or train existing employees to understand the technical and strategic effects of ICT and additional 
smart programmes.  
3.2.5 Act 
The city council should now get an understanding of the scale and potential of possible projects and 
programmes, and then invest and implement the best projects and programmes. It should also invest in 
long term local community capabilities to ensure that the local government can deal with the future 
challenges, e.g. by encourage training of employees and consider organisational programmes and 
structural changes that support the capacity and development of the smart city. 
3.2.6 Check and reflect 
By the use of both qualitative and quantitative measurement systems, the progress, impacts and operation 
of projects and programmes should be checked at various stages throughout the lifecycle to verify that the 
delivered projects perform desirably. This might require partnership with external advisors or universities 
specialised in evaluating projects in terms of technology, and their social and economic impacts.    
3.2.7 Repeat and share learning 
The outcomes, learnings, and the critical reflections throughout the process need to be fed into the 
planning cycle of future processes to support continuous improvement. It is therefore important to share 
reflections and learnings within the council on a regular basis, report it and distribute it to relevant 
departments in the city administration, partners and possibly city stakeholder. The open approach will 
increase the feeling of responsibility and transparency, and it will reduce the risk of replicating mistakes in 
the future. The learnings can be shared by open access web platforms accessible for all relevant authorities.  
3.3 Key efforts for a smart governance 
The smart governance model utilizes efforts as public participation, transparency, partnerships, strategies, 
and visions that are all important for smart governances according to several conceptual models presented 
in Chapter 2.2 (see Table 2, Table 3, and Table 4). However, it is important to underline that there is no 
==PAGE:31==
19 
 
single plan for smart city action and no universally right way to create smart governance, and it is therefore 
impossible to create a standard model for how best to govern a smart city. Individual cities needs to 
formulate their own plans for how to invest resources to obtain their unique ambitions (Cosgrave & Doody 
2014). The previously described model and the following key efforts are only guidelines for how to foster 
smart governance that can handle the growth in population, the changing habits due to technological 
changes, and the increasing complexity of urban systems.   
3.3.1 Leadership 
The city governance should embrace the needs of all relevant stakeholders of the city, and they must 
therefore ensure a comprehensive understanding and insight into the local behaviours, patterns and 
desires. They should make sure that the staff is well familiar with the newest opportunities of digital 
services, safety, data sharing, etc. To do all this, a strong leadership within the city government is needed 
(Lee et al. 2014). According to research made by the UK government supported by the centre for the 
advancement of smart cities: the Future Cities Catapult (2017), a key challenge that prevents cities from 
realising smart city initiatives is the absence of strong leadership, and the lack of skills and capacity in local 
governments. Along with the necessary resources to adopt new practices and a good strategy, leadership is 
important to support harmonisation of efforts across relevant departments in the city administration, and 
ensure effective and integrated processes. A strong, brave, open, inclusive, and most of all, an active 
leadership style is recommended to provide visibility and confidence in the smart development (Cosgrave & 
Doody 2014; Lee et al. 2014; Hilton & Marsh 2017). Hilton & Marsh (2017) also underline the necessity to 
adapt a more directive approach that lead to actual behavioural changes, and hence, the leadership of a 
smart city should start with the acceptance of the need for disruptive social changes. In reality this is 
difficult as this approach is not an easy vote-winner.  
3.3.2 Partnerships 
The smart development is often said to be driven by private technology firms. However, they often fail to 
align their solutions with the abilities and desires of the city. When the problems of the city continuously 
become more complex and multidimensional, and when the problems cannot be addressed by single 
applications anymore, the city government and the industry would benefit from a closer partnership and 
alliance. This would allow the industry to get a more in-depth understanding of the needs for explicit 
services, and the city government will obtain increased level of skills and capacity to address their needs 
(Cosgrave & Doody 2014; Lee et al. 2014).  
Public-private partnerships (PPPs) can enable large-scale developments in the city without the public having 
the full responsibility for the development and funding requirements. In this type of PPP, public owned land 
is leased to private developers who can then make development under a set of legal rules and guidelines 
set by the city council, ensuring that the development is in line with the overall strategy of the city. In this 
way, the council still have some control over the developments whilst private companies conduct the 
actual construction for an economic return (Cosgrave & Doody 2014). This type of PPP is called LDO or BDO: 
Lease-Develop-Operate or Build-Develop-Operate (General Accounting Office 1999), and it is used more and 
more, especially in the United States in cases where cities and states find it difficult to shoulder the 
enormous cost of building and maintaining roads and bridges. California, Illinois, Michigan, Kentucky, and 
Georgia are all courting investors, hoping to strike deals in which corporations take responsibility of 
expenses and risks in exchange for a share in the profits (Wolf 2011). In Indiana, this type of PPP was used 
for the operation and maintenance of the public roadways. The partnership of private companies (Cintra 
Concesiones de Infraestructuras de Transporte and Macquarie Infrastructure Partners) paid the state a one-
time fee of $3.8 billion for a 75-year agreement to operate the roadway in exchange for the revenue from 
==PAGE:32==
20 
 
the tolls. The project is estimated to save the state of Indiana about $100 million per year in operating costs 
(Wolf 2011; Pearl 2013). 
Partnerships also encompasses the forming of closer alliances and collaborations between cities and closer 
cross-sector partnerships. A national (or even international) network of cities can create an opportunity to 
share knowledge, experience, resources and capabilities. The network can be created and supported (with 
resources and guidance) by the national government. A coordination of actions among cities and 
municipalities improves the potential for creating interoperability of technologies and standards, and to 
team up when buying systems and services by utilizing the benefits of economy of scale. It also creates a 
more holistic impression of the potential of smart development that is not only reserved for a few of the 
largest cities in a country (Cosgrave & Doody 2014; European Commission 2017b).  
3.3.3 Stakeholder involvement 
The original introduction of smart cities was highly concentrated on the technology aspects, and many 
people still see technology as the main feature of smart cities. However, over the past few years there has 
been an increasing emphasis on the individual citizens and their needs. The citizens are the actual owner of 
the problem as they experience the consequences of an inefficient system (Hilton & Marsh 2017). The 
European Commission (2017b) clarifies in a recent report that the lack of appropriate involvement and 
consultation with the public and relevant departments in the city administration, is a major problem when 
introducing new ICT initiatives. Their research confirms that the increased involvement of stakeholders 
leads to a more successful results, as the intervention then reflects the requirements of citizens. The 
involvement of stakeholders can be relevant in many parts of the project process, e.g. in the preliminary 
problem definition, design phase or in the co-creation of apps and services. Relevant city stakeholders are 
those who can provide information, expertise, and resources required for effective policy making, people 
who are key players in ensuring successful implementation, or simply people who have interest in the issue 
in question. City stakeholders covers citizens (neighbours and users), experts within the field, governmental 
agencies, firms and operators affected by the issue, non-governmental organisations (NGOs), and data and 
content providers. Consultation with other relevant departments in the city administration is also 
important to avoid inefficiency and conflicts.   
3.3.4 Strategy formulation 
A comprehensive formulated strategy of the smart city development generates synergy of efforts among 
agencies. Creating a strategy requires a realistic and practical understanding of the role of ICT, and of the 
developments and implementations within the field on a global level (Lee et al. 2014). The strategy should 
(profitably) be formulated using a collaborative approach, as this approach is more likely to result in overall 
support, resulting in a strategy that is linked with increased ownership, awareness, and transparency. It will 
reflect the citizens’ needs and the capabilities of businesses, and therefore, it will increase the chance of 
realizing the strategy. In order to use this approach, it is necessary to create a plan that specifies the person 
or team responsible for communicating with businesses, citizens, and other stakeholders to ensure broad 
collaboration. Note that a collaborative approach usually involves an increased need for resources, and that 
the decision processes takes a longer time. Hence, it is insufficient for fast evolving areas as those in China 
and India (Future Cities Catapult 2017).  
3.3.5  Governance structures 
Today strategy formulations, and general work within city administrations, are highly fragmented within 
the different departments (or siloes), which was also underlined by a UK city mayor in Hilton & Marsh 
(2017):  
==PAGE:33==
21 
 
“City operations have become specialised and fragmented. Functions, services, and domains are 
managed within individual procurements, and this creates significant challenges when introducing 
anything new. While cities may conventionally implement one technology at a time, a smart city can 
aspire to a connected ecosystem of different technologies and data sources that collectively improve the 
city’s efficiency, security, sustainability and lives of its citizens” (Hilton & Marsh 2017). 
It should be easier to share knowledge, information and cooperate across organisations and between 
departments. To do that, rearrangements of governance structures might be advisable. Cosgrave & Doody 
(2014) identifies a five-level curve that reflect the maturity of smart city development within a city (see 
Figure 4). The maturity curve is highly correlated with how embedded the smart city actions and concepts 
are within the governance structure, and it reflects the level of political support and the realisation of the 
value of the smart development. It is intended as a means to realise that the governance structures and the 
level of political support affect a city’s ability to meet strategies, and that the governance structure should 
reflect the expectations and ambitions of smart development. The curve is not intended to point out that 
some levels of maturity are entirely better than others, but it is intended to illustrate that governance 
structures and political support affect a city’s ability to achieve strategies, and that the governance 
structure should reflect the expectations and ambitions of smart development.

No smart city action 
Lowest in the curve is No smart city action. Cities placed here might be aware of the smart city concept but 
they have not yet acted upon it. These cities might have rebranded existing projects as smart city initiatives 
for marketing reasons, but they have not demonstrated or considered new approaches that are orientated 
towards a smart goal (Cosgrave & Doody 2014).  
==PAGE:34==
22 
 
Individual projects 
Cities at the level Individual projects have started exploring smart city projects. These are usually siloed in 
single departments in the city administration, e.g. transport, energy, or waste departments, and they are 
often used as pilots for new technologies and applications (Cosgrave & Doody 2014).  
Individual projects with political endorsement 
Political acts and leadership have endorsed and compelled the city to actively invest in smart city initiatives 
and programmes. With political endorsement, relevant departments in the city administration can get 
support in seeking funding for smart developments, which means that the city can invest more freely in a 
broad spectrum of programs and technologies. It makes the city more free to develop new partnerships 
with city stakeholders and companies (Cosgrave & Doody 2014).  
Organisational shift 
This includes cities that have created functional departments and administrations that specifically address 
smart city developments, which makes them able to act outside the everyday functioning of the city. These 
cities have an integral citywide vision and strategy for the development based on the strengths, 
opportunities, and challenges of the city, which are not separated in the different city siloes. The 
organisational shift allows the city to easily test innovative cross-departmental programmes and 
technologies. These departments and administrations deliver significant capacity upturns and increased 
freedom for the development and it makes decisions and programs faster to implement. However, it also 
makes the city vulnerable to changes in political persuasions, as it is fairly easy for newly elected political 
parties to eliminate the smart city department and/or administration (Cosgrave & Doody 2014).  
Embedded 
These cities have clear smart city values and principles, which clearly appear from their integral citywide 
strategic priorities. For the organisational structure, the chief information officer or chief executive officer 
(CIO or CEO) has executive authority to work cohesively between relevant departments in the city 
administration. The strategic mandate can increase the administrative city department’s flexibility and 
freedom to act without having to take all decisions up to the political level. Instead, they simply have to 
decide on initiatives that are in line with the strategic priorities, and report the progress against the visions 
and goals in the integrated management system incorporated in the annual budget (Cosgrave & Doody 
2014).  
3.4 Local governance experiences 
3.4.1 Copenhagen 
Strategies and leadership 
Copenhagen has already integrated several smart initiatives. However, many of the initiatives stand alone 
without any overall strategy as Copenhagen has no overall strategy for how to develop into a smart city. 
Yet, the municipality has several strategies within the field of smart cities. It has a climate strategy stating 
that Copenhagen will reduce CO2 emissions by effective utilization of big data for regulation of traffic lights, 
and reduce the amount of parking searching traffic. It has a bike strategy clarifying that the city will 
minimise bike thefts by using cheap chip technology, and improve route planning for bikes by allowing for 
citywide prioritization of bike traffic. It has a green mobility action plan for how to use intelligent traffic 
control for optimizing traffic signals with green waves and eco-driving. Furthermore, it has an open data 
strategy, a cloudburst strategy, a parking strategy, a resource and waste plan, and many more strategies 
==PAGE:35==
23 
 
(Nyegaard 2015). Of multidimensional strategies, relevant for several departments within the city 
administration, is The Common-Public Digitalisation Strategy 2016-2020.  
According to an interview with business managers from Siemens and TDC Group by Jung et al. (2016), the 
lack of an overall strategy of the smart city development in Copenhagen means that the departments in the 
city administration currently pull in different directions without communicating with each other. The 
cooperation between the public and private sector are further complicated by the lack of communication 
among city departments, which can result in other departments accidently spoiling a partnership by 
creating similar agreements with competing firms behind the back of the established PPP (Jung et al. 2016). 
In the interview, the smart city strategist from TDC Group, Søren Nørgaard, elaborates on a previous 
partnership between TDC Group and Copenhagen municipality concerning a test of new products. Here he 
experienced that another department in the city administration suddenly had purchased thousands copies 
of a similar product from another firm, completely independent of the innovation project partnership that 
they were having. According to him, this could have been avoided with an overall strategy clarifying 
responsibilities.  
Besides the lack of an overall smart city strategy for the municipality, the interview by Jung et al. (2016) 
expresses a need for a national smart city strategy. Denmark invests millions of DKK (Danish Kroner) in the 
area of smart cities, and each time a development option is possible, the initiative needs to pass a heavy 
bureaucratic process to obtain funding. It is possible to receive such funds from a public development pool. 
However, the pool is small and only pilot projects have the opportunity to receive funds. Practice shows 
that these pilot projects rarely get the chance to become real developments, even though the projects have 
been successfully implemented in other countries. Søren Nørgaard calls it the “the controlling pilot hells” 
(Jung et al. 2016), and Laurids Hovgaard from the Danish newspaper Ingeniøren states that “Danish smart 
cities suffer from pilot illness” (Hovgaard 2018). This pilot project illness in Copenhagen (and Denmark in 
general), means that too many projects ‘die’ before they even get the chance to begin (Jung et al. 2016; 
Hovgaard 2018). The illness can be caused by the fact that politicians still need to consider and commit to 
organisational changes and financing issues, but it could also be caused by the lack of a national 
architecture on the smart city development. Arup & CEDI (2016) recommend Denmark to develop a 
national smart city vision and strategy with clear outcomes and targets. They highlight that it will help 
generate a better understanding of the value of smart cities among citizens, businesses and municipalities, 
and it will convey commitment. They also emphasize the economic dimension, as a vision and strategy 
allow for economic analyses clarifying priority areas that makes it easier for businesses to focus their 
developments. The urgent need for a national smart city architecture is confirmed by Bo Fristed, the person 
in charge of smart city development in Aarhus municipality, in an interview by Hovgaard (2018). He states 
that a national architecture will ensure interoperability between cities and regions if it outlines the 
framework of standards and best practices. Without a national architecture, the 98 Danish municipalities, 
in theory, all risk utilizing smart city opportunities in individual ways with systems that cannot communicate 
or cooperate with one another. 
The need for a national strategy for the smart city development, inside the field of transport, is further 
stressed on a meeting (conducted within this project) with two employees from Copenhagen municipality: 
Annette Kayser and Kasper Brenøe Isbrand. When they were asked whether they miss a national strategy, 
they immediately responded with “Very much”. They wish for more activity and support at the national 
level, and they can only look with envy towards other countries where the national government is leading 
the development and help with campaigns and funding. It hinders social cohesion, and unfortunately traffic 
does not distinguish between the boarders of municipalities, they say. The lack of a national strategy makes 
==PAGE:36==
24 
 
it almost practically impossible to create strategies for the overall connection between public transport, 
bikes, cars, park-and-ride, etc. However, Kasper Isbrand underlines that it is not only national 
responsibilities that lacks. They express that it is often a challenge to get funding for new projects if they 
are not a clear part of the city strategy, and it is often only at the annual budget discussions that their 
desires and requests are heard. This indicates lack of political focus within the area. 
Partnerships 
Due to a strong desire from different Danish smart city actors, several networks have been established for 
the purpose of sharing knowledge and experience and creating increased influence on the smart 
development. These networks include the Danish Smart City Network (comprised of the Danish City lab, 
Gate 21, State of Green, and City Pack), the Danish Industrial Smart City Network, the Danish Standards 
Working Group, and the annual national smart city conference by NRGi and Gate 21 (Jung et al. 2016; 
CityPack 2017). However, an extended smart city network for public authorities supported by the national 
government is missing. A network between municipalities and public institutions would support 
cooperation, knowledge and data sharing, and it will increase the potential for creating larger-scaled 
projects (Arup & CEDI 2016).  
The traditional, deep-rooted customer-supplier relationship between the public and private sector in 
Denmark has created a gap between the sectors, which now makes cooperation difficult. The public sector 
lacks the understanding of business, and the private sector tends to hold on tight to their own business. 
PPPs are beneficial when creating complex, integrated and extended smart city solutions as the 
infrastructure and data is usually owned by the public sector and the high level of expertise is often found 
in the private sector (Copenhagen Cleantech Cluster 2013). Even though PPPs are complicated by Danish 
structures, Copenhagen municipality have used the instrument in several occasions. One of these was in 
2013 during the development process of new Intelligent Transport Solution (ITS), where 16 companies and 
educational institutions collaborated in the creation of innovate ITS concepts. The player’s different 
interests and experiences led to a successful collaboration that resulted in new insights into customer 
needs, a deeper understanding of city problems, early tests of prototypes, more data and more feedback. 
However, the collaboration also demonstrated that PPPs are complicated in Denmark, and that they are 
resource demanding and sometimes controlled by bureaucratic processes (Copenhagen Municipality & 
Resonans n.d.). Successful PPPs requires support from the remaining organization and from managers 
(Forum for sustainable procurement n.d.).  
Stakeholder involvement 
Arup & CEDI (2016) recommend that Copenhagen (and other Danish municipalities) responds to the 
concerns and needs of citizens to a greater extent, especially in the preliminary activities to project 
decisions. According to Annette Kayser and Kasper Brenøe Isbrand, Copenhagen municipality involves 
citizens in different ways. They have tried to use citizen panels, Facebook sites, surveys, hearings, and 
workshops. Their experience shows that the best results and discussions are obtained when the 
involvements are concentrated around a specific project. Thus, they have obtained limited profit from 
citizen involvement concentrated on overall needs and requests, where the focus has been to identify 
problems and arrive at the best overall project based on a needs assessment.  
Governance structures and the stagnation of development 
With the current emphasis on pilot projects within separate functions of the city administration and the 
lack of political endorsement, it is evident that Copenhagen is at the second step: Individual projects on the 
==PAGE:37==
25 
 
maturity curve in Figure 4. Copenhagen municipality does, however, have an IT group concentrating on 
how to utilize the potential of modern technologies to create a better life for the citizens across city 
functions, but it does not have a smart city department within the city administration that can put all the 
different strategies together and give out responsibilities.  
The ‘pilot illness’ and the lack of governmental support to the smart development stunt the smart city 
development in Copenhagen. In an interview conducted within the scope of the thesis, the IT programme 
director of Copenhagen Municipality, Mads Gaml, confirms that Copenhagen only invests in ICT solutions 
that are limited in scale both physically and geographically, though he underlines that all solutions 
implemented in Copenhagen are scalable. Furthermore, he explains that funding for up-scaling smart city 
initiatives cannot be obtained unless there is evidence of the impact on efficiency, climate improvements, 
safety, etc. According to Hilton & Marsh (2017), it will take some time before that is possible: 
“there is a long way before we can evidence and safely conclude that Smart City developments reliably 
deliver value to the public. This is something that is being researched and evaluated but at the present it 
is still to be demonstrated” (Hilton & Marsh 2017). 
Thus, it seems like Copenhagen will suffer from pilot illness for a long time. The criticism of the investment 
procedures in Copenhagen, and the associated lack of development potential, is confirmed by Copenhagen 
Cleantech Cluster (2013). Here it is argued that Danish municipalities primarily focus on the costs of new 
actions, and neglect the wider perspective and the non-monetary externalities when deciding on projects. 
Each time a new smart city project is implemented, it contributes to a greater network of smart solutions, 
and therefore, the project benefits lie beyond the individual project. These positive externalities are rarely 
recognized straight after implementation, but will appear some time after the development and expansion 
of the system. Thus, complicating the act of separating the positive externalities for the different project 
from one another. The price of the project is unavoidably important, but the described positive 
externalities should also be considered, otherwise the smart city development of Copenhagen is likely to 
stagnate as soon as the development process requires the implementation of more holistic, cost-
demanding urban solutions that out-scale pilot projects (Copenhagen Cleantech Cluster 2013). In March 
2017, Denmark felt the consequence of the public money fixation and lack of external and political smart 
city perspective, when the government decided to turn off all variable information signs along the Danish 
motorways (McGhie 2017). According to Arup & CEDI (2016), the problem could be remedied by developing 
a national smart city architecture that clarifies the value of smart cities and convey commitment to the 
smart growth in Denmark if clear outcomes and targets are included. 
3.4.2 Barcelona 
Strategy and vision 
Barcelona has a vision of becoming ‘the city of people’ by using technology as an enabler for more efficient 
and sustainable urban mobility, attracting capital, creating social cohesion, and ensure communication with 
people across cultures and educations (Angelidou 2015). To obtain the vision, the city phrased a holistic 
smart city strategy in 2011 with objectives divided into 22 programs about everything from data, lighting, 
mobility, citizenship, education, health to culture. Each program has its own initiatives, projects and 
strategies. All programs are embedded in an overall strategy focusing on open technological solutions, 
shared platforms that enhance cross-cutting approaches, promotion of alliances between private and 
public partners, and knowledge-sharing between cities, both nationally and internationally (Ferrer 2017).

An overall goal in the smart city vision is to break with the traditional silos in which the city is organised.  
Therefore, two out of the 22 programs concentrate directly on increasing cross-cutting activities. The 
introduction of the strategy and the two programs: Telecommunications Network and Urban Platform, have 
increased communication between relevant departments within the city administration, and made it easier 
to collect, manage, and share city data and services (Ventura 2014). Thereby, Barcelona has managed to 
create an integral citywide vision and strategy and move beyond the fragmented procedures of 
departments in a city administration.  
Partnerships 
The City Council of Barcelona frequently uses PPPs to encourage innovation and to strengthen the smart 
development in areas as transport, shopping, street lighting and environmental monitoring (Moskvitch 
2016). The city mayor has already facilitated alliances with several companies such as CISCO, IBM, and 
Philips and with research centres and universities (Ferrer 2017).  
Spanish cities are frontrunners when it comes to the implementation of successful city governance 
networks. The Spanish network of smart cities, in Spanish: Red Española de Ciudades Inteligentes (RECI), 
was founded in 2012 by a group of 22 Spanish cities, and now it has more than 75 member cities. RECI is 
sponsored by elected councillors in Spain, and the main objective of the network is to share experience, 
knowledge and to work together on the development of a smart city model (Palomo-Navarro & Navío-

Barcelona comply with the job of Repeat and share learning (from the 7 step model, see Chapter 3.2.7) on 
an international level by supporting international networks for knowledge-sharing and the development of 
collaborations. They are hosting international events as the Smart City Expo World Congress, the World 
Bank’s Sixth Urban Research, and Knowledge Symposium on Rethinking Cities: Framing the Future, and they 
are participating in international collaborations such as the City Protocol (Ferrer 2017). 
Stakeholder involvement and governance structures 
Until 2015, Barcelona had a smart city department in the city administration, but it was closed after the 
election of a new city government. The majority of the citizens have other priorities than creating a smart 
environment. One of the reason for the lack of priority concerning the smart city development could be 
that the citizens do not have the impression of living in a smart city, as many of the initiatives are directed 
towards tourists. Henceforth, the city needs to face the challenge of creating widespread, holistic costumer 
experiences, and propagate the great thoughts and benefits of smart development to the citizens 
(Moskvitch 2016).  
Before the election in 2015, Barcelona managed to be on the fourth step: Organisational shift on the 
maturity curve in Figure 4. After the closure of the smart city department, Barcelona moved a bit 
downwards the curve, though it is still well above the third step as the city still has several characteristics of 
the fourth step such as a citywide vision and strategy and cross-cutting activities between departments in 
the city administration. The main challenges for Barcelona is to convince the citizens that smart city 
initiatives benefit their daily life. The first step is to refocus the political approach, by moving away from the 
top-down-styles and instead involve citizens more in the political processes with a bottom-up approach 
(Smith 2018).

4 Smart mobility 
With the growing population in cities, public administrations are facing several challenging tasks as 
described in the previous chapter. They need to attract and retain skilled, highly-educated people that can 
support relevant companies and create job opportunities. They want to create a sustainable urban 
environments, attract tourists, and in general ensure quality of life for citizens and tourists. These are 
difficult tasks involving environmental, economic and technical challenges, and they require a willingness to 
change behaviour of both citizens and public administrations. The transport aspect of these challenges is an 
important and decisive parameter that affects several dimensions of a smart city. Transport initiatives can 
address challenges in a city such as pollution, congestion, noise, safety, and the time to cross a city, 
affecting the balance between work and private life. Smart mobility is one of the most important ways to 
improve urban development, and a subject that is likely to create high benefits for a large range of 
stakeholders, as everybody is affected by transport issues (Benevolo et al. 2016).  
According to Benevolo et al. (2016), smart mobility could be defined as: 
“coordinated actions addressed at improving the efficiency, the effectiveness and the environmental 
sustainability of cities. In other words Smart Mobility could consist of a hypothetically infinite number 
of initiatives other (but not always) characterized by the use of ICT” (Benevolo et al. 2016) 
In this quote, Benevolo et al. (2016) stress that smart mobility cannot be obtained by a single project, but it 
requires a complex set of projects and actions that differ in content and the use of technology, and that 
smart mobility is largely permeated by ICT to optimize the flow. They highlight the importance of 
coordinated actions and combinations of initiatives that together create a more efficient and effective 
urban environment. Pinna et al. (2017) also emphasize the importance of connectivity within the smart 
mobility concept, which, combined by the use of big data, makes it possible for travellers to transmit traffic 
information in real time, while the public administration can conduct dynamic management simultaneously 
to improve the overall traffic flow.   
Benevolo et al. (2016) state that the following six objectives are most important when addressing smart 
mobility initiatives: 
1. Reduce pollution 
2. Reduce traffic congestion 
3. Increase people safety 
4. Reduce noise pollution 
5. Improve transfer speed 
6. Reduce transfer costs 
Technology and ICT are most often necessary to realise smart mobility initiatives, even though ICT is not the 
goal but simply instruments. Note that the intensity of ICT in the actions can vary. E.g., bike sharing in its 
basic setting does not rely on ICT. However, many bike sharing solutions incorporate ICT elements. The 
construction of bicycle lanes is also a smart mobility action, as it aims at changing mobility to a mode that 
reduces pollution, noise, costs, and congestion levels, but it does not involve ICT. There are a number of 
such actions, either physical initiatives or political regulations related to smart mobility, that do not include 
ICT, but there is an inevitable maturity towards a correlation between smart mobility actions and ICT, and 
especially when the actions involve integration and extension of smart city programs (Benevolo et al. 2016). 
Therefore, this chapter (and the following chapter) will only focus on ITS actions that involve ICT.

4.1 Intelligent Transport Systems 
ICT in the transport sector plays a key role in the next generation of transport that it is not just about 
getting from A to B, but getting there in a safer, greener and more convenient way. Intelligent transport 
systems (ITS), a subcategory of ICT applied to transportation, are applications of advanced technology that 
aim to solve transportation problems, improve transport management, and increase coordination and the 
flow of information to get the most out of the existing transport system (Hasan et al. 2013). The EU 
Directive 2010/40/EU defines ITS as: 
“Intelligent Transport Systems’ or ‘ITS’ means systems in which information and communication 
technologies are applied in the field of road transport, including infrastructure, vehicles and users, and in 
traffic management and mobility management, as well as for interfaces with other modes of transport” 
(European Union 2010). 
Traffic congestion is one of the most significant problems in cities rapidly growing due to increased 
population and urbanization rates. As a consequence, air and noise pollutions and fuel consumptions 
increase, making city life more difficult and unpleasant. ITS aims to help managers of the infrastructure 
exploit the system to overcome current transport problems and help end-users obtain maximum 
satisfaction with the transport system by allowing access to more information and more tools (Perallos et 
al. 2016). Overall, ITS covers the entire flow of data/information, from the collection of data (e.g. using 
sensor technology or camera surveillance), to the aggregation and translation of the collected data (often 
at Traffic Management Centres (TMCs)), and to the information dissemination (e.g. using variable traffic 
signs and maps). With the use of data collection, processing and communication technologies in vehicles 
and devices across the transportation infrastructure, ITS solutions can help achieve the six objectives 
identified by Benevolo et al. (2016). In other words, ITS applications can help cities realize some of the 
criteria to become a smart city (or describing attributes associated with the smart city concept), see Table 
5. For example, ITS can help promote public transport and biking, and consequently, help create a more 
sustainable city. 
The main components of ITS systems are Traffic Management Centres (TMCs), communication features, 
roadside devices/infrastructures and agreements. TMCs represent the heart of the systems as it is 
responsible for all command and control. Communication features are electronics, software and network 
gear that run the systems, and bring all the data to TMC. Roadside devices are the eyes and ears of the 
system (this could be video surveillance or radar systems), and they collect information and disseminate 
the information to the travellers. The agreements can be related to operation, incident management, 
infrastructure shaping, and data sharing, and they bring all features together and are necessary to make the 
smart mobility actions realisable.  
4.2 Cooperative Intelligent Transport Systems 
The ITS term was born in the beginning of 1990s, but not until the beginning of the 2000s did the concept 
really started to bloom. Recently, a new era of ITS applications has begun called Cooperative Intelligent 
Transport Systems (C-ITS). This is a group of ITS technologies where service provision is enabled by, or 
enhanced by, the use of wireless communication to communicate dynamic data between the actors in a 
transport systems, e.g. vehicles, infrastructure and personal devices (Sun et al. 2016; Aramrattana et al. 
2017). The difference between any ITS system and a C-ITS system is basically that C-ITS depends on 
interactions with other ITS systems. A C-ITS system could be dependent on receiving data from other 
systems to make it function, or conversely, it might provide data to other actors to enable functionality of 
==PAGE:43==
31 
 
their C-ITS systems. Note that C-ITS is not an end-system itself. It is a combination of techniques and 
systems that together enable cooperative and collaborative service provision (Sun et al. 2016). 
Figure 7 shows a schematic diagram of the differences between ITS and C-ITS systems. ITS systems simply 
use ITS technologies (see Chapter 4.3) to function whereas C-ITS systems can communicate with any core in 
the system enabling interaction and cooperation between all actors. C-ITS systems can share data and 
information, and to some degree, they can control other systems. The communication between vehicle 
systems is often referred to as vehicle-to-vehicle (V2V) communication, whereas the interaction between 
vehicle systems and roadside systems is called vehicle-to-infrastructure (V2I) communication; both classes 
of C-ITS are crucial to the future autonomous vehicles and the upcoming developments of the overall 
transport system (Cartwright & Knoop 2016). V2V communication, also called Connected Vehicle 
Technology (CVT), can be used to avoid traffic incidents and improve traffic flow, e.g., a driving vehicle can 
alert the vehicle driving behind it when it is breaking to avoid a collision. V2I enables vehicles to 
communicate with traffic signals and other infrastructure elements, allowing traffic signals to return 
information about the time to green or red. The vehicle can then make the driver aware of the optimal 
speed to avoiding stopping for red light, or it can warn the driver to stop.

4.3.1 Positioning technologies 
Positioning technologies enable real-time location determination of vehicles or people. The most known 
positioning systems use Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS) such as the Global Positioning System 
(GPS), GALILEO and GLONASS, but positioning methods do not necessarily use satellites. Overall, there are 
three types of positioning technologies: signpost systems, dead reckoning systems, and wave-based 
positioning. The wave-based positioning system is the most recognised positioning method, and it uses by 
GNSS. 
4.3.2 Wireless Communication (WiC) 
Wireless communication (WiC) is an electromagnetic wave-based technology. Common for all 
subcategories of WiC is the way data is transferred between components (see Figure 8). The system 
consists of a transmitter, a receiver, and a communication channel that connects the transmitter and 
receiver. The communication channel may be wired (e.g. with coaxial cables or fiber-optic cables) or 
wireless (e.g. using Bluetooth or Wi-Fi). The transmitter first encodes the message (the text, image or 
video) into bit sequences. The transmitter converts the encoded message into electrical signals 
(modulation) and it transmits the signal over the channel. At the receiver, the signal is converted back into 
a bit sequence (demodulation) and decoded. Thus, allowing the receiver to read the original message (Zabic 
& Nielsen 2011). 
 
Figure 8 – Schematically diagram of the data transferring process behind all WiC technologies 
4.3.3 Sensor technology 
Sensors are devices that respond to physical stimulations. When a stimulation is recognised they transmit 
an analogue or electronic impulse to a receiver. The stimulation could be light, pressure, sound, 
magnetism, temperature or movement. Sensors do not necessarily have to be electronic, but nowadays 
most are. A classic example of an analogue sensor is the quicksilver used in thermometers. Sensors are 
used everywhere in our daily life, e.g. it is used in elevators, flashlights, and keys for cars with an electronic 
lock. Eror et al. (1995) divide sensors into six categories: mechanical, thermal, electrical, magnetic, radiant, 
and chemical sensors, which all have various stimulation methods. Note that cameras falls into the category 
of sensor technology as they use small photoelectric sensors to measure light-waves and intensities, and it 
converts the input into an image matrix. 
4.3.4 Computational logics 
Computational logic, also called logic in computer science, is the scientific field behind countless 
engineering and mathematical programs, and it is the root of artificial intelligence (EMCL 2018). Within the 
field of computer science is machine learning, which covers the field of making computer systems ‘learn’ 
from data (without being explicitly programmed), e.g. based on pattern recognition using recognition 
algorithms. Pattern recognition is used in Automatic Number Plate Recognition (ANPR) to decrypt the 
numbers and letters at a picture of a number plate (NP). Computer science is also the basis behind 
simulation programs aiming at imitating operations of a real-world process or system such as a transport 
system.

5.3 Clarification of applications 
5.3.1 Traffic signal optimization 
Traffic controlled signals 
Many traffic signals use pre-set time periods with peak and off-peak programs to control the lighting based 
on traffic arrival rates, either estimated or forecasted (Paruchuri et al. 2013). The most commonly 
recognised intelligent technique for optimizing traffic signals it to adjust the setting of signals to real time 
data based on the registration of approaching vehicles. The recognition of a vehicle measured by sensors 
(laser, infrared light, radar) or cameras can extend the time for green light up to a given maximum. Traffic 
controlled signals can also be coordinated to avoid stop-and-go traffic between closely located signals 
(green waves) – addressed to cars, bikes, buses, etc. A special type among the traffic coordinated signal 
systems is adaptive traffic signal control. In adaptive systems, signals are fully traffic-driven and 
coordinated based on continuous calculations of coordination patterns, green times, and cycle times. 
Theoretically, adaptive systems will provide better traffic performance than coordinated systems. However, 
in intersections with many bicycles the use of the adaptive traffic signal control is limited due to difficulties 
in detecting bikes (Gaml & Friis 2018). 
According to the Danish Road Directorate, traffic controlled signals will improve the traffic flow on 
distances with many traffic signals, and it can reduce fuel consumption, air pollution and CO2 emission. 
Fewer stops result in higher road capacity, less congestion, and fewer accidents. CBA (cost-benefit analysis) 
studies show that the investments in traffic controlled signals, in general, will be returned 40 times to the 
society during the first year of operation (The Danish Road Directorate 2012a), as time savings have a 
positive impact on socio-economics.  
Bus prioritisation  
Prioritizations of buses, light rails, and trams in traffic signals will reduce the risk of them being delayed, 
increasing the satisfaction of public transport. Intelligent prioritization of buses, light rails and trams at 
intersections can be achieved by the use of cameras, infrared bus detectors or inductive loop sensors that 
identify the arrival of a bus, and wired or wireless communication between the bus and the signal. The bus, 
light rail, or tram tells the signal to extend the green phase. A newer bus prioritisation technology utilizes a 
built-in GNSS receiver in buses to detect the position of buses, light rails or trams. When the bus, light-rail 
or tram gets within a certain, predefined detection range before the traffic signal, a priority request is sent 
to the signal controller via WiC. The request can be compared to the actual time-table to decide if the bus, 
light rail or tram is ahead or behind the time-table, and thereby decide if prioritization is necessary.   
On the one hand, bus prioritization increases the level of service for the users of the public transport 
system, but on the other hand it induces reductions in the mobility for cars, bikes, trucks, and pedestrians 
waiting for green light in the intersection, which leaves cars idling, causing negative impacts on the 
environment and noise level in the city (Morellato & Sdun 2010). 
Bike prioritisation and safety precautions 
Several initiatives have been developed to prioritize bikes in intersections, to increase safety, and especially 
to avoid right turn accidents. Several technologies have been tested to detect bikes before their arrival at 
the intersection. These include radar, laser, thermal and magnetic sensors, RFID, and video surveillance. In 
Copenhagen municipality the best results have been achieved by cameras and recognition patterns, called 
ViSense. ViSense is very precise in recognising bikes and the people riding the bikes, and consequently, it 
can deduce their routes within the city by comparing the input from cameras at different intersections. Due 
==PAGE:48==
36 
 
to privacy policies, this part of ViSense is not utilized, and it is merely used to detect bikes. The detection of 
a bike is communicated to the signal controller via wired or wireless technologies, allowing for extended 
green lights for bikes (Gaml & Friis 2018).  
The detection of a bike can be used to turn on a light that will illuminate the conflict zone between 
motorized vehicles and bikes (Aarhus Municipality 2016), or to turn on a light at the top of the signal poles 
indicating a potential conflict. In Odense and Aarhus in Denmark, the bike detecting system is combined 
with rain sensors. In the event of rain, a longer green wave is triggered in the nearby intersections to allow 
for increased mobility for VRUs that are exposed to the rain (Cykelportalen 2017). Of other bike 
prioritisation initiatives in signalized intersections utilizing the detection of bikes are the Dutch invention of 
a ‘crowd-phase’ (where bikes and pedestrians from all directions in the intersection have green at the same 
time), and traffic signals with two green phases for bikes per cycle in directions with many detected bikes.  
Detection of bikes at intersections will increase the mobility for bikes, increase traffic safety and improve 
the sense of security for bikes (Aarhus Municipality 2016).  
Pedestrian detection and optimization 
Detection of pedestrians (using cameras and recognition patterns, lasers or radar sensors mounted in the 
signal poles) reduces the waiting time for pedestrians in intersections. The detectors can be used to 
activate the pedestrian traffic control device, it can be used to extend the green light for pedestrians in the 
crosswalk in case of slow pedestrians, or it can be used to shorten the crossing time if the pedestrian have 
already cleared the crosswalk. The detection of a pedestrian can also be connected to the street lights 
increasing the pedestrian visibility at night. For intersections with pedestrian phases in all cycles, pedestrian 
detection will increase mobility for all modes. When the waiting time for pedestrians is reduced, the red 
light is usually more respected and the number of pedestrians crossing for red is reduced, resulting in 
improved traffic safety (Aarhus Municipality 2016). 
Cooperative intersection collision avoidance systems (CICAS) 
Many vehicle collisions are reported at urban and rural intersections. CICAS seeks to prevent such accidents 
using sensors and computational logic by informing travellers in (or approaching) an intersection about 
potential dangers. This could be an alert warning the driver that another vehicle has entered the 
intersection in red light, or simply an alert informing the driver that the light turns red in 2 seconds. The 
system utilizes sensors in the road that detect arriving vehicles and measure driving speed, e.g. by using 
inductors, radar technology, camera surveillance and recognition patterns. The information is then sent via 
wired or wireless communications to a processing unit, either in a unit next to road or in the signalling unit. 
In the processing unit, an algorithm determines whether the risk of a collision is high, and alerts the drivers 
depending on the risk determination. The alert may be sent as V2I via DSRC to the vehicle for the driver to 
read (or hear), or through a variable message sign installed at the road-site (Basma et al. 2011; Dopart et al. 
2018) 
There are currently several pilot projects in the USA, e.g. in the state of Minnesota (Holtzman 2015). CICAS 
projects have shown significant decreases in accidents at intersections during the pilot phase, indicating 
that the ITS application has a positive impact on traffic safety (Holtzman 2015; Dopart et al. 2018).  
Eco-driving at signalized intersections 
Eco-driving is an application aiming at minimizing stop-and-go traffic in urban areas. It informs vehicles of 
the optimal speed before a signalized intersection to avoid stopping for red light, and to ensure smoother 
==PAGE:49==
37 
 
driving. The application was originally addressed to trucks, as they induce major impact on the urban traffic 
flow and environment. In Copenhagen, several trucks are equipped with an on-board unit (OBU) that 
communicates through WiC (WLAN) with a signal controller telling the phases of the traffic signal. The 
information is communicated to the OBU, which then converts the information to an optimal speed that is 
displayed in the vehicle. An important measure is the current speed, which may be estimated in the OBU 
via GNSS and/or by in-vehicle sensors (e.g. gyroscopes and accelerometers) (Gaml & Friis 2018).  
A new application called GreenCatch in Copenhagen enables cars or bike riders to utilize the signal phase 
information. This phone application works across several transport modes, and is an alternative to an OBU 
as it offers the same information without the need to implement a device in the vehicle (Copenhagen 
Municipality 2017a). 
Eco-driving applications increase mobility in urban areas with many intersections, but the main benefits 
comes with the reductions in CO2 emissions and energy consumptions when trucks can pass multiple 
intersections with a minimum of stops (Aarhus Municipality 2016).  
5.3.2 ITS for VRUs 
Bicycle barometers  
Bicycle barometers are installations on major bike paths that count the number of passing bikes and shows 
it on a sign for the travellers to observe. The aim is to visualise the importance pf bicycling in city centres 
and to promote and motivate traveling by bike. Bicycle barometers comprise of two elements: a detector 
and a display. The detectors detect the bikes, usually using inductors or fibre optic sensors. The fibre optic 
sensors measure strain, while the inductors reacts to the metal in the bike chassis. Fibre optic sensors 
usually provide the most accurate measurements (Aarhus Municipality 2016). Other systems use cameras 
and pattern recognitions for counting the number of bikes, including the system used in signalized 
intersections in Copenhagen: ViSense. Bicycle barometers can be combined with weather information, 
where thermal sensors are installed on the barometer for temperature readings. All measurements are 
sent via wired or wireless connections to the counter box to be shown to passing bikers. The display can 
show the total number of cyclists on a day and/or in another given period (Gaml & Friis 2018).    
Studies of the bicycle barometers in Copenhagen and Odense (in Denmark) show that the bike barometers 
have no direct impact on mobility, but the increase in motivation and promotion of bicycling have a minor 
impact on the environment as it indirectly affects the number of tours by bike (Gaml & Friis 2018; Aarhus 
Municipality 2016). 
Dynamic bike signs 
Variable message signs for bikes provides all kinds of information to bikers. This could be information about 
congestion, accidents, road maintenance, weather forecasts, advised speed for green waves in combination 
with the actual speed of the bicycle, and advices for routes with less congestion or routes that are cleared 
for snow (Gaml & Friis 2018). In the Dutch city of Utrecht, ‘Flo-signs’ are placed alongside bike paths, 
detecting the velocity of passing bikes, and app. 100 m before a signalized intersection. The Flo-sign gives 
the rider of the bike speed advices. The advices are in form of animals, were a rabbit means speed up, a 
turtle means slow down, and a thumbs up means that you should keep the speed to catch the green light 
(Hümbs 2017).  
In Copenhagen, the dynamic bike signs are used to show comparative travel times for bikes and cars to a 
specific location to demonstrate the benefits of choosing the bike. As for the bicycle barometers and the 
==PAGE:50==
38 
 
detection of vehicles at signalized intersections, the system is based on sensor data and video surveillance 
counting the number of bicycles and estimating the level of congestion. The application can also be used to 
show route advises, which is done in Copenhagen. These are based on a specified set of scenarios and 
route alternatives set by the municipality of Copenhagen. The route advices are alternative ways with less 
congestion that are no longer than 300 meter compared with the direct route. In the near future, the signs 
will also inform bicycles about alternative routes will less air pollution (Gaml & Friis 2018).  
It is expected that bike signs have the same impact on society as bicycle barometers. Though, it is also 
expected that the information on route advices improves mobility for bikes. 
Dynamic street lights 
Intelligent street light applications detect bikes and/or pedestrians at night and increase the level of lighting 
when VRUs are detected (in Copenhagen the lighting is increased from 50 % to 100 %). By using motion 
sensors (passive infrared sensors), the street lamps can detect changes in infrared radiation, which can be 
interpreted as motion nearby. This is useful for VRU detection. The infrared sensors can be supplemented 
by ambient light sensors allowing the street lamps to adjust to current conditions, and so conserve energy 
on bright evenings or shine brighter on foggy nights. The sensors communicate the inputs to the street light 
controller via wired or wireless communications. The lamps may also be connected in an IoT-manner, 
allowing central control of all city lights (Citelum 2016).  
Intelligent street lights induce energy and economic savings, and they increase traffic safety and the sense 
of security for bikes and pedestrians by alerting passing vehicles of their presence (Citelum 2016).  
LED lane lights 
LED lane lights are strings of lights embedded in the bike paths that flashes in order to help bikes see the 
progress of the path in areas with low street lighting. The LED lights can be powered by build-in solar cells, 
and they can be connected to sensors detecting an approaching bike, and thereby ensure lighting only 
when bikes are on the path. This feature also serves as an alert for vehicles, as it informs drivers about the 
presence of a bike. In Copenhagen, some LED lane lights are controlled by traffic signal programs by 
communicating via a wired or wireless connection. A string of green lights in the path indicates a green 
wave for bicycles biking with a speed of 18 km/h. The green wave is activated by the bike when it passes a 
detector app. 200 meter before the intersection. LED lane lights can also be placed around pedestrian 
crossings that lights when a pedestrian approaches the crossing area (Cardwell 2014). 
LED lane lights have a positive impact on traffic safety and the sense of security for bikes and pedestrians. 
LED Lane lights connected to signals improve the mobility for bikes, and they indirectly motivate travels by 
bike, which improves the environment and noise level in urban areas (Aarhus Municipality 2016). 
5.3.3 Parking systems  
Single space detection and information 
Single space detection and information systems are applications that detect the occupation of individual 
parking spaces and deliver information about the availability to drivers. The information is continuously 
stored in databases, allowing parking companies to monitor real time occupancies.  
Two common sensor technologies are used for detecting vehicles. These are ultrasound (mechanical sensor 
technology) and magnetometers (magnetic sensor). Infrared sensors (using radiant sensor technology) may 
be used instead of ultrasound. Ultrasound sensors emit an acoustic pulse inaudible to humans, which is 
==PAGE:51==
39 
 
then measured as an echo (reflection) by the sensor. Based on the time difference between transmission 
and reception, it is possible to calculate the distance travelled. If the distance changes from some baseline 
scenario to a shorter distance, the system has detected a vehicle. Ultrasound sensors are useful for indoor 
parking where the equipment is protected against the weather. Magnetometers detect changes in 
magnetic fields using inductors mounted in the road pavement of individual parking spaces. When a vehicle 
passes, it induces a current in the inductor, which can be measured and used in deciding whether a vehicle 
is present or not. Magnetometers are useful for outdoor parking as the magnetic field is not corrupted by 
magnetic fields from other floors, which can be the case in parking houses (ITSTeknik 2018; ATKI 2015; MSR 
Traffic 2017).  
The information is usually communicated to users (to variable message signs or communicated through LED 
lights) via wired or wireless communications. The signs may show the current number of unoccupied spaces 
in a parking lot combined with an indication of the direction towards these spaces. These signs can be 
employed both roadside in the city centre (directing travellers to a parking lot with free spaces) and inside 
parking lots (directing travellers to a free space). LED lights can be used to visualise whether a parking space 
is occupied or not, which is often indicated by red and green lights (Q-Free 2018).  
Studies by Aarhus Municipality (2016) show that single space detection applications contribute to better 
mobility for cars, and they induce minor environmental improvements.  
Electronic parking payment systems 
Electronic parking payment systems facilitate ticket-free parking by use of different technologies combined 
in many ways. Some of the possible applications are (adapted from Q-Park (2018)): 
 
Scanning of electronic payment card at entry and exit (using the RFID chip in the card) 
 
Scanning of electronic payment card at entry combined with ANPR at entry and exit 
 
Booking of parking space with electronic payment card, which identifies the user and open barriers 
on entry and exit 
 
Booking of parking space with electronic payment card and registration of NP prior to visit; ANPR is 
used to open barriers on entry and exit 
 
Vehicles equipped with electronic tags (using RFID or DSRC) that identifies the user and open 
barriers on entry and exit 
 
Mobile phone using RFID or NFC at entry and exit 
In general, electronic parking payment systems use WiC technologies (RFID, NFC or DSRC) combined with 
surveillance systems (cameras) that delivers input to algorithms in the ANPR system.  
5.3.4 Road traffic control 
Variable message signs 
Variable message signs (VMS) are used to deliver dynamic information to travellers by motorized vehicles. 
Like dynamic bike signs, VMS have many applications, some of which are listed below:  
 
Traffic information (e.g. about accidents, construction or congestion) 
 
Variable speed limit signs  
 
Speed measurement (‘Your Speed’-signs) 
 
Warning signs (e.g. dynamic indication of traffic from side roads, school children on the road, 
slippery roads, heavy winds) 
==PAGE:52==
40 
 
 
Parking guidance systems 
 
Lane control indicators (e.g. used for reversible road-lanes or closure of lanes)  
 
Over height vehicle warnings before tunnels  
The information can be controlled by TMCs or by sensors. Sensor technologies used in combination with 
VMSs include: inductors/coils, radar, cameras, etc. Inductors mounted in the pavement can trigger vehicle-
activated signs, and if two sensors are placed together, the speed can also be measured. Radar sensors can 
measure speed and detect vehicles by analysing the frequency change in the reflected signal. VMS can also 
be controlled by cameras and computational logics detecting elements as NPs, vehicle speeds, congestion, 
foreign objects on the road and wrong-way drivers. VMS sometimes show local weather information, 
triggered by temperature or wind sensors, informing travellers about slippery roads or high winds (VSM 
2018; The Danish Road Directorate 2012b).  
Depending on the type of VMS and the information they provide, VMS can improve traffic safety, mobility 
for motorized vehicles, and (possibly) improve the environment and induce time savings (Rambøll 2015; 
Aarhus Municipality 2016), which in turn improves socio-economics. 
Dynamic bollards  
Dynamic bollards can keep some kinds of traffic away from a specific area, while allowing other kinds. The 
application can be attractive in pedestrianised zones, where only delivery and emergency vehicles are 
permitted entrance. The allowed vehicles are detected just before the entrance to the zone, e.g. by use of 
RFID tags in the vehicle and a reader at the bollards (Aarhus Municipality 2016). If the vehicle has 
permission, the bollard will go down.  
Studies by Aarhus Municipality (2016) confirm that dynamic bollards increase traffic safety and mobility for 
VRUs, and they can have a positive impact on the environment (noise and air pollution) in urban areas by 
minimizing motorized traffic. 
Dynamic speed bumps 
Dynamic speed bumps reduce speeds in the same way as traditional speed bumps, but in contrast to 
traditional ones, dynamic speed bumps can be activated and deactivated. Dynamic speed bumps may 
reduce the inconvenience for bus drivers by deactivating when a bus is detected (e.g. by RFID tag or DSRC). 
Some dynamic speed bumps are purely mechanical (using mechanical sensors), where the bump will flatten 
if hit at low speeds, e.g. lower than 30 km/h, while the bump will harden if hit by a vehicle traveling faster 
than the speed limit. Other ones use radar sensors to measure speeds, while a logical control unit decides 
whether to activate the bump based on the speed measurement. These bumps usually involves a hatch 
integrated into the road. When activated, the hatch lowers a few centimetres creating an inverse bump 
(Edeva 2017; Stevens 2008; Curtin University 2017).  
Dynamic speed bumps have a positive impact on traffic safety (Aarhus Municipality 2016). 
Lane management 
Lane management covers all aspects of controlling the use of road lanes. This could be the control of 
tunnels, bridges or highway lanes. On bridges and in tunnels, the application is used to close for passage in 
cases where the road is blocked by an accident. On highways, where the traffic direction changes 
significantly during the day, the application can be used for creating reversible road lanes. For the latter, 
VMS or lane control lights can be used to inform the travellers of the status of the lane (with open or closed 
status information and direction information). Lane control lights are specific types of traffic lights used to 
==PAGE:53==
41 
 
manage traffic by the use of lighting symbols with different colours. Often they allow or forbid traffic to use 
one or more of the available lanes by the use of green lighting arrows (to permit) or by red lighting crosses 
(to prohibit), repeated at regular distances to provide a continuous reminder of the lane status.  
Similar to reversible road lanes are rush hour lanes and peak hour lines. Rush hour lanes are applications 
where an extra lane on the hard-shoulder or on the left side of the runway is opened for a certain period of 
time during peak periods. Peak hour lines cover the establishment of additional lanes on a multi-lane road 
that divide a motorway with e.g. 2 lanes in each direction in 3 narrow lanes. The narrow lanes are marked 
with LED lights that only light during rush hours (Kjemtrup 2017).  
In the city centres, lane management can be used for the creation of flexible road areas. Flexible road 
areas, or shared spaces, is a way of optimizing the use of urban spaces in areas with a significant change in 
modes during the day. In France, they have made an experiment with variable pavement where a specific 
road area changes function during the day, e.g. from bike path in the morning hours to pedestrian zone or 
road lane during the afternoon. The function of the space is marked with dynamic symbols on the 
pavement and on variable signs next to the road area (Gaml & Friis 2018). 
The effects of variable road areas are still not documented, but it is expected that it can increase mobility 
for VRUs and/or motorized vehicles depending on the type of lane management. The effects on traffic 
safety are uncertain. On the one hand, the extra space can induce increased safety as some travellers have 
more space to navigate, but on the other hand, the confusion concerning who has the right to use the area 
might reduce traffic safety. Clear signs or lights are decisive for achieving satisfactory traffic safety.  
Automatic speed control 
Automatic speed control can be carried out in many ways. The traditional way involves a sensor device (a 
speed camera) containing a laser emitter, a receiver and a camera. The speed camera measures the speed 
of the approaching vehicles and, if necessary, takes a picture of the offender (Danish Police 2015). The 
speed cameras can be used in two different ways. Firstly, speed cameras can be used to check drivers from 
the roadside, where the police physically stops the offenders. This method involves a manned observation 
unit (with the speed camera) and a manned apprehension unit. Secondly, the speed cameras can be 
unmanned at fixed positions or mobile. When the speed camera detects a speeding driver, the offender is 
sent a fine. The two approaches are both examples of point speed control (European Commission 2018b).  
Average speed control applications measure the average speed across a section of a road, by placing a 
gantry at each end of the desired section where entering and exiting vehicles are identified using ANPR. As 
the distance of the section and time difference can be measured, it is possible to calculate the average 
speed for each vehicle.  
It has been shown that fixed speed cameras are more efficient at reducing speed and crashes than mobile 
speed cameras, and that fixed speed cameras are more effective than physical police control. Point speed 
control generates improved road safety (European Commission 2018b). The average speed control system 
(implemented in the Netherlands and in Austria) have shown significant decreases in accident rates (up to 
47%) and improved mobility due to improved traffic flow in rush hours through reduced traffic congestion 
and bottlenecking. After the implementation of the application in Netherlands, only 0.5% were detected 
speeding. Several studies are investigating the application’s impact on fuel consumption, but the 
conclusions of these studies are still unknown (Stefan 2006; European Commission 2018b).

5.3.5 Bus traffic optimization 
Bus passenger counting 
Bus passenger counting applications enable bus operators to monitor the occupancy of buses. Counting of 
passengers in buses is usually done with cameras. The camera, mounted in the ceiling above the doors, 
transmits images to an image processor that uses recognition algorithms to count passengers. To achieve 
satisfactory counting results, a camera must be mounted at all doors to count both leaving and entering 
passengers, though a single sensor can count people getting both on and off. The application has economic 
implications, as it allow operators to optimize bus routes and bus sizes. The optimization results can have 
implications on the environment and the mobility for bus passengers (Highlight 2018; Trastem Giken 2018). 
Automated fare collection system (AFC) 
Automated fare collection (AFC) systems are applications that allow for fast and easy payment of transport 
activities. AFCs use a fare medium (e.g. a smartcard with a magnetic stripe or a RFID chip) and electronic 
devices that functions as readers of the fare medium. The medium will carry information on the current 
credit balance. The reader device can be implemented at automatic gate machines in a subway system or 
as handheld devices for conductors (Advantech 2018).  
Data from AFS systems can be used for the analysis of route performance and optimizations in route 
planning. The application can improve mobility for passengers, the level of service, and attract more users 
to the system, resulting in positive economic impacts (Vasudevan & Mulukutla 2009).  
Automatic vehicle identification (AVI) 
Automatic vehicle identification (AVI) is a fleet management system that identifies the position of a vehicle 
either continuously or periodically. It is a technique that enables organizations with a fleet of vehicles 
(buses, taxis, ships, etc.) to efficiently manage and monitor their fleet and drivers. For continuous 
positioning and identification, a GNSS receiver that collects and process satellite signals must be installed in 
the vehicle (e.g. using an OBU). If the vehicle is connected to a cellular or satellite network through an on-
board transmitter module (using GSM or similar cellular technologies, see Appendix), ongoing positioning 
and identification information can be forwarded to the fleet management software centre. Otherwise, the 
information will be extracted at the fleet base at the end of the journey (Vasudevan & Mulukutla 2009). 
For periodic positioning and identification of the fleet, long range RFIDs have to be installed in all vehicles. 
These will ensure communication with readers installed on the entrance and exit point of bus terminals, 
traffic centres, bus stops, maintenance halls, etc. Both the continuous and periodic technology can locate 
vehicles and ensure data collection and optimization of the fleet. The periodic technique can also be used 
for access control in areas where only certain vehicles are allowed.  
Note that AVI is a fundamental technique that can also be used for electronic payment systems, dynamic 
bollards and other ITS applications (Nedap 2018). 
The collected data from a fleet of buses (cars or trucks) can be used to improve traffic flow management, as 
the solution allow operators to monitor unscheduled stoppings, deviations from scheduled routes, and 
over-speeding. This makes the technique useful for route and schedule optimization, resulting in improved 
accuracy of arrivals, increased mobility for VRUs, and reduced operation costs (Vasudevan & Mulukutla 
2009). 
==PAGE:55==
43 
 
Driver and fuel monitoring 
Driver and fuel monitoring applications represent another type of fleet management system. A collector 
device logs data from the vehicle on an OBU. The device can collect data on fuel usage, driver behaviour, 
mileage, etc., but it is also possible to log the state of the vehicle such as the state of the doors (open or 
closed), or of the fuel hatch. The data can be sent to a central server over cellular or satellite networks for 
continuous information collection, which requires a transmitter module (GSM or similar technologies, see 
Appendix) (Vasudevan & Mulukutla 2009), or it can be extracted manually at the end of the journey. 
Driver and fuel monitoring data can assist in deciding on maintenance needs, or it can be used to identify 
drivers with dangerous habits. Driver and fuel monitoring data is also important in minimizing fuel 
expenses. The application can be used as a tool to make drivers drive more fuel efficiently. The technology 
can thereby contribute to a cleaner environment and economic savings on fuel. By monitoring the 
mechanical state of vehicles in the fleet, faults may be repaired before they turn into catastrophic failures, 
which results in increased traffic safety (Malacarne 2017). 
Transit information system 
Transit information systems using real-time traffic information are applications aiming at improving the 
transit experience. Transit information systems may include predictions on arrival and departure time, the 
causes of disruptions, or even the seat availability in the next coming bus. The application utilizes 
technologies and elements from several of the already introduced applications. The data is based on the 
AVI system using GNSS to derive information on the current location of the bus, and on algorithms and 
simulations to deduce the arrival time based the location information, which can be shown on VMSs. The 
information can also be used by journey planners on IT platforms (Vasudevan & Mulukutla 2009; Harmony 
& Gayah 2017).   
The application is particularly useful for low-frequency routes and for buses that often deviate from 
scheduled time estimates due to unforeseen circumstances. The application is beneficial for public 
transport passengers in general, and it is now a widespread application used for all kinds of bus routes in 
city centres. The application enables passengers to plan their journey and use time more efficiently. It 
improves the convenience for passengers and it improves their mobility, as rescheduling becomes easy in 
case of delayed or cancelled departures (Harmony & Gayah 2017).  
5.3.6 Urban traffic management 
City logistics 
City logistic is a concept aiming at minimizing the number of half-empty trucks driving into city centres. 
Instead of operators and shippers delivering all types of goods from different manufacturers individually, 
the goods are delivered to a central outside city. The goods are here consolidated, sorted, and repacked in 
environmentally friendly vans, and it is then delivered to the customer collectively. To encourage central 
stores to utilize the city logistic concept, value-added-services can be used as a motivation factor. This 
could be everything from repacking of products, assembly product components from different 
manufactures before delivering the product to the shop, return of products, or it could be collection of 
waste. Further, the distribution centre could be used as a storage or depot for shops, as facilities in the city 
centre are expensive. The stored products can be delivered to the shop whenever needed, but preferably in 
connection with delivery of other goods in the city (Gammelgaard et al. 2016).   
==PAGE:56==
44 
 
Strictly speaking, city logistic is not an ITS application but simply a smart mobility solution that contributes 
to a cleaner environment in the city centre. The concept helps improve mobility for motorized vehicles as 
the number of space-demanding, half-empty trucks in the city are reduced, and it can have a positive 
impact on the finances of central stores as they can save money on storage facilities. However, in 
cooperation with ITS technologies, city logistics concept can be further improved. AVI applications can be 
used to manage and control the fleet of vans in real time and to optimize the routes depending on the 
traffic flow in the city. Driver and fuel monitoring technologies can be used to ensure that drivers drive in a 
safe and efficient manner. IT platforms can be used to help the shops control their inventory at the 
distribution centre, and to call for stored products (Oskarbski & Kaszubowski 2016). 
Waste collection and monitoring 
In a city, waste management is essential. By using ITS technologies (surveillance systems and IoT devices 
placed on trash cans) the waste collection system can be optimized. The IoT devices could be sensors that 
measure, log and transmit data wirelessly to the cloud. In Copenhagen, experiments with ultrasound 
sensors in trash cans have been tested that measure the height of trash in the bins. The system can also be 
combined with temperature, pollution, weight or movement sensors. In the Copenhagen experiment, 
sensor data were sent to the operation centre on a daily basis by WiC technologies, where different 
algorithms decide if the trash can should be emptied or not. Then, route optimization algorithms determine 
the shortest and fastest route for collecting the trash. Furthermore, they use artificial intelligence (or 
machine learning) to forecast future waste levels and to learn how best to collect the cans. Artificial 
intelligence assists the regular decision processes in cooperation with Geographic Information Systems 
(GIS) tools that are used to illustrate data analyses (Gutierrez et al. 2015).  
Another aspect of this application is the immediate support to truck drivers experiencing unexpected 
situations. This could be inaccessible trash cans. Drivers document the problem on location using cameras, 
apps or other means of digital communication, and if necessary, send in a short voice message describing 
the problem. The generated report is then handled semi-automatically by algorithms in the operation 
centre, and meanwhile the route is dynamically updated and the driver is free to continue to the next stop 
without the need to wait for response. Another advantage of dynamic route planning is the use of GNSS to 
provide actual traffic information. Then, in the case of congestion or accidents, the route can be changed 
dynamically (Medvedev et al. 2015). 
Experiments with smart waste management systems have shown that the application has relatively high 
deployment costs, but when implemented, the application can result in significant increases on cost-
effectiveness (Gutierrez et al. 2015; Medvedev et al. 2015). It is also expected that the optimization of 
routes containing only full trash cans will minimize the need for driving, and thereby have a positive impact 
on the environment and general mobility for motorized vehicles in the city. 
Shared vehicles 
Shared vehicles is an umbrella concept that refers to the share of cars and other vehicles. This can either be 
privately owned vehicles, vehicles owned by organizations or publicly owned vehicles. The concept in 
general does not involve ITS, but many of the services involve IT platforms or other electronic services that 
ease the operation and allow for communication with and between customers. In general, three different 
concepts of shared vehicles exists: privately owned shared vehicles (peer-to-peer), city cars (free floating), 
and shared vehicles with a fixed parking space (SVFPS) (Kristensen et al. 2018).    
==PAGE:57==
45 
 
Peer-to-peer shared vehicles is a concept where private people offer their car to interested tenants in a 
given period in exchange for a fee. Examples of organizations running this service is GoMore and SnappCar 
(Kristensen et al. 2018).    
City cars is a fleet of cars owned by an organization. These cars can be picked up and delivered anywhere 
inside the operating service area and at any time. Many city cars are electrical, and therefore, they must 
often be parked at a charging stations. Most city cars have an on-board unit (OBU) with a GNSS receiver to 
allow customers and operators to locate the cars, and allow customers to book a specific free car (see AVI 
specification for more details). Before the ride, the user may identify him/herself by using an NFC/RFID chip 
in a smart card or smart phone, and the payment will then be electronically transferred to the bank. The 
use of the car (the minute driven) determines the price of the ride (Kristensen et al. 2018).    
SVFPS are vehicles that are owned by a company. Users sign up for a subscription, allowing them to use the 
vehicles. In this concept, users pay based on mileage, time spent driving, and they pay a monthly fee. 
SVFPSs must be returned to the same fixed parking space after use. Municipalities can support this kind of 
shared vehicles by offering discounts on special parking spaces for the cars (Kristensen et al. 2018).    
The effects of shared vehicles are uncertain. Some studies suggest that shared vehicles will contribute to 
more traffic as drivers who normally would not invest in a car, now have the opportunity of driving 
(Kristensen et al. 2018). Other studies show that shared cars result in less traffic jam, more flexible mobility, 
and a reduced need for parking spaces in the city. These studies further emphasize that shared vehicles are 
expected to reduce environmental impacts (Kayser 2017). Beside the mobility and environmental aspects, 
shared vehicles might cause the users to save money. However, this advantage depends on the frequency 
of use, as short and infrequent trips are more economically beneficial than frequent and longer trips, 
compared to owning a car. Short and infrequent trips can be financially beneficial as the alternative may be 
taxi trips or a privately owned car with many regular expenses. On the other hand, a frequent use of this 
service can be more expensive than simply owning a private car or using ridesharing services. Shared 
vehicles can also impact socio-economics as the use of shared vehicles may steal travellers from public 
transportation, changing the profitability of public transportation (Kristensen et al. 2018). Overall, the 
effects on shared vehicles remain so far unknown.  
Bike sharing systems 
Bike sharing systems are services where (primarily publicly owned) bicycles are rented (especially by 
tourists) on a short term basis. The bikes can be picked up and delivered at specific points in the city (e.g. 
bike charging stations if the bikes are electrical). These points are usually near stations, shopping centres, 
tourist attractions, or major companies, as the intention with bike sharing systems is to deliver ‘last mile’ 
mobility options from stations to final location. Shared bikes are often equipped with several intelligent 
systems to make the bikes more attractive and to increase security (Mátrai & Tóth 2016). This could be a 
touch-screen with a build-in GNSS receiver (as in the case of Copenhagen) that allow customers to find the 
nearest bike and to navigate in the city. It also allow operators to monitor the fleet of bikes. The 
Copenhagen City Bikes have a locking system that communicates with the tablet, and it only unlocks if 
correct user and payment information is entered (Visit Copenhagen 2014).  
Several studies confirm that bike sharing systems have positive effects on the environment and private 
health (Bullock et al. 2017). A new study shows that bike sharing systems improve urban economy as well 
(see further explanations in Bullock et al. (2017)).  
==PAGE:58==
46 
 
Ridesharing 
Ridesharing fulfils the need for transport for more than one person, and it is usually used in cases where 
the driver and passenger have the same need for travel (in contrast to ordinary taxis and ‘ride-hailing’ 
services as Uber, where only the passenger has a need for travel). Three different kinds of ridesharing exist: 
open peer-to-peer ridesharing, co-worker ridesharing and shared taxis (Kristensen et al. 2018).  
Open peer-to-peer ridesharing allows commuters and travellers to coordinate rides, typically through an IT 
platforms. Examples of large platforms for open peer-to-peer ridesharing are GoMore and BlaBlaCar. Trips 
with open peer-to-peer ridesharing are often infrequent leisure or commuting trips (Kristensen et al. 2018).  
In the co-worker ridesharing concept, travellers share the same final destination, which most often is the 
workplace. Co-worker ridesharing has been an important focus point for political initiatives using IT 
platforms or interactive whiteboards at workplaces for motivating and facilitating the concept (Kristensen 
et al. 2018).  
Lastly, shared taxis aim to group travellers in the same taxi who would not normally travel together. Shared 
taxis can be an important part of public transportation in remote areas where public transportation is not a 
feasible solution. In some cities, Uber (Uber-Pool) facilitate this service, and sometimes it is seen at airports 
with limited or no public transport possibilities (Kristensen et al. 2018).   
A Danish study estimates that open peer-to-peer and co-worker ridesharing concepts contribute to 
increased mobility, and that the concepts result in released capacity of public transport. For shared taxis, 
experiences from metropolitan areas such as New York have shown that shared trips can be cheaper than a 
taxi trips, and that they can substitute ordinary taxis, cars and public transport options. For cities with lower 
population densities (such as Copenhagen), shared taxis have limited usability. Ridesharing concepts 
generally have a positive impact on the environment and private economics (Kristensen et al. 2018).   
Electronic toll collection 
Electronic toll collection (ETC) are applications that charge vehicles for driving on a road or for passing a 
tunnel or bridge without the vehicles having to stop. They eliminate the time-consuming and costly 
operations of manual payment. A variety of different ETC systems are used around world and they are 
based on different technologies. The congestion charging systems in Stockholm and London is based on 
ANPR technologies. Systems using this technology can be deployed relatively fast and are cost-effective, 
but the system can only handle domestic vehicles (ITS International 2010). 
Other ETC solutions require the driver to have an on-board unit (OBU) that identifies the vehicle and tells 
roadside readers that it is passing a toll point. These systems are often supplied by video surveillance to 
identify vehicles without an OBU. Most OBUs utilize DSRC technology to ensure WiC between the vehicle 
and the roadside unit. Newer ETC technologies use satellite positioning to charge vehicles for the actual 
distance driven on toll roads. This is seen in Germany were several technologies work together in an ETC 
application. The system (mainly directed towards trucks) consists of an OBU with DSRC and GNSS modules. 
The OBU measures speed and direction using on-board sensors (gyroscopes and accelerometers). The toll is 
charged automatically via a central data centre, cross-checking the vehicles position with known toll roads. 
Furthermore, gantries placed at key positions scan the vehicles to verify the position using ANPR, but they 
also identify the vehicle using the OBU. The gantries work as a redundancy check (Toll Collect 2018).  
ETC solutions improves the traffic flow on toll roads, and they reduce the extent of stop-and-go traffic 
which is fuel consuming and bad for the environment (ITS International 2010). 
==PAGE:59==
47 
 
E-Call 
The European Commission V2I initiative: E-Call, is an emergency system (installed in all new cars from April 
2018) that automatically dials 112 in the case of a serious road accident. The system is automatically 
activated in the moment of a serious accident via in-vehicle sensors (e.g. if the airbag is deployed), but it 
can also be activated by pressing a button. The latter is relevant for cars passing a road accident. When 
activated, the car uses public mobile wireless communications networks (cellular mobile phone networks) 
and the build-in GNSS system to immediately call 112. At the same time it sends a data-package telling the 
time of the accident, the location of the accident, the direction of travel (relevant for motorway or tunnel 
accidents), the triggering mode (either automatic or manual), and a vehicle identification number. The 
system also enables voice communication to the people in the car. The data message contains enough 
information for ambulances to respond even though no one in the car is capable of replying to questions 
from the emergency call centre. The European Commission has issued several regulations concerning data 
transmission, technical and functional specifications, which all ensure interoperability of E-Call within EU 
(Dossa 2018; Diani et al. 2018). 
With E-Call, the appropriate emergency call centre immediately gets informed about an accident and its 
position. Researchers expect reductions in the time of emergency response with up to 50 % outside cities 
and 40% in urban areas. The time savings reduce the severity of injuries and fatalities, and the accident site 
can be cleared faster. This means (presumably) increased mobility for motorized vehicles, fuel savings and 
reductions in CO2 emissions (Diani et al. 2018). 
Mobility as a Service (MaaS) 
The invention Mobility as a Service (MaaS), integrated in Helsinki in 2016 with the Whim application, is an 
initiative combining all journey planning in one platform, whether it is by train, bus, taxi, bike sharing 
systems or by car sharing concepts. MaaS combines several of the previously described technologies and 
applications in one system. Based on real-time traffic information on bus departures received from AVI 
systems, the road congestion information received by the TMC, and personal preferences, the application 
can select the most preferable combination of modes and departures. Furthermore, the application offers 
booking, electronic ticketing, and payment services across all modes (Warwick et al. 2017). 
The introduction of MaaS is still relatively new, and many cities are struggling with the creation of the 
comprehensive legwork needed for the roll-out of MaaS. MaaS’s impact on society remains unknown 
(Warwick et al. 2017), however, a pilot project in Gothenburg, Sweden, with 70 involved families showed 
overall economic savings on transport expenditures (Sochor et al. 2016). It is expected that the application 
can improve the general mobility for citizens in urban areas for both cars and VRUs.

logics.

6 Planning ITS projects 
ITS applications, and other smart mobility solutions, are beneficial for urban areas in many ways. A 
comprehensive network of ITS applications impact urban areas in a broad sense, but several applications 
affect the society in similar ways. Many application results in improved traffic safety or cleaner 
environments. Selecting the most appropriate ITS solution can seem difficult. Which way is the best to 
decide on the solution to implement? This chapter describes the fundamentals of system engineering and 
traditional transport planning processes, and it will elaborate on how traditional transport planning 
processes are insufficient for deciding on ITS solutions. Studies have shown that it is valuable to use a 
special type of system engineering process, the V-model, for the design and implementation of complex 
and multidimensional smart mobility solutions. This chapter will describe the V-model. 
6.1 System Engineering 
System engineering is an interdisciplinary methodology that aims to help develop a deployment plan for 
complex projects, and it helps identify the best solutions and their risks. System engineering supports 
holistic system developments by focusing on the entire life cycle of systems, including the description of 
goals and needs (conceptual design), the design of system and components (preliminary design, detailed 
design and development), the construction/production of components, and the final operation, 
maintenance and retirement of the system. System engineering supports the ‘system thinking’ mind-set 
through a sequential process, where the developer goes through one step at the time, and at each level 
more details are added. It transforms needs and requirements into system products and at the same time, 
it delivers support for decision makers (Zabic & Nielsen 2011).  
System engineering was originally used for projects involving complex requirements and high technical risks 
in the US Department of Defence program in the 1950s. Later, it has been applied in many other fields, 
especially those involving projects with high risks in case of program failures. Now, system engineering is 
used for many types of projects, both small, large, simple, and complex ones, as the degree of formality for 
system engineering process can vary depending on project scale and complexity (Zabic & Nielsen 2011).  
6.2 Traditional transport planning 
Traditional transport projects follow iterative system engineering procedures. They usually start by 
identifying local, regional, national (or even international) visions and goals concerning the planning of the 
overall transportation network. When a specific problem that needs to be resolved is recognized, a 
sequence of project tasks begins (Figure 9). First, the project needs and boundaries are recognised during 
the project scope and the overall financial limits are established. This leads to a brainstorming session 
where project alternatives are developed, and the best and most robust performing alternatives are 
selected and screened. Afterwards, baseline studies are performed where the existing conditions are 
analysed and evaluated such as transport facilities and usage, travel patterns, and land utilization. These 
studies clarify the actual need for interventions and they set the base for the project performance, as any 
intervention should outperform the ‘do-nothing’ alternative. Preliminary design suggestions are then 
developed. Project alternatives that are expected to have a major impact on the environment must 
afterwards undergo an environmental impact assessment (EIA) that aims to clarify, reduce, and monitor the 
expected environmental impacts. Next, the final project alternative is selected and consultants prepare the 
final design specifications based on inspections and field-studies, and they carry out the technical drawings 
and calculations. The contractor that submits a low bid and has sufficient experience with the type of 
project construction, constructs the project in accordance to the detailed specifications. After the opening, 
the relevant authority operate and maintain the infrastructure (Federal Highway Administration 2017; 
==PAGE:61==
49 
 
Lawrence 2005). At the end of its life (planned life-time is often 50 years), the development undergoes 
rehabilitation. For more information about traditional transport planning, see Olesen (2016).

6.3 How ITS projects stand out from traditional transport projects 
ITS solutions use other means to solve traffic congestion than the traditional way of increasing the roadway 
capacity by constructing new roads or more lanes. ITS solutions address the problem with advanced traffic 
control, incident management, electronic toll collection or advanced vehicle systems. These solutions are 
technically complicated and they often integrate different modes of transport and different technical 
disciplines. Thus, they requiring planning across multiple authorities and professions. Partnerships and 
networking, both subjects important in smart governance, are essential for the realisation and operation of 
ITS applications. The use of advanced technologies creates challenges for ITS project managers, as they 
constitute new sources of failures. The technology is changing rapidly, which entails a high need for 
maintenance, operation and upgrade elements (investment), but it also means that ITS projects usually 
have a shorter lifetime before they need replacement. ITS projects rarely need an environmental clearance, 
and they are narrow in geographical scope. In the traditional transport planning process, consultants 
prepare design specifications and contractors implement the project. This constellation is unattractive for 
ITS projects, as these projects require close collaboration and integration between the design, 
development, and implementation phases. Many ITS projects remain unproven and untested, which means 
that the lists of specifications are short and the level of experience is low. The traditional transport process 
is often based on existing routines and experiences, e.g. each project usually consider the same generic list 
of impacts (see more in Olesen (2016)).

The above arguments imply that the traditional transport process is insufficient for ITS projects. Studies by

system approvals, but the tasks and activities within each phase varies for the two processes.  
6.4 The Vee Development Model 
The Vee Development Model (or the V-model) is the model within system engineering recommended for 
ITS projects (Zabic & Nielsen 2011; Federal Highway Administration 2009) in the US. The V-model is a top-
down and bottom-up process5 that helps create an understanding of the complexity of a system, and helps 
ensure that the final execution is in accordance with the identified needs and requirements. Figure 11 
illustrates the V-model and its relationship with the life cycle tasks for ITS projects (shown in Figure 10). It 
shows how the traditional linear system life cycle has been modified into a V-shaped technical 
development model, indicating the on-process of designing the project in more and more detail (top-
down), and afterwards, the continuous executing and testing of more and more elements (bottom-up).

The V-model was originally shaped like an actual ‘V’, but after the adaptation of the V-model for ITS 
projects, wings were added to show how ITS project developments using V-models fits within the broader 
transportation planning process (and governance models in general, as the one in Figure 3). The left wing 
indicates the overall transport planning process using ITS architectures and general project specific 
activities. This includes feasibility studies and the definition of regional needs and concerns. The right wing 
shows the tasks after the project development, including operation, maintenance and upgrade activities. 
The gap between phase 0 and 1 and between phase 5 and 6 indicates where the process goes from overall 
(political) planning/management to project-specific planning/management.  
The V-model is divided into two sides. The left side of the model (until the bottom of the V) decomposes 
the system into progressively more detail; from the overall concept and into system specifications, 
definition of subsystems and, finally, into component design. This side of the V-model specifies needs, 
requirements, goals and design specifications, and it is driven by smart governance key activities such as 
stakeholder involvement and strategy formulation. At the bottom of the V-model, the system is 
made/developed/fabricated. The right side of model ensures verification, validation and integration of the 
components, subsystems and overall system. This side of the model includes the long-term operation and 
maintenance period. The symmetry of the V-model reflects the relationships between the two sides, e.g., 
the system definition and requirements on the left side is used for system verification on the right side. 
Tasks on both sides of the V-model have iterative interactions, though in the ideal use of the model, 
iterative interactions are unnecessary. Especially backward movement is undesirable, but it is often 
impossible to avoid, e.g., it might turn out that a design option cannot be implemented, and that the design 
specifications needs to be adjusted on component, subsystem or system levels (Zabic & Nielsen 2011). To 
minimize the risk of having to move backwards in the model, and avoid using extra resources on the 
development process, each task in the V-model ends at a decision gate. The intention of the decision 
points/gates is to gain support and approval from the system’s owner and stakeholders. Consequently, they 
ensure that the work prepared in the current phase has been completed successfully based on the entrance 
and exit criteria for each phase. If so, they give acceptance to move forward to the next phase of the 
project life cycle (Federal Highway Administration 2009). 
6.4.1 Process activities 
This section describes the activities in the V-model systematically based on information in Federal Highway 
Administration (2009) and US Department of Transportation (2007). If any other references are used, they 
are noted in the text.  
 ITS Architecture 
An ITS architecture is developed at an overall governance level, and the intention is to use it as a general 
tool for planning, defining, designing and implementing all kinds of ITS applications, and it should be 
connected with general long term transportation plans (Molinete et al. 2016). The aim is to ensure that 
implemented systems have compatibility, expandability, interoperability, and that they fulfil certain 
standards (Mandzuka 2015). It usually covers an explanation of functions required for ITS systems, 
requirements for system partitioning, and an explanation of information and data flows that connect 
entities into an integrated system (Molinete et al. 2016). A well-functioning ITS architecture allows for 
economy of scale in production and distribution, which minimizes costs of system components. It also helps 
create an open market by having standard interfaces, and thereby, it helps avoid vendor lock-in situations. 
It eases the integration between multiple systems by creating interoperability between systems produced 
by different manufactures, and it creates consistency in the information reaching end-users.  
==PAGE:64==
52 
 
There are significant differences between ITS architecture approaches around the world. In Europe, the ITS 
architecture from 2000 (called the European ITS Framework Architecture – in short called FRAME) mainly 
focuses on users’ needs and functionalities. The US architecture (called the National ITS Architecture) 
covers several elements, but primarily it emphasizes physical abilities allowing for interoperability and 
economy of scale. The ITS architecture in Japan uses an object oriented methodology (Mandzuka 2015). 
FRAME is not a real ITS architecture but rather a flexible framework that is targeted to help individual 
European countries and regions tailor their own architecture and requirements (Molinete et al. 2016; 
Mandzuka 2015).  
The introduction of FRAME has had limited effects on the ITS development as only very few countries6 have 
adopted it. Many ITS architecture intentions have therefore been moved to EU Directive 2010/40/EU laying 
out the framework for a coordinated and effective deployment and use of ITS within Europe. However, as 
for any EU directives, this directive is not directly applicable in each member country, and it should 
therefore be adapted to national legislation in order to achieve its goals (Mandzuka 2015). South-east 
European countries have used the directive to create their own ITS architecture (called SEE-ITS), which is a 
framework for ITS deployment in the field of road transport. It seeks at creating cooperation, 
harmonization and interoperability between isolated ITS projects in the involved countries (Aifadopoulou et 
al. 2014). 
Concept exploration/feasibility study 
Based on prioritisations and designations of important needs, problems and goals related to the local 
transport system (ideally highlighted in a local ITS architecture), a project team and a group of stakeholders 
make a broad analysis and assessment of several fundamentally different conceptual alternatives that 
accommodate the overall needs, problems, and goals. For each alternative, an initial CBA is created, the 
technical, political, and operational feasibility and viability is evaluated, and the major project risks are 
identified. In the concept exploration step, all analyses remain on a high level, but at the same time, the 
assessments must be sufficiently detailed to verify the concepts’ workability and reliability.   
Based on feasibility and viability studies of the different concepts, the most feasible (and still viable) 
concept is selected using a trade study technique. The selection process should consider combining or 
dividing conceptual alternatives in order to obtain a better result. After deciding on an alternative, the 
system owner develops a master plan and schedules clarifying the plans and deadlines needed in the 
projects (only on a high-level basis). This makes up the project framework.  
Concept of operations 
The Concept of operation sets the initial definition and frame of the overall system. This includes a 
description of system capabilities and of how the system will be operated and maintained seen from the 
viewpoint of operators, maintainers and managers. A detailed assessment of the needs should be made, 
and it should include a description of how the system will meet the needs and expectations of 
stakeholders. The Concept of operation should also include an agreement on KPIs on how the system will 
be validated and evaluated at the end of the project development process.  
Overall, the Concept of operations will answer the following who, what, where, when, why and how 
questions: Who is to be involved in the project (stakeholders)? What are the high-level capabilities of the 
system? Where will the system to be implemented? When is the different activities to be performed? Why 
                                                          
6 Austria, Czech Republic, Italy, and France. Whereas the Netherlands, UK, and Scotland have made their own version 
of a national ITS architecture. 
==PAGE:65==
53 
 
is the system important and how will the system address the existing problems? How will the system be 
developed, operated and maintained? These questions defines the fundamental basis of the upcoming 
step: System requirements.  
System requirements 
In this step, the stakeholder needs identified under the Concept of operations are reviewed, analysed, and 
transformed into verifiable requirements. The requirements state what the system is to do (but not how to 
do it), how well it must accomplish the tasks, and under which conditions. The requirements should be 
directly linked to the needs assessment. A number of stakeholders are advised to follow this process 
closely. The requirements should then be transformed into verifications in a system verification plan. 
High-level design and Detailed design 
The steps until now primarily focused on problem identification. The system designs (high-level and 
detailed design), also called the project architecture, are the first steps that really focus on the solution. 
Systems are divided into subsystems that are further divided into components of hardware, software, 
database elements, processes, etc. All of the previously defined system requirements are assigned to the 
subsystems until the system is fully defined and decomposed. For each component and subsystem, details 
are designed and specifications are documented. The interfaces between the subsystems and components 
need to be defined and described in detail too. All subsystem and component specifications are 
transformed into verifications that are documented in the subsystem verification plan and unit verification 
plan, respectively. The design steps also include consideration and calculation of development and 
operation costs. These must be in line with the specified requirements concerning costs. The design steps 
terminate at the decision gate, sometimes called the Critical Design Review.  
Software/hardware development 
The hardware is fabricated, the software is coded, databases are implanted, physical elements are 
constructed, and off-the shelf items are configured and modified after the detailed specifications. In short, 
all the components specified in the system design are developed. The activities should be periodically 
reviewed and inspected by the system’s owner, partners and stakeholders. Unit test procedures can be 
developed to demonstrate how the components will meet the detailed design specifications.  
Unit testing 
All components are tested in accordance with the unit verification plan to verify if all delivered components 
meet the detailed design plan. The Unit testing aims to identify as many defects as possible. All coding are 
tested for all conceived error cases, and test cases are set up to exercise the hardware/software 
components. All recovered defects are analysed and corrected, and the testing processes do not stop until 
all known defects are resolved.    
Subsystem and system verification 
The components are integrated in the subsystems, which are then verified according to the subsystem 
verification plan. The same is done for the systems based on the system verification plan. The system 
verification is divided into two parts. In part one, the system is verified in a controlled environment (factory 
test), and in part two, the system is verified in the environment that it is about to operate in (on-site 
test/verification). After a successful system verification, the system is integrated into its planned 
operational environment. The system deployment can take some time, as many system issues do not 
appear until it is integrated in the real environment for a longer time. These issues could be memory leaks, 
==PAGE:66==
54 
 
unexpected delays between signals, or other errors that might only occur under specific and uncommon 
conditions. Finally, the project development team hands over the project to the organisation, agency, or 
another entity, owning the system.  
System validation 
When the system has been accepted and paid for, the system’s owner makes its own validation to confirm 
that the system fulfils its intended use. Preferably, these activities are performed in a close partnership 
with experts in the area. This is a key step for the system’s owner and stakeholders, where they assess the 
system performance against the identified needs, goals, and the listed expectations from the Concept of 
operations. The identified new needs and requirements may then constitute targets for the next evolution 
of similar systems.  
Operation, maintenance, changes and upgrades 
In these steps, the intended operation is carried out, and routine system maintenance and staff training are 
performed based on performance measures. New releases of hardware and software are installed, and 
identified issues, suggested improvements, and technical upgrades are documented and implemented 
when funds become available. These steps make up the longest part of the project that does not end until 
the final retirement or replacement of the system. 
Retirement/replacement 
Transport planners and political agencies continuously monitor and assess the needs for transport changes 
and the utilization of the systems, and they decide on changes/upgrades of different developments. These 
decisions include retirement or replacement decisions. Maybe the need for the system has disappeared 
after the introduction of another system, maybe the system is not cost-effective to operate anymore 
compared with the costs of developing a new system, or the system might no longer be maintainable due 
to obsolescence of system components.   
Cross cutting activities 
Stakeholder involvement is a key element in the V-model gaining a valid set of system requirements - 
requirements that the entire process depends on. Without stakeholder involvement, the project team 
cannot get insight the needed understanding of the current problems and needs, and they cannot construct 
satisfactory system requirements. 
Project planning is a central element in the process. Project planning activities starts early in the process 
and it involves the planning for project activities, resources, budget and timelines. The two most important 
plans in the V-models are: the Project Plan (PP) and the System Engineering Management Plan (SEMP). The 
PP documents are all administrative and technical tasks needed for each project activity, and the SEMP 
describes how the different engineers and designers will be integrated, monitored, and controlled during 
the process. The PP and SEMP are also used for project monitoring, ensuring that the project follows time 
and cost schedules.  
Risk management is used for identification and evaluation of risky impacts and their probabilities, and it 
helps set the foundation for the mitigation and surveillance measures of potential risks during the entire 
process. Risk management seeks to identify potential problems before they occur, and to monitor the 
system to allow for action when risky tasks take place. Risk management can be an essential activity to 
ensure that the cost and time schedule can be followed.  
==PAGE:67==
55 
 
Configuration management is an activity that ensures system integrity throughout the process. The activity 
continuously confirms whether the project’s performance, functionality, and physical abilities match the 
requirements, standards and planned design. The observations and changes to items throughout the 
process are documented in a Configuration Management Plan. The documentation ensures that all needs 
can be traced back to stakeholder requirements, and that all requirements are implemented, verified, and 
validated. The documentation should also include considerations concerning what worked and which 
activities could be improved. These thoughts are important when learning for future projects, and they 
enable future work to be more effective. Hence, it is an important element in the Repeat and share 
learning activity in the governance model in Figure 3. 
6.4.2 The benefits of using the V-model 
Identify the problem before implementing the solution - that is a key principle behind the V-model. It 
sounds like a very basic procedure, but it is not unusual that the process goes the other way around. 
Sometimes, new applications, construction projects or technologies appear to be extremely good. So good 
that it is decided to realise the project without considerations of local needs. These are then created 
afterwards to fit the project benefits (Sureit 2017). Problems can usually be solved in many ways, and 
having decided on a solution impede the ability to discover if other solutions can address the problem in a 
better way. In the V-model, problems and needs for a new system are clarified at the beginning of the 
process by the use of stakeholder participation. The model helps transform customer problems and needs 
to design requirements and technical competencies, which increase the likelihood of the final 
implementation meeting users’ needs. Furthermore, technologies are constantly changing making it 
inopportune to decide on technologies early in the process. The V-model delay technical choices until right 
before the development task (Federal Highway Administration 2009; US Department of Transportation 
2007). 
The V-model decomposes a system into smaller subsystems that are manageable and easy to understand. 
This makes the model beneficial for large and complex projects that use state-of-the-art technologies, 
involve many expenditures, long time scales and/or involve significant risks. The V-model reduces the risk 
of schedule and cost overruns, and it reduces the risk of having to make costly project changes at the end 
of the project. The latter is ensured by an early creation of customer requirements and through ongoing 
approvals of the work by stakeholders and system owners (Federal Highway Administration 2009; US 
Department of Transportation 2007). 
Documentation is a central element of the V-model. Having good documentation ensures traceability 
between the project tasks and with it comes a higher certainty that the final system is directly connected to 
the users’ needs identified in the project start-up phase. Documentation of all project related processes 
leads to improved verification of functionalities, resulting in fewer defects (Federal Highway Administration 
2009; US Department of Transportation 2007). 
6.5 Individual process selection depending on the degree of risk involved 
Not all ITS applications involve major risks associated with the use of technology, and not all ITS 
applications are multidimensional and depends on several transport authorities or technical disciplines. The 
need for the comprehensive V-model may differ from project to project. For some projects it might be 
sufficient to simply use the traditional transport planning process or other similar processes.

6.6 The V-model in a smart governance perspective 
The V-model respects the key aspects of smart governance presented in Chapter 3.2: 1) It is based on 
stakeholder involvement and clear strategies, 2) It requires strong leadership to finish the comprehensive 
model with the many tasks within the time and cost schedule, 3) it imposes transparency requirements, 
and 4) the best results are often obtained by the use of partnerships. The V-model can also be seen as a 
process within the smart governance framework in Figure 3. Comparing the steps of the V-model with the

steps in the smart governance model, it can be seen that the two models cover similar actions. Figure 13

Figure 13 – The V-model seen in the perspective of the smart governance model 
An ITS architecture specifies how different ITS components should interact with one another to help 
address a regional transport problem. It should serve as a general framework upon which to plan, design, 
organise and integrate systems in the region to realize identified visions (Chrowdhury & Sadek 2003). The 
ITS architecture is based on regional drivers of changes, local value propositions, and regional visions. A 
regional ITS architecture is usually devised by multiple jurisdictions and stakeholders in common operations 
of adjacent areas (Chrowdhury & Sadek 2003). This indicates that ITS architectures require engagement of 
relevant departments in the city administration and stakeholders. The ITS architecture should also consider 
financial aspects, lead to preparations on administrative city department capacities, and it should include 
considerations concerning organisational structures. Thus, the ITS architecture includes the smart 
governance activities: Clarify opportunities, Take ownership, Engage, and Prepare. 
After the agreement and investment in a project or program, the Act step in the smart governance model 
begins, covering the top-down activities in the V-model. The Act step is continuously supplemented by the 
cross-cutting Check and reflect activity by the use of stakeholder involvement, decision gates and 
configuration management, aiming at checking the system performance against the requirements. The 
primary Check and reflect activity takes place under the system verification and validation steps in the V-
model, where the actual performance is measured. The Repeat and share learning activities take place 
regularly using cross-cutting activities as documentation, process improvement considerations, and 
configuration management. After the project phase, learnings should be shared with a regional, national 
and/or international network of relevant stakeholders.

6.7 ITS project planning in Denmark 
Online research and examination of publicly available documents concerning recommendations addressing 
the planning process of Danish ITS projects yielded no results. The research did not reveal how Danish ITS 
projects are planned, nor did it show consistency in the planning methods of different ITS projects. 
However, Denmark has a national instruction addressed to the planning of IT projects. It is a mandatory 
tool for IT project managers at state institutions to support the daily work with IT projects, but the 
presented instructions are superficial and not addressed towards transport projects. The instruction merely 
highlights a five step phase model including: an idea phase, analysis phase, acquisition phase, development 
phase, and an implementation phase. The idea phase examines if the overall idea is sustainable 
economically, and whether the idea is good enough to proceed to the analysis phase. In the analysis phase, 
the project is defined and described, and risks are estimated. The requirements, needs and the acquisition 
process is described in the acquisition phase, and in the development phase, the project is designed and 
tested. The implementation phase includes project launching (Agency for Digitalisation 2016). Thus, Danish 
IT projects can currently be planned, evaluated and selected based on different sporadic processes, 
information and requests as long as they have an idea, analysis, acquisition, development, and 
implementation phase, which are relatively generic parts of any projects. 
Consequently, it has been concluded in this study that Denmark has no official, publicly available 
requirements for how to plan, evaluate, design and implement ITS projects specifically. Maybe the lack of 
requirements are caused by the fact that most ITS projects remain limited in scale, and it has therefore not 
been relevant to create any planning specifications for ITS projects.

7 Case study 
There are no official, publicly available requirements in Denmark that specifies how to plan, design, and 
evaluate ITS projects. The traditional transport planning procedure is insufficient for ITS projects with high 
risk characteristics, such as projects relying on new technologies or projects relying on multiple agencies for 
their implementation and operation. US studies have concluded that the lack of clear ITS process 
specifications results in time and schedule overruns. As a result, they have created a handbook for how to 
plan ITS projects recommending the use of V-model (see Chapter 6.4). Their studies conclude that the V-
model is a valuable model for certain ITS projects. Whether the V-model is beneficial for ITS projects in a 
Danish context, is unknown. This question is investigated in the follow chapter using a case study. 
The case will examine the benefits and limitations of the top-down activities of the V-model for an ITS 
project in Danish settings. The case study is based on the processes, lists and system designs described in 
Chapter 6, and it builds on the context of the Copenhagen area (covering Copenhagen municipality and 
Frederiksberg municipality, see Figure 14). The red markings at Figure 15 illustrate the phases and activities 
included in the case. As illustrated, the case study will focus on the left side of the V-model. It aims at 
exemplifying how this part of the process works and how different methods can be utilized. The case study

conclusions typically require extensive data collections, detailed evaluations, analyses and interactions with 
stakeholders. The time and resources for such evaluations have not been available to the extent required

Copenhagen is facing several challenges in the near future. The population increases by 1000 inhabitants

failures. However, the consequences are small as a failure in the system just brings the function of the 
parking spaces back to the current situation. These alternatives are expected to have significant effects on 
the limitation of non-permitted cars parking on the reserved parking spaces, but the LED lighting alternative 
is cheaper to implement and operate than the alternative with dynamic signs. The alternative suggesting 
dynamic bollards is very effective and expensive, but it comes with major risks as a failure in the system can 
cause the parking space to be inaccessible (if the bollard will not lower).

alternative is chosen due to the many benefits, the relatively low costs and the limited risks. Even though 
the inspection alternative seems unattractive in the quasi-CBA and risk analysis, the alternative can be 
attractive when combined with the LED lighting alternative. The high costs of the inspection alternative are 
associated with the manually checking procedures. This risk can be minimized by integrating automatic 
legality checks in the system. The high risks concerning the lack of prioritisation in the public parking 
attendant sector can be reduced by contracting with a private parking firm that will be in charge of all the 
automatic inspection activities and invoice creations (assuming that the municipalities accept inspections

7.4 System requirements 
The System requirement step in the V-model (Figure 19) clarifies requirements directly or indirectly related 
to the system. System requirements are defined as “something that governs what, how well, and under 
what conditions a product will achieve a given purpose" (US Department of Transportation 2007). System 
requirements specify what the system is intended to do, not how. This subsection of the case study will 
consider system requirements on four levels, and it will present a segment of the associated system 
verification plan. 
==PAGE:90==
78 
 
 
Figure 19 - The activities in the project clarifying the system requirements 
7.4.1 Development of requirements 
System requirements define the functionality of the system (what it is supposed/required to do) and under 
which conditions it is required to operate (constraints). The system requirements are divided into four 
levels (inspired by Zabic & Nielsen (2011)): 
 
Functional requirements 
 
Non-functional requirements (or performance requirements) 
 
Operational requirements  
 
Policy requirements 
The functional requirements define what the system is supposed to do, and which functions the system 
must have to fulfil the project goal. The non-functional requirements are also called performance 
requirements or quality attributes (Gross & Yu 2001). They define how the system is supposed to fulfil the 
requirements, and how well they must be fulfilled to obtain satisfactory results. The operational 
requirements clarify the operational part of the system by defining who/what ensures operation of system 
functions and how the functions are to be operated. Finally, the policy requirements cover general business 
constraints such as construction and maintenance costs, human resources, laws, requirements, schedules, 
etc. Most of these are usually defined in the early steps by the system client (Zabic & Nielsen 2011). 
This case study will focus on defining the functional requirements, but it will also highlight some of the non-
functional and operational requirements. The policy requirements are assumed of minor relevance to the 
case study as they are primarily defined by the system client. Therefore, the policy requirements are 
excluded from the case study. Note that all requirements are based on presumed numbers and capabilities, 
though many of the numbers and capabilities are based on inspiration from similar products. 
Functional requirements 
The basic functional requirements for the VDI system are already given by the high-level capabilities in the 
Concept of operation step. These defined that the VDI system must “detect a vehicle on a specific parking

7.4.2 System Verification Plan 
The system verification plan focuses on the verification procedures for the defined system requirements. It 
is important to include all relevant system requirements in the system verification plan, as this is the only 
mean of system requirements that is included in the final system requirement document. In the system 
verification plan, a verification method is identified for each requirement. The typical verification 
techniques are: test, demonstration, inspection and analysis. In the test technique, direct measurements 
are performed of the system operation, and the outputs of the test are then be used to verify if the 
requirements are met. For the demonstration technique, the system operation is witnessed (usually this is 
only done for the complete system, such as the demonstration of an alarm). The demonstration verification 
does not depend on measurement data. Inspection verifications include direct observations such as 
dimensions, physical characteristics, and software language. Finally, the analysis verification is based on 
mathematical, logical, simulation and geographical techniques which heavily rely on available data.  
A verification plan usually includes a brief explanation of the purpose of the requirement, the 
method/technique for verification, who is to perform the verification and how often, and when the 
verification should be completed. A verification plan with the above listed information has not been made 
in the case due to time limitations. Instead, a short segment of the verification plan has been created for 
the purpose of demonstration. The verification plan in Table 25 only contains the functional requirements 
in the building block: User Service Management Function and Vehicle Parking Determination Function.

7.5 System design 
The V-model is systematically divided into two system design phases, a high-level design (or architectural 
design) and a detail design phase (Figure 20). The architectural design defines the overall functional or 
logical structure of the systems and subsystems, and it estimates detailed development and operation costs 
that comply with the cost specifications. In the detailed design, subsystems are divided into components 
that are carefully described and defined to meet system requirements. 
The case study focuses on the high-level functional architecture of the system. It divides the functional 
building blocks (presented earlier in the case) into subsystems. The subsystems are then assigned to a 
physical unit (field unit, central unit, or customer unit), and the subsystem activities are described using 
activity diagrams. For the sake of demonstration, a short detailed design subsection has been included in 
the case study, otherwise the case will not demonstrate the detail design phase. The detail design 
subsection divides a segment of the VDI functions and sub-functions into components. The system design 
should include the creation of a subsystem verification plan, unit test plan, and calculations of detailed 
development and operation costs that comply with the cost specifications. These parts have been excluded 
from the case study due to time, data and resource limitations.

7.7 Reflections on the case study  
The selection of alternatives and technologies has been based on literature studies of well-known ITS 
applications and the technologies they use. The idea behind the case study was also to demonstrate how 
different technologies can be used and how the V-model might be beneficial in Danish settings. The 
selected technologies demonstrate how sensor technologies, WiC, and computational logics can be used. 
However, a project goal can always be achieved in more than one way. The case study merely looked at 
one combination of technologies, and the best combination may not be the one selected in this study. 
Installing cameras on each parking space is expensive, and the cameras might be vulnerable to vandalisms 
(e.g. colouring the lenses). Vehicle detection can also be obtained by WiC technologies, where a chip is 
installed in all vehicles and a reader is placed at each parking space. The reader and chip can then 
communicate using DSRC or RFID, and the communication range can be adjusted to an appropriate 
distance. The use of WiC might be cheaper to purchase and operate than the camera technology, but it 
would not allow for the same opportunities for invoice creation as the camera technology. In a real project, 
several detailed design opportunities should be considered, and new technical solutions or ITS applications 
under development that might be relevant for the project should also be taken into account. 
During the case study, several choices have been made and limitations have been set. This was necessary to 
arrive at a product design within the time restrictions of the thesis. It is possible that the output of the 
product would have looked different if fewer limitations had been necessary throughout the process. 
However, the purpose of the case study was not to arrive at the best product with the most realistic design. 
Instead, the focus has been on the demonstration of the V-model and its functionalities, and therefore, the 
limitations have not been critical for this case study, while in a real project process they would have.  
The case study followed the steps of the V-model to test its practicality in Danish settings, as Denmark have 
no official, publicly available requirements specifying procedures for ITS projects. In Denmark, it is likely 
that the specified project would simply undergo the traditional transport process – or maybe no system 
engineering process at all. In both cases, less time and energy would be spent on specifying and defining 
the problems and needs, and instead, the attention would be directed towards the solution specification 
earlier on. Many projects do not spend enough energy on planning before they get into the solutions, and it 
is not unusual that the project target changes during the process (Sureit 2017).The traditional process 
leaves out the comprehensive system requirement phase, and it is often seen that it omits the creation of 
the project specific goals (Olesen 2016). The result would be a reduced perspective of the needs that the 
problem should solve, and less perspective on the overall project and its functions. It is not unlikely that the 
project would be designed using trial-and-error techniques, which could easily result in missing functions 
and unnecessary initiatives due to the lack of overall project perspective.  
In the case study, the V-model originated from smart governance principles and not from an ITS 
architecture, as Copenhagen does not have an ITS architecture that specifies the local needs and problems, 
and outline and prioritize projects that could be solved by ITS applications. Thus, the clarification of the 
overall problem was identified based on a SWOT analysis, mobility visions and strategies of the city. In a 
real world project, the preliminary examinations should also contain an analysis of current and planned 
activities and projects, a value proposition that specifies short and long term values, a clarification of 
procurement and financial procedure, and much more. Making these analyses for all ITS projects would be 
repetitive. It would be more beneficial to base the problem identification for all projects on thorough 
studies of the city’s needs, problems and procedures presented in a local ITS architecture.

8 Discussion 
The analyses and evaluations in the previous chapters demonstrated the complexity of smart cities and 
how the current development evolves in different directions. This chapter elaborates on the complexity of 
creating smart cities and on how the development can be further challenged by the upcoming autonomous 
cars. It will also discuss some of the difficulties and opportunities of utilizing the V-model in Danish settings.   
8.1 The smart city as a wide-ranging, undefinable concept 
The literature study showed that the concept of smart cities covers several elements, including economy, 
environment, governance, ICT, quality of life, and mobility. It covers elements from numerous sectors, and 
it even covers other conceptual concepts such as sustainability, innovation, connectivity, or socially 
cohesive cities. To develop a smart city, planners argue that the city has to integrate economy, 
environment, governance, ICT, quality of life, and mobility aspects in one holistic vision, thereby boosting 
management efficiencies (Barrionuevo et al. 2012). With the broad concept, all initiatives and interventions 
can be argued to fall within the box labelled ‘smart city development’. Part of what makes the concept of 
smart city difficult to understand and agree on is its atomistic and self-contained nature. It is a concept, a 
movement, or a phenomenon that you cannot easily draw a boundary around, nor can you observe the 
whole. It is comprised by huge, sprawling, interacting systems – it is a system of systems. The concept is so 
complex that it is impossible to account for its past actions and to foresee the next (Sadowski 2016). But is 
it fair to include the many individual concepts, movements, systems, and city elements under one notion: 
the smart city? If the notion covers all our suggestions and ambitions to improve the way we live in cities, 
including our political systems, social aspects, environment initiatives and economic challenges, should we 
then question the means and ends, the rhetoric and actions of the concept? The technological 
development and the increasing technological capabilities naturally makes ICT a part of urban systems. In 
the future this tendency will undoubtedly continue. It might be redundant to place a smart city label on all 
of our technological and life-improving initiatives. Having one name for all urban development does not 
make sense.   
It will be easier for politicians, citizens and developers to discourse propagations, and to plan, understand, 
realise, and accept which ideologies drive the city forward, if we divide the perception of smart cities into 
smaller conceptual systems. Having a clear understanding of a concept is essential for united development, 
fair competition, and acceptance, and making a clear understanding is almost impossible for a concept that 
includes almost all current initiatives.    
8.2 Behavioural changes are equally important to technological interventions 
Technological changes control most of the present urban development projects using a top-down 
approach. Projects using the traditional top-down approach focus on technological uses, on the decision-
making processes, and on interactions between project actors such as governments, operators and the 
society as a whole. This thesis investigates the benefits and limitations of a top-down approach, and the 
related requirements for new governance systems. Smart city applications within the transport field, calls 
for an approach that involves more collaboration and interaction between actors, and a more goal-
orientated procedure that carefully examines the needs and problems before deciding on the intervention 
(European Commission 2014). Smart development cannot, and should not, only be controlled as pure top-
down projects. The way people interact and behave in their efforts to become more efficient are equally 
important.  
Bottom-up projects looks at how groups of people interact and behave with new technologies and what 
they do to live more efficiently. Saving energy through behavioural changes, giving up the personal 
==PAGE:106==
94 
 
freedom of owning a private car, or switching from the car mode to public transport are all bottom-up 
initiatives that can be much more difficult to overcome than top-down projects. Yet, these concerns are 
important for the realisation of smart visions. Hilton & Marsh (2017) put it this way: “Technology is only as 
good as the people that use it, so behaviour change, inspired by city leadership, is essential”.  
Bottom-up projects look into existing bottlenecks or barriers that prevent the uptake of new technologies 
(European Commission 2014). The rationality of humans is bounded by human cognitive abilities and 
affected by biases, and bottom-up projects can help appeal to cognitive systems that for some reason block 
the willingness to change behaviour. This could be cognitive filtering. Cognitive filtering covers the 
phenomenon of humans using intuition to filter information to choose the preferred option, which often 
leads to disregard of new information or over-confidence in own decisions. This bias is strongly affected by 
the fact that old habits die hard. We tend to do as we have always done, no matter if new research tells us 
that we could or should do something differently. Human behaviour is often habitual, based on shortcuts 
and routines, and often the outcome is not in our own best interest. Furthermore, humans respond to 
social pressure and social environments. We imitate what others do; especially those in our own social 
group or in a group that we want to be a part of. This makes it difficult to change people’s lifestyle as an 
entire social group has to decide upon the same change of behaviour (The World Bank 2015). Even though 
the V-model uses aspects of both the top-down and bottom-up approach, it does not cover the importance 
of recognizing human habits and appealing to human cognitive limitations in an attempt to achieve greater 
effects of urban initiatives. 
8.3 Evaluation of the potential of ITS applications 
In the transport sector, the use of ITS has been a reality for some time, and it is used as a key enabler for 
improved transport systems several places in the world with documented effects. The ITS solutions listed in 
this report seek to optimize traffic flow, safety, efficiency or reduce environmental impacts. Each 
application affects the society in different ways. However, for several applications the effects on society are 
small. Studies by the Danish Road Directorate have shown that dynamic traffic signs only increase the 
capacity on motorways by approximately 3 % (Rambøll 2015). For applications such as shared vehicle 
systems and dynamic signs for bikes, the effects have not or cannot be measured. These examples show 
that we need a better understanding of costs and benefits of ITS projects, including the effects that 
transcends a single application, but which are broad up by the system as a whole. An understanding and 
acceptance of the effect of ITS is essential for future development of technologies in the transport sector.  
A Danish expert group concludes that there are unrealized opportunities of ITS in Denmark - ITS that seeks 
to increase the capacity of the Danish transport system, especially when it comes to optimizations of traffic 
management and traffic signals (Kristensen et al. 2018). Improved setting and management of existing 
signal-controlled intersections can lead to high mobility gains in many places for limited investments. The 
group points out that the lack of realisation of the opportunities could be caused by organizational barriers 
such as the lack of cooperation between municipalities, and the fact that municipalities are unwilling to pay 
for the benefits of travellers from other municipalities travelling through their area. Though it could also be 
caused by a lack of awareness of the opportunities or invisibility of the gains. It is important to propagate 
the awareness of ITS opportunities.  
The expert group underline that coordinated traffic management across administrative boundaries and 
data sharing can improve traffic information and help handle operational disturbances. Traffic companies 
and transport operators currently collect large amounts of data about travellers, but the sharing and 
integration of data does not take place. Working across organizational silos would improve the future 
development of ITS and traffic systems in general.

8.4 The future of ITS applications 
The development of smart city initiatives continues around the world with increased utilization of IoT in 
urban spaces. Transport ticket machines are replaced by phone applications, driverless buses will take us 
where we want to go, and MaaS will be deployed (Kristensen et al. 2018). These ITS (or C-ITS) initiatives will 
change our transportation habits in ways that current ITS applications have not managed.  
The Danish expert group has recently published their view on the future transportation system in the 
report “Mobility for the future” (in Danish) (Kristensen et al. 2018). They explain how the increases in 
prosperity, population growth, urbanisation, and transport demand will lead to increases in traffic 
congestions, especially in major cities and on highways between these. However, the major focus point 
within the report is the impact on our traffic system when self-controlled and driverless vehicles become a 
reality. They estimate that by 2035 about half of the mileage driven in Denmark will be by self-controlled 
cars, though half of the mileage driven by driverless cars cannot be expected until approximately ten years 
after that. The self-controlled cars and (especially) the driverless cars will change the demand for transport 
and the way we use it. When autonomous cars are introduced, the traffic demand will increase, initially on 
the highways where autonomous features is expected to be allowed first. When the value of time for 
travelling decreases, which will happen when the travel time can be used for something else than driving, 
the transport demand will grow significantly. As a result, traffic congestion will increase in the future, even 
though autonomous cars will be able to utilize the road capacity better than ordinary cars. In urban areas, 
driverless cars will revolutionise the taxi, buses and shared vehicle concepts. When no taxi drivers or bus 
drivers are needed, the price for using taxis and buses will decrease. The price of taxis might then 
corresponds the price of driving a privately owned car. The attractiveness of using shared vehicles is also 
expected to increase in major cities, especially when they can be summoned wherever needed. Driverless 
cars and taxies will impose new demands on the arrangement of urban areas, where fewer parking spaces 
will be needed. Instead, more parking facilities will be needed outside dense areas. Thus, it is clear from the 
report argumentations that cities are not only challenged today, but that the challenges of traffic and 
congestion will continue in the future.   
The increasing use of IoT and automation is up against a growing threat of cyberattacks and a rising 
concern for data privacy. The cyberattacks and privacy concerns have lead to the creation of regulations, 
rules and directives. The national and international lists of requirements are soon so comprehensive that 
cities fear to utilize the potential of the technological development. In Copenhagen, the use of video 
surveillance with face recognition patterns on bicycles passing intersections has been rejected due to 
privacy concerns even though the technology is ready, the equipment is deployed, and the data would 
revolutionise the knowledge about bicycle routings (Gaml & Friis 2018). Consequently, the future of ITS not 
only depends on the technological developments, but also on policy agreements, and on the fear of our 
privacy being stolen. How the concerns will affect the development of IoT in urban areas remain unknown. 
8.5 Utilization of the V-model in the Danish transport sector 
Development of ITS projects following a V-model should originate from an ITS architecture that specifies 
the national or local ambitions, goals, needs and problems, and prioritises ITS projects. It is supposed to set 
the high level viewpoints to guide the planning and deployment of ITS, which is important for 
interoperability and coordinated actions. Neither Denmark nor Copenhagen have this type of ITS 
architecture. The lack of plans on the deployment of ITS is presumably one of the reasons for the current 
pilot-project barrier in Copenhagen. There are no overall plans or goals for how to utilize ITS, nor any 
description of how to ensure interoperability of systems between municipalities and regions. However, the 
effects and benefits of having only single ITS applications are limited. For Copenhagen and Denmark to use 
==PAGE:108==
96 
 
the V-model for ITS projects, and in general move beyond the step of pilot illness, an ITS architecture with 
general needs, visions, and ITS prioritisations is needed.  
With the many reports, specifications and plans included in the V-model, the procedure seems much more 
time consuming and resource demanding than the traditional transport procedures. The case study 
demonstrated some of the massive work needed in the V-model. In the case study, however, the real time 
consuming documentations such as the verification plans and master plans were excluded, and so were the 
resource demanding activities such as decision gates and stakeholder involvement. On a short time basis, it 
is expected that the V-model is more resource-intensive than traditional transport procedures during the 
planning process. However, studies from the US show that the likelihood of schedule and cost overruns 
decreases significantly for ITS project that followed the V-model (US Department of Transportation 2007). It 
is also assumed that the risk of having to re-design or abandon the project after just a few years decreases, 
due to the increased consideration of needs and problems. Time and schedule overruns and early disposal 
of projects are expensive. In the long time perspective, projects following the V-model are not necessarily 
more resource demanding and time consuming than those following a traditional process.  
The V-model requires extensive involvement of stakeholders. In the case study, stakeholders were not 
included due to time and resource limitations, although some might argue that the backbone of the V-
model is a broad and inclusive involvement of stakeholders, and that V-model should not be demonstrated 
without this key aspect (US Department of Transportation 2007). Nevertheless, this has been a necessity 
within the limits of the thesis, but it is not an option for a real project. Stakeholder involvement is a 
challenge even for the most experienced conductors of workshops. Project groups currently experience 
that prerequisites changes all the time, or that new and more urgent cases appear resulting in a sudden 
project closure. After a workshop (or another type of stakeholder involvement), rumours abound that the 
project will be in certain way, but after just a few weeks, the project prerequisites have changed, forcing 
the project group to reconsider the intervention. Many project groups have the feeling of ‘starting over 
again’ every time a new engagement process is executed, because that project has changed significantly 
after the appearance of new prerequisites. However, this feeling is also caused by the continuous presence 
of new stakeholders (Gamst 2007).  
There is a growing awareness of the value of early stakeholder involvement in Danish cities. The invited 
stakeholders generally express great satisfaction with the invitation to participate in the work. But at the 
same time, many people express that they find it difficult to find the necessary time, especially when the 
engagement processes are held during normal working hours (Gamst 2007). The latter is a relatively simple 
to solve, but no matter what, it will always be a challenge to gather the envisioned wide range of 
stakeholders and experts that represent different opinions and prioritisations concerning the development 
of the city.  
The V-model does not simplify the task of attracting a wide range of stakeholder for the sake of a good 
discussion, nor does it stop the continuous appearance of new stakeholders that need introductions and 
briefings. However, with the V-model, all requirements are defined in the beginning of the process. The 
time and resources needed to procure all requirements must be allowed. It is expected that when using the 
V-model, it will not be necessary to start a brainstorming session on each stakeholder engagement activity 
due to the presence of new prerequisites. It is expected that stakeholder activities follow a more steady 
flow and progress when using the V-model, though it is impossible to eliminate the risk of a new 
prerequisite appearing during the design phases.

9 Conclusion and perspective 
This report presented definitions and conceptual models of the smart city concept, the challenges of not 
having a shared definition of a smart city, and argued that the governance aspect of smart cities is 
neglected in favour of technological interventions. In the transport field, smart city development is 
concentrated on the use of intelligent transport systems (ITS). It analysed the characteristics of ITS 
applications and the procedures for planning and designing ITS projects. Through a case study, the report 
investigated how the US recommended process for ITS projects (the V-model) can benefit the ITS 
development in Denmark. The aim has been to answer the following research questions: What 
characterises a smart city and intelligent transport systems (ITS)? How can ITS benefit urban societies? How 
are ITS projects planned, evaluated, and selected in Denmark? And should it be considered to utilize 
different procedures than the traditional ones when evaluating and designing ITS solutions? 
Even though the smart city concept is widely used in many sectors, it still has no shared or definite 
definition. Researchers agree that the concept builds on ICT to help cities create a competitive advantage, 
but they also agree that ICT is not the only aspect of a smart city. ICT should be seen as complementary to 
investments in social and environmental sustainability that will lead to increases in the quality of life for the 
citizens. City governance and political choices play a central role in shaping the way cities become smart, 
and the smart mobility aspect is included as a key element. The lack of a shared definition might be caused 
by the diffusion of smart city initiatives happening in countries that have different needs and settings, and 
the fragmented use of the concept in isolated disciplinary lines. This makes it difficult to identify global 
trends. The many definitions have led to different ideas of which city is the smartest. A shared definition 
with guidelines for best practices would dampen the confusion of the concept, and it would permit for a 
more goal-oriented development that is not completely controlled by the technological industry. However, 
it might be necessary to consider that the concept covers too many aspects, and that it would be easier to 
arrive at a shared definition if the concept was not trying to include all city elements.  
Increased considerations of the political and social dimension of smart cities is necessary to obtain the full 
potential of smart cities. Technological interventions cannot solve deep-rooted conflicts and structural 
difficulties. These are often associated with human habits, norms, and ethical persuasions that must be 
handled politically and with social interventions. City governments need to respond to the changing context 
of society, otherwise they risk being unable to effectively deliver on their responsibilities of ensuring 
economic return and appropriate public welfare. The level of smart governance can be illustrated by a five 
level maturity curve. The curve reflects the maturity of the smart city development within a city, and the 
level of political support towards reaching smart goals. Highest on the curve are city governments that have 
successfully broken with the traditional fragmented work in city administrations. Smart governances use a 
collaborate approach that is based on the citizen’s needs and partnerships, and they lead the development 
using strong leadership. These elements can be ensured by a seven step smart governance model.  
Copenhagen – one of the smartest cities in the world when looking at smart city rankings - is a city with 
ambitious climate goals and a widespread biking culture. However, a deep look into the governance 
structure and procedures of the city reveals major governance challenges. These challenges place 
Copenhagen low on the maturity curve, even lower than cities that do not even appear on the rankings 
presenting the smartest cities in the world. Copenhagen suffers from ‘pilot project illness’, which means 
that too many projects ‘die’ before they even get the chance to really begin. The illness can be caused by 
the fact that politicians still need to consider and commit to organisational changes and financing issues, 
but it could also be caused by the lack of a national architecture on the smart city development. A national 
architecture that will ensure compatibility, expandability, interoperability between cities and regions with 
==PAGE:110==
98 
 
strategies that outline the framework of standards and best practices. Copenhagen should also consider 
earlier involvement of stakeholders to clarify the fundamental problems and needs of the city.  
Within the transport sector, technological developments are used for intelligent traffic control, intelligent 
route planning, smart parking applications, and much more. A list of the most well-known ITS applications 
and their impact on the environment showed, that ITS seeks to improve the quality of life for citizens in a 
broad sense, just as the smart city concept aims at improving cities in a broad sense. Some ITS solutions 
address the mobility aspect while others aim at improving traffic safety, urban environments, or city 
economy. ITS applications use different technologies that can be categorized into four groups: positioning 
technologies (e.g. using satellites), wireless communication technologies (e.g. Wi-Fi and Bluetooth), sensor 
technologies, and computational logics (simulation models and recognition algorithms). Most of the 
existing ITS applications use not only one type of technology, but a combination of several technologies.  
Search on publicly available documents that recommends specific planning methods and procedures for 
Danish ITS projects yielded no results, nor did it reveal how existing Danish ITS projects have been planned. 
Consequently, it was concluded that Denmark has no official, publicly available requirements for how to 
plan, evaluate, design and implement ITS projects, maybe because most ITS projects remain limited in 
scale. However, the traditional transport procedures are in many ways insufficient for ITS projects. ITS 
projects usually do not affect the environment in the same degree as traditional transport projects. They 
are more technically complicated than traditional ones, and they frequently integrate different modes and 
multiple technical disciplines. The advanced use of technologies creates challenges for ITS project managers 
as the technologies cause new sources of failures. The high use of technology requires a close collaboration 
and integration between the design, development, and implementation, and in a traditional transport 
process these are usually separated and accomplished by different players. Many ITS projects remain 
unproven and untested, which means that the lists of specifications are short and the level of experience is 
low. The traditional transport process is often based on existing routines and experiences. 
The US Department of Transportation requires US transport planners of ITS projects to follow a System 
Engineering Process, and they recommend the use of the V-model. The V-model is a top-down and bottom-
up process, where the top-down activities specifies all project details. The bottom-up activities ensure 
execution, testing and implementation of the designed systems. One of the main advantages of the V-
model is the continuous control of the final execution and performance being in accordance with the 
initially defined needs and requirements. The V-model incorporates the seven principles of smart 
governance, such as stakeholder engagement and classification of opportunities based on needs. Hence, 
the V-model helps cities create a smart governance. In the transport area, the V-model should originate 
from a local or national ITS architecture. The lack of ITS architectures in Denmark with specified needs, 
problems, visions, and ITS prioritisations complicates the use of the V-model. 
In a case study, the benefits and limitations of the top-down activities of the V-model were tested for a 
Danish ITS project. As Denmark has no ITS architecture, the V-model originated from a few general smart 
governance principles. The case study demonstrated the importance of the ITS architecture, by illustrating 
the comprehensive work related to the creation of general city analyses. It would be repetitive to create 
such analyses for all project. The case study also illustrated how many evaluations the V-model requires 
before the solution is designed and selected. In a traditional transport project, less time and energy would 
be spend on specifying and defining the problems and needs, and instead the attention would be directed 
towards the solution earlier on. The lack of perspective on the needs and problems often results in a trial-
and-error technique that could easily result in missing functions and unnecessary initiatives.  
==PAGE:111==
99 
 
The governance challenges and proposals presented in the report are widely accepted in literature. This 
includes the improved considerations of leadership, partnerships, stakeholder involvement, and clear 
strategy formulations. Several key players of the smart transport development in Denmark express that 
Copenhagen lacks certain fundamental governance aspects to evolve further. However, only relatively old 
literature from the US suggests the use of the V-model for ITS projects. Additional studies should therefore 
examine the benefits and drawbacks of using the V-model for ITS projects in Denmark, and examine which 
type of ITS projects that would benefit from following the V-model.

Signpost systems 
Signpost systems are the simplest method of positioning. It uses landmarks (or

Dead reckoning 
Dead reckoning systems can be used to determine the current position of a vehicle 
using vehicle-mounted sensors if a previously determined position (initial reference 
point) is known. The current position is determined using the known (or estimated) 
speed of the vehicle, the time, the distance travelled (based on the wheel 
odometers) and the course of direction (using the built-in electronic compasses). 
Dead reckoning systems allow fully autonomous positioning within a vehicle, though 
the disadvantage is the unbounded accumulation of errors. Thus, the uncertainty 
about the positioning increases the further away from the initial reference point the 
vehicle is moving. One of the major errors is the direction as magnetic compasses 
depend on the weak magnetic field of the Earth (Pagani 2005).

Wave-based positioning 
The wave-based positioning system is a method that is used by all GNSS systems. 
The concept utilises propagation properties of waves (usually electro-magnetic 
waves) to determine the position of an object based on the relative position of one 
or more reference sites. By knowing several relative positions of an object, the 
actual position can be determined using triangulation. The most famous GNSS 
system (the American GPS) has at least 24 satellites circulating round Earth in six 
orbital planes with normally four satellites in each plane. Satellites transmits signals 
send by the receivers, which gives the distance between the receiver and satellite. 
With only one visible satellite, the receiver could be anywhere on the surface of a 
circle with a specific radius. To calculate the exact three-dimensional position 
(latitude, longitude, altitude, and time) of the receiver using trilateration, the 
receiver should receive a signal from four satellites at the same time, though the 
fourth satellite is only needed for the time correction as the installed time in the 
receiver is rarely the same time as in the satellites (Abdalla 2016). The positioning 
can be achieved with an accuracy of 2-3 cm to app. 30 m depending on the device. 
The accuracy level for most handhold receiving devices is about 10 m (Haltli et al. 
2005).  
The wave-based positioning method highly depends on the availability of the waves. 
The system can only work if the receiving device can get undisturbed radio waves. 
Tunnels, trees, urban constructions, etc. could block the signal from the satellite. 
The accuracy of the wave-based positioning technology can also be affected by 
errors as the ‘multipath’ effect or the ionosphere delay. The multipath effect covers 
the phenomenon where a receiving antenna receive multiple signals from the same 
satellite that comes from different paths – one being the direct signal and others 
being reflected signals that create a new disordered signal. Physical conditions in 
the ionosphere and/or troposphere (temperature, pressure, relative humidity), 
which are dependent on the number of electrons, can lead to a delay of the signal 
and cause errors in the measurements (Abdalla 2016).

Wireless networks (WLAN) 
This technology uses radio waves as the propagation medium at frequencies in two 
bands: 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz. Each band is divided into channels of varying width, 
which allows for many simultaneous connections. In this technology, a client 
connects to the wireless local area network (WLAN) through a base station or access 
point (AP). The AP will broadcast a message for clients to discover and connect to 
the network. Generally, an AP connects the client to the internet through a router. 
The range of wireless networks varies depending on many parameters, e.g. on the 
type of antenna, the placement nearby obstructions (building, walls, etc.), 
interference from other electronical devices, and current weather. WLAN has a 
typical range of less than 100 m.  
Another wireless standard, called WiMAX (Worldwide Interoperability for 
Microwave Access), defines long range wireless communication in the 2-11 GHz 
range. WiMAX has a range of several kilometers. WLAN and/or WiMAX are used for 
V2I communication.

Dedicated Short Range Communications (DSRC) 
This technology shares many basic traits of the Wi-Fi standard such as radio waves 
as propagation medium and working frequencies in almost the same ranges as Wi-
Fi. However, it differs in one important aspect: the frequency band is reserved 
specifically for transport systems. DSRC operates in the 5.8 GHz band in Europe (5.9 
GHz in North America). The DSRC protocol defines two possible uses: one for safety 
and one for non-safety applications (Corser et al. 2014). The protocol defines 
different demands concerning delay times in message transfer for the two uses 
(Cseh 1998). The safety use demands significantly lower delay times than the non-
safety use. The range of the DSRC technology is app. 300 m (Xu et al. 2017). 
A typical use of DSRC is a tag-beacon setup (V2I communication). An electronic tag is 
located in cars that the beacon, placed in the roadside, can read. The protocol 
allows the tag to transfer information about its position, speed, direction apart, and 
its tag ID. DSRC can also be used for V2V communication.

and sending of commands. In Bluetooth topology, a master connects to one or more 
slaves through a pairing process. This makes it is more time consuming to establish 
a connection with Bluetooth compared to e.g. Wi-Fi.

Radio Frequency Identification (RFID) 
This technology is based on radio waves as carrier of messages. RFID is a tag-reader 
system, and it defines two kinds of tags: active (requiring power) and passive (no 
power required) tags. For the passive tags, a reader sends out a probe signal and the 
tag receives the signal and re-transmits its ID by backscatter. The active tag can send 
other information as well. RFID works on both low (approx. 135 kHz) and high (13 
MHz – 106 GHz) frequencies. The range of RFID varies depending on type and 
frequency from 10 cm to 200 m.  Near Field Communication (NFC) is a modern 
technology that is based upon RFID. In NFC, data is transferred over very short 
distances (less than 10 cm) and it is used in many applications ranging from cell 
phones to credit cards.

Sensor technologies 
The following technologies have no advantages and limitations, as they are purely measures of different real-world 
events. Sensors merely reflect different physical impulses and stimuli. Different applications using other 
technologies may have advantages and limitations. Implementation of these sensors may vary in accuracy (how 
close a measurement is to the ‘true’ value) and precision (how reproductible a measurement is). Depending on 
these characteristics, a device may have some limitations due to the accuracy and precision of its sensors.  
Mechanical 
Mechanical sensors can measure the following stimulations: length, area, volume, time derivatives such as 
velocity, acceleration, mass flow, force, torque, pressure, acoustic wavelength and acoustic intensity. 
An example of a mechanical sensor are ultrasound sensors for detection of vehicles in parking spaces. Another use 
is measurements of weight of a vehicle passing a mechanical sensor placed in the road pavement.  
Thermal 
Thermal sensors can measure temperature, specific heat, entropy, heat flow, and state of matter (solid, liquid, 
gas, and plasma) 
Typical examples of thermal sensors are weather stations.  
 
Electrical 
Electrical sensors can measure voltage, current, charge, resistance, inductance, capacitance, dielectric constant, 
polarization, electric field, frequency, dipole moment 
Magnetic 
Magnetic sensors can measure field intensity, flux density, magnetic moment, permeability.  
An example of a magnetic sensor is inductors embedded in the road pavement. As a vehicle passes over, it 
induces a flux in the inductor, which can be measured as an alternating current.

Radiant 
Radiant sensors measure intensity, phase, wavelength, polarization, reflectance, transmittance, refractive index. 
Cameras use many small photoelectric sensors that measure light-waves and intensities and converts the input 
into an image matrix. Laser and radar technologies are also radiant sensors that use light-waves and radio waves, 
respectively, to determine the range, angle, or velocity of objects 
Chemical 
Chemical sensors can measure composition, concentration, reaction rate, pH, oxidation/reduction potential. 
Chemical sensors may be seen in fuel consumption sensors in vehicles.

Pattern recognition 
Pattern recognition aims at recognising patterns and symmetries in data to provide 
reasonable ‘most likely’ matches of the inputs or provide a fast judgement of the 
current system status. Classification is one type of pattern recognition, where 
algorithms assign all input values to a given set a specific classification class, which 
could be the numbers of letters. ANPR utilizes this type of pattern recognition. 
However, pattern recognition is much more than that. It is used almost everywhere 
in intelligent transport systems to process and analyse real-time traffic data through 
various algorithms, and hence, it is an indispensable element for real-time 
intelligent traffic condition judgements and controls.

Simulation models intend to imitate a real-world process or system. The simulation 
represents the operations in the system, and the models allow for graphical user 
interfaces that illustrate the inputs. Traffic simulations (simulations of transport 
systems) on a micro and macro level, helps the planning, designing, and operation 
process of transport systems, and it is an important tool for experimental studies 
and examinations of relation patterns. Traffic models often use stochastic 
simulation, which means that variables change randomly in order to represent the 
unreliable reality.
