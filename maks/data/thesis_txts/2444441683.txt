Abstract 
The sources of x-ray bursts are neutron stars in low mass x-ray binaries. Accreted 
matter from the donor star accumulate on the surface of the neutron star till a critical 
point is reached and triggers a runaway nuclear burning, radiating a large amount of 
x-ray. 
An x-ray burst can be analysed with a time resolved spectral analysis where the 
bursts is divided in to intervals which are then modelled. 
In this project a program is written that takes x-ray bursts observed with JEM-
X/INTERGRAL and preforms an automatic time resolved spectral analysis on them. 
It is found that the best systematic division of the bursts are when they are divided in 
to the rise, peak and tail parts. 
The best combined persistence flux and burst modelling is found to be a power law 
model for the persistence and a black body model for the burst. 
The results from the program are validated by comparing with the data from 
PCA/RXTE of 10 bursts that were observed by both instruments. 
To get the optimal results from the program the bursts are found to need a higher 
peak value than 2 cts/s/cm^2 and to have been observed at an off-angel lower than 
240 arcmin.

1 Introduction 
Type-I thermonuclear x-ray bursts occur on the surface of neutron stars in low mass 
binary systems. X-ray bursts are an unstable nuclear burning of matter that are 
accreted from the companion star and have accumulated on the surface. The 
structure of the bursts is dependent on the matter that are present, in most cases is it 
hydrogen, helium or a combination of the two. [1] 
X-ray bursts have short rise times, typically ~1 to 10 seconds. After the bursts have 
peaked, they exponentially decay. Most bursts last under a minute but they can last 
many hours. After a burst has occurred the accreted matter will build up again till 
the critical point is reached again. [1] 
Orbiting x-ray telescopes observe the x-ray bursts. They observe the time and energy 
of the incoming photons from the sources. The data used in this project is coming 
from the JEM-X instruments. 
A burst spectrum can be approximated as a black body spectrum and the burst can 
be modelled as such. This modelling gives a radius of the source dependent on the 
distance to the source and a temperature of the source. 
To get the development of a burst over time, it is divided into intervals and the 
spectrum is modelled as a black body spectrum. This analysis process is a time 
resolved spectral analysis. 
In this project a program is written that will systematically divide the burst into 
fitting intervals. The program then automatically models the persistence flux and the 
burst intervals. 
To determine the best way to describe the persistent flux and the burst, five models 
are tested. The five models are compared to each other to determine the best option.  
10 bursts that have been observed with the JEM-X have also been observed with the 
PCA instruments. The two sets of time resolved spectral analyses result from the 10 
bursts that are compered to validate the chosen model. 
The program is executed with a random sample of bursts to provide a limitation of 
which bursts can be run by the program. The limitations are in form of off-angel and 
peak flux. 
Lastly, the program is executed on all bursts, from one source, within the determined 
limit, providing an example data set from that source, to be used for further analysis.

2 Theory 
This section describes the theory of the sources of x-ray bursts and the spectral 
analyses of x-ray bursts. A short introduction to the JEM-X and PCA instruments is 
also included in this section. 
2.1 X-ray binaries 
In a binary star system two stars are orbiting each other. At one point the most 
massive of the two stars will reach its final step of its evolution and become a 
compact object. The compact object the star evolves into is dependent on how 
massive the star was initially. 
If the starting mass was roughly 4-8 solar mass the star will evolve into a white 
dwarf. The maximum mass of a white dwarf is 1.4 solar mass (called the 
Chandrasekhar limit). This is the limit of how large a pressure a degenerate electron 
can produce.  
If the start mass of the most massive star was around 8 solar mass or over, the stars 
evolution will end in a supernova and the stars core can remain afterwards. The 
remain will be a neutron star or a black hole dependent on the mass. [1] 
The remains are a neutron star if the mass lies from 1.4 to 2-3 solar mass and have a 
small radius of approximate 10-20 km. Neutron stars exceeds the Chandrasekhar 
limit, meaning that protons combines with electrons and creates neutrons and 
neutrinos. Neutron stars can have an extremely strong magnetic field, that effects the 
nature of how matter falls upon it.  
If the remains after the supernova exceeds 2-3 solar mass, it will be a black hole. 
The gravitational pressure will overcome all internal pressures, resulting in a higher 
density than a neutron star. This compress the remains to a radius that lies under the 
Schwarzschild radius. The Schwarzschild radius is the distance from the centre to 
the event horizon. At the event horizon the escape velocity is equal to the speed of 
light. 
X-ray binary star systems are divided into two groups - The Low Mass X-ray 
Binaries (LMXB) and The High Mass X-ray Binaries (HMXB). The masses 
reference the mass of the companion star. [1] 
2.1.1 HMXB 
HMXB is defined by the donor star in the system - a massive star over 10 solar 
masses. The high mass of the donor star means that the compact object had a higher 
starting mass. The object must therefore be a neutron star or a black hole. 
The high mass donor star means that the system consists of relatively young stars, 
since the higher mass stars have a shorter evolution time. 
The HMXB mass accretion is driven by a powerful stellar wind from the donor star. 
The material from the stellar wind have a small angular momentum and therefore

results in a small accretion disc if one is formed. If the compact object is a neutron 
star, then the accretion from the accretion disc upon the surface is determined by the 
strong magnetic field. The matter falls in on the magnetic poles of the neutron star 
and form an x-ray fan beam.  
The HMXB does not produce the type-1 x-ray bursts this project revolves around. [1] 
2.1.2 LMXB  
LMXB’s donor mass is typical around 1 solar mass and often lower. This means that 
the compact object can be a white dwarf, a neutron star or a black hole. These 
systems are far older than the HMXB, since low mass stars have a far longer 
evolution time. 
If the system lies closer that ~100 pc and has a weak luminosity less than 
~1033𝑒𝑟𝑔𝑠 𝑠−1 the compact object is most likely a white dwarf. At a higher 
luminosity that this value the compact object is a neutron star or a black hole. If the 
system is a source to any x-ray burst or pulsation, then the compact object is a 
neutron star. Black holes have no surface and can therefore not have any nuclear 
burning on the surface. 
In a LMXB the mass donor star has too weak a stellar wind for that to be the mass 
transfer mechanism. The accretion in LMXB happens instead when the donor star 
fills the Roche lobe and mass is transferred through the inner Lagrangian point. The 
Roche lobe is the distance from the object where matter is gravitational bound to the 
object. The inner Lagrangian point is the point between the two objects where the 
gravitational pull equals zero. 
The matter that is accreted to the compact object is dependent on the donor star. The 
donor star is a low mass star, typically with a lower mass than one solar mass. This 
means that the stars outer regions are mostly composed of hydrogen. The matter that 
accrete towards the compact object is therefore mostly hydrogen. 
Matter that flows from the donor star to the compact object has a large angular 
momentum and forms a large accretion disk around the compact object. The material 
travel inwards from the outer part of the disk to inner. The material then falls upon 
the surface of the neutron star or beyond the Schwarzschild radius of a black hole. If 
it is a neutron star in the system, the matter on the surface will burn till the process is 
unstable resulting at a critical time in a thermonuclear explosion observed as an x-
ray burst. [1]

2.3 X-ray bursts 
As mentioned, thermonuclear explosions occur on the surface of a neutron star in a 
binary system with a star with the properties like the sun. Matter, mainly hydrogen, 
transfers from the star to an accretion disk and then onto the neutron star surface. 
The transfer of matter from the star to the neutron star heats up the material in the 
accretion disk and gives a persistent x-ray flux.   
On the surface of the neutron star the matter will nuclear burn, and at one point be a 
runaway fusion that consumes the build-up matter and let out a huge amount of 
energy that can be observed as an x-ray burst. [1] 
The structure of the burst is dependent on the composition of the matter that is 
consumed by the runaway burning. The burning of normal type 1 x-bursts consists 
of helium and hydrogen and has a duration from a few seconds up to 10’s of 
minutes. Super bursts can last hours and are likely the result of a runaway carbon 
burning. [7] 
The normal burst can be divided into 3 classes dependent on the rate of the mass 
transfer from the accretion disk to the neutron star. The accretion rate is normally 
given in fractions of the local Eddington rate 𝑚̇ 𝐸𝑑𝑑  
The 3 ways are described by the state of the hydrogen burning on the surface of the 
neutron star. 
In the case with the lowest accretion rate (lower than ⁓ 0.01 𝑚̇ 𝐸𝑑𝑑) the burning 
temperature is too low for stable hydrogen burning. The hydrogen ignites unstably, 
and this starts the helium burning starting an x-ray burst in a hydrogen rich 
environment that consists of hydrogen and helium.

In the second case where the accretion rate is between ⁓ 0.01 𝑚̇ 𝐸𝑑𝑑 and ⁓ 0.1 𝑚̇ 𝐸𝑑𝑑 
the temperature rises to where stable hydrogen burning is possible. At this rate the 
accreted material is processed as fast as it is accreted. This results in that the helium 
zone is growing in mass. Heat flows from the hydrogen burning shell to the helium 
layer. When the condition reaches the critical point a helium flash is triggered. 
The 3rd case is where the accretion rate is between ⁓ 0.1 𝑚̇ 𝐸𝑑𝑑 and ⁓ 1. 𝑚̇ 𝐸𝑑𝑑. Here 
the temperature is high enough for stable hydrogen burning. The accretion rate is 
higher than the processing rate of the material. This means that when the helium 
shell is growing in mass so is the hydrogen mass. When the helium flash occurs, it is 
in a hydrogen rich environment and the burst consist of both helium and hydrogen.

2.3.1 Photospheric radius expansion (PRE) burst 
The Eddington limit is a limit on the luminosity and flux of a star or a compact 
object. The limit is naturally also a limit on an x-ray burst. The neutron stars that 
produces the brightest bursts, have a massive outgoing force that succeed in lifting 
the top layers of the neutron star. 
When the layers are lifted the temperature of those layers drops and when the layers 
fall down again the temperature rises again. This can be seen in time resolved 
spectral analysis, an expansion of the source’s radius and a dive in temperature can 
be observed. The light curve will also have two peaks starting where the luminosity 
hits the Eddington limit. [1] 
2.3.2 Black body 
A good approximation to x-ray bursts is a cooling black body model. A black body 
object has a non-reflective surface (total black), all radiation that falls upon the 
surface of a black body is absorbed. 
The radiation that comes from a black body is therefore a result of the temperature 
of the black body. The nature of the radiation is dependent on the temperature of the 
black body, higher temperatures produces radiation with shorter wavelengths.

(PCA).

2.4.1 JEM-X 
JEM-X are two x-ray telescopes that are some of the instruments that are on the 
INTERGRAL satellite. INTERGRAL was launched on October 17th, 2002 and has 
an elliptical orbit period of 72 hours. 
The telescope uses a coded mask, where a mask of absorbing material is placed in 
front of the detector. The mask has a pattern of open areas in it, that lets the radiation 
to the detector. The coded mask casts an illuminated pattern on to the detector. This 
pattern can then be correlated with the one from the open areas mask, and theis 
provides the direction of the source. The coded mask gives a much larger field of 
view than a reflecting telescope. 
The coded masks pattern that is used on the JEM-X instruments have 22501 
elements where only 25 % is open area. The masks are placed 3.4 meters over the 
detectors. 
The detector is a microstrip gas chamber that has a sensitive geometric area of 500  
𝑐𝑚2 per detector. The gas in the detectors is a mixture of 10 % methane and 90 % 
xenon at 1.5 bar pressure.   
The photons that hits the detector are absorbed by the xenon by photo-electric 
absorption. The ionization is amplified by the strong electric field near the 
microstrip anodes. Strong electric charges are detected as an electric impulse, at the 
microstrip and the coordinates time and energy of the photon can then be measured. 
The instrument has a field of view up till 13.2° and it is fully illuminated till 4.8°. 
The energy range is from 3 to 35 keV and an energy resolution of ∆𝐸/𝐸= 0.40 ∙
(1/𝐸 𝑘𝑒𝑉+ 1/120 𝑘𝑒𝑉 )1/2. [3] 
2.4.2 PCA 
PCA is an instrument that is placed on The Rossi X-ray Timing Explorer (RXTE) 
mission. The mission was launched on the 30th December 1995 and had an orbit 
period of around 90 minutes. 
PCA had a large collection area that consisted of five xenon and methane filled 
proportional counters. Proportional counters are a gas filled with a window that lets 
through weak x-ray and keeps the gas in. The x-rays that hits the detector gets 
absorbed by the gas and produces a tail - a short track of electrons and ions. The 
process is amplified, and the electron is collected by the central wire and the time 
and energy of the photon is detected. The field of view is reduced to be able to focus 
on only one source, without the noise from the surrounding sky.  A meter-high 
honeycomb collimator reduces the field of view to 1°. [4]

3 Data description 
The data used to perform the time resolved spectral analyses comes from one or both 
two JEM-X instruments. To determine the optimal model to fit the data 
automatically, 10 bursts are used. The bursts are observed with the JEM-X 
instruments and with RXTE, and the model can be verified, or not, by comparing the 
two time resolved spectral analyses. 
The JEM-X data sets are named with a unique observation id. The observation id 
contains the satellite orbit number and a number for the science window. Science 
windows are time intervals that the whole orbit observation is divided into. 
The raw data from the JEM-X instruments are not directly used in this project, 
instead the light curve and the spectra will be extracted from the raw data. To this 
end the JEM-X analysis software “jemx_scw_analysis” is used. The program 
receives a start and an end time (given in JEM-X time), the timesteps wanted, and 
the number of energy bands wanted. The output is the light curve and the spectrum. 
The JEM-X instruments have a large angel of sight and can have multiple sources in 
view at a given time. The light curve and spectrum files therefore contain a data set 
for each source in the instruments view. To obtain the right data sets the placements 
of the source data is needed. This is given as a row number. The row numbers are 
sorted by intensity of the sources in a chosen time interval and varies even for time 
intervals in the same science window. 
The light curve data file contains a time vector in JEM-X time, a count rate vector in 
counts per second and a rate error vector. There is a vector for each source and for 
each energy band in the sources. The spectrum file contains the intensity of the 
energy bands.   
The PCA data used is already analysed from the 10 bursts that are also observed 
with JEM-X. Because the PCA telescope had a small field of view and a large 
colleting area the data have a lot larger number of counts. It is therefore possible to 
make a time resolved spectral analysis on much smaller timesteps than on the JEM-
X data.  
The dataset from PCA therefore contain the time, count rate, count rate error, 
bolometric flux, bolometric flux error, temperature, temperature error, norm and 
norm error. The timestep for this data is 0.25 second.

4 Method 
The method used to analyse a burst can be split in four parts; the analysis of the light 
curve, the analysis of the persistent flux from the source and the analysis of the 
development of the burst. In this project five different approaches to model the 
persistent flux and burst are tried. The last part is the analysis of the results from the 
time resolved spectral analyses. 
4.1 Light curve 
The light curve data is made with one second timesteps and have two energy bands 
one high energy and one low. 
In the light curve analysis there are multiple objectives; to find the beginning and 
end of the burst, the rise time of the burst, the exponential decay time, a defined 
interval for the persistent flux modelling and the division of the burst into fitting 
time intervals. 
The light curve analysis is the same for all the five approaches. The observation id 
where the bursts are located is coming from the MINBAR database and therefor the 
verification of the bursts is not done in this analysis process. 
First, is to locate the peak of the burst, here defined as where the maximum rise in 
count rate in the two energy bands occurs. 
The beginning and end of the bursts will for this project be defined to be the time 
where the count rate reach 25 % of the peak value and where it drops to 15 % peak 
value again. Rise time is found by the time from the beginning of the burst to the 
time the count rate reaches 90 % peak value. 
Exponential decay time is found by fitting the tail to an exponential function given 
by: 
𝒓(𝒕) = 𝒄𝟏𝒆−𝒄𝟐𝒕+ 𝒄𝟑 
Where r(t) is the count rate depending on the time, c 1 to 3 are the three constants 
that will be fitted, and t is the time. 
Exponential decay time is given by the inverse of the 𝒄𝟐 constant from the fit. 
The time intervals that later will be used in the time resolved spectral analysis is 
found next. Instead of having a set time per interval or a fixed number of counts per 
time interval the burst will be divided into a rise interval, a peak interval and tail 
intervals. 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
The rise interval is here defined to be the start time to the 90 % peak intensity time. 
This may result in an interval with too low counts to achieve a good fit, but this will 
provide a better interval for the peak. 
The peak interval is from where the rise interval ended to the time where the interval 
contains 450 counts, if this interval is shorter than 1 second it will be extended to be 
1 second. 
The tail intervals are here defined to be where the count is over 500 and over 1 
second for 1 interval. For each interval found the counts per interval will be 
increased with 50 counts. The increase after the peak interval and further down the 
tail is to try to keep high counts in the higher energy bins. 
Lastly in the light curve analysis the time intervals to model the persistent flux is 
found. The time interval that lies right before the burst is used as the base quiet 
period. If another x-ray event lies in this period, the period till that event is used. If 
this period is too short the time after the burst will be used instead. 
To get a large data set to model the persistent flux the science window that lies 
before the one containing the burst is also used. To ensure that the conditions is 
close to the conditions before the bursts, the relationship between the high and low 
energies is compared to the relationship from the first interval. If the two intervals 
are relatively close the data from the new science window will also be used. The 
science window will be rejected if the off-angel lies more than 1 degree from the 
first science window’s off-angel.

4.4 Analysis of time spectral analysis data 
In this part of the process, the results from the time resolved spectral analysis are 
used to compute burst related parameters. These parameters are gamma, tau, the 
bolometric fluence, the fluence and the colour-colour values. 
Gamma is the normalized persistent flux, the bolometric persistent flux divided by 
the peak bolometric flux of the burst. 
Tau is the burst timescale and is given as the fluence over the max peak count rate 
per 𝑐𝑚2. 
The bolometric fluence is the total energy per 𝑐𝑚2 after the absorptions are 
deducted. This comes from the xspec fitting. 
Fluence is the total number of counts over the duration of the burst per 𝑐𝑚2. The 
counts are from the light curve and the area is the effective area of the detector for a 
given source. [6] 
The colour-colour values are two values, the soft colour ratio and the hard colour 
ratio. These ratios are of the persistence flux from the sources. The soft colour ratio 
will here be the ratio 4.16-5.6keV/3.04-4.16keV and the hard colour ratio 5.6-
10.4keV/10.4-26.24keV. These values can be used to produce a colour-colour plot 
of sources.

5 Program description 
The program made in this project is made to automatically perform a time resolved 
spectral analyses of x-ray bursts observed by the JEM-X instruments. It is based on 
a program I made for my synthesis project “Automatic time spectral analysis of x-
ray bursts, observed from NuSTAR and JEM-X”. The program has undergone a 
large change since that project and is mostly new. 
The program is a collection of scripts that are executed in an automatic process. The 
program is composed of three types of base script forms; shell scripts, IDL scripts 
and xspec scripts. 
The following description of the program (scripts) will contain all the different 
xspec models that are tested in this project and a description of the optimal found 
way to divide the burst in time intervals.  
5.1 IDL 1 and 2 
The program uses two IDL scripts to perform the time resolved spectral analysis. 
The first script divides the bursts and defines the time intervals. The other script 
collects the time resolved spectral analyses results from the xspec output and plots 
the results. It also computes the last results as mention in the method section. 
The first IDL script that divides the bursts in time intervals needs the name of the 
source as an input to find the row number. 
The IDL script use the light curve to divide the burst into the time intervals. It loads 
the light curve from the science window containing the bursts and from the previous 
science window. The files with the source names and their respective row numbers 
are likewise loaded for both science windows. 
The IDL scripts main outputs are; the beginning and end times for the time intervals 
the burst is divided into, and the x-ray quiet periods before and/or after the burst. 
The duration, risetime and exponential decay time of the burst are also found by this 
script. 
First, the row number that belongs to the given source is found, for both science 
windows. The row number is used to extract the count rate from the source for the 
whole science window, for both science windows. The count rate is in two vectors; 
the count rates for energies from 3 to 11 keV and the count rates for energies 11 to 
25 keV.  
Time vectors matching the count rate vectors are made with 1 second timesteps 
beginning at zero at the science windows start. 
The structure of the burst and the persistent flux can be difficult to observe due to 
the fluctuation in count rate, when the timesteps are 1 second. For better observing 
the largescale structure two new vectors are made.  
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
Firstly, one where the count rate is re-binned to have a 3 seconds timestep. This is 
done by adding the two count rates and taking the mean over 3 seconds for the 
whole science window. 
Secondly, one where both count rates are re-binned to have a 200 seconds timestep. 
This is done by taking the mean of every 200 seconds.  
Before dividing the burst into time intervals, the parameters need to be found. The 
peak, the beginning, the 90 % peak value time and the end of the burst are found. To 
find the peak of the burst the low and the high energy vectors are multiplied. Due to 
the increase in count rates in both energy bands the moment of the peak will give a 
large spike and is easier located in an automatic process, especially for weaker 
bursts. 
The persistent flux count rate is found by meaning a time interval before the peak. 
All the computing of the data is done where the mean persistent flux has been 
subtracted. 
The beginning and the 90 % peak value times are found by using the re-binned 3 
seconds timestep count rate. By going from the peak towards the science window’s 
start, finding the time where the count rate is below 90 % and 25 % of the peak 
intensity. 
From that point additional 9 seconds is subtracted from the found point, then moves 
towards the peak again till the count rate exceed the 90 % and the 25 % of peak 
intensity. This is done to ensure that it is not a local minimum that is found. 
Then the 1 second timestep count rate is used to enhance the precision of the time. 
Lastly it is assumed that from the found point and the one before the count rate is 
linear and the time is now found for the 25 % peak value and the 90 % peak value. 
This last step is to avoid getting the beginning the 90 % peak value time and the 
peak time at the same instance. 
The end time is found the same way just mirrored, so the 15 % peak value time is 
found after the peak. 
As mentioned in the method section, the first time interval that the burst is divided 
into is the time from the beginning of the burst till the 90 % peak value time (the rise 
time). If the rise time exceeds 5 seconds it will be divided into two or more intervals. 
After the first interval, the next interval, the one mentioned as the peak interval, is 
found by adding counts from 0.1 second at a time till the counts in the interval 
exceed 450 and the intervals length exceeds 1 second. 
The next interval or intervals are the tail intervals and are found like the peak 
interval, 50 counts are added to the limit per interval starting with the first tail 
interval. 
The last interval does not contain enough counts and will be added to the second to 
last interval. 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
To model the persistent flux one or preferable two intervals needs to be defined. 
These time intervals cannot contain any x-ray events of any significance. The x-ray 
flux needs to lie as close to the condition right before the burst start. 
The firsts time periods that are checked is the time from the start of the science 
window to the beginning of the burst. The high and low energies are multiplied to 
check this period. If there is not any x-ray event in over 70 % of the bursts mean 
count rate, the period 60 second after the start of the science window and 60 seconds 
before the start of the burst is chosen. If an event is in this period, the period 60 
seconds after that event and 60 seconds before the burst is chosen. 
The high-low energy ratio in this period will be used as a normal ratio for right 
before the burst. For the next part the 200 seconds re-bin data is used. The absolute 
maximum ratio between the high and low energies for the period are used to check 
the two other periods.   
The science window before the one containing the burst is checked. If the maximum 
count rate ratio in this science window, do not exceed 110 % of the maximum count 
rate ratio of the period found before, the whole science window plus/minus 60 
seconds from the start and end of the science window is chosen. If this is not the 
case, the time after the 110 % count rate ratio was exceeded is used. 
If the period is not accepted, the period after the burst is checked. This is done by 
checking if the maximum count rate ratio of the period stating 120 seconds after the 
burst and until 60 seconds before the end of the science window. is greater than the 
maximum count rate ratio of the pre-burst period. If the count rate ratio is greater the 
period will also be rejected. 
Last, the script will fit an exponential curve to the tail of the burst obtained by the 
IDL function “CURVEFIT”. 
All the time intervals and the parameters are saved in txt files to be used later. 
The second IDL script purpose is to collect and visualize the analysis results, and 
calculate the fluence, tau and gamma, and the errors of this.

5.2 Xspec 
The xspec scripts specify the parameters and the xspec models that are used to 
describe the persistent flux and the x-ray bursts. There are used 3 types of xspec 
scripts in this program; one to describe the persistent flux, one to describe the burst 
where the persistent flux could be modelled and one where it could not. The two 
first types varies as mentioned in the method section, the third stays the same for all 
5 variations.    
To model the persistent flux and the burst 6 xspec models are used in different 
combinations. These 6 models are TBabs, powerlaw, cutoffpl, constant, bbodyrad 
and cflux. 
Tbabs is the The Tuebingen-Boulder ISM absorption model. This model calculates 
the x-ray absorption from the ISM. [5] 
The powerlaw model is a simple photon power law that models the persistent flux. 
[5] 
The cutoffpl is a powerlaw with an exponential cut of value for high energies.  
Bbodyrad is a blackbody spectrum model with normalize proportional to the source 
surface area. [5] 
Cflux is a model that calculate the flux of other model components. [5] 
There are four scripts that are used to model the persistent flux. The four scripts are 
for the situations where there are one or two combined spectra available and one or 
two non-combined spectra. The scripts have four input parameters; the column 
density of the source [9], a vector with the row numbers of the spectra, a vector with 
an id number of the spectra, that are given when the spectra are defined, and last a 
vector telling which instrument the spectra are from. 
The script loads the spectrum, response and arf files of all the spectrums available. 
The energy under 3.0keV and over 26.0keV are ignored, this means that the 8th 
energy band is removed. The model is then defined by one of the following three 
models: 
Tbabs*(powerlaw+ Bbodyrad) 
Tbabs*(powerlaw) 
Tbabs*(cutoffpl) 
Tbabs input parameter is the column density. The parameter is set and frozen. Each 
source has a column density. The other parameters from the models are the ones that 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
will be determined by fitting to the persistent flux data. If the model could be fitted 
to the data, the model parameters is saved for later use. 
The model is then changed to contain cflux after the absorption model and fitted 
again to find the unabsorbed flux. This is also saved for later use.  
There are two scripts there are used to model the x-ray burst, if there are fitted a 
model for the persistent flux. One script for if the burst is observed with only one 
instrument and one for if the burst if observed with both instruments. These two 
scripts load the same four parameters as the persistent flux scripts do, additionally 
the parameters found from the persistent flux model is also loaded. 
The spectrum, response and arf files are loaded and the energy under 3keV and over 
25keV are ignored. The models that are used in these scripts are 
TBabs*(bbodyrad+persistent _model) 
TBabs*(bbodyrad+C*persistent _model) 
The column density and the persistent flux parameters are then frozen, and the 
parameters fitted to the spectrum. The errors are computed if the reduced chi 
squared lies between 0 and 2.  
Cflux is added like for the persistent flux scripts and fitted. 
Four xspec scripts are for modelling the bursts if the persistent flux could not be 
modelled. The four scripts are for one and two combined spectra and one and two 
non-combined spectra. They load the same 4 parameters as the persistent flux scripts 
do. The two with the non-combined spectra loads two extra vectors. One vector with 
the persistent flux row number and one with a persistent flux reference number. 
The spectrum, response and arf files are loaded, a background file is also loaded. 
This will be the combined or non-combined persistent flux spectrums. The energy 
under 3keV and over 26keV are ignored. The models that are used in these scripts 
are TBabs*(bbodyrad). When the model is fitted, the model parameters are saved 
and the cflux is added to the model after the absorption model. When the unabsorbed 
flux is fitted it is saved. [5] 
All the xspec scripts can be found is the Appendix. 
5.3 Shell script 
The shell script is the controlling script that calls all xspec and idl scripts and moves 
and copies necessary folders and files. It is a large script containing many paths and 
choices depending on the bursts. This description will therefore be a rough outlining 
of the processes that the scripts performs.  
The script starts by setting the base conditions, for running the JEM-X analysis 
software. The file containing the bursts information drawn from MINBAR is loaded. 
The file contains the source name and the observation id where the burst is 
contained. 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
The script then starts the main loop that will loop once for every burst, in the loop 
modelling of the persistent flux and time resolved spectral analysestime resolved 
spectral analyses is computed.  
All the parameters like the science window, orbit number and the column density are 
defined. The column density is loaded for the source that is analysed in the given 
loop. It is loaded for a file that contain the column density from all the sources [9]. 
The “jemx_scw_analysis” is then executed. This is a JEM-X analysis software that 
from the raw data can provide light curve and spectra of the wanted science window. 
“Jemx_scw_analysis” provides the light curves for the first IDL script and therefore 
is set to two energy bands and a timestep of 1 second. This is done for the burst’s 
science window and for the science window before. 
The lights curve file is converted to a text file for easier use in IDL. The first 
timestamp is extracted to be used as a time zero for the science windows. 
The first IDL script is then executed. The start and end times of the found time 
intervals are saved as parameters and likewise the other relevant burst parameters 
created from IDL. 
The persistent flux is modelled next, and these steps are dependent on how many 
useable time intervals the IDL script found and if the burst is observed with one or 
two instruments. 
If two useable time intervals are observed with the same instrument this 
“jemx_scw_analysis” will be executed on both intervals and create two spectra with 
8 energy bands and a time resolution of 10 seconds. These two spectra will then be 
combined into one and the relevant xspec script will be executed. 
This is also done if the burst is observed with both instruments, and each instrument 
have two useable intervals. This results in two combined spectra, one for each 
instrument. 
If only one time interval is found the resulting spectrum will be the only one. If only 
one interval is found for each instrument these two will be used for the modelling. 
The resulting xspec output file is read and the model parameters are saved for later 
use. If the model could not be fitted this is noted for later. 
The script then preforms the actual time spectral analysis, a loop is started that will 
loop once for each time intervals the burst has been divided in to by IDL. This step 
is dependent on if the persistent flux in the earlier step was modelled and if the burst 
is observed on one or two instruments. 
The time intervals spectra are made by executing “jemx_scw_analysis”. The spectra 
are made with 8 energy bands and a 0.1 time timestep. If the burst is observed by 
two instruments a spectrum for each is made. 
If the persistent flux is modelled the xspec scripts with the added persistent flux 
modelling is executed. If the persistent flux is not modelled, the xspec scripts with 
the persistent flux spectrum subtracted is executed. 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
When all the time intervals have been analysed the second IDL script is executed. 
If none of the time intervals was fitted, a mean analysis is tested by trying to fit the 
model over the whole duration of the burst. This is preformed like a time interval, 
and end by executing the second IDL script again. 
The last task in a burst loop is to save all the found peak values and burst parameters 
in a file over all the bursts. 
Then the working folder is reset and ready for the next loop.

7 Conclusion 
In this project a program has been developed to perform systematic automatic time 
resolved spectral analysis of x-ray bursts that have been observed with the JEM-X 
instruments. 
To this end the best way to divide the bursts into time intervals was determined, as 
was the minimum number of counts per interval. The optimal way to divide the 
bursts systematically was not to set a count rate or time limit on the intervals. 
Instead the bursts are divided into three major parts; the rise, the peak and the tail. 
The rise is set to be from the beginning to the 90 % peak-value time of the burst. 
This will be one interval unless it is over 5 seconds then is divided into two or more 
intervals dependent on the total number of counts in the interval. 
The peak interval is only one interval, from the 90 % peak-value time till the interval 
contains 450 counts and lasts for more than 1 second. 
The tail intervals start at the end of the peak interval and continues till the end of the 
burst. Each tail interval increases the counts limit to try to achieve enough counts in 
each energy band to get a good fit. 
In section 6.1 Model it is found that the best way for modelling the persistent flux 
and the bursts is where the persistent flux is modelled with a simple power law 
model and the bursts are modelled with a simple black body model. 
When the 10 double observed bursts were compared only two out of the 10 were 
able to be modelled in a convincing way, shows that under the right conditions the 
program produces a good time resolved spectral analysis from JEM-X data. 
A random sample set of bursts observed with JEM-X was selected from the 
MINBAR database and run through the program to set the limitations of the 
program. It was found that the optimal number of successful versus unsuccessful 
modelling of bursts was achieved when the bursts have a peak flux of 2.0 cts/s/cm^2 
and are observed at an off-angel lower than 240 arcmin. 
Lastly, the program is executed on bursts form source U4 1636-536 to show some of 
the potential. The colour-colour diagram from this test is similar to the shape that 
other works has found for this source [7]. From the alpha values, peak flux and 
fluence results it is shown that the bursts are divided into two groups in the colour-
colour diagram, where the dividing line was at hard colour value at 0.4. This short 
analysis does not go deeper into the reasoning for this separation. The purpose was 
to show the features of the program. 
The burst data coming from JEM-X is often not good enough to perform a time 
resolved spectral analysis. There are simply too few counts per second to divide 
most of the bursts into good intervals, especially weak short bursts are difficult to 
handle.
