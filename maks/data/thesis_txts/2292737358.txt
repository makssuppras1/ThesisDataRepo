Abstract 
This thesis is in collaboration with Ingeniører Uten Grenser (IUG) Norway and concerns the issue of how 
to activate professional members at IUG. The facilitator, an MSc student in Engineering Management at 
DTU, facilitated the problem solving process using interactive and participative methods. The framework 
of the project is made up of the four phases of facilitation, the Interactive Planning (IP) paradigm and the 
6-stage Innovative Problem Solving (IPS) model. The role of a facilitator is defined and elaborated using 
the four levels of facilitation. The main findings of this thesis are that; (1) the participative methodology 
produced well-rooted implementable solutions that the organisation are confident to carry out; (2) the 
combination of the IP paradigm and the 6-stage IPS model complemented each other to produce an 
effective approach; (3) the four levels of facilitation and four themes of creativity are highly overlapping 
and interconnected; (4) the efforts of the facilitator started at the intellectual level before moving to 
emotional level at the start of each task; and (5) the project contributed to a deep level of learning at IUG.

1 Introduction 
This section outlines the purpose, the aims of this thesis and the research paradigm in which the thesis is 
rooted. The user group, Ingeniører Uten Grenser, is introduced and their current situation is described. 
Finally, each chapter of the thesis report will be outlined.   
1.1 The Purpose and Aims of this Thesis 
The general aim of this master thesis project is to work with Engineers without Borders / Ingeniører Uten 
Grenser Norway (IUG) and facilitate the development of their strategy. Engineers without Borders is an 
international organisation whose core competence is providing engineers to projects in the developing 
world. IUG Norway is the Norwegian branch of the international organisation.  
Since its foundation in 2011, the organisation has grown to serve over 400 members including students 
and graduated professionals. Whilst this is an accomplishment in itself, the decision-makers at IUG are 
now looking to revise their strategy and create a plan for developing the organisation. The focus of this 
thesis project is to address a problem that relates to this new strategy. 
The problem is that: “while IUG is successful in establishing regional subunits which engage and mobilize 
students, they are experiencing some problems in achieving the same success with adult professional 
engineers who are in fulltime jobs. As professionals are also an important group of members for IUG, they 
would like to understand better why they are struggling with this and furthermore receive advice on what 
measures should be taken to improve the situation.” 
Therefore, the specific aim of this project is to investigate the root cause for this problem and generate 
potential solutions that IUG will be able to create an action plan from. The project will utilise interactive 
methods to accomplish this aim.  
The research programme is framed by investigating and answering the following questions: 
1. What makes interactive methodology appropriate for the case? And what is interactive 
methodology? 
2. How to define creativity, problem solving and creative problem solving (CPS)? Furthermore, what 
are the main creative problem solving models and which is most relevant to the case? 
3. What is interactive planning, including its context, strengths and weaknesses, and its application 
to the case? 
4. What is the role of the facilitator and how can facilitation contribute to creative problem solving?  
5. How are creativity and facilitation interlinked in this case? 
These questions will provide a basis of theory to build the appropriate methods for IUG in order for them 
to be able to find a solution to their problem. Ultimately, this theory will also be central to evaluating the 
methods in hindsight. 
Expected outcomes within the scope of the project are enlightenment on the reasons why professional 
members are less involved in IUG than students, for ideas to be generated at a workshop, and for these 
ideas to be transferred to action plans in the phase following the workshop.   
The writer of this thesis, Katrina Marshall, is hereafter referred to as “the facilitator”.

1.3 User Group – Ingeniør Uten Grenser (IUG) 
Engineers without Borders Norway is an organisation whose core competence is providing technical 
engineering assistance for development projects. Its vision is to alleviate suffering through engineering 
assistance (EWB-I, 2014).  
IUG NO is a Member Association of Engineers without Borders International, which is a federation of 
national EWB Member Associations. The first Engineers without Borders organization was founded in 
France in 1982 and now the federation has grown to include over 37 countries. Each member organization 
is run autonomously but with the same underlying vision and values (EWB-I, 2014). 
IUG NO was established in February 2011 and grew from a local aid commitment among both engineers 
and engineering students across the country. The founders spoke to Engineers without Borders Denmark 
which had been established in 2001 for inspiration on how to set up the organisation in Norway. The 
organization has also been supported by NITO, Norway’s Engineer and Technology Organisation, and 
Tekna, the Norwegian Society of Graduate Technical and Scientific Professionals. 
IUG NO’s objectives are: 
1. To create channels for- and develop technological competences and assistance solutions 
2. To contribute to sustainable and ‘green’ development through technology transfer 
3. To work together with Engineer Without Borders International whenever possible 
The organization currently operates at both a national level and at a regional level. These groups are 
visualised in Figure 1-1. IUG has only two paid employees; the CEO, Bjørn Nissen, and a project 
coordinator, Haykush Olsen, who are both based in Oslo.  The CEO and project coordinator are sometimes 
referred to as “the Administration”. The CEO is broadly responsible for leading the overall organization, 
deciding the direction in which IUG should go, ensuring resources are allocated efficiently and building 
the organisation’s culture. As a project coordinator, Haykush is responsible for many administrative tasks 
including gaining funding, coordinating projects abroad and finding the correct people to assign to 
projects. The administration serve as the coordinating body that brings the strategic and operational

2 Relevance of Interactive Methods and Creative Problem Solving Theory 
This section builds the theoretical context for the specific methodology chosen and designed for the IUG 
case. Firstly, to justify the use of interactive methodology, an overview is given of organisational 
development paradigms, loops of learning and knowledge transfer related to the organisation. Next, a 
definition of creativity, problem solving and creative problem solving. Finally, touching upon the various 
models for Creative Problem Solving (CPS) and the one most relevant to this thesis. Within each section, 
theory is presented and then applied to the IUG case. 
2.1 Organisational Paradigms, Learning and Knowledge 
At the very highest (and broadest) level, it is important to identify the paradigm for organisational 
development in order to justify why interactive methods are appropriate for this case.  
2.1.1 Paradigms for Organisational Development 
There are two main paradigms for organisational development; the Command and Control Paradigm 
(CCP) and the Interaction and Learning Paradigm (ILP). The following definitions are based on the overview 
of the paradigms given by Rasmussen, 2011.  
The CCP utilises a “top-down” approach where the organisation is treated much like a machine. A decision 
is made at the top of the organisation and workers at the bottom of the organisation are to follow 
instructions in order to implement the decision. This shifts all of the responsibility for the organisation 
from the workers to the management, and works for hierarchically structured organisations. The workers 
are trained and monitored, with the expectation that they should perform as best as possible. 
The ILP is more related to a network-structured approach where the organisation is considered as an 
interactive system. This is to say that the organisation is made up of many parts that are interconnected; 
no part of the whole is unrelated to another part. To better understand this ‘connectedness’, a higher 
level of cross-communication and knowledge is necessary. This paradigm therefore requires a flatter 
structure for increased communication. The organisation requires participative decision making and 
interactive leadership. 
In general, CCP is more appropriate for organisations that produce a standardised product and need to 
operate under time and quality constraints. ILP is more appropriate for organisations that produce a 
customised and complex product, and need to reflect and communicate to improve the product 
(Rasmussen L. , 2011).  
Organisations often have elements of both paradigms, particularly at different levels within the 
organisation. The paradigms could also be considered as a lens with which to view an organisation and 
different elements of the organisation are highlighted when looking through each lens.  
It is understood, within this project, that IUG has more elements of the Command and Control paradigm 
at the strategic level. Here, decisions must be made at the top of the organisation. At the operational 
level, IUG has more foundations in the Interaction and Learning Paradigm. The top-level management 
decide the destination that they want to work their way towards, and how they get there is for everyone 
to be involved in. 
As a voluntary organisation, IUG’s volunteers want to be involved in the “how” aspect. There are three 
core reasons for people to volunteer their time; a self-serving drive, a relational drive and to be part of a 
cause that they believe in (McKee, 2003). The volunteers at IUG have a commitment and loyalty to the 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
organisation, they are there to interact with others and to gain knowledge which all relate to the 
Interaction and Learning Paradigm. 
In the same time frame that this project is occurring, the chairman of the steering committee and the CEO 
of IUG are putting together a strategy for the organisation. This strategy document will outline the 
direction that they want IUG to move in, this is in line with the CCP. This project, will contribute to figuring 
out how they will get to the destination, partially driven and supported by the volunteers. As the project 
relates more to the operational level of IUG, and requires inputs from many stakeholders, it is better 
aligned with the ILP. While working within the ILP, interactive methods can be highly appropriate. 
2.1.2 Learning  
As mentioned in the introduction to IUG, the organisation is currently undergoing some major changes. 
These changes are in response to the CEO and administration looking back at what has succeeded and 
what needs improvement, and then making a decision on the improvements to be made. Learning is 
therefore of central importance to IUG. Learning can be defined as “the process of developing new skills, 
abilities and competences as individual and/ or organizational level”. Learning relates to changes within 
the organisation’s behaviour, norms and values. There are three types of learning, these are single loop, 
double loop and triple loop learning (Rasmussen L. , 2011), they are visualised in Figure 2-1 and described 
below.

the behaviour and instigate the change. And third, to detect whether the change in behaviour has

2.1.3 Types of Knowledge, Defining Knowledge and Knowledge Creation 
A central element to learning is understanding what knowledge is and how it can be transferred. 
Knowledge is, of course, essential to learning, in particular the flows of knowledge creation, sharing 
accumulation and practice.  
Heron, 2000, suggests that there are four ways of knowing (Heron, 2000): 
 
Presentational knowing is the knowledge of how to communicate through visualization, 
movement, verbally, etc.  
 
Propositional knowing is intellectual knowledge based on understanding through concepts and 
theories. 
 
Practical knowing is knowing how to do something. 
 
Experiential knowing is achieved by being face-to-face with a person or a group. 
Interactive methods have a higher focus on using presentational knowing to communicate thoughts and 
ideas whilst simultaneously increasing experiential knowing through the face-to-face time that is included. 
There are two basic types of knowledge: tacit and explicit knowledge. Tacit knowledge resides within 
individuals and teams, it is made up of experience, perceptions, insights and know-how. Explicit 
knowledge is codified knowledge, it can be accessed in documents, records and so on. 
Nonaka and Takeuchi, 2001, present the SECI model for how tacit and explicit knowledge interact with 
each other and are transferred to create knowledge. This is a circular process involving socialization, 
externalization, combination, and internalization. It is visualized in Figure 2-2 below. 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
 
Figure 2-2 The SECI Model for Knowledge Creation 
 
Rasmussen, 2011, summarises the four processes as follows: 
 
Socialisation is non-verbalised transformation of tacit-to-tacit knowledge 
 
Externalisation is codifying tacit knowledge which transforms it into explicit knowledge, it could 
be through metaphors, narratives or diagrams. 
 
Combination is the process of combining and exchanging various explicit knowledge to transform 
it from explicit conceptual knowledge to explicit systematic knowledge 
 
Internalisation is the process of transforming explicit systematic knowledge to operational tacit 
knowledge 
These processes for knowledge creation will be relevant for enhancing the knowledge creation possible 
at the workshop. It is also relevant for the entire process of the facilitator communication with IUG, this 
will be reflected on in the evaluation phase.

2.2.1 What is creativity? 
There are many different understandings of the term creativity. Therefore it is necessary to define what 
creativity means in this thesis.  
Gryskiewicz, 1987, defines creativity as “novel associations that are useful” and building further upon that, 
Onarheim and Friis-Olivarius, 2013, define creativity as the “forming of associative elements into new 
combinations which either meet specified requirements or are in some way useful” (Onarheim & Friis-
Olivarius, 2013).

The creativity of people: individuals have different levels of natural creativity and varying levels of

2.2.2 What is problem solving? 
A definition of problem solving is the “process of closing the gap between what is and what is desired” 
and the “act of answering questions, clearing up uncertainties, or explaining something that is not 
previously understood”. It should also be noted that a problem is not necessarily a bad thing (Isaksen, 
Dorval, & Treffinger, 2011).  
A problem, according to Ackoff, 1981, must satisfy 3 conditions (Ackoff R. L., 1981): 
1. A decision-making individual or group has alternative courses of action available. 
2. The choice made can have a significant effect. 
3. The decision maker has some doubt as to which alternative should be selected. 
In the systems view, a problem is not seen as independent from other problems, instead the organisation 
needs to consider interactions of problems. The chunk of interacting problems is defined as a “mess”. This 
term is described in more detail in Chapter 4.

2.2.3 What is creative problem solving? 
Effective problem solving is a balance of creative thinking and critical thinking. Creative thinking is making 
and communicating meaningful new connections by devising unusual new possibilities. Critical thinking is 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
analysing and developing mechanisms to compare and contrast ideas; improve and refine concepts; 
screen, select and support alternatives; making judgments and effective decisions (Isaksen, 1988).  
A creative approach to problem solving should encompass the four themes of creativity; people, process, 
context, and product. Creative problem solving it is “an attempt to advance toward an outcome that is 
new, unstructured and open-ended” and should “help to move from the familiar to the potentially 
unknown” (Isaksen, Dorval, & Treffinger, 2011). This is opposed to non-creative problem solving which 
could be sticking to the status quo, seeing a problem from one direction only, etc.  
By linking creativity with problem solving it diversifies the collection of strategies, tools and approaches 
available which enables a wider range of challenges and opportunities to be handled (Isaksen, Dorval, & 
Treffinger, 2011). 
A new model that shows the relationship between creativity, the creative problem solving process and 
the facilitation process is suggested at the end of Chapter 4. 
2.3 Creative Problem Solving Models 
There are many different Creative Problem Solving (CPS) models that have been developed since the 
1920s, and particularly since the 1940s when the need for an explicit or defined creative process first 
came about (Treffinger & Isaksen, 2005).  Some well-known CPS models include:  
 
Wallas’  (1926) CPS stages preparation, incubation, illumination and verification 
 
Parnes, Noller, and Biondi (1977) Five-Step CPS Process: Fact finding, problem defining, idea 
finding, solution finding and acceptance finding 
 
Amabile (1988) Five CPS Stages: Presentation, preparation, generation, validation and acceptance 
 
Basadur (2004) Four stage model: Generating, conceptualising, optimising and implementing. 
Basadur also recognises CPS as an ongoing cycle: as new problems are discovered and new 
solutions are implemented, new problems and opportunities arise. 
Many of these models have similarities and they can serve as the historical context for the models 
elaborated in this thesis. The models presented in the remainder of this section are those deemed most 
useful for this particular project.

2.3.1 The Creativity Continuum and Selecting Workshop Tools 
The creativity continuum is important to consider when choosing or designing the workshop tools. When 
designing these methods, the facilitator needs to consider what the purpose of the activity is and then 
choose an appropriate tool. Vidal, 2008, suggest there are three types of tools; paradigm preserving tools, 
paradigm stretching tools and paradigm breaking tools. 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
 
Figure 2-5 Three Types of Tools Related to the Creativity Continuum 
Paradigm preserving tools are useful for when neither new elements nor new relationships between 
elements of the problem are introduced. Examples of tools are force field analysis, asking the 5 W’s (who, 
what, where, when, why), a word diamond for new associations. Associations are “an idea, image, feeling, 
etc. suggested by or connected with something other than itself” (Dictionary.com, 2015).  
Paradigm stretching tools are useful for when either new elements or new relationship between elements 
are introduced. The tools utilize unrelated stimulus and could include metaphors, role storming 
(pretending to be another person to see it from their perspective), reversing the problem or reversing the 
assumptions. 
Paradigm breaking tools are relevant when both new elements and new relationships between elements 
are introduced.  These tools could include wishful thinking, rich pictures and creating a wild story, to name 
a few. 
The creativity continuum ranges from ‘safe’ paradigm preserving creativity to ‘unsafe’ paradigm breaking 
creativity. The safety element refers to the fact that people move further out of their comfort zone as 
they stretch and then break the paradigm.  
There is also a continuum for the types of tools that can be used which ranges from structured to less 
structured tools. Structured tools might involve checklists and charts whereas less structured tools might 
involve picture stimulation. People often have a preference for a side of the spectrum; for example, 
engineers tend to prefer more structured tools (Vidal, 2008). 
In selecting tools, Vidal states that there are three considerations, these are the organizational culture, 
the task or problem and its complexity, and the facilitator’s ‘style’. 
These paradigm preserving, stretching and breaking tools will be integrated into the design of the 
workshop, outlined in Chapter 5, and then used for evaluating the methods chosen.

3.1 What is facilitation in general? 
In simple terms, a facilitator supports a group in problem solving. A facilitator should be able to design 
and manage problem solving processes, create a pro-active atmosphere and synergetic effects (Vidal, 
2005). A visualisation of the facilitation of problem solving processes including its main components can 
be seen in Figure 3-1 below. 
 
Figure 3-1 Vidal's Facilitation Process Model (Vidal, 2005) 
Isaksen, 1988, claims that there are three main roles within creative problem solving: the client, the 
facilitator and the resource group. The person or entity with the greatest degree of ownership person is 
the client, this could be a single person, group of people or a company. The facilitator helps the client use 
the problem solving process, and the resource group provides input and ideas. The resource group 
includes all people that are involved in the process, not just the participants of a workshop. It is important 
for each person involved in the process to understand what their role is (Isaksen, 1988). In this case the 
resource group is made up of selected stakeholders and the ‘group’ referred to in Figure 3-1 is the group 
that takes part in the workshop includes the resource group and client. This group should ideally represent 
the interests of key stakeholders affected by the problem. 
For smaller companies or organisations, the resource group is likely to overlap with the client. This is true 
for this case, where IUG is the client and the resource group will consist of people who are leading, 
volunteering and part of the membership of IUG.

organisation. However, because of the voluntary nature of this organisation, the decision is only valuable

3.3 The Levels of Facilitation 
Rasmussen, 2011, argues that there are four levels of facilitation; these are the physical, intellectual, 
emotional and synergistic levels. The components of these levels are important for the facilitator to 
understand before the process, to prepare so as to be able to optimize the process when it happens. The 
levels of facilitation are also integrated into a new model, linking them with the CPS process at the end of 
this Chapter.  
3.3.1 The Physical Level of Facilitation 
The physical level deals with all of the external factors that can distract or make workshop participants 
feel uncomfortable.  
Whilst planning the event, the facilitator needs to ensure that: 
 
The space is the right size for the number of participants. This is dependent upon the number of 
participants and also the kind of activities they will be doing.  
 
The characteristics of the space, or room, are suitable for the event 
 
The seating in the room should be organised so that participants can see each other and see the 
facilitator when necessary (Isaksen, 1988). Circular, or semi-circular seating arrangements can be 
particularly useful for allowing face-to-face contact with many people and is also effective in 
promoting equality amongst the participants as no one has to be the ‘head of the table’. With 
larger groups, seating may have to be split into several sub-groups, in which case it is also 
important that the facilitator has access to each table. 
 
Temperature and flow of fresh air should be monitored, is there a window to open if it gets too 
hot and stuffy? Or a heater in case the room is too cold? 
 
There are toilet facilities and spaces for breaks (other than the room) 
 
Enough tables and chairs will be available for the event. 
During the event, the facilitator needs to be make sure that: 
 
The appropriate materials are available for the planned methodology. 
 
Beverages and food are available. 
 
There are no distracting noises and that there is sufficient lighting for participants to be able to 
hear and see each other. 
These considerations will be noted at the preparation phase, during the workshop, and be reflected on in 
the evaluation of the workshop.

3.3.2 The Intellectual Level  
The intellectual level of facilitation relates to managing the creative problem solving process. This includes 
the tasks summarised in Figure 3-2. 
 
Figure 3-2 Intellectual Tasks of Facilitation 
These stages are summarised below based on the description in Rasmussen, 2011. 
1. Clarifying process rules and roles: The facilitator sets the ground rules, shares the agenda for the 
session and what the participant’s role is in the process. Whilst sharing the agenda, it is not 
necessary to share the timing because there may be need to change the time schedule during the 
process. 
2. Guiding problem formulation: If the problem has been formulated beforehand, the facilitator 
should guide the participants to enable them to make more precise and concrete formulations of 
sub-questions related to the general problem formulation, to ensure that their understanding of 
the matter at hand is aligned. If the problem needs to be formulated by the participants, the 
facilitator should guide them through the appropriate methodology to achieve this. 
3. Initiating creative processes: The facilitator needs to explain the steps of the creative process, 
answering the questions ‘what do we need to do’, ‘why should we do it’, and to a certain extent 
‘how should we do this’, to kick start the process.  
4. Enforcing strategic development possibilities: This is where the facilitator needs to support the 
group in answering ‘how should we do this’ if they get stuck. The participants may not ask for 
help, in which case the facilitator needs to look out for the need to support the group in this way. 
5. Supporting action planning: In this final step, the facilitator supports the group by asking critical 
questions to ensure that the participants are keeping the main goals and sub-goals in mind. 
Additionally, to make sure that resource allocation, possible barriers, plans to overcome barriers 
and expected results are considered. 
It is important that the facilitator achieves these tasks without interfering in the content of the discussion 
(Rasmussen L. , Chapter Fifteen: The Art of Facilitation, 2011). This is the difference between suggesting 
an idea and supporting a participant’s understanding of the task so that they can suggest an idea.

3.3.3 The Emotional Level  
The emotional level of facilitation is about handling participants’ and their own feelings during the 
process. The crucial aim, at this level, is to creative a ‘safe’, informal and open atmosphere in which the 
participants will interact. To achieve this aim, the facilitator will endeavour to create resonance among 
the participants. Resonance is a feeling of general trust and acceptance, even if the participants are 
disagreeing with one another (Rasmussen L. , 2011). If resonance is achieved within a group, the 
participants will be listening to one another, withholding judgement, experiencing intensified discussion 
and open up the possibility to think creatively. 
The facilitator should be able to deal with extreme positive and negative feelings of the participants. 
Whilst paying attention to the emotions of the participants, the facilitator must also attention to his or 
her own feelings during the process. If the facilitator’s behaviour is weighted with a particular emotion, 
that can also affect the emotions of the participants. 
In this case, the emotions of the participants are not anticipated to be extreme because the subject of the 
workshop should not be highly conflicting or stressful for them. The main focus will be to create an 
atmosphere of trust and safety for the group to operate in. This will also link to managing the group 
dynamics, which is discussed in the following Section 3.4. 
3.3.4 The Synergistic Level 
The synergistic level of facilitation is reached when all of the other levels culminate to synthesise a ‘flow’ 
within the process. Here, the participants feel integrated into the process, are interested in listening to 
one another and become a collaborative community motivated to move forward. When synergy occurs, 
it unlocks and expands the creative potential of a group. At this point, the facilitator needs to step aside 
and allow the process to “establish its own momentum” (Rasmussen L. , 2011).  
This level may not be reached if other levels are not sufficiently successful, for example if all the 
participants are distrustful of each other to the degree that they will not dare to speak their minds. This 
level can also vary in its timeframe ranging from a matter of minutes to many hours. 
3.4 Group Selection, Development and Dynamics 
A facilitator needs to be aware of group selection, group development and group dynamics, particularly 
for working on emotional and intellectual levels during the workshop. A definition of group dynamics is 
“the way groups and individuals act and react to changing circumstances” (Forsyth, 2006). 
3.4.1 Group Selection 
There are two major considerations in group selection for the workshop, these are group size and the 
inclusion of major stakeholders. 
The ideal group size ranges from 4 to 12 people depending on the task (Slocum, 2005). In terms of the 
output of creative ideas, many researchers have found that dyads, a group of two, will generate the most 
ideas (Thornburg, 1991; Taylor & Faust, 1952; Murray, 1964)  and that productivity decreases significantly 
as the group increases above 5 people (Bouchard & Hare, 1970). Groups of 5 to 7, including the facilitator 
are typical for IPS (Isaksen S. , 1988).

In this case it is also necessary to consider that various stakeholders need to mix in groups to allow for 
diverse perspectives to be included. In selecting groups it is important to consider the diversity of people, 
jobs, ages and levels of expertise (Isaksen, 1988).



3.4.4 Icebreakers 
An icebreaker is a short activity that the facilitator can use to activate the participants. Icebreakers can 
used as a fun break, to introduce a topic or as a part of the session (Heathfield, 2015). They can be 
particularly useful when the topics are new or unfamiliar to many of the participants involved, the 
participants come from different cultural and/or social backgrounds, the group is newly formed and needs 
to get to know one another, or when the participants need to be motivated to buy into the purpose of 
the event (Rasmussen L. , 2014). 
Potential effects of ice-breakers can include helping the participants to relax, break down cultural, social 
and/or psychological barriers, energizing and motivating participants to be alert towards the aims of the 
workshop, helping the participants to “think outside the box” and creating a positive group atmosphere 
(Rasmussen L. , 2014). 
Icebreakers will be used in the workshop with IUG. The icebreakers will be presented in Chapter 5 and the 
purpose for each icebreaker will be presented together with the instructions for the activity.

4 Justification for the Specific Methodology 
Interactive Planning (IP) is at the core of the framework for the problem solving approach designed for 
the IUG case. The purpose of this chapter is to present a summary of the reason IP was originally 
developed, important factors that form the grounds for IP, a critique of IP as well as strengths and 
weaknesses. This is followed by an overview of the phases that make up the IP method and, finally, how 
IP has been adapted for this case. 
4.1 A Historical Context for Interactive Planning 
Russell L. Ackoff was the founder of Interactive Planning methods. This section presents a summary of 
Strategies, Systems, and Organisation: An Interview with Russell L. Ackoff (Ackoff R. , 1997) in order to 
present that historical context for Interactive Planning and the major differences between Interactive 
Planning and the planning methodology in status quo at the time. 
Up until the 1970s, operations research and management science, which contributed to the “plan and 
prepare” school of planning, was at its peak of popularity within US corporations. However, by this point 
this method of planning was becoming decreasingly relevant and its limitations were moving into focus. 
Ackoff states that he and his team recognised the need for a more holistic view, to use design rather than 
research as a way of handling systems of interacting problems and to dissolve problems rather than solve 
problem components. 
Ackoff introduced the Interactive Planning (IP) paradigm in 1981. This was in response to the need for a 
‘systems view’ methodology with several factors setting it apart from planning methods already in the 
status quo.  
Before IP, planning was based on forecasting through current and past data and then planning accordingly 
for the future. However, in an increasingly complex world, Ackoff states that these forecasts are certain 
to be wrong. Instead of planning based on the present situation, IP is centred on where the organisation 
wants to be in the future and then planning accordingly. Essentially, the key difference is a reversal in the 
planning based on the future to the present rather than from the present to the future.  
This also means that planning is based on assumptions rather than predictions. To clarify, assumptions 
are founded through possibilities whereas predictions are grounded in probabilities. Various 
combinations of assumptions allow planning for multiple scenarios, or contingency planning. The main 
advantage being the understanding that it is not possible to control the future, and therefore IP allows an 
organisation to prepare for a future that they cannot control. 
The ‘systems view’ aspect to IP, mentioned above, is another key deviation from planning methods at the 
time. The organisation is to be viewed as a system with component parts that are interlinked and related 
to one another. The core argument being that by resolving a problem within the component part may not 
resolve the overall issue. Ackoff uses the analogy of a broken-down car; a car is made up of many 
components and if you take them all apart then you no longer have a car, so it is necessary to understand 
how all of the components connect to one another in order to fix it. 
To build on this, it is not only necessary to view the organisation as a system but also to view “the 
problem” in more complex terms. This is the recognition that problems are not independent of one 
another. Often, finding a solution to one problem creates several new problems. Ackoff calls these 
systems of interacting problems “messes”. 
==PAGE:40==
40 
 
Another key difference to IP is a higher focus on control and monitoring. Feedback is central to 
understanding whether the organisation is on track or on a deviating path in relation to their planning. 
Collecting data for analysis is one element of the control, and organisational learning is another. 
Organisational learning infers that the organisation can “remember”, reflect on itself and make necessary 
changes (see Section 2.1.2). In order to properly learn, Ackoff argues that it must detect both errors of 
omission; something is not done that should have been done, and errors of commission; something that 
has been done that should not have been done (Jackson, 2000). 
The final factor that sets IP apart is its design approach, which is elaborated in the following section. 
4.1.1 Ackoff’s types of problem solving and approaches 
Ackoff states that a design approach is at the core of the Interactive Planning paradigm. To gain a 
definition of the design approach, it is necessary to recognise Ackoff’s three types of problem solving and 
associated ‘solution devising’ approaches (Ackoff R. L., 1981): 
Resolving and the Clinical Approach: Resolving is selecting a solution that is “good enough”; it is a result 
of using a clinical approach. The clinical approach relies heavily on past experience and current trial and 
error for its inputs. In other words, the approach includes more qualitative methods than quantitative. 
The problem is resolved, often only in the short term. 
Solving and the Research Approach: Solving is selecting the “best-possible” solution or optimal solution. 
This is related to the research approach which is based on a quantitative scientific methods, techniques 
and tools. This approach is often employed by managers or decision-makers that are seeking growth 
rather than development. 
Dissolving and the Design Approach: Dissolving is changing the nature, and/or the environment, of the 
entity in which the problem is embedded so as to remove the problem. This utilises the design approach 
which uses methods of both the clinical and the research approaches. This approach is employed by 
decision-makers whose principal organisational goal is development, not just growth. Dissolving a mess 
results in a longer term solution than the other two approaches. 
The design approach is central to the IP paradigm, meaning that messes are dissolved rather than finding 
short-term solutions. This requires a holistic perspective of the organisation and methodology that 
incorporates both qualitative and quantitative elements. As IUG is currently making changes to their 
organisation, they are looking to make changes that have a long-term impact. The design approach is 
therefore appropriate in helping the organisation to dissolve the mess at hand. 
4.2 Principles of Interactive Planning 
In short, IP is creating a future “by continuously closing the gap between where it is at any moment of 
time and where it would most like to be” (Ackoff, 2001). Ackoff distinguishes Interactive Planning by 
comparing it to Reactive Planning, Inactive Planning and Pre-active Planning. The differentiating factor for 
IP is that it is the only type of planning that considers the past, present and the future of the organisation 
(Hansen & Rasmussen, 2011).  
There are three principles that provide the basis for IP; some of them have been touched upon in the 
previous sections; the participative principle, the principle of continuity, and the holistic principle 
(Jackson, 2000). 
The participative principle emphasizes the importance of involving stakeholders with a higher focus on 
the process of planning rather than the actual plan itself. To quote Ackoff; “It is through involvement in 
==PAGE:41==
41 
 
the planning that stakeholders come to understand the organization and the role they can play in it. It 
follows, of course, that no one can plan for anyone else – because this would take away the main benefit 
of planning” (Ackoff R. , 1970). 
This principle is supplemented by the following statements from Coffman, Revit and Hayes in more recent 
literature. Coffman encourages “engaging stakeholders in co-design rather than simply getting their 
input” (Coffman, 2014). Participative methods enable the group members to feel more engaged and 
means that they will be more motivated to carry the actions through (Revit, 2007). Nadler also argues that 
involving people in the decision making process helps to increase their motivation to change (Hayes, 
2010).  
The principle of continuity underlines the importance of knowing that stakeholders’ values, wants and 
needs are going to change over time. It is also drawing attention to the fact that it is impossible to predict 
the future and that unexpected events can occur. Accordingly, it is important for an organisation to 
continuously monitor stakeholder needs and external factors to check that a plan is still valid.  
Finally, the holistic principle relates to the systems view which has already been described in previous 
sections. To put the systems view into more specific terminology, two groups of thought are reductionism 
and expansionism. Reductionism is breaking the problem into small parts and analysing each part 
separately. Reductionist thinking leads to the consideration of maximising benefits for its shareholders or 
“owners”. Expansionism focuses on the parts in relation to the whole. Expansionist thinking allows for 
consideration of many stakeholders and their interests (Hansen & Rasmussen, 2011). IP belongs to the 
expansionist group of thought. 
Within the systems view of an organisation, Ackoff states that there are three main aspects for managers 
to consider. The main objective is for development to occur with all of these aspects in mind (Ackoff R. , 
1999): 
 
The control aspect: responsibility for themselves through the running and management of the 
organisation. 
 
The humanisation aspect: responsibility for their parts through the values, norms and behaviour 
of the members. 
 
The environmental aspect: responsibility for the wider system in which they exist through the 
organisation’s relationship with a larger community. 
4.3 IP Methodology 
There are four thought processes and five main phases in IP. The four thought processes are idealisation, 
realisation, idealised design and making design decisions. 
The five phases of Interactive Planning are elaborated in the following sub-sections. It should be noted 
that these phases can be started in any order and that none of the phases should ever be considered 
‘completed’ (Jackson, 2000). 
4.3.1 Formulating the mess 
This phase is also known as the situational analysis. The organisation is analysed for a ‘mess’, which is a 
system of interconnecting parts, of threats and opportunities. The purpose of the phase is to “determine 
how the organisation would eventually destroy itself, if it were to continue behaving as it is currently” 
(Ackoff, 2001). 
There are four steps within this phase (Hansen & Rasmussen, 2011): 
==PAGE:42==
42 
 
1. A systems analysis: a detailed description of how the system currently operates. 
2. An obstruction analysis: an identification and definition of obstructions to corporate 
development. 
3. Reference projections: projections of aspects of the organisation’s future under the assumption 
that no changes are made and that no changes will happen in the organisation’s environment. 
4. Reference scenario: The outcome of steps 1, 2 and 3 are combined to create reference scenario 
which describes how and why the organisation would destroy itself if no change is made. 
 
The result of this phase is a scenario describing the future of organisation if no changes are made.  
4.3.2 Ends planning 
The purpose of the ends planning phase is to develop an understanding of where the organisation wants 
to go. This is done by idealisation of what the organisation should be and how it should be ‘redesigned’ 
accordingly (Ackoff, 2001). 
There are only two constraints for the idealisation; (1) that the design should be technically feasible, (2) 
and that the designed systems should be capable of surviving in the current environment.  Furthermore, 
the new design should enable the organisation to learn and adapt its performance (Hansen & Rasmussen, 
2011). 
The idealised design process has three steps: 
1. Formulation of a mission statement which outlines the organisation’s general aspirations and 
reasons for existence. 
2. Elaboration on specifications of desired properties for the idealised organisation. 
3. Designing an organisation that has the attributes developed in previous steps.  
The result of the ends planning phase is an idealised scenario describing how the organisation desires to 
be. 
4.3.3 Means planning 
In this phase the reference scenario from phase 1 and the idealised scenario from phase 2 are compared 
to recognise the gap between the two scenarios. Means planning is the identification of means by which 
to fill the gap (Hansen & Rasmussen, 2011, p. 146). 
The steps of this phase are to formulate alternatives and then evaluate them. In formulating alternatives, 
the problems in reaching the idealised design need to be either resolved, solved, or dissolved (Hansen & 
Rasmussen, 2011). 
4.3.4 Resources planning



Additionally, to know what to do about shortages or excesses that are identified in time (Ackoff, 2001).

4.3.5 Design of Implementation and Control

4.4 Application and Critique of IP 
Interactive Planning can be used for many types of groups and organisations, from corporations to 
government agencies and NGOs of various sizes and with various goals. The approach’s flexibility and wide 
applicability is one of its main strengths (Lumbo, 2007). It can be used in relation to global issues such as 
urban planning and environment, as well as in connection with organisational change, product 
development or strategic planning (Hansen & Rasmussen, 2011). 
Ackoff claims that the following advantages can be gained through the IP methodology (Ackoff R. L., 1981), 
saying that it: 
 
Facilitates the participation of all stakeholders and gives them an opportunity to have a say in 
their own future 
 
Allows incorporation of aesthetic values into planning 
 
Generates consensus 
 
Generates commitment and mobilizes participants 
 
Releases suppressed creativity and harnesses it to individual and organisational development 
 
Expands the participants’ concept of feasibility 
 
Eases implementation 
Ackoff named the field of science related to IP, Social Systems Science (S3). Jackson, 2000, recognises 
Ackoff’s work has had a “colossal impact” on operations research and systems communities, in some ways 
revolutionising the status quo. His work has a far reaching impact in many dimensions including 
geographically and across disciplines. Jackson, 2000, also recognises the following points as main 
advantages to the IP approach (Jackson, 2000): 
 
IP seeks to minimise petty differences of opinion between individuals and groups by focusing their 
attention on the future they desire for their organisation. 
 
The approach allows different stakeholders to incorporate their own aesthetic views into the 
idealised design. 
 
IP allows an organisation to respond to diverse aspects of messes through multi-perspective view 
of the system.  
It is an advantage that Ackoff does not see IP as replacing machine-age thinking, but instead sees the two 
as complementary approaches. Related to the organisational development paradigms previously 
mentioned, Ackoff’s approach recognises and integrates both the Interaction and Learning paradigm and 
the Command and Control paradigm. 
In spite of wide set ‘success’ of the method there is, of course, negative criticism of the method. There 
are four main criticisms that are important to note (Jackson, 2000): 
1. IP is based on the belief that it is always possible to obtain consensus. Critics argue that instances 
exist where stakeholders have deep-set value differences or conflicts which would make 
consensus impossible.  
==PAGE:44==
44 
 
2. The obstacles to participation are largely omitted, and that Ackoff takes participation for granted. 
He assumes that stakeholders will want to participate and be allowed to participate (Haftor, 
2011). Furthermore, there may be extreme variances in the power of the stakeholders which 
means that the stakeholders cannot participate equally, as the method intends. It is argued that 
the methodology is inevitably constrained by its high dependence on participation. 
3. Critics state that Ackoff limits the scope of a project so as not to challenge his client’s or sponsor’s 
fundamental interests. In such a circumstance, Ackoff claims he can go only as far as 
“circumstances permit” but critics argue that these circumstances are consequential of his 
approach. 
4. Critics state that IP is too much based in realms of “subjectivism” and “idealism”, not recognising 
the structure of reality, or “real life issues”, such as power divides or conflict. Moreover, that this 
“idealism” means that Ackoff is unable to truly understand how a change comes about and can 
be promoted.  
Ackoff has vehemently disagreed with his critics about consensus, standing by his beliefs that there is no 
such thing as an unresolvable conflict. He also stands by his approach in regards to spending large amounts 
of time with both managers and sponsors, and argues that sponsors are often highly enlightened about 
the organisation. Ackoff absolutely disputes that structures of social reality exist, in relation to the fourth 
criticism (Jackson, 2000). 
As regards the second criticism, about the obstacles related to participation, Ackoff agrees that there are 
challenges but argues that there are ways around these challenges. He also argues that, even if full and 
equal participation is not always possible, the incremental changes that result from the approach are 
better than waiting for “a judgement day when all wrongs will be corrected” (Jackson, 2000).  
Lumbo, 2007, lists five reasons that organisations are hesitant to use IP methodology, some of them 
overlap the criticism presented in the section above: 
1. It can be difficult for some organisations to imagine that they have to build their organisation 
from scratch. This approach requires that participants move out of their comfort zone, which can 
be especially hard if the organisation is currently doing well. 
2. The democracy of the process is not often welcome in organisations that have been used to 
making top-down decisions. 
3. It requires a lot of coordination both in terms of managing the flow of information and in terms 
of engaging participants. As an extension, it therefore demands a top-level executive who believes 
in the methodology and is enthusiastic about what it can do for the organisation. 
4. It can be time consuming. 
5. It requires a skilled and experienced facilitator to obtain favourable results. 
In relation to the IUG case, it is assumed that the advantages that can be gained from using the approach 
outweigh the criticism and disadvantages pointed out in the latter of this section. It is not anticipated that 
there will be with unresolvable conflicts in this case and it is recognised that full participation is not 
possible, but that the participation achieved will be sufficient to provide a useful solution for IUG. 
IUG is still a young organisation so there is likely to be less resistance from stakeholders and easier for 
participants to “build the organisation from scratch”. Furthermore, the leaders within the organisation 
are on-board with the methodology, having already begun to think about their desired future for IUG 
before the start of the project.

4.4.1 IP and the Facilitator 
Based on interviews with Interactive Planning facilitators, including Russell Ackoff himself, Lumbo, 2007, 
argues that there are specific competences required by an IP facilitator. The following competences are 
valuable to the facilitator (Lumbo, 2007): 
 
Understanding of group dynamics and the differences in personalities and ideas of the 
participants. 
o Ability to read and analyse group dynamics on the spot in order to guide the group in a 
productive way. 
 
Ability to keep the participant’s energy levels up and give them a sense of wanting to stay focused 
on the task and on wanting to get to the final product. 
 
Ability to create a sense of ownership for participants - ownership stems from both understanding 
the process and believing in it. 
o Good communication skills to be able to clearly explain the rationale and activities of the 
methodology. 
o Charisma to make the participants believe in the importance of the tasks. 
 
Have strong communication skills. 
o Ability to quickly boil down things being said by different mouths into a very concise 
statement. 
o Efficiency in recording responses of the participants on a board or on flipcharts which 
makes participants feel listened to. 
 
Communication skills need to be coupled with strong analytical skills. 
o Need to know what questions to ask, when to ask them, and how to structure these 
questions to elicit good answers without making the participants feel uneasy or defensive. 
o Need to know how to rephrase or reframe statements to enhance understanding and 
highlight agreements and disagreements. 
 
Need knowledge of the organisation and industry and know the language that the organisation 
uses. 
 
Have strong interpersonal or ‘people skills’ and ability to establish an open and non-threatening 
atmosphere. 
 
Ability to quickly establish a rapport with the participants because it is imperative that participants 
have respect for the facilitator. 
 
Have highly developed negotiation skills and the ability to influence a group to quickly reach a 
decision. 
This list of competences will be considered by the facilitator for this project before the workshop and used 
for reflection in the evaluation phase.

Next, the facilitator will explain that all of the people present here today come with slightly different 
perspectives and that this is valuable to the session. Furthermore, that we should embrace these different 
perspectives by discussing them. The purpose of saying this is to encourage the participants to feel 
individually valued and to begin laying the ground rules for the session. 
Finally, the facilitator talk a little about idea generation which is a creative process. Thinking creatively 
can be fun but it can also be tiring so the facilitator will inform the participants that she has planned breaks

guiding the creative process. 
To assist with the creativity of the groups, there will be four ground rules for brainstorming (Vidal, 2008): 
1. No criticism of ideas or people 
2. Activities are about quantity, screening will come afterwards 
3. Free-wheeling is welcome, meaning it is ok to be random 
4. Ideas can be combined, added to, or modified as often as the group feels necessary 
The facilitator will endeavour to speak enthusiastically and positively in tone but also confident and firm 
in her statements. The hope is that this will influence the participants by setting the mood whilst at the 
same time trying to build a rapport, and trust, between the participants and the facilitator.  
5.5.6 Icebreaker 2: Blind Art 
 
Main purpose: Creativity & communication 
This icebreaker will happen just before all the brainstorming activities begin. The aim, therefore, is to 
emphasize the importance of communication and make the groups feel more comfortable with each other 
which in turn reduces barriers to creativity.  
Icebreaker Instructions: Blind Art 
The whole group is divided into their sub-groups of 5-6 people. One person needs to volunteer or be 
chosen to be the “artist”.  
Each group is at a table with a large sheet of paper and a marker pen. 
The “artist” should blindfold themselves or close their eyes, hold the pen over the paper and await 
instructions from their group. 
The rest of the group is given a post-it which tells them what their “artist” should draw. It should be 
something that everyone knows and can describe (e.g. an animal, well known building, etc.).  
The group then instructs the “artist” how to draw the object without saying what the object is. For 
example, draw a small circle then move your hand to the right and draw a vertical line, etc.  
The group has 5 minutes to create the picture.  
After the 5 minutes, each sub-group should show their picture to the whole group.  
Time: 10 minutes

and try different ways of communicating their directions. It is expected that the directions will get more 
specific as the task goes on, as participants begin to see the task from the blindfolded person’s 
perspective. 
This activity could be considered as part of the ‘storming’ process of the group formation. Perhaps certain 
members will be starting to take certain roles and have disagreements on how to approach the task.

5.5.8 Icebreaker 3: The ‘HA’ Game 
Reasoning: The purpose of this activity is to inject some energy into the room. It gives the opportunity for

exhalation (followed by an inhale). Forced breathing is intended to refresh the mind and encourage the

The four overlapping elements of creativity are people, process, context and product. It is argued that the 
result of the CPS process is the creative product. This product will come to existence through the 
combination of the creative context, creativity of the people involved and the creative process utilised. 
Some of the components are uncontrollable to the facilitator but should at least be recognised, for

temperature. Although there are uncontrollable aspects, it is argued that there are many ways that the 
facilitator can influence the creativity of the context, people, process and therefore product. This

that the participants start clustering thoughts at a very early stage. For example, writing certain ideas on 
one specific colour. In this instance it did not appear to be destructive. If a facilitator wants to completely 
split the diverging and converging stages, it might be a good idea to make the post-its (or materials) as 
uniform as possible. This could also be an area for future research. 
Negative Circumstances can Create Unity: The venue was too small with no windows to open for fresh 
air or temperate control which meant that the room was too warm. There was also a conference across 
the hallway which meant the doors could not be left open for long periods of time. This was, of course, a 
negative influence on the process and the facilitator was unable to change these factors at the time. 
However, it is argued that a small amount of good came out of the situation because the group were 
unified in their complaining about the warmth and it gave them common grounds to talk about. 
8.2.2 The Emotional Level and the Creativity of People 
Group Formation: The room was quite small which meant that, practically, there could only be four 
groups. This meant that there would be two groups of 5 and two groups of 6. (This is an example of the 
physical and emotional levels overlapping.) This amount of subgroup members worked well for the 
activities because it was small enough for all members to easily interact and hear each other. At the same 
time, it was enough to have representatives from different local chapters, the steering committee and 
the administration. 
The sub-groups were planned to include a combination of people with little experience and a lot of 
experience at IUG. The sub-groups ended up being a combination of experience, gender, locations and 
administrative levels. This mixing within groups was planned to a certain extent, an unanticipated 
precursor to this mixture is that people will naturally prefer to stand next to someone they know, which 
ultimately meant that they ended up in different groups from the people with similar backgrounds. In 
addition to the planning, luck also played a small part in how well mixed the groups were.  
==PAGE:88==
88 
 
Group Development: The groups probably didn’t get a chance to progress to the ‘performing’ stage 
because they only met each other in the morning. It helped that IUG decided to have them in groups from 
the beginning of the day rather than just in the afternoon session. The groups were able to work together 
to enough of a degree to make their work useful. All subgroups reached the norming stage because they 
all appeared to be cohesive, respecting each other and working together. 
Group Dynamics: It was more difficult to manage group dynamics for a large group with several sub-
groups than one group of people. The facilitator was constantly scanning the room to look out for 
disruptive behaviour such as people talking to people in other groups, people sitting back looking 
uninterested, or anything unusual.  
Individual Roles in the Group: In all subgroups, a participant emerged as the coordinator, often at the 
same time fulfilling the role of initiator-contributor and orienter. This person could be considered to be 
the “leader” of the subgroup, taking on the responsibility to ensure the subgroup is on task. In some tasks 
a person emerged as the recorder, who would write on the poster or place post-its on the poster, this 
person was sometimes also an opinion seeker.  
One of the subgroups had a person who took a dominant evaluator-critic role and the facilitator had to 
step during the brainstorming stages as a procedural technician to remind the subgroup that they needed 
to be ‘criticism free’ at this point.  
There were instances with several subgroups where the facilitator embodied the opinion seeker role, as 
well as encouraging another participant to be an elaborator when she visited each subgroup and wanted 
to draw the participants out. The facilitator personified these roles using communication techniques 
which is reflected on in the next section. Throughout the process, the facilitator took on the energizer and 
encourager roles. 
All of the participants were agreeable and friendly with one another. None of the participants displayed 
the very disruptive individual roles like the aggressor or the blocker. Some individuals may have been 
more dominant than others but not to the extent that they appeared to manipulate the entire process. 
The fact that the subgroups ended up with quite similar ideas supports the argument that there wasn’t a 
single individual leading a group off track.  
Overall, it was difficult to identify individual roles such as the playboy, recognition-seeker, self-confessor 
or help-seeker because the facilitator was not present long enough to observe the entire conversation for 
each subgroup. 
Energy Levels: Despite including breaks, ice breakers and trying to monitor energy levels, it was difficult 
to truly prevent the participants from becoming very tired and towards the end becoming reluctant to 
participate. It was a good decision to have the most important topic first and this will be considered at a 
future workshop. 
Facilitator Energy Levels: The facilitator could also feel that she was getting more tired as the session 
went on. She addressed this feeling by putting extra effort into remaining positive and enthusiastic.

8.2.3.2 
Applying Communication Techniques 
Throughout the workshop, the facilitator visited each sub-group multiple times during each exercise. The 
facilitator did not necessarily intervene each time but observed how the sub-group was working together. 
When the facilitator did intervene, she used the facilitation techniques outlined in Section 3.3.2 (simple 
echoing, selective echoing, paraphrasing, drawing the speaker out, racking, general encouraging, selective 
encouraging, balancing, linking and summarising). Some examples of how the communication techniques 
were applied are described below. 
There were quieter members of each sub-group, often younger participants, and the facilitator used 
selective encouraging to ask the person if they wanted to contribute. Because the facilitator could not be 
present to observe the sub-group throughout the process, she would sometimes ask for an update from 
the sub-group and in particular direct the question to a quieter participant. To give the quiet person an 
opportunity but not force them to speak, the facilitator asked the entire group and made eye contact with 
the participant at the end of the question.  
Towards the end of the session, when participants were all getting a bit tired, some groups became slightly 
disengaged with some members losing focus. The facilitator used general encouraging to get the sub-
group back on track, by inviting everyone to discuss the topic at hand. Specifically, the facilitator would 
ask something like “so what do you think about this…” whilst looking each sub-group member in the eye. 
At the end of each exercise, all sub-groups presented their main findings to the whole group. Often similar 
words were used to present the ideas that had been generated within each sub-group so to give more 
meaning, the facilitator would ask the presenter to give an example. The intention was to ensure that the 
participants were not just saying the keywords and assuming that “everyone knows what we mean” 
through drawing out the speaker.  
During the presentation the facilitator used paraphrasing, simple echoing and selective echoing to ensure 
that the entire room had a common understanding of the ideas presented. The facilitator also used these 
techniques when being updated on what each sub-group was doing, in order to understand the sub-group 
and to ensure that the subgroup also had the same understanding of the ideas being discussed. 
During the force field activity one of the subgroups became highly focused on the time constraints of 
volunteers, so most of their suggestions were related to lack of time. Upon observing this, the facilitator 
used the balancing communication techniques to ask the participants if they can think of other elements 
unrelated to time. The subgroup continued and were able to think about the question from different 
perspectives.  
==PAGE:91==
91 
 
Finally, summarising was key for closing each exercise and activity, the facilitator took the many ideas that 
had been presented from different subgroups and tried to summarise with some recurring themes. The 
intention was to acknowledge the participants’ ideas, to ensure them that their time is being well-spent 
and to motivate them to continue with the next activity. 
8.2.4 The Synergistic Level and the Creative Product  
Because of the scale of the group it is difficult to note exactly if and when each sub-group reached the 
synergistic level. With one facilitator for a 22 people, it means that the facilitator’s focus is on optimizing 
the physical, emotional and intellectual levels. The facilitator is constantly observing, reflecting and 
deciding whether it is necessary to intervene – in other words the facilitator is looking out for the 
negatives. The facilitator not being there to witness the synergistic level is not considered as a negative 
because, as mentioned in the theory, when a group reaches the synergistic level, the facilitator needs to 
take a step back and let them continue. 
As the facilitator did not witness the synergistic level, how do we know it happened at all? When the 
synergistic level is achieved, the group becomes a ‘creative collaborate community’. A proposed 
description is that it is when ‘it just clicks’; the purpose and task are clear to the participants and they are 
in a state of resonance which makes them feel safe to speak freely with one another. The facilitator has 
experienced this as a sense of relief; when the turbulence of trying to understand the process and the 
frustration of trying to communicate with team mates is overcome (for a period at least). During this 
period the participant feels that the group is being productive and feel engaged in the process.  
At the workshop, there were times when sub-groups would all stand up without being told to do so from 
the facilitator, were engaged in the task and animated in their expressions. By taking a step closer to the 
sub-group, it was possible to hear that dialogue was bouncing from person to person.  When these things 
occurred, it is assumed that the synergistic level was reached and the facilitator therefore left the 
subgroup to continue uninterrupted. 
The creative product exists tangibly in the form of the posters; the ideas written down and prioritized. 
This explicit knowledge is used after the workshop to create an action plan which will lead to tangible 
results. For example, the development of an online ‘Humanitarian Engineering Hub”. The creative product 
can also be taken to include:  
 
The relationships formed during the process,  
 
The knowledge of the process that the participants gained and can re-use, 
 
The experience that participants gained in their own creativity, 
 
The knowledge that the facilitator gained. 
8.2.5 Final reflection on the Levels of Facilitation and Creativity 
It is argued that the four levels of facilitation and the four themes of creativity align in a very 
complementary way. The components of both models have similar aspects and by combining the two, it 
creates a link between facilitation research and creativity research. For example, that creativity training 
could be relevant for organisations that want to use participative methods.

8.3.1 Strengths of the Approach 
IUG was pleased with the result of the approach, which indicates to a certain extent that this was an 
appropriate problem solving approach. Because the facilitator took part in only a fraction of devising a 
solution to the mess, it could be argued that the approach had already been adopted by the organisation 
before she arrived. For example, the decision had already been made at IUG that major changes had to 
be made. This facilitates the idea central to IP which is imagining that the organisation needs to be built 
from scratch. There was also already a sense of urgency to finding a solution that had been developed 
before the facilitator arrived. These factors that aligned IUG with the IP methodology before the facilitator 
arrived surely contributed to the success of the approach. 
The approach was also successful because of its underlying principles. The participative principle showed 
to be the most valued principle in this project. In the final meeting with IUG, the administration stated 
that one of the strong points of the project was the involvement of key stakeholders. The administration 
and steering committee could probably have generating many of the ideas during the “means planning” 
phase but because many key stakeholders were involved, the outcome is more rooted in the organization. 
The administration said that gives them confidence in implementing the actions and it also motivates 
them.  
The final result of the project, once integrated into IUG’s overall strategy, will “change the nature of the 
entity involved” which is intended to dissolve the initial mess, or problem, at hand. Only time will tell if 
the problem is truly dissolved but the impact is anticipated to be positive regardless of that.  
8.3.2 Weaknesses of the Approach 
The Interactive Planning phase of “formulating the mess”, according to Ackoff, should have four elements; 
a systems analysis, obstruction analysis, reference projections and reference scenario. In this case, these 
four elements were inferred but did not have corresponding reports. The facilitator gained a systems view 
of the organization and overview of obstructions by asking stakeholders with different perspectives about 
the strengths and weaknesses of IUG. The reference projections are related to the membership survey 
indicating such a low percentage of active members, assuming without specific forecasts that there are 
not enough active members for IUG to be sustainable. The reference scenario, in many ways, is to 
continue as IUG is currently operating with some moderately content and activated members and many 
unsatisfied and inactive members. As regards the entire “formulating the mess” phase, the four elements 
served as direction for the facilitator but not as “assignments” that were completed. Perhaps this is how 
Ackoff intended it to be, in saying that each stage cannot be completed.  
Detailed and specific resources planning did not occur in the facilitator’s presence and neither did the 
implementation and control phase. This is due to the geographical limitations of the project, with the 
==PAGE:94==
94 
 
facilitator based in Denmark and IUG based in Norway. However, the main reason that the facilitator was 
not present was that it was out of the scope of the main project. The facilitator’s main role was to assist 
with the phases up until means planning and then the chosen ideas could be integrated into the strategy. 
The main criticism of the IP in relation to this approach is related to the participative principle that lies at 
its foundation. A difficult question to answer is “how much participation is enough?” The number of 
participants at the workshop was sufficient in giving multiple perspectives but would it have been better 
to have more participants? It is understood that a high level of participation is needed, but it is difficult to 
draw lines under exactly how many people and who should participate. The process may become more 
complex as more participants are included. Another issue lies with non-participating stakeholders and to 
what extent they should be included. It seems difficult to find a balance between informing the non-
participating stakeholders and boring them with too much unrelated information. In this instance, the 
final product which is IUG’s strategy will be communicated to all stakeholders, participating and non-
participating, but the results of the workshop were only sent to participating stakeholders. 
8.3.3 The Approach and the Facilitator 
Lumbo, 2007, described some characteristics of a facilitator for IP and Vidal, 2008, states that a facilitator 
should use tools that complement their style. The facilitator for this project has the opinion that the IP 
methodology was suitable for the principles it is based on. These principles align with the facilitator’s 
beliefs in the importance of participation, understanding a situation from multiple perspective and of 
monitoring and control. The facilitator also believes in the methodology, as far as creating a future based 
on desires rather than predictions.  
The characteristics that Lumbo mentions include ability to understand group dynamics, having ability to 
keep energy levels up and participants focused, and having strong interpersonal skills. Reading about 
group dynamics, about the organisation and the industry were preparation activities that contributed to 
the facilitator’s ability to use this methodology. Naturally, the facilitator is positive, approachable and 
social which makes it easier to build connections with the participants. The ability to create a sense of 
ownership is made up of “both understanding the process and believing in it”. The facilitator’s belief in IP, 
mentioned above, will have contributed to her ability to create a sense of ownership within the 
participants. 
In general, IP was a good fit for the facilitator and the 6-stage IPS model provided the structure that 
facilitator required. 
8.3.4 Final Reflections on the Facilitator and the Approach 
The approach used in this project was suitable for both the client, IUG, and the facilitator because of the 
underlying principles of participation, continuity, and using a multi-view, holistic approach. The IP 
approach was used more as a guideline than as specific instructions and the 6-stage IPS model served to 
help give the facilitator a better idea of how to allocate her time. The overall combination of IP and 6-
stage IPS, was an effective combination for this case.

 
The facilitator would use the same caution in setting up contracts and discussing the project scope 
so as to manage the expectations of the client. There would be constant updates to these 
expectations to ensure everyone is on the same wavelength. 
 
A change would be for the facilitator to spend more time with the organisation in determining 
the topics for the workshop. The first topic was a good topic to discuss but the second topic might 
turn out differently with greater interaction at the problem finding stage. 
Workshop Methods: 
As a general point, the workshop was effective with only one facilitator and one observer but at least one 
other facilitator might have improved the quality of the facilitation for the session. This would be 
particularly relevant if there was a higher level of conflict between the participants or more difficult 
behaviour. Even with the relatively low level of conflict and cooperative behaviour, the facilitator was 
unable to observe subgroups for long periods of time. This meant that certain behaviours could have been 
missed or simply that opportunities to enhance the process were missed.  
Other key lessons learned during the workshop are: 
 
The facilitator would prepare some written instructions to complement the verbal instructions. 
This would help to guide the participants and enable the facilitator to pay more attention to the 
emotional level of facilitation from the start. 
 
The purpose of the icebreakers should be well explained and the facilitator will practice the 
icebreaker in a familiar environment before using it at a workshop. 
 
Negative formulation would be re-used and has shown to be both popular and effective as a 
paradigm stretching tool. Another paradigm stretching tool would be chosen or designed for the 
second topic because the paradigm preserving tool that was used, 3-6-5 brainwriting, was 
unsatisfying. If the 3-6-5 brainwriting technique was to be used, it should be used at the beginning 
of a session because of the high intensity of the task.

9 Conclusion 
This thesis has been successful in providing a process for IUG to gain important information about their 
members and key stakeholders, as well as to utilise the tacit knowledge from various stakeholders to 
generate ideas that were then turned into actions. These actions were then integrated into IUG’s overall 
strategy for the coming years. 
The four phases of facilitation served as an adequate framework for the facilitator to use as a basic 
checklist of tasks that should be done at each phase. Not all tasks were carried out by the facilitator herself 
but she was aware of the person carrying out the task and the status. There was a variation in the level of 
interaction throughout the project with high interaction in the initial stages, the most interaction at the 
workshop and then a decrease in interaction as the project moved to the implementation stage. This was 
advantageous because the administration of IUG gained a higher level of ownership for the process as 
time went on, and ended with the administration fully responsible and motivated to implement the action 
plan. 
The principles of Interactive Planning; participation, continuity and a holistic view, were highly relevant to 
IUG and the problem solving approach enabled the development of implementable action points. The 
administration had confidence in the results produced because of the participative methodology. The 
phases of Interactive Planning were used as guidance for what to do at different times of the project and 
the 6-stage Innovative Problem Solving model served as a tool to help the facilitator in allocating her time 
and efforts; knowing when to do each task. This combination of the IP paradigm and the 6-stage IPS model 
formed a balanced approach that the facilitator could use effectively. 
The facilitator operated on the four levels of facilitation during the workshop and it has been argued that 
the levels of facilitation (physical, emotional, intellectual, and synergistic) overlap with the themes of 
creativity (context, people, process, and product) and visualisations have been presented to show this 
relationship. An area for future research could be investigating this link further to discover how a 
facilitator can be more effective in increasing the creativity of a workshop. 
During the workshop with IUG, the facilitator noticed that her efforts were more at the intellectual level 
at the beginning of each task to ensure that the participants are on the right track and afterwards her 
attention could shift to a balance of the emotional and intellectual levels as the task progressed. To ensure 
attention at both the intellectual and emotional levels from the start, more than one facilitator should be 
present for a group of this size (22 participants). 
Finally, the methods and icebreakers chosen for the workshop were generally positively received. The 
facilitator should be aware of the purpose of each activity and be able to explain the reasoning of the 
activity to the participants. The facilitator could also use written or visual aids to help explain instructions. 
The facilitator should test out icebreakers in a familiar setting before using it at a workshop so that she 
can have confidence in knowing what the experience will be like rather than just reading about it. 
The entire problem solving approach including the creative problem solving process at the workshop 
contributed to IUG’s organisational learning. It is suggested that the depth of learning is seen on a 
spectrum instead of only categorizing it into the three types of learning loops. This project was a part of 
IUG’s strategy development process which relates to a deep level of learning that affects the core activities 
at IUG and will affect the entire organisation and all of its stakeholders.
