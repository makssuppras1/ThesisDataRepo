Abstract
International shipping is at the brink of signiﬁcant changes and decisions regarding decarbonization.
Te era of non-GHG measures has oﬃcially come to an end with the ”Initial IMO Strategy” in 2018. Among
other measures, a highly controversial issue is the Market Based Measures (MBMs). Te main focus of the
study is to investigate a potential imposition of a levy scheme and the consequences on the environment
and the maritime supply chain. Moreover, a viable option of an alternative low carbon fuel that could
replace conventional fossil fuels is discussed. For the analysis of the above scenarios, economic, logistics
and environmental criteria were taken into account, leading to speed reduction and consequently CO2
reduction. Te proposed fuel achieved higher levels of carbon reduction. However, it seemed that this
option is not viable when it exceeds the price levels of the levy scheme therefore, making it non-atractive
for ship operators. Overall, opportunities for further development towards a more sustainable path exist.
What is lef, though, is for them to be implemented.

Introduction
International shipping carries approximately 90% of global trade, and it is expected that this percentage
will be extended. One of the challenges of the shipping sector is its contribution to climate change and
speciﬁcally to the reductions of Greenhouse Gas emissions by ships. It is estimated that the international
shipping industry is responsible for the 2,2% of the total CO2 emissions and in particular, containerships,
bulk carriers and oil tankers to be the three major ship classes that are the main responsible for the ma-
jority of CO2.
Forecasts are not so optimistic and thus the main aim of the current years is a future towards a more
zero-carbon activity or at least closer to this direction. However, to achieve this, it is considered essential
that the environmental performance of the maritime transport logistical supply chain to respect traditional
economic criteria and deliver win-win solutions.
For the mitigation of CO2 emissions, the measures are grouped in three categories, logistics, techno-
logical and MBMs, even though in some cases are directly correlated and interdependent. Furthermore,
it has been extensively studied that the speed reduction can achieve acceptable environmental outcomes
and not only, decreasing the fuel cost, improving the overcapacity problem and reducing the emissions
of carbon dioxide. However, in the cases where the speed exceeds the optimal, they are side eﬀects like
unsatisﬁed demand from the increased throughput, increasing inventory costs and freight rates, and it
induces the danger of modal shif. As a result, the environmental challenge of the shipping sector is not
so straightforward.
Examining the regulatory activity of the shipping sector, it is noticed that much progress has been
remarked since the establishment of the International Maritime Organisation (IMO), for the mitigation
of GHG emissions. In particular, in 2011, the EEDI energy eﬃcient design index and the Ship Energy
Eﬃciency Management Plan (SEEMP) were put into action. In 2017 EU Monitoring, Reporting and Veriﬁ-
cation (MRV) was introduced by IMO, and in 2018, the Initial Strategy for the reduction of CO2 emissions
is proposed.
Te Initial Strategy of IMO refers, among others, to the possible implementation of Market-Based Mea-
sures (MBMs) and the use of more alternative fuels, promising, further action, regulations, guidelines, short

and long term measures. One of the ten original IMO MBM proposals that have been under consideration
the last years is the levy Scheme proposed by several Member states.
Te scope of the current thesis project is ﬁrstly to examine the economic, logistics and environmental
implications of a potential bunker levy scheme and secondly to examine the implications of an alternative
low carbon fuel use. In the later case, it would also be taken into account a possible retroﬁt in the ship
and thus an investment, so that the alternative fuel will be compatible with it. At the end of the study,
the comparison between the two scenarios will provide ﬁndings regarding the most proﬁtable investment
of the two and examine the circumstances under which the scenarios will be viable and beneﬁcial for the
shipping industry.
In this way, it is expected that there would be a proﬁt decline, depended on the prevailing market
conditions and the structure of the levy. Another expected result would be speed and fuel-consumption
reduction.
Te project is structured in 10 chapters. Te introduction outlines the main problem associated with
the existing mitigation measures and policies, mentioning the need for supplementary actions. Te rest
of the project is organized as follows, chapter 2 describes the current situation of the shipping sector, and
chapter 3 reports the environmental impact of it. Chapter 4 analyzes the measures for reducing the GHG
emissions and the existing regulatory activity and Chapter 5 deﬁnes the concept of Market-Based measures
(MBMs). Chapter 6 describes the main characteristics of the examined case study together with chapter
7 and 8 that provide the overall modelling approach and results of a levy scheme and a retroﬁt scenario,
respectively. Chapter 9 performs a comparative assessment and a Cost-Beneﬁt analysis for the ﬁnancial
evaluation of the projects. Chapter 10 includes the limitations of the current study and the summary of
the most signiﬁcant ﬁndings of the study, together with the proposals that should be addressed.

Te shipping industry
Te shipping industry has a fundamental role in the development of the global economy, playing a
signiﬁcant role among the several transport modes. According to (UNCTAD, 2018), it carries about 90% of
the international trade and estimated that it would be doubled by 2030. More than 70 per cent of its value
being transported on ships and handled by seaports worldwide (UNCTAD, 2018). Taking into account its
values of productivity and the percentage of environmental footprint that has on the planet, it has the least
contribution to the ecosystem damages. Furthermore, it has the power to connect people, goods and oﬀer
a range amount of services covering long distances across the globe. All of these are accomplished by the
low costs, the signiﬁcantly big capacity and the ability to cover long distances in an eco-friendly way in
comparison with other modes.

Environmental impact
As a transportation leader of goods, the shipping industry is responsible for its contribution to the most
challenging issues of the decade, which is climate change. Rising sea levels, changing weather paterns or
even transforming biodiversity are some of the few physical consequences of climate change. Tus, among
others, signiﬁcant responsibility of the shipping industry is to ensure that all its relevant activities and
services are working systematically and sustainably. Tat means working in a green way that adds value to
the society, brings proﬁt to the company and reduce the environmental footprint of the industry. In other
words and according to (Psarafis, 2016), green maritime transportation means achieving an acceptable
environmental performance of the maritime supply chain while at the same time satisfying traditional
economic criteria, therefore trying to make a win-win outcome.
Figure 3.1: Green = Sustainable (Rosche, 2016)
Te last years, the shipping industry has made signiﬁcant progress to follow a more sustainable path.
Regarding emissions, much progress has remarked. In early 1997 in Kyoto, the United Nations Framework
Conference on Climate Change (UNFCCC) has deﬁned the International Maritime Organization (IMO) as
the United Nations distinct oﬃce with responsibility regarding the wellbeing and security of transportation
and the prospect of marine pollution by ships. IMO is in charge of managing maritime air emissions.
Further down, more regulatory activities have been presented by IMO and the shipping industry.

3.1
Maritime Emissions
Concerning maritime emissions, they are divided into several classes. Green House Gases (GHGs), of
which CO2 is the most prevalent, methane (CH4), and nitrous oxide (N2O), among others. Non-GHGs
include mainly sulfur oxides (SOx) and nitrogen oxides (NOx). Also, several others pollutants like, for
example, particulate mater (PM), volatile organic compounds (VOC), black carbon and more, are also
released.
Te impacts of the majority of the above gases on the worldwide atmosphere are diverse, and most
are considered harmful if they are not managed. Among diﬀerent consequences, GHGs results in global
warming, SOx cause acid rain and deforestation, and NOx cause human health problems and not only.
However, its widely acceptable that carbon dioxide, the main GHG emission, is mostly responsible for the
climate change.

4
Measures for reducing GHG emissions
Emissions produced by the shipping industry are growing fast (IMO, 2014), regardless of several ac-
tivities demonstrated to have surprisingly critical results. For example, in some cases, notwithstanding
handling NOx and SOx may prompt an expansion in CO2 emissions or the opposite. (Patricksson, et al.,
2015). Even though new approaches and strategies to inﬂuence and improve the shipping industry environ-
mental performance are essential, it is also fundamental that their economic and environmental outcomes
are previously totally understood. According to Kosmas and Acciaro (Kosmas et al.,2017), the main obsta-
cle of maritime transport is the global character and particularly the business-to-business nature of it. In
comparison with other transportation segments that regulations and measures for emission reductions are
already successfully implemented in diﬀerent ways and various countries around the globe, in the ship-
ping industry things go slower. One single example is the tax collection in the car sector. To the sections
following, there is a discussion about the main three categories of measures for reducing GHG emissions,
the already regulatory activity of the industry and a reference to the potential use of alternative fuels.
4.1
Tree categories of measures
Te range of measures for the reduction of GHG emissions from the maritime industry can be classiﬁed
into the following three signiﬁcant categories: technological measures, logistics- based (or operational)
measures and last but not least the market-based measures or MBMs.
Talking about the ﬁrst category of measures, (Gkonis et al., 2012) it is stated that it includes more ef-
ﬁcient (energy- savings) engines, more eﬃcient ship hull and designs, more eﬃcient propellers, cleaner
fuels (low carbon content, LNG), alternative fuels (fuel cells, biofuels, etc), devices to trap exhaust emis-
sions (scrubbers, etc), energy recuperation devices, cold ironing in ports, various kites and other kind of
measures. Or it could also be in a more strategic way, that is, build future ships with reduced horsepower
put so they can’t sail quicker than a prescribed speed. (Psarafis et al., 2013).
Logistics-based (or operational) measures involve speed optimization, weather routing optimization,
optimal ﬂeet management and deployment, eﬃcient supply chain management, and others that impact
the logistical operation.
Tird, there are the so-called market-based measures (MBMs) that are recommended as medium-term
measures and are yet to be enforced and decided upon between 2023 and 2030. Tese constitute Emissions
Trading Schemes (ETS), Carbon Tax or Levy on the bunker fuel and a list of others that are discussed later.

However, it is essential to note here that the division of the three categories is artiﬁcial, in some cases.
For instance, the imposition of an MBM proposal possible push the shipowner to embrace into logistics-

is increased by a bunker levy, a shipowner may operate in slow steaming in the short run and he may
purchase a more energy eﬃcient fuel or build a more energy eﬃcient ship in the long run. As a result,
applying one of the three categories is not necessarily the aim, nor always the desirable strategy. All the
categories are correlated and interdependent.
According to (Psarafis and Kontovas,2013), reducing speed has obtained an signiﬁcant role as a mea-
sure for reducing emissions. In general, several research has been already done for speed reduction and
this is because, it has various beneﬁts and a signiﬁcant power to control the market. Speciﬁcally and ac-
cording to (Psarafis, 2016), speed reduction can cause: fuel cost reduction, improvement of a depressed
market to overcome the recent problem of overcapacity. From an environmental point of view, the main
beneﬁt, is the reduction of air emissions. Tis is because the fuel consumption is a cubic function of speed
and not only linear.In this way, win-win solutions are achieved.
However, it could not be so easy and obvious. Behind speed reduction, there is not always win-win
solutions, but there are some side eﬀects as well. For example, speed reduction means that the same level
of throughput should be maintained, either by buying more ships, or building bigger ships or both. Tis
cause extra ﬁnancial costs, environmental, as well as logistics like the addition of extra ships in the global
ﬂeet. Furthermore, speed reduction will lead to an increase in the freight rate and in the inventory costs
because there would be hauling cargo at a reduced speed. Last but not least, speed reduction will push
shippers to choose another mode of transport.
Afer all, what is essential to take a note of, is that if the aim is the greener shipping it is more com-
mon to discuss about economic criteria and less norm about environmental ones. In some cases, such
ﬁnancial criteria lead straightforwardly to environmental criteria, as it is mentioned. So, win-win solu-
tions achieved. In some other cases, where the objectives modiﬁed, the ﬁnancial criteria lead to reverse
outcomes.

4.2.1
EEDI
In 2011, the period of not - taking into account GHG emissions oﬃcially arrived to the end and IMO
adopted the Energy Eﬃciency Design Index (EEDI) for new ships. EEDI is designed for ships more than
400 GRT that built afer the year of 2012 (IMO, 2018c). Tis means that covers 95% of all ships according to
(Fenhann, 2017). From the EEDI, domestic shipping is excluded and some cruise ships are yet to included
into the EEDI. Following the requirements, the EEDI of the ships should not extend a speciﬁc upper bound
of the required EEDI, which is only a function of dead weight and the type of the ship. Aim of the IMO
policy makers is that the required EEDI become more strict through the years and new ships become
more energy eﬃcient (Psarafis, 2018). Generally, EEDI is a benchmarking plan which gives a sign of a
merchant ship’s CO2 emissions in connection to its transport work. It analyzes the CO2 level emissions
and the transport work of a vessel and compare this ratio with the IMO set necessity. Te decision of EEDI
was one of the initial steps of IMO’s drive to eliminate CO2 emissions from sea transport.
4.2.2
SEEMP
In 2011, IMO presented the Ship Energy Eﬃciency Management Plan (SEEMP), which has been en-
forced since the year 2013. SEEMP is used by ship-owners as a mechanism/administration tool to control
the environmental performance of every ship and also to improve the energy eﬃciency of ship operators.
Being a mandatory management plan, SEEMP is applicable to all (existing and new) vessels above 400GT
so as to report fuel consumption trends and energy eﬃcient atitude (Fenhann, 2017). Furthermore, it
has four steps: planning, implementation, monitoring and self-evaluation and improvement. Te Energy
Eﬃciency Operational Indicator (EEOI) has been proposed by the IMO as a monitoring tool in the SEEMP.
4.2.3
EU Monitoring, Reporting and Veriﬁcation (MRV)
MRV was ﬁrstly agreed in 2015. However, MRV was then introduced in August 2017 and since then it
has a few stages. MRV is the EU’s CO2 information gathering system for all ships geting inside harbors in
the EU area. Moreover, it is responsible to give incentives for energy eﬃciency improvements by making
this information publicly available. Speciﬁcally, the basic requirement of MRV is the daily measurement
of CO2 emissions by shipowners whose ships exceed 5000 GT. Every ship should provide a veriﬁed annual
report and enclose data on its route, as well as cargo and fuel data (Europa, 2016).

transportation developed. However, according to IMO (IMO, 2014) in 2030, the shipping fuel consumption
will be covered by alternative fuels, on a bigger scale than now.

Market-Based Measures (MBMs)
During the period 2006 to 2011, IMO recognized the need for an extra plan work, that apart from tech-
nical and operational measures, includes Market Based Measures (MBMs) for the reduction of the Green
House Gas (GHG) emissions and the energy eﬃciency improvement of the maritime shipping sector. For
the submission of the MBMs, several member states and organizations, with diﬀerent backgrounds, con-
tributed. However, in May 2013 this plan work was cancelled because of a conﬂict between developed
and developing Member States. Tus, MBMs were added in the list of future candidate measures for the
reduction of GHG emissions but are yet to be enforced.
According to (IMO, 2016), MBMs are considered as an eﬀective GHG emission relief, and their role is
double, in particular:
• to give economic incentives to the shippers so that they invest in more ecological technologies and
improve the energy eﬃciency of their shipping operations
• to oﬀer a tool able to eliminate and balance the CO2 production
Furthermore, the possible role of MBMs has either in-sector GHG reductions or out-of-sector GHG
reductions. In-sector reductions come either by paying more for buying fuel, and this induces slow steam-
ing or by purchasing a ship that is more fuel eﬃcient and cannot go faster, and this also results in slow
steaming. Out-of-sector reductions come from making a shipowner pay for his CO2 emissions as bans and
the taking advantage of this money to invest them outside of the maritime sector (Psarafis, 2012).

operator decides among a range of 10 to 14 knots, to which speed the ship will sail.
In our cases though, there are two diﬀerent speeds of the tanker, the laden and the ballast speed. And
this is because in the one leg the ship is full of cargo and in the way back is empty. As a result, the outcome
will give us one laden speed with range from to 10 to 14 knots and one ballast speed with range from 10
to 14 knots. For purposes of the optimization simpliﬁcation, the values of the variable (speed) are discrete
numbers, with a step of 0.5 knots. So, laden speed can take a value among this range of speeds: 10.0, 10.5,
11.0, 11.5, 12.0, 12.5, 13.0, 13.5, 14.0 and the same happens with the ballast. In other words, there are 81
pair of laden and ballast speeds that are examined in order to ﬁnd the one pair under which the revenue
is maximized.

Conclusion - Limitations
Nowadays, in the maritime industry, there is an increased interest in the reduction of GHG emissions
and the incentives that would lead the ship operators and owners into taking more environment-friendly
decisions. It seems that the last years and especially with the ”Initial IMO Strategy”, there is much progress
towards a more ecological direction but there is still room for more drastic measures.
Tis project presented a modelling approach with two primary goals: the ﬁrst one was to study the
consequences of a levy scheme under the MBMs context, and the second, was to examine the implications
of alternative fuels’ use. Te case study was performed under three diﬀerent aspects: the optimal speeds,
the emited CO2 and the proﬁtability of ship operators. Te analysis conducted led to signiﬁcant conclu-
sions which are presented below.
For the levy scheme, it was concluded that a levy imposed on the top of the current bunker prices
would lead to a speed reduction and proﬁt loss, but also it would have a direct reduction on the CO2
emissions. On the other side, the case of purchasing an alternative fuel and retroﬁting a ship will induce
similar results. However, the vital diﬀerence is that even with the revenue loss and speed reduction, the
level of CO2 emissions would have a wide range. Speciﬁcally, CO2 emissions can be reduced up to 70%
given the use of low carbon fuel.
From the comparative assessment of the scenarios, it was concluded that the use of alternative fuel,
was ﬁnancially more atractive under speciﬁc values of a given levy. More speciﬁcally, the price of the
alternative fuel should be lower than that of the levy scheme, and higher than the current fuel prices.
Within the framework of a thesis project, assumptions are made for simpliﬁcations or sometimes be-
cause there is no available data and knowledge around a topic. For this reason, a couple of assumptions
made. Speciﬁcally, it is assumed that for purposes of simpliﬁcations, the Emissions Control Areas (ECAs)
excluded by the sailing areas of the examining ship. Te load factor of the shipping operations is 100% for
the laden leg and 0% for the ballast. An important limitation of the case study, however, was the fact that
the conventional and the alternative fuels have the same fuel curve. Te last limitation of the project is
that the carbon coeﬃcient of the non-fossil fuel assumed because of the limited sources of knowledge. In
any case, with limitations or not, research will not put an end in this controversial issues. Each one of us
50
==PAGE:52==
th
will put a small piece of his study, contributing towards to the main goal.
Overall, it is clear that the proﬁt loss leads to speed reduction and it is not easy to oﬀset what is gained
and what is lost. Behind every future action, always, there will be side eﬀects and side reactions. Probably,
it is not obvious but solutions inducing environmental optimal performance are not always the ones which
lead to optimal economic performance. Tus, It is vital for policy makers to ﬁnd the best way to inﬂuence
