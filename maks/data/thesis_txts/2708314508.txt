 III 
Abstract  
Organic synthetic chemists are often faced with time consuming sequences of individual batch experiments to find 
a suitable set of reaction conditions for a desired transformation or when optimizing a reaction to achieve better 
yields. High throughput experimentation (HTE) can significantly decrease the duration of this process by screening 
various reactions in parallel, with a minimum usage of starting material as well as enabling the testing of a wide 
range of chemical conditions at once. The main bottleneck of HTE is however the data acquisition, analysis, and 
interpretation, as these are often time demanding processes. To obtain rapid data analysis and interpretation, in 
this thesis I have developed an automated data analysis tool of LC-MS data. The data analysis tool consists of 
multiple modules with their own function, such as data loading, integration, calculation of product conversion 
based on an internal standard, detection of new significant peaks, m/z-value search, and more. Various 
visualisations can be generated both to display spectral information and plate-based results. Furthermore, this 
project established a HTE platform for photochemical reactions as well as a detailed workflow, allowing the user 
to change the experimental procedure from batch to plate-based screening. The focus for this platform is 
photochemistry, but the workflow and analysis tool can be extended to other chemical reactions. Photochemical 
reactions have many interesting properties, as they can ease the synthetic route and good control of reaction 
propagation is achieved. The HTE platform and analysis tool was successfully tested on a known reaction from 
the literature and applied to discover a new photo induced [2+2] cycloaddition between N-methylmaleimide and 
a N-boc-nortropinone-derived alkene.

1 Introduction  
1.1 Automation in chemistry  
Automation is becoming an integrated and popular tool in numerous scientific fields, including chemical organic 
synthesis, where it especially has seen an increase in applications during the last decade.1 With this comes the 
opportunity to conduct chemistry more efficiently by running reactions in parallel, gain enhanced reproducibility 
by robotics and reduce the risk of safety hazards by carrying out synthesis in enclosed spaces.1 To obtain maximum 
use of the new techniques it requires a deep understanding of the different steps and components in the semi- or 
fully-automated workflow. In a paper by Christensen et al.,1 the authors argue that automation is not fully 
automatic, but instead an iterative process in which careful decisions and constant adjustments are needed. Many 
considerations are similarly required before building or investing in automated tools for synthesis. Ideally 
automated steps or platforms for synthesis of chemical compounds should be applicable for multiple reaction 
types, and not specific for one type only. Many academic groups as well as companies are implementing 
automation in their laboratories today, and several approaches exists depending on the desired outcome. This 
could be high-throughput experimentation (HTE) in microtitre plates (MPs), flow chemistry or the incorporation 
of robotic units.  
 
1.1.1 High throughput experimentation 
HTE is one of the most popular ways to implement automation, new technologies are constantly being developed 
and is already carried out in many pharmaceutical companies. Chemists seek to optimize and find new synthetic 
routes and here HTE can be used to search in a broader chemical space compared to the previous classical "one 
at a time" batch setups.2 With HTE it is possible to screen 1536 reactions in one plate, which would not be possible 
with traditional batch chemistry in the laboratory.1 The more general definition of HTE is a workflow where 
several reactions run in parallel.2  HTE has a broad application area and can be designed to answer various 
chemical questions. Within biological research HTE is already an established technique and is carried out on a 
standard practice.3 Screening of fragment libraries against a protein to determine the biological activity in low to 
medium throughput is also common procedure. However, recently HTE has gained great interest in chemistry, 
since it is possible to screen a large collection of reaction conditions, such as different reagents, catalysts, 
additivities, concentrations, etc., in tandem to optimise the synthetic route of new lead molecules or discover novel 
reactions.4 
 
==PAGE:13==
 
 2 
There are several benefits of HTE compared to the traditional experimental setup in the laboratory. Using robotics 
and pipettes, it is possible to conduct experiments on a nano- and microgram scale. This reduces the amount of 
material consumed and enables the screening of a wide range of reaction conditions.4 HTE allow to screen more 
conditions and generate more data in a reduced time compared to manually conducting all the experiments in a 
laboratory. It can also be desirable to include conditions which are not expected to give high yields, and thereby 
broaden the search in the chemical space. This can result in new unexpected findings or at least a deeper initial 
testing of the reaction conditions.5 By screening a larger set of random reactions the chance of being “lucky” and 
discovering new unexpected hits increase.6  
 
From a practical point of view, it is often easier to conduct biological experiments in high throughput (HT), since 
they are done in aqueous solutions and close to room temperature. Whereas in chemistry, various solvents are 
used, different temperatures can be required and often the solutions are heterogeneous, hence it can be more 
problematic to run.3 Another challenge with HTE is handling of solids, where only few successful examples are 
seen in the literature.4 Due to the low amount of material used and the larger surface area of the well plates 
relatively, it is desired to use non-volatile solvents which have a higher boiling point, such as dimethyl sulfoxide 
(DMSO).4 If volatile solvents are used it is important to seal the MPs, so the solvent does not evaporate during 
the acquisition. In general, sealing of MPs are favourable, as a plate with 96 samples easily can take several hours 
to analyse, to reduce evaporation of solvents.  
 
The larger data set generated from HTE needs to be accurate and efficiently analysed, both quantitatively and 
qualitatively.4 Since reactions are run in parallel, even up to hundreds or thousands reactions at a time, a lot of 
data are generated and each reaction needs to be examined, which can be very time consuming. Especially if 
having long experimental times and data interpretation is done manually. For example, analysing 96 samples with 
a 5-minute liquid chromatography (LC) program will take 8 hours, not including start up and rinsing time for the 
LC instrument. Thus, both the data acquisition and interpretation should be fast. The number of reactions screened 
can increase rapidly, when planning a HT experiment. Consequently, if material or time is limited, it can be 
necessary to consider which reaction parameters are expected to affect the reaction outcome the most, and thereby 
prioritise these parameters when selecting screening conditions.3 Furthermore, as a consequence of the increasing 
amount of chemical data, new computational and statistical tools to efficiently interpret the data are in high 
demand.2

1.1.2 Flow chemistry  
Flow chemistry is a complete topic of its own, but in short it involves the flowing stream of chemical reactants 
through a reactor in a continuous manner, instead of during chemistry in batches. Figure 1 illustrates a setup of a 
continuous flow reactor. Some of the advantages are easier scale up, enhanced heat transfer, better mixing, 
safety and reproducibility.7  
 
Figure 1: Continuous flow setup for a microreactor. The incoming reagents (purple and red circle) react to produce the 
continues flow of the product (blue circle). 
Aligned real-time analysis can make it possible to continuously measure the product flow and the effect different 
variables have, to discover the optimal conditions. Compared to HTE in plates, flow chemistry is less suitable to 
screen discrete variables, such as different solvents, reagents, catalysts, etc. and conduct initial screenings for 
reaction components. In HTE the different reaction components in each well are screened concurrently, where in 
flow they are screened sequentially. However, for continuous variables, such as temperature, concentration and 
time, flow is often more applicable. For example, by varying the solvent and reagent flow rates, the reaction time 
and concentration can be altered.7 Different concentrations can be screened in HTE, but only for a discrete number 
of values. Temperature screening in HTE it is much more difficult to conduct, as this requires individual plates 
and small batches to be set up. In conclusion, for screening of different reaction matrices HTE is more efficient 
and flexible, as flow is sequentially screening and thus it takes longer to test several conditions.  Additionally, 
HTE is often easier to implement, as many institutions already have some of the required equipment, thus reducing 
the cost, and it is possible to use much of the know-how obtained from biological HT screening.  
 
HTE and flow chemistry have their own advantages and limitations, however for this project it is desired to do 
initial screenings, optimisation of reaction yields and discovery of new reactions, and for this HTE is the best 
choice. Later optimisation or scale-up of photochemical reactions, however, inevitably makes flow chemistry the 
obvious choice.

1.2 HTE workflow 
To obtain the best results most efficiently it is necessary to consider and design every step in a workflow carefully 
prior to starting a HT experiment.  Depending on whether the desired setup is semi- or fully-automated, the 
workflow varies, and it is important to considered when automation is beneficial. Figure 3 illustrates a general 
HTE workflow, which is also utilised in this project. The individual steps are, 1) Plan and design the experiments 
and the experimental setup, 2) Prepare stock solutions and dispense the solutions, 3) Plate sealing, 4) Run 
reactions, 5) Conduct analytical analysis, 6) Analysis of the generated data in automation, 7) Interpretation of 
data, 8) Scale-up the optimum or novel reaction(s).  
 
Figure 3: HTE workflow utilised in this project.a 
 
a Image references are listed in the reference section.  
==PAGE:16==
 
 5 
The first step in the workflow should not be underestimated, as it is here that the reaction parameters are defined, 
and careful planning increases the chance of being successful. Even if a completely random screen is carried out 
the reaction parameters still need to be defined. When selecting reaction variables, it can be beneficial to search 
in existing literature, apply knowledge from previous cases or simply use chemical intuition. Depending on 
purpose, time and what is feasible in the given laboratory, the number of samples screened needs to be outlined. 
For semi-automated systems it is normal to employ 96-MPs, and for more automated systems 384 and 1536 MPs 
are common. Choice of solvent and dispensing system depends on several factors and is described in more detail 
in section 1.3. Sealing of the MPs should happen immediately after solvent dispensing. The most common HTE 
seals are made of aluminium, however for this project optical seals are utilised, as this is necessary for 
photochemistry.  
The execution of the reactions in HT, varies depending on the reaction type screened and the experimental setup 
must be altered accordingly. For photochemical reactions, a light source is required, whilst other reactions can 
require temperature control, etc. It is important to set up the experiment, so the same outer conditions are applied 
to all reaction wells of the plate, to reduce the with-in plate variability and acquire comparable results. Another 
important factor is timing and should not be underestimated. If a reaction reacts as soon as the reagents are mixed 
and can form side products over time, it can be necessary to reduce the number of reactions screened in parallel, 
to limit the waiting time between the first and last reaction analysed.1 However, as the focus of this project is 
photochemistry, this is less of an issue as the reactions is essentially quenched once the light source is removed. 
The analytic acquisition time need to be as short as possible, as it quickly adds up when acquiring data from 
numerous samples or having long sampling times. Different analytical techniques commonly used are explained 
in section 1.4.  Large quantities of data can be generated in HTE, and it is therefore essential to have fast data 
analysis, as well as easy interpretation of the results. As this is one of the biggest bottlenecks in HTE, the main 
objective in this project is to make an analysis tool which automates these steps. After interpretation of the data, 
it can be concluded which reaction(s) performed best. If the result is satisfying, the reaction can be up scaled and 
its performance on larger scales can be assessed. However, if the result is not satisfying, a completely new screen 
or a narrower search for a given reaction can be conducted.  
 
If a workflow for HTE is intelligently planned, it will give the best results. This means combining both 
nonautomation and automation techniques.2 Thus, it is important to consider all aspects of the setup and choose 
the solutions which best suits the question being investigated.

1.3 Liquid handling systems and solvents  
HTE is done on nano- and microscale so material use is limited, hence it is important to have a system that 
accurately can dispense the solutions. In this regard several automated pipetting systems have been developed. 
Automated liquid handling systems are built on robotic dispensing platforms. Labcyte Echo and SPT Labtech 
mosquito are two examples utilised in many laboratories.4 The Echo system applies an acoustic droplet ejection 
technique to accurately transfer droplets down to a volume of 2.5 nL via sound waves.9 Figure 4 illustrates the 
general principle for the Echo acoustic liquid handler. 
 
 
Figure 4: Illustration of the Echo Liquid Handler from Labcyte. Bellow the MP is a transducer which transmits sound 
waves through the solutions, to transfer droplets of fluid to the inverted plate. Numerous droplets are transferred until the 
desired amount is reached in the inverted well plate. Reproduced from Labcyte.9 
The LabTech mosquito is a multichannel pipetting system which likewise enables miniaturization sampling.10 
Both techniques can without difficulties be coupled to HTE, and save both time and a significant amount of 
reagents. However, these robotic dispensing systems are often quite expensive and require development of 
expertise.3 Another challenge is that some liquid handling systems are only compatible with specific solvents. 
Subsequently, even though the automatic dispensing of liquids gives the most accurate result, it can sometimes 
be beneficial to manually dispense the solutions, since it is straightforward, inexpensive, gives more flexibility in 
the experimental setup and in choice of solvent.3 
 
In a recent paper by Mennen et al.2 a survey was conducted regarding the use of HTE and HTE equipment in 
academia and industry. Some of the participants were AstraZeneca, Merck, and University of Pennsylvania. Here 
it was evident that an essential step for a good HTE workflow was the accuracy and speed to which the substrates 
can be sampled on a nano- and micro-level.2 All the participants had implemented some automation handling 
==PAGE:18==
 
 7 
system for liquids or solids in their HTE laboratories, to get an efficient workflow with high accuracy. Yet, manual 
single and multichannel pipettes were still utilised a lot, due to the ease of use and flexibility when having arrays 
up to 96- well plates.2  
 
Multiple factors can influence the choice of solvent and depending on the liquid handling system different solvents 
can be more or less feasible. Very low boiling point solvents are generally a challenge in HTE, as it is chemistry 
on miniature scale. Higher boiling point solvents are thus more suited, especially with manual pipetting as this 
process is time consuming compared to robotic dispensing. The solubility of the substrates is another 
consideration regarding choice of proper solvent, as it is preferred to have homogenous solutions in HTE.11 
Furthermore, as most HT experiments are conducted in plastic plates (polypropylene or polystyrene4), solvents 
which can dissolve plastic should be avoided or used with careful consideration, some examples are 
tetrahydrofuran (THF), dichloromethane and other halogenated solvents.11 
 
1.4 Analytical techniques 
Acquiring hundreds to thousands of experiments produces some requirements for the analysis method of choice.2  
The analysis time for each experiment should be short without reducing the quality of the data or at least to a 
minimal extent. Frequently applied analytical techniques in HTE are ultra-high-performance liquid 
chromatography (UPLC), high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) and mass spectrometry (MS).2  
 
1.4.1 Mass spectrometry  
MS is one of the most common analytical techniques in HT analysis as it enables fast sampling and data 
acquisition.4 In MS the mass to charge ratio (m/z) of ions derived from fragments and molecules are determined. 
Separation of the ions is done based on their m/z-values and multiple mass analysers exist. The result is a spectrum 
of the relative abundance of the ions versus the m/z ratio, which can be used to find out which compounds are 
present in a solution. MS are often coupled to LC, and in the survey done by Mennen et al.2 most HTE groups 
applied either HPLC or UPLC and 40% were coupled with MS.  LC is a technique which separates molecules in 
a mixture based on the compound’s polarity. Liquids are passed through a column under pressure and the analytes 
in the sample will elute differently depending on their interaction with the stationary phase in the column. LC-
MS is a very good technique for both separation of components in a reaction mixture and detection of the separated 
molecules.4 If the LC-MS instrument is equipped with a ultraviolet (UV) detector as well, it is possible to generate 
both UV and MS chromatograms and spectra.  
 
==PAGE:19==
 
 8 
The throughput of LC-MS can be improved even further by reducing the run times with multiple injections in a 
single experimental run (MISER).4 MISER chromatography is an alternative to flow injection analysis (FIA), 
where some chromatographic retention is introduced to avoid the analyte co-eluting with the rest of the matrix 
components.12 In FLA there is a constant flow of a carrier liquid containing one reagent, to which a sample is 
injected. The reagent in the carrier liquid and sample can when react to form a product. Formation of the product 
depends on rate of mixing between the reagent and sample as well as the reaction time, the time it takes the sample 
to travel from the injector to the detector. FLA can easily be done in automation, is fast and permits to analyse 
many samples in one go.13 For MISER to work it needs to be able to run on standard LC-MS machines and in 
FLA mode.4 MISER have demonstrated run times down to 10 seconds per sample, making it excellent for HTE.4 
In a study done by Grainger et al.14  it was possible to analyse 1536 samples in approximately 6 hours using 
MISER UPLC-MS. This is a significant reduction compared to their previous chromatographic setups, which 
analysed 1536 samples in 52 hours.4 
 
Matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization (MALDI) and desorption electrospray ionization (DESI) are two 
ionisation methods for MS. Both techniques can be applied in HTE settings.4 The main advantage of MALDI and 
DESI is the very fast acquisition time, it is possible to scan 1536 samples in less than 10 minutes.4 The drawbacks 
regarding MALDI is that the matrix can cause problems and may need to be changed during the different runs.4 
If the analyte has a low molecular weight, there is also a chance that it will interfere with the components in the 
matrix.4 Another difficulty with MALDI is that post-reaction processing of the samples often are necessary, such 
as matrix embedding or solvent removal, which adds a significant amount of time and handling to the workflow. 
 
1.4.2 NMR spectroscopy 
Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) is the go-to method for structural analysis of compounds and provides high 
quality structural information. However, NMR has not yet been successfully applied in HT, due to the time 
consuming data acquisition and interpretation.4 Automated NMR analysis have already been developed and 
applied, yet the big barrier is the acquisition of the samples as it take a long time to run NMR experiments. New 
flow-NMR instruments and setups are being developed, which could make it possible to run NMR in HT. The 
limitations to benchtop NMR is that the spectra are of lower resolution compared to conventional NMR.4

1.5 Data analysis tools  
To exploit the full potential of HTE it is not enough to only have fast data acquisition, the data analysis needs to 
be equally efficient. Otherwise, the time saved on acquisition will be lost in the analysis of the large data sets. 
Several programs have been developed to quickly analyse and visualise the experimental arrays. Two 
commercially available programs are Virscidian and Spotfire. Both programmes have been applied several times 
in the literature4,15, the user can interact directly with the generated data, make corrections in e.g. peak integrals 
or assignments, etc.15,16 Spotfire is a visualisation tool suitable for data analysis and interpretation of large data 
sets.17 New commercial software systems manufactured mainly for analysis of HTE are also starting to emerge, 
such as Katalyst D2D.18 Katalyst D2D facilitates automated planning, execution and analysis of HTE.18 The 
drawbacks of the commercial options are the often high cost and that it requires that the software is compatible 
==PAGE:21==
 
 10 
with the given experimental setup and equipment available. For these reasons it can be beneficial to program the 
software manually. Programming languages, such as Python or MATLAB, can be utilised for computational 
analysis of HTE data. Python is an open-source and free programming language. However, it requires knowledge 
within software and programming to build a tool, where the commercial platforms are more straight-forward and 
user-friendly, but not freely available. Manually programming the software enables to customise it to the wishes 
from a research group and the available analytical instrument, avoiding problems regarding specific 
instrumentation and data file formats. Additionally, if new features in the tool are desired it can easily be added 
to the existing scripts, as the code and structure of the software is well-known.

1.7 Photochemistry 
Photochemical reactions can in general be divided into the following categories: photo-dissociation, photo-
induced rearrangements, photo-addition, photo-substitution and photo-redox reactions.20 However, in this project 
the focus is on photo-cycloadditions, section 1.7.2 describes this in detail. Some benefits with photochemical 
reactions are that they can initialise a reaction which would otherwise not be feasible, ease the synthetic route of 
organic compounds, and control when a reaction should propagate. Furthermore, the light source is not an additive 
or reagent that should be removed after reaction completion, consequently the reaction is quenched when the light 
is removed. 
 
1.7.1 Jablonski diagram and excited states  
Photochemical reactions require light to propagate and are initiated by absorption of a photon. When a compound 
absorbs light it goes from the ground state to an excited state, and this induces a change in the chemical and 
==PAGE:23==
 
 12 
physical properties of the molecule. Figure 5 illustrates a Jablonski diagram, also known as an energy state 
diagram, which includes both radiative and non-radiative transitions.21 The black horizontal lines are vibrational 
energy levels in different electronic states, and the arrows indicates different radiative (solid arrows) and non-
radiative (dotted arrows) transitions.13  
 
Figure 5: Jablonski diagram for an arbitrary molecule. The arrows specify different transitions. Blue arrows are 
absorption, green arrow is fluorescence, purple arrow is phosphorescence, cyan dotted arrow is IC, light purple dotted 
arrow is ISC and yellow dotted arrows are vibrational relaxation. Inspired by Harris et al.13 
When a molecule absorbs a photon an electron moves from the ground state, S0, to a vibrational level in an excited 
electronic state, this could be S1 or S2 as shown in figure 5. The excited electron will start to relax back via 
vibrational relaxation until it reaches the lowest vibrational level in Sn. This is a non-radiative process, where 
energy is transferred through collision to other molecules, this could be solvent molecules. The excited electron 
can then undergo internal conversion (IC), where the electron is transferred to another excited state of same 
energy, e.g. from S2 to S1, but to a higher vibronic state. In the S1 state, several processes can happen after reaching 
the lowest vibrational excited level. Direct emission of a photon will lead to fluorescence, a radiative process 
where the molecule is transferred from S1 to S0 and release energy in the form of light. The excited electron can 
also undergo intersystem crossing (ISC), where the molecule transits to the first excited triplet state, T1. ISC is a 
spin forbidden process as it requires the excited electron to change spin. According to Pauli exclusion principle a 
pair of electrons in the same energy level need to have opposite spins. ISC is more likely to occur if S1 and T1 are 
close in energy. If ISC takes place the excited electron relaxes to the lowest vibrational level in T1, here it is 
trapped as the lowest vibrational level in T1 is of lower energy than the lowest vibrational level in S1. At this point 
the electron can relax back to S0 through the reverse ISC, which is spin forbidden, or phosphorescence. 
Phosphorescence is a much slower process than fluorescence.21 Fluorescence is normally on the time scale from 
10-8 to 10-4 seconds, where phosphorescence is from 10-4 to 102 seconds.13  Because the first process is spin 
==PAGE:24==
 
 13 
forbidden and the second process is very slow, the excited triplet state molecule is allowed time to undergo 
chemical transformations or react with surrounding molecules by transfer of energy or via photoinduced electron 
transfer, hence photochemical reactions may proceed.

1.8 Chemometrics  
Chemometrics is an interdisciplinary field combining chemistry with data science, where statistical and 
mathematical models are applied on chemical systems. Chemometric has many applications, such as investigating 
and selecting optimal reaction parameters, extracting the relevant chemical information from a data set, or analyse 
chemical data more efficiently.28 As the chemical data quantity and quality increase, chemometrics becomes more 
and more relevant. A variety of statistical methods and other tools have been implemented in several analytical 
workflows depending on the desired analysis. For the development of the analysis software as well as for 
validation of the HTE platform and results in this project, tensor nomenclature and analysis of variance (ANOVA) 
are discussed in the subsequent sections.

2 Aim and objective 
The aim of this project is to establish a screening platform to discover and develop photochemical reactions. In 
the existing experimental setup, reactions are analysed one at a time using LC-MS and data interpretation are done 
individually for each spectrum. With this project, the experimental setup is reconfigured to HTE, enabling 
screening of reactions with different conditions using automated LC-MS, and direct quantitative as well as 
qualitative analysis of LC-MS data from all samples to discover optimal reaction conditions. This will allow for 
a more rapid screening of reaction conditions and the discovery of new chemical reactions without the labour 
intensive and often inefficient one-by-one batch approach traditionally performed in many laboratories. 
 
The development of an automated and rapid data analysis tool, based on the generated LC-MS data, for chemical 
analysis is the main objective of this project. It is essential that the tool gives easily interpretable results and is 
user-friendly, so chemists with no programming experience can apply the software with minimum guidance. To 
achieve this, three key steps are defined. The first step for developing a HTE platform, is to set up an experimental 
workflow for HT, this is illustrated in figure 3, enabling to go from batch to plate-based chemistry. 
 
The analysing program will consist of multiple modules, which will be programmed individually and is the second 
key objective. This will include data mining and loading, visualisations, integration, m/z search, and more. The 
visualisation should enable easy interpretation of the results from all samples in one HT experiment, as well as 
single experiments in the HT experiment. This could be achieved by producing MP plots, heat maps or 
conventional spectra.  
 
The last step in constructing the analysis tool is to combine the individual scripts and make the software 
intelligible, robust, and flexible. The tool will be validated on real samples with a known reference reaction. 
Afterwards it will be used to screen photochemical reactions in HT as these reactions are the future focus of this 
project. If successful, the experimental setup and data analysis tool can be expanded to other chemical reaction or 
research areas.

3 Results and discussion  
This section is divided into two main parts. The first section focusses on the development of the analysis software 
tool for HTE. The second part describes how the platform has been tested and validated, as well as utilised for 
screening of photochemical reactions for both optimisation and discovery of a new reaction.   
 
3.1 Development of HTE software  
To be able to analyse all samples in a plate efficiently and avoiding manually investigating the LC-MS data sets 
individually, a software for chemical analysis has been developed. The freely available programming language 
Python was applied to build the software. The code for the main script can be seen in appendix C and all scripts 
are available upon request. The objective was to screen 96 samples in one plate, analyse if product had been 
produced or other side reactions had taken place, and visualize the results in an informal and intuitive manner. 
The program is developed based on the idea that people with little or no programming experience can apply it in 
their research with minimum training and guidance. The program is constructed around separate modules, where 
each module has a set of functions regarding a specific feature. The scripts are gathered in a zip-folder termed 
HTE_analysis_tool.zip, containing nine scripts in total. All scripts are listed in figure 12.  
 
Figure 12: Scripts for data analysis and randomisation.  
The main script, called “Main.py”, is where the user interacts with the program to conduct the data analysis. The 
main script is divided into individual sections and loads the relevant functions from each module/script 
automatically. User inputs are highlighted with “# Input” in the main script, to make it simple for the operator. In 
the next sections the different parts of the program are discussed. The relevant scripts for each section are referred 
to by the titles above and can be found in HTE_analysis_tool.zip, together with a data set as an example. 
Experimental section 6.2 includes a manual for applications of the program, to promote usability.  
 
3.1.1 Data extraction, loading and storing  
Data inputs for HTE analysis derives from various sources. The LC-MS data are acquired from both a UV/Vis

3.1.2 Integration of UV/Vis peaks 
To qualify and quantify the compounds in a sample, the UV/Vis peaks in a spectrum need to be identified and 
integrated. The LC-MS instrument calculate the integrals of the recognized peaks automatically for one 
wavelength. However, the instrument does not include peaks in the beginning of the UV spectrum, which means 
compounds that are very polar and elute rapidly will not be detected. It was furthermore observed that the intervals 
selected for integration on the instrument were greater than the true peak width. As an example, the peak 
highlighted in figure 16 is defined to span over the interval of 4.008-4.984 minutes on the LC-MS instrument, 
==PAGE:36==
 
 25 
which is clearly too wide. Hence, it was necessary to develop a new integration method, enabling quantification 
of early eluting compounds and implement narrower peak widths.  
 
Figure 16: UV/Vis spectrum for the reference reaction (scheme 3) with 4-bromobiphenyl as the ISTD at 254 nm. The red 
circle highlights the peak of the ISTD. The LC-MS peak width is calculated to 4.008-4.984 minutes for the ISTD, whereas 
it is calculated to 4.076-4.252 minutes with the developed program.

define when a peak start and ends. In “Integration.py” the number of data points for calculation of the slope as 
well as the threshold can be changed if needed for future applications. After the peaks in the UV spectrum had

3.1.3 Detection of starting materials, product, and unexpected compounds 
Some key trends in the UV/Vis spectra are important when identifying successful reactions. These are a decrease 
in starting material signal and an increase in product signal, which are inversely linked if product is formed. 
However, if a decrease in starting material is observed and no product signal is detected, it indicates that side 
reactions have occurred or that the starting material is unstable and has been degraded. Thus, the analysis software 
should include features for detection of starting materials, product, and formation of unexpected compounds.   
 
Equal amount of internal standard (ISTD) was added to the analysed samples, so the ratio between starting 
material or product and the ISTD could be measured. This allows to compare reactions across a plate and to see 
if starting material and product conversion follow the inverse trend. The ISTD, starting materials, and product 
were identified in the spectra based on their respective retention times. In figure 58 and 59 in appendix E a 96-
well plate plot of the ratio between product/ISTD and stating material/ISTD are presented respectively. The plots 
are inspired from a 96-MP layout, where the size of the circles highlights the ratio between the compound and 
ISTD. In the MP visualisations one or two variables (chemical conditions) can be depicted, by different colours 
or shapes. This can be applied to predict if there is a trend among the screened conditions, e.g. if one component 
influence the outcome more compared to the other component. An example with one variable is given in section 
3.2.1, where different solvents are tested, and for an example with multiple variables see section 3.2.2 or appendix 
E. The MP visualisations enables the user to identify the reactions where product have been formed and recognise 
==PAGE:38==
 
 27 
trends or patterns among the tested reactions. The reactions resulting in the highest conversion of product, 
product/ISTD, can be saved in a CSV file, together with the corresponding reaction conditions. The user defines 
an integer 𝑛, equivalent to the number of top reactions to be included in the CSV file. 
 
The ratios between ISTD and product or starting material do not give an exact yield or measure of starting material 
consumption. However, this approach gives flexibility as it does not require that the product is known beforehand 
and can still be utilised to discover the best performing reactions. To calculate the actual yield or conversion a 
standard curve for the product or starting reagent must be created. This is done by measuring the intensity of 
varying concentrations of a compound of interest and comparing it to a fixed quantity of ISTD. A linear curve can 
then be plotted and applied to determine how much of the compound there is in a sample. If the product is unknown 
or has not been produced previously, the substrate needs to be isolated and purified before the yield can be 
determined. In this project, the starting material peak was not always separatable, which makes it difficult to 
measure the accurate peak intensity and calculate the consumption.  
 
To examine if unexpected compounds have been produced, a function searching for new significant peaks in the 
UV/Vis chromatogram was developed. The result is reported in 96-well plate format (figure 18). The colour codes 
indicates if other significant peaks are present (blue) or not (red) in the samples. This is done by evaluating if the 
new peaks exceed a set threshold for the integral. For detection of unexpected/new compounds, it is recommended 
to calculate the integrals based on the total sum over all wavelengths, because the absorption maxima are unknown 
for the unidentified compound peak.

The position of the individual samples in the MP should be spread out randomly, to reduce bias in the plate setup.

randomised this can cause a small time dependent artefact in the results. However, if randomisation is applied and 
replicates are screened the bias can be avoided.38 To minimize bias in the plate setup, the script 
“Randomise_plate.py” can be run before allocating the position of the individual samples. The script is run on its

3.2.2 Optimisation of reagent concentrations  
The next step was to conduct a broader HT optimisation experiment, screening significantly more reaction 
conditions. The solvents selected were ACN and THF based on the results from the previous experiment, as well 
as dioxane since it is the solvent utilised in the literature. Four different reaction conditions were varied, solvent 
(dioxane, ACN, and THF), concentration (0.042 M, 0.083 M, and 0.167 M), equivalents of N,N-dimethylaniline 
(1, 2, 3, 5, and 7) and reaction time (18, 4.5, and 2 hours). A total of 45 different combinations were tested and 
screened in duplicates. Selecting many different parameters for screening, also entails that several stock solutions 
need to be prepared, which add extra preparation time and should be kept in mind for the HT analysis. The study 
was conducted to see if it was possible to find a better set of reaction conditions, but also to test the analysis 
software when having several reaction parameters. Solvents were plated in order of boiling point to avoid 
evaporation. However, after exposure to light low solvent volumes were observed by visual inspection for 13% 
and 57% of samples in ACN and THF respectively. The evaporated samples were mainly located in bottom left 
square of the MP, which could indicate that sealing was loose in the bottom left corner. It was decided to add 150 
µL to all evaporated samples and 100 µL to all other samples, to ensure that the LC-MS instrument could collect 
the samples. Thus, the final concentration in each sample is an estimate but this will not affect the analysis as 
everything is related to the ISTD. Although while reacting, a lower or higher concentration than planned will 
impact the results to some degree. Consequently, running reactions in duplicates or higher becomes of even more 
important to mitigate or lower the potential significance of any deviations.

4 Conclusion 
The preceding HT experiments demonstrate how the HT platform including the analysis tool can be applied to 
discover new reactions and emphasise the usefulness of HTE, as the results have been produced in significantly 
less time than if the reactions had been carried out in batch and analysed manually. In this project a HT workflow 
and an analysis tool have been developed, successfully validated, and tested on a literature reaction as well as a 
new reaction to both optimise and discover photochemical transformations. The developed analysis tool is 
composed of several modules, where each module has its own function and can be run together or individually 
depending on the purpose of the HT experiment. The optimisation of the literature reference reaction indicates 
that lower concentrations are better, and that THF is the best solvent. THF is however not the best solvent for 
HTE, which is important to be aware of due to its low boiling point. 7 equivalents of N,N-dimethylaniline (2) is 
not necessarily needed for good conversion as the results in table 6 and figure 58 indicates that even 5 or 3 
equivalents may also be feasible. A more extended time study could be conducted, yet longer reaction times 
appears better, as almost no product had been formed after 4.5 hours. For the novel reaction between N-
methylmaleimide (1) and the N-boc-nortropinone-derived alkene (2) two different sets of reaction conditions were

5 Outlook  
The direct continuation from a chemical perspective is to screen other photochemical reactions as well as to 
expand the usage of the workflow and analysis tool to new chemical fields, hopefully leading to a valuable go-to 
tool for the synthetic chemists in the Laraia group and more generally. A more user-friendly tool could be achieved 
by developing an interactive program, with pop up functions and a graphical user interface. This would enhance 
usability and require less training for users unknown to Python. To automate the workflow even further, automated 
liquid dispensing systems could be incorporated, thereby reducing human interactions and errors. However, the 
disadvantage here is that the Echo liquid handling dispensing system at hand is only compatible with specific 
solvents like DMSO, where we encountered troubles due to contaminant build-ups on the LC column from 
previous runs being pulled through and co-elutes. The throughput could likewise be enhanced by screening 384 
or even 1536 MPs.   
 
To obtain more information of the underlying structure in the data, statistical models and unsupervised clustering 
methods could be applied to search for significant variables and carry out exploratory analysis, especially if the 
throughput is increased and it is desired to search for new reactions. One approach could be applying principal 
component analysis (PCA), where the observations are clustered together in a subspace to analyse which reactions 
behave similarly, examine if several reactions have taken place or identify the variables which have the largest 
effect. As PCA will enable to cluster together different products and see what variables (reaction conditions) are 
significant for each cluster. Depending on the goal of the chemical analysis, many more features could be 
integrated in the analysis tool, to ease and accelerate the reaction optimisation and discovery.

6 Experimental  
The following sections include a general HTE protocol, a manual for applying the analysis tool as well as a guide 
for setting up the MPs in automation and extracting the data on the LC-MS.  
 
6.1 HTE reaction protocol - general directions 
Preparation of stock solutions  
 
Stock solutions with the relevant reagents were prepared in an open atmosphere. Commercially available reagents 
and chemicals were used as received without further purification and all solvents were of HPLC grade (DMSO, 
DMF, dioxane, IPA, ACN, and THF).  
 
Dispensing, sealing, and MP details  
Stock solutions and solvents were dispensed manually with a single- or multi-channel pipette (Thermo Scientific 
Finnpipette F2, Biohit mLine M1000 or M20) and mixed directly into a 96 MP in an open atmosphere. The script 
“Randomise_plate.py” was applied where specified to randomise the positions of the samples in the MPs. The 
total sample volume in each well was either 150 µL or 300 µL. The 96 MPs applied were polypropene plastic 
plates (v-bottom, chimney well, 14.6 x 85.5 x 127.8 mm) from Greiner Bio-One or glass reactor plates (standard, 
15 x 85 x 127 mm, cat. no. 3600500) from Zinsser Analytic made from special high purity and temperature 
resistant borosilicate glass. MPs were sealed with 4titude optical clear heat seals (125 x 78 mm, 4ti-0541 from 
swab.dk) using a MicroTS (SN 958-1276) plate sealer for 10 seconds. 
 
Light setup  
Sealed plates were reacted under blue or UV light setups, as shown in figure 52 and 53 respectively. For the 
reactions conducted under blue light, a 50W LED floodlight (IP65, Amazon.de) lamp was applied and set to blue 
with max intensity. The distance between light source and plate was 4 cm. Cooling of the plate was done with 
either a Satzuma USB 5 V desk top fan or a Goobay fan (cat. no. 20737 from AcXperten.dk). For reactions 
conducted under UV light, a Nailstar professional 4x96W (NS-01-UK&EU, Amazon.de) lamp was applied. The 
plate was positioned in the middle of the UV chamber. Note that the UV chamber automatically turns off every 
30 minutes. Cooling was carried out with a Goobay fan. Reactions in schemes 3 and 5 were under light for 18 
hours, if not stated otherwise, whereas the reaction times for scheme 6 reactions varied between 3 and 12 hours.

LC-MS analysis of 96-MPs was acquired on the LCMS-8045 from Shimandzu40 equipped with a photodiode array 
detector and a triple quadrupole mass spectrometer. A Kinetex C18 column (d 2.6 µm, 100 x 2.1 mm) equipped 
with a security guard (ULTRA Cartridges and Holder) from Phenomenex were applied. In this setup reverse-
phase LC was applied, with a flow rate of 0.6 mL/min. Eluents A (H2O with 0.1% HCO2H) and B (ACN with 
0.1% HCO2H) were used with the following 5 minute LC method: 5% B for the first 1.25 min, linear gradient 
(5% B to 50% B) from 1.25 min to 2.00 min, hold for 0.25 min at 50% B, another linear gradient (50% B to 100% 
B) from 2.25 min to 3.00 min, hold for 1.00 min at 100% B and then linear gradient (100% B to 5% b) from 4.00 
min to 4.50 min, 5% B was held for 0.50 min. The acquisition time interval was from 0.2 minutes to 5.0 minutes. 
Autosampler temperature was 16°𝐶. Injection volume varied between 1, 2, 4, and 5 µL.

6.2 How to apply the analysis tool   
Before using the tool – Arrangement of scripts and data   
Scripts and folder(s) containing data from HT analysis of one or several MPs 
should be placed in the same overall folder, as illustrated in figure 54. It is 
important to have this overall structure as well as to name the files correctly for 
the analysis tool to function properly. The folders ‘MS_data_positive_mode’, 
‘MS_data_negative_mode’, and ‘UV_data’ will be generated automatically when 
running the first section in the main script called ‘Data mining’. The folder 
‘__pycache__’ is likewise generated automatically when running the scripts. 
Initially, the generated data from one plate should be placed in one folder termed 
PX, where X is the plate number, e.g. P1 or P2.   
 
The naming of the generated files is important. Three files for each sample are 
generated (UV/Vis, MS+, and MS-) on the LC-MS instrument, and these should 
be titled accordingly to table 12. The names for the 96 samples can be generated 
automatically when setting up the batch file on the LC-MS instrument (see LC-MS 
guide in section 6.3). No other files than the UV, MS+, and MS- files as well as 
one meta-data file should be present in a plate folder (e.g. P1). Plates analysed at different reaction times, should 
have different plate numbers, and is treated as independent plates.
