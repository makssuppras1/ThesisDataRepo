Abstract
This thesis delves into the understanding of hydrofoil theory, exploring its principles and
underlying concepts. It further investigates various methods for achieving stability in hy­
drofoil crafts. Additionally, it provides an overview of the broader idea of stabilization
and dynamic stability. A detailed stability matrix is developed upon various sources en­
compassing aircraft and hydrofoil stability research. By solving a subset of the stability
problem, we gain a deeper understanding of the longitudinal stability characteristics of
the hydrofoil. It enables us to assess the stability modes, determines the effect of control
inputs, and make design or control adjustments to enhance the overall stability and perfor­
mance of the system. This matrix serves as a crucial component to be later implemented
in the subsequent model, ensuring accuracy and reliability in the analysis and evaluation
of hydrofoil stability. In conclusion, this thesis uses a model to develop dynamic stability
calculations for a specific hydrofoil configuration. The measures examine various vari­
ables that can influence the stability outcome. Considering these variables, an analysis is
conducted to understand the impact on the longitudinal dynamic stability of the hydrofoil
setup.

Introduction
1.1
Background of Hydrofoils
One practical approach to enhance boat speed and reduce energy consumption is to uti­
lize hydrofoils, which are submerged wings attached to the boat’s hull. Hydrofoils can
raise the boat hull out of the water by generating a lift similar to aircraft wings. This lifting
action leads to a reduction in water resistance proportional to the area of the hull lifted,
resulting in diminished wave disturbance and improved sea­keeping capabilities. Hydro­
foils can achieve this lift using smaller dimensions than aircraft wings, as water density
is approximately 800 times greater than air. Consequently, hydrofoils can be added to
monohull boats without incurring significant weight and drag penalties. [1]
Fundamental design characteristics that need to be incorporated into a hydrofoil:
• Lift Generation: The hydrofoil system must generate sufficient forces to lift the
boat hull out of the water to a height that allows it to clear the tops of waves, even
in challenging sea conditions.
• Weight and Balance: The lift generated by the foils should balance the craft’s
weight and maintain equilibrium through the centre of gravity. Hydrofoil should also
provide righting forces to stabilize the boat against roll or pitch motions.
• Stability: The desired ride height of the hydrofoil should exhibit static and dynamic
stability. This can be achieved by automatically adjusting lift forces due to changes
in immersion or by utilizing control surfaces that can be operated automatically to
maintain stability.
Hydrofoils are known for their many advantages, a few of which are mentioned below:
• Increased speed: By lifting the hull out of the water, hydrofoils effectively decrease
drag and reduce the resistance caused by wave interaction. This streamlined design
allows hydrofoil­equipped boats to achieve higher speeds and benefit from more
efficient propulsion.
• Fuel­Efficiency: Hydrofoil crafts are significantly more fuel­efficient than traditional
boats due to their drag reduction capabilities, as previously mentioned.
• Wave impact reduction: The environment is an important factor to consider, es­
pecially when reducing waves that can harm underwater creatures.
Also worth mentioning are the disadvantages of hydrofoil boats which cannot be omitted
during design planning.
• Complexity and Cost: Hydrofoils require intricate engineering and construction,
leading to higher production and maintenance costs than traditional watercraft.
• Vulnerability to Damage: Hydrofoil components exposed to the water’s surface
are susceptible to damage from debris, submerged objects, or impacts, resulting in
time­consuming and costly repairs or replacements.
• Stability and Maneuverability: Hydrofoil vessels may have reduced stability, es­
pecially in rough seas, due to their smaller contact area with the water and elevated
centre of gravity. They can be more prone to tipping or rolling. Manoeuvrability may
also be limited at lower speeds.

• Sensitivity to Conditions: Hydrofoils perform best in calm or moderate sea condi­
tions, and their ride quality can deteriorate significantly in rough or choppy waters.
This can lead to discomfort for passengers and limit the usability and reliability of
hydrofoil vessels.
1.2
Structure of this thesis
In the beginning, the thesis discusses aspects of stability and control using the theory de­
veloped from aviation, later examples of solutions which can be implemented to hydrofoil
and how a designer can control them. Further, the theoretical stability model is presented
from aircraft design and a simplified model for hydrofoil design. The next part presents
the model, which incorporates equations and algorithms to analyze the stability of the
hydrofoils. Stability criteria, such as righting moment and pitch stability, are evaluated
to assess the overall stability characteristics. A series of sensitivity analyses were con­
ducted to gain a comprehensive understanding of the factors that influence the stability
of the hydrofoil. These findings provide valuable insights that can inform future design
decisions and improve the overall performance of the hydrofoil.
The outcomes of the stability model provide significant insights into the hydrofoil’s sta­
bility characteristics. These revelations enable designers to develop a more robust and
productive hydrofoil­based vessel while enhancing our understanding of hydrofoil perfor­
mance.
1.3
Goal
The thesis aims to analyze the dynamic stability of a hydrofoil setup for small motor boats.
The thesis explores the feasibility and potential benefits of incorporating hydrofoil tech­
nology into small watercraft, focusing on manoeuvrability.
Overall, the thesis aims to contribute to understanding hydrofoil technology in the context
of motor boats and provide insights into the design considerations and dynamic stability
analysis required for implementing hydrofoil systems in this specific domain. The the­
sis findings can potentially guide future developments and applications of hydrofoils in
small watercraft, benefiting boat manufacturers and enthusiasts seeking enhanced per­
formance and efficiency.

2
Hydrofoil crafts
2.1
Introduction
One effective way to enhance boat speed involves lifting the hull out of the water. By
achieving this, the water resistance will decrease proportionally to the area of the hull
lifted above the water’s surface, resulting in a reduction in wave disturbance and an over­
all improvement in the boat’s sea­keeping quality. Hydrofoils, which are submerged wings
attached to struts beneath and to the hull’s sides, can generate lift similar to aircraft wings.
This lift enables the hydrofoils to lift the boat’s hull out of the water, as illustrated in fig. 2.1.
Since water density is significantly higher than air (approximately 800 times higher), hydro­
foils can be designed with smaller dimensions than aircraft wings. This advantage allows
hydrofoils to be added to monohull boats without significant additional weight­related dif­
ficulties. The unique characteristics of a foil craft introduce additional complexities in its
design process, specifically concerning stability requirements and the craft’s response to
disturbances in a seaway. Consequently, there is often a need to develop a performance
prediction model that can assist in fine­tuning the control system’s response rates. One
critical aspect is the non­linear hysteresis effect when the craft accelerates after lifting the
hull out of the water. This effect must be carefully balanced against the sudden decrease
in speed when the foil lift reduces at slower speeds. Designing an effective control system
for a foil craft involves understanding and accounting for these dynamic factors, anticipat­
ing the craft’s response to various conditions, and optimizing the control response rates
accordingly. This requires a comprehensive understanding of the craft’s behaviour, hydro­
dynamics, and the interplay between the lifting forces generated by the hydrofoils and the
craft’s overall performance. By developing a reliable performance prediction model, de­
signers can gain valuable insights into the craft’s behaviour and make informed decisions
to ensure stability, manoeuvrability, and performance in different operating conditions.

2.2
Hydrofoil boat assumption
Hydrofoil design requires careful consideration of several key characteristics. As a hydro­
foil nears the water surface, its lift decreases. It maintains a quasi­constant lift below a
certain immersion depth exceeding the foil’s chord length (which also has a drag penalty).
Firstly, cavitation is the formation of vapour bubbles within a liquid at low­pressure regions
that occur in places where the liquid has been accelerated to high velocities [2]. To mit­
igate this, hydrofoils with surface­piercing lifting foil arrays utilize design techniques that
account for cavitation in the surface­piercing section and incorporate fence structures to
safeguard the lower foils. In hydrofoil design, the foil cross­section often features a sharp
leading edge, which promotes the occurrence of cavitation over the upper surface expe­
riencing low pressure. This intentional cavitation helps create a clean and stable cavity,
preventing pitting damage to the foil. Additionally, hydrofoil sections typically have a rel­
atively shallow camber.
2.2.1
Lifting force
Hydrofoils can be considered airfoils adapted for operation in water. As a result, designers
can leverage aerodynamic principles when developing the hydrodynamic characteristics
of a hydrofoil. Wing lift is generated through several aerodynamic principles, including
Bernoulli’s principle, the Coanda effect, and Newton’s Third Law of Motion which will not
be discussed in this thesis. Overall, wing lift is a complex phenomenon resulting from a
combination of these aerodynamic principles, working together to enable the aircraft to
overcome gravity and stay aloft. All these theories are distilled in a simple formula:
L = 1
2 · ρ · V 2 · A · CL

where
• ρ is the density of the fluid
• V is the velocity of the fluid relative to the wing
• Ar is the reference area of the wing/foil
• CL is the coefficient of lift

erations due to their operation in a denser fluid (water) than aircraft wings in the air. This
difference in fluid density leads to varied flow characteristics and lift generation mecha­
nisms. Moreover, hydrofoils face specific challenges like cavitation and the high­speed
requirement for stability and control. As a result, hydrofoil design and lift theory consider
these factors to optimize their performance in water­based applications. Pure, dry air has

2.2.2
Foil systems
This section aims to clarify different foil systems, enabling readers to grasp the terminolo­
gies used later and have a basic knowledge of the design configurations of hydrofoils. This
section explains and depicts different types of foil systems to clarify terminology used later
and familiarise the reader with hydrofoils design setups.
Submerged hydrofoil
A submerged hydrofoil is a configuration where the lifting foils are positioned entirely un­
derwater during operation fig. 2.2. Unlike surface­piercing foils, where the lifting hydro­
foils partially emerge from the water’s surface, submerged hydrofoils remain fully sub­
merged, attached to the hull by struts that do not lift the system. Submerged hydrofoil
configurations are commonly utilized in various applications, including military vessels,
high­speed ferries, and specialized underwater vehicles. The design considerations for
submerged hydrofoils revolve around optimizing hydrodynamic efficiency, stability, and
control to achieve the desired performance characteristics in different operating condi­
tions. This configuration offers specific advantages and characteristics:
1. Reduced Wave Interference: Submerged hydrofoils operate below the water’s sur­
face, minimising interaction with surface waves. This can improve ride comfort and
stability, especially in rough sea conditions.
2. Increased Immersion Range: Submerged hydrofoils can maintain lift and stability
over a wider range of immersion depths. They are not restricted to a specific im­
mersion range like surface­piercing foils, allowing more flexibility in varying water
depths.
3. Enhanced Maneuverability: Submerged hydrofoils typically offer better manoeu­
vrability than surface­piercing foils. With the entire foil submerged, they can provide
more precise control of pitch, roll, and yaw motions, contributing to improved han­
dling and responsiveness.
4. Reduced Ventilation Risk: Submerged hydrofoils are less prone to ventilation is­
sues than surface­piercing foils. When a hydrofoil makes contact with the water’s
surface, a phenomenon known as ventilation occurs, causing air to be drawn down
the surface of the foil responsible for generating lift.

Surface piercing hydrofoils
A surface­piercing hydrofoil refers to a hydrofoil configuration where the foils partially
emerge from the water’s surface during operation. In this configuration, a portion of the
hydrofoil remains submerged while the upper portion extends above the water. Surface­
piercing hydrofoil configurations are used in various applications, such as high­speed

==PAGE:19==
boats, racing vessels, and certain military craft. The design and optimization of these
configurations focus on achieving the right balance between lift, drag reduction, stability,
and control to maximize performance in varying hydrodynamic conditions. This design
offers specific advantages and characteristics:
1. Reduced Wetted Area: Surface­piercing hydrofoils have a smaller wetted area
than fully submerged foils at design speed. With a portion of the foils lifted up to the
air higher with increasing speed, there is less contact between the hydrofoil and the
water, resulting in reduced friction and improved efficiency (if induced drag is not
exceedingly increasing and the ventilation is avoided).
2. Enhanced Speed Potential: Surface­piercing hydrofoils are well­suited for high­
speed applications. By reducing drag and utilizing the lifting capabilities of the par­
tially emerged foils, these configurations can enable hydrofoil craft to achieve higher
speeds compared to fully submerged designs.
3. Wave Interaction: The interaction between surface­piercing foils and waves can
have positive and negative effects. On the positive side, the foils can help dampen
the effects of wave impacts and improve stability. However, certain wave conditions
can also cause disruptions and impacts on the foils, leading to potential instability
and performance limitations.
4. Ventilation Considerations: Surface­piercing hydrofoils need to be designed with
ventilation in mind. As the foils pierce the water surface, there is an increased risk
of ventilation occurring on the upper surface due to lower pressure. Proper design
considerations are necessary to minimise ventilation­related issues, including foil
shape, surface treatments, and ventilation plates or fences.

Stability and Maneuverability
To clarify, the word hydrofoil. It is a wing that flies in the water. The wing’s cross­section,
called a profile, may have the shape of an aircraft wing or a modification thereof. In a
typical hydrofoil configuration, the primary lifting wing is coupled with an additional wing,

the hull and causing it to elevate above the water’s surface. In this case, the words

3.1
Stability and control
Stability, as the dictionary says, is the capacity of an object to return to equilibrium or its
original position after being displaced. [10] The left column presents two primary avenues

classes, as shown in Fig. 3.1 from Anderson’s book [11]. The difference between these
categories is described below.

3.1.1
Static stability
Static stability is in equilibrium when the sum of all forces and all moments equals zero.
The static stability of a system is defined by the initial tendency to return to equilibrium
conditions following some disturbance from equilibrium.[12]

If an object is disturbed from equilibrium, the resulting motion’s time history indicates
the system’s dynamic stability. The system will generally demonstrate positive dynamic

The difference between static and dynamic stability is the oscillation over a period of time.
Static stability is the tendency to return directly to equilibrium. Dynamic stability is
when equilibrium is reached through a series of oscillations over a period of time. It is
important to note that while all dynamically stable systems possess static stability, not all

Controllability and stability lie on a continuum between two extremes [13] this is a de­

Height and Pitch control
In aviation, altitude is commonly employed to denote the vertical distance above sea level,
typically measured in hundreds or thousands of meters. Conversely, the corresponding
metric in hydrofoils is referred to as height or flying height, typically expressed in centime­
tres or values close to a meter. Due to this fact, height control has to be more precise,
and the range is relatively tiny compared to aircraft. Being near the air­water interface
grants hydrofoils a significant advantage. This proximity enables their height sensors
to accurately gather height information from the water’s surface. These height sensors
are connected to a system that adjusts the lift accordingly, increasing or decreasing it as
necessary. By doing so, precise height control is consistently maintained. The primary
responsibility of height control is the front foil, making it a crucial aspect for hydrofoils of
all sizes and speeds.
Without proper height control, a front foil becomes dynamically unstable. For example,
the front foil is completely submerged when the boat rests. As power is applied and the
speed of the boat increases, the lift generated by the foil also increases, proportional to
the square of the speed eq. (2.1). Eventually, the lift surpasses the boat’s weight, causing
the foil to rise and exit the water. As presented, a particular control system is necessary
to maintain the craft’s stability.

6.1
Mausyama model
Masuyama developed a relevant study on the stability dynamics of hydrofoil boats [15]
The small­disturbance theory examined the interaction between the longitudinal and lat­
eral movements, considering the hydrofoils and sail. Stability derivatives were computed
for these components. The analysis revealed a critical sailing condition that limits the
maximum achievable velocity of the boat, contrary to conventional predictions for hydrofoil
sailing boats. If the velocity surpasses this critical sailing condition, the sailboat becomes
statically unstable or diverges from its intended path.

Longitudinal stability model
Certain simplifications were made based on stability analysis to simplify the problem and

simulation, including the entire boat, this would result in significantly more complicated
calculations. Instead, the model focused on simulating the rear and middle foil. In simpler

involved in longitudinal motions. Analyzing directional stability revolves around the rota­

Bague’s thesis.[16]

Results
The initial test results were analyzed to develop a detailed model that needed adjustments
to improve accuracy. This process helped to gain a deeper understanding of the system’s
characteristics and dynamics. Through multiple analyses, the following findings were
discovered:
Feeler’s sensitivity analyses
The initial experiment aimed to verify the impact of controlling the front and rear foils on
stability. An illustration of this control mechanism can be found in Chapter 4, in the section
on feelers/skeemers. As previously explained, skeemers regulate the lift coefficient by
adjusting the angle of attack (AOA), resulting in a steady cruising state. Three approaches
attempted to solve this problem: controlling only the front foil, the rear foil, and both foils
together for both setups of foils.
Setup 1 longitudinal stability
This section investigates whether the principles and theories used in aircraft design can
be applied to hydrofoil design to achieve improved and enhanced stability. It explores
the potential of implementing aircraft design concepts to optimize hydrofoil design and
attain stability outcomes. In this setup, 2 different moments of inertia are tested, depicting
different mass distributions on the craft, such as crew members’ movement.
Setup 2 longitudinal stability
The subsequent section explores setup 2 of hydrofoils, which consists of straight horizon­
tal foils without a dihedral angle. Additionally, two moments of inertia are tested to analyze
their effects. Finally, a case involving the control of only the rear foils is conducted to verify
its impact on stability.
8.1
Setup 1: Feelers’ sensitivity analysis
One of the drivers in the model stability is the response from stability feelers. How feelers
work is presented in Chapter 4. The control mechanism for the front and rear foils involves
a pitching action proportional to the foil’s downward/upward displacement. Variables in
the model:
• KFrontFoil
• KRearFoil
E.g., KFrontFoil = 30, for every meter that the front foil is pushed down, it pitches 30
degrees upwards (nose up). As a result, if the front foil (at its centre) moves downward
by 1 cm (equivalent to 0.01 m), it will pitch up by 0.3 degrees. This control mechanism
counteracts vertical displacements of the front of the hydrofoil. Higher values of this con­
trol parameter indicate a more aggressive control response, which may result in a stiffer
and potentially rougher ride, especially in wave conditions. Subsequently, in this thesis,
an examination assesses these variables’ influence on the hydrofoil craft’s stability. The
analysis focuses on investigating how variations in these specific parameters can affect
the overall stability characteristics of the boat. In the tables below real values marked red
have a positive value, thus leading to system instability.

only the front foil was controlled. This aspect should be taken into consideration in future

9
Conclusion and future work
9.1
Conclusion
In conclusion, this thesis has delved into understanding the hydrofoil concept, explor­
ing its principles and underlying concepts. It has provided an overview of stabilization
methods and dynamic stability, focusing specifically on hydrofoil crafts. This thesis has
addressed the longitudinal stability characteristics of hydrofoils by developing a stability
matrix, incorporating insights from aircraft and hydrofoil stability research. Solving a sub­
set of the stability problem has contributed to a deeper understanding of the hydrofoil’s
stability modes, the effects of control inputs, and the potential for design or control adjust­
ments to enhance overall stability and performance. The stability matrix developed serves
as a crucial component for subsequent implementation in the model, ensuring accuracy
and reliability in the analysis and evaluation of hydrofoil stability. Furthermore, this thesis
has employed a model to perform dynamic stability calculations for a specific hydrofoil
configuration. These calculations have considered various variables that can influence
the stability outcome, allowing for an analysis of their impact on the dynamic stability of
the hydrofoil setup. The findings of this research provide insights into the design and oper­
ation of hydrofoil crafts, enabling engineers and researchers to make informed decisions
regarding stability and performance optimization. By combining theoretical understand­
ing with practical calculations, this thesis has contributed to advancing the knowledge and
capabilities of hydrofoil stability. The research conducted in this thesis lays a foundation
for future exploration and progress in hydrofoil stability. The information and techniques
presented have the potential to elevate designs, propel hydrofoil technology forward, and
ultimately generate more dependable, efficient, and secure hydrofoil vessels.
9.2
Future work
The overarching objective of this thesis and the preceding research conducted by other
students is to design a hydrofoil boat that can leverage the accumulated knowledge and
insights. However, several crucial aspects need to be considered to achieve this goal.
The following step of this thesis would involve developing a comprehensive stability model
encompassing all degrees of freedom. Subsequently, the focus would shift towards the
structural design, height and pitch control mechanism, and ultimately, the conceptualisa­
tion of the hull and steering equipment. Although these tasks require substantial effort, it is
feasible to accomplish them without significant financial investments. While considerable
work is still ahead, this systematic approach can pave the way for realising a well­designed
hydrofoil boat, capitalising on the findings and advancements made thus far.
