Choice modelling and machine learning in travel behavior analysis 
Abstract 
Comparisons between machine learning (ML) techniques and conventional discrete choice 
models (DCMs) have become more common in the prediction of travel mode choice.  Neural 
networks have powerful predictive powers by capturing complex nonlinear relationships and 
interaction effects.  Despite this, their behavioural interpretability remains restricted, which 
poses a challenge to their applicability in the context of policy-making and planning.  On the 
other hand, DCMs, particularly the Random Utility Maximisation (RUM) theory-based 
Multinomial Logit (MNL) model, are praised for their theoretical clarity and interpretability 
but are limited by strict presumptions and little room for behavioural heterogeneity and 
nonlinearities. 
This study attempts to close the performance gap between ML and DCMs by presenting a 
thorough evaluation utilising the London Travel Survey.  The conventional MNL model is 
improved by adding interaction factors and nonlinear transformations.  Furthermore, 
unobserved taste heterogeneity is addressed by estimating Mixed Logit models, while cross-
alternative impacts are considered using the Mother Logit model.  In addition to these logit-
based models, two neural architectures based on RUM theory are created: (i) an Alternative-
Specific Neural Network (ASNN), which uses different subnetworks for every utility function, 
and (ii) a hybrid model that combines neural network layers for socioeconomic variables with 
MNL for modelling essential travel attributes like time, cost, and distance. 
A panel-aware data splitting technique is employed to avoid individual-level data leakage and 
guarantee robust model evaluation, exposing that previous neural network performance was 
somewhat exaggerated as a result of data leaking. According to the results, the performance 
gap with double hidden layer neural network is reduced by 6.3% when the MNL is enhanced 
with nonlinearities, and it is further reduced by 3.5% when the Mother Logit is used. The 
suggested hybrid model attains near-parity with a gap of just 1.44%, while the ASNN model 
reduces the gap by 3.15%. Deterministic predictions are replaced with probabilistic assessment 
metrics, resolving well-known problems like bias in aggregate-level predictions brought up by 
past research. In order to enhance interpretability and provide behaviourally significant insights 
into variable contributions and model behaviour, SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) are 
also applied to the neural models. Furthermore, for 10% increase in driving cost, double hidden 
layer neural network shows a counterintuitive negative elasticity for cycling mode. This 
highlights a key limitation of lacking built-in behavioral constraints whereas logit-based and

1. Introduction 
With the emergence of new transportation options such as vehicle sharing, bicycle-sharing, 
ridesourcing, & micro-transit make urban travel more complicated, it is more important than 
ever to accurately model mode choice for good transportation planning and policy development 
(Hagenauer & Helbich, 2017). The goal of travel mode choice modelling is to look at how 
travellers make decisions and act when they have to choose between different modes of 
transport, based on a certain set of characteristics. Transport planners and policymakers have 
to analyse alternative transportation options and forecast patterns for the future.  To assess their 
travel habits, statistical survey methods like revealed preference and stated preference, which 
require respondents to disclose their preferences and ideal situations, accordingly, are 
frequently employed (De Carvalho, et al., 1998). Travel mode choice models can be calibrated 
using the discrete choice data provided by the surveys.  These models are integral components 
of disaggregate travel demand modelling techniques such as activity-based modelling (ABM). 
Additionally, the challenges associated with integrating explanatory components into the 
function of utility, including conditional and threshold effects, sometimes result in the neglect 
of interactions among them, such as the nonlinearity of characteristics. One approach to address 
this issue inside the linear function of utility is to incorporate additional variables, including 
interaction and polynomial factors. This can be accomplished by examining every conceivable 
pairing of explanatory variables or by looking at real-world correlations (Bentz & Merunka, 
2000). Determining all of the interactions among the components and the necessary variables 
is nearly impossible, though. The predetermined structures and linear characteristics of the 
underlying functions make it difficult to identify or infer significant nonlinearity in a dataset, 
even though flexible random utility model techniques like mixed logit (Train, 2009) perform 
better for modelling than multinomial logit because they do not assume that the data is 
independent and identically distributed (IID) (Train, 2009). Mixed Logit models are more 
flexible since they can account for random differences in taste, but they also require more 
computing power and still depend on specified utility structures (Train, 2009).

Meanwhile, there is a evolving trend in employing machine learning techniques to model travel 
demand, extending beyond the limitations of classic discrete choice models (DCMs) that have 
been in place for decades (Cantarella & Luca, 2005). Algorithmic non-parametric approaches 
have shown exceptional effectiveness in the analysis of urban systems (Derrible, 2016). 
Machine learning techniques, including neural networks, provide robust, non-parametric 
options that do not enforce strict assumptions regarding data structure or functional form. These 
capabilities allow for the identification of complex, high-dimensional associations directly 
from observed data, frequently resulting in enhanced forecast accuracy across various travel 
behaviour scenarios (Cheng, et al., 2019). In the last ten years, Machine Learning (ML) models 
have emerged as alternatives to RUM models in travel mode choice analysis. Nonetheless, 
despite their robust performance, machine learning classifiers, such as neural network, are 
frequently denounced as “black boxes,” without of the transparency required to explain 
behavioural relationships, including the direction, magnitude, and elasticities of essential 
determinants of travel choices, which are vital for policy analysis and the formulation of 
effective interventions (Cheng, et al., 2019). The lack of interpretability, combined with their 
restricted ability to integrate previously acquired domain knowledge, presents considerable 
obstacles for the investigation of travel behaviour using machine learning techniques. These 
issues underscore the necessity for novel approaches to explain ML model outputs and 
investigate improved normalisation techniques. Furthermore, modern explainability 
approaches like Shapley Additive Explanations (Lundberg & Lee, 2017) are still inadequately 
employed for analysing travel behaviour, suggesting a potential area for further investigation. 
In addition to knowing which factors affect travel behaviour, policymakers also need to know 
how and to what degree these factors affect travel behaviour (Wang & Ross, 2018). Therefore, 
figuring out if machine learning methods are good in mode choice modelling requires checking 
how well they can predict the future and their capacity to generate significant and interpretable 
behavioural insights (Cheng, et al., 2019). This research presents a thorough benchmark 
assessing the efficacy of machine learning models, neural networks, and random utility models 
in travel mode decision modelling. The comparison includes the systematic approaches 
identified in contemporary literature, along with novel promising techniques. 
Nevertheless, a lot of these studies make generalisations about the superiority of machine 
learning based on small datasets or specific applications, frequently ignoring high model 
performance variability and the critical role of variables like sample size and data 
dimensionality. To support this concern, (Hillel, et al., 2020) examined about 70 research 
papers and found serious technical flaws in most of them. Majority of the research regards 
==PAGE:6==
Page 6 of 75 
 
choice model and machine learning techniques as deterministic and overlooks the probabilistic 
characteristics of analytical frameworks. Log-likelihood, maybe the most pertinent metric for 
assessing probabilistic discrete alternatives, is one of the assessment metrics that receives too 
little attention. 
Moreover, utilising deterministic predictions instead of probabilistic outputs to ascertain 
market shares, as is frequently standard in machine learning research, may lead to issues like 
class inequality and aggregate biassed difficulties (Hillel, et al., 2020). This poses significant 
queries regarding the potential effects of performance indicator selection on the results of 
machine learning and choice modelling approach comparisons. 
Furthermore, contemporary comparisons between logit models and machine learning 
techniques primarily emphasise the MNL model, the most basic logit variant, while 
overlooking more sophisticated models like Mixed Logit, which are more suitable when the 
IIA assumption is violated, particularly in panel data scenarios with repeated observations from 
the same subjects (Train, 2009). There is currently a sizable gap in the literature because there 
hasn't been a systematic evaluation of Mixed Logit models' prediction performance and 
behavioural interpretability in contrast to ML approaches. Moreover, there are essential 
distinctions in the treatment of alternative-specific attributes, input data structures, and output 
formats between ML methods and logit models, which have considerable implications for 
practical modelling and methodological progress, yet remain inadequately examined (Omrani 
, et al., 2013). 
This study lies in its integrated evaluation of discrete choice models and machine learning 
approaches, with a focus on enhancing behavioral realism and interpretability. Unlike many 
prior studies that have not focus much on lowering the gap between discrete choice model and 
ML, as addressed by (Bentz & Merunka, 2000), who noted that traditional linear utility 
specifications often overlook meaningful variable interactions and threshold effects.  this study 
systematically incorporates nonlinearity and interaction effects within discrete choice models. 
Additionally, this research presents a comprehensive assessment of advanced logit models, 
including Mixed Logit (MXL) with random ASCs and trip-specific variables, and the Mother 
Logit model, which remains underexplored despite its capacity to relax IIA assumptions. This 
addresses the lack of systematic evaluation in the literature, as noted by (Train, 2009) and 
(Omrani , et al., 2013). While existing studies frequently benchmark only basic MNL models, 
this work expands the scope to more flexible and realistic logit structures. 
Moreover, the study employs a panel-aware cross-validation strategy, evaluates probabilistic 
prediction metrics instead of relying solely on deterministic classifications (argmax), and 
==PAGE:7==
Page 7 of 75 
 
reports log-likelihood to better align with the probabilistic foundations of discrete choice 
models, as recommended by (Hillel, et al., 2020). The analysis also includes SHAP-based 
explainability and elasticity estimation, addressing the growing concern that machine learning 
models, despite their high accuracy, often lack interpretability and behavioral coherence 
(Cheng, et al., 2019) & (Wang & Ross, 2018). 
All of these findings point to a crucial trade-off: ML models offer flexibility and predictive 
power, while DCMs offer interpretability and theoretical foundation. A growing interest in 
hybrid techniques that incorporate knowledge from both modelling paradigms has resulted 
from the recognition of this equilibrium. This integration promises to enhance prediction 
performance while maintaining the ability to produce actionable behavioural insights, which is 
essential for developing travel behaviour modelling and facilitating better transportation 
planning and policy formulation. 
Recent studies have produced novel approaches that specifically integrate the advantages of 
machine learning and discrete choice models into cohesive model frameworks, reflecting the 
increasing interest in hybrid modelling approaches. The creation of hybrid discrete choice 
models, which combine the adaptability and predictive potential of machine learning 
architectures, especially neural networks, with the interpretability of traditional econometric 
techniques, is one noteworthy achievement in this field. According to (Sifringer, et al., 2020), 
the Learning Multinomial Logit (L-MNL) model is a well-known illustration of this 
integration. The L-MNL framework signifies notable progression by partitioning the 
systematic utility component into two separate elements: a knowledge-driven term that 
preserves the classical linear structure and interpretability of traditional discrete choice models, 
and a data-driven term sourced from neural networks, adept at identifying complex patterns 
within the data. This approach enables the model to partially substitute manual utility 
specification with automated learning, while preserving interpretability in critical areas. The 
L-MNL model presents a viable advancement by integrating the clarity of economic theory 
with the representational power of machine learning, thus improving forecast accuracy while 
maintaining behavioural understanding (Sifringer, et al., 2020). The same approach has also 
been implemented in this study and referring as Hybrid model, only difference is the knowledge 
driven term (trip specific variables) are non-linear in this study. 
In a similar vein, recent research in choice modeling has focused specifically on designing 
neural network architectures that embed behavioral theory directly into their structure to 
address the challenges of interpretability and domain relevance. (Wang, et al., 2020a) have 
introduced models such as the Alternative-Specific Utility Deep Neural Network (ASU-DNN) 
==PAGE:8==
Page 8 of 75 
 
who have suggested the direct integration of behavioural knowledge into the design of neural 
network architectures. Rather than treating neural networks purely as black-box predictors, 
ASU-DNN frames network design as a utility specification problem inspired by the theory of 
random utility maximization (RUM), enabling domain-specific behavioral insights to shape the 
neural network’s structure. This approach aims to enhance both predictive performance and 
interpretability in choice modeling by mirroring the utility-based framework of classical 
discrete choice models within neural network architectures. Building on this concept, the 
present study adopts a similar alternative-specific structure but implements an Alternative-
Specific Utility Neural Network (ASNN) with a double hidden layer architecture in each 
subnetwork. Unlike (Wang, et al., 2020a), who process individual-specific variables through 
separate neural layers and merge them into alternative-specific subnetworks only after several 
transformations, this research ASNN integrates both ways, detailed procedure has been explain 
in methodology. This adaptation seeks to balance the flexibility and predictive accuracy of 
neural networks with the need for behavioral interpretability, representing a step forward in 
leveraging domain knowledge for neural network–based discrete choice analysis.  
2. Literature 
There are several uses for travel mode choice analysis, such as anticipating travel demand, 
formulating policies, and better understanding the factors that influence it (Chang, et al., 2019). 
It represents a fundamental component of the four-step travel demand model. 
One of the most researched and intriguing topics in travel behaviour research is the 
investigation of travel mode choice. Current literature demonstrates that travel mode selection 
is influenced by various factors, including travellers' socio-demographic characteristics (De 
Vos, et al., 2016); trip attributes such as distance, cost, and purpose (Hagenauer & Helbich, 
2017); built environment features (Ye & Titheridge, 2017); individual attitudes (De Vos, et al., 
2016); weather conditions (Liu, et al., 2015); urban land use (Zhang, 2004); and external factors 
such as comfort, convenience, security, and travel satisfaction (Zhou, et al., 2015). 
Discrete choice models, grounded in Random Utility Maximization (RUM) theory, have been 
extensively used for travel mode choice prediction. Common examples include the 
Multinomial Logit (MNL), Mixed Logit (MXL), and Nested Logit models. These models are 
valued for their closed-form mathematical structure and the interpretability of their parameter 
estimates, which provide insights into individual preferences and trade-offs. 
However, these models also come with notable limitations. They rely on strict statistical 
assumptions, such as the independence and identical distribution (i.i.d.) of error terms, that, if 
violated, can result in biased or inconsistent estimates. Conversely, machine learning models 
==PAGE:9==
Page 9 of 75 
 
do not depend on rigid prior assumptions. They allow computers to investigate fundamental 
data patterns, provide more adaptable modelling frameworks, and often attain enhanced 
forecast accuracy (Zahid Khattak, et al., 2020). 
Furthermore, behavioural heterogeneity and individual preferences in mode selection are 
influenced by characteristics including gender, age, driving license, income group, vehicle 
ownership, level of education, and household composition. (Cheng, et al., 2019) discovered 
that women utilise public transportation more often than men. (Li, et al., 2012) noted that 
automobile usage generally rises with advancing age. Additionally, individuals with higher 
incomes and those possessing a valid driving licence are more inclined to opt for automobile 
transport (Soria-Lara, et al., 2017). 
Numerous research indicates that those with higher educational attainment generally favour 
public transit for most regular journeys, especially those conducted for recreational reasons 
(Cheng, et al., 2019). Moreover, individuals in bigger households typically exhibit a lower 
propensity for selecting non-motorized travel options than those in smaller households (Ryley, 
2006). Various aspects of the built environment strongly affect travellers' mode choice, 
including land-use diversity, building density, closeness to amenities, dedicated infrastructure 
for cycling and pedestrians, and overall transportation provisions (Chen, et al., 2008). Mixed 
land use and high-density areas generally facilitate active transportation modes, such as 
walking and cycling, due to reduced travel distances and the presence of infrastructure that 
supports these activities and public transit use. 
Recent years have seen a growing focus on how several aspects of travellers' attitudes, such as 
travel happiness, comfort, and convenience, affect their mode selection (De Vos, et al., 2016). 
Studies indicate that attitudes may be more potent determinants of mode selection than 
conventional objective metrics. A study indicated that travellers in Guangzhou, China, 
exhibited more favourable attitudes towards automobiles than public transportation, resulting 
in a higher propensity for car ownership (He & Thøgersen, 2017). The presence of well-
constructed park-and-ride facilities adjacent to public transit stations has been shown to 
encourage travellers to utilise public transit (Ortega, et al., 2021). 
Discrete choice models, especially different types of logit models, have historically been used 
in mode choice analysis and travel behaviour research. Logit models are a subset of statistical 
models based on the concepts of random utility maximisation (RUM) for the analysis of travel 
mode selection. Within the RUM framework, it is presumed that travellers possess complete 
knowledge of the benefits and drawbacks of all accessible options and make logical choices to 
select the mode that provides the greatest utility. McFadden and Stopher were innovators in the 
==PAGE:10==
Page 10 of 75 
 
implementation of the multinomial logit (MNL) model for analysing travel behaviour 
(McFadden, 1974). Ben-Akiva and Lerman subsequently defined the model's structure and its 
applicability in travel behaviour modelling (Ben-Akiva & Lerman, 2018). 
A recent review by (Koushik, et al., 2020) effectively highlighted the utilisation of machine 
learning approaches in the study of activity-travel behaviour. The evaluation indicated an 
increasing tendency in the volume of published papers in this domain, suggesting that machine 
learning is becoming a valuable instrument for analysing travel behaviour. Nonetheless, it 
underscored a prevalent issue: several methodologies presented in the literature are deficient 
in interpretability, hence constraining their practical applicability in decision-making and 
policy formulation. Integrating feature engineering and behavioural elements may alleviate this 
difficulty. An overview of some notable studies between machine learning and discrete choice

3.4 Neural network 
Both single and double hidden layer neural networks were built to predict multi class travel 
mode choice using panel data and cross-sectional data (without panel data) for a comparison 
between them, but panel data was selected for further modeling and evaluating neural network 
with discrete choice model. Although most of the machine learning research studies have 
focused on cross sectional data (without panel data), assuming each row (trip) is independent 
of each other, this risk data leakage if multiple trips from the same individual appear in both 
training and test sets. To prevent this, our modeling respected the panel structure by splitting 
data at the individual level. 
Before modelling, data has been gone through some initial steps as stated at the start of the 
methodology like one hot encoding categorical variables and handling missing values using 
median and mode. Data spits were performed so no individual appeared in more than one 
partition (training, validation and test). For hyperparameter tuning and validation, splits used 
Group 5-Fold cross validation using Keras Tuner. Each fold maintained the panel structure. In 
each of the 5 folds, individuals were split into training and validation groups. Approximately 
20% of individuals from the training portion were used for validation, while 80% of the training 
set were used for training. This ensured that validation data in each fold consisted of entirely 
separate individuals, preserving the panel structure and hyperparameter candidates were 
evaluated based on validation accuracy averaged across folds. The hypertuning parameter 
architecture has shown below in Table 4:

Learning rate was tuned as a hyperparameter for the Adam optimizer which basically controls 
the update speed of all network weights during training and softmax function converts raw 
outputs into probabilities summing to one, allowing the model to predict the most likely travel 
mode. Once optimal hyperparameter were found using 5-fold cross validation, a separate panel

overfitting during the final model training and guide early stopping. The final model was 
retained on the reduced training set and validated on this separate hold out set. Early stopping 
with a patience of 20 ecophs was applied based on validation loss. Finally, model performance 
was assessed on a 30% holding test set ensuring no individual overlapping with training or 
validation sets.  
3.5 Discrete choice model using RUM (MNL): 
The modeling framework follows the principle of Random Utility Maximization (RUM), 
assuming individuals choose the travel mode providing the highest perceived utility. In MNL 
the unobserved components of utility are assumed to be independently and identically 
distributed (i.i.d.) Gumbel, which leads to the Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives (IIA) 
==PAGE:25==
Page 25 of 75 
 
property. This implies that the relative odds between two alternatives are unaffected by the 
presence or attributes of other alternatives. Additionally, alternative-specific variables 
influence only the utility of their own alternative, consistent with the MNL formulation. The 
models were estimated using Biogeme has been used which integrates the Gumbel-distributed 
unobserved component analytically to compute closed-form choice probabilities. 
The utility functions are formulated as linear-in-parameters functions, meaning that model 
coefficients enter linearly, and the explanatory variables have been placed as linear, non-linear 
and as interactions between two variables.

3.6 Mixed Logit (MXL): 
Mixed logit framework was implemented to capture random taste heterogeneity in travel mode 
choice. Due to computational time, MNL base model has been used to build several MXL 
versions on two main aspects: a) random alternative specific constants (ASCs) only, and b) 
random trip specific variables including time, cost and distance variables only. 
For random alternative-specific constants (ASCs) only, each travel mode (walk, cycle, drive, 
public transport) was in turn specified as the base alternative with its ASC fixed to zero. The 
==PAGE:28==
Page 28 of 75 
 
remaining ASCs were treated as random parameters, following a normal distribution. For each 
model, standard deviation estimates of the random ASCs were examined, and the base utility 
leading to the lowest standard deviation was selected as the preferred reference alternative. The 
initial estimation used 100 Modified Latin Hypercube Sampling (MLHS) draws, and 
subsequently, 200 draws were applied to the best base specification to examine changes in 
model performance. 
For random trip-specific variables (time, cost, and distance) only, drive utility has been kept 
base, in order to compare with MNL base model, such as how introducing randomness into trip 
specific variables will change its performance. The variables were modeled as normal 
distributed random coefficients, random = mean +  * draw. MLHS was again employed for 
draws, starting with 100 and extending to 200 draws for the best-performing models. 
The initial values for mean parameters were sourced from the best-fitting Multinomial Logit 
(MNL) models to facilitate convergence. While, standard deviations for each random 
parameters (e.g.  for ASCs, or for time, cost and distance) were constrained between -15 and 
+15 bounds with an initial values of 0, but in this case some of the standard deviations for each 
random parameters came as negative, since standard deviations can not be negative in 
mathematical term, thus the lower and upper bounds has been changed with 0 to 10 with an 
initial values of 0.5. 
3.7 Mother logit Model 
Mother logit extends the standard multinomial logit (MNL) framework by explicitly 
incorporating cross-alternative dependencies. Unlike MNL assuming independence of 
irrelevant alternatives (IIA) and evaluates choices based solely on alternative’s own attributes, 
the mother logit relaxes this restriction by allowing the utility to depend on the characteristics 
of competing alternatives. MNL base model has been used as an initial model to start with 
mother logit where cross influence of travel time between drive and public transport has been 
incorporated since in real life they kind of influence each other and people are more concerned 
about time compared to other attributes. While trying to incorporate cross influence, distance 
variables has been omitted from the base model of mother logit due to multicollinearity effects 
with time and cost, also seen in the correlation graph in figure 2(a&b). Afterwards, the 
socioeconomic variables of MNL has been incorporated in the mother logit model along with 
“age x travel time” interactions, since “age x travel” interactions has shown better result on 
unseen data set (test set) compare to “purpose x travel time” & “car ownership x cost”. Utility

3.8 Alternative specific neural network (ASNN) 
Two types of alternative specific neural network (ASNN) has been built following the 
principles of random utility maximization, to model travel mode choice. In both approaches, 
each alternative’s utility is modeled independently in a dedicated subnetwork, ensuring that 
utilities remain alternative specific and are not influenced by the attributes of other modes. In 
ASNN 1, trip specific variables (e.g. time cost and distance) and socio economic variables (e.g 
age, gender etc) are jointly pass into the hidden layers together of each alternative specific 
subnetwork, allowing the model to learn interactions among all the variables directly from the 
first hidden layer. On the other hand, in ASNN 2, trip specific variables are processed first 
through the first hidden layer of each subnetwork and at the second hidden layer, socio 
economic variables join, allowing the model to first learn trip specific variables before 
integrating user-specific attributes. Both the model ensures panel data structure and the data 
splitting and evaluation are the same as other models.

3.9 Hybrid Model 
A hybrid travel mode choice model has been developed by integrating traditional MNL 
framework (MNL base model) with a double hidden layer, since it gave better result compared 
to single hidden layer. Panel data has been maintained to avoid data leakage and data splitting 
of 70% training, 10% validation of training and 30% testing set has been followed. Two groups 
of predictors have been formed which are travel-related attributes for the MNL component, and 
socioeconomic variables for the NN component. Both groups of features are scaled 
independently using standardization to improve model convergence. The MNL model 
computes utilities for each alternative via a linear combination of the transformed travel 
attributes and alternative-specific constants (ASCs). Parallelly, the NN learns additional 
nonlinear contributions to the utility using two hidden layers. The outputs of both components 
are summed and converted into choice probabilities through a softmax function.

Notably, log of age in the utility of public transport (beta_log_age_pt_ is positive, suggesting 
that older individuals show a higher preference for public transport, suggesting that elderly 
often reply on PT. Conversely, coefficients involving age for walk and cycle are negative, 
implying as age increases, the utility of walking and cycling decreases, reflecting physical 
limitations or preference shifts away from active modes among older populations. 
The presence of a driving license negatively influences the utility of walking, cycling, and 
public transport, highlighting that licensed individuals are more inclined to use private vehicles. 
Interestingly, the driving traffic percent shows positive coefficients for non-car modes, cycle, 
walk and PT, indicating a modal shift away from car use under high congestion. 
Gender-specific effects shows that female travelers are more likely to prefer public transport, 
which they show lower preferences for cycling and walking possibly due to safety and comfort. 
Car ownership is negatively associated with choosing non-car modes (walk, cycle, PT), as it 
has shown negative signs. However, its interaction with cost (car ownership × cost driving total 
and car ownership  x cost transit) shows positive coefficients for both driving and PT, 
suggesting reduced cost sensitivity among car owners, although car ownership in this 
==PAGE:35==
Page 35 of 75 
 
interaction  case has been used as a proxy for income, and the sign does make sense since 
people with higher income or having more cars will be less aware or less care about cost. 
Finally, several interaction terms between age and travel time (e.g., age × duration for walk, 
cycle, and PT) are negative, highlighting that older individuals are more sensitive to increased 
travel time, reducing the likelihood of choosing time consuming modes. Furthermore, all 
purpose × travel time interactions exhibit negative signs, reflecting that longer durations reduce 
utility across all trip purposes. 
Among the MNL models, MNL 2 (incorporating age x travel time interactions) consistently 
demonstrated superior performance. It achieved highest probabilistic metrics such as accuracy 
(0.6322 ± 0.0048), precision (0.4996 ± 0.0046), recall (0.5008 ± 0.0047) and F1 score (0.5000 
± 0.0046) among all MNL variants, while maintaining a balanced calibration brier score of 
0.1839 ± 0.0024. Despite MNL 3 and MNL 4 has shown better results but on test dataset 
(unseen data) MNL 2 has given better metrics comparatively.  
When compare to double hidden layer NN (with panel data), MNL 2 model demonstrated a 
competitive performance. Neural network achieved higher values across predictive metrics 
such as precision (0.6945 ± 0.0032), recall (0.6952 ± 0.0030), F1 (0.6948 ± 0.0030) and 
accuracy of (0.6952 ± 0.0030) confirming its capacity to learn complex non-linear patterns.  
However, MNL 2 reduced the performance gap such as for accuracy 6.3% (0.6952 ± 0.0030 
vs 0.6322 ± 0.0048), precision 19.49% (MNL 2 vs NN), recall 19.44% (MNL 2 vs NN), F1 
score 19.48% (MNL 2 vs NN) and brier score 10.67 % (MNL 2 vs NN). This outcome 
highlights a key methodological insight, such as the inclusion of log-transformed variables and 
behavioral interactions, can substantially enhance predictive validity even when compred with 
more complex black box models. 
4.3 Mother logit 
Mother logit models retained core behavioral consistency with MNL such as negative signs for 
travel time and cost, age- related declines in walk/cycle preferences, but it went beyond 
traditional discrete choice of MNL by explicitly modelling cross alternative influences. This 
design enabled the utility of one mode to be affected by attributes of another, as stated in the 
utility equations in methodology, where dur_driving is placed in public transport utility and 
dur_pt_total in drive utility. 
For instance, the positive coefficient of beta_log_time_drive_pt implies that as driving time 
increases, the utility of public transport improves, suggesting a substitution effect where longer 
car travel causes modal shift to PT. Similarly, the positive beta_log_time_pt_drive coefficient 
reflects the reciprocal pattern, indicating that longer public transport durations can increase the 
==PAGE:36==
Page 36 of 75 
 
relative appeal of driving. These bidirectional effects demonstrate how travelers dynamically 
evaluate trade-offs not only within a mode but across competing options, uncovering 
behavioral compensation that traditional MNL misses.

The ASNN2 model architecture, in which trip specific variables are first processed 
independently before integrating socio-economic variables at the second hidden layer, 
demonstrates slightly better overall performance than ASNN 1, across most evaluation metrics. 
Specifically, improvements are observed in precision (from 0.6635 ± 0.0035 to 0.6667 ± 
0.0030), recall (from 0.6630 ± 0.0035 to 0.6637 ± 0.0029), F1 score (from 0.6637 ± 0.0029 to 
0.6650 ± 0.0030), accuracy (from 0.6630 ± 0.0035 to 0.6637 ± 0.0029), Cohen’s kappa (from 
0.4921 ± 0.0052 to 0.4925 ± 0.0043), and misclassification rate (from 0.3370 ± 0.0035 to 
0.3363 ± 0.0029). Although ASNN 2 shows marginally lower values for ROC-AUC and PR-
AUC compared to ASNN1, its overall performance remains superior.  
In contrast, the Hybrid model (MNL + NN) outperforms ASNN2 across all metrics. This 
improvement arises from the ability to capture interactions between socio-economic variables 
across all alternatives, which ASNN structures cannot represent due to their strict adherence to 
the Random Utility Maximization (RUM) framework. Moreover, the hybrid architecture 
relaxes the Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives (IIA) assumption, enhancing its flexibility 
and predictive power relative to the more behaviorally constrained ASNN models. 
When comparing ASNN & hybrid models to traditional discrete choice models such as MNL 
2 and Mother Logit 2, both demonstrate significantly improved predictive performance across 
all metrics. However, relative to the fully connected double hidden layer neural network with 
panel data, both ASNN (specially ASNN2) & hybrid perform slightly lower in terms of overall 
metrics. 
==PAGE:43==
Page 43 of 75 
 
Specifically, the accuracy gap between ASNN 2 and double hidden layer NN was 3.15% 
(0.6952 ± 0.0030 vs 0.6637 ± 0.0029), whereas Hybrid model reduced this gap to just 1.44% 
(0.6952 ± 0.0030 vs 0.6808 ± 0.0030). Similar improvements were observed across other 
evaluation metrics. The precision gap dropped from 2.78% (ASNN 2 vs NN) to 1.72% (Hybrid 
vs NN), recall from 3.15% now 1.44%, F1 score from 2.98% to 1.4% and brier score improved 
from 0.58% to 0.35%. This improvement represents the further gap improvement between 
interpretable RUM-based models and black-box neural networks, facilitated through structured 
hybridization. 
Although the observation of ASNN contrasts with the findings by (Wang, et al., 2020a), where 
ASNN2 outperformed a fully connected deep neural network. In the present study, although 
the performance gap between ASNN2 and double hidden layer is not substantial, the 
discrepancy may be attributed to key structural differences in this study. Specifically, the 
ASNN2 model strictly adheres to RUM principles by (i) fixing the base utility’s ASC to zero, 
(ii) excluding socio-economic variables from the base utility, and (iii) employing a panel data 
structure to prevent data leakage across train and test sets. Although it captures complex 
behavioral interactions better than traditional discrete choice models, its structured separation 
of utility components limits its flexibility compared to a fully connected architecture. 
Furthermore, Hybrid model has shown similar results compare to (Sifringer, et al., 2020) where 
the gap between the hybrid model and NN was 2.1% (79.2 vs. 81.3 in accuracy) but the MNL 
component was specified with linear effects, the current study employs logarithmic 
transformations for time, cost, and distance in the MNL component. This non-linear utility 
specification contributes to the hybrid model’s enhanced performance and smaller gap with the 
fully connected neural network.

(TRPs) ranging from 54.3% to 58.2%. The hybrid model performs best (58.2%), closely

persistent challenge across all models is misclassification of walk trips into motorized 
categories, particularly drive and public transport. This likely results from overlapping 
characteristics, such as short trip distances, moderate travel times, and similar spatial contexts, 
especially in dense urban areas where mode substitution is common.

Cycle 
Cycle is the most challenging mode to classify with all models achieving true positive below 
7%. This poor performance is primarily due to the extreme class imbalance present in the 
dataset, where cycling trips are underrepresented. More behavioral overlaps are happening 
between cycle and PT, since these two modes share several overlapping attributes: both 
typically cover short to medium distances, have similar or close travel times in some of the 
dataset, and do not require private vehicle ownership. 
In contrast, less confusion between Cycle and Drive, likely because Drive involves higher 
costs, longer distances, and vehicle ownership, make it behaviorally distinct. Walk is also less 
confusing with Cycle, as walking trips are usually shorter in both distance and duration 
compared to cycling. 
Drive 
Drive is the most consistently well-classified mode, with TPRs ranging from 65.3% to 76.5%. 
The DHL model achieves the best performance (76.5%), closely followed by the Hybrid model 
(76.1%), In contrast, MNL 2 underperforms substantially, with a TPR of only 65.3%, one thing 
to noted is drive utility was base among MNL 2 and Mother Logit 1. Misclassification of drive 
trips into PT is lowest in the DHL model (13.01%) followed by Hybrid model (13.4%) and 
highest in MNL 2 (20.2%). This confusion may stem from overlaps in travel time and route 
structure, especially in congested urban corridors where public transit and driving options share 
similar spatial characteristics. 
PT 
Public transport classification performance is uniformly strong across models, with TPRs 
exceeding 68% in all cases. The DHL model again leads with the highest TPR (73.5%), 
followed by ASNN 2 (72.4%) and ASNN 1 (71.3%) while MNL 2 model performs the worst 
at 68.9%. Confusion with Drive is lowest in ASNN 2 (13.6%), followed by Hybrid (14.8%), 
and highest in MNL 2 (18.6%). However, some misclassification persists due to shared cost 
and distance characteristics between PT and Drive, especially in cases where PT trips involve 
longer distances. ASNN 2 demonstrates strong generalization for PT classification due to its 
utility-specific design, but DHL remains the top performer, confirming its robustness across 
multiple mode types. The Hybrid model balances accuracy with reduced misclassification to 
Drive, further validating the power of hybrid architecture.

modal distribution observed in the dataset. The probabilistic class report compares observed 
and predicted mode shares across all test samples, interpreting how models capture aggregate

interpretability and nonlinearity, leading to a well-calibrated distribution.

5.0 Conclusion: 
This study provided a rigorous and comprehensive comparison between traditional discrete 
choice models and advanced neural network architectures for travel mode choice prediction. 
In contrast to many prior works that rely solely on deterministic predictions (i.e., highest 
predicted class via argmax), this research adopted probabilistic evaluation metrics and 
incorporated panel-aware data splitting to ensure methodologically sound and behaviorally 
valid model assessments. This approach mitigated the risk of data leakage and enabled fairer 
performance comparisons across model families. 
Among the tested models, the double hidden layer neural network achieved the highest 
predictive performance; however, panel-aware validation revealed overestimated accuracy in 
previous approaches that neglected individual-level data structure. Importantly, the MNL 2 
model narrowed the prediction gap with the neural network by 6.3%, while Mother Logit 2 
reduced it further to 3.5%, illustrating that relaxing the IIA assumption and capturing cross-
utility interactions significantly enhances predictive accuracy. Notably, ASNN 2 decreased the 
gap to 3.15%, and the hybrid model (MNL + NN) achieved the closest performance to the 
neural network, reducing the gap to just 1.44%, while offering superior interpretability. 
Although socio-economic variables were not included in MXL 7, it still outperformed the MNL 
and Mother Logit base models, showing 0.57% and 0.06% improvements respectively. These 
results reinforce the utility of Mixed Logit models in capturing unobserved heterogeneity. 
Future research could further explore performance variations by increasing the number of 
Modified Latin Hypercube Sampling (MLHS) draws and testing alternative draw types to 
improve MXL robustness such as Halton Draws.  
Mode-wise classification analysis indicated that Cycle remains the most difficult class to 
predict due to extreme class imbalance. To address this, future studies should incorporate data 
balancing techniques such as SMOTE and undersampling to improve model learning. 
Confusion matrices and probabilistic class reports revealed that the hybrid model most 
accurately replicated observed mode shares, demonstrating strong calibration properties 
compared to other models. 
Elasticity analysis showed that both logit-based and hybrid models produced economically 
meaningful and behaviorally plausible substitution patterns in response to cost increases, 
particularly showing rational shifts toward public transport and cycling. In contrast, standard 
neural networks struggled to reproduce consistent substitution behavior, likely due to their lack 
of structural constraints, although this was improved in ASNN and hybrid variants. This 
==PAGE:53==
Page 53 of 75 
 
observation aligns with findings in prior literature where neural networks demonstrated 
inconsistencies in elasticity estimation (e.g., Wang et al., 2020b; Zhao et al., 2020). 
As highlighted by Hillel et al. (2020), much of the current literature on machine learning 
models generalizes their superiority based on small or domain-specific datasets, often ignoring 
model variability due to factors such as sample size and data dimensionality. Our findings 
reinforce the importance of systematic, fair comparisons that account for these factors. 
Finally, SHAP-based interpretability analysis revealed that MNL and Mother Logit models 
provided more transparent and behaviorally aligned feature explanations. Although SHAP 
visualizations for neural networks were less interpretable—due to non-linear model structures 
and computational constraints—future work should extend SHAP analysis using the full 
dataset and explore model-agnostic explainability tools to improve transparency in complex 
architectures. 
In conclusion, this study bridges the methodological gap between machine learning and 
discrete choice models, offering a robust, interpretable, and behaviorally grounded framework 
for travel mode choice modeling.

Project Proposal: Choice modelling and machine learning in travel behavior analysis 
Research Context 
Mode choice prediction is essential in transportation research, providing insights into how 
individuals select travel modes based on factors such as travel time, cost, and personal 
characteristics. Accurate predictions are crucial for understanding travel behavior and improving 
transportation models. 
Discrete choice mode based on Random Utility Models (RUM), including Multinomial Logit 
(MNL) and Mixed Logit, are widely used due to their interpretability and foundation in utility 
maximization. However, their predictive performance is constrained by parametric assumptions, 
such as the Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives (IIA) in MNL, which can lead to unrealistic 
substitution patterns. While Mixed Logit addresses some limitations by allowing random taste 
heterogeneity, it increases computational complexity and still relies on predefined functional forms. 
In contrast, Machine Learning (ML) models, such as Neural Networks (NN), excel in predictive 
accuracy by capturing complex, high-dimensional relationships without predefined functional 
forms. However, ML models often lack interpretability, making it difficult to extract behavioral 
insights, and they require large datasets and significant computational resources. 
Given these trade-offs, a detailed examination of RUM and ML models is necessary to assess their 
relative predictive performance and identify the assumptions that constrain RUM’s effectiveness. 
This research will explore insights from NN (e.g., through SHAP analysis) to refine mixed logit 
models, develop non-RUM mixed logit models that relax traditional assumptions, and 
create hybrid approaches that combine the interpretability of RUM with the predictive power of 
ML, advancing mode choice modeling and transportation research. 
 
Research Aims 
This research aims to compare discrete choice models based on Random Utility Maximization 
(RUM) with machine learning (ML) models, Neural Networks, to evaluate how closely RUM 
models can match the predictive power of ML models and to identify the assumptions within RUM 
that constrain their predictive performance. Insights from neural networks, obtained through SHAP 
analysis, will be used to refine the mixed logit model by incorporating important features, non-
linearities, and interactions. The study will develop hybrid models combining RUM and ML, such 
as a Neural-Embedded Discrete Choice Model and a restricted neural network aligned with mixed 
logit structure, to capture non-linearities while retaining interpretability. Additionally, a non-RUM 
mixed logit model will relax traditional RUM assumptions. The performance of all models will be 
compared, and trade-offs between interpretability and predictive power will be analyzed to guide 
model selection in travel behavior analysis. 
 
Learning Objectives 
1. Explain the principles, assumptions, strengths, and weaknesses of discrete choice models 
based on RUM and Machine Learning (ML) models for travel mode prediction. 
2. Analyze travel behavior datasets and implement RUM-based models (e.g., MNL & Mixed 
Logit) and ML models (e.g., Neural Networks) using statistical software (e.g., Python, R, 
Biogeme, scikit-learn). 
3. Refine mixed logit models by incorporating insights from Neural Networks (NN) (e.g., 
SHAP analysis) to capture non-linearities and feature interactions. 
4. Develop non-RUM mixed logit models that relax traditional RUM assumptions, such as 
allowing features of all alternatives to influence each alternative’s utility. 
5. Evaluate the performance of hybrid approaches that combine the interpretability of RUM 
with the predictive power of ML and recommend appropriate modeling approaches for 
specific travel behavior analysis problems. 
6. Write a methodologically sound report with data description, model estimation, results 
interpretation, and critical discussion.
