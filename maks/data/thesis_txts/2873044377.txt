Abstract
Reading interfaces have evolved significantly with digital technologies, but typography
remains a critical factor affecting readability, especially for individuals with reading diffi-
culties. While users can manually adjust font size, spacing, or type, the optimal config-
uration for enhancing reading performance and comfort is often unknown and can vary
across individuals. This thesis addresses this challenge by systematically investigating
how typographic parameters such as font type, font size, and letter spacing affect reading
behavior and user experience.
To conduct this study, a web-based experimental platform was developed, integrating Tobii
eye-tracking technology to capture detailed gaze data during reading tasks. Twenty-three
participants, including three with diagnosed reading difficulties, read short paragraphs
presented in eight typographic configurations (T1 to T8), which combined variations of
Arial and Verdana fonts, two font sizes (18px / 13.5pt and 24px / 18pt), and two letter
spacings (10% / 0.10em and 28% / 0.28em). Key eye-tracking metrics such as fixation
count, regression count, and skip rate were analyzed using ANOVA and post hoc tests,
alongside subjective preference ratings.
The results show that skip rate was the only metric significantly influenced by typography
(p < 0.05), while other metrics such as fixation count and total reading duration remained
unaffected. Configurations T7 (Verdana, 18px / 13.5pt, 10%) and T8 (Verdana, 18px /
13.5pt, 28%) were most preferred by participants, demonstrating a balance between flu-
ency and careful reading. These findings challenge the common assumption that wider
letter spacing always improves readability, emphasizing the importance of evaluating mul-
tiple parameters together.
By combining a custom experimental platform, objective eye-tracking data, and subjective
feedback, this thesis contributes both a methodological framework and empirical evidence
to guide the design of accessible digital typography. The outcomes provide valuable in-
sights for creating user-centered reading interfaces and open pathways for future research
on rereading behavior, comprehension, and broader font comparisons.

Introduction
Reading is a fundamental part of everyday life, essential for learning, communication,
and navigating both digital and physical environments. However, for many individuals,
reading is not always seamless. Poor text presentation, such as inadequate layout, font
choice, or letter spacing, can hinder readability and cause discomfort, even for those
without diagnosed reading disabilities [1]. Meanwhile, individuals with reading difficulties,
such as dyslexia, often face additional challenges including frustration, fatigue, and a
persistent sense of inadequacy, which may lead to emotional strain and social withdrawal
[2]. Improving readability is therefore crucial to supporting a wide spectrum of readers
with varying abilities.
Typography, the design and arrangement of text, plays a central role in shaping the read-
ing experience. It includes visual elements such as font type, size, spacing, and align-
ment. While often seen as a stylistic concern, typography significantly affects how easily
text is perceived and processed. Research shows that thoughtful typographic design can
enhance reading fluency and comprehension for all readers, while offering added support
for those with specific challenges [2, 3].
Readers with dyslexia often struggle with decoding, letter recognition, and phonological
processing. They may also face visual issues like letter crowding, eye-tracking difficulties,
and increased fatigue [4]. Adjustments such as using sans-serif fonts and increasing letter
spacing have been shown to ease these challenges [5]. Fonts like Arial and Verdana,
when paired with wider spacing, can improve letter recognition and reduce crowding.
Importantly, these changes also benefit neurotypical readers, demonstrating a universal
design potential [4].
Reading the Reader is a research project at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), in
collaboration with the Royal Danish Academy. It explores how adaptive text presentation
can improve accessibility, especially for visually impaired users. By leveraging real-time
eye-tracking, the system adjusts elements like font size and letter spacing to optimize
comfort as users read. While the project focuses on dynamic interfaces, it reinforces a
broader understanding that typography is vital for inclusive design. This thesis builds on
that foundation by investigating how static typographic choices, those not reliant on real-
time adaptation, can improve digital readability in an evidence-based, inclusive manner.
Effective typography remains critical in digital reading. Strategies like larger font sizes
and letter spacing can guide attention and reduce cognitive load for all readers [1]. Al-
though fonts like Dyslexie and OpenDyslexic have been designed for dyslexic users, stud-
ies show mixed results compared to standard fonts [6, 7, 8, 9, 10]. Yet, many platforms
still prioritize visual aesthetics or space efficiency over accessibility. There is a clear need
for empirically grounded, user-centered typographic design that accommodates diverse
reader needs [2].
This thesis contributes to the Reading the Reader project by examining how static design
parameters, specifically font type, size, and letter spacing, affect digital readability for
individuals with and without reading difficulties. While the project emphasizes adaptive
systems, this study offers complementary insights into which static settings may serve as
optimal defaults. Using eye-tracking data and user feedback, the findings aim to inform
more inclusive, accessible digital reading experiences.

1.1
Motivation: Why is this interesting
Despite growing awareness of inclusive digital design, typographic accessibility is still
inconsistently addressed. Many interfaces default to dense layouts, small fonts, or tightly
packed text, often making reading unnecessarily difficult, especially for individuals with
dyslexia [9],[11],[12]. Even for the general population, poorly optimized text can cause
visual discomfort or disengagement.
While adaptive systems are being explored, they require complex infrastructure and user
calibration. On the other hand, static typography, if well-designed, offers a practical and
scalable way to enhance readability. However, most existing research isolates individual
variables (e.g., only font size) or focuses on user preferences rather than observable
reading behavior.
This thesis addresses that gap by using eye-tracking data to analyze how combinations of
font type, size, and inter-letter spacing influence real-time reading. The goal is to identify
configurations that enhance readability without sacrificing usability or aesthetics, support-
ing more inclusive digital design.
1.2
Initial Problem Statement(IPS): Research objective and
Significance
Static typography remains an underutilized tool in accessible digital design. While adap-
tive systems can adjust content in real time, there is limited empirical understanding of
how fixed typographic combinations affect reading behavior across diverse user groups.
This study aims to fill that gap by using eye-tracking to investigate how different font styles
and settings influence attention, reading flow, and comprehension. By comparing partic-
ipants with and without dyslexia, it identifies which typographic features support more
inclusive reading experiences, without requiring dynamic adjustment.
The results will inform practical design guidelines that can be applied in everyday digital
platforms. This contribution supports the broader Reading the Reader project by providing
evidence-based insights into how visual text design can improve readability, accessibility,
and user comfort in static reading environments.
1.1
Research Objective
• To investigate how font type, font size, and inter-letter spacing influence readability
in digital reading environments.
• To compare the reading behavior of individuals with or without dyslexia, focusing on
how they interact with different typographic styles.
• To use a Tobii eye-tracker to collect and analyze visual reading patterns such as fixa-
tion duration, saccadic movements, and reading speed across different typographic
layouts.
• To understand how typographic choices affect the reading experience for both user
groups.
1.2
Research Significance
The significance of this study lies in its potential to address a gap in current research on
digital typography. Although typography has been explored in general, there is limited em-
pirical evidence using real-time eye-tracking data to understand how specific typographic
features, such as font type, font size, and inter-letter spacing, affect digital reading be-
havior in individuals with and without dyslexia. This study combines both objective gaze

Final Problem Statement: Research Question and
Hypothesis
This study is motivated by the need to evaluate how typographic adjustments can improve
the readability and user experience for individuals with reading disabilities, particularly in
digital formats. The Reading the Reader concept, introduced in Section 1.1, serves as
the basis for this study, which aims to assess the effectiveness of different typographic
interventions through eye-tracking data. This research specifically focuses on how typo-
graphic features (font type, size, letter spacing, contrast, etc.) influence reading behavior
by measuring eye metrics such as fixation duration, saccadic movements, and regression
counts.
1. Research Question 1. How do cognitive and visual processing difficulties in individ-
uals with reading difficulties (e.g., dyslexia, autism) influence their interaction with
digital text, and how are these differences reflected in eye-tracking metrics (e.g.,
fixation duration, regression rate, saccades) when comparing their preferred and
non-preferred typographic conditions (e.g., font size, letter spacing, and size)?
• Hypothesis (H1): Individuals with reading difficulties exhibit improved eye-
tracking performance (e.g., shorter fixation durations, fewer regressions, higher
reading speed) when reading digital text in their preferred typographic settings
(i.e., larger font sizes or increased letter spacing), compared to non-preferred
conditions.
2. Research Question 2. How have specific typographic features (i.e., fonts, size,
and letter spacing) been applied in previous studies, and what limitations remain
in understanding their effectiveness for neurodiverse readers (e.g., individuals with
dyslexia or autism) in terms of eye-tracking metrics and reading behavior?
• Hypothesis (H2): Typographic configurations that improve eye-tracking out-
comes (shorter fixations, fewer regressions, and faster reading) may differ from
those recommended in prior studies, even when some parameters like font size
or letter spacing are similar. This highlights that overall effectiveness depends
on the combination of features, not just individual elements.
3. Research Question 3. How do specific typographic interventions (e.g., changes in
font type, font size, letter spacing) influence reading performance and user experi-
ence for individuals with or without reading difficulties?
• Hypothesis (H3a): Verdana will yield faster reading speeds and shorter fixa-
tion durations than Arial across both reader groups, due to its larger x-height
and more open letterforms.
• Hypothesis (H3b): Text set in 24px font size will result in smoother reading
patterns and higher word-per-minute rates than 18px, as larger text reduces
visual effort and improves character recognition.
• Hypothesis (H3c): Letter spacing of 28% will lead to fewer regressions and
lower fixation counts than 10% spacing, as increased spacing reduces visual
crowding and eases word segmentation.

Literature Review
2.1
Search Strategy and Literature Discovery Approach
An iterative and exploratory search process guided the selection of relevant literature for
this study. Instead of relying solely on structured keyword-based queries, a combination
of direct searches (using terms like “typographic readability,” “eye-tracking and dyslexia,”
and “font spacing in digital texts”) and snowballing techniques was employed.
In particular, several key studies were initially identified through academic databases such
as Google Scholar, DTU Findit, IEEE Explorer, and ACM Digital Library. From these, cited
and citing works were reviewed to discover additional relevant papers, a process often
referred to as backward and forward citation tracking. This approach allowed for a broader
exploration of related themes, including font size and readability, inter-letter spacing in
dyslexic populations, and design best practices in accessibility.
This method, while less rigid than a systematic review, aligns with common exploratory
practices in HCI and accessibility research where conceptual depth and design implica-
tions are as important as methodological exhaustiveness.
2.2
Typography and Readability in Digital Contexts
Typography plays a crucial role in shaping how readers engage with text, particularly in
digital contexts where design elements, such as font type, size, and spacing, significantly
impact readability. Early research on dyslexia focused largely on phonological deficits;
however, more recent studies highlight that visual processing challenges such as visual
crowding, lateral masking, and letter confusion also play a critical role in reading difficulties
experienced by individuals with dyslexia [13]. These insights have paved the way for
typographic research aimed at reducing perceptual barriers and enhancing text legibility
through optimized design. Similarly, individuals with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)
may exhibit reading difficulties, particularly in comprehension, despite having intact or
even advanced decoding skills. These challenges are often linked to broader deficits
in language processing, attention, or theory of mind, rather than phonological impairment
[14, 15]. As such, inclusive typography design must consider a range of cognitive profiles,
not just traditional reading difficulties, to ensure accessibility for neurodiverse populations.
It is important to note that, in this thesis, the term ”reading disabilities” is intended to en-
compass a broad range of reading difficulties rather than to label individuals as disabled.
This includes readers with dyslexia and, where relevant, individuals on the autism spec-
trum who may experience visual or cognitive challenges affecting reading fluency and
comprehension. While autism itself is not classified as a reading disability, individuals on
the autism spectrum often experience reading difficulties, particularly in comprehension,
despite having adequate decoding skills [16]. This underlines the importance of designing
accessible typographic features that support diverse cognitive and perceptual profiles.
2.3
Influence of Font Type on Readability
Font type has consistently emerged as a significant variable in readability. Sans-serif
fonts like Arial and Verdana are widely recommended for their open, clean letterforms
and larger x-heights, which facilitate easier character recognition. Verdana, in particular,
has been noted for reducing visual noise and promoting faster reading speeds, especially
among readers with dyslexia [17]. In contrast, serif fonts such as Times New Roman
and Georgia often hinder readability due to their compact design and intricate letterforms,
which can exacerbate crowding effects in dense textual environments [5], [13].

While dyslexia-specific fonts like Dyslexie and OpenDyslexic were developed to address
these challenges, research reveals no consistent performance benefit in reading speed
or accuracy compared to standard sans-serif fonts [6], [8], [9]. Subjective preferences do
not necessarily correlate with reading performance, underscoring the need for objective
assessment tools like eye-tracking.
2.4
Influence of Letter Spacing on Reading Behavior
Inter-letter spacing is a critical typographic feature that has been widely studied about
reading comprehension and cognitive processing. [18] conducted an eye-tracking ex-
periment using an EyeLink 1000 eye tracker at 1,000 Hz to investigate the effects of
inter-letter spacing on reading. They found that wider letter spacing led to shorter fixation
durations, improving reading speed and efficiency. Additionally, subtle adjustments in
letter spacing enhanced word encoding by enlarging the visible letter group, thus reduc-
ing eye movement times and fixation durations, which made the reading process more
efficient.
However, the benefits of spacing adjustments have limits. [19] and [20] found that ex-
cessive spacing between letters can lead to reading delays, even with shorter fixation
durations. [21] further reported that while reduced visual crowding leads to shorter fix-
ations, excessive letter spacing increases cognitive load due to more complex saccadic
planning, ultimately slowing reading speed. These findings suggest that optimal letter
spacing is essential for reducing crowding effects without incurring cognitive costs from
excessive spacing.
Research has also shown that increased letter spacing improves reading speed and accu-
racy, particularly among dyslexic children. Moderate spacing enhancements can reduce
fixation durations and regressions, helping to mitigate visual crowding and supporting
letter recognition. Conversely, reduced letter spacing can increase visual stress, raise
cognitive load, and negatively affect comprehension.
2.5
Influence of Font Size in Digital Reading
Font size further influences readability. In one eye-tracking study, various font sizes rang-
ing from 10pt to 26pt (13px to 35px) were tested using Helvetica. Larger sizes, particularly
between 18pt and 26pt (∼24px–35px), resulted in faster reading speeds, reduced visual
strain, and higher user preference scores[22]. These findings align with the broader litera-
ture suggesting that moderate-to-large font sizes support readability across both dyslexic
and typical populations. Notably, research by [12] reported reduced reading speed for
smaller sizes (10pt, ∼13px), [23] found impaired judgment accuracy below 24pt (∼32px),
and [24] demonstrated that font size alters eye movement patterns during reading [12].
Further supporting these findings, an eye-tracking study by [25] found that 18pt and 24pt
font sizes led to significantly better cognitive outcomes and reduced fixation durations
compared to smaller or larger text, regardless of device (computer or tablet) [25]. This
reinforces the principle that optimal font size exists within a mid-range and that both un-
dersized and oversized text can hinder reading efficiency.

2.6
From Literature to Research Design
Emerging innovations like SeeType, which uses animated letters and visual phoneme
cues, illustrate how dynamic and multisensory approaches may enhance readability [31].
However, such technologies remain in early validation stages.
Despite the growing literature, substantial gaps remain. Many studies lack biometric track-
ing, limit their participant scope to neurotypical readers, or explore typographic features in
isolation. This thesis addresses these gaps through a reader-centered experimental de-
sign that integrates eye-tracking metrics and subjective user evaluations to explore how
typography affects reading behavior in both typical and dyslexic populations.
This literature foundation (See table 2.2) also guided the selection of experimental tools,
most notably the use of eye-tracking technology, to enable both quantitative and experi-
ential evaluation of typographic accessibility.
2.1
Parameter Selection: Theoretical Rationale
To investigate the typographic factors that most influence readability and user experience,
this study builds on prior research (See table 2.1) to identify three key variables: font
type, font size, and inter-letter spacing. These parameters have been repeatedly shown
to impact cognitive processing, eye movement patterns, and overall user experience,
particularly for individuals with reading disabilities.
2.1
Initial Experimental Scope
The study initially proposed testing only two parameters: font type (Arial, Helvetica) and
inter-letter spacing (+10%, +30%). This decision was guided by early accessibility re-
search emphasizing the role of clean, sans-serif fonts and enhanced spacing in improving
readability for dyslexic readers [4], [5].
2.2
Design Revision and Literature-Driven Expansion
However, early reviews and pilot feedback suggested that Helvetica and Arial were visu-
ally too similar to produce measurable perceptual differences. Furthermore, literature on
readability and eye-tracking increasingly emphasized font size as a critical yet previously
overlooked factor in the original design [22], [25].

2.2
Eye-Tracking Technology in Reading Research
Eye-tracking is a powerful tool for understanding visual attention and reading behavior. It
records where and for how long a person fixates on various parts of a screen, offering ob-
jective insight into cognitive processing during tasks such as reading. Eye trackers detect
pupil position and corneal reflection using infrared light(desktop or glasses-type trackers),
and from this information, estimate gaze coordinates in real time. High-quality eye track-
ers such as Tobii or Smart Eye offer precise measurements, but webcam-based tracking
has gained attention due to its accessibility. Despite its lower precision, recent research
8
Typography’s Impact on Readability
==PAGE:17==
showed that webcam tracking, when calibrated correctly, can still produce valuable data
for distinguishing between typical and dyslexic reading patterns[34] [35].
This study employs the Tobii Eye Tracker 4C, a consumer-grade remote infrared eye-
tracking device designed for desktop environments. The 4C model uses dark-pupil track-
ing and dual infrared illuminators to detect gaze points at a sampling rate of 90 Hz. Though
not as precise as high-end research-grade eye trackers (e.g., Tobii Pro Spectrum), the 4C
strikes a practical balance between accuracy and accessibility for controlled experimental
settings. When used under stable lighting with proper calibration, it is capable of reliably
capturing key reading-related metrics, such as fixations and regressions, at a level of
detail suitable for readability research.
Eye tracking systems typically rely on either the bright-pupil or dark-pupil method to detect
gaze based on corneal reflections using infrared light. Bright-pupil methods offer high
contrast between pupil and iris, which is useful for indoor, consistent lighting. Dark-pupil
methods, on the other hand, can operate better in varying lighting conditions and are less
prone to reflection-based interference [13].
In the context of dyslexia, eye-tracking has been validated to capture increased fixation
durations, frequent regressions, and shorter saccades. These markers reflect reading
difficulty and can be used to assess the impact of different typographic settings on visual
and cognitive effort [36]. Moreover, eye-tracking helps evaluate whether accessible typo-
graphic interventions (e.g., increased letter spacing or use of specific fonts) lead to more
fluid eye movement behavior and reduced processing load.
2.3
Eye Tracking Metrics and Computational Approaches
Key metrics used to analyze reading behavior include:
• Fixation Duration: This is how long the eye stays still on a word or phrase. During
these pauses, the brain processes the information and stores it in memory. Longer
fixations usually mean the text is harder to understand, requiring more cognitive
effort.
• Saccades: These are the quick movements the eyes make between words. During
these movements, the brain’s visual processing is temporarily paused. The length
and direction of these eye movements can tell us how smoothly someone is reading.
[37]
• Regressions: These occur when the eyes move backward to reread a part of the
text. This often happens when the reader is struggling to understand or needs to
review information.
• Gaze Path: This is the overall movement of the eyes through the text, showing us
how the reader navigates from one word to the next. It helps visualize the reading
process.[36].
2.4
Statistical Techniques for Analyzing Eye Tracking Data
Eye-tracking tools primarily focus on measuring fixation duration, as it reflects the amount
of time the eyes remain focused on a specific area or word in the text. This metric is often
associated with cognitive processing load, as longer fixation durations tend to indicate
that the reader is encountering more difficulty with visual or lexical properties of the words
being read [38].

To analyze eye-tracking data, several processing steps are involved. First, the raw data is
cleaned by removing any tracking errors and blinking instances. Once the data is filtered,
algorithms are applied to detect key eye movements, such as fixations, saccades, and
regressions. These movements are identified using established time thresholds and po-
sitional criteria, enabling researchers to categorize and quantify the eye-tracking metrics
effectively.
2.1
Dispersion-Threshold(I-DT)
The IDT algorithm is commonly used in eye-tracking studies to detect fixation points by
identifying when the gaze stays stable within a defined area [39]. It uses a sliding window
to assess spatial dispersion and fixation duration, with typical thresholds set at 3 degrees
for spherical stimuli and 60 pixels for static images. While the IDT algorithm is simple
and efficient, its accuracy depends on proper calibration, which can be affected by ex-
perimental conditions, device noise, and individual eye movement patterns. It may also
struggle in dynamic environments or with complex stimuli, leading to missed fixations or
inaccurate saccade identification.
2.2
Identification by Velocity-Threshold(I-VT)
The IVT algorithm is a widely used method for classifying eye movements into fixations
and saccades based on gaze velocity. It calculates the velocity of gaze coordinate sam-
ples and compares it to a predefined threshold. If the velocity is below the threshold,
the gaze is classified as a fixation, while velocities above the threshold indicate saccadic
movements. This algorithm is often favored in reading studies due to its simplicity and
efficiency in processing large amounts of data quickly [40]. However, its performance is
sensitive to the calibration of the velocity threshold, as improper calibration can lead to
incorrect classifications, particularly in noisy data or specific experimental settings.
2.3
Identification by Two-Means Clustering (I2MC)
The I2MC algorithm [41] is a robust and adaptive method for detecting fixations in eye-
tracking data, especially when the data is noisy or incomplete. It applies a sliding window
to the gaze stream and uses two-means clustering to separate stable gaze points (fixa-
tions) from rapid movements (saccades). This clustering approach allows the algorithm
to dynamically adapt to variations in the data, offering improved accuracy over threshold-
based methods.
Compared to simpler algorithms like I-DT (Identification by Dispersion Threshold) or I-
VT (Identification by Velocity Threshold), I2MC does not rely on rigid spatial or velocity
thresholds. These traditional methods often misclassify fixations in the presence of signal
noise, data loss, or participant movement. In contrast, I2MC is designed to manage such
irregularities, making it well-suited for experiments involving natural reading behavior and
remote eye-tracking setups like the Tobii 4C.
In this study, I2MC was chosen because of its strong performance under real-world con-
ditions. Participants were exposed to different typographic configurations (T1–T8), and
variations in font size, spacing, or layout could impact tracking quality. I2MC ensures
consistent and reliable fixation detection across all conditions, which is critical for accu-
rately measuring reading behavior and assessing the impact of typography on readability
(further described in 3.2).

Methods
This chapter describes the methodology used to investigate how specific typographic in-
terventions namely font type (Arial vs. Verdana), font size (18 px vs. 24 px), and letter
spacing (+10% vs. +28%) influence reading performance and user experience in individu-
als with and without diagnosed reading difficulties (RQ 3). The experiment was conducted
in a controlled laboratory setting using a Tobii eye-tracker to capture key eye movement
metrics, including fixation duration, fixation count, saccade amplitude, and regression fre-
quency, which serve as objective indicators of visual processing effort. Following each
reading trial, participants completed a short multiple-choice comprehension quiz and rated
the readability and comfort of the text on a subjective survey, providing both quantitative
and qualitative insight into their reading experience under each typographic condition.

3.2
Experiment Design
This section explains the main parts of the experiment. Each part was important in shaping
how the study was planned, making sure the results are trustworthy, and reducing outside
influences. The setup helps us understand how changes in typography affect reading
behavior. Everything was designed to focus on the impact of the text styles used in the

3.1
Design Type
This study employs a within-subjects design to evaluate the impact of various typographic
configurations (font type, size, and inter-letter spacing) on readability and user experience.
The within-subjects design is chosen because it allows each participant to experience all
eight typography conditions, facilitating direct comparisons of their performance across
conditions. This design minimizes the influence of individual differences, such as baseline
reading ability and attention, by using the same participants across all conditions. As a
result, it provides a more controlled analysis of typographic effects on reading behavior,
allowing for a clearer understanding of how each typographic element influences reading.
To avoid potential biases from the order in which conditions are presented, the typog-
raphy conditions are randomized across participants. This randomization ensures that
each participant experiences a unique sequence of typography variations, minimizing the
risk of order effects where the sequence of exposure might influence a participant’s per-
formance. By doing this, the study ensures that each typography condition is assessed
independently, without being influenced by the order in which participants encounter them.
Thus, the study uses a randomized within-subjects design. This approach combines the
benefits of within-subject comparisons, which control for individual differences, with ran-
domization to minimize biases and confounding factors. The result is a more robust and
reliable analysis of how typography affects readability and user experience, while also
accounting for variables like individual reading habits, attention span, and cognitive pro-
cessing differences.

When the user finishes a trial, the front end first calls POST /stop, locking in every sample
in FastAPI’s buffer. It then immediately issues a single GET /gaze request, which returns
the full list of sanitized gaze points. Because all samples remain in memory until fetched:
• Zero data loss: No samples are missed, provided the /gaze call occurs before any
buffer reset.
• Minimal latency: On localhost, the HTTP round-trip and JSON serialization take only
1–3ms, imperceptible for offline data collection.

3.2
Fixation Detection Using the I2MC Algorithm
The Identification by Two-Means Clustering (I2MC) algorithm, introduced by [41], is a ro-
bust, unsupervised method designed to detect fixations in noisy eye-tracking datasets.
It was originally developed to accommodate the challenges associated with lower cost,
remote eye trackers such as the Tobii 4C where signal interruptions, calibration drift, and
data dropout are more common. This made I2MC particularly well-suited for our experi-
ment, where natural reading behavior was observed under varying typography conditions
using a non-invasive tracking setup.
Understanding Fixations and Saccades in Reading
According to [43] and [44], in visual tasks like reading, eye movements primarily consist
of two patterns: fixations and saccades. Fixations refer to brief periods where the eyes
remain relatively stationary, allowing the brain to process the visual content in focus such
as a word or group of letters. Saccades, in contrast, are the quick, ballistic movements
that shift the gaze from one point to another. These two behaviors alternate constantly
during reading.
The characteristics of these movements vary depending on the cognitive load and the
nature of the task. In continuous reading, for example, saccades generally occur every
200–250 ms, and each saccade typically spans a few degrees of visual angle. Fixations
last between 150–300 ms, depending on the reader’s familiarity with the content, font
properties, and text structure. In our study, we observed similar dynamics, with fixation
durations often increasing under more visually complex or crowded text conditions, such
as tightly spaced paragraphs.
Why I2MC?
Several alternative algorithms for detecting fixations and saccades could have been uti-
lized, such as those included in the set of 10 methods evaluated by [45]in their 2017 study.
Traditional algorithms, like I-VT and I-DT, often rely on hard-coded thresholds for veloc-
ity or dispersion to classify fixations. However, these thresholds may not generalize well
across participants or adapt to variations in data quality. In contrast, the I2MC algorithm
leverages statistical differences between fixation and saccade velocity patterns through a
two-means clustering process. This approach allows I2MC to automatically adjust to the
specific signal characteristics of each dataset, eliminating the need for user-defined cutoff
values. The selection of I2MC was further guided by recommendations from the Read-
ing the Reader [46] research project and by its ease of implementation, as ready-to-use
scripts for the algorithm were already available (also preferred by [47]).
Implementation in This Study
In this study, the I2MC algorithm was integrated into the data analysis pipeline as a post-
processing step, following gaze data collection through the Typexperiment platform. Each
paragraph in the experiment was isolated and time-bounded, eliminating the need for
additional temporal alignment between gaze and task data.
Raw gaze data was first exported from the backend, where a custom validation routine
was used to sanitize the data. This included flagging any samples containing invalid
numeric entries, such as NaN or inf, ensuring only usable data were passed forward for
fixation analysis.
The cleaned data was then processed using the I2MC algorithm implemented in Python.
Gaze positions over time were transformed into velocity profiles, and a two-means cluster-
ing model (k = 2) was applied to separate low-velocity (fixation) periods from high-velocity
(saccade or noise) intervals. The algorithm also interpolated short signal gaps and auto-
matically selected the most reliable eye for analysis based on sample validity.

Selection of Eye-tracking and Behavioral Metrics
To evaluate the impact of typographic variations on reading behavior, several eye-tracking
and behavioral metrics were computed. These indicators offer insights into visual atten-
tion, cognitive effort, and reading fluency across different font types, sizes, and letter
spacings.
• Normalized Fixation Duration: This metric represents the average duration of fix-
ations, normalized by passage length and reading time, to enable fair comparisons
across participants and text lengths. Longer durations often suggest higher visual
or cognitive load.
• Fixation Rate (fixations per second): Describes the number of fixations made per
second. A high fixation rate may indicate inefficient reading, often associated with
dense or complex typography, while a lower rate suggests more fluent processing.
• Mean Fixation Duration: Represents the unnormalized average duration (in mil-
liseconds) of each fixation during a paragraph. This value helps assess how long
the eye remains still on words and is often influenced by text difficulty and layout.
• Fixation Count: The total number of fixations during a test paragraph reading ex-
periment. A high count may imply difficulty navigating the text, whereas a lower
count typically suggests smoother reading flow.
• Mean Saccade Amplitude: The average distance (in degrees or pixels) between
consecutive fixations. Longer saccade amplitudes are typically associated with flu-
ent and confident reading of familiar or easily legible text, whereas shorter saccades
often indicate reading difficulty, increased cognitive load, or visual clutter [43].
• Regression Count: Counts the number of times the gaze moved backwards within
the paragraph. This often reflects the reader’s need to reprocess previously seen
information due to confusion or poor layout.
• Regression Ratio: The ratio of regressions to total saccades. A higher ratio in-
dicates more frequent rereading, which may occur under challenging typographic
settings or cognitive overload.
• Mean Pupil Diameter (mm): The average size of the left and right pupils through-
out the reading trial. Since pupil dilation is a physiological correlate of cognitive
workload, this measure was used to infer mental effort across conditions.

While descriptive visualizations such as boxplots provide useful insights into the distribu-

T-Test
Independent sample t-tests were performed to compare pairs of typographic conditions
and determine whether specific factors (e.g., font type, font size, and letter spacing) signif-
icantly influenced reading performance. These tests were chosen because they allow for
direct pairwise comparisons, making it possible to assess how individual typographic pa-
rameters affect eye-tracking metrics such as fixation rate, regression ratio, reading speed,
and skip rate.
• t-statistic: Indicates how large the observed difference between two group means
is relative to the within-group variability. Larger absolute t-values suggest stronger
evidence that the difference is real and not due to random chance.
• p-value: Represents the probability of observing a difference as large (or larger)
than the one detected if the null hypothesis (no difference) were true. A p-value
less than 0.05 is considered statistically significant.

metrics differed significantly across all eight typographic configurations (T1–T8). Unlike
t-tests that compare only two groups, ANOVA can simultaneously compare the means of
multiple groups while controlling for Type I error, making it the ideal choice for analyzing
the effects of multiple typographic settings.
• F-statistic: This statistic reflects the ratio of between-group variance to within-group
variance. A higher F-value suggests that the differences between conditions are
greater than would be expected by chance.
• p-value: A p-value below 0.05 indicates that at least one typographic condition
differs significantly from the others.
In this study, one-way ANOVA was performed on metrics such as fixation count, regres-
sion ratio, reading duration, and skip rate. The analysis revealed that skip rate was
the only metric that showed a statistically significant difference across all configurations
(p < 0.05), while other metrics, including fixation and regression measures, did not show
significant effects.
Post-hoc Comparisons: To identify which specific configurations differed in skip rate, a
Tukey’s Honest Significant Difference (HSD) test was conducted. However, no pairwise
comparisons were statistically significant after correction (padj > 0.05), suggesting that
while overall differences existed, no single configuration dominated.
Interpretation: ANOVA results indicate that typography primarily influences word-skipping
behavior rather than fixation or regression metrics. Configurations T7 (Verdana, 18px,
0.10em) and T8 (Verdana, 18px, 0.28em) emerged as the most balanced and preferred
conditions, aligning with subjective ratings.

5.1
Preference Metrics Across All Participants
The ANOVA results indicated that skip rate was the only metric significantly influenced
by typographic configuration (p < 0.05), whereas other metrics, such as fixation count, re-
gression count, and reading duration, did not exhibit significant differences across T1–T8
(see Table 4.1). This outcome suggests that typography primarily influenced how fre-
quently readers skipped words rather than altering overall fixation or regression behavior.
This finding is particularly relevant because skip rate can be sensitive to text legibility,
spacing, and font choice, which directly impact eye movement patterns.
A Tukey HSD post-hoc test further explored pairwise differences in skip rate but found
no significant pairwise comparisons between specific configurations (adjusted p > 0.05).
This indicates that while a global effect was present, the skip rate variations were relatively
distributed across conditions, with no single configuration standing out. This could be due
to the moderate differences in skip rate across the eight conditions, combined with the
conservative nature of the Tukey correction for multiple comparisons.
Skip Rate Interpretation
Skip rate reflects the proportion of words skipped during reading. While a lower skip rate
often implies careful reading, a higher skip rate can indicate either fluent reading (due
to predictable words) or superficial scanning. Thus, skip rate must be interpreted in the
context of other metrics, such as reading speed (WPM), regressions, and comprehension.
In the context of this study:
• T6 recorded the highest skip rate (0.739), which, combined with its high WPM
(130.10), suggests fast and possibly fluent reading.
• T7 had the lowest skip rate (0.692), indicating fewer skipped words and a more
deliberate reading style.
• T8, the baseline, showed a moderate skip rate (0.697), aligning with its second-
highest WPM (128.72), suggesting an efficient balance between speed and atten-
tion.
Interestingly, T7 and T8 were also subjectively preferred by participants. This indicates
that skip rate alone cannot determine readability; its interpretation depends on comple-
mentary metrics like WPM, fixation duration, and comprehension accuracy. It is possible
that the slightly higher skip rate observed for T8 was not detrimental but rather a sign of
more efficient text processing due to optimal font size and spacing.
Regression Ratio Insights
Regression ratio analysis revealed that:
• T7 had the lowest regression ratio (0.1732), reflecting smoother forward reading
with fewer backward eye movements, which is often associated with easier text
processing.
• T6 had the highest regression ratio (0.2115), which may indicate that despite its
high reading speed, readers needed to re-read certain portions for comprehension.
• T8 (0.1886) performed moderately, combining low regression ratio with high WPM,
reinforcing its efficiency.

These findings highlight that T7 and T8 not only scored well in subjective preference
but also demonstrated favorable regression behavior, suggesting strong readability. In

Unequal Sample Size Limitation
A critical limitation of this study is the unequal group sizes:
• T8 was read by all 23 participants, while other configurations (T1–T7) were read by

• Larger samples (like T8) produce more stable means and narrower confidence in-

Although ANOVA is robust to moderate sample imbalances, Tukey HSD is more conser-

be interpreted cautiously. A repeated-measures design, where each participant reads all
typographic configurations, could have mitigated this limitation.
Implications and Future Work
The results suggest that typography influences skip rate and regression patterns, with
T7 and T8 emerging as strong candidates for supporting smoother and efficient read-
ing. However, skip rate is not a standalone indicator of readability; it must be evaluated
alongside speed (WPM), fixation patterns, and subjective preferences.
Additionally, the observed trends across WPM, regression ratio, and subjective feedback
suggest that optimal typography might involve a trade-off between speed and visual com-
fort. For instance, T6 was fast but induced more regressions, whereas T7 was slower but
required fewer corrections.
Future research should adopt a balanced repeated-measures design, ensuring equal
sample sizes per configuration, and should incorporate comprehension tests to validate
whether faster reading (high WPM and skip rate) corresponds to effective understanding.
This would also provide stronger evidence for which typographic settings are optimal,
particularly for readers with difficulties. Including qualitative feedback on reading comfort
and fatigue would further strengthen the interpretation of eye-tracking metrics about user
experience.

spacing (0.10 em) combined with a clear, legible font (Verdana) was found to support
both faster and more comfortable reading. These results suggest that readability benefits
arise not from a single parameter but from the optimal combination of font type, size, and
spacing, aligning with the hypothesis (H2) that typography effectiveness is context- and
combination-dependent.
5.5
Limitations
The main limitations of this study are: (i) typographic variables might interact with each
other (as mentioned by [26]), (ii) limited representation of participants with reading difficul-
ties, (iii) environmental constraints of the lab setup, (iv) potential influence of text difficulty
and content, and (vi) limited generalizability of the findings.
First, typographic variables might interact with each other. Typographic parameters often
influence reading behavior in complex and interdependent ways. Some studies have re-
ported significant interactions between typeface and font size [49], [50], while others did
not find such effects [51]. For example, [50] demonstrated that typeface and font size are
interdependent. In their experiment with 35 participants, two typefaces: Arial and Times
were tested at 10 and 12 points, measuring reading time, preference, and reading errors.
The results showed that 10-point Arial was read more slowly, while 12-point Arial was
preferred over the other configurations. Font size can also cause varying letter shapes
and proportions across typefaces, which may partly account for these observed effects.
In contrast, [51] used eye-tracking to examine font size and type during online reading
with 82 participants. They reported that smaller font sizes (e.g., 10 points) produced sig-
nificantly longer fixation durations compared to larger fonts (14 points), but no significant
differences were observed between serif and sans-serif fonts. Moreover, [49] found that
long line lengths, very small type sizes, and combinations of these factors slowed reading
speed, whereas [52] reported that the thickest line widths improved reading speeds.
While prior studies such as [27] and [26] reported benefits of increased inter-letter spac-
ing, our results suggest that tighter spacing (0.10 em) combined with appropriate font and
size can be equally or more effective, particularly when measured alongside subjective
preference. Building on these observations, the current study with 23 participants (includ-
ing 3 with reading difficulties) found that skip rate was the only metric significantly affected
by typographic configuration across T1–T8 (p < 0.05), whereas other metrics such as fix-
ation count, regression count, and total reading duration remained statistically unaffected.
This suggests that typography primarily influences word-skipping behavior rather than fix-
ation or regression patterns. Notably, configurations T7 (Verdana, 18px, 0.10em) and T8
(Verdana, 18px, 0.28em) were both subjectively preferred by participants and showed
favorable skip rate and regression ratios, indicating a balance between fluency and care-
ful reading. These results partially align with earlier studies emphasizing the importance

of font size but highlight that optimal readability depends on the interaction of multiple
factors—font type, size, and spacing—rather than any single parameter.
Second, the representation of participants with reading difficulties was limited. Out of 23
participants, only 3 reported diagnosed reading difficulties (e.g., dyslexia). This small sub-
group size limits the ability to generalize findings to readers with disabilities and reduces
the statistical power to identify clear typographic effects for this population.
Third, environmental constraints might have influenced the results. The experiment was
conducted in a controlled lab environment using a fixed Tobii eye-tracker setup. Although
this ensured consistent and accurate data collection, it may not fully reflect natural reading
conditions where variables such as device type, ambient lighting, and posture differ.
Fourth, text content and difficulty could have impacted results. Although all paragraphs
were standardized to same length, some passages might have been perceived as more
difficult or less engaging due to differences in vocabulary, sentence structure, or topic fa-
miliarity. These variations could influence reading performance metrics, including fixation
counts and regressions, independent of typographic variables.
Finally, the generalizability of the findings is limited. This study focused on specific typo-
graphic parameters two font types (Arial and Verdana), two font sizes (18px and 24px),
and two letter spacings (0.10em and 0.28em). Therefore, the findings may not be di-
rectly applicable to other fonts, content types (e.g., long-form articles, mobile reading), or
different cultural and linguistic contexts.
5.6
Future Work
This study primarily analyzed first-time reading behavior across different typographic con-
figurations, but a deeper investigation into re-reading and skimming patterns could re-
veal additional insights. The eye-tracking data collected during second or third readings
can be analyzed to understand how gaze metrics such as fixation count, skip rate, and
regressions change when participants revisit a text. Preliminary observations during the
experiment suggested that many participants re-read or skimmed paragraphs when given
the option to go back. This behavior could be systematically analyzed to understand how
typography affects scanning versus careful reading, and whether certain configurations
encourage better information retrieval during re-reading.
Another promising direction is the independent analysis of typographic parameters.
While this study used combined font, size, and spacing configurations (T1–T8), future
studies could isolate each parameter (e.g., varying only letter spacing while keeping font
and size constant) to better identify their individual contributions to readability. Extending
the font set beyond sans-serif fonts such as Arial and Verdana could also reveal whether
visual differences between font families (serif, monospaced, dyslexia-oriented fonts) pro-
duce stronger effects than subtle variations within similar sans-serif fonts.
Future research could also examine reading comprehension outcomes in more depth.
While comprehension was measured through multiple-choice questions in this study, the
data were not analyzed extensively in relation to eye-tracking behavior. Analyzing how
typography affects comprehension accuracy especially when readers revisit text could
provide a clearer link between visual processing and cognitive understanding.
Additionally, this research can be expanded by including a larger and more diverse
participant pool, particularly individuals with diagnosed reading difficulties (e.g., dyslexia
or ADHD). The small sample size of such participants in this study (n=3) limits the ability to

Other potential areas include:

in real-time based on user eye movements or detected fatigue.
• Qualitative feedback integration: Collecting in-depth interviews or think-aloud

6
Conclusion
This thesis examined how font type, font size, and letter spacing influence digital reading
behavior using eye-tracking metrics and subjective user preferences. Through a con-
trolled experiment with 23 participants (including 3 with reading difficulties) across eight
typographic configurations (T1–T8), the study identified key factors affecting reading flu-
ency and visual comfort.
The findings show that typography significantly impacts word-skipping behavior, with skip
rate being the only metric affected at a statistically significant level (p < 0.05). Con-
figurations T7 (Verdana, 18px, 0.10em) and T8 (Verdana, 18px, 0.28em) were both pre-
ferred by participants and exhibited favorable skip rate and regression patterns, suggest-
ing a balance between smooth reading and focused processing. While previous studies
emphasized larger font sizes and increased letter spacing, this study demonstrates that
tighter spacing (0.10em), combined with an accessible font and appropriate size, can
perform equally well or better.
A key contribution of this work is the integration of objective eye-tracking data with sub-
jective preferences to evaluate typographic design. The results highlight that optimal
readability depends on the interaction of multiple typographic parameters rather than
on any single factor, challenging the conventional “one-size-fits-all” design recommenda-
tions.
Although the study’s scope was limited by the small subgroup of readers with difficulties
and the controlled lab environment, the findings provide valuable insights for designing
accessible digital text. By focusing on a user-centered approach to typography, this re-
search offers evidence-based guidelines that can enhance both reading performance and
user experience.

Glossary of Terms
• Dyslexia: A neurodevelopmental learning difference characterized by difficulties
with accurate or fluent word recognition, poor spelling, and decoding abilities, de-
spite normal intelligence and educational opportunity. Dyslexia reflects differences
in the way the brain processes written language, not a problem of the eyes them-
selves.
• Refractive Errors):
1. Myopia (Nearsightedness): Clear close vision but blurred distance vision due
to the eye’s length, causing light to focus in front of the retina.
2. Hyperopia (Farsightedness): Clear distance vision but blurred near vision
because light focuses behind the retina.
3. Astigmatism:Irregular curvature of the cornea or lens causes distorted or blurred
vision at all distances.
• Presbyopia: Age-related loss of near focusing ability due to decreased elasticity of
the eye’s lens, typically becoming noticeable in the early to mid-40s.
• Color Vision Deficiency (“Color Blindness”): A reduced ability to distinguish be-
tween certain colors, most commonly red and green; caused by absent or malfunc-
tioning cone photoreceptors in the retina.
• Central Vision Loss: Loss or reduction of vision in the central visual field, usually
due to macular disease (e.g., age-related macular degeneration, diabetic macular
edema), leading to difficulties with reading, face recognition, and fine-detail tasks.
• Amblyopia (“Lazy Eye”): Reduced visual acuity in one eye that is not correctable
by glasses or contact lenses and is not due to any eye disease. It arises when the
brain favors input from the stronger eye, leading to underdevelopment of the weaker
eye’s visual pathways.
• Strabismus (“Crossed Eyes”): A misalignment of the eyes whereby one eye turns
in, out, up, or down relative to the other. If untreated in childhood, it can lead to
amblyopia.
