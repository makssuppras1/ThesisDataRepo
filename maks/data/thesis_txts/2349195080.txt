Abstract 
 
Vibration Damping of a High Pressure Washer Lance 
By Rihab el-Wali 
 
In cooperation with the Danish company Nilfisk A/S, this project studies various effects of 
vibration control methods on a high pressure washer lance. Different models are analyzed and then 
compared to find the most efficient one. 
Four passive vibration control methods are discussed with an emphasis on the three that are most 
relevant for Nilfisk: localized additions, added damping and vibration isolation. 
Starting with an initial vibration absorber that Nilfisk A/S had already designed, which falls under 
the category of localized additions, the reduction in the lance vibrations are almost unnoticeable. As 
such, and in an attempt to improve the absorber‟s design, it is modified by increasing its mass. After 
testing the new model, some improvement is observed, but not satisfactory enough to be considered 
a final solution. 
Moving on from the absorber model, two main methods are studied further. The first is added 
damping with a viscoelastic material, and the second is vibration isolation with anti-vibration 
gloves.   
Testing the viscoelastic material leads to decent results with room for improvement in the technique 
in which the viscoelastic layer is applied to the lance. 
After performing the measurements with a viscoelastic material, it is noticed that having a 
separating material between the hand and the gun reduces the severity of vibration exposure on the 
hand. Accordingly, it is deduced that so-called “anti-vibration gloves” could provide a similar 
effect. Since the vibration transmissibility characteristics of the gloves vary with changes in the grip 
force and excitation level, one cannot rely on them to provide sufficient and consistent protection. 
All the methods seem to have at least some effect; but the most promising one is the method of 
viscoelastic damping. Even though the results are not exceptionally good, they are still the best ones 
obtained among the other vibration methods tested; and it is predicted that with some enhancement 
in the application of the viscoelastic layer, better results will follow.

1 Introduction 
 
The Danish company Nilfisk A/S manufactures High Pressure Washers (HPWs) among other types 
of cleaning machines. An HPW uses a high speed water jet to remove dirt from surfaces. The main 
components of an HPW are shown in Figure 1.1. The HPW and its parts are more thoroughly 
described in section 2.3. 
In order to avoid a very concentrated water jet, many HPWs use a rotary nozzle, where the water jet 
is ejected from a small circular nozzle element that rotates on a conical surface inside the nozzle‟s 
housing. In Section 2.3.2, the nozzle and its mechanism are more elaborately explained. 
Currently, the time varying force of the rotary jet causes the gun (specially the lance and nozzle) to 
vibrate strongly. Since the gun is held by an operator, its vibrations need to be controlled for health 
and comfort reasons. 
 
1.1. 
Motivation 
Long exposure periods using hand-held vibrating equipment has been linked to diseases affecting 
blood vessels, limb articulation and blood circulation [1]. According to the safety standard for 
HPWs (EN 60335-2-79, Annex DD, ISO 20643), the vibrating machine shall be designed and 
constructed in such a way that risks resulting from vibrations produced by the machine are reduced 
to a very low level. In addition, the handles shall be designed and constructed in such a way as to 
reduce vibrations transmitted to the upper limbs of the operator.

1.1.1. General Considerations 
According to the International Standard ISO 5349-1, „Mechanical vibrations – Measurement and 
evaluation of human exposure to hand-transmitted vibrations – part 1‟, when operating a vibrating 
machine, one needs to take into account the following factors which are known to influence the 
effects of human exposure to hand-transmitted vibrations. 
 The vibration‟s frequency spectrum  
 The vibration‟s magnitude 
 The exposure duration  per working day 
 The cumulative exposure to date 
 
1.1.2. Vibration-Induced White Finger 
Vibration-induced white finger is triggered by continuous use of vibrating hand-held machinery. It 
is a disorder that affects the blood vessels, nerves, muscles, and joints of the hand, wrist and arm 
[2]. According to the International Standard ISO 5349-1, Annex C, the RMS values of the daily 
vibration exposure       (in an 8-h energy-equivalent vibration total value at a surface in contact

1.2. 
Current Knowledge 
In his recent state-of-the-art review, Saeed [3], introduces a general literature review for all types of 
vibrations control systems that have appeared so far. These systems can be classified into four main 
groups: passive, semi-active, active and hybrid – depending on their operational mechanisms. He 
briefly describes each of these main groups and their subgroups, with their corresponding 
advantages and disadvantages.  
As for passive vibration control, which is this work‟s focus, there are four basic approaches [4]: 
 Vibration control by structural design 
 Vibration control by localized additions 
 Vibration control by added damping 
 Vibration control by resilient isolation 
In more recent years, passive vibration control methods have become increasingly popular due to 
their versatility. New methods have thus been proposed for a number of industries such as the wind 
industry where there have been many proposals for dampening vibrations on wind turbine blades; 
some examples are to dampen the vibrations by means of a circular liquid damper (CLCD) [5] or a 
tuned liquid column damper (TLCD) [6] among many others discussed in a review about 
==PAGE:19==

performance enhancement of wind turbine systems using passive, active and semi-active vibration 
control [7]. Aerospace is another industry in which passive vibration control has become more 
prominent by using methods such as the application of a particle damper on an electronic package 
of a spacecraft [8]. 
Prior to this project, Nilfisk A/S had chosen the approach of „vibration control by localized 
additions‟ as a starting point since it is the most practical and inexpensive one out of the four 
passive vibration control methods. Therefore, the company designed a single degree-of-freedom 
(SDOF) vibration absorber and performed vibration measurements on it and on the gun, but the 
results were inconclusive. A more detailed description and analysis of these measurements is 
presented in Section 2.4. 
Nilfisk A/S has therefore started this project aiming to ultimately reduce the vibrations of the hand-
held gun of the HPW. 
  
1.3. 
Problem, Objective and Plan 
This work investigates the implementation of different vibration control methods developed to 
reduce the vibrations on the lance caused by the nozzle. 
The main focus of this report will be: 
 Performing new measurements on the initial vibration absorber (previously designed) 
 Improving the initial vibration absorber and testing the new improved model 
 Looking into alternative options for vibration control and testing them 
 Using theoretical work to test the effectiveness of some vibration control methods

2.1. The Company 
Nilfisk A/S is one of the world‟s leading manufacturers of professional cleaning equipment. Their 
headquarters is based in Denmark with production facilities located across Asia, Europe and 
America.  
The company was founded in 1906 in a backyard workshop in Copenhagen by the school teacher 
and motor enthusiast Peder Andersen Fisker. P. A. Fisker had a strong desire to found a global 
company with a high technological potential, which was the driving force behind him founding his 
company 110 years ago [9]. 
 
2.2. Machine Vibrations 
Since man started building machines for industrial applications, and especially since motors were 
put in use to power them, problems of vibration reduction and isolation have engaged engineers. As 
vibration isolation and reduction have become an integral part of machine design, interest in 
measurement techniques and analysis of mechanical vibrations has grown. 
Vibrations occur for several reasons. They are normally due to dynamic effects of manufacturing 
tolerances, clearances, rolling and rubbing contact between machine parts and out-of-balance forces 
in rotating members. Often, small insignificant vibrations can excite the resonant frequencies of 
some other structural parts which are then amplified into major vibration and noise sources [10]. 
 
2.3. The High Pressure Washer 
An HPW is a type of cleaning equipment used to remove mold, grime, dust, mud, and dirt from 
surfaces and objects such as buildings, vehicles and concrete surfaces [11]. 
The HPW System is sketched in Figure 2.1, and it consists of four main parts: 
1. The low-pressure hose: This hose connects the water supply to the pump unit. It is a typical 
plastic hose similar to the one used for gardening. 
==PAGE:22==
6     The High Pressure Washer 
 
2. The high-pressure hose: This hose connects the pump-unit to the gun. It is made of plastic or 
rubber and reinforced by woven glass or steel fibers. 
3. The pump unit: The pump unit contains a high pressure piston pump (100-200 bar), which 
pumps water out of the lance through a nozzle with a small orifice, an electric motor with a fan 
for cooling and other electric devices. The interior components of the pump unit are shielded by 
surrounding plastic shells. 
4. The gun: The gun is held by the operator to guide the water jet and has a trigger mechanism for 
activating the pump unit. The gun consists of a grip, a lance and a nozzle. The aim is to get a 
high velocity and abrasive water jet for cleaning purposes. 
This report mainly focuses on the different elements of the gun with a special emphasis on the lance 
and the nozzle.  This is elaborated on in the following two subsections. 
 
2.3.1. The Gun 
As mentioned before, the gun is composed of 3 parts: a grip, a lance and a nozzle as shown in 
Figure 2.2 Since all parts are connected to each other, the vibrations are transmitted through the 
lance to the grip when the nozzle vibrates. That is why a nozzle that vibrates excessively causes a 
bad user experience and health issues. Note that throughout this project only one nozzle is used and 
that is the previously mentioned „rotary nozzle‟, since said nozzle is the one causing the biggest 
vibrational problems. The reason Nilfisk would like to focus on that nozzle specifically is that it is 
the newest one with the most effective cleaning performance.

2.3.2. The Nozzle 
The nozzle is inserted into the lance through a “bayonet mount” which is a fastening mechanism in 
which the nozzle is grasped firmly by the user, inserted into the lance and turned 45° anticlockwise 
in order to lock the two parts together. In order to avoid water leakage, the lance and nozzle are 
sealed together by a rubber O-ring. 
The water jet shape and intensity as well as the amount of water coming out vary depending on the 
design of the nozzle. Therefore, the water jet is expected to be fixed when the nozzle design is 
fixed. 
Figure 2.3 shows the nozzle and its dimensions. It is inserted into the end of the lance through its 
left-most part. 
Figure 2.4 illustrates the inner parts of the nozzle. The water enters the nozzle through the lance; 
once water is inside the nozzle, it goes through a stationary disk that has two holes tangent to the 
housing‟s inner wall; the high water velocity coming out of the two holes results in a rotating 
stream. The rotating current then pulls the loose nozzle element which causes it to rotate as well. 
Finally, water exits the nozzle through its tip.

3 Initial Vibration Absorber 
 
The first absorber model is identical to that used in the experiments that Nilfisk had performed 
before the start of this project, it can be seen in Figures 3.1(a)-(b). The reason behind using the 
same model as the first one is to redo measurements on it since, previously, they were insufficient 
and rather inconclusive. 
The software used is PULSE by B&K. It applies a Fourier transform on the input (force measured 
by a force transducer) and on the output (acceleration measured by an accelerometer); the 
acceleration is then divided by the force resulting in a frequency response function.  
 
3.1. Measurements I: Properties of the Absorber 
In order to find some of the absorber‟s properties, the  frequency response function is obtained in 
the frequency range   ,     Hz by using a shaker (B&K type 4810),  which is connected to an 
accelerometer (B&K type 4371) and a force transducer (B&K type 8230), both of which are 
calibrated prior to starting the measurements. The shaker is connected to an amplifier through 
which one can control the shaker‟s vibrations. The signal transmitted through the amplifier is 
filtered random noise, which, in principle, contains all frequencies at the same time in the frequency 
range 50 to 2000 Hz. However, for the purpose of having a better signal to noise ratio and thus 
having a higher coherence between force and acceleration, the signal was filtered to enhance certain 
frequency ranges. 
The experiments are divided into two sets. The first is only with the nozzle and the second is with 
the gun (grip + lance + nozzle). The experimental setups along with the results are shared in the two 
following subsections.

3.2.4. Conclusions 
Objectively, as a user of the HPW without and then with the absorber, I would say that the 
difference in vibrations is barely noticeable and that the gun is very hard to hold on to due to the 
excessive vibrations. Therefore, the vibration reduction from using the current model of the 
absorber is not significant enough to give the user a notably more comfortable experience. 
Another important observation is that there does not need to be so many variations in the 
measurements. For example, the variation in the positioning of the accelerometer is not necessary (it 
was expected that the accelerometer close to the hand would give smaller amplitudes than the one 
close to the nozzle). Another variation that has given almost the same results is the variation 
between large male and smaller female. Therefore, for any upcoming measurements, the 
accelerometer will only be placed in one position in the middle of the lance and only one person 
will be performing the measurements. 
 
3.3. Summary and Conclusions 
Finally, after studying the effect of the initial vibration absorber on the vibrations of the lance a 
better idea is formed leading to the next step of improving its design.

4.7. Other Options 
An absorber does not necessarily need to be shaped like a steel cylinder with a foam lining on the 
inside (as demonstrated in this project); there are other ways to model a vibration absorber. 
For example, the steel cylinder can be substituted with another mass that has a different shape (such 
as a beam) or even made of a different kind of material. Moreover, even if the concept of a steel 
cylinder is not changed, its design can be altered by making it shorter, longer, thinner, thicker, etc. 
The foam lining can also be substituted with a damping material other than polyethylene. It is also 
possible to only partially line the damping material on the mass. One may also try to 
increase/decrease the thicknesses of the foam rather than sticking to the 1 cm thickness adapted in 
this project. 
In addition to the option of changing the design on the absorber itself, there are numerous more 
alternatives and combinations as to where to place it (close to the grip of the gun, in the middle of 
the lance, on the nozzle, etc.).

5 Alternative Vibration Control Methods 
 
After performing numerous measurements on different sizes and positions of the absorber model, 
which did not lead to satisfying results, this chapter discusses other methods for passive vibration 
control which might be more efficient as well as the specific methods chosen for this project. It also 
studies the different design considerations when designing a model for vibration control (such as 
material, position…). 
 
5.1. Passive Vibration Control Methods  
As mentioned in Section 1.2, there are four forms of passive vibration control: 
1.  Structural Design: 
The method of controlling vibrations through structural design is often incorporated into the 
starting phases of a design when a structure is expected to experience high vibration levels. In 
this case, the decision to reduce vibration levels came after the machine was designed; it is 
therefore unpractical and expensive to use this method. 
This form of vibration control is often applied by controlling the stiffness and/or mass 
characteristics of the structure. This could be done, for example, by choosing the right materials 
or by optimizing the structural geometry. 
 
2. Localized Additions: 
This method is one of the most economic and effective ways in reducing vibration levels in 
some situations. It is characterized by adding an extra system at a particular point on the 
existing structure. The two most popular examples of localized additions are: 
  Vibration absorber: A vibration absorber is a system similar to the one Nilfisk used in 
their previous measurements and to the system discussed in Chapters 2.5 and 0. It normally 
consists of a mass, a spring and a damper. 
 
  Tuned mass damper (TMD): A TMD is a system (consisting of a mass, a spring and a 
damper) that is attached to a structure in order to reduce its dynamic response. The 
frequency of the damper is tuned to the resonant frequency of the structure so that when 
that frequency is excited, the damper resonates out of phase with the structural motion. 
Since the purpose of this project is to control the vibrations of more than one HPW model 
and since the HPW models do not necessarily share the same resonant frequency (since 
each one has a different water speed and pressure), it is impractical and inefficient to design 
a TMD since a TMD works only on one frequency. Another issue with using a TMD is that 
even within the same HPW model, neither the frequency of the gun nor that of the 
excitation (the water jet) is constant. That is due to the many contributing variables such as 
==PAGE:64==
48     Passive Vibration Control Methods 
 
the user‟s grip on the gun, how much the gun is being moved rotated, the surfaces that the 
water jet is pointed at, the distance between the surface and the gun, etc.   
Other forms of a tuned damper are a tuned liquid damper (TLD) and a tuned liquid column 
damper (TLCD) [20]. 
 
3. Added Damping: 
If one cannot avoid resonance by design, then one of the options to control vibrations is to add 
some type of damping. Damping in general is defined as any means of dissipating energy which 
is added to the system at resonance. 
Passive damping may be introduced in many ways, two of the most popular ones are: 
 
  Viscous damping: Viscous dampers aid in converting mechanical vibrational energy of a 
body into heat energy, a piston is often attached to the vibrating body and is arranged to 
move through liquid or air in a cylinder or bellows that is attached to a support. 
Viscous damping is not studied any further in this project since its design is not practical for 
an HPW gun; there are other methods that are cheaper to design and implement. 
 
  Viscoelastic damping: Viscoelasticity is the property of materials that exhibit both 
viscous and elastic characteristics when undergoing deformation. Synthetic polymers, wood, 
and human tissue – as well as metals at high temperatures – display significant viscoelastic 
effects. 
More on viscoelasticity and the material chosen for testing in this project is discussed in 
Section 5.2. 
 
4.  Vibration isolation: 
A vibration isolation problem is often described with two structures: a source structure, which is 
the source of vibrations (such as an earthquake or a washing machine), and a receiver structure, 
which gets affected by excessive vibrations (such as a building affected by an earthquake or a 
floor affected by a washing machine). The vibration isolation is a substructure incorporated 
between the two structures; the objective of vibration isolation is to reduce the vibrations in 
some specific portion of the receiver structure. 
Vibration isolation can be realized in many different ways; it therefore falls upon the designer to 
arrive at an isolation system design well-suited to the specific situation. 
More on vibration isolation for the purpose of this project can be found in Section 0.

5.2.2. Constrained-Layer Damping 
The technique of constrained-layer damping is illustrated in Figure 5.2 where a viscoelastic layer is 
sandwiched between a beam and a stiff layer. 
In [22], the effectiveness of constraining a viscoelastic layer on damping the vibrations of a 
clamped-free beam is examined. Measurements are therefore performed with a free and with a 
constrained viscoelastic layer (were the layer is bonded uniformly on the beam). After performing 
experiments showing the loss factors corresponding to the first four natural frequencies in a free vs. 
constrained viscoelastic layer of 0.5 and 1 mm thickness, it is observed that the beam with a 
constrained viscoelastic layer provides higher loss factors than that with a free layer. 
 
5.2.3. Ross, Kerwin, and Ungar Damping Model 
Ross, Kerwin, and Ungar (RKU) developed one of the earliest damping models for three-layered 
sandwich beams based on damping of flexural waves by a constrained viscoelastic layer. The 
system in Figure 5.3 is one to which the RKU equations could be applied under numerous 
assumptions [23]. 
The RKU model can be employed for several purposes such as analyzing and improving the design 
parameters for constrained layer damping structures [24]. It has also been used as a basis to check 
the accuracy of other models. 
The RKU theory, however, does not fit to a partially covered beam. So, in an attempt to find the 
effects of a constrained viscoelastic layer that is only partially covering the beam, finite element 
modeling shows that – with the exception of the first mode - the loss factor is smaller than the 
corresponding value on the fully covered beam [25].

5.2.8. Conclusion 
An important remark to make on the measurements is that they are only preliminary. For any future 
measurements, it is recommended that the viscoelastic layer be constrained with material that is 
stiffer than tape (as suggested in Section 5.2.2 on the technique of constrained layer damping) and 
to bond the layer more uniformly along the part of the lance to which it is applied. Tape in these 
measurements, is only used due to the limited timeframe to purchase something more suitable. 
After a comparison between the vibration spectrums of Cases 1 and 3, it is concluded that adding 
more materials does not necessarily lead to better results. Moreover, in contrast to the other 
methods mentioned in in Chapter 0, it is established that using the viscoelastic material discussed in 
this chapter has given the best results so far.   
Using Viscoelastic materials, however, can be pricey. Not only is the material itself expensive, but 
it would also be very costly to implement this vibration method since - in the final design - it will 
not be applied on the outer surface of the lance (as shown in the experimental setup in Section 
5.2.6) but rather underneath the shell, which will require resizing the whole gun. 
Moreover, from Case 2, it is realized that having a material which separates the hand from the lance 
gives a much more comfortable user experience. Therefore, should Nilfisk forgo the method of 
damping by viscoelastic materials (due to their high cost), they could use yet another common 
method for vibration control, namely vibration isolation discussed in Section 0.

5.3.2. Vibration-Reducing Gloves 
After performing the measurements with a viscoelastic material around the lance in Section 5.2, it is 
noticed by the user (with the second case in Figure 5.6) that having a material between the hand and 
the lance reduces the severity of vibration exposure on the hand. Accordingly, it is deduced that 
certain kinds of gloves could provide a similar effect. 
Vibration-reducing gloves (also called anti-vibration (AV) gloves) are a common way to reduce the 
transmission of vibrations of a hand-held machine since they provide an isolation system between 
the hand and the machine. Some factors that influence the potential efficacy of an AV glove include 
the frequency range of the vibration for which protection is sought, how much hand contact force or 
pressure is applied and the physical limitations due to the glove‟s material and construction [29]. 
ISO 10819 (2013) defines a method for testing and evaluating the effectiveness of AV gloves, the 
results from performing these tests at the palm of the hand and the fingers show that AV gloves are 
unreliable as devices for controlling hand-transmitted vibration exposure [30]. 
In another study evaluating the vibration isolation performance of the commercially available AV 
hand gloves, the vibration transmission characteristics of the human hand-arm system are 
investigated through measurement of vibration transmitted to the fingers, knuckle and wrist in the 
10-500 Hz frequency range, under different magnitudes of grip force and vibration excitations [31]. 
The results show that the measured vibration transmissibility characteristics of the hand revealed 
that only 1% of the vibration is transmitted to the wrist at high frequencies (500 Hz), and vibration 
transmitted to the hand increased as the grip force increased. From the measurements performed on 
nine gloves it is concluded that the gloves do not attenuate hand-transmitted vibration in the entire 
frequency range of interest. Some gloves, however, yield vibration attenuation in certain frequency 
bands. 
Since the vibration transmissibility characteristics of the gloves vary with the variations in the grip 
force and excitation level, one cannot rely on them to provide sufficient and consistent protection 
for the purpose of this project, seeing as the grip force varies from person to person and the 
excitation level varies among the different HPW models. 
On the other hand, even though AV gloves (for which an example can be seen in Figure 5.8) are 
thought to be less effective than other available means of vibration control, one can use them as a 
temporary alternative when exposed to machines whose vibrations have not been properly 
mitigated.

6 Conclusions 
 
This project revolves around finding and implementing different vibration control methods in order 
to reduce vibrations of a high pressure washer lance, which are caused by rotary jet going through 
the nozzle. 
Before initiating new experiments, the company‟s previous work is investigated in order to 
potentially build on it for future findings. The company had earlier used an absorber consisting of a 
hollow steel cylinder with a piece of foam lined on the inside. The measurements done by the 
company, however, show a lack of consistency, and are therefore not enough to draw a solid 
conclusion. 
Therefore, the same model used previously is tested more rigorously with results showing that the 
difference in vibrations is barely noticeable and that the lance is still very hard to grasp due to the 
excessive vibrations. 
Accordingly, new ways to improve the absorber are sought until finally an increase in the mass of 
the cylinder and a change in the position of the absorber are decided upon. The corresponding 
results show that while an increase in mass helps damp the vibrations more than the initial model, it 
still does not give the desirable results, and that changing the position of the absorber to the middle 
of the lance disturbs the vibrations even more than when no modifications are made. 
After performing numerous measurements on different sizes and positions of the absorber model, 
which did not lead to satisfactory results, two other methods for passive vibration control are 
studied further. The first is added damping with a viscoelastic material, and the second is vibration 
isolation with anti-vibration gloves. 
Added damping with a viscoelastic material is obtained through constraining a layer of a 
viscoelastic urethane polymer on the lance. Comparing measurements corresponding to different 
placements of the material on the lance shows that the best results are achieved when the material is 
placed on the area of the lance that has the highest curvature. This is understandable since this is 
exactly the position where the structure has the largest strains which are partially „transferred‟ to the 
viscoelastic layer. 
Since the layer is not constrained properly, a modification constraining it more uniformly and with a 
stiffer material is expected to further improve results. 
After performing the measurements with a viscoelastic material, it is noticed that having a 
separating material between the hand and the lance is far more user friendly Accordingly, it is 
deduced that so-called “anti-vibration gloves” could provide a similar effect. Since the vibration 
transmissibility characteristics of the gloves vary with changes in the grip force and excitation level, 
one cannot rely on them to provide sufficient and consistent protection. 
All the methods seem to have at least some effect; but the most promising one is the method of 
viscoelastic damping. Even though the results are not exceptionally good, they are still the best ones 
==PAGE:76==
60     Vibration Control by Isolation 
 
obtained among the other vibration methods tested; and it is predicted that with some enhancement 
in constraining the viscoelastic layer, better results will follow. 
However, since using and adding viscoelastic materials to the design of the lance could be pricey, 
the company is still looking for alternatives that could be cheaper with the same - or even better - 
outcome. 
On the other hand, the company could fix the source of the problem instead of spending time and 
money on trying to „mask it‟. They could either change the operating speed of the  PW (in order to 
avoid coinciding with structural resonance) or change the natural frequencies of the system by 
improving the structural design (in order to avoid coinciding with the excitation frequency). 
It is important to note that with all the experimentations performed throughout this project, there is 
some uncertainty due to the small variations in some parameters that are described in the setups as 
being constant. Some examples of those parameters are the grip of the hand, the movement/rotation 
of the gun, the position of the gun (whether horizontal or tilted at a 45° angle), etc. 
Since all the measurements are only performed once, there is also some ambiguity in the accuracy 
of the results in that aspect, because results of a measurement on a single setup tend to change at 
least a little bit when it is repeated. So unless the difference between results is too big to deny the 
effect of a variable change, it is probable that the compared cases give very similar results even if 
there is some difference in the output vibration spectrum. 
Finally, there is still more room for academic and theoretical work, which could be used as an 
extension to the broad range of executed measurements; especially since working with 
measurements can encompass a lot of ambivalence. All the methods for vibration control can also 
be delved into on a deeper level, and researched further. 
 
Future Perspectives 
In order to minimize the time to find the effects of making a change to the system (such as changing 
the position or the size of the chosen method) on the vibrations, experimental work can be 
substituted with finite element modeling on the current model of the gun using frequencies in the 
troublesome range [120; 160] Hz. 
Experimental work is very demanding and one can only perform a small number of experiments a 
day as opposed to simulations, which can be performed more frequently and are much more 
convenient. After obtaining results from modeling, however, experiments can be carried out to 
check the accuracy of the simulation and vice versa to make sure that one can trust the results.
