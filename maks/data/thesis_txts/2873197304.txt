Abstract
CRI-SPA is a high-throughput screening tool that helps in screening for genetic modi-
fications. Although CRI-SPA has shown great potential for high-throughput screening
of genetic changes, its application on an industrial scale has been limited by ambiguity
surrounding the patent rights for the nuclease, Cas9. To circumvent this, CRI-SPA 2.0
was made. It replaced the Cas9 with Mad7, a non-patented nuclease. This thesis aimed
to finish the construction of the CRI-SPA 2.0 donor strain by inserting a TK marker
near a gene landing pad and a BTX pathway. The finished CRI-SPA 2.0 strain then
underwent a standard CRI-SPA screen and was compared to the previous CRI-SPA
screening results to determine if the two were comparable.
Throughout the construction process, the inserted TK marker function was con-
firmed, and it was ensured that the promoter would not be compromised or lose its
functionality, thereby preventing the cell from losing its ability to perform pseudo-
apoptosis.
For the insertion of the BTX pathway, new gRNA plasmids were constructed to
guide the mad7, both for the insertion of the BTX pathway and to provide the donor
strain with the ability to guide the mad7 in the CRI-SPA screening.
In this thesis, two different types of donor strains were created: one with high
transformation potential and one that matches the original CRI-SPA donor strain. The
one that matches the original strain was selected for screening.
The CRI-SPA screening process was unsuccessful because CRI-SPA 2.0 did not
properly insert the BTX pathway into the recipient strains. However, the few cells that
successfully completed the screening process demonstrated the potential of CRI-SPA
2.0.
As a concurrent project, the metabolic deletions of the BY4741 and BY4742 strains
were analyzed and attempted to be visualized in preparation for the restoration of the
deleted metabolic genes. Of the five deletions, four were visualized and prepared for
further analysis.
Although this thesis did not fully succeed in reaching all the milestones required to
finalize the CRI-SPA 2.0 technology, it still managed to lay a solid groundwork. This
groundwork will support the future development of CRI-SPA 2.0.

Introduction
Originally used in both baking and alcohol fermentation, Saccharomyces cerevisiae (S.
cerevisiae) is now employed in many more processes, including the fermentation of
various chemical compounds. The common use of S. cerevisiae is due to its simple
genome structure, while still sharing some of the useful abilities of more advanced
eukaryotes.
Furthermore, it also has some unique abilities of its own, such as the
ability to reproduce either sexually or asexually [1]. S. cerevisiae is one of, if not the
most studied, eukaryotes, being the first eukaryote to have its entire genome sequenced
[2]. This makes its genome well understood, and many of the pathways and sequences
have been mapped out.
This makes S. Saccharomyces easier to genetically modify,
either by inserting new pathways into the yeast organism or by knocking out (KO)
or overexpressing (OEx) specific genes.
This allows inserting pathways to produce
biologics that would otherwise be difficult to synthesize chemically and optimizing the
inserted pathway by either knocking out or overexpressing specific genes. Though S.
cerevisiae is an effective fermentation platform; it still has its upsides and downsides
compared to other fermentation platforms. S. cerevisiae has the advantage of having
resistance towards phages and can ferment at a lower pH than bacteria [3].
These
advantages make bacterial contamination of the fermentation process less likely and
therefore favorable [3]. However, the yield from S. cerevisiae fermentation is less than
that of prokaryotic fermentation organisms like Escherichia coli (E. coli) [4]. Meaning S.
cerevisiae fermentation still needs to be further optimized to compete with prokaryotic
fermentation.
CRISPR-Selective Ploidy Ablation (CRI-SPA) is an ideal tool for effective, high-
throughput genetic editing and analysis of these changes. The CRI-SPA system com-
prises two distinct tools: CRISPR and Selective Ploidy Ablation (SPA). CRISPR can
make precise cuts using a nuclease and a specific piece of DNA called a guide RNA
(gRNA) to target an area, followed by repair of the cut using a template matching the
cut area [5]. SPA can transfer a plasmid to a recipient library [6]. Combined, you can
transfer entire genes or pathways to a recipient library using nuclease-mediated transfer
[7].
1.1
Overview of the project goals
This project aims to complete the CRI-SPA 2.0 system in S. cerevisiae and have it ready
for high-throughput use. CRI-SPA 2.0 is an updated system that uses the nuclease Mad7
instead of Cas9. Once it’s constructed, it will be used in a CRI-SPA cycle that involves
introducing a gene that produces yellow pigment, and the intensity of the pigment is
then analyzed to compare it to the old version of CRI-SPA. Furthermore, as a secondary
objective, the metabolic markers in the yeast strains will be studied to visualize how
the deletions were performed, so that they can be restored in the future to resemble
their wild-type form (Figure 1).

1.2
Brief history of yeast
As mentioned in the previous section, yeast has been utilized as an industrial tool for
many years, primarily in the production of bread and the fermentation of alcoholic bev-
erages [8]. This understanding did not develop further over the next couple of thousand
years. It was in the 20th century that a deeper understanding of yeast began to emerge,
driven by the growing interest in microbiology. This led to a deeper understanding of
the structure of DNA and its functions, as well as the role of genes in collaboration with
each other. This led to the development of genetic tools, which began to see use in the
1970s. This made it possible to separate microorganisms into specific strains, allowing
for the consistent presence of the same organisms. Furthermore, it also allowed for the
manipulation of organisms like yeast, making it possible to genetically modify the yeast
to produce products they originally were unable to make. Combining these technologies
made it possible to make the first types of ”cell factories” [9].
1.3
Industrial use of yeast
Cell factories are understood as engineered microbes and plant cells that can produce
chemicals, polymers, and proteins. These are very practical as they can be fed simple
materials, such as sugar and salts, and with these, they can produce complex chemicals
that would otherwise require many more steps and materials to produce using standard
synthetic chemistry [10]. In industrial use S. cerevisiae is one of the most used platforms.
This is because, as mentioned earlier, it’s one of the most well-known biological systems
and therefore is much easier to modify. Furthermore, there are many years of already
established modified yeast strains, which make continued modification simpler and less
time-consuming. It is currently the system with the most developed tools. It is currently
the system with the most tools specifically developed for it. Due to these advantages S.
cerevisiae is often the ideal background to use for fermentation processes [9]. Although
it is possible to use cells that naturally produce a specific compound, it is not often
recommended, as these organisms are not well-suited for use in industrial settings.
Instead, it is easier to clone the pathway that produces the product of interest and insert
it into an organism that is better adapted to industrial production, like S. cerevisiae.
Although this is efficient, these pathways do not adapt perfectly to their new host
because they are two different species.
This is because the backbone has different
metabolic enzymes than the pathway donor. This can be addressed by either inserting
metabolic enzymes that match the pathway donor, or by designing a new type of enzyme
using computer simulations to optimize the enzyme design for the pathway and its
backbone [10].

Although the insertion of compatible enzymes improves the output of the product
of interest, one of the main issues is that metabolites do not follow the correct pathway.
Often, the chassis organism has a great interest in either producing its natural product
or keeping itself alive and growing [11]. To ensure that metabolites follow the pathway of
interest, the organism’s metabolic pathway must be optimized. This can be done either
through adaptive laboratory evolution or rational design. Adaptive laboratory evolution
enables the modified organism to adapt to the new pathway and its corresponding
growth medium by allowing it to grow over a prolonged period. This makes it adapt to
the settings of industrial production [11]. The issue with this method is the amount of
time and resources required to perform it, and the mutation that occurs is not something
that can be controlled; therefore, it is not possible to know if further modifications will
have a negative impact on performance. Another method is rational design, where the
metabolic pressure in the organism is altered by modifying different components of the
pathway, such as overexpressing or reducing a specific enzyme [11]. For rational design
to be effective, a thorough understanding of the organism’s metabolic system and its
interaction with the new pathway is required. This is often not the case, so a method
to test multiple modifications simultaneously would greatly simplify the process. That
is why this project is developing the high-throughput method CRI-SPA 2.0 to provide
an effective tool for modification.
1.4
Yeast mating types
S. cerevisiae has two different mating types. They are called mating type a (MATa)
and mating type α (MATα). S. cerevisiae can either be haploid, where it is one of the
two mating types, or diploid, where it is both of the mating types (MATa/α) [12]. The
mating type of the S. cerevisiae depends on which gene is expressed in the yeast’s MAT
locus on chromosome III. The MATa mating type is expressed through the MATa1 and
MATa2 genes, whereas MATα is expressed through the two genes MATα1 and MATα2
[12]. A diploid S. cerevisiae go through meiosis, splitting their chromosomes, producing
four haploid spores, two MATa and two MATα. They then start germinating. If they
come into contact with other yeast cells of a different mating type, they can mate and
form a diploid again, combining their chromosomes [12].
If there are no nearby yeasts of a different mating type S. cerevisiae can undergo
mating type switching. At the MAT locus, there is always a silent copy of one of the
mating types; these are called hidden MAT right a (HMRa) and hidden MAT left α
(HMLα) figure. (2). When the yeast cell wants to switch mating type, it uses the HO
endonuclease to make a double-strand break in the MAT locus, removing the current
mating type, then it copies the silent copy of the not currently active mating type
and uses that as a repair template, successfully switching the mating type of the S.
cerevisiae [13]. Only the mother cell can initiate transcription of the HO endonuclease,
and therefore, it is the only one able to perform mating type switching, thereby avoiding
both cells switching and becoming incompatible again [13].
Mating is an essential function for S. cerevisiae to ensure diversity in their offspring,
ensuring a greater chance of survival. Furthermore, a haploid cell has the ability to
sporulate, meaning mating also ensures survivability in harsh environments [13]. Yeast
mating is also essential in science as it allows for the easy transfer of specific traits

1.5
Yeast repair mechanisms
Double-strand breaks (DSBs) disrupt DNA stability and must be repaired quickly to
maintain genomic stability. Eukaryotic cells, including yeast, use two main pathways to
repair these breaks: Non-Homologous End Joining (NHEJ) and Homologous Recombi-
nation (HR). NHEJ is a faster but error-prone method that directly joins broken DNA
ends without using a template (Figure 3), often employed when no repair template is
present in the immediate vicinity [14]. The other method, homologous recombination,
uses templates that match nucleotide sequences with the damaged area. This makes
it helpful in maintaining genome integrity and performing precise DNA repairs. Ho-
mologous recombination occurs naturally in both mitotic and meiotic cells, but its role
differs significantly between these contexts [15].
Figure 3: Simple model showing the two different methods to repair DNA when a double-stranded break (DSB)
occurs. Homologous Recombination (HR) occurs when a sequence with homology to the broken area is present
and used as a template for the repair. Non-Homologous End Joining (NHEJ) occurs when there are no homologous
sequences present and the two broken ends are fused together with a risk of mutations occurring, either removing
a bp or adding new ones.
Source of figure: made by author.
In mitotic cells, homologous recombination primarily serves as a mechanism for the
5

error-free repair of DNA DSBs. These breaks may result from sources outside the cell,
such as radiation, or from cellular processes, like meiosis, where they occur intentionally,
or during replication, where they occur due to an accident [15]. Upon the induction of
a DSB, the cell initiates a complex repair cascade in which the broken DNA ends are
trimmed to make single-stranded DNA. This single-stranded DNA is then compared to
other DNA sequences to see if there are any sequence that matches the break area. New
DNA is made using the intact DNA strand as a template. After that, the repair process
can follow two paths: one in which the new strand simply returns and pairs with the
original DNA (called synthesis-dependent strand annealing (SDSA)), or one in which
the DNA strands form a special structure called a double Holliday Junction (dHJ). This
structure can be cut in different ways, leading to either an exchange of genetic material
(crossover) or no exchange (non-crossover) (Figure 4) [16].
In meiotic cells, homologous recombination serves an additional purpose: it increases
genetic diversity through gene crossover. During prophase I of meiosis, homologous
chromosomes (one from each parent) pair up and form close physical connections. En-
zymes create intentional DSBs in the DNA, which trigger recombination between these
chromosome pairs. Some of these repair events result in a crossover, where segments
of DNA are exchanged between the chromosomes. This exchange of genetic material
produces new combinations of alleles, creating variation in the cell’s offspring [18].
In biotechnology, homologous recombination is used for precise gene editing. For
example, CRISPR-Cas9 creates targeted DSBs that the cell can repair using homology-
directed repair (HDR) if a matching donor DNA template is provided. This enables
scientists to insert, delete, or modify genes with precision. While CRISPR doesn’t carry
out homologous recombination itself, it enables the cell’s own recombination machinery
to do so by triggering DNA breaks in specific locations [19].
1.6
BY474 strains
In this project, the two S288C-derived strains By4741 and By4742 are used. S288C
was the first eukaryote to have its entire genome sequenced [2]. This means it is easier
to create strains with desired abilities from it.
From this, the two strains, By4741
and By4742, were created. They both have their HO endonuclease deleted, meaning

mating type of the S. cerevisiae like SPA experiments [20].
To differentiate between the two strains, in addition to their mating types, they
were modified with their own unique markers. These are auxotrophic markers, meaning
that a metabolic gene has been deleted and, therefore, the organism can only grow on
plates with added metabolites that supplement the metabolites the deleted gene would
otherwise produce through metabolism.
[21].
The By4741 strain has the following
genes deleted: his3∆1, leu2∆0, met15∆0, ura3∆0, meaning it requires histidine, leucine,
methionine, and uracil to grow. Whereas the By4742 strain has the genes his3∆1 leu2∆0
lys2∆0 ura3∆0 deleted, meaning it requires histidine, leucine, lysine, and uracil to grow.
Without their four specific metabolites, they will not be able to grow on plates, making
it easy to select for them [20].

1.7
Markers’ effect on S. cerevisiae metabolic network
Usually, when studying gene interaction, following the genetic modification of an organ-
ism, the physical traits of the organism are examined, such as whether the morphology
(shape) varies from what is expected or whether color changes have occurred.
The
organism’s fitness is also typically examined to determine if it grows at the expected
rate and produces the expected amount of product.
Often, when conducting these
types of studies, markers are used to ensure that the inserts are successfully inserted
without requiring a more extensive analysis. These markers are usually achieved by
knocking out a specific metabolic gene, rendering it unable to grow on media that lack
7
==PAGE:15==
Master Thesis
the corresponding metabolite, such as uracil or histidine, as mentioned in 1.4.
It was previously believed that it did not substantially affect the organism’s per-
formance, but recent research suggests that this might not be the case [22]. A recent
study of S. cerevisiae showed that gene expression is dependent on each other, meaning
deletion of specific genes results in different metabolic gene expression than what can
be found in a wild-type strain. This research was conducted on 16 different auxotrophic
yeast strains that possessed at least one of the selection markers (HIS3, LEU2, MET15,
URA3). They allowed them to grow on plates with supplementation that matched their
specific markers to observe how their metabolic pathways would react.
The study revealed that 85% of the metabolic genes had changed expression com-
pared to a prototrophic reference strain.
Of the 85% affected genes, around 9.7%
showed almost twofold change in their expression [22]. Then they examined the general
transcriptional interactions, finding that 88% were regulated by epistasis—interactions
where the effect of one gene deletion resulted in other genes being expressed differently
due to their dependence on the expression of the deleted gene. 77% of the genes that
were expressed differently due to selection markers had epistatic regulation, showing
that genes affect each other differently depending on the cell’s metabolic state, and it
doesn’t always follow a predictable or straightforward pattern [22]. Furthermore, 83% of
transcriptional changes overlapped between genetically unrelated auxotrophic strains,
indicating that different deletions caused similar systemic disruptions.
Additionally,
about 35% of the gene activity changes were also seen in yeast strains with unrelated
gene deletions, suggesting that some changes might not be caused by the specific gene
that was deleted, but by the general background of the cell [22].
As a final measurement, they examined the amount of protein and metabolites made
by the S. cerevisiae strains. 11% of the proteins and 75% of the metabolites produced
by the strains showed different levels between the strains. Meaning that the deletion
of metabolic genes not only affects the interactions between the genes but also the
production of proteins and other products in the yeast [22]. What can be concluded from
this is that you have to take into consideration when you measure the performance of S.
cerevisiae strains, when they have markers, as they will perform differently compared
to wild types and therefore might give skewed or incorrect data.
1.8
Mad7
The current CRISPR and, by extension, CRI-SPA gene-editing systems use the Cas9
endonuclease. However, there are legal issues around who owns the rights to Cas9. As
a result, some companies opt not to use Cas9, making it less commercially attractive
[23].
Fortunately, alternatives to Cas9 exist, such as Cpf1, also known as Cas12a. Cpf1
comes from the bacteria Acidaminococcus, from which an optimized version called Mad7
was made. Unlike Cas9, Mad7 is freely available for most research and commercial
purposes without requiring royalties. This makes Mad7 the only gene-editing protein
with a freely available commercial license, making it attractive for use in industry and
research [23].
A crucial function in the CRISPR system is the PAM sequence, which is essential
for the protein to cleave DNA. Different proteins recognize different PAM sequences.
8
==PAGE:16==
Master Thesis
Cas9 looks for the sequence 5’-NGG-3’, which works well for GC-rich DNA but not as
well for AT-rich regions. Mad7 looks for 5’-YTTN-3’, where Y can be either C or T and
N can be any of the four basepair types (C, T, A, or G), which works better in AT-rich
areas (Figure 5). Cas9 and Mad7 also cut DNA in different ways. Cas9 makes a blunt
cut close to the PAM site, whereas Mad7 makes a staggered cut farther away, leaving
overhangs. Cas9 cuts 3 base pairs upstream of the PAM site, while Mad7 cuts 19 base
pairs downstream of the PAM site (Figure 5) [23].
Another advantage of Mad7 is that it utilizes a simpler gRNA system compared to
Cas9, which is somewhat more complex. The gRNAs that both of them use are called
CRISPR RNA (crRNA). Mad7 only needs one natural crRNA, whereas Cas9 requires
a combination of two gRNAs, a crRNA and a trans-activating crRNA (tracrRNA) that
have to be fused together before it can use it and be guided towards the PAM site
(Figure 5) [23].
Figure 5: The figure compares the two nucleases Cas9 and Mad7. For Cas9, the guide RNA is a single-guide
RNA (sgRNA), which is formed by combining crRNA and tracrRNA. In contrast, Mad7 naturally has a single
guide RNA, as its crRNA contains the protospacer sequence.
Cas9 makes a DSB with blunt ends, whereas
Mad7 makes a DSB with staggered ends. Cas9 targets GC-rich regions of DNA by recognizing the ’NGG’ PAM
sequence, whereas Mad7 targets AT-rich regions by recognizing the ’TTTN’ PAM sequence.
Source of figure: modified figure from [23]
A further advantage of the Mad7 compared to the Cas9 is its ability to release its
own gRNA. Mad7 has intrinsic RNase activity, which makes it able to recognize short
direct repeats (DR) and cut them, releasing the gRNA [23].
1.9
Betaxanthin fluorescent reporter gene
For easy analysis of the success rate of the CRI-SPA system, the fluorescent pigment
Betaxanthin (BTX) is used. BTX is a pigment found initially in plants like swiss chard
and beets, giving them their yellow colour [24]. Due to its small molecular size and
intense colour and fluorescence, the yeast cell should not be under severe metabolic
strain while still having a clear yellow colour that makes it easy to identify whether
9
==PAGE:17==
Master Thesis
or not the yeast cell has the BTX pathway inserted [24]. Furthermore, it is the same
fluorescent pathway that was also used in the prior version of the CRI-SPA, making
comparative analysis easier, as they will be compared on the same baseline.
BTX does not naturally occur in yeast, meaning the pathway has to be inserted into
the yeast system. This can be simply done by inserting the plant-specific dioxygenase
DOPA dioxygenase (DOD) into the yeast, in combination with a tyrosine hydroxylase
[25]. The tyrosine hydroxylase enables the yeast to produce Levodopa (L-DOPA). The
DOD then oxidizes the L-DOPA into betalamic acid, a reactive aldehyde that immedi-
ately reacts with nearby amino acids and amines through condensation, forming BTX
(Figure 6) [25].

The effectiveness of the CRI-SPA method will be measured through the intensity
of the BTX fluorescence. The more BTX pathways inserted into the yeast, the more
BTX will be produced, and therefore the more intense the fluorescence will be. As
this has been done before, the fluorescence intensity can be compared to that of prior
experiments to determine if the effectiveness is comparable.
1.10
CRI-SPA
CRI-SPA is a powerful, high-throughput method that combines two different techniques,
CRISPR and SPA.
CRISPR is a effective gene-editing tool that works like molecular scissors to precisely
modify DNA. It uses a gRNA designed to match a specific DNA sequence in the genome.
CRISPR is paired up with a guide enzyme, often the Cas9 enzyme, which is directed by
this gRNA to the target site, where it makes a precise cut in the DNA. Once the cut is
made, the cell uses homologous recombination to repair the cut, either disrupting the
gene or allowing for insertion of new genetic material. In this project, a BTX pathway
and a TK marker are used. Although it is limited to modifying only one organism at a
time, making it a low-throughput method [7].
This can be circumvented by pairing it with the SPA method. SPA is a genetic
technique used in yeast to selectively remove one set of chromosomes from a diploid cell,
10
==PAGE:18==
Master Thesis
allowing for the isolation of haploid cells with the desired genetic content. The process
begins by mating a donor strain, which carries unstable chromosomes, with a recipient
strain to form a diploid. Then, through an external trigger, in this case, galactose, the
donor chromosomes are selectively destabilized and die off. This leaves behind haploid
cells that retain only the recipient’s chromosomes, along with specific transferred genes.
SPA is handy for high-throughput genetic studies because it allows efficient screening
of gene functions and interactions without the need for complex selection steps. Its
scalability and precision make it a powerful tool in functional genomics and large-scale
genetic mapping [6].
Combining these two methods allows CRI-SPA to perform high-throughput screen-
ing (HTS) of thousands of gene-pathway interactions in a single round, facilitating the
identification of genes that influence the pathway and revealing interactions between
different genes. Each screening cycle takes approximately one week, and by selecting
the appropriate library, researchers can assess which gene knockouts or overexpressions
enhance pathway output [7].
In CRI-SPA, CRISPR introduces a double-stranded break at the desired insertion
site in the recipient chromosome. Homology arms flanking the donor sequence guide
homologous recombination, allowing for efficient integration into the recipient genome.
When the recipient strain has received the donor strain’s gene of interest, the colonies
are introduced to galactose, which causes haploidization, ensuring that all resulting
yeast cells are haploid and carry the genetic background of the recipient strain along
with the inserted donor sequence. This setup enables consistent comparison of donor
gene function across diverse genomic contexts [7].
1.11
CRI-SPA 2.0 donor strain
The procedure is the same for both CRI-SPA methods; only the donor strain varies.
The new donor strain is engineered with a CRISPR system and its own gRNA,
which contains a HYG resistance gene. The cassette is designed with a BTX pathway
and an NTC resistance gene, which will be given to the recipient strain. There is a
TK marker located near each of the yeast cells’ chromosomes’ centromeres, as well as
one near the gene cassette, to prevent crossover events. The TK markers become active
upon interaction with galactose. The TK markers near the chromosomes destabilize
the chromosomes’ structure, ultimately leading to their destruction.
This causes a
diploid yeast cell to reject the dying chromosomes, reverting to a haploid form. To
avoid crossover, the TK marker near the insert site, in combination with the prodrug
5-fluorodeoxyuridine (FUdR), converts FUdR into FUdR monophosphate (FdUMP), a
drug that inhibits thymidate synthesis and DNA replication [7].
The old CRI-SPA method used auxotrophic URA3 markers. As a result, the CRI-
SPA system could only be used with URA3-compatible libraries—those designed for
strains lacking a functional URA3 gene. This restricts flexibility, as it cannot be easily
combined with other genetic tools or libraries that rely on a functional URA3 back-
ground. Additionally, it limits the number of selectable markers available in strains
where URA3 is already in use, making it less suitable for experiments requiring multi-
ple genetic modifications [26].
This is avoided in CRI-SPA 2.0 by using Hygromycin (Hyg), nourseothricin (NTC),
11
==PAGE:19==
Master Thesis
and kanamycin (Kan) resistance markers instead. These resistance markers are less re-
strictive than the auxotrophic URA3 marker because they provide antibiotic resistance
that does not depend on the genetic background of the host strain. Unlike the URA3
marker, which requires a strain to be URA3-deficient for selection, Hyg, NTC, and Kan
markers allow selection simply by adding the corresponding antibiotic to the growth
medium. This enables their use in virtually any strain, regardless of auxotrophic status
or metabolic capabilities. Because these antibiotic resistance markers operate indepen-
dently of the cell’s metabolism, they offer greater flexibility and can be easily combined
with other genetic tools. This makes Hyg, NTC, and Kan markers more versatile and
less limiting compared to the auxotrophic URA3 marker, especially in experiments in-
volving multiple genetic modifications [26].
Furthermore, the new CRI-SPA 2.0 uses the Mad7 enzyme instead of Cas9, which
the original CRI-SPA used. It was explained in Section 1.8 that using Mad7 instead
of Cas9, the main reason for the swap, also has the benefit of allowing simpler gRNA
sequences, as it only requires a single gRNA, whereas Cas9 requires two [23].
1.12
CRI-SPA 2.0 recipient strain
For this project, the donor strain that is used is the yeast knock-out (YKO) library. A
library containing over 5500 yeast colonies, each with a different gene deletion, resulting
in a collection with almost all genes in S. cerevisiae deleted [27]. The YKO library was
created by replacing various target genes with a deletion cassette containing the KanMX
gene. The cassettes were inserted between the start and stop codons of the target genes.
The deletions were done using mitotic recombination. The KanMX gene was flanked
by two unique 20-bp sequences (Figure 7), which functioned as molecular barcodes to
enable the identification of gene deletions [27]. Overall, the library consists of 5,917
gene knockouts, covering approximately 96% of the known open reading frames (ORFs)
in S. cerevisiae genome [27]. The YKO library will be utilized in this thesis to conduct
a comparative analysis between the new CRI-SPA 2.0 and the previous CRI-SPA.

Fusion PCR
Some of the fragments used in transformations required their overhang to be extended
to increase the success rate of the transformations. To do this, fusion PCR was used.
Fusion PCR followed standard PCR amplification using Phusion U Hot Start DNA
Polymerase (Thermo Fisher Scientific) (Appendix 5).
The procedure was the same
17
==PAGE:25==
Master Thesis
as standard PCR amplification, except that no primers were added at the beginning.
Instead, the fragments to be combined were added in volumes of 2 µL. The mix was
run for 10 cycles (following standard PCR amplification settings). After the 10 cycles,
primers that covered the entire combined fragment were added. After adding primers,
the mixture was run for 25 cycles.
2.3.3
Gel electrophoresis
For quick confirmation of the presence of PCR fragments, gel electrophoresis was em-
ployed. Gels containing 1% agarose and 10-20 µL of SYBR Safe DNA gel stain were
used. Each column was loaded with 2 µL of PCR mixture and 1 µL of 6x DNA loading
dye to measure the size of the bands. A DNA ladder mix was added to adjacent columns
of the PCR mix being run. The gels were then run at 100 mV in 1x TAE buffer for
20 minutes. The size of the ladders was either 1000 bp or 100 bp, depending on the
expected size of the fragment being run.
2.3.4
Gel purification
After PCR amplification and the presence of the fragment of interest, the fragment had
to be purified and isolated. Depending on whether impurities were present in the PCR
mix, it could be purified either directly or by gel purification.
If multiple bands were observed in the gel, the PCR mix required gel purification.
This was achieved by loading the entire PCR mix, stained with 6x DNA loading dye,
into a well of the appropriate size. The gel was then run for 20 minutes at 100 mV.
After running, the gel was placed under a UV light to visualize the bands, and the band
of the correct size was then cut out using a scalpel.
The isolated gel piece was then dissolved in 200 µL NTI/ 100 mg gel and allowed

2.3.7
Isolation of genomic DNA
Most of the fragments used in transformation were made from genomic DNA (gDNA),
which was extracted from S. cerevisiae cells.
The isolation and purification method used was homemade and therefore does not
have an appropriate written protocol. The procedure was performed as follows.
Using a 10 µl inoculation loop, a large amount of a S. cerevisiae colony was collected
and inoculated in 500 µl breaking buffer with LiAc (2% Triton X-100, 1% SDS, 100 mM
NaCl, 10 mM Tris-HCl, 1 mM EDTA, 200 mM LiAc) and 200 µl of glass beads in a
FastPrep tube. A mini vortex mixer then shook the mixture for 60 seconds. Afterwards,
the mixture was spun down using a tabletop mini centrifuge.
From the spun-down mixture, 150 µl of the supernatant was transferred to a 1.5
ml Eppendorf tube. Afterward, 15 µl of 5 M NaCl and 400 µl of ice-cold 96% ethanol
were added to the Eppendorf tube and centrifuged at 10,000 × g for 3 minutes. The
supernatant was discarded, and the resulting pellet was allowed to dry in a heating
block at 50°C for 10 minutes. After drying, the pellet was resuspended in 100 µl MQ
water.
The purified gDNA was measured for concentration using a Nanodrop Lite and then
stored at −20◦C, ready for amplification.

Plasmids in the cloning were made using USER fusion cloning [28]. The full protocol

No vector was used in the cloning; only fragments made using PCR amplification

If there

After mixing and vortexing the mixture, it was heated at 37°C in a heating block

Simultaneously with the mixture being heated at 20°C, E. coli DH5α cells were

O/N.

of correct USER cloning.
2.3.10

To construct the new yeast strains in the thesis, the LiAc/PEG/ssDNA transformation
method was used. The protocol used can be found in Appendix 5.
The day before transformation, the template yeast is grown overnight (O/N) in 3
mL of YPD. On the following day, 0.5 mL is taken from the O/N culture and inoculated
into 50 mL YPD in a 500 mL shake flask. The media is then incubated for 5 hours at
30°C and 180 RPM. To get active cells.
After the cells were activated, they were added to a 50 mL sterile Falcon tube and
spun down for 5 minutes at 3000g. The supernatant was removed, and the cell mass
was reinoculated in 1 mL of MQ water and transferred to a 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube.
The cells are spun down again, and the volume is measured to estimate the number
of transformations that can be formed. The water is removed, and 90 µL per transfor-
mation MQ water is added to the cells. The cells are inoculated again, and 100 µL of
the cell mix is added to a 2 mL Eppendorf tube for each transformation.
The Eppendorf tubes were then spun down again, and water was removed depending
on the amount of DNA and plasmid added to the mix afterwards, resulting in a final
volume of 100 µL. Then, PEG and 1 M lithium acetate were added to the mixture,
causing it to become homogenized.
The tubes containing the transformation mix were then placed in a heating block

Discussion
4.1
Construction and performance of gRNA plasmids
To produce a properly functioning CRI-SPA 2.0 strain, multiple gRNA plasmids were
constructed with different resistance genes, making them more flexible for selecting the
appropriate selection media.
Two iterations of the gRNA plasmids were made, one with only a single gRNA and
the other with two different gRNA sequences. The double gRNA showed a significant
improvement in transformation efficiency compared to the single gRNA plasmids, as
evident by the significantly higher number of colonies and higher transformation success
rate. Therefore, the double gRNA plasmid was used for the final CRI-SPA 2.0 strain
and not the single gRNA plasmid.
Comparing the sequenced plasmids with the theoretical models created in Benchling
revealed no issues with the plasmids, thereby allowing for the use of other antibiotics
in future experiments.
The plasmids used for comparison did not contain the same resistance gene; this
is an important consideration when examining how the different plasmids were con-
structed. The double gRNA plasmid with NTC resistance (pDIV1129) was made using
two different fragments from the single gRNA plasmid with NTC resistance (pDIV939),
whereas pDIV939 was compared with the Hyg resistance containing pDIV1131, con-
structed using fragments from pDIV939 and pDIV1128. This difference should not be
significant enough to show any major improvement in transformation ability, but should
still be considered.
Another consideration is the lack of testing done on the addition of the new gRNA
sequence in the double gRNA plasmid, which makes it hard to determine whether the
improvement in transformation came from the new gRNA or the combination of the
two. To determine if the double gRNA truly enhanced transformation ability, a new
plasmid with only the new gRNA sequence was created and used in a transformation,
and then compared to pDIV939 and pDIV1131 to assess whether it yielded a result
closer to either of them. If the transformation success was closer to pDIV939, it meant
that the combination of the two sequences made the gRNA plasmids effective. However,
if the results were close to pDIV1131, it meant that the new sequence was the actual
improvement, and the design had to be rethought.
4.2
Construction of the initial CRI-SPA 2.0 donor strain
For the final design of the CRI-SPA 2.0 strain, multiple transformation steps were per-
formed. First, the TK marker was inserted and confirmed to be functional; subsequently,
multiple BTX pathways were inserted using different gRNA plasmids to enhance trans-
formation capabilities. Some of the individual steps were incorrect and will need to be
revised or reworked in the future.
4.2.1
The TK marker
A single strain was constructed containing a TK marker located near the planned gene
landing site, as well as one TK marker near each chromosome centromere. The strain
50
==PAGE:58==
Master Thesis
was called TKtran.
Another strain, TKtest, containing only the TK marker near the gene landing site,
was also constructed for further testing.
The two different gRNA plasmids, pDIV1061 and pDIV1062, were used to create
the TKtran and TKtest strains, as the efficiency of both was unknown and needed
to be documented. Upon examining the successful transformation, pDIV1062 showed
significantly greater results than pDIV1061. It was assumed that the success was due to
the pDIV1061 binding site having more disadvantageous secondary structures compared
to that of pDIV1062. To further test this theory, a model visualization tool like PyMOL
can be used to inspect the binding area or the entire chromosome to see how it folds
and how much access Mad7 has to the area. Another more practical method would
be Circular Dichroism Spectroscopy. It does not directly show how the binding works,
but it’s a simple method to determine if the binding of Mad7 is stronger when using
pDIV1062 compared to pDIV1061.
There was an interest in determining whether TKtran had retained its ability to
induce apoptosis after modification and whether the newly inserted TK marker was
capable of killing the cell alone. This was tested by plating TKtran, TKtest, and the
two backbones used for each new strain, sDIV519 and sDIV504, on SC-media plates
containing FuDR, galactose, both galactose and FuDR, and a standard SC-plate.
Observing their individual behaviour, they all acted as expected. The TKtran was
still able to kill itself when it was plated on galactose and galactose + FUdR plates.
What was also noted was that it did not kill itself when plated on a FUdR plate, which
was of great interest as an addition of new TK marker would add the risk of a leaky
promoter making the TK marker promoter active even when no galactose was present
and killing itself, but as that did not happen the new promoter was not leaky.
As for the TKtest, the promoter for that one was not leaky either, which makes
sense as it shared the promoter and insert with the TKtran. As for the new insert, it
was unable to kill itself when plated on only galactose; this was, of course, expected, as a
singular TK marker is not enough to kill an entire cell, especially when it is not located
as close to the centromere as the other TK markers. When plated on the galactose +
FUdR plate, it killed itself without any issue, indicating that it would be capable of
destabilizing the gene landing site during the CRI-SPA screening, thereby avoiding any
unwanted crossover events.
As it stands, the TKtran strain does not need further improvement and is a stable
foundation if a new CRI-SPA 2.0 donor were to be constructed.
4.2.2
The BTX pathways
Two different yeast strains were constructed using two different BTX pathways. BTXds
got its pathway from the E. coli strain pBTX002, which contains the BTX gene and
an NTC resistance. The other strain, BTXdg, acquired the BTX path from the E. coli
pDIV574, which only contained the BTX gene.
BTXdg demonstrated excellent transformation capabilities, with a significant in-
crease in transformation success following the acquisition of a double gRNA plasmid.
BTXdg would have been an ideal strain to use as a donor strain for the CRI-SPA 2.0
screening, though there was a concern about the difference in pathway between the
51
==PAGE:59==
Master Thesis
original CRI-SPA donor strain and BTXdg, as they varied both in resistance and also
the flanking regions, as shown, there were no issues with gRNA cutting the insert. The
concern about different BTX pathways affecting the final screening results meant that
there were greater BTX donors than BTXdg, even though the transformation abilities
of BTXdg appeared to be better or easier to work with.
There were some difficulties constructing BTXds as the Mad7 cut part of the gene
fragment, which had to be repaired using the primers PR DIV4061 and PR DIV4062.
Only a single colony successfully transformed, meaning it appeared to have worse trans-
formation ability than BTXdg, though this was more likely due to Mad7 killing most
of the transformation than the strain’s transformation capabilities. Even with only a
single colony, the intense yellow colour from it, confirmation of an insert in the gene
landing site, and the matching BTX pathway to the original CRI-SPA donor strain made
BTXds more attractive for testing and ultimately led to its selection over BTXdg.
However, if necessary, BTXdg would be an ideal backup donor strain in case a
comparison between BTXds and another functional CRI-SPA 2.0 donor strain is needed.
4.3
CRI-SPA 2.0 screening results
A CRI-SPA 2.0 screening was performed using the BTXds strain.
Examining the overall fitness of the cells throughout the screening process revealed
no apparent issues. Although some strains did not survive the initial mating steps,
this was expected and not unusual. Both generally, as every time the YKO library gets
plated, some of the strains don’t grow; furthermore, the screening plates were compared
to another CRI-SPA screening using the same library, here the some of the same strains
also didn’t grow, therefore it was concluded that there was no issue for BTXds and the
YKO library to mate as most of the strains grew without any issues on the selection
plates.
The issues began to appear after the donor strain BTXds was killed and the YKO
strains were plated onto the final selection plate.
Here, approximately 50% of the
strains died or did not grow sufficiently to be detected by the script. This indicated
that BTXds had issues with successfully transferring the BTX pathway into the recipient
strain. When it did transfer successfully, it appeared to have done so imperfectly, as a
significant portion of the growing cells exhibited unhealthy growth patterns.
Analyzing and comparing the final selection plates of the CRI-SPA 2.0 to the original
CRI-SPA screening also revealed that none of the 2.0 colonies ever reached the same
level of yellow intensity as the original colonies did. Furthermore, none of the colonies
from 2.0 grew close to the original either. This would indicate that CRI-SPA 2.0 was
worse than the original; however, it is far more likely, given the issues with BTXds and
the unhealthiness of the transformed colonies, that the issue lies with the insert rather
than the Mad7 system.
There is still potential in the system, as the few hits that did grow typically showed
a yellow intensity comparable to that of the original CRI-SPA screening. Meaning when
the donor strain gets improved, the system is almost certainly on par with the original
one.
To test these things, several steps can be taken. Firstly, checking the SC FUdR
plate is recommended. Performing colony PCR on some of the strains that died in the
52
==PAGE:60==
Master Thesis
final selection plates, as well as some of the strains that survived, to check if the inserts
were in the strain to begin with, and to see if it is located in the correct place. If there
were no insert in the strains that died, that would explain their inability to handle the
antibiotics. Furthermore, if the inserts in the ones that did survive were located in the
wrong area, it could have affected their ability to grow.
If the prior methods did not yield any concrete answers, repeating the experiment
using BTXds again and BTXdg would make it possible to determine if the issues were
with the system or the pathway.
If BTXds yielded promising results in the second
screening, the errors that occurred during the initial screening were due to human
error, and further experimentation with BTXds could be conducted. However, if BTXds
yielded poor results again and BTXdg did not encounter any issues, then it could be
concluded that the pathway was the problem.
4.4
The future of CRI-SPA 2.0
It can be concluded that the CRI-SPA 2.0 screening failed, as most of the results were
not comparable to the original CRI-SPA screening data, which was the primary goal of
the thesis.
To move forward, several steps can be taken. The screening process can be done us-
ing the BTXdg strain. Although not entirely symmetrical for the original BTX pathway,
the similarities between them should provide a rough idea of the potential of CRI-SPA
2.0.
Then the BTXds have to be fixed, ideally the strain should be redone, using TKtran
as backbone again, either modifying the insert beforehand so Mad7 does not cut it or
changing the gRNA in the double plasmid, as only one of the two gRNA in the double
gRNA plasmids should aim for the insert, whereas the other one should not. A third
option is to insert an NTC resistance in the BTXdg strain, bringing it closer to BTXds
and the original CRI-SPA donor pathway. Though the one with the most potential is
modifying the gRNA.
Ideally, BTXds gets fixed, and a full screening is performed where the results show
that it is comparable to the original CRI-SPA screen. From here, the strains should be
returned to their wild-type form by restoring the metabolic mutations his3∆1, leu2∆0,
met15∆0, ura3∆0, and lys2∆0. This should make them more flexible for future trans-
formation as their auxotrophic markers no longer limit them. Furthermore, it would also
ensure that the data is not skewed due to the changed interaction between metabolic
genes when a gene is not present. The foundation for this restoration has already been
established through the discovery and mapping of four out of the five deletions. Though
the last gene deletion, met15∆0, still needs to be identified before the restoration can
begin.
If these goals were to be completed, CRI-SPA 2.0 should be ready to be used as
an efficient high-throughput modification tool (for S. cerevisiae). Making it easier to
generate data for improving cells and cell factories.

5
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to construct a CRI-SPA 2.0 donor strain inserting a TK marker and
a BTX pathway. Afterwards, the donor strain was used to complete a full CRI-SPA
screening cycle, comparing the generated data with the original CRI-SPA screening
method. Concurrently, an attempt was made to identify and map out the deletions in
the BY474 strains for future restoration of said genes.
The TK marker was confirmed to be successfully inserted in the correct area of
the yeast cell. Testing the TK marker strain revealed that there was no unintended
expression of TK when it was not in the presence of galactose, indicating no leakiness
from the promoter. This meant that a fully functional strain with all necessary TK
markers was ready for use.
The two different yeast strains were made using the finished TK marker strains as
their backbone and different BTX pathways as inserts. Both were tested, and their
inserts were located in the correct area. BTXds, the strain that had the same BTX
pathway as the original CRI-SPA screening, had troubles with transformation due to
mad7 cutting it. DGBTX, the strain with a different BTX pathway, exhibited significant
transformation abilities but lacked resistance and was significantly different from the
original CRI-SPA donor strain. For these reasons, BTXds was chosen as the donor
strain in the CRI-SPA 2.0 screening.
The CRI-SPA 2.0 screening was completed without any difficulties, demonstrating
normal growth throughout the various plating steps. However, at the final selection
plate, the transformed plates began to exhibit unhealthy growth, and almost 50% did
not grow. The screening was deemed a failure as it was supposed to compare the entire
CRI-SPA 2.0 modified YKO library with the original CRI-SPA modified YKO library.
Even though the screening failed, some data could still be derived from it; the few
cells that did grow showed colour intensity comparable to that of the original screening.
Meaning there is potential for the 2.0 system to be as good as the original.
Groundwork was also laid for the future preparation of restoring removed metabolic
genes by mapping out how the deletions were performed and identifying potential PAM
sequences for future modification.
In conclusion, the screening failed due to the donor strain having issues with deliv-
ering the insert correctly; however, Preliminary work has been laid to construct a new
donor strain and repeat the screening process.
