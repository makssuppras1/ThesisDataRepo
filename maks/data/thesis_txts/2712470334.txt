Abstract
Most nation states have signed the Paris Agreement in an effort to reduce greenhouse gas
emissions to keep the rise in the global average temperature below 2 °C. A major part
of the greenhouse gas emissions worldwide is from heat production, both domestic and
industrial. In this context heat pumps will play a major role in the electrification of heat
production, as heat pumps offer energy eﬀicient conversion of low-level heat to usable high
level heat. Digital twins of heat pumps are used for verification and optimization of the
physical plants. As sensor- and computing technology become cheaper, digital twin will
become more widespread and common. Sensors are also used to control and monitor heat
pump plants. But sensors can have malfunctions which causes degradation of heat pump
performance and faulty monitoring of heat pump plants. The goal of this thesis was to
study the impact of malfunctioning sensors, and study if it is possible to detect malfunc-
tioning sensors. The study also intended to study what effect malfunctioning sensors has
on digital twins of heat pumps, more specifically virtual sensors of heat pump.
A model of a booster heat pump for ultra low temperature district heating, developed in
the Modelica environment Dymola was used as the study case. New controls were imple-
mented on the heat pump, such that the effect of sensor malfunctions could be studied.
The malfunctioning of the forward temperature sensor showed to have a large impact on
the heat pump performance, with a bias of the sensor of −5 K causing a increase in heat
pump electricity consumption of 10% and an decrease of the COP of the same amount.
Similar to the forward temperature sensor, a bias of the return temperature sensor of 5 K
resulted in an increase in compressor consumption of 20%, and a decrease in COP of 15%.
The malfunction of the district heating temperature sensor and the water flow sensor on
the condenser side had similar effect on the heat pump in that they both cause an increase
in electricity consumption of 40% at a bias of 5 K and 25%, respectively. The malfunction
of the superheating sensor did not have a major impact on the heat pump performance.
From the heat pumps response to sensor malfunctions some insight was gained, which led
to the development of rule-based sensor malfunction detection algorithms, which monitor
the pressure levels in the heat pump, which are closely coupled to the accuracy of the
water temperature sensors. The rules were implemented on the forward and return tem-
perature sensors and were tested at different operating conditions. The results showed the
rule-based sensor detection algorithms were able to detect sensor malfunctions down to a
bias of 1.5 K for both the forward and return temperature sensors.
A few select virtual sensors were implemented in the model and verified on their accuracy
in predicting the modelled parameters. All the virtual sensor showed good accuracy at
normal operating conditions. Sensor malfunctions were introduced on the underlying sen-
sors of the virtual sensors, and it showed that most of the virtual sensors’ accuracy was
sensitive to sensor malfunction, which emphasizes the importance of sensor malfunction
detection for digital twins.
The virtual sensor uncertainty was calculated, based on the sensors innate inaccuracy. It
showed that the virtual sensors with many underlying physical- and virtual sensors have
a large uncertainty associated with the evaluation of the virtual parameters. The results
from the uncertainty analysis can be used to set the lower bound for when the virtual sen-
sor is detecting a malfunction and when the malfunction is detected because of underlying
sensor inaccuracy.
An architecture was presented for the detection of water temperature sensor malfunctions
of the heat pump. The architecture frames how some of the virtual sensors and rule based
algorithms, introduced in this work, can be implemented together for detection of sensors
faults.
Lastly, simulations were made with component malfunctions.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The EU, and all its countries, signed the Paris Agreement, to pursue efforts to keep the
global average temperature increase below 2 degrees Celsius [1]. According to the IPCC,
this goal is only possible if massive and immediate cuts in greenhouse gas emissions are
made [2]. Heating accounts for 63.6% of the energy consumption in the residential sector
in the EU [3]. In the EU domestic hot water and space heating is most commonly pro-
duced by use of natural gas boilers [4]. Heat pumps offer a cost-eﬀicient alternative for
production of domestic hot water and space heating by use of electricity from renewable
sources. Heat pumps are also used commercially and are becoming more common for high
temperature industrial applications as well. Heat pumps therefore play a major role in
ensuring a reduction in greenhouse gas emissions, both in the EU and worldwide.
Heat pumps require precise control to ensure optimal operations. Sensors are used to
gather the data that is used to control the heat pumps. If the precision of the sensors
is degraded, this can lead to a decrease in eﬀiciency and higher energy consumption [5].
The effect of sensor malfunctions on heat pumps has not been studied thoroughly in the
literature, but it has been done more extensively on other HVAC equipment. In a 2003
study by Wang et al. it was demonstrated that a 20% sensor bias of a flow sensor resulted
in a 14% increase in energy consumption on an air handling unit [6].
Fault detection and diagnostics (FDD) is a field of study for detection of system faults.
System faults can be both component faults, such as compressor valve leakage and heat
exchanger fouling, or sensor malfunctions [7]. FDD can be divided into three categories.
The first category is the Quantitative model based methods, which use first principle equa-
tions and understanding for the detection of system faults.
The second category are
the Qualitative model based methods, which use understanding of a systems normal and
faulty operations for detection of system faults. Lastly, the Process history based methods,
which use data driven methods for system fault detection, and little system understanding
is needed.
For detection of component malfunctions all of the above mentioned methods have been
applied extensively. However, most of the literature on sensor malfunction detection has
been done based on so called Principal Component Analysis (PCA), which is a process
history based, black box, method used in FDD for both detection of components and
sensors [8]. But very little work has been done on utilizing white and grey box models for
the detection of sensor malfunctions, specifically.
Digital twins of heat pump plants are becoming increasingly common as sensors, storage
and availability of data becomes cheaper and more available. Digital twins use data from
the physical plant to feed into the digital twin for verification, optimization and identi-
fication of system faults. Virtual sensors are one of the ways in which digital twins are
used for fault detection. Virtual sensors use real time sensor data from the physical plant
to calculate some parameter, that cannot, or is too expensive, to be measured otherwise.
This can include refrigerant charge, heat exchanger fouling and compressor malfunctions
[9] [10]. But these calculations are not reliable, if the underlying physical sensor is mal-
functioning. In the literature it is always assumed, that the underlying data, that is fed
into the virtual sensors is reliable.

Background
2.1
Heat pumps
Heat pumps are well known to most people in a domestic setting in the form of air-to-air-,
air-to-water- or geothermal heat pumps. These can be seen installed in both private homes
and on apartment buildings all around the world. Heat pumps for domestic hot water and
space heating have seen a growth in market share in recent years. They are also projected
to continue the growth in the future, with the number of heat pumps projected to double
for air-to-air, water sourced, and geothermal heat pumps by 2026 [11].
Large scale heat pumps are being used in the production of district heating. Here, heat
pumps in the MW scale produce heat for hundreds or even thousands of homes throughout
entire municipalities [12]. Normally the source is waste heat from industrial or incinera-
tion plants. Heat pumps using seawater as a source are also seeing a rise, as sea water is
an abundant heat source [13].
The industrial sector is also starting to invest in heat pump technology. One of the main
hurdles is that many industrial applications require steam at high temperature and pres-
sure, where it can be diﬀicult to achieve high COP’s [14].
For industrial applications, heat pumps are the ideal technology for waste heat recovery
and can increase the energy eﬀiciency of industrial plants. This is because heat pumps en-
able industrial plants to utilize low grade heat, that would otherwise have been discarded.
Applications of heat pumps is seeing a growth in many industrial sectors, including the
dairy, food processing and manufacturing sectors [15].
2.2
District heating and booster heat pumps
District heating is a system for distributing heat from a central heat generation plant
to both residential and commercial consumers. District heating can be produced from
various sources, such as CHP plants, heat pumps and boilers.
The temperature on a normal district heating network is usually below 100 °C, normally
in the range of 55-70 °C [16].
District heating is most widespread in the EU, Russia and China, that produce 85% of
the total district heating worldwide [17].
Because of higher energy eﬀiciency low temperature district heating (LTDH) and ultra low
temperature district heating (ULTDH), is being researched and implemented, especially in
Denmark.
With LTDH the temperature of the district heating supply is as low as 50 °C. With space
heating (assuming floor heating) having a temperature of 20-22 °C and domestic hot water
having a temperature of 50-60 °C, simple heat exchange can be suﬀicient to produce the
necessary temperature for the user.
ULTDH has a supply temperature as low as 30 °C, with most sources stating a supply
temperature of around 40 °C. This means, that the domestic hot water cannot be produced
by heat exchange alone. It is therefore necessary to boost or increase the temperature
locally close to the consumers [18].
Booster heat pumps are applied in ULTDH networks, to boost the temperature of the
water from the network such that in can be used for production of domestic hot water.

2.3
Digital twins and Industry 4.0 and heat pumps
A digital twin is real time digital representation of a physical plant. The digital twin is
fed real time data from the physical plant, and this data is used to simulate the processes
digitally. It can be applied for finding malfunctions in the physical plant or conducting
digital experiments for improvements of the plant [19].
Digital twins and sensors give the opportunity for virtual sensors. Virtual sensors take
measurement data from plants and use it as input in the estimation of some variable,
that can not, or is costly, to measure otherwise. For the virtual sensors to be accurate, it
requires accurate and adequate sensor readings.
Increasing size of heat pump units for district heating applications and the rise of high
temperature heat pumps in industry, sensor technology in heat pumps will play a role in
ensuring optimal operations as well as monitoring for maintenance for these larger plants
[20].
With the rise of Industry 4.0, where among other things, plants will become increasingly
self-monitoring, reliable sensor readings is crucial. Remote monitoring, self-optimization
and predictive maintenance are not possible without reliable sensor readings. It is therefore
crucial to gain knowledge on how faulty sensors affect the performance of the plants, and
how these can be detected [21].
2.4
Sensor technology principles
All sensors operate on the basics on principles from physics and mechanics.
Sensors
transform a parameter into an electrical signal, which again is interpreted to a physical
measurement.
There are four main categories of physical parameters on which most sensors are based
upon. These categories are dimensional-, kinematic-, dynamic-, and thermal parameters.
One example of a sensor based on dimensional metrology is a strain gauge, that uses the
Poisson- and Raoult effect to measure the strain on a component.
A pitot tube can measure the velocity of an aircraft bases on the principles from kinemat-
ics, more specifically Berloulli’s equation.
Newton’s second law states that the acceleration of an object is dependent on the force
acting on an object and the mass of the object. This principle from dynamics is used in
accelerometers.
The Seebeck effect is a thermal phenomenon where the voltage difference across a junc-
tion with two different materials depends on the temperature of the materials.
This
phenomenon is the foundation of the temperature sensing thermocouples [22].

2.5
Heat pump control and sensors
Heat pumps require effective controls for accurate and optimal operations. The control
method depends on the type of system and the application. The control can be as simple
as an ON/OFF control strategy, where the compressor is switched on and off based on
the measurement of some parameter, such as a temperature switch in a tank. The control
can also be more complicated, such that PI(D) controllers are necessary to maintain some
internal or external parameter at set point values. There are also examples of multiple
input, multiple output (MIMO) controls, where multiple parameters, such as temperature
and pressure, are being controlled at the same time, for optimal operations of the plants
[23]. All the control strategies require functioning sensors, in varying numbers [24].
2.6
Sensor technology applied in heat pumps
Domestic heat pump units usually only have a few sensors installed. These are usually only
two to four temperature sensors within the heat pump cycle, as well as a source and a sink
temperature sensor. Additionally, some small heat pump units have two pressure sensors,
one for each pressure level in the heat pump. It is also common to have differential pressure
sensors measuring pressure difference across filters and other components, for maintenance
purposes.
Various other sensors are applied depending on the application of the heat pump. Air
sourced heat pumps can have an air humidity sensor and room quality sensors if it is
an air-to-air heat pump. Water- and ground sourced heat pumps often have water flow
sensors and water temperature sensors for control purposes [25].
Larger heat pump units, for example for district heating, have multiple sensors installed.
These include multiple pressure, temperature and flow sensors. The pressure sensors are
used to monitor the evaporation and condensation pressure, including the intermediate
pressure, in case of a two-stage system. The condensation pressure inside the heat pump
is sometimes used to control the forward temperature on the heat sink [26].
Heat pumps for industrial applications are similar to the large scale heat pumps. Addi-
tional sensors installed internally in the components might also be necessary depending
on the scale of the plant. This might include pressure sensors on the inlet and outlet of
the heat exchangers, internal temperature and pressure sensors in the compressor.
2.6.1
Temperature sensors
The four most common types of electrical temperature sensors are: Thermistors, Resis-
tance Temperature Detectors (RTD’s), Thermocouples and Semiconductor-Based Sensors.

All these technologies depend on the measurement of the electric potential difference across
a device. The measured electric potential difference is then translated into a temperature.
In heat pumps, thermocouples, thermistors and resistance temperature detectors are most
commonly used1.
The theory behind the design of thermocouples depends on the Seebeck effect. The Seebeck
effect is an electromotive force 2, that occurs when there is a temperature difference be-
tween two reference points. Two different materials that conduct electricity are connected
in a junction. The temperature difference, that produces the electromotive force, occurs
because the two materials conduct heat at a different rate. The two materials produce a
voltage that is dependent on the temperature, and the voltage can then be interpreted as
a temperature.
Equation 2.1 shows the equation for the Seebeck effect. It states that the gradient of
the voltage, ∇V , is proportional to the gradient of the temperature, ∇T. S(T) is the
temperature dependent Seebeck coeﬀicient, which is a material property that describes
the induced thermo-electric voltage at a certain temperature.
∇V = −S(T)∇T
(2.1)
Thermocouples have the advantage that they are produced according to standards, such
that they are interchangeable with regards to dimension and connections. There are many
different standard types of thermocouples, that are chosen depending on the application
and temperature range.
Thermocouples do not require any external power, as the electromotive force itself powers
the sensor.
Thermocouples have the disadvantage, that they have a larger inaccuracy than some other
types of temperature sensors. The limits of error for production according to standards
vary depending on the type of thermocouple. But an error between ±0.5 K to ±2.5 K is
the limit, depending on the type [27]. This means that thermocouples are not the optimal
choice for accurate controls.
Thermistors can be divided into two categories: negative thermal coeﬀicient (NTC) ther-
mistors and positive thermal coeﬀicient (PTC) thermistors. NTC’s are more common.
When a thermistor is an NTC thermistor, it means that the electrical resistance decreases
as the temperature increases through the device. The increase in resistance can be mea-
sured as a decrease in electric potential difference through the device, and vice versa.
Equation 2.2 shows that a change in electrical resistance is dependent on a change in tem-

a NTC thermistor.
∆R = k∆T

2.6.2
Pressure sensors
There exist multiple types of pressure sensors. The most common type of pressure sensor
for HVAC applications are resistive pressure sensors as they provide adequate accuracy
along with reasonable cost. Pressure switches are also used for safety reasons, but these
will not be discussed here.
The design of the pressure sensors varies depending on if it reads absolute pressure, gauge
pressure or pressure difference.
Resistive pressure sensors, like the temperature sensors, use a change in electric resistance
to measure pressure. In resistive pressure sensors, a strain gauge is placed on a diaphragm,
that deforms with varying pressure. When the strain gauge deforms, the electric potential
difference changes.
The measurement of the difference in electric potential difference
across the strain gauge is then converted into a pressure reading.
Resistive pressure
sensor strain gauges can be made out of various different materials like metal, ceramics or
semiconductor materials. Semiconductor materials utilize the so-called piezoelectric effect.
The piezoelectric effect is the phenomena when the electric resistivity increases when a
mechanical strain is applied. Piezoresistive pressure transmitters are frequently used for
HVAC applications and in general [30].
Piezoresistive pressure transmitters have an accuracy in the range of ±0.25% to ±1.25%,
depending on the application [31].
2.6.3
Flow sensors
There exist multiple types of flow meters, all with advantages and disadvantages. The
functioning of each of types of flow meters will not be discussed in this work.
Flow meters typically have an accuracy range of ±0.5% to ±5%, depending on the type,
application, and phase of the fluid [32].

As all other components, sensors can malfunction. The cause of the sensor malfunctions
tends to vary depending on the type of sensor, the application of the sensor and where

A positive bias or drift of a sensor means that the signal that the sensor transmits is higher
than the real value. For example, if a sensor signals a temperature of 60 °C with a bias of
5 K, the real process temperature is 55 °C. If a sensor measures a temperature 60 °C with
a bias of -5 K, the real process temperature is 65 °C.
Sensor bias is when the sensor has a constant offset between the real process value and
the sensor output value.
Sensor bias is often caused by faulty calibration, but it can
also be caused by faulty installation or sudden vibrations or impact, or general hardware
degradation [35].
Depending on the cause of the bias, it can be mitigated by calibrating the sensor or by
adjusting the position or mounting of the sensor. Sometimes it might be necessary to
replace the sensor [36].
Sensor drift is when the output value of a sensor shifts gradually over time. Sensor drift can
be caused by degradation of the sensor material, environmental contamination, vibration
or extreme fluctuations.
Sensor drift can be mitigated by ensuring that the sensors are calibrated regularly and can
be detected by observing the heat pump performance over time [37] [35]. It can however

2.8
Fault detection and diagnostics
Fault detection and diagnostics (FDD) is a field within HVAC that is concerned with the
automatic identification of malfunction on HVAC equipment. The diagnostics methods
can be divided into three main categories. These categories are Quantitative Model-Based,
Qualitative Model-Based and Process History Based diagnostics methods [7].
The quantitative model based methods are based on physics and first principles, and
are also known as white box models. The quantitative models can be sub-divided into
detailed physical models and simplified physical models. In the detailed physical models,
the mass-, momentum- and energy balances for the system are solved for identification of
faulty operations. An example of this is a digital twin of a heat pump in Modelica. In
simplified physical models, lumped approaches are used for identification of faults. This
can for example be the application of an energy balance on a component, for estimating
a temperature or a mass flow.
The qualitative model based methods are based on prior knowledge of the system to
judge whether the operation is faulty or normal. Rule based models are a part of the
qualitative models. Rule based models can be based on expert systems, first principles or
limits/alarms. Most applicable to heat pumps are the first principles and the limits and
alarms methods.
The process history based methods use logged operational data to train the FDD model,
either purely through data, in black box models, or with combining first principle models
with data, in grey box models.

Virtual sensors for vapor compression systems
Virtual sensors can be used for fault detection and diagnostics. Virtual sensors are nor-
mally either simplified physical models, black box models or grey box models, as described
in the previous section.
Virtual sensors are applied within digital twins of heat pump to measure some parameter,
that cannot be measured with a physical sensor.
There have been developed multiple virtual sensors for applications on vapor compression
systems. In a review by Li et al. from 2011 more than 30 different virtual sensors are
listed [40]. The virtual sensors are for various types of vapor compression systems, such
as air-conditioners, chillers, heat pumps and so on. Although some of the sensors were
developed for a specific type of vapor compression system, they can be applied to other
types of vapor compression systems without major alterations to the underlying models.
Examples of virtual sensors that have been developed are virtual flow meters, virtual
refrigerant charge sensors, virtual pressure sensors and virtual fouling sensors.

3.1
Heat pumps and the vapor compression cycle
In this section the fundamentals of the vapor compression cycle are described.
Heat pumps remove heat from a low temperature source and lift the temperature of this
heat so that it can be supplied to a higher temperature sink.
The vapor compression cycle is one of the refrigeration cycles that is commonly used for
refrigeration and heat pump applications. In the cycle, a refrigerant flows, while undergo-
ing phase changes. For a fluid to undergo phase changes, energy has to be either added
or removed from the fluid. If this is done at constant pressure, the fluid will take up,
or expel, energy at constant temperature until it is fully evaporated or condensed. The
amount of energy needed to fully evaporate or condense a refrigerant is called the latent
heat of evaporation or condensation. In the evaporator, the refrigerant evaporates, and
absorbs energy from the source equal to the latent heat of evaporation, in addition to any
superheating of the refrigerant. When the refrigerant is fully evaporated, the pressure,
and temperature, is lifted through the compressor, where external work is applied. The
refrigerant is then cooled in the condenser, where the heat is supplied to the sink, equal
to the latent heat of condensation, in addition to any additional de-superheating and sub-

3.1.1
The state points
The red dots on Figure 3.1 are the state points of the cycle. If no superheating or sub-
cooling are assumed, there are four state points in a single stage vapor compression cycle.
Since it is not part of the scope of this thesis to build a model of the heat pump, the
derivation of the calculations for the state points will not be made here. Instead, the logic
behind the understanding of each state point will be made. To settle a thermodynamic
state, two independent variables have to be known. In the two phase region these vari-
ables cannot be the pressure and temperature, because these two are dependent in the
two phase region. Instead it can be the pressure and the enthalpy or the pressure and the
quality, as these are independent variables.
State point 1
The first state point is at the suction side of the compressor, or the exit of the evapora-
tor. The evaporation temperature is set, such that the temperature difference is suﬀicient
14
Challenges in digital twins of heat pumps
==PAGE:31==
between the source and the evaporation temperature.
The pressure in state point 1 is known as the saturation pressure at the evaporation tem-
perature, Psat(Tevap). If no superheating is assumed, the state is determined by equating
the quality to x1 = 1. If superheating is present, the state is determined by acknowledging
that the temperature must be T1 = Tevap + ∆TSH.
State point 2
State point 2 is at the discharge line of the compressor. From the condensation temperature
the condensation pressure is known as in state point 1 with Psat(Tcond).
If isentropic compression is assumed, the state is settled because the entropy in state point
1 and state point 2 are equal. If the compression is not isentropic the isentropic eﬀiciency
of the compressor is needed to calculate the enthalpy in state point 2.
State point 3
State point 3 is at the discharge of the condenser. The pressure is known, because it
is the same as in state point 2, if no pressure drop in the condenser is assumed. If no
subcooling is assumed, the state is determined by equating the quality to x3 = 0.
If
subcooling is present, the state is determined by acknowledging that the temperature
must be T3 = Tcond −∆TSC.
State point 4
State point 4 is after expansion in the expansion devise. The expansion is assumed isen-
thalpic, which means that the enthalpy is the same in state point 4 as it is in state point
3.
The pressure is also known as it is the same as in state point 1, if no pressure drop in the
evaporator is assumed.

The last component of the vapor compression cycle is the refrigerant. The function of the

The specific virtual sensors were chosen for various reasons. The virtual state point sensor

Inaccurate sensor calibration and uncertainty in measurements affect the operation of a

3.6
The Modelica modelling language, Dymola and TIL
In this section a short introduction will first be made to the Modelica language and the
Dymola environment and the library used in this thesis.
Modelica is an object oriented programming language specifically intended for modelling of
dynamic physical systems. The language utilizes objects in form of system components and
sub-components [48]. Modelica is used in a wide array of fields such as electrical circuits,
multi-body dynamics, hydraulic systems, and as in this work, thermal systems.
The
Modelica standard library is an open source library developed by the Modelica Association.
The library contains components in for a wide array of different types of systems, including
controls, fluid and thermal system. Some of these components are used in this work.
The Modelica language is implemented through a wide array of platforms, both open-
source and commercial platforms. In this work the Dymola platform, which is a commercial
platform, developed by Dassult Systemes, is used. Compared to open source platforms,
such as OpenModelica, Dymola offers a more user friendly interface, faster algorithms,
along with better debugging, plotting and visualization features.
The TIL Suite is a commercial library containing components for modelling of thermal
systems and the TILMedia property library [49].
TILMedia is based on REFPROP and
contains both thermodynamic and transport properties for various refrigerants and media.
The DaVe package is used for visualisation of some of the results in this thesis.

component stores all the fluids and substances that are used in the model. This makes it

SIM also calculates the mass of all the fluids and substances used in the model.

Filling station
The filling station model enables the possibility to control the total refrigerant mass within
the vapor compressor model. It does so by interacting with the SIM, that calculates the

The flow rate can be set in the settings as well as the maximum filling level. The start-

Pressure state
The pressure state is shown by index 13 on Figure 3.6. This component is added to the
models for numerical reasons and is not an active component in the model. As the symbol
for the component indicates, the component’s function relates to the change in pressure
over time, dp

The pressure state component is used to calculate the time derivative of the pressure
globally. Here, globally means that the time derivative of the pressure is calculated for
all the components and cells at the pressure level where the pressure state component is
located. If the pressure state was not implemented, the time derivative of the pressure
would have to be calculated for each cell in all components.
This means that the pressure state component imposes the assumption and simplification
to the models that the pressure propagates instantaneously throughout the pressure level,
instead of propagating with the speed of sound, as it would on a physical plant.
Sensors
Sensors in TIL have the same function as on physical plants. They are often used as input
for controllers and are also used for monitoring the processes in the model.
There are different sensors in the TIL library such as pressure sensors, temperature sensor
and flow sensors, which are common sensors, that also exist in a physical form. But there
are also mass fraction (quality) and state point sensors.
All the sensors use the TILMedia library to determine the output value of the sensor.
3.7
The given heat pump model
The heat pump model that is used in this work was developed in the bachelor’s thesis
by Pedersen and Wolf [42]. The model was developed as a digital twin to the physical
heat pump implemented in DTU Mechanical Engineering Section for Thermal Energy’s
experimental facilities. The physical heat pump was developed in cooperation between
DTU and Danfoss.
The model was developed in Dymola using the TIL library. The graphical representation
of the model is shown in Figure 3.5. The model is a single stage vapor compression cycle.
The active components of the model are two plate heat exchangers, a scroll compressor, a
filling station, pipes, an expansion valve, a superheating sensor and a controller.
The compressor is a scroll compressor, with the name ScrollCompressor, and extends
the PartialPhysicsBasedCompressor model in the TIL library. In the PartialPhysics-
BasedCompressor the ideal isentropic compression is combined with various losses, such
as pressure drop, leakage and friction, to model the real compression with losses. In the
ScrollCompressor model, the values for the loss factors have been adjusted to fit the real
compressor implemented in the experimental set up. The ScrollCompressor is different
from the PartialPhysicsBasedCompressor in that there is no refrigerant re-expansion,
no heat loss/ingress on the suction side, and the only heat loss is due to the friction be-
tween the scrolls. See Pedersen and Wolf’s thesis for further details.
The compressor in the given model is not controlled through a PI-controller, although the
RPM can be altered through the rotary input of the compressor.
The trapezoid source into the compressor is used to ramp up the compressor speed after

the filling station has altered the refrigerant charge of the system.
The heat exchangers are modelled with the Plate.VLEFluidLiquid.ParallelFlowHX
model in the TIL heat exchanger library.
The geometry and heat exchange value are

no control of the water flowing through the heat exchangers. The properties (flow rate,

The only controller on the heat pump model is the control of the super-heating degree.

3.8
Adjustments on heat pump model
In the present work the model described in the previous section was altered such that the
controls are more similar to what a real implementation of the booster heat pump would
be like. All the models of the individual components are unaltered. This means that the
nominal capacity of the booster heat pump is the same, but the heat pump is more flexible
than previously.
The first change made to the model was that the water flowing to the condenser and
Challenges in digital twins of heat pumps
31
==PAGE:48==
evaporator comes from the same source, as it would when implemented on a real district
heating network, see section 2.10. In the unaltered version of the model, the water flow-
ing through the heat exchangers comes from overdetermined boundaries, which are not
coupled. This means that in principle, the inlet temperature of the evaporator and the
condenser can be different, which is not the way it would be implemented on a real district
heating network. The change in flow to one heat exchanger does not affect the flow to the
other heat exchanger. Therefore, this change to the model, where the source is coupled,
more closely resembles the real implementation of the booster heat pump.
Secondly, the forward temperature from the booster heat pump is controlled by use of
a variable area valve controlled by a PI-controller. Previously, the forward temperature
was altered by manually adjusting the flow through the condenser.
Therefore, to get
the desired forward temperature, the required flow would have to be computed. In the
altered version of the model a valve installed downstream from the condenser regulates
the flow through the condenser by adjusting the opening degree, such that the set point
temperature can be reached.
Furthermore, the return temperature from the heat pump is controlled in the altered
model. This is done by adjusting the flow that the pump delivers to the booster heat
pump with a PI-controller.
A valve is also installed downstream from the evaporator. Because there is no pressure
drop in the heat exchangers, adding a valve only on one side would results in all the flow
going to the side without a valve. Adding the valve on the evaporator side ensures that
there is resistance on both sides, such that the pressure drop can be used to control the
flow to both heat exchangers.
Lastly, frequency control on the compressor was added. This makes it possible to control
the condenser load of the heat pump. The heat pump capacity can be extracted from the
condenser component within Dymola. A more realistic approach was chosen here, such
that the capacity is calculated by applying energy balance on the condenser, with a flow
sensor, and two temperature sensors.
Various sensors have been added, such that the control is possible.
Three additional
temperature measurements are necessary: The district heating temperature (from the
water pump), the forward temperature (from the condenser) and the return temperature
(from the evaporator). This is to control the temperatures and the load of the compressor.
A single flow sensor is also necessary for the capacity control. This is a flow sensor on the
condenser side. This sensor is used combined with two temperature sensors to calculate
the load on the condenser, and is used to capacity control the booster heat pump with the
frequency controlled compressor.
The state points in the model are not used for any control in the model. They are however
important for verification of the results and visualisation of the simulations.

Introducing sensor malfunctions
For the study on the heat pumps performance with sensor malfunctions, a way to introduce
sensor faults first had to be established.
The sensor malfunctions were introduced in Dymola by adding the sensor values from
the various sensors with a disturbance. The components that were used to introduce the
malfunction were the Ramp block combined with the MultiSum block. The Ramp block
signals a ramping up until the set value is reached. This simulates a bias or a drift of the
sensor. This signal from the Ramp block was then added to the signal from the sensor,

==PAGE:52==
such that the value delivered to the PI-controller was not the sensor value, but instead
the malfunctioning sensor value.
Other blocks than the ramp block can also be used to implement other types of mal-
functions. The UniformNoise block can be used to implement a precision degradation
malfunction. The UniformNoise block signals a normally distributed random value, where
the maximum and minimum value is defined as an input.

Introducing component malfunctions
Component malfunctions were introduced such that it would be possible to study the dis-
tinction between component malfunction and sensor malfunction. There were two com-
ponent malfunctions introduced into the model.
The first component malfunction that was introduced was valve leakage of the compressor.
Physically in a heat pump, valve leakage refers to leakage of refrigerant from the discharge
line to the suction line on the compressor [50].
The second component malfunction that was introduced was fouling of the condenser. The
fouling was implemented on the water side of the condenser.
The malfunctions were introduced into the models by altering the inputs into the compo-
nents.
In the compressor model it is possible to set the areaLeakage in the settings.
The
areaLeakage is the effective flow area for internal leakage. The higher the areaLeak-
age the higher the valve leakage within the compressor. This value had to be increased
from 1.35·10−7m2 to 50·10−7m2 to see any noticeable difference in the simulation results.
The fouling of the condenser was implemented by increasing the wall resistance and im-
plementing a pressure drop on the water side of the condenser. The wall resistance was
implemented by altering the material, such that the thermal conductivity was lowered.
The pressure drop was implemented by changing the pressure drop model of the con-
denser.

In this section the accuracy and robustness of the controls of the altered heat pump is

First, the capacity controller of the heat pump was tested. The rotational speed of the
compressor is used to control the capacity of the heat pump, which directly impacts the
heat load on the condenser.
While the set point condenser load was altered, the set
points for all the other controllers was kept constant at: Tf = 60 °C, Tr = 28.8 °C and
∆TSH = 5 K.
The condenser load at 50 Hz, which is the rated operating condition, is ∼13.9 kW. In the
simulations shown in Figure 4.3 the set point value for the controller is varied from +50%
to -50% of the rated condenser load, from ∼20 kW to ∼6.5 kW.
Figure 4.3a shows that the set point load and the actual load perfectly overlap.
The
rotational speed is 90 Hz at the highest capacity, and 25 Hz at the lowest capacity, as
shown in Figure 4.4b.
Only at the lowest capacities the control starts to get slightly unstable at the intersection
when changing from one condenser heat load to another.
Figure 4.4 shows how the capacity controller is able to deal with sudden large changes in
heat demand. The simulations were set up such that the capacity at the start is +50%
from the nominal condenser load, and at 300 s the condenser load drops to -50%. The
increase and drop in setpoint capacity then become smaller every 300 s.
At 600 s the system struggles to adequately adjust to a sudden increase of the capacity
from 6.5 kW to 17.5 kW. In fact, for the simulation to run at all, the change from 6.5 kW
to 17.5 kW had to occur over 2 s. Any faster than this, and the simulations crashed. The
model can handle a sudden decrease in capacity better than a sudden increase.
This is however still deemed acceptable, as such a large and sudden increase in demand is
unlikely for the application of the booster heat pump.

4.3
Sensor malfunctions
After the controls of the heat pump were tested and deemed suﬀicient, the effect of sensor
malfunctions on the heat pump could be tested.
In this section, sensor malfunctions will be introduced to the sensors.
Although there are multiple sensors installed on the heat pump, both on the refrigerant
and the water side, there are only five sensors that are used for controlling the heat pump.
These five sensors are therefore the only sensors, that directly influence the heat pump
operations if a sensor malfunction should occur.
These five sensors are: the district heating temperature sensor, the forward water temper-
ature sensor, the return water temperature sensor, the water flow sensor on the condenser
side and the superheating sensor.
The district heating temperature sensor is used when calculating the energy balance on the
condenser for capacity control of the compressor. The forward water temperature sensor is
used both to calculate the energy balance on the condenser and for controlling the forward
temperature from the heat pump. The return water temperature sensors is used for con-
trolling the return water temperature. The water flow sensor on the condenser side is also
used to calculate the energy balance on the condenser. Lastly, the superheating sensor is
used for controlling the superheating degree of the refrigerant out of the evaporator.
In all simulations in section 4.3 the setpoint values for the controllers were held constant.
The set point values and other simulation inputs are shown in Table 4.8. The values were
chosen such that they are close to, what they actual operations of a booster heat pump
would have been, and the controllers are stable at a large range around these values. The
refrigerant charge was set such that the subcooling in the condenser was 5 K.
The value ˙Qcond is used to control the rotational speed of the compressor.

4.3.1
Steady state response to sensor malfunctions
In this section, the steady state response of sensor malfunctions on the heat pump will be
presented for one of the crucial sensors at a time.
Malfunction of forward water temperature sensor
The first sensor where sensor malfunctions were introduced was the forward water tem-
perature sensor.
The bias of the forward water temperature sensor has little effect on the evaporation tem-
perature, as shown on Figure 4.9a. On the other hand, the effect on the condensation
temperature is quite large. At 5 K bias of the sensor, the condensation temperature de-
creases more than −4 K. This is because the sensor signals to the controller, that the
temperature is 60 °C, while the true temperature is 55 °C, and the condensing tempera-
ture decreases accordingly. With a bias of −5 K the condensation temperature increases
by 4 K.
Figure 4.9b shows the steady state effect of a bias or drift of the forward temperature
sensor on the heat pump operations.
The bias of the sensor has an apparent linear relation on most of the parameters shown.
The largest relative change happens to the COP and the compressor work. A 5 K bias of
the sensor results in a more than 10% increase in the COP, and a close to 10% decrease
in the compressor work. The reason for the increase in COP and decrease in compressor
work, is that the real forward water temperature decreases with 5 K to 55 °C. As a result
of this the condenser pressure decreases, and the lift that the compressor has to deliver
decreases. The decrease of the compressor work, while maintaining the same condenser
load results in an increased COP.
The eﬀiciency of the compressor decreases with decreased compressor work. This is what
is expected, as the relative losses are greater at lower compressor capacities.
The condenser load is constant, as it is controlled by the compressor. The evaporator load
slightly increases with a positive sensor bias.

4.3.2
Multiple sensor malfunctions
The effect of multiple sensor malfunctions in steady state was also studied. These simu-
lations were done as parameter sweeps in Dymola.
On Figure 4.14a the COP is shown with varying biases of the forward and return tem-
perature sensors. Here, the rampF.height is the bias of the forward temperature sensor,
and rampR.height is the bias of the return temperature sensor. The largest effect on the
COP occurs when the bias of the two sensors is opposite, i.e. when one is negative and
the other is positive. When the forward temperature sensor has a bias of −5 K and the
return temperature sensor has a bias of 5 K, the COP is 2.4, compared to the base line of
4.17. In the opposite case, the COP is 5.65.
The main reason for this is that when the forward temperature sensor has a negative bias
and the return temperature sensor has a positive bias the pressure difference between the
high a low pressures is the largest, and the compressor work therefore the highest. In the
opposite case, the pressure difference between the high and low pressures is the lowest.
On Figure 4.14b the COP is shown as a function of the bias of the water flow sensor on
the condenser side and the bias of the district heating temperature sensor.
Here, rampV.height is the bias of the water flow sensor and rampDH.height is the bias of

Malfunction of forward temperature sensor
Figure 4.15 shows the response of the heat pump to a 5 K bias in the forward temperature
sensor. The bias of 5 K means that the signal the sensor sends to the controller is 5 K
higher than the actual temperature. The bias occurs at 50 s in the simulation, with a
duration of 5 s.
The sensor continues to signal to the controller that the temperature is 60 °C, while the
actual forward temperature is 55 °C, as shown in Figure 4.15a.
State point 2 is at the refrigerant inlet of the condenser and state point 3 is at the refriger-

reason for this is that the decrease in forward temperature causes a decrease in pressure
in the condenser. This decrease in pressure decreases the condensation temperature and
the temperature in state point 2. The reason that the temperature in state point 3 is not
affected greatly is because the subcooling degree decreases from 5 K to less than 2 K.
The actual condenser capacity decreases from 14 kW to 11 kW. The reason for this is
that the temperature difference across the condenser decreases to 20 °C while still being
perceived as 25 °C.
The COP of the heat pump increases from 4.1 to 5.5. But this is because the lift of the
compressor decreases, because of the lower condensing temperature.
Because of the bias of the forward temperature sensor of 5 K, the heat pump does not
deliver the desired forward temperature nor the desired capacity.

4.3.4
Rule based sensor malfunction detection based on observations
from simulations of sensor malfunctions
In this section the observations from the simulations on the heat pump with sensor mal-
functions were used to generate some rules, that can be implemented in a sensor detection
algorithm,
The simulations of malfunctions in the sensors, that are used for control in the heat pump
give some insights into how the heat pump operations are affected by sensor malfunctions
and how sensor malfunctions can be detected.
A bias or drift of the forward and return water temperature sensors causes a change in
the condensation or evaporation temperature and pressure, without a perceived change
in the forward or return temperatures of the water. The condensation and evaporation
temperature are highly coupled to the forward and return water temperatures. Therefore,
if a change in the high or low side pressure is observed without a change to the perceived
water outlet temperatures, this could be an indication of a sensor malfunction.
A change in high or low side pressure can have other causes than sensor malfunction. This
can for example be a change in capacity or a change in district heating temperature. So
if an algorithm were to be implemented, that supervises the high and low side pressure
compared to the perceived forward and return temperatures, these other factors will have
to be taken into account.
Based on these insights, some rules can be made that can be implemented into an algo-
rithm for detection of sensor malfunctions. These rules can only be used for detection of
malfunctions on the sensors that are used for control of the heat pump. This is because
the sensors that are used for control of the heat pump are the only sensors that actually
affect the operations of the heat pump.
These rules are outlined on Table 4.10. In this work, there was only time to implement
these rule based algorithms on the forward and return water temperature sensors. It would
be optimal to implement a similar algorithm on the district heating temperature sensor
as well, as it also affects the heat pump operations to a certain degree.
To detect a sensor error in the forward temperature sensor, the observed variable is the
high side pressure, or pressure in state point 3, p3. The pressure in state point 3 has
the strongest coupling to the forward temperature, the district heating temperature and
the condenser load. The coupling between the forward temperature and the pressure in
state point 3 is by far the strongest, followed by the condenser load and lastly the district
heating temperature.
To get the most accurate forward temperature sensor malfunction detection, a 3D array
should be constructed with the expected pressure in state point 3 at any given forward
temperature, district heating temperature and condenser load. But since the coupling is
strongest between the forward temperature and the pressure, a simple lookup table with
the expected pressure at a given forward temperature might be suﬀicient.
The rule to detect a forward temperature sensor malfunction is that the difference between
the expected pressure at the given operating condition and the actual pressure should not
be greater than the innate inaccuracy of the pressure sensor. If the difference is greater
than the limit, this is an indication of a sensor malfunction.
The rule for detection of sensor malfunction of the return temperature sensor is similar
to the forward temperature malfunction rule. As with the forward temperature sensor,
it is the pressure, in this case the low side pressure, that is the observed variable for
detection of return temperature sensor malfunction. Here the pressure in state point 1

return temperature, but also a quite strong coupling to the district heating temperature

heating temperatures and the condenser load.
As with the forward temperature sensor, the rule to detect a return temperature sensor
malfunction is that the difference between the expected pressure at the given operating

On Figure 4.20 the pseudocode for the rule based sensor malfunction detection algorithm
is outlined. The example algorithm is for the forward temperature sensor, but it would
be similar for the return temperature sensor.
The inputs into the algorithm are four sensor measurements from the heat pump. These
are the forward temperature sensor value, the district heating temperature sensor value,
the condenser load based on energy balance and the high side pressure.
Based on the first three inputs, the expected pressure value is calculated based on either
a lookup table, or a data fit to experiments.
The difference between the actual measured pressure and the expected pressure at the
operating condition is calculated. If this difference is larger than the innate sensor inac-
curacy, this is an indication of a forward temperature sensor malfunction.
Implementation of this algorithm presumes that the sensors that are used as input into
the algorithm have no malfunction. If the sensors are malfunctioning, this algorithm will
wrongly detect a sensor malfunction on one of the sensors that are used for control of the
heat pump.
Because the forward and return temperatures are so strongly coupled with the high and
low pressure, there is no need train the model. Lookup tables with the expected pressures
with a certain temperature can be generated from a few simulations, and these can be
used for the detection of sensor malfunctions.
For the simple lookup table, where the expected pressure, pexp(T), only is a function of
either the high or low temperature, there was made one sweep with the pressure as a func-
tion of the temperature. This table, with the pressure as the function of the temperature
was then used in the algorithm with the simple lookup table.
For the more complicated lookup table, where the pressure is a function of the forward/re-
turn temperature, the condenser load and the district heating temperature, the expected
pressure was generated with multiple simulations in Dymola. A total of nine simulations

4.6
Uncertainty propagation analysis
All sensors have some uncertainty associated with the measurements. These uncertain-
ties are not directly sensor malfunctions, but rather a natural phenomena associated with
all measurements. But just like sensor malfunctions propagate, sensor uncertainties also
propagate and cause an uncertainty in the evaluation of the virtual sensor.
Temperature and pressure sensors are used as inputs into all the virtual sensors in this
thesis. A flow rate sensor are also used in the evaluation of the condenser load.
Temperature sensors have an accuracy that is typically ±0.5 K, independent of the mea-
surement range. Pressure sensor accuracy is dependent on the range in which it is mea-
sured, usually around ±0.5%-±1%. Flow rate sensors accuracy depends on the phase of
the media and the type of sensor, but the accuracy is usually in the range of ±2%-±5%.
Based on the uncertainties described above, the innate uncertainty of the virtual sensors
applied in this work can be calculated.
The evaluation of the enthalpy with the virtual state point sensors is not affected greatly
by the uncertainty of the sensor’s inputs, with a maximum uncertainty of 0.09% and
0.07%, at the two tested state points, state point 1 and state point 2. This is similar to
the results shown in the previous sections, where these state point sensors were not greatly
affected by sensor malfunctions.
The uncertainty of the virtual subcooling sensor is directly proportionate to the uncer-
tainty of the underlying temperature sensor of 0.5 K. Additionally the uncertainty of the
underlying pressure sensor adds an uncertainty of 0.34 K, which gives the subcooling sen-
sor a total uncertainty of 0.84 K.
The refrigerant charge sensor only has one sensor input, which is the virtual subcooling
sensor. The uncertainty of the virtual subcooling sensor gives the refrigerant charge sensor
a total uncertainty of 0.23%. This error is so small, that it does not play a role, if the
virtual refrigerant charge sensor were implemented on a real plant.
The virtual refrigerant mass flow sensor has many of underlying sensors and virtual sen-
sors. The total uncertainty propagation results in a total uncertainty of the virtual mass
flow sensor of 3.96%.
The virtual temperature sensor has multiple sensors and virtual sensors as input. The
largest uncertainties is associated with the underlying virtual mass flow sensor and the
water flow sensors, which both have large uncertainties in the range of 2-5%. This results
in a total uncertainty of the virtual temperature sensor of 1.5-2.3 K, depending on if is
applied on the condenser or the evaporator side.
These values can be used as the lower bound to when a malfunction is detected. If the
evaluated parameter is within the bounds described on the table it can be due to innate
inaccuracies of the underlying sensors and virtual sensors.

4.7
Sensor malfunction detection
In this section, the sensor malfunction detection methods implemented and tested in this
thesis will be recapped. First, the different methods will be described, and after this,
a proposed architecture for the implementation of the fault detection methods will be
described.
The focus here will be on the temperature sensors, because these are most crucial to the
controls implemented on the booster heat pump in this thesis. The supply and return
temperatures and the capacity are the parameters being controlled for on the booster
heat pump. The detection of malfunctions in the temperature sensors therefore the most
crucial for this booster heat pump implemented in this thesis.
Rule based sensor malfunction detection
The rule based temperature sensor malfunction detection algorithms were developed and
tested in subsection 4.3.4. The fault detection algorithm is a qualitative model based diag-
nostics method [7]. The rule based sensor malfunction detection is based on observations
on how the heat pump reacts to a malfunction of a temperature sensor, specifically the
forward and return temperature sensors.
The rule based temperature sensor malfunction detection algorithm compares the pressure
sensor reading with a lookup table value of the expected pressure at the given operating
conditions. A malfunction of the water temperature sensors is detected when the pressure
deviates from the expected pressure more than the limit.
Virtual pressure sensor
The virtual pressure sensor is described in subsection 3.4.2 and tested in subsection 4.4.2.
This virtual sensor is a quantitative white box diagnostics model.
Temperature readings in state points 3 and 4 are used as input into the virtual sensors.
To get the pressure, the temperature readings are used as input into a saturation pressure
function, which gives the high and low pressures as output.
Virtual temperature sensor
The virtual temperature sensor described in section subsection 3.4.6 and tested in subsec-
tion 4.4.6. This virtual sensor is also a quantitative white box diagnostics model.
The temperature is evaluated by calculating the heat flow from the refrigerant side to the
water. When the heat flow is known, the outlet temperature of the water is calculated
based on energy balance on the heat exchanger.

Component malfunctions
In this section simulations of the booster heat pump with component malfunctions intro-
duced into the model will be presented. The goal is to establish, if it is possible to visibly
distinguish between component malfunctions and sensor malfunctions.
The first component malfunction that was introduced was valve leakage of the compressor.
With valve leakage of the compressor, there is a leakage of refrigerant from the discharge
side to the suction side of the compressor.
With valve leakage, the expected result would be, that the discharge temperature in-
creases, because the isentropic eﬀiciency decreases with valve leakage. Therefore, a sensor
malfunction of the discharge line temperature sensor, T2, was simulated for comparison, to
see, if it is possible to distinguish between compressor malfunction and sensor malfunction.
Figure 4.57 shows the pressure enthalpy diagrams for the simulations with compressor
valve leakage and malfunction of the temperature sensor in state point 2, respectively.
Shown in red is the cycle with no malfunctions; that is no compressor valve leakage and
no temperature sensor malfunction.
On Figure 4.57a it is shown that the valve leakage in the compressor results in a higher
discharge temperature, as expected. The low pressure increases and the high pressure
decreases as a results of the compressor valve leakage. It is clear that the compressor
valve leakage has an influence on the entire cycle.
On Figure 4.57b the cycle with 5 K bias of the temperature sensor in state point 2 is
shown. Clearly, the temperature in state point 2 is higher than in the base cycle without
malfunctions, as expected.
It is clear, that a valve leakage of the compressor can be distinguished from a temperature
sensor bias, as the entire cycle changes, both the temperatures and the pressure levels
with valve leakage in the compressor.
However, if the pressures are unknown, due to pressure sensors not being mounted, it might
have been diﬀicult to distinguish between a compressor malfunction and a temperature
sensor bias, as the discharge temperatures are similar in both cases.
The second malfunction that was introduced was condenser fouling. On a physical plant,
fouling is the accumulation of unwanted material on the heat exchanger surface. Fouling
results in increased heat transfer resistance and increased pressure drop in the heat ex-
changer.
With condenser fouling it would be expected that the COP decreases. The condensing
temperature should increase, because the temperature difference would increase with a
decreased overall heat transfer coeﬀicient, with the LMTD method.
Figure 4.58 shows the pressure enthalpy diagrams for the simulations with condenser foul-
ing and a temperature sensor malfunction of the forwards water temperature bias of −2 K.
Again, the simulations without malfunctions are shown in red, and the simulations with
malfunctions are shown in green.
Figure 4.58a shows that, as expected, the condenser fouling results in a higher condensing
temperature in comparison to the base case. The high pressure side enthalpy difference
decreases, which indicates a higher compressor mass flow, as the condenser load is con-
trolled for. This indicates, that the COP of the heat pump has decreased.
Figure 4.58b shows the pressure enthalpy diagram for the simulations with the forward
temperature sensor bias of −2 K. The setpoint value for the forward temperature is 60 °C,
which means, that with the bias, the true temperature of the forward water is 62 °C. The
high pressure increases as a result of the higher forward temperature.
Looking exclusively at the pressure enthalpy diagrams, it is hard to distinguish between

Discussion
The altered model gave very similar results to given model by Pedersen and Wolf, when
simulated [42]. This was the basis for continuing the work with the sensor malfunctions.
The small deviations in the results were due to set points into the controllers. In the
model by Pedersen and Wolf, the return and forwards temperatures, and the condenser
load were not controlled.
This meant that when the altered model was simulated for
comparison with the given model, the outputs from the given model were used as input
into the altered model. Here, some decimal points were omitted causing small differences
in the two simulations. But overall, the results from the simulations were very similar,
and within acceptable deviation.
The model by Pedersen and Wolf has the limitation, that it uses constant heat transfer
coeﬀicient in the heat exchangers despite varying operating conditions. Pedersen and Wolf
did parameter studies with varying district heating, forward and return temperatures. The
results showed that the overall heat transfer coeﬀicient did not deviate much more than
15% from the overall heat transfer coeﬀicient used in the model. However, the work by
Pedersen and Wolf did not account for varying rotational speed of the compressor. This
was not changed in this work either. Most likely, the rotational speed of the compressor
has a larger impact on the overall heat transfer coeﬀicients of the heat exchangers, than
the water temperatures.
In a paper by Yan et al. it was demonstrated that a doubling of the mass flux in a plate
type condenser resulted in a 30% increase of the heat transfer coeﬀicient on the refrigerant
side [53]. A 30% increase in the heat transfer coeﬀicient on the refrigerant side would
result in a 20% increase of the overall heat transfer coeﬀicient on the condenser in the
model. Although a 20% increase in the overall heat transfer coeﬀicient is quite significant,
it should be noted that this is when doubling the refrigerant mass flux. In most cases in
this thesis, the mass flow is withing 10% of the range used as input in the heat exchanger
selection software by Pedersen and Wolf.
The malfunction of the forward temperature sensor had multiple impacts on the heat
pump. Firstly, the condensation temperature is closely coupled to the forward temper-
ature. The condensation temperature changes almost proportionately to the size of the
forward temperature sensor malfunction. This is what was expected, as the condensation
temperature is dependent on the water inlet and outlet temperatures. But when the out-
let temperature is suﬀiciently higher than the water inlet temperature, the water outlet
temperature becomes dominant in determining the condensation temperature.
Because the bias of the forward temperature sensor has such a large impact on the con-
densation temperature, it has a large impact on the lift of the compressor. When there
is a positive bias of the forward temperature sensor, the real forward temperature drops,
which causes the condensation temperature to drop, and this causes a drop in the com-
pressor lift. This means that the COP increases, and the compressor work decreases with
a positive bias, and vice versa. The negative consequence is of course the required process
parameter is not reached.
The malfunctioning of the return temperature sensor has similar affects on the heat pump
operations as with the forward temperature sensor.
As with the forward temperature
sensor, a positive bias of the return temperature sensor causes the evaporation temperature
drop.
But the effect is inverse as with the forward temperature sensor, as a drop in
98
Challenges in digital twins of heat pumps
==PAGE:115==
evaporation temperature increases the lift, causing the compressor work to increase and
the COP to drop.
A malfunction of the district heating temperature sensor does not have a large effect on
the condensation and evaporation temperatures. This is because the forward and return
temperature set points have a much large impact on the condensation and evaporation
temperature, respectively, than the district heating temperature. Moreover, a malfunc-
tion of the district heating temperature sensor does not alter the actual district heating
temperature, as with the other two temperature sensors.
On the other hand, a bias of the district heating temperature sensor does have a large
impact on the compressor work. This is because the district heating sensor is used to
evaluate the energy balance on the condenser to calculate the condenser load. A change
to the perceived district heating also changes the perceived condenser load, which cause
the compressor to do more or less work, depending on if the district heating sensor has a
positive or negative bias.
The magnitude of the impact that the bias of district heating temperature is dependent
on the temperature difference across the condenser. With a larger temperature difference,
a certain bias of the district heating temperature sensor has less impact on the energy
balance on the condenser. This means, that if the district heating supply temperature is
25 °C, and the forward temperature is 60 °C, the effect of a bias of, say, 5 K is smaller
than if the district heating supply temperature was 40 °C and the forward temperature
was 60 °C.
The malfunction of the condenser water flow sensor has a similar, but inverse effect of the
district heating temperature sensor. This is because this sensor is also exclusively used to
evaluate the condenser load. However, it requires a 25% bias of the water flow sensor for
it to have a similar impact that a 5 K bias of the district heating sensor.
The malfunctioning of the superheating sensor has the least effect on the heat pump of
all the sensors. Because the superheating sensor is only used to ensure that superheated
refrigerant enters the compressor, a malfunction of this sensor does not have a large impact
on the entire system. However, compressor work on the refrigerant increases with positive
bias of the superheating sensor. This is because with a positive sensor bias, the actual
superheating decreases, which causes an increase in the compressor work.
Multiple sensor malfunctions prove to have a larger effect on the COP of the heat pump
than a single malfunction. The lowest COP occurred when the difference in pressures
between the high and low pressure is increased as a result of the biases.
The rule based sensor malfunction detection algorithm was developed based on the results
from the sensor malfunction simulations.
The algorithm proved to be able to detect
temperature sensor malfunctions for all of the test cases, with sensor faults down to 1.5 K.
The forward temperature sensor algorithm could be implemented with only having the
pressure as a function of the forward temperature, and still be suﬀiciently accurate. This
is however not true for the return temperature sensor algorithm, as the return temperature
is coupled to the condenser load and district heating temperature to a higher degree.
The virtual state point sensors were all accurate, when no sensor malfunction was present.
With sensor malfunctions, all of the virtual state point sensors were accurate at small
biases, apart from virtual state point sensor 4. As mentioned, this is because this virtual
state point sensor is in the two phase region, where pressure and temperature are coupled,
and since only pressure and temperature are used as input, sensor malfunctions cause

input instead of the temperature in state point 4. In this way the virtual state point sensor
would be as stable as state point sensor 3.
The virtual pressure sensor based on the temperature in state point 4 was accurate in
all of the conditions tested. On the other hand, the virtual pressure sensor based on the
temperature in state point 3 was not accurate in conditions where the subcooling was
affected. This was especially when varying the district heating temperature and when
varying the refrigerant charge.
This would not be an issue in systems with a liquid receiver and therefore no subcooling.
To combat this issue in the booster heat pump in this work, regression of the subcooling

The virtual refrigerant charge sensor was implemented in three different version, where
the parameters were based on physical values, default parameters and tuned parameters
based on regression. The tuned version of the virtual refrigerant charge sensor proved
to be the most accurate in predicting the actual refrigerant charge. However, all of the
versions were able to predict if the system was over- or undercharged in comparison to the
rated refrigerant charge.
A sensor bias of the temperature in state point 3 of ±5 K resulted in a error in the
evaluation of the refrigerant charge of ±10%. A sensor bias of the pressure in state point
3 of ±2 bar resulted in a error in the evaluation of the refrigerant charge of ±10%.
In the modelling of the virtual refrigerant charge, the term with the superheating was
excluded, because there is an electronic expansion valve installed. Because of the electronic
expansion valve, the operating superheating and the rated superheating are always equal,
which causes the term in the equation where the superheating is accounted for to equal
zero. In the case of superheating sensor malfunction, this is still true, as the perceived
superheating will still be at the setpoint value, because the controller adjusts to the bias.
The real superheating degree will deviate from perceived superheating degree, but this can
not be observed unless a virtual superheating degree sensor is implemented for verification
of the superheating temperature sensor.
The virtual compressor mass flow sensor was implemented in three versions. The version
with the most accurate prediction of the mass flow was the sensor where the heat loss from
the compressor was based on a heat transfer coeﬀicient and the temperature difference
between the ambient and discharge temperatures.
This may however not be possible
to implement on a real plant, as the heat transfer coeﬀicient is not easily obtainable.
Instead, it would be more practical to use a constant heat loss factor, if the operating
conditions do not vary too much. If the operating conditions vary widely, regression will
have to be implemented with the heat loss factor as a function of the suction and discharge
temperatures and pressures, or the compressor work.
The virtual temperature sensor was able to predict the temperatures at the water outlets
of both heat exchangers accurately. It was also able to detect sensor malfunction of both
water temperature sensors simultaneously and independently.
Due to the many underlying sensors and virtual sensor of the virtual temperature sensor,
malfunctions of the underlying sensors can have quite a large impact on the evaluation of
the virtual temperature. One example is that a bias of −5 K on the temperature sensor
in state point 2 results in a 10 K error in the evaluation of the virtual temperature on the
condenser outlet.

Importantly, for both the virtual sensor applied on the condenser and the evaporator side,
is that they rely on an accurate reading of the district heating temperature, as it is used
in both equations. This emphasizes the importance of applying fault detection on district
heating temperature sensor as well. Applying the virtual temperature sensor on the dis-
trict heating temperature sensor would result in circular reasoning, as the district heating
temperature is used for the other virtual temperature sensors.
A rule based malfunction algorithm could be implemented for the district heating tem-
perature sensor. The district heating has strong coupling to the subcooling, which could
be a possible coupling factor for the rule based district heating temperature algorithm.
But other parameters, such as the refrigerant charge, also have a strong coupling to the
subcooling. So additional coupling factors would have to be found for the implementation
of a rule based algorithm for the district heating temperature sensor. The details of this
have not been worked out in this work.
Pressure drop in the condenser and the evaporator was neglected in the model. If the
pressure drop was included, this would change how all the equations for the virtual sensors
would have to be formulated. There are only two pressure sensors implemented on the
booster heat pump model, one at the condenser outlet and the other at the evaporator
outlet. This means, that, if pressure drop is included, the pressures in state points 2 and
4 would be unknown.
There are ways to get around this. The most obvious way would be to install pressure
sensors in state points 2 and 4, and use these as input into the equations.
If this is not a possibility, the easiest method would be to implement a constant pressure
drop, where the normal operating conditions are used. This would be available from heat
exchanger selection software.
A correlation for pressure drop in plate heat exchangers could also be implemented for
more accurate results.
The uncertainty propagation analysis was done to demonstrate that even without sensor
malfunctions, there are uncertainties associated with the virtual sensors. These uncertain-
ties arise because of the innate uncertainty of the physical sensors.
Most significantly, the virtual temperature sensor has a maximum uncertainty of 1.5-2.3 K,
depending on where it is applied. This is because this virtual sensor has many underlying
sensors and virtual sensors, that all have a certain uncertainty associated with them. Most
notably, the virtual mass flow sensor, that is used as input into the virtual temperature
sensor has a maximum uncertainty of 4%.
It should be noted that these are the maximum uncertainties, and that the probability of
these uncertainties occurring is small, because it requires that all the underlying sensors
and virtual sensors are at their maximum uncertainty at the same time. Therefore, to get
the real limit of these virtual sensors for fault detection, the probability density function
of the each of the physical sensors should be used. This was however outside of the scope
of this work.
The proposed solutions for detection of sensor malfunctions all have the limitations, that
they rely on other sensors as input for detection of the malfunctions. Some of them depend
on multiple underlying sensors and virtual sensors. The many layers of sensors and virtual
sensors increases the risk of missed detection of sensor malfunctions and also increases the
risk of a wrong sensor malfunction detection.
Principles from risk management can be implemented to avoid these risks. One such prin-
ciple is system redundancy.
Redundancy is the inclusion of extra components which are not strictly necessary to func-
tioning, in case of failure in other components.
Redundancy could be introduced in

multiple ways. The most obvious way to introduce redundancy is to install multiple of
the sensors that are most essential for control and can be used for detection of sensor
malfunctions. The second way to introduce redundancy is have multiple virtual sensors
and/or algorithms to detect sensor malfunctions in the same sensors.
This was the reasoning behind the design of the proposed architecture for sensor mal-
function detection. Here, multiple sensors and virtual sensors are used to verify the other
virtual sensors, and only when all of the virtual sensors agree that there is a sensor mal-
function it can be concluded that a sensor malfunction has been detected.
Similar architectures, where the principle of redundancy is used for sensor malfunction
detection in HVAC equipment, have been presented in other works [54] [55]. But gener-
ally, these methods have been based on data driven, black box models for the detection
of sensor malfunctions. The methods presented in this work have the advantage that they
generally do not require too much training data, compared to the pure data driven meth-
ods. A total of 20-30 steady state training runs would be enough to train the rule based
fault detection algorithms, depending on the range of the operations of the specific heat
pump. These runs could also be used to train the virtual mass flow sensor. The virtual
pressure- and temperature sensor do not require any training at all.
It can be diﬀicult to distinguish between component degradation and sensor malfunctions
malfunction in some cases. Two examples were presented in this project where compressor
valve leakage and condenser fouling were introduced into the booster heat pump model.
Both of these malfunctions could be interpreted as a sensor malfunction. For example, in
the rule based algorithm introduced in this work, condenser fouling could be interpreted
as a water temperature sensor malfunction, because fouling increases the pressure in the
condenser.
This fact emphasizes the importance of implementing systems for detection of component
malfunctions along with the sensor malfunction detection systems. In the literature there
are multiple virtual sensors that have been developed for detection of component malfunc-
tions.
One example of a virtual sensor for component malfunction detection is the compressor
module by described by Li and Braun [44]. Here, the discharge temperature is used as
the decoupling factor. The expected discharge temperature is calculated based on the
compressor polynomial, and if the sensor reading of the discharge temperature deviates
from the expected discharge temperature, this is an indication of compressor malfunction.
Other virtual sensors have also been developed for fouling of heat exchangers, defective
expansion valves, pipeline blockages and other components [56].

6
Conclusion
6.1
Findings
The booster heat pump model was altered, with the implementation of controls of the
forward and return temperatures, as well as the condenser load control. For this to be
possible sensors had to be implemented, so that the parameters that were controlled for
could be observed. A total of four sensors were implemented on the booster heat pump,
so that the control was possible. An additional six sensors, two pressure sensors and four
temperature sensors, were implemented within the heat pump, for use on analysis later in
the thesis and for implementation of the virtual sensors.
The altered model was tested in comparison to the original given model, and this showed,
that the altered model still gave the same results after alteration.
The controls of the heat pump was tested in a wide range of conditions. This verified
that the controls worked as intended, and that the work with sensor malfunction could be
commenced.
Sensor malfunctions were introduced on the sensors that were used to control the heat
pump.
A bias of the forward temperature sensor of −5 K proved to result in a decrease of the COP
of 10%, and a bias of 5 K resulted in an increase of the COP of 11%. More importantly,
with a bias of the forward temperature sensor, the setpoint temperatures were not reached.
A bias of the return temperature sensor of 5 K resulted a decrease of the COP of 14%.
The reason for the change in COP from the bias of the forward and return temperature
sensors was identified as a change in high and low pressure, respectively, changing the lift,
and work, of the compressor as a result.
The district heating and the water flow sensor on the condenser side were used for con-
denser load control. A malfunction of these sensors caused a fault in the evaluation of the
condenser load. A 3 K bias of the district heating temperature sensor resulted in a 12%
decrease of the COP, while a -25% bias of the water flow sensor resulted in a 10% decrease
of the COP.
The malfunctioning of the superheating sensor had the least affect on the performance of
the heat pump, with a 5 K bias resulting in a 4% decrease of the COP.
Multiple sensor malfunctions proved to have a larger effect on the heat pump operations
than single sensor malfunctions.
The simulations of the booster heat pump with sensor malfunctions gave insight into
how the heat pump reacts to a sensor malfunction.
Based on these insights, a rule-
based algorithm was developed, that identified forward and return temperature sensor
malfunctions based on changes to the high and low pressures.
The rule-based algorithm was tested in various operating conditions, and proved to be
able to detect biases down to 1.5 K for both the forward and return temperature sensors.
Five different virtual sensors were implemented and tested. They all proved to be able
to predict the modelled parameter under normal operating conditions. The virtual sen-
sors that were implemented were: state point sensor, pressure sensor, subcooling sensor,
refrigerant charge sensor, mass flow sensor and temperature sensor.
Malfunctions were introduced to the underlying physical sensors of the virtual sensors.
This proved that the virtual sensors are dependent on accurate sensor readings, as the
Challenges in digital twins of heat pumps
103
==PAGE:120==
introduction of malfunctions resulted in the virtual sensors being unable to accurately
estimate the modeled parameters.
An analysis of the uncertainty of the virtual sensors, because of the innate inaccuracy of the
underlying physical sensors, was conducted. This proved that, even without malfunctions,
the virtual sensors have uncertainty associated with them. This uncertainty can be used
as the lower bound, for when the virtual sensors for certain have detected a malfunction.
An architecture for the detection of sensor malfunctions was proposed. This architecture
combines some of the virtual sensors and the rule based algorithm for detection of sensor
malfunctions. This reduces the risk of a wrong detection of a sensor malfunction, because
the different fault detection methods have to agree before a fault detection for certain has
been detected.
Lastly, component malfunctions were introduced into the model. The simulations with
component malfunctions were compared to simulations with sensor malfunctions. Certain
component malfunctions can be interpreted as sensor malfunctions. Especially fouling of
the condenser can be interpreted as a forward temperature malfunctions by the rule-based
algorithm.
This illustrated the importance of the implementation of fault detection methods for the
components as well, if the fault detection methods for the sensor are to be accurate.
6.2
Future work
The details of the rules for the algorithm for detection of malfunctions of the district
heating temperature sensor have to be worked out. The bias of the district heating tem-
perature sensor has a strong coupling to the compressor work and the subcooling. But
the exact way to establish this algorithm was not done in this work.
For more accurate results of the effect of sensor malfunctions, the heat exchanger model
on the booster heat pump model should be extended. The heat transfer models of the
heat exchangers should be changed so they account for varying flow rates on both the
water and refrigerant sides. Furthermore, a pressure drop model for the heat exchangers
could be implemented.
The proposed sensor malfunction detection architecture can be expanded to include com-
ponent fault detection methods. This should be done to verify, that the detection of sensor
malfunctions in fact are sensor malfunctions and not component malfunctions.
