Abstract
Hydrogen economy and ”green” hydrogen is thought to become a significant contributor
in the transition towards a more sustainable energy sector [1].
Especially for energy
storage purposes with the intrinsically fluctuating power supply from wind and solar. The
proton exchange membrane electrolyzer cell (PEMEC) offers advantages over the alkaline
electrolyzer cell (AEC), notably through its ability to handle dynamic power input without
degrading [2]. A significant drawback of the PEMEC is its use of scarce metals, such
as platinum in the cathode and iridium in the anode. Finding new electrocatalysts, or
increasing the intrinsic activity (or mass activity) of these scarce metals, is critical in
facilitating a widespread use of this technology [2].
This work investigates the electrocatalytic activity of small mass-selected entities in the
form of platinum single atoms and dimers deposited on polycrystalline gold. The single
atoms and dimers offer the significant advantage of utilizing virtually all the catalyst,
and exhibit fundamentally different electronic structure [3]. The single atoms and dimers
were deposited with a cluster source system, connected to a time-of-flight mass filter.
HER, HOR, ORR and OER activity was measured, but as the samples showed hardly any
activity for ORR and OER, the project later focused on only HER measurements. A total
of 9 SA and 4 dimer samples, both with two different mass loadings of 13 ng/cm2 and
4.5 ng/cm2, were measured. The activity was measured through cyclic voltammetry and
chronoamperometry in an RDE setup, in two different electrolytes; 0.1 M perchloric acid
and 0.5 M sulfuric acid. The TOF of hydrogen at 100 mV overpotential ranged typically
between 100-300 H2 s−1, but one sample also showed TOF as high as 600 H2 s−1. The
activities were approximately 10 times lower than previous HER measurements of 3.8 nm
nanoparticles deposited on HOPG [4].

Resumé
Brint økonomi og ”grøn” hydrogen er spået til at blive en signifikant faktor i overgangen til
en mere bæredygtig energisektor [1]. Specielt i forbindelse med lagring af energi, hvilket er
essentielt med et fluktuerende energi input fra vind og sol. Proton-udvekslings-membran
elektrolyse cellen (PEMEC) tilbyder fordele over basiske elektrolyse celler, specielt gen-
nem dens evne til at håndtere et dynamisk energi input uden at degradere [2]. En vigtig
ulempe ved PEMEC’er er deres brug af sjældne metaller, såsom platin i katoden of irid-
ium i anoden. At finde nye elektrokatalysatorer, eller øge den intrisiske aktivitet (eller
masseaktiviteten) af disse sjældne metaller er derfor en kritisk parameter, i forbindelse
med at facilitere et udbredt brug af denne teknologi [2].
Dette projekt undersøger elektrokatalytisk aktivitet af små masseselekterede enheder i
form af platin enkelt atomer og dimerer deponeret på polykrystallinsk guld. Enkeltatomerne
og dimérerne har bl.a.
fordelen at de udnytter al massen af katalysatoren, og de har
også fundamentalt anderledes elektronstruktur (REF). Enkeltatomerne og dimérerne blev
deponeret med et ”cluster source” system efterfulgt af at time-of-flight masse filter. Hy-
drogen udvikling (HER), hydrogen oxidation (HOR), oxygen reduktion (ORR) og oxygen
udviklings (OER) aktiviteten er undersøgt, men eftersom de første prøver udviste lav til
ingen aktivitet for ORR og OER, er kun HER aktivitet blevet undersøgt for de senere
prøver. Der er i alt blevet forberedt og målt 9 enkeltatom og 4 dimér prøver, begge med to
forskellige masselæs på 13 ng/cm2 og 4.5 ng/cm2. Aktiviteten er blevet målt ved cyklisk
voltammetri og chronoamperometri i en RDE opsætning, i to forskellige elektrolytter; 0.1
molær perchlorsyre og 0.5 molær svovlsyre. Omsætningsfrekvensen af hydrogen ved et
100 mV overpotentiale rangerede typisk mellem 100-300 H2 s−1, men én prøve udviste en
omsætningsfrekvens på 600 H2 s−1. Aktiviterne var omtrent 10 gange lavere end tidligere
målte HER målinger af 3.8 nm nanopartikler deponeret på HOPG [4].

Preface
This master thesis is submitted in partial fulfillment of a Master’s degree in Physics and
Nanotechnology at the Technical University of Denmark. The work was carried out be-
tween August 23rd 2021 and January 23rd 2022, and has been supervised by Jakob Kib-
sgaard, Ib Chorkendorff and Degenhart Hochfilzer. The electrochemical measurements
were done by me and the sample making and preparation was done by Carlos Perez Es-
pinar, Rikke Plougmann, Julius Lucas Needham and Karl Krøjer Toudahl. This work is
the first experimentally focused project I have carried out, and as frustrating as it has
been, it has also given me new perspective and respect for the obstacles accompanied with
experimental work.
The thesis is comprised of 5 sections; Introduction, Theory, Experimental methods, Re-
sults & Discussion and Conclusion & Outlook. The introduction motivates the project
in a broader perspective. The theory section goes through most of the electrochemistry
concepts used in this project. It is here assumed that the reader has basic knowledge
in physical chemistry, but in principle no knowledge of electrochemistry is assumed. I
have tried to keep the written tone less formal than what is probably usual, as well as
being honest with any mistakes I have made in the lab, while working on the samples 😇.
Some figure texts are very long, as I wanted them to stand fairly independently of the
surrounding text.
I would like to thank Carlos, Rikke, Julius and Karl for their endless effort in supplying
me samples and Degenhart for always having time to explain things or help me in the
lab, when it was needed. I would also like to thank Chao Wei for his guidance, as well as
his giving nature in always having a Luggin capillary, iridium counter electrode, platinum
disk and more, for me to borrow. I would also like to thank Ib and Jakob for insightful
Thursday meetings and their candid nature.
A formal requirement for the master thesis at DTU is the formulation and submission of
a project plan with an overall description, timeline and link to the learning objectives. An
evaluation of the project plan along with the original plan must be included in the final
thesis. This information is available in appendix B.

1.2
Electrocatalysis as a solution
The most familiar example of electrolysis is likely that of water splitting. I remember
chemistry in eighth grade, where we passed a current through water and later ignited the
hydrogen that had built up at the cathode. Hydrogen as an energy carrier has the advan-
tage that the subsequent burning/oxidation of hydrogen has water as the only product,
i.e. no pollutants or GHGs. Other advantages include its relatively high energy density
and its storage abilities [1]. In these terms, it is no match to liquid carbohydrates such
as methanol, ethanol, gasoline and diesel, but it’s specific energy is unbeaten. Hydrogen
use today is dominated by industries such as oil refining, ammonia production, methanol
production and steel production. Virtually all this hydrogen is supplied by fossil fuels, as
less than 0.1% of dedicated hydrogen production comes from water electrolysis [1].
”Hydrogen economy” has gained increased political interest [1] over the last two decades.
This is both in terms of shifting the use from fossil hydrogen to ”green” hydrogen, in the
already established industries, but also in the transport sector, in power generation and
storage, via electrolyzer cells and fuel cells.
Water splitting is in many ways an established technology, where alkaline electrolyzer cells
(AEC) have dominated the electrochemical production of hydrogen [16]. This technology
is very mature, but problems in terms of low current density, low operating pressure, and
especially problems associated with running the system dynamically (altering the incoming
current/voltage or completely cutting it) [2], limits its use in storing fluctuating surplus
energy from wind and solar.
Acidic proton exchange membrane electrolyzer cells (PEMEC) were invented in the 1960’s
by General Electric to overcome some of these problems. They have higher power density,
can operate at higher pressures and the produced hydrogen is usually purer [2]. More
importantly, it offers more flexible operation without ruining the cell.
An important
drawback of the PEMEC is the use of scarce metals such as platinum and iridium, used
at the cathode and anode respectively, which both limits its widespread use, and increase
its costs. Research into new electrocatalysts for the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER)
at the cathode and especially the sluggish oxygen evolution reaction (OER) at the anode
is then a critical component in releasing its full potential [2].
Electrocatalysis is not limited to its use in water splitting. Some noteworthy reactions
are the CO2 reduction reaction (CO2RR) and both the two and four-electron oxygen
reduction reaction (ORR). CO2RR has gained increased attention in the production of
liquid carbohydrates for energy storage, but also in the chemical industry, where the
building blocks for synthesis largely rely on fossil fuels [17]. The four-electron ORR is used
in fuel cells, whereas the two-electron ORR results in the production of hydrogen peroxide.
Hydrogen peroxide is mostly used in the pulp and paper industry, but electrochemical
hydrogen peroxide shows promise in decentralized water cleaning [18].
1.3
This project
This project focuses on fundamental research on electrocatalytic properties of small mass-
selected entities. Small in this project is single atoms and dimers, and it doesn’t really get
much smaller than this. These catalysts have limited commercial value as of yet, as the
catalysts are produced in a cluster source system, which will later be explained in more
detail. It is a very exciting field of catalysts, first of all because they utilize all the mass

called ”scaling relations” that fundamentally limit existing catalysts. The specific catalysts

Theory
This chapter introduces general concepts and theories used in electrochemistry and het-
erogeneous catalysis. It serves as a brief (though longer than I had planned) theoretical
background to the later chapters of the thesis. The chapter describes thermodynamics
and some kinetics in the interface between an electrode and an electrolyte, the three-

At the heart of electrochemistry lies the electrode/electrolyte interface.
We look at a

R ⇌O+ + ne−

most often, conducting electrode. In this example the redox pair is 2H+ +2e ⇌H2, where

The driving force behind this equilibrium can be understood through the fermi level at the
electrode and the HOMO/LUMO of the reactants and products, as depicted in figure 2.2.
If the fermi level of the metal is below the HOMO, electrons are donated to the electrode.
As electrons are donated, the potential decreases i.e. the fermi level rises, until the energy
is comparable to what was earlier, and still is for a lot of the species, the HOMO. The
LUMO of the now oxidized species may then be below the fermi level of the electrode (the
LUMO is likely lower, since the molecule is now positively charged), and hence reduces,
until a dynamic equilibrium is attained. Charge has now been built up in the electrode,
and excess cations/ions outside the electrode, which comprises the so called Helmholtz
double layer.

2.1.4
Non-faradaic processes
The processes where charge is transferred across the electrode-electrolyte interface are
called faradaic. Processes such as adsorption, desorption and charging of the double layer
are called non-faradaic processes. Even though charge does not pass the interface, external
currents can flow at least transiently. Often, one is interested in the faradaic processes,
but to understand the data, the non-faradaic processes must be explained briefly as well.
First we consider the charging of the double-layer. If we increase the potential on an
electrode through potentials where no faradaic processes occur, we have put charge in
the form of electrons on the inner edge of the electrode. The anion concentration then
increases just outside the electrode. This process resembles that of a capacitor, and the
charge passed is equal to the potential multiplied by the capacitance:
∆Q = C∆E
(2.38)
The double-layer differs from that of an ideal capacitor, because the capacitance might
change with the potential. Other non-faradaic processes involve that of adsorption, such as
the hydrogen underpotential deposition, HUPD, which is used in this project. It is worth
to note that the current associated with non-faradaic processes is proportional to the scan
rate, instead of the square root dependence of the Randles-Sevcik limiting current. At
faster scan speeds, non-faradaic processes are thus responsible for a larger fraction of the
current.
2.1.5
The three electrode setup & reference electrodes
Generally, electrochemical investigations are concerned with only one electrode, namely
the working electrode (WE). To pass a current, a counter electrode (CE) is also present
in the electrolyte. In a measurement of the potential between these two electrodes, it is
not possible to distinguish what potential either of the electrode is at, only the difference
is detectable. A third electrode, the reference electrode (RE), is then introduced. The
idea is that the current passed through the RE is infinitely small (just enough to measure
the potential) and the potential of the reference electrode is fixed during an experiment.
The RE used in this project is the mercury/mercury-sulfate electrode. A depiction of the
electrochemical cell is shown in figure 2.4. This project does not use the jacket inlet, which

2.1.8
Electrocapillarity
It is already evident that understanding the entirety of electrochemistry is not easy, and
is probably a task that will entertain many coming generations. A concept that is often
skipped in introductions to electrochemistry, but one I have found to maybe have some
interest in this project, is that of electrocapillarity.
The electrode is not simply a static entity, but the surface changes both with electrolyte
and applied potential, among others. The latter has through history been investigated with
the dropping mercury electrode (DME), invented by Heyrovský, for which he received
the Nobel prize in chemistry in 1959. In DME measurements, one can investigate the
surface tension of the liquid mercury electrode as a function of potential, which also gives
information of the potential of zero charge (PZC). A qualitative explanation is that as
the surface is charged, ions at the interface interact more with the surface, which lowers
the surface free energy and the surface tension. The PZC is then found as the potential
that gives the maximum surface tension. Figure 2.9 shows the drop time as a function
of the potential in a DME measurement with 0.1 M KCl as the electrolyte. The drop
time is directly proportional to the surface tension, and the PZC is thus found to be
approximately -0.5 V vs SCE which translates to 0.26 V vs SHE.
It’s important to note that the PZC changes with the electrolyte, and that the surface
tension decreases both above and below the PZC. PZC of Au(111) is found experimentally
to be 0.5 V vs SHE in aqueuos electrolyte, and the PZC of Pt(111) is 0.3 V vs SHE [26],
but it is not clearly defined for Pt single atoms.

2.2
Heterogeneous catalysis
An (electro)catalyst is a substance that increases the rate of an (electro)chemical reaction,
while not being affected or consumed itself. In heterogeneous catalysis, the catalyst is not
in the same phase as the reactant/product. Most often, the catalyst is a solid, and the
reactants and products are liquid or gas.
One of the advantages of heterogeneous vs.
homogeneous catalysis, is then that the catalyst doesn’t have to be separated from the
products afterwards, and that the reaction can occur continuously, as opposed to a batch
process.

If we have the reaction a + b ⇌p, we can imagine a free energy diagram as the one in

be able to lower the energy level of the transition state by binding a and b to the surface,

energy barriers while the thermodynamics remain the same.
Ideally, one would like to lower the transition state (TS) energy, without lowering the
adsorbed state energy too much, since that would cause a high energy barrier further
down the diagram. This is in general hard to accomplish, and was first described in the
Brønsted-Evans-Polanyi (BEP) relations which state that the TS energy and the binding
energy are related, so if you decrease/increase one, you decrease/increase both. This is a
special case of general scaling relations, that also predict a correlation in binding energies
between different molecules, say O and OH.

the molecules and the catalyst, but can be somewhat circumvented by geometric effects,
which in essence may lower the TS energy, without lowering the binding energy. The
geometric effect comes from different surface geometries that provide different configu-
rations for the molecule to bond. A step in a surface offers both electronic changes as
well as geometric effects, but the two are phenomenologically distinguished by the former
changing both TS and binding energy, whereas the latter changes the TS energy. In a
plot of the TS energy vs. the binding energy, the electronic effect works parallel to the
scaling, whereas the geometric effect is vertical, as seen in figure 2.12. The figure shows
scaling relations between the dissociation energy of N2, O2 and NO and their transition

surfaces 100 and especially 110 have lower TS energy relative to the dissociation energy.
More open surfaces will also have stronger binding in general, which is merely due to an
upshift in the so called d-band center, rather than the different geometry.
The electronic factor can be modeled through the d-band model [22] that successfully
explains energy trends in the late transition metals. In the simplest framework it considers
only the d-band center, which is however connected to the filling and the width of the d-
band. The higher the d-band center, the higher the more reactive the metal is. A typical
example of the applicability of the model is the d-band center shift of a strained metal. If
e.g. an overlayer copper is grown on gold, the d-band center will move to higher energies.
The single copper layer will be forced to have the same larger lattice constant as the
gold. This in turn decreases the overlap of the Cu d-orbitals, and since the filling is the
same, the d-band center must move up, making copper more reactive. Another example is
the increased reactivity of steps and kinks. Steps, kinks and corners are undercoordinated

2.3
Properties of single-atom alloys
Single atoms (SA) and single atom alloys (SAA) are interesting for catalytic purposes,
first of all for their utilization of the entire catalyst mass, as almost all of the catalyst is
exposed. Secondly, their electronic structure is very different from their bulk/nanoparticle
counterparts. Thirdly, the highly defined geometry motif may stabilize or destabilize some
intermediates relative to others, that are usually correlated, i.e. break scaling relations
[29]. All these effects may increase both selectivity and activity. This section is heavily
inspired by the review of single atom alloys by Sykes et al. [3]. The treatment considers
SAA’s where the host metal is either Cu, Ag or Au, and the dopant is one of the 9 late
transition metals; Fe, Ru, Os, Cu, Rh, Ir, Ni, Pd and Pt.
The d-band model explains much of the reactivity trends of bulk nanoparticles, as well
as the hardships in breaking scaling relations. Results from Andersson et al. in 2006
suggest that a simple interpolation scheme predicts reactivity for alloys, at least for the
FeNi alloys considered in the paper [30]. Recent results suggest that in the special case
of SAA’s simple interpolation is not exact, and that the dopants d-orbitals resemble more
those of the atom in gas phase, i.e. the d-band is much more narrow [31]. Figure 2.13
(top) shows the projected density of states (p-DOS) of different host (Cu, Ag, Au) and
dopant (Pt, Pd, Ir, Rh, Ni) metals, and shows that especially for the Ag and Au host
metals, the dopants d-states are very narrow in energy. Following the d-band model, one
would expect greater binding, since the d-band center is lifted. Other calculations show

2.5
X-ray diffraction (XRD)
X-ray diffraction (XRD) is only used in one instance in this project, and the explanation
of the theory will thus be rather brief. It is a relatively old technique used to identify crys-
talline phases in a material [23]. A periodic crystal is illuminated with a monochromatic
X-ray beam. Elastically scattered beams will interfere constructively in angles described
by the Bragg relation:
nλ = 2d sin θ
(2.43)
where λ is the wavelength of the X-ray, d is the distance between two lattice planes and θ
is the angle between incoming X-ray, and the normal to the reflecting lattice plane. n is
an integer describing the order of the reflection. Figure 2.19 shows the geometry visually,
where one can see that one ray has to travel 2d sin θ further, which is what gives the Bragg
relation.
Two types of XRD measurements are carried out in this project, namely ”symmetric XRD
scans” and ”rocking curves”. In symmetric scans, only planes parallel to the surface are
gauged, as the incident (probing) and reflected (measured) ray has the same angle to the
surface plane. This gives information about the different crystal facets present, parallel to
the surface.
In rocking curves, one is interested in a specific crystal facet, such as the one present in
a single crystal. Small changes to the angle of the incoming ray, ω, gives an indication
of how large a fraction of the facets, is parallel to the surface, and how much is tilted
with respect to the surface. If the rocking curve is very narrow, the facet normals are
in the same direction. If it is broad, the facet normals point in different directions. The
curve often show a bell-shaped distribution, and the full-width-half-max (FWHM) gives
a quantitative estimate of the difference in orientation.

3.4
How measurements are carried out
The measurements are carried out by first filling the electrochemical cell with electrolyte.
Two types, of electrolyte are used in this project, namely 0.1 M perchloric acid, HClO4
(Merck, Suprapur 70%) and 0.5 M Sulfuric acid, H2SO4 (Merck, Suprapur 96%). The
Suprapur brand are extra pure chemicals, but there are still contaminants in the ppb
range. Contaminant levels are included in appendix A. The perchloric acid has an iron
contamination level below 20 ppb, whereas the sulfuric acid iron contamination is below
5 ppb.
An intrinsic ”contamination” in perchloric acid, arising simply from chemical
equilibrium, is that of chlorine where up to 1000 ppb can be expected.
The concentrated acids are obviously diluted.
The perchloric acid is diluted with ap-
proximately 8 mL in 1 L, whereas the sulfuric acid is ≈30 mL in 1 L. About 200 mL is
used in the glass cell, which means that 1 ppb will become 1.6 ng or 6 ng (perchloric or
sulfuric acid) in the cell. For iron this corresponds to 17·1012 iron atoms (in perchloric
acid) which means that there are 3.44·1014 iron atoms in one cell perchloric acid! That
is more than 100 times the amount of Pt atoms deposited. Even with this super pure,
diluted electrolyte, contamination from the electrolyte may still be an issue.

3.4.1
Potential control
When the disk is inserted in the electrolyte, the electrode quickly goes to open circuit
voltage, which might not be at an appropriate potential, in terms of stability, for the de-
posited platinum. This is avoided by shorting the electrode with a gold auxiliary electrode
in the solution. The measurement is started, and the potential is held at e.g. 0 V vs RHE,
and the working electrode is since introduced to the solution. This ensures that when the
electrode enters the solution, the potential is also 0 V vs RHE. The connection to the
auxiliary electrode can since be removed.
3.4.2
Cyclic voltammetry and chronoamperometry
Cyclic voltammetry (CV) is possibly the electrochemical method that’s used most in
research. One alters the current linearly to an upper and lower potential. By changing
the scan rate and potential limits, lots of information can be gained of the sample. CVs
are carried out in the beginning of this project, and the scan rate is kept fixed at 20 mV/s
throughout. This rather low scan rate limits the non-faradaic contributions. To gauge the
activity for different reactions, the potential limits are changed.
When the potential is taken below 0 V vs RHE, HER will occur if the catalyst is active.
HOR will happen above 0 V. It is very important to purge the solution with 1 bar H2
continuously, as otherwise there will be a nernstian shift to higher potentials, and the
catalyst will seem more HER active than what is the case. This is a common pitfall in
HER measurements, and it should be ensured that the HER/HOR onset potential is at 0
V. HER/HOR measurements in this project are typically carried out between -0.3 V and
0.3 V.
OER/ORR happens above/below 1.23 V vs RHE when the solution is purged with 1 bar
O2. Both reactions are usually pretty sluggish however, which means that OER current
is typically not significant below 1.5 V and ORR is typically not seen above 0.8 V. In
this project, ORR measurements are carried out between 1 V and 0.2 V, whereas OER
measurements are measured as high as 1.8 V, though mostly just up til 1.6 V.
Later in this project, chronoamperometry (CA) measurements, also known as ”potential
holds” are carried out instead. At the point of the introduction of CAs, only HER activity
was measured. In CAs the non-faradaic contributions are essentially eliminated, as these
happen on a ms time scale. These measurements also mimic more realistic conditions in
an industrial setting, and thus give more genuine stability assessment. Furthermore, it
allows for the Koutecký-Levich analysis to be carried out.
3.4.3
CO strips
The CO strips are carried out by purging the electrolyte with CO for a small amount of
time ≈1-2 min , while the potential of the WE is held at 0.2 V. The CO adsorps to the
surface, and it is usually assumed that 1 CO per atom adsorps. After the adsorption, the
electrolyte is purged with Ar for approximately 15 min, to ensure that all CO from the
solution is gone. One then scans the potential, first to −0.1 V and then to +1 V, and
a few background cycles are carried out after this. The potential is scanned negatively
to see if the catalyst is HER/HOR active, where no activity indicates proper blocking by

3.4.4
Hydrogen underpotential deposition
Hydrogen underpotential deposition (HUPD) is another technique used for evaluating the
amount of active sites. For bulk platinum, hydrogen binds at potentials usually below 0.3
V in the reaction H++e−+∗→H∗, similar to the Volmer step [38]. The name comes from
the fact that hydrogen binds ”before” the potential of 0 V. One electron is transferred in
the process, but otherwise the treatment is similar to the CO stripping. HUPD is not seen
seen on gold, making this well suited for evaluating the amount of Pt. None one of these
experiments were successful, however.
3.4.5
Measuring resistance
The uncompensated resistance is measured by EIS. This is done to correct for the Ohmic
resistance between the reference electrode and the working electrode, to get a better
estimate of potential at the working electrode. This is mostly relevant for higher currents,
where the difference can be up to 50 mV potential.
The theory behind gauging the
uncompensated resistance in EIS is already presented in the theory section. The resistances
vary depending on the age of the RDE head which was around 25 Ωfor the older ones
and 5 Ωfor the newer ones.
3.4.6
Calibrating the reference electrode
The reference electrode is calibrated with each preparation of a new electrolyte.
The
potential of the reference electrode is measured against a platinum electrode, where the
electrolyte is purged in hydrogen. The open circuit voltage (OCV) will yield the potential
of the reference electrode vs RHE, but even better, one can carry out CVs between say
½-½0.1 V and 0.1 V vs the OCV, and the potential of the reference electrode can be
extracted from where the current crosses 0. The current crosses 0 when HER and HOR
currents at the electrode are equivalent, and precisely marks the potential of the RHE.
Typical values for the Hg/Hg2SO4 reference electrode used are 0.72 V vs RHE in the 0.1
M perchloric acid and 0.7 V vs RHE in the 0.5 M sulphuric acid.

Results & Discussion
4.1
Electrochemical measurements
This section is comprised of electrochemical measurements of 16 samples of platinum on
polycrystalline gold. 9 samples are Pt single atoms on polycrystalline gold, 4 samples
are Pt dimers on polycrystalline gold and three samples are 2nm Pt nanoparticles on
polycrystalline gold. The changes in measurement procedure bear witness to the uncharted
nature of these samples.
The first measurements gauge the activity of the hydrogen
evolution reaction (HER), hydrogen oxidation reaction (HOR), oxygen reduction reaction
(ORR) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER). CO-strips and HUPD measurements were
carried out to estimate the amount active sites. The later measurements are only concerned
with the HER activity, but also CO-stripping and HUPD is attempted.
For every measurement, something new has been learned, and the measurement strategy
has been adjusted accordingly. This helped continually optimize the procedure, to inves-
tigate what was actually interesting, but comes at a cost of reproducibility. Furthermore,
the samples take a long time to produce and the cluster source was continually struck with
bad luck which elongated the process further. The consequence of these circumstances is
that there have been many different ways of measuring the samples, which makes it con-
fusing for the reader. Additionally, the low amount of samples make it hard to conclude
much, since the results have been somewhat irreproducible.
I have chosen to include measurements from all samples, but only the measurements where
we have learned something. The first samples will be gone through in more detail, to make
the reader more familiar with the setup and the samples. The later part of this chapter
focuses on the results I have deemed the most interesting, and the results that showed
consistently through the different samples. First however, I will present some key results
from the project, for overview purposes.

4.1.5
Sample 10 and 11, dimers
Sample 10 and 11 are again Pt dimers deposited on gold stubs. Fairly large changes to the
procedure are made after discussions in the group. First of all, the electrolyte is changed
from 0.1 M Perchloric acid to 0.5 M sulfuric acid, as the sulfuric acid can be made cleaner.
A downside is that it adsorps as sulfate on gold at potentials relevant for OER and ORR
measurements at approximately 1 V vs RHE [41]. This is not a problem for HER/HOR
measurements however, so we decided to switch, with the hope that maybe this would
improve stability to decrease potential poisoning.
The procedure itself was also changed, and instead of doing CVs, we switched to chronoam-
perometry (CA), also known as ”potential holds” in a more common tongue. (Chrono
54
Investigating electrocatalytic activity of small mass-selected entities
==PAGE:75==
refers to time, so chronoamperometry is measuring the current over time.) CVs offer a
lot of information over a wide potential window, and can also give information about re-
versibility of the reaction (if the kinetics are faster than the mass transport). One can also
alter scan rates to obtain information about possible chemical steps involved in reactions
[19], but there are not many reasons to do it for the investigations at hand in this project.
CAs on the other hand first of all eliminate the ambiguity involved in interpreting CVs
with different scan rates. They are also more realistic in terms of how industrial HER
electrolysis operates [2], and give a more relevant estimate of the stability of the samples.
Furthermore, it gives the opportunity to carry out a so-called Koutecký-Levich analysis,
which in theory allows one to measure true kinetic activity, and eliminate mass transport
dependencies.
The Koutecký-Levich (KL) analysis is explained in the theory section, and the principle
is to simply alter the rotation speed, hereafter extrapolate to ”infinite” rotation speed to
obtain the ”true” kinetic activity. The KL analysis is pursued in all the following samples,
but for all samples except sample 14, the current fluctuates more intrinsically, compared
to how much it changes with rotation speed, leaving the KL analysis useless. Sample 14
left results that have somewhat proper square root dependency on the current, but it is
still in this case doubtful if true kinetic parameters can be extracted. It is included in this
thesis as a curiosity mostly.
Last but not least, the Pt amount was altered to get a 1% coverage on gold, in order to
avoid interactions between individual Pt atoms. In hindsight, it would probably have been
fine, if not better, to stick with the same coverage as before, just to get a larger, measurable
current and keep the results comparable. The coverage is calculated on the assumption
that the gold surface is comprised mostly of (111) planes, which is a fair assumption, given
that it is the most densely packed surface, and thereby has the lowest surface energy. The
new amount of deposited platinum is n = 2.76 · 1012, a factor of 2.7 times less than the
previous 7.5 · 1012.
The new parameters for the measurements are as follows:
• Dopant: Pt dimers, n = 2.76 · 1012 *
• Substrate: (Old) Polycrystalline gold stubs
• Electrolyte: 0.5M Sulfuric acid, H2SO4, changed between measurements
• Counter electrode: Ir foil
• Reference electrode: Hg/Hg2SO4
• Auxiliary electrode: Gold wire
• RDE rotation: 1600 rpm
The procedure is centered around a staircase CA experiment, where the samples are held
at a potential for 1 min, which should be plenty time to reach a steady state in terms of
mass transport. Non-faradaic contributions such as double-layer charging happen on a ms
timescale [19], and are thus no issue as well. The sample is taken to a potential of -100
mV in steps of 25 mV. A quick anodic cleaning cycle to 0.7 V is then carried out, and then
a second staircase CA. After this, a KL analysis is attempted with little luck. Finally;
free play, where the samples are taken as far as -250 mV. No CO strip is attempted, since
most previous samples didn’t show any peak. Looking back, it might have been a good
idea just to try it, regardless.
1. Potential control at 0 V
2. Staircase HER: -25 mV →-50 mV →-75 mV →-100 mV →-75 mV →-50 mV →
-25 mV →0 mV (1 min at each potential)

3. Cleaning cycle to +0.7 V at 100 mV/s
4. Staircase HER again
5. Koutecký-Levich analysis at different potentials
The procedure is also displayed visually in figure 4.24, which shows the current at the
different potentials. First off, we see that sample 11 is more active than sample 10. Sec-
ondly, sample 10 gains some activity after the cleaning cycle, whereas sample 11 loses
some. Thirdly, this displays the phenomena of cathodic activation clearer than any pre-
vious measurements. When the sample goes from e.g. 75 to 100 mV overpotential, the
current increases monotonically throughout the minute of the hold. Mass transport can
not account for this, since it would have the opposite effect. As more hydrogen is pro-
duced at the interface, the current would taper off and reach a lower steady state. It
was suggested at one meeting, that over time, the dimers sinter into nanoparticles and
become more active. This is an appealing argument, however the current also decreases
continually when going from 100 mV back to 75 mV overpotential, and looks to reach the
same current it did at 75 mV before. It is not impossible for the nanoparticles to split
back into single atoms/dimers, but it is hard to justify any mechanisms or reasons that
should be responsible for this, and it hardly seems like this is the case. The phenomena
of increasing current is what I have found most interesting in this thesis, and possible
explanations will be discussed more thoroughly later in this chapter.

Koutecký-Levich for sample 14
A proper Koutecký-Levich analysis has been one of my grand wishes for this project. Only
sample 14 showed activity that had proper dependence on the rotation rate of the RDE.
Unfortunately, sample 14 was one of the samples that had activity much worse than the
previous SA samples. Figure 4.38 shows the inverse current plotted as a function of the
inverse square root of the rotation rate. A line for the best fit at each potential is also
shown. The intersects with 0 (infinite rotation rate) are 5, 25 and 23 for 150, 100 and 50
mV. This translates to a kinetic current of 0.2, 0.04 and 0.043 mA cm−1 and a TOF of 45,
9 and 10 s−1. Obviously, the kinetic current shouldn’t be higher for a lower overpotential,
which is one sign of the failure of the analysis. For a classic KL analysis the slope should
be equal, but for the reversible KL analysis, the slope depends on the applied potential,
which for HER should give a difference of a factor 1 −exp(−2Fη
RT ) as derived in [24]. This
does not explain the different slopes however, as this predicts a maximum difference of a
factor 1.02.
The failed Koutecký-Levich analysis is a consequence of the intrinsic fluctuation in the
activity of the samples, and would probably be more precise, had the loading been larger,
or ideally, if the samples just had constant activity for a given overpotential and rotation
rate. In any case, it probably gives a closer estimate of the kinetic current/TOF, than
what can be obtained by the actual currents measured.

4.3
Stability remarks
Stability for the single atoms and dimers has been an issue, especially through the initial
measurements, where the samples were repeatedly taken to oxidizing potentials. Further-
more, the HER measurements post CO strip showed no activity, indicating either that the
CO strip removes the platinum, or that the catalyst simply dissolves over time, as the CO
strips do take a ”long” time, ∼20 min, to carry out. The lost activity after oxidizing po-
tentials was especially highlighted in sample 8 and 9, where the anodic cleaning effectively
removed all of sample 9’s activity and half the activity of sample 8. A simple explana-
tion is offered through oxidation of platinum. Bulk platinum has a standard potential of
1.188 V vs NHE in the reaction Pt2+ + 2e−⇌Pt, but since the concentration of Pt2+
Investigating electrocatalytic activity of small mass-selected entities
71
==PAGE:92==
is virtually 0 in the electrolyte, this will be shifted to much lower potentials, through the
Nernst equation. As seen in the canonical CVs, figure 2.7, platinum oxidizes already at
1 V, even though most of it is not dissolved, but simply oxidized and reduced again. It
is also important to remember that this is not bulk platinum. The interaction with gold
makes it hard to gauge whether this makes the Pt more or less noble, but a true gas phase
single atom of Pt is definitely more reactive. The dimers sample 10 and 11 showed great
stability when kept at negative potentials vs. RHE, which indicates that the stability is
greater, as long as the platinum is kept at reducing potentials.
Regarding the CO strips, it is again hard to gauge whether this is just time (they are kept
at +0.2 V for a long time), or if it really is the CO that removes the platinum. In 2013
Gerber et al. reported CO induced Smulochowski ripening of Pt clusters on graphene on
Ir(111) [40]. Smulochowski ripening is characterized by the Pt diffusing and coalescing on
the surface, rather than a typical Ostwald ripening, which happens via volatile species. For
some scholars it is also known as the skyhook effect. This indicates that the CO increases
the mobility of the platinum on the surface, and one can speculate whether this has the
ability to remove the Pt totally. In a very trivialized treatment one can say that the Pt
bonds with the CO, leaving less bonding with the gold substrate. It is interesting that
the CO strips did work for some samples, and that they subsequently showed no activity,
indicating that the Pt is removed with the oxidation of CO. It is possible that the CO
strip did not measure the amount of platinum, but simply some other metal contaminant,
however the amount is very close to that of deposited platinum.
An important note is that the deposited nanoparticles also showed a loss of activity after
the CO strips, even though the CO strips were definitely present. The skyhook effect
shows no mercy...
4.4
Activity remarks
There have been large differences in the activities of the samples, with the highest and
lowest having a TOF of 600 and 100 H2 s−1 at 100 mV overpotential, respectively. Fur-
thermore, as already noted multiple times, the activity became highly irreproducible from
sample 14 and onward. This was maybe due to the newer stubs or a new batch of elec-
trolyte, but there is plenty of room for speculation still. It seems the case however, that
the SAs and dimers are less active than the 3.8 nm NPs deposited on HOPG. There are
explanations for this.
The first explanation is the effective elimination of the Tafel step in the reaction mech-
anism. For the Tafel step to happen, two hydrogen atoms need to be adjacent to each
other, and as the coverage on gold at this potential is much lower than for bulk platinum,
this is unlikely to happen. For dimers, the chance is greater as it has a neighbour, but for
an FCC (111) surface, one site has 6 neighbours. The Tafel slopes generally had values
around 100 mV per decade, signifying that it is either the Volmer or Heyrovský step that
is the RDS, if one trusts the microkinetic modelling of Shinagawa et al. This is not much
new info however, as the Tafel step can be thought eliminated already due to the above
arguments. Furthermore, saying that either the Volmer or Heyrovský step is the RDS is
basically saying that the hydrogen binds either too strongly or too weakly, which is not
very illuminating.
A second explanation is that of the binding energies. Sykes et al. showed through DFT
calculations, that the hydrogen dissociation energy is approximately 0.7 eV weaker. This
has to be divided by two to get the usual ∆EH∗, but still represents a huge difference.
Bear in mind that this is DFT calculations, and the fact that the activity is only around
72
Investigating electrocatalytic activity of small mass-selected entities
==PAGE:93==
10 times lower, indicates that the difference is maybe not as significant as the calculations
show. It is still likely that the bonding strength is very different from bulk platinum. A
little weaker would be better, but the bond is probably too weak in this case. The NPs
deposited in this project are only slightly better than most of the other samples, but it has
to be remarked that these were done with the newer stubs and a new batch of electrolyte,
so it is questionable to conclude that the activities were true to bulk platinum or platinum
NPs.
It has been suggested that the samples for which the CO strips worked, were probably
because the sample had sintered to NPs.
This suggests that they should have higher
activity, but there is no connection between the CO strip working (sample 2, 3 and 15)
and activity, as seen in figure 4.44. The figure also shows how the first sample measured
differs from that of the second. The first sample has been in open atmosphere for about
30 minutes, whereas the second sample has been exposed for about 3 hours. The open
atmosphere leaves the platinum more vulnerable to oxidation, but likely also increases the
amount of adventitious carbon that can deposit on the sample. For the single atoms, it
looks like there is a slight difference. For the dimers and NPs not much can be concluded.
A phenomena that has been consistent throughout the samples is that of cathodic activa-
tion. It is personally the results that piqued my interest the most, probably because we
had a hard time finding an explanation for it. Two mentionable explanations are those of
cathodic activation through continuous reduction and cathodic activation through changes
in surface free energies.
The simplest explanation of these is that of continuous reduction, and is a phenomenon
already reported in literature. Indeed Lister et al. in 2003 observed cathodic activation
of RuO2 for HER, and ascribe the reason behind to be formation of more active metallic
ruthenium, i.e. reduced ruthenium oxide [44]. As already noted, the platinum standard
potential is at 1.188 V, but since there is effectively no platinum in the solution, this
is shifted negatively, and the Pt is more easily oxidized.
The oxidized platinum may
not necessarily dissolute, but the oxidized platinum may have way lower activity. As the
potential is held at negative, the Pt may continually reduce, until it reaches an equilibrium
for that potential. As it is taken to a slightly more positive potential some Pt oxidizes, until
it reaches another equilibrium for that potential. This explains quite well the reversible
behaviour.
The other slightly more exotic explanation can be found through surface free energies.
(This explanation is a little bit ”grasping for straws”, but I found it too interesting, not to
include.) Surface free energies, among others, will dictate how an alloy forms. In vacuum,
when Pt is the minority element, it will dissolve in bulk Au, meaning that it will not
be present at the surface [45]. When the substrate is taken out of vacuum however, the
amount of Pt will be enriched at the surface however, due to bonding with adsorping gas
molecules. This will also happen with adsorbing species in the electrolyte. The surface
free energies are not independent of the applied potential however, as was seen in the
theory section on electrocapillarity. The PZC of Au(111) is 0.5 V vs SHE in aqueuos
solvent, which means that as the potential is taken lower than 0 V, the surface free energy
decreases. The PZC of Pt(111) is found to be 0.3 V vs SHE, but the PZC for the single
atoms is not clearly defined. As the potential and surface free energy changes, it is possible
that some platinum segregates to (or dissolves in) the surface, making the catalyst more (or
less) active. This explanation calls for more investigations before any validation, possibly
through in-situ STM.
The segregation does not explain the cathodic activation of the NPs, where it is more
Investigating electrocatalytic activity of small mass-selected entities
73
==PAGE:94==
likely the continuous reduction. The cathodic activation seemed to happen faster than for
the single atoms, however. One explanation, if we follow the train of thoughts of surface
free energies, is that of increased wetting of the NP. As the surface energy towards the
electrolyte lowers, the NP is more prone to wetting, if we imagine that the interaction
with gold is constant over the same potential change.

5
Conclusion & Outlook
This project sought out to investigate the electrocatalytic activity of small mass-selected
entities.
The specific system was Pt single atoms (SAs) and Pt dimers deposited on
polycrystalline gold, in reality making them single-atom alloys (SAAs) and dimer alloys.
The platinum was deposited by use of a cluster source system, and the samples were
subsequently measured electrochemically in an RDE setup. A total of nine single atom
samples, four dimer samples and three 2 nm nanoparticles (NPs) samples were made and
measured. The electrolyte used was initially 0.1 M perchloric acid (samples 1-9) which was
later changed to 0.5 M sulfuric acid (samples 10-18). The mass loading was also changed
midway, from 13 ng/cm2 (sample 1-9) to 4.5 ng/cm2 (sample 10-18).
For the initial
measurements, both HER, HOR, ORR and OER activity was measured, but from sample
10 and onward, only the HER activity was gauged.
The HER measurements showed
somewhat irreproducible, especially through sample 14-18, where the gold substrate was
changed, and another batch of electrolyte was used.
The TOF of hydrogen at 100 mV overpotential ranged typically between 100-300 H2 s−1,
but one sample also showed TOF as high as 600 H2 s−1. The most reproducible results
showed that the HER activity in terms of the TOF of hydrogen was approximately 10
times lower than previous measurements on 3.8 nm Pt NPs deposited on HOPG. The
lower activity is likely a result of difference in binding energy as well as the effective
elimination of the Tafel step in the HER reaction mechanism, as the hydrogen coverage
on gold is much lower than on Pt.
Both SAs, dimers and NPs showed behaviour of cathodic activation, though more promi-
nent for the single atoms and dimers. There is no ready explanation for this, but possible
causes are those of increased reduction of platinum, and the more exotic explanation of
segregation due to changes in the surface free energy with changing potential.
The single atoms, dimers and NPs have shown very vulnerable to positive/oxidizing po-
tentials as well as CO strips. The vulnerability to positive potentials can be explained
through simple oxidation of platinum followed by dissolution. The vulnerability to CO
strips can be explained through the ”skyhook” effect, where bonding CO mobilizes the
platinum on the surface, making it more likely to dissolute.
It is important to note, that in principle it is not proven that the single atoms and dimers
have not sintered to nanoparticles on the gold surface. The missing CO stripping peak for
the smallest entities, and the presence of a CO stripping peak for all the NPs do suggest
that the single atoms and dimers have not simply aggregated on the surface, however.
5.1
Improvements to the project - hindsight is 20/20
It is no secret that an STM, preferably an in-situ STM, would have been a great aid in
the investigation of these entities. It would shine light on the nature of the deposited
platinum, alleviating the underlying doubt of whether the SAs and dimers aggregate or
not. Furthermore, it could demystify some of the causes of the cathodic activation, if
the Pt actually segregated to the surface, or if it was reversible oxidation and reduction
causing the changes in activity, as the potential was held. Unfortunately, the STMs at
Surfcat have been out of operation during the time of this project.
In hindsight we should probably have stuck with the older gold stubs, if only for the
sake of reproducibility. It is unknown, what has caused the difference in activity from
Investigating electrocatalytic activity of small mass-selected entities
75
==PAGE:96==
sample 14 and onward, as many things were changed with these measurements including;
newer gold substrates, another batch of electrolyte and the introduction of a molybdenum
mask during the deposition in the cluster source. The introduction of the newer gold
stubs were thought to only increase the reproducibility, as they supposedly would be less
contaminated.
The change to lower mass loading was likely also unnecessary, as we were already working
with a very low loading. This was done to ensure proper dispersion of the Pt atoms as
well as further eliminate the issues of mass transfer. The dispersion was probably already
fine, and though mass transfer is a big concern in HER, it is not that big of an issue at low
overpotentials ≲0.1 V. Additionally a higher loading would make the Koutecký-Levich
analysis more consistent, and in principle one could then cancel mass transfer problems.
The switch from CVs to staircase measurements showed fruitful, especially for illuminating
the cathodic activation and increasing the stability of the samples.
The switch from
perchloric to sulfuric acid was in principle not a bad idea, but maybe one specific batch of
sulfuric acid had irregular contaminant levels.
5.2
Future prospects for HER on SAAs
One success story from this project is that the SA and dimer alloys did yield different
results from that of the 3.8 nm nanoparticles on HOPG. One can hope that this is caused
not only by the elimination of the Tafel step, but also by electronic structure effects, as
this provides yet another lever in the tuning of catalysts. As already discussed, SAAs
have been treated in much more detail in the review by Sykes et al., where it is shown
computationally, that especially BEP relations can be broken, and the activation energy
lowered. For the case of single atom alloys of HER one would have to look for something
that binds a little weaker than platinum, where it is adequate to be a little on the weak
binding side of the volcano, as the new BEP relations suggest that the activation energy
for hydrogen bonding is much lower on these SAAs. Working from the figure of hydro-
gen dissociation energies, figure 2.14, a reasonable and cheap candidate could be that of
nickel deposited on copper, NiCu. Whether this system is stable in acid is another story,
however...

Project plan
Title: Investigating electrocatalytic activity of small mass-selected entities
Goal: To develop methods within and characterize the electrocatalytic activity of small
mass-selected entities
PROJECT SPECIFIC GOALS/METHODS:
• Be able to conduct electrochemical experiments using:
Cyclic voltammetry (CV), electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), CO-stripping,
chronoamperometry (CA), rotating disc electrode (RDE), rotating ring disc electrode
(RRDE), electrochemical mass spectrometry (EC-MS)
• Obtain knowledge on the fundamentals behind a good electrocatalyst.
• Evaluate showstoppers, e.g.
poor activity and stability, and how to potentially
circumvent them with small mass-selected entities.
• Evaluate potential errors in an electrochemical experiment, through knowledge of
fundamental electrochemistry, as well as the physics at play in the given experiment.
BROADER GOALS:
• Read, interpret and communicate scientific literature e.g. articles.
• Take part in scientific discussions in meetings in the department.
• Identify a solvable problem in a broader, societal context.
• Work independently and realistically.
• Work in a chemical lab.
TIMELINE:
September & October: Gain knowledge in the methods and the field of electrocatalysis.
November & December: Conduct experiments to investigate electrocatalytic activity of
small mass-selected entities.
January: Report writing
EVALUATION:
September & October: During September and the start of October, I had time to learn
how to work in the chemical lab, how to conduct CVs, EIS, CO stripping, how to work
with the RDE and RRDE, but unfortunately I did not get to work with the EC-MS. I had
time to read literature on small entities and electrochemistry in general. Already some
measurements were carried out in the end of October.
November & December: By this time, premature measurements were available, which we
were able to discuss at Thursday meetings. I could work independently in the lab, and
more samples were measured. Unfortunately, the cluster source system was repeatedly
struck with bad luck which meant that fewer samples than what one could hope for, were
available for measuring.
January: Some samples were still being measured in the beginning of January, pushing

Overall, the project went quite according to the project plan, which is probably due to the
fairly unspecific goals. It is unfortunate that not more samples could be measured, but
alas, that is the contract one signs when working with samples from the cluster source.
