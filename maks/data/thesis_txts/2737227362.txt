Summary
This master’s thesis explores the strategic development and optimization of the
North Sea Energy Island, an initiative by the Danish government designed to strengthen
Europe’s renewable energy framework and significantly advance the continent’s de-
carbonization objectives. Positioned to capitalize on extensive renewable resources,
particularly offshore wind, the Energy Island project directly addresses the Euro-
pean Union’s imperative to boost energy security and diminish its dependence on
fossil fuel imports amidst evolving geopolitical and energy market dynamics. Uti-
lizing a linear optimization tool, TIMES, this study introduces a novel model that
diverges from the prevalent demand-driven approach by adopting a price-driven
strategy aimed at maximizing the economic returns for investors and developers.
This model facilitates informed decision-making regarding the production of vari-
ous e-fuels—Hydrogen, Ammonia, Methanol, and Kerosene—and the management
of electricity transmission. Employing an hourly resolution, the model provides a
detailed understanding of the island’s configuration and operations, enhancing the
reliability of the results.
The research evaluates various scenarios to assess how
differing conditions might affect the island’s operations, capacity, investment, and
profitability. While some simplifications were necessary concerning electricity mar-
ket and transport operations, the developed model-tool has proven reliable and can
be integrated with other demand-driven studies to determine optimal operational
strategies and future projections. The findings indicate that Germany and Denmark
are the most viable markets for exporting the island’s electricity. However, produc-
ing hydrogen for export to the Netherlands and Belgium appears to be the most
lucrative option, given the high industrial demand and pricing in these regions. The
study also notes that producing other e-fuels on the island would be economically
feasible only under specific conditions with suﬀiciently high prices, suggesting that
the island’s most effective role may be as a Hydrogen Hub. Additionally, the use of
an hourly resolution has proven instrumental in understanding storage operations
on the island and achieving more dependable outcomes.

1. Introduction
The transition to renewable energy sources has become a paramount global endeavor
in combating climate change and securing sustainable energy futures. In the quest
for innovative solutions, in 2021 the Danish government approved that Denmark will
embark on a groundbreaking project that promises to revolutionize offshore energy
infrastructure: the development of VindØ, the North Sea energy island. Situated
in the North Sea, this visionary undertaking aims to harness the region’s vast wind
energy potential to establish a multifaceted energy hub, facilitating the transmis-
sion of electricity and the production of green hydrogen and e-fuels to neighboring
countries.[1]
The concept of VindØ emerged as a response to the growing need for sustainable
energy solutions and Denmark’s commitment to ambitious climate targets. With
its strategic location in the North Sea, VindØ is poised to capitalize on the region’s
strong and consistent wind resources, offering an unparalleled opportunity to ex-
pand renewable energy capacities and drive the green transition forward[2]. This
master thesis is orientated to be presented to potential investors in the project of the
North Sea energy island, leveraging market externalities like different e-fuels prices
to determine the suitable level for producing each e-fuel. Moreover, the island’s
design facilitates connections to various Northern European countries, including the
western part of Denmark, Norway, Germany, the Netherlands, Belgium, and the
UK, when refering to e-fuels, the Netherlands and Belgium are going to be con-
sidered as one unique region. These connections, crucial for both electricity and
e-fuels transmission, take into account future constraints such as transmission lines
capacities.[3]
The decision to embark on the construction of VindØ marks a significant mile-
stone in Denmark’s energy landscape.
In recent years, the country has been at
the forefront of renewable energy deployment, with wind power playing a pivotal
role in its energy mix. Building upon this momentum, the Danish government an-
nounced plans for the construction of VindØ, envisioning it as a flagship project that
epitomizes Denmark’s commitment to sustainability, innovation, and international
collaboration.
Scheduled for implementation in multiple phases, VindØ’s ambitious agenda in-
cludes the installation of 3 GW of offshore wind capacity in its initial phase between
2030 and 2033, followed by an additional 7 GW in the subsequent phase before 2040.
This formidable scale underscores VindØ’s potential to emerge as a leading offshore

Beyond its role as an electricity hub, VindØ is poised to become a pivotal player
in the emerging hydrogen economy. Hydrogen will play a major role in most re-
newable energy systems, as it can be used to balance variances from renewable
production and to produce alternative fuels. Leveraging surplus renewable energy,
the island will host facilities for green hydrogen production, offering a clean and sus-
tainable alternative to conventional fossil fuels. VindØ’s aspirations extend to the
production of e-fuels such as e-Ammonia, e-Methanol, and e-Kerosene, which hold
immense promise in decarbonizing sectors such as transportation and industry.[4]
The primary objective of this study is to develop a model implemented in TIMES
that diverges from the traditional focus of previous research on future energy islands,
which has predominantly been driven by the demands of the system and connected
countries. In contrast, this model adopts a price-driven approach, aimed at maxi-
mizing the economic operations of the island for potential investors and operators.
This shift to a price-driven perspective allows for a nuanced exploration of fi-
nancial strategies that can significantly enhance the profitability and viability of
energy islands. By simulating various scenarios within the model, the goal is to vali-
date its effectiveness under differing conditions. This process demonstrates not only
the model’s accuracy and robustness but also its adaptability to diverse operational
challenges.
The versatility of this tool paves the way for future studies that can explore a
range of strategic approaches to the configuration and operation of energy islands.
By enabling detailed scenario testing, the model assists in identifying the most
economically advantageous strategies, thereby helping stakeholders make informed
decisions that align with their financial and operational goals.
The model’s ability to flexibly handle various price inputs and economic condi-
tions makes it a valuable asset in the strategic planning of energy island projects. It
stands as a forward-thinking resource that can guide the development and execution
of future energy projects, ensuring that they are both sustainable and economically
sound.
The research delves into various scenarios encompassing electricity transmission,
hydrogen production, e-fuels generation, and energy storage. It incorporates practi-
cal considerations such as instrumentation and electrical installations necessary for
VindØ to function as an electricity hub, enabling the transmission of electricity to
other countries, with elements like transformers, cable transmissions on the sea and
DC/AC converters among others. Additionally, the study examines different types
of electrolyzer plants (PEM, SOEC, and Alkaline), carbon capture technologies, and
various power-to-X plants like Ammonia generation, methanol, and kerosene. The
logistics of transporting these fuels via ships and pipes, where feasible, are consid-
ered, alongside the expected price of commodities in different countries and across
different years analyzed. This economic perspective aids in determining the optimal

Furthermore, this thesis contributes to current research in the renewable energy
sector by shedding light on several key aspects. It demonstrates the competitiveness
of power-to-X technologies offshore compared to traditional onshore plants, particu-

Inputs from other models developed by the Energy Modelling Lab (EML), particu-

and storages have been meticulously modeled, offering insights into the optimal
layouts and investment costs necessary for VindØ’s success.[7].
Building upon the foundational contributions delineated earlier, this thesis ex-
tends its analytical purview through a comprehensive temporal analysis designed to
chart the evolution of the proposed energy systems from their inception in 2033—
aligned with the commissioning of the initial 3GW offshore wind capacity—up to the
year 2050. Diverging from traditional full-year simulations, this study adopts an in-
novative approach by implementing an hourly resolution analysis that encompasses
4380 hours annually, selecting every second hour throughout the year to conduct
its examination. This refined methodological strategy effectively balances the dual
aims of enhancing model eﬀiciency and ensuring a high degree of representativeness.
The decision to analyze the system at two-hour intervals across the entire year,
rather than concentrating on specific months to represent seasonal variations, stems
from a commitment to mitigate the computational challenges associated with pro-
cessing the full spectrum of 8760 hours each year. This approach facilitates a more
extensive scenario exploration without compromising the integrity of the analysis.
By engaging with 4380 hours annually, the study is adept at capturing the fluc-
tuations in wind profiles and market prices with a precision that enables accurate
estimations of storage requirements and the optimal distribution of electricity and
E-fuels.
This methodology is not only tailored to the specific objectives of this thesis but
is also corroborated by existing literature, which suggests that a selective hourly
analysis can deliver findings that are robust and closely aligned with those derived
from more comprehensive full-year simulations. Consequently, this approach signif-
icantly contributes to the thesis by offering a judicious blend of analytical eﬀiciency
and accuracy, thereby reinforcing the validity of its conclusions [8].
Ultimately, this research not only advances academic knowledge but also offers
practical insights for stakeholders involved in the planning and implementation of
offshore energy projects. By addressing critical aspects of VindØ’s operation and its

2. Methodology, Input Data and Model
Construction
2.1
TIMES Model
The Integrated MARKAL-EFOM System (TIMES) model, developed by the In-
ternational Energy Agency’s Energy Technology Systems Analysis Program (IEA-
ETSAP), represents a comprehensive framework for energy-environmental modeling.
It serves to facilitate detailed analysis of energy systems, focusing on the optimiza-
tion of total system costs over a specified timeline. This involves considering a wide
range of energy processes and technologies, alongside their respective capital and
operational expenditures, adjusted to their present value.
The TIMES model is
particularly adept at inter-temporal optimization, allowing for the exploration of
strategic decisions today and their implications on future economic and environmen-
tal outcomes.[10].
The most important factor of the hole TIMES model is probably the objective
function.The objective function is the criterion that is minimized by the TIMES
model. It represents the total discounted cost of the entire, possibly multi-regional
system over the selected planning horizon. The objective function is the sum of
all regional objectives, all of them discounted to the same user-selected base year.

and the eleventh term is the salvage value of all capital costs of technologies whose
life extends beyond EOH.[10]
It is crucial to acknowledge that the model developed for the energy island in
this study operates as a single-region system.
This unique setup necessitates a
different approach to managing the objective function, which in this case is designed
to maximize the total profit from operating the island. This consideration includes
everything from initial investments and operational costs to profits generated from
exporting commodities.
Given the model’s singular regional focus, a novel method was implemented
to effectively manipulate the objective function. Specifically, a ”simulated dummy
import” was introduced. This mechanism artificially inflates the objective function
by simulating an import process that compels the system to engage in purchasing
activities. This setup is strategic, as the import process increases the initial value
of the objective function significantly, setting a high baseline for costs.
The true operational strategy of the island, which involves actual production and
export activities, then acts to reduce this artificially high expenditure. This method
==PAGE:14==
2.1 TIMES Model
8
allows for a clear evaluation of the island’s eﬀiciency by comparing the reduced
expenditure against the inflated baseline established by the dummy import.
To calculate the discounted profit, which is the primary goal of each scenario
within the model, the value of the objective function from the desired scenario
is subtracted from the value obtained in an additional scenario. This additional
scenario only considers the dummy import and disallows any investments. By setting
up the model in this manner, the actual profit maximization can be accurately
assessed by observing the reduction in total costs from the baseline set by the dummy
scenario to the operational scenario.
This approach ensures that the model’s profitability calculations are grounded
in the realistic operational dynamics of the island, despite the initial complexity
introduced by the dummy import. It provides a clear and quantifiable measure of
how well the energy island manages its resources and optimizes its economic output
under various conditions.
The modeling of the energy island as a single region within the TIMES frame-
work presented a unique challenge: the objective function, representing economic
profitability, should not be negative, but a profitable island inherently suggests a
negative outcome. To address this, a ”dummy import” was introduced to artifi-
cially inflate the initial value of the objective function. This method ensures that
the model operates within TIMES constraints by starting with a positive objective
function value.
This approach allows the model to assess profitability more straightforwardly.
If operational activities reduce this artificially inflated figure to zero or maintain
it positive, it indicates that the island is economically viable. This simplification
avoids the complexities of multi-regional modeling and focuses on optimizing eco-
nomic outcomes for potential investors and operators, ensuring the model’s practical
relevance in assessing single-region energy projects.
In the context of this study, the TIMES model was deployed to scrutinize the
development and operational intricacies of the VindØ Energy Island, a singular focus
aimed at bolstering renewable energy generation and distribution. This modeling
exercise was designed to capture the complex energy processes associated with the
island, as well as its interactions with external energy markets both electricity and
e-fuels. The objective was to evaluate the most viable strategies for optimizing the
construction and operation of the island, with a particular lens on the years 2040
and 2050. These timeframes are critical, as they correspond with the expectation
of the island operating at full capacity, making a significant contribution to the
renewable energy landscape.
To gain a nuanced understanding of the economic dynamics governing the energy
system, it’s essential to consider the temporal granularity with which the model
assesses the hourly prices of various commodities. The study employs a resolution

that meticulously analyzes the system over 4380 hours, effectively capturing data at

The adoption of an hourly resolution, particularly at two-hour increments, is in-

For instance, under-

planning of energy production and procurement to ensure economic eﬀiciency and
maximize revenue opportunities.
By incorporating a comprehensive array of energy technologies and accounting
for their costs and performance characteristics, the analysis facilitated a deep dive
into the energy flows of the Energy Island and its interactions with the surround-
ing countries. The flexible and dynamic nature of the TIMES model allowed for
the simulation of various scenarios, regarding different future prices for e-fuels and
implementation of PPAs among others. This was instrumental in identifying cost-
effective pathways for the Energy Island, highlighting strategic construction and
operational decisions that align with broader energy and environmental goals.

2.3
Technologies in the island
In the preceding sections, it was explored the conceptual underpinnings and the
potential of employing energy islands as pivotal hubs in the transition towards a
more sustainable and interconnected energy landscape. This part of the thesis delves
into the multifaceted investment opportunities that energy islands present, with a
particular focus on their role as electricity conduits linking various nations. The core
premise hinges on leveraging these islands as crucial nodes for distributing electricity
generated by offshore wind farms to neighboring countries, thereby augmenting the
green energy supply chain across borders[6].
Furthermore, this analysis casts light on the exploration of Power to X (PtX)
technologies by investors, a frontier deemed vital for the model under investigation.
PtX technologies stand at the cusp of innovation in transforming surplus renewable
electricity into storable and transportable forms, marking a significant leap in energy
utilization eﬀiciency. Specifically, the model scrutinizes the deployment phases of
offshore wind farms-envisaged at 3GW by 2033 in the first phase, followed by an
additional 7GW by 2040 in the second phase—alongside the integration of various
electrolysis plants[15]. An important consideration made, is the fact that for the

second phase offshore wind farms, the cost of 66/275 kV transformers have been
considered. This transformers will be platforms on the sea that will connect the
farthest wind-farms to the island, since the distance will be too long to just use
66kV cables as can be used in the first phase wind farms. This wind farms will be
build in modules of 1GW and the connections to the island are already included
in the investment cost, but for the second phase ones the mentioned cost has been
added [16].
The investment framework within the model is inclusive, permitting investments
across the three principal types of electrolyzers: Polymer Electrolyte Membrane
(PEM), Alkaline, and Solid Oxide Electrolyzer Cells (SOEC). These technologies
are pivotal for the electrolysis process, facilitating the conversion of electricity into
hydrogen, a clean fuel. The model extends the investment horizon to include plants
for producing E-methanol, E-ammonia, and E-kerosene. These facilities are instru-
mental in pioneering the future of sustainable fuels within Europe, addressing the
burgeoning demand for cleaner energy sources in the transportation sector and be-
yond[17].
Of particular interest is the production of E-kerosene and E-methanol, which
necessitates carbon dioxide (CO2). This requirement underscores the integration
of Direct Air Capture (DAC) plants within the investment model, highlighting a
holistic approach to carbon management and utilization in renewable energy systems.
The inclusion of DAC technology not only facilitates the sustainable production
of essential fuels but also embodies a critical step towards carbon neutrality by
extracting CO2 directly from the atmosphere for use in synthetic fuel synthesis[18].
Certain processes, such as the SOEC electrolyzer and the DAC plant, require heat
input. There are options to reuse the surplus heat from other processes or to deploy
an industrial electric boiler, which would allow each process to operate independently.
This arrangement applies even though the production of ammonia, kerosene, and
methanol is inherently linked to hydrogen production [19].
Additionally, a heat
pump has been modeled to address the temperature disparity: while the process
heat output ranges from 50-80°C, the SOEC electrolyzers need steam at 150°C for
their heat input[17]. Thus, if the model recommends using SOEC electrolyzers, a
heat pump with a COP of 3 will be employed [19]. On the other side, the heat used
as input in the DAC plant is at 100ºC [18].
Moreover, the model accounts for the logistical aspects of hydrogen transporta-
tion, incorporating investments in hydrogen compression infrastructure. This addi-
tion is crucial for enhancing the hydrogen value chain, enabling the compression of
hydrogen from the electrolysis output pressure of 35 Bar to 140 Bar. Such compres-
sion levels are requisite for its integration into existing hydrogen pipeline networks,
thereby streamlining the distribution process and ensuring the eﬀicient delivery of
hydrogen as a clean energy carrier to various demand centers[20]. The eﬀiciency of

2.4
Size estimation for Caisson Island
In the process of modeling the energy island, determining its optimal size emerges
as a critical consideration. This determination is intricately linked to the spatial
requirements of each system and technology that the island will host.
Drawing
upon the comprehensive analysis provided by the COWI report on the North Sea
energy island, detailed estimates of the space required by each component have been
derived [16]. Some of the values have also been taken from the DEA technology cat-
alogues [17]. These estimates are meticulously cataloged in Table 1 of the appendix,
offering a clear overview of the spatial allocation for each technological and system
component within the island. Nevertheless, for simplicity the space requirements

2.5
Storage Technologies
In the development and optimization of sustainable energy systems, the integration
and modeling of multiple storage technologies is critical. This section of the the-
sis elaborates on the many storage technologies that have been integrated into the
energy model, highlighting the need of having a specific storage solution for each
commodity. Notably, a dedicated CO2 storage facility has been established, specifi-
cally designed to handle CO2 prior to its use in operations that require it as an input.
This invention emphasizes a comprehensive approach to carbon control inside the
energy system.
Table 2 in the appendix has a complete collection of essential metrics such as cost,
round-trip eﬀiciency, and related losses for each storage technology[21]. Furthermore,
this chart gives insight on the electrical energy requirements for the operation of
different storage options. Among the options studied, hydrogen storage is the most
capital and energy-intensive alternative.
This necessity stems from the need to
incorporate a compressor that increases hydrogen pressure to 200 Bar, making it
suitable for storage and transport. When hydrogen is ready for export or use in e-fuel
production, it must be depressurized to meet the technical and safety specifications
of downstream systems. This depressurization process is crucial to maintain the
hydrogen’s integrity and ensure it meets the required standards for safe and effective
use in pipelines or as a feedstock in e-fuel synthesis processes. This step, essential
for operational safety and eﬀiciency, must be carefully managed to preserve the
quality of the hydrogen. . This need is separate from the other e-fuels (Ammonia,
Methanol and Kerosene), which do not require significant electrical inputs or extra
storage devices, since they are stored in liquid form. [21].
The model incorporates Lithium-ion battery technology as a potential option for
electrical energy storage. However, due to their high costs, large-scale investments in
lithium-ion battery storage are not currently favored within the model’s framework,
particularly because the model is designed to maximize financial returns. The high
initial and maintenance costs associated with lithium-ion batteries make them a
less attractive option for immediate selection, especially in a model that prioritizes
cost-effectiveness and profit maximization.[4].
While lithium-ion batteries are not extensively deployed in the current model
scenarios, further studies might be warranted to explore their long-term utility and
economic viability in the energy market. Lithium-ion batteries offer significant ben-
efits for electrical systems, including high energy density, rapid charging capabilities,
and a relatively long lifecycle. These attributes can be extremely valuable in man-
aging the variability of renewable energy sources and enhancing grid stability. Over
time, as technology advances and costs potentially decrease, lithium-ion batteries

could become more financially viable, thus meriting reevaluation within the model’s
future iterations.
This nuanced approach acknowledges the potential of lithium-ion technology to
contribute significantly to the energy market, especially in scenarios where long-term
energy storage and flexibility are paramount. Future studies should consider not only
the declining cost trends of battery technologies but also their role in supporting a

Through the incorpora-

suggestions are directly impacted by the temporal granularity of the power price
data, which also affects the model’s capacity to replicate the real-world operation
of the energy system.

Another important simplification made in this study is the use of electricity
prices solely from Day-Ahead markets, without considering other markets that are
extremely important for the various zones connected to the energy island. The Day-
Ahead market, where electricity prices are set one day before the actual delivery, is
just one component of the broader electricity market landscape[24].
Intraday markets allow trading of electricity on the same day as delivery and
are essential for managing fluctuations in electricity supply and demand throughout
the day. These markets are particularly crucial for systems heavily reliant on inter-
mittent renewable energy sources like wind and solar, enabling adjustments to be
made closer to real-time and offering a mechanism to respond to unforeseen changes
in weather conditions or system operation.
Balancing markets are used by transmission system operators to ensure that
the electricity grid remains stable, balancing supply and demand across the grid in
real time. Participation in these markets can be financially beneficial, especially for
entities that can provide quick adjustments to their output, such as battery storage
operators or flexible gas turbines.
Capacity markets are designed to ensure that there is suﬀicient capacity to meet
peak demand, paying participants to guarantee availability during periods of high
demand. This market is crucial for system reliability, ensuring that there are enough
resources to meet demand spikes or unexpected failures in the system.
Ancillary services markets are crucial for maintaining the quality and security of
electricity supply, providing services such as frequency control, voltage control, and
==PAGE:27==

black start capabilities. Energy islands could potentially provide valuable ancillary
services, given their substantial generation and storage capabilities.
In future studies, it would be essential to consider these additional electricity
markets as they can significantly impact the economic performance and operational
strategy of the energy island. Including these markets could provide a more detailed
and realistic analysis of potential revenue streams and operational flexibilities. It
would also enhance the model’s accuracy in forecasting the economic viability of
the energy island under various market conditions and regulatory environments.
This broader approach would allow for a more comprehensive assessment of how
the energy island can integrate into and benefit from the complex dynamics of the
regional electricity market.

In this chapter, it is presented and analyzed the results derived from the scenarios

These variations

methods available for these commodities. Ammonia benefits from pipeline transport,
which offers annual availability, whereas methanol transportation is restricted to
maritime shipping, available only three times per year.
These findings suggest that logistical considerations play a crucial role in deter-
mining the economic feasibility of these commodities. A more thorough discussion
of these issues and their broader implications will be provided in the conclusion
chapter, where we will integrate these insights with the overall study findings to
propose comprehensive recommendations and future research directions.

4.4
Economic results
This section is dedicated to providing a detailed overview of the economic outcomes
produced by the model across various scenarios, with a specific focus on comparing
these outcomes against external benchmarks to determine if they align with pro-
jected future expectations. A critical component of these economic outcomes is the
Levelized Cost of Hydrogen (LCOH).
The LCOH is a measure used to assess the cost eﬀiciency of hydrogen production
over the lifespan of a facility. It calculates the average cost per unit of hydrogen
produced, considering all the operational expenses over the system’s operational
life[27]. This metric is particularly vital in the energy sector as it helps stakeholders
understand the economic viability of hydrogen as an alternative fuel source, com-
paring it to other energy sources in terms of both cost and sustainability. Given the
global shift towards renewable energy sources, accurately gauging the LCOH allows
==PAGE:46==
4.4 Economic results
40
investors and policymakers to make informed decisions regarding the deployment of
hydrogen technologies.
In the context of the island’s energy infrastructure, the LCOH is particularly
relevant where there are investments in electrolyzers—key technologies for hydrogen
production.
As previously mentioned, and detailed in Table 4.1, both Alkaline
and Solid Oxide Electrolysis Cells (SOEC) electrolyzers are installed on the island.
However, the Alkaline electrolyzers are identified as the primary hydrogen producers,
with ongoing investments slated for 2040 and 2050, whereas the capacity for SOEC
is maintained at a constant level from 2040 onwards.
Figure 4.9 presents the LCOH for the different scenarios and technologies over
the years studied.
It is evident that there are no substantial differences in the
LCOH between the scenarios, which suggests a level of stability and predictability
in hydrogen production costs. Specifically, in 2040, the LCOH for hydrogen pro-
duced by Alkaline technology is approximately 2.45 EUR/kg, while for SOEC, it
is slightly higher at 2.47 EUR/kg. This minor difference is largely attributable
to the higher costs associated with SOEC technology, which, despite its higher eﬀi-
ciency in converting electricity to hydrogen, involves more expensive materials and
requires additional heat management systems.
By 2050, the LCOH for hydrogen from newly installed Alkaline electrolyzers
shows a slight reduction to 2.41 EUR/kg. Although this reduction is not drastic,
it reflects incremental improvements in technology and operational eﬀiciencies over
the decade. These figures highlight the evolving dynamics of hydrogen production
costs and underscore the importance of continual investment in technology to drive
down costs in the long term.
In the context of the North Sea energy island model, the Levelized Cost of Hydro-
gen (LCOH) for the SOEC electrolyzer is not calculated for the year 2050, as there
are no new investments in SOEC capacity planned for that period. Investments are
crucial for LCOH calculations because this metric integrates all costs associated with
the production of hydrogen—including capital expenditures, operational expenses,
and energy inputs—over the lifetime of the technology.
LCOH is typically calculated when there is an investment in capacity because the
installation of new equipment or the expansion of existing capacity can significantly
affect the overall costs of production. These costs include the initial capital outlay
required to purchase and install the technology, the operating and maintenance
costs incurred throughout its operational life, and the energy costs associated with
running the equipment. Without new investments in 2050 for the SOEC, the model
does not recalibrate or consider new cost inputs for this technology, hence no LCOH
is calculated.
In contrast, Alkaline electrolyzers receive continued investments in both 2040 and
2050 in the scenarios analyzed. This ongoing investment triggers a recalculation of
==PAGE:47==

the LCOH for Alkaline technology, as each new or continued investment adjusts the
cost structure—potentially introducing more eﬀicient technologies at lower costs or
reflecting changes in operational or energy expenses over time.
Therefore, the absence of new investments in SOEC electrolyzers in 2050 means
that previous cost calculations remain static with no updates, as no new financial
input or technological changes are introduced to affect the production costs. This
distinction in investment patterns between SOEC and Alkaline technologies within
the model directly influences the ability to calculate and update LCOH, highlight-
ing how financial strategies impact the economic evaluation of different hydrogen
production technologies.

For the e-Kerosene PPA scenario, it’s crucial to evaluate both the Levelized Cost
of Production (LCO) and the marginal price of production, as each provides different
insights into the economic dynamics of e-kerosene production.
The Levelized Cost of Production encapsulates the average total cost to produce
one unit of a product over its operational lifetime. This includes initial capital ex-
penditures, ongoing operational and maintenance costs, and financing costs. It’s cal-
culated by dividing the total lifetime costs by the total amount of product produced,
offering a comprehensive view of the overall financial viability of the production pro-
cess across its entire lifespan. This metric is particularly useful for understanding
long-term investment requirements and pricing strategies.
On the other hand, the marginal price of production focuses on the cost of pro-
ducing one additional unit of product beyond the current production volume. This
cost reflects the immediate expense related to increasing production output, such as
additional raw materials, energy, and potentially increased labor costs if the produc-
tion rate must be adjusted to meet higher demand. Unlike the LCO, which averages
costs over time and production volume, the marginal price is specifically concerned
with the cost implications of production scale changes at a specific moment.

4.6
Discounted Profit
As outlined in Section 2.1, the TIMES model operates by maximizing the discounted
objective function, which, for the purposes of this study, focuses on maximizing
the profits derived from operating the energy island in the way explained in the
mentioned section. TIMES model conventionally aims to minimize the objective
function. However, the primary goal of the model developed for this study is to
maximize the profits from the operation of the North Sea energy island. To align the
model’s function with this goal, a fictional mechanism termed a ”dummy import”
was introduced to artificially inflate the objective function. This adjustment was
necessary because, without it, a profitable operation would result in a negative
objective function value, which TIMES does not permit.
The concept of a ”dummy import” in the TIMES model serves a strategic func-
tion for simulating the economic operations of the North Sea energy island, since
the model just have one region ( the energy island) and is price driven this ”trick”
was necessary. This artificial import is created within the model to ensure that
the initial objective function is set artificially at a very high value . In the TIMES
framework, imports are generally treated as costs; thus, by integrating a significant,
albeit fictional, import cost, the model inflates the objective function substantially
above potential revenues.
==PAGE:65==
4.6 Discounted Profit
59
The primary purpose behind setting the objective function so high initially is to
provide a clear baseline against which the economic impacts of various investments
and operational activities can be measured. As the model runs various scenarios,
each operational change or investment made on the energy island works to reduce the
objective function. The strategic aim is to lower this inflated objective function as
much as possible. The difference between the elevated starting point of the objective
function and its value after implementing energy island operations represents the
profit.
To utilize this approach effectively, a ”dummy import” scenario is established
where the only action is this artificial import, with no other investments or op-
erational activities allowed. This scenario sets the benchmark for the maximum
possible objective function value. In subsequent scenarios, all operations, includ-
ing the dummy import, are permitted. Comparing the objective function from the
”dummy import” scenario to that of other scenarios with actual investments and
operations provides a quantitative measure of profit by showing how much the ob-
jective function is reduced due to the productive activities of the energy island.
This modeling technique allows stakeholders to clearly see the financial benefits

5. Discussion
The construction of energy islands by the Danish government and private actors
represents a pioneering endeavor fraught with technical and regulatory challenges,
many of which have emerged and will likely continue to unfold in the coming years.
This study aims to provide a robust tool for both public and private entities to
configure and understand the optimal operation of the North Sea energy island,
with economic performance as the primary driver of the model.
It is crucial to acknowledge that, despite its utility, this tool has limitations and
should not be regarded as an infallible source of truth. This caution is particularly
pertinent given the ongoing uncertainties surrounding the technologies that may be
installed on the island and the price expectations of various commodities. However,
the tool has proven valuable and serves as a solid foundation for future research,
thanks to its adaptability—the inputs are easily modifiable, allowing for extensive
scenario testing and optimization studies.
One significant limitation stems from the use of export prices for e-fuels derived
from the TIMES-NEU model by the Energy Modelling Lab. As discussed in earlier
chapters, while this model provides a useful starting point for price comparisons
across different countries, it incorporates assumptions that may not perfectly capture
market realities.
A critical aspect not considered in this study is the potential impact on electricity
prices in regions connected to the island when it begins exporting electricity. Current
results indicate that connections to Denmark and Germany are maximized based on
available transmission capacities, as shown in Table 4.1. In contrast, the connection
to the Netherlands is underutilized, suggesting that no investor or developer would
pursue this smaller connection due to its limited practicality. The influx of electricity
to Denmark and Germany would likely depress prices in these markets, a factor
not accounted for in the current model. Additionally, the model does not explore
the possibility of expanding connection capacities beyond current maximum, which
might reveal further opportunities for optimization. Other electricity markets such
as intra-day markets are not studied, just day-ahead[24].
Another noteworthy point is the annual availability factor used for hydrogen
and ammonia pipelines, which significantly influences the model’s outcomes, partic-
ularly in reducing the perceived need for storage. This contrasts with the storage
requirements for methanol and kerosene, which rely on ship transport and thus face
different logistical challenges.

The model’s assumption regarding the annual availability of pipelines and the

The annual availability factor used in the model assumes that the pipelines can
operate at their maximum capacity for a fixed percentage of the year, which sim-

minimum input limit for the hydrogen pipeline, which is typically necessary in ac-

require a certain level of throughput to ensure that the pressure remains stable and
to prevent issues such as gas condensation or integrity risks to the pipeline infras-
tructure. This minimum flow requirement would naturally influence how storage is
utilized, as any excess hydrogen produced beyond what is immediately needed for
export or domestic use would need to be stored until it can be piped through.
The lack of a minimum flow requirement in the model could lead to an underesti-
mation of the importance of storage facilities in the operational strategy. In a more
realistic scenario, storage would not only serve as a buffer for excess production but
also as a crucial component in managing pipeline operations, ensuring that there is
always suﬀicient hydrogen available to meet the minimum flow requirements.
To address these issues, future iterations of the model should consider integrating
more detailed pipeline operation rules, including minimum and maximum flow rates
and the impact of these parameters on storage needs. Additionally, the model could
benefit from a more dynamic approach to storage management, taking into account
the variable availability of pipelines and the actual consumption patterns of exported
hydrogen and ammonia.
Adjusting the model to include these factors would provide a more accurate rep-
resentation of the logistical challenges and operational costs associated with running
an energy island. This would enhance the tool’s utility for stakeholders who need
to make informed decisions about infrastructure investment, operational planning,
and the economic viability of the energy island project.

and potential points of failure in the system. Furthermore, the operating conditions

but also to governments seeking to harness renewable energy sources eﬀiciently and
profitably within the framework of Europe’s broader energy strategies.
Most existing studies on energy islands have been demand-driven, yet the utility
of a model that employs prices as a primary driver offers a valuable alternative
perspective.
This approach can provide fresh insights into future planning and
highlight economic aspects that might otherwise be overlooked. The model’s findings
also suggest that the energy islands could play a pivotal role in future energy systems,
with offshore wind farms and hydrogen production poised to become key components
of a competitive and sustainable energy landscape.
Looking ahead, there is scope for expanding the model to accommodate broader
variables and more complex scenarios. Future iterations could include modifications
such as dynamic electricity pricing to reflect changes in market conditions when
electricity is exported. A more detailed examination of pipeline utilization is also
warranted, such as establishing a minimum flow of hydrogen to maintain pressure
and reduce reliance on onshore storage. Additionally, incorporating sensitivity anal-
yses around varying investment and operational costs could provide deeper insights,
especially given the high degree of uncertainty about future market conditions.
These enhancements would not only refine the model’s accuracy and applicability
but also help stakeholders better understand and navigate the evolving dynamics
of global energy markets.
By extending the model’s capabilities and addressing
these critical factors, future studies can offer even more robust tools for planning
and developing energy infrastructure that aligns with the sustainable energy goals
of tomorrow.
Furthermore, considering the environmental impacts associated with the develop-
ment and operation of energy islands is crucial. Integrating environmental impact
assessments into the model could provide a holistic view of the ecological conse-
quences of various operational strategies. This would enable stakeholders to make
more sustainable choices and align projects with environmental regulations and pub-
lic expectations.
In summary, while the current model provides a solid foundation for understand-

ing and optimizing the operation of energy islands, there is a substantial opportunity
to broaden its scope and depth. Addressing the technological, environmental, and
socio-economic aspects of energy island development in future iterations of the model
will ensure that it remains relevant and valuable as an analytical tool in the rapidly
evolving landscape of global renewable energy.
