Summary
The current research aims to study the Fluid-Structure Interaction of a ﬂexible wing
(kite) using Immersed Boundary methods. The aerodynamic pressure distribution over
a kite causes the wing to deform and this deformation in turn alters the distribution of
external pressure over the surface of kite. The whole interaction is complex and depends
on the solution of higher order partial diﬀerential equations. The study of this aero-elastic
interaction becomes important since it allows to deﬁne a correct trajectory of the power
kite.
In the current research, a Numerical Modeling technique is applied to study the Fluid-
Structure Interaction of a ﬂexible wing. The research work is conducted in co-operation
with DTU and TU-Delft.
Two diﬀerent high ﬁdelity ﬂow solvers - Ellypsys2D (Finite Volume Methods) and Fluidity
(Finite Element Methods) are used to determine the external aerodynamic load. The
turbulent and transition ﬂow behavior is required to determine the drag and skin-friction.
Skin-friction is responsible for the shear eﬀects. This shearing can cause local buckling
phenomena like wrinkling which is a highly non-linear behavior and requires a diﬀerent
approach to model the governing equation. Drag is also an important factor to consider
because kites (canopy) have weak compressive properties. The analyses are performed to
ﬁnd the moderate external aerodynamic loads for which the structural deformations can
be large using Ellypsys2D.
A continuous forcing based Immersed Boundary solver based on Finite Element Meth-
ods (Fluidity) is used to study the incompressible laminar ﬂow behavior over the 2D
cylindrical immersed solid. The eﬀect of increasing Re can be captured with a very ﬁne
mesh discretization which increases the solution time and makes the current ﬂow solver
computationally expensive.
Berens’ Structural model [3] is reviewed and following observations are made:
• Triangular elements help in addressing shear locking
• Levy analytical method is used to verify the linear static kite model
• The amplitude of displacement is not converging in the static non-linear model
implementation

Summary
• The contribution of beam formulation has negligible eﬀect on the deformation when
the thickness of a non-planar conﬁguration is small compared to the cross-sectional
dimensions. Therefore, the whole solver becomes expensive in computation
• The contribution of beam in the internal force computation is not included for the
Newton-Raphson iterative method
A reduced ”Resin-Fibre” Finite Element static structural model is developed in Python
with respect to its application for thin composite structures. A slender massless plate of
0.3 m (chord) x 1 m (span) in cross-sectional dimension is used to represent the crude kite
geometry. The plate is clamped and loaded on either of the shorter edges respectively.
The validation of the proposed model is accomplished by varying the input parameters:
load, Young’s modulus and thickness.
In general, the thickness of canopy is less than 0.001 m.
Most of the kite materials
fail in this region and do not satisfy the linear theories even for a small external load.
Thus, iterative procedures or grid reﬁnement techniques are used to predict the non-linear
deformation of the ﬂexible wing.
The constant thickness (0.002 m) test shows that for a load greater than 1 N the Glass
Fibre Reinforced Plastic plate fails to satisfy the linear theory. Therefore, an iterative
solver is required to be incorporated to address the behavior of a thin plate below a
thickness of 0.001 m for an external aerodynamic load greater than 1 N.
Nearest Neighbor and Radial Basis interpolation algorithms are developed in Python
for the mesh interpolation required to study the Fluid-Structure Interaction. The study
reﬂected that the mesh interpolation using Radial Basis functions have better convergence
than the Nearest Neighbor approach. Further, oscillations in the interpolation of pressure
are observed in a conservative coupling approach which can be reduced by increasing the
number of discretization.
Two diﬀerent coupling algorithms are discussed based on the position of the mid-surface
in the ﬂow solver (Fluidity) [47]. In the present coupling algorithm implementation in
Python, the external aerodynamic pressure distribution (nodal loads) on the top and
bottom surface (mesh) is separately interpolated on the mid-surface structural mesh and
afterwards the total external pressure (load) is computed. The deﬂection obtained from
the structural Finite Element Analysis using the proposed model is transferred back to the
Immersed Boundary surface meshes in the ﬂow solver. The top and bottom surface (mesh)
deﬂection values are identical to the mid-surface deﬂection obtained via structural solver.
The interpolation of ﬁeld variables are carried out by using Nearest Neighbor algorithm
which is unconditionally consistent.
Fluid-Structure interaction study requires the correction in internal force when consid-
ering high external aerodynamic loads in the structural model(s) which is based on the
non-linear element formulation and requires considerable time. Moreover, the force resid-
uals are not corrected on the boundaries in the structural model(s). Force Residuals play
an important role in case of dynamic/mixed boundary conditions.
Thus, a next step
should be to add these two corrections in the structural model(s) and then proceed with
the Newton-Raphson load control iterative methods to perform a non-linear analysis at
high external loads.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Kites have always been used for recreational purposes by humans. Ancient civilizations
sometime used kites to propel the ground loads like ships, carriages etc. ”The Aeropleustic
Art or Navigation in the Air by the use of Kites, or Buoyant Sails” by George Pocock in
1827 is one of the ﬁrst books describing the kite maneuvering mechanisms. In literature,
the kite physics was well deﬁned but the dominance of fossil fuel in power generation and
advances in aerospace engineering hindered the growth of kite engineering for more than
a century.
The energy crisis in 1970s led the researchers to turn towards the renewable energy re-
sources for power production. This helped the kite to resurface on the engineering plat-
form once again. In 1976, Payne and McCutchen registered the ﬁrst patent [35] covering
almost all the concepts of airborne wind energy for electric power generation.
During that period, Bryan Roberts also conducted the ﬁrst experiments on ﬂying electric
generators to harness the energy of high altitude wind [37]. The year 1980 is considered
as the break through year when Loyd [25] published a paper on ”Crosswind Kite Power”
describing the concept of large scale power generation by using aerodynamically eﬃcient
kites.
The advent in the ﬁeld of control and tether technologies in the last decade proved fruitful
for the research in kite power. Around 50 commercial companies and academic institu-
tions [1]; Makani, KiteGen, Tu-Delft, DTU-Wind to name a few; are actively exploring,
researching and developing kites for power generation.
The two completely diﬀerent systems in their advance development phase are TU-Delft
kite and Makani Energy Kite [26]. The kite at TU-Deﬂt is a single inﬂatable membrane
wing ﬂying in the ﬁgure-of-eight or circling perpendicular to the wind.
The Makani Energy kite behaves like the tip of a wind turbine, responsible for most of the
energy. The kite follows a circular trajectory. The rotors on the kite act like propellers
on a helicopter to launch it from the ground station.

the research and development of air-borne systems. Some of the work presented in the

1.1
Pros and Cons of Kite Power
Airborne Wind Energy (AWE) system exploits the relative velocity between the air-mass
and the ground. AWE systems need to maintain a strong force against the relative motion
in order to harvest power from the wind. The advantages of using an AWE system for
the electrical power generation are [26, 1]
• The AWE system can access the wind at high altitudes which is much stronger and
more stable when compared to the wind near the ground
• The AWE system requires less material investment per unit of usable power than
the wind turbines
• The AWE system creates minimal adverse visual impact and does not make the
landscape unavailable for other usage when compared to the wind turbines
There are some adversities involved in harvesting high altitude wind using AWE system,
they are [1]:
• The kite when upscaled requires a larger area but then requires better control system
• For a large scale production, airborne generators can increase the weight of the
system
• The kite trajectory should be well deﬁned using active control system. It is a diﬃcult
task because the kite is continuously interacting with the ﬂow

1.2
Problem Introduction
The development of a power kite requires an understanding of a complex interaction
between the ﬂow, the structure and the ﬂight dynamics. A power kite is a ﬂexible con-
ﬁguration and the continuously changing force distribution over the kite’s surface alters
the deformation. In addition, the coupling of ﬂight dynamics to the ﬂow-structure system
makes the whole system extremely diﬃcult to comprehend.
Analytical solution of these complex interactions is tedious. Ignoring the ﬂight dynamics,
this interaction results in aero-elastic analysis of the power kite in a ﬂow.
The aero-
elastic study helps in deﬁning the trajectory of the kite and in general, helps in ﬁnding
the instability regions when a structure interacts with the ﬂow. In case of a ﬂexible wing
this interaction is highly non-linear. An accurate prediction of this complex aero-elastic
behavior helps in controlling the path in which a kite progresses. Considering a ﬂexible
kite in the ﬂow, there are two kind of non-linearities associated which requires to be
addressed to study the aero-elastic interaction:
• Flow non-linearities - generally occurring at a higher Re.
In the current work
Ellypsys 2D is used to address the ﬂow non-linearity which allows to ﬁnd the extreme
external loads.
• Structural non-linearities - In structural mechanics, three kind of non-linearities
are encountered [10] when an external force is applied.
– Material - the properties of a material are function of the state of stress or
strain. Some, examples are plasticity, creep etc. The current research restricts
to linear material assumption.
– Contact - happens when there is sliding contact with frictional forces, or when
the contact area is changed by altering contact forces. This is also ignored in
the current research.
– Geometrical - the deformation becomes large enough and the linear theories
fail to explain the deformation behavior. Thus, the equilibrium equations are
re-written with respect to the deformed structural geometry. The loads can
also change with altering deformed structural geometry. This is one of the
major sources of non-linearity in a power kite.
Thickness of kite is very small when compared to the other two cross-sectional di-
mensions. And, the canopy of a power kite has negligible stiﬀness in the out of
plane direction. The stiﬀness in this direction is added by the help of inﬂatable

Plate theory [2] is one of the simplest theories to describe the aircraft aerodynamics. The
wing under consideration is modeled as a rigid plate and can be used for understanding
the basics of a kite design. The ﬂaw in this theory of kite is that the rigid body model does
not simulate the ﬂow around the kite accurately because the kite is a ﬂexible structure
and disobeys the rigid body motion.
The Vortex Lattice Method (VLM)
[38] is used to describe the aerodynamics which
models the lifting surface.
It models the wing surface as a thin sheet comprising of
discrete vortexes to compute the lift and the induced drag required in an early design
stage. The limitation of this method is that the assumption of a small angle of attack for
a kite is crude. The inﬂuence of the thickness and viscosity are neglected in VLM.
A computationally eﬃcient aerodynamic model to study the eﬀects on a highly non-planar
structural conﬁgurations, like a kite, is done by coupling the VLM with the sectional
coeﬃcients to include the eﬀects of airfoil thickness and viscosity [18].
The Breukels
model
[8] uses 2D Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD) simulations on the airfoils
with varying physical properties (camber, angle of attack and thickness). Further, the
kite is divided into various sections along the span and the aerodynamic forces on each
section is determined using the ﬁtted functions for the inﬂatable tube and aerodynamic
constants. The ﬁtted functions are used to interpolate and extrapolate to ﬁt beams of
all diameters and internal pressure using the available test data. The weight factors are
used to distribute the load over the surface. This is the only model discussed so far which
takes the deformation of a kite into account. The kite is conﬁgured based on a multi-
body model and thus, depends on the values of parameters obtained via experiments to
perform the simulations. The demerit is that these artiﬁcial elements and parameters
have no direct physical meaning in order to simulate a kite. It is also a quasi-steady
model which ignores the dynamic ﬂow behavior [7].

Kite Structural Models
The diﬃculties in modeling a kite is accounted to its high ﬂexibility and the constant
interaction with the aerodynamic forces.
In past a lot of eﬀort has been put in the
development of kite models. Point mass models are the simplest physical kite models
with limited usage. The kite is treated as a point mass attached to the end of tether on
which lift, drag and gravity forces act [49]. The aerodynamic forces are determined using
the simple plate models. The drawbacks of this model are the absence of the ﬂexibility
and the dynamics of the kite. Further, the control is introduced directly by controlling
the pitch and yaw angles to change the aerodynamic forces, making it unsuitable to study
the diﬀerent kite behaviors.
Rigid body models are also implemented to model the kites similar to the technique used
in the aircraft modeling. A six degrees of freedom node based rigid model including the
moments of inertia of a kite
[14] and a model excluding the local moments of inertia
using three degrees of freedom [19] for a node are used to simulate the kite. The rigid
body model is questionable when it comes to accurately model the non-linearity which
arises due to the ﬂexibility of a kite [11].
Kite is also modeled using a number of lumped masses hinged together and connected
to the rigid rods [17] called as particle based models as shown in Figure 2.3a. Though,
the kite deformation is taken under consideration using this model which applies ﬂexible
constraints on the kite’s motion, the drawback is the non-ﬂexibility of the separate com-
ponents. Multiple plate conﬁguration is also used to model kites
[48, 49] as shown in
Figure 2.3b. The kite is supposed to be comprised of a ﬁnite number of plates hinged
together at the leading edge. The aerodynamic forces are determined by applying the
plate theory on individual plates. The model includes some of the deformation modes
but the rigid plate assumption makes the kite model inﬂexible and the model itself has
diﬃculty in ﬁnding the equilibrium position.
The software package Madymo is used in the development of FE modeling of a kite [39].
It models the inﬂatable leading edge and the canopy in a very realistic way and the
performed simulations correctly model the behavior of a kite without the inclusion of any
artiﬁcial parameters. The detailed nature of the model comprising of more than 30000
elements makes it computationally expensive.

[7] is developed using non-linear

2.3
Coupling at Fluid-Structure Interface
One of the key factor in performing an accurate FSI simulation is the interpolation method
used to satisfy the geometrical and equilibrium conditions on the Fluid-Structure inter-
face. The fundamental continuum and discretized equations are solved using either the
strong or the weak coupling algorithms.
The strong coupling methods uses a direct/monolithic approach to simultaneously run
the ﬂow and structural solver. The convergence rate obtained for the direct method is
of order two and is well suited for the problems where the mutual interaction between
the ﬂuid and structure is high
[50]. The direct coupling method is generally avoided
because it requires modiﬁcation in the existing ﬂuid and structural solver, it also reduces
the ﬂexibility of both the solvers for both spatial and temporal discretization and is
computationally expensive [51].
The partitioned methods solve the ﬂow and structural solver separately and thus, re-
quires a coupling algorithm to incorporate the interaction between the two solvers. These
methods maintain the ﬂexibility and are less expensive but have convergence issues for
strong interactions compared to the monolithic approaches
[12, 51]. The partitioned
coupling methods are classiﬁed as explicit, implicit and semi-implicit. Assuming y is the
ﬂuid traction, force per unit area which is normal to the surface, and x is the structural

iterations within a time step.

Chapter 3
Thesis Goal and Methodology
The research work is carried to address the Fluid-Structure Interaction (FSI) of a power
kite. This interaction is a complex problem because the kite is a highly ﬂexible structure
and thus, interface boundary becomes unsteady. The ﬂexible geometry of kite changes
the ﬂow behavior which in turn alters the kite shape. The study of this interaction is
important because it helps in determining the trajectory of a power kite.
3.1
Goal, Objective and Motivation
Numerical modeling is a credible, eﬃcient and accurate approximation method if it ad-
heres to, and can explain, the physics of the problem. Thus, the goal of the current work is
set as: ”Develop an eﬃcient Numerical approach to study the Fluid-Structure
Interaction of a ﬂexible wing”
The objective of the methodology is to yield a numerical FSI model which adheres to the
physics and the dependency on any artiﬁcial or ﬁtted parameter is minimal. The model
should also be eﬃcient which should make the approach fast without losing any essential
information important in capturing the kite behavior.
FSI in a kite poses a strong aero-elastic problem. The challenges in the mathematical
formulation, the visionary research at TU-Delft and DTU in the ﬁeld of Airborne Wind
Engineering and also the inclination of the author towards Numerical Methods provides
the inspiration and motivation to conduct this research.

The current research is based on the hypothesis that the FSI in a power kite can be
explained by ignoring the weight and damping of the kite. Material non-linearity is also
ignored in the present work.
The ﬁrst proposition is made on the basis that the structural weight and damping con-
tributions are negligible when compared to the aerodynamic load and damping. Thus, a
strict requirement applies on the research to use Navier-Stokes based ﬂow solver which
can be used to predict the dynamic behavior accurately. The second assumption makes
the FE structural model simple and robust. As discussed earlier, a general FSI problem
requires a structural solver, a ﬂow solver and a coupling algorithm at the interface, three
main tasks are deﬁned to successfully accomplish the set goal:
• Develop an eﬃcient static FEM based structural solver
• Perform Immersed Boundary simulations using Fluidity (ﬂow solver)
• Develop a mesh interpolation algorithm to couple the ﬂow and structural solvers
3.2.1
Finite Element Kite Modeling
A kite is a highly ﬂexible structure and on the application of external loads, does not
follow the linear Hooke’s law. Geometrical non-linearity comes from the altering stiﬀness
contribution arising from the non-linear displacements. This is caused from the altering
stiﬀness in these regions where linear theories fail to ﬁnd the correct deﬂection. This might
also change the direction of force as in case of inﬂated membranes. Iterative methods like
Newton-Raphson are used to solve the non-linear problem in FEM.
The geometrical non-linear FEM based structural solver [44] using Allman’s triangular
shell element with drilling degree of freedom is a good starting point. Bosch’s [7] work
is similar to the Tiso’s implementation [44] for the beam element formulation. The ge-
ometrical non-linearity can be addressed by both Newton-Raphson explicit and implicit
iterative methods. The other way to solve a non-linear equation is by using a very ﬁne
discretization on the grid. The non-linear static equation solved by Bosch’s [7] model is
given as:
K(q)q = Faero(t)
(3.1)
where K is stiﬀness, q is displacement and Faero(t) is the external aerodynamic load. The
non-linear static equation solved by Berens’ model, [3], is given by Equation 3.2 and in
the assembly of stiﬀness the element contributions are not directly added:
Ks(q) + Kb(q)q = Faero(t)
(3.2)
where subscripts s and b refer to shell and beam contributions respectively.

4.2
Description of EllipSys2D
The ﬂow solver used to analyze the ﬂow across a 2D kite airfoil is an in house software
of DTU called EllipSys2D, developed in co-operation with the Department of Mechanical
Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark and the Department of Wind Energy
at Risø National Laboratory [41].
The ﬂow equations are governed by incompressible Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes
(RANS), where the instantaneous ﬁeld variables are converted to time averaged vari-
ables. The pressure correction is implemented using the Semi-Implicit Method for Pres-
sure Linked Equations (SIMPLE) [34].
At ﬁrst, the momentum equation is solved to march forward in time then the continuity
equation is imposed by correcting the pressure. Since the procedure takes two step for
a single sub-iteration, the process is repeated until a convergent solution is obtained for
one time step. The variables are updated once a convergent solution is obtained and the
equations are solved for the next time step.
The non-linearity in NS momentum equation is addressed by under-relaxing the velocity

Upwind Scheme (QUICK) [22] with the interpolation order of 2. The pressure residual is

5.1

The FEM is a numerical method to solve Partial Diﬀerential Equations (PDEs).
A
PDE represents a ﬁeld problem. A ﬁeld problem is characterized by the determination
of a spatial distribution of one or more dependent variables [10]. For example, seeking
the displacements distribution of a beam under aerodynamic loading. The body under

Numerically, an FE mesh represents a system of algebraic equations, required to be solved,

5.2
Geometrical and Material Non-Linearity in Kite
In the current research work, the steady state pressure distribution over the kite’s surface
is calculated by solving the incompressible RANS ﬂow equation and is applied on the
structure as the external forces. The out of plane stiﬀness is negligible for the canopy
of the kite and a non-linear treatment is required to ﬁnd the deﬂection. Therefore, an
iterative procedure is applied (Newton-Raphson (NR)) to solve this geometrical non-
linear problem. One of these iterative procedures based on predictor-corrector method is
used by Bosch [6] to study the dynamic non-linear aero-elastic behavior of a leading edge
inﬂatable kite.
Material non-linearity arises due to the change in behavior of a material in diﬀerent
dimensions and in the current research it is considered to be linear.
This is a good
approximation since the material properties in a power kite are generally between 20-70
GPa for the leading and trailing edge beams, struts and for the canopy, it is 15-30 GPa.
5.3
Previous Models - Features and Drawbacks
Bosch’s [7] structural kite model uses Allman’s shell elements for the canopy and beam
elements for the leading and trailing edges. Berens [3] extended the work and considered
the whole kite as one surface, where each element has a shell as well as a beam contribu-
tion. Inﬂatable beams and canopy are treated separately. The beam element’s ﬂexural
rigidity is a third order function of thickness whereas, it is a linear function of thickness in
shell elements. Non-linear isoparametric beam formulation is used to describe the element
behavior.
Berens’ idea of using both the beam and shell behavior in the same element is novel
because it allows to capture the resin-ﬁbre behavior. Further, it allows to represent the
kite geometry in more realistic way (Figure 5.2).
Berens’ [3] implementation has a problem where the amplitude of displacement is always
increasing Appendix A. It also includes only the contribution of shell element in non-
linear strain displacement matrix.
This computes less internal force on each element
because the contribution of the beam is ignored (D.5). Both the above structural models
use Tiso’s [44] idea to add the contributions of higher order deformation gradients.

forcing approach. The method is simple to implement and is independent of the
spatial discretization scheme used.
The disadvantages of this method are the lack of control over numerical accuracy,
stability and conservation of properties at discrete level. This method also becomes
cumbersome for high Reynolds number ﬂow problems as the number of grid points
increases signiﬁcantly inside the immersed boundary. The structural points do not
coincide with ﬂow points used for discretization. This makes the continuous forcing

approach unable to provide sharp representation of IB and thus, it is inadequate for
high Reynolds number ﬂow.
• Discrete Forcing - The source term is included after discretization of the governing
equations.
Firstly, the equations are discretized over the whole domain on the
Cartesian grid without considering the IB. Afterwards, the presence of IB is included
by adjusting the discretization of the cells near the interface.
The discrete forcing approach is further classiﬁed into two types based on the im-
position of boundary conditions (BC) on the immersed boundary as:
– Direct BC imposition - The local accuracy of the solution assumes more
importance and the smooth spreading of forcing function distribution is of less
importance in case of high Re ﬂow. The direct BC imposition helps in retaining
the sharp interface of the IB with no spreading and gives better local accuracy.
This is achieved by modifying the stencil near the IB and afterwards, the BCs
are imposed directly.
– Indirect BC imposition - The Navier-Stokes equations cannot be integrated
analytically to determine the ﬁeld variable. In case of complex problems, the
forcing term cannot be found using simpliﬁed load models. A reasoned estimate
of velocity is assumed to solve the NS equation. This method removes the
stability issues encountered in continuous forcing approach but as the forcing
is required to be extended in the ﬂuid region the method becomes erroneous
for high Re ﬂow.
Since, a power kite generally ﬂies at high Re ﬂow, the best suited method is discrete
forcing approach with direct imposition of boundary condition.

7.3
Mesh Interpolation at Interface
It is mentioned earlier that the development of present coupling algorithm is an artiﬁcial
one. It is because the actual coupling taking place using the IB method is done by the
conservative projection methods. The immersed boundary treatment requires a volume
mesh for a 3D analysis whereas the structural solver needs only the mid-surface mesh to
perform a 3D analysis.
Therefore, instead of using two separate meshes as required in IB, the current method
requires at least three surface meshes, two for the analysis in ﬂuidity and one to conduct
the structural analysis. The structural solver makes it computationally eﬃcient but be-
comes expensive when it used to study FSI in the current framework. Figure 7.7 shows
the current requirement to carry out FSI of a kite using the IB method.
7.3.1
Selection of Interpolation Algorithm
A matching mesh is generally not found when studying the FSI because the grid require-
ment for a structural solver is completely diﬀerent from the ﬂow solver. In general, a ﬁne
mesh is used for the ﬂow solvers whereas less nodes are required to perform the structural
analyses.
In the current scenario, a two-way interface interpolation is required to transfer the pres-
sure from the surfaces to the mid-surface of structure, and after performing the FEA the
displacements of the mid-surface are again interpolated back to the immersed solid in
Fluidity.
Based on [4, 46], radial basis and NN mesh interpolation algorithms are developed in
Python for a 2D case ??. The RBF gives better accuracy but takes more time. NN is easy
to implement, has a good accuracy when used for a reﬁned mesh. The implementation
can be referred in the special course report.
In the current development, extension of NN interpolation is done for a 3D point. Apart
from being simple in implementation NN is unconditionally consistent.
Further, the
requirement is to externally couple the solver with Fluidity and thus, RBF can be ignored
which is time consuming.

7.3.2
Nearest Neighbor Interpolation
Nearest Neighbor (NN) is amongst the simplest method to transfer data from one mesh
(A) to another mesh (B). A search algorithm is used to determine the point qA in mesh
A that is closest to a given point qB in mesh B. Afterwards, the variable in qB is assigned
the same value as in qA. Thus, the transformation matrix HAB becomes a boolean matrix
and makes this method unconditionally consistent.
7.4
FSI Algorithms
In Fluidity, one of the surface mesh of implicit solid cannot be used for the interpolation
since it can never be the true representation of the mid-surface mesh of the immersed
body in the ﬂow solver.
Thus, the interpolation is done by ﬁnding the external loads on the top and bottom
surfaces at ﬁrst. These values are then interpolated on the mid-surface of the structural
solver and ﬁnally, the vector addition of the forces at each node on the mid-surface of
structural mesh is carried out. This completes the interpolation of external loads from
ﬂow solver to the structural solver.
The structural equations are solved for the interpolated external loads and the deﬂections
in each direction are obtained. A second NN interpolation is performed to transfer the
deﬂection values to the ﬂow solver. This is achieved by interpolating the mid-surface
deﬂection values identically on both the surfaces of the solid mesh in the ﬂow solver.

Conclusions
The ”Numerical Modeling of Fluid Structure Interaction in a power kite” is
discussed in the current work. Although, the research work restricts to a low Re and
laminar ﬂow kite analysis, Computational Fluid Dynamics analysis around a 2D airfoil in
a turbulent ﬂow allowed to ﬁnd the extreme loading points. These ’loads’ help in ﬁnding
the critical failing region of the composite materials.
There are two versions of the structural model. The one in Matlab has geometrical non-
linearity included in the solver. The model is validated for diﬀerent boundary conditions
in a static sense. The results show that convergence is obtained in NR implicit methods
but it requires engineering judgement to deﬁne the load steps.
The second version of the structural model is an open source development in coherence
with the previous model. This model is currently a linear solver representation of the
full kite mechanics. The limitation of Berens’ [3] model is overcome by the proposed
”Resin-Fibre” model. The new structural model testing is done on the two important
composites, GFRP and Glass-Epoxy, used in Kite and Wind Turbine Engineering. The
critical load and thickness regions are deﬁned for these materials based on the thin plate
cantilever test.
The test on varying thickness is an important ﬁnding because the ﬂow solver has a limi-
tation on the thickness of an element. The thickness of thin wings are very small. In that
case, IB method requires a large number of discretization near the interface. The time
needed to solve the governing equations increases and to overcome this discrete forcing
approach can be used. In a 3D geometry, these volumes are supposed to increase more
and thus, for a high Re ﬂow problems like in case of kite continuous immersed boundary
approach should be avoided.
The varying load test allowed to ﬁnd the failing materials at diﬀerent external load values
for a 0.002 m thick plate. The results of the varying load test show that a kite made
of GFRP requires a non-linear treatment if the applied external load is greater than 1
N. This can be achieved by performing the Newton-Raphson iterations but the model
requires a correction for the internal force computation.

Also, a complete FEM analysis is only accomplished when the residuals are computed so,
this needs to be computed also. The residual computation is important when a mixed

Chapter 9
Recommendations
The aero-elastic analysis of a ﬂexible wing is a complex process and the technique used
to solve this problem can vary based on the methods used. The current work uses a
Numerical Method based approach to address the problem. Resin-Fibre structural model
is a ﬁrst step non-linear solution for any given load. As discussed earlier, for a kite made
of low strength materials, the deformation obtained by solving the equations are large
and requires non-linear treatment.
Therefore, an iterative method (Newton-Raphson) is required to be added to the whole
structure model else for a large geometry the grid based method can become computa-
tionally expensive. The addition of this iterative method should be carried but a prior
step would be to correct the internal force calculation in the model.
The work on mixed boundary conditions can be taken up, where the focus should be on
the study of absorbing and reﬂecting boundaries for the structural analysis. This can help
in disassociating the Rayleigh-Ritz method used for dynamic analysis, which is based on
artiﬁcial damping. In case of ﬁxed boundaries, the residual correction in forces can be
ignored but in case of a mixed/dynamic boundary, it is questionable to not correct the
force residuals.
The 2D Radial Basis Function (RBF) interpolation can be extended to a 3D geometry.
This can help in comparing the eﬃciency of Nearest Neighbor interpolation based coupling
algorithm developed in the current work.
The work on higher order shape functions for the Finite Element based Immersed Bound-
ary ﬂow solver is a wide area of exploration for the Researchers. It is also found from
the literature [32] that continuous forcing approach based IB methods are not suitable
for modeling the high Re ﬂow and thus, the discrete methods should be adopted. In
particular, discrete forcing based IB with direct boundary condition imposition can be
taken forward.
These developments can be further extended to the Arbitrary Lagrange-Euler (ALE) grid
formulation to study the Fluid-Structure Interaction. This can help in a comparative aero-
elastic study of a power kite using Immersed Boundary methods and ALE formulation.
