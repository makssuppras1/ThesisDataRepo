Abstract
According to the International Maritime Organization, the shipping industry is responsible for the 3% of
the global emissions in the environment. Because of this, the IMO is setting strict limitations on levels of
pollution that can be emitted by vessels. At the beginning of 2016 the organization has introduced, under the
Tier III program, peculiar emission control areas. The legislation emitted represent a great challenge for the
shipping industry. Now-a-days vessels are mainly power by low reﬁned crude oil which contains high levels
of sulphur and it is responsible for the emissions of considerable amount of nitrogen oxides. As a ﬁrst action,
a switch to a clearer fuel could represent a ﬁrst step to meet the introduced regulations. Implementation on
board of selective catalytic reduction unit and exhaust gas recirculation unit also contribute in a signiﬁcant
way to achieve the emission level set by the IMO. Other solutions have been highlighted in various studies.
A considerable amount of them suggest the introduction of waste heart recovery systems on board vessels.
The objective of this project is to assess the potential of installing a waste heat recovery system on board
two speciﬁc size of container ships. The two vessels are LNG fuelled and their engines have been evaluated
working under Tier II and Tier III programs. In order to fulﬁll the Tier III program, the ships are provided with
the installation of a selective catalytic reduction unit and an exhaust gas recirculation unit. The conducted
study has been carried out evaluating the design and off design performances of various conﬁgurations of
organic Rankine cycle mounted on board the two ships.
Two main different layouts of waste heat recovery systems have been constructed using the software Matlab
and results have been compared. The ﬁrst conﬁguration contemplate the installation of an heat exchanger
positioned at the outlet of an SCR or EGR unit. The exhaust gases are conveyed to a boiler where the heat
can be transferred to an organic compound. The heat harvested is exploited by a turbine that generates power
that could be sent to the propeller of the ship thus reducing the fuel consumption and therefore the pollution
emissions.
The second layout foresee an enlargement of the steam drum,a component already present on board. In the
latter unit a greater amount of steam could be generated. The excess steam could be sent to the boiler where
the heat can be collected by the organic Rankine cycle. Results from the model optimization suggest that the
preferable conﬁguration is the one that directly provides the organic compound with the heat available at the
exhaust gases, the recirculating gases (where available) and the jacket water.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
According to the Danish Ecocouncil [12] [42] about 90 % of the global cargo is transported by ships making
the shipping the primary source of cargo transportation around the world. The ability to exploit the water as
a viable solution to emigrates and to commerce dates back to the very beginning of the human history.
In the modern days shipping still counts as the primary solution of global cargo transportation. Shipping
transportation has been proven to be the most eco-friendly way of transportation [35] when considering
the amount of CO2 per unit of weight. This is due to the fact that modern day vessels and cargo ships
are able to stock and carry a greater amount of goods (stored in containers) compared to all the other well
known transportation machinery. In particular, emissions from transportation of cargos by train has 2-5 times
higher CO2 emission per ton while by truck has CO2 emitted 5-15 times higher. Consequently, shipping is
a favourable transport in regards to global warming. However, shipping emits above hundred times more
SO2 and particles compared to modern trucks per ton cargo and above 10 times more NOx per ton cargo.
Given the scale and the importance of the shipping industry today it is therefore crucial to take initiative act
at contrasting this trend.
Usually, anti-pollution regulations are enacted from single nations or,for example, the European Union. The
problem that has to be faced concern, at ﬁrst glance, the trade routes themselves. Cargo ships in fact are
constantly travelling around the world, from one side to another, and the majority of the time they are sailing
in international waters. The main problem derives from the lack of a uniﬁed international legislation act
at overruling the single nation’s regulations in this matter. Shipowners, thanks to the so called re-ﬂagging
strategy, can very easily decide under which nation’s regulation they want to subsist. Doing so, vessels can
avoid the more severe environmental restrictions [12].
Why and how this happens are simple questions that ﬁnd a uniﬁed and corresponding answer in the running
costs of the shipping. Concentrations of particulars, SOx and NOx emitted ﬁnds their cause in the type of
fuel exploit for the propulsion of the ship.
At the moment HFO (Heavy Fuel Oil) is still the most used fuel for vessels [8]. Economic reasons overrule
the shipping industry and the need of a cheap fuel takes a higher priority.
HFO is a pure residual oil roughly equivalent to No. 6 fuel oil that contains high quantity of sulphur and
nitrogen. Its low price derives from its very poor reﬁned nature and its high availability.
The intent of this project is to give an enlightening prospect about the possibility of exploiting a cleaner fuel
while highlighting is beneﬁts when considering the operations of a ship.

1.1
Fuels
As mentioned above heavy fuel oil is still playing a key role in the shipping industry.Although HFO is so
highly diffused there are on the market some fuels as an alternative. Now-a-days the main fuels in use are
the following:
• MGO (Marine Gas Oil)
• MDO (Marine Diesel Oil)
• IFO (Intermediate Fuel Oil)
• MFO (Medium Fuel Oil)
• HFO (Heavy Fuel Oil)
The list above highlight the main different kind of fuel on the market. As it can be seen they are all oil
derivatives. All of them are in fact products of different level of the same reﬁnement process of the crude oil.
The list also is in order of reﬁnement with the marine gas oil being the most reﬁned. HFO,as previously
stated, is nearly fully composed by residuals of the reﬁnement processes.
The more reﬁned the fuel is the less amount of sulphur it contains. An higher level of reﬁnement also
means an higher price of purchase justifying why, without a proper legislation, shipowners can save costs by
exploiting HFO price’s advantages.
Beyond its economic attraction heavy fuel oil contains quantity of sulphur equal to about 4% of its original
composition.

1.4
Liqueﬁed Natural Gas
Natural gas is presented today as an attractive alternative instead of the conventional HFO. It presents
concentration of nitrogen much inferior when compared to heavy fuel oil and almost no traces of sulphur
at all. Exact values can’t be given since natural gas composition slightly vary depending on where it is
extracted from.
NG is mainly composed of methane for more than its 90% with smaller percentages of other hydrocarbons.
Its caloriﬁc value is higher than the HFO and it’s assessed to be around 50 MJ/kg.
Most importantly, now-a-days natural gas is cheaper than the conventional fuel [8]. An increase in the cost
of the traditional fuels is also expected in the near future [41]. Considering the environmental and economic
beneﬁts, natural gas sets itself as an attractive fuel. As pointed out in the DNV report natural gas represent the
most promising solution in the maritime industry [17]. Furthermore, natural gas is a consolidated technology
in on-land applications and considered to be a good method for tackling the environmental pollution issues.
The idea of implying LNG (liqueﬁed natural gas) as the main fuel on-board a vessel it’s not new either.
Worldwide LNG-carriers naturally adopted this source to power the propeller [5] and there are also some
examples of other type of ships LNG-ﬁred as well [14, 32].
So far then liqueﬁed natural gas is shaping up without presenting any major ﬂaws and it’s ﬁtting very well
for the future scenarios that IMO are outlining for the maritime shipping industry. Other stronger points in
its favour are: a non toxicity nature, an 80% reduced emission of NOx and up to 75% reduced emission of
particles compared to a standard oil derived fuel, the CO2 generated in the combustion process is reduced by
a 20-30% ﬁgure when compared to standard Diesel fuel [8]. The liquefaction process and its storage are
consolidated technologies and, once liqueﬁed, natural gas takes 1/600th of its space.
That said some drawbacks can be found. Implementation of LNG tanks on board will lead to a more complex
ships building process as a consequence of a more complicated design. In its liqueﬁed state, compared to the
diesel,the gas requires double the space for the same energy content [8]. Moreover,LNG is stock in tanks at
temperatures equal to -160◦C [35],tank insulation takes therefore a critical part of the design process. Before
the gas can be injected in the combustion chamber it needs to be preheated.
Gas engines can be affected by leaks of unburned methane, the so-called methane slip [37]. Methane present
a 25-time higher global warming potential than CO2 [29]. Methane is also very ﬂammable creating the
necessity for a higher level of safety solutions [65]. Ideas of implementing LNG as a primary fuel on
board ships that are not LNG carriers have already been shown in several studies [3, 5, 8, 26, 43], including
bunkering infrastructure related matters [7]. About this last topic the LNG bunkering facilities have to be
extended to the majority of the ports. Introduction of further ECAs in the future could accelerate this process.

CHAPTER 2
Waste heat recovery systems
This chapter will be focus on describing the technologies suited for the waste heat recovery(WHR). The
attention of this project is directed on organic Rankine cycles that could exploit different heat sources on
board a vessel. Container ships and large vessels are usually provided with big and slow-pace two-stroke
turbocharged engines mainly powered by diesel under the form of HFO. Generally, these engines have
proved to perform at high levels of efﬁciency, with values of around 50 % [54]. This is already consider to
be a reasonable achievements in terms of efﬁciency. The remaining 48-51% of the total energy though, is
trapped in the exhaust gases under a form of heat which is discharged directly in the ambient [48].
On vessels, a waste heat recovery unit partially recuperate the otherwise wasted heat thanks to the presence
of a drum that generate steam for the service on board [35].The steam drum is usually undersized and is not
able to harvest the entire potential of the exhaust gases.
With the increasing limitations on emission levels issued by the International Maritime Organization,
implementations of waste heat recovery systems on boards could serve the purpose of reducing the fuel
consumption and therefore the environmental impact of the shipping industry.
2.1
Waste Heat recovery systems
A waste heat recovery system (WHRS) is a unit aimed at capture and exploit the energy contained in hot
streams which can be used to improve the energy efﬁciency of a particular process. Now-a-days waste heat
recovery are largely adopted in the industry sector.
As mentioned above, a WHRS installed on board could convert the thermal energy discharged by the engine
into either supporting the propulsion of the ship or delivering electricity on board a vessel [48]. For the
purposes of this work the entire amount of energy recuperated will be delivered to a generator positioned
between the engine and the propeller [2]. By doing so, the power eventually generated by the waste heat
recovery unit will supply mechanical power at the main shaft thus decreasing the engine load helping
reducing the amount of pollutant generated in the combustion chambers of the main engine.
The beneﬁts of implementing a WHR unit aimed at assisting the engine in the propulsion is not only
perceivable in terms of emissions reductions, which helps in meeting the legislation issued by the IMO. It
also contributes to a reduction of fuel consumption and therefore a reduction in fuel costs.
Albeit all the positive aspects highlighted, the installation of such unit implies an increase in the capital cost
of the ship and an increase in the maintenance costs.
For the implementation of waste heat recovery on board a variety of alternatives has been investigated by the
literature:
• Kalina cycle [46, 59] introduced by Dr.Kalina in 1983 [4]

2.2
Exploitable heat sources on board
On a ship it is possible to identify four sources of exploitable heat [48]. The exhaust gases, the jacket cooling
water, the scavenge air and the lubricating oil. For the purposes of this project, only the heat available from
the main engine is considered. In the ﬁgure below a Sankey’s diagram illustrate how the wasted energy is
distributed among this four main heat sources in a 12K98ME/MC engine manufactured by MAN Diesel &
Turbo and investigated by Shu et al. [23].
Figure 2.1: Sankey’s diagram of the wasted heat on board a vessel [23]
As it can be seen the four streams of discharged energy are differentiate by their relative quantity of heat.
Although the speciﬁc percentages might not be the same as the ones of the engines under investigation
in this work, it is reasonable to assume that the proportions highlighted above present more or less the
same distribution for the engines investigated in this project. In any case they will not play a role in the
calculations. Percentages have been highlighted for the sake of consideration.
The scavenge air (or air cooler) is the air that is sent to the engine after it has passed trough the turbocharger
unit. Due to the high level of compression the air is heated to considerable temperatures. The pressurized
ﬂuid is conventionally sent directly to the main engine where the combustion process occurs. Its disadvantage

is that the temperature of the air greatly vary upon the engine load of the engine. The data extracted from the
engines investigated in this work shows temperature levels between 150◦C and 200◦C when considering the
nominal load. On the contrary, at minimum load the temperature values are practically halfed. In any case,
the mass ﬂow of air received by the combustion chamber is considerable and therefore it contains a sensible
amount of heat that could be exploited by a waste heat recovery system.
The exhaust gases represent the main stream and they are the product of the chemical reaction that occur in
the combustion chambers of the engine. The compressed air is combusted with the injected fuel and the
results of this mix is conventionally sent directly to the chimney. The amount of exhaust gases is marginally
larger than the scavenge air because it is the combination of the exhaust air and the burnt fuel. Exhaust gases
represent the high temperature source which make them the primary source of heat when designing a waste
heat recovery unit.
In order to keep the engine at acceptable temperatures, or rather in order to prevent problems of overheating,
a stream of water is forced into the jacket of the engine. The jacket cooling water is usually then sent
to a condenser where it can discharge the accumulated heat directly in the sea. The jacket water present
temperatures of around 85 ◦C [41] which makes it a intermediate heat source. It has the advantage of
been basically constant for every step in the engine load. In this work, the jacket cooling water has been
considered constant in every step of engine power [16].
The lubricating oil can also be considered an eligible source of heat but given its small amount and the low
temperatures its use for a WHRS is discouraged. The ﬁrst three streams have been considered in this project.
2.3
Organic Rankine cycle
As mentioned before, organic Rankine cycle proved to be the most suitable unit to install on board as a waste
heat recovery system. The ORC is based on the traditional steam Rankine cycle which is a consolidated
technology that ﬁnd a numerous applications on land.
The steam Rankine cycle is directly based on the ideal Carnot cycle. In the latter work the french military
engineer and physicist Carnot suggested the possibility of extracting work from a thermodynamic process
composed by two iso-thermal and two iso-entropic transformations operating between two heat sources
characterized by different temperatures [9].
Figure 2.2: The Carnot cycle
Although the Carnot cycle is able to achieve the highest efﬁciency level among all the other cycles working
==PAGE:18==
CHAPTER 2. WASTE HEAT RECOVERY SYSTEMS
8
between two heat sources [10], it presents various technical limitations :
• the ﬁrst two-phase transformation between state 1 and 2 can be accomplished with ease. Such
transformation will imply a strong constrain on the maximum temperature the cycle can achieve.
In fact, it can not be higher than the critical point of the working ﬂuid. If water is considered, the
maximum temperature of the cycle cannot be higher than 374◦C thus limiting the efﬁciency.
• The iso-entropic transformation between state 2 and 3 is possible thanks to an expander unit such as a
turbine. As the process moves towards state 3 the quality of the vapour decreases thus condensating
droplets of ﬂuids. The bumps caused by the droplets have the effect of greatly eroding the blades of
the turbine.
• between state 3 and 4 the ﬂuid is partly condensed. The working vector is condensed till a speciﬁc
value of vapour quality which is something that is of very difﬁcult control.
• the iso-entropic transformation between state 4 and state 1 contemplate the implementation of a
compressor that will increase the pressure (and therefore the temperature) of the ﬂuid. At point 4
the ﬂuid is a two-phase state and presents vapour with droplets of liquid. Such mixture is of difﬁcult
pressurization.
Carnot’s work was published in 1824 and it is the foundation of the Rankine cycle.

(b) Scheme of the rankine cycle
Figure 2.3: Ideal Rankine cycle
The Rankine cycle addresses the issues and technical limitation of its predecessor:
• the vapours is super-heated, thus enabling the possibility to achieve higher temperatures.
• The super-heated steam can be expanded in the turbine without condensating droplets of liquid on the
blades.
• Once the ﬂuid passes trough the turbine it is fully condensed thus avoiding the operating machine to
work with a two-phase ﬂuid.
In ﬁgure 2.3a a steam Rankine cycle is represented in a T-s diagram. Steam Rankine cycle, as mentioned in
the previous paragraph, can represent a way of recovering the dissipated heat on board. They utilize water as

the working ﬂuid.
The four components highlighted in ﬁgure 2.3(b) allow the four thermodynamic transformations.
• the water is pressurized by a pump (state 1 to state 2).
• the ﬂuid enters the evaporator. Here the water absorb the heat from the hot heat source and it vaporizes.
The super heated steam is presented at the inlet of the expander (state 2 to state 3).
• the hot steam enters the turbine and mechanical work is extracted from the turbomachinery (state 3 to
state 4).
• The exhaust steam is condensed in an heat exhcanger where it discharges the remaining heat to a lower
temperature sources and returns to the pump (state 4 to state 1).
The success of the steam Rankine cycle lies in the double-phase of the water. When the water is evaporated
the ﬂuid is able to absorb a consistent quantity of energy while keeping the temperature constant. The
accumulated energy can then be exploited by the expander.
Organic Rankine cycle are based on the maturity of the technology of the corresponding steam cycle. The
only difference is the working ﬂuid implemented. Instead of water, ORCs make use of organic compounds.
The thermodynamic processes are unchanged. The organic ﬂuid is chose among the following characteristics:
• low cost and great availability
• good stability at high temperature to avoid the deterioration of the substance.
• a low level of viscosity, ensuring a lower level of friction losses and high heat transfer coefﬁcients
[49] .
• high value of latent heat which ensure a higher absorption of energy.
• an Iso-entropic or positive saturation vapour curve.
• For a matter of safety the ﬂuid should not be toxic, ﬂammable nor corrosive.
• low environmental impact in terms of global warming potential (GWP) and ozone depletion potential
(ODP).
In the work carried out one of the objective is to correctly tie the three heat sources present on board
(described in the previous paragraph) to an organic Rankine cycle in the most efﬁcient way. Guaranteeing
therefore the extraction of the maximum amount of net power output achievable.
Exhaust gases represent the most valuable source of heat on board. The heat contained in them can be
exploited by evaporating the organic compound. At the outlet of the engine unit the exhaust gases are
conveyed to a once-through boiler where the heat transfer with the organic compound occurs. Such heat
exchanger is the evaporator showed in the ﬁgure 2.3(b). When the organic compound is presented at the
boiler it will be heated and evaporated under speciﬁc values of pressure and temperatures.
The evaporated and super-heated ﬂuid will then enter a turbine (centrifugal or axial). The turbine is attached
to a generator which convert the mechanical energy to supply the propeller of the ship. The exhaust organic
compound is subsequently sent to a sea water condenser to discharge the remained heat. A pump close the
base cycle.
The lay out described represent a simple conﬁguration of ORC but the architecture of the cycle can be of
various nature: simple, regenerated, multi-pressure levels or cascaded [47]. In the literature there is a vast

Fluids
The selection of the appropriate organic compound is a crucial step that determines what the organic Rankine
cycle can achieve in terms of power output and efﬁciency. There is a vast literature that focus on the research
of suitable ﬂuids for ORC on board a vessel. The selection of the appropriate organic compound vary upon
the decision of which kind of heat source has been considered.
In the work of Andreasen at al. [30], considering exhaust gas as the only heat source, dry hydrocarbon such
as benzene, toluene and the siloexane MDM reached the highest power output when keeping the total area
of the heat exchanger constant. With the same heat source, Larsen et al. [58] individuate benzene,MM

pure ﬂuids. R236fa overtake the R245fa when harvesting the heat from the jacket cooling water and the
scavenge air [24].
Refrigerants also abound the literature when only exploiting low heat sources, such as the jacket cooling
water of the engine. R245fa is found by Andreasen et al. [30] to be the best choice when ﬁxing the heat
exchangers area in terms of power output. Yang [63] investigate organic compounds such as R245fa, R245ca,
R1233zd, R1234zf,R600 and R1234ze. Particular credit has been given, from the results of the work of Song
et al. [31], to R236fa and R245fa compounds. R245fa has been exploited for the organic Rankine cycle
developed by Calnetix Technologies [18]. Baldasso et al. [16] introduced in the pool of ﬂuids n-pentane,
n-butane, isopentane, isobutane as eligible compounds suited for the exploitation of heat from exhaust gases
and jacket cooling water.
Albeit the considerable number of ﬂuids investigated, attention has to be given to the actual legislation
concerning organic compounds. According to the Marpol Annex VI [44], for example, CFC are banned from
any on board installation since 2005. The Montreal Protocol [60] ,coupled with the regulations issued by
the European parliament [61], will limit and subsequently phase-out substances with high ozone deplection
and high global warming potential. The EU is particularly focusing on banning ﬂuorinated compounds.
HCFC (hydrochloroﬂuorocarbons) will ﬁnally been banned from January 2020. Although substances such
as Hydroﬂuorocarbons (HFC) are considered non-ozone-deplecting and they are therefore tolerated from the
Montreal protocol, they will might be banned in the European Union in the next future. In the HFC category
are grouped compounds such as R245fa and R236fa. On the other hand, hydrocarbons are not submit to
particular restrictions. The main concerns derives from their high ﬂammability but, as for now, no actions
have been taken so far.
Alongside hydrocarbons and siloexanes (MM and MDM), McLinden et al. [40] and Liu et al. [62] suggested
the implementation of hydroﬂuorooleﬁns(HFO). Such substances presents very low level of global warming
potential and low ozone depletion potential. HFO such as R1233zd(E), R1234yz are investigated in the work
of Baldasso et al. [16].

3.2
Selective Catalytic Reduction
The Selective Catalytic Reduction reactor (SCR) make use of high temperature and introduction of ammo-
nia,as urea, to transform, thanks to a chemical reaction, the NOx into nitrogen and water [ﬁgure 3.1].
The high temperature in the unit is made necessary in order to make the reaction to happen. For such a
consolidated technology temperatures of around 330◦C-350◦C, when the engine is operated by HFO [53],
are the targets.
Usually, maritime exhaust engine gases reach a maximum temperature of 230◦C-260◦C. These are therefore
not high enough to favor the chemical reaction in the SCR reactor.. On the CAES software [52] it has been
possible to conﬁgure a low pressure SCR unit for both the two engines considered. This means that the SCR
unit is displaced after the turbocharging unit and the temperature are found to be approximately between
230◦-260◦. In order to make the gas achieve the set temperature the engine is therefore forced to work at
a point in which it’s not possible to achieve the highest efﬁciency (low-load method), the goal is only to
respect the Tier III restriction [53].
Man Diesel & Turbo is also working for a solution in which the SCR is implemented before the turbocharging
unit thus exploiting higher temperature without making the engine to work in an off design condition [53].
When considering the Tier II restriction, the SCR unit can be bypassed because of the low generation of
nitrogen oxides in the combustion chamber thanks to the utilization of liqueﬁed natural gas.

The Exhaust Gas Recirculation unit (EGR) is a different solution aimed at meeting the Tier III restrictions

CHAPTER 4
Methodology: Design Conditions
The thesis purpose is to evaluate the possibility to design two particular layouts act at accommodating an
organic Rankine cycle on-board two different sized container ships. The main focus will be to evaluate how
the disposition of an organic Rankine cycle can affect its power output.
Conventional vessels now a days present one or more engines act at giving the propulsion to the ship.This
large engines can generate a considerable amount of exhaust gases at high temperatures. The exploitation of
this generated heat has already brought to a system in which a steam drum is design to accumulate part of
this otherwise wasted heat to generate steam for the on board service.
The steam drum generator is a simple way of exploit the heat transported by exhaust gases but its largely
undersized given what the exhaust gas contained in terms of energy.
This study mainly focus on the possibility of extract as much heat as possible to either directly feed an
organic Rankine cycle or feed a larger steam drum that will feed a water cycle that will substitute the exhaust
gas as the heat source of the organic Rankine cycle.
4.1
Standard conﬁguration
4.1.1
The Rankine cycle
In order to construct the organic Rankine cycle the ﬁrst evaluation attempt is based on the Baldasso et all.
procedure [16].
The organic Rankine cycle is developed with the software Matlab [38]. In order to be designed, the organic
Rankine cycle has the necessity to be built upon a number of theoretical thresholds. As suggested by the
literature, the starting point is to be found in the condenser [18, 21, 25, 36, 57]. After the organic compound
has been exploited by the turbine, the ﬂuid is fully condensated. On board a vessel the condenser is an heat
exchanger that allow the working compound to discharge the remaining heat in the sea.
The water of the oceans sets therefore a very ﬁrst important threshold for the optimization of the cycle. In
the research it has been assumed a sea temperature of 20◦C ( This might vary from the location where the
ship working state is evaluated. In the northern seas, the sea temperature is lower thus giving the possibility
to further decrease this value).
Given the temperature at which the water ﬂows in the heat exchanger the condensing temperature is therefore
set for the organic ﬂuid [19, 33, 34]. With the temperature, the minimal pressure is set accordingly. In this
evaluation the minimal difference of temperature is set to be 20◦C. At the end of the condensation process
the ﬂuid is found in its state 1 [ﬁgure 4.1].
Before the exhaust gas heat exchanger a pump is displaced,this will deﬁne the state 2 of the cycle [ﬁgure 4.1].

The layout of the ORC, with the presence of an EGR component installed on board, is conceived in a

In this second case, therefore, there are two gas streams. About 70% of the entire generated exhaust gases

Because of the presence of two different streams, it has been decided to implement two subsequent heat
exchangers tailored to the organic Rankine cycle. While the exhaust gases transfer the heat ﬁrst to the organic
compound, the recirculated gases have the duty of ﬁnally evaporate and super heat the working ﬂuid of the

Methodology: Part Load
The methodology behind this thesis so far has been to develop conﬁgurations that highlight different results
regarding the implementation of an organic Rankine cycle on board a vessel.
With the implementation of the software Matlab, it has been made possible to ﬁnd an ideal operating point
for a particular engine load proﬁle which is the design condition.
All the different component are in fact mathematically construct to work in a steady state condition and are
shaped for working at the provided speciﬁcation. Although those results will count the most, since the design
condition are picked based on the engine load proﬁle of the vessels. This doesn’t provide any particularly
relevant clue of how the cycles will work in off-design conditions. Therefore it has been made reasonable to
add the second part of the evaluation of the study with a part-load model that has been built once more on
Matlab.
A crucial consideration has to be made upon the off-design conditions of the engines.
The thread connecting the work done so far to this second part lies in the consideration that the components
of which the cycle is formed are set to work in ideal conditions.
This results in a deterioration in the operating efﬁciencies of the aforementioned ones. The goal is to quantify
this reduction in terms of power output and compare the results among the different studied conﬁgurations in
order to evaluate which one can provide the more ﬂexibility.
The main focus of this study will be the implementation of a layout that make use of the traditional
drum,generally always present in large ships, used for the heating steam service on board. This boiler is
conﬁgured as a drum in which water and steam are at the equilibrium, the pressure is usually set to 7 bar and
the temperature inside is 166 °C.
The drum is heated thanks to a waste heat recovery system exploiting an heat exchanger crossed by the
exhausted gases on the outlet of the main engine. Although this technology is consolidated the idea is to
keep this drum as it is while increasing the amount of mass ﬂow that goes into it.
The augmented steam generation will still be use for the services in the same amount but the rest could be
splitted and then directed into a boiler heat exchanger for a conventional ORC system described above.
The exhausted steam from the boiler and the services can then be condensed, pumped and pre-heated by the
scavenge air and an EGR system.
In order to investigate and therefore highlight which of the preferable conﬁguration might be MATLAB
software have been implemented. With an original standard model already built, based on “props” function
and the particle swarm simulation is it possible to establish the best optimization for the cycle with a particular
ﬂuid. The ﬁle will be modiﬁed later on and expanded with all the different conﬁgurations explained above in

7.1.2
Part load - off-design condition
When considering the experimental layout of the steam drum, it’s important to understand what kind of
limitation this conﬁguration implies.
Compared to the standard design, now the ﬂuids that are involved in the construction of the organic Rankine
cycle are three. The water is the carrying vector delivering heat to the organic ﬂuid. Due to the drum unit,
the vapour contained cannot be super-heated, giving a considerable limitation to the energy that could absorb.
The second limitation comes from an high steam pressure that limits the amount of heat that the water
can harvest from the exhaust gas. As a consequence of this limitation, the amount of steam that ﬂows in
the "middle" cycle is relatively low. As the engine load decreases, the mass ﬂow of the steam decreases.
As showed in tables 7.1 and 7.2, the amount of steam generated is considerably lower than the mass ﬂow
circulating in the organic Rankine cycle. The steam doesn’t seem to provide a sufﬁcient amount of heat as
the engine load is decreasing.
As a results, in the large amount of cases the software can’t provided a whole solution that covers the engine
load in question, from the design condition (at 75%) till the minimum requested (25%).
As showed in the previous paragraph, four sub-conﬁgurations have been introduced.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
For what has been possible to analyze in this project, the bigger impact of the two systems are to be found
on the inlet of the heat exchangers considered, when analyzing the exhaust gas characteristic. Proofs of a
different set-up for the two main dual-fuel engines investigated in this project, can be found in the different
set of data provided by the CAES software [52].
When vessels are entering an emission control area (namely ECA) the regulations under which they have to
submit switch from the Tier II program to the Tier III. Ships powered by liqueﬁed natural gas don’t ﬁnd Tier
II restrictive in terms of emissions since the nature of the fuel itself; leaving therefore pollution abatement
system unused. When in Tier II systems as SCR or EGR are therefore by-passed.
On the contrary, these units have to be mandatorily activated in the emission control areas. The results
obtained in the previous chapter highlight the difference in the net power output that the organic Rankine
cycle is able to produce when the engine is conﬁgured to respond to the Tier II or Tier III programs.
The power ﬁgures that ORC is obtaining with both SCR or EGR system installed on board are un-matched
by the corresponding engine without any of the system switched on.
In normal conditions, under Tier II legislation, the engine works at its designed point, the one at which the
engine perform with its best efﬁciency. SCR unit forces the shipowner to switch the working point to a lower
value of efﬁciency in order to have higher temperature levels. In order to function as intended in fact, it
require a particular engine tuning.
Higher temperature levels are needed at the SCR in order to make the chemical reaction of the nitrogen with
the urea to happen. When EGR is implemented, exhaust gas are freely sent to the chimney while recirculated
gas are extracted from the collector at very high temperature. The latter stream is immediately at the disposal
of the heat exchanger for the conﬁgurations illustrated before.
For the purposes of this project, these considerations, make the implementation of the waste heat recovery
system particularly favourable.

8.1
Standard Conﬁguration
8.1.1
Design condition
Considerations now are addressed to the only Organic Rankine cycle. According to the results extracted it is
possible to conclude that the exhaust gas recirculation unit is achieving higher ﬁgures in terms of net power
output and efﬁciency in both the dual fuel engines.
The reasons behind this higher values lies in the fact that although SCR is able to present higher temperature
of the gases when the unit is active,EGR system provides an even higher temperature at the inlet of the
heat exchanger. Therefore the organic ﬂuid is able to achieve an higher temperature when it is presented at
the turbine. Higher temperatures translated in a higher difference of enthalpy level, thus a high quantity of
exploitable energy for the turbine.
From the results it is also possible to understand that higher temperature levels play a bigger role compared
to the amount of exhaust gases crossing the heat exchanger. Setting an inlet temperature for the gas, an
increased amount of mass ﬂow will lead to an increase in the mass ﬂow of the organic Rankine cycle. On the
other hand, if the mass ﬂow of the exhaust gases are ﬁxed and the temperature of them could hypothetically
be increased, the ORC could respond setting a higher pressure difference between state 1 and state 2. The
higher the pressure difference, the higher the power output. The temperature of the gases is therefore
responsible for the high pressure of the organic Rankine cycle and partly responsible for the organic ﬂuid
mass ﬂow. Quantity of exhaust gases are only affecting the ORC mass ﬂow value. The net power output is a
combination of mass ﬂow and temperature of the organic compound. Increasing the latter variables insure an
higher value of power generation compared to an increase of the only mass ﬂow.
The positioning of the jacket water preheater on the cycle is also signiﬁcantly contributing to the net power
output. Although the unit is able to heat the ﬂuid till a temperature of 80◦C, its contribution is crucial in
order to help the ﬂuid harvesting the exhaust gas heat. In all the cases evaluated, with toluene and R245fa,
the ﬂuid is found in its liquid state and it is then presented at the inlet of the exhaust gas heat exchanger.
As mentioned, when the organic ﬂuid is entering the evaporator sector, a pinch point of 20◦C is set. When
running the software the minimum temperature difference is found at this point, establishing therefore the
steepness of the exhaust gas curve (line). Looking into the heat transfer graphs obtained in chapter 6, it is
noticeable how, speciﬁcally in the economizer sector, the ﬂuid is increasing its temperature at high pace. The
function present a steeper curve compared to the exhaust gas heat transfer curve. This means that beyond
the pinch point, the heat exchanged between the two streams is occurring at lowering level of heat transfer
efﬁciencies. The temperature difference are increasing thus meaning an higher level of entropy generated.
The jacket water preheater cover the sector from state 2 to state 2A insuring a medium heat source that leads
the organic ﬂuid to the exhaust gas in a more efﬁcient way.
According to the literature available, the implementation of the both the jacket water preheater and the
exhaust gas as exploitable heat sources guarantees the highest net power production from the ORC [16]. The
same can’t be said of the recuperator which, especially when the jacket water is in place, has been found
irrelevant as contributor of increasing the net power output.

A different kind of considerations have to be taken into account when analyzing the steam drum conﬁguration.
The lack of results for the 7GS60ME-C10.5-GI engine bring to some immediate conclusions. The lower
temperatures of the exhaust gases of the smaller engines are not able to generate enough vapour to fulﬁll the
job of transporting enough heat at the ORC. This gives already an idea on the limitations that this kind of
layout implies.
At ﬁrst it’s worth mentioning that for design purposes the steam generated has to be pressurized at least till 7
bars. For the water, the saturation temperature is 165◦C. This value is already quite high for the exhaust
gases temperatures achieved by the smaller engine.
The focus is therefore directed to the only 9G96ME-C9.5-GI engine. For this unit a set of four different
layouts have been constructed on Matlab. In particular, the differences among them concern the positioning
of the jacket water preheater and the recuperator unit inside the ORC. As it has been showed in tables 7.3,
7.6 and 7.9.
In the A and B layout the jacket water heat exchanger has the scope of preheating the organic ﬂuid in the
organic Rankine cycle, later presenting it at the main heat exchanger. In C and D the jacket water is instead
moved to the steam cycle. It serves the purpose of presenting the steam at the scavenge air heat exchanger
at a higher temperature. Speciﬁcally, the water, in its liquid state, has a temperature of 50 ◦C after the
circulation pump. The jacket water unit preheat the ﬂuid till a maximum temperature of 80◦C. Subsequently,
the water is conveyed at the scavenge air heat exchanger where its temperature its further increased. Leaving
the latter unit, the water is presented at the steam drum.
Results illustrates that when the jacket water preheater is mounted on the ORC (conﬁguration A and B), the
net power output achievable is higher. The reasons for this increase on power is to be found in the amount
of heat harvested from the jacket water unit. As speciﬁed before, when it is mounted on the steam cycle,
this unit heat the water for a maximum temperature difference of 30◦C. Moreover, the mass ﬂow of the
water is relatively low,compared to the quantity of organic ﬂuid that circulate in the ORC. Therefore the
heat provided by the jacket water is higher when mounted according to the A and B layout. Also, at the
only design conditions, the scavenge air is fully capable of bringing the water from the 50 ◦C mark to the
maximum temperature achievable for the unit.
As mentioned before, the recuperator has been proved to not be that decisive in term of power output
achievable. In the A and D conﬁgurations, the recuperator has been removed. The latter conﬁgurations are
constructed in order to simulate a complete by-pass of the unit in design and off-design conditions. It will be
discussed later on how, the recuperator implementation, will affect the ORC in off-design conditions.
Differences between the implementation of an SCR or EGR unit are showed in the table below. When
considering the exhaust gas recirculation system two different layouts have been built on the software. When
the EGR heat exchanger is positioned on the steam drum, it is mounted in parallel to the exhaust gas heat
exchanger. As said in the results, two separate stream of steam are generated and then collected in the steam
drum. In this case, the recirculated gas are therefore responsible for an increase value for the mass ﬂow of
the steam.
In its alternative construction, the EGR heat exchanger serves the purpose of heat directly the organic Rankine
cycle. When analyzing tables 7.4 and 7.7, it is immediate to see a larger contribution of the recirculated
gases in terms of heat transfer directed to the organic Rankine cycle. Because of the limitations explained
above, the exhaust gases, with their relatively low temperature, are generating an a very small amount of
steam which, alone wouldn’t have been able to feed the ORC. With the major advantage of a very high inlet

evaluating the performances of the ORC, a lower difference (compared to the standard conﬁgurations) in

value the maximum temperature is unchanged, what is forced is a further decrease in the pressure that will
lead to a decrease in the mass ﬂow. This implementation helps the software ﬁnding a solution for a maximum
of 1 or 2 steps when decreasing the engine load but alone is not particularly signiﬁcant. In ﬁgure 8.3, the
25% mark of the toluene has been achieved thanks to the presence of the above-mentioned thermostatic
valve.
Other controlling systems could be provided at the organic Rankine cycle in order to help increasing the
ﬂexibility of the waste heat recovery. A bypass of the boiler and the turbine could lower the amount of
organic ﬂuid mass ﬂow exploited by the turbine. A lower mass ﬂow will insure a better heat transfer between
the steam and the organic compound.
Another viable solution could be to lower even further the power provided at the pump. The pump has
already been provided with an inverter that already helps setting the pressure difference in the cycle. A
further decrease in the rotating speed of the blades will affect, as a consequence, the pressures (and therefore
the maximum temperatures) of the cycle.

Given all the limitations that the steam drum conﬁguration is suffering of, it is no surprise that the amount
of fuel saved is lower than the comparable standard conﬁguration layouts. In particular, when considering
the SCR installation the steam drum shows a reduction of fuel saving of 1% compared to its corresponding
in standard conﬁguration. For the EGR systems the steam drum alternative suffer a 3% difference in fuel
savings. Here, the amount of heat contained in the exhaust gas are "wasted" on the steam generation. In the
case of the toluene, it is important to say that in the drum, the steam is generated at levels of pressure equal
to 11.5 bar. At this pressure, the water evaporates between 185◦C and 190◦C neglecting the exhaust gas to
provide a relevant amount of heat.
Lower ﬁgures of fuel saving are also expected because of a second ﬂuid involved in the process of providing
the heat to the ORC. The transfer of the heat from the gas to the organic ﬂuid occurs at reduced temperature
differences(thanks to the steam) thus ensuring a lower level of entropy generation. This beneﬁt is though
heavily counterbalanced by the addiction of a further heat transfer which affects the amount of heat that can
be provided at the ORC. Limitations on the maximum temperature of the ORC, thanks to a not super heated
steam , only add negatively to the values of net power output obtained thus affecting the fuel saving.
It is worth mentioning that the ﬁgures of fuel saving have been calculated considering the 100% of the
vessel time inside and outside an emission control area. The real fuel savings will be accurate estimating the
number of hours that the particular vessel is spending in ECAs.For the small container ship it is reasonable
to assume that 50% of its time is spent inside one of this Tier III zones. for the larger vessel instead, a 5%
time could be more accurate given the international trade route it is crossing during the year.
A brief comparison with the literature available shows that the results gathered for the standard conﬁguration
are aligned to results of other authors. In particular Baldasso et all [16] achieved a fuel saving of 6,89%
for the 7G95ME-C9.5 engine tuned with an SCR unit installed on board. The ship proﬁle implemented in
this study is based on his work. In other studies, when considering HFO fuelled vessels the results vary
from 5% [59] to 7% [20]. When tuning an engine accordingly and further optimising the ORC in part load
conﬁguration it has been possible to achieve values of around 11 % [20].
The steam drum conﬁguration has not been investigated yet by other authors so a comparison is still not
possible.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
The work carried out in this project is aimed at evaluating the possibility of installing on board a vessel a
suitable organic Rankine cycle for two different type of container ships provided with the indicated engines.
Since the 1st of January, 2016 the IMO has introduced the Tier III program act at limiting the amount of SOx
and NOx when a ship is entering particular areas of the world. One of which is the Baltic sea. The idea of
fuelling the vessels with LNG and providing it with an organic Rankine cycle could be an appealing solution.
Nowadays liqueﬁed natural gas is getting an increasing attentions thanks to its properties. It presents only
traces of sulfur and when exploit in a combustion reaction the amount of NOx generated is up to 80%
less than a traditional oil-based marine fuel. Moreover, LNG is available in high quantities and its price is
lower compared to traditional fuels suited for the shipping industry. When implementing LNG as the main
fuel a series of sulfur oxides abatement systems,such as scrubbers, can be neglected improving even more
the appeal of the natural gas. Therefore LNG suits well the scenario drawn by the International Maritime
Organization and further studies are needed in this ﬁeld.
The two dual fuel engines extracted from the CAES software are suited for the implementation of NOx
abatement systems. The two units investigated are the selective catalytic reduction and the exhaust gas
recirculation unit. In this project, two main design layout have been carried out for each engine. The ﬁrst
one, called "standard conﬁguration", contemplate the possibility of installing an heat exchanger where the
exhaust gases transfer the heat to an organic compound. The ﬂuid will then feed a turbine in order to generate
power that will be send to the propeller. The second layout, called "steam drum conﬁguration", shifts the
position of the ORC. The idea is to enlarge the drum at which the exhaust gases usually generate the steam
necessary for on-board service. The larger amount of steam could then be sent to provide the heat to the
organic Rankine cycle thanks to the implementation of an heat exhcanger.
The two main conﬁgurations have been evaluated in a number of different cases. As it has been possible to
extract from the results gathered for both engines, the EGR installation is found to be the preferable solution.
Although with a relatively lower mass ﬂow compared to the exhaust gases, the recirculated stream is more
suited for an ORC installation. The reasons behind such conclusion are displayed in the higher amount of
power output that the turbine is able to generate. As it has been possible to understand, an higher level of
temperature of the heat source guarantees an increase in the maximum pressure of the organic Rankine cycle.
This is validated for both the standard and the steam drum conﬁguration. The results extracted show that the
standard conﬁguration is preferable under any circumstances. The combination of exhaust gases, recirculated
gases (where available) and the jacket water heat exchangers situated on the organic Rankine cycle gives the
highest values of net power output. On the other hand, the steam drum conﬁguration presents its weaker
point in the "middle" cycle. The amount of steam generated in fact doesn’t seem to be suited for the job of
providing the ORC with the necessary heat. This shows particularly when analyzing the off design conditions

of it. In the majority of the cases analyzed, the software isn’t able to extract valid solutions for low engine
loads. In order to avoid calculation problems a set of solution has been further implemented. The major
problem lies in the low amount of steam mass ﬂow that is possible to generate: for design reasons the steam
for the on board service needs to be pressurized till a value of minimum 7 bar. The level of the pressure also
affect the temperature at which the water is harvesting the heat of the exhaust gases thus limiting the heat
transfer. When in off-load design, the low amount of steam is not capable of handling the heat required at
the ORC resulting in considerable temperature drops in the heat exchanger. The main solution was then to
design the steam to exit the ORC heat exchanger at higher temperature thus ensuring an increased ﬂexibility
when trying to reach lower engine loads.
Furthermore, when considering the 7GS60ME-C10.5-GI engine,the steam drum conﬁguration is only able to
ﬁnd approximate results with poor levels of net power output. The steam generated with the smaller engine
is inconsistent for this layout.
On its plus side though, the steam drum conﬁguration could give a more compact design and,when func-
tioning, is able to achieve smoother power output differences for each step of the decreasing engine load
compared to the standard conﬁguration.
Giving the limitations of the steam drum conﬁgurations an alternative solution have been explored. Namely
the possibility of implementing the recirculation gas heat exchanger directly on the ORC while the exhaust
gases are dedicated to the production of steam that can eventually partly contribute as a heat source for the
organic compound. Results show that this "hybrid" conﬁguration is able to reach the higher net power output
among the steam drum conﬁgurations evaluated.
The 7GS60ME-C10.5-GI engine is suitable for small container ships which could spend around half of their
time in the ECA (imagining a trade route from Rotterdam to a port situated on the Baltic sea).From the
results extracted the best way of implementing a waste heat recovery system on board is to install an EGR
unit provided with a standard conﬁguration layout thus also ensuring higher percentages of fuel saving.
On the other hand, the 9G96ME-C9.5-GI engine is suitable for big container ships that be imagined to
spend around 5% of their time in Emission control areas. In the future is reasonable to assume that the
International Maritime Organization will expand the boundaries where the Tier III program is active. On this
bases, gathering the results collected in this study the best conﬁguration forecast the implementation of an
EGR system provided with a "standard conﬁguration" layout. The reasons are the same that applies to the
smaller engine.
