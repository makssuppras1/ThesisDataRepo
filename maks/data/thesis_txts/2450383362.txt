Abstract
This master’s thesis has been motivated by the struggle to comply with the Water
Framework Directive legislation for stream water. A part of the issue resides within the
need to understand the temporal and spatial dynamics of peri-urban catchments which
is needed to make more sustainable policies aimed at returning impacted stream systems
to good chemical and ecological status. In order to deal with this issues a ﬁeld campaign
located in the peri-urban setting of Usserød was initiated with the purpose of collecting a
high spatio-temporal data set, exploration of possible proxy parameters from unmanned
aerial systems with data from the study sites has been conducted, and a foundation of a
water quality model in system dynamics has been created.
Two ﬁeld campaigns, in October and December, was conducted where both temporal
and spatial variations was present and inﬂuence from waste water treatment plants was
noticeable.
There were found signiﬁcant correlation between TSS and turbidity and also between
CDOM and NVOC. It hasn’t been able to establish a relation between chlorophyll a and
nutrients based on the current data set.
The system dynamics water quality model includes the essential elements from water
quality modeling and were able to simulate the overall seasonal trends when compared to
observed date from the study site. The model failed one of the validity tests because it
only can simulate a single reach, but the modeling concept is, however, an iterative process
and the next step could be to implement a transport module to connect the reaches.

Introduction
1.1
Problem Statement
Motivated by a desire to collectively protect European freshwater resources, the European
Water Framework Directive (WFD) was enacted in 2000 (2000/60/EC). This legislation
sets overall goals for the quantitative and qualitative status, such as good ecological and
chemical status of surface water bodies, for all EU Member States (European Commission,
2000). The initial planning period went from 2000-2009, where e.g. monitoring networks
were established, river basin management plans (RBMPs) created, and initial pressure,
impact and economic analyses conducted. Much has been achieved since the enactment of
the WFD, however, Member States are still facing challenges to comply with many of its
speciﬁc targets (Voulvoulis et al., 2017). For Denmark, results from the ﬁrst river basin
management plan (RBPM) reporting (2009-2015) for the river basin district of Zealand
found that, out of the 2,765 km of streams, only 365 km had managed to reach a high or
good ecological state (Styrelsen for Vand-og Naturforvaltning, 2016). This indicates that
streams do not comply with the guidelines for general water quality, such as biochemical
oxygen demand (BOD) or dissolved oxygen (DO) (EPA, 2016). It is therefore critical to
continue investigating the state of the general water quality parameters in streams.
The Danish landscape is aﬀected by urban growth, agricultural expansion, and sprawl,
which drives the development of peri-urban landscapes. One part of the struggle to comply
with the WFD resides with the diﬃculties in assessing the multiple chemical stressors
originating from various sources that may impact water resources in a catchment (Sonne
et al., 2017). Another part of the struggle is our fragmented sampling locations throughout
a catchment. Understanding the temporal and spatial dynamics in the streams are needed
in order to make more sustainable policies aimed at returning impacted stream systems
to good chemical and ecological status (Bigi et al., 2018).
In order to cope with the challenges regarding the WFD, the national monitoring
program for water and environment (NOVANA) which were launched in 2004 began a
new and updated sampling period spanning from 2017-2021. However, the program is
still only aiming for one sampling in the full period or a couple of times a year at best,
and only single point sampling in the streams will be conducted (Miljøstyrelsen, DCE -
Aarhus Universitet and GEUS, 2017). Sampling in Danish streams will therefor still lack
an adequate spatio-temporal coverage. High frequency (Davis et al., 2010; Wasson et al.,
2003; Bigi et al., 2018; Miljøstyrelsen, DCE - Aarhus Universitet and GEUS, 2017) and
high spatial density monitoring (Kirchner et al., 2004; England et al., 2008; Bigi et al.,
1
==PAGE:18==
2018) are important in order to obtain representative observations for actual conditions,
e.g. seasonal variations and extreme events (Kirchner et al., 2004; Halliday et al., 2012;
Madird and Zayas, 2007; Bigi et al., 2018), but stream monitoring campaigns are expensive
and time-consuming (Bigi et al., 2018), which often leads to low spatial and temporal
monitoring densities. It is therefor essential to be able to achieve data from the ﬁeld that
both has a high spatio-temporal density meanwhile it is cost eﬃcient.
Our understanding of the natural environment typically comes through the coupling
of ﬁeld data in conjunction with modeling techniques. The collection of data is critical for
the calibration and veriﬁcation of models, such as AQUATOX (Park and Clough, 2018)
and WASP (Wool et al., 2008), which in turn provide the basis of support for manage-
ment decisions especially when data is scarce (Bigi et al., 2018; Brack et al., 2017; England
et al., 2008). AQUATOX and WASP also represent two WQ models which have been ap-
plied in a variety of WQ challenges including: modelling heavy metals, nutrients, and DO
(Sourisseau et al., 2008; Environmental Consultancy Co., 2016; Nikolaidis et al., 2006;
Stansbury and Admiraal, 2004). Despite this, these models are generally programmed in
computer languages (e.g. Fortran C) which makes them generally opaque to those with-
out a computer science background. This creates diﬃculties for communicating system
interactions and assumptions, which hampers their transferability and ﬂexibility, which
is needed to cope with the ambitious and comprehensive WDF policy.
There is furthermore a need to reduce the growing gap between more technology
adnvanced observational technology and current water quality models (Hellweger, 2015).
To date, WQ models play an important role in management and decision making, both in
their capacity for supporting better integrated understanding (Ford, 1999, 2010), but also
for their predictive capacity (Barwell and Krenkel, 1982; Scholten et al., 2007; Jørgensen
and Fath, 2011; Davis et al., 2010). Despite their widespread use, they are subject to the
inherent issue of reliance on data and/or assumptions, which often dictates the level of
complexity (Barwell and Krenkel, 1982; Scholten et al., 2007; Jørgensen and Fath, 2011;
Davis et al., 2010).
One possible solution is to utilize remote sensing techniques, such as unmanned aerial
systems (UAS), to collect data through high resolution imaging spectroscopy (Fichot et al.,
2016). UAS is a game changer bringing the possibility to collect spatial (and possibly
increased temporal) data sets that may eventually be used to inform WQ models. Current
models, however, may not be equipped to handle such novel data sources.
However,
linking these observations with the current models could be found diﬃcult due to their
inﬂexibility.
Instead, system dynamics (SD) is a one such alternative as a WQ model due to the
proven use as a holistic, ﬂexible, and transparent method (Mirchi et al., 2012; Khan, 2009;
Winz et al., 2009). SD has already shown to be robust and have predictive capacity in the
ﬁeld of water resources management (Sehlke and Jacobsen, 2005; Ahmad and Simonovic,
2000; Liu et al., 2015; Khan, 2009; Winz et al., 2009; Mcknight and Finkel, 2013), e.g.
hydrologic simulation, decision support system/tool (DSS/DST) for ﬂood mitigation, and
water quality management of lakes.
The development of a WQ model in SD would be able to include the spatial information
from UAS meanwhile it can produce the temporal data needed for decision making. The
2
==PAGE:19==
model process is begun by implementing the foundation of WQ models, Streeter and
Phelps, who created a WQ model based in DO and BOD, and then through an iterative
process the model would continuously expand and include more important WQ parameters
and priority hazardous substances according to the WFD.

Objectives
The overall aim of this study was to begin addressing the gap in knowledge concerning the
spatio-temporal dynamics of key water quality variables. Variables that are critical for
water quality modeling, preferably linked to environmental quality standards, and with a
potential for being sensed using novel observational tools such as were identiﬁed.
1. Data Foundation. Initiate the creation of a spatio-temporal dataset of relevant
water quality parameters for a peri-urban catchment. Water quality parameters
were chosen both in relation to the possible connection to drones and the develop-
ment of a WQ model within an SD platform.
2. Model development and application. Developing an SD model capable of sim-
ulating key water quality variables, with the aim of creating a model capable of
utilizing data from drones. Focus is especially on simulating dissolved oxygen and

include in a WQ model, and because the WFD legislation is in place for the results

3. Exploring proxy parameters connected to UASs. Investigation of the possi-

Data foundation
In order to understand the stream systems, how they are impacted and what status there
in, we need to obtain a proper data foundation. At the moment there are too few temporal
and spatial data in order to access the stressors on the stream system (Bigi et al., 2018).
NOVANA whom are in charge of data collection of streams, are collecting data for WQ

know to little about the stream system.
Studies has found it possible to measure chromophoric dissolved organic matter (CDOM),

3.1
Dissolved oxygen and biochemical oxygen demand
Aquatic ecosystems are highly dependent on DO which makes it one of the most important
factors in aqueous environment (Wetzel, 2001). The sources for DO in water are from
photosynthesis from plankton, algae or macrophytes and aeration (Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-
Cohen, 2001). The DO sinks are e.g. ﬁsh, phytoplankton and macrophytes which consume
oxygen due to respiration. Furthermore, bacteria and fungi utilize DO in the microbial
decomposition of e.g. detritus and dead algae (Wetzel, 2001).
The reduction of oxygen is highly dependent on the BOD and the relations between
the two parameters were the foundations for the ﬁrst WQ model Streeter and Phelps
(1925). BOD is the oxygen needed by aerobic organisms to decompose organic matter in
the water Harremoes and Malmgren-Hansen (1990) and is thereby an important factor
for DO simulations. BOD also aﬀects the biodiversity, and there are found correlation
between population density in macroinvertabretas and BOD (Friberg et al., 2010) which
the guidelines in Table 2.2 are based on.
Organic material in streams consist of approximately 40% carbon (Chapra, 1997)
and there has previously been found correlations between BOD and dissolved organic
carbon (DOC) Kwak et al. (2013). Investigation of relations between BOD and DOC are
therefore interesting. Non-volatile organic carbon (NVOC) are often used as an proxy
for DOC because only a small amount of organic material are purgable in the natural
environment, and the same is done in this thesis.
BOD can be split in two sub categories; the carbonaceous biochemical oxygen demand
(CBOD) which mainly comes from detritus and the nitrogenous biochemical oxygen de-
14
==PAGE:31==
mand (NBOD). The NBOD has historically been neglected as a factor but can have great
impact on the DO especially after WWTP (Chapra, 1997; Harremoes and Malmgren-
Hansen, 1990). Organic nitrogen and ammonia in streams can originate from point sources
(e.g. sewage) or diﬀuse sources (e.g. agriculture). The input of organic nitrogen com-
pounds will over time turn in to additional ammonia through hydrolysis. Nitriﬁcation uses
two steps for turning ammonia into ﬁrst nitrite and then nitrate (Chapra, 1997). The
denitriﬁcation process is then converted ﬁrstly into nitrite and then in to free nitrogen.
The free nitrogen can now be lost to the system or be ﬁxated and "reused" (Brezonik,
1973; Chapra, 1997).
3.2
Nutrients and chlorophyll a
Algal and macrophyte growth are limited by available nutrients, usually nitrogen and
phosphorus in the system. The possible sources for nutrients in peri-urban catchments
both consist of diﬀuse and point sources (Harremoes and Malmgren-Hansen, 1990; Nazaroﬀ
and Alvarez-Cohen, 2001):
1. Agriculture: fertilizers, animal manure.
2. Urban: CSOs, WWTP, industry discharges, and contaminated sites.
An excessive amounts of nutrients can lead to excessive growth of phyoplankton and
periphyton (Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). This can result in very high DO during
the day, caused by enhanced photosynthesis, but too low DO levels during the night due
to excessive respiration compared to a nutritionally balanced stream. Ultimately the vast
amount of algae will die and decomposition will take place and consume oxygen in form
of BOD (Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-Cohen, 2001).
However, both periphyton and phytoplankton are diﬃcult to sample so chlorophyll a
are often used as an proxy. There are six diﬀerent types of chlorophyll, but it is mainly
the a type that are used in relation to phytoplankton. This is because it is found in
all photosynthesizing organisms and the ratio from chlorophyll a to b are in general 3:1
which makes it the most suitable chlorophyll to sample.
3.3
Turbidity, Total Suspended Solids, and PAHs
Turbidity is an optical measure that represent how clear or cloudy water is, which means
that the higher the turbidity the more light is reﬂected or absorbed in the water and
thereby the water become less transparent (Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). There
are found linear relations between turbidity and total suspended solids (TSS) in streams
(Rügner et al., 2013, 2014). TSS is a quantitaive measurement of all suspended material
in the water column (Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). There are multiple events which
aﬀect TSS and turbidity e.g. soil erosion, waste discharge, urban runoﬀ, algae growth,
and of sediment from high ﬂow. TSS are especially an important parameter for estimating
wet-weather pollution from combined sewer overﬂows (Rossi et al., 2006).
15
==PAGE:32==
There are several eﬀects of turbidity and TSS;
1. Suspended solids can aﬀect the temperature as they absorb more heat than water
molecules (Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). As an eﬀect the DO levels decrease
as warm water have a lower DO saturation.
2. The clarity of the water is important for the photosynthesis (Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-
Cohen, 2001), a decreased penetration of light will have a reduced oxygen output
from plants and a increase in decomposition of plants would lower the oxygen levels
even more. Ultimately the reduced vegetation will decrease habitats and hiding
places for prey ﬁsh and crustacean and show a decline in diversity (Howick et al.
1993).
3. TSS can be a carrier of potential damaging substances such as polycyclic aromatic
hydrocarbons (PAHs) and heavy metals which are adsorbed by TSS (Rossi et al.,
2006; Obarska-Pempkowiak et al., 2015).
PAHs are interesting because animals and human exposure to PAHs have been linked
to cancer (Bostrom et al., 2006). It is especially dangerous in the aqueous environment
as PAHs have a low solubility in water and bond well with fats which cause accumulation
of PAHs in the foodchain (Salman et al., 2014).

Water Quality modeling
The ﬁrst WQ models focused on DO and BOD and were initiated by the research of
Streeter and Phelps (1925). Up until 1960s, WQ modeling were focused on the DO-BOD
relationship with improvement of BOD degradation pathways and distinction between
CBOD and NBOD (Ambrose et al., 2009). From this stage several variables were included
in the models such as nutrients cycles, sediment oxygen demand (SOD), toxicant fate,
and biological growth based on light, temperature, and nutrients (Wang et al., 2013).
Todays WQ models has proven robust, shown predictive capacity, and have been applied
wide range of issues WQ challenges e.g. for modelling heavy metals, nutrients, and DO

5.2
System dynamics method
System dynamics was invented by Jay Forrester in the 1960s as approach of analyzing
dynamic behavior of complex systems (Forrester, 1971; cited by Simonovic, 2002).
Winz et al. (2009) has summarized the main strengths of the SD approach as ﬂexible,
transparent and adaptable, and great for stakeholder participation.
SD has been applied to a wide range of environmental ﬁelds where it has proven both
robust and with predictive capacity (Sehlke and Jacobsen, 2005; Ahmad and Simonovic,
2000; Liu et al., 2015; Khan, 2009; Winz et al., 2009; Mcknight and Finkel, 2013). How-
ever, there are not an SD model which encompasses the need for the project which is why
a new model shall be created.
5.2.1
System Dynamics approach
SD models are built using four fundamental components (Ford, 1999, 2010), shown as a
stock and ﬂow (SF) diagram in Figure 5.2, and explained in the following:
• Stocks, are representing the storage of a system where accumulation can take place.
The content of the stock may increase of decrease over time depending on the system.
• Flows represents the ﬂow direction in the system. They are a direct inﬂuence of the
stocks, and can either represent the ﬂow between diﬀerent stocks or from sources
and sinks outside the system, which is represented as a cloud.
• Converters, represents the rates of
• Connectors, deﬁne the cause and eﬀect relationships between system components
with ﬂow of information. Is visually represented as the arrow between system com-
ponents.
23
==PAGE:40==
Figure 5.2. Simple stock and ﬂow diagram built in Vensim. Stock is shown as the rectangle, ﬂows are
the big double lined arrows, and the converter is connected to the ﬂow by the coonector, shown as the
blue arrow.
Creating an SD model is an iterative process where the model is developed in steps of
increasing complexity until the model is capable of reproduce the behavior of the system
Ford (1999, 2010). The process of developing an SD model can be divided into ﬁve stages,
based on Ford (2010) and Stave (2003):
1. Deﬁne the problem.
2. Build a conceptual model that describes the system.
3. Build the SD model based on the conceptual model.
4. Build conﬁdence in the model - This can be done through variety of validity test,
e.g. recommended by Barlas (1996).
5. Use the model for testing impact of policies.

6.4
Model validity and validation
The evaluation of the SD model will be done through the standard approach recommended
by Barlas (1996). General WQ models are black-box models, and are often assessed by their
capability to match model outputs with observed data. System dynamics are on the contrary
white-box models where the ultimate objective is to validate the internal structure of the model
"Right behavior for the right reasons" (Barlas, 1996). Only after the structure of the model is
validated can can the model’s simulation of real behaviour be evaluated (Barlas, 1996), thus,
both a structure- and behavior validity test. Validation of a SD model is an iterative process
which follows the sequence of validation found in Figure 6.30.
It should be noted that the model is still under development. These tests will, however, serve
to clarity of its current state as well as areas for future development.
6.4.1
Structure validity
This section are split into two tests: direct structure test and structure oriented test.
Direct structure test
This is a non-simulation test where the model’s relationships or mathematical equations and
parameters are compared to observations in real systems and/or known information available
in literature (Forrester and Senge, 1980). The theoretical structure test is conducted through
literature search of surface water quality modeling and investigation of current state of the art
WQ models. The mathematical relations and parameters are presented in the previous section.
Structure-oriented behavior test
Extreme behavior test. Also called stress testing. Parameters will be assigned with extreme
values.
It will hereafter be observed if the model complies with the anticipated behavior of
the real system, whether the model is able to run when it is stressed produces unrealistic or
inexplicable behavior or receive any result of numerical error.

stream depth and temperature from the same period. It is seen that the the model doesn’t ﬁt
in the ﬁrst two months, but hereafter follow the same trend of decline in DO in the late spring

large oscillations which is present during the summer. This is most likely due to the generic
photosynthesis-respiration approach which is used in the model. However, it can not be rejected
that there might have been some sort of problems with the measurements from April to August.

fact that the model can’t simulate the transport of neither dissolved matters nor water. This
means that if there is a CSO in the last reach, it would not be seen in the simulation but would
be present in the observed DO. This indicates that a model where the reaches are coupled is
essential.

6.4.4
Overall validity discussion
The SD model has been investigated through a standard validation analysis for system dynamics
models. It was validated for most of the tests but in the boundary-adequacy test it was found
that the model is missing some vital modules and need further development. However, the the
validation is an iterative process in which learning about forces and shortcomings of the models
is essential.
Looking more general at models and validation there is no great consensus on what is the
right way to approach the validation or when a model is good enough (Eker et al., 2018). There
is e.g. a disagreement whether models for to explore multiple plausible futures and prediction
models should be validated on the same way or diﬀerent, and also the degree of precision a model
need to capture historical data based on the models purpose.

Conclusion
The overall aim of this study is to improve our understanding of the spatio-temporal dynamics
of water quality variables in streams, which could enable sustainable policies aiming at returning
impacted stream systems to good chemical and ecological status.
This project has initiated a ﬁeld campaign which collects WQ variables important for WQ
modeling and policy making. The ﬁeld campaign is located in the peri-urban landscape of Nivå-
Usserød stream system where there will be sampled every six weeks at 11 station throughout the
stream in order to improve the spatio-temporal understanding. Within the scope of the project
two ﬁeld campaigns have been conducted, the ﬁrst from 23-25/10/2018, and the second from
4-5/12/2018. Even though only two ﬁeld campaigns have been conducted it is possible to see
some trends from the data sets. Temporal changes from October to December were detected,
especially for DO and temperature but also for EC. There is also seen spatial variation e.g. TSS
and turbidity which increased in downstream direction, and WWTPs are presumably increasing
water temperature and EC. However, most of the spatial variations are without any obvious
tendencies, so far. From the two ﬁeld campaigns it has been conﬁrmed that it is not without
signiﬁcance where and when sampling takes place in order to determine the status of the stream.
Data collected during the ﬁeld campaigns has been used to investigate the relations between
important WQ variables and parameters which can be observed by UASs in order to highlight
the possibility to utilize UASs as observational technology. There were found a signiﬁcant corre-
lation between TSS and turbidity for both October(p<0.01) and December(p<0.01). Signiﬁcant
relations was also found between CDOM and NVOC (proxy for DOC), but in this case there were
a clear distinction between the samples upstream of station 5 and those downstream of station
5, which indicates impacts by the peri-urban landscape. There are not found any relations for
neither BOD-NVOC nor nutrients-Chlorophyll a, by investigating the current data set. These
relations could be established by continued investigation when more data is available
An SD WQ model has been created based on state of the art WQ models and literature
through an iterative modeling process.
The model contains a DO balance module including
CBOD, SOD, photosynthesis and respiration, and NBOD along with a simple nitrogen cycle.
The model has been evaluated by using the standard validity and validation procedure for SD
models. It was found that the model don’t have any inherent ﬂaws which cause simulation error
under extreme conditions. The most sensitive parameters in relation to the DO are temperature,
depth, and velocity. The model did not pass the boundary-adequacy test because it only can
simulate DO for a single reach and and the transport between diﬀerent reaches. This is, however,
an iterative process and the coupling of the reaches could be one of the next tasks in the modeling
process. The model was ﬁnally compared to observed DO from the study site. This showed that
the model was somehow able to replicate the overall seasonal trends, but fails when it comes
to simulate the increased DO ﬂuctuations in the summer. This is most likely due to a generic
photosynthesis-respiration simulation and lack of the previously mentioned transport within
reaches.

Future outlook
This project has been the foundation for further research and development of the model. In
the following will suggested areas of interest for the model and project be therfore be described
brieﬂy.
Coupling nutrients and phytoplankton
Improving the nitrogen cycle and creating a phosphorus cycle in the model is important as they
are important water quality parameters both for policies and modeling of DO. These are nutrients
which are interdependent with phytoplankton. It is therefor possible to create a module where
phytoplankton is dependent on available nitrogen and phosphorus and vice versa is the nutrients
dependent on the death and degradation of phytoplankton. A module for phytoplankton could
thereby also alternatively replace the current photosynthesis simulation as the phytoplankton
would be the a source and sink for DO instead of photosynthesis.
Flow and coupling of reaches
As mentioned in Section 6.4.1, the model is only simulating one reach at the time and is therefor
not able to include any ﬂow or water quality aspects from upstream or connect to downstream.
This is an essential feature to include in the model otherwise it will be impossible to assess where
the problems origin or what the consequences may be.
Precipitation
In extension of last section, it is important to include precipitation to the model and the hydro-
dynamic module. This is both to relate precipitation to ﬂow in the stream and to CSOs and
SSOs and their inﬂuence on the stream WQ.
Further exploration of parameters
This project has explored the possibility of using parameters detected by UASs as proxies im-
portant parameters concerning WQ models and policy making. However, further investigation is
needed, both to strengthen the known relations through further data mining but also look into
other relevant WQ parameters.

Fieldplan for Usserød å
D.1
Field campaign
Campaign goal: To measure water quality parameters approximately every 6 weeks,
starting in October, in the Nivå stream system along an 8-km stream stretch in order to
begin collecting a temporal dataset. The parameters to be monitored are chosen as those
currently used in determining overall stream water quality especially in urban streams,
including some additional parameters that can be potentially linked at a later stage to
data collection using UAS.
Purpose: Collection of a series of water quality data to be used to benchmark a System
Dynamics model.
Objectives:
• Collection of water quality parameters in order to verify a System Dynamics Decision
Support Tool.
• Collection of water quality parameters that potentially can be linked to the UASs.
• Test the choice of sampling depth for dissolved oxygen using a vertical transect
approach.
The plan: consists of collecting water samples at the 11 stations labelled Station 1-11,
as shown in Figure D.1, starting from the most downstream point (Station 11) to ensure
undisturbed conditions for each station.
For each station and parameter, duplicate measurements will be conducted. Water sam-
ples will be taken midstream in the middle of the water column. The following parameters
are to be assessed at each station: CDOM, chlorophyll-a, turbidity, TSS, TN, TP, NVOC,
NH4, CBOD.
Temperature, pH, EC and oxygen will be measured using a ﬂow- through cell; the dis-
solved oxygen concentration will furthermore be measured at 5 diﬀerent depths at Station
1, 2 and 5 to see whether this has an inﬂuence on the result. The stream discharge will be
measured by using an OTT MF Pro ﬂow meter at all sampling locations, except station

7 and 11 because we can use the combined ﬂow from station 5-6 and 9-10 to estimate the
ﬂow at these stations..
