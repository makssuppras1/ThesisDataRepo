Abstract
Methane is one of the greenhouse gasses, which contributes to an imbalance in the global
energy budget and thus enhances global warming. Since there is no methane monitoring
network in Denmark, the aim of this project is to assess whether the SentinelВӯ5P can serve
as a tool for the monitoring of atmospheric methane in this country. Data captured with
SentinelВӯ5P is publicly available through the Copernicus Open Access Hub. The acquired
data of methane dry air mixing ratios were analyzed in terms of spatial and temporal trends.
It was discovered that a lot of observations, especially around the coastline of Denmark
have low validity and that the dataset lacks observations from late Autumn and Winter.
The SentinelВӯ5P dataset was merged with the meteorological dataset which enabled to
find correlations between the methane dry air mixing ratio, surface albedo, and temperaВӯ
ture. The discovered temporal pattern shows that the methane dry air mixing ratio is low in
the Summer and high in early Spring. There were no spatial trends discovered, however,
visualization of the yearly methane average over 4 years of measurements showed that
this average is increasing year by year. Due to methane global warming potential, such a
trend implies that methane concentrations over Denmark should be observed. NevertheВӯ
less, due to the inability of the instrument to capture methane concentrations over DenВӯ
mark in late Autumn and Winter, and a large number of observations having low quality,
SentinelВӯ5P alone is not an optimal tool to observe methane concentrations over Denmark
and should be supported with i.e. ground measurements.

1
Introduction
1.1
Global warming
It is important to acknowledge why methane emissions are relevant from the global perВӯ
spective. This section is going to introduce the topic of global warming and its conseВӯ
quences.
Global warming refers to the rise of the temperature at the EarthвҖҷs surface and the atВӯ
mosphere, caused by the lack of balance between the incoming radiation from the Sun
and radiation reflected by the EarthвҖҷs surface [1]. Alteration of this balance is caused by
the greenhouse effect Вӯ some of the gasses present in the atmosphere, known as the
greenhouse gasses (GHG), are able to absorb the reflected infrared radiation [1]. In conВӯ
sequence, the absorbed radiation is reflected back and accumulated in the atmosphere.
The more GHGs are present in the atmosphere, the larger the imbalance, thus the atВӯ
mosphere heats up more quickly. Examples of greenhouse gasses are: carbon dioxide,
methane, nitrous oxide, chlorofluorocarbons, and water vapor [1]. The only greenhouse
gas that is independent of human activity is water vapor as it is emitted through evapoВӯ
ration from oceans [1]. Remaining greenhouse gasses can also be emitted from natural
sources, however, since the industrial revolution they mainly come from anthropogenic
sources [1]. The consequences of global warming are: raising sea levels, depleting freshВӯ
water resources, increase in intensity and frequency of extreme weather, and abundance
of food due to dry soil [1].
1.2
Methane
As mentioned in the previous section, methane (CH4) is one of the greenhouse gasses. Its
concentration in 2018 was approximately 2.6 times higher than the equilibrium value estiВӯ
mated in 1750 [2]. The global warming potential of methane is 28 times larger, compared
to carbon dioxide (CO2) [2]. Its lifetime in the atmosphere is approximately 9 years, which
is shorter than the lifetime of CO2, therefore reducing its concentration in the atmosphere
can rapidly mitigate climate change [2]. Moreover, methane contributes to the increase of
tropospheric ozone, which is dangerous to humans, by its reactions with hydroxyl radicals
[3].
1.2.1
Emission sources
Emission sources of methane can be grouped into three categories: thermogenic, bioВӯ
genic, and pyrogenic [3]. Thermogenic sources include methane, as a fossil fuel formed
through geological processes for millions of years. It can be released into the atmosphere
through the exploitation of fossil fuels such as natural gas, oil, and coal but also through
natural features such as marine seeps, terrestrial steps, and mud volcanoes [3]. Biogenic
sources include methane released through compartments such as deposits of organic
waste вҖ“ landfills, manure, and sewage, natural wetlands, water reservoirs with low oxyВӯ
gen content, and rice paddies [3]. Pyrogenic sources include methane, as a product of
incomplete combustion of fossil fuels, biofuels or soil, and biomass during wildfires [3].
About 70% of worldwide methane emissions are anthropogenic [4].

1.5
Problem formulation
Previous sections showed that methane is a powerful GHG, and the increase in its conВӯ
centration induces the global warming effect. While Denmark is emitting methane, there
are no monitoring establishments that enable to observe its concentration in the atmoВӯ
sphere [6]. In this project, the methane dry air mixing ratio data from the SentinelВӯ5P is
going to be analyzed with a focus on spatial and temporal trends. It is going to be investiВӯ

The data will also be merged with the meteorological dataset, to investigate the depenВӯ
dencies between methane dry air mixing ratio and weather components. The objective of
this project is to assess whether the SentinelВӯ5P satellite can serve as a methane moniВӯ
toring tool in Denmark and the assessment is going to be based on the outcome of the
aboveВӯmentioned investigations.

Materials and Methods
2.1
Hyperspectral imaging
Hyperspectral image refers to an image where one continuous spectrum is measured for
each of the pixels [8]. A unique parameter to this technology is the spectral resolution,
which is defined as an interval between different wavelengths within a defined range [8].
The higher the spectral range is, the higher is the quality of the image. Satellite or airborne
hyperspectral imaging can be applied in environmental monitoring but also in different
fields such as agriculture, mineralogy, oceanography, and vegetation [8].
2.2
Principle of hyperspectral satellite measurements
Figure 2.1: Principle of hyperspectral measurements with the satellite [9]
Figure 2.1 illustrates the principle of hyperspectral measurements performed with a satelВӯ
lite. The satellite is measuring the spectra of the sunlight backscattered by the surface of
the Earth at the top of the atmosphere [10]. The solar zenith angle is the angle between
the zenith Вӯ line perpendicular to the point on the ground and the line of incidence of the
sunlight [9]. During Winter, this angle tends to be large in Denmark as the Sun is close to
the Earth at that time. The viewing zenith angle is the angle between the reflected sunlight
and the zenith [9].
Retrieval of methane atmospheric concentration at the lower atmosphere and the ground
is possible due to its absorption features [10]. However, the measurement of the reflected
sunlight can be contaminated with atmospheric scattering or optically thick clouds as they
modify the light path [10]. While cloud contamination is easy to filter out, optical scattering
is harder to detect, and thus their presence can lead to underestimation or overestimation
of the methane column [10].

2.3.3
Operational aspects
The definition of an orbit is spacecraft midnight until spacecraft midnight вҖ“ the halfway time
between nadir dayВӯnight and nadir nightВӯday terminators. Spacecraft midnight is used as
a starting point both for instrument operations and data processing therefore to facilitate
it, no measurements should be taken around the spacecraft midnight. Each orbit has a
day side where the instrument flies from south to north and a night side where the instruВӯ
ment flies from north to south. The length of the cycle is 226 orbits which amounts to 16
days. On the day side of the orbit, Earth radiance measurements are performed while on
the night side background measurements and calibration are performed, although they
can only be conducted once the instrument is in a full eclipse. Calibration measurements
must be repeated in a fixed interval and the South Atlantic Anomaly (SAA) area should
be avoided as much as possible. SAA is the area where high radiation is observed close
to Earth due to particles trapped in EarthвҖҷs magnetic field and the offset tilt of the magВӯ
netic axis with respect to rotational axis of the Earth. These particles, mostly protons and
electrons cause signal spikes when being dumped at the surface of the detector.
2.3.4
Acquisition of the data
Raw satellite output with a time order but without temporal overlap, is also called a LevelВӯ0
product [11]. LevelВӯ0 data and calibration key data are processed into the LevelВӯ1b prodВӯ
uct, which is radiometrically corrected and geoВӯlocated top of the atmosphere radiances in
all spectral bands. LevelВӯ1b product consists of the following outputs: LevelВӯ1b radiance,
LevelВӯ1b irradiance, LevelВӯ1b calibration, and LevelВӯ1b engineering. Earth radiance data
along with annotations (i.e. geolocation) are included in the LevelВӯ1b radiance while solar
irradiance data is a component of the LevelВӯ1b irradiance product. These two outputs
are further used in developing the LevelВӯ2 product. The data is processed in two modes:
standard product processing and nearВӯrealВӯtime (NRT) product processing which differ
in a way that standard product has a higher quality but there are less strict time requireВӯ
ments while NRT product is available in 2 hours 15 minutes from observation for LevelВӯ1b

2.4.4
Overview of the algorithm
Section 2.4 presented equations relevant to understand how the light from different sources
arriving at the telescope is transformed into the readВӯout signal. The telescope captures
signals from five different light sources, which are used to calculate the spectral flux at
the slit. The spectral flux is further projected on the detector by the spectrometer, taking
into account the contribution of the straylight. Then, photons collected by the detector are
converted into electrons. Lastly, several conversions take place to transform the charge
into voltage.
2.4.5
Reverse model
The reverse model is defined as a reverse of the forward model. However, some of the
steps cannot be reversed properly and others do not have a fixed position in time thereВӯ
fore the position for them must be assigned. During the reverse modeling, specific error
propagations are performed at every step. There are also corrections specific to UVN and
SWIR modules.

Having the preВӯprocessed SentinelВӯ5P and meteorological data, the two datsets can be

4.2.3
Discussion
The LevelВӯ2 product only enabled to draw conclusions based on the data provided within
the SentinelВӯ5P product, however, some seasonal dependencies such as the CH4 dry air
mixing ratio monthly mean being lower during Summer than during Spring were noticed.
To investigate these dependencies, the SentinelВӯ5P dataset was merged with the ERAВӯ5
dataset containing meteorological observations. The method chosen for this merge was
biВӯlinear interpolation as measurements were located on different grids. The interpolated
values were sometimes very outlying with respect to observations from the ERAВӯ5 dataset
and therefore they had to be discarded. These corrupted values may have been caused
by i.e. location of ERAВӯ5 observations on the given day being far from SentinelВӯ5P obВӯ
servations. The issue may also be caused by an error in the python package used to
interpolate values. This has raised the awareness that the quality of the interpolated valВӯ
ues calculated with this python package may not be accurate. An alternative method for
merging datasets could be reВӯgridding the SentinelВӯ5P with the HARP package to match
the grid from the ERAВӯ5 dataset. However, that would reduce the grid resolution and thus
the number of observations.
The main outcome of merging SentinelВӯ5P and ERAВӯ5 datasets is the ability to correlate
the CH4 dry air mixing ratio with meteorological factors such as temperature, direct soВӯ
lar radiation cloud fraction, wind speed, and direction. The analysis showed a negative
correlation between temperature and direct solar radiation which backs up the findings
from LevelВӯ2 product analytics. Similar trends were observed in the study performed by
F. Artuso et.al. [24] where inВӯsitu measurements of CH4 were carried out for 10 years at
the island of Lampedusa. The study showed a yearly pattern where the peak methane
concentrations are in Winter and Spring and the minimum are in the early Summer. The
justification for such a pattern is the reaction of CH4 with the OH radical which causes the
sinking of methane. As OH is produced with a photochemical reaction, its concentration
is higher in Summer, where the solar radiation is higher [24].
While the surface albedo was provided with the LevelВӯ2 product, its analytics was carried
out in the merged dataset section, when it was correlated with other factors. There is little
data available from Autumn and Winter where its values are expected to be high, due to
rain and snow reflecting the sunlight. Assuming, these observations would be reflected
in the dataset, the observed correlation would be even stronger.
The merged dataset also enabled to correlate the CH4 dry air mixing ratio with the speed
and direction of the wind. The main observations are that with the increasing wind speed,
the CH4 dry air mixing ratio decreases, especially when the wind is Southward. This may
be explained by clear air columns from the Arctic diluting the local air. On the other hand,
with decreasing wind speed and NorthВӯEastward or NorthВӯWestward wind, CH4 dry air
mixing ratio is increasing which can be explained by polluted air from other parts of EuВӯ

rope mixing with local air. Generally, the CH4 dry air mixing ratio increases at low wind
speeds and decreases at high wind speeds which can be explained by air mixing. The
lifetime of the methane in the atmosphere is 9 years [2] therefore its migration is possible.
Migration of methane from the SouthВӯEast could be possible since there are operating

4.4
Assesment of the quality of the dataset
The LevelВӯ2 product has different flags of quality which are assessed based on factors
such as cloud fraction, surface albedo, and terrain roughness [23]. These factors can disВӯ
turb the signal received by the TROPOMI instrument and thus affect the output. The fact
that the sensors are sensitive to circumstances like high cloud coverage makes it difficult
to collect the data in countries such as Denmark where cloud coverage often tends to be
high [26]. Likely for that cause, there was no data available for the month of December and
a small amount of data available for November, January, and February. The availability
of the data should be similar for areas with an alike climate which makes it a disadvanВӯ
tage to analyze the SentinelВӯ5P observations from NorthВӯEuropean countries. Moreover,
as shown in Figure 4.6, in months where the number of observations was low, the mean
validity was also low, which decreases the credibility of these measurements. When it
comes to the influence of the surface albedo on the quality of the data, even though the
bias correction is performed [10], a pattern that led to bias correction is still visible in FigВӯ
ure 4.11. The pattern is that with low surface albedo, the CH4 dry air mixing ratio tends
to be underestimated while with high surface albedo, it tends to be overestimated comВӯ
pared to measurements performed with other spectrometers [10]. Even though the bias
correction was performed, the surface albedo and CH4 dry air mixing ratio are correlated
therefore there may still be some misestimation in place, especially with very high or very
low values of surface albedo. Lastly, the quality of the data is also dependent on the terВӯ
rain roughness, which makes the data unreliable at regions with terrain roughness higher
than 80m [23]. Another study [27] has shown that the height has an influence on the CH4
dry air mixing ratio due to the weight of air which is elevation dependant. According to the
findings, decrease in CH4 concentration happens with increase in terrain elevation [27].
Another reason for lack of the data in given months may be the solar zenith angle beВӯ
ing higher than 70o or the viewing zenith angle being higher than 60o. In the example
case from the Algorithm Technical Baseline Document [10] which covered the data from
November, data around Denmark and other parts of Europe were discarded due to those
reasons. The product readme file [23] explains that when the solar zenith angle is higher
than 70o the data is labeled with quality 40 but when itвҖҷs higher than 75o the quality label
is 0.
While there are some good quality data available, especially for the Spring and SumВӯ
mer, overall SentinelВӯ5P is not the most optimal CH4 monitoring tool for Denmark, to be
used alone. Primarily to a lack of availability of data throughout the year. To obtain such
data, ideally multiple ground measuring stations would have to be established. This may
not be possible due to large costs, as well as terrain properties or ownership. However,
SentinelВӯ5P measurements are still a valuable source of information as they enable to easВӯ
ily investigate the spatial distribution of methane over the country. Monitoring of methane
should be performed continuously due to its global warming potential and that cannot be
achieved without ground measurements.
As SentinelВӯ5P measures CH4 not only in Denmark, data from other regions could be used
for investigations of the pollution sources since in a lot of places methane concentration
in air is not monitored on an ongoing basis. A study performed by E. Ouerghi et.al. [28]
proposed a method for methane plume detection where an alternative algorithm to proВӯ
cess LevelВӯ1B data was developed. The main difference was that the one proposed by E.
Ouerghi et.al. included a wavelength mask and an angle correction. The LevelВӯ1B data
are also available publicly, therefore, independent researchers can develop their own alВӯ
gorithms depending on the purpose of analytics. Another benefit of the public availability
of the data is that it is independent of those in whose interest is not to disclose such data.

4.5
Limitations
4.5.1
Lack of continuous timeline
As mentioned in previous sections, one of the biggest limitations of the dataset is the low
availability of data from Winter. This causes inability to investigate CH4 dry air mixing
ratios during seasons, where they are expected to be the highest. Throughout the meaВӯ
surement time, no data was captured during December and only a few observations were
available in November, January, and February, compared to other months of the year.
The main disadvantage associated with that is the lack of ability to investigate yearly patВӯ
terns. Based on available data and calculated correlation factors, some assumptions can
be made, i.e. low direct solar radiation and low temperature are expected in the Winter,
therefore high CH4 dry air mixing ratios are also expected. However, these are only asВӯ
sumptions and real observations may differ. Reasons behind lack of the data in these
seasons are listed and discussed in the section 4.4. As the reasons are related to the atВӯ
mospheric conditions, there is no way these observations could be retrieved. Therefore,
to obtain a data frame continuous for the whole year, the data could be retrieved from other
sources such as satellite or ground measurements. However, the GOSAT instrument also
does not provide as many observations from Winter in Central Europe, compared to other
months [25]. In terms of ground measurements, it is also impossible to obtain data on
such a highВӯresolution grid as hyperspectral measurements provide. It would require esВӯ
tablishing stations at each grid cell of 7x7 km, which first of all would be very expensive
and secondly may be impossible due to terrain properties or ownership. Nevertheless,
having some ground measuring stations that provide publicly available data on methane
concentrations would enable higher spatial resolution data through interpolation.
Despite the lack of data from Winter, there are also missing data in other seasons. Figure
4.5 shows that the amount of the data during different months differs from around 8000
observations in most of them to even approximately 23 000 observations. That circumВӯ
stances make it challenging to create a continuous timeline, even in months when the
amount of observations is high. That is why it is more representative to analyze monthly
or seasonal trends in this dataset. If there was a need to look at time series with the daily
resolution, the data could be interpolated, however, in months with a small number of
observations, the result of such interpolation could be vague.
4.5.2
The area of interest being only Denmark
The area of interest for this study covers only Denmark, however, SentinelВӯ5P collects
observations from nearly the whole globe. There was an assumption that methane is
being brought to Denmark from countries that have operating coal mines, which could
be proved or discarded based on observations from those countries. As some countries
have higher emission rates than others and methane has a lifetime long enough to travel
in the atmosphere, looking at least at the areas neighboring to the area of interest would
potentially bring value. Moreover, emissions can also come from natural sources such as
wetlands [2], which are not restricted by the political boundaries. Having a larger picture of
the CH4 dry air mixing ratio could also enable to discover additional spatial and temporal
patterns. A larger study area would bring value but to to perform it, one has to be aware
that it requires a large storage capacity and computing power. Such study is out of scope
of this project, however for the future work it would be beneficial to consider a larger area.

was available to download in March 2019 [7]. While the first observations were dated
March 2018, their amount and quality were much smaller compared to next years. Taking
that into account, only 4 years of measurements are available. While some trends can be

5
Conclusion
In this project, SentinelВӯ5P data retrieved with algorithms described in sections 2.4, and
2.5 was downloaded and analyzed in terms of spatial and temporal trends. The statistical
analysis shows that the majority of the data is flagged with validity 40, which is below the
threshold proposed by the TROPOMI readme file [23]. Nevertheless, these observations
were not discarded due to the fact that around the coastline, there were no observations
with a validity equal to 100. Data with validity lower than 100 have higher standard deviВӯ
ation and more extreme maximum and minimum values than data with validity equal to
100. The dataset is missing observations from late Autumn and Winter due to high cloud
coverage and large solar zenith angle in Denmark during that months [26]. Visualization
of the data showed that there are no locations where the CH4 dry air mixing ratio is sigВӯ
nificantly higher than in others, even around the 5 largest emitting landfills in Denmark.
Merging the SentinelВӯ5P LevelВӯ2 dataset with the meteorological dataset helped to underВӯ
stand the temporal pattern. It was discovered that CH4 dry air mixing ratio is positively
correlated with the surface albedo and negatively correlated with the temperature, which
indicated higher CH4 dry air mixing ratios in AutumnВӯWinter and lower in Summer. This
pattern can also be explained by the atmospheric reaction of methane and OH radicals.
In the Summer, the atmospheric OH radical concentration is higher, what causes more
reactions with methane and in consequence, the sinking of methane [2]. Moreover, corВӯ
relations with wind speed and direction were observed. At high wind speeds CH4 dry air
mixing ratio tends to decrease and analogically, at low wind speeds it tends to decrease.
When the wind is blowing towards the South, the CH4 dry air mixing ratio tends to deВӯ
crease, what can be caused by clean air columns brought from the North. When the wind
is blowing towards the Northern West and West, an increase in CH4 dry air mixing ratio
is observed, what can be explained by polluted air brought with that wind.
The investigation showed that the yearly average methane dry air mixing ratio was inВӯ
creasing year by year during the period of interest. Knowing the impact of methane on
global warming brings a conclusion that its concentration in the air should be monitored.
As measurements with SentinelВӯ5P are ongoing and the data is publicly available, it could
be used as a monitoring tool in Denmark. However, it is not an optimal tool to be used
alone. Due to the specification of TROPOMI, this instrument is not able to detect methane
concentration over Denmark in the Winter, where it is expected to be the highest. Another
reason is that observations along the coastline of Denmark are flagged with a low qualВӯ
ity, what poses uncertainties to observations from big cities, where large emissions could
occur i.e. from industrial activities. SentinelВӯ5P as a methane monitoring tool should be
supported with additional onВӯground measuring devices, to achieve continuity of the meaВӯ
surements throughout the whole year. Having an additional data source could also enable
to focus on the SentinelВӯ5P data with the highest quality, as the additional source could
provide data from the coastline.
The recommendation is to establish a monitoring network relying on ground measureВӯ
ments that would serve as a complementary tool for SentinelВӯ5P. Such a solution would
