Abstract
The microfold cells (M cells) are specialized epithelial cells with the transcytotic capacity to ensure
uptake of diﬀerent molecules and microorganisms into Peyer’s patches. It is of great importance to
have a model where the uptake of the antigen through M cells can be investigated in the evaluation
of oral vaccine delivery.
A human in vitro M-like cell from a Caco-2/Raji B co-culture was developed and characterized for
this purpose. The model was characterized by studying the cell monolayer integrity, the presence of
M-like cells and the transport of nanoparticles through the model. The integrity was evaluated by
transepithelial electrical resistance and the permeability to the paracellular marker lucifer yellow.
The integrity suggested the conversion of Caco-2 cells into M-like cells due to a decreased integrity
compared to the Caco-2 cell monolayer used as control. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was
used to examine the morphological characteristics of the cell monolayer in order to identify M-like
cells. However, the presence of M-like cells in the co-culture could not be conﬁrmed by SEM, but
several holes in the cell layer were identiﬁed.
In addition, the co-culture was stained for actin
ﬁlaments and nuclei and visualized by confocal laser scanning microscopy. Some unidentiﬁable cells
with a diameter of 20-25 µm were observed and it could not be deduced whether they were M-like
cells or clumps of Raji B cells. The presence of M-like cells could neither be conﬁrmed by studying
the expression of the glycoprotein-2 or the alkaline phosphatase activity of the co-culture.
Furthermore, the transport of the nanoparticles FluoSpheres was investigated to demonstrate
the transcytotic capacity known of M cells. An increased level of transport was observed for the
co-culture, however further transport experiments were conducted to verify that the transport was
mediated by the energy- and actin-dependent mechanism, endocytosis, but it could not be con-
ﬁrmed. The transport was deduced to be caused by holes in the cell monolayer of the model. The
in vitro M-like cell model was not applicable due to the lack of M-like cells.
The in vitro M-like cell model was examined to be used for evaluation of oral vaccine delivery. SU-8
microcontainers have been discussed to be used as carrier for oral vaccine delivery. Their cytotoxic
eﬀect was assessed to demonstrate that they do not damage the intestinal epithelium. The SU-
8 microcontainers showed no evidence of cytotoxic eﬀects on the co-culture which increases their
potential to be used as oral delivery system. In addition, the uptake of the model antigen ovalbumin
(OVA) through the model was examined. An increased uptake of OVA was shown for the co-culture
but the experiment was deduced to be not representative due to a very low cell viability. Thus, the
model established in this project could not be used for evaluation of oral vaccine delivery due to
the lack of M-like cells in the model.

Introduction
Vaccines play a key role in preventing outbreak of infectious diseases worldwide. They remain the
most successful medical intervention together with clean water and sanitation for human health. Ap-
proximately 2.5 million deaths yearly have been prevented by using vaccines to generate protective
immunity against several infectious diseases [1].
The majority of vaccines today are administered parenterally which requires trained personnel
and sterile facilities. The parenteral vaccines are eﬃcient to induce systemic humoral immunity.
However, limited T-cell mediated respones and insuﬃcient mucosal response are elicited [2]. Mu-
cosal immune responses are most eﬃciently elicited by administrating the vaccines to the mucosal
surface of the body. Accordingly, the oral administration route could be useful due to the enormous
mucosal surface area of the gastrointestinal tract (GI tract) [3].
Oral administration is the most preferred route of administration due to the high patient com-
pliance, convenience and minimal invasiveness [4]. These advantages can be adapted to the oral
administration of vaccines. Oral vaccination has the beneﬁt of inducing both systemic and mucosal
responses [2]. The mucosal immunity can only be induced by oral or nasal routes. The oral ad-
ministration of vaccines do not require the use of needles which ensures an eﬀortless administration
and a reduction of cross-infections caused by the injections. The oral vaccines can be taken by the
patients themselves without supervision from trained personnel which give rise to mass vaccination
programs of larger population which is a huge advantage in developing countries [5, 6]. Further-
more, the manufacturing costs of oral vaccines are lower due to less extensive puriﬁcation steps as
for injected formulations. The regulation of the production can thus be lowered since the process
do not require the same grade of aseptic environment [2].
Despite these beneﬁts, the oral administration route still faces various challenges. First, the
vaccine needs to pass through the GI tract to the small intestine where it must be taken up by
specialized epithelial cells. Some of the barriers in the GI tract are the very low pH, high enzymatic
activity and chemical degradation which represent a harsh environment [4]. Second, the uptake
through the epithelial cell layer is limited by the presence of mucus, intestinal transit time, en-
zymes, and antimicrobial peptides [7].
Today, only seven oral vaccines are approved worldwide and they are predominantly live-attenuated
vaccines [2].
These vaccines have shown to induce unwanted side eﬀects since the weakened
pathogenic microorganism may be able to reverse to its virulent form and thus causing disease.
The modern subunit vaccines are therefore based on a highly puriﬁed antigenic fragment from the
pathogen and an immunostimulatory adjuvant [8, 9].

The subunit vaccines have shown a high safety proﬁle, but also to be less immunogenic than the
live-attenuated vaccines. The fragments used in subunit vaccines are peptides and proteins which
are easily degraded, hydrolyzed, and damaged by the harsh environment of the GI tract [8, 9].
An oral delivery system is needed in order to obtain a successful delivery of an intact antigen to
the small intestine. Diﬀerent nanoparticles (NPs) which act as carriers for the antigen have been
developed, such as liposomes, polymers, micelles, and other lipid-based delivery systems. Common
to all of them is that the NPs aim to protect the loaded cargo from degradation in the GI tract [10].
1.1
Gastrointestinal Tract
The main purpose of the GI tract is to digest food and liquids so the nutrients can be absorbed
and used for energy in the body. Furthermore, the GI tract excretes toxic compounds and waste
products from the body. The GI tract consists of the mouth, esophagus, stomach, small intestine,
large intestine, and rectum (Figure 1).
The small intestine is further divided into duodenum,
jejunum, and ileum as well as the large intestine is divided into the caecum, colon, and rectum [2].
Figure 1: The anatomy of the human GI tract, including the mouth, esophagus, stomach, small intestine,
and large intestine. Created with BioRender.com.
The ﬁrst degradation takes place when the food enters the mouth where it is chewed and mixed
with degradation enzymes. The transit time is fast in the mouth, and by swallowing it will be
transported through the esophagus into the stomach [11]. The stomach is designed to store, degrade
and slowly release the food into the small intestine. The transit time in the stomach is reported to
approximately 2.5-4 hours. The pH of the stomach is 1.0-2.5 due to the high content of gastric acid
[12, 13]. The harsh acidic environment, the presence of digestive enzymes, and salts break down
the ingested food further to allow it to enter the duodenum of the small intestine. Furthermore, the
low pH ensures that the majority of the microorganisms are killed in the stomach. The digestive
secretion entering the duodenum is called chyme [4, 13].

The acidic chyme is neutralized in the duodenum by the pancreatic secretion which composed
of water, electrolytes, and enzymes. The most important electrolyte in the pancreatic secretion
is bicarbonat (HCO−
3 ) that increases the pH. The pancreatic digestive enzymes ensure a further
degradation of lipids, carbohydrates, and proteins before passing to the jejunum and ileum for
absorption [4, 13]. The environment of the small intestine is neutral and the pH is about 6.0-7.5.
The small intestine is mainly responsible for the absorption of nutrients, including carbohydrates,
proteins and lipids and the transit time is approximately 3-4 hours. The pH values in the GI tract
can vary according to age, sex, genetics and fasting or fed state. The remaining indigestible material
will be mixed with water in the large intestine and subsequently excreted by rectum as feces [11, 14].
1.2
Small Intestine
The small intestine is divided into the three sections duodenum, jejunum, and ileum. The passage
in the 25-30 cm long duodenum is fast and therefore a relatively poor absorption occurs.
The
jejunum has a highly active peristalsis (wave-like contractions in the intestinal wall to move the
chyme forward) which favors absorption. The residue chyme will be absorbed in the ileum which
has similar absorption capacity [15]. The small intestine is the most absorptive region in the GI
tract and approximately 90% of all absorption occurs in the small intestine due to the large surface
area [16]. The epithelial cell layer covering the small intestine has a total surface area of around
400 m2 which is 200 times more than the surface area of the skin [17]. The epithelial cell layer is
arranged into villi and microvilli (Figure 2) which increase the surface area by 30-fold and 600-fold,
respectively. The extremely large surface area of the small intestine provides an unique interface
between the host and its external environment [16].

A wide variety of cells with diﬀerent characteristics comprise the small intestine, including entero-
cytes, goblet cells, Paneth cells, endocrine cells, and microfold cells (M cells). The epithelial layer of
the small intestine is covered by mucus containing the secretory immunoglobulin (sIg) A and antimi-
crobial peptides (Figure 3). However, the follicle-associated epithelium (FAE) diﬀers by encompass
M cells and the absence of mucus which allows a closer interactions between foreign material with
the epithelial layer. The FAE covers the lymphoid follicles Peyer’s patches (PPs) which are rich of
immune cells [18].

The

Enterocytes are the most common cells present in the small intestine. They are absorptive epithelial
cells responsible for the main uptake of nutrients and drugs in the small intestine [16]. Enterocytes
are highly polarized cells with diﬀerent apical and basolateral membranes. The apical membrane
has the characteristic structure of a brush border membrane consisting of lots of closely packed
microvilli [19]. The enterocytes are tightly bound to each other by tight junctions, adherens junc-
tions, desmosomes, and gap junctions (Figure 2C). The enterocytes form a semi-permeable sheet
that regulates the exchange of molecules between the mucosal layer. Tight junctions are oriented
at the upper part of the lateral surface [20].

They provide an intercellular barrier that allows paracellular transport of small molecules. The
adherens junctions and desmosomes ensure adherent strength and cell-to-cell adhesion between the
enterocytes. The gap junctions allow diﬀusion of ions, second messengers, and other small molecules
between the neighboring cells [20].
The second most commonly found cells are the mucus-secreting goblet cells which contribute
to the protective mucus layer on the surface of the small intestine. The Paneth cells are exocrine
cells located in the base of the crypts where they secrete antimicrobial proteins and peptides. In
addition, a low number of endocrine cells are present in the epithelial cell layer to secrete hormones.
Only about 1% of the total cells in the small intestine are M cells, but they play an essential role
in the uptake of antigens, pathogens, and small molecules from the lumen to lamina propria where
the dendritic cells can processes it. The M cells are located in FAE of PPs where the mucus layer is
absent [18]. PPs are organized lymphoid tissues with a dome shaped structure located within the
small intestine. They are formed by diﬀerent types of immune cells. The heterogeneous population
of cells in the small intestine interact in a community and serves as a protective barrier of the GI
tract [2].
1.3
Mucus layer
The large surface area of the GI tract is daily exposed to a large amount of foreign particles and
pathogens and therefore equipped with a protective barrier consisting of enzymatic, chemical, and
physical barriers to protect the epithelial cell layer against invasion [4]. The enzymatic and chemical
barriers cover the low pH of the GI tract and the presence of digestive enzymes. The physical barrier
is the internal epithelial surface covered by mucus in the GI tract. The mucus protects the body
from permeation of foreign pathogens by eﬃciently trapping the foreign particles and pathogens
and get rid of them [2].
Mucus is a complex hydrogel consisting of a mixture of water, carbohydrates, proteins, lipids,
inorganic salts, and immunoglobulins (Ig). The main element of the mucus layer is the protein
mucin secreted mainly by goblet cells which counts for 2-5% of the total volume. There are over
twenty diﬀerent mucin proteins encoded in the MUC gene family of which MUC2, MUC5AC, and
MUC6 are the most abundant in the GI tract. The mucin proteins are secreted as monomers that
can be linked via disulﬁde bonds to form large dynamic complexes with viscoelastic and shear-
thinning behaviour. These properties of the mucus are essential in maintaining homeostasis in the
GI tract [21]. Furthermore, the mucus layer is important in protecting the epithelial layer through
the peristaltic movements in the GI tract by lubricating the undigested material [2].

secrete mucin and antimicrobial peptides, respectively. The loosely mucus layer contains the microbiota

The mucus layer can be divided into two layers: the ﬁrmly adherent mucus layer closer to the
epithelial cell layer and the overlying loosely adherent mucus layer facing the lumen (Figure 4). It
has been widely discussed whether the small intestine composed of both layers or only one without
a concrete answer [22]. The thickness of the mucus varies depending on the location in the GI tract.
The mucus layer in rats was found to range from 125 to 830 µm [21]. In general, the mucus layerhas
been found to be thickest in the stomach followed by the colon and to be thinnest in the small
intestine. The mucus layer is thinnest in the small intestine in order to allow a higher absorption
of nutrients whereas the stomach and colon have completely diﬀerent functions [23]. Accordingly,
the small intestine is the most optimal target in the GI tract for oral delivery.

1.4
The Immune System
The immune system can be divided into the innate and adaptive immune response. The innate
immune response acts as the ﬁrst line of defence, occur rapidly within few hours, and is not speciﬁc
to a particular pathogen. The innate immune cells recognize the foreign pathogens via pattern-
recognition receptors (PRRs). The PRRs bind to pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs)
expressed by the pathogen. Although the innate immune response lacks speciﬁcity, it has the feature
of distinguish non-self antigens from self. This is crucial in when the adaptive immune response
should be activated [24].
In contrast, the adaptive immune response is antigen-speciﬁc and takes several days to estab-
lish. The antigen-speciﬁcity that the lymphocytes carries is an unique variant of an antigen-receptor
that recognizes and binds the equivalent antigen [24]. The unique feature of the adaptive immune
response is the immunological memory. Some of the activated T- and B-lymphocytes during the pri-
mary infection will diﬀerentiate into memory lymphocytes providing long-lasting immunity towards
the infectious agent. If the host is exposed to the infectious agent again, the memory lymphocytes
will rapidly diﬀerentiate into eﬀector lymphocytes and act with an immediate stronger response [17].
Various defence mechanisms can be initiated depending on the type of pathogen. The innate defence
mechanisms can be mediated through phagocytosis, cytotoxicity or by secretion of complement fac-
tors, cytokines/chemokines and antimicrobial peptides [25]. The innate immune response will most
of the time deal with the pathogenic microorganisms, but if the innate responses are not able to
eradicate the pathogen, the adaptive immune response will be activated [17].
The adaptive immune system consists of a humoral and cellular-mediated immune response. The
humoral immune response is mediated by the production of antibodies from B-lymphocytes. The
cellular-mediated immune response is the activation of the cytotoxic T-lymphocytes, macrophages
and natural killer cells as well as the release of cytokines.
Activation of the adaptive immune
response depends on how the antigen is presented by the dendritic cells (DCs) [17].
DCs are the major group of the antigen-presenting cells (APCs) and they act as the connection
between the innate and adaptive responses. Immature DCs migrate from the bone marrow into
the peripheral tissue where they reside until they are recruited into the inﬂamed site of infection
where they perform phagocytosis of the pathogen. The antigen from the pathogenic microorganism
is picked up by the DCs which will subsequently migrate to the lymph nodes. In the lymph nodes,
the antigen is presented on the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules of the DCs
to activate the näive T-lymphocytes. Antigens from extracellular and intracellular pathogens are
presented on MHC-II and MHC-I molecules, respectively [24, 26].

When the näive T-lymphocytes are activated, they diﬀerentiated into one of the eﬀector T-lymphocyte
subsets. Cytotoxic (CD8+) T-lymphocytes is the ﬁrst subset and they have the capability to kill
infected cells that express the antigen on MHC-I molecules on their cell surface. The second subset
is T-helper (CD4+) lymphocytes which can diﬀerentiate into either Th1, Th2 or Th17 lymphocytes
that diﬀer in type of cytokines secretion. T-helper lymphocytes secrete diﬀerent types of cytokines
which provide help to diﬀerent target cells in order to eradicate a certain pathogen. They assist
in the maturation of B-lymphocytes into plasma cells and provide help in activation of cytotoxic
T-lymphocytes. The third subset, the regulatory T-lymphocytes, suppresses the activation of more
lymphocytes and thus maintain immunological tolerance. In general for all of the subsets, the eﬀec-
tor T-lymphocytes will upregulate the presence of T-cell receptors (TCR) with antigen-speciﬁcity
to the antigen from the pathogen [27, 17].
On their surface, B-lymphocytes carry the transmembrane Ig called B-cell receptor (BCR) which
recognize native proteins. CD4+ T-lymphocytes stimulate the proliferation and diﬀerentiation of B-
lymphocytes into antibody-secreting lymphocytes, also called plasma cells [24]. The plasma cells can
produce ﬁve diﬀerent classes of immunoglobulins: IgA, IgD, IgE, IgG and IgM. Näive B-lymphocytes
express monomeric IgM and IgD on their surface. Both have a low antigen-aﬃnity compared with
IgA, IgE and IgG which require isotype switching and aﬃnity maturation to produce antibodies
with a higher antigen-aﬃnity [17]. IgM is the ﬁrst type of antibody that is secreted by the plasma
cells. The IgM antibodies are mainly found in the plasma due to the large size of pentamers that
they form.
The pentamers are eﬀective in activcating the complement system, however IgM is
also the antibody class with the lowest aﬃnity. IgG is the predominately class in the serum and
extracellular ﬂuid of the body. IgE is essential against parasitic infection but play also a central role
in allergy and hypersensitivity. IgA is found as a monomer in the serum and as a secretory dimer
at mucosal surfaces [28].
1.4.1
Mucosal Immunity
The mucosal immune system consists of the GI tract, the upper and lower respiratory tract, the
urogenital tract and the inner ear. Common to all of them is that they are a part of the internal
body surfaces that are in directly contact with the external environment. The mucosal surfaces are
all protected by the mucus-secreting epithelium layer [29]. The mucosal immune system contains
approximately 30% of all lymphocytes in the body and is responsible for the production of the
majority of antibodies [17]. The advantages of the mucosal immune system are that both mucosal
and systemic immune responses can be elicited [30].

==PAGE:20==
Master Thesis
January 2021
Furthermore, the production of IgA and IgG antibodies is another beneﬁt compared to the systemic
immunization which not allow production of mucosal IgA antibodies [30]. Focus from here will be
on the mucosal immunity of the GI tract.
The GI tract is continuously exposed to a large amount of particles, microorganisms, and pathogens
where only some of them are harmful. Certain locations in the GI tract are colonized by commensal
microorganisms that are not damaging for the host. They act in a symbiotic relationships with
the host and maintain important functions, such as the bacteria in the stomach which assist in
cellulose digestion [17]. Furthermore, the GI tract is exposed to many harmless food proteins which
should not activate the mucosal immune system. It is essential that the mucosal immune system
maintains the immunological homeostasis of the GI tract due to the constantly exposure to the
external environment [31].
In addition, immune tolerance to self-antigens are vital in order to avoid activate immune re-
sponses against the host’s tissues. DCs are highly involved in this by two mechanisms: central
and peripheral tolerance. The central tolerance occurs in the thymus where the T lymphocytes are
generated. T lymphocytes with a TCR that recognizes self-antigens are eliminated. Moreover, the
peripheral tolerance involved induction of anergic T lymphocytes. DCs with captured self-antigens
will migrate to the lymph nodes and present it on the MHC complex to näive T lymphocytes.
However, in the absence of co-stimulatory molecules, it will result in either anergic T lymphocytes
or deletion [24, 26].
The mucosal immune system can be functionally and anatomically divided into the organized
mucosa-associated lymphoid tissues (MALTs) and lamina propria (LP). The MALTs refer to all the
mucosal inductive sites, including the gut-associated lymphoid tissues (GALTs) and nasopharyngeal-
associated lymphoid tissue. The inductive lymphoid sites take care of initiating antigen-speciﬁc
immune responses. The GALT comprises the PPs which are only present in the small intestine.
LP is an eﬀector site for the humoral and cellular-mediated immune responses. The migration of
activated immune cells from the inductive sites to eﬀector sites via the lymphatic system is the
cellular basis of the mucosal immunity [17, 31].
The enterocytes of the epithelium act as a part of the innate mucosal defence. These express toll-like
receptors (TLRs) which are PRRs that can recognize speciﬁc PAMPs from dangerous microorgan-
isms. If they recognize a PAMP, the enterocytes respond by secreting chemo- and cytokines to
the surrounding tissue in order to provoke APCs to the site of infection. Moverover, mucus and
antimicrobial proteins present on the epithelial surface are also parts of the innate defences [3].

The IgA antibodies are an important adaptive immune defence at the mucosal surfaces. The major
advantage of IgA antibodies is that they are resistant to degradation in the protease-rich environment
of the epithelial surfaces. The dimerization of the sIgA and high degree of glycosylation leads to the
resistance to proteases [32]. The sIgA antibodies provide entrapment of harmful microorganisms
and antigens present in the mucus of the small intestine before reaching the mucosal surface, thus
avoiding any damage to the epithelium. Furthermore, sIgAs prevent mucosal-adhesion by blocking
and sterically hindering the surface proteins of pathogen that provide epithelial attachment [33].
In the small intestine, only 5-15% of the plasma cells secrete IgG antibodies. There is a low
presence of IgG antibodies because they are susceptible to degradation by proteases. However, they
play a signiﬁcant role in neutralizing the pathogens that enter the mucosa and thus preventing
systemic spread [3, 34].
Initiation of the mucosal immune responses occur in PPs located in the intestinal wall. The small
intestine comprises of about 100-200 PPs which are rich in dendritic cells, T-lymphocytes and B-
lymphocytes. The FAE is the overlying epithelium of the mucosal lymphoid follicles forming the
PPs. The FAE contains enterocytes and a small proportion of the specialized epithelial cells called
M cells. M cells are responsible for transcytosis of molecules from the lumen to LP where a large
population of diﬀerent immune cells are present [18].
1.5
Microfold Cells
M cells encompass about 3-5% of the intestinal cells in the FAE and less than 1% of the total surface
area of the small intestine [18]. As mentioned before, they act as entrance for mucosal invasion and
are involved in the initiation of the adaptive mucosal immune response as well as food tolerance.
The M cells form intraepithelial pockets facing LP where APCs, T-, and B-lymphocytes are inﬁl-
trated. Molecules from the intestinal lumen are rapidly transported by transcytosis directly into
the pockets of the M cells. Subsequently, the dendritic cells can take up the antigen, process and
present it to näive T-lymphocytes [31].
The characteristics of M cells are distinguish from those of enterocytes in the absence of surface
microvilli and reduced enzymatic activity on the apical membrane. The M cells show a poorly
organized brush border membrane lacking the microvilli (Figure 5) and they can be observed using
scanning electron microscopy (SEM). This morphology of M cells is restricted to human M cells. In
contrast, murine M cells present short and irregular microvilli on their apical surface. Due to the
large variations in the morphology and characteristics of M cells among species, it is challenging to
use non-human models to make conclusions about human M cells [35].

1.5.1
Diﬀerentiation into M cells
The epithelial layer of the small intestine is continuously renewed and the epithelial cells emerge
from the adjacent crypts surrounding mucosal lymphoid follicles. The epithelial cells start to migrate
from the crypts to the underlying lymphoid follicle of the FAE. The underlying lymphoid cells in
PPs are thought to regulate the diﬀerentiation of the follicle-associated cells since these cells have
an unique and distinct gene expression proﬁle than the villus epithelial cells. The presence of M
cells has shown to be increased within a few hours after exposure to a foreign bacterium which
suggests that some neighbouring cells manage the diﬀerentiation of M cells [41].
A study with severe combined immunodeﬁciency mice showed that lymphocytes play an essen-
tial role in the diﬀerentiation into M cells. The B cell-deﬁcient mice comprise T-lymphocytes areas
and they showed a signiﬁcantly reduced number of M cells. In contrast, small PPs with follicles and
M cells were observed in T cell-deﬁcient mice [42]. It indicates that the diﬀerentiation of M cells
was strongly inﬂuenced by B-lymphocytes.
Some cytokines have been identiﬁed to be important in the diﬀerentiation of M cells. RANKL is
one of the members of the tumor necrosis factor family cytokines and it has been shown in vivo to
be involved in M cell diﬀerentiation [43]. RANKL is a transmembrane protein that is released from
the cell surface following by cleavage [44]. The cytokine binds to its receptor RANK which activates
a downstream pathway where TRAF6 and NF-B are thought to be involved. RANKL is expressed
by subepithelial stromal cells in PPs and the RANK receptor is expressed by enterocytes of the FAE
[43, 45]. A study by Knoop et al. [46] demonstrated that the cytokine RANKL plays a key role
in the diﬀerentiation of the FAE to mature M cells. The study was based on the characterization
of RANKL null mice in order to study the functionality of the PPs in the absence of RANKL. It
revealed that these mice had less than 2% of the wild type levels of M cells and that they were
insuﬃciently to transport particles [46]. The result suggests that the cytokine RANKL is important
for the diﬀerentiation of M cells.
Furthermore, the Ets transcription factor Spi-B was found to be highly upregulated shortly
after RANKL treatment. Spi-B is known to be involved in B-cell function and development in-
cluding BCR signaling, formation of germinal centers and the antibody responses. Kanaya et al.
demonstrated that Spib−/−mice completely lacked the presence of M cells which indicates that the
diﬀerentiation of M cells is highly regulated by the transcription factor Spi-B [47].
Additionally, GP2 is argued to be a marker of terminally diﬀerentiated M cells due to the importance
of the receptor’s role in uptake of pathogenic bacteria [48]. It was based on a study of a co-culture
of Caco-2/Raji B-lymphocytes by Kim et al. [49].

1.5.2
Cellular Pathways of Uptake
After penetration of the mucus layer, the molecules or microorganisms need to cross the epithelial
layer to get access to LP. The transport through the epithelial membrane in the small intestine can
be divided into two routes: paracellular and transcellular transport (Figure 6). Transport can occur
in both directions of the epithelium [17, 50], but focus here will be on the direction from the apical
to the basolateral side.
Figure 6: The main transport pathways through the epithelial layer including the paracellular, transcellular,
receptor-mediated transport, and transcytosis by M cells. Created with BioRender.com.
The paracellular transport occurs through the intercellular junctions between the adjacent cells. The
pathway is passive and do not require cell energy to occur, however only very small molecules, salts,
and water can pass through the paracellular pathway. The transport depends on the concentration
gradient across the epithelial membrane. The tight junctions between the adjacent cells are the
limiting factor for the size of molecules that can be transported.
They do not allow molecules
greater then 2 nm to pass through. In general, small hydrophilic molecules and ions are restricted
to the paracellular route [51, 50]. Mannitol and Lucifer Yellow (LY) are often used as paracellular
markers in vitro by considering the permeability of the epithelium [52].

==PAGE:25==

The transcellular transport occurs through adjacent cells and allows larger molecules and microor-
ganisms to be transported trough the epithelium. The pathway is mainly energy-dependent, however
small lipophilic molecules can pass through by passive transport. The uptake of antigens or mi-
croorganisms by the M cells involves transcytosis, where the material is taken up by the M cell into
vesicles. The cellular process of taken up the material is called endocytosis and the process where the
content of the vesicles is released from the cell is called exocytosis [53]. The transcellular transport
can be mediated with or without membrane receptors. The pathway is highly regulated in which
the cell-surface receptor recognizes and binds its ligand resulting in the formation of endocytotic
vesicles [50].
Multiple endocytotic pathways have been described, but the major endocytotic route is called
clathrin-mediated endocytosis. Assembling of the vesicles occurs by membrane bending where the
plasma membrane of the cell transforms into a vesicle with clathrin-proteins on the surface. More
than 50 proteins are involved in the formation of the clathrin-coated endocytotic vesicles.
The
coated clathrin proteins will be removed after scission of the vesicle from the plasma membrane,
and thereby allows intracellular transport. Furthermore, the actin cytoskeleton has been described
as a major factor in the membrane bending during the formation of the endocytotic vesicles. Studies
in both mammalian and yeast cells showed that in the presence of an inhibitor aﬀecting the actin
polymerization, the endocytosis was blocked [54].
One of the common mucosal receptor-mediated transport mechanisms is the trancytosis of IgA
antibodies in the basolateral to apical direction. When the sIgA antibody has been produced by
plasma cells in LP, it binds to the polymeric immunoglobulin receptor. This binding facilitates
the transport of sIgA by trancytosis to the surface of the lumen. sIgA is released from the apical
membrane and is then ready to act as a part of the mucosal immune response [17, 53].
1.5.3
Active Targeting of M Cells
Delivering of an antigen to the M cells cannot guarantee a potent oral mucosal immune response
although the M cells are known as the entry to intestinal immunity. The uptake and presentation of
the antigen by APCs in PPs are the key aspects to elicit mucosal immunity. However, targeted de-
livery of antigens to M cells may hopefully provide an increased eﬃciency of the antigen presentation
and thus initiation of the mucosal immune response [38].

Active targeted delivery systems have been used to reduce dose-related toxicity and improving the
mucosal immune response. The targeting is described as active when a certain ligand is linked to
the surface of the delivery system to direct it to the target destination. Many diﬀerent approaches
of M cell targeting have been investigated in the recent years. Most of them use a M cell targeted
ligand conjugated on the surface of the NP. The NP ensures that the antigen is delivered intact
to the small intestine and the M cell targeted ligand increases the transport eﬃciency of the NP
trough the M cells to the PPs [30].
Lectin ligands have been widely studied to target M cells in mice. Ulex europaeus agglutinin-1
(UEA-1) is a lectin that recognizes ↵1,2-fucose residues on glycoproteins located on the surface of
murine M cells. Conjugation of UEA-1 to the surface of diﬀerent NPs have shown an increased
uptake in vivo by binding to M cells in PPs in mice and afterwards successfully transporting the
NPs to the basolateral membrane. [18, 30]. However, UEA-1 can not be adapted to targeting of
human M cells due to the recognition of only M cells of mouse origin [55].
The transcytotic receptor GP2 mentioned earlier has also been investigated as target for M cells.
Shima et al. established an oral administrated vaccine containing the fusion protein anti-GP2-SA
that showed to induce mucosal immunity by targeting GP2 on M cells. The fusion protein consists
of streptavidin (SA) fused with the antigen-binding fragment of an anti-GP2 antibody and it was
used for delivery of ovalbumin (OVA). Eﬃciently production of OVA-speciﬁc IgA antibodies was
shown after oral administration in mice [56]. The study showed the potential of using GP2-mediated
targeting to increase the uptake by M cells. Furthermore, a lot of other promising M cell targets
have been described such as targeting of PRRs, other lectin mimetics, the claudin-4 protein, and
the complement C5a receptor [18].
1.6
Vaccines
Edward Jenner demonstrated in 1796 that by inoculating a young boy with substances from a fresh
cowpox lesion from a dairymaid, the boy was protected towards infections of smallpox. E. Jenner
decided to name the new procedure of protection vaccination from the latin word for cow vacca and
cowpox vaccinia [57]. This investigation was epoch-making and a big step in the right direction of
understanding immunization. The aim of a vaccine is to elicit immunity towards a target pathogen
causing disease. Vaccinations plays an essential role in preventing outbreak of infectious diseases
worldwide and they have saved milions of lives from a variety of infectious diseases. Furthermore,
vaccines can also provide community protection by reducing the transmission of the disease if
suﬃcient individuals in a population are vaccinated, also called herd immunity [58].
Page 15 of 84
==PAGE:27==
Master Thesis
January 2021
There are diﬀerent types of vaccines including live-attenuated, killed, inactivated, and subunit
vaccines. The live-attenuated vaccines contain a weakened version of the original pathogen. This
type of vaccine has shown to be best in elicit a potent cellular and humoral immune responses.
However, the safety proﬁle is poor since the vaccine contains the pathogen which has the possibility
to reverse to its virulent state and causing the disease [59].
The killed and inactivated vaccines can be placed in a common category. The killed vaccines do
not contain any living pathogens and the inactivated vaccines are based on inactivated pathogens
and thereby can both of them not cause disease. Diﬀerent agents can be used to inactivate the
pathogen such as, heat, radiation or chemicals. By inactivating the pathogen, it will not have the
ability to causing the disease anymore. Common to the two types of vaccines are the high safety pro-
ﬁle but a less immunogenicity and a short duration of protection. Adjuvants and repeated doses of
the vaccines can be given to achieve higher immunogenicity and long-term immunity, respectively [9].
Advances in genetic engineering have made it possible to develop safer approaches of vaccinating
which enable the production of subunit vaccines [2]. Subunit vaccines are based on a highly puriﬁed
antigenic fragment from the target pathogen that is not virulent. The antigenic fragments can be
polysaccharides, small fragile peptides or virus-like particles if the pathogen is a virus [9]. However,
they have shown a poor uptake through the intestinal epithelium and lack the ability to induce
immunogenicity in absent of an adjuvant [8].
Adjuvants are substances capable of enhancing and modulating eﬀective immune responses.
The subunit vaccines are administrated with an immunostimulatory adjuvant sine the vaccines only
contain one or few components from the pathogen which are not enough to elicit a strong immune
response. The majority of the fragments used in subunit vaccines are easily degraded, hydrolyzed,
and damaged by the harsh environment of the GI tract. A delivery system carrying the antigen
and protecting it from the external environment, as well as an adjuvant to stimulate the immune
response, are needed to overcome these challenges of oraly administrated subunit vaccines [8].

1.6.2
Oral Delivery of Vaccines
Most of the licensed vaccines worldwide are administrated parenterally either by intramuscular or
subcutaneous administration.
These vaccines have shown a high eﬃcacy and the ability to in-
duce systemic humoral immunity by the production of antibodies against the antigen of the target
pathogen. Limited T cell-mediated responses as well as insuﬃcient mucosal protection are achieved
by these injected vaccines [2].
Several aspects have forced the pharmaceutical industry to work within the ﬁeld of oral drug delivery,
and such aspects include high patient compliance, safety, low production costs, and the convenience
of the oral administration. In addition, the oral administration gives the opportunity to medicate
patients at home and thereby lower the costs due to avoidance of trained personnel and sterile
manufacturing compared to the injected formulations [4, 65]. The major advantage of oral vaccines
is the ability to induce both cell-mediated- and humoral immune responses including the production
of the mucosal antibody IgA and the systemic antibody IgG [2].
Furthermore, the oral vaccines are very attractive in developing countries and allows mass im-
munization of large populations.
The oral vaccines does not require needles for administration
which is important due to a high risk of contaminated needles and non-sterile medical facilities in
these countries. Another aspect is the storage and logistic of oral vaccines. If they are produced as
heat stable capsules or tablets which can be self-administrated, the logistic and storage of the oral
vaccines are more simple and inexpensive which is preferred in especially developing countries [66].
In general, oral vaccines have shown a poor eﬃcacy compared to parenterally administrated vaccines.
The major limitation of oral delivered vaccines is the poor solubility, fast enzymatic and chemical
degradation of the antigen in the harsh environment of the GI tract as well as limited permeability
trough the epithelial layer. The transit time in the small intestine is limited to 3-4 hours which
means that the absorption of the antigen must take place within this time frame [4, 14].

antigen from degradation in the environment of the GI tract. In addition, they should be able
to control the release of the antigen over an extended period to increase the absorption time and
optimally provide targeting of the M cells in the small intestine for eliciting an eﬀective immune
response [67]. Despite all the challenges of oral delivery of vaccines, the presence of few licensed
oral vaccines has shown that it is possible to achieve immunization by oral vaccination.

and thereby prolong the transit time in the small intestine [70]. The transit time in the intestine

1.7.1
Microcontainers for Oral Vaccine Delivery
The concept of the microcontainers can easily be adapted for oral delivery of vaccines. As previously
mentioned in section 1.6, the antigens in subunit vaccines are often fragile and easily degraded in
the acidic environment of the stomach. The microcontainers can be a potential solution for that.
They function as a protective carrier of the antigen and can have the ability to induce a particulate
uptake of the M cells (Figure 8). The microcontainers can be loaded with the vaccine formulation
including, the antigen of interest, an adjuvant, antibiotics, and stabilizing agents. The structure of
the microcontainers can resist the acidic environment of the stomach in the GI tract making them
ideal as a delivery system of vaccines to the small intestine [67].
The controlled released achieved by coating the microcontainers with a pH-sensitive polymer is
beneﬁcial to oral vaccine delivery. The oral vaccines aim to target the M cells located in PPs of the
small intestine due to their transcytotic ability. By selecting the appropriate coating, the antigen
can be delivered near the M cells with a controlled release [51]. Some of the most commonly used
pH-sensitive polymers are Eudragit L100-55, L100, and S100 which dissolve at pH of 5.5, 6.0, and
7.0, respectively. Eudragit L100 and S100 could be a good choice to apply for vaccine delivery to
the small intestine due to the pH of 6.0 to 7.5 in the small intestine [67, 78].

1.8
In Vitro Cell Based Small Intestinal Models
In vitro models are of great interest since they are cheaper and more accessible compared to in vivo
models. Furthermore, they have a higher throughput meaning that more compounds can be tested
within less time. The use of in vitro models could reduces the number of animals used in the research
[79]. The in vitro models are relatively economical compared with in vivo models, but it could be
a resource-intensive and time-consuming process to develop a fully functional cell monolayer and
maintain the cell cultures in the laboratory. It has been estimated that a normal permeability study
on a 21-day culturing monolayer of Caco-2 cells costs around US$19 per compound for the drug
discovery organizations [16].
The intestinal barrier of the GI tract is important in regard to how drugs, particles, and microor-
ganisms are transported through the epithelium. Therefore, it is important to have an in vitro
model mimicking the epithelial layer in the small intestine to study the uptake through the M
cells. Several limitations for studying the uptake in in vitro cell based small intestinal models have
been described. Factors as the digestive processes, including salts, low pH, enzymes as well as the
intestinal motility are factors that are not represented in the cell based in vitro models [80].

1.8.1
Commonly Used Cell Lines
Isolation and handling of primary human intestinal epithelial cells are very challenging. They have a
short viability in vitro and they are diﬃcult to maintain in the laboratory. Furthermore, they must
be obtained from liver biopsies of patients suﬀering from a liver disease. Thus, the use of primary
human intestinal epithelial cells is limited due to availability and individual variability of the donors.
Therefore, it is important to make use of other cell lines that share most possible characteristics
and functionalities with the primary human intestinal enterocytes [82].
Caco-2 cells are the most well-known cell line used for mimicking the epithelium of the small intes-
tine and for permeability studies. The cells are derived from a human colorectal adenocarcinoma cell
line which share some essential characteristics with the enterocytes present in the human epithelium
of the small intestine. These characteristics are microvilli on the apical membrane, formation of
tight junctions and the expression of receptors and enzymes presented normally by the enterocytes
[79]. Furthermore, the Caco-2 cells are polarized cells that can be diﬀerentiated into heterogeneous
cells which is an important feature [16].

The Caco-2 cells start to diﬀerentiate when they reach conﬂuence and after 14-21 days they will
appear as small intestinal enterocytes with microvilli on the apical surface [80]. Some of the disad-
vantages of using Caco-2 cells are that they have a lower expression of metabolic enzymes speciﬁcally
CYP3A4 which is the main CYP enzyme presented by the enterocytes. In addition, they lack mu-
cus, have a long diﬀerentiation period and a large inter- and intra-laboratory variability [74, 83, 84].
Madin-Darby Canine Kidney (MDCK) cells has also been used in the prediction of in vitro
absorption. The cell line is derived from dog kidney cells. They have shown lower transepithelial
electrical resistance (TEER) values than Caco-2 cells, which is closer to the in vivo TEER values of
the small intestine in humans. Furthermore, they are polarized cells that rapidly form a monolayer
in 3-5 days with tight junctions between adjacent cells. Lower activity of some transporters, such
as P-glycoprotein, limits the MDCK cell line.
Nevertheless, the human MDR1 gene have been
transfected into MDCK cells and thereby allow them to express P-gp. The major limitation is the
diﬀerence among species which should be considered in the evaluation of the absorption [15, 16].
1.8.2
Co-Culture Systems
Today, most of the in vitro models of the intestinal barrier are developed by the growth of a
monolayer of Caco-2 cells since it is a human cell line. The major limitation of using Caco-2 is
the absence of the mucus layer as well as the fact that they lack M cells which both are essential
parts of the epithelial layer. Several diﬀerent co-culture systems have been established to overcome
these limitations of the original mono-cultured models and to come closer to represent the human
epithelium of the small intestine. The following described co-culture systems are all based on Caco-2
cells.
Caco-2/HT29-MTX Co-culture
The mucus layer acts as an important protective barrier in
limiting transport or trapping the molecules into the mucus. A co-culture of Caco-2 and HT29-MTX
cells has been developed to obtain a more representative model in terms of the mucus layer [51].
HT29-MTX is a subgroup of the human colon adenocarcinoma cell line HT29 that has been isolated
from a primary tumor. They are polarized cells able to form tight junctions and the typical apical
brush border of enterocytes. HT29-MTX cells diﬀerentiate into mature goblet cells that produce
mucin [15, 80].
One of the disadvantages of this co-culture model is that the HT29-MTX cells express some
cell surface receptors that are not equivalent to the normal enterocytes of the small intestine.
Furthermore, although they show similar characteristics of the enterocytes in the small intestine,
they are originally colonic cells [80, 85].

Another limitation is that the model does not contain M cells and can therefore not be used to
study uptake through the epithelial layer. The Caco-2/HT29-MTX co-culture is typically used in
the evaluation of enhancers and mucoadhesive drug delivery systems as well as for bacterial adhesion
and invasion due to the mucus layer covering the cell monolayer [15].
The FAE encompasses the important cell type, M cells, which play an essential role in uptake of
particles from the lumen to the PPs. This is the reason why these cells have a great potential
in studying oral delivery. It is important to have a model to study the uptake of particles and
microorganisms by the M cells due to the limit presence of M cells in the small intestine of humans
[36, 86]. However, conducting in vivo studies are challenging and not always relevant since the
M cells varies in proportions and phenotype between species which increases the importance of a
human model containing M cells [87].
Caco-2/Murine Lymphocytes
Kerneis et al. [88] established an in vitro model of the human
FAE from a co-culture of Caco-2 cells and lymphocytes isolated from PPs of BALB/c mice. The
co-culture was cultivated on inverted culture inserts to ensure migration of the lymphoid cells into
the epithelial layer of Caco-2 cells. The co-culture mimiced the main characteristics of M cells by
reorganization of the brush border membrane and that particle transportation was temperature-
dependent indicating the transcytotic capacity of the M cells. However, the co-culture required the
isolation of primary murine lymphocytes to establish the model which makes the development of
the co-culture very challenging [88].
Caco-2/Raji B Co-culture
A co-culture of two human cell lines, Caco-2 cells and Raji B cells,
has been established in order to mimic the FAE of the small intestine. The co-culture does not
contain any mucus-producing cells since the presence of goblet cells are limited in FAE. As described
in section 1.5.1, B-lymphocytes are required for the M cells diﬀerentiation. Raji B cells are human
lymphoblast-like cells derived from a Burkitt’s lymphoma patient [15, 43]. The cell line is infected
by the Epstein-Barr virus (EPV) and thus called EPV-positive. The transformation by EPV is
associated to stimulate DNA synthesis and the antibody production of the cells which allows them
to develop into proliferating B-lymphocytes.
The Raji B-lymphocytes induce diﬀerentiation of
Caco-2 cells into cells with M-like morphology [89, 90].

The co-culture was developed in the Transwell system to allow cell-cell communication between the
Caco-2 and Raji B cells [80]. A normal and an inverted orientation of the co-culture have been
developed. In the normal orientation of the co-culture, the Raji B cells have been seedded directly
onto a Caco-2 monolayer grown on the membrane of the Transwell. However, it allows that the
Caco-2 cells to migrate through the pores into the opposite side of the membrane which was not
of interest. The inverted co-culture was therefore developed to prevent migration of the Caco-2
cells. After a monolayer of Caco-2 cells had been established, the insert was inverted and the Raji
B cells were seeded on the basolateral side of the membrane. By inverting the model, it allows a
closer contact between the two cell lines which result in an improved conversion rate of M-like cells
compared to the normal orientated model [91]. The presence of M like cells is conﬁrmed by the
loss of microvilli on the apical membrane. The Caco-2/Raji B co-culture is a M-like cell system
typically used to study the M cell mediated uptake of particles and microorganisms [51].
The advantage of this co-culture is that it is based on well-establish diﬀerentiated human cell
lines. Both of the cell lines are easy to maintain in the laboratory and do not require any unusual
media compositions. The model does not require the isolation of primary cells which is very chal-
lenging due to a limited access of human tissue as well as that the primary cells are complicated to
maintain in the laboratory [86]. However, the proportion of M like cells are 15-30% in the inverted
M cell-like model and the proportion of M cells in the human FAE is stated to approximately 3-5%
[18, 86]. The higher presence of M-like cells is a limitation of the model and needs to be considered
in the amount of uptake through the cell monolayer.
1.8.3
Primary M Cell Model
A primary M cell model has been established by RANKL-stimulated diﬀerentiation of intestinal
stem cells. Primary human intestinal crypts were grown in the presence and absence of recombinant
RANKL. The M cell markers, Spi-B and GP2, were assessed to measure the M cell formation during
the experiment. The expression of Spi-B and GP2 was increased after 4 days and it reached its
maximum at day 7. The primary M cell model showed several similar characteristics and cell surface
markers of M cells. Furthermore, the uptake of microparticles and the bacteria S. Typhimurium
were demonstrated [92]. The model have shown physiological relevance and a gene expression proﬁle
close to the in vivo M cells. However, a limitation is that it requires supply of human tissue for
developing the M cell model. In addition, it is challenging to maintain the diﬀerentiated primary
human intestinal M cells in the laboratory [40].

2
Hypothesis
The hypothesis is that the in vitro M-like cell model would mimic the human intestinal epithelium of
the small intestine by containing specialized epithelial M-like cells with transcytotic capacity. This
would be employed to study uptake of orally administered vaccines or drugs along with identiﬁca-
tion of M-cell speciﬁc target for enhancement of the permeation and transcytosis, thus increasing
absorption through the epithelial layer. Furthermore, the model would be used in the screening
process of novel oral delivery systems including micro- and nanoparticles encapsulating antigens or
drugs [86, 36].
3
Purpose
The aim of this master project is to develop a human in vitro M-like cell model by cultivation of a co-
culture of Caco-2 and Raji B cells. Subsequently, it was aimed to characterize and evaluate the cell
model for oral vaccine delivery. The characterization was performed by studying the cell monolayer
integrity, the permeability, transport of nanoparticles, and the M-like cell presence in the cell model
by two microscopy methods. Furthermore, the uptake of the model antigen ovalbumin was studied
through the monolayer to show the potential for the cell model to evaluate oral vaccine delivery
systems. In addition, the cell viability of the co-culture was assessed after exposure to empty SU-8
microcontainers for three hours to ensure that they do not aﬀect the viability negatively and thus
could be a potential application for oral vaccine delivery.

had been used [106].

5.4
Evaluation of the Cell Monolayer Integrity
5.4.1
Measurement of Transepithelial Electrical Resistance
TEER is a quantitative method to measure the barrier integrity of the tight junctions in a cell
monolayer. The measurement is performed in real-time and the resistance across the cell monolayer
is measured without damaging the cell monolayer [111].
During the development of the in vitro M-like cell model, the TEER could only be monitored on
day 5 before inversion of the inserts and on day 19 where the model was fully developed (Table 2).
The limited monitoring of the model during the development was because of the wrapped silicon
tube around the inserts (see methods section 4.3). The silicon tube did not allow the electrodes to
be placed in both the apical and basolateral compartment at the same time. The measurement of
TEER on day 5 was conducted to ensure the integrity of the cell monolayer before inverting it.
The inverted orientation of the inserts is stressful to the cells and therefore a robust monolayer
with tight junctions was preferred. A decrease in the TEER values from day 5 to 19 would be
expected due to the loss of tight junctions during the diﬀerentiation of the Caco-2 cells into M-like
cells as well as the inverted orientation of the inserts.

made with the paracellular marker LY.
5.4.2
Paracellular Permeability of Lucifer Yellow
The integrity of the monolayer was further controlled by studying the permeability of LY which is
a small, hydrophillic salt that is very easily detectable at 428/536 nm due to its ﬂuorescence. LY
is useful to evaluate the functionality of the tight junctions in the cell layer and thereby give an
indication of the membrane integrity [95]. Paracellular permeability studies are often addressed by
the use of a paracellular marker, such as LY due to its capability to cross in vitro epithelial barriers
through the intercellular tight junctions. The permeability would thus give information about any
leakages of the tight junctions which will aﬀect the permeation of LY [113, 114].
The hypothesis is that LY will not be able to pass the epithelial barrier of the mono-culture
since the tight junctions are maintained. However, the conversion of Caco-2 cells into M-like cells
in the co-culture is considered to aﬀect the integrity of the tight junctions and thereby increase the
permeability of LY [36]. The ﬂux of the two concentrations, 2250 nM and 4500 nM, of LY were
calculated and evaluated for the mono- and co-cultures after 150 minutes (Figure 18).

5.5
Assessment of the Presence of M-like Cells in the In Vitro Model
The assessment of the presence of M-like cells was crucial to further evaluate whether the model had
been established. In order to obtain the successful in vitro M-like cell model some of the Caco-2 cells
should diﬀerentiated into M-like cells within the cell monolayer. The morphological characteristics
and the GP2 expression of the M-like cell model were investigated to assess the presence of M-like
cells. The two methods, SEM and CLSM, were used to examine the purpose.
5.5.1
Morphological Characterization of M-like Cells using SEM
The morphological characteristics of the M-like cells in the monolayer were investigated to conﬁrm
the presence of M-like cells since no human-speciﬁc M cell marker has yet been identiﬁed [40]. M-
like cells have instead been identiﬁed by their odd brush border membrane lacking microvilli on the
apical surface as opposed to the well-organized brush border membrane of enterocytes encompass-
ing numerous microvilli [88]. The morphological characterization of the mono- and co-cultures was
conducted via SEM (Figure 19).
Carrying out the SEM of the cell monolayers for both cultures was challenging due to diﬀerent
growth levels of cells. Accordingly, all of the cells could not be in focus at the same time which
can be sighted from the diﬀerent contrasts of the cells within an image. The brightest cells are
topmost whereas the darker cells are further down in the monolayer. Only by looking at the images,
it was diﬃcult to assess whether the dark zones were holes or cells presented further down in the
cell monolayer. Furthermore, the membranes containing the cell monolayers were dried before they
were observed by SEM. The membranes were crinkled during the drying process which also made
it challenging to have an even membrane when inspected them by SEM.

5.5.3
Identiﬁcation of M-like Cells by GP2 Expression
The expression of diﬀerent surface proteins is a good approach in the identiﬁcation of M-like cells
due to the lack of a human-speciﬁc M cell marker [40]. Very often the same surface proteins are

demonstrated by Hase et al. [48]. The study showed that the GP2 expression was localized intensely
on the M-cell apcial membrane, however it was also expressed in the subapical compartment (SAC),
most probably within endosomes.
The SAC plays a key role in endocytic pathways including
maintenance of distinct membrane domains.
The expression of GP2 within the SAC could be
argued by the receptor’s function as an endocytic receptor [48, 118].

Transport Studies with the Nanoparticles FluoSpheres
The most signiﬁcant property of the M cells is their high transcytotic capacity. The M cells have
the ability to transport a broad range of molecules, including particles, bacteria, and vira through
the epithelial layer [31]. Therefore, the transport of nanoparticles through the co-culture was ex-
amined in order to evaluate the conversion of Caco-2 cells into M-like cells in the in vitro model.
The 0.2 µm carboxylated polystyrene nanoparticles FluoSpheres were used in all of the transport
experiments. 100 µL was sampled from the basolateral compartment for each time point and it
has been estimated in the calculation for the number of particles transported in the subsequent
time point. The ﬂux through the cell monolayers of the cultures could not be calculated due to the
molecular weight of the nanoparticles was unknown. Therefore, the transport of the nanoparticles
was evaluated from the number of transported nanoparticles over time.
The cell viability of the cultures after each completed transport experiment was evaluated by a
quantitative colorimetric assay using MTT as reagent. The assessment of the cell viability was
important to show that the transport experiments were conducted on healthy cell monolayers.
The MTT reagent is a colorless substrate that is converted into the purple colored product
formazan by living metabolically active cells and not by dead cells. There exists several diﬀerent
assays which measure the cell viability, however the MTT cell viability assay has some remarkable
advantages. The MTT reagent is colorless and does not interfere with the colored product formazan
when measuring the absorbance. The assay does not contain any washing steps which minimize
the variability between the samples. Additionally, it makes the assay easier and faster to conduct.
Moreover, the assay can be used on a large range of cell lines which is preferred especially in the
work of co-cultures [96].
The transport rate of the nanoparticles FluoSpheres was examined through Matrigel coated inserts
without cells to be able to compare with the transport rate through cell monolayers. It was expected
that the FluoSpheres would have a high transport rate at the beginning of the experiment due to the
pores of the membrane. The pore size of 3 µm in the membrane of the inserts allows free passage of
the 0.2 µm nanoparticles. The number of transported nanoparticles through coated inserts without
cells at 37◦C for 180 minutes is shown in Figure 25.
The transport rate of the nanoparticles was observed to be highest in the beginning of the
experiment and about 5 x 108 nanoparticles were transported after only 30 minutes. Subsequent,
the transport rate was declined and about 5 x 107 particles were transported after 180 minutes.
This observation was not in accordance with the expectation of a high transport during all of the
time of the experiment.

The transport of nanoparticles by M cells is known to be mainly mediated by endocytosis, which is
an energy-dependent mechanism [36, 53]. The endocytic mechanism will be inhibited by lowering
the temperature to 4◦C because it is energy-dependent. The transport rate at 4◦C was evaluated
to verify that the nanoparticles were transported by endocytosis through the M-like cells present in
the in vitro cell model (Figure 27A).

5.7.2
Inhibition of Actin-Dependent Endocytosis by Cytochalasin D
Cytochalasin D is known as an inhibitor of the actin polymerization. It functions by blocking the
actin-dependent uptake mechanisms on the apical surface of epithelial cells. As described previously
in section 1.5.2, the actin cytoskeleton appears to play a vital role in the apical endocytic processes.
The inhibitor cytochalasin D can be used to study processes requiring polymerization of the actin
ﬁlaments, such as phagocytosis, macropinocytosis and receptor-mediated endocytosis [124].
The hypothesis of this experiment would be that even though M-like cells are present in the co-
culture, no transport will be observed due to that the endocytotic process used by the M-like cells is
actin-dependent and will therefore be inhibited. Correspondingly, the nanoparticle transportation
would be fully inhibited for the mono-culture. The transport of the nanoparticles was evaluated
at 37◦C on cultures pretreated with cytochalasin D to assess if the transport was mediated by an
actin-dependent endocytic process (Figure 28A).
The experiment showed that the transport of the nanoparticles in presence of cytochalasin D in
the co-culture was not inhibited. However, the transport through the mono-culture was totally
inhibited by cytochalasin D which substantiates that the inhibitor works. The inhibited transport of
the mono-culture indicated that the nanoparticles were not able to pass through the cell monolayer
if the actin-dependent endocytic mechanism was inhibited. In addition, it conﬁrms a functional
cell monolayer of the mono-culture and that the nanoparticles were not transported through the
paracellular pathway. Therefore, it could be deduced that the increased level of transport through
the co-culture was not caused by either endocytosis or through the paracellular pathway.

because no more time in the laboratory was left. Small aspects in the use of SU-8 microcontainers
as oral delivery system of vaccines was assessed in continuation of the evaluation of the in vitro
M-like cell model for oral vaccine delivery.
5.9

The application of microcontainers for oral vaccine delivery has shown great potential for targeted
and sustained delivery in the GI tract. The concept of using these microfabricated devices for oral
delivery is that the fragile antigen can be loaded into the cavity of the microcontainers. The antigen
can thereby obtain protection from the harsh environment of the GI tract until it will be released
in the small intestine near the FAE to increase the uptake by the M cells [67].

5.10
Factors Inﬂuencing the Development of the M-like Cell Model
A lot of diﬀerent small factors could have inﬂuence the development of the in vitro M-like cell model
and thereby have disturbed the conversion of Caco-2 cells into M-like cells. The ﬁrst observation
was a problem with the coating of the inserts before seeding the Caco-2 cells. The problem was
observed two times during the laboratory work where the medium just go through the membrane
and into the basolateral compartment when the Caco-2 cells were seeded (step 1 in the model). It
was thought to be a consequence of a deﬁcient coated membrane. The Matrigel coating is essential
to ensure adhesion of the Caco-2 cells to the membrane [86].
Villadaliu et al. [128] have evaluated the growth of Caco-2 cells for 21 days on unmodiﬁed and
modiﬁed inserts, respectively. The substrates from the membrane coating were demonstrated to
have a big inﬂuence on the epithelial cell behaviour. The unmodiﬁed inserts had limited areas of
Caco-2 cell growth whereas the modiﬁed insert showed a conﬂuent monolayer of Caco-2 cells. SEM
and CLSM revealed that the morphological characteristics of the Caco-2 cells also were diﬀerent.
The Caco-2 cells cultivated on unmodiﬁed inserts showed a larger abundance of microvilli [128].
These results suggested the great importance of coating the membrane of the inserts to ensure a
conﬂuent monolayer of Caco-2 cells forming intact tight junctions. It also showed that the coating
is essential to ensure adhesion of the Caco-2 cells to the membrane. This is of great important in
the construction of the in vito M-like cell model since the inserts are inverted after 5 days and this
step is known to be very stressful to the cells.
In the protocol [86] for the in vitro M-like cell model, it was stated that the coating should be
adsorbed for 1 hour at room temperature. However, the manufacturer of the Matrigel recommended
that the inserts were incubated for at least one hour or overnight at 37◦C to improve cell attachment.
This could be the reason why the coating failed twice.
Furthermore, the Matrigel concentration of 100 µg/mL was used in the construction of the
in vitro M-like cell model which were in accordance with the protocol [86].
The concentration
was low in comparison with other studies that also use Matrigel. Other studies examined Caco-2
monolayers on Matrigel coated Transwells for diﬀerent purposes used Matrigel concentrations in the
range of 300-500 µg/mL [122, 129]. However, a study examined the Caco-2/Raji B co-culture used
a concentration of 3.33 µg/mL and they do not stated any problems by using this concentration
[87]. However, the manufacturer Corning recommended that the Matrigel should not be diluted to
a ﬁnal concentration below 300 µg/mL in order to prevent incomplete gel formation [130]. In regard
to this, it could suggest that the Matrigel coating has been too low in the construction of the in
vitro M-like cell model.

Another observation during the construction of the in vitro M-like cell model was that the basolat-
eral medium in the silicon tube was observed to be lower for the co-cultures than the mono-cultures
after the Raji B seeding on day 19. Some of the medium for the co-cultures had a tendency to
go through the insert. A suggestion could be that it was due to a decrease in the cell viability. It
was based on the observations from cultures with extremely low cell viability where the basolateral
medium run through within few hours. This could also indicates holes in the cell monolayer of the
co-culture which have been identiﬁed by microscopy.
The seeding density of the Caco-2 cells was also crucial in order to reach a conﬂuent monolayer before
the Raji B cells were seeded. The seeding density used in this model was 5 x 105 Caco-2 cells. From
the seeding density, it could be calculated that a conﬂuent monolayer will be established after 6
days of cultivation. This means that an almost conﬂuent monolayer was reached before inverting
the model. Then the monolayer was cultivated for 9 days before the Raji B cells were added. The
diﬀerentiation of the Caco-2 cells occurs 14-21 days after a conﬂuent cell layer has been reached
[80]. Therefore, the days between the steps in the construction of the in vitro M-like cell model are
crucial in order to schedule when the Caco-2 cell monolayer is fully diﬀerentiated. It could suggests
that the cell monolayer must be cultivated for further days before adding the Raji B cells.
However, the seeding density of the Caco-2 cells should not be too high either in order to avoid
that the Caco-2 cells grow on top of each other and thereby forming a multilayer. The in vitro
M-like cell model aims to obtain a monolayer to ensure close contact to the Raji B cells seeded on
the basolateral side of the membrane. This could possibly be the reason why very thick cell layers
were observed for both the mono- and co-cultures. If the seeding density has been too high, the
Caco-2 cells will grow in a thick multilayer which do not allow close contact with the Raji B cells
and thereby prevent the M cell diﬀerentiation to take place.
Furthermore, the ratio of the Caco-2 and Raji B cells should be taken into consideration as it is
essential to obtain a successful M cell diﬀerentiation. If the Caco-2 cell monolayer is too thick in
proportion to the amount of Raji B cells seeded into the basolateral comportant, it could be diﬃcult
to allow the contact between the two cell types and thereby aﬀecting the M cells diﬀerentiation.
The localization of the Raji B cells has been reported to be on the basolateral surface and in the
bottom of the cell monolayer towards the membrane [36]. This suggests that the Raji B cells are
able to migrate into the Caco-2 cell monolayer, however they are only found near the membrane. If
the cell monolayer is very thick, it could limit the diﬀerentiation of Caco-2 cells in the upper part
of the cell monolayer due to the lack of contact with the Raji B cells.

Conclusion
The purpose of this master project was to develop a human in vitro M-like cell model from a Caco-
2/Raji B co-culture. Several experiments have been conducted in order to characterize and evaluate
the model. Furthermore, the project aimed to evaluate the cell model for oral vaccine delivery. For
this, the uptake of the model antigen ovalbumin was examined and an assessment of the SU-8 mi-
crocontainers’ cytotoxic eﬀect on the model was made.
The cell monolayer integrity was evaluated by TEER and studied by the paracellular permeability
to LY. The results indicated that the model had a decreased integrity compared to the control which
could be an indication of the conversion of Caco-2 cells into M-like cells. The morphological char-
acterization of the model was conducted by SEM and CLSM in order to identify M-like cells. The
SEM analysis revealed a thick cell monolayer containing several holes. However, it was not possible
to identify the M-like cells from their odd brush border membrane lacking microvilli. Furthermore,
some unidentiﬁable cells with a diameter of 20-25 µm were observed by the CLSM analysis, but it
could not be deduced whether the unidentiﬁable cells were either clumps of Raji B cells or M-like
cells.
Other characteristics of M cells were also investigated to identify M-like cells in the model. It had
been described that M cells had an increased expression of the GP2 receptor in order to recognize
certain Gram-negative bacteria. Neither an upregulated GP2 expression nor the identiﬁcation of
M-like cells by GP2-expression could be demonstrated from the CLSM analysis. A decreased APA
on the apical membrane was another characteristic reported for the presence of M-like cells in the
co-culture. However, no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the activity of the co-culture in comparison to the
control could be demonstrated.
The transport rate of the nanoparticles FluoSpheres through the model was investigated in order
to demonstrate the transcytotic capacity of the M-like cells. At ﬁrst, a highly increased level of
transported nanoparticles was observed for the model. However, it could not be shown that the
transport was mediated by the energy-, and actin-dependent mechanism, endocytosis. The increased
level of nanopaticles transportation through the model was later deduced to be caused by holes in
the cell monolayer. In conclusion, the presence of M-like cells in the in vitro M-like cell model could
not be ﬁnally conﬁrmed from the diﬀerent characterization experiments.

Additionally, the human in vitro M-like cell model was evaluated, as a system for oral vaccine
delivery screening. SU-8 microcontainers have shown great potential for targeted delivery in the GI
tract, which also can be applied for oral vaccine delivery. Therefore, it was of great importance to
investigate which impact the SU-8 microcontainers would have on the cell viability of the model.
The SU-8 microcontainers showed no evidence for having cytotoxic eﬀects on the cell monolayer
which meant that they potentially could be used as oral delivery system without damaging the
intestinal epithelium.
Afterwards, the uptake of the antigen OVA through the model was measured and an increased
uptake was observed for the model. However, the experiment was deduced to be not representative
due to a signiﬁcantly low cell viability for both the model and the control. This concludes that
the in vitro M-like cell model established in this project could not be used to evaluate oral vaccine
delivery since the model was unsuccessfully developed.

Perspectives
The diﬀerentiation of Caco-2 cells into M-like cells was the demand for developing an applicable in
vitro M-like cell model, however this could not be conﬁrmed for the model developed in this project.
Consequently, there has been an identiﬁcation and discussion of several factors which could have
had an inﬂuence on why the development of the model was not successful. It could be interesting
to improve a number of them or to further investigate how they aﬀect the development of the model.
Optimization of the concentration of Matrigel should be investigated to ensure the best adhesion
of the Caco-2 cells to the membrane of the insert. In addition, diﬀerent concentrations of Matrigel
could be used to observe which eﬀects they have for the cell viability of the monolayers when they
are inverted. During the construction of the model, it was observed that the seeding step of Raji
B cells on day 14 was stressful for the Caco-2 cells. Therefore, it is important to ensure the best
possible adhesion of the Caco-2 cells to the membrane before the inversion occurs. This could pos-
sibly prevent that the Caco-2 cells are released from the membrane in the inverted orientation when
they are stressed. Accordingly, it could be useful to know whether it could prevent the formation
of holes in the cell monolayer of the in vitro M-like cell model.
Other improvements in the construction of the model could be to examine the ratio of the Caco-2
and Raji B cells’ seeding densities in the model. The ratio of the cell lines is crucial to ensure the
diﬀerentiation of Caco-2 cells into M-like cells. However, if the presence of Caco-2 cells is too high
compared to the Raji B cells, it could limit the diﬀerentiation. A too thick Caco-2 cell layer was
stated in this project to be one of the limitations of why the model was not developed correctly.
Diﬀerent seeding densities of the two cell lines could therefore be useful to investigate in order to
establish a functional M-like cell model.
Furthermore, a suggestion to improve the cell viability of the Raji B cells could be to seed them
in RPMI+ medium in the construction of the model instead of DMEM+ medium. This project
showed that the Raji B cells are able to grow in DMEM+ medium, but they did not behavior as
they do in RPMI+ medium. It could be of interest to examine how the change of the basolateral
medium to RPMI+ medium would aﬀect the M cell diﬀerentiation in the model.

The well-establish in vitro M-like cell model can be used to study the uptake of diﬀerent materials,
including particles, antigens, and microorganisms through the human M-like cells. The model can
be used to screen how orally administrated drugs or vaccine formulations are taken up by the M
cells. This project focused on how the model could be used to evaluate SU-8 microcontainers as
oral vaccine delivery system. The well-established model will be useful to evaluate the uptake of the
antigen through the M-like cells and thereby whether the antigen will have possibility of entering
the PPs to elicit an immune response. However, the model could also be used to study other oral
delivery systems.
Moreover, the mechanisms used by diﬀerent pathogenic microorganisms to invade the mucosal
surface can be investigated by this model. This knowledge will be useful in order to prevent the
invasion of the pathogenic microorganisms. Furthermore, the model could be applied in the screening
of speciﬁc surfaces markers on the M-like cells. These are of great importance in oral delivery systems
to active targeting the M cells and thereby enhance the permeation [18]. The identiﬁcation of M
cell targets could be useful in oral delivery of both drugs and vaccines.
