Abstract
This research introduces a novel approach to multiôÙcamera extrinsic calibraôÙ
tion leveraging the human pose as a calibration object, comparing the results
with a ChArUco board calibration. OpenPose is employed for human pose estiôÙ
mation. The study evaluates the accuracy and reliability of keypoints detected
by OpenPose, revealing disparities among them. Further analysis of the perôÙ
formance of cv2.recoverPose function, shows its behaviours to controlled noise
and outliers. While the traditional ChArUco method performs more accurately,
the human pose based calibration offers an interesting alternative, particularly
beneficial when flexibility is required. An automatic reprojection error based
recalibration method is also introduced. The proposed method presents sevôÙ
eral limitations, indicating potential directions for future developments. These
cover extrinsic parameter optimization, camera synchronization and scaling
methods. Despite the challenges, the calibration method highlights a promisôÙ
ing area for camera calibration that deserves more investigation.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Computer vision technology is a constantly developing field of study, especially
in recent years, and has paved the way for the development and deployment of
multiôÙcamera systems in a wide range of applications such as robotics, surveilôÙ
lance, sports analytics, virtual reality, and many others. [3, 77, 9, 80, 63]
A highly important step in the deployment of a multiôÙcamera system is the exôÙ
trinsic calibration. This step involves estimating the relative position and orienôÙ
tation of the cameras with respect to a common coordinate system. Achieving
high accuracy is crucial as it enables tasks like autonomous robot navigation
and object tracking.[22]
Traditionally, extrinsic calibration is performed using a calibration board with
known measurements and patterns, such as chessboards.[20, 92] These methôÙ
ods can yield the accuracy required but can be cumbersome due to the need of
a physical board and may not be appropriate for all use cases, for example unôÙ
controlled and dynamic environments that may require periodic recalibration.
To remove these issues from the process, there has been a growing interest
in exploring natural featureôÙbased approaches for extrinsic calibration, which
rely on the features extracted from objects in the scene of cameras with overôÙ
lapping fields of view.[87]
1.2
Research Question and Objectives
This paper aims to assess the efficacy of using natural features, particularly the
human pose, to determine the optimal extrinsic paramaters that minimize the
reprojection error, in a multiôÙcamera system with overlapping fields of view.
The core research question is:
ãÂ How effective is the estimated human pose compared to calibration boards
in determining accurate extrinsic calibration parameters?
Furthermore, the paper seeks to identify which aspects of the human pose are
most reliable for this purpose. The research will focus on identifying which
approach results in a calibration that is both accurate and replicable. It will
identify and measure sources of error in the natural featureôÙbased approach
and explore mitigation strategies. Lastly, it will review the recent literature on
similar approaches to extrinsic calibration of multiôÙcamera systems, applying
relevant findings to the study.
1.3
Relevance
Considering the increasing trend of deploying multiôÙcamera systems for variôÙ
ous applications, this research has the potential to make a significant contriôÙ
bution to the field of extrinsic calibration through testing and quantifying the
accuracy of using the human pose for this purpose. This investigation can help
guide the development of more versatile calibration techniques using popular
openôÙsource tools.

All the information presented in this section is based on the paper [49], unless

2.2.1
Hardware setup
The hardware setup described in this paper consist of a synchronised binocular
camera setup along with a multiôÙview camera system with overlapping views.
The binocular setup consists of RGB cameras with 1280x720 resolution and
the multiôÙview system consists of four cameras.
2.2.2
Calibration method
The calibration method proposed in this paper center around using the 2D
keypoints of the human pose as calibration points. The data that was used
ãÂ 2D Pose Estimation. OpenPose is employed to extract the human pose
keypoints in images captured by the camera.
These keypoints will be
used to establish correspondences between the camera views.
ãÂ Extrinsic Parameter Optimization. The fundamental matrix is deterôÙ
mined using a set of epipolar geometric constraint equations formed by
the detected keypoints with high confidence score and RANSAC is used
for outlier handling. With the fundamental matrix, the essential matrix
can be calculated.
Four sets of rotation and translations are retrieved
upon matrix decomposition and the solution with the positive depth are
selected as the real extrinsic parameters.
ãÂ Intrinsic Parameter Optimization. The intrinsic parameters are first
set to factory default parameters. With the extrinsic parameters known,
the triangulation of the keypoints can be performed. The keypoints are
then reprojected back onto the image plane. The reprojection error is
used to create a loss function and then the intrinsic parameters are optiôÙ
mised using the least square method.
ãÂ Joint Optimization. This step aims to optimize both intrinsic and extrinôÙ
sic parameters simultaneously, aiming for a global optimal solution in an
iterative manner until the reprojection error passes a certain threshold.

3.1
Camera calibration
Camera calibration or camera resectioning is an essential process in computer
vision. It involves estimating the parameters of a cameraãs lens and image
sensor. Camera calibration is used in various applications such as 3D reconôÙ
struction, object tracking, augmented reality, image analysis, machine vision
for object detection and measurement, robotics for navigation systems and 3D
scene reconstruction. The main objective of camera calibration is to correct
lens distortion and to determine the realôÙworld dimensions of objects, while
establishing the exact position of a camera in a given scene. This calibration
process is essential because it allows the transformation from 3D world coordiôÙ
nates to 2D image coordinates. Camera calibration is therefore a prerequisite
for any application that requires an understanding of the relationship between
2D images and the 3D world.[54, 13]
Camera calibration has four categories of calibration objects:
ãÂ 3D Reference ObjectôÙBased Calibration: This calibration method is
based on the observation of a calibration object with precisely known 3D
geometry. This object has two or three planes positioned orthogonally to
one another. In some cases, when a plane undergoes a precisely known
translation can also serve as a 3D reference, providing reference points
in 3D space. [94]
ãÂ 2D PlaneôÙBased Calibration: This category requires the observation
of a planar pattern displayed at various orientations. Unlike some other
calibration techniques, this plane does not require any prior knowledge
of the planeãs motion. The advantage of this method is that it does not
require prior knowledge due to its simplicity, allowing individuals to create
and use these calibration models.[94]
ãÂ 1D LineôÙBased Calibration: The 1D lineôÙbased method is a calibration
approach characterised by the use of calibration objects composed of
points aligned on a single straight line. It is used in camera calibration,
where a practical application involves calibrating a camera by observing
the movement of a line as it rotates around a fixed point.[94]
ãÂ SelfôÙCalibration: In this method, calibration is achieved independently
of physical calibration objects.
It is often described as a 0D approach
because it relies on correspondences between points in images. When
a camera is moved in a static scene, the inherent rigidity of the scene
provides two constraints on the internal camera parameters, based on
changes in the image data. As a result, when multiple images are capôÙ
tured using the same camera with fixed internal parameters, it is imôÙ
portant to establish correspondences between three of these images to
recover internal and external parameters. This process allows the reconôÙ
struction of 3D structures up to a similarity transformation.[94]

3.1.1
Intrinsic calibration
Intrinsic calibration refers to the determination and adjustment of cameraôÙ
specific parameters such as focal length, radial distortion and optical center.
This allows the transformation of 3D cameraãs coordinates into precise 2D coôÙ
ordinates.[54]
ãÂ Focal length.
The focal length, often denoted in millimeters (mm),
serves as a fundamental attribute of a photographic lens. It is the optical
distance from the point where light rays converge to create a sharp image
of an object to the focal plane within the camera. This determination is
made when the lens is focused at infinity. In particular, the focal length
is distinct from the lensãs physical length.[64]
ãÂ Optical center. The optical center indicates the exact point where all
the light rays converge to form an image when the lens is simplified as a
single thin lens.[69]
ãÂ Pixel aspect ratio. Pixel aspect ratio (PAR) is the ratio of a pixelãs width
to its height.[16]
ãÂ Principal point. The principal point is the image point where the optical
axis intersects the plane. Usually, the lower left corner of the picture is
considered the origin for convenience.[33]
These parameters form the 3x3 camera calibration matrix K. Intrinsic calibraôÙ
tion involves estimating the internal camera parameters, which are specific to
the camera and lens. These parameters determine how a 3D point in the world
is projected onto the 2D image plane. The most common camera model used
in intrinsic calibration is:
ãÂ Pinhole. The pinhole camera model (fig. 3.1) is a simple mathematical
representation of a camera, where light enters through a single point
(ãpinholeã) and forms an inverted image on the image sensor or film
plane. For this type of model, the 3x3 camera matrix is used during the
intrinsic calibration process.[54]

3.1.2
Extrinsic calibration
Extrinsic calibration, on the other hand, consists of a 3x3 rotation matrix and
a 3x1 translation vector, and represents a rigid transformation from the 3D
world coordinate system to the 2D cameraãs coordinate system.[54, 78] ExtrinôÙ
sic calibration in multiôÙcamera systems is important for applications like robotic
navigation and augmented reality. It ensures accurate alignment of cameras,
enabling precise scene reconstruction, depth perception, and seamless inteôÙ
gration of virtual objects. Proper calibration optimizes resources, making it
essential for costôÙeffective and efficient spatially aware technologies.[49] In
the context of multiôÙcamera systems, understanding the spatial relationships
and orientations within each camera setup is essential. This knowledge is enôÙ
coded in coordinate systems, such as the camera coordinate system. This has
an important role in the precise calibration of individual cameras and their colôÙ
laborative integration within multiôÙcamera environments. Camera calibration
is based on three linked coordinate systems that allow precise mapping of 3D

Extrinsic Recalibration
In a dynamic environment, multiôÙcamera systems are under the risk of beôÙ

useless. This can lead to a cascade of errors depending on what purpose the
camera system is deployed for.
In this section a recalibration technique is

human pose based extrinsic calibration section.
The recalibration of the system is designed in a way that leverages the reproôÙ

case, in a real world scenario, it should be assumed that the cameras operate
synchronously to facilitate an automatic data collection method and ensure
correspondences with other cameras in the system.

unfolds as so:
ãÂ Initial extrinsic calibration.
When the camera system is setup, an

ãÂ Image capture. Periodically, new images should be collected from all
cameras.
ãÂ 2D Keypoints extraction. OpenPose can be used on these images to
extract keypoints from people present in the camera view.
ãÂ Triangulation. Leveraging the initial calibration data, these new 2D keyôÙ

to the camera.

6.1
Overview of results
In order to understand the capabilities of OpenPose for the purpose of extrinsic
camera calibration, the paper conducted a series of experiments.
Each of
these experiments shed some light on the various components that make up
calibration pipeline.
The evaluation of OpenPose on its ablity to estimate the human pose has had
immense importance on the study. The model has had its performance tested
on the COCO validation set from 2017 and it became clear that OpenPose is
a powerful tool, but it also has nuances. The keypoints associated with the
head have performed the best, while others, like the hips, have lagged behind
in detection accuracy. The inconsistency of OpenPoseãs performance across
the 17 keypoint skeleton sets the stage for the challenges that were faced in
calibration. When high accuracy is necessary for a system to be usable in most
use cases, the detection accuracy of the keypoints becomes crucial. And so,
the varying accuracy of detection directly influences the calibration results.
The study explored the robustness of the cv2.recoverPose function. While it
has proven relatively resilient against noise and outliers, it is not without flaws.
When faced with noise there have been occasional spikes in error and the
function also expressed sensitivity to outliers to a degree where even one
outlier can skew the results drastically.
This performance underscores the
importance of high quality data.
As even the most robust algorithms can
degrade if the data fed into them is peppered with imperfections.
The peak of this research was defined by the investigation into the foreign
territory of human pose based extrinsic calibration. Replacing the traditional
calibration objects with naturalôÙfeatures and even the human pose is an inôÙ
creasing trend in the industry. The results of this research, while promising,
have shown potential rather than perfection. When compared to such popular
methods like the ChArUco board, the results spoke their truth. While the huôÙ
man pose based extrinsic calibration is more innovative, it currently stands in
the shadow of more traditional approaches in terms of accuracy.
Looking back on the hypothesis, the research journey clearly shows that huôÙ
man poses can be used in camera calibration.
The goal of surpassing the
traditional methods in accuracy is not reached yet. This is due to many facôÙ
tors, from the quality of keypoint detection, the dynamic nature of the human
pose, matching and cleaning data, desynchronised cameras.
Each of these
has the potential to make or break the results.
6.2
Significance to industry
In an age where cameras and multiôÙcamera systems are present in most places
that humans can reach, from surveillance systems, robotics, AR and VR, the
need for fast and accurate calibration method is only growing.
Traditional
methods are effective but often demand specialized equipment or setups, such

6.3
Limitations
Every study has its constraints, an over the course of this investigation, sevôÙ
eral limitations have emerges that hinder the performance of the calibration
algorithm.
ãÂ Dynamic Nature of Human Poses. Humans are hardly rigid structures.
During the data collection, even if small movements occur, they can and
will introduce discrepancies in the correspondences. This factor leads to
challenges in achieving consistent and accurate calibrations.
ãÂ Camera desynchronization. In multiôÙcamera setups, the synchronizaôÙ
tion of cameras is of the utmost importance as it was also observed in the
literature. The lack of it can lead to significant errors, especially combined
with human dynamism. To mitigate this factor, during data collection the
author kept poses for a few seconds, striving for perfect matches, but
even so some discrepancies were observable.
ãÂ Sensitivity to noise and outliers. RealôÙworld scenarios can cause data
to be littered with noise and outliers. While cv2.recoverPose is equipped
to deal with noise and outliers, it is still prone to exhibiting notable senôÙ
sitivity.
ãÂ Keypoint Accuracy. The accuracy of keypoints varies across body parts
as was shown in the paper. These detection errors directly influence the
outcome of the calibration.
ãÂ Environmental factors. Factors such as occlusions, lighting conditions,
backgrounds that make the subject blend in can all have an impact on
the image quality which bleeds into the pose estimation accuracy.
ãÂ Limited Dataset Variety. Testing OpenPose on the COCO dataset, while
comprehensive, does not cover all the possible scenarios, poses, backôÙ
ground and occlusions. The comparison is also performed only on the 17
common body keypoints between COCO and OpenPose, so leaves out the
other 8 keypoints used in the calibration from the analysis.
ãÂ Computatinal demands.
RealôÙtime processing of OpenPose over all
the 135 keypoints can become very expensive to run, especially for vast

ãÂ Hardware limitations. The cameras used in the study are themselves

6.4
Future developments
The road forward splits into many different paths, each with their own opporôÙ
tunities and challenges:
ãÂ Bundle Adjustment. A stage of refinement is one of the most promisôÙ
ing ventures that can be undertaken to increase the calibration accuracy.
This technique can optimize the camera parameters and 3D points simulôÙ
taneously.
ãÂ Better scaling method. At the moment, the scaling is performed using
the manually measured distance between the cameras. This is a cumôÙ
bersome approach the requires human intervention. A more automated
way of grounding the system in reality must be implemented. One potenôÙ
tial solution to this could be using the average anatomical human body
proportions of people based on an initial height measurement.
ãÂ Improved Pose Estimator. OpenPose is just one among many, and it
is not the best.
There are plenty of other human pose estimators deôÙ
signed for different scenarios, with varying accuracy. If data continues
to be single person, there may be other pose estimator that specialize on
solving this problem.
ãÂ Camera Synchronization Techniques. The synchronization is a vital
part of data collection. This improvement would greatly boost the data
collection and the volume of images or videos that can be extracted and
matched successfully.
This would also make the multiôÙcamera system
more versatile in case of reconfiguration.
ãÂ RealôÙtime calibration refinement. To increase the adaptability of the
system to changes in the environment, techniques that recalibrate on live
data continuously could be implemented.
To summarize, while the results of the study highlight the potential of human
pose based calibration, there are plenty of features that could be implemented,
which will increase the extrinsic estimation accuracy.

7
Conclusion
The research explored a novel approach to multiôÙcamera extrinsic calibration
and recalibration using natural features. The basis of this method revolved
around leveraging the human pose as a dynamic calibration object, aiming to
provide a flexible and accessible solution to the camera calibration problem.
The primary objective of the paper was to assess the feasibility and accuracy of
the proposed method in comparison to the traditional ChArUco board camera
calibration, which served as the ground truth. The results have revealed that
the human pose calibration method has its limitations in terms of accuracy, in
its current implementation. The calibration board consistently outperformed
the proposed method, highlighting its reliability and precision.
An error analysis revealed a number of factors influencing the results. OpenôÙ
Pose, the human pose estimator used in this paper, has shown relatively high
accuracy in many instances. The detection accuracy of the keypoints assoôÙ
ciated with the head proved superior in comparison to the other body parts,
especially the hips. The noise and outliers experiments further demonstrated
robustness to a certain degree and the challenges of the cv2.recoverPose funcôÙ
tion, highlighting the intricate relationship between data quality and calibration
accuracy.
Even if the proposed method has shown potential, it is evident that it is not
yet comparable with stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart extrinsic calibration techniques in terms
of accuracy. However, the dynamic and accessible nature of the human pose
is an advantage, especially for applications that can tolerate some calibration
imprecision.
The study has made clear several limitations and areas for future developôÙ
ment. From using optimization algorithms to improve the calibration results,
synchronising the cameras, using a better scaling method and testing more
specialised pose estimators. There are plenty of solid paths this research could
take.
In summary, while the human pose based calibration method has its conôÙ
straints, it also introduces a fresh perspective in the camera calibration doôÙ
main, suggesting various possibilities of future research.
