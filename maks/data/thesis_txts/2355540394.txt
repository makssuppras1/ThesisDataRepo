Abstract
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate the impact of a stochastic wind load on the
99 line topology optimization Matlab code TopOpt written at DTU in 2001. The deﬁned
shape and boundary conditions depend on a two dimensional half circle which corresponds
to an Arctic research station.
A model was built with a radius of 7.5cm and a width of 10cm. The solid was digitally
modelled in the Software Rhino and printed with a Zortrax 3D printer. The model was
equipped with 5 measure rows along the vaulted surface with each 29 pressure tabs.
The wind pressure was measured with the DPMS 3102 device from FORCE Technology
including a R-Tac simulation.
Pressures have been measured corresponding to wind
speeds of 2, 5, 10, 15, 20 and 25 m/s. Additionally, a ﬂow visualisation test was carried
out using a fog machine and laser light in the wind tunnel.
The measured pressure distributions have been statistically prepared in order to apply
them on the topology optimisation algorithm. First the pressure was applied for each
measured time step, i.e.
750 time steps per second.
Therefore, clear layout changes
are visible. However, in order to satisfy the inertia conditions of the structure a mean
distribution over three seconds was applied. Then the topology optimisation algorithm
acted relatively static on the lower variability of the pressure mean.
Finally a interpretation of the Topology Optimised material distribution was done. A
ﬁnal structure was found.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Since the beginning of mankind, humans have been thinking about the way they construct
their habitats and other places as shelters, work places, social sites or similar. The ﬁrst
civilisations constructed their dwellings based on try and error method to ﬁnd applicable
materials, shapes and connections. This method got more and more replaced by smart
solutions built on the experienced errors. Like anything else in nature, the ﬁnding of
solutions is a evolutionary process depending on the building sight, temperature, utility
and many more. However, if the utilities, load or other circumstances change to a already
completed evolutionary design, it takes generations to ﬁnd new solutions.
Natural structural information, saved in a DNA strip is similar, such as the structure of
a tree or a bone. It took generations to develop the optimum shape for its function. Is
the utility or the duration suddenly changing, e.g. a higher life expectancy, the process
takes time to adapt those changes. Until changes are adapted, the shift towards a higher
life expectancy would mean for a bone, that the structure will fail easily in the ages the
component was not designed for.
Over time humans tried to develop procedures to predict structural shapes and material
use for one speciﬁc kind. The try and error method was then moved to the theoretical
world. Thickness and material type could be predicted for the designed shape. Since
the technological age, this can be done quicker and more and more advanced tools are
developed in order to calculate structures. A optimising process, as used in this thesis,
makes it possible to ﬁnd relatively economical structures [2].
The aim is to simulate the in nature appearing evolutionary and optimising process be-
fore the structure is produced. The automatised topology optimization of the structures
due to the appearing deﬂections and stresses are in the main focus of this work.
Topology optimisation describes a function principle which gives a material distribution
over a deﬁned area, i.e. the area where the material of the program can be freely dis-
tributed. The included iteration process optimises the layout for each step. The outcome
can support the ﬁnding of a design. Issues appear as well, that e.g. the proposed de-
sign of the topology optimisation is not applicable for conservative kinds of constructing.
Thus, an interpretation of the topology optimisation has to be found which respects also
common ways of structural design.
In 2001 Ole Sigmund and Martin Philip Bendsøe developed and described within the
Page 3
==PAGE:26==
book ’Topology Optimization - Theory, Method and Application’, a Matlab optimization
algorithm which uses Finite Element Analysis in order to do the above described, i.e.
optimisation of a material layout. The code has been used in earlier works to ﬁnd a
design proposal. However, the objective of this thesis will be to investigate a over time
stochastic distributed wind load acting on the structure and how the material distribu-
tion in the deﬁned area will react on that. Diﬀerent wind speeds will be investigated and
thus varying Reynolds numbers.
1.1
Objectives
This thesis will treat the variabilities of a topology optimisation shape loaded with a
stochastic distributed wind load. Thus, a wind load ﬁrst has to be found. A scaled ver-
sion of the wind pressure distribution will be measured during a test execution in a wind
tunnel. The model shape will be inspired by a Arctic research station in Greenland. The
structures have to serve the purpose of being very light weight and resist very high wind
speeds of up to 49m/s [25].
The scaled version of the wind ﬂow and its pressure distribution outcome will have to be
statistically and analytically prepared in order to make them useful for the application
on the topology optimisation. The load will then be implemented in diﬀerent ways in the
algorithm.
Further investigations will show how the dynamic loading will act on the structure.
Boundary conditions such as restrains and supports will be changed and investigated
in manner of the load type. The ﬁnal objective of this thesis will be the interpretation
of the material layout. Hence, the optimisation will be transformed into a practicable
structure and the respective forces in the components will be calculated.
1.2
Structure
This work will be divided into ﬁve parts. The ﬁrst and as well introducing part will
describe the researched area and the boundary conditions of the topic. In the second
and third part of the thesis a load ﬁnding process in form of wind tunnel testing and
data assessment will be carried out. Following, the topology optimising algorithm will be
applied in the manner of the before found support and boundary conditions. Last and
ﬁfth part will treat an interpretation of the outcome of the optimisation layouts.
The structure may vary from the common structure of a thesis. However, the results are
still presented towards the second half of the work. Simply the experimental executions
and the theoretical background is blended to give the reader a better understanding.

==PAGE:27==
1.3
Limitations, Assumptions and Understanding of
this Work
In order to counteract a misunderstanding of this work the following paragraphs will
explain the authors own understanding of it and explain assumptions made throughout
the work, followed by the research content.
The broad ﬁeld of aero dynamics included in the wind tunnel testing and its outcome
and the ﬁeld of topology optimisation is diﬃcult to weight. The aim of researching about
the variability of the optimisation outcome due to the stochastic wind load needs to treat
both, the understanding of the occurring wind ﬂuctuation and the optimisation process.
The treated topics of this report will be designed to satisfy the claims of understanding
the ﬁnal results of the algorithm. Hence, the main aim of this report will not be to
develop a optimum dimensioned structure but rather to investigate the topology opti-
misation outcome due to the wind load and other changing boundary conditions. Thus,
also no self weight will be taken into account in calculations at any point.
1.3.1
Assumptions
Especially in order to use the topology optimisation algorithm, some assumptions have to
be made, to simplify results and limit the thesis to a reasonable volume. In the following,
made assumptions are explained.
Non Linearity
The FE analysis of the TopOpt algorithm does not include in this work any form of non
linear calculations. The outcome is thus also not dimensioned for large scale deﬂections
and buckling behaviour.
Isotropic Materials
The material used is in all cases an isotropic one. The added material properties are the
Young’s modulus and Poison’s ratio, mostly used in a uniﬁed manner. A stress criteria
has not been included in the algorithm.
Density Matrix
The mean density per element of the density matrix was used in order to compare diﬀerent
outcomes. Since the stresses can be vary in the deﬁned material areas, the comparison
can be not understood as a transferable one to a real structure. However, the optimisation
algorithm tries to even out stress diﬀerences and has the attempt to create a uniformly
distributed stress ﬁeld. Thus, the approach was made in such a manner that the material
Page 5
==PAGE:28==
density matrix can be used with restrictions for the comparison of diﬀerent cases in a
quantitative manner but not in a qualitative.
Dynamic Structural Behaviour
Even though it will be investigated how the stochastically distributed wind load is acting
on the topology optimisation algorithm, the structural outcome will not be investigated
on its dynamical behaviour. The approach and therefore the assumption is here that
the outcome is not dominantly inﬂuenced by a dynamical behaviour. This assumption
was made in order to narrow the ﬁeld of this work but should be considered in terms of
further research especially for light weight structures.

Chapter 2
Concept and Application
Arctic research stations, e.g. in Greenland are erected in an extreme environment. This
implies, that the construction itself has to fulﬁl special requirements to be suitable for
those locations. The structure has to be produced in high strength and stiﬀness. Due to
the extreme wind and snow load, the buildings have to resist low temperatures and the
structure needs to be light on weight due to logistic reasons. Figure 2.0.1 shows one of
those shelters as a housing version.
(a) Housing shelter of the company Alaska
structures [4]
(b) Research station ’Dome Concordia’ in for
the European Project for Ice Coring (EPICA)
[5]
Figure 2.0.1: Shelter for the Arctic environment
The shapes of these research station is very distinct. It can be described as a two dimen-
sional planar half circular shape which is extruded in a straight, perpendicular direction
towards the described planar. The length therefore can be varied with the extrusion, and
the width and height can be inﬂuenced by the radius of the two dimensional half circle.
The dimensioning of the carrying structures due to the wind load can be approached in
terms of a wind ﬂow towards the front face of the shelter. In Figure 2.0.1 would that
be the entrance side. The frame construction will therefore be stiﬀened. When the wind
direction changes and the wind load comes from the transverse direction, the length of
the building can be simpliﬁed in terms of a inﬂuence length. Then, the length of the
Page 7
==PAGE:30==
building itself does not need to be taken into account in order to dimension one of the
frames.
The way those constructions are built is visible in some extend in Figure 2.0.1a. The load
from the before mentioned inﬂuence length is for each separate part carried by a frame.
This frame is again described by the two dimensional planar half circular shape.
One of those frames will be the objective of this work. The inﬂuence length for each
frame is roughly assumed by the ratio of inﬂuence length to radius of 2
3. Such an inﬂu-
ence length of 6.67m would add up in terms of a 10m high building.
The light weight of such a structure has especially for the Arctic environment relevance.
Material is rare in the most snow and ice covered landscape. Such, the material needs
to be shipped, driven, carried or ﬂown over. Material in terms of weight is so with a big
factor of the ﬁnancial part of the research projects.
2.1
Model
In order to simulate the wind loads on the structure a model which reﬂects the boundary
conditions of the structure in reality in terms of its impact on the wind ﬂow has to be
fabricated. The fabrication of the model has to take place in an exact scale ratio to the
original situation. Thereby, the pressure measuring taps show an applicable allocation of
magnitudes to transfer to a full scale load.

Chapter 3
Wind Tunnel Experiments
The aim is to investigate the stochastic loading caused by wind. The given turbulences
and so with varying pressures acting on a full scale building can be simulated in smaller
scale in a wind tunnel and the structure can be built in form of the described scale model.
The pressure on the surface can then be measured with the here used Dynamic Pressure
Measurement System (DPMS) which is linked with steel pipes and elastic tubes to each
tap in which the wind pressure can be measured on the models surface. This set-up
makes it possible to record in a set frequency over the respective sampling time.
3.1
Wind Tunnel and Experimental Set-up
A Closed Circuit Wind Tunnel (CCWT) was used to carry out the experiments. A wind
ﬂow is produced with a fan which circulates inside of the closed built loop. With the
aid of guide vanes, the ﬂow direction can be changed without interfering the air ﬂow
distribution. Air is transported due to suction at the back of the fan and pressure at
the obverse side. It can be concluded that a close to laminar wind ﬂow is produced. To
generate a realistic wind ﬂow with a turbulent boundary layer, the laminar movement
needs to be distorted. This can be done with a boundary layer generator.

3.1.1
Circuit
Figure 3.1.1: Wind Tunnel Circuit and its process (based on [20])
The wind tunnel shown in Figure 3.1.1 is seen as a closed circuit wind tunnel since the air
is moved with the fan and lead through the system before it turns back to the fan. The
wind tunnel is such a closed system. A closed circuit wind tunnel has the advantage that
the wind ﬂow can be controlled in a high amount and is independent of environmental
eﬀects, e.g. moving humans, open doors or the weather [19, p. 27].

ﬂow direction. Thus, the fan produces a suction on right hand side p′ which is smaller
than the atmospheric pressure and a pressure on the left p′′ which is greater than the
atmospheric pressure. It follows that p′ < p′′ and furthermore with the acceleration of
the engine v′ < v′′ as respective velocities.

3.1.6
Boundary Layer Generator
Figure 3.1.6: Boundary Layer Generator/Flow processing unit with spires and ﬂoor roughness
To produce a realistic ﬂow in the test section a turbulent boundary layer generator
(TBLG) is used. The device simulates the terrain roughness of the environment.
A boundary layer is produced when a viscous ﬂuid ﬂows along a surface of undeﬁned
roughness. The D’Alembert’s paradox [24] says that the velocity directly over the surface
is equal to zero. The zone in which the velocity is changing over the height due to that
phenomenon is called boundary layer.
Boundary layers in full scale are inﬂuenced by weather and roughness of environment [8,
ch.3.1]. Latter is mostly deﬁned by vegetation or human made constructions. In the scale
version, roughness is imitated by wooden cubes or other out standing objects. The size
and density of speciﬁed objects inﬂuence the roughness and so with the thickness and
properties of the boundary layer.
The spires in the left side of Figure 3.1.6 produce a close to planetary boundary layer in
the wind tunnel [22, p. 361]. The ﬂoor roughness is given by little wooden blocks in two

3.1.7
Turbulent Wind Flow
Figure 3.1.7: Wind ﬂow procession and preparation for test section throughout the wind
tunnel (based on [20])
’Laminar’ and ’turbulent’ describe diﬀerent behaviours of ﬂow. While laminar ﬂow de-
scribes ﬂuid movement along straight lines without interfering each other, a turbulent
state is characterised by chaotic change of ﬂow and interference between the layers. Thus,
a laminar ﬂow is at most a idealised model and is hard to create in reality.
In order to produce a realistic turbulent airﬂow in the test section, the close to laminar
ﬂow produced by the Flow straightener (Section 3.1.5) has to be distorted. That can
happen with triangular spines. The inclined edges inﬂuence the ﬂow in the horizontal
and vertical direction and produce so with a realistic turbulent ﬂow. Additionally the
ﬂow is distorted by the mentioned wooden bricks, i.e. ﬂoor roughness, in order to create
a realistic scenario of the environment. Rough ﬂoors create more turbulence and such a
broader turbulence intensity Iv (see Figure 3.1.7). The turbulence intensity and the wind
speed distribution changes if the ﬂoor roughness is higher/lower.The wind ﬂow is now
prepared to enter the test section and simulate planetary wind properties.

Sampling Frequency and Aliasing
The sampling frequency was set to 750Hz in order to prevent aliasing. Aliasing is the
eﬀect of a wrong identity of a sampled oscillation. I.e. the ’real’ frequency of the wind
turbulences are measured at some time within the sampling frequency. Is the sampling
Page 22
==PAGE:45==
frequency too low, it appears that a period of an oscillation is just described with one
measurement. This single value alone can not describe the full oscillation, such it gives
a wrong identity and wrong results. In order to prevent this phenomenon, one needs to
sample with the Nyquist frequency, which says that the sampling frequency fs should
be at least two times the frequency of the sampled signal ft (see Equation 3.1.9). [9, p.
48]
fs ≥2 · ft
(3.1.9)
An example of a wrong identity shows Figure 3.1.14. The signal is sampled with a lower
frequency as the Nyquist frequency. Such the identity, the sampling produces (dashed)
is a diﬀerent one to the signal.
Figure 3.1.14: Random frequency sampled with a lower frequency as the Nyquist theorem
To prevent false identities, the sampling frequency was set to 750Hz, in addition to the
oversampling of the device. The device’s oversampling uses a frequency of 3000Hz but
just produces a signal which is 750Hz. The aliasing eﬀect should therefore be low.

Instrument Zero and Zero Measurement
A measurement device connected to a wind tunnel model measuring pressure needs to
be set to zero, i.e. it is calibrated to show a zero for the diﬀerential pressure between
reference and model without wind. Thus, it has to be ensured that the wind velocity
inside the wind tunnel is equal to zero in order to be able to connect a zero pressure on
the surface with a zero wind speed.
Instrument zeros have to be done regulary, also while testing, to ensure that the inner
circuits of the DPMS do not build up a pressure memory. That is the case when over a
long time a e.g. positive pressure is measured. Is the pressure then taken away that the
zeroed pressure should appear again, then it can happen that the pressure is still a bit
over zero, thus a pressure memory appears.
To observe the above explained phenomenon, zero measurements had been performed.
I.e. the pressure taps were recorded without any wind speed in the tunnel. Measured
zeros were carried out over a sampling length of 10sec before and after a instrument zero
as well as after every ﬁve experimental runs.

Measurement of correlation between two data series’ can be understood with the ’ten-
dency towards high-high pairings and low-low pairings’ [18, ch.3.2.7 l. 5 f.]. I.e. if both

two data rows are acting independent of each other. Thus, it is a very important tool for
wind engineering to use and test if and in which manner two pressure taps are dependant
on each other. The correlation coeﬃcient is for linear problems deﬁned between −1 (full
negative correlation) and +1 (full positive correlation). A no correlation interaction is
presented when the coeﬃcient is 0.

Structure Reaction Time Loading
The structure is subjected to a discrete wind loading but can just react to fragment of

dimensioned for every maximum which lasts for a fraction of a second.
7.3.1
Reaction time on structures
According to Hook’s law, the stress σ in a material is deﬁned by Equation 7.3.1.
σ = E · ϵ
(7.3.1)
With ϵ as the strain and E as the Young’s Modulus in the location of interest. Corre-

stress without a strain in a material. Hence, to create a stress in a structure it has to
be in some way dislocated. The dislocation in case of wind loading happens with an
applied varying pressure on the buildings surface. To dislocate the building in a manner

The load application in Section 7.3 has been approached within diﬀerent topology op-

optimised shape design. In a real approach, it is unpractised and not applicable to built
diﬀerent structures for diﬀerent loads. Thus the diﬀerent load cases have to be combined
in some way and applied on the structure.
In dimensioning problems, load cases are applied on a theoretical structure and load
combination factors are found. The topology optimisation Matlab code of Ole Sigmund
[12] can be extended for multiple load cases by including the sum of the compliance

compliance.

Topology Optimisation - Boundary
Conditions
To diﬀer the topology optimising algorithm boundary conditions, such as supports and
passive internal space can be changed in order to be closer to a realistic object and in
order to minimise material use. Boundary conditions especially in smaller scale buildings
are easy to change and can still have a large impact on the structural shape and the
material use. The inner available space is of course also dependant on the latter but as
well on how much inner space and in which dimensions it is built. If diﬀerent boundary
conditions give diﬀerent inner shapes and usable area, then also the inner space, has to
be chosen based on the respective boundary conditions outcome shape design in order
to safe material. Thus, initially layouts based on boundary conditions and furthermore
ﬁnd usable indoor shapes which do not lead to more material or to the initial objective
of more structural variation due to the loading.

Combination of Multiple Wind
Directions
Wind in nature does usually travel in one direction at a time. However, this direction
can change. Thus, especially when designing a light-weight structure which might be
erected at more than one place and in diﬀerent arrangements, it is necessary to design a
building to be able to carry the wind load from all directions. To this point, the topology
optimisations are based on the direction of wind. Further, it will be necessary to apply
multiple load directions on the cross section.
The dimensioned girder of the structure is assumed to carry a two dimensional loading.
Thus, the multiple direction loading will limit itself on two diﬀerent kinds. The wind
ﬂow can come from the left side or from the right side seen for the topology domain. In
order to do so, the wind load has to be just mirrored in itself and applied on the already
set-up algorithm. The multiple load case application works the same way as explained in
Section 6.5.

Chapter 10
Stress calculations
The topology optimised designs have been investigated in terms of shape. The density
distribution over the domain shows where maximum stresses are detected and they can be
lead in the most practical and stabilising way to a support. However, the shapes do not
give any result on how much stresses in reality there are and thus also not which strength
of material is needed, i.e. the dimensions. Hence, the stresses have to be investigated,
ﬁrst qualitative but later also quantitative.
As all calculations have been carried out in a non-linear manner, compression bars are
expected to buckle fast when transferred to reality. Changes in the design have to be done
here and more material has to be added. The topology optimisation program does just
calculate tension and compression forces. Thus, there are no moments in the structure.
The compression bars would add such a moment due to the buckling moment forces and
more material would be needed. The tension areas are seen as more beneﬁcial, since the
bar is just exposed to a tension force. The cross section is then easy to determine.
The ﬁrst step is thus to deﬁne tension and compression bars and see where they appear.
In order to create a structure which is later possible to transfer to reality also in a light
weight manner, a structure with as much tension bars as possible compared to compres-
sion columns has to be found.
Tension and compression bars are deﬁned after the largest principle stresses as calculated
in Section Section 5.1.4. If e.g. an element is loaded in compression and in tension in two
diﬀerent directions, the deﬁnition will assign the element as a kind for which the stresses
are bigger, i.e. is the compression stress greater as the tension the graph will just show
the compression.

However, a material
strength is not added to the program. I.e. when a material strength limit is exceeded,
the TopOpt software does not add more density to the material density matrix if not
possible. Thus, the shape proposition of the Topology optimising program can not be

Additionally, the FE analysis in TopOpt does not include non-linear calculations. Such,

Chapter 13
Conclusion
This work investigated the impact of a stochastic wind load on a topology optimisation
algorithm. An example of a research station in the Arctic environment was considered,
where wind speeds up to 49m/s appear according to the Greenlandic Euro Code Annex.
Light weight structures are necessary in order to be able to construct whole buildings in
remote areas with low accessibility and thus high transportation costs per unit weight. A
half cylindrical shape was considered as an object with the on site dimensions of a 10m
height and a inﬂuence width of 6.67m per girder. According to those dimensions a scaled
model with a height of 7.5cm and a width of 10cm was produced with aids of the 3D
modelling software Rhino and a 3D printer. 145 pressure taps have been aligned in ﬁve
rows over the vaulted surface of the model with a total inﬂuence width to radius ratio of
2/3 according to the full scale object. In order to measure the variability of the pressure
distribution along the taps, the model has been exposed to a wind tunnel testing with
diﬀerent wind speeds. It was tested for velocities of 2, 5, 10, 15, 20, and 25m/s over a
sampling time of around 60s with 750Hz. Additionally, ﬂow visualisation was done in
order to understand the air ﬂow around the model and the produced boundary layer.
After statistically preparing the results, it was conspicuous that higher wind speeds show
similarity in terms of the mean pressure distribution and so with also ﬂow similarities.
Hence, high Reynolds numbers showed ﬂow similarity. Since the scaling of the model was
roughly approximated, it was assumed that the pressure distribution according to the
highest wind speed was the most similar to the full scale problem.
The used topology optimisation algorithm is a uniﬁed program which works on the basis
of a FE-analysis. The distributions were thus scaled to a common reference and com-
pared. The comparison of the TopOpt material density matrices lead then to the same
conclusions as the before stated ﬂow analysis. With higher wind velocities than 5m/s a
shape similarity is conspicuous. The two subjects are thus directly correlated.
Furthermore the variability of the loading was discussed in terms of topology optimisa-
tion. First the outcome was investigated of the variability of one pressure tap line. Since
no average over the inﬂuence width, i.e. the 5 pressure tap lines, was taken, the vari-
ability showed its maxima. The ﬁrst 10th of a second for the sampling time was created
as a video animation of the respective topology optimisations for each tested Reynolds
number. Even though the variability was clearly visible, the underlying material distri-
Page 111
==PAGE:134==
bution concepts did not change fundamentally and the impact was relatively small. In
order to create a more realistic load acting on the structure, the mean of the pressure
distribution over the inﬂuence width and a time span was taken. It is assumed structures
do not react on a wind load of 750Hz. The mean over a time of 3s was assumed. The
variability of the structure for those means can be seen as very low and barely visible.
Further investigations on terms of variability were done by adding indoor space as a re-
strain condition of the topology optimisation, which were designed in diﬀerent shapes,
sizes and amounts. However, the placement was more acting as a support restrain, than
inﬂuencing directly components of the layout. As a deduction the variabilities in support
restrains have been investigated. The most applicable support condition was therefore a
restrain of all horizontal and vertical DOF’s at the lower side of the deﬁnition area.
Finally, a interpretation of the topology optimisation was done in order to get an idea
about the dimensions of the components in reality. A function was written which calcu-
lates the stresses in the topology optimisation outcome due to the given deﬂection matrix.
When applied on the diﬀerent layouts, the outcome showed that most of the topology
optimisation is subjected to tension and that just the pressure zone of the load builds
out some struts which are in compression. It was concluded, that the ﬁnal structure can
be a cable structure taking the tension and a regular girder structure at the pressure
zone taking the moment. Overall stresses and moments were calculated in a commercial
FEM software. The occurring moment around the vaulted girder without the interpreted
topology optimised spider web construction depending on the support conditions is 8
times as high as with it. This shows a high advantage of the application.

Chapter 14
Further Studies
Limitations made in this thesis and the reviewed topic give space for further studies in
many directions. Throughout this thesis, the looked at topic got more and more speciﬁc
and the directions chosen just show a very speciﬁc part of the general researched topic
of interest. If the wind tunnel testing is a staring point, more speciﬁc tests due to the
scaling problem can be carried out where some assumptions have been taken in this thesis.
Higher velocities, surface roughness of the element or diﬀerent boundary layer generators
would produce more data and space for comparisons. The looked into shape can also
vary in many possibilities.
A more computational based scenario would be an application of the problem to a CFD
software. Eventually it would be even possible to combine the already existing Rhino
add on for TopOpt with a CFD software.
The topology optimisation algorithm was not applied including non linear calculation,
stress constrains, anisotropic and multiple materials, dynamic structural behaviour and
multiple sheet thickness’. All this can be investigated with the respect to the stochastic
wind load. Additionally, more load cases can be considered. Loads such as dead load,
snow and eventually internal imposed loads were not considered. Especially snow load
gives possibility on further research. The snow accumulations inﬂuenced by the wind are
parts of other research work. How those appearing snow accumulations act as a load on
a topology optimisation would be important for the further study with a view on the
Arctic environment.
Last but not least the structural concept needs to be investigated more in terms of
the architectural, energetic, environmental content and the out of those concluded dead
load. Furthermore, for each speciﬁc use another indoor space is needed. When designing
shelters, in which the whole diameter is needed as free space, diﬀerent solutions have to
be found.

using a higher number of elements and can be neglected.
However, for further use, the code shall be investigated more.
