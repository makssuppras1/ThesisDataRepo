Abstract  
 
For minimizing global warming potential (GWP) impacts, a more renewable hydrogen (H2) produced 
from biomass feedstocks instead of conventional fossil natural gas (NG) is a promising step toward a 
cleaner energy future [1]. Both consequential and attributional life cycle analysis (LCA) approaches at 
various electricity mix scenarios were investigated using the LCIA model “EASETECH” to determine the 
most environmentally beneficial pathway for hydrogen production from Portuguese feedstocks via 
gasification, steam methane reforming (SMR), water gas shift reaction (WGSR) and pressure swing 
adsorption (PSA) processes. Also, introducing carbon capture & storage (CCS) and energy recovery 
technologies were analysed. No significant variation between LCA approaches and electricity mix 
scenarios was found. Waste biomasses (1.3 - 3 kg CO2-eq/kg H2) performed better than the agricultural 
source Miscanthus (6-8 kg CO2-eq/kg H2) and NG (9.5 - 10 kg CO2-eq/kg H2) for GWP, although in water 
and land use impacts, NG presented lower environmental impacts. CCS technology reduced the GWP 
score for NG and more significantly for the biomass sources by 5 - 6 and 11 - 18 kg CO2-eq/kg H2, 
respectively, leading to negative scores for the biomass pathways. Hydrogen from biomass feedstocks, 
especially from waste products, may play an important role in clean renewable fuels and CO2 
reduction; hence, further research, optimization and upscaling of this technology are vital for a future 
of sustainability.

1. Introduction 
Decarbonization on a large and rapid scale is needed to limit global warming by 1.5 ◦C or 2 ◦C, 
according to the Paris climate goal from the “Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change” (IPCC) [2]. 
The global carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions from the power and transportation industries are 20-25 
GtCO2 per year, which are about 60% of the global CO2 emissions [3]. Hydrogen (H2) is predicted to be 
an important part of many scenarios related to the decarbonization effort, as it can be used as a 
carbon-free energy carrier for fuel, heat, and power instead of carbon-emitting fossil fuels. Moreover, 
H2 can be produces from carbon-neutral or non-carbon sources as biomass or water hydrolysis, 
respectively [1].  
 
As of 2019, over 95% of global hydrogen is obtained from fossil fuels, consequently releasing about 
830 million tons of CO2 per year that contributing to climate change [4]. Hydrogen can be produced 
from fossil hydrocarbons reforming, pyrolysis of coal or natural gas (NG), water electrolysis, and 
thermochemical or biological processing of biomass feedstocks (BM) [5]. Steam methane reforming 
(SMR) of fossil natural gas (conventional hydrogen) is the most common used and it is estimated to 
account for about 50% of the world’s total hydrogen production [6]. Depleting the finite fossil fuel 
sources may lead to a global energy crisis and escalation of global warming [7]. 
 
Hydrogen can be produced alternatively from gasification of a renewable carbon-neutral biomass 
source (bio-hydrogen) to reduce the global warming potential (GWP) of H2 production [8] [9] [10]. In 
the gasification process, the biomass is thermochemically processed a to obtain syngas using a high 
temperature (700-1000 °C), air or oxygen, water, and catalyst [11]. Syngas is a mix of gases, mainly 
carbon monoxide (CO), methane (CH4), nitrogen (N2), ethane (C2H6), CO2, and H2. The syngas can then 
be used as a source for hydrogen production using the processing units: steam methane reformer 
(SMR), water gas shift reactor (WGSR), and pressure swing adsorption (PSA) [11] [12]. In the SMR, 
methane and ethane are reacting with water to obtain carbon monoxide (CO) and H2 from the 
methane steam reforming endothermic reaction. Afterwards, CO react with water to create CO2 and 
H2 in the WGSR through an exothermic reaction called water-gas shift reaction. The PSA is used to 
separate the hydrogen from the rest of the gases. Using waste biomass from other activities can be 
beneficial compared to crops grown directly for energy production. Unlike biomass grown for energy, 
waste biomass does not account for CO2 emissions related to cultivation, direct, and indirect land use 
change (iLUC) [13]. Cultivation’s emissions are from activities used for growing the energy crop. Direct 
land use change is greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions resulting from clearing the land from its former 
vegetation for the new use. The iLUC is GHG emissions resulting from clearing new lands elsewhere 
for growing food instead of the lands used for growing the energy crops [13]. The aim of this study is 
to determine which pathway has the least impact on global warming, land use, water use, and other 
environmental impacts, and to support deciding whether investing in a biomass-to-hydrogen plant is 
environmentally justified. 
 
Hydrogen production is a complex system that has different inputs depends on many variables e.g. 
distance from plant, input gas molecular content, energy content, water demand, etc. Each source has 
different properties and in addition, has different environmental impacts depends on whether it is a 
fossil source, a waste product or an energy crop. In order to properly compare between the 
environmental impacts of the different pathways, a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) can be executed [14].  
 
Life Cycle Assessment is a standardized method for assessing and compare between technologies and 
systems. There are two different approaches for LCA: attributional (A-LCA) and consequential (C-LCA) 
[15]. A-LCA aims to describe the physical flows related to the life cycle and its subsystems, utilizing 
average data presenting the actual physical flows. C-LCA strives to depict how physical flows will 
change according to decision-making, using marginal data relevant to model consequences [16]. The

A-LCA approach attributes inputs and outputs to the product by linking and/or partitioning the unit 
processes of the system. While the C-LCA approach links activities to the product in a way that they 
modify in consequence of the change in demand [17].  
 
This study focuses on compiling the life cycle inventory (LCI) of alternative bio-hydrogen production 
from biomass based syngases after the gasification process and of conventional hydrogen production 
via the steam reforming process of natural gas as part of the wider framework of the project " 
Move2LowC " [18] in Portugal (PT) using the life cycle analysis (LCA) model software “EASETECH 
v3.4.1”. The “Move2LowC” Project aims to create renewable and lower carbon emitting fuels. It uses 
Aspen+ simulations and pilot scale units for its research. The main environmental impact of interest is 
global warming potential (GWP). Secondary focus includes land and water use impact categories. 
 
Since there are different approaches in LCA, both approaches have been used to check if the preferred 
pathway for H2 production is the same regardless the LCA approach. The two approaches are tested 
in this study to compare impacts and subsequently support decision-making. 
 
These hydrogen production pathways and their environmental impacts are affected by the 
characteristic of the specific biomass and the background technosphere, both related to geographical 
location [19]. Thus, an LCA study of the specific target biomasses with Portuguese infrastructures is 
vital for a renewable fuel project in Portugal. Benchmark fossil fuel sources and renewable Portuguese 
biomass sources are compared in this study to demonstrate their environmental impacts. The 
available Portuguese biomass sources for hydrogen production process include eucalyptus (euc) waste 
residues from the paper and pulp industry (mainly trunk and roots), lignin (lig) by-product after 
ethanol production from wheat straw and the energy crop Miscanthus (misc).  
 
Another important parameter is the background electricity mix and its GHG emissions, known as its 
emission factor. Each country has their own grid and technologies in use, resulting in various emission 
factors. Furthermore, countries dedicated to the IPCC goal of reducing carbon emissions e.g. Portugal, 
are striving to implement new and cleaner energy technologies. The emission factor of Portugal is 
predicted to be lower overtime [20]. Thus, an analysis of the system at various electricity mixes and 
emission factors should be carried to verify the time relevancy of the results.  
  
Other technologies may enhance the sustainability of the process such as energy recovery and carbon 
capture and storage (CCS). Burning the off-gas in gas-turbine can be used for energy recovery and can 
aid reducing auxiliary energy demands [11]. The off-gas contain high energy content gasses e.g. CH4 
and H2. CCS can support reducing CO2 emissions to the environment by storing the carbon instead of 
releasing it to the atmosphere [21]. Investigating both processing units’ effect on GWP and on the 
comparison between bio-hydrogen to conventional hydrogen is important for considering whether it 
is sensible to invest in those technologies. 
 
Biomass sources are connected to a geographical place where they are being produced. The 
transportation distance of the biomass source to the hydrogen production plant is affecting the 
environmental performance of bio-hydrogen. This is an important aspect for a continuous process 
that require constant supply of seasonal biomass feedstocks. Collecting feedstocks from other regions 
can aid preventing short supply and up scaling the plant size. Based on its results, this study will try to 
provide answer whether, and how far, can a biomass feedstock be transported to the hydrogen 
production plant before it is not environmentally beneficial.

2. Background 
 
H2 production from NG and syngas via steam methane reforming has the same processing unit. 
Unlike NG, BM undergo an additional gasification process prior to SMR to produce the syngas.  
Gasification is a thermochemical process at a temperature range of 200 to more than 1250 °C 
using water and catalyst to turn the raw BM into syngas. There are a variety of technologies with 
different conditions e.g. bubbling fluidized bed, circulating fluidized bed, entrained flow bed, ect. 
[19]. After the syngas is produced and conditioned by a sulphur removal unit, it continues to the 
SMR unit for converting methane and ethane with water to obtain H2 and CO. Typically, SMR 
operates at temperature of 815-870 °C, pressure range of 15-30 bar and a catalyst [11]. Then the 
CO and water are converted to H2 and CO2 in the WGSR unit. WGSR has two steps: a high 
temperature shift (HTS) and a low temperature shift (LTS) units. Each unit can have different 
catalyst. The temperature ranges between 250-550 °C [19]. For separating the H2 from the rest of 
the gases, a PSA unit is used to obtain pure hydrogen. Numerous PSA technologies are available 
e.g. multi-bed, etc. [19]. Since the off-gas of the PSA contain GHG such as methane, an off-gas 
management unit is used. A flare can be used to reduce GWP mainly from methane and heat can 
be recovered [11]. 
The processing units of the studied model and the main reactions are based on P. Spath 
(2001,2005) [11] [12]: 
- 
Gasification (for BM): Using a low-pressure indirectly heated entrained flow gasifier at a 
temperature of 870 °C and a pressure of 1.6 bar [11]. After the gasification, a sulphur 
removal unit is used to remove sulphuric compounds from the syngas to avoid catalyst 
pollution in further processes. 
 
- 
SMR: Methane and ethane react with water to create CO and H2 conditions are 850 °C and 
28 bar. The main reactions (endothermic): 
Methane steam reforming: 𝐶𝐻4 + 𝐻2𝑂⇄𝐶𝑂+ 3𝐻2        (∆𝐻298 = 205.9 (𝑘𝐽/𝑚𝑜𝑙)) 
Ethane steam reforming: 𝐶2𝐻6 + 2𝐻2𝑂⇄2𝐶𝑂+ 4𝐻2        (∆𝐻298 = 164.7 (𝑘𝐽/𝑚𝑜𝑙)) 
 
 
- 
WGSR: CO and water react to create H2 and CO2. Main reaction (exothermic): 
 
Water gas shift reaction: 𝐶𝑂+ 𝐻2𝑂⇄𝐶𝑂2 + 𝐻2        (∆𝐻298 −41.0 (𝑘𝐽/𝑚𝑜𝑙)) 
 
- 
Cleaning: 90% tar removal and 99% water removal unit to waste treatment. 
- 
PSA: Separation unit with efficiency of 80%. Output 99.9% pure H2 stream at pressure of 
15 bar and temperature of 35 °C. The rest of the gas goes to an off-gas management unit.  
- 
H2 compression: product H2 is compressed from 15 to 200 bar. 
- 
Off gas management: flare to reduce GHG emissions e.g. methane at 95% efficiency.

3. Materials and methods 
This study performed a literature review (section 3.1) to understand and compile inventories for 
conventional and biomass-based hydrogen production. Then, an LCA was performed with the 
characteristics outlined (section 3.2). the LCA results were interpreted with sensitivity, uncertainty 
and scenario analysis (section 3.3). 
3.1. Literature review 
Data from literature was obtained from searching scientific articles in “Google scholar” about 
hydrogen production via steam reforming of natural gas and syngas from biomass gasification. 
Both LCA and technological reports were investigated for the conventional natural gas and the 
alternative biomass pathways. The keywords were hydrogen, syngas, biomass, gasification,  
natural gas, steam reforming, LCA. Data about the process’s inventories e.g. electricity demand, 
catalysts, etc., was collected and is shown in Table 3. 
 
A review of LCA’s on hydrogen production from natural gas and biomass gasification was carried 
out. None of these identified research papers were conducted in Portugal for the mentioned 
target biomass sources. Most data were retrieved from various simulation software and 
agricultural biomass feedstocks. For the GWP impact the common equivalency factor is kg CO2-
eq. It is rarely mentioned which LCA approach is utilized, as well as showing data uncertainty and 
data quality. Another important factor, iLUC, is either out of scope or not mentioned whether it 
has been accounted for in most studies.  
 
Moreno and Dufour (2013) [23] performed a cradle-to-grave analysis including plant materials LCA 
on hydrogen production from Spanish waste by-products of eucalyptus, pine, grape vine, and 
almond compared to conventional H2 from NG based on earlier studies from 2009 [9] and 2011 
[24]. LCA approach was not mentioned. It focused on GWP, acidification, and eutrophication 
impact according to the CML2001 method and the gross energy necessary for H2 production. The 
background electricity mix was from Spain 2010. Moreno and Dufour included energy recovery 
investigations from non-converted methane after gasification and syngas treatment. They found 
that in terms of GWP, waste biomass had better results than NG for hydrogen production and 
energy recovery from off-gas reduce GWP. Moreno and Dufour based their data on Ecoinvent [25] 
and P. Spath (2001) [12]. Spath investigated the LCA of hydrogen production from natural gas and 
later created a detailed design of hydrogen production from hybrid poplar by gasification, SMR, 
WGSR, and PSA (2005) [11]. Spath’s data was based on the SRI international report (1994) [26] 
and the TEAM© database.  
==PAGE:14==
5 
 
Susmozas et al. (2013) [8] used the LCA software Sima-pro 7, process data from Aspen Plus 
simulation and background processes data, including feedstock production, from the Ecoinvent 
v2.0 database to study and compare the cradle-to-gate analysis. It excluded plant materials LCA 
of hydrogen production from poplar and NG. Using the CML method according to ISO 14040:2006 
standards, they evaluated the following environmental impacts: abiotic depletion, global 
warming, ozone layer depletion, photochemical oxidant formation, land competition, 
acidification, and eutrophication. Susmozas et al. focused on the cumulative (non-renewable) 
fossil and nuclear energy demand. The electricity mix was collected from Switzerland and other 
countries members in the Union for the Co-ordination of Transmission of Elecctricity (UCTE) in 
2007. The results showed that bio-hydrogen performs better in GWP, abiotic depletion, ozone 
layer depletion, and photochemical oxidant formation compared to NG. However, it has higher 
impacts on land competition, acidification and eutrophication mainly due to poplar production 
that not a residue. 
 
G. Li et al. (2020) [27] investigated hydrogen production from the corn-straw process and the 
benefits of methane tri-reforming technology. Their cradle-to-gate analysis, excluding plant 
materials LCA, followed the ISO 14040 standard and used Aspen Plus 8.8 simulation data and Gabi 
software to assess GWP and energy consumption. Process electricity was incorporated as 
electricity production from fossil fuels, mainly coal. G. Li et al. found that the H2 production 
process performs better with methane reforming.  
 
Salkuyeh et al. (2018) [28] performed a cradle-to-gate analysis, that excluded plant materials LCA 
of hydrogen production from Canadian pine wood, using data from Aspen Plus version 8.6 
simulation and the GREET-2015 (version 1.3) [29] for GWP thermal efficiency calculations of the 
process, with and without carbon capture technology. Hydrogen from biomass production 
modules were connected to the upstream biomass preparation, transportation processes and 
electricity from the Canadian natural gas combined cycle electricity generation model available in 
GREET-2015. Hydrogen from NG was based on their previous work from 2017. Salkuyeh et al. 
concluded that it is beneficial to introduce CCS for decreasing GWP; however, it increases H2 price.  
 
The GWP impact of miscanthus in terms of cultivation and indirect land use change was calculated 
thoroughly by Tonini (2012) [30]. He found that those impacts are accounted for 54,705 kg CO2-
eq per hectare by the IPCC AR4 methodology for 100 years. The production potential and 
properties were reported by A. Mariana et. al. (2020) [31]. According to their findings, miscanthus 
yield is approximately 15-30 ton of dry matter per hectare per year, and each plant provide 18 
harvests over the lifespan of 20 years. 
 
Emission factor of the future electricity grid is important for understanding the system 
environmental performance in the future. According to the “ROTEIRO PARA A NEUTRALIDADE 
CARBÓNICA 2050 (RNC2050)” report (2019) [20], Portugal is predicted to implement lower carbon 
emitting energy production technologies, which will result in lower CO2-eq emission per kwh 
produced. The emission factors were estimated to be 20.47, 4.46, and 1.69 gr CO2-eq/kWh for the 
years 2030, 2040, and 2050, respectively. 
 
CCS may contribute to GWP reduction from a rich CO2 stream such as in H2 production process. 
Christensen and Bisinella (2021) [21] performed an C-LCA on a municipal solid waste incineration 
power plant and investigated CCS by amine unit and its benefits. They found that CCS can 
substantially decrease CO2 emissions and reduce GWP impact. However, it has higher energy 
demand and other environmental impacts.

Energy recovery might reduce the GWP of the H2 production process. M. Khaljani et. al (2015) [32] 
provided a review about energy recovery efficiencies of combined heat and power gas turbines. 
An electricity recovery  of 25% and heat recovery of 45% were reported. 
 
This dissertation intends to fill some gaps in research, including: evaluation of the differences 
between attributional and consequential approaches and checking if it affects decision-making; 
updating data for fossil hydrogen production benchmarks; presenting data quality and uncertainty 
assessment; considering IPCC AR6 100 years equivalency factors.

3.2.2. Functional unit  
A functional unit (FU) is 1 kg of H2 product (99.9% purity, 200 bar, 35 C) in Portugal from 2023-
2050. System boundaries are from syngas or NG to H2 products (Figure 1). 
The readiness level of the technology is 9 (large scale) for NG reforming, 7 (mid-size scale) for 
biomass gasification and 8 (small scale) for biomass steam reforming [10]. Although the time 
scope is 2023-2043, the data collected is recent, mostly from 2022. Any future improvements 
in technology and future changes in the fuel and energy field are out of scope as this field may 
quickly change due to vast investments and research. 
3.2.3. Assessment method 
This analysis used the Environmental Footprint (E.F 3.0) method [14], derived from the 
International Life Cycle Data system (ILCD) scheme. It was developed by the European 
Commission [15] and embedded in EASETECH. The main emphasis is on GWP; the secondary 
focus is on water and land use impact categories. Both A-LCA and C-LCA approaches were 
conducted using the Ecoinvent v3.7.1 A-LCA and C-LCA databases, following the ISO 14040-
1044 guidelines. The A-LCA system modelling was “allocation at the point of substitution” 
(APOS). 
 
According to Ecoinvent database [35], in the A-LCA, APOS, the responsibility over wastes is 
connected to the producers and the users benefiting from the treatment processes by using 
valuable products produced by them. The allocation key governs the share of each input and 
emission assigned to the reference product and to the by-products that have economic value. 
The A-LCA uses expansion of product systems to prevent allocation that is within the 
treatment systems. Finding appropriate allocation factors for these specific steps may be hard 
to achieve. Instead, it is allocating the valuable by-products of treatment systems together 
with the activity that produced the material for treatment, e.g. waste. This type of expansion 
of the product system may sometimes be impossible, depending on the scope of a given study. 
The basic viewpoint of this approach is that allocation within end-of-life, e.g. waste treatment, 
should be avoided. Every activity whose product requires further treatment before becoming 
valuable is accounted with all treatment activities needed for that product in an individual 
system. Then, the exchanges of the different activities are allocated to the different valuable 
by-products. The point of substitution is in the first activity in the downstream supply chain 
after a treatment activity that produces a valuable product. 
 
The C-LCA has different basic assumptions to assess the change in a current system. In C-LCA, 
by-products that can replace other products provide credits to the activity producing them. 
They are moved to the input side with a negative sign to preserve the mass balance. The C-
LCA system applies substitution to credit processes with the avoided burdens from supply 
chains that are replaced by the by-products generated within them. C-LCA can be used for 
future changes predictions. For example, using water reclaimed from a cooling plant instead 
of river water. Since allocation is not in use, the activity is burdened with the impact of all its 
inputs and emissions. The two basic concepts of C-LCA are the use of marginal supply and 
using substitution to resolve multi-functionality. A marginal or an unconstrained supplier can 
respond to an increase in demand by increasing supply. A supply of a product is unconstrained, 
if one activity producing it as a reference product. By-products are constrained. They are not 
==PAGE:17==
8 
the economic drivers of an activity. Thus, it is not reasonable to increase production to meet 
demand. Another reason for constrained supply can be the technological level e.g. 
technologies that are no longer used [35]. 
 
3.2.4. Base case scenarios (BC) 
Two LCA approaches: A-LCA and C-LCA, were used to assess hydrogen production from steam 
reforming of five different syngases produces from a gasification of biomass feedstocks, 
compared with a conventional pathway of hydrogen production via steam reforming of NG. 
The process for syngas and NG uses the same processing units. The electricity mix is according 
to the Portuguese electricity mix in the Ecoinvent database v3.7.1 [25] with the replacement 
of electricity produced from coal. Coal power plants have been shut down in Portugal since 
December 2021 [36]. Input gas properties are shown in Table 1. 
3.2.5. Assumptions 
1. Plant construction materials, transportation, social impacts and economic analysis are out 
of scope. Both conventional and alternative syngas paths have the same processing units. 
2. No external heat source is needed. The demand for heat for SMR is met by heat produced 
from the exothermic WGSR reaction [37]. 
3. Heavier hydrocarbons other than methane and ethane are modelled as ethane because 
their molar fraction is less than 5%. 
4. Land use change of miscanthus is within the BC scope and was modelled only with CO2-eq 
emissions thus has only GWP environmental impacts. 
5. Heat is modelled as heat from electricity for energy recovery and CCS consumption. Heat 
is used internally in the process and replaces heat generated from electricity.

5. Discussion 
5.1. BC results 
The energy efficiencies (Table 4) results- 30-40% for biomass and 84% for natural gas- are in range 
compared to the literature findings [27], [56], [57], [58]. The efficiencies were calculated without 
extraction, production, or transportation that would decrease the overall efficiency. The energy 
efficiency is mainly affected by the required mass input of the sources and its available reactive 
carbons for H2 production reactions. CH4 can produce four times more H2 per mol, while C2H6 
produces six times more than CO; CO2 does not undergo an H2 production reaction in this process. 
In the case of syngases, about 70% of the carbons are in CO and CO2 forms after gasification. 
Consequently, this means that more carbons (and more biomass) are needed per mole of H2 
compared to NG.  The gasification process oxidizes an important portion of the biomass carbons 
and use additional energy. Another important matter is that the system was modelled in a steady 
state condition under the assumption that no external heat source was required. However, the 
initiation of the process demands heat from burning a portion of the source. Thus, the overall 
efficiencies are predicted to be lower.  
GWP results have been cross-checked with literature and are found to be in range [8], [23],  [27], 
[28], [50]. Lignin and eucalyptus residues perform best, miscanthus second and natural gas last in 
both LCA approaches, according to the findings in this analysis (Figure 2Figure 7). The main reason 
the C-LCA approach has a lower GWP score is due to a lower emission factor related to different 
electricity mix technologies (Table 2). The C-LCA electricity mix renewable share is higher by more 
than 40% than that of A-LCA. The main GWP difference between natural gas and biomass is the 
direct emissions accounting. Biomass pathways emit more CO2 per H2 than natural gas; however, 
the biogenic carbon value is 0, while fossil carbon is accounted for with its full value. The main 
GWP difference between miscanthus and the other sources is that miscanthus is not a by-product. 
Thus, emissions related to cultivation and iLUC are taken into account. As long as lignin and 
eucalyptus are considered by-products from another industry, they are likely to perform better 
than miscanthus and natural gas.  
The water-use impact score for all the sources does not differ substantially (Figure 3). However, 
between the two LCA approaches, C-LCA presents higher values than A-LCA. Regarding process 
water, the water input values per unit of ionised water from Ecoinvent are higher for C-LCA. The 
reason for that is unclear. Regarding electricity water use, the C-LCA BC electricity mix is modelled 
with a share of 68% from water related electricity production technologies (e.g., run-off-river), 
while the A-LCA is 14%.  
 As for the land use impact category, the values presented are in the dimensionless unit of “soil 
quality index” according to E.F 3.0 [14]. It represents the impact indicators: erosion resistance, 
mechanical filtration, physicochemical filtration, groundwater regeneration, and biotic 
production. It is not related to direct and indirect land use change, which account for GWP impact. 
A-LCA has higher land use impact than C-LCA mainly due to the occupation of natural urban green 
areas related to electricity production technologies mix. Moreover, values of the land use impact 
per kWh for the same electricity production technology are lower for C-LCA in the Ecoinvent v3.7.1 
database.  
As shown in Figure 5, the largest environmental impact is the ecotoxicity of freshwater (l.) It is 
related to the ion exchange process of process water. The natural gas pathway uses the highest 
amount of process water per FU (Table 4) among the sources. In C-LCA, the Ecoinvent process 
==PAGE:34==
25 
uses 50% more aluminium sulphate than in the A-LCA for the same process of producing deionized 
water. 
This analysis found no dramatic difference between euc3 and euc4 or between lig1 and lig5. The 
gasification conditioning processes provided similar environmental impacts. Additionally, it was 
assumed that catalysts usage for conditioning had minor effects on the results based on the minor 
effect of other catalysts in the BC process. According to this analysis, conditioning treatment after 
gasification is not required. 
A-LCA and C-LCA approaches both give the same preferred lignin and eucalyptus sources for 
hydrogen production in GWP terms. However, there are differences in the environmental impact 
scores between the LCA approaches.  
The assumption of no external heat for H2 is more reasonable for BM than for NG. The BM high 
CO content for the exothermic WGSR is likely to supply the heat demand for the methane steam 
reforming endothermic reaction. On the contrary, NG has a higher share of CH4, and an auxiliary 
heat source is likely needed. However, that will only increase the GWP score for the already 
highest natural gas pathway. It will also increase the other impact categories. 
5.2. Sensitivity analysis 
5.2.1 Perturbation analysis 
For the base case system, PSA efficiency and CO2 content are sensitive parameters in most 
impacts. PSA efficiency affects the entire system inputs per FU. CO2 content is a main 
component in biomass syngases. A change in the content has the most impact on the other 
reactive components and the whole efficiency of the process. Syngas content data was taken 
from experimental pilot scale gasifier of the biomass sources and data regarding PSA efficiency 
was taken from the Move2LowC project and literature review. Therefore, both CO2 content 
and PSA efficiency are considered to have good reliability.  
 
For GWP, the system is most sensitive to CH4 emissions (flaring and CH4 conversion rate) 
because CH4 has 25 times higher GWP value than CO2. Secondly it is sensitive to PSA efficiency.  
 
Water use impact is affected by PSA efficiency. Other factors are more directly related to 
water use. A decrease in CO conversion rate, particularly in CO rich BM syngas, increase the 
amount of water going to the water reclaiming wastewater treatment process. The same 
trend is seen for the CH4 rich NG with the CH4 conversion rate. Another sensitive parameter is 
the change in CO2 content in CO2 rich syngases. A change in its fraction has a higher impact on 
the more reactive components fraction which determine the water demand for the reactions. 
There is no significant difference between LCA approaches.  
 
Land use impact in this system is mostly influenced by PSA efficiency and CO2 content for both 
LCA approaches.  In A-LCA another sensitive parameter is H2 compression electricity demand; 
in C-LCA, it is SMR water ratio. The reason is that the land use impact in A-LCA is mainly from 
electricity, while in C-LCA it is from water use, as shown in Figure 4. 
 
Other impact categories are sensitive to PSA efficiency, CO2 content, and H2 compression 
electricity demand. As mentioned, PSA efficiency and CO2 content are affecting the entire 
system performance per FU and the overall process efficiency. H2 compression electricity 
==PAGE:35==
26 
demand is the most energy intensive part within the BC system boundary, so when electricity 
use has strong impacts, this component has the highest impact. 
5.2.2 Uncertainty  
The main reason that the uncertainty of lignin and eucalyptus is higher than miscanthus and 
natural gas is that for miscanthus and natural gas the main GWP donors have fewer 
parameters. For miscanthus- yield and iLUC, and for natural gas it is direct emissions of CO2. 
The uncertainty of the other parameters has lower effect on the total score. Fewer parameters 
result in lower overall uncertainty. For lignin and eucalyptus, most of the GWP score is related 
to activities with a relatively high number of parameters (process units). The relatively high 
GWP uncertainty does not change the preferred feedstocks (Figure 2). 
5.2.3 Data quality 
The essential differences between the processes’ data quality scores are the temporal and 
geographical correlation. For this thermo-chemical industrial H2 production process, the 
geographical correlation was assumed to have a lower impact on the process than the 
temporal correlation because if properly isolated, the surrounding temperatures should not 
have a major effect on the process. Temporal correlation, on the contrary, is more important 
because energy technologies are in global focus and are under research [59]. New 
improvements may change the efficiencies and materials used. However, gasification, STR and 
WGSR technologies are mature [10], thus it is reasonable to consider less dramatic changes 
over recent years. Another aspect is that most of the data is based on simulations as real 
information was challenging to retrieve due to industrial confidentiality. Overall, the data used 
for this study is relevant, and as shown in Figure 2 & Figure 7, the process units GWP impact 
are second in influence in the comparison between BM to NG. Furthermore, improvements in 
the processes, especially gasification, will improve the BM scores compared to NG, where 
most of its GWP (7.8 CO2-eq/FU) is from direct carbon emissions from the chemical reactions. 
5.4. Energy system analysis 
The GWP difference between eucalyptus and lignin compared to natural gas and miscanthus are 
due to direct emissions and land use change, respectively. These parameters are not related to 
the electricity mix. Moreover, all BC pathways have similar electricity demands. Therefore, the 
GWP benefits of lignin and eucalyptus over natural gas and miscanthus are not expected to change 
with changing electricity mix. Further energy scenario analysis with pre-process is recommended 
because the gasification and desulfurization processes for biomasses are more energy intensive 
than for natural gas. Although increasing emission factor will result in a higher GWP, it is unlikely 
to happen as long Portugal’s energy goal is to reduce emission factor. 
5.4. Scenario analysis 
5.4.1. Pre-process 
Both approaches provide the same preferred lignin and eucalyptus sources for H2 production 
in terms of GWP when including the pre-process (Figure 7). Even in the case of A-LCA, when 
the additional score for biomass is greater than for natural gas by 30-60%.  
Biomass has higher impacts than natural gas in both land and water use impact categories. It 
comes from biomass transportation and additional electricity use for gasification and 
compressing the syngases for desulfurization. Another factor, the amount of biomass per FU, 
affects these activities attributed to the environmental impacts. Drying the biomass prior to 
transportation will reduce transportation impacts. Since the wet biomass undergoes drying 
==PAGE:36==
27 
before the gasification process, it is reasonable to consider drying before transportation. 
Similarly, investigating and applying a less electricity intensive gasification and syngas 
desulfurization method can reduce related environmental impacts. 
Miscanthus has the production and land use change impacts, where by-product biomass 
sources are not accounted for. This makes miscanthus less desirable for H2 production 
compare to waste biomass. Nevertheless, miscanthus’s GWP score is better than natural gas. 
As the biomass gasification process is a crucial part of hydrogen production a more detailed 
analysis of this system is recommended.  
Other renewable gases (e.g. bio methane) can be used as feedstocks for the SMR and WGSR 
processes. Other sources should be further studied and compared to find the best biomass to 
hydrogen routes.  
Miscanthus’s other environmental impacts, in particular freshwater ecotoxicity (l) and 
freshwater eutrophication (j), are a result of fertilizers used on the ground. Applying a more 
ecosystem-friendly fertilizer may help reduce those impacts and make miscanthus a more 
attractive energy source. 
5.4.2. Energy recovery 
Using the off-gas energy content from residual H2, CO, CH4 and C2H6 may reduce GWP impact 
and supply some of the energy demands. Heat recovery can be implemented for the SMR heat 
demand. Electricity recovery may supply the electricity demands of the process. This unit 
model was simplified and based on recovery efficiencies without input activities for this 
process that may change the results. Also, the heat was modelled as heat from electricity 
while it may be produced from the source itself. A deeper study with more impacts related to 
the recovery unit should be executed for a better understanding of its benefits and related 
impacts.   
5.4.3. CCS 
Since bio-hydrogen has 2-3 higher CO2 emissions per FU than conventional ones (Table 4), 
including CCS technology makes the biomass paths more attractive with negative GWP scores 
(Figure 12). This processing unit increased the maximal transportation distances of biomasses 
by 2,000 – 3,000 km. However, in this study, the CCS model is simplified. The handling of 
captured CO2 after the CCS unit (e.g., transportation, compression, etc.) is out of scope. For a 
better understanding of the benefits of CCS, more activities should be investigated and 
accounted for their environmental impacts, which are predicted to increase depending on the 
technology used, transportation method and distance. 
5.4.4. Combination of pre-process, energy recovery and CCS 
Biomass feedstocks have better GWP than NG due to the high amount of CO2 in the syngas 
and off-gas per FU in both LCA approaches. CCS attributed the most for the bio-hydrogen 
pathways. 
5.4. Transportation distance  
CCS increases the distance for lignin, eucalyptus, and miscanthus by about 70-80%, 90-100%, and 
270-370%, respectively. The transportation distance calculated means that biomass can be 
sourced and shipped from outside of Portugal and still have better GWP scores than natural gas-
based H2. This is important in case of a short supply of biomass in Portugal and when choosing a 
==PAGE:37==
28 
location for the plant in a changing industrial and agricultural environment. The calculation did 
not include the sea voyage of the biomass, which may change the results. 
5.5. LCA approaches 
Using different LCA approaches did not result in a different best GWP performing source according 
to this analysis in all scenarios. One of the reasons is that the A-LCA and C-LCA activities and inputs 
were essentially similar for each approach. Also, the main disparity between them was the 
activities values from Ecoinvent. An important difference between the two approaches is the 
technology mix for electricity production. Different mixes will provide different results in the same 
LCA approach. Furthermore, direct emissions and iLUC are accounted for regardless of the LCA 
approach and are the main GWP contributors for natural gas and miscanthus, respectively.  
5.6. EASETECH 
EASETECH is an adequate LCA modelling software for executing an energy LCA although it is a 
waste management-oriented software. 
Improvement suggestions: 
- 
Energy calculation: mainly system efficiency. 
- 
Using moles for the mass flow. 
- 
Using equations and constants in material generation – material fraction. 
- 
Linking VS/TS to other new materials. 
- 
Adding comments for constants 
- 
Using equation and constants for constants or sensitivity parameters. 
- 
Have a temporary back up for when the program crashes so a not to lose what has been done 
after the last saving. 
 
6. Conclusion 
1. The biomass feedstocks are the preferred sources for hydrogen production from gasification 
and steam reforming in terms of GWP compare to the fossil natural. Moreover, lignin and 
eucalyptus perform better than miscanthus because miscanthus is not considered a waste 
product. Impacts related to production, direct and indirect LUC are only accounted for 
miscanthus. Waste biomass products are likely to perform better than energy crops. However, 
in terms of land and water use impact categories, natural gas performs better than biomass 
sources. The main parameters influence water use impact are the technologies use for 
electricity production and biomass production (relevant only for miscanthus). For land use 
impact, the main contributors are firstly biomass production (if relevant), and secondly are 
the technologies used for electricity production and biomass transportation. 
2. No fundamental variation between the two LCA approaches for this system was found. There 
are differences in the values between A-LCA and C-LCA, but both approaches lead to the same 
preferred feedstocks. 
3. Changing electricity mix did not change the favorable biomass sources in terms of GWP. In all 
cases lignin and eucalyptus performed best, miscanthus second, and natural gas last. Most 
emissions are not related to the electricity use.  
4. The energy recovery unit can lower GWP scores and improve system performance for all 
feedstocks. It did not change the favorable biomass sources in terms of GWP. 
5. CCS unit can capture 13-20 kg CO2-eq/FU for biomass feedstocks and 5-6 kg CO2-eq/FU for 
natural gas. This unit is beneficial for all feedstock, but for miscanthus it attributes the most, 
==PAGE:38==
29 
making it compatible with the other biomass sources. CCS improve GWP scores to negative 
values for the CO2 rich syngas from biomass sources, making them perform even better than 
natural gas. 
6. Wet biomass can be transported from areas outside of Portugal and still have lower GWP than 
conventional hydrogen. Wet miscanthus can be produced and transported from Spain and still 
be better than natural gas for H2 production in terms of GWP. Wet lignin can be transported 
2,300-2,600 km and wet eucalyptus 2,900-3,200 km, which means they can be transported 
from Denmark and Poland before its FU GWP is equal to that of natural gas. Using a CCS unit 
increased substantially the transportation distance, reaching to more than 4,000 km for 
miscanthus and lignin, and more than 6,000 km for eucalyptus. That means that collecting 
these feedstocks from parts of Russia and Turkey and produce hydrogen from them in 
Portugal is still better than the conventional pathway. If the biomass is dried before 
transportation it will increase maximal transportation distance by 12% for miscanthus, 32% 
for eucalyptus, and 55% for lignin. Since wet biomass is dried before gasification, drying it 
prior to transportation is recommended.  A bio-hydrogen production plant can be up scaled 
to treat sources from other countries. 
7. The after gasification catalytic conditioning treatment (ni+ and limestone) was not found to 
have significant environmental benefits on hydrogen production process. 
8. EASETECH is a sufficient model for conducting an LCIA for the hydrogen production process. 
9. A more detailed study of the transportation and the gasification process should be carried out 
as they are vital for the bio-hydrogen production. 
10. A more detailed study of the CCS and energy recovery units should be carried out to provide 
a more accurate depiction of their environmental benefits. 
11. More sources and pre-process for Hydrogen production, e.g biogas from anerobic digestion, 
should be investigated to find the optimal pathway for bio-hydrogen production.
