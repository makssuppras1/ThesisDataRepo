ABSTRACT 
The first documented interpretation of the groundwater resource of Basanti, a block in the Sundarban 
zone of West Bengal, India. Over the past years a decreasing trend of groundwater levels has been 
suspected and raised the vulnerability of the area. The following report assesses the sustainability of the 
groundwater resource in Basanti by means of a water balance. It is supported by a detailed review of the 
area and an analysis of the hydrostratigraphy of Basanti’s subsurface. On-site field work allowed for 
direct measurements of water levels and the electric conductivity throughout Basanti that provided data 
for the description of aquifer properties and the current state of salt water intrusion. Moreover, available 
long-and short term water level data of South 24 Parganas and Basanti were analyzed to inform about 
the trend of groundwater levels and about the presence of annual recharge. Finally, a total and a deep 
aquifer water balance were developed that enabled a sustainability assessment on the deep aquifer with 
different pumping scenarios. The study concluded that the groundwater resource in Basanti is under 
stress. It was observed that groundwater levels were going down, and range close to sea level increasing 
the danger of saltwater intrusion. Increased groundwater use for irrigation has put further pressure on 
the resource. The water balance indicated that extractions are similar and even higher than water inflows, 
in particular in the irrigation months. However, conductivity measurements found no obvious trend of 
saltwater intrusion over the last four years, but show possible problems in the Northeast of Basanti. The 
critical state of the groundwater resource in Basanti raises the necessity to react within the next decade. 
The application of mitigation strategies in order to prevent groundwater depletion, as well as, the 
monitoring of monthly conductivity measurements in the area are suggested as important actions and 
next steps for the coming years.

1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Prelude 
The earth is viewed as a Blue Planet from outer space because more than two thirds of the planet is made 
up of water (USGS, 2016). However, only 2.5 percent of this water is fresh and is the only usable source 
for human needs. According to Du Guerney (1994), water is the most essential natural resource as it is 
vital for the survival of humankind. He also stated that,  
“ensuring that present and future generations will have adequate food and water, and 
concurrent maintenance of the resource base and the environment, are two of the most 
challenging tasks that have ever faced mankind (Du Guerney, 1994).” 
In addition, Sophocleous (2000) mentioned that the needs of the present should never comprise the 
ability of future generations to meet their own needs in terms of water resources. However, the 
sustainability of a water resource is affected by factors such as population growth; urbanization; 
pollution; higher demands for food security and socioeconomic well-being; and competitions among 
users make successfully securing a water resource much more difficult (UNESCO, 2015). Moreover, 
global warming and the resulting climate change have led to a movement of global precipitation patterns 
in new directions and may have affected water availability and its sustainability in the long run (Bates, 
2008). According to Brooks (2007), the lack of attention in better water management practices such as 
legislations or measurements to increase water conservation, effective water use, water recycling, and 
water infrastructure, are contributing factors for countries who are trying to deal with water scarcity, as 
can be seen also in India now. 
In India, only 4 percent of the world’s freshwater resources are available for 16percent of the global 
population (Sustainalytics, 2016). However, water was viewed as an unlimited resource with no need to 
be managed as a scarce commodity or provided as basic human right. This way of thinking and the lack 
in water management has led 54 percent of the country facing high to extremely high water stress (Shiao, 
2015). In the state of West Bengal, which is located in the eastern part of India and spreads from the 
northern Himalayas to the southern Bay of Bengal, water resources are also scarce. Although the state 
is drained by the Eastern Ganges Basin with its three major rivers: the Ganga, the Brahmaputra, and the 
Subarnarekha (Bandyopadhyay, 2014; Rajmohan, 2013), the increasing pollution of rivers and streams 
used by the Bengal Basin inhabitants as pathways for sewage and industrial waste made the surface 
water undrinkable. The resulting poor quality of surface water containing harmful pathogenic 
microorganisms in the early 1970s is a major contributing factor for the water policy of West Bengal to

part of the Bengal basin (approximately 21,000 km²), respectively. They analyzed the effects of pumping 
at present and projected future rates on the flow patterns in order to predict the future status of the 
groundwater resource in their study areas. According to Michael (2007), the impact of pumping has 
substantially changed the shallow groundwater budget and flow paths from predevelopment conditions. 
This is in accordance with Mukherjee (2006), who observed that in all examined districts, except the 
southern half of South 24 Parganas, natural flow paths have been highly disturbed by the formation of 
cones of depression due to pumping. In southern South 24 Parganas, flow reversal has developed along 
with inflow from the Bay of Bengal leading to saltwater intrusion.

future water related disasters,” a tendency of groundwater resources getting scarcer in the project’s 
region has been noticed over the past years. The project is being conducted by two project leaders,

Joygoplapur Gram Vikash Kendra (JGVK). It aims to improve the resilience to future climate and water

specifically groundwater deterioration (Project-UBU-JGVK, 2015). In Basanti, located in South 24

found beneath Basanti. Their study recommends to continue the initiated work in order to determine the 
sustainability of the groundwater resources in Basanti.  
The majority of water supply in this region comes from highly stressed groundwater resources, mostly 
due to intensive farming and concentration of population (Bandyopadhyay, 2014). With future 
population growth and their associated food and water demand, the pressure on the present water 
resource will increase and might cease the availability of water in Basanti (HDRCC, 2009). In order to 
meet an increasing demand, development and management strategies need to be formulated. For this 
purpose, a proper understanding of the groundwater resources in Basanti is important and therefore the 
focus of this report. 
1.2 Objectives 
The present study aims to assess the sustainability of the groundwater resources in Basanti, West Bengal. 
The sustainability is assessed by means of a total water balance of the study area. The following aims 
are used to provide input for the assessment.  
Obtain available knowledge about the geology and the groundwater resources in Basanti. Moreover, 
determine the geologic underground of Basanti by means of a subsurface model. Additionally, assess 
the hydraulic interaction between the groundwater and the surface water by direct measurements and a 
following wave response analysis. Furthermore, characterize water budget parameters for the entire area, 
as well as, separately for the deep aquifer. Hereby, apply a sustainability assessment on the deep aquifer 
with different pumping scenarios. Also verify arithmetic correctness of total water budget by an 
uncertainty analysis. In addition, illustrate the trend of water availability over the past years by means 
of long- and short term water level measurements around and in Basanti. Thereby, make statements on

1.3 Study area

up to the mouth of the Bay of Bengal. The study area, Basanti block, is situated in the proximity of the 
Bay of Bengal; in South 24 Parganas, which is the most southern district of the State of West Bengal. 
Basanti lies between 21°57΄00˝ N to 22°20΄00˝ N and 88°38΄00˝ E to 88°47΄0˝E, and is situated

2 LITERATURE RESEARCH 
2.1 Hydrogeologic overview of the Bengal Delta Plain 
The study area lies within a complex hydrogeological region, the Bengal Delta Plain (BDP). This region 
is characterized by depositional processes and tidal fluctuations. Moreover, a heterogenic subsurface of 
variable sediments as well as different degrees of salinity dependent on depth are present. Therefore, the 
current hydrogeologic situation is clarified first.  
2.1.1 Depositional activity in the Bengal Basin 
The Bengal Basin is the world’s largest fluvio-deltaic system. It comprises an area of 2,500 million km² 
and is drained by the Ganges, Meghna, and Brahmaputra rivers (Alam, 2003; Dowling, 2003). A picture 
of the Bengal basin, the river draining the rivers, and its physiographic units can be seen in Figure A. 1 
in the Appendix. The Delta contains 5x105 km³ of sediments and moves from a northeast trending hinge 
zone across the eastern district of India, West Bengal, and Bangladesh since Miocene (i.e. 24 Ma) 
(Goodbred, 2000).  
The sedimentation process taking place at the BDP is sensitive to sea level fluctuations which naturally 
occur in the coastal regions (Dowling, 2003) and as well is highly productive. The present estimate of 
the total drainage area in the BDP is 2x106 km² (Coleman, 1969), which supplies a sediment load of 
approximately one billion tons (9.1 x 1011 kg) to the Bay of Bengal (Milliman, 1992). The deposition of 
sediments by fluvial processes taking place in the BDP has formed the present aquifers consisting of 
Quaternary sediments in age from Pleistocene (1.6 Ma – 10 ka) to Holocene (10 ka – present) (Islam,

2.1.2 Geology of West Bengal  
The western part of the Bengal Basin is situated within the district of West Bengal and starts from central 
West Bengal southward to the Bay of Bengal (Mukherjee, 2007a), see Figure 1.  
The topography is flat with a maximum elevation of 35 m in Murshidabad and Janingpur, and from there 
decreases with a gradient of ~0.1 m/km to the south (Mukherjee, 2009a). The vegetation in between is 
characterized by a monotonous surface and occasionally crossed by channels of tributaries and 
distributaries of the river Ganga (81 percent of area), the Brahmaputra (12 percent), and the Subarnarkha 
(4 percent) (Bandyopadhyay, 2014; Sharma, 2014). Hereby, the river Bhagirathi – Hooghly and

The subsurface of the district West Bengal consists of two different sediment types, the fissured hard

The fissured formation are hard rock formations consisting of crystalline, metasedimentary, and volcanic 
rocks. These formations are mostly found in the western part of West Bengal in form of Pre-Cambrian 
granites and granite gneisses, as well as, crystalline granite gneiss of Archaean age which are along with 
other formations such as sedimentary sandstone and shale of lower Gondwana age, and quaternary 
alluvium. Furthermore, consolidated formations (metamorphic rock), semi-consolidated formations 
(sedimentary formations) and unconsolidated formations (alluvial deposit) cover the northwestern part 
of West Bengal, including the Darjeeling District (Rajmohan, 2013). 
The rest of the state i.e. 2/3 of West Bengal, consists of a thick alluvial formation. It is characterized by 
sand and gravel layers of different textures and represents the main aquifer (Rajmohan, 2013; Roy, 
2015). From the southern part of South 24 Parganas up to the Sundarbans similar geologic formations 
are found in the subsurface. Here, Quaternary deltaic sediments composed of clay, silt, and sand of 
various grades, gravels, etc., are underlain by a Tertiary alluvium (CGWB, 2009b).

An aquifer is defined as a water-bearing layer consisting of porous and permeable sediments such as 
sand and gravel. These sediments allow water to flow through their pores and facilitate water abstraction 
by pumps if needed. In general, an aquifer is regarded as unconfined. However, it can be under confined 
conditions if it is overlain by an impermeable layer of sediments such as clay. This impermeable layer 
is represents an aquitard. It makes it difficult for water to infiltrate and penetrate through, and hence 
puts the water in the aquifer under pressure (Karanth, 1987). 
The subsurface geological picture at the head of the western Bengal basin in Murshidabad and Nadia, 
contains a 150-250 m thick granular zone under unconfined conditions. It is called Sonar Bangla aquifer 
(Golden Bengal) and acts as a recharge zone for the confined and deeper aquifers further south. This 
aquifer is probably formed over the Murshidabad aquitard, a thick basal clay layer, due to depositional 
processes in the delta of the Ganges-Brahmaputra. The Murshidabad aquitard divides the Sonar Bangla 
aquifer and any other aquifer below ~300 m mean surface level (MSL). From the head of the district 
North 24 Parganas, a top clay layer to a depth of 60 m is present, extending south to Kolkata, and 
continues with a depth of 12 to 25 m below ground level (bgl) toward Diamond Harbor in South 24 
Parganas. This surficial aquitard prevent greater direct rainfall recharge into the aquifers below and thus 
confine the aquifer from the surface water. Besides the top clay layer, another clay layer at 
approximately 150 m depth spreads from latitude 22.7°N (in the vicinity of Kolkata) with a thickness of 
20 to 30 m and increases to 50 and 60 m farther south. It is called Ichamati-Bhagirathi-Hooghly aquitard, 
named after the two major rivers that drain the area. It divides the main aquifer into a shallow and deeper 
aquifer (Mukherjee, 2006). According to Mukherjee et al. (2007), shallow and deep aquifer systems in 
the western Bengal Basin have no distinct hydraulic and physical boundaries. They are more likely to 
be connected with each other in some areas due to cross-cuttings. Furthermore, aquifer tests in 
Bangladesh, which is representative for the Bengal basin, performed by the British Geological Survey 
reported a leaky confined response on short term basis, whereas showed an unconfined or semi-confined 
behavior for longer periods (i.e. months) (BGS&DPHE, 2001). 
Additionally, alternating sequences of sand and clay layers of different thicknesses occur in the Delta, 
as well as, the flood plains down to a depth of about 300 m bgl and deeper (Roy, 2015; Rajmohan, 2013; 
Pal, 2009). In the southern part of South 24 Parganas, a two-aquifer system in depth range of 22 m to 
168 m bgl and 160 m to 360 m bgl occurs. Moreover, deeper aquifer systems consisting of four aquifers 
with depths ranging from 180 m to 490 m bgl can also be present in this area. Even five aquifers within 
a depth 190 m to 400 m bgl are found in Canning where the bottom aquifer continues up to Gosaba and 
attains a thickness of 45 m (CGWB, 2009b; CGWB, 2015; Mukherjee, 2006).  
Moreover, the special structure of multiple aquitards in the Delta and flood plains, as well as, a possible 
influence from excessive pumping, lead to different water qualities. Here, the aforementioned clay layers 
divide the aquifer into fresh-brackish-fresh water aquifers (Mukherjee, 2007a). Near the top, in the first

2.1.4 Characteristics of the groundwater flow 
The depositional history of the BDP has formed a complicated inter-layering of coarse and fine grained 
sediments in the delta which resulted in an equally complex groundwater flow (Dowling, 2003). 
According to Ravenscroft et al. (2005), groundwater flow is described by means of three distinct scales. 
On a village scale, the local flow system is predominant which discharges groundwater only several 
kilometers from recharge zones and has residence times in the order of decades. Whereas groundwater 
flows on an intermediate scale and basin/regional scale have lateral flow paths around 10 kilometers and 
up to hundreds of kilometers, respectively. Intermediate and regional scale flows likely remain 
undisturbed from anthropogenic influences and have groundwater residence times of 102-103 and greater 
than 104 years, respectively (Harvey, 2006).

southward to the Bay of Bengal. Hereby, the Ganges area, as well as, several wetlands e.g.east of

recharge and outflow for parts of the Sonar Banglar aquifer which are below 200 m and deeper, and thus 
indicates relatively long residence times and negligible flushing. 
In the entire basin the principal controls on groundwater levels are extensive irrigation combined with 
the monsoonal climate of the region. The South Asian monsoon dominates the natural hydrology of the 
Bengal Basin. Surface water bodies often merge during monsoon period, whereas in the dry period 
rivers, small distributaries, lakes, and several ponds predominate (Michael, 2009a). Groundwater heads 
decrease every year in the dry season where the need for pumping is highest, and tend to return to aquifer 
full conditions after the monsoonal period. Subsequently, sufficient recharge from rainfall and other 
sources like major rivers and ponds may be suggested (Harvey, 2006). Also in the study of Michael et 
al. (2009), it is stated that major rivers, lakes, small distributaries and ponds have a considerable 
influence on the local groundwater flow system. 
In particular, rivers and their seasonal effect on surrounding aquifers have complicated the shallow 
hydrology (Harvey, 2006; Mukherjee, 2007a). When pumping begins from January (in the dry period), 
the river water level slows the decline in hydraulic head caused by pumping. But the decline still 
increases to that extent that the hydraulic head in the aquifer falls below the one in the river hence, 
resulting in an inflow from the river to the aquifer. When monsoon flooding begins in June, both the 
water levels in the river, as well as, in the aquifer, are recharged by rain water into the aquifer below 
and possibly by an increased groundwater flow. Water heads increase, but with the hydraulic head of 
the shallow aquifer being greater than the river head, and subsequently leading to an outflow to the river 
(Harvey, 2006).

2.2 Characteristics of the coastal region 
In this Section the coastal region of the western Bengal basin comprising the district South 24 Parganas 
are described in more detail in order to give an understanding of the environment which is present in 
and around the study area 
2.2.1 Climate 
The climate of the coastal region is warm and hot (Falkenmark, 1989), with an average temperature 
varying from a maximum of 38 °C in June to a minimum of around 13.5 °C during January. The delta 
plain gets a high, relative humidity between 70 to 88 percent as it lies in the vicinity of the Bay of Bengal 
and is affected by seasonal climate. The seasonal climate is driven by an annual south-west monsoon 
which lasts from June until September/October. Within the monsoonal period, approximately 82 percent 
of the annual, average rainfall of 1800 mm is falling in the area, see Figure 5. The rest of rainfall is 
distributed over post-monsoon (2 percent), from November until December, and pre-monsoon 
(16 percent), from January until May. (CGWB, 2009b; GoWB, 2015; Mukherjee, 2015; Mukherjee, 
2006; Prasari, 2011). 
 
2.2.2 Vulnerabilities 
“The Sundarbans is one of the most visible victims of the ravages of climate change 
in India. Fragility of the ecosystem coupled with underdevelopment has made this 
region particularly vulnerable (Gosh, 2012)”.

deltaic environment as a world heritage site owing to its fertile, low-lying alluvial plains. Tidal variations 
are high in this region and have the highest tidal range along the east coast of India (Rose, 2015). The 
average tidal amplitude ranges between 4.32 m to 6.73 m during high tides and occurs twice a day. The 
coastal areas in South 24 Parganas, including Basanti, are drained by multiple river channels of varying

reverses in the dry period and the salinity increases to about 25 ppt (Sarkar, 2013). Salinity is an 
important parameter which governs many biological and chemical processes in the coastal zone of South 
24 Parganas. It can also affect the groundwater, as well as, the fresh water resources on islands like 
Basanti (Prasari, 2011). Due to influent river discharge in the dry period, salt water intrusion into 
groundwater may take place (Bhadra, 2015). Combined with excessive pumping for irrigation and 
irrigation with low quality (brackish) water during pre-monsoon season, has led to a shallow water table 
enriched with salts contributing to salinity build-up in soils throughout the year (Bandyopadhyay, 2003). 
Subsequently, soil salinity during the dry season likely and may affect cultivation productivity of the 
farmers. Values of soil salinity range between 0.4 to 1 ppt (Choudhury, 2015).

2003). According to the Harmonized World Soil Database Viewer (HWSD, 2012), the surface soil 
consists of mostly thionic fluvisol with a topsoil (0-30 cm) fraction of 20.8 percent sand, 38.5 percent

39.8 percent clay. In Basanti, the clay content varies between 27 percent and 40 percent and can be 
described as a fine-loamy soil (Bandyopadhyay, 2003). Due to the low permeability of clay, only minor 
amounts of water can infiltrate into ground, thus limiting recharge into underlying aquifers.

2.3 Basanti at a glance 
The district of South 24 Parganas comprises a total area of 9,960 km² and consists of five Sub-Divisions 
namely Alipore (Sadar), Baruipur, Diamond Harbour, Kakdwip, and Canning (CGWB, 2009b; GoWB, 
2015). Canning Sub-Division is further divided into four community development blocks, including 
Basanti. Basanti consists of three land parts: Basanti Mainland, i.e. 46 percent of the total area; Basanti 
Island (41 percent); and an island of Reserved Forest (12 percent). The block Basanti has a total area of 
389.45 km², out of which 342.06 km² is populated and comprises mainland and island. The study area 
is surrounded and drained by the rivers Matla and Bidyadhari flowing into the Bay of Bengal.  
2.3.1 Social aspect 
Basanti block is a rural region and is governed by 13 local self-government organizations, called 
gram panchayats (GPs), which also contain numerous smaller villages (GoWB, 2012; Ashur, 2012). 
The total population living on Basanti was 336,717 in 2011 and counted in average 5 persons per 
household (Census, 2011). They are distributed over two parts of the block. Approximately 2/3 of the 
total population, i.e. 12.8 persons/ha land, lives on Basanti Mainland, whereas the remaining 1/3, i.e. 
6.5 persons/ha land, occupies Basanti Island (UBU, 2015). 
According to District Census (2011) an employment rate of 40 percent of the total population with an 
equal share of 50 percent among main and marginal workers is present in Basanti. Apart from marine 
fishing, the primary traditional occupation in Basanti is agriculture. In this sector are 66 percent of the 
total workers employed and their distribution by type and cultivated land is listed in Table 1. 
 
Small, medium, and big farmers own land, whereas patta holders get wasted land from the government 
and bargaders cultivate foreign land if the owner is not able to cultivate it by himself. In contrast to 
agriculture, there is no big industry on Basanti. The residual workers are employed either in the 
household and small scale industry sector (10 percent of total workers) or in the service sector 
(24 percent) (Census, 2011). However, possibilities for small-scale industry and emerging handicraft 
activities are tremendous in the area of South 24 Parganas (HDRCC, 2009).

Borehole construction 
In general, the majority of borehole constructions is under governmental ownership. The GP office, 
which is a lower level office of the government, the Sundarban Development Board (SDB), the Forest 
Department, as well as, Public Health Engineering Department (PHED), provide financing in order to 
construct boreholes. They are also the ones who decide the location of a new tube well, but based on 
wishes of the local community, and are responsible for supervision of the borehole construction, as well 
as, maintenance of the tube wells afterwards (Ashur, 2012). Generally, a tube well is said to supply 
250 people with water on an average runtime of 10 years in average. However, most of the tube wells 
are older (Maharana, 2016).  
In most cases, the actual drilling is commissioned from the government to different agencies and NGO’s. 
The most common drilling technique is wash boring. In wash boring, water is pumped into the borehole 
to wash out drilled sediments and thus clean the bottom of the borehole for 
further drillings through the drilling pipe. A more precise explanation of the 
applied drilling technique “wash boring” is presented in the Appendix under 
section A.2. 
In Basanti, the majority of boreholes are 300 m deep and are classified as deep 
boreholes. The screen is mostly not placed before 300 m and has a length of 
24 m (Naskar, 2016). 
The exact position of the screen in depth depends on a specific procedure. After 
drilling of the first 250 m, sand is collected for every 6 m. Moreover, water is 
tasted to determine its salt content. Subsequently, the screen is placed at the 
depth where sand with the least salt content was present. The placement of the 
tube well is solely decided by the tube well instructor, as well as, on the basis

Water situation 
As mentioned earlier water resources in the study area are vital for the inhabitants as they depend solely 
on hand pumps. The evolution of geology and drainage of West Bengal has largely shaped its 
groundwater potential. The alluvial eastern and mid-northern areas are characterized as having large 
yields (Bandyopadhyay, 2014). However, excessive pumping upstream of the western Bengal basin has 
led to a decline in water table, thus affecting the water availability of the region. Extensive pumping 
upstream may already put pressure on the water resource leading to a decline in water level. By a study 
on the hydrodynamics of the lower Ganges basin by Khan (2013), data from environmental satellite 
(ENVISAT) mission and microgravity data from the Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment 
(GRACE) mission was used to indicate average seasonal river fluctuations and monitor integrated water 
volume changes, respectively. During the period from 2003 to 2007, both datasets observed a decrease 
of the seasonal amplitude of the equivalent water thickness and thus indicated a definite trend of total 
water depletion. 
 
The decline may have an effect on Basanti and puts its water resources development and socioeconomic 
sustainability to danger. In order to be able to formulate any water resource development and 
management plans, it should be the foremost priority to assess water availability and demand in a proper 
way beforehand.

3.2.1 Recharge from other sources 
According to Harvey et al. (2005), pond bottoms, streambeds and cultivated fields can provide

only cultivated field during the non-monsoon season are considered in the return flow calculations as

3.2.2 Horizontal groundwater flow 
The groundwater flow is an important parameter for assessing the sustainability of the groundwater 
resources. For this purpose, a groundwater flow model is taken in order to assess the groundwater flow 
system and to derive the water flows reaching Basanti. However, with a local area model, the flow 
system beneath Basanti is difficult to assess because groundwater flow paths may extend regionally. 
Moreover, minor recharge from the top, as well as, occurring pumping upstream of Basanti may 
influence groundwater flow southward also has to be considered. For this purpose, the application of a 
regional groundwater flow model on the study is preferred.

regional-scale groundwater flow model developed by Professor Dr. Holly A. Michael, from the

4.2.2.2 Electric conductivity measurements 
In general, electric conductivity measurements can be undertaken in order to obtain proper information 
about the salinity of a specific media, e.g. water. The electric conductivity is a measure of the water’s 
ability to conduct electrical current. It describes how well water will allow electrical flow to travel 
through. The ease of electric flow through the water is dependent on the ions due to their positive and 
negative charges. Thus, the ability is directly related to the concentration of ions in the water wherein 
the amount of electrons being transferred increases with the amount of ions in the solution. As a result, 
the higher the ion concentration in a media is, the higher the electrical flow thus, the conductivity is also 
higher. Seawater constitute a media which has a very high conductivity, whereas distilled water, which 
is also used as an insulator, has only low or negligible conductivity (Kemker, 2014). 
In the course of this study the conductivity at different tube wells throughout Basanti was measured in 
order to obtain knowledge of the water’s degree of salinity and thus to determine the state of 
vulnerability of Basanti in terms of its drinking water resource. Salinity is defined as the total 
concentration of all dissolved salts in water (Kemker, 2014). It is also a strong contributor to 
conductivity. For this purpose, the conductivity meter Handheld meter Cond 330i from WTW was used 
for field work. The used meter is courtesy of DTU, Environment. Since conductivity results are sensitive 
to temperature and the study area in Basanti has a very warm environment, specific conductance 
temperature coefficients for a KCl-solution (0.01 mol/l) had to be applied in order to correct the 
measurements to the standard at 25 °C. 
Conductivity measurements of groundwater from deep-set aquifers were taken at different locations on 
the island and the mainland. The raw data showing measured locations by name, geographical 
coordinates, electric conductivity, and salinity, can be seen in Table B. 13 and Table B. 14. The 
performed measurements were taken with a GPS device (datum WGS84) and are shown in Figure 13.

4.3.1 Hydrostratigraphic modelling 
By importing gathered borehole log data into a hydrostratigraphic model, an illustration of the aquifer-
aquitard framework in the underground of Basanti is possible. 
4.3.1.1 Data acquisition and sediment classification 
The obtained detailed elevations were acquired by a digital elevation model (DEM) from the Shuttle 
Radar Topography Mission (SRTM 30) of NASA. The void filled dataset is at resolution of 1 arc-second 
(30 m) and processed for the terrain of the study area. 
In order to obtain a hydrostratigraphic model of Basanti’s subsurface, lithologic data was collected from 
one driller (30 lithologs) in the Basanti region and from Prof. Dr. Abhijit Mukherjee (3 lithologs). The 
data accumulated various lithologs which contain detailed information about the composition and 
texture of the sediments in the subsurface. In total, 33 lithologs of drinking water wells (irrigation wells 
and observation wells) drilled in and around the study area were processed. A list of used lithologs can 
be seen in the Appendix in Table B. 2. The obtained lithologs had a depth of 144 - 432 meters bgl., with 
a mean of 288 meters. Sediment samples were taken every 3 m and 1 m in vertical depth according to 
the observations of the drillers and as reported by Mukherjee et al. (2007), respectively.  
In general, the lithology consist of four types of unconsolidated sediments: clay, poli, sand, and 
sandstone. The sediments can be further subdivided in terms of grain size and color. Sand is described 
in terms of color (black, red, and white) and cohesiveness (coarse, fine, and normal). Clay is sometimes 
characterized as hard clay. However, only the major types of sediments were used for the 
hydrostratigraphic model. 
From a hydrogeologic point of view, the layers were considered as aquifer when fine sand is present. 
Whereas layers of clay, sandstone, and poli determined an aquitard. According to the driller, poli is 
characterized as very powdery sediment consisting of very fine sand and silt. Sandstone is characterized 
as a sedimentary rock type. Its hydraulic conductivity is assumed to be the same as of silt. The thickness 
and the hydraulic conductivity (K) value of these less permeable layers are crucial as they govern the 
local, as well as, regional groundwater flow and thus influence pumping in different aquifers 
(Mukherjee, 2006). Associated K values can be seen in the Appendix in Table B. 1.

4.3.2 Aquifer response analysis 
In the following step the interaction of found water bearing layers with the top surface was analyzed in 
more detail. Hereby, taken water level measurements, see Section 4.2.2.1, at various tide gauges and 
nearby wells were used in order to examine the occurring aquifer response and thus answering whether 
a hydraulic connection between the deep aquifers with the top is present. The response of measured 
water levels were hereby analyzed by an approach postulated by Townley. Based on complex algebra, 
the approach is an efficient method for computing an analytical solution for periodic flow and gives 
insight into the spatial variations of amplitudes of head fluctuations. As a result, a function of water 
level over time and space was computed by using the changing water head in the river, the period of 
recurrent tides, the aquifer characteristics such as storage coefficient (storativity), transmissivity, aquifer 
thickness, and the distance from the location of measurement to the riverbank (Townley, 1995). In the 
Appendix under Section B.2.1, the method and applied equations are described in more detail. 
For the analysis, measured water heads were plotted along a time axis into a figure in Maple and overlain 
by the resulting curve from the Townley approach. By adjusting the storage coefficient and the 
transmissivity of the computed Townley function, the resulting amplitude was fitted to the measured 
water heads. With the detected storage coefficient and aquifer transmissivity, it was possible to

4.3.5 Water balance of Basanti 
Water is in constant movement and follows a complex flow system. In Basanti, the major flows were 
characterized by: rainfall and surface runoff, in particular in the monsoon time; recharge from rainfall 
and surface water bodies; evapotranspiration from agricultural lands and vegetation, mostly highest in 
the dry period where sunny days are ample; pumping from deep aquifers and surface water bodies for 
irrigation purposes and subsequently leading to return flow in the shallow aquifer; and a received 
horizontal groundwater flow from adjoining areas. All elements are interconnected thus, all of them 
were considered for a proper assessment of Basanti’s groundwater resources, Figure 19. 
 
The water balance is intended to provide information on the potential water availability in Basanti. This 
shall facility conclusions on the sustainability and answer the question whether pumping exceeds 
potential inflows.

4.3.4.4 Uncertainty analysis  
Finally, an uncertainty analysis was performed in order to assess the potential error of the outcome from 
the water balance analysis. 
The applied uncertainty analysis used estimates gathered from literature research in order to compute 
the mean and the standard deviation for precipitation, evapotranspiration, recharge, and surface runoff. 
In addition, for estimates based on calculations such as parameter IRF, SP, GWinflow and GWoutflow, as 
well as, Qdomestic and Qirrigation, an upper and lower boundary was introduced. The range applied to 
groundwater flow and Qirrigation was ±15 percent, and for Qdomestic, IRF, and SP was ±5 percent. In order 
to judge their quality, an uncertainty analysis of all mentioned elements was carried out and associated 
equations from Section 3.3 were taken. For a simple uncertainty analysis, all elements were assumed to 
be statistically uncorrelated. Finally, the total uncertainty of the water budget was calculated separately 
for natural recharge, inflow from other sources, as well as, total outflow, and was described by the square 
root of all individual standard uncertainties with Equation ( 11 ). The outcome of natural recharge was 
dependent on the uncertainty of precipitation, evapotranspiration, and runoff. Moreover, the inflow from 
other sources was represented by the uncertainty of IRF, SP, and GWinflow. Outflow comprised 
uncertainties of pumping and groundwater outflow.

5.3.1.2 Parameters of the hydrologic cycle 
Based on average monthly precipitation and temperature measurements, the monthly evapotranspiration 
ET resulted in a water loss of 58.5 percent of total rainfall. This estimate was in accordance with the 
found values in literature.  
By applying the average total rainfall of Basanti on the Chaturvedi formula, a natural recharge Grainfall 
of 14 percent of the average total rainfall was calculated. The formula was applied on alluvial soils, 
which is also present in West Bengal. However, Basanti has a top clay layer with very low infiltration. 
From coastal areas of West Bengal and Bangladesh, which have the same soil properties, values were 
compared and a recharge rate of 7 percent was assumed as possible. Finally, the value was verified by 
an interview of Prof. Dr. Abhijit Mukherjee from the IIT Kharagpur.  
By applying the Formula ( 6 ), a surface runoff of 683 mm/year, i.e. 37 percent of total rainfall was 
estimated. Moreover, applying previously estimated values on the main equation of the hydrologic cycle, 
Equation ( 1 ), a surface runoff R of 34.5 percent of the mean annual rainfall was calculated and was 
taken for further calculations. By taking into account recharge from other sources (explained later), a 
reduced runoff Rtotal of 26.8 percent was estimated.

113,172 m³/month.

6 DISCUSSION 
The purpose of the present study has been the evaluation of the sustainability of the groundwater 
resources in Basanti, a community block covering 380 km² land in the Sundarban area of West Bengal. 
6.1 Underground hydrogeology and its interaction with the surface  
For this purpose, knowledge about the present aquifer-aquitard framework in the study area was 
obtained. Lithologic data was analyzed by a hydrostratigraphic model and the outcome was shown by 
transects presented in Section 4.1.1. The profiles indicate the presence of alternating sequences of clay 
and sand layers, wherein clay represented an aquitard and sand an aquifer. In total, five potential aquifers 
and six aquitards varying in thickness are discovered in the subsurface. The top layer is marked by a 15 
to 30 m thick clay layer that is present throughout Basanti. In the South of Basanti, this layer thickens 
to such an extent that it partly disconnects the first aquifer layer. However, throughout the rest of Basanti 
this first aquifer layer has an average thickness of 15 m and is constantly enclosed by an upper and a 
lower clay layer. Also, connections between aquifers occur and are observed between the second and 
the third aquifer in the North-South profile. In a depth of 140 m bgl to 200 m bgl, the largest clay layer 
is found. According to the North-South profile, this aquitard partly connects to an underlying aquitard 
thus, disconnecting the fourth aquifer along the passage from Basanti mainland and its island. 
Furthermore, all four profiles indicate the largest aquifer in a depth range between 260 and 320 m bgl. 
As minor lithologs only recorded sediments past 300 m, it can only be assumed that there is a sixth clay 
layer from a depth of between 310 and 320 m bgl. 
The aforementioned results are in accordance with a study published by Mukherjee (2006), who 
observed that the hydrostratigraphy of the southern South 24 Parganas is dominated by multiple 
aquitards. Moreover, by means of interviews with farmers, Ashur (2012) determined the depth of applied 
irrigation pumps to be between 200 and 240 m. The determined range goes with the results obtained 
from the hydrostratigraphic model and is equal to the fourth aquifer interpolated by the model. 
Moreover, hand pumps are installed between 280 and 320 m bgl. It is equal to the depth of the fifth 
aquifer from the hydrostratigraphic model thus, corresponding well to the model’s outcome. 
In regards to an undertaken wave-response analysis of measured water level data from tube wells and 
tide gauges, it allowed conclusions on the characteristics of the aquifer system and its interaction with 
the surface water bodies. According to Voss (2016), tides can communicate with water pressures below 
clay layers, even if there is no direct connection (i.e. no break in the clay layer). The tides change the 
weight on top of the clay and the unbroken clay transmits this weight change (called effective stress) 
directly to the fresh aquifer below. This effective stress is guided by the difference in total stress and 
==PAGE:79==
6 DISSCUSION 
 
DTU ENVIRONMENT 
65 
pore pressure. In soils, total stress is produced by weight of the soil and any other external loads such as 
free water or the weight of tidal waves. Through the loading and unloading of the aquifer skeleton by 
water level fluctuation of the surface water bodies, the resulting wave migration leads to water level 
response in wells of confined aquifers (Halbert, 1970).  
In Basanti, measured water levels follow the movement of river heads and show a direct correspondence 
to the change in river head over time. Moreover, taken tube well water heads indicate a certain 
attenuation to the river amplitude, which differs from tube well to tube well and most likely occurs due 
to different depths, distances to the river, and characteristics of their hydrogeologic environment. The 
observed response may already indicate a confined nature of the aquifer below Basanti. By means of a 
wave response analysis of measured water level a more comprehensive description of the aquifer was 
possible. According to fitted storativity values, the aquifers show confinement except for the aquifer in 
Goranbose. Here, a leaky/semiconfined aquifer is found. In addition to the adjusted storativtiy, the 
adjusted transmissivity Tfit is at the lower boundary of the range as stated by Mukherjee (2007a). In 
general, a transmissivity of around 1300 m/day describes a good aquifer for water well abstraction 
(Stewart, 2003). Following Table 4 in Section 5.1.2, obtained values are only half of the general 
transmissivity used for pumping. As Basanti is located in a depositional active zone of very fine sand 
and clay sediments, the interconnected pore spaces are likely to be smaller than of standard sediments. 
A smaller interconnected pore space may explain a lower transmissivity hence, a lower rate of flow 
towards a well. Subsequently, the values are acceptable and show that in the subsurface of Basanti only 
slow groundwater flows may be present. Moreover, the calculated specific storativity, as well as, the 
hydraulic conductivity, indicate aquifer sediments in the range of clay and sandy gravel. By taking a 
closer look on the hydraulic conductivity, the resulting aquifer sediment range between sand, i.e. 
21 m/day and very fine sand, i.e. 10 m/day. The obtained horizontal conductivity aligns to the applied 
conductivity from the groundwater flow model developed by Michael (2007a). Here, a value of 
14.7 m/day was chosen best to reflect the general flow of the southern Delta. Finally, the aquifer 
diffusivity, as well as, the non-dimensional response time indicate a quick response of the aquifers to 
the occurring flood wave from the river, which is also in accordance with the curves of taken water level 
measurements compared to the associated river heads.  
All computed and resulting values are within the range stated in the literature, except for the hand pump 
in Bermajur I. Here, two different values for the best fitting type curve of both, the adjusted storage 
coefficient, and the adjusted transmissivity were found. The first storage coefficient indicates a confined 
aquifer with a very low transmissivity, i.e. 21.60 m/day and a low conductivity of 0.3 m/day. The second 
found storage coefficient shows a semiconfined/unconfined aquifer behavior, i.e. 0.07. Its adjusted 
transmissivity is 720 m²/day which is in the range of found values in the literature. Also, the horizontal 
conductivity reflects a rather correct value of 10 m/day indicating very fine sand. Although the hand 
pump in Bermajur is only 50 m away from the river and a river head amplitude of 1.8 m is present, the 
==PAGE:80==

observed hand pump amplitude only accounts for 0.45 m and thus may be too low for being a confined 
aquifer. Subsequently, a semi or unconfined aquifer might be supposed for the aquifer at the hand pump 
in Bermajur I. 
6.2 Present groundwater situation 
Within this mentioned aquifer-aquitard framework water is in continuous movement. On Basanti Island, 
water level measurements from four regions between 2015 and 2016 were analyzed. All regions indicate 
a sufficient recharge during the monsoon period which was above or at the same water level of the year 
before. Therefore, the water storage tends to be recharged from April and stops with the onset of 
pumping in December/January. This may indicate that the deeper aquifers get sufficient recharge from 
layers above and from groundwater flow in the deeper aquifers. 
Moreover, it can also be seen that the gram panchayats Jotishpur, Bharatgarh, and Nafarganj have higher 
recharge rates than Jharkhali. It may be the result from applied tube wells in the regions. The higher the 
installed number of tube wells in a gram panchayat, the higher the recharge rate. Due to pumping, 
vertical gradients might have increased and might led to an increase in recharge. This is also in 
accordance with the groundwater flow model from Michael (2009a), who observed a recharge rate of 
7 cm/year in the predevelopment case (with no pumping) and 25 cm/year in the base case with pumping.  
On a regional scale, water level distributions throughout South 24 Parganas within 2013 were analyzed 
in order to inform about their annual fluctuations, as well as, about the direction of groundwater flow 
by means of a hydraulic gradient. The results indicate that in all four measurement periods in 2013 a 
hydraulic gradient between the region of Kolkata and Basanti is present. Going southeastward from high 
water heads in the Kolkata region to low ones in the region around Basanti thus, leading to a water flow 
towards Basanti. The gradient goes southeastward throughout the year. It hereby increases in the dry 
months, as well as, decreases in the wet months. Despite extensive pumping in Kolkata, it indicates that 
a certain amount of water may still flow into the direction of Basanti and, most likely, drain into the Bay 
of Bengal. 
By focusing on water levels of different time periods within a year, it is seen that an increase in all water 
levels during wet months occur and thus indicate a present recharge of the area. With the onset of 
pumping in the dry months, these water levels decrease again, especially around the study area. Here, 
the interpolation showed that water levels from November until March were rated negative, with March 
being the worst. However, caution is required by interpreting interpolated water levels in the region of 
the study area. Negative water levels in most stages of the year may already indicate that groundwater 
resources are unsustainable. However, gained results are dependent on a proper measurement and 
processing of water level data. Moreover, the use of a DEM with a grid size of 30 m x 30 m, does not 
account for differences in height within the raster. Pumps, like in Basanti, are often installed on a 
platform with varies in height between 0.5 m and 1 m. Furthermore, pumps are most often located on 
==PAGE:81==
6 DISSCUSION 
 
DTU ENVIRONMENT 
67 
roads near fields, which are situated lower than the roads, i.e. up to 1 m. This, a DEM cannot reflect in 
the data, and an uncertainty of at least 2 m has to be taken into account. For this purpose it can only be 
said that a certain groundwater flow from Kolkata to Basanti is present with a higher gradient in dry 
months getting lower in the wet months. Moreover, it can be concluded that the negative water levels 
found around Basanti may signify a critical state of the water resources and that a further decline in 
water levels might have severe consequences for the population living downstream by e.g. intrusion of 
saltwater. 
In a second analysis, water level measurements throughout South 24 Parganas over the past 19 years 
were examined in order to answer whether the area around Basanti is facing a decline in water storage. 
According to the gathered results, a decline in water levels in the region of the study area is present. The 
time when the decline occurs is dependent on the distance to Kolkata region. No decline was found in 
Kolkata region. In the first and second zone, a decrease in water levels started in 2009 and 2006, 
respectively. In the last zone, which covers the study area, a decline from 1996 or at least from 2001 is 
present depending on the location of the measurement. It may indicate that the decline downstream has 
happened much earlier than in regions upstream. Moreover, it may show that the aquifers downstream 
are put under high pressure leading to decrease in the water storage. An earlier decline downstream may 
have several possible reasons: First, an increased outflow might be present compared to a steady inflow 
from adjoining areas. This scenario is possible as within the last decade the installation of tube wells 
strongly increased in the study area that would explain a higher outflow; A second possible scenario 
might be an increased outflow, as well as, and decreased inflow. A decrease in inflow might be explained 
by Section 2.3.4 which showed a decrease in the water available storage in the Lower Ganges region. 
As it is the major draining system for the coastal areas, it might also have an effect on the situation 
downstream and might lead to smaller inflow. As the start of groundwater depletion varies spatially, it 
may indicate that the aquifers are drained unequally despite the presence of a southeastward hydraulic 
gradient. It may also show that the aquifers not only get the water from southeastward but also from the 
north, and that the water streams in the vicinity of Kolkata have more water than the ones more 
southeastward. 
However, it can be summarized that groundwater levels in the vicinity of the study area have declined 
and may have an effect on Basanti as well. 
In order to assess the present water situation with occurring water flows in the study area, a total water 
balance was developed and is interpreted by the sustainable yield of the groundwater resource. The 
sustainable yield represents the equilibrium of storage over time that allows acceptable levels of stress 
and protects dependent ecological, social, and environmental values (Rudestam, 2015). 
In Basanti, the first months of the year are characterized by pumping and lead to a decrease in storage 
to -121 mm by March. After the irrigation period the aquifer systems seem to relax and increase the 
storage to an accumulated storage surplus of 151 mm, i.e. 51.5 million m³, by the end of the year. Hence,

In order to allow conclusions on the deeper freshwater bearing aquifer between 250 m and 320 m bgl, a

recharge rate seemed to have a great influence and therefore three recharge scenarios were developed 
for the deep water budget. 
Both, the scenario with an average vertical recharge rate of 4.5 percent, as well as, the probable 
conservative rate of 2 percent, indicated that pumping exceeded inflows in the first three months. It may 
already imply that in these months the groundwater resource is heavily depleted and may indicate an 
unsustainable use. With April and the end of pumping, the aquifer relaxes and increases. Hereby, the 
accumulated storage of 4.5 percent gets positive between May and June, whereas the one with 2 percent 
only between August and October. Under least probable conditions and a vertical recharge rate of 
7 percent shows sustainable conditions as inflows outweighed outflow throughout the year. 
The different outcomes may show that a sustainable condition of the deeper aquifer depend on the 
vertical recharge rate. Moreover, the presented deep aquifer balance for average and probable 
conservative recharge rates also indicate that the groundwater use may be unsustainable. 
 
To conclude, Basanti’s aquifer may be provided with sufficient water during monsoonal season, but the 
distribution is unequal as the top aquifers may receive more than the lower ones. Moreover, the first 
aquifer may be sufficiently recharged by surface infiltration, whereas the deeper aquifer relies on deep 
vertical recharge. This vertical recharge may provide the aquifers with enough water at the moment but 
may vanish with an increase in pumping. Furthermore, with no agricultural pumping the deeper aquifer 
may be considered as sustainable on local scale. 
Finally, it has to be noted that the outcome of the water balance has to be taken with caution. Due to an 
uncertainty of 47 percent of the recharge value, it has a great influence on the accumulated storage value 
as it is also dependent on the surface recharge. In order to allow more precise assessments of the current 
situation of the groundwater resource, conclusions on the conductivity and by this on the saltwater 
intrusion were done.

6.3 Current state of saltwater intrusion 
In general, saltwater intrusion occurs in coastal regions. Due to density differences, freshwater shifts 
over saltwater. Given this, freshwater flows prevent saltwater from coming into the aquifer. However, 
natural heterogeneity in the subsurface leading to certain flow conditions, as well as, extensive pumping 
may facilitate saltwater intrusion. Based on an interview with Mukherjee (2016), a vertically bell-shaped 
saltwater intrusion is likely in the western Bengal basin, see Figure 48. 
 
Higher groundwater flows at the top, also observed with the groundwater flow model present in this 
work, may prevent the saltwater from inflow. Although water flow is very low at the bottom, the 
freshwater pressure from the total water mass extending land inward to the recharge zones is high and 
may still prevent saltwater to intrude. Between the top and the bottom aquifer, the figure shows an 
intrusion of saline water, which is in accordance with drillers reporting the presence of brackish water 
layers in depths from 50 to 250 m. In this underground region, a high heterogeneity with partly 
disconnected aquifers and depth differences of aquitards over tens of meters might have resulted in a 
deceleration of groundwater flow. Paired with a lower depth comparable to less water pressure, the water 
flows might have a lower total water pressure compared to the seawater pressure, resulting in an 
intrusion of saltwater. 
The degree of salinity being the main indicator for a vulnerable groundwater resource was examined by 
electric conductivity measurements throughout Basanti in order to inform about possible sea water 
intrusion. The comparison of taken measurements on Basanti Island from April/May 2016 with ones 
from July/August 2012, found in the report by Ashur (2012), showed only minor differences in electric 
conductivity. The average conductivity in the southern part decreased by 200 µS/cm, whereas in the 
central part of the island it increased by the same value. In the North of the island only some areas 
showed an increase to about 200 µS/cm, the rest remained constant. Hence, the electric conductivity has 
not much changed over the past four years. The test also indicates that in the depth of hand pumps

6.4 Sustainability assessment 
In the following, the development of the potential groundwater storage is interpreted by means of a 
sustainability assessment. Three pumping scenarios over a time period of 10 years are hereby assessed: 
First, an increase of pumping by +3 percent of the irrigated area from tube wells, which is equal to the 
amount of pumps installed annually at 300 m, i.e. 18 pumps. By using a pump efficiency of 80 percent, 
an average pumping rate of 62 m³/h, and 10 hours of pumping for 30 days, a monthly discharge of 
267,840 m³/month is added to the initial amount of discharge, i.e. 669,600 m³/month. The second 
scenario assumed no changes of the irrigated area from well at 300m depth. A third scenario runs through 
a dismantling of 18 irrigation tube wells, i.e. -3 percent annually. 
As the deeper aquifer is dependent on the vertical recharge, the assessment is undertaken for the average, 
i.e. 4.5 percent, and the probable conservative case, i.e. 2 percent. The least conservative was not 
assessed as the value may be too high for the high heterogeneous aquifer-aquitard underground.

2 percent, are presented in Figure 49 and Figure 50, respectively.

6.4.1 Approach to mitigate stress on the groundwater resource in Basanti 
The groundwater resource in Basanti is under stress having led to a critical state of becoming 
unsustainable in the near future. For this purpose mitigation strategies addressing the problem of 
groundwater depletion have to be introduced. Groundwater depletion can have more reasons, but often 
occurs due to extensive irrigation practices over years. In Basanti, irrigation already contributes to 
groundwater use by 41 percent and thus raises the absolute necessity to involve farmers in the strategy 
process. In the following, two possible strategies, a change in the cropping pattern and the use of surface 
water, are suggested that may have the possibility to significantly reduce the amount of groundwater 
required for agricultural purposes. 
6.4.1.1 Change in cropping pattern 
From January until March Boro paddy is used to be the dominating crop, i.e. 84 percent of all cultivated 
crops. During this time, farmers utilize three to four times the amount of water than for an equivalent 
vegetable cultivation. Hereby, groundwater is considered to account for 100 percent in Boro cultivation.

6.4.1.2 Pond farming 
Another solution is a change of the water source for irrigation practices. Ponds can store rainfall during 
monsoon season. In West Bengal, the excavation of ponds is subsidised by the government, however 
just a few farmers applying for the fund get it. Depending on the welfare of the family, some of the 
farmers have enough money to finance digging out a pond without governmental support. Land shaping 
concepts can hereby help to motivate farmers as it provides an increase of income. In a long term run 
farmers would also benefit from digging out their own pond when combining the purpose of irrigation 
with aquaculture. Following Table 9, aquaculture is possible throughout the year and can be combined

To conclude, the scheme is a good and adequate solution and may contribute towards reducing the 
amount of groundwater by using pond water for irrigation. However, main limitation factor for 
realisation is the dependence on governmental support.  
6.4.1.3 Rainwater harvesting 
In regards to drinking water supply, an alternative source is the collection of rainwater. Hereby, a rooftop 
provide the most adequate catchment area as it is relatively clean and facilitates rainwater collection. 
Due to natural filtration processes rainwater itself is considered to be pure. However, it can be 
contaminated by air pollutants while passing the atmosphere or bird faeces. Hence, if it is used for 
drinking purpose an adequate treatment must be ensured. Slow-sand filters or sand-gravel filters reduce 
the amount of bacteria and ensure a safe drinking water source. In Joygopalpur, two rainwater-
harvesting-systems are utilized on school level which shall also promote the collection of rainwater to 
familiarize people with the alternative water source. 60 years ago, people in the Basanti area were used 
to consume rainwater as hand pumps were not established yet. People collected rainwater by putting a 
pot under a cloth fixed on 4 sticks. Nowadays, on household level, a few percentage collect rainwater 
from the rooftop using simple methods. A metal sheet is bended and fixed on the roof and used as a 
gutter. A pot is placed underneath the roof and rainwater is collected and used for household, hygienic 
and cooking purposes (Tauber, 2016).

6.5 Limitations 
In the course of this report some limitations may have affected the outcome. During data survey and 
investigations on-site, a degree of uncertainty goes with the gathered data. It had to be relied on 
statements on the lithologic data from the driller, on available water level data for Basanti and South 24 
Parganas, as well as, on made assumptions for the hydrologic parameters. However, the uncertainty was 
accepted as no verification of the needed data was possible. If planning a more detailed work on Basanti 
it can be overcome by collaborating with universities in and around Kolkata which might help to provide 
important tools which enable the exploration of the subsurface in more detail and allow more precise 
measurements on the hydrologic parameters. Moreover, in this report a published regional-scale 
groundwater flow model was applied that didn’t allow for recharge comparison with calculated values. 
In order to be able to compute the regional flow no adjustments on the grid were made. Subsequently, 
recharge beneath the raster of 5 km x 5 km, which was used in the regional scale model, was not 
accounted in the zonal budget. As the local flows system is guided by local topography, the use of a 
finer grid of 100 mx100 m with a good topographic model would allow to capture the local flow system. 
Finally, the recharge from the river was not accounted in the water budget that might have an effect on 
the total water balance.

7 CONCLUSION 
Aim of the master thesis was the assessment of the sustainability of the groundwater resource in Basanti, 
West Bengal. For this purpose an extensive collection of literature data on the subsurface including a 
large scale modeling of the upstream aquifer was conducted. Moreover, field work on site provided data 
of the region’s geology and water supply. Hereby, direct measurements of water levels and electric 
conductivity were undertaken throughout Basanti. In regards to the characterization of the subsurface, 
the data was examined by means of a hydrostratigraphic model and a wave response analysis. In 
addition, the present water availability was investigated by a water balance of the study area, as well as, 
by long- and short term water level analysis of the region in and around the study Basanti. Ultimately, 
conclusions on the sustainability were completed by comparison of electric conductivities of the study 
area and by assessing the future groundwater resource development of the deep fresh water bearing 
aquifer in regards to three pumping scenarios. The application of aforementioned components led to the 
final conclusion that the groundwater resource of Basanti is under stress. Thereby, the application of 
pumping from deep aquifers for agricultural purposes is the major influencing factor which is supposed 
to increase within the next decade and thus may put the resource under higher pressure. Despite a present 
groundwater flow to Basanti and sufficient annual recharge in the area, long term water levels have 
indicated decreasing groundwater levels around the study area over the past years. Being close to zero, 
the low groundwater levels raise the danger of saltwater intrusion which might increase within the next 
years when following the trend. Especially the coastal blocks, among them also Basanti, are situated in 
an alternating sand-clay layer formation that may result in a deceleration of flows within medium depth 
and thus may facilitate the appealed intrusion of saltwater. In the subsurface of the study area five 
confined aquifers are present that show a hydraulic connection with the subsurface but only the top and 
the bottom contain freshwater. Water availability of the top aquifers seem to be sufficient over the 
hydrologic year as recharge from subsurface is high with the onset of monsoon. However, the deep 
aquifer is dependent on a deep vertical recharge and here pumped water is similar to or even exceeds 
inflows in the dry months. Finally, undertaken conductivity measurements throughout Basanti indicated 
no obvious trend of saltwater intrusion over the last four years. Only in the North higher values were 
observed and may need future monitoring.  
It can therefore be noted, that found results indicate a critical state of groundwater resource in Basanti. 
Due to uncertainties of the hydrologic parameters a complete assessment of the sustainability is limited. 
In order to make statements about medium- and long term development also in the context of 
sustainability, it is important for following research, to introduce monthly monitoring of electric 
conductivity throughout Basanti over the next years. Furthermore, site-specific solutions for irrigation 
and drinking water supply may improve the situation and mitigate the pressure on the deep aquifer.

A.3 The principle of wash boring 
The technique “wash boring” is most common in Basanti and is used as it does not need heavy machines, 
which are foremost not available, and thus are cheap compared to other techniques.  
In Figure A. 4, the structure for wash boring is presented. 
 
At the top of the boring walls are supported by a drilling casing (1) to prevent the hole from collapsing. 
Moreover, water is pumped from a nearby canal or pond down through a drilling pipe (2). At the end of 
this drilling pipe is a cutting tool (3). The entire construction is supported by a large tripod. 
The drilling itself is done by moving the drilling pipe by hand. Thereby, the pipe is turned back and 
forth, raised and lowered, in order to loosen the sediment layers in the depth. As water is pumped down 
the applied water pressure at the cutting washes out loosened sediments and bring it up to the surface. 
During borehole construction the borehole itself is stabilized by the surrounding water pressure.  If put 
on hold, cow faeces are used to prevent borehole sides from collapsing. If drilling continues water 
washes cow faeces out (Ashur, 2012; Naskar, 2016).

B.3.2.1 Groundwater flow model 
The applied groundwater flow model is used in previous studies by Michael (Michael, 2009a; Michael, 
2009b). The model’s geometry is shown in Figure B. 4. 
 
In order to understand the flow system in the deep (greater than ~150 m) part of the Bengal basin aquifer 
system, which may be regional in scale, Michael defined the model’s extent to the natural boundaries of 
the deep system. By means of several deep boreholes, the bottom of the flow model was identified. Here, 
the bottom is marked by the Miocene Boka Bil Formation where shale exists, and Precambrian basement 
is chosen where the shale is absent or not identified. This boundary is assumed as no flow boundary.

B.3.2.3 Estimate on the number of tube wells in Basanti 
At first, the number of deep tube wells is estimated by tube well densities over the area of Basanti Island. 
These densities are the result of processed data gathered from technical engineers of JGVK. The 
engineers have recorded every pump in the area of Basanti Island. In figure XXX, a map of Basanti 
Island representing the locations of already recorded irrigation deep tube wells is shown. No data has 
been recorded yet in the northern part of the island and the area is therefore marked in angled lines. The 
area of no wells accounted for 21 percent of the total area of Basanti Island, i.e. 4,321 ha. The southern 
part, which encloses the tube wells, comprises an area of 12,730 ha and contains 218 irrigation pumps. 
This leads to a tube well density of 0.017 pumps/ha. The tube well density is similar to the one found in 
Basanti in 2010 from the Agricultural census (2011). Here, 499 pumps are distributed over a total 
holding area of 32,424 ha, resulting in a tube well density of 0.015 pumps/ha. 
In a next step the tube well density found on Basanti Island is extrapolated over the entire populated 
land area of Basanti, i.e. 34,206 ha. It gives a total number of 581 deep tube wells for the entire Basanti 
block.
