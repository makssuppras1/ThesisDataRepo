Abstract 
 
A study on the design of a spiral heat exchanger immersed in a thermal storage tank 
for application in a CSP plant for heat and electricity production. The system consists of 
a parabolic trough field with thermal storage connected to both an organic Rankine 
cycle unit and the district heating network, so that solar power is used for both for 
electricity generation and providing heat for district heating. The heat exchanger 
connects the thermal oil running through the solar collector loop with the molten salt 
used as storage medium, for what external shell and tube heat exchangers have been 
usually employed. An analysis of heat exchanger design parameters and the effect of 
varying them on the heat exchange capacity rate, thermal fluid temperatures and 
pressure drops is made. Results showed higher spiral lengths result in higher heat 
exchange capacities, while increasing at the same time the pressure drop. A higher 
pressure drop along the spirals implies more pumping power is necessary and 
therefore an increase in investment and operation costs. The study of maximizing the 
heat exchange capacity rate should therefore be done alongside the analysis of the 
pressure drop in the spirals. The convenience of analyzing economical parameters to 
secure a convenient heat exchanger design is stated, as well as setting an adequate 
control strategy due to the unpredictability of solar power.

1. Introduction 
This Master Thesis falls under the Full Spectrum Solar Energy (FSSE) project, which 
aims to contribute to Danish carbon emissions reduction targets by developing a 
concentrated solar power (CSP) energy system with thermal storage coupled with an 
organic Rankine cycle (ORC) for both district heating and electricity production. The 
aim is that this technology can contribute to reducing district heating emissions and 
provide coverage for peak hour demand, which is often covered by fossil fuel-based 
systems. 
Coupling solar thermal energy counting with storage with ORCs is useful to support 
solar energy systems, especially for energy optimization and planification given the 
unpredictability of solar energy.   
A prototype of the system is to be installed and connected to local distribution 
networks. The FSSE project is formed by two systems: on the one hand, the 
concentrated solar power system counting with parabolic trough collectors with thermal 
energy storage; on the other hand, a high temperature ORC to produce both electricity 
and heat. The considered technologies have been previously tested.  
The system is flexible and scalable for different number of solar collectors and variable 
heat exchangers and ORC designs.

1.1. Literature review 
Current urge to tackle and mitigate climate change issue has resulted in pressures to 
official institutions to take action and favour the transition to renewable energy sources. 
Renewable energy sources are clean and do not imply release of greenhouse gases, 
but they impose another challenge to the energy system [1][2]. Renewables such as 
solar or wind depend on meteorological conditions, which are variable and hard to 
predict with a high certainty level. This leads to the convenience of storage systems to 
determine an appropriate control strategy to ensure the optimal performance of these 
systems [3][4].  
==PAGE:23==
CSP solar energy plant for electricity and heat production                             3 
Classification: Confidential 
Solar thermal energy, and more specifically concentrated solar power (CSP) plants are 
characterized for concentrating the solar radiation on a receiver by means of mirrors, 
generating heat [5]. This solar heat can be stored in a thermal fluid such as molten salt 
or thermal oil, or used to produce electricity by, as an example, driving a steam turbine 
[6]. In the case of parabolic trough CSP technology, the mirrors present a parabolic 
shape that concentrates the solar radiation onto a receiver tube located in the focal line 
of the parabola [7]. The receiver tube is filled with a thermal fluid that is heated up by 
means of the concentrated solar radiation [8]. 
When solar thermal energy is coupled with thermal energy storage, the flexibility of the 
system is enhanced, allowing for its functioning in periods with zero solar irradiance [9]. 
When the thermal fluid flowing through the collector field is thermal oil, which is the 
most common in the case of parabolic trough, its storage becomes challenging due to 
the high costs of the oil and the requirement of pressurized storage vessels. For this 
matter, molten salt are usually used for storing energy in solar thermal systems [10]. In 
these cases, the thermal storage fluid is not the same as that flowing through the solar 
collector field, which raises the issue of choosing the best way to transfer heat from 
one to another, making heat exchangers an important part of solar thermal energy 
systems. A heat exchanger is a device that transfers heat between two fluids at 
different temperatures [11] [12]. The application field of heat exchangers is wide, from 
small industrial processes to large power plants, and many types of heat exchangers 
can be distinguished based on criteria such as heat transfer process and mechanism, 
device construction and geometry or fluid arrangement. Choosing an appropriate heat 
exchanger for a determined application usually aims at maximizing the heat transfer 
between both fluids. Some of the main types of heat exchanger used today are [13]: 
• 
Shell and tube heat exchanger. This heat exchanger design comprises a sealed 
cylindrical vessel or shell filled with one of the fluids, while the other fluid flows 
through one or several tubes. It is the most widely used heat exchanger type for 
industrial processes [14]. Figure 2 shows a type of shell and tube heat 
exchanger.  
==PAGE:24==

• 
Double pipe heat exchangers. Double pipe heat exchangers are a type of shell 
and tube heat exchanger consisting of concentric tubes, one larger (shell) and 
one or more smaller tubes. One of the fluids flows through the larger while the 
other flows through the smaller one(s). They are usually designed with counter-
current flow arrangement. Figure 3 shows a schematic drawing of a double pipe

• 
Plate heat exchangers.  These exchangers are composed of many thin plates 
that are slightly separated and contain fluid passages for heat transfer. Its main 
advantage is the large heat transfer area shown by the plates, allowing for high 
heat transfer coefficient and good efficiencies.  Figure 4 shows a schematic 
drawing of a plate heat exchanger [17].

Spiral tube heat exchangers. Spiral tube heat exchanger consists of one or

transfer fluids, while the coil(s) contains the other one. Figure 5 shows a

• 
Scraped surface heat exchangers. This type of heat exchanger provides a high 
heat transfer rate with nearly constant heat flux and a great increase in flow-
mixing. It is more commonly used in the food, chemical and pharmaceutical 
industries [19].  
• 
Boilers, evaporators and condensers. In these heat exchangers one of the 
fluids goes through a phase change: liquid to gas in the case of boilers and 
evaporators and gas to liquid in the case of condensers [20].

Heat exchangers and storage systems for solar thermal application have been 
developed before in several studies. He et al (2020) highlighted how state-of-the-art 
thermal storage for concentrating solar power systems consisted of two-tank storage 
with molten salt as storage medium [21]. Maliki et al (2021) made a simulation of a 
parabolic trough plant with two-tank thermal storage. The collector fluid was thermal oil, 
while the thermal storage fluid was molten salt, so a heat exchanger located outside of 
the storage tank was used to transfer heat from the oil to the salt [22]. Bonilla et al 
(2015) studied the experimental calibration of heat transfer and thermal losses in a 
shell and tube heat exchanger used to charge and discharge a storage tank in a solar 
thermal plant with parabolic trough collectors.  The plant included both a hot and a cold 
storage tank and a thermal oil to molten salt heat exchanger. The chosen heat 
exchanger for the plant was located outside of the storage tank and consisted of a shell 
and tube with F-type shell, which is the most common for commercial parabolic trough 
application, with molten salt in the shell side and thermal oil in the tube side [23]. 
Zaversky et al (2014) made a transient numerical model of a multi-pass shell and tube 
heat exchanger for a concentrated solar power plant with thermal oil on the tube side 
and molten salt on the shell side. Again, the storage system of this study was an 
external heat exchanger and two storage tanks (a hot molten salt tank and a cold one) 
[24]. Maliki et al (2016) made a dynamic simulation of an existing parabolic trough plant 
in the south of Spain. The solar collector fluid was thermal oil and the plant counted 
with a two-tank thermal storage system using molten salt as storage fluid with a heat 
exchanger used to charge and discharge the tank [25]. Hermann et al (2004) studied a 
two-tank storage system in parabolic trough solar power plants, with molten salt as a 
storage medium given the high costs implied by storing the heat transfer fluid in the 
solar collector loop, usually thermal oil. The heat transfer between thermal oil and 
molten salt was made with an external shell and tube heat exchanger, with thermal oil 
in the tube side and molten salt in the shell side [26]. 
Although the most common choice of thermal oil to molten salt heat exchanger for solar 
thermal application is an external shell and tube heat exchanger, another solution for 
solar thermal plants can be submerging the heat exchanger inside the storage tank [27] 
[28]. These heat exchangers are used for both charge and discharge of the tank [29].  
Most applications of immersed heat exchangers to the date are for small scale solar 
thermal application such as domestic hot water tanks [30] [31], so the implementation 
of this type of heat exchanger solution on larger solar thermal plants implies a great 
challenge today.

The present study aims to achieve a submerged spiral heat exchanger design suitable 
for a solar thermal plant for heat and power generation. The plant is composed of a 
parabolic trough field and an organic Rankine cycle, also counting with a thermal 
storage tank. The parabolic trough field uses thermal oil as a working fluid, while the 
storage tank is filled with molten salt, for what the use of an appropriate heat 
exchanger connecting both fluids is necessary [32].  
 
1.2. Problem definition, aim of the study and motivation 
The present study presents a concentrated solar thermal plant for district heating and 
electricity generation within the FSSE Project. Figure 6 shows the scheme of the 
reference plant, which includes a parabolic trough solar collector field formed by 9 
collectors with a total area of 274.5 m2. The solar field working fluid is heat transfer 
thermal oil manufactured by Paratherm [33], which enters the upper storage tank once 
heated up to high temperatures. The storage tank is filled with molten salt, which was 
chosen as working fluid to reduce storage costs. The heat needs to be transferred from 
the thermal oil to the molten salt within the tank, for what a spiral heat exchanger is 
chosen. A spiral heat exchanger consists of a set of parallel heat exchanger spirals 
submerged in the storage tank. The fluid that flows through the spirals is therefore 
thermal oil.  
 
Figure 6. Reference solar thermal plant for district heating and electricity generation. 
 
==PAGE:28==
8                             CSP solar energy plant for electricity and heat production                                                              
Classification: Confidential 
The solar heat is used either for district heating, for what another heat exchanger is 
necessary to transfer heat to the water; or to generate electricity in an Organic Rankine 
Cycle (ORC), which is made by discharging the storage tank through the spiral heat 
exchanger. An ORC works in a similar way as a conventional steam Rankine Cycle, 
but the main difference is that its working fluid employs organic compounds, such as 
hydrocarbons or refrigerants as a working fluid. These working fluids boil at lower 
temperature and pressure compared to steam [34][35]. This makes the ORC a reliable 
option when considering renewable heat sources such as solar thermal [36]. The 
discharge of the storage tank is used in the ORC to heat the refrigerant fluid until 
evaporation for it to then spin the turbine that generates electricity. The condenser of 
the ORC is fed with water coming from the district heating network at a low 
temperature that, after being used for condensing the ORC fluid, goes back to the 
network at a higher temperature.  
The thermal oil exits the storage tank at a lower temperature after the heat has been 
transferred to the molten salt and goes back to the solar collector field to be heated up 
again. 
The present study focuses on the study of the most adequate spiral heat exchanger 
design to meet the energy system needs. For that, different parameters have been 
analysed for each of the proposed designs to determine the most feasible and 
convenient one. The design is established by selecting inner and outer diameter of the 
pipes, number of spirals, spiral length, and spiral geometry and distribution within the 
tank. The main parameters to analyse to determine the feasibility of each of the design 
options are heat exchange capacity rate, forward and return thermal oil temperatures, 
and pressure drop within heat exchanger spirals.  
The importance of this study lies on the necessity of establishing relationships between 
different heat exchanger design characteristics and thermodynamic parameters to ease 
the selection of an optimal spiral heat exchanger design that can be adapted to an 
existing storage tank. The novelty implied in the study consists of the selection of a 
heat exchanger that is submerged in the storage tank, as external heat exchangers 
have been the most common choice for solar thermal application to the date. An 
internal heat exchanger presents challenges that are not present when an external 
heat exchanger is selected, as it must be adapted to the capacity and geometry of the 
storage tank, but it can affect positively plant modelling and optimization due to 
important reduction of costs and simplification of the plant. If an appropriate set of 
steps and conditions to be fulfilled by this type of heat exchanger is achieved, this

solution can present higher flexibility and capability to adapt the thermal storage unit to 
the solar collector loop compared to conventional external heat exchangers used in 
concentrated solar power systems.

4.2. Control strategy  
The system is subject to changes in weather conditions throughout the year. The solar 
radiation determines the power delivered by the solar collector field to the heat 
exchanger and therefore the performance of the system is highly dependent on the 
time of the year. To account for these weather-related uncertainties, a control strategy 
is required. 
To search for a possible control strategy for the operation of the system, calculations 
with the design reference year (DRY) have been used and analysed. A DRY is a set of 
hourly weather data comprising a year of data that is built adding the months that 
present data that is close to the typical meteorological variation for each month of the 
year [44]. The DRY is useful to examine the performance of weather-dependent 
systems. In this study, it was used to separate the year in two different periods of six 
months each, called summer period and winter period, respectively. These periods do 
not correspond to real summer or winter and are selected based on the average total 
solar irradiance on the collector surface of each of the months, calculated from the 
DRY data. That is, the 6 months with the highest average total solar irradiance on the 
collector surface form the summer period, while the months corresponding to the 
lowest averages form the winter period. Table 6 shows the average total irradiance on 
the collector surface for each of the months in kWh/m2, considering all the hours within 
each month, even those with zero solar radiation (24 hours a day).

5. Recommendations on spiral dimensions 
The following section describes a design option for the studied heat exchanger spirals 
considering the tank volume constraints stablished. The main reason to do this is to 
analyse whether the heat exchanger could actually be built within feasible tank 
dimensions for the reference system. 
The option studied has been the last design proposal, considering 88 spirals with 150 
m spiral length and 4 and 6 mm inner and outer diameter, respectively. The tank 
volume is 12 m3 and it is assumed to be cylindrical. As the tank height and diameter 
are not known beforehand, they are set to values 3.82 m and 2 m, respectively, making 
the desired 12 m3 volume. 
The spiral length and height are known and equal to 150 m and 3.82 m, respectively. 
To determine the dimensions of the heat exchanger immersed in the storage tank, 
spiral geometry needs to be analysed. The spiral geometry is shown in Figure 98. 
 
Figure 98. Spiral geometry. 
To establish an appropriate and suitable spiral design, some requirements need to be 
met 
• 
The pitch or distance between spiral turns must consider and respect the 
dimensions (i.e., inner and outer diameter) of the pipes. 
• 
The spirals should fit within the storage tank, meaning the spiral height 
should be equal or less than that of the storage and must secure the full 
spiral length. 
==PAGE:75==
CSP solar energy plant for electricity and heat production                             55 
Classification: Confidential 
• 
The total number of spirals, considering the spiral diameter, must fit within 
the storage tank leaving tank volume for the molten salt as well as enough 
space between spirals so that heat transfer between thermal oil and molten 
salt is not compromised. 
The pitch is set to 1.6 cm. This is done considering the outer diameter of the pipes (6 
mm). Given that the pitch covers the distance between spiral turns from centre to 
centre of the pipe, the spacing between pipe surfaces from turn to turn would be 1 cm.  
The number of turns is obtained from dividing the height of the spiral (3.82 m) by the 
pitch (1.62 cm). The number of turns results in 238.75, which is rounded down to 238 
considering the spirals need to fit inside the storage tank. The only thing that is not 
known about the spirals would be the spiral diameter, which is obtained from the spiral 
length formula (Equation 9): 
𝐿= 𝑛√𝜋𝐷𝑠𝑝𝑖𝑟𝑎𝑙+ 𝑝2                                                           [9] 
Being: 
𝐿 the spiral length [m]. 
𝑛 the number of turns in the spiral [-]. 
𝐷𝑠𝑝𝑖𝑟𝑎𝑙 the spiral diameter [m]. 
𝑝 the spiral pitch [m]. 
The diameter is calculated, giving the value 12.1 cm. 
The next concern is finding out whether 88 spirals with the calculated spiral diameter fit 
inside the storage tank taking into account its diameter. To check this, an online 
calculator to check the number of smaller circles that can fit inside a bigger one is used 
[45]. In this case, the smaller circle would be that with diameter equal as the spiral 
diameter (12.1 cm), while the bigger one is that corresponding to the base of the 
cylindrical storage tank (2 m). The calculator shows the number of spirals with that 
diameter that could fit inside the storage tank would be 226 and would occupy 83.27% 
of the base area. This numbers suggest it would be possible to fit the 88 spirals with 
length 150 m in the storage tank while leaving enough space in between spirals to 
favour heat transfer and leave space for the molten salt.

6. Conclusions and further study 
This study focuses on the design and optimization of a spiral heat exchanger 
submerged in the storage tank of a CSP energy plant for electricity and heat 
production. The heat exchanger is in charge of connecting both the solar thermal fluid 
and the storage medium, being thermal oil and molten salt, respectively. The study is 
carried out by presenting and describing several heat exchanger designs and working 
parameters such as volume flow, power delivered by the solar field and temperature of 
the molten salts in the tank. An analysis of the heat exchanger design characteristics in 
relation to the heat exchange capacity rate, thermal oil forward and return 
temperatures, heat transfer coefficients and pressure drop along the spirals is carried 
out to determine the convenience and feasibility of each of the designs for the desired 
application.  
The novelty of the work resides in the choice of a heat exchanger immersed in a 
storage tank to transfer heat between thermal oil and molten salts for solar thermal 
application, given that state-of-the art systems rely on external heat exchangers and 
two-tank storage systems.  
Several conclusions can be extracted from the developed analysis regarding the 
design of the heat exchanger and how the different parameters can affect its 
performance and heat transfer capacity:  
• 
The length of each of the spirals should be maximized to achieve a sufficiently 
high heat exchange capacity rate and therefore enhanced performance of the 
overall system. 
• 
Pressure drop is a parameter that needs to be studied in detail when 
considering a heat exchanger design, as high pressure drops imply a powerful 
pump is needed to make the thermal oil go through the spirals. This is 
especially important given the necessity of having long spirals to favor heat 
exchange capacity, as a higher spiral length implies a higher pressure drop. 
Therefore, these two parameters should be studied in a complementary way. 
• 
The design of the heat exchanger spirals, including spiral length, spiral diameter 
and inner and outer diameter of the pipes must secure the heat exchanger can 
fit inside the storage tank with enough separation between spirals so that the 
heat transfer is not compromised, while, at the same time, leaving enough 
volume for the molten salt side. 
==PAGE:78==
58                             CSP solar energy plant for electricity and heat production                                                              
Classification: Confidential 
• 
Economical parameters should be evaluated alongside the analysis of heat 
exchange capacity rates to determine a convenient heat exchanger design. 
• 
An adequate control strategy should be applied to the performance of the 
system based on the unpredictability of solar thermal systems. This implies an 
analysis of weather conditions throughout a whole year of operation. In the case 
studied, it was concluded that the ORC would only operate during months with 
high solar irradiance, based on the needs of the ORC unit which implies a high 
power and thermal oil temperature must be secured for its functioning. In 
months with lower solar irradiance, therefore, the system should be used for 
district heating, given lower temperatures of the solar collector fluid are 
necessary for this purpose. 
Further study within this field could include an economic analysis to gain a wider 
overview of each design. Economic calculations connect the study with the commercial 
world and help understand the feasibility of carrying out a particular design. This could 
include a study of the commercially available pumps and how both the investment and 
operation costs can vary with different spiral heat exchanger designs. 
A comparative study between the spiral heat exchanger and a conventional external 
heat exchanger solution could also help gain insight on this particular need for this kind 
of energy systems. Different heat exchanger geometries could also be considered and 
compared to be immersed in the storage tank, as well as using other thermal fluids that 
could allow for higher spiral forward and return temperatures.
