Summary
The Internet of Things infrastructure connects billions of devices embedded in
industrial and everyday objects, providing them with the ability to connect and
cooperate with each other. While the possibilities and advancements that come
with this technology are great, so are its challenges, specially the security ones.
Fog computing is a concept in the making, born as the enabling technology
that will help unleash the potential of the IoT. By bringing extra intelligence
and computation closer to the edge of the network it promises to enable novel
applications and deal with the consequences of the almost exponential increase
in the number of connected devices. Its addition to the equation might bring a
new set of tools to help deal with the IoT security problem, as well as its own
set of challenges...
This work examines a previous solution attempt: AntibIoTic, ﬁnding legal issues
that prevent its deployment. Learning from its ﬂaws, a new solution is proposed
within the context of Fog computing, supported by a threat and risk analysis
of the system and a proof of concept showing its feasibility and some of its
functionality.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The Internet of Things (IoT) promises to make our lives easier and more com-
fortable and our businesses more successful by enhancing industrial and every-
day objects with the possibility to connect to the Internet and to each other.
While this technology has many applications, it also comes with its share of
challenges. As companies rush to produce IoT devices to be as cheap as possi-
ble, security is left aside, resulting in a vast quantity of basically unprotected
devices that end up being part of a giant botnet performing malicious activities
such as Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks [KAKA16, KCS17].
After the notorious Mirai attack in October 2016 [Hil16], which performed one
of the largest DDoS attacks ever recorded using a botnet comprised mainly
of IoT devices, several proposals were made to overcome the lack of security
of the current IoT. One of said proposals was AntibIoTic [DDDGM17], which
proposed to use a similar method as the one Mirai used to protect IoT devices.
However, while it could work in practice, its unrestricted deployment would
mean breaking the law.
Fog computing is a recent concept [BMZA12], meant as an intermediate layer
between the end-devices and the Cloud, Fog computing is supposed to help
unleash the potential of the IoT, bringing a solution to some of the most diﬃcult
challenges it faces, such as mobility support and latency problems. With this
new technology also comes a new opportunity to propose a system that may

help mitigate the lack of security of the IoT.
1.1
Objectives
The main goal of the thesis is to provide a new design of the AntibIoTic solution
within the Fog computing context that solves the main ﬂaws of the previous
work, mainly the legal issues that prevent its deployment. As such, the list of
objectives is as follows:
• Study the Internet of Things and Fog computing to build the necessary
background to properly design a system that takes full advantage of the
technology.
• Analyze AntibIoTic, learn of its strong and weak points in order to design
a solution that keeps or improves the good, and mitigates or removes the
bad. For this, we will need to dive into the current EU regulations for
information security related crimes.
• Propose a detailed design of the new AntibIoTic system using Fog com-
puting.
• Provide a small proof of concept showing the feasibility of the design, if
there is enough time.

2.1

The Internet of Things (IoT) is a vital concept to this work. The thesis’ most

==PAGE:18==
6
Theoretical background
2.1.1
Deﬁnition
While the creation of the term is attributed to the Auto-ID Labs at the Mas-
sachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), while they were working on RFID
technology in 1999 [REC15]. The term has evolved since then [WF15] and there
are many deﬁnitions of the Internet of Things. Depending on the approach of
the author, the deﬁnition could be "Internet oriented" focusing on networks
or protocols, "Thing oriented" such as RFID technology or wireless sensors, or
"Semantic oriented" looking into the processing and storage of large quantities
of data [WF15, AIM10].
In this report, we will consider the deﬁnition provided by the International
Telecommunication Union (ITU). Internet of Things (IoT): "A global in-
frastructure for the information society, enabling advanced services by intercon-
necting (physical and virtual) things based on existing and evolving interoperable
information and communication technologies" [ITU12]. And with regard to the
Internet of Things, a thing is: "an object of the physical world (physical things)
or the information world (virtual things), which is capable of being identiﬁed
and integrated into communication networks". Additionally, a device within
this context is: "a piece of equipment with the mandatory capabilities of com-
munication and the optional capabilities of sensing, actuation, data capture, data
storage and data processing".
In other words, the Internet of Things takes advantage of current technologi-
cal advancements to enable the communication among "things" both physical
and virtual to oﬀer a wide variety of new and advanced services, normally by
enhancing everyday objects. [REC15, ITU12].
Another interesting concept is the Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT).
Deﬁned as the application of the IoT to the industrial sector, it follows the
same principles as the IoT: interconnected smart devices enabling new services
such as remote sensing or data collection. The diﬀerences between the two are
in the stricter requirements of the IIoT, specially in continuous operation and
safety, as the potential consequences of failure in the industrial environment
are generally larger. These diﬀerences have led to the interest of the industrial
sector in specialized applications, strategy and technology regarding the IoT
[SW18].

2.1 The Internet of Things
7
2.1.2
Characteristics
The main characteristics of the Internet of Things as found in the literature
[LCCB13, ITU12, ITU16] are as follows:
• Interconnectivity - Regarding the IoT, anything can be interconnected
with the global information and communication infrastructure.
• Things-related services - The IoT is capable of providing thing-related
services within the constraints of things, such as privacy protection and
semantic consistency between physical things and their associated virtual
things.
• Heterogeneity - IoT devices are heterogeneous both in hardware and net-
works. They can interact with other devices or services through diﬀerent
networks.
• Constrained resources - The IoT often involves devices which show lim-
ited energetic and computational resources (e.g. battery powered devices,
sensors, etc.).
• Dynamic changes - The state (e.g. connected and disconnected), the con-
text (e.g.
location and speed) and the number of devices can change
dynamically.
• Enormous scale - The number of devices in need of management and com-
municating with one another will increase substantially, at least one order
of magnitude larger than the devices connected to the current Internet.
The ratio of communications triggered by devices as compared to those
triggered by humans will shift towards the device-triggered. Some sources
predict up to 20 billion IoT devices by 2020 [vdMG17].
2.1.3
Architecture
The architecture for both IoT and IIoT is often shown as a four layer model
[ITU12, ITU16, SW18, DXHL14] as can be seen in Figure 2.1.
• Application layer - The top layer contains the IoT applications, containing
the business logic that processes and delivers the information collected by
the device layer.
==PAGE:20==

Figure 2.1: Four-layer IoT architectural model
• Service layer - The service layer features both generic and service / application-
speciﬁc functionality that enable IoT applications and services. The generic
capabilities include functionality which can be used by diﬀerent IoT appli-
cations such as data storage or data processing. The speciﬁc capabilities
are built focusing on speciﬁc applications and services, since one may have
diﬀerent requirements than another.
• Network layer - Encompasses two types of capabilities: networking and
transport capabilities. Networking capabilities focus on providing control
functions of network connectivity such as access, authentication, mobility
management, etc. Transport capabilities focus on providing connectivity
for the application speciﬁc data. In other words transmitting the infor-
mation collected by the devices properly to its destination.
• Device layer - The device layer includes the functionality of devices and
communication gateways.
It contains the devices that gather the data
needed by the speciﬁc application (RFID, sensors, actuators, etc.).
2.1.4
Challenges
The Internet of Things can bring great change to the Internet and bring many
economic beneﬁts, but it faces many challenges. In this section we will very
brieﬂy introduce some of the most important ones [KKZK12, MS14, GLA+17,
WF15].
==PAGE:21==
2.1 The Internet of Things
9
• Identity management - The IoT will connect many devices across the Inter-
net in order to provide its services. Each "thing" needs a unique identity,
thus an eﬃcient system is needed that is able to dynamically assign and
manage such a large number of identities.
• Interoperability and standardization: - Many manufacturers create their
devices using their own technology that may not be available to others.
The standardization of IoT would bring many beneﬁts in terms of inter-
operability of devices and the services they could provide.
• Information privacy - IoT devices today rely in technology like RFID for
identiﬁcation. Since daily use objects will carry these identiﬁcation tags,
it is necessary to prevent unauthorized access and protect privacy.
• Object safety - The IoT relies on a large number of devices spread across
a big area. It is necessary to deal with or prevent intruders from gaining
physical access to the devices to damage or modify them.
• Data conﬁdentiality and encryption - The IoT system is the one that
should handle who sees the gathered information. Therefore, it is neces-
sary to ensure the conﬁdentiality and integrity of the data since it leaves
the IoT device and until it reaches its destination. That means that the
IoT device should be able to encrypt data before it is transmitted over
the network. Due to the general limited computational power or these
devices, a lightweight encryption system is essential.
• Network security - The data generated by the IoT devices is sent some-
where else over a network.
The network should be able to handle the
transmissions from a large quantity of objects without losing data and
without exposing it to third parties.
• Energy consumption - At the rate the number of IoT devices is increasing,
so will the energy consumption. Making the devices as energy eﬃcient as
possible not only will be good for the environment and for reducing costs,
but for extending the autonomous life of the devices, which are more than
often relying on batteries for power.

functions to create new products that oﬀer new capabilities not only on a local
scale, but also globally [WF15]. The practice of creating and selling "smart"

2.2
Malware
Through the report, malware will be a concept that is constantly referred to.
Both AntibIoTic and the new design proposed in this solution aim to rid the
IoT devices they protect of malware, and the mechanism that they use in order
to get inside the devices are similar to those that some malware use. Therefore,
this section will provide a very quick overview on malware and its types, focusing
on those that are most relevant to this work.
2.2.1
Deﬁnition
Malware is a general term used to describe all forms of malicious software.
As stated in [Sha09], a possible deﬁnition is "Software which is used with the
aim of attempting to breach a computer system’s security policy with respect to
Conﬁdentiality, Integrity or Availability". Or in other words, a software that
intends to disclose a computer system’s information, alter its data in some way
or disrupt its normal purpose by making it unavailable.
==PAGE:24==
12
Theoretical background
2.2.2
Classiﬁcation
Malware is often classiﬁed depending on the way it infects the target and its
purpose [Sha09]. The traditional classiﬁcation of malware is as follows:
• Virus - Malware that spreads from one computer to another by embedding
copies of itself into ﬁles, which are transported to the target by diﬀerent
means, like the virus initiating the transport itself, or relying on unsus-
pecting humans to distribute the virus carrying ﬁle around.
• Worm - Malware that, diﬀerently to the virus, spreads copies of itself
without the aid of infected ﬁles, but simply by transmitting these copies
using a network connecting these computers.
• Trojan horse - Malware embedded in a piece of software which usually
has a useful eﬀect. The user is only aware of the useful eﬀect, while the
malware is kept hidden.
• Logic bomb - Malware triggered by a speciﬁc event. Like the arrival for a
speciﬁc date, or the creation or deletion of a speciﬁc ﬁle.
• Rabbit (aka. Bacterium) - Malware that uses up all of a particular type
of resource, such as message buﬀers or ﬁle space.
• Backdoor - Malware that once it is inside the target, it allows the perpe-
trator access to that system without having to go through normal authen-
tication procedures.
• Spyware - Malware which sends information about the user’s activities or
computer’s software or hardware to the attacker.
• Ransomware - Malware which encrypts the target’s entire disk or selected
ﬁles and asks for money in return for decrypting them again.
Of course, in reality it might be impossible to fully classify a piece of malware
into one of the aforementioned categories, since it may combine several of the
previous in the same software, such as a worm containing spyware, or a Trojan
horse containing ransomware.
2.2.3
Botnets
Botnets use worms or Trojan horses to set up a private communication infras-
tructure that can then be used for malicious purposes. Their aim is to control
==PAGE:25==
2.2 Malware
13
as many computers as possible by installing a backdoor in each of them. The
computers in the botnet are basically under remote control and are called bots.
These bots are can be given orders by the botmaster to perform the aforemen-
tioned malicious tasks.
We will go in more detail about these tasks further
ahead [Sha09].
Botnets are specially relevant for this project as they are very common in the
Internet of Things. IoT devices are generally small and resource-limited, but
they are many, they have access to the internet and are normally poorly secured,
making possible that malware with relatively simple infection mechanisms get
to control a great number of devices [KCS17, KKSV17].
Botnet’s activity phases
1. Search - Search for a target that looks suitable to attack. Mostly because
they have a known vulnerability that we can exploit.
2. Distribution - The backdoor code is propagated to the targets, trying to
install it or to trick the user into installing it. If successful, the targets
become bots.
3. Sign on - The bots connect to the infrastructure, informing the server(s)
that they are ready to execute commands from Command and Control
(CNC).
4. CNC - The bots receive orders from the botmaster and start the sequence
again, looking for more targets to attack.
Uses
A Botnet is but a tool, an army of bots at the service of the botmaster. There
are many possible ways to use a bonet, with diﬀerent motivations that go from
economical gain to making a political statement. These are some of them:
• DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service) - One of the most common usages
of a botnet (specially an IoT botnet) is to perform DDoS attacks. These
attacks are very diverse, and there are many diﬀerent techniques and ways
to make them work [DDDGS17], but the result is always the loss of service
of the target. With the current Internet infrastructure, DDoS attacks are
incredibly eﬀective, and will always be if the resources of the attacker are
greater than those of the victim. DDoS can also be very proﬁtable sold as a
service, in which the botmaster oﬀers performing DDoS attacks on targets

chosen by the clients getting money in return [SST+16]. Speciﬁc sites or
services can also be targeted as a way of making a political statement.
• SPAM - Using a big botnet it is possible to send a large volume of SPAM
mail. This mail could contain links to malicious sites hosting viruses or
have embedded malware themselves, that could be used, for example, to
spread the botnet further.
• Spyware - Once inside a device, the bot could monitor its network traﬃc,

• Spreading new malware - Of course, the botnet can be used to spread

2.3
Fog computing
Fog computing is also a very important concept for this work, as it is the enabling
technology that allows the proposed solution. It is deeply related to the IoT
and Cloud concepts. This section will give an overview about the deﬁnition,
characteristics, architecture, challenges and applications of this technology.
2.3.1
Deﬁnition
Fog computing is a relatively new term introduced in 2012 in [BMZA12]. As it
happens with newly introduced concepts, it takes time for them to take shape.
Several deﬁnitions have been proposed, the original Fog computing was deﬁned
as an extension of the Cloud, closer to the edge of the network enabling new
applications and services.
In 2014, [VRM14] proposed a diﬀerent deﬁnition,
focusing on a multitude of heterogeneous, ubiquitous and decentralized devices
connecting to each other and cooperating. But as [YHQL15] pointed out, it
disregards the connection of Fog computing with the Cloud. In 2017 [ADA17]
oﬀers a similar view as the original idea in [BMZA12].
In this work, the deﬁnition we consider is the one by the OpenFog Consortium
in [G+16, G+17]: "Fog computing is a system-level horizontal architecture that
distributes resources and services of computing, storage, control and networking
anywhere along the continuum from Cloud to Things, thereby accelerating the
velocity of decision making. Fog-centric architecture serves a speciﬁc subset of
business problems that cannot be successfully implemented using only traditional
cloud based architectures or solely intelligent edge devices".

2.3.3
Architecture
While some sources provide a 4-layer architecture for the Fog [ADA17], the ma-
jority present a 3-layer architecture [HDNQ17, YHQL15, BMZA12] practically
identical to the one seen in Figure 2.4. These layers are, from bottom (edge) to
top (core): the terminal layer, the Fog layer, and the Cloud layer.
• Terminal layer - Layer closest to the physical environment that contains
the (IoT) devices which generate the data that the system needs to process
and store in order to provide a service.
• Fog layer - The Fog computing layer could be anywhere between the end-
user and the Cloud, but it is most eﬀective when on the Edge of the
network. It is comprised of Fog nodes, which can be routers, gateways,
speciﬁc Fog servers, etc. and are located on a wide variety of geographical
positions and they could be ﬁxed or mobile. The end devices connect to
these nodes in order to obtain services, which the Fog nodes provide if
they can or request from the Cloud otherwise.
• Cloud layer - The cloud computing layer consists of multiple high-performance
servers and storage devices and provides various applications. It is the
layer in charge of performing the most expensive operations computation-
ally wise, and permanently store the data.

2.3.4
Challenges
While Fog computing is a very promising technology that would enable us to
further reap the beneﬁts of the Internet of Things, there are many problems
to be addressed before it can become a reality. This section provides a brief
introduction to some of the most important ones [VRM14, YHQL15, HDNQ17].
• Control and management - In addition to the conﬁguration of communi-
cation routes across nodes, the speciﬁc IoT devices and applications need
to be properly setup for them to work. Having such a large number of de-
vices and applications makes this task challenging, and the Fog will most
probably rely on decentralized management mechanisms and declarative
conﬁguration techniques (what needs to happen, rather than how).
• Security and privacy - There are many security concerns regarding Fog
computing. The fact that a great number of Fog nodes can be resource-
constrained devices installed at the edge of the network and with no di-
rect surveillance makes them vulnerable to classic attacks like man-in-the-
middle or physical manipulation of the device. As Fog nodes are expected
to work with IoT devices, other Fog nodes and the Cloud in order to pro-
vide their services, they will process and store data which will be diﬃcult
to protect. They will also need to be able to authenticate other devices
without the help of the Cloud, as this would increase the latency, and the
reduction of latency is one of the pillars of Fog computing.
• Programmability - In order to develop applications for Fog computing,
programmers need a proper abstraction to hide the massive complexity of
the Fog and be able to run the same programs on a variety of platforms
and devices.
• Latency - Moving part of the computing to the edge of network in the form
of Fog nodes might not be enough to deal with the latency requirement,
particularly when speciﬁc requirements are met. To support mobility, the
end-device will expect an application to work as they move. While the
end-device is moving, it will need to constantly connect to diﬀerent Fog
nodes, which will need the information and the context that the device
requires. Depending on the number of users, the computational power of
the involved Fog nodes, the saturation of the network, and how heavy the
operation is to perform, the latency requirements could be not so easy to
fulﬁll.
• Connectivity - With the number of devices connected to the Internet in-
creasing as they are, the network itself might become the bottleneck.

In September and October 2016, three of the largest DDoS attacks that the

These attacks served as a clear indication that not only there are many IoT
devices with serious security ﬂaws, but that their existence means a security
threat for us all. But what makes IoT devices so attractive to attackers? The
fact that there are so many of them, in so many diﬀerent locations and that they
are designed to stay always online already makes them a good target. Moreover,
they are mostly designed to be as cheap as possible, leaving security in hands
of the users, who are very often unaware of how they should be securing their
devices [KCS17, KKSV17].

3.1
AntibIoTic
This section will present the AntibIoTic solution. It will start by describing the
main idea behind it, followed by explaining its main features and will ﬁnish with
a description of its architecture.
3.1.1
The idea
AntibIoTic can be described as a white worm with the intention of infecting very
vulnerable or virtually unprotected devices, securing them in the process. This
would avoid these devices from getting infected by other malware, becoming
part of malicious botnets and being used for dangerous illicit purposes, such as
performing DDoS attacks [DDDGM17].
3.1.2
Functionality
In order to understand how does AntibIoTic intend to protect IoT devices, let
us take a closer look at its main features as described in [DDDGM17].
• Publish data and statistics - A successful botnet controls thousands of
devices.
Therefore, it is in a good position to show relevant statistics
regarding the geography, architecture, type, manufacturer, security ﬂaws,
etc. of the infected devices. This provides an interesting insight on the
==PAGE:35==
3.1 AntibIoTic
23
current state of IoT device’s security, raising awareness among users and
manufacturers and giving useful data to researchers and security experts.
• Expose interactive interfaces - Allow users enough privileges to give sug-
gestions on how to improve the current AntibIoTic that the admins could
review and eventually implement. It would also have a set of restricted
functions only available for admins to control de botnet, e.g. a "shut down
button" to stop the botnet in case it gets out of control.
• Sanitize infected devices - Infect weakly secured devices, eliminate any
present malware, and secure the perimeter to avoid future intrusions. This
is done mainly by scanning the memory in search of known malware sig-
natures (killing the processes if found) and closing usually exploited ports
(such as 22, 23, 80, 5555).
• Notify the device’s owner - After sanitizing the device, AntibIoTic pro-
ceeds to inform the owner of the device. It does so by sending a report
with both the found vulnerabilities and advice on how to ﬁx them, en-
couraging the owner to ﬁx them.
• Secure vulnerable devices - If after notifying the owner the device is still
insecure, AntibIoTic proceeds to secure the device, for example, changing
the device’s password or updating its ﬁrmware.
• Resistance to reboot - AntibIoTic has two mechanisms in order to persist
in the vulnerable devices even in case of reboot. One of them is the simple
fact that AntibIoTic keeps track of all spotted devices, therefore, if it fails
to communicate with one, it may assume a reboot and try to infect it
again. Furthermore, it will try to modify the device’s startup settings in
order to boot along with the device.

right. It is debatable, however, if a very poorly secured IoT device such as the

While this article is about disrupting the functioning of systems, which is some-

device is securing its perimeter, which is done mainly by closing ports normally
used by other malware to break into devices. It is not impossible to think of

without right, however, it would not be done intentionally. As for the gravity of
the the case, it would depend on the nature of the device and the circumstances.

These changes would be made intentionally and without right.
As for the gravity of the case, the directive does not provide speciﬁc guidelines.
However, let us consider some possibilities. Again, if AntibIoTic is successful
in its implementation, it is designed to infect thousands of devices, this could
already be considered more than a minor case, but even if we considered each
device a diﬀerent case, AntibIoTic is performing delicate operations on the in-
fected devices, the most delicate being the ﬁrmware update.
Updating the
ﬁrmware of a device is a delicate process because the ﬁrmware is in charge of
the basic input/output functionality of the device, so any errors occurring dur-
ing the update could render the device useless ("bricking" the device) [BV14].
Not only does this interfere with the data held in the device, but also with its
general availability, thus also aﬀecting Article 4. Furthermore, virtually destroy-
ing the device costs the user the price of the device itself, the time necessary
to return said device to the manufacturer for repairs (or replacing it entirely)
and potentially, the losses for the user’s organization while they are not able
to provide whatever service was the device providing. While this may be an
uncommon case while updating ﬁrmware, if we take into consideration that this
procedure may be done for thousands of devices with diﬀerent manufacturer,
version installed, architecture, etc. Then it is not impossible that such cases
could happen.

4.1
The idea
As discussed in section 2.3, Fog computing is basically an intermediate layer
between the ﬁnal device and the Cloud computing center. This intermediate
layer manifests in the form of the so called Fog nodes. These nodes can stand
anywhere between the Cloud and the device, though they are normally thought
to be at the edge of the network, as close to the device as possible. This set-
up brings many beneﬁts, the most relevant ones being decreasing the latency
of communications and decreasing the computational load of the fewer, more
centralized Cloud computing centers.
This new layer is born out of necessity, as the growth in numbers of IoT devices
has exploded in recent years, with some statistics showing almost a doubling
of the amount of IoT devices every two years (see Figure 3.3).
The sudden
increase in number of connected devices and the very nature of IoT devices
call for shorter range communications. The fact that in the near future most
IoT devices could be connecting to the internet through a Fog node brings new
possibilities in many ﬁelds, security being one of them.
The main idea behind the new design of AntibIoTic is to use the Fog nodes to
ensure that the IoT devices that are connected to it are free of malware, only
allowing safe devices to reach the Internet. This would be done by providing the
Fog node with an antivirus that would scan the IoT devices that connect to it
from the inside, looking for malware, and ﬁxing vulnerabilities before allowing
them go through. The IoT devices would allow themselves to be scanned and
sanitized, as it is the only way they will manage to connect to the Internet. This
way, the Fog node would scan and patch the devices with the authorization of
the owners not infringing any of the articles discussed in section 3.2, as can be
seen in Table 4.1.
This antivirus would look a lot like the AntibIoTic worm, and would share
most of its sanitizing and securing features but with extra advantages coming
from the fact that it counts with the cooperation of the devices, gathering
very useful information and securing them while avoiding any possible damage.
Furthermore, it is designed to be as easy to use as possible (just substitute the
existing gateway with a Fog node), and work with all kind of devices, including
those that were not designed speciﬁcally to communicate with the Fog node.
These features will be explained in detail in the following sections.

nodes are trusted.
If a Fog node were to be compromised, the whole
system would crumble. However, the securing of the Fog node itself is not
as interesting as describing how it could be used to secure the IoT devices
connected to it. And as more powerful machines than IoT devices, regular
ways of securing it (antivirus, ﬁrewall, IDS) are a possibility. Describing
how to secure a node that is visible from the internet is not in the scope
of this project, and will be considered future work. There is, however,
promising research on this topic like the one based in crowd attestation in
[ZZZA17], that could be relevant in the case where a system could grow
as to have several Fog nodes.
• Communication between the Fog node and the bot is secure -
In order to trust that the devices are secured or not, the Fog node must
communicate with the bot on a regular basis. As a result, the device will
be allowed to continue on to the Internet or not. Therefore, the Fog node
needs to be able to trust that the AntibIoTic bot is not compromised.
Thus, we need a way of authenticating the bot and securely transmitting
its information to the Fog node. Securely transmitting the information
over the network could be achieved by using encryption, and, as IoT de-
vices could be limited in their computational power, lightweight encryption
is preferred [EK07]. Authenticating the bot could be achieved by storing a
==PAGE:59==
4.2 Threat and risk analysis

diﬀerent unique key for each instance of the bot. Even then, as the bot is
running in the memory of the device, the bot might become compromised.
As such, even if we could never fully trust the bot, a mechanism to realize
when the bot has been compromised becomes necessary.
• Fog node is the only gateway - The whole idea of forcing the devices
to be secure if they want to perform their function only makes sense if the
Fog node is their only way of reaching the Internet, thus achieving their
function. Therefore, we will consider the Fog node the only gateway in
the system. And consider the other scenario part of future work.
4.2.3
Assets and threats
Let us start by examining the valuable parts of the system that require protec-
tion and the potential causes that could harm them. Each of the threats will be
assigned a severity level based on how grave the consequences are to the system.
4.2.3.1
Fog node
The assets of the Fog node are both the device and all the information that
it holds. The Fog node is the most critical device in the system, as it regulates
which devices have access to the Internet and those which do not, provides the
instances of the AntibIoTic bots to each of the IoT devices, stores the all of the
vulnerability reports and interacts with the system administrator.
Threats
• Data disclosure (conﬁdentiality) - If some information stored in the Fog
node was made available to a third party, they could potentially access
all of the IoT devices supervised by the Fog node remotely (disclosing
credentials for all devices). Severity: High.
• Data loss (integrity, availability) - If the Fog node lost access to certain
vital information, it could hinder all operations. Depending on the data
that is lost, it could go from minor inconvenience (losing a vulnerabil-
ity report), to stopping all operations (losing conﬁguration data from all
devices). Severity: High.
• Service interruption (availability) - If the Fog node stopped working, none
of the IoT devices that connect to it could continue performing their ser-
vice. Severity: High.

4.2.3.2
IoT device
The IoT device symbolizes every IoT device that is trying to access the Internet
through the Fog node. Its assets are the device itself and the information

Threats
• Data disclosure (conﬁdentiality) - Normally, IoT devices are limited and
do not hold much information themselves, it is instead stored in the Cloud.
However, depending on the type of device, this can have a lesser or greater

from several cameras, may contain several hours of recording. However,

• Data modiﬁcation (integrity) - Data modiﬁcation is a more serious threat,

• Service interruption (availability) - Depending on the type of device, its

a device that stops working may cause diﬀerent consequences. However,
the purpose of the system is to protect the devices without aﬀecting how
they work. Therefore, we will assume that for a device to stop working is
not acceptable. Severity: High.
• Data loss (availability) - Depending on the data that is lost and the type
of device, this could mean several things. For example, a DVR losing all
its recordings aﬀects the purpose for which the DVR was placed there.
Losing some conﬁguration data, could imply that the device is unable to
operate until ﬁxed. Therefore it is a serious threat, but not all data is
vital to the device. Severity: Medium.
• Service slowdown (quality) - An IoT device that experiences a slow down,
could aﬀect the quality of its performance, hindering its use, but will not
bring serious consequences to the system. Severity: Low.

4.2.3.3
AntibIoTic bot
The AntibIoTic bot is the software that ideally runs in every IoT device, scans
them for vulnerabilities, sanitizes them, and communicates this information
back to the Fog node.
Its assets are the bot (software) itself and all the
information that it holds, including the results from scanning and securing

Threats
• Data disclosure (conﬁdentiality) - The data held by the bot is mainly a log
of its actions taken since it was initiated, including the vulnerability scan
of the device and the sanitizing actions performed. While the disclosure
about the vulnerabilities of one device is problematic, it does not have
severe consequences for the functioning of the system. Severity: Low.
• Data modiﬁcation (integrity) - The modiﬁcation of the data held by the
bot is dangerous. Since that data is the one that is sent to the Fog node to
decide whether the device is secure or not. If this information is modiﬁed
before it leaves for the Fog node, a wrong report could be delivered, al-

from reaching the Internet. However, by itself it would not stop the bot
from securing the device, thus not completely interrupting the functioning
of the system. Severity: Medium.
• Service interruption (availability) - The AntibIoTic bot not working is a
serious issue in the general functioning of the system.
If the bot does
not work, that means that the IoT device is potentially unsafe, and that
depending on how lenient the Fog node is, the IoT device may not be able
to access the Internet, and thus achieve its purpose, without running the

the majority of cases, it would not be very long, as the Fog node would
realize that one of the devices does not have the bot anymore, and install
it again. Severity: Medium.
• Data loss (availability) - The data held by the AntibIoTic bot is constantly
being updated, if the data was lost, it would not take long to re-generate
it, thus, the loss of availability would happen only during a short period
of time. Severity: Low.

4.2.6
Countermeasures
The previous section listed the threats to the system and the risk they meant to
it. The most dangerous threats turned out to be those involving the Fog node,
and the IoT device itself, specially regarding its availability and the integrity of
its data. This section will present some of the countermeasures that could be
employed to reduce or eliminate the identiﬁed risks.
4.2.6.1
Emphasize a correct initial conﬁguration
A correct initial conﬁguration is vital for the security of the system.
All of
the IoT devices that are going to be protected should be properly conﬁgured
in the Fog node, and tested one by one to check for any possible errors in
conﬁguration. This step would already reduce the likelihood of the vulnerability
about the system administrator making mistakes in the conﬁguration of the
system (section 4.2.4.5). Furthermore, if before starting the system, all of the
devices are turned oﬀ, and then turned on one by one, it will ensure that all
of them get the bot installed before any malware is able to take hold of them.
==PAGE:71==
4.2 Threat and risk analysis
59
By going one by one, the possibility of rogue nodes infecting devices as the
conﬁguration progresses is greatly reduced. Starting from a point where the
system has the bot protecting all the IoT devices greatly reduces the likelihood of
malware taking over the IoT device, which is the necessary step to perform many
of the described attacks, and would greatly reduce the severity of vulnerabilities
such as the lack of safety of the IoT device (section 4.2.4.1), the bot running
in memory of device (because malware needs to be inside the device in order
to attack the bot, section 4.2.4.2) or the LAN not being completely secured (as
rogue nodes would ﬁnd more diﬃcult getting a hold of devices that are protected
by the bot, section 4.2.4.4).
4.2.6.2
Redundancy
In order to avoid the Fog node being a single point of failure (section 4.2.4.3),
redundancy could be added. While bringing additional costs to the system, it
solves the issue. It is also easy to scale depending on the importance of the
system, and the probabilities of it being attacked.
4.2.6.3
Separate the networks
For the Fog node to watch over a network in which there are IoT devices that
are supposed to be protected, IoT devices that are not, and non-IoT devices is a
complex scenario that brings many problems, as seen in section 4.2.4.4. Making
a separation and the leaving only the Fog node and the IoT devices that are
conﬁgured to be protected, is both more secure and easier to manage. This way,
the Fog node would only allow traﬃc to go through from devices it has been
conﬁgured to recognize and protect.
4.2.6.4
Reduce interaction between Fog node and system adminis-
trator
The proposed system requires the system administrator to perform a thorough
initial conﬁguration.
However, after that, the system is designed to be au-
tonomous. The collection of statistics and consulting the status of the system
could be done from non-root user accounts, reducing the risk of modifying in-
formation or changing the conﬁguration. By reducing the overall interaction
that the system administrator has with the Fog node to a minimum, accidents
would happen less frequently. The need for accessing the Fog node with root
privileges would also be lower, making it easier to spot if an insider decides to

60

attack the system. This would help reduce the likeliness of attacks that exploit
the vulnerability system administrator is human seen in section 4.2.4.5.
4.2.6.5
Unique and obfuscated shared keys
In order to have safer communications between the AntibIoTic bot and the Fog
node, the communications need to be encrypted. That means the bot needs to
have access to a secret key to encrypt messages with. However, running in the
memory of the device complicates things. Depending on the implementation,
the Fog node might need to compile the bot for every device, as they could have
diﬀerent architectures. During this process, a unique key could be embedded in
the bot. Additionally, the binaries could be stripped during compilation. This
would mean that every bot would have a diﬀerent key, and the stripping would
make it much harder to extract. This makes the life of malware much harder,
since if they wanted to fool the Fog node, they would need to ﬁnd the shared
key in the memory of the bot. And ﬁnding it once, means controlling only one
device. Furthermore, the moment this device restarts, the key will change once
again. This would help reduce the likelihood of attacks trying to exploit the bot
running in memory of device vulnerability seen in section 4.2.4.2.
4.2.7
Summary
After conducting the threat and risk analysis it can be seen that the most wor-
risome points have to do either with the Fog node or the IoT device.
In
particular, great care is needed with the Fog node, as it is the central compo-
nent to the whole architecture. It holds very important information about the
system, and its lack of availability would jeopardize the functioning of the rest
of IoT devices in the network. The most important points regarding the IoT
device are its availability and the integrity of its data. This is because we
consider a priority the normal functioning of the IoT devices in our system, so
modiﬁcation of data used to provide a service or the stopping of the service al-
together are to be avoided. The system in general is better at repelling external
threats than handling internal ones e.g. an already infected IoT device. That
is why a careful initial conﬁguration is essential for the security of the sys-
tem. The system presents several vulnerabilities that could be exploited.
However, countermeasures have been proposed that address all of the found
vulnerabilities.

4.3.1.1
Fog node
The Fog node is an essential component in the design and what makes it dif-

diﬃcult to know exactly what form the Fog nodes will take, but we do know
the capabilities they are supposed to have.
In this design, the Fog node is a trusted element that is placed between the
Internet and the IoT devices, fulﬁlling its original purpose (e.g. a smart traﬃc
light) and on top of that, securing the IoT devices that connect to it. The Fog
node’s main features regarding the security of the devices are as follows:
• Receive connections from nearby IoT devices.
• Install the AntibIoTic bot in all connected devices to make sure they are
the least vulnerable possible.
• Keep the AntibIoTic bot in its most recent version at all times.
• Gather useful data to help improve the bot and keep it updated against
the latest threats.
• Serve as an interface between the devices and the administrator, allowing
to monitor the devices’ status and to carry out speciﬁc security related
actions.

protecting them in real time without interfering with their functioning. However,
as the securing intends to close down dangerous ports, it could still interfere with
some devices’ functionality. Furthermore, even though it would allow Internet
access to the great majority of devices, it could be updated to block devices
running a particular malware, which could help mitigate or stop attacks in
emergency situations.
The standard mode is designed to be a reasonable choice for all devices in all
types of Fog computing deployment.

4.4
Implementation
The presented design shares many similarities in architecture with the original
AntibIoTic design, which in turn shares many similarities with the infrastruc-
ture used by Mirai. That is, two main components, the Server and the Bot. The
server, which receives information, manages all the connections and sends the
appropriate commands to all the bot instances. And the bot, which scans the
network in search of vulnerable devices, infects them, and reports to the server.
Our design is diﬀerent in several aspects, e.g. it is not necessary to scan for vul-
nerable devices, since the only devices that it will be protecting will be the ones
connecting voluntarily to the Fog node. Nevertheless, the main components are
the same. It needs the AntibIoTic bot, which runs inside each IoT device that
it is protecting and the Fog node, acting as the Server, which issues commands
and receives reports from the bot instance in each device.
As it was already mentioned in the original AntibIoTic report [DDDGM17], the
server and the bot implementations could be implemented very similarly to how
Mirai did it. The original AntibIotic design expanded on Mirai’s capabilities,
proposing a wide variety of sanitizing actions. Implementing those particular
features, e.g. ﬁrmware update, or resistance to reboot is as challenging today
as it was back then, and heavily dependant on each IoT device’s architecture.
As an example, resistance to reboot could be unachievable if the device did not
possess any writable hard memory.
To summarize, both the server and the bot implementations for AntibIoTic have
been already deemed challenging but achievable. In fact, a proof of concept
implementing several of the main features has been developed along with this
report (see Section 5 for more details).
This section will explore the challenges that come with the new design in terms
of its implementation, and provide some ideas as to how it could be carried out.
4.4.1
Challenges of the new design
In the new design, the features of the bot once it is running in each IoT device
presents no signiﬁcant diﬀerences from the one described in the original AntibI-
oTic report. It is instead in the way the bot is introduced in each device that
most changes have occurred. Originally, AntibIoTic’s intention was to raise the
security of the very vulnerable devices, using Mirai’s infection mechanism to get
in through exposed telnet ports with default credentials. If the AntibIoTic bot
could not get into a device, that meant that it was somewhat secure, and it was
==PAGE:88==
76
New AntibIoTic design using Fog computing
not necessary to infect it to protect it.
Now, with the new solution, even though it is unclear in which way Fog com-
puting will be implemented, the Fog node will be the link between some IoT
devices and the Internet, and it is expected that the Fog node makes sure that
those IoT devices are secure. As having an instance of the bot running in each
device greatly contributes to the assurance that the device is secure, managing
to introduce the bot into each device becomes a priority. Let us take a closer
look to the feasibility of the implementation of introducing the AntibIoTic bot
into IoT devices designed to work with our solution, and devices which were
not.
4.4.1.1
IoT devices designed to work with this solution
If the IoT device was designed to work along the Fog node, as speciﬁed in Figure
4.4, then the problem of getting the AntibIoTic bot inside the IoT device would
have a trivial solution. As the IoT device would be utilizing a protocol through
which it expects to download the bot from the Fog node and run it. This is the
preferred solution, as it would be truly transparent to the user, and would ensure
that the bot would be able to run in the device. It is also the only way in which
this solution would be able to deal with situations like self driving vehicles, in
which the IoT devices connected to the Fog node are changing constantly over
time.
4.4.1.2
IoT devices not designed to work with this solution
If the IoT device was just a normal device, not designed to work along with the
Fog node, but seeing it instead as just a router. This presents a problem, as the
IoT device will not collaborate and facilitate the installation of the AntibIoTic
bot. This means that it is up to the Fog node to install the AntibIoTic bot into
these devices, and make them run it. As AntibIoTic performs actions that can
only be performed by the administrator of the device, as scanning the device’s
memory and killing dangerous processes or closing unused ports, the Fog node
needs to obtain a shell in the device with root privileges. Once such a shell is
obtained, uploading and running the bot is trivial. However, in order to obtain
a shell with root privileges without the cooperation of the device we need both
the previous agreement of the owner and a method to get in. What needs to be
done is:
1. Identify the IoT device - We need the permission of the owner in order

to break into the device legally. Even if we have it, it is necessary to make
sure that the device that we are dealing with, is one we have been allowed
to break into. Additionally, knowing the exact device will be very useful in
knowing what method we are going to use to break into it. Furthermore,
if the Fog node is going to compile a bot for every device, it is necessary
to know its architecture, so a compatible version is used for the device.
2. Get a shell - Once we have identiﬁed the device as one we have permission
to break into, it is needed to somehow get a shell with root permissions,
so we can download the AntibIoTic bot from the Fog node and run it.
In order to achieve these necessary objectives, a manual or an automatic ap-
proach can be followed.

– As an alternative, a netmask could also be provided.
That way,

• Registering the MAC addresses of the devices in the Fog node - Since

Manual approach
The manual approach to identifying the IoT device and installing the bot is
based on the system administrator registering the IoT devices in the Fog node
beforehand. This way, the Fog node is aware of which devices it is supposed to
protect and it can identify them. In order to get a shell in the device and
install the bot, the Fog node relies on scripts or credentials provided by the
system administrator.
In this proof of concept, this has been simulated by hard-coding the IP address
(see Appendix C.1) and the speciﬁc command that must be run in order to
upload and run the script (see Appendix C.2).
Internet access decision
In the proposed design, the access decision (whether the Fog node allows an
IoT device to access the Internet or not) is taken by requesting the vulnerability
report from the bot, examining and taking a decision based on its contents.
After the ﬁrst time, the keep-alive message inform the Fog node of any changes.
If any changes are detected, the report is requested and analyzed again, and a
new decision is taken based on that.
However, in the current implementation of the bot, the vulnerability report is
only generated once a vulnerability is ﬁxed. Such as a port closed, or a malware
signature found and its process killed. Thus, the report provided by the bot
does not provide information on whether the device is secure or not, only if a
problem has been found and ﬁxed. While this is a ﬁrst step and is useful for
collecting information about the vulnerabilities that were found and ﬁxed by
the bot, it does not provide useful information on whether the device is secure
or not. Because, even if a vulnerability was ﬁxed, there could be more. And in
the opposite case, if no vulnerabilities were found, it does not necessarily mean
that the device is either safe or not.
Modifying the bot to perform a vulnerability assessment and implementing a
way of parsing and taking a decision based on this analysis is outside of the
scope of this proof of concept. Therefore, it was decided that for now, it is
enough for us to know that the AntibIoTic bot is running on the device, so the
Fog node will allow the device to have access to Internet as long as it maintains

Evaluation
This section shows the results of the proof of concept. What assumptions were

5.4.1
Assumptions
Due to lack of time, this proof of concept lacks both complexity and ﬂexibility,
and some assumptions must be made in order to cover for them:
• Only IoT devices that have been conﬁgured by the system administrator
will access the Internet through the Fog node. The Fog node has a for-
warding policy in which it does not allow any traﬃc to go through unless
it is coming from a device in which the AntibIoTic bot is running. Thus,
any other normal devices that do not need the AntibIoTic bot, but try to
connect through it, will be rejected as well. However, the Fog node only
handling connections from devices it is supposed to protect is a recom-
mended countermeasure that helps reduce the risk of attacks as seen in
section 4.2.6.
• The AntibIoTic bot does its job properly. As already mentioned, the bot
lacks the ability to detect threats that it cannot solve.
Thus, it lacks
the ability of generating reports that can determine whether a device is
secure or not. But even if it did, the Fog node lacks the ability to properly
==PAGE:103==
5.4 Evaluation
91
parse these reports, and take a decision based on them. Therefore, as a
simpliﬁcation, we assume that as long as the bot is running, it is doing its
job.
5.4.2
Goals achieved
Before starting to implement the proof of concept, a set of goals were established
(see section 5.1). This section will explain which of them were satisﬁed and
which were not, and why.
1. Simulate the interaction between the main components - Achieved.
The component interaction is as expected.
The Fog node acts as the
gateway for the IoT device and it can decide whether to allow it to connect
to the Internet or not. The AntibIoTic bot is successfully installed and
executed in the device, and it is able to contact the server running in the
Fog node.
2. Test a minimum of sanitizing and securing actions - Achieved / untested.
This proof of concept was mainly focused on achieving the interaction be-
tween the components, rather than on implementing the bot. In fact, the
bot used in this current implementation is taken from the work [SL18b]. In
that work, the author has already tested that the bot is able to close ports,
and scan the processes for speciﬁc malware signatures. Mainly because of
this, this proof of concept was made under the assumption that we trust
the bot to do its job, as explained before. So since [SL18b] has already
tested that a minimum of sanitizing and securing actions are performed,
it was not tested in this proof of concept.
3. Implement the Lockdown operation mode - Achieved partially.
The lockdown operation mode was implemented. However, the part of the
design where an access decision is taken based on the report is missing. As
explained above, the bot that is used does not fully support the generation
of reports, and implementing the necessary functionality in both the bot
and the Fog node is out of the scope of this project.
4. Show the design works for the main cases of the Lockdown operation mode
- Achieved partially.
• Device not running the bot: Internet blocked - Achieved.
If there is no AntibIoTic bot running in the device, it will not be
allowed to access the Internet.

• Infected device running the bot: Internet blocked - Unachieved.

Future work
This proof of concept was but a small ﬁrst step, a very small project meant to
be put together in a very short period of time to showcase how the proposed
design could work and its feasibility. But if it were to be implemented in full,
there are many things to do. These are the most important ones:
• Expand the bot to generate a detailed vulnerability report in addition to
the sanitizing and securing actions taken.
• Expand the Fog node to parse said report, analyzing it and being able
to extract intelligent decisions on whether the bot should perform extra
sanitizing or securing actions, and whether the device should be allowed
access or not.
• Look into the possibility of automatically compiling the bot for any device,
no matter the architecture.

shell on devices to reduce the amount of eﬀort needed by the system
administrator.
• Expand the bot’s keep-alive signal to include a quick status of the device.
• Expand the Fog node to react when the IoT device ﬁrst connects to it
instead of the Fog node actively searching for the device when it starts.
• Implement the rest of Operation Modes, not only Lockdown.

6.1
Contributions
The aim of the thesis was to study Fog computing and its relation to the Internet
of Things, as well as the AntibIoTic system, and propose a new design that
improved on the previous system’s shortcomings.
Accordingly, the goals of

Legal analysis of the AntibIoTic solution
The original AntibIoTic system’s biggest ﬂaw was the legal issues that prevented
its deployment. The EU directive on attacks against information systems of
2013 [Dir13] is studied and its most relevant articles explained and applied to
the AntibIoTic solution. By extension, the same points that are raised against
AntibIoTic could be raised against any similar solutions trying to ﬁght the lack
of security of IoT devices using a similar approach.
==PAGE:108==

Conclusion
Proposed new design of AntibIoTic using Fog computing
The main contribution of this work is the proposal of the new AntibIoTic. After
studying the original one, an improved version is proposed. The new system
overcomes the biggest ﬂaw of its predecessor: it is designed to only act on devices
for which it has been cleared to do so. The system’s architecture allow it to be
very scalable: it could be deployed to secure a handful of devices such as those
belonging to a smart-house or secure all the sensors in an electric grid. It’s made
to be transparent, so that it does not require interaction with the user, only a
careful initial conﬁguration.
Proof of concept
Even though the provided proof of concept is simplistic and is missing the vul-
nerability report functionality, it achieves its purpose: simulating the interaction
between components and showing that the system is feasible. It provides an ex-
ample in which the Fog node installs and runs the AntibIoTic bot in the IoT
device. Said IoT device is only allowed to access the Internet when the bot
running inside it is in contact with the Fog node. Additionally, it retains the
original spirit of securing the whole Internet, as securing one’s own devices will
help the general security of the Internet by not letting them be taken over by
malware and used for malicious purposes. The in-depth description of this de-
sign is accompanied by a detailed threat and risk analysis of the system, stating
its weak points and proposing solutions on how to quell them.

Future work
While this thesis succeeds in achieving its initial goals, we are aware that there is
much work to be done: improving the current solution, expanding it to consider
realistic scenarios that have not been considered because they were too complex
and implementing a complete version of the design. Without going into too
much detail, here are some of the most relevant issues that could beneﬁt from
future work.
• Improving current solution - The proposed design works under a number
of assumptions stated during the threat model (section 4.2.2). For the
design to be complete, those assumptions have to be removed and proper
solutions put in their place.
– Research has to be done in order to secure the Fog nodes, a prob-
lem not only of this design, but inherited from the Fog computing

architecture and suﬀering from example, of being physically accessi-
ble by possible attackers.
– The communication between the AntibIoTic bot and the Fog

sure that the encryption that is used is lightweight enough for the
vast majority of IoT devices.
• Expanding current solution - The proposed solution could be expanded to
deal with complicated scenarios such as supporting mobility: Fog nodes

func keepAliveReceiver() {
for {
// Listen on all interfaces
ln, err := net.Listen("tcp", ":4321")
check(err)
// Accept connection
conn, err := ln.Accept()
check(err)
// Receive keep-alive messages
for {
msg := make([]byte, 2)
_, err = io.ReadFull(conn, msg)
if err != nil {
fmt.Println("Keep alive socket err:", err)
// if connection is severed, block IP address
ipAddress :=
extractIpAddress(conn.RemoteAddr().String())
blockIPAddress(ipAddress)
break
}
fmt.Println("\nReceived keep-alive message")
// if properly received, allow Ip address
ipAddress :=
extractIpAddress(conn.RemoteAddr().String())
allowIPAddress(ipAddress)
}
conn.Close()
ln.Close()
return
}
}
