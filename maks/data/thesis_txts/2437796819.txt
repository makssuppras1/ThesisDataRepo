Abstract
The construction and optimization of cell factories is dependent on compli-
cated, conventional methods, such as analytical chemistry, for measurements
of outputs. Biosensors are sustainable alternatives to this, as they enable eas-
ily quantiable outputs of relevant cell factory parameters, and they are often
based on transcription factors.
The cAMP receptor protein (CRP) of Es-
cherichia coli is one of the most well-known transcriptional regulators, which
has recently been studied using a synthetic evolution assay. The assay causes
starvation in an E. coli strain decient in cAMP production. In the absence of
cAMP, CRP is a target of mutagenesis, and cAMP-independent CRP variants
termed CRP* arise. This is speculated to occur by a hypothetical evolution-
ary mechanism termed retromutagenesis. The objective of this study was to
investigate, whether this evolutionary mechanism could be exploited to con-
vert CRP into a versatile small molecule biosensor. The mutational solution
space of CRP is aected by the presence of small molecules in the environ-
ment, and thus synthetic evolution may generate CRP* variants with changed
eector specicity that act as biosensors of these compounds. In order to gain
high-throughput mutational data from the synthetic evolution assay, a work-
ow based on next-generation sequencing (NGS) was developed to achieve
population-level as opposed to colony-level information. This workow was
applied successfully, and an alternative small molecule eector was shown to
aect the mutational solution space. The hypothesis of retromutagenesis was
also tested. The data was not found to be in agreement with the hypothesis,
but further work is required, before any conclusions can be drawn. This ap-
proach of this study, along with a variety of established CRP activity assays,
could prove to be a valuable tool for use in cell factory construction.

Introduction
Synthetic biology is a novel eld concerning the construction of synthetic gene circuits, which
enable sustainable cell factories and other highly specic biological functions. Biosensors are
critical parts of synthetic gene circuits, as they provide a response to a specic signal, which
enable direct measurements of outputs in cell factories. This reaction is mediated by a sensory
element and a response element. The sensory element is activated by a signal, e.g. an eector, and
then activates the response element. The response is often transcriptional regulation, resulting
in a quantiable output such as uorescence or β-galactosidase activity [1].
In the context of bacterial biosensors, the two biosensor elements are either separate proteins
(two-component) or domains within the same protein (one-component) [1, 2]. Two-component
systems consist of a membrane-bound sensory kinase, which phosphorylates a response regula-
tor [3]. This phosphorylation pattern is highly conserved, easing the swapping of components
between systems [4].
The potential combinations are, however, limited in the availability of
components. One-component systems usually consists of an eector domain and a DNA-binding
domain, and they are often transcriptional regulators.
The abundance of diverse transcrip-
tion factors enables virtually endless combinations of domains [5].
However, the domains of
one-component biosensors are dicult to swap due to complex protein interactions. A reliable
approach for changing the specicity of domains has yet to be presented.
Most applications of synthetic biology combine top-down approaches for modifying existing sys-
tems with bottom-up approaches for novel design of systems [6, 7]. An alternative approach to
synthetic biology is synthetic evolution, which also acts top-down but relies on functional selection
in a designed environment. Some researchers have emphasized the potential of evolution-based
approaches to synthetic biology, arguing that evolution may provide solutions that design can-
not [810]. Application of synthetic evolution in the construction of one-component biosensors
enables the possibility of evolving existing domains to new functions, as opposed to the tedious
protein engineering associated with the swapping of domains.
The global regulator cAMP-receptor protein (CRP) is one of the most well-studied transcriptional
regulators of Escherichia coli. In CRP, the eector domain binds cyclic adenosine monophosphate
(cAMP), and the ensuing conformational change enables the DNA-binding domain, resulting
in regulation hundreds of operons on the E. coli genome [11]. Applying CRP as a biosensor
could be relevant for application within synthetic biology, where alternative signal and response
combinations would expand on the possibilities and complexity of synthetic gene circuits.
Recently, a synthetic evolution assay, hereafter termed the papillae assay, was seen to mainly
target CRP [12]. The assay has since been applied to study phenotypic eects of CRP mutations
(previous work), and it may be applied in the study of CRP evolution for alternative eector
responses.
An alternative eector, cyclic guanine monophosphate (cGMP), binds CRP as a
competitive inhibitor [13], and so the evolution of cGMP-sensitive CRP is not implausible. cGMP
may be ideal for signaling, as the cGMP concentration in E. coli is estimated at two orders of
magnitude below that of cAMP, with no conclusive evidence of physiological function [14].
The further advancement of synthetic evolution has been hindered by cumbersome and low-
throughput screening methods, and thus the development of a high-thoughput workow for the
papillae assay is imperative for its future applications. The emergence of ecient screening meth-
ods could also contribute to a better understanding of evolutionary mechanisms. Understanding
evolution on a molecular level could help improve stability of cell factories and provide invaluable
data in the future study of mutagenic diseases such as cancer.

The cAMP receptor protein (CRP)
The cAMP receptor protein (CRP) of E. coli is a global regulator, which, when activated by the
allosteric eector cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP), regulates more than 300 operons [15,
16]. The most famous of these encode enzymes for utilization of certain sugars, thus justifying its
other name, catabolite activator protein (CAP). CRP activates transcription by direct interaction
with RNA polymerase at e.g. the gal, lac and malT promoters, which control the expression of
genes required for metabolization of galactose, lactose and maltose, respectively. However, some
operons are also negatively regulated by CRP, as in the case of the gene encoding the adenylate
cyclase Cya, as well as CRP by negative autoregulation [15, 17, 18].
1.1.1
The glucose phosphotransferase system (PTS) of E. coli
The phenomenon of glucose preference over other sugars in bacteria was discovered more than 70
years ago [19]. It was observed as two distinct exponential growth phases (termed diauxie) in the
presence of glucose and another sugar. Sugars were then classied based on their metabolization
in the presence of glucose; those that did not show diauxie (fructose, mannose and mannitol,
class A), and those that did show diauxie (i.a. lactose and maltose, class B). The phenomenon of
preferential carbon sources was later termed carbon catabolite repression, and has been observed
in many microorganisms including both bacteria and fungi [1921].

a complex regulatory phenomenon involv-
ing the phosphotransferase system (PTS,
[23]), which is responsible for the active
import of certain sugars. The proteins in
a PTS can be separated into those that
are sugar-specic (EII) and those that are
not (EI and Hpr). At least 21 dierent
EII complexes have been identied, which
function as transporters for i.a. glucose,
fructose and mannitol [2426].
In the glucose PTS, glucose is imported
by the EII-Glc complex and phosphory-

The phosphate originates from
phosphoenolpyruvate in the glycolysis,
and is transferred through EI and Hpr
to EII-Glc (Figure 1).
If glucose is
abundant, EII will continuously transfer
the phosphate to glucose.
If glucose is
not present, EII will phosphorylate Cya,
which in turn produces cAMP. As cAMP activates CRP, this allows CRP to activate transcription
of operons, which encode enzymes for utilization of alternative sugars.

Each CRP monomer consists of two main domains, the N-terminal domain (residues 1-140), and

(residues 136-137 and 171-181) [31]. In this study, mentions of cAMP binding will refer to the
former site alone, unless otherwise mentioned.
In the closed conformation, DNA binding anity is low [32]. Upon binding, cAMP interacts
with both the monomer it is binding within and the other monomer (Figure 2, B, [30]). This
cAMP binding does not promote dimerization but rather stabilizes the dimer [33]. However,
cAMP binding causes tension in the tertiary structures, leading to repositioning of the C-helices
and the N-terminal domains though the hinge region, resulting in the open conformation of
CRP [34]. In open conformation, the DNA-binding HTH motif is protruding from the protein,
enabling DNA binding (Figure 2, B) [28, 29]. The CRP dimer binds cAMP with a dissociation
constant of 1-20 µM, indicating favourable association [27, 33, 35, 36]. The dimer also binds
cGMP at a similar dissociation rate as cAMP (residues 72-73, 83-84 and 131, [37]), but this does
not cause conformational change, and cGMP is thus a competitive inhibitor of CRP [13, 33,
35, 38]. However, the concentrations of cGMP required for competitive binding are most likely
physiologically irrelevant, as the intracellular cGMP concentration has been shown to two orders

Research have shown distinct CRP behaviours depending on in vitro cAMP concentration and

1.1.3
Mutations in CRP
Mutations in CRP, which aect cAMP and DNA binding anity, have been studied for more than
40 years [40, 41]. The rst mutant CRP proteins discovered exhibited only little transcriptional
activation no matter the cAMP concentration [40]. These proteins might have been decient in
cAMP or DNA binding. Since then, CRP mutants have been studied extensively (Figure 3).
Mutant CRP proteins causing altered transcriptional activation, e.g. E56K and Q171K, may
have mutations disrupting RNA polymerase interactions [42, 43]. This is often recognized by
distinct behaviours at dierent promoters, as activating CRP binding sites are located on average
either 61 bp (class I promoters) or 41 bp (class II promoters) from the transcriptional start site.
On their own, K53N causes increased transcription from class II promoters, while H160L renders
the protein inactive; however, a combination of the two reverses the protein activity to wild-

Another class of mutants, which show cAMP-independent CRP phenotypes, has been termed

These can be separated into two categories; those that shift the protein equilibrium

also part of the cAMP binding site [34, 52, 53]. These mutations may cause a CRP* phenotype
by simulating cAMP binding, thus causing a complete eector-independent active state.
Although many CRP mutants have been isolated following mutagenesis using e.g. UV or X-ray
techniques, some have also occurred during laboratory selection, whether intended or not. It
is not dicult to imagine, evolutionary, why mutations in CRP may be advantageous during
stress, as even single mutations in a global regulator could rapidly result in changed behaviour,
resulting in a large chance of escaping the selective pressure [54].

Theories of evolution
Figure 4: Common illustration of paradigms within evo-
lutionary theory. Top: Lamarckism. Use vs. disuse; due
to limited food resources, the giraes stretch their necks,
which then become longer. Bottom: Darwinism. Muta-

to reach their food will die, and only tall giraes will live

In
the
early
19th
centry,
Jean-Baptiste
Lamarck pioneered the biological eld of evo-
lution by formulating the rst evolutionary
theory. His theory, known as the Lamarckian
paradigm, concerned two concepts; use versus
disuse, in which organ function will change in
response to selection, and inheritance of ac-
quired traits, in which all traits acquired dur-
ing a lifetime will be passed on to ospring
[55].
This paradigm assumes that any kind
of selection causes advantageous mutations,
enabling the accumulation of such mutations
during a lifetime (Figure 4, top).
In 1859, Charles Darwin published the rst
work to suggest independence between mu-
tation and selection [56]. This paradigm in-
ferred that since individuals vary due to her-
itable genetic diversity, individuals have un-
equal chances for survival and reproduction,
and thus populations will change over time (Figure 4, bottom). In the following century, the
works of Darwin were widely accepted, and subsequently modelled into modern evolutionary
theory also known as neo-Darwinism [57, 58].

Synthetic evolution
Synthetic evolution was rst applied in the late 19th century [59], and ever since the emergence
of modern microbiology in the mid-20th century, synthetic evolution has been applied to study
mechanisms and phenomenas of bacterial growth [6063].
Synthetic evolution, like the natural phenomenon, concerns the generation of genetic diversity and
subsequent selection. Genetic diversity can be generated by tools imitating natural mechanisms,
e.g. error-prone PCR, or genetic shuing of DNA or exons [64], resulting in a diverse library of
mutants. This library can then be subjected to selection by deliberate experimental design.
A signicant concern within synthetic evolution is the generation of genetic diversity. Too little or
too slow, and experiments will not be feasible; too much or too fast, and the eect of each single
mutation will not be possible to elucidate. Some assays depend on mutator backgrounds, which
increase the general mutation rate and may enable not so easily available solutions. However,
this makes it dicult to maintain a stable genotype, leading to diculties in reproducibility
of experiments.
Furthermore, mutator phenotypes often involve a knockout of DNA repair
systems, resulting in potential accumulation of disadvantageous mutations and a loss of viability.
Thus, mutator backgrounds are not sustainable sources of genomic diversity [63]. Spontaneous
mutations, in accordance with neo-Darwinism, have been shown to occur rarely and randomly
in growing cultures, and thus constitutes a slow but natural source of genetic diversity [65].
Methods such as Adaptive Laboratory Evolution (ALE) depend on spontaneous mutations and
selects for certain traits without regard to the mutations by which they are achieved, which may
complicate downstream or commercial applications of the strain [62].

1.3.1
Adaptive mutations
One eld within synthetic evolution has questioned the randomness of spontaneous mutations as
proposed by neo-Darwinian. In the late 1980s, Cairns et al. studied an E. coli strain expressing
a translational fusion of lacI-lacZ with a +1 frameshift in a ∆lacZ background [66, 67]. The
frameshift rendered the strain Lac-, however Lac+ revertants were observed when the strain
was studied as a supposedly non-dividing (quiescent) population on lactose minimal medium
agar plates. The revertants were not observed until the second day of incubation, indicating a
lack of the preformed mutations. The authors themselves suggested a directed mechanism of
mutagenesis in non-dividing populations, which allows for the resumption of growth.
Thus, the denition of adaptive mutations are those arising in response to selection as opposed to
true spontaneous mutations. The phenomenon has been studied for decades [6671], and it has
been a subject of controversy, as it suggests mutagenesis induced by selection, which contradicts
neo-Darwinism.
Because of growth biases, the phenomenon is dicult to study in dividing
populations, and thus most studies concern adaptive mutations in non-dividing populations. It
is, however, dicult to prove that populations are non-dividing, and so this is often questioned
in studies of adaptive mutations.
Many authors throughout the years have observed the phenomenon, resulting in dierent models
for adaptive mutations. The rst model, termed stress-induced directed mutagenesis, was pro-
posed by Cairns et al. to explain the results mentioned above. The model assumes that cells are
able to direct mutations to targets, which will lead to optimal outcomes. Some mechanisms pro-
posed to mediate this have been disproven [72]. The second model is the general hypermutable
state, which proposes stress-induced hypermutagenesis in only a subset of a population [71]. In
the context of the Cairns experiment, this state is not found to be the major mechanism respon-
sible for reversion [73, 74], and it has been shown to be theoretically unfeasible [75]. Both of the
above models contradict neo-Darwinism by assuming programmed, stress-induced mutagenesis
and thus mutation in response to selection.
The Cairns experiments were widely questioned due to the supposed Lamarckian observation.
A crucial aspect of the observation was that the study concerned a non-dividing population.
This aspect was questioned, as a third model, the amplication model, was proposed to account
for the results of the Cairns experiment in a minimally dividing population. The amplication
model assumes a random, preformed duplication of the defect lacZ allele, so that when growth
begins to stall, the multiple defect lacZ alleles provide a growth benet. The selection imposed
then selects for lacZ amplication events, as opposed to reversion, although an increase in lacZ
copy number gradually increases chances of reversion of that gene [74, 76]. This model accounts
for all data from the Cairns experiment from a neo-Darwinian perspective, however assumes a
dividing population.
Another model, which also rejects the Lamarckian paradigm, is transcription-associated muta-
genesis (TAM). This model assumes that single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) is more susceptible to
DNA lesions, and as DNA is single-stranded within the transcription bubble, transcribed regions
will accumulate more spontaneous DNA lesions that non-transcribed regions [77]. However, un-
der this model, the coding strand would be favoured for mutagenesis, as this is purely ssDNA in
the transcription bubble, while the transcribed strand is partially protected. Thus, at least one
cell division, xing a coding strand mutation on both strands, would be required for outgrowth
of mutants.

1.3.2
Retromutagenesis
Observations of adaptive mutations have also been made that cannot be accounted for by the
above models. An E. coli strain carrying an ochre mutation in tyrA, which changes a sense
codon into a UAA stop codon, was subjected to tyrosine starvation as a non-dividing popula-
tion. Tyrosine-independent mutants then emerged, carrying mutations not associated with tRNA
suppressors or reversion of the ochre mutation. This indicated a mechanism for advantageous
mutations, which cannot be explained by the amplication of the defect tyrA [78]. The author
instead proposed another model, termed retromutagenesis, which describes how a mutation may
be advantageous to the cell before being xed on the genome.
Retromutagenesis occurs in non-dividing cells, where ssDNA lesions will accumulate over time,
in accordance with the TAM model. Should a miscoding DNA lesion occur on the transcribed
strand, it enables the production of a mutant protein, which may then enable replication and
cell division, resulting in xation of that mutation in the genome (Figure 5). As DNA lesions
will accumulate faster within transcribed regions, the otherwise completely random mutations
will be biased in favor of regions associated with stress responses [79].
Observations of directed mutagenesis in non-dividing populations support the model of retro-
mutagenesis. Furthermore, two types of point mutations have been shown to dominate dur-
ing stationary phase, cytosine deamination (C:G→T:A, C-deam [80]), and oxidation of guanine
(G:C→T:A, 8-oxoG [81]) [82]. 8-oxoG mutations increase in frequency over time due to increased
oxidative stress in ageing populations [8385]. C-deam is one of the most frequent spontaneous
DNA lesions, and it has been shown to occur at 100-fold the rate in ssDNA than in dsDNA
[86]. C-deam has also been shown to be associated with oxidative stress [80]. These mutation
types are thus prime candidatse for observations in accordance with retromutagenesis and TAM.
Besides experimental observations of adaptive mutations, retromutagenesis is thought to play a
role in the development of tumours and antibiotic resistance [87].

1.3.3
The papillae assay
The most recent evidence in favor of retromutagenesis appeared in the work of Sekowska et al.,
who studied adaptive mutations in an E. coli MG1655 cyaA mutant [12]. This mutant is decient
in the production of cAMP, and thus it cannot utilize certain sugars, whose utilization requires
transcriptional activation by the cAMP-CRP complex.
The strain was plated on maltose MacConkey medium [88], which enables the strain to form
small, white colonies due to peptones in the medium, before growth stalls and the cells are
caught in a quiescent state. Mutations, which enabled the utilization of maltose, would result
in a red colony due to a pH indicator in the medium. These red, protruding colonies (papillae)
arose from day 4 and onward, indicating that mutations were not preformed (Figure 6, C). This
assay, termed the papillae assay in this study, was monitored for seven weeks, and 96 papillae
were picked up, characterized and genome sequenced.
The genome sequencing revealed the presence of targeted regions throughout the genome. The
most extreme target was crp, which encodes the CRP protein, with an average of more than
one crp mutation per papillae. Of all missense single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), more
than 95% were of types C-deam or 8-oxoG, and at least 84% of these mutations occurred on
the transcribed strand (Figure 6, A). Sequences of the crp gene of further >500 papillae showed
that more than 88% of missense SNPs were of types C-deam or 8-oxoG, and more than 99% of
these mutations occurred on the transcribed strand (Figure 6, B). This distinct bias in favor of
the transcribed strand, along with the frequency of mutation types associated with stationary
phase, is to date the clearest evidence in favor of retromutagenesis.
It is not dicult to imagine, why the CRP protein would be targeted in adaptive mutations,
as a mutant of this protein might circumpass the need for cAMP-mediated activation and thus
allow the strain to grow. Such mutations, resulting in cAMP-independent CRP (CRP*), have
previously been shown to be rare during exponential growth [89]. Despite this, 93% of the genome
sequenced papillae harboured A145T or A145E mutations in crp, which have been shown to result
in CRP*. It is remarkable that position A145 is such a large part of the mutational solution
space of crp, when several CRP* mutants have been characterized previously (section 1.1.3).

study of genetic diseases such as cancer.
This study attempted to direct adaptive mutations in CRP, by studying the change in mutational
solution spaces during supplementation with alternative small molecule eectors such as cGMP.
By changing the conditions for retromutagenesis rescue, mutants with outgrowth depending on

These residues are thus prime candidates for evolving CRP to be a cGMP receptor protein by
synthetic evolution.

tended for application within the same workow as other strains. The CRP of Pseudomonas
putida was applied for heterologous expression, as this protein is homologous to the E. coli CRP
in all but intracellular function, with the property of higher cAMP sensitivity. This would serve
to study whether the functional eects of adaptive mutations are evolutionarily conserved. Two
recoded versions of CRP from E. coli were also constructed with identical amino acid sequences
but altered GC% content. The GC% content is important in this context, as 8-oxoG and C-deam
dominate adaptive mutations by retromutagenesis [12]. Thus, by varying the GC% content, the
mutational potential of each codon changes, in terms of probability and possible amino acid
changes.

Illumina® Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS)
Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS) is a group of sequencing technologies developed during the
1990s, all recognized by being high-throughput methods with up to billions of bases sequenced for
each run. In this study, NGS will be used to refer to Illumina® sequencing technology. Illumina®
sequencing, also known as sequencing by synthesis, is a multiplex sequencing technology, which
enables the sequencing of many DNA molecules at once in one ow cell.
Before sequencing, DNA molecules are prepared by two rounds of PCR amplication (Figure 8).
In the rst round, the DNA sequences are amplied from a template, and adapters are added
to enable library preparation. During the second round, the library preparation PCR, these
adapters are used for annealing of indexing oligonucleotides, which adds an index as well as the
sequencing adapter required for annealing to the ow cell. The index is a short DNA sequence,
which functions as a barcode for identication of each DNA molecule, thus enabling multiplex
sequencing.
The DNA molecules to be sequenced are added to the ow cell, and copies of the DNA molecules
will be synthesized from the adapters. This process takes place until clusters of identical DNA

in question were made electrocompetent by washing an over night or over day culture with ice

Allelic exchange of E. coli crp
The crp gene of E. coli, crpE.coli was sought exchanged with the homologous gene from P. putida,
crpP.putida, as well as with two synonymously recoded versions of crpE.coli with a lower and higher
GC content, crpLM and crpMM, respectively.
When recoding crp, the codon for each amino acid was evaluated for potential exchange. Not all
codons were changed, in order to avoid unnecessary eects on translation. First of all, codons
for some amino acids were left unchanged, as only one codon was available, or the mutation
potential by 8-oxoG and Cdeam mutations was deemed too insignicant.
Secondly, if some
amino acids were encoded by at least two codons, of which two had equal GC content, only
codons with non-optimal GC content were changed. Finally, rare codons were avoided as much
as possible [96]. All codons for recoding can be seen in Table 6. Constructs carrying the E. coli
crp promoter and a coding sequence (crpP.putida, crpLM, or crpMM, seen in Appendix D), were
previously constructed based on plasmid PF130, resulting in plasmids PF118-PF120 (previous

Processing of amplicon sequencing data
For data processing, sucient data capacity was necessary, in order to minimize potential biases
against rare sequences. Furthermore, the chosen platform for data processing was required to be
applicable as well as suciently functionally transparent for amplicon sequencing experiments.
For this reason, CLC Genomics Workbench (CLC bio) was chosen.
Several workows for NGS sequencing experiments were consulted online, but no ideal workow
for the exact experiment of this study existed.
The nal workow presented below and in
Figure 11 largely follows those recommended by CLC bio for resequencing experiments [99] and
the GATK Best Practices workow for germline short variant discovery (Broad Institute, [100]),
adapted for CLC Genomics Workbench functions.
FastQ les were imported into CLC Genomics Workbench for data processing as non-paired
reads. Reads were trimmed by average quality scores (discarding those with p > 0.001), length
(discarding those < 80 bp), ambiguous nucleotides (not allowed), and adapters. For adapter
trimming, detection of the crp-annealing part of the adapter oligos MSB3602-MSB3607 would
result in trimming of the said sequence and the entire 5' sequence before that (Table 9, left).
Following initial data analysis (section 3.4), another round of sequence-based trimming was
added. The second trimming was based on a 10 bp sequence in common between forward and
reverse adapters, in order to remove faulty read-through adapter errors formed during library
prep (Table 9, right). All sequence-based trimming followed standard alignment settings with a
mismatch cost of 2, a gap cost of 3, a minimum internal score of 10, and a minimum end score
of 4.
Trimmed reads were then mapped to a reference sequence (Figure 10) using global alignment
with standard alignment settings of a match score of 1, a mismatch cost of 2, a linear gap cost
of 3, and length fraction and similarity fraction at 0.5 and 0.8, respectively. Following this,
the reads were examined for structural variants (quality p < 0.0001 and a maximum number
of mismatches = 3 for end breakpoints), including deletions and insertions, and based on this
information, were realigned using local realignment (two iterations, maximum guidance-variant
length 200 bp).
Nucleotide variants were called and annotated by the software with the condition that variants
were called only, if coverage of that position was above 100, and if that specic variant was
present in at least two reads. Other variant call conditions included a neighbourhood quality
lter of p < 0.0003 in a 5 base radius and no set ploidy. As a nal action, variant ltering should
be performed, however for this study, no existing background data set for crp mutations existed,
and thus this was not done.

Results and discussion
Throughout this study, parallel eorts was put into strain characterization and construction, as
well as development of a high-throughput workow for mutational data from the same strains.
One signicant hurdle in the processing and analysis of NGS data was the lack of previous data
sets or experience with the experimental setup. Due to this lack of comparable data, this study
leans heavily on previous knowledge about the mutational solution space of CRP [12], as well as
the above mentioned characterizations of strains.

cyaA strains show distinct phenotypes on cyclic nucleotides
The phenotypes of cyaA strains in the presence of alternative nucleotide eectors were charac-
terized in vivo on maltose MacConkey plates. Without any nucleotide supplements, MSB779
(cyaA), MSB1034 (cyaA crp(A145T)), and MSB1035 (cyaA crp(A145E)) are all white (Fig-

however, papillae form at an indetermined rate far surpassing that of MSB1034 without cAMP
(Figure 55, Appendix F). Increased mutagenesis could indicate higher genetic diversity, which
may be an evolutionary advantage during partial eector starvation.

Partial CRP activation causes a shift in the mutational solution space
The similarity between MSB779 phenotypes on 0.1 mM cAMP and 0.5 mM cGMP (Figure 13)
indicates a cNMP-specic mechanism more sensitive to cAMP. When considering MSB1034 and
MSB1035 as well, the mutational behaviour of increased papillae formation may be associated
with partial CRP activation. These genotypes and environmental conditions will be kept in mind
as potential ways to generate many papillae and achieve higher genetic diversity.
An increase in genetic diversity may be caused by an increase and/or shift of the mutational
solution space. According to the hypothesis of retromutagenesis, the potential of the mutational
solution space is limited to the ssDNA lesions in non-dividing populations.
However, when
dealing with partial CRP activation, the population can no longer be assumed to be non-dividing.
This aects the mutational solution space, as non-rescuing ssDNA lesions may become xed in
the genome.
According to the hypothesis of this study, supplementing with cAMP or cGMP may rescue cells
with mutations not rescued by retromutagenesis under normal conditions. When supplementing
MSB779 with 0.5 mM cGMP, A145T/E/V mutations constitute more than 40% of the mutational
solution space. This is a decrease compared to no nucleotide supplement (90%, [12]), and the
A145V variant has not previously been seen in the papillae assay. Another 40% of the mutational
solution space consists of G142 mutations, of which two have been spotted rarely for MSB780
(G142S/D), and one has not been seen before (G142R). The latter is known to be associated
with a cGMP-specic CRP homolog (section 1.4), but the phenotype of the mutant does not
deviate from the parent strain. The prevalence of previously rare G142S/D mutation is also
seen for MSB779 in the presence of 0.1 mM cAMP (I. Lauritsen, unpublished), indicating that
these may be CRP mutants, which depend on a cyclic nucleotide for activity, but has decreased
eector specicity. Although the mutational solution spaces are similar, they are not identical,
which arms the possibility of eector-induced shifts in the mutational solution space of CRP.
In an attempt to shift CRP from cAMP to cGMP-specic activation, MSB1034 and MSB1035
were grown in the presence of both 0.5 mM cAMP, which is toxic, and 0.1 mM cGMP. To a
cGMP-specic CRP* mutant, the low concentration of cGMP should not be toxic, and therefore
specicity-shifting mutations in a population should be rescued if present. Under these condi-
tion, 55% of MSB1034 and MSB1035 papillae had additional CRP mutations. When restreaked,
one MSB1034 papillae was white without nucleotide supplements, red on 0.5 mM cGMP, and
white on 0.5 mM cAMP, a phenotype which might be associated with cGMP specicity. However,
that mutant had no additional CRP mutations. Of the CRP mutations, half were previously
known variants, and the other half are assumed to disrupt DNA binding (G185R, A199E, Q171
frame shift). Thus, no cGMP-specic CRP* were found. This might be due to excessive stress
by the total of 0.6 mM added cyclic nucleotides, or the requirement of more than one mutation
(e.g. residues 84, 128-129 or 131) for a shift in specicity.

Alternative CRP alleles may aect the mutational solution space
Alternative CRP alleles were also considered for application within the papillae assay, in order to
study the mutational solution space of CRP. The characterization of the strains with alternative
CRP alleles is seen in Figure 15 and Figure 60-65, Appendix F.
In strains PF266 and PF269, the crp gene was exchanged with the homologous CRP from P.
putida in a MSB559 (WT) or MSB779 (cyaA) background, respectively. When streaked and
plated on maltose MacConkey, colonies were white no matter the concentration of added cAMP.
Papillae formed rapidly in PF266 without cAMP, potentially indicating partial CRP activity as
discussed in the previous section. Thus, CRPP.putida cannot directly complement the native CRP.
Interestingly, all PF266 papillae arising were red when restreaked without nucleotide supplement,
indicating WT-like metabolization of maltose. When a few of these were sequenced, none had
mutations in CRP. Of PF269, when plated on cAMP, one papillae had the CRP V2I A89V
genotype, which did not result in a CRP* phenotype.
Due to the lack of functional CRP
complementation, strains PF266 and PF269 were not applied in further characteristics.
The LM-CRP and MM-CRP strains (PF267-PF268 in MSB559 background, and PF270-PF271
in MSB779 background) were characterized to see whether the recoded alleles could complement
the native allele. PF267 and PF268 were red after one day of incubation when plated without
nucleotide supplement, although the color was not as intense as for MSB559. PF270 and PF271
were white under the same conditions, as expected, and when supplemented with 0.1 mM cAMP,
the strains showed increased papillae formation as seen for MSB779. However, in the presence
of 0.5 mM cAMP, at which the cyaA knockout of MSB779 is usually fully complemented, PF270
and PF271 form white colonies with increased papillae formation rates.
This indicates that
the LM-CRP and MM-CRP alleles cannot complement native CRP function, potentially due to
translation issues following the recoding process.
Four papillae from each of PF270 and PF271 were sequenced. For PF270, two papillae had
A145T mutations, one had an G75C mutation, and one had a synonymous P6P mutation with
reversion to the native codon. Although mutations in G75 have not previously been seen, this
mutational solution is available to the recoded as well as the native allele. The synonymous P6P
mutation indicates that translation issues might be the cause of the inadequate complementation.
For PF271, no papillae had CRP mutations, and therefore translation issues cannot be ruled out.
In summary, no evidence in this study suggests that the mutational solution space can be shifted
by the recoding of genes. Unfortunately, construction of strains PF266-PF271 was not nished
in time to thoroughly assay and assess the mutational solution space of the strains. Further
characterization and optimization of these strains is required, if they are to be applied with
condence in high-throughput assays.

Perspectives
4.1
|
Studies of the papillae assay
Compared to the study of Sekowska et al. [12] (in the following termed the previous study),
which applied the same papillae assay, the NGS data of the current study (in the following termed
this study) points to a dierent conclusion regarding the hypothesis of retromutagenesis. It is
worth noting that the two studies concern dierent aspects of retromutagenesis, as the previous
study concerned mutants rescued from quiescence, and this study concerns a population of CRP
variants, whether these have rescued a cell from quiescence or not. In this context, the previous
study only considered missense mutations for data analysis, as the likelihood of rescuing cells from
quiescence by loss of function or silent mutations is unlikely. This study, however, regarded all
SNP variants equally, as the hypothesized increase in 8-oxoG variants and general accumulation
of variants in the population over time should be unbiased in regards to functional eects of the
variants.
The strongest evidence in favor of retromutagenesis in the previous study was 1) the bias for
mutations on the transcribed DNA strand, and 2) the increased formation of 8-oxoG mutations
over time.
In this study, many papillae variants detected by NGS (Table 10) were variants
previously seen, including both potential C-deam and 8-oxoG variants. However, 8-oxoG variants
did not increase in frequency over time, and the relative number of C-deam and 8-oxoG variants
over time remained constant. These are the key observations in this study, which seem to refute
retromutagenesis.
The observations could be due to a dierence in protocol. In the previous study, papillae were
selected as soon as they appeared, while this study applied populations of a set age. Thus,
in this study, some papillae may have been weeks old when sampled. As some papillae CRP
variants, e.g. A145T or A145E, are known to cause increased mutagenesis, the prolonged growth
of papillae may cause additional mutagenesis. The mutational solution space of MSB1034 has
been shown to be dierent than that of MSB780, which may result in variants occurring after
A145T being unrecognizable from true MSB780 variants. Furthermore, this study introduces
many amplication biases through PCR, library preparation and sequencing, and 8-oxoG has
been shown to cause decreased elongated speed of DNA [81], potentially obscuring these variants
as well. However, any eect of the above scenarios would be assumed to be either constant for
all samples or erratic, and thus the presence of clearly constant trends for variants is noteworthy
(Figure 19 and 23).
Another interesting observation in this study is that of papillae variants being observed in the
pre-plating sample of day 0, which suggests preformed variants. Although such low frequency
variants could be sequencing artefacts, this is unlikely, as specic papillae variants such as A145T
persist at low frequencies throughout all time points, with the exception of papillae formation.
One would assume SNP-like sequencing artefacts to be random. Furthermore, as the patterns
of papillae development in regards to time-dependence and papillae genotypes remain consistent
with the previous study, the detected pre-plating variants may have been present, although
undetected, in the previous study as well.

Evaluation of NGS workow
Throughout this study, the workow for NGS was continuously evaluated and updated. However,
several potential issues still remain.
The most prominent question remaining is, whether detection of variants could be improved
by increasing sequencing coverage. However, as stated, it is dicult to know whether detected
variants, the number of which is shown to increase as a function of coverage, are sequencing
artefacts or an innite solution space of non-rescuing variants. This complicates the application
of the high-throughput workow, if no background information is available.
Furthermore, as more than 1010 cells may be present on a plate, and the MiSeq sequencing
system can only generate 20 million reads of 150 bp per run, it is economically unfeasible to
obtain a coverage high enough to condently read every single allele in a population. In fact, the
high identities of the applied libraries, with 15 million bases per sample mapped to a reference
sequence of only 727 bp, causes the internal quality lter of the sequencing system to believe an
error has occurred, resulting in a large amount of sequencing data being discarded before the
output of a run is generated. For these reasons, increasing coverage may cause more problems
that the value it adds.
Another approach for increased condence in the data would be to ensure equal coverage across
the reference sequence. In the data sets for run 1 and 2, coverage within a single sample varies
60-fold between regions, potentially resulting in variants of low coverage regions being called
at a higher frequency than they should and vice versa. The low-coverage regions were found
in the middle of amplicons, as short read lengths of 150 bp from each end was insucient for
amplicons of up to 400 bp. Besides resulting in unequal representation of the reference sequence,
it complicates the comparison of variants, and makes it dicult to establish a condence-level
of frequency, should one wish to do so. This could be remedied by longer read lengths, as was
applied for run 3, with 2x300 bp reads as opposed to 2x150 bp reads.
With 300 bp reads, the reads for each end of the same sequence overlaps, which due to paired
processing enables more ecient trimming for adapter sequences.
Run 3 was unfortunately
not completed in time for sucient data processing and analysis. Preliminary data processing
suggests a large amount of discarded reads due to the paired data processing, but only a 6-fold
dierence between highest and lowest coverage within a sample.
This should result in more
reliable variant calls and higher condence of correlated variants.

Directing adaptive mutations within CRP
When only considering sample-specic variants, it is clear that certain variants are promoted in
the presence of specic cyclic nucleotides. This arms the possibility of applying NGS on several
populations to determine papillae variants in a high-throughput manner. However, under the
conditions applied in this study, no cGMP-specic CRP* variants were discovered.
Variants such as T141P and G142R occur as cGMP-specic papillae variants, and may be asso-
ciated with cGMP specicity within CRP. These variants do not, however, cause cGMP-specic
activation of CRP. The absence of cGMP-specic CRP* variants within the data set is best
explained by the requirement of more than one mutation for a change in specicity. The ex-
perimental set up was designed to promote the occurrence of CRP* variants, and thus cGMP
supplementation may results in the rescue of CRP* variants depending on any cyclic nucleotide
as opposed to cGMP-specic CRP*.

Promoting cGMP-specic CRP* mutants would require accumulation of the necessary DNA le-

it might occur under dierent circumstances. The pTIG plasmid of MSB780 causes increased
papillae formation, presumably by expediting the folding of mutant proteins, which would be
undesired if the objective is increased accumulation of DNA lesions. Furthermore, since utiliza-

carbon source will limit rescue of these. In such a case, large genetic diversity could be gener-

could then be transferred to a medium with cGMP and a CRP-dependent carbon source. This
may promote growth of both CRP* and cGMP-specic CRP* mutants, increasing the chance of
discovering such variants using NGS.

Further work
Other areas of interest for future work include the alternative CRP alleles constructed during this
study, which may be applied for studies into the mechanisms of adaptive mutations. This would,
however, require optimization of the alleles, as they are currently not functioning optimally.
For alternative alleles, heterologous or synthetic alike, it is important to ensure optimal transla-
tion. This potential issue is observed in the form of otherwise functionally irrelevant mutations
in the beginning of the alleles, such as V2I for CRPP.putida and P6P for CRPLM . CRPP.putida
can currently not complement native CRP function, potentially due to a dierence in recognition
sequence of the CRP homologs. This should also be adressed before CRPP.putida can be applied
in the papillae assay.
The study of dierent CRP alleles during the papillae assay could contribute to an increased
understanding of evolutionary mechanisms. It may also be relevant to study other potential
small-molecule eectors. The alternative nucleotides AMP and CMP should be revisited, and
other potential eectors for investigation could be messengers such as c-di-GMP or ppGpp.
Studying CRP function and evolution in combination may nally elucidate elements of CRP
regulation which have been studied for decades.

Conclusion
Throughout this study, the directed evolution of CRP was attempted through application of a
synthetic evolution assay and specically designed selective conditions. A workow for high-
throughput population-level data was also developed and deployed.
The next-generation sequencing workow was veried to be applicable, and revealed high genetic
diversity of crp within the assayed populations. Papillae variants in both small and large popu-
lations were successfully detected. Through both high and low-throughput characterizations of
the mutational solution space, it was determined that supplementation with cyclic nucleotides
to MSB780 signicantly aects papillae variants in a population. This conrms the hypothesis
that the synthetic evolution of crp may be directed by supplementation with small molecules,
in this case alternative nucleotide eectors. However, no cGMP-specic CRP* mutants were
discovered, and the experimental conditions should be reevaluated for ideal outgrowth of such
mutants.
The data presented in this study does not provide any evidence in favor of retromutagenesis, but
rather indicates that the hypothesis should be refuted. However, as the data evaluated consists
mostly of missense variants, it is not a reection of unbiased DNA lesions as hypothesized.
The data also contains noise in the form of false variance, resulting in less condence in the
conclusions drawn. The noise could be minimized by improving the workow for next-generation
sequencing. This should be completed in future work, in order to gain conclusive evidence for or
against retromutagenesis as a mechanism for adaptive mutations.
