Abstract
There is a growing interest in reducing overall energy consumption and transforming
to a more sustainable energy supply network. The energy consumption of buildings,
especially the residential stock, contribute to a signiﬁcant portion of ﬁnal energy con-
sumption in the European Union. To be able to plan and facilitate higher eﬃciency,
it is advantageous to have accurate models for estimating the demand of a district or
a city. Having a repository of high resolution energy demand time-series of buildings
which represent an entire building stock can provide these models with necessary input.
This paper aimed to advance the work carried out in the TABULA project by pro-
ducing high resolution energy demand time-series of the Danish single-family house
typologies/archetypes. The work is simulated in the dynamic simulation program IDA
ICE. The results are gauged against a set of measurements from Sønderborg by com-
paring the data to an aggregated sum of the modelled archetype energy demand.
Having initially overestimated the demand of the buildings, the models were adapted
by applying the same method used in the TABULA calculations. The adapted results
showed better agreement at annual levels however when more detailed examination was
conducted with higher resolution data points, there were signiﬁcant errors.
The prediction of annual energy consumption is a more straight forward process, how-
ever, to be able to accurately model an average building at high resolution requires a
vast amount of detailed empirically measured consumption data to provide accurate
validation. Having measurements from every city in a country would advance the work
greatly. This data is becoming more available as measurements are being made across
Denmark but due to processing it was not made available on time for this work.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Buildings are one of the most cherished assets we hold, providing shelter and security
to our lives. We generally strive to achieve the highest levels of comfort in our spaces,
and to maintain or even reach these levels, a very large amount of energy is required.
For many of the population, the understanding of energy demand within our homes or
building is vague and incomplete and may primarily be considered from an economic
perspective.
However, the reduction of the energy demand of buildings has been a
major focus for research in the past number of decades, be it governmental, academic
or private. With the rapid growth of the human population since the beginning of the
20th century and the equally rapid growth of the fossil fuel industry, we are now in
the situation of being predominantly dependant on ﬁnite resources. Other than the
ultimately ﬁnite nature of the resource, current supplies can not be guaranteed given
the complex political and ﬁnancial instability embedded within the industry. [7]
Estimates suggest that 40 % of Europe’s total ﬁnal energy consumption is associated to
building energy use, with 27% of the total being residential households [3]. Improving
the eﬃciency of the building stock is therefore paramount in order to reduce and provide
for the overall energy requirements. The European Union has implemented directives
obliging all member states to design and uphold policies to improve the energy eﬃciency
of their building stocks. Much of this work relates to new projects for the obvious reason
of practicality but the existing stock must also be carefully examined and advanced.
Renewable energy sources are becoming evermore economically and technically viable
and should therefore continue to expand within the current energy infrastructure. As

this type of energy source is also unstable due to reliance on climatic conditions such as
solar penetration and wind speeds, energy production levels are not guaranteed at all
times. This makes it important to approach and develop a strategy that can utilise and
control the harvested energy to its optimum potential. This puts a lot of focus on the
demand side of the energy equation by exploring methods to shift the demand towards
high production times along with other methods for reducing it. To plan and undertake
such a goal it is useful to have a methodological approach for estimating the energy
demands which exist. Whether it is for individual micro-generation units or large scale
power farms including district heating, it is important to understand and to be able to
estimate the magnitude of load on the systems.
The TABULA project which will be discussed further on in the report has assembled
a vast amount of data from participating partners. One beneﬁt of its method is the
simplicity and standardisation for undertaking the task of estimating energy demand,
but some shortfalls include the fact that it only provides its users with an annual result
for overall consumption. These results of course can be used in many ways and have
high value, but for the purpose of research into areas such as that mentioned in the
previous paragraph, it is much more beneﬁcial to have higher detailed resolution energy
demand results for a building. This allows a researcher to examine a predicted demand
at diﬀerent times of a year, month or day and focus on the best methods to meet this.
1.1.1
Project focus
It is intended to explore the methods which are used for estimating the energy demand
of a large number of buildings based upon models of statistically averaged buildings
which representing all types and characteristics within. This project is carried out with
a focus on modelling the energy demand for space heating and domestic hot water
(DHW) consumption of a series of representative residential buildings from the Danish
single family house housing stock.
Assessing the exact energy demand of each individual building in a stock requires huge
amounts of time and data collection to do so, and therefore to apply a statistical and
engineering approach of using archetypical building typologies created from statistical
data allows for energy demand modelling of large clusters of buildings with much less
eﬀort. By reviewing the existing modelling methods and sourcing available data, ad-
vancements can be made by using diﬀerent approaches and producing more detailed
results. This can help progress the research of the subject by providing additional data
and evaluating the methodology.

1.2
Aim and Objective
Having considering the motivation for the project a clear set of objectives can be deﬁned.
• Investigate the methods and approaches which are used to estimate residential
buildings energy use.
• Apply a methodology to create representative building energy models of a housing
stock and simulate detailed time-series results.
• Evaluate, verify and calibrate the models once complete.
• Assemble and store the models, the results and other relevant data in a repository
for future access.
• Oﬀer proposals and ideas of how to expand and improve the methodology based
upon the gained knowledge.
1.3
Research question
1. What is the importance of having a collection of archetype buildings?
2. What trends can be determined when comparing the results of modelled cases vs
real measured data?
3. Are simpliﬁed calibration methods accurate for detailed energy demand results?
1.4
Research approach
The approach of the project is to create a collection of building energy models which
should be representative of the Danish building stock. The intention of creating such
models is to assist the process of modelling a district or city using an aggregated ap-
proach and also provide reference material for other research which required detailed
data results. While the methodology will provide a framework for modelling all cat-
egories of buildings, it will only be applied to model the single family house (SFH)
category. These models will be simulated to provide detailed time-series results which
can then be compiled in a repository and used for analytic purposes. An overview chart
can be seen in ﬁgure 1.1 which includes the proposed steps for undertaking the project.
The type of models which are to be created would be considered grey-box/parameter
estimation approach based upon statistical data from the overall building stock. The
data used to create the models of the buildings is extracted from the the Danish inclu-
sion of the TABULA project in which the typology classiﬁcations were deﬁned. These

1.5
Limitations
Given that models can only approximate natural phenomena they are inherently inex-
act. The mathematical parameters and boundary conditions which are used to describe
the real systems are often uncertain and general assumptions must be made to proceed.
Despite these weaknesses, models are still robust tools for understanding the behaviour
of a system and can be strengthened by calibration and veriﬁcation.
Within the scope of the project and binding time limitations it is not always possible
or feasible to collect primary data. Being the case, this can put a number of limitations
on the researcher when dealing with existing data-sets. With a lack of transparency or
a detailed background, misunderstanding or uncertainties may occur. The collection of
data from residential buildings can also be hindered by privacy and legal issues which
protect an occupants information.
To perform an accurate validation of any model it is important to perform a detailed
evidence based investigation and calibrate accordingly if necessary. For this, detailed
high resolution measurements from a large sample of buildings is necessary. Some of the
TABULA data which which is required for the the adaptation process is not available for
further investigation and detailed description of its source or the methodology behind
its creation was not listed in the report.

Background
2.1
Energy modelling methods
Modelling the heat demand of a single building or an urban collection of buildings
can be undertaken in numerous ways and depending on the approach it can require a
signiﬁcant amount of data and can be a very complex task. The methods of tackling
this objective generally depend on the level of data and computational eﬀort which is
available for the task.
Common inputs for these models would include information about the physical prop-
erties of a building, the occupants, the climatic zone, historical energy demand data
or macroscopic (environmental or economic) indicators for a region. The acquisition of
this data can take substantial eﬀort and the level of detail or the range of which the
information covers will determine its usefulness for a model. Depending on the tech-
nique, a models can have diﬀerent applicability and ﬂexibility for estimating part of, or
a total system.
2.1.1
Overview approaches
2.1.1.1
Top-down and bottom-up
Two of the most commonly explored approaches for modelling a large system include
top-down and bottom-up. In ﬁgure 2.1, Swan [1] breaks down the two approaches into
the common techniques which exist today.
When considering a top-down approach the energy consumption of the residential sector
is seen as as a total energy sink and does not focus on speciﬁc individual cases. Various
known input and output variable of the systems are known and then it is attempted
to describe the system mathematically and afterwards estimate its parameters. It is
common to
Typical examples of inputs which can be used are historical aggregated levels indicators
such as national energy consumption or CO2 emissions [8] along with other technological
or economic indicators such as GDP, climate conditions and construction rates. Eco-
nomic models are mainly focused on price and income whilst the technological models
use broad characteristics of the entire housing stock. The advantage of using top down
methods is in its simplicity and low amount of input variables and the data is easier to
attain due to its aggregated and less private nature. [1]
A bottom up modelling approach considers input data which is at the “hierarchical
level less than that of the sector as a whole”. [1] The method attempts to determine
the demand for the diﬀerent types of individual cases in a system and extrapolate the
data in a weighted manner depending on the frequency of the type.

2.1 Energy modelling methods
9
This approach can be divided into both a statistical and a technical approach. The
statistical method works using historical data related to the end use energy of particular
cases and with the use of regression analysis it can be applied to representative dwellings
of the building stock for overall energy consumption estimation. On the other hand,
the engineering method works by explicitly modelling the expected energy use of a
particular case based upon it’s energy systems, appliances, climatic conditions and
other characteristics and properties of the building

2.1.1.2
White box, black box and grey box
To better understand building energy simulations Coakley et al. describe the diﬀer-
ent scientiﬁc modelling approaches. [2] They are broken up into either diagnostic or
prognostic and either law-driven or data-driven.
Diagnostic models are used to identify the nature or cause of a system behaviours. On
the other hand prognostic models are set to predict the system based upon a set of
speciﬁc well deﬁned laws that govern the system.
Law-driven models attempt to describe the system based on a given set of laws (e.g
heat/mass transfer) and will often require a large set of parameters. An advantage
of this sort of model is that it can predict unobserved conditions of a system through
simulation. Data-driven models on the other hand require an observation of a system
behaviour to predict its properties which is not always possible in reality.
Models can also be described as white-box, black-box and grey-box [9]. A white box
model is one which is solely based on physical knowledge of the system. This would be
considered a prognostic law driven model. Black box models are based upon responses
of the system which are monitored and then related to some of the potential inﬂuential
inputs. Grey-box models are diﬀerent in that they use some inputs which are not based

2.1.2
Urban building energy modelling
The concept of urban building energy modelling (UBEM) has become popular in recent
times especially within the realm of city planning, environment and energy.
When
describing the task of creating one of these large scale models Reinhart [10] outlines the
following subtasks: data input, thermal modelling and validation. These three tasks
will be discussed in later sections and subsections to gain further insight.
A number of computer applications have been developed over a number of years to carry
out UBEM with high levels of detail and in various formats. 3D spacial models are now
widely used which build a virtual 3D city model which can store geometrical and other

Nouvel [11] describes work carried out in Germany using the application CityGML
which provides a platform for 3D geospacial visualisation and analysing along with a
simulation platform SimStadt. An advantage of the program CityGML is its object

between a simple rectangular block for level 1 and a detailed modelling of the indoor

The work carried out in the project used information collected from both population

as building function codes which can be linked to the building IDs in the 3D model. It is
also mentioned the ability to add other various databases which can provide more local
data if applicable. When it comes to performing the heading load calculation for the
buildings, it is not possible to use customary building performance simulation software.
The programs generally require higher levels of input thermal data and are not designed
to handle the geometry from the city models. The solution for this is to utilise a quasi
static monthly energy balance calculation standardised with the ISO 13790.

2.1.4
Heat load modelling
Heat modelling techniques can vary signiﬁcantly by providing either simplistic rough
annual estimates, to more high resolution detailed results over a given year. Techniques
can generally be divided into quasi-steady-state method which works on the principle
where temperatures and ﬂow rates are kept constant over a suﬃciently long period of
time (month or season) and dynamic simulations which model a systems as it dynami-
cally changes over time. [14]
2.1.4.1
TABULA energy balance calculation
The energy balance calculations for residential buildings used by the TABULA project
are based upon the standard reference calculation of the quasi-steady-state EN ISO
13790 seasonal method and the EN ISO 15316 level B method for modelling energy
systems.
The overall approach was set up and simpliﬁed to increase transparency
between the experts in the diﬀerent countries and to allow for fast generation of models.
For the space heating calculations, the included partners in the project chose diﬀerent
modelling approaches depending on the statistical data which was available to them.
Certain external boundary conditions for the calculations are chosen to suit each coun-
try (external temperatures and solar gains) while internal utilisation conditions (room
temperature, internal gains, air exchange rate) are kept similar amongst partners.
The energy need for the building was calculated by calculating the following variables
in a one-zone model; heat transfer coeﬃcient by transmission, heat transfer coeﬃcient
by ventilation, solar heat gain and internal heat gain.
For the transmission losses, the area and U-value of roof, ﬂoor, walls and windows are
considered with a soil adjustment factor of 0.5 used for the ﬂoor transmission. Thermal
bridges are also included in the transmission losses as a surcharge add-on to the U-
values. Three categories (low/medium/high) are assigned for the procedure ranging
from 0.05 - 0.15 W/K per m2 of envelope area.

2.1.6
Empirical calibration
When a model is created it can be expected that there will be a deviation from the
real life instance. In the case of building energy models, lack of information about the
utilisation of the building such as temperature set point and air exchange rate along
with uncertainties about the actual thermal properties of the building and its heating
systems, make it diﬃcult to set absolutely precise parameters in the model.
It is general practice to compare model results to empirically measured data which can
provide a frame of reference for its accuracy. The TABULA project worked on a method
of calibration using an adaption factor to be multiplied by the standard calculation
results. This gives a second value referred to as "typical measured consumption". The
calibration factor is the ratio between typical measured consumption of energy demand
and the standard calculation result. The measured consumption values for this process
were intended to be created by each member and is linked to diﬀerent sources such as
representative samples or census data. TABULA provided three accuracy levels (A-C)
for the adaptation factor the which ranged between a value determined for the whole
building stock or a value estimated on the basis of a few example buildings. [21]
In his work [11], Nouvel describes what is called a "user-factor" which is introduced in
the algorithm aimed at adjusting the standardised heating demand result to get it closer
to the on-site reality. This method was developed after they realised that high energy
bills often inﬂuenced tenants to reduce their temperature set-points or only partially
heat their house in order to save energy. The values of adaptation generally ranged
from 0.85 for old houses to 1.1 for PassivHaus (a rigorous housing standard).
In a work which focuses on building energy models [22], Raaferty et al. discuss the
beneﬁts of calibration including the ability to gain knowledge during the process which
can help advance both the simulation tools and also the development of future models
by identifying common mistakes and assumptions in the models. He goes on however
to discuss some of the issues with the process of calibration given the sheer number of
parameters involved in a whole building model along with a general lack of suﬃcient
information available which can deem it an under-determined parameter space.

2.3
Energy consumption in buildings
The overall energy consumption of a building accounts for the sum of all energy services
within a building including heating and cooling systems, ventilation systems, domestic
hot water (DHW) production, electrical appliances, cooking and lighting. Electrical
appliances now consume a signiﬁcant amount of energy considering the signiﬁcant in-
crease in the number of units which are operating with a building in recent times. [3] A
big factor which determines the level of consumption in a building related to occupants
behaviour and the nature of the systems installed but also the characteristics mentioned
in the previous section such as the thermal characteristics and location.
When we examine the reported total annual energy consumption values for buildings
in Europe there are very large variations between diﬀerent countries. As revealed in a
audit of 193 buildings across Europe by Balaras et al. [29], annual consumption can
vary between a minimum of 31 kWh/m2 in Greece to a maximum of 763 kWh/m2
in Poland. The average for both countries were 108.4 kWh/m2 in Greece and 261.1
kWh/m2 in Poland respectively. Of course these large variations is due to the inﬂuence
of climatic conditions and the fact that the number of heating days is signiﬁcantly less
in the southern countries.
2.3.1
Energy eﬃciency of buildings
The energy eﬃciency of a building is an indicator of it’s performance level relative to
its energy demand. To achieve the desired indoor comfort standards in a building and
maintain a low level of ﬁnal energy use implies that a building has a high eﬃciency.
Many factors determine the eﬃciency of a building but the most critical characteristics
are the thermal properties of the overall building envelope. Important factors include
the heat transfer coeﬃcients of the building elements, the heat storage capacity of the
materials and also the level of losses from thermal bridging and low air tightness.
The main means of improving the eﬃciency of a building is to eliminate as many losses
in the overall building system as possible. Methods to achieve this include lowering the
overall U-value of the envelope by increasing the levels of thermal insulation, installation
of high quality windows and doors, eliminate thermal losses through inﬁltration and also
ensuring the most eﬃciency energy systems and appliances are installed. The use of
advanced heat recovery systems and automated building control systems are becoming
eﬀective methods for keeping the energy demand to a lower level.
2.3.2
Occupants’ behaviour
Occupancy behaviour patterns have a signiﬁcant impact on the overall energy use of a
building due to the variations in how the building systems are controlled. Occupants
==PAGE:38==
24
Background
generally endeavour to alter their indoor environments to promote health, well-being
and comfort. The comfort levels of an occupant can depend on a number of factors. Al
Horr et al. suggest that it is related to the thermal adaptation of an individual which is
dependent on factors such as geographical location, climate, time of year, gender, race
and age [18]. The human body attempts to maintain a temperature of around 37 ◦C
and this will depend on its thermodynamic relationship with the environment. For this
reason, thermal comfort will have an impact on energy consumption as users may be
inclined to change heating controls above or below optimum levels.
A realistic temperature proﬁle of an particular space over the course of a year is therefore
diﬃcult to accurately model given that it’s dependence on the users control. Control
options such as thermostatic control, timer control or simple on/oﬀswitching result in
diﬀerent amounts of delivered energy to a building.
Another factor which inﬂuences the energy consumption of a building is the window
opening behaviour. Anderson describes how it is discomfort which is the driving factor
for occupants to make adaptive actions [30]. Window opening is generally related to
thermal comfort but other critical reasons for opening include airing of the space to
improve the indoor air quality.
2.3.3
Building automation systems
Building automation systems (BAS) can be deﬁned as " all products and engineering
services for automatic controls, monitoring, optimisation, for operation, human inter-
vention, and management to achieve energy-eﬃcient, economical, and safe operation of
building services systems ” [31]. These goals have to achieve a balance between energy
consumption and occupant comfort in the building.
By applying these sensors or control mechanisms to the building spaces, systems can
be optimised to achieve required comfort levels for the occupants when the demand
is vital and therefore save on energy when a space is unused. Other than this, sun
shading control or window opening can be controled at necessary times to help achieve
more passive savings. There also exists studies which investigate the use of combining
weather forecasts within the operation controls of a system [32],[33]. This could work
on the basis of providing more or less night cooling to a building depending on the

2.3.4
Energy policies
In order to manage the increasing trends in energy consumption and curb the negative
environmental impact of excessive fossil fuel dependency, the European Commission set
clear objectives in 2007 when they created the goals to reduce the CO2 emissions and EU
primary energy consumption by 20% compared to 1990 levels. They also set to achieve
a 20% share of renewable energy within the network. [35] Beyond this, there is also
a road map strategy in place for ’decarbonisation’ by 2050 which envisions an 80-95%
decrease in emissions compared to 1990. The Energy Performance of Building Directive
(EPBD) and the Energy Eﬃciency Directive (EED) are two of the main frameworks
which are established as legislation for actions to achieve the goals.
2.3.4.1
EPBD
The EPBD was created in 2002 and updated in 2010 requires member states to establish
particular standards for their national building stock [36]. The Directive had to be
implemented by each EU Member State by the start of 2006. The main objectives of
the up to date directive included:
• countries setting up minimum energy performance standards for new buildings
and existing buildings undergoing ’major renovation’.
• the creation of a general frame work for the calculation of the energy performance
of buildings.
• the requirement for energy certiﬁcation of new and existing buildings when they
are being constructed, sold or rented out.
• a regime for inspection and assessment of large heating systems and air condi-
tioning systems
• the implementation of the ’nearly zero energy building’ concept which must be
adopted by all new buildings within each MS by 2020

With regards to renovation, a ’cost-optimal level’ is deﬁned for each MS which is deﬁned
as "the energy performance level which leads to the lost costs during the estimated
economic life-cycle".
2.3.4.2
EED
The EED sets out a framework of measures to promote energy eﬃciency in the private
and service sector across the EU [37]. It was adopted in 2012 and was required to be
in national legislation by 2014. Some of the main obligations are placed on government
bodies to lead by example and promote eﬃciency by procuring the most eﬃcient equip-
ment, products and also buildings. This goes further in that there is a requirement for
MS governmental buildings to be renovated at a rate of at least 3% a year.
Another aspect of the directive which was discussed is the use of smart metering should
it be assessed as a positive move. It states that at least 80% of consumers should be
equipped with intelligent metering systems by 2020. The intention is to engage and
provide customers with detailed and more frequent data with regards to there energy
consumption and promote them to reduce their usage as was mentioned earlier..
2.3.5
Building Regulations
For many countries the ﬁrst EPBD issued in 2002 was a means of adding extra elements
to their building codes which may not have contained energy performance requirements
concerning the building as a whole or for speciﬁc elements. For many countries where
there were prescriptive requirements existing before 2002 such as Belgium, Ireland and
Poland, there was a shift towards a more holistic approach with tighter controls.
With regards to the whole building and element based approaches, some countries such
as Denmark require both approaches with the single element approach considered a
supplementary demand while in other countries such as Norway, Spain, Poland and
Switzerland the two methods exist as alternatives parallel with each other. In Switzer-
land, the holistic approach is more common for new building while the element based
would be used during light renovation and the holistic would sometimes be used during
deep renovation.
In a detailed table created by Economidou et al. [3], it can be seen that many diﬀerent
approaches exist between the diﬀerent EU member states and no two are identical. Each
country has its own general requirements and many diﬀerent deﬁnitions and procedures
which would not be compatible with another countries work.
The addition of a legally binding performance targets has also been instated in a number
of countries which would be based on a kW/m2 target that could not be exceeded or else
on a percentage improvement based upon a reference building of similar characteristics.

Chapter 3
Methodology
This chapter will describe the approach used to set up the series of energy models which
can be used to represent the Danish single-family housing stock. The models would be
considered a grey-box modelling approach being composed of statistical data of the
archetypal buildings available from the TABULA project. This consists of averaged
values of the building element areas and area-weighted U-values. The models are set
up using the same input data from the TABULA methodology but assumptions and
generalisations must be made with regards to some chosen model parameters and these
will be stated further on in the chapter.
The geometries of the models are ﬁrst created in Autodesk Revit before they are ex-
ported using an IFC format into the whole-year dynamic simulation application IDA
ICE. The models will be set up using the speciﬁc data for each of the TABULA typolo-
gies before the simulations are carried out. The results produce detailed hourly energy
demand data.
These can be analysed and ﬁrst compared against the calculations results from the
TABULA methodology to determine if there are similarities. Then a collection of mea-
surements from Danish SFH can be compared to the modelled energy proﬁles to deter-
mine the levels of deviation from one another. From here, validationa and calibration
will be explored.

3.2.1.4
Thermal losses
To input the air inﬁltration rate and the value of thermal bridging the respective win-
dows can be found from the ’General’ tab. For thermal bridging a value of W/Km2
based on the total envelope area (including roof and ﬂoors) is entered. These values
are taken from TABULA and represent the losses around a building based upon its
construction quality. For inﬁltration rate is set as a ﬁxed value of air changes per hour
(ACH) in the units of h−1 is chosen. The TABULA procedure use a ﬁxed value of
0.4 h−1 representative of the air change rate from utilisation of the building with an
additional surcharge for the inﬁltration due to the buildings air tightness. The addi-
tional value ranges from 0.05 to 0.4 h−1. The TABULA method considers a speciﬁc
room height of 2.5m in its calculation for all building models. Given that the IDA ICE
models will consider the full volume of the geometry which is in all cases have a height
greater than 2.5 m, a value of 0.4 h−1 is assumed for all models.

Sønderborg
The measured data comes from Sønderborg, a small city in the south of Denmark which

the city to become carbon neutral by the year 2029 by increasing the share of renewable
energy sources. It is intended to utilise green district heating systems powered by solar,
geothermal, bio-gases and also heat pumps.
ZeroHome is a subset of ProjectZero which deals with bringing the existing dwellings of
Sønderborg to a higher level of energy eﬃciency by oﬀering free energy consultations by
an independent assessor and access to a network of other skilled personnel for advise.
[6]
It is suggested that the energy savings in Sønderborg can also be extrapolated into the
entire building stock to evaluate the potential of the whole countries building stock.
If a city of this size can successfully lower its consumption rates with a series of well
planned and technological advancements it can act as a model for other localities to
follow.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
Being able to provide a detailed and accurate estimation of the energy demand for
a district or city is practical for aiding with the planning and design of large energy
production facilities and also examining the energy savings potential of the area. Resi-
dential buildings consist of a large proportion of the energy demand in a city. To assist
with analysing the load which they exert on an energy system, detailed energy demand
time-series of the various types of buildings in a stock can be a useful tool as they can
show the potential energy demand at speciﬁc times in a year.
Having a set of archetypical building typologies or archetypes deﬁned for a nations
which have been adequately categorised allow for much work to be built upon them.
The typologies deﬁned in the TABULA project were used for the project in an attempt
to model an average representative building of the various periods. The models were set
up in a methodological manner using available data which consists of the statistically
averaged U-values and areas from the diﬀerent time periods.
The models were set up as simpliﬁed rectangular boxes comprising of one zone and
incorporate an ’Ideal Heater’ which simulated the required energy demand for space
heating. The demand for domestic hot waster is also determined in the simulation.
Other than envelope properties, input parameters included, inﬁltration rate, thermal
bridging, equipment gain and temperature set-point.
From the results the hourly energy demand for space heating and domestic hot water
consumption were extracted and processed for analysis. The initial models created in
IDA ICE produced similar results to the standard calculation method of TABULA.
To perform an empirical veriﬁcation, measurements from 13 SFH buildings in Sønder-
==PAGE:76==
62

borg were used. The construction year of the 13 buildings ranged between 1935 and 1959
which matched two of the typology models. When an aggregated energy demand for
the buildings was estimated using the representative model method, the result showed
a 68% deviation from the actual measurements.
To calibrate the models, the same method used by the TABULA method was adopted by
utilising the adaptation factors which were determined in their study. The two models
were adapted accordingly and the aggregated heat demand was once again calculated for
the 13 buildings. The results were closer to the actual measurements with a deviation
of 19%.
To consider a model properly calibrated requires an assessment of results more detailed
than annual values.
The hourlyresults from the two models which corresponded to
the measured buildings were tested to determine how well they matched with the data.
The mean bias error (MBE) % and the coeﬃcient of variation of root mean square error
CV(RMSE) % were determined for each period of buildings. Both values were over the
acceptable criteria for the SyAv.03 model while SyAv.04 performed slightly better but
still produced a very high CV(RMSE). This suggests that the two models had high
levels of oﬀsetting errors from the measurements at hourly level.
A second investigation was conducted by plotting the model results on a data-set of the
consumption measurements from two gas companies. The models generally underesti-
mated the consumption contrary to the Sønderborg measurements. The older models
in general underestimated the values for their periods but there was a better match for
the later generations.
The overall work of the project does not conclusively validate the series of models
produced.
The sample size of high detailed measurements to properly validate the
entire series was not available and the results from the Sønderborg analysis show that
the values may be quite accurate on an annual basis but once higher resolution data is
observed, values may have signiﬁcant errors.
A repository of the completed work was created in the form of a HTML web page which
oﬀers an insight into the models, data and results produced during the project. Within
the repository there is a section for ’Models’, ’Measurements’ and ’Publications’ related
to the ﬁeld of research. Within the ’Models’ section, model ﬁles and energy demand
time-series in a number formats are available for download. The measurements used in
the project are also available in various formats. Having more detailed measurement
data available is crucial to perform more accurate validation and more advanced cali-
bration method and the repository poses as a ﬁrst step towards creating this platform
for future work.

Chapter 6
Discussion
The research in this project intended to examine the methods for estimating the energy
demand of various types of buildings which could be used to estimate the demand of
an urban district in an aggregated manner. For this, it is important to have a series
of typologies deﬁned which can break down and accurately categorise a total building
stock.
The objectives stated that a repository of energy demand models and time-
series would be created and veriﬁed. By utilising the data which had been collected
in the TABULA project, a series of energy models were created using the program
IDA ICE, providing results which could be analysed and then compared other methods
and available building energy measurements. Only the single family houses have been
modelled in this project due to time factor of preparing and assembling each model.
6.1
Validation
The models which were initially created using the extracted input data and general
assumptions and the annual results were ﬁrst gauged against the standard calculation
method used in TABULA project. The models matched quite well with a maximum
deviation of 16% for one model. It was understood that the initial models would however
not accurately depict the results from a real life scenario building and would require
calibration.
Using a small data-set from Sønderborg, a validation was carried out by testing the
models in an aggregated manner against results from 13 building measurements. Only
two models represented the whole set of building measurements based on their con-
struction ages. The initial aggregated results showed to overestimate the measurements
==PAGE:78==
64
Discussion
by 72%.
In an attempt to calibrate the models The TABULA adaptation method was applied
by factoring all the result values by the provided range of adaptation factors. Having
done this, the annual values appeared to be more in harmony with the Sønderborg
measurements. Overall deviations were reduced to 19% overestimation when the results
were aggregated and when plotted these were found to be mostly in the winter months.
To examine the performance of the two models on a more detailed individual level, the
buildings which were measured were grouped into the same periods as the typologies.
The hourly measurements for both groups were averaged and the appropriate models
calibration performance was assessed. The coeﬃcient of variation of root mean square
error which determines how well a model ﬁts the measurements was very high for both
cases, more than double the acceptable value stated in the literature [2].
A further investigation suggested the opposite of the one carried out with the Sæn-
derborg data.
The plot containing annual gas consumption values for thousands of
buildings showed higher average consumption value for all buildings when compared
to the results for the typology models. The models did match the trends in consump-
tion quite well.
Complete losses in the heating systems are not taken into account
in the models which could explain a lower value compared to the values from the gas
consumption plot.
6.2
Limitations
When carrying out such modelling experiments, there are obvious limitations given that
a number assumptions and simpliﬁcations must be made. Creating a model for the
average building of a period is not easy considering the number of varying parameters
which can not be explicitly known. Some of the input values in the model such as the
air inﬁltration rate or the internal gains from equipment have to be chosen based on the
input valued from TABULA but these values are in general diﬃcult to predict. These
can have quite a large eﬀect on the energy demand of the model.
Due to data privacy issues and also the lack of comprehensive sub-metering of the end
use energy in buildings, the amount of detailed available empirical energy demand data
to compare modelled results with can be limited. [1] Having a larger sample of this
data is necessary to perform a better veriﬁcation of the models as this gives the best
representation of an actual buildings energy demand and allows for detailed calibration
to be attempted. In this project only a small sample was used to verify the ﬁnal adapted
models. It could be seen that the models represented a number of the buildings quite
accurately on an annual basis but on average there were still some large variations at
higher resolution observations.
==PAGE:79==
6.3 Further studies
65
The investigation of the weather did suggest that during the winter months in which
the average weekly temperatures were overestimated could be justiﬁed. This is the issue
with verifying against one year of measurements which may not entirely represent the
average climatic conditions of the area.
The data sample used for the adaptation process of TABULA was also unavailable for
inspection upon request from the authors of the report. The adaptation process used
a range of factors determined "by a large number of example buildings" but no further
method or details were disclosed. This would have added insight into their methodology
and helped propose further works.
6.3
Further studies
Given that these models are expected to represent the entire building stock it would
be of interest to investigate more into the adaptation process which has been used
and perform a whole new methodology in due time.
Considering that the general
engineering modelling methods generally do not accurately predict the energy demand
of the measured buildings alone, it is apparent that the adaptation methodology is an
important process.
With a high enough sample of detailed measurements from buildings along with basic
knowledge about the buildings characteristics, a statistical method of accurately pre-
dicting the energy demand is more possible. Understanding the stochastic nature of a
buildings energy demand could also be further investigated if substantial amounts of
data was available as it would be of interest to understand the variations in consumption
for similar buildings types.
Regardless of an up to date adaptation method, it is still of use to complete the repos-
itory by adding the terraced houses and apartment blocks which are included in the
TABULA project. The TABULA provided a wonderful collection of data and results
but something which was not created was a detailed time-series for each model. Having
the baseline models completed will always allow for further adaptations and adjustments
to be made. The completed repository would give researchers such as those mentioned
in the case example, an opportunity to have relatively accurate detailed energy demand
time series available at demand for undertaking their projects.
Another step which can be added to this work is to compile and include diﬀerent
variations of renovated states of the buildings which are also available on TABULA.
This could open up options for their inclusion within the aggregated calculation method
for buildings which are known to have undertaken renovation.

energy by energyware.

mand, carbon dioxide emissions and also determination of the adaptation factor
the calibrate the energy demand to typical measured results.
