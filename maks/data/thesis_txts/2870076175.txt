Abstract
Logical fallacies are a persistent problem in the realm of argumentation, where they
tend to contribute to misinformation and flawed reasoning across numerous fields
such as media, politics, and online debate.
This work investigates the feasibility
of using state-of-the-art instruction-tuned large language models (LLMs) to detect
logical fallacies. We specifically examine whether LLMs can classify a variety of fal-
lacies in a zero-shot setting, that is, without any fine-tuning on labeled fallacy data.
We further explore how prompt design, explicit fallacy definitions, and multi-round
classification approaches can influence performance. By employing both Copi’s and
Aristotle’s hierarchical categorization frameworks, the study covers a wide spectrum
of broad and subtle fallacy categories to examine each model’s capability on the task.
Experimental findings show that, while LLMs exhibit moderate success in detecting
logical fallacies, they can struggle with more nuanced or similar categories while the
prompt sensitivity remains a challenge. Chain-of-thought prompting and hierarchi-
cal classification-shifting from broad categories to fine-grained fallacy types- offers a
modest increase in classification quality for the strongest models even though overall
accuracy dropped. A key additional experiment replaces familiar fallacy names with
semantically unrelated labels, revealing that stronger models can compensate for lost
semantic clues by relying on contextual cues. These results point to both the promise
and current limitations of LLMs in fallacy detection, underscoring the need for im-
proved prompt strategies, external knowledge integration, and more sophisticated
reasoning methods to handle the complexity and variety of flawed arguments.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Reasoning is a cognitive process by which humans use prior knowledge to draw infer-
ences, generate explanations, and evaluate statements rationally using logical frame-
works [2]. The process is essential for critical thinking, argumentation, and decision-
making across various fields, from philosophy to science, public discourse, and arti-
ficial intelligence (AI). However, human reasoning is susceptible to systematic flaws
that result in logical fallacies—arguments that are potentially convincing but not
logically sound.
Logical fallacies are everywhere in human communication and can be observed in
day-to-day conversation, mass media, political discourse, and internet forums. Even
if they are benign when they result from mere misunderstandings, they are also
very effective tools of rhetorical manipulation. The advent of online platforms and
social media has, in many respects, amplified the effects of these flawed arguments:
misinformation can spread far and wide, and engaging but logically unsound claims
can swiftly affect the opinion of the public. Therefore, the need to automate fallacy
detection in order to enhance more sensible, evidence-based discussion has reached
an all-time high.
During an era of online disinformation, the detection and refutation of logical falla-
cies are crucial to sound discourse and fact-based decision-making. Fallacy detection,
once tested in philosophy and rhetoric, has become a key concern of computational
linguistics, artificial intelligence, and information analysis.
This thesis deals with logical fallacy detection using large language models (LLMs).
Our approach leverages state-of-the-art, instruction-tuned models in a zero-shot set-
ting. In experimenting with various models, we aim to determine whether modern
LLMs have the ability to generalize to identify diverse fallacies without specialized
fine-tuning. Here we address the potential of the models in identifying both subtle fal-
lacies as well as broad categories, as outlined by notable classifications such as Copi’s
and Aristotle’s frameworks. Furthermore, we try to understand the impact of sev-
eral prompting strategies on the models’performance while introducing a multi-round
hierarchical classification approach to improve interpretability and accuracy.
The central questions addressed in this thesis are:
• Zero-Shot Feasibility: Can large language models reliably detect fallacies
even when they have not been trained or fine-tuned on dedicated fallacy cor-
pora?
==PAGE:11==
1 Introduction
2
• Prompting Strategies: Does the inclusion of explicit fallacy definitions or
changes in prompt design (e.g., prompt wording) significantly impact classifica-
tion performance?
• Multi-Round Classification: Can a hierarchical, chain-of-thought (CoT) ap-
proach that moves from coarse- to fine-grained labels improve accuracy and
interpretability relative to single-step classification?
• Semantically Unrelated Labels: Recognizing that LLMs often rely on se-
mantic cues from familiar label names, we replace standard fallacy labels (e.g.,
“Ad Hominem,” “Appeal to Emotion”) with abstract placeholders (e.g., “Fal-
lacy_1,” “Fallacy_2”). Comparing performance in this altered label space clar-
ifies how deeply each model depends on memorized label semantics from its
training data as opposed to in-context information.
To explore these questions, we conduct a series of experiments on the LOGIC [19]
dataset, a benchmark with 13 distinct logical fallacies drawn from a range of sources,
including educational materials and online quizzes. Our evaluation includes multiple
metrics—accuracy and macro F1 primarily—to account for label imbalance. We also
compare model predictions against two baseline approaches: random assignment and
distribution-based inference. Ultimately, this work provides insights into the extent to
which current LLMs, operating under zero-shot constraints, can address logical fallacy
detection, along with the limitations they encounter and the potential strategies for
further enhancement.

The systematic study of logical fallacies can be traced back to Aristotle (350 BCE),
who, in Sophistical Refutations [2], provided one of the earliest known classifications of
flawed reasoning patterns. His foundational work laid the groundwork for subsequent
inquiries into the nature and structure of fallacious reasoning. Several definitions of
argument and fallacy have been introduced; in Figure 2.1 we present the definitions
of argument and fallacy as outlined by Copi et al. [6].

premises, where the premises are intended to es-

or conclusions can be implicit in an argument.
Fallacy:
A fallacy is an argument where the
premises do not entail the conclusion.
If reasoning is flawed

for automating fallacy detection, making the study of fallacies not only a theoretical
pursuit but also an essential component of contemporary information analysis.
2.2
Fallacy Detection
The field of logical fallacy detection has seen considerable progress in recent years,
driven by the development of novel datasets, innovative methodologies, and advance-
ments in Natural Language Processing (NLP). This section offers a chronological view
of the main contributions to the field, highlighting significant milestones in dataset
creation and the growing role of large language models (LLMs) in advancing research.
By tracing these developments, we aim to provide a comprehensive understanding of
how the field has evolved and where it stands today.
In 2017, Habernal et al. [15] were the first to identify the absence of datasets specif-
ically focused on fallacious argumentation. To address this, they developed Argotario,
a gamified platform aimed at crowdsourcing annotated data for logical fallacies. The
platform employed a competitive gameplay model, where participants either created
or identified fallacious arguments created by other players. This approach resulted
in a dataset encompassing five types of fallacies, including ad hominem and hasty
generalization.
Argotario functioned both as a data collection tool as well as an
educational tool, raising public awareness about logical fallacies. The study demon-
strated how gamification could effectively tackle the challenges of data annotation
while simultaneously engaging users in meaningful ways.
Da San Martino et al. (2019) [7] introduced the PROPAGANDA dataset. Un-
like previous approaches that classified entire news articles as propagandistic, they
focused on identifying propaganda techniques within text fragments of varying length.
This shift allowed for a more interpretable analysis of how propaganda techniques are
used in the written news context. The dataset included news articles annotated with
18 distinct propaganda techniques, with some of them being loaded language, name-
calling, appeal to fear, and whataboutism, providing a comprehensive framework for

studying these methods in detail. The authors made several key contributions to the
field. First, the dataset creation where they curated a corpus of news articles anno-
tated at the fragment level by expert annotators. Second, they proposed two novel
tasks: sentence-level classification, where a whole sentence is classified, and fragment-
level classification, where a text span is identified as propagandistic alongside the
corresponding propaganda technique.
Finally, they developed a multi-granularity
model that outperformed several strong BERT-based [21] baselines, demonstrating
its effectiveness in detecting propaganda at varying levels of granularity. Their ap-
proach offered a more precise and explainable framework for analyzing propaganda
in news articles.
Vorakitphan et al. (2021) [34] introduced PROTECT (PROpaganda Text dEteC-
Tion), propaganda detection and classification tool. PROTECT operates through a
two-step pipeline. First, text snippets are identified as propagandistic, and second,
these snippets are classified according to the specific propaganda technique employed.
To achieve this, the system utilizes transformer-based language models, such as BERT
and RoBERTa [21, 24], and integrates semantic and argumentation features to im-
prove classification accuracy. The system was evaluated using the PROPAGANDA
dataset developed by Da San Martino et al. (2019) [7], demonstrating state-of-the art
results. Beyond its technical capabilities, PROTECT was designed to be accessible
to the public, featuring a user-friendly web interface that enables users to analyze
submitted texts. This functionality serves an educational purpose, helping users rec-
ognize and understand the manipulative content in media. By providing a practical
and accessible tool, this work made a significant contribution to the field, bridging
the gap between technical research and public awareness. It highlights the potential
of combining advanced NLP techniques with user-centric design to address complex
challenges in media analysis and education.
In 2021, Sahai et al. expanded the domain of fallacy detection to online discus-
sions by constructing a dataset from Reddit. The main contribution of this work
was the construction of a large, balanced dataset of fallacies. The dataset is created
using a two-stage annotation process. First, comments that explicitly mention fal-
lacies were automatically collected from Reddit, and secondly they were manuallly
validated via crowdsourcing. The final dataset consisted of eight common fallacies,
including Appeal to Authority, Black-or-White Fallacy, Slippery Slope, and Hasty
Generalization.
To classify these fallacies, the authors evaluated multiple models,
including BERT-based fine-tuned classifiers and the multi-granularity networks intro-
duced by Da San Martino et al. (2019) [7]. In addition, conversational context from
the relevant Reddit threads was added which improved classification performance,
highlighting the importance of discourse structure in fallacy detection. This work
built upon previous research in propaganda detection but unlike prior approaches
that focused on detecting fallacies in journalistic articles, Sahai et al. (2021) focused
on informal and unstructured online discussions, making their findings particularly
relevant for misinformation analysis and online discourse quality assessment, while
highlighting the importance of context in fallacy detection.
Jin et al. (2022) [19] introduced the LOGIC dataset, a large-scale benchmark for

detecting 13 types of logical fallacies in natural language text with 2,449 samples. In
addition, they propose LOGICCLIMATE, a specialized dataset for detecting fallacies
in climate change claims. Their study highlights the diﬀiculty of logical fallacy classi-
fication, as existing models exhibit limited performance, achieving only 53.31% micro
F1 at best. The dataset includes fallacy types such as Faulty Generalization, Ad

The authors demonstrate that pretrained models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa, DeBERTa,
and Electra) struggle to generalize, especially to out-of-domain fallacies in climate

cal patterns. This approach outperforms previous approaches by 5.46% F1 on LOGIC
and 4.51% F1 on LOGICCLIMATE, highlighting the importance of formal logic struc-
tures in reasoning tasks. Compared to previous work in propaganda detection (Da
San Martino et al., 2019) and fallacy identification in Reddit discussions (Sahai et al.,
2021), Jin et al. (2022) contribute a more comprehensive dataset covering multiple fal-
lacy types, making it a significant benchmark for argument quality assessment. Their
approach aligns with recent advances in reasoning-aware NLP, providing a foundation
for future work in automated argument analysis and misinformation detection.
Alhindi et al. (2022) [1] proposed a unified multitask instruction-based prompt-
ing approach for fallacy detection that generalizes across multiple datasets. Unlike
previous works, which focused on single-domain fallacy classification [30] [19], their
approach integrated five diverse datasets covering 28 fallacy types across different
domains (news, dialogue, social media, fact-checking).

The authors fine-tuned a T5 transformer model [28] using instruction-based prompt-
ing, enabling it to recognize fallacies across various annotation schemes and linguistic
structures. Their results demonstrate that multitask prompting significantly outper-
forms task-specific models, improving macro F1 scores by up to 16% over single-
dataset models and achieving better generalization to new domains. Moreover, they
analyze the impact of model size, different prompting strategies, and dataset-specific
challenges. Furthermore, they conducted a thorough error analysis, revealing the dif-
ficulty of the fallacy detection task by comparing an expert annotator’s performance
to the model’s predictions and the dataset labels. The annotator agreed with the
dataset’s label in 75% of the cases, with the model’s predictions in 15% of the cases
whereas they provided a different label in 10% of the cases. Compared to earlier
approaches that use BERT and RoBERTa-based classifiers for propaganda detection
[7] and logical fallacy identification [19], this work provides a more scalable and
adaptable model. By leveraging multitask instruction tuning, their system captures
cross-domain patterns in fallacious reasoning, making it a valuable contribution to
automated argument assessment and misinformation analysis. This study highlights
the importance of unified instruction-based prompting for fallacy recognition in real-
world settings, emphasizing the need for robust and generalizable models that can
handle diverse fallacy types and linguistic structures.
Goffredo et al. (2023) [12] focused on the automatic detection and classification
of fallacies in political debates by extending an existing dataset and developing a
transformer-based detection model.

the ElecDeb60To16 dataset, a corpus of U.S. presidential debates from 1960 to 2016,
by adding the 2020 Trump-Biden debates and incorporating argumentative compo-
nents (i.e., claims and premises) and the relations between these components (i.e.,
support and attack). To detect and classify fallacies, the authors proposed MultiFu-

with argumentative features. Their model enhanced fallacy detection by integrating

are not available in this context.
Finally, while this study was being carried out, a paper trying to answer some
of the questions we are trying to answer with this research was released by Pan et
al. (2024) [26] exploring the potential for zero-shot fallacy classification with Large
Language Models (LLMs), overcoming some of the limitations of standard supervised
approaches needing plentiful labeled data and weak performance at generalization.
The paper presents some single-round and multi-round prompting techniques to test
the reasoning and fallacy detection abilities of state-of-the-art large language mod-
els. The authors observe that LLMs can match or even outperform state-of-the-art
fine-tuned models in zero-shot scenarios. Single-step prompting where LLMs are di-
rectly asked to classify fallacies with or without definitions is competitive, but multi-
step prompting—through intermediate reasoning steps such as premise-extraction,
definition-generation, and Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning—enhances the perfor-
mance even for smaller LLMs. One of the main conclusions of the research is that
LLMs’ classification capability is a question of the type of dataset: models excel at bal-
anced and easy datasets (e.g., ARGOTARIO, REDDIT), but not at denser fallacies
needing more incisive logical examination (e.g., LOGIC). The paper also indicates
the tendency of LLMs to confuse similar types of fallacies, revealing the inherent
subjectivity of fallacy classification.
Collectively, these studies demonstrate the evolution of fallacy detection, from
gamified annotation platforms to sophisticated LLM-based methodologies. Each con-
tribution has addressed unique challenges, from scaling annotation processes to im-
proving model interpretability and reasoning capabilities. These advancements have
not only deepened our understanding of logical fallacies but also expanded the appli-
cations of NLP in domains such as misinformation detection, argumentation analysis,
and critical thinking education.

2.3
Large Language Models (LLMs)
Large language models development has been in the forefront by the evolution of nat-
ural language processing (NLP) from statistical methods to deep learning approaches.
Traditional methods worked well in basic NLP but had drawbacks of restricted con-
text understanding. Then, though, came the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et
al. [33]), which changed the game for NLP by using a self-attention mechanism to
enable models to be trained on whole input sequences in parallel. This innovation
made training much larger models possible and gave rise to the first generation of
LLMs, including OpenAI’s GPT [27] and Google’s BERT [21].
LLMs have been developed through incremental advances in model scale, training
data size, and computational eﬀiciency. Scaling laws research (Kaplan et al. (2020)
[20]) showed that model performance improves predictably with increased model size
and training data. Subsequent models like GPT-3 (Brown et al., [4]) and LLama
(Touvron et al. 2023 [32]) used billion-parameter models, attaining excellent gener-
alization on a wide range of NLP tasks. Furthermore, recent research has proposed
new novel architectures such as Mamba (Gu and Dao 2024 [14]) a novel linear-time
sequence modeling framework that tackles the computational ineﬀiciencies of stan-
dard Transformers while being capable of matching or outperforming Transformers
on language datasets while scaling more readily to longer context.
One aspect of the Large Language Models that we are particualry interested in
for this work, is their reasoning ability which has been a subject of extensive research.
LLMs have been tested both for commonsense causality reasoning [22]. and abstract
reasoning [11]. Yet, empirical research [5] has shown that LLMs, even the most recent
versions like GPT-4, have inconsistencies in their reasoning.
Commonsense reasoning evaluates the ability of an LLM to apply common sense
while making decisions. The work of Kıcıman et al. (2023) [22] and Talmor et al.
(2020) [31] has evaluated the performance of LLMs on causal understanding tasks.
LLMs perform well on formal causal tasks but do poorly on real-world, complex
scenarios involving implicit knowledge.
Abstract reasoning, as studied by Gendron et al. (2023) [11] , which consists of
finding and applying a general pattern from few data, argued that while models excel
at syntactic pattern recognition, they often fail at true abstract generalization.
Recently, Bubeck et al. [5], despite their amazing performance in several tasks,
has identified essential limitations in large language models (LLMs), specifically their
consistency in reasoning tasks. Even the most advanced models (GPT-4) tend to
derive contradictory conclusions from differences in prompts, suggesting that their
reasoning is shaped more by the wording of inputs than by a deep understanding of
logical principles.
Building on these findings, we find it essential to examine the reasoning capabilities
of LLMs in detecting logical fallacies, a task that requires a solid understanding of
logical principles and the ability to detect flawed patterns of reasoning. Through an
evaluation of LLMs’ performance in fallacy detection tasks, we aim to understand
their reasoning abilities and identify potential areas of improvement.

2.4 Chain of Thought
10
2.4
Chain of Thought
Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting has greatly enhanced the reasoning ability of
large language models (LLMs) by allowing them to decompose challenging problems
into a series of intermediate steps. Conventional LLMs are usually poor at multi-
step reasoning tasks, where they give straightforward but sometimes wrong responses
because of the absence of systematic cognitive processes. CoT prompting overcomes
this restriction by promoting the generation of explicit intermediate reasoning steps
in models, thereby mimicking human-like logical reasoning.
The concept of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting was originally defined by Wei
et al. (2022) [36], where they demonstrated that breaking complex issues into con-
secutive reasoning steps significantly improved the performance of large language
models (LLMs) on multi-step reasoning tasks. Their research demonstrated that if
LLMs were prompted to provide intermediate reasoning steps, they were better on
math word problems and logical reasoning tasks. The innovation diverged from the
classical ”end-to-end” responding mode of LLMs, where generally immediate answers
were produced by models without actually reasoning out the problem.
Follow-on research extended optimization of CoT prompting on varied reasoning
tasks. Kojima et al. (2022) [23] in their effort to address the significant drawback
in Wei et al.’s method which was the requirement for manually created exemplars,
showed that when LLMs were prompted to ”think step by step,” generated sizeable
increases in logical consistency. The authors of this paper demonstrated that reason-
ing using CoT-type reasoning is effective on reasoning-trained models but can also
automatically occur on very large LLMs.
Zhou et al. (2023) [38] built upon the research of CoT prompting and formulated
Least-to-Most (LtM) prompting. LtM tries to address CoT’s problems in handling
easier and harder tasks. Unlike standard CoT, which solves a problem step by step,
LtM first breaks down a diﬀicult task into easier, smaller components and solves them
in turn, making use of prior responses along the way. This method substantially im-
proves compositional generalization and multi-step reasoning performance compared
to conventional CoT prompting. Through hierarchical structuring of problem-solving,
LtM improves the flexibility and development of reasoning in LLMs, bringing AI closer
to human thinking abilities.
The CoT prompting studies have shown that it is a highly effective technique for
enhancing the reasoning capability of LLMs to enable them to resolve diﬀicult multi-
step problems more accurately and consistently. By breaking down problems into
a series of intermediate steps, CoT prompting mimics human-like logical reasoning,
providing a systematic problem-solving approach that can significantly improve the
performance of LLMs on an extremely wide range of reasoning tasks. Therefore, we
decided to use this technique in our work to explore how diffetent chain-of-thought
techniques could enhance the capabilities of LLMs in detecting logical fallacies.

By structuring the fallacies in a hierarchical manner, we aimed to explore how well
the models could generalize their reasoning across different levels of categorization.
Specifically, we sought to determine whether the models exhibited stronger classifi-
cation abilities at the coarse-grained level (recognizing broad fallacy types) or the

3.4.1
Coarse-grained classes by Copi
Copi’s [6] classification system of fallacies of reasoning comes from formal and in-
formal logic, an ordered method toward evaluating faulty reasoning. Copi’s classifi-
cation,revolves around argument soundness and argument validity, placing it as an
essential cornerstone for modern educational materials on logic and critical thinking.
Copi classifies fallacies of reasoning under four general categories, namely, Fallacies
of Relevance, Fallacies of Defective Induction, Fallacies of Presumption, and Falla-
cies of Ambiguity. The mappings between the fine-grained LOGIC classes and these
categories can be found in Figure 3.2.
Fallacies of Relevance involve premises that bear no relevance to an argument’s
conclusion, even if these premises sound plausible. Ad hominem, an attack against
the speaker, not the claim, ad populum, an argument that makes an appeal to people,
not facts, and appeal to emotion, an argument playing on emotion, not reason, fall
under this classification. Relevance fallacies, such as intentional fallacy, an argument
distorting an argument’s meaning so as to change an argument’s emphasis, fall under
this classification too.
Fallacies of Defective Induction occur when an argument makes an unwarranted
conclusion from incomplete or unreliable evidence. These fallacies involve false causal-

ity, assuming causation without proof; false dilemma, posing an incomplete list of
choices when there are many, many more; and faulty generalization, drawing general
conclusions from an incomplete, unrepresentative sample. Others involve the fallacy
of credibility, relying upon unverified authority, and deductive fallacy, applying the
rule of reasoning improperly.
Fallacies of Presumption involve argument presupposing that what it seeks to
confirm, including circular reasoning, where an argument’s conclusion is contained
within the premise(s).
Lastly, Fallacies of Ambiguity play upon linguistic uncertainty, utilizing impre-

modes of persuasion—Ethos (credibility), Pathos (emotion), and Logos (logic)—as
described in his work Rhetoric [29]. These modes represent the fundamental ways
in which arguments persuade an audience, and they serve as the basis for Aristotle’s
categorization of rhetoric schemes. We use these categories to classifiy the fallacies

of the LOGIC dataset based on the work of Helwe et al. (2024) [16] and the exact

The Fallacy of Credibility (Ethos fallacies) occurs when an argument cites an
untrustworthy source or authority over sound reasoning. Ad hominem, whereby the
speaker attacks one’s character, not one’s argument, ad populum, whereby one cites
popular opinion as proof of truth, and the fallacy of credibility, whereby an argument
rests upon nothing other than an authority figure’s approval, even if the authority
figure has no expertise, fall under this category. These fallacies play upon apparent
credibility, not verifiable facts, so are very influential in political and social contexts.
The Fallacy of Logic (Logos-based fallacies) entails reasoning fallacies. Some fal-
lacies that fall under this category are false causality, wherein causation gets mixed
up with correlation; false dilemma, wherein only two out of several possible choices
is shown; and faulty generalization, wherein a conclusion is drawn from an incom-
plete, unbalanced sample.
Other fallacies here are circular reasoning, wherein an
argument takes an argument as one of its premises, and equivocation, wherein one
utilizes ambiguous terms.
Lastly, the Fallacy of Emotion (Pathos-based fallacies) utilizes emotional appeal
to persuade, not reason. This category involve appeal to emotion, wherein one utilizes
emotion over facts; intentional fallacy, wherein one distorts one’s intent as proof of
one’s accuracy; and fallacy of extension, wherein one distorts an opponent’s argument
so it is easy to demolish.
By categorizing fallacies under these categories of rhetoric, this classification has
an orderly means of analyzing faulty reasoning, as well as an excellent tool by which
one may analyze how large language models discriminate various logical fallacies.

As described in Chapter 2, Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting has significantly ad-

By comparing the model’s responses with and without CoT prompts, we aimed
to determine how much step-by-step reasoning contributes to more reliable fallacy
detection. The findings could provide insight into how reasoning-based prompting
techniques can be leveraged to enhance LLM performance in the fallacy detection
task.

presented to it.

4.1.2
Model Selection and Baselines
We evaluate three instruction-tuned large language models: Falcon-Mamba-7B-Instruct,
Meta-Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 using the API offered by
huggingface [10]. Each model is tested under zero-shot conditions without any addi-
tional fine-tuning on fallacy-related corpora. We compare these models against two
baselines: a random baseline that assigns each instance a label uniformly at random
among the 13 possible classes, and a distribution baseline that predicts labels pro-
portionally to their frequencies in the dataset. Both baselines serve as references for
how well the models perform compared to random guessing and a simple distribution-
based approach.
4.1.3
Evaluation Metrics
We focus on two primary metrics:
1. Accuracy: the fraction of correctly classified instances among all test samples.
==PAGE:36==
4.2 Zero-shot
27
2. Macro F1: the average of the F1 scores across all categories, giving equal
emphasis to minority classes.
Due to the dataset’s imbalance, macro F1 is especially informative in revealing
whether a model disproportionately favors common fallacies. We also examine the
effect of Definitions (providing or omitting a brief explanation of each fallacy) and
compare two different Prompt variants (Prompt 1 vs. Prompt 2). Both prompts
request fallacy classification in JSON format, but they differ in wording and instruc-
tions. We systematically vary these conditions to evaluate how they influence model
performance.
Furthermore, we report two more metrics in our tables. The failed cases and the
uknown labels. The failed cases correspond to the percentage of examples that we
failed to parse the model’s response which was asked to be in a json format. The
unknown labels correspond to the percentage of examples that the model classified
using other labels not included in the dataset.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
This thesis examined whether large language models, used in a zero-shot capacity,
could effectively identify logical fallacies across a range of categories, a task that has
been found to be diﬀicult even for human annotators who are capable of meaningfully
perceiving fallacies in different ways. Drawing on the LOGIC dataset, our experi-
ments examined both fine-grained and coarse-grained fallacy classifications through
various prompting strategies, including explicit definitions, chain-of-thought (CoT)
methods, and multi-round hierarchical approaches. The results underscore several
key observations.
Regarding Zero-Shot Viability, even without domain-specific fine-tuning, models
such as Meta-Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 show non-trivial
capabilities in fallacy recognition. Nevertheless, accuracy and macro F1 scores re-
main far from perfect, indicating a persistent gap between zero-shot classification and
human-level reasoning. Furthermore, the explicit inclusion of definitions also offered
mixed results, suggesting that while additional textual cues can sometimes enhance
classification, they can also introduce noise or confusion. Additionally, the results
suggest - from mapping the model’s fine-grained predictions to Copi’s and Aristotle’s
broader catecories - that the models possess inherent logical fallacy understanding,
even if they do not always apply it perfectly at the most specific level.
On Impact of Prompt Design, prompt structure—particularly the choice between
two slightly different prompts—proved to be a critical factor, often shifting accuracy
and F1 by notable margins. This sensitivity underscores the importance of prompt
engineering in zero-shot classification settings.
For Hierarchical Classification, Multi-round CoT schemes, in which a model pre-
dicts a coarse category before refining to a specific subtype, tended to introduce
an extra point of failure. If the model misclassified the top-level category, its final
prediction was certainly wrong. As a result, overall performance did not consistently
surpass that of single-step zero-shot classification but did offer some gains in F1-score
especially in the strongest models.
Finally, familiar labels (e.g., “Ad Hominem”) were replaced with neutral placehold-
ers (e.g., “Fallacy_1”). Interestingly, Meta-Llama and Mistral sometimes preserved
similar or even improved macro F1, indicating they could compensate for lost seman-
tic clues by relying on contextual cues in the definitions, and thus moving away from
any bias from their training data. Falcon-Mamba, however, suffered significant per-
formance declines, highlighting its heavier dependence on memorized label semantics
==PAGE:53==
5 Conclusion
44
rather than in-context learning.
In conclusion, our work contibutes in understanding not only how large language
models might assist in logical fallacy detection, but also what their current limita-
tions are. As misinformation and flawed reasoning persist in public discourse, the
continued evolution of fallacy detection methods could play a crucial role in foster-
ing more critical, reasoned dialogue. By refining prompt engineering, exploring more
sophisticated reasoning strategies, and integrating external knowledge sources, we
can move closer to automated systems that not only detect fallacies but also provide
transparent justifications for their decisions.

CHAPTER 6
Future Work
Moving forward, several directions could help refine and extend the work presented
in this thesis.
First, an appropriate path for further research would be to try out more recent
iterations of the models employed in this work. Large language model development is
moving very fast, and each new iteration improves on reasoning capacity and under-
standing of context. Trying out current models like newer iterations of Meta-Llama,
Falcon-Mamba, and Mistral might give more accurate results and give us a contem-
porary report on state-of-the-art fallacy detection capabilities.
Also, future research would investigate how model size affects performance mea-
sures.
This research compared models of roughly equal parameter sizes; however,
a comparison of a wider range of models—including smaller as well as larger and
more powerful models—would enable a better understanding of the trade-offs that
can be made between computational eﬀiciency and classification accuracy. This kind
of analysis would also inform us whether smaller models, can achieve the performance
of their larger versions and consequently improve accessibility for applications with
limited resources.
Another path could be adding justification generation to the fallacy detection
process. Detecting a fallacy is often less meaningful if users cannot understand the
model’s reasoning. Future work might couple classification outputs with short, auto-
matically generated justifications that illuminate why a particular argument is falla-
cious, thereby supporting both interpretability and educational objectives.
Finally, the inclusion of external knowledge sources, would also enhance logical
fallacy detection. Hybrid approaches leveraging LLMs and retrieval-augmented gen-
eration (RAG) can further enhance the ability of LLMs in fallacy-detection tasks.
