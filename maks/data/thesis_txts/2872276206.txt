Abstract
Most of the global railway network is built using traditional ballasted track systems, which
consist of rails supported by sleepers embedded in crushed stone ballast. These systems are
cost-effective and offer good vibration damping, helping to reduce noise levels and ground-
borne vibrations. However, they are prone to issues such as ballast degradation, differential
settlement, and increased maintenance needs over time. In contrast, some forms of ballastless
systems employ a concrete slab instead of loose ballast, offering improved structural integrity
and reduced maintenance, but are accompanied by increased noise and vibration levels. This
study employs finite element modelling using PLAXIS 3D to compare the performance of a
typical ballasted track with the Rheda 2000 slab track, with a focus on noise and vibration
behaviour.
To address the vibration challenges associated with ballastless systems, this study explores
modifications to components within the Rheda 2000 system that can be adjusted without
altering its fundamental design. Several design alternatives were tested to minimise rail-borne
noise, and the most promising configuration—combining stiffer rail pads with softer under-slab
mats—was also evaluated for ground-borne vibration performance. The results suggest that
this hybrid solution can significantly reduce both noise and vibrations, offering a viable path
for optimising slab track systems.

Introduction
Railway transport has long played a crucial role in the economic and social development of
nations, facilitating the movement of people and goods over long distances with efficiency
and reliability. Among the critical infrastructural components of any railway system is the
track superstructure, which provides the physical interface between the rolling stock and the
underlying subgrade. Traditionally, most railways worldwide have employed a ballasted track
system—rails resting on sleepers laid upon a bed of crushed stone (ballast)—as the standard
design for the track. However, in recent decades, particularly in Europe and other industrialised
regions, there has been a shift towards the adoption of ballastless track systems, particularly
slab tracks, especially in high-speed and urban rail networks.
This transition reflects broader efforts to modernise rail infrastructure in response to
changing transportation demands, technological advancements, and policy objectives. In the
European Union (EU), such efforts are driven not only by the need for increased performance
and capacity but also by a strong commitment to sustainability, interoperability, and compet-
itiveness in the global transport sector, discussed further later on in this paper. The develop-
ment of high-speed rail networks—capable of exceeding 300 km/h—has particularly catalysed
the search for more stable, durable, and low-maintenance track systems, of which the slab track
is a prominent example [11].
Ballasted track has historically been the dominant form of railway track construction
due to its relatively low initial cost, ease of installation, and adaptability to a variety of ground
conditions. The system consists of rails mounted on sleepers (traditionally made of timber, but

distributes loads and facilitates drainage [7]. The cross-section of a typical ballasted track is

Since the advent of railway transport in the 19th century, this method has proven highly
effective in supporting the heavy and dynamic loads generated by train operations. Ballast
provides a degree of elasticity and damping, allowing the track to respond flexibly to axle loads
and thermal expansion. Moreover, maintenance activities such as tamping and ballast cleaning
are well understood and can be carried out with established machinery.
Despite these advantages, ballasted track systems face significant challenges under con-
ditions of high-speed and high-frequency usage. With increased speeds and axle loads, the
rate of ballast degradation accelerates, leading to frequent maintenance interventions, track
settlement, and alignment issues.
Another one of the most significant problems associated
with ballasted track is ballast fouling, contamination of the ballast layer by fine grain material,
which significantly reduces the track’s resistance to loads and energy absorption capacity [38].

types exist, including embedded rail systems, precast concrete panels, and monolithic in-situ
concrete slabs. The concept is not new—early implementations date back to the 1970s [4]—but
has gained renewed interest in light of modern demands.
The defining characteristic of slab track is its rigidity and long-term stability. By pro-
viding a fixed and immovable platform for the rail, slab track minimises track geometry degra-
dation and reduces the need for frequent maintenance. This is particularly advantageous in
tunnels, viaducts, and high-speed corridors, where access for maintenance is restricted or where
alignment tolerances are critical.

create a unified and interoperable transport infrastructure across the continent. Rail transport
is a cornerstone of this strategy, both as a low-emission alternative to road and air travel and
as a means of fostering regional integration.
High-speed rail (HSR) has been prioritised within the TEN-T framework, with core
corridors connecting major cities across national borders.
In this context, the limitations
of traditional ballasted track systems have become increasingly apparent. The high speeds
promised—often exceeding 250 km/h—require precisely aligned and stable tracks. Further-

1.2
Ballasted vs. slab Track
The ballasted and slab tracks are two fundamentally different approaches to constructing rail-
way superstructures while serving the same main objectives. Some advantages and disadvan-
tages of slab tracks compared to the ballasted alternative are discussed in this section.
1.2.1
Advantages of slab track systems
The transition to slab track technology offers numerous advantages that justify its adoption in
specific contexts. Foremost amongst these is the dramatic reduction in maintenance require-
ments. Studies indicate that slab tracks can reduce routine maintenance needs by up to 90%
compared to ballasted systems, as shown in Figure 3. This maintenance efficiency translates

The improved ride quality enhances the passenger experience whilst addressing environmental
concerns in urban areas.
1.2.2
Disadvantages and limitations of slab track systems
Despite their advantages, slab track systems present significant challenges that must be carefully
considered. The initial capital cost typically exceeds that of ballasted track by 30-60% [10],
representing a substantial investment barrier. This cost differential is particularly pronounced
in regions with readily available ballast materials and established supply chains for conventional

The construction of slab tracks demands exceptional precision and quality control, re-
quiring specialised expertise and equipment. The monolithic nature of the system means that
errors during construction can have long-lasting consequences, with remedial works being com-
plex and costly. The inflexibility of slab tracks poses challenges for future modifications or
realignments, limiting adaptability to changing operational requirements [10].
From an environmental perspective, slab tracks have a severe disadvantage in their
significant use of concrete required for their construction [1]. However, considering the longer
lifespan of the slab tracks, they have the potential of being more environmentally friendly than
the ballasted tracks [34].
Slab tracks exhibit higher noise and vibration levels compared to ballasted systems,
particularly in the frequency ranges most perceptible to human hearing. Whilst mitigation
measures exist, they add complexity and cost to the system. The rigid support structure trans-
mits more dynamic forces to underlying structures, necessitating robust subgrade preparation
and potentially limiting applications in areas with poor ground conditions [17].
Higher noise levels in slab tracks can affect the ride quality of the passengers. Both
the noise and vibrations are also perceivable to the residents of the buildings near slab tracks.
Several different methodologies have been proposed to measure or mitigate the noise and vi-
brations of slab tracks [25]. Zhang et al. [46] have analysed two methods of reducing the noise
levels of a 1:5 slab track model. While the higher vibration levels have not shown any impact
on the track itself, Ngamkhanong and Kaewunruen [33] showed that they can negatively affect
specific railway equipment such as the overhead catenary system.

1.3
State of the art
Despite the growing popularity of ballastless systems, ballasted track continues to account
for the majority of railway infrastructure worldwide, particularly in conventional and freight-
dominated corridors [40]. The enduring use of ballasted systems stems from their well-established
performance record, cost-effectiveness in certain contexts, and flexibility during installation and
maintenance.
In Europe, traditional ballasted track remains the default choice for regional, suburban,
and freight lines, where operational speeds are typically below 200 km/h. Its use is prevalent
in countries such as Poland, Romania, and parts of France and Germany, especially for routes
not included in high-speed rail development plans. Likewise, in rural and less densely popu-
lated regions, ballasted track offers a cost-effective solution with lower capital investment and
adequate service performance.
Modern ballasted track construction employs high-quality materials and precise engi-
neering to improve durability and alignment retention. Innovations such as under-sleeper pads,
geosynthetics, and improved tamping technologies have extended the performance envelope of
ballasted tracks. Moreover, mechanised maintenance practices have helped mitigate some of
the traditional drawbacks, such as geometry degradation and high life-cycle costs [10].

1.3.1
Slab track as a high-performance alternative
Slab track systems are now widely regarded as the preferred solution for high-speed rail, urban
transit lines, and infrastructure in geotechnically complex or access-restricted environments [9,
39, 45]. Their global adoption is expanding, with notable implementations in Europe, Asia,
and increasingly, the Middle East.
In Europe, slab track is extensively used in the construction of high-speed lines, such
as Spain’s AVE, Germany’s ICE corridors, and France’s LGV extensions, enabling operating
speeds of up to 300–350 km/h with minimal geometric deviation. Austria’s Semmering Base
Tunnel [36] and the Brenner Base Tunnel [5]—two of the most ambitious engineering projects in
the EU—also adopt slab track to ensure long-term performance in challenging alpine conditions.
Urban metro systems across the continent—such as those in Vienna, Paris, and Copen-
hagen—have likewise shifted towards slab track to take advantage of its reduced maintenance
demands and space-efficient design. In these settings, the constrained geometry of tunnels and
the need for minimal service disruption render slab track particularly attractive.
Outside Europe, Japan has employed slab track in its Shinkansen network since the
1970s, demonstrating the long-term durability and stability of the system.
China has also

1.3.2
Hybrid and context-specific design approaches
Contemporary railway construction is increasingly characterised by hybrid approaches that inte-
grate both ballasted and slab track systems within the same corridor. This allows infrastructure
designers to optimise performance and cost by tailoring track structures to local conditions.
For instance, on the Paris–Strasbourg LGV Est Europ´eenne [30], slab track is used
in tunnels and on viaducts, while ballasted track is employed on open stretches to reduce
construction costs.
A similar strategy is evident in the UK’s High Speed 2 (HS2) project,
where slab track is designated for tunnel sections and sections requiring high resilience and
precision, while high-performance ballasted track is planned for more conventional segments.
This pragmatic combination reflects an emerging consensus in the industry: that no single
solution is universally optimal, and the choice of track form must reflect a balance of technical,
economic, and environmental considerations.
However, the double use of ballasted and ballastless tracks in one lane comes at the cost
of the transition zones, which are extremely complicated zones of differing stiffness, leading
to high differential settlements and increased dynamic loads [21]. This particular fact, along
with the environmental impacts of the increased noise and vibrations of slab tracks, is the most
challenging technical disadvantage when utilising slab tracks.

1.3.3
Current noise and vibration mitigation methods
Railway noise and vibration mitigation has become increasingly critical as rail networks expand
into densely populated areas and operating speeds continue to rise. Contemporary mitigation
strategies can be broadly categorised into measures addressing the source (track and vehi-
cle), the propagation path, and the receiver. The selection of appropriate mitigation methods
depends on numerous factors, including the type of railway operation, local environmental
conditions, regulatory requirements, and economic constraints.
Track-based mitigation measures represent the most direct approach to controlling
noise and vibration at the source. Rail grinding and maintenance programmes have proven
effective in reducing rolling noise by up to 10 dB through the elimination of rail corrugation and
surface irregularities. Modern rail grinding techniques employ computer-controlled equipment
that can restore optimal rail profiles whilst minimising material removal. Additionally, some
countries like Germany have employed special rail grinding techniques according to acoustic
criteria, which have shown reductions in rail noise by up to 6 dB compared to normal grinding
methods [44].
Vehicle-based mitigation focuses on reducing noise and vibration generation at the
wheel-rail interface and within the rolling stock itself. Wheel dampers and resilient wheels
incorporating rubber or polymer elements between the wheel centre and tyre can reduce wheel
radiation by up to 6 dB. Optimisations in the wheel shape have also helped reduce the wheel
noise. Modern bogie designs incorporating primary and secondary suspension tuning can min-
imise the transmission of vibrations to the vehicle body and reduce the dynamic forces trans-
mitted to the track. The type of brake system also can significantly affect the noise level during

Noise barriers and screens remain the most visible and widely implemented mitiga-
tion measure along railway corridors. Traditional concrete or metal barriers can achieve noise
reductions of 5-15 dB depending on their height, length, and proximity to the source [29, 27].
Recent innovations include low-height barriers placed close to the rail that target rolling noise
specifically, achieving similar or even superior performance to full-height barriers for this domi-
nant noise source whilst maintaining visual permeability [42]. Absorptive treatments on barrier
surfaces facing the track can provide additional reductions of 1-3 dB by minimising multiple
reflections [31].
Novel barrier designs incorporating Helmholtz resonators [8] or sonic crys-
tals [35] show promise for enhanced performance at specific frequency ranges, though practical
implementation remains limited.
Ground vibration mitigation through wave barriers or vibration screens involves
interrupting the propagation path between source and receiver. Open trenches can provide
substantial vibration reduction but are often impractical due to stability and drainage concerns.
In-filled trenches using materials with impedance mismatch to the surrounding soil, such as
expanded polystyrene or rubber chips, offer a more practical solution with vibration reductions
of 5-15 dB achievable. Sheet pile walls and diaphragm walls have been successfully implemented
in urban areas, though their effectiveness diminishes at low frequencies where wavelengths
exceed the barrier dimensions [44].
The effectiveness of these mitigation methods varies considerably depending on local
conditions, frequency content of the disturbance, and implementation quality. Whilst individual
measures may achieve modest reductions, the combination of complementary approaches can

1.4
Scope of this research
This study focused on the noise and vibration problems of the Rheda 2000 slab track, which has
widespread popularity not only in Germany but also in the surrounding nations, particularly
Norway and Denmark. Finite element (FE) modelling was utilised to study these parameters
and compare the slab track to a typical ballasted design. Three models were constructed from
the bottom up in the PLAXIS 3D software for this research.
The models focus on ballasted and slab tracks in a straight line with no curves or gradient
constructed directly on a bedrock layer and do not take into consideration the transition zone
between the two tracks. The proper models consider only a train speed of 200 km/h as a
representative of slower lines where ballasted track is adequate and the start of higher speeds
where the advantages of slab tracks are more highlighted.
However, a sensitivity analysis
explores the behaviour of the models at different velocities, among other parameters.
In addition to the analytical models, some experiments were also deemed necessary in
order to gather needed data for the software in order to properly simulate a close-to-real-life
situation. These experiments measured the damping parameters of a number of under-ballast
mats, and used the resulting values for the under-slab mats (direct values) and rail pads (indirect
estimation) in the FE models.

The thesis pays particular attention to changes in the Rheda 2000 system that do not
alter the core design and rather can be implemented on already constructed tracks with varying
degrees of difficulty. Specifically, the rail pads and under-sleeper mats (USMs), the main elastic
components that are also present in ballasted tracks (under-ballast mats (UBMs) instead of

The material properties of rail pads and USMs are very susceptible to temperature.
However, in this study, the ambient temperature was considered to be constantly at 20 degrees
Celsius.
The main question this thesis tries to answer is: Is there a particular configuration of rail
pads and under-ballast mats that succeeds in eliminating the difference in noise and vibrations
between ballasted and slab tracks? To answer this, firstly, there is a need to define the scope
at which the noise and vibrations are compared.
The noise associated with railway systems has multiple different sources.
From the
trackside, rails and sleepers are considered the noisiest components. The rolling stock also
significantly contributes to this noise from the wheels and the air resistance of the trains them-
selves. Also, in any acoustic analysis, one of the most important factors is the distance from
the source of the noise being measured. In this study, only the rail-borne noise directly at the
source of a specific section of a rail is considered. This was deemed a fair compromise, as the
rolling stock are not directly related to the research question, and studying the noise at the
source reduces the complexity of calculations.
As for the vibrations, they are similarly only considered as the ground-borne vibrations
and are only measured at the source, directly under the middle of the track superstructure at

The governing equation of dynamic systems is Equation 1.

(1)

2.3
Increasing the rail pad stiffness
A common misconception in the railway industry is that the more rigid the overall track is, the
more noise it will generate. This could partially be true and has an intuitive reasoning behind
it. If one compares the noise a metallic material makes when hitting a surface compared to
rubber, it is obvious that the metallic material, having the more rigid structure, emits the
higher sound level. However, comparing an impact noise to the noise radiated by the rails of
a railway system is not a fair comparison. The mechanisms behind the noise generation of a
railway system are more complex and different from those of a simple impact test.
Increasing the stiffness, however, decreases the damping ratio of a system, as Equation 12
clearly shows the inverse relationship of ζ and k in a single degree of freedom (SDOF) system.

one can show that increasing the rail pad stiffness can lead to a decrease in the rail velocity
amplitude, it could be said that the sound level emitted from the rail will be reduced. This
is very likely to be true, as for a typical system, the higher the overall stiffness and damping
capability, the lower the responses would be, as indicated by Equation 1.

3.4
Limitations
The methodology described in this section has a number of limitations that can potentially
affect the research’s resulting conclusions to varying degrees.
First and foremost, the transformation of the UBM correlations to the corresponding
rail pad ones relies on the assumption that extrapolating the data from the softer UBMs to the
more rigid rail pads is realistic and that the range of rail pad elastic modulus can be correctly
used in this context. In absence of more reliable samples to test or previous results from other
sources, this method was the only valid way of obtaining necessary data needed for this project,
but at the very least a direct test on rail pad samples must be performed before the results
here can be fully trusted.
The testing methodology, as mentioned before, was limited due to the resources available
to the author. More appropriate apparatus would have been preferred when performing the
experiments.
The methodology of comparing the noise emission between the different tracks is also
very simplified. The most accurate method of this comparison is to measure this on-site. The
method described here is simple and can potentially be a source of inaccuracy for the results.
The PLAXIS models all utilise the linear elastic material model. This potentially could
affect the results, as the rail pads in real life have a hyperelastic behaviour, and the ballast and

5
Discussion
The experimental and numerical results demonstrate that increasing rail pad stiffness, contrary
to conventional practice, can significantly improve the acoustic performance of slab track sys-
tems. This section examines the implications of these findings for railway infrastructure design
and maintenance strategies.
5.1
Optimal rail pad stiffness and acoustic performance
The experimental results reveal a non-linear relationship between rail pad elastic modulus and
damping performance. The free vibration decay tests conducted on UBM samples indicated
an optimal damping coefficient at approximately 650 MPa, whilst the finite element analysis
suggested an optimal value of 800 MPa for minimising rail-borne noise.
The difference in
these values can be attributed to the simplified nature of the experimental approach, which
analysed only two vibration modes, compared to the comprehensive three-dimensional dynamic
behaviour captured in the FE model.
The existence of an optimal stiffness value challenges the current assumption in the
railway industry that softer components inherently provide superior vibration isolation. As
demonstrated in chapter 2.3, whilst increased stiffness reduces the damping ratio according to
Equation 12, it simultaneously decreases rail deflections and velocities. The optimal point at
800 MPa represents the balance where reduced vibration amplitude outweighs the decreased
damping capacity, resulting in minimal sound radiation according to the Rayleigh integral
formulation.

5.2
Analysis of track configuration performance
Figure 27 exhibits remarkable consistency across all tested slab track configurations. The char-
acteristic response curve—initial gradual decrease, accelerated reduction, and eventual reversal
after the minimum—appears in all three variants. This behavioural similarity, despite different
USM properties, suggests that the fundamental noise generation mechanisms remain consistent,
with the USM characteristics changing the magnitude of the noise level rather than altering
the underlying physics.
The quantitative improvements compared to the current industry standard (150 MPa
rail pads on a plain slab track) are substantial:
1. Plain slab track achieves 6.2 dB reduction
2. Configuration with stiff USM (D1519) achieves 7.9 dB reduction
3. Configuration with soft USM (D319) achieves 8.2 dB reduction
These reductions represent significant acoustic benefits, as each 3 dB reduction corre-
sponds to a halving of sound intensity. The 8.2 dB improvement with the optimal configuration
represents a reduction of roughly 85% in acoustic energy emission.
Compared to the ballasted track with soft rail pads, the combination also performs
admirably. The noise levels are reduced by 5.0 dB compared to the current implementation of
ballasted tracks. This value was reduced to a reduction of 3.1 dB, however, when comparing
it to a ballasted track fitted with the same rail pads. The reduction in sound level is lower in
this case but still significant.

5.3
Ground-borne vibration behaviour
The vibration analysis reveals frequency-dependent behaviour that underscores the complexity
of track optimisation. Figure 28 demonstrates that increasing rail pad stiffness in plain slab
tracks produces divergent effects: vibrations above 1100 Hz decrease substantially, whilst those
below 600 Hz increase, sometimes by an order of magnitude. This phenomenon results from
the shift in natural frequencies of the rail-pad-sleeper assembly with increased stiffness.
The critical frequency ranges for human comfort (0.1-80 Hz) and building response
(30-250 Hz), as noted by Esveld [10], fall precisely within the region where vibrations are
amplified. Figure 30 illustrates this concern more clearly, showing vibration magnitudes at
these frequencies can exceed the baseline configuration by factors of 10 or more.

5.4
Practical implementation considerations
The findings suggest significant potential for improving existing slab track systems, though
several practical aspects require consideration.
Rail pad replacement represents a routine maintenance operation that can be performed
without major track reconstruction. This contrasts sharply with USM replacement, which re-
quires slab removal and represents a significant infrastructure intervention. Consequently, exist-
ing tracks could potentially achieve improved acoustic performance through rail pad upgrades
alone, though vibration performance would remain compromised without USM installation.
The incorporation of USMs, whilst acoustically beneficial, introduces a vulnerability into
the robust slab track design. Their typical service life is shorter than other track components,
and their inaccessible location contradicts the low-maintenance philosophy of slab track systems.
This ”weakest link” characteristic necessitates careful consideration in design decisions.
Cost implications must also be evaluated comprehensively. Whilst stiffer rail pads may
have comparable unit costs to softer alternatives, USM inclusion adds both material and instal-
lation expenses. However, the 8.2 dB noise reduction could eliminate requirements for expensive
noise barriers, potentially providing overall cost savings in noise-sensitive areas.
5.5
Study limitations and future research
Several limitations of this research warrant acknowledgement. The use of linear elastic material
models, whilst providing reliable comparative results, simplifies the complex behaviour of track

Rail pads exhibit hyperelastic characteristics, and ballast behaviour includes
particle rearrangement effects not captured in the current models.
The experimental correlation between stiffness and damping relied on UBM samples with
extrapolation to rail pad properties. Direct testing of rail pad samples across the full stiffness
range would strengthen these findings. Additionally, the simplified acoustic comparison method
requires validation against field measurements or more sophisticated acoustic modelling.
The analysis focused on a single train speed (200 km/h) and idealised track geometry.
Real-world conditions include varying speeds, track irregularities, and environmental factors
affecting material properties. Investigation of optimisation robustness under these varied con-
ditions would enhance practical applicability.

6
Conclusion
This research has investigated the potential for improving the acoustic and vibration perfor-
mance of slab track railway systems through optimisation of their elastic component properties.
The study focused specifically on the Rheda 2000 system, examining various configurations of
rail pad stiffness and under-sleeper mat properties through both experimental testing and finite
element analysis using the PLAXIS 3D software.
6.1
Principal findings
The investigation has yielded several significant findings that challenge conventional approaches
to railway track design. Contrary to current industry practice favouring soft rail pads, this
study demonstrates that an optimal rail pad stiffness exists at approximately 800 MPa for
minimising rail-borne noise.
This finding was supported by both experimental correlation
(predicting 650 MPa) and comprehensive FE analysis (indicating 800 MPa). The existence
of this optimum reflects the balance between reduced rail deflection amplitudes and decreased
system damping, with the former effect dominating at the optimal stiffness value.
The most effective configuration identified combines relatively stiff rail pads (elastic
modulus of 800 MPa) with soft under-slab mats (Getzner’s D319, with bedding modulus of
30 MN/m3). This combination achieved:
1. 8.2 dB reduction in rail-borne noise compared to standard configurations
2. 3.1 dB improvement over optimised ballasted track systems

3. Consistent vibration reduction across almost all frequencies from 0 to 1500 Hz
These improvements represent substantial acoustic benefits that could significantly im-
pact railway noise management strategies in urban environments.
The research also revealed complex frequency-dependent behaviour in ground-borne vi-
brations. Whilst increasing rail pad stiffness alone reduces high-frequency vibrations (above
1100 Hz), it amplifies low-frequency vibrations in the critical ranges for human comfort and
structural response. The inclusion of soft USMs somewhat effectively mitigates this adverse
effect (depending on the frequency range), creating a dual-isolation system that performs ef-
fectively across the entire frequency spectrum.
6.2
Contributions to railway engineering
The study provides quantitative evidence for the relationship between rail pad stiffness and
acoustic performance, establishing that the conventional preference for soft components may be
suboptimal for noise control. The experimental correlation between elastic modulus and damp-
ing parameters, whilst requiring further validation, offers a framework for predicting acoustic
behaviour of track components.
For railway infrastructure designers, this research offers specific guidance:
1. Rail pad selection should consider acoustic optimisation, not merely load distribution
2. Combined stiff rail pad/soft USM systems can achieve superior acoustic performance to
either component alone

6.3
Implications for infrastructure development
The findings have significant implications for railway infrastructure planning and development
and challenge established practices, suggesting a fundamental reconsideration of track compo-
nent selection criteria. The demonstration that properly configured elastic components can
simultaneously reduce noise and vibrations opens new possibilities for urban railway design,
where both constraints are critical. In such cases, the optimised configuration offers a means
of achieving compliance without extensive additional mitigation measures. The 8.2 dB noise
reduction could eliminate the need for noise barriers in many situations, potentially reducing
infrastructure costs and visual impact.
The methodology developed—combining experimental characterisation, numerical mod-
elling, and parametric studies—provides a framework applicable to other track systems. Differ-
ent slab track designs, transition zones, and ballasted track improvements could benefit from
similar analytical approaches.

6.6
Concluding remarks
This research has demonstrated that significant improvements in the acoustic and vibration
performance of slab track systems are achievable through careful selection of elastic component
properties. The finding that stiffer rail pads, when properly combined with soft under-slab
mats, can reduce both noise and vibrations challenges conventional wisdom and opens new
possibilities for track design optimisation.
The potential for 8.2 dB noise reduction through component optimisation represents a
major opportunity for the railway industry to address one of the primary concerns associated
with slab track adoption. As railway networks continue to expand in urban areas and operating
speeds increase, the importance of effective noise and vibration control without compromising
some environmental aspects of the track will only grow.
The methodology and findings presented provide a foundation for rethinking how railway
tracks are designed and optimised. Rather than treating noise and vibration as problems to be
mitigated after design, this research shows they can be effectively controlled through informed
component selection. This integrated approach to track design offers the potential for more
sustainable, cost-effective, and community-friendly railway infrastructure.
Implementation of these findings will require collaboration between infrastructure man-
agers, component manufacturers, and researchers to validate performance, develop standards,
and refine designs. With appropriate development and validation, the optimised configura-
tions identified in this research could contribute significantly to the advancement of railway
technology and the broader adoption of sustainable transport systems.

Abstract. Most of the global railway network is built using traditional
ballasted track systems, which consist of rails supported by sleepers em-
bedded in crushed stone ballast. These systems are cost-effective and
offer good vibration damping, helping to reduce noise levels and ground-
borne vibrations. However, they are prone to issues such as ballast degra-
dation, differential settlement, and increased maintenance needs over
time. In contrast, ballastless systems employ a concrete slab instead of
loose ballast, offering improved structural integrity and reduced main-
tenance, but are accompanied by increased noise and vibration levels.
This study employs finite element modelling using PLAXIS 3D to com-
pare the performance of a typical ballasted track with the Rheda 2000
slab track, with a focus on noise and vibration behaviour. To address the
vibration challenges associated with ballastless systems, this study ex-
plores modifications to components within the Rheda 2000 system that
can be adjusted without altering its fundamental design. Several de-
sign alternatives were tested to minimise rail-borne noise, and the most
promising configuration—combining stiffer rail pads with softer under-
slab mats—was also evaluated for ground-borne vibration performance.
The results suggest that this hybrid solution can significantly reduce both
noise and vibrations, offering a viable path for optimising slab track sys-
tems.
Keywords: Railway · Vibration · Noise · Slab · Ballasted · Bal-
lastless · Rheda 2000.
1
Introduction
The ballasted track system has been the most widely used track structure in the
world since the 1930s. It consists of rails supported by sleepers, which are em-
bedded in a layer of crushed stone ballast. The ballast layer serves three primary
functions: it transfers stresses from the superstructure to the subgrade, provides
longitudinal and lateral stability to the track, and facilitates rapid drainage of
precipitation from the track surface [4].
==PAGE:73==
2
S. Aghaie et al.
Despite their widespread use, ballasted track systems exhibit several limita-
tions. Common issues include ballast fouling, the infiltration of fine-grained soils
or other foreign materials into the ballast layer, which degrades drainage capac-
ity and mechanical performance. In addition, these systems are characterized by
high maintenance demands, relatively short service lifetimes, and irregular track
settlements. To address these deficiencies, alternative systems such as concrete
slab tracks have been developed and increasingly adopted. The most widely
used slab track configuration replaces the ballast layer with a reinforced con-
crete slab in which the sleepers are embedded. Compared to ballasted tracks,
slab systems typically offer a longer service life, reduced subgrade settlement,
and minimal maintenance requirements [3]. From an environmental perspective,
their extended durability also suggests a lower life-cycle environmental impact
[12].
Despite their technological advantages, slab track systems present several no-
table drawbacks. While they offer improved durability and reduced maintenance
compared to traditional ballasted tracks, they are associated with higher initial
construction costs and, when maintenance is required, potentially greater repair
expenses [5]. Additionally, slab tracks typically generate higher levels of noise
and ground-borne vibrations, which can adversely affect nearby structures and
environments.
To address these issues, attention has turned to the vibration-damping com-
ponents within the track structure. In the Rheda 2000 system—a widely im-
plemented slab track design in Norway—the primary components that can be
modified without compromising the system’s core design are the rail pads and
under-slab mats (USMs). These elastic elements play a crucial role in mitigating
vibrations. Rail pads can be relatively easily replaced in existing tracks, whereas
the installation of USMs is generally limited to new construction projects, due
to their integration beneath the slab.
The use of softer rail pads became prevalent following observations that ex-
cessively stiff pads contributed to sleeper damage in older ballasted tracks [8].
This trend has continued in modern track systems, including slab tracks, de-
spite established findings that stiffer rail pads can reduce overall track noise in
ballasted configurations [15]. However, to date, no comparable studies have sys-
tematically evaluated the influence of rail pad and USM stiffness on noise and
vibration in ballastless systems.
This paper seeks to address that gap by evaluating strategies to reduce noise
and vibration in the Rheda 2000 slab track system. Various configurations of
rail pad and USM stiffness are explored to identify optimal combinations that
minimize both rail-borne noise and ground-borne vibrations while remaining
practical for either retrofit or new construction scenarios.

3.1
Characterization of USM and rail pad properties
The rail pads are one of the main components investigated in this study, requir-
ing a correlation between their stiffness and damping characteristics. As no direct
data were available, a small experiment was conducted to estimate this relation-
ship. A baseline was established using the rail pad applied in both the ballasted
and initial ballastless models. Based on the works of Sainz-Aja et al. [13] and
Li et al. [11], a TPE rail pad with a vertical stiffness of K = 150 kN/mm and
Rayleigh damping parameters of α = 21.0 s−1 and β = 2.3 × 10−4 s was chosen.
The dimensions of the rail pad were set to 0.1 × 0.1 × 0.01 m, resulting in a
elastic modulus of E = 150 MPa. To estimate the correlation, four samples of
Under Ballast Mats (UBMs) were used. These have very similar properties to
USMs, and their materials are sufficiently close to that of the rail pads that it
was assumed any relationship between stiffness and Rayleigh parameters would
be similar enough to apply to the rail pads. Each sample was excited twice at dif-
ferent frequencies, and their accelerations were measured using an accelerometer.

Modelling framework
In the present study, three different Finite Element (FE) models were created to
simulate the rail track systems: one ballasted track, one Rheda 2000 system with
USM, and one without. The models were constructed using the “very fine” mesh
option built into PLAXIS. It was assumed that the environment of the model is
inside a tunnel—like most use cases of slab tracks—so the subgrade was modelled

to have bedrock properties. As a result, the ballast and concrete slab layers were
directly placed on the bedrock, with no subballast or hydraulically bound layers
(HBL). The length of the models was set to 48 metres, with a 5-metre-deep
bedrock layer. A reference train load with wheel loads of 150 kN, four wheelsets,
and wheelset distances of 3.75, 7.5, and 3.75 metres was used in the models.

simulation time of each iteration was 0.8 seconds. Each simulation had 2400
calculation steps, resulting in a sampling rate of 3000 Hz.

Conclusions
The main findings of this study are summarized as follows: (i) Increasing the
stiffness of the rail pads in combination with soft under-slab mats (USMs) sig-
nificantly enhances the performance of the Rheda 2000 system, enabling it to
outperform both ballasted and alternative ballastless track systems in terms of
rail-borne noise and ground-borne vibrations. (ii) In terms of noise reduction,
this configuration resulted in emissions that were 1.9 dB lower than plain slab
tracks, 0.4 dB lower than slab tracks with stiffer USMs, and 5.0 dB lower than
ballasted tracks with soft rail pads, all evaluated at their respective optimal rail
pad stiffness values. Compared to current state-of-the-art configurations, the im-
provement reached 8.2 dB. Even if the ballasted track were upgraded with stiffer
800 MPa rail pads, the proposed configuration would still provide a 3.1 dB ad-
vantage.
Regarding ground-borne vibrations, the proposed combination consistently
exhibited the lowest vibration levels across all frequencies. The plain slab track
with soft rail pads performed the worst at higher frequencies, with vibration
magnitudes exceeding those of the best-performing configuration by more than
==PAGE:81==
10
S. Aghaie et al.
two orders of magnitude. While ballasted tracks with stiff rail pads showed com-
petitive performance at higher frequencies, the proposed slab track configuration
outperformed all alternatives throughout the full frequency spectrum.
Further research is essential to establish a reliable correlation between rail
pad stiffness and damping properties, and to quantify noise radiation differences
across various track types. The next step should be the construction of a full-scale
test track to experimentally validate these findings, with particular attention to
the long-term durability and maintenance performance of the proposed design
compared to existing solutions.
