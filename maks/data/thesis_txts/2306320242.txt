Abstract (English)
The use of mobile devices has been increasing drasticly over the last few
years and they are being used more and more to store and access data.
Some of that data might need to be protected in a way where a simple
PIN-number will be insuﬃcient. As iris recognition in the visual spectrum
has received more attention over the years it opens up the possibility of using
the camera of a mobile device for iris recognition to access data. However,
the camera in a mobile device is usually not able to capture high quality
images which means a feature extractor might not be able to extract enough
information to securely distinguish between an imposter and a genuine access
attempt. Since some feature extractors collect diﬀerent types of information
a fusion of diﬀerent types of feature extractors might complement each other
to increase the possibility to distinguish between an imposter and a genuine
access attempt.
For this project, some conventional feature extractors, key point based
feature extractors and general image descriptors were provided.
As an
addition two soft biometric feature extractors were implemented where the
colour of the iris were the information source. All tests were carried out on a
subset of the UBIRIS v2 database and the training dataset of the MobBIO
database. Both databases contain images taken in the visible wavelength.
The images in the MobBIO database were acquired by using a mobile device.
The tests showed that fusing feature extractors of diﬀerent types had a better
performance than a fusion of feature extractors from the same type. Tests
were also performed to see if improvements could be gained by fusing two,
three and four diﬀerent feature extractor types and they showed that the
best results could be obtained by using four diﬀerent feature extractors. It
was also discovered that the general image descriptor LBP (Local Binary
Pattern) actually had a better performance than all other feature extractors
used in the project. This is interesting since LBP has been reported to not
perform as well as conventional feature extractors on NIR (Near-infrared)
images.

Abstract (Danish)
Brugen af mobile enheder er steget kraftigt i de seneste ˚ar og de bliver brugt
mere og mere til at gemme og tilg˚a data.
Noget af det data der bliver
gemt eller tilg˚aet, har muligvis behov for at blive beskyttet p˚a en m˚ade
hvor, en simpel PIN-kode ikke vil være sikker nok.
Da iris genkendelse
i det visuelle spektrum har f˚aet mere opmærksomhed over de seneste ˚ar,
s˚a ˚abner det op for muligheden for at bruge kameraet i en mobil enhed
til at tilg˚a data og information.
Dog er kvaliteten af kameraet i mobile
enheder som regel ikke i stand til at tage billeder i høj kvalitet. Det gør
at feature extractors sandsynligvis ikke kan ﬁnde nok information til, at
der med høj nok sandsynlighed, vil kunne laves en korrekt afvisning af en
bedrager og en korrekt godkendelse af en person der m˚a tilg˚a det speciﬁkke
data.
Der ﬁndes dog forskellige typer af feature extractors som vil ﬁnde
forskellige typer af information i et iris. Det vil derfor være en mulighed,
at brugen af ﬂere forskellige typer feature extractors p˚a det samme iris vil
kunne komplementere hinanden s˚aledes, at sandsynligheden for en korrekt
afvisning og korrekt godkendelse vil kunne blive forbedret.
Til dette projekt blev der stillet nogle konventionelle feature extractors,
key point feature extractors og general image descriptors til r˚adighed. Ud
over det, blev der udviklet to soft biometric feature extractor variationer
som brugte farven i en iris som kilde til information. Alle tests der er blevet
lavet igennem hele projektet er blevet udført p˚a en mindre del af UBIRIS v2
databasen og trænings sættet fra MobBIO. Billederne i begge databaser er
taget i visuelt lys. Billederne fra MobBIO databasen er desuden taget af en
mobil enhed. Nogle af de test der blev udført viste, at brugen af forskellige
typer feature extractors i en genkendelses proces gav bedre resultater, end
hvis der blev brugt feature extractors af samme type i en genkendelses proces.
Der blev ogs˚a foretaget tests for at se om der ville være forbedringer ved at
bruge to, tre og ﬁre forskellige typer feature extractors i en genkendelses
process.
Der kom de bedste resultater ved at bruge ﬁre forskellige typer
af feature extractors. Ud fra de test der blev udført, s˚a blev det opdaget
at feature extractor’en LBP (Local Binary Pattern) generelt havde bedre
resultater end alle andre feature extractors der blev brugt i projektet. Det
er interessant da andet forskning tyder p˚a at LBP ikke har haft helt s˚a gode
resultater p˚a billeder taget i NIR lys, som for eksempel de mere konventionelle
feature extractors.

Introduction
A human being contains many physiological and behavioural traits and
these can vary quite a lot from person to person.
Since these traits
diﬀer from one person to another it is possible to perform measurements
on these characteristics and use that to deﬁne a person.
This is called
biometrics.
One of the key areas in which biometrics have been used is
on authentication/security systems. This is because many of the traits such
as ﬁngerprints or the iris are unique from person to person and are very
diﬃcult to create fake versions of.
The iris has received a great deal of
attention during the last few years due to the fact that it is believed to be
the most unique and stable trait for a person. The iris is the coloured part of
the eye, which can be seen in ﬁgure 1, and it is made of furrows, crypts, etc.
The features of the iris are stable throughout the lifetime and it is protected
by the eyelids so it is rarely damaged.
To create an iris recognition system four steps needs to be performed to
make users able to authenticate themselves. The four steps can be seen in
ﬁgure 2. The ﬁrst step is acquisition and in this step an image containing a
subjects iris needs to be captured. Once that has been done the second step
can begin. This step is called preprocessing and the major objective of this
step is to ﬁnd the iris in the captured image. The reason for locating the
iris in the image is because the iris is rarely in the exact same location in
the image or of the same size. The size and location of the iris tend to vary
based on the position of the subject i.e. distance and angle and the pupils
dilation also have an eﬀect on the size of the iris. Therefore, some sort of
boundary detection is necessary to ﬁnd the limbic and pupillary boundaries.
Another important part of the preprocessing step is to normalize or unroll
the iris. This is done because the comparison of two irises of diﬀerent sizes
will make it a lot more diﬃcult and time consuming. Therefore, the circular
iris shape is transformed into a rectangular shape of a standard size. The
third step begins once a normalized iris template has been created and a
feature extraction method is then used on the normalized iris template to
ﬁnd information about the distinctive/unique features.
After the feature
information has been extracted the comparison step is carried out. During
this step a comparator will compare the feature information of the newly
acquired iris against the feature information of an iris which has been enrolled
into the system beforehand. The comparator will then output a similarity or
dissimilarity score which can be used to authenticate the user or reject the
user.
The common iris recognition systems used nowadays use a NIR
(Near-infrared) sensor for acquiring images of a subject.
However, much

Literature research
As mentioned in the previous chapter the usual iris recognition system
consists of four modules; acquisition, preprocessing, feature extraction and
comparison. Even though the focus of the project is on fusion of feature
types and the performance feature types, literature research has been done
for all four modules to get a broad insight into the state-of-the-art within
iris recognition. This chapter brieﬂy summarizes proposed methods from the
last few years on iris acquisition, iris segmentation and iris feature extraction.
Articles on the soft biometric of iris colour will also be presented and
information about some of the most widely used comparators for matching
and fusion techniques in iris recognition systems will be explained.
2.1
Iris acquisition
For an iris recognition system to work it requires images in the enrolment
phase and in the query phase. It is therefore necessary to use some kind
of camera sensor that is able to take an image of a subjects eye so that
the subject can either be enrolled into the system or try to authenticate
him-/herself.
Figure 4: Iris acquisition on a NIR sensor. The image is taken from the
Biometrics ideal test website (CASIA database) [51].
The most common used sensor type for iris acquisition is a type that uses
NIR light (700-900 nm). Images taken in NIR light have the possibility to
greatly show the iris texture. However, a requirement for using a NIR light
sensor to take images of the iris is that the subject needs to be within 1-3 feet
(About 30.5-91.5 cm.) of the sensor [7]. This, of course, requires cooperation
from the subject when taking the picture of an eye.

2.2
Preprocessing
When an image has been acquired by an iris recognition system and before
the actual recognition can begin, the system needs to ﬁgure out where the
iris is located in the image. For this reason a recognition system will need
to perform some segmentation so that the pupillary and limbic boundaries
of the iris can be found. The segmentation can however be a bit diﬃcult
if the iris is occluded by specular reﬂections, eyelashes, eyelids or shadows.
A pre-processing step is usually performed before segmentation to ﬁnd all
possible occlusion sources. The pre-processing step also helps to create a
binary mask indicating which parts of the iris that are occluded so when the
actual recognition is done only the parts of the iris without any occlusions
are used in the recognition process. Once the boundaries have been detected
the iris will then usually be normalized/unrolled to a standard rectangular
format to make comparison easier. The most widely used method for this is
the Rubber sheet model proposed by Daugman [1]. The normalization of the
iris tends to stretch the iris features so they might seem unclear. A texture
enhancement is therefore used to make the features clearer which for instance
could be done by a histogram normalization of the contrast.
In 2008 a competition on the performance of segmentation methods was
held which was called NICE.I [35].
The idea of the competition was to
have people propose segmentation methods and evaluate them on the same
dataset. To evaluate the performance of a proposed method the proportion
of disagreeing bits in the segmentation result and the ground truth from
an image is calculated.
This is deﬁned as the segmentation error.

Wildes et al.
[32] suggest a segmentation method where a lowpass
Gaussian ﬁlter and a spatial subsampling is performed to reduce the eﬀects of
noise and to get a smaller image on which the rest of the segmentation will be
performed. Further the authors perform a gradient based edge detection with
a vertical orientation tuning to ﬁnd the edge points of the limbic boundary.
A Hough transform is used with the detected limbic edge points to ﬁnd the
best possible center coordinates and a radius to model the limbic boundary
as a circle. The pupillary boundary is located and modeled in the exact same
way except the gradient based edge detection is not tuned to any orientation.
A constraint is also added for the detection of the pupillary boundary which
is that all points must be within the limbic boundary. Subsequently, the
same procedure is performed to ﬁnd the upper and lower eyelids where the
gradient edge detector is horizontally tuned. Another diﬀerence is also that
the eyelids are modelled as parabolic arcs and not circles as the limbic and
pupillary boundary. Lastly the authors deﬁne that the iris is the area of the
image contained within the limbic and pupillary boundaries and below and
above the upper and lower eyelids, respectively. To evaluate their method the
authors used an In-house collection. The authors do not report any speciﬁc
values on how the segmentation performed.
Shah and Ross [33] start by smoothing the image with a 2D median ﬁlter
in their proposed segmentation method and inpainting of specular reﬂections
near the pupil. The minimum pixel value is found and used in a thresholding
to binarize the image.
The 2D median ﬁlter is applied to the binarized
image. The result is the possible pupil pixels though some are not true pupil
pixels. A tracing method is used to ﬁnd the boundaries of the possible pupil
pixel regions and circle ﬁtting is done based on the region boundaries. The
circle with the largest amount of possible pupil pixels is selected as the pupil
boundary. Next the authors then want to detect the limbic boundary. This
is done by using a combination of geodesics and active contours. The method
works by evolving or pushing a curve outwards based on the gradients and
geometric characteristics localized around a point on the curve. The basis of
the curve to be evolved is just beyond the pupillary boundary and the curve
is evolved until a stopping criterion is met. A circle ﬁtting is done based on
curve points between the iris and the sclera. The ﬁtted circle is subsequently
used to ﬁnd the center and radius of the iris so that it can be normalized.
The authors have tested their method on two databases (WVU non-ideal
and CASIA v3-interval). Both databases were split into two dataset of right
and left eyes. The authors used 2D Gabor wavelets as feature extraction
and they report that the left and right eyes of the WVU non-ideal got an
equal error rate of 14.19% and 12.03%, respectively. The right eye dataset
of CASIA v3-interval got an equal error rate of 3.1% and the left eye dataset
10
==PAGE:22==
got 2.48%.
Tomeo-Reyes et al. [34] has proposed a new scheme for normalization
of the iris.
The authors have deﬁned an equation for calculating the
displacement of features at a radius. The way they perform normalization
is by ﬁrstly calculating the dilation level based on the pupil radius of the
query and gallery image. Further the iris region is discretized into a number
of uniformly distributed rings. The displacement equation is subsequently
used to calculate the displacement of each of the rings up until a ring with
the same radius as the query pupils radius. Based on the original radius of a
ring and the corresponding displacement the iris is then mapped to a polar
coordinate system using a set of equations. The authors have tested their
proposed normalization method on the WVU light reﬂex database.
The
segmentation is performed using a circular Hough transform to model the
limbic and pupillary boundaries and a linear Hough transform is used to
model the eyelids. Feature extraction is based on 1D Log-Gabor ﬁlters and
the Hamming distance is used as a comparator. The authors report that
by using their normalization method they achieved a matching error rate of
about 2% (FNMR at FMR=1%).
Liu et al. [47] suggest that the use of convolution neural networks can be
used for the segmentation of an iris.
The authors propose two methods
of convolution neural networks which can be used for iris segmentation.
One of the methods is a hierarchical convolution neural network where a
convolution neural network consists of convolution layers, pooling layers and
fully connected layers. For this method three patch sizes are used (56x56,
112x112 and 224x224 pixels) around a single pixel. For each pixel in the
image three convolution neural networks are used where the input will be
one of the patches (i.e. there will be a convolution neural network for each
patch size). The result of the three neural networks for the pixel will be
fused to classify the pixel as iris or non-iris. The ﬁnal result will be a binary
mask.
The other method proposed by the authors is a multi-scale fully
convolutional network which consists of 31 convolution layers and 6 pooling
layers. The convolution and pooling layers are divided into networks over
six diﬀerent scales. The network of each scale will produce a classiﬁcation
prediction of each pixel and the fusion of all six network results for a pixel
will determine whether the pixel should be marked as iris or non-iris. The
authors have tested their proposed segmentation method on a dataset from
UBIRIS v2 and CASIA v4. The authors report that the multi-scale approach
achieved a segmentation error of 0.90% and 0.59% on UBIRIS v2 and CASIA
v4, respectively.
They also report that their hierarchical approach got a
segmentation error of 1.11% on UBIRIS v2.
Tan et al. [2] propose a four step iris segmentation approach. The ﬁrst
11
==PAGE:23==
step is a coarse localization based on clustering. The ﬁrst clustering step
is to ﬁnd candidate skin and iris region.
This is done by looking at the
pixel intensity. They mark the top 30% brightest points as skin candidate
pixels and the top 20% darkest points as iris candidate pixels. After that
the average grey level and standard deviation is calculated.
A random
region is chosen and the grey level distance between the region and an
un-clustered pixel is calculated. The pixel is added to the cluster region if the
distance is less than a threshold of 2.5 and if there exists an eight neighbour
connection path between the region and the un-clustered pixel. The actual
iris region is located by looking at the shape and position of the candidate
iris regions. The second step of the proposed segmentation method is to ﬁnd
the pupillary and limbic boundaries. The authors propose to use a modiﬁed
version of the integrodiﬀerential operator for this step. The authors call this
modiﬁed version the integrodiﬀerential constellation. The integrodiﬀerential
constellation is faster than the integrodiﬀerential operator because it does not
look at every possible point. To reﬁne the limbic and pupillary boundaries
they use intensity statistics on the boundaries to try to remove localization
inaccuracies. In the third step the authors perform eyelid localization. They
use a 1-D horizontal rank ﬁlter to ﬁlter out eyelashes. After that they use a
Canny operator in a vertical direction to ﬁnd points on the edge of the eyelid.
The points gained from the Canny operator could be noisy so the authors
created some eyelid-curvature models based on the statistical curvature of
eyelids. These models helps to remove noisy eyelid points. The last step is
eyelash and shadow detection. In this step the iris region is split into two
sub-regions (ES-candidate and ES-free). An intensity histogram is created
for each region and the Chi-Square distance of the histograms is calculated.
Based on the distance a threshold is created to be used for detecting eyelashes
and shadows. The proposed segmentation method is tested on the UBIRIS
v1 session 1, UBIRIS v1 session 2 and the UBIRIS v2 Train set. By visual
inspection the authors report that the clustering part is having an accuracy
of above 99% on all three databases. They also report that when the entire
segmentation method is used on the UBIRIS v2 Train and Test sets they get
a segmentation error of 1.29% and 1.31%, respectively.
Proenca’s [3] segmentation method is primarily created for images in the
visible wavelength but it can be modiﬁed to be used on NIR images. The
segmentation method is made up of two overall parts Detection of noise-free
iris regions which is performed ﬁrst and Iris shape parameterization which
is performed last. The ﬁrst part is is also split into two parts Detection of
sclera and Detection of iris. The detection of the sclera is the ﬁrst step to
be performed. For this step the author has deﬁned a 20-dimensional feature
set of information for a pixel.
The idea is that a full feature set will be
12
==PAGE:24==
created for each pixel. All of the feature sets will be sent to a neural network
which will classify some pixels as sclera region. The 20-dimensional feature
set holds information about the location of the pixel, the mean and standard
deviation of the pixels hue, blue chroma and red chroma for three diﬀerent
radius distances (0, 3 and 7) around the pixel. In the detection of iris stage
the information about the sclera, gained from the previous step, is used in
another feature set deﬁned by the author. The feature set for this stage is
an 18-dimension feature set with information about the pixels location, the
mean and standard deviation of the pixels saturation and blue chroma for
three diﬀerent radius distances (0, 3 and 7) around the pixel. The feature
set also contains information about sclera proportion in the directions north,
south, east and west from the pixel. All the feature sets created for this
stage are sent to a neural network, which will classify pixels as iris region.
After the iris detection is done the iris shape parameterization step will be
performed. This step is used to reﬁne the limbic boundary and to ﬁnd the
pupillary boundary. Based on the iris region found in the previous steps
an iris center is roughly located.
This center is then used as a reference
point from which the iris region is turned into a polar coordinate system. A
polynomial regression is performed. The polynomials gained is subsequently
remapped into the Cartesian space to yield the parameterized limbic and
pupillary boundaries. The author has tested the segmentation method on
four databases (UBIRIS v2, FRGC, FERET, ICE(2006)) and against 3 other
segmentation methods (Tan et al. [2], Integro diﬀerential [37], Fourier active
contours [38]). The author reports that the proposed method is a lot faster
than any of the 3 other segmentation methods and it has about the same
low classiﬁcation error on iris pixels as “Tan et al.” on the visible wavelength
databases (UBIRIS v2, FRGC, FERET) where the other two segmentation
methods got a higher classiﬁcation error. On the NIR database (ICE(2006))
the proposed method got a little bit higher classiﬁcation error than the three
others, but not much higher.
Pundlik et al.
[4] propose a segmentation method using graph cuts.
The method proposed by the authors is based on graph cuts and uses
texture measurements to distinguish between eyelash-regions and non-eyelash
regions.
To detect eyelash-regions based on texture the authors propose
to use two measures to create a texture map; a threshold hessian measure
and an intensity gradient magnitude measurement. In the next step they
use grey-scale intensity histograms to try and further segment the image.
Based on the histograms they locate the pupil. When the pupil is segmented
they ﬁnd the approximate pupil center. Based on the pupil center they use
a starburst algorithm to try to ﬁnd the limbic and pupillary boundaries.
Once that is done they model the diﬀerent regions using a Gaussian mixture
13
==PAGE:25==
model. Based on that they generate new histograms to try and distinguish
between background and iris regions. They use a multiway-cut algorithm
while considering the image as a weighted graph. In the end they perform
an ellipse ﬁtting based on the limbic and pupillary boundaries found earlier.
The authors tested their proposed segmentation method on two databases
(WVU Non-ideal and WVU Oﬀaxis) both databases contain images taken
with NIR-light.
They used two other segmentation methods to compare
with their method (Masek’s implementation of Daugman’s algorithm and an
edge-based segmentation method which the authors developed). They report
that on both the WVU databases their method gains the lowest segmentation
error rate. On the WVU Non-ideal the proposed method gets a segmentation
error of 15% and on the WVU Oﬀ-axis database the proposed method gets
6%.
Zuo and Schmid [5] propose a segmentation method to be used on NIR
images. They have created this method to be able to work on images which
may have poor quality. The ﬁrst step in the proposed segmentation method
is inpainting of specular reﬂections. Firstly a potential pupil region is found
based on a threshold value which might contain holes. These holes are closed
and a pupil mask is obtained. Two threshold values are determined (one for
iris and one for pupil). These threshold values are used to detect possible
specular reﬂections in the image and the possible specular reﬂection areas
are dilated to obtain a specular reﬂection mask.
The intersection of the
pupil mask and the specular reﬂection mask shows the areas needed to be
inpainted inside the pupil. The specular reﬂections in the pupil are then
inpainted and the intensity values are adjusted which results in a new image
with an inpainted pupil. By using the thresholds again on the inpainted pupil
image all possible specular reﬂections outside the pupil are found dilated
and in the end inpainted.
Denoising is performed using a 2-D adaptive
Wiener ﬁlter where the mean and variance are locally estimated for each
pixel in the original image. The second step is to segment the pupil. In
this step the authors propose to perform contrast stretching to make the
boundaries clearer.
They also propose to smooth the image by using a
median ﬁlter. They further apply a circular Hough transform to ﬁnd possible
pupil candidates in the image and the optimal pupil is chosen based on the
intensity of the possible pupil candidates and the location in the image. A
5x5 neighbourhood is then created around the optimal pupil area and the
minimum and maximum intensity values are found in the neighbourhood.
The minimum and maximum intensity values are reﬁned based on the rough
pupil shape and the gradient information.
The rough pupil boundary is
subsequently evaluated based on its size, the average gradient value and the
diﬀerence to the original shape. They then perform a reﬁnement of the pupil
14
==PAGE:26==
boundary by trying to remove spike-regions caused by eyelashes. They ﬁnd
the spike-regions by looking at the distance from the center to the boundary
of the pupil area and by looking at the angular orientation of a spike. Some
eyelashes may cross the entire pupil area, which are removed through erosion
and dilation.
Ellipse ﬁtting is performed to get a more precise pupillary
boundary. The ﬁtted ellipse is evaluated based on the diﬀerence between the
boundary before and after ellipse ﬁtting and the amount of occlusion. After
that the segmentation of the iris begins. The ﬁrst step which is performed is
a translation so that the center of the pupil is also the center of the image.
The authors use a method similar to the integrodiﬀerential operator to ﬁnd
the limbic boundary and they also perform ellipse ﬁtting.
The authors
also suggest that normalizing the gradient on the limbic boundary before
ellipse ﬁtting may help making it more precise. Further they try to detect
how much of the iris texture is occluded. This is done by performing an
illumination compensation technique to brighten shadowed areas and an
edge detection is performed to locate eyelashes.
Lastly they unwrap the
iris by using a technique similar to the one created by Camus and Wilde.
The proposed segmentation method is evaluated by visual inspection of the
limbic and pupillary boundaries. The segmentation method is used on four
databases (CASIA III INT, ICE, WVU and WVU-OA). The performance of
the proposed method is compared against two segmentation methods, one by
Masek and one by Camus and Wilde. The authors report that the success
rate of their method is a good deal better than the other two segmentation
methods on all four databases.
Tan and Kumar [6] suggest a segmentation method for less constrained
images acquired at a distance. The authors use AdaBoost face and eye-pair
detection to locate the face and eyes in an image. Once they detect an eye,
they perform a preprocessing step where they use the Retinex algorithm to
improve the image quality and a Gaussian ﬁlter to smooth the image. As
a part of the preprocessing they also perform reﬂection removal. After the
preprocessing step they use a random walker algorithm to classify each pixel
as either iris or non-iris pixel. The next step in the proposed segmentation
method is to estimate the iris center. This is done by removing pixel columns
found by the random walker algorithm that are not actual iris areas but has
been marked as possible iris.
This is done by looking at the heights of
columns within possible iris regions found by the random walker algorithm.
If the heights are not within a calculated mean value the column is discarded
as a possible iris part. The center of the remaining region is calculated. After
this is done the image is smoothed. A canny edge detector will be used on the
smoothed image to ﬁnd the limbic and pupillary boundaries. The authors
subsequently perform boundary reﬁnement by deﬁning two circular regions
15
==PAGE:27==
on the limbic and pupillary boundary.
To make sure that occlusions are
not used to aﬀect the reﬁnement only the lower half of circular region (i.e.
a half-ring) on the pupillary boundary is used. They calculate statistical
information based on the half-ring to create an adaptive threshold.
The
threshold is used on the circular region on the limbic boundary to ﬁgure
out which parts are within the limbic boundary and which parts are not
(eyelashes, eyelid). The same information used for creating the circular region
on the limbic boundary is used for creating a region for the upper and lower
eyelid. The edge points of an eyelid (either upper or lower) are extracted
based on the intersection of the eyelid region and the iris boundary. Three
eyelid models are created for each eyelid region as second degree polynomials.
Based on the models some edge points are removed as invalid points and the
rest are used to create a second degree polynomial curve to describe the
eyelid. The last step in the segmentation method is to detect eyelashes and
shadows. This is done by dividing the iris into two regions, an upper region
called ”ES region” and a lower region called ”IR region”. Two thresholds are
calculated based on the mean and deviation of the intensity in the IR region,
which is used to detect pixels as shadows or eyelashes in the ES region. The
authors tested their iris segmentation method on three databases UBIRIS
v2, FRGC and CASIA v4. The two ﬁrst databases contain images acquired
in the visible wavelength and the last contains images acquired in NIR light.
The authors used ground truth information to ﬁgure out the segmentation
error of their proposed solution. They report that their segmentation method
had a segmentation error of 1.72% on the UBIRIS v2 database, 1.76% on the
FRGC database and 0.81% on the CASIA v4 database.
Further, Tan and Kumar [7] propose a segmentation method for images
acquired at a distance for both visible wavelength and NIR images. The ﬁrst
thing the authors propose to do is to use AdaBoost face and eye-pair detection
to ﬁnd the face of a person in an image and the two eyes of the person. They
use a single scale retinex algorithm to improve the colour consistency on the
eye images gained through the face and eye-pair detection. The authors have
deﬁned a 6 dimensional feature vector to hold information about the location
of a pixel, colour channel information and Zernike Moments at three diﬀerent
radii. A 6 dimensional feature vector is created for each pixel. The authors
use two trained classiﬁers, feed forward neural network and SVM, to label
pixels as either iris or non-iris. Both classiﬁers use the 6 dimensional feature
vector to classify a pixel. Based on the classiﬁcation of pixels an edgemap
will be generated and circle ﬁtting is done. The center of the mass inside
the ﬁtted circle is subsequently calculated and is used as an estimated center
for the iris, the radius is also found. The authors then use the estimated
iris center, radius and a Canny edge detector to ﬁnd possible edge points for
16
==PAGE:28==
the eyelid. The edge points are used to create a second degree polynomial
curve for each eyelid. The next step in the proposed segmentation method
is to remove reﬂections which is done in the following way: Firstly they ﬁnd
possible reﬂection regions by looking at the top 20% brightest intensities. The
regions found, which are not within the iris are discarded. The remaining
regions are used to calculate a threshold. The threshold is used to mark
pixels in a neighbourhood close to the reﬂection areas as reﬂection points.
The authors remove eyelashes and shadows from the iris, by dividing the iris
into two regions; ES and IR. A histogram is created for each region and based
on those histograms a threshold is deﬁned to remove eyelashes and shadows
in the ES region of the iris. The last step in the proposed segmentation
method is to ﬁnd the pupil within the iris region. This is done by using
the retinex algorithm to improve the region and then by using the grey level
distribution to create a threshold that can be used to ﬁnd a possible pupil
region within the iris region. The authors tested the proposed segmentation
method on three databases; UBIRIS v2, FRGC and CASIA v4-distance. The
authors report that the best segmentation error for the databases UBIRIS v2
and FRGC is received by using feed forward neural network as a classiﬁer.
Respectively they get 1.90% and 1.84%. On the CASIA v4-distance database
they get the best segmentation error by using the SVM classiﬁer where they
get 1.09%.
Du et al. [8] propose an iris segmentation method for videos. The authors
start by downsampling the images in the video depending on the size of the
images. After that they use a high-pass ﬁlter to to detect specular reﬂections.
If no specular reﬂections could be found then the image would be discarded.
After that the authors cluster pixels into regions based on their intensity and
the intensity of their neighbours. The clustering helps to quickly ﬁnd a rough
pupil area. The authors designed this step in a way so that if there were an
image before the current one then that information would be used to help
quickly cluster the current image. Based on the clustering and the high-pass
ﬁlter information they choose a possible pupil area which is the darkest with
a specular reﬂection inside it or very close to the area. If the previous image
had a valid pupil boundary, that information is then used to ﬁnd the pupil
boundary in the current image. If the previous image did not have a valid
pupil boundary then an edge map will be created for the entire current image.
If no boundary can be found for the pupil in either case the current image
will be discarded. The authors make use of a direct least squares ellipse
ﬁtting method.
The rough pupil boundary found in the previous step is
used by taking some edge points and using the above mentioned method
to ﬁt an ellipse to the pupil boundary which yields a more precise pupil
boundary. To ﬁnd points for the limbic boundary to be used with the direct
17
==PAGE:29==
least squares ellipse ﬁtting method they use an angular derivative. Based on
the assumption that shadows and eyelashes have stronger edges and either
higher or lower intensity than the iris features the authors have chosen to
use a window based variance and intensity algorithm to detect noise in the
iris. They use thresholds based in information about the pupil and the iris.
Two second degree polynomials are also used to model the eyelids, one as
a convex for the top eyelid and one as a concave for the bottom eyelid.
The authors have tested their proposed segmentation method on an in-house
database containing six videos. The authors report that overall they can
segment 88.2% of all images in the six videos. The authors have also made
their own evaluation method which they used on the 88.2% segmented images
and they report that based on their evaluation method they get an average
segmentation score of 0.9008 with a standard deviation of 0.0652.
Roy et al. [9] start their proposed segmentation method by removing
specular reﬂections in the pupil. This is done by ﬁnding the inverse intensity
value of a pixel which makes the specular reﬂections show as dark spots.
These dark spots are inpainted by using a four pixel connectivity to look at
the background intensity. The authors inverse the intensity of all the pixels
to gain the actual image and then they use a Gaussian ﬁlter to smooth the
image. The next step in the proposed segmentation method is to estimate
the pupilary and limbic boundaries. This is done by using a Direct least
squares based ellipse ﬁtting. Before they perform the actual ellipse ﬁtting
they use a morphological operation to minimize the eﬀect of eyelashes on the
ellipse ﬁtting process. The authors reﬁne the boundaries by using a level set
with a variational formulation. In short they try to change the curve of the
estimated boundaries by pushing or pulling it towards the actual boundary
by looking at a narrow band along the estimated boundaries. The last step in
the proposed segmentation method is to detect eyelashes which the authors
do in two ways. To detect single eyelashes they use a 1D Gabor ﬁlter and
to detect multiple eyelashes i.e. a cluster of eyelashes they use a window
based method where they look at the variance intensity in the window and
based on a threshold the window is either deﬁned as eyelash or not. The
authors have tested their proposed segmentation method on three diﬀerent
databases (ICE 2005, WVU Unideal and UBIRIS v1). They have also created
a fourth database which is just a combination of the three other databases.
They report that they have a total of 6163 images and that their proposed
segmentation method could successfully segment 6028 of those images. The
authors do not report any segmentation error to show the performance of the
segmentation method. Instead the authors chose to use Daubechies Wavelet
Transform as a feature extractor on their own segmentation method and on
four other segmentation methods to compare the recognition rate and the
18
==PAGE:30==
equal error rate. They report that on the ICE 2005 and UBIRIS v1 their
proposed method has the second best recognition rate (98.31% and 97.50%
respectively, GAR at 0.001% FAR). On the WVU database and the combined
database they report that their method got the best recognition rate with
respectively 96.43% and 97.60%. They also report that their method gets
the best equal error rate on all four databases (ICE 2005 = 0.31%, WVU
Unideal = 0.73%, UBIRIS v1 = 0.50% and the combine database got 0.49%).
Uhl and Wild [10] propose a segmentation method which should be usable
for both NIR and visible wavelength images. Their proposed segmentation
method consists of three parts. The ﬁrst part is image enhancement which
is done by selecting all pixels whose intensity is the highest 15%. All the
pixels selected are dilated over two iterations with a structuring element. All
regions which contain more than 2000 pixels are discarded and the remaining
areas are inpainted using the Navier-Stokes method. The second part in the
proposed method is to ﬁnd a center point for the iris. First they calculate
the gradient magnitude and orientation for the image. They then use an
adaptive Hough transform with a 10x10 accumulator grid with 0.5 pixel
precision threshold. By starting with some candidate points found by doing
the gradient magnitude and orientation calculations the adaptive Hough
transform will iteratively look for the most promising candidate point as the
iris center. The third and ﬁnal part of the proposed segmentation method
is the detection of the pupillary and limbic boundaries.
First they polar
unwrap the image based on the iris center found in the second part. The
unwrapped image will be convolved over two iterations by using a set of
oriented Gabor kernels. A k-means clustering algorithm is then used to ﬁnd
the three best vertical ﬁlter responses to be used for clustering of r-values.
The bottom and top 20% distances from the center are evaluated and missing
values are linearly interpolated based on their neighbours. A Fourier series
is used for contour ﬁtting. The steps performed in the third part is done
twice because the result from the ﬁrst iteration can be used with a pushing
and pulling method to create a new and more precise iris center which in
turn can be used to get more precise pupillary and limbic boundaries in the
second iteration. The authors have tested their segmentation method on four
datasets: CASIA-I (A left-eye set interval subset of CASIA v4), CASIA-L
(A left-eye set Lamp subset of CASIA v4), UBIRIS (A subset of UBIRIS v1)
and ND (A subset of ND-IRIS-0405). They compare their results against
an OSIRIS segmentation method and a segmentation method called Pcode.
They do not report any segmentation classiﬁcation error, instead they use a
dyadic wavelet transform feature extractor on the segmentation result from
all three segmentation methods and compare the equal error rates.
The
authors report the following results: On CASIA-I the Pcode got an equal
19
==PAGE:31==
error rate of 0.74%, OSIRIS got 16.40% and their own method got 8.07%. On
CASIA-L Pcode got 28.77%, OSIRIS got 14.89% and the proposed method
got 12.90%. For the ND dataset Pcode got 22.01%, OSIRIS got 15.45% and
the authors segmentation method got 19.04%. On the UBIRIS dataset Pcode
got 39.65%, OSIRIS got 33.51% and the proposed method got 34.56%.
Frucci et al. [11] propose a segmentation method where they ﬁrst apply
a median ﬁlter to smooth the image and a Sobel ﬁlter to get gradient
information about the image. The authors use the gradient information with
a watershed transform to divide the image into small regions. Subsequently
they calculate the mean colour intensity for each of the regions. Further they
ﬁnd the Euclidean distance for a region and all of the neighbouring region and
merge the two with the smallest distance if it is below a threshold. Once no
more regions can be merged they calculate the mean colour intensity of all the
regions and a quantized image is received. Based on the mean colour of each
region they compute the mean white and black colour distance over all the
regions and use that to ﬁnd a threshold. The threshold is used to binarize all
of the regions from the merged watershed transform. The authors then reﬁne
the binarized image by switching a region from black to white or the other
way around based on a colour distance criteria. If the colour distance criteria
is not met for a region it is not switched. The authors use a morphological
operator on the ﬁnal binarized image to generate an edge map. Further the
authors use a curvature analysis to ﬁnd possible edge points for the iris and
Taubin’s algorithm is then used to create circles based on the edge points
and the circle with the best rank is chosen as the limbic boundary. The
authors evaluated their suggested iris segmentation method on the UBIRIS
v1 session 2 dataset and they compare their method against a segmentation
method by Wilde and a segmentation method called ISIS. They evaluate
the performance by looking at the iris center found by the methods and the
radius. They have manually found the center and radius and used that as
ground truth to see the diﬀerence. The authors report that their method
performs better than Wildes segmentation method and has a comparable
performance to the ISIS [48] segmentation method.
Based on the articles reviewed in this chapter is is clear that there have
been a lot of research done on the area of segmentation since there are
many diﬀerent ways to perform segmentation. The results of the articles
also show that many of the segmentation methods can be applied almost
equally successfully on both NIR and visible wavelength images.

Feature extraction
The iris texture of a person is made up of crypts, furrows, ridges etc. which
create a unique pattern in the iris.
The purpose of feature extraction is
therefore to analyse the iris pattern of a person to ﬁnd the unique information
of that iris. The information found should be created in a way so that it is
possible to verify whether two sets of information are from the same source
or not.
Many articles mentioned in this chapter reports their recognition
performance by using decidability index (d′). An explanation how to ﬁnd
the decidability index can be found in chapter 4.2.

Daugman [1] suggests to use 2D Gabor ﬁlters to extract feature
information about a preprocessed iris texture. Based on a 2D Gabor wavelet
family Daugman devised a new wavelet family that would be able to work
with dilation and rotational changes. These wavelets would be used in the
2D Gabor ﬁlters to construct a code based on the iris texture of a person.
21
==PAGE:33==
The code would be on a binary form and Daugman therefore suggests that
the use of Hamming distances on two diﬀerent codes could be used to ﬁgure
out how diﬀerent the two codes are.
Over time a lot of proposed feature extraction methods using wavelet
ﬁlters have been made. For instance Masek [49] proposed to use 1D Gabor
ﬁlters instead of 2D Gabor ﬁlters like Daugman and Krichen et al.
[50]
proposed to use packets of Gabor wavelets instead of using wavelets at
diﬀerent scales.
Szewczyk et al. [12] propose to use a discrete wavelet transform as a
feature extractor on a normalized iris template. The authors report that
they tested an evaluated many possible wavelets which could be used in the
discrete wavelet transform. They chose to create a discrete wavelet transform
by using a reverse bi-orthogonal wavelet with a scale of 3 and a shift of 1.
As a comparator the authors decided to use the Hamming distance. In their
evaluation of all the diﬀerent wavelets they used a small subset of images
from the NICE.II training set. They report that the chosen wavelet got an
equal error rate of 15.68% and the wavelet got a decidability value of 2.1185.
The authors also submitted their proposed feature extraction method to the
NICE.II competition and came in eighth place. The proposed method got a
decidability value of 1.0931 in the NICE.II competition.
Tan and Kumar [13] propose a feature extraction method to be used
for both NIR and visible wavelength images acquired at a distance. The
proposed method consists of two feature extraction methods.
The ﬁrst
feature extraction method starts by using an overlapping block strategy to
create a remapped and larger version of the normalized iris texture. The

2.5
Comparators
To be able to make a decision on whether the information or features
extracted from two irises came from the same source a comparator is needed.
The result of a comparator will usually be a score indicating the similarity or
dissimilarity of two sets of features. Based on the result of the comparator
is should then be possible to make a decision.
One of the most used comparators for matching is the fractional Hamming
distance, which was proposed as a comparator by Daugman in [1].
This
comparator method compares two binary codes/vectors by XOR’ing them.
The total number of 1’s in the result is found and divided by the total number
of bits in the codes/vectors. The two codes/vectors of course needs to be of
the same length.
In the end a number between 0 and 1 is found which
indicates the dissimilarity. The closer the number is to 1 the more diﬀerent
the two codes/vectors were and the closer the number is to 0 the bigger
the similarity of the two codes. Usually there will be some kind of noise
or occlusion of an iris so most iris systems will generate a binary mask to
indicate which pixels are noise or occlusion. The masks belonging to the iris
codes can be taken into account when using the Hamming distance. This is
done by using the bitwise operator AND to AND the XOR’ed result with
both masks. This makes sure that the bits in the result which is noise or
occlusion in either iris is not taken into consideration as mentioned in the
article [27]. The equation for the Hamming distance with masks can be seen
in equation 2:

2.6
Fusion and normalization
When using information from diﬀerent feature extractors or modalities in
biometric systems, the scores will most likely not have the same statistical
distribution and/or numerical score range.
Therefore it is necessary to
normalize the scores in a way where they can be fused together. The article
[31] discuss the performance of several score normalization techniques:
• Min-Max: This is the simplest normalization technique used and it
can be used to change the scores of a comparison into the range of
[0,1]. In the article it is mentioned that this technique will retain the
statistical distribution of the scores but there will be a diﬀerence in
scale. It is also mentioned that the technique is not very robust since
it is aﬀected by outlying scores in a score set.
• Decimal scaling: The decimal scaling normalization technique can be
used when score ranges of several comparison types are on a logarithmic
scale. However, if the score ranges are not on a logarithmic scale the
31
==PAGE:43==
technique is not usable. This normalization technique is also aﬀected
by outliers in a score set and it is not very robust.
• Z-score: This is the most commonly used technique for normalizing
scores. This technique uses the arithmetic mean and standard deviation
of a score set to normalize scores. The technique is not very robust
since the results of mean and standard deviation are highly aﬀected
by sets with outliers.
The technique is very eﬃcient on score sets
with a Gaussian distribution. The technique can be used on score sets
that are not a Gaussian distribution but the technique is then less
eﬀective in ﬁnding the optimal values for mean and deviation. The
scores from diﬀerent comparison types might not be normalized to the
same numerical range.
• Median and median absolute deviation: This technique is very
robust since it uses median values and the absolute distance to median
values and they are not aﬀected by outliers in a score set. However,
this technique is even less eﬃcient than the Z-score technique when the
score set is not Gaussian distributed.
• Double sigmoid:
This technique uses two sigmoid functions to
normalize scores within a range of [0,1] just like the Min-max
normalization technique.
The scheme also makes use of a reference
point and two edge points for normalization. To make sure that the
technique is eﬃcient some careful tuning is required. Based on a score
set the reference point is usually chosen as the center of the overlapping
region of the impostor and genuine distributions of a score set. The edge
points should then be chosen on the right and left side of the reference
point so that there is an equal overlap in the impostor and genuine
distributions. When the edge points are chosen/found the technique
is more robust if outlying values in the score set are not taken into
account.
• Tanh-estimators: This is a normalization technique which is both,
robust and eﬃcient. It makes use of Hampel estimators and an inﬂuence
function to reduce the eﬀect of outliers in a score set.
Based on
the results of the inﬂuence function an arithmetic mean and standard
deviation is calculated. The technique also uses a tanh function and a
constant value to normalize the deviation of scores.
• Biweight estimators: This technique is only usable for score sets
which have a Gaussian distribution.
The technique is robust and
32
==PAGE:44==
eﬃcient. To use this technique some biweight estimators have to be
calculated. To start the calculations some estimations have to be made
and then the estimators will be calculated more precisely based on the
initial estimate and an iterative approach on a training score set.
After normalization has been performed the comparison scores from two
or more feature extractors then need to be fused in a way so that a decision
can be made based on all of the results. In [31] they also mention several
ways of fusing scores. One way to fuse the results is to ﬁnd the posterior
probabilities of the comparison scores. Based on the posterior probabilities
several rules can then be used to assign the query image to a class. The rules
using posterior probabilities are the following:
• Product rule: In this rule the product of the posterior probabilities
are calculated and used to assign the query image to a class.
• Sum rule: This rule is just like the product rule except it calculates
the sum instead of the product.
• Max rule: This rule ﬁnds an approximated mean of all the posterior
probabilities based on the maximum posterior probability value of all
the values.
• Min rule: In this rule the product of the posterior probabilities are
bounded based on the minimum value.
Another way is proposed by Jain et al. [31] where non-parametric density
estimation is done and a likelihood ratio test is used to make the decision.
The authors also mention an article in which Bayesian statistics is used to
ﬁnd an estimated accuracy of the diﬀerent comparisons of diﬀerent feature
types. The authors also mention that a weighted sum can be used to make a
total decision. In the weighted sum a weight is created for each comparison
score type such that the total weights equal 1. The sum of the scores and
their corresponding weights are then found to be the total score. The authors
also mention that they use a simple min-method and a simple max-method
to ﬁnd the ﬁnal scores. Basically for the simple min and max methods the
lowest or highest value respectively are used as the total score.
Based on the articles reviewed throughout the literature research chapter
the following has become apparent:
• Many proposed segmentation algorithms are robust and have a
comparable performance on both NIR and visible wavelength images.

==PAGE:45==
• Even with robust segmentation methods the recognition performance
in visible wavelength is poor compared to iris recognition systems using
NIR images.
• Fusion of either multiple information sources or multiple algorithms
can improve performance.
• Fusion of diﬀerent feature types on the iris in the visible wavelength
has not been explored.
Based on this the focus of the project will therefore be to look into the
possibility of fusing diﬀerent feature extractor types to ﬁgure out if it is a
possible way to improve the performance of iris recognition systems.

3.1.1
Conventional features
The USIT v2 Toolkit contains several conventional feature extractor types,
such as LG, QSW, DCT, KO and CR, which has been used for testing and
evaluation in this project. Two of the best performing feature extractors
will be explained in this section. Research obtained from the remaining are
summarized in Appendix A.1.
• LG [28]: The LG feature extractor is a 1D Log-Gabor feature extractor.
It works by, when given a normalized iris texture as an input image,
dividing the input image into 10 bands of the size 512x5 pixels. This is
done from the top of the image so the bottom 14 pixels will not be used.
The grey-scale value is then averaged within a 1x5 pixel region and then
normalized to get a 1D signal for each band. A complex Log-Gabor
ﬁlter is then used to perform a convolution on the 1D signals which
results in a set of complex values. The phase angle from those complex
values are then discretized to get two bits. In the end an image will be
returned by the feature extractor containing the binary result. With
this feature extractor it is possible to also give a normalized binary
37
==PAGE:49==
mask for the iris texture so that occlusion and noise can be masked
out. An example of the output code from the LG feature extractor can
be seen in ﬁgure 8a.
• QSW [28]: The ﬁrst part of the QSW feature extractor is much like
the LG where, given a normalized iris texture, it will be divided into
10 bands and a grey-scale averaging and normalization will be done
in the same way. A quadratic spline wavelet transform is then used
on each of the 10 1D signals and two predeﬁned sub-bands from the
transform of each 1D signal is used and local minima and maxima in
the sub-bands ﬁlter responses are located and encoded as either 1 or 0
depending on whether they are ascending or descending. The result of
the feature extractor will produce a binary code based on the binary
encoding of the local minima and maxima. Just like LG it is possible
to add a normalized mask to mask out noise in the iris. An example of
the output can be seen in ﬁgure 8b.

3.1.2
Key point features
The USIT v2 Toolkit also contains two feature extractors which extract
key point features. Both of these feature extractors have been tested and
evaluated in this project and they are explained below:
• SIFT [28]: The ﬁrst thing to do in this type of feature extractor is
to ﬁnd the key points. This is done by convolving Gaussian ﬁlters of
diﬀerent scales with the normalized iris texture image received as input.
This results in a set of smoothed images and the diﬀerences between
the set of images is then found yielding in a set of diﬀerence images.
Key points are then selected as local maxima and minima. The maxima
and minima points are found by comparing each pixel in an diﬀerence
image against their eight neighbours and comparing that set of pixels
against the corresponding pixels in the corresponding diﬀerence images
created with a diﬀerent scale.
A contrast threshold of 0.04 and an
38
==PAGE:50==
edge threshold of 10 is then used to remove noisy key points.
The
feature extractor then creates a descriptor for each key point. A key
point descriptor is created by forming a 16x16 window around the key
point. The window is then split into cells of size 4x4, within each cell
the dominant gradient orientation are found. A histogram is created
based on the orientation gradient information for each cell where the
histogram has 8 bins. The total descriptor will be the cumulation of
all bins in all histograms so the key point descriptor will consist of
128 bins. The result of the feature extractor is then a set of key point
descriptors. See ﬁgure 9 for an example of the result from the SIFT
feature extractor.
• SURF [30]: The SURF feature extractor works by using a Hessian
matrix with two types of box ﬁlters. The determinant of the Hessian
matrix is found for each pixel in the image and for each pixel several
Hessian matrices are used where the size of the box ﬁlters diﬀer which
correspond to diﬀerent scales.
The key points are then selected by
the found maxima points.
This is done by using a non-maximum
suppression which is performed on a 3x3x3 neighbourhood. After all
of the key points have been found a descriptor is created for each key
point. The ﬁrst thing which is done is using Haar wavelets to get ﬁlter
responses in the horizontal and vertical direction. This is done within
a circular region of the key point. The Haar wavelets are performed
within a window which slides across the circular region. Based on the
ﬁlter responses an orientation vector is found to be the orientation of the
key point. A square region is then created around the key point where
the key point orientation is used to orient the square. The square is then
split into smaller regions of size 4x4. The Haar wavelet ﬁlter responses
are then found for each subregion. For each sub region a vector of size
four is created containing the sum of the horizontal ﬁlter responses,
the sum of vertical ﬁlter responses, the sum of absolute values from the
horizontal and vertical ﬁlter responses. The total size of the feature
descriptor set for each key point will then be 64 bins.

iris in ﬁgure 7d.

3.1.3
General image descriptors
This section explains how the two feature extractors of the type general
image descriptors work. LBP is from the USIT v2 Toolkit and BSIF has
been provided.
• LBP [29]: This feature extractor works by splitting the image into
cells of size 16x16 pixels. A 9x9 multi-block LBP operator is then used
on all the cells. The multi-block LBP operator is made up of 9 blocks
where each block contain 9 pixels. The operator works by calculating
an average grey-scale value for each of the 9 blocks. A binary code is
then created by using the value of the center block in the multi-block
LBP operator as a threshold value. The binary value of the other blocks
will be 1 if their value is equal to or greater than the value of the center
block. Otherwise a block will get the binary value 0. The binary codes
generated over all the cells will then be stored in a histogram. The
procedure is also performed on two shifted textures where the texture
has been shifted by +/- 8. Figure 10a shows an example of the result
of running LBP on a normalized iris.
• BSIF: Based on a normalized iris texture image this feature extractor
will perform feature extraction on the regular texture image and two
shifted textures just like LBP. Each of the three textures will then be
split into cells of size 32x32. Within each cell twelve pre-trained ﬁlters
of size 15x15 will slide across the cell horizontally and vertically to
create a ﬁlter response for each of the pixels. This will create twelve
ﬁlter responses for each pixel and they will be binarized so that a
positive ﬁlter response will equal the bit value 1 and a negative ﬁlter
response will equal the bit value of 0. The binarized result will then
be turned into a corresponding integer value. Based on all the integer
values within a cell a histogram will be generated. See ﬁgure 10b for
an example of running BSIF on a normalized iris texture.

• Mean colour version: Based on the binary mask image this feature

works by creating three histograms, one for red values, one for green
values and one for blue values. All three histograms consist of 256 bins
to hold values from 0 to 255. The feature extraction will go through
all the pixels marked as iris by the binary mask image and for each
pixel it will look at the red, green and blue values and increase the
corresponding bin count for the corresponding colour histogram. After
the histograms has been created they will be normalized based on the
amount of iris pixels in the image.

pairs. The score of two irises is then found by taking the amount of

3.3
Score normalization
For this project two normalization techniques have been used. The reason
for using normalization is because the diﬀerent feature extractors and
comparators will give diﬀerent types of scores. So to be able to fuse the results
normalization has to be performed. In this project the two normalization
techniques used to fuse scores is the Z-normalization and Tanh-normalization.
The reason for using two diﬀerent normalization techniques to fuse scores is
to see if the fusion performance and results will be diﬀerent. Both of the
normalization techniques are explained below. For both Z-normalization and
Tanh-normalization some values need to be calculated to be able to normalize
a score. In this project two databases are used (MobBIO and UBIRIS v2).
So when normalization with either technique is performed, a set of scores

Since many diﬀerent types of feature extractors and their comparators are

project this is done by a weighted sum-rule. Once all of the scores have been

weights are used for each feature extracted score type. The point of this is to
see if the performance of the combination can be improved by changing the

Performance evaluation
To evaluate the recognition performances tested in this project FMR/FNMR,
equal error rate (EER) and decidability index (d’) has been used. FMR (False
Match Rate) shows the probability rate of having an impostor comparison
accepted and FNMR (False Non-Match Rate) shows the probability rate
of having a genuine comparison rejected. Both FMR and FNMR are used
to create ROC (Receiver Operating Characteristics) curves. A ROC curve
shows the trade-oﬀbetween the probability of getting a genuine comparison
accepted and the probability of getting an impostor accepted. EER is the
point at which FMR and FNMR is the same. The decidability index has been
calculated by using equation 10 which was used in the NICE.II competition
[36].
d′ =
|avg(DI) −avg(DE)|
p
0.5 ∗(std(DI)2 + std(DE)2)
(10)
Where DI and DE are the set of genuine and impostor dissimilarity scores,
respectively. avg() denote the average value of a set and std() denote the
standard deviation of a set.
It should be noted that EER has been used as the primary source for
evaluating the performances. As mentioned earlier in the report two types
of normalization techniques have been used during the project. It should
also be noted that Z-normalization has been the default normalization and
unless anything is stated from this point on the scores have been normalized
50
==PAGE:62==
using Z-normalization. Tanh-normalization has been used to verify that the
possible score changes found by fusing also happen in some degree with
another normalization technique. This will be shown later in this chapter.

and QSW in general performed better than the others.
Therefore it was
decided to focus the further fusion experiments by only using LG and QSW
as the feature extractors for the conventional feature extraction type.

5
Conclusion
For this project the deﬁned framework was successfully implemented and
used to test and evaluate the performance of several feature extractors and
how diﬀerent fusions aﬀected the recognition rate. As mentioned the ﬁrst
experiments focused on the performance of the single feature extractors
which interestingly showed that LBP performed better on both the MobBIO
and UBIRIS v2 datasets compared to the conventional, key point and soft
biometric feature extractors. The experiments also showed that both SIFT
and SURF had comparable performances to the more conventional feature
extractors. This means that conventional feature extractors might not always
be the best type to use for iris recognition systems in visible wavelength.
With the experiments on fusion of two feature extractors it was shown
that feature extractors of diﬀerent types actually can complement each other
and improve the performance of a recognition system compared to either
using only a single feature extractor or two of the same type.
Through the experiments it has also been shown that using several
diﬀerent feature types for a recognition system on visible wavelength images
can improve the recognition rate signiﬁcantly.
The total improvement in
performance can be seen in table 12.
The experiments also showed that blurring caused by motion or bad focus,
reﬂections and occlusions can have a big impact on reducing the quality of
an image and thereby the recognition performance. Eye images from the
same subject but with diﬀerent angles also tend to have a lower recognition
rate. By visual inspection it was also noticed that the size of the iris had
some inﬂuence because once an iris was normalized/unrolled the details were
generally clearer in the UBIRIS v2 database compared to the ones in the
MobBIO database. This happens because a smaller sized iris will be stretched
more than a larger iris and the iris texture then tend to be blurred in the
normalized iris texture which of course results in a reduced image quality
and also a reduced recognition performance.
This proves that feature type fusion using diﬀerent types can help improve
the performance of an iris recognition system. However, these results are
still not as good as on a iris recognition system using NIR images. Although
the technology for mobile devices becomes better and better every year so
it might not be that long before the cameras used in a mobile device will
be able to capture images of high enough quality so that the recognition
performance could be comparable to a NIR iris recognition system.
As the additional experiments showed it is however possible to further
improve a recognition system by fusing both eyes or using periocular. These
multi-source fusions would most likely be possible to achieve on mobile
