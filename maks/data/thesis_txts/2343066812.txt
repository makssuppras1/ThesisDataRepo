Abstract (English)
Strategic planing is an important element in games of the real-time strategy genre.
The
games are played in continuous time as opposed to turn-based games like chess. Games of the
genre are often described as war simulations; players have to built up a base and an army and
attack and defeat the enemy. The order in which structures and troops are built and trained
is referred to as a build order. Typically most of the strategy of a player is dened by the
build order and it can be important for victory that it is somewhat optimized. Furthermore,
responding correctly to the strategy used by the enemy can be important as well, among
other reasons because some unit types counter others, e.g. if the enemy is making units that
y, anti-air units should be made.
This thesis explores automated generation and optimization of build orders as well as
opponent modeling, which is used for determining the strategy of the enemy. The real-time
strategy game StarCraft: Brood War is used as a case study. A mathematical model that
denes a feasible build order is presented. Simulated Annealing is used to search the space of
feasible build orders for a near-optimal build order, based on one of two build order evaluation
methods presented. Bayesian goal recognition is used to identify the goal of the enemy player,
and an appropriate response is determined based on unit counters.
All of the methods presented are implemented and applied to an already existing StarCraft
bot, UAlbertaBot. The resulting bot uses Simulated Annealing to generate opening build
orders before games, but also to generate new build orders while playing when necessary.
Tests of the Simulated Annealing implementation show that some level of intensication
in the search improves the quality of the build orders found. The performance of the bot
is measured by matching it against a handful of other competitive bots. The bot performs
well both with a proactive aggressive strategy and with a reactive strategy based on goal
recognition. The search time used for nding a new build order, when reacting to the enemy
strategy, does not aect the performance of the bot much.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Planning in Computer Games
Many computer games contains some elements of planning and/or optimization and this is
especially true for the strategy genre. Real-time strategy, or simply RTS, is a genre of games
where the player has to make strategic choices in continuous time, based on a changing
environment.
In other words, the game does not revolve around taking turns like most
strategic board games, instead players can act at all times. This makes acting fast important
because time is valuable, however, acting and reacting according to the situation is important
as well.
RTS games are often referred to as war simulations. Most games of the genre revolve
around gathering resources; building up a base and an army; and then using the army to
defeat the enemy, which is the ultimate goal of the game. Typically the term units is used
to refer to army and workers, which are in most games trained at buildings, some games also
includes upgrades that boost the power of units or buildings. Typically a game plays out
in a two-dimensional battleeld, that is only partially observable, which makes exploration
or scouting important. In some games, players could have to explore to nd resources, or it
could be that the players may or may not initially know the position of their enemy(s).
When playing an RTS game, one has to solve multiple planning problems, some of which
are: what buildings and units to build and in which order, this is typically referred to as a
build order (BO); where to build the buildings; and when and where to attack the opponent.
Most RTS games use a concept called a technology tree or tech tree. The tech tree denes
some requirements between buildings and units, e.g. in order to build some building in the
tree, one rst needs to have built its predecessors.
The simplest way to imagine a build order is as a sequence of instructions, with a number
of constraints e.g. the technology tree and resources.
RTS games can technically be played by more than 2 players, however, the most compet-
itive environment and the focus of this project is 1 vs 1 matches.
This thesis looks at three main problems: how to generate near-optimal build orders; how
to determine the strategy of the enemy; and how to change the build order in the middle of
a game when the enemy strategy demands it. To answer these questions methods from both
Operations Research (OR) and Articial Intelligence (AI) are combined, hence it is assumed
that the reader has some basic knowledge within both elds.
This thesis uses the RTS game StarCraft: Brood War as a case study, hereinafter mostly
referred to simply as StarCraft.

1.2
StarCraft: Brood War
StarCraft is a science ction game, in which the player can choose to play one of three races,
the Protoss, the Terran, and the Zerg. All of the races have completely dierent buildings
and units, and some dierent mechanics specic to that race.
StarCraft along with its expansion pack Brood War are from 1998, however, despite its
old age it still has thousands of active players and writers [teamliquid, 2016d] and most
players of the genre puts it as the best RTS of all time [thetoptens, 2016]. It has been played
competitively for more than a decade and players have reinvented their strategies dozens of
times. One of its greatest selling points has always been its balance between the three races,
historically any matchup between two of the races has had about a 50% winrate, something
that other games of the genre has a hard time achieving. This and the existence of software
like BWAPI [BWAPI-An, 2015], which makes it easy to interact with the game and build AIs
that can play the game, is why StarCraft has been used by many in AI research (see Chapter
2).
This project focuses on planning for one of the three races, the Protoss. This is simply
to limit the amount of basic work and maximize the eort towards theoretical development,
however, everything can rather easily be expanded to the two other races.
1.2.1
Personal knowledge about StarCraft
There exists a lot of common knowledge among StarCraft players about the game, how
humans play it, etc.
This knowledge is normally obtained by playing the game, follow-
ing the professional scene, or by reading articles at StarCraft fora or encyclopedias like
[teamliquid, 2016d] and [teamliquid, 2016c]. To the author's best knowledge there does not
exist formal papers explaining these things. Instead this section will be referenced multiple
times in this thesis, whenever a claim is made about something that is considered common
knowledge by StarCraft players.
One of these things is the concept of unit counters which will be explained here.
Most RTS games including StarCraft gives the players multiple dierent unit types to
choose from; naturally each type has its strengths and weaknesses. A unit type is said to
counter another unit type if it has an advantage against that unit type. Likewise a unit
type is said to be countered by another unit type if it has a disadvantage against that unit
type. Counters in general are commonly known from games like rock-paper-scissors, where
rock counters scissors, etc. However, in RTS games, the counters are often not as eective as
in rock-paper scissors, where they are completely binary. Rather than rock always beating
scissors, maybe one unit of type A beats one unit of type B, however, two units of type B
might be able to beat one unit of type A, in which case we would still say that type A counters
type B. In actuality, it is not the number of units that counts, it might be that A is able to
beat B, but if A is worth 10 times as many resources than B that is to be expected. In order
for a unit to counter another it should be resource eective against that unit, i.e. to some
extend convincingly win a battle where both sides has about the same amount of resources
worth of units.
1.3
Outline of the Report
This report presents a mathematical model that denes a feasible build order for Protoss in
StarCraft, meaning a build order that respects the rules of the game, e.g. the restrictions of
the tech tree. The model could easily be specialized to the other two races or even to another
RTS game.

1.3 Outline of the Report
3
Two ways of evaluating build orders are presented, and an implementation of the meta-
heuristic Simulated Annealing (SA) is used to search for near-optimal build orders.
A complete mathematical model including the evaluation is too complex to solve using
branch-and-bound. Initial tests with GAMS showed that even the simplest of models were
computationally hard to solve and that this method scaled very poorly with increasing prob-
lem complexity (see Section 9.4).
Having near-optimal build orders can make a dierence, since having more units at a given
time or having the units faster can change the outcome of a game.
The problem of planning a build order is in some ways related to the Job Shop Schedul-
ing Problem (JSSP), a well known problem in operations research, in which a number
of jobs (units and upgrades) are to be scheduled on a number of machines (buildings)
[Van Laarhoven et al., 1992]. However, in this variation, the machines (buildings) are part of
the schedule as well. Most variants of JSSP are NP-hard and hence impossible to solve in
polynomial time [Zhang, 2013]. This thesis does not prove whether the build order problem is
NP-complete, however, given its complexity it is very likely that it contains an NP-complete
sub-problem, which would make itself NP-complete.
Bayesian goal recognition, as explained by [Gener and Bonet, 2013], is a form of opponent
modeling.
A version of bayesian goal recognition specialized for RTS games, specically
StarCraft, is presented in this thesis. Some form of opponent modeling is necessary to be
able to identify the strategy of the opponent and react accordingly. In order to determine
the strategy of the enemy rst a set of possible strategies is dened. Then given a set of
observations about the enemy Bayes' rule (6.1) is applied to determine the probability map
of those strategies. The concept of unit counters (see Section 1.2.1) is then used to determine
a good strategy against that of the enemy.
All of the above is implemented in an already existing StarCraft AI, sometimes referred to
as a "bot", in order to test its performance within the game. For this purpose the StarCraft
bot UAlbertaBot [Churchill, 2016e] was modied and used.
When the strategy of the enemy is determined it could be a good idea to have the bot
react to the enemy and generate a new build order. This is done multiple times throughout
a game as the bot gets more information about the enemy through scouting. The process
of generating new build orders is referred to as replanning. Since the replanning is done in
real-time while the bot keeps playing some problems can arise, these problems are adressed in
Section 7.5. It is of course important that the bot is able to replan fast, because the replanning
time essentially is the time it takes the bot to react to the strategy of the enemy. The new
build orders are generated using a version of the Simulated Annealing implementation that
was tuned to perform optimally for the short search time that is available for replanning while
playing. For more information about the specics of UAlbertaBot see Section 7.1.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
This chapter intends to get the reader up to speed on the current state of Articial Intelligence
in computer games.
It is probably well known to the reader that AI has been used in board games for some
time now: in 1997 the chess computer Deep Blue defeated the then-reigning World Chess
Champion Garry Kasparov [Campbell et al., 2002], and recently in 2016, Google's Go AI
AlphaGo defeated the 18-time world champion Lee Sedol [Wikipedia, 2016].
[Laird, 2002] explains how computer games can and have been used in AI research. In
contrast to robotics, which is probably more commonly associated with AI, computer games
do not have the same problems related to low-level sensing and acting in the real world.
However, as explained by [Nareyek, 2004] and [Fairclough et al., 2001], AI is not only used
for research purposes in computer games, but also to increase the user experience.
2.1
AI in RTS Games
In 2004, a call for AI research in RTS games was made [Buro, 2004]. The article lists a number
of reasons why the AI performance in RTS games was poor at the time, one of them is the
sheer complexity of games of the genre; RTS games gives the player endless options with
countless interacting objects. Another is that players must work with imperfect information.
In contrast the options in board games like chess are much more limited, and there is perfect
information, the entire board is visible to all players at all times. Furthermore, RTS games
are played in continuous time rather than being based on turns. Another reason is that most
multi-player games like RTS games are meant to be played by humans against other humans,
hence they do not require world-class AIs, and since video games afterall are made to make
money this is an obvious place for the game developers to save resources.
Note that the AI performance in RTS games could still be considered poor today. Even
in a game as heavily researched as StarCraft, the best AIs are still nowhere near the best
human players [Ackerman, 2015].
[Buro, 2004] also lists some arguments in favor of AI research in RTS games, some of
which are: the games present well dened environments for testing algorithms used elsewhere
in AI; good AI will likely be more important in commercial games in the future, at the time
game graphics were beginning to saturate, hence opening up for focus in other areas; progress
in RTS game AI could be of interest to the military, e.g. in battle simulations.
Below is presented several studies within dierent areas of AI in RTS.
[Buro and Kovarsky, 2007] motivates research on the problem of generating build orders
for RTS games.
It also proposes a method of modeling them in PDDL, a standardized

planning language used in the International Planning Competition (IPC).
[Churchill and Buro, 2011] continues the research in build order search and optimization
by implementing a planner that can make build orders for StarCraft. The method used here
is a depth-rst branch and bound search that, given some goal, tries to nd a build order
with minimal execution time that fullls the goal. 100 replays of games featuring professional
StarCraft players were analyzed and the goals of the build orders used were extracted, the
search engine was then used to nd build orders for those goals. The quality of the build
orders found by the planner was comparable to that of the original build orders used by the
human players. This is an interesting study, because it was managed to reach human like
performance in one of the sub-problems of RTS games. Furthermore, the approach is very
dynamic in that plans can be determined online, only the goals needs to be specied.
Alternatively, [Weber and Mateas, 2009], uses case-based reasoning to determine a build
order for an AI playing Wargus, a clone of Warcraft II which was developed by Blizzard
EntertainmentTM. The AI uses game states that include information about the player and
the enemy technological state to choose from a number of build order actions, e.g. train
worker, train combat unit. According to the state, the AI will nd the best matching build
order from a predened library of build orders. The performance of these were tested to ensure
that the AI would choose the best performing build order for a given state. This approach
is in general more rigid than that of [Churchill and Buro, 2011], because build orders have
to be predetermined. However, it allows the AI to plan according to the state of the game,
which includes reacting to the enemy.
[Köstler and Gmeiner, 2013] uses genetic algorithms to search for build orders given a goal,
hence using the same approach as [Churchill and Buro, 2011] but with a dierent search algo-
rithm and for StarCraft II rather that StarCraft: Brood War. Very similarly, [Brandy, 2010]
also used genetic algorithms in build order optimization for StarCraft II.
[Barriga et al., 2014] use genetic algorithms to optimize building placement. A combat
simulator, SparCraft [Churchill, 2016b, Churchill, 2013], is used to test dierent battle setups
extracted from human and other AI StarCraft games. It is shown that the system is actually
able to defend against attacks by using clever placement of buildings.
SparCraft [Churchill, 2016b] is a combat simulator for StarCraft, that in contrast to the
other articles here is used for tactical control. Whereas build order optimization is a strategic
problem, SparCraft is used to determine when to attack, which is a tactical problem. Spar-
Craft can determine the outcome of a battle of up to 32 vs 32 units. SparCraft uses a model of
the game that, among other things, includes the position of each unit, and their current health.
It uses combat AI algorithms including Alpha-Beta, UCT, and PortfolioGreedySearch.
Much like [Weber and Mateas, 2009], [Hsieh and Sun, 2008] uses case-based reasoning to
determine build orders, this time in StarCraft. Strategies and their performance against each
other are mined from replays. The system represents a game state by 6 dierent features.
Given a game state, the system uses a distance measure to deduce the strategy of the enemy.
It is then able to propose a strategy to use against that of the enemy, based on past cases.
[Schadd et al., 2007] implements a hierarchical approach to opponent modeling in the
game Spring. The highest level is used to model the playstyle of the enemy, which is ei-
ther considered to be aggressive or defensive. At the lowest level, individual aggressive and
defensive strategies are considered. The system uses goal recognition based on in-game obser-
vations to determine the probability of each of the possible enemy strategies. As an example,
if the enemy is attacking for a large portion of the game, it is more likely that he follows an
aggressive strategy. This sort of opponent playstyle or mood modeling is something that was
considered early on in this project as well.
[Kabanza et al., 2010], also uses a form of goal recognition to determine the plan of an
opponent in StarCraft. They mention that the system relies heavily on the priori probabilities
of the enemy plans, which is something that will be discussed further in Section 6.3.3. They

Observe that all of the AIs examined in [Ontanón et al., 2013] use domain knowledge
in some way or another, however, this is hard-coded into the behavior of the AI. What
[Ontanón et al., 2013] propose is a more generalized and automatic approach that can mine
the domain knowledge of a game and incorporate it into the algorithms used by an AI. The
concept of unit counters is a very general concept in most RTS games, so basing the reaction
to enemy strategies on this could eventually allow for an AI that is able to read a unit guide
like the one from StarCraft II and use the information when planning.

3.1.4
Resources
There are two types of resources in StarCraft, minerals and gas. Minerals is the basic of the
two, since everything in the game costs some amount of minerals. Gas is a scarcer, more
precious resource which is required for high-tech elements. The gas cost of an element is
generally somewhat proportional with how high tech it is. Minerals can be gathered directly
by Probes, while in order to gather gas, one rst needs to build an Assimilator at a geyser
and then use Probes to gather the gas. A map has multiple geographical positions at which
one can naturally expand his base with a Nexus, these positions have minerals and typically
one geyser from which gas can be farmed when equipped with an Assimilator.
Since an
Assimilator is necessary to gather gas, it is an indirect requirement to all element types that
require gas.
3.1.5
Supply
As a nal restriction there is supply. Supply is a typical concept in the RTS genre; units
use up supply; and some of the buildings, typically houses or such, provide supply. For the
Protoss supply is called Psi, and is provided by Pylons. Pylons provide 8 units of psi, and
furthermore acts as a power grid for the other buildings of the Protoss. A Nexus also provides
psi, 9 units at that, however, a Nexus is much more expensive and takes longer time to build
than a Pylon, since it can also train Probes and act as a resource drop-o. Furthermore there
is a hard cap of 200 supply for all races in StarCraft. If the supply in use equals the provided
supply or exceeds it, e.g. if a supply providing building is destroyed, all unit production is
halted until the provided supply is once again greater the the supply in use.

The goal is to make a stepwise build order with individual instructions consisting of a building
or unit and an index which we will refer to as a handle, to avoid confusion with the indexes in

4.2.1

information about the initial amount of minerals and gas.

4.6
Estimating Resource Gathering Rates in StarCraft
The simulation used to evaluate build orders uses the mineral gathering rate per worker α
and the gas gathering rate per worker β. In this project, these values were assumed to be
constant and estimated by making some tests within the game. In reality α is not constant,
but depends on the number of workers already working at a base, so another approach would
be to keep track of the number of workers at each base and make α a function of that.
4.6.1
Estimating the mineral gathering rate α
A typical base location in StarCraft has exactly nine mineral patches and one geyser. When
workers move from the Nexus to the mineral patches, they try to nd a patch where there
is no worker already, since there can only be one worker at a time at each patch, this causes
some workers to move longer than others and sometimes to move back and forth between
patches looking for one without another worker on it. The end result is that the rate at
which workers gathers minerals, denoted as α, is not a constant, rather it is a function of the
number of workers at the individual base.
α was estimated to 0.70 having 9 Probes mining at the same base, one for each mineral
patch, for two minutes. Another estimate of α was made to 0.57 having 18 Probes mining at
the same base. Since for almost all of the game a player has between 9 and 18 workers per base
0.63 was chosen as a reasonable compromise between the two estimates. The biggest problem
with using an estimate like α = 0.63 is that it undervalues workers early on. When starting
construction of a building, the player has to move a Probe to the location where the building
is to be built. The Probe cannot mine minerals in the time it spends walking back and forth.
In the early stages of the game, where the player has very few workers, this actually has a real
cost, however, if the player has many workers it does not matter much. Furthermore a player
usually sends out a scouting worker early on, which means that the player will have one less
worker mining minerals than what the model predicts. Together these two eects help reduce
the underestimation of α = 0.63 early on. In the later stages of the game, it does not matter
much that α = 0.63 is an overestimation, the worst case scenario is that the model predicts a
larger production than possible, and therefore tends to value too many production facilities.
In StarCraft having too many production facilities is not a very big concern since the cost
of production facilities is very low compared to the cost of upgrades and units, however, if
it was a big problem the solution would be a better estimate of α, maybe by having it as a
function of the number of workers.

This chapter briey introduces the reader to the eld of search heuristics referred to as meta-

5.1
Meta-heuristics
Meta-heuristics are algorithms that search a space of solutions iteratively. A meta-heuristic
has no way of knowing if the optimal solution is reached, hence it is simply given some amount
of time for searching and will return the best solution found along the way when it runs out
of time, i.e. it nds an approximation of the optimal solution. There exists many dierent
meta-heuristics, but they all share some concepts, which are explained below.
5.1.1
Moves and neighborhoods
In each iteration of the search a meta-heuristic uses one or multiple solutions to generate new
solutions, this is done using what is referred to as a move. A move can be the simplest thing,
like adding or removing something from a solution in order to create a new solution, but some
meta-heuristics also uses more complex moves.
The neighborhood of a solution is dened as all the solutions that can be reached using
just one move.
5.1.2
Specialization
Typically a meta-heuristic has some parameters that can be changed e.g. randomness of the
search. The meta in meta-heuristic implies that it is a general idea that can be specialized
to a particular problem by tuning of the parameters and by dening the set of moves.
5.1.3
Hill-climber
In general a meta-heuristic starts with some initial solution or a set of initial solutions and
generates other solutions from that.
The simplest possible meta-heuristic is probably a Hill-climber, Algorithm (5.1) shows an
overview of the algorithm. First, a feasible solution to the problem is generated, see Section

better than the old solution, meaning that a local optimum is reached, the algorithm will nd

Another approach is to simply choose a random solution from the neighborhood, if not
better than the old, another random solution is picked from the neighborhood. With this
approach one would not be able to detect a local optimum, since it does not explore the
neighborhood systematically.
The hill-climber has the problem, that since it never explores solutions that are worse
than the best known it will eventually get stuck in a local optimum, that rarely is the global
optimum. One solution to this problem is to accept solutions worse than the best known with
some probability, this is what Simulated Annealing does.
5.2
Simulated Annealing
In contrast to a Hill-climber, Simulated Annealing allows exploration of solutions worse than
the current one. Algorithm 5.2 shows the general framework of a Simulated Annealing search
algorithm. First, an initial solution is generated and saved as the best known solution yet,
sbest. Then the algorithm starts an iterative process that runs until time runs out, after which
the best solution is returned. In this process a new solution snew is created from the old s
using some sort of change. Then the new solution is evaluated and if it passes some acceptance
criterion s is updated to snew. In a hill-climber solutions are only accepted if strictly better,
however, in Simulated Annealing the accept criterion is stochastic and based on the quality
of the new solution. Finally, if the new solution evaluates better than the best known, the
best known solution is updated as well. Observe that in Algorithm 5.2, snew is considered
better than sbest, based on some evaluation c(s) if c(snew) < c(sbest), which corresponds to
minimizing like with Evaluation 1. When instead maximizing like with Evaluation 2, <
should be exchanged with >.

5.3.1
Ordering of elements
When the algorithm loops over and adds all elements in d, the element types are added in
an ordering based on their depth level in the tech tree; rst, the elements closest to the root,
Nexus, Pylon, and Assimilator, are added, we dene that these elements are at level 1 in
the tree; then, the children of those elements, Gateway, and Forge; etc. Some elements have
multiple requirements (parents) at dierent levels in the tree, e.g. an Arbitor Tribunal, which
requires both a Templar Archives, level 5, and a Stargate, level 4. This leads to the general
denition that the level of an element is one more than the highest level of its requirements.
5.3.2
Adding elements and their required elements
Elements are added to the build order using the add_element method shown in Algorithm
5.3. When an element e is added with add_element all required elements of e, that are not
already at an earlier index in the build order or in the initial state, are added before e in a
feasible order. This is done by sorting the required elements using the ordering above. Having
to sort these elements is not computationally demanding, since the tech tree contains very
few elements in total, which is an upper bound to the number of elements that have to be
sorted.
The ordering of the required elements ensures that the build order is always feasible
with respect to the tech tree. The only way this could be false, is if some element A with
requirement B that furthermore has requirement C are added to a build order, that already
includes B. In this case A would be added at the end and then C before A, but after B,
which makes the build order infeasible. However, this can never happen, because if B is
already in the build order, C would have been added as well when B was added, because C
is a requirement to B.
When a building is added to the build order it is given the rst unused handle of buildings
of that type, e.g. the rst Gateway is gateway 1, then Gateway 2, etc.
If e is a unit or an upgrade, the build order also needs to include the appropriate production
building. By simply giving all units the handle 1, we ensure that said production facility has

element types, however, by limiting the insertion list, the probability that something relevant
is added to the build order is higher. An idea here would be to have the insertion list only
include element types that have a positive coecient in at least one of the evaluation steps,
and the element types required by those. After having chosen an element type, the index
where the element is to be inserted in the build order is chosen randomly as well.

Delta Evaluation
Typically most of the computation time in Simulated Annealing, and in general in meta-
heuristics, is spent evaluating and comparing solutions, i.e. determining δ. The simplest way
of doing this is to evaluate each new solution snew when it is generated, and then simply
compare evaluations in order to compute δ, in this way one complete evaluation is made
in every iteration of the algorithm.
For some problems, however, one can apply what is
commonly referred to as delta evaluation.
Depending on the problem, changing just one
element in the solution might not change a whole lot in the evaluation of the solution. A
simple example would be an assignment problem of jobs to workers, where each match has
a cost. A simple update for this problem could be to have two workers swap jobs, which
would only change the cost for those two workers, hence instead of reevaluating the entire
solution, one should simply evaluate the contribution of the two workers in the old solution
and compare it to that in the new, in order to nd δ.
For the problem at hand, however, it is very hard to know what other elements are

Chapter 6
Replanning with Goal Recognition
Most RTS games forces the player to be highly reactive. Some strategies counters others,
e.g. if one player is using a very economically greedy strategy, his opponent might be able to
punish him by making a lot of combat units and attacking while the greedy player has no army.
Furthermore, most RTS games incoporate the concept of unit counters (see Section 1.2.1).
Replanning is also relevant outside the domain of games, for example in train scheduling,
where delays or blocked rails could demand changes to be made to the plan.
Reacting after having seen the plan of the enemy is often not fast enough, instead one
might want to be more proactive and recognize it along the way, in order to be able to adapt
as soon as possible.
This chapter discusses some ideas as to how a player, human or AI, can recognize an
enemy's strategy and replan accordingly. Then a specialization of Bayes' rule to goal recog-
nition is presented following [Gener and Bonet, 2013]. Finally it is explained how this form
of Bayesian goal recognition can be applied to a strategy game. We will at times refer to the
enemy as the planning agent.
6.1
Reactive Replanning and Opponent Modeling
Reactive replanning is used here as an umbrella term for all types of replanning that in-
corporates some kind of reaction to the enemy. Below are discussed some approaches for
reacting to an enemy strategy, some of which are opponent modeling, and some approaches
for replanning.
6.1.1
Rule based replanning
As explained in [Ontanón et al., 2013] most StarCraft bots uses some kind of rule based
replanning. This means that they have some predened build orders and choose one based
on a set of rules e.g. if the enemy is building a fast second base, use build order A; if not, use
build order B. This is probably the most rigid and unadaptive form of replanning, however,
it is very simple to implement and can work well.
6.1.2
Heuristic replanning
The above can be expanded by having goals instead of build orders, and using some search
to then nd build orders that fulll the goals. This is what UAlbertaBot [Churchill, 2016e]
does. A set of rules is dened, for example if enemy does A, then set goal to G. UAlbertaBot
uses a goal vector with a number of each unit type, like d from 4.3. Finally the bot uses a

depth-rst branch and bound search with some evaluation, like Evaluation 1, to minimize the
total build time [Churchill and Buro, 2011]. Alternatively one could use a meta-heuristic like
Simulated Annealing in the search.
6.1.3
Triggers and non-triggers
A common method among most professional StarCraft players, is to use the concept of triggers
and non-triggers to determine the strategy of the enemy (see Section 1.2.1). Triggers and non-
triggers can actually both be expressed in rules like the ones discussed above. A trigger is
simply seeing the enemy do something whereas a non-trigger is seeing the enemy not do
something. Observe that the latter is very dierent from not seeing the enemy do something;
both triggers and non-trigger relies on knowledge and not a lack thereof.
An example of a trigger is, the enemy is building a fast second base, hence he is probably
not going to attack early. An example of a non-trigger is the enemy has not begun collecting
some resource yet, hence he cannot build anything that costs that resource at this time.
Typically players will have a predened tree of strategies, and branch out based on these
rules, at least this is the case in the early stages of the game. This method is much like how
professional Chess players play.
This approach has its aws, at the highest level of play, players do sometimes abuse this
system by hiding buildings at remote locations of the map or by changing their actual strategy
based on what the enemy has scouted. Manipulation of knowledge like this is above the level
of current AIs, however, it is something that could be very interesting to look further into.
6.1.4
Goal recognition
Goal or plan recognition revolves around determining the goal of someone, here the enemy
player, having seen a part of their plan.
Many texts use the term plan recognition, but
in [Gener and Bonet, 2013], the two terms seems to be used interchangeably; others use
opponent behavior recognition or opponent modeling, here we decide to use the term goal
recognition.
[Schadd et al., 2007, Kabanza et al., 2010, Hsieh and Sun, 2008], primarily use; case-based
reasoning (CBR), solving problems by learning from past cases; and goal recognition.
[Synnaeve and Bessiere, 2011] use a Bayesian model to determine the probability map of the
possible strategies the enemy could be using, and apply it to StarCraft. This involves dening
a number of enemy goals, here strategies, and a number of observations that can be made,
furthermore the strategies have to be linked to the observations. Finally having observed
something, the natural goal behind that can be recognized.
Consider the simple example of your friend going on a vacation. You know that he is
either going skiing or on a beach holiday, you also know that in order to go skiing he needs
his ski boots and in order to go on a beach holiday he needs his sun lotion. When you see
him pack the ski boots you recognize that his goal is to go skiing.
Observe that goal recognition is only an alternative to the rule based reactions described
above, one would still need some method for the actual replanning, like a search or predeter-
mined responses to each enemy strategy.

game, following the general idea of Bayesian goal recognition presented by
[Gener and Bonet, 2013].

In order to establish a Bayesian model we need to dene what a goal, an observation and a

that the enemy can use and dene very specic observations that depend on at which in-

element supertypes buildings and units as presented in Chapter 3, some RTS games might
not use these exact element types, but in that case the appropriate changes can be made.
Recall from Chapter 3 that the set of all unit types is denoted as U and the set of all building
types as B.
6.3.1
Dening goals and observations
We dene a goal as a single unit type, i.e. G ∈U, which means that the set of possible goals G
is a subset of U, G ⊆U. Furthermore an observation is dened as a set of building types, i.e.
Obs ⊆B. This allows us to determine the probability that the planning agent will make some
unit type G, having seen that he has already made the buildings Obs. As mentioned earlier,
the model assumes that the planning agent has only one goal, when in reality an enemy will
probably mix dierent unit types, this is discussed further in Section 6.5.2.
6.3.2
Dening plans
We dene a plan P the same way as an observation, as a set of buildings, i.e. P ⊆B. This
is a big simplication compared to the denition of a build order presented in Section 3.1.6,
rst of all a plan only contains buildings and therefore no handles are needed as well, but
more importantly there is no ordering of the elements in a plan and each building type can
only appear once, since a plan is a set of types. This in return means that the exact timing
of the individual elements in a plan is ignored, as well as the timing of when each observation
is made. The assumption here is that having observed some set of buildings always means
the same thing regardless of the timing and ordering of those buildings, when in reality this
is another big simplication.
Similarly to done earlier in Section 5.3, based on the transitivity of technological require-
ments, the set of required buildings by goal G is denoted B(G). Observe that if it is observed
that the enemy has made a unit type of type G, then it can be deduced that he must have all
buildings in B(G). In other words, the observation of unit type G s handled as an observation
of all building types in B(G).
We dene that a plan P is a plan for the goal G if P includes all building types that are
required by G, i.e. B(G) ⊆P. Observe that this denition does not care about the building
types that are not required by G, meaning that a plan P can be a plan for G even if P includes
a building type i without including all requirements of i, as long as i is not required by G. As
an example if the goal is A, and A requires B, and C requires D, then P = {B, C} is a plan for
G = A. Even though C is in the plan and D is not, because C /∈B(G). Another idea would
be to exclude all plans that include an element type without including all of that element
types requirements. However, the denition used here was chosen because it is simpler.
We denote the set of plans for goal G as P(G). The total number of plans for goal G can
be dened as well; every building type in B(G) is necessary in a plan for G, whereas every
building type that is not in B(G) can be in a plan for G, but does not have to be. This means
that there are |B| −|B(G)| free building types, which means that the total number of plans
for G is |P(G)| = 2|B|−|B(G)|.
Finally the set of all plans, denoted P, is dened as the set of all plans that is a plan for
a goal G ∈G. Formally stated by P = {P|P ∈P(G) ∧G ∈G}.

Determining the probability distribution of goals: P(G)
Next the priori probabilities P(G) must be determined. This can be done in numerous ways,
four are presented here.
1. As done by [Synnaeve and Bessiere, 2011] one could assume a uniform distribution of
P(G), this means that the planning agent is equally likely to choose every goal.
2. Another idea is to analyze a large number of replays of games of good players playing
and use the data to establish P(G), this could be done once or when applied to an AI it could
learn dynamically as it played games.
3. (2) could be extended by having player proles, instead of just one P(G) there would
instead be a specic P(G) for each enemy player.
Doing something elaborate like (2) or (3) is outside the scope of this project instead a
fourth approach is used.
4. P(G) is determined based on the fraction of all plans that are plans for G, formally
stated P(G) = |P(G)|
|P|
. The idea that the planning agent chooses his goal based on how many
plans there are for that goal might seem a bit odd, however, it has the desirable property that
goals with fewer elements in B(G) are considered more likely. Why this can be considered
a desirable property in the domain of RTS games can be explained by the following simple
example.
Imagine a two player game where Player 1 is trying to identify, and defend against, the
attack strategy of Player 2, the opponent. Player 2 has two possible strategies/goals G1 and
G2, A is the only element required for G1 but both A and B are required for G2. Having
observed only A, Player 1 is in much greater danger from the immediate threat of G1 as
opposed to G2, because Player two would need to construct element B rst in order to
unlock G2, whereas everything required for G1 is already there. (4) handles this by simply
considering G1 a more likely strategy than G2, however, there are of course some problems
with this approach; it could be that G1 is not a strong strategy and therefore not very likely
to be used; or, in the example, maybe Player 2 actually already has constructed an element
B, but Player 1 just have not found out yet, in this situation it would arguable be better to
consider the two strategies equally likely as in (1). Despite these potential issues (4) is used
here.

6.5
Replanning Based on Goal Recognition
This section presents a method to how goal recognition can be used for replanning for an AI.
This section will refer to the goals of the planning agent, here the enemy, as strategies, and
the goals of the AI as goals.
When the probability map of strategies of the enemy is determined one needs to nd an
appropriate reaction. As mentioned earlier, there are many ways of addressing this issue.
Possibly the simplest idea is to dene a complete plan for each of the enemy strategies
and choose the plan corresponding to the most likely enemy strategy.
Here we present a less rigid approach that instead denes a goal for each of the enemy
strategies and then uses Simulated Annealing as a search engine to nd a plan for the goal
corresponding to the most likely enemy strategy. With this approach one has to rst dene
what a goal for the AI is, and then link one goal to each of the enemy strategies. These issues
are discussed in the next section.
6.5.1
Dening goals for the AI
We choose to link an evaluation step, with goal coecient vector g as dened in Section 4.4.1,
to each of the enemy strategies, this allows specifying the prot of each element type against
each enemy strategy. This type of goal was chosen over the goal vectors of Evaluation 1,
because of the issues discussed earlier. One could use multiple evaluation steps for each of
the enemy strategies, but for simplicity just one is used here and the evaluation time for all
enemy strategies is xed to the same time Θ.
The coecients for each element type in each goal coecient vector should ideally reect
how good that specic element is against the corresponding enemy strategy. One way of
determining the goal coecient vectors is to test and tune various values over a large number
of games and nd the ones that wins the highest percentage of games, however, this is outside
the scope of this project. Instead the goal coecient vectors are determined based on unit
counters (see Section 1.2.1), which seems like a very natural approach having dened the
enemy strategies as units themselves.
One could likewise test and tune for the best value of Θ or even have dierent θ for each
enemy strategy.
Here Θ is chosen to 300 time steps1, because it seems like a reasonable
planning horizon.
Having decided to use goals as in Evaluation 2, this is of course the evaluation that should
be used in the search.
Remember that the goal recognition presented above is based on a model of the planning
agent, hence it naturally assumes multiple things, which are mentioned together here.
This project implements subjective values derived entirely from the personal experience
and opinion of the author about unit counters.
1300 time steps corresponds to approximately 188 seconds in real life, when playing on normal speed.

6.5.2
One goal
First of all the bayesian model assumes that the planning agent has one single goal. This is
a rather big simplication since most strategies in an RTS game will use a mixture of unit
types. However, the model determines the probability distribution P(G|Obs) for G ∈G and
this can actually be used in a solution to this problem.
The simplest idea is to only react to the most likely strategy, but this has the problem
that the enemy will most likely make more than one unit type.
Another idea is to calculate the weighed average of the goal coecient vectors for each of
the enemy strategies, using the probability distribution as the weights, however, this approach
has the problem that the resulting plan most likely will not be good against any of the
strategies of the opponent, since it will be a mix of too many things.
Instead the approach presented here, is somewhere in between the two above, by iden-
tifying the two most likely enemy strategies and calculate the weighted average of those
two corresponding goal coecient vectors. The sum of the weights of the two strategies are
normalized to 1, i.e. name the two most likely strategies of the enemy strategy A at proba-
bility P(A|Obs), with corresponding goal coecient vector gA and strategy B at probability
P(B|Obs), with corresponding goal coecient vector gB, the weighed goal coecient vec-
tor g∗is dened by g∗= P (A|Obs)·ga+P (B|Obs)·gb
P (A|Obs)+P (B|Obs)
. The choice of using exactly two goals is
something that could be experimented with.
Furthermore this approach was extended by adding the rule that if a single strategy is
more than 50% likely, the AI will not consider a secondary strategy, and simply use the
goal coecient vector of that one strategy. The idea behind this rule is that if one strategy
dominates the others, it is probably better to just react to that one strategy, however, this is
an assumption, it might not be the case.
6.5.3
Goals as unit types
In the model presented above units are used as goals of the enemy, this is simple, but maybe
not the best approach. In a game like StarCraft, there are many dierent strategies that use
the same unit types, and some strategies that does not rely on specic unit types, rather on
timings of when to take bases and such. In order to model this better, one would probably
need to dene more elaborate goals of the enemy, as well as redening plans and observations
accordingly; this is closer to what [Synnaeve and Bessiere, 2011] do. Some other simplica-
tions are that plans are simply considered sets of buildings types and that the cost of a plan
P is dened as |P|.
6.5.4
Estimation of P(G)
The estimation of P(G) used here, based on the simplicity of the plan seems to work well
when it comes to considering the most immediate threat more likely, which was the idea of
using that kind of estimation. However, many other approaches could be used, a uniform
distribution like what [Synnaeve and Bessiere, 2011] do, or some sort of distribution based on
statistics over a large number of games.
6.5.5
Enemy does not react
The approach of replanning based on goal recognition assumes that the enemy does not react
to the AI. The enemy simply choose a goal and the AI then tries to recognize that goal and
replan accordingly. In reality it could be that the enemy was doing some sort of reactive
replanning as well.

Implementation
This project tests the performance of the various theory explained above by applying it to the
already existing Articial Intelligence (AI) UAlbertaBot. This chapter explains the details of

7.1
UAlbertaBot
UAlbertaBot [Churchill, 2016e] is a StarCraft AI or Bot made by David Churchill of the
University of Alberta. It is implemented entirely in C++. The bot consists of three primary
parts; Build Order Search System (BOSS) that is used to generate near-optimal build orders
given a goal; SparCraft is a combat simulator that can simulate the outcome of a battle of
up to 32 vs 32 units; and the last part which will be referred to here as the Runner.
The Runner handles all interaction with the game, movement of individual units etc. The
bot initially follows a predetermined opening build order, however, when it runs out, the
Runner parses strategic goals to BOSS, which then searches for a good plan to achieve those
goals. The goals are dened in the same way as in Evaluation 1 here, i.e. as a goal vector d.
In a few very specic cases, the Runner will parse new goals to BOSS before the old build
order is nished. This will prompt BOSS to make a new build order that includes these new
goals as well as the old goals.
The bot is continuously trying to attack the enemy in a direct path from its own base to
the base of the enemy. SparCraft is used to constantly analyze the front line of UAlbertaBot
and of the enemy and determine when UAlbertaBot can win a battle. If SparCraft decides
that UAlbertaBot cannot win, it will pull back its units and regroup.

7.4
Online Replanning
One of the challenges when replanning for an RTS game is the real-time part, it is simply not
possible to pause the game and take time thinking. Instead the replanning is done while the
old plan is still being executed, this is referred to as online replanning.
This of course leads to some problems; the game state will change while the replanning
is done which in return can make the new plan invalid or simply bad. This issue is one of
the major reasons that it is very interesting to examine planning for RTS games, since online
replanning is needed in many real life scenarios as well.
Below are discussed some modications to the Planner that could help with these prob-
lems.
7.4.1
Replanning and goal recognition separated
One way to help solve the issue explained above is to replan very fast, however, that might
lead to suboptimal plans anyway.
Another idea is to split the planner in two, one part that can determine if replanning is
needed, based on the criterion explained above; and a second part that does the replanning.
This allows the planner to test while replanning if the current goal coecients are outdated.
This was not implemented in this project, instead the Planner will only test if replanning is
needed between the actual replanning. The two dierent solutions are shown in Figure 7.2.
Version 1 is the one implemented here, in which the Planner will only update goal coecients
between full run-times of the replanner. Version 2 has the upside that the Planner could
be restarted with new goal coecients at any time. This version would make the Planner
slightly more reactive by cutting the reaction time of the Planner in half at best.
Regardless of whether Version 1 or Version 2 is used the Planner will still need to manage
the fact that it starts Planning from one game state, but the output build order will start
being executed in another game state. We will refer to this problem as state inconsistency
and a solution to the problem is presented in the section below.

7.5
Handling State Inconsistency
The solution used in this project to the problem of state inconsistency before and after
planning, is to have the planner estimate how far in the old plan the Runner will be when the
new plan is done. The corresponding elements of the old plan are not allowed to be removed
from the plan under replanning, meaning that when the new plan is done, the rst some
number of items in the plan have already been executed in the game. In order to not have the
Runner execute those elements once more it keeps track of how many elements in the plan it
has started since the replanning began, it can then remove said items from the start of the
new plan and skip to where it currently made it to.
Observe that since the Planner is using a model of the game whereas the Runner is playing
the actual game the estimate made by the Planner can be wrong. However, as long as it is
ensured that the Planner overestimates how far the Runner will get in the plan while the
Planner is replanning, the worst outcome is that the Planner nds a slightly suboptimal plan
due to not allowing itself to change parts in the plan that could in reality have been changed.
e.g. it estimates not to change the rst 5 elements where in reality the Runner only does the
rst 3 before the replanning is done.

and without handles. The Planner then adds handles to the elements; rst, to the buildings
by giving the rst of each type handle 1, the next of that type handle 2, etc; then, to units and
upgrades by assuming that every unit and upgrade is produced at the appropriate building

Finally the build order with handles is used in Algorithm 4.1 to determine the element

7.5.2
Build Order Infeasibility
Recall that the Planner makes its estimate based on the assumption that the build order is
serialized, however, it is not necessarily. The Runner allows for parallelization in the build
order, which means that the Planner could underestimate how far in the build order the
Runner will be when the new plan is ready, which in turn can lead to some problems.
Since the Runner simply removes the number of elements that it has started since the
replanning began, it could remove an element which the Planner made changes to, which in
the most extreme case could lead to an infeasible build order.
Figure 7.4 shows an example where the Planner underestimates the number of elements
that the Runner will start construction of while the Planner is replanning.
The Runner estimates that the Planner will start two elements, marked with red in the
old build order. In the new build order, found by searching, the Runner has changed the
two last elements. However, in reality the Runner is faster than expected and starts three
elements, shown in green in the actual build order; the Runner simply follows the old build
order until it receives a new one. Having started three elements, when the Runner receives
the new plan, it will remove the rst three elements, including the element of type B, which is
why it is not in the actual plan. Now the problem is that if element type C requires element
type B, the actual plan is no longer feasible.
The approximation of the handles for the old build order can lead to an underestimation
like this, since the Runner could be doing things in parallel.
Another thing that can lead to infeasibility in the build order, is when the enemy destroys
one of the buildings of the AI. The model from Chapter 3 assumes that no units and buildings
are lost across the game. Losing a unit does not impact the build order in any other way than
freeing up some supply, unless it is a Probe, in which case it will lower the income. However,
losing a building could cause the build order to be infeasible.
If the Runner tries to execute an infeasible build order it will simply stop following the
build order when it reaches the infeasibility. It will still play the game, and try to attack the
enemy, but it will stop all construction of new elements in the build order. This, of course,
highly reduces the AI's chance of success.
7.5.3
Handling infeasibility
The ideal situation is of course to completely avoid infeasible build orders.

By choosing a very long buer time, it becomes very unlikely that the Planner makes an
underestimation. Furthermore, by choosing a small replan time, that Runner will only be
able to start a very limited number of elements, meaning that the possible parallelization is
rather limited as well, which makes it more likely that it will be covered by the buer time.
A more thorough solution would be to have a better estimate of how far in the build
order the Planner will get while the Runner is replanning. This would involve considering
parallelization possible in the estimate.
Even if a very good estimate was used, the build order could still become infeasible if the
enemy destroyed one of the buildings of the AI. In this situation one would need to completely
erase the current and infeasible build order and replan from the current state.
The Planner will never send an infeasible build order to the Runner. However, as explained
above, the Runner might remove elements from the build order and make it infeasible. When
the Runner reaches the element in the build order that it cannot make due to a lack of
its requirements, it will stop following the build order and wait. If the Planner receives an
infeasible build order from the Runner, it will completely ignore it and replan from the empty
build order. When the Runner then receives the new build order it can continue. This should
ideally also work if the AI loses a building, depending on the type of the building. If the lost
building is a Pylon, the initial state could become infeasible due to using more supply than
what is available. Observe that this is actually handled in the model from Chapter 3, where
the supply constraint (3.6) only is activated if the next element in the build order is a unit.
However, this is not implemented in the current version of the Planner, which cannot handle
this specic situation, and simply will consider any plan infeasible if the initial state does not
respect supply.
In this project the simple approach, using a serialized estimate with a large buer, was
used. The buer time was dened to 500 frames, the same as the plan time.
7.6
Updating the Goal Coecient Vector
A goal coecient vector g associates a prot with each of the element types. In relation to
replanning, the idea is that it should dene how good each element type is against some goal
of the enemy. If the enemy is mixing two unit types, the model should ideally recognize this
and plan for its own mixture of units that are good against those of the enemy. The reasoning
behind this can be best understood with the rock-paper-scissors reference used earlier; if the
enemy is making rocks and scissors, we would want to make some mixture of papers and
rocks. However, with the approach used here, this is most likely not what will happen.
When the weighed goal coecient vector g∗is determined, one element type will have the
highest prot in relation to its cost, which means that the corresponding plan found by the
search most likely will include almost exclusively that element type. This in return means
that the plan potentially only is good against one of the enemy unit types.
A much more desirable outcome is that the plan includes a mixture of elements based on
the relation between the corresponding coecients, e.g. if the coecient of one element is
twice as high as that of another we would eventually want twice as many of those elements.
A solution to the problem is presented below, followed by an example.
7.6.1
Updating prots based on current game state
The solution used here is to update g∗before searching for a plan. The update simply is
to divide every coecient of g∗by the number of that element type that is already in the
army of the AI, i.e. in the initial state. In this way, even if some element has a very high
coecient, it will not be the most protable element type if the player already has many of
it. In the case that the player hos 0 of some element type, the coecient is left untouched.

With this approach after some number of replannings, the player should eventually end up
with a mixture of units that ts the coecients of g∗reasonably well. This solution has the
problem that it needs a couple of replannings to get close to the optimal mixture of units.
Another approach would be to dene the coecient of each element type as a function
of the number of that element type in the initial state plus the build order. In this way, the
prot of some element type can be lowered if the build order and initial state includes many
elements of that type, e.g. by dividing the coecient of each element type by the number of
elements of that type, like in the rst approach. However, this approach is still very dierent
from the rst, since coecients are based on the number of elements in the initial state as well
as the individual build orders, which in return means that the coecients are updated every
time a build order is evaluated, rather than once before the search, as in the rst approach.
The second approach has not been tested here.

Parameter Tuning
Tuning of the parameters of a meta-heuristic can make a huge dierence; it is important
that they are tuned for the decided running time. In this chapter, the parameters of the
implementation of Simulated Annealing are tuned for planning using Evaluation 1 and 2 and
for replanning using Evaluation 2 only. For each of these, a test set of two goals is used. To
eliminate some stochastic variance in the tests, every goal was tested ve times.
8.1
Parameters
Recall the parameter list (t1, t∞, ρ, ) from Section 5.7.
These parameters were all tested
initially for three dierent values, these are t1 ∈{10, 100, 1000}, t∞∈{0.01, 0.1, 1}, and
ρ ∈{0, 50, 500}. In the case of ρ, the three values are supposed to correspond to no, some,
and a lot of intensication. For t1 and t∞, in the case that the best performance was with
an extreme value i.e. either the maximum or minimum value tested for the parameter, even
more extreme values were tested.
As explained in Section 5.4, three dierent moves are used in the implementation of
Simulated Annealing. At each iteration one of the three is chosen randomly.
Since a relocation move actually is just an insertion at the new index and a deletion at the
old index of the element that is to be moved, one could argue that relocation is not needed,
but on the other hand, since this approach uses two moves, the solution has to be feasible
in between the moves in order to be accepted. In some cases insertion of the element before
deletion will cause the build order to be infeasible e.g. due to supply limitations; in other
cases deletion before insertion could do the same. In the cases where both insertion followed
by deletion and vice versa leads to an infeasible intermediate solution, the only way to make
the move is with relocate. In any case, it would be interesting to test the performance of the
search both with and without relocation.

limited to the buildings and units from Figure 3.1 and Table 3.1. This includes 9 unit types
and 16 building types to a total of 25 element types, i.e. the upgrades are not currently part
of the implementation. This means that the number of possible build orders of for example
length 20 are 2520 ≈9.1 · 1027, which means that even if it was possible to evaluate a build
order in a nano second, it would still take almost 300 billion years to evaluate them all. Some

Chapter 10
AI performance
One could potentially measure the strength of an AI for StarCraft in multiple ways. One
idea would be to play against human players in a 1 vs.
1 battle.
There exists multiple
tournament ladders for human players online where people compete and are scored based
on their performance. However, this is usually not possible, because such online ladders use
software to detect if the players are using add-ons, which could help them win. This means
that an AI cannot join or host a game, since the add-ons required to feed information about
the game to the AI are mistaken for cheating software.
Instead StarCraft AIs are often matched up against other AIs. The built-in AI in StarCraft
is not very strong, however, it can be looked at as a rst challenge when building an AI.
There exists multiple rather big tournaments for StarCraft: Brood War AIs, [SSCAIT, 2016]
and [Churchill, 2016a] are two yearly competitions that each feature more than 20 bots.
[Churchill, 2016a] is actually partially organized by Dave Churchill, the creator of UAl-
bertaBot.
In these kinds of tournaments the AIs have to play on a number of dierent
maps that vary in size and environment.
This chapter tests the performance of the original UAlbertaBot, as well as the AI imple-
mented here (the modied UAlbertaBot) against other AIs in a tournament like environment.
10.1
Maps
A map is a unique battleeld with multiple player starting locations, which are usually close
to symmetric. When a game starts, the bots know everything about the map, just like a
human would after having played that map before. However, everything is covered in what
is referred to as the Fog of war, which is a common concept in many games in the genre.
Figure 10.1 shows a picture from the game. To the left is an areas covered by the fog of war,
to the right is a visible area. Units like the Probe and buildings like the Nexus lift the fog of
war and gives vision in an area around them. When no unit or building is near, the fog of
war returns. Therefore it is necessary to scout the enemy continuously to gain knowledge.
Figure 10.2 shows the map Fortress from above.
The map has four player starting
locations, which are marked by red circles. The blue arrows marks the only way out of each
of the starting locations, everything else being surrounded by impassable terrain, which in
general is marked by the green lines. When defending against early aggression, players will
usually try to ght in that choke point or even use buildings to make a wall there. Some
maps have a ramp in the choke point, which usually gives an even larger advantage to the
defending player.
Figure 10.3 shows a close-up image of a ramp; a ramp always connects a higher level of
terrain with a lower level of terrain. The blue arrow marks the path from high to low. Units

Usually the player starting location is at the higher level terrain, in which case the direction
of the blue arrow would correspond to those from Figure 10.2.
The path of the blue arrows leads directly to the natural location for a second base. In
general a base location is marked by the crystal blue mineral patches and a geyser.
The map has a nice rotational symmetry around the middle. When a game is started,
the two players are given random starting locations. In the case that these are opposite, the
distance between the players will be larger than if they are adjacent; this in return means
that early game aggression can be less potent. Because of the fog of war and the random
starting locations, the players do not know the position of the enemy when a game is started.
10.1.1
Maps used in tests
Other than Fortress two other maps were used for the tests: Benzene and Aztec.
Benzene, shown in Figure 10.4, has two starting locations with ramps that go from high-
ground inside the base to low-ground outside, hence the defending player has the high-ground
advantage. The shortest path between the two starting locations is marked by the purple line;
this distance is unusually large compared to other maps, especially compared to Fortress. To-
gether, the defensive ramps and the long distance between bases, makes early game aggression
rather weak, which makes this map especially interesting.
Aztec, shown in Figure 10.5, has three starting locations. It also has ramps at the starting
bases, but these are reversed, going from low-ground inside the base to high-ground outside.
The distance between bases is also much shorter than on Benzene, all of which makes early
aggression more potent on this map.

10.2.2
Goal recognition
The Yes option under goal recognition refers to the goal recognition explained in Section
6.5, in which a goal coecient vector g∗is determined based on the enemy strategy, and the
replanning is based on that goal coecient vector.
The No option means that the bot does not use goal recognition in any way, instead it
simply replans for the same goal. The only version that uses this option is version A, which
opens with the Zealot opening. The goal coecient vector used when replanning for version
A only has a positive coecient for Zealots, hence when it replans it just continues making
Zealots. Since the bot does not use goal recognition and only replans for one unit type, it
will only replan when it is about to run out of a build order (see Section 7.3.2).
A comparison of version A and version B is comparing a proactive play style to a reactive
play style, replanning without vs with goal recognition. However, another idea is to test how
using a bad goal recognition would aect the performance of the bot.
This was done by using a completely random goal recognition in version D. A random
goal distribution is generated once at the beginning of the game, this is done by giving each
goal a random number between 0 and 1 and normalizing these probabilities by the total
probability of all the goals. For example, if the bot has two possible goal, G1 and G2, the
bot could randomly guess that the enemy were to use goal G1 with 30% probability and G2
with 70% probability. This is only done once in the beginning of the game, because initial
tests showed that if done every time the bot considers replanning, it will constantly change
its beliefs about the strategy of the enemy and therefore ruin its own build order by having to
change its own strategy all the time. Whenever the bot considers replanning it instead uses
the goal distribution dened at the beginning. It still applies the coecient update explained
in Section 7.6.1 and only looks at the two most probably goals, or one if it has more than
50% probability.

10.2.3
Replanning time
The replanning time of 5 seconds was the original replanning time used when tuning the search
heuristic and therefore the one used in most of the versions. Observe that the replanning time
is in fact dened as 500 frames, which corresponds to 5 seconds at 4 times normal game speed,
which is the speed used when testing.
The idea behind the very low replanning time used by version E is to try to determine
the importance of a highly optimized build order. Based on the results from Section 9.3 we
know that the worst build orders based on the evaluation of the model are found when using
a very low replanning time. With a replanning time of 0.1 seconds the results show that build
orders could be up to 60% away from optimal. The buer time is set to 100 as in Section 9.3
for the reasons explained there.
According to the results from Section 9.3, the best replanning time is 2 seconds, therefore
it makes sense to test this setting, which is done by version F.
Version A uses replaning, however, since it does not use goal recognition and only produces
one unit type, Zealots, it only replans when it runs out of its current plan, which is rather
rare.
Therefore there does not seem to be much to gain for this version by lowering the
replanning time, hence the 2 seconds replanning time was only tested with goal recognition.
Since version C, which uses the Economical opening proved to perform a bit better than
version B, which uses the Zealot opening, only the Economical opening was tested with a 2
second replanning time here.
10.3
Bugs in UAlbertaBot
There seems to be a number of bugs in the original version of UAlbertaBot. In some of the
tests, even in the ones that uses the original unmodied version of the bot, it would fail in
one of multiple ways.
10.3.1
Stops following the build order
1. In some games, the bot would build an Assimilator but fail to allocate Probes to it, hence
it would never collect gas and not be able to build anything on the build order that cost gas.
When this happened, the bot would simply stop building anything when it reached the rst
element that cost gas in the build order.
2. In other games, the bot would follow the build order for the rst approximately 10
elements and then stop completely.
In both of the above cases, the bot had not started
replanning yet, so there was absolutely no chance the build order could be infeasible and that
that somehow could be the cause.
3. In some cases, the bot would get to the later stages in the game, and at some point
completely stop following the build order and instead build nothing. There is a possibility that
this error could be caused by an infeasibility in the build order from having state inconsistency
(see Section 7.5), however, this is very unlikely for multiple reasons. First of all, the same error
appeared multiple times early on, as explained in 2. Secondly, even if the applied handling of
state inconsistency would have caused a build order infeasibility, the Planner is equipped to
notice and handle this; if the Runner gets stuck in an infeasible build order, the Planner will
simply completely remove the current build order and replan from an empty build order (see
Section 7.5.3). Finally, having observed many of the errors as the bots were playing, in none
of those cases were the build orders infeasible and the Planner kept replanning, the Runner
just did not build anything.
Observe that none of the errors above were caused by the enemy player, i.e. by having
destroyed one of the buildings of UAlbertaBot.

10.3.4
Dealing with bugs in the tests
Since all the bugs discussed above are in the original version of UAlbertaBot, which obviously
translates to the modied version, bug-xing of these are considered outside of the scope of
this project. Nevertheless, it is very hard to compare UAlbertaBot and the dierent modied
versions from Section 10.2 if the tests are aected by noise from randomly appearing bugs.
Therefore it was decided, in all tests below, to replay every game in which UAlbertaBot
was aected by a bug.
This would obviously not be fair in a competition, however, the
purpose of the tests below is not to compare UAlbertaBot and the modied version to other
bots, but instead to compare them internally, in which case the tests are only made more fair
by removing the random bugs. Furthermore, the bugs can ideally be removed, in which case
the data below would actually reect the performance the bots would have in a competition.
None of the other bots, used in the tests below, showed any errors like this.
For every test made, the number of games with bugs, and hence number of replayed games,

Chapter 11
Discussion and Future Work
This chapter discusses the work presented in this thesis by its individual parts but also as a
whole, and suggests some areas for future work.
11.1
Build Order Optimization
The mathematical model of Chapter 3 denes a feasible build order in StarCraft. Some of
the constraints in the model are very specic for StarCraft, however, others are general for
the genre of RTS games. Therefore, this model could be considered a step towards a general
model of build orders in RTS games, which could eventually be used in a general AI that is
able to play any RTS game. There are of course many other areas that an AI for an RTS
game have to master, so a general RTS AI might still be far away in the future.
The two forms of build order evaluation presented in Chapter 4 each have their strengths
and weaknesses. In general Evaluation 2 is more dynamic than Evaluation 1 and it also easily
allows for multiple goals (multiple evaluation steps). However, it really depends on what
the user wants; if the desire is to nd the build order that can produce some unit type as
quickly as possible, i.e. a technology rush, then Evaluation 1 is the most tting; if instead
the desire is to maximize army size at some game time, then Evaluation 2 is more tting. In
relation to replanning Evaluation 2 was chosen here, because it easily allows for reacting to
multiple enemy strategies, like done here when the average goal coecient vector g∗of the
goal coecient vectors associated with the two most likely enemy strategies is used.
The method of generating build orders using Simulated Annealing has been used success-
fully to both generate opening build orders and for replaning purposes.
In the parameter tuning of the implementation of Simulated Annealing in Chapter 8, one
could potentially nd a better performing parameter setting by using a higher graduation in
the values tested for the parameters. Interestingly, the tests showed that the search performs
best when some amount of intensication is applied to it. This is usually not something that
is used in Simulated Annealing, but it seems to be a good idea, at least for this problem.
Furthermore, the tests showed that, with the lower search time when replanning, it is actually
benecial to use even more intensication. This could be because intensication makes the
search more targeted towards the area of the best know solution, and when the search time is
very limited, there is no time to progress through the search space with a high randomness.
It was also tested whether the third move used in the search, relocation, was important,
which proved to be the case; the tests using relocation were consistently better than those
without. Maybe one could even consider using more problem-specic moves, e.g. a move that
tries to add a worker to the build order. Since adding workers is the only way of increasing
income, they are probably more important than the other units. Another way of doing this
==PAGE:104==
92
Discussion and Future Work
would be to associate probabilities with the individual elements in the insertion list (see
Section 5.4.1). However, changes like these could negatively impact the search by limiting its
randomness too much. A better idea could be to remove all units from the insertion list that
are not part of the goal, and maybe even remove the buildings that are not required by an
element in the goal as well. These elements can never benet the goal, so it makes sense to
simply never try to include them in the build order.
The tests made in Chapter 9 shows that when planning for opening build orders, the
search time does not have to be very long in order to nd a decent solution. For all four
problems tested, the average error was within approximately 5% of the best known solution
after just 60 seconds search time. Since the build orders found are based on a model of the
game, which is simplied in multiple ways, the last 5% are most likely not very important.
The replanning tests showed that the optimal replanning time is around 2 seconds, where
the tests showed an average error of about 10% When the replanning time is chosen very
high, a large portion of the new build order has to be unchanged from the old build order,
which can lower the overall evaluation of the build order.
In both the tests for planning for opening build orders and replanning, the standard
deviation was quite low, almost always below 5%, meaning that the search is quite stable in
its results.
Observe that only two test problems were used for each test. Even though each test was
run 10 times, it could maybe also be a good idea to make tests on more problems.
11.2
Goal Recognition and Replanning
The goal recognition presented for StarCraft and in general RTS games, in Chapter 6, could
be considered simple in that, instead of trying to determine the precise strategy of the enemy,
the model determines what unit types the enemy most likely is going to make. This could be
considered a weakness, because very dierent strategies could demand dierent reaction but
be based on the same unit type. However, it could also be considered a strength, because it
allows to base the replanning on the concept of unit counters, which is a general concept in
most RTS games. Furthermore, in order to implement this kind of goal recognition in another
game, one would only need to know about the technology tree, the possible unit types the
enemy can produce, and the unit counters of those units. All of which is very basic knowledge
that could automatically be acquired by reading a manual about the game. Hence, this form
of goal recognition could be considered another step towards a general RTS AI.
One of the subjects that this thesis does not go in depth with, is how to determine the
exact goal coecient vectors based on unit counters. The values used here are loosely based
of o unit costs, however, if one were to use the system in a general RTS AI, one would
need to somehow dene the exact values based on the game knowledge provided. Another
approach would be to test dierent values over a large number of games and choose the
best performing one. Or maybe even a combination of the two, one could start with values
somehow determined from unit counters and then do some ne tuning.
The goal recognition presented here assumes a number of things, the big ones being: That
the enemy only has one goal, however, a simple solution to this was presented earlier, where
the two most likely enemy goals are considered, rather than just the single most likely, unless
the most likely has more than 50% likeliness. Furthermore, it does not take into account that
the enemy might also be replanning according to what the AI is doing. Finally, the model
assumes that the priori probabilities P(G) rely directly on the simplicity of the goal, i.e. the
number of dierent technological buildings required to reach the goal, with the most simple
goals being the most likely. This might not be the best way of determining P(G), however, it
works as intended in this application, by making the most immediate threats the most likely.

11.3
Bot Performance
All of the theory discussed above was implemented in the already existing AI UAlbertaBot.
The modied UAlbertaBot was tested against multiple other bots, including the built-in AI
of StarCraft.
The bot won all but one game against the built-in AI of StarCraft and generally performed
well against the other bots compared to the original UAlbertaBot. 6 dierent versions of the
modied UAlbertaBot were tested, 5 of which had win percentages between 76% and 82%.
The last version was used to test the importance of the goal recognition being reasonable.
Rather than using the method from Chapter 6 this version would randomly guess the goal of
the opponent. This lowered the overall win percent to 45% , conrming that the quality of
the goal recognition is important, at least in the extreme case.
The tests showed that the versions using a reactive playstyle, based on goal recognition,
generally performed a little bit worse, but almost as well as the version that played very
aggressively and purely proactively. This could be because the tactics used by UAlbertaBot
t a proactive playstyle the best, seeing that this is the playstyle of the original bot. The bot
is constantly trying to attack and only pulls its forces back if the combat simulator SparCraft
determines that it cannot win the current battle. This means that the bot generally has an
aggressive position of its units, which could be a bad idea with some opening build orders.
Another big point is that the bot does not currently incorporate upgrades. Upgrades are
generally slow to research and so are strongest in long games. Most versions of the bot that
used goal recognition, would tend to play longer games than the very aggressive version, which
means that they suered more from not being able to use upgrades.
The dierent versions using goal recognition with dierent opening build orders performed
very dierently based on map. On some maps a very aggressive opening was better and on
others a more economical opening was better.
The win percentage of the bot could be
improved by using a map specic opening build order, which makes a lot of sense, because
this is already what high level human players do.
The tests also showed that the replanning time was very unimportant for the performance
of the bot. Even though the tests from Section 9.3 showed that the quality of the build orders
should suer from having a 0.1 seconds replanning time, that did not impact the performance
of the bot. It would be interesting to make further tests of how the quality of the opening
build order impacts the performance of the bot. The bot is only following the opening build
order the portion of a game until it replans. However, since that is the rst part of the game
the quality of the build order is likely more important then than later, e.g. the lack of a
worker early on is much more important than late, because the early worker is a bigger part
of the entire economy and because it aects everything thereafter.
All the tests were repeated 5 times on each map. There were some randomness associated
with some of the games and this could be reduced with an increased number of tests.
Finally, there were some issues with UAlbertaBot crashing in multiple ways. Since all
of the versions tested are modied version of UAlbertaBot, the issues were present in all of
them. There is a chance that the bot could fail because of the buer time when replanning
being too low (see Section 7.5). Some of the cases of crashes were able to be examined and

11.4
Future Work
As mentioned above, there are a number of further tests that would be interesting to do, some
of which are, somehow testing the performance of the goal recognition system, and testing
the impact on bot performance of having less optimized opening build orders.
Since the
replanning system should be able to work for dierent game speeds, it would be interesting
to test the performance of the search at varying game speeds. Observe that this is a dierent
test from the one in Section 9.3, where the search time is changed. When the game speed
is changed, the search time changes as well, however, the search time relative to the game
speed stays the same, i.e. the in-game replanning time is unchanged. This means that the
number of elements in the old build order that have to be in the new build order would be
unchanged, as opposed to the test in Section 9.3 where it would change.
Both of the evaluation methods rely on a constant estimate of the mineral gathering rate
α. However, in the game, the mineral gathering rate is actually unique at each base and is a
function of the number of workers mining at that base. A better estimate would improve the
quality of the simulation of build orders.
The build order goals in the two opening build orders used were determined based on
personal knowledge about the game, a method commonly used when making bots. However,
it would be interesting to analyze more thoroughly what is a good goal, either by analyzing
replays of games featuring high level players or by testing the performance of dierent goals. In
a general RTS AI, the process of generating opening build orders would need to be automated,
this work presents an automated method of generating the build orders given a goal, so the
problem becomes how to automatically generate build order goals.
The presented goal recognition system could be changed in many ways. A more elaborate
method for determining the goal coecient vectors for each enemy goal could be applied. The
priori probabilities P(G) could also be determined using a dierent approach, potentially one
could examine a large number of replays of games and base P(G) of o that. At a larger
scale, rather than just dening goals as unit types, it would be interesting to implement an
approach like the one described by [Synnaeve and Bessiere, 2011], where the goals are actual
strategies and where the timing of observations is taken into account.
An obvious improvement to the modied bot would be to include upgrades in the Planner,
since this would most likely improve its performance in longer games. Another idea would be
to improve the system that handles state inconsistency between the Planner and the Runner
(see Section 7.5); right now the Planner uses a serialized estimate of how far in the build order
the Runner will be when the replanning is done, but it would be better to use a parallelized
estimate.
Given much more time, it would be natural for multiple reasons to implement the entire
bot in Java completely independently of UAlbertaBot. First of all this would remove the
errors caused by UAlbertaBot, however, they could of course also be removed by repairing
the bot. Secondly, this would allow for tactical control that could be specialized to dierent
strategies.
UAlbertaBot is constantly trying to attack the enemy, but this might not be
the best tactic when using a build order that is very weak oensively early on. Lastly, it
would make communication between the Planner and the Runner much better. The Runner
could actually use the handles on the elements in the build order provided by the Planner.
==PAGE:107==

Now that the handles would never be removed from the build order, the Planner would
not need to estimate the handles when replanning. This would make infeasibility errors in
the build order extremely unlikely. However, there is still the chance that the enemy could
cause the build order of the bot to become infeasible by destroying a building. The Planner
should be improved to handle this better, especially the problem with supply. The current
implementation of the Planner can never recover if it receives a build order from the Runner
in which the supply used is higher than the supply provided and this can happen if a supply
providing building is destroyed by the enemy. However, within the game a player can simply
build more supply providing buildings and continue building units again when the provided
supply permits it.
Finally, it would be extremely interesting to continue researching into the area of creating
a general AI for RTS games. However, there are a lot of technical issues associated with
this idea.
The current RTS bots do not play like humans do, with mouse and keyboard
inputs, rather they access and control the game through a custom interface, like BWAPI for
StarCraft; one would probably need to create a common interface for all the games the bot
should be able to play. Some of the ideas presented here could be used in a generalized RTS
AI, specically the mathematical model and the goal recognition based on unit counters.

Chapter 12
Conclusion
RTS games pose a variety of problems that are relevant within the elds of both Operations
Research and Articial Intelligence. Furthermore, the games can be used in AI research,
because they provide a well-dened environment with no external inuences.
This thesis provides solutions to three problems related to strategic planning in RTS
games: how to generate near-optimal build orders; how to determine the strategy of the
enemy; and how to change the build order in the middle of a game, when the enemy strategy
demands it.
The mathematical model presented in Chapter 3 denes a feasible build order in StarCraft,
but it could easily be specialized to other RTS games. This thesis presents a version of the
meta-heuristic Simulated Annealing specialized to searching for build orders in RTS games.
The search space of feasible build orders is dened by the mathematical model, and the build
orders are evaluated using one of two evaluation methods proposed.
The thesis explains goal recognition (a method of opponent modeling), and shows how
it can be used to determine the goal of an enemy player based on a set of observations. A
version specied to RTS games is presented, in which the dierent unit types are considered
to be the goals of the opponent, and buildings types are the observations. The method uses
the domain knowledge of unit counters to determine a tting strategic goal against the goal
of the enemy. Unit counters are a common concept in many RTS games, hence the system
could easily be specialized to another game of the genre. The Simulated Annealing search
engine can be used to generate new build orders based on these new strategic goals.
In the tuning of the Simulated Annealing implementation, there was experimentation
with intensication. The tests showed that some level of intensication does indeed aect the
performance of the search in a positive way.
All of the theory presented was implemented and tested within the framework of the
already existing StarCraft bot UAlbertaBot. The performances of dierent versions of the
bot were measured by matching them in 1 vs 1 battles against a eld of other bots. The
results showed that the best performing version of the bot was the one that used a purely
aggressive strategy without reacting through goal recognition to the strategy of the enemy.
However, the performance of the bots using goal recognition in a more reactive strategy were
almost as good. Maybe the aggressive bot had an edge because the original UAlbertaBot also
uses an aggressive strategy, hence the tactical control in the bot is strongest with that kind
of strategy.
Dierent opening build orders proved strongest on dierent maps, meaning that the per-
formance of the bot could be increased by using map-specic opening build orders.
The tests also showed that the search time used when replanning does not impact the
performance of the bot. A long replanning time does improve quality of the build orders, but
reduces the portion of the old build order that can be changed.
==PAGE:110==
98
Conclusion
All things considered, this work demonstrates potential in the application of a reactive
playstyle based on goal recognition in StarCraft bots. Ideally, this thesis will motivate more
research, specically within the area of opponent modeling. Lastly, the model, search engine
and goal recognition system presented here could all very easily be specialized to other RTS
games, and hence could hopefully aid the endeavor of creating a generalized RTS AI.
