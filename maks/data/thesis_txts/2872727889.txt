Abstract
At former industrial sites, contaminants are often found in the aquifer, leading
to contaminate the groundwater, and even surface waters, such as streams,
when groundwater is discharged to streams.
This is the case of Grindsted
stream, where a plume of chlorinated solvents is discharged. In order to protect
the environment and the people, it is important to remediate this pollution
and fulfil the quality criteria defined by the Danish EPA.
The aim of this project was to better understand the dynamics of the ground-
water discharge to Grindsted stream in order to help the Region defining an
adapted remediation strategy. Thus, the hydrogeological conditions of ground-
water in the area are investigated. First, data collected at the site are analysed.
Then, they are used to fit a 2D model simulating the temporal variations in
groundwater discharge. Meanwhile, the model is used to perform a sensitivity
analysis of the aquifer parameters on the discharge.
The model is built on COMSOL Multiphysics software, using the Finite Ele-
ment Method. It represents a transect of the aquifer along one of the ground-
water flowline, crossing the stream at the chlorinated solvents main discharge.
The study showed that the groundwater discharge is affected both by the prop-
erties of the low-permeability layer, located under the streambed, and by the
amount of groundwater recharge in the transect. The temporal variations of
the groundwater discharge are sinusoidal, with the highest discharge happen-
ing in winter. Recently, the temporal variations of the discharge are different
than usual, showing a nearly constant/decreasing trend since last summer. It
was found out that this is due to the unusual recharge pattern of the last
months. This model can be further used as a quantitative predictive tool of
the groundwater seepage velocity and discharge to the stream, helping the
Region to design a remediation strategy.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Groundwater is the Earth’s largest and most accessible freshwater reservoir, representing almost
half of the drinking water source [Lall et al. 2020]. Ensuring its quality is important. That’s why,
quality criteria have been established by the Danish Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) to
protect groundwater and drinking water resources [Miljøstyrelsen 2021]. Quality criteria also exist
for the aquatic environment, plants and animals to protect surface waters (lakes, streams and seas)
[Miljøstyrelsen 2023].
Indeed, groundwater is contaminated by humans due to the release of chemicals such as pesticides,
pharmaceuticals and solvents originating from, for example, manufacturing activities, agriculture
and human waste [Lall et al. 2020]. Contaminants are transported by the groundwater flow in
the aquifer, in a plume, expending according to the groundwater flow path and velocity. In some
cases, groundwater is discharging to surface water such as gaining streams [Woessner 2020], thus,
contaminating the stream as well.
In order to control the pollution and set up a remediation strategy, it is important to know how
much contamination is discharged to the stream.
This is measured by the contaminant mass
discharge (CMD), describing the quantity of contaminant (mass) discharged per unit of time. CMD
is estimated from the contaminant concentrations and the groundwater velocity. There is usually
high uncertainty in its estimation, mainly because the groundwater flux is diﬀicult to measure or
estimate, especially in an heterogeneous aquifer with low permeability layers [Rønde et al. 2017],
or at the groundwater-stream water interface.
In fact, at the surface water-groundwater interface, complex interactions are happening, hard to
observe and affected by natural processes, such as the shape of the streambed [Winter et al. 1998]
or the variations in permeability close to the streambed. These are leading to high spatial and
temporal variations in the groundwater discharge to the stream. In fact, the exchange conditions
are changing with time, in long or short term periods, due to variations of the hydrogeologic
conditions at the interface [Woessner 2020].
In this project, the study site is located in Grindsted city (Jutland, Denmark) where a contaminated
plume is discharging to Grindsted stream. The contamination originates from an old pharmaceuti-
cals factory that operated from 1924 to 2000, and is located 2 km northwards of the stream. The
main contaminants are chlorinated solvents, BTEX and pharmaceuticals [Rambøll 2024].
Here, the contaminants are not a problem yet in the aquifer, as there is no drinking water well in
the area. But, when entering the stream, the concentrations are very high and threaten humans
and the environment. Near the streambed, high degradation of chlorinated solvents was observed
[Ottosen et al. 2020], leading to the creation of vinyl chloride (VC). The presence of this compound

Objectives
The main goal of this project is to get a better understanding of the groundwater flow conditions
in the aquifer, partly due to its heterogeneity (with the presence of the low permeability layer,
probably lignite material) and quantify the dynamic discharge to the stream. It will be achieved
by literature review, analysis of the collected data and a 2D modelling of the area.
The objectives of the project are multiple:
1. Analysis of the data: Investigate possible trends in temporal variations in hydraulic head and
flow by analysing the data collected in the area.
2. Parameters contributions: Investigate the effects of the aquifer parameters on the hydraulic
head distribution and on the groundwater discharge to the stream. The parameters inves-
tigated are : the extent, shape, location and hydraulic properties of the low permeability
layer under the streambed, the heterogeneous state of the aquifer (hydraulic conductivities
distribution and anisotropy) and the groundwater recharge due to precipitation.
3. Temporal contributions: Investigate the influence of time-dependent boundaries on the hy-
draulic head variations in the aquifer and on the groundwater dynamic discharge to the
stream.
The time-dependent boundaries include the stream water level and groundwater
recharge time variations.
Using COMSOL Multiphysics v6.3 software, it will be possible to build a 2D model of the transect
along the flowline of the groundwater: from the factory to the main discharge area of chlorinated
solvents in the stream. As mentioned earlier, the model will be used to better understand the
groundwater flow conditions in the aquifer and the temporal variations of the discharge to the
stream. The latest informations collected at the site will be included in the model.
The long term perspective is to use the model that matches the best the observations, to predict
the groundwater discharge to the stream, and thus the CMD. Rambøll also mentions in their report
[Rambøll 2024], the possibility to predict the effects of their remediation actions on the groundwater

2.1
Groundwater flow
Groundwater is water that is present below the ground surface, filling the empty spaces of porous
media [Woessner et al. 2020]. The water table separates the unsaturated zone (shallowest zone)
and the saturated zone (deeper), where all the empty spaces are filled with water, having a water
pressure higher than the atmospheric pressure. The presence and storage of water in the aquifer is
governed by the porous media properties, especially its porosity.

2.2
Groundwater-stream water interface
The groundwater-surface water interface is a complex hydrogeological and biochemical zone [Conant
et al. 2004], also called the hyporheic zone. Several exchange processes are occurring there. They
are driven by the stream water level, the near-stream hydraulic head distribution, the composition
of the streambed and the hydraulic properties of the streambed, stream banks and surroundings
[Woessner 2020].
The size and the geometry of the hyporheic zone are changing with time because of the surface
water-groundwater interaction. In fact, due to their mixing, the chemical properties of the zone
vary [Winter et al. 1998], as well as the hydraulic head distribution: the hyporheic zone is extended
during dry periods and reduced in wet periods [Boano et al. 2014]. The streambed conditions are
spatially and temporally variable and subject to large hydraulic and geochemical gradients [Conant
et al. 2004], affecting the composition and fate of a contaminated groundwater plume discharging
to a stream.
A stream can be either a gaining or a losing stream (see Figure 2) depending on whether it receives
groundwater or lose water to the aquifer.
This flow direction can vary rapidly as a result of
individual storms, floods or transpiration of groundwater by streamside vegetation [Winter et al.
1998]. In fact, near the stream, the water table is close to the ground, allowing the plants to transpire
water directly from the groundwater system, reducing the amount of groundwater discharged to
the stream. These variations can be observed during 24 hours when the groundwater discharge
is higher during the night because of transpiration occurring during daylight [Winter et al. 1998].
The hydraulic heads around the streambed are also highly influenced by the water level in the
stream, as it was proven in a previous study [Rinaldi et al. 2014]. Thus, it influences rapidly the
groundwater flow direction and velocity.

In surface-water beds, seepage velocity patterns are affected not only by the meteorological condi-
tions but also by small-scale geologic features such as size, shape and orientation of the sediment
grains, and permeabilities spatial variations [Winter et al. 1998]. The groundwater flow to a stream
is usually multi-directional including large spatial and temporal variations [Rønde et al. 2017], due
to precipitation, to the stream geometry [Balbarini et al. 2020] and to variations in hydraulic con-
ductivity.
In fact, some studies have shown that the hydraulic conductivity of the soil in the near-stream
area is different, usually smaller than the hydraulic conductivity in the surroundings and with a
higher anisotropy ratio [Conant et al. 2004]. Because of these heterogeneities, residence times in
the hyporheic zone are found to increase with roughness and decrease with permeability [Boano
et al. 2014].

2.3
Heterogeneities in the aquifer and at the streambed
Heterogeneities and anisotropy in the aquifer and in the streambed have a high influence on the
groundwater flow path and velocity. Fine-scale variations in hydraulic conductivity (K), have the
potential to produce complex patterns of groundwater flow [Beckwith et al. 2002b]. It was shown
in Beckwith et al. (2002) paper’s that the heterogeneity of the aquifer plays a bigger role in the
complexity of the groundwater flow than the anisotropy.
Low permeability layers are common in the aquifer material, leading to an heterogeneous situation.
They can be composed of different kind of material such as clay, coal, lignite, organic matter.
Because of their low permeability, the groundwater flow path is affected. Also, the composition of
these layers can be in favour of contaminant degradation, leading to a complex composition of the
contamination plume.
2.3.1
Effect on the groundwater-stream water interface
At the streambed, it is important to know the hydraulic conductivity distribution because it is of
high influence on the exchange rates happening at the groundwater-surface water interface [Chen
2000]. It governs the stream infiltration rate to the aquifer and/or the groundwater discharge to
the stream depending on the flow direction. But, measuring the hydraulic conductivity at the
streambed is most of the time, a destructive method, such as the grain-size analysis method, where
the sediments are extracted from the site to be analysed in the lab, thus destroying the initial
structure of the streambed and modifying its properties.
High spatial and temporal variability of streambed properties have been documented [Sebok et al.
2015], using several methods to measure K: slug tests, in situ permeameter, combination of seepage
meters and piezometers, grain-size analysis, tracer tests. Chen (2000), has used an in-situ method,
the standpipe method, to measure horizontal and vertical hydraulic conductivities. The results
revealed that the vertical hydraulic conductivities were several times smaller than the horizontal
one, showing a very anisotropic area close to the streambed.
Spatial variability of K has also been documented, especially for vertical hydraulic conductivities
by Sebok et al. (2015).
Groundwater flow is not only driven by streambed heterogeneities but also by low permeability
layers that are present under the streambed and that modify the groundwater flow path. In fact,
beds of coal are common in the aquifers, especially soft lignite coal, which are materials of low-
permeability. Usually, they are of the Tertiary or Cretaceous age and measure less than 10-20 m
thick with most of them smaller than 2 m thick [Freeze et al. 1979]. Lignite material is a sedimentary
rock, the second phase in the coalification process. It is formed from compressed peat (first phase
of the coalification process), which is a mix of decomposed vegetation and organic matter. The
hydrogeologic properties of these materials can help understanding the groundwater flow path and
dynamics in and around the geologic layers. In fact, even a very thin organic layer can induce a
high spatial variability in hydraulic head and thus, influences the groundwater flow velocity [Sebok
et al. 2015]. This is for example the case in a river studied in Conant et al.’s (2004) paper, where
different dynamics of groundwater discharge to the stream are defined depending on the underlying

2.3.2
Hydrogeological properties of peat and decaying vegetation materials
Little is known about hydraulic properties of coal and its formation materials: peat, lignite, bitumi-
nous, semi-anthracite. Some investigations have been carried out in different places where values
of K have been estimated for these materials.
Different values of hydraulic conductivities have been found depending on the type of material in
the coal formation process, the tool used to measure and the location of the measurement. In the
studies, K value for lignite material range in [1.1.10−10; 6.10−3] m/s. A summary of the values
found in literature is available in Table 1.
One of the first investigations of these materials has been carried out by Van Voast et al. (1977) at
mine sites of shallow lignite coal, indicating decreasing K values at depths greater than 50-100 m
[Freeze et al. 1979]. Overall, the studies found out that lignite is a highly anisotropic material, and
that its hydraulic conductivity and anisotropy ratio are varying with depth [Beckwith et al. 2002a].
Schobert (1995) has even defined a relationship between hydraulic conductivity and depth below
ground surface for these materials: logK = −4.4759 −0.0636 × d.
More recent investigations have been carried in Czech Republic, by Noseck et al. (2009), with slug
tests in clay/lignite material.
Karakuş et al. (2021) has used the inversed auger hole method to determine the hydraulic conduc-
tivity of lignite layers in a site located in western Türkiye. In his paper, he mentioned that the
hydraulic conductivity of lignite is varying a lot depending on the geologic conditions of the site.
Thus, he recommends to carry out the measure of the K value at each site rather than relying on
literature [Karakuş et al. 2021].
Conant et al. (2004) found out that the semi-confining deposits (silt, clay and peat), located close
to the streambed are low permeable and have a higher porosity than the rest of the aquifer: 63%,
compared to the sand aquifer porosity: 30-40%.
Finally, Schobert (1995) compiled mean hydraulic conductivities of North Dakota lignites, Falkirk
area and Dunn Center area as well as Rehm et al. (1980) who compiled a comprehensive data set
for various coal formations in the USA and Canada. He reported in this study that the frequency
distribution of hydraulic conductivities follow a log-normal distribution with a geometric mean of
3.10−6 m/s.

2.4
Temporal variations in contaminant discharge to stream
To assess the contamination risk in a stream, the contaminant mass discharge (CMD) to the stream
is important to know. To obtain it, the groundwater flow velocity has to be known. In fact, the
general equation of CMD (J) is:
J = q × c × A
(2.10)
where q (m/s) is the groundwater flow, c (kg/m3) is the contaminant concentration and A (m2) is
the cross-sectional area.
In contaminated sites investigations, it was often observed that the contaminant mass discharge
(CMD) could not be completely closed, indicating the existence of additional pathways of contami-
nation not detected. Sometimes, it was observed high contaminant concentrations in river despite
of high stream flow, that should dilute the contaminant. Thus, it is important to understand the
contaminant source dynamics, pathways to explain these not intuitive observations [Lemaire et al.
2020]. In this case, it was the contribution of multiple contaminant sources during high stream
flow regimes that counterbalanced the dilution effect.
CMD to a stream is highly correlated to the groundwater discharge dynamics. The spatial varia-
tions of the groundwater discharge to a stream have been highly investigated in studies but not as
much as the temporal variations [Boano et al. 2014]. The variations are mainly driven by precip-
itation and snowmelt on short-term scales [Boano et al. 2014], and by periodic changes (such as
evapotranspiration) or episodic changes (such as flood pulse) on long-term scales.
The groundwater discharge to a stream is periodic, changing with seasons, because of precipitation
patterns. It was shown that from July to September, the difference between the groundwater levels
and the stream water level decreases, leading to a decrease in the groundwater discharge [Hobza
et al. 2018], because it is dependent on the hydraulic head gradient at the stream-groundwater
interface. Thus, the temporal variations of the discharge are driven by the temporal variations in
the difference between stream water level and groundwater heads.
In the study of Lemaire et al. (2020), it was observed that the highest CMD occurred in Jan-
uary/February, and the lowest in previous December/September/October.

3.1
Grindsted area
Grindsted (Jutland, Denmark) is a Danish town where a huge contamination plume is present in
the aquifer, making it qualified as a mega site. The contamination originates from a factory that
produced pharmaceuticals between 1924 and 2000. Because of their use of chemicals and of their

concentrations are too high in the river and not complying with the Environmental Quality Stan-
dards (EQS). There is a need for remediation of the groundwater and the river water to avoid
human contamination.

3.2
Stream
Grindsted stream is 8 to 12 m wide and 1 to 2.5 m deep. The stream water is flowing westwards at
a rate of 1200 to 4500 L/s, with an annual average of 2000 L/s [Balbarini et al. 2017]. The stream
is draining a catchment of around 200 km² and the annual average precipitation in the area is 980
mm/year (2011-2024) [DMI 2025].
The stream is a gaining stream [Woessner 2020], meaning that groundwater is discharging to the
stream (see Section 2.2), contrary to a loosing stream where the stream water is leaking to the
aquifer.
3.3
Geology
The aquifer in the area is 80 m thick, underlined by a Tertiary clay layer. It is an unconfined
aquifer [Balbarini et al. 2017].
The first 10-15 m.b.g.
of the aquifer are made of Quaternary
deposits, mainly meltwater sand.
Below 15 m.b.g., there are Miocene sediments, this zone is
characterised by a succession of sand and clay layers [Maurya et al. 2018]. Between the two types
of sand (Quaternary and Miocene), there are clay/lignite layers, at some locations that are more
or less thick, leading to consider that the deepest part of the aquifer is a semi-confined aquifer
[Balbarini et al. 2020].
Moreover, a thick layer of clay/lignite have been discovered under the stream, but its extent, shape
and thickness are uncertain. Close to the stream, the layer is [0.5-5] m thick and up to 10 m-thick at
some locations, it gets thinner in the northern part of the transect [Balbarini et al. 2020, Rambøll
2024]. Moreover, it was observed that the lignite acts as a hydraulic barrier for the groundwater
flow, because of its lower hydraulic conductivity [Balbarini et al. 2020].
Rambøll has produced a conceptual drawing of the geology in the transect between the factory site
and the stream (see Figure 6) [Rambøll 2024]. The drawing shows clearly the presence of meltwater

4.1
Modelling approach
The general approach of the project is to model the 2D transect of the area in order to understand
better the groundwater flow field in the transect, predict the groundwater discharge to the stream
and determine the aquifer parameters.
In fact, several parameters are unknown or not known
precisely (such as the hydrogeologic properties and the extent of the clay/lignite layer), and they
will be determine during the project.
When building a model, the first step is the model calibration [Fetter 2001], performed in steady-
state. The calibration is made on the mean hydraulic head observations in the transect. The goal
is to obtain the optimal parameter set that gives the smallest Root Mean Square Error (RMSE)
between the simulated and mean observed hydraulic heads. Thus, the aquifer parameters are varied
until the model closely reproduces the known hydraulic head conditions. During this process, a
sensitivity analysis is performed, in order to understand how sensitive is the model to changes
in aquifer parameters or boundary conditions.
The second step is the model verification [Fetter 2001] once it has been calibrated. This time, the
model is run with a transient study. The results are compared to the hydraulic head time variations,
recorded in the field. The final output of the model is the temporal variations of the groundwater
discharge to the stream. If the model does not reproduce the observations at a satisfying degree, a
new calibration of the parameters can be done.

4.3
Numerical model
Equations and solver
The model is used to simulate the groundwater flow, defined by Partial Differential Equations
(PDE) (see Section 2.1.1), where the variable is the hydraulic head h. The equations solved in
COMSOL are:
• For the steady-state model: −∇· (K∇h) = R
• For the transient model: Sy ∂h
∂t −∇· (K∇h) = R(t)
where, K is the hydraulic conductivity tensor, which is diagonal with Ky = aniso × Kx, R is the
groundwater recharge and Sy is the specific storage (defined in Section 2.1.1).
The governing equations are defined and solved in COMSOL. The initial model provided for my
project was using the Darcy’s Law module of the software. Because of the low number of licenses,
available at DTU, allowing the use of that module, the first step was to build a new model, using
the Mathematical Module and the PDE Equations module (Partial Differentiate Equations).
In fact, this transfer was possible because the equations governing the groundwater flow are PDE.
After implementing the equations in COMSOL, the steady-state results were compared to the ones
obtained with the Darcy’s Law module. The new model was improved until reaching nearly the
same results as in the Darcy’s Law module. The comparison of the results obtained with the 2
models is available in Appendix A.3.1.
Mesh
COMSOL Multiphysics is based on the Finite Element Method (FEM) in order to numerically
resolve the equations defined in the model. With this method, the domain is divided in elements,
constituting a mesh. In this project, the mesh elements are triangles.
FEM gives a continuous output of the estimated function (here, the hydraulic head h), by defining
an interpolation function in each triangle, based on the calculated head at the 3 edges of the
triangular element [Anderson et al. 2015].
The mesh of the domain is defined as described in Table 4 and visible in Figure 13. It is refined
around the boundaries, especially around the lignite layer, the streambed and the bottom boundary.

Computations
The steady-state model runs for approximately 10-30 seconds, whereas the transient simulation,
lasts for 15-20 minutes. The time range of the transient simulation is restricted by the available
range in the boundary conditions time series. The range where all boundary conditions are defined
is from 29/07/2021 to 27/03/2025, corresponding to a period of 1337 days (3.66 years = 3
years and 242 days).
Optimizations of the model parameters are run during this project, by using the Parameter
Estimation module in COMSOL. The solver method used in COMSOL is the Nelder-Mead method.
During the computation, the simulation is run several times, with new parameters used each time. A
Least-Squares error is calculated by comparing the observed hydraulic head values to the simulated
ones, until reaching a satisfying value or reaching the maximum number of computations.
For the steady-state simulation, the model is compared to the mean hydraulic head observed during
the last years (calculation method in Section 4.7.1), in the screens located in the northern part of
the transect (black and blue points in the wells in Figure 14).
For the transient simulation, the time-dependent hydraulic head recorded with divers are used
as a reference (blue points in Figure 14). The original recordings were hourly variations of the
hydraulic head, thus, they were modified to daily variations, by using the average head in 24 hours
(see Section 4.5.2). The divers installed in FG1 are not used for the optimization module because

Vertical hydraulic head difference
The time variations of the vertical hydraulic head difference are plotted in Figure 32 and Figure 33.

the geology composition of the aquifer and the temporal variations in hydraulic heads.
From this section, it was found that:
• The lignite layer located under the streambed acts as an hydraulic barrier, because of the

compared to the surroundings.
• The geology distribution revealed high heterogeneity in the types of aquifer material, espe-

• The temporal variations of the hydraulic head showed that the water table is higher in win-

from the factory to the stream, with higher velocities in wet season than in dry season.
• The vertical head gradient highlighted that the groundwater flows upwards (from the
bottom of the aquifer to the surface), except close to the factory where the groundwater flows
downwards. The gradient is higher during the wet season and lower during the dry season.

Discussion
This chapter is a discussion of the results obtained during the project. First, there is a critical
review of the assumptions made in the model, and the uncertainties that still exists.
Then, a
discussion of the results, interpreted and compared to the observations and literature is done.
Finally, recommendations for future investigations are done.
6.1
Model assumptions and uncertainties
Some assumptions have been made in the model and can be discussed. First, the anisotropy has
been assumed to be the same in the entire domain. This is probably not the case, especially in
the lignite layer where it was shown in Beckwith et al. (2002) that lignite is a highly anisotropic
material and that the anisotropic ratio can change with depth.
Also, the lignite layer has been assumed to be a rectangle shape which is probably not the case in
the aquifer. This shape can also create bias in the groundwater flow because of the boundary effect
at the edges of the shape. Other shapes of lignite layer could have change the results.
The 2 main domains of the aquifer (Quaternary and Miocene) are modelled with an hydraulic con-
ductivity based on measurements performed in the transect. The number and spatial distribution
of the measurements of K values is not very high, especially in the southern part of the transect.
In this part, there are high uncertainties about the aquifer properties, thus on the groundwater
flow conditions.
In fact, the flow conditions in the southern part of the transect could not be
compared to observations as too few measurements have been done in this area. Thus, the effect
of the groundwater originating from the south part on the discharge to the stream is uncertain.
In the transect, there is also a canal located south of the stream. This canal has not been imple-
mented in the model, but considered as a vegetation groundwater recharge area. As the stream, it
could have been considered as a hydraulic head condition, depending on the water level in it. This
canal may have an influence on the groundwater flow path and velocity in the southern part of the
transect.
The groundwater recharge at the top boundary of the model has been assumed to be driven by
precipitation and evaporation variations. This is a simplification of the processes involved in the
groundwater recharge. In fact, human activities can increase or decrease this water recharge. For
example, the sewer system is reducing the amount of precipitation infiltrating the ground, irrigation
is an artificial source of water infiltrating.
Also, the infiltration capacity of the aquifer is evolving with time, depending on the previous
precipitation trends. If the soil is too wet because of intense precipitation, there is little chance
that the water will continue to infiltrate. That is not taken into account in the recharge function

6.2
Comparison of the results to observations/literature
The main aims of the project were to investigate the aquifer properties and the dynamics of the
groundwater flow at the streambed interface. This section is an interpretation and discussion of
the results obtained with the model, compared to the observations and the literature.
6.2.1
Aquifer properties
The model built in this study showed that the aquifer have the following properties:
• Klignite = 3.6.10−6 m/s
• Hlignite = 5.87 m
• aniso = 0.29

First, the hydraulic conductivity of the lignite layer found with the model corresponds to the range
of values described in literature and presented in Section 2.3.2. This was easy to achieve as the
range of values found in literature is very large: from 1.1.10−10 to 6.10−3 m/s. Still, this value is
coherent as it is representing a low-permeability layer, thus it had to be of a lower conductivity
than the rest of the aquifer, which is the case.
Then, the height of the layer was found to be 5.87 m.
This value is coherent with what was
stated in the previous studies. In fact, Rambøll and Balbarini et al. (2020) stated that this lignite
layer was 0.5 to 5 m thick under the streambed. But, they also mentioned the fact that this layer
becomes thinner in the northern part of the transect and that its thickness could reach 10 m at
some locations. Thus, this estimated thickness in the model is subject to uncertainties, knowing
that it should not be constant with the distance. A new shape of the layer, including an evolving
thickness could have been tried to confirm the observations.
Concerning the anisotropy, the value found is different than the one used by Balbarini et al. (2020)
in his 3D model of Grindsted stream, where he used an anisotropy ratio of 0.1 for the sand material
and 0.01 for the lignite layer. Close to the streambed, Baggers (2023) measured an anisotropy
ratio between 0.2 and 0.8. It would have been interesting to try different anisotropy values for the
different zones in the model (Quaternary, Miocene, lignite and around the streambed).
Finally, knowing that typical values for Sy are between 0.02 to 0.3 [Fetter 2001, Freeze et al. 1979]
for unconfined aquifers, and less than 0.005 for confined aquifers, the value of Sy used in the model
(0.0014), indicates that the semi-confined aquifer behaves like a confined aquifer. The presence of
the lignite layer is huge and this result indicates that the length of the layer should be higher than
150 m, used in this model.

Groundwater discharge to the stream
Temporal variations
The study showed that the temporal variations of the groundwater discharge and velocities at the
streambed boundary are not always following the traditional trends. In fact, usually, the velocity
and discharge are sinusoidal with time: higher in winter/spring and lower in summer/autumn. It
was observed, both with the iFlux measurements and the model, that the discharge and seepage
velocities are decreasing since August 2024 although they should have increased during winter, to
follow the trend of the previous years. It seems that the unusual precipitation trends observed
these recent months have disturbed the sinusoidal pattern of the discharge.

6.2.5
Validity of the model
The model can be used as a quantitative tool as soon as the simulated hydraulic heads are match-
ing pretty well the observations. Thus, the outputs of the model must be in the right order of
magnitude.
The overall temporal trend in groundwater discharge simulated in 2024-2025 period is less reliable,
as stated before. But, this statement should be mitigated because the RMSE is still relatively
low for these years (0.15 and 0.14).
However, the temporal variations in seepage velocity and
groundwater discharge in short-term periods are well reflecting the reality, and could be used to
predict the short term variations.
6.3
Future investigations
As discussed previously, the model had some assumptions that can be discussed. Furthermore, the
model can be further improved to have a more precise simulation of the area. Thus, the following
are some ideas for future investigations, in order to continue the work done during this 5 months:
• Investigate the effect of other properties of the lignite layer in the model: shape, length,
anisotropic ratio, discontinuity of the layer, a hydraulic conductivity changing with depth.
• Investigate the effect of the aquifer properties of the southern part of the transect.
• Investigate more the southern part of the transect to have a better understanding of its
implication on the discharge. For that, more field investigations have to be perform in this
area, especially hydraulic conductivity measurements and hydraulic head monitoring.
• Add the canal water level, located south of the stream, as a top boundary condition in the
model.
• Measure the groundwater flow at the streambed through the entire width of the stream to
have a better estimate of the total groundwater discharge (in L/s) and compare it to the
model.
• Reduce the model extent to the area close to the streambed-groundwater interface to inves-
tigate the effects of small scale variations of the geology: spatial and temporal variations of
the streambed shape, hydraulic conductivities and anisotropy.
• Implement the chlorinated solvents contaminant plume and simulate its evolution with time,
in order to estimate and predict the CMD in the stream.
• Transform the model into a 3D model in order to take the horizontal flow variations into
account, and the meanders effect [Balbarini et al. 2017].
6.4
Perspectives of the study
The long term perspective of this project and model, is to help the Region to remediate the area.
In fact, they could use this model to:
• Predict the time variations of the contaminant mass discharge to the stream.
• Identify the location of the future targeted area in the remediation plan.
• Choose how they could best remove the contamination, by designing their remediation strat-
egy: choosing the type of remediation, the locations, the amount of pumping wells needed for
example, or the time scale of the remediation operation.
• Predict the effect of the remediation action on the contaminated plume. The Region could
model their remediation actions and predict its eﬀiciency, the spatial and temporal evolution
of the plume and the final rate of decontamination.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The aim of the project was to investigate the temporal variations in groundwater discharge to
Grindsted stream, where a contaminant plume is present. This main aim has been separated in
3 objectives: analyse the measurements made at the site, analyse the effect of the hydrogeologic
properties of the aquifer on the discharge and simulate the temporal variations of the groundwater
discharge to the stream.
All of this have been done by performing a sensitivity analysis of the aquifer parameters, creating
a 2D model of the transect and simulating the temporal groundwater discharge to the stream.
Analysing the dataset of field investigations revealed a highly heterogeneous aquifer, especially
close to the streambed where a low-permeability layer is found, probably a lignite layer. Also, the
recorded hydraulic heads with time in the aquifer showed sinusoidal variations over the years, with
higher values in winter/spring and lower values in summer/autumn. The iFlux sensors, recently
installed in the streambed to record the groundwater vertical velocity shows a surprising decreasing
trend since last summer.
The sensitivity analysis done on the model parameters, with a steady-state study, revealed that
the low-permeability layer located under the streambed has a high influence on the hydraulic head
distribution and thus, on the groundwater discharge value. The groundwater recharge amount is
also a high sensitive parameter of the model.
The steady-state model has been optimized in order to find the best set of aquifer parameters,
compared to the mean hydraulic head observed at the site. Then, these parameters have been
implemented in the transient model.
This 2D model quantifies the groundwater discharge to
the stream, based on time-dependent hydraulic head boundaries and time-dependent groundwater
recharge, calculated from precipitation and evaporation data aver the last 4 years.
During the calibration of the transient model, a maximum daily recharge amount has been imple-
mented, because the hydraulic heads were overestimated after summer 2023. The optimization of
the two transient parameters (Sy and Rmax), led to obtain an new optimized set of parameters:

• Hlignite = 5.87 m
• k = 0.23
• aniso = 0.29
