Abstract
On the 7th of November 1940 the newly constructed Tacoma Narrows Bridge suffered from large torsional oscillations
with amplitudes of 35◦until destruction. Since then a number of investigations have been made in order to ﬁnd an
explanation of the collapse, but no ﬁnal conclusion has yet been fully accepted. In the research work performed in the
present project, new appreciation of the wind direction at the day of the collapse is elaborated and the effect of this on
the aerodynamic instability is examined through experiments of a section model in a wind a tunnel.
The results from the experimental work and the ﬁndings in the present project are summarised in the following
conclusions:
• The wind direction at the day of the failure is found to be approximately 45◦relative to the bridge span. This
aspect has not been considered in previous experiments known to the authors.
• It is proven that the presence of yawed wind relative to the model deck inﬂuences the onset of aerodynamic
instability for the bridge section model. The critical wind velocity is increased by a factor
√
2 for the wind
directed 45◦relative to the model deck, in relation to the perpendicular wind case.
• From visualisations of the coherent vortex structure, the vortex seems to be the source of the aerodynamic
instability. For the onset of torsional instability the vortex is travelling in the mean wind direction across the
model deck with a speed corresponding to approximately a quarter of the free wind velocity.
• The effect of the travelling vortex is classiﬁed as aerodynamic damping and is found to be dependent on the
reduced wind velocity and oscillation amplitudes above 10◦. This is opposed to previous investigations where it
was concluded that the aerodynamic damping depended on the reduced wind velocity only.
• By extrapolating the results for the wind direction of 45◦, a reduced velocity of 10.4 is found for 35◦oscillation
amplitudes of the section model compared to the reduced velocity of 8 recorded at the day of the failure.

Preface
The project described in the present report is a master thesis project completed by Maja Rønne and Marlene Grundvig
Malthe. The thesis is prepared at the institute DTU Mechanical Engineering and is the ﬁnal project of the Master’s
degree programme in Civil Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark. The project counts for 30 ECTS points
and is completed in the time frame 22nd of August 2016 to 22nd of January 2017.
This report is the ﬁnal result of the master project "Wind Tunnel Investigation of Self-Induced Oscillations and Vortex
Formation for the Tacoma Narrows Bridge Exposed to Yawed Wind". A combination of research work, experimental
work and analysis of the experimental results is conducted and the work load is distributed equally between the group
members. The experimental work in the project is preformed at FORCE Technology in Lyngby.
We would like to thank our advisers Jens Honore Walther, Allan Larsen and Henrik Juul Spietz for excellent guidance
and constructive feedback throughout the project. Many useful ideas have been developed and solutions to problems
have been solved after discussions with these advisers. An extra thanks goes to Søren Vestergaard Larsen and the
technical crew at FORCE Technology for guidance and lending equipments during the experiments. Furthermore we
are grateful for additional components created by the workshop at DTU Mechanical Engineering.
DTU, 22nd of January 2017

Chapter 1
Introduction
On the 7th of November 1940, the newly constructed Tacoma Narrows Bridge (TNB) was subject to self-induced
torsional oscillations due to the aerodynamic forces present on that day. At this day a storm with the wind speed
of 19 m/s was recorded. The bridge was excited into a one-nodal torsional motion with an oscillation amplitude of
approximately ±35◦at the quarter points of the bridge span (Ammann et al. 1941). At this state the TNB could not
withstand the large forces and the bridge collapsed due to the violent motions.
Immediately after the collapse of the TNB, investigations were initiated to explain the reason for its collapse and reduce
the risk of designing such structures in the future. A number of section model tests and one full bridge model test were
conducted in wind tunnels by the US Federal Works Administration and The University of Washington. These tests
however resulted in the conclusion that the TNB would have collapsed at a wind speed corresponding to approximately
half of the observed wind speed at the day of the collapse. The phenomena causing the failure of the TNB was hereby
still a mystery. Other attempts to explain the collapse of the TNB have been made throughout the decades but none of
these theories have been fully accepted and a ﬁnal explanation has not yet been found.
Recent research reveals that the wind was coming directly from south at the day of the failure, corresponding to a
yawed wind of 45◦in relation to the bridge span. This wind direction have been conﬁrmed by eyewitness narratives
and an extract from the log book of the coast guard cutter USCG Atalanta which was responsible for the rescue on this
day. The yawed wind have not been considered in the previously conducted wind tunnel investigations, which were all
carried out for wind perpendicular to the model bridge span. In the present master thesis a bridge section model is
designed and wind tunnel experiments are performed for the section model exposed to perpendicular and yawed wind
respectively. The tests conducted for perpendicular wind are applied as the reference situation and the results received
from the yawed wind case are related to the collapse of the TNB. Furthermore the difference in these two situations is
studied.
The vortex formation on the bridge section model is visualised and studied in relation to an expanding vortex model
presented by Larsen (2016). The expanding vortex model suggests that the vortex is travelling across the bridge deck
while it is expanding in size. Furthermore the displacement time history and wind velocity are measured and the
damping and oscillation amplitudes are analysed.
The observations made in the present project can be used to improve the understanding of the vortex development
across an H-shaped cross section. More knowledge in this ﬁeld can introduce economical and simple designs where the
vortex development is controlled and the risk of enhanced self-induced oscillations is reduced.

2.1
Evolution of Cable Supported Bridges
Cable supported bridges such as suspension bridges and cable stayed bridges are characterized by their ability to span
long distances. The main functions of the cable system is supporting the bridge deck and carrying the loads. The ﬁrst
suspension bridge was built by the use of iron chains in Tibet in 1430 with a central span of 137 m. After this, the use
of suspended cables to carry loads have been seen through the history, but it was not until 1823 that the ﬁrst permanent
bridge supported by wire-cables was built in Geneva. The knowledge about the suspension bridge history in this section
is obtained from Gimsing & Georgakis (2012) unless other is stated.
In May 1883 the Brooklyn Bridge (US) opened, with a main span of 486 m and a total cable supported span of 1058 m.
The bridge was detailed by John Roebling and the cable supporting system consisted of a combination of a suspension
system and a cable stayed system. Roebling had experience with designing several suspension bridges and showed a
profound understanding of aerodynamic problems. He had investigated accidents, such as the collapse of the Wheeling
Bridge (US) in 1854, which was due to torsional movement of the bridge deck by wind action. The successes of his
bridge designs were due to the stability of their long spans under both trafﬁc and wind loading Petroski (1992).
During the second half of the 19th century the calculations of suspension bridges were based on linear elastic theory,
such as Rankine, where the deck consisted of two- or three-hinged girders. This includes the Brooklyn Bridge where
overall equilibrium was achieved based on elastic theory. After Roebling’s death in 1869 a new generation of engineers
came through. These engineers focused on calculation that required stricter mathematical modelling and more numerical
work.
In 1909 the Manhattan Bridge (US) opened and was the ﬁrst major suspension bridge to be analysed by "deﬂection
theory". The theory is a nonlinear elastic theory that takes into account the deﬂection of the main cable under lateral

deﬂection theory was developed in 1932 by L.S. Moisseiff and F. Lienhard, and the theory was expanded to a three
dimensional deﬂection theory. This theory led to a signiﬁcant reduction of the lateral load to be carried by the deck
itself which made it possible to increase the slenderness of the girder. A result of Moisseiff’s deﬂection theory was the
Golden Gate Bridge (US) which opened in 1937 and was an extremely slender bridge with a depth-to-span ratio of only
1/168 and a width-to-span ratio of 1/47.
In the late 19th century to the early 20th century the aim in design of suspension bridges was to provide rigidity against
excessive deformation and oscillation due to trafﬁc and static wind pressure. Rigidity under dynamic wind loading
was on the other hand not getting any attention (Ammann et al. 1941). Though the function of the stiffening girder
became more clear by the development of elastic theory. Through the ﬁrst third of the 20th century the suspension
bridges evolved to become more slender, span longer and trend towards more ﬂexible stiffening girders for aesthetic
and economical reasons (Petroski 1992). Though the trend to build more slender bridges took a substantial turn with
the collapse of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge (US) (Petroski 1992).
The Tacoma Narrows Bridge opened on the 1st of July in 1940 and had a main span of 853 m. Originally the bridge was
designed by Clark Eldridge where the bridge deck was arranged as a deep truss, but due to the high costs, Moisseiff was
hired to redesign the bridge (Plaut 2008). Based on the Moisseiff’s deﬂection theory the depth-to-span ratio was 1/350
and the width-to-span ratio was 1/72. These ratios were much lower than for the Golden Gate Bridge and for the sister
bridge Bronx-Whitestone Bridge (US) which opened in 1939 and had a depth-to-span ratio of 1/209 and width-to-span
ratio of 1/31. The deck of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge had low torsion rigidity as only one lateral bracing was present,
but the bridge obeyed the safety margin against the action of the trafﬁc load and static wind pressure (drag). From the
opening of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge it had a tendency to oscillate in the wind and got the nickname "Galloping
Gertie". In the ﬁrst 4 months vertical oscillations were observed. Then on the 7th of November, the bridge started to
oscillate in a twisting movement which became more and more dominant and after an hour the hangers broke and the
bridge collapsed.
At that time, oscillations of suspension bridges was not an unknown phenomena and a number of bridges had suffered
by major damages or collapse in wind storms during the years (Farquharson 1950). Even the sister bridge to the Tacoma
Narrows Bridge, the Bronx-Whitestone Bridge, also had oscillation problems, but extra cables and stiffening devices
had been installed to reduce the motions caused by the wind (Petroski 1992). The collapse of the Tacoma Narrows
Bridge was signiﬁcant as it introduced new aspects of problems of wind action in suspension bridge designs. The
inﬂuence of the form of the cross section on the oscillation characteristics and possibility of aerodynamic instability
became of great interest, although aerodynamic forces due to wind always had been considered in design of suspension
bridges (Ammann et al. 1941). After the collapse of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge aerodynamic studies and model testing
became more important in design of suspension bridges and already built suspension bridges were investigated for
aerodynamic instability. In the design of new suspension bridges the bridge deck depth increased and the deck section
tended to go from open section to closed in the design. Also existing bridges were strengthened and the deck depth of
the Bronx-Whitstone Bridge was in 1946 doubled to ensure safety. After World War II the Tacoma Narrows Bridge was
rebuilt with a truss girder with signiﬁcantly larger dimensions than the original bridge.
Throughout the 20th century aerodynamic performance had been more examined and understood, and according to
Larsen & Jacobsen (1992) it has become a governing factor in the design process of suspension bridges. With the better
understanding of the aerodynamic action of the wind on the bridge girder it has been possible to make long, slender
suspension bridges which provide aerodynamic stability. A notable bridge in this context is the Great Belt East Bridge
(DK) which opened in 1998 with a 1624 m long main span and a total cable supported length of 2694 m. The deck

2.2
Description of the Original Tacoma Narrows Bridge
The following description of the ﬁrst Tacoma Narrows Bridge is made from detailed descriptions given both by
Farquharson (1950) and Ammann et al. (1941).
The TNB was a suspension bridge structure with a main span of 853 m (2800 ft) and two side spans of 335 m (1100 ft)
each. The approach span on the west side was 152 m (497.16 ft) and 80 m (261.55 ft) on the east side. This made the
total horizontal length of the bridge between the strand shoes 1755 m (5758.71 ft), see Figure 2.1. The roadway was 8
m (26 ft) wide and the two sidewalks provided were each 1.8 m (5 ft 9.5 in) wide.
The west main pier was excavated and sealed at elevation -53.3 m (-175.0 ft) and the east main pier to elevation -68.3
m (-224.0 ft). Both piers had a dimension of approximately 20 m × 36 m (64 ft 6 in × 117 ft 6 in) and were made of
a cellular construction. The two pylons were 128 m (425 ft) high and formed a rigid frame of two towers without
diagonal bracing. The shafts of the towers were of a cellular construction and spaced 12 m (39 ft) center to center at the
top and 15 m (50 ft) at the base. Each of the tower shafts was of a cruciform section and increased in the longitudinal
direction of the tower from 4 m (13 ft) thickness at the top to 6 m (19 ft) at the bottom. The width of the tower shaft in
the transverse direction was maintained at 4 m (13 ft).
The two main cables each consisted of 19 strands with 332 parallel cold drawn galvanized wires of type no. 6 and
had a diameter under the rapping of 0.44 m (17.125 in). The sag of the main cable under dead load corresponded to a
sag ratio of 1/12. The small cable sag produced greater rigidity against vertical and lateral deformation under static
loading. The suspenders were placed 15.2 m (50 ft) apart along each main cable and consisted of 4 parts of 0.03 (1.25
in) diameter galvanized steel ropes. The anchors to hold the main cables consisted of solid concrete blocks. The east
anchor had an area of 21 m × 52 m (70 ft × 170 ft) and a height of 15 m (50 ft) and the west anchor block had an area
of 21 m × 49 m (70 ft × 160 ft) and a height of 17 m (55 ft).
The suspension structure itself consisted of two stiffening girders, transverse ﬂoorbeams, ﬁve lines of longitudinal
stringers and the lateral system of the reinforced concrete roadway, curbs and sidewalks. The two stiffening girders
were spaced 11.9 m (39 ft) center to center. Each stiffening girder consisted of a cross section web of 2.4 m × 0.01 m
(96 in × 0.5 in), four angles of 0.20 m × 0.15 m × 0.01 m (8 in × 6 in × 0.5 in) and two covers of 0.51 m × 0.01 m
(20 in × 0.5 in). The stiffening girders were stiffened in both horizontal and vertical direction, and were made of
structural silicon steel. The construction of the stiffening girder can be seen on Figure 2.1.
The transverse plate girder ﬂoorbeams were spaced 7.6 m (25 ft) center to center and were 1.3 m (52 in) deep. The
stringers consisted of 0.53 m (21 in) rolled I-beams and were spaced 1.8 m (5 ft 9 in) center to center. The thickness
of the roadway slab and the sidewalk slab was 0.13 m (5.25 in) and 0.06 m (2.5 in) respectively. The most important
dimensions of the suspension structure are summarised in Table 2.1. The dimensions given in this table are considered
in the design of the TNB model in the present project, which is described in chapter 4.

2.3
The Behaviour of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge Prior to the Failure
From the opening of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge in July 1940 oscillations were observed. Actually, oscillations were
noted already under the construction of the bridge (Ammann et al. 1941). Studies of the Bronx-Whitstone Bridge
showed that oscillations could occur for bridges with shallow stiffening girder, and therefore devices to reduce the
amplitudes of the center ties and hydraulic buffers at the towers were installed before the opening of the Tacoma
Narrows Bridge.
In the ﬁrst 4 months of the life time of the TNB vertical oscillations of different modes were observed and the bridge
was kept under constant observation. The modes of the oscillations changed frequently and the different modes seemed
to appear under same wind conditions independently. In an extract of a report from the 18th of September by Mr. L.
R. Durkee, the Project Engineer of TNB, printed in Ammann et al. (1941), it is stated that the most common type
of vertical oscillation appeared to be with two nodes at the center span and 12 cycles per minute corresponding to a
frequency of 0.2 Hz. These oscillations occurred constantly, but for different time intervals extending from few seconds
and up to 8 hours. To reduce the motion of the bridge deck, side span "hold-dawn" were installed in October 1940.
Extract from another report by Mr. L. R. Durkee from the 4th of November given in Ammann et al. (1941), conﬁrmed
observation of motions with considerable magnitude up to 1.22 m (48 in) with frequency of 0.27 Hz (16 cpm) for wind
velocity as low as 3-4 mph corresponding to 1.3-1.8 m/s. It is further stated in the report, that motions of different
degrees of violence had been noted up to wind velocities of 21,5 m/s (48 mph). Durkee also concludes that trafﬁc did
not seem to have a notable effect of the motion.
2.4
The Collapse of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge
On the morning of November the 7th, vertical oscillations with 8 to 9 nodes and with frequencies around 0.6 Hz (36-38
cpm) were observed at the center span of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge. According to eyewitnesses narratives given in
Ammann et al. (1941) the amplitudes of these oscillations were not greater than previously recorded.
On the morning of the failure, Walter Miles from the Paciﬁc Bridge Company and Professor Farquharson were ﬁlming
the motion of the TNB. At 9:30 A.M. longitudinal motion of the center cables relative to the center of the suspended
span was observed and recorded. This motion had not been observed before. Figure 2.2 shows single frames from the
ﬁlm by Walter Miles, where the longitudinal motion can be observed. Furthermore a slack of the temporary diagonal
ties is noted from the pictures. The slackness of the temporary guys appeared due to the fact that the bottom nuts had
unscrewed half an inch according to the description by Kenneth Arkin (Ammann et al. 1941, Appendix V). Due to this
slackness, the ties were ineffective and greater amplitudes of the relative motion of the suspended span cables were
allowed which inﬂuenced the stresses in the center tie.

2.4.1
The Wind Velocity and Wind Direction
The Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapsed in a storm with a wind velocity of around 42 mph which corresponds to
18.8 m/s. This velocity is conﬁrmed by eyewitness narratives given in the so called Carmody report, made by the US
Federal Works Administration (Ammann et al. 1941), and by Farquharson (1950). A statement from the eyewitness
Kenneth Arkin is given in the Carmody report: "Drove back to mid span - anemometer reading showed wind velocity
42 miles per hour directly from the south." (Ammann et al. 1941). The wind speed is further investigated in the ﬁlm
of the TNB at the day of the failure made by Barney et al. (1940). From the ﬁlm, white foam on the waves are noted
and the trees are swaying. These observations correspond to a wind force around 6 on the Beaufort scale (Beaufort
1805) which gives a wind speed of 10.8 −13.9 m/s at sea level. By use of Eurocode 1 (Standard 2007) the wind speed
proﬁle is scaled up to the elevation of the bridge at 63.4 m (208 ft) to ﬁnd the wind velocity at this level. By assuming a
terrain category I, the resulting wind speed is found to be around 16.1 −20.7 m/s at the deck of the Tacoma Narrows
Bridge on the day of the incident. This is in good agreement with the wind velocity 18.8 m/s as reported by Ammann
et al. (1941).
From the statement of Kenneth Arkin the wind direction was observed to be directly from the south. Extracts from a
log book of the coast guard cutter USCG Atalanta, see Appendix A, which was crossing the Narrows at the day of the
failure, conﬁrms this wind direction. From the log book it clearly appears that the wind direction was coming from
south. With this wind direction, the wind was approaching the main span of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge at an angle of
approximately 45◦as seen in Figure 2.4. Evidence of the wind direction can also be found from the ﬁlm by Barney
et al. (1940). From the ﬁlm a single frame of the wave pattern is shown in Figure 2.5(a) and the angle the ﬁlm was
recorded from is indicated in Figure 2.5(b). From the picture shown in Figure 2.5(a) it is seen that there are no waves in
the stretch of water between Point Fosdick and Fox Island. From Figure 2.5(b) it can be concluded that if the wind is
coming from south west, perpendicular to the bridge, waves would likely have formed of the north eastern tip of Fox
Island and would have washed up on the shore at Point Fosdick. As no waves is observed in this stretch of water, the
water at the north eastern point of Fox Island is in lee of the land, meaning the wind was coming from south.
The wind speed recorded at the day of the collapse was far below the wind speed the bridge was designed to withstand
(Gimsing & Georgakis 2012). However the dynamic action of the cross-ﬂow wind was unforeseen, and there had been
no investigation of this in relation to pulsating wind eddies, created at the sharp corners on the stiffener (Petroski 1992).

2.4.2
Previous Experimental Work
During the time after the failure of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge different experimental investigations and theories have
been developed to explain the motion and collapse of the famous oscillating bridge. Nevertheless none of these theories
have been fully accepted and a ﬁnal explanation has not yet been found. A short description of a few theories developed
since the collapse of the TNB is given in Appendix B. In this section some of the early experimental investigations and
their results will be described.
Shortly after the collapse of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge the Federal Works Administration (Ammann et al. 1941) made
a profound investigation of the bridge, summarised in the Carmody report. Several section models of the elastically
suspended system were tested in a wind tunnel at the California Institute of Technology. From records of the oscillation
section models, diagrams of the aerodynamic damping given as logarithmic decrement, for different wind velocities
were obtained for the models and transformed to apply to the prototype of the TNB. Though all the tests were conducted
with wind perpendicular to the model, which was not the case on the day of the collapse of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge,
as outlined in section 2.4.1. The results of these experimental tests can be seen in Figure 2.6. The application of
experimental work to the prototype were based on the assumption that the ﬂow pattern around geometrically similar
bodies are geometrically similar, independent on scale.
Figure 2.6: The aerodynamic damping with respect to velocity [Ammann et al. (1941)].
From the investigations by Ammann et al. (1941) positive aerodynamic damping was found for low velocities below a
critical value. With positive damping the forced torsional oscillations of the models decayed with time. At higher wind
speeds the aerodynamic damping became negative meaning that the structure imported energy from the surrounding air.
When the aerodynamic damping was negative the structural damping became an important factor. When the negative
value of the aerodynamic damping was numerically larger than the structural damping, aerodynamic instability was
obtained.

As the torsional oscillations of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge were not observed before the day of the failure, the structure

Theory
In the project described in the present report, experimental work is preformed in a wind tunnel to investigate how the
aerodynamic ﬂow is acting on a structure. The theory linked to this investigation is presented in this section. First, the
basic theory of ﬂow around bodies will be explained, followed by dynamics of structures. Finally, theories of vortex
formation around an H-shaped suspension bridge structure will be presented.
3.1
Aerodynamic Flow around Bodies
In this report aerodynamic ﬂow acting on a bluff body in the form of a suspension bridge with an H-shaped cross-section
is investigated. The characteristics of an aerodynamic ﬂow is described by the general theory of ﬂuids. This section will
thereby present the basic theory of ﬂuids in motion and how a ﬂuid in motion can inﬂuence a bluff body and vice versa.

3.1.2
Fluid Motion around Bluff Bodies
If a ﬂuid is acting upon a bluff body, the motion of the ﬂuid will change. The change of the streamlines in the ﬂow is
depending on the geometry of the body and the velocity of the ﬂuid, expressed by the Reynolds number.
When the ﬂow is approaching a body the ﬂow will separate at a stagnation point. This point is located in the middle of
the upstream surface of the H-proﬁle seen in Figure 3.1. At this point the local pressure is large relative to the rest of
the body surface. A shear layer is formed around the body where the ﬂow is inﬂuenced by the friction created by the
body. For low Reynolds numbers, viscous forces are dominating and the streamlines will follow the surface of the body.
However for high Reynolds numbers, a separation point is created where the streamlines may detach from the body
and a wake region is created downstream of the structure. The location of the separation points can be moved further
upstream, for continuous-curvature cross-sections, if the ﬂow velocity and turbulence increase. The separation points
are ﬁxed for structures with sharp edges and plane surfaces in the upstream face (Simiu & Scanlan 1978). This can be
seen in Figure 3.1 where the vortex shedding is fully developed for an H-shaped cross-section.
Figure 3.1: H-proﬁle exposed to ﬂow with Re = 1200 and height to depth ratio of H/B = 5 [Nakashima (1985)].

seen in Figure 3.1 where a Von Karman vortex street is observed downstream of the structure. For an H-proﬁle with
B/H = 5 the vortex formation is fully developed for Reynolds numbers Re = 1200 according to Nakashima (1985),
which is shown in Figure 3.1. When a structure is subject to dynamic forces by a ﬂuid, the structure can be accelerated
into motion. The motion of the structure may create a vortex shedding as a result of this motion.

3.3.1
Expanding Vortex Model
An expanding vortex model is introduced by Larsen (2016) to describe the dynamic motion in relation to the arising
vortex on an H-proﬁled bridge girder such as the TNB. The vortex is acting on the bridge deck with a force directed
outwards from the bridge section and the inﬂuence of the vortex in relation to the bridge deck motion is described in
this model.
Large leading edge vortices have a tendency to be formed for cross sections with a bluff windward edge, such as the
H-proﬁle in the TNB. The stagnant air in the lee side of the ﬂange above and below the bridge deck is activated by
mixing up with the incoming ﬂow that is separated from the leading ﬂanges. A new vortex is created every time the
bridge girder moves through a horizontal line parallel to the wind direction, referred to as zero position, in torsional
oscillation. The ﬂow is formed into a vortex behind the leading ﬂange rotating clockwise above the bridge deck creating
suction. Below the bridge deck the vortex is rotating counter clockwise. The suction present on the bridge deck can be
expressed by single forces directed outwards from the bridge deck creating a torsional oscillation when the vortices
are formed alternately. The vortex formation above the H-proﬁle is pulling the H-proﬁle in nose up direction until it
reaches the rotation center and the vortex below the H-proﬁle is resulting in torsional motion in the opposite direction.
The vortex is travelling across the bridge deck after forming behind the leading edge and the force is thereby moving
across the bridge deck with time. The vortex formation is shown in Figure 3.3.

reaches the rotation center of the cross section as seen in Figure 3.3. After this the source of circulation detach from the
leading edge and when it reaches three quarters length of the cross sectional width, the vortex is lifted off the bridge

3.3.2
Von Karman Vortices in H-proﬁles
An H-proﬁled structure is susceptible to another vortex shedding phenomena than described above, called Von Karman
vortex. The phenomena rely on the velocity of the wind and the geometry of the structure exposed to the wind. The
Von Karman vortex shedding is a double-layer ﬂow instability formed, after the separation points, in the unstable shear
layer of a structure. In the presence of a Von Karman street, the vortices are formed alternately downstream of the
ﬂow separation points. The Von Karman vortices inﬂuence the motion of the structure and is mainly causing a vertical
translation of an H-proﬁled structure. For an H-proﬁle the shear layers formed behind each end of the leading ﬂange
are separated and are unstable. In experimental visualisations by Nakashima (1985) an H-proﬁle is investigated. In
this analysis the separated shear layers are proven to roll up and escape behind the leading edges cyclically before
being organized as Von Karman vortices downstream of the H-proﬁle. Furthermore the experiments are made with
and without separation of the ﬂow downstream of the H-proﬁle when the proﬁle is subject to vertical translation. In
these experiments the ﬂow pattern above and below the girder stays unchanged but the vortex street downstream of the
structure is changed for the two different cases.

Design of the Experimental Set-Up
In order to analyse the torsional oscillations and the aerodynamics related to these oscillations, that caused the collapse
of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge, a section model of the bridge deck is designed and investigated in a wind tunnel. The
model is designed with regards to the dimensions of the wind tunnel, and dimensional and mass related parameters
from the TNB. The deck cross section of the TNB is applied to form the bridge section model. Furthermore a frame
supporting the bridge model is designed. This frame is keeping the model in the right position in relation to air ﬂow
from the wind tunnel. The elements included and set-up of the model are described in this chapter.
4.1
Requirements of the Experimental Model
The original bridge deck of the TNB oscillated torsionally with one modal node in the middle of the bridge span. The
model is designed so that the bridge model is oscillating about the central axis performing a single degree of freedom
rotational movement without any modal nodes, as the model is only representing one section of the TNB deck.
4.1.1
The Wind Tunnel
The wind tunnel test section is 1 m wide and 0.7 m high. The walls of the test section is removed during the experiments
so that the ﬂow area runs through an open space. This leads to the advantage that the model can to rotate in different
angles in relation to the airﬂow. For a closer description of the wind tunnel see section 4.4. In the experiments it is
necessary to do measurements when the wind ﬂow is acting with a horizontal angle of 45◦on the model deck. Thereby
the model and the frame length is built according to this demand, which makes the bridge section model expand outside
the wind tunnel section when the wind ﬂow is directed perpendicular to the bridge model.
4.1.2
Flow Visualisation
When the bridge section is oscillating the vortex formation is visualised by introducing smoke through holes in the
horizontal deck plate. The ﬂow formation around the section model is hereby captured on video by a reﬂex camera. In
order to get accurate visualisations, a minimum requirement is set to the frame rate, so that 20 frames per oscillation is
captured. For convenience, the frequency of the oscillations is kept low enough to be able to follow the oscillation with
the naked eye, yielding a maximum frequency of approximately 3 Hz. Hence the frame rate of the camera, applied to
visualise the ﬂow, should capture at least 60 fps. A laser is used to illuminate the smoke in one plane so that the ﬂow is
caught by the camera. The smoke is led out at the mid-section of the bridge section model. This solution is chosen as it
is favourable that the smoke is introduced through the model surface, as it is expected that the vortex is formed in this
area. If the smoke is introduced upstream of the bridge section there is a risk that the smoke will be caught mainly in
the external ﬂow streaming past the bridge section’s upwind ﬂange without reaching the separation zone.

Reynolds Number in the Model
As mentioned in section 3.1.1 the Reynolds number for the model and the prototype should be equal, unless the inertia
forces are dominating. This is the case for the present experiments. From visualisations preformed by Nakashima
(1985) a Reynolds number of Re = 1200 was found for fully developed vortex formation around H-proﬁles. To ensure
that the same vortex formation is present in the experiments as for the TNB and the experiments by Nakashima (1985),
==PAGE:55==

the Reynolds number for the model should be above this value. The Reynolds number for the original TNB for reduced
velocity of Vr = 8 is found to be Re = 155 · 105. For the same reduced velocity the Reynolds number for the model
is calculated to be Re = 82 · 103. As this value is in between the Reynolds number found by Nakashima (1985) and
present during the collapse of the original TNB, the model is assumed to obtain the same vortex formations as for these
two situations.

5.2
Investigation of the Natural Frequencies
The bridge section model is able to oscillate in different modes due to the spring supports of the system. The main
oscillation modes present in the system is vertical, roll and torsional rigid body oscillations. Vertical oscillation is
obtained when the whole bridge section model is translating in vertical direction. Roll oscillation is present when the
model is translating alternately in vertical direction at each end of the bridge section model. The torsional oscillation
corresponds to the motion where the H-proﬁled cross section is rotating around the center axis in the bridge model.
These modes are related to the system, consisting of the springs and the bridge section model. However, in some
situations the model itself is in resonance with the vortex shedding, vibrating vertically in its natural bending mode. It
is important that none of these frequencies are coinciding with the torsional natural frequency, so that the torsional
movement of the model can be examined. In this section the different frequencies are thereby investigated for Model 1
only by experimental tests, as the majority of the results are obtained for this model.
The experimental tests for the main oscillation modes are performed where the different modes of the system are
excited by hand in still air, and the decay of amplitude is recorded with time. The natural frequency for the three main
modes present in the system is found by a spectral analysis of the displacement time history by making use of FFT.
The natural frequency investigation is done continuously throughout the project duration when the experiments are
conducted, to make sure that all experiments are consistent and the natural frequencies of the system stays the same.

In the investigation of the oscillating system, the structural damping have to be considered, as it is a crucial factor for
the development of the oscillation amplitude. Experiments are made throughout the whole test period, to make sure
that there are no signiﬁcant changes of the damping in the system. The structural damping is found when the model is
excited by hand in still air, and the decay of amplitude is recorded with time after the external force is removed. The
bridge model is oscillating freely in 30 seconds and in the data analysis, the 20 ﬁrst oscillations are excluded from the
results. This selection is made in order to avoid disturbance or inﬂuence from the initial manual excitation.
The structural damping is determined by calculating the logarithmic decrement, given in Eq. (3.16) in section 3.2, for
the oscillation time history . The structural damping is a sum of the atmospheric still air damping, from the still air
surrounding the structure, and the damping from the spring supports, given as:
δs = δ0 + δsprings
(5.1)
where δ0 is the contribution for the atmospheric still air damping and δsprings is the contribution from the suspension
system. The damping from the suspension system is found from experiments where the bridge section is removed and
one cylinder rod with the mass corresponding to the half of the model is connecting the four springs at each end. The
damping from the springs is obtained when this set-up is excited in torsional movement by hand in still air, and the
decrease in amplitude is recorded. The extraction of the data is following the same procedure as described for the total
structural damping. The tests made to determine the damping contribution from the springs are only made once. The
damping obtained in these tests is assumed to account for all the experiments made for this model. The total structural
damping for the three different modes of Model 1 found throughout the experiment period is shown in Figure 5.4.

Chapter 6
Results from Experimental Investigations
The results from the experimental tests made in the wind tunnel are analysed and discussed in this chapter in order to
describe the aerodynamic behaviour of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge. Furthermore the test procedure for the different
experiments is be explained to ensure reproducibility. In this section Model 1 is considered unless other is stated.
6.1
Test and Analysis Procedure
All the tests are made when the main part of the model is placed in the air stream of the wind tunnel. When the wind
tunnel is stabilised, the wind velocity in the wind tunnel is considered constant and the torsional oscillation of the model
is recorded. In general, the tests are made in the order of increased wind velocity. For lower wind speeds the oscillations
are excited by hand and the decrease in amplitude is recorded. For each of these measurements the model starts from
rest. When the oscillations for a given wind speed seem to be in equilibrium, oscillating with a constant amplitude
the motion is recorded without any external force applied to the model. Hereafter the wind speed is varied in small
steps and the recording procedure is repeated. At higher wind speeds the torsional oscillations become self-induced
and the application of an external force is unnecessary. The model is starting from rest in these measurements and the
amplitude is hereafter increasing. The oscillations of the model is stopped when the motion becomes too violent, which
for Model 1 corresponds to an oscillation amplitude around θ = 25◦. In some cases for higher wind speeds resonance
occur and the natural frequency for the eigenmode of the model in vertical bending is excited. To avoid this motion, a
small external force is applied so the torsional oscillations can overcome the natural frequency of the model.
The procedure described above is applied for all the tests when the bridge deck model is rotated at different angles, β,
relative to the wind stream. From the recorded pressure and oscillation time histories, the velocity and the logarithmic
decrement for the total damping are analysed. To investigate the aerodynamic behaviour of the bridge, the aerodynamic
damping is separated from the total damping by subtracting the structural damping. The aerodynamic damping is
analysed in section 6.5.
To keep consistency in the results and in the analysis of these, only parts of the data is extracted from each recorded
time histories. The data used for the results is extracted for a given amplitude interval, depending on the damping
situation, which is illustrated in Figure 6.1. The amplitude intervals is furthermore given in Table 6.1. These intervals
are chosen because the amplitudes within these intervals are observed in all the time histories. Furthermore the majority
of each time series is systematically extracted within this interval.

Investigation of Yawed Wind Effect
In this section the total damping is examined for different wind speeds when wind is approaching from different
directions relative to the bridge model. When the total damping is positive, the structure is oscillating with decreasing

is zero the recorded velocity is corresponding to the critical velocity.
6.2.1
Damping in Different Yawed Wind Situations
The results obtained from the recorded time histories of the oscillating model exposed to yawed wind at different
angles can be seen in Figure 6.2 where the total damping, δ, as a function of the reduced velocity, Vr, is shown. Figure
6.2(a) shows the results where the data of θ0 = 3◦start amplitude is extracted for all the measurements including the
equilibrium situation. The equilibrium states obtained for other amplitudes are also included in Figure 6.2(b).
From Figure 6.2 it is observed that when the wind direction is going from perpendicular to the bridge deck, β = 0◦, to
yawed wind, β > 0◦, the reduced velocity for which the model is oscillating in equilibrium, is increased. Generally a
given damping for perpendicular wind is refound for yawed wind at higher velocities, except for the lower reduced
velocities, Vr < 2.5. The reason why this is not present for low wind velocities might be the measurement uncertainty
of the wind velocity from the pressure recorded by the pitot tube. For wind speeds below ∼2 m/s corresponding to
Vr ≈3, the pitot tube do not measure the pressure with sufﬁcient accuracy and the velocity of the wind is therefore less
accurate. The maximum difference between the obtained velocity from the pitot tube and an anemometer is found to be
approximately 20 % for low wind speeds as seen in Appendix G.
By comparing Figure 6.2(a) with Figure 6.2(b), a ﬂat plateau is observed in Figure 6.2(b) where the total damping is
equal to zero. This suggests that the damping is amplitude dependent as well as dependent on the reduced velocity. Thus
equilibrium at different amplitudes is reached for different velocities. A further analysis of the amplitude dependency at
the equilibrium state is made in section 6.4.1.
The main focus of the present report is based on the torsional motion of the model exposed to yawed wind of β = 45◦
relative to the deck. The results achieved with this wind direction are hereby shown in Figure 6.3 along with the results
for wind at β = 0◦which is used as a reference.
It is seen from Figure 6.3(a) that the value for the critical reduced velocity, for which the model oscillates in equilibrium
with an amplitude of θ = 3◦, is increased from Vr = 3.4 to Vr = 5.0 when the wind direction is changed from β = 0◦
to β = 45◦. This means that the motion of the model becomes unstable for a higher critical wind velocity when a
yawed wind of β = 45◦is acting on the deck than when the wind is perpendicular to the bridge deck. The ratio between
the two values for the critical reduced velocity for θ = 3◦oscillation amplitude is 3.4/5.0 = 0.68 corresponding to
approximately cos(45◦) = 0.71. This indicate that the value for critical wind velocity perpendicular to the deck simply
have to be multiplied with 1/ cos(β), for angles of yawed wind up to β = 45◦, to ﬁnd the critical wind velocity for
yawed wind, β. This can further be conﬁrmed by evaluating the equilibriums of θ = 3◦oscillation amplitude with
different β values which is shown in Figure 6.4. In this ﬁgure the obtained equilibrium states at θ = 3◦for the different
wind directions are shown along with the regression for the critical velocity. In the regression Vr,c(0◦) represents the
critical velocity obtained from for perpendicular wind and is found to be Vr,c(0◦) = 3.4 from experimental analysis.

6.5
Aerodynamic Damping
The aerodynamic behaviour of the model is now studied and presented in this section. In order to relate the aerodynamic
damping of the model with the original Tacoma Narrows Bridge and compare the results with the results obtained in
the Carmody report (Ammann et al. 1941), some corrections of the data are made. The corrections and the ﬁnal results
for the aerodynamic damping are explained and examined in the following sections for the case of perpendicular wind
of β = 0◦and yawed wind of β = 45◦relative to the bridge deck. Furthermore results from measurements of Model 2
are studied and compeered with the results obtained from Model 1.
6.5.1
Corrected Aerodynamic Damping for Perpendicular Wind
The aerodynamic damping for perpendicular wind is elaborated in this section. When the wind is perpendicular to the
model, β = 0◦, only 1 m of the model length is located inside the wind stream. As the length of the model bridge deck
is 1.8 m, it means that 0.8 m of the deck is outside the width of the wind stream. The aerodynamic forces from the

still air is moved due to the oscillation of the model, while the contribution of the damping from the spring support,

6.6
Summary of the Obtained Results
From the observation and analysis of the results in the present chapter, an estimated structural damping of δs = 0.045
for the original Tacoma Narrow Bridge is found and validated from the experimental investigation of the section model.
Throughout the analyses of the results, the amplitude of the motion in the bridge model have shown to have an effect on
the aerodynamic damping. Furthermore the yawed wind case is proven to have a great importance for the aerodynamic
instability and hereby the value of of the aerodynamic damping. This is supported by the visualisation analyses of the
vortex formation on the deck of the model. The reduced velocity at the day of the collapse was measured to be Vr = 8
and an oscillation amplitude θ = 35◦was observed. Furthermore a yawed wind of β = 45◦relative to the bridge was
acting on the bridge as presented in section 2.4.1. By including these aspects in the investigation, a reduced velocity of
Vr = 7.9 and Vr = 10.4 is found for θ = 18◦and θ = 35◦respectively. As the results found for θ = 35◦is based on
an extrapolation where large uncertainties are present, deviation from the original observation are to be expected. A
non-dimensional wind speed for the failure of the TNB is in the present investigation is hereby found to be Vr = 10.4
compared to the Vr = 8 observed on the day of the failure.

Chapter 7
Future Work
The present project is centred around the torsional oscillation of an H-shaped proﬁle. In the experiments, oscillations
up to θ = 25◦are obtained and the damping is investigated in relation to amplitude dependency. This dependency is a
new and interesting ﬁnding which could be further investigated by obtaining higher oscillation amplitudes. Hereby the
question whether the amplitude dependency for higher amplitudes increase or stays the same as for low amplitudes
above θ = 10◦might be answered. From this observation it might be possible to ﬁnd a more accurate reduced velocity
when considering amplitudes of θ = 35◦as for the prototype.
In the present analysis the vortex formed above the bridge deck is examined only. Though it could be an idea to visualise
the vortex formation created below the bridge deck if this analysis is expanded. Hereby the correlation between the
vortices made above and below the bridge deck could be examined. Furthermore a short investigation of the three
dimensional effect in the ﬂow is presented in this report. This advanced ﬂow formation could be extended and examined
for a larger section of the model. The ﬂow formation could be investigated experimentally for wind directed from
additional angles of attack and the change in the three dimensional ﬂow pattern with respect to angle of attack could be
the scope of work. To obtain a better understanding of the forces applied by the ﬂow, pressure transducers could be
included on the model bridge deck or PIV analysis could be conducted. By analysing the forces on the bridge deck
or the velocity of the ﬂow around the bridge deck, a better knowledge can be achieved of the vortex’s effect on the
structure and the motion it creates.
In the present analysis the model is tested in an open wind tunnel with wind directed perpendicular and with yawed
wind up to an angle of β = 45◦relative to the model. Corrections are made to account for the bridge deck that expands
outside the wind stream in the perpendicular case. In order to clarify whether these corrections are valid for this
experiment, the model could be placed in a wind tunnel where the wind stream is surrounding the model in all scenarios.
In a further analysis of the model design applied in the present project it could be interesting to analyse other oscillation
modes. For instance, the ﬂow present in a vertical oscillation mode where the Von Karman vortex is predominant could
be investigated.
In the experiments conducted throughout the present project the designed model of Tacoma Narrows Bridge is simpliﬁed
to the geometrical shape of an H-proﬁle. The original bridge was a more advanced structure and the inﬂuence of the
geometrical details could be examined by inspecting the vortex formation when adding more details to the bridge model.
Other details deviating from the original bridge could also be added to the model with the intention to break the vortex
formation or movement of the vortex across the bridge deck. Experiments with these additional details might give a
solution for an H-shaped structure where the torsional oscillation is avoided and the risk of collapse for an H-shaped
structure is reduced. A possible solution could be to include vertical girders below the deck that stop the vortice’s

Chapter 8
Conclusion
In the present project the collapse of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge is investigated through experimental work conducted
in a wind tunnel. Research work is preformed where information about the wind direction present at the day of the
collapse is achieved. Reliable sources show that the wind was coming directly from south, corresponding to a wind
direction of 45◦relative to the bridge span. This aspect has not been considered in previous investigations known to the
authors and is implemented in the experimental work performed in the present project. The aerodynamic, torsional
instability is examined for wind streams with an angle up to 45◦relative to the bridge model deck.
The results achieved for the bridge model exposed to perpendicular wind is compared with the results for wind direction
of 45◦relative to the bridge deck. It is hereby observed that the onset of aerodynamic instability is obtained at reduced
velocity of 5.0 for the yawed wind case opposed to the reduced wind velocity of 3.4 for the perpendicular case. The
critical velocity is therefore increased with a factor of approximately
√
2 when the model deck is exposed to winds
of 45◦. The critical velocity obtained for the perpendicular case is similar to the critical velocity found to be 4 from
previous experiments conducted with the same wind direction.
Visualisations of the ﬂow around the oscillating bridge model show that vortex formations around the model girder is a
likely source of the aerodynamic instability. The vortex is travelling in the direction of the mean wind stream across
the deck while expanding in size. The visualisations show that the onset of instability occur for a free wind stream
corresponding to the vortex travelling speed multiplied by a factor four. This coincides with the fact that the vortex has
reached the center width of the model deck after approximately one half torsional oscillation.
The vortex’s inﬂuence on the bridge deck oscillation is expressed by the aerodynamic damping. The aerodynamic
damping from the vortex is proven to be a function of the non-dimensional wind speed and torsional oscillation
amplitudes above 10◦. This amplitude dependency was not discovered in previous investigations for Tacoma Narrows
Bridge models and the aerodynamic damping was assumed to be a function of non-dimensional wind speed only.
From the results obtained in the present investigation a torsional amplitude of 18◦is found for reduced velocity of
approximately 8, which coincides with the non-dimensional wind speed measured at the day of the collapse. By
extrapolating these results to the oscillation amplitude of 35◦, obtained for the Tacoma Narrows Bridge before the
collapse, a non-dimensional wind speed of 10.4 is estimated and compared with the reduced velocity of 8 recorded at
the day of the failure.

Appendix B
Previous Nonlinear Theories
According to the report by the US Federal Works Administration (Ammann et al. 1941) it is stated that the TNB was
well designed and built to withstand all static forces, including static wind. The collapse of the bridge was due to
excessive oscillations from wind action. In the US Federal Works Administrations report the investigation showed that
the ﬁrst failure was the slipping of the cable band on the north side which probably initiated the torsional oscillations.
The violent vertical, and in the end torsional oscillations, were obtained due to the ﬂexibility of the cross section and the
small capacity to absorb dynamic forces. The vertical oscillations were according to Ammann et al. (1941) probably
induced by the turbulence of the wind action and the amplitude of the oscillations may have been inﬂuenced by the
cross section geometry. However Ammann et al. (1941) stated that there was no convincing evidence of aerodynamic
instability (negative damping) for the vertical motion and their conclusions were subjected to development of further
data. Although aerodynamic instability in relation to both vertical and torsional oscillations have been mentioned and
discussed as causes to the accident. Also resonance with oscillating forces due to Von Kármán vortex shedding has
been mentioned as a possible inducement to the large amplitude vibrations.
Plaut & Davis (2007) support the explanation given by Ammann et al. (1941) that the torsional oscillations start due
to the slipping of the north cable band. Plaut & Davis (2007) stated that the loosening of the main suspension cable
at the north side got the cable band to slip back and forth in the longitudinal direction, which then created a sudden
lateral asymmetry that initiate the torsional motion. Also loss of one or more hangers on one side of the bridge could
have led to a sudden lateral asymmetry. According to Plaut & Davis (2007) the TNB did not have sufﬁcient torsional
stiffness. The reason why the bridge did not start torsional oscillations before the day of the collapse, was because of the
sudden lateral asymmetry due to the loosening of the cable band. Nonlinear behaviour of hangers, the slackening and
tightening, was in Plaut & Davis (2007) opinion an important factor in the collapse of the TNB. Model tests with 2 and 4
degrees-of-freedom (DOF) supported by springs and dashpots, conﬁrms this explanation of the collapse. From test with
nonlinear damping (Van der Pol type) and nonlinear forces in hangers (cubic stiffening), torsional oscillations reach
a limit cycle with approximately constant amplitude, as was observed for the original bridge. In the used governing
equations for the models, geometric non-linearities was included. Plaut & Davis (2007) suggests that for the high wind
speeds that caused torsional ﬂutter other non-linearities may have to be considered.
The theory given by Plaut & Davis (2007) was further developed by Plaut (2008) where it is suggested that the loosening
of the cable band at midspan was due to snap load from the diagonal ties connecting the band to the bridge deck. The
asymmetric system with longitudinal motion of the north cable had a higher wind speed than the critical wind speed for
torsional ﬂutter and torsional motion appeared. Plaut (2008) believed that the hangers on the bridge remained in tension
and highlighted that the shift from vertical to torsional oscillations was due to change in the system where the ﬂutter
response quickly went into a limited cycle dominated by torsion. The torsional oscillation was thereby not induced by

The approach that the torsional oscillations was due to the sudden lateral asymmetry because of the loosening of the
main cable band was also used by Malík (2013). But Malík (2013) suggests a linear approach to the ﬂutter problem and

Arioli & Gazzola (2013) claimed "... it appears impossible that an irregular wind might generate a regular torsional

phenomenon inside the bridge. In the light of a 4 DOF model with coupled nonlinear oscillations Arioli & Gazzola
(2013) found that a nonlinear restoring force played a major role in the behaviour of the bridge. A nonlinear force in
the hangers could give rise to a sudden transfer in energy between the two oscillators, vertical and torsional motion, and
internal resonance would occur. The only important parameter for which the switch between vertical and torsional
oscillation occur, was found to be the critical energy threshold, the minimum energy level where internal resonance
occur. By Arioli & Gazzola (2013) opinion the wind on November 7, 1940, inserted enough energy into the TNB to
overcome the critical energy threshold and therefore the torsional motion suddenly appeared. The phenomena described
above is further studied and explained in Arioli & Gazzola (2015), where it is stated that after internal resonance, the
aerodynamic forces initiated torsional motion and self-excited these oscillations until the collapse.
In general many theories for the collapse of the TNB are build on nonlinear phenomenons, such as nonlinearity of
the cable system and nonlinear theory for wind-induced excitations. For further description of these theories see for
instance Arena & Lacarbonara (2012) and Herrmann & Hauger (1973). Some theories are looking more into the
aeroelastic behaviour of the bridge and aerodynamics of vortex induced vibrations and ﬂutter due to the shape of the
deck and the angle of attack of the wind (Schewe 1988). Other explanations for the collapse of the TNB are related to
structural failure and resonance between the oscillation mode of the bridge and the excited wind (Arioli & Gazzola
2013).
It appears that the explanations for the collapse have developed throughout the decades from being caused by vortex-
induced vibration to be related to ﬂutter and galloping instability with self-excited oscillations (Schewe 1988). According
to Schewe (1988) vortex resonance excitation were by Kármán and Dunn (Farquharson 1950) belived to be the main
cause to the failure, while Schewe himself believed that small oscillations due to subharmonic resonance could initiate
the self-excited large galloping torsional instability.

Appendix C
Galloping
Galloping is the phenomena covering the 1 degree-of-freedom (DOF) instability of bluff structures in winds and
currents. All non-circular cross sections are susceptible to galloping (Simiu & Scanlan 1978). The theory is traditionally
covering structures oscillating perpendicularly to the wind ﬂow, referred to as cross-ﬂow vibrations. An attempt to
transform the theory to account for the torsional oscillations case is made by Blevins (2001) and is presented in this
appendix.
In most analysis quasisteady ﬂuid dynamics are utilized and the ﬂuid force acting on the structure is thereby exclusively
determined by the instantaneous relative velocity. This assumption is only valid if the frequency of the periodic
components of ﬂuid force is sufﬁciently larger than the natural frequency of the structural. This often occurs at high
reduced velocities above Vr > 20.

Experimental Determination of Mass Moment of
Inertia
To validate the experimental set-up, the mass moment of inertia is determined experimentally and compared with
the calculated mass moment of inertia presented in section 4.2.3. The experiments are conducted by changing the
placement of the four 400 g mass components on the support rods at the ends of the model. The inﬂuence of this change
on the torsional frequency is examined by analysing the recorded data. The torsional movement of the model is excited
by hand and positive damped oscillations are logged when no wind is applied. The torsional frequency of the system
is found from these loggings. The mass moment of inertia is calculated for the four 400 g mass components on the
cylindrical rod alone for each placement. The mass moment of inertia of the rest of the model stays constant and the
