Abstract
Over the course of this master’s thesis, modelling of features found in glacial clay till including fractures,
sand lenses and macropores was performed in order to qualitatively and quantitatively assess their effects
on flow and solute transport. Models of different scales, dimensions and complexity were used to obtain
an understanding of the individual and combined influence of these features.
Individual studies examined the effects of specific flow rate, the aperture, angle, spacing and pene-
tration of fractures, the presence and angle of sand lenses, and macropores on flow and solute transport.
Features from the individual studies were combined in a configuration based on a field site in Havdrup
from the CLAYFRAC project, and modelling of different flow scenarios was performed for qualitative
interpretation of solute breakthrough in the different scenarios over a period of 20 years.
A model was constructed based closely on an PESTPORE project infiltration experiment performed
at a field site in Flakkebjerg, the focus of which was flow and transport in macropores. Results from
the simulations were intended to be compared to field data in a quantitative assessment, but modelling
complications ultimately rendered this unsuccessful. However, there is potential for this model to be
further developed in the future.
Accurate modelling of flow and transport in heterogeneous clay till is beneficial for assessing the
vulnerability of clay types to pesticide leaching, and more broadly to research and decision-making in
the field of water supply.

Preface
This master’s thesis was prepared at the department of Environmental Engineering at the Technical
University of Denmark (DTU). The work was conducted in the period from August 27th 2018 to Jan-
uary 27th 2019 and corresponds to 30 ECTS. The supervisors of this project were:
Massimo Rolle
Associate Professor
Department of Environmental Engineering
DTU, Technical University of Denmark
Klaus Mosthaf
Researcher
Department of Environmental Engineering
DTU, Technical University of Denmark
Peter R. Jørgensen
Director/Project Leader
PJ-Bluetech ApS
The work of this thesis is part of CLAYFRAC, a collaborative research project to map Denmark’s
clay till in terms of vulnerability to pesticide leaching with work by researchers from institutions the
Geological Survey of Denmark and Greenland (GEUS), DTU, University of Copenhagen (KU) and the
private company PJ-Bluetech. I am thankful to Jens Aamand from GEUS to have been a part of the
CLAYFRAC meetings and for getting the opportunity to hear about the work of other researchers.
Thank you also to Peter, whose extensive knowledge and experience with clay tills helped to guide
this work with ideas and advice.
My biggest thanks go to Klaus and Massimo for all their help and guidance, for spending many
hours answering my questions and always bringing a good atmosphere to our meetings.
Kongens Lyngby, January 27, 2019

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Groundwater
An important resource agriculturally as well as for municipal and industrial use, groundwater is the
main source of freshwater for many countries in Europe and North America (Zekster, 2000).
Denmark is no exception in this regard, relying almost entirely on groundwater for its domestic water
supply, irrigation and fish farming, and industry, with a total of 800 million m3 of water abstracted
every year (GEUS, 2018b).
Importantly, almost 100% of Denmark’s drinking water is supplied by
groundwater (Naturstyrelsen, 2018), the quality of which is considered good, with minimal purification
required apart from aeration, pH adjustment and filtration (GEUS, 2018b).
1.2
Groundwater contamination
The modern use of chemicals in industry, especially for agriculture, is an ongoing threat to the water
supply of many countries including Denmark, with nitrates from fertilisers and an array of pesticides
of particular concern (GEUS, 2018b). These compounds can leach into the soil after application, where
they can eventually infiltrate to the groundwater table. In Denmark, the contamination of aquifers has
led to the closing of an increasing number of waterworks in recent years (GEUS, 2018b)(Jørgensen et al.,
2002), due to risks to human health including damage to the reproductive organs, lungs, and the nervous,
immune and endocrine systems, as well as increased incidence of birth defects and cancer (Rosenfeld &
Feng, 2011)(Landrigan & Claudio, 2008). Legislation at the state and EU levels determines acceptable
concentrations of different chemicals in groundwater, which are called quality criteria; exceedance of
these concentrations will result in the decommissioning of a water supply well (Erbach, 2012)(The
European Parliament and the Council of the European Union, 2008).
Figure 1.1 represents the detection of pesticides and their degradation products in active water
supply wells in Denmark in the five year period 2012-2016.
Red points show wells where at least
one pesticide or degradation product was detected above the quality criteria (>0.1 µg/L), blue points
show wells where at least one pesticide or degradation product was detected under the quality criteria
(0.01-0.1 µg/L), and grey points show wells with no pesticides or degradation products detected.
As shown by the map, pesticides and breakdown products were found in many of the wells in the
capital region in particular, and in other regions with relatively high population density where there
are also many wells. These regions coincide with the areas where the surface geology is predominantly
clay, as shown in Figure 1.2.
As in a significant proportion of the northern hemisphere, Denmark’s upper geological layer is
glacial clay-rich till (Jørgensen et al., 2002)(Kessler, Klint, Nilsson, & Bjerg, 2012). While clay was
long thought to provide protection to underlying aquifers due to its low hydraulic conductivity and
results from small-scale laboratory testing (Fredericia, 1990), aquifer contamination has nevertheless
occurred in many countries (Pankow & Cherry, 1996)(GEUS, 2018a).
This is due to the presence
of inhomogeneities frequently found in clay, including fractures, sand lenses and macropores such as
those created by earthworms and plant roots, which can act as preferred paths for flow and conduct
contaminants to the aquifer (Jørgensen, Helstrup, Urup, & Seifert, 2004)(Jørgensen & Fredericia, 1992).
Not only do these features have higher hydraulic conductivities than the surrounding clay matrix, leading
to advective transport of solutes to greater depths, they can also facilitate diffusion into the surrounding

1.3
CLAYFRAC project
A research project called CLAYFRAC was begun in 2017, coordinated by the Geological Survey of
Denmark and Greenland (GEUS) that involves the collaboration of the additional institutions the
Technical University of Denmark (DTU), the University of Copenhagen (KU), and the private company
PJ Bluetech. CLAYFRAC aims to produce a vulnerability map of Denmark, showing the areas most
susceptible to pesticide leaching (Aamand, 2017). The pesticides in focus are MCPA, bentazon and
tebuconazole. The project involves polymorphological mapping of Zealand and selection of sites for
field investigations, geological characterisation of the field sites including biopores, laboratory testing of
the pesticides to determine sorption and degradation behaviour in clay matrix and fractures, hydraulic
testing of flow and transport in large undisturbed columns (LUCs) with focus on fractures and biopores,
and pesticide transport modelling for selected polymorphological types, aiming to rank clay types by
vulnerability (Aamand, 2017).
The work package this thesis fits into is the pesticide transport modelling; this part of the project
focuses on polymorphological types, using modelling of flow and transport to predict pesticide leaching
and eventually rank clay types by vulnerability (Aamand, 2017).
Investigations performed for this
thesis are intended to provide understanding of the influence of fractures, macropore and sand lenses
in clay till that can aid in the realisation of these goals.

1.4
PESTPORE project
A collaborative research project from 2011 to 2014 between PJ Bluetech, KU and Aarhus University, the
focus of PESTPORE was the deeper macropores in clay and their role in the transport of pesticides from
surface to aquifer. Of particular concern was the connectivity of the deeper macropores to the largest
macropores in the upper depths, and how this can facilitate transport. Findings were that leaching did
occur into shallow groundwater, but there is a low risk of leaching into the deeper aquifer through these
pathways (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2017a). Results of an infiltration experiment performed for
this project present an opportunity for replication in a simulation focused on the macropores.
PESTPORE II, a follow up study to the project, is currently underway and will further investigate
deeper macropore systems (Department of Plant and Environmental Sciences, 2017). Figures 1.4 and
1.5 show examples of macropores and branching roots respectively, stained blue from a dye tracer test
performed during investigations for PESTPORE II.

1.5 Aim

1.5
Aim
The objective of this work is to show, both qualitatively and quantitatively, the effects of different
feature properties on flow and transport in clay till. These features include fractures, sand lenses and
macropores.
To achieve this, 2D and 3D models of increasing scale and complexity will be used to investigate
the effects of features in isolation and in combination.
Models for qualitative analysis will be built using parameters and variables based on values within
ranges observed in the field, while the qualitative study will be closely modelled on the specific field
site for comparison.
1.6
Overall method
The thesis can be arranged into four main groups of study of flow and solute transport. The first consists
of relatively small-scale, simple two-dimensional (2D) simulations varying and combining features such
as fractures and sand lenses. The second is a group of small-scale three-dimensional (3D) simulations
of a macropore, based on the roots and earthworm burrows found in the upper clay. These two groups
of study are of a theoretical nature, intended to isolate the effects of one property at once. Third
is a larger-scale 2D model that combines many of the features from the smaller-scale 2D simulations.
This model is loosely based on a site in Havdrup, Denmark, excavated for CLAYFRAC, and while
features are arranged similarly to those observed at the site, the results are intended to be interpreted
qualitatively rather than quantitatively.
The fourth group of study is closely based on a bromide
infiltration experiment carried out in Flakkebjerg, Denmark, for which data were available as part of
the PESTPORE project.
A description of the modelling approach is found in Chapter 2. The subsequent chapters concern
one study group each, discussing their setup and results.

2.1
Governing equations
The models were given both a flow and a transport component. Steady state conditions are assumed for
the flow model, while the transport model has transient conditions. The two are linked by the velocity
field, which is computed in the flow model; the flow model is therefore required to be solved before the
transport model. Both models are defined by their own governing equation.
2.1.1
Flow
The flow model, with steady state conditions assumed, is based on Darcy’s law describing one-dimensional
(1D) flow through a saturated porous medium as given by Fetter (2001):
q = −K ∆h
L
(2.1)
Where q (m/s) is specific discharge, K (m/s) is hydraulic conductivity and ∆h/L (-) is the hydraulic
gradient denoting the difference in hydraulic head (m) over distance (m) in 1D.
In the domains of the model such as the clay matrix or the sand lenses, Equation 2.1 can be written
in its 3D form (Fetter, 2001):
q = −K∇h
(2.2)
Where q and K will vary depending on the medium in question.
Using the Darcy flux in a mass balance gives the governing equation for flow, which balances the
flux difference with regards to a control volume with the source within it (Wang & Anderson, 1995):
ρSs
∂h
∂t −ρ∇· (K∇h) = W
(2.3)
Where ρ (kg/m3) is the density of the fluid, Ss (1/m) is the specific storage, t (s) is time and W
(kg/m3/s) is the source term. Since the fluid used is water, which is assumed incompressible, ρ can be
divided out, and W is set to zero since there is assumed to be no source. With the flow model assumed
steady-state the equation can be simplified to:
−∇· (K∇h) = 0
(2.4)
2.1.2
Transport
For the transport of solute, the PDE taking into account diffusion, dispersion, advection, sorption and
degradation, modified from Tang (1981), was assigned as the governing equation:
n∂C
∂t + ρb
∂sm
∂t −∇· (nD∇C) + ∇· (nvC) + R = 0
(2.5)
Where n (-) is porosity, C (kg/m3) is solute concentration, ρb (kg/m3) is bulk density, sm (-) is mass
of solute adsorbed per mass of solid in the matrix, D (m2/s) is the hydrodynamic dispersion coefficient,

v (m/s) is velocity and R (kg/m3/s) represents the degradation term, which is different depending on
whether zero order kinetics, first order decay or Monod kinetics are used.
Processes of sorption and degradation are disregarded in the simulations, corresponding to use of
either a conservative tracer such as bromide or a pesticide like bentazone, which behaves similarly. Zero

n∂C
∂t −∇· (nD∇C) + ∇· (nvC) = 0
(2.6)
In this simplified equation, diffusion and dispersion are included in the term with the hydrodynamic
dispersion coefficient D, while the term with the velocity v describes advection.
2.2
Geometry
In this section important features of the 2D and 3D models will be introduced. An examples of each
type of geometry is used to describe the direction of flow and the incorporation of fractures, macropores
and sand lenses.
2.2.1
2D setup
Shown in Figure 2.1 is an example of 2D model geometry with fractures, sand lenses and clay matrix. In
this example, the main block 5 m wide and 1 m high is the clay matrix, with flow occurring vertically,
entering from the upper horizontal boundary and exiting at the lower. The two thinner rectangles
within the matrix are sand lenses, and three vertical fractures are represented as lines, also within the
matrix. It is important to note that the fractures are discrete inputs, meaning they are incorporated
as lines and therefore have no physical width in the model geometry; the aperture is included in the
simulation through the equations used for the boundary conditions, as discussed below.
Figure 2.1: Example of 2D model geometry, showing the clay matrix, sand lenses, fractures and spacers
At the top and bottom of the main block two rectangles of 0.01 m height were included, which
are used in all models and will be referred to as spacers. The top spacer is included so as to allow
the flow to develop naturally at the inflow of the model, since without it water and solute would be
forced into the fractures and clay matrix at the same rate over the entire inflow boundary, while in
reality a strong preferential flow in the fractures is expected. The advantage of the bottom spacer is
for the measurement of flux at the outflow boundary, which is greatly simplified in comparison to its
calculation without it.
The 2D studies involve model setups with different dimensions as well as changes to the properties
and combination of features such as sand lenses and fractures. The individual geometry for each study
will be shown in the relevant section.

2.3
Boundary conditions
Boundary conditions are applied to the models, of special interest being the lower (outflow) boundary
and upper (inflow) boundaries. Conditions were also applied to the fractures and macropores. Different
conditions are used in the flow and transport models.
2.3.1
Flow model
The outflow boundary always has a hydraulic head h set to 1 m. At the upper boundary, either the
specific flow rate, q or the hydraulic head is specified.
Recalling Equation 2.1, since the hydraulic
conductivity K is defined throughout the model domain, if two out of the specific flow rate q, upper
head and lower head (together making ∆h) are specified, the third can be calculated.
In one study, different values of q are used as the inflow boundary condition to compare the effects
of flow rate on breakthrough at the outflow and concentration distribution. For all other studies a
value of h is designated in order to ensure ∆h is equal to the distance from the inflow boundary to the
outflow boundary, so that the hydraulic gradient is equal to 1; for example, in Figure 2.1 a head of 2.02
m would be used at the inflow boundary.
Conditions applied at the fractures are a means to calculate the flow within them. First the fracture

2.5
Numerical solution
The flow and transport models described above are solved using the finite element method (FEM),
where the model domain is broken into smaller parts called elements. The PDEs applied to the whole
domain are discretised on each of these elements, and then recombined in to a system of equations for
the final solution, calculated from the model’s initial values. The solver trials functions in the PDEs in
steps, with the error of approximation aimed to be minimised. Steady state problems are solved with
algebraic equations, and transient problems with ordinary differential equations (Strang & Fix, 1973).
The elements in the model are represented by a triangular (in 2D) or tetrahedral (in 3D) mesh,
the fineness of which can be adjusted at different domains, boundaries and points. Mesh refinement is
important, since a fine mesh will be more computationally taxing, but a too coarse mesh may lead to a
solution with poor resolution, or even an inability of the the programme to find a solution. An optimal
mesh provides sufficient detail in the solution while maintaining efficiency with regards to simulation
time. In general, a finer mesh was required on boundaries such as that between the matrix and the
fracture, macropore and spacer, since the matrix has a much lower hydraulic conductivity than these
other features.
The software used to build and solve the models is COMSOL Multiphysics 5.3a, a powerful tool
that can be used to model geometries, apply material and physical properties as well as underlying
expressions, and perform studies including stationary and transient (COMSOL, 2012).

CHAPTER 3
Sand lenses and fractures
A 5 m by 1 m 2D base model of saturated, fractured clay was constructed, and adapted to study
the effects of various fracture and sand lens properties on breakthrough behaviour and concentration
distribution of a tracer flowing vertically downwards through the model domain. Properties and features
that were varied were: flow rate; aperture, angle and spacing of fractures; and the angle and presence
versus absence of sand lenses connecting fractures. This base model was adjusted to have dimensions of
5 m by 3 m in order to study additional features: different fracture apertures at different depths; and
different lengths of fractures such that not all would penetrate the full depth of the clay matrix of the
model.
It is important to note that while these models were built using parameters within reasonable ranges
for clay tills and features found in Denmark wherever possible, the studies are not based on any specific
real-life site. Their use is analytical and theoretical, and are intended as a method for isolating the
effects of individual changes to features.
While selected figures are used in this chapter to illustrate the results, Appendix A can be referred
to for the full set.

3.9
Summary
These 2D studies fulfilled their purpose of providing important understanding of the impacts of the
variation of fracture and sand lens properties.
It was found that specific flow rate and aperture could have a large influence on the breakthrough,
although there was a smaller difference between the results with higher values. Higher specific flow rates
and larger apertures resulted in a faster breakthrough, while at the smallest values tested there was no
increase in relative concentration in the simulation period. The spacing of fractures is not significant,
except when the spacing is small enough to allow diffusion from adjacent fractures to overlap; this
also depends on the simulation time. Fracture angle does not appear to be an important property for
values within the normal observed range, with almost no difference between the breakthrough curves.
However, deep fractures with angles outside this range have been found at Havdrup, and for these values

CHAPTER 4
Macropores
A small-scale study was performed to simulate flow through a macropore, in this case in 3D. While a 2D
model assumes that there are no changes in the third dimension, the cylindrical shape of a macropore
does not allow a good representation in 2D; the diffusion from the surface in all directions is of particular
concern in this regard.
Macropores, most commonly found as earthworm burrows or root channels, have been found to
be important to the rate and behaviour of infiltration of water into soil (Smettem & Collis-George,
1985)(Booltink, Hatano, & Bouma, 1993), and are therefore important elements to understand for
inclusion in larger models. Earthworm burrows are most common in the uppermost soil layers, and
their radius can vary from 2 to 5 mm. While active root channels are also predominantly found at
shallower depths, abandoned root channels are present much deeper; the radius of these macropores
can vary from <1 to 6 mm (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2017a).
4.1
Macropore and fracture with equivalent volume
Shown in Figure 4.1 is the geometry of the macropore model, which has a matrix block 0.07 m by
0.07 m by 0.1 m and top and bottom spacers each 0.005 m thick. The macropore has a radius of 0.4
mm, which is a realistic value for a fine root channel (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2017a), and it is
implemented within the matrix from the bottom of the top spacer to the top of the bottom spacer.
Figure 4.1: Geometry of the small-scale macropore model
While the radius used in the simulation is reasonable for a small macropore, earthworm burrows
and larger root channels can have radii of up to 5 and 6 mm respectively (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet,
2017a). Simulation attempts with radii above 0.4 mm were not successful, due to problems with the
mesh in COMSOL Multiphysics. However, it must be noted that the radius used in simulations is the
hydraulic radius and not the measured radius. A physical macropore will have an inconsistent radius
with depth, with some places narrower and others wider, as well as an imperfectly circular cross section.
The hydraulic radius is a theoretical radius that is associated with the right velocity when used in the
calculation of hydraulic conductivity. Therefore, the value for the radius used in simulations need not
be the same as those measured physically and is usually smaller in comparison.
==PAGE:46==
32
4 Macropores
The very large difference between the matrix conductivity and macropore conductivity required a
mesh sufficiently fine to resolve dramatically large gradients at the boundary, caused for example by
the very different hydraulic conductivities. At the same time, a very fine mesh can also cause problems
with parameters that change very little, or not at all with rounding, such that changes made by the
solver are interpreted by COMSOL to produce no difference between time steps in the solution. To run
this simulation, these limitations must be balanced by carefully constructing a mesh with appropriate
fineness at the different boundaries. Nevertheless, 0.4 mm was the largest radius that was able to be
used successfully.
Considering this limitation on radius, the macropore model was compared with a 3D fracture model
in order to assess how the flow and transport behaviour would differ between them. The dimensions of

different aperture was used in each case to fulfil the volume requirement. Figure 4.2 shows the geometry

Seasonal flow
With an adjustment made to reflect an additional aspect of reality, another simulation was run using
two different flow rates. While the annual flow rate of 120 mm/year was maintained on average, high
and low flow rates of 200 and 40 mm/year respectively were each applied for one half of the year. This

wetter and drier period (Cappelen, 2018).
Due to the different flow rates, there was a higher head difference in the case with the higher flow,
with a maximum of 3.26 compared to 1.45 m (with a minimum of 1 m at the outflow boundary as

the higher q producing a much steeper curve. The lower flow rate actually produces a slight decrease
in the relative concentration at the outflow boundary, which is likely due to back diffusion.
After
approximately 15 years the curve for the low q begins to flatten as the relative concentration in the
matrix approaches 1 and there is less back diffusion due to the smaller concentration gradient from the

flow rate allows the solute to diffuse, while less solute is arriving at the outflow boundary due to the
lower flow. At the end of one cycle of high and low flow, which corresponds to one full year, the relative
concentration in the first half of the simulation period is the same as that of the constant flow curve. In

curve more, reducing the solute reaching the outflow boundary and the relative concentration there. As
the time approaches 20 years both curves approach 1, overlapping once more.

Summary
The models used in this chapter are more complex than those in Chapter 3, constructed as a combination
of fractures and sand lenses with different properties depending on depth.
Based on the Havdrup
excavation, the models represent possible scenarios with different flow conditions.
The results from the study show the long time scales involved in vertical groundwater flow, with
these simulations running over a period of 20 years. The solute was conveyed through the fractures
quickly compared to the advancement of the solute front, which reflects the increased risk to ground-
water contamination posed by the higher conductivity fractures (Jørgensen & Fredericia, 1992)(Danish
Environmental Protection Agency, 2013). Allocating the annual rainfall more heavily to one half of the
year resulted in seasonal differences in the breakthrough curves, but the long-term trend was the same.
A significant difference in diffusion behaviour was also observed between the seasons. Including lateral
flow in one direction delayed the breakthrough curve somewhat; however, the shape of the curve was
very similar to the constant flow model, despite the changes to the geometry of the sand lenses.
The findings from Chapter 3 were useful for both the design of the model and the analysis of the
results. Knowing for example that fracture angle most commonly found has little effect on the results
meant that this property could be disregarded when deciding the geometry of the model, showing that
such knowledge can lead to greater efficiency in modelling. The results from the sand lens angle study
were helpful in interpreting the uneven advancement of solute, since it was found that this property has
an effect on the head throughout the model domain and can lead to a different distribution of solute.
It should be noted that these simulations involve a chemical such as a tracer or pesticide with no
sorption or degradation behaviour, as well as a constant concentration and source, meaning that the
results are conservative with regards to real-life scenarios.

breakthrough curve in Figure 6.2.

m/s, which was used as the boundary condition at the inflow. The outflow boundary had a fixed head

6.3 Summary
53
6.3
Summary
This study attempted to accurately model a real infiltration test based on its site characteristics and data.
Setbacks associated with meshing along curved surfaces, model divergence and long simulation times
necessitated the gradual simplification of the model, and even after extensive adjustments and many
simulations, the breakthrough curve obtained from the field experiment was not able to be replicated.
Further adjustments could possibly lead to a simulated breakthrough more similar to the one from
the field, which is proposed as further work in this area. One possible improvement could be reducing
the number of elements by using an extruded mesh, which involves meshing a cross section and then
stretching the elements until they are very long in the z direction, forming triangular prisms. This could
be applied here since in much of the model it is not expected that there will be significant variation
in the x and y directions, although a high mesh resolution would still be required where gradients are
large. It could also be useful to try a different approach to flow in the macropore, using for example
the Brinkman equation. The Brinkman equation is in a way a combination of Darcy’s law and the
Navier-Stokes equations, describing either slow flow in porous media (as in the matrix) or fast flow in
channels (as in the macropore) determined by properties such as conductivity and porosity (Liu, Patil,
& Narusawa, 2007). Use of the Navier-Stokes equation in the macropore or of the Brinkman equation
was outside the scope of this work, but presents an opportunity for future extension.
Successful modelling of connected macropores that can accurately replicate results from the field
would be very useful for the characterisation of clay tills. Due to the prevalence of macropores such
as earthworm burrows and root channels, especially in the upper layers, an understanding of the flow
and transport behaviour in these feature could provide crucial information regarding the vulnerability
of clays to the leaching of contaminants such as pesticides. It is important to investigate macropores
both in a simulation such as the one described in this chapter, and in combination with other features
such as fractures and sand lenses.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Investigations into the effects on flow and transport of fractures, sand lenses and macropores within clay
till were conducted with 2D and 3D models. By isolating features and varying different characteristics,
simulations were able to indicate the properties with the largest influence.
Simulations of fractures showed there was a strong preferential flow through these features compared
to the surrounding matrix, although diffusion was clearly occurring in each case, both from the fractures
and the top spacer into the matrix. Small fracture apertures below approximately 10 µm dramatically
restricted flow in the model and delayed the breakthrough, while larger apertures up to 75 µm resulted
in clear preferential pathways through the fractures rather than the adjacent clay matrix. Fracture
angle did not have a significant effect on the breakthrough for angles within the range usually observed
in the field; however, simulations using angles outside this range showed a delayed breakthrough asso-
ciated with angles further from the vertical. The spacing of fractures was found to be important when
the distance between them was less than the distance solute penetrated into the matrix by diffusion. A
different breakthrough behaviour was observed in the simulation with the smallest spacing of 0.05 m
compared to all other values. Simulations of different fracture apertures at different depths showed that
there is a correlation between the proportion of a fracture with a larger aperture and a faster break-
through. Interestingly, as long as the proportion was the same, there did not seem to be a significant
effect from the order in which the sections with different aperture sizes were organised. Finally, the
addition of dead-end fractures delayed the breakthrough compared to a model with only throughgoing
fractures, since solute transported into them could not reach the outflow boundary. A higher rate of
diffusion was observed in the dead-end fractures compared to those that were throughgoing.
Sand lenses were found to have an important role in the flow and transport of solutes. They were
shown to dramatically change the breakthrough behaviour and concentration distribution when added
to a model with three previously unconnected fractures; with the fractures unconnected, the solute did
not reach the outflow boundary even after 150 days, while the addition of sand lenses created a flow
path that quickly facilitated transport with a rapid breakthrough. Increasing the angle of sand lenses
was found to affect the steepest part of the breakthrough curve, with more solute becoming transported
into the lenses rather than continuing to flow through the fracture to the outflow.
A small-scale 3D model was used to simulate flow and transport in a macropore compared to
fractures of the same volume but of varying lengths. The breakthrough curve of the macropore was
very steep and rapid compared to the longer fractures, which were more s-shaped. The breakthrough
and concentration distribution of the shortest fracture were most similar to those of the macropore,
showing a clear preferential flow path in these features. In comparison, the behaviour of models with
the longer fractures indicated that the small apertures were restricting the flow through them, as the
solute front advanced quite uniformly.
The individual features analysed in the small-scale 2D models were combined in a model based on
a profile of an excavation in Havdrup, Denmark, conducted for the CLAYFRAC project. The model
included different layers of angled sand lenses, fractures with different spacings, apertures and depths,
and varying clay hydraulic conductivities with depth. Three separate simulations were conducted over
a 20 year period, with a constant flow rate, seasonal flow rates, and constant flow rate with lateral flow
added. The seasonal flow simulation showed very different concentration distributions for the high and
low flow periods, with diffusion much more dominant in the latter. The breakthrough curve became
stepped, with steep increases for periods of high flow and flat or even decreasing sections for those of low
flow, but the overall long-term trend was very close to that of the constant flow simulation. Including

56

lateral flow caused a delay to the breakthrough, although as the relative concentration approached 1
the difference had almost disappeared. These simulations were closer to reality than the smaller-scale
2D simulations, using for example realistic annual infiltration rates and hydraulic conductivities from
the field; however, the results should still be interpreted qualitatively since breakthrough data was not
available for comparison.
The initial design of the Flakkebjerg model was closely based on site characteristics recorded dur-
ing the PESTPORE project, with a model of infiltration through macropores from the surface to an
underground well. Setbacks with model convergence and meshing difficulties led to the model being
simplified while still representative of the scenario. While the simulations could be completed success-
fully, the breakthrough curve from the field experiment could not be replicated with the model setup
used. While the quantitative part of this work was not successful, this work has significant potential for
further investigation and application, since macropores can play an important role in flow and transport.
Individually, the studies demonstrate the effects that physical properties of features such as fractures,
macropores and sand lenses can have on flow and transport in clay. When combined together, more
complex interactions are demonstrated. Some results can streamline future modelling, since if a para-
meter is known to have a negligible effect it can be disregarded in the design of the model. Modelling
with a combination of features has significant potential for the simulation of scenarios at real field sites
and comparison with measured data, provided sufficient data for the setup is available. Further work
is required for the macropore model, both for comparison with measured data and for inclusion in a
model with features investigated in the 2D studies.
Sorption and degradation behaviour can easily be implemented in the model, which would allow the
modelling of different chemicals such as pesticides. There would be a difference in the breakthrough and
concentration distribution with the use of such chemicals, and the model could be tailored to a specific
scenario, chemical of concern or even a combination of chemicals. The solute used in these simulations
had the properties of a conservative tracer or a pesticide with very little sorption and degradation such
as bentazone, but in any case they represent the worst case scenario, where a contaminant is not slowed
or prevented from reaching the groundwater. Many pesticides do undergo significant degradation or
sorption or both, so for a particular scenario it may be useful to include these properties.
A model that can replicate flow and transport results from the field would be a powerful tool for
risk assessment, and could play an important role in the classification of clay types by vulnerability
to pesticide leaching for the CLAYFRAC project. Further development of the models created for this
thesis could be beneficial for research and decision-making.
