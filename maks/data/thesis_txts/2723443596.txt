Background: 
Øresund: 
Øresund, which corresponds to ICES subdivision 23, is a strait between Denmark and Sweeden connecting 
the Baltic Sea to the Kattegat. Øresund stretches from the northern boundary between Gilleleje and 
Kullaberg to the southern boundary between Stevns Klint and Falsterbo (Wikipedia, 2022).  
Øresund have a great variation in water depth, salinity, and sediment, which provide many different 
habitats for a variety of unique fish and shellfish species (Sørensen et al, 2016). The southern part of 
Øresund and the coastal part of northern Øresund is shallow (10-15 meters), while water can reach a depth 
of up to 45 meters in northern part of Øresund. 
The brackish water flowing in from south from the Baltic Sea into Øresund have a lower salinity and is 
lighter than the heavy, salty water flowing in from Kattegat (Miljøstyrelsen, n.d.a). This results in a halocline 
in Øresund, hence mixing between the layers are at a minimal. Water below the halocline can have a 
salinity up two 33 promille, while the water above can have a salinity more than thrice as low (down to 8 
promille) (miljødata.dk, n.d.). Additionally, the absence of mixing result in less oxygenated water below the 
halocline, where oxygen depletion can occur in period with less inflow of water from Kattegat 
(Miljøstyrelsen, n.d.). The water temperature remains fairly high between 8-13 °C (measured at 25 meters 
depth) even in the deeper parts of Øresund (miljødata.dk, n.d.)).  
 
Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua): 
The cod spawn in schools from December to March in deeper water multiple places in Øresund (Sørensen 
et al, 2016). The pelagic eggs hatches after three to four weeks, and after three month the larvae moves to 
the bottom and become demersal. The cod moves to deeper water at fall when it reaches 12-15 cm in 
length. By adulthood (around age two to three) the cod lives in the demersal zone, feeds at the bottom, but 
hunts and migrate in pelagic zone (figure 1). According to interviews of Danish fishers there is a great 
variety in the propagation of adult cod in Øresund, but mostly spawns in the most northern part of 
Øresund.

mio) since 2005 (ICES, 2022a). This has caused the spawning stock biomass (SSB) to decline a couple of 
years after and the SSB has stayed below the reference point of the biomass (Btrigger) at maximum 
sustainable yield (MSY) since 2007.

In Øresund (sd23), where trawling has been banned since 1932, the recreational landings contributed to 
around 1/10, 1/8 and 1/3 in 2001,2011 and 2021 respectively relative the total landings for sd22-24 
(landings for sd23 taken from ICES (2022b)). Hence, the contribution of landings in Øresund relative to the 
total landings of the western Baltic cod stock have increased in the past two decades, due to the decrease 
in TAC for the whole area. The recreational harvest of cod in Øresund from Denmark alone was estimated 
to 154 tonnes in 2021 (data provided by DTU Aqua). 
 
Recreational fishing: 
Recreational fishing is a popular leisure activity, especially in Denmark where it was estimated that around 
one out of ten of the Danish citizen had went angling within the last year in 2010 (Olesen et al, 2020). There 
exist two kinds of recreational fishers in Denmark, anglers and passive gear fishers. Anglers fish with fishing 
rod, and if the angler is above the age of 18 and not yet retired, the angler is, in Denmark, permitted to 
carry a fishing license (Fiskeristyrelsen, n.d.a). passive gear fishers often use traps or gill nets. When fishing

with gillnets or similar gear in Denmark, you must carry a ‘recreational fishing license’ (Fiskeristyrelsen, 
n.d.b). Number of license issued varies between the four license type ‘angling license for a year’, ‘angling 
license for a week’, ‘angling license for a day’ and ‘passive gear license’, but is dominated by ‘angling license 
for a year’ with more than a 150.000 issued in 2021 (Fiskeristyrelsen, n.d.c).   
Certain regulations exist for recreational fishers and can vary between years, within years and for different 
areas and can affect the catch of cod by recreational fishing in Øresund. The closing period in Øresund is 
during the spawning time of cod in February and March (including second half of January from 2022), 
where bag limit is typically set lower than rest of the year. The bag limit is a limitation of the number of cod 
a fisher may retain per day. The purpose is to minimize the number of cod harvested in recreational fishing 
that may contribute to the total fishing mortality of the stock. Bag limit was implemented in 2017 and 
varies between most years. The minimum landing size is 35cm in total length of cod and used as an attempt 
to secure cod to reach maturation and spawn before being caught (Fiskeristyrelsen, n.d.g). Based on otolith 
age determination from cod caught in Øresund this will in most cases corresponding to age below 2 (Olesen 
et al, 2020). An overview on fish base (FishBase, n.d.) of the length and age at maturity of cod indicates that 
fish caught at minimum landing size may not have reach maturation stage yet.  
Back in 2016 Thünen Institute of Baltic Sea Fisheries (Thünen-OF) evaluated the effect of two different 
management option, namely ‘closing season’ and ‘bag limit’, for the recreational fishery in the western 
Baltic Sea (Thünen-Institut (OF), 2016). It was assessed using German available data from 2005 to 2015. The 
data consisted of the angler catch share from total recreational catch for different number of retained cod 
per day within ‘charter boat, private boat ad trolling anglers’ and within ‘surf anglers and wading’. E.g. 
angler catching 1 cod per day shared around 375 tonnes of cod catches from charter boat, private boat and 
trolling anglers of the total recreational catch (of 2534 tonnes), while angler catching 15 cod per day shared 
around 50 tonnes of cod catches from charter boat, private boat and trolling anglers of the total 
recreational catch in 2015. The mean cod catch from 2013 to 2015 was estimated to 2558 tonnes, however, 
with a great yearly variation. It was evaluated that closing season from February to mid-March could lead 
to the potential estimated reduction of catch of 292 tonnes in average, corresponding to 11% reduction. 
The effect of bag limit was estimated with a bag limit from 3 to 10 cod per day, where a bag limit of 5 cod 
per day would result in a reduction (together with closed season) of 900 tonnes, corresponding to a 
reduction of 47%. The effect of bag limit varied greatly between the years, and for instance, a bag limit of 5 
lead to a potential greater reduction in harvest in 2014 compared to a bag limit of 3 in 2013. Reduction may 
hence depend on availability of cod and weather conditions.

Article manuscript (title: Standardizing catch rates on cod caught by 
recreational fishers – Angling from charter boats in a sound) 
Abstract:  
Catches from recreational fishing are increasingly included in stock assessments worldwide. Ambiguities 
remain in relation to treating and modelling observational catch data from recreational fisheries. To 
accommodate, this study standardized retained catch of cod (Gadus morhua) from onsite surveys of Danish 
recreational fishers angling from charter boats in the sound Øresund by a generalized linear model. The 
relationships between catch per unit effort (CPUE) to five variables related to the angler (age, sex, license, 
target species and effort), three related to time of fishing (duration, quarter, year) and one related to 
management regulations (bag limit) were investigated with distributions between multiple candidates 
(including Poisson, negative binomial, zero-inflated negative binomial, Tweedie and hurdle). Model 
predictions had smaller standard errors on the mean CPUE compared to the observed means within each 
year. The model predicted twice as low CPUE in 2019 (0.75 ± 0.5)(mean ± s.e.) compared to the years 
included in the model (2017-2020), and more than twice as low CPUE in the fourth quarter (1 ±0.4)(mean ± 
s.e.) compared to other quarters. Bag limit had a significant effect on CPUE, with the greatest impact on 
CPUE in those years (year 2020 and 2018) and quarters (second and third quarter) where the CPUE was 
relatively high compared to the year 2019 and the fourth quarter respectively. This approach can be used 
to investigate influence from different factors on catch rates of recreational fishers, including bag limit, and 
enlighten the impact on regulatory tools in recreational fisheries. It is suggested to study release rates of 
cod to investigate year-to-year variation on the entire catch (instead of retained catch). 
 
Introduction: 
Recreational fishing is globally a popular leisure activity (Lewing et al, 2021 and Grundelund et al, 2021), 
and it has been estimated that recreational fishers catch 47.1 billion fish annually on a global scale (Tate et 
al, 2020). Estimates on fishing mortality from recreational catches are, in some areas, comparable with or 
even above the observed fishing mortality from commercial catches (Sparrevohn and Storr-Paulsen, 2012),  
There has been a rising awareness of the impact from recreational fishing (Sparrevohn and Storr-Paulsen, 
2012), and more and more evidence indicate that recreational fishing does have a substantial effect on 
both fresh and marine fish stocks (Lewin et al, 2021). 
Member states in the EU has been obliged to monitor recreational harvest since 2008 (Sparrevohn and 
Storr-Paulsen, 2012). To be able to manage the recreational fisheries sustainability, insight is needed both 
on effort, catch and within human dimensions (Grudelund et al, 2021), making it potentially possible to 
contribute the harvest (the retained catch) and catch rate from recreational fishing to stock assessments 
(Tate et al, 2020).  
The objective of stock assessments is to understand and estimate the changes in stock abundance in 
connection to fishing pressure and predict the abundance (and effect of fishing pressure) in following years 
(NOAA, 2012). Data from standardized surveys, such as the Database of Trawl Surveys (DATRAS) (ICES, 
2023) provide biological information of a given fish stock, while surveys on catch rate contribute to 
understand induced fishing mortality.  
The Danish contribution to the total harvest of cod per year is estimated based on data collected during 
offsite recall surveys (Olesen et al, 2020). The offsite recall survey provides information on number of cod 
==PAGE:8==
Side 7 af 39 
 
 
caught per respondent, in a specific area, from marine recreational fisheries during a given quarter of the 
year. Additionally, onsite surveys were conducted to contribute with a correction factor on the offsite recall 
survey to correct the weight of cod caught from charter boat, private boat and shore-based fishing (Olesen 
et al, 2020) to confine recall bias.  
By collecting data on catch rate from recreational fishers, together with additional knowledge on variables 
that may explain the variation seen in the number of fish caught from different types of platforms and 
fishers, it is possible to standardize the catch rates. Generalized linear models (GLMs) is a statistical 
modelling approach, that has been commonly used for standardizing catch and effort data (Maunder and 
Punt, 2004). GLMs have been applied to detect year-to-year variation in abundance by using harvest or 
catch rate as a dependent variable (Tate et al, 2020) and ‘year’ as the most important explanatory variable 
(Maunder and Punt, 2004). This can potentially be used as an index of relative abundance in stock 
assessment (Maunder and Punt (2004)). 
Several management tools are used in recreational fishing, bag limit being one of the most typically applied 
(NSW, n.d.). Bag limit is a restriction on the maximum number of retained fish per person per day and is put 
in force to preserve fish stock and protect vulnerable species amongst others (NSW, n.d.). There are still 
uncertainties associated with how the different regulatory tools affect the catch rate of fish caught in 
recreational fisheries (Thünen-Institut (OF), 2016). 
The objective of this study is to standardize catch rates from anglers on charter boats in Øresund, by 
explaining the variation seen in the catch rates using information about anglers and their catches gain from 
onsite surveys. This can: 
1) Contribute with minimizing uncertainty related to the correction factor used on offsite survey, and 
possibly provide information on catch rate from different types of recreational fishers to evaluate 
whether this should be considered in the offsite survey. 
2) Give an indication of the variation in the population size if other explanatory variables can be 
excluded. 
3) Explain the effect of bag limit on the catch (retained) rate on cod. This can provide an indication 
whether bag limit fulfills the desired effect.

Materials and Method: 
Site:  
The onsite surveys were conducted in Øresund (ICES subdivision 23), however, as only a minimal of fishery 
takes place in and outside of Køge Bugt, it is assumed that the southern boundary of the study site is above 
Køge Bugt (see figure 1). For the additional length distribution analysis survey hauls within same area and 
period from DATRAS (DATRAS, 2023) is used for comparison.  
 
Figure 1: Black square: Study site. Red rhombus: Hauls from DATRAS trawl surveys (ICES, 2023). 
 
Data from onsite survey: 
The data used in this study originates from a project called “Forbedring af forvaltningsgrundlaget for 
bestande I det rekreative fiskeri (REKREA)” (Olesen et al, 2020). The overall purpose of REKREA was to 
improve the management foundation of the recreational fishery in Denmark, as it has become mandatory 
to monitor certain fish stocks caught in the recreational fishery according to EU legislation. The abundance 
of the Western Baltic Cod Stock is assumed to be affected by the fishing pressure from recreational fishery, 
and results from REKREA reflecting the fishing mortality and biological knowledge of this stock has therefor 
been included in recent stock assessments.  
==PAGE:10==

The data provided for this study is specific from roving creel survey performed on charter boats in Øresund 
from the second half of 2016 to the first half of 2020. The roving creel survey was design to gain a 
representative coverage of charter boats and the anglers on board (Olesen et al, 2020). As charter boats 
target different species and are fishing in different areas, and additionally may have different types of 
anglers on board targeting different species, it was important to gain a good coverage of the charter boat 
trips. Sampling was stratified for each quarter, as the number of anglers varied among the trips.  
The data were collected by trained interviewers in the format as a questionnaire survey. At the beginning 
of each interview, the anglers were informed about the purpose of the survey and asked if they wanted to 
participate in the survey. Next the anglers were asked a series of questions, here amongst questions related 
to fishing effort, target species, the demography and not least about fishing license. The anglers were also 
asked, if DTU Aqua may collect information on number/weight of harvest and released fish, and the length 
and ear stone of caught fish during the trip. It was however quickly recognized that it was difficult to collect 
data of released fish, especially in relation to age reading, as it was not approved of by the anglers to kill 
fish that were either too small or believed to belong to a vulnerable species. Additionally, it was difficult to 
reach the anglers for length measurement purposes of released fish, before they had released the fish 
again. However, at the end of each trip the amount of harvest (and released with some uncertainty) were 
notated and the total length of the fish were measured for each fisher who participated in the survey.  
 
Analytical analysis: 
Data treatment: 
Questions from the onsite survey in REKREA, contributed with variables that could influence the CPUE of a 
recreational fisher. It was decided only to use data from the roving creel survey on charter boats due to 
poor coverage from private boat and coastline, as it was difficult for interviewers to reach the anglers.

The datafile from the roving cruel survey (REKREA – onsite survey) consist of 89 variables with up to 5789 
observations. Each sail trip was given a unique trip ID (Trip ID). It was indicated from the ID what type of 
fishing trip it was (charter boat, private boat or from pier). As this study only concern charter boats all other 
trips were removed from datafile.  
 
Response variable: 
CPUE: For each anglers catch the aim were to register the ID of the respondent (‘ID (Respondent)’), what 
kind of species (‘Species’) the angler caught, if the catch was harvest or released (‘Catch category’) and the 
number of fish the angler caught. Additionally, 0 harvest cod were notated for those anglers, who didn’t 
harvest any cod. During data treatment, all other kinds of species than cod were removed from the dataset. 
Due to inconsistency during data collection of the released fish, only harvest cod were kept. The total 
number of cod consumed for each angler, where then estimated, resulting in The Catch per Unit Effort 
(CPUE) as the number of harvest cod per respondent per trip.    
Unfortunately, it wasn’t possible to collect the active fishing time of an angler during the boat trip, hence 
the effort during the trip is kept as ‘trip’, and not converted to a given unit of time (e.g. hour). However,

trip could be converted to ‘day’, as it seems unlikely that anglers have more than one trip per day. This 
makes it relatable to whether they stay within the given bag limit.  
 
Explanatory variables:  
Duration: The datafile contain the time at the beginning of the trip (‘Trip start’) and end of the trip (‘Trip 
end’). Some ‘NA’ and outliers, where time were registered as 00:00:00, existed in the datafile. These were 
removed from the dataset. Trip durations were estimated as the time from the beginning of the trip, when 
the charter boat was leaving the harbor and to the end of the trip, when the boat was back at the harbor 
again. Even though it is not the active fishing time, a longer trip may still reflect longer fishing time and a 
possible increase in both catch and harvest. It was discussed whether to keep it as an explanatory variable 
or to have it as an offset. However, there were no indication of an increase in harvest, as the duration 
increased and it was therefore not included as an offset. 
Age: The age of each angler was registered in the raw datafile. As the age might be an indication of the 
experience of the angler, it was decided to include it in the full model. Missing data were indicated by ‘NA’. 
Gender: The respondents were given the choose of answering male or female as gender. As recreational 
fishing is a male dominated leisure activity, the gender was included in the full model. 
License: Each respondent was asked, if they had a fishing license. In case they had a license, they were 
asked, what kind of license it was (angling license for a year, angling license for a week, angling license for a 
day, recreational fishing license or ‘refuse to answer’). In case they didn’t have a license or didn’t want to 
answer, they were given four possible options (‘retired‘, ‘age below 18’, ‘refuse to answer’ or ‘don’t know’).  
Those few who had a license, but still answered a reason not to carry license, where edited to not have 
answered this question. In regard to license type, only few refused to answer and these were therefor 
edited to ‘NA’. Additionally, ‘don’t know’ as reason for not having a license were edited to ‘NA’ as well. It 
was then gathered into one attributed and factorized, hence leading to 7 categories (angling license for a 
year, angling license for a week, angling license for a day, recreational fishing license, retired, age below 18 
and refuse to answer as reason for not having a license). 
The fishing license may reflect the experience of the angler, and may also reflect, if people without a 
license have a higher CPUE. Additionally, if it turns out, that anglers carrying different license type do 
indeed have different CPUE, it may later be an advantage in regard to up scaling as data on the amount of 
purchased license is available at fiskeristyrelsen (n.d.c). 
Target: Each respondent was given the opportunity to name up to three target species. Only the first target 
species is included as response variable in the full model. Every other species than cod were renamed 
“Other”, so the variable only consist of 2 categories (“Cod” and “Other”), which were factorized. Outliers 
assumed to be typos for cod were corrected.  
Effort: Each respondent was asked how many fishing trip he/she have had in Øresund within the last 3 
month and last 12 month. The numbers were asked specifically for each category of fishing trip ‘charter 
boat’, ‘private boat’ and ‘pier’ respectively. Additionally, the respondent was asked about the total number 
of fishing trip in any marine waters he/she have had within the last 3 and last 12 month.

Model development: 
GLM model: 
A generalized linear model (GLM) is an extension of a linear model (Zuur et al, 2009). A GLM can take in a 
non-normal distribution as the response variable and have a relationship (also known as the link) between 
the response and the explanatory variable that is different from a linear regression model. This can be an 
advantage for ecological data, as these often occur to be non-gaussian, e.g. as count data (or discrete), 
which always consist of non-negative data. The GLM models the expected mean as a function of 
explanatory variables. 
Zuur et al (2009) suggest following three steps for building a GLM: 
1. Choose the distribution for the response variable 
2. Define the systematic part in terms of the covariates 
3. Specify relationship (link) between expected value of the response variable and the systematic part 
 
Distributions in the exponential family: 
Two distributions, the Poisson and negative binomial distribution, are typical options for count data (Zuur 
et al, 2009). In the Poisson distribution, the Y has to be integer and no-negative, however the mean does 
not have to be an integer. For the Poisson distribution the expected Y is equal to the mean and the variance 
is also equal to the mean, hence a Poisson distribution only uses one parameter, the mean. The advantage 
of this distribution is, that it allows for heterogeneity, however it often occur in ecological data, that the 
variance is larger than the mean leading to overdispersion. A Negative Binomial distribution, on the other 
hand, have two parameters, the mean and the dispersion parameter. The variation of a Negative Binomial 
distribution consists of two terms added together. The first term is the mean, while the second term is 
mean squared divided by the dispersion parameter. If the dispersion parameter is large, relative to the 
mean squared, the second term is approximately zero and hence, the distribution is then similar to a 
Poisson distribution. However, if the dispersion parameter is small, there exist overdispersion in data. If the 
dispersion parameter equal one it is said to be a geometric distribution, where the variance increases as a 
quadratic function of the mean. In the software R and the glmmTMB package, it is possible to assume 
quadratic, and the parameter is then estimated based on the assumption.  
Looking at the distribution of the response variable CPUE (figure 2) both Poisson (P) and negative binomial 
(NB) is a possibility. Both are there for tested from the full model, however, it is assumed that the negative 
binomial is going to be the most likely, as the variance (~7.5) for the response variable is greater than the 
mean (~2.1).

Zero-inflated and zero-truncated models:  
Zero-truncated models are models where it is assumed that there is no possibility of having zeroes 
observations in the response variable (Zuur et al, 2009). Distribution like Poisson and negative binomial 
allows for zeroes, and it can therefor be an issue when these distributions are applied for data where 
zeroes don’t occur. Zero-truncated Poisson and zero-truncated negative binomial can take the ‘lake’ of 
zeroes into account. Zero-inflated models are, on the other hand, models where the response variable have 
more zeroes than what the distribution assumes.  Zuur et al (2009) states that ‘In ecological research, you 
need to search very hard to find zero-truncated data. Most count data are zero inflated’. In this study, both 
zero-truncated negative binomial and zero-inflated negative binomial is tested. Based on the distribution of 
the response variable CPUE (FIGURE 3) it is assume that zero-inflated model will have ‘the best fit’. It is 
important to investigate reason for having zeroes, to know whether they are “true zeroes” or “false 
zeroes”. Two types of models that handles observed zeroes in response variable differently are ‘mixture 
models’ and ‘two-part models’. In a mixture model some zeroes are used for the count model and some for 
the zero-inflated model, while in a two-part model the zeroes are only used in the zero-inflated model. 
Zero-inflated Poisson (ZIP) and zero-inflated negative binomial (ZINB) are example of mixture models, while 
zero-altered Poisson (ZAP) and zero-altered negative binomial (ZANB) are example of two-part models.

The selected ZANB model is a two-part model consisting of a conditional model and a zero-altered model. 
Observed zeroes of CPUE are not included in the conditional model in regards to ‘license’, ‘target’ and 
‘quarter’, as these are included in the zero-altered model.  Estimated coefficient, standard error, z value and 
p-value of both parts can be seen in appendix C. The appendix D also includes the statistical diagnostic 
assessed with the ‘DHARMa’ package to address the normality and the homogeneity of the model. 
The final model predicts the probability of CPUE of a respondent based on license (license type or reasons 
for not having a license), target species (cod or other), effort (number of charter trip within last twelve 
month in Øresund), quarter, year and bag limit. The reference points of each explanatory variable are 
‘refuse to answer’, ‘other’, lowest effort, ‘Q1’, ‘2017’ and lowest bag limit respectively. 
In the conditional model positive estimates indicates a likelihood of an increase in CPUE compared to the 
reference, e.g. targeting cod result in an increase in CPUE compared to targeting other species. Negative 
estimates indicate a likelihood of a decrease in CPUE compared to reference, e.g. anglers fishing in year 
2019 shows a decrease in CPUE compared to anglers fishing in year 2017. Higher bag limit has a higher 
likelihood of increasing the CPUE. In contrast, the zero-altered model indicates a likelihood of a CPUE of 
zero. This means that negative estimates indicate a likelihood of an increase in CPUE compared to the 
reference, e.g. targeting cod have a lower likelihood of ‘resulting’ in a CPUE of zero.

Discussion: 
The ZANB model is relatively similar to the ZINB model (despite being a two-part model instead of a 
mixture-model) and had additionally a lower AIC value. This could indicate that the function ‘predict’ in the 
‘glmmMTB’ package (version 1.1.3) might have issues when estimating the predictions from a ZANB model 
(instead of a ZINB model). When estimating prediction from the ZANB model using a different approach 
(described by Simpson (2018)), the predicted mean was higher than when using the predict function from 
the glmmTMB package (appendix G). 
The standardization in this study only include data from charter boats. To include it as a correction factor 
for the offsite survey, the same approach would be needed on land-based fisheries as well as from private 
boats. However, onsite surveys of these types of fisheries were involved with some difficulties in relation to 
coverage of fishers, which suggest a different approach might be needed.  
The results (figure 4) indicate that the offsite survey possibly needs to include a correction factor of the 
CPUE between fishers with different license type, as the off-site survey only include information from the 
two license types ‘angling license for a year’ and ‘passive gear license’.  
The estimated numbers of angling license for a year purchased is however around 150.000 in 2009, which is 
more than 100.000 more purchased compared to the three other types of licenses purchased within a year 
(Sparrevohn and Storr-Paulsen, 2010). The data used in this survey indicates an average number of charter 
trips for anglers with this kind of license is 6 within a year and that the predicted harvest of cod per fisher 
carrying an angling license for a year per charter trip is 2.5. 
The offsite survey provides information from anglers with either a ‘passive gear license’ or an ‘angling 
license for a year’ on catch rate from both land-based fishing, fishing from private boats and charter boats 
and indicates that around 40% of fishers carrying this kind of license, had not been out fishing within the 
last half year. This is not accounted for in either of the license type in the onsite survey, even though some 
of the angler’s effort includes zero trips within the last twelve month.   
It would be beneficial to standardize data from the offsite survey, to understand the variability in catch rate 
of anglers within land-based fishing, fishing from private boats and charter boats, as the onsite only 
includes angling from charter boats.   
Assuming The model did includes all variablity introduced by the anglers should be taken with caution. it is 
recognize, that multiple factors concerning the trip, has not been investigated e.g. time of trip, experience 
of the skipper, weather coditions and number of passengers on board. The duration of the trip was 
investigated, however turned out to be insignificant. It is unknown whether anglers having a difference in 
their active fishing time during a charter trip, can have an effect on their catch rate. It would be beneficial 
to investigate this, as this would provide an effort in the CPUE that it related to time unit (instead of 
‘charter trip’). 
The length frequncy of cod harvested (see figure 7 and 8) indicated only a few below MLS and suggest a 
size-selectivite fishing from around 40cm to around 70cm. It was not possibly to include the number of 
release cod due to inconsistency when collecting the data, however observations do indicate respondents 
having a release rate as high as 20. Weltersbach and Strehlow (2013) estimated the post-release mortality 
rate of Atlantic cod caught by recreational fishers and found an overall mean post-release mortality rate of 
11.2% and it should therefore be taking into consideration.

As the dataset from the REKREA onsite survey used in this study and the dataset in the trawl survey have 
been standardized separately and have a different effort, these cannot be directly compared in their 
amount of CPUE. This means that the greater CPUE in REKREA compared to DATRAS in the first quarter of 
2018 of cod belonging to size class of 60cm does not indicate, that more cod are caught in this size class in 
the on-site survey compared to the trawl survey. It could however give an indication of why the 
recreational fishers catches smaller cod in 2019, as the number of cod caught above mean in the trawl 
survey in 2018 in the size class around 60cm disappear in 2019. Similarly, could be said about the fourth 
quarter of 2018, where the largest proportion of cod harvested from the angler onboard charter boats 
belongs to a smaller size class compared to the first quarter. The onsite survey does not include the release 
rate of cod caught by anglers.  
The first quarter is the only quarter to include all bag limits that were in force during these four years and is  
also the quarter that is most consistent to the observed CPUE (see appendix F). The variation in CPUE 
explained by the year-to-year variation may in some cases be explained by bag limit (or vice versa), as only 
a few of the implemented bag limits (3 and 5) are in force multiple years (3 in the closing season in 2017 
and 2018, and 5 in 2017 and 2018 outside closing season, and 2020 during the whole year). In addition, the 
year 2019 is the only year with the same bag limit in and outside of the closing period and is additionally 
also the year with the highest bag limit (of 7). The predictions are cohesive to the observed CPUE this year, 
as the observation indicates the highest mean in the second quarter and the lowest mean in the fourth 
quarter. Even though a bag limit of 7 is included in this study, results also suggest that 2019 is a year with a 
relative low abundance compared to the other years.  
The selected model assumes an exponential relationship between CPUE and bag limit, which is statistically 
tested to be significant. from the observed CPUE in the other quarters, an exponential increase in CPUE 
from a bag limit of 5 to 7 is not visually clear, and it may even seem to decrease.    
Testing the same ZANB model with bag limit as a discrete variable and without year as an explanatory 
variable suggest a decline in CPUE when bag limit increased from 5 to 7. However, as the relative 
abundance index regarding year-to-year variation suggested 2019 as a year with a low abundance, it was 
not possible to exclude the possibility, of whether the decline in CPUE from a bag limit of 5 to 7 were due to 
a year with relative low abundance. Due to autocorrelation between year and bag limit, both factors could 
not be included as discrete variables in the model, as the same variation is explained by the explanatory 
variable ‘year’ as ‘bag limit’ (e.g. 2019 is the only year with a bag limit at 7).   
Additionally, the selected model is limited between the bag limits from 2 to 7, as these bag limits were the 
only one introduced during the onsite survey, and furthermore cannot be compared to a year without a 
bag limit, as this was not included in the model. 
The estimated spawning stock biomass (SSB) of the western Baltic cod stock in the ICES advice on fishing 
opportunities catch and effort (ICES, 2022a) estimated the greatest SSB (in tonnes) in 2019 relative to the 
years included in this study (2017-2020). An overview of maturity studies on Gadus morhua (FishBase, n.d.) 
suggest that cod caught by anglers in Øresund have reach maturity and as these are manage with the 
western Baltic Cod stock it is striking that the results in this study indicates the opposite. Even when 
considering the size class, the biggest proportion of cod caught by anglers from charter boats in 2019 were 
smaller than in 2018.  
This suggest either that the cod harvested in this onsite survey is not a representative representation of the 
stock or that cod reaches maturity at a smaller size class in the ICES advice than the cod harvested in this 
survey. The stock assessment of the western Baltic cod stock estimated the SSB with 60% of the stock 
==PAGE:28==
Side 27 af 39 
 
 
reaching maturity at the age of 2 (stockassessment.org, 2022). According to McQueen et al (2018), about 
estimating growth parameter of cod (Gadus Morhua) in the western Baltic Sea, a length of 30cm 
corresponds to age of 2. In 2019 the trawl survey had the highest CPUE in the size class 30 to 40cm, while 
the highest CPUE for recreational fishers were at 45 to 55cm, indicating that ICES includes cod in their 
estimating of SSB at a smaller size class than what most anglers harvested that year.   
The regulation regarding MLS of cod in Øresund (Fiskeristyrelsen, n.d.f) are for cod that have just become 
sexual mature (McQueen et al, 2018), but this onsite survey in the first quarter of both year 2017-2019 all 
suggest anglers prefer harvesting larger cod with the highest CPUE around 50cm. Ohlberger et al (2021) 
investigated whether population productivity in Northeast Artic cod were affected by age structure. Their 
results suggested a threefold difference in productivity between the age of 7 and the age of 11, suggesting 
that size-selective fishery targeting larger fish can lead to both a reduction in spawner biomass, but also can 
alter the age structure of the spawning stock (Ohlberger, 2021). When recreational fishing contributes 
around 1/3 in Øresund to the total landings of cod, anglers preferring to harvesting larger cod, may impact 
the sustainable of the fishery, by altering the age structure amounts the spawners.  
The potential effect of an increase in bag limit were estimated in an assessment of recreational fisheries for 
Seabass (Armstrong et al, 2014). Based on surveys from France, Netherlands and UK a potential increase in 
reduction is estimated as bag limit increases but indicating less impact between high back limits (4 and 5), 
supporting the trend seen in the model with bag limit as a discrete variable rather than in the selected 
ZANB model with bag limit as a continues variable.  Although bag limit is a widely used regulation tool, it 
has only gain little attention in the economics literature (Woodward and Griffin, 2003), making it difficult to 
know the effect of the implemented bag limits. Woodward and Griffin (2003) made a Bioeconomic Fisheries 
Simulation Model including relationship between bag limit, size limit and fish landed. The model suggests 
that changes in bag limit have a greater impact on the number of fish landed at smaller size limits 
compared to larger size limits (Woodward and Griffen, 2003), however they do emphasize that the model 
does not consider the possibility of high-grading, nor sharing limits amongst fishers. 
 
Conclusion: 
Angler catch rates (exclusive of release rate) were standardized by a zero-altered negative binomial model 
(alias hurdle model) described in a generalized linear model. The model suggests that factors related to 
license type, target species, and effort of the angler significantly affect catch rates, but also importantly the 
year and quarter at the time of fishing. 
The model can reduce the standard error related to the mean of catch per unit effort within each year, 
thereby reducing the uncertainty for an onsite survey, that is used as a correction factor on an offsite 
survey, with the purpose of minimizing recall bias amongst others.  
By this standardization approach of the catch rates, it was possible to explicitly look at the year-to-year 
(and quarter-to-quarter) variation by accounting for variation explained by other factors, thereby providing 
a indication of a relative abundance index of cod caught by anglers in Øresund. This was however not 
consistent with the index of the spawning stock biomass of the western Baltic cod stock provided by ICES.  
The model also provides new insight into the aspect of the widely use, but relatively newly introduced, 
regulation tool ‘bag limit’. It indicated a significant relationship between bag limit and catch (exclusive of 
release) per unit effort, where catch rate decreased with a decrease in bag limit.
