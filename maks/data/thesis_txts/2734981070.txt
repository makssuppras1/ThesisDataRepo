Abstract
Rolling element bearings play a crucial role in allowing rotating machines to smoothly
operate with reduced friction. However, due to their intrinsic design and to the high solic-
itations they are required to withstand, they are subjected to fatigue, they wear and get
damaged, eventually failing and causing catastrophic failure if not detected in time. The
faulty vibration response given by a damaged bearing can be artificially simulated making
use of non-linear analytical models reproducing the physical system, and can be used as a
useful diagnostic tool in machine condition monitoring, along with some real experiments.
In this regard, the current project allowed to investigate the vibration signature generated
by a faulty bearing, to gain insightful signal processing knowledge to extract the diagnostic
information and permitted to delineate a relationship between severity and vibration trend.
The project was not accompanied by real experiments, but a thorough theoretical investi-
gation has been conducted in order to provide, to possible future work as well, a detailed
description of the most updated techniques and the state-of-the-art necessary to model
and analyze a faulty bearing case. Overall, the project reached interesting and promising
results, and laid down a robust basis for possible future improvements and expansions.

1
Introduction
Rolling element bearings are a fundamental mechanical component. Being one of the
most used elements in machines, their failure is one of the most frequent causes of their
breakdown [1].
According to SKF [2], the world’s largest bearing manufacturer and supplier [3], 0.5% of
the 10 billions manufactured bearings worldwide, i.e. 50 million bearings, are replaced
every year due to failure and damage. Bearings can fail for several reasons and the
amount of time from the initial damage to the failure can vary considerably. It could take
months in large and slow rotating machines, or a few seconds in high speed machines.
Therefore, the best practice in order to prevent a catastrophic failure of the component and
consequent secondary damage to the machine is monitoring the condition of the bearing.
This can be extremely helpful to avoid a faulty bearing goes undiagnosed, and allows to
identify the root cause of the damage, which can even become an impossible task after
catastrophic failure occurs.
Generally speaking, the reasons why bearings are damaged and fail can be categorised
as follows [2]:
• 1/3 fail due to fatigue;
• 1/3 fail due to lubrication issues;
• 1/6 fail due to contamination;
• 1/6 fail for other causes.
The peculiarity of these events resides in the fact that each of them produces a unique
damage imprint, called a pattern. Thus, the root cause of the occurring fault, once di-
agnosed, can be determined in the majority of cases carefully examining the bearing.
Indeed, path patterns, especially in the bearings’ raceways, can indicate the presence of
a problem when detected during an inspection.
For these reasons, advanced condition monitoring becomes a central practice for service
engineers and professionals in the industry. A common used method is to “listen” to a
machine, monitoring noise and vibration to identify deterioration or damage of a bearing
[2]. This allows to obtain the vibration signal of the actual vibration levels and to detect a
fault at an early stage. Generally, a fault is known as a spall taking place on the raceways
of the bearing, either the inner or the outer.
Therefore, the following report aims at investigating the relationship between the topogra-
phy of the surface of rolling element bearings, i.e. surface profile, roughness or waviness,
and the vibration signature, i.e. the vibration pattern resulting from the faulty condition of
the bearings.
Secondly, it aims at investigating and possibly determining the relationship between the
damage severity and the vibration signal in a rolling element bearing. Overall, it aims at
proposing a theoretical model of a physical system, i.e. a rolling element bearing, affected
by a damage, with multiple complexity features.

2.1
Fundamentals on Rolling Element
Bearings
Rolling element bearings (REB) are without doubt one of the most essential and crucial
components in modern machinery [4]. Specifically, they support and guide, with minimal
friction, rotating or oscillating machine elements and have the important role of transferring
the loads between machine components [5].
Moreover, REB are one of the most widely used elements in machines and their failure
is one of the most frequent reasons for machine breakdown [1]. These are the reasons
why numerous studies have been conducted on REB vibration analysis, with the aim of
developing effective and powerful diagnostics techniques.
The project focuses on radial ball bearings type, with inner ring rotation and stationary
outer ring. Specifically, the single row deep groove ball bearings, which are characterised
by a groove guiding the balls around the bearing rings.

These tribological aspects determine the load-carrying capacity of the 2 bearing types:
ball bearings can accommodate high speeds, but have a limited load-carrying capacity,
due to the small contact area. On the other hand, roller bearings allow heavier loads, but
lower speeds, because of the larger contact area leading to higher friction.
In addition, rolling bearings are distinguished in 2 main categories, given the direction of
the load they are required to carry: radial and thrust bearings, as Figure 2.4a depicts.

(b) Bearing terminology

Finally, Figure 2.4b presents the most important sub-components of a bearing accompa-
nied by their standardised terminology: B is the bearing width, d the bore diameter, D
the outside diameter, r the chamfer dimension and α the contact angle. A value of the α
smaller than or equal to 45° defines the class of radial bearings. Moreover, the bearing
type shown in Figure 2.4b is known as angular contact ball bearing, which are character-
ized by inner and outer ring raceways displaced relative to each other in the direction of
the bearing axis. The direct consequence of this is the fact that they can accommodate
combined loads, i.e. radial and axial.
A suitable bearing selection for a certain application is based on a careful analysis of
the overall requirements dictated by the operation and other important factors. Between
these, the most relevant operating parameters are load, speed, temperature, lubricant and
lubricant cleanliness [5]. Only once all requirements and considerations on the required
performance and made assumptions are fulfilled, the proper bearing can be chosen.
2.1.2
Radial Load Ball Bearings
An acting radial load is transferred by the rolling elements to the outer ring, which has a
varying load distribution: it is widest in the direction of the load, and gradually decreasing
from that point [2]. Figure 2.5 shows the load distribution and the respective path patterns
created by the passage of the balls on the bearing rings. The red downwards arrow
represents the applied radial load, whereas the line-contoured arrows depict the load
distribution on the outer ring.
Moreover, for a pure radial load acting on a deep groove ball bearing, the contact area
between the ball and the raceways is an ellipse [2], centered in the middle of the raceway.
This can be appreciated in Figure 2.6, where for a pure radial load scenario the contact
area (fig. 2.6a) and a view of the load distribution on the outer ring (fig. 2.6b) are shown.

The load distribution among the balls (or rollers) can be determined from an analysis on
how each rolling element carries the load transmitted from the inner ring to the outer ring
or vice versa [6]. This depends on the internal geometry of the bearing and on the nature
of the applied load to it.
In fact, the contact angle α identifies the line of contact, the load carrying direction between
rolling element and raceway, and how well the rolling elements accept the applied loads
will determine, in most applications, the bearing endurance. For example, a bearing can
rapidly fail if subjected to a light radial load applied to a 90° contact angle thrust bearing,
or if subjected to an axial load applied to a 0° contact angle radial ball bearing.
In addition to the external applied load, other loads can affect the rolling elements and
consequently their performance: these can be inertial loads or preload, for example. The
former is a dynamic loading of the rolling elements due to speed effects [6] causing cen-
trifugal load. The latter, instead, identifies the usual practice of inducing a load, i.e. the
preload, in the bearing before it is put in operation.
Therefore, these considerations highlight the importance of determining the bearing load
distribution. This can be achieved by using the knowledge on how the rolling elements
transfer the load to develop load-deflection relationships for rolling elements contacting
raceways [6]: these are based on Hertz contact theory, which allows the determination of
contact deformations and stresses, and will be further discussed in Section 2.4.

2.1.3
Bearing Preload and Clearance
Preloading and diametral clearance are relevant parameters in a bearing, affecting its be-
haviour and response in operation. The characteristics of the preloading are related to the
initial bearing clearance and can vary depending on the application and on the respec-
tive mounting technique on a shaft: for example a mounting with interference fit, which
is a usual practice to prevent fretting corrosion (Section 2.2.1), can cause the bearing
clearance to decrease, vanish or even produce negative clearance [6].
It is important to note that in the current project, no preload and zero clearance have been
assumed in the bearing developed model, but their definition is briefly presented below
with the purpose of presenting a complete overview.
Preloading
Preloading can be distinguished in axial and radial types, which have different aims.
The first is normally realized by axially preloading angular contact ball bearings in order to
let them operate in the deflection vs. load range where the deflection rate is minimal under
load conditions: the deflection-load curve (Figure 2.7) has a knee at low loads, meaning
that the deflection rate is larger than for high loads. The advantage of preloading in this
case is that the bearing, once put in operational loading condition, will experience lower
deflection as the load changes.
Figure 2.7: Deflection vs. load characteristics for ball bearings, picture from [6]. As can
be seen, when the bearing is preloaded the deflection rate is reduced, causing a lower
bearing deflection under additional loading.
The second, instead, is usually used to obtain a greater number of rolling elements under
load, distributing the load between a larger number of rolling elements, thus reducing the

2.1.4
Lubrication Regime
The lubricant in rolling bearings has several functions. Among the most important ones, it
provides separation films between the bearing parts, it provides corrosion protection, and
can even serve to flush out wear debris, which are removed thanks to a filter.
Rolling element bearings are typically characterized by the so-called elastohydrodynamic
lubrication (EHL) regime between the raceways and the rolling elements, a form of hy-
drodynamic lubrication (HL, where the fluid separates the two surfaces) where elastic
deformation of the lubricated surfaces becomes significant [7].
Usually, a partial lubrication regime can also develop if the high pressures are applied or
running speeds are too low, because the lubricant film is penetrated and some contact
between the surface asperities will take place [7]. Furthermore, hydrodynamic lubrication
(HL) occurs between the bearing cage and its locating surface [7].
2.1.5
Lubrication Systems
The majority of rolling element bearings is lubricated by liquids, which can consist of
greases (a 2-phase fluid composed by fluid phase and a thickener) or liquids provided
to the bearing from dedicated systems [7].
The appropriate grease for an application can be selected depending on the operational
conditions of the bearing: temperature, speed and ambient pressure.

2.2
Failure Modes of Rolling Element
Bearings
Rolling bearings appear to be relatively simple mechanisms, but they actually experience
complex internal operations [8], and in order to successfully perform they require attention
to many different details. These can be the correct design and size for a given applica-
tion, the proper mounting or lubrication system, or the protection against the ingress of
contaminants. Ideally, a properly installed, lubricated, aligned, loaded bearing would only
manifest a failure mode, i.e. surface fatigue, also called fatigue spalling or pitting [9].
Although all these fundamental aspects are well known, it sometimes happens that the
required or expected service life is not attained [2] and premature bearing failure occurs.
Different failure mechanisms arise and appear in the bearing as [8]: excessive deflection,
excessive vibration or noise, unacceptably high friction torque and temperature or bearing
seizure. However, each failure is characterized by its own special imprint on a bearing [2],
which in the majority of cases allow to determine its root cause and to define the proper
corrective actions to avoid recurrence.
Therefore, the following section 2.2.1 aims at exposing the typical failure modes that rolling
bearings can encounter during operation. These can often develop in sequence in a same
bearing, which starts failing in a mode, but then evolves to another one.
Moreover, there are multiple causes of deterioration, such as poor maintenance practices
or incorrect installation, which can lead to bearing life reduction and premature surface
fatigue [9]. These are described in section 2.2.2.

2.2.2
Typical Damages and Reasons of Deterioration
Three main categories of rolling element bearings damages can be identified [9]: lo-
calised, extended and distributed defects. The first 2 are further described below. The
causes of these damages can be poor maintenance practices, mishandling, incorrect in-
stallation, misalignment and inadequate lubrication [9].
Localised defects
These class of bearing defects includes cracks, pits and spalls on various components
(rolling surfaces) of a rolling element bearing: outer raceway, inner raceway and rolling
elements [9]. They are fatigue generated damages on bearings which correspond to an
ultimate failure mode of a correctly installed and lubricated bearing during normal opera-
tion conditions.
These types of faults are the ones on which the current project focuses on. More specif-
ically, the outer raceway defects: an analytical model for the prediction of the vibration
response caused by this damage on a rolling bearing has been developed.
The geometry of the localised defect can be of different shape: point spalls, circular spalls,
elliptical spalls ad line (rectangular) spalls [9]. Depending on the characteristic shape of
the considered defect, different non-linear dynamic analytical models for predicting the
vibration response of rolling element bearings have been developed in the past, and a
review of them is available in [9].
Extended defects
Extended defects can be identified as defects that are larger than localised defects, but
smaller than distributed defects (as a raceway waviness). Their size, for example, can be
greater than the angular spacing between two rolling elements.
The typical cause of this defect type is a consequence of a localised defect (a spall). As
the rolling elements continuously and repetitively pass over the spall, generating impulsive
contact forces, the edges of the spall are worn, causing it to gradually grow and expand
in size, eventually resulting in an extended defect [9].

2.3.1
Surface Profile Characterization
The actual surface profile of a solid can be represented as the superposition of three main
contributions, each of them identifying a component of the overall surface texture [7]:
• Error of form: the surface deviation from a well-defined pattern because of errors
inherent in the manufacturing process;
• Waviness: relatively long waves in a surface profile, often associated with undesired
vibrations occurring in machine tool systems;
• Roughness: inherent irregularities, excluding the two above mentioned, of cutting
and polishing production processes.
Furthermore, in tribology the characterization of surfaces includes both the vertical direc-
tion, or amplitude parameter, and the horizontal direction, or wavelength, both bringing
relevant information [7].
2.3.2
The Relation between Topography and Vibrations
Vibrations in rolling bearings and surface topography are extremely interconnected. In-
deed, the predominant source of vibration in rolling bearings is surface roughness [12].
In addition, there can be different modes of surface interaction, and surface features can
be related to their influence in vibration, as done by Sayles and Poon in [12], describing
three mechanisms of noise generation in rolling bearings:
• Rolling response due to form and waviness effects: waviness topography feature
can be considered prominent over a scale greater than the Hertz elastic contact
dimension (cf. section 2.4). A mechanism contributing most of the low-band noise
and part of the mid-band noise. Instead, at increased speeds the effect is to shift
the generated energy into the higher bands of the spectrum.
• Shock noise due to local elastic deformations: this mechanism includes features
which are on a scale comparable to, or slightly less than, the Hertz elastic contact
dimension (cf. section 2.4), but which do not completely break through the lubricant
film. These generate local elastic deformations when passing through the contact
area, causing shocks and consequently vibrations.
• Impulsive shocks due to asperity collisions and debris: created by the interactions
due to microslip and collision of surface features with debris, as the surfaces come
together.
A direct effect of these highlighted mechanisms is that methods to fulfill the surface finish
requirements are fundamental in reducing the generated vibrations.

2.5
Varying Compliance Vibrations
Rolling element bearings generate vibrations during operation even if they are geometri-
cally and elastically perfect. These vibrations, known as varying compliance vibrations,
are the most fundamental cause of noise and unsteady running of rolling bearings and
have been thoroughly investigated by Sunnersjö in [16].
Due to an inherent feature of the bearing type and to the fact that a finite number of rolling
elements is used to carry the external load, these vibrations are parametrically excited
and occur irrespective of the quality and accuracy of the bearing.
The main cause of varying compliance vibrations is the variation of the number of rolling
elements under load with the angular position of the cage. In fact, this gives rise to a
periodical variation of the total stiffness of the bearing assembly, which eventually gener-
ates vibrations: it will be shown that the bearing (quasi-static) stiffness matrix Kb varies
with the cage position, even for a defect-free bearing. As an anticipation, this is also the
reason why the time-frequency characteristics of the developed model below, i.e. the
natural frequencies of the quasi-static linearized dynamic system, vary periodically at the
ball pass frequency [17].

Condition-based Maintenance (CBM) is being recognised as the most efficient strategy for
carrying out maintenance in many industries. Machine Condition Monitoring is a relevant
part of CBM, as it allows to monitor the current condition, but also to predict the future
condition of machines while in operation [18].

2.6.1
Corrective Maintenance
In the past, CBM was not possible, and maintenance strategies were inappropriate and
could result in high costs. Indeed, the typical maintenance approach was the so-called
corrective [19] or run-to-break [18]. This method simply meant that machines were run
until failure. Then, maintenance action was taken.
One can easily recognise that this approach extends the maintenance interval, but with
the expense of an increased risk of equipment unavailability [19]. In fact, a primary failure
can also happen to cause the consecutive failure of other components, inducing increased
time and cost of repair.
2.6.2
Preventive Maintenance
Therefore, preventive maintenance [19] or time-based maintenance [18] was developed
with the aim of avoiding unplanned downtime through periodic inspections and replace-
ments. The main advantage of this approach is that the majority of maintenance tasks
can be planned in advance and failure occurrence is drastically reduced. Nevertheless,
this method brings 2 main disadvantages: firstly, despite in a small number, failures can
still occur. Secondly, too much maintenance is carried out and an excessive number of
components’ replacements is consumed [18].
2.6.3
Predictive Maintenance
Both the exposed previous methods not being an optimal option, mainly cost-wise, a
third approach has been introduced in recent times: predictive maintenance. With this
method, the potential breakdown of a machine is predicted and service is carried out when
required, typically shortly before a fault is expected. Indeed, maintenance is a crucial and
indispensable practice in almost every industrial production process, and thanks to the
development of modern technologies it can be optimized to increase asset availability
and reduce maintenance costs [19].
That is why, in the last 20 years, thanks to a wider range of available monitoring tech-
niques, predictive maintenance has been recognised as the best maintenance strategy in
the majority of cases [18].
It allows to determine the current condition of a machine, but also to predict the health
of a machine based on repeated analysis of known characteristics, giving reasonable
predictions of remaining useful life (RUL) [18], [19]. Therefore, based on current and
historical indicators, future performance can be predicted.
The advantage of this technique is the reduction in planned and unplanned downtime [19].
The first meaning that preventive actions can be scheduled in a better way, whereas the
second meaning that unexpected failures can be avoided. The equipment is constantly
monitored and action can be basically taken only when needed and at an optimal time.

surements including the following analyses [19]:
• Vibration analysis: trend analysis for vibration levels (frequency ranges or signal
profile analysis), to determine RUL and evaluate component deterioration. It allows
rapid diagnostics developing analytical methods, e.g. based on machine learning.
• Thermography: temperature measurements to predict and diagnose the equipment
condition.

2.7
Rotating Machines Vibration Analysis
Machines constantly generate vibrations, even when they are in good condition, and the
vast majority of them can be linked to periodic events characterizing the machine’s oper-
ation. These machines can be rotating shafts, for example. When in standard condition,
a machine has a certain vibration signature, but when a fault arises, it changes that sig-
nature in a way that can be related to the fault [18].
The advantage of applying vibration monitoring is that a damage can be detected at an
early stage, and not when it is already problematic. As Figure 2.26 shows, monitoring
gives a pre-warning time, crucial to avoid either severe or catastrophic failures.
The fundamental information about these events is the frequency with which they re-
peat. Frequency can give a clear indication of the source of such events, and this is the
reason why frequency analysis is the base for many powerful diagnostic techniques. Fur-
thermore, the response pulses’ repetition frequency from a bearing fault is an important
information, and is contained in the so-called envelope signal [18], presented below in
Section 2.7.2 and further discussed in Section 2.8.2.
Vibration signals are classified in a variety of signal types. The main distinction, for exam-
ple, distinguishes stationary from non-stationary signals, the first being characterized by
statistical properties that do not change with time. Other random signals, instead, named
cyclostationary (see Section 2.7.3) are often generated by machines running at constant
speed and have statistical properties which vary periodically [18].

(multiples of the nominal frequency) and subharmonics. These faults are [18]:
• Unbalance: causing excitation by forces rotating at the shaft speed when the lo-
cal centre of mass of the shaft cross section does not correspond to the centre of
rotation. It mainly gives vibration responses in the radial direction.
• Misalignment: induces bending moments, ”disturbing” the shafts’ bending deflec-
tions, that have to be counterbalanced by forces at the bearings and the foundations.
In the case the shaft bending stiffness varies with the rotation angle, fluctuating mo-
ments and forces will arise, eventually generating vibrations. This happens when
for example two machines are coupled together and a shaft has multiple bearings,
and the presence of misalignment is potential. This can be parallel, with one of
the shafts laterally displaced and still parallel to the other, or angular misalignment,
where the axis of one is at an angle to that of the other.
• Bent shaft: corresponds to a combination of the previous 2 cases, when an initially
balanced shaft acquires a permanent bow (bend) for some reason.
Furthermore, the development of a crack in a shaft is another very relevant concern, as
one of the most serious faults to be detected in condition monitoring.
Nowadays, it is becoming more common to develop mathematical models of a rotor/bear-
ing/foundation system, in order to be able to better predict the changes given by various
types and levels of faults [18]. This is due to the fact that simple rules for distinguish-
ing specific fault types cannot be given. In addition to this, finite element models have
advanced to a point where they reach high accuracy levels.
Furthermore, for condition monitoring purposes, it is not required that a simulation model
predicts the absolute values of measurements parameters completely accurately, as long
as reasonably good predictions of changes due to typical faults are reached [18].

Rolling bearings are characterized by motions that are not restricted to simple movements.

2.7.3
Cyclostationary Signals
Cyclostationarity represents a category of signals which, although not necessarily peri-
odic, are produced by a hidden periodic mechanism. Cyclostationarity comprises most
signals generated by rotating (and reciprocating) machines [1].
An nth order cyclostationary signal is defined as the one whose nth order statistics are
periodic. A first order cyclostationary signal for example has a periodic mean value (e.g.
a periodic signal plus noise). Instead, a second order cyclostationary signal has a periodic
autocorrelation function (e.g. white noise amplitude modulated by a periodic signal) [18].
The so-called autocorrelation function is a second order statistic that gives an indication
about how well a signal correlates with a displaced version of itself.
As reported in [1], strictly speaking an nth order cyclostationary signal can also be defined
as one which produces a peak in its Fourier transform after passing through any non-linear
transformation involving nth power.

The Interpretation of the Vibration Signature
Initially, Sawalhi and Randall [22], confirming the interpretation from Epps et al. in [23,
24] and Dowling in [25], found the presence of double impulses in the experimental mea-
surements they ran and found good agreement with the simulated results. In fact, the
response can be divided in 2 main parts: the point of entry and the point of impact/exit,
i.e. the entry and exit of a rolling element into and out of the defect [9]. They coined this
as the double-impulse phenomenon.
Epps et al. and Dowling identified the first (entry) as a low-frequency event with no ev-
idence of impulsiveness and the second (exit) as a high-frequency impulsive event that
can excite a broad range of frequencies. On the other hand, Sawalhi and Randall in [22]
interpreted both as high-frequency events, yielding to a contrast with the earlier findings.
As stated by Singh et al. in [9], it is possible that there is an error associated with the
results in [22].
Subsequently, Sawalhi and Randall in a more recent study [21] found results aligning with
the initial interpretation, i.e. confirming the entry of the rolling elements into a defect as
a low-frequency event. Interestingly, they found that experiments run at various shaft
rotational speed resulted in the same time separation between the 2 impulses due to the
bearing spall, concluding that they were not directly linked to the size of the fault as initially
suggested. Moreover, they suggested that the phenomenon may represent a ”beating”
effect related to a small difference in the bearing resonance frequencies, possibly caused
by stiffness non-linearities [21].
Overall, a lot of research has been conducted with the objective of identifying the causes of
this double-impulse phenomenon, but as reported by Singh et al. in [9] in 2015 the reason
for the occurrence of multiple impulses as a result of a single rolling element traversing a
defect resulting in typically measured bearing vibration signals is still not clearly known,
and the presented research has been interpreted as the most updated findings.
Incipient Faults in Bearings
Typically, bearing faults manifest themselves starting as small pits or spalls, and giving
sharp impulses in the early stages, covering a very wide frequency range that can reach
frequencies up to the ultrasonic frequency range to 100 kHz [18].
Furthermore, bearing faults initiate manifesting themselves at high frequencies, giving the
need to use a high sampling rate (even larger than 40 kHz) [26].

2.8.1
Vibration-Based Fault Detection
Fault detection is of primary importance as it is the first step in the overall process of
detection, diagnostics and prognostics. Despite the fact that this project assumes the
presence of a known induced fault on the outer raceway of a rolling bearing, in day-to-day
practice fault detection is peculiar in order to consequently conduct a precise diagnosis
of the issue.
Depending on the machine type, i.e. rotating and reciprocating, faults reveal themselves
in very different ways [18]. For the purpose of the project, the former only will be briefly
further described.
The most reliable method to detect faults in machines is to compare the measured vibra-
tion levels with standard criteria for vibration severity. Indeed, some charts are available
as guideline to judge the severity of vibrations. Randall in [18] reports 2 main sources: a
set of criteria in the form of a chart making no distinction between machine classes and
Table C.1 from ISO 20816-1, which gives the recommended ranges of boundary values
for different vibration severity zones in terms of RMS velocity in mm/s.
It is also noted that individual machines can vary a lot from the average, and that a more
reliable alternative to the 2 criteria is to detect changes in the vibration level at each mea-
surement point on each machine, as recommended by Downham and Woods in [27, 28].
In fact, vibration levels will normally remain stable when machines are in good condition
and operated at the same load and speed, but will change when conditions change.
Moreover, according to [4], difficulties arise experimentally-wise with regards to early de-
tection of the fault: small defects are challenging to detect, because in the early stage
noise is overwhelming the faulty signature, and this is a relevant issue as earliness is of
crucial importance. Signal analysis should detect the defect as early as possible to allow
action to fix it. In artificially introduced defect experiments, instead, the fault severity is
”large” enough to be detected (a guarantee of availability of impulsive response). How-
ever, this goes into conflict with natural accelerated testing, which highlight the difficulty
to detect a defect at early stages and to track its evolution. These are all empirical facts
witnessing the capabilities, but most importantly the limitations of the monitoring methods.
It is finally worth mentioning that the reported sources for determining acceptable vibration
limits are only some of the various that are available.
Fault Simulation
The possibility of simulating faults in machines is of great advantage in real practice. Real
faults cannot be made to appear at will, and cannot be predicted or arranged to occur when
and where desired. Therefore, being able to simulate or reproduce faults in machines
could be considered to be the only way of obtaining a sufficient number of signals, which
can be used to train an algorithm (e.g. Artificial Neural Networks in Machine Learning) to
detect and classify the range of faults that may be encountered [29].
In addition, the development of simulation models for whole machines can be very use-
ful to obtain a physical understanding of complex interactions that might characterize a
system, as the effects of faults in complex machines might be really difficult to foresee
[29]. Also, it allows to give a better understanding of signal characteristics that have been
experienced in practice and which prediction might be difficult.

2.8.2
Introduction to Signal Processing Techniques
In order to extract the defect features from the raw signal of a faulty rolling element bearing,
many signal analysis methods are needed. These can have a different nature and can be
statistical measures or frequency domain methods, for example [4]. Frequency analysis
is the most common approach used to process the raw available data [30].
Between the statistical measures one can find the Root-Mean-Square (RMS) value, the
mean, the kurtosis, etc. Instead, between the frequency-based methods some of the
most commonly used are the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) or the Short Time Fourier
Transform (STFT). The aim of this section is therefore to present the techniques that
have been implemented later in the project to provide the needed insights in the vibration
responses generated by the developed analytical model.
Advanced techniques are necessary to analyze and gain useful knowledge from the raw
vibration signals. This is also needed because a number of different factors or distur-
bances affect the responses and increase the difficulty in detecting the patterns of inter-
est. Between those, some of the main challenges the signal methods try to cope with are:
remove the speed fluctuations, remove the noise effect, extract clear fault features and
track the evolution of the fault-induced signal [4].
Envelope Analysis
Envelope analysis is a powerful technique to gain important diagnostic information from
the vibration response of a machine. It is well suited for the analysis of bearings, in the
instance they are damaged or running in a faulty condition. In fact, the envelope signal re-
sulting from this analysis contains the important information about the repetition frequency
of the response pulses [18].
The importance of this method can be appreciated from the following Figure 2.29. For
a given vibration response of a bearing with a simulated (outer race) fault, the envelope
spectrum is capable of highlighting clear peaks placed at the characteristic frequencies
of the specific fault and their harmonics, i.e. 1,2,3, etc. times one of the fault frequencies
presented in Section 2.7.2. That is the case even if there is a certain amount of slip, also
introduced in Section 2.7.2, included in the raw signal.
Conversely, the raw spectrum of a faulty signal does often not show any diagnostic in-
formation. As shown in Figure 2.29, the raw spectrum only depicts one resonance (the
resonance frequency is 2 orders of magnitude higher than the repetition frequency of the
impacts), which could actually be the lowest of a series. The issue using the raw spectrum
is due to the fact that the low harmonics of the repetition frequency of the impacts have a
very low amplitude and can easily be masked by other components in the spectrum [18].
Therefore, envelope analysis is the benchmark method for bearing diagnostics, and has
been used in the course of the current project. In addition, further advantages arise from
performing the amplitude demodulation step using the Hilbert transform, described below.

Hilbert Transform and Demodulation
As defined by Randall in [18], the Hilbert transform can be identified as the relationship
between the real and imaginary parts of the Fourier transform of a one-sided function (e.g.
a signal that is one-sided in the time domain).
Without deeping into detail of the theoretical definition of this transform, it is worth noting
that it is extremely useful when applied in the envelope analysis procedure. In fact, multi-
ple benefits arise from performing the amplitude demodulation using Hilbert transform [1]
(below the procedure is explained) when investigating a raw signal from a (faulty) bearing.
As anticipated above, the envelope analysis is the benchmark method for bearing diag-
nostics and its working principle can be explained in the following steps, also depicted in
Figure 2.30:
• The raw signal frequency spectra is defined using FFT;
• The signal is then bandpass filtered in a high frequency band where the fault impulse
are amplified by structural resonances, the so-called carrier signal which amplitude
is periodically modulated by the fault impulses;
• The selected frequency band is then amplitude demodulated using Hilbert trans-
form, which transforms the information back to the time domain in the form of the
so-called analytic signal, a complex time signal, which imaginary part is the Hilbert
transform of the real part (real part is plotted in Figure 2.30);
• The envelope signal is determined from the analytic (complex time) signal modulus
and its spectrum is determined using FFT;
• If the envelope is calculated from the squared amplitude of the analytic signal, i.e.
the modulus of the complex time signal (as shown in Figure 2.30), and consequently
Fourier transformed to obtain the frequency spectrum, the so-called squared enve-
lope spectrum is obtained. This is normally preferred to the ”simple” envelope [1].
Indeed, it has been found that analyzing the squared envelope can improve the
Signal-To-Noise Ratio (SNR) in certain situations: if the SNR is higher than unity,
there is an advantage in analyzing the squared envelope because the SNR would
increase when squared [31].

Spectral kurtosis and Kurtogram
The spectral kurtosis (SK) is a statistical tool that can be efficiently used in vibration-based
condition monitoring of rotating machines. It is an extension of the kurtosis indicator, which
provides a measure of the impulsiveness of a signal, taking high values if it has a transient
nature, such as the one typical of a fault (cf. Section 2.7.4). The advantage of the spectral
kurtosis is that it does not provide a global measure as in the case of the ”simple” kurtosis,
but gives information in the entire frequency spectra, i.e. locally, providing information
about how the impulsiveness is distributed in the frequency domain [1].
In fact, two are the advantages of using the SK technique, from the conducted analysis
by Antoni and Randall in [32]:
• The SK has the ability to scrutinize the whole frequency domain and determine the
frequency bands where the fault signal can be detected. This means that the SK is
a robust and powerful tool for detecting the presence of incipient faults;
• The SK offers an almost unique way of designing optimal filters for filtering out the
mechanical signature of faults. Therefore, a very useful tool for diagnostics pur-
poses.

the PSD is to be interpreted as a measure of position (time-average), whereas the SK as a

has been selected as maximum, centered at f).

Special Techniques
In real practice application, the measured raw signals are affected by noise, and result
as a superposition of different signals coming from individual sources. It is therefore of
primary importance to separate these, which normally means separating deterministic
signals (from gears, for example) from random signals [18].
Deterministic signals belong to the signal category of stationary signals, and their name
reflects the fact that they are composed entirely of discrete frequency sinusoids and that
their value can be predicted (once frequency, amplitude and initial phase are known).
Random signals can be identified in different ways: general background noise, includ-
ing measurement noise, noisy source signals (from fluid flow in a pump or turbine, for
example) and cyclostationary signals (from rolling element bearings).
As a consequence to the presence of these disturbances in the raw signals, various de-
noising and discrete/random separation techniques have been developed. This allows to
proceed with bearing diagnostic analysis ensuring the discrete frequency noise has been
removed.
Between these techniques, one can find linear prediction, adaptive noise cancellation,
self-adaptive noise cancellation (SANC), discrete/random separation (DRS), time syn-
chronous averaging (TSA). Moreover, other methods such as the mimimum entropy de-
convolution (MED) can be used to remove the remaining noise that may still mask the
bearing signal in many frequency bands, even after the removal of discrete frequency
”noise”. A thorough explanation of these methods can be found in paper [1] or equiva-
lently in the book by Randall [18].
For the purpose of the current project, which simulates a faulty signal of a damaged bear-
ing, the output raw signal is not affected by these real case disturbances. However, in
order to test the effectiveness of some of the listed methods, Gaussian White noise has
been introduced in the signal and methods like SANC and MED have been tried.

As anticipated in the previous sections, the analytical simulation of faulty bearings gives
various benefits. In fact, different analytical and Finite Element (FE) models are avail-

Therefore, this section aims at presenting an overview of the most recent and useful mod-

that is affecting its operation (here an outer race fault).
In the following, it is assumed that the reader has knowledge in the application of the
Finite Element Method (FEM), in this case applied to rotordynamics for the analysis of
the vibration response of rotating machineries. The FEM allows to achieve mathematical
models for representing physical systems, such as a rolling element bearing, but also
more complex systems as a rotor-bearing assembly. More on this can be found in the
literature. A very useful source of important notions and basics can also be found in the
Dynamics of Machinery DTU course notes Vibrations in Rotating Machinery - Modelling,
Analysis, Experimental Tests, Vibration Monitoring & Diagnosis by Professor Ilmar Santos
[34], supervisor of the current project.
At this point, however, it should also be noted that the main difficulty with using com-
plex dynamic models lies in verifying the predicted results through experiments. In fact,
2 typical testing approaches are used to aim at this: naturally accelerated or artificially
introduced defect tests [4]. The second is widely used for its simplicity and low complex-
ity, because it focuses on a single artificial defect of known shape and size, but even
though it is very helpful in terms of diagnosis, it does not replicate the evolution of the real
bearing wear progress: in reality, fault features change over time due to topological and
tribological changes [4].
2.9.1
Bearing Models from The Literature
Singh et al. in [9] provide a detailed overview of all types of bearing models, for bearings
that have localised defects. These can be analytical or FE models. Although a vast
variety of models can be identified, depending on the complexity, the model basic type,
the type of localised defect to be included, in the following a synthetic and project-focused
discussion is provided.
The first model type imply that the faulty bearing signal is analytically generated, in some
cases even not including the physical parameters of a bearing, nor attempting to simulate
the deformation at the rolling element - raceway contact interfaces (based on Hertz the-
ory). A sub-category of these also includes some random fluctuations to simulate the slip
between the rolling elements and the raceways.
Another important sub-category of these are the non-linear multi-body dynamic models,
which compose the analytical model assuming lumped parameters. This means that the
components of the system are simulated as simplified rigid masses connected by a series
of springs and dampers. These kind of models consider the localised non-linear Hertzian
contact deformation at the contact interfaces, and can include different types of localised
defects (point or line spall, for example).
The current project is based on this modelling approach, and the assumptions at its basis

2.9.2
Types of Modelled Defect on the Outer-Race
The extensive overview presented by Singh et al. in [9] also provides a list of the most
common modelled rolling element bearing defects. Note that they can affect both the
bearing raceways, but also the rolling elements. These are divided in 3 main categories,
which also distinguish the different designed non-linear multi-body dynamic models de-
pending on the characteristic defect shape:
• Point spall: this defect assumes a hole on the raceway with a regular (circular)
geometry (e.g. by Sassi et al. in [35]). The cross section of this can be imagined as
a half circle;
• Circular and elliptical spalls: this defect assumes a pocket/cup shape with circular
or elliptical circumference/profile on the raceway surface. In this case the geometry
of the defect ”entering” the raceway material is sinusoidal (as done by Ashtekar et
al. in [36]) or ellipsoidal;
• Line (rectangular) spall: the defect is characterized by a length in circumferential
direction (along a raceway, for example) and by a height/depth (as performed by
Sopanen and Mikkola in [15]). It is imagined that the defect is extended along the
axial direction (crossing the entire raceway, for example).
The third defect shape is the one assumed in the actual project.
It is relevant to note that the influence of the chosen and then modelled profile has great
influence on the behaviour of the model. This was noticed when testing different profile
shapes when running the developed model, and will be further discussed below.

computationally affect the model. In fact, in a rotor-bearing system for example, a
large number of DOFs implies a greater system of equations to be solved.
2.9.4
The Measure of Damage Severity
The aim of the project is to investigate the fault severity in a rolling element bearing, inves-
tigating the relationship between the damage severity and the vibration signature. This
can be achieved by applying multiple approaches. One can be assessing the amplitude
of the vibration response, through the (squared) envelope of the signal, as the impact
severity is in relation with the size of the damaged area and the sharpness of the edges
of the defect, i.e. it is affected by the defect topology [4]. Another is to evaluate the char-
acteristics of the impact, i.e. the acceleration time-series, for example investigating the
time interval between consecutive pulses.
Additionally, there are two interesting approaches delineated through the literature:
One is to build an analytical non-linear vibration model that given the defect/damage size
on the outer race simulates the vibration response signal of the bearing (for different fault
sizes). Then, the resulting signals can be compared for analogous bearing types with
experimental data [40].
Fault Severity Detection in Rolling Bearings
35
==PAGE:46==
Another approach would be to develop an algorithm applying an analytical model that can
estimate the size of the fault zone in a bearing by using the vibration acceleration signal.
The outputs of the algorithm can then be compared with the known defect size on the
outer race used in the different experiments [41].
It is to note that both these additional approaches assume experiments to be run in par-
allel to the analytical simulations, in order to conduct a comparison between the obtained
results. However, this was not possible in the actual project, due to a lack of resources.
Despite this fact, interpretations, considerations and a thorough analysis of the analytical
findings have been conducted and are reported below in the following chapters. It is worth
anticipating that reasonably accurate and promising results have been found.
Furthermore, the severity-signature relationship is strongly related to the evolution of the
defect, i.e. to the trending of diagnostics parameters.
2.9.5
Fault Trend Analysis
Fault trending, besides fault detection, is extremely beneficial in regard to making reliable
predictions about the so-called Remaining-Useful-Life (RUL) of a component/machine,
and is at the basis of prognostics.
As presented by Randall in [18], simple data trending techniques, but also more complex
prognostics methods, are available. Between the former, 2 are the preferred ones, which
are the following:
• Trending of Impulsiveness: localised faults are characterized, at least initially, by
impulsive components in the response signal, typically as a series of sharp impacts
with a spall. Impulsiveness is measured by means of SK (see Section 2.8.2).
The SK showed to perform very well in various applications and to correspond well
with bearing degree of degradation. However, SK does not always show clear in-
creasing patterns. Randall, in fact, reports that the SK development flattened out in
one of the case histories he analyzed. Moreover, he reports that experience shows
the SK can fluctuate a lot and that it is not unusual for it to decrease (cf. also [42]):
a justification to this is assumed to be the wear of the spall edges.
He also notes that tests are normally run at close to full load conditions, which is not
a realistic operational condition, and outlines that bearings in different applications
are replaced once having reached different fault sizes.
• Trending of Spall Size: this approach involves the analysis of the separation be-
tween the entry into and exit from the defect events. Thus, the time-to-impact Ti
is defined as half the total time of passage Tsp of a rolling element centre over the
spall length. For a spall sufficiently deep to not allow the rolling element touches
its bottom, i.e. for a spall length to rolling element sufficiently high ratio, it can be
estimated by the formula

Numerical Investigation
After having presented all the necessary background knowledge in Chapter 2, this sec-
tion aims at recalling the conducted steps for the development of the designed numerical
model, together with the necessary assumptions.
The model, configured as a non-linear multi-body dynamic model of a rolling element
bearing with a localised defect on the outer raceway, has been designed following an
equivalent procedure to the one proposed by Petersen et al. in [17]. Additional consider-
ations are also based on the consultation of other relevant literature: [4], [22], [39].
The model has been built following a similar line to the process exposed in the Dynamics
of Machinery course at DTU, given by Prof. Ilmar Santos. Starting from an assumed
physical system, passing through a mechanical model and applying a FEM procedure,
the system equations of motion have been composed and the model could be tested for
generating a defective bearing raw output signal.
Additionally, the bearing model has been considered to be applied to applications with
operating low speed (in the tested cases the maximum tested speed has been 1200 rpm)
and imagining reasonably high accuracy levels, both in terms of fault detection and fault
trending/evolution.
3.1
Model Assumptions
The model requires the definition of a series of assumptions, as typically done in numerical
modelling, in order to set the basis and visualize the starting point. These are the following
(based on previous considerations in Section 2.9.3):
• The model is configured as a lumped parameter model, which the system compo-
nents considered as rigid masses connected with springs and dampers;
• The model has 6 DOFs, presented below;
• The bearing rolling elements are considered massless and inertial/cetrifugal effects
are neglected for slow speed considerations;
• Slippage is introduced as a random uniformly distributed process between [−φslip,
φslip], with φslip rolling element phase variation (due to slippage), which can be as-
sumed to have a value of 0.01-0.02 rad. In Matlab, this can be accomplished using
unifrnd() function;
• The bearing is subjected to a full radial load wy applied in the negative vertical di-
rection;
• Potentially, the model allows to include a localised fault in one of both raceways. In
the current project, the affected race is the outer one, at a 6 o’clock position (270°
from zero position);
• Contacts between the rolling elements and the raceways are Hertzian, and are mod-
eled by time-varying non-linear springs kj(t) ;
• Lubricant film damping is introduced using dampers cj(t) ;

• The inner race is rigidly connected with the shaft;
• Zero clearance assumed in the bearing: no preload and 180° load distribution.

3.4
The High Frequency Resonant Mode
The high frequency resonance mode included in the model is an additional DOF (in both
x and y directions) consisting of a small sprung mass with relatively high damping, which
aims at representing a typical high frequency resonant response of the bearing [22]. It
is included in the model in order to measure the high frequency bearing response. In
fact, faulty incipient conditions typically start showing at high frequencies, as anticipated
in Section 2.7.4. The high frequency ”device” acts as a carrier for the main diagnostic
information, i.e. the vibration signature resulting from the rolling elements’ impacts with
the defect on the outer raceway: from this point of view it can be imagined as an amplifier
of the faulty signal.
This ”device” properties, i.e. natural frequency and damping, are selected so that a typ-
ical high frequency resonant response of the bearing is excited. For example, in the
bearing-pedestal model proposed by Sawalhi and Randall in [22], stiffness and damp-
ing are selected to excite a 15 kHz frequency with 5% damping. Petersen et al. in [17],
instead, use 10.3 kHz with 1% damping.
This device somehow acts as an accelerometer in reality, imagining that the resonance at
high frequency, generated by a defective bearing, is then measured by a transducer. In
the model, it simulates the high frequency bearing response and configures itself as the
signal that one would obtain in reality.
Of course, this has to be considered as a quite strong assumption, which should find
positive feedback with practical experiments, but at this stage can be considered as the
measured vibration response, as conducted by Sawalhi and Randall, and Petersen ([22],
[17]).
Experiments would most probably allow to tune/fit the high frequency resonant mode
characteristics to the observed measurements (obtained in artificially induced defect con-
ditions). However, it is considered that assuming these to be in a similar range to the
previously proposed values (for a quite similar bearing type) is reasonable: 10-15 kHz.
Also, for the purpose of the project (and from the conducted simulations) it does not seem
of primary importance that the tuning is ”perfect”, acknowledging that no experiments
could be run and as long as the produced responses behave as described in the literature
and in the Chapter 2.

then starts decreasing again to reach 360° with a null value.
As long as showing agreement with the literature results, the distributions reflect the ex-
pected behaviour: when one ball is on the defect, the load it was carrying is re-distributed
between the others, with an amplitude somehow proportional to the ”distance” from the
ball on the defect.

Considerations on the Dynamic Forces
The analysis of the dynamic forces allowed to understand and visualize the impact events
in a better way, especially inducing reflections on the defect profile definition.
These can be summarized in the following considerations:
• As expected, the contact forces fluctuate around the quasi-static loads, with the
rapid stiffness changes producing high magnitude impulsive force fluctuations/peaks;
• The fitting with Petersen et al. results is not perfect, but this is due to the fact that in
the publication a 2 rows bearing is used, i.e. a smoother load re-distribution at the
impact. In fact, summing the load increases (of the balls adjacent to the ball on the
defect) in both rows, approximately gives a resulting peak with an amplitude close
to the simulated one (≈80 N);
• The most crucial element in the modelling of the faulty bearing is the defect shape/pro-
file definition. The model is very sensitive to it, even for a very small depth change.
Initially, the defect depth was tested with a deeper height, but the obtained peaks
were unrealistically high. This is the reason why in the simulations following the vali-
dation, different maximum defect depths have been tested at different shaft speeds,
in order to investigate how these choices can affect the built model. Moreover, the
relationship between the defect shape definition and the actual rolling element path
needed more attention.
4.3.2
Considerations on the Rolling Element Path
The dynamic response of the system highly depends on 2 characteristics of the defect
shape: depth and depth variation rate. These are important as the model, given how it
has been designed, imposes a depth to the ball crossing the defect and with it comes the
rate at which it changes in time. However, this goes into contrast with the fact that the ball
Fault Severity Detection in Rolling Bearings
53
==PAGE:64==
is geometrically larger than the defect, i.e. the ball-diameter-to-defect-length ratio is quite
high (≈9 in the current tested case). From this point of view the ball could be forced to
reach the bottom of the defect even if not realistic in practice.
For this reason, a possible way to overcome this issue has been thought. It is to note
that similar considerations were found in the literature, but not as detailed. Moreover,
for example from Sawalhi and Randall in [44] (prior to [22], from the same authors), it is
suggested to use a more gradual changing defect depth, but the suggested model was
still under investigation. Nevertheless, the basic idea was to shape the defect in a more
realistic way, therefore implemented using the Tukey window function proposed in [17].
The shape can also be designed imagining to follow the centre of the rolling element,
its trajectory when passing through the defect. Then, realistically imagining that the ball,
as the defect increases its depth, does not always reach its bottom, the maximum depth
can be chosen assuming it equals the maximum depth that the ball in the given condi-
tions would be able to reach (ideally touching the bottom at one point). This reflects the
procedure followed by Petersen et al. ([17]).
Moreover, reducing the cosine fraction r defining the Tukey shaped defect, influences the
slope of the profile. It becomes steeper and gives a flat section at the bottom of the defect,
as shown in Section 3.2.4. This contributes increasing the dynamic effects and shaping
the defect in a more similar way to the unrealistic line (rectangular) one. A possible idea to
reduce this effects was thought (and tested) to be by reducing/smoothening the derivative
of the defect profile making use of the ball-diameter-to-defect-length ratio (or a % of it, e.g.
25%). However, this proved to render the modelling less robust, as arbitrary choices were
needed to use the ratio.
Eventually, it was considered that the Tukey window shaped defect was the appropri-
ate way to proceed and the depth of the defect was chosen to be defined assuming the
maximum reachable depth for the given length of the defect.
Finally, the dependency in terms of the cage speed was also noticed, as it is a factor in
the definition of the profile derivative function (needed to determine the rate of change of
the contact deformation ˙δj). This is a relevant aspect, which influences the response.
Therefore, in the following simulation these aspects were taken into account and consid-
ering a higher rotational speed of the shaft, the defect was modelled trying to ensure that
for the tested depth, length and shaft speed the impulsive peaks were not unrealistic.

5.1
Model Adjustments
The primary aim before the actual implementation of the built model is to lay down some
relevant comments after the validation process (Chapter 4). These are mostly related to
the defect geometry definition process that was conducted during the project.
At the very first stages of the model definition, the defect was introduced as a line (rect-
angular) shaped defect, and the imposed depth (of about 50-300 µm) demonstrated to
be too high. It was generating a very impulsive behaviour of the dynamic forces, which
translated in unrealistic acceleration values of the raw signal.
Therefore, with the objective of remedying this, it was decided to link the geometry of
the fault on the outer raceway with one of the main geometries of the bearing, i.e. the
balls’ diameter, Db. This was thought to be implemented using a scaling factor that could
scale/reduce the defect depth or its derivative, in order to contain the dynamic reactions,
and take into account the fact that the ball was not actually touching the defect bottom
part for large defect depths h. However, in a secondary moment, this solution appeared
to be not realistic and the definition of the scaling factor depending on the initial assumed
defect depth quite complex to be generalized.
Therefore a similar approach to the proposed in the literature was re-considered and ex-
ploiting the validation and of a lot of trial and error, it found confirmation to be the most
suitable one. This assumed a Tukey window shape. However, the imposed defect depth,
despite the fact that the cosine tapered smoothens the defect edges, was still too large.
Eventually, the refinement of the defect definition came to a final formulation, as already
anticipated: assume that the depth of the defect corresponds to the vertical deviation of
the centre of the ball, which actually ”follows” the exact trajectory of the bottom part of the
ball when rolling in and through the defect. This implies to choose a max defect depth
quite contained, e.g. 10 µm as eventually assumed, so that it is realistic to imagine the
ball will touch the bottom of the defect, also considering the action of the load.
Subsequently, the option of widening the defect length-wise was considered, as one can
consider that in reality the defect does not increase its severity only in terms of depth.
Nevertheless, due to the previous assumption and mostly to the fact that no experimental
measurement was available to confidently determine a relationship between depth and
length increase, it was preferred to focus on the defect depth evolution only.
This has been considered a reasonable assumption as the increase in length would
smoothen the defect (reducing the impulsiveness), and secondly the interaction depth-
length would increase the complexity of the results to be interpreted.

5.2.3
Discussion on the Results
Overall, the obtained results allowed to verify and somehow confirm some expectations,
but also outlined some additional levels of criticality. This also taking into account some
comments that arose during other simulations and investigations.
Therefore, in the following a general discussion is provided. Firstly, with various consider-
ations of general character, afterwards focusing on the role of the high frequency resonant
mode included in the model.
General Discussion
Keeping in mind the initial objective of the project, i.e. investigating and possibly determin-
ing a relationship between fault severity and the resulting vibration signature, the following
considerations have been delineated:
• The expected increase of diagnostic parameters (as the SK) along with the increas-
ing severity of the fault found some confirmation in the early stage development
phase of the defect. Indeed, within the approximately 10-20 simulations (of the to-
tal 50), a gradual increase can be noticed, both looking at the waterfall plots, but
also at the scatter plots displaying the maximum SK values. This for all the tested
cases, validation excluded as a too rough depth evolution was applied. On the other
hand, for the second part of simulations, the diagnostic parameters showed to flat-
ten, sometimes even decreasing, but this can be considered as confirming the be-
haviour that one can expect in reality according to Randall [18] (see Section 2.9.5);
• The model seems to well perform in modulating its response depending on the
severity of the introduced fault, which was the aim of the project. However, a dif-
ferent approach, particularly well-suited to experimental measurements, could have
been to shape a defect on a real bearing and then compare the experimental faulty
signal to the analytical one (modelling the presence of the real defect). This would
allow to quantitatively assess the model performance when compared to a real sce-
nario;
• The added White Gaussian noise to reduce the discrepancy with real measure-
ments seemed to ”perform” very well in masking the initial very impulsive raw signals
and therefore reducing the global kurtosis. However, critically thinking this method
presents a limit, in the sense that it is artificially induced (imposing a certain SNR)
and that the applied de-noising (in the form of bandpass filter through envelope anal-
ysis, but also other digital filtering techniques that were tested, e.g. Self-Adaptive
Noise Cancellation (SANC) or Minimum Entropy Deconvolution (MED)) could be
configured as cheating, because the added noise is somehow known a priori. This
is not the case in reality, where noise is intrinsic to the signal;
• The central role of the defect shape definition could be confirmed. The shape, in
fact, directly influences the system dynamic response and qualitative differences in
vibration signatures produced by defects with different depth profiles can be directly
correlated to the induced variations in the time-varying non-linear contact stiffness
of the rolling elements. The same can be concluded if talking about the quasi-static
stiffness matrix Kb;
• The decision to only focus on the defect development in terms of depth, instead of
including its extension length-wise was also due to the following consideration: for
the same imposed defect depth, a wider spall smoothens the defect shape. This
means a reduction of its derivative (the rate of change of the profile as a function
Fault Severity Detection in Rolling Bearings

==PAGE:82==
of the attitude angle φj), which is linked to the contact deformation derivative. The
final effect would be a less impulsive response, and entry/exit events becoming less
clear. Therefore, the inclusion of this variable would have introduced an opposite
effect to the primary searched one.
The effect can of course be further investigated, but as the interpretation complexity
seemed already quite relevant, it was considered to not mix together the 2 effects;
• An interesting fact was noticed when performing the post-processing of the same
dataset of generated faulty signal: the (graphical) results showed to slightly vary in
different runs. This has been assumed to be caused by the different values that the
added White noise can assume, as of random source even if uniformly distributed;
• The imposed different values of shaft speed showed to play 2 main roles in the
generated responses. Firstly, it was noticed that an increase in speed leads to a
higher amount of intrinsic noise witnessed in the raw signal, most probably due to
the faster stiffness variations caused by the larger speed of the cage. Secondly, a
larger speed translates in more energy, which resulted in overall higher amplitude
values of the diagnostic parameters;
• The missing of an experimental counterpart necessities an important note: indeed,
in usual practice (as noticed in the vast majority of cases in the literature), this is
central to the development of a model representing the physical system. It allows to
set better initial parameters, to adjust the model assumptions and most importantly
to concretely evaluate the fault wear evolution. There would still be an artificially
induced defect on the bearing, and not a naturally developed one, but the resulting
measurements would be very helpful.
The option to find and use some log data from performed experiments1 with an outer
race faulty bearing was considered, due to the impossibility to practically run them,
so that a comparison could be done. However, this presented the main limit of not
having the same bearing type. Also, the match between the model (e.g. the set
parameters) and the real system used for those experiments was not ensured;
• Computationally-wise, the model has responded positively. It did require a rather
long time to be run, due to the number of simulations to be iteratively run and to
the time span of about 0.75 seconds assumed for each simulation (so that initial
transients could disappear), but did never exceed a computational time of ≈2.5
hours. Also, the post-processing graphical representations were quite heavy to be
run by the used PC, but the taken time was reasonable;
• The overall performance of the model and of the post-processing handling of the
generated data has been judged as reasonably satisfying. Of course, room for im-
provement and further investigation can be seen, but given the ”limits” in which
the project has to be conducted (e.g. time-wise and resource-wise), the positive
outcomes confirm the model is rather solid and delineate a quite clear vibration in-
creasing pattern as a result to the increasing fault severity.

The Role of the High Frequency Resonant Mode
The role of the high frequency resonant ”device” can be critically discussed. Together with
the defect shape definition, it is an important factor in influencing the generated results
and deserves some additional comments:
• On one hand, it can positively be commented, as its magnifying action of the faulty
signal and its subsequent ability to carry the diagnostic information could be con-
firmed by the conducted analysis. The fact that it resonates and excites a frequency
equivalent to the one that would be excited in reality makes it very useful in the
model. Also, it could be imagined as an accelerometer, measuring the excited high
frequency range by the presence of a fault on the raceway. In fact, this allowed to
delineate the listed considerations above.
Also, the ”device” does not actually limit the model applicability to different types
of bearing fault. In fact, as long as the tuning frequency is realistically assumed,
the device will excite the frequency that is most likely to be excited when the fault
develops in reality;
• On the other hand, it can be critically interpreted: the tuning process of this ”device”
needs to be conducted each time the assumed fault is different, as the excited fre-
quency (according to a different BPFO) will be different. This is a flexibility given
by the model, but in reality an accelerometer cannot be tuned at desire.
The same would be necessary for a case of varying speed. However, a varying
speed (real) system induces an overall higher complexity level (as stated by Randall
in [18], for example).
Moreover, the assumed tuning resonance frequency could be questioned, as no ev-
idence has been used to choose it, other than following similar procedures done in
the literature. A few tests were performed trying to tune the resonant mode reso-
nance at a higher frequency, e.g. 25 kHz, instead of the 10-15 kHz as indirectly sug-
gested by the literature. However, quite similar results to the exposed were found,
with the only difference of displaying the peak at the higher selected frequency. With
the attempt to completely exclude the given resonance at high frequency, using a
filter to filter out all frequencies above 15 kHz for example, the response did not
show any other peak other than the ones at low frequency. Nevertheless, these are
not to be used for diagnostic purposes as the fault would start appearing at high
frequency in reality, but also because the noise affecting the frequency range would
be large.
Overall, it is therefore assumed that ensuring to choose a plausible value, accom-
panied by evidence of a real scenario experiment, should be preferred. This should
guarantee that a value analogue to the real excited high frequency range is selected.

Conclusions
Overall, the project reached satisfying and reasonably accurate results, which somehow
showed to be in line with the found literature and expected behaviour. Indeed, the goal
was to delineate a relationship between the severity of a defect in a rolling element bearing
and the vibration response generated by its presence.
The model performed positively and the application of the diagnostic techniques showed
to give promising results in detecting and assessing the severity of the fault on the outer
raceway of a rolling bearing.
Even though useful and interesting answers were found, the problem will need additional
investigations in order to deepen the analysis. Moreover, it has to be acknowledged that
the impossibility to perform some practical experiments limited the overall understanding
and outcomes of the conducted analysis, as a direct comparison between simulated and
measured results was not possible.
Furthermore, considering that the exposed and then used diagnostic techniques were
completely new at the project start, it can be considered that a more complex model
would have been hardly reachable, even though a more ambitious result could have been
reached if supported by some experiments. This is the reason why high level of detail on
the presented knowledge in the introductory Chapter 2 was used, with a secondary aim to
be useful for future work on the same topic. In addition, the required computational time,
even if not obstructing the developments, contributed to slow down the proceeding pace.
Moreover, the development of the analytical model, the main focus of the project, has been
challenging, despite the fact that analogous procedures had been previously proposed in
the literature by numerous researchers. Despite that, probably even more arduous has
been the deep search related to the signal processing techniques and advanced methods
that are needed to best interpret the generated data. Much more time than the initially
planned had to be invested in investigating, understanding and confidently applying the
different tools that one can use to successfully reach the diagnostics objective.
In conclusion, the found results appear to be promising for subsequent work, which could
further develop the 6-DOF faulty bearing model or use it in a more complex model. In this
regard, the model could be combined to a finite element model of a rotor, i.e. resulting
in a rotor-bearing system. This would allow to include the dynamics of the rotor, together
with the ones of the faulty bearing.

Accessing the DTU Cluster
The DTU cluster, suitable to run computationally heavy jobs, can be accessed following
the instructions available at the following link: https://www.hpc.dtu.dk. This allows the
user to get the basic required information on how the High Performance Computing (HPC)
environment works.
The cluster can be accessed in different ways, depending on the user desire. A flexible
option is the remote access using the available DTU VPN, for example. This requires to
setup a safe VPN connection following the instructions given in the HPC website:
Home » HPC LSF clusters » LSF User Guides » Accessing the LSF 10 Cluster.
Note that VPN access requires to access the cluster through a Terminal window (or em-
ulator) and using a command line access with ssh. The ssh-access using a terminal
requires a basic knowledge of UNIX commands. Basic Unix commands can be found at
the website https://www.gbar.dtu.dk/index.php/faq/48-unix-commands.
Once the VPN connection is set, the access to the cluster can be done through a Terminal
window following these steps:
• Type the command:
1
ssh s123456@login1.hpc.dtu.dk
using the personal DTU username. Different ”login-nodes” can be accessed, lo-
gin1.hpc.dtu.dk is one of those. More instructions on this can be found in the HPC
guides provided above;
• Type in the password when prompted and press Enter. Note that when writing the
password the cursor is not moving. The password is being written, but without being
shown on the Terminal cursor line.
Once the cluster is accessed, it is required to upload a ”job”, i.e. a program or application
that one would like to run. This can be done with the following steps:
• Prepare the so-called job script followiing the instructions given at the path: Home
» HPC LSF clusters » LSF User Guides » Batch Jobs under LSF 10. Tis file is to be
submitted in the cluster and is interpreted by the cluster’s Resource Manager pro-
gram, which dedicates the necessary resources for the submitted job. An example
of job script is reported below.
The generic name My-Job-Name stands for the jobID given to the Resource Man-
ager. The .mat file Main-File-To-Run-Name (with NO .m extension!) stands for the
main file to run to start the program, which will save the command window output in
the .txt file Output-File-Name.
