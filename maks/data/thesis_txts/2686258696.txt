Abstract
Research and development of large two-stroke marine diesel engines is expensive
and time consuming. Reduction of time and cost are potentially gained with the
use of computational fluid dynamics simulations. Setup and running of in-cylinder
simulations is no simple task and requires expert knowledge within CFD. To sim-
plify in-cylinder simulations, Siemens released the add-on package Star-ICE, which
automates and simplifies in-cylinder setup and running.
This thesis is conducted entirely in Star-ICE where a single cylinder in-cylinder
simulation was created based on MAN Energy Solutions 4T50ME-X test engine. The
ease of use and adaptation of the automated setup was investigated on normal swirl,
high swirl, and full cycle setups. Full cycle simulation was investigated with the
Star-ICE ”simple” ECFM-CLEH combustion model. Furthermore was the numerical
performance and accuracy investigated for potential reductions in computation time.
Performance investigations were done through comparison to a similar Star-ccm+
in-cylinder model where several test cases were compared.
Star-ICE was shown to yield good results through validation of velocities, mass
flow, and pressure compared to experimental results. The model was further used
to investigate the correlation between initial conditions and full cycle results, which
showed good agreement. Several implementations of transient exhaust boundaries
were investigated indicating possible accuracy gains over the use of constant exhaust
boundaries. Finally, the numerical comparison of Star-ICE and Star-ccm+ showed
on-par results for raw performance. The comparison showed the benefit of using the
simulation operation in Star-ICE over Java run file in Star-ccm+ with a total run
time reduction of 60% due to dynamic time stepping.

Introduction
1.1
Introduction
The need to have reliable power sources which can convert some form of energy
into a cyclic motion has existed for centuries, with the first watermills ranging back
to the 1st century BC[1].
Different technologies and methods sprung from this
need which led to the invention of the compression-ignition (CI) engine in 1892
by Rudolf Diesel and the spark-ignition (SI) engine by Nikolaius Otto in 1876[2].
The creation of internal combustion engines led to simpler and far more effective
engines compared to the steam engine which was eventually replaced by the internal
combustion engines. The more effective internal combustion engines paved way for
increasing globalization of the world economy. Becoming the preferred power source
for transportation along with many other power needing applications.
Sizes of combustion engines range from small two and four-stroke engines in applica-
tions as lawnmowers to some of the largest two-stroke CI engines in container ships
with weights of more than 2300 tons [3]. 90% of world trade is done by shipping
at sea, the majority of the ships used are container ships driven by large two-stroke
marine diesel engines[4]. The eﬀiciency of these engines reaches around 50% which
is better than automotive engines, this increase is partly caused by marine engines
large compression ratio. However, the shear size of marine diesel engines in cargo
ships make research and development (R&D) of these engines diﬀicult and expensive.
By assisting R&D with concurrent simulations it is possible to aid reducing the cost
and time used in exploring new designs and solutions. Reduction of cost and time
is caused due to the possibility to approximate test results beforehand, resulting
in fewer equipment parts and tests being produced. Computational fluid dynamics
(CFD) simulations are one type of simulation tool which can assist development by
simulating the flow of a chosen domain. CFD have the advantage of being able to
capture flow variations everywhere in the simulated domain without compromising
the flow in the process. Deeper understanding of potential problems and solutions
can be gained during such simulations. It is however important to remember that
all simulations are numerical approximations of real-life cases which is why the
simulations require reference data. Especially CFD is dependent on reference data
for validation due to the simplification of turbulence done to make the computational
cost feasible.
Simulation of large engines require complex setup due to the large displacements of
valves and cylinders, as well as interaction between separate regions. To simplify
the setup process Siemens have developed an add-on module to Star-ccm+ named
Star-ICE1[5]. Star-ICE is aimed towards industrial use with the possibility to sim-
ulate cold flow, charge flow and combustion with good correlation to physical tests
results[6]. This leads to the aim of study for this thesis.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.2
Aim of study
This study aims to qualitatively and quantitatively investigate the two-stroke capa-
bilities of Star-ICE compared to Star-ccm+. A functioning two-stroke marine diesel
engine capable of simulating a full engine cycle should be created within Star-ICE
and validated against experimental results.
The objectives of this study can be
summarized in the following points:
• Create a fully working dynamic two-stroke engine model capable of a full 360
degree cycle. The model is to be validated against the MAN Energy solutions
4T50ME-X test engine in Copenhagen.
• Investigate the ease of adaptation of the Star-ICE model and its accuracy on
High swirl and transient exhaust pressure cases.
• Compare numerical performance, accuracy, and usability against the similar
model created in Star-ccm+ by Arash Nemati.
• Compare computational performance of the inbuilt combustion models to
known complex chemistry models.

2
Theory
2.1
Two-stroke engines
For large container ships two-stroke engines are the preferred choice due to the high
power to weight ratio and simpler design. The power to weight ratios for large two-
stroke marine engines are among the most eﬀicient CI engines due to large stroke
and combustion during every cycle. The main difference between two-stroke and
four-stroke engines is the absence of the intake and exhaust stroke in two-stroke
engines, which are instead combined in the scavenging process. An illustration of
the working principle of a uniflow two-stroke engine can be seen in figure 2.1
Figure 2.1: Two-stroke engine working principle. 1) Compression, 2) Combustion,
3) Expansion, 4) scavenging.[7]
The engine cycle can be expressed through the 4 steps in figure 2.1
1. The compression stroke: Exhaust valve and inlet ports are closed allowing for
no mass transfer from the engine. During this stroke the fresh air is compressed
before injection of fuel and ignition.
2. Combustion: The piston is near Top Dead Center (TDC) when the fuel is
injected and ignited due to the pressure end temperature in the cylinder.
3. Expansion: The burnt fuel increased pressure and temperature. This is the
working stroke of the engine.
4. The scavenging stroke: The piston just after Bottom Dead Center (BDC).
At this time the exhaust valve is open and the exhaust is being driven out
by the pressure gradient between the exhaust and scavenging port called the
scavenging process. The fresh air is forced into the cylinder through the port
in the liner which physically displace the exhaust gas out of the cylinder.
The scavenging process of a two-stroke engine can have different designs depending

Turbulence modeling
Characterization of a flow is regularly done by the magnitude of the Reynolds num-
ber (Re) which can be seen in equation 2.11. Re is a dimensionless value determined
from the properties of the flow density, viscosity, and velocity along with a char-
acteristic length. Re relates the ratio of inertial forces to the viscous forces, a low
Re typically implies laminar flow due to the viscous forces being large enough to
suppress random fluctuations[12, p. 350].
Two-stroke marine engines typically have a flow with Re number in the range of 106,
which can be assumed to be turbulent. For comparison, a pipe flow is proven to be
turbulent when the Re number exceeds Re ≳4000 [12].

Turbulent flow is characterized as disorderly and rapid fluctuations called eddies,
which are by nature chaotic and random. This chaotic nature of turbulence can
be diﬀicult to capture as it can exist anywhere in a given flow, working in three
dimensions affecting the flow at no single point. Further adding to the complexity of
turbulence is the lack of memory in turbulence compared to the mean flow, meaning
that eddies do not necessarily exist in the same place or behave equally at different
times. Properties as strong non-linear, diffusitity and dissipation are as well part of
the turbulence in a given flow. The properties of turbulence means that rotational
large eddies are created in the flow due to the turbulent shear stresses against the
main shear. These large eddies then break up into continuously smaller eddies due
to nonlinear interaction which ensures a continuously cascade of energy from large
to small Eddie until they seize to exist and becomes heat. The rotational Eddies
ensure an increased transfer of momentum, mass, and energy as different regions of
the flow mix much faster compared to regular molecular diffusion due to turbulence
diffusion[12, pp. 361-381].
In any given engineering application, the resources needed must be weighed against
the potential outcome. For most CFD application it is simply not feasible or possible
to resolve all turbulent length scales why Reynolds Average Navier-Stoke (RANS) is
one of the most used models since turbulence is modeled and not resolved. However
with recent advances and lower cost of high Performance Computing (HPC) Large
Eddy Simulation (LES) have become more popular as the large scales of turbulence
are resolved yielding results of higher detail.

2.4.1
Region interfaces
Two-stroke marine engines consist of one valve where the exhaust gases leave through
and a set of ports in the bottom of the cylinder which replenish the cylinder with
fresh air through the scavenging process. Whenever the engine is not within the
scavenging period, no air can be exchanged between the cylinder and the scavenging
box. Through use of interfaces it is possible to separate the two regions outside of
the scavenging process. The interface allow the faces between the two regions to
change boundary type depending on the state whether they align or not.
Interface creation can be region or contact based depending on the geometry of the
setup. The region based interface creates an interface between two separate regions
where the entire region chosen becomes part of the interface. Contact interface work
on a part surface level where it connect two contacting surfaces regardless of which
regions the faces are placed within. Both methods has pro and cons, for this study
the region based method is used as it allow more freedom to remove and add surfaces
to the interface, without removing and creating the interface again.
Different types of interface are likewise available depending on the usage of the in-
terface. Relevant for this study is the sliding interface method which enable the
possibility to combine the scavenging and engine region during the scavenging pro-
cess. The sliding interface make use of the In-place topology which enable the CFD
model to connect regions if they are not separated in space, enabling the possibility
of only a portion of the interface to be connected while the rest remain as a bound-
ary. For the interface type the internal interface is used which allow the separate
regions to share continuum.
The final sliding interface works by having a slave and a master patch, where the
slave patch mesh is recomputed at each time-step while trying to conform to the
mesh of the master patch. To tune how much the slave patch can deform to match
the master patch, the Interface intersection Tolerance can be adjusted. The toler-
ance can be adjusted between 0 to 1 and determines how many % of the smallest
cell length the slave patch can move it vertices [20, pp. 2137-2200].
In figure 2.5 can the interface for different conditions be seen with figure 2.5a showing
the interface components, where grey is the port and blue is the liner. In figure 2.5b
can a developed interface at BDC be seen where the sharp edge outlines the interface
which can be seen as the white area. In figure 2.5c can a interface with the default
tolerance of 5% be seen, here gaps in the white interface can be seen which indicate
a too low tolerance. The final tolerance value must be tuned as too high values will
result in a bad mesh as well.

Morpher
Motion in two-stroke marine engines are diﬀicult to accommodate due to the large
stroke of the piston. Different methods can be used to allow the numerical solver
to enable this movement, i.e. morphing of the mesh which is used in this study.
The morpher works by moving cell vertices based on the movement of a set of
control points, distributed in the mesh as a point cloud. The point cloud can either
be manually specified through a position table or automatically generated by the
morpher algorithm. A general overview of how the morpher algorithm works can be
described as seen in the bullet points, while an illustration of the morphing can be
seen in figure 2.6 [20].
1. Control points are collected, along with their know displacements.
2. The RBF automatic thinning optimize the number of control points.
3. The linear fitter updates the current locations of the vertices.
4. The control points and their displacement is used to create the interpolation
field.
5. The interpolation field is applied to the region.
6. Final optimization is done.
Special attention must be drawn to the mesh quality as the morpher algorithm does
not control cell quality. The lack of control can lead to negative volume cells resulting
in breakdown of the numerical solver. Mesh quality, cell skewness, and cell aspect
ratio must be monitored to ensure adequate mesh. When one or a combination of
the monitored values drop below a defined threshold a re-mesh must be initiated to
produce a new non-morphed mesh which can then be morphed further. Star-ICE

The re-mesh criteria are primarily based on four parameters the cell quality, cell

the four re-mesh groupings defined by the base, cell skewnnes, bad morphed cell, and

compared to details in the model.
The Cell quality is an algorithm which check the relative geometric distribution of
the cell as well as checking the cell face orientations. A good quality cell could be a
perfect square which would have a value of 1, while the cell is morphed and distorted
its value will decrease towards 0. If the a cell quality is below 1E-5 it is generally
considered bad, which will lead to poor convergence, and possibly affect solution
validity[20].
The cell skewness parameter measures the skewness between neighbouring cells to
ensure diffusion of quantities to be resolved well. The skewness is measuring the
angle between the cell face normal and the direct line between the centroids of
the cells. Star-ccm+ allows for skewness angles of 90°and greater, convergence are
however typically reduced as a result due to the dot product of the diffusive terms.
Generally, angles above 85°are considered as bad cells[20].
The face validity control measures the correctness of the face normals relative to
the cell centroid. The face validity is used to ensure no negative cells are created
during the morphing. Values of 1 means all normals are pointing directly from the
centroid, once the value drops below 1 the cell is considered bad. Values below 0.5
are casued by negative volume cells which will result in crashed simulations[20].
Lastly the aspect ratio of the cell controls how much a cell is stretched or skewed.
Values closer to 1 signifies a regular cell where values closer to 0 indicates a highly
stretched of skewed cell. For morphing meshes the aspect ratio must allow to be
reduced from 1, but must be limited for the trimmer mesh to ensure close to isotropic
cells.

Numerical Solver
Solving of the temporal and spatial fields in this study are done with the Semi
Implicit Method for Pressure Linked Equations, also known as SIMPLE. SIMPLE is
a pressure-based solver which is commonly used for solving CFD problems where an
iterative approach is used to satisfy the continuity, momentum and energy equations.
SIMPLE is known for being robust while when solved with the implicit time scheme
also being able to handle large time steps[19, pp. 253-257]. A flow chart of the
transient SIMPLE algorithm numerical solver can be seen in figure 2.7.
The Flowchart illustrates the main events in the SIMPLE algorithm. Engine simu-
lations require additional sub-models which i.e handle motions and interfaces com-
putations, these are omitted from the flow chart. Two stopping criterion’s can be
seen in the flow chart, first being for the convergence of the solution while the sec-
ond is for the time loop. Stability between each convergence simulation is reached
by using relaxation factors which are multiplied with the gradients of the solution
field. Relaxation factors range between 0 - 1 affecting how much of the calculated
gradient is added to the new solution. Effectively slowing down the convergence
while adding robustness from large gradients and truncation errors.
Once convergence is achieved the time loop updates the time step and reinitiate the
convergence loop. The time step must be chosen carefully even though SIMPLE
is robust at large time steps. Truncation errors increase with the time step which
in turn have an impact on the solution accuracy. To avoid truncation errors, the
Courant–Friedrichs–Lewy condition (CLF) which can be seen in equation 2.31 is
desired to be kept as low as possible. A general rule is to keep the CFL number
below 5 in areas of interest for implicit schemes[19, p. 257].

2.4.4
Combustion
Modelling of internal combustion within engines plays an increasingly important
role in the aspect of engine simulation.
The possibility to simulate combustion
offers insight into the highly complex behavior of an in-cylinder combustion at much
greater detail than else possible. Combustion happens at the molecular level within
the gaseous phase of mixed fuel and air. Simulation of combustion is usually done
through chemical mechanisms which can consist of hundreds to thousand reactions
and species.
Each species in the chemistry requires the CFD program to solve
and additional transport equation which can severely increase computation time of
combustion simulations.
Simplification of combustion simulations can be obtained with use of the Extended
Coherent Flame Model (ECFM). ECFM models uses subgrid models which simulate
different zones of the combustion process. In this study the Combustion Limited
by Equilibrium Enthalpy (CLEH) ECFM model is used as it show improved diesel
accuracy over the 3Z model[21].
ECFM-CLEH is a four zone combustion model which solves four subgrid models for
different zones in the combustion. The four zone modelled are the unmixed (UM),
premixed (PM), Diffusion Flame (DF), and post-oxidation combustion (PSTOX)
zones[20].
The three zones where combustion can occur are the PM, DF, and
PSTOX zones which consist of two transport equations. The transport equations
are addresses as Z which are calculate the mixture fraction of the fuel and Y which
calculate the mass fraction. The UM zone only consists of a mass fraction equation
as combustion can not occur in this zone.
The ECFM-CLEH model collects the zones in the global fuel tracer equation seen in
equation 2.32 and the global fuel mass equation seen in equation 2.33 The ECFM-
CLEH model then uses the fuel tracer and mass fraction to describe the reaction

CAD geometry preparations
Requirements from Star-ICE specifies that the Computer Aided Design (CAD)
model is created and imported in a specific way. The requirements enable Star-
ICE to recognise the different regions of the engine allowing the automated setup of
the IC-engine simulation. Requirements for the imported CAD body can be found
in the Star-cmm+ user guide[20, pp. 7263-7267]. For this study version 15.004.008
of star-ccm+ is used. This version does not fully support the implementation of two-
stroke engines. Which is why the scavenging inlet had to be implemented manually
through Star-ccm+.
The requirements can be divided into two categories bodies and faces. Furthermore,
is it necessary to modify the valve model to simplify the mesh in this area. A full
step-by-step guide to set up the Star-ICE simulation can be found in appendix A.4.
3.1.1
Body requirements
The body requirements are expressed in the following bullet points, and allow star-
ICE to recognize and create the position and movement of the engine.
• One CAD body for the entire wetted surface and separate CAD bodies for
each valve.
This is necessary for Star-ICE to recognise the engine and vales to create the
simulation domain.
• The piston must be at Top Dead Center (TDC) with the firedeck located at z
= 0 during import.
• The center of the cylinder must be at x,y = 0, while the movement of the
piston must be in the positive z direction during the compression stroke.
• All valves must be closed at import.
Closed valves allow for Star-ICE to create a ”valve-curtain” which is used to
cut off the exhaust/intake domain from the engine domain when these valves
are closed with use of a baffle boundary.
An example of the requirements can be seen in figure 3.1 where the coordinate
system illustrates the position, while the piston can be seen to be at TDC and
have closed valves. Additionally, when importing the geometry, the normal distance
between the valve face and the closing face of the cylinder head should not exceed
one millimeter as this can cause trouble with recognition of the valve. The normal
distance should likewise not be zero or less as this can cause trouble with creating
the full engine geometry.

Initial and Boundary conditions
This section will focus on how the Initial Condition (IC) and Boundary Condition
(BC) are applied and the reasoning behind the chosen values.
To solve a CFD
simulation it is necessary to apply IC and BC which express the flow field in relation
to pressure, velocity, temperature, turbulence, and so on. The IC and BC can have
a large impact on the solution yielded by a CFD simulation depending on which
kind of process is being simulated.
For engine simulations, it is often not feasible to run several cycles to ensure con-
ditions are correct, due to computational cost and complexity when including com-
bustion, multi-component gas and liquid while also resolving the flow. It is therefore
common to initiate the engine simulation just prior to the CAD range of interest,
which increases the dependency on the IC. In this study, the initial conditions are
based on experimental data gained from the MAN Diesel test engine.
Initialization of the IC and BC within the cylinder is easily done in the add-on
packaged star-ICE as a CAD dependent IC and BC profiles can be loaded as tables.
Within Star-ICE it is then possible to choose the desired start angle, from which
Star-ICE interpolates the initial condition to match the loaded profile. The same
procedure can be used within Star-ICE to set BC for inlet, exhaust, and wall tem-
perature. The graphical user interface (GUI) of the Star-ICE IC and BC interface

Validation
Validation of numerical models is a necessity which is often rushed, driven by the
desire to gain fast results. This approach is however flawed as the confidence of the
simulation is never established which renders the results worthless. CFD simula-
tions are principally dependent on model validation due to the non-linear nature of
turbulence and how it is modelled.
Validation of large two-stroke diesel marine engines is no easy task, as the amount
of quantifiable good data are scarce.
Fortunately, MAN Energy Solutions, who
is a collaborator of this thesis, performed PIV measurements on their in-house test
engine, the 4T50ME-X in 2014. The PIV measurements results are published in [23]
where tangential and axial velocities was captured for Normal Swirl (NS) and High
Swirl (HS) case at 25% load. The engine simulated is a digital twin of the 4T50ME-
X why the validation is based on the measured PIV data along with pressure curves
and motion comparison of the test engine and the simulated engine.
4.1
Engine cases
The Two validation cases hold many similarities given that they were performed on
the same engine which was driven under different conditions. The purpose of the
test performed by J. Hult in [23] was to see the effect of increased in-cylinder swirl
on the scavenging flow. This was achieved by fitting a belt inside the scavenging
box closing every second port. An illustration of how the belt was mounted can be
seen in figure 4.1. Based on the assumption that the engine flow is restricted by the
exhaust valve, the same mass flow should be achieved for both test cases. However,
since 50% of the ports are closed, the velocity through the ports should be increased.
4T50ME-X Test engine is a four-cylinder two-stroke uniflow engine which is used
by MAN diesel for testing and research. The technical specifications for 4T50ME-X
can be found in table 4.1 along with the case operation condition for the engine
in table 4.2. The specification shows that the 4T50ME-X is no small engine, while
only being a test engine, it further stresses the need to be able to produce reliable
results based on simulations.
It is possible from the operation conditions in table 4.2 to see that the test cases
was not performed under exactly identical operation conditions. This was due to
the belt which was only added to one of the four cylinders, increased the pressure
drop over the engine. This necessitated a higher pressure in the scavenging box for
the High Swirl (HS) case to ensure the same mass flow as for the Normal Swirl (NS)
case.

4.6
Mesh sensitivity
This section will touch the mesh sensitivity through evaluation of the necessary
discretization to capture accurate results. The purpose of a mesh sensitivity study
is to evaluate at which spatial discretization the solution of the domain is no longer
sensitive to refinements in the mesh. The spatial discretization would ideally be
refined to resolve all length scales. However since the computation time must be
addressed as well, it is necessary to find the coarsest mesh which provides valid
results. Local refinement zone plays a crucial role as these can be used to resolve
large gradients in ares of interest. The use of refinements zones can improve the
results greatly at a relatively small increase in cell count.
Reduction in the element size requires the time step be reduced as well due to an
increase in CFL number seen in equation 2.31 since large CFL values contribute to
errors in the solution. A Temporal sensitivity is not made for this study since a
complete sensitivity study requires a lot of resources and time to complete. Instead,
a suﬀicient small time step is chosen which is used in all the mesh sensitivity studies
to ensure the same temporal error for the different meshes.
Star-ICE allow the user to specify a time step table which is based on the valve
opening. Valve dependent time stepping is not optimal for two-stroke engines as
port opening are critical for the flow characteristic. However, it allows the user to
easily change to time step during the simulation, which is why it was used during the
validation and in future simulations to reduce the computation time. The timestep
table can be seen in table 4.4.
For the mesh sensitivity, the three different meshes used are seen in figure 4.8. All
three meshes are created with the same local refinements which are refined relative
to the base size. The base size can be seen in table 4.5.

Star-ICE or Star-ccm+: model
comparison
This study have been made entirely in Star-ICE, to investigate whether it was pos-
sible to increase usability and performance of in-cylinder simulations. As mentioned
throughout this study is executing in-cylinder simulations is no simple task which
requires a high level of knowledge within CFD and engines. Further does in-cylinder
simulation require large amounts of CPU-hours, primarily available in HPC envi-
ronments, to get accurate results. To Create a overview of the pro’s and con’s of
the Star-ICE add-on package a comparison of these parameters have been made.
This section will compare the usability, computation time, accuracy and model
stability of a simulation created in Star-ccm+ and Star-ICE. The simulation in Star-
ccm+ is created by Arash Nemati who has during the time span of this thesis been
working on his PhD within two-stroke Engine simulation. For the comparison cases
the supplied model has been modified to ensure a fair comparison of the models.
6.1
Usability
This section touches whether Star-ICE allows the user to save time creating and
adapting an in-cylinder simulation based on a qualitative assessment from the au-
thor of this study. It should be noted that the author has limited experience with
in-cylinder simulations created using only Star-ccm+, which potentially bias the
assessment of Star-ICE.
Through this study, it is clear that Star-ICE provide several benefits to the setup
and adaption of in-cylinder simulations compared to Star-ccm+.
The simplified
node tree leave the user with only the most necessary options to create and adapt
in-cylinder simulations. The simplicity can potentially allow the user to spend few
hours from lunching Star-ICE to being able to run the first simulation. The time
used to set-up a similar simulation in Star-ccm+ requires far more time due to the
diﬀiculty of creating Java run files, motion, interfaces, meshing, and time conver-
sion, which all requires a lot of expertise to get running properly. Once the Star-ICE
simulation is created, it can easily be adapted within Star-ICE regarding start an-
gle, running duration BC etc. For Star-ccm+ possible changes must be taken into
consideration when creating the setup, which can lead to increased creation time.
It should however be possible to create an engine simulation in Star-ccm+ with
equal adaptation as in Star-ICE. The star-ICE simulation is adapted through the
parameters node and the simulation loop is made within the Simulations operations
node.
Automation of the Star-ICE simulation however, limits the setup and running of
in-cylinder simulations to run the simulation as wanted by Siemens. This allows for
very little modification to the automated setup which can leave the user applying
workarounds to achieve the desired setup. Each workaround applied to the Star-
ICE simulation through the Star-ccm+ tree increase the instability of the simulation.

6.1
This study was done with Star-ICE V15.04.008 which was the first version to partly
support two-stroke engine simulation. The scavenging box for uniflow engine had to
be added manually through star-ccm+. The manual addition of the scavenging box
required workarounds which in turn made the Star-ICE user interface redundant.
Most changes after the addition had to be done in the Star-ccm+ tree to avoid
crashes when adapting the setup. During combustion simulations, the simulation
instability had grown to such a level that all changes had to be done in a specific
order if they were to be successfully applied. Running simulations at this stage also
required a specific approach if the SIGSEGV: memory access exception error was
to be avoided. The simulation was tested on several computers as well as Windows
and Linux operating systems, however with no change in stability.
To successfully run an in-cylinder simulation the model must be able to perform
several operations. The operations needed to be performed to ensure stability of
the model includes i.e. re-meshing and interface updating. Star-ICE controls the
simulations through the Simulations operations node, where the Star-ccm+ model
use a Java file. The Java file allows for unlimited customization but has a steep
learning curve which can consume large amount of time to get working likewise
to adapt. The simulation loop made in the Star-ICE model does not require any
further adaptation after initial creation. The simulation is made as replacement for
Java files as it supplies logic controls within the simulation which have access to
most simulations parameters without interrupting the simulation. The simplicity of
the simulation loop allows the user to further save time when adapting the Sta-ICE
simulations as a new Java does not need to be created and validated.
During the study, version v15.06.007 of Star-ICE was released with full support of
two-stroke engines. The author tried moving to the new version but was faced with
same restrictions which necessitated workarounds. The migration to the new version
of Star-ICE was therefore not deemed feasible due to the outcome most likely being
the same as the old version of Star-ICE.
With the pro’s and con’s provided in this section it is possible to sum up the us-
ability of Star-ICE in different use cases. Star-ICE is a powerful in-cylinder tool
which allows a mediocre CFD user to rapidly create engine simulations. The simple
interface along with automatized setup, aims Star-ICE toward the user or company
who want results without spending unnecessary time fine-tuning a model or simply
lack the expertise to do so. However, for expert users or academia where validation
and fine tuning is a core task of CFD the Star-ICE package falls short. limited by
the fixed setup of Star-ICE and the increasing instability caused by workarounds
leave expert users unable to fully optimize or adapt the setup to match the desired
simulation case.

Conclusion
A fully working and validated model was created capable of performing full cycle
simulation. The model was validated during the scavenging period, while combus-
tion was added to the model using both the complex chemistry and ECFM-CLEH
models. The full cycle capabilities of the model were not limited and can be solved
for indefinite amount of cycles. Preliminary results indicated that the initial condi-
tions matched the velocities from the full cycle well, at the measuring area. Several
axial and radial probes were used to analyse the full cycle velocities where a more
dynamic velocity field could be seen compared to the initial conditions.
This study adapted the model to use transient exhaust pressure and a high swirl
scavenging geometry. The adaptation using transient exhaust pressure resulted in
increased accuracy by shifting the transient pressure to achieve an equal mean pres-
sure as in the constant pressure. Generally, the adaptation of the geometry, BC and
IC in the Star-ICE model could be changed effortless from the user.
Comparison of the ECFM-CLEH ”simple” combustion model to the complex chem-
istry combustion model with regard to computational performance was successfully
done. This study compared two complex chemistry mechanisms of different seizes
to investigate whether the ECFM-CLEH model could be used to reduce CPU cost.
The comparison resulted in the ECFM-CLEH being slower than the small Muller
mechanism of 9 species while being faster than the kar mechanism of 32 species.
A thorough comparison between Star-ICE and an identical engine model created
in Star-ccm+ was investigated.
Computation performance comparison indicated
performance gains up to 11% mainly gained from geometry setup, with raw com-
putation being on-par. Accuracy comparison of the models yielded on-par results
based on the velocities during the scavenging period. Usability of the models was
qualitatively discussed where the Star-ICE Ease of use and adaptation is stressed
with the ability to create fully working engine simulations within hours. Similar
level of adaptation for the Star-ccm+ model was believed to be achievable as no
proprietary models are used for the setup.
Finally it was discussed that the Star-ICE add-on package is suitable for companies
or users who wish to create results with the least amount of time used on tweaking a
model. Expert users and academia would most likely be limited by the strict setup
of the Star-ICE model.

Future work
In this study many of the capabilities of Star-ICE are investigated. The ECFM-
CLEH combustion model is however only investigated for computational perfor-
mance and not accuracy. The accuracy of the ECFM-CLEH model should be further
investigated and validated yielding complete confidence in the full cycle simulation.
Equilibrium and tabulated kinetic ignition tables for the used fuel should be cre-
ated to yield a fair comparison between the complex chemistry and ECFM-CLEH
combustion models.
The rigid structure of the Star-ICE setup is not desirable for academia as research
requires control of the setup. The Star-ICE model should therefore be used to create
a in-cylinder setup in Star-ccm+ with the simple adaptation from Star-ICE.

Star-ICE setup step by step guide
This appendix cover the step necessary to create and run a Star-ICE simulation
case. The section will contain the dos and don’ts as well as practical workarounds
found doing this study.
It is assumed the user have basic knowledge of the Star-ccm+ interface nad use.
Add Star-ICE to Star-ccm+
• Open Star-ccm+ then enable the add on Star-ICE by right-clicking to top bar
and check mark Simcenter Star-ccm+ in-cylinder.
Figure A.5: Caption
• Click the little engine logo to open the Star-ICE simulation tree.
Import and edit geometry
• Right click Geometry and choose import CAD model, then chose one CAD
file for the full wetted area of the engine, This CAD file will include piston,
liner, head gasket, and exhaust port. Next import valves and scavenging box
chose one CAD file for each geometry.
– Dos: Ensure the valve extends out of the engine more than the max valve
movement.
– Dos: Ensure there is not above 1mm between valve face and valve seat.
– Dos: Ensure there is not above 1mm between scavenging ports and liner.
– Don’ts: Do not close the valve completely as the model will not function.
• To apply any changes to the model i.e. rename faces, translate CAD models
right click the geometry node, -> edit 3D CAD and then roll back the quick
Terminal feature before the start of editing.

Workarounds and tips
Workarounds and tips for Star-iCE are descried in this section:
• Manually assigning re-mesh CAD’s:
The remesh of Star-ICE is controlled by the In-cylinder remesh criterion stop-
ping criteria which is linked to the similar named report. In this report equa-
tion A.1 can be added to force a remesh at an desired CAD. ”CAD” is the
desired Crank angle for the remesh, ”Tol” is a half time step at the remesh
CAD. If several manually remesh are made, then these can be collected in a
separate report which can be added to the remesh report.
(abs(${CrankAngleCycle}−< ”CAD”deg >) << ”Tol”deg >)
(A.1)
• Creating of scenes and plots is easier done through the normal Star-ccm+ as
normally done.
• When creating reports or field functions which is dependent on the CAD, use
the ”Crank angle cycle” instead of ”Crank angle”. The ”Crank angle cycle”
reset every cycle where the ”Crank angle” keeps counting.
• When changing operation parameters which requires a re-mesh, before re-
meshing clear the mesh and solution. This will make sure the simulation do
not crash.
• It is not possible to split the automatic created mesh and region. If this is
done Star-ICE will move the new regions back into the original placement.
