Abstract
Resistance to microbial drugs is characterised as one of the ultimate threats to the human
health worldwide. One of the fungi that causes most death is Candida albicans, which has
a mortality rate up to 40% among hospitalized patients even when they receive medicine
and is the most common fungal disease among hospitalized patients.
To treat these
diseases new anti-fungal drugs are needed. Natural products are a promising source for
drug candidates such as anti-fungal agents because they can provide complex structures,
with high speciﬁcity and new pharmacophores.
Fungi, among others, are a typical
source for natural products due to their high production of secondary metabolites. In
this project, extracts from eight fungal strains were tested for bio-activity against C.
albicans, IBT= 656. The following species showed to have anti-fungal activity, Penicillium
astrolabium, IBT 28865, Aspergillus californicus, IBT =16748, A. homomorphus, IBT
21983, and P. frequentans, IBT 34879, whereas A. turcosus, IBT 2792, A. eucalypticola,
IBT29274, P. galathea, IBT 30014, and A. sulphureoviridis IBT 21868 had no activity.
To prioritize between the four hits dereplication was performed. Two species showed to
produce mycophenolic acid and secalonic acid. Therefore, A. californicus IBT =16748
was selected due to both promising separation abilities of the active compound, and the
fact that this fungus apparently did not to produce any known anti-fungal compounds.
Initially, a large cultivation and, extract was puriﬁed with washing steps via heptane,
dichloromethane, and methanol/water. Next, a crude fractionation was performed via
ﬂash chromatography using an amino based column material. This was followed by several
semi-preparative HPLC column puriﬁcations (e.g. C18, phenyl hexyl columns, four chiral
columns, Pentaﬂuorophenyl column and F5 column).
All puriﬁcation initiatives were
subjected to bioassays and LC-DAD-MS analysis. Based on LC-MS data and bioassay
results, a peak with m/z=604.3633 was suspected to be responsible for bio-activity.
After several puriﬁcation steps and method development a total of 0.65 mg of the
compound was obtained and analysed via 1H-NMR. The 1H-NMR spectrum showed

that more than one compound was in the sample. Furthermore, a bioassay showed that
the sample with m/z=604.3633 was not active against C. albicans, IBT =656. These

was picked as target compound.
Based on this data analysis it was not possible to
come up with a new hypothesis of which peak(s) that could be responsible for the

A detailed MS/MS analysis of the ﬁnal puriﬁed sample showed
that three diﬀerent compounds were present. Furthermore, these three compounds with

pathway. A. californicus, IBT =16748 still seems to hide several unknown compounds
even a novel anti-fungal compound based on the dereplication. Additionally, a group
of compounds related to the same bio-synthetic pathway was found.
Altogether this
project has demonstrated A. californicus to be a promising species for discovery of novel
potentially bioactive natural products.

Introduction
All healthy humans are exposed for thousand of fungal spores every day and are even
colonized by fungi without getting sick [23]. But in individuals with a reduced immune
system these pathogenic fungi can cause diseases and even lead to death [6].
One of
these fungi is Candida albicans, which is present in healthy humans without causing
harm as they are kept in check [1]. On the other hand C. albicans can overgrow and
causes infections in individuals with alternation the host immune response, changes in
the host microbiota, or variation of the local environment [56; 81; 8]. Candida infection
is the most common fungal disease among hospitalized patients, which often hits
immunocompromised individuals e.g. HIV patients, cancer patients and healthy people
with implanted medical devices [80; 36; 15]. Furthermore, candidiasis have a mortality rate
up to 40% among hospitalized patients even when they receive medicine due to resistance.
An increasing number of fungi strains are reported as resistance to anti-fungal agents and
the development of new anti-fungal drugs has lag compared to the clinical needs [58].
Therefore, novel anti-fungal drugs are essential, especially with new pharmacophores to
prevent fast resistance. The nature can be an essential source for new drug candidates
due to complexity of natural compounds and millions of years of optimization of eﬃcient
compounds. Natural products are typically collected from fungi, bacteria, or plants, which
produce a wide range of secondary metabolites to optimize their survival.

Natural Products
Products produced by the nature have been used as medicine for thousands of years.
Medicines were mostly consisting of herbs and potion before the twentieth century.
Before the twentieth century medicines were mostly consisted of herbs and potion. In
the mid of nineteenth century started a new era where isolation of pure active compounds
began. These compounds can be isolated from animals, plants and microbes and are
known as natural products (NPs).
NPs have been a major player in the history of
drug discovery and have had a great importance of our increased life expectancy from
44 years in early 20th century to around 77 years today [29]. However, the expansion
of synthetic medicinal chemistry in the 1990’s led to a drop of approved drugs based
on NPs to 50% from 80%.
NPs still have a great importance in drug discovery
due to certain beneﬁts: 1) Complex stereochemistry which increases the speciﬁcity 2)
Organisms evolutionary optimization to produce the most beneﬁcial compound [29; 7].
NP´s importance is illustrated by a study which discovered that 65% of approved
pharmaceuticals from 1981-2014 were either natural products, derived from a natural
product (ND) or synthetic with a NP pharmacophore [47]. The NP proﬁles for a lot of
microbes are not mapped [34; 30; 40]. Additionally, researchers still ﬁnd new plants [10; 11]
and new strains of microorganisms[61], which potentially can produce novel compounds
and provide new pharmaceutical candidates.
NPs are often isolated from organisms such as ﬁlamentous fungi, bacteria and plants.
These organisms convert nutrients from their environments into metabolites, which are
necessary for their growth and survival. These metabolites can be classiﬁed as primary and
secondary metabolites. Primary metabolites are compounds that are involved in essential
pathways for growth and development for the organism by modifying or synthesizing
carbohydrates, proteins, fats and/or nucleic acids
[19; 17].
The variation of primary
metabolites across organisms is limited. Secondary metabolites (SM), on the other hand,
2 of 111
==PAGE:14==
have much more variation across organisms. These organisms have developed the ability
to produce certain SMs such as toxins, signalling molecules or pigments over time to
optimize its survival. Some SMs are only produced by speciﬁc organisms under certain
circumstances such as speciﬁc media, pH, growing state, or presence of another organism
[17].
Therefore, one strain can produce many compounds which is termed OSMAC
depending on the environment [59]. The big library of SMs each microbiome is hiding
gives rise to usage/utilisation of SMs to ﬁnd new bioactive compounds, which can lead to
new pharmaceuticals.
SMs are structured of certain building blocks derived by the primary metabolism. Building
blocks and bio-synthetic pathways are used to classify SM-subgroups [17]. An overview of

• The Acetate Pathway: Fatty acids and Polyketides
Polyketides and fatty acids are produced via the acetate pathway. Polyketides are
the most abundant secondary metabolites from fungi.
They are synthesized by
a group of enzyme-complexes named polyketide synthases (PKS) via Claisen acyl
condensation pathways with acyl-CoA compounds, often with acetyl-CoA as the
precursors. These reactions will grow a poly-β-keto chain, which can undergo an
intramolecular Claisen or Aldol reaction (ﬁgure 4.2). This leads to formation of
polyketides. Fatty acids are also assembled of acyl-CoA compounds by repeated
head to tail linkages until the speciﬁc length is reached.
The process starts

• The Shikimate Pathway: Aromatic amino acids and phenylpropanoids
The Shikimate Pathway is a biosynthetic pathway which takes place in microorgan-
isms and plants, that provides compounds with C6-aromatic-C3- side chain struc-
tures e.g. aromatic amino acids, cinnamic acids, ﬂavonoids, lignin constituents etc.
Furthermore, resulting amino acids are often precursors for a wide range of alka-
loid structures [76; 17; 55]. The shikimic pathway is not as common in fungi as other
pathways are, but is highly abundant in plants [22].
• The Mevalonate Pathways: Terpenoids and Steroids.
The Mevalonate pathway is responsible for the production of terpenoids and steroids.
Terpenoids make-up the largest group of natural products and are build up by head
to tail reactions with isoprene (ﬁgure 4.4) often with further cyclization reaction.
The isoprene units can be derived from mevalonic acid (ﬁgure 4.3). Mevalonic acid
is produced from the acetate pathway and is a precursor for isopentenyl PP(IPP)
as shown in ﬁgure 4.3. An overview of the mevalonate pathway is shown in ﬁgure
4.3, where it is illustrated that steroids are generated from terpenoids [17]. Figure
4.4 illustrates the terpenoid paciltaxel which is an antitumour agent and the steroid
lovastatin which is a lowering cholesterol agent [20].

Alkaloids are nitrogen-containing molecules with a relative low molecular weight.
They are primarily produced in plants (around 77%) but also in microorganisms
and typically contain primary, secondary, or tertiary amines.
Alkaloids can be
constructed with amino acids in combination with building blocks from polyketides,
shikimic acid or mevalonic acid pathways.
The nitrogen atoms in alkaloids are
retained from amino acid and the amino acid precursor carbon skeleton is typically
retained in the alkaloid structure.
The carboxyl acid from the amino acid is
typically lost due to decarboxylation. Alkaloids are often derived from few amino
acids: ornithine, lysine, phenylalanine, glutamic acid, glycine, anthranilic acid,
tyrosine, and tryptophan. Many alkaloids have been used as pharmaceuticals or
templates for pharmaceuticals e.g. morphine an analgenic, reserpine a tranquilizer,
quinine an antimalarial, atropine as heart medicine, and vincristine is an antitumour
agents(ﬁgure 4.4) [78; 17].

Dereplication
The aim of most natural product research is to ﬁnd new molecules or a new production of
secondary metabolites from a speciﬁc strain e.g. fungus, plant, bacteria. To be able to ﬁnd
new compounds an identiﬁcation of already known compounds in the sample is needed.
This process is called dereplication.
Dereplication can save time in natural product
research, which is often really time consuming.
The dereplication process normally
combines chromatographic, mass spectrometry and spectroscopic data from e.g. LC-DAD-
MS with database searching. The LC-DAD-MS data provides a total ion chromatogram
(TIC). which is the created by summing up intensities of all mass spectral peaks belonging
to the same scan [45]. A base peak chromatogram (BPC) can be extracted from the TIC,
which is typically simpler to get an overview from. The BPC only shows the most intense
peak in the spectrum, where the TIC shows all the mass spectral peaks. If a speciﬁc
a compound is studied, an extracted ion chromatogram (EIC) can be extracted for this
ion’s m/z-value. This tool can also be used to ﬁgure out if one peak hides two ions.
By extracting two m/z-values in two diﬀerent EICs, it can be observed that the ﬁrst
EIC represent the ﬁrst part of the peak and the second EIC the seond part of the peak.
This process is termed deconvolution. The ﬁrst step in dereplication is normally to look
at the MS data of a certain chromatogram peak. On the basis of the MS spectrum it
is possible to ﬁnd the [M+H]+ ion via calculation of the mass diﬀerence to other ion-
adducts e.g. [M+Na]+ (table 4.5) and/ or ﬁnd [M-H]- in ESI- spectrum. When the mass
is determined, some conceivable molecular formulas can be calculated via Mass Hunter´s
tool "formula calculator". A number of molecular formulas can be excluded because of
a too high mass diﬀerence between adduct-ion’s mass and the molecular formula’s mass.
The next sorting process is performed by looking at the isotope pattern and predicts the
molecular formula’s isotopic pattern via an isotope distribution calculator. The highest
peak, X, comprises the most abundant isotope of each element in the molecule. A peak
at one mass unit higher, X+1, consist of a molecule with less abundant isotopes e.g.
C13. Additionally, a peak with two mass units, X+2, can appear which is often from
halogens such as Chloride or Bromide. If the molecule consists of chloride the intensity
ratio between X peak and X+2 peak is 1/3. In table 4.1, the isotopes of the common
atoms are listed[50]. When a molecular formula is determined, database searching is the

next step. Some popular databases for nature compounds are Antibase, Dictionary of
Natural Products and Reaxys. The molecular formula is used as entry in the database,
which often gives a couple of hits. To clarify if the sample molecule is present among
those hits, UV max absorbances and MS patterns can be compared. Additionally, the
molecular formula can be entry in an in-house library, which contains MS/MS information
at three energies performed at DTU Bioengineering [49]. LC-DAD-MS data is generally a
great tool to provide information for dereplication. However, some drawbacks are present

5.
High-Performance Liquid Chromatography
In HPLC the sample of interest is streamed via a solvent solution, termed the mobile
phase, with a high pressure through a column packed with solid material known as the
stationary phase. In the column the sample compounds will be separated due to physical-
and chemical properties e.g. polarity. Afterwards, the separated compounds hit a detector
that generates an electric signal the computer can read. The computer is now able to
produce a chromatogram based on the electrical signals.
When the compounds have
passed the detector they will be collected in a reservoir. The entire operation can be
automatic, so the only manual invention is the placement of the sample in the holder as
shown in Figure 1 [67].
Figure 1. Overview of HPLC process
The sample is placed in the orange holder for automatic injection or inject manually into the
system. Solvents from the solvent ﬂasks also known as the mobile phase reservoir is constantly
pumped through the system. The diﬀerent sample compounds are separated in the column known
as the stationary phase on the basis of physical- and chemical properties. A detector sense the
separated compounds after they leave the column and sends electrical signals to the computer.
The chromatogram displayed on the computer illustrates intensity plotted against time. At last
the sample and solvent will be collected in a reservoir. The system can either have one solvent a
run with a isocractic ﬂow or have two solvent which allows a gradient ﬂow [66; 44].
The strategy for separation is determined mostly from the column choice. HPLC columns
can be used for separation dependent on polarity, size, charge or speciﬁc interactions. The
column is typically ﬁlled with spherical particles mostly consist of porous silica. Figure

5.1
Parameters
For tailoring the separation of a sample, there are some diﬀerent parameters which can
be adjusted. The most utilize parameters according to the stationary phase are particle
size, pore size, and internal diameter. Additionally, the pump pressure and the mobile
phases gradient are often regulated as well.
5.1.1
Pump pressure
: Diﬀerent pumps have diﬀerent pressure capacities, but an optimal pump has to deliver
a stable pressure and a reproducible ﬂow rate. A high pressure is often favorable because
it allows smaller particles to be used [42]. Furthermore, an increase in the ﬂow-rate can
lead to sharper peaks because the compound is pressed out in a shorter time.
5.1.2
Mobile Phase gradient
: The mobile phase is typically a mixture of organic solvents and water e.g. methanol
and/or acetonitrile and water with a gradient elution. To separate compounds in a reverse
phase HPLC is a gradient elution often used e.g. 10%Methanol / 90% water to 100%
Methanol 0%water. The most optimal separation is performed with small gradient jumps
but it is time consuming.
5.1.3
Column
Particle size: As described are functional groups often attached to the surface of the
small spherical silica particles in the HPLC column. Small particles are often used because
14 of 111
==PAGE:26==
they provide a greater surface area and thereby a better separation. Additionally, small
particles can decrease the run time and increase the sample throughput. A drawback is
higher pressure is needed to get an optimum linear velosity [42; 67].
Pore size: The stationary phase is typically porous to provide a larger surface area.
Small pores lead to a larger surface area and large pores provide better kinetics. The size
of the pores deﬁnes the compounds ability to penetrate the particles and interact with
the inner surface of the particle. Smaller pores with pore diameters between 8-12 nm are
used to separation of small-molecules with a mass <10,000 Da. These pores will provide
a surface area around 125-400 m2/g, that allows the injected sample weights to be around
50 µg, if the internal column inner diameter is 4-5 mm. Larger pores with a pore diameter
between 15-100 nm are used to separation of macromolecules with a mass >10,000 Da.
The larger pores are required to get an optimal diﬀusion of the macromolecules through
the column. Wider pores will give a smaller surface area, which decrease sample weights.
The pore size is essential because the ratio between the inner surface area and the outer
one is around 1000:1 [42; 67].
Internal diameter: The internal diameter determines the amount of sample that the
column can be loaded with, furthermore, eﬀects the sensitivity. Small internal diameter
columns provide better sensitivity and lower solvent consumption, which are beneﬁcial
for research laboratories. Larger columns are usually seen in downstream processing in
the industry e.g. Pharmaceutical industry[42; 67].
Attached groups To the particles can diﬀerent groups be attached dependent on which
compounds that are puriﬁed. The diﬀerent groups can make diﬀerent interactions with
the sample molecules e.g. π-π interactions or hydrogen interactions. Other columns can
interact via a speciﬁc enatiomer, which is the case for chiral columns. Furthermore, the
groups can carry a charge and separate groups according to which functional group they
have e.g. carboxyl acid or amines

Diode Array Detector
As described does the HPLC system often have a coupling to a detector. One of the
most used detectors for HPLC systems is the diode array detector (DAD), which is used
to record the ultra violet and visible absorption spectrum. Natural products often have
some conjugated systems, which can absorb light. The wavelength the compound absorbs
light is dependent on the chromophore of the compound, which is the light-absorbing
part of the molecule. The UV-spectra of compounds are often used for dereplication of
natural products. In the DAD detector a deuterium lamp emits white light containing all
wavelengths, which will travel through a ﬂow cell that contains sample liquid. The light
will afterwards hit a diﬀraction grating that spreads out the light to a photodiode array,
which often has 512 or 1024 diodes. The individual photodiode generates an electrical
signal which can be read by the computer. Since all wavelengths are detected at the same
time can the DAD contributes to peak identiﬁcation, which single-wavelength monitoring
cannot. Furthermore, DAD can detect UV absorbtion of more analytes in a sample, which
will give a UV-spectrum for each point at the chromatogram. DAD can also distinguish
two peaks which elute with an overlap. Figure 5 illustrates a combined peak from X and
Y due to the overlap at 260 nm Figure 5b, which will occur if the solutes are monitored at
a wavelength where both analytes have an absorbance such as 260nm. If the analytes are
monitored at diﬀerent wavelengths it might be possible to distinguish the two peaks e.g.
240 nm and 280 nm. If the analytes are monitored at 240 nm only a peak for X appears
whereas only Y appears at 280 nm. DAD is able to collect data simultaneously from 240
nm and 280 nm during the chromatographic run. It is possible to distinguish X and Y
via the individual chromatograms for the wavelengths 240 nm and 280 nm. DAD can
also be used to determine the peak purity. The ratio between 240nm and 280nm can be
determine in every point across the peak in Figure 5b. If the ratio is constant the point is
pure X or Y, whereas X would be predominate if >1 and Y would be predominate if <1.
A non-constant ratio can be used to determine a peak mixture even under overlapped
conditions as Figure 5a [69].

Mass Spectrometry
Hyphenated detectors are independent detectors which are coupled to the HPLC system.
The most used hyphenated detector is mass spectrometry detector referred to as LC-MS,
but techniques such as nuclear magnetic resonance and infrared spectroscopy have also
been used.
LC-MS has been the standard analytical method to solve analytical task
with a wide range of biological molecules e.g. ﬁnd a certain pharmaceutical compound/
metabolite in a body ﬂuid or in R&D to provide structural information and purify an
unknown compound. There are two commonly used mass spectrometry conﬁgurations:
Single-stage detector (LC-MS) and multiple reaction monitoring (LC-MS/MS). Single-
state detector measures a single ionic fragment of each compound, which is often
protonated molecular ion (M+H). In multiple reaction monitoring can the precursor ion
and a range of fragments from the precursor ion i.e. daughter ions be detected [69; 52].

Mass Spectrometry
The ions need to be in gasous phase to be detected via mass spectrometer, so the mobile
phase must be evaporated and ions must be generated before MS can be used as a HPLC
detector. The two most used interfaces for these purposes are electrospray ionization
(ESI) and atmospheric pressure chemical ionization (APCI).
Electrospray Ionization An overview of the ESI interface is shown in Figure 6. The
sample solution is pumped through a high voltage capillary (2-5 kV), which can be positive
or negative charge depending on compounds chosen (positive charge in Figure 6). The
charge diﬀerence between entrance and exit of the interface system leads to a formation of
the Taylor cone, when the liquid leaves the entrance capillary. The solution is sprayed into
the vacuum-heated interface with a constant inﬂow of heated nitrogen gas, which leads to
solvent evaporation. When the solution reaches the point where Coulombic repulsion is
equal to the surface tension, known as Rayleigh limit, will droplets be generated [9; 71; 69].
These droplets further decompose to single ions by one of several proposed mechanisms
[33]. One of the mechanisms is known as the ion evaporation mechanism suppose that
evaporation of the solvent increases the charge density and thereby increase the Coulombic
repulsion. The increased repulsion will overcome the liquid’s surface tension and release
ions from the droplet surface [31]. The second mechanism is known as the coulomb ﬁssion
mechanism which suggests the evaporation of solvent increases the charge density which
cause larger droplets to split into smaller and smaller droplets until free ions are left
[18]. No matter which mechanism the ions are form by is the outcome the same. The
positively ions will be trapped to the mass spectrometer due to the negatively charge at
the exit of the interface. ESI can operate in negative- or positive ion mode resulting in
isolation of negatively- or positively charged ions. The formed positive ions will travel
through the chamber due to the negatively charge at the exit of the interface.
ESI
generates gaseous phase ions from liquid phase ions, which can be analyzed via the mass
spectrometer [9].
A major disadvantage of the ESI interface is that very little often
no fragmentation is generated this can often be overcome through the use of tandem
mass spectrometric methods e.g. MS/MS [27]. ESI is the most used MS interface for
bio-analytical applications because is a “soft” ionization method which is less likely to
produce undesirable degradation of the analytes [69].

more common. TOF works by the ions are given a speciﬁc energy and travel through
the tube to the detector. The energy given to the ions is related to the velocity of ions
through the tube according to Newton’s second law. For ions given the same applied
energy will lower mass ions travel faster to the detector than larger mass ions. The mass
to charge ratio (m/z) of the ions can be calculated on basis of the time the ions spend
to reach the detector. The length of the drift tube is related to the mass resolution. To
minimize the space of the TOF instrument and still get high resolution is TOF system
often equipped with an electrostatic mirror. A collision cell can be connected to two
TOF sections known as TOF-TOF to provide similar properties as a triple quadrupole
system.
It is also possible to connect a quadrupole and a TOF system known as Q-
TOF seen in ﬁgure 9 [69]. The Q-TOF system is bulid-up by a quadrupole that works
as an ion-selector, collision cell which lead to fragmentation of the selected ion, and a
ﬂight tube used to determine the accurate mass. After ionization the ions are lead to
the quadrupole. The quadrupole can be tuned to let speciﬁc ions through or let all ions
through termed a full scan. The selected ions are applied with energy as they enter the
collision cell. In the collision cell do the ions collide with inert gas molecules typically
nitrogen or helium. Afterwards, will the ions (fragments or original ions) travel to the
ﬂight tube (TOF). In the ﬂight tube are the ions given a speciﬁc energy by a ion pulser

Explorative Solid-Phase extraction (E-SPE) can help to design a puriﬁcation strategy if
no information is available about the target compound. This is often the case with natural
products thus is E-SPE highly useful in natural product research especially in combination
with bioassays. In E-SPE are compounds separated due to they physical- and chemical
properties via a column with similar properties as the HPLC columns. The columns can
separate molecules in regard to the acidic properties, size, polarity, etc. and can be run
fast (less than an hour). In 5.2 are some of the commonly used E-SPE methods listed. The
columns are ﬁrstly equilibrated with diﬀerent solvents to remove trapped air or solvents
and to maximize interaction between the stationary phase and the sample compounds.

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance
Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) is a type analytical techniques used by researchers
to identify the structure of a molecule.
The basic concept of NMR is that nuclei of
elemental isotopes e.g.
1H, 13C and 15N can spin and thereby generate a characteristic
magnetic ﬁeld by applying an external magnetic ﬁeld. The most used NMR isotopes are
13C and 1H. 1H-NMR can be preformed in a one-dimensional (1D) experiment where the
chemical shifts of the peaks expresses the electron density around the hydrogen nucleus.
A deshielded nucleus has a higher chemical shift (ppm)-more downﬁeld. The multiplicity
26 of 111
==PAGE:38==
and coupling constant of a peak indicates the number of neighbouring protons and how
they are organised.
These 1D concepts also comprise for 1D NMR with other nuclei
than hydrogen. Two-dimensional (2D) NMR spectroscopy provides data from two 1D
NMR along the vertical and horizontal axes. Heteronuclear multiple-bond correlation
spectroscopy (HMBC) is a 2D spectrum with 13C NMR on the vertical axis and 1H-
NMR on horizontal axis which can be used to determine the connectivity between carbon
and hydrogen atoms in the molecule.
Correlation spectroscopy (COSY) is 2D NMR
spectroscopy with both axes as 1H NMR spectra to determine the connectivity between
the protons in the molecule [37].

Another explanation, several compounds
work together in synergy to generate bio-activity and one of them might have been lost
through puriﬁcation or got below the amount to generate bio-activity. We might also have
a compound which is diﬃcult to ionize, therefore, cannot be observed by LC-DAD-MS.
LC-MS is not 100% reliable to see ratios between molecules in the samples, because some
molecules might ionize easier than other molecules. However, NMR can measure ratios
of organic molecules in a sample because they all have hydrogens and carbon atoms.
To ﬁgure out which peak that could be responsible for bio-activity, the active fractions

Conclusion
Exacts from eight fungal strains have been tested against C.albicans, IBT 656. Following
four fungi, Penicillium astrolabium, IBT 28865, Aspergillus califonicus, IBT 16748,
A.homomorphus, IBT 21983, and P.frequentans, IBT 34879, produced compounds which
generate anti-fungal activity. Based on a roughly dereplication, the fungi: A.californicus,
IBT 16748 and P.astrolabium, IBT 28865 both seem to produce a novel anti-fungal
compound with activity against C.albicans, IBT 656. 240 agar plates (120 with CYA
medium and 120 with YES medium) with A.californicus, IBT 16748 were extracted.
Dereplication was performed via LC-DAD-MS data of the crude exact for A.californicus,
IBT 16748, cultivated on CYA and YES media.
Based on the dereplication, the
compounds responsible for the major peaks in the chromatograms were all unknown. After
several puriﬁcation steps, comparison of chromatograms for active and non-active fractions
was performed and m/z=604.3633 was selected as the most likely compound responsible
for the activity. Ten columns, semi-preparative and analytical columns, were used with
diﬀerent solvents to purify the m/z=604.3633 compound. After several puriﬁcation step
and method development 0.65 mg of product was obtained. The sample was analysed
via 1H-NMR, but to little amount of product was present. Furthermore, the 1H-NMR
spectrum showed the sample contained more than one compound.
The bioassay results showed the active compound is mostly produced, when A.californicus
is cultivated on CYA medium. Therefore, extracts from CYA and YES media should not
have been combined.
A detailed MS/MS analyse showed the ﬁnal puriﬁed sample contained three compounds
with adduct-ions m/z=398.2414, m/z=578.3111, and m/=604.3633. Furthermore, these
three compounds and a compound with adduct-ion m/z=732.4459 and RT=10.48 min
share the same bio-synthetic pathway. The four compounds seems to be precursors for
each other in regard to MS/MS data.

Future perspectives
The fungus A.californicus is still very interesting for future studies due to the unknown
natural proﬁle.
One study could investigate the compounds with adduct-ions m/z=398.2414, m/z=578.3111,
m/=604.3633 and m/z=732.4459 by performing a huge extraction with A.californicus on
CYA medium to get enough of active compound and develop a puriﬁcation method. Af-
terwards, characterise the compounds with NMR spectroscopy and test bioassay against
C.albicans IBT=656 to see if the amount of product was too low in this project to gener-
ate activity. Additionally, tested the compounds bio-activity against several target such
as cancer cells, bacteria cells, etc.
Another study to investigate the bio-synthetic pathway for compounds found in this
project share could be established. If these four compounds do not have activity against
C.albicans, IBT 656 could several other compounds be separated from A.californicus,
IBT 16748 exacts, which based on dereplication information from this project contains
novel compounds structures. These isolated compounds activity could be tested to ﬁnd
the active compound. Furthermore, a synergy bioassay could be performed to investi-
gate of several compounds work together to generate activity or improve activity.
A
study to investigate the bio-synthetic pathway of the compounds with the adduct-ion’s
m/z=398.2414, m/z=578.3111, m/=604.3633 and m/z=732.4459 share could be estab-
lished and genes responsible for each step could be investigated by knocking diﬀerent
genes out and via diﬀerent bio-informatic tools. Further work to purify the active anti-
fungal compound from P.astrolabium IBT 28865 is also promising.

Shikimate Pathway
Figure 30. Shikimate pathway
The shikimate pathway starts with an aldol condensation of D-erythrose-4-phosphate and phos-
phoenolpyruvic acid which lead to 3-deoxy-D-arabino-heptulosonic acid-7-phosphate (DAHP).
DAHP phosphoric acid can undergo elimination and afterwards enter into an intramolecular
aldol reaction to 3-dehydroquinic acid.
Further elimination of water and reduction generates
shikimic acid. This pathway also generates by-products. One of the byproducts is protocatechuic
acid, which is form by dehydration and enolization of 3-hydroshikimic acid. Gallic acid also
formed from 3-hydroshikimic acid via a dehydrogenation and enolization. 3-dehydroquinic acid
can via reduction generate quinic acid.
