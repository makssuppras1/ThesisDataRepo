ABSTRACT
S
alt deposits on automotive bodies are able to create thin electrolyte layers when a certain
humidity level of the surrounding environment is reached. Hygroscopicity of de-icing
road salts is, thus, one of the key factors for the enhanced risk of atmospheric corrosion.
Automotive industry is making signiﬁcant efforts in order to decrease the cost related to corrosion
maintenance and reach optimal corrosion management. For this purpose, the aim of this thesis
is to investigate the corrosivity of road de-cing salt deposits in humid conditions. In order to do
so, a deeper understanding is needed on the process of thin electrolyte formation and corrosion
processes under thin electrolyte layers. In the present work, hygroscopic behavior of the main road
salts will be studied ﬁrst. NaCl, MgCl2, CaCl2 and mixture between them will be considered
since they represent the main road salts used. By studying water uptake and water release
the characteristic features of the electrolyte layer in humid conditions will be shown. Secondly,
a setup was designed to perform the study of corrosion behavior of automotive alloys under
thin electrolyte ﬁlms by adapting the concepts of an electrochemical cell used for bulk solution
analysis. OCP and potentiostatic measurement have been performed on AA6016 (T4) under thin
electrolyte by using the developed setup. Different corrosivity of salts on AA6016 (T4) have been
conﬁrmed but are in disagreement with bulk corrosion analysis obtained in previous works even
though the corrosion current density results in the same order of magnitude.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Introduction and research aim
C
orrosion can cause serious and expensive damage. The economic impact of corrosion on
industry has been widely investigated. A study published in 1995 by NIST stated that
in 1975 metallic corrosion cost the United States 82 billion $, which was 4,9% of its gross
natural product (GDP). [1] From that moment great improvements have been accomplished. In
fact, a study by FHWA in 2002 identiﬁed the costs related to corrosion in the United States in 3,1%
of the GDP. [2] In this landscape also automotive industry is clearly affected by corrosion both
economically and from a safety point of view. In particular, even though the corrosion resistance
of vehicles has been increased in the last decades by using corrosion resistant materials or by
corrosive protection methods such as protective coatings or plastic covers, the total annual cost in
2002 was estimated in 23.4 billion $ with 14.46 billion attributed to corrosion related depreciation
of vehicles [2]. The protective solutions used improved considerably, however if corrosion occurs
on critical parts both the lifetime of the vehicle and the safety of passengers can be at high risk.
Hence, optimization of corrosion management and development of better solutions are always
needed. Furthermore, also the different surrounding environments need to be studied since the
consciousness of the possible risks can help to implement an optimal corrosion control. (ﬁgure
1.1).
From corrosion point of view, one of the harmful substances present on the road are de-icing salts
which are used for road maintenance all over the world. De-icing salts are hygroscopic substances.
Therefore, they are able to form a water layer when the external humidity reaches values above
their deliquescence point. Indeed, the hygroscopicity of salts is the cause of several corrosion
problems in industry related issues. When a chloride salt is deposited on a metal surface it leads

The aim of this project is to investigate the hygroscopic behavior of road salts and the corrosion
of automotive alloys under humid conditions. Moreover, it aims to quantitatively characterize the
relationship between solid-liquid phase transition of road de-icing salts and resulting corrosion
response. The hygroscopicity of salts is fundamental to deeply understand the salts behavior
under different environmental conditions and their dependence on humidity levels as well as on
temperature. Afterwards, the identiﬁcation of corrosivity of those salts under the thin electrolyte
layer formed on AA6016 T4 will be studied with changing humidity.
1.2
Approach
Corrosion studies are commonly performed by combining bulk electrochemical analysis together
with surface analysis after the development of corrosion products or by accelerated corrosion tests.
The ﬁrst approach gives insights into the corrosion kinetics without considering the variation of
the external environment condition like in the ﬁeld. On the other hand, accelerated corrosion
tests give a more quantitative analysis of corrosion in ﬁeld conditions but are not able to give
insights into the corrosion mechanism since can be performed only ex-situ.
For quality control, automotive industry commonly refers to accelerated corrosion tests which
are an alternation of dry and wet phases after spraying NaCl. Due to contamination of the

instruments, however it is not possible to perform studies with salts different from NaCl and
hence, corrosion investigation with other de-icing salts is usually only possible by using bulk

A more detailed approach is given in this project to give insight into the corrosion mechanisms
when road de-icing salts deposit on the car. This approach combines the ability to follow the corro-

the accelerated corrosion tests. A new setup has been designed to study the corrosion resistance
of automotive alloys in ﬁeld conditions with different salt deposits.
In order to understand how salt deposits behave under changing humidity, the hygroscopicity of
the studied salts has been investigated by the building up of the moisture sorption isotherms and
by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy. The deliquescence point and the crystallization point
have been deﬁned for both methods by applying increasing and decreasing humidity. The effects
of temperature, salt mass per surface area and time have also been considered in order to further
correlate the various phases of the humidity cycles and the features such as thickness and salt
concentration of the electrolyte layer.
By converting corrosion tests for macroscopic studies to corrosion studies under thin electrolyte
layers, a new setup has been developed taking into consideration existing localized corrosion se-
tups. OCP measurements and potentiostatic measurements have been conducted under changing
humidity in order to record corrosion kinetics and simulate ﬁeld conditions. The humidity cycle
used summarizes the features of accelerated the corrosion tests of some of the main automotive
players.

1.3
Outline
The thesis is divided in 6 chapters. First the the de-icing salts used for road maintenance will
be discussed. Besides the legislation and techniques for their application within Europe, North
America, Russia and China are presented as well as their physical properties. Automotive
materials and the development of lightweight materials are also introduced. Simultaneously,
references to corrosion studies on automotive alloys are given to show the background in which it
has been worked. The third chapter introduces the experimental techniques used for both the
procedure investigation on hygroscopic behavior of road salts and the corrosion studies under
thin electrolyte layers. The presentation of the two main topics is followed in chapter 4 and 5.
Chapter 4 shows the hygroscopic behavior of road salts with the experimental approach used to
deﬁne the salts features at changing humidity. Chapter 5 provides background information on
atmospheric corrosion followed by the development of the setup and the experimental procedure
to conduct corrosion test. Obtained results are discussed taking into consideration previous works
under thin electrolyte layers. Finally, the main conclusions are summarized in Chapter 6 and
some suggestions for future work are given.

2
LITERATURE REVIEW
T
he present chapter aims to show the main road de-icing salts used worldwide and the
automotive materials that have been developed until now. Studies on corrosion behavior
especially when coupled with chloride based salt solutions will also be taken into consider-
ation. Road maintenance has been described as one of the biggest causes for the formation of salt
environments, which are deﬁnitely harmful for ferrous materials and other metals. Preventive
solutions such as protective coating are examined, since they are the widest and most diffuse
technique to prevent corrosion and consequently extend the lifetime of car materials.
2.1
De-Icing Salts
The increased demand for road trafﬁc safety due to the overpopulation of roads caused an ex-
ponential need of de-icing substances for road maintenance. In fact, preventive measures are
necessary in order to not let the road freeze and avoid slippering, particularly during winter.
There are different methods and substances applicable for this purpose. Each of those is chosen
based on different parameters that taking into account various factors. Those parameters in-
clude: air temperature, surface temperature, wind direction and strength, weather effectiveness,
precipitation, type of precipitation, intensity, snowfall limit, quantity of fresh snow and remarks
of the meteorologist.[4] On the other hand, the overuse of de-icing substances would affect the
society also ﬁnancially, hence, in some countries including Canada, France and Germany, winter
indexes are used. Those indexes give the number of days per year with icy conditions, leading
to an improvement in the efﬁciency and the effectiveness of maintenance by limiting the use of
those agents while keeping safety levels. [4][5]
The analysis of the previous parameters produces a series of options which can vary from the
choice of the chemical used, to the techniques of application of de-icing agents. The optimization

Among the de-icing agents available there are two main categories: abrasives and road salts.
The use of abrasives aims at increasing the friction of ice or compacted snow layers on the road
surface.[6] Instead, road salts as other chemical substances, are able to lower the freezing point of
water molecules to values below 0 °C. [5] Road salts can be divided in two subcategories: inorganic
based salts and organic based salts. [7] The ﬁrst group consists of sodium chloride, magnesium
chloride, calcium chloride, potassium chloride and carbonates, while for the organic based salts
its representatives are sodium acetate, calcium acetate, calcium magnesium acetate, calcium
formate, sodium formate, propylenglycol and urea. (ﬁg.2.1)
Chloride based salts compared to other de-icing substances are more employed due to their lower
costs, easier storing and the ones involved in breaking the passivated layer during corrosion of
metals. Therefore, the abrasives and the organic based salts will be left out from this work and
no longer taken into consideration.
Many studies have been carried out on road salts. Their environmental impacts, their depo-
sition on roads and the inﬂuence of salt particle size on the interaction with water molecules
and the formation of aerosol particles are some of the ones that aroused the greatest interest. It
has been revealed that the deposition of salts on automotive materials can occur in two different
ways: by means of salt solution droplets from the road (wet deposition) or due to condensation of

aerosol particles (dry deposition). Dry deposition usually occurs in higher extent compared to
wet deposition [9] since it can develop far from where the salt has been spread. In fact, aerosols
consist partially of salt particles which rise from the environment or from the road surface. This
type of deposition decreases with the inverse of the distance from the roadway, but can reach a
distance 100 times higher than the radius where wet deposition occurs. [9] On the other hand,
wet deposition is more likely to happen close to treated roads due to droplets jumping from the
pavement while it occurs unlikely far from the road. Nevertheless, salts embedded in aerosols
can absorb water, and, with an increased weight they fall on surfaces.
There are different techniques for road salt application: spreading of dry salt, of pre-wetted salt,
of brine or of a mix salt/brine. Each technique requires different quantities of salt used, according
to climatic conditions, trafﬁc level, time of application and type of pavement. For example, for
preventive treatments an amount between 5 and 30 g/m2 of dry salt is used, while in the case of
pre-wetted salt between 5 and 10 g/m2 of salt needs to be used. [5] If from one side it is efﬁcient to
have different application techniques for each situation, from the other point of view the volume
of salt deposition on vehicle surfaces becomes quantitatively unpredictable. Due to its instantly
acting, pre-wetted salts has been increasingly used and it is common practice worldwide. It
consists of a mix of dry salt and saturated brine. In such way, the long lasting effect of dry salt
and the acting speed of the brine are combined. [8] Moreover, the pre-wetted salt adheres easily
to the pavement compared to the dry salt, it is blown less easily from the carriageway, and it can
also lead to cost reduction compared to brine due to its effectiveness. [5] On the other hand, brine
is better for preventive measures and is made up usually with NaCl together with MgCl2 and
CaCl2 which are more efﬁcient at low temperatures.
Looking at the national country reports [4][5][8] the most used de-icing salts are NaCl, CaCl2
and MgCl2 and mixtures between them (ﬁgure 2.3). Though NaCl is the most economically
advantageous, handy, easy to store and to spread for road maintenance, CaCl2 and MgCl2
have been extensively used in those areas where the adverse atmospheric conditions require an
extreme lowering of the ice freezing point. [10]
The phase diagrams of those chloride based salts, explain why different salts are used in different
climatic conditions (ﬁg.2.2(a)). The eutectic temperature differs from salt to salt and deﬁnes the
lowest reachable freezing point by adding a certain amount of salt to water. Furthermore, the
depression of the freezing point is a colligative property, and hence, depends as well on the ratio
between the number of solute particles and the number of solvent molecules in solution. In fact,
as can be seen from ﬁg.2.2(a) until the eutectic temperature, the higher the concentration of the
salts the lower the freezing point will be. A further increase beyond this concentration will lead
to precipitation of salts(ﬁg.3.3.1). Melting of snow or ice occurs when the freezing point of the
brine is lower than the temperature of the surrounding environment. The higher melting ice rate
will appear for those salts with a greater difference between their eutectic point in water and the
ambient temperature. In order to deﬁne the temperature at which the salt remains efﬁcient, it

Sodium chloride is provided by means of rock salt or obtained through evaporation of sea
water. Its effectiveness depends on the particle size as well as its melting capacity. Fine particles
act quicker than coarser particles but have a less wide spreading. Additionally, a coarser particle
size causes a maximum service temperature increase from -21,2 °C to higher values.

can immediately act. The process of dissolution of NaCl is an endothermic process: heat from the
surrounding is converted to solvation energy to allow the salt to dissolve. This causes the surface
temperature to slightly cool down [8] and the variation of temperature in the surrounding, can
affect the melting capacity of NaCl. Consequently, NaCl is not effective at -21,2 °C anymore and
its maximum service temperature is in a range between -8 °C and -15 °C.
In order to store and transport sodium chloride in its dry state, an anti-caking agent is usually
added. This agent can also inﬂuence the melting properties of NaCl, hence, its use needs particu-
lar cautiousness.

It gives off heat to the surroundings, eventually helping to heat up the surface temperature of
the road. The biggest issue arising with the use of calcium chloride is that especially in its brine

2.2
Automotive materials
Automotive materials have seen several changes during the last 50 years. Prior to 1970s plain
carbon steel and cast iron were used almost for each part of the vehicle. [14] Afterwards, the
demand for stronger steels ﬁrst and the increased demand for lighter vehicles in recent years
led to the development of new materials and new manufacturing techniques. On the other hand,
the focus on mechanical properties and performance of the car as well, could not be put on the
back burner. Hence, a compromise between mechanical properties and density has always been a
guideline for the material selection and development.
To have a wide overview of the material chosen within this scenario, it is simpler to ﬁrst deﬁne
the main parts of a vehicle since the selection and manufacturing process can be different for
each part in order to fulﬁll determined functions. A vehicle can be divided in 5 systems:
• Body;
– it consists of all those parts which contribute to the exterior model of the vehicle,
including the structure, panels, windows and trims. Before the painting processes, the
parts that are welded together are called body-in-white (BIW) and it does not include
the movable parts such as doors, hoods, lids and fenders.
• The electronic system;
– all the devices which need current to perform their function are considered.
• Power-train and chassis;
– braking system, the engine, the engine cooling system, the fuel supply system, the
transmission system, the suspensions and the steering system. Since some of them are
positioned in the bottom part of the car, this system can also be called "underbody".
• Interior;
– it consist of those parts that are within the car in contact with the driver.
• Miscellaneous (air conditioning system, air-water system, radio,...).
In total, a vehicle counts several thousands parts. Each of those needs a clever design in order to
reduce as much as possible the weight, for example reducing the thickness, while providing cost
efﬁciency. These requirements are fundamental to provide low fuel consumption, low environ-
mental impact and exhaust emissions. [15] Of course, the customer segment to which the car is
manufactured for, inﬂuences the decision making in the material selection process. For instance,
aluminum alloys and magnesium alloys are extensively used for luxury cars, while their use is
more restrained in all the other cases. Lightweight materials are gradually substituting steels
even though momentarily the latter still represents the majority of the automotive vehicles. In

coating. The methods used by car industry are hot-dip galvanization (HDG), electrogalvanization
(EZ), galvannealing and the deposition of the Duplex/Alloy. [14] [21] Being able to increase the
reliability of the steel, they turned out to be excellent solution for the lifetime warranties of
vehicles. [21] Each of those methods differ by the thickness of coating, surface ﬁnish and adhesion
properties. Hence, according to individual preferences, car manufacturers use different kinds of

2.2.3
Aluminum alloys
Aluminum alloys have gained increasing interest in the last decades due to its low density, high
speciﬁc energy absorption performance and good speciﬁc strength. [17][24] Secondly aluminum
has very high formability which allows easier manufacturing and less invasive joining processes.
Lastly, the highly protective thin oxide barrier which is formed instantaneously on its surface in
contact with the atmosphere gives it a very high corrosion resistance.
Both cast and wrought aluminum alloys are used in car industry. For instance the engine, the
transmission and the suspension are made by cast aluminum alloys while wrought aluminum
alloys are used for the body structure and body panels. Hence, all the series 2xxx, 3xxx, 5xxx and
6xxx contribute for ﬁnalizing the manufacturing. Even though both the 5xxx (AlMg alloys) and
6xxx (AlMgSi alloys) series can be theoretically used for outer application and the BIW due to
the similar combination of properties, only the 6xxx is preferred for body structure and panels
because of more homogeneous visual surface appearance compared to the 5xxx series, which
instead develops strain marks during forming. [24] The 6xxx series contain both Mg and Si which
give them the age-hardening behavior at a speciﬁc temperature range of the annealing process,
while the 5xxx series looses its strength with heat treatments. Hence, AlMgSi alloys are often
cold rolled, pre-aged and then aged (temper T4). The strengthening effect of the T4 temper is not
only determined by the content of the main alloying elements but also by less present elements
such as copper and manganese. The very ﬁne precipitated elements or the phase formed typically

Figure 2.8 shows the Pourbaix diagram for pure aluminum in water. Even though it seems that

In corrosive environment, intermetallic phases can be the initiation site for corrosion due to the
galvanic coupling formed between the more noble phase and the less noble phase. [26] Those
micro-galvanic cells result in selective dissolution of grain boundaries and the depletion of the
solute elements in the surrounding area. [26] Of course, the bigger the population of those
micro-phases or solute elements the higher the number of pit present on the surface will be.
This last explained corrosion mechanism is called intergranular corrosion and is mostly seen
in all aluminum alloys which have more than 3% Mg. [26] However, this is not the only type of
corrosion present within aluminum alloys.
Cosmetic, crevice and ﬁliform corrosion are forms of corrosion occurring in automotive aluminum.
[21] Corrosion mechanisms have been reported in relation to aluminum alloy 6016 T4 since this
heat treated alloy has been extensively used for body structure, panels, hood and fenders and it
is more exposed to corrosion related issues. Besides the advantages of having alloying elements
which allow the strengthening effect, the presence of low levels of copper in the AlMgSi increases
the corrosion susceptibility to ﬁliform corrosion in 6016 T4. [21]
In the 6016 T4 the intermetallic phases of magnesium silicide (Mg2Si) remains less noble
than the matrix in Al-alloys. As a consequence of this, Mg2Si remains anodic and undergoes
selective dissolution resulting in intergranular corrosion. [25] Hence, Mg2Si provides cathodic
protection reducing or postponing corrosion of the Al matrix by making it the cathode of an
electrochemical cell. This results in cathodic polarization of aluminum, which tends to suppress
oxidation reactions in favor of reduction reactions such as oxygen reduction. However, with the
dissolution of Mg from the precipitates, the precipitates will become cathoidc toward the matrix
and the matrix will start to corrode.
On the contrary, Mn and Si reduce the cathodic current density decreasing the galvanic effect
between the matrix and iron particles. This was evident in a study where 6016 T4 aluminum
alloy was studied with iron bearing particles which are more noble with respect to the Al matrix.
A small amount of Mn and Si was able to increase the localized corrosion resistance and retarding
pitting initiation lowering the galvanic action. [26]
In conclusion, it can be said that if from one side the amount of solute strengthens the alloy, on
the other hand it decreases the corrosion resistance, affecting the corrosion potential, pit mor-
phologies, pit repassivation, population, size, repassivation rate, distribution of crystallographic
pits, behavior of intermetallic cathodes, weight loss and the initial position of the pit. [26] Hence,
the features of the microstructure are highly affecting the way corrosion occurs and, appropriate

2.2.5
Corrosion protection solutions
Since metals are participating to electrochemical processes leading to deterioration of the material
and to a decrease in mechanical properties, car companies needed to improve the lifetime of
the outer and inner panels. Thus, the development of protective layers was necessary in order
to slow down corrosion processes and enhance resistance to harmful conditions. One of those
solutions includes the application of a metal coating which acts either as a barrier or as a cathodic
protection for the upperbody and underbody. Plastic covers are also used to protect parts which
are more susceptible to stone chipping. Another solution focuses on the development of painting
systems in such a way that besides being decorative it can also provide corrosion protection.
Lastly, wax and sealers have also been taken into consideration in order to protect car materials
especially from corrosion perforation.
Conversion layers
Figure 2.9 shows all the layers which are applied on a metal substrate during vehicle manufac-
turing.
The ﬁrst coating layer above the galvanized substrate or the uncoated metal is the conversion
coating, which usually consists of a tri-cationic (zinc, nickel, manganese) phosphate thin coating
by mixed spray-dipping. [18][27] This chemical conversion coatings are inorganic crystalline or
amorphous, adherent and insoluble layers which are formed by dipping the metal into a solution
by means of a chemical reaction when the metal is dipped into a solution. In this process, the
metal surface is converted into one of the components present in the bath. The new protective ﬁlm
is more resistant to corrosion than the base material and it neutralizes the formation of micro
galvanic cells of the base alloy. The conversion coating for automotive alloys commonly consists of

a phosporous layer, although Zr based layers are gaining interest. This type of protection is also
used because it enables better adhesion of the second layer: the ED-coating. [28]
ED-coatings
The e-coat is an organic coating made by an epoxy based paint resin and pigments, and deposited
by a cathodic process. A uniform smooth surface can be achieved for this layer by controlling
the deposition thickness. In fact, monitoring the parameters of the bath such as the applied
voltage, the bath temperature and the resin concentration, it is possible to create a more compact
coating. Pinholes and cratering are also reduced by a strict parameter control. [21] The result
is a protective coating able to act as a barrier for scratching or stone chipping. Furthermore an
increased energy absorption is provided due to an enhanced adhesion of the multilayer. [21]
Paint coatings
Paint coatings are the last layers applied on the upperbody after the ED coating. They consist
commonly of a primer, a basecoat and a clearcoat. Besides giving the aesthetics properties, it also
provides protection to the underlying layers. The painting is a mixture of a resin and a binder
and is applied by spraying. Prior to spraying, adequate cleaning is performed in order to allow
better adhesion between the paint and the surface. Painting can also be used in combination with
additives which inhibit corrosion.
Damage of the paint will put at risk full functionality of the paint, which will be detrimental for
the substrate. Cosmetic corrosion or delamination can suddenly initiate in this conditions. The
initiation is visible in early stage on the exposed layer, but the propagation of it depends on the
depth of the damage and which layer has reached. [21]
Waxes and Sealers
Within the BIW, there are some areas on which application of coatings results from difﬁcult
accomplishment. In fact, electrocoating requires a homogeneous current density on the surface of
the parts in order to have a uniform thickness and quality of the plated layer. Typically, these
areas like hem ﬂanges, lap joints and cavities have lower current density or lower homogeneity,
because they are acting like a Faraday cage preventing deposition. [29][21] Hence, this areas are
particularly affected by perforation due to the wedging of salts and water, and can be susceptible
to the aggressive environment formed by the de-icing salts and the natural environment. Wax
and sealers are hence used as satisfactory option to protect the underlying metals. Nevertheless,
an appropriate design is required in order to enhance coatability. [21]
Cavities have extensively been studied [30] in the frame of corrosion related issues. In this
sense, electrocoat primer, anti rust agent and further adhesives were able to lower the corrosion

3.1
Gravimetric measurements
Gravimetric methods are based on weight measurements to make a quantitative analysis of
the concentration of an element within a blend. The principle behind this type of analysis is
to extrapolate the mass of a compound starting from a known mass of the same analyte of the
unique compound when a ion’s mass has been determined. In fact, as long as the weight of only
one compound varies duringheating, the gain or loss of weight can be determined. In such a way,
assuming that the only molecule which are participating to this reaction is water, each element
can be quantiﬁed precisely. In this work the reverse process is used. By measuring the gain of
weight starting from a dry compound the amount of water is determined. [32]
3.2
Electrochemical measurements
3.2.1
The Electrochemical cell
Electrochemical cells are used to investigate the corrosion parameters, represented mostly by the
potential and current, that occurs during an anodic and cathodic reaction due to spontaneous
reaction or induce by applying a potential. Commonly, an electrochemical cell consists of of two
or three electrodes, a working electrode (WE), a reference electrode (RE) for the 2 electrode
setup and additionally, a counter electrode (CE) for the 3 electrode setup. Those electrodes are
immersed in an electrolyte which enables the development of the redox reactions. [33]
The working electrode is the material to be tested and is where the half cell reaction takes
place while the reference electrode, which has a stable and well known potential, is used to
measure the working electrode potential. The most used reference electrodes are the saturated
calomel electrode and the saturated silver/silver chloride electrode which are aqueous electrodes
based around a saturated aqueous solution. The counter electrode is often fabricated from
electrochemically inert materials such as gold, platinum, or carbon. [33] It balances the reaction
occurring at the working electrode using the same magnitude of current without compromising
the stability of the reference electrode by passing current over it. Hence, the CE is used to
measure the current. If oxidation occurs at the WE, reduction occurs at the CE and no current
ﬂows between the WE and the RE develops, enabling to follow changes in the working electrode
potential. In the ﬁgure 3.1 a 3 setup conﬁguration for an electrochemical cell is shown.

3.2.2
Open circuit potential
The open circuit (OCP) potential is the potential of the working electrode relative to the reference
electrode when no potential or current is being applied to the electrochemical cell. The OCP
is used to measure the tendency of the working electrode to participate to corrosion when it is
surrounded by a corrosive medium. On a substrate both half cell reactions will appear due to the
presence of different phases. At the anodic areas oxidation will occur while at the cathodes one or
more reduction reactions develop. The OCP will give insights about the equilibrium potential
between anodic and cathodic reactions and the time needed to reach equilibrium. [33]
During OCP measurements, the anodic and cathodic current densities are equal. [33] The time
needed to reach equilibrium depends on the medium and the surface of the sample. Moreover,
the OCP gives an indication of how noble is a material in a determined electrolyte. By comparing
OCP values it can be understood which are the conditions in which a determined substrate is
more resistant, where it can passivate and where is more active.

Potentiostatic measurements
During potentiostatic measurements the current response as a function of time is investigated.
Potentiostatic measurements are a DC test which provides information on the corrosion rate.
A potential is applied at the working electrode while the potential difference between the WE
and RE is kept constant. The current density can then be measured knowing the exposed
surface. In this tests the net current measured is the difference between the anodic current and
the cathodic current. The potential applied will give insights about the kinetics of the process

3.2.4
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS)
EIS is a method used to monitor the response of an electrochemical system to an applied potential.
Contrarily to voltammetry, in EIS experiments, a small electrical perturbation signal is applied
in order to give a response which is pseudo-linear.
It is well known that every circuit has an ability to resist to the current ﬂow. This ability for
a non-ideal resistor however is given by the impedance, which differently from a resistance, is
dependent on the frequency of the current. Hence, the EIS measures this values. A potentiostat
applies a sinusoidal signal which is superimposed on the DC polarization U0 and and can be
deﬁned as,
u(t) = U0 · sin(wt).
As a consequence the resulting current I is as well a sinusoidal function and is recorded for
each value of w (the angular excitation energy), which is varied in a wide range of frequencies.
This current signal can be analyzed as a sum of sinusoidal signals and converted in a Fourier
series.[34] Applying different frequencies it is possible to isolate, and theoretically simulate,
different phenomena such as the the double layer close to the surface or the conductivity of an
electrolyte. In conclusion, this method is of fundamental importance to understand different
features of an electrochemical process.
This tool is used for the characterization of coatings, batteries, fuel cells and corrosion phenomena.
For the latter, it is particularly interesting because the small AC current does not compromise
the surface morphology of the tested sample.[34]
The data are recorded in function of the time and then converted with the Fourier transform to
complex data depending on the angular excitation energy (ω) which can be represented in polar
coordinates or cartesian coordinates. The resulting values give the impedance (Z) and shift phase
(Θ) in the so-called Bode plot where the impedance and the shift phase are linked as shown in

3.3.1
Scanning Electron Microscope-Energy dispersive X-ray analysis
(SEM-EDX)
SEM-EDX is a surface analysis technique used to characterize and identify the morphology and
chemical composition of a surface. It has several advantages compared to a traditional optical
microscope, since it has very high magniﬁcation, lateral resolution in the range of microns and
combined with EDX allows to determine the chemical composition of a surface. For this reasons,
SEM-EDX is a powerful and widely used tool, for instance, for the evaluation of coatings or
surfaces after electrochemical experiments. On the other hand, SEM-EDX has also its limits such
as the necessity to operate in vacuum and the ability to scan only conductive materials which
slightly reduces its range of applicability. [35]
A primary beam of electrons is generated by a ﬁeld emission or a thermal gun. The ﬁlament
releases electrons when heated by a current of around 60 µA. Those primary electrons are
then accelerated by an electric ﬁeld within the gun up to 40kV. Before impinging the sample
surface, the primary electrons pass through a series of condenser lens, a scan coil and objective
lens which enable them to focus on small spots.[36] Figure 3.2 shows a schematic view of the
interaction volume, which represents the volume of the sample which is reache by the primary
beam electrons. Here the primary electrons are deﬂected due to interactions with the metal
sample. The process in which primary electrons are deﬂected within the interaction volume is
called scattering. During the scattering different interactions can be generated, either elastic or
anelastic and consists of 4 different signals: Secondary electrons, Auger electrons, X-rays and
backscattered electrons. While backscattered electrons are formed by an elastic interaction, the
other are generated by an inelastic interaction.

The secondary electrons (SE) are low energy electrons and are generated in the ﬁrst 100nm

4.2
Hygroscopic behavior
The hygroscopicity of a substance is the capacity to take up water from the external environment
and it induces the growth of particles and the conversion of solid particles to aqueous droplets
after deliquescence. [39] The hygroscopic behavior occurs in contact both with liquid or moisture.
This work will focus on this last factor: the moisture. De-icing salts are strongly inﬂuenced by the
external atmosphere and the water vapor present within it. Clear effects on automotive metals
can raise from this interaction. Indeed, the absorption of water layers by de-icing salts as well as
the invisible water layer absorbed by the metal surfaces at critical humidity levels create wet
corrosion and atmospheric corrosion. [40]
If a hygroscopic substance (e.g. an inorganic salt) is exposed to a humid environment, it is able to
create hydrogen bonds with the water molecules present in the moisture. There are 5 different
water-solid interaction reported between a crystalline salt and a water molecule in humid environ-
ment to describe the hygroscopic phenomena: adsorption, capillary condensation, deliquescence,
dehydration and crystallization (or efﬂorescence). [41][42] Each of those interactions occur at
a characteristic water activity (aw). The aw is the ratio between the water vapor pressure in
solution and the vapor pressure of pure water and it is directly linked to the relative humidity,
since they can be assumed equal with the only difference of the percentage notation. Thefore
from now on, the relative humidity will be considered instead of the water activity.
4.2.1
Water Solid Interactions
In ﬁg. 4.1 the water solid-interactions have been shown when a substance is subjected to an
increasing humidity. The ﬁrst interaction which appears at low humidity is called adsorption.
During this phenomenon the water molecules get in contact with the salt surface either via
hydrogen bonding, either electrostatic or change transfer. Usually during this period, only few
layers are absorbed on the crystalline surface [42][43]. At increased relative humidity, capillary
condensation occurs. It consists of a liquid bridge between two close surfaces or pores. This occurs
when the thermodynamic activity of the surrounding water vapor drops off due to the formation
of meniscus in contact areas between the solid particles and the vapor, resulting in condensed
water particle. A further increase in humidity, when it exceeds a threshold value characteristic
of the road salt, a gradient between the water chemical potential on the external layers of the
crystalline solid and the water in the atmosphere is established and deliquescence occurs due
to the driving force formed by the chemical potential gradient between the water in the ﬁlm of
saturated solution and the pure water in vapor. [44] Deliquescence is a phase transformation
wherein ﬁrst the salt surface and then the bulk dissolves until a salt saturated solution is formed
from condensation of water vapor at a deﬁned relative humidity at a ﬁxed temperatures and
isobaric conditions. [42] In crystalline solids deliquescence occurs abruptly, even though for values

just above the deliquescence point the process is very slow. When the surrounding environment
reaches higher values of relative humidity and deliquescence has reached equilibrium, complete
dilution of the salt in water occurs.
If the relative humidity of the surrounding environment decreases, a reverse process called
dehydration appears. Water molecules are pulled out in this reversed process from the solution
until a critical value of relative humidity (deﬁned as CRH) is reached. Here an abrupt complete
crystallization (or efﬂorescence) occurs. The expulsion of water from the crystallized material
and re-crystallization in its ordered structure brings the salt back in its dry state. [42]
The reason why salts crystallize is because the system tend to reach the state with its minimum
in free energy and the free energy of the initial solution is greater than the sum of the crystalline
phase plus that of the ﬁnal solution. Hence, the driving force can be expressed as a change in
the chemical potential of the substance (∆µ) which measures the free energy gain or cost for
substance transferring from one phase to the other.

shown elsewhere in literature for the MgCl2 while for CaCl2 it is a bit lower compared to the

and MgCl2 it is possible to see a less abrupt change in morphology of the salt and a two phase
transformation, one at the DRH of pure MgCl2 and one at values closer to the DRH of NaCl
(around 75%) can be identiﬁed. This two phase transformation for binary mixtures has already
been explained elsewhere [44][47] and two main causes have been found. The ﬁrst one involves
the composition of the mixture. It has been proved that part of a blended salt will deliquesce at
a shared DRH which can be deﬁned approximately with the Ross equation, called mixed DRH
(MDRH). The part that will deliquesce ﬁrst will have a composition called the eutonic composition.
The remaining blended salt in solid state will deliquesce at a DRH closer to the value of one of the
pure compounds depending on the molar fraction of the remaining salt in solid state. The second
explanation is related to the intimate contact between the salts. If the established contact is not
good, or if there are some areas with higher concentration of one salt, various DRH will develop.
[44] As an example it is possible to consider the mixture between NaCl and MgCl2 (ﬁgure 4.8). It

4.5.2
Dependence on Mass per Surface Area
In ﬁgure 4.10 the water uptake for samples with 30 mg, 60 mg and 500 mg of salt are compared
when spread on the same surface area. The water uptake signiﬁcantly increases after the deli-
quescence point for the sample with 30 mg while in the same time interval the water uptake of
the sample with higher mass per surface area is considerably lower.
A different water uptake behavior has been exploited when comparing the moisture sorption
isotherms of sodium chloride, magnesium chloride and calcium chloride (ﬁg. 4.10 and Appendix
A.2 ). It is seen that, the water uptake decreases with the amount of salt per surface area when
the time between each measurements it is not long enough to reach thermodynamic equilibrium.
This phenomenon has been called in this work coverage effect. The suggestion here is that the
absorption of water is governed by diffusion through the salt crystals. Hence, water molecules
are not able to reach the lowest salt layers on the sample during the interval used. Previous
studies have been studied that deliquescence is initiated by capillary condensation for values
just below the DRH. In case of multiple salt layers this capillary condensation does not occur ho-
mogeneously, since the water molecules are able to reach only few salt monolayers. [42] Moreover
it has been suggested that deliquescence is also affected by the physical accommodation of water
and gas/liquid partitioning which of course results to be lower for the deepest salt layers. The
uptake process can hence be considered as a set of various phenomena. [50]

Besides the effect of the amount of deposited salt and the effect of the temperature also the effect

should crystallize mostly in the center part due to the higher thickness of the water layer in the
central part of the samples caused by the higher surface tension of aluminium.

5.1
Literature review
The deposition of road de-icing salts is one of the key factors affecting the corrosion resistance of
metals exposed to the atmosphere. Indeed, the favoring of surface electrolyte formation through
the absorption of water vapor from the moisture lead to electrochemical instability and to the ac-
tivation of corrosion processes. Both the nature and the composition of the salts heavily inﬂuence
46
==PAGE:56==
CHAPTER 5. ELECTROCHEMICAL TESTS OF AA6016T4 UNDER HUMID CONDITIONS IN
THIN ELECTROLYTE LAYER
the water absorption phenomena as pointed out in the previous part of this study (chapter 4).
As a consequence, when electrolyte droplets are absorbed by de-icing salts, the electrochemical
situation of the surface changes from homogeneous to heterogeneous. This means that local
anodes and cathodes are established faster on the metal and corrosion can occur via galvanic
coupling in very short time compared to when the process is not driven by soluble particles.
[54][55]
Even though for many years it was generally considered that cations had limited effect on the
atmospheric corrosion, it has been recently proved that this adopted view was misleading. [3][53]
Moreover, cations have a strong inﬂuence on the solubility of the salt, hydrolysis, and the pH
of the water layer formed. Moreover, they have the ability to form insoluble corrosion products.
Hence, the investigation of corrosion under humid conditions due to road salts deposits can be
converted into two different studies: corrosion under thin electrolyte layer and the inﬂuence of
concentration of the salt on corrosion.
Corrosion under thin electrolyte layers (TEL) was ﬁrst pointed out by Tomashov when he intro-
duced the concept of atmospheric corrosion. This occurs in humid environment in thin electrolyte
ﬁlms or even adsorbed layers. [56] He suggested that the thickness of the electrolyte had rele-
vant inﬂuence on several processes during corrosion phenomena such as the mass transport of
dissolved oxygen, the accumulation of corrosion products, and the hydration of dissolved metal
ions. [57] He also tried to interpret its result to differentiate the type of corrosion according to
the thickness of the water layer. His model distinguished four different processes: "dry atmo-
spheric corrosion" (X f < 10nm), "moist atmospheric corrosion" (X f : 10nm−1µm), "wet corrosion"
(X f : 1µm−1mm) and "complete immersion" (X f > 1mm). His studies revealed that during the
initial stage of the atmospheric corrosion of iron, the process is governed by anodic control for
thinner layers and by cathodic control in thicker layers. Above 1mm instead, he noticed that the
results obtained were the same as for bulk solutions indicating that the thin water layer consisted
of 2 layers: a diffusion layer and a convection layer. As a result the change in X f affected the
convection layer and thus the mass transport of oxygen.[56][57] This model has been for long
time accepted, however the boundary condition shown in his studies differs from metal to metal,
and from electrolyte to electrolyte.[53]
Since Tomashov has introduced this concept, atmospheric corrosion has been widely investigated.
Surface corrosion studies are present on iron, steel [56] [58][53], zinc [59][53], magnesium [60],
tin [61] and aluminum [57]. From their studies it has been conﬁrmed that corrosion is already
present in electrolyte layer with the thickness in the order of a few microns. However, they are
in disagreement with the subdivision of the different process made by Tomashov.In some cases
"complete immersion" can occur already at thicknesses lower 300µ [61].
A consolidated approach to study corrosion under thin electrolyte layers has not been developed
yet. The common difﬁculties in the thin electrolyte layer measurements are the use of a correct
design of experiments and a correct testing method. Up to now, the most used techniques are: the
47
==PAGE:57==

THIN ELECTROLYTE LAYER
zero ammeter resistance method, polarization method with the use of a Kelvin probe, the holo-
graphic interferometry technique, EIS and linear polarization techniques with a three electrode
electrochemical cell disposed in a clever design.[56] [58][53][59][60][61][57] [62]
Techniques to investigate corrosion under thin electrolyte
In the zero ammeter resistance (ZRA) studies, two electrodes of the same metal specimen are
placed close to each other and covered by a thin layers of electrolyte solution. The current is
measured at the open-circuit potential. The current ﬂowing between the two electrodes normally
occurs due to the absence of the electrical resistance in the substrate/electrolyte interface and
the presence of electronic noise. [62] However, for being able to measure correctly the corrosion
rate, the noise resistance needs to be evaluated. This procedure might introduce some errors in
the measurement because the noise within ZRA depends also on the impedance of the electrodes.
Secondly, the behavior of the corrosion process is affected by the generated noise. [63]
Scanning Kelvin probe instead, uses a vibrating capacitor (the Kelvin probe) which measures
the potential generated between the specimen and its vibrating tip. [64] The tip works as a
reference electrode and the potential difference measured maps the corrosion potential of the
surface. [65] The disadvantage of this technique is that the potential measured deviates from the
real corrosion potential due to the presence of oxygen generated by the oscillating probe tip. [60]
On the other hand, the KPS is able to make a detailed map of the anodic and cathodic areas of
the working electrode.
The holography interferometry technique can be used as an optical transducer to characterize
electromagnetic ﬁeld. If on the electrode surface in aqueous solution an anodic reaction takes
place, dissolution of the metal occurs. As a consequence the reﬂected light coming from the
optical interferometry will result in a different phase and amplitude. The resulting variation of
phase and amplitude of the reﬂected light on the surface and a mathematical correlation of the
electromagnetic ﬁeld will lead to the measurement of the corrosion kinetics and electrochemical
properties. This method works by an electromagnetic mean rather than electronic mean. Thus, it
does not comprise neither electrical resistance nor electronic noise. [62]
Lastly, EIS and the linear polarization method, deal with a three electrodes electrochemical cell.
Hence, both methods need the development of a correct setup and correct testing method in order
to minimize the polarization resistance that commonly develops across the interface between the
sample surface and the electrolyte, and consequently affects the potential difference between the
working electrode and the reference electrode. [62]
Among those techniques, the EIS method and the linear polarization method have regained
interest because of their more predictable measurement deviations and easier development. [60]

5.3
Humidity cycle
In this study corrosion under TEL with de-icing salts have been investigated during humidity
cycles. The features of the dry phases and the wet phases of the applied cycle have been chosen
considering the important characteristics of different automotive accelerated corrosion test cycles
in order to reproduce ﬁeld conditions. [13] This allows to subsequently understand what happens
in each phase of the cycle and how corrosion proceeds during the different phases. Figure 5.3
shows the ﬁnal cycle used for the OCP and potentiostatic measurements. Since the kinetics
of salt deliquescence is governed by how much the relative humidity exceeds the DRH of the
salt, studying corrosion parameters at different humidity levels will deﬁnitely be affected by the
hygroscopicity kinetics. Hence, only one relative humidity to represent salt spray phase, one for
the wet phase, and one for the dry phase have been chosen in order to have more comparable
result. It can be noticed that the salt phase has been considered as a wet phase where 98% RH
(which is the maximum which can be achieved with the humidity chamber) is applied since in
the accelerated corrosion tests this phase is distinguished by the application of salt solution. The
features of the dry phase differs heavily between different automotive industries. It has been
reported that the dry phase is commonly performed at humidity levels below 35% RH. Hence, a
humidity of 20% was used in order to get closer to the CRH of the salts used. In addition, a higher
kinetics for the crystallization process should be expected at 20% RH compare to higher values of
humidity. The temperature is kept constant at 25 °C. Even though the corrosion is affected by
temperature variation, doing experiments varying both parameters would make it impossible to

electrode as reference electrode and a counter electrode made of graphite. The potentiostat used is

interface) in combination with the Corrware software. The electrochemical cell was positioned
inside the humidity chamber CTS T-40 to expose it to the humidity cycle described in the previous

Prior to testing, the electrochemical cell and the aluminum sample have been rinsed with water,
cleaned with Isopropanol and aerated with a pressurized air. After, sealing with Silicone and
tape is accomplished in order to avoid unwanted leakage of the thin electrolyte layer. For all the
electrochemical measurements the temperature is kept constant at 25 °C. The amount of dry
salt deposited on the exposed aluminum surface is 27mg/cm2. Every measurement has been
conducted 2 times per each salt to ensure reproducibility. Figure 5.4 shows the setup consisting
of the electrochemical cell, humidity chamber, potentiostat and CorrWare software.
5.4.1

reached due to pitting initiation and localized chemical reactions that occurs on the surface when
dealing with aluminum alloys [66], which tends to slightly oscillate the OCP. The OCP has been
recorded after 2 hours since the oscillation seemed to decrease in amplitude.

The corrosion behavior of AA6016 T4 has been investigated under thin electrolyte layers which
are due to the hygroscopicity of road de-icing salts. OCP measurements revealed that during
the humidity cycle chosen, the corrosion potential under TEL formed by different road de-icing
salt deposits varies with the time due to the hygroscopic behavior. This leads to a modiﬁcation
of the thickness layer and a modiﬁcation of the salt concentration. AA6016 T4 with CaCl2 salt
deposits however showed the lowest differences in OCP variation while NaCl showed the biggest
OCP variation because of the almost complete crystallization that NaCl and the commercial

6.1
Introduction
In this project the relationship between solid-phase transition of road de-icing salts through the
study of the hygroscopic behavior and the corrosion response of AA6016 T4 has been characterized.
To do so an electrochemical setup has been developed. 6 different salts were included in this
study of the hygroscopic behavior of pure salts and of mixture of salts. Instead 5 different salts
were used to conduct the electrochemical analysis on AA6016 T4 under thin electrolyte layers
formed by a changing humidity.
6.2
Inﬂuence of humidity on the physical state of de-icing salts
Deliquescence, which is the phenomenon of uptaking water by chemical substance resulting in the
formation of water droplets was deﬁned. In particular, the deliquescence point (DRH), which is
the characteristic relative humidity at which this phenomenon occurs, has been characterized by
using two methods: the moisture sorption isotherm and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy.
Crystallization which is the process of releasing water and formation of the salt crystal from
solution has also been investigated. The study conﬁrmed what was already found in literature. For
all the salts DRH occurs at higher relative humidity compared to the CRH leading to the presence
of a water droplet for longer time. On the other hand, mixture of salts showed deliquescence
lowering, which means that the phenomenon of deliquescence and efﬂorescence occurs at lower
relative humidity compared to the pure compounds. The inﬂuence of time, effect of salt mass
per surface area and temperature were also investigated. This parameters besides affecting the
kinetics of the water uptake and water release, did not inﬂuence the DRH and CRH.
Deposition of salts on metal surfaces has been studied in order to study the wettability of the

6.3
Development of the setup for electrochemical analysis
under thin ﬁlm electrolytes due to changing humidity
Corrosion studies of automotive alloys were performed under humid conditions with salt deposits
to deeply understand ﬁeld conditions. This particular study was converted as a study of corrosion
under thin electrolyte layer in humid conditions. A complete new setup was developed based
on speciﬁc requirements and based on research on previous similar studies. This result in an
electrochemical cell made by 3D printing which allowed to perform correct tests.
6.4
Electrochemical analysis - Effect of humidity on corrosion
behavior of AA6016 T4
Corrosion studies were performed by changing the humidity between 98% RH and 20% RH in
order to simulate automotive accelerated corrosion tests. OCP measurements during the full
cycle (with an extra wet phase at the beginning) on AA6016 T4 showed that the OCP varies with
the changing humidity during the full cycle for MgCl2, CaCl2 and the mixture between NaCl
and MgCl2 while commercial salt and NaCl showed a similar behavior. However NaCl showed a
higher difference in OCP compared to the wet phase during the dry phase. Hence, potentiostatic
measurements cannot be considered as a completely reliable tool to study the corrosion activity
under thin electrolyte layers with changing humidity. However, this is not the case for CaCl2
since the OCP varies in a smaller range. Corrosion current density is thus measured in the
ﬁrst part of the cycle at 98% RH and shows that the corrosivity of the salts follow this order:
NaCl>MgCl2>NaCl:>MgCl2>CaCl2>commercial salt.
6.5
Future works
Corrosion studies under thin electrolyte layer are widely studied. However insights of corrosion
under thin electrolyte layer simulating ﬁeld condition is an undiscovered area. Due to the uncer-
tainty of the measurement it is suggested to conduct more electrochemical test on AA6016 T4
in order to compare the results of different salts and metal substrates. Also tests on galvanized
steel and steel with the same developed setup should be performed. In order to come to a fully
understanding of the corrosion behavior of AA6016 T4 however, it is suggested as well to conduct
electrochemical tests with other setups. By doing so, side effects on the design used in the present

work could be better understood. During this project, a graphite rod counter electrode has been
used, it would be interesting to use a platinum counter electrode to see the effects of the CE on
the measurements. It is also recommended to use a bigger surface area of the counter electrode
in order to not risk the eating of the counter electrode during current measurement. It has been
noticed that the reference electrode mostly upside down can create some small bubbles on top of
the column, which are in direct contact with the thin electrolyte layer. Hence, a setup which is
able to position the reference electrode in a way that does not create bubbles is suggested.
Another recommendation is as well to set consecutive OCP and potentiodynamic measurement
with a limited potential range at different stages of a humidity cycle in order to have more
accurate current measurement. In such way, the risk of polarization far from the OCP will be
avoided.
Furthermore, in order to understand the effect of each de-icing salt on the corrosivity of auto-
motive alloys, tests with ﬁxed thickness of the water layer or ﬁxed salt concentration should be
performed. Hence, a detailed comparison would be possible, understanding as well the role of the
thickness of the electrolyte later on AA6016 T4 and other automotive alloys.
Lastly, due to the crucial role of oxygen in thin electrolyte layer, XPS analysis and SEM analy-
sis should be conducted in order to bring evidence of precipitates formation and corrosion products.
