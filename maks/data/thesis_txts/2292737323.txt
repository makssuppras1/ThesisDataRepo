Abstract
The present report is a design proposal for a demountable dome built of concrete elements. The
architecture is inspired by the plywood dome used at previous Roskilde Festivals, designed by K.
Tejlgaard and H. Almegaard. The dome is of the type hexagonated geodesic sphere, with a diameter
of 21 m and a height of 7.3 m. Each lattice in the dome is 1.2 m wide, framing large hexagon and
pentagon openings.
With this project, we aim to show the way towards a design and building method for concrete
domes, which is more economically, aesthetic and environmentally sustainable, than the traditional
shell constructions, cast in-situ.
The dome is analysed by means of FEM with two diﬀerent models: a beam model in STAAD.Pro
and a shell model in ABAQUS. The two models complement each other as basis for understanding
the behaviour of the dome and for designing the elements. The theory of solid shell domes is used as
reference for analysis, but cannot be compared directly as the geodesic dome with its openings works
as a frame-structure rather than a pure membrane shell.
The dome is analysed with a proper combination of loads. Results show that the dome constitute
a rather stiﬀstructure, like it is the case for solid shell domes. Stress concentrations are found at the
corners of the geodesic frame-structure, induced by large in-plane moments.
Analysis results show also that tensile forces occur in the lower region for some of the horizontal-
oriented members. Therefore discontinuous post-tensioning is proposed for prestressing of the ele-
ments. Furthermore, post-tensioning is a simple way of connecting pre-cast elements that allows them
to later be disassembled.
The report also discusses pros and cons of two diﬀerent shapes for the repetitive structural element,
and proposes a detailed design of a Y-shape, having connections where the forces are the lowest. For
this element shape, results show that one post-tensioning monostrand in each of the three directions
is suﬃcient for load carrying capacity.
The report includes preliminary design of a foundation to take up the vertical load and counter
the ring force generated by the dome. Finally, proposals for the production of the concrete elements
and the erection at site are given.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
At the last four Roskilde Festivals, a geodesic dome of plywood-elements has been erected, used for
diﬀerent kinds of activities and smaller concerts. Architect of the Dome is Kristoﬀer Tejlgaard with
Henrik Almegaard as engineer. According to Tejlgaard, the plywood dome has been a great success
and an attractive meeting point, situated at the camping cite of the festival. The connections of the
plywood elements, however, were considered worn out after four erections and take-downs. A more
permanent, but still demountable solution is sought by the Roskilde Festival, able to resist the load
from a green roof.
1.1
Roskilde Festival
Situated near Roskilde, Denmark, the festival is the largest music festival in Northern Europe, at-
tracting around 130.000 visitors and volunteers, and some of the world’s greatest rock and pop stars.
Roskilde Festival is a non-proﬁt foundation, where proﬁt goes to charity. The festival is much into
sustainable solutions and communication of these ”green-tech” concepts to their visitors - powered
by a collaboration with students from DTU. The next dome at Roskilde Festival must also be in line
with the sustainable approach.
1.2
Concrete
Concrete is the most used material in the building sector. Especially in Denmark, concrete elements
are widely used as the main structural component, as it provides a stable and rigid building with
short erection time. Therefore concrete is an obvious option to investigate for the next version of the
Dome, and element structures have the opportunity to be disassembled into elements again, unlike
in-situ cast structures.
1.2.1
The Material
The material properties of concrete are in many ways beneﬁcial for dome structures, and most struc-
tures in general:
• High self-weight enhances stability
• Utilising compressive strength
• High durability and low maintenance
• Good ﬁre resistance
• High thermal mass
Some of them are described more into detail later in this report. Moreover, concrete is a cast material,
making in principle every shape possible.
Eco-cement, a new sustainable alternative to Portland cement could be considered for this project
to improve sustainability signiﬁcantly. However eco-cement in its present state of development has
some critical limitations, e.g. leaching of alkalis when in contact with water and lack of applicability
for prestressed concrete.
1.2.2
History of Concrete Domes
Domes and arch-like structures have been built in concrete from ancient time, as it is the case for
Pantheon in Rome built in 128 A.D or Villa Hadrian near Rome built at the same time. Churches,
temples and other large-scale projects from the Roman period did rely on the nature of compression
in domes, vaults and arches as to be built of stone, bricks or early versions of concrete without steel
reinforcement at all. So the Pantheon is still one of the largest concrete domes ever built without
steel reinforcement [David Moore (1995)]. A diameter of 43.3 m, however, requires a massive dome
structure of 5.9 m in thickness at the base, decreasing to 1.5 m at the top.
Palazetto dello Sport, also located in Rome built in 1957 by engineer and architect Pier Luigi Nervi
is one of the most famous modern concrete domes, having a diameter of 60 m. Constructing the dome
in only 30 days was made possible with the clever use of 25 mm thin prefabricated concrete elements

outside [Iori and Poretti (2013)]. Compared to wooden formwork for in-situ casting the dome, the

Another well-known method of constructing concrete domes is with the use of a large air balloon
or ”air-form”. The concrete is then poured or sprayed onto the surface of the balloon, often with
some kind of reinforcement mesh attached. After curing of the concrete the balloon can be deﬂated,

History shows that domes have been used extensively for religious and other large-scale buildings
from the past. Also hundreds of cost-eﬃcient small housing-domes have been built with air-forms
during the 20th century, especially in the US. Today concrete domes are not that attractive due to
high labour costs for building up the scaﬀolding, or the fact that the dome-houses couldn’t compete
with more traditional housing shapes, architecturally and practically.
1.2.3
Demountable Concrete Structures
Suggestions for demountable concrete element building systems have been developed and investigated
for many years. Research included in the symposium proceedings of Reinhardt and Bouvy (1985)
mainly focus on traditional building systems (connections for columns, walls, beams and slabs) pre-
pared for disassembly with the use of bolts and nuts. Some of the solutions were also tested on real
projects, especially in the Netherlands, but apparently these concepts have not been adopted widely
in the building sector. The most plausible reason is that an (un)likely future structural conversion
or complete disassembly of e.g. an oﬃce building cannot justify higher construction costs because
of the advanced connections. For structures like geodesic domes, very limited information exist on
demountable solutions.
1.3
Problem Statement
Inspired by the architectural ideas from the Roskilde Festival plywood dome, it may be possible to
reinvent the way of building concrete domes to be economical, aesthetic and environmental more
sustainable, than domes build with traditional methods.
With this hypothesis in mind, the task is to design a demountable dome to be built of pre-cast
concrete elements.
Attention must be paid to water tightness and robustness.
The report must
include a realistic design proposal of the Dome containing information enough for contractors to be
able to give a qualiﬁed tender.

METHOD
2
Method
The method used in this project is a combination of computational modelling of the structure and
iterative design phases regarding all aspects of the dome.
The project is basically divided into an analysis phase and a design phase as it is considered
important to understand the behaviour of the structure in order to know how to design.
2.1
Understanding the Dome
First step in understanding the Dome is to investigate the special and strict geometry. The architec-
ture is mainly inspired by the works of Danish engineer Ture Wester, especially Structural Order in
Space - The Plate-Lattice Dualism (1984). Wester was one of the leading authorities in the research
of optimized spatial structures inspired by nature.
The commercial FEM-software STAAD.Pro is used for initial and rough analysis of the structure,
as it gives an easy statical overview of section forces and deﬂection for diﬀerent load cases and bound-
ary conditions. For detailed stress distributions and buckling analysis the FEM-software ABAQUS
is used.
Results from the FEM calculations are compared to the theory of dome analysis. Design and
Analysis of Shell Structures (1992) by M. Farshad is used as reference. No direct theory of analysis
of geodesic domes with similar geometry as the Roskilde Festival Dome was found, so the theory of
domes can only be used as indicative.
2.2
Designing the Dome
With results from the analysis, connections and elements can be designed. This is an iterative design
process, as connection properties and element thickness change the model results and vice versa.
The design phase includes research and development for ﬁnding the best solutions, which are
discussed by ”pros and cons” evaluation where necessary. The process also includes contact with
relevant suppliers/companies to provide input for a realistic design proposal.

STRUCTURAL CONCEPT
3
Structural Concept
The structural concept to be used in this design is that of a compression only dome. Due to gravity the
dome is prestressed by its own weight. In that sense concrete is beneﬁcial, both due to its compressive
strength and its high self-weight compared to other building materials. Adding earth load for a green
roof is assumed to further prestress the concrete. Extra prestressing forces can be added by pre- or
post-tensioning steel tendons, for best possible utilisation of the concrete cross section.
The compression force in the dome generates a tensile ring force at the bottom to be countered by
a post-tensioned concrete foundation ring. The ring prestress is assumed to equal the outward force
from the dome, so that the foundation can be regarded as ﬁxed for outward movement.
The dome elements are regarded as curved shell-elements able to take both shear and bending,
in- and out-of-plane. Around the openings ”window-frames” also as concrete elements are attached
to the structure. They are not considered in the general static system, but can be regarded as extra
safety for local stability reasons such as lateral buckling or as extra safety for the connection between
elements, depending on the design.
The structural system built up by elements is assumed to be designed so that rigidity and behaviour
is as if the structure was cast in-situ.

THEORY OF DOME GEOMETRY AND STRUCTURAL BEHAVIOUR
4
Theory of Dome Geometry and Structural Behaviour
In order to better understand the structure and its behaviour, the special geometry of the dome and
its theory is described.
4.1
Theory of Geodesic Geometry
The structure of the Roskilde Festival Dome is of the type geodesic sphere, patented by Buckminster
Fuller back in 1954 [Buckminster Fuller Institute]. The deﬁnition of geodesic by Oxford Dictionaries:
Relating to or denoting the shortest possible line between two points on a sphere or
other curved surface.
(Of a dome or other structure) constructed from struts which follow geodesic lines and
typically form an open framework of triangles and polygons.
The geodesic dome is geometrically evolved from the icosahedron and dodecahedron, which are
members of the ﬁve Platonic polyhedra. There are two types of Platonic polyhedra, described by
Wester (1984):
• Triangulated faces (∆)
• 3-way vertices (Y)
Figure 1: The 5 Platonic Solids, Wester (1984)
4.1.1
Hexagonation
Building up a geodesic structure with either triangulation or hexagonation can be performed by the
method described in Wester (1984) and shown in ﬁgure 2: The basic triangular face of the icosahedron
is divided into a number of smaller triangles, from which the corners are projected onto the surface
of the circumscribed sphere. For hexagonation, tangent planes to the sphere surface are constructed
in the projected corner points. The tangent planes will intersect one another, shaping hexagons.
Note that pentagons are created at the vertices of the icosahedron, with a total of 12 pentagons on
a hexagonated sphere. Note also that the above described method takes its starting point in the
icosahedron, which can be seen at the ”mother” geometry for both triangulated and hexagonated
geodesic spheres. However, due to dualism between icosahedron and dodecahedron, the latter is seen
as the origin for hexagonated plate structures, as seen in ﬁgure 3.
The number divisions of each edge of the regular basic triangle into smaller triangles is called the
frequency. The triangulation pattern of the basic triangle can be divided into two classes, I and II
(look for the diﬀerence in ﬁgure 2). The frequented lattice and plate structures are illustrated and
compared in ﬁgure 3. It must be noted that only a few elements in the hexagonated structure are
regular (the pentagons and the ”central” hexagons, if any). This means also that there is a number
of diﬀerent edge lengths, i.e. varying element lengths.
4.1.2
Examples of Geodesic Structures
Most of the geodesic domes built by Buckminster Fuller (or inspired by his works) are a lattice
structures, often built of steel or wooden bars. This also applies for Tejlgaard & Jepsens Dome of
Visions. In nature, the Y-structure is more common, e.g. sea urchin or the carbon molecules called
fullerenes (named after Buckminster Fuller). Examples are given in ﬁgure 4.

4.2
Structural Theory of the Dome
The two geometrical types of geodesy (∆and Y) depicts two ways of structural behaviour [Wester
and Feilberg Hansen (1985)]:
• ∆: The triangulated structures will remain stable by lattice action alone.
• Y: The 3-way vertex structures will remain stable by plate action alone.
4.2.1
From Plate to Frame Structure
As the Roskilde Festival dome is a Y-polyhedra, it must work in plate action. However, the dome
has large openings in each penta- or hexagon, indicated with b in ﬁgure 5. In order to maintain the
same stability as a solid plate structure, the dome must be able to work as a frame-structure. This
induces in-plane moments in the corners/vertices.
Figure 5: Transformation from plate to frame action by increasing the opening in the plate. At least
3 stiﬀcorners are required in each hexagon-frame to remain stable, Wester (1984)
4.2.2
Shell Theory
It is assumed that the Dome, however, still can be considered as a shell structure, as the dome
elements follow the curvature of the circumscribing sphere. Thus the Dome can be compared to
classical shell theory, where the following is assumed [Farshad (1992)]:
• The shell is thin (thickness ≪radius).
• Plane sections normal to the shell mid-surface remain plane and perpendicular to the deformed
mid-surface.
• Stress normal to the shell mid-surface can be neglected.
• Displacements and strains are so small that their higher orders can be neglected.
Shell structures are also characterised by being very stiﬀand strong compared to other structural
forms because of their spatial curvature. The internal forces in a shell can be divided into two ﬁelds,
the membrane and the bending force ﬁeld. External forces in shells are mostly carried by membrane

4.3
Geometry of the Dome
In the Dome at Roskilde Festival, each element represents one intersection line, shared between two

5
Analysis Parameters
The dome is modelled and analysed with commercial ﬁnite element (FEM) software. Initially the
structure is modelled in STAAD.Pro (version V8i, SS5) with beam elements. This rough model is
fast in computation time and gives an overview of section forces and structural behaviour. A more
detailed analysis is performed in ABAQUS as a shell model with a proper mesh, giving stress and
deﬂection values along with eigenvalues and mode shapes for buckling analysis. In this section the
input parameters for analysing the Dome are given. They are mainly based on the guidelines of
Eurocode.
5.1
Material Properties
The material used for the Dome must meet diﬀerent types of criteria related to e.g.:
• Applied loads
• Fire
• Exposure to the environment
• Architectural expression (also over time)
Concrete is, as stated in the introduction, a good choice, meeting most of these criteria. The
exposure class of the concrete is taken to be aggressive (XF3), meaning that the strength must be
at least fck = 35MPa [Jensen (2008)]. The modulus of elasticity used for analysis is Ecm = 34GPa,
corresponding to 35 MPa concrete [DS/EN 1992-1-1 FU (2013)] and is expected to be on the safe side
for deﬂection calculations as the maximum stresses in the Dome will be in the range of 0.05−0.10fck,
see ﬁgure 11. For Poisson’s ratio, ν = 0.2 is used, as concrete is assumed to be uncracked for the
majority of the Dome [DS/EN 1992-1-1 FU (2013)].
Concrete is here analysed as linear-elastic isotropic material, i.e. with equal properties for ten-
sion and compression, assuming that reinforcement will be able to take up tension where needed.
Furthermore the modulus of elasticity of concrete is approximately the same for both tension and
compression for uncracked sections [Yoshitake et al. (2012)].
Figure 11: Stress-strain-relationship for normal concrete, showing the secant modulus of elasticity,
Ecm. [DS/EN 1992-1-1 FU (2013)]
5.2
Boundary Conditions
The support conditions of the dome must represent the behaviour of the contact between the lower-
most concrete elements and the foundation-ring. The support is modelled as a simple line-support,
i.e. free to rotate out of plane, but ﬁxed for movements.

5.3
Loads
Even though the loads are based on Eurocode, important choices and assumptions must be made
in order to get realistic loadings for this special structure, and still be sure that calculations are on
the safe side. The dome should also be able to withstand other situations than just at the Roskilde
Festival site, in order to make the design more robust and to be able to raise the Dome elsewhere.
5.3.1
Self-Weight
The self-weight of the structure includes all load-bearing and non-loadbearing structural elements and
ﬁxed loads. The characteristic density of the reinforced concrete structure is taken as ρc = 25kN/m3.
Self-weight of the windows is disregarded, but the weight of the frames is accounted for. Earth load
for green roofs is regarded as a permanent load and is therefore to be included in the self-weight
[DS/EN 1991-1-1 (2007) 2.1(4)P]. Assuming a growing medium of 100 mm and a build up as shown
in ﬁgure 12, the load is set to Gearth = 1.5kN/m2. Earth load is disregarded if self-weight acts in
favour, as it is not sure that the green roof system will be used in general for a mass-produced dome.

6.1.3
The eﬀect of earth load
Similar test as for increasing the thickness of the dome has been carried out for earth load on the
dome, as this is expected to prestress the dome beneﬁcially. This is to some extend true, as earth
load will counteract tensile forces from e.g. upward wind load. Meanwhile the structure does not
gain stiﬀness from adding earth load. Tensile forces will, according to the STAAD.Pro-model occur
in some of the lower elements for element thickness less than 400 mm, regardless the amount of earth.
Actually the tensile force increases for increased earth load, corresponding to the theory of shells
(equation 2).
6.1.4
Conclusion on the shell thickness
Conclusion to this investigation on the thickness eﬀect is that the Dome gains both stiﬀness and ben-
eﬁcial compression with increasing thickness. The meridional forces follow the theory of shell domes,
whereas the hoop-forces in the tensile, lower region, contrary to the theory, tend to be compressive
(or less tensile) due to the geometry of the Dome. To have a compression only dome for all load
combinations, however, it requires an excessive thickness. Adding extra earth load will only increase
tension at some places and can therefore not compensate for adding structural thickness.

6.7
Conclusion on FEM Results
Thickness analysis proves that it is possible to have a compression only dome, but an excessive thick-
ness is required. Therefore it is assumed to be more economical and environmentally sustainable to
use prestressing to overcome tensile forces whereby also the concrete material is better utilised. Alter-
natively, heavy reinforcement would be required in order to prevent severe cracking of the concrete.
Deﬂections are quite small, reﬂecting well structural shell behaviour. A connection at the mid-edge
of the polygons will add robustness to the structure.
The section forces from the STAAD.Pro beam model were found reliable, except for the out-of-
plane bending and the corresponding shear force. There, it has been necessary to account for the
curvature of elements, which in most cases eliminate the presence of bending, but in some cases also
adds moment to the element.
Even though stress calculations for the maximum positive and negative stresses might be aﬀected
by singularity problems, stress-levels below 3 MPa are acceptable and their distribution across the
element does look like normal linear elastic distribution. It is also a fair assumption to use linear
elastic theory for modelling the FEM-models due to these low stress levels.
Regarding buckling, Riks-analysis gave the most reliable results, stating that there is no risk for
buckling or dynamic issues, even though the Dome might in reality be less stiﬀdue to connections
and cracking of concrete than it is modelled. So the design of the Dome can be based on the results
from the section forces found in STAAD.Pro, with reﬁnement for the out-of-plane forces.

Connection Type
Connecting the elements require proper attention, as this deﬁnes the way the structure can work. The
aim is to make connections strong enough to withstand the forces found in chapter 6. The simplest
form of connection is to have concrete in pure compression, end to end. However, shear, moments and
tension require mechanical connection between elements, together with a water-tightening appliance.
Furthermore it must be kept in mind that the connection must be able to be separated again in order
to be a demountable structure.

7.3
Post-Tensioning
Results from the FEM calculations show that there will be elements subjected to axial tension. To
overcome this, post-tensioning can be applied to the elements in axial direction, in order to utilise the
strength of the concrete. Furthermore post-tensioning can be an integrated way for the connections,
too. Research by O.E. Pedersen at Aarhus School of Architecture (DK) demonstrates the possibilities
of discontinuous post-tensioning for a concrete-wood pavilion, see ﬁgure 45.
For the Utzon:40 Pavilion threaded steel bars of ø4 mm were post-installed through plastic ducts
[Pedersen, Larsen and Pigram (2014)], and due to simple nuts at the ends, the elements are de-
mountable, too. Scaling this principle up to ﬁt the Dome, commercial post-tensioning systems such
as Macalloy bars could be used. However, bars are only recommended to be used as straight bars
[Macalloy (2012)], and due to curvature of the elements high-strength steel cables are preferable in the
case of the Dome. According to Skandinavisk Spændbeton, methods exist for releasing the tensioned
tendons again, in order to have a demountable dome. This require the tendon ends to remain uncut
to be able to do a reversed jacking task.

8.1
Prestress Loss in Post-Tensioning
Prestress loss is calculated for the eﬀects of:
• Friction between strand and coating
• Draw-in of the anchorage wedge (wedge set)
• Relaxation of steel strand
• Creep and shrinkage of concrete
Friction loss is generated during jacking, wedge set just after releasing, whereas relaxation and creep
and shrinkage is longterm loss, calculated for inﬁnite time to be on the safe side. A friction coeﬃcient
µ = 0.05 is used [Skandinavisk Spændbeton (2010)]. The curvature change in the tendon depends
on the ﬂow of the duct, thus the curvature is not always equal the radius of the dome, as the ducts
must lay on top of each other at the crossing. As Ø is smaller than Din, the tendon will straighten
out when tensioned, within the boundaries of the duct. In ﬁgure 48 the duct-ﬂow giving the largest
angular variation of the tendon is A with θ = 14.3°.

8.4
Buckling due to Prestressing
Prestress forces will always search for equilibrium within the structure itself. This means that if post-
tensioning has a resulting force at the center of the cross-section along the length of the element, the
shape remains unchanged, even if the elements are curved. Buckling due to prestressing is impossible
for members with internal tendons with contact points between its anchorages, which is the case for
curved members. The prestressing force will support the structure in its given shape. [Libby (1990)]

PRODUCTION AND CONSTRUCTION
10
Production and Construction
Throughout the report, production and construction of the Dome has been kept in mind and described
where necessary. In this chapter methods and challenges of the production and construction of the
Dome are summed up and discussed.
10.1
Element Production
10.1.1
Formwork
The dome element is quite diﬀerent to normal precast concrete elements. First of all, the double
curvature of the Y-element is a challenge regarding form and casting. The double curvature of the
form can however be produced by joining three single-curved steel plates to form the Y.
A real challenge occur when recalling that there are 3 variations of the Y-element, with diﬀerent
lengths of the ”legs” in the Y, but also with slightly diﬀerent angles, varying from 108° to 123°
(measured in the common plane between two legs). In order not to be forced to produce three forms,
the form must be adjustable. The length variation can easily be overcome with sliding the end plate
fourth and back. The angle change can be overcome with inserting triangular pieces at the vertex
to ﬁt the exact angles, but ﬁxing of the three individual plates to the surroundings is required, as
welding is precluded.
The element can be produced with window-frames as a cast-in-one element, but the easiest way
to do this is by casting the inside up.
Figure 66: Formwork for dome element with the principles of adjustable lengths and angles
10.1.2
Concreting
For the window frames a self-compacting synthetic ﬁbre reinforced concrete is preferred, whereas this
is not necessary for the main part of the element. Thus the frames can be cast with the special
concrete just before casting the rest with standard concrete. The risk for poor concreting will be
largest at the top of the frames (bottom of form), where ﬁnishing might be necessary.
Depending on the concrete mixture used for the main part, the curvature and thickness of the
casting can possibly be controlled by the workers at the factory, avoiding an extra form for the
upside. As the visible inside of the Dome is cast as upside, many opportunities exist for adding an
architectonic expression to the surface, but a smooth ﬁnish might be preferred.
10.2
Dome Construction
Dome construction can be done in the straight-forward sequence:
1. Excavate for the foundation
2. Place and post-tension foundation elements
3. Erect bottom dome elements at their right position with propping at the free ends
4. Earth is ﬁlled back around foundation and bottom elements and compressed
53
==PAGE:62==
Design of a Demountable Concrete Dome
5. Continue lifting elements in place successively with temporary propping
6. Tendons are run through each completed span of two elements and tensioned during the erection
phase
7. The last top-element is lifted in place with the use of ﬂat-jacks, pressing the elements slightly
away from each other, giving space for installation
Three lifting-anchors are integrated into the dome-elements. With a proper preparation of the
lifting-chains, the element can be lifted from ground to the ﬁnal position with the right inclination for
faster installation. The individual chain-lengths for each element can be calculated from the β-angles
used for turning the beams in STAAD.Pro, explained in section 5.4.1. The β-angles are given in
Appendix A in degrees.
The stepwise post-tensioning of the elements as mentioned above has several advantages. First
of all it is easier to adjust the elements to each other before the Dome is completed, in order to be
able to run the tendon through the connection. The structure also gains stiﬀness by its own when
post-tensioned successively, avoiding extensively falsework and thus only reusable propping is needed.
The study of Pedersen, Larsen and Pigram (2014) has shown that the structure in ﬁgure 45 is able
to cantilever before completion of the wooden top part, i.e. without propping at all.

CONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION
11
Conclusion and Discussion
This project shows through computational and theoretical analysis that a new version of the Roskilde
Festival Dome can be built of post-tensioned concrete elements. The Dome is demountable, because
unbonded post-tensioning tendons are the only physical connection between the elements.
11.1
Main Conclusions and Findings
The theory of geodesic geometry constitutes a basis for the design of the Dome. In chapter 4, this
theory has been introduced and it has been shown how the geometry of the Dome can be described
by a geodesic sphere, hexagonated to frequency 4, class II. Opposite a pure lattice structure, the
Dome must work as a frame structure in order to remain stable. This induces in-plane moments to
be taken by the 1200 mm wide elements.
In the project, the Dome has been analysed with FEM 3D models in both STAAD.Pro and
ABAQUS to give both statical overview and detailed analysis, respectively. All relevant input for
the analysis of the Dome has been presented in chapter 5. Here also the loads and combinations are
described, including self-weight, earth-, person-, snow- and wind-load, based on Eurocode and Danish
National Annex.
The material is assumed to be linear-elastic and isotropic, without considering
amount of reinforcement or post-tensioning in the models.
The results of the computational analyses have been presented and discussed in chapter 6. The
computational analysis of the eﬀect of shell thickness showed that 700 mm of massive concrete would
be required to ensure a compression-only dome. Adding extra earth load would not eliminate tensile
forces in the lower region of the Dome.
Therefore, the idea of a compression-only dome due to self-weight was abandoned and the mini-
mum thickness of the elements turned out instead to be limited by considerations regarding placement
of ducts for post-tensioning and the height of the cover for reinforcements, as described in chapter
9. This led to a minimum required thickness of t=200 mm, which turned out to be suﬃcient for the
relevant analyses to give acceptable results, as discussed in the following.
The computational analysis of deﬂection showed that the Dome is in general a quite stiﬀstructure
and can be regarded as a shell. The maximum deﬂection in ABAQUS is of 0.7 mm whereas it is 1 mm
in STAAD.Pro (chapter 6). Compared to theory of solid shell domes, the deﬂection of the Dome in
ABAQUS, loaded by self-weight, is 2.4 times higher, and therefore has only about 2/5 of the stiﬀness
of a solid shell dome regarding deﬂection.
As further results of the analyses, section forces have been found for the beam model in STAAD.Pro,
also in chapter 6. The largest forces are located at the vertices and mainly found for the load combi-
nation of self-weight and symmetrically snow load. Axial tension will be present in the lower region
of the Dome with a magnitude of 25 kN, whereas the largest axial compression reaches 202 kN near
foundation level. Due to the presence of tension, prestress of the concrete is needed to obtain a com-
pression only dome. Largest in-plane moment is found to be 107 kNm with a corresponding shear of
101 kN. For the out-of-plane forces, the beam model with straight beams is considered not accurate,
as curved elements will act like arches and so the beam results must be revised. Taking assumptions
on the safe side, the maximum out-of-plane moment is found to be 19 kNm.
Calculations in ABAQUS (see chapter 6) turned out to be aﬀected by singularity problems at the
corners of the polygons, where stress concentrations were found. Maximum tensile stress of 2.1 MPa
and compressive stress of 2.7 MPa are, however, accepted as a guideline. The eigenvalue analysis in
ABAQUS was equally aﬀected by singularity problems, but a non-linear Riks-analysis turned out to
give reliable results with a buckling load of 744 times the symmetrical snow load. Meanwhile a design
factor of 0.05 was used for compensation for imperfections and stiﬀness change due to ﬂexure, giving
a critical design buckling load of 114kN/m2, which is still far above applied load magnitudes.
The element shape was discussed in chapter 7 and chosen to be of a Y-shape. This gives 78
elements in total for the Dome with three variations regarding lengths of the ”legs” and the angle
between them. This is the minimum number of both elements and variations possible with a repetitive
use of similarly shaped elements. Furthermore, with the choice of Y-shaped elements, the connection
between two elements is placed where section forces in general are the lowest. Also in chapter 7
the type of connection was discussed, concluding that post-tensioning can both be used for applying
prestress to the elements and at the same time provide a simple form of connection. The tendons can
be released during disassembly in order to obtain a demountable structure.
55
==PAGE:64==
Design of a Demountable Concrete Dome
To make the Dome demountable, the tendon ends must remain uncut, sticking approximately
300 mm out at the corners, at least in one of the ends in order to release the tendon by jacking, see
chapter 9.
Calculations in chapter 8 show that Ø15.7 mm tendon placed unbonded in a duct in the centre
of the cross section gives suﬃcient load bearing capacity, applying at least 169 kN of prestress to the
element. Water-tightness is assumed suﬃcient with a rubber strip along the element, as the opening
at the connection due to in-plane moment is found to be 0.0003 mm (chapter 8).
In order to ensure distribution of the prestressing force and to ensure some load bearing capacity
when not prestressed, normal reinforcement is required. Minimum requirements regarding reinforce-
ment combined with speciﬁc bursting reinforcement in the anchorage zone is suﬃcient (chapter 9).
Window frames are not assumed to contribute to the overall load bearing capacity, but must be
able to withstand local load from e.g. persons or snow. The frames have been designed in chapter
9 as integrated in the dome-element, i.e. a cast-in-one element. The thickness of the frames varies
from 50 mm at the bottom to 30 mm at the top and can withstand the load of several normal weight
persons standing on its edge.
Finally, calculations show that the foundation must be able to take a tensile ring force from the
Dome of 201 kN, which can be countered by two Ø15.7 mm tendons circumscribing the foundation
ring, shown in chapter 9. Jacking of both ends of the tendon is required. The foundation must also
transfer vertical load from the dome to the soil, for which the speciﬁc conditions is unknown. The
foundation ring is divided into 18 similar elements, corresponding to one element for each bottom
dome element.
For the production of the dome elements, an up-side down casting form has been proposed in
chapter 10, able to cast the frames and the main element part in one piece. For the erection of the
Dome also described in chapter 10, a successive approach is recommended with post-tensioning each
complete pair of two elements. This might give a partly self-supporting structure during erection,
and so avoiding extensive use of scaﬀolding.
11.2
Discussion on the Method
The Dome has been analysed through FEM and compared to the theory of solid shell domes. Using
two diﬀerent models for analysis makes results more reliable, as the special structure of the Dome is
not directly comparable to theory. STAAD.Pro has been very useful in understanding the behaviour
of the Dome under diﬀerent loads, boundary conditions and connection properties, even though
STAAD.Pro was not able to model curved members.
The shell model in ABAQUS did provide detailed information regarding stress and buckling.
However, singularity problems could have been avoided by adding a corner-ﬁllet. In order to get the
full performance of the ABAQUS model, it should have been provided with connection properties
and integrated post-tensioning and normal reinforcement steel, according to the ﬁnal design. In that
case, the material properties could have been set to reﬂect real concrete behaviour.
For the evaluation of the capacity of the elements, simple hand calculations used have been useful
in understanding the principles of load carrying capacity of post-tensioned members. More detailed
calculation methods exist, but the used expressions approximately reﬂects the accuracy of the FEM
models to which they are compared.
As stated in chapter 2, the analysis and design process has been iterative. With only one variable,
the thickness, the process have been quite easy to control.
It is, however, seen as inevitable to
continuously redo work when going further into detail with the design, as the ﬁnal thickness could
not have been foreseen in advance.
11.3
Discussion on Sustainability
The hypothesis of this project was to design a more sustainable concrete dome (see chapter 1). This
project does not contain speciﬁc numbers on cost, aesthetic value or environmental impact, because
these numbers are only interesting in relation to a similar structure in an alternative conﬁguration.
It should, however, be possible to evaluate these parameters with the right expertise (contractors,
architects and specialists into sustainability and environment). Nevertheless, the sustainability will
be discussed brieﬂy in the following, based on the ﬁndings and discussions made throughout this
report.

11
CONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION
Economically the Dome has an advantage regarding site work, compared to an in-situ cast dome.
Erection time is assumed to be short, with only few steps and a minimum use of scaﬀolding. An
in-situ dome of the same shape would most likely be thinner, saving concrete material, but extensive
shaﬀolding and window frame work would be diﬃcult for in-situ casting, too. A considerably cost of
the proposed design for the Dome goes to production and transportation. One must, however, keep
in mind that the Dome remains with a certain value after end of use at the given location. This
might be the determining factor for being economically, and environmentally more sustainable than
an equivalent in-situ dome.
Aesthetically the Dome is kept as neat as possible, with only the free tendon ends as visible
indications of a demountable dome. Compared to a solid dome which encloses the inner, the Dome
opens up with plenty of daylight. From the inside the expression is a massive and safe shelter, whereas
the outside seems more light and sculptural, due to the high window frames. It is believed that the
Dome is a more welcoming and atmospheric room due to the natural light and openness compared to
a solid dome. Together with savings on electrical lightning, it adds some sustainability to the Dome.
The discussion on whether concrete is a sustainable material lies beyond the scope of this project.
But the Dome is environmentally sustainable compared to a similar non-demountable structure if
the period of use at its initial location is short compared to expected lifetime of e.g. 50 years. Since
the reinforcements are not utilised for ULS capacity at all, it seems possible to get a lower cost per
covered square meter by building a larger dome. On the other hand, the proposed dimensions adds
robustness to the Dome for longer lifetime with several re-erections as a result.
11.4
Future Work
In order to realize the Dome of concrete elements as proposed in this project, some areas still remain
unsolved. This applies to e.g. the windows, their fastening to the frames and how they are attached
at ground level. Both the window frames and the foundation must be designed further into detail,
when necessary input is available. Carrying out the production and testing of two Y-elements post-
tensioned together might reveal unforeseen challenges which can cause changes in the detailed design.
The concept of the Dome can also be scaled up in size. According to architect Kristoﬀer Tejlgaard
a proper method could be to increase the frequency (as in ﬁgure 2 and 3) of the hexagonation of the
geodesic structure to scale up the total size but to keep the element size and entrance opening
unchanged. It would be an interesting task to investigate which dome size would be appropriate for
full utilisation of the element and its reinforcement with the thickness of 200 mm.
11.5
Putting the Dome into Perspective
The project concerns a very speciﬁc case, the Dome at Roskilde Festival. The solutions proposed are,
however, general for erection at other locations, too. In order to utilise the use of the Dome, it could
for example be moved to the town centre of Roskilde in the period between the festivals.
Besides domes, the concept can be used for other shell structures as well. Examples could be
oval-shaped sports halls or indoor swimming pools. Also cantilevered structures are possible with the
concept of post-tensioning.
A demountable system of concrete elements might also be a beneﬁcial solution for the changing
location of events like the Eurovision Song Contest, the World Cup, Olympic Games ect. where new
arenas are built for this speciﬁc event, but not often used afterwards. They could either be sold to
the next hosting country or moved or reshaped to ﬁt the future purpose.
Bringing the idea of demountable concrete structures a step further, it can be imagined that
companies expanding to new markets of higher risk could beneﬁt from building their oﬃces or factories
ready for disassembly and reassembly at another location, if the basis for business changes.
