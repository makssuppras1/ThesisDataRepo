ABSTRACT 
As the global economic growth moves at an ever-accelerating pace, the continued innovation, re-
search, and development of biotechnology have become a necessity in order to solve the world’s 
most urgent matters. The rise in the global population has increased the total food demand and the 
loss of crops on fields and during storage due to the ubiquitous existence of soil-borne plant patho-
gens is currently the major obstacle that needs to be resolved. To do so, traditional agricultural ap-
proaches, use of chemical pesticides, breed of resistant crops and development of genetically modi-
fied (GM) crops have either been employed or improved on to a great extent. However, due to vari-
ous complications associated with these solutions, alternative thinking has slowly made its way as 
biotechnology and synthetic biology advance and sustainability has become the focal point of the 
discussion. Given these circumstances, the knowledge that exists regarding the concept of plant 
growth-promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR), whole-cell biosensors and the antimicrobial compound 
2,4-diacetylphloroglucinol (DAPG) has shown great promise as a potential solution in previous 
studies over the recent years. However, the screening and detection of PGPR capable of utilizing 
their biocontrol capability to prevent the emergence and spread of diseases targeting crops and 
plants are less explored areas in the grand scheme of things. 
Throughout this project, promising results have been found regarding DAPG whole-cell biosensors 
that have been constructed, tested and utilized. Both the DAPG-(lacZ)- and DAPG-(luxCDABE) 
whole-cell biosensor have shown high sensitivity and high selectivity for DAPG when compared to 
the precursors monophloroglucinol (MAPG) and phloroglucinol (PG). The DAPG-(lacZ) whole-
cell biosensor could effectively be used to screen for DAPG producers in a spot-assay setup. How-
ever, the obtained data from the kinetic assay were ambiguous when the DAPG biosensor plasmid 
harboring the luxCDABE operon was transferred to DAPG producing Pseudomonas spp. and a mu-
tant strain. The influence of the medium composition on the position, pattern and size of the biolu-
minescence peak could not be determined and using the supernatant from DAPG producers to in-
duce the DAPG-(luxCDABE) whole-cell biosensor showed encouraging results. In conclusion, the 
optimization of both the DAPG whole-cell biosensors and experimental assays is deemed as a ne-
cessity going forward. To summarize, this Master’s Thesis presents a detailed overview of DAPG 
whole-cell biosensors, showcasing strengths and limitations in their ability to detect DAPG pro-
duced from Pseudomonads.

verdens mest presserende problemer. Stigningen i den globale verdensbefolkning har øget det sam-
lede fødevarebehov og tabet af afgrøder på marker og under opbevaring, forårsaget af den allesteds-
nærværende eksistens af jordbaseret plantepatogener, er i øjeblikket den største hindring der skal 
løses. For at gøre dette, så er traditionelle landbrugsmetoder, anvendelse af kemiske pesticider, avling 
af resistente afgrøder og udvikling af genmodificeret (GM) afgrøder enten blevet taget i brug eller 
forbedret i høj grad. Men på grund af adskillige komplikationer forbundet med disse løsninger, så har 
alternativ tænkning langsomt meldt sig på banen, i og med, at fremskridtet indenfor bioteknologi og 
syntesebiologi og bæredygtighed er blevet gjort til fokuspunktet i diskussionen. På baggrund af disse 
omstændigheder har den viden, der eksisterer omkring begrebet af ”plant growth-promoting rhi-
zobacteria” (PGPR), helcellebiosensorer og det antimikrobielle stof 2,4-diacetylphloroglucinol 
(DAPG) vist lovende udsigt som en potentiel løsning i tidligere studier de seneste par år. Med det 
sagt så er screeningen og detekteringen af PGPR som er i stand til at udnytte deres biokontrol evner 
til at forhindre fremkomsten og spredningen af sygdomme, der er målrettet mod planter og afgrøder, 
mindre udforskede områder med det store perspektiv taget i betragtning.

osensoren har vist høj sensitivitet og selektivitet mod DAPG sammenlignet med mellemprodukterne 
monophloroglucinol (MAPG) og phloroglucinol (PG). DAPG-(lacZ) helcellebiosensoren kunne ef-
fektivt anvendes til screening af DAPG producenter i et spot-assay setup. Med det sagt, så var den 
opnået data fra det kinetiske assay tvetydig når DAPG biosensor plasmidet med luxCDABE operonen 
blev overført til DAPG producerende Pseudomonas arter og en mutant stamme. Indflydelsen af me-
diekompositionen på placeringen, mønstret og størrelsen af bioluminescens spidsen kunne ikke fast-
lægges og anvendelsen af supernatanten fra DAPG producenterne til at inducere DAPG-(luxCDABE) 
helcellbiosensoren viste lovende resultater. Som konklusion betragtes optimeringen af både DAPG 
helcellebiosorerne og de eksperimentelle assays som en nødvendighed fremover. Afslutningsvis, så 
presentærer denne kandidat tese en detaljeret oversigt over styrker og begrænsninger af DAPG hel-
cellebiosensorers evne til at detektere DAPG produceret af Pseudomonader.

1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Food Demand and World Hunger – A Global Agricultural Challenge 
For thousands of years, humanity has been cultivating the land, sowing seeds, and harvesting the 
crops ever since the invention of agriculture. Owing to the steady growth in the global population, 
urbanization, and the influences of climate changes on the availability of landmasses, the demand for 
food is rapidly rising. Estimations predict that the global population will reach approximately 9 billion 
people by the middle of the current century. The contributing factor to this observed phenomenon can 
be pinpointed to the increase in wealth that correlates to higher purchase power and a higher con-
sumption rate of the current food depots (Muir et al. 2010). As a result of these underlying issues, 
there are three major challenges that need to be addressed (Braun 2007; Muir et al. 2010; UN 2015): 
 To meet the rapidly increasing food demand caused by the economic growth and rising popula-
tion. 
 To ensure zero hunger of the world’s poorest population despite the increasing gap between rich 
and poor (One of the United Nations’ 17 goals → #2). 
 Finally, both of these goals should be fulfilled using environmental and sustainable approaches 
(One of the United Nations’ 17 goals → #12). 
Encouraging efforts have been initiated to address these challenges by attempting to close the yield 
gap, which is defined as the difference between theoretical productivity and the most optimal produc-
tivity that can be achieved with current resources and technology. Additionally, increasing the pro-
duction limits to improve crop yield by assessing the efficiency of water-, fertilizer-, and nutrient-use 
and looking into modern genetic techniques to directly select for preferable traits like improved re-
sistance are attractive ideas within the scientific scope (Muir et al. 2010). Other approaches to increase 
yield and quality from current food manufacturing systems include the potential use of symbiotic 
microorganisms as co-fertilizer to reduce the use of inorganic fertilizers (Adesemoye, Torbert, and 
Kloepper 2009), and as inducers of phytostimulation1 to reduce the presence of inorganic and organic 
pollutants (Singh and Singh 2017). 
                                                          
1 Phytostimulation is characterized as a process where inorganic and organic compounds are broken down due to the effect of microbial activity 
caused by the inhabitants of the rhizosphere environment. Naturally, these inhabitants are recruited by the plants via the secretion of root exudates, 
providing habitat, energy, oxygen and other essential metabolites for them to thrive. This has also been developed into a plant-based bioremediation 
technique known as phytoremediation to remove heavy metals, pesticides and aromatic compounds from the environment (Anderson, Guthrie, and 
Walton 1993).

Despite promising initiatives, one of the main concerns in relation to these attempts to address the 
overall problem revolves around the harmful influence of soil-borne plant pathogens causing plant 
diseases during growth and post-harvest storage. The emergence of new plant diseases along with the 
rapid spread of existing ones pose a huge threat to the sustainable and attractive approaches in ques-
tion. Soil-borne plant pathogens belong to several different phyla that include fungi, bacteria, and 
nematodes (Haas and Défago 2005; Khater, Escosura-muñiz, and Merkoçi 2017), and the presence 
of these is a worldwide phenomenon (Park 2010; Pascal et al. 2019; Raaijmakers and Paulitz 2009).  
Notable examples include Fusarium spp. causing wilting in various crops (Alabouvette 1986), Strep-
tomyces spp. causing scab in potatoes (Kreuze et al. 2007), a complex of different fungi causing 
stunting and poor growth of apple trees (Mazzola 1998) and Gaeumannomyces graminis var. tritici 
causing take-all, a devastating root disease of wheat that has been observed in most part of the world 
(Kwak and Weller 2013; Weller et al. 2002). 
To give an example, rough calculations conducted by the University of Georgia, U.S, estimated the 
economic losses due to plant diseases to be approximately 821 million dollars in 2016, which was 
around 12.8% of the total crop value estimation (in Georgia) set at approximately 6,596 million dol-
lars (Little 2016). In other cases, losses were seen for various crops including wheat, cotton, soybean, 
maize, rice, and potatoes at respective numbers; ~50%, >80%, 26-29%, 31%, 37%, and 40% owing 
to a combination of abiotic and biotic factors as shown in Figure 1 (p. 2) (Oerke 2006).

1.1.1 Current Preventative Practices  
As a result of the ever-existing plague that contributes to huge losses of crops in fields as well as 
during growth, processing, and storage, the protection of crops and plants by using various methods 
have significantly improved the output of yield and quality of the product over the recent years. By 
applying means to control and monitor plant diseases, losses can be minimized to an acceptable level 
that does not conflict with the challenges that need to be met in the near future (Oerke 2006). In order 
to realize the impact of crop protection, estimations and predictions have been used to map out hypo-
thetical scenarios that compare the actual yield to the attainable yield and the overall effect of applied 
crop protection as illustrated in Figure 2 (p. 3). This includes several protective approaches such as 
traps, nets, scarecrows, traditional agricultural practices and the ideas regarding selective plant breed-
ing and genetic modification and more importantly, the use of chemical pesticides (Oerke 2006).  
 
Figure 2: The loss potential of crops is reduced while the actual yield is increased upon applying crop protection that 
includes traps, nets, scarecrows, traditional agricultural practices and the ideas regarding selective plant breeding and 
genetic modification. Meanwhile, higher yields are preserved due to the effects of chemical pesticide usage (Oerke 2006). 
Regardless of the application of preventative measures, plant diseases and general health concerns 
persist due to limitations of current knowledge and technology. To give examples of this, the crucial 
root disease of wheat, take-all, caused by potent soil-borne plant pathogens, is usually minimized by

utilizing traditional agricultural practices that consist of crop rotation2 and tillage3 (Raaijmakers and 
Weller 1998). However, crop rotation is often not economically feasible in the long run while tillage 
consequently leads to erosion of the healthy, fertile soil (Raaijmakers and Weller 1998). Breeding for 
beneficial traits such as disease-resistance in plants and using modern genetic engineering to supple-

The first scientific example of genetic engineering was the accomplishment of transferring foreign 
DNA from one microorganism to another, which eventually lead to the birth of the modern-day field 
of biotechnology (Cohen et al. 1973). Ever since then, institutional research departments and the ever-
growing biotechnology industry have moved beyond initial groundwork and taken upon more chal-
lenging tasks that involve solving some of the world’s most urgent matters. Genetically modified 
(GM) crops have been successfully used for animal feed but growing these types of crops for the sake 
of human consumption has only been seen for very few countries, one of them being the Republic of 
South Africa (Paarlberg 2014). The trend that has existed ever since the introduction of gene modifi-
cation in food products, is the effort of not-for-profit nongovernmental organizations in western coun-
tries running campaigns to advertise against the used of GM crops, thus contributing to the difficult 
circumstances of popularizing this technology (Paarlberg 2014).   
With the lack of better alternatives, agricultural companies, and their associated farmers currently 
rely on chemical control, in other words, pesticides to combat weeds, animal pests, and soil-borne 
plant pathogens. The growing concern regarding pesticide toxicity, when accumulated in trace 
amounts, has been well-documented and has been speculated to cause disruption in the male

1.1.2 An Attractive Solution  
To maintain the high yields and productivity of healthy soil, the potential of applied biology is vast 
and should, therefore, be the main priority going forward in the pursuit of answers leading to solu-
tions. During recent times, the idea of using plant growth-promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR) has gotten 
notable attention as a replacement for chemical pesticides. Based on the understanding of agroecol-
ogy6, the PGPR isolated from the rhizosphere have an inherent capability to enhance the growth of 
plants and fight off diseases that are commonly seen in the soil environment. The important genera 
of PGPR include Azospirillum, Azotobacter, Klebsiella, Enterobacter, Alcaligenes, Arthrobacter, 
Burkholderia, Bacillus, Serratia, and Pseudomonas, which are either commercialized at present mo-
ment (Haas and Défago 2005) or have the potential to be impactful biocontrol agents (Singh and 
Singh 2017).         
1.2 The Rhizosphere - A Society Beneath 
The rhizosphere is characterized as a complex, dynamic and heterogeneous soil environment, inhab-
ited by fungi, nematodes, protozoa, and bacteria that are surrounding plant roots. Due to the close 
proximity of these living biotic factors, the several-fold increased microbial activity in rhizosphere 
soil (Haas and Défago 2005) in contrast to bulk soil7, contributes to the modulation of the community 
as a whole (Broeckling et al. 2008; Hinsinger 2014). This can be facilitated by the interaction between

Examples of the former are direct interactions upon root colonization or indirect interactions via se-

beneficial for the plant’s wellbeing (Hinsinger 2014). Aside from root exudates, the type of host, type 
of soil and presence/absence of soil-borne plant pathogens also influence the microbial community 
and these parameters are especially important when assessing the rhizosphere competence9 of com-
mercial biocontrol agents (Schreiter et al. 2018).   
1.2.1 Types of Interactions 
The particular interactions between roots, soil, and microbial community that affect biological, bio-
chemical and physical properties of this particular microenvironment can be divided into three groups 
(Hinsinger 2014); 1) biological interactions, 2) physical interactions, and 3) chemical interactions. 
Physical interactions are based on the plant’s growth trajectory to deal with soil porosity and physical 
elements like stones and hardpans10 (Kolb, Legué, and Bogeat-Triboulot 2017). Chemical interactions 
involve parameters such as nutrient availability, oxygen supply, iron acquisition, pH and temperature 
(Kolb, Legué, and Bogeat-Triboulot 2017) with specific examples being the effect of nitrogen source 
and clay mineralogy on metabolite production by PGPR (Almario et al. 2013; Hultberg and Alsanius 
2008). More interestingly, the biological interactions encompass all the different types of relation-
ships (namely mutualism11, amensalism12, and competition13) found in the rhizosphere environment 
meaning that expansion of this knowledge within the field of soil ecology can be of grand value, as 
potential solutions can emerge for the challenges in the foreseeable future. To put emphasis on the

of these represent biocontrol Pseudomonads under natural conditions (Chatterton, Sutton, and Boland 
2004; Haas and Défago 2005), thus detecting and isolating these might be a challenge in itself.    
1.3 Pseudomonads as Biocontrol Agents 
Pseudomonas is a broad genus of Gammaproteobacteria consisting of many different members and 
is known for their abundance in many different environments and importance for medical and envi-
ronmental processes (Hesse et al. 2018) with the following characteristics; 1) gram-negative, 2) non-
spore-forming, 3) rod-shaped, 4) strictly aerobic, and 5) motile (Gilardi 2018). A recent phylogenetic 
framework of the Pseudomonas spp. has made it easier to identify and characterize phylogenetic 
relationships, elucidate novel gene functions and phenotypes that could be of usefulness (Hesse et al. 
2018).  
Due to their ability to adapt to different ecological niches that include the complex soil environment, 
certain Pseudomonas spp. are considered as being highly effective biological facilitators that can aid 
in biological control as a result of biosynthesis of diverse secondary metabolites (Gross and Loper 
2009). Specific examples can be found across the spacious field of scientific publications. In (Sun et 
==PAGE:18==

al. 2017), they isolated a P. putida A1 strain from the rhizosphere soil that was found to be capable 
of delaying and reducing the severity of wilt disease in tomato plants. In (Weststeijn 1990), they 
discovered a Pseudomonas isolate called E11.3 capable of functioning as a biocontrol agent against 
Pythium spp. causing root rot in tulips. Also, (Biosci, Rahanandeh, and Moshaiedy 2014) isolated a 
P. fluorescens strain with biocontrol attributes against root-knot nematodes. To sum up, by separating 
the favorable microorganisms from the undesirable, the ambiguity of being able to prevent disease 
and cause disease, certain members belonging to the Pseudomonas genus have been some of the most 
well-studied in the field of disease suppression in soil environments (Ramos and Filloux 2007).   
1.3.1 Pseudomonas fluorescens 
As a result of the undeniable innovation and accelerated development within the field of molecular 
techniques, the use of metagenomic analysis has been an important key for unlocking and providing 
an understanding of the complex and vast Pseudomonas community. Currently, there are more than 
180 named Pseudomonas spp. (Euzéby 1997). A revamp of existing phylogenetic knowledge was 
done by multilocus sequence analysis of individual type strains based on 16S rRNA and the house-
keeping sigma factor RpoD (Hesse et al. 2018). By working with a set of genomes of 166 type strains 
along with 189 other strains, the authors were able to expand and represent the diversity of Pseudo-
monas genus in a more precise manner compared to previous efforts (Hesse et al. 2018). This study 
gave important insight into a particular group called Pseudomonas fluorescens that is further divided 
into several subgroups as illustrated in Figure 4 (p. 10). The number of subgroups went from nine 
(Garrido-Sanz et al. 2016) to ten (Hesse et al. 2018). 
One obvious characteristic of the P. fluorescens group is its ability to light up when exposed to UV-
light. This is caused by a pigment called pyoverdine (Pvd) that is synthesized by non-ribosomal pep-
tide synthases along with other genes in a cluster (Hesse et al. 2018). Fluorescent Pseudomonads have 
been proven to be very effective biocontrol agents in various settings. In (Yang et al. 2011), biocon-
trolling P. fluorescens strains against the devastating root disease of wheat, take-all, were isolated 
from irrigated fields in provinces of China. Another case includes isolation of P. fluorescens strains 
from the rhizosphere soil of Lotus corniculatus to protect against phytopathogenic fungi like Pythium 
ultimum and Rhizoctonia solani (Bagnasco et al. 1998).

Although observations may suggest that the sole cause of disease suppression is due to the synthesis 
of Pvd, the actual main function of this metabolite is to acquire iron that is present in the rhizosphere 
environment given that it is also a siderophore besides being a pigment (J. M. Meyer and Abdallah

bacteria and fungi. Consequently, by the addition of iron, this disease suppression disappears 
(Kloepper et al. 1980). The proposed hypothesis is that these siderophores are not the main reason 
for PGPR biocontrol capabilities but partly contribute to the disease suppression by chelating the 
available iron to starve the soil-borne plant pathogens (Haas and Défago 2005; Kloepper et al. 1980). 
As previously mentioned, the array of secondary metabolites produced by Pseudomonads is impres-
sive in the context of biological control against soil-borne plant pathogens. One of these commercially 
relevant secondary metabolites with antimicrobial effect is a phloroglucinol called 2,4-diace-
tylphloroglucinol (DAPG). The biosynthesis of this compound is a highly conserved trait found 
among the P. fluorescens group (Almario et al. 2017; Moynihan et al. 2009). The distribution of 
DAPG biosynthetic genes based on PCR amplification of the DAPG biosynthetic gene cluster 
(phlACBD) on different Pseudomonas spp. revealed that P. kilonensis, P. thirvervalensis, and P. 
brassicacearum within the P. corrugate subgroup were able to produce DAPG. The phlACBD gene 
cluster was also found in the P. protegens subgroup (Almario et al. 2017). Though, it should be noted 
that neither Pvd nor DAPG production is limited to the P. fluorescens group (Almario et al. 2017; 
Hesse et al. 2018). 
1.4 The Importance Of 2,4-Diacetylphloroglucinol 
Phloroglucinols are phenolic secondary metabolites with broad-spectrum antimicrobial effects that 
are produced by bacteria in the rhizosphere environment (Marchand, Weller, and Bonsall 2000). Ear-
lier studies (Kwak and Weller 2013; Marchand, Weller, and Bonsall 2000; Raaijmakers and Paulitz 
2009; Raaijmakers and Weller 1998; Weller et al. 2002) have proven that one of the main determi-
nants of biological control of several plant diseases is due to the effect of a particular phloroglucinol 
called DAPG and the biosynthesis of this compound is a conserved trait among a fluorescent group 
of Pseudomonads called P. fluorescens (Almario et al. 2017; Moynihan et al. 2009). The structure of 
the compound can be found in Figure 5 (p. 12). Despite the fact that the exact mechanism of action, 
has not been elucidated yet, DAPG has been found to be an ISR eliciting compound and hence indi-

1.4.1 DAPG Biosynthesis 
DAPG is synthesized in a 3-step reaction by the proteins encoded by the phlACBD operon. The first 
step is a condensation reaction that is catalyzed by a type III polyketide synthase (encoded by phlD) 
of three 3-malonyl-CoA that have reacted together. This causes the structure to cyclize into the 
phloroglucinol (PG) precursor. Next, an enzyme complex (encoded by phlACB) that functions as an 
acetyltransferase is responsible for acetylating the PG precursor in the second step to make monoa-
cetylphloroglucinol (MAPG) and in the third step of the reaction to make DAPG as highlighted in 
Figure 6 (p. 13). Additional phl genes include a transporter (encoded by phlE), regulators (encoded 
by phlF and phlH), a hydrolase (encoded by phlG) that allows the reverse reaction and a protein 
(encoded by phlI) with unknown function (Almario et al. 2017; Vacheron, Bellvert, and Muller 2015).

acetyltransferase complex (encoded by phlACB) add on acetyl groups to make a monophloroglucinol (MAPG) and sub-

1.4.3 Gac/Rsm Pathway 
Besides the regulators in the phl gene cluster that are responsible for the post-translational- and tran-
scriptional regulation, the biosynthesis of DAPG and other relevant secondary metabolites are also 
under the control of post-transcriptional regulation by the conserved GacS/GacA two-component reg-
ulatory system which is found in many Gammaproteobacteria (Haas and Défago 2005; Heeb and 
Haas 2001; Workentine et al. 2009; Q. Yan et al. 2018). This particular system along with other two-
component regulatory systems are used by the microorganisms to adapt to environmental conditions 
upon stimulation by external stimuli (Heeb and Haas 2001).  
==PAGE:24==
Side 15 af 107 
 
Two-component regulatory systems consist of a sensor kinase and a response regulator. For the 
GacS/GacA two-component system, the sensor kinase is GacS while the response regulator is GacA. 
Thus, upon stimulation by a signal in the periplasmic space, the GacS sensor kinase autophosphory-
lates and transfers the phosphate group to GacA by a 3-step phosphor-relay mechanism (Haas and 
Défago 2005; Workentine et al. 2009). Once GacA has been phosphorylated, it activates the expres-
sion of three small RNAs (RsmX, RsmY, and RsmZ) that can alleviate the post-transcriptional re-
pression of target genes by binding to two repressor proteins (RsmA and RsmE), which interfere with 
the ribosomal binding site (RBS) of mRNA (Haas and Défago 2005; Workentine et al. 2009). Alto-
gether, this regulatory system is also called the Gac/Rsm pathway. A simplified illustration of this 
can be found in Appendix A (p. 81).  
In addition to this discovery, GacS/GacA two-component system knock-out mutants have been ob-
served to have reduced or complete loss of biocontrol capabilities in well-known biocontrol P. fluo-
rescens strains (Bull et al. 2001; Corbell and Loper 1995; Duffy and Defago 2000; Gaffney et al. 
1994; Laville et al. 1992; Pfender, Kraus, and Loper 1993; Sacherer, Défago, and Haas 1994). Despite 
the extensive knowledge of this global regulatory mechanism, the stimulus/stimuli that activate(s) the 
autophosphorylation of GacS and the mechanism by which GacA activates the transcription of the 
small regulatory RNAs still remain unknown (Haas and Défago 2005).  
1.4.4 Pseudomonas Sigma Regulator A 
So far, both post-translational-, transcriptional- (PhlH and PhlF) and post-transcriptional regulation 
(Gac/Rsm pathway) have been discussed but the regulation of DAPG biosynthesis is plentiful and 
includes several regulatory systems in addition to the ones already mentioned which have been dis-
covered in model strains such as P. fluorescens 2P24 and P. fluorescens CHA0. Upstream (approx. 
120 bp) of the pho site (binding site for PhlF) in the promoter region of phlACBD, another binding 
site is located for a self-regulating regulator called Pseudomonas sigma regulator A (PsrA). When 
PsrA binds to this site, the expression of phlACBD is transcriptionally repressed (Wu et al. 2012). 
Additionally, by constructing knock-out mutants, (Wu et al. 2012) also discovered that PsrA posi-
tively regulates RpoS (σ38,  a stress/stationary sigma factor), which further leads to positive regulation 
of RsmA (the repressor protein in the Gac/Rsm pathway). Thus, by increasing the amounts of RsmA, 
the overall production of DAPG is negatively controlled at a post-transcriptional level. A simplified 
illustration of this can be found in Appendix A (p. 82). It must be noted that this specific regulatory 
mechanism is independent of the Gac/Rsm pathway because it was shown that a GacS/GacA knock-
out mutant had no effect on the expression of PsrA. For this particular study, the 2P24 strain was 
==PAGE:25==
Side 16 af 107 
 
used, but in another study using the CHA0 strain (Heeb et al. 2005), evidence concluded that RpoS 
did not influence the concentration of RsmA, so all things considered, these observations may suggest 
that these regulatory systems are strain-dependent.     
1.4.5 Sigma Factors 
Besides RpoS, two other sigma factors, RpoD (σ70) and RpoN (σ54) are equally as important for the 
biocontrol capability. In a mutant with an in-frame-deletion of the rpoN gene, an increase of DAPG 
and PhlA was observed coupled with a decrease of pyoluteorin (PLT) suggesting that RpoN is re-
sponsible for controlling the balance of antimicrobial compound production (Péchy-Tarr et al. 2005). 
While the authors could not determine a consensus binding motif near the promoter region of phlA, 
they found a potential one near the promoter region of pltR, which encodes for a transcriptional acti-
vator for the pltLABCDEFG gene cluster responsible for PLT biosynthesis. This is connected to the 
fact that the production of PLT has shown to interfere with the production of DAPG in order to main-
tain a certain balance (Schnider-Keel et al. 2000). A simplified illustration of the regulatory mecha-
nism can be found in Appendix A (p. 83).   
Additionally, by overexpressing the housekeeping sigma factor RpoD, enhanced production of 
DAPG and PLT was observed (Schnider et al. 1995). RpoD is a global transcription factor that is 
involved in the expression of many essential genes. The ratio of RpoD and RpoS in the stationary 
phase is relevant for the biosynthesis of antibiotics given that RpoS negatively controls the production 
of DAPG by regulating RsmA positively (Wu et al. 2012). A simplified illustration of this regulatory 
mechanism can be found in Appendix A (p. 84). Altogether, the unique interplay between these three 
sigma factors contributes to the complexity of DAPG biosynthesis regulation.

1.5 Other Essential Metabolites Found in The Rhizosphere Environment 
Auxins are specific metabolites that can be found in the rhizosphere environment and are known to 
be important for plant growth and development (Dierckx et al. 2015). These were first described by 
France and Charles Darwin as mobile growth regulators in their book called, The Power of Movement 
in Plants, published in 1880 (Leyser 2010). However, in the 1980s, auxins were deeply investigated 
with improved molecular techniques, and discoveries came to the conclusion that auxins act as sig-
nals/hormones to regulate transcription of genes that are associated with the developmental stages in 
the plant life cycle (Woodward and Bartel 2005). This involves processes such as embryogenesis17, 
vascular differentiation18, organogenesis19, tropic growth20 and root/shoot architecture (Quint and 
Gray 2006). As for the promoter regions that lie upstream of the auxin-responsive genes, one common 
element to be found in these is the presence of one or more auxin-responsive elements (AuxREs) 
(Ulmasov et al. 1995). Similar to phloroglucinols, the array of different molecules within the auxin 
group is vast but comparable to DAPG and one of the more important and commonly observed auxins 
found in the rhizosphere environment is called indole-3-acetic acid (IAA). The structure of the com-
pound can be found in Figure 9 (p. 19).  
The biosynthesis of IAA is facilitated by both tryptophan(Trp)-dependent and Trp-independent path-
ways. These include the indole-3-pyruvic acid (IPA) pathway, the indole-3-acetamide (IAM) path-
way, the indole-3-acetaldoxime (IAOx) pathway and the tryptamine (TAM) pathway (Woodward and 
Bartel 2005). Although IAA is one of the most thoroughly studied auxins, certain enzymes that cata-
lyze the reactions in the Trp-dependent pathways have not yet been identified. The Trp-independent 
pathways are less studied but two scenarios that have been observed are the conversion of indole-3-
acetonitrile (IAN) to IAA under alkaline conditions and the conversion of an early Trp-precursor 
called indole-3-glycerol to indole and subsequently to IAA (Woodward and Bartel 2005). A list of 
the pathways can be found in Appendix C (p. 86).

1.5.1 Quorum-Sensing Regulatory System 
The first example of a quorum-sensing (QS) regulatory system was discovered in the bacterium Vib-
rio fischeri. Transcriptional regulators involved in regulating the expression of lux genes (genes re-
sponsible for bioluminescence) are controlled by the unique concept that is cell-density based regu-
lation (Fuqua, Winnans, and Greenberg 1994). To describe it in a simplistic manner, the main com-
ponents of this process is the interaction between LuxI and LuxR. The LuxI protein is capable of 
producing diffusible metabolites that move in and out via the cellular membrane. These metabolites 
can be recognized by the LuxR protein and upon binding, conformational changes of the LuxR protein 
occur to change its affinity to specific target sequences (lux-boxes) at the promoter region in order to 
drive gene expression of target genes (Karlovsky 2008).   
For a large majority of ecological, physiological and community-based bacterial processes, the idea 
of QS is often involved as the main concept with diffusible molecules being the driving catalyst for 
initiation of said processes. It has been quite a common and apparent perception that bacteria are 
capable of interacting and communicating through intercellular communication systems like QS 
throughout the years (Andersen et al. 2001).  
To dive further into the specific metabolites in question, one of the most thoroughly studied diffusible 
metabolites involved in QS systems are N-acyl-L-homoserine lactone (AHL) signaling molecules 
(Williams 2007). The backbone of the compound can be found in Figure 10 (p. 20). Once a threshold

of AHLs has been reached, activation and expression of certain target genes can be achieved making 
this an intelligent cell-density based regulatory gene expression system (Andersen et al. 2001). Some-
times, a sensor kinase and a response regulator is involved in the upstream processing when the sig-
naling molecules are peptides instead (Williams 2007). Many AHLs are produced by gram-negative 
bacteria including some from the Pseudomonas genus (Huang et al. 2013) such as P. aeruginosa, P. 
putida, P. syringae and one that is a subgroup of P. fluorescens called P. chlororaphis (Karlovsky 
2008).  
In order to compare with Vibrio fischeri, it is important to look at the homologs of the LuxI/LuxR 
protein family such as those found in P. chlororaphis named PhzI/PhzR (Pierson, Keppenne, and 
Wood 1994). This particular PGPR is capable of producing the AHL called N-hexanoyl-L-homoser-

complex and bind to lux-box-like sequences at the promoter region upstream of the phzXYFABCD 
operon to regulate the expression of phenazine production in order to combat Gaeumannomyces 
graminis var. tritici, the causative agent of the wheat disease take-all (Wood et al. 1997). Another 
intriguing study discovered the homologs PcoI/PcoR in a P. fluorescens strain called 2P24 as illus-
trated in Figure 11 (p. 21). This species is a known DAPG producer and while the deletion of the 
pcoI gene in the strain did not affect the production of antimicrobial compounds, it did abolish the 
production of AHL. The result of this was decreased biofilm formation, decreased colonization on 
wheat roots and an overall reduction of biocontrol capability (Wei and Zhang 2006), which correlates 
well with the observation that the density of PGPR needs to reach a certain root colonization threshold 
in order to exert their biocontrol ability (Raaijmakers and Weller 1998).

1.6 The Principle of Whole-Cell Biosensors 
DAPG producers in natural disease suppressive soil have been thoroughly investigated in the past as 
highlighted in the previous sections. The most notable example is the showcasing of the effectiveness 
of DAPG against the devastating wheat disease, take-all (Raaijmakers and Weller 1998). On top of 
the accumulated knowledge regarding these potential biocontrol agents, the current trend is slowly 
shifting, going from discovery to detection of secondary metabolites and the producers of these. Cur-
rently, the common approaches that are being used to analyze and detect the compounds in the com-
plex rhizosphere environment include chemical analytical assays such as GC-MS and HPLC 
(Atkinson and Haggett 1993; Liu et al. 2010). Obtaining information in such ways is not only time-
consuming, complicated (due to the requirement of specialized equipment) and expensive but the 
outcome of such assays tend to overestimate the total concentration of metabolites without consider-
ing that only a fraction of this total concentration is significantly affecting nearby surroundings (Liu 
et al. 2010).  
B 
A 
==PAGE:31==

In order to streamline the working process, the idea of biosensors has undergone more than 20 years 
of development (Atkinson and Haggett 1993; Gui et al. 2017). In a general sense, the different mod-
ules that make up the word biosensor are the recognition element that can bind to the analyte of 
interest, the transducer that can convert the biological reaction upon detection to a signal, the amplifier 
that can increase the signal and the detector that can take in the signal and generate a detectable 
readout (Wilson 2014). While this setup is very common for electrochemical biosensors, other vari-
ations exist which mainly focus on the recognition element and the transducer. One of these variations 
is the molecular-based biosensors that utilize biological elements such as enzymes, DNA, anti-
gens/antibodies as the recognition element (Gui et al. 2017). This type of biosensor can be highly 
sensitive due to the precise and specific molecular interactions that have been shaped by evolution. 
However, the cost of isolating the biological elements and the short lifespan of these biosensors make 
them limited in various settings such as environmental monitoring and clinical diagnostics (Gui et al. 
2017). Another type of biosensors is called whole-cell biosensors, which, in contrast to molecular-
based biosensors are less sensitive. Nonetheless, these can easily be modified through genetic engi-
neering by simple molecular techniques to detect a wide range of different metabolites. Furthermore, 
these can potentially provide additional information about cell physiology and toxicology that mo-
lecular-based biosensors are incapable of (Gui et al. 2017).  
In comparison to traditional chemical assays, the cost, simplicity of usage and rapid responses of 
whole-cell biosensors make them highly efficient tools for analyzing complex environmental sam-
ples. Moreover, whole-cell biosensors provide unique advantages not found elsewhere. Whereas tra-
ditional chemical assays effectively generate analytical information21, whole-cell biosensors have the 
capability of providing functional information22 in addition to analytical information (Bousse 1996). 
Another characteristic that can be assessed using whole-cell biosensors is the bioavailability23. This 
is important in different contexts where the total concentration of a metabolite or pollutant is often 
presented, which leads to wrongly interpreted results and a false impression of the actualities in the 
sample of interest (Liu et al. 2010).  
                                                          
21 Analytical information is information that tells whether something is present or not (qualitative) and if present, it also reveals how much of it is 
present (quantitative) (Bousse 1996). 
22 Functional information is information that reveals the effect of stimuli on a living system (Bousse 1996). 
23 Bioavailability is described as the fraction (extent and rate) at which a substance is impacting (absorbed into) a living system or is made available 
to the living organisms in the environment (Parker 2003).

To further describe whole-cell biosensors in the context of analyzing environmental samples, the 
detection system is usually localized on a plasmid construct and subsequently transformed into a cell 
that fits well with the environmental setting. There are several important considerations to be made 
in the design process of such whole-cell biosensor plasmid; 1) the choice of recognition element 
(analyte-specific regulatory protein that is coupled to an output promoter), which depends on the 
compound of interest (A. J. Meyer et al. 2019), 2) the choice of reporter gene (lacZ, luxCDABE, gfp, 
etc. each with its own advantages and disadvantages), which depends on the environmental setting 
and choice of experiment (Gui et al. 2017), and 3) how to achieve appropriate response, which is high 
sensitivity and high selectivity (A. J. Meyer et al. 2019). The overall concept of a whole-cell biosensor 
can be found in Figure 12 (p. 23).

Despite the promising outlook, whole-cell biosensors are not perfect by any means. Constructing and 
presenting these biologically relevant detectors to the public is considered a highly competitive field 
and success is rarely guaranteed. Leaky and insufficient output response are common occurrences

along with poor detection limits and low selectivity. Also, using them for in situ purposes could pos-
sibly clash with GMO legislations and regulations (Wan, Ho, and Wang 2019). With that being said, 
in the context of DAPG, whole-cell biosensors can be utilized for the detection of this compound 
using a simple design based on genetic elements from the phl gene cluster. 
1.6.1 Elements of A DAPG Whole-Cell Biosensor and Detection of Other Essential Metabolites 
The fundamentals for the construction of a DAPG biosensor plasmid is based on the work by (A. J. 
Meyer et al. 2019). By following a logical workflow of iterative cycles of negative selection to re-
move leaky biosensors along with positive selection to select for highly responsive biosensors with 
minimal cross-activity and mutational optimization, the blueprint for the genetic circuit consists of a 
constitutive promoter upstream of the mutated phlF gene (called phlFAM) encoding for the regulatory 
PhlF repressor protein and an output promoter which PhlF can bind onto. This genetic circuit can 
then be modified downstream of the output promoter by inserting a reporter gene of own choice as 
illustrated in Figure 13 (p. 24) (A. J. Meyer et al. 2019). The genetic circuit can potentially be used 
in many different ways and it can be made into an exceptional tool for high-throughput screening of 
DAPG producers from the rhizosphere soil.  
 
Figure 13: The design of genetic circuit to detect DAPG as illustrated by (A. J. Meyer et al. 2019). The gene encoding 
for the regulatory PhlF protein is found downstream of the constitutive promoter. To optimize the design of the genetic 
circuit, both synonymous (grey/black lines) and nonsynonymous (red lines) mutations were done on the constitutive pro-
moter and the RBS to influence the expression of the phlF gene, and on the phlF gene itself to influence the affinity and 
specificity of the regulatory repressor protein (called phlFAM) to the output promoter. An interchangeable site for the 
reporter gene of own choice can be found downstream of the output promoter.  
Due to the flexibility and availability of genetic manipulation techniques, constructing a wide range 
of different whole-cell biosensors is possible as showcased by (A. J. Meyer et al. 2019). In a similar 
manner, by looking beyond DAPG, whole-cell biosensors detecting the other essential metabolites 
previously mentioned, auxins and AHLs, could be constructed. For the detection of auxins, (Dierckx 
==PAGE:34==

et al. 2015) managed to construct two different whole-cell biosensors based on their own methodol-
ogy. These whole-cell biosensors could detect 2-phenylacetic acid and 4-hydroxyphenylacetic acid 
respectively, both, which can be found in the rhizosphere soil. This was done by creating two different 
genetic sensor circuits with AuxREs capable of detecting the presence of the specific auxins. Addi-
tionally, the authors demonstrated that the two different output systems in use, expression of GFP and 
pyocyanin, revealed differences in the sensitivity and selectivity (Dierckx et al. 2015). For the detec-
tion of AHLs, more specifically, N-3-oxohexanoyl -L-homoserine, (Andersen et al. 2001) managed 
to construct a GFP-based AHL-biosensor by implementing a luxR-Plux-box (from Vibrio fischeri) mod-
ule upstream of the reporter gene. 
In conclusion, the considerable amount of attention that whole-cell biosensors have received in recent 
years has allowed scientists to achieve insightful information from complex soil environments in a 
convenient way compared to earlier. The availability of these vastly diversified whole-cell biosensors 
has been marked as a huge accomplishment due to years of development within synthetic biology 
coupled with the expansion of knowledge in the field of genetic engineering. Altogether, this has 
allowed scientists and researchers alike to broaden their comprehension about the complexity of life 
and all of its interactions in various environmental settings as each day goes by.  
2 AIM OF THE STUDY   
Due to the global agricultural challenge and the undiscovered potential of using whole-cell biosensors 
for the detection of PGPR capable of producing antimicrobial compounds, the aim of this study is to 
evaluate a fully constructed DAPG whole-cell biosensor that can detect fluorescent DAPG producing 
Pseudomonads. The evaluation includes bioassays that determine both the sensitivity and selectivity 
of the biosensor in multiple ways. Fluorescent Pseudomonads from soil samples will be isolated and 
identified and the application of the DAPG whole-cell biosensor on these isolates will reveal the 
presence/absence of DAPG producing Pseudomonads in a simple screening assay. Furthermore, in 
order to gain an understanding of influential parameters that affect DAPG production, the output 
system will be switched out, from a lacZ gene to a luxCDABE operon, and the new DAPG biosensor 
plasmid will be transferred into known DAPG producing Pseudomonads and a mutant strain. Simple 
kinetic assays will be conducted to get an idea of how the DAPG that is being produced by the strains 
themselves interacts with the DAPG biosensor plasmid. Finally, additional assays may be included 
based on the obtained results in the initial work packages.

3.3 Evaluating the Sensitivity and Selectivity of the DAPG-(lacZ) Whole-Cell Biosensor  
The experimental setup is based on a β-galactosidase assay (Miller 1972) to determine the sensitivity 
and selectivity of the DAPG whole-cell biosensor harboring the lacZ reporter gene (referred to 
DAPG-(lacZ) from this point onward). As presented in Section 3.2, the lacZ gene is located down-
stream of the PhlF-specific promoter (PPhlF) that is under the tight control of the PhlF repressor. In 
the presence of DAPG, the PhlF repressor binds to DAPG, relieving the repression, thus enabling 
transcription of the lacZ gene. 
The lacZ gene encodes for the enzyme β-galactosidase that catalyzes the hydrolysis of β-galactosides 
disaccharides such as lactose and the galactoside analog called o-nitrophenyl-β-D-galactopyranoside 
(ONPG) into monosaccharides. There are two main reasons for using lacZ as a reporter gene; 1) β-
==PAGE:38==
Side 29 af 107 
 
galactosidase is very stable and resistant to digestion and 2) β-galactosidase can be used to screen 
DAPG producers on agar plates supplemented with X-gal. 
Cleavage of the colorless ONPG yields galactose and the chromophore o-nitrophenol that gives a 
bright yellow solution. The absorbance can be measured at 420 nm and the β-galactosidase activity 
can be determined by calculating the Miller Units (MUs). 
3.3.1 Materials and Methods 
The DAPG-(lacZ) whole-cell biosensor was grown O/N in selective liquid LB medium in triplicates 
at 37⁰C on shake while the stable components of the permeabilization buffer and substrate solution 
were partly prepared by mixing. See Appendix E (pp. 88-89) for a recipe of permeabilization buffer 
and substrate solution. On the day of the experiment, 100 µl of either DAPG, MAPG or PG in the 
concentrations of 0 µM, 0.625 µM, 1.25 µM, 2.5 µM, 5 µM, 7.5 µM, 10 µM, and 15 µM was added 
in each row ordered from A-H in a transparent 96-microtiter plate. Next, the biosensor was diluted 
using the Ultrospec 10 spectrophotometer (Amersham Bioscience) to an initial OD600 of 0.02 and 100 
µl of OD normalized culture from each replicate was transferred to 3 columns yielding a total of 3 
technical replicates per biological replicate. In a single column, the liquid LB medium was added as 
a blank sample. The 96-microtiter plate was grown at 37⁰C on shake for 3 hours and during this 
period, the unstable components of the permeabilization buffer and substrate solution were added to 
the mixtures. Once the 3 hours had passed, the OD600 values were measured in the Cytation5 plate 
reader (BioTek) using the Gen5 2.07 software and 20 µl of the culture grown in different concentra-
tions of either DAPG, MAPG or PG was transferred to a new transparent 96-microtiter plate. This 
was followed by the addition of 80 µl of the permeabilization buffer for a total of 100 µl in each well. 
For the blank, 20 µl of liquid LB medium was added to 3 wells instead of the grown culture. The 96-
microtiter plate was incubated at 30⁰C on shake for 30 minutes along with the substrate solution. 
Once finished incubating, 28 µl from each well was transferred to a black 96-microtiter plate followed 
by the addition of 172 µl substrate solution for a total of 200 µl in each well. The black 96-microtiter 
plate was sealed with a semipermeable membrane and initial values of OD600 and OD420 were meas-
ured as well as OD420/time at 37⁰C for the duration of 16 hours on shake in the Cytation5 plate reader 
using the Gen5 2.07 software and the data were subsequently collected. An illustration of the work-
flow can be found in Appendix G (p. 92). The assay was conducted 3 times, once for each of the 
compounds in question (DAPG, MAPG, and PG).

3.7 Constructing a luxCDABE Variant of the DAPG Whole-Cell Biosensor 
While the lacZ reporter gene is a widely used output system, replacing it with a luxCDABE operon to 
construct a luxCDABE variant (referred to DAPG-(luxCDABE) from this point onward) via PCR-
==PAGE:41==
Side 32 af 107 
 
based cloning enables the possibility to assess the influences on DAPG production in a convenient 
way. In short, luxAB encodes for the heterodimeric bacterial luciferase that is involved in the synthesis 
of fatty acids via oxidation of aliphatic aldehydes by using reduced flavin mononucleotide (FMNH2) 
as the co-factor (Brodl, Winkler, and Macheroux 2018). This reaction results in the emission of bio-
luminescence, which can be detected at a wavelength at approx. 490 nm (Brodl, Winkler, and 
Macheroux 2018; Welham and Stekel 2009). Since both FMNH2 and aldehydes are necessary for the 
reaction to occur, the resulting FMN is reduced in a reaction with H2O catalyzed by a flavin oxidore-
ductase. Similarly, luxDEC (an acyltransferase, a synthetase, and a reductase respectively) encodes 
for a fatty acid reductase complex capable of reducing/converting the fatty acids back to being alde-
hydes, thus establishing a unique self-sustaining cycle (Brodl, Winkler, and Macheroux 2018; 
Welham and Stekel 2009). 
3.7.1 Materials and Methods 
First, E. coli FH2927 cells were grown in tubes with liquid LB medium O/N at 37⁰C on shake for the 
preparation of chemically competent cells. E. coli K12/MG1655 containing the pUC18 plasmid car-
rying the luxCDABE operon were grown in selective liquid LB medium O/N at 37⁰C on shake along 
with the DAPG-(lacZ) whole-cell biosensor. Plasmids (pUC18 and pSEVA225::DAPG-lacZ) were

4.5 Successful Construction of the DAPG-(luxCDABE) Whole-Cell Biosensor

5 Discussion 
5.1 Interpretation of High Sensitivity and High Selectivity of Both DAPG Biosensor Plas-
mids 
Two important parameters that biosensor plasmids are evaluated on are the sensitivity and the selec-
tivity. High selectivity is desired so that the biosensor only reacts to the compound of interest while 
a high sensitivity allows the detection of the compound of interest at very low levels. For both the 
DAPG-(lacZ)- and DAPG-(luxCDABE) whole-cell biosensor this seems to be the case. The selectiv-
ity is high when comparing DAPG to its precursors MAPG and PG. Based on the statistics that have 
been applied to the results, the dynamic output range of the DAPG-(lacZ) whole-cell biosensor could 
be estimated to be from 0.625 µM to 5 µM while the range of the DAPG-(luxCDABE) whole-cell 
biosensor could be estimated to be from 0.078 µM to 10 µM. With that being said, the lower detection 
limit of the DAPG-(lacZ) whole-cell biosensor might not represent the true value and an additional 
β-galactosidase assay would be required to test the concentrations between 0 µM and 0.625 µM in a 
similar manner to how it was done for the DAPG-(luxCDABE) whole-cell biosensor. Moreover, the 
higher detection limit might not be precise either, which is due to the use of a substrate solution in 
the assay. Here, a fixed concentration of ONPG is used, which eventually leads to the depletion of 
substrate.  
5.1.1 Comparing the Dynamic Output Range to DAPG Concentrations in Other Studies 
Despite the estimations of the dynamic output ranges, comparing these values to physiological DAPG 
levels that are found in the rhizosphere- and bulk soil is not a straightforward matter. This is mostly 
due to the fact that the DAPG concentrations that are detected from soil samples are presented in the 
units of weight/weight instead of mass or molar concentrations. For instance, (Bergsma-Vlami, Prins, 
and Raaijmakers 2005) reported DAPG concentrations between 0 and 150 ng per gram of fresh root 
depending on the type of rhizosphere environments (variation in the plant host and soil). Meanwhile, 
20 ng DAPG per gram of fresh root was reported by (Raaijmakers and Weller 1998). It is difficult to 
convert concentration in this format into something meaningful. Another way to compare is to look 
at in vitro studies that use DAPG concentrations that are capable of inhibiting soil-borne plant path-
ogens. It is logical to assume that these concentrations are somewhat valid based on the vast amount 
of studies contributing to the scientifical consensus of disease suppression. In one example, (De Souza 
et al. 2003) showed that DAPG concentration ranging from 32 to 72 µg/ml was enough to completely 
inhibit mycelial growth of Pythium spp. By converting this range to µM, the values become approx. 
==PAGE:66==
Side 57 af 107 
 
152 to 342 µM. Also, other components of the Pythium spp, such as the zoosporangia, the zoospores, 
and zoospore cysts were adversely affected in a concentration range between 0.8 ng/ml to 6.4 µg/ml, 
which corresponds to approx. 0.0038 µM to 30 µM. In (Gong et al. 2016), they used chemically 
synthesized DAPG analogs at a concentration of 25 µg/ml against Penicillium spp. which corresponds 
to a molar concentration of approx. 119 µM and in (S. L. F. Meyer et al. 2009), the nematode, X. 
americanum, had a LD50 of 8.3 µg/ml DAPG corresponding to approx. 39 µM. Values between 150-
300 µM seem to be abnormally high when comparing to the findings in (Brazelton et al. 2008) where 
DAPG concentrationa of >50 µM were found to inhibit primary growth of plant roots and it is difficult 
to conclude if these are the actual bioavailable amounts that are found in nature or some random 
numbers that were capable of inhibition. Nonetheless, the DAPG concentrations in natural environ-
ments seem to vary quite a lot and using the DAPG whole-cell biosensors to quantify the amount of 
DAPG being produced might not be that simple compared to using them as a tool to qualify and detect 
the presence of DAPG production.   
5.2 Leakiness of the DAPG Whole-Cell Biosensors 
In Figure 16 (p. 40), by looking at the images where the DAPG-(lacZ) whole-cell biosensor has been 
spread on top of LB plates supplemented with different concentrations of DAPG and X-gal, a faint 
blue color can be seen within the colonies that had grown on plates at a DAPG concentration of 0 
µM. These images were taken after approx. 24 hours of growth and by the time of approx. 48 hours 
of growth, the intensity of blue color had increased (no image available). Thus, it is highly plausible 
that the repression of the lacZ reporter gene is weaker than initially thought and this is critical for the 
credibility of the DAPG-(lacZ) whole-cell biosensor if it was to be employed to solve urgent problems 
in real-life scenarios. Similar goes for the DAPG-(luxCDABE) whole-cell biosensor as the genetic 
circuit is identical bar the reporter gene. The novelty of whole-cell biosensors has prompted advance-
ment within synthetic biology and as a result of this, there are ways to optimize the whole-cell bio-
sensors in a general sense as explained by (Wan, Ho, and Wang 2019).  
5.2.1 Optimization of the Genetic Gene Circuit to Reduce Leakiness 
In the case of leaky expression observed in the DAPG-(lacZ) whole-cell biosensor, two approaches 
can be used to solve this issue which include; 1) random mutagenesis of the phlF gene, the RBSs or 
the promoters. Random mutagenesis on the phlF gene had already been done by (A. J. Meyer et al. 
2019) as denoted by the AM initials in phlFAM and the same goes for the PLacIQ promoter and phl2 
RBS. However, the PPhlF promoter and B0062 RBS in the genetic circuit had no changes whatsoever 
==PAGE:67==
Side 58 af 107 
 
and 2) the addition of more phlO operator sequences in the PPhlF promoter. This allows enhanced 
repression due to the binding of several PhlF repressor proteins (Wan, Ho, and Wang 2019).  
Nonetheless, no advantage comes without disadvantage and the tradeoff of these attempts to reduce 
leakiness usually comes in the form of an increase of the limit of detection (LOD) and a decrease of 
the dynamic output range to compromise for the enhanced repression. A way to circumvent the 
tradeoff involving a low dynamic output range would be to implement a transcriptional amplifier in 
the genetic circuit (Wan, Ho, and Wang 2019). The phlF gene would remain downstream of the PLacIQ 
promoter, however, the PPhlF promoter, with an increased number of phlO sequences, would be lo-
cated upstream of a transcription factor instead of the reporter gene. The transcription factor would 
then be able to bind to a PTF promoter, which would be located upstream of the reporter gene allowing 
signal amplification. To account for the increase of the LOD, when dealing with repressor proteins, 
additional random mutagenesis on the PhlF repressor protein would be one way to deal with the issue 
in hopes of finding a mutant variant showcasing higher affinity to DAPG so that a lower concentration 
of DAPG is needed for expression of the reporter gene. Also, reducing the concentration of PhlF 
repressor protein by implementing a weaker PLacIQ promoter would result in a lower LOD, but this 
would simultaneously enhance the leakiness which is not optimal, however, if several phlO operator 
sequences were to be present in the PPhlF promoter region, an appropriate equilibrium could be 
achieved giving rise to the perfect balance between the LOD and leakiness (Wan, Ho, and Wang 
2019). An illustration of the optimization process can be found in Appendix J (p. 107). All in all, the 
optimization of whole-cell biosensors is a necessity for these to work as intended if they were to be 
utilized beyond lab-scale work, and the approaches to do so are plentiful. 
5.3 Applying the DAPG-(lacZ) Whole-Cell Biosensor for High Throughput Screening of 
DAPG producers 
When the DAPG-(lacZ) whole-cell biosensor was applied on the identified Pseudomonas spp. from 
Table 5 (p. 41), it revealed that no DAPG producers could be detected from the two different rhizo-
sphere soil samples. One possible explanation for this is the small sample size. A total of 5 isolates 
from each sample site is extremely limited when considering the extent of diversity in a microbial 
community. Moreover, as significant evidence can be derived from the spot-assay showing that the 
DAPG-(lacZ) whole-cell biosensor functions as expected when comparing negative and positive con-
trol, the whole setup might need to be revised due to the fact that spot-assay conducted in this manner, 
by spotting single tubes of O/N cultures, lacks the aspect of being high throughput. Efficient screening 
==PAGE:68==
Side 59 af 107 
 
of DAPG producers require analysis of hundreds, even thousands of isolates in order to find some 
that can be used in a commercial setting. In (Picard et al. 2000), the authors managed to identify 188 
DAPG producing Pseudomonas isolates from a total of 1716 isolates that they analyzed giving a ratio 
of approx. 1 DAPG producer for every 9 isolate. Lastly, it was observed that P. jessenii were com-
monly found in the two rhizosphere soil samples. Despite, their inability to produce DAPG, a study 
by (Qin et al. 2016) described that P. jessenii have biocontrol traits and the ability to suppress diseases 
and protect crops and plants from soilborne plant pathogens presumably by the production of antimi-
crobial compounds that are different from DAPG or by direct antagonistic behavior.  
5.4 Rationalizing the Unpredictable Dynamics of the pSEVA226::DAPG-luxCDABE Plas-
mid when Harbored within DAPG Producing Strains  
The results that were seen in Figure 23 (p. 49) are not so straightforward to interpret compared to the 
results seen in Figure 20 (p. 45). The main reason for this is due to the dynamics and behavior of the 
pSEVA226::DAPG-luxCDABE plasmid when induced by either DAPG or other reactive biomole-
cules produced by the bacteria themselves. Also, the elements from the genetic circuit, mainly the 
PLacIQ and PPhlF promoters and the RBSs, are constructed and optimized to work in E. coli rather than 
in Pseudomonas spp. However, certain observations were apparent and the discussion of these is 
inevitable.  
5.4.1 Comparing P. protegens DTU9.1 WT and P. kilonensis 5.36 WT Conjugants 
As briefly mentioned, when highlighting the results from this particular assay, the P. protegens 
DTU9.1 WT conjugant was the strain that had the highest bioluminescence peak even though the P. 
kilonensis 5.36 WT conjugant was the one hypothesized to have the highest peak based on a spot-
assay on a KBmalt plate supplemented with X-gal and with the DAPG-(lacZ) whole-cell biosensor 
spread on top. Three possible explanations for this could be; 1) the strains behave differently when 
grown on solid medium compared to in liquid medium. A common approach to optimize secondary 
metabolite production in bacteria and fungi is to find the optimal medium and growth conditions as 
shown by (Manteca and Yagüe 2018; VanderMolen et al. 2013) so it would not be out of the ordinary 
to think that the metabolic profile of Pseudomonas spp. would be impacted by the differences in 
sessile and planktonic growth, 2) the design of the genetic circuit by (A. J. Meyer et al. 2019) is 
flawed in the sense that the phlF gene originates from a P. protegens while the PPhlF promoter origi-
nates from a P. kilonensis. Thus, mutation of the phlF gene (phlFAM) was required for it to recognize 
and bind to the promoter. In P. kilonensis 5.36 WT, there is additional chromosomal production of 
==PAGE:69==

PhlF that can recognize the PPhlF promoter in contrast to the P. protegens DTU9.1 WT, which conse-
quently promote stronger repression of the reporter gene, and 3) the PLacIQ promoter located upstream 
of the phlFAM gene could potentially have a diminished effect in P. protegens or enhanced effect in 
P. kilonensis due to the strength and affinity of the innate RNA polymerase. Consequently, this would 
result in decreased or increased synthesis of the PhlF repressor protein in the respective species. 
5.4.2 Comparing P. protegens DTU9.1 WT Conjugant and its Mutant Strain

5.4.4 A Flaw in the Experimental Setup 
Regarding DAPG, a major flaw of the assay conducted in Section 3.10/3.10.1 is that the biolumines-
cence peak occurs at a time point where DAPG levels are low. The emission of bioluminescence 
naturally peaks in the early- to the mid-exponential phase when the cell density is low as shown by 
(Nealson, Platt, and Hastings 1970). The reason for this has not yet been elucidated but it is hypoth-
esized that it could be due to the competition for electrons as FMNH2 is also used for transporting 
electrons for oxidative phosphorylation or it could be due to the behavior of the lux genes, which are 
only activated at a specific time point such as the early- to the mid-exponential phase. Nonetheless, 
as with many other secondary metabolites, DAPG is mostly accumulated in the late exponential to 
the early stationary phase (Schnider-Keel et al. 2000). Therefore, based on this particular fact, it was 
concluded that it would be insightful to grow the DAPG producers (W/O biosensor plasmid) and 
extract the supernatant and use this as the source of DAPG to induce the DAPG-(luxCDABE) whole-
cell biosensor as done in Section 3.12/3.12.1.

5.5 The Carbon Source and its Role in Bioluminescence Emission 
As highlighted by Figure 23 (p. 49) and Figure 24 (p. 51), it is reasonable to think that the medium 
composition or the way that the DAPG producing strains interact with the medium in the lag phase 
influence both the position, pattern and size of the bioluminescence peak. Both KBmalt and LB me-
dium are rich in nutrients that allow effective growth. KBmalt consists of salts and proteose peptone 
that contains all the necessary nutrient requirements in the form of proteins, peptides and free amino 
acids (CSH Protocols 2019). Furthermore, it is supplemented with glycerol and malt extract, which 
mainly consists of maltose and complex carbohydrates (CSH Protocols 2019; Sigma Aldrich 2019b). 
Meanwhile, LB contains a mix of peptides, free amino acids, and carbohydrates (Sigma Aldrich 
2019a). By looking at it in this unique way, it can be speculated how the different strains interact with 
all the different carbon sources that are present in the two types of media. Certain carbon sources 
could potentially be metabolized more efficiently and cause the strain to peak earlier as indicated by 
Figure 23E (p. 49). Also, multiple carbon sources could be the cause of multiple bioluminescence 
peaks and certain carbon sources could influence the size of bioluminescence peak as indicated by 
Figure 23CD (p. 49). 
Despite the failure of this assay, the PM1 BIOLOG assay is a convincing approach to assess how the 
DAPG producers are influenced by the medium composition. By growing the conjugant harboring 
the pSEVA226::DAPG-luxCDABE plasmid in a medium containing only one carbon source, this as-
say would had been able to demonstrate how the different carbon sources would influence the biolu-
minescence peak position, pattern and size, and thereby, either the DAPG production or some other 
reactive biomolecules. Scientific evidence is already out there, showing that the components in the 
medium can affect the DAPG production as demonstrated by (Hultberg and Alsanius 2008) where 
different nitrogen sources were evaluated. 
5.6 Supernatant from O/N Cultures of DAPG Producers Induces Detectable Response in 
the DAPG-(luxCDABE) Whole-Cell Biosensor Based on the Type of Strain 
Due to the inherent nature of the luxCDABE operon, the occurrence of reaction and emission of bio-
luminescence takes place in the early- to the mid-exponential phase. This is at a time point where the 
levels of DAPG are expected to be low given that DAPG is a secondary metabolite that is mostly 
==PAGE:72==
Side 63 af 107 
 
being accumulated in the late exponential to early stationary phase as explained in Section 5.4.4. 
Therefore, because of this phenomenon, the assay that was conducted in Section 3.10/3.10.1 and the 
results presented in Section 4.8/4.8.1/4.8.2 are not the most scientifically reassured findings. By using 
the supernatant from O/N cultures of DAPG producers, it was hypothesized that the concentration of 
DAPG would be equal to the amounts found in the late exponential to stationary phase. Furthermore, 
this experiment was conducted to see if the DAPG-(luxCDABE) whole-cell biosensor could be in-
duced from DAPG that was extracted from a soil sample. The latter could potentially be developed 
into a powerful assay by assessing the levels of DAPG in different types of soils. 
5.6.1 Analysis of the Acquired Results 
By looking at Figure 26 (p. 54), when comparing the normalized bioluminescence data over time to 
the negative control, only the supernatant from P. kilonensis 5.36 WT was capable of inducing a 
response in the DAPG-(luxCDABE) whole-cell biosensor. These results were unexpected in due to 
the following two reasons; 1) based on a previous spot-assay conducted in the IMG, confirmation of 
P. protegens DTU9.1 WT being a DAPG producer is experimentally proven and 2) bioluminescence 
peaks for the P. protegens DTU9.1 WT conjugant were seen in Figure 23C (p. 49) when grown in 
both liquid KBmalt and LB medium. A possible explanation for this could be that P. protegens DTU9.1 
WT only produces minimal amounts of DAPG when grown in a liquid medium compared to solid 
agar as confirmed by HPLC data of the P. protegens DTU9.1 WT (from a previous experiment con-
ducted in the IMG). When grown on solid KBmalt and LB medium, the HPLC analysis revealed de-
tectable levels of DAPG, but when grown in liquid medium of the same two types of media, DAPG 
was only detected in KBmalt at a limited amount that was barely detectable (data not available). One 
major limitation of this assay is that the DAPG producers and the mutant strain were only grown in 
liquid LB medium despite having the HPLC information available. Nonetheless, the bioluminescence 
peaks that are seen in Figure 23C (p. 49) are, therefore, most likely, not caused by the induction of 
DAPG, but due to the incompatibility of the pSEVA226::DAPG-luxCDABE plasmid when harbored 
within Pseudomonas spp. Bioluminescence peaks that were similar to the peaks seen in Figure 20 
(p. 45) both position- and pattern-wise were observed when using the supernatant from P. kilonensis 
5.36 WT, which indicates that it is possible to extract the DAPG produced in the late exponential/sta-
tionary phase and use this to induce the DAPG-(luxCDABE) whole-cell biosensor. No HPLC data 
were available for this strain, however, these observations correlate well with the expectations at the 
pre-experimental stage that P. kilonensis 5.36 WT were found to produce more DAPG based on a 
spot-assay. Finally, results also showed that the supernatant from a soil sample that had been 
==PAGE:73==
Side 64 af 107 
 
suspended in sterile DI water was incapable of inducing a significant output response. How the DAPG 
interacts with the soil particles is not completely understood yet, so optimization of this singular part 
of the assay is therefore required.  
Taken together, when assessing all the kinetic assays that have been conducted, supernatant from 
certain DAPG producers can be used to induce a response in the DAPG-(luxCDABE) whole-cell bi-
osensor in a comparable manner to when pure DAPG is utilized. The amount of DAPG produced 
depends on the type of medium when assessing the HPLC data (KBmalt vs LB and sessile vs planktonic 
growth) and lastly, no definite conclusion can be derived from the data obtained when the 
pSEVA226::DAPG-luxCDABE plasmid is harbored within the DAPG producers themselves due to 
the unpredictable nature of the intracellular processes.

6 Conclusion 
The society moves forward at an ever-accelerating pace and the demand for food increases faster than 
ever before. Both the rapid increase in the global economy and population have contributed to this 
global challenge and the crops that are lost to diseases caused by soil-borne plant pathogens presents 
a prominent obstacle, which cannot be solved in a sustainable manner by conventional means. As a 
solution to this proposed issue, PGPR that are capable of producing a wide array of antimicrobial 
compounds can be utilized as biocontrol agents. In order to find suitable PGPR candidates from soil 
samples, the application of whole-cell biosensors offers a renewable, environmentally-friendly and 
cost-effective way of doing so.  
In this project, the point of interest was to test, utilize and optimize whole-cell biosensors that can 
detect the antimicrobial compound called DAPG, which is produced by fluorescent Pseudomonads. 
Two different DAPG biosensor plasmids harbored in an E. coli host were assessed, one with lacZ as 
the reporter gene and one with the luxCDABE operon. Both showed high sensitivity and high selec-
tivity when induced with pure DAPG compared to MAPG and PG. The sensitivity of the DAPG 
biosensor plasmid with the lacZ reporter gene was further analyzed with imaging giving somewhat 
comparable results. To test the DAPG whole-cell biosensor with the lacZ reporter gene, fluorescent 
Pseudomonads were isolated and identified. 10 isolates were tested in a spot-assay setup, however, 
none of them had the ability to produce DAPG. Furthermore, the DAPG biosensor plasmid with the 
luxCDABE operon was transferred to known DAPG producers and a mutant strain to assess the dy-
namics but no conclusive statements could be made due to the incompatibility between plasmid and 
hosts. Moreover, the medium influence on the output response could not be determined due to expired 
material in one of the assays. Finally, by using the supernatant from O/N cultures of certain DAPG 
producers, the DAPG whole-cell biosensor with the luxCDABE operon could be induced to generate 
an output response that was comparable to the output response obtained using pure DAPG.  
All things considered, the advancement in the fields of genetic engineering and synthetic biology 
has opened up new ways of thinking. The findings that have been presented in this thesis provide a 
healthy and insightful discussion regarding the promising potential that whole-cell biosensors offer 
for the detection of secondary metabolites produced by Pseudomonads. However, as the concept is 
not entirely novel, the smallest deviation from the high standards that are established means that op-
timization of the whole-cell biosensors is required in order to thrive in the highly competitive mar-
ket with meaningful assays to support the idea itself.

7 Future Research 
The idea of using whole-cell biosensors to detect producers of antimicrobial compounds is great and 
innovative but if things do not work as intended and if results are ambiguous, selling such an idea 
might be close to impossible. Therefore, striving for knowledge and understanding should be desired 
and this comes with hard work and effort. In the end, both scientific credibility and consensus can be 
achieved and this is how the world of science and technology works, and if this needs to continue, 
things must be assessed with a curious mind and be judged with critical eyes. The same statement 
applies to this project and there are all sorts of things that can be looked at.  
Based on the studies that use DAPG concentrations that are capable of inhibiting soil-borne plant 
pathogens, the dynamic output range might not be sufficient if quantification of DAPG production is 
desired. Therefore, a complete assessment of the dynamic output range should at least be conducted 
for the DAPG-(lacZ) whole-cell biosensor by increasing the amounts of ONPG in the β-galactosidase 
assay. Moreover, the dynamic output range can be further optimized by implementing a transcrip-
tional amplifier and the leaky expression that was seen in Figure 16 (p. 40) could be optimized by 
random mutagenesis of the elements in the genetic circuit or by implementing more phlO operator 
sequences in the PPhlF promoter combined with the addition of the transcriptional amplifier. To use 
the DAPG-(lacZ) whole-cell biosensor in a high throughput manner for the detection of DAPG pro-
ducers, the assay that was conducted in Section 3.6/3.6.1 needs to be revamped. One way to do so is 
by selecting isolates from a plate and inoculate a 96-microtiter plate and use this plate to make freeze 
stock cultures in another 96-microtiter plate. By doing so, all isolates could be grown at once and 
spotted at the same time. The results from Section 4.8/4.8.1/4.8.2 show quite a lot but the interpreta-
tion of the things that are seen is not as straightforward. The only possible way to improve this assay 
would be to change the elements in the genetic circuit so that the pSEVA226::DAPG-luxCDABE 
plasmid is optimized to work in Pseudomonas spp. With that being said, the assay did show that the 
medium composition could have a possible impact on the bioluminescence peaks so it would be ideal 
to redo the PM1 BIOLOG assay with new plates in triplicates, though it would be necessary to con-
firm that P. protegens DTU9.1 WT is capable of producing DAPG in a liquid medium. In the last 
assay that was presented in this thesis, the supernatant from P. protegens DTU9.1 WT was incapable 
of inducing a response in the DAPG-(luxCDABE) whole-cell biosensor. By comparing the HPLC 
data that was done in the IMG, small amounts of DAPG can be detected when grown in liquid KBmalt 
medium compared to liquid LB medium. Therefore, it would be obvious to redo the assay and grow 
the strains in liquid KBmalt medium. Alternatively, cutting out a spotted strain on either solid KBmalt 
==PAGE:76==
Side 67 af 107 
 
or LB agar followed by dissolvement and centrifugation to obtain supernatant could also be an inter-
esting way to conduct the assay as the concentrations of DAPG are much higher on the solid medium 
based on the HPLC data. Finally, the supernatant from the soil sample was also incapable of inducing 
a significant response. This particular soil sample was two weeks old, so it is difficult to know the 
exact fate of the DAPG, both in terms of composition and availability. In this case, a fresh soil sample 
should be used and variation in the processing of the soil sample could be applied.  
As a last remark, while DAPG is a highly relevant antimicrobial compound of interest, which can be 
detected in nature, it is only one compound among many others. Based on the brilliant work by (A. 
J. Meyer et al. 2019), constructing whole-cell biosensors for the detection of other antimicrobial com-
pounds that are also being synthesized by fluorescent Pseudomonas spp. such as pyoluteorin, phena-
zines, pyrrolnitrin, etc. is most likely feasible due to the availability of complete genome sequences 
of many strains providing the source of the recognition element. It is even imaginable that the detec-
tion of non-antimicrobial compounds such as auxins and AHLs could be done by following the prin-
ciples of (A. J. Meyer et al. 2019) as it provides a valuable framework that is both scientifically proven 
and universal in its nature.
