Abstract 
 
As a result of rapid changes in technology, companies increasingly adopt social media 
in the workplace to support their activities. Although social media are on top of the 
agenda for companies, studies related to the impact on wellbeing of employees are 
still somewhat scarce. This thesis aims to investigate how the use of social media 
affects employees’ wellbeing. Specifically, implementing a conceptual model which 
attempts to link the use of social media in the workplace with positive or negative 
effects. This model entails three aspects of wellbeing; subjective, eudaimonic and 
social wellbeing. The case organization which is analyzed for this purpose is a Danish 
medical manufacturing company that has adopted social media. The data was 
collected through the execution of eight interviews with employees of the company. 
The findings presented, emerged from thematic qualitative analysis based on the 
components of the selected model. Results show most positive effects on eudaimonic 
and social wellbeing but also some negative effects on subjective wellbeing. 
Organizations should pay more attention to social media and provide policies for the 
use to employees. Overall, the evidence suggests that social media use at work may 
enhance the wellbeing of employees. 
 
Key Words: Employee wellbeing, Social media, Subjective, Eudaimonic, Social, 
Workplace, Case study

1. Introduction 
 
Undoubtedly, the world nowadays is facing many changes as a result of the 
technological development. Progress in information and communication technology 
have significantly  influenced business operations and communication strategies in 
organizations and businesses have been using information and communication 
technologies in nearly every area of their activities (M. Wang & Wang, 2018). Currently 
highly competitive companies are increasingly interested in implementing emerging 
technologies for their business operations (Cetinkaya & Rashid, 2018). The emerging 
penetration of digital networks into our personal, social and professional lives 
accentuates more attention to the various ways in which such technologies affect 
wellbeing (Shankardass, Robertson, Shaughnessy, Sykora, & Feick, 2019).  
Following the growth of technology, organizations and their employees have initiated 
to progressively use various technologies at the workplace (Thomas & Akdere, 2013). 
The formal definition of these technologies is enterprise social media, but often called 
internal social media or social media (Leftheriotis & Giannakos, 2014; Leonardi, 
Huysman, & Steinfield, 2013). Social media are increasingly being introduced as tools 
for collaboration among employees in work organizations (Leftheriotis & Giannakos, 
2014).  
Adoption of social media is reducing the boundaries within organizations and 
reconstructs the way contemporary enterprises are managed (Howard-Jones, 2011). 
A key success factor in a fast-growing global business environment is the ability to 
communicate and collaborate with people inside and outside the organization (Gao, 
Wang, & Wang, 2019).   
Although employees are increasingly using social media at work, management 
scholars have received little evidence of how employees use these resources and what 
role they play in professional networks (Fusi & Zhang, 2018). Social media technology 
can however be seen as a double-edged sword; has benefits and negative effects and 
so far, less is known about the effect of social media use in organizations 
(Charoensukmongkol, 2015). Despite the fact that companies have social media at the 
top of their agenda, there is little evidence about the usage of social media for work 
purposes and the benefits as well as the negative effects are still somewhat scarce 
(Men, O’Neil, & Ewing, 2020).  
The digitized workplace provides unexpected opportunities for organizations, even 
though the use of internal social media may emerge some challenges or risks (Mishra, 
Boynton, and Mishra 2014). These risks might impact both the organization and 
employees. For instance, employees can access social media outside of their working 
hours for job related purposes and that can interrupt the balance between work and 
family life (Ayyagari, Grover, & Purvis, 2011; Bucher, Fieseler, & Suphan, 2013). For

the organization, a risk can be that employees might reveal content of the 
organization to their private network.  
The latest global coronavirus outbreak that the entire planet has been experiencing, 
has affected different aspects of people’s life and urged the use of social media not 
only for private use but for work related purposes as well. To overcome the challenges 
of the new way of working, collaboration and networking companies are indicating 
that employees continue to accelerate the adoption of digital technologies globally. 
The majority of the organizations started working remotely, encouraged employees 
to work at their own place and particularly changing their behaviours to help prevent 
the spread of COVID-19. As daily routines altered overnight, more of peoples’ daily 
and work lives altered to online, and social media became an even more important 
tool for connection (Gong, 2020).  
Employees are the ones who contribute to the success of an organization and are the 
real assets. For this reason, organizations have started taking action to ensure their 
employees’ satisfaction (Bauman & Skitka, 2012). Interest in employee wellbeing has 
risen greatly in recent years (Ilies, Aw, & Pluut, 2015). However, the questions of how 
it should be defined remains unanswered and there is no universal definition (Dodge, 
Daly, Huyton, & Sanders, 2012). Researchers have been measuring aspects of 
satisfaction, happiness, or wellbeing at work for nearly a century and a great number 
of constructs have been developed (Fisher, 2014). An important finding of the 
management scholars states that wellbeing at work is multidimensional (Dodge et al., 
2012; Fisher, 2014; Forgeard, Jayawickreme, Kern, & Seligman, 2011; Simone, 2014). 
These elements are job satisfaction, job involvement, affective organizational 
commitment, work engagement, positive and negative emotions and moods at work, 
ﬂow states, intrinsic motivation, thriving, and vigour (Fisher, 2014).  
Many definitions and concepts of wellbeing at work have been suggested, often 
covering different dimensions of wellbeing. The exploration of wellbeing at the 
workplace is a prominent niche for different reasons. For instance, employees bring 
their personal emotional or social experiences at work and usually they don’t leave 
their work related issues at the workplace, so there is a reciprocal relation between 
wellbeing of the employees and their workplace (Simone, 2014).  
The changes in organizations’ workplace caused by the revolution of technology are 
related to the advent of new communication technologies, particularly social media. 
As the usage of social media has become progressively popular among organizations, 
employees feel fostered by the organization, have the resources to socialize with their 
colleagues, build social relationships, share their knowledge and learn; feel cognitive 
and emotionally fulfilled (Naim & Bulinska-Stangrecka, 2019). On the other hand, use 
of social media at the workplace may have negative effects on employees. For 
example, an employee may experience feelings of stress, invasion of privacy or

overload, which might affect employee’s productivity or job performance (Heymann, 
Fischer, & Fischbach, 2020).  
The need for this research has emanated from the calls for enquiry into the effect of 
workplace use of social media on employees’ wellbeing. The social media usage in the 
workplace has been mostly focused to job satisfaction, happiness and employee 
engagement (Benson, Johnson, & Kuchinke, 2002; Cao, Guo, Vogel, & Zhang, 2016;

use of social media in the workplace.  
This thesis is commissioned by a high technology manufacturing company;

Nevertheless, the implications of such activities have not been evaluated by the

1.1 Problem statement 
 
The main purpose of this study is to investigate the relationship between the use of 
social media in the workplace and employees’ wellbeing. To examine this relation, 
interviews were held in a company’s employees. The question that will be answered 
is: 
“How the use of social media in the workplace affects employees’ wellbeing?”

2. Theoretical Framework 
 
The literature review section presents an overview of the central theories, terms and 
research which constitute the framework of this study. These include wellbeing in life 
and in the workplace and social media at the workplace. Then the specific social media 
of this case study are presented. Lastly, the relationship between social media use and 
wellbeing at the workplace is presented along with the model selected for this study. 
 
2.1 History of wellbeing  
 
Wellbeing is a growing area of research, the question of how it should be defined has 
not been answered yet and is a difficult term to define precisely (Dodge et al., 2012; 
Fisher, 2014; White, 2010). There are main theoretical perspectives, from the work of 
Aristotle since today. The research in wellbeing has been growing in during the last 
decades but as the question of the definition of wellbeing continues being unresolved 
and “has given rise to blurred and overly broad definitions of wellbeing” (Forgeard et 
al., 2011). This is confirmed by the phrase “intangible, difficult to define and even 
harder to measure” referring to wellbeing (Dodge et al., 2012).  
There is a difference between the description and the definition of wellbeing. As the 
interest in the measurement of wellbeing is constantly growing, it is of high need to 
have a clear understanding of what it is measured, how the data should be analysed 
in order to guarantee a valid assessment. Consequently, every new concept 
must go beyond an account or explanation of wellbeing itself, and must be able to 
state explicitly and simply the exact meaning of the term (Dodge et al., 2012). It is 
important to see what constitutes wellbeing and is described as: “a complex, multi-
faceted construct that has continued to elude researchers’ attempts to define and 
measure”(Pollard & Lee, 2003). 
“Doing well - feeling good” is a relatively popular wellbeing statement that 
incorporates the dual dimension of wellbeing. “Doing well” conveys the material 
aspect of welfare or living conditions, implying a basis for economic growth, but it 
should not be limited to this. “Feeling good” represents the subjective aspect of 
personal experiences and satisfaction levels. The second line, “doing good – feeling 
well” illustrates more the opinion in developed countries.  
 
 
==PAGE:14==
 
8 
 
Figure 2: Conceptualising Wellbeing. Source: (White, 2010). 
 
In ancient times, Aristotle’s idea of eudaimonia, which is now more commonly 
translated as wellbeing, could help understand where this term has its origins. In 
Aristotle’s Nicomachean Ethics the concept of Eudaimonia can be traced. The most 
simple translation of eudaimonia is often taken to mean happiness (van den Eijnden, 
Meerkerk, Vermulst, Spijkerman, & Engels, 2008). To provide more context, 
eudaimonia is a combination of the prefix eu (which means good, or well), 
and daimon (which means spirit) (Oxford University Press, 2019).  
 
Aristotle’s idea, continues to shape how we study flourishing and wellbeing (Huta & 
Waterman, 2014). The term of eudaimonia appears within modern psychology in 
many recent studies, some of the aspects that have been studied are the “definition – 
not only conceptualizing the idea of eudaimonia in terms of psychology, wellbeing, 
and happiness, but also trying to operationalize the concept” (Huta & Waterman, 
2014; Ryan & Deci, 2001) and the relation between eudaimonia and subjective 
wellbeing (Diener, 1994).   
 
The historical background of the studies of wellbeing it is essential knowledge to the 
definition of it (Dodge et al., 2012). There were two approaches loomed: the hedonic 
wellbeing, which emphasizes aspects such as happiness, positive affect, low negative 
affect, and satisfaction with life (Bradburn, 1969; Diener, 1994; Lyubomirsky & Lepper, 
1999) and the eudaimonic wellbeing  which accentuated constructs like positive 
psychological functioning and human development (Ryff, 1989; Waterman, 1993). 
Nonetheless, despite the differences in the studies and the different approaches, 
scholars and practioners tend to agree that wellbeing is  a multi-dimensional term 
(Diener, 1984; Fisher, 2014; Oades, Steger, Fave, & Passmore, 2016; Simone, 2014). 
As a consequence, the multidimensional approach has created “confusing and 
contradictory research base” (Pollard & Lee, 2003).  
 
2.1.1 Wellbeing in life 
 
Philosophers and scholars have various ways of describing happiness and wellbeing. 
There is a great number of conceptual and practical meanings of these terms and 
==PAGE:15==
 
9 
various writers used the same terminology incoherently. Some conceptualisations are 
well known, while others are much newer and less consensual. (Fisher, 2014). The 
primary division is between hedonic views of wellbeing as nice feelings and 
evaluations, as opposed to eudaimonic views which suggest that wellbeing involves 
behaviour that is self-realizing, meaningful and produces growth (Fisher, 2014; Ryan 
& Deci, 2001).  
 
There are three main categories for defining the wellbeing in life according to Fisher, 
(2014). These categories are: 
• Hedonic wellbeing 
• Eudaimonic wellbeing 
• Social wellbeing 
 
Hedonic wellbeing 
 
Most of the wellbeing work has concentrated on the hedonic dimension of living an 
enjoyable life. "Subjective wellbeing"  is a concept developed and often studied in this 
tradition (Fisher, 2014). Subjective wellbeing is consisted of three aspects (Diener, 
1984): the frequent experience of positive affect, the infrequent experience of 
negative affect, and positive cognitive evaluations of life satisfaction.  
 
There has been some controversy as to whether or not positive and negative 
outcomes are opposite ends of the same bipolar spectrum, or are less than fully 
compatible unipolar features which are separable, where the latter opinion is being 
supported the most nowadays (Schimmack, 2007). Positive and negative effects 
contribute uniquely to the estimation of such outcomes and their relative frequency 
have significant consequences. Hence, all results should be included in subjective 
wellbeing concepts and measurements.  
 
Nonetheless, this does not address the question of how positive and negative 
emotions can better be conceptualised and assessed. Sometimes, the affective aspect 
of wellbeing is generally conceptualized as hedonic expression, good versus negative 
moods and emotions (Fredrickson & Losada, 2005).  
 
Eudaimonic Wellbeing 
 
Philosophers consider eudaimonic wellbeing as significant as the other aspects of 
wellbeing (Sheldon & Elliot, 1999). This comprises living a good life, not just an 
acceptable one (Fisher, 2014). Eudaimonic approaches are related to the fulfilment of 
fundamental human needs for competence, autonomy, connectedness and self-
acceptance. The emphasis is on development, life purpose, achievement of self-
==PAGE:16==
 
10
conforming goals, self-realization and virtue (Warr, 2011). This theory has origins in 
Greek philosophy but a much newer and less established measurement and scientific 
analysis basis than subjective wellbeing (Waterman, 2008).  
 
Kashdan (2008) note that any dimension of wellbeing that is not directly affective 
tends to be eudaimonic in nature. Another definition related to eudaimonic wellbeing 
is the lifelong experience of intention or meaning in life (e.g The Meaning in Life 
Questionnaire) (Huta, 2016; Oades et al., 2016).  
 
The literature is currently debating whether hedonic and eudaimonic aspects of 
wellbeing can be conceptually and empirically distinguished (Kashdan et al., 2008). 
They are strongly associated in practice. There is ample evidence that behaviour in 
eudaimonic ways predicts hedonic pleasure (Kashdan et al., 2008; Steger, Frazier, 
Kaler, & Oishi, 2006; Waterman, 2008). Waterman argues that is anticipated, since 
being self-actualizing is pleasant and satisfying. He also points out that eudaimonic 
wellbeing is important but not necessary for hedonic satisfaction, and that hedonic 
and eudaimonic wellbeing indicators have rather different triggers and different 
predictive relations with outcomes. Nevertheless, both kind of wellbeing are 
significant and should be investigated.  
 
Social wellbeing 
 
There might also be a third distinct dimension of wellbeing complementing the 
hedonic component of inner satisfaction and the aspect eudaimonic of development, 
autonomy and self-realization (Fisher, 2014). It's the more outward-looking dimension 
of social wellbeing (Forgeard et al., 2011). This is consistent with the theories of 
fundamental need which all recognize the value of social relations. Another analysis 
reveals that social interactions are of high importance for human wellbeing and they 
conclude that in healthy relationships with others, individuals need regular 
interactions which involve giving and receiving care (Fisher, 2014). 
 
An empirical support for a hierarchical structure of wellbeing is suggested by 
(Gallagher, Lopez, & Preacher, 2009) and is presented in Figure 3. The three categories 
of wellbeing: hedonic (or subjective), eudaimonic and social wellbeing, have distinct 
indicators but are closely correlated and the combination of them suggest the overall 
wellbeing. Some researchers propose that each element should be evaluated 
separately (Oades et al., 2016).

2.2 Wellbeing at work 
 
More and more researchers take an interest in wellbeing in the workplace over the 
years. Wellbeing at work has been the subject of research for organizational 
researchers for nearly a century. Overall wellbeing at work is a multidimensional 
function (Fisher, 2014; Oades et al., 2016; Simone, 2014).  
Researcher suggest that the key dimensions of wellbeing may be: 
 a) subjective wellbeing,  
 b) eudaimonic wellbeing  
 c) social wellbeing, and each has subdimensions.  
The separate aspects of wellbeing at work might fit together to comprise overall 
wellbeing in the workplace as shown in Figure 4. Components of overall wellbeing at 
work are analysed explicitly below. 
 
2.2.1 Subjective wellbeing at work  
 
As mentioned above, subjective wellbeing defines the holistic experience in life and 
mirrors an individual’s self-explanation of happiness. It encompasses positive 
attitudinal decisions but also knowledge of both positive and negative effect. Job 
satisfaction is a significant element of subjective wellbeing, as it is a positive emotional 
mode of being that derives from an assessment of a person’s job or job experiences.  
==PAGE:18==
 
12
It is the subject that has been investigated the most in the field of organizational 
behaviour discipline (Fisher, 2014; Simone, 2014). 
 
Another important element of wellbeing at work’s attitude that has been usually 
evaluated is organizational commitment. It reflects to the normative obligation that is 
based on the personal identification with organizational goals and values; and to the 
affective commitment that is based on feeling a member of the organizational 
community. The last 25 years researchers took great interest in the impact positive or 
negative it has at work, described as standard or temporary tempers (mood swings) 
employees encounter while working (Fisher, 2014). 
 
Other elements it encompasses are positive and negative effects. As mentioned by De 
Simone (2014) “positive effect includes the levels of energy, excitement, enthusiasm, 
joy, interest, appreciation, vigour or physical strength, and cognitive liveliness”. These 
emotions stimulate social interplay. In contrast negative effect, mirrors an 
unsympathetic mood of being filled with feeling such “as anger, anxiety, depression, 
tiredness and fearfulness”. 
 
These emotions inevitably are a part of organizational life, the same way as they are 
part of every aspect of life. Negative emotions don’t necessarily associate to 
consequent adverse outcomes. The same way as positive emotions don’t necessarily 
associate to positive outcomes following the emotional experience. As in life, negative 
emotions are often a source of useful signals to employees in regard to areas where 
learning is essential (Fisher, 2014).  
 
In this study, job satisfaction, organisational commitment, and affect at work will be 
examined in relation to social media use in the workplace. 
 
2.2.2 Eudaimonic wellbeing at work  
 
Organizational behaviour constructs show a fractional overlap to eudaimonic 
wellbeing. This overlap contains elements of a) job involvement, b) work engagement, 
c) thriving, d) flow e) intrinsic motivation, and f) meaning in work.  
Job involvement entails the state of psychological identification to one’s work, 
meaning the extent to which a job is key point to a person’s identity. To the 
organizational perspective, it entails the secret to unlock employee motivation and to 
increase productivity. Work engagement is described as harnessing of one’s self to his 
work role in a positive work-related state of mind that allows him to express himself 
physically, cognitively, emotionally and mentally. Vigour refers to high levels of 
physical and cognitive energy, spunk and mental resilience while working. Dedication 
entails strong involvement in one’s work and the feeling of importance, enthusiasm, 
==PAGE:19==
 
13
and challenge. Absorption refers to being focused and cheerfully absorbed at work, in 
which time passes quickly, and having difficulties detaching from work (Fisher, 2014; 
Simone, 2014). 
In addition, thriving is a condition beyond mere survival, inferring to growth (learning, 
developing) and positive development (progress toward self-actualization). Flow 
arises when an employee is fully absorbed in using his skills to progress on a 
challenging task. Intrinsic motivation is usually quantified as the subjective experience 
of interest or pleasure while involved in a task. It can overlap to subjective wellbeing.    
Finally meaning in work is linked to the work role itself, which translates to having an 
important and self-actualizing role (Fisher, 2014; Simone, 2014).   
For this research, specific subdimensions of eudaimonic wellbeing will be on the focus 
and these are: job involvement, work engagement and thriving at work. 
 
2.2.3 Social wellbeing at work  
 
The third aspect of wellbeing at work is known as social wellbeing. This last module 
has been investigated the least from researchers in organizational literature. It reflects 
to being part of communities, establishing meaningful relations, and having 
substantial short-term communication, and long-term relations with others while 
working (Fisher, 2014). 
Fisher (2014) mentions that quality in engagement with others at work, nurtures a 
climate of trust and respect. This engagement relates to eudaimonic wellbeing and it 
promotes growth and thriving as it is well known that individuals are more likely to be 
involved in working environments when not only leaders but also peers care for them. 
As she mentions individuals are more engaged when they have a best friend at work, 
and they can spend time with other employees whose company appreciate. These 
relations among peers in the organisation are categorised an important construct of 
social wellbeing, called quality connections. Even though social wellbeing at work is in 
its early stages, existing models provide insight.  
Another significant subdimension of social wellbeing is social support, which has been 
mostly studied as a factor that reduces stress at the workplace and it can be simply 
translated as giving and receiving support. Though, there is evidence that giving and 
receiving support, is an important factor of social wellbeing (Fisher, 2014). Lastly, 
there is an additional subdimension of social wellbeing feelings of belonging or being 
embedded in communities whether this happens in teams, departments or the 
organization as a whole (Fisher, 2014).  
Regarding social wellbeing, the aspects that are covered in this study are quality 
connections, belonging in communities and giving and receiving support.

2.3 Social Media 
 
In a rapidly changing world, social media came to reshape the nature of people’s 
relationships and communication. They offer new and exciting ways of interacting 
with stakeholders and gain intimate and real-time knowledge of how family, friends, 
colleagues think, act and discuss (Bucher et al., 2013). Along with the new possibilities, 
social media brought also new challenges for the workplace and the work itself (Carr 
& Hayes, 2015).  
Social media encompasses bundles of information and communication tools, which 
provide multiple channels of communication in social and work environments 
(Cetinkaya & Rashid, 2018). The changing world has made social media imperative 
need for every organization. Social media allows organizations to understand their 
customers' needs and desires better (Cao et al., 2016). Organizations are getting more 
interested in the use of social media in the workplace. Nonetheless, less is known 
about the effect of social media on work (Gao et al., 2019).  
Social media consists of three sections these are: devices that create and disseminate 
the material, devices that gather the material, and individuals that use the information 
for official and personal use (Carr & Hayes, 2015). Such three parts allow its users to 
be linked to one platform. These platforms provide users without any time and space 
constraints to submit and exchange information (Gao et al., 2019).  
Social media networks affect corporate activities greatly. Organizations use social 
media for official benefits such as attracting and engaging their clients (Macnamara & 
Zerfass, 2012). Customers, on the other hand, often look for these social media 
resources that help them make buying decisions and establish ties with organizations 
(Chan & Guillet, 2011). Although, the customer point of view regarding social media 
will not be examined in this research.  
 
2.3.1 Social media at the workplace 
 
As social media have recently appeared in the organizations and have taken great 
place in the organizations context; there are two fundamental ways that they are used 
(Leonardi et al., 2013). The most studied by researchers, is the way that the 
organization communicates with external parties, customers, sellers and the public in 
general. Companies usually maintain websites or pages on popular social media 
platforms like Twitter and LinkedIn. So, it this way communication is considered 
external (Leonardi et al., 2013). 
The second way of using social media within an organization, which is less studied, 
refers to the social media use for internal communication and interaction in the 
company (Leonardi et al., 2013). Therefore, this way will be investigated in detail and 
==PAGE:21==
 
15
has more focus. The majority of the organizations, implement a combined social 
media platform that accommodates various operations (Thomas & Akdere, 2013).  
These internal communication tools present significant resemblances in the structure 
and the functions with popular platforms like Facebook, but usually come with 
embedded features such as document sharing and search, instant chatting and 
meeting scheduling (Leonardi et al., 2013). The use of these platforms is always 
protected by firewalls, they usually run on the organization’s servers and the use is 
restricted to the employees (Jarrahi & Sawyer, 2012). These characteristics make the 
internal social media platforms hard to distinguish according to their functions, though 
it is agreed to define them as enterprise social media platforms (ESM) (Benson et al., 
2002).  
The definition of the “Enterprise Social Media” comes as: “Web-based networks 
allowing employees to (a) transmit messages to specific employees or broadcast 
messages to everyone in the organization; (b) specifically or indirectly report specific 
employees as contact partners; (c) publish, edit, and sort text and files linked to 
themselves or others; and (d) display messages, links, text and files exchanged, 
uploaded, edited, and sorted by anyone in the organization at any time of their 
preference” (Leonardi et al., 2013).  
The enterprise social media, in this research referred as internal social media or just 
social media, allows employees to exchange messages directly with people they 
already know or publish messages to a larger group of people. It is also permitted to 
post documents, files, images where specific people or a broader audience has access. 
These posts remain online for later view and use by other employees at any time and 
this fact distinguishes them from other social media platforms. Last but not least, all 
the aforementioned activities of the definition, can be conducted all in one platform 
and this is an advantage for the organizations (Carr & Hayes, 2015).

2.4 Wellbeing and social media at the workplace 
 
Organizations have become increasingly immersed in technology, especially in social 
media. Though it is important to understand the implications of the online life for 
wellbeing and to explore the implications of using interactive technologies (Howard-
Jones, 2011). Despite the rapid adoption of social media by organizations, yet very 
little is known about the effects of social media on employees (Leftheriotis & 
Giannakos, 2014).  
Internal social media can help establish internal communities and connections, 
therefore promoting collaboration, knowledge sharing, organizational learning, and 
innovation (Leftheriotis & Giannakos, 2014; Leonardi et al., 2013; Men et al., 2020). 
As social media promote information transfer, thus employees improve their own 
knowledge on different work related topics (Thomas & Akdere, 2013). 
It has been increasingly acknowledged among scholars the connection between social 
media and employee engagement and the relation with employee work performance 
as well (Leftheriotis & Giannakos, 2014; Men et al., 2020; Naim & Bulinska-Stangrecka, 
2019). On the other hand, social media use inside the company has been blamed for 
reducing productivity as employees spend great percentage of their work time on 
these platforms (Leftheriotis & Giannakos, 2014). 
Studies, that have examined internal social media and its effectiveness has widely 
taken an organization-centric approach, exploring the communicative and 
administrative strategies that organizations endorse internally to capitalize on the 
benefits of social media (Men et al., 2020). To date, there is limited research with focus 
on employees’ perspective with regards to social media. Questions like how 
employees use internal social media, and how their use of internal social media aﬀects 
their organizational attitudes and behaviours, remain blur (Cao et al., 2016; Pitafi et 
al., 2018; Shankardass et al., 2019).  
Given recent developments of digitalization of organizations, researchers contribute 
to the growing body of knowledge of social media in the workplace. Though, there is 
little evidence on how the use of social media affects employees’ wellbeing. This study 
seeks to fill this gap, using a conceptual model of the wellbeing in the workplace and 
attempts to examine the effects of social media use. 
From the literature, it is very clear that wellbeing at work is multidimensional (Fisher, 
2014; Oades et al., 2016; Simone, 2014). Researcher suggest that the key dimensions 
==PAGE:25==
 
19
of wellbeing may be subjective wellbeing, eudaimonic wellbeing and social wellbeing, 
and each has subdimensions. The separate aspects of wellbeing at work might fit 
together to comprise overall wellbeing in the workplace as shown in Figure 4.   
Fisher (2014) proposed a holistic approach in which she argues that overall wellbeing 
at work should contain subjective wellbeing including positive and negative effect, 
eudaimonic wellbeing and social wellbeing. This blend would be a fit model to assess 
perceptions towards the organization as a whole (affective commitment), perceptions 
about the working position, including contextual elements (job satisfaction), and job 
involvement and satisfaction of the work itself (engagement) (Fisher, 2014; Simone, 
2014).  
 
Explaining the figure below, starting with the inner circle, happiness, is the perception 
of good moods and emotions when working and it is one of three elements of 
subjective wellbeing at work. The second element, which also involves negative 
moods and feelings at workplace and cognitive work satisfaction judgements and 
related attitudes. The outer part of the circle, higher level of workplace wellbeing adds 
to eudaimonic and social wellbeing (Fisher, 2014).  
 
As such, for this thesis a conceptual model entailing three dimensions of wellbeing at 
work will be implemented in order to investigate employees’ wellbeing relation to the 
use of social media.

3.1 Research methodology 
 
The research method that researchers choose for their studies, qualitative or 
quantitative and which is more valid, is it still a controversial topic and the discussion 
doesn’t seem to have an end. For this reason, the characteristics of each method will 
be discussed shortly. 
 
3.1.1 Discussion of qualitative and quantitative method 
 
Quantitative methods are usually standardized procedures and aim to measure 
phenomena by numbers while using fixed variables. As they have measures which are 
standard they are more suitable for large samples and simplify the finding of 
generalized data (Pole & Lampard, 2015; Silverman, 2004). Some argue that the use 
of quantitative methods has little interaction with people or there is no interaction at 
all, so variables can be randomly defined. In addition, in some cases, the phenomena 
are not measurable by numbers, with statistics or with the technique of sampling 
randomly. These situations can lead the researcher to a dead end or limit the research 
or missing important elements in the study (Pole & Lampard, 2015).  
Snider (2010) made an observation that “numbers impress, but unfortunately, also 
conceal far more than they reveal” and would also agree with another researcher that 
stated “good qualitative research has equalled, if not exceeded, quantitative research 
in status, relevance, and methodological rigor” (Suter, 2014). The qualitative method 
doesn’t focus on numbers; it lies on in-depth understanding of words, opinions and 
experiences. It concentrates more on the individual rather than the general 
(Williamson, Given, & Scifleet, 2018). This method is always backed up with literature 
review, but the data lead the study and not the theory (Taylor, S. J., Bogdan, R., & 
DeVault, 2015).  
The aspects of validity and reliability have an important role when using the qualitative 
method, thus this method has received a lot of criticism towards these aspects. In 
addition, qualitative research can be considered as subjective because of the personal 
involvement of the researcher in the study (Suter, 2014). There are some limitations 
regarding the generalization of the research as firstly the sample is rarely picked 
randomly and secondly because is looking on individual’s cases and not groups of 
people (Ridder, Miles, Michael Huberman, & Saldaña, 2014). Even though, the main 
purpose of this kind of method is not generalize the results but to investigate a certain 
case and gain understanding of this specific content (Bryman, 2008).  
The nature of the research question determines which method should be selected. 
For this thesis, which is an explorative study, the qualitative method is more 
appropriate and has been selected (Suter, 2014). In this thesis, open questions are 
==PAGE:29==
 
23
used in order to explore employees’ perspectives towards the use of social media. The 
explorative character of this study is supported by existing theoretical frameworks in 
order to understand better the concept of wellbeing. Through exploration and 
observation of Radiometer employees’ statements, this study focuses mainly in 
understanding how the use of social media in the workplace affects employees.  
 
3.1.2 Verification of qualitative method 
 
Despite the choice of the method, it is always recommended in a study, to consider 
the validity and reliability as they help to determine the objectivity of the research.  
The question of if we can trust the findings of a qualitative study has been born. Thus, 
the focus will be on issues concerning the qualitative method, as this is used for this 
thesis.   
Reliability and validity could be seen as two different measurement instruments that 
illustrate the level of trustworthiness and credibility of a research. Reliability and 
validity are separated into internal and external concepts (Bryman, 2008). The term 
internal reliability refers to the number of researchers involved in the study, whether 
there is more than one they can agree on what they hear and observe. External 
reliability refers to what extent a study can be completed again with same results 
compared to the original study. A definition by Silverman (2004) is “A repetition by 
different researchers or by the same researcher at another time and place would come 
to the same result” but achieving reliability is especially difficult in qualitative studies 
(Ridder et al., 2014).  
It could be challenging to attain high levels of external reliability, as the environment 
and the circumstances may vary from case to case. An approach suggested by Bryman 
and Bell (2007) in order to replicate an original study, is to adapt a similar role taken 
on by the original researcher. Other strategies suggested by Silverman (2004), in order 
to conduct a reliable qualitative research study, include the display of the research 
process as well as the choice of the theoretical framework transparently, so other 
researchers can follow the same steps. Moreover, he proposes that readers of a 
scientific study should be able to gain access to the detailed observations made and 
not only summaries. For this purpose, transcripts of interviews are provided. 
In consideration of the above principles to attain high reliability for this study, the 
interviews are voice recorded and transcribed using different means, like “Otter” an 
application for smartphones that records and transcribes at the same, Google Drive 
that has the same function as the application and a traditional voice recording device. 
In addition, direct quotes from the transcripts are included in the analysis part, the 
process of gathering data and how the interviews are performed is described in detail. 
The interview questions are presented and the transcripts of the interviews; the latter 
==PAGE:30==
 
24
can be found in Appendix A4-A11, to enhance reliability. The detailed descriptions of 
the processes increase the ability for other researchers to repeat this study under 
same conditions with comparable results.  
The validity of qualitative research is often referred to as trustworthiness or credibility 
(Suter, 2014). The internal validity refers to what degree the researchers are able to 
agree and come to same conclusions (Bryman & Bell, 2007). A research study lies over 
a long period of time and researchers can observe the social phenomena for long, so 
this can be considered as a strength for the qualitative studies. Contrarily, external 
validity can be perceived as a problem because it refers to the extent that findings can 
be applicable in other social settings and qualitative analysts typically make use of 
small samples and case studies (Bryman, 2008; Bryman & Bell, 2007).  
To increase the validity in this thesis, the transcripts of the interviews were sent for 
revision to the interviewees and received their approval for the context. In this way, 
the possibility of using the authors own interpretation of the data is decreased. At this 
time, it should be highlighted that the participants of this study are employees of a 
company and they have busy schedules and they can’t spend a lot of time to revise 
word by word the text that was provided. Therefore, some words have been removed 
in case they were not clearly recorded.  
 
3.2 Data collection 
 
A suitable research method should be found for the data collection of the study. The  
technique for collecting data can be either primary or secondary (Gronhaug, 
Falkenberg, Eden, & Spender, 1998). The primary data is the information that the 
researchers collect on their own for example transcripts from interviews or data from 
questionnaires (Bryman & Bell, 2007). According to the previous mentioned 
researchers, secondary data is literature, papers, articles that have been gathered by 
others. A combination of both techniques is used for this study; primary and 
secondary. The primary data derive from the interviews. As for the secondary data 
that are used in this thesis, have been carefully assessed and originate from scientific 
papers, company’s report (Radiometer CSR report) and reliable resources from the 
internet. 
 
3.2.1 Choice of method 
 
Many different methods can be used in qualitative research like interviews and focus 
groups. Interview methodology is perhaps the oldest of all the social science 
methodologies (Bryman, 2008) and is a proper and reasonable method, it allows to 
use open ended questions and addressing them in a small sample and permits to 
==PAGE:31==
 
25
investigate individual’s perspectives and opinions towards the topic. The degree of 
structure varies, the interviews can be unstructured, semi-structured, structured and 
focus group interviews and every type has its advantages and disadvantages (Alsaawi, 
2016). In quantitative research, interviews are usually highly structured in order to 
reach a high validity and reliability (Bryman & Bell, 2007).  
Structured interviews are more reliable than unstructured ones (Savenye & Robinson, 
1996). The structured interviews have been selected as a series of pre-determined 
questions that all interviewees answer in the same order and help to target the 
specific phenomenon. Also, this kind of interview is standardized and the same open-
ended questions are asked to all interviewees; this approach enables quicker 
interviews that can be more easily analysed and compared (Kvale, 2011). Another 
aspect is that the concrete structure of the interview ensures that the theoretical 
framework is covered and that the significant elements of the literature are included. 
Thus, a semi-structured interview may have permitted the interviewee to probe 
irrelevant information and topics. With structured interviews the participant could go 
deeper into the topic. 
Interviews have limitations as any other method. The interviewer may be flavoured 
by the answers and the opinion of the interviewee. Another issue, is that 
misunderstandings and misinterpretations of words may occur during the analysis of 
the interview (Kvale, 2011).

3.2.3 Interviews 
 
In order to collect primary data, qualitative method was selected. The questions were 
open ended, and they are made based on the model that covers the three different 
aspects of wellbeing and compromises overall wellbeing at work. An example of an 
open-ended question included in the interview schedule is “Do you have problems 
concentrating because you are using social media?”. Open ended questions like this, 
allow the participant more scope to express thoughts and feelings and can offer more 
details on the research subject (Ridder et al., 2014).  
In this way, the questions were built on three different pillars. The first pillar was about 
subjective wellbeing and covered the elements of this aspect. The main elements were 
job satisfaction, organizational commitment and affect at work. So, the formulation of 
the questions for investigating subjective wellbeing was driven by these three 
elements. For instance, organizational commitment is interpreted as one’s personally 
identifying with the organization’s goals and values. So, in order to investigate the 
relation between commitment and social media use, the employees were asked: “Do 
you feel personally connected to your organization’s values while you are using social 
media?”. The bucket of questions regarding subjective wellbeing included all these 
elements. 
The second pillar encompassed eudaimonic wellbeing which has some main elements. 
The specific elements are job involvement, work engagement and thriving at work. As 
thriving at work is described as a positive, fulﬁlling, work-related state of mind that is 
characterized by vigour, dedication, and absorption, led the formulation of this 
question: “Do you have the possibility of learning new things while you are  using 
social media?”. Another example of questions is “Do you feel more engaged to your 
work/tasks if you continuously use social media?”. With this question the researcher 
aims to investigate the element of work engagement. 
Finally, the third pillar was constructed based on social wellbeing. These questions 
were focused on three elements of social wellbeing; quality connections, belonging in 
communities and giving-receiving support. Each of these, has an interpretation which 
was the basis to make the questions. For scholars, quality connections mean positive 
short-term transactions and longer-term relationships with others encountered while 
working. A question derived from this meaning was “. Are your colleagues willing to 
listen to your problems and help you through social media?”. All the questions 
followed this rationale.

question or the answer.  
A trial interview was conducted prior to the commencement of the actual research. 
An employee was approached and voluntarily helped. This process allowed the 
interviewee to express opinions and feelings related to the questions. In this way, 
difficulties with the language of the questions and the structure were resolved. This 
interview was not officially part of this study, so the data collected was not included 
in the analysis. 
During the primary data collection process there is always the risk of interviewee bias 
(Savenye & Robinson, 1996) and this would seriously affect the validity of the thesis 
findings. To avoid that, the interviewer did not overreact to responses of the 
interviewee, kept open mind and refrained from displaying disagreements or 
expression of personal opinion.  
 
3.3 Analysis 
 
After the interviews the researcher then moved to the analysis phase. Upon 
transcription, the data were coded, analysed and interpreted. The analysis part is 
complicated because of the huge amount of data that are collected. The interviews 
they were transcribed, in order to be easier to analyse them and according to Bryman 
(2008) transcription has the merit of “…keeping intact the interviewee’s words”. The 
process of transcribing the interviews help the author to improve the understanding 
of the answers by repeatedly listening to and reading the interviews.  
For better data management, a software tool was used named Atlas.ti, designed for 
qualitative analysis needs. The collected data was inserted in the software not only in 
text documents but also as audio format. With the use of the software, the process 
becomes faster and the data are well organized. Atlas.ti gives the opportunity to 
arrange and manage the material in creative, yet systematic way. 
As a first step of the analysis the material should be defined. The interviewees should 
be addressed, how and why this sample was chosen and how the interviews 
conducted, all these have been analysed in the previous section. Prior to the analysis, 
the theoretical background and the research question must be examined carefully and 
also define the intention of the research. The literature review (see chapter 2) as well 
and the research question (see chapter 1) have been defined.  
The categories were established in a deductive way, including theoretical aspects in 
the development of the categories and determining them in advance, the model 
==PAGE:36==
 
30
presented in figure 4, set the basis for the categories. For instance, the component of 
social wellbeing has the subdimension of quality connections, that compromised one 
category. Nonetheless, some categories were developed in an inductive way and they 
were guided through data. For example, organizational guidance emerged as a new 
category. Another key point according to Silverman (2004), is that when creating 
categories there is always the risk of missing important data that doesn’t match with 
the categories. With this is mind, the researcher paid attention not to overlook 
information. 
The data were coded and organized into categories and sub-categories using the 
model of overall wellbeing. To ensure a logical and valid analysis the categories were 
backed by examples. For the differentiation of the categories, coding rules were 
developed. Consequently, the author listened and read the transcript to underline the 
first relevant information that seemed to fit the first category. With consideration to 
the defined categories, the text was structured using a naming code.  
The next step was to interpret the data by identifying any recurring themes, and to 
shed light into any similarities and differences in the data. The last phase of the 
analysis included data verification, this process involves a process of checking the 
validity of understanding by re-examining transcripts and codes, allowing the 
researcher to verify or modify hypotheses that have already been reached (Bryman, 
2008).

5. Discussion 
 
This section will present an interpretation of the findings obtained; it will explain why 
the findings are relevant to the research and relate the findings to other research 
performed.  The findings of this study are based on the interpretation and analysis of 
data collected through the process of structured interviews of eight employees who 
are employed in a Danish organization, Radiometer. 
 
5.1 Subjective wellbeing 
 
The interviewees’ answers regarding satisfaction through social media, indicated that 
there are differences among employees. All participants reported that social media 
have a positive impact on job satisfaction, but impact varied among respondents. As 
the findings of this study highlight, the effects of the use of social media on the 
employees’ wellbeing showed a) feelings of satisfaction, b) job appraisal, and c) 
positive experiences thus, there is a good reason to use these platforms. For some 
participants, work satisfaction remained the same while for others increased due to 
encouraging comments received by their managers or colleagues. Also, they get 
satisfaction when appraisals are posted on channel visible in the whole organization, 
which stimulates feelings of self-esteem. 
Job satisfaction is one of the key elements of subjective wellbeing and half of the 
participants totally agree that they can get great level of satisfaction through social 
media. It is clear from the findings that employees seek appraisal and complimentary 
comments for their job performance in face-to-face meetings with colleagues or their 
manager because they see it as more valuable. Thus, they get greater satisfaction than 
when it is given through social media. 
The findings concur with literature where job satisfaction for employees, comes from 
using social media where they can be appraised or receive positive comments. Recent 
studies propose that organizations using social media in workplace tend to report 
higher satisfaction with their employees (Cetinkaya & Rashid, 2018) and that social 
media can promote satisfaction (Charoensukmongkol, 2015).  
It is clear from the findings that impact of social media use on employees’ 
organizational commitment is significant. Participants argued that social media 
contributes to the level of commitment they feel to the organization. When they 
receive news, they are eager to post their opinion or share their thoughts on social 
media and company encourages them to do so. Important components of wellbeing, 
normative commitment, which represents personally identifying with the 
organization’s goals and values and affective commitment, which means feeling part 
of the organizational family, both have been identified in the findings of this study.  
==PAGE:58==
 
52
Most participants achieved commitment and felt connected to the organization with 
the use of social media. For some employees belonging to smaller groups, for example 
their team or department, makes them feel more connected to the organization, thus 
part of the organizational family. Affective commitment that is identified here, can 
according to Fisher (2014) touch elements of eudaimonic and/or social wellbeing in 
terms of identifying with the important goals of a group. Participants indicated that 
using social media and being committed to the organization reinforced their roles as 
ambassadors of the values of their organization. When employees take their 
organization as their “own” and feel a sense of belonging which touches the aspect of 
social wellbeing, feeling of belonging to a team or a community (Men et al., 2020).  
The findings parallel results of previous studies that have presented how employees’ 
commitment to organizations is associated with the use of social media. Indeed, social 
media serves as a channel for organizations to share information and show that they 
care for employees, thus the more information, news, policies that they demonstrate 
on social media, employees are more likely to value the organization (Men et al., 
2020). As resulted from the interviews, employees seek to receive information from 
the organization regarding values and strategies so employees are more likely to find 
commonality with organization, hence being more committed (Men et al., 2020).  
The outbreak of COVID-19 had also a positive effect on employees’ commitment to 
the organization. People faced an unusual situation; working from home on their own 
every day, therefore every single communication and meeting had to be done with 
the help of social media, and for some of the employees it was the only option. They 
were forced to use these platforms more as they didn’t have the chance to meet in 
person and that was the trigger to reach higher levels of commitment to the 
organization during COVID-19 as participants underlined in the interviews.  
As mentioned in the findings above, employees experience positive effects but also 
there is a negative influence using social media. This is not a surprise, as in the 
literature these technologies have been associated with negative effects on 
employees (Heymann et al., 2020). The majority of employees is not experiencing 
negative feelings like stress. Particularly, only one interviewee mentioned that 
sometimes has signs of stress regarding a specific social media, but that relate to her 
age, as is in the third age and the oldest participant of the study.  
The role of age regarding technostress has been explicitly investigated even though 
results are contradictory (Tams, Thatcher, & Grover, 2018). Some studies have shown 
that older people tend to experience higher level of stress than younger people (K. 
Wang, Shu, & Tu, 2008). Nevertheless, this study does not encourage age 
discrimination to employees. Thus, managers should be aware of these results and try 
to invest on trainings related to social media platforms. In this regard, managerial 
guidance can leverage the strengths of older employees in order to improve the 
==PAGE:59==
 
53
wellbeing and performance of the employee, consequently, increase organizational 
productivity. 
Another important finding of this study is that in general employees don’t feel stressed 
with the use of social media. One participant interestingly mentions that he is not 
annoyed by social media, but from the fact that through social media he might receive 
a huge amount of information that leads to information overload. This is a negative 
effect on employees as it is difficult to find the golden ratio of information they 
receive, and this potentially can lead to stress. That is confirmed by another study, 
which showed that information overload influences negatively employees, thus there 
is negative effect also on employees’ wellbeing (Heymann et al., 2020). 
Another factor emerging from the study findings was that some participants feel 
distracted from their job or tasks when they receive notifications from social media 
and that concurs with the study of Bucher (2013), that social media present countless 
opportunities for distraction and interruption and can influence damagingly the 
employees. Although investigating the “Big Five Personality Traits” of psychological 
trait theory (John & Srivastava, 1999) is out of the scope of this study, from the findings 
it is obvious that some interviewees responded differently to distraction or stress 
depending on their personality traits. In the interviews, participants characterized 
themselves as organised, structured and methodical which are traits of 
“conscientiousness”; one dimensions of the big five traits (Akhtar, Boustani, Tsivrikos, 
& Chamorro-Premuzic, 2015).  
The negative effect of interruption is moderated when employees appear to be more 
structured and organised. It is worth mentioning that four out of eight participants set 
their own rules regarding the time they spent on social media every day or the 
frequency that they access social media. Some employees also choose to turn off 
every notification of social media in order to stay focused. So, employee personality 
traits could be a factor affecting wellbeing as came up from the findings, and that traits 
play a role on the impact of social media on employees (Heymann et al., 2020).  
Positive effect of the use of social media has been identified in the findings. This is 
aligned with findings of another study which confirms that by using collaborative 
media tools, members of an organization can communicate frequently and easily 
(Thomas & Akdere, 2013).  
 
5.2 Eudaimonic wellbeing 
 
All participants reported that they find meaning in their jobs, that social media is a 
means to execute many of their tasks, and absence of social media in workplace would 
make job difficult. Employees have self-esteem on their work role and they think that 
==PAGE:60==
 
54
social media contribute positively. A consistent theme throughout the interviews 
relates to work engagement as a component of eudaimonic wellbeing, that employees 
feel when they use social media. What was surprising from analysing the data 
obtained was that one of the participants acknowledged that she would feel 
redundant without social media and her job is related to them. Her role is being a 
team leader and facilitating workshops across different departments or different 
teams in the world to ensure alignment of goals, and that cannot be achieved without 
the help of social media. This highlights the different degrees of personal impact which 
were experienced by some employees as a result of using social media.  
While engagement was felt by almost all, its consequences manifested differently 
between participants – for example one participant mentioned that it’s not the social 
media who keeps him engaged but is the tasks or the work itself. In this case, social 
media always have a supportive role as most of the tasks cannot be completed without 
them, thus there should be a good fit between social media and employees’ tasks and 
needs. The “Task-Technology Fit Model” asserts that social media technology should 
be a good fit with the tasks it supports and subsequently affect positively employees’ 
performance (El Said, 2015). In this study, it has not been investigated if social media 
are a good fit for the employees’ needs but it can constitute a suggestion for future 
research. 
Recent research has shown the positive role of internal social media in driving 
employee engagement (Men et al., 2020; Oksa, Kaakinen, Savela, Ellonen, & Oksanen, 
2020). The findings confirm this, as employees that participated reported that they 
feel more engaged not only to their tasks and to organization but also to their 
colleagues. Social media are -as stated by one employee- “the centre point and the 
checkpoint and the plan and everything is social media right now”. So, we can 
associate this positive influence of social media on engagement as positive effect also 
on employees’ wellbeing. 
The findings presented the relation between the use of social media with an important 
element of eudaimonic wellbeing; thriving at work. According to the findings there is 
no doubt that the use of social media in the workplace contributes positively to the 
process of learning, developing and making progress. Learning workplace it is defined 
as “the process used by individuals when engaged in training programs, education and 
development courses or some type of experiential learning activity for the purpose of 
acquiring the competence necessary to meet current and future work requirements” 
(Jacobs & Park, 2009) and employees seem to experience this process of learning 
within the organization as they can have online trainings or virtual classes.  
Many participants reported that they have numerous opportunities to develop 
themselves through online educational material that is stored in different channels. In 
addition, all trainings are now online because of the COVID-19 situation, so this gives 
==PAGE:61==
 
55
the opportunity to employees to continue their learning even if they are working from 
home as trainings are provided on social media and everyone can have access. Such a 
flexible and learn centre approach can be beneficial for both the employees but for 
the organization as well (Thomas & Akdere, 2013).  
One thing that derived from the interviews is that even though material for learning 
and development exists, a main issue for some employees is the difficulty to access it. 
One employee reported that everything can be found online within minutes after its 
completion or after a presentation, but the problem is for people to know where to 
look for this information. This has a negative effect on employee’s wellbeing as they 
spent time to look up for training material and fell disappointed if they don’t find it. 
Another employee was also concerned about the coordination of the material 
provided to the employees as it challenging to organise it and also to distribute it in a 
systematic way. Thus, sometimes employees are prevented from learning even if they 
are eager to learn because they simply can’t reach this information. This can be 
considered as a negative side effect on wellbeing when they are prohibited from 
learning towards self-actualization. So, it of high need the implementation of a 
knowledge management system to ensure that the knowledge can be captured and 
shared, as this serves also the purposes of social media (Thomas & Akdere, 2013).  
 
5.3 Social wellbeing 
 
Important findings were identified related to social wellbeing of employees. 
Employees seek interaction with their peers and try to build quality connections. Most 
of the time employees have the opportunity to connect with their colleagues for work 
related purposes and try to keep friendly relationships as colleagues. It is evident from 
the findings that employees are satisfied with their connections especially during 
COVID-19 times as they create bonds through regular meetings on social media. For 
instance, they have established a meeting called “coffee chat” in order to catch up and 
socialize.  These days that all employees are home-based try to be connected, not only 
with their team but also with other people from the organization. Now is easier to do 
it because according to one employee everyone is online all day so it’s easier to speak 
with them. On the other hand, being always online or connected may lurk the risk of 
stress, burnout or work and family life conflict (Gajendran & Harrison, 2007). 
Additionally, interviewees do not feel lonely because they are home based, on the 
contrary relationships with colleagues became stronger as everyone is more 
empathetic and people try to connect through social media. 
The sense of community in the organization or being embedded in communities within 
the organization also arose from the responses of the interviewees. Employees feel 
the sense of being part of communities while using social media, as channels represent 
==PAGE:62==
 
56
different communities employees can choose where they want to belong to. A key 
benefit of social media in the organization is that foster a sense of community and 
enhances collaboration (De Choudhury & Counts, 2013). The relationship between the 
use of social media and the feeling of community has a positive connection to 
wellbeing of employees (Oksa et al., 2020). Moreover, the sense of belonging in 
communities has increased recently due to COVID-19 pandemic as the use of social 
media is essential these days. Employees stated that they feel emotional connection 
to their organization which is also that is confirmed by Oksa (2020). 
It is interesting to note that giving and receiving support is another important factor 
of social wellbeing at the workplace. Most of the participants highlighted that the 
reach out for help very often and that can happen multiple times a day or sometimes 
during the week. They usually get responses very soon and everyone is willing to help. 
So, an important part of employees’ work is spending time on social media for giving 
and receiving support. Employees instead of addressing a question only to a specific 
person, they can post it on social media and someone that they might not even know 
can answer quickly the question. These results strengthen the view that social media 
can increase the power of group and feel social support (Kirchner & Seim, 2019). This 
is another factor affecting positively the wellbeing of employees.  
As employees give and receive support from their co-workers or their managers, they 
create good relationships and they feel more connected to each other, consequently, 
they are more motivated to use social media, to communicate and interact more 
often. Employees can easily reach other employees and discuss even if they are not 
located in the same place. Notably, one employee mentioned that during the COVID-
19 outbreak, she realized that her network was significantly enlarged, as now she is 
not limited to people in the same office or on the same floor and can interact with 
everyone, no matter where they are. The present study confirms previous findings 
from Charoensukmongkol (2014) confirming that building quality connections in the 
workplace can lead to increased use of social media.   
One significant issue has been revealed in the analysis. Working from home is a new 
organization policy for all employees and gradually they get used to it and the use of 
social media has an important role for them. They explored new way of working and 
communicating in distance as everything was on social media. Although employees 
reported many advantages of using social media while working from home the 
majority underlined the importance of meeting people in person. Remarkably, they 
state that no technology can replace any physical interaction. It may be speculated 
that this is probably because with social media you might lose some aspects of being 
in face-to-face meetings.  
Even if social media give various ways to share content like text, pictures, videos or 
giving video calls, the physical presence in the same place gives another sense of 
==PAGE:63==
 
57
connection with people. The participants are looking forward getting back to their 
offices, as they miss social interaction with other employees. Even though it has 
worked well so far, they still need to have face-to-face meetings again. Employees 
express their preference to have in person meetings instead of online, so this might 
have moderate negative affect on their wellbeing. 
As employees show preference to face-to-face meetings, it is interesting to see what 
people miss from online meetings and what satisfies them when they meet in person. 
According to the interviews of this study, employees want to receive nonverbal cues 
of other people in order to understand them better, something that can be fulfilled by 
using the camera but usually they don’t enable this function because they might have 
their kids in the background or they are not dressed formally. So, in face-to-face 
meetings they can see also the body language thus, have clearer understanding of the 
message transmitted.  
Moreover, when people have physical meetings there are no technology related 
problems, like losing connection or hearing the participants, so they don’t spend time 
solving these issues. Also, it’s easier to meet people that you don’t know when you 
are in person instead of introducing yourself via a screen. Lastly, as participants 
mentioned, they miss socializing for example meeting in person to have a coffee 
together before or after meetings. In contrast, when an online meeting is over, all 
participants leave the virtual room and they don’t have the opportunity to socialize.  
Interestingly, the findings suggest that employees face lack of organizational guidance 
regarding the use of social media. Some participants reported that they don’t have 
guidance about the use of platforms. This creates confusion about which platform to 
select every time. Also, employees have doubts about how each platform is used and 
they spend a lot of time learning new features of social media. Even managers 
experienced in using social media for years, feel the fear of utilizing new tools (Thomas 
& Akdere, 2013). 
The company that was investigated provides a variety of social media that have similar 
functions hence, employees need to know how to select the most appropriate social 
media in order to serve their purposes. Interviewees mentioned that they prefer 
written policies for social media and there is a lot of discussion about organizational 
policies for the use of social media in the workplace (Fusi & Zhang, 2018). Even if the 
culture of the company encourages the use of social media, employees may be 
prevented from using them if they don’t have policies (Thomas & Akdere, 2013). So, 
companies must establish organizational policies about the use of social media and 
provide relevant trainings. In this regard, the use of social media will be facilitated, 
and employees will feel sure about which one to use. As a consequence, employees’ 
wellbeing will be affected in a positive way. Though it is important that these policies 
==PAGE:64==
 
58
are not rigid in order to ensure flexibility and open communication (Kirchner & Seim, 
2019).  
 
5.4 Work life conflict 
 
In general, work/life conflict; the interference of work with life (Gajendran & Harrison, 
2007), is not currently included in the components of employee wellbeing. For this 
project it was relevant to be investigated. Social media make the role transitions of 
employees more difficult as they usually take their laptop at home or they have all the 
social media applications on their smartphone. That prohibits employees get 
disconnected from social media and in general from their professional life.  
 
In this study effects on employees’ wellbeing associated with work/life conflict has 
been investigated during the interviews by inquiring if participants feel this conflict. 
Unexpectedly, none of the participants mentioned that experiences work/life conflict 
because of social media. There are employees that they might check some of the social 
media outside of their working hours, but this is not something that they are forced 
to do, it’s more of a personal choice. A participant mentioned that he checks the 
updates on social media out of habit. It can be assumed that this habit may endanger 
potential work/life conflict if it consumes time out of his family. 
Nevertheless, it would be beneficial to acknowledge the different life domains of 
employees and policies for social media accordingly. As suggested above, these 
guidelines should be formulated to educate employees on the use of social media, 
both on the job and for personal use (van Zoonen, Verhoeven, & Vliegenthart, 2016). 
Additionally, these policies must help moderate negative consequences for 
employees and organizations address topics like the separation of professional and 
private life (Macnamara & Zerfass, 2012). 
 
5.4 Conclusion of discussion 
 
All in all, this study contributes to the growing body of knowledge of employees’ 
wellbeing in relation to the use of social media. Implementing a conceptual model of 
overall wellbeing in the workplace which covers three different aspects, arose positive 
as well as negative effects on wellbeing of employees. In particular, subdimensions of 
subjective wellbeing like job satisfaction and organizational commitment have been 
positively affected, though few employees had negative experiences that caused them 
stress. Employees appear happy, satisfied and they benefit from social media. 
Moreover, regarding eudaimonic wellbeing, employees appear highly involved in their 
tasks using social media and they find meaning in their role. Work engagement 
==PAGE:65==
 
59
increased even more during COVID-19. Positive outcomes came out from the 
experience of learning and developing in the company while utilizing social media that 
contributed positively on overall wellbeing. 
Social media use influenced also the social wellbeing of employees. Employees are 
found strongly connected through social media and they haven’t felt lonely during the 
COVID-19 period. Feeling of belonging to communities, as employees feel part of 
different teams or part of the organizational family has a positive effect on wellbeing.

6. Conclusion 
 
The objective of this research was the investigation of the effects of social media use 
in the workplace on employees’ wellbeing.  The research was mainly driven by the 
limited existing knowledge on how the use of social media in an organization affects 
the wellbeing of employees. The literature introduced the concept of wellbeing in the 
workplace covering three different components; subjective, eudaimonic and social 
wellbeing. The role of social media in the workplace was then investigated. Among the 
major deductions was the fundamental importance of social media for organizations 
as integrated part of employees’ activities. 
The thesis investigated the impact of social media on employees’ wellbeing in relation 
to the key stakeholders’ perspectives through a case study in a Danish organization; 
Radiometer, where employees make extensive use of social media. Furthermore, the 
focus was placed on employees who use social media daily and communicate inside 
and outside of the organization but also across the world. The participants were 
particularly working in the global sales department.  
The data collection was conducted through qualitative approach in the form of 
structured interviews.  The use of structured interviews allowed for in-depth insight 
into the personal experiences of the use of social media allowing the positive and 
negative effects to be discussed in an open and truthful way, which made the 
interpretation and analysis of the findings accurate. The thematic qualitative analysis 
based on the selected research model was applied for the analysis of the interviews’ 
data. One of the major findings that resulted from the analysis was the great positive 
influence of social media and its contribution on employees’ social wellbeing. In 
addition, job satisfaction, organizational commitment and work engagement are 
increased when employees use social media. Use of social media contributes to strong 
relationships among employees, enhances learning in organization and advocates for 
progress and personal development.  In order to get the best out of social media, 
policy implementation is a critical requirement. 
Finally, the findings were discussed, along with why the results were relevant to the 
research and in comparison to other available studies. The exploration of the effects 
of social media use on employees’ wellbeing through the analysis of the case study 
contributes to the workplace wellbeing literature concerning the use of social media. 
Moreover, the investigation of the positive and negative effects on employees 
provides significant intel to the organizational digital management.

6.1 Limitations of the study 
 
This section presents the limitations concerning the research and the analysis while 
undertaking this study. 
In particular, the small number of participants means that results should be 
interpreted with caution. When conducting any method of research, it is valuable to 
conduct research on a wider and more in-depth scale to allow a more thorough 
review. In this study, only one company was investigated using a qualitative 
methodology and, thus, the generalizability of the findings is limited. Therefore, the 
employees’ use of social media and their perspectives might differ from one 
organization to the other. 
In addition, the restricted timespan of this thesis led to the execution of a limited 
number of interviews with people only from one department of the organization. This 
choice was also driven by the COVID-19 outbreak that limited the interaction with 
employees from other departments. Thus, the interviewing of other associated parties 
like Human Resources department that have several communication activities and 
make extensive use of social media was not rendered possible and their perspectives 
were not taken into consideration. Furthermore, the majority of the participants hold 
managerial position to the organization, only one employee is associate and 
consequently this fact might have an impact on the study findings. Managers usually 
have many years of professional experience and they have used social media for a 
longer period, so they might have mitigated the impact on wellbeing.  
Moreover, the fact that the analysis of the findings was conducted only by one person 
might consist a limitation. Therefore, the possibility of disregarding important findings 
is higher compared to a case where the research and the analysis is performed by 
more than one person. However, the methodology of this thesis is clearly presented, 
and the analysis follows a specific model which didn’t allow misinterpretations.  
Lastly, this thesis was conducted during the outbreak of COVID-19, which may have 
an impact on the results. First of all, this situation prohibited the researcher from face-
to-face meetings and physical interaction with the participants. All the interviews 
were conducted online. In other words, non-verbal cues and facial expressions which 
are worthwhile observations for the interviewer, might have helped understand the 
message given and interpret better the data. Also, interviews were held during COVID-
19 and employees’ use of social media was significantly increased because they were 
forced to use these platforms as means of communication and collaboration tools. 
Employees may have seen social media in a different way because it was important to 
stay connected, thus the results might be seen in the picture of COVD-19.

6.2 Future research 
 
Future research proposals are based on the limitations mentioned in the previous 
section and on specific findings that were discussed. 
At first, the extension of the scope of this thesis could be a future research study. In 
this respect, investigating a larger number of organizations or expanding the number 
of interviewees or conducting interviews with employees from different departments 
could be a future research initiative. Another area that should be explored is the 
hierarchy level of the employees in the organization in relation to the impact of social 
media on wellbeing. So, it would be beneficial to investigate employees who don’t 
hold managerial positions.  
Another area for exploration could initiate from the effects of social media use on 
employees’ wellbeing in relation to their personal traits, as from the findings of this 
study it emerged that people may react differently to social media according to their 
personality traits. Thus, the relation of personality traits and positive or negative 
effects can be a new focal area. Moreover, the role of the age could be a trigger to 
investigate the differences on the impact of social media on wellbeing. 
Moreover, the future research could include the investigation of use of social media 
in organizations that already apply policies regarding use of the platforms. This will 
allow to measure the impact on wellbeing when policies are provided to employees 
and compare the differences when rules are not applied.

Appendix B 
Appendix B1: Learning Objectives 
 
According to the research question, the main objective of this thesis is to investigate 
the relation between employees’ wellbeing and social media use at the workplace.  As 
the study is conducted in Denmark, it has been decided to collaborate with a Danish 
organization. Employees’ personal experiences will be taken into consideration as 
they are members of organizations where they make extensive use of social media 
daily. 
The main learning objectives include: 
1. Collect and evaluate literature in connection with wellbeing and social media. 
2. Evaluate models of conceptualizing wellbeing and select the most appropriate for 
the problem. 
3. Recognise different aspects of wellbeing and analyse accordingly. 
4. Select appropriate research method for investigating wellbeing in relation with 
social media use. 
5. Formulate interview questions based on the selected research model. 
6. Collect data from employees and classify on the categories of the research model.  
7. Create recommendations for future study based on the limitations of the thesis. 
8. Dynamically adjust project plan in relation to current project status and obtained                                     
results.
