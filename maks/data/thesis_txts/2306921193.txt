Abstract 
The purpose of the present Master’s thesis project was to evaluate the building flexibility 
potential using different HVAC systems. This work was born as part of a real scale experiment 
called EnergyLab, in which it was created an example of smart city district, able to show the 
potential of the new technologies for the integration of renewable energies in different energy 
infrastructures. The experiment takes place in Nordhavn district, in the northeast area of 
Copenhagen; in an historical moment in which the energy produced by renewable sources is 
increasing and its fluctuating character introduces instability in the energy system and threatens 
the security of supply. The demand-side-management represents one solution to deal with the new 
challenges. It acts on the users side where higher energy flexibility is required to suite the supply 
fluctuation and make the demand and production profiles matching. 
Considering a specific building of this area, the simulations were conducted, using the 
software IDA ICE, on one apartment provided with different HVAC systems. The investigations 
were carried out on three different type of heating systems, starting from the floor heating system, 
installed physically in the building and then presenting two more alternatives, radiators system 
and fan coils. Furthermore, the ventilation system was analysed too, to see which type of 
ventilation is more suitable to achieve high flexibility potential. The last two investigations 
involved the use of more real conditions, such as a daily heating program with different heating 
periods spread during the day and a realistic weather file with variable conditions.  
Furthermore, it was assessed that the thermal flexibility potential was thoroughly influenced 
by solar radiations and outdoor temperature, but not the same high impact was seen for the 
occupants’ schedule.  The results provided some support to consider the floor heating system as 
the most flexible system, even though this was paid in higher consumption peak, which means 
higher capacity required by the building installations.

Chapter 1 Introduction 
In the last 50 years, the number of people who live in cities increased and it is forecasted that, 
by 2050, 70 % of the world population will live in cities. The increased number of inhabitants and 
the consequent needs that have to be fulfilled, will be a huge challenge for the future of the cities. 
However, nowadays, the biggest challenge to face, in the cities, is the achievement of the energy 
efficiency, paying specific attention to the environmental impact [1]. It is exactly in this moment 
that the renewable resources come on the scene, playing a leading role. The increase of their share 
will, though, require a higher synergy between different type of energy systems, such as electric, 
district heating and transport systems in order to obtain “the optimal solution for each sector as 
well as for the overall energy system” [2]. However, the higher penetration of renewable energy 
sources into the energy system increases its instability, since most of these sources are weather 
conditions dependent. Definitely, the physical integration of the sources as well as the 
management of the expected demand will become the next step of the challenge. Hereafter, the 
energy systems will be upgraded towards the so-called “smart cities” [3]. 
In this challenge, cannot be forgotten that, firstly, the increase in energy consumption is 
something to deal with, in order to achieve the energy efficiency goal. Indeed, in the last decades, 
the world’s energy consumption, as The International Energy Agency calculated, has increased 
with an alarming trend: in twenty years, between 1984 and 2004, the primary energy consumption 
has growth by 49 %, while the carbon dioxide emissions increased by 43 %. This trend is 
supposed to continue since the energy use of the emerging nations increases at a higher rate than 
the developed countries, and by 2020, their energy use will likely exceed the one of the developed 
economies [4]. 
In order to face the increment of energy consumption and CO2 emissions, the European Union 
aims to rearrange the energy sector mitigating the effect of the human impact. The target is to 
reach a reduction of greenhouse gas emissions between 80 and 90 % by 2050 compared to the 
1990’s levels. To reach this goal, the European Union designed the “Energy Roadmap 2050”, 
where the members highly support the introduction of renewable energy sources which share 
should reach 75 % of the gross final energy consumption in 2050. However, beside the 
decarbonisation objective, the security of energy supply and the competitiveness have to be 
guaranteed [5]. 
In Europe, the energy demand in the building sector accounts about 30 % of the total energy 
consumption and among this, the higher percentage, about 70 %, is due to space heating of the 
buildings, while the remaining part is split between domestic hot water use, energy for cooking 
and electricity used for appliances [6]. Therefore, the energy savings potential in the buildings 
sector can achieve important results. In particular, a EU directive about Energy Performance of 
Buildings defines the requirements of the new buildings and establishes that the ones built after 
the 2020 have to be “nearly zero energy buildings” [7]. 
The energy system requires flexibility since the energy production has always to match with 
the variable demand. However, aware of the renewable sources fluctuation with the weather 
conditions, new actions in terms of flexibility have to be designed and implemented in the grid 
[8].  In the last decades, the term “demand-side management” has been introduced in order to 
define all the possible actions that can be applied to the user side, with the aim of modifying the 
customer demand profile to match the production side. Some of the most used solutions refer to 
==PAGE:14==
 
13 
peak shaving or load shifting. In particular, in the last years the attention was focused on the 
possibility of implementing energy storage at the end-users level, so that the energy can be stored 
when there is a surplus of production and then used in a second moment [8].  
1.1 
Context and case study 
The EnergyLab Nordhavn is a real scale experiment where an example of smart city district is 
created and used as demonstration of the latest technologies in terms of sustainable energy 
integration and energy flexibility. Furthermore, the district will work as real scale laboratory 
where new technologies for future smart cities will be developed.  
This project takes place in the Nordhavn district in the northeast area of Copenhagen, where 
the synergy of the electric and heating systems will be also enforced with smart transportation, 
electricity storage and new sustainable buildings. 
The city of Copenhagen has the aim of being CO2 neutral by 2025 and the EnergyLab 
Nordhavn is part of this project. The experiment can be the first step of a new arrangement of the 
city and the technologies used, which can be later implemented in the rest of the city [9]. 
In the Nordhavn project, the work is organised in the so-called work packages (WPs). In 
particular, the WP3 Smart Energy Buildings deals with the low-energy buildings implemented in 
the project. The aim of this package is to give a clear vision of how the new buildings can have an 
active and primary role in the energy management of a smart district as well as develop new 
control solutions to ensure smart operation of the dwellings. 
The present master thesis is connected to the mentioned work package and it focuses on the 
evaluation of the flexibility potential that the building service systems can offer to the energy 
system. As it was already mentioned, in the next years the buildings have to guarantee a certain 
level of flexibility and the building service systems have an important role on how it can be 
reached. In the dwellings, different building service systems can be implemented; consequently, 
the flexibility potential can be affected in different ways.  
The aim of this study is to evaluate the flexibility potential offered by different HVAC 
systems that could be installed in a real building in the Nordhavn area. In particular, the real space 
heating system (floor heating system) will be investigated as well as two alternative heating 
systems will be considered (radiators system and fan coils system). Besides that, an investigation 
on the ventilation system will be conducted as well. 
In particular, the main parameters of the systems will be changed as well as the heating supply 
schedule, with a special focus on the advantages that it offers to the district-heating network.

Chapter 2 Literature review 
2.1 
Energy flexibility concept 
The following chapter introduces the concept of “energy flexibility in buildings” with an 
overview of the terminology involved in this field of study, as well as the technologies and 
reasons why it became important for the building sector, especially for building design.   
Nowadays, the growth of Renewable Energy Sources (RES) in the energy market brought to a 
necessary redefinition of the whole energy system. Compared to the traditional power plants, this 
new type of energy sources are spread on the territory and they are not available on demand [10]. 
Most of these technologies are highly reliant on weather conditions (e.g. sunshine and wind) and 
this lead definitely to new challenges. Besides the physical integration of these distributed 
sources, many problems are related to the lack of constant production throughout the day; 
therefore, the management of these fluctuations is one of the main issues that need to be faced in 
the next years[11].  
In order to make them working with the traditional power system, a smart grid system has to 
be introduced, designed to ensure a stable communication between sources, loads and components 
[12][8]. The “smart grid” concept is not completely defined yet, but it can be extracted from the 
definition given by different working groups. For instance, it was defined as “the electrical grid of 
the future that through information and communication technologies will manage the power 
produced in a distributed way” [10]. Therefore, the smart grid can be seen as an upgradable 
electricity network, which aims are to take advantage of the additional power flexibility of the 
currently installed power grid [12].  
One of the most important features of the smart grid is the “power flexibility”, but also this 
concept does not have a clear definition yet. Many working groups are actually occupied to 
determine it, both for mathematical and theoretical aspects. In the study presented in [12], 
flexibility was described as “the ability to cost effectively balance electricity supply and demand, 
while maintaining good services” [12] [13]. In another case, energy flexibility was defined in two 
dimensions way: one dimension representing the flexibility of the energy profile and the second 
one representing the flexibility in terms of time scheduling. Based on this, flexibility was defined 
as “the amount of energy and the duration of time, which the device’s energy profile and/or 
activation time can be changed” [11]. Besides the merely definition of power flexibility, the 
concept of flexible smart grid was defined in [8] [12] [14] as the ability of the energy grid to fulfil 
the request of unscheduled load, changes in weather with the related changes in production and 
also provision of storage  [12] [15]. 
In the studies presented in [8] [12] [14] a detailed definition of flexibility was considered, 
based on the outline of two different groups, where the considered actors in the power system 
were: 
 
Supply side flexibility (SSF): defined as the flexibility introduced by different kind of 
power plant on the supply side of the power system. 
 
Demand side flexibility (DSF): defined as the flexibility introduced on the demand side 
by the smart integration of the loads, controlled by demand side management (DSM). 
==PAGE:16==
 
15 
Considering the structure of the energy system, due to the high maintenance and operating 
costs of ensuring flexibility on the supply-side, the goal of increasing flexibility in the energy 
system is focused mainly on the demand side with demand response (DR) control and demand 
driven measures [12] [16] [17]. A high share of flexibility can be guaranteed by the buildings 
when the balance between the production side and the demand side can be met at any time by 
using storage capacity, or increasing and reducing the consumption patterns while maintaining the 
desirable indoor comfort for the occupants [12] [18].  
The increased share of RES in the energy system introduced instability on the supply side, as 
well as a certain level of unpredictability. In order to handle this, new kind of relations between 
suppliers and consumers are recommended and an important role in the power network 
management is played by both demand side management (DSM) and demand response (DR).  
As already mentioned, the supply side management is not the ideal solution to support the 
new structure of the energy system, due to the high costs. Since the late 1980s, Mr. Gellings [19] 
coined the term “demand side management” to indicate the planning, implementation and 
monitoring of the activities, which have the aim of “influence the consumers’ energy 
consumption, while achieving the desired changes in the consumers’ load shape” [19]. Many 
utility programs were related to the DSM such as: load management, new uses, strategic 
conservation, electrification, costumer generation and adjustments in market share [19].  In the 
study presented in [12] [20], demand side management was defined as the actions and solutions, 
which have to be done in order to adapt the energy consumption to the energy production. In the 
following paragraphs all the different programs and results are shown and discussed.  
DSM can be divided into five main elements that compose the overall planning framework, 
which are: set objectives, identify alternatives, evaluate and select program, implement program 
and monitor program [19]. 
In the “set objectives” step the goal has to be set, so that it is possible to define in which way 
the demand shape has to be changed [19]. 
The options for changing the demand shape are mainly represented by the following 
alternatives [19]: 
 
Peak shaving (or clipping): is one of the most common methods of load management and 
it consists in the reduction of the system peak loads.  
 
Valley filling: it is based on building off-peak loads. It is desirable when the long-run 
incremental cost is less than the average price of energy. 
 
Load shifting: it is obtained by shifting the load from on-peak to off-peak periods and it 
can be done by introducing storage technologies. 
 
Strategic conservation: it is obtained with the reduction of the consumers demand; it is 
usually achieved with an awareness program on the possible energy and costs savings. 
 
Strategic load growth: it is the load-shape change that refers to a general increase in sales 
beyond the valley filling described previously.  
 
Flexible load shape: it is a concept related to electric system reliability, which is a 
planning constraint. The costumers are presented with options as the variations in quality 
of service that they are willing to provide in exchange for various incentives. 
==PAGE:17==
 
16 
 The second element of the DSM regards the identification of possible alternatives. It is 
important to define to which end-user the new load shape solution is addressed. Therefore, once 
this is defined, it is also possible to choose which is the best technology to reach the goal. The 
third step involves the evaluation and selection of the program. The strong point of the DSM is 
the relationship between costumers and supplier. Therefore, the program has to be evaluated in 
order to be advantageous for both sides. The fourth element of the DSM is the implementation of 
the program. Before taking place in reality, the program has to be tested and validated through 
pilot experiments. Only if the results are positive it can be implemented in real scale in the 
everyday life. The last element is based on the program monitoring, in order to identify possible 
deviations from the expected performance and thereby improve the existing and planned DSM 
program [19].  
In the last two decades, RES technologies increased their share among the energy sources and 
the buildings sector has been also influenced by this change. Many households installed 
renewable sources, such that DSM programs could be implemented.  Every single dwelling can 
ensure a certain amount of flexibility, but usually they are aggregated in groups in order to be 
more consistent in the energy market. Besides the electricity consumption, households have a 
significant consumption of thermal energy, which can be stored in different ways. DSM program 
can be implemented in the building sector as management of HVAC system mainly when thermal 
storage capacity can be used [8]. 
When considering flexibility provided by the consumers in buildings, for all the cases 
(residential, industrial and non-residential) the DSM strategies can be classified as incentive based 
or price based [12]. For incentive based is meant all the programs that modify the overall power 
consumption through regulation based services. The prices based are the ones that are regulated 
by the final users’ desire to reduce cost of electricity. The structure of the Danish energy market 
and its correlation between energy price and amount of renewables production ensures that 
flexible consumers can benefit from lower electricity prices [11].  
As it was mentioned in the previous paragraphs, energy flexibility is a relatively recent 
concept and many people are working to give a unique definition. In order to evaluate the 
flexibility, the load at which it is applied has to be defined. The loads are mainly defined as non-
shiftable, shiftable and controllable. A deeper classification was made in [21], where the loads 
were split in:  
 
Shiftable load units, where the total load must be met but it can be moved which can be 
further slit in shiftable profile and shiftable volume. 
 
Curtailable load units: the loads that can be reduced without being replaced, where two 
categories can be recognised; the reducible load units that can be reduced without being 
disconnected and disconnectable load units that can be disconnected. 
 
 Inflexible load units: where the load has to be met under any circumstance. 
The terminology used in [11] was different and gave a description for the loads in terms of 
costs: 
 
Fully-flexible: are those devices representing repeated patterns, which allow high 
flexibility of the operations without compromising the comfort wanted by the user. High 
benefits for the users while the costs are kept low. 
==PAGE:18==
 
17 
 
Semi-flexible: for those devices that can be shifted in time, but depends on the users 
willing to move the load in another moment. The benefits and the costs are comparable. 
 
Non-flexible: for those appliances that cannot be shifted or which bring a high 
dissatisfaction of the users in case of changes. These include low benefits for the users 
and high costs. 
Among all the terms used to define the concepts in the energy flexibility field, sometimes 
there could be misunderstandings about the differences between demand side management and 
demand response. The first one is seen as the whole field of studies that focus on the possible 
solutions to modify the costumer load in order to adapt it to the production side. The second one 
is defined as the change of the user consumption pattern in response to a change in the price or 
other solutions [10] [22]. The DR can be considered as the set of tools used to introduce flexibility 
on the demand side [10]. 
In another study [23], a different definition of flexibility was given where it was explained as 
the “ability of a system to deviate from the plan”, where also the cost of this deviation has to be 
considered. Energy flexibility in the building sector can be introduced by the domestic appliances 
adopted at the consumers’ level, such as dishwashers, washing machines and tumble driers that 
can run when it is convenient [23].  Besides that, the possibility of storing thermal energy, with 
active or passive thermal energy storage technologies (TES), allows the building to implement 
load shifting programs, in order to implement energy flexibility for both heating and cooling 
operations [23]. In particular, HVAC systems and heat pumps can be controlled based on the 
renewables production. This is an encouraging possibility towards the dynamic relation between 
the heating and the electricity demand.  
In residential dwellings, the energy consumption is mainly characterised by three categories of 
devices [10]: 
 
HVAC systems 
 
Lighting 
 
Plug-loads 
These three categories have different DR potential and they can guarantee different level of 
flexibility. The first category has the highest potential in terms of implementation of DR 
programs. The second one has the lowest potential given that is related to the users behaviour but 
without the possibility of load shifting. The flexibility of the third category depends mainly by the 
users’ willingness to use the device [10]. In order to take advantage of the flexibility potential 
guaranteed by the different devices a higher amount of information and communications has to be 
implemented in order to control the devices in a smart way. In fact, awareness of the consumers 
regarding the energy consumption, monitoring of indoor climate conditions and weather forecast, 
smart control of local production and storage and electricity prices can guarantee a better use on 
the energy, achieving energy saving and money saving  [10].  
The implementation of DSM in buildings uses power saving technologies, electricity tariffs, 
incentives as well as government policies in order to influence the load demand. Therefore, if the 
demand is reduced, it is not required to increase the generation capacity or reinforce the 
transmission and distribution network. The main aim of the DSM is to reduce the demand during 
the peak hours and redistribute it during off-peak hours [24]. Moreover, a smart control of the 
==PAGE:19==
 
18 
loads can lead to a cost minimization in buildings as well as reduction and stabilisation of the 
energy produced in the grid [25]. 
2.1.1 
Flexibility indicators in the literature 
Besides the definition of energy flexibility, it is necessary to quantify it, in order to assess the 
amount of flexibility offered by a considered system. Therefore, many research groups tried to 
define a flexibility indicator. 
In the study presented in [26], a definition of flexibility was given in terms of time, amount 
and both time and amount. Besides that, a new concept was presented, the flex-offer, which was 
used to describe the flexibility in terms of operating time and energy amounts of the 
devices/users. They introduced eight possible flexibility measures that can be applied to the flex-
offer definition: time, energy, product, vector, time-series, assignments, absolute area-based and 
relative area-based. These measures can be used for comparing the flexibility of individual or sets 
of flex-offers, but they do not have the desired characteristics and they can be used only in 
specific circumstances. Therefore, it was not found a unique indicator that can be valid under 
every circumstance [26]. 
A relevant case study regarding the flexibility indicators was presented in [27], where the 
flexibility was defined as a percentage. As already mentioned, energy flexibility can be used to 
shift the load consumption from peak hours to off-peak hours. During the day, the electricity price 
fluctuates and presents high or low peaks. In the mentioned study [27], a heat pump system was 
tested and the average electricity price of one day was calculated. In the same way, the minimum 
and the maximum price were considered. Based on these numbers, the flexibility of the system 
was defined as: 
𝐹𝑙𝑒𝑥𝑖𝑏𝑖𝑙𝑖𝑡𝑦= 𝑝𝑒𝑙,max  − 𝑝𝑒𝑙,𝑎𝑣𝑔
𝑝𝑒𝑙,max   − 𝑝𝑒𝑙,𝑚𝑖𝑛
 
𝑝𝑒𝑙,max   is the maximum price of the electricity; 
𝑝𝑒𝑙,min  is the minimum price of the electricity; 
𝑝𝑒𝑙,avg  is the average price of the electricity. 
Thus, in relation to the daily energy consumption and its price, the flexibility indicator 
resulted to be equal to one when the electricity was consumed at the lowest price applied [27]. 
In the second part of the study, the flexibility was defined as the volume of shifted load and in 
terms of procurement cost avoided. Considering the procurement costs, it was defined as:

2.2 
Thermal energy storage technologies 
The main reason for increasing the use of thermal energy storage (TES) technologies is due to 
the increased penetration of renewable energy sources in the energy market. Since most of these 
energy sources are highly dependent on the weather conditions, the introduction of TES is one of 
the solutions to achieve energy flexibility. The TES technologies are used to match the demand 
side with the production side when the RES are not able to fulfil the demand. In the opposite way, 
they can be charged when there is production surplus from those sources. Energy can be stored 
and kept for later utilisation, so that the energy redistribution is ensured [14]. TES technologies 
increase the use of base load generating equipment and reduce the reliance on peaking units [28]. 
The thermal storage implemented in the HVAC systems, from a costumer point of view 
ensures the same thermal comfort of a system that does not use it. However, the benefits are 
related to the costs point of view due to the lower electricity bills and a possible lower capital 
investment cost, since a smaller plant can be installed. From the utility point of view, the 
efficiency of a possible existing plant can be increased, without the need to install new capacity.  
DSM programs are implemented when TES technologies are considered, in order to produce 
the desired changes in the utilities’ demand shape. The DSM is implemented in terms of new 
solution of energy efficient end-use devices, control systems for the load shaping, control systems 
for the user devices and communication systems between users and suppliers [28]. 
The basic idea of the TES technologies is to store the thermal energy by cooling, heating, 
melting, solidifying or vaporizing a material. There are different types of TES depending on 
which process is used. The sensible heat storage is obtained when the material’s temperature rises 
or falls until the desired temperature is reached and then it is kept for later use. On the other hand, 
the latent thermal storage uses the property of the materials called “phase changing”. The last 
category is represented by the thermochemical heat storage that is obtained when the heat is 
stored by means of a reversible chemical reaction, which is characterised by energy demanding in 
one direction and energy yielding in the opposite one  [28].  
The TES technologies can be divided into two main categories: active and passive solutions. 
The passive thermal storage is characterised by charging and discharging processes that are driven 
by the temperature difference between the storage and the surrounding. On the other hand, in the 
active storage systems the two processes are characterised by the utilisation of pumps or fans. 
However, it is possible to have a combination of both active and passive systems, where the 
charging process is active and the discharging is passive or vice versa [29]. 
One of the main aims of the TES technologies is to implement load shifting. They can be used 
in the electric load management when the demand is moved from peak periods to off-peak 
periods. In fact, during peak-off hours the TES can be charged, in order to be exploited during a 
period of peak demand, so that the customer’s demand profile becomes flatter [28].

20 
2.2.1 
Sensible heat TES 
In the sensible heat TES technologies, the energy is stored by changing the temperature of the 
storage mean. The amount of the heat stored in the device is proportional to the density, the 
specific heat, volume and temperature variation of the storage material. The performance is 
mainly influenced by the density and the specific heat of the material used for the storage. 
Different materials can be used for storage, for instance: water, rock beds, bricks and soil. 
2.2.1.1 
Water tank 
The water storage tank is one of the prevalent TES technologies used either in residential or 
commercial buildings, for heating and air-conditioning applications [28]. In particular, thermally 
stratified tanks are characterised by separated volumes of water at different temperatures and they 
are designed to reduce the mixing between these volumes. Because of the different water density 
caused by the temperature gradient, the cold water stays on the bottom of the tank and the hot 
water on the top  [30]. The intermediate region is called thermocline. The stratification is 
characterised by many factors: shape of the tank, location and geometry of inlets and outlets as 
well as the temperature and flow rates during the charging and discharging processes. Many 
different strategies were tested in order to implement the stratification and many parameters were 
used to explain the stratification degree [28]. Thermal insulation is used to reduce heat loss. They 
are widely used in the DHW system because they can help to supply the large energy required 
during short periods (e.g. shower) allowing peak shaving [29]. The following figure (Figure 1) 
represents possible designs of the water tanks. 
 
Figure 1 - Representation of different water tank designs 
In residential buildings the most common material used for storing is water, but other 
materials can be used as storage mean in the tank (e.g. molten and salts). Moreover, in order to 
improve the storage efficiency, phase change materials (PCM) can be introduced in the tank to 
==PAGE:22==
 
21 
increase the stratification of the water. This solution allows the implementation of smaller tanks in 
systems where a small temperature difference is given, such as solar panel systems [28]. 
2.2.1.2 
Building structure TES 
An interesting solution for thermal energy storage in building is the building structure itself. 
This kind of heat storage can be used for both existing and new buildings. The most common 
construction materials are ceramic bricks or concrete. The concrete can be used for passive or 
active storage since it can be integrated with air, water or electric distribution systems. For 
instance, the floor heating system is an active storage system, where the floor structure acts as low 
temperature radiating surface and at the same time, it works as heat storage medium. The 
concrete, due to the relatively high thermal conductivity requires thick layer of material in order 
to guarantee good thermal storage properties [31]. 
2.2.1.3 
Rock and water/rock TES 
Rock is also a cheap and wide available material. It has a lower volumetric thermal capacity if 
compared with water but it allows using temperatures above 100 °C. It can be used in some 
domestic application either for air conditioning system or for domestic hot water coupled with 
solar collectors. Another type of storage possibility is the use of large amount of water 
represented by underground reservoirs or solar ponds. However, this solution is not widely 
diffused because not applicable to single-family buildings [28]. 
2.2.2 
Latent heat TES 
In this type of TES, the energy storage process is characterised by the phase change of the 
medium. In fact, the materials used in the process are known as phase change materials (PCMs). 
Given that the latent heat is much higher than the sensible heat, it is possible to reduce the volume 
needed to store the same amount of energy; moreover, the temperature is constant during the 
phase changing. The PCMs can be solid-solid, solid-liquid or liquid-gas. The most suitable PCMs 
are the solid-liquid ones. They can be further classified in organic compounds, inorganic 
compounds and eutectics. These materials can be used both for cooling (cold thermal energy 
storage) or heating  [28]. 
However, during the undertaken project some boundaries were set in order to delimitate the 
dimension of the research method and the PCMs were not considered as storage solution. 
2.2.3 
Thermochemical energy storage 
Another way to store energy is the thermochemical energy storage (TCM). It consists of a 
reversible chemical reaction, which is used to store heat. The heat source is used to supply an 
endothermic chemical reaction. In fact, during the charging process of TCM storages, the heat is 
added to split a compound into the two components, which then are stored separately [29]. 
Afterwards, when the heat is required, the two components are put together, so that the reverse 
reaction can be obtained. Since it is a exothermic reaction, the heat can be used to fulfil the 
demand. This solution ensures a high storage energy density as well as long storage duration. An 
example of chemical energy storage is represented by the chemical heat pump storage, which 
operates discontinuously using an adsorption heat pump with storage function. It can be used for 
heating, cooling and air-conditioning [28].

2.3 
Implementation of TES technologies in a real context 
TES technologies can be used for space heating and domestic hot water systems, where the 
heat can be produced electrically. Indeed, the electric load can be managed so that the demand can 
be shifted from peak hours to off-peak hours. Focusing on the space heating (SH) system, heat 
storage can be applied to both new and existing dwellings and it can be integrated with water or 
air distribution systems. Among the storage technologies presented, the water tank is one of the 
most used solutions. Besides that, also the building mass resulted to be an interesting storage 
solution that does not have to be underestimated. It can be used as an active or passive storage 
mean. For the DSM, the most interesting solutions are the active ones as the floor heating. In fact, 
the floor represents a large low temperature surface and the concrete act as thermal storage mean, 
which is heated by water pipes or electric cables right below the surface  [28]. 
The following paragraphs present the review of articles where the HVAC systems were 
investigated in order to implement DSM strategy in the building sector. Most of the recent works 
considered the implementation of heat pumps in dwellings as source for the heating system. This 
allows the possibility of coupling the heating demand with the electricity market. The energy 
flexibility ensured by the dwellings could be used to move the heating demand to off-peak 
periods. However, the heating system can be connected to the district heating network where the 
flexibility offered by the buildings is more interesting for the supply side, since the thermal 
storage can be used to distribute better the demand throughout the day and achieve a flatter 
demand profile. 
The first example was described in the article proposed in [32], where they considered a 
detached house located in the Northern Ireland (UK), which represents a case with a high surface 
to volume ratio; therefore, higher losses were expected compared to other type of dwellings. The 
building was equipped with a heating system with an air to water heat pump (AWHP) and two 
different emission systems were considered: radiators and floor heating. In this system, low 
temperature radiators were considered, supplied with hot water between 45 °C and 55 °C, while 
for the floor heating system the water was supplied at a maximum of 40 °C. 
The study compared the two heat emission systems when a DSM program was applied. In 
fact, in order to lower the peak demand consumption and consequently flatter the demand profile, 
the heat pump was switched-off during the peak period, between 16:00 and 19:00. The aim of the 
study was to compare the two systems in terms of indoor comfort and consumption cost when a 
so-called “time of use tariff” was applied. The systems were tested both with and without the use 
of a water tank as thermal storage [32].  
The test of the two systems gave some interesting results. The energy consumption was lower 
when the DSM was applied to the floor heating system without the implementation of the TES 
technology, while it was slightly higher when it was considered, due to the fact that the heat pump 
had to top up the tank. Regarding the indoor comfort, the TES showed a better control of the 
temperature. In case of the water radiators system, the TES was able to ensure a better indoor 
comfort compared to the case without any thermal storage, but less significantly than the floor 
heating case. The energy consumption was, again, higher with the TES, because the heat pump 
had to top up the storage. The authors assessed that the heat pumps had a good role in the DSM 
programs without affecting the indoor comfort. Whereas, the TES is necessary when smart 
strategy controls are involved, mainly with distribution systems with low thermal inertia. The 
==PAGE:24==
 
23 
TES technology was useful to guarantee a better control of the indoor comfort but with a small 
increase of energy consumption. This can be considered an acceptable result when renewable 
sources are used and consequently smart tariffs are applied to the consumers. Comparing the two 
distribution systems, the floor heating system ensures better control of the thermal comfort 
compared to radiators, even though it requires higher energy consumption [32]. 
Another case study was reported in [33], where a single-family house was simulated with 
different buffer storages, control strategies and heat pump capacities. The aim of the study was to 
implement DSM strategies applied to the heat pump in order to evaluate the possibility of load 
shifting. The model was composed by an air-to-water heat pump where it was also considered an 
electrical back-up heater and water storage, connected to a floor heating distribution system. The 
study based the DSM strategy on increasing the use of the power produced by the RES 
technology and therefore move the consumption when this power is available.  
As results of the simulated model, it was found a dependence on the buffer storage size: the 
smaller the storage, the higher the frequency of the heat pump operation, due to the lower amount 
of heat stored before. From the results it was possible to assess that the heat pump coupled with a 
storage device was also a good combination to reach peak shaving and load shifting, thanks to a 
smart control strategy. However, the load shifting increases the total electricity consumption up to 
19 %, mainly due to the higher thermal losses when a bigger thermal storage was considered [33]. 
The last case study presented in [27] was about a new residential building with a total heated 
volume of 457 m3, divided into four zones: living zone, staircase, sleeping zone and bathroom. 
Also in this case, the building was equipped with an air-to-water heat pump connected to two 
different distribution systems represented by floor heating system and radiators system. The 
control of the system was based on the simulation of three rule-based controls (RBCs) and two 
optimal predictive controls (OPCs). The RBCs solutions involved either continuous or 
intermittent heating strategies, with indoor temperature based control, adopting different solutions 
for each of the three cases. The first two scenarios were implemented without any thermal storage, 
while the third one included a TES solution with night charging, chosen on the base of its lower 
price. In the same way, the OPCs strategies used either continuous or intermittent heating, but 
they operated with a smart grid based control, considering also tariffs.  For the RBC strategies, it 
was found that radiators needed more electricity since they require higher temperatures. Where 
considered, the water tank allowed some energy savings if coupled with the radiators (7 %). In 
case of floor heating with intermittent heating, the electricity saving was about 2% compared to 
the continuous solution. The operation costs considered were influenced by the difference 
between night and day tariffs. The smart grid based control drives to a higher energy consumption 
(up to 20%). An important result was that almost the 80% of the electricity consumption could be 
shifted to off-peak periods [27].

As it was defined at the beginning, the goal of the study is to investigate the flexibility 
potential offered by residential buildings and how it can be used in the energy management of a 
smart district. The case study building in located in the Nordhavn district, in the northern side of 
Copenhagen. In particular, the analysis was focused on the impact that HVAC systems 
implemented in a residential building can have on the flexibility potential of the building. 
After the goal definition, it was necessary to investigate the actual trends in the energy sector, 
with a specific focus on the energy flexibility in buildings. From that, it was possible to direct the 
study in a specific way, considering which HVAC systems are the most common in the residential 
dwellings and how they can be used to improve the flexibility in buildings, not only in terms of 
different design but also with a dedicated management of the heating schedule. 
From the literature review, it was possible to define on which building services systems it was 
necessary to focus the most. Since the space heating demand is responsible of almost 70 % of the 
heating consumption in the residential dwellings [6], particular attention was paid to the systems 
that fulfil the requirements. Three different heating distribution systems where selected: 
 
Hydronic floor heating system 
 
Radiators system 
 
Fan coils system 
The choice of the mentioned systems was based on the will of investigating how different 
kinds of distribution systems can have an impact on the building flexibility. Besides that, the 
ventilation system was considered and investigated. 
Since the case study was based on a real building located in an area where a district heating 
network supplies the heating demand, it was decided to study mainly the thermal flexibility that 
the building can provide to the heating operator. In fact, since the thermal market is not regulated 
yet and it does not have dynamic prices as the electricity market, the flexibility provided by the 
building is more interesting for the heating operator than the users. In other cases, electrical 
devices (e.g. heat pumps) can provide the heating demand; therefore, the electrical flexibility 
would be relevant to be studied, considering the dynamic prices of electricity. 
As already mentioned, the case study was based on an existing building. Since the aim is to 
study the impact of the HVAC systems on the flexibility, it was decided to reduce the scale of the

3.2 
Flexibility indicators 
From the literature review, it was possible to understand that in order to quantify the 
flexibility potential, an indicator has to be defined. As it was shown in the study presented in

electrical flexibility. In the following case study, the aim was to assess the flexibility potential 
offered by a specific building when the use of different HVAC systems is considered. Since the 
aim of these devices is to guarantee an acceptable indoor climate for the occupants, the main 
indicator used was specifically based on the assessment of the thermal comfort in the apartment.  
The requirements provided by the European standard DS/EN 15251 [35] for a building in 
category II were followed and in particular, the operative temperature was used as indicator to 
evaluate the thermal comfort. The indicator chosen gives a representation of the flexibility in 
terms of the numbers of hours that the operative temperature is kept above the lowest limit 
required by the standard, once the heating was switched off in the apartment. For this reason, a

Chapter 5 Preliminary investigation 
Once the IDA ICE model was built, it was possible to proceed with the investigations. Firstly, 
it was decided to evaluate the influence of some model parameters, which do not belong to the 
HVAC system, i.e. weather conditions and internal gains. The aim of this preliminary 
investigation was to define a basic model, on which the variation of the building energy system 
parameters would be applied.  
The investigations were conducted for three different temperature set points (20 °C, 21 °C and 
22 °C). The heating program was based on a continuous control for each selected set point from 
the 1st of January until the 15th of January at 6 a.m. After that, the heating supply was cut-off in 
order to see for how many hours would the indoor temperature of the rooms be kept above the 
lowest limit allowed (20 °C). This time-based indicator was used to assess the flexibility potential, 
through the analysis of each parameter’s impact. Since it was seen that the flexibility indicator 
was showing the same patterns at the different set point temperatures in the rooms (but different 
number in terms of absolute values), in order to make the explanation more clear, it was decided 
to plot in the following graphs only the flexibility indicator obtained with the heating set point at 
21 °C. 
For this investigation and throughout this report, two rooms were selected as reference. The 
first one called Bedroom1 (indicated as bedroom in the remaining part of the report) in the IDA 
ICE model and the second one called Living room. Those two rooms were chosen since they 
represent two different “end-use” rooms. The bedroom is mainly occupied during night, while the 
living room is mainly occupied during the day. These rooms resulted to be the ones with the 
highest (living room) and the lowest (bedroom) temperatures during the simulation period.

Chapter 7 Floor heating investigation

However, the high increment of flexibility observed in the bedroom, when the slab was increased 
from 12 cm to 18 cm, is not due to the benefits obtained from the slab thickness, but it is related 
to the sensitivity of the indicator to the solar gains. In fact, due to the low thermal capacity of the 
concrete, a significant increment of the thickness does not lead to a relevant increase of the 
flexibility indicator.  
Furthermore, the influence of the solar gains can be also seen in the different increase of the 
flexibility indicator in the two rooms. Since the living room is oriented to the south, the solar 
gains help to keep the surface temperature of the floor more stable throughout the day, so that the 
influence of the heating period is less significant compared to the bedroom. This is seen in a lower 
increase of the indicator when the slab was increased. 
On the other, it can be seen that the depth of the pipes under the surface slightly influences the 
indicator. For the 6 cm slab, the numbers of hours above 20 °C are not remarkably affected by the 
depth of the pipes. They are almost steady around 45 h in the bedroom and 89 h in the living 
room. On the other hand, when the slab thickness is increased to 12 cm and 18 cm, it can be 
noticed from the that the depth slightly influences the flexibility indicator. For pipes depths within 
8 cm, the indicator slowly increases, whereas for depths from 10 cm to 12 cm it slowly decreases, 
becoming almost stable for deeper solutions. In fact, when the pipes are placed close to the 
surface, the thinner concrete layer activated releases the heat accumulated faster and it results in a 
faster temperature drop in the room. For pipes placed around 8 cm the thicker layer of concrete 
activated can store more energy and deliver it slower to the zone, which is reflected in a slower 
temperature drop. For deeper pipes, it was noticed in the IDA ICE results that due to the higher 
energy stored in the slab, the temperature of the slab close to the pipes was higher. This reduced 
the heat flux from the pipes to the slab and then to the rooms. The lower energy supplied to the 
slab resulted a slightly faster temperature drop in the rooms. Moreover, the heat losses to the 
lower layer are higher and this further reduced the heat flux to the rooms.

Discussion and sub-conclusions 
From the changes in the design parameters of the floor heating system, it was noticed that the 
increase in thickness of the concrete layer lead to a higher flexibility factor, thanks to the higher 
thermal mass and the possibility of higher heat storage. However, it was also assessed that when 
the layer thickness was increased two and three times the flexibility potential was not 
substantially increased due to the low thermal capacity of the concrete. Therefore, an excessive 
layer of concrete would lead to new structural challenges and higher construction costs without a 
relevant increment of flexibility.  
The implementation of the cut-off program, in order to allow energy storage and then achieve 
the flexibility, requires a higher consumption peak compared to the continuous control of the 
heating system. The maximum increase factor is 6.55 times. On the other hand, the flexibility 
reached with the implementation of the daily heating program lead to a consumption peak 3 times 
higher than the continuous control. The higher peak consumption has to be considered when the 
building is designed since appropriate equipment need to be installed in order to face higher 
peaks. This has also to be considered on the district heating network side, which could require a 
network upgrade. Considering the primary energy consumption for a one-month simulation, the 
introduction of the daily heating program lead to increase the energy consumption up to 3.8 % 
compared to the continuous control. 
It can be concluded  that in order to achieve higher flexibility the slab thickness can be 
increased. However, a high increase of the thickness would not lead to a proportional increase of 
the flexibility. From the investigation, it was found that 12 cm of slab could be considered the

a better understanding of the optimal solution.

Chapter 10   Ventilation investigation 
The last HVAC system considered in the study is the ventilation system, which purpose is to 
guarantee the comfort for the occupants in terms of indoor air quality. The reference system 
provides a constant airflow to the apartment at 17 °C. In order to keep the temperature stable, the 
system is equipped with a heat recovery unit that uses the outlet flow to heat up the inlet one. 
However, when the unit is not sufficient, a water-heating coil connected to the district heating 
network supplies the necessary heat to the air. 
The aim of this analysis is to evaluate whether a change of the inlet air temperature can affect 
the flexibility potential. However, the change of the air temperature can lead to a different use of 
the space heating system. Therefore, it is interesting to see how the temperature of the inlet air 
affects the power consumption delivered by the district heating network. A simulation was run to 
show the influence of the supplied air temperature on the space heating consumption. The results 
are shown in Appendix B.   
The model simulated was based on the reference building. The space heating system 
considered was the floor heating system. The rooms’ set points applied during the heating period 
were based on the previous investigation (Chapter 7Floor heating investigation), equal to 22 °C 
for the cut-off program and 20.5 °C for the daily heating program. It is important to mention that 
the ventilation was not switched-off when the heating from the district heating network was 
removed, since it is important to guarantee an optimal indoor air quality at each moment of the 
day. This solution could affect the temperature of the supplied air, since the water-heating coil 
could not supply heat to the inlet air. However, the ventilation system was equipped with a heat 
recovery, which would help to keep constant the supply temperature, even if the heating heat 
supply was switched-off.  
The investigation considered the implementation of two different types of ventilation systems. 
The first one was the CAV system implemented in the real building, while the second one was a 
VAV (variable air volume) system. The VAV solution based the inlet and outlet airflows on the 
rooms’ occupancy. This resulted in an unbalanced ventilation system. This solution was chosen 
on purpose (even though it goes against the standards) in order to test if it is possible to use an 
unbalanced solution to reduce the energy consumption.  
As it was mentioned, when the heat supply from the district heating network was switched off,  
only the heat recovery could help to keep the supply air temperature constant. The configuration 
with the water-heating coil and the heat recovery was simulated for both CAV and VAV system. 
Besides that, it was decided to simulate also two cases in which an electric heater was used to 
keep constant the supply air temperature when the heat supply from the district heating network is 
removed.

10.2 Discussion and sub-conclusions 
From the ventilation analysis, it was seen that the impact of the supplied air temperature could 
affect the flexibility potential of the apartment, mainly when the temperature is kept constant 
throughout the day. However, if an electric heater is needed to ensure the heat supply to the inlet 
air when the district heating is disconnected, the increase of primary energy consumption can 
result considerably high and increase up to 70 % compared to the consumption obtained with the 
continuous control of the reference system. Therefore, it can be said that, increasing the flexibility 
potential by keeping the supply air temperature constant with an electric heater causes a too high 
increase of the primary energy consumption. 
It was assessed that the consumption peak of the heating system is lower if higher air 
temperature is supplied and kept constant throughout the day. However, the total primary energy 
consumption for ventilation and space heating has to be evaluated in order to see if it is 
convenient to keep a higher supply air temperature. In fact, as it was already said, the use of an 
electric heater lead to higher primary energy consumption.  
In order to reduce the increment of primary energy, it can be considered to decouple the space 
heating system from the ventilation system. In this way, even though the heat supply from the 
district heating network in removed for the space heating system, it still can be used by the 
ventilation system. 
The implementation of an unbalanced VAV system is not feasible if an extra heater is not 
considered besides the heat recovery and the water-heating coil connected to the district heating. 
This is necessary to cover the heat demand when the air temperature is lower than the set point 
and the district heating network does not supply the heat demand. Further investigation can be 
also conducted about the possibility of implementing a balanced VAV system. 
==PAGE:85==
 
84 
It can be concluded that for the present study case the ideal solution is represented by the 
CAV system, since when the daily heating program was implemented, it resulted the ventilation 
system with the lowest increase in terms of primary energy consumption (3.41 %) compared to 
the continuous control of the heating system. The solution with supply air temperature at 20 °C 
ensures slightly more flexibility than the other cases, although the increase was about few hours. 
Regarding the consumption peaks during the daily heating program, the changes in the supply air 
temperature of CAV system do not affect the peaks, which were found to be around 3 times 
higher than the continuous control. On the other hand, for the cut-off program, the higher supply 
temperature leads to a slightly higher peak, which was found around 6.5 times higher than the 
continuous control of the heating system.

Chapter 11  New daily heating program investigation 
The daily heating program applied in the previous investigations can result as an example of 
the DSM management, where the entire heating load is moved when the heat demand is lower. 
However, the complete shut-off of the heating supply from the district heating network is an 
extreme scenario. The previous daily heating program considered the heating supply only during 
night. This solution can create dissatisfaction for the occupants that want to sleep with a lower 
temperature in the bedrooms. Based on this consideration, it was decided to investigate the 
influence of a different daily heating program in terms of thermal comfort and energy 
consumption. 
In this analysis, it was not considered a complete cut-off of the heating supply outside the 
heating period, but it was only decided to lower the rooms’ set point below the lowest limit 
required by the standard (19 °C). This solution ensures a minimum indoor comfort in cases of 
very low outdoor temperature. The heating program worked as preheating, where the set point 
was increased during the heating period, so that it was possible to enable some energy storage. 
It is important to mention that, as it was done for the previous heating program, from the 1st of 
January to the 14th of January, the heating system was controlled with the continuous control. 
During this period, the rooms’ set point was set at 20 °C. Afterwards, from the 15th of January the 
new daily program was implemented in the model. 
The new heating program considered a higher heating set point during the heating period, 20.5 
°C for the floor heating system and 21.5 °C for the radiators system. It was increased from 4:00 
a.m. to 7:00 a.m. and from 3:00 p.m. to 7:00 p.m. This solution can be interesting for the heating 
operator since it can provide the heat demand to the residential building in the early morning and 
afterwards to the offices. On the other hand, in the afternoon, the energy accumulated in the 
offices can allow some flexibility, so that the heating set point could be lowered for those 
buildings. Consequently, the heat supply can be moved to the residential buildings. From the 
point of view of the occupants in the apartments, the new heating program ensure a good indoor 
comfort when they wake up in the morning. Furthermore, when they come back after work, the 
rooms’ temperature ensures good thermal comfort. The heating set point was lowered again at 
7:00 p.m., so that it was avoided to consume during the peak hours, between 7:00 p.m. and 8:00 
p.m, as it was presented in [29]. 
The heating systems considered were chosen based on the analysis made before. The fan coils 
system was not considered in this analysis. It resulted the most difficult system to control in the 
simulation program; moreover in reality it is the least used system in residential buildings. It was 
decided to simulate the floor heating system and the radiators system. From the simulations run 
before on the floor heating system, it was decided to use the 12 cm slab with pipes placed 8 cm 
below the surface and in the deepest position, 14 cm below the surface. Furthermore, also the 
floor heating system of the reference case was simulated (6cm slab, 4 cm pipe depth).  
The same weather conditions used in the previous investigations were used also in this case, 0 
°C outdoor temperature and narrow solar gains.

11.2 Discussion and sub-conclusions 
The investigation conducted for the new heating program gave a better understanding of how 
the heat supply can be managed during the day. The results showed that the heating set point in an 
apartment can be lowered during some hours of the day. With this solution, the heat supplied by 
the space heating system is reduced during those hours. On the other hand, the heating set point 
has to be set higher during the heating period (compared to the continuous control); in this way it 
is enabled the energy storage in the building mass. Consequently, the thermal comfort for the 
occupants can be guaranteed. 
The new daily heating program was designed only as a theoretical solution based on literature 
studies and some consideration of the convenience for a hypothetical heating provider. Therefore, 
further investigations should be conducted before apply it in reality. 
The introduction of such daily heating program lead to higher consumption peaks compared to 
the ones obtained with the continuous control of the heating system. This is due to the fact that 
higher set point is defined during the heating period as well as the temperature in the room drops 
when the heat is not supplied. Therefore, in order to use the flexibility offered by the building, a 
higher consumption peak has to be faced during the heating period. 
Compared to the daily heating program applied to all the previous investigation, the new daily 
heating program showed that dividing the heat supply in more periods during the day is better in 
terms of indoor temperature. The introduction of more periods leads to a solution closer to the 
continuous control that ensures more stable temperatures in the rooms. Furthermore, the 
intermittent heating lead to the possibility of using the flexibility offered by the building. 
==PAGE:94==
 
93 
When comparing the two systems simulated, it was seen that the floor heating system ensured 
lower primary energy consumption (-8 %) due to the higher thermal mass of the system that allow 
more energy storage and consequently lower consumption peaks. 
The new daily heating program was assessed to be better than the one proposed in the 
previous investigations. Even though the heating time was increased by one hour, the primary 
energy consumption increased by 2.85 % for the radiators system, while for the floor heating 
system there was no differences.

12.2 Discussion and sub-conclusions 
The present investigation analysed the possibility of introducing a daily heating program 
when realistic weather conditions were implemented in the simulation model. In this case, the 
new daily heating program was considered.  
The results showed that in a real condition it is possible to supply the heat demand only in 
certain periods of the day, without considerably affecting the thermal comfort for the occupants. 
This is obtained using the flexibility offered by the building, which is ensured by energy 
accumulation in the building mass. It was noticed that the indoor temperature dropped slightly 
below 20 °C when the outdoor temperature was quite lower than 0 °C. However, for the entire 
year the number of hours below the limit was found to be less than 5 % of the time, as required by 
the standard DS EN 15251 (17 h for the floor heating system and 66 h for the radiators system). 
Furthermore, it was noticed that the thermal comfort could be affected when the outdoor 
temperature is stable lower than 0 °C for more than three days. In that case, the thermal losses of 
the apartment cannot be covered by the energy accumulated during the heating period. 
Also in this case the floor heating system resulted the best solution to guarantee the indoor 
comfort. The higher thermal mass can store more energy in the building and ensure lower 
fluctuations of the temperature. 
==PAGE:101==
 
100
It was observed that the daily heating program ensured temperature rather higher than the 
lowest limit when the outdoor temperature is above 0 °C. This is due to level of the set point 
during the heating period. In fact, it was designed to ensure the optimal indoor comfort in the 
apartment for an outdoor temperature at 0 °C. Therefore, as soon as the outdoor temperature rises, 
the heating demand in the apartment is reduced and less energy accumulation in the building mass 
is required. In this way a lower set point would be enough to ensure the thermal comfort for all 
the day. A more dynamic control of the heating set point could be designed based on the outdoor 
temperature. In fact, it can be lowered when it is not necessary, mainly for late February and 
March, but also October, November and the beginning of December, since the outside 
temperature is usually above 0 °C. In the case study, the radiators system resulted more affected 
since it presented a higher set point than the floor heating system. This resulted in higher primary 
energy consumption after one year. 
It can be concluded that the floor heating system showed higher flexibility potential since it 
ensured better indoor comfort and lower energy consumption than the radiators system. 
Furthermore, the increase of primary energy consumption compared to the continuous control was 
limited, +6 % after the simulation of the entire year. On the other hand, the radiators system 
increased the primary energy consumption by 24 % for the one-year simulation compared to the 
continuous control. However, it has to be considered that the floor heating system showed higher 
peak consumption than the radiators system (1.5 times higher), which requires the correct design 
of the building equipment, in order to bear the higher peak.

Chapter 13  Final discussion 
The development of this study case was based on the influence that the building service 
systems can have on the flexibility potential of the building itself. During the investigation 
procedure, many assumptions had to be made in order to simplify the comprehension of the 
parameters changed. For this reason, the model created led to results that needs to be treated 
carefully according to the specific real case. However, they represent a start-up point for further 
investigations, in which all the assumptions could be sharpen in order to evolve it in a more 
generic model.  
The first important remark that needs to be underlined regards the chosen building. As already 
mentioned, this case study was part of a relevant project that aims to analyse in a “real scale 
laboratory” the development of a smart city. In the EnergyLab project, the buildings are designed 
under the latest construction technologies and respecting the guidelines in terms of energy 
consumption and indoor comfort. This means that the results obtained from the investigations are 
referring to a new building; therefore, using different type of buildings (different age of 
construction and not refurbished) to run the same simulations would lead to different flexibility 
potentials. In particular, with the perspective of extending the smart grid concept to a larger area, 
other types of buildings need to be studied in order to evaluate the impact of the relation between 
different constructions on the flexibility potential of the new area.  
Besides the type of building, also the choice of the specific apartment had an influence on the 
simulation results. Indeed, in this case study the apartment is located on an intermediate floor, 
with the majority of glazing walls facing south. Considering another apartment on the top floor or 
located on the corner of the building, the results would have been affected by different heat losses. 
Further investigations can demonstrate the influence that such apartment location has on the 
flexibility potential.  
The main part of the investigations conducted was based on a simulation model that 
considered the weather conditions, i.e. outdoor temperature and solar radiation, constant 
throughout the period. This assumption was thought in order to limit the influence of external 
parameters not related with the HVAC systems. Definitely, different weather conditions would 
have led to different results in terms of flexibility as it was shown in the preliminary investigation.  
However, the constant temperature and solar radiation were estimate on the base of the average 
conditions during the heating season. This makes the assumption consistent and the results more 
indicative. To verify it, one more analysis was conducted at the end of the investigations, 
considering the weather conditions. Therefore, it was seen that until the outdoor temperature was 
around the constant value considered in the previous cases, the two situations were comparable. 
Once the outdoor temperature went out of this range, the results obtained demonstrate that a 
dynamic control of the heating supply was required under real conditions. Further simulation can 
be run to examine in depth the dynamic control. 
One more assumption regards the occupancy. The number of occupants and the schedule that 
regulate their presence were defined equally for each weekday and for each weekend. In reality, 
people behave in a more random way and this could influence the results. In a further study, it 
would be worth to implement a stochastic model for the occupancy, assessing the impact on the 
results.  
==PAGE:104==
 
103 
Another important remark has to be made about the occupied hours and the heating set points. 
To simplify the investigations, the occupied hours were not distinguished from the non-occupied 
hours and the minimum indoor temperature was set at 20 °C according to Category II of the 
Standard DS/EN 15251. Anyway, each occupant present a different level of acceptability and it 
could be interesting for further analysis to investigate if different set point temperatures in the 
rooms can influence the flexibility.  
Moreover, the present study case was intentionally done only considering the flexibility 
related to the heating systems, without implementing any additional thermal energy storage such 
as water tank. However, for systems like radiators or fan coils, the introduction of a water tank 
would help the energy flexibility, making those systems comparable to the floor heating, which 
has its own storage into the slabs.  
A final remark has to be done about the heating source considered during the simulations. In 
the Nordhavn area, where the studied building is located, a district heating network fulfils the 
heating demand of the buildings. However, an alternative heat source is represented by the heat 
pump, which uses electricity to supply heat. Comparing the two systems, it can be said that the 
“thermal market” does not consider a dynamic price as it is for the electricity market. Therefore, 
in the first case (district heating) the flexibility potential of a building is advantageous only for the 
heat supplier, whereas, the heat pump system can be convenient also for the costumers because 
the consumption can be concentrated in the moment in which the electricity price is low. Lastly, it 
can be very interesting to conduct further analysis about the energy costs comparing the two 
different systems.

Chapter 14  Conclusions 
The aim of the present master thesis was to carry out an investigation of how different 
parameter changes in the HVAC systems can influence the building flexibility potential. In 
particular, in order to give a better understanding of these changes an apartment was recreated and 
simulated with the simulation software IDA ICE. To assess the flexibility potential of each system 
selected, a dedicated heating program was applied, in which the cut-off of the heat supply was 
considered. Besides that, the daily heating program was evaluated, with the aim of providing 
information to a hypothetical heating operator about how to exploit the building flexibility. 
The preliminary investigation confirmed that the outdoor temperature can highly influence the 
flexibility potential of the building, whereas, the occupants’ schedule affect less the value of the 
indicator. Moreover, the solar radiation, especially in case of large solar gains, can considerably 
influence the thermal comfort in the apartment and, therefore, the flexibility indicator used. 
From the evaluation of the floor heating system, it came out that the flexibility indicator 
measured for different concrete layers, does not enhance significantly with the increase of the slab 
thickness. Therefore, 12 cm slab was considered as the most convenient solution in terms of 
flexibility. Being the improvement in flexibility from 12 to 18 cm slab low, it is not justifiable the 
choice of a thicker concrete layer without enough benefits, since a thicker layer would also 
increase the construction costs. Regarding the depth of the pipes in the concrete slab, the results 
showed that the flexibility indicator is not highly influenced by the depth of the pipes. However, it 
was seen in correspondence of 8 cm depth a slight increase of the indicator pattern. 
At the end of the investigations conducted for the three different heating systems, applying the 
cut-off program, it can be concluded that the floor heating system offers the highest flexibility 
potential to the building. In fact, compared to the radiators system, the flexibility was up to 90 % 
higher. In the same way, in comparison with the fan coils system, the percentage was found even 
higher.  
However, the aim of obtaining the maximum flexibility potential with the cut-off program led 
to a change in the consumption peak. The floor heating system presented a peak 6 times higher 
than in the continuous control case. This means that, despite of the good result found in terms of 
flexibility, the capacity required by this system is considerably high. Different situation was found 
for the other two systems: the radiators showed a consumption peak about 3 times higher than the 
continuous control and the fan coils system almost 4 times higher. Furthermore, comparing the 
consumption peak of the floor heating system with the other two systems, it was seen that it 
showed a higher peak (around 2 times higher). Therefore, the floor heating system is presented as 
the most flexible system for this type of building, but at the same time, to achieve this flexibility, 
the building’s systems need to be designed in order to bear the highest peak requested. This 
should also be considered on the district heating network side. 
Using the building flexibility, to implement the daily heating program, it was seen that the 
floor heating system showed a consumption peak 3 times higher than in the continuous control 
case. In the same way, radiators and fan coils increased their consumption peak respectively of 3 
and 4 times, compared to the continuous control. The power consumption peaks of the three 
systems became comparable, reaching a value around 2000 W. Therefore, from the point of view 
of a daily evaluation, the floor heating system resulted to be the ideal solutions, since it showed 
==PAGE:106==
 
105 
less temperature fluctuations throughout the day. Furthermore, focusing on the primary energy 
consumption of the three systems, it came out that the daily heating program lead to an increase of 
the primary energy consumption for all the three cases between 4 % and 12 %. However, the 
radiators and the fan coils systems increase the primary energy consumption respectively of 7 % 
and 10% compared to the floor heating system, which resulted the ideal solution also in terms of 
primary energy consumption when the building flexibility is used.  
The last HVAC system investigated was the ventilation system. It was seen that, for the cut-
off program, increasing the inlet air temperature and keeping it constant throughout the day, 
makes the flexibility indicator growing up to 90 %. Besides that, the consumption peak is not very 
influenced by the inlet air temperature and the type of ventilation system. However, switching to 
the daily heating program, it was noticed that the use of an electric heater, able to ensure constant 
supply air temperature, lead to higher primary energy consumption, around 70 %, even though the 
use of the space heating system is reduced. Therefore, the implementation of an electric heater in 
the ventilation system in order to supply constant air temperature resulted to be a non-ideal 
solution. It can be concluded that supplying a higher air temperature would lead to a higher 
flexibility potential; however, the impact in terms of energy consumption has to be evaluated. 
The last two analysis were conducted in order to investigate the impact of changing the daily 
heating program and the weather condition. The results obtained from the introduction of the new 
daily heating program, based on two different heating period, showed that even though the heat 
supply period was increased by 16 %, the primary energy consumption was almost not affected, 
when compared to the previous heating program applied to the floor heating system. Whereas, the 
radiators system, with the new daily heating program, increased their primary consumption by 3 
%. Therefore, it can be concluded that it could be convenient to spread the heating period in 
different moment of the day, instead of concentrating all the hours in one heating cycle. The 
implementation of the realistic weather condition confirmed that the new daily heating program 
could be applied to exploit the flexibility offered by the building and at the same time is able to 
guarantee the thermal comfort in the apartment. However, the power consumption peak was 
noticed to be higher than the standard weather conditions. This means that the building equipment 
have to be designed in order to bear the higher peak. Compared to the continuous control case, the 
primary energy consumption resulted almost 6 % higher for the floor heating system and 24 % 
higher for the radiators system. In conclusion, also in the realistic weather conditions, the floor 
heating system demonstrated to be the most flexible system even though it maintains the 
consumption peak higher.

Appendix A.3 Internal gains 
In the case study were considered three possible solutions regarding the number of occupants 
(2, 3 and 4 occupants) and two solutions for the schedules (working and retired). The working 
solution considered all the people at home during night (23:00 to 7:00), part of the occupancy 
during morning (7:00 to 9:00) and evening (17:00 to 23:00) and nobody at home during the day 
(9:00 to 17:00) for the weekdays. During the weekend it was set that part of the people were in the 
apartment during the morning, day (with lower occupancy) and evening, while the full occupancy 
was set at home during night. For the retired solution, the weekend pattern used in the working 
schedule was applied also for the weekdays. 
Regarding the heat gains from the occupants, it was set 0.7 MET for people sleeping, 1 MET 
for seated people and 1.2 MET for standing people. 
For the heat gains from the equipment, it was set 1 W/m2 in the bathrooms, while it was set a 
maximum load of 5 W/m2 for the living room, which was lowered for the hours with lower 
occupancy. In the bathrooms no equipment was considered.

Appendix B Ventilation system investigation 
Appendix B.1 Supply air temperature 
The following graphs (Figure B. 1 and Figure B. 2) show the heating consumption pattern of 
the floor heating system when the air supplied in the apartment by the CAV system changes 
temperature. It can be seen how the peak consumption is 40 % higher when the supply 
temperature pass from 20 °C to 17 °C. Therefore, a higher supply air temperature reduces the use 
of the space heating system. However, an evaluation of the primary energy consumption provided 
by the district heating for both ventilation and space heating shows that for the supply air 
temperature at  17 °C the primary energy consumption resulted 2,88 kWh/m2 , which is 2.7 % 
lower than what resulted for the air supplied at 20 °C ( 2,96 kWh/m2). The use of higher 
temperature for the inlet air reduces the space heating consumption, but the increases the power 
consumption of ventilation system. This is reflected in almost the same primary energy 
consumption.

Appendix B.2 CAV and VAV inputs 
The first ventilation system simulated during the investigation was the reference system. 
Afterwards, it was considered to simulate an unbalanced VAV system, in which the control of the 
exhausted air or the inlet air supply was based on the CO2 in the rooms. The maximum airflow 
(exhaust/return) in a room was set for a CO2 concentration of 900 ppm, which is the maximum 
concentration allowed for a building in category II. The minimum ventilation rate was set at 0.35 
l/s/m2 for a CO2 concentration of 450 ppm. This guaranteed always a minimum supply or return 
airflow in the rooms. It was decided to use the CO2 to control the ventilation because it is a clear 
indicator of the occupants’ presence in the rooms. In this way, the airflow rate is adapted to the 
occupancy hours. The choice implementing a flow rate lower than the standard, was done with the 
perspective of further changes in the regulations which could ask for larger limits about the indoor 
environmental in order to achieve a possible reduction in terms of energy consumption.
