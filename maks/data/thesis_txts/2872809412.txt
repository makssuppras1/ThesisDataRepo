Abstract
The growing environmental burden of petroleum based plastics in
single use biomedical applications has created a demand for sus­
tainable alternatives. This thesis investigates the development of
a biodegradable polymer cartridge suitable for alcohol based med­
ical fluids, focusing on blends of silk fibroin (SF) and polyvinyl al­
cohol (PVA) chemically crosslinked using glutaraldehyde (GA). The
aim was to develop materials that balance mechanical integrity with
solvent resistance in ethanol rich environments. SF and PVA were
blended in various ratios and with optional incorporation of Laponite
to enhance stiffness. The formulations were characterized through
compression testing, Fourier­transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR),
ethanol absorption and degradation tests. Results showed that a
50/50 SF/PVA blend provided an optimal balance of strength, tough­
ness, and processability, while Laponite improved stiffness but re­
duced toughness. GA confirmed successful chemical crosslinking
via Schiff base and acetal formation. Ethanol immersion tests re­
vealed substantial weight gain due to solvent absorption, particu­
larly in PVA­rich formulations. Post­drying weights indicated mini­
mal degradation. While the absorption was significant, future appli­
cation of biodegradable coatings may help mitigate this limitation.
Environmental sustainability was assessed through literature based
life cycle and carbon footprint comparisons, showing that despite
high production emissions, SF based blends offer significant ad­
vantages in biodegradability and circularity. The findings support
the feasibility of SF/PVA blends as a potentially viable alternative
to conventional plastics for biomedical packaging, with need for fur­
ther optimization in mechanical performance, ethanol stability and
manufacturing scalability.

Introduction
The widespread use of synthetic polymers, primarily derived from
fossil fuels, has contributed to severe environmental challenges, these
challenges include long­term plastic waste accumulation, micro­plastic
accumulation and greenhouse gas emissions. Synthetic polymers
such as polypropylene (PP), polyethylene (PE), and polyethylene
terephthalate (PET) are integral to modern manufacturing, including
in the healthcare sector, where they are commonly used in pack­
aging applications and dispensing systems for alcohol­based fluids
[1]. These polymers are often single­use due to strict regulatory re­
quirements for medical devices. However, these materials are not
biodegradable and are primarily produced from non­renewable re­
sources, contributing to a linear consumption model that lacks sus­
tainability.
According to the International Energy Agency, approximately 12%
of global oil demand is linked to petrochemical production, a figure
expected to rise due to growing plastic consumption [2]. Alternative
estimates indicate that 4–6% of global oil and gas consumption is
directly allocated to the production of synthetic plastics. [3]. This
highlights the urgent need for alternative materials with a lower en­
vironmental footprint.
1.1
Bio­Based and Biodegradable Polymers
Recent advancements in polymer science have led to the creation of
bio­based polymers, which are produced from renewable biological
feedstock such as sugarcane or corn. Common examples include
bio­PE, bio­PP, and bio­PET. These materials retain the structural
and functional properties of their fossil­based counterparts but offer
improved environmental profiles through reduced carbon emissions
and greater circularity [4]. However, although derived from biological
sources, these polymers are not inherently biodegradable and can
still contribute to long­term waste if not effectively recycled.
In contrast, biopolymers are naturally occurring macromolecules pro­
duced by living organisms. Examples include cellulose, chitosan,
and protein­based materials like gelatin, casein, and SF. These ma­

1.2
Silk Fibroin as a Sustainable Material
SF, a protein extracted from the Bombyx mori silkworm, has at­
tracted significant interest due to its combination of biodegradability,
biocompatibility, mechanical strength, and processability. Tradition­
ally used in textiles and surgical sutures, SF has been increasingly
explored in biomedical and sustainable product design. Notably, its
stability in aqueous and alcohol­rich environments, combined with
tunable degradation rates, positions it as a viable material for ap­
plications such as drug delivery systems and structural hydrogels
[6].
SF exhibits high potential for replacing synthetic polymers in single­
use healthcare applications, particularly in packaging solutions for
alcohol­based containers. It can be processed into coatings, hydro­
gels or stiff structures that resist premature breakdown while ensur­
ing safe biodegradation post­use. However, SF in its pure form may
suffer from brittleness and limited flexibility, posing challenges for
applications requiring mechanical resilience.

1.3
Polyvinyl Alcohol in Biomedical Applications
PVA is a synthetic, water­soluble polymer widely used in biomedi­
cal, pharmaceutical, and packaging applications due to its excellent
film­forming ability, biocompatibility, and mechanical flexibility. It is
typically produced by hydrolyzing polyvinyl acetate, and its back­
bone contains abundant hydroxyl (–OH) groups that promote hy­
drogen bonding with other polymers and crosslinkers. Although not
biodegradable in all environments, PVA is considered environmen­
tally friendly under specific composting or microbial conditions, and
its use has gained traction in the development of eco­conscious ma­
terials [7, 8].
PVA’s favorable physical properties­such as high transparency, ten­
sile strength, and resistance to oils and greases­make it particularly
useful in applications involving controlled fluid diffusion. However, in
alcohol­rich environments, such as ethanol­based hand sanitizers,
PVA may swell or dissolve unless chemically crosslinked. By com­
bining PVA with SF and applying appropriate crosslinking strategies,
a hybrid material can be produced that balances flexibility with sol­
vent resistance, making it suitable for single­use biomedical pack­
aging systems.
1.4
State of the Art
In current commercial hand sanitizer packaging, the most commonly
used polymers are high­density polyethylene (HDPE), PET, and PP.
HDPE is favored for its chemical resistance and durability, while
PET offers transparency and rigidity, especially for consumer­facing
products. PP is often used in pump mechanisms and closures due
to its stiffness and processability [1]. While these materials perform
well in alcohol­containing formulations, they are petroleum­derived
and not biodegradable, contributing to long­term plastic waste. The
widespread use of such materials underscores the need for sus­
tainable alternatives that can match their mechanical and chemical
resistance without relying on fossil resources.
This context motivates the development of biodegradable polymer
blends, such as SF and PVA, that can provide the structural integrity
and alcohol resistance required for sanitizing cartridges, while align­
ing with circular economy and environmental sustainability goals.

1.5
Research Objective
This thesis explores the development of chemically crosslinked SF/PVA
blends for use in alcohol­resistant biomedical packaging. By blend­
ing SF with PVA, a flexible, water­soluble synthetic polymer, and
introducing GA as a crosslinking agent, the resulting composite ma­
terial aims to combine the biodegradability and stability of SF with
the flexibility and processability of PVA. The goal is to create a sus­
tainable, biodegradable alternative to conventional plastic cartridges
used for packaging of alcohol based fluids, capable of withstanding
ethanol­rich environments without compromising structural integrity.

2
Background and Theory
This chapter provides an overview of the scientific principles and ma­
terial systems that form the foundation for this thesis. The focus is on
the polymers used in the study, SF and PVA, and the role of GA as
a chemical crosslinker. Understanding the structural, chemical, and
physical interactions of these components is essential to designing
biodegradable polymer systems with tailored properties for alcohol­
resistant biomedical applications.
Additionally, this chapter intro­
duces the concept of crosslinking and discusses its impact on ma­
terial performance. The final sections cover the relevant chemical
structures and material properties that influence the design, charac­
terization, and mechanical behavior of the studied polymer blends,
and briefly introduce the key characterization techniques used to
evaluate them.
2.1
Polymer Systems and Components
2.1.1
Silk Fibroin
SF is a natural protein polymer obtained from the Bombyx mori silk­
worm. It has been extensively studied for its exceptional combina­
tion of mechanical strength, biocompatibility, biodegradability, and
ease of processing, making it a highly promising material for biomed­
ical and sustainable engineering applications [9, 10].
The robust mechanical behavior of SF is largely attributed to its hi­
erarchical structure, which consists of β­sheet crystalline domains
dispersed within an amorphous matrix [9]. This structural arrange­
ment allows SF to achieve high tensile strength while maintaining a
degree of flexibility. Two main structural conformations exist within
SF: Silk I, a metastable form composed primarily of random coils
and α­helices, and Silk II, a thermodynamically stable form domi­
nated by antiparallel β­sheets [10, 11, 12]. The Silk II configuration
is particularly important due to its contribution to enhanced thermal
stability, water resistance, and mechanical performance.
SF can be fabricated into a variety of formats including films, fibers,
hydrogels, sponges, and bulk solids [9]. Advanced processing meth­
ods such as thermal molding, casting, electrospinning, and self­
assembly allow precise control over the final material structure and

==PAGE:18==
properties. These processing parameters influence the formation of
β­sheet content, and thus directly affect the degradation rate and
stiffness of the final product [9].
Due to its amphiphilic nature, SF exhibits favorable interactions with
both hydrophilic and hydrophobic compounds, contributing to its use
in drug delivery, tissue scaffolds, and barrier coatings.
Its long­
standing clinical use as a suture material underlines its excellent
immune tolerance and biocompatibility [10].
Recent studies have shown that SF maintains its structural integrity
in ethanol­rich environments due to ethanol­induced crystallization,
which increases β­sheet content and enhances material stability [13,
6]. This makes SF particularly well­suited for use in alcohol­based
formulations, such as hand sanitizer cartridges, where sustained
durability and solvent resistance are required.
Incorporating additives such as Laponite, a synthetic nanosilicate,
has been shown to further enhance the performance of silk­based
materials. Laponite increases β­sheet content and mechanical strength
through electrostatic interactions and acts as a structural reinforce­
ment agent [14]. However, optimizing the concentration of Laponite
is critical, as excessive amounts may lead to embrittlement due to
aggregation effects.
Overall, the tunability, processability, and environmental resilience of
SF make it an ideal material for sustainable applications, particularly
in biomedical packaging systems designed to interface with alcohol­
based fluids.
2.1.2
Polyvinyl Alcohol (PVA)
PVA is a synthetic polymer widely used in biomedical and packaging
applications due to its film­forming capability, high tensile strength,
biocompatibility, and water solubility [7, 8]. It is produced by the
hydrolysis of polyvinyl acetate, since vinyl alcohol itself is unstable
in monomeric form.
PVA is highly hydrophilic due to the large number of hydroxyl (­OH)
groups on its backbone, enabling it to readily absorb water and swell
in aqueous environments [15]. Its lack of bulky side chains further
enhances compatibility with water and polar solvents, contributing
to its versatility as a hydrogel­forming material.

In alcohol­based systems, such as ethanol­containing cartridges,
the permeability and stability of PVA become critical. While PVA is
generally stable in water, its interaction with alcohols like ethanol can
lead to swelling or even partial dissolution. To address this, physical
or chemical crosslinking strategies are often employed to reinforce
the polymer structure and limit solvent­induced degradation [16].
Importantly, PVA is biodegradable under specific environmental con­
ditions. Exposure to microbial activity in composting environments
can lead to its degradation, making it a viable option for sustainable
product design [17]. When combined with biopolymers such as SF,
PVA can contribute to the formation of advanced hybrid materials
with enhanced mechanical properties and improved solvent resis­
tance.
2.1.3
Glutaraldehyde
GA is a small bi­functional aldehyde widely used as a chemical crosslinker
in biomedical materials. Its reactivity arises from its two terminal
aldehyde groups, which can form covalent bonds with functional
groups, most notably primary amines (–NH2) and hydroxyls (–OH)
on biopolymers such as proteins and synthetic polymers. In aque­
ous acidic conditions, GA reacts readily with these groups to create
imine (Schiff base) or acetal linkages (C=O), leading to a chemically
crosslinked network with enhanced mechanical and chemical stabil­
ity [16, 18, 19].
Due to its strong crosslinking ability, GA is commonly used to mod­
ify the structural properties of protein­based biomaterials like SF,
as well as synthetic polymers like PVA. Crosslinking with GA has
been shown to reduce polymer solubility, increase thermal and sol­
vent resistance, and improve mechanical integrity, key features for
alcohol­resistant and load­bearing biomedical applications [20, 21].
Despite its utility, the use of GA must be carefully controlled, as ex­
cess unreacted GA can be cytotoxic. Therefore, post­crosslinking
purification steps and dosage optimization are essential to balance
performance and biocompatibility [21].
2.1.4
Silk Fibroin­Polyvinyl Alcohol Polymer Blends
SF, a biopolymer valued for its strength, biodegradability, and bio­
compatibility, is limited in fluid packaging applications due to its in­
herent brittleness and poor flexibility. To address these shortcom­

==PAGE:20==
ings, SF is often blended with PVA, a flexible, water­soluble syn­
thetic polymer known for its excellent film­forming properties. The
resulting SF­PVA blend films show significantly improved mechan­
ical properties, including increased tensile strength and elongation
at break, which effectively reduce the brittleness of pure SF. These
improvements are crucial for applications involving alcohol­based
fluids, where structural integrity and resistance to cracking or defor­
mation are essential. Moreover, the blends exhibit enhanced ther­
mal stability and a more uniform microstructure, attributed to strong
intermolecular interactions between the SF and PVA phases. This
makes SF­PVA blends particularly promising for the development of
biodegradable and sustainable containers designed to hold alcohol­
based formulations [22].
2.2
Crosslinking
Crosslinking enhances polymer properties by forming a three­dimensional
network by forming new bonds between polymer chains, this is the
most common approach to modifying polymeric materials. Three
types of crosslinking can be classified: chemical, physical, and en­
zymatic [23, 24]. This can be viewed in Figure 2.1.
2.2.1
Chemical crosslinking
Covalent bonding is the primary mechanism involved in forming per­
manent interactions in chemically crosslinked materials. These ma­
terials can be produced through various approaches, including the
chemical reaction of complementary functional groups, polymer­polymer
crosslinking, high­energy irradiation, and enzyme­mediated crosslink­
ing [23]. Common chemical crosslinkers include GA, which is often
used to stabilize SF­based or PVA­based hydrogels.
2.2.2
Physical crosslinking
Physical crosslinking involves the formation of polymer networks
through non­covalent interactions. It primarily occurs through mech­
anisms such as ionic interactions, protein associations, and physical
forces like hydrogen bonding, which facilitate the interpenetration
of multiple polymers to create macromolecular structures. These
physical interactions enable crosslinking without forming covalent
bonds, distinguishing this method from chemically crosslinked sys­
tems [25]. Physical crosslinking has gained traction in recent years
due to avoiding toxic crosslinking agents. This is particularly rele­

vant in systems involving SF, PVA, or semi­interpenetrating polymer
networks [23].
2.2.3
Enzymatic crosslinking
Enzymatic crosslinking utilizes enzymes to facilitate the formation
of covalent bonds between polymer chains. This method offers high
specificity, minimal side products, and low energy demand, mak­
ing it an attractive alternative to traditional chemical crosslinking.
One of the major advantages of enzymatic crosslinking is its abil­
ity to operate under mild aqueous conditions, which is beneficial for
biomedical applications where harsh chemical treatments may com­
promise the material properties or biocompatibility. However, enzy­
matic crosslinking can be expensive, and its efficiency may be influ­
enced by substrate specificity, which can limit its application [24].

2.3
Chemical structures and interactions
Understanding polymer behavior at the molecular level requires a
basic comprehension of chemical structure, specifically, the arrange­
ment of atoms, the presence of functional groups, and the types of
interactions these groups can form. In this context, functional groups
such as hydroxyl (–OH), amine (–NH2), and carbonyl (C=O) play
central roles in intermolecular bonding, including hydrogen bonding
and covalent crosslinking. Visualizing these structures helps inter­
pret how polymers like SF and PVA interact and how crosslinkers like
GA can alter material properties. The following sections outline the
relevant chemical features of each polymer and illustrate the mecha­
nisms that govern their structural stability and crosslinking behavior.
2.3.1
Silk fibroin
SF is composed primarily of heavy and light polypeptide chains linked
by disulfide bonds. The heavy chain is rich in glycine, alanine, and
serine, forming repeated sequences that promote β­sheet crystallinity,
which is key to its mechanical performance and structural stability
[10]. These β­sheet regions are formed through hydrogen bonding
between adjacent peptide chains and provide strength, rigidity, and
resistance to environmental degradation.

2.3.3
SF­PVA Polymer Blends
Blending SF with PVA combines the advantageous properties of
both materials, including the mechanical strength and protein­based
biocompatibility of SF and the processability and hydrophilicity of
PVA. The chemical compatibility between these two polymers pri­
marily arises from their ability to form extensive hydrogen bonding
networks between functional groups on their polymer backbones [7,
8].
SF contains amide groups (–NH and C=O) from its peptide back­
bone, while PVA has abundant hydroxyl (–OH) groups along its car­
bon chain. These functional groups can interact through hydrogen
bonding, which enhances miscibility, facilitates structural reinforce­
ment, and supports the formation of semi­interpenetrating polymer
networks. These interactions do not result in covalent bonding under
mild blending conditions but significantly affect the material’s physi­
cal properties such as swelling behavior, thermal stability, and mod­
ulus [9, 18].
To further improve the structural integrity and solvent resistance of
SF­PVA blends, chemical crosslinking agents such as GA are often
introduced. GA can form covalent bonds with both protein amine
groups in SF and hydroxyl groups in PVA. Under acidic conditions,
GA reacts with these groups to form imine (Schiff base)(SF) or ac­
etal linkages(PVA), creating a covalently crosslinked polymer matrix
[20, 21]. These linkages significantly reduce chain mobility and free

beneficial in applications requiring both flexibility and resistance to
alcohol­based solutions, such as in sustainable cartridge systems
for hand sanitizers or disinfectants.

2.5.1
Compression test
Compression testing is a simple method used to characterize key
mechanical properties by applying a uniaxial load to a sample and

2.5.2
Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy
FTIR spectroscopy is a widely used analytical technique for iden­
tifying molecular structures and functional groups in both organic
and inorganic materials. It operates by measuring the absorption of
infrared radiation by the material as a function of wavelength, pro­
ducing a spectrum that represents the vibrational transitions of the
chemical bonds within the sample [32, 33].
The technique uses a mathematical process known as the Fourier
Transform to convert raw interferometric data into a usable spec­
trum. This allows for rapid, high­resolution detection of a wide range
of molecular vibrations within a single scan.
In polymer characterization, FTIR is particularly valuable for detect­
ing characteristic vibrational modes associated with functional groups
such as hydroxyl (–OH), amine (NH2), carbonyl (C=O), and ether

==PAGE:30==
(C–O–C) groups. This enables researchers to assess the chemi­
cal composition, confirm the presence of specific components, and
monitor structural changes due to processing or chemical modifica­
tion, such as crosslinking [32, 34].
FTIR spectra are typically recorded in the mid­infrared range (4000−
400 cm−1), where most molecular vibrations occur. For protein­based
polymers like SF, prominent peaks include the amide I band (around
1650 cm−1), associated with C=O stretching, and the amide II band
(around 1540 cm−1), related to N­H bending and C–N stretching. In
synthetic polymers like PVA, broad O–H stretching peaks around
3300–3500 cm−1 and C–O stretching around 1000–1200 cm−1 are
commonly observed [8, 34].
Second Derivative Processing in FTIR
To enhance the resolution of overlapping peaks and detect subtle
structural changes, FTIR data can be further processed using math­
ematical techniques such as derivative spectroscopy. In particular,
the second derivative of the FTIR spectrum is commonly used to re­
solve closely spaced or hidden vibrational bands that may not be dis­
tinguishable in the original spectrum due to band broadening, base­
line variation, or signal overlap. In complex polymer or protein­based
systems, FTIR spectra often contain overlapping peaks, baseline
drifts, or subtle bands that are difficult to distinguish in their raw form.
To enhance spectral resolution and interpret hidden features, sec­
ond derivative processing is commonly applied. Mathematically, the
first derivative of a function provides the slope, or the rate of change
of the signal. The second derivative measures the rate of change of
the slope, i.e., the curvature of the original signal, this allows nearby
or overlapping peaks to become distinguishable.

Materials and Methods
This chapter details the experimental procedures used in the de­
velopment and analysis of SF/PVA polymer blends. It outlines the
preparation methods for SF and PVA, crosslinking protocols, and the
characterization techniques employed to assess mechanical, chem­
ical, and absorption properties.
3.1
Synthesis of Silk Fibroin
SF solution was prepared from Bombyx mori cocoons using a multi­
step process: degumming, dissolution, dialysis, centrifugation, and
concentration.
3.1.1
Degumming
Silk cocoons were cut into small pieces and boiled in 0.02 M sodium
carbonate (Na2CO3) for 30 minutes to remove sericin. The fibers
were then rinsed thoroughly with Milli­Q water (MQ ) and dried overnight
at room temperature.
3.1.2
Dissolution
Dried SF was dissolved in 9.3 M lithium bromide (LiBr) at 60◦C for 4
hours, using a concentration of 22,wt% silk relative to LiBr solution.
The resulting solution was allowed to cool to room temperature.
3.1.3
Dialysis
The SF­LiBr solution was dialyzed against MQ water using SnakeSkinTM
dialysis tubing with a 3,500 Da molecular weight cut­off and a 35 mm
inner diameter. The dialysis was carried out for 48 hours with 6–8
water changes to ensure complete removal of LiBr.
3.1.4
Centrifugation
After dialysis, the SF solution was centrifuged 3 times at 9,000 rpm
for 20 minutes at 4◦C to remove aggregates and undissolved par­
ticles. The supernatant was collected and stored at 4◦C. The SF
extraction process is illustrated in Figure 3.1
3.1.5
Concentration via polyethylene glycol (PEG) Dialysis
To increase the SF concentration, the dialyzed solution was placed
in the SnakeSkinTM dialysis tubing and immersed in a 10,wt% PEG
(MW 8,000) solution for 48–72 hours. The PEG bath was prepared
by dissolving 450 g PEG in 3 L MQ water and adjusting the final
volume to 4.5 L.

Crosslinking Procedures
3.3.1
SF and PVA Crosslinking
Both SF and PVA solutions were chemically crosslinked using GA
in the presence of 1 M hydrochloric acid (HCl) as a catalyst. For
each milliliter of polymer solution, 5 µL of HCl was added to reduce
the pH and initiate the crosslinking reaction. In SF, the acidic condi­
tions promoted gelation, while in PVA, the GA reacted with hydroxyl
groups to form acetal linkages. All samples were allowed to crosslink
overnight at room temperature to ensure complete network forma­
tion.

50/50 (wt/wt) formulations. The concentration of SF was adjusted to
15 wt% by dilution with MQ water. The required volumes of 15 wt%

5
Discussion
This chapter analyzes and interprets the experimental results in the
context of the study’s objectives. Trends in mechanical performance,
chemical crosslinking, and solvent resistance are discussed, with
comparisons made between different formulations and relevant liter­
ature. Limitations and potential implications for material optimization
are also addressed.
5.1
Compression Test Results
The mechanical performance of the tested material formulations re­
vealed distinct trends in stiffness and energy absorption behavior.
The pure SF and PVA samples served as reference materials, while
blends aimed to improve their mechanical synergy through crosslink­
ing and nanoparticle reinforcement.
The SF/PVA (50/50 wt%) blend exhibited improved mechanical prop­
erties compared to either pure component. Specifically, the com­
pressive modulus increased relative to pure SF, and toughness at
both 0.1 and 0.2 strain showed a marked enhancement (Tables 4.3
and 4.4, Figure 4.1). This indicates that blending SF with PVA re­
sults in a stiffer and more energy­absorbing material, likely due to
hydrogen bonding and partial crosslinking between the polymers.
The SiPo/PVA composite, which incorporated Laponite nanoparti­
cles into the 50/50 SF/PVA matrix, showed the highest compres­
sive modulus of all tested groups, consistent with the known rein­
forcing effect of nanoclay particles. The increased stiffness likely
arises from the Laponite creating a more interconnected polymer
network. However, the toughness at 0.2 strain could not be deter­
mined for SiPo/PVA samples, as these exceeded the 300 N load
threshold imposed during testing. Although the Instron machine was
equipped with a 500 N load cell, the test software was inadvertently
configured with a 300 N limit. As a result, the samples were prema­
turely stopped, and data beyond this load were not recorded. This
limitation affected the ability to determine maximum compressive
stress and full­area toughness values, particularly for formulations
with high stiffness and load­bearing capacity. The calculated tough­
Design and Development of a Sustainable Polymer Cartridge for a Medical Device
39
==PAGE:52==
ness at 0.1 strain for SiPo/PVA remains valid, as it falls within the
measurable range.
The stress­strain profiles of the SiPo/PVA samples further suggest a
brittle­like response (Figure 4.2). Although failure was not reached
due to the 300 N load limit, the stress–strain curves display a sharp,
steep increase in stress with minimal curvature, particularly when
compared to the more ductile behavior observed in the other formu­
lations. This behavior is indicative of limited plastic deformation and
high stiffness, characteristics often associated with brittle materials.
Previous studies have shown that excessive Laponite loading can
increase brittleness by restricting polymer chain mobility and leading
to poor stress distribution [14]. These findings highlight the impor­
tance of optimizing Laponite content in composite blends to achieve
a desirable balance between stiffness and ductility.
To facilitate direct comparison of stress­strain curves, the strain data
were realigned such that strain = 0 corresponded to the point at
which compressive stress reached 0.5MPa.
This correction was
applied to mitigate differences arising from slight variations in ini­
tial sample height and loading contact during test setup. The align­
ment allowed for clearer visualization of material behavior in the
early stages of compression and more consistent calculation of me­
chanical parameters across samples. In addition, the compressive
modulus was calculated from the slope of the stress­strain curve
in the range of 0.05–0.1 strain, providing a more standardized esti­
mate of stiffness across samples and minimizing variability from the
toe region of the curve.
To more accurately characterize material behavior under large de­
formations, both engineering and true stress­strain curves were an­
alyzed. The true stress and strain calculations account for geomet­
ric changes during compression, offering a more realistic assess­
ment of material performance under densification. As expected, true
stress and modulus values were generally higher than their engi­
neering counterparts, particularly at greater strain levels. This re­
flects the increasing influence of sample deformation on the appar­
ent stress response. In materials with significant plasticity or com­
pressive densification, the true stress­strain curve provides a more
meaningful measure of mechanical performance.

The 80/20 SF/PVA blend was excluded from the final mechanical
analysis due to inconsistencies in sample performance and quality.
While initially included, the samples in this group exhibited erratic
mechanical behavior and variability not observed in other formula­
tions. These deviations were likely the result of fabrication inconsis­
tencies or inhomogeneous crosslinking, and the decision to exclude
them was made to ensure the robustness of comparative analysis.
All formulations experienced substantial volumetric shrinkage dur­
ing drying, ranging from approximately 81% to 92% (Table 4.2). The
shrinkage reflects both solvent loss and network densification during
film formation but does not appear to strongly correlate with mechan­
ical performance trends across the groups.
Taken together, the results suggest that SF/PVA­based materials of­
fer tunable mechanical performance through blend ratio adjustment.
The SiPo/PVA composite in particular demonstrated promising stiff­
ness and early­stage toughness, although its full mechanical poten­
tial could not be assessed under the current testing constraints. Fu­
ture testing with higher load thresholds and extended strain tracking
is warranted to fully characterize high­strength formulations and to
better leverage the insight provided by true stress­strain analysis.
5.2
FTIR Spectroscopy Analysis
FTIR spectroscopy was used to identify functional groups and evalu­
ate chemical interactions in the pure materials and their crosslinked
blends. Spectra of pure PVA, SF, and their crosslinked systems,
including SF/PVA 80/20, SF/PVA 50/50, and SiPo, were compared.
Pure PVA (Figure 4.3) exhibited characteristic bands at 3300cm−1
(O–H stretching), 2940 and 2910cm−1 (CH2 asymmetric and sym­
metric stretching), and 1090cm−1 (C–O stretching). While residual
acetate C=O stretching is typically observed near 1730cm−1, it was
not clearly visible in the pure PVA spectrum, likely due to a high de­
gree of hydrolysis or suppression by baseline noise.
In contrast, the SF/PVA 50/50 blend (Figure 4.5) showed a more
distinct, although very minimal, feature at 1697cm−1, consistent with
the C=O stretching vibration of residual acetate. This peak is more
distinguishable in the blend, possibly because SF does not strongly
absorb in this region, allowing minor PVA peaks to emerge more
Design and Development of a Sustainable Polymer Cartridge for a Medical Device
41
==PAGE:54==
clearly. A second derivative analysis (Figure 4.12) performed on
the SiPo/PVA sample confirmed the presence of this subtle shoul­
der, demonstrating the utility of derivative spectroscopy for identify­
ing weak or overlapping bands.
The amide I band, arising from C=O stretching in the protein back­
bone of SF, appears at 1643cm−1 in the pure SF spectrum. This
band is distinct from the residual acetate peak at 1697cm−1, even
though both originate from carbonyl groups. The ester C=O of resid­
ual acetate is more isolated and less involved in hydrogen bonding,
resulting in a higher stretching frequency, while the amide I peak
originates from peptide­linked carbonyls where hydrogen bonding
and resonance with adjacent N–H groups reduce bond stiffness and
shift the peak to a lower frequency.
In crosslinked SF samples, this amide I band shifts from 1643cm−1 to
approximately 1622cm−1, as seen in Figure 4.11. This shift is likely
attributable to the formation of Schiff base linkages (C=N), a known
reaction product of GA crosslinking with primary amines in SF. Al­
though the characteristic C=N stretching band near 1629cm−1 over­
laps heavily with amide I, a shift is visible in the zoomed­in region
and the stacked spectra (Figure 4.10), providing indirect evidence
of Schiff base formation.
Due to the proximity and partial overlap of the residual acetate and
amide I peaks in the wavenumber range, second derivative anal­
ysis was employed to enhance resolution and differentiate them.
This method accentuates inflection points, allowing adjacent or hid­
den peaks to be detected. The second derivative of the SiPo/PVA
sample (Figure 4.12) revealed clear minima at both 1697cm−1 and
1643cm−1, confirming the presence of both residual acetate and pro­
tein amide signals in the crosslinked system.
The SF/PVA 80/20 blend (Figure 4.6) presents spectral features that
closely resemble those of crosslinked SF, with only minor influence
from the PVA component. This is expected, as the SF fraction domi­
nates the formulation. The O–H and C–O stretching vibrations char­
acteristic of PVA are present but less pronounced, indicating that
the spectral signature is largely governed by SF. The stacked spec­
tra comparison (Figure 4.10) illustrates this clearly, as the 80/20
blend clusters more closely with SF and SF­GA spectra than with
42

==PAGE:55==
PVA­containing systems. This visual grouping reflects the dominant
chemical character contributed by SF in high­SF­ratio composites.
The SiPo blend (Figure 4.7) exhibited additional peaks at approxi­
mately 1030cm−1 and a subtle dip at 680cm−1, corresponding to Si–
O stretching and bending vibrations, respectively. These features
confirm the successful incorporation of Laponite into the polymer
matrix.
When interpreting FTIR data, several factors must be considered:
sample thickness and homogeneity can affect transmittance inten­
sity; overlapping peaks and hydrogen bonding may broaden or shift
characteristic bands; and baseline noise can obscure weak signals,
particularly in complex blends with multiple interacting components.
Overall, FTIR spectroscopy confirmed the preservation of character­
istic polymer functionalities, the presence of residual acetate in par­
tially hydrolyzed PVA, successful Laponite incorporation in the SiPo
blend, and likely crosslinking via Schiff base formation in GA­treated
systems. The analysis also demonstrated how blend composition
influences spectral profiles, for example, the 80/20 SF/PVA blend
aligning closely with SF­rich spectra. In challenging cases involving
spectral overlap, second derivative analysis proved especially use­
ful, enabling resolution of key features such as amide I and residual
acetate (Ester) bands.

Absorption and Degradation Test Results
The absorption data suggest that blends containing PVA, particularly
the SiPo/PVA formulation, are highly susceptible to ethanol uptake,
as expected due to PVA’s hydrophilic nature. The sharp increase
in weight in these samples likely reflects both fluid absorption and
partial loosening of the polymer network.
Despite the limitation of single measurements per time point, clear
trends were observed. All samples increased in weight over time,
indicating ethanol absorption and possible structural changes in the
material. Among the formulations, SiPo/PVA exhibited the highest
relative weight gain by day 7 (up to 291%), followed by SF/PVA
(212%), and pure SF (117%). The 80/20 SF/PVA blend showed the
lowest absorption at day 1 (75%) but reached 134% by day 7, indi­
cating a slower rate of ethanol uptake.

Interestingly, the results suggest that higher silk content reduces
ethanol absorption. Formulations with a greater proportion of SF,
such as the 80/20 SF/PVA and pure SF, consistently showed lower
uptake compared to those with higher PVA content. This may re­

compared to PVA, making it more resistant to ethanol penetration.
To further reduce ethanol absorption, particularly in blends with high
PVA content, future studies could explore the application of thin,
biodegradable coatings. Such coatings may serve as diffusion bar­

and biocompatibility of the system.
To evaluate possible degradation after ethanol immersion, the sam­
ples were dried and reweighed. As shown in Table 4.7, there was
no notable weight loss in any of the formulations after drying. All
measured weights remained effectively unchanged, with variations
well within the accuracy margin of the analytical balance used (typ­

or mass loss occurred during ethanol exposure for up to 7 days.
The lack of replicate samples limits statistical interpretation, but the
consistent weight values across time points reinforce the conclu­
sion that the materials remained chemically and physically stable in
ethanol over the test duration. The planned 14­day data point could
not be evaluated due to accidental sample loss.
However, it is important to note that surface­level degradation, such
as microcracking, erosion, or morphological changes, could occur
without a detectable change in total weight. Such changes would
not be captured by gravimetric measurements but could be revealed
through surface characterization techniques like scanning electron
microscopy (SEM). Future work should therefore include SEM anal­
ysis to visually assess potential surface degradation and further vali­
date the chemical and structural stability of the formulations in ethanol.
Finally, it should be emphasized that this test only assessed degra­
dation over a 7­day period. While the results suggest short­term
chemical stability in ethanol, long­term exposure may lead to differ­
ent outcomes. Further testing over extended durations would be
necessary to confirm stability over the full intended shelf life or use

5.4
Casting of Cartridge
The casting process showed progressive improvements in both shape
fidelity and structural integrity with each iteration. Notably, Cartridge
1, which was dried at 60°C, exhibited greater deformation and shrink­
age compared to the others dried at 40°C. This suggests that slower,
lower­temperature drying conditions may better preserve the geom­
etry of soft polymeric structures, likely due to reduced drying stress
and slower solvent evaporation.
In Cartridges 3 and 4, an additional step was introduced to improve
structural stability: a similarly shaped support (or ”swamp”) was in­
serted into the crosslinked cartridge immediately after demolding.
This helped retain the cylindrical form during drying and reduced the
risk of collapse or warping. Cartridge 5 remained partially embed­
ded in the mold during demolding, and was therefore dried directly
within the outer shell of the mold. Interestingly, this led to excel­
lent geometric retention, likely due to the mold acting as a physical
constraint during drying.
Cartridge 6 was freeze­dried instead of air­dried, resulting in a vis­
ibly larger and more uniform structure. This may be attributed to
sublimation­driven solvent removal, which avoids capillary collapse
and shrinkage typically seen in conventional drying. Freeze­drying
removes water by sublimation under vacuum, bypassing the liquid
phase and thereby minimizing shrinkage due to capillary forces [42].
This drying method is particularly effective for maintaining the struc­
tural integrity of porous or delicate hydrogel materials.
While freeze­drying showed promising results in terms of geome­
try preservation, further investigation is needed to evaluate whether
it consistently improves dimensional stability and how it affects the
mechanical properties of the final structure. Future work should as­
sess the impact of drying method on strength, brittleness, and poros­
ity, particularly for applications in biomedical packaging.

6.1
Introduction
Single­use plastic components are prevalent in medical devices and
packaging, but their disposal poses significant environmental chal­
lenges [43]. Materials like PP and PET are derived from fossil fu­
els and are not biodegradable, leading to persistent waste and mi­
croplastic pollution [44]. In response, biopolymer alternatives are
being explored for medical cartridges (e.g. vials or fluid reservoirs)
that store alcohol­based solutions. SF, a protein from Bombyx mori
silkworm cocoons, has attracted attention due to its renewability,
biocompatibility, and biodegradability [45, 46]. Blends of SF with
PVA are of particular interest because they can combine SF’s bioma­
terial advantages with improved mechanical stability from PVA [45].
This chapter analyzes the environmental sustainability of SF and
SF/PVA blend cartridges versus conventional PP and PET, focusing
on four key metrics: life cycle assessment (LCA), carbon footprint,
biodegradability, and recyclability. Strengths and limitations of the
SF­based systems in a medical context are also discussed. Note,
this is a literature based analysis, for more details on the specific
cartridge discussed in this thesis, an LCA would need to be con­
ducted.
6.2
Life Cycle and Carbon Footprint Comparison
From a life­cycle perspective, SF and petroleum­based plastics have
fundamentally different environmental profiles. SF is a bio­based
material, produced through the cultivation of mulberry trees and rear­
ing of silkworms, followed by cocoon processing to extract fibroin.
This process is resource­intensive: one LCA reported a cumulative
energy demand of over 1800 MJ per kg of raw silk [47, 48], which,
using a global average energy emission factor of 0.0548 kg CO2­
eq/MJ [49], equates to approximately 98.6 kg CO2­eq/kg:
1800 MJ × 0.0548 kg CO2/MJ = 98.6 kg CO2­eq
Design and Development of a Sustainable Polymer Cartridge for a Medical Device
47
==PAGE:60==
By contrast, 1 kg of virgin PP requires only 75–85 MJ and emits
1.5–1.6 kg CO2­eq [50, 51](with some estimates reaching 4.4 kg
assuming the global average energy emission factor), while virgin
PET production consumes 70–83 MJ/kg and emits 2.2–2.7 kg CO2­
eq [52] (3.8 kg assuming the global average energy emission fac­
tor). Despite being fossil­derived, these polymers are manufactured
in highly optimized industrial settings, resulting in relatively efficient
production­phase emissions.
PVA, while sometimes considered more environmentally friendly due
to its partial biodegradability, also carries a notable production­phase
carbon footprint. According to a life cycle assessment by Kuraray
Europe GmbH, the cradle­to­gate GHG emissions for PVA (Kuraray
Poval™) are approximately 2.5 kg CO2­eq per kg of polymer [53].
This places PVA’s emissions between those of PP and PET. While
blending PVA with SF improves mechanical and processing proper­
ties, it does contribute additional fossil­based emissions to the final
material. As such, the sustainability of SF/PVA blends depends not
only on the bio­based content but also on the energy and feedstock
sources used during synthesis.
However, SF’s carbon profile is biogenic. The carbon in fibroin orig­
inates from atmospheric CO2 fixed by mulberry plants, and when
the material biodegrades or is incinerated, that CO2 returns to the
atmosphere as part of the short­term carbon cycle. Still, the produc­
tion process can generate significant emissions if powered by fossil
fuels emissions which could be drastically reduced using renewable
energy. For example, Iceland’s energy grid emits just 0.006 kg CO2­
eq/MJ [49], which would lower silk’s cradle­to­gate footprint to only
10.9 kg CO2­eq/kg:
1800 MJ × 0.006 kg CO2/MJ = 10.9 kg CO2­eq
At end­of­life, SF and SF/PVA blends offer clear sustainability ad­
vantages. They are biodegradable in compost or soil, breaking down
into biomass, water, and CO2, and in some cases even enriching the
soil with nitrogen [54]. Their disposal has negligible long­term en­
vironmental impact, especially when compared to petroleum­based
plastics. In contrast, PP and PET are not biodegradable and can
persist for decades if landfilled [44]. Incineration of PP/PET releases
48
Design and Development of a Sustainable Polymer Cartridge for a Medical Device
==PAGE:61==
fossil­derived CO2, contributing directly to climate change. While en­
ergy can be recovered from incineration, the carbon emissions re­
main a concern. Conversely, incineration of SF materials releases
biogenic carbon, maintaining neutrality in the global carbon cycle
[43].
Recycling offers one way to mitigate the footprint of conventional
plastics. For instance, recycled PET has a reported emission of just
0.38 kg CO2­eq/kg which is an 87% reduction compared to virgin
PET [55]. Yet, recycling rates remain low, particularly for contami­
nated or mixed­use medical waste.
In summary, SF­based systems shift environmental burden toward
the production phase due to agricultural inputs and processing en­
ergy, but they dramatically reduce end­of­life impacts. In contrast,
PP and PET have lower production­phase emissions but contribute
to long­term waste and fossil carbon release. A full LCA would need
to evaluate these trade­offs based on local energy infrastructure,
disposal routes, and recycling feasibility.
6.3
Biodegradability
One of the clearest sustainability advantages of SF materials is their
biodegradability. SF is a natural protein composed of amino acids,
and it is subject to biological degradation by microorganisms (bac­
teria, fungi) and enzymatic processes (e.g., proteases) in the envi­
ronment. The degradation behavior of SF depends significantly on
its molecular structure.
SF processed into the Silk II structure, the highly crystalline β­sheet
conformation typical of native silk fibers, exhibits enhanced mechan­
ical and chemical stability compared to less­ordered Silk I structures.
Silk II is notably more resistant to water dissolution and enzymatic
degradation, making it highly durable. However, studies have con­
firmed that even Silk II materials are biodegradable under environ­
mental and composting conditions, although at a slower rate than
Silk I [56].
Experimental results demonstrate that proteolytic enzymes such as
protease XIV and proteinase K can cleave the polypeptide chains
within the Silk II matrix over time, leading to fragmentation and even­
tual mineralization [56]. The degradation rate depends on several
Design and Development of a Sustainable Polymer Cartridge for a Medical Device
49
==PAGE:62==
factors, including the degree of crystallinity, environmental moisture,
temperature, and the presence of active microbial communities. Un­
der composting or biologically active soil conditions, Silk II materi­
als can degrade completely over a timescale ranging from several
weeks to months.
SF/PVA blends are also designed to be biodegradable. PVA is a
synthetic polymer but unusual in that it is water­soluble and can be
mineralized by microbes under the right conditions. Recent studies
confirm that certain PVA grades are readily biodegradable in aquatic
environments: for instance, a standard test found a fully hydrolyzed
PVA (88% hydrolysis, medium molecular weight) achieved complete
mineralization in OECD biodegradation assays [57]. This means
SF/PVA composites should not leave long­lasting plastic fragments;
the PVA component can eventually be consumed by microorgan­
isms, especially in wastewater treatment facilities or soil, while the
SF component is naturally digestible as a protein.
It should be noted, however, that environmental conditions matter.
PVA that biodegrades in soil or activated sludge may not break down
in cold ocean water, one report noted negligible biodegradation of
PVA in marine environments [57]. Likewise, SF buried in very dry
or cold conditions might persist longer until microbes and moisture
facilitate decay.
In stark contrast, PP and PET are effectively non­biodegradable on
any reasonable timescale. Polypropylene and PET are stable poly­
mers that do not readily hydrolyze or metabolize; when disposed in
the environment, they may persist for decades to centuries. They
undergo slow physical degradation, UV exposure and weathering
can cause embrittlement and fragmentation, but this leads to mi­
croplastics rather than true biodegradation [44]. A United Nations re­
view pointed out that most synthetic polymers, whether fossil­based
or bio­based, do not biodegrade under normal conditions and in­
stead simply fragment over time [44].
Thus, a discarded PP or PET cartridge could linger in ecosystems,
contributing to plastic pollution and harm to wildlife. The inability of
PP/PET to biodegrade is a major sustainability drawback, especially
for single­use items. By using a biodegradable SF/PVA system, the
end­of­life impact is greatly reduced: the material will fully break

down rather than accumulate. There is also a public health benefit
in the context of biodegradability, SF is biocompatible and its degra­
dation products are non­toxic peptides/amino acids [46], whereas
microplastics from PP/PET can absorb and transport contaminants
in the environment.
In summary, SF and SF/PVA excel in biodegradability, essentially
eliminating long­term environmental persistence, whereas PP and
PET are persistent pollutants unless actively recovered.
6.3.1
Microplastic Pollution and Public Health Concerns
Beyond carbon emissions and end­of­life degradation, an increasing
environmental and health concern associated with synthetic plastics
is the generation of microplastics, small polymer fragments formed
through weathering and mechanical breakdown of larger plastic items.
PP and PET, due to their extreme durability and resistance to biodegra­
dation, are major contributors to microplastic pollution, particularly
from improperly disposed single­use items. These microplastics ac­
cumulate in terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems and have been de­
tected in drinking water, air, and even human tissue [58, 59]. As
microplastics can adsorb and transport environmental toxins, they
pose additional risks to both ecological and human health.
Biodegradable materials like SF and SF/PVA blends do not persist
or fragment in the same way. Instead of forming microplastics, these
materials degrade into non­toxic byproducts, peptides, amino acids,
and, in the case of PVA, eventually water and CO2 under the right mi­
crobial conditions. Thus, they offer a preventive strategy against mi­
croplastic generation, aligning with global initiatives to reduce long­
term plastic particle accumulation in the environment. Especially in
the medical field, where single­use plastic components are abundant
and often incinerated or landfilled, adopting biodegradable alterna­
tives provides a forward­looking approach to minimizing both macro­
and micro­scale pollution.
6.4
Recyclability and Reuse
Recyclability is another important facet of sustainability, referring to
the ability to reclaim and reprocess material from used products.
Conventional thermoplastics like PP and PET have an advantage
here: they can be melted and remolded, or chemically depolymer­
ized, to create new plastic. In practice, PET is one of the most re­
Design and Development of a Sustainable Polymer Cartridge for a Medical Device
51
==PAGE:64==
cycled plastics due to bottle deposit schemes and established re­
cycling streams. PET bottles have a recycling rate around 29% in
the U.S. (2018 data) and even higher in some countries [60]. PP’s
recycling rate has historically been much lower, for example, only
≈3–10% of post­consumer PP in the U.S. is recycled, with the vast
majority landfilled [61]. This is due to challenges in collecting and
sorting PP and historically lower economic incentives. Europe has
achieved around 30% recycling for PP by implementing strict regula­
tions [62], indicating that improvement is possible. When PP or PET
are effectively recycled, the environmental burden per use drops
significantly (since the material’s initial production cost is amortized
over multiple lifecycles, and less new polymer needs to be made).
However, in medical applications, recycling is often not feasible be­
cause used cartridges may be biohazardous or contaminated. Ad­
ditionally, medical­grade plastics are subject to strict regulatory re­
quirements, particularly based on their intended use, which often
prohibit the use of recycled materials due to concerns over safety,
traceability, and consistency [63, 64]. Hospital waste protocols usu­
ally mandate incineration or disposal of used medical consumables
rather than recycling. Thus, the theoretical recyclability of PP/PET
often cannot be realized for medical cartridges, they typically be­
come waste after a single use.
SF and SF/PVA materials, on the other hand, are not recycled in the
conventional sense. There is no industrial process to recover and
reprocess SF from a used device, partly because the material will
begin to biologically degrade and also because it is not a thermo­
plastic that can be remelted. SF/PVA composites are intended for
one­time use followed by safe biodegradation or composting rather
than mechanical recycling. One could consider composting as a
form of “organic recycling,” but it yields biomass and biogas, not new
SF material. The lack of recyclability for SF­based materials is mit­
igated by their biodegradability, instead of entering a recycle loop,
they return to nature without harm. This is an advantage in scenar­
ios where recycling infrastructure is lacking or where contamination
is an issue (like medical waste). Another end­of­life option is incin­
eration with energy recovery: SF/PVA can be incinerated similar to
plastics, and because SF is biomass­derived, the CO2 emissions
are biogenic. PVA incineration would release fossil CO2, but PVA

typically constitutes a smaller fraction of the blend. In terms of circu­
larity, one limitation is that SF/PVA does not currently contribute to a
circular material economy, each cartridge is grown, used, and then
decomposed, requiring continuous silk farming to replace stock. In
contrast, a PET cartridge could, in principle, be recycled into a new
PET product, reducing the need for new petroleum extraction if ex­
ecuted perfectly.
In summary, PP and PET are technically recyclable, but medical
use often precludes actual recycling, leading to disposal. SF and
SF/PVA are not recyclable into new cartridges, but they offer a dif­
ferent kind of sustainability by eliminating persistent waste.
The
choice between these approaches depends on context: if a closed­
loop recycling system could be implemented (which is challenging
for medical disposables), plastics might be sustainable in that loop;
if not, biodegradable SF/PVA ensures that even single­use items
won’t accumulate in the environment. One could argue the SF/PVA
route aligns with a compostable product paradigm (use and return
to earth), whereas PP/PET align with a recyclable product paradigm
(use and remanufacture). Both strategies aim to reduce waste, but
currently SF/PVA provides more certainty that no long­term pollution
remains, at the expense of not being able to reuse the material.

6.6
Conclusion
This chapter has provided a comprehensive cradle­to­grave com­
parison of SF/PVA blends versus conventional plastics (PP and PET)
for single­use biomedical cartridge applications. The analysis fo­
cused on production­ and end­of­life greenhouse gas emissions, re­
cyclability, biodegradability, and material persistence.
Quantitatively, the results show a sharp contrast between biopoly­
mer systems and synthetic plastics in terms of emissions. PP and
PET exhibit low total carbon footprints of 5.0kgCO2­eq/kg and 4.8kgCO2­
eq/kg, respectively, due to efficient industrial production and rela­
tively lower incineration emissions. In contrast, SF/PVA blends show
a significantly higher footprint of up to 50.6kgCO2­eq/kg when silk is
produced using average grid electricity. However, this value drops
substantially to 6.7kgCO2­eq/kg when silk rearing is powered by re­
newable energy, with a reduction of nearly 87%.
Moreover, pure silk produced under the standard grid reaches a foot­
print of 98.6kgCO2­eq/kg while with renewable energy conditions it
reaches just 10.9kgCO2­eq/kg, emphasizing the critical role of en­
ergy source in determining biopolymer sustainability. These mate­
rials also avoid fossil­derived carbon inputs and are biodegradable
under controlled conditions, reducing environmental persistence and
microplastic generation.
Despite the currently higher emissions for SF/PVA blends, they present
clear environmental advantages in end­of­life scenarios. Unlike PP
and PET, which are often incinerated or landfilled with minimal re­
cycling, SF/PVA systems can degrade in biological environments or
industrial composting setups. This property aligns with the grow­
ing demand for transient, environmentally reintegrable materials in
healthcare and packaging.
Therefore, while SF/PVA blends may not yet outperform synthetic
polymers on all carbon metrics, they embody a transition toward cir­
cular material systems, especially when supported by clean energy
infrastructure and bio­based sourcing of PVA. As energy grids con­
tinue to decarbonize and biopolymer processing becomes more ef­
ficient, the relative emissions disadvantage will diminish further.
In conclusion, sustainability assessments should weigh not only car­

renewability. Under such a holistic framework, SF/PVA blends emerge

croplastic pollution. By fully degrading rather than fragmenting, SF­

Conclusions
This thesis explored the design, fabrication, and characterization of
chemically crosslinked SF and PVA blends as sustainable alterna­
tives to conventional petroleum­based plastics for use in alcohol­
resistant biomedical packaging. By combining the structural strength
and biodegradability of SF with the flexibility and film­forming capa­
bilities of PVA, several composite formulations were developed and
evaluated for their mechanical, chemical, and environmental perfor­
mance.
Compression testing revealed that the 50/50 SF/PVA blend provided
a favorable balance between stiffness and toughness, outperform­
ing both pure SF and pure PVA in energy absorption under compres­
sive strain. The addition of Laponite nanoparticles in the SiPo/PVA
formulation significantly increased stiffness but introduced a more
brittle response. FTIR confirmed successful chemical crosslinking
through the formation of Schiff base (C=N) and acetal (C–O–C) link­
ages, which contributed to improved ethanol resistance. Ethanol
immersion tests further demonstrated that the materials maintained
their structural integrity over 7 days, and re­dried weights showed
no measurable degradation beyond the instrument’s sensitivity.
However, a substantial degree of absorption was observed in all for­
mulations during ethanol exposure, which could compromise long­
term dimensional stability in certain applications. To mitigate this,
the incorporation of an inner or surface coating layer may be con­
sidered in future designs to limit solvent ingress while maintaining
biodegradability.
The fabricated cartridges illustrated the feasibility of molding com­
plex geometries using these biodegradable materials.
However,
significant volumetric shrinkage and surface deformation were ob­
served during drying, indicating the need for further optimization in
processing methods.
From a sustainability perspective, literature­based carbon footprint
comparisons showed that SF/PVA blends may offer meaningful ad­
vantages over PP and PET, particularly due to their biodegradabil­

ity and renewable feedstocks. While current cradle­to­gate emis­
sions are relatively high for SF, especially due to energy­intensive
processing steps such as degumming and dialysis, future process
improvements, such as solvent recovery, enzyme­assisted degum­
ming, or use of renewable energy, could significantly lower the en­
vironmental impact. If such reductions are realized, SF­based sys­
tems could surpass both PP and PET in cradle­to­grave CO2 emis­
sions, making them a more competitive sustainable alternative.
7.1
Future Work and Potential Applications
While this study provides a strong foundation for the use of SF/PVA
blends in biodegradable packaging systems, several avenues re­
main for further exploration.
Future research should expand the mechanical characterization to
include tensile and fracture behavior, and examine the response to
cyclic loading or fluid pressure. Additional degradation studies in
real­world disposal environments (e.g., compost, soil, marine) are
also necessary to validate the environmental claims under practical
conditions.
Process improvements are critical to reduce shrinkage and improve
surface finish, potentially through controlled drying methods, mold
redesign, or the use of plasticizers. Scaling production and stan­
dardized methods should also be assessed for industrial relevance.
Additionally, optimizing the processing steps for SF could not only
improve material quality but also reduce energy demand and asso­
ciated emissions.
Moreover, incorporating functional additives, such as antimicrobial
agents or surface coatings, could extend the material’s utility in high­
risk healthcare settings. Applications could include:
• Single­use cartridges for alcohol­based hand sanitizer dispensers
• Biodegradable containers for gels, ointments, or topical drug de­
livery
• Reservoirs for diagnostic reagents or therapeutic fluids in med­
ical devices
• Inner linings for reusable medical housings requiring sterile in­
