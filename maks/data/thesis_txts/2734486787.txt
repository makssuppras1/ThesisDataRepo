Abstract
This report explores the relationship between sustainability and reuse in the context of con-
struction practice. The author’s exploration reveals that sustainability includes environmental,
social and economic considerations, making it necessary to address all three areas when assessing
the resources of the investigation.
Qualitative interviews conducted within the construction industry revealed the challenges and
opportunities associated with recycling. While sustainability standards proved to have an impact
on the sectors, opinions on recycling and the scalability issues and regulatory hurdles. A ﬁeld
study investigating direct recycling revealed challenges in ﬁnding suitable recycled products and
the need to adapt projects accordingly. This led to a shift in focus and it was decided to investigate
recycled materials for insulation purposes.
The evaluation of insulation materials across the sustainability areas highlighted variations in
performance, with paper insulation emerging as a favorable choice. Despite the beneﬁts of recycled
materials, challenges remain with their integration into construction processes, particularly due
to complexity and time constraints. However, with ongoing developments in sustainability and
building practices, there is optimism that recycling will become more integrated in the future,
o↵ering promising prospects for sustainable building practices.

Introduction
”Reuse”, a phenomenon that receives a lot of attention in today’s Denmark. The interest for both
physical and online ﬂea markets is increasing [3]. But why are we so enthusiastic about reuse?
Some do it to save money, others want to be environmentally responsible, and there are plenty
of other reasons but one thing is for sure, reuse is popular, especially among young people born
between 1996 and 2015 [4] [5].
For me, reuse is craftsmanship and a passing down of a story. When I stand with a used chair and
can see the various usage marks, I feel like I become a part of the previous story. When I sand,
prepare, and paint the chair, I make it a part of my own story. This is precisely why I love reuse.
The fact that it is also environmentally conscious and does not encourage overconsumption is
just a bonus for me.
How is reuse used in the construction industry? We talk a lot about sustainability in the built
environment. In the Danish construction industry, a remarkable journey toward improved sustai-
nability has been marked by the introduction of the voluntary sustainability class [6]. The volun-
tary sustainability class is carefully designed to promote and support sustainable construction
with a strong focus on critical aspects such as, Life Cycle Analysis, LCA. November 2023 the
voluntary sustainability class transitioned from being optional to becoming mandatory [6]. This
transformation compels all stakeholders to elevate their commitment to sustainability.
However, despite the increased focus on sustainability, I do not see much use of recycled materials
in this industry. Is it a lack of knowledge on my part? Perhaps, but it has certainly motivated
me to explore the interaction between sustainability and reuse in the construction environment
in Denmark.
To address this challenge, a holistic approach to sustainability and its three dimensions will be
applied: Social Sustainability, Environmental Sustainability, and Economic Sustainability [7] [8]
[9].
To investigate how sustainability and reused materials work together I will use the principles of
The Funnel Model, where the information gathered, gradually will help to narrow down the track
to the ﬁnal conclusion. Figure 1.1 provides an overview of the steps included in this process.

Background
The background study will involve an investigation of past perspectives on the essence and needs
of sustainability in general. Later on it will be narrowing down to how sustainability is used in
the construction sector.
2.1
Sustainability
Sustainability embodies the idea of meeting the needs of today and at the same time ensuring
the needs of tomorrows generations. As Simone Sæderup Nielsen pointed out in her article, What
is Sustainability?[10],
”En udvikling, der opfylder nuværende generationers behov uden at bringe fremtidens
generationers muligheder for at møde deres behov i fare.”
Translation by Marie Viu↵Rodriguez: ”A development that meets the needs of current
generations without jeopardizing the opportunities of future generations to meet their needs.”
This message will generally form the design choices that will be made throughout the report.
Sustainability is the term for the three basic areas: Environmental Sustainability, Social Sustai-
nability and Economic Sustainability.
1. Environmental Sustainability:
Environmental sustainability deals with responsible management and conservation of resour-
ces. Environmental sustainability must strive to minimize damage to the environment,
reduce pollution and promote biodiversity [7].
2. Social Sustainability:
Social sustainability is centered around the well-being of individuals and communities.
Social sustainability aims to ensure that everyone, regardless of background, has equal
access to the most essential services in society [8].
3. Economic Sustainability:
Economic sustainability focuses on the long-term viability and resilience of economic sy-
stems. The goal is to ensure that economic beneﬁts reach a broad portion of the population,
not just a selected few [9].
2.2
The Development of the Term Sustainability
People have been talking about climate changes for a long time, but it was in the 20th century that
it started to get a lot of political attention. The Stockholm Conference was the ﬁrst conference

Climate change and its seriousness cannot be overlooked today, as it is something that has a
major impact on all of our everyday lives. So, during the last decades, sustainability has been
given more and more talking time. In connection with this, there have been many important
milestones for sustainability and its development, both nationally and internationally [12].
Figure 2.1 introduces some of the milestones for sustainability, both on a national and interna-

Figur 2.1: Overview of some Important Events in the History of Sustainability
After the Stockholm conference, momentum for sustainability measures increased signiﬁcantly.
In 1987, the Brundtland Commission presented its landmark report which introduced the term
”Our Common Future”. This phrase has since become a central concept that is often invoked
when deﬁning the goals of sustainability [13].
As the 19th century gave way to the 20th, the momentum around environmental development
and sustainability gained substantial traction, such as the ”Rio Declaration on Environment and
Development”and United Nation (UN)’s Conference of the Parties (COP)15 and COP21.
The ”Rio Declaration on Environment and Development”is a landmark conference that was held
in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil in the year 1992. The Rio Declaration consists of 27 principles which
aim to promote sustainable development and the integration of environmental and development
goals [14].
The Conference of the Parties (COP) meetings are part of (UNFCCC) the United Nations Fra-
mework Convention on Climate Change which is held annually. COP15 and COP21 have in
common that they deal with critical climate issues. COP15, which took place in Copenhagen in
2009, had the primary objective of negotiating a successor to the Kyoto Protocol. The purpose
was to create some new global climate goals and commitments, of which there were to be a
particular focus on how to get these commitments established in the more developed nations.
COP15 wasn’t legally binding but was the ﬁrst of its kind, and thereby it has played a central

role in the design of the future COPs [15].
COP21 was the successor to COP15, and resulted in the landmark ”The Paris Agreement”.
COP21 set the stage for global action against climate change and its impact on the environment
[16]. Unlike COP15, COP21 led to a binding and comprehensive international commitment that
aims to have common goals for dealing with the climate crisis.
Looking at this timeline (Figure 2.1), especially in terms of the danish construction industry,
2012 stands out. This year, the Danish construction industry was introduced to DGNB certiﬁca-
tion, which is a point-based system that rewards sustainable initiatives in construction projects
(Section: 2.4.2).
In the years after the voluntary sustainability classiﬁcation system was introduced in the Danish
construction industry. This voluntary classiﬁcation served as a test phase before sustainability
became a legal requirement in Denmark in 2023 (Section: 2.4.1).
All the previous milestones emphasize the importance of integrating sustainability from the
beginning of any project, whether it concerns construction, industry, community development
ect. because by doing so, we can better meet current needs without compromising the well-being
of future generations.
2.3
Sustainability in the Built Environment
The construction industry is responsible for approximately 40% of the total worldwide CO2
emissions [17]. Because of this, it is important to take special measures to make the construction
industry greener. In the text below, some of the approaches that work towards making the
construction industry greener will be introduced.
2.3.1
United Nations Sustainable Development Goals, SDGs
The Sustainable Development Goals (SDG) 9, 11 and 12 are particularly relevant to the built
environment as they include infrastructure development, sustainable urbanization, responsible
consumption and production.

2.3.2
The World Green Building Council

2.4.3
Environmental Product Declaration - EPD
EPD aims to document the environmental properties that a building material has. This form
of documentation has been developed in line with European and international standards. EPD
should generally be a method which is designed to easily provide the right information regarding
energy and resource consumption, waste generation and the environmental impacts from the
production of the given construction product, as well as the use and disposal of a construction
product.
Overall, there will be two di↵erent EPDs that you can use:
• Industry EPD: An industry EPD will indicate a data average within a speciﬁc product
type. This could, for example, be products such as concrete elements, bricks, wood etc.
The data average is based on the dominant proportion of representative producers that
exist within the industries in question.
• Product-speciﬁc EPD: A product-speciﬁc EPD will be determined from a speciﬁc product
that occurs from a speciﬁc manufacturer. This could, for example, be insulation material
from RockWool, Pumpe well from Wawin or similar. A product-speciﬁc EPD will also be
available where di↵erent types of surface treatments occur.
EPD is used to a great extent in life cycle assessment programs such as LCAByg (Life Cycle
Assessment)[25], in which the EPDs make it easier to map the right environmental properties
for the construction product in question.

In recent times, a number of signiﬁcant events have triggered various crises. For property owners,

ﬁnances. The causes of this energy crisis have many sides and with the conﬂict in Ukraine

Statistics has also examined the price development of various fuel types over the last 10 years

especially for gas prices.
This crisis has heightened awareness among consumers, compelling them to adopt more energy-
conscious practices. The new normal involves entering homes with lower temperatures, closely
monitoring energy apps to optimize laundry times, encountering unexpectedly cold public bu-
ildings, and strolling through Copenhagen’s Christmas streets with fewer festive lights [38] [39]
[40] [41].
For me, the energy crisis has served as a motivational factor to explore sustainable solutions

4.1.1
Recycling
As this report particularly focuses on the use of recycled materials, it is essential to deﬁne the cri-
teria a material must meet to be considered recycled and understand how recycled materials di↵er
from recyclable materials [44]. Recycled and recyclable materials are often used interchangeably,
but are clearly di↵erent concepts.
Recycled materials refer to materials that have been used in a di↵erent context and are now
used again without undergoing signiﬁcant processing or alteration. For example, old wood from
a previous construction can be used to build a new structure without being transformed. An
example of such construction is the recycled sheds that the company ’Næste’ builds [45].
Recyclable materials refer to materials that can be collected, processed, and transformed into new
products or materials without losing their fundamental properties. For instance, plastic bottles
can be collected, melted down, and ultimately transformed into new plastic products [44].
Both recycling and reusing are considered parts of sustainable material management, contributing
to resource eﬃciency and awareness. By using recycled materials, waste is reduced, and the need
for new resources is diminished. Similarly, the use of recyclable materials reduces the need for
new raw materials for expanding new products.

Stone Wool, Loose Fill
Stone wool, as an insulation material, falls into the category of mineral wool and is manufactured
from mineral raw materials such as stone and sand [48]. Stone wool insulation is inorganic and
a breathable material. This means that the risk of moisture problems is reduced since moisture
will naturally be transported through the material without causing condensation. Additionally,
this characteristic gives stone wool a water-repellent e↵ect, ensuring that the shape of the stone
wool does not collapse over time [49].
Stone wool is often associated with RockWool. One of the notable properties of stone wool, such as
RockWool, is its ﬁre-retardant, sound-insulating, and energy-eﬃcient insulation characteristics.
Stone wool insulation materials are available in various forms, including rigid batts, mats, and
blown-in granules [50].
Glass Wool, Loose Fill
Glass wool shares many of the same properties as stone wool. Like stone wool, glass wool is also
categorized as mineral wool but, in contrast to stone wool, is made from ﬁbrous glass. Fibrous
glass is produced by heating raw materials and spinning them into thin threads. Glass wool
can be both bonded and compressed into mats or in loose form. Glass wool potentially can
be manufactured from recycled glass, with the caveat of maintaining color uniformity and the
presence of organic/ceramic materials [51].
Similar to stone wool and glass wool is an inorganic material with many favorable qualities
such as being non-combustible, not susceptible to moisture, good insulation capabilities, and the
potential for production from recycled materials [52].
EPS
EPS, as an insulation material, is made from expanded polystyrene. Expanded polystyrene con-
sists of air-ﬁlled closed cells, composed of air with only a small percentage of polystyrene. This ef-
fective interplay between air and polystyrene gives EPS excellent insulating properties, moisture-
resistant characteristics, and the ability to retain strength and shape even in high humidity [53].

6.3
Partial Conclusion
In conclusion, the examination of environmental sustainability within the context of energy
renovations highlights the critical importance of considering the earth’s resources and their sustai-
nable utilization, especially given the recent scarcity of raw materials. The study focused on
assessing pollution levels associated with di↵erent building materials, utilizing the LCAByg tool
for comprehensive analysis.
Despite challenges, clear goals and assumptions were outlined, emphasizing the need for speciﬁc
material data like EPDs to ensure accurate LCA calculations. Insulation material selection was
based on industry-speciﬁc EPDs, and the user-friendly LCAByg tool facilitated analysis across
various building life cycle phases.
Results revealed varying environmental footprints of insulation materials, with paper wool de-
monstrating a negative GWP, indicating a reduction in greenhouse gas emissions, while stone
wool, glass wool, and EPS exhibited positive GWPs, suggesting potential environmental harm.
Further analysis of GWP distribution showed that paper wool has a positive e↵ect on reducing
CO2 emissions into the atmosphere, attributed to factors such as carbon capture and lower
energy-intensive production processes compared to mineral wool and EPS. Consequently, paper
wool emerges as the most sustainable choice based on LCAByg calculations.
Overall, these ﬁndings emphasize the importance of adopting environmentally sustainable pra-
ctices in construction and renovation projects to mitigate environmental harm and promote a
healthier planet.

Conclusion
”Reuse”, is a popular phenomenon with its own set of challenges.
Through my exploration of sustainability and its three areas, I have learned that sustainability
primarily revolves around the responsible use of the Earth’s resources. Responsibility must ma-
nifest in environmental, social, and economic aspects. In my view, one cannot deem a resource
sustainable without considering all three areas of sustainability.
By incorporating various Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) and Sustainability Standards
and Classiﬁcations, I laid a solid foundation for ﬁnding answers to uncovering the many questions
I needed to address. Throughout my background study, I found it puzzling why sustainability
and reuse are not more frequently discussed together. To me, sustainability and reuse are two
inseparable aspects.
By conducting qualitative interviews within the construction industry signiﬁcantly broadened
my understanding. There was a consensus that sustainability and its accompanying Standards
and Classiﬁcations have greatly inﬂuenced the construction sector. However, opinions on reuse
were more divided. While some interviewees were optimistic about increasing the use of reuse,
they struggled to envision its practical implementation due to scalability issues and regulatory
challenges.
This prompted me to investigate further. It turned out that direct reuse (1:1 reuse) wasn’t
as straightforward as I initially imagined. I spent considerable time in a ﬁeld study exploring
the possibilities of replacing windows with reused ones. This proved challenging as ﬁnding the
desired product and adequate information about it was diﬃcult. It became apparent that one
would have to adapt the project based on the available reused products at any given time, which
could limit planning. Additionally, using reused materials would involve numerous assumptions
about their technical properties. Consequently, I abandoned the idea of using 1:1 reuse as a case
study and shifted focus to investigating reused materials in other contexts. I decided to examine
reused materials as insulation. I chose to investigate the retroﬁtting of cavity walls using various
insulation materials, where one of the insulation material are made from reuse material.
The insulation materials were evaluated across all three sustainability areas. It became clear
that a material’s performance varied signiﬁcantly depending on the area examined. For instance,
glass wool excelled in social sustainability, while paper wool insulation performed better in en-
vironmental and economic sustainability. After discussing various ﬁndings within environmental,
social, and economic sustainability and conducting a comparative analysis, it became evident
that despite initial concerns about using reused materials in construction processes, paper wool
insulation was the best choice based on the measured parameters.
After conducting all these studies, I can conclude that reused materials, particularly from an
environmental and economic standpoint, o↵er clear advantages. However, there is still some
way to go to make reuse the preferred option in construction processes. The availability and
process of using reused materials in construction are, to me, more complex and time-consuming
experiences compared to using new materials. For all the renovation cases I examined, except
Side 60 af 93

Building Renovation Projects
February, 2024
cavity wall insulation, they proved to be extremely time-consuming, and I never succeeded in
completing them as I had to make too many assumptions, and my case building didn’t allow me
to build my project around the reused materials I wanted to use.
Nevertheless, reuse has come a long way, and considering the developments within sustainability
in the construction industry, I believe it won’t be long before reuse becomes a more integrated
part of the construction process. I, for one, look forward to monitoring this progress.

1. Hvordan deﬁnerer du begrebet bæredygtighed, og hvorfor er det vigtigt i da-
gens samfund?
Bæredygtighed indebærer at opfylde nutidens behov uden at overskride de planetære græn-
ser, der omfatter klimaforandringer, biodiversitetstab, kemisk forurening og mere. Det er
afgørende for nudagens og fremtidens samfund, da overskridelse af disse grænser kan føre til
katastrofale konsekvenser, hvor der primært er fokus pa klimaforandringer, som vi allerede
mærker i stor stil med eksempelvis ekstreme vejrhændelser, som denne sommer har været
et klart eksempel pa. I byggebranchen er der meget fokus netop pa klimaforandringer og
CO2, men der er et stigende fokus pa andre aspekter sasom ressourceknaphed og biodiver-
sitet. Bæredygtighed er afgørende for at bevare planetens sundhed og sikre en levedygtig
fremtid for kommende generationer, hvilket kræver at vi mennesker ændre vores adfærd i
form af livsstil, forretningspraksis og politiske beslutninger.
2. Hvordan ser I pa bæredygtighedens rolle i fremtidens byggeri, og hvilke ten-
denser eller ændringer forventer I at se inden for bæredygtigt byggeri?
Der har været meget fokus pa LCA i form af det nye lovkrav der tradte i kraft i starten
af 2023. Det har helt sikkert rykket et fokus, og været en øjenabner for mange for hvilken
CO2-pavirkning bygninger har. Den nuværende grænseværdi er sat højt, og jeg forventer
den vil reduceres mere end først angivet frem mod 2025. Nar først grænseværdien bliver
lavere ned mod 6-8 kg CO2- ækv./m2/ar, vil man begynde at ﬁnde lidt alternative mader
at bygge pa, og være mere aben overfor andre materialevalg end blot beton, mineraluld
og tegl. Det skal siges, at før vi kan nærme os noget som kan betegnes som bæredygtigt
byggeri, skal vi væsentligt længere ned endnu ift. miljøpavirkning! Derudover tror jeg der
vil komme mere fokus pa biodiversitet, da det ogsa er en planitær grænse som har passeret
safe operating space markant.
