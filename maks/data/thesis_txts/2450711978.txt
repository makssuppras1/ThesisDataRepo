Abstract
In this Master’s thesis the potentials and limitations for the large scaled ambient air sourced heat pump
analysed. This is done through a case study of an operational 0.85MW indirect heat pump located
in Sig-Denmark. For the analysis of this have operational data been extracted over a two-month pe-
riod from 2019-01-01 to 2019-01-27. In this paper are two diﬀerent models created; The ﬁrst is a
quasi-static discrete heat transfer model of the herringboned plate ﬁnned-tube heat exchanger used
in a staggered V-shaped heat absorber. This is based on the Kim Yun Webb heat transfer correlations.
The model is validated against the case study with a precision on heat production of +/- 20The second
model is a FEM discrete defrosting model for the plain plate ﬁnned-tube geometry, where a single ﬁn
with tube is simulated for various conditions, creating averaged heat ﬂow polynomials, used for mod-
elling of an entire ﬁnned-tube row section. The model is created for constant secondary refrigerant
properties.
A CFD model is made for analysis of two 18 fans V-shaped heat absorbers, which are located in paral-
lel. The model is created as a constant suction boundary. The distance is varied between them [3,4,5,8]m
and the wind direction- and speed independently. The result of this show highest overall recirculation
at 4m distance, with, and lowest at 8.
The two models; Quasi-staic & FEM are created in this paper can be used as tools for understanding
of large scaled heat pump operation and optimization of these. Both models are based on Python.

Introduction
According to the International Energy Agency IEA heat is the largest energy end-use. This means
the energy which is directly consumed by the user, traditionally through sources as gasoline, natural
gas and electricity. Where approximately 50% of the total energy consumption goes to heat energy
production. The heat is provided for heating for homes, industrial processes and other applications.
Over half of this amount is consumed by industry, for process heat as drying and hot water use. 46%
of the heat energy is used for space- and water heating, and cooking in the building sector. Where the
remainder is used in agriculture.
In 2017 was only 10% of the total heat energy consumption produced from renewables, but is ex-
pected to grow 20% within the period 2018 – 2023, with bioenergy contributing the most. Yet this
would only increase the share of renewables in the heating sector to 12% in 2023. The current renew-
able heat consumption has grown by estimated 2.6% annually, but is required instead to increase by
4% annually to meet the SDS goals for 2030. More action is therefore required to meet the set goals.

This paper looks into the area of renewable heat energy, as highlighted on the ﬁgure, with the focus on
renewable electricity for district heating on large scale. Electricity accounts for about 7% of the global
heat demand, mainly in buildings. The electriﬁcation of industrial processes is gaining popularity,
and the use of heat pumps in buildings has become more widespread. The current sales of heat pumps
and renewable heating equipment represented 10% in 2018, while more than 50% of sales was fossil-
fuel equipment. Less eﬃcient conventional electric heating equipment stands for 30% sales, The SDS
target requires a 25% of sales in 2030 to be in renewable technologies, and 33% after 2030.

2.1
Heat pump principle
This section will brieﬂy describe the principle of the heat pump, and more speciﬁcally the two-staged
open intercooler heat pump, as this is the most common choice for district heating, due to the high
temperature lift required- and is also the type which is used for the case study.
The heat pump is a unit which in principle moves heat from a low temperature level to a higher, by
utilising the thermophysical properties of ﬂuids to change its phase-change temperature between liquid-
and gaseous state, by change of pressure. The pressure can either be increased by a compressor, which
requires energy to perform a work on the ﬂuid, this energy can either be electric or by internal com-
bustion. Opposite can the pressure can be lowered by an expansion device, as e.g. expansion valve.
Between the heat source- and receiver is the phenomenon of latent heat for phase change utilised for
carrying the heat energy. The ﬁgure 2 below presents a sketch of the simple one-staged heat pump.
Figure 2: Single stage heat pump
The one-staged compressor consists of four main components as seen on the ﬁgure 2, the compres-
sor(), condenser(), expansion() valve and the evaporator(). The diﬀerent stages are listed below;
1-2: Compression of gaseous refrigerant, increasing pressure and temperature.
2-3: Condensation of the gaseous refrigerant into liquid, which releases the latent heat from the refrig-
erant to the heat receiver, e.g. district heating network.
3-4: Expansion of liquid refrigerant into vapour phase (Throttling), only lowering the pressure.
4-1: Evaporation from vapour phase into gaseous, this requires the refrigerant to receive heat at the
amount of latent heat.
2.1.1
Two staged heat pump w. open intercooler
For ammonia the two staged cycle is used instead of the one staged if the pressure ratio between the
evaporator and condenser is greater than 8. If the heat pump cycle has highly varying loads, and re-
peatedly turned oﬀand on, is the closed intercooler advised [14]. The two staged heat pump serves
the same principle as the one staged. The diﬀerence is that the refrigerant is compressed twice, and an
intercooler is introduced between these two. First a low pressure compressor, and then a high pressure
compressor. This means that more work can be added to the refrigerant, increasing the temperature for
the condenser. The intercooler cools the refrigerant from the condenser, where it is throttled before-
hand. The ﬁgure ?? below presents a sketch of the simple one-staged heat pump

2.2
Properties of air as heat source
Diﬀerent heat sources are to be considered for use in a heat pump. These diﬀerent sources can be
rated in terms of their heat capacity together with their energy density and variations throughout the
year. Besides this, is it also important to consider its availability, both in terms of location for district
heating distribution but also the expected cost of the energy extraction.
Ambient air is a heat source of relatively low energy density in comparison to the other heat sources.
Though ambient air is a resource which is available everywhere, this can be considered the greatest
advantage of this source, giving it a signiﬁcant potential. An estimation of the required airﬂow for
a heat pump of 1 MW scale, would be of approximately 300.000 m3 / hr; such volume ﬂow would
furthermore have huge requirements for the heat absorber surface area and fan power.
The temperature of the ambient air is low during the heating season, while being high during the sum-
mer period - where almost no district heating is required. This variation of source temperature aﬀects
the heat pump COP, whereas other sources might have a more stable performance curve. Figure 4
presents the mean outdoors ambient air temperature in Denmark for the period of 2006 – 2015, mea-
sured at 2 meters above ground level.

presents relatively high air humidity on average of 80%, with the highest humidity during the winter
period / heating season. To better understand the variation in humidity and its inﬂuence, it is impor-
tant recognise the phenomenon in moist air.
Air has the property of holding water vapour, where warmer air is able to contain more than colder.
This can be measured as the humidity ratio, describing the mass of water vapour in the humid air –
to the mass of the dry air [W = mw/ma], or as a ratio between the partial pressure of water vapour
in the air – to the partial pressure of the dry air. [W = 0.62198pw/(pa −pw)]. The vapour content
is also measured in percentage as the Relative humidity (RH), being the ratio of the partial pressure
of the water vapour, to the equilibrium vapour pressure of water, at a given temperature. A maximum
amount of water vapour in the air can be achieved when the saturation pressure of the water vapour is
reached for the given temperature. –Which would be a relative humidity of 100%. This phenomenon
of humidity is best explained through a psychrometric chart. Such chart is presented on section 5.4,
ﬁgure 20.
If the ﬁnned-tube outer surface reaches sub-zero temperatures, then the condensed water deposits on
the surface will freeze. The frost growth on the outer surface of the heat absorber is a side eﬀect of the
humid air. These deposits of frost will reduce the eﬀectiveness of the heat pump, which is the most
severe consequence of humid air. It is therefore required for air sourced heat pumps to implement a
strategy for defrost, to avoid high performance degradation and ensure a cost-eﬃcient heat production.
In combination with defrosting it is also important to ensure that the heat demand is fully covered.
From this it is often suggested to deploy more heat absorbing units than the demand, and to ensure

2.2.1
Pros and cons
The low temperatures during the heating season make the eﬀectiveness of air as a heat source lower
than other sources. Though air can be an adequate heat source for areas where there are no other heat
source alternatives. In addition are the initial costs for an ambient air sourced heat pump signiﬁcantly
lower than for other sources [13]. This can thereby make up for the low eﬀectiveness during the winter
period.
Pros of air as heat source
1. Accessible heat source and available everywhere
2. Widely used technology on smaller scale, though still limited on larger scale
3. Simpler regulatory process, than sources as e.g. groundwater.
Cons of air as heat source
1. Low temperatures during the heating season
2. Required defrosting, which reduces the eﬀectiveness at temperatures below 5oC
3. The fans for sustaining air ﬂow can induce noise, which has to be accounted for during initial
planning. This might be a limitation within regulatory areas as urban.
4. Larger surface real estate is required for the heat absorbers, [13] estimates an area of 102m2 /
MW.

Operational strategy
Drainage is mounted on the roof to collect defrost melt water, while also being heated during the de-
frosting period. The system is able to adjust its fan speed depending on the temperature diﬀerence be-
tween in- and outlet glycol temperature, and has recently introduced the use of HBDF-MK2 conductor
defrost sensors. This was though not in use during the visit. Their defrost strategy is following:
A defrost-cycle is initiated if the temperature diﬀerence between glycol-in and outlet is < 4oC. Hot
glycol of 25oC is then pumped through the heat absorber, until the glycol outlet temperature is > 5oC.
A dry out phase is then initiated for 300 seconds with fans at 100%, before normal operation is re-
stored. All of the absorbers are defrosted at once, and fans are set to 10%, creating an air cushion be-
low. The system have been designed for future implementation of individual defrosting.

4.1
Heat pump systems, Direct & Indirect
This paper looks into ambient air being the heat source for a large scaled heat pump. The air itself is
a low temperature heat source, and requires therefore the heat pump to lift its temperature up to the
standards of the district heating network. In Denmark this temperature can depend on the region, but
ranges from [70 - 90]oC. [22]
When ambient air is used as the heat source to the heat pump, are large heat absorbers required. For
these purposes dry-coolers are used to absorb the ambient air temperature, see section 2.2.
In general there exist two types of heat pump conﬁgurations; the direct and the in-direct. Direct-system
means that there is a direct heat exchange between the heat source and the heat pump primary refrig-
erant (e.g. ammonia / R717), meaning that the heat absorber is an evaporator. For the in-direct-system
exist the heat absorber in a secondary loop instead, this contains a secondary refrigerant, often a brine
(e.g. Ethylene Glycol). Brine is a term for a ﬂuid of mixture between water and another substance
with properties that result in a lowering or raising of the freezing point for the mixture. A heat ex-
changer is then coupled in-between the secondary loop and the heat pump evaporator containing the
primary refrigerant. In short this means that the in-direct system requires a lower primary refrigerant
charge than the direct, but the in-direct has a lowered eﬃciency due to the addition of an intermediate
heat exchanger. The two system principles are sketched on the ﬁgure 9
Figure 9: Sketch of Direct and Indirect system principle
A techno-economic analysis of the diﬀerences between the two systems has been conducted by [14].
The primary outline diﬀerences between the two systems are presented below;
• The indirect would properly has a short initial period since its operation has been tested by its
manufacturer.
• The direct system has a possible better heat transfer coeﬃcient.
• The initial cost is lower for the indirect system.
• The direct system is 100,000DKK cheaper compared to the indirect.

4.2
Plate ﬁnned-tube heat absorber
A heat exchanger is used for absorbing the heat from the ambient air, and transferring this heat to the
heat pump itself. This heat exchanger is referred to as the heat absorber.
The most common way of exchanging heat between gases and liquids are through a ﬁnned-tube ex-
changer. Here the refrigerant ﬂows inside the tubes and the air passes on the outside surface of the
tubes and ﬁns. The ﬁns attached to the tubes acts as an extended heat transferring surface, which en-
hances the contact area between air and refrigerant. This section describes the ﬁnned-tube geometry
and its variation. This is a technology that has been used for many years, and most commonly in re-
frigeration, where it is referred to as an air cooler, or gas cooler.
The large scale heat pump requires a heat absorber of suﬃcient surface area, and airﬂow. The current
market solutions to this are dry coolers. These are large ﬁnned-tube geometries designed for removal
of excess heat through air cooling, rejecting heat to the surroundings; refrigeration purpose. These
units are instead used as heat absorbers, were the process is reversed, taking heat from the air. Two
common designs of the dry cooler are shown in the ﬁgure 10, a V-shaped and a Flatbed – design.
Figure 10: G¨uentner GFD Dry cooler (V-shape coil) (Left ﬁgure) [3], Alfa Laval Blue BD Dry cooler (Flat-bed)
(Right ﬁgure) [4]
These units are standardised and prefabricated at a supplier, scaled to be transported in containers, and
serving an easy process of instalment on-site. This is one of the key beneﬁts on initial costs for using
ambient air as a heat source.
The working principles of the V-shaped and a Flatbed designs as heat absorbers are sketched on ﬁg-
ure 11. V-shaped sucks air in through the ﬁnned-tube side panels, which cools the air, and is then
discharged from the top passing the fan. Opposite does the ﬂatbed suck air in from the top, and dis-
charges it through the horizontally placed ﬁnned-tube panels. By having air suction placed higher than
the discharge, is the basic principle of buoyancy in hot and cool air utilised, in the avoidance of re-
circulating already cooled air. The challenge of re-circulation is analysed in the section 10.

4.2.2
Flow characteristics
Diﬀerent numerical studies have been conducted on the ﬁnned-tube geometry for understanding of
the ﬂow characteristics as air passes by the structure, e.g. [24]. The ﬂow characteristics stand as an
important part in the heat transfer, which can be discretised into local regions for thorough modelling,
though for this paper the heat transfer modelling is based on the Kim Yun Webb [25], [26] correlations
which represents a mean of the heat transfer over the geometry.
In the perspective of frosting is the phenomenon of ﬂow separation at the leading ﬁn edge an impor-
tant factor for understanding of the local frost growth on the ﬁnned-structure. The louver ﬁns, oﬀ-set

strip ﬁns, and any surface interruption serve the purpose of breaking the ﬂow boundary layer; this re-
sult in a thinner boundary layer, which enhances the heat transfer coeﬃcient, with the inclusion of an
increase in friction factor [27]. An unwanted eﬀect of any surface interruption is the three parts; lo-
cal separation, recirculation and reattachment of the ﬂow, as this may increase the friction factor more
than heat transfer [27]. The ﬁgure 13 inspired from [27] represents a sketch of the ﬂow phenomenon
with the diﬀerent phases.

As a ﬂow encounters an abrupt leading edge, ﬂow separation will occur as it can not turn the sharp
corner of the ﬁn thickness. This creates a small local region of recirculation right after the edge- also
called a separation bubble. At this region the ﬂow is relatively slow moving in a large eddy(vortex)
pattern [27]. The heat transfer is therefore low at this region, with the inclusion of a thermal isolation
due to the recirculation. It may be discussed how this region at low temperature conditions would in-
crease the frost growth due to the mostly stationary conditions. Above this is a free shear layer, which
is highly unstable, and it can therefore be expected that the velocity ﬂuctuation will be developed in
this region. These ﬂuctuations or perturbations will be transported downstream to the reattachment
region and result in a higher heat transfer due to turbulent mixture.
If the ﬂow does not reattach to the surface after separation, a wake is formed. This would increase
drag, and thus pressure loss which would increase fan power usage with no additional heat transfer
beneﬁt [27]. For systems of very low pumping power, it is as a general rule advised to avoid ﬂow sep-
aration by surface design. For the plate ﬁnned-tube heat exchanger under frosting conditions it can
be seen as any design with high possibility of frost clogging, therefore is any discrete surface design
advised against [23].

Development of quasi-static heat transfer model
A product of this thesis is the development of a quasi-static heat transfer model for the plate ﬁnned-
tube geometry. Speciﬁcally with the intention of modelling large scale ambient air sourced heat pumps,
under cold ambient temperatures, ranging from about 15oC to −10oC, with air reaching up to 100%
relative humidity. The model is therefore required to take condensation of water into account, and un-
der frosting conditions be able to model the frost growth on the ﬁnned surface. The result of this is to
be shown as performance degradation over time, following the ambient condition.
This section describes the general code structure of the developed model, which has been developed
within the Python environment. This is followed up by a theoretical section, which describes the meth-
ods and equations behind the code.
The model was intended to be developed in EES, though complications occurred with reading and
writing to multiple ﬁles, furthermore limited to the features of the programming language and limited
variable count. The choice was therefore to model in the free programming language Python, this can
be supplemented by the CoolProp [28] library which is a ﬂuid property library. This includes; cor-
relations of properties for ideal gasses, incompressible ﬂuids, brines, psychrometric properties and
much more. – This substitutes to some extent the use of EES. - The model is developed in Python
3.7.1 through the Spyder 3.3.2 interpreter run from the Anaconda Navigator [29].
The developed model is designed to simulate an indirect heat absorber system, where a brine is used
as the heat absorbing ﬂuid. The model is primarily designed to evaluate the V-shaped heat absorbers,
but limited to a simple pass pattern. The limitations of the model are to be found in the correlations
used for calculation of heat transfer, as these are based empirical research.
1. Input time dependent measurements
2. Input Geometric parameters
3. Conduct simulation
4. Result presentation

the model to work, these parameters are required to be;
• Refrigerant inlet temperature (Tc,i)
• Ambient air inlet temperature (Th,i) and humidity (RHi)
• Mass ﬂow of the air passing though the fan ( ˙mh)
• Refrigerant mass ﬂow ( ˙mc)
These parameters must be received as an .xlsx or .CSV ﬁle, which have been logged over a time pe-
riod. This input is conﬁgured in an Excel-sheet that has been developed as the GUI interface for the
model. This interface connects the time period parameters, with the geometric parameters, and the
simulation conﬁguration, all in one place.

5.2
Code description
The heat transfer model is designed to split the plate ﬁnned-tube heat exchanger into a smaller more
manageable section, instead of the unit as a whole. This is possible as the unit is divided into several
sections of tube rows, which are then aligned parallel to each other on the horizontal plane. Figure 18
shows how the heat absorber is splitted up into its elements.
The model takes a single row of tubes from the transverse direction, and follows this longitudinally
with the number of passes.
Figure 18: Sketch of how the heat absorbers plate ﬁnned-tube geometry is splitted up in a smaller portion
This row of tubes is then divided into smaller elements of single ﬁn- and each tube, this is the deﬁned
heat transferring area to be modelled. The modelling procedure is a an iterative process, ﬁrst stepping
through- ﬁn for ﬁn. This is required to know how much heat is transferred from the air to the refrig-
erant within the tube, and how much the air have been cooled, before entering the neighbouring pass
region. Due to computational load of this process, a factor of resolution has been included, referred to
as “res”. This collects a number of ﬁns, for each calculation and reduces the number of computational
steps e.g. a total amount of 1620 ﬁns = 1620 steps, can with a resolution of 20 be reduced to 81 steps.
It is though important when scaling the resolution, not to compromise the result quality over computa-
tion time. The iterative process is explained later in this section.
Structure
The program is initialised by retrieving the information from the GUI; this is the geometric input and
time step ﬁles. From the GUI can a time interval for logged measurements be deﬁned, from which
also a certain time step size is required. The program is therefore initialised to prepare a number of
lookup ﬁles with the logged measurement data for the speciﬁed time step within the interval. These
lookup ﬁles are then loaded accordingly with the quasi-static model. The geometric parameters for the
selected heat absorber are constants and therefore only loaded once. The outer dimensions of these
can be updated during the iterative model, accounting for the frost deposit thickness.
An overall for-loop is then established, which runs through the entire program. This loop steps through
all of the created time-step data ﬁles; which is being called at the beginning of each loop, together
with the accumulated values from the previous time step. These are water and frost masses together
with the thickness of the frost zfrost & water thickness zw. Here a water ﬁlm surface is assumed. This
is added to the external structure, aﬀecting the heat transfer properties, as seen on eq. 1 & 2

Friction factor and pressure loss
Mean values are used for calculation of the friction factor and the pressure drop as seen below;
• Mean cold side temperature, the tube temperature averaged between two neighbouring elements
on the same pipe
• Mean hot side temperature, the air temperature averaged between the two neighbouring inter-
faces, row-wise.
• Mean surface temperature, set equal to the cold side mean.
• Mean outer diameter, a mean is made row-wise for the outer tube diameter, creating an array of
mean values.
• Mean ﬁn pitch, averaged row-wise creating an array
• Mean duct area, averaged row-wise creating an array
The reason for averaging the temperatures is an approximation for estimating the intermediate ﬂows.
Averaging the geometric sizes is due to the collected frost deposits, this is a simpliﬁcation which help
to capture the general state of the air ﬂow throughout the ﬁnned-tube depth.
Finding the pressure loss is an iterative process, constructed around a while loop. The loop contains
two parts, one calculating the friction factor from the outer plate ﬁnned tube geometry, which is found
by use of the Kim Yun Webb correlations [25], together with ﬁnding the pressure loss. –Another part
for calculating the pressure loss –and velocity relationship between the ﬁnned-tube inlet section and
the fan outlet, in terms of the fan power, presented on section 5.3 .
Air humidity
Air humidity is an important factor for the estimation of condensation. The cooling of the air which
happens by the heat absorber acts as a de-humidifying process. It is therefore required to estimate the
outlet air humidity from row to row as the air passes through the plate ﬁnned structure, each time be-
ing cooled further, with the possibility of precipitating water. This is based on the inlet air tempera-
ture, humidity and its mass ﬂow, together with the surface temperature and an estimate for how much
the air is cooled for each section. The theory behind condensation calculation is explained in section
5.4.

With all of the sections above deﬁned, can the main iterative heat transfer model can be initiated. The
process is required to iterate as the ﬂow is co-current and therefore based on initial guess values. For
each iteration are the initial guess values updated until the result residuals reaches a certain criteria of
precision. The iteration follows down to a precision of 10−3.
The model is created as two loops which collectively follows the cold ﬂow around from its inlet; a for-
loop runs along the tube length “for x in range(0,Finsres)” and another in the depth, following the
number of passes “for y in range(0,rows)”. Following this structure the heat transfer is calculated for
each ﬁn and stepped to the neighbouring ﬁn element.
When the set precision is reached, the iterative process ends. From the heat transfer calculation have
condensate mass, and frost mass on the outer surface been calculated. These are then summed up and
added to any previous deposit mass of water or frost, as an accumulation. The frost thickness is also
deﬁned here, dependent on the frost density, which has been set constant. See section 2.3.1 for frost
density approximation.
At the end of each time step calculation are all of the relevant results stored into a large matrix and
saved. These time step result ﬁles are accessed after calculations and collected for post processing to
plot the overall results.

Pressure loss
This section presents the correlations used for estimation of the pressure loss throughout the plate
ﬁnned-tube geometry, in relationship to the fan power and ﬂow velocity. This is made as a while-
loop to ensure convergence between the two parts. This means that ﬁrst a pressure loss is found for

Defrosting model for plate ﬁnned tubes
7.1
Defrosting methods
The purpose for defrosting is to restore the degraded performance of the heat absorber; this is done
either to restore its capacity to deliver the required heat demand, or to reduce excessive energy usage
with poor eﬃciency. The process of defrosting works by melting the accumulated frost deposits on the
plate ﬁnned-tube heat absorber surface, and afterwards to remove as much melt water as possible, to
avoid this from freezing immediately again after the defrost cycle, when normal operation is resumed.
Under high heat demand is a quick defrost cycle desired to minimize valuable downtime, it is therefore
important to choose an eﬃcient defrost method, with small loss.
For retroﬁtting frost prevention methods please see the paper [14], which goes in detail with a techno
economical perspective. Only operational defrost strategy is covered in this current paper.
The process of defrosting can be divided into diﬀerent stages, Muehlbauer 2006; Donnellan 2007.
proposed a model of ﬁve stages; Pre-defrost, Pre-heat, Melting and Permutation, Dry out, and Re-
cooling [37]
Pre-defrost
In advance of the defrost is the system operation measured to detect reduced eﬃciency. This is com-
monly indicated as a higher pressure drop over the airside, temperature diﬀerentials, COP reduction,
optical- or resistance sensor and time (pre-set operating period). Thresholds are set for these features,
which would initiate the defrost-cycle.
Pre-heat
When the defrosting is initiated, is the cold refrigerant ﬂow terminated, and the heat absorber is heated.
This can e.g. be done by one of the following methods; refrigerant replaced by hot-gas from bypass
(direct systems), reversed cycle or electrically. The airﬂow is typically shut oﬀto minimize energy
loss as any latent and sensible heat ingression into the defrosting domain. The applied energy is ﬁrst
absorbed by the ﬁnned-tube heat absorber structure itself and the outer frost layer, increasing their
temperatures. This happens until the melting point of ice is reached. During all of this is some energy
lost through convective and conductive heat transfer to the surrounding structure, while a small per-
centage of the water from the frost is sublimed into the surrounding air [37].
Melting and Permutation
As the heating process continues, the temperature on the interface between the ﬁnned-tube and the
frost layer will rise above the freezing point of ice, and begin to melt the frost deposit layer in contact.
As frost is melted, meltwater will start to permeate into and saturate the remaining frost layer, as this
is a porous medium of ice crystals and air. The addition of water to the frost layer beneﬁts the heat
transfer, increasing the defrost speed. During this process the surface temperature will ﬁnd equilib-
rium and remain mostly constant at about 0C, until all of the frost in surface contact has melted [36].
When the frost layer becomes saturated, will the surplus melt water begin to drain from the structure.
Once the frost at the interface between ﬁnned-tube surface and frost has melted away, is the frost layer
adhesion signiﬁcantly reduced; this increases the chance of slumping / sloughing of the remaining
frost layer, in which large sections of frost layers peels oﬀthe ﬁnned surface without melting ??. Any
discharge of melt water or frost debris should be drained away. The slumping of frost drastically im-
proves the defrosting eﬃciency, reducing the required latent amount heat for melting. Nearly all of the
heat supplied is absorbed by the melting frost layer, where little is lost to the environment through.
Dry out
The temperature of the ﬁnned-tube surface will increase again as the majority of the frost layer has
been detached from the ﬁnned surface. The remains of this is melt water deposits on the surface which
does not overcome the surface tension, together with some loose frost deposits. This is referred to as

==PAGE:69==

the dry-out stage where heat is kept transferred, a small part of the melt water remaining will slowly
be vaporised, and lost to the surroundings as latent heat. Furthermore is a large amount of heat is lost
to the environment through convection. The dry-out phase has the lowest defrost eﬃciency. For the
heat absorbers of the large scaled heat pump systems, is the fan turned on again to increase the blow-
out eﬀect, while heat is still applied. It is important to stop the dry-out process when it is determined
to be suﬃciently dry, to avoid any excess energy use, though not too early as any remaining deposit
would instantly freeze when the defrost-cycle is ended, speeding up the whole frosting process.
Re-cooling
The defrost-cycle is ended by some threshold, measured by e.g. ﬁnned surface temperature, defrost-
ﬂuid-return temperature, conductivity, time, etc. This threshold should deﬁne a heat absorber sur-
face which has been suﬃciently cleared of water- and frost deposits. Normal operation is resumed;
if any defrost ﬂuid, this present, this is substituted by cold refrigerant. The air ﬂow is typically shut oﬀ
when the heat absorber is pre-cooled [37] to reduce any heat rejection of sensible and latent heat. The
remaining heat in the ﬁnned-tube material is absorbed by the refrigerant, initially reducing the heat
pump load.
7.2
Defrost control
Diﬀerent methods exist on detecting frosting, and to determine the optimal time to initiate defrosting,
along with when the defrost cycle is complete. These can be categorized as direct and indirect tech-
niques. The direct method measures the actual frost deposit as optical characteristics or conductance.
Indirect are most commonly used, measuring properties as air pressure- and temperature diﬀerences
between ambient air in and outlet, refrigerant in-and outlet, fan power consumption, COP, time, etc.
and some control strategies have also been based on artiﬁcial neural networks [36]. The case which
happens to occur with the indirect method is a misprediction of the ﬁnished defrost state, resulting
in early ﬂow blockage and performance reduction. Because of this to ensure a proper defrosting it is
often set to deal with the worst-case scenario, resulting in either a too- frequent or long defrosting cy-
cle [36].
The simplest control strategy is the T-T based on Temperature and Time since last defrost-cycle; though
this strategy have shown to initiate unnecessary defrosting at some conditions, as the frost growth rate
is associated with the air humidity [36]
7.3
Defrosting methods
The defrosting of a heat pump can be done by numerous methods. This section will brieﬂy describe
some of the most commonly used.
7.3.1
Passive defrosting
For this defrost-cycle is the heat absorber shut oﬀ, meaning that refrigerant ﬂow is stropped. This
method requires a sub-zero ambient air temperature, and it is recommended not to be applied for tem-
peratures below 1 degree [37]. The fan is kept on, to move this sub-zero temperature past the ﬁnned-
tube structure, melting the frost deposits. The advantage of this method is the low operational cost,
and simple control method, which makes this widely applied [38]. The downside is long defrost time,
as heat is absorbed by convection from the ambient air.
7.3.2
Electric heating defrosting
The electric defrosting is done with resistive electric heating of the ﬁnned-tube surface, to melt the
frost layer. During this is the heat absorber completely shut oﬀ, both refrigerant ﬂow and air ﬂow [37].
The initial cost of installing this is low and can easily be retroﬁtted. Though this uses electrical energy
directly to melt the frost, which is high quality energy that can be spent better in the heat pump.

7.3.3
Hot gas bypass
The hot-gas bypass defrosting method is applied for the direct heat pump systems, and is the most
commonly used in industry [37]. The process takes the superheated refrigerant vapour which is dis-
charged from the compressor, and “bypassed” directly to the heat absorber being an evaporator. The
latent heat of condensation for the refrigerant vapour is used as a heat source, though sensible heat of
highly superheated refrigerant vapour can also be used [38]. The hot-gas bypass defrosting is slower
as it is the heat losses from running the compressor which is used [36]. There exist also a risk for the
compressor to suck in liquid if insuﬃcient energy supply should occur.
7.3.4
Reversed cycle
For the reversed cycle is the heat pump operated opposite, where either the heat absorber outside is
considered as a condenser, or for the indirect system will the intermediate heat exchanger acts as a
condenser. This can be reversed by using a four-way valve. This solution does not require any more
space and can easily be installed , in addition of being quick and eﬃcient [36]. Though in large scaled
heat pumps for district heating is the heating energy consumed from the DHW, reducing the heat sup-
ply.
7.3.5
Indirect source
The indirect source defrosting is used for the indirect large scaled heat pump. This is a defrosting loop
which through a dedicated heat exchanger is heated by an external heat source, being e.g. return- or
forward DHW, or any other waste heat source. This heated defrosting loop is connected to the sec-
ondary indirect loop through valves, ﬂushing out the cold refrigerant from the heat absorbers. As with
the reversed cycle the heat energy is consumed from the source, which in DHW reduces the heat sup-
ply.
Most commonly for large scaled heat pumps in DHW is a hot water storage tank connected, this acts
as a buﬀer on the DHW distribution network, and therefore not aﬀecting the costumer heat quality.

Defrost Model
Defrost/melting/dry out is combination of both heat and mass transfer. As mentioned in chapter 7.1
the defrost process consist of; pre-defrost, preheat, melting/permutation and dry out. The Defrost
model focus on preheat stage and melting and permutation stage. To simulate the defrost process of
a absorbers a 3d model of a melting ﬁn and pipe is made by use of conductance method (see chapter
8). From this solution the melting process history is stored and used to simulate a large number of ﬁns
melting at the same time with varying defrost ﬂuid temperature through the pipe.
9.1
Defrost of a single ﬁn and Pipe
The melting process is a dynamic problem and impossible to solve algebraic. By use of Conductance
method a quasi-static numerical solution of the mass and heat transfer during defrost is made. The
procedure is illustrated in ﬁgure 41.
Figure 41: Iterative procedure
The model consist of tree separate meshes; inside pipe, outside pipe and ﬁn attached to the pipe. The
meshes will be made in such way that it allows for summation of energy ﬂows and still secure energy
conservation (1.a). Each domain has additionally its own property matrices, and boundary conditions.
Calculation of heat ﬂows are done for all domains (2.a),(2.b),(2.c). For the frosted surface attached
to the outer pipe wall and the ﬁn the melting phase happens. This is carefully controlled and will be
explained later (3.a,3.b). Knowing the heat ﬂows in all domains the heat summation can be made (4).

9.1.5
Melting Process
The preheat stage also called diﬀusion stage is the time period where metal and frost are heated un-
til the Ice temperature reaches melting point 0 [Co] . After the frost temperature reaches 0 [Co] the
melting process initiate. During the melting period, melt water permeate the porous frost surface so
the frost layer consist of a water-ice mixture. Properties of the frost layer changes signiﬁcantly, which
result in changes in heat transfer from the defrost ﬂuid. The temperature of the frost layer can be esti-
mated to remain at 0 [Co] through the melting period. Water will drain from the frost layer when the
ice is saturated. The temperature of water leaving the frost surface can be estimated to be close to 0
[Co].
In the model these consideration mentioned above are controlled by use of if-statements and for-loops
checking all cells in the frost layer on both ﬁn and pipe. The melting stage control is divided into 2 if-
statements. One controlling the melting part and one controlling the drainage of water after melting.
In the report only control for the ﬁn is shown, but the principle is the same for the outer pipe domain.

Discussion

sistance between the frost layer and the metal. Another possible reason for the deviation could be the
assumption of constant properties of the EG-mixture.The viscousity of the EG mixture more that dou-
bles from 30 [oC] to 0 [oC] [23]. From perspective the heat transfer coeﬃcient will be rapidly lowered
due to the Reynolds number.

CFD model for recirculation
One of the challenges the large scaled ambient air sourced heat pump faces is the phenomenon of
recirculation of air. This means that some of the cold air exhaust from the heat absorber is being re-
turned into the intake of the heat absorber. Receiving this cooled air, instead of the warmer ambient
air degrades the system performance, -as a higher workload is put on the compressor to compensate
by a suﬃcient DeltaT. This phenomenon is recognized by the industry, though have not yet been in-
vestigated thoroughly. The risk of possible recirculation increases with the amount of heat absorber
units installed together, as the ambient heat source might become limited, by the “pollution” of cold
air. This section looks into the possibility for using CFD as a tool for modelling of recirculation. For
this have two V-shaped heat absorbers been modelled in CAD. This model is then imported into the
commercial CFD programme StarCCM+, where the investigation is conducted.
Method
The CFD model is created to investigate how ambient wind from diﬀerent directions would aﬀect the
inlet temperature for the two heat absorbers. The two V-shaped units are placed parallel to each other
at a given distance. Three cases are created for distances of [4 , 5 , 8] meters in-between the units. For
these three cases are the wind speed and direction varied independently. Three wind speeds [5 , 10 ,
15] m/s are modelled for each direction and each parallel distance. The wind directions in reference
to the units are, parallel along its length, perpendicular to the side and a 45 degree angler in-between
these two. The ﬁgure 54 below presents a sketch of the measurement domain expressed in compass
directions, and including all of the abovementioned cases.
Figure 54: CFD model
The ﬂuid simulated is air and at low velocities, and assumed to be steady. The ﬂow is therefore as-
sumed to be compressible, and therefore available for changes in density, which is essential for mod-
elling the small temperature diﬀerences of the ﬂow. A segregated ﬂuid temperature model is conse-
quently also used, meaning that it solves the total energy equation, having temperature as the indepen-
dent variable. Gravity is also taken into account, supporting the model with natural buoyancy within
the air.
A hexahedral mesh type is created for the volume domain and a quadrilateral surface mesh for the heat
absorbers. The mesh is then reﬁned for increased accuracy close around the absorbers, with a slightly
lower mesh resolution further away to capture any wake. The surface mesh is shown on ﬁgure 55

12. DISCUSSION
June 14, 2019
12
Discussion
The heat transfer model made for simulating the absorber has an accuracy within 20% when calcu-
lating the energy production. From parametric analysis of; ﬁn pitch, secondary ﬂuid mass ﬂow, fan
power input and change of operating ∆T there will exist a optimal design of the heat absorber to gain
a high capacity. A small ﬁn pitch will enhance the energy production but too small ﬁn pitch results
in short production period due to high frost sensitivity. A high mass ﬂow of secondary refrigerant do
also increase the heat production, bu again this would also increase frost growth. By use of a small
operational ∆T the production time can be extended for a very long period and sometimes without
any frost growth at all. This open for the use of variable speed compressors and fans so the production
adjust according to the condition.
For the defrosting model it was validated by use of measurements in Sig and it was around 4-5 min-
utes to quick. The model still showed expected tendencies when doing a parameter analysis. Here it
was clear that use defrost ﬂuid with large energy density is crucial to the time of defrosting. By hav-
ing a short absorber with regards to individual pipe length the time to defrost would also be shorten.
Lastly the choice of secondary is important to consider with care. Most secondary ﬂuid has rapid
changes in properties in the temperature interval [0 −30oC] and this may inﬂuence the heat trans-
ferring capabilities during defrosting.

13. CONCLUSION
June 14, 2019
13
Conclusion
The subject of large scaled ambient air sourced heat pumps is a wide area, with diﬀerent challenges
which in this have tried to be enlightened. During this period have two models been created as tools
for crucial understanding and future optimization of the heat absorber system. These tools help to es-
tablish a picture of the inﬂuence of operating under frosting conditions, and how this can impact the
heat absorber performance. In conclusion have an in-direct quasi-static discrete heat transfer model
been developed for simulation of under the Danish winter weather conditions, and has been validated
against the Sig-indirect heat pump. The validation shows a deviation of +/- 20% from the operational
data, in terms of heat energy production, showing the inﬂuence of diﬀerent frost densities. The model
does not take weather conditions as rain, wind or sun into account.
A FEM discrete plate ﬁnned-tube model has been developed for a detailed simulation of the defrosting
process, following through the diﬀerent stages of pre-heating, melting and natural convection when
the frost is melted. The FEM model is collected through averaged heat ﬂow polynomials into a larger
collection of ﬁns for simulation of an entire row of ﬁns. The model uses constant properties for the
defrost ﬂuid, and approximates on-site measurements close to 5 minutes of precision; though only
validated for one scenario.
