Abstract
In this master thesis, a free-ﬁeld approach in estimating the acoustic characteristics
of a large material sample is investigated. A spherical array of microphones is used
to recover spatial information of the sound ﬁeld. The studied sound ﬁeld is composed
of a direct and a reﬂected component, which originate respectively from a source and
an image source. This sound ﬁeld is represented as a set of equivalent sources, which
are chosen so as to cover all directions in space while including good approximations
of the two sources. This allows a sparse representation of the setup, which justiﬁes
the use of compressive sensing to express the sound ﬁeld with the equivalent sources.
As a next step, the equivalent sources enable to reconstruct the sound ﬁeld on the
surface of the tested material, from which the surface impedance, the reﬂection factor
and the absorption coeﬃcient can be derived.
The simulations and measurements performed in this project showed the potential of
such a technique, which is characterised by a robust reconstruction of the sound ﬁeld
and a satisfactory estimation of the acoustic properties of the sample. Nevertheless,
some limitations were also identiﬁed. Firstly, the setup proved to be sensitive to
noise especially at low frequencies and it requires extensive knowledge of the back-
ground noise for optimal results. Moreover, improvements are possible if a better
knowledge of the sound ﬁeld is obtained, for instance in the wave reﬂection model or
the consideration of the absorber’s size.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The acoustic qualities of a room depend not only on its geometry but also on the
acoustical properties of the materials present in it. They are usually described in
terms of absorption and reﬂection, by comparing the incident sound ﬁeld to the re-
ﬂected one. These properties depend on frequency and the direction of incoming
waves. Additionally, a given material can also be characterised in terms of surface
impedance. The knowledge of such acoustic parameters can be used in simulations
to predict and optimise the sound quality of a room.
However such measurements prove to be quite limited. In terms of absorption coeﬃ-
cient, only two methods are standardised: using an impedance tube or a reverberation
chamber. The main drawback of these methods is that they both require the proper
equipment, and a sample of the tested material. That is why extensive research is
being carried out in order to develop a non-destructive way to measure the absorption
in-situ. Moreover, the angle dependence of the absorption coeﬃcient is not recovered,
thus leading to a partial knowledge of the actual inﬂuence of the tested material on
the sound ﬁeld.
In-situ measurements imply an extensive knowledge of the sound ﬁeld, which can be
acquired with the use of an array of microphones. Such arrays enable the simulta-
neous measurement of the sound ﬁeld in diﬀerent directions, thus providing better
spatial information. In the scope of this project, a spherical microphone array is
positioned near a large sample of a material to be tested. The measured pressures
at every microphone are used to express the sound ﬁeld as a sum of elementary
waves, which are then combined to reconstruct the sound ﬁeld on the material. This
method is knows as the Equivalent Source Method (ESM). The acoustical properties
are ﬁnally derived from the reconstructed ﬁeld on the tested sample. The sound
ﬁeld being ideally composed of only the incident contribution and the reﬂected one,
a sparse solution is expected, therefore the setup corresponds well to the framework
of compressive sensing. Compressive sensing consists of recovering the signal under
the assumption that it is sparse, which allows a more precise and more robust solution.
In this project, the ESM is implemented using a compressive sensing algorithm in
order to reconstruct the sound ﬁeld on the material sample and infer its acoustical
properties. The method is tested under diﬀerent setups in order to validate the cal-

2.2.3
Matrix form
In both cases, the resulting pressure is the product of a term depending on the
problem setup and a term representing the source strength (p0 for the plane wave
ﬁeld, Q for the spherical ﬁeld). If the sound ﬁeld is composed of L sources, the result
is a linear combination of the contribution of these L sources. Finally, if the array is
composed of K microphones, the pressure is measured at K diﬀerent positions. The
problem can therefore be interpreted as a matrix system, in the form:
pmeasured = Hp0,
(2.2.15)
where pmeasured is a vector of size K containing the measured pressures on the
array’s microphones, p0 is the vector of size L representing the sources’ strengths
(pressure amplitude for plane wave sources, volume velocity for point sources), and
H is a K × L transfer matrix between the two vectors. H depends on such parame-
ters as the sources’ types, the sources’ positions, the array’s size and the considered
frequency.

2.3
Sound Field Reconstruction
The idea behind the Equivalent Source Method (ESM) is to choose an arbitrary set
of L equivalent sources supposed to account for the K pressure measurements on
the spherical array. In this project, the equivalent sources are either point sources
or plane waves uniformly distributed over all possible angles in order to characterise
all directions of arrival. Inverting the material system (2.2.15) then yields the source
strengths which would result in the measurements on the K microphones. The so-
lution of this system can then be used to reconstruct the sound ﬁeld, and to infer
properties such as the surface impedance of a material. This, however, is an under-
determined problem, as the number of equivalent sources L is usually much higher
than the number of measurements K. This means that several p0 vectors are solu-
tions. Several ways of solving the problem and selecting the best ﬁtting solution are
possible.

Absorption coeﬃcient
The absorption coeﬃcient α of a material is deﬁned as the ratio between the acous-
tic power at this material and the incident acoustic power. It represents the energy
which was absorbed by the material, as opposed to the energy that was reﬂected
or transmitted.
Contrary to the surface impedance and the reﬂection factor, the
absorption coeﬃcient is a real number, therefore the phase information is lost. It
ranges from 0 (corresponding to a totally reﬂecting surface) to 1 (a totally absorbing
surface). However, due to its explicit meaning, it is widely used to describe acoustic
materials in the industry.

2.4.3
Measurement method
Absorbers are generally characterised with their absorption coeﬃcients. Two stan-
dardised methods enable a limited measurement of this value, whereas a spherical
array measurement could yield a more complete understanding of the acoustical prop-
erties of the tested material.
Established methods to measure the absorption coeﬃcient
The only standardised methods to measure the absorption coeﬃcient of a given ma-
terial are the reverberation chamber method, standardised in [12] and the impedance
tube method standardised in [13].
The reverberation method consists in placing a sample of the tested absorber in a
reverberation room and measuring the reverberation time of the room with and with-
out the sample. The use of Sabine’s formula then yields an estimate of the random
incidence absorption coeﬃcient. However, the method assumes that the sound ﬁeld
is diﬀuse, which instores a lower frequency limit. Moreover the resulting absorption
coeﬃcient is sometimes higher than 1, thus underlining clear limitations.
In the impedance tube method, a sample is placed at the end of a tube, in which
two microphones are mounted. In the experimental conditions, the sound ﬁeld can
be considered as a plane wave ﬁeld. In a ﬁrst version, the tube is driven with a pure
tone, which enables one to measure the standing-wave ratio and to infer the absorp-
tion coeﬃcient. However, a second version known as the transfer function method, is
based on the calculation of the transfer function between the two microphones and
has the advantage of providing a measurement for a given frequency range instead
of a single frequency. The absorption coeﬃcient calculated with the impedance tube
is, however, the normal incidence absorption coeﬃcient (the angle dependence is not
taken into account). Furthermore, the method presents a lower and an upper fre-
quency limit.
13
==PAGE:36==

A common drawback of these standardised methods is that they are performed in a
laboratory with very speciﬁc equipment. Much research focuses on developing in-situ
methods to measure the absorption. A review of the diﬀerent techniques is presented
in [14], where diﬀerent approaches are distinguished. Firstly, some methods are based
on the study of temporal measurements, by separating the direct and the reﬂected
component, either using an impulsive sound source or with more advanced signal
processing techniques. On the other hand, research is done to derive the acoustical
properties from a model of the sound ﬁeld: in these approaches, the pressure and the
particle velocity are estimated on the absorber surface, assuming certain hypotheses
on the sound ﬁeld (for instance composed of plane waves). The measurements are
done with two or more microphones. The article also hints at alternative methods,
such as adapting the impedance tube technique to an in-situ conﬁguration, deriving
the impedance from an intensity measurement or using an array of microphones.
Calculation from the reconstructed ﬁeld
As presented in section 2.3, it is possible to reconstruct the sound ﬁeld from pressure
measurements on the spherical array at a close distance while compensating the eﬀect
of the array itself. The pressure and the particle velocity are reconstructed in a set
of points situated on the material’s surface. These points are situated close to each
other so that the calculated parameters can be averaged to increase the robustness
in actual measurements.
From the reconstructed pressures and particle velocities, the surface impedance is
calculated from (2.4.3). Then one can derive the plane wave reﬂection factor with
(2.4.6) and the spherical reﬂection factor with (2.4.7) if the source is a spherical one.
Finally, (2.4.9) is used to ﬁnd the absorption coeﬃcient.
The advantage of the method is that it enables a robust and complete recovery of
the sound ﬁeld, providing extensive information on the material’s properties. Fur-
thermore, the measurement takes into account the angle of incidence on the material,
thus allowing the calculation of the acoustic parameters at diﬀerent angles. Finally,
the non-destructivity and the fact that the equipment is limited to the spherical ar-
ray and the source shows potential to test materials in-situ, in their actual mounting
conditions.
It must nevertheless be noted that modeling the sound ﬁeld as the contribution of two
sources is a rather simplistic approach when dealing with spherical waves: diﬀraction
phenomena occur at the surface as well and local reaction needs to be assumed.
Moreover, in a real setup, the assumption of an inﬁnite absorber might not hold
anymore at low frequencies.

2.5. CONCLUDING REMARKS
2.5
Concluding remarks
The proposed method is based on reconstructing the sound ﬁeld as a sum of elemen-
tary waves from the pressures measured on the array. Using knowledge on the setup,
such as its sparsity, it is possible with a compressive sensing algorithm to obtain an
accurate model of the sound ﬁeld that can be reconstructed on the tested material’s
surface.
Provided the reconstruction is accurate, parameters such as the surface impedance
can be recovered with accuracy, thus enabling the characterisation of the material.
For comparison with traditional methods, the absorption coeﬃcient is also recovered.
Many advantages arise from this method, such as the possibility to study the angle
dependence of absorption properties.
Only the general concept has been introduced in this theoretical chapter.
Simu-
lations and measurements need to be done in order to study the eﬃciency of the
reconstruction and to determine the limitations of the calculation.

Simulation
The measurement of absorption with a spherical array was ﬁrstly put to test through
MATLAB simulations. The optimisation problem posed by (2.3.5) is handled by a
MATLAB toolbox called CVX and provided by Grant and Boyd [15]. This toolbox
is conceived to solve convex problems, which is the case of (2.3.5).
The method was ﬁrst tested with a simplistic plane wave ﬁeld, and then extended to
a sound ﬁeld due to a point source, which is more realistic. The diﬀerent parameters
intervening in the model were varied in order to draw some recommendations to
ensure an optimal reconstruction.
3.1
Simulation setup
This section summarises the choices made for the simulation, including the geomet-
rical setup, the material model and the parameters for the reconstruction method.
3.1.1
Array and absorber positions
The spherical array is modeled after the actual array used in the measurements; its
radius is 9.75 cm and it counts 64 microphones. The array was characterised with a
truncation order of 7, which is why the expansions in spherical harmonics are trun-
cated at N = 7.
The array is placed at the origin of the coordinate system, and the tested material
is represented as an inﬁnite horizontal plate, situated at a distance d = 20 cm from
the array’s center, as shown in Figure 3.1.

The sound ﬁeld is modeled as the combination of an incident ﬁeld originating from a

One idea to improve the low frequency result is to select only the highest coeﬃcients

3.5
Discussion
The simulations carried out throughout the project proved the feasibility of the re-
construction method using L1-regularisation. From a very simple setup where the
ﬁeld is composed of plane waves, the model was then made more complex, including
spherical waves and such parameters as a noise component in the measurements.
Even if the result can still be improved, a clear trend was identiﬁed in the calculation
of the absorption coeﬃcient. The method also allows the measurement of the ab-
sorption at diﬀerent angles of incidence, which is a clear advantage compared to the
traditional methods of absorption measurement. The theoretical frequency range,
assuming a Helmholtz number between 0.1 and 7 proved to be valid, except in the
presence of noise, which tended to disrupt the low frequency results. Nevertheless,
an accurate result could still be achieved if the noise level was not too high (a SNR of
30 dB seemed reasonable) and if the lower frequency limit was raised to about 200 Hz.
Moreover, it was recommended to avoid grazing incidence, as a good estimate was
ensured for incidence angles which were smaller than 50◦.
Still, the low frequency domain still requires more investigation, as it presents the
largest deviations. It was shown that these errors did not originate from the method
itself, but from the modeling of the reﬂection. Indeed, several approximations were
made in the calculation of the spherical reﬂection factor used to simulate the power
of the image source. Therefore, it is important to reﬁne the model in order to extend
it at lower frequencies. Once this more precise model is implemented, the reconstruc-
tion is expected to be ideal.
Finally, it should be highlighted that the model used in the simulation remains very
simplistic. The absorber is supposed to be inﬁnite, which is diﬃcult to achieve at low
frequencies in measurements. Furthermore, although a noise component was added
to the measured pressure, the actual noise is expected to be more complex in reality.
Another shortcoming is the simplicity of the sound ﬁeld in the model, which is simply
composed of an incident and a reﬂected ﬁeld, both being scattered by the rigid sphere.
However, additional diﬀraction components from the surrounding environment might
inﬂuence the sound ﬁeld. In the same way, back-scattering was neglected, which is a
fair assumption if the array is not too close to the material’s surface.
Overall, the simulation showed the model is eﬃcient and quite robust, provided the
sound ﬁeld model is improved.

array, but this limits the interest of averaging at lower frequencies. Finally, despite
what was recommended in the simulations chapter, the distance between the array
and the absorber is quite large (37 cm) due to the experimental setup’s limitations.
A closer distance would result in a more accurate reconstruction preventing such
instabilities.

4.2.7
Conclusions on the data processing
It was shown in this section that the calculation of the absorption coeﬃcient depends
mostly on the estimation of the noise ﬂoor.
Although no precise and robust method was found to predict it, a simple frequency
dependent estimate was used to account for the lower SNR at low and high frequen-
cies. Indeed, it is assumed that the SNR is equal to 28 dB except at low and high
frequencies where it is lowered to 20 dB. Additional corrections were made at fre-
quencies at which the SNR dropped.
Furthermore, the robustness to noise was improved by introducing a reweighting al-
gorithm favouring the sparsity of the solution.
The described process remains improvable in many aspects as no explicit relation was
found between the actual SNR and the estimated one. In addition, high frequency
estimates suﬀered from a too large reconstruction area. Nevertheless, it yields stable
and coherent results in most cases.

4.4. DISCUSSION
4.4
Discussion
The measurements showed that it is possible to recover a satisfactory estimate of the
surface impedance and the absorption coeﬃcient by sound ﬁeld reconstruction: the
trend indicated by the theoretical models is generally well followed. Some deviations
which were not predicted by the simulations could however be observed, due to the
experimental setup itself.
4.4.1
Absorber size
This point was identiﬁed as the major diﬀerence between the simulation and the
experimental setup. The simulations assumed an inﬁnite plate, whereas the absorbers
used in the measurements had a characteristic dimension of around 3 to 3.5 m, and
the closest edge to the array was at a distance of around 70 cm. Therefore, diﬀraction
eﬀects could be observed for frequencies below 500 Hz. In this frequency range, the
assumption on the inﬁnity of the absorber does not hold anymore, thus leading to
oscillations in the absorption curve. This eﬀect is more dominant if the source is
placed further away from the sample [26].
4.4.2
Limitations due to the environment
The simulations assumed totally free ﬁeld conditions which of course are not totally
attainable in reality.
Firstly, as mentioned in Section 4.1.1, the anechoic room presents a low frequency
limit at 50 Hz, under which reﬂections from the walls can occur. It is therefore im-
possible to obtain good results below 50 Hz, also because of the array itself, which
requires ka ≥0.1. Furthermore, the door had to be left open for practical reasons,
which resulted in possible additional reﬂections. In the same way, the room was not
totally empty, which could result in even more reﬂected and scattered waves inﬂuenc-
ing the measured pressures on the array. These additional reﬂections which should
be considered as noise are mainly weakened by distance so that the signal remains
predominant.
Similarly, the cables and the rods to which the source and the array were ﬁxed
were not taken into account in the simulation. Their presence might result in some
interference at high frequencies, thus adding diﬀraction terms to the sound ﬁeld.
4.4.3
Background noise
Mostly due to the unwanted reﬂections described earlier, the background noise proved
to be much more complex than the model used in the simulations.
Indeed, the study of the noise measurement showed a clear frequency dependence.
The most important noise components were identiﬁed at low frequencies, probably
due to the room’s setup and a mismatch between the microphones, and at selected
peaks which were identiﬁed as coming from the transformator’s fan.
69
==PAGE:92==
CHAPTER 4. MEASUREMENTS
As a result, although the SNR remained quite high over the studied frequency range,
attention was brought on the sensitivity of the calculation to a correct choice of the
noise ﬂoor estimate. A frequency dependent model of this estimate was investigated,
but it left still much room for improvement.
4.4.4
Distance measurements
Distances were measured with a laser, but depending on the setup some measurement
inaccuracies are possible. Moreover, both the array and the source were hung from
the ceiling with metal rods. When moved, they adopted a pendulum motion, which
needed to be attenuated before the measurements were performed. Such uncertain-
ties might have an impact on high frequency measurements.

Chapter 5
Discussion
The simulation and the measurement results proved the validity of the proposed
method, especially as it allows a reasonable reconstruction even without a detailed
knowledge of the sound ﬁeld. It was possible to identify a reasonable trend following
the theoretical properties of the studied materials. This chapter details the shortcom-
ings identiﬁed in the tests and indicates how the estimation of both the impedance
and the absorption coeﬃcient can be improved.
5.1
Calculation method
Firstly, the proposed method proved to be eﬃcient in localising the sources and their
relative strengths and to reconstruct the sound ﬁeld on the absorber. However, some
improvements can be made, especially by making use of a priori knowledge on the
sound ﬁeld itself.
5.1.1
Equivalent source method
The choice of the equivalent sources was motivated by the fact that they described
quite well the real source and its image, but this could be further improved. For
simplicity, all the measurements were done with the equivalent sources placed on a
sphere of radius 2 m centered on the array. The radius was chosen as a general ap-
proximation of the distance between the source and the array r0, varying from 1.75 m
to 3 m. The diﬀerence with the actual distance between the source and the array can
explain why the source and its image were commonly recovered as a set of several
equivalent sources.
A ﬁrst improvement could be to set the distance of the equivalent point sources to
exactly r0 in order to describe the source more precisely. Moreover, r0 diﬀers slightly
from r1, the distance between the array and the image source. An idea to address
this issue could be to position the equivalent sources on two half spheres of radii r0
and r1, or to position the equivalent sources around a sphere that has the material’s
surface as a plane of symmetry. Such setups would ensure a better representation
of both the direct source and its image in an even sparser solution. As a result, the
sound ﬁeld would be represented by a conﬁguration that is closer to the actual setup,
thus leading to a more robust and more eﬃcient reconstruction on the sample.
71
==PAGE:94==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
5.1.2
Reconstruction on the sample
In parallel, it was seen that the reconstruction on the material was less consistent
at high frequencies due to a too large reconstruction area and a higher Helmholtz
number. In addition to a better choice of equivalent sources described above, the
area of reconstruction could be reduced with frequency in order to recover a more
stable estimate of the surface impedance.
Moreover, the resulting pressure and particle velocity were quite uniform on the grid
at low frequencies. The high frequency reconstruction, however, showed important
variations. The averaging is therefore mostly needed for higher frequencies. For sim-
plicity, the reconstruction grid was a square, on which the accuracy is not evenly
distributed. The shape of the reconstruction area could therefore be investigated
further. In addition, averaging the surface impedance instead of calculating the ab-
sorption coeﬃcient point by point proved to be a safer approach in order to avoid
reﬂection factor values that were too high.
5.1.3
Additional constraints for the reﬂected sound ﬁeld
The results showed the possibility of obtaining a negative absorption coeﬃcient,
which of course has no physical sense. This could typically emanate from the overes-
timation of the power of the reﬂected ﬁeld. In order to prevent it, it was considered
to add a constraint to the minimisation problem, forcing the incident power corre-
sponding to the higher hemisphere of the equivalent sources to be higher than the
reﬂected one at the lower hemisphere. However, such a framework was not supported
by the CVX toolbox.
5.2
Absorber model
A model issue showed an overestimation of the absorption coeﬃcient at low frequen-
cies. Even if this deviation was less clear in the experimental results, due to the
inﬂuence of the ﬁniteness of the sample, it still appeared in some cases, especially for
Absorber 2.
5.2.1
Reﬂection
The sound ﬁeld was represented as the combination of two spherical ﬁelds originating
from the direct source and its image source. The strength of the second one was
calculated using a spherical reﬂection factor Q, which should account for spherical
spreading. However, this model proved to be imprecise at low frequencies. This could
be due to the approximations made for the calculation of Q, as it is shown in [27].
For instance, local reaction was assumed, and the calculation of the integral in the
function erfc was a numerical approximation.
72
==PAGE:95==
5.3. MEASUREMENT
5.2.2
Finiteness
The measurement results justiﬁed the need of a more complete absorber model, which
would especially take the size of the sample into account. At low frequencies par-
ticularly, diﬀraction from the edges results in a wrong estimation of α. However,
measurements of ﬁnite absorbers are usually done on a small sample mounted in
a large rigid baﬄe for more convenient calculation [21]. It would be interesting to
adapt the sound ﬁeld modeling and the experimental setup to such a conﬁguration in
order to account for the ﬁnite dimensions of the absorber and yield a more realistic
absorption coeﬃcient in the low frequency range.
5.3
Measurement
The measurement process could also beneﬁt from some modiﬁcations, especially in
the setup itself and the estimation of the noise ﬂoor.
5.3.1
Simulation guidelines
The simulations guaranteed a good potential estimation in a precise framework ex-
posed in Section 3.4. Diverging from these guidelines may lead to deviations in the
results. However, it is possible that some of these conditions can not be fulﬁlled
due to practical constraints. It was the case in the measurements, where the dis-
tance between the array and the absorber reached 42 cm for Absorber 2. This led to
more discrepancies, especially at higher frequencies. In that case, it is important to
adapt the results to remain within a relatively good agreement with these guidelines.
For example, in the measurement setup of the project, the larger distance yields a
less accurate sound ﬁeld reconstruction on the material at high frequencies. A better
estimate could be found by reducing the size of the reconstruction grid on the sample.
5.3.2
Background noise
The main concern of the measurement processing is the study of the background
noise: a strong relation between the SNR and the optimal estimated noise ﬂoor was
needed to fulﬁll a satisfactory solution to the equivalent source problem. From the
measurements, it seems from the measurements that the optimal noise ﬂoor is related
to the SNR, but its variations appeared less pronounced. That is why a calculation
technique providing a noise ﬂoor estimate depending on the signal, the measured
noise and the measurement position was investigated. However, the noise model is
still perfectible, and more research must be done in order to obtain a proper estimate
that varies with frequency.

CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
5.3.3
Setup
The experimental setup can still be improved, for instance, by allowing a shorter
distance between the array and the absorber, although limitations could still exist,
for example, if back-scattering occurs.
Furthermore, having the array hung from
the ceiling is quite impractical to set up. The spherical array could alternatively be
attached to a tripod so it is easier to position. Such a conﬁguration would, however,
require investigation on possible diﬀraction eﬀects at high frequencies, especially for
the reﬂected ﬁeld.
5.4
Conclusion
The main idea of the tested method was merely a problem of source detection, as
the sound ﬁeld was assumed to be due to a given source and its image. However, the
reﬂection proved to be much more complex, especially due to additional diﬀraction
components. In addition, the problem can be redeﬁned in order to represent the en-
vironment better, for instance in the choice of the equivalent sources. These elements
would lead to a more robust and more reliable calculation.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This project presented a new method to assess the acoustical properties of a plane
material. These properties are measured with the surface impedance, from which the
absorption coeﬃcient is derived. The proposed method is based on the measurement
of a sound ﬁeld and its reﬂection with a spherical array and its representation with
equivalent sources. The sparse conﬁguration justiﬁes the use of compressive sensing,
which allows for a more precise and robust sound ﬁeld reconstruction.
The method was tested both with simulations and measurements. Generally, the re-
sults showed a clear potential in the estimation of the impedance and the absorption
coeﬃcient, although several limitations and needed improvements were identiﬁed.
Indeed, the simulations showed that the reconstruction was not eﬃcient at very low
frequencies due to the high coherence between the measurement channels. That is
why the low frequency limit was set to 100 Hz. Additionally, a high frequency limit
exists around 4 kHz, due to the spacing between the microphones. The simulations
also showed the possibility to obtain satisfactory results up to an angle of incidence
of about 50◦. As for the measurements, they underlined the sensitivity of the minimi-
sation algorithm to noise: if the estimate is too low, additional noise sources appear
in the solution, whereas if it is too high, the strength of the image source tends to
be underestimated, resulting in an increase in the calculated absorption coeﬃcient.
Furthermore, additional low frequency discrepancies were caused by the ﬁniteness
of the absorber.
In order to substantially improve the low frequency results, the
calculation should include the size of the absorber. Additional measurements would
therefore be required to validate this more complex setup.
In general, the obtained estimations are quite remarkable, especially as not much
a priori information on the sound ﬁeld was used; the equivalent sources’s strengths
resulted only from the measurement itself and an estimation of the noise ﬂoor. As a
result, a better knowledge of the sound ﬁeld could lead to improved results. Firstly,
the equivalent source method can be optimised in order to ﬁt better with the actual
conﬁguration: a representation close to reality would lead to a more robust sound
ﬁeld reconstruction, both at higher distances and at higher frequencies. More inves-
tigation on the noise estimation would also be beneﬁcial in ﬁnding the best sparse
solution to the problem. Then, additional components should be modeled, in order
to account for eﬀects such as diﬀraction from the edges of the absorber. A better
75
==PAGE:98==
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION
model of the sound ﬁeld and the environment would therefore lead to an improved
estimation of the acoustical parameters of the material.
The next step is to test the method in more complex setups. Firstly, the free-ﬁeld
assumption that was made in this project is idealistic for in-situ measurements. That
is why the eﬀect of enclosures will be investigated in a future project. Moreover, the
detection of secondary reﬂections could be tested, provided the sound ﬁeld can still
have a sparse representation.
If these additional steps are conclusive, a robust method of impedance and absorption
estimation could be implemented, which would enable a more complete knowledge
of the acoustical properties of absorbers, both in terms of frequency and direction of
arrival of the sound ﬁeld. Such knowledge could then be implemented in simulations
to improve predictions of the acoustic qualities of a given environment, which would
be beneﬁcial for acoustic consultancy projects.
