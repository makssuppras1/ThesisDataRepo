Abstract 
Casted seaweed (CS) appears to constitute a major environmental, financial and social issue for 
many coastal areas around the globe. Eutrophication is considered the main reason behind the 
constant increase on the amounts of CS washed on shore. While prevention of the problem should 
be privileged, recycling appears to be the best short term solution. However no literature or data 
exist in regard to possible utilization of this kind of biomass. In this study, we apply the life cycle 
assessment methodology following recommendations from the European Commission in order to 
quantify the environmental impacts of different waste management techniques and identify the 
most suitable one for the CS. 
Four scenarios refer to anaerobic digestion (AD) of the seaweed and one to composting: (i) AD of 
the seaweed (AD-SW), (ii) anaerobic co-digestion of seaweed with cattle manure (COAD-M), (iii) 
anaerobic co-digestion with manure and sugar beet pulp (COAD-MSBP), (iv) anaerobic co-
digestion sugar beet pulp (COAD-SBP) and finally (v) composting, using only the seaweed as 
substrate (COMP). 
Results indicate that COAD-M delivers the most environmental benefits in the majority of the 
impact categories considered. This result is evaluated of high importance for Denmark since large 
quantities of manure are produced annually. On the other hand, COAD-MSBP would be the least 
favorable option. A scenario analysis where energy produced substitutes electricity production 
from offshore wind power, indicates that results are highly susceptible to long term technological 
changes. In addition, scores are very dependent on core assumptions made during the modelling 
such as means of marginal energy production.

1. Introduction  
Large quantities of casted seaweed (CS) are reported to washed on the southern shore of 
Copenhagen metropolitan area. CS is referred to all species of macro algae found, whilst 
eutrophication is considered the primary reason behind the constant increase on the amount washed 
on shore. Taking no action, leads to uncontrolled degradation with foul odors being presumably 
attributed to sulphur compounds as a result of natural anaerobic conditions. Inaccessibility of the 
sea is the first consequence due to odor nuisances with financial losses coming along. While 
several environmental, economic, and social issues are experienced, no data regarding the potential 
emissions from the natural and uncontrolled degradation of seaweed are reported.  
Recently, local municipalities established collection and treatment of CS in co-digestion with other 
various substrates (Fredenslund et al., 2010). Decisions were possibly based on locally available 
possibilities and environmental agendas (Laurent et al., 2014a), while not being based on 
comprehensive environmental considerations. In general, as prevention cannot be made effective 
in short terms, according to the waste hierarchy recycling should be the preferable strategy (CEC, 
2008), given that CS is classified as a solid waste.  
Different options for treatment and recycling of CS may exist, including digestion, co-digestion 
and composting. It is not clear and yet not proved which waste management technique is more 
suitable for this kind of biomass. An informed decision on the most preferable approach should 
thus include several technical and system parameters, as well as the yet rather unknown emissions 
and/or savings linked to each of these techniques. Currently, no documentation can be found 
regarding CS being a substrate in anaerobic digestion (AD), nor data can be found about CS being 
exploited in waste management plants. Michalak and Chojnacka (2013) and Eyras et al. (1998) 
==PAGE:8==
 
2 
 
report that casted seaweed can be used as compost to boost cultivation soils, without providing a 
thorough and comparative evaluation of this option. 
The objective of this study is to provide a comparative assessment of different scenarios for the 
handling of CS. This is done by: i) quantifying all the emissions originating from possible waste 
management techniques of CS; ii) comparing different scenarios for management CS to identify 
the option with the best environmental performance; iii) identifying hotspots and critical processes 
in CS management.

2. Materials and Methods 
2.1. LCA approach 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is a structured, comprehensive and internationally standardized 
method. It quantifies all relevant emissions and resources consumed and the related environmental 
and health impacts and resource depletion issues that are associated with any goods or services 
(“products”) (EC, 2010a). LCA is often used in evaluating waste management systems in order 
to identify the environmental impacts of the different management techniques (Laurent et al., 
2014a). Additionally, the EU waste directive (CEC, 2008) suggests that life cycle thinking should 
be introduced in all waste management decision-making, in order to encourage options that deliver 
the best environmental outcome. Assessing environmental impacts for waste management systems 
can be a very complex task followed by various challenges, LCA however has proved that constitutes 
a great decision-support tool at quantifying consistently environmental impacts of the aforementioned 
systems (Laurent et al., 2014b). 
2.2. Goal definition 
The goal of this study was to assess the environmental impacts of the management of the seaweed 
found in the southern shore of Copenhagen (Denmark) metropolitan area. Intended application of 
this study is to compare and evaluate, under an environmental view, different waste management 
techniques for CS. Decision context, lies on a micro-level decision support (Situation A). Results 
can assist locally to assess different options in regard to CS management and might also help to 
form an environmental policy, however no large scale changes are expected. Additionally, the 
study is not disclosed to the public and hence no comparative assertion with other LCAs took 
place. LCA modelling was performed using EASETECH (Clavreul et al., 2014) a dedicated 
software for LCA in waste management. The life cycle assessment was performed following 
recommendations from the European Commission (EC 2010a). The study makes systematic use 
==PAGE:10==
 
4 
 
of system expansion to avoid allocations and is based on the consequential approach (EC,2010a), 
meaning that marginal technologies are used when modelling changes in the foreground and 
background systems. For this reason, different waste management scenarios were formulated in 
order to identify the optimal one in terms of environmental performance and/or environmental 
savings. Thus, all scenarios are defined within expanded systems where the inputs (apart from the 
seaweed) are not completely identical whilst at the same time offer the same service, which is the 
management of the seaweed. This is in line with the consequential approach as was mentioned 
earlier.  
2.3. Scope definition 
The functional unit (FU) includes both quantitative and qualitative aspects, and is defined as ‘the 
management of one Mg of casted seaweed (CS) as found on the sea shore, including sand and 
water content’. The CS composition adopted for the modelling is provided in annexes. 
The model consists of five different scenarios meant to simulate feasible waste management 
techniques. Four scenarios refer to anaerobic digestion (AD) of the seaweed and one to 
composting. More specifically, AD is modelled with or without co-substrates along with the 
seaweed as described: (i) AD of the seaweed as sole substrate (AD-SW), (ii) anaerobic co-
digestion of seaweed with cattle manure (COAD-M), (iii) anaerobic co-digestion with manure and 
sugar beet pulp (COAD-MSBP), (iv) anaerobic co-digestion sugar beet pulp (COAD-SBP) and 
finally (v) composting, using only the seaweed as substrate (COMP). In regard to 
representativeness and appropriateness of the LCI data, the study considers the use of modern and 
advanced technology (currently available or in use in Denmark) in the majority of the processes 
used. The time scope of the study is 2015-2040, whilst the geographical scope is primarily set to 
Denmark, where the waste management facilities are modelled and assumed to be located.

2.4. System boundaries 
A description of the scenarios modelled is very useful in order to understand the processes and the 
assumptions made. In this section, system boundaries from COAD-MSBP is analyzed since it is 
the most complex scenario while the rest can be found in the annexes. The system boundaries 
begin with the CS and the respective amounts of manure and SBP that are scaled to the FU. System 
boundaries include collection and transportation of the CS (though not shown) and there after the 
CS is driven to pretreatment and finally treatment facility. After, fugitive loses the amount of 
biogas driven to the CHP unit can be seen (MJ), while the actual amount of energy produced (both 
heat and electricity including efficiencies) is shown in kWh. The latter amount (423.8 kWh) is then 
accounts for environmental savings due to the avoided production of marginal heat and electricity 
(grey dash boxes and lines). Co product of the AD process, digestate is responsible for the avoided 
production and thereafter use of mineral fertilizers in regard to N, P and K. The system is expanded 
to include possible uses of manure and SBP. Manure is assumed that would have been spread on 
land and emissions from its storage (prior to spreading) are modelled as well as the induced need 
for artificial fertilizers. Regarding SBP, soybean and maize are assumed to substitute the need for 
animal feed that was induced by the SBP being driven to the AD plant. This system expansion as 
well as impacts from “land use” for cultivation of soybeans and maize were modelled according 
to Tonini et al. (2015). Finally emissions from the zero solution approach (ZESA) can be seen for 
three impact categories global warming potential, marine and freshwater eutrophication.

2.1. Impact assessment, Normalization and Weighting  
The impact categories refer to the three default protection areas (EC,2010a) which are: human 
health, natural environment, and natural resources. The following impact categories were assessed: 
climate change (GWP), terrestrial acidification (TA), terrestrial eutrophication (TE), freshwater

eutrophication (FEP), marine eutrophication (MEP), human toxicity, carcinogenic (HTc),

weighting system by including (factor=1) or excluding (factor=0) some categories. The selection

2.2. LCI 
A large share of the inventory data were based on existing datasets in EASETECH or slightly 
modified, as later described. 
2.2.1. Zero Solution 
Seaweed was till lately not collected nor handled, but left to naturally degrade on the shore. This 
zero solution approach (ZESA) is thus the baseline situation to which all the alternative options 
are to be compared to. Potential impacts from ZESA are related to the uncontrolled degradation of 
the seaweed, where no active waste treatment takes place. The ZESA was modelled in all scenarios 
with the same identical avoided process. The initial assumption is that CS is degraded naturally on 
shore with various emissions occurring in all surrounding environmental compartments. However, 
emissions were modelled for air and water (marine) whilst leaching and emissions to soil were 
excluded for the sake of simplicity. According to the basic assumption, it was considered that CS 
==PAGE:13==
 
7 
 
is degraded in a similar to an uncontrolled composting process. Hence, it was assumed aerobic and 
anaerobic degradation of 90 and 10% respectively of the initial mass of the CS. In general, carbon 
nitrogen and phosphorus, were assumed to be the elements that can react during this process and 
thus deliver new compounds to the environment. Four main air emissions regarding carbon and 
nitrogen degradation were identified: CH4, CO2, N2O and NH3. However, CO2 was soon 
disregarded since it was considered to originate from a biomass stock (biogenic carbon) and thus 
not contributing to further impacts. CH4 was linked to the 10% (in terms of the carbon mass) 
anaerobic state of the degradation while quantitative assumptions for N2O and NH3 were based on 
Pu et al. (2010). Nonetheless, no phosphorus was assumed to escape and emitted to air (Nielsen 
and Kristensen, 2005). More and quantitative details are included in the annex.  
Marine water was the second environmental body to receive the degradation products which were 
assumed to be nitrate, phosphorus and phosphate. For the two latter ones, 10% of the total P was 
assumed to leak as it is in the sea while the rest was assumed to form phosphate before it reaches 
the water body. It is however acknowledged that phosphorus poses a higher environmental threat 
compared to phosphate since it has three times higher characterization factor for marine 
eutrophication. However, it was considered rather unlikely that the P content of the CS exists in a 
pure form of P. A mass balance calculation took place to quantify nitrate after excluding the air 
emissions. Finally, no volatilization or degradation of metals or other elements present in the CS 
composition assumed to take place, but on the contrary it was assumed that they were directly 
washed into the marine water. 
2.2.2. Biochemical methane Potential (BMP) 
The BMP data used in the model consisted of primary data derived from experimental work. 
Thermophilic digested manure from Snertinge plant was used as inoculum for all BMP 
==PAGE:14==
 
8 
 
experiments. CS was collected from Køge bay and delivered to the lab as was sampled without 
treatment at all. Manure was collected at the same biogas plant as the inoculum. SBP, which is a 
residue from sugar beet crop was delivered by Nordic Sugar. Both manure (substrate) and SBP 
were frozen prior to any experimental use. Manure additionally was sieved while CS was washed 
to remove sand, rods and other lignocellulosic materials. Biomethane potential was determined 
with batch experiments according to Angelidaki et al. (2009). Substrate and active manure 
inoculum were placed into bottles which were then flushed with gas nitrogen to trigger anaerobic 
conditions. The bottles were then sealed and stored into an incubator at 55±0.5 oC (thermophilic 
conditions). All experiments were running in triplicates and methane production was measured 
every five days. In addition, controls with cellulose and glucose were running, as well as blank 
bottles (only inoculum). Regarding the co-substrates the following combinations were tested 
(analogies expresses as VS content): CS:M at ratio 20:80, CS:M:SBP at 20:50:30 and CS:SBP, 
20:80. All experimental work conducted by A. Merola (2014). 
2.2.3. Pretreatment and treatment facility 
After collection and transportation, CS is washed with water in a process that is assumed to remove 
the whole amount of sand and salt content. After that stage and according to scenarios, CS is either 
fed directly to anaerobic reactors or is mixed with another substrate (manure or SBP or both) prior 
to the AD or it is composted. Methane yield is assumed to be 70% of the experimental BMP values, 
whereas the methane content in biogas is assumed to be 70%. Composting is modelled as an 
enclosed channel facility where greenhouse gases (GHG) accounting was based on Boldrin et al. 
(2009). Finally capital goods of both facilities were modelled according to Brogaard et al. (2015). 
Comprehensive description of the LCI is provided in the annex. 
==PAGE:15==
 
9 
 
2.2.4. Use on land, avoided fertilizers 
Finally, after the process, digestate or compost (from AD or composting respectively) is 
transported locally and applied on land as a substitute to mineral fertilizers. This process accounts 
for all emissions to air, surface water, groundwater and soil accumulation from land application of 
the digestate or compost and it is based on its respective composition. A direct consequence of this 
process is the avoided production and further use of mineral fertilizers instead. P and K present in 
the digestate are modelled to substitute fully respective amounts of mineral fertilizers (substitution 
rate equal to one) while N in the digestate substitute nitrogen fertilizer in a 0.75 ratio. However, it 
is acknowledged here, that the maximum substitution rate imposed by legislation for cattle manure 
is 0.70 (Danish Ministry of Food, Agriculture and Fisheries, 2008) 
2.2.5. Energy 
Energy assumptions should always be clarified, and especially in our case, where the LCA results 
are strongly based on them. Marginal electricity was modelled as a mix fuel as input consisting 
mostly of hard coal (99% in terms of mass) and minor quantities of oil (1% in terms of mass) while 
the output is 1kWh (ELCD3.2, 2008). Losses in transmission and distribution till the point where 
electricity reaches the final consumer are also accounted, along with impacts coming from 
extraction, processing and transportation of the fossil fuels. Same approach is applied to heat 
substitution, where emissions are modelled based on heat marginal production adjusted in Danish 
standards (EASETECH-database, 2016).  
Produced biogas is directly driven to a state-of-the-art combustion engine (i.e. Jenbacher 420) with 
0.40 and 0.42 electrical and thermal efficiencies respectively (Electric, 2016). Fugitive losses of 
biogas in the AD plant are modelled to be 3.1% v/v (Boldrin; et al., 2016). Electricity produced is 
sent to the grid and assumed to substitute electricity production from marginal means.

2.2.6. SBP as animal feed 
Sugar beet pulp is a byproduct from the sugar industry that is widely used in the animal feed 
market. Removing this product from the market induces a need for other kinds of animal feed. 
Soymeal and maize were assumed marginal protein and energy feed respectively following the full 
elasticity of supply assumption as was documented by Weidema et al. (2009). Substitution 
quantities were calculated regarding calorific and protein content according to Tonini et al. (2015). 
When soymeal is produced however, soy oil is co-produced as well. As the latter holds the ability 
to substitute other oil products, a new system expansion is needed in order to account for the 
avoided (now) impacts of the other oil product (that is substituted by the soy oil). However, the 
substituted oil will be probably co-produced by another protein based product, since most oils are 
produced that way. Hence, a new system expansion is again needed in order to include the afore-
mentioned products and systems. This never-ending system known as soybean-rapeseed loop 
(Weidema, 1999) calls for iterative calculations in order to define the final and total amount of 
soybeans and maize needed. This iterative process that was adopted in our study is described in a 
very detailed way by Tonini et al. (2015). Furthermore, land use change was modelled again 
according to Tonini et al. (2015) as well as electricity consumption (processing the crop) and 
transportation to the final destination of use. More details in the annex.

2.4. Uncertainty Propagation 
Initially it was assumed that all uncertainties can be represented as normal probability distributions 
with an arithmetic mean and two times standard deviation (Frischknecht et al., 2007). Monte Carlo 
approach was employed with 50,000 iterations for every scenario. Additionally, it was assumed 
that variances of the parameters do not interfere with each other (i.e. parameters are independent), 
==PAGE:18==
 
12 
 
though it is acknowledged that this statement might not fully reflect reality. This can be understood 
if we consider a state-of-the-art biogas combustion engine where electrical and thermal efficiencies 
(twin parameters) are greatly affected by each other and the value of one cannot increase without 
any consequence on its twin parameter.  
Uncertainty was assigned to individual parameters using the pedigree matrix approach (Weidema 
& Wesnæs, 1996), where data quality is assessed based on six indicators (i.e. “reliability”, 
“completeness”, “temporal correlation”, “further technological correlation”, “sample size”), each 
receiving a score from one to five, according to predefined criteria. Subsequently, the uncertainty 
of a parameter is calculated according to equation (3) (Frischknecht et al., 2007):

supporting information. 
2.5. Discernibility analysis 
The Monte Carlo approach provides in many cases overlapping frequency distributions between 
scenarios, meaning that when taking into consideration uncertainties it is not possible to univocally 
==PAGE:19==
 
13 
 
identify the most preferable scenarios. Discernibility analysis, is thus used to evaluate and quantify 
the probability of a scenario being better than another. More specifically, Monte Carlo simulation 
yield 50,000 results (as many as iterations) for each impact category, each time sampling a random 
value inside the variance limits set for all parameters. Comparison begins by placing side by side 
results for two scenarios (same IC) and subtracting one from the other. A negative result in this 
pair-to-pair comparison implies that the first scenario is better than the second. Finally a simple 
calculation takes place in order to extract the percentage of cases were one scenario obtains more 
favorable scores than another, which might be easier to communicate (Bisinella et al., 2016).

3. Results 
In this section, results of the assessment are described in their entirety. Because of space 
limitations, only few selected results are also displayed graphically, whilst the remaining graphs 
are found in the supporting information. 
3.1 Normalized scores 
Figure 2 shows the normalized scores (mPE) for all scenarios modelled and for the selected impact 
categories. The scores differ greatly among scenarios and impact categories, and variations are 
also observed in orders of magnitude. Overall, the highest scores can be observed for FEP, MEP, 
TE and TA. On the other hand, RDfos, HTc and RD exhibit the lowest scores respectively.  
<Figure 2> 
3.2 Zero Solution 
The ZESA corresponds to the baseline scenario. FEP, MEP and GWP are the impact categories 
mostly affected when modelling the degradation of seaweed on the shore. When looking at GWP, 
N2O accounts for about 70% of the potential impacts, while the rest is credited to CH4. Potential 
impacts on FEP are related to water emissions of phosphorus and phosphate, where the latter 
accounts for about 90% of the total score. On the other hand, MEP is solely due to nitrate emissions 
into marine water. Finally, RDfos and RD show no impact, while lead, a heavy metals carried by 
seaweed, is responsible for the minor score in HTc category. 
3.3 Contribution analysis 
Figure 3 depicts the contribution of different processes to the overall results for all scenarios and 
selected impact categories (GWP, TA and TE). “Biogas production” includes fugitive methane 
losses while “other” represent the impacts of the capital goods for the respective facility as well as 
the impacts for the sand separation of seaweed. “Collection and transportation” includes all 
processes related to collecting inputs as well as transporting products to final recipients. “Feed 
==PAGE:21==
 
15 
 
production” accounts for all the land used for maize and soybean production as well as resources 
consumed to transform the grains into a final feed product for animals. 
In general, collection and transportation, composting and other processes account only for a small 
share of the impact score in all categories presented in Figure 3. In GWP, largest savings are found 
in COAD-M, whilst AD-SW follows with minor difference. The ZESA is the most dominant 
process, contributing with equal savings in all scenarios. Those savings are attributed to avoided 
emissions of CH4 and N2O, occurring during the natural degradation of seaweed on the shore. In 
scenarios COAD-M and COAD-MSBP, where manure is used for anaerobic digestion, “use on 
land” is responsible for N2O emissions in relation to the production and on-land application of 
mineral fertilizers. However, the latter emissions are partly compensated by the avoided manure 
storage. Finally, land use changes related to maize and soybean productions constitute a great 
impact, because of the CO2, CH4 and N2O emissions occurring in those processes. 
In TA and TE, COAD-M and COAD-MSBP are the only scenarios having environmental benefits, 
with COAD-M exhibiting more than double savings in comparison to COAD-MSBP. The reason 
beneath those savings is attributed to manure storage as well as avoided use of mineral fertilizers. 
In both processes, avoided emissions of NH3 is the reason for this environmental saving. On the 
other hand, the largest impact for both impact categories can be seen in COAD-SBP, where NH3 
and NOx are emitted from the cultivation of maize and soybeans (induced need for animal feed).

that can be either fresh (lakes and rivers) or marine (sea and oceans). The first one is primarily 
linked to the presence of phosphorous compounds, whilst the latter one is mainly attributed to

nitrogen compounds. For FEP and MEP, the ZESA appears having a relative small impact in 
comparison to other impact categories such as GWP, TA and TE. Collection and transportation, 
as well as other processes, again account for an almost insignificant share of the potential impacts. 
Score of COMP is dominated by the ZESA savings, meaning that composting as a waste 
management technique has nearly negligible impacts on those two categories. Similarly, energy 
and biogas production (for anaerobic digestion as waste management technique) also contribute 
with rather small impacts. COAD-M and COAD-MSBP exhibit the highest savings in both 
categories. In FEP, savings are attributed to the avoided use of phosphorous fertilizers when using 
the digestate from anaerobic digestion. The difference between COAD-M and COAD-MSBP is 
related to the difference on the initial mass input of manure. Finally, COAD-SBP and COAD-
MSBP are responsible for phosphorus and nitrogen emissions to water and ground water due to 
the cultivation of maize and soybeans. Those emissions justify the highest scores found 
respectively in FEP and MEP. 
In regard to depletion of resources (RDfos), large savings can be seen for COAD-SBP and COAD-
MSBP, followed by COAD-M and AD-SW. In fact, that specific result is linked with the 
substitution of marginal electricity, which production was assumed being based on hard coal. 
Indeed, savings for scenarios COAD-SBP, COAD-MSBP, COAD-M and AD-SW are completely 
dependent on the amount of biogas produced, which is then converted to energy through 
combustion. However, the operation of the plant requires inputs of heat and electricity, in amounts 
which are directly linked to the mass of feedstock to AD, thereby justifying the high score seen for 
COAD-M and COAD-MSBP. However, even in the worst case scenario (COAD-M), AD remains 
a net producer of energy. Additionally, RDfos is the only category where the occupation of land 
can be quantified as a use of resource. High scores are found for COAD-SBP and COAD-MSBP

3.4 Case study scenario: effect of marginal energy 
The choice of marginal energy substitution was tested by changing the marginal energy production 
from hard coal to offshore wind power. In other words, electricity produced by the biogas 
combustion engine is now substituting electricity produced by offshore wind power. This alteration 
may represent the effect of a long term development of the energy sector towards increasing 
==PAGE:24==
 
18 
 
penetration of renewable sources. However, all other electricity inputs (in all scenarios) remain 
unaltered, thus being produced by the marginal means. Consequently, COMP remains unaltered.  
In regard to GWP, savings are lower than the original scenarios, with differences being 
proportional to the amount of biogas produced. COAD-MSBP and COAD-SBP recorded the 
largest changes, with savings diminishing by 189 and 36% respectively. In TA and TE, the change 
in marginal electricity worsened the environmental performance of all scenarios. This owes to the 
fact that, compared to hard coal, wind power has significantly lower emissions of NOx, which 
typically is the main contributor to TA and TE. Same situation was observed for MEP, whilst the 
differences for FEP were negligible. On the contrary, scores in RDfos increased by 3 to 74 times 
compared with the original result, indicating that this assumption was mostly critical towards the 
results for this impact category.  
Finally, in RD (reserve base) and HTc, the change in marginal electricity provided better scores 
for all scenarios. Excess use of nickel is the main reason scores in RD are shifted as well. Nickel 
is presumably used in in the manufacturing of parts of a wind turbine. As the marginal electricity 
substitution is now assumed to be from wind energy, all this amount of nickel is saved due to the 
avoided production of that electricity. 
3.5 Sensitivity Analysis 
Sensitivity analysis is fully discussed here, while a complete overview of the calculated SR and 
NSRs is provided in the supporting information. Results show that certain scores in some impact 
categories solely depend on one or maybe two parameters, which simultaneously provide a small 
contribution in this category. Therefore, those categories had considerably higher SR in 
comparison to scores of other impact categories that rely on multiple parameter sets. In some cases, 
SRs differ by orders of magnitude, hence not allowing a direct comparison and hence quality 
==PAGE:25==
 
19 
 
discussion. The problem was addressed with the use of NSRs (as described earlier), allowing for 
a safe comparison of the parameters’ sensitivity across different scenarios and categories. 
Global warming potential is an impact category that is affected by many parameters and processes. 
For instance, AD-SW is highly sensitive to N2O emissions from the ZESA, whilst secondary 
sensitivity is observed for BMP, CH4 emissions (zero solution), methane yield and electrical 
efficiency. In COAD-M and COAD-MSBP, methane emission from manure storage represents 
also a highly sensitive parameter. In addition, sensitivity of methane yield is linearly related to the 
respective BMP value of each substrate: this is seen in all scenarios including AD, with NSRs 
increasing as BMP values increase. Conversely, a similar pattern is not reported for the BMP 
values, which sensitivity does not linearly increase when the BMP increases. 
In TA and TE, high sensitivity is credited to NH3 emissions from ZESA for AD-SW. In COAD-
M and COAD-MSBP, NH3 emission from ZESA have NSR below 0.06 and 0.05 due to the 
ammonia emission from the indoor and outdoor manure storage. Finally, COMP appears to be the 
least sensitive, only affected by emissions from the ZESA process.  
The FEP impact category is highly sensitive to phosphate and to a lesser extend to phosphorus, 
whilst MEP is solely sensitive to nitrate emitted to marine waters. It is worth mentioning though 
that FEP and MEP only presented three parameters (those mentioned here) scoring a NSR above 
0.1 for all scenarios. 
With regards to human toxicity, carcinogenic, energy related parameters were found to be the most 
sensitive ones. Electricity consumption (seaweed from sand separation), CH4 content in biogas, 
biogas leaking and thermal efficiency all had NSRs above 0.9, whilst electrical efficiency had a 
==PAGE:26==
 
20 
 
NSR slightly above 0.5. The aforementioned result applies for all scenarios, an exception being 
COMP (composting), where biogas related processes were not present. 
In RDfos, as expected, results were sensitive to processes that can affect the substitution of 
electricity production, including the electrical efficiencies, and to a lesser extent CH4 yield and the 
BMP of substrates. On the contrary, RD was subjected to alterations only due to collection, which 
is justified by the fact that consumption of diesel (for transportation and collection) includes all 
the processes related to oil refineries and oil extraction.  
3.6 Uncertainty Propagation 
Monte Carlo results indicated that the magnitude of uncertainty varies significantly across impact 
categories, while being similar when comparing different scenarios (for the same impact category), 
possibly because of the relative high number of common parameters shared by the scenarios. 
Namely, all impact categories that represent natural environment as endpoint (GWP, TA, TE, FEP, 
MEP) showed high levels of variance compared to RDfos, RD and HTc with the two latter ones 
having almost zero variance. This result is attributed primarily to the ZESA process, where high 
values of uncertainty were assigned to all parameters that were assumed on an initial base. 
Uncertainty related to FEP and MEP cannot be assigned to ZESA, since its contribution to those 
impact categories can only affect scores to a lesser extent than GWP, TA and TE. Nonetheless, no 
uncertainty contribution analysis took place and therefore it is difficult to trace the actual link 
between the input variance of our parameters and the final scores.  
3.7 Discernibility Analysis 
Since GWP exhibited large variance, discernibility analysis focused on that impact category. More 
specifically, AD-SW has more savings than COAD-M in 43.5% of the cases and 63% in regard to 
COMP. Hence, there is no safe claim weather AD-SW is better than COAD-M whilst it can be 
==PAGE:27==
 
21 
 
more likely that AD-SW obtains more benefits in comparison to COMP. In addition, AD-SW is 
91% of the cases more beneficial comparatively to COAD-MSBP. In regard to the other scenarios, 
there was no case were COAD-SBP was any better than all the rest individually compared. COAD-
M had more savings tested against COAD-MSBP and COMP in 93 and 69% of the cases. Finally, 
COMP correlated to COAD-MSBP showed more benefits in 85% of the cases. 
Considering the remaining impact categories, no solid conclusion can be drawn for AD-SW and 
COMP for FEP where both have the same chances (50%) to have higher savings over the other. 
Similarly, in MEP, AD-SW is 55% more beneficial towards COAD-MSBP whilst the latter one 
underperforms in 68% of the cases over COMP. Lastly, AD-SW has 32.5% chances to be more 
beneficial than COMP. In RDfos, COAD-MSBP is 60% of the cases more beneficial than AD-SW 
and COAD-MSBP is better than COAD-SBP in only 24% of the cases.  
Finally, for TA, TE, HTc and RD, discernibility analysis showed that the original results were 
enough to extract robust discussion on weather a scenario is environmentally favorable than 
another. Detailed values and graphs can be found in supporting information.

4. Discussion 
Normalized results indicate that, even when including uncertainty, COAD-M has the best 
environmental performance (either highest savings or lowest impacts) in seven out of the eight 
impact categories (in GWP COAD-M is 57% better than AD-SW), with RDfos being the only 
impact category that COAD-M did not dominated over the others. COAD-MSBP and AD-SW 
appear to be second and third most environmental friendly options with minor differences in RDfos 
and MEP. However, COAD-MSBP appears to have highest savings in FEP, TE and TA. The actual 
reason for the high savings of COAD-M and COAD-MSBP lies on the utilization of manure, a 
process that saves air emissions in terms of CH4, N2O and NOx.  
Considering the fact that the geographical scope of this study is set primarily to Denmark, a country 
where massive amounts of manure are produced annually, results are definitely useful in regard to 
manure management. However a new study based on manure as functional unit might be essential 
in order to derive more robust results. Furthermore, exploring additional scenarios in regard to 
manure utilization might be also useful.  
Due to the consequential approach of this LCA, we see that large emissions and related impacts 
originate from processes meant to substitute an induced need in the market by a product or service 
used in the analyzed scenario. For example, production of maize and soybean, meant to substitute 
SBP in COAD-MSBP and COAD-SBP, is the major reason for the underperformance of these two 
scenarios. The use of SBP in the anaerobic digestion has indeed an enhanced effect on the amount 
of biogas produced, which subsequently increases the amount of energy produced and thus 
environmental savings. However, the vast emissions induced by the maize and soybean production 
do not justify its use in the AD process.  
==PAGE:29==
 
23 
 
Collection and transportation as processes account for a modest contribution to the final scores in 
almost all impact categories examined. Infrastructure investments needed for facility plants have 
even or less contribution compared to collection and transportation with an exception in resources 
depletion. 
Anaerobic digestion is in many countries promoted by a greener public agenda, while plants 
perform an active effort to increase the amounts of biogas produced. This is because a monetary 
value is assigned to the rich energy biogas, in a way that influences the financial sustainability of 
the plant itself. Exploring different kinds of organic wastes is sometimes done without considering 
induced impacts of the substrates. In this study, the LCA approach has proved that financial 
sustainability (assumed fully dependent on the amount of biogas) might be damaging for the 
environment and a careful consideration should take place. 
The case study scenario, with offshore wind energy as marginal electricity substitution, indicates 
that large and long term technological changes can significantly affect the results of the assessment 
and shift the focus towards other impact categories (RD for instance) than GWP, FEP and MEP. 
Uncertainty propagation using Monte Carlo highlights the result fluctuation due to the ZESA. 
Though it is the first time that effort is made to model environmental impacts of a natural process, 
the uncertainty results imply the need for data of improved quality.

5. Conclusions 
In this study, a complete life cycle assessment was carried out to quantify the potential 
environmental impacts arising from handling one Mg of cast seaweed. Five different waste 
management scenarios were modelled, including digestion (AD-SW), co-digestion (AD-SW:M, 
AD-SW:M:SBP, AD-SW:SBP) and composting (COMP) of the seaweed. COAD-M exhibited the 
best performance in the majority of the impact categories considered, followed by AD-SW, while 
the highest environmental impact was found for COAD-SBP and COAD-MSBP. In fact, the use 
of manure in anaerobic digestion, and hence avoiding its storage, results in large environmental 
savings mostly related to GWP, TA and TE. Substrate of seaweed with SBP seems to be a 
promising substrate as it has a BMP value which is circa 99% of the respective theoretical one. 
However, removing SBP from the animal feed market induces activities (i.e. production of maize 
and soy) that proved not to be environmentally friendly in such a manner that all savings were 
totally offset.  
In regard to the case study scenario with the offshore wind power generation, it is concluded that 
composting is a very stable process in comparison to anaerobic digestion, always considering the 
environmental aspect of both. However each process has its own advantages as well as drawbacks. 
More specifically, the afore mentioned waste management techniques appear to have certain weak 
points subjected to certain impact categories and results can vary significantly according to the 
initial scenario assumptions (i.e.: marginal products).

1 Goal  
The goal of this study was to assess the environmental impacts of the management of the seaweed 
found in the southern shore of Copenhagen (Denmark) metropolitan area. Intended application of this 
study is to compare and evaluate, under an environmental view, different waste management 
techniques for CS. Decision context, lies on a micro-level decision support (Situation A). Results can 
assist locally to assess different options in regard to CS management and might also help to form an 
environmental policy, however no large scale changes are expected. Additionally, the study is not 
disclosed to the public and hence no comparative assertion with other LCAs was performed. LCA 
modelling was performed using EASETECH (Clavreul et al., 2014) a dedicated software for LCA in 
waste management. The life cycle assessment was performed following recommendations from the 
European Commission (EC 2010a). The study makes systematic use of system expansion to avoid 
allocations and is based on the consequential approach (EC,2010a), meaning that marginal 
technologies are used when modelling changes in the foreground and background systems. For this 
reason, different waste management scenarios were formulated in order to identify the optimal one in 
terms of environmental performance and/or environmental savings. Thus, all scenarios are defined 
within expanded systems where the inputs (apart from the seaweed) are not completely identical whilst 
at the same time offer the same service, which is the management of the seaweed. This is in line with 
the consequential approach as was mentioned earlier.

2 Scope 
The functional unit (FU) includes both quantitative and qualitative aspects, and is defined as ‘the 
management of one Mg of casted seaweed (CS) as found on the sea shore, including sand and water 
content’. The CS composition adopted for the modelling is provided in annexes. 
The model consists of five different scenarios meant to simulate feasible waste management 
techniques. Four scenarios refer to anaerobic digestion (AD) of the seaweed and one to composting. 
More specifically, AD is modelled with or without co-substrates along with the seaweed as described: 
(i) AD of the seaweed as sole substrate (AD-SW), (ii) anaerobic co-digestion of seaweed with cattle 
manure (COAD-M), (iii) anaerobic co-digestion with manure and sugar beet pulp (COAD-MSBP), 
(iv) anaerobic co-digestion sugar beet pulp (COAD-SBP) and finally (v) composting, using only the 
seaweed as substrate (COMP). In regard to representativeness and appropriateness of the LCI data, 
the study considers the use of modern and advanced technology (currently available or in use in 
Denmark) in the majority of the processes used. The time scope of the study is 2015-2040, whilst the 
geographical scope is primarily set to Denmark, where the waste management facilities are modelled 
and assumed to be located.

3.2 Zero solution 
Seaweed is currently not collected nor handled, but left to naturally degrade on the shore. This 
zero solution approach (ZESA) is thus the baseline situation to which all the alternative options 
are to be compared to. Impacts related to the ZESA are related to the uncontrolled degradation of 
the seaweed, where no active waste treatment takes place. The ZESA was modelled in all scenarios 
with the same identical avoided process, which is described later. Initially all emissions are 
modelled for water (marine) and air compartments whilst leaching and emissions to soil are 
excluded for the sake of simplicity. From the initial composition of CS carbon, nitrogen and 
phosphorus are analyzed while the rest of the elements is assumed to be washed back to sea.  
The initial assumption is that CS is degraded naturally on shore. This degradation is then further 
assumed to be 90% aerobic and 10% anaerobic. Percentages apply on the initial amount of each 
element. For instance 10% of the carbon content is assumed to transform to CH4 through anaerobic 
degradation. The remaining carbon degrading aerobically emits CO2 which in our case is biogenic 
and thus does not contribute any further to global warming. 
Nitrogen was modelled for both air and water. For air, nitrogen assumed to form ammonia and 
dinitrogen monoxide, the latter one being emitted from both aerobic and anaerobic conditions. 
Assumptions were exclusively based on (Pu et al., 2010) as gaseous nitrogen emissions were 
recorded following biosolids application on soil. Therefore, ammonia emitted to air represents 4% 
of the nitrogen content which was later trimmed down to 3.6% following the amount of CS 
degraded aerobically.  
Dinitrogen monoxide was assumed to be emitted to air from both aerobic and anaerobic conditions 
in 29% and 24% of the total nitrogen. The aforementioned percentages were then adjusted to the 
aerobic and anaerboic conditions to finally yield 26.1% and 2.4% of the total nitrogen from aerobic 
and anaerobic conditions respectively. 
Both values assumed for dinitrogen monoxide emissions are the actual values that (Pu et al., 2010) 
measured in their experiment. However, in their study they also calculate a possible nitrogen mass 
balance where dinitrogen monoxide emissions are slightly higher.  
Nitrate was modelled to be the only water emission related to nitrogen. Thus the rest of the nitrogen 
(mass balance after the air emissions) was modelled are nitrate emissions. Possible ammonia or 
nitrite emission into marine water would yield no change on the result since they do not have a 
characterization factor according to the method that was used. 
Regarding phosphorus, 10% of it assumed to be emitted directly as phosphorus into water whilst 
the rest is assumed to be emitted into the water as phosphate. It is however acknowledged that 
phosphorus poses a higher environmental threat compared to phosphate since it has three times 
higher characterization factor for marine eutrophication. However, it was considered rather 
unlikely that the phosphorus content of the CS exists in a pure form of phosphorus. It was finally 
assumed that no phosphorus can be lost or emitted into air (Nielsen and Kristensen, 2005).

3.3 Collection 
Perhaps one of the most costly processes (Fredenslund et al., 2010) is related to collection of the 
seaweed. It is performed with machinery that uses fossil fuels and this is the main parameter that 
is interesting is model-wise cases. Emissions account for all pollutants caused by the combustion 
of the fossil fuels but in addition environmental burden related to the extraction of fossil fuels is 
also accounted according to Larsen et al. (2009). More specifically, technology refers to 
combustion of 1 liter diesel oil in a small truck (Gross Vehicle Weight: 10 tonnes), driving in urban 
areas complying with the Euro3 European Emission Standard. In contrast to transportation, 
production of diesel oil is also included (Larsen et al., 2009). 
3.4 Transportation 
Transportation is inevitably linked with almost all industries and is mainly accounted for supply 
of raw materials as well as delivery of the final products. In this case transportation of materials 
and products takes place in the following cases (Fredenslund et al., 2010).  
 
a) From the seaweed collection point (shore) to the plant. 
b) Digestate or compost from the plant to the farmers and 
c) manure from farmers to the plant. 
 
In all cases transportation is performed with trucks powered by fossil fuels which have a 30 and 
28 tons (Fredenslund et al., 2010) of nominal and working load capacity.

3.5 Seaweed Pretreatment 
Seaweed is sometimes filled with sand which can negatively affect the anaerobic reactors as well 
as slow down the composting process. Washing of the seaweed initially takes place in order to 
clean the seaweed biomass from sand. According to the stages of the pretreatment a float/sink 
separator, is used for the initial stage of washing. The main byproducts of the washing pretreatment 
is sand and water. The sand is returned and placed back to the shore where initially the seaweed 
was collected (Fredenslund et al., 2010). Water is assumed to be discharged in the drainage system 
with no impacts considered for it.  
This float/sink separator is powered by an electric motor and thus electricity consumption is 
modelled based on the Danish average standards (ELCD3.2, 2002).  
3.6 Anaerobic digestion 
This process is based on data from biogas production from organic waste at Karpalund biogas-
production plant, southern Sweden. The process is mesophilic (38 degrees oC) with heat exchange. 
Use of thermal energy and electricity has been assumed to 0.025 kWh and 0.0054 kWh t 
respectively per kg of wet weight input. Methane losses have been estimated to 0.5-1% of produced 
biogas. Heat and electricity inputs were assumed to be marginal. Fugitive losses of biogas in the 
AD plant are modelled to be 3.1% v/v (Boldrin; et al., 2016). 
3.7 Biochemical methane potential 
The BMP data used in the modeling consisted of primary data derived from experimental work. 
Biomethane potential was determined with batch experiments according to (Angelidaki et al., 
2009). Substrate and active manure inoculum were placed into 323 mL serum bottles which were 
then flushed with gas nitrogen to trigger anaerobic conditions. The bottles were then sealed and 
stored into an incubator at 55±0.5 oC (thermophilic conditions). All experiments were running in 
triplicates and methane production was measured every five days. In addition, controls with 
cellulose and glucose were running, as well as blank bottles (only inoculum). Regarding the co-
substrates the following combinations were tested (analogies expresses as VS content): CS:M at 
ratio 20:80, CS:M:SBP at 20:50:30 and CS:SBP, 20:80 (A. Merola, 2014). 
3.7.1 Inoculum 
Thermophilic digested manure from Snertinge plant was used as inoculum for all BMP 
experiments. The inoculum was transferred from the effluent bottles of the reactors to a tank and 
placed in a thermophilic incubator (A. Merola, 2014).

3.7.2 Casted seaweed 
CS is reffered to all species of macro algae that are found, washed on shore. CS was collected from 
Køge bay and delivered to the lab untreated and unsieved. It was cleaned from rods and other 
lignocellulosic material and washed in order to remove most of the sand. In addition, the seaweed 
was roughly chopped prior to use. 
3.7.3 Manure 
Manure was collected at the same biogas plant as the inoculum. After collection it was sieved to 
remove straw and was homogenized. Soon after determination of TS, VS, NH3 and total nitrogen 
were determined. Hence it was frozen till its potential use.  
3.7.4 Sugar beet pulp 
SBP, is a residue from sugar beet crop. Quantity equal to circa 30 kg was delivered by Nordic 
Sugar. After characterization, SBP was frozen prior to any experimental use.  
3.8 Energy 
This section describes any type of input or output energy in terms of electricity or heat. Energy 
used is always modelled as energy produced by marginal means. Energy produced by the anaerobic 
digestion is assumed to substitute energy produced by marginal means. However in the case study 
scenario of the off shore wind energy substitution, the energy use is modelled as it comes by 
marginal means while the energy produced is modelled to substitute energy produced by off shore 
wind power installations. 
3.8.1 Energy used 
3.8.1.1 Marginal electricity 
Production of 1 kWh electricity as an average of seven Danish power plants (six combined heat 
and power (CHP) plants, one plant producing power only). These plants are identified as the ones 
capable of adjusting their electricity production to the demands of the market, thus being the 
"marginal" plants. Input for this process is coal (main fuel) and oil whilst output is 1 kWh 
electricity delivered to the consumer (ELCD3.2, 2008b). The LCI includes a 2% loss in 
transmission and 5% loss in distribution. In addition, extraction, processing and transport of coal 
and oil are included in the LCI. Additionally to hard coal, heavy fuel (ELCD3.2, 2003) is also used 
as input in 1% on a mass basis (coal accounts for 99% in term of mass) (EASETECH-database, 
2016).

3.8.2 Energy produced 
Produced biogas is directly driven to a state-of-the-art combustion engine (i.e. Jenbacher 420) with 
0.40 and 0.42 electrical and thermal efficiencies respectively (General Electric, 2016). This 
electricity produced is assumed to substitute electricity produced by marginal means as those were 
defined earlier on.  
In the scenario analysis the electricity produced is meant to substitute electricity produced by 
offshore wind power as described by (Turconi et al., 2014). However, in the scenario analysis the 
all energy inputs (both heat and electricity) remain unaltered, thus being produced by the marginal 
means. 
3.9 Composting 
Composting is modelled as an enclosed channel facility. The average electricity consumption was 
estimated to be 53.41 kWh per Mg waste input, based on real consumption data. No data about 
fuel consumption were available and a value of 1 l of diesel per Mg of waste was assumed based 
on (Boldrin et al., 2009). Based on material and substance flow analysis, the degradation of VS 
and C was estimated being in average 63.8% (73.5% for kitchen waste, 54.6% for garden waste) 
for the whole process. Using data regarding composition of the compost material transferred to 
maturation boxes, it was estimated that approximately 68.6% of the VS degradation occurs during 
the tunnel phase, while the remaining 31.4% occurs during the maturation phase. The same 
distribution between the two stages of the composting process was then assumed for degradation 
of other compounds. With regards to nitrogen, 71 % of the total N input is degraded during the 
composting process. In regard to air emissions, and for the modelling purposes, values measured

for windrow composting technologies (2.2% and 83% of the degraded C and N emitted as CH4 
and NH3 respectively) were assumed as everything was documented by (Boldrin et al., 2009).  
3.10 Use on land 
This process refer to the application on land of the digestate from the AD process or the compost 
from the composting facility. This process accounts for air, surface water groundwater and soil 
accumulation from this land application. Soil is assumed to be sandy loam soil with average Danish 
crop rotation with N application rate below the maximum plant uptake (high response phase). 
Diesel consumption needed for spreading the digestate on land (or compost) assumed to be  
0.00063L of diesel (Bernstad and la Cour Jansen, 2011). Substitution rate is also subjective to 
change. In our case substitution rate for N was set to 0.75 whilst it was set to 1 for P and K. 
However, it is acknowledged here, that the maximum substitution rate for nitrogen present in cattle 
manure imposed by legislation is 0.70 (Danish Ministry of Food, Agriculture and Fisheries) 
Methane emissions were assumed to be 0.05% of carbon input (Ambus; et al., 2001). The fate of 
carbon (C) and nitrogen (N) is simulated by the Daisy, one dimensional, deterministic agro-
ecosystem model. The model couples hydrological, crop growth, mineral nitrogen, and soil organic 
matter models (Hansen S. et al., 2012). The weather data was recorded from the Taastrup climate 
station in Eastern Denmark from 1960 to 1990, adjusted to the Danish average precipitation of 605 
mm/year. Maximum rooting depth (in soil) of 100 cm was assumed. 50% reduction of nitrate in 
the subsoil layer before reaching the groundwater table was also included (Børgesen; et al., 2001). 
The emissions factors associated with application of anaerobically digested were calculated by 
fitting a linear mixed model to the outcome of simulations (EASETECH-database, 2016). The data 
included in this database represent the emission factors in the high response phase, where the 
fertilizer application level does not exceed the plant nutrient needs. Detail of the simulation is 
available on (Yoshida et al., 2015). In this database, the plant uptake of 84.1%, and leaching and 
erosion loss rate of 0.94% was assumed and divided equally to ground and surface water source, 
based on the Danish national scale P budget study (Klinglmair et al., 2015).

3.12 Manure storage 
This process was built exclusively for the modelling purposes as it was not present in 
EASETECH’s library. Manure storage refers to storage of manure at its production site till is used. 
This storage is divided in two main categories: indoor and outdoor. The first one, refers to the 
period where manure is produced till the day it is removed from the animal pit in order to be stored 
outdoor. Indoor manure storage assumed to last for approximately 14 days (Sommer et al., 2009). 
Regarding the outdoor manure storage, Danish law requires the storage tank to be covered in order 
to mitigate emissions and odor nuisance. In our case, the cover of the outdoor manure storage was 
assumed to be a floating layer of straw which is the minimum requirement by law. Hamelin et al. 
(2011) report that this is the most inexpensive method while it is the most widespread as well. 
Emissions were quantified in terms of CH4, NH3-N, N2O-N (direct), N2O-N (indirect, from NH3 
and NOx) and finally NOx-N. All the following values that were used derive from Hamelin et al. 
(2011). More specifically, for indoor storage, 17% of the maximum CH4 producing capacity (0.24 
m3 CH4 per kg VS) was assumed to be emitted into air, while for outdoor this amount was assumed 
to be 10%. NH3-N assumed to be emitted as 8% and 2% for indoor and outdoor storage 
respectively. Furthermore for N2O-N (direct), 0.002 and 0.005 kg of N2O-N per kg N. Indirect 
N2O-N was assumed to be emitted was set to 0.001 kg of N2O-N per kg N for both cases of storage. 
Finally, also for both cases, NOx-N were assumed to be double the amount of direct emissions of 
N2O-N resulting in 0.004 and 0.010 kg of NOx-N per kg N.

3.13 Soybean and Maize production 
In this section, all the processes that are related to the animal feed substitution due to the use of 
SBP in different scenarios, are listed and described. 
3.13.1.1 Cultivation 
Sugar beet pulp is a byproduct from the sugar industry that is widely used in the animal feed 
market. Removing this product from the market induces a need for other kinds of animal feed. In 
order to substitute the animal feed, one should consider the calorific value along with the protein 
content of that feed and then proceed to find the right substitute. In our case marginal feed in terms 
of protein was assumed to be soymeal whilst marginal feed in terms of energy assumed to be 
maize. However, soybean meal is produced by soybeans and soy oil is co-produced as well. This 
oil can introduce the need for another system expansion as it holds the ability to substitute other 
oil products. Yet again the new oil products could be the reason for another system expansion since 
they would be produced along with another protein based product. This never ending system 
known as soybean-rapeseed loop (Weidema, 1999) calls for iterative calculations in order to define 
the final amount of soybeans needed. This iterative process that was adopted in our study is 
described in a very detailed way by Tonini et al. (2015). In regard to the energy and protein content 
for each animal feed data used using the same literature source.  
In our case, a cultivation mixture was assumed for various locations in the world and modelled 
respectivesly according to (Tonini et al., 2015). South, America (SA), North America (rNA), 
Central America (CA), South East Asia (SEAS), Asia (rAS), Africa (AF), Europe (EU) and East 
Europe (rEU) and United Stakes (US). This assumption was made for both maize and soybeans 
with cultivated amounts being subjective to change in regard to the general geographical 
cultivation trends. Amounts of maize and soybeans are found in tables bellow.

3.13.1.2 Land use change 
Land use change impacts quantified according with the deterministic (causal) land use change 
(LUC) model described in Tonini et al. (2015). The impacts reflect the occupation of 1 hectar of 
land for arable purposes for the duration of one year (unit ha*y).Carbon and nitrogen emissions 
are accounted as well as N, P and K fertilizer production. In regard to the actual land occupation 
it was calculated that 0.0002758 ha are needed for the cultivation of one kg ww of maize. The 
respective value for soybean was 0.0004065 ha, both values according to information found in 
Tonini et al. (2015). 
3.13.1.3 Feed processing and Transportation 
Both maize and soybeans need to be processed in order to derive the final product which can be 
used for animal feed. 12 kWh electricity were modelled for the soybean turned into soymeal 
(Dalgaard et al., 2007). No data were found for maize and the aforementioned value was assumed 
for it. This time, electricity consumption was assumed to follow the average of the European 
standards (ELCD3.2, 2002). Finally, transportation was assumed to take place with a bulk carrier 
cargo ship (ELCD3.2, 2008a) for a distance of 12000 km. 
3.14 Capital goods 
Finally, capital goods (materials and energy used) needed for the construction of both facilities 
were modelled according to Brogaard et al. (2015). Though at this point, it is acknowledged that 
capital goods for the composting facility refer to a windrow facility but in our model an enclosed 
channel facility was assumed and modelled.

4.3 Sensitivity Analysis 
In this section all the sensitivity ratios calculated can be found. In Table 17: Sensitivity ratios for 
electrical efficiency of the biogas combustion engine.Table 17 zero values in COMP imply that 
this parameter, electrical efficiency of the biogas combustion engine, is not present in the scenario. 
The same applies for the following parameters: biogas leaking (fugitive losses), thermal efficiency 
of the biogas combustion engine, methane yield and methane content in the biogas and the BMP 
values (Table 18, Table 19, Table 41, Table 42 and Table 43 respectively).  
Furthermore, Table 43 shows the sensitivity ratios for all BMP values and for the respective 
scenario that the BMP parameters are present. For instance BMP value of seaweed only affects 
scenario AD-SW and thus having zero sensitivity for the rest. 
Finally between Table 31 and Table 40 all ten parameters regarding the manure storage (both 
indoor and outdoor) can be found. Again zero values indicate that the process is not present in the 
scenario tested.  
Sensitivity ratios can be positive or negative depending on the effect of the parameter (if it can 
induce savings in emissions or actively create emissions) in combination with the original result 
and weather this is positive or negative.  
This can be better understood when looking sensitivity ratios for GWP and electrical efficiency of 
the biogas combustion engine (Table 17). An increase on this parameter value can only be 
translated to more kg CO2-eq savings. However COAD-SBP has a negative sensitivity ratio while 
the rest AD scenarios have a positive. The latter one implies that the original result of COAD-SBP 
was positive (impact to the environment) and by testing the sensitivity of the electrical efficiency 
indeed yields more savings but the result still remains positive. On the other hand the rest AD 
scenarios have a positive SR which means that the original result was negative and more savings 
found after testing sensitivity of electrical efficiency. It is therefore advised that the absolute value 
of the SR should be considered when comparing sensitivity ratios among scenarios in the same 
impact category, or totally disregard the sign of the SR.
