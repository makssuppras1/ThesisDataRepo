Comparing Neural Network Architectures for
Non-Intrusive Speech Quality Prediction
Leif Førland Schill
DTU Compute
Technical University of Denmark
Lyngby, Denmark
lfirlandschill@gmail.com
Abstract—Non-intrusive speech quality predictors evaluate
speech quality without the use of a reference signal, making them
useful in many practical applications. Recently, neural networks
have shown the best performance for this task. Two such models
in the literature are the convolutional neural network based
DNSMOS and the bi-directional long short-term memory based
Quality-Net, which were originally trained to predict subjective
targets and intrusive PESQ scores, respectively. In this paper,
these two architectures are trained on a single dataset, and used
to predict the intrusive ViSQOL score. The evaluation is done
on a number of test sets with a variety of mismatch conditions,
including unseen speech and noise corpora, and common voice
over IP distortions. The experiments show that the models achieve
similar predictive ability on the training distribution, and overall
good generalization to new noise and speech corpora. Unseen
distortions are identified as an area where both models generalize
poorly, especially DNSMOS. Our results also suggest that a
pervasiveness of ambient noise in the training set can cause
problems when generalizing to certain types of noise. Finally, we
detail how the ViSQOL score can have undesirable dependencies
on the reference pressure level and the voice activity level.
Index Terms—speech quality prediction, non-intrusive, neural
network, generalization
I. INTRODUCTION
Speech transmitted digitally for Voice over IP (VoIP) or
hearing aid applications can include background noise, as well
as various distortions due to signal processing or poor network
connections. It is important to have a metric for evaluating
speech quality, in order to measure user experience and judge
the effects of any signal processing methods being employed.
Ideally these are subjective ratings, collected from human
trials. International Telecommunication Union Telecommuni-
cation Standardization Sector (ITU-T) recommendation P-800
[1] is a testing framework in which subjects rate the quality of
a speech sample on a five-point scale. A mean opinion score
(MOS) is obtained by averaging these ratings over multiple
subjects. In practice, it may be desirable to evaluate speech
quality at greater speeds or in larger quantities than human
trials allow for. In these cases, objective metrics provide a
useful way to predict human perception.
One class of objective metrics are the full-reference, or
“intrusive” methods which compare the degraded signal with
a reference clean speech signal. Generally, the two signals
are aligned in time and transformed into the time-frequency
domain, after which some perceptual model is applied (e.g.,
log mel power). Then, some similarity measure is calculated
across the time-frequency bins and aggregated into a single
value. Perceptual Evaluation of Speech Quality (PESQ) [2]
was the second-generation ITU-T recommendation. It was
superseded by Perceptual Objective Listening Quality As-
sessment (POLQA) [3], which provided better correlation
with human ratings across a wider range of conditions, and
super wideband capabilities. Virtual Speech Quality Objective
Listener (ViSQOL) [4] is an open-source metric that has been
shown to produce results comparable to PESQ and POLQA
[5].
The drawback of the intrusive methods is the need for a
clean reference, which is usually not available in practical
applications. Furthermore, the ground-truth used for evaluating
these methods has been produced by humans without using a
reference. This suggests that predicting speech quality without
a reference is not a limitation, so long as human perception
can be adequately modelled. ITU-T rec. P.563 [6] used vocal
tract analysis to reconstruct a quasi-clean signal, then applied a
full-reference model, but did not match the performance of the
intrusive PESQ. Recently, neural network based approaches
have proved capable of producing predictions better than
P.563, and in some cases better than intrusive metrics like
PESQ [7], [8]. In [9], low-dimensional features were extracted
from the audio spectrum with an autoencoder and used as
input to a neural network (NN). Deep Noise Suppression MOS
(DNSMOS) is a convolutional neural network (CNN) based
model that was trained on a large dataset of subjective ratings
[10], [11]. Quality-Net [12] used a bidirectional long short-
term memory (BLSTM) architecture to predict PESQ scores
with good accuracy.
An attractive feature of NN based metrics is that they are in-
herently fully differentiable, meaning that it is straightforward
to use them for training speech enhancement NNs. In [13], the
Quality-Net authors trained an intrusive model to predict non-
differentiable PESQ scores. This prediction model was frozen
and concatenated on the end of an enhancement model, which
was then trained to optimize the approximated PESQ. Based
on this method, a non-intrusive model could be used to train
enhancement models on large amounts of noisy data, or tune
their parameters in real-time.
==PAGE:2==
In this paper, the CNN and BLSTM architectures will
be compared using the models originally described in the
DNSMOS [10] and Quality-Net [12] papers as points of
departure, and ViSQOL as the prediction target. They will
be trained on a large dataset containing speech with different
amounts of noise, reverb, and enhancement. This will provide
a controlled comparison of the models, as they were originally
trained on different metrics and different data.
In addition to the evaluation of the models on this general
training distribution, the generalization ability of the models
will be evaluated by creating a number of test sets with various
mismatch conditions. It has been shown that NNs do not
always generalize well to new speech corpora for tasks like
speech enhancement [14]. However, it can be hard to draw
conclusions about the generalization ability of a model when
predicting on new data, because there is also an effect from
the relative difficulty of the new data for the task at hand.
By generating multiple test sets for each mismatch condition
(speech corpus, noise corpus, distortion type, etc.) it will be
possible to obtain a clearer picture of which conditions affect
generalization ability the most for each model. This investiga-
tion will provide insight into the operating mechanisms of the
models and also provide information on how well they can be
expected to perform in real-world scenarios.
II. MODEL ARCHITECTURES
The DNSMOS and Quality-Net architectures are described
here, with some modifications from the original models [10]
and [12]. The modifications are described and motivated as
they appear.
A. DNSMOS model
The DNSMOS model relies mainly on convolutional layers,
which apply filter kernels invariantly across the spectrogram.
The first layer does basic feature extraction like edge detection
and thresholding, and subsequent layers develop increasingly
abstract features. Pooling layers are used to reduce dimen-
sionality between the convolutions. The final global pooling
aggregates each feature across the time-frequency domain into
a single value. These values are fed into two dense layers and
mapped to an output score.

CNN architecture is that there is no longer an assumption of
invariance in the frequency dimension. That is, each frequency
bin is now treated as its own unique feature. The LSTM cell
uses weight matrices, called gates, to update a hidden state as

III. MODEL EVALUATION
The goal of the data generation was to produce a general
training set, a corresponding test set from the same distri-
bution, and a number of test sets with various mismatch
conditions.
A. Training set
100, 000 reference-degraded pairs of 10s length (277 hours)
were generated by randomly combining noise and single-
speaker clean speech. Reverberation was applied to the clean
==PAGE:4==
speech with probability 30%, after which a SNR was sampled
in the range [−10, 40] dB, and the noise was scaled to the
sampled SNR in relation to the overall reverberant speech
level. Enhancement was then applied to the noisy speech with
probability 50%. Finally, the degraded speech was scaled to
a level of −30 dB, and the visqol score was calculated using
ViSQOL v3.0.0.
1) Clean speech: The clean speech was created by con-
catenating single-speaker utterances from the VCTK corpus
[16].
2) Noise: The noise was created by combining background
noise from the TUT Acoustic Scenes 2017 dataset [17] with
foreground noise from freesound [18], using the open source
Scaper library [19].
3) Reverberation: 900 IRS were generated virtually us-
ing [20]. The room size, absorption coefficent, and speaker-
microphone-distance were sampled to reflect a range of normal
close-mic VoIP conditions.
4) Enhancement:

B. Mismatched test sets
Each test set contains 1000 noisy files with corresponding
ViSQOL targets. The generation parameters were the same as
in the training set, unless stated otherwise.
1) Training distribution: A test set was generated from the
training distribution, to use for reference.
2) Speech mismatch: Four test sets were generated using
clean speech from different corpora (TSP [24], ARCTIC [25],
Arabic Speech Corpus [26], and Att-hack expressive French
speech [27]). A test set was generated using recordings made
in the GN lab in accordance with standard IEC 268-13 [28],
using a head and torso simulator (HATS) and a close-mic array
composed of 4 microphones placed at different distances from
the hats to simulate recordings from a headset.
3) Noise mismatch: Four test sets were generated using
noise from different corpora (DEMAND [29], Noisex [30],
ARTE [31], and TAU [32]). A test set was generated using
white noise.
4) Distortion mismatch: A test set was generated using
classical spectral subtraction enhancement [33] with [34],
instead of the NN model. Two test sets were generated by
simulating codec distortion and packet loss with [35]. A test
set was generated by introducing pitch shift and time stretch
artifacts with [36]. The codec distortion, packet loss, and
strech/shift artifact test sets did not contain noise, reverb, or
enhancement.
5) Miscellaneous mismatch: Two test sets were created by
generating shorter (6s) or longer (16s) signals. Two test sets
were generated with very high (95%) and very low (25%)
speech activity. A test set was generated where the speaker
changed every few seconds. For the low activity test set, the
SNR was calculated using the active part of the speech, so as
not to overly misrepresent the effective SNR.
C. ViSQOL Limitations
Although the ViSQOL score is being used as a truth value
for the predictions, the authors do not guarantee that it will cor-
relate well to subjective scores for certain applications which
include denoising [21]. Nor will the scores always reflect the
overall quality of the test signal, since only three half-second
patches are used in the calculation. Lastly, the ViSQOL score
may have unexpected correlations with parameters it should
be invariant to. Some of these are described in the following.
1) Effect of reference level on ViSQOL score: Subjective
MOS ratings decrease at low levels, since the signal be-
comes hard for humans to hear. Like PESQ, ViSQOL is
not designed to take this effect into consideration. The loss
function between the reference and degraded spectrograms is
theoretically invariant to level [22]. However, we found that
the ViSQOL output depends significantly on the reference
input level (Fig. 6). The output did not depend very much
on the noisy level, which should be the case given that the
both signals are normalized to the reference level in ViSQOL’s
preprocessing [4]. The level-dependent behaviour may be due
to the noise floor that ViSQOL uses to keep values from
becoming too small in the log power spectogram [21]. As the
==PAGE:5==
level decreases, more bin values may reach the noise floor,
causing the spectrograms to become more and more similar.
Following these results, we calculated all the ViSQOL scores
using reference levels of approximately −30 dB, which were
in the most well behaved range of Fig. 6.

2) Effect of voice activity on ViSQOL score: The ViSQOL
authors mention in [37] that low voice activity can cause

It is interesting that the models trained in this report had
such biased fits to the training data, therefore benefiting
from the polynomial mappings shown in Fig. 2 and 4. The
scatterplots provided in the DNSMOS and Quality-Net papers
do not show biased test errors. Also, one would expect such
large models to be able to perform something equivalent to
a four-parameter polynomial map. One question to ask might
be whether the models are inherently incapable of representing
this mapping, or if it simply wasn’t learned during training.
Given the size of the models, one would expect the latter to
be true. The difference in the shapes of the biases could be
explained by the Quality-Net model placing more importance
on correctly predicting the high MOS targets due to the
increased contribution to the loss from the frame constraint

their fit in some region of the scatterplot. That the Quality-Net
model trained on the range of levels successfully learned an

Another goal of this study was to compare the generalization
ability of the models across the mismatch cases. Some of the
speech and noise corpora had only slightly increased errors
for both models (Fig. V, N1-N3, S2-S3). These errors give an
idea of what performance to expect when testing on an unseen
corpus, in a best-case scenario.
There were also cases where the models did not generalize
as well. The TAU noise corpus test showed significantly worse
performance for both models. This noise corpus was different
from the others, in that it contained only local noise events, and
no ambient noise. Since the training noise was always a mix of
ambient and local noise, with the levels of the two even being
correlated, the models probably learned to associate periods
of near silence with high MOS scores. This is supported by
the layer 1 activations in Fig. 7, where it can be seen that
DNSMOS identifies the silent parts of the signal. It is also
consistent with both models over-predicting the TAU scores.
It can be concluded that an important consideration when
designing training data is to ensure some of the noises are
purely non-ambient. The levels of ambient and non-ambient
noise should ideally be uncorrelated in the training signals.
The white noise test showed reduced performance mainly
for Quality-Net. Since the BLSTM architecture does not
assume invariance in the frequency dimension, it could be
expected that Quality-Net would learn the frequencies as-
sociated with the training noise. This may have caused the
reduced performance, as the white noise occurs uniformly in
all frequencies. This would suggest that Quality-Net is more
prone to overfitting specifics of the frequency distributions in
the training noise and speech.
The codec distortion, packet loss, and stretch/shift artifact
test set errors were very poor for both models, especially
DNSMOS. Even when the distortions were significant enough
for the ViSQOL score to decrease substantially, the NN
models predicted high scores. These types of “information
loss” distortions were not present at all in the training set,
==PAGE:8==
so it might not be surprising that they could not be identified
as problematic during testing. Training a model to identify
these distortions would be an interesting topic for another
experiment.
Fig. 10 shows a similar but lesser effect for the spectral
subtraction test, where the models over-estimate the quality
of the enhanced signals. This could be due to the “musical
noise” characteristic of the spectral subtraction method, in
which subtraction of the estimated noise spectrum from the
signal produces short-lived sinusoidal distortions with random
frequencies [38]. This type of artifact was apparently different
enough from the NN enhancement artifacts in the training data
to produce worse predictions, but not different enough to be
completely unidentifiable.
There were some results where the validity of the ViSQOL
targets had to be questioned. Looking at the target distributions
in Fig. VI, it seems like the high speech activity test set
had higher MOS targets than the low speech activity test set.
This should not be the case, and was likely due to the level
dependence of ViSQOL described in Table III. The Arabic
and French test sets also had skewed distributions, and it
was determined that they also had higher and lower voice
activity distributions than the training data. Figure 9 shows
the similarities in the predictions between these two pairs of
test sets. It is hard to make judgements about the general-
ization ability of the models here, because the MSE might
also measuring the ability of the model to fit the unwanted
behaviour of ViSQOL from the variations in voice activity
during training. Given the speculation that silent regions may
be important to the models when estimating quality, looking
into the effects of voice activity on predictions could be worth
investigating in another experiment, where a different metric
is being predicted.
In general, when predicting an intrusive metric, care should
be taken to avoid situations where it does not represent
subjective scores. In this report an assumption was made that
the ViSQOL score is most reliable when calculated with a
high reference level. This seems reasonable given the results
in Fig. 6, but it has not been verified. It would also be good to
do a more in-depth investigation of the effect of voice-activity
described in Table III, to determine a range where the metric
is stable.
This also relates to the IEC test set, in which the reference
recording contained a small amount of reverb. This caused
the prediction shape seen in Appendix VI S1, where the
high-ViSQOL signals that were essentially unmodified “clean”
recordings produced low ratings from the NNs, which had
been trained on ViSQOL targets using a completely dry
reference. In the view of the dry-reference ViSQOL as a
ground truth, the predictions from the NNs would be good.
However, it can be discussed how much small amounts of
reverb really decrease subjective quality. In either case it is
probably best to make a motivated choice about the reference
recordings, and remain consistent with it during training.
Both models performed worse for the short 6s signal test.
Although the signal length should not impact the ViSQOL
score (Section III-C3), the generation process may have pro-
duced different voice activity levels for the short signal as
well. The predictions did not suffer for the longer 16s signal.
In fact, they were significantly better for both models. This
can be explained by the models having more information to
produce a lower variance score.
The test with multiple speakers did not show a change
in performance for either model. This might suggest that
the Quality-Net LSTM cells do not learn anything about the
particular speaker as the signal is being traversed in time.
The layer 4 activations in Fig. 7 might suggest that DNS-
MOS is able to do some separation of the noise and speech
from the noisy signal. Specifically, the middle activation
resembles the noise component, and the bottom activation
resembles the speech. Comparing these features may be a
mechanism by which the quality score is produced.
VI. CONCLUSION
The DNSMOS and Quality-Net architectures were trained
on one dataset to predict the same ViSQOL target. They
showed similar predictive ability on these data, and good
generalization to most new speech and noise corpora. Poor
generalization was observed when testing on a noise corpus
without ambient noise, which suggests that areas of silence
will become an important predictor of quality if the noise
in the training set is not diverse enough. Especially poor
generalization was observed for unseen distortions like codec
distortion and packet loss. Both models failed to make un-
biased predictions without a polynomial mapping, which is a
behaviour unseen when predicting other metrics. The ViSQOL
score may not be representative of the true quality when the
reference level is too low, or the voice activity is in a certain
range, and care should be taken to avoid these situations when
generating training targets.
