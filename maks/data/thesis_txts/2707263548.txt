ABSTRACT 
Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) are a broad group of gram-positive bacteria that have actively been 
used worldwide in food productions due to their nutritional, technological and flavour-related 
characteristics, which define them as powerful biological tools to be used in food applications. 
Mainly, their use is focused on the production of fermented food products such as cheese, 
yogurt and kimchi (Quinto et al., 2014). Apart from their promising and diverse phenotypes, 
some of the LAB species have been classified as GRAS or QPS which determines their 
innocuousness traits, allowing them to be used for food consumption (Mora‐Villalobos et al., 
2020).  
LAB are ubiquitous in nature and have been found in multiple natural sources such as plants, 
animals and soils, among others (Mora‐Villalobos et al., 2020). Nevertheless, environmental 
sources have complex microbiomes, making the culture of every microorganism that is present 
in a sample burdensome for most microbiologists (Sarhan et al., 2019). Food fermentations have 
widely been divided into spontaneous and inoculated fermentations; the former is performed 
by using the natural microbiome present in a food matrix and stablishing certain fermentative 
conditions to generate safe, nutritional and flavour-enhanced food products, while the latter is 
based on the addition (inoculation) of well-characterized bacteria (isolated from a different 
source) into a food matrix to dominate the food environment under specific fermentative 
conditions. The inoculated bacteria strains are known as starter cultures and have popularly 
been genotypic- and phenotypically characterized to maximumly exploit their potential abilities 
into specific environments (Di Cagno et al., 2013). 
In this project, some of the starter culture development phases have been applied. Firstly, the 
isolation of bacteria (including LAB) and yeasts from plant and insect samples using different 
approaches has been investigated. The isolation process optimization was based on: the use of 
three different growth media to compare the culturable biodiversity insights reached on each 
media, and the performance of two different isolation methods, the plate culturing traditional 
method and the droplet-based microfluidics. After the isolation process, the isolated species 
were identified. Then, a strain screening process was compared by using both isolation methods. 
Finally, from all the LAB strains isolated from those sources, some were selected for further 
genotypical and phenotypical characterization, to be applied to plant-based fermentations. 
Therefore, technological, metabolic and flavour-related fermentative abilities have been studied 
of those LAB strains isolated, considering the complexity of plants as food fermentation 
matrices. 
From the beginning of the research project, two main objectives have been pursued. 
• 
The optimization of the LAB isolation process, based on the best suited isolation 
conditions and methods, in order to obtain the highest culturable LAB biodiversity. 
• 
The design of a phenotypical characterization platform for testing and applying LAB 
strains into plant-based foods.

1. INTRODUCTION TO THE PROJECT 
Lactic Acid Bacteria (LAB) are among the best-studied microorganisms due to the number of new 
potential applications in the food industry. LAB are present in multiple sources such as milk, 
plants, meat, animal microbiomes, among others, and they have been used in food production 
for centuries, before their existence was even known (Konings et al., 2000). Nowadays, LAB have 
been extensively applied to traditional and industrial food fermentations such as yogurt, cheeses 
and butter, for example, as dairy products, but they have also been involved in plant-based 
fermented products such as sauerkraut, kimchi and olives (“Microbiol. Fermented Foods,” 
1997). In the past centuries, foods were fermented traditionally by using the autochthon 
bacteria (and sometimes yeast) as the source of the fermentative activity but, in the last century, 
applied microbiology studies have led to the selection of specific bacteria to be applied into 
specific food matrices to obtain, final specific technological and organoleptic properties desired 
(Konings et al., 2000). Therefore, the transition of traditional food fermentations to industrial 
food fermentations has been a gigantic step in the lacto-fermented food industry. Although 
there are products that are still traditionally fermented, industrial lacto-fermented foods require 
the inoculation of pure mono- or cocultured LAB strains.  
An example of an industrial lacto-fermented food is the standard industrial yogurt production, 
which requires the coculture of Lactobacillus delbruecki spp bulgaricus and Streptococcus 
thermophilus, creating a symbiotic microbial relationship during the fermentation process. In 
this symbiosis, S. thermophilus starts the development of lactic acid fermenting lactose, and 
grows rapidly until the milk reaches a pH of 5.5, where the growth of L. bulgaricus starts, which 
eventually outnumbers S. thermophilus, at the end of the fermentation (“Microbiol. Fermented 
Foods,” 1997).  
Due to the need to develop final formulations of starter cultures for industrial processes, the 
isolation, screening and characterization of LAB strains from different environmental sources 
has been a focus for the industry. Collections of LAB strains, belonging to different genus and 
species, have been created with the main purpose of finding further food applications. 
Therefore, an enormous attention has been paid to the optimization of the isolation process to 
obtain as many LAB strains as possible, which can then be tested for their performance and 
phenotypes in relation to their pursued food applications. 
In this project, two different isolation process variables were considered; the growth media 
used, and the isolation method applied to the selected environmental samples. Three different 
growth media have been compared when isolating bacteria and yeast from different 
environmental samples, and two different methods, traditional plate culturing method (TM) and 
droplet-based microfluidics (MF), have been contrasted. The culturable biodiversity parameters 
have been calculated and statistically compared within the mentioned conditions. 
Furthermore, the undoubtable transition from animal-based to plant-based food products has 
been the focus for lacto-fermented food industries. Plant-based fermented products have been 
popularly increasing their market every year due to its notable higher sustainability, health-
related benefits such as lactose free content, lifestyle, and dietary reasons, in comparison to 
animal-based products (Tangyu et al., 2019). Nevertheless, there are still challenges in terms of 
flavour and nutritional values. For instance, many of the potential nutrients present in those 
sources are not bioavailable for humans (Tangyu et al., 2019). For this reason, some strains 
isolated from plants and insects, found during the isolation experiments performed in this 
project, have been phenotypically tested to be used in plant-based food applications.

1.1. CULTURABLE AND NON-CULTURABLE BACTERIA SPECIES 
It has been well-established that only around 1% of bacteria on Earth can be cultivated in vitro. 
The phenomenon called “great plate count anomaly” was defined based on the fact the 
microscopical observational counts were larger than the total viable counts when they were 
grown in the laboratory (Vartoukian et al., 2010). Therefore, the unculturable traits of most 
bacteria underestimates the biodiversity of complex microbial communities when using 
traditional cultivation methods (Vartoukian et al., 2010). Based on this, other culturing methods 
need to be explored to enable the isolation of non-culturable bacteria, trying to minimize the 
gap between the microbial richness in nature and the number of species in culture (Sarhan et 
al., 2019). 
Thus, the explanations for those unculturability traits must be defined to understand the 
inability to identify by culturing, some bacterial species. Some reasons might be microorganisms 
that have a low prevalence or are slow-growers and may have been overlooked when isolating 
in agar plates. Otherwise, some bacteria are resistant to be cultured or have specific growth 
requirements such as specific nutrients, pH conditions, temperatures or levels of oxygen 
(Vartoukian et al., 2010). Thus, when the nutritional requirements are not supplied, and there is 
a competition for nutrients among microbial communities cultured together, some bacteria may 
not grow. Also, there could be other kind of competition relations, such as the production of 
antimicrobial substances which are sensitive to specific microorganisms (Vartoukian et al., 
2010). Nevertheless, the growth of some bacteria could cross-feed other type of bacteria, for 
instance, when the lactic acid produced by LAB, then is utilized as C-source by Veillonella sp 
(Vartoukian et al., 2010), among a lot of positive or negative competition events. 
“Culturomics” term has been applied to the science of culturing microorganisms from 
environmental samples. The strategies to culture what was thought to be unculturable, have 
focused on four main points: in situ and high throughput cultivation, culture media 
development, the incubation conditions and genome-derived cultivation information (omics). In 
this project, the main focus has been the study of the culture media and the in situ high 
throughput cultivation in comparison with standard cultivation method. In some studies, some 
of the common strategies to optimize the isolation process, trying to identify the inherent 
natural biodiversity, have been: lowering the nutrient concentrations in standard media 
together with longer incubation times, co-incubating cells individually encapsulated into 
microdroplets under low flux nutrient conditions, adding signalling compounds and/or co-
cultivation to trigger microbial growth, and mimicking natural conditions applying in situ 
cultivation techniques such as diffusion chambers, among others (Sarhan et al., 2019).

1.2. LACTIC ACID BACTERIA (LAB) 
Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) are a group of gram-positive bacteria, non-sporulating, anaerobic or 
facultative aerobic cocci or rods, which produce lactic acid as one of the main fermentation 
products of the metabolism of carbohydrates. LAB were firstly classified by Orla-Jensen in the 
early 20th century, and further reviewed, subclassifying the four main LAB groups into 
subcategories based on genotype- and phenotypical characteristics (Quinto et al., 2014). LAB 
are included in the phylum Firmicutes, class Bacilli and order Lactobacillales and divided into six 
main families, Aerococcaceae, Carnobacteriaceae, Enterococcaceae, Lactobacillaceae, 
Leuconostocaceae, and Streptococcaceae, over 30 genera, and more than 300 species. The 
genus Bifidobacterium is also included to LAB group, although it belongs to the phylum 
Actinobacteria (Mora‐Villalobos et al., 2020).  
LAB are ubiquitous in nature, present in a variety of habitats such as food, feed, humans, animals 
and soil (Mora‐Villalobos et al., 2020). LAB have specific nutritional requirements that may vary 
within species and strains, but they are considered fast growing microorganisms, which might 
be associated to their broad metabolic activities such as organic acid and antimicrobial 
compounds production. Those characteristics, among others, are the key to LAB benefits and 
applications. Nevertheless, their fast growing and metabolic characteristics are influenced by 
their biochemical and biophysical environments (Hayek & Ibrahim, 2013). Their phenotypic 
traits make them suitable for a broad range of applications such as antifungal and anti-
mycotoxigenic agents, nutraceutical producers, probiotics, starter cultures, among other 
applications in the food and medical industry, as they are classified as GRAS by the FDA and QPS 
by EFSA (with some LAB species exemptions) (Mora‐Villalobos et al., 2020). 
In terms of fermented foods, LAB have been used worldwide in alcoholic drinks, condiments, 
dairy, meat and plant fermented foods for centuries, to enhance the food safety, food 
nutritional values, organoleptic (colour, texture and flavour) and preservation properties. For 
instance, Leuconostoc mesenteroides and Lactobacillus sakei play an important role of

biopreservation during the production of sake, the traditional Japanese wine made from rice 
(Robinson, 2014). Furthermore, the Thai fermented mussel called Hoi-dong, is traditionally 
fermented by predominant species of Tetragenococcus halophilus and Lactobacillus farciminis 
or the traditional fermented salted shrimp Balao-balao, typically produced in the Philippines, 
where the presence of L. mesenteroides, Pediococcus sp. and L. plantarum influences the final 
fermented product taste (Robinson, 2014). Moreover, condiments such as Tempoyak, made 
from the durian fruit in Malaysia and Indonesia, which is fermented by the metabolic activity of 
Leuconostoc spp. and Fructobacillus durionis (Robinson, 2014), or meats such as salami (Italy and 
Hungary) or narezushi (Japan) are fermented by L. plantarum, which extends their shelf-life and 
enhances their organoleptic properties. Nevertheless, dairy products have been, by history, the 
products where LAB have been most extensively involved. Cheeses, yogurts, buttermilk, sour 
cream, kefir, among others, are mainly fermented by LAB strains such as Lactococcus lactis, 
Streptococcus thermophilus and Lactobacillus helveticus, among others (Gänzle, 2015a). 
Of all the aforementioned usages of LAB in fermented foods, plant-based fermentations are the 
main focus of this project. Plant-based fermentations include vegetables, fruits, grains, beans, 
cereals and nuts. The focus on this group of fermented products is due the consumer trend 
towards fresh, highly nutritional value, sustainable, health-promoting and rich flavour ready-to-
eat characteristics that can be obtained with plant-based fermentations (Di Cagno et al., 2013).  
A huge variety of plant-based LAB fermented products can be found worldwide. For example, 
fermented rice cake called “puto” (Philippines), where Leuconostoc spp. dextran producing 
strains are dominant in the fermentation, or kimchi which is fermented Chinese cabbage where 
LAB species such as Weisella confusa, Leuconostoc citreum and Lactobacillus spp. have been 
isolated (Robinson, 2014). For industrially based fermentations, starter cultures for plant 
fermentations may come from the autochthonous microbiota or allochthonous strains may be 
used for that purpose. Normally, autochthonous strains are more adapted to the plant matrix 
to be used, and are exempted of growth obstacles such as long latency phases, less exo-
polysaccharide (EPS) production or the synthesis volatile components that may cause off-
flavours which are related to the usage of allochthonous instead of autochthonous strains (Di 
Cagno et al., 2013). 
Anyway, not all autochthonous or allochthonous strains can be used in all plant matrices. The 
plant matrix to be fermented needs to be characterized as well as the performance of the strains 
in those conditions should be tested. There are main criteria points for the selection of the 
strains, and the corresponding metabolic traits, that are usually divided into three main  
categories:  pro-technological traits such as growth rate, acidification rate, pectinolytic activity, 
low temperature growth, or sensory traits such as the synthesis of aroma compounds (diacetyl); 
and nutritional traits such as synthesis of biogenic compounds and EPS (Di Cagno et al., 2013).

1.3.2.  INSECTS 
Insects, mainly honeybees have been the focus of isolation sources of LAB strains. LAB species 
belonging to Lactobacillus, Leuconostoc, Pediococcus, Weisella, Enterococcus and Micrococcus 
where isolated from different parts of the honeybee and its related parts and environment 
(honey stomach, honey, bee pollen, bee bread and royal jelly) from different honeybee species 
such as Apis mellifera (Mathialagan et al., 2018). Lactobacillus kunkeei has been reported as the 
most common fructophilic LAB species (Mathialagan et al., 2018). Recent studies have 
demonstrated the abundance of novel species of Bifidobacterium spp. in Apis honeybee species 
as well as the importance of L. kunkeei in bee microbiota, which was predominantly found in the 
majority of honeybees collected from different continents (Vásquez et al., 2012). 
Bumblebees from the genus Bombus spp., have been also reported as LAB sources of many novel 
species. Based on MALDI-TOF clusters, strains of Fructobacillus tropeaoli, Leuconostoc 
mesenteroides, Lactobacillus kunkeei, Enterococcus faecalis and Lactococcus lactis and some 
clusters that required metagenomic analysis, found out novel species named Lactobacillus 
bombicola and Weisella bombi (Praet et al., 2015a). Furthermore, Lactobacillus, 
Carnobacterium, Bifidobacterium and Fructobacillus genera have been found in bumblebees 
collected from Europe (Koch & Schmid-Hempel, 2011). Some studies have revealed the 
importance of the presence of LAB in bumblebee guts that protect the host against infections of 
the bacterium Paenibacillus larvae, the pathogenic agent of the American foulbrood, apart from 
other parasites (Koch & Schmid-Hempel, 2011). 
Moreover, fructophilic lactic acid bacteria are commonly found in fructose rich niches such as 
bumblebees, but also in fruit flies and Camponotus ants. Fructobacillus spp., which are classified 
as Leuconostoc sp. and L. kunkeei are the most frequently present species in those niches. These 
specific niche species have unique biochemical properties rarely found in other species isolated 
from other niches. Those species grow better in fructose than in glucose, in which grow poorly 
if other final electron acceptors (such as oxygen) are not present (Endo, 2012). 
Apart from bees and bumblebees, other insects such as vinegar flies or crickets have been 
sources of potential LAB species of industrial interest. In the former, L. plantarum was the most 
found species although other Lactobacillus (L. sanfranciscensis and L. paracasei), Leuconostoc 
and Pediococcus (GROENEWALD et al., 2006). In the latter, cricket powder was subjected to 
spontaneous fermentation, and strains of L. plantarum, L. curvatus, L. garvieae and W. confusa 
were isolated (Galli et al., 2020).

1.4. ISOLATION METHODS OF BACTERIA FROM ENVIRONMENTAL SAMPLES 
Nowadays, most of the microbial biodiversity analysis performed from environmental samples 
is made based on metagenomics and phylogenetic data analysis but, due to the fact of the 
majority of bacterial and archaeal species remain uncultured, new experimental culturing 
experiments require to be explored to understand ecological roles, cell biology and species 
characterization (Lewis et al., 2020). Culturing is overall needed for species that need to have an 
industrial application. Furthermore, genomic data does not provide which genes are functionally 
expressed or how active their proteome adapts into certain biochemical conditions. Therefore, 
species isolation and culturing are necessary to determine metabolism, physiology, single cell 
biology and characterization (Lewis et al., 2020). 
To have an approximated idea, based on 16S rRNA sequencing, 400000 archaeal and bacterial 
species have been identified but only 140000 have been cultured, normally belonging to four 
bacterial phyla: Bacteroides, Proteobacteria, Firmicutes and Actinobacteria (Lewis et al., 2020).  
Despite new methodologies and technologies have been exploited for isolation and culturomics, 
the most used experimental basis is the standard/traditional culturing method, where the 
environmental samples are grown in specific liquid media, plated into agar plates, and the 
different colonies are picked for further identification and pure culture generation. In the 
traditional plate culturing method, the combination of multiple variables such as the selective 
nutrient media used, physicochemical conditions set up and the randomness of colony picking 
from agar media, are normally studied for optimization, based on the growth requirements of 
the microorganisms of interest. Apart of the previously mentioned traditional isolation protocol, 
some other also considered traditional and have been used to successfully isolate 
microorganisms with specific phenotypes. As shown in Figure 2, other traditional methods as 
motility-based enrichment, which is based on motility phenotypes as phototaxis or 
magnetotaxis, density-based separation by using centrifugation, dilution to extinction and size 
selection by filtration, are specific microbial methods that have been created based on specific 
microbial traits (Lewis et al., 2020). Although they have been specifically designed, all of them 
are still labour-intensive, with low throughput but simple to implement because any high-
technology or expensive utilities are normally needed (Lewis et al., 2020).

Considering the basis of those methods, only the microorganisms with specific traits or the fast-
growers (or high prevalent) in specific medias and conditions, will be the ones finally isolated. 
The competition factor between species in specific growth setup is not eliminated by using these 
isolation methodologies. In this case, LAB belong to the Firmicutes phyla which their species are 
not considered difficult to be cultured. Despite of this fact, the microbial communities where 
they live, characterized by their species relative abundance, environmental conditions and 
metabolic similarities within species, may cause difficulties on isolating all the LAB species and 
strains present in those environments with using traditional methods.  
Innovative isolation methodologies aimed to overcome those competition and prevalence 
problems. They are based on: scaling-up the number of cell isolation, to increase the probability 
of isolating interesting species (high-throughput isolation and cultivation), or selectively isolate 
microorganisms with specific phenotypical characteristics (targeted isolation). Membrane 
diffusion cultivation, microfluidic systems, and cell sorting-based are examples of innovative 
methods used for overcoming culturing limitations stablished by traditional culturing methods 
(Lewis et al., 2020). 
Membrane diffusion-based cultivation method relies on the inability of sufficiently mimic the 
natural environment media when designing the synthetic culture media that is going to be used 
for isolation. Thus, this technique is based on spatial separation between cells and natural media 
(for example soil) by a membrane, with a pore size sufficiently small to diffuse necessary 
molecules such growth factors to the cells. With this technique, the excessive concentrations of 
nutrients used in classical media is avoided, which may negatively affect the growth of some 
species (Lewis et al., 2020). 
Microfluidics cultivation system is a high scalable and throughput isolation method which allows 
the manipulation of large number of single cells into single droplets eliminating the competition 
factor between species and allowing the slow growing species or low prevalent species to grow, 
due to the physical separation provided by single droplets (Lewis et al., 2020). 
Finally, cell-sorting based cultivation normally is used with many different technologies, 
including droplet-based or microfluidic-based sorters. FACS, FISH and genome-resolved 
metagenomics are some of the techniques included in this approach. The sorting is generally 
based on fluorescence (or autofluorescence) or absorbance. In the following figure, the 
innovative isolation approaches are represented including different techniques employed on

1.5. DROPLET-BASED MICROFLUIDICS 
The microfluidic system has many applications, but due to the limitations provided by traditional 
cultivation methods, has been employed for high-throughput isolation and cultivation 
strategies. Examples of technologies that use microfluidics system are SlipChip, droplet-based 
cultivation and NMMI. SlipChips works by incubating single cells separately in thousands of 
microcompartments, which can be modified and use different growth media and substrates in 
parallel, and then the compartments are slipped apart, and can be screened for growth and 
taxonomic identification (Lewis et al., 2020). 
Droplet-based microfluidics is an emerging technology where picolitre-sized droplets are used 
as compartments for microbiological cultivation experiments. The droplets are normally water 
dispersed in fluorinated oil, where each interface is stable due to the presence of surfactants. 
Its basis provides the understanding of having single cells growing in single diminutive 
bioreactors (Chen et al., 2018). Droplet-based systems achieve the benefit of manipulating 
environmental samples with large numbers of cells in parallel and being able to introduce single-
cells into single-droplets which has overcome one of the most important limitations, the 
competition factor provided by growing cells in a shared synthetic growth media. Also, the 
droplet-based microfluidic system has favoured the growth of anaerobic microorganisms 
because some systems can keep low levels of O2 inside the droplets (Lewis et al., 2020). Due to 
the small size of the droplets, droplet-based microfluidics can achieve a throughput over 1000 
events/second (Chen et al., 2018).  
The droplet-based microfluidics setup is based on the creation of monodisperse water-in-oil-
droplets that can be generated by a standard approach, where an aqueous phase, which 
contains the environmental microbial community dispersed in a growth medium, and an oil 
phase that contains surfactants, are flowed through a microfluidic chip, thus, generating 
droplets. In order to encapsulate single cells into single droplets, the concentration of cells is a 
==PAGE:15==
10 
 
determining factor to consider (Chen et al., 2018). The distribution of cells inside droplets 
follows the Poisson statistics and it can be modified by adjusting the cell concentration in the 
aqueous phase. Empirically, the λ parameter is defined as the average number of cells inside 
each droplet, which is normally adjusted to below 0.5 (Chen et al., 2018). The droplet generation 
is based on the microfluidic chip geometry, concretely, the nozzle architecture, that can typically 
be T-junction, flow focusing, coflowing and DropChop geometry depending on the experiment 
to be performed (Ning et al., 2021). 
Once the cells are encapsulated into droplets, they are incubated and can be sorted or directly 
plated, according to the purpose of the study. If a sorting procedure is stablished to be 
continued, a fluorescence-activated droplet sorting (FADS) is usually used, based on a sorting 
chip that is embedded with two saline-based/electrodes connections that allow the electric field 
to flow, and the sorting power to separate empty from cell-containing droplets. The laser 
illuminates the droplets already incubated, that are sorted based on fluorescence intensities 
(Chen et al., 2018).

plate incubation (Chen et al., 2018). 
Apart from isolation purposes, droplet-based microfluidics can be used for screening purposes. 
Production of fluorescent molecules such as vitamins (Chen et al., 2018), secretion of fluorescent 
attached (GFP) proteins (Balasubramanian et al., 2021), antibiotic producers (Mahler et al., 
2021), antimicrobial substances (Ning et al., 2021) or specific defined growth media can be 
designed for screening.  
This technique has been used in several different species such as Corynebacterium glutamicum 
(Balasubramanian et al., 2021), filamentous fungi (Aspergillus niger), Streptococcus 
pneumoniae, Salmonella typhimurium (Ning et al., 2021) and environmental samples as soils 
(Mahler et al., 2021) and gut microbiomes (Watterson et al., 2020). Nevertheless, only few 
studies have applied droplet-based microfluidics into LAB, such Chen et al., 2018 which after a 
spontaneous mutagenesis screening for riboflavin production in LAB, used this technique for 
differentiate high vitamin producers, and was able to isolate strains with 3-4 fold higher 
production after a sorting process (Chen et al., 2018) or Hernandez-Valdes et al., 2020 which 
used droplets for screening of amino acid secretion of mutated L. lactis obtaining three mutated 
strains with 5-10 fold amino acid secretion.

1.6. IDENTIFICATION METHODS OF LAB SPECIES 
The LAB species identification, at the beginning, was performed by simple morphological 
characterization, and over the time, phenotypic and lately, due to the obstacles provided by the 
latter, genotypic typing tools became popular to classify LAB. LAB are a promising group of 
bacteria with a lot of medical and food applications which, novel species and strains might be 
relevant to identify, and genotypically characterize to find relevant traits for specific purposes. 
Most of the identification methods for LAB are molecular typing tools based on PCR 
amplification and sequencing-based approaches. There are a lot of molecular techniques that 
have their strengths and weaknesses, therefore the best strategy is to follow multiphasic 
techniques to identify LAB correctly (A. Sharma et al., 2020).  
Some molecular techniques for identification of LAB are ribotyping, where ribosomal genes are 
recognized using nucleic acid probes and a Southern hybridization is performed using rDNA 
cistrons. Others such as amplified ribosomal DNA restriction analysis or random amplified 
polymorphic DNA are used for bacterial identification from microbial communities. Real-time 
PCR is a DNA-based technique broadly used to quantify LAB species from samples because its 
principle is based on the intensity measure of the fluorescence of the products generated of 
each amplification cycle of the PCR, where its intensity is proportional to the increase in the 
amount of the amplicon at that specific time. To perform this method, fluorescent 
oligonucleotides and non-sequence specific double DNA fluorescent are required (A. Sharma et 
al., 2020).  
Differently, mass spectrometry is an identification method that is not based on genomics. Mass 
spectrometry includes MALDI-TOF, LC-MS/MS and targeted LC-MS/MS which are based on 
protein/peptide analysis. In MALDI-TOF, the bacteria are radiated with a laser, which are 
transferred through the matrix, and different ions are generated that move towards the 
detector at different speeds, because their m/z values are different. Protein peaks are obtained 
and compared to the database. The most advantages of mass spectrometry in comparison to 
DNA based techniques are consistency, robustness, and overall, they are cheap. Also, it requires 
small amount of sample and low time of processing. Nevertheless, strain levels are not possible 
to differentiate, non-culturable microorganisms neither and there is a database dependency for 
identification (A. Sharma et al., 2020). 
For accurate metagenomic and full genome analysis, whole-genome sequencing (WGS) might 
be a useful tool for identification of pure strains. The analysis of the whole-genome DNA data 
between strains can be performed by single nucleotide polymorphism, to obtain differences at 
nucleotide level, or gene by gene-based approaches. Nevertheless, this technique is much 
expensive and bioinformatician skills are required to handle technical data (A. Sharma et al., 
2020).

1.7. CHARACTERIZATION OF LAB 
1.7.1.  PLANT-BASED FERMENTATIONS USING LAB 
1.7.1.1. CEREALS, BEANS AND VEGETABLES 
Plant-based fermentations include a broad range of vegetables and fruits, but also refer to 
beans, cereals and grains, which have been fermented since ancient times. Some plant-based 
materials require the metabolic activity of microorganisms to be edible for humans, but 
generally, LAB fermentations provide preservation, flavour, texture and nutritional traits. The 
current market is demanding a transition from animal-based products to plant-based products 
due to sustainability, lifestyle choices, food-related diseases (lactose intolerance, milk protein 
allergy) and green labels. Mainly, the research is focused on finding suitable starter cultures that 
could provide biopreservation, elimination of off-flavours and antinutritional factors, antifungal, 
anti-mycotoxigenic and mycotoxin-binding capabilities, proteolytic (to release flavour-
contributing amino acids) and most important, the ability to ferment sugars present on plant-
based materials (Waters et al., 2014). 
A huge variety of cereal-based beverages are currently lacto-fermented, mainly in Africa and 
South America, as a way of transforming them into tasteful and safe food products. Cereals 
include, sorghum, oat, barley, malt, wheat, millet, among others. One of the main problems of 
cereal-based products is their porridge-like texture that are too thick to feed child and young 
children. Therefore, in some countries the porridges are diluted with fermentation supernatant 
to transform thick porridges into liquid gruels with a creamy texture and yogurt-like products, 
such as ogi, which is produced in Nigeria, typically made from millet, sorghum or maize (Waters 
et al., 2014). Cereals are characterized for containing water-soluble fibers, such as β-glucan, 
arabinoxylan, galactose and fructose and starch which have been linked to the prebiotic 
concept, enhancing the proliferation of the LAB strains into fermented cereal beverages. 
==PAGE:18==
13 
 
Furthermore, the reduction of antinutrients by fermentation performances, has been achieved 
by the metabolic abilities of LAB. Phytates, protease inhibitors, raffinose and stachyose, tannins, 
and polyphenols are some of the antinutrients mostly present in cereals that have been able to 
be reduced by LAB action. Phytate degradation has been demonstrated to be performed by the 
pH reached by LAB fermentation (4.5-5) which increases the cereal phytase activity, or by 
unspecific phytases-like proteins similar to phosphatases which are able to utilize P-source 
provided by phytates. On the other hand, tannins and protein inhibitors are responsible for the 
indigestibility of cereal proteins, where LAB activity had shown to increase the availability of 
essential amino acids from proteinase inhibitors (Waters et al., 2014). 
Moreover, beans have been also widely lacto-fermented, specifically, soybean. They are 
characterized for containing high contents of sucrose (50%), raffinose (10%) and stachyose 
(40%), which raffinose and stachyose are both poorly digestible, and cause flatulence. 
Moreover, to develop soy-based fermented products, the challenge starts by eliminating off-
flavours (beany and green) and/or potentiate hedonic flavours to create consumer appreciated 
food products (Cai et al., 2021). Enzymatic LAB capabilities that allow the catabolism of complex 
bean-based carbohydrates, are genotypically and phenotypically important when screening for 
LAB strains. Levansucrases, which hydrolyse β-1,2 linkages, and ɑ-galactosidases or β-invertases 
which can metabolize raffinose and stachyose by different linkages, are some of the enzymes of 
interest (Harlé et al., 2020). Also, soy isoflavones are nutritional compounds that when they are 
in glycoside forms, they lose much of their bioavailability. Furthermore, aglycone forms are less 
present in unfermented soy products than glycoside forms. Therefore, LAB fermentations have 
demonstrated to increase the bioavailability of isoflavones by reducing their glycosylated 
functional groups (Cai et al., 2021). 
Thirdly, fermentation of vegetables has also limitations to consider. Technologically, pectin-rich 
matrices are not easily degradable by LAB strains due to their complexity. Nutritionally, tannins 
and phenolic compounds are antinutrients found in vegetable-based foods, which decrease the 
nutrient value of raw foods. Thus, pectinases, tannases and gallate descarboxylases activities 
are relevant phenotypical characteristics to consider when screening for LAB to be applied in 
those complex matrices (Sáez et al., 2018). Moreover, aroma and texture organoleptic 
properties of vegetables are crucial for fermentation. These matrices are rich in aroma 
compounds such as phenolic compounds, fatty acids, glycosides and amino acids that are good 
substrates for LAB strains. For instance, the production of alcohols, ketones and terpenes may 
be associated to fruity and floral aromas while aldehydes would manifest off-flavours. Also, 
acetoin and diacetyl compounds from the pyruvate metabolism are characterized for providing 
buttery odour and flavour, while lactic acid and acetic acid are associated to the sour flavour 
(Sáez et al., 2018). Apart from technological and aroma aspects, there are a wide range of traits 
to be focus when fermenting vegetable-based products, such as EPS which affect the rheology 
of fermented juices and therefore their consumer acceptance, vitamins which are metabolised 
or released by the action of LAB such as ascorbic acid which was increased by the metabolic 
performance of L. plantarum into cashew apple juice, that affect the nutritional properties of 
final fermented products (Szutowska, 2020). 
There are many technological, flavour/aroma related and nutritional properties to be focused 
on, when fermenting plant-based food products. Those phenotypical characteristics provided 
by LAB strains, can be experimentally tested for further plant-based applications. Some of the 
above-mentioned phenotypes, belonging to the three categorical properties, have been 
searched from the LAB strains isolated from insects and plants. Pectinolytic activities which

1.7.1.3. PECTIN DEGRADATION 
Pectin is a complex polysaccharide present in the middle lamella of the majority of plant cells 
walls. It mostly consists of three structurally polysaccharide motifs: the homogalacturonan, 
which is composed by multiple units of D-galacturonic acid bond by ɑ-(1,4) glycosidic linkages, 
the rhamnogalactoruonan I and II which contained branched molecules normally corresponding 
to sugars as arabinose, galactose, mannose and L-rhamnopyranose (Chen et al., 2015). All 
residues of pectin may be utilised as a source of energy if the corresponding degrading abilities 
are fulfilled. Pectinolytic enzymes are found in plants and microorganisms including LAB species 
and constituting four specific enzymes: pectin methyl esterase (EC 3.1.1.11), pectinase (EC 
3.1.1.15), pectin lyase (EC 3.1.1.15) and polygalacturonases (EC 3.2.1.15) which allow the 
complete degradation of pectin and its consequent release of galacturonic acids oligomers. 
Pectinases are industrially relevant for food manufacturing, in industries such juices and oil, 
which are used for extraction and clarification. Industrially, pectinases are mainly produced by 
fungal sources, specifically, Aspergillus niger is the major host producer (Patidar et al., 2018). 
To understand its complex structure, Figure 7 represents the parts and residues that form pectin, 
and the degradation pathway, including the enzymes involved in each reaction. Protopectinases 
are the responsible of degrading the insoluble protopectin, present in unripe fruits, producing a 
highly polymerized soluble pectin. From having a polymerized soluble pectin, the action of 
pectin methylesterases, which are hydrolases that remove the methoxyl esters and release 
pectic acids and methanol, and different galacturonate lyases that are able to break down the 
pectin polymer releasing simple, methyl or ethyl galacturonate oligomers. Finally, 
==PAGE:21==
16 
 
polygalacturonases are hydrolases that cleave the polymeric galacturonic acid chain in the 
presence of water releasing oligo galacturonic acids from pectic acids (Patidar et al., 2018). 
LAB species with pectinolytic activities have interestingly been studied for food applications such 
cocoa fermentation, table olives, fruit-based juices and wine productions, among others. 
Specific strains of Lactobacillus casei, Lactobacillus plantarum and Lactococcus lactis produced 
polygalacturonases and pectin methylesterases, and were able to clarify pectin, out of 80 strains 
tested (Karam & Belarbi, 1995a). Moreover, strains of P. pentosaceus and L. pentosus 
demonstrated to have the maximum pectinolytic activity among the strains tested in idly and 
uttapam batters (Vidhyasagar et al., 2013). In addition, a strain of Leuconostoc mesenteroides 
showed its pectinolytic ability when using tomato juice serum, in which viscosity was reduced 
after LAB fermentation (JUVEN et al., 1985). Therefore, there are evidence of the presence of 
pectinolytic related enzymes in LAB species.

In terms of hexoses, there are two basic fermentation pathways in which LAB are widely divided; 
the homofermentative (homolactic) pathway which is based on glycolysis (Embden-Meyerhof-
Parnas pathway) where its main product is lactic acid, and the heterofermentative (heterolactic) 
pathway (known as pentose phosphoketolase pathway) which apart from producing lactic acid, 
significant amounts of CO2, ethanol and acetic acid are produced (Kandler, 1983b). Overall, 
homolactic fermentation produced 2 moles of ATP per mole of glucose consumed, and 2 
molecules of lactic acid. In contrast, heterolactic fermentation produces only 1 mole of ATP, if 
the acetyl phosphate formed is reduced to ethanol. However, if acetyl phosphate is converted 
to acetic acid, in the presence of alternative electron acceptors such as O2 or fructose, an extra 
ATP molecule is formed. The fermentation pathway of the hexoses is related to the taxonomic 
criterion (Wright & Axelsson, 2019). The genera Leuconostoc, Oenococcus, Weisella and 
Fructobacillus, as well as lactobacilli Group III (L. brevis, L. reuteri, L. fermentum, etc) are 
classified as obligate heterofermentative. However, Group II of lactobacilli (L. casei, L. curvatus 
and L. sakei) is classified as facultative heterofermentative and finally, Group I of lactobacilli, and 
enterococci, lactococci, pediococci, streptococci, tetragenococci and vagococci are 
homofermentative. When homofermentative LAB grow on typical hexoses such as glucose, the 
standard homolactic fermentation pattern can be observed (Wright & Axelsson, 2019). Pyruvate 
is reduced to lactic acid by NAD+ - dependent lactate dehydrogenase, thus reoxidizing the NADH 
formed during the glycolytic pathway. Nevertheless, under certain conditions, glycolysis might 
also lead to a mixed acid fermentation, producing ethanol, diacetyl, acetoin, 2,3-butanediol and 
mannitol as end products, which are conducted by alternative pathways of NAD+ regeneration. 
However, when an heterofermentative LAB grow on typical hexoses such as glucose, the 
pyruvate is converted to equimolar amounts of lactic acid, ethanol and CO2. Depending on the 
energy requirements of the cells, the acetyl phosphate may produce acetic acid instead of 
ethanol, gaining a mole of ATP. Nevertheless, the regeneration of NAD+ must be supplied by an 
alternative electron acceptor such as oxygen, pyruvate or fructose reduction, producing 
mannitol, among other subproducts (Wisselink et al., 2002). Figure 8 represents the 
homofermentative and heterofermentative hexose degradation pathways followed by LAB.

Di and tri- saccharides continue the same metabolic pathways when they are hydrolysed into 
monosaccharides. Thus, the transport of hexoses, pentoses and di and tri-saccharides is 
important to understand, the way in which sugars are internally transported and modified. 
Generally, saccharides and oligosaccharides are taken up with the help of specific permeases 
and are phosphorylated inside the cell. Moreover, oligosaccharides are broken down by 
respective glycosidases prior to phosphorylation of the resulting monosaccharides. Apart from 
specific permeases, phosphoenolpyruvate-dependent phosphotransferase system or PEP:PTS 
are also used for specific sugar transportation as lactose and sucrose, specifically in lactococci. 
For example, in lactococci, a sucrose-specific PEP:PTS system  accompanied  by  sucrose-6-
phosphate  hydrolase  is  functional,  producing  glucose-6-phosphate and fructose, while in L. 
mesenteroides sucrose is cleaved by dextransucrase, and the glucose moiety is used for dextran 
synthesis, while fructose is normally fermented. Another example is maltose, which its 
permease system is functional in most of the strains. Pentoses are also transported by specific 
permeases (Wright & Axelsson, 2019). 
Raffinose is a trisaccharide formed by galactose, glucose and fructose (α-D-galactopyranosyl-
[1→6]-α-D-glucopyranosyl-[1→2]-β-D-fructofuranoside) and it is considered a ɑ-galactosyl 
extension of sucrose, which is mostly found in plants (DESAI et al., 2002). Mainly, raffinose can 
be hydrolysed by two different types of enzymes which can break down each oligosaccharide’s 
bonds. Overall, the majority of studies have been focused to ɑ-galactosidases (EC 3.2.1.22) 
which are carbohydrases that hydrolyse the ɑ-1,6 bond between galactose and glucose releasing 
sucrose and galactose as C-sources. However, invertases (EC 3.2.1.26) are carbohydrases less 
commonly studied, but they catalyze the breakage of the ɑ-1,2 bond releasing melibiose, which 
has nutritional health attributes, and fructose (Zhou et al., 2017)*(this paragraph has been

1.7.2.2. ACIDS AND SUBPRODUCTS 
In this section, the main focus will be on the main subproducts generated by the metabolism of 
LAB, that are lactic acid, acetic acid, ethanol and mannitol. In homofermentative species, the 
production of lactic acid is carried from the pyruvate metabolism which is reduced to lactate by 
regenerating the NAD+ used in the glycolysis, by the action of the lactate deshydrogenase. On 
the other hand, pyruvate can alternatively by hydrolysed into formate and acetyl-CoA which can 
be either reduced into acetaldehyde and then producing ethanol or phosphorylated, producing 
acetyl-P which is dephosphorylated (ATP production) to acetate. The generation of acetyl-CoA 
from pyruvate is performed by the action of pyruvate formate lyase, on one hand, the 
production of ethanol is mediated by acetaldehyde and alcohol deshydrogenases, and on the 
other hand, the acetate generated is caused by the action of phosphotransacetylase and acetate 
kinase. In contrast, in heterofermentative LAB, the production of lactate is mediated from 
xylulose-5-P to glyceraldehyde-3-P, to pyruvate and finally to lactate. These reactions are 
mediated by phosphoketolase and lactate deshydrogenase (apart from more enzymes). 
However the production of ethanol is carried from the acetyl-P generated from xylulose-5-P, by 
the action of phosphotransacetylase, acetaldehyde dehydrogenase and alcohol dehydrogenase, 
in anaerobic conditions, while the production of acetate is performed from acetyl-P as well, but 
proceeded by the action of acetate kinase instead, releasing a molecule of ATP, and mostly in 
aerobic conditions (Gänzle, 2015a). In Figure 11, the metabolism of products generation by LAB 
species, homofermentative and heterofermentative is illustrated.

Mannitol production, also, differs into both metabolic groups. Mannitol biosynthesis in 
homofermentative LAB is carried with fructose 6-phosphate, where mannitol 1-phosphate 
dehydrogenase catalyses the reduction of fructose 6-phosphate, and also the reverse reaction, 
the oxidation of mannitol 1-phosphate, depending on the cell metabolic balance. Mannitol 1- 
phosphate is dephosphorylated to mannitol, by mannitol phosphatase. When grown on glucose 
or other hexoses, no or very low mannitol 1-phosphate dehydrogenase activity, but when lactate 
dehydrogenase is not useful (mutated), the regeneration of NAD+ by mannitol production is 
performed. Differently, fructose 6-phosphate is not used by heterofermentative LAB. Fructose 
is reduced to mannitol by mannitol dehydrogenase, releasing the regenerative NAD+. It has been 
demonstrated that when glucose and fructose are present, 2 moles of mannitol are produced 
per mol of glucose and 2 moles of fructose. However, when fructose is the only C-source, 2 moles 
of mannitol (or sometimes 1.5 moles of ethanol and 1 mannitol) are also produced every 3 moles 
of fructose (Wisselink et al., 2002). As shown in Figure 8, the mannitol pathway production is

1.7.2.3. CITRATE METABOLISM AND DIACETYL PRODUCTION 
Citrate is present in many substrates which are used for food fermentations such as fruit and 
vegetables. Its degradation results in the formation of unusual fermentation subproducts such 
as diacetyl, acetoin, butanediol and acetaldehyde. Diacetyl is an aroma compound which is 
characterized to provide the buttery flavour and odour. Metabolically, citrate is transported to 
the cell by citrate permeases which are pH dependent with maximum activity at around 5-6 of 
pH (Hugenholtz, 1993).  
Once the citrate is inside the cell, is converted to oxaloacetate and acetate by enzyme citrate 
lyase. Oxaloacetate is decarboxylated to pyruvate. Nevertheless, in lactobacilli species, two 
different mechanisms of citrate utilization have been reported. The first, from L. pentosus 
reduces oxaloacetate to succinate using malate dehydrogenase, fumarase and fumarate 
reductase, while L. plantarum strains are able to use both citrate conversions, to succinate and 
to pyruvate. From pyruvate, ɑ-acetolactate is synthetized by ɑ-acetolactate synthase from two 
molecules of it, and consequently decarboxylated to acetoin by the action of ɑ-acetolactate 
decarboxylase. Acetoin can be excreted or reduced to butanediol by acetoin reductase. Through 
this metabolic pathway, diacetyl can only be formed by a chemical decarboxylation (caused by 
O2) of ɑ-acetolactate. Diacetyl is only formed under aerobic and at low pH. These facts normally 
occur in homofermentative LAB, which are more complex, in terms of citrate metabolism, than 
heterofermentative ones. In heterolactic, almost all pyruvate formed from citrate is transformed 
to D-lactate. Only under extreme conditions of low pH and absence of sugar, some citrate can 
be converted to acetoin and butanediol. The acetoin reductase or the butanediol 
dehydrogenase catalyse the irreversible reduction of diacetyl to acetoin and reversible 
reduction of acetoin to butanediol. However, when both acetoin and diacetyl are present, the 
reduction of acetoin is preferred as a substrate than the diacetyl, due to the high affinity of 
acetoin reductase for acetoin, which explains the stability of diacetyl in dairy products 
(Hugenholtz, 1993). Figure 12 shows the citrate metabolism from heterofermentative LAB 
(Gänzle, 2015b).

The microorganism isolation was performed as shown in Figure 14. The frozen stocks were

isolating the 10 colonies. This was decided to obtain the most realistic culturable biodiversity 
possible.

identified by MALDI-TOF or grown to be stored into <-80ºC, with a 2% (v/v) of glycerol, for

2.3. SPECIES IDENTIFICATION 
2.3.1. MALDI-TOF 
The species identification was mainly performed using MALDI-TOF biotyper (Bruker) which was 
able to identify species based on the protein spectra. Protein extraction method was used as the 
standard method of purification. Single-colonies were grown into 1mL liquid growth media 
(MRSG, MRSF or NBF) for between 16-24h at 30ºC. Once the pellet was visually identified, the 
samples were washed with miliQ water (or 0.85% NaCl) twice, to remove any remaining protein 
present in the growth media that could interfere with the final protein spectra. The supernatant 
was removed and 300µL of miliQ were added into the pellet and vortexed. Once having the 
pellet dispersed, 900µL of 96% (v/v) ethanol solution was added and vortexed again. A 
centrifugation process (3 min, 14000 rpm) and a removal of the supernatant were followed. 
50µL of a solution of 99% (v/v) of formic acid and consecutively 50µL of pure acetonitrile were 
added into the remaining pellet and vortexed. Finally, a centrifugation step (3min, 14000rpm) 
and spotting 1µL of the supernatant into the MALDI-TOF plate (triplicates) was performed. The 
final step was adding the matrix formed by mixing 475µL of miliQ water, 500µL of pure 
acetonitrile, 25µL of pure trifluoroacetic acid and α-cyano-4-hydroxycinnamic acid (green 
powder) and add 1µL into the already dried spots. The following Figure 17 shows the protein 
extraction protocol followed.

MALDI-TOF provides an identification score that normally ranges between 1 and 2.60. From 1 to 
1.69 the identification is considered undefined, and the genus/species cannot be considered as 
representative. Furthermore, between 1.70 and 1.99 the identification can only be considered 
at genus level, for example, Lactococcus sp. A score above 2 concludes a species positive 
identification ensuring genus and species level. Although this is the standardized rule, when the 
triplicates were coincident in the species match, the sample was considered at species level. For 
instance, if the triplicates showed Leuconostoc pseudomesenteroides as the best species match 
but the scores were 1.71, 1.80 and 1.90, the species level was considered as significant.

2.5. STATISTICAL ANALYSIS OF BIODIVERSITY 
Two different statistical analysis were performed: to compare the biodiversity parameters 
obtained among the different growth media used by using the traditional method or 
microfluidics, and to compare the biodiversity parameters obtained by using traditional method 
in contrast to the use of droplet-based microfluidics methods using the same samples. The data 
was not normally distributed because the values did not follow the pattern, and the variables 
were unpaired because they were independent to each other (growth media, methods). 
Therefore, two different tests were performed: Kruskal Wallis test for non-parametric and 
==PAGE:36==

unpaired data for more than two variables, and Mann-Whitney U test for non-parametric and 
unpaired data for two variables.  
Kruskal-Wallis test, by the addition of post-hoc test, were performed to calculate the p-values 
of the biodiversity parameters (Shannon, Simpson, Margalef and evenness index) obtained 
when using the three different growth media. The post-hoc test was used to compare each 
media in pairs (MRSG-MRSF, MRSG-NBF, MRSF-NBF) when using the three different media. 
Differently, Mann-Whitney U test was used to compare MRSG-NBF media when using 
microfluidics as isolation method. Moreover, this test was used to compare the biodiversity 
parameters obtained using traditional method (TM) and droplet-based microfluidics (MF). 
All tests were carried using RStudio (version 0.99.491) with the following commands, as 
examples, providing the p-values for each data compared: 
kruskal.test(Evenness ~ Media, shannon_index) – for Kruskal-Wallis test

2.6.4.  PECTIN DEGRADATION  
The pectinolytic activity of the selected strains was qualitatively analysed by pectin degradation 
of a created pectin rich agar media. Strains were grown in MRSG, and 5µL of an OD=0.3 washed 
dilution was spot-inoculated (5 replicates) into MRS agar plates, substituting glucose to 1% (w/v) 
citric pectin as main C-source. Plates were incubated at 30ºC for 48h. Then, an iodine solution 
was added for 15min to detect pectin depolymerization clearance zones, and distilled water was 
used to wash the iodine solution and visualize the clear zones (Ruiz Rodríguez et al., 2019). The 
results were classified into negative (-), when there were not clear zones, weak (+), when the 
clear zones were small or low-intense, moderate (++) when the clear zones were small but high-
intense and strong (+++), when the clear zones were big and high-intense. P. pentosaceus was 
used as positive control.

More plant samples were collected than insect samples, due to the difference in the process 
ease. The main idea was having, at least, triplicates of each species sample in order to observe 
microbiological differences within species. Nevertheless, the insect species identification is not 
immediate, due to phenotypic similarities among bees and bumble bees and therefore only one 
representative sample of each Bombus species and Andrena nigroaenea were collected.  
The differentiation within Bombus sp. has been widely studied because of its difficulties when 
classifying all the species analysed. Therefore, the subgenera classification has been updated 
numerous times throughout the last century (Williams et al., 2008). The taxonomic classification 
performed in this study was only based on visual external morphology and geographical 
location, identifying different phenotypes such as bumblebees’ colour and hair among the 
bumblebees found in Denmark. Nevertheless, the routine taxonomic classification is based in 
more parameters other than morphology. For instance, male-mate searching behaviour, pollen 
==PAGE:43==
38 
 
and nectar storage and nest material used for assembling, are some of the characteristics used 
to classify Bombus sp. into subgenera (Williams et al., 2008). 
The bumblebees collected belong to four different Bombus sp. subgenera. B. terrestris and B. 
lucorum belong to the sensu stricto. subgenera. Otherwise, B. pratorum belong to Pyrobombus 
while B. sylvarum and B. campestris belong to Thoracobombus and Psithyrus subgenera, 
respectively.  
Their classification was considered individually and based on iNaturalist app images similarities. 
For the identification of B. pratorum, an orange colour tip in the tail with black and yellow stripes 
in the abdomen and thorax were the visual characteristics that determined its classification 
(Plowright & Owen, 1980). Furthermore, B. sylvarum was yellowish with a black band across the 
thorax and two black bands in the abdomen.  B. campestris are variable but the sample was 
classified according to its fully black thorax and abdomen (Plowright & Owen, 1980). In contrast, 
B. terrestris and B. lucorum, that belong to the same Bombus sp.  subgenera, were differentiated 
based on yellow tone – B. lucorum has a lemon-yellow thorax and abdomen while B. terrestris 
has a yellow-orange colour. Both have a white tail (Lecocq et al., 2013). 
Moreover, Apis mellifera was easily identified based on its stocky body, abundant thoracal and 
sparse abdominal hair, which is brown, and its overall dark coloration with yellow stripes (Engel, 
1999). Also, Andrena nigroaenea was classified due to its dark brown abdomen and contrasting 
orange hairs on the hind legs and thorax (Meena et al., 2018). 
In the case of plants, the species were identified based on their fruits collected and the 
classification was easier due to the lower species diversity and more outstanding phenotypic 
differences within species. 
Out of the 49 samples collected, only 32 were further processed. Specifically, 23 samples (3 
Prunus spinosa, 4 Rubus fruticosus, 3 Sorbus aucuparia, 3 Rosa rugosa and all insects) were 
processed for biodiversity studies and 9 (4 Rosa glauca, 5 Rosa rugosa) were only used for 
isolation and identification experiments.

Focusing on LAB species, Lactobacillus paralimentarius, Leuconostoc citreum and Weisella 
cibaria were only isolated from Andrena nigroaenea. Furthermore, Lactococcus spp. and 
Leuconostoc pseudomesenteroides were found in both insects and plants. In contrast, Weisella 
minor, Weisella viridescens and Enterococcus spp. were only found in insects while Leuconostoc 
mesenteroides strains were only found in plants. Interestingly, Lactobacillus kunkeei species was 
uniquely isolated from Apis mellifera samples. 
According to the relation between isolation growth media and the LAB species isolated, 
Lactobacillus paralimentarius, Lactobacillus kunkeei, Weisella cibaria and Weisella viridescens 
were exclusively isolated when using MRSG as growth media while Lactococcus lactis ssp. lactis, 
Leuconostoc citreum and Enterococcus faecalis were exclusively isolated when using MRSF as 
growth media. On the other hand, Lactococcus lactis and Weisella minor were found in all three 
isolation growth media. 
There is evidence of the high presence of Leuconostoc mesenteroides strains in plants in 
comparison to other LAB species such as lactococci when performing isolation processes. Some 
studies used Leuconostoc spp. restrictive media in order to favour the growth of other LAB 
species when isolating (Mundt & Hammer, 1968). Moreover, Leuconostoc mesenteroides is one 
of the most important species present in plant microbiome, that is one of the key points when 
performing traditional (spontaneous fermentation) plant-based food fermentations such as 
sauerkraut (“Microbiol. Fermented Foods,” 1997). Thus, through the sample isolation process, 
all Leuconostoc mesenteroides species and subspecies isolated came from plant samples.

On the other hand, there have been biodiversity studies of microbes (including LAB) in Apis 
mellifera bee species. There have been described gram-negative species such as Erwinia and 
Klebsiella sp. as well as gram-positive species such as Enterococcus and Lactobacillus sp. in bee’s 
gut (Carina Audisio et al., 2011). Some studies reported that some of those bacteria could be 
endemic in the alimentary tract or neither dependent on seasonal nor food factors (Gilliam, 
1997). Nevertheless, Erwinia and Klebsiella sp. were found, among others, as gram-negative 
bacteria in Apis mellifera processed samples, and Lactococcus spp, Leuconostoc 
pseudomesenteroides, Weisella spp, Lactobacillus kunkeei and Enterococcus faecalis species 
were also found as culturable gram-positive LAB bacteria. Particularly, Lactobacillus kunkeei has 
been worldwide associated to honeybee due to its presence in flowers, beehives, and their food 
stores, but it has not been included as part of the honeybee core gut bacteria. However, this 
species is associated to the core beehive bacteria and therefore related to nurse workers and 
developing larvae (Corby-Harris et al., 2014). 
Furthermore, genera such as Lactococcus, Weisella and Leuconostoc sp. have not been reported 
to be found in bee’s microbiota (Corby-Harris et al., 2014) (Kešnerová et al., 2019). However, 
they are diversely found in high-fructose content sources flowers and plants suggesting that they 
might be present in bee’s external surfaces (parts in contact with plants), being part of the whole 
bee biodiversity instead (Behare et al., 2020). 
Apart from bees, a study showed that a variety of Lactobacillus, Lactococcus, Leuconostoc and 
Weisella spp. have been isolated from Bombus spp. (Praet et al., 2015b). Various species found 
in that study are in contrast with the ones isolated in this project. In common, Lactococcus lactis 
strains were isolated in both studies but differences in Leuconostoc and Weisella sp. have been 
compared. In the current study, L. pseudomesenteroides and W. minor were found instead of L. 
mesenteroides ssp. mesenteroides and W. hellenica and W. bombi isolated in the contrasted 
study (Praet et al., 2015b). Interestingly, the MALDI-TOF scores obtained when analysing W. 
minor strains were around the genus level, therefore, it may suggest that further whole DNA or 
16S DNA sequencing of rRNA clusters is needed to completely ensure their species level. 
Finally, there are not studies of the gut microbiota found in Andrena nigroaenea, but there have 
been some studies about the copresence of Leuconostoc citreum and Weisella cibaria species in 
fermented beverages with probiotic characteristics, such as human Gastrointestinal Tract (GIT) 
condition’s resistance, of both species (Silva et al., 2017). This might suggest a positive 
coexistence of both species in shared environments.

3.2.3.  DROPLET-BASED MICROFLUIDICS 
Droplet-based microfluidics has been used as an alternative method to isolate culturable 
microorganisms. The idea was to encapsulate environmental samples into single emulsion 
droplets in order to keep one single cell per each droplet and avoid the competition factor that 
appears when the cells are not compartimelized, which is the case of applying the traditional 
method of isolation. With using this technique, some of the cells that were not able to grow, or 
to grow that fast, compared to the others, have the chance to grow as if they were alone in the 
environment. 
Due to the more focus into insects’ samples that were grown in three different media, the 
droplet-based microfluidics has been only applied to those samples. 
Insect samples were taken from the frozen stock, encapsulated into droplets, and were grown 
into two different growth media, MRSG and NBF. Once the cells were grown at 30ºC, 24h inside 
the droplets, those were plated into the respective agar plates, and consequently studied for 
species identification. Specifically, IS1, IS2, IS7, IS8, IS9, IS10 were grown into MRSG and all (but 
IS9) were also grown into NBF.  
Species that were not observed using the traditional method of isolation appeared by using this 
method. Appendix 4 show all species that were identified on each sample by using droplet-based 
microfluidics. Nevertheless, Table 9 compiles the new species found by applying this method.

by the microfluidics method. 
On the other hand, more Enterobacter sp., Erwinia sp. and Pantoea sp. were identified when 
using NBF in comparison with the traditional method. All those genera are characterized to be 
facultative anaerobic as well as the LAB species (Donnenberg, 2014)(Morin & Parveen, 
1999)(González et al., 2007). Apart from the main difference between the traditional and the 
microfluidics method of being compartmentalized, the metabolic conditions might also change 
inside the droplets and therefore, different species might be faster growers in those. The oxygen 
diffusion through the oil and the single-droplet emulsions might be low effective, allowing to 
those which are faster growers in anaerobic conditions, grow faster (Mazutis et al., 2013). Thus, 
the difference in obtaining more/less biodiversity and new species, might be also influenced 
because of the change in the oxygen environment, a part of the elimination of the competition 
factor between species. Then, even if all species are facultative anaerobes, some might grow 
faster in those conditions than the others. Furthermore, the quorum sensing phenomena within 
species might also influence the growth of some species over others. Quorum sensing has been 
demonstrated to potentialize the growth of cells from same species, as a way to favour the 
environment surveillance. Nevertheless, when the microorganisms are grown in shared 
==PAGE:51==
46 
 
environments, the quorum sensing among same species might be mitigated by others, triggering 
a less effective molecule transfer. However, when they are growing in monocultures or 
compartmentalized in droplets, the quorum sensing is more effective and might increase the 
growth of specific species (Watterson et al., 2019).

A larger data would probably show less variability within biodiversity values and more visible 
differences. However, NBF media has shown a higher biodiversity than MRSG, by using 
microfluidics as the isolation method. This fact might be explained by a similar reason than the 
one mentioned above adding specific points. NBF is a broad growth media which does not 
potentiate the growth of specific bacteria such as MRS for LAB species. Nevertheless, by using 
microfluidics as the isolation method, the competition factor among species is eliminated by 
their compartmentalization in single droplets. Thus, all species had the same chance to grow in 
NBF without being influenced by the growth of any other strain. Specifically, NBF potentiates 
the growth of those species that do not possess high specific nutrient requirements and their 
metabolism is more adapted to scarce environments, while MRSG, which was designed for LAB 
growth, supplies specific nutrients that are utilised by specific species. In this case, in contrast 
with the traditional method, the species isolated using NBF, should be found in MRSG cultivation 
in droplets, because the competition factor is overcome. On the other hand, the species isolated 
using MRSG, should also be found in NBF, if the unspecific ingredients such as beef extracts, 
contain essential nutrients for LAB growth, for the mentioned reason. 
Nevertheless, none of the species isolated from both growth media (MRSG and NBF) were found 
in the corresponding other. This might be explained by some reasons: MRSG inhibits the growth 
of some bacteria because of the amount of citrate and acetate compounds which decrease the 
==PAGE:57==
52 
 
growth of those which are pH sensitive, the droplet incubation times were not sufficiently long 
to favour the growth of LAB in NBF, or the NBF did not supply all the nutrient requirements for 
LAB growth. The last explanation, that probably is applicable to all situations, is the fact that the 
droplets were plated into agar plates after incubating them into droplets. In other words, the 
cells were transferred from a non-competitive environment (droplets) to a low competitive 
environment (agar plates) for the identification of isolated colonies. Thereby, if some cells were 
growing slowly into MRSG or NBF media, when they were lately plated, the fast growers 
colonized the agar plates hiding those which were slower.

screen for antimicrobial activity which compared it to using agar plate standard method for 
isolation. In one of the biodiversity experiments, they incubated the environmental samples in 
droplets for 28 days and instead of sorting them, they created a method for inoculating 10-40 
droplets/s into agar plates that were spinning spirally, trying to separate them as much as 
possible, to be able to isolate as many culturable strains possible, afterwards. They achieved 
higher biodiversity when using microfluidics in comparison to traditional method of isolation, 
also identifying genera that was not identified in standard isolation method. 
Basically, droplet-based microfluidics technique achieved higher biodiversity because of the 
absence in competition of single-cell confinement (Mahler et al., 2021) and because of the 
increase of quorum sensing molecules accumulated into droplets (Watterson et al., 2020). Slow 
growers were not completely favoured in this experiment, because of the time of incubation. 
Nevertheless, the strains that were growing slow because of the competition provided by the 
other strains, or the strains that were potentialize by the quorum sensing molecules secreted 
between same species/strains, were favoured, leading to isolate and obtained new species as 
the mentioned above. 
Firmicutes (as LAB) showed a strong fitness in another media (YCFA) when growing in standard 
plates, demonstrating an enormous difference when the same samples were grown in droplets 
with the same media (Watterson et al., 2020). Furthermore, apart from having been obtained 
higher biodiversity in droplets, new species have been isolated and identified too, using the 
same growth media. Therefore, growth conditions such as oxygen transfer, quorum sensing and 
other biochemical changes might have changed the growth performance of the bacteria, leading 
to enhance the growth of some, instead of others. 
In this study, the droplets were not either sorted or inoculated by 10-40 droplets/s as mentioned 
above, they were plated and spread over the agar plates before the isolation, breaking down 
the droplets while spreading them over the surface. The competition factor between species in 
agar plates is not the same that the one suffered in liquid cultures, but after growing them 
separately in droplets, and culture them in plates afterwards, the competition factor is not 
completely eliminated. This experimental limitation might suggest that we would have obtained 
a higher difference in biodiversity, if a sorting process or a spiral inoculation would have been 
performed after the droplet incubation, because the main microfluidics characteristics, such as 
competition factor and quorum sensing would have been completely reduced or potentialized, 
respectively. 
In conclusion, changing the experimental set up by applying larger incubation times, sorting or 
spirally inoculating, after the droplet incubation might favour the benefits of using droplet-
based microfluidics in comparison to the traditional culturing methods.

3.4.2.  COLONY’S VISUAL CHARACTERIZATION 
Grown colonies were characterized by shape, size, margin, elevation, and pigmentation. 
Appendix 7.2 includes visual colony features when grown on plates. The visual characterization 
of the colonies is the beginning step when isolating and identifying unknown species. It might 
be a useful tool if the species have specific visual characteristics when grown on plates. For 
instance, M. pulcherrima has a distinctive pigmentation defined by a middle red dot surrounded 
by white colony colour due to the presence of iron(III), which forms an insoluble red pigment 
named pulcherrimin (Melvydas et al., 2016). In those cases, it is easier to identify species without 
the need for an advanced identification assay, such as MALDI-TOF or 16S-RNA sequencing. 
Nevertheless, the visual differences within LAB species are not easily distinguishable. All the 
selected species were circular, entire and raised while most were small, some seemed to be 
punctiform, specifically, some L. lactis. Furthermore, W. minor and L. kunkeei were more 
transparent than the rest, which are completely opaque white. Therefore, the visual differences 
are not sufficiently diverse or specific to enable the determination of the species level visually. 
3.4.3.  CARBOHYDRATE METABOLISM 
The eighteen strains isolated that were chosen for characterization, were exposed to seven 
different sugars, mostly found in plant sources, in order to observe their abilities and study their 
sugar metabolism, for their further application into plant-based food matrices. The sugars were 
D-glucose, D-fructose, D-galactose, maltose, L-arabinose, sucrose and raffinose. Overall, 
glucose, fructose, maltose and sucrose are the major sugars in plants (Halford et al., 2011), while 
galactose and arabinose are mainly attached to plant hemicelluloses (Holtzapple, 2003), and 
raffinose is an antinutritional factor derived from sucrose, predominantly found in mature leaves 
and seeds (Kuo et al., 1988). 
The microbial growth over 24 hours was analysed based on OD measurements and the specific 
µ (µesp) was calculated for each strain exposed to each sugar, based on the performance 
through the exponential phase.  
The negative control consisted of growing the strains into the same growth media but without 
the presence of any specific sugar, to be able to compare metabolism capabilities. Some of the 
strains grew in the media without sugar due to the presence of undefined components such as 
beef extract, therefore, this fact was considered when calculating their µesp on each sugar. 
Figure 34 represents the µesp of each strain corresponding to the presence of each sugar. 
Appendix 7.3 shows the growth curves of each strain grown on each media and the values of 
the µesp (h-1).

arabinose (0.26 h-1) in comparison to the other strains belonging to the same species. Apart from 
this strain, only three more strains of L. lactis were able to grow in the presence of arabinose 
(strain 1, 11 and 15). None of them were considered good performants with raffinose (max. 0.08 
h-1) while some grew slightly in galactose, having one (strain 4) as the most well-suited to this 
sugar (0.14 h-1). 
Moreover, most of L. pseudomesenteroides strains performed a proper degradation of glucose, 
fructose, sucrose and maltose, except for one (strain 3), when it had to catabolize sucrose. In 
contrast to L. lactis, there were two strains of L. pseudomesenteroides (strains 7 and 17) that 
achieved a significant degradation of arabinose, while all of them were able to metabolize 
raffinose. Only one strain (strain 17) was able to metabolize galactose above 0.1 h-1. The other 
species L. mesenteroides spp. mesenteroides and L. citreum, belonging to the same genus, 
performed similarly to L. pseudomesenteroides but both were able to degrade arabinose at 
higher rates, but none of them raffinose.  
Also, only one of W. minor strains (strain 9) was growing properly in the presence of glucose, 
fructose, galactose, arabinose and maltose, but was not able to degrade raffinose. The second 
W. minor strain was barely performing good in the present of any of the sugars. Apart from W. 
minor species, W. cibaria was able to grow in the same sugars than strain 9, but it was not able 
to metabolize galactose. Nevertheless, it was growing significantly better in sucrose, arabinose 
and maltose when contrasting with it. 
Lastly, the two Lactobacillus strains, L. paralimentarius and L. kunkeei were extremely different 
in terms of sugar metabolism. L. kunkeei was mainly growing good in fructose, but it was a slow 
grower in glucose, sucrose and raffinose. Unlikely, L. paralimentarius had the ability to 
metabolize glucose, fructose, galactose and sucrose significantly although barely maltose, 
arabinose and raffinose. 
Specifically, L. pseudomesenteroides (strain 17) was the best degrading glucose, L. lactis ssp. 
lactis (strain 11) fructose while W. minor (strain 9) galactose. Furthermore, L. lactis ssp. lactis 
(strain 14) was best performer in the presence of maltose and L. pseudomesenteroides (strain 7) 
was best in arabinose and raffinose. Finally, W. cibaria was the fastest grower when sucrose was 
added as main C-source. Nevertheless, L. pseudomesenteroides (strain 17) was the only one that 
was growing in all sugars properly. 
In general, pentoses such as L-arabinose are taken up by specific permeases and converted into 
a common intermediate named D-xylulose-5P that is converted to lactate and acetate after 
serial reactions due to the phosphoketolase action (Kandler, 1983a). Nevertheless, obligate 
homofermentative LAB, such as Lactococcus sp. and some Lactobacillus sp., usually lack of 
phosphoketolase enzyme and any production of acids is performed (Kandler, 1983a) (Gundogan, 
2000). Nevertheless, 4 strains of L. lactis (strain 1, 11, 14 and 15) demonstrated to be able to 
degrade pentoses. Most of Leuconostoc spp. such as L. citreum, L. pseudomesenteroides and L. 
mesenteroides were able to degrade arabinose as well as few Weisella spp. as W. cibaria 
according to literature. Nevertheless, most of the W. minor strains are not (Gundogan, 2000). 
Also, D-galactose is mostly degraded by all Lactobacillus spp. and Lactococcus spp. by the 
tagatose or Leloir pathways, but some Leuconostoc spp. and Weisella spp. have been lacking this

ability (Gundogan, 2000). For instance, over the 90% of the strains of L. citreum, W. cibaria and 
W. minor are not able to metabolize D-galactose while the 90% of L. pseudomesenteroides 
demonstrated the opposite (Gundogan, 2000).  
In this study, one out of two strains of W. minor was able to degrade D-galactose and L-arabinose 
suggesting that perhaps the strains were not belonging to this species. Moreover, the MALDI 
scores of this species identification were not over two, which might be explained by the same 
reason. Based on the carbohydrate profile and belonging to Weisella sp., W. hellenica (or W. 
thailandiensis) might be the species isolated instead (Gundogan, 2000), as previously found in 
bumble bee (Praet et al., 2015b). Nevertheless, after the 16S-RNA sequencing results, those 
strains were concluded to belong to W. minor species demonstrating that niches may vary 
phenotypes among specific species. 
Furthermore, raffinose is not generally metabolized by Lactococcus spp. and by most of Weisella 
spp. because of the absence of ɑ-galactosidase and/or β-fructosidase (Fritsch et al., 2015). 
Contrarily, L. pseudomesenteroides is the only species found in this study that literature suggests 
its ability to degrade raffinose, and this fact is supported by the results obtained. L. 
mesenteroides spp. mesenteroides or L. citreum are barely able to perform its catabolism 
(Gundogan, 2000).  
Apart of those, L. kunkeei has been defined as a strict fructophilic LAB which might explain why 
was only able to degrade fructose in an optimal way. MRS media does not contain a high 
concentration of fructose (without considering the C-source added) which might need in order 
to perform a cometabolism with any other carbohydrate to be metabolised (Endo et al., 2012). 
Finally, L. paralimentarius is an homofermentative species of Lactobacillus sp. which might 
suggest the lack of the ability to catabolise pentoses such as arabinose. Nevertheless, it 
degraded glucose, fructose and maltose as mentioned in Paramithiotis et al., 2007 but also 
sucrose, in contrast with the previously mentioned article that characterized  L. paralimentarius, 
isolated from in sourdough.

NADH+H+ to NAD+ (Wisselink et al., 2002). In general, the production of secondary metabolites 
such as mannitol is hampered by the lactate dehydrogenase activity. Nevertheless, the major 
end-product of homofermentative LAB is lactic acid. In rare cases, other metabolites are

In terms of ethanol production, all heterofermentative species produced more or less

strains of homofermentative LAB, Lactococcus lactis ssp. lactis (strain 10, 11 and 14) produced 
high amount of ethanol (817, 1860 and 3697 mg/L) at the end of the fermentation. 
Homofermentative LAB metabolic flux is mostly focused on the production of lactic acid as major 
compound, when sugar substrates are in present in excess. Moreover, both strains produced 
high concentrations of lactic acid (8648, 8484 and 14034 mg/L) but consumed glucose and 
fructose (the two major present sugars) into almost their totality. Pyruvate metabolism in 
homolactic species is normally directed into lactate dehydrogenase activity reducing pyruvate 
into lactic acid. Anyway, pyruvate has other redox pathways that could generate NAD+ or ATP 
for the cell. Pyruvate can be transformed to acetyl-CoA by pyruvate-formate lyase or by pyruvate 
dehydrogenase, while can be also converted into ɑ-acetolactate by ɑ-acetolactate synthase 
leading to the end-product formation of ethanol, acetic acid or 2,3-butanediol (Cocaign-
Bousquet et al., 1996), respectively. It has been studied that when the sugars are not in excess 
(limiting) in homofermentative species, there might be occurring a switch on pathway, 
redirecting the generation of lactic acid into other metabolic subproducts such as ethanol 
(Cocaign-Bousquet et al., 1996). In this case, a high ethanol and lactic acid production by homo-
LAB might be explained by successive metabolic changes over the fermentation time. First, 
glucose and fructose were preferably consumed leading to a production of lactic acid as major 
or unique subproduct. When the concentration of glucose/fructose was below certain limit (not 
in excess), the pyruvate degradation pathway suffered a switch into pyruvate-formate lyase 
conducted pathway leading to the production of acetyl-CoA and finally ethanol. 
Generally, a low production of acetic acid has been achieved by all the strains. In 
homofermentative strains, it was expected due to its major pyruvate reduction into lactic acid. 
Nevertheless, in the case of heterofermentative strains, the acetyl-P formed from xylulose 5-P 
can be oxidized to the formation of acetic acid or reduced into ethanol by the oxidation of NADH-
H+ to NAD+ by the bifunctional acetaldehyde and alcohol dehydrogenase. Normally, when 
==PAGE:71==
66 
 
glucose is the major sugar the pathway is lead to the production of 1 ethanol by the use of 4H+ 
generated through the decarboxylation of fructose-6P (Zaunmüller et al., 2006). Also, the 
production of acetic acid is favoured by the presence of other kind of sugars such as fructose 
that act as final electron acceptor at similar or higher concentrations than glucose, and the 
acetyl-P is no longer required as electron acceptor. Acetic acid is also favoured by the presence 
of oxygen (Zaunmüller et al., 2006). In this case, the major substrate was glucose and 
consequently, the final electron acceptors were acetyl-CoA and pyruvate and therefore lactic 
acid and ethanol were mainly produced from each respectively.

Based on the HPLC results showed in Figure 35, the best consumers of citrate were strains 7, 10, 
11, 13 and 17 under concentrations of 450 mg/L at the end of the fermentation. In common, the 
qualitative plate assay showed strains 7, 11, 13 and 17 as the most effective citrate degrading 
strains based on blue colour formation. Nevertheless, strain 10 did not form blue colour after 
24h of fermentation while strain 8 did at high level, but in the latter, this citrate decrease was 
not reached when measuring in the HPLC. Either strains 5 or 9 changed colour during the plate 
assay while degraded citrate to its more than half initial concentration when analysed in the 
HPLC.  
In terms of diacetyl production, only three strains, L. paralimentarius and two L. lactis spp. lactis 
were able to produce diacetyl after 48h of growth in MRS glucose under 140 rpm of agitation. 
The diacetyl production is triggered by a non-enzymatic reaction from ɑ-acetolactate when 
oxygen is present. There is another possible explanation based on the action of diacetyl 
synthetase but there are not direct evidences (Wang et al., 2019). 
The relation between citrate metabolism and diacetyl production is close due to its catabolic 
pathway. Citrate is converted to oxalacetate, pyruvate and then two molecules of the latter are 
condensed into ɑ-acetolactate and finally oxidized into diacetyl by a decarboxylation reaction 
(Wang et al., 2019). Nevertheless, the production of diacetyl is also related to glycolytic 
pathways when citrate is present at lower concentrations. One of the major problems of diacetyl 
production is that this molecule is quite unstable and easily converted into acetoin, by a 
reduction reaction carried out by acetoin reductase, oxidizing NADH+H+ into NAD+. In the 
presence of oxygen, the accumulation of diacetyl in the media is higher because oxygen can 
oxidize NADH (by NADH oxidase) and decrease the reductive ratio of diacetyl into acetoin (Wang 
et al., 2019). 
As showed in Figure 35, L. paralimentarius and both strains of L. lactis (strains 14 and 15) that 
were producing diacetyl, relatively consumed citrate until concentrations of 871, 527 and 1712 
mg/L after 24h of fermentation. Also, those strains consumed glucose and fructose in high rates. 
Therefore, the production of diacetyl might be explained by both citrate and hexose metabolic 
pathways of producing ɑ-acetolactate. Nevertheless, two main differences of both experiments 
(citrate and diacetyl) are specified; the fermentation time of the diacetyl experiment was the 
double which could explain a higher accumulation of diacetyl due to a higher consumption of 
citrate and glucose, that could lead to the production of other secondary metabolites such as 
diacetyl from pyruvate metabolism, and the agitation in the second experiment, that would lead 
to a change in the redox balance and therefore a change in the metabolism behaviour. 
3.4.6.  PECTIN AND PHYTIC ACID DEGRADATION 
Pectin and phytic acid degradation were qualitatively analysed based on two different plate 
assays that were based on measuring clearance degradation zones after performing a spot 
inoculation of each strain. 
==PAGE:73==

Overall, as observed in Table 12, Lactococcus sp. strains were the best performers on degrading 
pectin and phytic acid. Specifically, strains 4, 14 and 15 had the highest pectinolytic activity while 
strain 1, 4, 8, 10 performed a better degradation of phytic acid after 48h of fermentation at 
30ºC. Interestingly, L. paralimentarius and L. kunkeei were also able to degrade pectin and phytic 
acid, respectively, in a proper way.  
The pectin degradation includes three different enzymatic activities: two depolymerisation 
enzymes called polygalacturonase that cleaves the ɑ-1,4 glycosidic bonds between two non-
methylated galacturonic acid residues, and pectin lyase that catalyses the β-elimination reaction 
between two methylated residues. The third catalytic activity is a demethoxylating action, which 
consist of a demethylation of pectin by the action of pectin-esterase (Karam & Belarbi, 1995b). 
Furthermore, Lactococcus spp. isolated from plants demonstrated to be able to degrade pectin 
by polyglalacturonase and pectin-esterase activities (Karam & Belarbi, 1995b). Even that the 
characterized strains came from insect microbiota, their diets mostly include pectin-rich 
substrates which might explain the strain niche adaptation ability to degrade this C-source 
(Siezen et al., 2008). 
In terms of phytic acid degradation, phytases are phosphohydrolases that initiate the stepwise 
removal of phosphate from phytate/phytic acid (IP6) which is dephosphorylated to lower myo-
inositol phosphates. Nevertheless, alkaline phosphatases present in Lactococcus spp., isolated 
from plants, were studied for phytate degradation. When some Lactococcus sp. strains were 
genotypically characterized by PCR in phytase gene coding, none of them showed a positive 
result. Otherwise, all showed an amplification of alkaline phosphatases and phosphorus 
solubilization (de Lacerda et al., 2016). Due to this fact, the phytate degradation of the 
qualitative assay might be related to the action of alkaline phosphatases present in the four 
Lactococcus sp. strains. Nevertheless, some of the strains did not have conclusive results due to 
the not complete visibility of the clear zones formed in plates. The assay was not optimized due 
to the absence of some reactants that have not been delivered at the needed time.

Soy (Glycine max) and oat (Avena sativa) were the plant-based food matrices used for 
fermentation. Nonetheless, they are largely different in composition, basically because the 
former is a legume, and the latter is a cereal. Soy is principally composed by protein (40%), 
mostly divided in two fractions, β-conglycinin and glycinin parts, in which methionine is the least 
abundant amino acid. Soy is also composed by lipids (20%), predominantly polyunsaturated fatty 
acids, and carbohydrates (25-30%), apart from vitamins, minerals and bioactive compounds, 
including isoflavones and phenolic molecules. In contrast, oats are cereals and therefore, 
carbohydrates constitute a large part of its composition, because it contains 60% of starch. 
Moreover, protein content ranges from 11-15%, mostly constituted by avenalins, albumins, 
prolamins and glutelins. Those contain low concentrations of sulphur-amino acids such cysteine 
and methionine but are rich in asparagine and glutamine. Furthermore, the lipid content in oats 
is 7%, and oats also contain phenolic compounds as flavonoids and avenanthramides. Apart 
from those components, oats are characterized for containing β-glucans, which are attributed 
to their bioactive benefits (Moretto et al., 2021).  
Based on the composition of each food matrix and the results obtained from the acidification 
curves, the cereal matrix was potentializing the microbial growth, and therefore, the pH 
==PAGE:76==
71 
 
decrease rate, was in a higher way than the legume matrix. As mentioned above, oats have 
higher carbohydrate content than soy, which is the principal energy source for LAB growth. 
Proteins, lipids and other compounds also contribute to the growth performance but in different 
ways, such as cell membrane formation, cofactors for enzymatic activity, protein synthesis, etc. 
Despite the high carbohydrate content in oats, LAB, as concluded above, are not able to 
metabolize all kind of mono- or polysaccharides, and they are different strain to strain.  
Soy carbohydrates are generally composed by sucrose (50%), raffinose (10%) and stachyose 
(40%) (which is a larger form of raffinose) (Cai et al., 2021). As experimentally tested in the 
carbohydrate metabolism, sucrose was overall metabolised by the all the strains, while raffinose 
(and consequently stachyose) was not. For instance, L. pseudomesenteroides (strain 7), L. lactis 
spp. lactis (strain 15), W. minor (strain 13) and L. paralimentarius (strain 5) were classified among 
the best sucrose-degrading performers, and some of them also able to degrade raffinose, and 
at the same time showed the lowest pH values achieved after soy fermentation.  
Differently, oats carbohydrates are principally constituted by starch, which is formed by amylose 
and amylopectin polymers, which their degradation is conducted by ɑ- and β- amylases, ɑ-
glucosidases, among others.  When the starch is degraded, the release of glucose occurred, 
which can be used for energy source by LAB. Apart from starch, small portion of the 
carbohydrate content in oats belongs to β-glucan, which is also a polysaccharide composed by 
D-glucopyranosyl and glucose residues that can be metabolized by β-glucanase and amylase 
enzymatic activities (Angelov et al., 2018). Therefore, the pH decrease performance of the 
selected strains, might be related to their growth capabilities, and therefore, to the amylase, 
glucosidase and glucanase metabolic activities. For example, L. pseudomesenteroides (strain 17) 
demonstrated the highest pH decrease rate and lowest final pH at the end of the fermentation 
which might suggest its high metabolic activity for starch degradation. 
4. CONCLUSION 
The isolation, identification, screening and characterization of LAB species were performed 
throughout the project. Thirty-two environmental samples were collected and processed, 
dividing them into plants (22) and insects (10). Species classified as Rubus fruticosus (4), Rosa 
glauca (4), Prunus spinosa (3), Rosa rugosa (8), Sorbus aucuparia (3) as plants, and Bombus 
sylvarum (1), Apis mellifera (4), Bombus campestris (1), Bombus pratorum (1), Andrena 
nigroaenea (1), Bombus lucorum (1) and Bombus terrestris (1), as insect species. 
The isolation of a total of 71 different strains belonging to 28 different species of yeast and 
bacteria using the traditional culturing method and 29 different strains belonging to 16 different 
species of bacteria using the droplet-based microfluidics method of isolation, was performed, 
and the species were further identified using MALDI-TOF as the principal identification tool. 
From those species identified, 14 species (L. lactis, L. mesenteroides, L. citreum, L. 
pseudomesenteroides, W. minor, W. cibaria, L. kunkeei, L. paralimentarius, E. faecalis, E. 
faecium, E. moraviensis, W. viridescens, L. lactis ssp. lactis, L. mesenteroides ssp. mesenteroides) 
and 41 strains belonged to the LAB group.  
Two isolation variables were tested and compared: the growth media and the isolation method 
used. Three different growth media were utilised: MRSG and MRSF, with the purpose of 
contrasting if changing the C-source might influence the biodiversity when isolating, and NBF, 
which is an unspecific growth media. Moreover, two isolation methods were contrasted, the 
traditional plate culturing method, which a general, low-tech and simple, mostly applied 
==PAGE:77==
72 
 
isolation method, and droplet-based microfluidics, which is an alternative method that 
eliminates the competition factor between species, potentiating the growth of sensitive slow 
growers and quorum-sensing performer species, by using droplets as single-cell compartments. 
Based on experimental and statistical data analysis, NBF growth media showed the highest 
culturable biodiversity using both methods, but a low number of LAB species grew on it. 
Moreover, droplet-based microfluidics isolation method demonstrated to achieve higher 
biodiversity than the traditional culturing method. Nevertheless, any of those results were 
statistically significant based on p-value, although clear differences were graphically illustrated. 
This fact might be explained by the use of a small dataset. 
Also, a simulated (using four known strains) screening for raffinose degradation comparing both 
screening (TM and MF) methods was performed and contrasted with the raffinose abilities that 
the four strains showed when they were grown individually in raffinose. Significant differences 
were obtained by using both methods, in which droplet-based microfluidics determined 
unexpected results of species prevalence, when comparing to their phenotypical performance. 
Competing factors and the mono- or disaccharide carbohydrate released might be the 
explanations to that fact. 
Eighteen LAB strains were selected for further genotypical and phenotypical characterization, 
based on the purpose to be used in plant-based fermentations. Eighteen phenotypical 
characteristics were chosen to be tested considering plant matrices and general metabolic LAB 
features. The glucose, fructose, galactose, maltose, arabinose, sucrose, raffinose and citrate 
metabolism, the pectin and phytic acid degradation, the diacetyl, lactic acid, ethanol, mannitol, 
acetoin and acetic acid production and soy and oat drink performance, were experimentally 
tested using monocultures of those strains. The eighteen phenotypes and the eighteen strains 
were evaluated in the following heatmap graph.

As shown in the heatmap, the selected strains performed differently in relation of each 
phenotype. For example, L. paralimentarius was good degrading fructose and galactose, 
performed a fast acidification rate in soy, and produced high concentrations of lactic acid, acetic 
acid and diacetyl. Depending on the phenotypes to consider, some of the strains were well-
adapted to plant-based matrices or they were not. Overall, strains of L. lactis (4, 14 and 15) and 
L. pseudomesenteroides (7, 17 and 19) and L. paralimentarius, demonstrated to be best suited 
to plant-based fermentations considering flavour-related and technological traits.  
As mentioned at the beginning of the project, the two main objectives were the optimization of 
the isolation process (growth media and method) and the creation of a plant-based 
characterization platform of LAB. In the former, NBF was not useful to isolate LAB, while MRS 
did not achieve high LAB biodiversity. Thus, the results suggested further optimization of the 
formulation of the isolation media, considering an in-between growth media, keeping LAB 
essential nutrients, such as Mg2- and Mn2+ but formulating it in a less specific way for LAB growth, 
for example, decreasing some of the specific C-sources, or pH-related ingredients such as the 
acetate and citrate present in MRS. There was a clear evidence that droplet-based microfluidics 
was a potential tool to obtain higher biodiversity of bacteria in comparison to the traditional 
method. Nevertheless, further MF optimization might improve those achievements, such as a 
sorting procedure after droplet-incubation, longer incubation times, or a more sophisticated and 
optimized manner of plating droplets, after their incubation. 
In the latter, a plant-based phenotypical characterization platform has been successfully 
created, which is able to define the best LAB strains depending on their purpose. Some assays 
such as the phytic acid degradation needed to be optimized as well, for further utilization. Also, 
a mixture of different strains and their metabolism in co-cultures might be further studied, in 
order to take advantage of individual phenotypes that can be potentially applied in combination.

6.2. Appendix 2: New method of MALDI-TOF processing. 
Independently from the current research study, a new MALDI-TOF processing method has been 
created and evaluated for optimizing the analysis time when working with a large number of 
cultured colonies. The new processing method has been contrasted to one of the existing 
methods, the protein extraction method. There is another process called “direct plating process” 
that never provided reliable results from the beginning of this study, therefore it was not

is formed by 10 processing steps. The evaluation of the time needed to perform both processes, 
in contraposition to the number of samples that must be processed, has been performed, in 
duplicates, when dealing with 5, 10 and 15 samples at once. All the step times were measured, 
and the total process time was calculated based on the sum of the step times: 
𝑃𝑟𝑜𝑐𝑒𝑠𝑠 𝑡𝑖𝑚𝑒= 𝑡1 + 𝑡2 + 𝑡3 + 𝑡4 … 
Moreover, the following equations describe the time required according to the number of 
samples that need to be processed using MALDI-TOF for both processes: 
New method: 
𝑦= 1.853 ∗𝑥+ 48.797 
Protein extraction: 
𝑦= 0.0416 ∗𝑥2 + 2.517 ∗𝑥+ 48.113 
Where y is time (min) and x is the number of samples to work with. 
Figure 41 represents the time difference required for both processes over increasing the number 
of samples. As it has been observed, when processing low number of samples at once, both 
processes need similar timing, but the difference is increased when running a higher number of 
samples. In other words, one sample is the cross-section of both equations, meaning that above 
one sample, the use of the new method will save processing time.
