Abstract
In Holbæk, Denmark, the implementation of a district heating system is underway to re-
place the existing natural gas-based heating infrastructure. However, the optimal energy
source mix for this system remains uncertain, particularly regarding the integration of re-
newable energy sources. This thesis investigates how geothermal energy, seawater heat
pumps, and waste heat recovery can influence the optimal energy mix for Holbæk’s DH
system. The objective is to determine the most cost-effective configuration of heat sources
through a techno-economic analysis using a custom-built optimisation model developed
in Julia, a programming language for technical computing. The model is calibrated with
data provided by project stakeholders, including Rambøll, and Holbæk Kommune, as well
as other available data. Additionally, the thesis assesses the policy framework needed
to support the integration of these technologies. The results indicate that seawater heat
pumps are the most cost-effective option under current conditions, while geothermal en-
ergy requires a substantial subsidy to compete, though significantly less than waste heat
recovery, which demands the highest financial support. The study highlights the role of
policy interventions in facilitating the adoption of these technologies through subsidies. A
balanced approach, focusing on financial support for the most feasible solutions, address-
ing local implementation challenges, and enforcing mandatory connections, supports a
resilient, sustainable, and equitable DH system in Holbæk.

Introduction
The global energy sector is under pressure to transition from fossil fuels to sustainable
energy sources. Traditional methods in energy production, heavily reliant on fossil fu-
els, contribute significantly to greenhouse gas emissions, driving climate change. This
transition is particularly challenging as climate change alters energy demand patterns [1],
[2].
Heating is one of the most significant end-use sectors, representing close to half of world-
wide final energy consumption and around 40% of CO2 emissions from energy in 2023
[3]. While efficient and low-carbon heating technologies exist, fossil fuels still supply over
60% of heating energy [4]. The recent global energy crisis has emphasised the need
for a quicker transition away from fossil-based heating, where energy security and af-
fordability are essential. To reach the goal of net zero emissions by 2050, renewable
heating solutions must be widely adopted. This also requires improvements in energy
efficiency, incorporating secondary heat sources, and developing robust infrastructure in
high-demand areas [4].
District heating (DH) offers a sustainable and efficient solution to these challenges by
centralising heat production and distributing it to buildings through insulated pipes. This
system can reduce carbon emissions by utilising renewable energy sources (RES), mak-
ing it particularly effective in urban areas [1], [2]. The flexibility of DH systems allows them
to integrate various energy sources, enhancing energy efficiency and supporting global
efforts to combat climate change [1].
In Denmark, DH systems play a critical role in the country’s energy transition, supply-
ing approximately two-thirds of Danish households with heating and hot water through a
highly efficient and sustainable infrastructure [5]. These systems have been shaped by
national policies like the Heat Supply Act, which mandates socio-economic assessments
for projects and prioritises collective heat infrastructure [5]. In alignment with the Euro-
pean Union’s Renewable Energy Directive (RED), Denmark has committed to ambitious
renewable energy targets, contributing to the EU-wide goal of achieving climate neutrality
by 2050 [6]. The RED mandates that Member States achieve a binding target of 42.5%
renewable energy in gross final consumption by 2030 while encouraging innovative re-
newable technologies and streamlining administrative processes for their deployment [6].
By accelerating the integration of renewable sources, DH systems align with both Den-
mark’s national climate goals and the EU’s objectives under the European Green Deal
[6].
Holbæk is Denmark’s largest town that predominantly relies on gas heating, with around
6,200 gas and oil customers in the town. This high dependency on fossil fuels has made
Holbæk a focal point for transitioning to more sustainable energy sources, as highlighted
in the ”Strategisk Varmeplan 2022-2030” by Holbæk Kommune [7]. The strategic plan
outlines the need to phase out natural gas (NG) and replace it with RES. This shift is
motivated not only by environmental concerns but also by the need to align with national
policies and regulations that support the reduction of fossil fuel use in favour of renewable
energy. The strategic plan reflects the broader national objective to reduce Denmark’s
greenhouse gas emissions by 70% by 2030, in accordance with the Danish Climate Act
[8], as well as becoming carbon neutral by 2050 [7], [8]. Additionally, recent changes
in national legislation now allow local municipalities to prioritise fossil-free alternatives

over traditional gas heating solutions when assessing the most socio-economically viable
options for future energy systems. This regulatory shift has provided Holbæk with the
opportunity to revisit previous assessments and consider more sustainable alternatives
for the city’s heating needs [7]. To realise this vision, Holbæk has outlined a plan to
implement DH over six phases between 2025 and 2036 [9], [7].
The key problem addressed by this thesis is determining the most cost-effective renew-
able energy source mix for the DH system in Holbæk. As Holbæk transitions from NG,
the challenge lies in optimising the energy source mix and effectively integrating RES
for the DH system while ensuring both economic viability and alignment with Denmark’s
ambitious climate targets. Current research on DH systems and energy mix optimisation
has predominantly focused on exploring technologies like biomass and traditional heat
pumps. However, there is a significant gap in studies that specifically address the poten-
tial of geothermal energy (GEO), seawater heat pumps (SWHP) and waste heat recovery
(WHR) in municipalities like Holbæk, where local conditions and resource availability differ
markedly from those in larger cities. Additionally, the unique challenges associated with
phased implementation strategies for integrating these technologies into DH systems in
a cost-effective manner remain under-explored.
This thesis will specifically examine the integration of three renewable energy technolo-
gies as a source for the new DH system in Holbæk: geothermal energy, seawater heat
pumps, and industrial waste heat recovery from Kalundborg. The primary focus will be
on thoroughly investigating the costs, feasibility, and optimal integration of GEO, WHR,
and SWHP into Holbæk’s DH system. For the WHR, work has already been conducted
by stakeholders to explore the feasibility of transporting industrial waste heat via pipelines
over the distance from Kalundborg to Holbæk. This thesis will build upon the existing anal-
yses and data provided by Holbæk municipality, integrating this knowledge into the overall
system design. Additionally, it will evaluate the impact of WHR on the optimal energy mix,
particularly its cost implications and substitution effects on the other technologies.
Economic incentives can play a fundamental role in shaping the adoption and competitive-
ness of renewable energy technologies, especially in district heating systems undergoing
a transition from fossil fuels [10]. These incentives can bridge the cost gap between re-
newable technologies and traditional fossil-based systems. In the context of this thesis,
the viability of GEO, SWHPs, and WHR will be explored, along with their potential need—
or lack thereof—for economic incentives. Furthermore, the analysis will examine how
different policy scenarios can incentivise the adoption of different renewable technologies
and influence the cost and sustainability of district heating systems in Holbæk.
By answering the research question, ”How can geothermal energy, seawater heat pumps,
and waste heat recovery influence the optimal energy source mix for the district heating
system in Holbæk, Denmark, in terms of cost?”, this master’s thesis aims to contribute
to the development of sustainable heating solutions through a comprehensive techno-
economic analysis. The thesis will also address the following additional questions:
• What is the most effective timeline and strategy for integrating the heat sources into
the DH system?
• What policy support frameworks, if any, are required to make the integration of
SWHP, GEO and WHR viable?
The objective is to provide strategic insights for decision-makers in municipal energy plan-
ning, ensuring that the proposed energy solutions meet both economic and environmental
goals.

Literature review
2.1
Overview of district heating systems
Denmark’s energy system is globally recognised for its excellence in energy security, eq-
uity, and sustainability, according to the World Energy Council [11]. As the first Nordic
country to implement DH, Denmark has long been a leader in DH technologies, particu-
larly in using combined heat and power (CHP) as a heat source. In 1974, the oil crisis
highlighted the need for a more self-reliant heating solution, leading to a significant in-
crease in DH usage, which by then covered 30% of homes in Denmark’s larger cities.
Today, DH supplies approximately 64% of Danish households [12].
Denmark’s DH system is renowned for its diversity of heat sources, which include both
fossil fuels and renewable energy. Most of the heat is generated at CHP plants using
fuels such as biomass, waste, NG, and coal. According to 2023 data, biomass accounts
for the largest share of DH production, followed by waste and electricity (see Table 2.1)
[13]. While renewable sources dominate DH production, fossil fuels, like coal, NG, and
oil, still contribute approximately 11% of total heat production. Denmark faces challenges
in fully decarbonising the DH system, including increasing reliance on biomass imports
and the integration of non-combustion heat generation methods into DH systems. Coal,
in particular, has been gradually phased out but still accounts for 6%, while natural gas
and oil contribute 5% and 1%, respectively [13].

air-source heat pumps, while other sources include sewage, solar heat, and sea water.
However, sea water heat pumps accounted for less than 1% of total heat pump produc-
tion [13]. Despite this, the sea water heat pump sector is rapidly expanding. Notably,
the world’s largest sea water heat pump is under development in Esbjerg, with a poten-

contributes around 4% to DH, although it has the potential to meet a larger share of de-

2.2
State of the art
The transition to a sustainable DH system requires not only the adoption of renewable
heat sources but also the effective use of analytical and optimisation methods to support
decision-making. This chapter explores how different methodologies have been applied
in the literature to evaluate and optimise DH systems, ensuring cost-effectiveness, envi-
ronmental benefits, and technical feasibility. To establish a structured understanding, this
chapter first examines techno-economic and cost optimisation models, to help evaluate
the interplay between investment decisions, operational expenditures, and environmen-
tal considerations. Following this, the chapter explores studies that have investigated
the competitiveness and synergies of hybrid energy systems, including the integration of
multiple RES into DH networks. Lastly, the chapter presents an overview of the existing
literature on DH heat sources, identifying under-explored technologies.
2.2.1
Techno-Economic and Cost Optimisations: Insights from Existing
Literature
Techno-economic models have been used in multiple studies for optimising energy sys-
tems and determining cost optimal solutions [17],[18], [19], [20], [21]. For example, [17]
applied a techno-economic optimisation model to Holbæk using TIMES, a model gener-
ator that uses linear-programming [22], to evaluate different DH configurations, consid-
ering HPs, EBs, bio GBs, solar DH, and WHR from Kalundborg [17]. The study found
that WHR showed particularly promising results, but its feasibility, along with that of other
technologies, was highly dependent on long-term policy alignment and clear strategies
for integrating WHR into the DH network. It concluded that further research is needed to
explore additional technological options for heat decarbonisation in rural areas.
Cost optimisation plays a central role in DH system planning, often aiming to balance
economic feasibility with sustainability goals. Many studies [23], [24], [25], [26], [27], [20]
have employed frameworks to optimise cost, using a variety of modelling approaches to
explore trade-offs between investment, operational expenses, and environmental impact.
For example, [26] used EnergyPLAN and MODEST to evaluate the feasibility of large-
scale heat pumps across Denmark, finding that they have a significant socioeconomic
potential. However, the study shows that existing public regulations do not incentivise
large-scale HP investments in district heating, creating a barrier to realising this potential.
Sensitivity analyses further indicate that the feasibility is strongly dependent on electricity
and fuel price dynamics, while investment costs and COP have a lesser impact. Similarly,
[27] employed Balmorel to analyse large-scale heat pump integration in Copenhagen,
highlighting its potential in the Greater Copenhagen district heating system. While sea-
sonal variations in COP were modelled, their overall impact on system-wide performance
was found to be limited, as the COPs of water-based heat pumps remain relatively stable
throughout the year.
Several studies have applied different optimisation frameworks to identify cost-effective
and environmentally sustainable DH configurations, often incorporating multi-objective
approaches. A study by [25] combined EnergyPLAN with MOPSO (Multi-objective particle
swarm optimisation algorithm) to optimise Aalborg’s district heating mix, demonstrating
that a combination of heat pumps and industrial excess heat improves economic and
environmental performance [23] optimises urban CHP systems using Mixed integer linear

Collectively, these studies demonstrate that cost optimisation is crucial in shaping DH sys-

pathways for integrating renewable technologies.
2.2.2
Synergies in Hybrid Energy Systems
The integration of two or more different heat technologies (a hybrid energy system) plays
a key role in optimising DH systems and has proven to be able to improve DH systems in
terms of efficiency, heating capacity, system costs and flexibility of the system [28]. Nu-
merous studies have explored hybrid systems and the integration of multiple technologies,
such as [19], [27], [23], [25], [29], [30]. Studies such as [25] and [29] explore the integra-
tion of WHR and report promising results in terms of cost optimality and carbon emissions
reduction. In Norway, [29] found that coupling SWHP with WHR reduced electricity con-
sumption and CO2 emissions by 88% and 65%, respectively. Additionally, SWHP alone
reduced emissions by 35% compared to EB coupling [29]. Another study found that using
geothermal energy in hybrid systems is crucial for its viability, as hybridisation can help
mitigate its inherent limitations [30]. The study concluded that incorporating GEO in hy-
brid systems can reduce peak load impacts, minimise negative environmental effects, and
lower OPEX. Furthermore, studies indicate that coupling GEO with other heat sources,
such as solar thermal systems, can enhance efficiency and even reduce the initial costs
associated with GEO deployment [30].
2.2.3
Overview of DH heat sources in existing literature
The integration of RES in DH systems has been extensively explored in academic liter-
ature, as has been covered in this chapter. Various studies have investigated different
heat sources, assessing their technical feasibility, cost-effectiveness and potential contri-
butions to decarbonising DH networks. However, the level of focus on each technology
varies, with some heat sources receiving substantial attention, while others remain under-
explored.
Table 2.2 provides an overview of heat sources discussed in existing studies, categorised
by geographic focus. The table highlights that bioenergy, conventional heat pumps and
WHR have been widely studied across Europe. In Denmark, studies have particularly
focused on the role of large-scale HPs, WHR, and bioenergy as key contributors to future
DH systems. Several Danish case studies, such as [17], [26], and [25], have explored the
integration of HPs and WHR, often in hybrid configurations, to balance cost and sustain-
ability objectives. The role of solar thermal in DH systems has also been explored in both
Denmark and other European countries, with studies examining how it can be coupled
with seasonal storage to enhance system performance [20], [31], [32].
Sea water heat pumps are less frequently studied but have been considered in some
Scandinavian and Northern European contexts. In Denmark, studies such as [27] and
[33] have analysed SWHPs, while [29] investigated their potential in Norway and Estonia,
highlighting synergies with WHR. Similarly, geothermal energy remains under-explored in
Denmark, although it has been studied in other European countries [34], [35].

2.3
Existing Policy Frameworks for DH
Policy frameworks shape the development of district heating by influencing competitive-
ness, renewable integration, and long-term viability. This chapter examines the policy
landscape relevant to district heating, starting with an overview of EU-level directives that
set the broader regulatory framework, then exploring Denmark’s national policies. Finally,
policy approaches from other European countries are reviewed to provide comparative in-
sights into alternative strategies for district heating deployment.
2.3.1
EU Policy Frameworks for District Heating
The European Union’s energy policy is driven by the principles of decarbonisation, com-
petitiveness, security of supply, and sustainability, as established in the Treaty on the
Functioning of the European Union (TFEU). These guiding principles are implemented
through various directives and strategies, most notably the Energy Union and the ”Fit for
55” package, which is aimed at reducing net emissions by no less than 55% by 2030 com-
pared to 1990 and aligns EU member states with the goal of achieving climate neutrality
[6]. Among the principles, the Renewable Energy Directive requires member states to
enact national policies that support the expansion of renewable energy [6]. Complement-
ing this is the Energy Efficiency Directive, which establishes an 11.7% target for reducing
primary and final energy consumption by 2030, emphasising the ”energy efficiency first”
principle that ensures energy-saving measures are prioritised in national policies, directly
impacting the efficiency and sustainability of district heating systems [6]. Together, these
directives establish a policy framework designed to accelerate renewable energy integra-
tion and enhance energy efficiency, directly influencing the sustainability and efficiency of
DH systems across Europe. However, the EU framework leaves the specifics of imple-
mentation to member states, which introduces variability and requires careful adaptation
to local conditions.
2.3.2
Denmark’s National Policies and Regulations
Building on the EU framework, Denmark has developed a policy landscape to support
the integration of renewable energy into its DH systems. Denmark’s Heat Supply Act is
6

==PAGE:18==
a cornerstone of the country’s DH policy, regulating public heating installations, includ-
ing large-scale DH systems and smaller block heating stations [44]. The act mandates
that public heating supply installations near existing DH networks connect to these sys-
tems. A key component of the act is the non-profit principle, which ensures that DH
services operate on a cost-recovery basis. This means that DH companies cannot make
a profit or impose indirect taxes on consumers, and only necessary expenses related to
production, distribution, and administration can be included in heating prices [44]. This
regulation supports price stability and consumer protection, addressing challenges seen
in deregulated DH markets. In Sweden, the absence of price regulation has led to price
differences across cities, fostering consumer mistrust, while the UK lacks DH price caps,
offering fewer price protections than gas and electricity markets [45]. In Denmark, price
regulation recognises DH’s monopoly-like nature and ensures cost stability by factoring
depreciation and financing costs into prices [45].
Until 2019, municipalities were able to impose a mandatory grid access for consumers,
requiring them to connect to the DH system. However, this provision was removed from
the Heat Supply Act, giving new consumers the option to choose alternative heating solu-
tions [46]. Despite this change, existing obligations still apply, covering around half of all
DH customers in Denmark. These existing obligations require those customers to remain
connected to the DH network, even though mandatory connection is no longer imposed on
new consumers [46]. Additionally, the Heat Supply act regulates fuel choices for heating,
imposing taxes on fossil fuels although biomass is exempt from these taxes.[44].
Precedent for subsidising renewable energy technologies is evident in Denmark’s pol-
icy landscape. One significant example is the ”Subsidy for Investments in Electric Heat
Pumps for District Heating Production”, introduced in 2021. This program allocated a total
of 8.2 M€ across two application rounds, subsidising up to 15% of eligible costs, includ-
ing purchase, environmental studies, and installation, with a maximum of 5 MDKK per
undertaking [47]. Although this specific subsidy program has ended, its objectives have
been succeeded by the District Heating Pool (Fjernvarmepuljen), established under the
Climate Agreement of 2020. The pool provides funding to district heating companies for
projects converting individual oil and gas boilers to district heating and expanding energy-
efficient district heating networks into new areas. The District Heating Pool has been
extended into 2024 with an additional 265 MDKK allocated for 2024 and 200 MDKK for
2025, contributing to an estimated annual CO2 reduction of 700,000 tonnes [48].
In line with Denmark’s broader efforts to expand the use of renewable energy, new regula-
tions were introduced in December 2021 to promote geothermal energy [49]. These rules
abolished the previous price regulation, allowing prices to be determined through con-
tracts between district heating companies and geothermal operators. These contracts
include a price cap to protect consumers from excessive costs. To further safeguard con-
sumers, the rules specify that they will not bear the financial risks associated with deep
drilling for geothermal projects [49]. In the context of this thesis, Denmarks DH policies are
crucial for understanding how national regulations shape DH development and influence
technology adoption.
2.3.3
Comparative Policy Insights from Other European Countries
Across Europe, DH systems are shaped by diverse regulatory frameworks, financial sup-
port mechanisms, and varying levels of renewable energy integration, offering valuable
insights for designing effective policy strategies. In Norway, municipalities can impose a
legal requirement for buildings to connect to DH networks in designated areas. Similarly,
in Germany and France, local governments have the authority to mandate DH connec-

tion, which has encouraged RES integration in heating systems [46]. The Netherlands
has taken a more flexible approach through heat planning systems, where municipalities
are responsible for developing 10-year heat plans that evaluate all heating options in con-
sultation with citizens and stakeholders. Additionally, the country enforces mandatory DH
connections for new buildings. However, disconnection is permitted under specific condi-
tions, and consumers who choose to disconnect must legally ensure that their alternative
heating system is at least as sustainable as DH [45].
To accelerate the integration of RES into DH systems, subsidies play a crucial role in off-
setting high capital costs and mitigating financial risks associated with DH development
[45]. Many European countries have introduced substantial financial support to promote
renewable DH. Germany’s Special Climate and Transformation Fund (KTF) allocated €3
billion in 2022 to support new and existing DH networks with renewable energy integra-
tion. Similarly, the UK’s Green Heat Network Fund awarded £288 million in 2023 for heat
network projects. Czechia and other Eastern European countries have also launched
new funding schemes aimed at phasing out fossil fuels in DH [50]. Germany provides
heat pump funding through the Federal Funding for Efficient Heating Networks (BEW).
Similarly, France’s Heat Fund (Fonds Chaleur) and Green Loan (Prêt Vert) both support
heat pumps using geothermal energy and waste heat recovery. The Netherlands’ SDE++
scheme provides funding for industrial heat pumps reusing residual heat, geothermal heat
pumps, air-water heat pumps and heat pumps combined with solar thermal panels. Ad-
ditionally, Norway has subsidy schemes to support waste heat recovery technologies,
ensuring that industrial heat pumps play a role in optimising energy use in industries [51].
Sweden has implemented a combination of subsidies and market-based policies to sup-
port DH expansion and decarbonisation. In Sweden, government programmes such as
the Local Investment Programmes (LIP) and the Climate Investment Programmes (Klimp)
were introduced to support environmental initiatives, reduce greenhouse gas emissions,
and promote DH expansion [45]. Additionally, between 2006 and 2010, direct subsidies
were provided to households to incentivise the transition from oil and direct electric heat-
ing to DH. In addition to these financial mechanisms, Sweden’s carbon taxation policy
has been a crucial driver of DH decarbonisation since the 1990s. The implementation of
high carbon taxes has played a pivotal role in reducing fossil fuel use in DH, encouraging
the transition to renewable heat sources. A study on local climate policies in Gothenburg
demonstrates that increasing carbon taxes can accelerate the phase-out of fossil fuels
more effectively than direct fossil fuel bans, which only take effect at a fixed target year
[52].
Tailoring policy frameworks to the specific challenges and opportunities of DH systems
is essential for achieving its economic and environmental goals. By examining these
international examples, the analysis underscores the importance of designing policies
that align with Holbæk’s infrastructure, market conditions, and long-term decarbonisation
targets.

2.4.1
SWHP: An Emerging but Under-Explored Technology
Sea water heat pumps are an emerging technology where Denmark has demonstrated
leadership, with two of the largest operational SWHP systems globally, located in Esbjerg
and Aalborg [53]. These systems represent a major step forward in utilising sea water
as a heat source for large-scale district heating. According to a mapping of potential
heat sources for heat pumps in Denmark, sea water offers substantial potential due to
Denmark’s extensive coastline and relatively stable sea water temperatures, which are
favourable for heat pump operation [33]. However, the article does not address the eco-
nomic feasibility of potential SWHP systems. As shown in Table 2.2, SWHPs remain
relatively under-explored in DH integration studies compared to other renewable heat
sources. This can partly be attributed to the technology’s relative novelty, as one of the
first large-scale systems was commissioned in Drammen, Norway, in 2011 [54]. This nov-
elty contributes to its under-exploration, as practical experience and research remain lim-
ited compared to more established technologies. As a result, further studies are needed
to evaluate the economic feasibility and integration of SWHPs into hybrid systems with
other renewable heat sources, ensuring their broader adoption in Denmark’s district heat-
ing networks. As mentioned above, [29] demonstrated the promising potential of SWHP
in hybrid systems. However, further research is required to explore its wider applicability
and effectiveness in different district heating contexts.
2.4.2
GEO in Denmark: Untapped Potential and Barriers
Geothermal energy remains underutilised in Denmark, with only three operational geother-
mal plants providing a combined heating capacity of 33 MW, of which only one consis-
tently produces around 7 MW [55]. [56] highlights the significant potential of Denmark’s
subsurface for geothermal energy exploitation, noting that only a small fraction of this po-
tential is currently being utilised. Additionally, a separate study on geothermal energy for
district heating and cooling in Europe notes that medium-deep and deep geothermal sys-
tems are receiving more attention and funding. It suggests that advances in technology,
particularly those adopted from the petroleum industry, could help accelerate their devel-
opment, which may enhance their applicability in Denmark [57]. While solar photovoltaic
and wind energy have garnered widespread support, with policies implemented in over
100 countries, geothermal energy remains comparatively under-represented. Fewer than
30 countries have established frameworks specifically tailored to geothermal energy, and
these are predominantly focused on electricity generation rather than heating applications
[58]. Despite this promise, high exploration and drilling costs, coupled with uncertainties
about resource availability, have posed major barriers to wider adoption. Studies from
other European countries, shown in Table 2.2, show that geothermal energy can serve as
a reliable base-load heat source for district heating when supported by favourable policies.
This shows the importance of continued research in Denmark, particularly to evaluate the
economic feasibility and competitiveness of geothermal energy in comparison to other
renewable heat sources.

2.4.4
Gaps in Strategic Deployment, Technology Trade-Offs, and Policy
Support for District Heating
Phased implementation strategies for district heating technologies are largely absent in
the current literature. None of the studies in Table 2.2 consider how incremental deploy-
ment over time could influence decisions about heat source selection, network design, or
overall system costs. Addressing this, the thesis evaluates how phased implementation
can influence deployment strategies for district heating systems, providing a more flexible
and adaptive approach to energy planning. Another important aspect to consider is the
substitution effect among renewable heat sources, which is under-explored in the cur-
rent literature. Few studies evaluate the trade-offs between different technologies under
varying conditions, such as resource availability, cost, and policy incentives. This thesis
addresses these substitution effects by optimising the choice of heat sources for district
heating systems based on cost-effectiveness and system integration. Additionally, it is
worth investigating whether emerging technologies like SWHPs, GEO, and WHR could
contribute to a more robust, diversified and sustainable district heating system. Further-
more, evaluating whether these technologies require policy support to bridge potential
economic gaps is essential to determining their scalability and feasibility in the Danish
context.
2.4.5
Addressing the Gaps: Scope of This Thesis
This thesis aims to fill existing knowledge gaps by examining the integration of WHR,
GEO, and SWHPs into Holbæk’s DH system while also assessing the influence of key
policy mechanisms.
Specifically, mandatory connection policies and subsidy support
schemes have been selected for further examination, forming the basis for two policy sce-

an area of interest. By evaluating Holbæk’s district heating system under these policy

of sustainable and context-specific DH systems but also to provide a foundation for fu-
ture policy and planning efforts, offering a nuanced understanding of how regulatory and
financial frameworks shape the feasibility and adoption of district heating technologies.

3.3
Modelling
The model aims to optimise the total system cost of the district heating system over a 23-
year horizon, from 2027 to 2050. Using a MILP framework, the model minimises the TSC
while ensuring energy demand requirements are met. The optimisation includes finan-
cial, technical, and policy-driven factors, such as annuity-based capital cost, operational
constraints, and technology-specific constraints. The decision variables represent the ca-
pacity of each technology as a function of years and units. This structure allows the model
to determine the optimal size and installation schedule for each technology. The model is
implemented with the programming language Julia using the JuMP package, with Gurobi
as the solver. Further details on the tool selection are provided in Appendix A as well as
the full mathematical model can be found in Appendix B.
The discount rate, r, used in the model is set to 4%, based on the standard value provided
by the Danish Energy Agency in its District Heating Assessment Tool [64]. This rate aligns
with Danish financial standards and reflects the economic framework typically used for
evaluating district heating projects in Denmark.

value beyond the analysis period.

3.3.3.7
Policy Scenario Constraints
For the three policy scenarios, the following changes are made:
• Mandatory Connection Policy Scenario: The power and heat demand are in-
creased from an 85% connection rate to 100%, corresponding to a 17.6% increase.
No other adjustments are made to the model.
• Subsidy Scenarios: As stated in section 3.2, this scenario explores the financial
support required to make GEO, WHR, and SWHP competitive in the district heat-
ing system. Through iteration, the threshold is determined where the technologies
become competitive with other technologies. This involves adjusting CAPEXu,y
(where u is 7 for GEO, 8 for SWHP or 10 for WHR), until the model substitutes the
relevant technology as the solution. The result is a CAPEX reduction factor between
0 (full subsidy, i.e., 0% of the original CAPEX value) and 1 (no subsidy). In the case
of GEO, the development cost would also be reduced to 0.

interest for geothermal energy in Denmark [15].
Geothermal energy technology incurs an initial cost for development and studies, typically
ranging between 1.5–3 M€ per site [53]. For the purpose of this thesis, a midpoint value of
2.25 M€ is applied if the model selects geothermal as a source. Another constraint related
to geothermal energy is the requirement for a constant flow within the geothermal plant.
However, this flow can be up- or down-regulated due to the involvement of a heat pump
in the system. The minimum down-regulation is 20%, meaning the geothermal plant must
operate at a minimum of 20% capacity [53].

3.5
Sensitivity analysis
To assess the robustness of the optimisation model, a sensitivity analysis explores how
variations in electricity price, gas price, and the discount rate influence the TSC and en-
ergy mix. These parameters were chosen due to their impact on investment decisions and
overall system performance, and their ranges are summarised in Table 3.11. The analysis
focuses on policy scenarios, as these introduce interventions that can affect technology
adoption and system costs. By examining these variations, the analysis evaluates the
resilience of the proposed strategies under changing economic and energy conditions.
3.5.1
Electricity Price Variations
Electricity prices can strongly influence the viability of renewable technologies. The sensi-
tivity analysis tests a range from a 50% reduction to a 50% increase, with 10% increments,
reflecting potential market fluctuations. For context, the yearly average electricity price
for 2023 was 81.25 €/MWh, with peak monthly averages reaching 100.65 €/MWh [67].
Energistyrelsen’s projections for 2030 indicate lower electricity prices due to increasing
renewable energy integration and efficiency improvements[65]. The analysis assesses
how deviations from these forecasts could impact system performance and investment
decisions.
3.5.2
Gas Price Variations
The analysis considers a range from -50% to +50%, with 10% increments, reflecting po-
tential market shifts or policy delays in the fossil fuel phase-out. Lower gas prices could
make gas boilers more competitive, whereas higher prices favour other alternatives. De-
lays in the transition to biogas, expected by 2030, could sustain lower gas prices and

influence technology adoption. By including these variations, the analysis ensures the
model captures the full range of potential impacts on costs, emissions, and the energy
mix.

Discount Rate

attractiveness of high-CAPEX technologies. The baseline discount rate is set at 4%, as

The emissions in the Cost Optimal Scenario amount to 667.5 tCO2, as shown in Table 4.1.

Discussions
This thesis evaluates how GEO, SWHPs, and WHR influence the optimal energy source
mix for the DH system in Holbæk, Denmark. The results show that SWHP is the most
cost-effective solution under current conditions, while GEO and WHR require subsidies
to become competitive. GEO emerges as the most viable subsidised option, requiring a
relatively modest subsidy (22.1 M€) compared to WHR (58.8 M€). The thesis also finds
that policy support can play an important role in enabling these technologies, particularly
through cost reductions. The optimal timeline for integrating these heat sources follows
projected demand growth, with key investments occurring in 2030, 2031, 2032, and 2036.
The results highlight key differences between the Base Case and Cost Optimal Scenarios,
particularly in system costs and energy mix. The Base Case includes 145 MW of capacity,
77% above needed demand, whereas the Cost Optimal Scenario limits capacity to 107
MW. This adjustment reduces TSC by 20%, from 132.5 M€ to 106.5 M€. While the Base
Case has a lower cost per MW (1.3 M€/MW vs. 1.7 M€/MW), this is due to its reliance on
GBs, which account for 40% of capacity and are the cheapest available units. The Cost
Optimal Scenario instead prioritises electricity-based technologies that reduce long-term
dependency on gas. The shift from NG to biogas expected by 2030 further reinforces
the advantages of the Cost Optimal Scenario. While this transition decreases fossil fuel
reliance, it also increases gas prices and exposes systems heavily reliant on GBs to cost
and supply uncertainties. The optimisation model used in this thesis identifies a cheaper
solution than the Base Case and demonstrates that systems can reduce reliance on gas
boilers in a cost-efficient manner.
A key finding is the strong role of SWHP in the Cost Optimal Scenario. Unlike GEO and
WHR, which require subsidies to become competitive, SWHP is selected without policy
incentives. This underscores its potential as a cost-effective and environmentally sustain-
able solution, which may be underutilised in traditional planning approaches and could
play a greater role in future DH planning. Traditional planning approaches may under-
utilise SWHP’s potential, and future DH strategies should consider its wider adoption.
The sensitivity analysis demonstrates that the model’s results are generally robust to
variations in the analysed parameters. A notable outcome is the consistent selection
of SWHPs across all scenarios and parameter variations. SWHPs are chosen in the Cost
Optimal Scenario and remain a dominant investment choice under higher discount rates,
lower electricity prices, and higher gas prices, showcasing their adaptability to diverse
economic conditions. This robustness simplifies planning and reduces risk for decision-
makers, underscoring the reliability of SWHP as a core technology for DH systems. Con-
versely, GEO and WHR exhibit strengths under specific conditions, such as high electricity
prices, where their high COP enables heat output with minimal electricity use. This feature
positions GEO and WHR as valuable options for shielding consumers against increase
in electricity prices, reinforcing the importance of these technologies in diversifying and
stabilising DH systems.
Emissions are determined by Phase 1 investments, as no new technologies are imple-
mented before 2030. Scenarios with identical demand show identical emissions during
this period due to reliance on pre-existing units. Higher demand scenarios, like Mandatory
Connections, exhibit a disproportionate emissions increase (e.g., a 17.6% rise in demand
causes a 73% emissions increase) as the GB meets additional demand, being cheaper

than the EB while the HP operates at full capacity. The Base Case Scenario has the
lowest emissions, as HPs provide 90% of the heat and EBs 10%, avoiding GB use. How-
ever, this outcome is driven by operational decision-making rather than cost optimisation.
Cost-driven models would favour the GB over the EB, highlighting a trade-off between

The subsidy amounts required to bridge the cost gap for GEO and WHR differ signifi-
cantly, with the Geothermal Subsidy Scenario requiring 22.1 M€ compared to 58.8 M€
for WHR. These figures highlight the substantial financial commitment needed to make
these technologies competitive under current market conditions. For Holbæk, the high
cost of WHR positions GEO as a more attractive option. This advantage is further rein-
forced by the anticipated 25% reduction in CAPEX for geothermal plants, as outlined in
the Teknologikatalog [53], which could substantially lower the subsidy required for GEO
adoption. Another potential advantage for GEO is Denmark’s new geothermal regula-
tions, as mentioned in section 2.3, which removed previous price restrictions, allowing
prices to be determined through contracts between DH companies and GEO operators.
These policy changes could further improve the viability of GEO.
Given that government-funded subsidies ultimately rely on taxpayer contributions, their
allocation and justification warrant careful consideration. This raises critical equity con-
cerns: while taxpayers collectively fund these initiatives, the direct benefits are primarily
enjoyed by DH users. For these end users, a reduced TSC translates to more afford-
able heating costs, improved accessibility, and enhanced energy security. Additionally,
DH companies could pursue external funding opportunities, such as the national subsidy
pool mentioned in section 2.3. These opportunities could play a pivotal role in financing
sustainable energy transitions without disproportionately impacting any single stakeholder
group.
The findings from this study highlight several actionable policy recommendations to sup-
port the transition to sustainable DH. While subsidies are essential for enabling technolo-
gies like GEO and WHR, their allocation requires careful justification. GEO emerges as
the more cost-effective option, while WHR presents a more complex case. Although WHR
requires a substantial subsidy (58.8 M€ for a 30 MW system), its cost should be contex-
tualised. The estimated 72.2 M€ required to construct the pipeline from Kalundborg to
Holbæk suggests that WHR could become highly viable when deployed locally. Future
research should explore technical advancements to reduce the costs of both GEO and
WHR, potentially increasing their competitiveness without large subsidies. The Mandatory
Connections Scenario introduces additional considerations. While its TSC is higher, the
cost is distributed across a broader user base, potentially lowering heating costs for indi-
vidual households. Additionally, this policy achieves net-negative emissions, as avoided
emissions from decentralised systems exceed those generated within the DH network,
reinforcing the environmental benefits of mandatory connections.
The results suggest a nuanced policy approach: focusing subsidies on cost-effective tech-
nologies like GEO, addressing WHR’s local implementation challenges, and supporting
mandatory connections to maximise environmental and social benefits. Policymakers
must strike a balance between short-term costs and long-term gains to foster a resilient,
sustainable, and equitable DH system.

Conclusions
This thesis has demonstrated the potential for optimising the energy source mix for Hol-
bæk’s DH system by leveraging innovative and sustainable technologies. The Cost Opti-
mal Scenario highlights SWHP as a cost-effective and environmentally favourable option,
achieving significant reductions in total system cost while aligning capacity with demand.
Policy interventions, such as subsidies for GEO and WHR, enhance the competitiveness
of these technologies, diversifying energy sources and ensuring system resilience.
Sensitivity analyses confirmed the robustness of SWHP as a primary technology, resilient
to variations in energy prices and financial parameters. By prioritising high-efficiency and
low-emission technologies, the proposed solutions align with Denmark’s ambitious cli-
mate goals while offering strategic insights for policymakers in balancing economic and
environmental priorities.
The analysis highlights trade-offs between cost efficiency and emissions reduction. Gas
boilers offer a cost-effective solution for peak demands but face higher costs and sup-
ply challenges with biogas post-2030. Mandatory DH connections achieve net-negative
emissions by offsetting emissions from individual heating systems, underscoring the value
of coordinated policy measures.
This thesis set out to answer the question: How can GEO, SWHP, and WHR influence
the optimal energy source mix for Holbæk’s DH system in terms of cost? and addressed
two sub-questions regarding the integration timeline and required policy support mech-
anisms. The findings reveal that SWHP is the most cost-effective option, making it the
ideal backbone of Holbæk’s DH system. With targeted subsidies, GEO can play a cen-
tral role as a promising base-load option, while WHR, despite its potential, is limited by
high transmission costs. The optimal integration timeline prioritises SWHP deployment
from 2030 to meet growing demand, with GEO as a promising base-load option in later
phases if CAPEX is partly subsidised. Policy mechanisms, particularly targeted subsidies,
are essential for ensuring the competitiveness of WHR and GEO within the energy mix.
Mandatory connections to DH networks enhance adoption, distribute costs equitably, and
achieve significant emissions reductions.
Based on the findings of this thesis, the most favourable policy recommendations are
to implement the GEO subsidy and enforce mandatory connections to the DH network.
SWHP emerges as the most economical and robust option, forming the backbone of the
DH system with no need for additional policy support for its implementation. GEO offers a
cost-effective and scalable base-load solution, with its high COP ensuring minimal elec-
tricity consumption. This makes GEO resilient to energy price fluctuations and a reliable
long-term investment. Additionally, expected reductions in GEO CAPEX, as projected in
the Teknologikatalog, further enhance its feasibility and long-term affordability, maximising
the impact of public funding. Mandatory connections complement this by ensuring higher
adoption rates, distributing costs equitably across users, and achieving significant emis-
sions reductions. This policy offsets emissions from individual heating systems, resulting
in a net-negative emissions balance. Together, SWHPs, subsidies and a mandatory con-
nection policy provide a balanced approach that prioritises cost-efficiency, sustainability,
and resilience for Holbæk’s DH system, aligning with Denmark’s climate goals while en-
suring equitable energy access for all. While substantial support is required in both sub-
sidy scenarios, the GEO subsidy entails a lower public expenditure, making it the more

6.1
Limitations
This section outlines the key limitations of the optimisation model and the assumptions
used in this thesis, highlighting challenges in balancing model complexity, computational
feasibility, and real-world applicability.
The model assumes perfect foresight, meaning it has complete knowledge of all future
prices, demands, and other parameters. This assumption can lead to unrealistic decision-
making, such as perfectly timing maintenance schedules or technology usage to minimise
costs. In reality, decision-makers face uncertainty and must make choices based on in-
complete information. Incorporating non-perfect foresight would make the model more
realistic, for example, by generating multiple scenarios with varying price trends and de-
mands to reflect the inherent uncertainty in electricity and fuel markets.
Forecasting assumptions further contribute to the model’s limitations. Key inputs, such as
tariffs, taxes, and fuel prices, are fixed beyond certain points, with yearly electricity prices
assumed constant after 2030 and gas prices after 2034. While these forecasts are based
on the best available data, they fail to capture potential market shifts or unforeseen events.
This fixed approach limits the model’s ability to account for long-term uncertainties and
could affect conclusions about the cost-effectiveness of specific technologies.
The model also assumes a strict adherence to Denmark’s policy of phasing out fossil
fuels by 2030. However, delays in implementing this phase-out could significantly affect
gas prices and the availability of fossil fuels, introducing uncertainty that is not reflected
in the current model. Such delays could result in lower gas prices, making gas-based
technologies more competitive in situations where renewable energy alternatives would
otherwise dominate. This uncertainty underscores the importance of sensitivity analyses
to explore the impact of such potential delays on technology selection and system costs.
While the transition to a fossil-free energy system by 2030 assumes biogas will replace
natural gas and electricity becoming fully renewable, several challenges that may dis-
rupt these assumptions. Fossil fuels could still play a role in NG used by the GB, and
imported electricity from neighbouring countries may not be entirely renewable. Delays
in achieving full fossil-free energy production could further impact emissions trajectories.
Additionally, considering gross emissions, including CO2 from biogas combustion, would
provide a more comprehensive understanding of the system’s carbon footprint. Although
biogas emissions are offset through its lifecycle, the direct CO2 emissions from combus-
tion still contribute to overall emissions accounting. Including these emissions could offer
valuable insights into the total environmental impact of renewable and bio-based energy
sources. Future research could explore this to better assess for the role of bio-based fuels
in decarbonisation efforts.
The model also simplifies real-world district heating dynamics by assuming uniform in-
frastructure and excluding transmission constraints (apart from the Kalundborg pipeline).
This abstraction does not account for spatial variations in demand or the feasibility of
specific infrastructure layouts. Additionally, the demand forecast, provided by Rambøll
and modelled in EnergyPRO, assumes static growth over time. While this reflects cur-
rent projections, it may not fully account for trends such as improved building efficiency
or behavioural changes. Finally, while the model optimises the capacity and weekly pro-
duction of technologies over the 24-year horizon, it does not explicitly simulate real-time
or sub-weekly operational dynamics, such as ramping rates, maintenance schedules, or

priority-based dispatching during unforeseen demand fluctuations. These simplifications
ensure the model remains tractable for long-term planning, while acknowledging that ad-

The WHR analysis relies on averaged cost data, as detailed earlier in the thesis. While
the original data assessed the feasibility of utilising waste heat from multiple industrial
sources, significant variability in heat supply potentials and uncertainties regarding par-
ticipation necessitated the use of an average cost based on the viable contributors. Ad-

not fully reflect the specific costs or technical feasibility of individual contributors, intro-
ducing uncertainty into the WHR-related results.
Another limitation of this thesis is the inability to comprehensively calculate gross emis-
sions due to the lack of detailed data on biomass-based electricity generation and its as-
sociated emissions in Denmark. While it is possible to estimate the approximate share of
electricity generated from biomass, determining the emissions specifically resulting from
biomass and biogas combustion is more complex. Biogas emissions are often treated as
equivalent to natural gas in gross emission calculations, but biomass emissions are harder
to quantify. This is particularly true for solid biomass, where factors such as combus-
tion efficiency and carbon content can vary widely. Without reliable emission factors for
biomass-based electricity, it becomes challenging to accurately account for gross emis-
sions, especially when biomass and biogas play a significant role in energy production.
The reliance on data from the Teknologikatalog also introduces some uncertainty, partic-
ularly for SWHP and GEO technologies. The 20 MW SWHP data is based on a single
installation from 2020, while GEO estimates rely on just three installations. While these
benchmarks are useful, they lack the robustness of more widely implemented technolo-
gies. Additionally, WHR costs were derived from a different source, introducing potential
inconsistencies in cost comparisons across technologies.
A further assumption in this thesis is that the conditions in Holbæk Fjord are suitable
for implementing a SWHP. In reality, deploying a SWHP requires an environmental as-
sessment to determine whether local conditions allow for its installation. Future research
should evaluate this to ensure feasibility and identify any potential constraints.
Finally, the model uses a single-objective optimisation approach, focusing solely on cost
minimisation. While this effectively identifies the most cost-efficient energy mix, it does
not explicitly account for emissions as a parallel objective. Incorporating a multi-objective
optimisation model could enable the system to balance cost reduction with emissions
minimisation, enforcing earlier investments in low-emission technologies. This approach
could help reduce emissions, ensuring that environmental goals are met more aggres-
sively. Future research could explore such a framework, integrating cost and emissions
objectives to provide a more holistic strategy for DH system optimisation.

Python and MATLAB. Its just-in-time (JIT) compilation enables near-C-level performance,
making it highly efficient for computationally intensive tasks. Furthermore, Julia’s alge-
braic modelling package, JuMP, significantly outperforms Python-based tools like Pyomo,
particularly for large-scale optimisation problems, while maintaining a user-friendly syn-
tax. Julia’s advanced metaprogramming capabilities and superior handling of optimisation
through symbolic differentiation allow for the efficient implementation of complex algo-
rithms, ensuring that even the most challenging optimisations are executed swiftly and
effectively [81]. These features make Julia a compelling tool for addressing the compu-
tational requirements of this thesis. The decision to use Julia is further supported by its
demonstrated success in similar optimisation problems.
Research demonstrates Julia’s suitability for solving large-scale optimisation problems.
Studies like [82] and [83] demonstrated the use of Julia in techno-economic optimisa-
tion models for demand-side flexibility and offshore wind power, respectively. Both stud-
ies highlighted Julia’s ability to efficiently handle large-scale optimisation problems. The
study in [82] focused on optimising demand-side flexibility, using Julia to model consumer
behaviour and electricity distribution efficiency, while [83] applied Mixed Integer Linear
Programming (MILP) in Julia to determine the cost-optimal transmission infrastructure for
offshore wind power. These examples support the decision to use Julia as a tool in this
thesis due to its efficiency in handling computationally intensive problems. Given this
proven efficiency, Julia is well-suited to developing the district heating optimisation model
detailed in this thesis.
Julia provides the computational foundation for this thesis’s optimisation model. This the-
sis employs Julia to develop a MILP optimisation model for district heating systems, fo-
cusing on minimising system costs. JuMP serves as the modelling framework, paired with
Gurobi—a commercial solver renowned for its performance in both linear programming
(LP) and mixed-integer programming (MIP) [84]. This combination ensures robust and
efficient optimisation tailored to the needs of the district heating heat source mix analysis.

consumption. Since lower COP values result in higher electricity usage, the operational
expenditures are reduced in this case, making capital costs the dominant factor.

E.3
Combined Policies Scenario
The Combined Policies Scenario assesses the impact of mandatory connection require-
ments and financial support where applicable on technology adoption, system costs, and
emissions. By integrating both policy measures, this scenario evaluates whether their
combined implementation improves the competitiveness of SWHP, GEO, and WHR rela-
tive to other technologies and how it affects overall system costs.
However, the results did not reveal significant new insights beyond those already ob-
served in the individual policy scenarios. The combined effects largely aligned with ex-
pectations based on the separate impacts of mandatory connections and financial sup-
port, without introducing fundamentally different trends or interactions. Due to the lack of
additional value in the main discussion, this scenario has been included in the appendix
for completeness.
In this combined scenario, the model prioritises WHR as the primary investment, favouring
it over GEO. Notably, two out of the three evaluated technologies, SWHP and WHR, are
chosen, reflecting their competitiveness under the combined policy framework. However,
it is important to note that the subsidy for WHR (58.8 M€) is significantly higher than that
for GEO (22.1 M€). The TSC for this scenario is 124.1 M€, or about 17.6 M€ higher
than in the Cost Optimal Scenario. This is due to the larger capacity installed for the
mandatory connections policy. However, due to that, the capacity cost is lower in this
scenario compared to the Cost Optimal Scenario, with 1.53 M€/MW while in the Cost
Optimal Scenario it’s 1.72 M€/MW. Figure E.4 illustrates the capacity changes relative to
the Cost Optimal Scenario, showing how combining policies shifts investment priorities.

## Minimise total system cost, including:
## - Investment costs (CAPEX) with annuity factors.
## - Development costs for geothermal (if activated).
## - Variable and fixed OPEX for all units.
## - Extra gas costs based on tiered consumption.
