Abstract
The sustainable development of energy has become a focal point in today’s society, driven
by the escalating consumption of traditional energy sources and the pressing need for
environmental protection. Consequently, the demand for new energy solutions has
reached unprecedented levels. Wind energy emerges as a promising alternative that aligns
perfectly with the requirements of social development. The exponential growth of wind
power as a renewable energy source is a testament to the intense interest and investment
in this field.
In light of the global energy landscape, it is evident that wind power will play a vital role
in meeting the world’s energy needs. As the installed capacity of wind turbines continues
to increase, an increasing number of wind farms will be constructed in cold regions
characterized by high latitudes or altitudes. Consequently, the issue of ice accretion on
wind turbine blades needs to be carefully considered.
The primary objective of this project, conducted in collaboration with Force Technology, is
to gain a comprehensive understanding of the scaling effects of ice formation under
different operating conditions. Specifically, the study will explore the influence of factors
such as varying tip speed ratios (TSR), angle of attack (AoA), and chord length, and their
effects on ice formation. The investigations will be carried out utilizing a climate wind
tunnel provided by Force Technology. Advanced camera systems will be employed for 3D
scanning of wind turbine blades, followed by sophisticated post-processing techniques to
extract precise ice shape data.

Introduction
The sustainable development of energy has become a crucial global concern, driven by the
escalating consumption of traditional energy sources and the pressing need for
environmental preservation. In this context, wind energy has emerged as a promising
alternative, offering significant potential for meeting the world’s energy needs while
reducing greenhouse gas emissions. However, the operation of wind turbines in cold
climate environments poses unique challenges, particularly with regard to ice accretion on
the turbine blades. The formation of ice can have detrimental effects on the performance,
efficiency, and safety of wind turbines, necessitating a thorough investigation of the
importance of operating conditions in shaping ice formations.
The primary objective of this thesis is to investigate the importance of operating
conditions for ice shapes on vertical axis wind turbines (VAWT) operating in cold climate
environments. The focus of this project will be on conducting experiments in a climate
wind tunnel, utilizing advanced camera systems for 3D scanning and post-processing
techniques to analyze and quantify ice shapes on VAWT blades. By systematically varying
operating conditions and studying their influence on ice formation, this research aims to
enhance our understanding of the complex interaction between wind turbine operation,
environmental conditions, and ice accumulation.
The investigation of ice shapes on VAWT blades under different operating conditions holds
significant practical and theoretical importance. First, understanding how operating
conditions influence ice formation is crucial for developing effective mitigation strategies
to minimize the adverse effects of ice on wind turbine performance and reliability. Such
insights can contribute to the development of improved blade designs, ice detection and
prevention systems, and optimized operational strategies for wind turbines operating in
cold climates.
Second, this research can facilitate the advancement of numerical modeling and
simulation techniques for predicting ice shapes on wind turbine blades. The experimental
data obtained in this study will serve as a valuable validation resource for numerical
models, enabling more accurate predictions of ice accumulation and its impact on turbine
performance.
1
==PAGE:10==
The research methodology for this thesis involves conducting experiments in a climate
wind tunnel provided by Force technology. The wind tunnel will replicate cold climate
conditions, enabling controlled investigations of ice formation on VAWT blades. Advanced
camera systems will be employed to capture high-resolution 3D scans of the blades, which
will be subjected to post-processing techniques for extracting accurate ice shape data.
This thesis is structured into several chapters to systematically explore and present the
research findings. Chapter 2 provides a comprehensive review of the literature,
summarizing existing knowledge on wind turbine icing and the influence of operating
conditions. Chapter 3 describes the experimental setup, including details of the climate
wind tunnel, camera systems, and data acquisition methods. Chapter 4 presents the results
and analysis of the experiments, discussing the impact of various operating conditions on
ice shape formation. Finally, Chapter 5 concludes the thesis, summarizing the key findings,
discussing their implications, and providing recommendations for future research.
By investigating the importance of operating conditions for ice shapes on VAWT blades,
this research aims to contribute to the advancement of wind turbine technology and
facilitate the effective deployment of wind energy in cold climate regions.

2.1 VAWT
According to the orientation of the rotor axis, wind turbines can be categorized into two
types: Vertical Axis Wind Turbines (VAWTs) and Horizontal Axis Wind Turbines (HAWTs).
While HAWTs are more commonly used, VAWTs offer several advantages that make them
an appealing choice. Their benefits include a simpler design, capturing wind from all
directions, and ease of maintenance.
In this project, a vertical axis wind turbine model was used for the experiments. VAWTs
have their main rotor axis oriented perpendicular to the wind direction. The experimental
setup in the wind tunnel involves simulating cloud and fog conditions by spraying water
droplets, with a fixed strip acting as the cloud generator. Choosing a vertical axis wind
turbine allows for more consistent exposure of the turbine blades to the cloud-like
conditions. This decision was made to ensure optimal experimental results and accurate
assessment of the turbine’s performance under simulated cloud conditions. The use of a
VAWT is expected to provide valuable insights into the behavior of wind turbines in icing
conditions and contribute to a better understanding of their efficiency and reliability in
such environments.

2.2 Ice
2.2.1 The Occurrence of Freezing
Freezing conditions are prevalent in numerous regions across the globe. The Figure 2.2
depicts a widespread coverage of snow and ice across most parts of Europe on January,
2019. This illustrates the widespread occurrence of freezing phenomena in the region.
Freezing events, such as snowfall and ice formation, are common in many areas of the
world, particularly during winter seasons or in colder climates. Freezing temperatures can
lead to various phenomena, including the formation of ice, frost, and the freezing of
bodies of water such as lakes and rivers. Freezing conditions have a notable impact on
wind turbines and power generation. When ice forms on wind turbine blades, it can
significantly affect their aerodynamic performance and overall efficiency. The
accumulation of ice on the blades adds extra weight and disrupts the smooth airflow,
leading to increased drag and reduced power output.

Meanwhile, the formation of ice is influenced by many parameters, such as:
• Velocity: The speed of the air or wind.
• Droplet Size (Media Volumetric Diameter): The Median Volumetric Diameter
(MVD) is a metric used to describe the size of water droplets in a cloud or aerosol
distribution. It is a statistical measure that represents the midpoint of the droplet
size distribution, where half of the droplets are larger and half are smaller than the
MVD[3].
• Liquid Water Content: LWC is defined as the mass of water contained in a unit
volume of air, and the commonly used unit is g/m3. For icing on wind turbine
blades, LWC affects the type of ice accretion (i.e. rime or glaze) and the rate of ice
accretion on the blade surface[4].
• Temperature: In the context of ice accretion on wind turbine blades, temperature
refers to the ambient air temperature surrounding the wind turbine.
• Accretion Time: Accretion time is the duration over which ice accretion takes place
on the surfaces of wind turbine blades.
2.3 Icing type
Different icing Parameters create different types of ice, there are three main types of ice:
• Glaze ice is a type of ice coating characterized by its high density, transparency,
smoothness, and homogeneous appearance, similar to clear ice. It forms when there
is a high concentration of water in a cloud between temperatures of 0°C and -4°C.
The rate at which glaze ice accumulates depends on factors such as the precipitation
rate, wind speed, and temperature. Glaze ice is known for its strong adhesion and
cohesion forces, making it difficult to remove once formed.
Studies have shown that the presence of glaze ice on an airfoil, such as a wind
turbine blade, leads to the formation of double or multiple horn-shaped ice
structures. These ice formations cause a decrease in lift, an increase in drag, and an
increase in pitching moment. Additionally, the formation of horn ice increases the
projected height of the airfoil, resulting in higher turbulence intensity.
• Rime ice, is a white and opaque ice that contains trapped air. It forms when
supercooled water droplets freeze on surfaces, creating icing structures on the

windward side. Rime ice has a brittle crystalline structure and can be further
classified as soft rime or hard rime based on temperature. Soft rime, similar to hoar
frost, forms below -8°C and is less dense and more crystalline compared to hard
rime. Hard rime forms between -2°C and -8°C, has a higher density, and appears
more opaque.
The rate of rime ice accretion depends on various factors, including the exposed
object’s dimensions, wind speed, water content in the air, and temperature. The
aerodynamic effects of rime ice on an airfoil include a reduction in lift and an
increase in drag. However, due to its streamlined shape, rime ice has a less
pronounced impact on airfoil aerodynamics compared to glaze ice and mixed ice.
• Mixed ice is a combination of glaze ice and rime ice and is one of the most common
forms of ice encountered in real-life scenarios. It appears as layers of clear and
opaque ice, resulting from variations in liquid water content, air temperature, and
droplet size. Mixed ice formations can exhibit horn ice and are generally more
challenging to remove than rime ice. Similar to glaze ice, the presence of mixed ice
significantly degrades lift and increases drag.

2.4 Icing accretion process
Ice accretion is a complex process that involves the formation and buildup of ice on
surfaces exposed to freezing conditions. The process typically occurs when supercooled
water droplets or liquid water below the freezing point come into contact with a solid
surface and freeze upon impact.
The ice accretion process can be described in several stages:
• Nucleation: Nucleation is the initial stage of ice formation. Freezing produces small
ice nuclei when liquid or super-cooled water comes into contact with a surface.
Nucleation can be affected by factors such as temperature, humidity, and the
presence of ice nucleating agents.
• Growth: Further ice growth occurs after the formation of the ice nuclei.
Super-cooled water droplets in the atmosphere hit the existing ice nuclei and freeze,
causing the growth of ice layer. The growth rate depends on various factors,
including the size and concentration of the liquid droplets and the velocity of airflow
and the .
• Aggregation: At the process of ice continues to grow, adjacent ice particles can meet
each other and merge, which can form larger ice structures. This process is
influenced by factors such as collision rates, airflow patterns, and the properties of
the ice surface[6].
• Shape development: The shape of the ice accretion depends on the airflow
characteristics and the geometric features of the surface. For example, on an airfoil
surface, ice can accumulate more at specific locations, such as the leading edge or
near stagnation points, due to variations in airflow velocity and pressure
distribution. The shape of the ice accretion can affect aerodynamic performance and
increase drag on the surface[7].
• Runback and shedding: As the ice layer grows, some of the ice that has
accumulated may begin to melt or fall off due to aerodynamic influences such as the
wind or the rotation of wind turbine blades. Runback is when the ice melts and flows
along the surface before shedding, while shedding is when the ice is completely
detached from the surface. The rate of runback and shedding depends on factors
such as surface temperature, air velocity, and the adhesion strength of the ice[8].
In particular, there is an equation can calculate the icing rate dependent with the specific
parameters[9].

3.2.2 Angle of Attack Clips
The initial experiments conducted in the project utilized angle of attack(AoA) clips with
angles of 0 degrees and ±2 degrees. However, after conducting preliminary tests, it was
found that the results obtained with the ±2 degrees AoA clips were not as clear and
distinct as desired.
Considering the common operational range of wind turbines in practical applications, it
was decided to redesign the AoA clips with angles of ±4 degrees. This wider range of
angle adjustments allows for a more comprehensive investigation into the effects of
different angles of attack on ice accretion.
Given the small change in the angle of attack (AoA) in the experimental design, manual
adjustments may indeed introduce significant errors and affect the accuracy of the results.
In order to reduce errors caused by manual adjustments and obtain accurate angle of
attack (AoA) measurements, precision instruments and automated systems can be utilized.
The 3D design software Fusion 360 was used to achieve the goal.
To start the process, two objects were imported into the program, with one representing
the 3D printed insert in the horizontal blade, and the other representing the AoA clip. The
next step was to rotate the AoA clip by ±4 degrees as needed for the experiment.
Once the rotation was done, the two objects were combined using a suitable tool that
allowed for joining them or leaving the intersection or cutting one based on the other. In
this case, the option chosen was to cut the clips based on the insert, while leaving both
objects intact after the operation.
Finally, any gaps or spaces between the objects were filled to ensure a seamless and
accurate representation of the experimental setup.

4.4.1 Import Dense Cloud
Before proceeding with the following steps, the dry blade data needs to be processed.
Import the dry blade data into the MATLAB script, rotate and scale it to align with the
original coordinate system, and then export it in the format [x, y, z]. These dry blade data
will be used as a reference for further alignment and scaling when importing other
experimental dense cloud data.
Next, import the experimental dense cloud data file, which has a similar format to the dry
blade data, containing the coordinates of all the points. Separate the dry blade data and
the dense cloud data of the icing blade into three vectors x, y, z. As these vectors may have
random placements, subtract each of them by their respective means to center all the
vectors. This subtraction ensures that all the vectors are centered properly.

4.4.2 PCA
In this step, Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is utilized to transform the original data
into a new coordinate system. The new axes, known as principal components, are
orthogonal to each other. The first principal component captures the direction of
maximum variance in the data, while the subsequent components represent orthogonal
directions of decreasing variance. By projecting the data onto this new low-dimensional
subspace defined by the principal components, PCA effectively reduces the complexity of
data representation.
This step is done by computing the covariance matrix of the input data and using the
eigenvalues and eigenvectors of this matrix to determine the direction and magnitude of
the principal components. Therefore, the angles between dense cloud coordinate and the
original coordinate can be determined.
4.4.3 Rotate Dense Cloud
After getting the angles between coordinates, rotation can be made. However, due to the
large size of the dense cloud data file, which contains more than 400,000 points and may
have noise caused by icing on the blades, manual adjustments are necessary to achieve
accurate alignment of the coordinate axes after rotation.

The aim of this step is to scale the dense cloud to the same size as the dry blade. As shown

4.4.5 Pcregistericp function
The last step is to use Pcregistericp function to complete the final alignment. The
Pcregistericp function is a powerful tool in the field of point cloud processing and
registration. Its main purpose is to align and register two or more point clouds to find the
best transformation that best aligns them together.
This function uses the ICP algorithm, which is an iterative process that iteratively
minimizes the distance between corresponding points in two point clouds. It works by
estimating a rigid transformation (translation and rotation) that minimizes the sum of
squared distances between corresponding points in the source and target point clouds.

5.4.1 Influence of TSR
For the experiments conducted using blades with a chord length of 100mm, three tests
were performed, each with a different tip speed ratio (TSR) value of 0, 1, and 3. The ice
shape diagrams obtained at the position of the blade’s center are presented in Figure 5.8.
The results clearly demonstrate the influence of the TSR on ice accretion patterns.
In the test with a TSR of 0, where the blade remains stationary, the ice formation at the
trailing edge is minimal. This is expected since the blade’s non-rotating state limits the
exposure of the trailing edge to the airflow and water droplets. Consequently, the absence
of rotation leads to reduced ice accretion at this particular location.
Fig. 5.8.: TSR = 0 vs TSR = 1 vs TSR = 3 for chord length = 10cm, y position at 0cm
However, as the tip speed ratio increases, a notable change in ice accumulation is
observed. With a TSR of 1, both the leading and trailing edges of the blade exhibit a
greater amount of ice formation. The rotation of the blade introduces rotational forces,
altering the airflow patterns and enabling the interaction between the blade’s surfaces and
water droplets. As a result, ice begins to accumulate at both the leading and trailing edges.
The most significant increase in ice accretion is observed in the test with a TSR of 3. At
this higher rotational speed, the centrifugal force becomes more pronounced, causing
water droplets to be thrown outward with greater momentum. Consequently, a larger
amount of ice accumulates at both the leading and trailing edges of the blade.

This phenomenon may be attributed to the reduced size of the airfoil, which has
implications for the aerodynamic forces acting on its surface, particularly the deflection
forces. Smaller airfoils are more susceptible to changes in airflow patterns and may
experience higher deflection forces, causing an increase in ice accumulation. In contrast,
larger airfoils tend to have more stable airflow, which may result in lower ice buildup.
Indeed, another possible explanation for the observed differences in ice accumulation
between the 80mm chord and 100mm chord blades is related to the reduction of the
blade chord length, which leads to a decrease in rotor solidity. Rotor solidity refers to the
ratio of the total blade area to the area swept by the rotor. In the case of a constant wind
speed and sprayed water mist, a smaller rotor solidity may result in a higher droplet
collision efficiency[12].
With a smaller rotor solidity, the spacing between adjacent blades is increased, creating
more open spaces for water droplets to collide with the airfoil surfaces. This increased

the airfoil surfaces, leading to a higher accumulation of ice.

potentially less ice accumulation.

6
Conclusion and Future Work
The purpose of this study is to investigate the effect of operating conditions on icing in a
vertical axis wind turbine (VAWT). Through detailed analysis and experiments, the study
aims to understand how different operating conditions affect the shape of ice on VAWT
blades.
In this project, the utilization of reused wind turbine models offered advantages in terms
of time and cost efficiency. However, it also presented certain challenges, particularly
concerning blade quality and component aging. Given the turbine’s prior use, the blades
may have experienced wear, potentially affecting their performance and structural
integrity. To address this, improvements in blade quality were necessary, which involved
either fabricating new blades or implementing treatments to mitigate wear.
Additionally, the aging of specific components, such as rotary stepper motors and belts,
introduced uncertainties during the experiment. Due to time constraints, temporary
measures were implemented during the project. Moving forward, addressing these
components becomes crucial to ensure smooth and reliable experimental operations.
The 3D scan method has proven to be highly automated, significantly saving valuable time
during the experimental process. However, it is crucial to emphasize there are still some
key steps needs to be done manually. The interval time of the camera must be
synchronized with the rotation angle and delay time of the spinning stepper motor, and
this coordination requires manual adjustment. Additionally, relying solely on autofocus
may not address all shooting challenges. When the blade reaches certain angles, manual
adjustments to the camera’s focus become necessary. To streamline the experiment and
minimize time wastage, it is crucial to practice and fine-tune these settings before
conducting the formal experiment. Moreover, Post-processing in Agisoft and MATLAB
highly depends on the picture quality. Therefore, taking pictures is the key part of the
whole project.
In this project, the Heat Plate method served as a classic approach to validate the accuracy
of the 3D scanning method. The experimental results demonstrated a strong correlation
between the two methods. However, certain factors, such as the ice condition and the
limited freezing time, contributed to the formation of relatively thin ice layers.
Consequently, this thin ice presented some challenges and resulted in minor errors when
55
==PAGE:64==
capturing the ice shape using the Heat Plate method. For future investigations, it is
recommended to explore variations in ice conditions or extend the freezing time to obtain
thicker ice layers. By adjusting the ice conditions or allowing for longer freezing periods
(e.g., 30 minutes), the resulting ice will be more substantial and distinct, leading to clearer
and more accurate comparison results between the Heat Plate and 3D scanning methods.
Post-processing in Agisoft can indeed be time-consuming and highly depends on the
configuration of PC. Under the condition that initial photos captured are of good quality
and well-suited for the 3D reconstruction process, post-processing in Agisoft becomes a
smooth and automated experience. Indeed, despite Agisoft’s powerful automatic features,
some level of manual intervention is often necessary, especially during the masking step,
to ensure the accuracy and quality of the 3D reconstruction. As each photo may have
variations in lighting conditions, exposure, and image quality, it is essential to carefully
examine and adjust the masks for each image.
The visualization results obtained from the experiments demonstrate that the chosen
operating conditions, such as Tip Speed Ratio (TSR), Angle of Attack (AoA), and chord
length, significantly influence the shape of the ice accretion on the wind turbine blades.
The project faced limitations in terms of equipment and time, resulting in the completion
of only 11 out of the initially planned 28 experiments. However, to enhance the
comprehensiveness and reliability of the study, future work can focus on conducting
additional experiments with varied operating conditions. One approach is to introduce
different parameters, such as the number of blades and freezing time, to investigate their
effects on ice accretion. This will provide a more comprehensive understanding of how
these factors influence ice formation on wind turbine blades. Moreover, performing
repeated experiments can help reduce uncertainty and increase the statistical significance
of the results. By repeating experiments under similar conditions, the consistency and
reliability of the findings can be improved.
Not using the cloud droplet probe to measure the water characteristics is indeed a
significant regret in this project. The cloud droplet probe is a valuable tool for studying
cloud microphysical properties, including droplet size distribution and liquid water
content. Its inclusion would have provided crucial data for understanding the behavior of
water droplets in the atmospheric conditions relevant to the wind turbine icing
phenomenon. While not having access to the cloud droplet probe is a limitation in this
project, it also presents an opportunity for future research. If possible, incorporating the
cloud droplet probe in subsequent studies could yield valuable insights into the complex
interactions between cloud microphysics and wind turbine icing.
