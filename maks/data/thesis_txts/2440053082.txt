Abstract
All of the standard turbulence models rely on the Kolmogorov theory, which has thor-
oughly been questioned in recent papers. The knowledge on turbulence can be improved
by directly testing the K41 theory, and its reconsiderations, collecting non-equilibrium
data on the largely underexplored unsteady ﬂows.
The presented project constitutes a valuable ﬁrst attempt on the high-resolution mea-
surement of a turbulent unsteady free jet, designed with the purpose of providing large
and slow scales, easier to be solved. The used measurement technique is a high-speed
Particle Image Velocimetry 2C2D system, which provides time-resolved data.
The obtained ﬂow resembles an instantaneous pulse more than an accelerating free jet.
Despite that, the data collected conﬁrmed that the Kolmogorov hypotheses do not
always hold true, as the gradients tendency witnessed that the ﬂow is not local isotropy
and the ﬂow scales show an opposite behavior of what is expected from the ’steady theory’.
Though, the second order structure functions reveal an almost null development in time,
indicating that the ﬂow small scales may have already reached the equilibrium, as assumed
by Kolmogorov.
Moreover, the equilibrium similarity theory for forced accelerating turbulence seems
to have found some validation concerning the time dependency of the variance.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
A ﬁrst look into the turbulence
Nowadays, turbulence remains an unsolved problem from both a physical and a mathe-
matical point of view [1]. It has been, indeed, addressed as the 19th century problem that
still immensely challenges researchers [2] and creates numerous disagreements within the
scientiﬁc community. The reason for this division comes from the very nature of turbu-
lence. Indeed it is a highly complex and chaotic phenomenon whose ﬂuid velocity ﬁeld’s
characteristic is to vary signiﬁcantly and irregularly in both space and time [3]. Conse-
quently, the hydrodynamic quantities are often treated as random variables and thus their
instantaneous value ui is written by using the Reynolds decomposition: ui = Ui + u′
i;
where u′
i is its ﬂuctuating part and Ui its mean. It follows a high non-linearity of the
equations that govern the motion of ﬂuids, the Navier-Stokes equations, which leads to a
system with 10 unknowns but only 4 equations. This impossibility of providing analytic
solutions without using strong assumptions and boundary conditions is known as the clo-
sure problem of turbulence.
It is however possible to solve the NS-equations numerically, utilizing the Direct Numerical
Simulation 1. which requires a grid resolution ﬁne enough to resolve down to the smallest
eddies, also known as the Kolmogorov microscales.

Nevertheless, the DNS has limited applications outside the research ﬁeld, due to the rapid
increase of the number of grid points with the Reynolds number [5].
The DNS constraints and the large cost of some experiments (e.g. the cost of a wind
tunnel) or their impossibility of reproduction in reality (e.g. nuclear accident) lead to the
need of using turbulence models in computational ﬂuid mechanics. The most popular are:
• the Eddy-viscosity model: 1st order closure model that supposes the Eddy viscosity
to be a function of the turbulent kinetic energy i.e. µt = f(k). The model is used
especially in aeronautics, since it is easy to integrate numerically. Unfortunately,
it is too simple to predict a wide range of ﬂows, as complex or 3D ﬂows [5]. For
instance, it overestimates the expansion rate by more than 40% in jet ﬂows as in [6];
• the Reynolds stress models: 2nd order closure models which are considered the most
complete classical turbulence modeling approach. As in the Eddy-viscosity models,
the Reynolds stress is expressed as the product of a turbulence viscosity and the
mean strain rate. Though, here, the turbulence viscosity is computed in terms of
the turbulence kinetic energy;
• the Larges Eddie Simulations (LES): is based on the idea of averaging the N.S.
equations over a small volume of space. It can resolve the energetic scales but not
the dissipative scales, however, it can be a reliable method for ﬁne grids but its cost
is as high as the DNS one [5].
In industry, the most utilized models are among the second group: the so-called turbulence-
energy two-equation models k −ϵ and k −ω. Where k, only for the model description, is
the speciﬁc turbulent kinetic energy i.e. the integral of E across wavenumbers, ϵ is the
rate of dissipation of turbulence kinetic energy per unit time and ω ∝ϵ/k is enstrophy 2.
Even though these two models certainly have some advantages, they are characterized
by several limitations. For instance, k −ω strongly overestimates the expansion rate of
both axisymmetric and plane jets and it is not applicable to external aerodynamics. The
classic k−ϵ cannot correctly reproduce complex ﬂows or always predict well the separation
points, it is not sensitive at all to the eﬀects due to the streamline curvatures, or solid
body rotation, and it is not valid in near-wall regions [5]. For this latter reason, the k −ω
is often used as a near-wall model associated to the k−ϵ model far from the wall. It seems
clear that there is a certain need of improving the turbulence models.
Turbulent ﬂows are widespread in nature and technology and play essential roles in a
variety of transport phenomena.
Indeed, turbulence is a cornerstone concept in opti-
mizing vital applications in industries such as injection of ﬂuids, heat-exchange or power
generation, and to understand and predict the evolution of the climate. Note that this
ﬂow propriety can be beneﬁcial and problematic at the same time. For instance, in air-
breathing combustion systems, turbulence is exploited for mixing reactants but it also
leads to noise and eﬃciency losses [7].
In conclusion, as the turbulence understanding is fundamental to core applications
in many areas and the existing turbulence models are inadequate for several real ﬂow
problems, the need of an updated understanding of the turbulence is becoming increasingly
obvious.

2.1.1
The classic Kolmogorov 1941 theory
As previously stated, the hydrodynamic quantities are random variables and the most
common way of studying the turbulence is by mean of statistical techniques. Probably
the most ingenious attempt to understand the statistics of turbulence was made, in the
1941, by the Soviet mathematician A.N. Kolmogorov who advanced a theory which on
which most of the 20th century knowledge on turbulence laid its foundations [2].
His theory rely on the assumption of analogy between turbulent eddies and molecules
in a thermodynamic equilibrium: the small scales would be eﬀectively decoupled from the
large scales, just as molecules in a thermodynamic system. The theory is valid only for
6
==PAGE:23==
suﬃciently high Reynolds number ﬂows2 and originally it was formally based on three
main hypotheses [3]:
• Hypothesis of local isotropy:
the small-scale turbulent motions (l << L) are statistically isotropic
• First similarity hypothesis:
the statistics of the small-scales motions (l < lEI) have a universal form that is
uniquely determined by ν and ϵ
• Second similarity hypothesis:
the statistics of the motions of scale l in the range L >> l >> ηk have a universal
form that is uniquely determined by ϵ and independent of ν
The scale dimensions are depicted, as a reference, in ﬁgure (2.1). With L as the charac-
teristic length scale of the ﬂow and ηk = (ν3/ϵ)1/4 as the Kolmogorov microscale, which
is the smallest scale in a turbulent ﬂow. Remember that the length [m] is inversely pro-
portional to the wavenumber [m−1], hence large eddies correspond to low wavenumbers
and vice versa. Note also that, during the years, many built upon and contributed to
Kolmogorov’s original work to construct what is known as the Classic Theory and "im-
properly"[8] referred as K41.
The Universal Equilibrium Range
With his hypotheses, Kolmogorov established that, for high Reynolds number, in the pro-
cess of turbulent energy transfer from small to large wavenumbers, there is a point beyond
which the turbulence will forget the inﬂuence of the initial large-scales motion. It there-
fore does exist a range, for wavenumbers suﬃciently higher than those characterizing the
energy-containing range, in which the character of turbulence will be the same in all ﬂows
[11]. Batchelor called this range Universal Equilibrium range, introducing the concept of
local equilibrium, only implied by Kolmogorov.
In the UER, ∂E/∂t ≈0 and hence ∂E/∂t is negligible relative to the non-linear transfer
and dissipation. The basic argument in support of this hypothesis is that the time scales
of the smallest eddies are so much smaller than the time scales of the energy-containing
eddies that the smallest scales are eﬀectively in statistical equilibrium. Emphasis has to
be put on the fact that this whole line of reasoning assumes the small eddies to act quite
independently of the large scales [12].
Neglecting the left-hand-side of equation 2.1, the local equilibrium hypothesis implies
there is no diﬀerence between any kind of turbulence at the small scales, hence the kinetic
energy is not function of time, but only of the wavenumber:

2.1.2
K41 reconsiderations
First of all, the Richardson - Kolmogorov cascade model is the mechanism for turbulence
dissipation that dominated both turbulence textbooks and research activity over the past
seven decades [9]. Many of the ideas that underlie the K41 have been accepted as facts
and still blindly trusted nowadays, even if the model does not describe accurately enough
the vast majority of actually occurring ﬂows. Moreover, the text books commonly report
only reviews of Kolmogorov’s hypotheses about the small scale motions, while very few
discuss the assumption of local equilibrium [8]. It has also to be considered that, back in
the 40’s, the knowledge about the details of what turbulence actually looked like was not
very broad. It was therefore reasonable to imagine small eddies acting independently of
the large ones. However, modern ﬂow visualization and DNS provided information about
coherent structures and showed the tendency of turbulence to appear as a nest of many
small vortical structures. [12]
It also seems to be usually forgotten, that the considered equations, since they are
ensemble-averaged, can have a time dependency e.g. in decaying, accelerating or homoge-
neous shear ﬂow turbulence. Indeed, in the case of a starting jet, the averaging would take
place over e.g. the same phase of the cycle, leading to a time dependent solution. Yet,
the time independent equation 2.3 is used by many models (e.g. from Onsager, Heisen-
berg etc...) to describe all turbulent ﬂow at high Reynolds number, steady or unsteady,
considering the non-homogeneous ones to be locally homogeneous.
It is therefore natural to ask why, after so many years (almost 80!), the hypothesis of
local equilibrium is still considered valid and strongly defended by many. The answer to
the question seems to lie on the fact that the classical theory has been validated by means
of statistically homogeneous stationary ﬂows, which are in statistical equilibrium not only
at the small scales, and considered anomalous the ﬂows for which the theory failed.
It has to be noticed that this section is a summary of George’s thoughts, and theories,
about the topic, mostly extrapolated by his paper [8].

The Equilibrium similarity theory
As stated before, a direct consequence of the existence of the Universal Equilibrium Range
is the so-called Kolmogorov similarity. Therefore, if there is a lack of validity in the local
equilibrium theory, also the universal similarity form of the spectrum fails. fKol(k+) can-
not be considered the same for all turbulent ﬂows anymore.
In the past years, researches developed a new theory in which the spectral energy equa-
tion’s self-preserving solutions are valid at all wavenumbers: the so-called Equilibrium
similarity theory. Diﬀerently than the classical K41, in the new approach the spectrum
and the nonlinear spectral transfer terms are not assumed a priori to scale with a single
length and velocity scale [15]. For instance, for the decay of homogeneous isotropic tur-
bulence, the decay rate is shown to be of power-law form, and to depend on the initial
conditions so that the decay rate constants cannot be universal except possibly in the limit
of inﬁnite Reynolds number.
The Equilibrium similarity provided solutions which have been conﬁrmed by experi-
ment for a number of time-evolving turbulent ﬂows and for boundary free developing shear
ﬂows [16]. The spectral equation of all the time-dependent ﬂows collapses at any scales of
motion, with a single length scale proportional to the Taylor microscale. But, since all the
terms that can be included in the averaged equations are in mutual balance, or in equilib-
rium, each ﬂow requires a diﬀerent solution depending on the ﬂow setting. Such solutions

3.1
Turbulent jet
The theory of the following sections concerns steady turbulent jets, hence it was expected
the possibility that the results obtained from the actual experiment may diﬀer.
The
reasons why the calculations for the unsteady jet are based on a ’steady theory’ are due
to both the fact that, according to the K41, the turbulence at the small scales, at high
Re, is considered to be universal and the fact that the unsteady ﬂows seems to be largely
underexplored and rarely mentioned in study texts.
Indeed, if the study presented in
this thesis aims to understand how the accelerating jet develops, its behavior cannot be
predicted by a theory that has not been validate yet.
3.1.1
Round turbulent free jet characteristics
The axisymmetric free jet is considered a benchmark for the research into the physics
of turbulent ﬂuid ﬂow [14].
Indeed, it is not only the ﬁrst ﬂow for which the theory
of Kolmogorov was validated by showing the -5/3 range in the power spectrum, but it
also satisﬁes the k−5/3 law even at low Reynolds number. In addition, the turbulent jet
evolves rapidly enough to be practical on laboratory scales and it is easily accessible from
the optical devices during the measurements. It can therefore be considered the ideal ﬂow
to investigate the K41 and its re-visitation.
The classical turbulence experiments have to be carried out using Newtonian ﬂuids,
usually water or air. Even though the kinematic viscosity of the former is 10 times lower
than the one of the latter (νwater = 1.0035 · 10−6 while νair = 15.111 · 10−6), allowing
therefore experiments in water to reach higher Reynolds number at equal geometrical and
velocity conditions, air has been chosen as the ﬂuid for this experiment.
The reasons
behind this choice is mainly due to the large amount of space required by the experiment,
in order not to conﬁne the jet. As it will be further explained in section 3.1.4, if the
room, or aquarium in case of water, is too small, the ﬂow can be subjected to undesired
eﬀect such as further acceleration and back ﬂows. Clearly, the aquarium’s high cost and
practicability make the use of air more convenient.

As ′free′ implies, the ﬂow is remote from walls, and the turbulence arises because of
mean-velocity diﬀerences [3]. Therefore, the following concepts have been of particular
importance for the set-up positioning and building the enclosure around the set-up, which
is needed for preventing the seeding to start the ﬁre alarm on the ceiling and, especially,
to ensure a right and uniform density all over the volume.
Two predominant free jet phenomena are the momentum conservation and the increase
of the mass ﬂux along the x-axis. From the boundary-layer approximation, it is known
that there is a zero pressure gradient impressed upon the jet ﬂow, therefore, no net force
acts on the surfaces of the control volume [7]. This leads to the fact that the momen-
tum ﬂux across a cross-section at any position x must be constant and hence equal to
the rate at which it is being added at the jet exit. The return ﬂow contains instead a
negative momentum, which ’removes’ momentum from the main jet momentum, causing
then a violation of the momentum conservation [14]. Consequently, in order to recreate
accurately a free ﬂow and ensure the presence of any signiﬁcant return ﬂow along x1, the

distance between the jet and the wall must be large enough. The dimension for avoiding
any negative momentum in the main ﬂow direction x1 has been inferred from the ﬂow
visualization.

Indeed, if the jet is compressed along x2 or x3, the jet is accelerated and the dissipation

3.2
Particle Image Velocimetry
In this section a summary of the necessary basic knowledge on PIV is provided. Note
that [31] has been used as main source for both the ﬂow measurement technique and the
evaluation and post-process of the data.
Particle image velocimetry is an optical technique which records the position over time,
i.e. the displacement, of small tracer particles that have been introduced into the ﬂow, in
order to acquire the instantaneous ﬂuid velocity in a cross-section of the ﬂow [32]. PIV
is the newest entrant to the ﬁeld of non-intrusive ﬂuid ﬂow measurement and it possesses
the unique feature of recording images of large parts of ﬂow ﬁelds. The latter character-
istic is in contrast with the standard laser anemometric technique such as laser-Doppler
anemometry (LDA), or the intrusive Hot-wire, since their measurement is limited to a rel-
atively small region, a ‘point’. However, the advantage of this broad spatial resolution is
counterbalanced by the temporal resolution, i.e. the frame rate of recording PIV images,
which is limited due to the cameras’ current technological restrictions.
3.2.1
Principle of operation
The principle of the PIV measurement technique relies on the physical deﬁnition of ve-
locity as a diﬀerential quotient. The ﬂuid elements velocity are indirectly measured by
illuminating a plane in the ﬂow with two very short light pulses within a time diﬀerence
(∆t) and then capturing with a CCD- or CMOS-camera the light scattered by the small
tracer particles advected by the ﬂow itself [33].
• The time delay between pulses must be chosen with respect to the ﬂow velocity and
the desired magniﬁcation.
• The particles have usually been previously added to the ﬂow. Material and dimen-
sions are carefully selected and checked for each type of experiment.
Indeed, the particles have to be small enough to strictly follow the motion of the ﬂuid
elements, avoiding velocity lag, but, in contradiction, they have to be big enough to
have a better light scattering eﬃciency.
• The light scattered by the tracer particles is recorded by means of a high quality
lens on two separate frames of a cross-correlation digital camera. Then the output
of the camera sensor is transferred to a computer.
• For the evaluation the PIV recording is divided in small subareas called ’interrogation
areas’. The local displacement vector for the images of the tracer particles of the ﬁrst
and second illumination is determined for each interrogation area using a statistical
methods called cross-correlation.
The projection of the vector of the local ﬂow
velocity into the plane of the light sheet (e.g.

a 2C-2D PIV) is computed taking into account the time delay between the two
illuminations and the calibration of the imaging system.

High-Speed PIV technique
The gap between the measurements oﬀered by precise but single-point techniques, as HWA
and LDA, and by the coarser but global measurements provided by the classic PIV can
be ﬁlled up with a new technique called High speed PIV [36].
Usually, PIV cannot provide a spatially resolved instantaneous map of the investigated
ﬂow, but that can be possible by means of this high speed technique. The combination
of a large image number and a high frame rate is possible by using a high-speed camera
with suﬃciently large memory, coupled with a laser light source that can operate at cor-
respondingly high frame rates, and reducing the ﬁeld of view to a narrow strip. These
characteristics allow to capture continuous time traces of velocity data, from which both
statistical quantities and spectra can be computed. Moreover, instantaneous image cap-
ture and high spatial resolution of PIV allow the detection of spatial structures even in
unsteady ﬂow ﬁelds, as the one considered in this thesis.
3.2.2
Experimental PIV system
Seeding system
The aim for all PIV measurements is to have a maximum number of particle images equally
distributed over the whole area of interest. It has to be noted that having a high particle
images density leads to problems on identifying each particle separately. Therefore, areas
with too many particles will lead to the same gaps in the evaluated velocity ﬁeld as areas
with an insuﬃcient number of particle. The strobe illumination typically used for PIV
emphasizes potential inhomogeneities in seeding density and therefore increases the eﬀort
required for an adequate particle supply. [31]

The aerosol generator consists of a closed cylindrical container with two air inlets and one
aerosol outlet. It works on the principle of the Laskin nozzle and it is designed to work

From the aerosol generator, the particles pass trough another container where, in order to

the measurements will be biased towards whichever part of the ﬂow is seeded. Due to the
gravity, the seeding has the tendency of gathering towards the ﬂoor, leading therefore to
an inhomogeneous seeding distribution inside the enclosure. In order to avoid it, a fan has
been placed behind the seeding probe. The fan runs only while the jet is not operating.
Image acquisition system: High-speed camera
The high speed camera used in the experiment is a IX-Cameras model iSpeed-726 which
can be controlled by a computer by mean of i-SPEED software. The camera has a capacity
of 26 [Gpixel/s] and 96 GB of on-board memory for long recordings. It is equipped with a
high-resolution 2048x1536 pixel CMOS sensor. Each squared shape pixel has a dimension
of 13.5[µm] x 13.5[µm], for a sensor size of 27.972[mm] x 20.736[mm]. The CMOS sensor
is an active pixel architecture, which allows to choose the number of pixel to be active by
the deﬁnition of a sub-domain, the so-called region of interest ROI. This allows to come
to higher framing rates, exchanging though the image resolution. It is also provided with
a ﬂexible frame synchronization from 10 to 200 [kHz], which triggers the laser when the
former is in "master sync mode".
The high-speed camera has been placed perpendicular to the measurement plane illumi-
nated by the laser sheet. Moreover, the camera is equipped with a Nikon micro-nikkor
200/4 lens, which means the lens has a focal distance f = 200 [mm] and a f-number
f# = 4. For all measurement locations its working distance (lens-laser) has been kept
equal to 370[mm], resulting in a magniﬁcations factor of M = 0.70, while the total distance
laser-camera was 358 [mm]. Note that both distances are bigger than the jet spreading at
the measurements point i.e. R(30D) = 317 [mm], hence the camera does not disturb the
ﬂow.

3.3
Set-up automation
The will to guarantee high repeatability and reproducibility, along with the diﬃculty of
accessing the various set-up components, due to the tend full of seeding, created the
necessity of having a set-up as automatized as possible.
3.3.1
Piston driving system
Initially, the jet was supposed to be completely driven by a pneumatic system. The use
of compressed air seemed a smart, easy and aﬀordable way to run the piston at diﬀerent
conditions. Indeed, just placing adjustable pressure regulators, before the cylinder, allows
to obtain the air compressed at diﬀerent pressures and, consequently, to run the piston
at several velocities and accelerations. Since the Institute of Propulsion Technology labo-
ratory has an available air compressed distribution, before any reduction, of 12[bar], the
range of possible accelerations would have therefore been quite broad. The piston has
therefore been mounted on a DNC-32-500-PPV-A Festo cylinder, which is air compressed
driven. With 500[mm] of rod, the cylinder allows to exploit the whole pipe length, hence
the whole jet volume. A solenoid valve 5/2 way was supposed to change conﬁguration,
hence to let the air pass in one way or the other, as it detects the electric signal from the
computer. Within this system, in one conﬁguration the compress air would ﬂow from the
back of the cylinder, pushing the piston towards the nozzle, while in the second conﬁgura-
tion, the compress air would ﬂow from the front of the cylinder, bringing back the piston
to the initial position.
Figure 3.16: 5/2 valve schematic
Unfortunately, the system tests resulted in the impossibility to have a uniform acceleration.
Indeed, as it is shown in Figure 3.16, the compressed air inside the Festo cylinder creates an
air ’pillow’, which acts as a suspension and decelerates the piston movement. Consequently,
it has been decided to use the pneumatic system only to bring the piston back to the initial
position and hold it there until, thanks to the trigger that opens the 5/2 valve, a gravity
driven system pushes the ﬂow outside the jet. The new system is based on the simple
concept of creating a force by mean of a weight and a pulleys system. A schematic of it
can be seen in Figure 3.20.
However, even with the weight system, the piston could not run as smooth as desired
at the required velocities. The reason seemed to be the material on which the jet’s pipe
has been constructed, silicone, which has malleable proprieties. That makes it easier to be
worked, but also make it easily deformable. Indeed, the pipe’s cross-section resulted to be
neither perfectly circular, nor perfectly constant along the pipe length. Consequently, the
31
==PAGE:48==
force, created by the weight, needed to be strong enough to win against the friction forces
created by this non-uniformity. This led to the impossibility of running the experiment
with the maximum Reynolds number of 6500.
A draw-wire displacement sensor, which is a low-cost solution simple to evaluate, has
been coupled to the piston to ensure the success of the experiment, since it is important
to track the exact position of the piston as the time passes. This linear measuring encoder
produced by Kübler converts linear movements by extending and retracting a steel cable
into rotational motions and provides an analogue signal in Volt as output. The draw-wire
data, acquired with a sample rate of ≈500 [Hz] and manipulated by a simple Python
script, veriﬁed that the piston motion was accurately reproducible and allowed to ﬁnd the
nozzle exit velocity inserting the maximum piston velocity into the Bernoulli equation.
The calculation has been veriﬁed it with the acquisition of 15 runs at the nozzle exit with
the PIV system. At the minimum allowed weight, the maximum nozzle exit velocity has
been found to be Uj ≈7 [m/s], giving an expected Reynolds numbers Re ≈30000, 4.6
times higher than the Re for which the experiment has been designed. The piston velocity
tendency in time is given in Figure 3.18 and indicates that the piston reaches a maximum
velocity of about 0.7 [m/s] towards the end of the stroke.

for 4/5 times without data acquisition, in order to check that everything works smoothly
and to avoid diﬀerences in the piston movement.

The camera has been previously set on Master sync mode which couples its frequency

memory: since lower frequencies correspond to less images and, consequently, to more

move too fast to be acquired in two following pictures. Therefore, for the downstream
measurement points, the frequency has been set to a value for which both the correlation
and the tools’ performance are considered acceptable, i.e. 10 [kHz], while at the nozzle,
where the velocity is higher, the frequency has been set to 20 [kHz].

3.4.3
Image evaluation
Once that the images are downloaded from the camera, they are evaluated by mean of a
conventional processing program called PIVview2C, written by C. Willert, with the feature
of a coarse-to-ﬁne resolution pyramid with intermediate image deformation. The program
also convert the camera reference system to the conventional one. Indeed, the camera
has x (u) along the radial and y (v) as stream-wise component. However, thanks to the
program, several parameters for the evaluation and the cross-correlation can be easily set.
For evaluating the PIV data, the multi-grid interrogation method has been chosen, which
sets the initial interrogation window to 128x128[pixels2] and then gradually reduces it
until obtaining a 16x16[pixels2] ﬁnal sampling windows.
Note that, after having seen
the low reliability of the data evaluated with such parameter, through computing their
spectra, the ﬁnal interrogation window has been set as twice the previous one (32 x 32
[pixels2]), as viscous scales appeared much larger than expected from the calculations,
especially at 30d. Moreover, always in order to improve the signal to noise ratio (SNR),
an image-stride of 2 has been used instead of 1, as the displacements are very low.
At M=19.32 [µm/pixel], the square region on which each vector is calculated has a size
of 309x309[µm2] (or 618x618[µm2] for the re-processed data). The processing code com-
putes the cross-correlation between the interrogation windows of two consecutive images
and calculates the displacement of the entire group of particles inside the considered in-
terrogation window. Note that the image evaluation also included the subtraction of the
mean image intensity to obtain a uniform background intensity and the intensity clipping
to make particles similar in intensity. Finally, the time delay between the two consecutive
images allows the program to determine the velocities. Note that the amount of seeding
plays an important role on the success of the correlation. Indeed, the program needs to
still be able to distinguish between the particles albeit a high number of them is wanted
for optimum results. In order to reduce the uncertainty, even though it increases the cal-
culation time, when one interrogation window has been completely calculated, the window
is shifted overlapping of 50% the previous one. With an overlap of 50% the sample step
corresponds to 8 [pixel], i.e. 156 [µm].
The quality of the correlation between images can be seen from the displayed vector ﬁeld.
For instance in Figure 3.24 a failed correlation after a measurement at 5 [kHz] is reported.

show slightly lower values at the centerline than at the half-width for x=20d, and the
opposite for x=30d. The diﬀerence is more evident as the velocity gets higher.
Moreover, diﬀerent questions rise from these results:
• Does the previously mentioned time diﬀerence between consecutive runs cause more
variation in the statistics, or can it be considered random and uncorrelated such that
its contribution cancels out in the statistics to a certain degree?
• If the ﬂow delay is irrelevant, would the ’large’ rms value be only due to the high
turbulent nature of the ﬂow or also widely connected to its unsteadiness? Usually,
in a stationary process, the mean velocity is constant, so one can average also across
the time. Instead, in such a highly accelerating pulse, the time averaging can be
done only between too few data.

4.5
Spatial structure function
The structure functions, found converting the time into space exploiting Taylor frozen
hypothesis, are shown in Figures 4.37 and 4.38. The goal of having approximately the
same velocity across the time interval, in order to be able to consider the ﬂow locally
steady, had to be dropped. Indeed, the low velocity, if multiplied for a small interval
of time, provided a spatial record lower than the dimension of the expected macroscales.
Consequently, the time interval corresponding to the decay (0.5 - 2 [s]) has been divided in
six sub intervals of 0.25[s] each. With such a large time range, the ﬂow can be considered
neither steady nor homogeneous. Therefore, the structure functions (and thus the spectra
since they are both based on the same theory) may not provide a true physical meaning,
but only a direct indication of the dimension of the scales and dissipation taking part in
the ﬂow decay.
Second order
The second order spatial structure functions are shown in the logarithmic plot of Figure
4.37, which includes the four downstream positions for two time intervals.
Figure 4.37: Second order structure function for both x=20d and x=30d
All the curves approximating the 2/3 slope, even if with some deviation, may indicate an
ongoing development of turbulent scales, as it is observed in the non-equilibrium region
of the companion steady jet of [24].
Most of the turbulent kinetic energy is dominated by the large scales. The kinetic energy
proves to be higher at the position closer to the nozzle exit, and lower at the half-width
respect to the centerline.
Contrary to what the ’steady theory’ states, and to what is seen in [24], the large scales
seem to be bigger at 20d rather than at 30d. This behavior is particularly remarkable
if the half-width curves are compared: the purple one, which stands for 30d, reaches a
plateau around a displacement of 0.03 [m], while the yellow one (20d) reaches it around
0.06 [m]. Moreover, the scales are supposed to be of the same dimension along the radius
of a certain downstream position. Instead here, as for λT , the large scales possess the
same length at 20d, while at 30d the half-width large scales result to be smaller than the
centerline ones. The same incongruity with this aspect of the classical theory has also
65
==PAGE:82==
been observed with the Taylor microscales.
Since these trends go against the one expected in a steady free jet, the author believes
that a mapping of this unsteady free jet, consisting of more measurement points along
both x1 and x2, is required before drawing proper conclusions.
Moreover, particularly interesting is the fact that there is almost no diﬀerence between
the second order structures function of diﬀerent time phases at each measurement point.
Indeed, the structure function tendencies all collapse into the same curve, revealing that
the ﬂow small scales may have been already equalized, as assumed by Kolmogorov. This
could indicate that the acceleration is too small to push the ﬂow out of the equilibrium
and, consequently, that the ﬂow utilized in this project may not be a non-equilibrium jet.
Notice that the almost null development in the structures has been also seen in another
experiment conducted previously by the author of this thesis and part of the DTU TLR
group, where a ﬂap has been used to inject a single large-scale Fourier component into a
free steady jet ﬂow [44]. In that experiment, all spectral peaks are indeed seen from the

Chapter 5
Conclusion
5.1
Summary
First of all, it has to be noted that both the jet cylinder and the piston driving system
aﬀected the direction of the project in a relevant way. The utilized piston driving system
resulted to be not suitable to control, as desired, neither the velocity nor the acceleration.
This produced a mass ﬂow rate that resulted to be too elevate, making the ﬂow an instan-
taneous pulse instead an accelerating free jet. The use of a lower Reynolds number ﬂow
would have reduced the rate and better served the project purposes.
These issues, along with a not high enough spatial resolution, led to the impossibility of
measuring the terms in the dissipation transport equation, and hence to directly measure
the non-equilibrium status of the ﬂow (equation 2.5).
Furthermore, it is unknown what is the extent of the impact that the temporal diﬀer-
ence between run, due to the tendency of the jet ﬂow to deviate from a straight path, had
on the data statistics, and consequently on the overall results.
Despite everything, the data collected seems to conﬁrm that the Kolmogorov hypothe-
ses do not always hold true.
Indeed, the equilibrium similarity theory for forced accelerating turbulence seems to
have found some validation concerning the time dependency of the variance in both the
fully developed and the developing regions.
The gradients tendency clearly witnessed that the hypothesis of local isotropy does not
hold true, and hence the turbulence models rely on a non-accurate deﬁnition of dissipation,
underestimating or overestimating it depending on the case under analysis.
Of great interest is the curious behavior showed by both the tendency of the Taylor
microscale dimension and the second order structure function, which is the opposite of
what is expected from the ’steady theory’ of the free jet. The scales’ size seems to become
smaller as the ﬂow goes downstream, and gets non-constant, though it increases, along
the radius.
Since these trends go against the general knowledge of the steady free jet, the acqui-
sition of data at more measurement points both along x1 and x2 of this unsteady free jet
is required before drawing any legitimate inferences. A detailed mapping of the unsteady
jet region could provide further useful knowledge at the reconsideration theory about the
local equilibrium.
On the contrary, the fact that there is no diﬀerence between the second order structure
functions of diﬀerent time phases at each measurement point, i.e. the tendencies all col-
lapse into the same curve, seems to reveal that the ﬂow small scales have already reached
the equilibrium, as assumed by Kolmogorov. This could indicate that the acceleration
is too small to push the ﬂow out of the equilibrium. Therefore, the ﬂow utilized in this
71
==PAGE:88==
project may not be a non-equilibrium jet.
In conclusion, even though the data presented are not suﬃcient to claim a proper val-
idation of the reconsideration-theory, the project constitutes a valuable ﬁrst attempt on
the high-resolution measurement of a turbulent unsteady free jet. The experience earned
during these months, about jet design, measurement technique and ﬂow behavior, will
well serve as a starting point for further experiments.
As the author believes that the research on unsteady ﬂows should be pursued, below
are listed some ideas and suggestions, that came up along the progression of this thesis,
that may be useful for future projects.
5.2
Future works
As stated before, the piston driving system, and hence the ﬂow acceleration control, plays
a crucial role. It would be wise to purchase a system that can easily be programmed to run
at diﬀerent conditions. An interesting idea would be to buy a servo-motor, which would
move the piston in a smoother way and at the adequate velocity and acceleration. The
possibility of establishing a higher acceleration would also help to ensure a non-equilibrium
behavior of the ﬂow. Moreover, a longer cylinder length could be employed in order to
extend the ﬂow velocity increasing ramp in time and hence turn the ’short puﬀ’ into a
proper jet.
One of the initial goals of this dissertation was to resolve the 99% of the dissipation
of the dissipation, and hence to measure each term of the unreduced dissipation trans-
port equation. Unfortunately, the spatial resolution of the collected data did not allow
its achievement, leaving it as an unresolved and challenging task. The author personally
ﬁnds the topic quite interesting and hopes that it will be analyzed soon. That is why, it
would be important to design a jet with a bigger nozzle, in order to scale up more the
eddies.
Time and energy should be employed to ﬁnd a way to make the ﬂow path less aleatory.
A starting jet immersed a big water tunnel would get less inclined to change, as it
cannot be deviated by any external conditions. On the other hand however, the experiment
would become more expensive and with a high risk of water inﬁltration that would damage
the measurement system.
Another option could be to stop a continuous steady jet by decelerating it. The jet
would be run at several Reynolds number and then stopped using with diﬀerent negative
accelerations.
Lastly, the velocity derivatives could be computed utilizing the circulation method to
reduce the noise.
