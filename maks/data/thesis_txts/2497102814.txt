Abstract
In the course of the last decade, many achievements in the ﬁeld of satellite altimetry
have been made. Certainly, it is a higher resolution of Synthetic Aperture Radar
(SAR) altimeter over pulse-limited altimeters, greater global coverage and increased
temporal resolution of measurements over the Arctic region. The latter was ac-
complished by CryoSat-2 with a ﬁrst SAR altimeter on-board along with Sentinel-3
mission carrying SAR altimeter (SRAL) as well.
Due to rapid weather changes in the Arctic region, it is of utter importance to
measure rivers’ surface height (SH) with suﬃcient periodicity. Hence, the purpose
of this thesis is to identify best performing retracking method for both missions,
limit detrimental noise and combine observations from both missions in order to
produce time series of rivers’ SH imitating their real behavior.
The study presented here is divided into three main parts. The ﬁrst deals with
the handling of raw data and processing steps needed for further incorporation of
the seasonal ﬁlter which is the main focus of the second part. The third part concen-
trates on the actual freshwater ﬂux calculation based on the rating curve approach.
Waveforms retrieved by satellites, when measuring arctic rivers’ height, have diﬀer-
ent shapes and peakiness inﬂuenced by the topography and diﬀerent water bodies
along the riverbanks’ vicinity. Arctic rivers are very speciﬁc due to the presence of
ice and snow along their course. Hence, the right retracker method must be chosen.
Primary Peak Threshold PPT 80% retracker, identifying the bin where the ﬁrst peak
exceeding 80% of the maximum power, performed the best in case of Cryosat-2 data
while the same holds true for the simple threshold retracker in Sentinel-3 dataset.
There are several important reasons to keep only a particular percentile of SH ob-
servations and to discard the rest. It is an inherent part of the seasonal ﬁlter which
preserves the peaks during spring thawing and ﬂood periods while reduces noise to
an acceptable rate. This seasonal ﬁlter was used for six arctic rivers diﬀering in size,
shape and orientation and managed to produce time series which were validated in
cases where in-situ data were available.
Based on the produced time series and in-situ discharge measurements, the an-
nual river discharge was estimated for the Yenisei River during 2011-2018 and the
Mackenzie River during 2011-2017.
Results are compared with observed annual
values.

Preface
Not only this master thesis underlines the end of my studies, but it serves also as an
instruction when trying to calculate or validate the river ﬂux in a hostile environ-
ment such as the Arctic region. The Arctic region is a gateway to the development
of the global climate. The present climatic situation forces us to look more deeply
into the thermohaline circulation processes which are driven by freshwater ﬂuxes and
surface heat. This and much more causes the need for more information and knowl-
edge in this respect. The development of the so-called seasonal ﬁltering method and
other noise removing steps contributes to reaching this aim and can be used as the
primary guidelines.
The master thesis topic emerged and was conducted at DTU Space which has more
than 50 years of experience with space sciences. DTU’s participation in more than
100 space missions gave the essential solid foundation when considering where this
project should be carried out.
I would like to express my gratitude to Louise Sandberg Sørensen for her sup-
port and guidance throughout the entire project development. Thank you for being
open-minded and bringing new ideas during our weekly meetings which made them
enjoyable while still respecting the aim of this thesis. I highly respect your immense
experience from polar regions and your positive attitude when coming across some-
thing new. My gratitude stretches further to the researcher Karina Nielsen. Her vast
knowledge of multi-mission altimetry, potentials and limitations of the instruments
and general expertise in this regard are invaluable. Thank you for being so kind and
keeping an eye on details during our mutual work. Your feedbacks had a big impact
on this project.
The never-ending support of my girlfriend kept my spirit high and made this work

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Freshwater as a carrier of energy in form of heat, nutrients and various chemical
substances play a major role in support of life around the entire globe. The role of
freshwater has an even more profound eﬀect in the Arctic region than anywhere else.
It is a place where 10% of global river freshwater ﬂux is aggregated and released
into the Arctic Ocean in which these rivers mouth. It is in sharp contrast with the
Arctic Ocean itself, representing only 3% of the globe’s surface [1] [2].
Global warming is giving rise to more evaporation from soil and vegetation surfaces
and transpiration (evapotranspiration), moving the moisture from lower latitudes
towards the pole, which in turn leads to more precipitation in the form of either
snow or rain. This cycle just promotes the idea that the Arctic region experiences
a continuous increase in the volume of the Arctic’s freshwater system [3].
One of the key eﬀects of mixing saltwater with freshwater is so-called stratiﬁca-
tion that makes less dense and less saline water sit on top of denser seawater [4].
The less saline water is prone to freeze and produce ice which increases albedo and
therefore reinforces Earth’s cooling. Another result is the change of the Northern
hemisphere’s weather system related to freshwater inﬂux, jet streams and storm
tracks shifts and it can not be omitted that such special territory as the Arctic is
home to multiple migratory and non-migratory species.
Given that the Arctic region is not isolated but linked to the global thermohaline
circulation it poses a great value in understanding this water system and changes in
freshwater ﬂuxes.
This thesis is intended to quantify freshwater ﬂuxes of the Arctic Ocean tributary
rivers by using satellite altimetry data, compare results with measurements in-situ
where possible and to identify the limitations of altimetry data usage.

2.1
Satellite altimetry principles
Multiple dictionaries deﬁne altimetry in comparatively close fashion. As the name
implies, altimetry is a science of measuring altitudes. In the case of satellite al-
timetry, it is the distance D between the satellite and the surface. The instrument
securing these measurements is called an altimeter. The distance in question is de-
noted as a range.
To measure a range, an altimeter emits a short microwave pulse that propagates
through the atmosphere, interacts with the surface and part of the incident pulse is
reﬂected back towards the altimeter. In essence, a range is calculated based on the
precisely measured time t which is the time it took one pulse to travel round trip
between the satellite and the surface. Emitted pulses travel at the speed-of-light
velocity. A general overview of satellite altimetry is portrayed in Figure 2.1.
To determine sea surface height (SSH) it is also vital to know the satellite altitude H.
It is accomplished by the so-called Precise Orbit Determination (POD). POD takes
into account satellite’s dynamics where two main systems are employed, namely
Doppler Orbitography and Radiopositioning Integrated by Satellite (DORIS) and
Global Positioning System (GPS) (see ﬁgure 2.1 on page 6).
Radial orbit performance accomplished the precision of 1cm root mean square RMS
since the year 2003 [20], referenced to an Earth ellipsoid. Having altitude (H ) and
distance (D) it is possible to calculate sea surface height (SSH ) based on the equa-
tion 2.1:

2.1.1
Atmospheric corrections
In order to estimate range accurately, it is needed to account for the physical prop-
erties and composition of the atmosphere. Factors such as temperature, pressure,
water vapor (WV) density, cloud liquid water density, and ionospheric electron den-
sity [22] degrade the range measurements, hence three sets of corrections ought to
be addressed:
1. Ionospheric correction.
2. Dry tropospheric correction.
3. Wet tropospheric correction.
2.1.1.1
Ionospheric correction
Region of negatively charged electrons (ionized plasma) extends from 50km to
1000km above the earth’s surface, creating the ﬁrst medium through which the
signal travels from the satellite. As this medium is dispersive for the satellite echoes
it gives rise to a path delay (PD).
Given that the state of the ionosphere is mostly sun-dependent, the PD varies from
several cms up to 30 cm depending on solar activity such as day, night, solar maxima
or ionospheric storm. Ionospheric path delay depends on the frequency and so by
employing dual-frequency measurements one can determine the correction.
Using Doppler transmitters in order to establish the total electron content (TEC)
below the satellite is documented in Leitinger et al. [23] [24] while Fleury et al. [25]
showed its feasibility to estimate the ionospheric correction by Doppler Orbitogra-
phy and Radiopositioning Integrated by Satellite (DORIS) system.
2.1.1.2
Dry tropospheric correction
By far the largest contributing correction applied to the signal, dry tropospheric
correction is caused by dry gasses (dinitrogen N2, oxygen O2, argon Ar, etc.) dis-
sipated within the Earth’s troposphere. Its eﬀect causes the height discrepancy of
approximately 2.3m. Owing to the fact that the gases’ redistribution is almost con-
stant (change of less than 1% in a few hours [26]), it can be well modeled by global
atmospheric models like the European Center for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts
(ECMWF) [27].
2.1.1.3
Wet tropospheric correction
The wet tropospheric correction is responsible for approximately 25cm diﬀerence in
range measurements. Mainly due to cloud liquid water droplets and water content.
Since it is weather dependent, the path delay varies with seasons mostly between
20°-40°N less so between 20°-40°S. It has a minimum of 8 cm and a maximum of
15 cm above 40°N and only around 2cm above 40°S. It can be noticed, that this
component varies signiﬁcantly in space and is therefore arduous to model or predict.

2.1.2
Tidal correction
Since the thesis deals with surface height over the rivers which mouth into the Arc-
tic Ocean, it is necessary to describe the tidal correction. Tides are governed by
celestial bodies (the Sun and the Moon) and Earth’s structure which are perceived
as noise in altimetry signals.
The weight of solid tide forces the Earth’s crust to deform. Such vertical deforma-
tion varies spatially 20cm on average. Preliminary Reference Earth Model (PREM)
developed by Dziewonski and Anderson [28] proved that this solid tide can be well
modeled and stored in Geophysical Data Records (GDRs).
Another type of tides is the polar tide caused by the ﬂatness of the Earth. However,
the polar tide varies signiﬁcantly less than the solid tide, only by a few centimeters
[20]. This variation is recorded in GDRs as well. Variation of the polar tide ranges
from 2 to 3 cm [22].
2.2
Radar altimetry methods
As a major variable aﬀecting climate [29] [30], sea surface height (SSH) estimates
receive a lot of attention from the scientiﬁc community around the globe. In order to
acquire information about SSH, satellite altimetry was developed and went through
a tremendous evolution. Nevertheless, not all of the worlds’ regions are mapped
adequately. The Arctic Ocean is an example. Its coverage is nowadays far from op-
timal due to favored lower satellites’ orbit inclination and poor temporal resolution
in satellites where inclination suﬃces.
During the last 16 years, several satellite altimetry missions have been launched
with a purpose to circumvent some of the most troublesome obstacles in mapping
the Arctic region. These missions are ICESat, CryoSat-2, Sentinel-3A, Sentinel-3B
and SARAL/AltiKa satellite. Data from each instrument can be attained in a dif-
ferent mode. The Low Resolution Mode (LRM), Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR)
and SAR Interferometric mode (SARIn) are described in subsection 2.2.1,2.2.2 and
2.6 respectively. Basically, the satellite altimetry fundamentals remain the same for
all three methods however the acquisition of a range measurements diﬀer from mode
to mode.
2.2.1
Low Resolution Mode (LRM)
The most fundamental distinction between the above-mentioned modes is the size of
a footprint. While in the case of general the low-resolution mode is approximately
100km2, SAR and SARIn modes oﬀer a much higher resolution of roughly 4km2
(between 2 and 7 km depending on the signiﬁcant wave height).
Given that the footprint of the LRM is in order of tens of square kilometers, an
object keeps being intercepted through longer satellite crossing not just while the
satellite is at the zenith of this object. The LRM utilizes the so-called pulse limited
altimetry at a frequency within the 1-2kHz range for echo decorrelation.
Thanks to the large footprint, the LRM mode works best over the open ocean
(300km oﬀshore) and above the ice sheet interiors where the surface is more homo-

3.1
Waveforms
Each echo governed by PRF reﬂected back towards the altimeter exhibits distinct
properties and eventually shape which is called a waveform.
The shape of the
waveform is given by the echo’s power as a function of time, distance or frequency.
Waveforms are then archived and averaged over an altimeter’s duty cycle period to
lessen the impact of speckle (granular interference).
Distinct “stable” surfaces such as open ocean can be represented in the waveform
domain as shown in Figure 3.1 below. This recognizable shape is called “Brown-like”
or the “ocean-like” waveform [41].

data until the signal is found again and get stabilized. The diﬀerence of the wave-
form’s leading edge from the altimeter tracking gate must be corrected (retracking
correction). It is carried out by retracking which could be simply explained as a
process of estimating the distance of the closest approach to the measured point.
Several retracking algorithms have been developed to facilitate surface recognition.
3.1.1
Waveform parameters
Parameters from returned echo waveforms can be possibly translated into the infor-
mation about the surface it has interacted with. This information can say something
about the surface smoothness or roughness, sea state, wind speed (WS), freeboard
height and thickness, etc.
In general, the three most important estimated parameters are power, pulse peaki-
ness and surface roughness (SR) or more speciﬁcally - signiﬁcant wave height (SWH)
in case of oceans, however additional parameters such as the width of the leading
edge, slope of the trailing edge or skewness can be explanatory as well [20].

to high values in the case of wetlands or ﬂat surfaces. It is also the reason why
dihedral corner reﬂectors are used for the SAR images calibration [43]. In literature,
the measure of the backscatter coeﬃcient is dB (decibel), typically within the range
from 0 to 100.

3.1.1.3
Sea surface height (SSH)
In the same manner, as land’s topography is rugged and creased, ocean dynamic
topography exhibits highs and lows. To measure this variation, a sea surface height
parameter has been established as an altitudinal diﬀerence between a satellite and
a reference surface (ellipsoid).
To map SSH is, however, very challenging due to many inﬂuencing factors aﬀecting
SSH at diﬀerent timescales. One of the instantaneous factors is the Moon and the
Sun which create tidal forces. On the other hand, ocean circulation and changes in
Earth’s gravitational ﬁeld play an important role in (much) longer timescale. Be-
sides the above-mentioned factors, the Earth experiences seasonal cycles that cause
an alteration of the temperature and surface wind. All of this makes an adjustment
to measured mean SSH.
For information regarding signiﬁcant wave height SWH please refer to Appendix B,
on page 67.
3.1.2
Snagging
Snagging can be explained as an echo’s response to bright oﬀ-nadir targets which
give rise to waveform peaks at a longer delay (visible in trailing edge) [56]. This
phenomenon was hence named “oﬀ-nadir hooking” by Peacock and Laxon [53]. It
creates large errors due to violation of the assumption that measured SSH is at the
nearest reﬂecting surface, which would normally be at nadir [56]. So bright targets
are then incorrectly followed by the retracking algorithms [57].
These bright targets can be leads at the open ocean [58] or ponds and lakes in
case of monitoring of river surface heights.
In order to reduce the snagging eﬀect, several methods have been developed. Gomez-
Enri et al. [57] proposed a modeling technique with replication of bright targets
features and subsequently subtracting them from waveforms. Quartly [59] made an
automatic method by ﬁtting to waveforms hyperbolic features whereas Santos da
Silva et al. [60] tried to eliminate oﬀ-nadir bright targets over rivers and lakes.

3.2
Retracking
There are two systems on-board of a satellite that handle acquirement and track-
ing of waveforms.
First is the Radio Frequency Unit (RFU) responsible for the
acquirement followed by the waveform processing unit operated by the Processing
and Control Unit (PCU). The main purpose of this tracking unit is to keep the
returned pulse at the nominal tracking position in the ﬁlter bank [61]. However, due
to rapid changes of topography in the range over land, the tracking unit is failing
to yield good results and generates so-called tracker oﬀset. To achieve the highest
correctness, waveforms are downlinked to the Earth where retracking algorithms
attempt to deduce the geophysical parameters described earlier in Section 3.1.1 by
ﬁtting a model or functional form to waveforms [62]. The ﬁnal step in retracking is
the range estimation where the range of the analysis window and retrieved epoch is
combined.
Diﬀerent surfaces have a diﬀerent impact on the waveforms’ shape. That is a reason
why for that variety of illuminated surfaces various retracking algorithms (retrack-
ers) have been developed to infer necessary characterizing parameters. Therefore,
diﬀerent retracker will also give diﬀerent SH estimates. In general, retrackers fall
into two categories:
• Physical retrackers.
• Empirical retrackers.
It is, however, beyond the scope of this thesis to characterize retracker algorithms
in greater detail. Hence, only short functional overview and retracking principles of
physical and empirical retrackers are given below.
3.2.1
Physical retrackers
As the name implies, physical retrackers utilize a theoretical knowledge of the return
power of a scattering surface at nadir. In order to retrieve a surface elevation (or
diﬀerent physical attributes), a simple model is ﬁt to the waveform which mimics
a waveform’s behavior. Physical retrackers are used mainly in an open ocean area
where waveforms are of ocean-type.
The complexity of a physical retracker depends on a selection and quantity of pa-
rameters being ﬁt in the ﬁtting algorithm. Very frequently the Brown model [41] is
used in ocean area where the assumption is made that no signals from other surfaces
like land or ice occur.
Brown model parameters have been improved by the work of MacArthur [63] and
in Hayne’s reﬁned analytical ocean return model [64]. Challenor and Srokosz [65]
accounted for non-linearities (skewness) of ocean waves. In case of Sentinel-3, the
SAMOSA SAR the waveform model is the standard model for retracking [66] [67].
Some algorithms for land ice retracking are in use as well, for instance, Cryosat’s
newly implemented Land-Ice ReTracker (LIRT) which ﬁts the Brown model to the
waveform [68]. Since the Brown model does not perform very well with disparate
surfaces [69], it is better to use an empirical method.

3.2.2
Empirical retrackers
As opposed to physical, empirical retrackers disregard the physics of the reﬂection of
returned echoes. These retrackers can be subdivided into those ﬁtting an empirical
function to the waveform and those based on statistical characteristics.
Wingham et al. [70] developed, now highly used, statistically-based retracker called
the oﬀset center of gravity (OCOG). Its simplicity lies in the creation of the rect-
angle around the center of gravity of the waveform followed by calculation of the
amplitude and the width of waveform. The OCOG retracker performs best for con-
tinental ice data.

Chapter 6
Choice of retrackers
This chapter is intended to explain the reader reasons why certain retracker performs
better and what could be a possible explanation. Diﬀerent surface heights obtained
by various retrackers are portrayed which enables for visual comparison (Sections 6.1
and 6.2). The choice of the best performing retracker is of utter importance and
should be one of the ﬁrst steps when dealing with data over surfaces covered by snow
and/or ice. This process is the same for Cryosat-2 and Sentinel-3 mission. The link
between noise and diﬀerent waveform parameters available from Sentinel-3 data are
discussed in Chapter 7.
6.1
Available retrackers: Sentinel-3
Surface heights derived from Sentinel-3 retracking methods are divided into standard
retrackers and primary peak threshold (PPT) retrackers conveying elevation values
with respect to the Earth Gravitational Model 2008 (EGM2008). Please note that
description of standard retrackers can be found in Section 3.2. There are available
two standard and three PPT retrackers in case of Sentinel-3 mission:
• Hoce – Fully analytical retracker
• Hocog – Empirical retracker (oﬀset center of gravity)
• PPT 25%
• PPT 50%
• PPT 80%
PPT retrackers are a modiﬁcation of conventional ones in pulse limited altimetry.
They identify the bin at which the peak of the waveform ﬁrst exceeds a given per-
centage (25%, 50% or 80%) of the maximum power of the waveform. More speciﬁc
information regarding standard retrackers can be found in Davis (1997) [71].
Figure 6.4 shows the altimetry data from ﬁve diﬀerent retrackers for the Yenisei
river. As described earlier in this work (5.3 on page 24), most of the year the river
course is frozen. Spike of the water level signal should be located during the spring
period (“spring break-up”) and followed by approximately stable low-level elevation
when river heights come back to normal.
If there are many values signiﬁcantly exceeding low water levels in winter periods, it
can be almost certainly attributed to noise. Examples of noisy areas are highlighted

7.4.1
Inter-mission bias
If datasets from multiple missions are utilized, so-called inter-mission bias must be
accounted for. It is an average diﬀerence in surface height observations perceived (in
case of this work) by Cryosat-2 and Sentinel-3A/Sentinel-3B satellites. Given that
water level is not referenced (only the Mackenzie river posses in-situ water stage
data, although missing in many cases) the exact bias can not be calculated, though
some approximation can be deduced.
To establish the aforesaid bias, surface height observations at speciﬁc times have
been compared in both missions to see the measure of their discrepancy.
Winter periods were chosen given their more stable water level conditions. Mul-
tiple points from Cryosat-2 and Sentinel-3 observations, as close in time as possible,
were compared, subtracted from each other and averaged. This produced a diﬀer-
ence of 1.93m in case of the Yenisei river.

Chapter 8
Results summary
As this study takes a continuous approach into how to deal with satellite altimetry
data from the beginning, where only raw (noisy) observations are available, several
conclusions are drawn and respective results are explained in this chapter subse-
quently.
One of the most important tasks to begin with when proceeding towards the time
series computation is the choice of the right retracking method. The primary peak
threshold retracker, identifying the height of the ﬁrst peak exceeding 80% of max-
imum power proved to be the best performing in Sentinel-3 altimetry data for all
rivers except the Ob river where this retracker was not available but the corrected
narrow primary peak threshold retracker gave the best result. As for the Cryosat-2
retrackers, the simple threshold retracker exhibited superior results over the rest of
the available methods. This conclusion comes mostly from the visual comparison
of diﬀerent retracking methods and their inherent noise. However, when taking the
nature of Arctic rivers’ surroundings into consideration (waveform shapes), it agrees
with what would be expected as well. Nevertheless, it is not viable to quantify to
what extent each of the retrackers is better due to the lack of real reference (noise
limited) data.
Sentinel-3 provides three additional waveform parameters along with the surface
height elevation. It was shown in Section 7.2 that there is a link between noise and
the pulse width parameter. While adopting a speciﬁc range for pulse width it is
possible to remove much of noise while still intercept the spring break-up peaks.
Pulse peakiness and power itself showed no palpable relationship with noise. It was
not further elaborated on these parameter correlations and no ﬁltering was made in
this study based on any of the aforementioned waveform parameters.
After the choice of retracker, still raw data were cut based on upper and lower bound-
aries. These boundaries were distinguished for each river separately and ranges are
shown in the Table 7.1 on page 39. To compensate for inter-mission bias between
Cryosat-2 and Sentinel-3 datasets, a diﬀerent positive constant had to be added to
Cryosat-2 observations. This constant (see Table 8.1) was retrieved during winter
periods where the water level is low and many observations are available and very
close in time.

Chapter 9
Conclusions and recommendations
As this study focuses on the new continuous approach for time series and discharge
determination of arctic rivers, there are several conclusions and many recommen-
dations presented in this chapter to further improve the implementation of this
research.
9.1
Conclusions
In 2010, Cryosat-2 has been launched which led to great improvement in the ﬁeld
of satellite altimetry. This was followed by Sentinel-3 mission in 2016 which further
enhanced the spatial and temporal resolution of data available in this remote part
of the world as is the Arctic region. Although the SAR performs well in many types
of surfaces, weather conditions and rapid seasonal changes above the 60th parallel
north make it particularly diﬃcult to obtain good estimates of surface height. This
urges for a method that could eliminate noise present in datasets to the least pos-
sible level in order to produce time series from which discharge can be calculated.
The purpose of this Master thesis is as followed:
The main goals of this project are to quantify freshwater ﬂuxes of
the Arctic Ocean tributary rivers by using satellites altimetry data
and compare the results with measurements in situ where possible,
and to identify the limitations of altimetry data usage (e.g. how
small rivers can be still intercepted and estimated and how small
freshwater ﬂuxes can be monitored).
To calculate, or at least approximate the freshwater ﬂowing into the Arctic Ocean,
it is necessary to start with the largest contributors such as the Yenisei, Lena, Ob
and the Mackenzie river. There are, however, numerous other smaller tributaries
that also play a signiﬁcant role in the global atmospheric cycle. When conducting
the method proposed in this study, several principal factors came up as governing.
The river width is logically an important factor, however, the river orientation is
also very important. When the river copies the satellite ground track (so they go
alongside), it degrades the quality of the altimetry surface height data due to worse
along-track resolution compared to across-track resolution. Another factor is the
58
==PAGE:72==
9.1. CONCLUSIONS
59
topography. Detrimental to data quality are many lakes or diﬀerent water bodies
that freeze and can reﬂect the radar signal from a higher elevation than is the actual
river surface. This constitutes a considerable part of the noise in data.
The slope of the arctic river bed can be only guessed based on altimetry surface
height data due to a lack of gauging stations. Nevertheless, it is a vital factor when
validation of surface height with in-situ is performed since data need to be projected
to the location of the gauging station. In many cases, the river bed slope is just
approximated linearly.
As could be seen in this study, altimetry data for all rivers in the Arctic region
are very noisy and this needs to be alleviated.
The Sentinel-3 dataset provided
three additional waveform parameters besides diﬀerent retracking SH estimates. In
this study it was shown (in Section 7.2 on page 40) that there is no link between the
power or pulse peakiness (PP) and noise but noise dependency on the pulse width
is palpable.
The seasonal ﬁlter developed for this project was applied to all rivers to reduce
the noise level as much as possible while preserving the spring break-up or sum-
mer/autumn ﬂoods peaks in surface height observations. It has been proven that
this method is robust and produced the time series SH estimates, which in the case
of the Ob and the Mackenzie river, were possible to validate with in-situ data. The
RMSE in surface heights in the case of the Ob river was only 0.67m and 1.1m in the
case of the Mackenzie. Better performance in the ﬁrst case can be attributed to the
water regime. Lots of bogs are situated in the Ob’s watershed and the ﬂoodplain
is ten times wider than the channel width. This results in high natural regulation
capacity which manifests into prolonged ﬂoods which in turn gives a chance for
satellites with a lower temporal resolution to intercept SH.
For the days where no SH estimates are available, the Kalman and simple linear
interpolation performed the best as shown in RMSE Table 8.2 in Chapter 8 above.
Time series with clear visible signal were accomplished for all large-sized rivers but
the Yana river time series starts to exhibit the limitations of altimetry data usage.
Altimetry data of the smallest river in this study, the Mezen, failed to produce any
good result. This can be attributed to not enough data since the river reach is
narrow and the occurrence of water in the water mask varies signiﬁcantly from one
bank to its opposite. As was shown, the TS including estimated SH where no stan-
dard deviation could be calculated due to missing data can produce a more resolved
outcome.
The discharge was calculated based on the rating curves for the Mackenzie and
the Yenisei river. It it has been demonstrated that in order to produce much better
discharge estimates, the rating curve must be updated and re-calibrated for at least
each year or even seasons where ice is present and where it is not. Therefore, the
average estimated discharge for the Mackenzie river was almost the same as observed
during 2011-2017. However, this was not the case for the Yenisei, where using the
same rating curve for all years proved to be ineﬃcient and estimates diﬀered signif-
icantly from the observed values.
In conclusion, this work shows that even the simple proposed approach together
with the right choice of retracker can produce time series which well describes the

9.2
Recommendations
Since this project touched upon many diﬀerent approaches to eliminate noise and
to estimate what are altimetry data usage limitations, several recommendations can
be placed for the upcoming research in this ﬁeld.
Pulse width and noise dependency
As described earlier in this work (Section 7.2), the link between the pulse width
waveform parameter exists and can be one of the solutions for noise removal. This
link, however, was not the goal of this project since it can be case-speciﬁc and the
proposed seasonal ﬁlter was a robust method that substantially eliminated noise
nonetheless. Since the purpose of this work was to create a method that is appli-
cable to as many rivers as it allows, no further elaboration on this dependency has
been made. It is recommended to further improve quality of observations based on
the pulse width range dependency on noise.
Manual ﬁltering method
When treating one river at a time, the surface height limits during the winter pe-
riod could be established and all points above this limit removed as another ﬁltering
method. This method, however, requires some knowledge of discharge or SH devel-
opment. For instance, in the case of the Yenisei river, it is very unlikely that SH
will exceed 2.5m at the Salekhard station between August-April. Such SH limits
could be estimated by a curve rather than a proposed straight line due to a more
gradual decrease of river SH in cases such as the Mackenzie and other large-sized
arctic rivers.
Time series computation
The limits of altimetry data usage have been shown on the Yana and the Mezen
river. In both cases though, resulting time series were visually improved by incor-
porated surface height values where no standard deviation could be computed due
to lack of data. This happens when only one observation is left after applying the
seasonal ﬁlter. In any case, this value still holds some credibility since it measures
the lowest water level which should be the case when measuring rivers. Therefore,
it is recommended to consider this approach when working with rivers with diﬃcult
orientation, low quantity of altimetry data and/or low water occurrence as was the
case of the Mezen river presented here.
Discharge calculation
It has been proven in the case of the Yenisei river, that applying one rating curve
for multiple years is not satisfactory for the discharge calculation. Even though it
may produce good results for certain annual periods, it is more a coincidence than a

When applying the manual ﬁltering method explained earlier in this chapter, it

Appendix B
Signiﬁcant Wave height SWH
The signiﬁcant wave height (SWH) is deﬁned as an average of the highest one-third
(33%) of waves (measured from trough to crest) that occur in a given period. As
the name implies, the higher value for SWH, the more signiﬁcant wave is and vice
versa. If the overﬂown area has a high average SWH then a footprint of a radar
altimeter will be larger compared to areas with lower SWH and in case of overland
measurements, this is governed by the backscatter coeﬃcient and the antenna aper-
ture [20].
The signiﬁcant wave height has an impact particularly on a slope of echo’s leading
edge (smaller slope in case of high SWH and vice versa) as it is seen in Figure B.1.
More generally, SWH could be translated into how rough is a surface, hence the
surface roughness. [41].
