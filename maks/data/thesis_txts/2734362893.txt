Abstract 
Copenhagen Limestone is a highly heterogeneous material with its stiffness and strength 
properties displaying a variability spanning two orders of magnitude. It is characterized by 
varying degrees of induration, ranging from unlithified H1 to strongly indurated H5, resulting 
in the presence of both soft and hard layers. Additionally, the existence of fractures further 
contributes to the overall variability of the rock. For materials with such heterogeneity, 
probabilistic methods are usually employed so that the uncertainties, which in such cases are 
significant, can be readily detected and considered. Extensive research has been carried out, 
primarily focused on the mechanical properties of intact samples rather than the rock mass. In 
the current study, rock mass characteristics are calibrated probabilistically by comparing 
stress-strain responses obtained by triaxial testing on intermediate scale samples comprised of 
layered limestone with different degree of indurations to numerical model simulations. The 
triaxial tests used in this study were carried out during the site investigation campaign for the 
construction of the Øresund Bridge, a pivotal link connecting Denmark and Sweden. Initially, 
the optimal discretization of the layers (number and thickness) was investigated by comparing 
the stress-strain response of various sample configurations. Thereafter, three multivariate 
distributions that were created based on intact samples describing the behavior for different 
induration degrees via the Hoek-Brown criterion, were combined. The intermediate-scale 
triaxial test T2 consisted of layered Copenhagen Limestone was simulated in PLAXIS 2D and 
the geotechnical parameters of each layer were back-analyzed. This approach allows 
geotechnical engineers to back-analyze considering the contribution of each layer individually, 
leading to improved rock characterization and consequently safer and more sustainable 
constructions, such as the new metro line in Copenhagen.

Introduction 
Background 
An investigation took place in Lernacken, an area close to Malmø, by the Swedish 
Geotechnical Institute (SGI) and the Danish Geotechnical Institute (DGI), prior to the 
construction of the Øresund fixed link between Denmark and Sweden. The Øresund Bridge 
was founded in Danian Copenhagen limestone which was indurated to varying degrees and 
layers of flint were formed in. The investigation comprised of laboratory and field tests on 
unlithified lime H1, slightly indurated H2, indurated H3 and strongly indurated H4 Copenhagen 
limestone where samples of different sizes were prepared. In particular, small scale laboratory 
tests (<100 mm) on core samples, intermediate scale laboratory triaxial and direct simple shear 
tests (Ø500 mm) on samples cut out in the field, as well as large scale (1.0-3.0 m2) plate load 
tests in the test field were performed. The aim of the investigation was to determine the strength 
and the deformation of the Copenhagen Limestone, where a significant part of the bridge 
construction was conducted on. 
Objectives of the Research 
The current study emphases on the calibration of rock mass characteristics through 
triaxial testing on intermediate scale samples, specifically employing the PLAXIS 2D 
software. The overarching goal is to replicate these tests and compare the results of the 
simulations to those of the laboratory testing. Furthermore, an additional objective involves 
revising the probability density functions for all parameters to predict the behavior of the rock 
mass (strength, stiffness and GSI values). This investigation seeks to bridge the gap between 
theoretical simulations and real-world conditions while at the same time to enhance our 
understanding of Copenhagen Limestone's response under different induration levels, in 
models that include diverse layer configurations, employing an innovative approach that 
integrates numerical simulations with laboratory data.

Structure of the Thesis 
Section 2 (Literature Review), insights into the Øresund project, offering a concise 
overview of the geological conditions within the specified region are provided. Additionally, 
this section delves into comprehensive studies on the Copenhagen Limestone, shedding light 
at the same time on the employed constitutive models. Moving on to Section 3, Data, Tools 
and Methodology, this part details the formulation of small-scale and intermediate-scale tests, 
describing the applied methodology so as to derive the desired model. Moreover, the 
simulation of the updated triaxial test after the acquisition of all the missing information is 
described. The initial steps of the study involve a critical decision-making process regarding 
the selection of criteria, with a particular focus on the Mohr-Coulomb and Hoek-Brown failure 
criteria. To validate and refine the chosen criteria, the study utilizes existing models based on 
small-scale triaxial tests. These models, while not exclusively designed based on induration 
levels but densities, offer a valuable baseline for selecting the most appropriate criterion. The 
stress-strain curves derived from these small-scale tests serve as benchmarks for comparison 
with results obtained through PLAXIS simulations. Furthermore, as the study progresses to 
the intermediate-scale tests, models are constructed with a finer granularity, precisely tailored 
to the varying degrees of induration described in the Øresund project reports. In Section 4, 
under the Results and Discussion umbrella, the outcomes of the probabilistic back analysis for 
the intermediate-scale triaxial are presented providing a thorough examination of the findings.

Literature Review  
Geological conditions 
In the Copenhagen area, the bedrock consists of Danian Copenhagen Limestone, 
overlaid by Quaternary deposits containing mainly glacial tills and meltwater deposits 
[Hanssen et al. (2015)]. The Copenhagen Limestone, forming a 40-50 m thick unit, constitutes 
the pre-Quaternary surface east of the Carlsberg fault. To the west of the fault, the Copenhagen 
Limestone rests on approximately 50 m of bryozoan limestone, shaping the pre-Quaternary 
surface. Copenhagen Limestone (CL), formally defined by Stenestad in 1976, is a carbonate 
rock that exhibits significant anisotropy, comprising alternating indurated and less indurated 
sections [Katić, N. & Christensen, H.F. (2015)]. According to Stenestad Copenhagen 
Limestone can be divided lithologically in three subunits. The lower subunit was described as 
consists of marly beds [Stenestad, (1976)]. Nevertheless, chemical analyses indicate a higher 
clay content than that found in the overlying limestone; however, the concentration remains 
insufficient to categorize the unit as marly. The unit, that is also described as ‘laminated’, is 
approximately 4 to 8 meters thick while it has relatively high content of glauconite and pyrite 
contributing to its dark colour. The middle subunit is described as ‘bioturbated’ by Stenestad, 
(1976). It is approximately 20 meters thick and preserves flint nodules [Knudsen et al. (1995)]. 
Lastly, the upper subunit, is a regularly bedded with less flint elements 14-meters thick unit. 
Formed in a shallow marine environment, Copenhagen Limestone is a blend of skeletal 
remains of planktonic organisms within a matrix of diagenetically precipitated calcite crystals. 
Due to the different degrees of cementation and silicification, the limestone's diagenesis varies 
from unlithified to very strongly lithified. The low rate of sedimentation and high degrees of 
lithification occurred in periods of low sea depth, while the rapid sedimentation resulted in less 
lithified sediments [Hanssen et al., (2015)].

Øresund Bridge Project Information 
The Øresund Bridge is the main component of the fixed link between Denmark and 
Sweden. Additionally, the link consists of an artificial peninsula, a submerged tunnel with 
ramps 3.7 km, two artificial islands connected with a low bridge south Saltholm 4.2 km and a 
long bridge to Lernacken 7.5 km including a high bridge across the fairway, ‘Flintrännan’, 1.1 
km. A great part of the construction took place in Danian Limestone, primarily the Copenhagen 
Limestone. The aim of the additional investigation of the Copenhagen Limestone was to try 
and calibrate site investigation technics while determine the strength and deformation 
parameters. In Figure 1, the stratigraphy of the area is illustrated while it can be observed that 
the water depth below the Øresund Bridge is around -8 m. The seabed is composed of 
postglacial sands, typically less than 1 meter thick, with occasional thin layers of peat or 
organic mud in certain locations. Beneath these deposits, 0 to 5 meters of glacial clay till and 
glacial meltwater sands lie.

Triaxial Test  
Triaxial test is a laboratory testing method widely used to obtain shear strength 
parameters for various types of soil and rock. It consists of a cylindrical sample enclosed within 
a rubber membrane, subjected in a constant confining stress (radial stress σ3) while undergoing 
controlled incremental axial stress (vertical stress σ1) or axial displacement. The rubber 
membrane prevents the fluid from entering the specimen and changing the water content. A 
motorized drive applies the axial stress by elevating the specimen against a piston that interacts 
with a load frame. The force, F, is measured by a proving ring positioned between the piston 
and load frame and from this measurement, the principal stress difference or deviatoric stress 
(p=σ1-σ3) is determined. In Figure 2 the triaxial apparatus can be observed. [Barnes, G. E., 
(1995)]

Constitutive Models: Mohr-Coulomb & Hoek Brown 
Mohr-Coulomb 
Mohr-Coulomb is a widely used and relatively simple-to-implement constitutive model 
when the behavior of the material is well-described. It assumes a linear stress-strain behavior 
which makes it is suitable for soil and rock materials that are not highly fractured or jointed 
[Tian, D & Zheng, H. (2023)]. It consists of linear equations in the principal stress space that 
define the conditions leading to failure in an isotropic material, disregarding any influence 
from the intermediate principal stress σ2. The Mohr-Coulomb criterion can be expressed either 
in terms of the major σ1 and minor σ3 principal stresses or the normal stress σ and shear stress 
τ acting on the failure plane. The Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion can be written as the equation 
for the line that represents the failure envelope. The general equation (1) can be seen below.

Advantages and Limitations 
One of the advantages of the Hoek-Brown criterion is the non-linear regime that agrees 
with the experimental data within a specific range of stresses. Furthermore, it was formulated 
based on extensive laboratory test data encompassing a diverse array of intact rock types while 
it offers straightforward empirical approaches for the estimation of the rock mass properties. 
However, significant limitations have been documented such as the independency of the 
criterion from the intermediate principal stress, σ2. Several studies have indicated that the 
intermediate principal stress affects the rock strength significantly. Examples include studies 
by Takahashi and Koide (1989), Colmenares and Zoback (2002), and Haimson (2006).

Statistical Theory 
Probability Density Function 
In probability theory, a probability density function (PDF), also known as a density function, 
characterizes an absolutely continuous random variable. It is a function where, at any specific 
point within the sample space (the range of possible values for the random variable), the value 
can be interpreted as representing the relative likelihood of the random variable taking on that 
particular value. [Grinstead, C. M.; Snell, J. L., (2009)]

Bimodal Distribution 
A bimodal distribution appears as a single distribution with two peaks that are 
characterized as maxima. It typically looks like two normal distributions on one graph 
displayed side by side, with two distinct bell curve shapes contained within it. It is important 
to be able to recognize when a data set consists of two or more distinct groups so that they may 
be analyzed separately, if appropriate. This can be seen in a histogram as a distinct gap between 
two cohesive groups of bars. The two groups have to be large enough, individually cohesive, 
and either have a fair gap between them or else represent a large enough sample to be sure that 
the lower frequencies between the groups are not just random fluctuations [Siegel A. F., 
Wagner, M. R., (2022)].

assessing goodness of fit generally capture the disparity between observed values and the 
values anticipated by the model. These metrics find application in statistical hypothesis testing, 
such as evaluating the normality of residuals or determining whether two samples are drawn 
from the same distributions (as in the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test) [Huber et al. (2002)].

Multivariate Distribution Model 
The probabilistic calibration of the intermediate-scale triaxial tests was based on 
multivariate models that were created by Panagiotis E., (2024)*, based on intact samples.

Hoek-Brown constitutive model within compression zone. It employs 8 parameters, including 
the bulk density 𝜌bulk, the initial void ratio 𝑒0, the primary wave velocity 𝑉𝑝, the shear wave

ref, the unconfined compression strength 𝜎𝑐𝑖, the material

tests. The 𝜎𝑐𝑖 and 𝑚𝑖, 𝑐 were interpreted by curve fitting based on the Hoek- Brown criterion.

Data, Tools and Methodology  
To investigate the behavior of Copenhagen Limestone under varying stress conditions, 
a series of triaxial tests with a diameter of approximately 500 mm was decided to be performed. 
The focus of these triaxial tests lies particularly on layers mainly composed of H2 material. 
The meticulous process involved cutting samples at predetermined levels from two sampling 
locations A and B at the test field at Lernacken, utilizing a diamond saw. These samples, 
encapsulated in a confining rubber membrane and shielded by a steel cover, were then 
transported to the laboratories of the Danish Geotechnical Institute (DGI) and the Swedish 
Geotechnical Institute (SGI). The laboratory work comprised of a comprehensive geological 
description of the specimens, their classification and the execution of the triaxial testing. In the 
current case, an established multivariate model derived from small-scale tests was initially 
employed to identify the most appropriate criterion for simulating the tests. Additionally, these 
small-scale tests served to validate the simulation process, given the availability and validation 
of laboratory triaxial test data.  
Model Development (PLAXIS 2D Software) 
PLAXIS 2D is a widely known finite-element (FE) software for 2D analysis of 
deformation and stability in geotechnical engineering and rock mechanics [Bentley, (2022)]. 
In order to simulate the cylindrical small and intermediate scale triaxial tests, an axisymmetric 
model needed to be formulated. It is assumed that the behavior of the material is rotationally 
symmetric around the axis of the cylinder. By taking into consideration this assumption and in 
order to reduce the complexity of the simulation, a two-dimensional section of a cylindrical 
sample was constructed instead of a three-dimensional volume. Additionally, a 2D model 
requires less computational time as fewer nodes and elements are needed. The description of 
the layers regarding the geology is presented in the next chapter. The model was simulated on 
the vertical axis using a load instead of a displacement as it was proved that the results are 
similar in both cases. The boundaries for X minimum and Y minimum axes were set to 
Normally fixed, which practically means that horizontal and vertical fixity is applied, whereas 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
 
             Technical University of Denmark 
 
 25 | P a g e   
 
 
for X maximum and Y maximum the boundaries were set to Free. The mesh that was generated 
was fine while global flow conditions were applied.

Input Data 
In order to compare the stress-strain responses obtained by the triaxial testing on the 
intermediate scale samples to the numerical model simulation, the rock mass parameters were 
calibrated probabilistically. A set of input values was provided for each layer, from the 
multivariate existing model [Panagiotis et al. (2024*)]. In particular, the secant modulus E50, 
the unconfined compression strength σci and the material constant for the intact rock mi were 
allocated while formulated with respect to the bulk density of each layer that was introduced 
in the classification parameters of the Øresund reports by the DGI. Input data for 10000 
samples were provided while an automated process was conducted for the generation of the 
random combinations of the aforementioned parameters along with the investigation of a range 
of GSI values from 30 to 100. This way, the random samples that are generated are not biased. 
In Figure 20, the input parameters for the six layers of triaxial test T2 are presented. The E50 
value is observed to follow a Lognormal Distribution across all layers. In contrast, the 
unconfined compressive strength σci and the material constant mi exhibit different distribution 
patterns. Specifically, σci is subject to the Weibull distribution in the weak layers (1, 4, and 6), 
while in the stronger layers (2, 3, and 5), it adheres to the Normal distribution. It should be 
underlined that the current data respond to intact samples. In Table 9, the statistical parameters 
of the input data are presented. It can be observed from the coefficient of variation that the σci 
is the most variable parameter of the three, in the weak layers (1,4 and 6).

Results and Discussion 
In this chapter the results of the probabilistic calibration of the intermediate scale 
triaxial tests are discussed. Around 7000 analyses were performed for 1000 samples that were 
generated from the multivariate model. The randomly generated input values were imported 
in PLAXIS software through an automated Python script, where the simulation of the 
laboratory intermediate triaxial test was performed while investigating different GSI values 
with a range from 30 to 100. The presentation of findings will be presented in histograms and 
tables in an attempt to illustrate the characteristic values of the secant modulus E50, the 
unconfined compression strength σci, and the material constant mi for the representative 
samples centered around the point of interest. It is important to be noted that these input 
parameters apply to intact samples. With the inclusion of the additional parameter of the GSI, 
the results will become comparable to those obtained from laboratory testing. The graphical 
and tabular representations aim to provide a clearer understanding of the calibrated results and 
the variability observed in the key parameters. 
In Figure 21, the stress-strain peak points of all the analyses are presented for the shear 
stage with the strain rate of 6%. The red 'X' mark on the plot signifies the peak stress-strain 
value of the laboratory test, pinpointed at coordinates (0.013, 1689). This serves as the 
reference point for comparison across all simulated tests. The simulation points in proximity 
to this value were chosen for investigation, considering uncertainties of 1%, 5%, and 10%. The 
colored boxes surrounding the 'X' mark represent the three different uncertainty levels. Only 7 
points were observed within the uncertainty of 1%, possibly due to the limited number of 
samples that were simulated, whereas 37 points were noted within a 5% of uncertainty and 76 
with a 10% of uncertainty.

Conclusions 
The current study concerns the probabilistic calibration of rock mass characteristics 
through the simulation of triaxial testing on intermediate-scale samples employing the 
PLAXIS 2D software.   The overarching goal was to simulate three out of the nine available 
tests, yet one test was simulated eventually, comparing the results with these from laboratory 
testing. The plan involved an investigation of the response of Copenhagen Limestone that 
contains various amounts of different induration levels, considering diverse layer 
configurations and calibrating rock mass parameters in the numerical simulations by 
comparison with laboratory results. The structured approach outlined in the thesis involved a 
literature review on the Øresund project, insights into geological conditions, and a detailed 
methodology for small-scale and intermediate-scale tests. The validation and refinement of 
chosen criteria, such as the Mohr-Coulomb and Hoek-Brown criteria, were based on a 
multivariate distribution model from small-scale triaxial tests. As the study progressed to 
intermediate-scale tests, models were tailored to varying number of layers for the different 
degrees of induration and compared to the simulated results. After the acquisition of significant 
for the research information an updated model was formulated where the number of layers and 
the classification parameters used were the representatives from the laboratory testing.  
The results are presented through histograms and tables, shedding light on characteristic 
values such as the secant modulus (E50), the unconfined compression strength (σci), and the 
material constant (mi) for the representative samples, centered around the point of interest, 
with different results for uncertainty of 1%, 5% and 10%. Slight differences (below 10 %) were 
observed in the characteristic values of stiffness and strength when comparing 1%, 5% and 
10%, except for layer 3. This layer is much stiffer compare to the others which might be the 
reason for the difference observed. Notably, no proportional correlation is evident between 
strength and stiffness while comparing the values among the layers, particularly in those 
belonging in the low-density group. Regarding the analyses based on the GSI values a possibly 
bimodal distribution is observed as the representative samples are grouped in two populations, 
one around GSI=40 and the other around GSI=70. Additionally, the samples with a GSI value 
==PAGE:55==
 
 
 
             Technical University of Denmark 
 
 55 | P a g e   
 
 
of 40 were mainly stiffer but less strong compared to the samples with GSI=70, unlike with 
the expectations. This behavior indicates a greater variability of the strength and the stiffness 
parameters, not only of each layer individually, but also as a rock mass. This can be attributed 
to the existence of three layers with lower density (weak) that govern the observed behavior. 
To conclude, comparing the strength and stiffness parameters with the literature (Table 1), 
it can be observed that for the layers that mainly employ H2 induration, the stiffness values are 
from 2000 MPa to 5000 MPa, numbers that fall into the ranges of the corresponding category 
of H2. Regarding the stiff layer 3, the degree of induration falls into both H3 and H4 (lower 
bounds of H4 and upper bounds of H3) with the resulted values to validate that. Similarly, the 
resulted strength values for the layers 1,2,4,5, and 6 lay in the category of induration H2 in 
Table 1 while for the layer 3 in the category of induration H3. 
Future research 
Further research on the current project can be carried out in order to enhance the accuracy of 
the results and to validate the observed distributions. In particular, the T2 test that was 
simulated in this study, could undertake further analyses and explore more samples in order to 
validate the results. Moreover, the other two triaxial tests T3 and T4 that employ indurations 
of H2, H3 and H4 which can also be simulated using the same multivariate model distribution. 
The results of all three tests can then be interpreted statistically and compared. This project 
constitutes an important asset for geotechnical engineering as it can lead to improved rock 
characterization and consequently safer and more sustainable constructions, such as the new 
metro line in Copenhagen.
