Summary (English)
This thesis presents a comprehensive analysis and comparison of two software
tools the Ctsmr and CtsmrTMB estimators, used for parameter estimation in
stochastic differential equations (SDEs) specifically focusing on the widely stud-
ied Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process. The study’s aim is to assess their robustness
and sensitivity in various aspects, including initial parameter values, data bias,
and execution time efficiency.
The findings indicate that the CtsmrTMB estimator is less influenced by initial
values compared to Ctsmr. However, both estimators exhibit high sensitivity
to data bias, resulting in elevated standard errors. This can be mitigated by
reducing step sizes in simulations and observations. Additionally, a linear de-
pendence between the parameter θ’s overestimation and the size of the two noise
parameters, σx and σy, was observed.
Moreover, the CtsmrTMB estimator demonstrates faster execution time and
reduced sensitivity to initial values and σx and σy sizes compared to Ctsmr.
Overall, this thesis contributes valuable insights into the performance and limita-
tions of the Ctsmr and CtsmrTMB estimators for parameter estimation in SDEs,
specifically focusing on the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process. These findings will be
beneficial for researchers and practitioners in the field of stochastic differential
equations, aiding them in selecting appropriate software tools and improving
parameter estimation accuracy.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Stochastic differential equations (SDEs) are widely used mathematical models
in various fields, including physics, finance, and biology. They provide a frame-
work to describe dynamic systems affected by both deterministic and random
forces.
Estimating the parameters in SDEs is crucial for understanding and
predicting the behaviour of these systems. However, parameter estimation for
SDEs is a challenging task due to the inherent uncertainty and complexity of
the underlying stochastic processes.
The focus of this thesis is to analyze and compare the performance of two
software tools in estimating parameters for SDEs, specifically targeting the
Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process. The Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process is a well-known
SDE that describes the mean-reverting behaviour of a system influenced by
random fluctuations. The two software estimators under investigation are the
Ctsmr and CtsmrTMB estimators.
The Ctsm (Continuous Time Stochastic Modeling) software, along with its pre-
cursor, CTLSM, has been successfully employed in modelling diverse systems
[NRK03]. In 2010, Rune Juhl parallelized and implemented the Ctsm in the R
language, creating the Ctsmr package [Juh11],[Tea21]. However, a comprehen-
sive analysis of the performance and robustness of these programs is yet to be
conducted.
==PAGE:12==
2
Introduction
In 2022, Phillip Vetter rewrote the Ctsmr and introduced the CtsmrTMB soft-
ware [Vet23]. Similarly, an in-depth analysis of the sensitivity and robustness
of this newly developed estimator is yet to be undertaken.
The primary objective of this thesis is to conduct a thorough analysis and com-
parison of the general robustness and sensitivity of the Ctsmr and CtsmrTMB
estimators in estimating parameters for the Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process. The
specific research objectives are as follows: 1) To investigate the influence of
initial parameter values on the performance of the estimators. 2) To examine
the impact of bias in the data on the convergence and estimation accuracy of
the estimators. 3) To explore the relationship between the bias in parameter
estimation and the size of the random fluctuations in the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck
process as well as observation noise. 4) To assess the execution time efficiency
of the estimators. By conducting a thorough analysis and comparison of the
Ctsmr and CtsmrTMB estimators for parameter estimation in the Ornstein-
Uhlenbeck process, this thesis aims to contribute to the understanding of the
performance and limitations of these software tools, providing valuable insights
for researchers and practitioners working in the field of stochastic differential
equations.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
Multiple papers have been written on the uses of the Ctsm software ([JMM16,
Juh11, KMJ04]).In [KMJ04] the focus is on the estimation of unknown parame-
ters in grey-box models. And furthermore to introduce a "new" improved Ctsm
software. The paper outlines the difference from this to the MoCaVa tool, pre-
sented in [Boh01], and compares the results. The paper estimates parameters
of three variations of one nonlinear system with three different sets of data with
no, weak and strong diffusion and two data sets of a linear time invariant sys-
tem with no diffusion and with diffusion. The paper does not look into the
robustness of the influence of chosen initial values for the parameters. The the-
sis [Juh11] successfully implements the Ctsm in R language and presents a new
R interface for the software. Furthermore, the author parallelizes two loops in
the original Ctsm and tests the speed-up time on different non-linear models.
The best speed-up time observed is 10x. The R implementation from [Juh11]
is the Ctsmr software which is used throughout the present thesis. The paper
[JMM16] analyses the Ctsmr software and focuses on a thorough walk through
of the R package. The paper demonstrates how to construct a Continuous Time
Stochastic Model from multivariate time series data and how to estimate the
parameters within that time series. Furthermore, the paper demonstrates the
capabilities of the Ctsmr using three examples. As for the CtsmrTMB no re-
search has yet been published.
The literature contains multiple examples of comparison techniques for param-

Stochastic differential equations
Through the analysis the two estimators will be tested on some well-known
solutions to the general stochastic differential equation on the form
dXt = b(Xt)dt + σdWt.

Stochastic differential equations (SDEs) are a mathematical tool for modelling
systems which involves both deterministic and random components. SDEs ex-

cesses, which capture the inherent randomness or uncertainty in the system
dynamics. SDEs find applications in various fields, including physics, finance,
engineering, and biology [HJ16]. A stochastic differential equation describes the
evolution of a given system’s state over time when both random fluctuation and
deterministic phenomena are present. In (3.1) Xt is the state variable/vector
representing the systems state at time t. b(Xt) is the drift term, meaning that

3.4
The Extended Kalman Filter
The Kalman Filter is widely used in the field of parameter estimation and signal
processing. The idea behind the Kalman filter is to use a system model describ-
ing how the state of this system evolves over time and combine this model with
(noisy) measurements of the system to estimate the true state of the system at
any given time. The Kalman filter is a recursive process which updates measure-
ments of the inspected system as new measurements become available. It takes
in both the measurements, their uncertainty, the proposed (eventually updated)

Chapter 5
Discussion
For the analysis of the two estimators, many choices have been made. Such
as choosing the parameters for the Ornstien-Uhlenbeck process, choosing the
"standard" initial values, choosing the initial values to be tested for each of
the parameters and choosing the amount of system and observation noise to
test for. Changing any of these could lead to a different picture and different
conclusions. However, the choices made were believed to be decent choices to
base the analysis on.
For almost all of the tests, estimations with high standard errors were found. In
this paper, the reason behind the log-likelihood function giving these values was
not analysed. However, it could be a very interesting project for further research
to look into these instances and find the cause and maybe a solution. For this
paper it was chosen to discard estimates where the standard error for σy were
above 2 and for σx were above 4. 2 and 4 might seem like arbitrary numbers
and maybe they are. Of cause in cases where the standard error was above 100,
2 and 4 are very small values. However, when the true values were 0.01 and 1
having a confidence interval being 8 or 16 wide also very big. But the intervals
will also be very wide when the standard error is 1 and 2 respectively. Therefore
another interesting aspect to further research could be what the perfect break-off
value for the standard error for all four parameters would be.
Another interesting point which has not been analysed in this paper is whether
==PAGE:66==
56
Discussion
the combinations of different initial values have an influence on the performance.
Eg. would a low initial value for θ and a high initial value for σy cancel each other
out? Likewise one could raise the question if the "standard" initial values chosen
in this paper are beneficial values or if they are the opposite. This analysis would
cause an exponential number of cross-tests and therefore has not been done in
this paper. However, what could have been done was to take whichever initial
value that gave the best coverage probability for each parameter and a number
of simulations and estimations to see whether this leads to a higher coverage or
if the initial value was in fact correlated.
Multiple times throughout the analysis odd or unexpected behaviour in the
coverage probability occurred and whenever both estimators showed the same
pattern for specific initial values or noise levels it was interpreted as bias in the
specific data. This leads to questioning whether 500 simulations and estimations
were in fact enough. If more computational power were available, it would be
interesting to run 5000 simulations and estimations and see whether patterns
caused by bias in the data would still be visible. However, remember that in
order to investigate the influence of the initial value for one parameter means
running the eg. 5000 simulations 10 times. So raising the number of simulations
by a factor of 10 will increase the execution time by a factor of 100.
==PAGE:67==
Chapter 6
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to conduct a thorough analysis and comparison of
the Ctsmr and the CtsmrTMB estimators’ general robustness and sensibility.
It was found that in general the CtsmrTMB estimator was less influenced by
the initial values of the parameters to be estimated.
Most importantly, it was discovered how both of the estimators were extremely
sensitive to bias in data and that they would show it not by not converging
but by estimating extremely high standard errors for the estimates. This could
partly be solved by decreasing the step sizes of the simulations and observations,
meaning that if this problem is encountered with real observations, one would
have to collect data point closer together.
It was also found that specifically for the Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process there
would be a bias (overestimation) of θ linearly dependent on the size of σx and
σy.
Lastly, it was also found that the CtsmrTMB estimator was not only much
faster in terms of execution time than the Ctsmr but also much less influenced
by the initial values and sizes of σx and σy.
