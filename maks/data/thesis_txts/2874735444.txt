Abstract
Multiple sequence alignment (MSA) is a fundamental task in bioinformatics, supporting
downstream analyses such as phylogenetic inference, homology detection, and struc┬¡
tural modeling. Yet, evaluating their quality remains challenging, especially in the ab┬¡
sence of reference alignments. In this thesis, we present an image based framework for
evaluating MSAs that combines interpretable metrics with probabilistic modeling. Align┬¡
ments are represented as three┬¡color images which encode matches, mismatches, and
gaps, from which ten descriptive metrics are derived to capture conservation, variabil┬¡
ity, gap structure, and global blockiness. Using curated RNA families from Rfam, new
alignments were generated with Clustal Omega, MAFFT, MUSCLE, and T┬¡Coffee, and
ground truth labels were obtained from reference based Sum of Pairs (SP) scores. A lo┬¡
gistic regression model was trained on metric differences to output reliable probabilities of
which alignment is closer to the biological truth. Results show that the proposed approach
achieves strong discriminative performance (Ôëê74% baseline accuracy, and rising above
90% for confident predictions) while remaining computationally efficient and interpretable.
Compared to established reference┬¡free methods such as mean pairwise identity, infor┬¡
mation content, and the Transitive Consistency Score, the image┬¡based framework offers
a balanced view of alignment quality and reveals trade┬¡offs between competing metrics.
These findings show that visual representations combined with simple machine learning
can provide a practical and reliable way to evaluate MSAs.

Introduction
Multiple sequence alignment (MSA) is a fundamental task in bioinformatics, used in phy┬¡
logenetics, homology detection, and structure prediction [1]. MSAs are built by aligning
three or more biological sequences (DNA, RNA, or proteins) under a scoring scheme with
rewards and penalties. Evaluating the quality of an MSA is essential because downstream
analysis depends on the accuracy of the residue alignments [2].
When a trusted reference alignment is available, metrics like Sum of Pairs (SP) and the
Total Column (TC) score can measure the accuracy directly [3, 4]. However, in most
practical cases no reference is available, since the number of possible alignments is im┬¡
mense, making evaluation more challenging [5]. Existing methods for evaluation without
reference often lack interpretability and can be computationally expensive.
The goal of this thesis is to develop a fast and interpretable machine learning (ML) model
for comparing two MSAs by transforming them into three┬¡color images that encode matches,
mismatches, and gaps. This representation converts the alignment into a spatial structure
from which we derive descriptive metrics that capture conserved blocks, variable regions,
and coherent gap patterns. The resulting approach is both computationally efficient and
interpretable, as the features guiding the model are directly visible in the images.
The metrics are computed for MSAs generated by several state of the art tools (Clustal,
MAFFT, MUSCLE, T┬¡Coffee) and compared against curated reference alignments from
Rfam [6]. To reflect real usage, the dataset is filtered to include only alignments with more
than four sequences. The model is trained on direct pair comparisons, representing each
case as the difference between metric values for two MSAs. Antisymmetry is required so
that
P(A > B) = 1 ÔêÆP(B > A)
and logistic regression is well suited for this since it naturally models probabilities on a
symmetric scale. Finally, probability estimates are calibrated using cross┬¡validation to
maintain reliability on new unseen data.
This work makes two main contributions. First, it introduces 10 descriptive MSA quality
metrics with three┬¡color visualizations to illustrate their meaning and highlight alignment
strengths and weaknesses. Second, it proposes a logistic regression framework trained
on metric differences to estimate the probability that one MSA is better than another.
Beyond the metrics themselves, this work highlights the balance between them. MSA
tools such as MAFFT or Clustal must trade off among these metrics, where improving
one can and often will reduce another. These trade┬¡offs are often difficult to observe from
individual metrics but become clearer when two MSAs are compared directly.

Background
This chapter provides the background required to understand the work done in this thesis.
We first review the principles of pairwise sequence alignment and its extension to multiple
sequences, explaining the computational complexity and the difficulty of defining scoring
schemes. Next, we summarize how different algorithmic strategies form widely used MSA
tools, such as Clustal, MAFFT, Muscle, and T┬¡coffee, approach these challenges.
We then discuss the evaluation of MSAs, covering both reference┬¡based and reference┬¡
free metrics, and explain why defining a single universal measure of quality is challenging.
This motivates the use of direct comparisons, which reframe the problem as deciding
which of two alignments is closer to the biological truth rather than assigning absolute
scores. The chapter concludes with an introduction to representing alignments as images,
forming the foundation for the metrics developed in the following chapters.
2.1
Sequence Alignments
Pairwise sequence alignment, which is the alignment of just two sequences, can be for┬¡
mulated as a mathematical optimization problem. Given two sequences, we define a scor┬¡
ing scheme with rewards for matches and penalties for mismatches and gaps. Dynamic
programming algorithms such as Needleman┬¡Wunsch or Smith┬¡Waterman guarantee an
optimal alignment under the set scoring scheme [7, 8].
However, when we move from two sequences to three or more, the problem becomes
much more complex. In principle, dynamic programming can still find an optimal align┬¡
ment, but its time complexity grows as O(nk), where n is the sequence length and k is
the number of sequences, quickly becoming infeasible for even moderate k as illustrated
in Figure 2.1 [9].

Furthermore, choosing how much to reward matches and penalize mismatches, gaps,
and gap extensions is not straightforward. Different scoring choices can lead to very
different alignments, and there is no single scheme that will work best for all cases. Gap
penalties are designed to reflect biological patterns, favoring a few long gaps over many
short ones by using a higher opening cost and a lower extension cost [10, 11].
Even with the best scoring scheme, finding the optimal solution is infeasible due to the
time constraints. Thus, algorithms take a heuristic approach such as progressive align┬¡
ment, iterative refinement, and consistency methods. These heuristics donÔÇÖt guarantee
the optimal solution but can align hundreds of sequences in good time and be biologically
meaningful [12, 13, 11, 10].
2.2
Multiple Sequence Alignments
MSA tools arrange three or more biological sequences (DNA, RNA, or proteins) into a
matrix and maximize the number of homologous residues in the column. The resulting
alignments are extremely important as they are the foundation for many downstream anal┬¡
yses such as phylogenetic inference, protein structure and function prediction. For this
reason, development of efficient and accurate MSA algorithms is a mayor effort in bioin┬¡
formatics.
One of the earliest approaches was ClustalW, which was later followed by Clustal Omega.
Clustal uses a progressive alignment strategy. It starts with pairwise alignments, which
are used to compute a distance matrix. This matrix is used to construct a guide tree.
The sequences are then added subsequently following the branches of the tree. This
4

==PAGE:15==
hierarchical approach is computationally efficient but has a big limitation known as ÔÇ£once
a gap, always a gapÔÇØ. Early alignment errors propagate and are not corrected further on
[12].
As we have seen, progressive alignments start by constructing a distance matrix be┬¡
tween all pairs of sequences. This can be done with dynamic programming (Needleman┬¡
Wunsch) and then use pairwise identity to construct the matrix. MAFFT innovates in this
step and uses a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) to avoid full dynamic programming. By
representing sequences numerically as vectors and using FFT to compute correlations,
MAFFT reduces the computational cost of building the guide tree while maintaining good
accuracy. This allows MAFFT to scale up better than older versions of Clustal. However,
the newer version Clustal Omega incorporated HMM profile┬¡profile alignments which al┬¡
lowed it to handle very large datasets and remain competitive [13].
MUSCLE (Multiple Sequence Comparison by Log┬¡Expectation) approach starts like Clustal.
It begins with a progressive alignment based on an initial tree. However, contrary to
Clustal, it performs refinement iterations by rebuilding the guide tree from the current align┬¡
ment, realigning, and stopping when convergence is reached. This iterative approach
often yields higher accuracy, but it comes with a computational cost [11].
T┬¡Coffee adopted a new approach by introducing consistency┬¡based alignments. Instead
of relying on a single set of pairwise alignments, T┬¡Coffee combines multiple sources of
information, such as global and local pairwise alignments, into a library of residue pairs
[10]. The multiple alignment is then constructed to maximize consistency with this library.
While computationally more intensive, T┬¡Coffee often produces more biologically accurate
alignments, particularly for difficult cases involving distantly related sequences [10].
Together, these tools highlight the tradeoffs inherent in MSA construction. Progressive
methods are fast but prone to error propagation, while iterative refinement seeks to cor┬¡
rect these errors (at a computational cost), FFT┬¡based approaches provide scalability,
and consistency┬¡based methods improve biological accuracy at the cost of speed. This
diversity reflects the fundamental difficulty of the MSA problem. No single algorithm is
optimal for all datasets.

2.3
Scoring Multiple Sequence Alignments: With and
Without Reference
When a curated reference alignment is available, such as those provided by databases
like Rfam (for RNA), evaluation is straightforward. Reference based metrics like the SP
score and the TC score compare tool generated alignments directly to the reference [3,
4]. These scores provide a clear sense of accuracy, but in practice they can only be used
if the tool set of sequences is the exact same as the reference set of sequences. This is
rarely the case.
Most alignment problems therefore require evaluation with no reference. A wide vari┬¡
ety of no reference metrics have been proposed, each attempting to capture biologically
meaningful properties of alignments without relying on external truth. One common family
of methods relies on column conservation. Shannon entropy can be computed for each
alignment column, providing a measure of variability versus conservation. Columns with
low entropy (high conservation) may indicate functionally or structurally important posi┬¡
tions, while high entropy columns suggest little homology [14]. The overall distribution of
entropy values can be used as a summary of alignment quality. Mean pairwise identity
(MPI) provides a complementary perspective by averaging agreement across all pairs of
residues in a column. Both IC and MPI are fast and easy to compute, but they reduce
alignment quality to a single dimension and ignore gap structure and global organization
[5]. As a result, they may miss important evolutionary signals carried by indels. For ex┬¡
ample, MPI can be artificially inflated if an alignment introduces many gaps to maximize
pairwise similarity, even if this reduces biological plausibility.
Another approach focuses on gap statistics. Insertions and deletions are expected to
occur in certain evolutionary contexts. Thus, the distribution of gaps can provide insight
into alignment plausibility. For example, excessive fragmentation (many isolated single
gaps) may suggest poor alignment quality, whereas more coherent longer gaps may better
reflect real evolutionary events [5].
Consistency┬¡based evaluation takes a different approach. Instead of scoring columns or
gaps in isolation, it evaluates whether all pairwise relationships implied by the multiple
6

==PAGE:17==
alignment agree with one another. Typically, a library of residue pairs is first built from
global and/or local pairwise alignments. The final MSA is then scored by how many of its
aligned residue pairs are supported by this library, with higher scores indicating greater
transitive agreement. This principle was first formalized in T┬¡Coffee [10] and later ex┬¡
tended by the Transitive Consistency Score (TCS) [2], which measures how well each
column is supported by all possible alignment paths. Consistency scores are widely re┬¡
garded as robust indicators of alignment quality because they aggregate evidence from
the entire dataset and down weight uncertain regions. The main drawback is computa┬¡
tional cost. Generating the pairwise library and computing transitive consistency require
O(N2) pairwise alignments and scoring over all residue pairs, which can be impractical
for very large datasets.
Another approach is to estimate alignment reliability using statistical models. Posterior
decoding from profile HMMs, as used in ProbCons or FSA, yields probabilities for each
aligned residue pair [15, 16]. These can be aggregated into column scores or global
confidence scores. While probabilistically strong, such scores require a generative model
and are tied to the alignment algorithm that produced them, making them less practical
for external evaluation of alignments.
Overall, assessment with no reference is essential in practice but remains imperfect. En┬¡
tropy and MPI capture conservation but miss gap structure, gap┬¡based metrics capture
indel patterns but not column homogeneity, consistency scores are robust but computa┬¡
tionally expensive. These trade┬¡offs motivate the development of new approaches such
as those presented in this thesis, which aim to combine complementary signals into a
unified predictive framework.
2.4
How can we tell if MSA 1 is better than MSA 2?
Determining whether one multiple sequence alignment (MSA) is better than another is
a challenging problem. Reference scores such as SP can provide a clear answer when
available, but in most cases they are not. Moreover, even a mathematically optimal align┬¡
ment under a given scoring scheme may not necessarily be the most useful for a specific
biological question.
The fundamental difficulty in evaluating MSAs lies in the attempt to define a single univer┬¡
sal quality score. Such a score assumes that there is one ÔÇİbestÔÇØ alignment for any given
set of sequences. In reality, this assumption is problematic for two reasons.
First, the scoring scheme itself (match rewards, mismatch and gap penalties, substitution
models) reflects human choices rather than biological truth. For example, in RNA align┬¡
ments, using a simple JukesÔÇôCantor substitution model treats all nucleotide substitutions
equally [17], while a more realistic model such as GTR (General Time Reversible) assigns
different probabilities to transitions and transversions [18]. An alignment that is optimal
under JukesÔÇôCantor may not be optimal under GTR, yet both models are widely used in
practice.
Second, the usefulness of an alignment depends strongly on the biological context. For
example, phylogenetic studies prefer accurate gap placement that reflects evolutionary
events, because overly fragmented alignments or spread out gaps may distort inferred
trees. In these cases, we might accept slightly more mismatches in exchange for more
realistic gap patterns. In contrast, homology detection benefits from sensitivity, allowing
weaker or more permissive gaps to reveal distant relationships between homologs [19].
Structural modeling often tolerates suboptimal gaps in regions where maintaining core
Image┬¡Based Evaluation of Multiple Sequence Alignments
7
==PAGE:18==
structural features is more important than achieving the highest sequence similarity. Pre┬¡
serving residue to residue contacts can lead to better 3D models, and some studies show
that using suboptimal alignments instead of the single highest scoring one can improve
the accuracy of structure models [20].
Direct pair comparisons (MSA1 vs. MSA2) provide a way around this problem. Instead of
attempting to answer the question ÔÇ£how good is this alignment in general?ÔÇØ we reframe the
task as: given two alignments of the same sequences, which one is more likely to be closer
to the biological truth? This formulation has several advantages. The most important
is that it removes the need for a universal definition of quality, since now only relative
differences matter. It is this shift to relative evaluation, which makes the assessment
more robust and practical. Consider two families of sequences. Family A is biologically
gap rich, while Family B typically has few gaps. Suppose for Family A we observe two
alignments with gap scores MSA1A = 0.6 and MSA2A = 0.7, and for Family B we observe
MSA1B = 0.1 and MSA2B = 0.2. If we rely on absolute scores across families we would
conclude
MSA1B > MSA2B > MSA1A > MSA2A,
even though the score ranges are not comparable, as different families naturally produce
different absolute score levels. Direct comparison removes this bias by focusing only on
within family differences. For Family A we compute ÔêåA = 0.7ÔêÆ0.6 = 0.1, and for Family B
we compute ÔêåB = 0.2 ÔêÆ0.1 = 0.1. The learning algorithm sees two instances of a 0.1
improvement and can generalize what a ÔÇ£goodÔÇØ 0.1 improvement looks like, regardless
of absolute score scale. In this way, it learns patterns of difference rather than absolute
levels. This approach also enforces logical consistency. If alignment A is preferred to B,
then B cannot simultaneously be preferred to A. Formally,
P(A > B) = 1 ÔêÆP(B > A).
For instance, if the model predicts P(A > B) = 0.7, it must predict P(B > A) = 0.3.
Logistic regression satisfies this property automatically, because the logistic function is
antisymmetric: ¤â(ÔêÆx) = 1 ÔêÆ¤â(x). Other machine learning models such as decision trees
or random forests do not guarantee this relationship by default and may need additional
constraints or calibration to enforce it.
A probabilistic formulation is also more informative than a binary decision. Rather than
saying simply ÔÇØA is better than B,ÔÇØ the model outputs a probability such as P(A > B) =
0.55, which expresses that the preference is weak and nearly tied. If the model instead
outputs P(A > B) = 0.95, the user knows with high confidence that A is superior. These
calibrated probabilities allow researchers to trade off between precision and recall by se┬¡
lecting a probability threshold that matches their tolerance for uncertainty [21, 22, 23]. In
this way, the combination of pairwise comparisons and probabilistic modeling avoids the
challenges of absolute scoring while offering a robust, interpretable, and practically useful
framework for evaluating RNA MSAs.

Methods and Data
This chapter describes the data and methods used in this work. We first describe how
MSAs are represented and explain their transformation into a three component image
(gaps, matches, mismatches), which serves as the basis for a set of descriptive metrics.
We then detail the construction of the dataset from Rfam, including raw sequence ex┬¡

The entire processing pipeline, including dataset preparation, alignment generation, met┬¡
ric computation, model training, and evaluation, is publicly available on GitHub (Git┬¡repository),

3.2
Data Preparation
Our goal was to evaluate MSA tools on a large and diverse benchmark set. To do so, we
constructed a dataset derived from the Rfam database, which provides curated reference
alignments.
The goal was to evaluate MSAs tools with a diverse benchmark set. To do so, we con┬¡
structed a dataset that started with the curaterd reference alignments from Rfam. First,
we downloaded the Rfam.seed file, which has high┬¡quality reference alignments for thou┬¡
sands of RNA families in Stockholm format. We then split this file into per┬¡family align┬¡
ment files and removed families with fewer than four sequences (to ensure informative
alignments), and extracted ungapped raw sequences for each family. Using these raw
sequences, we generated new MSAs with four widely used tools: MAFFT, MUSCLE,
Clustal Omega, and T┬¡Coffee. Each tool was run with default parameters, and we stored

3.6

The final step in our pipeline is to construct a model that predicts which of two alignments

While logistic regression already outputs probabilities, they are not guaranteed to be well

raw logistic scores.

4.1
Metrics
The most natural reference for comparing sequences in an MSA is the consensus se┬¡
quence, which is defined column by column as the most frequent non gap character.
Once this reference is established, one way to imagine an MSA is as a simple greyscale
image with three colors: white, black, and grey. Each column corresponds to a pixel
position and each sequence to a row, so an alignment of m sequences and L columns
forms an m├ùL grid of pixels. The color of each cell is then determined by comparing that
sequenceÔÇÖs character to the consensus at the same position.
The first and most basic descriptors are compositional metrics, which measure simple
proportions of matches, mismatches and gaps with respect to the reference. However,
a major limitation arises. By reducing the truth of a column to a single consensus, all
mismatches are treated equally. This discards meaningful patterns. For instance, if ev┬¡
ery sequence consistently shows a different but shared substitution, the column is in fact
conserved, yet the consensus framework scores it as a set of mismatches. One possible
solution would be to avoid a single consensus altogether and instead compare each se┬¡
quence against several references chosen at random. While this could reduce the bias of
Image┬¡Based Evaluation of Multiple Sequence Alignments

consensus choice, it introduces new challenges. Which references should be selected,
how many are enough, and how to balance computational cost.
To overcome this limitation more directly, we require a metric that does not depend on a
single reference. This motivates the introduction of mean pairwise identity (MPI), which
measures agreement across all pairs of sequences. By doing so, it captures conserva┬¡
tion patterns that the consensus approach by itself might miss, distinguishing between
scattered mismatches and consistent substitutions. A related measure is the information
content (IC), which quantifies how strongly each column favors one residue over others
and thus provides a measure of certainty about the column. Combining MPI with IC allows
us to capture both the overall similarity across sequences and the strength of conservation
at each position.
Yet conservation is only one aspect of alignment quality. Gaps themselves contain struc┬¡
ture. Sometimes they appear in coherent vertical blocks, other times they are scattered.
This motivates the family of gap structure metrics, which describe both vertical correla┬¡
tions (GapAC, GapAC_lag2) and horizontal dynamics (GapOpenRate, GapRunMean).
Unlike other metrics that focus on local properties such as gap positions or column
consistency, LFPR captures a global signal. How match states are distributed across the
entire alignment. By transforming the match profile into the frequency domain, LFPR mea┬¡

profile, LFPR reflects the overall ÔÇ£lookÔÇØ of the alignment image. Smooth and blocky align┬¡

LFPR transforms a visual impression of alignment quality into a quantitative descriptor that
complements gap and conservation metrics.
These metrics begin with basic composition, then move beyond consensus to identity,
look at how gaps are structured, capture the blockiness of alignment patterns, and finally
consider alignment size. Each group builds on what the previous misses. In the next
chapters, each of these metrics is introduced with its motivation and examples.

SP compares each pair of sequences in the predicted alignment to the corresponding
pair in the reference alignment. For every pair of sequences, it checks whether the two
residues that appear in the same column of the prediction are also aligned together in
the reference. If they are, the pair counts as correct. The total SP score is then the
24
Image┬¡Based Evaluation of Multiple Sequence Alignments
==PAGE:35==
number of correctly aligned residue pairs divided by the total number of aligned pairs in
the reference. The resulting value ranges from 0 to 1, where 1 indicates perfect agreement
with the reference and 0 indicates no correctly aligned pairs. This definition ensures that
both the identity of the aligned residues and their relative positions are respected.
Alternative scoring methods exist, such as the total column score (TC), which measures
how many full columns are exactly matched, or secondary structure scores that consider
preservation areas in RNA. However, these alternatives have notable drawbacks for our
purposes. TC is highly sensitive and gives zero unless an entire column matches exactly,
which can be too harsh, especially for noisy alignments. Structure aware scores are
powerful but require detailed structural annotations that are not always available, and
they bias evaluation toward preservation rather than general alignment fidelity.
In contrast, SP offers a balanced trade┬¡off. It is robust to minor shifts, provides partial credit
for partially correct alignments, and does not depend on complete structural annotation.
Most importantly, we use the SP score as ground truth for supervised learning. Each
alignment is evaluated against the curated Rfam reference, and comparisons are framed
one to one. If alignment A has a higher SP than B, the label is ÔÇ£A better than BÔÇØ═¥ otherwise
the label is reversed. In this way, SP provides the reference of which alignment is closer
to the biological truth, while our descriptive metrics explain why.
4.2
Problem Construction
With SP as our reference measure, we can now compare alignments produced by different
tools. For each Rfam family, we generated alignments using Clustal, MAFFT, MUSCLE,
and T┬¡Coffee, and computed their SP scores against the Rfam reference. To turn these
scores into a machine learning problem, we adopt this formulation. When two tools pro┬¡
duce alignments of the same origin we compare their SP values. If tool A achieves a higher
SP than tool B, the pair is labeled as ÔÇ£A better than B,ÔÇØ and if the opposite holds, the label
is reversed. Ties are discarded.
We have therefore transformed the alignment evaluation into a binary classification task
where given two alignments of the same sequences, we want to predict which one is closer
to the biological truth. The input features are the differences between our descriptive
metrics, and the output is the label derived from SP.
Because the features are continuous, interpretable, and the labels are antisymmetric by
construction, logistic regression is a natural choice. More importantly, the antisymmetry
requirement can be enforced directly by removing the intercept term. This guarantees
that if the feature vector for a pair (A, B) is the negative of the vector for (B, A), then the
modelÔÇÖs predictions are exact complements:
P(A > B) = 1 ÔêÆP(B > A).
This property matches the way the training data are defined and ensures logical consis┬¡
tency across comparisons, something that other models do not guarantee without addi┬¡
tional constraints.
Beyond symmetry, logistic regression provides probabilistic outputs that can be calibrated,
making its predictions interpretable not only in terms of direction (which alignment is bet┬¡
ter) but also in terms of confidence. Equally important, its linear form allows us to inspect
the contribution of each metric directly through the learned coefficients, connecting model
predictions back to biological intuition. While more complex methods such as random
Image┬¡Based Evaluation of Multiple Sequence Alignments

==PAGE:36==
forests or neural networks could capture nonlinear interactions among metrics, they sac┬¡
rifice this interpretability and do not inherently enforce antisymmetry. For this reason, we
frame the problem as a pairwise logistic regression task trained on metric differences,
which simultaneously respects the structure of the data, provides calibrated probabilities,
and remains fully interpretable.
Before fitting the model, it is important to understand the structure of the metrics them┬¡
selves. The classification task is built on the difference between these variables, so their
distribution and correlations need to be explored.

Another perspective that adds to calibration and accuracyÔÇôcoverage analysis is to ask how

performance without a threshold.
The ROC curve plots the true positive rate (TPR, or sensitivity) against the false positive
rate (FPR) for all possible probability thresholds. At the left side of the plot, we only count

rising, but at the same time FPR also grows as more negatives are being misclassified.
A perfect classifier would trace a vertical line from (0, 0) to (0, 1) and then a horizontal line
to (1, 1), achieving an area under the curve (AUC) of 1.0.

initial rise indicates that many positives can be captured before FPR grows, confirming
that the model has meaningful discriminatory power.

Discussion
This chapter reflects on the results shown on Chapter 4. We first summarize the most im┬¡
portant findings of the work and discuss how the metrics capture complementary aspects
of alignment quality. We then consider the logistic regression model, focusing on what the
coefficients tell about the relative importance of the metrics and how probabilities guide

5.1
Overview of Findings
The primary objective of this work was to design a framework for comparing multiple se┬¡
quence alignments (MSAs) without relying on a reference. To achieve this, we introduced
a set of ten descriptive metrics that summarize complementary properties of an alignment,
ranging from simple compositional measures to gap structure and global blockiness. By
representing alignments as images of matches, mismatches, and gaps, the metrics can
capture not only the proportion of conserved residues but also the spatial organization of
variability, offering a more interpretable description of alignment quality.
The results demonstrate that the model achieves good discrimination, with a ROC AUC
of approximately 0.8 and well┬¡calibrated probabilities (Brier score Ôëê0.17). The accuracyÔÇô
coverage analysis shows that accuracy can be increased above 90% by excluding low
confidence predictions, confirming that the modelÔÇÖs probability estimates are meaningful
indicators of reliability. All together, these findings support that the combination of image┬¡
based metrics and logistic regression provides a practical and interpretable solution for
comparing MSAs.
5.2
Interpretation of Metric Design
The ten metrics capture different but complementary aspects of alignment quality. Basic
composition (match and gap fractions) reflects the trade┬¡off between conservation and
insertions, while MPI and IC add a clearer view of consistency and column conservation.
Gap related metrics describe whether gaps are scattered or form longer blocks, and LFPR
summarizes overall blockiness.
The regression analysis shows that no single feature dominates. Higher MPI and IC,
together with blocky structure (LFPR), support good alignments, while excessive gaps
or irregular patterns reduce quality. Importantly, the model combines many weak signals
rather than relying on one single metric, which makes predictions more robust across
diverse cases.
5.3
Model Performance and Practical Implications
The logistic regression model shows good discriminative power. It achieves a ROC AUC
of about 0.8, meaning it can correctly rank the better alignment in most pairs. Calibration
is also strong (Brier score Ôëê0.17), so the predicted probabilities are reliable indicators of
confidence.

The antisymmetry check confirms that the model respects the structure of the problem,

5.5
Limitations
The first limitation of this work is that it does not produce an absolute score for an individual
alignment. Because the model is trained to compare two alignments at a time, it can only
state which one is better, not how good an alignment is on its own. This is not a problem
when comparing candidate alignments from multiple tools, but it means that users cannot
directly rate a single alignment without a reference or a baseline alignment for comparison.
Another limitation is that the model was trained on Rfam data. While Rfam is one of the
most diverse RNA alignment collections available, it may still not represent every possible
alignment scenario. Finally, the method depends on a fixed set of metrics such as match
fraction, mean pairwise identity, gap statistics, and blockiness. If the difference between
two alignments lies in a feature not captured by these metrics (e.g., secondary structure),
the model will miss it.
A further limitation is that logistic regression assumes that each metric contributes inde┬¡
pendently and linearly to the decision. In reality, some effects may depend on combina┬¡
tions of features. For example, gap patterns may only become informative when conser┬¡
vation is high. Our model cannot learn such conditional effects unless interaction terms
such as ÔÇ£gap fraction ├ù MPIÔÇØ are explicitly added as new features.
In summary, this work favors interpretability and speed over complex absolute scoring.
By framing evaluation as a probabilistic comparison, it offers a practical way to judge
alignments. Future work could add richer metrics or more advanced models to further
improve accuracy.
5.6
Future Work
Several directions could extend this work. Future improvements could make the method
more context aware. Again, gap signals may matter differently depending on the level
of conservation, and local metrics could help distinguish conserved from flexible regions.
Richer models such as trees or boosting could also capture nonlinear effects while re┬¡
maining interpretable.
Another direction is to extend the approach to single alignments by comparing them to
a reliable baseline tool (such as MAFFT) and reporting the probability of being better or
worse. This would make the method more practical in everyday use.
Overall, future work should focus on adding context, testing slightly more powerful models,
and providing usable scores for single MSAs.

Conclusion
This thesis introduced an image┬¡based framework for evaluating multiple sequence align┬¡
ments (MSAs) without requiring a trusted reference alignment. By representing align┬¡
ments as two dimensional images, we were able to compute a set of complementary
metrics that capture conservation patterns, gap structure, and global blockiness in a way
that reflects the overall look of the alignment. This image approach allowed us to sum┬¡
marize complex alignment properties using a small number of interpretable features.
These metrics were used to train a logistic regression model that performs direct pair com┬¡
parisons, giving probabilities that one alignment is better than another. This probabilistic
formulation transforms alignment evaluation into a decision making problem. It does not
score alignments but provides a confidence value that can guide decision making.
Our results show that the proposed method achieves accuracy competitive with consistency┬¡
based scores such as the Transitive Consistency Score (TCS), while being computation┬¡
ally much faster. Unlike single descriptive metrics such as mean pairwise identity or in┬¡
formation content, the model combines multiple visual and statistical components into a
single decision rule. The use of image┬¡based metrics proves to capture global structure,
something that simpler metrics struggle with.
This work also highlights key trade┬¡offs. Direct pair comparisons avoid the challenge of
defining a universal absolute score but cannot rate a single alignment without a baseline
for comparison. Furthermore, the modelÔÇÖs performance depends on the set of metrics.
So, if two alignments differ in ways not captured by these features, the model may miss
the difference. Nonetheless, the approach provides an interpretable and scalable solution
that balances speed, and biological importance.
Overall, this thesis demonstrates that image based metrics, combined with probabilistic
comparisons, offer a powerful and practical framework for MSA evaluation. They offer a
middle ground between computationally heavy approaches such as consistency scores
or HMM and simple single metrics that fail to capture MSA quality fully. The result is a fast
and interpretable method that supports reliable comparison of alignments across many
families and can guide decision┬¡making in practice. This work sets the bases for adding
more features, exploring richer models, and developing practical tools that integrate di┬¡
rectly into alignment pipelines.
