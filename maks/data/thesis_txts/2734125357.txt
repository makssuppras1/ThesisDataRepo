Abstract
Ensuring the integrity of navigation sensors is paramount for safe maritime navigation. Un-
fortunately, cyber-attacks targeting satellite positioning information and onboard sensors
have exposed vulnerabilities, posing a significant threat to navigation safety. In near-coast
and urban environments with high traffic density and static structures, the risk of collisions
and grounding is elevated. Without a navigator’s sanity check of crucial information such
as position, heading, and velocity, cyber attacks could lead to accidents by compromising
accurate data.
This thesis addresses the challenge of enhancing the cyber-resilience of maritime surface
vessel navigation systems. Various sensor systems are evaluated, and auxiliary sensing
methods are proposed to create a GNSS-independent navigation system. The thesis
introduces two methods—point cloud registration and template matching—for an auxiliary
positioning system. Additionally, an observer is designed to leverage the existing array of
sensors within a framework supported by auxiliary measurements.
The developed navigation system is tested using real-world data collected in Copen-
hagen’s inner harbor. Despite the absence of GNSS measurements, the system pro-
duces sufficiently accurate positioning. The resulting position estimate demonstrates the
cyber-resilience of the proposed solution for GNSS-independent navigation.

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Ensuring navigation sensor integrity is a critical aspect of safe navigation. Still, cyber-
attacks that target satellite positioning information and onboard sensors have exposed
vulnerabilities, posing a significant threat to navigation safety [1].
While the protection of computer infrastructure, network, and radio communication serves
as the primary defense for cyber-physical systems, a secondary defense is imperative
due to the insufficient protection of most ships against cyber-physical infiltration. The
cybersecurity of the navigation system, coupled with the low cyber literacy of the onboard
crew, creates opportunities for cyber attackers to gain control of vessels.
The sensors available on commercial ships, such as global positioning system (GPS),
are known to be vulnerable and can be subject to jamming or spoofing [2]. Near-coast
and urban environments present areas with a high traffic density and static structures,
which carry a high risk of collisions and grounding. Without a navigator’s comprehensive
verification of fundamental information, such as position, heading, and velocity, cyber
attacks could present a high risk of accidents by compromising accurate data.
The future deployment of maritime autonomous surface ships (MASS) emphasizes the
importance of cybersecurity in global navigation satellite system (GNSS) integrity [3]. Ex-
ploiting existing information from available sensors can aid in generating positioning sys-
tems resilient against cyber attacks and faults. A cyber-resilient navigation system can
utilize auxiliary sensors and processes for graceful degradation in the face of system chal-
lenges [1].
This project will research methods and tools to achieve cyber-resilient navigation for au-
tonomous ships relying on a multi-modal sensing platform. The project focuses on inves-
tigating and improving the position estimation of ships in near-coast and urban navigation
utilizing multi-modal sensor inputs, thereby increasing robustness and resiliency against
cyber-physical attacks and faults.
1.2
Scope of investigation
This report aims to develop an auxiliary positioning system for navigation in coastal and
urban waters, adopting a multi-modal sensor fusion approach. This will, in turn, enable de-
velopment of a GNSS integrity monitoring system and allow for safe navigation in GNSS-
denied conditions.
First, an assessment of state-of-the-art solutions for positioning systems using multi-
modal sensor fusion is conducted to determine where current gaps and challenges lie.
Second, the report analyses and develops methods for making the navigation system
resilient against malfunctioning due to cyber-physical attacks or faults. A pipeline is con-
structed to produce auxiliary measurements of the vessel position.
The research and analysis are constrained to near-coast and urban waters where infor-
mation about static landmarks and shorelines can be utilized for estimating the ship’s
position.

1.3
State of the art - a literature review
While the automotive industry has extensively researched autonomous navigation, the
maritime domain is a relatively new area of focus. Significant advancements in this field
have only been made in the past decade in the area of MASS [3]. The area is still expand-
ing with contributions from researchers in various fields. Thus far, approaches to position
estimation have been sparse, with few works leading the research.
Furthermore, cyber-resilience for maritime navigation is an equally young and unexplored
field of research.
Hereon, the project will refer to cyber-resilience as fault-tolerance. Several advancements
in the past decades have been made regarding general fault-detection in navigation sys-
tems such as Blanke (2006) [4], Bosdelekidis et al. (2023) [5], and Dagdilelis et al. (2022)
[6].
A state-of-the-art study of ”Cyber-resilient multi-modal sensor fusion for autonomous ship
navigation” will be performed to ensure that this project contributes with new and mean-
ingful approaches. This chapter will overview the most essential existing research and
work done in the field. The preliminary review might be lackluster toward the end of the
project.
As mentioned, the scope of this project is to research multi-modal sensor fusion and fault-
tolerance in autonomous marine navigation. However, the review will also include notable
contributions from the automotive and robotics domains.
To the author’s best knowledge, no current solution exists covering the area of ”Cyber-
resilient multi-modal sensor fusion for autonomous ship navigation.” This chapter is split
into two sections based on multi-modal and fault-tolerant sensor fusion for navigation.
Based on the literature review, an approach will be chosen to advance the project.
1.3.1
Multi-modal sensor fusion for navigation
Position estimation is a heavily researched field in several domains of the vehicular indus-
try. The maritime domain, however, represents a novel field of research with few studies.
Some current studies propose GNSS-independent solutions [7] and [8] by use of onboard
sensors such as light detection and ranging (LiDAR), inertial measurement unit (IMU),
and radar.
Other studies propose solutions with sensor redundancy with pre-existent sensors on tra-
ditional ships.
In the automotive domain, plenty of work has already been done with multi-modality sen-
sor fusion for navigation. This study will focus on a few works considered most relevant.
Maritime studies:
Mapping and localization for autonomous ship using LiDAR SLAM on the sea
Authors: Ryohei Sawada and Koichi Hirata, Journal of Marine Science and Technology,

Japan (2023) [7]
This article presents a study of position and heading estimation of a small ship in a near-
coastal situation using simultaneous localization and mapping (SLAM) with LiDAR and
IMU.
The experiment for the study was carried out in the vicinity of Innoshima, Japan. A ship
was sailed in a short loop near the coast while simultaneous measurements were made
with LiDAR, IMU, GPS compass, and quazi-zenith satellite system (QZSS). The study
created a point cloud map of the coast using two SLAM methods: a normal distributions
transform (NDT) based without the IMU and using LIO-SAM [9] with the IMU. Subse-
quently, the generated point cloud was superimposed on open-source map data for com-
parison.
Results: The computed point cloud and the GPS measurements were compared against
the QZSS RTK-Fix positioning data as a reference. The results showed that SLAM-based
position estimation tends to produce more minor variations when compared to GPS. The
same could be concluded for heading. More concretely, the position error between the
best SLAM method and GPS can be seen in Table 1.1. The study concludes that within
600 meters of the coast, LiDAR SLAM is adequately accurate for, e.g., automatic berthing.

GNSS Independent Position Fixing using Multiple Navigational Features Registra-

Authors: Dimitrios Dagdilelis, Mogens Blanke, and Roberto Galeazzi, The Technical Uni-
versity of Denmark, Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark (2022) [8]
This study presents a method to estimate position independent of GNSS using radar and
the electronic navigational chart (ENC) in near-coastal situations. The study focuses on
near-coast navigation using simulated data from the Great Belt in Denmark. The radar
scans are perturbed with white Gaussian noise corresponding to a circular error proba-
ble (CEP) = 60m.
The study uses radar scans to detect static and dynamic targets. The stationary targets
from the ENC match the static targets, such as buoys, beacons, landmarks, and coast-
lines. The study expands on traditional triangulation and trilateration by including the
buoys as navigational landmarks to estimate the vessel’s pose.
Results: Without any prior knowledge of the ships’ pose, the results show a standard
deviation in both North and East directions of 12.6m, thus a worst-case CEP = 14.87m, a
four-fold improvement over the raw radar measurements. Furthermore, the heading error
standard deviation is σheading = 0.24 deg. The study provides ground for GNSS integrity
monitoring through its auxiliary positioning system.

Fault-tolerant sensor fusion for navigation
Fault detection is crucial to robust and autonomous systems, such as autonomous sur-
face vessels. Munim et al. (2022) [3] mentions cyber-security as one of the major focus
points of GNSS and automatic identification system (AIS) integrity in the future deploy-

faults or cyber-attacks. Some contemporary studies propose classic probabilistic meth-

Maritime studies:
A selection of notable research is listed below.
Fault-tolerant Sensor Fusion for Marine Navigation
Author: Mogens Blanke, Automation, The Technical University of Denmark, Denmark
(2006) [4]
This study addresses the topic of fault diagnosis in marine navigation. The included sen-
sors are 2 IMU units, a conventional gyro, the ship’s velocity log, and 2 GPS receivers.
The study proposes a systematic analysis through a parity space solution, including in-
cidence and dependency matrices, for addressing faults. A fault-tolerant sensor fusion
algorithm is sketched out as follows:
• Check sanity of input signals for signal age (timestamp) and outliers.
• Diagnose signals as normal or not normal.
• Make an optimal sensor fusion using a Kalman filter.
Furthermore, the data for these ships is collected asynchronously. Thus, the paper devel-
ops an automaton for dealing with asynchronously arriving data to decrease implemen-
tation defects. The change evaluation is proposed as a different version of a cumulative
sum (CUSUM) to avoid restarting the algorithm after faults.
Results: The complete fault-tolerant sensor fusion was implemented on several naval
vessels. Actual data arrived at 1Hz with a standard deviation of σf = 0.2 s. Furthermore,
implementation with a 30s GPS dropout showed that the algorithm could detect and ex-
clude faulty sensors without a significant drop in accuracy.

Background
This chapter will go into a brief description of the theoretical background needed. The
reader is assumed to have a general knowledge of linear algebra, basic probability theory,
algorithms, data structures, and Bayesian filtering.
2.1
Odometry
Odometry uses onboard sensors to collect data to estimate the robot’s or vehicle’s po-
sition, rotation, and movement. Cameras, LiDAR, IMU, and GPS are the most common
sensor types for odometry, specifically SLAM. Additional sensor types include, e.g., wheel
encoders, radar, and sonar.
A point cloud (set) is a finite set of discrete geometric points that are 2- or 3-dimensional.
Each point has cartesian coordinates (X, Y, Z). Often, but not always, point clouds are
sparse representations of real-world objects and can, for example, be obtained from
LiDAR or photogrammetry.
2.1.1
Point set registration
Point set registration, or point cloud registration, covers methods determining the trans-
formation between two point clouds. A wide selection of methods exist to determine the
rotation, translation, and scaling between pairs of point clouds. A rotation and translation
transformation, often called a rigid transformation, preserves distances between points in
both point clouds. In robotics, rigid transformations have the most applications.
Point cloud registration’s primary purposes include using the transformation to estimate a
pose and creating globally consistent maps.
Transformations that include scaling, also denoted as non-rigid, won’t be discussed in this
report.
2.1.2
Reference frames
Local east, north, and up (ENU) coordinates represent geographical positions, i.e., geode-
tic coordinates, in local tangent plane coordinates [22]. In a system operating within a con-
strained and small geographic area, where the earth’s curvature is negligible, it is often
advantageous to use local tangent plane coordinates for their simplicity.
Furthermore, several frames of reference exist for navigation applications: body frame,
navigational frame, and instrument frame, to mention a few. Body frame refers to the
vehicle of interest, usually attached at the center of gravity with the first axis as the forward
direction of movement.
The instrument frame is the frame to which the sensors are attached. This might be offset
from the vehicle’s body frame [22].
The navigational frame specifies the ”world” in which the vehicle is moving, for example,
the local tangent plane in ENU coordinates.
2.2
Complementary filter
The linear Kalman filter is a linear system’s optimal state estimation algorithm. In typical
navigation systems, using more advanced versions of the Kalman filter, such as the ex-
tended Kalman filter (EKF), is standard. The EKF, unlike the linear Kalman filter, is not
optimal. In data-reactive missions, the vehicle trajectory cannot be accurately predicted.

reference in seconds to the 1st of January 1970. In illustrations hereafter, it is denoted as
”time (Unix Epoch).”
3.3.1
Synchronization and processing the IMU data
The measurements collected in the campaign on the IMU are not synchronized with the
rest of the data collected. This means an additional preprocessing step was necessary
to synchronize the timestamps of the IMU. The timestamps from the IMU are Unix Epoch
timestamps; however, they are relative to when the IMU was turned on. Furthermore, the
IMU data was stored in .csv files of approximately 100 second durations each.
The time of each file’s last modification was available in the file’s metadata. Since the
data collection was semi-automatic, it was assumed that the last modification time corre-
sponded to the last measurement for that particular file. The relative time stamps in each
IMU .csv file were backward related to the file modification time.
Consequently, some files couldn’t be synchronized with this method due to an altered
modification time or inconsistent timestamps.
The primary disadvantage to this approach is the time of the last modification is only
known to a precision of seconds. The IMU data is collected at 400 Hz. That will lead
to imprecision in synchronization. More importantly, a practical synchronization between
the IMU and the faster sampling sensors such as the Compass and, perhaps, even the
LiDAR cannot be guaranteed.
Another important point on the IMU data regards the duration; each .csv file represents
100 seconds. A limited duration like this can cause inaccuracies or even significant tempo-
ral gaps between files when larger sample sets are utilized. This can significantly impact
the assumptions of the methods presented in Chapter 4.
Unfortunately, as seen in Figure 3.3, the IMU was mounted on a large steel plate with-
out proper calibration during the campaign, rendering the quaternion format orientation
measurements useless.

Ground truth
The dataset’s position samples are all collected using a regular GPS. The position mea-
surements will be used as ground truth to compare positioning methods. The same is valid
for the compass’s heading measurements, which are also used as ground truth. No other
more precise position and heading measuring methods are available for this dataset. It is
important to note that the GPS or compass cannot be guaranteed to provide as precise
measurements as other methods or sensors.

For each point cloud, each point’s nearest neighbors are determined using a hybrid K-

define the neighborhood of each point [29]. The K-D tree is a method for partitioning a
K-dimensional space into smaller sections. The smaller partitions make it fast to compute
the nearest neighbors. The nearest neighbors constitute a plane from which it is possible
to compute the normal to the point. Normal vectors pointing towards the origin are favored.
4.1.2
Global registration
An initial registration, also known as a global registration, is performed to roughly align
each pair of point clouds.
The global registration uses a heavily downsampled pair of point clouds using evidenti-
zation. Voxelization is a method for discretizing a point cloud into voxels. A voxel, like a
pixel in 2D, divides the space into discrete cubes in 3D, where all points within a voxel are
reduced to a single point in the box’s centroid (see also Figure 4.2). This is a method for
downsampling point clouds used to decrease processing time.
Figure 4.2: Illustration of voxelization of a point cloud
The normal to each point in the downsampled point cloud is computed using the hybrid
K-D tree method. Furthermore, a fast point feature histogram FPFH vector is calculated.

The FPFH is a 33-dimensional vector that describes a point’s local geometric structure.
A nearest neighbor search using K-D tree search in the 33-dimensional vector can return
points with similar local geometric structures [30]. The FPFH method, when used in point
cloud registration, is known to improve the registration results through added information
about the physical features [12], [11].
The global registration uses random samples and consensus (RANSAC) to sample n
points from the source point cloud. RANSAC is a method for randomly sampling points
from the source point cloud [31]. The corresponding points in the target point cloud are
determined by querying the nearest neighbor in the 33-dimensional FPFH vectors. Corre-
sponding points between the two point clouds are also known as correspondences. False
correspondences are rejected using two pruning approaches:
1. Checking if aligned point clouds are within a distance threshold
2. Checking if any two arbitrary edges (drawn from source and target correspondences)
are similar: ||edgesource|| > α||edgetarget|| and ||edgetarget|| > α||edgesource||.
Of the pruning approaches used, the former is empirically determined (see Appendix B).
The latter, with a threshold of α = 0.9, is taken from the empirical analysis of Choi et al.
(2015) [32]. Additionally, convergence criteria are set for the RANSAC global registration.
These values are also taken from the empirical study of Choi et al. (2015) [32]. One
criterion defines the number of iterations RANSAC can run, while the second describes
the confidence probability. The larger these are, the more accurate the results are.

4.1.5
Summary - point cloud registration
This section presented an algorithm for pairwise registration of point clouds. The algorithm
involved multiple steps to increase accuracy. Firstly, the point clouds were filtered and
then downsampled through voxelization. Local plane normal vectors were computed for
each point in the point clouds. Secondly, a global registration was performed on the
downsampled point clouds to achieve an approximate transformation. Thirdly, a local
registration known as Generalized ICP was used to refine the transformation. It was
demonstrated that it is possible to determine the relative odometry from transformations
between consecutive pairs of registered point clouds using a sequence of measurements.

Clustering the point cloud
The point cloud is recorded without explicit structural information about the data, making
it challenging to perform semantic segmentation [39]. Thus, a simple clustering algorithm
is implemented without additional knowledge about the point clouds.
The point cloud is clustered to determine which points in the point cloud represent note-
worthy features. The features selected for the ENC- and OSM-generated images are
generally prominent. Several points in the measured point clouds presumably represent
these prominent features (i.e., buildings, shorelines, etc.).
For this project, some simple but significant assumptions are made about the point cloud
clusters in the LiDAR scans.
1. Clusters less than a certain size, nmin, represent features without correspondences
in the ENC- or OSM-generated images.
2. The clusters radially closest to the ship (i.e., LiDAR) represent the shoreline, buoys,
or bridges.
3. The remaining clusters are structures on land, for example, buildings.
Given these assumptions, a density-based clustering, hierarchical density-based spa-
tial clustering of applications with noise (HDBSCAN), was chosen [40]. HDBSCAN is
a clustering algorithm derived from density-based spatial clustering of applications with
noise (DBSCAN) [41]. The working principle of HDBSCAN is to cluster regions based on
the density, i.e., the distribution of the points. Where the density exceeds a threshold, a
cluster is formed. HDBSCAN produces a hierarchical tree to explore all possible densities
of points.
The algorithm can produce clusters without the use of hyperparameters. It also labels

to outliers and noise in the point clouds. This clustering algorithm is well suited for this ap-

Template matching

between a large image and a smaller (template) image. Often, the smaller image is a
cropped version of the larger image. The result of the 2D convolution is a gray-scale

the large image. By converting the point cloud from 3D to a 2D gray-scale image similar to
the map-generated images, it becomes possible to match the LiDAR measurement with
the map.

4.4
Sensor fusion
The point cloud registration and auxiliary positioning system are helpful for their added
information about the ship’s pose. However, they are not very useful stand-alone. Thus,
a fusion method will be derived in this section to incorporate the registration, the auxiliary
positioning system, and the rest of the sensor suite’s measurements.
4.4.1
Error State Kalman Filter
The ship has a suite of sensors available (Table 3.1), and now, from the LiDAR, it has two
separate approaches to estimate its position and heading. The final solution should be
able to estimate the position and heading of the vessel, as a minimum.
The fastest sampling sensor is the IMU at approximately 400 Hz. Operating solely with an
IMU will lead to significant drift over time, which can be avoided by integrating absolute
position measurements using, e.g., GPS. For this purpose, it is possible to incorporate the
measurements into an ESKF that will be derived for an IMU-driven system. The ESKF is
an observer that estimates and corrects error states rather than the full state vectors of
a system. An ESKF works well for applications with more sensors with varying sample
rates and accuracy. An ESKF has a list of remarkable advantages [46]:
• The error state is always operating close to the origin. Thus, far from singularities,
gimbal-lock, etc.
• The error state is always small, which means higher-order terms are mostly negligi-
ble.
• The error dynamics are slow because the large-signal dynamics have been inte-
grated during the nominal state. This means Kalman filter corrections can be applied
at a lower rate.
The ESKF equations are derived from three state vectors: true- (xt), nominal- (x), and
error-state (δx).
xt = x + δx
(4.34)
The complete ESKF system is derived next. The ESKF, a complementary filter, is imple-
mented with a feedback configuration (also seen in Figure 2.1). The states of the model
are position, p ∈R3; velocity, v ∈R3; heading (aka yaw), θ ∈R; accelerometer bias,
ab ∈R3, angular velocity bias, ωb ∈R; and a LiDAR odometry bias, lb ∈R3. The specific
bias states are selected to help compensate for inaccurate modeling and measurements
from the true values. IMU measurements are known to have biases that either static or
dynamic offsets should compensate for. In total, the model consists of 14 states.

4.5
Chapter summary
This chapter has presented the three main methods used in the project to achieve the
objectives in Section 1.2.1.
First, a robust method for registration of pairwise point clouds was presented using RANSAC,
Generalized ICP, and a robust kernel loss function. It was shown that the registration can
be used to obtain a relative odometry in the ENU frame.
Subsequently, a method for generating images from map features was demonstrated. Ei-
ther ENC charts or open-source maps are used to generate an image that can be matched
with LiDAR measurements using a simple correlation method called template matching.
The final result of the method was a global position estimate without using GPS.
Finally, an observer, specifically the error state Kalman filter, was developed. It was shown
that while the IMU could drive the time propagation of the position, estimating a correction
step using the existing sensor suite or the aiding position methods can correct an accu-
mulated error. The error state Kalman filter can use many measurements to compute a
global position estimate.

happening, but analytical guesses have been made to explain the unexpected behavior.
5.4
Summary - results
This chapter has presented the results for the three main methods developed in Chap-
ter 4. It was shown that the point cloud registration could produce reasonably accurate
positioning estimates for periods with constant velocity. The results further showed that
periods with changes in velocity lead to worsened accuracy.
The second method, an auxiliary position estimate using template matching, was eval-
uated. An optimal angle for discarding occluded features was found to be θthreshold =
30 deg. Furthermore, with OSM and ENC, the two types of maps showed varying local-
ization accuracy evaluated on several sample sets. The OSM matching generated the
best and most stable position estimates. The localization produced by the use of the
ENC was able to produce accurate results. However, it wasn’t as stable.
Finally, the method for fusing sensor measurements was tested. It was seen that the
ESKF can produce accurate position and heading estimates when the GPS and compass
are used. Furthermore, it was observed that the ESKF produces adequate positioning
for GNSS- and compass-denial periods when using LiDAR and TM odometry. The ESKF
could maintain a low error rate in positioning and heading for long periods.
The ESKF was evaluated using GPS with a lower sampling rate than nominal. The test
showed that the system becomes more unstable with the lower frequency. Furthermore,
an attempt to increase stability by adding LiDAR odometry showed further deterioration
of the results. Several proposals for the cause of this issue were proposed without a
conclusive cause.

Discussion
This project incorporates simplified assumptions regarding the ship’s dynamics. Firstly, it
assumes that the ship remains consistently stable in the water without any heave. Con-
sequently, the consideration of heave is neglected, leading to the exclusion of perceived
roll and pitch in the analysis. This simplification may affect the accuracy of the results
obtained in characterizing the dynamics of the ship’s movement.
Secondly, the project assumes a water environment devoid of any currents, directly using
the measured heading as the course of the ship. However, this assumption cannot be
guaranteed in real-world scenarios. It is essential to acknowledge that this consideration
falls beyond the project’s defined scope and was not explicitly addressed in the analysis.
6.1
Considerations on methods and results
The template matching method demonstrated in Section 4.2.2 showed satisfying results
when used to replace GPS in GNSS-denied situations.
The current application of this method depends on GPS measurements, including com-
pass heading, to compute the necessary image for template matching. The map-generated
image is generated with knowledge of its center point. While, in a real-world scenario, the
position is not precisely known, this does not significantly impact the demonstrated re-
sults. Offline production of these images and a selection of GPS coordinates remains a
viable option.
A more substantial concern with this implementation lies in the rotation of LiDAR mea-
surements using the compass heading. The template matching method relies on images
oriented in the same direction to generate valid results. The actual heading may be un-
available in practical situations, given that the compass is part of the GPS sensor. How-
ever, the results indicate that the ESKF accurately estimated the heading from LiDAR
odometry even without the compass. Introducing an invariant version of the template
matching method could exploit the uncertainty in orientation, presenting an avenue for
improvement in future iterations of this work.

Conclusion
This thesis thoroughly analyzed existing research completed in the ”Cyber-resilient multi-
modal sensor fusion for autonomous ship navigation” domain with a state-of-the-art lit-
erature review. Additionally, the associated gaps and challenges were identified. The
research scope focused on developing an auxiliary positioning system to aid in the ab-
sence of GNSS.
The thesis successfully proposed and tested solutions for enhancing position estimation
via sensor fusion. Two methodologies, LiDAR- and TM odometry, were introduced for
estimating relative and global positioning. The LiDAR odometry was computed from a
sequence of pairwise point cloud registrations. The TM odometry used template matching
to associate LiDAR measurements with map-generated images from the ENC or OSM.
Demonstrating the efficacy of these solutions, it was established that accurate position
estimates could be obtained without relying on GNSS.
Furthermore, the thesis presented and developed a method for fusing several sensor
measurements to achieve position, velocity, heading, and sensor bias estimates. This
was achieved by leveraging the state-of-the-art estimation method, the error state Kalman
filter. The ESKF could fuse IMU, compass, and GPS measurements for accurate position
and heading estimates.
Benchmarking against ground truth using various sensor fusion compositions provided
valuable insights. The GNSS-independent sensor fusion, incorporating LiDAR- and TM
odometry, exhibited robust performance. LiDAR odometry fused with IMU in the ESKF
maintained adequate position estimates during shorter GNSS-denied periods. Notably,
the inclusion of TM odometry significantly improved accuracy over extended periods.
In summary, the developed navigation system demonstrated the ability to achieve local-
ization with sufficient accuracy even without GNSS. Consequently, the objectives outlined
in Section 1.2.1 have been successfully fulfilled, marking a significant contribution to the
advancement of cyber-resilient navigation for autonomous ships.
7.1

list of possibilities includes:
• GNSS-integrity monitoring system
• Improvement of point cloud registration
• Improvement of point cloud segmentation
• Scaling the result to larger sets of data
• Improvements to computational speed for real-time implementation

monitoring.

The registration performance cannot be guaranteed adequate without the proper hyper-
parameters. The most influential hyperparameters are:
• Voxel size for downsampling, vdown
• A global distance threshold for correspondences, dtg
• A local distance threshold for correspondences, dtl
• The robust kernel loss parameter, k.
The distance thresholds are related to the voxel by a distance factor (Equations (B.1)
and (B.2))
dtg = αcoarse · vdown
(B.1)
dtl = αfine · vdown
(B.2)
The Open3D implementation provides two ways of evaluating the registration numerically.
The first is inlier_rmse, a value between 0 and 1. This metric measures how large the
root mean squared error (RMSE) is for the inliers of the point cloud registration.
The second is fitness, which measures how well the two point clouds ”fit” after trans-
forming the source with the registration result. The fitness takes values from 0 to 1, with
1 being perfect fitness: #Inliercorrespondences = #Pointsintarget. It is also possible
to inspect how the accumulated odometry, Tn = T1 · · · Tn−2Tn−1Tcurrent, develops as de-
scribed in Section 4.1 to evaluate the registration performance.
The parameter pseudo-optimization was completed using set 7. The parameters to opti-
mize for were initialized in a region deemed suitable using [50].
The parameter tests were performed in 2847 iterations over this parameter space for
vdown, αcoarse, and αfine. For each iteration, the average inlier RMSE and fitness were
calculated. After that, the results were evaluated with three metrics: minimum average
inlier RMSE, maximum fitness, and a graphical inspection.
