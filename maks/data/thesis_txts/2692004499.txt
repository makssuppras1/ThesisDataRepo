Abstract
Muscle assessments are of great importance for various medical fields, including geriatrics,
neurology, rheumatology, and sports medicine.
However, the current golden standard
method of muscle assessment involves taking muscle biopsies and magnetic resonance
imaging, which are either invasive or of limited availability. Moreover, both require pro-
fessional clinicians in a hospital setting. The aim of this study is to assess the feasibility of
using non-invasive and readily available methods to assess muscle damage. These meth-
ods include musculoskeletal ultrasound imaging, multi-frequency bioimpedance (mfBIA),
myotonometry, and acoustic myography. The sensitivity of these methods in muscle as-
sessment was explored by inducing delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS) and muscle
fatigue in the m.gastrocnemius and the v.lateralis in a population of young, healthy fe-
males (20-30 yr). The non-invasive methods were applied pre, post, and 48 hours post a
strenuous exercise that mainly consisted of eccentric contractions. During the exercise,
signs of muscle fatigue were captured with acoustic myography. More muscle fibers were
recruited at a higher frequency with less eﬀiciency towards the end of the exercise. More-
over, DOMS was captured with increases in the muscle thickness, pennation angle, and
power doppler activity in the ultrasound, indicating edema and inflammation. In addition,
the bioimpedance measurements showed signs of loss of muscle mass and muscle integrity
and provided further evidence of edema. Finally, the myotonometry showed an increase
in the muscular stiffness that accompanied the perceived soreness. In conclusion, the here
applied non-invasive methods proved to be a feasible option in assessing delayed-onset
muscle soreness, making them promising tools for overall muscle health assessment.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Non-invasive methods enable the observation and evaluation of internal structures of the
body without breaching the barrier of the skin. Thus, these methods do not cause dis-
turbances to the body and carry minimal or no risk of adverse effects. Due to the many
advantages of such methods, it has become more prominent over recent years, replacing
previously invasive methods and opened up new possibilities of in vivo assessments that
were not possible before. However, although non-invasive technologies have been making
their way into the field of diagnostics and assessment, their full potential has still not been
reached.
Muscle biopsies have for decades been the golden standard of muscle assessments. How-
ever, muscle biopsies are a very invasive procedure that carries several adverse effects, such
as the minor risk of infection and the common effects of bleeding and discomfort (Meola
et al. 2012; Joyce, Oskarsson, and Jin 2012). Another standard method of assessing muscle
health is magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), a safe and non-invasive method. MRI has
shown to be a valuable diagnostic tool for the assessment of muscle tissue (Evans et al.
1998). Despite the many advantages of MRI, it is an expensive and time-consuming pro-
cedure and has limited availability. For example, Finland, the country estimated to have
the highest availability in Europe, has 2.7 units per 100.000 inhabitants (Eurostat 2020).
These two aforementioned methods provide a static assessment of the musculature. An
assessment of the muscle’s functional state is, however, equally important. Movement and
the electrical signals of active muscles have long been assessed using surface electromyo-
graphy (sEMG) (Cifrek et al. 2009; Adrian P Harrison 2018). sEMG strongest feature is
known to be its ease of use. On the other hand, it is also the cause of its most significant
weakness (De Luca and Kline 2011). Over the years, a lot of effort has been put into ad-
dressing the complex sEMG issues of noise, and interference, that affect recorded signals
from active muscles, making it diﬀicult and complex to interpret (Hermens et al. 2000).
Due to the importance of muscle assessment in various fields, such as geriatrics, neurology,
and sports science, the methods used should ideally be non-invasive, easy-to-use, and easily
reachable. Therefore, it is rational and vital to look into other methods that might provide
information about the muscular state more feasibly. In recent years, new methods and new
devices have emerged that show potential in measuring various muscle parameters that
could aid in muscle health assessment. These include the use of ultrasound to investigate
the structural state of the muscle, the use of acoustic myography (AMG) to observe the
functional state of the muscles, and the relatively new technologies of myotonometry,
used to assess the mechanical state of the muscle, and the multi-frequency bioimpedance
(mfBIA), that provides a way of measuring the metabolic state of the muscle.
The present study investigates the feasibility of applying these non-invasive methods to as-
sess muscle health by investigating their sensitivity in evaluating low-grade muscle damage
caused by strenuous exercise, namely delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS), and muscle
fatigue.

stiffness (Cheung, Hume, and Maxwell 2003; Cleak and Eston 1992; Gulick and Kimura

either the structural or the functional state of the muscle. The current golden standard
methods of assessing the structural state of the muscle following DOMS include muscle
biopsies and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). The use of biopsies and the following

Due to the limitations of muscle biopsies and MRI, ultrasound imaging has been proposed
as an alternative method in the assessment of muscle structure (Megliola et al. 2006; J.-Y.
Yu, Jeong, and B.-H. Lee 2015; Longo et al. 2016). Ultrasound is an attractive alternative
to muscle biopsies and MRI due to its non-invasiveness, availability, and ease of use.
Ultrasound imaging has furthermore been proven to be a reliable method in measuring
structural components of the muscle, such as the thickness, the pennation angle, and the
muscle size (J.-Y. Yu, Jeong, and B.-H. Lee 2015; Sarto et al. 2021). In the interest of
the advantages of ultrasound, its feasibility in assessing low-grade muscle injuries such as
the DOMS has been explored and compared against MRI findings (J.-Y. Yu, Jeong, and
B.-H. Lee 2015; Tagliafico et al. 2013; Longo et al. 2016). Yu et al. observed a significant
increase in muscle thickness and pennation angle of the m.gastrocnemius following DOMS-
inducing exercise (J.-Y. Yu, Jeong, and B.-H. Lee 2015). Muscle thickness and pennation
angle are variables that are highly correlated to muscle size, where an increase correlates to
the muscle enlargement observed in MRI studies (Takashi Abe et al. 2014). Furthermore,
a study by Tagliafico et al. showed a single case of increased echogenicity in the brachialis
muscle during DOMS (Tagliafico et al. 2013). The observed increase in the echogenicity
is considered to be correlated to muscle edema, where the edema is believed to create
an increase in interfaces resulting in more reflective echoes during imaging (Longo et al.
2016). Therefore, higher echogenicity has been proposed to showcase the edema findings
that MRI studies have reported.
Another promising method of assessing DOMS is the use of localized multi-frequency
bioimpedance (mfBIA). Bioimpedance is a well-known method that is based on how tissues
impede the flow of electrical current and is generally used to assess the whole body compo-
sition (Ivorra 2003). Due to recent advances in technology, multi-frequency bioimpedance
units have been introduced. These units provide a more advanced assessment of the tissue
and provide information about the tissue as a whole and at a cellular level (Ivorra 2003;
Else Marie Bartels, Sørensen, and Adrian Paul Harrison 2015; Else Marie Bartels, E. L.
Andersen, et al. 2019a). Its use in recent studies has helped identify aspects of muscle
injury as well as muscle health (Lexa Nescolarde, Núñez, et al. 2013; Lexa Nescolarde,
Yanguas, H. Lukaski, Rodas, et al. 2014; Lexa Nescolarde, Yanguas, H. Lukaski, Alomar,
et al. 2014; Else Marie Bartels, Sørensen, and Adrian Paul Harrison 2015; Pingel, A. Harri-
son, et al. 2020; Adrian Paul Harrison et al. 2015). Recently it was used successfully for the
assessment of muscle injury and recovery in athletes, as well as with individuals with cere-
bral palsy, in which several bioimpedance parameters were observed to differ significantly
between individuals with cerebral palsy and those of healthy individuals (Lexa Nesco-
larde, Yanguas, H. Lukaski, Rodas, et al. 2014; Lexa Nescolarde, Yanguas, H. Lukaski,
Alomar, et al. 2014; Pingel, A. Harrison, et al. 2020). Moreover, in theory, the edema
during DOMS should be detectable using the technology of bioimpedance. Edema is the
accumulation of extracellular fluid that would theoretically yield a decrease of the overall

An increase in muscular stiffness is one of the symptoms that has been reported to accom-
pany DOMS (Howell, Chleboun, and Conatser 1993; Whitehead et al. 2001). However,
it is only recently that the quantification of muscular stiffness has been made possible.
Before the introduction of these new methods, isokinetic dynamometers were applied to
quantify the stiffness. The dynamometers quantitively measured the overall passive joint
stiffness and were unable to isolate muscular stiffness from stiffness caused by other non-
muscular structures (Whitehead et al. 2001; Hoang, Herbert, and S. C. Gandevia 2007).
These relatively new methods, shear wave elastography (SWE) and myotonometry, can,
however, identify the stiffness of individual muscles. Moreover, they have proven to be
reliable methods in the quantification of stiffness and other biomechanical parameters
(Heales et al. 2018; Kisilewicz et al. 2020; Song et al. 2016; Sadeghi, Newman, and Cortes
2018; Agten et al. 2017; Ditroilo et al. 2011; Feng et al. 2018). The SWE method relies
on acoustic perturbations, called shear waves, generated with the ultrasound transducer
that propagates the tissue of interest. The velocity of these waves is related to the shear
modulus of the tissue, indicating its stiffness (Davis et al. 2019). SWE has been used to
assess the biomechanical changes in muscles following DOMS-inducing exercises (Agten
et al. 2017; Heales et al. 2018; Kisilewicz et al. 2020). Agten et al. (2017) applied SWE
to investigate the stiffness in the brachialis muscle following DOMS-inducing exercise for
females and males. In the population of females, a gradual increase in stiffness was ob-
served immediately after the exercise, which peaked 12 hours later. The population of
males, however, showed a slightly different trend, where the stiffness peaked 15 minutes
after the exercise, before gradually declining and reaching its baseline after seven days
(Agten et al. 2017). The myotonometry is another way of measuring the muscle’s biome-
chanical parameters that relies on the response of the muscle to mechanical perturbations.
The myotonometry has also been applied to investigate the muscular stiffness that ac-
companies DOMS. A study by Kong et al. (2018) observed a significant increase in the
dynamic stiffness of four lower-limb muscles 24 hours after a downhill run that induced
DOMS symptoms (Kong et al. 2018). Notably, the myotonometer can be used outside of
the laboratory setting, as it is a hand-held device, making it an attractive alternative op-
tion (Ditroilo et al. 2011; Feng et al. 2018). Regardless of the tool used to assess muscular
stiffness, increases in stiffness have been related to muscle injuries, suggesting that these
methods might be a valuable tool in the recovery of DOMS (Pruyn et al. 2012).
The natural response to an exercise is muscle fatigue. Muscle fatigue can be defined as the
decrease in the force capacity of the muscle (Hunter, Duchateau, and Enoka 2004). The
most standardized way of assessing muscle fatigue is by measuring the maximal voluntary
contraction (MVC) using a dynamometer at different time points. These measurements
give clear indications of muscle fatigue, where a decrease in MVC represents a loss of
muscle force (Wan et al. 2017). However, it does only observe the muscle fatigue after
it has occurred (Cifrek et al. 2009). Another method is continuously monitoring local
muscle fatigue using sEMG. The sEMG measures the myoelectric activity of a muscle of
interest and can provide evidence of muscle fatigue. These indications are, to name a few,
increases in the mean absolute value of the signal and a frequency shift towards lower
frequencies (Cifrek et al. 2009). However, there are many complications and limitations in
sEMG recordings and the analysis of muscle fatigue (Cifrek et al. 2009; Adrian P Harrison
2018). The main disadvantage of using the sEMG signal to assess muscle fatigue is its
composition of both neural and muscle depolarization signals. The sEMG is therefore not
always capable of capturing failure of muscle contractions, for example, if the failure of
the force generation is beyond the neuromuscular junction (Adrian P Harrison 2018). An-

Research Question
This thesis will investigate the feasibility of existing non-invasive technologies, i.e., ul-
trasonography, myotonometry, bioimpedance, and acoustic myography, to quantitatively
assess muscle health by investigating their sensitivity in evaluating DOMS.

2.1
The Muscle Tissue
Muscle tissue refers to a group of soft tissues that can contract and produce a force. Muscle
tissue is generally classified into three categories: cardiac-, smooth-, and skeletal muscle
(Hill and Olson 2012). The cardiac muscle tissue is only present in the heart, where it forms
the contractile wall of the heart, and the smooth muscle tissue is mainly found in the walls
of hollow organs, such as the blood vessels and in the organs of the gastrointestinal system
(Hill and Olson 2012). The cardiac and the smooth muscles are involuntarily controlled
by the autonomic nervous system (Hill and Olson 2012). In contrast, the skeletal muscle
tissue is voluntarily controlled by the somatic nervous system. As the name implies, the
skeletal muscles are attached to the skeletal system by tendons and connective tissue.
During a contraction, the muscle pulls the tendons attached to the bones and thus makes
movement possible. This process is called muscle force transmission and is essential for
human locomotion (Hill and Olson 2012).
2.1.1
Skeletal Muscle Structure
The skeletal muscle contains a rope-like hierarchical structure of muscle fibers and con-
nective tissue, further illustrated in figure 2.1 (Mukund and Subramaniam 2020). The
muscle consists of fascicles that are bound together with a sheath of connective tissue
known as the perimysium. The muscle fascicles are then further composed of numerous
muscle fibers, wrapped together with another connective tissue sheath, the endomysium
(Mukund and Subramaniam 2020). Each muscle fiber is then formed by a large number
of thread-like structures called myofibrils that run the length of the fibril and are com-
posed of sarcomeres connected in series. The sarcomere, further illustrated in figure 2.2,
is the functional unit of the muscle, responsible for the contraction of the muscle fiber,
and ultimately the whole muscle (Mukund and Subramaniam 2020; Hill and Olson 2012).

2.1.2
Contraction of the Skeletal Muscle
As previously mentioned, the sarcomeres are the functional unit of the muscle fibers re-
sponsible for muscle contraction. However, contraction in skeletal muscle can not take
place without stimulation. The skeletal muscle is consciously controlled and activated by
the somatic nervous system (Hill and Olson 2012). The skeletal muscle contraction begins
with a nerve impulse from the motor cortex that travels through the somatic nervous
system towards the muscle of interest. Briefly, this electrical signal leads to the release
and binding of acetylcholine, a neurotransmitter, at myofibril receptors. The binding then
causes a cascade of events that eventually causes a release of calcium (Ca2+) into the
sarcoplasm, which is vital in initiating the contractile apparatus. The calcium ions attach
to troponin, a regulatory protein blocking the binding sites of the actin, causing a change
in shape that moves the protein away from the binding site. The myosin heads can now
attach to the actin filaments, forming crossbridges (Petty and Cassidy 2005; Hryvniak
et al. 2021).
Before forming a cross-bridge, the myosin head is bound to adenosine diphosphate (ADP)
and phosphate (Pi) molecule. When the myosin-acting binding site is exposed, the myosin
head releases the Pi molecule and binds to the actin filament, allowing the sliding of the
two filaments to begin. The sliding is propelled by a rotation of the myosin head powered
by the chemical energy bound in their heads. During the sliding, the ADP molecule is
released. The sliding movement is then halted when an adenosine triphosphate (ATP)
molecule binds to the myosin head and severs the bond. The hydrolysis of the ATP (ATP
→ADP + Pi + chemical energy) and the movement of the head to its original position
make it ready for the next cycle of movement (Hill and Olson 2012; Hryvniak et al. 2021).
During muscle contraction, the muscle produces not only movement but also sound. The
muscle contraction causes vibrations that consequently produce pressure waves that can

The architecture of skeletal muscles can be defined as ”the arrangement of muscle fibers
within a muscle relative to the force generation axis” (Richard L. Lieber 1992). There
are various architectural differences between muscles, but the three most common muscle
designs can be seen in figure 2.3.

The v.lateralis and the m.gastrocnemius are examples of muscles that have a unipennate
muscle design.
The muscle architecture can be quantified using different parameters, such as the; muscle
fiber length, pennation angle and muscle thickness (Gans and Bock 1965; Carl Gans and
Vree 1987; Abe et al. 1997). The muscle fiber length, or the muscle ”fascicle” length,
represents the number of sarcomeres in series and is believed to be a major determinant
of the muscle function (Edman, Reggiani, and Kronnie 1985; Richard L Lieber and Jan
Fridén 2000). The pennation angle is also believed to be positively correlated with muscle
force output, as a larger pennation angle represents increased muscle volume and can allow
the sarcomere to exert their force at their optimum lengths (Rutherfrod and Jones 1992;
Fukunaga et al. 1997; Richard L Lieber and Jan Fridén 2000). At last, the muscle thickness
is significantly correlated to the cross-sectional area of the muscle, which is highly related
to the muscle strength (Abe et al. 1997; Ikai and Fukunaga 1968).
To obtain these parameters in vivo, imaging techniques such as MRI and ultrasound
imaging are applied (Richard L Lieber and Jan Fridén 2000; Ikai and Fukunaga 1968;
Fukunaga et al. 1997; Rutherfrod and Jones 1992; Agten et al. 2017). However, ultrasound

2.1.4
Skeletal Muscle Plasticity
The functional ability of the skeletal muscle is highly affected by its architecture. However,
it is worth noting that the architecture is not static but highly variable (Blazevich, Gill,
and Zhou 2006).
The skeletal muscle has a significant degree of plasticity that allows the muscle to respond
and adapt to its environment. It is, for example, able to change its mass, architecture,
i.e., pennation angle, and thickness, and metabolic activity in response to an altered
mechanical environment or intensity of muscle activity (Seynnes, Boer, and Narici 2007;
Alvehus et al. 2014).
A high amount of physical activity and exercise results in hypertrophy of the skeletal
muscle. Hypertrophy refers to the increase in muscle mass (Mccall et al. 1999). Favorable
metabolic alterations have also been linked to hypertrophy, such as an increase in size and
number of mitochondria, which further improves the aerobic capacity of the muscle (Wang
et al. 1993; Alvehus et al. 2014).
A decrease in mechanical load of muscle and usage causes muscle atrophy. Atrophy is
a decrease of muscle mass, often caused by immobilisation, aging, or, diseases (Willer-
slev‐Olsen et al. 2018; Zhu et al. 2019; Kilroe et al. 2020). Previous studies have shown
that atrophy coincides with a reduction in mitochondrial density, causing defects in oxy-
gen consumption and, consequently, muscle functionality (Kang et al. 2015; Ji and Yeo
2019).

of water (7̃5-80%). These factors contribute to the mechanical properties of the muscle
and cause it to be viscoelastic and anisotropic (Van Loocke, Lyons, and Simms 2006; Van
Loocke, Lyons, and Simms 2008).

The elasticity of soft tissues is highly dependent on their building blocks and the micro-

the contractile apparatus. In contrast, in the relaxed muscle, the passive stiffness depends
on the structural components of the muscle, such as the connective tissue encapsulating
the muscle fibers, weakly bound cross-bridges, and titin polypeptide linking the muscle
fibers Z-discs and myosin (Horowits 1999; Proske and Morgan 1999). Changes in muscle
stiffness are generally connected with some pathological changes, such as neurological con-

stiffness has been reported in the more affected limb of individuals with cerebral palsy, as
measured with shear-wave elastography (S. S. Lee et al. 2015).
The standard clinical method of assessing stiffness in soft tissues is with palpation. How-
ever, in recent years, new technologies have been emerging that have made it possible to
explore the properties of soft tissues. These include myotonometry, magnetic resonance
elastography, and shear-wave elastography (Ophir et al. 1999; Ditroilo et al. 2011). The
advantage of myotonometry is that it is a portable hand-held device that is relatively
cheap and easy to use. The technology behind myotonometry will be discussed in more
detail in chapter 2.3.3.
2.1.6
Muscle Fatigue
Muscle fatigue is a multifaceted phenomenon that can, in its simplest form, be defined as
the decreased capacity of muscle to produce force in response to contractile activity (Cifrek
et al. 2009; Wan et al. 2017). Muscle fatigue is experienced as an increase in the effort of
sustaining or performing a physical task. The cause of muscle fatigue can not be attributed
to any single mechanism, but rather various interconnected processes (Wallmann 2007;
Wan et al. 2017). It can emerge from different levels of the motor pathway, as is illustrated
in figure 2.5, and is commonly divided into central- and peripheral components (Wan et al.
2017; Wallmann 2007). Central fatigue refers to the processes that occur within the central
nervous system (CNS), causing a decrease in the neural activation of muscles (Wallmann
2007; Boyas and Guével 2011). In contrast, Peripheral fatigue refers to processes that
occur distal to the motor units, including the intrinsic factors of the muscle, that appear
in an attenuated contractile response (Wallmann 2007; Boyas and Guével 2011).

The processes involved in central fatigue include both psychological and physiological fac-
tors. The psychological factor incorporates, for example, lack of motivation which can be
caused by the discomfort that follows muscle fatigue (Wallmann 2007). The physiological
factors include the changes at the: primary motor cortex, the corticospinal tract, and the
activation of motor neurons. Decreased corticospinal excitation from the motor cortex has
been shown to contribute to the loss of force during isometric contractions (Søgaard, S.
Gandevia, et al. 2006; Boyas and Guével 2011). This decrease is still poorly understood,
but it is generally hypothesized to be caused by changes in the concentrations of various
neurotransmitters (Boyas and Guével 2011; Wan et al. 2017). Fatigue at the spinal level
can be caused by various factors that lead to a decrease in the firing frequency of motor
neurons (Taylor et al. 2016; Boyas and Guével 2011). The repetitive firing of motor neu-
rons during maximal contractions, or prolonged contractions, prompts them to become
less excitable to excitatory inputs, which decreases their firing rate (Taylor et al. 2016).
In addition, inhibitory muscle afferents (group III and group IV) increase their feedback
during muscle fatigue, causing a decline in the activity of motor neurons. These afferents
are believed to be stimulated by ischemia, hypoxia, and accumulation of potassium and
lactate (Taylor et al. 2016; Boyas and Guével 2011). Moreover, group Ia afferent neurons,
from muscle spindles, are thought to decrease their firing rate, causing an increasing presy-
naptic inhibition that further affects the decline of the firing rate of the motor neurons
(Taylor et al. 2016; Boyas and Guével 2011).
Multiple factors are believed to contribute to peripheral fatigue: alterations in the neu-
romuscular transmission, deficiency of the excitation-contraction coupling caused by im-
paired calcium signaling, the availability of metabolic substrates, reduced blood flow, and
accumulation of metabolites in the intracellular fluid (Boyas and Guével 2011).
Neuromuscular transmission is defined as the transforming of nerve action potential to
muscle action potential (AP). This process includes the release of the neurotransmitter
acetylcholine (ACh) into the neuromuscular junction (Oh 2014; Boyas and Guével 2011).

During fatigue, this process might be altered. These fatiguing conditions might involve; a
decrease in the propagation of the nerve potential at the nerve ending, reduced release or
depletion of neurotransmitters in the neuromuscular junction, and a decrease in the sen-
sitivity of the post-synaptic receptors caused by prolonged exposure of neurotransmitters.
A loss of action potentials would subsequently lead to force loss in the muscle (Boyas and
Guével 2011).
Excitation–contraction coupling is defined as the process that occurs between the genera-
tion of AP and the start of a muscle contraction (Wallmann 2007). The process involves
the generation of AP, which then propagates along the sarcolemma to the t-tubule, where
it induces calcium (Ca2+) release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) into the cytosol.
The Ca2+ then binds to the troponin making the formation of cross-bridges possible
(Wallmann 2007; Wan et al. 2017). During fatigue, this process is affected by the im-
paired release of Ca2+ (Wan et al. 2017). The mechanisms behind the impaired release of
Ca2+ are not fully understood. However, the accumulation of potassium (K+) in extracel-
lular fluid and increased phosphate (Pi) concentration has been proposed. Accumulation
of potassium (K+) in the extracellular fluid can lower the action potential amplitude,
causing a decrease in the release of Ca2+ from the SR (Adrian P Harrison 2018; Wan
et al. 2017). Furthermore, (Pi) might cause a reduction in the release of Ca2+ because it
can enter the SR and precipitate Ca2+ (Wan et al. 2017).
The availability of metabolic substrates is necessary for muscle force (Boyas and Guével
2011; Wan et al. 2017). Muscle activity relies on the availability of ATP for the genera-
tion of muscle contractions and relaxation through hydrolysis (Wan et al. 2017). During
prolonged or intense exercises, glycogen is the primary energy store for ATP production
(Wan et al. 2017). Glycogen depletion would therefore result in decreased ATP produc-
tion, leading to the inability of the muscle to maintain force production. Studies have
supported this relationship by showing the correlation between the glycogen content of
the muscles and time to exhaustion (Wan et al. 2017). Furthermore, the intake of glucose
has been shown to have a positive effect on endurance (Boyas and Guével 2011).
During physical activity, an increase in blood flow to the working muscles is necessary.
The blood flow supplies the muscle with the oxygen needed for aerobic ATP production,
removes by-products of metabolic processes, and dissipates heat (Wan et al. 2017; Boyas
and Guével 2011). However, during muscle contractions, a rise in intramuscular pressure
can cause compression of blood vessels, resulting in an insuﬀicient blood supply to the
working muscle. The occlusion of blood flow causes decreased oxygen availability in the
working muscle and is a well-known contributor to muscle fatigue (Wan et al. 2017). In
addition, the reduced blood flow causes the accumulation of metabolites as a result of
increased anaerobic metabolic pathways and incomplete removal of these by-products,
further contributing to muscle fatigue (Boyas and Guével 2011).
During prolonged or intense exercises, the intracellular medium changes due to the hydrol-
ysis of ATP and mitochondrial respiration. The concentration of ATP by-products, such
as hydrogen ions (H+), lactate, Pi, and reactive oxygen species (ROS), increases (Wan
et al. 2017; Wallmann 2007). The build-up of H+ and Pi is believed to impair muscle
force production by both disturbing the release of Ca2+ from the SR and desensitizing
troponin to Ca2+. The accumulation of lactate and H+, furthermore, causes a lowering of
the pH, which is believed to affect the contractile ability of the muscle even further (Wan
et al. 2017). At last, the accumulation of ROS is believed to contribute to muscle fatigue
through the oxidation of cell proteins, causing further disturbances in the release of Ca2+
from the SR.

It is apparent that the mechanism of muscle fatigue is multi-factorial and cannot be
attributed to any single cause. Instead, it is caused by the interplay of various mechanisms
occurring at different sites of the motor pathway. For example, a peripheral pathway, such
as insuﬀicient blood flow to the muscle, can cause accumulation of metabolites, which
further causes disturbances of the Ca2+ release of the SR, leading to a loss in muscle
force production. Moreover, the mechanisms of central- and peripheral fatigue are closely
connected. For instance, the accumulation of metabolites attributes to central fatigue,
causing a burning sensation in the muscles, which might cause a lack of motivation and
sensitizing of the inhibitory muscle afferents, resulting in the decreased firing of motor
neurons.

2.2
Delayed-Onset Muscle Soreness
Discomfort and stiffness in the muscles the morning after a strenuous and unaccustomed
exercise is a well-known phenomenon. This exercise-induced phenomenon is called delayed-
onset muscle soreness (DOMS) and entails symptoms of muscle soreness, stiffness, and
fatigue. The symptoms usually appear 12-24 hours after the exercise, peaking between 24
and 72 hours, before subsiding and disappearing entirely within 5 to 7 days (Armstrong
1984; Cleak and Eston 1992).
DOMS is precipitated by high-intensity and unaccustomed exercises that include eccentric
contractions (Talag 1974; Asmussen 1956). Eccentric contractions are characterized as a
lengthening of a muscle during contraction. It describes situations where an external load
exceeds the force produced within the muscle, forcing it to elongate while contracting
(Hody et al. 2019). Although eccentric and concentric contractions have been proven to
produce the same level of tension, fewer muscle fibers are recruited during an eccentric
contraction, resulting in high mechanical stress on the active muscle fibers. Muscle fibers
in eccentric contractions are therefore more susceptible to strain (Asmussen 1956; Stauber
1989).
2.2.1
Mechanisms of DOMS
Several theories have been introduced in an attempt to explain the etiology of DOMS and
its accompanying symptoms. These include the lactic acid theory, the muscle spasm theory,
the connective tissue damage theory, the muscle fiber damage theory, the inflammation
theory, the enzyme efflux theory, the bradykinin, and nerve growth factor (NGF) theory
(Gulick and Kimura 1996; Cleak and Eston 1992; Cheung, Hume, and Maxwell 2003).
The lactic acid theory suggests that lactate accumulation continues to be produced after
cessation of eccentric exercise (Gulick and Kimura 1996; Cleak and Eston 1992). The
build-up of lactic acid and other metabolites is believed to stimulate the nociceptors of the
muscles, causing the delayed pain sensation (Francis 1983; Cheung, Hume, and Maxwell
2003). However, this theory has, for the most part, been overruled. Firstly, concentric
contractions are more metabolic demanding than eccentric contractions but do not cause
DOMS (Asmussen 1956; Talag 1974). Secondly, several studies have made it evident that
the peak lactic acid concentration does not correspond with the delayed pain (Schwane
et al. 1981). Although the lactic acid might be responsible for the burning feeling during
the exercise, it is not believed to cause the pain perceived 24 hours later.
The muscle spasm theory, initially proposed by Vries, suggests that eccentric exercises
cause ischemia that further leads to the production of pain substances (Vries 1961). The
accumulation of these substances would subsequently stimulate the nociceptors, causing
pain. The pain would, in turn, produce more muscle reflex spasms that would prolong the
ischemia leading to a vicious cycle (Vries 1961; Gulick and Kimura 1996). This theory
was introduced following observations of increased resting electrical activity of the muscle
after eccentric contractions, reasoned by localized spasm of the motor units (Vries 1961;
Cheung, Hume, and Maxwell 2003). However, unfortunately, no other studies have been
able to verify this increased electrical activity following eccentric exercise or been able to
correlate it to the perception of soreness (Talag 1974; Abraham 1979; Cleak and Eston
1992).
The connective tissue damage theory, focusing on the connective tissue sheaths that make
up the structure of the muscle, originates from the idea initially proposed by Hough
(1902) that DOMS is associated with the mechanical stress propelled by the eccentric
contractions (Hough 1902). Since then, Amussen (1956) proposed that DOMS is caused

by the overstretching of the elastic components of the muscle, including the connective
tissue. He further reasoned that since the connective tissue does not possess the same
elasticity as the muscle fibers, it would be more prone to damage (Asmussen 1956). The
theory further suggests that damage to the connective tissue causes swelling, which leads
to soreness (Asmussen 1956). Further studies showing that soreness is mainly localized in
the musculotendinous junctions support this notion (Di Newham et al. 1983; Cleak and
Eston 1992). Newham et al. mapped the intensity of soreness in the thigh muscles using
a gridded sheet and a palpometer. The palpometer applied gradually increasing force at
chosen test sites, and the subjects were asked to indicate when the applied pressure caused
discomfort. This mapping showed that the musculotendinous junctions were the primary
sites of tenderness (Di Newham et al. 1983). Furthermore, a positive correlation between
urinary excretion of hydroxyproline (HP) and hydroxylysine (HL) and soreness has further
substantiated this theory (Sydney-Smith et al. 1992; Cleak and Eston 1992; Cheung,
Hume, and Maxwell 2003). HP and HL are amino acids that are components of mature
collagen, and its excretion provides strong evidence of collagen degradation (Stauber 1989;
Cheung, Hume, and Maxwell 2003). However, there is also evidence showing clear signs
of muscle fiber damage, indicating that the degradation of the connective tissue cannot be
the sole contributor to DOMS.
This leads to the muscle damage theory, originally proposed by Hough (1902) (Hough
1902). It proposes that the increased stress placed on the individual muscle fiber during
eccentric contractions causes weakened or susceptible muscle fibers to rupture (Hough
1902; Asmussen 1956). It is theorized that this damage causes stimulation of nociceptors
leading to the sensation of pain (Hough 1902; Asmussen 1956). This theory has been
supported further by considerable morphological evidence, showing structural damage to
the muscle tissue’s contractile component. Histological studies have demonstrated that
the micro-damages mainly include the Z-disc, showing both smearing and total disruption
of the Z-discs. Furthermore, it has been shown that type IIb muscle fibers are more prone
to injury due to their narrow and weak Z-discs (D.J Newham et al. 1983; Jan Fridén,
Sjostrom, and BA 1981; Fridén, Sjöström, and Ekblom 1983; Friden 1984; Macaluso,
Isaacs, and Myburgh 2012; Gulick and Kimura 1996). Macaluso et al. showed these struc-
tural damages with electron micrographs (figure 2.6) (Macaluso, Isaacs, and Myburgh
2012). Further evidence for muscle fiber disruption has been provided by blood measure-
ments of creatine kinase (CK) after eccentric exercise. CK is a muscle-specific enzyme and
is considered the best indicator of muscle damage (Clarkson et al. 1986; Cleak and Eston
1992). However, there is a discrepancy between the time-course of CK levels and reported
soreness, indicating that muscle damage can only partly explain the DOMS phenomenon
(D. J. Newham, Jones, and Edwards 1986; Cao et al. 2020; Cheung, Hume, and Maxwell
2003).
The inflammation theory is based on numerous findings of inflammatory responses, both
cell infiltration and edema, in affected muscle after repetitive eccentric contractions (Brend-
strup 1962; Friden, Sfakianos, and Hargens 1986; Mackey and Kjaer 2017; Cleak and Eston
1992; Cheung, Hume, and Maxwell 2003). Inflammation is the body’s normal response
to injury and is an essential step in removing damaged tissue and its repair (Medzhitov
2010). The rapid breakdown of muscle fibers and connective tissue and the accumulation
of noxious substances (i.e., histamine, bradykinin, prostaglandin E2) attract inflammatory
cells to the injury site. An influx of protein-rich fluid (exudate) furthermore accompanies
the inflammatory cells via increased permeability of the blood vessels (Medzhitov 2010;
Cheung, Hume, and Maxwell 2003). Eventually, the accumulation of noxious substances
or the osmotic pressure will sensitize the nociceptors causing a localized pain in the affected

is used to explain the DOMS phenomenon. These models start with eccentric exercises,
causing high tensile forces, causing muscle fiber and connective tissue damage. The dam-
age subsequently sets off an inflammatory process, causing edema and release of noxious
substances that lead to the stimulation of the nociceptors, finally causing the DOMS phe-
nomenon (Armstrong 1984; Armstrong 1990; Smith 1991; Cheung, Hume, and Maxwell

Ultrasonography
Sound is a vibration, a mechanical wave, that propagates through a medium and can be
heard by humans in the range of 20 Hz to 20kHz. If the sound goes above the audible
range (> 20 kHz), it is called ultrasound (Carovac, Smajlovic, and Junuzovic 2011). The
speed (c) at which ultrasound travels through a medium is constant and depends on the
density (ρ) and the acoustic impedance (Z) of the material:
Z = ρ · c
(2.1)
The acoustic impedance is the material’s resistance to bring its particles into motion
and is the foundation of ultrasonography. When a soundwave travels from a material of
one impedance to another, there is a reflection, generating the returning echo that gives
information about the examined structure (Carovac, Smajlovic, and Junuzovic 2011). The
impedance differs between different materials and tissues of the human body, as shown in
table 2.1.

Ultrasonography is a widely used non-invasive and non-ionizing diagnostic imaging tech-
nique that uses ultrasound to visualize internal body structures and organs (Carovac,
Smajlovic, and Junuzovic 2011). The ultrasound is generated by a transducer made from
arrays of piezoelectric crystals that send out and receive ultrasonic pulses. The piezoelec-
tric crystals can transform acoustic energy into electrical energy and vice versa, making this
translation possible. The transducer can then perceive the echoes produced when the prop-
agating ultrasound interacts with anatomical tissue. These echos are then processed and
used to construct a two-dimensional gray-scale image that distinguishes between tissues
of different absorbance and creates a mosaic image of lighter and darker pixels (Carovac,
Smajlovic, and Junuzovic 2011).
The ultrasound technique is also used to detect movements of tissues or body fluids. When
an ultrasonic pulse meets a moving target, it has a frequency shift, resulting in an echo
with a different frequency than the transmitted pulse. This phenomenon is based upon
the Doppler effect. Several types of ultrasound applications use this phenomenon, such as
Pulse wave-, Color-, and Power Doppler. The Color-Doppler is the most common. It takes
the direction, the speed, and the intensity of the moving target into account. However,
it is not very sensitive, which has disadvantages when detecting small vessels and targets
with a low-velocity flow. The Power-Doppler method does, on the other hand, disregard
the speed and the direction of the target, resulting in three times higher sensitivity. The
sensitivity of the Power-Doppler makes it relevant for demonstrating hyperemia in areas
of inflammation (Babcock et al. 1996; Bude and Rubin 1996).

2.3.2.2
Electrical Properties of Tissue
The electrical properties of tissues are depended upon their building blocks, which are
the cells and the extracellular fluid surrounding them. The extracellular fluid denotes all
body fluids outside of the cell. The extra- and intracellular fluids contain ions and proteins
and are considered as an ionic solution in an electrical sense, behaving as electrical charge
conductors. Conductors allow a flow of electrical charge. However, the cell membrane that
separates the extra- and intracellular media possesses dielectric properties. Dielectrics do
not allow a flow of electrical charge and can store energy. This characteristic originates
from the membrane being closed for the transfer of ions and only allowing lipids and
water molecules to pass through it.
This structure, formed by the extracellular fluid
(conductor), cell membrane (dielectric), and intracellular fluid (conductor), results in the
cell unit behaving as a capacitor. A capacitor is an electrical unit able to store energy.

The skeletal muscle shows viscoelastic behavior, as previously mentioned (chapter 2.1.5).
The viscoelastic parameters of the muscle and other biomechanical parameters, such as
stiffness, can be measured by recording the muscle reaction to a mechanical perturbation.
The muscle reacts to an impact by performing a damped oscillation, see figure 2.11 (Vain
and Kums 2002; Wilson, Wood, and Elliott 1991).
Figure 2.11: The damped oscillation curve as measured with the myotonometer. The short

This damped oscillation, along with the equation of motion of a damped spring model,
can be used to calculate the stiffness and other viscoelastic parameters of the muscle,
with a technology called Myotonometry (Vain and Kums 2002). The myotonometer is
a hand-held device that is placed on the skin above the muscle of interest. The device
applies a mechanical impact to the muscle and then records the following displacement
and the harmonic oscillation. An accelerometer transducer receives the raw signal from
the muscle, which is then filtered, leaving only the natural oscillation of the biological
tissue (Nair et al. 2015).
The myotonometry is a promising method of assessing the muscle condition objectively
(Ditroilo et al. 2011; Feng et al. 2018). For instance, Chuang et al. used it to observe how
the skeletal muscles of stroke patients respond to a rehabilitation program. Furthermore,

v.lateralis. The reliability of these muscles in resting position was shown to be excellent
(ICC = 0.93-0.99) (Bravo-Sánchez et al. 2021; Kelly et al. 2018). Because myotonometry
is a relatively new technology, pilot studies were conducted prior to the main study to

2.3.4
Acoustic Myography (AMG)
Skeletal muscle contraction generates sound. These sounds are audible and can be heard
by, for example, placing the palms against the ear with the fingers point upwards. The
sounds resemble the rumbling sound of heavy rain (Barry, Geiringer, and Ball 1985). These
sounds originate from the muscle contractions that produce vibrations or pressure waves.
When these pressure waves reach the skin surface, they are transmitted from the tissue to
air, forming audible sounds (Adrian P Harrison 2018).
Acoustic Myography (AMG) is, as the name implies, recording the sounds of muscles.
These sounds can be recorded using piezoelectric microphones that transfer the mechanical
waves into electrical signals (Adrian P Harrison 2018). AMG has been proven to be a
suitable method in measuring muscle force, activity, and fatigue (Oster and Jaffe 1980;
Adrian P Harrison, Danneskiold‐Samsøe, and Else M Bartels 2013; Pingel, I. T. Andersen,
et al. 2019; Adrian P Harrison 2018).
Moreover, new AMG devices using BlueTooth
technology offer an easy and suitable method of measuring muscle function.
AMG has furthermore been validated against surface electromyography (sEMG) (Barry,
Geiringer, and Ball 1985; Adrian P Harrison 2018). sEMG records the electrical activity
of muscles and has become the golden standard for assessing muscle functions (Stege-
man and Hermens 2007). These studies have proven that the AMG signal varies from
the sEMG signal because it measures muscle contraction solely. sEMG recordings are,
however, compounds of both the neuromuscular junction and the activation of muscle
fibers. AMG, therefore, provides the possibility of dissociation of the electrical and the
mechanical compound during muscle contraction (Barry, Geiringer, and Ball 1985; Adrian
P Harrison 2018). Figure 2.13 shows an EMG signal (upper trace) and an AMG signal
(lower trace) from the medial gastrocnemius of a subject performing a heal-to-rise activity
(Adrian P Harrison, Danneskiold‐Samsøe, and Else M Bartels 2013). The figure shows
clearly that the EMG signal starts prior to the AMG signal, showing the neuromuscular
junction contribution of it (Adrian P Harrison, Danneskiold‐Samsøe, and Else M Bartels
2013). The segregation of the electrical component from the muscle contraction signal
provides an attractive way of measuring muscle fatigue because the electrical signal could
be falsely interpreted as a successful contraction (Adrian P Harrison 2018).
Figure 2.13: A sample of typical acoustic myography and surface electromyography signals
taken from a study by Harrison et al. (Adrian P Harrison, Danneskiold‐Samsøe, and Else
M Bartels 2013). A: the sEMG signal, B: the AMG signal, from a recording from the
m.gastrocnemius during a heal-to-rise activity. The sEMG signals start prior to the AMG
signal, showing the neuromuscular junction contribution to the sEMG signal.

Methodology
This chapter describes the methods used to investigate the feasibility of non-invasive tech-
nologies in assessing muscle health.
The study was outlined as an intervention study,
where high-intensity exercise was used to induce DOMS. It was observed how different
non-invasive technologies, including ultrasound, bioimpedance, and myotonometry, cap-
tured the damage. In addition, the usefulness of an acoustic myography to observe muscle
fatigue was examined during the exercise.
The intervention study was conducted according to the Helsinki Declaration and was
approved by the local ethical committee of Copenhagen, Denmark (H-19024355).
All
participants approved to participate by providing a written consent before the experiment
(the document can be found in Appendix A.3).
3.1
Daily Management of the Study
During the time-course of this study, the author of this thesis was responsible for the overall
organization, including the planning of the study, a recruitment process, and day-to-day
logistics.
Planning: Prior to the onset of the study, several tasks were completed:
• Pilot studies were conducted to observe the use of the myotonometer (see chapter
2.3.3.2 for more details)
• The study was designed and a protocol was created (can be found in Appendix A.2)
• A pilot study with a single participant was conducted to inspect the feasibility of
the protocol, i.e., whether it was able to induce DOMS symptoms
• Familiarization with the applications of the devices used in the experiment
Recruitment: The recruitment process started by identifying a group of participants.
Due to the time limit of the study, it was decided to chose a homogeneous group of
healthy young females to avoid variances such as age and gender. The recruitment took
place by advertising the study in a local kickboxing class (Nørrebro Fighters) that included
potential eligible participants and by reaching out to acquaintances in the local area.
Day-to-day logistics: After finding interested participants, they were contacted via
telephone. The conversation included:
• Setting a day and time in consultation with the participant
• Informing them about relevant details of the study, i.e., the placement, and the
intervention
• Advising them to bring relevant clothes, i.e., shorts, to make the measurements more
comfortable, and running shoes, for the exercise
The day before the experiment, participants were contacted again and reminded of the
experiment and to bring shorts and running shoes. Finally, all participants were con-
tacted via phone to get information about their soreness and well-being between the two
measurement days.

Exercise Protocol
The exercise protocol used in this study to induce DOMS consisted of:
5 sets of:
20 Box-drop counter-movement jumps (height of box: 50 cm)

5
Discussion
This study aimed to explore the feasibility and sensitivity of non-invasive technology to
assess muscle health. This study clearly showed the potential of the presented methods
for muscle health assessments due to their ability to capture DOMS symptoms.
Fur-
thermore, the results from this study support the hypothesis that ultrasound imaging,
multi-frequency bioimpedance (mfBIA), and myotonometry each display distinctive fea-
tures of muscle damage after intense exercise. The ultrasound imaging can capture an
enlargement of the muscle, measured by an increase in its thickness and pennation an-
gle, indicating swelling of the muscle. In addition, the use of ultrasound power doppler
enabled the detection of increased blood flow. The bioimpedance parameters, reactance
(Xc), phase angle (PA), extracellular resistance (Re), and membrane capacitance (Mc),
changed significantly following the eccentric exercise, indicating signs of edema and muscle
damage. The myotonometry showed a significant increase of stiffness 48 hours after the
exercise in sore muscle (v.lateralis). In contrast, it showed decreased stiffness in the less
sore muscle (m.gastrocnemius) immediately after the exercise that returned to baseline
48 hours after the exercise, providing evidence of the relationship between soreness and
muscle stiffness.
Another aim of this study was to investigate the feasibility of applying acoustic myography
(AMG) to detect muscle fatigue. The acoustic myography measurements showed a high
inter-individual variability during the more technically complex movements in the exercise.
However, a trend towards higher variability between the S-score (indicative of the spatial
summation) was noted in the last set of the exercise, compared to the first set of the
exercise, indicating early signs of muscle fatigue. During a more simple movement in the
exercise, the calf raises, a trend towards decreased E- and T-score was observed, indicating
signs of muscle fatigue.

5.1
Increased Muscle Thickness and Pennation Angle
An increase in muscle size accompanying DOMS is well-reported. It was first discovered
by observations of increases in the circumference of DOMS-affected limbs. This increase
in circumference was believed to be caused by edema (Talag 1974; Friden, Sfakianos, and
Hargens 1986). These findings have since then been confirmed using MRI technology,
showing obvious signs of muscle enlargement during DOMS (Mandić et al. 2020; Megliola
et al. 2006; Evans et al. 1998). Recently, the feasibility of applying ultrasound technology
to observe DOMS-induced muscle enlargement has been considered. Muscle thickness and
pennation angle are two parameters that can easily be analyzed from ultrasound images
and are closely related to the muscle size, where an increase represents a muscle enlarge-
ment (Takashi Abe et al. 2014). Therefore, it was hypothesized in the present study that
ultrasound imaging would capture the muscle enlargement caused by edema by detect-
ing increases in these muscle size-specific parameters. This hypothesis was supported by
present study findings, which observed an increase in these parameters. An increase of 7%
was found in the muscular thickness for both the m.gastrocnemius and the v.lateralis, and
an increase of 13% and 21% was observed in the pennation angle for the m.gastrocnemius
and v.lateralis, respectively. This finding provides further evidence of the feasibility of
using ultrasound to visualize the DOMS-induced muscle enlargement. Additionally, the
observed increase in these size-specific parameters supports recent findings reported by
Yu et al. (2015). Yu and colleagues observed a gradual increase of the thickness and pen-
nation angle in the m.gastrocnemius after a DOMS-inducing exercise. A slight increase
was observed immediately after the exercise, and a significant increase, 48 hours after,
which corresponds well to the present study findings, where more significant increases
were observed 48 hours after the exercise (J.-Y. Yu, Jeong, and B.-H. Lee 2015).
It is, however, worth mentioning that the present study only observed a statistically signif-
icant increase in the muscle thickness for the m.gastrocnemius and not for the v.lateralis,
even though the v.lateralis was more affected by the DOMS, according to the perceived
soreness scores (m.gastrocnemius soreness: 0.7±1.1, v.lateralis soreness: 7.9±2.0). These
contradictive results might be explained by a methodological issue caused by an unsuitable
choice of depth setting of the ultrasound machine. The chosen depth setting caused the
problem that the lower aponeurosis of the v.lateralis was not visible for all of the subjects.
The m.gastrocnemius is more superficial than the v.lateralis and was therefore not affected
by this issue (figure 5.1).

5.2
Unaffected Echogenicity
Echogenicity (also referred to as echo intensity) refers to the ability of the tissue to re-
flect ultrasound waves, resulting in different pixel intensities in the ultrasound image. It
is measured as the mean pixel intensity within a defined region and is increasingly being
implemented as an index of skeletal muscle quality and composition (Stock and Thompson
2021; Watanabe et al. 2018; Sigrid Pillen, Boon, and Alfen 2016). The skeletal muscle has
a dark appearance (”hypoechogenic”), and the intramuscular adipose tissue has a bright
appearance (”hyperechogenic”) (Sigrid Pillen, Boon, and Alfen 2016; Stock and Thompson
2021). High echogenicity is indicative of low muscle quality (increased adipose tissue and
decreased muscle tissue) and has been associated with aging muscles and neuromuscular
disorders, such as hypotonia (Stock and Thompson 2021; Agten et al. 2017; Pillen et al.
2007). The effect of DOMS on echogenicity has previously been studied. It has been
proposed that the edema that accompanies DOMS would cause an increase of interfaces
that would subsequently produce reflective echoes during the imaging (Longo et al. 2016).
Moreover, a study by Tagliafico et al. (2013) showed a single case of increased echogenicity
in DOMS-affected brachialis muscle (Tagliafico et al. 2013). The present study hypoth-
esized that muscle edema would be observed with an increase in echogenicity. However,
no significant increase in echogenicity was observed at the presumed peak of the DOMS
symptoms (48h post exercise) in the present study. Nevertheless, the present study find-
ings align with observations made in a retrospective study by Longo et al. (2016), where
an increase in echogenicity was only observed in upper extremity muscles affected by
DOMS (Longo et al. 2016). As previously mentioned, DOMS is induced by strenuous,
unaccustomed exercises that are eccentric in nature. The muscles in the lower extremities
are used for ambulation and thus used for all sorts of activities during everyday life. In
contrast, the muscles in the upper extremities are not weight-bearing and therefore not
used as much in daily life, making it easier to induce high-intensity DOMS in them with
exercises. A trend towards an increase in the echogenicity was observed post-exercise (an
8% increase for the (m.gastrocnemius and v.lateralis). The increase observed directly after
the exercise could be regarded for as the increased blood flow to the muscles during the
exercise. The increase of blood flow might result in an effect similar to those of edema,
creating an increase in reflective interfaces.

5.3
Increased Flow
Power doppler imaging enables the observation of blood flow in tissue with high sensitivity.
The high sensitivity of the power doppler makes it a feasible option for the detection of
inflammation in the form of hyperemia (Babcock et al. 1996; Bude and Rubin 1996). The
present study observed a significant increase in power doppler activity post and 48h post
the DOMS-inducing exercise, supporting the hypothesis that an increase in flow would be
observed in DOMS-affected muscle. Longo et al. (2016) reported similar findings, where
increased flow, appearing as scattered pixels, was observed in of the 8 DOMS-affected
muscles in a retrospective study (Longo et al. 2016). The increase in flow immediately
after the exercise might also be explained by increased blood flow to the muscles during
the exercise.

5.4
Changes in Bioimpedance Parameters Indicative of
Edema and Muscle Damage
The multi-frequency bioimpedance (mfBIA) findings 48 hours after the DOMS-inducing
exercise showed a decrease in the following parameters; the impedance (Z), the resistance
(R), the reactance (Xc), and the extracellular resistance (Re), and an increase in the
phase angle (PA) and the membrane capacitance (Mc). Furthermore, the bioimpedance
parameters measured in this study, from the m.gastrocnemius and v.lateralis of healthy
females, match values obtained in two separate studies, one gender-matched and one
age- and gender-matched study, that consisted of handball athletes (Else Marie Bartels,
Sørensen, and Adrian Paul Harrison 2015; Else Marie Bartels, E. L. Andersen, et al.
2019a).
A decrease in impedance (Z), although not significant, was observed for the m.gastrocnemius
and the v.lateralis 48 post-exercise (m.gastrocnemius: 16 %, v.lateralis: 8 %). Further-
more, a significant decrease of 32% in the reactance (Xc) was observed in the m.gastrocnemius.
However, no change in reactance (Xc) was observed in the v.lateralis. The impedance (Z)
is representative of muscle mass, while the reactance (Xc) provides information about the
mass, integrity, and function of the cell membrane (Else Marie Bartels, Sørensen, and
Adrian Paul Harrison 2015; Lexa Nescolarde, Núñez, et al. 2013). Therefore, a decrease in
these parameters does collectively provide evidence of loss of muscle mass and muscle dam-
age. This observation is in line with findings from multiple morphological studies that have
shown the microstructural damage during DOMS (D.J Newham et al. 1983; Jan Fridén,
Sjostrom, and BA 1981; Fridén, Sjöström, and Ekblom 1983; Friden 1984; Macaluso,
Isaacs, and Myburgh 2012; Gulick and Kimura 1996). These findings might indicate the
temporary decrease in the muscle mass that occurs before the fibers are restored by the
satellite cells during the recovery period of DOMS (Mackey and Kjaer 2017). A decrease
in the reactance (Xc) has, moreover, been reported in response to muscle injury in studies
conducted by Nescolarde et al. (2011, 2013, 2014a, , 2014b) (Lexa Nescolarde, Yanguas,
Medina, et al. 2011; Nescolarde et al. 2013; Lexa Nescolarde, Yanguas, H. Lukaski, Rodas,
et al. 2014; Lexa Nescolarde, Yanguas, H. Lukaski, Alomar, et al. 2014). Nescolarde et
al. (2014b) investigated the change in the bioimpedance components in a group of muscle
injuries of different severity (grade I, grade II and grade III). Nescolarde and colleagues
observed that a decrease in the reactance (Xc) parameter was consistent with the severity
of the muscle injury compared to pre-injury values, i.e., more severe muscle injuries re-
sulted in a more significant decrease in the parameter. The injuries were classified based
on radiological assessments, where the grade I injury (defined as a minor muscle strain or
minor injury) resulted in a mean decrease of 17.5 % and a grade II injury (defined as mod-
erate tissue damage) resulted in a mean decrease of 32.9 % (Lexa Nescolarde, Yanguas,
H. Lukaski, Alomar, et al. 2014). Although DOMS injuries are classified as minor, the
decrease in the reactance (Xc) of the m.gastrocnemius correlated better with the grade II
injuries when compared to the study by Nescolarde et al. (2014b).
A decrease in the bioimpedance parameters resistance (R) and the extracellular resistance
(Re) were observed in response to the DOMS-inducing exercise.
The decrease in the
resistance (R) parameter, however not significant, was 16 % for the m.gastrocnemius and
8 % for the v.lateralis. A significant decrease of 17 % was observed in the extracellular
resistance (Re) for the m.gastrocnemius and a trend towards a decrease (of 6 %) for the
v.lateralis. The resistance (R) and the extracellular resistance (Re) are parameters that
are representative of the relative hypotonic state around muscle cells (Ivorra 2003; Else
Marie Bartels, E. L. Andersen, et al. 2019a). A decrease in these resistance parameters
represents an increase of fluid around the cells and indicates edema. These results comply
56

==PAGE:71==
with the ultrasound findings that provided evidence of edema and supported the present
study hypothesis that the bioimpedance measurements would be sensitive enough to detect
the edema. Furthermore, these results are in accordance with the studies by Nescolarde
et al.
(2013, 2014a, 2014b) that investigated muscle injuries and reported a decrease
in the bioimpedance parameter, resistance (R), (ranging from 11 - 31 %) in response to
muscle injuries in a population of soccer players (Nescolarde et al. 2013; Lexa Nescolarde,
Yanguas, H. Lukaski, Rodas, et al. 2014; Lexa Nescolarde, Yanguas, H. Lukaski, Alomar,
et al. 2014).
A significant increase of 12 % of the phase angle (PA) was noted in the v.lateralis 48 hours
post the exercise. The phase angle (PA) is the component of the geometric relationship
between the resistance (R) and the reactance (Xc) (arctan(Xc/R)). Thereby, the phase
angle (PA) is positively correlated with the reactance (Xc) and negatively correlated with
the resistance (R) and, by definition, associated with muscle health (Gupta et al. 2008;
Pingel, A. Harrison, et al. 2020; Else Marie Bartels, E. L. Andersen, et al. 2019a). There-
fore, an increase in phase angle might indicate improved health in the muscles, presented
with cellular health and integrity. However, as noted in studies by Harrison et al. (2015)
and Bartels et al. (2015), caution should be taken in the interpretation of this parameter
(Adrian Paul Harrison et al. 2015; Else Marie Bartels, E. L. Andersen, et al. 2019a). As
previously mentioned, the phase angle (PA) is dependent upon two variables, the reac-
tance (Xc) and the resistance (R), meaning that if only one of these variables changes
(and not the other), it can result in a false increase or a decrease (Adrian Paul Harrison
et al. 2015; Else Marie Bartels, E. L. Andersen, et al. 2019a). In the present study, the
increase in phase angle (PA) in the v.lateralis 48h post-exercise can be regarded as a false
increase, as it is influenced by a decrease in the resistance (R) and an unchanged value in
the reactance (Xc) (see figure 5.3). The findings in the present study further highlight the
importance of considering the changes in phase angle (PA) with regards to the changes in
the resistance (R) and reactance (Xc).

5.5
The relation between edema and soreness
The bioimpedance results showed more evidence of edema and muscle damage in the less
sore muscle, the m.gastrocnemius, than in the sore muscle, the v.lateralis. Several reasons
might explain this contradictive finding.
First of all, the relationship between soreness and edema has been questioned because of
the inconsistent time course of the edema compared to that of DOMS (Gulick and Kimura
1996). The primary evidence for this is that signs of muscle enlargement, indicating edema,
are evident immediately after the exercise, before the onset of muscle soreness, which
usually appears after 12-24 hours. A recent study by Agten et al. (2016) has furthermore
observed that the muscle edema persists after the disappearance of the soreness sensation
(> 7days after the DOMS-inducing exercise) (Agten et al. 2017). Because of this, it has
58
The Feasibility of Non-Invasive Technology in Muscle Health Assessment
==PAGE:73==
been proposed that it is not solely the edema that produces soreness. In the present study,
an increase of the mean in the muscle thickness (m.gastrocnemius: 6%, v.lateralis: 4%)
and pennation angle (m.gastrocnemius: 8%, v.lateralis: 12%) was observed immediately
post-exercise, before the onset of soreness. Similar findings were observed in a study done
by Yu et al. (2015), where an increase in the mean m.gastrocnemius thickness (9%) and
pennation angle (3%) were observed immediately post-exercise (J.-Y. Yu, Jeong, and B.-H.
Lee 2015). However, the increase in muscle thickness immediately after the exercise could
be produced by increased blood flow to the muscle, i.e., exercise hyperemia. Moreover,
several studies have observed that the peak edema coincides with peak soreness levels,
making this hypothesis disputable (Evans et al. 1998; Cheung, Hume, and Maxwell 2003).
Another reason might be that the high level of pain felt in the v.lateralis (mean soreness
score at 48 hours: 8.2 ± 1.5) could result in a change in gait where the v.lateralis would
be used to a lesser extent as a coping mechanism. As a consequence, the m.gastrocnemius
might be overused. The overuse would consequently lead to a longer recovery time of the
m.gastrocnemius. Altered kinematics have previously been reported as a consequence of
DOMS (Hyldahl and Hubal 2014; Paquette et al. 2017). A recent study by Paquette et al.
(2017) investigated which effect DOMS in the knee extensors (including the v.lateralis)
had on the lower limb kinematics during running. The study concluded that knee flexion
range of motion during stance was reduced during the period of DOMS (Paquette et al.
2017). This change in gait might cause less activation of quadriceps muscle but whether
the m.gastrocnemius muscle would compensate for that disuse is unclear.
Finally, the small sample size and the muscles’ different compositions might contribute
to these contradictory findings. However, the strong evidence for muscle edema in the
m.gastrocnemius provided by both the ultrasound analysis and the bioimpedance mea-
surements and lack of soreness undeniably supports the theory that edema might not be
the primary cause of soreness.

5.6
Different Mechanical Responses
Changes in the mechanical properties of muscles have been reported to accompany DOMS
(Hoang, Herbert, and S. C. Gandevia 2007). The assessment of the mechanical properties
of individual muscles has relatively recently been made possible with the emergence of new
methods, such as elastography and myotonometry (Lacourpaille et al. 2012; Ditroilo et al.
2011). This study applied myotonometry to investigate the biomechanical and viscoelastic
changes in the m.gastrocnemius and v.lateralis following DOMS-inducing exercise. It was
hypothesized that increased muscle stiffness would be observed at the presumed peak of
the DOMS symptoms, i.e., 48 hours after the DOMS-inducing exercise. This increase in
stiffness was observed in the sore muscle, the v.lateralis. However, the less sore muscle, the
m.gastrocnemius, presented a different trend, with a drop in stiffness immediately after
the exercise that returned to its baseline 48 hours after the exercise.
5.6.1
Increase in Muscular Stiffness in the Sore Muscle
The passive stiffness in the v.lateralis increased significantly, of a mean 11 %, at the peak
of the DOMS symptoms (48 hours post-exercise, at a mean soreness score of 7.9/10). This
finding is in coherence with several studies that have applied myotonometer to investigate
the mechanical response of the muscle during DOMS, following eccentric exercises (Kong
et al. 2018; Janecki et al. 2011). Janecki et al. (2011) observed an increase in stiffness
in the biceps brachii following a single bout of eccentric exercise. The stiffness increased
immediately after the exercise and peaked 24 hours after the exercise (Janecki et al.
2011).
Furthermore, a study by Kong et al.
(2018) observed a significant increase in
muscle stiffness in four lower limb muscles 24 hours after a DOMS-inducing exercise (a
downhill running) that remained elevated for up to 4 days post the exercise (Kong et al.
2018). The physiological mechanisms behind the increase in muscular hardness cannot
be concluded with the findings in the present study, but several mechanisms have been
proposed. Whitehead et al. proposed that the hardening of the muscle is due to an ”injury
contracture” caused by an increase of Ca2+ in the sarcoplasm (Whitehead et al. 2001).
An increase in the intramuscular pressure caused by edema has also been proposed to
be the cause of the increased stiffness (Hoang, Herbert, and S. C. Gandevia 2007; Green
et al. 2012). The remodeling of the extracellular matrix, which includes an increase in
collagen synthesis and a decrease in titin, has furthermore been proposed as a reason for
the increased passive stiffness (Hyldahl and Hubal 2014; J.-G. Yu, Fürst, and Thornell
2003).
However, the findings in the present study provide evidence about a possible relationship
between soreness and stiffness and the lack of relationship between edema and stiffness.
Agten et al. (2017) proposed that the increased stiffness observed in response to DOMS
could not be caused by edema due to the different time-course of the phenomenons, where
the muscular stiffness was observed to subside within seven days, while the edema was
persistent beyond that time point (Agten et al. 2017).
5.6.2
Decrease in Stiffness and Increase in Elasticity in the Less Sore
Muscle
Although the m.gastrocnemius did not increase in stiffness, its mechanical response changed
significantly following the eccentric exercise. Its stiffness and decrement decreased signifi-
cantly immediately after the exercise, before returning to baseline 48 hours post-exercise.
The decrease in stiffness is consistent with studies that have observed a similar trend in
the mechanical response of the muscle after eccentric exercises (Kisilewicz et al. 2020).
Kisilewicz et al. (2018) applied shear wave elastography and myotonometry to investigate
the effect eccentric exercise had on the upper trapezius muscle. A decrease of 14 % and
60
The Feasibility of Non-Invasive Technology in Muscle Health Assessment
==PAGE:75==
18 % decrease was observed, respectively, in the elastic modulus and dynamic stiffness
24 post-exercise (Kisilewicz et al. 2020). Furthermore, Andonian et al. (2016) showed
a decrease in the stiffness of three quadriceps muscles (v.lateralis, v.medialis, and rectus
femoris) immediately after an extreme mountain ultra-marathon. Two days after the run,
the stiffness parameter had partially recovered but had not yet reached the baseline levels
(Kisilewicz et al. 2020).
These contradictive responses (i.e., increase/decrease in stiffness) observed after eccentric
exercises in the present study and in the literature have been proposed to be caused by
several distinct factors. First, diverse protocols, composed of different variations of eccen-
tric contractions in relation to force and velocity, are believed to cause these contradicting
mechanical responses (Andonian et al. 2016). Furthermore, the different characteristics
between participants, especially their previous training status, have also been suggested to
contribute to these different responses (Green et al. 2012; Kong et al. 2018). For example,
all of the participants in the study by Andonian et al. (2016) were experienced runners
that participated in a long-distance run that includes low-intensity eccentric contractions.
In contrast, the study by Janecki et al. (2010) consisted of untrained individuals per-
forming short intensive eccentric contractions of the biceps brachii (Janecki et al. 2011;
Kong et al. 2018). The eccentric contractions in the present study exercise protocol were
different for the m.gastrocnemius and the v.lateralis, which might have contributed to
these contrasting mechanical responses.
The observed decrease in the decrement of the m.gastrocnemius indicates that the muscle
is more elastic immediately after the exercise. This decrease in stiffness and increase in
elasticity post-exercise might be explained with a concept called ”thixotropy”. Thixotropy
refers to the ability of a material to become less viscous after being subjected to stress
(Chhabra and Richardson 2008). The skeletal muscles are believed to possess thixotropic
properties, where the muscle stiffness and resting tension are affected by preceding con-
tractions (Proske, Morgan, and Gregory 1994). This concept relates to the idea of warming
up to reduce muscular stiffness prior to the performance of any physical activity (Bishop
2003). A reduction in muscular stiffness, as measured with the myotonometer, was, in
fact, observed in the pilot studies that examined the effect of exercise and the heating of
the muscle (see chapter 2.3.3.2).
5.6.3
Unchanged Muscular Tension
No significant increase was observed in the oscillation frequency between the pre-and 48
post-exercise in neither of the muscles.
However, a slight decrease in frequency post-
exercise in the m.gastrocnemius resulted in a significant increase when the frequency pa-
rameter reached baseline again 48 hours post-exercise.
The myotonometer oscillation
frequency indicates in this study the resting tone of the muscle (Ditroilo et al. 2011).
The slight decrease observed immediately after the exercise might also be explained with
the thixotropy concept, where the muscle becomes less tense after the exercise.
This
myotonometry parameter has in a previous study been applied to assess intramuscular
pressure. A study by Korhonen (2005) observed higher muscle frequency values in a pop-
ulation suffering from pathologically high intramuscular pressure values (possibly caused
by chronic compartment syndrome) when compared to a population that had lower pres-
sure values (Korhonen et al. 2005). However, this parameter was not sensitive enough to
capture the increased muscular edema in this study, showing no change, although clear
signs of edema were captured by ultrasound imaging and bioimpedance measurements.

5.7
Early Signs of Muscle Fatigue Captured with acoustic
myography
In order to produce DOMS symptoms in the general population, an exercise protocol
needs to be able to produce strenuous, unaccustomed exercises that constitutes bouts of
eccentric contractions (Talag 1974; Asmussen 1956). The present exercise protocol was
quite successful in regards to fatigue levels, proven by the high post-exercise Borg scale
(8.2 ± 1.5) and the high mean of blood lactate levels (13.2 ± 4.2 mmol/L), and heart rate
(166 ± 12.8 bpm) post-exercise. These markers are indicative of global fatigue, i.e., the
fatigue of the total lean body mass (Cifrek et al. 2009).
To observe the muscle fatigue in a single muscle, acoustic myography signals were recorded
from the m.gastrocnemius during the exercise protocol. Two movements within the proto-
col were then chosen for further analysis, the box-drop counter-movement jumps and the
calf-raises. The acoustic myography signals from these two movements were then com-
pared from the beginning of the exercise to the end of the exercise to investigate whether
muscle fatigue had occurred.
The box-drop counter-movement jump is a movement that includes a deep landing from
a jump of a 50 cm box and a subsequent counter-movement jump. A counter-movement
jump is a plyometric exercise that includes a rapid eccentric contraction followed by a rapid
concentric contraction (Davies, Riemann, and Manske 2015). The movement is technically
challenging to perform for the novice participant. During the study, a different interpre-
tation of the movement was observed between participants. The S-score was calculated
respectively for the landing and jumping in the box-drop counter-movement jump. The
E-score was not calculated due to the short duration of these movements, and the T-score
could not be calculated due to constrictions posed by the low sampling frequency. The S-
score represents the spatial summation of the acoustic myography signal, where a higher
score represents a smaller amplitude of the signal, i.e., fewer muscle fibers recruited to
perform the movement. A fall in S-score can be interpreted as a sign of muscle fatigue,
where more fibers are recruited to perform the same movement as before (Adrian P Har-
rison 2018). This muscle fatigue marker was observed for three of the subjects (ID: 1, 6,
and 9), where a significant fall in S-score was observed in the box-drop counter-movement
jumps in the last set of the exercise when compared to the first set of the exercise (see
Table 4.3). This finding is in line with observations made by Pingel et al. (2019), where
a gradual decrease in S-score was detected in the m.gastrocnemius of subjects running for
30-minutes on a treadmill (Pingel, I. T. Andersen, et al. 2019). Another sign of muscle
fatigue observed in the present study was the decrease in the mean value of S-score and
increased variation observed in the last set of the ten box-drop counter-movements jumps
(see figure 4.2 ), which is indicative of the failure of performing ”identical” jumps (Adrian
P Harrison 2018). This trend of decreased S-score and increased variation is also evident in
figure 4.3 for subject 1. Figure 4.3 further supports the inter-individual variation observed
in the depth jump movement, whereas subject 1 shows an evident sign of muscle fatigue
with a decrease in S-score and increase variability. Subject 2 performs the depth jumps
more eﬀiciently in the last set due to, perhaps, adaptation to the movement.
Calf raises include both a concentric phase when raising and an eccentric phase when
lowering back down. This movement does not allow for as much variability in performance
as the box-drop counter-movement jumps and is more straightforward in execution. A
decrease was observed, although not significant, in the E- and the T-scores in the last set
compared to the first set. The E score corresponds to the periods of inactive/active periods
within the movement duration and is representative of coordination and eﬀiciency (Adrian
62

==PAGE:77==
P Harrison 2018). A decrease in E-score, therefore, represents a loss in coordination. These
findings are in coherence with observations made during the experiments, where some
subjects were observed to lose balance when performing the calf raises towards the end of
the exercise. The decrease observed in the T-score corresponds to a higher firing frequency
of the active muscle fibers (Adrian P Harrison 2018). Both the decrease in E-score and
T-score represent muscle fatigue, where less coordination and more energy are needed to
perform the movements to compensate for the increased muscle fatigue (Adrian P Harrison
2018). The aforementioned study by Pingel et al. (2019) did also observe a decrease in
E-score and a slight decrease in T-score during the time-course of the 30-minute exercise
for both the healthy and the CP individuals (Pingel, I. T. Andersen, et al. 2019).
5.8
Limitations
There are several limitations to this study. The low sample size might have underpowered
statistical analyses. However, despite the low sample size, congruent findings were obtained
using different technologies, strengthening their credibility. For example, signs of edema
were found by both the ultrasound imaging and the bioimpedance measurements.
Another limitation of this study is that it included a homogeneous sample of young,
healthy females. Therefore these results cannot be generalized to males or different age
ranges, nor can they be extrapolated to a population of athletes or patients.
Gender
differences have been reported to influence the appearance of DOMS in regards to both
symptoms and the time-course (Agten et al. 2017). Moreover, food intake and activity
level in-between measurements could not be controlled. Diet supplements, such as D-ribose
and inflammatory medications, have been reported to alleviate the symptoms of DOMS,
reducing muscle soreness and speeding up the recovery of the muscle damage (Cao et al.
2020; Cheung, Hume, and Maxwell 2003). Moreover, although the subjects were asked
to refrain from physical activity between the measurements, it could not be enforced.
Biking is the most prevalent way of transportation in Denmark, accounting for 24 % of
all journeys within the capital region in the year 2016 (Denmark 2016). Therefore, it is
highly likely that some of the subjects did at least bike during the experimental period.
The design of the study does also includes some limitations. The muscles were only ob-
served and measured at a single landmark, at the belly of the muscles, using the ultrasound
and the myotonometer. DOMS has been reported to affect muscles heterogeneously, and
the localization of soreness has been observed to be the most pronounced in the musculo-
tendinous junctions (Di Newham et al. 1983; Cleak and Eston 1992; Kisilewicz et al. 2020;
Heredia-Rizo et al. 2020; Kawczyński et al. 2018). Kawczyñski et al. (2018) observed that
the stiffness at the musculotendinous sites of the trapezius muscle increased 24 hours after
eccentric exercise, whereas the stiffness in the muscle belly decreased (Kawczyński et al.
2018). Moreover, ultrasound imaging at the musculotendinous junction of the v.lateralis
would have been more convenient, as it is situated more superficially than the muscle belly.
More measuring landmarks might potentially have resulted in a more accurate estimation
of the DOMS symptoms and the mechanical response of the muscle. Another limitation of
the current design is that measurements were only taken pre, post, and 48 post-exercise. A
measurement 24 post-exercise might have provided further evidence about the time-course
of the regeneration of the muscle that follows DOMS. It was speculated that the lack of
muscle damage and edema observed in the v.lateralis with the bioimpedance measure-
ments might be because of faster regeneration of the muscle due to possible changes in the
gait pattern. An additional measurement at 24 hours post-exercise might have clarified
that speculation. Finally, the acoustic myography device was placed on the muscle that
resulted in being less sore. A placement of the acoustic myography device on the v.lateralis

instead of the m.gastrocnemius might have resulted in more drastic signs of muscle fatigue.
Another limitation of the exercise protocol is that it was unsuccessful in inducing tradi-
tional DOMS symptoms in the m.gastrocnemius. The soreness level in the m.gastrocnemius
at the 48h post measurements was close to none (0.67 ± 1.1). However, although the lack

it resulted in an exciting finding. The lack of soreness sensation in the m.gastrocnemius

relation between edema and soreness (Agten et al. 2017). It additionally showed that
although soreness sensation is absent, it does not mean that no muscle damage or edema
is present.
Moreover, it provided evidence about the relation between muscular stiff-

a gradual increase in stiffness in contrast with the decrease in stiffness observed in the
m.gastrocnemius, possibly caused by thixotropy.

Conclusion
The present study’s findings indicate that the here observed non-invasive technologies,
i.e., ultrasound imaging, multi-frequency bioimpedance, and myotonometry, successfully
captured the manifestation of DOMS. Furthermore, the present study’s findings showed
the feasibility of using acoustic myography to monitor muscle fatigue.
The ultrasound imaging captured the structural changes of the muscle in response to
DOMS. The increase in thickness and pennation angle observed with the ultrasound are
indicative of the edema-induced muscular enlargement. Moreover, more signs of inflamma-
tion were captured with the power doppler technique, showing increased flow in the muscle,
which is indicative of hyperemia. In addition, the bioimpedance was able to provide infor-
mation about changes in muscle structure and metabolism. The bioimpedance captured
signs of muscle damage, temporary loss of muscle mass, and clear signs of edema. At
last, the myotonometry showed the mechanical response of the muscle to DOMS, where
an increase in the muscular stiffness was observed in accordance with the self-reported
pain-levels
The acoustic myography findings in this study captured signs of muscle fatigue for some
of the subjects. The observed signs of muscle fatigue included a decrease in spatial- and
temporal summation in the last set of the exercise compared to the first set, indicating
that more muscle fibers were recruited and activated at a higher frequency to be able
to perform the same movements. Moreover, a higher variation was found in the spatial
summation in the last set of the exercise, showing a lack of ability to perform the move-
ments with consistency. In addition, a decrease in muscle eﬀiciency was observed, with
the parameter E-score, which shows that the muscle fibers were less successful in relaxing
between contractions in the last set when compared to the first.
Overall, ultrasound imaging, bioimpedance, myotonometry, and acoustic myography showed
promising ways of assessing low-grade muscle damage (DOMS) and muscle fatigue in a
non-invasive, relatively low-cost, and easy-to-use manner, making them exciting options
in overall muscle health assessment.

Perspectives
The present study demonstrated the feasibility of using non-invasive and relatively low-cost
methods to assess the changes in muscles that have been exposed to a DOMS-inducing
exercise. The sensitivity of the tested methods to assess the low-grade muscle damage
(DOMS) shows their potential to be used in a broader range of settings. The present
study provides a base for further research of the feasibility of the presented methods. For
example, to monitor muscular disorders’ progression, be used in sports settings, manage
healthy aging, and even in muscle assessment in the space-setting, where the effects of
microgravity are of particular interest.
The presented methods show potential in participating in the evaluation of different treat-
ments by providing information about changes in the structural, metabolical, and func-
tional state of muscles in response to medical treatments. For example, the treatment of
botulinum toxin A injections (BoNT/A), commonly used for muscular contractures, has in
recent years been under scrutiny (Gracies 2005; Pingel, Nielsen, et al. 2017). For example,
a recent study by Pingel et al. (2017) observed, by using muscle biopsies and synchrotron
imaging, that BoNT/A-injection in the muscles of rats resulted in severe microstructural
damages and atrophies three weeks post-treatment (Pingel, Nielsen, et al. 2017). The
methods presented in the present study might be helpful to observe the long-term effect of
BoNT/A-injections by observing muscular changes following the treatment. For example,
the bioimpedance might provide information about loss of muscle mass, indicating possible
muscle atrophy, and the acoustic myography might be applied to provide evidence about
the functional state of the muscle.
Moreover, the treatment of physical training could be well monitored using the presented
methods. Physical training has been proven to contribute to improved mobility and overall
quality of life and health in elderly populations, and it has been proposed as a treatment
for various muscular disorders (Weening-Dijksterhuis et al. 2011; Kell, Bell, and Quinney
2001; Csapo and Alegre 2016; Søgaard and Sjøgaard 2017; I. T. Andersen et al. 2018). The
presented methods might aid in choosing the appropriate training program, the follow-
up of its effects, and avoiding adverse effects, such as muscle damage, by regular muscle
assessments of, for example, the muscular stiffness and other signs of muscle damage.
Furthermore, the presented methods are highly suitable within sports and training set-
tings, being easy-to-use and relatively low-cost. Muscle injuries are one of the most com-
mon sport-related injures in athletes, accounting for more than 30 % of all injuries in
soccer (Mueller-Wohlfahrt et al. 2012). Frequent muscle assessment and early detection of
low-grade muscle injuries would greatly benefit the prevention of sports-related injuries.
For instance, it might aid in the making of informed decisions about the appropriate length
of recovery. For example, the quick and easy application of the myotonometer might give
valuable information about the state of the muscle, which could, in addition to occasional
bioimpedance measurements, be used to prevent injuries. In addition, ultrasound imaging
might be used as a tool to monitor the rehabilitation of an injured muscle.
At last, the presented methods might provide further evidence concerning the effect that
microgravity places on the skeletal muscles. The monitoring of health in microgravity is
important for successful space missions, and muscle assessment is of particular concern due
to the adverse effects microgravity places on skeletal muscles, such as loss of muscle mass
and general deconditioning (Schneider et al. 2015; Gopalakrishnan et al. 2010). Due to
66
The Feasibility of Non-Invasive Technology in Muscle Health Assessment
==PAGE:81==
the advantages of the methods tested in the presented study, especially the bioimpedance,
acoustic myography, and myotonometry, being small in size, hand-held, and easy to use
makes it an attractive option for muscle assessment in the constraints imposed by space
stations.
In summary, the present study has provided a base for continued research on how already
exciting non-invasive technology can assess the muscular state in various environments
and conditions.
