Abstract
The advent of general­purpose large language models (LLMs), especially the GPT series
by OpenAI, marks a pivotal shift in natural language processing (NLP). These models’
extensive use of unlabeled data, auto­regressive decoder­only transformer architectures,
and billions of parameters have set new benchmarks in a variety of NLP tasks. However,
their tendency for content ’hallucination’ necessitates the augmentation of LLMs with ex­
ternal tools.
This thesis evaluates the potential of open­source instruction LLMs for integration with
external tools using a function­agnostic zero­shot approach. Specifically, it explores the
development and effectiveness of a Wikipedia­augmented agent designed for medical
Open Domain Question Answering (ODQA). This agent is intended to perform a series of
actions to retrieve, refine, and filter medical knowledge, thereby anchoring the answers
in reliable information.
A function­agnostic zero­shot prompt, designed using Typescript type definitions, has
been extensively explored and refined. This prompt, when applied to the mixtral8x7b-
instructv0.1 open­source LLM, exhibited a success rate ranging from 84.2% to 99%.
This metric is based on the number of correctly structured output generations, which
varies depending on the specific function and user input. Empirical evaluation on med­
ical benchmarks (USMLE and MedMCQA) using ’Direct’ and ’5­shot Chain of Thought
(CoT)’ prompts revealed that the Wikipedia­augmented agent improved performance by
5.5%­5.7% for ’Direct’ prompts and by 0.9%­1.0% for ’5­shot CoT’ prompts. Albeit the en­
hancement for the ’5­shot CoT’ prompt is not significant, opposed to the ’Direct’ prompt,
it is still the superior approach, as evidenced by its higher overall performance (USMLE
65.6%, MedMCQA 58.1%) compared to the ’Direct’ approach (USMLE 62.0%, MedMCQA
56%).
These findings highlight that 1) open­source instruction LLMs can be equipped with ex­
ternal tools using a function­agnostic zero­shot prompt, which bridges the gap between
open­source models and proprietary models like OpenAI’s function calling gpt-3.5-turbo-
1106 model and 2) albeit not always statistically better than the non­augmented counter­
part, the tool­augmented agents do increase performance and are a step towards mitigat­
ing the inherent limitations of LLMs.
Keywords: Large Language Models, Open Domain Question Answering, Tool Augmen­
tation, Medical Domain, Wikipedia, Function­Agnostic Zero­Shot Prompting.

Introduction
Background and Motivation
The field of natural language processing (NLP) has witnessed a paradigm shift with the
development of general­purpose Large Language Models (LLMs), notably the Generative
Pre­trained Transformer (GPT) series. These general­purpose models are characterized
by their use of extensive unlabeled data, auto­regressive decoder­only transformer archi­
tectures, and billions of model parameters. The evolution from GPT­1 to its successors
has highlighted the impact of increasing model size in terms of parameters and training
data. This growth has enabled these models to capture and process a wide range of
linguistic nuances and knowledge. This advancement has set new SOTA performance
on many benchmarks across a multitude of NLP tasks. The SOTA performance in vari­
ous NLP tasks is increasingly being achieved through few­shot prompting techniques, a
method that leverages a small number of examples to guide the model’s responses[1].
This represents a significant trend away from the traditional approach of fine­tuning pre­
trained models on task­specific datasets towards employing general­purpose models and
tailoring their outputs with strategically designed prompts.
Despite their exceptional capabilities, often exceeding human performance, LLMs exhibit
significant limitations. A prominent issue is their tendency for ’hallucination’, where they
generate content that is plausible yet factually incorrect. This phenomenon highlights
a critical constraint of LLMs: their skills are intrinsically linked to the static parameters
acquired during training[2]. In response to these limitations, a consensus is emerging
in the NLP community on the necessity of augmenting LLMs with external tools. These
tools can offer dynamic, real­time data, and introduce interactive elements for adapting
to new information and contexts. This shift from reliance on static internal knowledge to
a more dynamic is crucial for enhancing the practical utility of the LLMs and in particular
the trustworthiness of generated question answers[3].
The literature surrounding augmented large language models is extensive, with numer­
ous methodologies proposed. Traditionally, these approaches have been task­ or tool­
specific, lacking generality. This paradigm shifted with OpenAI’s introduction of Function
Calling models in the summer of 2023. These models interpret a function signature sub­
mitted to their API, generating outputs consistent with the signature’s format. This tech­
nique, which I term as ’function­agnostic zero­shot prompting’, is function­agnostic due to
its ability to accommodate any function and zero­shot as it operates without task­specific
examples. This advancement marks a significant step towards the practical applicability
of LLMs. However, the specifics of OpenAI’s implementation remain proprietary.
A common method for assessing the knowledge capabilities of these models involves
Open Domain Question Answering (ODQA) tasks, where both proprietary models like
GPT3.5 and open­source counterparts such as Llama2­70b have demonstrated human­
level performance with few­shot prompting. Nevertheless, in critical areas like the medical
field, inaccuracies can have severe consequences. Therefore, this research endeavors
to augment state­of­the­art open­source models with external knowledge bases, aiming
to ground their responses more reliably, in the medical domain.
This thesis aims to apply deep learning techniques to ODQA tasks within the medical do­
main, emphasizing the use of open­source models like Meta’s Llama2 series and Mistral
AI models, while purposefully avoiding reliance on black­box APIs such as OpenAI. The

primary goal is to develop an agent that facilitates the interaction between various tools
and the LLM, providing relevant context for medical questions. This is anticipated to en­

tasks as evidenced by the USMLE and MedMCQA benchmarks. The motivation stems
from a pressing need: in the past decade, diagnostic errors in the Danish healthcare sys­
tem have led to significant patient harm, including over 170 deaths per year, underscoring
the urgency for improved diagnostic tools[4].
Objective
The objective of this thesis is two­fold:
1) Assess the capability of open­source instruction LLMs to integrate with external tools
using a function­agnostic zero­shot prompt, and
2) Explore what kind of Wikipedia­augmented agents can be build to generate context
for questions in the medical domain and evaluate whether the context enhances
performance on existing prompt approaches.
To achieve this, the objective is subdivided into several specific goals:
1. Acquire a thorough understanding of recent LLM research.
2. Develop a prompt for augmenting open­source LLMs in a function­agnostic zero­
shot manner.
3. Design an agent that seamlessly integrates Wikipedia with the LLM using the pre­
viously developed prompt.
4. Establish baseline performance for the ’Direct’ and ’5­shot CoT’ on the USMLE and
MedMCQA medical benchmarks.
5. Evaluate the Wikipedia­augmented agent on the USMLE and MedMCQA medical
benchmarks using ’Direct’ and ’5­shot CoT’ prompts.
6. Analyze the results to determine whether the Wikipedia­augmented approach en­
hances baseline performance.
These goals will address key research questions, such as identifying the most effective
open­source LLMs for following a function signature given a function­agnostic zero­shot
prompt, and determining the impact of agent­created context on LLM performance in med­
ical ODQA benchmarks.
Scope and Limitations
The field of LLMs is rapidly evolving, with frequent announcements of new models, tech­
niques, and frameworks. To manage this complexity and maintain focus, this thesis is
carefully scoped. Initiated in September 2023, amidst the buzz of OpenAI’s Function Call­
ing API release, this research concentrates on instruction­based open­source models,
perceived as the most promising candidates for following a function­agnostic zero­shot
approach. While several new open­source models, e.g. NexusRaven[5], have emerged
with claims of function calling capabilities, this thesis deliberately focuses on models from
established AI labs, such as Meta and Mistral.

2.1
Fundamentals of Machine Learning
Machine learning (ML), a subset of AI, empowers systems to autonomously learn and
enhance their performance from data, bypassing the need for explicit programming. The
primary focus in this field is the creation of parameterized models capable of learning
from data to make predictions or decisions. In other words, the overarching goal is to
construct models that can generalize from their learned experiences and adapt to novel
situations[6].
2.1.1
Core Concepts
The core concepts integral to machine learning include:
• Data: The cornerstone of ML, data comes in various forms such as images, text,
or numerical values and is pivotal for training models. Crucial for effective model
development, data is typically split into distinct subsets: the training set, used for
learning; the validation set, used for tuning hyperparameters and model validation;
and the test set, reserved for evaluating the model’s generalization capability.
• Features: Representing the measurable attributes or characteristics observed in
the phenomena that one wants to model. If the data of the phenomena is not nu­
merical values to start with, data is transformed to the numerical domain.
• Models: These refer to the mathematical formulations that process input data to
yield predictions or decisions.
• Learning Algorithms: These methodologies are employed to train models, focus­
ing on adjusting model parameters for optimal data fitting.
• Training: This phase involves a model learning from data, striving to reduce the
difference between predictions and labels, ultimately uncovering patterns and rela­
tionships within the data.
• Inference: The stage where a trained model is utilized to make predictions or deci­
sions.

2.1.2
Types of Machine Learning
Machine learning methodologies can be broadly classified into three primary types[7]:
1. Supervised Learning: This approach involves learning a mapping function from
input to output, guided by example input­output pairs. It leverages labeled training
data comprising various training examples.
2. Unsupervised Learning: In this method, the system learns to identify patterns and
structures within unlabeled data. Its aim is to model the data’s inherent structure or
distribution to deepen understanding.
3. Reinforcement Learning: This variant of machine learning involves an agent learn­
ing to navigate and act within an environment based on the feedback from its actions,
ultimately aimed at achieving defined goals in uncertain and potentially complex en­
vironments.
2.2
Neural Networks
Neural networks are the backbone of many modern machine learning algorithms, par­
ticularly in the field of deep learning. These networks are inspired by the structure and
functioning of the human brain, specifically its ability to learn from observational data. A
neural network is composed of layers of interconnected nodes or neurons, each layer per­
forming specific transformations on the data. The basic structure includes an input layer,
one or more hidden layers, and an output layer.
2.2.1

comprehensive description of their processes in transforming input data into outputs. This

2.3
Natural Language Processing: The Transformer
Architecture
After establishing the foundational concepts in the field of ML, it is time to turn our atten­
tion to one of the more challenging areas of machine learning: sequence­to­sequence
(Seq2Seq) and more broadly NLP. The simple neural networks presented in the previ­
ous sections have shown excellent performance on large datasets with a fixed input and
output vector dimensionality[12]. However, as natural language cannot be directly ex­
pressed as a fixed­length vector and the fact that a word in a sentence can have different
semantic meaning depending on the surrounding words, more sophisticated approaches
are needed.
2.3.1
Text to vectors
To model language, it is necessary to convert units of text, so called tokens, into vectors
of fixed dimensionality.
Tokenization
In the alphabetic languages, text can be decomposed into smaller units such as charac­
ters, syllables, or words. A tokenizer is an algorithm that maps text to tokens given a
token vocabulary V of size NV. The size of the vocabulary depends on the tokenization
strategy; a character­level tokenization results in a small vocabulary size but many tokens,
whereas a word­level tokenization results in fewer tokens but a much larger vocabulary.
A good trade­off between number of tokens and vocabulary size is found in subword to­
kenization, which also generalizes to unknown or rare words through splitting them up in
manageable pieces.
Embeddings

Until the Transformer architecture, language modelling was approached using encoder­
decoder architectures within the domain of recurrent neural networks (RNNs), specifically
employing long short­term memory (LSTM) units and gated recurrent units (GRU). Within
this framework, an encoder transforms an input sequence (e.g., a sentence in English)
into a context vector, which subsequently guides the decoder in generating an output
sequence (e.g., the corresponding translation in French). RNNs inherently process inputs
in a sequential manner, maintaining a hidden state that propagates information across
inputs. Nonetheless, RNNs are impeded by the vanishing gradient phenomenon, which
obstructs the learning of long­range dependencies. LSTMs address this limitation through
a specialized gating mechanism, facilitating the retention of information across extended
sequences[13, 14, 15].
The advent of attention mechanisms marked a pivotal shift in Seq2Seq model capabili­
ties. These mechanisms enable the model to dynamically attend to different segments
of the input sequence, thereby increasing its proficiency in handling long­distance de­
pendencies[16]. This advancement was instrumental in the creation of the Transformer
model introduced in Vaswani et al. 2017[17]. Distinct from its forerunners, the Trans­
former abandons recurrent methodologies in favor of a fully attention­based approach.
This architectural alteration allows for parallelized processing and better transfer­learning
capabilities, which have substantially enhanced performance across NLP tasks.

The transformer architecture, as depicted in figure 2.2, consists of an encoder (left side)

a sequence of continuous representations (z1, ..., zN). The decoder then generates the
next token in the output sequence accounting for the encoded input, (z1, ..., zN) and the
previous predicted tokens.

3
Theory II: Current LLM landscape
The current landscape of language models are dominated by auto­regressive decoder­
only large language models. The publication of ”GPT­3: Language Models are Few­Shot
Learners” by Brown et al. 2020[1] marked the start of the auto­regressive era, as the
175B parameter GPT­3 model achieved strong few­shot performances on several NLP
datasets. The model was 10x the size of any previous non­sparse language model and
the model was tested in a so­called few­shot setting opposed to the previous method of
fine­tuning models on downstream tasks.
Since the release of GPT­3, many LLMs have been developed which all have continued
to push the state of the art. A selection of the most powerful of these include; Gopher[19],
Chincilla[20], PaLM[21], Llama[22, 23], GPT­4[24] and Mistral[25] that all at the time of
release achieved state of the art on several benchmarks1.
3.1

3.2
From fine­tuning to prompt­engineering
The era of auto­regressive models also marks a shift in paradigm from fine­tuning to
prompt­engineering. Traditionally, the state of the art of a benchmark was achieved by
fine­tuning a pre­trained language model to a downstream task ­ i.e. updating the pa­
rameters of the model in a supervised fashion given the training split of the dataset. The
pre­trained model could thus be fine­tuned in many different ways, however it did require
a high quality and quantity dataset to train on.
The GPT­3 study [1] introduced significant advancements in language model scaling
(175B parameters) and a shift towards in­context learning (ICL), resulting in state­of­the­
art performance. In­context learning, also known as prompt engineering, involves condi­
tioning the model’s output generation based on a given text, aiming for more precise and
relevant responses. As LLMs are trained on extensive datasets, their capabilities can be
conceptualized as an array of functions. In this framework, a prompt works as a selector,
activating the appropriate function based on the task at hand.

3.3
Human Alignment
Large language models are often released in two primary forms: a foundation model and
an instruct/chat model. The foundation model represents the core, raw pre­trained version
of the model. This form is developed through extensive training on diverse datasets,
enabling the model to capture a wide range of human knowledge and language nuances.
In contrast, the instruct or chat model is an evolution of the foundation model, tailored
to align with human preferences and instructions. Throughout, the thesis the term chat
model and instruct model will be used interchangeably ­ the reason being is that some AI
labs name their instruct models chat and others instruct.
The process of aligning an LLM to human preferences involves additional training phases,
where the model is conditioned to respond in ways that are deemed more helpful, safe,
and aligned with ethical guidelines. The model weights are usually updated using super­
vised learning and reinforcement learning from human feedback [30, 31]. This aligning
process is crucial for commercial deployment, as it significantly mitigates the risks as­
sociated with generating harmful, toxic, hateful, or biased content. Thus, it is a form of
’red­teaming’ where potential undesirable behaviors are identified and corrected.
More concretely, the model is fine­tuned to adhere to a structured conversational frame­
work, involving interactions between the user and the assistant (model). The process is
initiated with a system message containing high­level instructions that sets the stage for
the conversation. Thereafter, the conversation is a round­trip dialogue between the user
and the assistant, as seen in figure 3.4.
In the theoretical chapters it was established that auto­regressive language models op­
erate by ingesting a sequence of text and subsequently predicting subsequent tokens in
a sequential manner. The differentiation of input sources (user, assistant, or system) is
achieved through the application of a chat template during the fine­tuning phase. This
template formats the input by explicitly encoding both the role (user, assistant, system)
and the corresponding message content. This encoding ensures that the model compre­
hends and processes each input segment appropriately.

Related work
The trend of augmenting Large Language models with the external environment, such
as document retrievers or tools is a growing research field[3]. This field seeks to ad­
dress some of the inherent limitations of LLMs, such as hallucinations and the non­linear
emergence of abilities when scaling the models, by grounding the responses in the out­
side world[2]. To ensure the reader is adequately equipped to digest the literature, it is
important to define the following terms:
• Tool: An external module such as a python interpreter, search engine, another
neural network or the LLM it self, that the agent can call to perform a task.
• Agent: In the context of tool­augmented large language models, an agent refers to
the primary AI entity that interacts with a user, processes requests, and utilizes var­
ious tools to perform tasks or retrieve information beyond its inherent capabilities.
The agent’s core function is to interpret user inputs, determine the most appropriate
tools to engage, and integrate the outputs from these tools into coherent, contex­
tually relevant responses. It acts as a mediator and orchestrator, leveraging exter­
nal resources to enhance its performance and extend its knowledge and functional
boundaries.
Given the vast nature of research in augmenting LLMs [3], this chapter selectively high­
lights key advancements that have influenced the field in recent years. The advance­
ments presented in this chapter leads to the development of a function­agnostic zero­
shot prompt, exemplified by OpenAIs Function Calling API. Due to the proprietary nature
of OpenAI’s methodology, which precludes a comprehensive analysis, it is not included
in this chapter. This chapter focuses on contributions with accessible methodologies and
transparent implementation details, which are essential for a thorough academic discus­
sion and critique.

model would not be able to navigate the web, the authors fine­tune the model using be­

a reward model mainly using data from the ELI dataset[37]. The result is an agent that
knows how to interact with the Microsoft Bing Web Search API1 and has a sophisticated
action space that includes; Search <query>, Click on link <link ID>, Lookup: <text>,

4.2
IALM: Internet­Augmented Language Models: Enhancing
LLMs with Web Browsing for Open­Domain Question
Answering
In ”Internet­Augmented Language Models through Few­Shot Prompting for Open­Domain
Question Answering” (IALM), by Lazaridou et al. 2022, the authors explore augmenting
large language models with web browsing capabilities to enhance their factual grounding
[38]. This approach involves integrating Google Search for information retrieval, thereby
enabling LLMs to condition on web­retrieved information without additional fine­tuning or
parameter learning. The methodology comprises three key stages:
1. Retrieval: Utilizing Google Search API2 to gather relevant documents, from the
Internet based on the raw question, extracting clean text for further processing.
2. Prompting: Applying few­shot prompting techniques to condition the language model
on the extracted paragraphs.
3. Reranking: Enhancing the model’s inference capabilities by generating multiple
answers from the retrieved documents, followed by reranking them using various
scoring algorithms.
Thus, compared to WebGPT, this approach does not train an agent to interact with the
environment, it instead shows that LLM can be few­shot prompted to understand a context
that has been retrieved using the Google Search Engine.
Empirical studies using the Gopher­280B[19] model demonstrated notable improvements
in open­domain question answering tasks, here among the HotpotQA[39] and Fever[40]
benchmarks, with the method proving especially effective for simpler, single­hop ques­
tions. The findings indicate that smaller models, when conditioned on web­retrieved evi­
dence, can sometimes surpass the performance of larger closed­book models, particularly
in tasks requiring reliable evidence extraction[38].
To conclude, the research highlights the potential of internet­augmented LLMs in improv­
ing factual accuracy without the need for fine­tuning or additional parameter learning.

Toolformer, Schick et al. 2023[42], demonstrates a novel approach in language models to
utilize external tools for enhanced task performance. A GPT­J 6.7B parameter model is
finetuned to automatically detect when to use one of five tools (Q&A System, Calculator,
Wikipedia Search, Machine Translation System and Calendar) in a zero­shot setting. The
approach is dataset agnostic, allowing the application on the same dataset used for pre­
training the model. This ensures retention of generality and core language modeling skills.
Approach: Toolformer integrates several steps:
1. Sampling API Calls: Identifying positions within a dataset to introduce API calls.

2. Executing API Calls: Performing all generated API calls.
3. Filtering API Calls: Filtering out API calls based on their effectiveness in reducing
the weighted cross­entropy loss.
4. Model Finetuning: Integrating the selected API calls with the original inputs for model
finetuning.
5. Inference: Generating text with the consideration of integrated API calls.
Thus, the model autonomously learns to use various tools for tasks where traditional LLMs
show limitations, such as arithmetic or factual lookup. The main contribution of Toolformer,
is thus that they train a model in a self­supervised way to decide which tools to use, when
to use them and with what arguments.

ToolLLM, Qin et al. 2023[43], aims to enhance the capability of open­source LLMs by
mastering a vast array of real­world APIs (16000+). In other words, the framework tries
to mimic the Function Calling feature of OpenAI, as presented in 3.3.1, along with com­
paring the ReAct/CoT reasoning methods with a Depth First Search­based Decision Tree
method to combat 1) error propagation and 2) limited exploration. This is done with the
framework’s five components:
Framework Components:
1. ToolBench: A high­quality dataset created using ChatGPT, tailored for tool use, fa­
cilitating the training of LLMs to interact effectively with various tools.
2. API Collection: Gathering a wide range of functionalities from RapidAPI, a platform
hosting extensive real­world APIs.
3. Instruction Generation: Generating diverse instructions for single­tool and multi­tool
scenarios, teaching the model to handle individual and combined tool interactions.
4. Solution Path Annotation: Implementing a novel depth­first search­based decision
tree (DFSDT) to improve annotation efficiency and complete complex instructions.
5. ToolEval: An automatic evaluation system using pass rate and win rate metrics to
assess tool­use capabilities of LLMs.
The authors fine­tune a Llama2­7b model to obtain ToolLlama, which shows robust capa­
bilities in handling both single­tool and complex multi­tool instructions and demonstrates
strong capabilities of out­of­distribution function calling. This is an improvement of Tool­

State­of­the­art in medical QA datasets

a clear distinction between open­source and closed­source models in this field.

6
Method
On a high­level, the aim of this thesis is to bridge the recent advancements in the field of
tool­augmented language models with the medical domain. The related work, presented
in Chapter 4, illustrates how the evolution of equipping language models with tools have
progressed from either very agent­specific and fine­tuning approaches (WebGPT) or sim­
ple Search Engine augmentation using few­shot (IALM) to include dynamic reasoning
(ReAct) to autonomously learn several tools (Toolformer) and finally to learn a vast of ar­
ray of tools and work in out­of­distribution settings (ToolLLM). Hence, there have been an
evolution towards more function­ and task­agnostic augmented language models. This
thesis follows the trend and 1) explores how open­source instruct models can be equipped
with functions in an in­context learning and function­agnostic zero­shot setting and 2) ex­
plores what tool­augmented agents can be build to generate context for questions in the
medical domain.
In this chapter my approach to explore the research questions is outlined. The experiential
work of this thesis is three fold. A baseline is established based on existing approaches
from the literature. Afterwards, a survey of SOTA open­source instruct models’ ability to
follow a function signature is assessed. If the outcome of the survey is positive, different
tool­augmented agents will be built to hopefully beat the baselines.

such as Python. In other words, the LLM must generate tokens that obey a predefined

6.3.1
Interfacing with external tools
The agent’s foremost functionality is its interaction with external tools. This process in­
volves prompting the LLM with a function signature corresponding to the search endpoint
of the external tool, along with a medical question. The LLM’s responsibility is to gener­
ate a relevant search query in response to the medical question. Subsequently, the agent
forwards this query to the external tool. The search API of Wikipedia yield a list of articles
ranked by relevance. In scenarios where only this action is employed, the agent selects
the top­ranked article, and the content of this article serves as the context for answering
the medical question.
6.3.2
Selection of articles
In practice, the query submitted to the search engine seldom corresponds precisely with
one of the articles returned in the search results. This situation is analogous to human
interaction with search engines, where the most relevant answer is not always found in the
top­ranked article. To address this variability, the agent incorporates an action designed
to evaluate and select the most relevant article based on the article titles from the top 10
search results. This added action enhances the agent’s capability to accurately pinpoint
information that best addresses the query.
6.3.3
Segmentation and Summarization of Articles
Due to the often extensive length of articles on Wikipedia, they may exceed the con­

6.3.4
Context relevance filtering
In the final extension of the agent’s process, the LLM is tasked with determining the rele­
vance of the summarized context to the posed question. The LLM not only determines if
the context is relevant but also provides a justification for its decision. The idea behind the
justification is that it is a way for the model to reason whether the context is relevant or not.
Should the LLM assess the context as irrelevant to the question, the context is substituted
with an empty string. This step functions as a filtering mechanism, safeguarding against
the incorporation of irrelevant information into the final prompt.

hypothesis are denoted as:
H0 : There is no difference in the proportions of correct answers between the baseline
and the best Wiki­augmented methods.
H1 : There is a difference in the proportions of correct answers between the baseline
and the best Wiki­augmented methods.

8
Discussion
The overall objective of this thesis is:
1) Assess the capability of open­source instruction LLMs to integrate with tools using a
function­agnostic zero­shot prompt, and
2) Explore what kind of Wikipedia­augmented agents can be build to generate context
for questions in the medical domain and evaluate whether the context enhances
performance on existing prompt approaches.
The results of the exploratory analysis indicate that the mistral7b­instv0.1 and mistral8x7b­
instv0.1 models are capable of adhering to a function signature while retaining medical
competences. The percentage of valid function arguments generated are for the Questio­
nAnswer function signature, figure 6.3, 98­99%, for the Wikipedia search 97.3%, and for
the context filter 84.2% using mixtral8x7b­instv0.11. Thus, a function­agnostic zero­shot
prompt compatible with the mistral8x7b­instv0.1 models have to an extent been found.
As mentioned in the exploratory analysis, it is merely one among possibly many prompts
that can equip open­source instruct LLMs with external tools.
The Wiki­augmented agents beat the non­augmented ’Direct’ (+5.5 and +5.7) and ’5­
shot CoT’ (+0.9 and +1.0) prompt baselines, albeit only the ’Direct’ prompt is significantly
improved. On the benchmarks the best Wiki­augmented agent consists of a divide and
summarize component and a filter component for the ’5­shot CoT’ prompt, whereas for
the ’Direct’ prompt the best results are achieved with the full top Wikipedia article.
The methodology and approaches I have used to obtain the above results, will now be dis­
cussed, analysed and alternative paths suggested in relation to the related work on both
augmenting LLMs with tools but also in relation to the USMLE and MedMCQA bench­
marks.
Function­agnostic zero­shot prompt. The development of a function­agnostic zero­
shot prompt represents a significant advancement over existing methodologies such as
those implemented in Toolformer [42] and ToolLLM [43], which utilize gradient learning to
integrate tools into LLMs. Unlike ReAct [41], this approach does not require tool­ or task­
specific shots. As demonstrated in Figure 7.6, the function­agnostic zero­shot prompt
achieves 84.2% to 99% success rate in generating valid function arguments. However,
it lacks the capability to autonomously determine the relevance or select among multiple
functions. This limitation can be mitigated by incorporating an additional decision­making
component into the agent’s workflow, albeit at the cost of increased computational demand
and latency.
Human­aligned LLMs. This thesis primarily focuses on instruct LLMs, given their en­
hanced proficiency in adhering to function signatures and producing structured outputs.
Literature indicates a potential trade­off in knowledge calibration when transitioning from
foundational models to human­aligned instruct models [24, 51].
The baseline perfor­
mance on USMLE and MedMCQA for the foundation and instruct models did not reveal a
strong trend. Some instruct models (llama7b, llama13b codellama7b, and codellama13b)
primarily showed superior performance compared to their foundational models, whereas
the opposite was primarily true for the mistral7b and mixtral8x7b models.

Investigating whether function adherence in LLMs originates from pre­training or human
alignment, experiments with the mixtral8x7b foundational model and the function­agnostic
zero­shot prompt were conducted. The foundational model failed entirely to follow the
QuestionAnswer, figure 6.3, function signature (0% success). This result is biased as

Tool­augmented LLMs in the medical domain. Building on the work of Liévin et al.
2023 [51], this thesis extends the range of open­source models evaluated with ’Direct’ and
’5­shot CoT’ prompts, incorporating the codellama series and mistral7b and mixtral8x7b
models. Further, the pinnacle of this thesis is the development of a Wikipedia­augmented
agent, which significantly enhances the ’Direct’ prompt’s performance when using the
mistral8x7b­instructv0.1 model. The absence of a significant performance increase for
the ’5­shot CoT’ prompt underscores the efficacy of the few­shot paradigm introduced by
Brown et al. 2020 [1]. This may at first sound as a dull result, however, in the critical
domain of medicine, providing a source for the information that a LLM response is based
on is vital, as it enhances the trustworthiness and reliability of the models. Therefore, the
Wikipedia­augmented agents can be seen as a step in the right direction.
In comparison with the USMLE and MedMCQA literature, table 5.2, the performance of
this thesis is best in its class (open­source and in­context learning). The mixtral8x7b­
instructv0.1 using the Wiki­augmented ’5­shot CoT’ prompt achieves a score of 65.6% on
the USMLE and 58.1% on the MedMCQA benchmark compared to the previous best of
respectively 62.5% and 53.6% [51].
Integration of Wikipedia as an external knowledge source. The selection of Wikipedia
as an external knowledge repository was an obvious decision, attributed to its open­
access nature, comprehensive API, and significant academic recognition. This approach
diverges from that of IALM [38], as the search query formulation in this thesis for the
Wikipedia search is delegated to the LLM. For the USMLE dataset, the agent is able to
generate self­evaluated relevant context for 60.3% of the questions. As it is the LLM­
based agent that generates the search query, summarizes the article, evaluates the sum­
mary, and finally uses the relevant summaries for answering the medical questions, it is
necessary to objectively assess the intermediate results of the agents, as in figure 7.9,
which for a single question shows that the context is beneficial and that it is sensible. Fur­
ther, in the same line of thought, is it interesting to reflect on the impact of the fact that the
LLM have been trained on the entire Wikipedia corpus. Assuming that the performance
on the medical benchmarks are an estimator for the performance of the agent flows, it is
interesting that the LLM is able to generate search queries that lead to relevant context,
but that the model is not able to select the most relevant article. These reflections are a
testimony to the black­box nature of deep learning models, here among LLMs.
Towards a structured output. The performance of various LLMs on the USMLE and
MedMCQA benchmarks, when utilizing the structured output of the QuestionAnswer type,
was observed to lie between the outcomes of ’Direct’ and ’5­shot CoT’ prompts. This is
somehow anticipated, given that the QuestionAnswer format essentially comprises an
explanation followed by a response, paralleling the structure of a zero­shot CoT prompt.
As the answer extraction process of LLMs are tedious, this approach could alleviate the
struggle of writing regular expressions in the hope of extracting the answer of the LLMs
and avoid the two­step generation required in zero­shot CoT prompts.

Conclusion
Results
This research aimed to enhance open­source instruction­based LLMs with tool augmenta­
tion in the medical domain. The primary goal was to investigate the extension challenges
of state­of­the­art approaches and potentially surpass their capabilities. This goal was
achieved by a foundational understanding of machine learning principles, an assessment
of the current landscape of LLMs, an extensive literature review on augmented language
models, the selection of medical benchmarks and tools, the implementation of an infer­
ence framework for LLMs, the exploration of a function­agnostic zero­shot prompting ap­
proach, and the evaluation of a Wikipedia­augmented agent against medical benchmarks
using established methodologies.
The empirical evaluation focused on the mixtral8x7b­instructv0.1 LLM, two medical bench­
marks, USMLE and MedMCQA and two types of prompts: ’Direct’ and ’5­shot CoT’. The
results indicate an improvement of 5.5%­5.7% and 0.9%­1.0% in performance, respec­
tively, when utilizing a Wikipedia­augmented agent for context generation. Albeit the en­
hancement for the ’5­shot CoT’ prompt is not significant, opposed to the ’Direct’ prompt,
it is still the superior approach, as evidenced by its higher overall performance (USMLE
65.6%, MedMCQA 58.1%) compared to the ’Direct’ approach (USMLE 62.0%, MedM­
CQA 56%). These findings highlight the importance of tailoring augmentation strategies
to suit the specific requirements and format of each prompt type. For the ’Direct’ prompt,
the most effective configuration involved the use of the complete top Wikipedia article.
Thus, this approach proved efficient in simple query scenarios, emphasizing the value of
direct integration. In contrast, for the ’5­shot CoT’ prompt, a more complex augmentation
strategy, involving the integration of Wikipedia with summarization and filtering, was more
effective, indicating the necessity for nuanced augmentation in reasoning­based prompts.
The pivotal component in Wikipedia­augmented agents is the function­agnostic zero­shot
prompt designed for generating Typescript code corresponding to a given Typescript type
definition function signature. This prompt instructs the model’s output to align with the
function signature, where the most capable model, mixtral8x7b­instructv0.1, exhibits a
success rate ranging from 84.2% to 99%, contingent upon the specific function and user
input. This prompt may not be perfect, however it does demonstrate the capability of an
open­source instruct LLM to incorporate tools for operational use, and thus, closes the
gap between open­source LLMs and the state­of­the­art OpenAI Function Calling API.
Future work
Due to the scope of the thesis and the findings, interesting questions that call for further
research have appeared.
Expanding the agent’s action space. Future work should explore broadening the agent’s
action space, such as integrating other medical­domain tools like FindZebra 1 [69]. Imple­
menting a decision­making mechanism to select between tools like Wikipedia, FindZebra,
or none could further refine the agent’s functionality. Additionally, enhancing the agent
with capabilities for recursive reasoning, reflection, and memory would allow for iterative
function calls until relevant context is obtained or confidence in the answer’s accuracy is

Shinn et al. 2023 [70], Hao et al. 2023 [71], and Madaan et al. 2023 [72].
Prompting versus vocabulary restriction. Recent trends in LLM research involve re­
stricting the model’s vocabulary during inference to enforce specific output formats2. This

A.3
McNemar’s test
McNemar’s Test is a statistical method used to determine whether there are differences
in paired proportions. This test is particularly suited for binary outcomes in a 2x2 contin­
gency table with a matched­pairs design. The test assumes that the data is paired binary
variables and that the pairss are independent of each other. As the comparison is be­
tween a baseline and a Wiki­augmented extension the data is paired and the variables
are binary. Furthermore, the samples of the datasets can be assumed to be independent.
Contingency Table
Data is arranged in a 2x2 contingency table:
Method Y: 1
Method Y: 0
Method X: 1
a
b
Method X: 0
c
d
where:
• a: Number of samples where both methods agree on the positive outcome.
• b: Number of samples with a positive outcome for method X and a negative outcome
for method Y.
• c: Number of samples with a negative outcome for method X and a positive outcome
for method Y.
• d: Number of samples where both methods agree on the negative outcome.
McNemar’s Test Statistic
The test statistic is calculated as:
χ2 = (b −c)2
b + c
This statistic follows a chi­square distribution with 1 degree of freedom under the null
hypothesis.
P­value Calculation
The p­value is the probability of observing a test statistic as extreme as, or more extreme
than, the observed value, under the null hypothesis. It is calculated using the chi­square
distribution:
p­value = P(χ2 ≥value)
Analysis of USMLE and MedMCQA
The contingency tables for the USMLE is presented in the main results chapter, however
for readability it will be provided here along with the contingency for the MedMCQA.
The test statistic for the ’Direct’ prompt on the USMLE is:
χ2 = (139 −69)2
139 + 69
= 23.6
Whereafter, the chi­squared distribution using 1 degree of freedom is used to calculate
the p­value. The other test statistics have been calculated using a similar approach.
