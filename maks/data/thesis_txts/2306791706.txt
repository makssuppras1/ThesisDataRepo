Abstract
Why do we do what we do? What future do we want?
The current conditions of the sociotechnological landscape do not encourage human reﬂection and hence,
individuals and society live in an unsustainable mode. Trapped in an endless ﬁght to reduce unsustainability,
which by deﬁnition will never lead to sustainability, we fail to recognise that we have created a system that is
simply not designed to create sustainability: the possibility that life on Earth will ﬂourish forever.
A theoretical model to create sustainability and its corresponding applied methodology have been developed.
Transitional and motivational theories are the theoretical foundation of a methodology that uses the transition
to the circular economy as a lever to engage businesses in the food system in the transition to sustainability;
simultaneously, it helps circular niche-innovations to be ready to enter the regime. The methodology is a tool
to make participants experience other ways to satisfy their natural human needs or concerns and ultimately
ﬂourish. Conversely to the behaviours that the current landscape motivates, these alternative actions and
behaviours are socially and environmentally sustainable, because in fact, sustainability is the essence of being
humans.
The methodology has been partially applied in three businesses in the city of Copenhagen with promising
results: i) feedback from participants suggests that in the co-ideation workshops conditions for the integration
and internalisation of extrinsic goals that can lead to authentic humans have been created; and ii) the rough
pre-assessment of the generated options proves their potential to be environmentally and ﬁnancially sound and
thus, to enter the regime. On the other hand, the two last steps (develop and deliver) do not only need to be put
in practice but also need to be properly designed.

Preface
This report is imperfect. I believe that what I have written is wrong or at least relatively wrong. It is the analysis of
reality through my lenses. Our perspective over reality is inﬂuenced by an inﬁnite array of circumstances; physical
(our body, brain, the way we perceive reality), historical (the current epoch and society), philosophical (the way
we understands life), daily contextual (what we have read each day, ours everyday mood), etc. circumstances
have moulded this work.
Thus, the aim is not to set out to pontiﬁcate on the topic. The intention is rather to encourage the reader
to do the same exercise I have done while working on this project, i.e. to explore, learn and act. This means to
ﬁrst analyse reality (the world, the universe, life, the level is really up to each of us) considering different ways
to see it and to select your own way(s); and then, to try to understand it, accept it and ﬁnally, to take actions
according to what one has seen. In my case, the actions that have led from the previous steps are: writing this
report and generating a guideline that aims to change circumstances so that society’s lenses may broaden up
and let human beings ﬂourish on Earth.

CHAPTER 1
INTRODUCTION
We, human beings, live on Earth. After at least 3.5 billion years of evolution, Earth has become a complex
ecosystem with uncountable compartments (subsystems)1 and connections among those subsystems. Different
ﬁelds have shown that Earth and its multiple ecosystems evolve to maximise their embodied emergy2(Odum,
1995) or to increase their ability to degrade incoming sun energy (Schneider and Kay, 1994). Their goal is to
optimise the functionality of the ecosystem, i.e. their ability to meet changes in their environment with only
little or almost no internal change (Nielsen, 2007), which matches with the Gaia hypothesis: the interaction of
organisms with their surrounding aims at maintaining the conditions for life on the planet.
Emergent properties of a system are attributes that arise from the interactions of its parts and are more than
the sum of properties of the individual parts. Different authors have listed a broad variety of emergent properties
that nature has followed during evolution (Table A.1 Appendix A). Nielsen (2007) lists the following ten:
• Complexity
• Evolution
• Compartmentalisation
• Flows and processes
• Feedbacks and controls
• Cycling
• Network properties
• Organisation and hierarchy
• Diversity
• Open systems and dissipation
The current scientiﬁc understanding of most of these properties is not enough to implement any guiding
principle and further research has been suggested. Flows and processes, cycling and thermodynamics are
better understood, yet a wider utilisation of available options and the development of techniques for more
implementation are recommended (Nielsen, 2007).
This work focuses on the cycling of matter. In nature matter cycles due to biological, chemical and geological
processes and waste as such does not exist: at any given stage matter is a useful resource for some compartment.
Some people might argue that humans do not need to accept emergent properties and that we can absolutely
engineer the planet, in other words that we are cleverer than nature. I cannot claim that they are wrong. Here we
come into a much deeper discussion: what future do we want? A wide spectrum of scenarios can be imagined.
On one end of the spectrum, we could imagine a matrix like world perfectly described by (Crist, 2012) as a
“totalitarian conversion of the natural world into a domain of resources to serve a human supremacist way of
life, and the consequent destruction of all the intrinsic wealth of its natural places, beings, and elements” and on
the other end of the spectrum, a “natural world” like that before the industrial revolution or even before any
human civilisation appeared, and that still sustains billions of human beings (Figure 1.1).
It is a matter of choosing and I think the decision is worth a thought and deserves an agreed decision. Indeed,
I would love to have a worldwide voting: what future do we want?
However, if one observes the current development of the world, it is obvious that the decision has already
been taken: we are far into the process of creating an engineered world. Thus, we might have already decided

The cure for blindness
Education is a powerful tool that can enable or inhibit the questioning habits of students (Shor, 1992). Substitute
the word education for learning and students for humanity and we come to the fact that learning can cure the
blindness of humanity. But, what if education/learning was also blind? Even more, what if it was doubly blind?
First, as indicated by Vare and W. Scott (2007), western education for sustainable development (ESD) is:
• promoting / facilitating changes in what we do,
• promoting (informed, skilled) behaviours and ways of thinking, where the need for this is clearly identiﬁed
and agreed and
• teaching us for sustainable development.
This has made sustainability become a frame of mind (Blüdorn, 2002) where the role of non-experts is to
be guided by “experts” (Vare and W. Scott, 2007). Second, Webster (2013) points out fallacies on which ESD is
operating, such as something as widely accepted as that recycling is always a good idea.
The truth is that we are facing a wicked problem that has no stopping rule, that is unique and for which
solutions are not right or wrong and above all, there are no given solutions. To overcome this situation these
authors agree on the need to add a complementary learning process to the current ESD that is be based on:
• building capacity to think critically about (and beyond) what experts say and to test ideas for sustainable
development,
• exploring the contradictions inherent in sustainable living and
• learning as sustainable development.
If that is done, ESD will become a collaborative and reﬂective learning process that captures the inter-generational
dimension and the idea of environmental limits (Vare and W. Scott, 2007). In this way, human beings will be
prepared for sustainable development understood as a process of making the emergent future ecologically
sound and humanly habitable as it emerges, through the continuous responsive learning, which is the most
characteristic endowment of the human species (Foster, 2002). In other words, because it is the conditions of
the system which are making people’s behaviour anew (Webster, 2013), we need to push people to act in a way
that is informed, yet unconstrained, by our understanding of the past (Grant, 2012)

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
A circular economy (CE) is one that is restorative and regenerative by design, and which aims to keep products,
components and materials at their highest utility and value at all times (Ellen McArthur Foundation, 2015b).
It seeks to deliberately create an anthroposphere, such as that in Figure 1.1, that resembles nature and always
treats matter as a resource.
The mission of the anthropogenic food system (AFS) is to feed the human population and to obey the planet’s
emergent properties (we focus on cycling above all). The following facts show how the current AFS fails to
achieve its mission:
• One ninth of the world population does not have enough food to lead a healthy active life (FAO, 2015).
• Up to one third of all food produced is lost or wasted(FAO, 2011).
• Food service packaging items in the United States of America make up 1.4 % of the total waste by weight
(Food service Packaging Insitute, 2016).
• Food production runs on inorganic fertilisers. Chemical fertiliser is the fuel that has powered the Green
Revolution´s 3 forward thrust (Borlaug, 1970).
• Irrigation and deforestation alter the water cycle at a local level and so does climate change (agriculture
alone stands for up to one third of global greenhousegas (GHG) emissions (Vermeulen et al., 2012)) at a

LITERATURE REVIEW
When trying to spot the origins of the Circular Economy concept the literature mentions different authors
such as Pearce and Turner (1989), Stahel and Reday-Mulvey (1981) or Frosch and Gallopoulos (1989). Over
time, schools of thoughts that deal with sustainability have steadily arisen. Examples are Industrial Ecology,
Biomimicry, Natural Capitalism, Cradle to Cradle, the Performance Economy or the Blue Economy. In 2009, the
Ellen McArthur Foundation (EMF) somehow uniﬁed previous and contemporary schools of thoughts when it
was founded with the goal of globally boosting the CE.
In this work the term circular economy is understood as a broad and abstract concept that encompasses
or works together with previous schools of thoughts and that aims to deliberately create an environmentally
sustainable world. Sustainability is understood as "the possibility that humans and other kinds of life will ﬂourish
on Earth forever". Flourishing is deﬁned as having meaning and love, feeling alive and being authentic, living in
a community and being valued for what we are and feeling that we are contributing to the good; in other words,
being human (Ehrenfeld, 2008). CE and not another school of thoughts has been chosen because at present it
seems to have caught the attention of relevant stakeholders to inspire a real transition towards environmental
and economic sustainability. In this work the focus is mainly placed on environmental sustainability because I
believe that scarcity of resources and the resulting inability of many human beings to survive are a basic cause of
social and environmental unsustainability. The social and economic pillars are also taken into consideration.
Considering this broad deﬁnition of CE, research on CE is absolutely extensive. Some of the main research
ﬁelds are (Lieder and Rashid, 2015):
• Economic concepts and analyses to the problems of formulating rational managerial decisions
• Enterprise operations, including controlling, leading, monitoring, organizing and planning
• Sustainability science simultaneously involving studies with economic, environmental and societal criteria
• Design, operation, control, and optimization of chemical, physical, and biological processes (process
engineering)
• Management and law containing studies on policies that are enforced through social institutions to govern
behaviour
• Studies concerning society and the relationships among individuals within a society
• Transformation of economic structures and business rationales
• Regenerative design and critical materials
• Industrial ecology
• Remanufacturing and closed-loop supply chains
• Supply chain management
• Resource conservative manufacturing
• Governmental CE initiatives
• Material and energy ﬂows
• Environmental impacts
This literature review has focused on those ﬁelds that could provide useful information to propose a transition
methodology. At a broader level the aim of this review is to put the methodology into the big picture and to

Deﬁnitions
• Biological materials: organic materials that, once used, can be disposed of in any natural environment

2.3
Limitations of CE
The broad deﬁnition of CE used, as well as the envisioned cycles previously presented (Figure 1.1) look perfect
in theory but encounter some practical limitations that the literature has already pointed out.
Recycling and material looping can cause health and safety problems due to chemicals contained in products
(Grundmann et al., 2013) and there is a potential of impurities to accumulate in metals (Løvik and D. B. Müller,
2014). Rammelt and Crisp (2014) challenge the shift from products to services arguing that the latter do also
require physical matter.
The shift to renewable energy is a hotspot; It is doubted not only how energy demand can be covered with
current technologies, but also whether material needs to build those technologies can be covered (Mentink,
2014). The limitations of technology as a standalone solution are stressed by Rammelt and Crisp 2014 arguing
that decoupling of environmental impact and gross domestic product (GDP) by efﬁciency improvements might
just be ocurring at local scale by means of worsening conditions in other areas in the world and ultimately at
global scale. A challenging question for which no answer is detected, is how a circular economy that further
stimulates economic growth will supply energy and matter for an ever growing demand (Rammelt and Crisp,
2014). Finally, Murray et al. (2015) mention how the social dimension has not been incorporated into the CE
dialogue and how this can damage CE because equity and social justice are at the heart of sustainability.
The good news is that all these limitations are based on the acceptance of current conditions. Although they
do limit the development of CE nowadays and should deﬁnitely be taken into account, they are not a limitation
for CE as such, if CE is seen as a learning process, the possibility to create.
Looking at the process of ﬁnding and creating solutions, a common limitation is that solutions are often
sought in the past or are highly inﬂuenced by it. For instance, suggestions like "a change back to home delivery
of milk in glass bottles" or "biomass based energy production" arise from “anti” attitudes that ﬁnd current
conditions wrong and thus, focus on putting an end to them. The problem is that a switch back to a past
condition leads to exactly the same condition where the current condition we are trying to chage emerged and
hence, chances for it to re-emerge are extremely high, making the whole process useless (Oretga y Gasset, 1930).
The review of CE´s limitations reinforces the previously mentioned need for a system change. Innovation,
search for alternatives, systems thinking, creativity and informed, yet unconstrained, understanding of the past
are core concepts of what is needed. Under this new perspective, limitations are transformed into challenges for
which the creation of alternatives is needed and CE becomes an inﬁnite, always evolving process of learning as
sustainable development. In this regard, it is useful to accept and assume that any envisioned system is just
something imagined and that it is not perfect nor an expected stage in the future. Learning as SD means that we
know that no ﬁnal envisioned world will ever be “the” world; the steady state system we want to live in forever
does not exist. CE is an endless process, a state of mind (Webster, 2013).

2.4
Transition theories
This perspective about CE can be a relief for practitioners and people who believe another world is necessary
and feel that not much advance is being done. If we accept that whatever we do is right or wrong only because
of our circumstance and that it might and will probably be wrong under the perspective of human beings in the
future and we simultaneously accept that we are just a small piece of a never ending process, we will understand
that achieving or failing any target we set for ourselves is not ﬁnal for the whole process. Indeed, what really
matters about whatever we do is that we try to generate a whole change in our system, that we intend to educate
humankind so that it is prepared to work under new conditions.
Socio-technical transition theory proposed Strategic Niche Management as a pathway to shift current
regimes into sustainable ones (Kemp et al., 1998) and has been mainly applied via Alternative Technology social
movements (Berkhout et al., 2004). Niches are experiments that aim to create something new and concrete (and
sustainable in our case) that has the potential to be scaled up to a societal level (Bulkeley and Castán Broto,
2013). Their emergence provides perfect conditions for different actors: for the developers to ﬁnd sensible and
user-friendly ways of providing services, for users to learn to use them and adapt them to their everyday life, and
for regulators to learn how to govern them (Jurgilevich et al., 2016). The widely used multi-level perspective on
transitions (Figure 2.2) suggests that when niche-innovations inﬂuenced by the socio-technological landscape
and regime get a momentum and in parallel landscape level changes create pressure on the regime, opportunity
windows for the niche-innovations to enter the regime and potentially become mainstream appear (F. W. Geels
and Schot, 2007).
Figure 2.2: Multi-level perspective on transitions (F. W. Geels and Schot, 2007)
The multi-level perspective has been discussed and criticised by the literature and F. W. Geels and Schot
(2007) have deﬁned four different pathways based on varying timing and nature of interactions between the
three levels:
• Transformation path: moderate landscape pressure when niche-innovations have not yet been sufﬁciently
developed to which regime actors will respond by modifying the direction of development paths and
innovation activities.
• De-alignment and re-alignment path: divergent, large and sudden landscape change leads to regime
problems that may cause regime actors to lose faith (de-alignment of the regime). If no niche-innovation is
9
==PAGE:22==

sufﬁciently developed a space for the emergence of multiple niche- innovations that co-exist and compete
for attention and resources is created until eventually, one niche-innovation becomes dominant, forming
the core for re-alignment of a new regime.
• Technological substitution: there is much landscape pressure at a moment when niche- innovations have
developed sufﬁciently, the latter will break through and replace the existing regime.
• Reconﬁguration pathway: symbiotic innovations, which developed in niches, are initially adopted in the
regime to solve local problems. They subsequently trigger further adjustments in the basic architecture of
the regime.
Although by deﬁnition all the pathways entail a change, it is obvious that the role different actors play and
the degree and direction of the change varies for each pathway. Thus, in order to succeed1 it might be interesting
to ﬁrst analyse current conditions, as well as to explore how to transform them for our beneﬁt. For the former,
the answer to the following questions might clarify what current chances for each pathway to materialise are:
• Are niche innovations (CE solutions) sufﬁciently developed?
• Is the current landscape changing? If so, how much?
• How big is the landscape pressure on the regime?
In his masterpiece, Sustainability by Design: A Subversive Strategy for Transforming Our Consumer Culture,
Ehrenfeld did not only give answers to some of those and other related questions, but he went on and suggested
a pragmatic strategy to transform the socio-technological landscape or the structures of signiﬁcation (as he
names the landscape). He theorised that the most pragmatic way to go is to design tangible tools with which
we daily interact so that they (i) build our skills in observation and reﬂection and (ii) embed scripts that help
us to replace unsustainable beliefs and norms. Regarding current institutional tools (collective processes), he
suggested to (iii) substitute those that reinforce unsustainable norms and beliefs with new ones. If that is done,
he argued, broader behavioral and structural changes will follow.
A last reading of the analysis of transitions, which is often pointed out by the literature, is the need to combine
bottom-up approaches with top-down changes in order to achieve the expected regime shift (Smith, 2006; Kemp
et al., 1998; Berkhout et al., 2004; Lieder and Rashid, 2015).
2.5
Human modes of existence
At a deeper level of analysis of the reality the opposed concepts of determinism and free will appear2. From a
deterministic point of view every event in life is predetermined by conditions and is what must happen. On the
other hand, the doctrine of the free will defends that human freedom of choice is not illusory. It then results
that to make people act in the way we consider appropriate it is essential to change the conditions. When this
happens, “deterministic” people will accept those conditions and act according to them; whereas “freely acting
people” will get the chance to learn, understand the limits of Earth, Earth’s development over time and the
complexity and relatedness of the whole system (Webster, 2013) and choose to act according to those "rules"
or not. In any case, nowadays the majority of people around the world have an indeterministic perspective
(Sarkissian et al., 2010) and hence, the second case applies more extensively.
Heidegger´s theory of existence modes is used by Ehrenfeld (2008) to argue in favour of the authentic mode.
This theory explains that human beings are unable to understand the essence of being because they fall in a
never ending loop: observers need to understand themselves ﬁrst, otherwise every observation they make is
self-referential, because observers interpret and give it a meaning according to the historical record of their life’s
experience. When what is being observed is oneself, we fall in an endless loop. Heidegger observed that people
take different postures towards this fact:
• Authentic: accept the groundlessness (inﬁnte loop) of being and act and be the way we present ourselves
to others.

Motivational theory deﬁnes motivation as a cyclical process where needs or desires push one to behave in a
way that will satisfy needs (sometimes through a reward) and where the ﬁnal performance affects our thoughts

Although imperfect and criticised by many, the hierarchy of needs by Maslow (1943) (Figure 2.4) explains
that after the ﬁrst two levels of needs are fulﬁllfed, humans have the need to feel love and belongingness, to feel
respected (esteem) and ultimately, to accomplish everything they can.

Ehrenfeld (2008) talks about concerns instead of needs: activities we engage in for the sake of something having

• Synergic: aim at one domain but also satisfy other domains. These are also called focal experiences.
• Inhibiting: address a speciﬁc concern but interfere with others.

Controlled motivation, such as ﬁnancial incentives, fear or punishments undermines human ability to solve

positive emotions. Mind openness, broad thinking, discovery of integrative solutions or pattern recognition are
at the same time triggered by positive emotions (Grant, 2012).
Ryan and Deci (2000) tried to understand how individuals acquire the motivation to perform nonintrinsically
motivated practices. The processes for that to happen are internalisation, taking in values and integration,
transforming those values into one’s own principles or judgments. These processes were found to be fostered by
contexts supportive of relatedness (the need to feel belongingness and connectedness with others), competence
and autonomy (Ryan and Deci, 2000). In a similar way, Pink (2009) mentions three elements to maintain the
motivation for 21st century tasks, tasks that ﬁnd solutions for wicked problems:
• autonomy: the urge to direct our own lives.
• mastery: the desire to get better at something that matters and
• purpose: the yearning to do what we do in the service of something larger than ourselves.
Arguing in the same direction, recent research has shown that motivation is usually strong when one has a
vision and faith in one’s abilities (Sasson, 2016) and that people are more productive when they are intrinsically
motivated by the work itself and have positive perceptions of their colleagues and the organisation (Kramer and
Amabile, 2011). Relevant as well are positive perceptions over the course of workdays that have been found to be
triggered by (Kramer and Amabile, 2011):
• steps forward or “small wins”,
• catalysts: actions that directly support work, including help from a person or group, and
• nourishers: events such as shows of respect and words of encouragement.

Current guidelines
Methodologies that try to guide businesses to enter the CE follow two main patterns. On the one hand, business
model innovation (BMI) tries to identify CE opportunities that generate value (ﬁnancial proﬁt). On the other
hand, material ﬂow analysis (MFA) based methodologies aim to optimise and improve internal processes within
companies. Guidelines in the latter group do not implicitly mention CE but evidence a similar focus. Table 2.1
and Table 2.2 compare guidelines in the non-scientiﬁc literature among each other and with the basic steps
of the design process. The ﬁrst six guidelines are BMI and the last two MFA based. In the following, the main
patterns of these methodologies are discussed and relevant points are highlighted.
Business model innovation guidelines
Prior to the discovery phase, Mentink (2014) includes a preparation phase where an innovation team that is
balanced in terms of knowledge, skills and attitude is formed, and where the size and the inclusion or not of
external parties are decided. Mentink (2014) does also include a learning step within this ﬁrst phase where
participants get to understand the concept of CE. This CE learning process is, indeed, how the discovery phase
starts in most guidelines. WBCSD (2011) goes further with an initial assessment of the issue, life cycle impacts,
stakeholders and solutions. In the deﬁnition of the point of view, stakeholder analysis is a widely used tool and
a tendency to expand its scope to the whole supply and value chains is perceived. The analysis of drivers and
barriers for CE is also suggested, as well as questioning business aspects such as value proposition, materials,
design or use and end of life of products. Mentink (2014) advises to think in functional needs: the performance a
product can deliver. For the generation of ideas creative processes such as brainstorming, analysis of existing
CE options or even a ﬁve step process for service based business models are put forward. The generated
ideas are invariably ﬁnancially evaluated and environmental indicators are also recommended. Additional
recommendations include a compatibility check of the ideas with the business model and company targets. The
development phase is not addressed in all the methodologies. The need to prepare a plan for implementation is
mentioned as well as the use of trials, prototypes and pilots. Mentink (2014) indicates the necessity to make sure
that feedback ﬂows and inﬂuences the coming trials. The ﬁnal step, deliver, is only addressed by WBCSD (2011),
who highlights the importance of assessing the performance after implementation, evaluating factors that have
shaped the outcome and ﬁnally, communicating the results.

2.8. Conditions for innovation
Regarding their reason to be, the aforementioned guidelines describe their goals with statements like identify
opportunities for creating circular business or start making the journey to new, circular business models in the
case of BMI and improve productivity, help businesses reap the beneﬁts of reducing waste, developing sustainable
products and using resources in an efﬁcient way or allow companies to be part of sustainable trade for the MFA
based ones.
How business model innovation for sustainability really happens
The presented BMI methodologies are conceptual guidelines based on theoretical foundations. However, after
the analysis of case studies, Roome and Louche (2015) showed that these conceptualisations might not exactly
match the real processes that take place when companies revise and transform their business model in order to
contribute to sustainable development. Rather than revising their business model ﬁrst and then transforming
themselves, the authors found out that the revised business model was the outcome of a transformation process
where companies aimed at reducing their damage on resources while they economically succeeded. Figure
2.8 depicts the empirically found process that starts with an exploration and identiﬁcation of ideas (changing
economic, social and environmental context; destruction of environmental value and human relationships by
their current business, etc.) from outside the company that are triggered by problematic event(s) and where
a cross organisational network acts as facilitator. Then, the learning is developed by a community within the
company into an operational reality that is adapted to the company setting. In the third step, the knowledge is
embedded into the company, profound changes take place and the business model is revised. Collaborative
structures of actors in- and outside the company are also created. Finally, these structures are consolidated and
the company´s new approach is shared with a wide internal and external audience.
Figure 2.8: A process model business model change for sustainability (Roome and Louche, 2015)
From these ﬁndings the relevance of setting up the proper conditions and triggers for the chain of steps to
occur is clear. As Hill (2014) remarks, innovation leaders create the proper playground for innovation to happen.
A review of what those conditions could be follows next.

Material Flow Analysis (MFA)
Material ﬂow analysis is a tool to quantify the ﬂows and stocks of materials or substances within a deﬁned system
in space and time (Baccini and Brunner, 2012). Its ﬁrst step is to deﬁne the system and list its variables; mass
balance equations (from system deﬁnition, without parameters) and model approach equations are formulated
then and ﬁnally, the equations system is solved for all system variables with an algebraic or matrix inversion
approach (D. Müller, 2015). Results are usually presented in sankey diagrams that can be done with, for example,
STAN software. Papargyropoulou et al. (2016) proposed a conceptual framework for the study of food waste
generation in hospitality that used MFA with data from a waste audit.
Life cycle assessment (LCA)
Life cycle assessment is an internationally standardised method that quantiﬁes all the resources consumed, the
emissions and the associated potential environmental impacts of a product or system throughout its whole
life-cycle (European Commission, 2010). This method has four basic steps that are iteratively performed:
goal and scope deﬁnition, life cycle inventory, life-cycle impact assessment and interpretation. Attributional
modelling is used when the impact of two alternatives with the same functional unit wants to be compared.
The environmental load of the co-products in multifunctional processes is determined by physical properties if
the process cannot be subdivided. SimaPro is an LCA software that includes the world’s most comprehensive
database: Ecoinvent (Goedkoop et al., 2016). For the impact assessment phase, European Commission Joint
Research Centre (2011) recommended models and characterisation factors for a European context that is
included as an impact assessment method in SimaPro. Global warming, acidiﬁcation, eutrophication, resource
depletion, water depletion and land use have been identiﬁed as some of the most relevant impact categories for
food production (Webb et al., 2013).
Cost-beneﬁt analysis (CBA)
Cost-beneﬁt analysis is a tool used to compare different projects for which costs and beneﬁts can be monetised.
The outcome of cost and beneﬁts over time is predicted and usually the net present value of the options is
estimated applying a discount rate (this has not been done in this study)(Commission, 2009).
Circularity indicators
The search in the literature for indicators that could help to assess the circular performance of businesses did
not point out at any widely accepted and used indicator. Ellen McArthur Foundation (2015c) proposes some
indicators that link material and ﬁnancial ﬂows and Ellen McArthur Foundation (2015b) suggests recycling rates
or emission per monetary ﬂows. Legrand et al. (2010) present a set of indicator to measure the level of sustainable
performance of restaurant but none of them focuses on the ﬂow of materials. The material circularity indicators
proposed in Ellen McArthur Foundation (2015a) (a project that attempted to create a standardise indicator
and tools to measure it) give hints on how circularity indicators might look like, but it focuses exclusively on
technical cycles.

CHAPTER 3
CE TRANSITION METHODOLOGY
The insights from the previous chapter have been used to propose a CE transition methodology. The place of the
methodology from the transition perspective and its engagement strategies are commented before stepping
into a description of its phases.
The big picture
The literature has shown that for a sociotechnological transition to occur different pathways are likely. Yet, which
of them or which of their combinations will prosper remains unknown. Hence, the aim of this methodology is to
make sure that a circular transition takes place regardless of the pathway we follow.
• Transformation path: we will show to regime actors with real case studies what CE is capable of achieving
so that the development paths they choose are environmentally sustainable.
• De-alignment and re-alignment path: we will foster the creation of competitive niche-innovations so that
they become the dominant. Trials, experimentation and learning will ensure that this happens.
• Technological substitution: we will develop niche-innovations enough so that they are prepared to replace
the current regime.
• Reconﬁguration pathway: we will show with case studies how niche-innovations match up perfectly with
the current regime and solve local problems.
In addition, the methodology seeks a change on conditions, circumstances or the socio-technological landscape
that amke visible for human beings an array of actions (focal experiences) that can help us to ﬂourish (satisfy all
the areas of concern) and implicitely create social and environmental sustainability. Although the transition
ﬁeld has not come up with a model to explain what makes the landscape change (the effect of regime shifts is
currently being discussed (F. Geels, 2016)), Ehrenfeld (2008) suggests that broader structural and behavioural
change will happen if people experience reﬂection moments that drive them to the authentic mode. In any case,
whatever the effect of niches and regimes on the landscape is, if these are circular it could be assumed that they
will inﬂuence it in a "positive" way.
Put it in other words, the methodology is a tool that tries to create conditions that allow us to learn how to
sustainably develop, while we develop. It plants a seed into individuals in our society that shows them how
another path is possible, a path that beneﬁts the nested entities: individuals, groups and the planet. Pollination
and spreading of such ideas and beliefs follow.
Methodology itself
The methodology has three different levers to make sure that participating businesses ﬁnd a reason to engage in
this journey. Levers are not used simultaneously, instead which one to use is selected during the process.
The need to adapt the way humanity operates to natural conditions and/or the unbeatable opportunity
the circular economy offers to do so in a ﬁnancially beneﬁcial manner have been the main incentives used by
sustainability transition advocates so far (Section 2.8). These incentives are either extrinsic (money) or rather
threats than incentives. However, motivational theory has proved the beneﬁts for individuals, companies and
eventually society of intrinsically motivated workers who work autonomously, aim to be masters and see how

Chapter 3. CE transition methodology
Ideation: co-ideation workshop
The main objective of the ideation phase as Table 3.3 presents is to generate circular options and make them
ready for the development phase or even start developing them. However, and because this is the phase where
the facilitator establishes most of the contact with staff, customers and other stakeholders, the workshop is also
used with these other purposes:
• Make clear how the Earth system works in terms of matter ﬂows.
• Present CE as a new paradigm with uncountable opportunities to be discovered: CE is about creating, not
ﬁxing.
Additionally, speciﬁc goals in function of the main focus of the business are set:
$ Financial focus
– Make visible to participants the inﬁnite array of new business opportunities that CE offers.
E Environmental focus
– Show to participants the chance that CE offers to create an environmentally sustainable food system
starting with changes at their hand.
H Focus on well-being
– Internalise and integrate ﬁrst-order goals to intrinsically motivate managers, staff and other stake-
holders. This goals are related to creating a circular system.
– Present a way to make employees feel autonomy and mastery at their workplace when trying to
achieve those goals.
– Improve motivation and willingness to work by giving employees the possibility to work together,
to see how the implementation of CE alternatives they have implemented are small steps towards
fulﬁlling their ﬁrst-order goals (indicators are used for that) and to gain admiration and respect from
other people.
– Provide a tool to align personal and organisational values.
F Flourishing
– Create a moment of reﬂection for participants, where they get to analyse why they do what they do.
– Present the idea of Authentic mode of being, of focal experiences and the human areas of concern
and present a humble way to generate focal experiences at work, that generate a ﬂourishing reality.
– Design CE solutions that are synergic and that create reﬂection moments for customers or other
stakeholders.
Goals for different business types/mindsets should be understood as nested like in a matryoshka doll: e.g.
those who focus on the environment do also pursue the same goals as those focused on ﬁnances, but prioritise
goals in their level. However, the last attitude, ﬂourishing, does not necessarily include any ﬁnancial interest.
The co-ideation workshop is also proposed with the goal of generating the spark for the change to sus-
tainability within the business and its stakeholders. A participatory and opened activity might enhance moral
encouragement and an exchange of ideas with actors out of the business might help to incorporate new perspec-
tives and open the mind for unknown alternatives. Also, it will create the relatedness, competence and autonomy
conditions to foster internalisation and integration that turn extrinsic motivations into self determined. An
informal atmosphere is sought through gamiﬁcation and storytelling to create fun and safe conditions that
foster participants’ active participation and diminishes their fear. The values of the facilitator are made explicit
to avoid being seen as irrational compared with the current regime. At ﬁrst, participants are guided to create
options that encompass their business, but then these can be expanded and involve any actor in the supply
chain or in the neighbourhood, city or any other larger scale.
The co-ideation workshop has no predeﬁned structure and is created and customised for each of the
participating businesses. In any case, the following structure is likely:

3.3. Develop
1. Storytelling: bring participants to a new "world" that provides conditions for them to create alternatives
with an understanding of the Earth and its processes but unconstrained by the current regime.
2. Idea generation: individual brainstorming session to create CE options suited for the business. It is usually
triggered by a broad How might we question, supported by more speciﬁc questions to help participants
that are stuck. Outcomes from the MFA help decide in what direction to point the questions.
3. First evaluation: game or role play that allows participants to evaluate the generated CE options: do they
match with core CE concepts? Are they feasible? Are they motivating? Do they address all the areas of
concern?
4. Development: group work in teams with possibility to (role) play that aims at creating more consistent CE
options built on top of generated ideas or combinations of them. How can we make it more futuristic?
What can we add so that it addresses other areas of concern besides "the world"? How would different
affected stakeholders think about it? How shall we change it to make it feasible nowadays (we humans
aren’t likely to abandon the old until we can glimpse the new (Moore Lappe 2010)? Who do we need to
collaborate with?
5. Evaluation, awards and food: prizes to select the most promising options and food for everybody for
creating such amazing ideas (food can also go at any other stage).
6. Invitation: invite participants to create working groups an develop the ideas and projects they liked most
in the near future.
7. Feedback: aimed at improving the co-ideation session. Includes questions such as: What did you learn?
Did you feel motivated? Was it comfortable think out of the box / generate? Suggestions?
Participants are not necessarily only employees o employers; any stakeholder (customers, suppliers, etc.) and
even people outside the business´s sphere are welcome.
3.3
Develop
The aim of the develop phase is to further develop the ideas generated and make them ready for implementation.
At the beginning of this phase the facilitator carries out a circularity, environmental and ﬁnancial pre-assessment
of the discussed options with LCA, CBA and the previously described circularity indicators. Main observed
barriers and possible alternatives to solve them are also discussed. None of these assessments is an exhaustive
analysis, but a rough analysis of what might need to be improved.
Ideally the working groups created in the workshop participates in this phase and the facilitator is just one
more of the group or an external advisor who can help to ﬁnd partners, to make performance assessments
or to develop technological solutions among other things. A self-managed working group is proposed: non-
hierarchical teams where tasks are divided and voluntarily carried out and decisions are made not by consensus
but just providing that no principled objection to them exist (Prize, n.d.). An iterative process is undergone
where the outcomes of the pre-assessment are used to develop ideas. When this leads to substantial variations
in the original idea, the assessment is carried out again and the iterative loop is closed. The results of the
pre-assessment are also used to set goals and motivate participants to pursue "small wins".
Experiment-try & Feedback
Early experimentation and testing of what is being developed is crucial. Simpliﬁed pilot tests and interaction
with customers or other affected stakeholders are recommended to make sure that the solution satisﬁes them.
Feedback ﬂows from the more conceptual idea to the applied one and vice versa are introduced. An option to do
this is to organise idea development workshops where the working group generates alternatives for one speciﬁc
idea making use of the outcomes of the performance pre-assessment and of the early feedback from testing.
A relevant attribute of this phase is that participants experience how it feels to immerse in a participatory
design process. In this way, the levers used to catch businesses’ interest start to become apparent.
3.4
Deliver
By the end of this phase the developed CE alternative(s) should be in place.

This chapter introduces the three case studies where the methodology has been applied. First, businesses are
described: main activity, current waste management, location, facilites, customers, organisation and goals. The
approaching experience is then presented. The results of the material ﬂow analysis and the main ﬁndings come

4.1
Royal Bagel
Royal Bagel is a franchise specialised in bagels, sandwiches and salads that also sells coffee to go, smoothies,
industrial ice-creams and drinks in cans or bottles. The bar where the methodology has been applied keeps the
franchise’s name and business model but runs fully autonomously. The shop is located in a residential area in
Lyngby (Denmark), at walking distance from the train station and from Lyngby lakes (see maps in Appendix C). It
has three rooms: a small toilet, a room used to store food in a freezer, a fridge and shelves, to keep accounts and
also used as ofﬁce; and the main room, not much bigger than the storage room, where customers order and food
is prepared. This last area includes a low table with a couch and two high tables with two high chairs each; up to
six customers can sit and eat there at the same time. With good weather conditions, up to four tables with four
chairs each can be place outside on the pavement (pictures in Appendix C). Currently all the waste generated is
put into the same container for incineration. The average customer, which is also a regular customer, is a young
student (14-18 years old) of one of the three schools in the area. Other less common customer proﬁles are young
people in their late 20s / early 30s coming in couples or groups and mothers with their children (6-10 years
old). Occasionally big orders from the schools or private parties come. The vast majority of customers order
take-away. The main chefs are the boss and Abil (my contact person), who get extra help from young assistants
for some hours once or twice per week. Their goal with the business is to generate an income that allows them
to live in Denmark.
Approach experience
The ﬁrst approach happened face to face in the shop. The details of the project were explained to Abil (the
boss does not speak English and I do not speak Danish) who conveyed the message to the boss and discussed
the idea. The project was marketed as a tool to improve environmental and ﬁnancial performance and they
accepted to join in a matter of days after the ﬁrst contact. However, I believe that their main motivation to join
was to help me to carry out the project.

CHAPTER 5
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
This chapter is divided into three sections: Section 5.1 presents a theoretical transition model for sustainability
and discusses engagement strategies. In the second section results of the case studies are summarised, the
appropriateness of CE options for different business types is discussed and the methodology is evaluated and its
applicability in other circumstances is explored. Lastly, in Section 5.3, a short self-implementation guideline for
businesses is presented.
5.1
Transition model for sustainability
The goal of the transition is sustainability, deﬁned as the possibility that life on Earth ﬂourishes for ever, i.e. that
we satisfy all of our areas of concern (Section 2.5). Two subgoals are set to achieve that:
i) change the conditions of the sociotechnological landscape so that it creates intrinsic (1st order) motivation
for sustainability and
ii) make sure that the circular economy becomes mainstream.
The ﬁrst subgoal aims at guiding us to perform actions that make us ﬂourish, which implicitly means that we
thrive and so does the Earth. This change in conditions is achieved through individual internalisation and
integration processes that create authentic human beings and that lead to a broader (society) behavioural
change; reﬂection moments (trough e.g. participatory design processes), scripts in daily artifacts and the
replacement of unsustainable institutional tools are used for that. The second subgoal aims at making CE ready
to enter the regime regardless of what transition pathway takes place. A positive side effect of this is that we will
partially satisfy the world area of concern.
The achievement of each subgoal requires some speciﬁc conditions. For i), conditions of relatedness,
autonomy and competence will ensure that the internalisation and integration of extrinsic values and goals as
intrinsic ones happen. For ii) a different way of human thinking that is prepared to solve wicked problems is
necessary. Such a thinking way involves being ready to create rather than ﬁx, thinking in systems, being open
minded and ﬁnding integrative solutions or being ready to interact with multiple stakeholders. Such abilities are
boosted by emotions triggered by intrinsic motivation. And positive feedback loops among themselves are also
created. Conditions of autonomy, mastery or purpose help to maintain intrinsic motivation and so do words of
encouragement, support or small "wins". Figure 5.1 displays the model in a schematic way.
Business types and engagement in CE
Businesses have different attitudes towards sustainability and thus, can engage in different manners in the
transition model. According to their current circularity and intentions, a categorisation of businesses was
proposed to adapt the co-ideation workshop (Section 3.2). A more detailed view on these categories is given in
Table 5.1. The ﬁrst three types have similarities with those suggested by other authors such as Schaltegger and
Burritt (2015). However, the ﬂourishing business type appears as something new, where the underlying goal of
growth is dropped and sustainability takes its place. Such a business would be run by authentic human beings
and would aim at creating focal experiences. Café Retro is a frontrunner within the category.

5.2. Transition methodology
Figure 5.1: Transition model for sustainability
To make sure that businesses truly engage in the transition to CE, it is of relevance to understand what type
of business we are dealing with and to adapt the methodology to it. Doing so not just in the deﬁne phase but
from the very ﬁrst contact with businesses seems interesting from a facilitator’s perspective.
General applicability of the transition model
Although it has been designed with a business in the food system in mind, the theoretical transition model
for sustainability seems to be applicable in other business sector and even in other areas of society. The left
side of Figure 5.1 would remain invariable in any case. For any business working with material resources the
same model would work, since incorporating circular economy principles is relevant for them. For those who
do not work with material resources, the model could be adapted and instead of seeking to help CE become
mainstream, the focus could switch to social sustainability.
The model can also be applied by other institutions, such as education centres, without even the necessity
to include any environmental or social focus in play: students, teachers and any other interested actors can
immerse in a design process that seeks to create the school of the future, the one that prepares young people for
sustainability.

suggests that an inspiring environment was created. It is not easy to assess whether deep reﬂection
moments, that can lead to authenticity, were created; nevertheless, steps in that direction are
appreciated from impressions like "I want to live in En", "we can create relevant forward thinking
ideas" or "I would like to know that these ideas are actually implemented". The workshops have
not generated further action by themselves yet; possible reasons for that vary from business to
business: staff in Royal Bagel did not participate in the workshop, the workshop at Sweet Surrender
was short and the previous steps were not carried out in Retro. In addition, in all the three cases
time constrains have made impossible for the facilitator to take the ﬁrst step for the development of
the ideas. However, some reactions from participants show their willingness to get involved in the
development and implementation of the ideas. For instance, the day after the workshop at Retro a
participant asked me: "which ideas from yesterday do you think we could implement?"
3. Develop
The performance pre-assessment is neither a fully reliable analysis nor a deﬁnitive one, instead a compro-
mise between time and reliable results has been sought. It is a ﬁrst rough assessment that uncovers the
main features of each option and helps to decide how to go on with the development.
The results showed that in environmental terms some of the options originate trade-offs among impact
categories and others savings. Only one of the options was ﬁnancially detrimental and potential beneﬁts
reached up to 14,000 DKK per year (for a business that wants to make sure that every three months at
least 1,500 DKK are donated to charity projects). Circularity indicators improved in three of the options,
although none of the options managed to improve C on its own. The analysis of barriers evidences that
willingness to act and generate changes is the main obstacle. Although it is true that some options require
help in the form of e.g. know-how, most of the ideas generated only require time and dedication.
This results are promising and can motivate action; however, it is not only the measurable performance
that counts. As Chapter 3 explained and Section 5.1 summarised the implementation of circular solutions
seeks to generate changes that are not accounted for by any of these assessment tools and whose effects
take time to become apparent. This, if we believe in the transition model here presented, means that
even those options that perform worse than current alternatives should be supported and implemented;
we would be talking of investments (in environmental, ﬁnancial or circular terms) for a brighter future:
sustainability. Also, it indicates that tools to measure improvements in that regard should be included as
part of the assessment.
This phase was not fully completed, therefore it is not possible to evaluate if all of its goals were met.

Evaluation of methodology vs its goals and next steps
Table 5.2 summarises the goals of the methodology, how they are pursued and whether they have been achieved
or not. Some of the goals such as preparing CE to become mainstream or making participants feel that they

attitude that wants to leave the present behind, the role play and the mix of backgrounds among participants has
generated some (sometimes virtual) stakeholder interaction and criteria to generate ideas have tried to add new
dimensions beyond the environmental to provide integrative solutions. Some participants have internalised the
meaningfulness of what they were creating and to some extent, they have seen that solutions are at their hands.

General applicability of guideline
Although initially designed to assist businesses in the food system and in an urban area in Denmark, the guideline
might be applicable under other circumstances. Table 5.3 shows the applicability of the steps in the guideline
under four different potential conditions: a business in another step of the food system, a bigger company, a
company in another sector and a company in another area of the world.
Establishing contact with businesses is feasible in every case and could only entail some problems with big
companies that already have a transition methodology in motion and might thus, not be interested. A material
ﬂow analysis is applicable in any case where material ﬂows exist and would just need the MFA model to be
adapted. The co-ideation workshop can be carried out in every case, although potential reluctance from big
companies to external stakeholders getting to know their inside, of companies not wanting to invest man-hours
in such an activity or of specialised companies feeling that external stakeholders might not add anything new
can be expected. Experimentation might be an obstacle for those companies that require big time or ﬁnancial
investments to produce pilot projects, but obtaining feedback should not be problematic, even for business to
business companies, if proper ways to do it are recognised. The steps in the deliver phase should not represent a
problem unless for companies in countries where LCA databases for background systems are not available and
hence, make the environmental performance assessment unreliable.
This analysis, based on personal impressions, although not fully meaningful, shows that the methodology is
in general terms transferable to businesses operating under other circumstances, although some phases would
be need small adaptations.
5.3
Short CE transition guideline for small and medium enterprises in the AFS
A simpliﬁed and short CE transition guideline can be a useful resource for those businesses that want to begin
the transition towards the circular economy on their own, following some simple and uncomplicated steps. Such
a tool aims to be a kick-starter to generate engagement that increases the interest of businesses in sustainability.
Discover
Multimedia products are effective tools to source the idea of ﬁniteness and inter-generational issue. Simple,
short and enjoyable videos such as the ones by Story of stuff project (2007) or McArthur (2015) can be used.
To grasp an understanding of sustainability beyond material and ﬁnancial ﬂows the videos by Jackson (2010),
Pistono2013 and The great transition (2010) are recommended. For those employees or employers willing to
know even more, the books by Ehrenfeld (2008) and Jackson (2009) (the former deeper and more abstract, the
latter more down to Earth) and the article by Grant (2012) (really inspirational) are suggested.
One hour long sessions where all the staff members together watch one of these videos and make a short
discussion about the topic might be interesting. Such sessions could be repeated over time to debate other
aspect of sustainability.

CHAPTER 6
CONCLUSIONS
Based on a light review of transitional and motivational theories, a methodology to assist businesses in the food
system in their transition to a circular economy has been designed. The methodology has its foundation on a
theoretical model (also developed in this study) that seeks to create sustainability by changing the conditions of
the sociotechnological landscape and thus, generating intrinsic motivation that promotes behaviours that allow
human ﬂourishing. Three different levers to engage businesses in the transition have been identiﬁed: business
and environment, well-being and ﬂourishing; and a categorisation of businesses according to them has been
suggested.
Integrating tools from Industrial Ecology (MFA, LCA) and concepts from design thinking, the methodology
follows the structure of the design process in an attempt to encourage "create rather than ﬁx". Although all its
steps have not been applied in real case studies yet, the implemented phases show some promising results:
feedback from participants suggests that conditions for integration and internalisation of extrinsic goals have
been created in the co-ideation workshops and the rough pre-assessment of the generated options proves their
potential to be environmentally and ﬁnancially sound and thus, to enter the regime. However, aspects such as
engaging participants fully or generating unconstrained thinking should be improved and the last steps (develop
and deliver) should not only be put in practice but ﬁrst and foremost, they should be properly deﬁned.
The limited number of case studies and of circular options analysed is not enough to understand what
circular option suits each business type best; however, the feeling is that imagination is the main limiting factor
for the generation of appropriate circular alternatives. The utilisation of both the guideline and the model under
conditions for which they were not designed seems feasible. Lastly, a simpliﬁed version of the circular economy
transition methodology in the form of a self-implementation circular economy transition guideline has been
proposed with the goal of engaging a wider audience.

Pareto analysis
Pareto analysis is a method of identifying the vital few causes (typically 20%) that can answer most of the
problems (typically 80%). Pareto analysis can be used in a wide variety of situations where there are a number of
variables contributing to a problem and you need to know which are the most important. It is particularly useful
in the task of selecting the option(s) with priority as well as implementation. Pareto analysis provides a strong
visual presentation of how to prioritize problems, and where to concentrate resources for the best results. While
interpreting the results, it is essential to use common sense as well as data to ascertain causes and priorities
(Asian Productivity Organization 2006).
