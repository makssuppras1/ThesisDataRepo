Abstract
Air-ﬁlled rectangular waveguides are used for transmission of microwave signals in high-energy
applications where low loss is essential. In this thesis the possibility of using gradient based
topology optimization for designing insert ﬁlters for WR-90 waveguides is investigated. A very
condensed introduction to electromagnetism is presented, such that readers with a background
in mechanics or optimization are able to gain a minimum of background knowledge.
The
topic of topology optimization is presented on the basis of optimization of metallic microwave
structures.
The emphasis is put on band-pass ﬁlters, but band-stop as well as high-pass ﬁlters are also
investigated. The study reveals that topology optimization can indeed be used to obtain a
wide range of ﬁlter characteristics with a ﬁlter that is no more than 32.6 mm long. The ﬁlter
designs obtained are very complex, but can easily be etched on printable circuit boards using
conventional production methods.
A selection of the ﬁlters are manufactured and tested experimentally. Good correlation
between the numerical and experimental results is obtained. A number of well performing
band-pass ﬁlters are presented with insertion loss below 0.6 dB and a minimum rejection outside
the passband of 10 dB.
A number of obstacles is still to be overcome, but generally the results show that topology
optimization is a promising tool to be used for waveguide ﬁlter design.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Transmission of radio waves is the foundation of a very large portion of modern technology
including satellite communication, radar technology, wireless networks and much more. Since
more and more technology depends on radio signals the radio frequency spectrum becomes sat-
urated and this forces the utilization of higher and higher frequencies. Signals in the super high
frequency (SHF) band, which spans 3-30 GHz, are used in all the aforementioned technologies.
Although the basis of the mentioned technologies are wireless, the signals must still be
transferred from a signal generator to the transmitting antenna and again from the reciev-
ing antenna to a receiver. For this, transmission lines are used. The most common type of
transmission line is a coaxial cable used as antenna cables in most modern households. Other
types of transmission lines are optical ﬁbres, microstrips and hollow waveguides. Each type of
transmission line serves a somewhat speciﬁc purpose and can not necessarily be replaced by
another. Transmission of signals over long ranges, e.g. satellites and radars, require a large
amount of energy. Loss in the transmission line handling such high energy levels will therefore
cause unwanted heat generation. Hollow metal waveguides are known for their very low loss
and are therefore indispensable in such applications [37].
Waveguides come in standard dimensions suited for speciﬁc frequency bands ranging from 1-
200 GHz. This thesis will focus exclusively on the rectangular, hollow, air-ﬁlled waveguide type
designated WR-90. Here, WR is an abbreviation for Rectangular Waveguide and 90 designates
the width of 0.90 inches. This speciﬁc waveguide is suited for transmission of frequencies be-
tween 8.2 and 12.4 GHz [18]. A small collection of standard components for WR-90 waveguides
are shown in Figure 1.1. For a mechanical engineer without experience in microwave engi-
neering, a waveguide system can perhaps most easily be comprehended by comparison with
a typical electrical circuit. We have simple transmission lines in a waveguide set-up, where
these are hollow, rectangular metal pipes and for an electrical circuit this would correspond to
the wires. Electrical circuits can be extremely complex and include junctions and components
such as resistors, capacitors etc. Waveguide systems can be equally complex with components
such as couplers, diplexers and ﬁlters. Exact examples and drawings of waveguide systems
can be hard to obtain, since the most typical application of waveguides are hidden or secret
installations for telecommunication, satellite technology and radars.

1.1
The Art of Waveguides Filter Technology
Wireless technology often utilizes a single frequency or a limited band of frequencies. Signals
with other frequencies received by the equipment therefore act as noise and should preferably
be eliminated in the transmission line before processing. Suppression of unwanted frequencies
are done using ﬁlters, which is no diﬀerent than how mechanical ﬁlters are used to sort out
unwanted particles from ﬂuids.
Waveguide ﬁlters have been in use and subject to active research for more than 80 years.
Some of the earlier literature available considering waveguide ﬁlters for hollow air-ﬁlled waveg-
uides is [45] where G. C. Southworth presents a state of the art for waveguides up until 1942.
A conceptual description of how ﬁlters can be designed by cascading resonant cavities is de-
scribed. These old ﬁlter types are very bulky, heavy and complex to manufacture and therefore
not very well suited for size- and weight critical applications.
Together with the commercialization of telecommunication much research on waveguides
and waveguide ﬁlters takes place during the late 1940’s. However, as late as in 1948 W. W.
Mumford designates the process of designing waveguide ﬁlters as an art [34] thus insinuating
that no exact systematic approach for ﬁlter design existed up to that point.
In the following years the cavity resonator ﬁlters are joined by entirely new ﬁlter types.
A lot of literature is available from this era, giving a thorough overview of the evolution of
waveguide ﬁlters. The new theory of ﬁlter design presented in each new article during this
period is immense and out of the scope of this thesis to comprehend. But because it has not
yet been possible to condense ﬁlter design into simple theories, the design of even simple ﬁlter
types still seems to be more of an art than a science for years to come.
During the 1970’s some new types of ﬁlters designated ﬁn-line ﬁlters and metal insert ﬁl-
ters become more common. These are both ﬁlter types where ladder-structures of metal are
positioned in the waveguide. P. J. Meier very brieﬂy discuss the possibility of these ﬁlters [33]
and ten years later J. Bornemann et. al. presents a study of both ﬁn-line ﬁlters and metal
insert ﬁlters [11]. Bornemann’s study is also one of the earliest actual automated computer
optimizations of waveguide ﬁlters. What is referred to as an evolution strategy method is used
3
==PAGE:13==
to determine the optimal dimensions of the design for speciﬁc ﬁlter characteristics. These new
ﬁlters are easier to produce and more compact than the earlier resonant chamber types, but
hardly sacriﬁce the performance.
Some of the most recent research on waveguide ﬁlters for rectangular air-ﬁlled waveguides
is [28, 29, 7] where A. Aydogan et. al. presents an approach to ﬁlter design using Levenberg-
Marquardt optimization. They consider a ﬁlter type where the waveguide is ﬁlled with slabs of
varying materials in the longitudinal direction. This ﬁlter type is somewhat rare in literature,
but very convenient for optimization purposes since only the thickness and permittivity of the
slabs can vary. The result of the optimization is very dependant on the initial design guess and
overall they are not able to obtain better performance or more compact designs than what is
otherwise known.
We see that generally waveguides are of huge interest and has been so for many years. The
ﬁlters are an important part of all kinds of waveguide installations. Since waveguides are used
in e.g. satellites, weight and size are an issue and as with most other modern technology we
should strive to make components as small and lightweight as possible. Components like these
are not produced in large amounts, but launching equipment into space is extremely expensive
and a reduction in weight or dimensions of a satellite can result in signiﬁcant savings or room
for other important equipment.
1.2
Topology Optimization of Radio Frequency Devices
Topology optimization is a method to determine the optimal distribution of material within a
limited space for a given objective. The concept was ﬁrst introduced by Bendsøe and Kikuchi in
1988 [9] and improved again by Bendsøe in 1989 with the Solid Isotropic Material with Penali-
sation (SIMP) interpolation method [8] intended for use on elastic structures. This application
of topology optimization has through the years shown to be very successful. Since then, topol-
ogy optimization has been under constant development and has since been applied to problems
within acoustics, ﬂuid mechanics, electromagnetism and much more. The contributions to the
ongoing development of topology optimization is too vast to be covered here, but a signiﬁcant
milestone in this development was the introduction of mesh independent ﬁlters, ﬁrst discussed
in [44]. Later the implementation of the Method of Moving Asymptotes by Svanberg [46] with
topology optimization showed to work very well [10] and are now more or less considered the
standard optimization procedure.
Topology optimization has seen numerous applications within wave propagation problems,
starting with photonic crystals [16] and acoustics [43] in 2D, where structures are optimized
for so-called band gaps, which can basically be seen as a speciﬁc type of ﬁlter. Later topology
optimization combined with full 3D solutions to electromagnetic problems has proven usable in
the design of meta-materials [17], antennas [5, 20, 23, 24], microstrip transmission line structures
[26] as well as coaxial-to-waveguide transitions [22].
Ouedraogo et. al. [35] presents an application of topology optimization used to design a
ﬁlter for a rectangular waveguide. Here, the ﬁlter is etched on a printed circuit board (PCB)
for a 4.90-7.05 GHz waveguide and validated experimentally. To design a ﬁlter with the desired
characteristic they use a genetic algorithm.
Genetic algorithms require a large amount of
computations and therefore limit the design freedom. This issue is addressed by Aage and
Johansen in [3] where a gradient based topology optimization routine is applied to the same type
of ﬁlter design. Here, a good ﬁlter characteristic is obtained, but not validated experimentally.
4
==PAGE:14==
Optimization of electromagnetic problems, and especially those involving interfaces be-
tween metals and other materials, are generally considered complicated, since the optimization
procedure is likely to get stuck at a suboptimal solution. Furthermore the computerized rep-
resentation of the physics must be very accurate, which increases the computational time.
Optimization algorithms require a minimum number of iterations in these calculations and
such optimization procedures may therefore take days to ﬁnish.
More recent contributions
within the ﬁeld of topology optimization are those of [4, 2] where a robust and generalized nu-
merical frameworks for large scale parallel computations are presented. Parallel computation
makes it possible to distribute the calculation over several processors which allows for topology
optimization of extremely large problems within a reasonable time frame.
When using topology optimization, even very simple design tasks within structural opti-
mization can result in very complex organic shapes. The designs may be too expensive or
complicated to manufacture with conventional methods and are therefore rarely realized. This
is a considerable weakness of topology optimization. As it will become evident throughout this
thesis, this project is therefore contrasting the common perception of topology optimization:
The design task is very complex, but the ﬁnal designs – although complex in shape – are easily
implemented and validated experimentally.
1.3
Thesis Objective
As it has become clear, designing ﬁlters for waveguides are no straight-forward task, since no
real general descriptions exist on how this is done. Based on the previous work on combin-
ing gradient based topology optimization and 3D electromagnetic problems, we believe that
topology optimization can provide a systematic, general and reliable tool for for ﬁlter design.
To the best of our knowledge, no material has been published, showing experimentally
validated results of gradient based topology optimized waveguide ﬁlters. The aim of this study
is to design a number of well-performing waveguide ﬁlters and validate the performance of these
experimentally. The predecessor of this project [3] proved – numerically – that a single good
ﬁlter characteristic could be obtained, but we strive to show that the numerical framework can
be used to generate any ﬁlter characteristic desired.
An eﬀort will be made to carry out a large amount of systematic and eﬃcient studies to
uncover a wide range of ﬁlter characteristics. Furthermore, diﬀerent solutions to improve the
performance of the optimization routine will be investigated.

Chapter 2
Electromagnetism
The theory used in this project mainly concerns electromagnetism and topology optimization.
Electromagnetism is a complex and comprehensive subject where even the simplest concepts
can be counter-intuitive and hard to grasp. The basic theories behind electromagnetism used
by researchers and engineers today are close to 150 years old. In contrast to electromagnetism,
topology optimization is a fairly young research subject which took its ﬁrst steps no more
than 29 years ago and where people with very little understanding of physics will be able to
understand the basic concepts.
Topology optimization and electromagnetism works together only when the physics of elec-
tromagnetism is discretized into a ﬁnite element model. It is assumed that the reader has basic
understanding of ﬁnite element analysis (FEA) and understands the concepts of FE meshes,
nodes, elements etc.1
2.1
Electromagnetism From a Waveguide Point-of-View
Of course, as the title of this thesis suggests, we aim at a study of ﬁltering mechanics for
waveguides. But to understand how these ﬁlters work and why they are necessary, we must
ﬁrst understand the working principles of the waveguide.
A typical section of a waveguide consists of a core surrounded by a conducting material.
A schematic drawing of such a section is shown in Figure 2.1. The inner dimensions of the
waveguide are deﬁned by the side lengths a and b, where a > b, for the WR standard type
waveguide considered here. The transmitted signal propagates from port 1 to port 2 through a
dielectric medium - usually air - with relative permittivity ϵr and permeability µr. The pipe is
usually produced from copper, brass or aluminum with the inner walls being bare exposed metal.
Concepts such as permittivity and permeability are very important for the basic understanding
of electromagnetism and we will therefore return to these in a little while.
The aim is to transmit microwave signals with frequency f [Hz] or ω = 2πf [rad/s] from
port 1 to port 2. Microwaves are electromagnetic radiation with a frequency range between 3

Electric and magnetic ﬁelds may exist independently of each other.

electricity will generate an electric ﬁeld without a dependant magnetic ﬁeld. This project does,

Common types of waveguide ﬁlters introduce some kind of geometric discontinuity in the form
of resonant cavities, copper ladders etc. of which the governing dimensions are related to the

Chapter 3
Topology Optimization
Topology optimization is a method to optimize material distribution within a given design
domain Ωbased on a speciﬁed objective function. The word topology was chosen, since the
optimization is allowed to entirely alter the topology of the material in the design domain, i.e.
create or remove holes. This is opposed to shape optimization, where only the shape of the
initial design is altered or parameter optimization where only material parameters are allowed
to vary.
A typical objective within solid mechanics could be to minimize the compliance of a structure
with speciﬁed supports and loads, by distributing a limited amount of material within a bounded
design domain. The design domain is divided into a ﬁnite number of design elements Ωe and the
optimization routine is set to vary the material density ρe in each element, where ρ = 0 equals
a void and ρ = 1 equals solid material. A schematic illustration of this is found in Figure 3.1.
We do, however, concern optimization of metallic devices within an electromagnetic ﬁeld, and
here a void therefore corresponds to a dielectric material (air in this case) and a solid to a
conducting material (copper in this case).
To evaluate the behaviour and thereby performance of the design a mathematical model
that describes the problem is needed. Maxwell’s equations are used to determine the electric
ﬁeld behaviour, from which the scattering parameters can be determined.
The need of a
mathematical model as well as the division into a ﬁnite number of elements makes topology
optimization a natural companion to ﬁnite element analysis which includes both of these.

described in Section 2.3 waveguide ﬁlters depend on resonators. The ﬁrst phase is used to
generate a number of resonators spaced apart, which is then used as initial guess for the second
phase. Good ﬁlter characteristics generally depend on more than one resonator and running the
optimization in a single phase will cause the optimization to get stuck in a local minimum with
only a single resonator [3]. Due to the extensive investigation that has gone into developing
this approach, it has been chosen to follow this. The phases will be referred to as phase 1 and 2
throughout the report. Generally phase 1 concerns the creation of a number of single frequency
resonators. Phase 2 uses these resonators as starting guess for a complete ﬁlter.

3.3
Sensitivity Analysis and the Adjoint Method
Diﬀerent optimizations algorithms exist, some very simple to interpret and others much more
complicated. One that has shown to work very well in relation to topology optimization is the
Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA) presented by Krister Svanberg in 1986 [46] as a general
optimization algorithm.
The algorithm has later been implemented in the present parallel
framework [4]. The MMA optimization routine is based on sensitivities, which basically is the
ﬁrst order derivatives – or gradients – of objectives and constraints with respect to the design
variable ρe.
In [14] several general methods are proposed for calculating the sensitivities. The numerical
method proposed requires a lot of computational resources to estimate. With this approach
the sensitivities are estimated with a truncated series, which means that one must accept
some error dependent on the size of the truncation. This is far from optimal and instead the
analytical approach is taken. Two analytical methods exist; the direct method and the adjoint
method. The direct method requires the solution of the global FE system matrices, which
is computational in-eﬀective. One can instead calculate the sensitivities by posing an adjoint
problem which only requires the solution of a reduced right-hand-side to obtain the desired
sensitivity, and is therefore more eﬀective with respect to computational time.

3.4
Filtering Methods
The designs obtained using the methods described in previous sections may perform very well
based on the mathematical model. The issue is, however, that the designs can be physically
20
==PAGE:30==
unrealisable, because the details may be so small that they cannot be manufactured. We there-
fore now introduce ﬁltering methods in relation to topology optimization, not to be confused
with the actual electromagnetic ﬁlters designed by the optimization routine.
Without ﬁltration methods, the smallest element in the FE model may determine the small-
est critical detail of the design. To control the physical dimensions of the details obtained one
or more of several diﬀerent ﬁltration methods can be included in the optimization routine. The
overall principle of ﬁltering methods is that they impose a transition between solid and void
elements speciﬁed by a given proﬁle and radius.
This smooth transition between solid and void is also necessary in regards to topology
optimization of metallic devices in electromagnetic problems.
This problem is discussed in
depth in [1]. The material interface between copper and air is critical to the optimization, and
too sharp transitions during optimization will cause the objective function to get stuck in local
minima. This can be remedied by imposing a relatively large ﬁlter radius. Using a large ﬁlter
radius only, will however limit the design freedom and introduce many intermediate density
elements, which represent the manufactured design poorly. It is therefore in our interest to
ﬁnish the optimization with as small a ﬁlter radius as possible, and a so-called ﬁlter continuation
scheme is therefore introduced.
This means that a large ﬁlter radius is used initially and
gradually reduced to the smallest possible size, decided by the manufacturing tolerances.
Diﬀerent ﬁltering methods each present their forces and drawbacks and are therefore used at
diﬀerent points during the continuation scheme. The numerical framework used in the present
work utilizes a standard density ﬁlter and a PDE ﬁlter based on a solution the the Helmholtz
PDE. As it will soon become clear these two ﬁlter types accompanies each other perfectly in
a ﬁlter continuation scheme, as the PDE ﬁlter is suited for larger ﬁlter radii and the standard
density ﬁlter for smaller ones.
For an in-depth explanation of the workings of the ﬁlters refer to Appendix C.

computers each delivering their own processor and memory. This is referred to as parallel com-
puting, and a parallelized version of the FE model used in this project is shown in Figure 3.3.

forward, since each partition of elements must know what is happening in the neighbouring
partition(s). The partitions communicate by use of a message passing interface (MPI). In a

Chapter 4
Preliminary Investigations
In this section we present a number of general investigations used in relation to designing the
waveguide and setting up the numerical model.
Earlier demonstrations of the program [3] modelled the ﬁlter as an insert-ﬁlter of solid
copper with voids of air. Such ﬁlters are, however, very hard – if not impossible – to produce
since pieces of copper ﬂoating in free space cannot be realized. The design must either be
etched on a PCB giving total two dimensional design freedom or stamped from a metal plate.
In the latter case the optimization routine would need a restriction or penalization, such that
it would not produce free-ﬂoating islands of copper. We have chosen to pursue the former. A
typical PCB for use in HF applications consist of a glass ﬁbre substrate with a thickness in the
order of 0.1-1.0 mm and a copper cladding with a thickness of 0.35 mm.
The substrate used in PCBs for HF applications available from Rogers Corporation typically
has a relative permittivity ϵr in the range of 2.17 to 10.2. Although very low, this is signiﬁcantly
higher than the relative permittivity ϵr = 1.0 used for modelling the air in the waveguide. The
substrate will therefore need to be introduced as a separate material in the FEM model. For
considerations regarding the choice of dielectric substrate please refer to Appendix E.
To keep the model and production as simple as possible and to rule out some sources of
uncertainty it has been chosen to etch the design on one side of the PCB only. Previously
the optimization has been run with the metal centred in the waveguide to ensure maximum
interference with the electrical ﬁeld. No matter if the copper or the substrate is chosen to be
centred in the waveguide this introduces asymmetry to the system. With regards to modelling
and optimization this is not a problem, since the full system is modelled anyway. It is, however,
necessary to choose which of the two possibilities are preferable. The initial part of this chapter
will include a number of investigations to support the decision on this.

5.2
Manufacturing of Filters
Based on the study presented in the previous section, it has been chosen to carry on the investi-
gation with three of the 21 ﬁlter designs. The choice of ﬁlters for production and experimental
validation is based on two criteria: ﬁlter performance and manufacturability. As discussed,
none of the ﬁlters exhibit good overall performance, but some of the post-processed ﬁlters do
actually show a somewhat ﬂat and well deﬁned passband with a steep cut-oﬀ. Manufactura-
bility is equally important, and in this context manufacturability means that the design has
the least amount of ﬁne and thin details. The etching process can cause ﬁne details to vanish
and choosing designs with coarser details will ensure better correlation in shape and topology
between the modelled and produced ﬁlter. Based on the two criteria the ﬁlters i = {8, 10, 11}
have been chosen.
The post-processed ﬁlter designs are shown in comparison with the produced ﬁlters in Fig-
ure 5.4. To ensure that the correlation can be inspected closely, the ﬁlters are photographed
with a light optical microscope (LOM). Inspection of the models in comparison with the pho-
tographs shows that the production quality is generally high. The geometry is reproduced very
well, but some changes in topology can be observed. These changes are typically seen as small
peninsulas of copper that tend to decouple from where they were connected. Furthermore some
holes have occurred in the middle of large copper sections due to local accumulation of etching
ﬂuid droplets. The largest discrepancy overall is caused by a over-etching of the copper, which
is seen as a uniform lack of material around the entire brim of each copper feature. Solutions
to problems regarding this type of over-etching (as well as under-etching) of designs obtained
by topology optimization were already presented in 2009 in [42] commonly known as the robust
formulation. At this point such formulations are not implemented in the numerical framework.
A robust formulation require multiple solutions of the system in each iteration and will in our
case at least double the calculation time.

Results and Discussion
Since it is not possible to isolate the behaviour of the ﬁlters entirely from the waveguide in which
it is positioned, it is important to characterize the behaviour of the waveguides produced for the
insert ﬁlters. Figure 5.7 shows the transmission parameters S21 of the two empty waveguides, i.e.
50 mm copper and 100 mm brass (see Figure 5.5). Besides the two manufactured waveguides
with ﬁlter slots, the transmission coeﬃcient of just the two yellow calibration pipes bolted
together are shown.
The calibration pipes show very little deviation from 0 dB, as should be expected, since the
system is calibrated to account for exactly these pipes. Based on the numerical investigations in
Chapter 4, the ﬁlter slots are not expected to aﬀect the transmission signiﬁcantly, which is also
what is seen experimentally. Generally, the ﬁlter pipes exhibit a more noisy behaviour, which
must be expected, partly because an extra ﬂange connection is introduced and partly because
of dust particles and surface imperfections inside the ﬁlter pipes. The copper pipe shows a
positive transmission coeﬃcient at most frequencies, which should not be possible, since this
means that energy is added over the pipe. No immediate explanation can be found for this
behaviour but, as it will become clear later the noise levels are suﬃciently small compared to
the overall ﬁlter characteristic, that the attenuation caused by these can be seen as insigniﬁcant.
The ﬁlters are now inserted into the waveguides one at a time. Transmission and reﬂection
are measured for each of the ﬁlters in both the brass and copper waveguide. The measured

measured scattering parameters are similar when comparing the brass and copper waveguides.

in exactly how the ﬁlter is positioned inside the waveguides.

5.4.3
General Considerations
The results obtained throughout this chapter show that this method of designing waveguide
ﬁlters indeed deserve a more thorough investigation. Good and usable ﬁlter characteristics
has unfortunately not been obtained. The correlation between the ﬁtted numerical model and
experimental data serves to show that ﬁlter designs that no person would ever be able to come up
with may be more optimal than any of those in use today. It is common to see computer models
of topology optimized structures that can never be realized due tu manufacturing complexity.
The application of topology optimization presented here has, however, proven to be absolutely
ideal, since the design and manufacturing freedom compliments each other very well.

Third Iteration: State of the Art
In the previous chapter it was shown that enhanced ﬁlter performance is obtained when altering
the ﬁlter continuation scheme, such that more iterations are allowed during the last ﬁlter radius.
By introduction of a ﬁtted numerical permittivity it was possible to obtain good correlation
between numerical and experimental results. The focus has until now been on band-pass ﬁlters,
but in this chapter it will be shown that the method can be used to design high-pass and band-
stop ﬁlters. A new approach of designing ﬁlters are also showed, which eﬀectively eliminates
the use of phase 1 designs for some applications.
In previous chapter a new ﬁlter continuation scheme have been proposed. Together with the
less restrictive distribution of discrete frequencies, this proved to produce better results. This
chapter will only only concerns phase 2 optimization and uses the ﬁlter continuation scheme of
(6.3)-(6.4) unless otherwise stated.
All ﬁlters in this chapter are manufactured with the same procedure and from the same
materials as in previous chapters, i.e. the design is etched on Rogers RT/duroid 6002. The
wafer and photo resist is, however, brand new in contrast to the materials used in previous
section. The measurements are carried out on the same equipment and with the same procedure

the 50 mm long copper waveguide.

High-pass Filter
It has not been possible to locate any commercial waveguide high-pass ﬁlters. High-pass ﬁlters
are usually manufactured as coaxial ﬁlters which suﬀers from large insertion losses (around 3
to 4 dB). An interesting task would therefore be to create a high-pass ﬁlter with low insertion
loss for waveguides. A high-pass ﬁlter should be designed, such that it allows transmission of
high frequencies and rejects low frequencies. Since the transmission is only considered within
the operating frequency range of the WR-90 the performance outside this range cannot be
guaranteed. The ﬁlter is therefore, what is commonly referred to as a pseudo high-pass ﬁlter,
i.e. a very wideband band-pass ﬁlter which extends outside the operating frequency. Such

7.3
Split Ring Resonators as Initial Guess
The two phase optimization procedure described in this project with the used model usually
takes around 2 days including phase 1 and 2. This section presents an alternative method,
which takes advantage of the well-known split ring resonator (SRR). The resonant behaviour of
the SRR should make it well suited as a starting guess for the second phase to design band-pass
and band-stop ﬁlters. We thereby eliminate the need for the ﬁrst phase, thus eﬀectively halving
the optimization time.1
SRR are widely used and described in the literature due to their (anti)resonant behaviour
which can be tailored to speciﬁc frequencies. The current design of conventional SRR and the
one used in the following work originates from [36]. This design is proposed due to the enlarged
range of magnetic properties introduced by the capacitance of the split rings. The capacitance
is especially concentrated in the gap where the ring is split. Notice that much newer litterature
concern double split ring resonators. Such designs require very ﬁne tolerances between the
inner and outer ring. The level of detail and the use of ﬁltering methods in the optimization
restricts us from using such designs as starting guess. The distance between the two rings is
much smaller than the ﬁlter radii used, thus the rings immediately combine into one.
To keep the consistency of using three resonators as starting guess, a structure consisting
of three SRR is used as an initial guess for the phase 2 optimization. The actual structure, as
the mesh looks in the model is illustrated in Figure 7.4. It is worth noticing that the design is
ﬁltered before any optimization is carried out, and the MMA will therefore experience a more
blurred design.
In this section two ﬁlter designs will be presented, a band-pass ﬁlter and a band-stop ﬁlter.

In the two previous chapters it has become evident that a frequency shift of up to 0.5 GHz of
the ﬁlter characteristic takes place when the design is post-processed (see Appendix F and J).

known prior to post-processing.

by post-processing.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
During this project gradient based topology optimization has been used to design ﬁlters for
air-ﬁlled rectangular waveguides. The designs have been manufactured and the performance
validated experimentally. Through a systematic study it has been shown that several measures
must be taken to obtain good ﬁlter characteristics. A good design and ﬁlter characteristic is not
granted and the quality of the result has been found to be very dependant on the choice of ﬁlter
continuation scheme and optimization frequencies. A method for reducing computational time
using split ring resonators has shown promising results. Furthermore, intermediate densities
have shown to cause signiﬁcant discrepancies between the behaviour of the modelled and post-
processed ﬁlter designs. A method using smooth Heaviside ﬁltering is proposed to remedy this
issue. The best ﬁlter characteristic presented in this thesis was obtained using this method.
Until now it has been unknown if gradient based topology optimization could be applied
to real-world problems regarding metallic microwave structures.
Topology optimization of
ﬁlters in waveguides has shown to be an ideal application.
The complex designs obtained
can be manufactured with relatively inexpensive equipment.
The designs are easily tested
experimentally.
8.1
Further Work
It is clear from the work presented in this project that the expected behaviour from the numer-
ical model can be found in the manufactured ﬁlter designs. Moving forward the focus should
therefore be on bettering the numerical model in-order to obtain even better ﬁlter characteris-
tics.
Re-write into PETSc
The DFEM framework is no longer maintained and updated, therefore solutions to the discov-
ered issues in the MPI communication will likely never be ﬁxed. The program should instead be
re-written into the parallel framework named Portable, Extensible Toolkit for Scientiﬁc Com-
putation (PETSc). The implementation of the code in the PETSc framework will also present
new opportunities e.g. the implementation of an eigenvalue solver with the PETSc extension
SLEPc (Scalable Library for Eigenvalue Problem Computations).

One ﬁlter - many methods
During the systematic study in this project many ﬁlters with diﬀerent operating frequencies
have been tried designed using the same method. The opposite approach could be an interesting
task to investigate i.e. one ﬁlter design obtained with diﬀerent design methods. This scenario
is not far fetched as it would be the approach if a commercial supplier of waveguide ﬁlters
were to develop a custom ﬁlter for a certain application. Diﬀerent approaches could be; further
development of the density ﬁltering with a Heaviside step function, investigating starting points
for the phase 2 optimization or diﬀerent ﬁlter continuation schemes.
Why the need for an eﬀective permittivity?
Initial investigations showed that the experimental data was parallel shifted approximately 0.5
GHz up in frequency. This behaviour was partly explained by over-etching from the manufac-
turing method. An eﬀective permittivity was introduced to account for this parallel shift. An
in-depth analysis should be carried out, such that the cause of this can be fully determined. As
of now one can not know how the model will behave if another dielectric substrate is chosen.
Robust formulation
It is known that the manufactured designs are over-etched and that this causes non-beneﬁcial
deviations in the frequency response. An introduction of a robust formulation that accounts
for the production method could be a way to remedy this behaviour. A robust formulation will
increase the required computational time signiﬁcantly. It is, however, known that the manu-
facturing process causes over-etching only, and this knowledge can be used to save expensive
computations in the robust formulation.
Manufactured ﬁlter in a high energy application
The ﬁlters have been tested in an experimental set-up where the input energy is far from that
of a real life application. An interesting task would be to test the manufactured waveguide
ﬁlters in a high energy system. Emphasis should especially be on the attenuation experienced
in the roll-oﬀof the passband as discussed in Section 6.1 and Figure 6.3. It is expected that the
attenuation is converted into heat and if the temperatures reaches high levels it might aﬀect
the ﬁlter performance.

Appendix D
Additions to the code
The DFEM framework is generally used as is, however several changes have been implemented.
Several minor additions have been made to the code, including the introduction of new variables,
elements and drivers. These changes have mainly been in the elements and in the drivers. A
few larger additions have also been made to the DFEM framework, the code parts are presented
in this section.
A new element is introduced to include the dielectric substrate in the model. This element
is however very similar to the element modelling the air in the waveguide and will therefore not
be presented in this section.
Multiple drivers have also been deﬁned. The drivers are tailored to solve/investigate speciﬁc
issues. One of the created driver is a dedicated driver used when applying the Heaviside ﬁlter.
Another added driver is used to model an abbreviation of the optimization problem, where the
optimization formulation is restated.
The Heaviside ﬁlter is implemented as an addition to the already implemented standard
density ﬁlter, since a Heaviside ﬁlter is an expansion of the standard density ﬁlter. The imple-
mentation is inspired by [6], however the implementing a Heaviside ﬁlter in DFEM is not as
straightforward. The implementation is presented below in code inserts. The boolean variable
”useHeavi” is deﬁned in a driver, dedicated to the Heaviside ﬁlter. The Heaviside is imple-
mented as an optional phase three optimization, such that it can be utilized after phase two, it
can also replace phase two entirely, if that is desirable. The β variable is denoted ”betaH” and
it is updated in the dedicated Heaviside driver, following user provided speciﬁcations. ”betaH”
is passed to the Heaviside ﬁlter with mpar which is included in the ﬁlter via the headerﬁle for
the ﬁlter functions.
Lastly a python script has been included in this section. The script is designed to control
runtime parameters and to run the job on the cluster, for each individual simulation. The
included python script is heavily modiﬁed from its original state. The most noteworthy change
is the removal of all variables in the script. These variables are now located in a global ﬁle. This
procedure is advantageous when many simulations, which shares a lot of common variables, are
simulated in a structured manner, where only a few variables changes between the simulations.
This allows the user to change the desired variable in one global ﬁle. The likelihood of a user
made mistake is reduced, when removing redundant tasks where type errors can happen. The
procedure is also beneﬁcial due to the easier and faster workﬂow it provides. An example could
be that 21 phase two designs are required (21 diﬀerent discrete frequency setups), this results
in 63 phase one resonators. So instead of having to go through 63 folders (and then 21 phase

E.2
Choice of PCB
One of the most important components when conducting the experimental work for this project
is the Printed Circuit Board (PCB). This section will present the most relevant substrates for
this project. It is important that the PCB is mechanically stable, this feature is achieved by
choosing a thick enough substrate. A suitable thickness has been chosen by visual inspection
in the workshop in building 348. The chosen thickness for all substrates is around 30 mil which
equals 0.762 mm. On the substrates a layer of copper with thickness 35 µm is placed on both
sides as per default from the workshop.
81
==PAGE:91==
Rogers 4003
This substrate consists of woven glass reinforced hydrocarbon/ceramics. The substrate has a
tensile modulus of 19.65 GPa and is therefore stiﬀer when compared to Rogers 5870 and Rogers
6002. This is due to reinforcement with hydrocarbon/ceramics. The substrate thickness is 32
mil which equals 0.8128 mm. The dielectric constant is ϵr = 3.55 and the dissipation factor is
δ = 0.0027 at 10 GHz.
Rogers 5870
This substrate is used in [35]. This article is also within the research topic of passive insertion
ﬁlters for waveguides. One can see that they obtain good experimental results with this PCB
for frequencies in the range of 5.75 GHz to 6.25 GHz. From the data sheet, it appears that the
dielectric constant is ϵr = 2.33 and loss tangent δ = 0.0012. The dielectric sheet is made of
PTFE composites reinforced with glass microﬁbers, with a tensile modulus equal to 1.3 GPa
and it has a thickness of 0.78 mm.
Rogers 6002
Given the information on Rogers website, this substrate looks like their ﬂagship when dealing
with high frequencies.
It has a wide range of applications within diﬀerent industries and
very broad operating range. It has very low loss and a uniform distribution of the dielectric
material. Also worth noticing is that the in-plane expansion coeﬃcient is matched to copper,
which makes this substrate ideal. The substrate has good dielectric properties with ϵr = 2.94.
The dissipation factor is δ = 0.0012. The dielectric sheet has a thickness of the tensile module
is 0.828 GPa.

Appendix I
Inﬂuence of Substrate Thickness
It is not possible to alter the thickness of the substrate after optimization. Since the substrate
is too thick in the numerical model it is, however, necessary to investigate how the thickness
inﬂuence the optimized result. From Section 4.1 it is known that the dielectric material generally
has a tendency to shift the response down in frequency. A thinner substrate will shift the
frequency less downwards. Since the error observed in Figure 5.8 is an upwards shift of the
measured results, some of the deviation could be caused by this error. The eﬀect is, however,
assumed to be small, since the produced substrate is only 1.02% thinner than the modelled.
A study has been carried out, to show how sensitive the optimization routine is to changes in
thickness. The phase 1 optimization as described in Subsection 5.1.1 is repeated for ﬁlter i = 10
with the exact same parameters (see (3.1)-(5.6)) but with a substrate thickness of ts = 0.778
mm corresponding to the measured value (see Table 5.3).
A number of iteration steps has been chosen, where the volume fraction distribution of
copper and air in the design domain for the two diﬀerent thickness is shown in Figure I.1.
It is seen that after 30 iterations the two designs are visually identical. At iteration 60 the
designs are no longer the same. The two ﬁrst resonators do show some of the same features
but mirrored about the yz-plane.
From this it can be seen that the code is extremely sensitive to even very small changes in
the mesh. It also serves to show that even very small changes in physical dimensions may result
in a design that is entirely diﬀerent in topology. The designs produced here (see Figure 5.4)
may therefore not be optimal for the substrate thickness.
Remark
As a result of the ﬂaw in the code, mentioned in the Preface, every obtained design
is unique, even for the same mesh with the same parameters. This further increase the codes
sensitivity to minor changes and are discussed in detail in Appendix A.
