Abstract
In this project, classical and stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart methods within robotic path and motion planôÙ
ning are reviewed and presented.
The primary objective of this project is to implement an endôÙtoôÙend pickôÙandôÙplace pipeline
for the Franka Emika Panda robotic arm. The system is designed to detect, track, and
manipulate various household objects placed on a moving conveyor belt. Objects are
picked and sorted while they are in motion. Furthermore, a standôÙalone 2D visualisation
tool for motion planning was developed with for release as openôÙsource software. It would
serve as an educational tool for students studying motion planning algorithms.
Classical and stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart samplingôÙbased planners are implemented and fielded both
in simulation and in a practical setting on the Franka Panda robot. The following planners
were delployed on the Franka Panda robot: RRT, RRT*, RRTôÙConnect, and PRM. The
planners are benchmarked against the widely used motion planning library ãMoveItã for
ROS.
Trajectory postôÙprocessing methods, including spline smoothing and timeôÙparametrisation,
are applied to convert planner output into smooth, dynamically feasible joint trajectories.
The implementation of RRTôÙConnect reached parity in performance with MoveIt! in some
metrics, and reached nearôÙparity in computation time.
The vision pipeline combines ArUco markerôÙbased calibration for estimating the extrinôÙ
sic transformation between the camera and workspace with a YOLOôÙbased perception
system for object classification, segmentation, and tracking. A grasp planning module
determined feasible grasp points by processing the segmented object masks, while an
Asynchronous Predictive Kalman Filter predicts object positions to enable interception of
moving targets.
Finally, a testing campaign was carried out to validate the implemented setup. In scenarios
with an obstacle, some planners exhibited excessively long planning times.
Static pickôÙandôÙplace without obstacles succeeded in almost 90 % of trials. Dynamic pickôÙ
ing without obstacles was tested using a moving conveyor belt. The system successfully
picked objects in 83 % of picking attempts for moving objects. The failures were caused
by errors in grasp site generation and a single failure in the object detection pipeline. The
planning and postôÙprocessing pipeline demonstrated solid performance throughout and
had zero failures during the final demonstrations.

2.1
Motion Planning Overview
Motion planning is a core domain within robotics focused on computing a collisionôÙfree
path that guides a robot from its initial configuration (position, orientation, joint states) to
a specified goal configuration [36, 18]. These paths must satisfy kinematic and dynamic
constraints while accounting for obstacles and interactions with the environment.
The field aims to enable robust, scalable, and autonomous operation in complex, realôÙ
world settings. Its significance is reflected in diverse applications, from agile Unmanned
Aerial Vehicles [16] to autonomous ground systems such as selfôÙdriving cars [37], as well
as robotic manipulators, which are the focus of this thesis.
Early work in robotic motion planning has its roots in algorithmic search and computaôÙ
tional geometry, formulated as the piano moverãs problem of navigating a robot without
collisions. A key concept that emerged is the configuration space (CôÙspace), introduced
by LozanoôÙPûˋrez [38], which represents each unique arrangement of a robot (e.g. the
joint angles of a 7ôÙDOF manipulator) as a point in an abstract space. Planning a robot
motion then becomes a matter of finding a continuos path between configurations entirely
within the collision free region of this space. Early planners relied on explicit modelling of
CôÙspace obstacles and deterministic search, but these approaches struggled with the exôÙ
ponential complexity of highôÙdimensional robots. For instance, exact cell decomposition
and visibility graph methods could solve mazeôÙlike planar problems optimally, yet became
intractable when extended to manipulators with many degrees of freedom [39]. The inôÙ
creasing need for scalability in 1990s shifted the focus to samplingôÙbased methods, which
sacrifice an exact CôÙspace model in favour of random sampling to explore feasible paths.
SamplingôÙbased planning proved especially influential for robotic arms and manipulators,
including typical 6 or 7ôÙDOF pickôÙandôÙplace robots. Methods like Exploring Random Trees
(RRT [40]) or probabilistic roadmap methods (PRM [41]) introduced the idea of building

2.2
Classical Planning Methods
Classical motion planning methods represent the foundational, deterministic approaches
to solving the motion planning problem. These algorithms are typically complete, as they
find a solution if one exists or report failure [18]. However, they scale poorly, as compuôÙ
tation grows exponentially with the number of degrees of freedom.
We will give a brief overview of classical methods before engaging in a more thorough
discussion of modern and stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart (SOTA) methods.
2.2.1
GraphôÙBased Search
GraphôÙbased search algorithms form the foundation of many classical path planning apôÙ
proaches (Roadmap, PRM, etc.). They operate on a discrete representation of the task
space or workspace, where each cell or configuration is represented as a vertex, and
edges denote feasible transitions between vertices. The underlying graph can be conôÙ
structed from regular or adaptive grids, occupancy maps, or lattices, depending on the
domain [8, 18].
This method is particularly effective in environments such as indoor or planar spaces,
where the workspace can be represented as an occupancy grid. In such cases, a highôÙ
level path can be computed on the grid and subsequently refined by local planners to
generate smooth, dynamically feasible trajectories [8].
GraphôÙbased search is wellôÙsuited to multiôÙquery planning problems, where the topology
of the environment remains largely constant at runtime. The graph can be computed
offline and stored in the system for later retrieval. Subsequent planning then happens onôÙ
line and benefits from the readyôÙmade graph. Once a graphôÙbased search has produced
a solution, it can be directly mapped back to the continuous space on which the graph
is based, enabling execution or further trajectory refinement. Common algorithms inôÙ
clude Dijkstraãs method, which finds the shortest path based purely on accumulated cost,
and A*, which incorporates heuristics to guide the search more efficiently. The choice of
heuristic in A* allows planners to optimise for additional criteria, such as swept volume,
or other applicationôÙspecific costs [8]. Many planning algorithms that rely on graphôÙbased
search can benefit from improvements in graph search without changing how the graph is
generated. Recent work by Duan et al. [42] has focused on improving the stateôÙofôÙthe art
in graph search algorithms, and breaking the soôÙcalled ãsorting barrierã that has existed
in shortestôÙpath problems.

Cell decomposition partitions the free configuration space Cfree into a finite set of simple,
convex, and nonôÙoverlapping regions called cells. Each cell is either fully free, fully in
collision, or partially in collision. Partially occupied cells can be further subdivided to more
accurately capture obstacle boundaries. Planning a path within a convex cell is trivial, as
any straightôÙline motion between two points in the cell will be collisionôÙfree.

Once decomposition is complete, a connectivity graph is constructed where each node
represents a free cell and edges indicate adjacency. Graph search algorithms such as A*
or Dijkstra are then used to find a discrete path through the graph, which can be refined
into a continuous trajectory through the interiors of the connected cells.
Cell decomposition is attractive in lowôÙdimensional problems, as both the cells and their
connectivity can be computed offline, enabling fast online planning. The convexity of each
cell guarantees that motion within a cell is collisionôÙfree. However, the method becomes
computationally intractable in high dimensions due to the curse of dimensionalityëƒ for exôÙ
ample, a 7ôÙDOF manipulator would require N7 cells for N subdivisions per joint.
A prominent example is the Vertical Cell Decomposition (VCD), which partitions a 2D
polygonal Cfree into trapezoidal and triangular cells by extending vertical lines from each
vertex of the CôÙobstacles until they intersect another obstacle or the workspace boundôÙ
ary. VCD can be implemented using a planeôÙsweep algorithm, where events are trigôÙ
gered when the sweep line intersects obstacle vertices or ãinterestingã features. In 3D,
the equivalent of a sweep line is a sweep plane. The plane becomes a hyperôÙplane in
higher dimensions, and finding intersections becomes increasingly complicated [18].
In VCD, 1ôÙcells are vertical lines that divide Cfree, while 2ôÙcells are open regions enclosed
by obstacles and 1ôÙcells.
To generate the backbone of a roadmap between these cells, a sample point is placed
within each cell, and edges are created between points of adjacent cells. This construction
preserves the connectivity of the free space Cfree. If a path exists in Cfree, it will have an

2.2.3
Roadmap Methods
Roadmap methods are a class of classical, deterministic algorithms that solve the motion
planning problem by capturing the connectivity of the free configuration space (CôÙfree) with
a oneôÙdimensional graph, known as a roadmap. The basic idea is to create a network of
paths that represents the essential structure of the free space [36, 18].
A useful approach to motion planning is to construct a representation of Cfree that can
be used to quickly generate valid paths when new planning problems arise. This is parôÙ
ticularly useful for robots operating in static or known environments for prolonged periôÙ
ods. One such reusable representation is known as a Configuration Space Roadmap. A
roadmap is a network of curves that effectively represents the free configuration space
Cfree. Roadmap methods create a graph of ãroadsã that guide the robot through the spaceëƒ
the robot simply needs to get on and get off again.
Path planning with Roadmap Methods is simplified into a threeôÙstep process:
ãÂ A graph is constructed whose nodes lie in CôÙfree and whose edges represent feaôÙ
sible, collisionôÙfree paths
ãÂ The initial and goal configurations, qinit and qgoal, are connected to accessible nodes
on the roadmap
ãÂ A graph search algorithm (e.g., Dijkstraãs or A*) is used to find a path in the roadmap
between the two connection points
This approach transforms the original continuous, highôÙdimensional problem into a disôÙ
crete graph search problem. Two of the most foundational roadmap techniques are the
Visibility Graph and the Generalized Voronoi Diagram [36, 18].
These methods operate on a graph where nodes represent collisionôÙfree configurations,
and edges between nodes represent feasible, obstacleôÙfree paths connecting these conôÙ
figurations. The roadmap is constructed offline and can be reused for multiple planning
queries, making this class of methods especially attractive in static and fully known enviôÙ
ronments.
Some classical roadmap methods focus on the visibility graph, where rays are cast from
start and goal positions to all visible vertices of obstacles. The algorithm is then repeated
by casting rays from visible vertices along their edges. Edges that point into obstacles
can be discarded, resulting in a roadmap that is strictly convex around the obstacles.
While classical, deterministic roadmap methods such as the Visibility Graph and the GenôÙ
eralized Voronoi Diagram are powerful in that they provide complete solutions with guarôÙ
antees on path properties (e.g., shortest length or maximum clearance), their practical
application is severely limited by a core requirement: they depend on an explicit geometôÙ
ric representation of the configuration space obstacles (CôÙobstacles).
Constructing this explicit representation is computationally intensive and becomes inôÙ
tractable for robots with many degrees of freedom. While modelling the CôÙspace for a

One wellôÙknown problem in APF methods is the presence of local minima: configurations
where the gradient vanishes, but which are not the goal. This can trap the robot and
prevent it from completing the task. Designing potential functions that guarantee a single
minimum at the goal is difficult, especially in cluttered environments.
To address this, hybrid approaches have been proposed [8]. In these, the robot normally
follows the gradient descent, but switches to random motion when convergence stalls
near a local minimum. Once it escapes, it resumes following the field. While practical,
such methods introduce tuning parameters that can negatively affect their reliability, as
they require careful tuning [8].

StateôÙOfôÙTheôÙArt Planning Methods
Research in the past 15 years has produced a large amount of motion planning algoôÙ
rithms. Many are refinements of the samplingôÙbased approach aimed at improving effiôÙ
ciency and optimality. For example, asymptotically optimal variants of the basic PRM/RRT
were proposed by Karaman and Frazzoli (2011) [47]: PRM* and RRT* modify the conôÙ
nection strategies. Asymptotically optimal planners guarantee that as computation time
increases, the solution path cost will approach the optimal (minimum possible) value.
These optimal planners converge slowly, so subsequent work introduced heuristics and
anytime techniques to accelerate them. Informed RRT* (Gammell et al., 2014, [48]) and
Batch Informed Trees (BIT*, Gammel et al., 2015, [49]) apply heuristic search principles

to improve sampling guidance towards promising regions of the space, often focusing on
improving the current best solution.
Similarly, the Fast Marching Tree (FMT*, Janson et al., 2013, [50]) algorithm explores
many samples at the same time using a lazy dynamic programming approach, which
allows it to converge faster to nearôÙoptimal paths.
These and related planners (e.g.
RRTôÙConnect and all other variants with different wiring strategies) belong to the famôÙ

optimal samplingôÙbased planners require more complex computations (nearestôÙneighbor

must be chosen accordingly for a given task. In robotic manipulation tasks asymptotically
optimal planners are often adopted when path quality (smoothnes of shortest distance) is
priority ôÙ for instance, in grasping or welding [51], the shortest or the most natural part is
often desired. On the other hand, many pickôÙandôÙplace operations need only a feasible

Another major direction in modern planning is to reformulate the problem as a trajecôÙ

and iteratively accepting changes that improve path quality, which helps it escape local
minima.

sequential convex optimisation. The most appealing part of optimisation based planners
is their output ôÙ the trajectories tend to be smooth by construction (no unnecessary or
weirdly placed waypoints, as can happen with samplingôÙbased planners) and can naturally
incorporate constraints like velocity or accelerations limits [54].

takes into account not only collision avoidance, but also dynamic feasibility of movement,
respecting constraints on velocity, acceleration, torque and other actuator limits [56]. Early
work such as RRT with differential constraints [57] extended sampling based planners to
state space (position and velocity), using short simulated trajectories from control inputs
rather than straight line extensions in configuration space. PRM variants with dynamic
feasibility checks have also been developed [56]. While industrial manipulators often rely
on a separate timeôÙparameterisation stage after geometric planning (since they move relôÙ

Another growing trend is leveraging machine learning and deep learning to improve
motion planning. LearningôÙbased methods can appear in various forms ôÙ from using
data to inform heuristic choices in traditional planners, to endôÙtoôÙend learned policies that
output trajectories directly. Some works such as [60] use Principal Component Analysis
(PCA) to generate a subôÙspace of the configuration space, or a soôÙcalled ãsampling volôÙ
umeã. The sampling volume helps the planner create valid sample in difficult regions by
reducing the dimensionality of the planning problem on a local scale, or by capturing the
coupling that there might exist between the degrees of freedom in the robot.
Another line of work uses neural networks to predict promising regions of the CôÙspace or
to generate initial path guesses that a planner can refine. For example, Motion Planning
Networks (Qureshi et al., 2019, [61]) train a neural encoder to take in a description of the
environment (e.g. an occupancy map) and directly produce waypoints for a feasible path,
effectively learning from examples how to shortcut the search process. These approaches
can speed up planning after an offline training phase, especially in environments that
follow patterns seen before. A 2024 paper [62] by Dalal et al. explores deep learning for
motion planning by training a neural network on the output of a stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart samplingôÙ

Another very recent development is the application of diffusion models and other generôÙ
ative models as trajectory samplers. Diffusion models, which have had success in image
generation, can learn a multimodal distribution of plausible robot motions from demonôÙ
stration data [63]. At runtime, such a model can generate diverse candidate trajectories
for a manipulation task, which a planner or controller can then evaluate and execute. This
allows to inject prior knowledge (training data) into the planning problem, potentially hanôÙ
dling scenarios that are hard to solve with pure sampling or optimisation. In manipulation
tasks, early successes of learning include grasp planning (where deep networks predict
grasp poses) and we are now seeing that approach extend to arm motion planning and
tool use [64, 65]. It is a promising direction, however it requires careful validation to ensure
safety and reliability alongside efficiency [66].
Finally, an important branch of recent research addresses Task and Motion Planning
(TAMP), which lies at the intersection of highôÙlevel decision making and continuous motion
planning. In realistic robotic manipulation, achieving a goal often includes a sequence of

of which requires a certain type of motions. TAMP frameworks [67, 68] integrate a task
planner (which can reason over discrete actions and constraints) with a motion planner

of two levels: choosing a different order of actions might make the motion planning easier
or harder and on the other hand, geometry might constrain which sequences are even
possible (e.g. some objects might need to be moved before others become reachable).
Classical motion planners assume fixed start and goal, in TAMP algorithms the goals can
be a variables to solve for (like which object to pick first or where to place an object so
the next steps are feasible). In practice, TAMP research is still largely in the lab, but it is
gradually influencing practical applications. For instance, industrial dualôÙarm robots perôÙ

Vision & Perception
Computer vision is a field of artificial intelligence that enables computers and software to
interpret and extract meaningful information from visual input such as images and video.
Its goal is to automate tasks that can also be performed by the human visual system,
including recognition, tracking, and scene understanding [71, 72].
In this project, computer vision is used to detect and track objects within a defined workspace.
Objects are detected and tracked using preôÙtrained neural networks, and the timeôÙseries
locations of objects is fed to a Kalman filter, which is responsible for predicting the future
states of the objetcs.
The OpenCV library [73] for Python underpins the vision system, and stock functions are
used whenever applicable.
2.4.1
Fiducial markers ôÙ April, ArUco, QR
In this project, the ArUco family of codes and detector are used to label and detect tarôÙ
gets in the workspace. These targets include static fiducials for calibration of the robotic
workcell. Markers are also mounted on dropôÙoff totes, where the robot will deliver picked
objects.
ArUco and April tags were invented for useôÙcases such as Augmented Reality (AR) and
robotics. They are easy to detect at a distance and are somewhat robust to occlusion.
ArUco codes have been designed to offer large Hamming distances within the same family
of codes, meaning that they offer good robustness towards incorrect detections. Classical
scannable codes, like QRôÙcodes or barcodes, can contain more information than either
ArUco or April tags, but these tags are less robust to scanning at a distance. April tags

State Estimation & Kalman Filter
The Kalman Filter is an optimal state estimation method developed by Rudolf E. Kalman.
It operates as a recursive twoôÙstep algorithm. The prediction step propagates the sysôÙ
tem state forward in time using a dynamic model, and the update step incorporates new
measurements to refine the state estimate. The weighting between prediction and meaôÙ
surement is determined by their respective uncertainties, encoded in the process and
measurement covariance matrices [86, 87, 88, 89].
A Kalman filter estimates the internal (or hidden) state of a system by combining a predicôÙ
tive model with incoming measurements, accounting for uncertainty in both. The state is
propagated forward in time using the systemãs dynamics, then corrected when new meaôÙ
surements arrive. This correction depends on the relative trust placed in the prediction
and the measurement.
Mathematically, the system is expressed as a linear, discreteôÙtime model with additive
Gaussian noise in both the process and the measurements. The prediction step advances
the previous state estimate and its covariance through the state transition model. The
update step computes the innovation, which is the difference between the measurement
and the prediction, and adjusts the state accordingly using the Kalman gain. This gain is
chosen to minimise the meanôÙsquare estimation error, which gives the filter its optimality
under the stated assumptions.
The recursive structure means the filter never needs the full measurement history, which
would otherwise grow with time. It also means that the filter is wellôÙsuited to realôÙtime
operation.
In this project, a Kalman filter is applied to forecast future positions of objects on a conôÙ
veyor belt. The Kalman filter in its standard form is wellôÙsuited to this, but some practical
considerations must be taken. The camera system for this thesis operates at a fixed
frameôÙrate of 30 fps. at full HD resolution. While the frameôÙrate is constant, a computer
vision pipeline is in charge of classifying and segmenting the image. The rate of this
pipeline is not constant, and frames are processed at anywhere between 10 and 20 fps.
Other functions or code might run in the background, and this increased overhead might
also influence the processing speed of the computer vision pipeline.
However, the standard form of the Kalman Filter assumes equally spaced measurements
and uninterrupted sensor output. In robotics, these conditions rarely hold. Vision systems,
for example, can produce irregular or missing detections because of occlusion, motion
blur, or processing delays.
A naû₤ve approach would be to construct some sort of buffer that always has the most
recent frame available at some lower frameôÙrate. This approach was not selected, as it
would leave some performance on the table.
Instead, this project uses a soôÙcalled Asynchronous Kalman filter, which selectively upôÙ
dates predictions whenever new information is available.
The implementation of the
ãAPKFã follows the same state machine as the one outlined by Kruuse in [32]. It is further
elaborated on in chapter 3.
One could argue that the innovation could simply be set to zero at timeôÙsteps where no
new measurements are available. If you set the innovation term to zero when no measureôÙ
ment is available, you are effectively forcing the update step to leave the state unchanged
from its prediction, but you would still be going through the full Kalman update equations
with a ãfakeã measurement. The APKF instead skips the measurement update entirely

Open Research Challenges
While significant progress has been made in robotic motion planning, several open chalôÙ
lenges continue to define the field and motivate ongoing research.
HighôÙdimensional configuration spaces. Robotic manipulators, particularly those with
redundant kinematics such as 7ôÙDOF arms, must plan in large, continuous configuration
spaces. Humanoid robots further increase complexity due to fullôÙbody motion coupling.
SamplingôÙbased planners like PRM and RRT can navigate such spaces, but suffer from
limitations when encountering narrow passages or highly constrained tasks. Balancing
planning efficiency and completeness remains an unresolved issue.
RealôÙtime constraints. Many applications require planners to generate or adapt trajecôÙ
tories in real time. In dynamic settingsãsuch as autonomous drones navigating through
clutter or autonomous vehicles responding to trafficãdelays in planning directly impact
system safety and reliability. Although optimal planners such as RRT* offer theoretical
guarantees, they often fail to meet realôÙtime requirements without significant simplificaôÙ
tion or approximation.
Integration with perception and control. Motion planning cannot operate in isolation
from the robotãs perception and control stack. Robust execution depends on the ability to
incorporate new sensor information and adjust plans dynamically. This tight integration is
especially critical in cluttered or dynamic scenes, where errors in perception or actuation
must be compensated through feedback. Developing reliable closedôÙloop systems with
responsive trajectory adaptation is still a major challenge.
Generalisation and scalability. Motion planning solutions are often engineered for speôÙ
cific platforms or tasks, making generalisation difficult. Transfer of planning strategies
across different environments or robots remains limited, particularly when model assumpôÙ
tions or sensing conditions change. LearningôÙbased approaches offer potential for genôÙ
eralisation, but issues such as data efficiency, generality of training data, and simôÙtoôÙreal
transfer still need to be addressed. Scaling motion planning to multiôÙagent systems introôÙ
duces further complexity in coordination and decentralisation.
ApplicationôÙspecific constraints. In industrial robotics, motion planners must operate
within strict temporal constraints and often in workspaces shared with humans or other
machines. Safety, reliability, and cycle time requirements limit the feasibility of complex
or highôÙlatency planning strategies. In autonomous driving, planners must reason about
interactions with other agents and maintain safety over long horizons. For drones, agôÙ
gressive manoeuvring and onboard compute constraints impose tight bounds on planôÙ
ning complexity. Even outside robotics, in domains like computer animation and virtual
reality, motion planning is critical for producing physically plausible and visually realistic
movement under realôÙtime constraints.

Motion planning for robotsôÙwhether mobile platforms, aerial vehicles, or manipulatorsôÙcan

serves a different purpose in planning and control.
Workspace
The workspace of a robot defines the set of all positions and orientations
that its endôÙeffector can reach, given its mechanical structure, joint limits, and physical
constraints. It is purely determined by the robotãs geometry and kinematics, and does not
depend on the specific application or task.

A configuration specifies a complete pose of the robotôÙi.e., the position of all joints or

continuous, obstacleôÙfree path in the valid subset of CôÙspace, called the free configuration
space (Cfree).

shape of the robot. This allows the robot to be treated as a point in CôÙspace, simplifying
collision checking. Any pointôÙrobot path in the inflated environment is guaranteed to be
safe for the fullôÙsized robot in the original environment.

3.3.1
RRT*
RRT*, was proposed by Karaman and Frazzoli in their 2011 paper [47]. The paper shows
that RRT often converges to a nonôÙoptimal solution. RRT*, in contrast, is asymptotically
optimal, in that the returned solution converges to the optimum given enough time. RRT*
was developed to a address a hole in the theory, namely that the quality of solutions from
RRT had not been formally treated.
RRT* is an extension of the basic RRT algorithm that guarantees asymptotic optimality
by incorporating an incremental rewiring step at each iteration. After a new node xnew is
added to the tree, RRT* identifies nearby nodes Xnear and checks if their path via xnew
would reduce the overall cost. If so, connections are reconfigured to ensure the tree
gradually improves the solution. This procedure ensures that, over time, the cost of the
path converges to the global optimum [48]. The drawback is additional time needed for
rewiring a tree after adding a new node.
Multiple variations of RRT* exist, allowing the rewiring strategy and heuristics to be adapted
to specific application needs. An example is the challenge of navigating marine vessels
in constrained environments. GroundôÙaware RRT*, for instance, can penalise motion toôÙ
wards shallow waters, enabling safer navigation [95].
RRT* follows the same foundational steps as the classical RRT. At each iteration, a ranôÙ
dom configuration xrand is sampled, and the nearest node xnear in the tree is identified. A
new candidate state xnew is generated by stepping from xnear towards xrand by a fixed step
size, and the connection is checked for collisions. If the path is valid, the node is added.
What distinguishes RRT* is its rewiring mechanism. After adding xnew, the algorithm
identifies all nodes within a given radius (can also be K nearest nodes) Xnear, and selects
the one that provides the lowest cost to become the parent of xnew. Once added, the
algorithm checks if connecting any of the nearby nodes back through xnew would reduce
their cost. If so, the tree is locally restructured. This incremental rewiring improves the
quality of the solution over time and leads to asymptotic optimality.

3.3.4
Informed RRT* and Informed Sampling
A 2014 paper by Gammell et al. [48] introduced a refinement of RRT* known as Informed
RRT*, which improves convergence by biasing the sampling process using heuristics
derived from the current best solution. Instead of sampling uniformly from the entire conôÙ
figuration space, Informed RRT* focuses exploration within an ellipsoidal subset of the
space, which is guaranteed to contain all states that could lead to a path shorter than the
current best. This idea is rooted in the observation that, in an obstacleôÙfree environment,
any path with cost less than the current best solution must lie within a hyperspheroid (i.e.,
an ellipse in 2D or ellipsoid in 3D and higher dimensions), whose foci are the start and
goal configurations.
The core planning loop of RRT* remains unchangedëƒ the difference lies in how new conôÙ
figurations are sampled after an initial solution is found. Once a valid path is discovered,
subsequent samples are drawn from the informed region defined by the current best cost,
cbest, thereby excluding regions of the space that cannot contain better paths. This draôÙ
matically reduces the size of the search space and leads to faster convergence towards
optimal or nearôÙoptimal solutions.

STOMP [53] is a trajectory optimization method that takes a stochastic, gradientôÙfree apôÙ
proach to improving an initial path. Instead of computing the gradient of a cost functional,
STOMP iteratively samples noisy perturbations of the current trajectory and updates it
based on the observed cost improvements.
HighôÙlevel idea:
Starting from an initial trajectory (often a straightôÙline interpolation in joint space), STOMP
performs the following steps at each iteration:
1. Generate several random perturbations by adding small zeroôÙmean noise to the
trajectory at each time step.
2. Evaluate the cost of each perturbed trajectory, where the cost typically includes
obstacle avoidance and smoothness terms (similar to CHOMP).
3. Compute an update to the trajectory as a weighted average of the perturbations,

where perturbations with lower cost receive higher weight, and those increasing
cost may receive negative weight or be discarded.

ãÂ No gradient calculation means that STOMP is suitable for nonôÙdifferentiable or highly
nonôÙlinear cost functions.
ãÂ Escapes local minima: stochastic perturbations can move the search into a better
basin even when the deterministic gradient is zero.
ãÂ Flexible: can be applied to a wide variety of robotic systems and cost formulations.
Limitations:
ãÂ Computationally expensive: requires many samples per iteration for reliable imôÙ
provement, especially in highôÙdimensional spaces.
ãÂ No global optimality guarantee: produces locally optimal solutions that depend on
the initial guess.

a trajectory generated by a samplingôÙbased planner, but it does so without requiring cost

3.4.2
Spline Fundamentals and Definitions
In mathematics, a spline is a piecewiseôÙpolynomial function with a specified smoothness
at the boundaries between segments [101]. The points where polynomial pieces meet
are called knots, and the ordered set of these points in the parameter domain is the knot
vector. Each segment spans the interval between two consecutive knots, and continuity
conditions at the knots provide smooth transitions between pieces.
A spline that is continuous in position (C0) and first derivative (C1) has no tangent disôÙ
continuities at segment boundaries. If second derivatives match as well, the spline is C2
continuous, and so on [102]. For a spline of degree n, derivatives are continuous up to
order n ã1 at the knots [101]. Increasing the continuity class improves path smoothness:
ãÂ C0: continuous position, sharp corners possible.
ãÂ C1: continuous velocity, but acceleration may change abruptly.
ãÂ C2: continuous acceleration, producing smoother curvature changes.
ãÂ C3: continuous jerk, reducing dynamic shocks.
Another important concept to understand is the spline order. The order (degree) of the
polynomial segments in a spline determines how many derivatives can be made continuôÙ
ous and how the motion characteristics will behave. In motion planning for manipulation,
the most common choices are quadratic, cubic, or quintic splines, corresponding to polyôÙ
nomial degrees 2, 3, and 5 respectively. HigherôÙdegree polynomials offer greater contiôÙ
nuity but come with increased complexity and potential for oscillation, so these three tend
to balance smoothness and practicality [103].
ãÂ Quadratic splines (n = 2): can achieve up to C1 continuity, ensuring smooth veôÙ
locity but allowing abrupt changes in acceleration.
ãÂ Cubic splines (n = 3): can achieve up to C2 continuity, ensuring smooth accelerôÙ
ation and curvature transitions.
ãÂ Quintic splines (n = 5): can achieve up to C4 continuity, ensuring smooth jerk (C3)
and snap (C4), suitable for highôÙspeed or precision tasks where dynamic shocks
must be minimised.
Interpolating vs. Approximating Splines:
ãÂ Interpolating splines pass exactly through all specified waypoints {q0, . . . , qn}, typôÙ
ically formulated as a boundaryôÙvalue problem with continuity constraints.
ãÂ Approximating splines, such as BôÙsplines or Bûˋzier curves, are influenced by a set
of control points {P0, . . . , Pm} but do not necessarily pass through all of them.

Contour Extraction & Surface Normals
The method operates directly on the segmentation masks output by the YOLO model.
The mask is used as basis for the further grasp planning. The external contour of the
binary mask is extracted using cv2.findContours. To reduce noise and simplify the conôÙ
tour geometry, polygonal approximation is applied via cv2.approxPolyDP, with a relative
epsilon parameter controlling the approximation tolerance. The choice of epsilon repreôÙ
sents a tradeôÙoff: high values can oversimplify the contour, producing too few points for
reliable grasp analysis, while low values may preserve excessive detail and noise.
Normals at each contour point are estimated using a central difference method over a
local window of size 2k + 1, where k is the neighbourhood size parameter. For a point
pi, the tangent vector is computed between points piãk and pi+k. The surface normal is
then obtained as the perpendicular vector to this tangent. The surface normal must be
outward facing. This is checked for using cv2.pointPolygonTest. InwardôÙfacing normals
are flipped.

wmax, where wmin is the minimum practical grasp width and wmax is the maximum

contour points ãp1 and ãp2 respectively, must be sufficiently opposed. This is quantified

checks. This behaviour leads to a counterintuitive situation where denser PRMs both plan
faster and produce shorter jointôÙspace paths.

Testing Planners In Simulation ôÙ Conclusion
This section evaluated multiple samplingôÙbased planners in a simulated environment, both
with and without obstacles. In the obstacleôÙfree case, most planners performed well, with
RRT* and RRT with connecting showing consistent execution and short paths when tuned
with high bias and large step sizes. PRM also achieved fast planning but produced longer
and more variable trajectories due to sparse sampling.
In the scenarios with obstacles, the difference between planners became more distinct.
Most RRT and RRT* runs failed to find a path within the allowed time, no matter the
configuration. PRM variants with dense graphs (100k samples) performed significantly
better than their sparse graphs equivalents, though they required longer execution due
to high path curvature and more waypoints. RRT with connecting turned out to be the
most practical option ôÙ it produced the the most smooth trajectories in tool space, while
maintaining acceptable planning times. PRM has great potential, but the roadmap must be
constructed differently to be practical in constrained environments. The main bottleneck
for all planners is the collision checking stage, which is computationally expensive and
dominates planning time when enabled.

application, which prioritises robust detection of known object classes.

Discussion & Conclusion
This chapter discusses the results in the context of the research objectives and relevant
literature, interpreting both quantitative metrics and qualitative observations from simulaôÙ
tion and realôÙworld tests. The chapter concludes with a summary of key findings and an
outline of promising directions for future work. This chapter presents the results in relation

ses both quantitative metrics and qualitative observations from simulation and physical
experiments. The discussion is followed by a conclusion and potential avenues for future
work.
These were the research questions discussed in this project:
ãÂ What are the tradeôÙoffs in trajectory quality, computation time, and success rate
between different samplingôÙbased planners (e.g., RRT, RRT*, PRM) for a 7ôÙDOF
manipulation task?
ãÂ How can samplingôÙbased motion planning algorithms be effectively applied to genôÙ
erate collisionôÙfree trajectories in a highôÙdimensional configuration space under realôÙ
world constraints?
ãÂ How can visionôÙbased calibration methods be employed to reliably and automatically
align the camera and robot coordinate frames in the workspace?
ãÂ How can postôÙprocessing techniques be used to convert discretized waypoints into
smooth, valid jointôÙspace trajectories for a robotic system?
7.1
Discussion
One of the main goals of this work was to gain a better understanding of motion planôÙ
ning algorithms. The interactive 2D visualisation tool served this purpose well, allowing
detailed inspection of planner behaviour, particularly in terms of node expansion, goal
biasing, and path quality. It provided an accessible environment for quickly testing ideas
and tuning parameters before applying them to higherôÙdimensional cases. The 2D visualiôÙ
sation app played a large role in the further implementation of motion planning algorithms,
as the visualisations made the algorithms ãtransparentã while making errors in implemenôÙ
tation easy to spot.
The transition to a real 7ôÙDOF robotic arm introduced a new set of challenges and insights.
It demonstrated the flexibility of redundant manipulators, but also exposed additional comôÙ
plexity caused by redundancy, such as unnatural goal configurations when weights were
chosen incorrectly. Working with a physical system also highlighted the importance of
robustness ôÙ while small errors or collisions might be tolerated in simulation, they often
result in failure on real hardware. Each system component (such as planning, calibration,
and detection) had to consistently produce valid outputs and exchange data correctly with
the others to ensure uninterrupted operation of the full pipeline.
The realôÙworld experiments confirmed that several of the implemented planners were suitôÙ
able for use in the final demonstration in an obstacleôÙfree environment. In this realôÙworld
scenario, all RRTôÙConnect variants outperformed the default MoveIt! planner by approxôÙ
imately 20 % in the total execution time. For tests with the obstacle, one PRM variant
and all RRTôÙConnect implementations consistently completed the task. They achieved a
success rate of 100 % and demonstrated stable behaviour across all tested cases. The
bestôÙperforming RRTôÙConnect variant was, on average, 32.6 % slower than the MoveIt!
implementation. While MoveIt! maintained an advantage in overall planning time under

these conditions, the performance gap remained within an acceptable margin, particularly
considering that the custom planners were not fully optimised.
The implemented cubic and quintic spline based postôÙprocessing showed good perforôÙ
mance. Quintic splines were, on average, four to five times slower to compute than their
cubic counterparts. Path length was comparable between interpolation types for cubic
Hermite splines and quintic polynomial splines, respectively. Quintic BôÙsplines exhibited
shortest pathôÙlengths overall at the cost of cornerôÙcutting of the desired trajectory. OverôÙ
all, quintic splines provided the most desirable characteristics and were thus chosen for
the final implementation.
The vision pipeline was able to find the extrinsic relationship between workspace and
camera to a satisfactory degree of precision. The robot was able to move a calibration
cube to a target with a precision of 8 mm or less. Furthermore, the implemented object
detection/segmentation system showed remarkably good performance.
The YOLOôÙbased pipeline exhibited a good recall and was able to locate moving objects
during testing. Object detection was only observed as a failure mode in one instance
out of 24 trials of dynamic picking with objects moving on a conveyor belt. This was
somewhat surprising, as the detection pipeline had shown middling results in prior static
recall testing.
The implemented Asynchronous Kalman Filter showed acceptable performance and was
able to successfully predict future object locations after three seconds of observation on
the conveyor belt. Dynamic picking had a time delay of up to eight seconds between
acquisition and picking, and the predicted future states from the Kalman filter proved acôÙ
curate enough for the picking trials. This delay is due to the total time taken for path
planning, postôÙprocessing, and execution on the robot.
The final demo test showed good performance with 24 picking trials. Out of those trials,
four were a failure. One failure to was attributable to object detection, while three failures
were due to incorrect grasp planning.
7.2
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was the exploration and integration of motion planning algorithms
for deployment on the Franka Panda robotic manipulator.
The implemented planners demonstrated solid performance for static and dynamic picking
in obstacleôÙfree scenarios. RRTôÙConnect was found to be the highestôÙperforming algoôÙ
rithm out of the tested set, and the implementation showed performance on par with the
commonly used MoveIt! planner. Planning times for some custom planners increased
significantly in scenes with static obstacles, making them less suitable for realôÙtime use
without further optimisation.
The implemented postôÙprocessing pipeline was able to transform discrete viaôÙpoints into
feasible trajectories that respects the limits of the robotic system. Quintic spline interpoôÙ
lation was chosen for the final implementation for its higher overall execution quality.
The implemented vision pipeline with YOLO and Kalman filter successfully detected, segôÙ
mented, and tracked a variety of everyday objects.
A final dynamic picking demo was undertaken. The system exhibited satisfactory overall
performance performance overall and was able to perform 20 successful dynamic picks
out of 24 attempts.
Furthermore, a 2D motion planning app has been developed as a sandbox and showcase
of motion planning algorithms.

7.3
Future Work
Based on the work presented in this thesis, a handful of avenues for future work can be
highlighted:
The literature review identified several promising stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart planning methods. FuôÙ
ture development of the system should prioritise extending the current set of planners to
include approaches introduced in Section 3.3. Especially optimisationôÙbased approaches
seem promising for highôÙperformance planning in higher dimensional CôÙspaces.
Grasp planning has been implemented as a simplistic 2D segmentation based approach.
A general picking system for industry could leverage more sophisticated 3DôÙbased apôÙ
proaches. Prior work at the NRTôÙlab [33] has focused on classification and manipulation
of fruits using a robotic system. A unified approach combining vision and tactile sensôÙ
ing would enable a host of smart applications for industry, such as waste sorting or food
processing.
The planners developed in this thesis were implemented in Python and not heavily optiôÙ
mised. In contrast, the MoveIt! framework for ROS provides a selection of stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart
planners implemented in C++. Achieving performance parity with MoveIt! would likely reôÙ
quire reôÙimplementing the planners from this thesis in C++.
