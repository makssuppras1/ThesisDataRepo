Understanding transmission routes and patterns in Danish AMDV
by Jacob Hellum
It is important for mink farmers to control and monitor the health of the animals living
on the farms. This makes it important to understand and track possible infection and
epidemic events that threaten the health of the mink population. In this thesis AMDV
sequences from 7 diﬀerent danish mink farms was analysed to try and understand the
relationships between the sequnces. Three diﬀerent programs was used to create phylo-
genic trees for the sequences, PAUP*, MrBayes and BEAST. These trees gave an overall
ida of the transmission route between the farms. FarmF and farmG got the virus ﬁrst
and infected farmA and farmE that infected farmD. FarmA infected farmB and farmC,
but a more clear picture of the transmission route between farmA, farmB and farmC
could not be made from the trees. A transmission tree was alo constructed with the
method developed by Matthew Hall [1]. This tree did however not come with a conclu-
sive result because of poor sampling.
To determine how credibly the BEAST results was despite poor sampling a syntethic
data set was generated with INDELible. This data set consist of 1000 alignments of 300
sequnces. Small samples were taken from each alignment and analysed with BEAST.
BEAST depicted the true situation in 80,8 % of the cases. However BEAST did manage
to depict the true situation of a sub tree in 97,2 % of the cases.
To get a better idea of the tranmission route and patterns between the danish mink
farms more samples whould be taken. Wild animals should also be checked for AMDV
and possible samples taken and incorporated in the analysis.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Mink fur production is a large business in Denmark. In 2014 there was sold fur for 7.8
billion DKK [2]. This makes it important to be able to monitor and be in control of the
health of the animals. If a mink farm gets infected with a virus that harms their animals,
that can potntially cost the farm a lot of money. If a farm gets infected it is important
to undrstand the transmission routes. If the route of transmission is discorvered the
farm may be able to prevent future infections from the same source by either changing
procedures or installing new procedures to minimize the risk of infection.
Aleutian Mink Disease (AMD) is caused by a small (25nm in diameter), icosahedral,
non-enveloped virus from the genus Amdoparvovirus [3]. AMD can cause spontaneous
abortion and it can be deadly in mink. It dysregulates the immune system, causing
systemic immune complex-mediated vasculitis; followed by a wide variety of clinical
symptoms, including renal failure, respiratory distress and death [4]. This makes Aleu-
tian Mink Disease Virus (AMDV) very unfortunate for farms to be infected of, and
possibly very expensive.
In this theisis the sequences of AMDV from 7 danish mink farms will be used to infer a
transmission rotue between the farms. From each farm up to 4 viruses have ben seqe-
unced. This poses another academic question this thesis will try and answer. On each
farm there is thousands of animals, many of which can be infected, and within each in-
fected animal there is sequence variety. A sample size of 3-4 from each farm is therefore
very narrow. This could mean that the results shown by analysing the sequences could
indicate a relationship between the sqeunces that is simpliﬁed and gives a wrong picture
of the situation, because a lot of information is not included in the models.
To answer these questions this thesis will use phylogenetic analysis of the real samples as
well as analysis of simulated sequences. The simulated sequences is used to understand
how often a small sample size, from a large sequence pool, will show a wrong picture of
reality. To do this a variety of software and tools has been used, which are listed and

2.2.1
Maximum likelihood phylogeny sing PAUP*
Maximum likelihood estimation is a standard statistical procedure and has been widely
used in phylogeneic analysis.
The basic idea is to calculate the likelihood, L, of an
observed dataset given a particular hypothesis and some assumed probabilistic model
[5, 6]:
L = Prob(data|hypothesis)
(2.1)
For a tree topology, τ, the likelihood would be written as:
L(τ) = Prob(D|τ)
(2.2)
PAUP* is a package with a command line interface that ﬁnds the maximum likelihood
tree with an iterative process, and a lot of possible options and criteria [5]. The procces
that PAUP* uses to ﬁnd the best tree can be by a heuristic search. A heuristic search
is carried out in the following way:
1. Construct initial tree and determine its likelihood.
2. Construct a set of neighbor trees and determine their likelihoods. The trees are
constructed by making small rearrangements to the original tree.
3. Check if any of the neighboring trees are better and select it/them.
4. Repeat steps 2 and 3 until a tree that is better than all the neighbors has been
found.
Note that the tree found by this aproach could be a local optimum and not a global op-
timum. There is therefore no guarantee that the tree found is the best of the entire tree
space. A heuristic search is quick, which is why it is often used in maximum likelihood
searches. It is however important to note that it is a tradeoﬀbetween execution time
and completeness. If it is enough to get one of the good trees, in the form of a local
optimum, then a heuristic search can be a very porverfull tool, but if it is essntial for
the analysis that the global best tree is found then another method should be used [7].

2.2.2
Selection of substitution model with PAUP*
The substituion model was selected with PAUP*. A script provided by Anders Gorm
(2015) was used to evaluate the models and select the best model based on the Akaike
information criterion (AIC). AIC is quality estimation for the models that estimates the
quality of each model relative to each other model, and can therefore be used to compare
models and select the best one. The formula for AIC is:
AIC = 2k −ln(L)
(2.3)
==PAGE:14==

8
where k is the number of estimated parameters in the model and L is the maximum
likelihood [11].
The model with the lowest AIC will have the best ﬁt of the model
candidates. Note that AIC only scores the models relative to each other. If all the
models ﬁt poorly AIC will not give any warning about it.
2.2.3
MrBayes for phylogeny
MrBayes is a program for bayesian inference of phylogenies that uses a commandline
interface [12, 13]. Bayesian inference is based on Bayes’ Theorem or Bayes’ rule which
states that probability of a given value of p given some sample (a, b) can be obtained
using the probability function [13]:
f(p|a, b) = f(p)f(a, b|p)
f(a, b)
(2.4)
where f(p|a, b) is called the posterior probability distribution, or in short the posterior.
It speciﬁes the probability of all values of p after the prior probability distribution has
been updated with all the avavible data.
Because the posterior, in most cases, is impossible to derive analytically another method
needs to be used. This is where Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling, or MCMC
for short, comes in. Markov chains converge towards an equilibrium state regardless of
starting point. Bayesian inference in phylogeny is therefore as simple as setting up a
MCMC that converges onto the posterior. This can be done with a variety of methods,
but MrBayes uses the Metropolis algorithm [14]. The idea is to make small random
changes to some current parameter values and then accept or reject those changes. The
algorithm can be described in four steps:
1. Start at an arbitraty point θ
2. Make a small random move to θ∗
3. Calculate the height ratio r of the new state to the old state
(a) if r > 1 then the new state is accepted
(b) if r < 1 then the new state is accepted with the probability r, if the new state
is rejected, then stay in the old state
4. Go to step 2
For some phylogenetic problems the MCMC can have diﬃculty or it can be impossible
for it to achieve convergence within a reasonable number of generations. Because of

this MrBayes also uses what is called Metropolis coupling [15], MCMCMC or (MC)3 for
short. This introduces a series of Markov chains that sample from a heated posterior
probability distribution. This ﬂattens out the posterior probability surface by raising
the posterior probability to a power smaller than 1 for the heating [13].
2.2.4
BEAST for phylogeny
The software package Bayesian Evolutionary Analysis by Sampling Trees (BEAST) is
used to analyze evolutionary data sets [16, 17].
BEAST uses, as MrBayes, the core
algorithm Metropolis-Hastings Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC) [14, 18]. MCMC is
a stochastical algoritm that, in BEAST, produces sample based estimates of the posterior
distribution of a set of evolutionary parameters given a set of molecular sequences [17].
BEAST along with the graphical user interface Bayesian Evolutionary Analysis Utility
(BEAUti) is a very powrfull and ﬂexible tool for analysing evolutionary data sets.
BEAST divide the evolutionary model into 5 diﬀerent components[17]:
• Substitution model
A homogeneous Markov prcocess that deﬁnes the relativere rates for the diﬀerent
substitutions
• Rate model among sites
The ditribution of relative rates of evolutionary change among sites
• Rate model among branches
The distribution of rates among branches and is also used to convert the tree,
which is in units of time, to units of substitutions
• Tree
A model of the phylogenetic or genealogical relationships of the sequences.
• Tree prior
A parameterized node height and tree topology prior distribution.
For each of these components there are a range of possibilities which gives the user a wide
array of evolutionary models that can easily be created. This creates a very powerfull
tool for analysing ancestral relationships between seqeunces. The possibility of adding
a clock model also gives rise to more elaborate models, with more data incorporated.

2.2.4.1
BEAST MCMC moves
BEAST use 5 diﬀerent proposal mechanisms (moves) in the MCMC. The moves are
Scaling move, Wilson-Balding move, Subtree exchange, subtree slide, Node age move
and random walk move [19].
The scaling move will scale the times ti associated with ancestral nodes, but will leave
leaf node times (which are part of the data) unchanged.
Wilson-Balding move moves a random subtree to a new branch. It is based on the
branch swapping move of Wilson and Balding [20]. An example of a wilson-balding
move has been visualized in ﬁgure 2.4. The operator for this move picks a random node.
The parent of that node is then pruned and reattached to a random position as long as
there is no height conﬂicts.
Figure 2.4: A diagram showing a wilson-balding move. The subtree consisting of A
and B, marked in blue, is moved to to the E branch, marked in green.
Subtree exchange is a move that switches two random subtrees with each other as
long as they have an aunt-niece relationship, i.e. the parent of one is the grandparent
of the other, but neither is parent to the other. An example of a subtree exchange can
be seen in ﬁgure 2.5. The operator for this move randomly selects two nodes in the
pyhlogeny that are not siblings or the root. The heights and parent heights can not
be such that swapping their parent would lead to a situation where a node is lower in
the tree than its child. With these criteria fuﬁlled the random nodes are slected and
swapped [1, 19].

The subtree slide move slides a subtree up or down in the tree topology. An example
can be seen in ﬁgure 2.6. The operator for this move picks a random node u. Then u’s
parent uP is moved a distance ∆up or down the tree along a path connecting the root

2.2.5
BEAST for transmission trees
A new method for inferring transmission trees has ben created by Matthew Hall [1].
This method has been implemented in the BEAST package, and this thsis will use this
method to create a transmission tree for the real data set. A transmission tree in this case
is a directed acyclic graph which describes which host (or case) directly infected which
others. In this case a transmission tree includes no timing information, that means that
edges have no meaningfull information. The transmission tree is, unlike phylogenies,
not binary, that is a node can have multiple children. Finally the the number of nodes
is the number of cases in the epidemic. An example of a transmission tree can be seen
in ﬁgure 2.7.
Figure 2.7: An example of a transmission tree with 9 cases. In this example A infected

2.2.5.1
Bayesian decomposition
To understand some of the diﬀerences between the regular BEAST model and the model
for transmission trees implemented by Matthew Hall [1] and what the transmission tree
models adds, a short review of the bayesian decomposition of each model will be given
here. The normal bayesian decomposition for BEAST is:
p(T, ω, ψ|D) ∝p(D|T, ω)p(T|ψ)p(ψ)p(ω)
(2.5)

substitution model, p(T|ψ) is the likelihood of the tree given the coalescent model and

(2.6)

eters are assumed to be independent of the transmission tree structure.
p(β|N, T inf, φ) is derived from Bayes’ Theorem and the law of total probability, also
asuming β and φ are independent:
p(β|N, T inf, φ) =
p(N, T inf|β, φ)p(β)

β p(N, T inf|β, φ)p(β)dβ
(2.7)
p(N, T inf|β, φ)p(β) is the probability of the epedemic given the transmission parameters.
p(T|N, T inf, ω) is the probability of all the sub phylogenies based on the coalescent
parameters.
p(N|T inf, φ) is a term that alows adjustment of the prior probability for cases with
known epidemiological links.
The changes to the BEAST procedure implemented with this method ensures that the
changes done to the tree topology correctly takes the transmission route into account.
This makes the resulting phylogenies better for inﬀering a transmission tree than with
the regular BEAST procedure.

2.2.6
Simulation of synthetic data with INDELible
To create a simulated data set INDELible version 1.03 was used. Unlike other programs
for simulation of sequences INDELible does not view each nucleotide, amino acid or
codon as the unit of evolution, but rather the entire sequence. The change from one
seqeunce to antoher is then described by a Markov chain, with the whole seqence being
the state of the chain[21]. INDELible uses the Gillespie algorithm [22] where it generates
exponentially distributed waiting times and samples from the jump chain.
Lets consider how INDELible determines if an event has occured at a given time. If the
sequence at the start of the branch as well as the branch length (t) is given and I, D,
and S is the total insertion, deletion and substitution rates respectivly, then the total
event rate for the current seqence can be written as:
λ = I + D + S
(2.8)
INDELible then generates a waiting time s1 by sampling from the exponential distri-
bution with mean 1/λ. If s1 > t then no event occurs before the end of the branch.
If not, an event occurs at s1 and it is randomly selcted to be an insertion, deletion
or substitution with the probabilities I/λ, D/λ or S/λ. The location of the event is
also determined by random sampling with probabilities proportional to the rates. This
creates a new seqeunce at time s1 and the sequence length L and the rates for the new
sequence are updated. The time remaning is now = t −s1 and a new waiting time s2 is
generated based on the rate for the current seqeunce. This is repeated until the length
of the branch has been reaches, i.e s1 + s2 . . . sn > t [21].

Deletions in INDELible follow the procedure of Cartwright [23]. The simulated se-

2.2.7
Creating maximum clade credibility trees with TreeAnnotator
The BEAST package creates a sample of trees based on the steps set by the user in
the MCMC. TreeAnnotater picks one of these trees as the maximum clade credibility
(MCC) tree. This is diﬀerent from MrBayes because with TreeAnnotator a tree that was
actually visited by the MCC is picked whereas in MrBayes (with the sumt command) a
consesus tree is produced. The tree Treeannotator program picks the one that has the
highest product of all the clade credibilities (posterior probabilities) for all the clades in
the tree. The tree is then annotated with summary information for the full set of trees
for the sample, f.x. average node heights, rates, credible intervals for the node heights
etc. [24].

4.1.5
Simulation tree
The tree seen in ﬁgure 3.5.1 show the tree structure used to create the simulated align-
ments. This tree was made to have a known situation to test in BEAST. The tree was
created to reﬂect the actual data set as much as possible which is why the substitution
model found in section 3.1 was used. To create the tree a hypothesis was created. The
hypotheis was that the picture seen in the phylogenetic trees was inccorect du to small
sample size. This would mean that the real tree would look diﬀerent. Then it was hy-
pothesised that the correct tree would have farmB and farmC as monophyletic subtrees
of farmA, and thus the sistuation that needed to be tested was created.
The simulated tree was created by simulating each individual tree with the depths of
subtrees observed in the real data. Then these three trees was merged in a way that
would most mimic the obeserved behavior of the real data. This was again one to have
a situation that was as much like the situation observed in the real data so it will be
possible to draw parrallels between the simulated data and the real data. It has two
situations that can be looked at. The tree reﬂect a situation were the host A infected
both B and C, but no cross infection was seen afterwards. This in seen by the fact that
both B and C are monophyletic subtrees under A. Because a known situation is now
had it is possible to test how often BEAST shows this picture when few randomized
samples are taken.
4.1.6
Transmission trees created with BEAST
When creating the transmission trees ﬁrst the scope of the epidemic needs to be deter-
mined. As seen in ﬁgure 3.6 and 3.8 the transmission route changes if looking at the
entire data set instead of only the recent samples. The fact that the samples has such a
large gap (2004 to 2014) aﬀects the model a lot. The biggest diﬀerence in the resulting
transmission tree is wheter or not FarmA infected FarmD and FarmE, as seen in ﬁgure
3.7 and 3.9. When taking the all the samples into account FarmD and FarmE was in-
fected by FarmG and if only looking at the recent then it is inferred that both FarmD
and FarmE was infected by FarmA. The days in which the infection has transpiered is
also vastly diﬀerent. When looking at the recent samples only the root of the tree is
about 27,2 days, but when taking all the samples it is close to 4900. For ﬁve farms
to be infected and have multiple infected animals that show the virus in just under 30
days seems like quite a short time period. This means that it is more reasonable to

4.1.7
Simulated data trees
The simlulated data trees are mad as an emulation of the real situation. A small num-
ber of samlpes are taken from a bigger picture, and then analyzed with BEAST to infer
some connection between the samples. When looking at the phylogenies done on the real
data it is always important to remember that the tree only represents a small part of a
theoretical much larger tree. This much larger tree is what ﬁgure 3.5.1 represents. When
taking small samples out of that tree there are four overall ways the phylogeny can look:
B is a monophyletic subtree under A while C is not, C is a monophyletic subtree under
A while B is not, neither B or C is a monophyletic subtree under A or both B and C
are monophyletic subtrees under A. Figures 3.10 to 3.13 show these diﬀerent outcomes
of the phylogenies. The phylogeny that reﬂects the real picture is the one where both
B and C are monophyletic trees under A (ﬁgure 3.10). This is the know situation that
was created in the simulated data with INDELible, and thus the picture that the tree
should depict.
Because of the random sampling there is a possibility in each tree that the samples taken
from A is all from either the subtree with B samples or with C samples. Figure 3.13
is an example of such a situation. This however still does not give the correct picture
of the true situation nor would it even if B was a monophyletic clade (as seen in ﬁgure
B.3). In this tree it is not clear that A infected C even though that is what happened.
This means that even though C is still monophyletic this tree is not a correct picture of

4.1.8
Tree analysis
With all the trees analysed it shows that BEAST gives a correct picture of the situation
in 80,8 % of the time, see table 3.2. The reason there are more situations of C not being
a monophyletic subtree of A than B not being a monophyletic subtree of A is probably
because the clade including C has fewer A sequences than the clade including the B
sequences. This means that the random sampling will more often give rise to a situation
were it picked no A sequences from the subtree including C. This could be avoided by
either renaming the A sequences in one of the subtrees to something diﬀrent, fx. D, or
contruct a tree that has exactly the same amount of A sequences in both subtrees. Both
of these solutions are viable and it varies from situation to situation which should be
chosen. It could be a totally diﬀernet situation in which it was interesteing to se how
often BEAST would represent the true picture. It is therefore important to ﬁrst identify
the situation that is interesting to examine, and then create the simulated data so it
most accuartly depicts that situation.
4.2
Overall discussion and comaprassion of the results
When looking at all the resluslts from the diﬀerent analysis there is no completely clear
picture of the transmission route between the farms. When including all the data, which
seems to be the most resonable, some consensus is however observed. The trees gener-
ally agrees that farmF and farmG are the ﬁrst to be infected. This is very reasonable
because the samples taken from these farms were taken 10 years before the others. It
would therefore seem very likely that these seqeunces are closer to the root of the tree
than the newer samples. The trees also seem to put farmD and farmE seperate from
farmA, farmB and farmC. This could indicate diﬀerent things. It could indicate that it
is two seperate epiemics, which seems unlikely given how closely related the sequences
are. A more resonable assumption is that two seperate infection events has occured, and
no cross infection between the two groups has happened.
The trees agree that of farmA, farmB and farmC, farmA was infected ﬁrst. The picture
after the inital infection is not completely clear, and it could seem like multiple sperate
infection events has occured as well as cross infection.
This would mean that there
is a somewhat free movement of animals between the farms. These animals could be
wild mink that travel between the farms, escaped animals from the diﬀerent farms, or

The great timespan betwen the samples leaves a lot to the unknown. It is impossible
to say for sure how the infection has occured, but some reasonabl situations may be

The analysis of the simulated data indicates that BEAST is reasoably good at represent-

tranmission route between the farms.
The clock model created with BEAST seems be give a reasonable result. There is still

of the transmission route. As discussed above the phylogenies created with the trans-

very good sampling. It is however clear that farmA infected farmB and farmC, and
this relationship could be investigated further with more sampling. This hypothesis is

trees.

Future work
It could be interesting to create a larger more reﬁned tree for INDELible to create
simulated data. This could be used to take diﬀerent amount of random samples and see
the change in percentage of times BEAST depicts the situation correclty. This could
help give an indication of how many samples is needed at minimum for BEAST to give
a credible result. This could be done quite easily as it does not require anything else
than a computer and time to be done. It would however take a long time to run the
BEAST procedure on all the created alignments for all the diﬀerent sample sizes. With
the laptop avaviable to do this thesis 100 alignments took about 12 hours to do. And
this was for the amount of samples used in section 3.7, i.e. 13 sequences (5 A, 4 B and
4 C). With larger and more data sets this would quickly become very time consuming
without the use of a more powerfull computer with way more computing power.
To get a clearer picture of the transmission route and patterns between the danish mink
farms more samples should be taken regularly over a period of time. This would give
much better basis for the transission tree method implemented in the BEAST packages
by Matthew Hall [1]. With this expanded analysis it would be possible to get a better
picture of when and where the infections took place.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to examine the possible AMDV tranmission routes between danish
mink farms. Transmission trees has been created with a method speciﬁc for that purpose
in BEAST, but because of poor sampling these transmission trees are not conlusive.
They, together with the phylogenetic trees created with BEAST, MrBayes and PAUP*
does give some indications of the transmission route. Overall farmF and farmG was
infected ﬁrst, farmD and farmE was infected seperatly from farmA, farmB and farmC
and farmA infected farmB and farmC. A more precise transmission route, especially
between farmA, farmB and farmC, is hard to determine based on the results in this
thesis. To give a better understanding and more accurate and credible transmission tree
more samples needs to be taken and analysed together with the current samples.
It was also a goal to determine how credible the results from BEAST was even because of
very poor sampling. For this 1000 syntethic alignments was created with 300 sequences in
each, see ﬁgure 3.5. From each of these alignments few samples were drawn and analysed
with BEAST. This resulted in 1000 trees that were reviewed and it was determined that
BEAST had depicted the known situation correctly 80,8 % of the times. It did however
also show that it correctly depicted a sub situation (were B was a monphyletic subtree
of A) 97,2 % of the times. This mean that BEAST is able to create phylogenies that
depicts the true situation quite well. This is of course only applicable in this situation,
because the simulated data was construcctd so it best resembled the real data set.
