Preface
This thesis was prepared at the National Institute of Aquatic Resources at the Technical University
of Denmark in fulfillment of the requirements for acquiring a Master of Science degree in Aquatic
Science and Technology.
This project explores the synergistic interactions between eelgrass meadows and blue mussel beds
and how knowledge of this can be used to improve marine coastal restoration efforts.
The report is divided into five sections. The first section contains an introduction to ecosystem
co-restoration, the study site, relevant species, and hypothesis. The second section presents the
methodology, data, main assumptions, statistics, and important concepts. The third and fourth
sections consist of the results and discussion related to these. The fifth and final section concludes
with the main findings.
I hope that this work inspires further research and discussion in the field of coastal ecosystem
restoration, with an emphasis on facilitative interspecies interactions and co-restoration among
ecosystem engineers.

Abstract
Eelgrass (Zostera marina) is a paramount seagrass in terms of ecosystem services and biodiversity
in the Northern Hemisphere. In Denmark, less than 30% of the historical eelgrass coverage remains,
highlighting the urgent need for action. In the literature, co-restoring eelgrass with suspension-
feeding mollusks, such as blue mussels (Mytilus edulis), has been argued to be a potentially effective
tool to enhance eelgrass restoration efforts. In this project, a synergistic relationship between blue
mussels and eelgrass was proposed. It was hypothesized that mussel filtration could improve light
conditions within eelgrass under optimal current regimes. Comprehensive high-resolution spatial
and temporal data from April to September 2023 were provided from Lovns Bredning, Limfjorden.
The data included measurements of photosynthetically active radiation (PAR), which were used
to calculate the direct light attenuation coefficient for PAR, Kd(PAR), and measurements of water
current regimes obtained from acoustic Doppler current profilers (ADCPs). I examined the light
conditions in the water column above eelgrass meadows and blue mussel beds to compare them. In
addition, parcels of water were followed across the habitats, evaluating the light conditions within
eelgrass located downstream of blue mussels. Lastly, generalized linear mixed models (GLMMs)
were constructed to investigate the temporal variation in Kd(PAR). In summary, the initial analysis
provided very strong evidence for better light conditions above blue mussel beds compared to
eelgrass meadows, likely due to filtration by blue mussels. Furthermore, when the eelgrass meadows
were located downstream of blue mussel beds, the light conditions were up to 104%-points better
in terms of Kd(PAR) compared to those upstream, supporting the initial hypothesis. Finally, the
GLMMs provided strong evidence that the temporal variation in Kd(PAR) was determined by
time, used as a proxy for temporal changes in chlorophyll a concentration, rather than current
velocity at the study site. This study provides strong evidence that blue mussels can be a promising
tool to improve water clarity within eelgrass meadows. However, the duration of the periods with
improved water clarity should be considered when assessing whether the growth of eelgrass is
affected. From an ecosystem perspective, such interactions between species can be crucial factors
to consider in an attempt to restore habitats. I suggest further research on co-restoration and
facilitative interactions among species in order to accelerate coastal restoration worldwide.

Introduction
In this section, I briefly introduce marine measures as ecosystem restoration tools and outline the
concept of facilitative interactions through co-restoration. Furthermore, I address Limfjorden as a
system, highlighting some of its main characteristics and pressure factors, as well as introducing
Lovns Bredning. Eelgrass and blue mussels are described thereafter, with emphasis on the services
provided by their ecosystem-engineering habitats. Lastly, the hypotheses of the project are pre-
sented. The aim of this section is to frame the reasoning behind the project and to provide a brief
understanding of the current conditions and the importance of restoring marine coastal habitats
in the inner Danish waters.
1.1
Ecosystem restoration and marine measures
A general definition of a marine measure is any activity that is undertaken to improve the ecologi-
cal conditions in the marine environment, including habitat improvements and nutrient reductions
(Petersen et al., 2024a). These measures are becoming increasingly important, as most of the
Danish coastal waters suffer from a wide range of pressure factors, e.g., excessive nutrient runoff,
global warming, and intensive fishing, causing eutrophication, hypoxia, and deterioration of bio-
genic reefs (Hansen and Høgslund, 2023). Efforts are being made to restore natural ecosystems,
with various degrees of success. The success rate of seagrass restoration attempts has been reported
as low as 37% (Van Katwijk et al., 2016), while for some marine bivalve mollusks, it is around
56% (Bayraktarov et al., 2015). The restoration outcome depends on environmental conditions,
such as levels of nutrients and hydrodynamic exposure, which might pose the biggest challenge
due to limited light availability and reduced anchoring capacity when restoring ecosystems (Bos
and Van Katwijk, 2007; Leschen et al., 2010; Riemann et al., 2016; Stæhr et al., 2019; Pajusalu
et al., 2023).
1.1.1
Facilitative interactions in co-restoration
The principle of facilitative interactions between eelgrass and blue mussels has been known for
decades (Petersen et al., 1997), and co-restoration as a tool to enhance seagrass restoration success
has been investigated previously (Gagnon et al., 2021; Pajusalu et al., 2023). Blue mussel beds and
eelgrass meadows are often located in close proximity to each other, and habitat prediction maps

==PAGE:8==
Master’s thesis

as well as historical records have shown the two to overlap, highlighting that the species already
naturally coexists (Bos and Van Katwijk, 2007; Petersen et al., 2024b).
In addition, eelgrass
meadows extent to deeper waters when nearby blue mussels (Bos and Van Katwijk, 2007, and
references therein). Eelgrass can facilitate the survival of blue mussel beds by reducing the water
flow above the reef, allowing for increased particle sedimentation, and supplying the mussels with
food (Petersen et al., 1997). Eelgrass can also provide physical protection for the blue mussels
and enhance larvae settlement (Gagnon et al., 2021). Blue mussels can facilitate eelgrass survival
too by reducing the hydrodynamic stress exerted on eelgrass (Bos and Van Katwijk, 2007; Gagnon
et al., 2021). Moreover, mussels are known to reduce the water column turbidity via filtration
(Riisgaard et al., 2004; Wall et al., 2008) and fertilize the sediment porewater with feces and pseudo-
feces, potentially stimulating the growth of eelgrass (Bos and Van Katwijk, 2007). Fertilization of
porewater is only beneficial to eelgrass in oligotrophic systems, whereas it can be detrimental in
eutrophic systems due to the production of sulfides (Castorani et al., 2015; Vinther and Holmer,
2008). As described, eelgrass and blue mussels are considered facilitators (Saurel et al., 2013).
Synergistic effects between the two can therefore be an important tool in the effort to mitigate
and reverse poor ecological conditions and to support coastal ecosystem recovery.
Light is the main factor regulating the abundance of seagrasses in deeper waters (Riemann
et al., 2016), and the light requirements vary seasonally with temperature (Stæhr and Borum,
2011). Eelgrass has a high requirement for light, with a maximum saturation of ∼450 µmol m−2 s−1,
and a minimum demand of ∼85 µmol m−2 s−1 (Kuusemäe et al., 2016). Measurements in the north-
ern part of Lovns Bredning revealed an average PAR of ∼200 µmol m−2 s−1 at ∼1 m depth in the
eelgrass meadows, which indicates that increasing the light availability in the eelgrass meadows will
likely promote growth. Previous research has primarily focused on the reduction of hydrodynamic
exposure as a synergistic benefit of co-restoring eelgrass and blue mussels (Petersen et al., 1997;
Bos and Van Katwijk, 2007; Gagnon et al., 2021). Meanwhile, the impact of mussel filtration on
improving water clarity and subsequent eelgrass growth in natural field settings remains understud-
ied. This project focuses on how mussel filtration can enhance light conditions in a downstream
eelgrass meadow. Light improvement through filtration will be the facilitative interaction of focus.

1.2
Lovns Bredning in Limfjorden
Limfjorden is located in the northern part of Jutland, Denmark, and extends ∼180 km from the
North Sea in the west to the Kattegat in the east.
Historically, Limfjorden was considered a
fjord, however, due to the opening of the fjord to the west, as a result of storm surges in the 19th
century, a more proper definition is now a shallow sound with coherent inlets, creeks, and broads
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2024). The polyhaline salinity gradient varies from ∼32‰ at the inlet from the
North Sea to ∼24‰ in the inner parts of the system (Dolmer, 2000), and the average depth is ∼4
m, with maximum depth of ∼28 m. With a coastline of more than 1000 km, a surface area of 1575
km2 and a catchment area of 7670 km2, Limfjorden is the biggest fjord system in Denmark (Dolmer,
2000). The catchment area makes up ∼18% of the land area in Denmark and is mainly dominated
by agriculture but also includes some industries and cities (Miljøstyrelsen, 2024). Limfjorden has a
total volume of ∼7.1 km3, and compared to ∼7.8 km3 annually flowing through, it has a relatively
short residence time of less than a year (Hofmeister et al., 2009). While the total nutrient input to
the Danish coastal waters has decreased considerably (Riemann et al., 2016), it is estimated that
75% of the nutrients added from the catchment area to Limfjorden originate from the surrounding
agriculture (Miljøstyrelsen, 2024). Limfjorden is considered a micro-tidal system, with maximum
variation in tides up to 0.2 m (Wiles et al., 2006).
The overall transport of water from west
to east is driven by winds and tides (Wiles et al., 2006; Riemann et al., 2016; Miljøstyrelsen,
2024), allowing cold, saline, and oxygenated water from the North Sea to feed into the system.
Along the way, freshwater is added from streams and outlets, explaining the declining salinity
gradient (Dolmer, 2000). In the deeper parts, and mainly in the summer months, Limfjorden
is stratified, especially upon large salt-rich inflows from the North Sea (Hofmeister et al., 2009).
Commercial fishing occurs in Limfjorden among other, for blue mussels, using bottom-contacting
gears (Miljøstyrelsen, 2024). In recent years, harvest from aquaculture systems has increasingly
contributed to blue mussel landings (Miljøstyrelsen, 2024).
Lovns Bredning is located in the southeastern part of Limfjorden (Fig. 1) and has a surface
area of ∼50 km2 and a maximum depth of ∼10 m (DOF, 2013). Lovns Bredning is a semi enclosed
water body, and the surrounding catchment area is heavily impacted by agriculture to an extent
where the Ministry of Environment has proposed to substitute agricultural lands with wetlands
in the north (Naturstyrelsen, 2022).
Although the yearly discharge of nutrients has declined,
the aquatic environment still suffers from seasonal severe anoxia (DCE, 2024). Recently, dead fish

sulfide, indicating complete oxygen depletion at the bottom (DCE, 2024). Eelgrass is the most

Framework Directive, Lovns Bredning suffers from poor ecological status (Miljøstyrelsen, 2024).
These conditions indicate that urgent and comprehensive measures must be taken to restore the
ecosystems in Lovns Bredning and Limfjorden.

1.3
Eelgrass
The cosmopolitan eelgrass (Zostera marina) is recognized as both an ecosystem engineering species
(Gagnon et al., 2021) and a foundation species (Van Katwijk et al., 2016). It is known to provide
a wide range of ecosystem services. Being a very productive primary producer, eelgrass has a
high uptake of carbon and nutrients (Riemann et al., 2016; Lange et al., 2022).
The carbon
uptake combats both the effects of climate change and the emerging acidification of the oceans
(Pajusalu et al., 2023). The nutrient uptake reduces the overall nutrient turnover by incorporating
them into more slowly degradable plant tissue (Lange et al., 2022). The latter enhances the light
conditions in the eelgrass bed, as fewer nutrients are available for opportunistic algal species (Lange
et al., 2022). The eelgrass rhizosphere stabilizes the sediment, thereby limiting sediment mobility
and resuspension (Riemann et al., 2016, and references therein), and contributing to coastline
protection (Pajusalu et al., 2023) and water clarity enhancement (Carstensen et al., 2013; DSC,
2024). The wave action is dampened by the leaf canopy, which promotes sedimentation of particles
and burial of organic matter (Lange et al., 2022). The sedimentation of particles enhances the
light availability within an eelgrass meadow (Centre for Science Advice, 2009; Orth et al., 2020),
which further accelerates its own thriving through positive feedback effects (Riemann et al., 2016).
Eelgrass meadows are known to be biodiversity hotspots, as their mosaic structure works as a
habitat and shelter for a range of fish and invertebrates (Lange et al., 2022).
The extent of eelgrass meadows is globally declining (Van Katwijk et al., 2016). Numerous
estimates exist regarding the extent of eelgrass loss and its current distribution.
However, a
common consensus among these estimates is that significant losses of eelgrass have occurred. Lange
et al. (2022, and references therein) report estimated eelgrass loss of 80-90% in Denmark since the
year 1900 and Stæhr et al. (2019) estimate that approximately a third of the historical eelgrass
coverage is currently suitable for eelgrass. Additionally, the depth limit of eelgrass is, in most
places, down to a few meters, whereas historical data indicate depth limits down to 10 m in fjords
and 11 m in the inner Danish waters (Krause-Jensen and Rasmussen, 2009). The reason for this
major decline is, historically, a combination of eutrophication and wasting disease (Krause-Jensen
and Rasmussen, 2009). In modern times, marine heatwaves and storms are becoming increasingly
important (Pajusalu et al., 2023).

1.4
Blue mussels
Blue mussels (Mytilus edulis, hereafter referred to as blue mussels or mussels), also known as com-
mon mussels, are boreo-temperate organisms, which means they live in both boreal and temperate
climates.
Blue mussels are also known as ecosystem engineers (Wiles et al., 2006).
They fall
within the umbrella term invertebrate, but more accurately, they are marine bivalve mollusc. Blue
mussels are filter feeders, and they use their large ciliated gills to feed on mainly phytoplankton
(Beyer et al., 2017). Blue mussels are semi-sessile, as they are attached to hard surfaces with the
ability to detach to reposition themselves according to their ecological preferences (Capelle et al.,
2023). Blue mussels are a highly relevant species to humans both for their role as seafood and for
their role as environmental sentinel species, which are used to monitor aquatic pollution (Beyer
et al., 2017). Moreover, blue mussels are of crucial ecological importance as they provide habitat
for other marine species, supporting high levels of biodiversity, stabilizing the sediment and serves
as food for higher trophic levels (Beyer et al., 2017). In the absence of blue mussels, particles can
remain suspended in the water column for longer periods of time, negatively affecting the light
conditions (Riemann et al., 2016). Blue mussels are known to cause depletion of phytoplankton
in the overlying water layer due to their strong ability to filter the water (Nielsen and Vismann,
2014). Under ideal conditions, blue mussel beds have been reported to filtrate as much as 170
m3 m−2 d−1 (Saurel et al., 2013).
The growth of blue mussels is closely linked to food availability, which depends on hydrody-
namic processes such as current velocity and turbulence (Nielsen and Vismann, 2014). It is also
linked to oxygen levels, temperature, and salinity. The growth of blue mussels is positively corre-
lated to oxygen levels, negatively correlated to temperature, and positively correlated to salinity.
In general, sensitivity to these variables depends on the availability of food (Almada-Villela, 1984;
Kamermans et al., 2022). Oxygen content has been found to be the main factor affecting the
clearance rate of blue mussels (Kamermans et al., 2022).
The filtration rate of blue mussels depends on, among other factors, food quality and quantity
(Steeves et al., 2022; Liénart et al., 2023). As a rule of thumb, low food concentrations give a higher
filtration rate, while high food concentrations give a lower filtration rate (Sprung, 1984). Food
retention efficiency is a measure of the fraction of a given size of food retained in the blue mussel
after a single passage of water across the gills. The highest food retention efficiency is found in
particles ranging from 3.5 µm (Sprung, 1984) to 11 µm (Marion et al., 2022), with some reporting

values up to 35 µm (Strohmeier et al., 2012). In this size range, given optimal food quality, the
clearance rate can approximate the filtration rate (Lüskow et al., 2018). Blue mussels are exposed
to particles of nutritious and non-nutritious value during filtration (Rosa et al., 2015). The filtered
particles are sorted based on parameters like size, shape, surface charge, and carbohydrate content.
The unwanted particles are rejected in pseudo-feces (Rosa et al., 2015).
1.5
Hypotheses
This project originates from the idea that the presence of blue mussels, due to their filtration, can
potentially increase the success rate of eelgrass restoration efforts. Based on this thought, it has
been hypothesized that there is a synergistic relationship between the two habitat types: eelgrass
meadows and blue mussel beds. It has further been hypothesized that blue mussels can increase
the water clarity within eelgrass located downstream of the mussel bed. A graphical representation
of these hypotheses is presented (Fig. 2).

Methodology
The following sections contain an overall introduction to the data gathered in the northern and
southern part of Lovns Bredning, specifically the acoustic Doppler current profiler (ADCP) and the
photosynthetically active radiation (PAR). I present a brief walkthrough of assumptions, caveats,
and clarifications relevant for understanding the report. Then, the statistics provided in the results
are described and justified. Hereafter, the concept of tracking parcels of water and the reasoning
behind shifting the measurements are explained. Lastly, an introduction to the three types of
habitats: blue mussels, eelgrass, and mixed is provided.
2.1
Introduction to data
The two study sites were chosen based on previous UAV surveys (unmanned aerial vehicle). The
data were collected in Lovns Bredning, Limfjorden, at two different locations during the growth
season of 2023 (May-September). One of the locations was in the northern part of Lovns Bredning,
where blue mussel beds and eelgrass meadows were easily distinguished, and the other was in the
southern part, where areas of mixed blue mussel beds and eelgrass meadows dominated. The
data were gathered by numerous PAR-loggers, ADCP-stations, orthophotos, and ground truthing
photos. Chlorophyll a data from an Aquatroll 600 sonde, which was deployed in both north and
south, were also provided. Chlorophyll a data were incomplete and uncalibrated, thus only used
to provide a fraction of the overall seasonal pattern. The sensors deployed in Lovns Bredning were
occasionally wiped off to remove anything interfering with the measurements. Fieldwork and data
collection were performed by scientific employees from the Danish Shellfish Center. Subsequent
data treatment and quality assurance were performed as part of this project.

2.1.2
Acoustic Doppler current profiler
An instrument called acoustic Doppler current profiler (ADCP) was deployed at two stations, one in
the north and one in the south. An ADCP uses the Doppler effect to measure the sound waves that
are scattered back from particles in the water, translating these into measures of current velocities
and directions (NOAA, 2024). The northern ADCP was a Teledyne Sentinel II Workhorse 600
kHz and the southern ADCP was a Nortek Signature 1000. Measurements from the two stations
were logged every five minutes as an average of continuous readings taken throughout each five-
minute period from May to September. Water current velocity and the water current direction
were calculated from easting and northing velocities. The northern ADCP was positioned in a
frame pointing upwards with the transponder face ∼0.75 m above the seafloor (∼2.5 m depth).
The southern ADCP was positioned on a mooring pointing downwards to the seafloor and the
transponder face was ∼0.25 m below the water surface (∼2 m depth).
The mean current direction was estimated using a rolling window function (Fig. 5). This
smoothed the data, imputing less weight to sudden extreme variations in the current direction. A
window size of six time steps (30 minutes) was chosen. A sensitivity analysis of different window
sizes was carried out, and the smoothed data were visually inspected, however, smaller windows
included much noise, while larger ones smoothed out hourly and daily variations. In order to
calculate the mean current direction, the measurements were divided into north-south and east-
west components, using the following equation:
CoordNS = mean(cos(Drad)) · v
and
CoordEW = mean(sin(Drad)) · v
(1)
where CoordNS was the north-south component [ms−1], Drad was the direction [rad], v was velocity
[ms−1] and CoordEW was the east-west component [ms−1]. These components could then be used
to calculate the mean current direction as follows:
Dmean, rad = arctangent(CoordEW, CoordNS)
(2)
where Dmean, rad was the mean current direction [rad], which could then be converted back to
degrees. After the calculation, the mean current direction was shifted up to align the data with
the time of measurements (Fig. 5). The number of measurements in each current direction and
the mean current velocity from the northern and southern station is shown as polar plots (Fig. 6
and 7). Upon calculating the mean current direction, the measurements were divided into north
(315° - 45°), east (45° - 135°), south (135° - 225°) and west (225° - 315°).

2.1.3
Photosynthetically active radiation and light attenuation coefficient
Photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) refers to the waveband of solar radiation that photo-
synthetic organisms can utilize for photosynthesis. PAR ranges from 400-700 nm and corresponds
roughly to the visible light spectrum (Lund-Hansen, 2024). A total of 34 stations were selected
for continuous measurements of PAR from the beginning of May to around mid-September with
Odyssey Xtreme PAR loggers fixed to rigid frames. 24 of the stations were distributed in the
southern part of Lovns Bredning, while the remaining 10 were distributed in the northern part.
At each station, one sensor was deployed, located 0.3 m above the seabed. Two stations had
sensors located at the surface: one in the north (station 22) and one in the south (station 23)
and they served as reference sensors. Along with depth measurements of the sensors, the PAR
values were used to calculate the direct light attenuation coefficient for photosynthetically active
radiation Kd(PAR) (Quartino et al., 2013). Kd(PAR) is defined by the amount of PAR penetrat-
ing the water surface which is attenuated vertically within a body of water, essentially a measure
of transparency (Quartino et al., 2013). The PAR-sensors were single planar (2π) light sensors
mounted on a ridged structure and pointing upwards to avoid reflection from bottom sediment and

to the diffuse light attenuation coefficient, which refers to Kd(PAR) calculated based on spherical
(4π) light sensors (Carruthers et al., 2001). The PAR measurements were logged every five minutes
as an average of continuous readings taken throughout each five-minute period during the whole
time. All the loggers were coated with antifouling paint and calibrated before deployment. For
stations 6, 9, 21, and 25 in the south, and stations 30 and 32 in the north, the PAR values at some

2.2
Assumptions, caveats, and clarifying remarks
The following bullet points sum up the key assumptions that apply in this project, as well as some
clarifying remarks. This is done to improve readability and avoid misperceptions.
• The water current data used in this project were based on measurements from one station in
the northern part of Lovns Bredning and one station in the southern part of Lovns Bredning.
The water current data were extrapolated and assumed to apply for the whole respective area
of the stations.
• In the northern part of Lovns Bredning, belts of eelgrass and blue mussels were located
parallel to the shoreline. It was assumed that these belts continued beyond what had been
mapped towards both east and west, parallel to the shoreline.
• In this report, north is not the geographic north, but rather shifted 20° clockwise. By doing
so, the new north was now parallel to the transects of the relevant stations in the north
(station 27, 28, 29, and 30, as well as station 34, 33, 32, and 31), and perpendicular to the
habitats. This enabled the tracking of water parcels across the habitats.
• Northern or northward currents implied that the water was flowing towards the north.
• The water current data were measured by ADCPs located at certain depths. It was assumed
that the currents measured at those depths applied to the rest of the water columns.
• The habitat under a certain station was ascribed to it by the dominating habitat within a
radius of 10 m from the station. This was partly due to GPS uncertainty of 5-8 m and partly
due to the fact that the effect of the habitat often can be seen beyond the edge of the habitat
(see e.g. Saurel et al. (2013)). A mussel station from now on refers to a station located in a
blue mussel bed, and an eelgrass station located in an eelgrass meadow.
• Effects and characteristics of habitats likely depend on the age and density of individuals,
e.g., eelgrass reaches its optimal ecological functionality years after being transplanted or
restored, depending on factors like shoot density (Timmermann, 2024). In this project, the
habitats were only assessed based on the species present.

2.3
Statistical analyses and software
The difference in light attenuation coefficient, Kd(PAR), among habitats was assessed using a
Kruskal-Wallis test, as the raw data were not normally distributed. The normality was inves-
tigated by applying a Kolmogorov-Smirnov test, which is suitable for large data sets. Various
transformations such as square root, log, and power were applied to the raw Kd(PAR) data, but
none of them yielded a normal distribution. Upon applying the Kruskal-Wallis test, the post hoc
Dunn test was used to compare between groups. Each station was assigned a habitat type, which
was the only explanatory variable included.
When analyzing the data in certain periods of northern and southern currents, a Shapiro-Wilk
test was used to assess normality within the stations during certain current regimes. Hereafter,
homogeneity of variance was tested using a Levene’s test. Where the assumptions of homogeneity
of variance and normality were met, a Student’s t-test was applied. Where the assumption of
normality was met but the homogeneity of variance was violated, a Welch’s t-test was used instead.
Where the assumption of normality was violated, but homogeneity of variance was met, a Wilcoxon
Rank-Sum test (also known as Mann-Whitney U Test) was used instead. Due to the comparison
of multiple independent tests, the significance level was adjusted using Bonferroni correction.
To investigate the temporal variation in Kd(PAR) in the northern part of Lovns Bredning,
generalized linear mixed models (GLMMs) were constructed assuming a log-normal distribution in
Kd(PAR). Models using different distributions were compared, and the log-normal model demon-
strated a better fit to the data and was therefore chosen. The log-normal assumption was visually
checked with a distribution plot showing a clear right-skewed tail. Temporal chlorophyll a data
from the study site were incomplete and uncalibrated (Fig. 10), so time measured as days from
May to September were used as a proxy for temporal changes in chlorophyll a. Chlorophyll a tends
to fluctuate in Lovns Bredning, but with a general increasing trend from spring to fall, which is
characteristic for the highly eutrophic inner parts of Limfjorden (Markager et al., 2006). Kd(PAR)
was modeled as the response variable in all models. The baseline model, null hypothesis, had
no predictor variables included, only the random effects and was denoted model 0. There were
two alternative hypothesis models, model 1 with time as the predictor variable, and model 2 with
16
==PAGE:23==
Master’s thesis
February 2025
time and current velocity as the predictor variables. This was done to test whether Kd(PAR)
was influenced by time and resuspended sediments. Since the models were nested with identical
random effects and temporal patterns, a likelihood ratio test was feasible. The Akaike Information
Criterion (AIC) was used for model comparison. A parameter penalty was included in the AIC,
preventing excessive model complexity. Sampling stations in the north were modeled as a random
effect, with both varying intercepts and slopes of Kd(PAR) against time for each station. This
was done to account for unequal sampling across the stations due to some of the sensors breaking.
Temporal autocorrelation was addressed by incorporating a random effect covariance structure
called Ornstein–Uhlenbeck. Daily averages of Kd(PAR) was used in the analysis instead of the
five-minutes time steps, due to computational limitations. Upon choosing the best model, the
residuals were evaluated visually.
The significance of the trends in the time series were tested by comparing the slopes with a
slope of 0, using the in-build linear regression function. P-values were translated to measures of
evidence, ranging from very strong evidence (p-value < 0.001) to little or no evidence (p-value >
0.1), according to Muff et al. (2022). Statistical analysis and data visualization were performed in
RStudio, Version 4.4.2. Other graphical representations were created in BioRender and Microsoft
Excel.
2.4
The concept of tracking parcels of water
This section explains the idea behind following parcels of water during specific current regimes
(Fig. 8). The concept is that the improved water clarity from the mussel filtration at a station can
be "carried" with the currents and measured at another station some time later. When a parcel of
water was at station 27 at a given time, that same parcel of water would be at station 28 after e.g.,
30 min and so on. To account for this, the measurements at each station was shifted in time. This
was done at two transects, namely stations 27, 28, 29, and 30, as well as stations 34, 33, 32, and
31. These transects (Fig. 15 and 3 for an overview of the stations) were given the most attention
because of three things: 1) the stations could clearly be distinguished from each other based on
their habitats, 2) the habitats were clearly separated from one another, and 3) the experimental
setup enabled a transect perpendicular to the belts of blue mussels and eelgrass.

3.1
Chlorophyll a and current velocity as temporal drivers of light at-
tenuation
The chlorophyll a measured in the northern part of Lovns Bredning is plotted as a function of
time (Fig. 10). The chlorophyll a data were incomplete and uncalibrated, so the raw fluorescence
values from the sonde were scaled to a local kernel interpolation of NOVANA records for corrected
chlorophyll a fluorescence values. Fluctuations were seen, as well as a phytoplankton peak during
the end of August. It is known that Lovns Bredning and other inner parts of Limfjorden have a
different yearly chlorophyll a pattern than the remaining parts. In temperate climate, chlorophyll
a normally peaks during spring bloom, is reduced during the summer and experiences a smaller
bloom during fall (Markager et al., 2006). However, Lovns Bredning experiences a small spring
bloom and increases continuously towards fall (Markager et al., 2006). This pattern is likely driven
by internal loading of nutrients due to anoxic conditions during summer. As the fall bloom was
more severe than the spring bloom (Fig. 10), the data in this project suggested that the pattern
in chlorophyll a still applied for Lovns Bredning, although this could not be fully confirmed due
to incomplete data.

4
Discussion
This project hypothesized that blue mussel beds and eelgrass meadows provided synergistic benefits
to each other. It suggested that the filtration capabilities of the mussels improved light conditions
for eelgrass growing downstream of the blue mussel bed.
My main findings point to the fact
that light conditions above mussel beds were, with very strong evidence, better than those above
eelgrass meadows. Additionally, given the optimal current direction, the increased light availability,
likely due to the filtration by blue mussels, could be seen in the eelgrass meadows downstream
of the mussels. These findings subsidize my hypotheses of a beneficial relationship between blue
mussel beds and eelgrass meadows, and demonstrate the effect of the mussel filtration on the light
conditions downstream of the blue mussel bed.
4.1
Spatial and temporal drivers of light attenuation
In order for a certain species to settle in an area, a range of optimal environmental conditions must
be met. Once the species is established, given is has ecosystem engineering abilities, it will start
to alter the surrounding environment, thereby creating its habitat. A habitat is a dynamic entity,
where the environmental conditions are an inherent part of that habitat and vice versa. Both
eelgrass and blue mussels are described as ecosystem engineers (Wiles et al., 2006; Van Katwijk
et al., 2016; Gagnon et al., 2021). For eelgrass, light is a necessary habitat requirement and often
limits growth, especially in eutrophic waters (Krause-Jensen et al., 2011), however, this is not
the case for blue mussels directly (Nielsen and Strömgren, 1985). Therefore, blue mussels often
dominate deeper waters relative to eelgrass (Bos and Van Katwijk, 2007), which was also the case
in the northern part of Lovns Bredning. Hence, parameters related to depth, e.g., light, current,
salinity, and temperature, becomes important factors to consider when comparing the two habitats.
The results of this project indicated with very strong evidence that the light attenuation
coefficient, Kd(PAR), varied with habitat type, where lower Kd(PAR) was found above blue mussels
compared to eelgrass (Fig. 14 and 15). Blue mussels are known to remove vast amounts of organic
particles from the water through filter feeding, which improves the light conditions (Dolmer, 2000;
Riisgaard et al., 2004; Wall et al., 2008; Nielsen and Vismann, 2014), and so is eelgrass via sediment
stabilization and increased sedimentation of particles due to damping of waves (Centre for Science
Advice, 2009; Orth et al., 2020). Blue mussels generally prefer particles of size ∼3.5 µm up to ∼11
36
==PAGE:43==
Master’s thesis
February 2025
µm (Sprung, 1984; Riisgaard, 1988; Marion et al., 2022), whereas eelgrass cause sedimentation of
relatively larger particles (>10 µm) (Granata et al., 2001). In this case blue mussels had the biggest
impact on the spatial variation in light conditions, and they were therefore suggested as the main
explanation for the differences in light conditions between habitats. The removal of particles is
associated with reduced water turbidity, benefiting near-bottom phototropic organisms like eelgrass
(Wall et al., 2008; Gagnon et al., 2021). Additionally, the results of the GLMMs showed that the
temporal variation in Kd(PAR) was mainly driven by temporal variation in chlorophyll a, which
further underscores the important role of blue mussels.
It has not been possible to statistically separate the effect of habitat and depth-related pa-
rameters on Kd(PAR) from each other. In shallow waters, sediment resuspension often dominate
the water column dynamics due to waves (Paladini de Mendoza et al., 2018) and currents (Canal-
Vergés et al., 2010). In deeper waters, sediment resuspension is likely restricted to impact the
bottom of the water column and mainly because of currents. Inorganic particles from sediment re-
suspension affect Kd(PAR) (Markager, 2004; Wen et al., 2018), hence depth becomes important to
consider. The low range of measurements of current velocity (Fig. 12), likely resulting in low levels
of resuspension, indicates that depth is not the main driver for differences in Kd(PAR) between
habitats. This is backed up by the constructed GLMMs (Tab. 1), which indicated that including
current velocity did not improve the models explanatory power regarding the temporal variation
in Kd(PAR). This is also supported by Olesen (1996), who found a more evident relationship
between chlorophyll a and Secchi depth compared to chlorophyll a and suspended inorganic parti-
cles in Lovns Bredning. Current velocity is known to affect resuspension (Olesen, 1996), and thus
Kd(PAR), however this is mainly prominent at current velocities higher than the ones measured
here (Canal-Vergés et al., 2010). The mean current velocity measured in the northern part of
Lovns Bredning was 0.084 ms−1, with 80% of the observations between 0.024 ms−1 to 0.157 ms−1.
In agreement with these results, Wiles et al. (2006) measured relatively weak current regimes in
Limfjorden, on average 0.1 ms−1 similar to the ones in this report (Fig. 12), and concluded that
current velocity alone could not explain any significant near-bed turbulence. Canal-Vergés et al.
(2010) found that depending on the absence of drifting macrophytes, resuspension occurred at
critical shear stress current velocities between 0.146 ms−1 and 0.27 ms−1, which was higher than
the velocities measured in Lovns Bredning, further indicating that Kd(PAR) was not driven by
currents.

In contrast to this, the higher Kd(PAR) observed above eelgrass beds could in fact stem from
shallow water sediment resuspension (Paladini de Mendoza et al., 2018). Paladini de Mendoza
et al. (2018) suggest that eelgrass located in shallow waters experiences a higher frequency of
resuspension events as a result of the ’monami’ effect, which is the synchronous waving of seagrass
canopies, possibly explaining the increased Kd(PAR) observed in the eelgrass meadow. Brito et al.
(2013) found that shallow water regions tend to have a higher Kd(PAR) due to increase terrestrial
runoff and resuspension compared to deeper waters (Brito et al., 2013). Nevertheless, given the
relatively low range of current velocities, it is implausible that the observed difference in Kd(PAR)
between habitats was driven by depth differences; rather, it was likely due to filtration by the
mussels.
Kd(PAR) values are not static and can change seasonally (Fig. 11), annually (Lund-Hansen,
2024) or with varying environmental conditions, e.g., terrestrial runoff (Bos and Van Katwijk, 2007;
Stæhr and Borum, 2011). In the literature, Kd(PAR) varies from 0.3 m−1 to 2.5 m−1, occasionally
up to 4 m−1, depending on the type of marine environment, i.e., open waters, coastal waters,
estuaries etc. (Domingues and Barbosa, 2023). Stæhr and Borum (2011) reported maximum and
minimum values between 0.1 m−1 and 2.2 m−1, respectively, as a normal range of conditions in
August in Danish estuaries. The Kd(PAR) calculated in this project corresponded roughly to these
values (Fig. 11). For comparison, some freshwater lakes measures Kd(PAR) values up to 15 m−1,
primarily explained by the high turbidity due to organic content (Shi et al., 2014).
4.2
Blue mussels as a facilitating tool
In this study, the light conditions in eelgrass located downstream of mussels were improved with up
to 104%-points relative to upstream (Fig. 17 and 19). Marine bivalve molluscs have been argued to
potentially facilitate eelgrass survival by several studies (Sharma et al., 2016; Gagnon et al., 2020;
Pajusalu et al., 2023; Petersen et al., 2024b). Facilitative interactions have also been directly shown
in both laboratory experiments and in the field (Bos and Van Katwijk, 2007; Wall et al., 2008;
Gagnon et al., 2021). Gagnon et al. (2021) demonstrated an increase in eelgrass growth, likely due
to biodeposition, when placed alongside blue mussels during laboratory experiments, but failed
to prove it during field experiments due to unfavorable hydrodynamic conditions. Biodeposition
is not a facilitative interaction in this case, partly due to the segregated nature of the habitats
at the northern study site and partly because addition of nutrients in eutrophic systems like
38
==PAGE:45==
Master’s thesis
February 2025
Limfjorden is needless (Castorani et al., 2015). Biodeposition by blue mussels can in fact have
local negative consequences in eutrophic systems due to increased production of sulfides (Castorani
et al., 2015), which can be relevant in cases where habitats are mixed like the southern part of
Lovns Bredning. Additionally, Bos and Van Katwijk (2007) showed an increase in the survival of
actively transplanted eelgrass shoots, located within a blue mussel bed relative to those located
outside, suggested to be explained by a reduction in hydrodynamic exposure. In the northern
part of the study site, the habitats were likely too far apart for the blue mussels to reduce the
hydrodynamic exposure exerted on the eelgrass. Wall et al. (2008) showed an increase in eelgrass
growth related to enhanced light availability from blue mussel filtration in a mesocosm experiment,
indicating that eelgrass growth can indeed increase in the presence of blue mussels. Increased light
availability in the eelgrass meadow was similarly observed in this project (Fig. 16 and 18), however
it was not coupled with increased eelgrass growth.
Contradicting the findings by Bos and Van Katwijk (2007), Wall et al. (2008) and Gagnon
et al. (2020), other studies have shown negative interactions between blue mussels and eelgrass.
During laboratory experiments, Vinther and Holmer (2008) found reduced growth in eelgrass due
to increased biodeposition below blue mussels, which enhanced the reduction of sulfates to toxic
sulfide compounds in the porewater (Castorani et al., 2015). They also showed an increase in
epiphyte growth on the eelgrass leaves in a laboratory setup, presumably nourished by blue mussel
excretion, resulting in a 20% reduction in eelgrass growth (Vinther and Holmer, 2008).
The
laboratory results from Vinther and Holmer (2008) translated into a subsequent field study on wild
eelgrass meadows and blue mussel beds in Flensborg fjord, Denmark, where previous findings were
confirmed (Vinther et al., 2008). Moreover, another study found a negative correlation between
epiphyte biomass and eelgrass photosynthesis, measured on freshly harvested eelgrass, with up to
a 49% reduction in eelgrass photosynthesis (Drake et al., 2003). Epiphytes affect the growth of
eelgrass, and underpins the potential negative effects from the fertilization of the porewater by
blue mussel excretion. In addition to increased biomass of epiphytes and concentration of sulfides,
dense beds of blue mussels can inhibit the growth of eelgrass likely due to spatial competition
(Allen and Williams, 2003). In the northern part of Lovns Bredning, spatial competition was not
a crucial factor when assessing interactions between blue mussels and eelgrass, mainly due to the
segregated nature of their habitats (Fig. 3b). However, these interactions would likely be highly
relevant if the mixed habitats in the southern part of Lovns Bredning were further investigated.

When assessing the possibility of co-restoring blue mussels and eelgrass, it is important to in-
clude other factors than their direct interactions with each other. Co-restoring efforts by Gagnon
et al. (2021) and Pajusalu et al. (2023) were both terminated due to unfavorable hydrodynamic
conditions. Hydrodynamic exposure is described as one of the paramount factors for most restora-
tion attempts, often of much greater importance than biotic factors (Bos and Van Katwijk, 2007;
Leschen et al., 2010; Pajusalu et al., 2023). The presence of, e.g., blue mussels is therefore consid-
ered irrelevant in the cases where the threshold for hydrodynamic exposure is exceeded (Bos and
Van Katwijk, 2007; Gagnon et al., 2021; Pajusalu et al., 2023). A study from Limfjorden suggests
that blue mussels have a lower effect on water column parameters in low energy systems than pre-
viously thought (Wiles et al., 2006). The main explanation for this is low flow velocities causing
limited turbulence and thus limited alteration of the environment (Wiles et al., 2006), highlighting
site-dependent variation in co-restoration efforts. Sediment characteristics and the scale of the
co-restoration initiative should also be considered. Eelgrass transplants are sensitive to bottom
substrates, with sandy sediment more suitable than muddy ones (Petersen et al., 2024b). Addi-
tionally, a global analysis found that transplanting many seagrass shoots increased the restoration
success due to increased resilience and due to the positive feedback occurring within larger seagrass
beds, i.e., lowered hydrodynamic exposure and spread of seeds (Van Katwijk et al., 2016).
In order to assess whether the filtration by blue mussels is sufficient to improve light conditions
for downstream eelgrass to a degree that affects its growth, several parameters must be considered.
First, the growth of eelgrass must be limited by light, which is the case given the amount of
light present at the study site and the light saturation point for eelgrass (200 µmol m−2 s−1 vs
450 µmol m−2 s−1) (Kuusemäe et al., 2016).
Second, the filtration efforts by the blue mussels
should be adequate. Samples of blue mussels were taken from both study sites in Lovns Bredning
in 0.25 m2 quadrants.
The mussels were counted and measured manually.
Filtration volume,
both individually and area-based, could be estimated from the simple correlation with its length
according to Riisgård et al. (2014). The filtration volume depends on food quantity and quality,
which depends on currents and hydrodynamic conditions (Nielsen and Vismann, 2014; Steeves
et al., 2022; Liénart et al., 2023). The filtration volume alone is not enough to assess if the light
conditions could be improved sufficiently to affect eelgrass growth. Mature beds of blue mussels can
filtrate tremendous amounts of water, and big impacts on the light conditions have been reported
(Dolmer, 2000; Riisgaard et al., 2004; Nielsen and Vismann, 2014; Gagnon et al., 2021). Direct
40
==PAGE:47==
Master’s thesis
February 2025
effects of mussel filtration on eelgrass growth has been observed in the laboratory (Wall et al.,
2008), however not in the field. Third, the filtered water must reach the eelgrass meadow in order
to obtain an effect. From the results of this study (Fig. 16 and 18), it seems to be the case.
Fourth and last, water current direction should be oriented in a way allowing the filtered water to
reach the eelgrass in periods long enough to have an effect. Based on the data from the northern
part of Lovns Bredning, the water current direction was rarely oriented in the desired way and
for sufficient consecutive time (Fig. 6), which indicates that the effect on the eelgrass growth is
likely restricted. In summary, mussel filtration has the potential to benefit eelgrass growth through
enhanced light conditions, however the effect in this case is likely limited due to the orientation of
the habitats and current direction. Nevertheless, in cases where the habitats are located optimally
in relation to the currents, mussel filtration can be an important factor to consider during active
restoration of eelgrass.
4.3
Experimental limitations and considerations
Kd(PAR) was calculated based on measurements of PAR, and used as the main expression for
light. When comparing values of Kd(PAR) across scientific research, one should pay attention
to the type of sensor used, whether it is a flat sensor (2π) or a spherical sensor (4π), since flat
sensors tends to underestimate the light availability (Carruthers et al., 2001) and round sensors
tends to be affected by bottom reflection (Brito et al., 2013). In addition, it is important to note
whether Kd(PAR) is calculated based on the natural logarithm or the common base-10 logarithm
(Carruthers et al., 2001), and whether Kd(PAR) is measured in a water column with or without
stratification (Markager, 2004).
Moreover, inaccurate values of Kd(PAR) arise from low solar
angles relative to when the sun is at its zenith.
An oblique solar angle yields a longer path
through the water for the light to reach a certain depth, mostly pronounced in clear waters,
resulting in an overestimation of Kd(PAR) (Carruthers et al., 2001). A common solution is to
limit measurements to two hours after sunrise and two hours before sunset, to rule out the most
oblique angles (Carruthers et al., 2001). Nevertheless, Kd(PAR) is still used as a universal measure
of the light environment, likely because it is relatively easy to obtain and because it is after all
comparable across scientific research.
Domingues and Barbosa (2023) point out that Kd(PAR) might be an inadequate measure of
light availability in water quality studies, and suggest light intensity in the mixed layer (Im) as a
41
==PAGE:48==
Master’s thesis
February 2025
more appropriate one. The main argument is that Kd(PAR) overlooks potentially stratified layers.
In the case of stratification, Markager (2004) suggest only to include measurements above the
pycnocline to obtain a more robust estimate of Kd(PAR). When the stratified layer is broken down,
the following estimations of Kd(PAR) will be elevated due to the reintroduction of nutrient rich
water in the sunlit surface (Rose, 2023). The deeper part of Limfjorden is periodically stratified,
which potentially challenges the usage of Kd(PAR) in this study (Wiles et al., 2006; Hofmeister
et al., 2009). Furthermore, stratification might also affect the water currents, where a deeper layer
could have distinct characteristics compared to the upper layer (Wiles et al., 2006). The deepest
sampling stations in this project were located in depths down to ∼3 m, where stratification is
likely not as strong and frequent as in the deeper areas. The absence of a clearly stratified layer
precludes the use of Im as a light parameter. In summary, the effect of stratification on Kd(PAR)
and water currents is likely limited, and thus Kd(PAR) is still the preferred measure of light.
A potential uncertainty occurs when spatially extrapolating the ADCP results. This was, how-
ever, likely not a big error due to the fact that Lovns Bredning is dominated by a counter-clockwise
estuarine circulation (Fig. 6). This implies that the currents in a subsection of Lovns Bredning,
such as the investigated northern or southern part, are of similar magnitude and direction, making
the extrapolation of the ADCP results a fair assumption.
In addition, the maneuver where the current direction was averaged to reduce the impact of
sudden extreme variations had the potential to mischaracterize the observed current conditions.
A small window size could be overly sensitive to short-term fluctuations in the data, whereas
a large window size could smooth out potentially important short-term features. Moreover, high
measurements of current velocity in periods shorter than the window size could be underestimated,
and vice versa with low measurements in short periods. However, when visually inspecting the
original data compared to the averaged data, the differences were minor. Lastly, the calculated
mean direction became a more robust measure when tracking water parcels through habitats, as
the current had to be in a certain direction over several consecutive time steps to be categorized
with that direction. Therefore, the potential error was estimated to be insignificant.
The reference sensor in the water surface often has considerable variation compared to the
sensor deeper down. The main reason for this is wave motion, which causes the light to scatter
yielding large fluctuations in the measurements (Markager, 2004). Lastly, during the field experi-
ments of this project, environmentally induced errors occurred. Upon deploying the PAR-sensors,
42
==PAGE:49==
Master’s thesis
February 2025
drifting seaweed sticking to the sensors affected the measurements, likely causing the PAR values
to be underestimated. A group of very high Kd(PAR) measurements in mid/end May corresponded
to a day where the PAR loggers were very dirty before cleaned, and were therefore removed. Car-
ruthers et al. (2001) mention, besides drifting seaweed, the settling of fine particles and formation
of biofilm on the sensor as points of attention. The sensors in this project were occasionally wiped
off to remove anything interfering with the measurements.
4.4
Future perspective and research
Due to the strong counter-clockwise estuarine circulation in Lovns Bredning (Fig. 6), the cur-
rents were rarely perpendicular to the shoreline, which limited the number of measurements and
restricted the effect of mussel filtration on eelgrass. I suggest that similar studies are performed
in areas where currents allow for the tracking of water parcels across habitats in longer consecu-
tive periods. This would facilitate a more robust understanding of the interactions between these
habitats. It would require a preliminary field study, where the water current patterns are surveyed
in relation to the habitat layout before deploying the PAR sensors.
Another aspect for further investigation is the effect of depth-related parameters on the light
conditions in habitats. Blue mussel beds located in deeper waters could be compared to blue
mussels located in shallow waters to investigate the effect of depth. Alternatively, blue mussel
beds located in shallow waters could be compared to eelgrass meadows in equally shallow waters
to remove depth-related parameters as potential confounding factors. Blue mussel beds can be
located in very shallow waters, essentially 0 cm relative to mean sea level (Bos and Van Katwijk,
2007), which allows for such analysis to be performed. Non of the PAR stations in Lovns Bredning
were located in waters shallow enough for such analysis.

Conclusion
This project investigated the synergistic effects between eelgrass and blue mussels, with emphasis
on mussel filtration, and revealed three major findings. First, I provided very strong evidence
that the light attenuation coefficient, Kd(PAR), was lower above mussel beds compared to eelgrass
meadows and mixed habitats, likely stemming from the filtration by blue mussels. I further demon-
strated, that blue mussels were able to improve light conditions in eelgrass located downstream
of the mussel bed. However, the duration of optimal current direction was limited to only a few
consecutive time periods in the northern part of Lovns Bredning, which likely caused the effect
on eelgrass growth to be restricted. Lastly, through the setup of GLMMs, I showed that current
velocity was not the main driver of temporal variation in Kd(PAR) at the study site, but rather
time, used as a proxy for the seasonal pattern in chlorophyll a. These results provide evidence
that eelgrass meadows and blue mussel beds can feature synergistic relationships, confirming my
hypothesis. With the potential to increase the success of eelgrass restoration efforts, these findings
offer crucial knowledge for future eelgrass restoration initiatives. This project will hopefully entail
further research on co-restoration and facilitative interactions as restoration tools.
