   Abstract 
 
This study addresses the growing need for environmentally friendly power generation 
technologies, particularly considering new sustainability requirements to be introduced in 
the Danish Building Code in 2023, which aim to reduce embedded carbon in the building 
stock. These regulations, based on the EN 15978 standard, focus primarily on operating 
energy and overlook the benefits of surplus and exported energy, thus hindering the 
deployment of photovoltaic systems. The aim of this research is to carry out a 
comprehensive Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) of four different PV systems, all based on 
the same PERC module technology, but differing in system capacity and final installation 
in Denmark. Using the ISO 14040 and 14044 standards and the methodological guidelines 
of the International Energy Agency's Photovoltaic Power Systems Programme, the study 
collected data through a systematic process consisting of defining the objective and scope, 
life cycle inventory, life cycle assessment using the IPCC 2021 (GWP 100 years) method 
and interpretation. The analysis showed that the BAPV rooftop system has the highest 
GWP impact with ranges between (5.77E-02 - 3.65E-02) kg CO2-Eq/kWh, followed by 
the BIPV system (5.40E-02 - 3. 28E-02) kg CO2-Eq/kWh, then the FT system (4. 24E-02 
- 3.29E-02) kg CO2-Eq/kWh, while the HSAT system has the lowest impact with (9.95 
E-02 - 2.92E-02) kg CO2-Eq/kWh, when excluding the distribution networks & cablings 
from the assessment of the ground-mounted systems. Factors influencing these results 
include system capacity, manufacturing sites and transport of components. Extending the 
lifetime of PV systems reduces their GWP, while the choice of materials and 
manufacturing processes also play a crucial role in their environmental performance. This 
study highlights the importance of considering the whole life cycle of PV systems in 
regulatory frameworks and decision-making processes to improve their sustainability.

Motivation & Objective of the Study 
 
Motivation 
 The main motivations for carrying out this study are:                                                                                    
The national and regional energy policies require environmentally friendly electricity 
generating technologies,                                                                                                    
Since the beginning of 2023 year, sustainability requirements have been introduced into 
the Danish Building Regulations with the aim of reducing the embedded carbon in the 
building stock. The legislation follows the EN 15978 standard, which focuses only on the 
building’s operational energy, and neglects  the benefits of excess  produced energy as 
well as exported energy, so the lack of these incentives, and  the regulatory frameworks 
is hindering the progress in the deployment of PV systems, and by implementing a LCA 
of different PV systems can help to know the environmental profile of each system, the 
latest data on PV performance and LCIA of each of them.    
 
Objective 
In this study, the LCA of the three systems enables us to identify opportunities for 
improving the environmental performance of the systems during the various phases of 
their life cycle, and to help decision makers in the PV industry or governmental and non-
governmental organizations make critical choices by: -   
▪ Evaluate the climate change impact indicator of their entire life cycle by using IPCC 
2021 (GWP 100 year) mothed, 
▪ Quantify the differences in GWP between PV systems, and with previous studies. 
▪ Analysis of the results of the LCIA.

 Introduction  
Solar photovoltaic (PV) technology has been developed and established for several decades 
and is now considered as the most important means to solve carbon neutrality [1]. This 
remarkable growth has been attributed to the recognition of solar energy as a critical solution 
to meeting the world's growing demand for electricity while reducing greenhouse gas emissions 
[2] . The range of PV technologies currently available on the market is extensive and diverse. 
Of these, higher-efficiency PERC (Passivated Emitter Rear Cell) technology stands out, with 
the largest market share according to the International Technology Roadmap for Photovoltaics 
(ITRPV) 2023 report [3], see Figure (1) below

Due to this growth and the continuous advances in the production of photovoltaic system 
components, especially photovoltaic modules, it is essential to assess the environmental impact 
of these systems using updated information through a comprehensive Life Cycle Assessment 
(LCA). The observed variation in the results of Life Cycle Assessments (LCA) in photovoltaic 
(PV) systems has been attributed to several factors. These include differences in key 
parameters such as PV module technology, module efficiency, installation type, installation 
location, solar irradiation, performance ratio (PR), system lifetime and system boundaries. 
Variations in the impact contributions of different components have also been identified, with 
all studies consistently identifying the PV module as the largest contributor to the overall 
environmental impact of the PV system. Furthermore, the different LCIA methodologies used 
in these studies contributed to the variation in results, mainly due to the different 
characterization factors used [4]. Some of these studies are discussed in detail in the next 
subsection to illustrate the wide variation in the results of existing PV LCAs.  
 
Literature review 
The rapid growth of photovoltaic (PV) systems in recent years, driven by increasing political 
interest and the absence of comprehensive e regulations, has raised concerns about the 
potential increase in environmental impacts associated with their expansion, particularly in 
developing countries. With a large increase in installed capacity expected in the coming 
decades as the world shifts towards cleaner energy production [33], it is becoming increasingly 
important to assess the environmental and energy performance of PV technologies. In addition, 
==PAGE:12==
 
8 
 
continuous improvements in material and energy efficiency make it essential to provide up-to-
date assessments to guide long-term energy strategy decisions [34]. 
To avoid a shift from climate change and air pollution to other environmental issues, designers 
of electrical systems must adopt a life cycle perspective [35] Life cycle assessments (LCA) are 
essential for assessing the sustainability of PV systems by measuring impacts such as energy 
payback time (EPBT) and climate change indicators such as global warming potential (GWP). 
This cradle-to-grave approach, from raw material extraction to disposal, ensures a complete 
understanding of the environmental footprint. 
There has been a review of a variety of studies that have analyzed detailed cases of 
photovoltaic systems in broad regions of the world. 
Some of these studies are based on primary data based on actual measurements, in particular 
regarding the mounting structures, the size of the cabling system and also the distribution 
network of the systems, in order to obtain more realistic and meaningful results and thus a more 
accurate environmental assessment of the photovoltaic systems studied, while others rely on 
an outdated inventory of data related to the life cycle of the system components, which may 
lead to an overestimation of the environmental impact and primary energy consumption due to 
the rapid increase in PV module efficiencies [27] and technological development of system 
components. Here is an overview of some of these studies conducted over the last decade 
In their 2012 study, Umberto Desideri et al. [36] evaluated the performance of a 1778.48 kWp 
ground-mounted photovoltaic plant built by Terni Energia S.p.A. in Marsciano (Perugia, Italy). 
Using Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) methodology and a "cradle to grave" approach, they 
highlighted the importance of collecting detailed on-site data during the construction phase, 
which added value to their findings. The study reported an Energy Payback Time (EPBT) of 
4.17 years, an Energy Return on Energy Investment (EROEI) of 4.826, CO2 emissions of 
88.743 kg/kWh, and a Global Warming Potential (GWP100) of 0.106 kg CO2 eq/kWh. 
Similarly, Beylot et al. [37], in their 2014 study, focused on large-scale, grid-connected, ground-
mounted PV installations (5 MWp), analyzing one module technology (mc-Si) with different 
mounting structures. Their research assessed four support types fixed mounting (aluminum 
and wood), single-axis trackers, and dual-axis trackers. The results emphasized that the type 
and configuration of the mounting structure had a significant impact on both energy production 
and the environmental footprint, with climate change impact potential ranging from 37.5 to 53.5 
g CO2 eq/kWh, depending on the scenario.  
Expanding on these assessments, Yu and Halog et al. [38], 2015 studied the environmental 
impacts of a 1.2 MW flat-roof PV installation at the University of Queensland, Australia. The 
study, however, excluded foreground data, which reduced the precision of the LCA results. 
==PAGE:13==
 
9 
 
With solar irradiation levels of 1,967 kWh/m²/year, a performance ratio (PR) of 75%, and a 
system lifetime of 30 years, the global warming impact was recorded at 6.94E−2 kg CO2 
eq/kWh, while the energy payback time (EPBT) was 2.33 years. 
Leccisi et al. [39], 2016 provided an important update on the lifecycle energy and environmental 
performance of different photovoltaic (PV) systems, including Si (sc-Si), Si (mc-Si), (CdTe) and 
(CIGS). Their analysis considered the country of production and adjusted the electricity mixes 
accordingly. The global warming potential (GWP) per kWh of electricity produced averages 
about 30 g CO2-eq, with CdTe PV performing particularly well, reaching values as low as 10 g 
CO2-eq under high irradiation. In contrast, Chinese sc-Si PV systems can reach up to 80 g 
CO2-eq under low irradiation conditions. Overall, CdTe PV stood out as the most 
environmentally friendly technology, with significant improvements in the latest production 
modules compared to earlier versions. The study also found that single axis tracking systems 
could improve the environmental profile of PV systems by around 10% in most impact 
categories.  
Furthermore, Szilagyi and Gróf [40], 2020 in recently estimated the environmental impact of a 
20 MW photovoltaic (PV) system in Felsőzsolca, Hungary, using the Product Environmental 
Footprint (PEF) Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) method. Their study compared two 
models with different system boundaries: a detailed system boundary and a simplified one. The 
simplified model resulted in a higher PEF score. In both models, the PV module contributed 
almost half of the total score, the mounting structure almost 30% and the inverter 6.6% of the 
total environmental impact.  
On the other hand, Kouloumpis et al. [41] ,2020 conducted a study to assess the life cycle 
environmental impact of a 500-kW ground-mounted photovoltaic farm compared to five 10 kW 
rooftop photovoltaic systems in northern Greece. The results showed that, excluding the 
transmission and distribution network, the global warming potential (GWP) for the ground-
mounted system was 42.7 g CO2-eq, while for the roof-mounted systems it was between 54.2 
and 54.5 g CO2-eq. However, when the transmission network was included, the ground-
mounted photovoltaic systems had a higher environmental impact.  
Mohd Nordin et al. [42] estimated the environmental performance and primary energy 
consumption of a newly installed large-scale solar PV system with a capacity of 37.57 MWp in 
Malaysia. Using a process-based life cycle assessment approach and the ReCiPe 2016 (H) 
methodology, both the midpoint and end-point impact categories were assessed. The study 
found a global warming impact of 30.95 g CO2-eq/kWh and an energy payback time of 3.43 
years, while sensitivity analysis showed that extending the lifetime of the system from 15 to 40 
years could reduce the climate change impact by 55%.

And in their paper, Mohd Nordin et al. [43] evaluated a life cycle assessment to quantify the 
environmental impacts of residential grid-connected PV systems in Malaysia using 
monocrystalline silicon PV modules. The LCA was carried out using OpenLCA 1.8 software, 
Ecoinvent 3.5 database and the impact assessment method of IMPACT2002+ and CED. The 
influence of varying system capacity from 3 to 12 kWp, system lifetime of 21, 25 and 30 years, 
and solar irradiation of 1560.8, 1651.8 & 1935.5 kWh/m2/yr were investigated. The results

systems in Malaysia ranged from 37.97 to 67.26 g CO2-eq/kWh, 4387.10 to 4699.99 MJ/m2

Badza et al. [22] in this work, the environmental performance of six different scenarios (mono 
c-Si, poly c-Si and CdTe) modules and (aluminum and steel) mounting structures of PY systems 
in sub-Saharan Africa were investigated using different photovoltaic module technologies with 
different installation methods. The systems were evaluated using the ReCiPe 2016 Midpoint 
(H) evaluation method and the results showed that the EPBT of the scenarios varied between 
1.47 and 1.95 years, GWP ranged from 37-48g CO2-eq/kWh.  
Riya Roy and Joshua. [44],2023 This study focused on the LCA of racking/mounting systems 
along with three sensitivity analyses compared to rooftop PV systems. Results showed that 
rooftops had 21-54% less energy, 18-59% less CO2eq, 1-12% less water/kWp, and 51-57% 
lower EPBT than ground-mounted solar systems. 
IEA PVPS Task 12. [11] This report is one of the most recent studies to investigate the 
environmental performance of PV systems, as the study evaluates and compares the 
environmental impact of two PV systems, one using mono-axis solar trackers for installation 
and the other fixed-tilt [34] and both systems using the same cell technology, state-of-the-art 
PERC modules. The study area is Northern Italy and Southern Italy, and the expected system 
lifespan is 25 years. The studies showed that the global warming results of the monoaxial solar 
trackers systems are lower than those of the fixed-tilt systems, as well as the location of the 
system and the efficiency of the modules are considered key parameters in the amount of solar 
irradiation and thus on the environmental performance results, since the higher amount of solar 
irradiation corresponds to a lower GWP impact.

such as those evaluated by Desideri et al. The studies also show that ground-mounted systems 
generally have higher GWPs than rooftop systems, mainly due to differences in mounting and 
distribution networks, with values ranging from 30 to 67 g CO2-eq/kWh. These results 
emphasize the impact of technological advances and location on the environmental 
performance of PV. A tabular summary of previous LCA studies on different PV technologies 
and different end-use installations is presented in Table 1, which illustrates the wide variation

1. Methodology 
In this study, four hypothetical systems were selected as references, each configured differently 
for end-use installation in Northern Europe, specifically Denmark, to perform a Life Cycle 
Assessment (LCA) of these systems. The study was carried out according to the LCA 
methodology outlined in the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) 14040 and 
14044 standards [5], [6] and the methodological guidelines of the International Energy Agency 
Photovoltaic Power Systems Programme (IEA-PVPS) Task 12 [7] and EPD Documents and 
Science Literature. The attribution approach, the most widely used in LCA, was adopted for this 
study [8], [9]. 
The process-based LCA consists of four phases: goal and scope definition, life cycle inventory 
(LCI), life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) and interpretation, as shown in Figure (4). The goal 
and scope phase defines the functional unit of the product system, the system boundary 
considered, and the system description. The LCI phase includes data collection, data validation 
and mapping of data to unit processes and functional units. In the LCIA phase, the LCI data are 
transformed into impact indicators, such as the climate change indicator, and one of its factors 
is the potential global warming, which is the main objective of conducting the study and is 
estimated using the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC's 100-year baseline 
model) (based on IPCC 2021),[10] in accordance with the IEA Task 12 methodological 
guidelines [7], by using OpenLCA 2.2 software ,and Ecoinvent dataset (3.5),(3.7.1),(3.8),(3.9.1) 
[30], and LCI reports  to model the life cycle of four PV Systems and assess theirs environmental 
impacts. 
Finally, the interpretation phase further analyses the LCIA results and draws conclusions and 
recommendations from the findings. The details of these processes are elaborated in the 
following subsections.

1.1 
Goal & Scope  
 
The main objective of this study is to assess the potential environmental impacts associated 
with the generation of electricity from photovoltaic (PV) systems using different types of 
installation configurations. This study assesses the environmental impacts of PV systems over 
their entire life cycle (from the extraction of raw materials (Cradle), through the manufacture of 
components and their use, to the end of life (Grave), (only end of life of the PV module) and 
sheds light on all the components and processes that contribute fundamentally to these 
environmental impacts, such as climate change. Specifically, this study evaluates the Life Cycle 
Impact Assessment (LCIA) of four different hypothetical PV systems using the same high-
efficiency technology, PERC based on a half-cut cell [11], with the same system lifetime, which 
is 25 years according to the data sheet of the modules used and the manufacturing data of the 
components it can see in see Annex1 of this study , except for the inverters, which have a 
lifetime of 17 years when used in ground-mounted systems , which have to be replaced. This 
study examines four different installation configurations with different scales as follows (on a 
small scale 3KWp for roof-integrated panels (BIPV), roof-mounted panels (BAPV) and on a 
large scale 10MWp for ground-mounted panels using a single-axis solar tracker mounting 
==PAGE:20==
 
16 
 
system (HSAT) and the second is a fixed-tilt mounting system (FT). In this way, the difference 
in the environmental performance of the systems can be assessed by highlighting the 
differences in the mounting system types of the PV systems. As the systems are hypothetical, 
the operation and maintenance (O&M) phase, the other minor components, and the 
transmission lines and the electrical substation into grid were excluded from the analysis of this 
study, due to the difficulty of obtaining information and PV farm specific design and construction 
may change case by case. The functional unit chosen for this study is 1kWh of AC electricity 
consumed in the building, as the system is bound to the building.  
The secondary objective of the study is to compare the results of the environmental assessment 
of the PV system in this study with the results of previous studies within the last ten years that 
are mentioned in table (1). 
In addition, as a sensitivity analysis option, the impact of extending the lifetime of the PV system 
by 5 years on the environmental impact per kWh produced is assessed. The second scenario 
evaluates the impact of using a new solar roof system. 
 
1.2 
System boundary 
 
The boundaries of the four systems are described by four main stages (material extraction, 
manufacturing, installation & use and end-of-life dismantling). During the production & 
manufacturing phase, the system boundaries should include the input of raw materials, the use 
of energy, the manufacture of panels, the mounting system, cables, inverters and all other 
components needed to produce electricity, that is included in this study. In addition, 
transportation, construction and installation, including supporting structures, should be included 
in the construction phase. In the phase certain parts, such as the repair and full replacement of 
166 kw inverters after 17 years, tracker actuators and control boards based on their failure rate, 
are included in the system boundaries. Finally, recycling and reuse, waste treatment and 
transport are included in the end-of-life phase for PV modules [23]. Figure (5) shows the system 
boundaries for the LCA study carried out, where all unit processes falling within the red dotted 
boundaries are included in the system under analysis, while unit processes outside the red 
dotted boundaries are excluded from the system under analysis. The modelling of the 
foreground and background data is based on the Eco invent database, updated literature and 
EPD declarations [19] [26] [28] [30], IEA Task12[11] [14], and manufacturing data sheets.

2. Life cycle inventory (LCI) 
 
The Life Cycle Inventory phase of the LCA involves the collection and modelling of primary & 
secondary data inputs for raw materials, energy consumption, product outputs and emissions 
for each process unit over the entire life cycle of the systems under analysis. The secondary & 
primary data sources for the four different system components (PV panels, mounting structures, 
inverters, cables) were obtained from the Ecoinvent database (3.5) (3.8), LCI reports (2024) 
[11] , (2020) [14] , published relevant literature, product datasheets and EPD declarations, as 
detailed for each component in the following subsections. Table (6) lists the reference unit, 
reference flow, data source and type of data source for each unit process. In this study, the 
reference unit of a unit process defines the relationship between the input and output flows 
within the unit process and is modelled according to the type of component involved. For 
example, if the reference unit is (1 m²), this means that the input and output flows are based on 
(1 m²) of the component. The mounting structure unit process is an exception as it is modelled 
based on (1 m²) of PV module area. The reference flow, on the other hand, quantifies the 
necessary inputs of a unit process used in the OpenLCA software within the product system to 
achieve the functional unit of 1 kWh and 1kwp.

3. Impact assessment method (LCIA) 
 
In order to evaluate the environmental performance of PV systems associated with the 
generation of 1 KWh of AC using PERC technology and four final installation configurations, 
and to highlight the main objective of this study, which is to assess and compare the total Global 
Warming Potential (GWP) results of the systems under study, the Intergovernmental Panel on 
Climate Change (IPCC) 100-year baseline model (based on IPCC 2021),[10] according to the 
IEA Task 12 methodological guidelines [7] was used. OpenLCA software was employed as a 
tool to build the LCA model for the four systems and to perform calculations and assess the 
carbon footprint of the systems through several LCI datasets and various impact assessment 
methods. Figure (12) as an example shows how the LCA model of the BAPV2 system contains

Conclusion  
This study presents an analysis and evaluation of the potential global warming impact of the 
life cycle of four photovoltaic systems, which differ in terms of final installation and system 
capacity, but are based on the same photovoltaic module technology, i.e. PERC in Denmark. 
The main objective of the study is to use an updated database that reflects the current reality 
of manufacturing processes, and the amount of energy used to produce and transport the 
components of PV systems. In general, the study shows that the BAPV rooftop system has the 
highest GWP impact with ranges between (5.77E-02 - 3.65E-02) kg CO2-Eq/kWh, followed by 
the BIPV system (5.40E-02 - 3. 28E-02) kg CO2-Eq/kWh, then the FT system (4.24E-02, 3.29E-
02) kg CO2-Eq/kWh, and finally the HSAT system (2.95E-02 - 2.92E-02) kg CO2-Eq/kWh, 
which is considered to have the lowest GWP impact of the other systems. It can be concluded 
from these results that there are many factors that have a clear impact on the environmental 
performance of the systems studied, including the system capacity, the manufacturing locations, 
which depend mainly on the practices and the electricity mix of the country where the 
components are manufactured, and the transport of the components to their place of use. For 
example, although the hybrid inverter is considered an updated version of inverters with less 
maintenance, longer life and better efficiency, it showed a second higher contribution share 
range between (43%-40%)  after  the PV module and has a clear effect in increasing the GWP 
for both systems BAPV and BIPV, unlike the 2.5kw inverter whose percentage ranged between 
(13% to 12%) in both systems. Despite the complexity of the manufacturing process and the 
obsolescence of the LCI dataset, the manufacturing process takes place in Europe and the 
medium voltage electricity mix ENTSO was used, in addition to shorter transport distances to 
the sites of use and thus less energy consumption. 
The results of the sensitivity analysis for the first scenario show that extending the lifetime of 
the PV systems increases the electricity production, which in turn reduces their GWP impact. 
In the second scenario, where a solar roof is used for the BIPV system instead of integrating 
the studied module into the roof, the GWP impact of the solar roof is found to be higher. 
Although the PERC module has the largest contribution to GWP in the baseline assessments 
(ranging from 50.2% to 81%), the solar roof still has the highest GWP at 4.28E-02 kg CO2-
eq/kWh. This increase is attributed to additional manufacturing and transport processes. The 
results suggest that the materials and energy consumed during manufacture have a significant 
impact on the overall GWP impact. 
 
==PAGE:65==
 
61 
 
 
Recommendations   
In the system design must consider key factors such as the country in which the components 
are manufactured, the practices used during the manufacturing phase (e.g. the percentage of 
recycled materials used in the production phase), the country's electricity mix and the transport 
distance to the final installation site. These factors have a significant impact on the overall 
environmental impact of the PV system, either reducing or increasing it.
