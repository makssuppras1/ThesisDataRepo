Abstract
The focus of this thesis is on developing machine learning models which can predict
named entities in documents Danish or English documents within the domain of law.
The project is done in collaboration with Cleardox which provided the use cases for this
thesis, the requirements for the models and the test documents. These requirements were
closely followed such that no GDPR regulations were broken and Cleardox guidelines
regarding the training of the models were followed.
Throughout this study, three different language models were looked into: spaCy, Flair and
BERT. Each of these models was compared and BERT was chosen as the model to use
for this specific task.
This thesis shows how to train and fine­tune two BERT models in such a way that it can
be used for Named Entity Recognition. The models have been tested on ”Danish Depen­
dency Treebank” (DaNE) for Danish and ”Annotated Corpus for Named Entity Recogni­
tion” (ACNER) for English which resulted in a F1­score of 0.65 and 0.61 respectively. The
system as­is could be used for anonymization and it is up to Cleardox if they wish to do
so but only within the specific domain where person names, locations and organizations
need to be recognized. The results and ways to enhance the performance of the system
will be discussed including a conclusion that proves that BERT can be useful within the
anonymization of personal information.

Introduction
Data sharing is the practice used for scholarly research for collaboration. This is important
as sharing of data is one of the most essential aspects of all technological innovations in­
cluding data sharing, current challenges such as secure data storage and secure sharing
of data amongst collaborators. When collaborators share data, for instance in a lawsuit or
research, the shared documents contain personal information about the people involved.
This information needs to comply with GDPR rules and thus personal information must
be redacted or deleted so that the people involved cannot be traced. While this is possi­
ble to some extent, the current approach at the company Cleardox [1] is far from perfect
since frequent false positives are found especially when checking for the names of people
and/or organizations.
In this thesis, multiple use cases for GDPR are used. The use cases are made in cooper­
ation with the company Cleardox, see appendix A. Cleardox provides an anonymization
tool to redact GDPR from documents, as seen in figure 1.1. The company does not cur­
rently make use of any machine learning techniques to find personal information.
Figure 1.1: Cleardox redaction model [2].
When the use cases have been discussed and settled, pre­trained language models within
the area of Named Entity Recognition (NER) will be used to test how they perform against
Cleardox’s already existing redacting tool. One of these models will be chosen to be
trained and fine­tuned to this specific NER task. One of the models will be trained in
English and the other in Danish.
The resulting model of this will be compared to Cleardox’s current approach by verifying
that they work on a chosen document provided by Cleardox and compared to other public
fine­tuned models within the area of NER. Lastly, the models will be presented in such a
way that Cleardox can use them to integrate with their platform and use them for future
work.

1.0.1
Research Question
Is it more recommended to use a language­based model instead of simple logic1 for
redacting personal information in guidelines with GDPR?
1.0.2
Limitations
• This work will not seek to replace the current approach used at Cleardox instead it
should be used as a proof of concept.
• This work does not train a model from the ground but instead uses a pre­trained
model.
• Labeled data within NER in Danish is limited.
• Limited computational resources are available.
• The system is not entirely automated and will require manual work.
1.1
Cleardox
Cleardox’s redaction software tool can automatically identify names, addresses, social
security numbers and more. It is up to the user whether they want to replace the infor­
mation with a label, remove it or keep it. This approach will save time when used on
larger documents where personal information has to be removed such as within e.g. the
research domain or the law domain.
1.1.1
GDPR
The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) went into effect the on 25th of May 2018
intending to provide a set of standardized data protection laws across all the member
countries within the EU. This section will describe who needs to adhere to the regulation,
which rules are necessary to ensure data protection and how GDPR relates to Cleardox
and NER.
1.1.2
Who is GDPR applicable to?
GDPR applies to organizations that operate within the EU, as well as organizations outside
of the EU that offer goods or services to customers or businesses in the EU. This means
that most major corporations in the world have to comply with GDPR even if they reside
outside of the EU.
1.1.3
How is the data protected?
Processing personal data without fulfilling the requirements of GDPR is by default prohib­
ited. Per article 6 of the GDPR, there are six cases where is it legal to process personal
data.

1.2
Terminology
• Benchmark: A way to compare the performance of models.
• Down­stream task: Supervised­learning task that utilizes a pre­trained model or
component.
• Language model: The purpose of a language model is to ”fill in the blank” based
on context.
• Model: A file that has been trained to recognize certain types of patterns.
• pre­trained models: A model that has been created by someone else to solve a
similar problem and trained on some data.
• Supervised learning: Relies on labeled input and output training data.
• Token: A part of a sentence that can either be the word itself or split into multiple
parts.
• Unsupervised learning: Processes unlabeled or raw data.
1.3
Related work
Throughout this project, Cleardox have been in contact with two machine learning com­
panies seeking to do the same or are already doing the same as what Cleardox are doing.
The first company that we have had a meeting with is Lovdata [5] which is a Norwegian
foundation that publishes judicial information of Norway. They have recently chosen to
use machine learning over simple logic to find named entities such as person names,
organizations and locations. While Lovdata do not want to share their results with the
public and thus can not be used for comparison with the results of this study, they have
been useful collaborators and provided an estimate for how many documents they used
to create their model. The second company that Cleardox reached out to but did not man­
age to arrange an interview with is the Alexandra Institute. The Alexandra Institute have
well­written documentation available online [6] where they talk about different frameworks
such as spaCy, Flair and BERT. They have used their own publicly available data to train
these frameworks so that they are more precise in Danish. In their paper DaNLP: An
open­source toolkit for Danish Natural Language Processing [7], they mention that the
best model for NER is a Danish pre­trained BERT model using the Transformer architec­
ture from Huggingface. Lastly, a Swedish thesis A compact language model for Swedish
text anonymization [8] has been looking at the concept just with another model; ALBERT
which is a Lite BERT for Self­Supervised Learning of Language Representations. While
the author has been looking at another model, the core concepts are still the same and
can be used for other models.

2
Analysis
In this chapter different machine learning concepts and frameworks will be introduced.
Each framework will be explored in more detail with consideration of its pros and cons.
The different frameworks will be compared and analyzed and from the results, one will be
chosen as the main focus.
2.1
Natural Language Processing
Natural Language Processing (NLP) is the field in machine learning that allows a computer
to understand human language and is used for technologies like auto­prediction, auto­
correction and speech recognition. A technique that is offered by NLP is Named Entity
Recognition (NER) which will be the focus of this report.
As the name suggests, NER is the process for identifying nouns like names, organizations
and locations. NER deals with the topic ”Given a word ’x’ what kind of entity is ’x’?.
In this chapter, three models; spaCy, Flair and BERT will be introduced and verified on
a document containing personal information, see Appendix A.4, approved by Cleardox.
The words underlined have manually been found and highlighted and it is expected that
the other models tested should return the same found entities. Each section will contain
an explanation of how the model works, verification of the model, the pros and cons of the
model and benchmarks to compare them. Lastly, one of the three models will be chosen
to be fine­tuned. It should be noted that performance of the model is not solely the reason
why to pick it. Other properties that influence the decision are among other; run time,
flexibility and languages that it supports.
2.1.1
Cleardox
Cleardox is able to find several entities automatically, however, some of those turns out
to be false positives. Currently, Cleardox make no use of machine learning to identify
entities which has its pros and cons. While it is not necessary to use machine learning
to find every entity such as e­mail addresses, it will reduce the number of false positives
for person names and such. For person names, Cleardox use a public list with most
common names [9] which is not ideal since new names are introduced each year and
more rare/non­Danish names are hard to detect. Here, machine learning will do a better
job since it will learn the pattern of which names occur and how they are written. For other
entities like phone numbers and social security numbers, regular expressions are used
to identify these numbers. For this task, machine learning will not necessarily be a better
alternative, because it will need to be fed with labeled data for this specific task which can
be difficult to gather and time­consuming.
Whilst companies like Lovdata have made the transition from no machine learning to only
machine learning, it may not necessarily be the best decision for everyone. It can be
time­consuming to find entities using machine learning, and many entities can be found
by using simple regular expressions. Therefore it is a good idea to have a mix of machine
learning for some entities and simple logic for other entities.

than 64 languages developed by Matthew Honnibal and Ines Montani that are founders

tok2vec
As the name suggests, tok2vec stands for ”token­to­vector”. This component is at the
start of the pipeline and is connected to other components with the ”listener layer”. These
listener layers act as proxies, passing the predictions from the tok2vec component into
downstream components, and communicating gradients2 back upstream [15]. At the be­
ginning of training, the tok2vec component will grab a reference to other listener layers
in the pipeline and pass its predictions to the listeners, allowing the listeners to reuse the
predictions [16].

2.4
BERT
BERT stands for Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers and a transformer­
based machine learning technique for NLP pre­training developed by Google which sup­
ports more than 100 languages.
The reason behind the creation of BERT was to enhance the two existing strategies for
applying pre­trained language representations to down­stream tasks: feature­based and
fine­tuning. Both of the approaches have the same objective during pre­training; to use
unidirectional language models to learn general language representation. In the BERT
paper [25] it is argued that the current unidirectional techniques restrict the power of the
pre­trained representations, especially for the fine­tuning approaches. Thus, BERT is
proposed, a language model which is bidirectionally trained and fixes the unidirectional
problem.
So how does BERT achieve bidirectional training? It makes use of two methods: Masked
Language Model (MLM) and Next sentence Prediction (NSP).
MLM will randomly mask some of the tokens from the input and then tries to predict them.
15% of the words in the input is replaced with [MASK] and is run through BERT encoders.
However, there is an issue with this ­ the model only tries to predict when the replaced
token is present in the input. The model should try to predict correct tokens regardless
of what token is present in the input. To solve this problem, out of the 15% of the tokens
selected for masking:
• 80% are replaced with the token [MASK].
• 10% are replaced with a random token.
• 10% are left unchanged.
This technique allows the model to look in both directions and uses the full context of the
sentence whereas other language models tend to only use left context or right context.
NSP is used to understand the relationship between two sentences. A pair of sentences
are given as input, and BERT is trained to predict if the second sentence is the next
sentence in the original text as well. During training, two input sentences are given at a
time where 50% of the time the second comes after the first one and 50% of the time it
is a random sentence from the corpus. BERT uses both of these techniques, MLM and
NSP, to minimize the loss function.

is built on top of as seen in figure 2.10. Some of these concepts will briefly be explained

RNN
A Recurrent Neural Network, RNN, is a special type of artificial neural network adapted
to work for time series data or data that involves sequences [28]. A Recurrent Neural
Network, in contrast to a Feedforward Neural Network, depends on the previous data
point as will thus need the recurrency layer such that RNNs can keep track of the memory
as seen in figure 2.11. This helps them store the states or information of previous inputs
to generate the next output of the sequence.
Figure 2.11: RNN vs FNN [29].
The problem with RNN, with respect to NLP, is that it can not learn long­range dependen­
cies for long sentences, even with LSTM/GRU. Therefore attention came along, to help

with this issue but also decrease the training time it took since RNN processed sequen­
tially.
LSTM
Long Short­Term Memory (LSTM) is an advanced RNN that extends the memory of RNN
and allows the information to persist. This means that LSTM can read, write and delete
information from its memory. The architecture of this model differs from other models in
that it uses three gates to determine its decisions, see figure 2.12.
Figure 2.12: LSTM three gates [29].
The input gate is used to determine whether or not to let new input in, the forget gate
determines whether or not to delete the information or keep it because it is not important
and the output gate determines if the information should impact the output at the current
timestep. LSTM solves the problematic issues of vanishing gradients since it keeps the
gradient steep enough resulting in faster training and higher accuracy.
Bi­LSTM
LSTM at its core only preserves information from inputs that it already has seen using
the hidden state. This information can either be done via forward LSTM or backward
LSTM. The issue with using this approach is that the model does not take the context into
consideration. Bidirectional LSTM (Bi­LSTM) runs the input in two ways; one from left to
right and one from right to left using two hidden states. This allows the model to preserve
information from both directions left and right. This bidirectional approach is what BERT
takes advantage of.
2.4.1
Attention
Attention is a core component of BERT and it is a way for a model to assign weight to input
features based on their importance to some task. While there is more to the concept, as
explained in Attention Is All You Need [26], the mechanics of attention in BERT is quite
simple. Let there be given a sequence X where each element is a vector, the attention
is a function that takes this sequence X as input and returns another sequence Y of the
same length containing the same vector lengths as of those for X as illustrated in figure
2.13.
18
Data Anonymization of Personal Data using Machine Learning
==PAGE:28==
Figure 2.13: Attention of sequence X [30].
The weights show how much the model attends to each input in X when computing the
weighted average and are referred to as attention weights.
As for attention to language, the sequence X represents a sequence where each vector is
a word with its corresponding word embedding. While the embedding number is not so in­
terpretable, it indicates how closely related the word is to another where words with similar
meaning are generally close to one another i.e. sofa and couch. It is, in fact, possible to
perform arithmetic on word embeddings i.e. embedding(king) −embedding(man) + em­
bedding(woman) ≈embedding(queen) [30]. By applying attention to the word embedding
in X, the weighted average will be produced in sequence Y. To comprehend language, it
is not sufficient to look at the individual words but instead look at how they relate to each
other in the context of the sentence which is what the attention mechanism enables the
BERT model to do.
Multi­Head Attention
Instead of performing a single attention function, BERT learns multiple attention mecha­
nisms, called heads thus Multi­Head Attention, which operate in parallel to one another.
This multiple attention mechanism enables the model to capture a broader range of rela­
tions between words than it would be plausible with a single attention function.

Transformers and BERT.

The encoder takes an input sequence of words and generates embeddings for every word
simultaneously. These embeddings consist of vectors that encapsulate the meaning of the
word where similar words have closer numbers in their vectors. This was also shown for
word embedding using GLOVE for Flair in figure 2.8. The decoder takes these embeddings
from the encoder and the previously generated words of a sentence and then uses them
to generate the next word in a sequence. This process is repeated one word at a time
until the end of the sequence is reached.
What makes the architecture of BERT conceptually better than a LSTM cell is the notice­
able separation in tasks ­ the encoder learns valuable information about the language,
such as, what is grammar and more importantly what is context; while the decoder learns
how to map words and map languages.
If decoders are stacked on top of each other without encoders then GPT Transformer
architecture3 is created. Conversely, if encoders are stacked then Bidirectional Encoder
Representations from Transformers is created (thus its name BERT).
BERT comes in multiple layers of encoders ­ BERT base and BERT large. BERT base
consists of 12 layers where a layer indicates a transformer block, 12 attention heads and
110 million parameters while BERT large consists of 24 layers, 16 attention heads and
340 million parameters. These encoders expect a sequence of tokens that are converted
to vectors and processed in the neural network. Before the processing takes place, the
input must have additional metadata as seen in figure 2.16.

2.4.3
BERT embeddings
The three different embeddings that are used in the BERT architecture is as follow:
1. Token embeddings: A [CLS] token is added at the beginning of the first sentence
and [SEP] token is inserted at the end of each sentence.
2. Segment embeddings: A marker is added to each token to indicate sentence A or
sentence B. This allows the encoder to distinguish between sentences.
3. Position embeddings: Each token is represented by its position in the sentence.
The input sequence it added to each encoding block, where it will first pass through a
Self­Attention layer and then to a feed­forward neural network. The Self­Attention layer,
also called the intra­attention layer, is an attention mechanism relating different positions
of a single sequence in order to compute a representation of the sequence [26]. At the
end each position will output a vector of size hidden_size (768 in BERT base). This is
the word embedding.
While BERT solves the unidirectional problem by using MLM, supports more than 100 lan­
guages and has capabilities to fine­tune data to a specific language context, it also comes
with its downsides. A paper [31] by Allyson Ettinger highlights some of these problems.
Besides having some minor problems with commonsense and pragmatic inference, the
more noticeable result is negation. As an example Ettinger gives the context: A robin is a
____. BERT large predicts: bird, robin, person, hunter, pigeon. Now for the negation: A
robin is not a ____. BERT large predicts: bird, robin, person, hunter, pigeon. This shows
that BERT can have a hard time finding the correct word for negated sentences. Another
disadvantage is that can be very compute­intensive at inference time which could mean
that production at scale can become costly.

Data Preprocessing
For this project, two Danish datasets and one English dataset were used: Danish Depen­
dency Treebank (DaNE), WikiANN and Annotated Corpus for Named Entity Recognition

3.1.2
Tokenization
BERT uses a WordPiece tokenizer which is the method of creating tokens from a string.
Instead of looking at words and letters, BERT uses tokens which are created by splitting
words into full forms (one word becomes one token) or into word pieces (each word is
broken into multiple tokens). Consider the following example:
text = ”My surfboard is broken”
After applying BERT’s pre­trained tokenizer bert-base-cased [40], the words will be split
into tokens:
tokenized = [’My’, ’surf’, ’##board’, ’is’, ’broken’]
Since surfboard is a long word, the tokenizer will split it into two tokens where it will rec­
ognize that it knows the word surf and board and thus leave it as it is. The reason why
## is added before board is to indicate that the token has been split and is connected to
the previous token. Depending on which tokenizer is used, different results will occur. If
a Danish tokenizer [41] is used the same text will provide a different output:
tokenized = [’my’, ’surf’, ’##board’, ’is’, ’brok’, ’##en’]
Additionally, each token has an ID associated to it. The same sentence as used before
will return the following token ID for bert-base-cased:
tokenized = [’My’, ’surf’, ’##board’, ’is’, ’broken’]
input_ids = [1422, 20114, 4015, 1110, 3088]
Each ID corresponds to a row in the vocabulary [40] which is a list of common words.
Note that some words in the vocabulary contains ## such as ##bed and bed. This is to
differentiate the use of the word bed as an independent word versus as part of a subword
in a larger word. Some of the token IDs will occur more often than others such as [0],
[100], [101] and [102]. These ID’s are the [PAD], [UNK], [CLS] and [SEP] tokens. The
[PAD] token is added whenever the sentence needs to match the maximum length of a
26
Data Anonymization of Personal Data using Machine Learning
==PAGE:36==
sentence. The [UNK] token occurs when a token is unknown to the tokenizer. The [CLS]
token is added at the beginning of the sentence while [SEP] is added at the end of a
sentence to separate sentences.
3.1.3
Wordpiece Tagging
When a word is split into multiple tokens one might wonder what happens with the label
for that specific word. Consider the previous example again:
text = ”My surfboard is broken”
tokenized = [’My’, ’surf’, ’##board’, ’is’, ’broken’]
Since the word surfboard has been split into two tokens, the label of the word should
still be the same. There are many solutions to handle this scenario such as creating a
specialized data structure based on label spans, the solution for this project will be more
simplistic.
Each word will be tokenized and the length of this tokenized word will be saved. The
tokenized word is then added to a final tokenized word list and the corresponding label
will be added as many times as the length of the tokenized word. By doing this, it will be
possible to keep track of labels for tokenized words while preserving the context of the
sentence.
3.1.4
Padding and Truncating
The BERT model has a maximum input sequence length of 512 tokens. The reason be­
hind this is the transformer’s attention mechanism used in BERT scales quadratically in
the sequence length which is a limitation currently being worked on. There are some
architectures that avoid this limitation such as Facebook’s lightconv and dynamic convo­
lution [42] that uses local attention with a learned position by position kernel and it scales
linearly O(n) in input length. Another option is Google’s reformer architecture [43] which
uses shared queries, keys and locality­sensitive hashing to bucket them for the positions
that should attend to each other. The complexity of this approach is O(nlog(n)).
In order to bypass this limitation for BERT, there are some options available. The first
option is supported by the paper How to Fine­Tune BERT for Text Classification? [44]
which gives three different methods of truncate text to perform BERT fine­tuning:
• head­only: keep the first 510 tokens1.
• tail­only: keep the last 510 tokens.
• head+tail: empirically select the first 128 and the last 382 tokens.
In the paper, their experiments on the IMDb movie review dataset showed that keeping
the first 128 tokens and the last 382 tokens performed best. While it would be optimal to
use this approach, it also depends on the dataset used. When writing something like a
movie review, we tend to put our high­level points in the introduction and conclusion.
Another approach is to divide the text into chunks before performing the final classification.
Each of these chunks will be classified and then combined back. This option is more
expensive than the first option since it will be computationally expensive when dealing
with lengthy documents. While it is possible to feed the output tokens for each subtext to
another network, it cannot be used to fine­tune BERT2.
For this project, the chunking approach was used at first. 512 tokens was handled at a time
by parsing the text into a method that will tokenize each word into a list and then iterate

3.1.6
Hyperparameters
The BERT authors [25] suggest the following hyperparameters when fine­tuning:
• Epoch: 2, 3, 4
• Batch size: 8, 16, 32, 64, 128
• Learning rate: 3e­4, 1e­4, 5e­5, 3e­5
The amount of epochs determines how many times the learning algorithm will train on
the entire training dataset. The amount of epochs is important since too many epochs
will result in overfitting. This means that the model will perform well on the training data
but poor on generalization to other data since the function is too closely fit to a limited set
of data points. Having too few epochs can result in the opposite: underfitting, where the
model performs poorly on the training data and poor generalization to other data.
The batch size is the number of training points utilized in one iteration. It is a good idea to
choose a value which is a power of 2 since the overall idea is to fit the mini­batch3 entirely
on the GPU which comes with a storage capacity in power of two.
The learning rate determines the step size at each iteration while moving toward a mini­
mum of a loss function.
For this project, the batch sizes used will only be 8 and 16 which is due to the GPU used:
NVIDIA GeForce GTX 1060 6GB. Batch sizes larger than 16 are unable to fit in memory on
the GPU. Each step of an epoch took approximately 30­40 minutes to complete depending
on the size of the dataset. This means that a full run of using these hyperparameters to
train one model, running nonstop, minimum took:

Metrics
This section will shortly describe the metrics used in the process of training and evaluating
this thesis and how they are calculated.
Classification
• True Positive (TP): Positive outcomes that the model predicted correctly.
• True Negative (TN): Negative outcomes that the model predicted correctly.
• False Postive (FP): Positive outcomes that the model predicted incorrectly.
• False Negative (FN): Negative outcomes that the model predicted incorrectly.
Accuracy
Accuracy is used for evaluating classification models. The accuracy is a measure for how
precise the model is and is calculated as follows:
Accuracy = Number of correct predictions
Total number of predictions
For binary classification, the accuracy can be calculated as follows:
Accuracy =
TP+TN
TP+TN+FP+FN
Precision
Precision attempts to answer the question of what proportion of positive identifications
was actually correct and is defined as:
Precision =
TP
TP+FP
Recall
Recall attempts to answer the question of what proportion of actual positives was identified
correctly and is defined as:
Recall =
TP
TP+FN
F1
The F1­score is a measure of a model’s accuracy on a dataset. The score is a combination
of precision and recall and can be calculated as follows:
F1 = 2 · precision · recall
precision + recall
Loss
Loss is a penalty given for a bad prediction. If the loss is zero, then the model’s prediction
is perfect otherwise the loss is greater than zero. In other words, the training loss indicates
how well the model fits the training data.
The BERT loss function will only take the masked values into consideration and thus
ignore the non­masked words.
Support
Support is the number of occurrences of each class in the dataset.
30
Data Anonymization of Personal Data using Machine Learning

Micro average
Micro average is computed by taking TP, TN, FP and FN for each class, adding them up,
then computing the metric of interest and lastly dividing by the total number of classes.
Gives an understanding of the performance when classes with little support are unimpor­
tant.
Macro average
Macro average is computed by taking TP, TN, FP and FN for each class, computing the
metric of interest, adding them up and lastly dividing by the total number of classes. Gives
an understanding of the performance when all classes are equally important.
Weighted average
Weighted average is computed by taking TP, TN, FP and FN for each class, computing
the metric of interest, then multiplying it by the size of the support of the class and lastly
normalizing it. Gives an understanding of the performance when the support of each class
determines their importance.

6.1
BERT and anonymization
Throughout this project, the BERT models were trained to identify the correct entities in
a sentence. BERT tries to predict whether a word is a name, organization, location or
something else. These prediction, while being handy, may not be that useful in terms
of anonymization since if you want to anonymize GDPR from a document, it should not
matter if it is a name, organization or location since all of them should be redacted.
The reason for its relevance is due to the fact that Cleardox provide their customers with
these labels such that they easily can determine if they want to keep locations and orga­
nizations in the text but not names. In some texts it is not necessary to remove entities
such as organizations and locations which would make it more demanding to go through
the document, especially if it is a long document. While the entities found may not al­
ways have the correct label associated with them, it is more important that they are found
instead of not recognized, therefore it is desired to have high precision.
Both of the datasets contained labels that seemed redundant within the area of NER such
as MISC for both datasets which do not follow a definition but are rather something that
is beyond a named entity and is not a person, location nor organization. This tag will be
ignored when looking at the F1­score since it gives no clear indication of how precise the
model is since it is difficult and not useful to determine which words are labeled as misc.
For the English dataset, it had multiple entities as listed in table 3.1. Since the same
test dataset was used on both models, which only had the entities PER, LOC, ORG and
MISC, the labels from the English dataset had to be converted to the same entities. The
entities time, gpe, art, eve and nat were set to the label O since the were deemed
not relevant for this specific task.
The Danish BERT model has an average F1­score of 0.65 while the English BERT model
has an average score of 0.61 excluding the entity MISC since it is difficult to determine.
It is, of course, possible to increase the average F1­score or the individual score for a
specific entity depending on the use case. By training the model on more data, the model
would also become more robust and better at identifying the correct entities. While the
main focus was on training the Danish model, it became an obstacle to make the model
better due to the data available. Looking at the available dataset [56], most of the public
dataset were used for other tasks than NER and did not have a lot of data. Furthermore,
whenever a new dataset was introduced, the data extraction method had to be changed
in order to fit this new dataset which is time­consuming since it had to fit the previous
data format. As for English NER data there is more available that had a similar format.
The only issue with this is that the training of the model took a longer time using the GPU
available.

Another reason why the results are lowered than desired could be due to the fact that the
indexes provided by the saved BERT model’s config file did not match what the model
predicted. This meant that if the true label for a sentence was ”Peter (B­PER) Hansen
(I­PER)”, the model would return its prediction as ”Peter (I­ORG) Hansen (B­LOC)” or
something similar. Therefore, the tags had to be manually changed such that they would
correspond with what it normally would predict. This issue has been was present during
testing and when outputting the found entities1.
Lastly, a minor reason which could have affected the training of the model was how the
words were split into sentences. The split was done every time a full stop appeared after
a word which is not ideal since it could be used to indicate an abbreviation. This would
mean that a sequence of words like ”Peter rejser meget til udlandet f.eks. USA” would
be split into three sentences even though it should only be split into one. While this is an
issue, it should not affect the results of the model too much. A possible workaround for
this is to make a list of common abbreviations and see if it is used in a sequence. If it is
used then the word should not be split but instead look for the next full stop.
Overall, the models performed decently within this specific area of finding names, orga­
nizations and locations in both English and Danish. The models, as­is, would be a poor
choice to use for other tasks such as identifying street addresses, phone numbers or sim­
ilar entities. It is not suggested that this implementation should be used to fully identify
named entities and rely on it to automatically redact these entities. This will never be the
case as a language model will never be 100% precise and find all labels. Therefore this
solution works well with Cleardox’s framework since they still require a manual inspection
of the found entities and do not automatically redact the found information.

how the words are split and how tokens are created which affects how BERT understands
the sentence, as seen in section 3.1.2 where a Danish tokenizer is used on an English
sentence. This affects how the words are split up and how the tokens are generated.
Hyperparameters
As mentioned in section 3.1.6, the hyperparameters used to train the BERT models were
suggested by the BERT authors. An interesting thing to do would be to train the model
again but with more learning rates (other than 3e­4, 1e­4, 5e­5 and 3e­5) to see if it would
provide better results for this specific task. Likewise, it would be ideal to train on higher
batch sizes and more epochs to see how beneficial it would be for the results of the model.
New entity
Originally, it was planned to get labeled data from Nævnenes Hus [57] but this idea was
scrapped due to time limitations. In order to get the correct permits and paperwork done,
it would take months while having to sign an NDA. Furthermore, they would have to manu­
ally label the data first which also would take a considerable amount of time. Therefore this
task is left for future work where the labeled data can be used to further train the model
with the existing entities PER, LOC and ORG but also introduce a new one; Cadastral
number. This number is used to identify buildings and can be written in many different
ways.

Conclusion and further perspectives
In this thesis, multiple language models were investigated, compared and analyzed, in
order to determine the most suitable model for finding named entities in documents. After
introducing the different language models and their pros and cons, the decision was made
to go with BERT. While this decision was based on various factors, it was mainly due to
its popularity and available resources.
Two BERT models were trained on Danish and English data respectively and evaluated on
different news articles and documents, provided by Cleardox. The models achieved the
best results on the name entity, which was the main entity that Cleardox was interested in.
While the models are available for use by Cleardox to use and edit, it is up to them whether
all of the entity tags should be used. Even though other companies, such as Lovdata, use
machine learning for personal information recognition, Cleardox do not currently want to
solely use this approach. Using a combination of simple logic and machine learning would
allow the company to best meet its requirements, due to the amount of publicly available
data.
While the models make it easier to identify personal data automatically, it will not be pos­
sible to use the models without manually going through the documents as well. This is to
ensure that the correct entities are identified and redacted. They do, however, make this
process less time­consuming and tedious, which makes it a useful component within the
area of NER for redacting personal information from certain domains, especially in law
and research.
