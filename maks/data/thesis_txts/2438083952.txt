Abstract
A polystyrene–b–polydimethylsiloxane (PS-PDMS) block-co-polymer (BCP) called "SD24" was
diluted in 8 mL cyclohexane (C6H12) and spin cast onto a Silicon (Si) wafer to form a circa 27.03
± 0.63 nm thin ﬁlm. Vapour annealing in methyl-cyclohexane (C7H14) caused the PS-blocks to
self-assemble into hexagonal arrays of nano cylinders with an average pitch of 73.03 ± 2.58 nm.
This pattern was transferred into the Silicon wafer as hexagonally arranged nano holes by two
subsequent reactive ion etching (RIE) steps; an 11 s Oxygen (O2) RIE step to remove PS-blocks
and turn the PDMS into a hard in situ silicon-oxycarbide (SiOxCy) mask, and a 20 s chlorine (Cl2)
RIE step to create shallow holes in the Silicon wafer. The pattern was then inverted by depositing
30 nm of alumina (Al2O3) all over and turning it into hexagonally arranged, straight nano pillars,
about 118.18 ± 9.52 nm high, by a 3 min 30 s Cl2 RIE. Subsequent sputter deposition of an 85
nm Nickel-Vanadium (NiV) seed layer allowed for the growth of a 350 µm Nickel (Ni) layer by
electroplating. The Silicon wafer could then be removed by potassium hydroxide (KOH) etching
for 7-8 hrs. at 80 ◦C, leaving only the Nickel shim with the NiV seed layer forming a pattern of
hexagonally arranged nano holes. The holes were 53.09 ± 5.87 nm in diameter and the pattern
uniformly covered the whole shim. Hexagonal arrays of densely spaced nano pillars were then
replicated in polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) discs, 2 mm thick and 50 mm in diameter (Ø50
mm), by using the shim as a master for injection molding.
By coating the Ø50 mm PMMA discs with 8 nm of Wolfram (W), the discs became super wetting
with contact angles below 5 ◦. These low contact angles were retained for at least 8 weeks by sim-
ply storing the discs in a plastic tray with a lid. The samples furthermore exhibited anti-reﬂective
properties, increasing the transmittance of visible light, compared to equivalent ﬂat PMMA sam-
ples. The transmission of the samples was only slightly lowered by the Wolfram coating. Macro-
and microscale fog condensation tests showed that the nano structured Wolfram coated samples
could act as anti-fogging lenses by spreading the condensed droplets into a continuous, transparent
ﬁlm. The spreading was presumably caused by the water hemi-wicking between the nano pillars
due to a negative Young-Laplace pressure initiated by the super hydrophilic pillars.

Resumé
En polystyrene-b-polydimethylsiloxane (PS-PDMS) block-co-polymer (BCP) kaldet "SD24" blev
opløst i 8 mL cyclohexan (C6H12) og blev spin-coatet på en silicium (Si) skive for at danne en 27.03
± 0.63 nm tyk tyndﬁlm. Derpåfølgende annealing i methyl-cyclohexan (C7H14) dampe medførte
at PS-blokkene selv-samledes til hexagonalt arrangerede nanocylindre, med en middel periode på
73.03 ± 2.58 nm. Dette mønster blev overført til siliciumskiven ved to påfølgende reaktive ion
ætse (RIE) trin: Først en 11 s oxygen (O2) RIE til at fjerne PS-blokkene og omdanne PDMS til en
hård in situ silicium-oxycarbid (SiOxCy) maske, og derpå en 20 s diklor (Cl2) RIE til at lave små
huller i siliciumskiven. Mønsteret blev så inverteret ved at deponere 30 nm aluminiumoxid Al2O3
over hele skiven og derefter omdanne mønsteret til hexagonalt arrangerede, lodrette nanosøjler,
omkring 118.18 ± 9.52 nm høje vha. en 3 min 30 s Cl2 RIE. Efterfølgende blev et 85 nm Nickel-
Vanadium (NiV) lag deponeret ved sputtering, sådan at et 350 µm nickel (Ni) lag kunne blive groet
ved elektroplatering. Siliciumskiven blev så fjernet ved 7-8 timers æts i kaliumhydroxid (KOH)
ved 80 ◦C. Dette efterlod kun nickel shimmen med NiV laget tilbage, til at udgøre et mønster
af hexagonalt arrangerede nanohuller. Hullerne var 53.09 ± 5.87 nm i diameter og mønsteret
dækkede hele skiven uniformt. Hexagonalt arrangerede rækker af tæt pakkede nanosøjler blev så
replikeret i skiver af polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA), 2 mm tykke og 50 mm i diameter (Ø50
mm), ved at bruge shimmen som støbeform i en sprøjtestøbeproces.
Ved at dække de Ø50 mm PMMA skiver med ca. 8 nm wolfram (W), blev skiverne superhydroﬁle
med kontaktvinker mindre end 5 ◦. Disse meget lave kontaktvinkler blev opretholdt i mindst otte
uger ved simpelthen at opbevare skiverne i en plastic bakke med et låg. Prøverne udviste ydermere
tegn på antireﬂektive egenskaber, idet de øgede transmittansen af synligt lys igennem sig i forhold
til tilsvarende ﬂade prøver. Transmittansen gennem prøverne blev kun sænket en lille smule, når
de blev dækket med wolfram. Makro- og mikroskala tågekondensationseksperimenter udført på
prøverne viste at, de nanostrukturerede wolframdækkede prøver kunne opføre sig som anti-dug
linser ved at sprede de kondenserede vanddråber ud til en kontinuert, gennemsigtig vandﬁlm.
Spredningen skyldtes antageligt at vandet "hemi-wickede" mellem nanosøjlerne pga. det negative
Young-Laplace tryk som de superhydroﬁle nanosøjler skabte.

Introduction and aim
The aim of this project is twofold.
• To fabricate a Nickel (Ni) shim using block-co-polymer (BCP) lithography, so that the shim
may be used for injection molding of super hydrophilic polymer surfaces.
• To ﬁnd a way to store the polymer surfaces such that they do not lose their super hydrophilic
properties over time.
A super hydrophilic - or super wetting - surface is a naturally hydrophilic surface, which has been
modiﬁed in such a way that a sessile drop of water placed on the surface will ideally spread out and
become a continuous ﬁlm. This property makes transparent super hydrophilic surfaces ideal for
anti-fogging lenses [1, 2, 3]. Fog is merely condensed water droplets on a surface, which diﬀracts
the incoming light, thus obscuring the surface [1, 2]. This is a general issue for cameras, solar
panels and ordinary eyeglasses among others [1, 2].
If instead the droplets could spread out to a continuous ﬁlm, the transparency will be restored
[1, 2]. In order to achieve this, the surface needs to be super hydrophilic [1, 2]. One way to achieve
super hydrophilicity is to plasma treat a naturally hydrophilic surface, to open up the chemical
bonds on the surface and increase the surface energy [4, 2, 3]. This will cause water molecules
from the ambient air to bind to the surface, making it super hydrophilic [4]. This eﬀect, however,
is short-lived due to what is known as hydrophobic recovery [4].
Another way to make the surface super hydrophilic is to uniformly nano pattern a naturally
hydrophilic surface to increase the surface roughness [5, 2, 3, 6]. Thus the water is drawn between
the nano structures on the surface by capillary forces [2, 6]. This is where BCP lithography comes
in handy. BCPs will under the right conditions self-assemble into micro domains of uniform nano
patterns covering large, macroscopic areas [7, 8, 2, 6]. By utilizing the self-assembled BCP as
a lithographic mask, the pattern can be transferred to a Silicon (Si) substrate, which may be
made into a shim that can be used for injection molding or hot embossing [2]. This may be
both cheaper and easier than ordinary masking lithography [7]. However, ageing eﬀects are still
present as hydrocarbons from the ambient air tend to deposit and adhere to the surface over time
[9, 2]. Thus, the super hydrophilic samples need to be stored under proper conditions in order
to increase the life-time of the super hydrophilic eﬀect. How exactly to do this, is still not fully
explored [2]. Currently1, the nano structured, super hydrophilic, polymer surfaces fabricated by
Telecka et al. (2018) [2, 10] retained their super hydrophilic properties for up to three months in
ambient atmosphere when coated with a thin, transparent layer of silica (SiOx) [2].

Theory
This section describes the underlying theory for the behaviour of water on super hydrophilic sur-
faces. It also regards the theory for BCP self-assembly and BCP lithography, along with basic
rheology for the injection molding process. Finally, the optical principles of anti-reﬂective coatings
are also touched upon.
2.1
Super hydrophilic surfaces
When placing a sessile drop on a level surface, a hydrophilic surface will have an apparent contact
angle below 90 degrees, whereas a hydrophobic surface will have an apparent contact angle above
90 degrees [9, 6]. The contact angle is the angle measured inside the water droplet from the
solid-liquid interface to the liquid-gas interface [11]. The point where solid, liquid and gas meet
is used as the origin. In general two contact angles are used, the Young contact angle and the
apparent contact angle. The Young contact angle is denoted θY and is the contact angle measured
for a completely ﬂat, homogeneous surface [5, 2]. The apparent contact angle is denoted θ∗and
describes the contact angle for a rough, or structured surface [5, 2]. The relationship between the
Young contact angle and the apparent contact angle, as deﬁned by Wenzel [5, 3, 2], is given as
θ∗= r cos θY .

and γlg is the liquid-gas interface surface tension [11].
There are two other contact angles worth mentioning, the receding and the advancing contact
angles. The receding contact angle, θR, is the contact angle measured when the water is just
about to de-wet or retract from a surface. The advancing contact angle, θA, is the contact angle
measured when the water droplet is just about to wet an un-wetted surface. Both can be measured
by tilting the surface until the roll-oﬀangle is achieved. The roll-oﬀangle is the angle, which the
surface needs be tilted before the sessile water droplet rolls oﬀ[11, 3, 2].

and pressure. The surface tension arising from a dangling bond can be roughly described by the

Hydrophilic surfaces are surfaces, which have a high free surface energy [5]. By wetting the solid
surface, the solid surface free energy is reduced [5] as the dangling bonds of the solid surface become
paired [11]. However, since the wetting of the surface will increase the eﬀective surface area of
the water, the water will eventually stop spreading when a common energy minimum is reached,
since all systems tend to minimize their Gibbs’ free energy [5, 11]. Likewise, a hydrophobic surface
has a low free surface energy. Thus, the liquid water will not spread, as the water is already in
its most thermodynamically stable form with the smallest possible surface area, namely a sphere
[5]. In essence, water spread is a spontaneous process [5] which, as all spontaneous processes, are
governed by the Gibbs’ free energy exchange given as,
∆G = ∆H −T∆S
(4)
where ∆G is the Gibbs’ free energy change, ∆H is the change in enthalpy, T is the temperature and
∆S is the entropy change [13, 14]. If ∆G is negative, water spread can occour. Super hydrophilic
surfaces have a high surface free energy, and thus a lot of energy can be gained by reducing this
surface energy, i.e. by wetting it [5]. The main driving force for the wetting is the enthalpy change,
∆H = ∆U + ∆(pV ), where ∆U is the internal energy change of the system and ∆(pV ) is the
change in pressure (p) and volume (V) [14]. While the total volume of the droplet-solid system
is conserved and the pressure is kept constant, it is the negative change in internal energy, ∆U,
of the droplet-solid system from a higher to a lower value due to the decreased total surface free

a smooth surface of the same material [5].

A ﬁnal note regarding the wicking ﬂow is, that Telecka et al. (2018) [2], in conjunction with the
ﬁndings of Ishino & Okumura (2008) [18], states that the droplets do reach a full stop at some point
when deposited on the plasma treated nanotextured surface. This happens when the underlying
wicking ﬁlm overtakes the macroscopic droplet, and thus the macroscopic droplet rests on a
heterogenous surface consisting of pillar tops and water ﬁlm. On this surface, a thermodynamical
equilibrium can be reached, causing the macroscopic droplet to stop its expansion at a ﬁnite,
positive apparent contact angle, according to Cassie’s law [16, 2]. The underlying wicking ﬁlm,
however, is not restricted, and can thus continue its expansion, however at an ever decreasing
pace [2]. This is what Ishino & Okumura (2008) [18] dubbed the "sunny-side up"-state, which is
depicted on ﬁgure 4. The droplet corresponds to the yolk, and the wicking ﬁlm corresponds to
the egg-white of the "fried egg". Telecka et al. (2018) [2], reported that the droplet on the plasma
treated nanotextured surface spread about 20 times faster than on the untreated nanotextured
surface. But, then it came to a complete halt when it was overtaken by the wicking ﬁlm, whereas
the droplet on the untreated nanotextured surface spread a lot slower, but never actually stopped,
only slowed its pace [2].

the monomer composition of the individual polymer [21, 6]. This, for instance, means that two
diﬀerent polymers in a diblock BCP might not mix very well, actually, this is often the case [19].

Polymer injection molding
In order to fabricate the nano pillars of the anti-fogging surface in polymer (plastic), the inverse
pattern (holes) is ﬁrst fabricated in a Nickel shim. The polymer then needs to ﬂow into these
holes and solidify. Polymers are viscoelastic materials [13], this means that the stress-strain curve
depend on the load-rate [13]. In essence, if a large strain is applied very quickly to the polymer,
the polymer acts more rigid and stiﬀ, and thus breaks faster, i.e. at a lower strain. However if the
strain is applied slowly, the polymer chains will have time to reorganize and elongate, thus making
the polymer more ﬂexible and while the polymer will not be as strong, the strain-to-failure is much
larger [13]. Increasing the temperature, eases the reorganization of the polymer and makes the
polymer become less viscous [13, 36]. The viscosity, η, of the polymer is directly proportional to
the shift factor, aT, which is linked to the relaxation time of the polymer in a static relaxation
test [13]. A relaxation test is a test of how long it takes a material to return to a steady, relaxed
state after it has been subjected to a certain deformation [13]. aT is given as

Having these orderly structured, transparent, high energy surfaces solves the problem with con-
densation, however, the problem of fouling and hydrophobic recovery still needs to be solved. It
has been observed by several independent sources, that super hydrophilic surfaces made by using
a plasma treatment to achieve a higher energy surface, quickly relapses into a more hydrophobic
state due to hydrophobic recovery [2, 50, 9, 4]. As stated, plasma treating a polymer surface
opens up the chemical bonds on the surface resulting in a higher energy surface [2]. Telecka et al.
(2018) [2] furthermore ﬁnds that treating the PMMA surface with an Argon plasma, introduces
acid groups on some of the polymer units through ester-group hydrolysis (see ﬁgure 8), causing
the surface to become more hydrophilic. Longer treatment times result in more open bonds, and
thus more acid groups attaching, resulting in an even higher surface energy and even lower contact
angles [2].
It would seem logical that the higher the surface energy, the more aggressive the fouling. However,
Drelich et al. (2011) [9] pointed out, based on the work done by Baier (2006) [51], that very high-
energy surfaces, actually experience less fouling and hydrophobic recovery. They proposed that
this was due to the high degree of water aﬃnity, which would immediately create a barrier over the
surface, slowing down the fouling process [9]. Vesel and Mozatic (2012) [50] noted the same after
studying the ageing (hydrophobic recovery) eﬀects of Oxygen-plasma treated PMMA samples.
Yet, they hypothesized that the slowed ageing for the higher energy surfaces, i.e. longer plasma
treatment times, was due to the plasma treatment increasing the crystallinity of the PMMA, thus
preventing polymer reorganization [50].
In any case, hydrophobic recovery has two main contributions, fouling by hydrocarbons from the
ambient air and polymer reorganization to a less high-energy state [4]. This project proposes that
the former problem could be solved by storage in an inert atmosphere. The latter might be solved
by coating the surface with a thin, transparent metal or ceramic layer. Telecka et al. (2018) [2]
found that coating the surface with Wolfram (W) or Silica (SiOx), not only increases the surface
energy but also prolongs the super wetting properties. In this project, it is hypothesized that the
combination of a super hydrophilic, high energy surface with a thin, transparent Wolfram coating
and storage under an inert Nitrogen (N2) atmosphere will prolong the super wetting properties of
the surfaces, long enough to be considered for commercial use.

Materials and Methods
This section describes the materials and methods used for what was found to be the optimal
process for making anti-fogging surfaces with BCP lithography. The process was developed on
the basis of work done by T. Li et al., [8, 22, 23] and A. Telecka et al. [3, 2]. The ﬁnal, optimized
process ﬂow can be seen in appendix A.
The methods of sample testing and evaluation are also described. The anti-fogging surfaces - or
samples - produced, are PMMA discs, 50 mm in diameter (Ø50 mm) and about 2 mm thick.
They are subsequently split into diﬀerent types based on whether they are structured or ﬂat, and
whether they are uncoated, Wolfram coated or plasma treated. Furthermore, they are separated
based on whether they were stored in a normal or a Nitrogen atmosphere.
All chemicals were purchased from SigmaAldrich, with the exception of the SD24 BCP itself,
which had been synthesized at DTU prior to this project2. The majority of this section is also
described in [6, 3, 10, 2]. An overview of the process is illustrated in ﬁgure 9.
Several iterations were necessary to develop the optimal process. Some wafers had to be sacriﬁced
along the way for individual process optimization steps, others had to be discarded due to process
failures and human errors. A summary of the process parameters used for each individual wafer
can be found in appendix B.

3.2
Pattern transfer and pattern inversion
To transfer the self-assembled BCP pattern to the silicon substrate, a series of reactive ion etch
(RIE) steps are needed. RIE processes combine the typically isotropic etching of chemicals with
the anisotropic, physical etching of ion bombardment to achieve fast and unidirectional etches in a
variety of substrates [53, 6]. This is done by having a radio frequency (RF) coil generate a plasma
of volatile etchant gases, which are directed towards the substrate by an electrical ﬁeld [53, 6]. In
this case an advanced silicon etcher (ASE) is used (STS MESC Multiplex ICP serial no. 30343).
First, a 15 s sulfur hexaﬂuoride (SF6) etch is used to penetrate the so-called wetting layer of the
BCP, which is essentailly a residual layer of PDMS [2, 22]. The etching parameters are, 50 sccm
gas ﬂow rate, 20 mTorr pressure, 20 ◦C platen temperature, 0 W platen power and 50 W coil
power [6, 3, 2].
Then an 11 s Oxygen (O2) etch is used to etch away the PS blocks of the BCP-ﬁlm while turn-
ing the PDMS part of the BCP-ﬁlm into a hard, silicon-oxycarbide mask as seen in ﬁgure 10
[8, 22, 23, 3, 2, 6]. The etching parameters are, 10 sccm gas ﬂow rate, 5 mTorr pressure, 20 ◦C
platen temperature, 10 W platen power and 200 W coil power [6, 3, 2]. These etching parameters
were chosen based on a pre-study made in the fall of 2017 [6].
The results of this RIE step was evaluated in a scanning electron microscope (SEM) as can be
seen in ﬁgure 20 in subsection 4.2. The SEM used for these images was a Zeiss Supra 40 VP. The
distance measurements on the SEM images were done afterwards in Microsoft PowerPoint 2013.
The average pitch values (center-to-center distances) measured between the nano holes, are found
in table 6 in subsection 4.2.
The hard, silicon-oxycarbide mask was then used as an etching mask in the next RIE step, in order
to transfer the pattern to the silicon substrate. This RIE step is conducted using an inductive
coupled plasma etcher (ICP). The machine used is a Metal ICP (SPTS Serial number MP0637).
The etching is done by a 35 s initial SF6 etch to break through any native oxide that might have
formed [23]. The etching parameters are, 20 sccm gas ﬂow rate, 5 mTorr pressure, 20 ◦C platen
temperature, 50 ◦C chamber heater temperature, 45 ◦C lid heater temperature, 10 W platen power
and 100 W coil power [6, 3, 2].
Then a 20 s chlorine (Cl2) etch is used to actually transfer the mask pattern and deepen the shal-
low holes in the silicon substrate (see ﬁgure 12) [3, 2]. The etching parameters are, 20 sccm gas
ﬂow rate, 5 mTorr pressure, 20 ◦C platen temperature, 50 ◦C chamber heater temperature, 45 ◦C
lid heater temperature, 40 W platen power and 300 W coil power [6, 3, 2]. The RIE is followed
by submerging the wafer in water for 5 min as the Cl2-molecules tend to stick to the sample [54].
The wafer is then dried oﬀusing a nitrogen gun. Nothing is left of the silicon-oxycarbide mask
after this RIE step [22, 23]. This step will henceforth be known as "ICP(1)" as it is also named in
the process ﬂow in appendix A.

After the ICP(1) step, the pattern needs to be inverted from holes to pillars.
This is done
by depositing a 30 nm alumina (Al2O3) layer on top of the patterned wafer using atomic layer
deposition (ALD) [3]. ALD is a chemical vapor deposition (CVD) method. It works by switching
between two or more gasses or precursors, which are introduced individually into the chamber, with
an intermediate purging step after each introduction [55]. This will cause the gases to deposit and
react on the substrate surface, thus forming an atomic monolayer [55]. The process is self-limiting
to one atomic layer at a time [53]. By cycling between the gases a number of times, a rather uniform
thin-ﬁlm with high conformity can be achieved, several nanometers thick [55, 53]. In this case the
ALD was conducted in a Picosun ALD model R200. A small chip of bare Silicon was introduced
in the chamber together with the wafers, in order to subsequently measure the Al2O3-thickness
using the Ellipsometer VASE. The ﬁlm was grown by ﬁrst introducing 97 % trimethylaluminium
(TMA) for 0.2 s at 200 ◦C, then purging the chamber for 25 s, then introducing deionized water
(DI-water) for 0.2 s and purging for 25 s at 200 ◦C, then repeating the process [3, 2]. After 300
cycles, a layer of about 30 nm was deposited all over the wafer, as can be seen in ﬁgure 23 and
table 8, in subsection 4.4. The thickness of the deposited Al2O3-layer was measured both on the
bare Silicon chip using the Ellipsometer VASE, and in SEM by looking at the cleaved edges of
"spec2 8ml-13 q1" and "spec2 8ml-13 q2" after the Al2O3-deposition.
Due to the nature of the ALD process and the size and pitch of the holes, a thicker layer of Al2O3
will be deposited in the holes (see ﬁgure 23 in subsection 4.4) [56]. This is used to invert the
pattern in the Silicon substrate from holes to pillars, as the etch rate in Silicon is higher than
in Al2O3[3]. The process is illustrated in ﬁgure 12. Again, the Metal ICP is used. The etching
itself consists of a 3 min 30 s Cl2-etch. The process parameters are 20 sccm gas ﬂow rate, 5
mTorr pressure, 20 ◦C platen temperature, 50 ◦C chamber heater temperature, 45 ◦C lid heater
temperature, 40 W platen power and 300 W coil power [3, 2]. This process step will henceforth
be known as "ICP(2)" as it is also called in the process ﬂow in appendix A. The etching time

3.3
Nickel shim
When the hexagonal hole pattern has been converted into a hexagonal pillar pattern by the ICP(2)
process, the actual Nickel shim can be formed. An illustration of the shim formation process can
be seen in ﬁgure 13.
Figure 13: Illustration of the ﬁnal shim forming process. First, a metallic seed layer, in this case,
Titanium/Gold (Ti/Au), is sputter deposited to replicate the inverse pattern of the Silicon pillars,
make good conduction for the electroplating process, and in the case of Ti/Au, act as an etch stop
for potassium hydroxide (KOH). Then, Nickel is electroplated on top of the seed layer. Then, the
Silicon wafer itself is removed in a KOH etch. Finally, the seed layer is covered in an anti-sticking
FDTS coating using MVD.
In order to get a suﬃciently thick shim, electroplating of Nickel is used. However, electroplating
requires a good electrical conductor, and Silicon, being only a semiconductor, is not4 [58, 55].
Thus a so-called "seed layer" metal is deposited onto the Silicon wafer, in order to have a suﬃ-
ciently conducting substrate for the electroplating process. As the shim is to be used for injection
molding, and thus will be exposed to massive pressure forces, the seed layer has to be quite hard.
Nickel-Vanadium (NiV) is usually used as it is very hard and has good adherence to Silicon [58].
However, it oxidizes rather quickly unless stored in an inert atmosphere, or unless Nickel is elec-
troplated very soon after the seed layer deposition [58]. Instead a Titanium/Gold (Ti/Au) seed
layer was tried out in this project. It is not as hard as NiV, but should still be hard enough for
4Unless it is heavily doped [57], which is not the case here.

this application. The advantages of this seed layer are that it does not oxidize as fast as NiV,
and that Gold and Titanium are insusceptible to potassium hydroxide (KOH) etching [59], which
is an advantage when etching away the Silicon wafer later. Furthermore, because of the golden
colour of the Ti/Au seed layer, compared to the grayish colour of the NiV seed layer, it will be
easier to see when the gray Silicon wafer has been etched away in the KOH process.
The Ti/Au seed layer is deposited by sputter deposition using a Lesker CMS 18 Sputter System
(Kurt J. Lesker Company). Sputtering works by ionizing a chemically inert gas like Argon - i.e.
essentially making an Argon plasma - and accelerating the ions towards a target by applying an
electrical ﬁeld over the plasma [60]. The Argon-ions then knock out (sputter) atoms from the
target, and these free atoms are then allowed to travel to, and condense on, the substrate that is
to be sputter coated [53, 60]. An RF bias is also applied to simultaneously sputter impurities and
oxides oﬀthe substrate, while depositing atoms from the target [53, 61]. This approach gives lower
surface roughness [53, 61]. Meanwhile, the substrate is also rotated to ensure the best possible
coverage [61, 53].
In principle there are many ways of depositing a seed layer, e.g. e-beam evaporation, ALD, and
other CVD-methods5 [60], however, sputtering is a good compromise between dense layers, good
step coverage and a fast growth rate [58, 60].
First, a ca. 10 nm layer of Titanium is sputtered onto the Silicon wafer. The sputtering is done
in an Argon plasma, the sputtering time is 30 s, at 3 mTorr pressure, 100 W power and 21 ◦C.
The RF-bias is 20 W and the sample is rotated slowly during the sputtering process.
The deposition of Titanium is done because Gold has poor adhesion to bare Silicon, so Titanium
is used as a mediator between the two, because it adheres well to both bare Silicon and Gold [58].
Then, an 80 nm Gold layer is deposited on top of the Titanium. The sputtering is again done in
an Argon plasma, the sputtering time is 500 s, at 3 mTorr pressure, 100 W power and 21 ◦C. The
RF-bias is 20 W and the sample is rotated during the deposition.
Gold is used here because it is a lot easier to electroplate Nickel on Gold compared to Titanium
[62]. Titanium oxidizes, making a passive Titanium-dioxide (TiO2)-ﬁlm which prevents suﬃcient
bonding of the Nickel [62].
During the sputtering process, the Silicon wafer is clamped with four tiny clamps, one in each
corner, so that as much of the wafer as possible is covered with the seed layer. This is done, in
order to get as good electrical contact as possible in the subsequent electroplating process [58].
A NiV seed layer was also deposited on a diﬀerent wafer ("spec2 8ml-11") in case the Ti/Au seed
layer proved too soft, or otherwise failed. This seed layer was deposited in the Lesker CMS 18
Sputter System as well. The sputtering parameters were 158 W DC power, a 20 W RF sub-
strate bias, 3 mTorr pressure and a temperature of 21 ◦C. The sputtering was done for 1000 s
(16 min 40 s), while rotating the substrate, which should result in an 85 nm thick layer of NiV
with excellent step coverage. For enhanced seed layer adhesion, the wafer was treated with a
soft Oxygen plasma before the sputtering, to activate and clean the surface. The Oxygen plasma
was done at 20 sccm gas ﬂowrate for 10 s at 3 mTorr pressure, 200 W coil power, 20 W platen
power, a platen temperature of 20 ◦C, a chamber temperature of 50 ◦C, and 45 ◦C on the lid heater.
After the seed layer deposition, 350 µm of Nickel is deposited on top of the seed layer, using elec-
troplating. Due to the aforementioned rapid oxidation rate of NiV, the electroplating process on
this wafer ("spec2 8ml-11") was started within 24 hrs. of seed layer deposition. If the NiV had
been allowed to oxidize too much, it would have passivated the surface, causing problems with
the electrical conductance [58].
The electroplating process is done using a Technotrans microform.200. The wafer is submerged
into an electrolyte bath of nickel sulfamate (H4N2NiO6S2), boric acid (H3BO3) and sulfamic acid

Current is applied to the anode, thus creating a potential diﬀerence between the anode and the
cathode. The anode in this case, is a Titanium basket ﬁlled with Nickel pellets and the cath-

and eﬀectively dissolves the Nickel-ions (Ni2+) in the electrolyte [58, 63]. The positively charged
Nickel-ions are then attracted to the negatively charged cathode, and then reduce to solid Nickel
upon contact with the metal-plated wafer [63].
The process runs for 6 hrs. 12 min 52 sec. It starts by slowly ramping up the current by 0.1 A
every three minutes for the ﬁrst 15 minutes, until it reaches 0.5 A. Then the ramping is increased
to 0.2 A every three minutes for the next 15 minutes, until the total current is 1.5 A. Then the
current sits idle at 1.5 A for 1 hr, before the ramping ramping is increased again to 1 A every
three minutes for six minutes, until the total current is 3.5 A. It stays at 3.5 A for the remainder
of the process. This gives a total charge of 18.3 Ah, which essentially determines the thickness of
the deposited Nickel, 350 µm in this case [58]. The program is optimized with respect to growth
rate, surface roughness and Nickel density, for the purpose of injection molding [58]. The lower
the current, the lower the growth rate, and the lower the surface roughness [58]. By starting the
electroplating process at a low current ramp rate, the replication of the Silicon structures should
be good, and the surface roughness low [58].
After the electroplating, the Silicon wafer needs to be removed so that only the Nickel shim with
the seed layer is left. This is done in a 28% potasssium hydroxide (KOH) bath [64].
The etch rate of Silicon is highly dependent on temperature, KOH concentration and crystal ori-
entation [65, 66]. Generally, the etch rate increases exponentially with increasing temperature
[65]. The etch rate peaks at about 20% concentration and then decreases with increasing KOH-
concentration [66]. It is hypothesized that this is because the real etchant is the hydroxy-ions
(OH−) of water, while KOH itself is used mainly to dissociate the water [66]. Thus, decreasing
the water content too much will decrease the reduction rate of water at the Silicon surface, and
thus decrease the etch rate [66]. The exponential temperature dependence of the etch rate is
hypothesized to be a classic Arrhenius dependence and the reason for the decreased etch rate of
<111> Silicon is hypothesized to be because the number of back-bonds is higher for this plane,
and it is these back-bonds that needs to be broken in order to dissolve the Silicon [66].
Since the wafer chosen was a <100> wafer, the etch rate at 80 ◦C and 28% KOH should be around

the nano structured samples. The injection molding parameters were optimized by evaluating the

Storage study
To evaluate the degree of hydrophilicity for the diﬀerent samples, and to see how this hydrophilic-
ity would evolve over time, contact angle measurements were conducted using an Attension Theta
droplet analyzer (Biolin Scientiﬁc, model C204A - see ﬁgure 17a). There were four types of sam-
ples; coated ﬂat, coated structured, uncoated ﬂat and uncoated structured. The samples were
injection molded using the parameters described in table 3. The coated samples were sputter
coated with Wolfram for 10 s as explained earlier. All samples were stored in plastic sample
holders made for Ø50 mm wafers.
Each Ø50 mm PMMA sample was divided into quadrants using a black marker pen to draw a
cross on the back side of the sample (see ﬁgure 17c). Then, the sample was placed on the stage
and a 6.5 µL drop of Milli-Q water was placed on the top side in three places within a quadrant
(see ﬁgure 17b). The experiment was conducted at room temperature, the Milli-Q water was kept
at room temperature as well. The contact angle for each droplet was measured using the Young-
Laplace ﬁtting method [3] embedded in the software of the system (Attention Theta software, ver.
4.2). After use, the used quadrant of the sample was dried oﬀwith a clean napkin and the used
quadrant was marked with an "X" on the back using the black marker pen. The sample was then
stored in a plastic sample holder until the next measurement session on it. Three samples of each

5
Discussion
This section discusses the process parameters that was decided on, and the results obtained
from the various investigations. The section is divided into subsections, each discussing diﬀerent
elements of the project.
5.1
Process parameters
This subsection discusses the used process parameters in a more or less chronological way, with
the exception of the seed layer deposition, electroplating, and KOH etching parameters, which are
discussed under the "Fish-scale investigation" in subsection 5.2.
In the pre-project made in the fall of 2017, the eﬀect of the cyclohexane dilution volume on the
pattern formation was investigated [6]. Here, it was found that 1 ml mother solution to 8.5 ml
cyclohexane was found to be optimal [6]. However, this result was obtained with the old mother
solution which had been prepared by A. Telecka [6]. Thus, a new mini-investigation was made,
where 1 ml of the new mother solution ("spec2") was diluted in 8 ml and 8.5 ml cyclohexane
respectively. They were named "spec2 8ml-1" and "spec2 8.5ml-1" respectively. After the spin
coat, annealing and ASE steps, it was found that the "spec2 8.5ml-1" had larger, circular patches
where there were no structures (see ﬁgure 21). "spec2 8ml-1", on the other hand, looked ﬁne. It
was thus decided to henceforth go with a dilution of 1 ml mother solution 8 ml cyclohexane. The
reason for the pitting could have been that the solution was too dilute, and thus there were too
little PS left to actually cover the surface. Why this was not seen with the old mother solution,
and why 8 ml cyclohexane was suddenly a lot better than 8.5 ml cyclohexane is unclear. However,
in the pre-project, dilutions ranging from 7 ml to 9 ml cyclohexane were spin coated, annealed and
etched in the ASE with only minor variations in pitch and coverage [6]. It might just have been
chance, that "spec2 8.5ml-1" caused pitting here. Similar pitting was observed for "spec2 8ml-7"
after the ASE step (see ﬁgure 77 in appendix C). Here, an 8 ml cyclohexane dilution was used,
and all the coating, annealing and etching parameters were the same as for the other "spec2 8ml-"
samples. However, still this pitting occoured. So, the cause of it remains a mystery, yet a likely
candidate could be insuﬃcient gas pressure during the annealing, perhaps due to an insuﬃcient
amount of methyl-cyclohexane.
The spin coating parameters used (see subsection 3.1) resulted in an average thickness of 27.03 ±
0.63 nm for all the "spec2 8ml-" samples (see table 5). This thickness is hypothesized to create
cylindrical rather than spherical PS-blocks, because of the restriction in the height dimension.
Since the thickness of the spin cast thin ﬁlm was so uniform, and resulted (generally) in well-
deﬁned hexagonal patterns, the parameters were kept as they were.
The annealing time was investigated a bit, by altering the annealing time ±30 min for the samples
"spec2 8ml-4" and "spec2 8ml-5". This had no apparent impact on the pattern or morphology,
and so they were continued as normal. SEM images of the two samples after the ASE step can
be seen in ﬁgures 78a and 78b in appendix C. As the coverage and uniformity in pitch seemed to
be alright, and since the morphology had the desired properties, the 2 hrs. 40 min annealing time
was kept.
After the ASE step, the pitch was measured. It appears that a trend occours where the ﬁrst half
of the samples have a lower pitch around 71 nm and the latter half has a higher pitch around 75

The eﬀects of altering the SF6 and O2 etching times in the ASE step had already been investigated
in the pre-project in the fall of 2017 [6]. Thus, they were kept as they were (see subsection 3.2),
as they seemed to produce consistent and good results. An attempt at getting a cross-sectional
SEM image of a wafer after the ASE step was tried on "spec2 8ml-13", however, it was not possible
to image the holes from the side. Either they were too shallow to image, or the contrast of the
SiOxCy mask was not high enough. In any case, the SiOxCy mask acted very well as an etch mask
in the subsequent ICP(1) step. No adjustment of the gas ﬂows nor the etchant gasses, was looked
into, as the results were as desired.
The Cl2 etching time of the ICP(1) step had already been investigated by Telecka et al. (2018)
[3], and thus the 20 s Cl2 etching time was kept. Nevertheless, a small investigation of the eﬀect
of increasing the etching time to 40 s or 60 s, was conducted on the pieces of "spec2 8ml-13", as
described in subsection 3.2. Based on the results seen in ﬁgure 22 in subsection 4.3, it was evident
that longer etching times gave deeper and narrower holes, which would have been harder to ﬁll
with Al2O3 [56]. When depositing in deep, narrow trenches, bridges might form at the top, which
eﬀectively seals the opening to the trench, thus preventing further deposition of metal inside the
trench [56]. This conﬁrmed that 20 s was the better Cl2 etching time, matching what was found
by Telecka et al. (2018) [3, 2]. No adjustment of gas ﬂows or the use of other etchant gasses was
deemed necessary.
The Al2O3 thickness of 30 nm, deposited by ALD, was also found to be quite good, as evident
from the image of "spec2 8ml-13 q1" in ﬁgure 23 in subsection 4.4. The uniformity and coverage
was good, and the desired thickness of 30 nm was thick enough to ﬁll the holes completely. While
no apparent bridging occurred for the deposition in the deeper trenches of "spec2 8ml-13 q2", and
thus a more profound etch mask would have come of etching the holes deeper in the ICP(1) step,
it was deemed unnecessary. The total thickness of the Al2O3 layer deposited in the holes of the
samples that were etched with Cl2 for just 20 s, was more than enough to create a suﬃcient etch
mask for the subsequent ICP(2) step. An even thicker etch mask would have just resulted in
longer etching times and thus higher pillars after the ICP(2) step, and this might have caused
further problems in the seed layer deposition step.
A thinner Al2O3 layer, however, might not have ﬁlled the holes completely. This could have al-
lowed for undercut structures as shown by Telecka et al. (2018) [3].
The optimal Cl2 etching time in the ICP(2) step was deemed to be 3 min 30 s. The result of this
etch can be seen on the SEM images of "spec2 8ml-2 q2" in ﬁgure 24. By comparing the images
on ﬁgure 24, it was deemed that the 3 min 30 s etch gave the best straight pillars, as also found
by Telecka et al. (2018) [3, 2]. Longer etching times resulted in more conical shapes and higher,
presumably more brittle pillars. Shorter etching times might have resulted in insuﬃcient contrast
for the seed layer deposition, or patches with residues of non-etched Al2O3.

The hydrophobic anti-stiction FDTS coating applied to the ﬁnal shim is made for exactly that
purpose, anti-stiction. By having this hydrophobic surface, the molten PMMA should not stick
to the shim as easily as it would have to the bare metallic seed layer, which is actually hydrophilic
in most cases [9]. This should prevent stretching and breakage of the PMMA nanopillars during
the demolding. A monolayer of FDTS is suﬃcient, as all it needs to do is lower the free surface
energy of the shim, without altering the morphology of it. The only requirement for the FDTS
coating to work, is that it has a thin oxide on the surface to interact with [67], and this oxide
is formed naturally whenever metals - including Titanium [62, 72] - are exposed to the atmosphere.
The injection molding parameters used, were decided based on which parameters gave the optimal
replication, with the least amount of ﬂash and diesel eﬀect. The parameters were developed by a
continuous optimization process as described in subsection 3.5. As the holes were nano sized, quite
high temperatures and pressures were needed in order to make the PMMA suﬃciently non-viscous
to ﬁll the cavities. Relatively long cooling times were needed, both due to the high temperatures,
as well as to prevent pillar elongation and breakage during the demolding.
These parameters were later optimized further to reduce the molding time per sample, as evident
in table 4. The main diﬀerence again lay in a lower molding temperature, both for the ﬂat and
structured samples, as well as a higher injection pressure for the structured samples to make up
for the lower temperature. The ﬁlling was still deemed suﬃcient.

5.2
Fish-scale and seed layer investigation
This subsection discusses the materials and methods, as well as results of the ﬁsh-scale investiga-
tion.
The initially chosen seed layer was made from Titanium and Gold, for reasons already explained
in subsection 3.3. Furthermore, it had been observed internally in the group that NiV seed layers
could give rise to ﬁsh-scales after the KOH etch, though it was never investigated what caused
them. Thus the Ti/Au seed layer was deemed a more robust candidate as the Gold and Titanium
was supposed to be inert to KOH [59].
From the images by C. H. Nielsen in ﬁgure 35, it appears that proper ﬁsh-scales arise from over-
etching of Nickel. While the 90 ◦C KOH etch of the "Lesker-2" shim did seem to attack the Ti/Au
seed layer in some way (see ﬁgure 34), ﬁsh-scales as such were not created. At least not over the
course of 6 hrs. The sample, "spec2 8ml-8", which did get ﬁsh-scales from a 6 hr. KOH etch at
90 ◦C, must have been over etched somehow. It is possible that the e-beam deposited seed layer,
maybe in conjunction with the higher aspect ratio pillars resulting from the 4 min Cl2 etch that it
got in the ICP(2) step, did not properly cover the Silicon, and thus allowed the underlying Nickel
to be attacked, resulting in an over etch (see discussion further down).
In any case, it was decided to go with sputter deposited seed layers, and a lower etching temper-
ature of 80 ◦C from then on, just to be sure.
It is still interesting that the Ti/Au seed layer was attacked by the KOH at all, as it should have
acted as an etch stop [59]. However, Powell et al. (2006) [73] found that Titanium at least, was
resistant to KOH only when the temperature was below 80 ◦C. This might give a clue as to why
surface damage was evident after the 90 ◦C KOH etch of "Lesker-2".
NiV is usually the chosen seed layer for Nickel shims to be used in the injection molder, because
it is quite hard and has good adhesion to Silicon [58]. Even though it seemed impossible to ﬁnd a
speciﬁc hardness value for it, NiV should be very hard and thus ideal for high-pressure processing
such as injection molding. Gold on the other hand should be softer, but the Titanium covering
it should be much harder. The Vickers’ hardness of the four elements individually, are shown in
table 12, the thermal expansion coeﬃcients of each element are also shown.

The SEM images of the PMMA samples and the shims after the injection molding process as

the samples for the purpose of making anti-fogging lenses by spreading water through a wicking

was believed to be poor sample handling during a toluene (C7H8) cleaning, which was applied
instead of the normal post-mold cleaning in ethyl acetate to really get rid of any excess polymer.

were imaged and scratches were found all over (see ﬁgure 71), though there were some pristine
patches as the one imaged in ﬁgure 27c. The sizes of the pristine patches seemed to be up to a
few mm2. The subsequent imaging of the shim itself (images in ﬁgure 69) showed that indeed the
shim was criss-crossed with lines, but that the desired hole-structure was still visible beneath the
lines as seen on ﬁgure 69c. This meant that the shim was not scratched at all, but that the lines
must have been either some sort of diesel eﬀect or simply strands of molten PMMA which was
stuck to the shim.

5.4
Transmission studies
This subsection concerns the transmission measurements that were done during the project. The
anti-reﬂective behaviour, transmission loss due to diﬀerent coating thicknesses and the develop-
ment of the transmissive behaviour over time are all discussed.
The nano structuring of the PMMA samples did actually make them anti-reﬂective, as can be
seen in ﬁgure 39. The gain in transmission is only about 1 %, but it is consistent and signiﬁcant.
The structures have in no way been optimized with anti-reﬂective properties in mind, though they
do look a bit like moth-eye structures [41]. Even though the gain is quite small, it is still relevant
as it ﬁrst and foremost tells that the samples are not obscured by the structuring. Secondly, the
anti-reﬂective properties enables the structured samples to be distinguished from the ﬂat samples,
as they cannot be told apart with the naked eye. Furthermore, it tells that the replication of the
nano structures in PMMA was successful, without the need for direct imaging in SEM.
As can also be seen on ﬁgure 39, the average transmission measured for the structured samples
does surpass the theoretical maximum transmission for the ﬂat samples (92.5 %), but fortunately
not the theoretical maximum transmission for the structured samples (94.3 %). The measured
average transmission for the ﬂat samples also stay below the theoretical maximum for ﬂat samples.
Thus both the theoretical derivations of maximum transmission, as well as the measured values,
seem to be valid.
A 10 s Wolfram coating, corresponding to roughly 8 nm, was deemed a good compromise between
obtaining a high energy surface, while not losing too much of the transparency. As is evident from
the transmission measurements over time for the open-air stored ﬂat samples coated with various
Wolfram thicknesses (ﬁgure 38), the transmission seems to initially increase over time and then
stabilize. Why this is so, remains unclear, however one suggestion is that the Wolfram oxidizes.
Wolfram can oxidize to Wolfram-monoxide (WO), Wolfram-dioxide (WO2) and Wolfram-trioxide
(WO3) at room temperature, but only slight amounts are oxidized [77]. This potential surface
oxidation would passivate the surface, ensuring that no more of the Wolfram below the surface
is oxidized. This would explain why the transmission remains stable after the initial increase.
Though WO3 in particular has a yellowish colour, it is hypothesized that the oxidation of Wolfram
decreases the permittivity of the coating, thus allowing more light to be transmitted. The samples
with the thickest Wolfram coating do have a slight yellow tint, which could be caused by the WO3.
This would also explain why the transmission through the samples with the thickest coating does
drop a little at week 16. In any case, it is good news for the applicability of these structured,
Wolfram coated PMMA samples as anti-fogging lenses.

5.5
Contact angle measurements
This subsection regards the contact angle measurements that were done over the course of this
project. The contact angle measurements were the main determinator for the degree of hydrophilic-
ity of the samples. The eﬀects of plasma treatment, coating and storage conditions, along with
the development of the contact angle measurements over time are all discussed. Ultimately, the
diﬀerent samples and storage conditions are evaluated in regards to their applicability for com-
mercial anti-fogging lenses.
By looking at ﬁgure 41 in subsection 4.11, quite a few interesting trends comes forth.
First and foremost, all the Wolfram coated samples start out with very low contact angles. The
Wolfram coated samples stored under open-air conditions exhibit and retains the lowest contact
angles by far, with the structured samples being the most hydrophilic.
The contact angles of the Wolfram coated samples stored in a Nitrogen atmosphere on the other
hand, sky-rockets. After a week, the contact angles of these samples lie together with all the un-
coated samples, and after two-three weeks, they are by far the least hydrophilic. This was rather
unexpected as the inert Nitrogen atmosphere was hypothesized to slow the ageing of the samples.
The reason for the faster ageing of the samples stored in Nitrogen is thought to be the pump-purge
process itself, since the samples may thus be disturbed more. Furthermore, the gas introduced is
maybe not clean, and thus the pump-purge procedure introduce and whirl up particles which may
settle on the surface of the samples causing increased hydrophobic recovery. The samples that were
just left in a box under a normal atmosphere, however, are less disturbed and thus get a smaller
degree of hydrophobic recovery. Nonetheless, this is good news for the commercial applicability of
the samples as anti-fogging lenses. If a thin Wolfram coating of the structured samples retains the
hydrophilic properties over a long period of time, and the best storage conditions are simply to re-
main undisturbed in a box under a normal atmosphere, this highly eases the handling of the lenses.
It is also interesting to notice, that the average contact angles for all the uncoated samples -
structured or ﬂat - lie close together in the middle, all within one st.dev. from each other. This
suggests, that the Wolfram coating has a role to play in regards to the increased fouling eﬀects of
the Nitrogen storage. If the increased fouling was only due to the pump-purge procedure of the
Nitrogen storage, an increased fouling of the uncoated samples would be expected as well. How-
ever, this is not seen. This could be because the Wolfram coating makes the free energy of the
surfaces a lot higher, and thus they are more susceptible to the possibly increased fouling caused
by the pump-purge procedure. This is very interesting, as it has been reported by Baier (2006)
[51], as well as Vesel and Mozatic (2012) [50], that increased surface energy actually resulted in
less fouling. Thus, it appears that there must be some threshold where increased surface energy
no longer result in an increase, but a decrease in the degree of fouling. This is also what was
reported by Baier (2006) [51]. The surface energy of the Wolfram coated samples produced in this
project, clearly must lie below this threshold. Yet for the case of the structured, Wolfram coated
samples, stored open-air, they still remain super hydrophilic.
In any case, it could be interesting to make a study where the samples stored in Nitrogen were
not subjected to the pump-purge procedure, but only to purging by Nitrogen. By removing the
pumping down part of the procedure, it is possible that whatever particles may have been intro-
duced when checking on the samples, would not be sucked towards and deposit on the samples in
the same amount. This will have to be investigated some other time, if it is deemed necessary at
all, since the stability of the open-air stored samples seems to be quite good and very long lasting.

Fog testing
The results of the "teapot test" gave a pretty good insight into the diﬀerences between the anti-
fogging properties of the diﬀerent sample types. It was clear that the untreated ﬂat sample became
obscured by the fogging, whereas the Wolfram coated ﬂat sample remained transparent (see ﬁgures
53 and 55).
The uncoated structured sample was obscured even more than the untreated ﬂat sample due to
larger droplets forming (see ﬁgure 54). This is attributed to the pinning eﬀects that were observed
to give locally increased contact angles in this project (see for instance ﬁgure 74), as well as by
Telecka et al. (2018) [3, 2]. The Wolfram coated structured sample on the other hand, had the
water spread out to a continuous, transparent ﬁlm exactly as desired (see ﬁgure 56).
The plasma treated ﬂat sample was obscured a little as well (see ﬁgure 57). The obscuring looked
a lot like the obscuring observed for the untreated ﬂat sample. This is further conﬁrmed by the
contact angles measured on the ﬂat plasma treated sample (see ﬁgure 51) which were only slightly
smaller than the contact angles observed for the untreated ﬂat samples (see ﬁgure 41).
The structured plasma treated sample (ﬁgure 58) exhibited a high degree of obscuring due to
fogging. This was unexpected, as the measured contact angles on the plasma treated structured
samples were rather low (see ﬁgure 51). However, the st.dev. on these contact angle measurements
were rather big, so this might have been one of the samples which was less hydrophilic. A larger
sampling size is needed in order to ﬁnd out if the plasma treatment of the structured samples is
as unreliable as it appears here.
A few ﬁnal comments about this experiment; ﬁrst of all, the temperature of the steam coming
from the kettle may have varied from test to test, this could maybe aﬀect the behaviour of the
condensation process. However, it was observed that the fog behaved rather similarly on the
untreated samples, but signiﬁcantly diﬀerent on the treated samples, so the inﬂuence of the steam
temperature is deemed minor. Secondly, the center image of ﬁgure 56 with the "ﬁngerprint" on,
clearly shows that the samples have to be treated carefully, and that the surfaces are rendered
useless if wiped oﬀor touched with the bare hands.
Regarding the development of the macro scale fog behaviour on the samples at day 1 compared to
day 10; The ﬁrst test was done on freshly treated samples, thus the contact angles have been very
low, as evident on the ﬁrst measuring points of ﬁgure 41, whereas the second test was done at day
10 when the wetting properties of the open-air stored samples would seem to have stabilized, at
least according to the contact angle measurements on ﬁgure 41.
Telecka et al. (2018) [2], found the surfaces to be anti-fogging as long as the Young contact an-
gle remained below 50◦, so from looking at ﬁgures 41 and 51, only the open-air stored ﬂat and
structured samples with the Wolfram coating would have remained super hydrophilic and thus
anti-fogging. However, from the images of the teapot test conducted on the ﬂat- and structured
Wolfram coated samples at day 10 (ﬁgures 60 and 62), only the structured Wolfram coated sample
seems to retain its super wetting and anti-fogging properties. Yet, the test was only done once
per sample type on day 1 and day 10 due to a shortage of samples, and thus there is no statistical
basis for saying that the ﬂat Wolfram coated samples do not remain anti-fogging.
Regarding the micro scale fogging study conducted in the environmental chamber, this test con-
ﬁrmed the ﬁndings of the macro scale teapot test, except for on one count.
The water ﬁlm on the plasma treated structured sample on day 1 of this experiment (see ﬁgure
64c) spread almost as well as on the structured Wolfram coated sample (ﬁgure 64b). This suggest
that this sample was one of the more hydrophilic of the plasma treated structured samples, and
thus questions the consistency of the plasma treatment.
The micro scale fog testing showed other interesting things as well. The droplets on the untreated
ﬂat sample (ﬁgure 63a) were more or less spherical, whereas the droplets on all the other samples

The spreading rate was also highly inﬂuenced by the type of surface treatment and structuring.
It was evident that the most hydrophilic samples had the fastest and most profound water spread

5.8
Applications
All the samples, and sample quarters which were used in contact angle or fog experiments during
this project were fresh. I.e. they had not been exposed to water or wetting beforehand. This
is also the immediate intended application for these anti-fogging lenses, namely as one-time use
consumables. This is ideal for e.g. laparoscopy examinations, but not for more permanent coatings,
such as on solar panels. Whether the surfaces would retain their super hydrophilic properties over
a prolonged period of time for continual wetting and drying/evaporation cycles has not been
investigated. It is possible that as long as the surface remains undisturbed by mechanical actions
such as wiping, the super hydrophilic surface properties will be retained. However, one can easily
imagine increased hydrophobic recovery when dust and minerals are deposited by rain, wind or
mist, which would result in rapid degrading of the super hydrophilic surface properties.

6
Conclusions
Fabrication of a Nickel shim capable of reproducing hexagonally arranged nano pillars in PMMA
was somewhat successful. It appeared that the molten polymer had a tendency to leave scorched
strands and polymer patches on the surface of the shim, which reduced the quality of the repli-
cated PMMA samples. However, the shim itself looked quite well and able, and this was the ﬁrst
aim of the project.
The shim was made by following the procedure of Telecka et al. (2018) [2]. During the fabrication
process it was learned that a sputtered seed layer of NiV was ideal for injection molding. A Ti/Au
seed layer was found too soft for injection molding purposes. E-beam deposition of the seed layer
was found to have too poor step coverage to properly replicate the nano structures.
It was found that a KOH etch at 80 ◦C for 7-8 hrs. would remove the Silicon wafer from the Nickel
shim without damaging the surface. Etching in KOH at 90 ◦C or over etching for several hrs. at
80 ◦C would cause scaly structures on the shim, even for a Ti/Au seed layer.
It was also found, in accordance with Telecka et al. (2018) [3, 2], that a 3 min 30 s Cl2 RIE was
ideal to invert the pattern into straight nano pillars when a 30 nm Al2O3 layer was used as an
in-situ etch mask.
The second aim of the project was to ﬁnd and evaluate a way to store the PMMA samples in a
manner so that they would still retain their super wetting properties. It was demonstrated that for
the case of the nano structured PMMA samples, they were capable of retaining a super hydrophilic
behavior for at least 8 weeks when coated with a circa 8 nm layer of Wolfram and stored in a plastic
tray with a lid. The 8 nm Wolfram coating lowered and retained the contact angles of the nano
structured PMMA samples at below 5 ◦. Further studies on likewise Wolfram coated ﬂat samples
showed, that the samples remained stable for at least 16 weeks, both in terms of wetting abilities
and transmissive properties. It was demonstrated that the nano structured samples exhibited an-
tireﬂective properties, increasing the total transmittance in the visual spectrum (410-700 nm) by
about 1 %. The transmittance was initially lowered from around 93 % to around 88 % by the 8 nm
Wolfram coating, but the eﬀect of the Wolfram coating on the hydrophilicity and stability of the
samples more than weighs up for the loss. Furthermore, the transmittance of the Wolfram coated
samples actually increased by about 2 % over time for the 8 nm coating on the ﬂat samples. It is
hypothesized that this increase in transmittance owes to oxidation of the very top layer of Wolfram.
The Wolfram coated PMMA samples that were stored in Nitrogen became almost hydrophobic
over time. It is believed that the cause of this is not the Nitrogen atmosphere, but rather the
pump-purge procedure the samples were exposed to whenever they had to be re-incubated. This
hypothesis was further strengthened by a test of the contact angles of some Gold coated samples
which had been exposed to the pump-purge procedure of the SEM they had been imaged in. The
Gold coated samples exhibited increased hydrophilic behavior prior to the SEM imaging - though
the contact angles were not as low as for the Wolfram coated samples - but exhibited decreased
hydrophilic behavior after the SEM imaging.
It is possible that just purging with Nitrogen had been better in terms of hydrophilic property
retention, but from the study of the ﬂat Wolfram coated samples, it seems redundant to go to such
complicated storage measures. The ﬂat Wolfram coated samples did lose some of their hydrophilic
properties after a week of storage, but remained stable thereafter. Even though the contact angles
of the ﬂat Wolfram coated samples remained below 40 ◦, the fog condensation tests showed that
only the structured Wolfram coated samples remained truly super wetting and thus anti-fogging.

Outlook
Fabricating anti-fogging lenses by utilizing superhydrophilic surface properties to make the con-
densed water droplets spread into a continuous transparent ﬁlm indeed seems possible.
The
PMMA samples fabricated in this project also seem to be able to retain their super wetting and
visually transparent properties for a long period of time when coated with a thin layer of Wolfram.
Thus, this method appears to be a very good candidate for large scale fabrication of industrial
anti-fogging lenses. It could be very interesting to see if the lenses could be scaled up by going to
larger shims and/or mounting the shims in a roll-to-roll process such as extrusion coating. Both
the BCP lithography, the plastic replication and the metallic coating all seem quite scalable at
least. It could also be interesting to see which other (cheaper) metals and ceramics might be
suitable for coating the PMMA samples, in terms of transparency, increased hydrophilicity and
long-term durability. Finally, it could be interesting to see if the Wolfram coated nano structured
samples would remain super wetting after continual wetting-drying cycles for a prolonged period
of time.

"spec2 8ml-1" was discontinued after the ICP(1) step due to over etching. The prolonged etching
time was used, because this was the etching time used back in the fall of 2017 when the pre-
project "33525 - Fabrication of Superhydrophilic Surfaces" [6] was written. That project ended at
the ICP(1) step, because of poor pattern transfer. The reason for the poor pattern transfer was
the over etching which became clear during the course of this project. The etching time should
have never been that long, which is also evident when reading "Nanotextured Si surfaces derived
from block-copolymer self-assembly with superhydrophobic, superhydrophilic, or superamphipho-
bic properties" by A. Telecka [3], published in January 2018. The long etching time of 5 min 50 s
was because someone had altered the process time in the recipe, without changing it back to its
original parameters of 20 s.
"spec2 8ml-2" was used for process optimization of the ICP(2) step, and was thus discontinued
after this step for obvious reasons.
"spec2 8ml-4" and "spec2 8ml-5" had their annealing times varied ±30 min compared to the usual
2 hrs. 40 min. After the ASE step, no real diﬀerence in pattern or surface morphology was ob-
served, so both wafers were just continued as normal.
"spec2 8ml-7" had circular, pattern free pits all over after the ASE step (see ﬁgure 77 in appendix
C), and was thus discontinued. It was the same type of pitting as had occurred for "spec2 8.5ml-1".
"spec2 8ml-3", "spec2 8ml-4" and "spec2 8ml-6" still looked under-etched after 3 min 30 s of Cl2
etch in the ICP(2) step when viewed from the top (breaking the wafer was obviously not an op-
tion). Thus, it was etched for an additional 30 s, but whether this had any eﬀect was unclear.
"spec2 8ml-6" had also only been etched with Cl2 for 15 s in the ICP(1) step. This, seemingly,
had no real eﬀect on the pattern transfer of the ICP(1) step. However, after the ICP(2) step, this
wafer looked even more non-uniform than "spec2 8ml-3", "spec2 8ml-4" and "spec2 8ml-5", and
thus it was discontinued.
"spec2 8ml-5", "spec2 8ml-8" and "spec2 8ml-9" were all etched for 4 min instead of 3 min 30 s,
because "spec2 8ml-3", "spec2 8ml-4" and "spec2 8ml-6" had looked under etched, as can be seen
in ﬁgures 80 and 79 in appendix C. However, it was later decided to return to the 3 min 30 s Cl2
etch in the ICP(2) step, after the images from the ICP(2) optimization on "spec2 8ml-2 quarters"
had been evaluated again.
"spec2 8ml-8" and "spec2 8ml-9 caught up to "spec2 8ml-3", "spec2 8ml-4" and "spec2 8ml-5" just
before the seed layer deposition step. To save time, it was decided to process them all at once,
and thus they were put in the Wordentec QCL 800. Titanium and Gold were not available for
sputtering at the time, but they were available for e-beam deposition. Thus the wafers had their
Ti/Au seed layer deposited by e-beam deposition, instead of sputtering, without taking into ac-
count the reduced step coverage of this deposition method. The step coverage was evaluated by
breaking "spec2 8ml-3" and imaging the cleaved edge as explained in subsection 3.4. The coverage
of the e-beam evaporated seed layer deposition was maybe okay, but the process itself was deemed
to be poor for the purpose of making an injection molding shim. The reason was, that the nano
grass pillars that would have been produced after etching away the Silicon wafer would likely have
been very thin and hollow, and likely incapable of withstanding the injection molding forces.
"spec2 8ml-8" had been deemed the prettiest of the wafers after the ICP(2) step and was thus

"spec2 8ml-8" was then etched in 28 % KOH for 6 hrs. at 90 ◦C, which turned out to cause ﬁsh

"Lesker-1" and "Lesker-2" were made to investigate the ﬁsh scale development as explained in
section 3.4. They had their seed layers sputter coated as they should have been, and then elec-
troplated and etched in KOH at diﬀerent temperatures until the Silicon wafer was gone. Based
on the SEM images of "Lesker-1" and "Lesker-2" the cause of the ﬁsh scales was deemed to be the
temperature being too high. The simultaneous investigation made by C. H. Nielsen, showed that
over etching, even at the lower temperature of 80 ◦C, caused ﬁsh scales as well.
Based on the successful removal of the Silicon substrate for "Lesker-1" at 80 ◦C, without damaging
the surface or seed layer, it was decided that all subsequent wafers should have their seed layer
sputter deposited and their Silicon substrate removed in KOH at 80 ◦C. Thus, all processing of
"spec2 8ml-3", "spec2 8ml-4", "spec2 8ml-5" and "spec2 8ml-9" was discontinued as it was deemed
too risky to electroplate them when their seed layer had been deposited by the wrong process.
Furthermore, "spec2 8ml-10", "spec2 8ml-11" and "spec2 8ml-12" had caught up and were ready
to be electroplated. This further discouraged the continuation on "spec2 8ml-3", "spec2 8ml-4",
"spec2 8ml-5" and "spec2 8ml-9".
In the mean time, the ICP(1) step had also been looked further into. "spec2 8ml-13" had been
split into three pieces, and each piece had been etched with varying Cl2 etching times as explained
in subsection 3.2. From the SEM images of the pieces, it was decided that 20 s Cl2 etching time,
as had generally also been used thus far, was optimal.
No process optimization has been done for the ALD step or the ASE step. The ASE step was
optimized already in the fall of 2017 during the pre-project [6]. The ALD process had been investi-
gated by A. Telecka [3], and it was deemed that a 30 nm Al2O3 layer was good for making straight
nano pillars [3]. A thinner layer of just 8 nm, gave rise to undercut structures or "hoodoos" in the
subsequent ICP(2) step [3].
On the basis of all that was learned, "spec2 8ml-10" had a Ti/Au seed layer deposited by sputter-
ing and was subsequently electroplated with Nickel. Then the wafer was removed in 28 % KOH
at 80 ◦C as decided on. The subsequent SEM imaging showed hexagonal structures replicated all
over the wafer, even if they were not as neat as the structures on the original shim made by A.
Telecka (see ﬁgure 26). Then the wafer was FDTS coated and cut to the appropriate size in the
Laser Micromachining Tool. When the shim, "spec2 8ml-10" was ﬁnally mounted in the injection
molder, and samples came out, it was clear that pattern replication was poor at best. Subsequent
imaging of the shim in SEM again, showed as explained, that the structures had been pressed ﬂat
and thus were incapable of replicating themselves in PMMA. Thus this shim was discarded.
After the failure of "spec2 8ml-10" with the Ti/Au seed layer, "spec2 8ml-11" had a harder NiV
seed layer sputter deposited instead. Furthermore, to improve adhesion the wafer was subjected
to a soft Oxygen plasma before sputter deposition, as explained in subsection 3.3. "spec2 8ml-11"
was then electroplated, and the wafer was removed in KOH at 80 ◦C as before. SEM imaging of

The shim that came out with the Ti/Au seed layer (“spec2 8ml-10”) did not work. Structures were

the surface, since an ultrasonic cleaning in DI-water had no effect. What did have an effect,

emerged in patches, and the remainder of the scale-free surface still looked amorphous and full of 
residues, so no further attempts at removing the scales were made.

 
Teapot tests and environmental chamber tests were also conducted on some old flat and 
structured samples in order to assess the anti-fogging properties of the samples. All sample types 
were tested, i.e. uncoated, Wolfram coated and plasma treated flat- and structured samples. The 
tests were done on day 0 of treatment and day 10 after treatment. While both the flat Wolfram 
coated, the structured Wolfram coated and the structured plasma treated samples exhibited super 
wetting properties on day 0 at least in the environmental chamber, only the Wolfram coated 
structured samples retained this super-wetting at day 10. 
 
A new shim (“spec2 8ml-11”) was finally made, using a harder NiV seed layer. The purpose of this 
shim was just to prove the concept that we could indeed make a shim which was capable of making 
anti-fogging surfaces in PMMA. As only two weeks were left of the project, when this shim came 
out, not much testing was done. The shim itself turned out quite nice, but the same type of 
‘scratches’ appeared on the shim after injection molding, as the scratches that was observed on the 
original shim by A. Telecka. This made the molded samples criss-crossed with lines and lowered the 
quality of the pattern replication in PMMA. Most profoundly, the transmission through the 
structured samples fell from about 93 % on the old samples to about 88 % on the new samples, 
which was lower than for the flat samples, meaning that the samples were diffracting rather than 
antireflective. Immediate contact angle measurements on the new samples, however, indicated 
that the newly molded samples might still be useful. Prolonged testing of the anti-fogging 
properties of these new samples could not be conducted prior to the hand-in of this report due to 
lack of time. 
 
An investigation into removal of these ‘scratches’, which were actually believed to be scorched 
polymer strands laying on top of the surface, was launched on the original shim, but no successful 
method was found prior to the hand-in of this report.
